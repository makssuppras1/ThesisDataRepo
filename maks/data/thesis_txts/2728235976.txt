Abstract
The thin film lithium niobate on insulator (TFLNOI) platform has gained a lot of interest within the
field of integrated photonics due to the recent advances in fabrication that have yielded remarkably
ultra-low losses. Lithium niobate has long been known for its unique optical properties such as its
high second-order nonlinearity and wide transparency window in the visible to infrared. Though with
the benefits of the nanometer-scale confinement that can be achieved in TFLNOI, the platform con-
stitutes a powerful and scalable platform for various classical and quantum applications. Typically,
efficient frequency conversion in TFLNOI is achieved by periodically poled waveguides. However,
the fabrication of poled waveguides in TFLNOI is complex. To provide a scalable alternative to
this method, this project considers modal phase matching in which the geometrical parameters of
the waveguide are adjusted to achieve efficient frequency conversion. Based on the TFLNOI plat-
form a double-ridge waveguide was designed for efficient and robust second-harmonic generation
(SHG). Perfect Type-0 phase matching was achieved between the fundamental TE waveguide mode
at 1550 nm wavelength and a higher-order TE mode at the second harmonic, allowing to access the
largest element of the second-order nonlinear susceptibility tensor. After thorough validation of the
numerical model, the theoretical maximal conversion efficiency was found to be comparable to the
state-of-the art efficiency in SHG. The fabrication tolerances of the phase-matching condition were
then simulated to ensure a decent performance, even in the worst cases considered. Complications
however arise when dealing with higher-order modes in parameter space. To resolve this matter, an
algorithm was developed that tracks higher-order modes as they evolve in parameter space. A chip
was designed with variations of the double ridge waveguide implemented. The chip is, as of the time
writing this thesis, being fabricated by F. Labbé in DTU Nanolab cleanroom facilities. Lastly, the po-
tential for using the proposed design for spontaneous parametric down-conversion to 1550 nm is then
investigated in a quantum mechanical framework. Here, the waveguide design displayed promising
results for generating very pure heralded single photons.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The journey of photonic integrated circuits (PICs) and nonlinear interactions goes all the way back
to the invention of the laser [1]. Solid-state sources quickly followed, and a few years in, all of these
processes in the micro-fabrication benefited from the shared or similar processes in Complementary
metal–oxide–semiconductor (CMOS) used in integrated electronics (IC).
Thus far, the development of PICs has been lagging behind their electronic counterparts in terms
of scalability and integration density, with the later onset of PICs industrialization in the 1980s.
While ICs have led to the immense commercialization of electronic devices, the field of PICs has to
a greater extent been a research topic. This can in part be attributed to the far greater complexity
associated with designing and fabricating PICs as compared to ICs. As referred to by L. Gasoania
et al. [2], this has led to the humorous saying: "Integrated photonics is the technology of the future
and will remain the technology of the future.". Though, the past couple of decades’ advances in
fabrications of PICs have led the field of integrated photonics to a much more mature state. Along
with the enhanced know-how for designing optical components, the industry of integrated photonics
has been radically boosted. This is not only within optical communication, where there is an ever-
increasing demand for higher data throughput and lower energy-per-bit-consumption but also optical
computing (classical and quantum), programmable photonics, on-chip systems for advanced imaging,
metrology, and sensing [3].
Considering the fact that modern society relies progressively more on digital infrastructure, the
adverse impact of cyberattacks is becoming more significant and critical to the well-functioning of our
civilization. It has been estimated that the global cost in 2020 was just under one trillion USD (i.e.
one million million USD); an increase of over 50% relative to the cost in 2018 [4]. It is well known
that Shor’s algorithm implemented on a quantum computer leads to the breakdown of encryption
methods such as RSA-encryption that relies on the use of trapdoor functions [5]. Consequently, the
demand for secure quantum encrypted telecommunication and its underpinning photonic hardware
is at an unprecedentedly high level. This has led to the global focus on integrated photonics and
the development of optical components for PICs. In late November 2022, an unbreakable quantum-
encrypted connection between the Niels Bohr Institute in Copenhagen and DTU in Lyngby was
made using a deterministic single-photon emitter (SPE) coupled to commercially available optical
fibers [6]. The mechanism of quantum encryption can be considered in a rather simple way. Since
the transmitted information is based on a series of single photons encrypted in what is known as
quantum key distribution, the only way that a third party can eavesdrop on this signal is by snatching
at least one of these photons. This can however almost instantly be detected by the receiver end,
where these photons will be missing; seen as an anomaly in the signal. The connection can then
immediately be shut down without information leaking. Hence, in theory, the quantum nature of
this communication method is such that it simply cannot be intruded, regardless of the computer
power used to intervene the connection. This is in contrast to classical encryption methods that rely
on the computational difficulty associated with decrypting complex trapdoor functions. A source for
single photons is not just a fundamental element in quantum communication and quantum computing
but also other exciting quantum schemes such as quantum sensing [7].

The earliest demonstration of an SPE used atomic transitions of sodium atoms [8].
At this
early stage, it was not a very efficient nor reliable source.
The device has since been optimized
by better cooling control of the atoms, making the SPE far more efficient. Though, this comes at
the cost of a complex setup [7], yielding it in-feasible for most on-chip applications. An alternative
and prevalent modality for generating single photons is to utilize the nonlinear process known as
spontaneous parametric down-conversion (SPDC) to generate pairs of photons. As the title of this
thesis indicates, this is the SPE modality of interest in this project. The conversion process in SPDC
occurs by a photon, denoted the pump photon, that interacts with a nonlinear medium such that
it splits into two separate photons. Now, as the name specifies, SPDC is a spontaneous or random
process and therefore does not constitute a deterministic source in which the photon pair is generated
on demand when triggered by a pump-pulse. However, the two generated photons are entangled,
meaning that the state of one of the generated photons cannot be described independently of the
other [9]. This highly correlated relation between the photon-pair means that the detecting of one
photon heralds the creation of the other photon [10]. Therefore SPDC is denoted a source of single
heralded photons. SPDC being parametric refers to the state of the medium being left unchanged
after its interaction with light. This simplifies matters a great deal since one does not need to consider
any energy lost to the material from the conversion process itself. Instead, all energy from the pump
photon can be considered to be transferred to the generated photon pair (see Figure 2.1).
As mentioned, the pump photons need to interact with a nonlinear medium in order for the con-
version process to take place. More specifically, since SPDC is a second-order nonlinear process, the
material needs to be crystalline and non-centrosymmetric, such to have a non-vanishing χ(2) electric
susceptibility [11, p.50]. In addition to the symmetry aspect, there are various other considerations
to make when choosing a material for frequency conversion in a given application. Though, it will
become apparent in Section 2.3 that the key material parameters for achieving efficient conversion
are a large magnitude of χ(2), low propagation loss, and a high refractive index for tight confinement
with a lower index insulator. In addition, a wide transparency window is attractive, since it provides
flexibility to convert to and from a large selection of frequencies. Numerous III–V semiconductors
materials possess these qualities typically in configurations with insulators of lower index to obtain
high confinement. Worth mentioning are GaAs and AlGaAs that are among the most nonlinear
optical crystals with χ(2) well above 200 pm V−1 [12, p.208][13]. Both of these have large refractive
indices and wide transparency windows in the infrared (IR). While GaAs suffers from two-photon
absorption, the fabrication methods for AlGaAs on insulator have been optimized to achieve propa-
gation losses below 1 dB cm−1 [13]. In the opposite spectral corner, Beta-Barium Borate (BBO) has
been the material of choice to generate light in the far-UV[14] with a χ(2) on the order of 5 pm V−1
[12, p.60]. One should keep in mind that the reported values of χ(2) for the same material do vary
somewhat throughout the literature (see Figure 2.4).
The aim of this project is to generate photons at the 1550 nm telecom-C wavelength by pump-
ing at 775 nm. For this application, Lithium Niobate (LN) is an optimal material due to its wide
transparency region ranging from 350 nm to 5 µm [15, p.247]. In addition, LN is known for its high
nonlinearity of χ(2) of up to 54 pm V−1[15, p.248] and losses reported as low as 2.4 dB m−1 in a
ridge-waveguide [16]. The design of the waveguide in this thesis is for this reason based on a X-cut
600 nm thin-film-lithium niobate (TFLN) layer on top of a 4.7 µm silica substrate.
As it will be stressed in Section 2.3, the central objective for achieving a good conversion efficiency
is to obtain phase matching. In this context, periodic poling of TFLN has widely been used to obtain
quasi-phase matching [17]. Here, the ferroelectric property of LN is utilized to periodically invert
domains separated at a fixed distance by applying an electrical field transversely across the waveguide.
While this method works very well, the fabrication setup for period poling is rather involved. As an
alternative method, phase matching can be achieved by employing the inherent birefringence of LN.
By varying the geometrical dimension of the waveguide, the effective index can be engineered such
to establish the condition for phase matching. This is known as modal-phase matching and is the
method used in this project.
Now, SPDC is a quantum mechanical phenomenon and should therefore be treated within a quan-
tum mechanical theoretical framework. Though, the same criteria that govern successful frequency

conversion in SPDC also apply to the reversed classical conversion process, namely sum of frequency

This particular case of SFG is therefore denoted second harmonic generation (SHG). The relation

In summary: a good design for SHG, is likely also a good design for SPDC. Since it is far more
convenient to work within the classical regime, the waveguide design is optimized for SHG of 775 nm
from a 1550 nm pump in a classical theoretical framework. It is important to note that even though
one can infer the performance of an SPDC from the performance SHG [18], there are significant
differences between the two. Therefore, a quantum mechanical description of SPDC is introduced in
Chapter 5 to properly evaluate the performance of the SPDC process.

for the propagation of power in a waveguide that becomes very useful in simulations and when
evaluating the contribution from the Kerr effect in Section2.5. In Chapter 3 the method used to

its frequency conversion performance. Section 3.5 then goes on to evaluate the fabrication tolerances

propagate through parameter space. Chapter 4 then regards the chip design and fabrication process.
Initially, Section 4.1 regards the considerations that were made for the mask design and in-coupling
into the chip. Section 4.2 then provides a rough description of the main fabrication steps that were
carried out by F. Labbé, followed by Section 4.3 where SEM images and AFM characterization of the

fundamental quantum mechanical framework, hereunder proper field quantization. Section 5.2 then
evaluates the purity of the generated photons by computing the joint spectral amplitude. Chapter 6
then finally summarises this thesis’ findings and the further work that could be interesting to carry
out.

Chapter 2
Nonlinear optics
The underlying mechanism for frequency conversion is the nonlinear interaction between light and
the medium in which light propagates. Nonlinearity is in itself purely a mathematical term referring
to the output of a function not being linearly related, or equivalently proportional, to the input.
Hence, nonlinear optics is a very broad subject encompassing all dynamics of light-matter interaction
that intrinsically are not linearly related. An interpretation of what constitutes a nonlinear optical
phenomenon is formulated by Rottwitt et al. [11, p.2], as when the propagation of an electromagnetic
field in a medium depends on the electromagnetic field itself. That is, the electromagnetic field alters
the optical properties of the medium, which in turn impacts the propagation of the electromagnetic
field that altered the medium in the first place. Here, the medium is not restricted to nonlinear
materials as even vacuum can become nonlinear if illuminated by high-intensity laser light [19].
As mentioned in Chapter 1, this project regards frequency conversion in lithium niobate (LN) by
utilizing its second-order nonlinear properties. This chapter aims to provide a thorough theoretical
framework to describe this second-order nonlinear interaction in LN with a focus on second harmonic
generation (SHG) and spontaneous parametric down-conversion (SPDC). Moreover, the theory pre-
sented in this chapter serves as the foundation for the simulations carried out in this project.
The first section introduces the fundamentals of classical nonlinear optics based on Maxwell’s
equations.
In addition, it will be argued why the numerical method used in this thesis is valid.
Restricting the focus solely to second-order nonlinear interactions, Section 2.3 covers the topic of
three-wave-mixing hereunder SHG and SPDC. Section 2.4 expands on this by incorporating symmetry
consideration and material parameters of LN, which further leads to the coupled amplitude equations.
For the specific case of SHG it is then argued why the phase matching condition is pivotal for successful
frequency conversion. Finally, Section 2.2 derives expressions for the propagation of power that are
later used in simulations as well as Section 2.5, where the impact of the Kerr effect will be evaluated.

the total fields as the superposition of these states. This allows one to operate within the frequency
domain, where the eigenmodes of a single frequency can be treated independently of the eigenmodes
of all other frequencies encompassed in the total field. This is very convenient in simulations since
it allows one to solve eq.(2.1.8) for only the single frequency component of interest. This approach
is therefore incorporated in the simulation method used in this project, denoted the finite difference
frequency domain (FDFD) method. Eq.(2.1.14) and eq.(2.1.15) are not strictly valid in nonlinear
optics [20, p.68], since both expressions rely on the linear superposition principle.
In nonlinear
optics, the frequency components are interdependent; in fact, it is the coupling between frequency
components of the field that constitutes the underlying mechanism of frequency conversion. So, what
argument makes the use of the superposition principle reasonable? As it will become apparent in
the next section, this is because the interaction between the three frequency components, casually
referred to as waves, is accounted for by the coupled amplitude equations eq.(2.4.3)-(2.4.5). It is
entirely valid first to employ the FDFD method to solve for the waveguide eigenmodes for each of
the three waves individually and then solve the coupled-amplitude equations to evaluate the power
relations between them. This is the basic procedure used to obtain the simulation results in Chapter 3.
With this settled, the fields are now defined in the frequency domain by taking the Fourier transform
of eq.(2.1.14) and eq.(2.1.15)

Chapter 3
Waveguide design and simulation
With a theoretical foundation established in the last chapter, the waveguide design process can now
be initiated. As it was argued in Chapter 1, a waveguide design optimized for SHG will in general
also be optimal for the reversed quantum mechanical process, namely SPDC. Particularly, the aim of
this project is to generate single heralded photons at the 1550 nm telecom-C wavelength by pumping
at 775 nm. Hence, the waveguide is optimized towards SHG of a 775 nm signal from a 1550 nm pump.
The optimization process is carried out by simulating waveguide designs using a FDFD eigenmode
solver. The aim is to find two modes, one for the pump and one for the signal, that interact under
the right circumstances such that frequency conversion process occur feasibly. In this regard, there
are a few main objectives. First and foremost, the modes need to be physical and well-guided by the
waveguide. The mode solver is able to output a large number of modes; however, only a selection
of these are physical. For example, some modes strongly interact with the boundaries of the mode
solver window. These modes are merely a representation of numerical artifacts and would not be
present in real waveguides. Hence these modes must be disregarded. Whether a mode is well-guided
or not is evaluated by the extent to which it is well-confined to the LN core. If the mode is not
well-confined to the core, it will leak out of the waveguide.
Secondly, the phase matching condition in eq.(2.4.11) must be fulfilled. As emphasized in Sec-
tion 2.4, this is a critical condition to be met in order to achieve feasible frequency conversion. In
addition, it is desirable to have a high coupling or modal overlap between the pump and signal modes
since this is strongly linked to high conversion efficiency (see eq.(2.4.6)).
The first section of this chapter covers the initial design considerations based on the provided LNOI
wafer and the fabrication capabilities at DTU Nanolab. Section 3.2 reviews the numerical method for
solving the coupled amplitude equations followed by Section 3.3 that validates the implementation
of this numerical method. In Section 3.4 the proposed design is revealed along with its simulation
results. Section 3.5 then evaluates how critical the phase matching condition is for the frequency
conversion process and investigates the extent to which the ideal design can be compromised from
fabrication uncertainties. Lastly, section 3.6 discusses the difficulties that arise from dealing with
higher-order modes and provides an algorithm for tracking higher-order modes.
3.1
Waveguide design
The template for the waveguide design is the LNOI wafer, illustrated in Figure 3.1, that was provided
by Y. Ding. The wafer consists of a 600 nm layer of X-cut lithium niobate (LN) on top of a 4.7 µm

confinement in the LN layer. Figure 3.1 also displays the crystal orientation given by the upper case
X, Y, and Z crystal coordinates. X-cut refers to the wafer being cleaved from its mother stone such
that all faces of the crystal are perpendicular to the X-axis.

3.2
Simulation method
The EMode finite difference frequency domain (FDFD) mode solver made by EMode Photonix was
used to solve for the mode-profiles e(x, y) and h(x, y) and their corresponding effective indices. Note
that the mode profile arguments, x and y, are given by the geometrical coordinate system in Figure 3.2
in agreement with the coordinates incorporated in the EMode SW. The design was implemented in the
EMode SW as depicted in Figure 3.2 with air on top of LNOI waveguide. The dispersion relations
for the ordinary and extraordinary refractive indices for LN were found from Sellmeier functions
provided by [31].
The primary source of linear loss in dielectric waveguides such as LNOI is scattering events [32,
p.93]. Scattering typically originates from surface roughness; especially side wall roughness has a
great impact on the linear loss[33]. The linear loss coefficients, αp and αs, were determined by using
EMode’s built-in scattering loss module. The module is based on the works of T. Barwicz et al. [34],
who model the scattering loss using a volume current method. The scattering loss in the waveguide
is evaluated based on two parameters: the correlation length and standard deviation of the surface
roughness. The correlation length is a measure of the distance between successive scatters, while
the standard deviation of the roughness quantifies the height of the scatters. These parameters are
highly dependent on fabrication and are difficult to quantify in practice. Hence, standard values
==PAGE:24==

were chosen. The standard deviations of the roughness were set to 2 nm and 0.2 nm for respectively
the x and y direction based on values provided by Y. Ding, while the correlation length was set to
default values of 80 nm and 100 nm for the x and y direction, respectively. This yielded loss coefficient
αp = 4.9 dB m−1 and αs = 7.3dB m−1, which are on the same order of magnitude as other LNOI

Tolerances
The design presented in the previous section showcases fine performance under ideal conditions. How-
ever, with an ambition to fabricate the design, one must embrace the fact that deviations from ideal
conditions are inevitable in practice. Various uncertainties are associated with each intermediate step
of fabricating waveguides. To address this, the margins of errors in fabrication must be considered,
and the impact that these have on the waveguide performance. The aim of this section is to determine
how robust the proposed design is to deviations from the ideal conditions given by the geometrical
parameters in Table 3.1 applied to the structure in Figure 3.2. What has been considered the most
likely fabrication aberrations are displayed in Figure 3.10. A cross-section of the ideal waveguide is
illustrated at the top of the figure while three types of deviations from the ideal design are considered
below. Ideally, the upper and lower ridge heights, Hur and Hur respectively, are equal at a an ideal

Since the design consists of two ridges, there are two separate etching steps. After the first etching
step, the etching rate can be determined and subsequently used to correct the second etching in
case the first etching step was too deep or shallow. With this in mind, four different scenarios are
considered as shown in Figure 3.10. (a1) and (a2) consider the cases in which the first etching step
is too deep or too shallow, respectively, but then the second etching step is corrected such that the

risk that need to be addressed.

The algorithm is initialized from a well-behaving state of the mode of interest (marked as the
green field in Figure 3.17) with a prior known mode index m and effective index neff,m. For the
sake of notation simplicity, the effective index is denoted by n, not to be confused with the refractive
index. The algorithm then steps from the initial element in parameter space denoted by i to the
next element i + 1. The known correct refractive index, n(correct) in i is then passed to element i + 1
as reference n(ref). The HOMTracker algorithm then recursively iterates through the number of
M modes until it finds a mode for which the effective index differs less than a specified threshold
value from reference n(ref) given by the previous element. Considering a mode with mode index m
somewhere in the parameter space specified by the element index i+1. If the corresponding effective
index of this mode deviates from its reference more than the threshold value, the mode index m is
not considered to represent the same mode as in element i. Therefore, the HOMTracker function
is recursively called until it finds the correct mode index that corresponds to a more continuous
evolution of the mode from i to i + 1. This newly found correct effective index for the i + 1 element
is then passed as a reference for the i + 2 element and so forth as shown in Figure 3.17. In case the
algorithm does not find a suitable index, for say element i+1, no reference is passed to the next i+2
element. The reference for the i + 2 element is instead given by the ith element.

Chip design and fabrication
With a promising waveguide design in place, the next step is to fabricate the waveguides on a chip,
such that it can be asses whether the simulation results actually resemble the performance of the
real waveguide. The first thing to consider is the design of the chip. The objective is to fabricate a
chip that showcases effective frequency conversion. To maximize the chances of generating a second
harmonic signal, even in the worst case of the fabrication uncertainties considered, a variety of
waveguides are fabricated on the chip. Specifically, two sets of 10 straight waveguides, with minor
variations from the optimal parameters (see Table 3.1), were fitted into the chip. The extra set of
waveguides was implemented as a backup, such that all unique waveguides could be characterized in
case one of the waveguides was damaged. As it was determined in the last chapter, the most critical
parameter for successful frequency conversion is the upper ridge height, Hur. To compensate for
possible deviations in Hur from its ideal value, the result in Figure 3.18 were used, in which perfect
phase matching for Hur ̸= Hideal was achieved by varying the width, W1, of the upper ridge. The
crosses in Figure 3.18 indicate the waveguide widths that were implemented on the chip.
To evaluate the waveguide loss, four different lengths of the waveguide were considered such
that a cutback loss measurement could be carried out. No backup sets were made for these longer
waveguides since that would take up too much of the limited space available on the chip, despite
the long waveguides being designed in a coiled structure to minimize their footprint (see Figure 4.1).
Moreover, the long waveguides are less of a priority since they are not expected to achieve proper
phase matching and hence are likely ineligible for frequency conversion. This is due to the bends in
which the wave propagation is distorted by e.g. change of the crystal direction. As mentioned, low
linear loss in a LNOI ridge-waveguide has already been reported [13]; it is whether the wave design
is eligible for frequency conversion that is interesting.

One of the great challenges in the field of integrated photonics is to maximize the amount of light
that is coupled from a source, typically a fiber, and into the chip. There are in general two ways that
light can be coupled into a chip: directly from the lateral sides of the chip and into the waveguide or,
secondly, vertical coupling in which the top surface of the chip is illuminated. The former requires
that the light incident from vertical is modified, such that the wave propagation vector is refracted
to the propagation direction of the waveguide. This is typically achieved by employing diffraction
grating couplers. Grating couplers are very beneficial since they allow one to access the chip at any
point and do not require any post-fabrication steps as in the case of edge-couplers, where the facets
need to be cleaved and polished. Though grating couplers are highly polarization and wavelength
sensitive, and therefore require careful design and optimization in order for them to function well [42].
Hence, for the sake of simplicity edge couplers were chosen as the modality for coupling light into
the chip. The in-coupling was designed for a 5µm single-moded lensed fiber. To maximize the modal
overlap between the Gaussian mode from the fiber and the fundamental TE mode in the waveguide,
the waveguide widths were tapered out to 5µm. While the width of the waveguides can be tapered
out to match the focused beam from the lensed fiber, the height of waveguides is limited by their
maximal height at 600 nm. An alternative way for obtaining good edge coupling that was considered
is by the opposite tapering process, where the waveguide widths are tapered down, resulting in the
mode becoming less confined and hence increasing the effective area of the modes [42].

Fabrication procedure
The following is a brief and by no means a comprehensive description of the fabrication process that
has been developed and carried out by F. Labbé in the DTU Nanolab cleanroom facilities. The main
fabrication steps are illustrated in Figure 4.4.
The 6 mm by 15 mm chip was initially cleaved manually from the 4” LNOI wafer followed by a
RCA1 cleaning such that following used tools are contaminated by the sample. To provide strong
adhesion between the resist and LN, a ∼20 nm layer of SiN was deposited on top of the LN layer.
For additional bonding, HMDS priming was carried out before a ∼600 nm positive CSAR-18 resist
was spin-coated on top.
Based on the waveguide structures contained in the GDS file shown in
Figure 4.1, the sample was subject to E-beam exposure with a 350 µC cm−2 dosage. The sample was
then manually developed in AR600/546 and subsequently rinsed in IPA. Using the ASE tool, the
chip was then physically etched by ion bombardment using a ICP source. The CSAR resist was then
striped followed by another RCA1 cleaning to remove leftover particles from the physical etching.
The SiN adhesion layer was then removed by a BHF wet etch.
The entire process just described, apart from the cleaving and initial RCA1 cleaning, was then
carried out once more to manufacture the lower ridge. After this second round of fabrication, the
lateral sides of the chip are to be cleaved at the lines marked by the green crosses (see Figure 4.2)
using a laser micromachining tool (this fabrication step is not included in Figure 4.4). This is however
not done yet, at the time of writing this thesis. Lastly, the lateral faces of the chip are to be polished
to reduce losses in in-coupling.

So far, the attention has been directed towards optimizing a waveguide design for the SHG process in
which two pump photons at λp = 1550 nm interact in the LN crystal such to generate a signal at the
double frequency ωs = 2ωp, or equivalently λs = 775 nm. A classical on-chip source at 775 nm in the
lower end of the near-infrared (NIR) spectrum has a wide range of applications within bio-sensing such
as NIR-Spectroscopy (NIRS) [43]. That said, the underlying motive for designing a device with great
conditions for SHG is that this also implies great conditions for the corresponding reversed quantum
mechanical conversion process [18], namely spontaneous parametric down-conversion (SPDC). That
is, the design meets the conditions for the degenerate case of SPDC where two entangled photons
at λs = λi = 1550 nm can be generated from pumping at λp = 775 nm as illustrated in Figure 2.1.
As motivated in Section 2.1, this is highly interesting since the design in theory is capable of gen-
erating entangled photons in the telecom C-band, which has a vast number of applications in the
revolutionizing quantum industry.
Now, there are a number of properties that are desired from a single photon emitter (SPE). Aside
from having a high brightness or emission rate, i.e. a short time period between successive single
photons, the photons also need to exhibit high indistinguishability and purity [7].
Distinguisha-
bility refers to the degree that two photons share the same quantum state.
That is, completely
indistinguishable photons have the exact same wavelength, polarization, and temporal and spatial
propagation. Distinguishability is typically determined by the Hong-Ou-Mandel two-photon exper-
iment as schematized in Figure 5.1, where the two photons enter a 50/50 beam splitter after which
there are four different scenarios. The photons can both cross over and exit the other channel than
the one that they entered (a). They can both exit through the same channel (b) and (c), or they can
exit the same channel that they entered (d). For distinguishable photons, labeled by red and blue,
this yields four different states quantum states. However, in the case of indistinguishable photons
the scenario in (a) and (d) will deconstructively interfere. This can hence be seen as dip in the
transmitted power[44].
Hence, for the non-degenerate case of SPDC where λi ̸= λs, the two photons are evidently highly
distinguishable simply by their wavelength. For the photon pair to be indistinguishable, they therefore
need a high spectral purity. To evaluate the purity the joint spectral amplitude will be computed in
Section 5.2. Now, SPDC is inherently a quantum phenomenon, hence the classical theory presented
so far will not suffice.
The initial step in entering the realm of quantum electrodynamics is to
quantize the field electric and magnetic fields and to identify the Hamiltonian. A common practice
in textbooks is to quantize the E and B fields and then use these to derive the Hamiltonian [46]
[1]. Though, as pointed out by M. Hillery [47] and N.Quesada et al [48] this seemingly reasonable
approach leads to inconsistencies with Maxwell’s equations when considering nonlinearities in non-
vacuum. To resolve this, the dual potential will be introduced, and the quantization will be in the D
and B fields instead. To properly quantize the field one needs to use a Lagrangian approach which

Chapter 6
Conclusion and Outlook
Based on the X-cut thin-film-lithium niobate platform (Figure 3.1), a double-ridge waveguide (Fig-
ure 3.2) was designed for second harmonic generation at the 1550 nm telecom-C wavelength. Perfect
Type-0 phase matching was achieved between the fundamental TE waveguide mode at 1550-nm
wavelength and a higher-order TE mode at the second harmonic (Figure 3.6). This allows to access
the largest element of the second-order nonlinear susceptibility tensor (Table 2.1). This was found
based on the thorough theoretical review of nonlinear optics applied to lithium niobate presented
in Chapter2.
From simulations, the generated second harmonic was found to have a full-width-
half-maximum (FWHM) of 0.17 nm (Figure 3.9). After careful validation of the numerical model
(Section 3.3), the maximal conversion efficiency was determined to be 17.9 W−1 at an interaction
length of 6 cm. Considering a more feasible waveguide length of 1 cm, the conversion efficiency was
determined to be 3.5 W−1. The conversion efficiency at 1 cm waveguide are one order of magnitude
smaller than state-of-the-art SHG in GaAs-on-insulator. However, GaAs absorbs at 775 nm, yielding
frequency conversion by SHG/SPDC difficult to be achieved.
The fabrication tolerances of the phase matching condition were then simulated using the EMode
finite-difference-frequency-domain (FDFD) solver. Here, it was found that the ridge height of the
upper ridge, Hur, is the most critical parameter for achieving efficient frequency conversion.
To
compensate for potentially non-ideal upper ridge heights, it was then investigated whether phase
matching could still be achieved by varying the width, W1, of the upper ridge. Though, as discussed
in Section 3.5, it is highly circumstantial to track higher order modes as they propagate through
parameter space. Hence, to ease the process of exploring parameter space for the phase matching
condition, the HOMTracker algorithm was developed in Section 3.6. Applying the HOMTracker
algorithm to the parameter space spanned by Hur and W1, it was then found that perfect phase
matching indeed could be achieved for non-ideal upper ridge heights (Figure 3.18). Based on these
findings, 10 waveguides with varying widths were then selected to be implemented on the chip that
was later to be fabricated by F. Labbé. As of the time of writing this thesis, the final fabrication
steps are yet to be carried out.
The various considerations involved in designing the chip were covered in Chapter 4. Hereunder
are the thoughts behind the mask layout used for E-beam lithography and how light is coupled into
the waveguides. Additionally, the basic steps of the fabrication process that were carried out by F.
Labbé were reviewed. Unfortunately, AFM measurements provided by F.Labbé revealed an unan-
ticipated high etching rate that was 4.4nm min−1 faster than expected from previous fabrications.
This resulted in a significantly larger upper ridge height of 267 nm compared to the ideal height of
200 nm that was aimed for. This is hence far beyond the ±20 nm uncertainty that was considered in
Section 3.5, and thereby also beyond what can be compensated by the 10 waveguides with varying
widths (Figure3.13). Following simulations were carried out to see if design changes could be made
such that phase matching still could be achieved, though without any success.
Lastly, the potential for using the design for spontaneous-parametric-down-conversion to the
telecom-C band was investigated in Chapter 5. Initially, efforts were put into a proper quantization
of fields, accentuating the pitfall of conventional quantization when considering nonlinearities in a

6.1
Outlook
Even though the fabricated chip may not be eligible for the SHG nor SPDC purposes that were
intended, it is still worthwhile to investigate whether the chip can be used for other up- or down-
conversion processes. There is the possibility for the chip to be applicable for the non-degenerate
case sum of frequency generation (SFG) or difference frequency generation (DFG). Furthermore,
as mentioned in Section3.1, the initial reason behind the double ridge waveguide design was the
restriction of a 200 nm etch depth.
However, now that it is known that LN can be etched even
deeper, this design restriction is relaxed, which opens up new design possibilities. Furthermore, the
design process can now be carried out much more efficiently by using the developed HOMTracker
algorithm.
The chip was designed for edge coupling due to its simplicity and the fact that edge couplers, in
general, have better coupling efficiencies and broader wavelength acceptance than grating couplers
[42]. Though, this was considering that the chip would be characterized classically for second har-
monic generation, where there is an inherently good overlap between the fundamental TE waveguide
mode, Figure 3.6(left), and the Gaussian pump mode from the fiber. Though, considering the case of
SPDC, the pump needs to couple to the non-Gaussian higher order mode in Figure 3.6(right). Here
difficulties arise since it will be difficult to only couple to a single specific higher-order mode without
also exciting the lower-ordered modes. To resolve this issue, it makes great sense to optimize a grat-
ing coupler design that modulates the pump light such that the modal overlap between the pump
and higher-order TE mode is maximized while reducing the amount of power from the pump that
couples to the lower-order modes. However, the initial step would be to characterize the performance
of SHG before advancing to the more complex experimental setup associated with detecting single
photons generated from SPDC.
Now, the prospect of using the design for SPDC was just getting started to be evaluated. While
the JSA in Figure 5.3 indicates that photon pairs can be generated at high purity, it is convenient
to have an actual measure of the purity. This can be done by a Schmidt decomposition of JSA [50].
Regrettably, time did not allow for this procedure to be carried out. Now, not only the purity but
also the conversion efficiency and repetition rate of from SPDC process could be highly interesting
to evaluate and compare, not only to other SPDC waveguide designs but also to state-of-the-art of
other single photon emitters such as color centers, quantum dots, and carbon nanotubes [7].
It is the hope, that there will be another fabrication run for the designed frequency converter chip.
This thesis has showcased the potential of SHG and SPDC in non-poled TFLNOI, that in turn reduces
the fabrication complexity. The simulations indicate an efficient converter, with high fabrication
tolerances, that in turn paves the way for a scalable SPDC source, using mature technologies.
