Abstract
This thesis tackles the problem of outdoor rough terrain navigation by incorporating
a 3D LiDAR sensor for providing environmental perception to a mobile robot. The goal
is to allow the robot to identify between traversable and non-traversable terrain and start
exploring the possibility of detection and classification of natural obstacles such as bushes,
grass, trees and more. The work started by mounting the LiDAR, acquiring and processing
the data and concluded with designing an algorithm for detection and classification. The
resulting output map proves the possibility of this design and can be used as a starting
point for developing further sophisticated outdoor systems for navigation.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Robot navigation is one of the main research areas within robotics that focuses on
enabling autonomous systems to perceive their surroundings and make smart decisions
that allow them to move safely and effectively through diverse environments [1]. This is
typically achieved by equipping robots with sensors such as cameras, LiDAR, or other
perception hardware and implementing advanced algorithms that transform raw sensor
readings into structured representations, usually maps[1, 2]. These maps are the founda-
tion for intelligent decision-making regarding where the robot can and cannot go. Some
of the most important uses of robot navigation in the world are autonomous automobiles,
mobile service robots, and delivery systems [2].
The primary requirement of navigation systems is to accurately model the environment
and classify its elements [1, 3]. As the complexity of the environment increases, so too
does the need for sophisticated algorithms capable of handling variability and uncertainty
[2]. For structured environments such as urban roads, warehouses, or factory buildings,
mapping and classification are simpler, as the objects encountered are well defined and
can be recognized using established techniques [1].
In contrast, unstructured environments present far greater challenges. These land-
scapes filled with vegetation, rocks, ditches, and variable ground require robust terrain
traversability analysis [4]. Unlike standardized or man-made objects, natural elements ex-
hibit significant variability in shape, size, and appearance, making them difficult to model
with conventional approaches [4, 5].
The purpose of this thesis is to address this problem by investigating methods for
terrain classification in rough outdoor environments. Specifically, it focuses on selecting
appropriate sensors, collecting and processing data, extracting relevant features, and
applying classification techniques to distinguish between different types of natural terrain.
By doing so, this work provides a foundation for more robust outdoor navigation systems
and opens the path to further applications such as autonomous livestock monitoring, large-
scale environmental surveying and exploration of previously inaccessible landscapes.
1.2
Objectives
The main objectives of this thesis are:
1. Develop a mapping system capable of distinguishing between traversable and non-
traversable terrain in rough outdoor environments.

2. Detect and classify different types of outdoor terrain.
3. Integrate odometry with the mapping and classification system to build a global map
that accurately represents the environment.
In this context, outdoor rough terrain means areas with natural obstacles such as
water, mud, tall grass, stones, trees, bushes, ditches, hills, and similar features that affect
mobility.

This thesis has a few limitations due to technical constraints and the unique require-

1. All processing must be performed locally on the robot without relying on internet
connectivity or external APIs.
2. The navigation system must operate in real time and cannot be really slow in reaction
to environmental changes.

2
Analysis
2.1
Situation
The project takes place in the context of enabling autonomous navigation in rough out-
door terrain. Such terrain presents significant challenges compared to structured indoor
or urban environments. Natural terrain can vary a lot, each of which requires different
handling by a mobile robot. For example, water and trees are generally non-traversable
but tall grass and bushes may be traversable. In order to operate safely, the robot must
therefore be able to recognize both traversable and non-traversable terrain while also
detecting and classifying natural obstacles.
2.2
State of the art
Addressing these challenges requires the development of specialized algorithms that
combine advanced perception with robust decision-making [4, 5, 6]. Some practical sys-
tems have already explored this domain: for instance, Sarcevic et al. propose an online
outdoor terrain classification algorithm for wheeled robots that discriminates between sur-
faces such as grass, sand, and pebbles with high accuracy [6]. Similarly, Guan et al.
present a traversability mapping and navigation system (TNS) for autonomous excavators
that integrates RGB and LiDAR data to operate effectively in highly unstructured outdoor
terrain [7]. Such methods highlight the importance of combining perception, mapping, and
planning in real-world environments.
2.3
Overall project decisions
For this project, a 3D Hokuyo LiDAR was selected as the primary sensor. This model
is widely used in research and robotics applications [8] and was chosen because it was
the most powerful LiDAR available for the work. The robot used in this thesis is CAPRA
Robot by CAPRA Robotics. It was used because it was already utilized in some out-
door navigation problems so it was suited for such tasks. The sensor was mounted on a
CAPRA robot using aluminum extrusions combined with anti-vibration screws to minimize
mechanical noise and ensure stable data acquisition.
It should be noted that the 3D camera could not be used in the project due to its
ineffectiveness in outdoor environments, particularly under direct sunlight, which will be
proven in the next chapter.
For data processing and all navigation algorithms, Python was used in combination
with ROS 2. This choice was made because ROS 2 is a widely adopted standard in

robotics research and allows for modular integration of perception, mapping, and navi-
gation components [9, 10]. Furthermore, Python-based ROS 2 packages can be readily
deployed to an embedded platform such as a Raspberry Pi, making the system easily
transferable from the development laptop to a smaller onboard computer.
The importance of processing the data in a manner that enables both intuitive visual-
ization and effective feature extraction was identified. Initially, the focus was placed on
extracting established features from the dataset. However, during this process, it became
evident that the inclusion of a new feature was necessary to more effectively capture the
relevant characteristics of the terrain.
Subsequently, these features were utilized as the foundation for classification tasks
and for supporting the map-building process.

3
Sensor Setup and Data Collection
The aim of this chapter is to determine which sensors could reliably provide data in natural
environments. To achieve this, a Hokuyo3D LiDAR (YVT-35LX-F0/FK) was mounted on
the robot, while the built-in Intel RealSense 3D camera was also evaluated. Both sensors
were used in combination with the robot’s odometry system, and synchronized datasets
were collected and analyzed. The evaluation of these datasets formed the basis for the
decision on which sensor technology to employ in the subsequent stages of the project.
3.1
Related work
The sensor integration relied on existing ROS and ROS 2 drivers. For the Intel Re-
alSense 3D camera, the official realsense-ros package was used to obtain RGB-D data
streams. For the Hokuyo3D LiDAR, an existing ROS driver available at hokuyo3d was
adapted and transformed to operate within the ROS 2 framework. Odometry data was
accessed through CAPRA’s internal systems, following the documentation provided by
CAPRA Robotics.
3.2
Design
The Hokuyo3D LiDAR was mounted on top of the CAPRA robot. This location was
chosen because it enables the sensor to capture both low and high obstacles, thereby
providing complete coverage of the robot’s surrounding environment. The RealSense
camera, which was already part of the platform, was also included in the evaluation to
assess its potential for outdoor applications.
Data synchronization was ensured by recording all sensor topics with ROS 2 bag files.
This allowed simultaneous collection of LiDAR, camera, and odometry data, which could
then be replayed and inspected consistently.
3.3
Function
The integrated system was designed to collect three main data streams:
• Hokuyo3D LiDAR: Provided dense and consistent 3D point cloud data.
• Intel RealSense 3D camera: Captured RGB-D data, including color and depth
streams.
• Odometry system: Supplied motion and positioning information from the robot.

3.4
Results
Field experiments were conducted in Greve, Denmark. During these experiments,
synchronized datasets were collected and later analyzed. The Hokuyo3D LiDAR and
odometry data were successfully captured and proved to be reliable. The LiDAR gener-
ated complete and consistent point clouds of the outdoor environment, while the odometry
data accurately reflected the robot’s movements.
In contrast, the RealSense 3D camera showed poor performance under outdoor light-
ing conditions. Visualizations of the depth topic /camera/depth/image_rect_raw showed
largely empty or noisy depth maps. However, the RGB camera stream /camera/color/image_raw
produced high-quality images of the environment. These findings confirmed that camera’s
depth sensing is not reliable in natural sunlight due to interference from ambient light.

3.5
Summary
The evaluation demonstrated that while both LiDAR and 3D cameras can be useful
in indoor environments, only the Hokuyo3D LiDAR was suitable for outdoor navigation
tasks on the CAPRA robot. Its ability to consistently generate reliable point clouds made
it the preferred choice for further development. By contrast, the RealSense 3D camera
was significantly affected by sunlight, resulting in incomplete or noisy depth maps, and
was therefore concluded unsuitable for outdoor navigation.
This outcome simplified the sensor configuration for subsequent project stages, ensur-
ing that development could proceed with a reliable sensing foundation. LiDAR, odometry,
and RGB camera data were successfully acquired and ready for analysis.

4
Point Cloud Processing and
Transformation
This chapter presents the development of a point cloud processing and transformation
system that converts raw 3D sensor data into structured representations appropriate for
terrain analysis and navigation. Data from the Hokuyo3D LiDAR is transformed from the
sensor frame to a robot-centric coordinate system and discretized into a grid structure.
The goal is to create a well-structured spatial representation that is appropriate for feature
extraction in the future and easier to visualize.
4.1
Related work
The system builds upon established ROS 2 infrastructure and computational geometry
libraries. Visualization and manipulation are supported by Open3D, which provides effi-
cient operators and rendering tools for point clouds. ROS 2 message handling relies on
the sensor_msgs_py.point_cloud2 module for compatibility with standard robotics data
formats, while numerical computation is implemented with NumPy. Temporal synchro-
nization and callback execution are provided through the rclpy framework, ensuring that
the system can operate in real time.
4.2
Design
The point cloud processing pipeline is divided into three main stages:
1. Acquisition: Hokuyo3D point clouds are received as ROS 2 messages.
2. Coordinate transformation: A transformation is applied to align the sensor’s con-
vention with ROS and to simplify visualization. In practice this ensures that “left”
in the camera image corresponds to “left” in the point cloud visualization, making
interpretation more intuitive.
3. Grid discretization: The transformed points are organized into an XY grid structure,
enabling structured spatial representation and efficient preparation for future terrain
feature extraction.

Function

The data stream is captured using a ROS 2 subscriber with a best-effort QoS policy.

The choice of grid size was a central design decision.
Different resolutions were
tested:
• Large cells (≥1 m): reduced computational load but merged multiple terrain fea-
tures into the same cell, resulting in significant loss of detail.
• Small cells (≤0.1 m): preserved detail but created an excessive number of cells,
increasing memory usage and processing latency.
• Intermediate cells (≈0.25–0.5 m): provided a practical balance, preserving terrain
structure while maintaining real-time responsiveness.
The final grid size was chosen from the intermediate range, ensuring that essential
features of the environment were retained without compromising processing performance.

Feature Extraction and Analysis
5.1
Feature Design
The feature extraction process was determined based on commonly recognized fea-
tures relevant to LiDAR point cloud analysis. The main emphasis was on metrics based
on height, which are essential indicators of the geometry of the terrain. The minimum,
maximum, and average height values were calculated for every grid cell. The dataset’s
descriptive ability was then improved by analyzing additional features that are known to be
helpful in terrain characterization, including height range, standard deviation, point count,
point density, roughness, and slope. Additionally, a new feature was added to enhance
obstacle detection and differentiation. A set of value ranges was established and used to
give the grid representation colors in order to aid in visualization and interpretation. This
visualization makes it possible to understand the pointcloud intuitively.

5.1.2
Conclusion — Average, Minimal, and Maximum Height Features
These features show a promising ability to discriminate between various terrain types,
including tall trees, medium vegetation, and flat ground. The consistency and variation
among these features, rather than the absolute height values, are what make them useful.
A surface is considered flat and traversable when the minimum, maximum, and average
height values inside a cell are all continuously low. On the other hand, the presence of
elevated structures, like tall vegetation or obstacles, is indicated by a significant difference
between the maximum and minimum height values. The height range feature, which was
created to specifically record and measure such vertical variations, was inspired by this
observation.
5.1.3

The height range is defined as
hrange = hmax −hmin,
where hmax represents the highest point within a cell and hmin the lowest. This feature
captures the vertical variation of points within each grid cell showing a clear distinction
between flat terrain (low height range) and elevated structures or obstacles (higher range
values).

5.3
New Feature: Occlusion
5.3.1
Motivation
Height-, slope-, and roughness-based features characterize terrain geometry but strug-
gle to reliably distinguish solid obstacles from sparse vegetation. Occlusion patterns pro-
32
Robot navigation
==PAGE:39==
vide some reasoning: solid surfaces tend to terminate many adjacent rays at nearly the
same range, while sparse objects show inconsistent or no terminations.
5.3.2
Operational Definition
Scene is scanned with many narrow beams (“rays”). Along each ray, two distances
are calculated:
• the distance to the last grid cell that still has points (the end of the detected surface),
and
• the distance to the first empty grid cell just after that (where no points are seen).
If these two distances are nearly the same (within about 1 m), the ray is occlusion-consistent.
When several neighboring rays are both short (end within 3 m of the robot) and occlusion-
consistent, they usually outline a solid boundary (e.g., tree trunk, pole, wall, very dense
bush). Sometimes the sensor self occludes for points at very small distances so a start off-
set is needed. If there are more then 3 adjacent occlusion-consistent rays that is detected
as a solid object.

6
Feature-Based Detection and
Recognition
This chapter analyzes feature behavior across a range of natural terrain types and eval-
uates how well those features detect and distinguish terrain. The natural terrain classes
considered are: flat ground, tall grass, bushes, trees and other solid objects, water,
ditches, and hills. The assumption is that each class exhibits distinctive, recognizable
combinations of features that can be used for classification.
6.1
Flat Ground
Flat ground is treated here as terrain with zero to small height (e.g., short grass or
a mud path). Specifically, flat ground should exhibit small to no height range, no large
slopes, and small std. dev. due to uniform, small readings. Cells that meet these con-
ditions are labeled as flat ground. Occlusion should not trigger on flat ground because
nothing in flat ground occludes the point cloud.
To test this, a program colors all cells that have flat-ground-like features (small height
range, small slopes, small std. dev.). Only cells with more than three points are consid-
ered, because classification is more reliable with sufficient data, a single reading in a cell
is not sufficient for high confidence. The specific filter is:
FILTER CRITERIA (Flat Ground): Height Range < 0.2 m AND Slope < 20.0◦
AND Std. Dev. < 0.06 m AND Point Count > 3.

This section groups solid objects and trees together because they share the defining

whether the object is tall or short, but the emphasis is on solidity rather than appearance.

bushes) are simply solid objects. A very dense bush can occasionally behave like a solid
and be labeled as such. The key distinction is that solids are untraversable, while flat
ground and tall grass/bushes are traversable.
For this classification the primary feature used is occlusion, with max height to hint at
size. Three visualizations were implemented to determine whether tall solid objects could

6.4
Hills and Ditches
Hills and ditches are common in natural terrain. They are often traversable but typically
not desired. The system therefore aims to detect and avoid them.
6.4.1
Hills
In LiDAR, hills can behave similarly to solid objects because they occlude, but unlike
trees they rise gradually in small steps. A program was created to detect such small,
gradual changes, results were good for larger hills.

Ditches are characterized by points with heights lower than 0 (relative to the local

6.5
Water
From a LiDAR perspective, water often does not reflect rays, so returns are absent.
However, the lack of data can be informative. If a group of adjacent cells with no points is
observed that is not due to occlusion, water is hypothesized. Only occlusion should cause
gaps. Therefore, if a gap is not explained by occlusion, it is marked as possible water.
Locally this is imperfect, but on a global map a large, approximately circular gap bounded
by dense readings (with not very tall average height around the edge) can indicate water.

6.6
Conclusion
This chapter examined what can and cannot be successfully recognized and classified
from LiDAR features alone:
• Flat ground can be reliably located using small height range, low slope, low std.
dev., and adequate point count (Figs. 6.1–6.3).
• Medium vegetation (tall grass/bushes) can be identified using medium height
range, medium/high slopes and roughness, and medium std. dev., provided occlu-
sion is absent (Figs. 6.4–6.6).
• Solid objects and trees are effectively marked using occlusion.
The specific
type (tree, cow, stone, dense bush) is not reliably distinguishable, but all are un-
traversable and should be avoided (Figs. 6.7–6.9).
• Hills and ditches can be detected as positive or negatively elevatated structures on
the grid (Figs. 6.10, 6.11), small-scale would benefit from more precise algorithm.
• Water remains difficult with LiDAR alone, lack of returns without occlusion suggests
water but is ambiguous (Fig. 6.12). Camera fusion is recommended.
Overall, this demonstrates a practical system: height-based features provide strong
traversability cues, occlusion provides solidity cues, simple grouping shows hills/ditches.
This is sufficient to guide a navigation stack and motivates the persistent mapping in the
next chapter.

7.1
Persistent Map Concept
A persistent map is defined as a 2D grid (robot-centric world frame) where each cell
stores the latest available classification for that location (e.g., flat ground, bushes/tall
grass, solid/trees, hill, ditch, water), along with simple metadata (timestamp of last up-
date and point count). In the implementation, the map is overwrite-on-latest: when a new
local classification arrives for a cell, it replaces the previous one. One thing also different
from the prevous chapter is that this algorithm colors ditches with their depth approxima-
tion by calculating the average height of the group. Also, both groups for hills and ditches
are filtered to only groups bigger than 20 points to eliminate noise.

The persistent map concept performs well enough for real-time operation while con-

• Occlusion result. It reliably lights up many dense bushes and also hints at slopes

Ditches and hills confuse the system a lot.

• Gaps in the map. There are a few blank spots caused by short glitches in the robot’s
position reading. Simple checks to ignore sudden jumps would reduce these.
• Precision limits. The occlusion view is approximate: it shows where an object

paths are stable, hill/ditch regions are highlighted, and solids (trees/poles) are identified
with some noise.

Perspective
8.1
Introduction
The previous chapter evaluated the mapping capabilities of the proposed algorithms.
Building on that analysis, this chapter assesses the results against the objectives set out
in Chapter 1 and outlines promising directions for future work.
8.2
Scenario
The test scenario was introduced in the previous chapter. It is a representative natural-
terrain environment with numerous natural obstacles. The data was collected according
to the procedure introduced in Chapter 3.
8.3
Test result analysis
• Objective 1 — Distinguish traversable vs. non-traversable terrain Largely achieved.
The occlusion feature provides a clear “do not traverse” signal by marking structures
that block the beams. The main issue is that dense bushes/plants are sometimes
marked as solid, whether such vegetation is traversable depends on the robot rather
than the map. Further work is required, but the results indicate feasibility with LiDAR.
• Objective 2 — Detect and classify outdoor terrain types Most challenging and
least complete. LiDAR performed well for flat ground, ditches, and hills. However, it
did not reliably separate the most critical categories (bushes, plants, tall grass, trees,
stones). The system can detect that “something is present,” but cannot consistently
assign the correct class.
• Objective 3 — Use odometry to build a global map Achieved. Combining odome-
try with local mapping produced useful global maps that indicate the locations of flat
ground, ditches, and hills, as well as regions that should be avoided (from occlusion).
More detailed global map building was beyond the present scope.
8.4
Future works
The approach proved efficient and promising, generating a useful global map that can
be further improved. The thesis relied on occlusion analysis, which occasionally labels
dense bushes as solid. Improvements could include additional features for dense vege-
tation or learning-based methods (e.g., neural networks) to exclude such cases from the
solid class.

A more computationally demanding persistent map that retains full measurement his-
tories per cell would also increase robustness. With all readings preserved, per-cell deci-
sion making could leverage richer statistics or learned models to generate more precise
classes.
Additional sensors or vision based algorithms could address natural objects that Li-
DAR alone struggles with, such as water.. Handling sudden bumps and uneven terrain
that bias local height estimates is also important, this could be mitigated via global slope
estimation and compensation. Finally, hill/ditch reasoning could benefit from more ad-
vanced slope calculation algorithms that could better separate large hills/ditches from
gentle up-/down-slopes.

9
Conclusion
This thesis provided a possibility for natural rough unstructured terrain mapping and per-
ception with processing procedures based on LiDAR. A feasible approach was introduced
for the detection and classification of different types of natural terrain and obstacles.
The approach started with the acquisition of data and then designing an effective point
cloud processing framework with grid segmentation. The choice of optimal grid size bal-
anced computational speed with spatial detail. A collection of height, statistical and geo-
metric features were examined in order to determine their usefulness, and a new feature
was added for enhanced detection of solid objects.
These features were subsequently used for the classification of important details of
the terrain like hills, ditches, bushes, and solid or dense obstacles. In these methods, a
persistence mapping technique was used for integrating LiDAR scans in an organized,
global representation of the terrain.
Overall, the final LiDAR processing pipeline produces environmental global maps from
raw sensor input. It uses extracted features for traversability evaluation, and makes use
of the newly created occlusion algorithm for detection of hard obstacles.
The outcomes of this work illustrate a real-time and lightweight rough-terrain mapping
that can detect non-traversable and traversable regions from LiDAR and grid-based com-
putations. The study at the same time reveals LiDAR’s limitations in natural environments
such as ambiguity of long grass and bushes, ambiguity between solid objects and dense
vegetation and limited detectability of water surfaces. It also highlights the challenges
of using only a LiDAR to detect and classify diverse natural terrains while not using any
machine learning algorithms.

A.7
Known Limitations and Notes
• Dense vegetation: occlusion often highlights dense bushes, sometimes making
them look like solid objects.
• Terrain edges: approaching a hill or ditch can produce apparent occlusion ahead
of the robot; this is expected and useful for early warnings.
• Near-field masking: if ∆is too small, near-field rays can be unreliable; increasing
∆reduces self-effects but misses very close objects.
• Precision of solids: occlusion markers are approximate.
In tight spaces they
should not be trusted for fine clearance.
• Odometry gaps: short errors create holes or small misalignments in global layers.
• Rendering artifact: tree detections appear slightly larger because they are drawn
as radius-0.5 circles for visibility.
A.8
Reproducibility Hints
