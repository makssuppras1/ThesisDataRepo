Abstract
This thesis investigates if a diffuser augmented wind turbine (DAWT) can outperform a
regular horizontal axis wind turbine (HAWT) looking at exit area CP . This is done by inves­
tigating three different diffuser designs using the κ−ω SST model in an 2D axisymmetrical
CFD simulation. Each of the diffusers undergo a parametric study, changing the shape
of the diffuser, with exit area CP being the measurement of success. Furthermore it is
investigated if a Donut shaped structure, present in all three models, should be included
as a part of the diffuser.
Results from the simulations demonstrate that a rise in exit area CP indeed can be achieved
for the DAWT, compared to a regular HAWT. Furthermore it’s shown that some of the pa­
rameters studied are of great importance to CP and some are insignificant.
A diffuser design capable of breaking Betz’ limit in simulations is presented. However, it
does not account for losses. The real life losses such a diffuser would experience are
discussed. Suggestions for what parameters should be investigated in order to further
maximise exit area CP are made.

Introduction
At the International Small Wind Turbine Competition (ISWTC) of 2020, the DTU bid reached
an electrical power coefficient (CPelc) of 0.423 corresponding to an aerodynamic power
coefficient (CP,aero) of 0.469. This is reaching the limits of achievable CP for a 3 bladed
system with a lift to drag ratio of 75 (a SD7032­ airfoil) as shown by Jamieson [1].
Figure 1.1: CP as a function of lambda for a three bladed system with different
lift to drag ratios.
To have a bid with a higher CP , a new system must be adopted. One obvious sugges­
tion is to increase the lift­to­drag ratio, thus increasing the possible power ceiling. This
would, however, only slightly increase the power potential, as seen above, and introduce
structural issues as discussed in the build report of the HAWT bid 2021 [2]. Another less
obvious suggestion could be the shrouded (aka. diffuser augmented) wind turbine„ as this
system, in theory, can break the Betz’ limit, even when considering the exit area of the
turbine system. This has been shown by Hansen [3] and Hjort [4] using CFD simulations.
This has not always been a plausible suggestion. In the first theoretical assessment of a
shrouded wind turbine, done by Lilley and Rainbird in 1956 [5], it concluded that no perfor­
mance increase was possible when considering the exit area of the shroud. The authors
did, however, emphasize the need for experimental testing. Shrouded wind turbine exper­
iments were done in 1963 by Kogan and Siginer from the Ben­Gurion University [6] and
later in 1975 and 1978 by Oman, Gilbert and Foremann from the Grummann Aerospace
Dept. of the United States ([7], [8]) and in 1976 and 1980 by Ozer Igra of the Ben­Gurion
University. These experiments showed a significant power increase of up to 4.25 times
the power of a bare bladed turbine. Especially Igra was enthusiastic, concluding that a
shrouded wind turbine was superior to any regular horizontal axis wind turbine. This, he

emphashized, was in part due to the significant reduction in the losses from the tip and the
low pressure at the exit plane of the shroud and corresponding high recovery rate, due to
the shrouded nature of the Diffuser Augmented Wind Turbine (DAWT). Lillay and Rainbird
were right; experiments were indeed needed. Having said this, the metric for success of
the DAWT in the ISWTC is that it can produce more power than a HAWT at similar wind
conditions and occupying the same area i.e. having the same exit area. The ISWTC only
allows for a total turbine area, with any extensions, not to overcome two square meters
[9]. This means that any shrouded system presented in this thesis must be compared to
a regular HAWT of two square metres.
The commercial value of a highly effective wind turbine was a hot topic at the the Wind
Energy Innovative Systems Conference of 1979 (the United States). Even though Igra,
Oman, Gilbert, Foremann, Kogan and Siginer made great strides in the theory behind
shrouded wind turbines, one of the conclusions from the conference proceedings was
that ”further DAWT model verification is necessary, including a field test phase” [10]. To
this day, no complete theory has been developed; however, the conference agreed that a
possibility of a sub 2 cent per kWh turbine was feasible. This was competitive in the late
70’s and early 80’s.
With the prospect of cheap electricity, the shrouded wind turbine Vortec 7 was developed.
The 7.3 m diameter turbine promised a CP of above 2 with reference to the Grummann
publication. This was the first commercial attempt at developing a shrouded turbine. How­
ever, in 1999 Flay, Phillips and Nash [11], tasked with monitoring the demonstrator Vortec
7 unit, could not observe the promised power predictions. As the Grummann theories
were based on uniform velocity across the blade, and not lower speeds close to the hub
and higher speeds surrounding the shroud, Flay found the model flawed and a CP of 2
not achievable for the Vortex 7. He and his coauthors did, however, stress that the air
through the blade plane is increased as a consequence of the shroud, and that, in order
to fully capitalise on this effect, CFD modelling was needed on the blades and diffuser.

The Theory Behind a Shrouded Wind

The method of designing a shrouded wind turbine differs from the classic blade element
method (BEM). This is due to the shrouds influence on the flow around the turbine system.
However as in BEM, a 1D momentum theory can be set up:
2.1
1­D Momentum Theory
Looking at a flow which is steady and inviscid, the rotor can be modelled as an actuator
disc. This entails the disc to induce a pressure jump in the plane section, creating a high
pressure in front of the plane and a low pressure behind it.
By drawing a control volume around the tubular flow, and applying the equations for con­
servation of mass and energy in 1D, the theoretical maximum limit for power withdrawal
can be calculated. The control volume is depicted in colour in the below illustrations.

actuator disk is referred to as the pressure­side, while the opposite side is re­

2.2
The Impact of Disc Thrust on the Diffuser Angle of Attack
Not only does the thrust of the diffuser play a critical role, the thrust of the actuator disc is
vital. This is because the diffuser will push the air, changing the direction of the air.
Figure 2.3: A cut through view of a turbine with an actuator disc deputising (deputiz­
ing) a rotor.
If the diffuser thrust is increased, the intake area is decreased, and the exit area increased.
The streamlines will, as a result, change direction at the disc. The diffuser, located at the
disc, will experience a change in angle of attack as a result. How the diffuser is loaded
will, therefore, directly impact the diffusers ability to induce increase flow velocity though
the turbine plane.
Figure 2.4: A velocity streamline hitting the stagnation point on a diffuser and

The changed flow angle means that the diffuser thrust direction also changes in accor­

the flow though the turbine plane. If the thrust from the actuator disc is zero, i.e. no disc,
the diffuser will have a thrust mainly in the axial direction. However if CT > 0 the flow
will be pushed and the angle of attack, seen by the diffuser, will change. The importance
of lift is eminent. From the 1­D Momentum Theory (section 2.1) only CT,D would seem
important. However, here it’s seen that lift does contribute to the flow through the rotor
hence making the AoA and rotor thrust a coupled issue. A simple diffuser that only exudes
thrust will therefore be far from optimal. This phenomenon is further investigated in the
results chapter.

3
CFD Verification & Validation
Unlike when examining HAWTs, there is currently no analytical solution for DAWTs, such
as 1D momentum theory or BEM. Modified 1D momentum theory can be applied, however
the thrust coefficient of the diffuser must still be assessed through CFD, and therefore it
still requires numerical methods. However, in order to obtain trustworthy results for this
project, a verification and validation of the models must be conducted. The verification
will deal with comparing CFD results to that of other methods, e.g. 1D momentum theory
or the results of other studies and experiments. The validation will deal with ensuring that
convergent results have been obtained, so that inaccurate results are not introduced, due
to improper meshing and physical models. These two steps will both be applied for both
the Actuator Disk and a diffuser­type geometry, individually.
3.1
Numerical Simulations
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) will be used in this thesis to compute the flow field
around diffusers and associated field functions. This is done by solving the Reynolds­
averaged Navier­Stokes (RANS) equations for a steady flow in the CFD program Siemens
Star­CCM + v 2021.1. As is the industry standard, Star­CCM discretises the governing
equations into a set of linear algebraic equations using a finite volume method, see ”Fun­
damental Equations” in the STAR­CCM+ manual. As this method is widely acknowledged
and well described in the manual it will not be further investigated in this work. This thesis
will use a κ −ω SST turbulence model. This is in contrast to Hjort ([20], [4]) who uses the
standard κ −ϵ model.
The flow has a velocity of zero at the surface however the flow does have a velocity off­
surface. This means that there is a transition between the two velocities as seen below.
Figure 3.1: Flow over a surface and its corresponding mesh [22].
To mimic this phenomenon, the mesh on the surface is finer than the mesh further away,
as it is here the velocity differences are largest, see figure 3.32. The mesh must be fine

approach thus generating a piecewise­linear interpretation of the flow. However the κ −ϵ
model uses a non­linear approach, a wall damping function, see figure 3.2.

points. However this method results in unreliable damping functions for untuned flow
velocities. The κ −ω model is a response to this, as it is less sensitive, has better near

3.4
The Ohya Approach
The Ohya diffuser design is based on brims rather than high lift. These brims are placed
at the trailing edge of the diffuser and serves as a barrier that creates a low pressure
region that in turn draws in more wind though the turbine plane.
Figure 3.32: A cross­sectional view of the Ohya DAWT[13]
This low pressure region is by default turbulent thus three dimensional. This is therefore
hard to mimic with a axis­symmetric 2D simulation, especially considering the size of the
brims and the resulting size of the turbulent region. In Hjorts paper [20] he finds that the
Ohya diffuser presented in 2011 [29], underperforms in terms of CP in the simulation by
16.8 % compared to wind tunnel tests. Hjort states that this is because the turbulence
enhances mixing by causing wake­meandering, which is a 3D problem. The Ohya effect

is therefore hard to find in any 2D simulation and can very well be underestimated.

Another method was attempted by Hjort, who focused mainly on creating high lift forces

Hub Scaling
When changing the geometry of the diffuser the hub stays unchanged. This is done as
they operate as two different body groups thus two different parts. This means that the
hub is disproportionally large compared to the diffuser.
As shown by Johansen et al. [36] the shape of the hub does very little to improve CP . They
even found that removing the hub only improved CP by + 0.4 % (Actuator disc simulation)
and + 0.0% (Full 3D simulation) at 8 m/s. The unproportionality of the hub is therefore
not considered an issue when comparing results from the axissymetrical simulations this
theses presents.

7.1
Verification & Validation of the CFD Models
7.1.1
Domain size and Mesh
In the validation section (section 3.3) the domain size and the mesh conditions are shown
not to influence the simulation. Perhaps the domain size could even be smaller without
it hurting the simulation, however with simulation times of less than 10 minutes, this is
hardly necessary.
7.1.2
Annular Wing
Pressure Crossing at the Leading Edge
As presented in section 3.3.6 the simulated pressure at the leading edge does cross itself.
This in contrast to the Hill and Lewis experiments ([34] [33]). To accommodate this the
model used in the simulation would have to be laminar at crossing point. However as
conferred in section 3.3.6 this does not interfere greatly with the simulation as it only
effects 4% of the entire surface of the annular wing simulation.
Higher Underside Pressure
Clearly a mesh on the underside of the diffuser resolved some of the issue by reducing
the overshoot from 10­11 % to 6­7%. However further refinement of the underside mesh
did not further resolve this divergence. An reason for this could be that, the lack of a
laminar flow model, at the leading edge, does impact the suction side, thus resulting in
the overshoot. This would further imply that the pressure prediction of the simulations are
higher than reality thus a lower suction effect. However, it was not found that any other
simulation’s pressure coefficient on the surface did not cross. The issue with the annular
is therefore believed not to have translated into other simulations. A pressure and velocity
scalar plot can be found in appendix A.
7.2
Expected Real Life Losses
The 1D momentum model is an idealised model that does not account for some essential
losses. The most commonly acknowledged losses in relation to 1D momentum are:
• Wake Rotation
• Tip
• Viscous
The effect of these are described for a regular HAWT in Loenbaek 2020 [37] and states
that the losses are 1.7%, 6.8% and 17.0% respectively for a turbine with λ = 7 and
Cl/Cd = 40. This means that an ideal actuator disc would produce a CP of 0.59 (16/27),
but a rotor of similar dimensions, with the above losses implemented, would only find a CP
of 0.43, a 27% difference. These differences are bound to TSR with an higher influence
of wake rotation and tip losses at a low TSR. In the following the losses, and it’s effect on
the DAWT will be discussed.
7.2.1
Wake Rotation
The wake rotation has been shown to be negligible by de Vries (1979) [38] and by E.
Branlard and M. Gaunaa (2015) [39]. They point out that the power loss due to wake
74
Design and Test of a Diffuser Augmented Wind Turbine
==PAGE:85==
rotation, originating from BEM, is countered by the decrease in the pressure in the wake
at the root. This decrease causes an increase in axial velocities near the root.
All three authors states that the wake rotation losses can and should be questioned. The
Branlard and Gaunaa paper even states that the wake rotations are an increase of thrust
and not a change of CP . With these findings the wake rotations can be considered less
important, even for low TSR.
7.2.2
Tip Losses
It’s assumed that the actuator disc has a infinite amount of blades. This is manifested
in the uniform change in momentum, which is equal for all particles entering the rotor
plane as they all meet a blade passing though, see section 2.1. However in reality, with a
finite blade system, the flow passing though the rotor plane will experience a larger local
axial induction close to the blades than between them. This local axial induction addition
will lead to a different local induction factor thus the local loads will change leading to an
change in CP . In BEM this change is conformed by the implementation of a tip loss factor.
This component of the tip loss is difficult to discredit for the DAWT, as it in fact will have a
finite element of blades. The reader is guided to Bernlard for further explanation [40].
The tip loss correction does not only account for the implementation of finite blades, but
also the flow that escapes the tip breaking the uniform pressure across the wing. This
means that the lift at the tip found in BEM is also corrected by the tip losses. However
as the flow is restricted by the diffuser, the tip of the rotor only has a 0.5 cm gab to the
diffuser, the flow is thought to be forced across the blade and not around it. This indicates
that the tip losses induces by the flow around the tip is reduced because of the diffuser.
The tip loss will be present at the DAWT, but it would be fair to assume that it would
display a lower loss than for it’s HAWT counterpart. An interesting approach is the one
by Visser and Kanya [14]. They have a rotor with a cut of tip referring to small tip losses
see appendix C.
7.2.3
Viscous Losses
The loss from the blades producing viscous drag is refereed to as the viscous losses,
the fluids resistance to be moved and the resistance to move across the blade foil. As
described by Lønbæk [37], the viscous losses are found to be linearly dependant of the
inverse­glide­ratio, loading CLT and local TSR. An increase in any of these parameter will
lead to an rise in the viscous loss. From the example in section 7.2 a glide ratio of 40 is
low. The glide ratio for the 2021 DTU bid for the ISWTC is about 75, thus a loss of 17.0%
is properly too high.
7.2.4
What are the expected losses on the DAWT?
Tip Losses
The tip losses would probably be reduced due to the close proximity between the diffuser
and the tip. However the loss when going from an infinite number of blade to a finite, would
persist. The conservative guess would be a similar loss to the HAWT case, however a bit
smaller, while the optimistic guess would be a large reduction.
Viscous Losses
As these originate from drag, a larger Cl/Cd ratio would produce a lower viscous loss.
A conservative guess if therefore a loss slightly better than the Cl/Cd = 40 case and an
optimistic one is a significantly better.

7.3
Hub Clearance and Wake
Throughout the thesis three different types of separation was observed.
• Separation forming from the hub and expanding into the wake.
• Separation forming on the pressure side of the diffuser.
• Separation forming on suction side of the diffuser and spreading into the wake.
Hjort [4] notes that a rotor with a uniform loading, a rotor without a clearance between
the roter and hub, does generate centerbody stall. He also testes a rotor with a uniform
loading with inner ramp­down, not a clearance between rotor and hub but a lowering of
thrust, and finds that this preforms better than any of the other instances, because the
hub stall is suppressed. From section 6.5 we see the same tendency. However we find
that the optimal clearance is very slim while Hjort [4] recommends a larger linear ramp up
extending 32 percent of the rotor radius.
Hjort [4] states that good results was obtained from having a low TSR and a ramp up
thrust of the rotor. Low TSR results in low swirl which is more wake stall resistant and
the ramp up creates a low pressure region along the hub which also help suppress wake
stall. However no study of the ramp up region has been conducted. The rotor size inves­
tigation 6.5 is made with a AD, this means that swirl are not included. It’s therefore of the
essence to simulate the shrouded turbine with swirl and with varying ramp up / clearance
configurations, however it’s seen that a uniform rotor loading generates stall and the ramp
up or clearance is indeed needed.

Discussion on the Method
As the design space is enormous it is necessary to have a pre­determined approach to
the task. However, it early input can also affect the perspective on how a solution is best
found. In this process, initially brimmed diffusers were serious contenders, however with

set of parameters would have been tested instead.

8
Conclusion
The results of the 2D axisymmetric CFD simulations performed in this thesis, are by most
accounts good indicators of the outcome if building the best DAWT presented. Various
experiments have been recreated with the purpose of verifying the models used in the
simulations, with generally satisfactory results. However, it is also shown that there are
limitations to the simulation setup. Although it enables a relatively efficient design pro­
cess by having a short set­up time, and fast meshing­ and simulation times, there are
also downsides.
Turbulent effects are not well­resolved in the 2D setup, and using a 1D actuator disk model
instead of a BEM­based disk, or even a fully resolved 3D model, are grounds for certain
limitations to the accuracy of the results. Using the actuator disk also meant limited design
recommendations for the rotor. The experimental results will certainly be lower than the
simulated results, due to the lack of viscous­ and tip losses in the simulations. Swirl was
not accounted for, and thus the importance of the TSR is based on the work of others,
and not taken into account in the simulations. However, the actuator disk was a com­
putationally inexpensive way of getting a rough idea of the performance of the diffusers
when loaded. It also helps in setting general guidelines for the gab between hub and
thrust­loading of the rotor. The thrust is unlikely to be uniform as in the report.
The results show an increase in CP based on diffuser exit area. This is an improvement
when compared to the bare actuator disk with a hub, which has a CP = 0.598. The best
CP obtained is 0.6366 (+ 6.5%) without Donut, and the best CP is 0.6273 (+ 5%) with
Donut. It is speculated however, that the Donut probably will provide structural stability.
The optimization of CP through the parameter study shows, that design decisions do not
vary depending on size of Geometric Converging geometry, as these largely remain the
same. Removing the Donut does however favor a large FBR of 1.3 compared to the
otherwise low optimum FBR of 0.7, and benefits from a smaller gap than with the Donut.
The most important parameters are found to be the gap, AoA, Donut, thrust and hub gab.
These all produce noticeable increases in CP . Some parameters are less important, such
as the FBR, overlap and GC. The maximum CP is in all instances between approx. 0.62
and 0.64, and may indicate that a ”ceiling” has been found for the design constraints of
this thesis. Outside of these parameters, the initial design study of the diffusers show that
separation on the suction side results in severe loss of performance. Performance also
takes notable hits with severe wake stall and minor hits with small­to­moderate pressure­
side separation.
The CT optimum is found to be approx. 0.9. If the thrust becomes too great, severe
pressure­side separation is observed, and a performance decline is seen if it is too small.
Recommendations are to utilize the benefits of the diffuser, however if another parameter
study should be undertaken, the parameters to be investigated should be reconsidered.
The parameters FBR and overlap could be replaced with e.g. thrust and rotor changes.

Future Work
As mentioned in the conclusion a future study should include a parametric study of thrust
and rotor size. This is because they were found to be important to CP , see section 6.4,
6.5, and 7.4.
The future work is divided into sections below.
9.1
Simulation
A fully resolved 3D mesh with a working BEM code could be made in order to design the
rotor blades. TSR should be found from this simulation and with that an estimate of the
losses could be made.
In order to make a design study of the rotor, the BEM­based actuator disk should be
made to work. This way, a simple and relatively effective design study of the rotor can be
conducted. This would also increase accuracy of DAWT performance, as thrust would be
more representative of the actual running conditions.
9.2
Diffuser
A study with different parameters could be conducted to optimize the diffuser, and in­
crease the CP further. Potentially, the structural aspect of the diffuser should be explored,
to determine if the stiffness from a large diffuser and Donut is needed. The overlap could
be further explored to evaluate whether high­overlap situations could avoid pressure­side
stall. As CP seemed to reach similar maxima, it could be investigated further if the con­
straints should be changed. Lastly, the consequence of making e.g. an octogonal diffuser
should be researched, as it could pose a production challenge to create a circular diffuser
with a bend plate.
9.3
Rotor
The TSR and optimal thrust distribution needs to be explored, in order to better evaluate
running conditions, as also mentioned earlier in this section.
