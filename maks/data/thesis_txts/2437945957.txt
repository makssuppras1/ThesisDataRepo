Abstract 
This report presents the process and results obtained in the 
development of a floor-cleaning device designed for people with 
age related impairments. With an ageing population across the 
world rising, due to multiple factors including longer life-spans, 
it is becoming more important than ever to focus on designing 
appropriate products to facilitate this demographic group. With 
the physical degeneration, that start as early 65+ years of age, 
keeping up with and undertaking even the simplest tasks, such 
as household chores, can become problematic, tiring and even 
dangerous making independent living more difficult.
By focussing the attention of this project on one aspect of living 
independently, in this case maintaining a hygienic and clean-
living environment, more specifically keeping the floors of the 
home clean I hope to improve the quality of life of people living 
under these conditions. Cleaning the floor can be dangerous and 
labour intensive as it often involves the person to bend over in 
uncomfortable positions or by using current floor-cleaning products 
that are often not ergonomically designed. 
With the help of primary and secondary research into the problem 
area as well as including multiple key stakeholders throughout 
the process by applying user-centred design mentality, a strong 
basis of knowledge was created on which to build the creative 
process of conceptualising a solution.
The solution is an ergonomically designed vacuum cleaner with a 
detachable robotic base to help clean underneath obstacles such 
as furniture, relieving the user of stress in their back, shoulders, 
and arms. In turn this allows them the ability to maintain a 
clean home as well as keep part of their independence for 
longer, benefitting them both physically as well as mentally.

1. Introduction
1.1 Project goal formulation 
Designing a floor cleaning product or accessory that makes cleaning 
comfortable, safe and, effective, enabling elderly people with age-
related impairments to maintain their independence for longer.
Design of a floor cleaning device for people with age-related 
impairments.
In today’s world, people are living longer and longer, and their 
ability to keep up with and undertake even the simplest tasks can 
become problematic by the aging and physical degeneration that 
can come with aging. 
On the other hand, people also want to live in their own environment 
and keep their independence and not rely on others to take care of 
every chore that has to be done. The independence of elderly people 
contains both a personal factor as well as a social economic 
factor, as the cost of providing social care is set to rise as the 
senior population increases.
One of the essential ways people will be able to remain independent 
for longer is by having the right tools and equipment to effectively 
take care of themselves and their environment. Keeping homes 
clean and hygienic is one aspect that can be a challenging 
and demanding task for elderly people with limited physical 
capabilities. Tasks such as keeping the kitchen clean, disposing 
trash, cleaning the floors and other surfaces can become tiresome, 
stressful, and even dangerous. Cleaning the floor can specifically 
be dangerous and labour intensive as it often involves the person 
having to either reach down to the ground or use traditional floor 
cleaning appliances which are often heavy and awkward, while 
the newer automated products are often very expensive and not as 
thorough (only removing easy-to-clean dirt).
1.3 Focus areas
The general stages that I will go through throughout this project 
include need interpretation, technology exploration and 
context exploration, research and analysis of the use 
situation, ideation, concept synthesis and detailing, and 
finally verification and assessment of the final design. 
This process will largely be based on the Encapsulation Design 
Method (Andreasen, Hansen & Cash, 2015, p. 4). Furthermore, 
using the principles of user-centred design (U.S. Department of 
Health & Human Services, n.d.; University of Cambridge, n.d.), I 
will develop a product that is designed to aid those with age-related 
impairments whilst having an appealing feel and appearance to a 
broad spectrum of users. 
Primary and secondary research will be conducted in order 
to understand the needs of the elderly users that are affected 
by disabilities, which will be carried out by observing, engaging 
and listening to their physical and emotional needs by means of 
interviews and questionnaires. Furthermore, thorough market 
analysis will be done in order to map out the existing products on 
the market and understand the ways in which I can improve them 
with my design. 
Lastly, understanding the different contexts and applications 
the floor cleaning product can be used in will be scoped out and 
investigated in order to get a deeper understanding of the use 
scenarios and use environment, which could have potential 
impact on the design of the final concept. 
These steps will give me the opportunity to develop and assess 
a multitude of product concepts that will address the defined 
problem.

2. Staging, approach, and strategy
2.1 Structure
The main structure that will be followed throughout the project is 
largely based on the Encapsulation Design Model (Andreasen et 
al., 2015, p. 4), in terms of following and carrying out the first three 
phases of the model: Exploration phase, Concept Synthesis 
phase and Product Synthesis phase (see figure 1). This can be 
translated with the corresponding results of each phase: Task, 
Concept, and Design. 
The Exploration phase acts as the foundation on which the 
rest of the project is based on, by leading to the initiation and 
argumentation of why and how a specific need or problem is 
addressed. Its main purpose is to supply information, insights, 
and knowledge throughout the project (Andreasen et al., 2015, p. 
115-116). 
The Concept Synthesis phase focusses on using the knowledge 
gained during the Exploration phase to ideate and create 
concepts that address the problem area. By bringing together 
creativity and methodology in a systematic way, the concepts are 
developed in such a way that meets the demands of the project 
by understanding the balance between the synthesis methodology, 
exploration, and creative behaviour (Andreasen et al., 2015, p. 
141-142). 
Finally, the Product Synthesis phase allows to define how the 
product design will be materialised by determining the composition 
and parts of the developed concept design. To achieve this, I used 
Andreasen’s Domain Theory (as cited in Andreasen et al., 2015, 
p. 198) as a foundation to establish the different domains, with a
focus on the: activity and organ (Andreasen et al., 2015, 193-194). 
These phases can be seen mapped out in Figure 2, which is a close 
representation of the ‘Double Diamond’ design process.
2.2 Tools and methods 
Apart from the general structure and approach used to guide 
this project, several tools and methods were used throughout the 
different phases mentioned in the previous section. These tools 
and methods regard both explorative, creative and evaluative/
analytical tools and methods. Some of the explorative 
methods include user observations, interviews, questionnaires, 
trend analysis, mind mapping, fieldwork and market analysis. 
The creative 
methods used for ideation and concept 
development as well as to aid the creative process include tools 
such as brainstorming, morphological charts, and how-to’s. Lastly, 
analytical and evaluative tools that were used include product 
concept evaluation, Harris Profile, C-box method, vALUe method, 
and Weighted Objectives method. The main sources used for the 
specific tools and methods used throughout the project are from the 
Delft Design Guide (van Boeijen et al., 2014), Engineering Design 
Methods (Cross, 2015), and Product Design and Development 
(Andreasen et al., 2015).

2.3 User-centred design 
An important design process that I aim to apply throughout 
my master thesis project as much as possible is User centred 
design (UCD). UCD is a design process that focusses on the user’s 
needs and requirements and  promotes the designer to keep these 
central throughout the development of a product concept. It aims 
is to develop and produce highly usable systems or products by 
understanding the user needs through the use of both investigative 
methods such as surveys and interviews and generative methods 
and tools such as brainstorming sessions (Interaction Design 
Foundation, n.d.). UCD heavily involves users in all the design 
phases throughout the design process.
In relation to the Encapsulation Design Model, the general phases 
I went through in order to have a UCD approach are the following 
(see figure 3):
o	 Specify the context of use: identifying the individuals 
that will be using the product and specifically for what task 
and in what context the product will be used.
o	 Formulate requirements: identifying all the requirements 
and user goals the target group has in order to develop the 
product to meet these.
o	 Develop concept solutions: this includes early ideation 
of a broad range of ideas, followed by more refined concept 
designs.
o	 Evaluate: evaluation of the final concepts by including the 
users in determining the best and most suitable solutions.

2.4 Team work 
Problem solving is a daunting task and product design is a very 
difficult process, one that is not easily achieved with the mind of a 
single person. Product design can almost be compared to be a team 
sport, making team-work an efficient problem-solving tool 
that could lead to a successful concept of a product (Czajka, 2016). 
Even though I am the sole writer of this thesis project, I also 
know and value the importance of it not becoming a solitary 
endeavour and therefore I decided it would be beneficial to have 
weekly meetings with other students who are also working on 
their personal master thesis project. By having weekly meetings 
to discuss each of our own projects and collaborating with them in 
crucial stages, such as brainstorming sessions, we are able to 
tap into the strengths of each other’s mixed backgrounds and 
disciplines in order to get different aspects and points-of-view 
and use those insights to our advantage. 
This collaboration could range from simple feedback sessions 
on each other’s work, to facilitating brainstorming sessions in 
groups, where interaction (Andreasen et al., 2015, p. 85-86) and 
communication is very important, which often allows to both 
give and receive feedback on different details that perhaps initially 
were not considered.
Furthermore, by being involved in other students projects it can be 
inspiring, despite they’re project topics are completely unrelated, 
as it indirectly benefits my own projects progression and my 
personal motivation.

Designers - fellow design students were also involved in different

3. Context Exploration
3.1 Trend analysis 
To get a deeper understanding of the current problem and 
trends that might give insights into the present state and 
future implications of this problem, a literature review was 
conducted and presented in a trend analysis to start of the 
research and exploration phase of this topic. In this case the 
trend analysis also serves as a validation as to why this thesis 
topic is important to investigate and solve. It also serves to 
help identify and analyse customer needs and business 
opportunities in order to develop design visions and product 
ideas (van Boeijen, Daalhuizen, Zijlstra & van der Schoor, 
2014, p. 59). 
3.1.1 Aging population 
When we look at the population trends concerning European 
countries, it’s evident that the population is slowly aging, 
meaning that the median age of the population is 
increasing. The regions old-age dependency ratio (number of 
people of 65+ year old in relation to those between 15-64 years 
old) is projected to double to 54% by 2050 (Carone & Costello, 
2006), which means for every elderly citizen there will 
only be two of working age, opposed to the current number 
of four working people per elderly citizen. In other words, the 
proportion of people of working age is decreasing while 
the number of those retired is increasing. This trend is 
steadily increasing in the near future as a greater proportion 
of the post-war baby-boom generation reaches retirement 
(Eurostat, 2018).
To give a specific example of how the trend has been developing 
in the last few years, we see that on the 1st of January 2016 the 
population of the EU member states combined was estimated 
at 510.3 million. Young people between ages 0 to 14 years 
old made up 15.6% of the population while people considered 
to be of working age (between 15 to 64 years old) accounted 
for 65.3% of the population. Older persons (aged 65 or over) 
had a 19.2% share (an increase of 0.3% compared with the 
previous year and an increase of 2.4% compared with 10 
years earlier). The median age in the EU-28 increased by 4.3 
years (on average, by 0.3 years per annum) between 2001 and 
2016, rising from 38.3 years to 42.6 years (Carone & Costello, 
2006) (see figure 4 and figure 5).
The trend of an aging population that we are seeing take place 
in Europe is occurring due to a couple of main demographic 
developments. Firstly, fertility rates in all EU countries 
are below the natural population replacement rate and are 
projected to remain so. Secondly, the recent decline in fertility 
rates combined with the post-war baby-boom will lead to a 
temporary increase in old-age dependency ratio. Lastly, the 
overall life-expectancy has increased by eight years since the 
1960s and are estimated to rise another six years by 2050.

The aging population trend that is taking place in Europe is 
important to address due to that fact that it is strongly related to 
the reasoning and purpose of the project goal of this thesis. The 
trend gives validation and reason to the fact that it is important 
to develop products and services that facilitate the increasing 
number of elderly people living on their own and their unmet 
needs therefore catering to a growing market. The development 
of these kind of products will allow elderly to live independently 
for longer, which will have both social economic value as well 
as mental and physical wellbeing of the individual elderly that 
benefit from them.
3.1.2 Economic impact 
The impact of an ageing population on the economy is inevitable 
due to the increased dependency ratio mentioned previously. 
There will, therefore, be an increase in government spending 
on health care and pensions (Pettinger, 2016). Part of this will be 
used for home care, which is support provided at home. This can 
include medical attention provided by health care professionals, 
but the largest segment of homecare consists of non-medical 
personnel who provide cleaning services in which help with 
cleaning the house and other chores around the household of the 
elderly.  
An example of the expected growth can be witnessed in the USA, 
where approximately 2.1 million homecare jobs were recorded 
in 2012 and according to Shanxi Medical Periodical Press this 
number is expected to rise to at least 3 million jobs by 2020 (Tao 
& McRoy, 2015). 
These services are often paid with government funds or insurance 
money, which means that there will be an increase in the amount 
of money spent on caring for these elderly, therefore having an 
economic impact on the society. In some cases, it could also have a 
personal economic impact on the people who need help. In the UK, 
e.g., the government is planning to introduce new reforms making
it a compulsory charge for every adult to pay an additional 20,000 
pounds to cover the costs of home care and nursing (Hough, 2010).
3.1.3 Elderly fear losing independence 
Another trend that is prominent among the majority of elderly 
people, is the fear of losing independence in old age. A study 
conducted in England by the national Disabled Living Foundation 
(DLF) charity, found that almost half of the people feared moving 
into an aged care facility such as retirement homes or nursing 
homes (Hough, 2010). Furthermore, a survey commissioned by a 
social care marketplace provider, cloudBuy, revealed that losing 
independence is among the top worries of individuals above 50 
years old (News Agencies, 2014). As elderly care becomes more 
expensive, it is leading to more than two out of three British people

3.1.4 Safety for independent elderly 
Safety is another important consideration to take into 
account when it comes to elderly people living independently. 
Some of the most common occurrences are trips and falls (Unity 
Care Solutions, n.d.), which can be very hazardous for fragile 
individuals. Elderly are involved in more than 2.3 million accidents 
in their own homes each year and approximately 7,000 of these 
elderly die in such accidents (CareOne, 2014). Physical changes 
and degradation, such as declining vision, hearing, sense of 
touch and smell, strength and bone density loss are factors 
that contribute to the accidents. It is also important to consider 
that there should be no trailing electrical leads running across the 
floor which could pose as tripping hazards. 
There are two terms that cover a range of activities that are a 
part of independent living: ADL (activities of daily living) and 
IADL (instrumental activities of daily living) (Health in Aging, 
n.d.). IADL refers to activities of maintaining a household and
independent life and includes the area which is most relevant 
to the topic as it includes activities such as cooking, cleaning, 
laundry, and similar tasks.  
This is an important aspect to consider when it comes to the 
development and design of products that could aid in people living 
more independently and taking care of the household. Especially 
when it comes to developing floor cleaning products/aids, as these 
are used on the surface on which the people are walking and, thus, 
closest to the source of the hazard. 
3.1.5 Age related impairments
As people grow older, they age both physically and mentally 
leading to a number of impairments or disabilities that limit them 
from being able to perform everyday tasks. The World Health 
Organization has defined disability as follows (Tidy, 2016):
“Disability is an umbrella term, covering impairments, activity 
limitations, and participation restrictions. An impairment is a 
problem in body function or structure; an activity limitation is 
a difficulty encountered by an individual in executing a task or 
action; while a participation restriction is a problem experienced by 
an individual in involvement in life situations. Thus, disability is 
a complex phenomenon, reflecting an interaction between features 
of a person’s body and features of the society in which he or she 
lives.”
According to a study conducted by the Department of Clinical 
Epidemiology and Biostatistics at the McMaster University 
(Griffith, Raina, Wu, Zhu & Stathokostas, 2010), there are five 
main types of chronic illnesses that largely contribute 
to the disability of people over the age of 65.  The results 
showed that: 
•
Foot problems, Arthritis, Cognitive impairment, Heart problems,
Loss of agility, Reduced strength/agility and Reduced vision
made the largest contribution to both ADL and IADL related 
functional disabilities. These results were based on data collected 
from over 9,000 individuals aged 65+ years from the Canadian 
Study of Health and Aging. There was variation in magnitude 
and ranking of population attributable risk (PAR) by age, sex and 
definition of disability.

3.1.6 Floor cleaning difficulties 
Floor cleaning can be one of the more difficult tasks for the 
elderly impaired as the surface of the floor or stairs is the lowest 
point making it hard to reach. A common trend with elderly 
people is that their range of motion, flexibility and strength 
are limited, which has a significant impact on their ability to 
complete everyday tasks. When looking specifically at floor 
cleaning tasks such as mopping, vacuuming and brushing, it 
is associated with a high cardiovascular and muscle load level as 
well as a large number of improper working postures putting 
strain on the neck, shoulder, and lower back (Søgaard, Fallentin 
& Nielsen, 1996). 
It is also worth mentioning that different rooms pose varying 
levels of risk for elderly. The living room being one of the locations 
where falls tend to happen much more regularly (second after 
bathrooms), about 30% of the time, which is directly linked to the 
fact that it is the location where they spend most of their time and 
where the main cleaning and household chores are carried out 
most frequently (“Where Do Most Falls Occur in the Home?”, n.d.).
Furthermore, common methods of cleaning the floor such as 
vacuum cleaners, brooms, or mops are not ergonomically 
designed for comfortable use, especially for elderly who lack 
strength, endurance, and flexibility. A prolonged use of a vacuum 
cleaner with a regular handle can result in discomfort at

3.2 Questionnaires
A questionnaire is a useful structured research tool to gain 
better understanding of the problem and gather insights 
by receiving information straight from the target group (van 
Boeijen et al., 2014, p. 49). 
3.2.1 Approach
As part of my research phase, I developed and used semi-
structured questionnaires, which focussed on gathering 
both qualitative data as well as some quantitative data, by 
retrieving the answers on a face-to-face basis with elderly people. 
These insights allowed me to give the target group a voice, 
which helped me better understand the use context, current 
problems with existing solutions and areas of improvement, 
common impairments they experience and possible directions 
the project should go on. Furthermore, it provided me with useful 
information to develop detailed personas, found in section 3.8 - 
Personas. 
Questionnaires were chosen as an initial information gathering 
tool and a semi-structured layout was chosen, as it is more suitable, 
as the consistency of the topics allowed me to draw conclusions 
while simultaneously asking open-ended, more flexible questions 
so as to create room for a more in-depth discussion with the 
interviewees (Qu & Dumay, 2011). The semi-structured approach 
also allows human conversation to take place, and I as the 
interviewer could modify the style, pace and ordering of questions 
to induce the more in-depth responses from the interviewee (Kvale 
& Brinkmann as cited in Qu & Dumay, 2011). Most importantly, it 
enables interviewees to provide responses in their own terms 
and in the way that they think and use language. 
“The semi-structured interview enjoys its popularity because it is 
flexible, accessible and intelligible and, more important, capable 
of disclosing important and often hidden facets of human and 
organizational behaviour” – Qu & Dumay, 2011
I chose to conduct the questionnaire in a face-to-face manner, 
as it allowed me to get a more in depth and well-rounded 
response and impression of the situation as well as creating more 
comfort for the respondent. According to a study conducted 
by the University of Florence, face-to-face surveys generate the 
most representative results (Szolnoki & Hoffmann, 2013). It is 
important to note that I chose to scope my research within the 
developed countries in order to have comparable factors in my 
research.
3.2.2 Quantitative results
The age of the respondents ranged from the youngest being 68 
years-old to the oldest respondent being 98 years-old. A total of 
eleven people was interviewed for this questionnaire, and due to 
the fact that online distribution wanted to be avoided as it is the 
least reliable (Szolnoki & Hoffmann, 2013), only face-to-face and 
telephone interviews were conducted which places much more 
value on each completed questionnaire as the results were more 
reliable and elaborative.  
Common used products 
The most common used product to clean the floor was a vacuum

81% of the respondents claiming they use mops to clean away

4.1.2 Manual floor cleaning
The manual floor cleaning product category includes some of the 
most basic and time-tested designs and techniques, which might 
make them outdated but at the same time very important to 
include. Often the simplest solutions to problems include some 
of the most efficient and easiest products to use, which when 
considering elderly people can be quite important. 
The downside of most of these manual products is that they require 
a substantial amount of physical labour and strength to use at 
their full potential. Movements often include repeated scrubbing, 
bending down, and applying force while doing so. Products such as 
mops and sponges also require additional external products such 
as buckets with water/soap which means extra work for the users 
and might be dangerous for the elderly. Lastly, any dirt swept or 
wiped by the majority of these products is mostly displaced instead 
of collected, meaning extra manoeuvres are needed to pick/remove 
the dirt cleaned.
An important aspect to take into account is that the majority of the 
products to clean the floor are designed to include long handles, 
in order to minimise the need to bend over in order to reach the 
floor. This shows that posture and ergonomics were often taken 
into account in order to minimise the strain on back and shoulders 
during use. On the downside of this is that by having long handles, 
it requires the user to apply more force to translate the adequate 
force needed to scrub/clean a stain of the surface of the floor. 
Furthermore, it makes it more difficult to store the products when 
not in use due to their long shapes.

4.1.3 Motorised floor cleaning products 
The motorised floor cleaning category includes both motorised 
manual equipment such as conventional vacuum cleaners and 
mops that have motorised mechanisms but still require human 
manoeuvres to function, but also includes automatic robotic 
cleaners that simply need to have minimal human interaction. 
The motorised products generally consist of vacuum cleaners, 
which use a suction as the main mechanism to remove dust, dirt, 
and debris from the surfaces. Some of the other products include 
some sort of mopping system which uses water/soap to remove 
more stain like marks from the surfaces. 
Vacuum cleaners are one of the most widely used floor cleaning 
equipment in the average western household, as they are quite 
effective in removing dust, debris, and dirt and quite easy to use. 
27
==PAGE:33==
But despite this, they are often quite heavy, bulky, and unhandy 
to drag around while cleaning, especially for elderly people or 
those with disabilities. Furthermore, the power cables can become 
a tripping and falling hazard for elderly with mobility issues. The 
products in this category also lack in space efficiency making them 
difficult to store and heavy for elderly to lift, making staircases 
being almost impossible to clean.  
Furthermore, the handle of many of the vacuum cleaners available 
today are not ergonomically designed for optimal usability, 
causing the user to grip the handle of the vacuum cleaner at an 
uncomfortable angle. Hu et al., has concluded that this can lead to 
strain put on the wrist, shoulder, and forearm during prolonged 
use (as cited in Lee et al., 2016). This discomfort could have even 
more impact on the elderly impaired and those who suffer from 
conditions such as arthritis and Carpal Tunnel syndrome.
Other products included in this category include automatic robotic 
cleaners. Cleaning robots also mostly focus on keeping the floor 
free of dirt, debris, and dust. They are meant to be able to work 
independently by moving around the room with the help of sensors 
to steer them in the right direction. There are also some cleaning 
robots that go beyond vacuuming, such as the Braava mopping 
robot (iRobot, n.d.) which can sweep and mop floors. Despite the 
robotic cleaners sounding like the optimal solution for people 
with age-related impairments, there are still many limitations to 
consider. They often need to be backed up by conventional vacuum 
cleaners on a regular basis (Seniors Matter, n.d.) and regular 
mopping, as well as having limited batteries which often need 
long charging times. Furthermore, they can be a serious tripping 
and falling hazard for elderly with impaired vision and mobility 
(Seniors Matter, n.d.), as they are often small and very low to the 
ground making them difficult to spot. Lastly, they are extremely 
expensive ranging from $300 up to $600, when considering that 
a household will most likely also need a conventional cleaner to 
support it.

5. Design dilemmas & challenges
The design dilemmas and possible challenges represent the 
conflicting or contradicting criteria, either between possible 
stakeholders or between certain features or qualities that need 
to be met in the concept design. These were developed based on 
insights gained from the interviews and questionnaires as well as 
the stakeholder analysis.
Elders vs. caretaker 
Elderly people might need homecare for certain tasks, but the 
caretaker should make sure not to complete every little day-to-day 
task, otherwise they might make the elderly person feel like they 
have lost their independence, and this could be mentally troubling.
Relatives vs. elderly 
Elderly want to have enough independence so that they stay more 
in control of their life and thus benefit mentally from it, but at the 
same time the relatives might worry about them doing day-to-day 
tasks on their own.
Designer 
It is a challenge for designers to consider elderly people’s 
impairments during the design process without creating a ‘medical’ 
or ‘handicapped’ product, as it should be part of an inclusive design 
and appealing to a wider audience. The user must not feel singled 
out, as though they are using a product especially for disabled 
people.
Based on all the insights gained from the Exploration phase of the 
project, a strong foundation of knowledge was built on which a set 
of requirements can be built. At this point in the process it was 
difficult to set specific performance specifications of the product or 
concept, instead I chose to develop a set of design objectives and 
functions. They are essentially statements of what the concept 
design must achieve in order to meet the needs and wishes of the 
target group (Cross, 2008, p. 105).
It is important that they are not too specific, which would lead 
to limiting my solution range too much and cause for possible 
acceptable solutions to be eliminated unnecessarily (Cross, 2008, 
p. 105). On the contrary, objectives or functions that are too broad
could lead to an unclear direction in which I take solution concepts 
(Cross, 2008, p. 105). To give a clearer overview of the objectives 
I categorised them according to the most important focus areas, 
mainly based on the insights gained from the user group interviews 
(section 3.2), which lead me to the following: Performance, 
Usability, Ergonomics, Aesthetics, Manoeuvrability, and 
Price.

7. Ideation  
Ideation is the diverging process in which a large amount of ideas 
and solutions are generated, and different solution spaces are 
explored (“An Introduction to Design Thinking”, n.d.). It is the 
first step, after creating a foundation of insights, in which I aim to 
find solutions to the various problems and discrepancies identified 
in the Exploration phase of the project. 
The aim of the ideation phase is to:
•	 Go beyond the obvious solutions and seek innovative ideas
•	 Discover unexpected solutions spaces and areas of exploration
•	 Get all initial solutions out of my head and move beyond them
•	 Work together with other design thinkers to provide a creative 
environment
Although ideation is not a single planned and focused activity 
(Andreasen et al., 2015, p. 171), as solutions emerge throughout 
the Exploration phase, during goal formulation and during many 
other stages of the project, it is important to dedicate a section 
on ideation in order to give it some much needed attention. 
Furthermore, this chapter is inspired by the four main elements of 
ideation (Andreasen et al., 2015, p. 171): create (idea generation), 
combine (ideas are combined into concept proposals), visualise 
(illustrations) and complete. Also note, some additional exploration 
will be conducted throughout the ideation process when the need 
for more information arises.

By identifying the general sub-functions, it draws the attention

7.2 Morphological chart
The morphological chart generates principal solutions in an 
analytical way, by deconstructing the overall function into sub-
functions the product should fulfil (van Boeijen, 2014, p .121). The 
sub-functions that were developed in section 7.1 will directly be 
used in the morphological chart in order to come up with different 
solutions that solve each of the sub-functions. 
The morphological chart acts as a source of inspiration during 
the ideation phase, as it contains solutions to a variety of sub-
functions that can be combined and implemented to create 
solutions. Conventionally, you take one solution from each sub-
function and combine them to create a concept. In my case I am 
not limiting myself to just one solution for each sub-function and 
believe that in some cases a combination of more than one solution, 
or aspects of a solution can be used. 
The morphological chart consists of six sub-functions and upto 
six solutions for each. It was important to not have too many 
solutions and sub-problems and the amount of potential ideas that 
comes out of it would be overwhelming (Cross, 2008). The six sub-
problems include:
  
1.  Cleaning function: the cleaning function refers to the cleaning 
mechanism/method used for cleaning. Which ranges from vacuum 
powered cleaner to steam or air pressure. 
2. Gripping material: the gripping material refers to the 
materials that can be used on any handle where the user will need 
to grip the product. This is an important detail to consider as it 
will affect the comfort and usability of a product. 
3.  Touch surface: the touch surface refers to any surface that 
will be in direct contact with the user. This can include handles, 
levers and straps, but also buttons. How a surface feels to the 
touch, influenced by the materials used, can have a great impact on 
the intuitiveness of a product and transfers clues back to the user 
certain functionalities of a product and helps their interpretation 
on how a product (or sub-function) functions.
4. Adjustable length: is an interesting sub-function as it 
influences the user experience both during use as well as after 
use. It is one of the most important sub-functions identified in the 
exploration phase, as it important for both comfort and the overall 
ergonomics of a product. Furthermore, it also relates to the storing 
of a product after use, making it as compact as possible for easier 
storage.
5. Manoeuvrability: this sub-function defines how the product 
will be moved around while cleaning the floor as well as simply 
moving it from one location to another. This is a sub-function 
where multiple solutions can be combined into a single design. 
6. Shape of head: the shape of the head refers to the cleanings 
head shape, whether this is from a vacuum cleaner or a mop/broom, 
the head shape could play an interesting role to make cleaning less 
time consuming, for example on how to clean in corners easier.

7.3.2 Ergonomics 
Ergonomics is the study concerned with the interactions 
between humans and elements of a system or product 
(International Ergonomics Association, 2000). The goal of using 
ergonomics studies for product design is to reduce human error, 
increase productivity, and enhance safety and comfort by addressing 
the interaction between the human and the product or service 
(Wickens, Gordon, Lui, 1997). 
To efficiently design a product that will be ergonomically sound for 
the intended target group, it is important to consider the physical 
and mental characteristics of the users in order to efficiently apply 
the principles of user-centred design. By doing so the designer 
ensures that the goal of achieving the best possible match between 
the product and its user is reached (Pheasant, 2003). 
It is important for me to consider the important criteria that are 
used to help develop an ergonomic design, as the targeted user 
group in this project has many physical limitations. The elderly 
impaired have a decreased amount of physical capabilities, which 
is one of the main reasons why there are so few products on the 
market that are comfortable and safe for them to use. Therefore, the 
design of an ergonomically sound product is vital when designing 
for elderly with age related impairments. Commonly important 
criteria include:
Anthropometry is a vital part of ergonomics and plays an 
important role in designing a product that is directly used by a 
human. Pheasant defines anthropometry as “the branch of the 
human sciences that deals with body measurements: particularly 
with measurements of body size, shape, strength and working 
capacity”. For this project I am primarily concerned with the 
anthropometric side of ergonomics, by using the physical form and 
dimensions of the user to define those of the product.
In order to get reliable anthropometric data, which I can use 
to determine certain attributes of the product solutions, such 
as the dimensions of the product, I used DINED. DINED is an 
anthropometric database initiated by the Applied Ergonomics and 
Design Department of the TU Delft faculty of Industrial Design 
Engineering (DINED, n.d.). 
Insights 
The main objective of using the DINED database and its tools 
is to establish an educated estimation of the dimensions 
the product should be in order to be as inclusive as possible. In 
order to do this I utilised two tools, 1D database tool and Reach 
Envelope tool, to get well rounded data and from there an overall 
height margin will be estimated. 
1D database tool
The 1D database tool (DINED, n.d.) allows you to compare 
anthropometric data from several studies, allowing you to select a 
population demographic and specific body dimensions. I chose to 
look at mixed adults aged 60+ years old, inline with my target 
group. 
I looked both at the extremes of the hip height and the elbow 
height, as this is the most comfortable height to hold the floor-
cleaning product during its use and in order to include both the 
shortest people and the tallest people to be as inclusive as possible. 
Note that this is strictly the y-axis height, and not directly the 
height of the product itself as there will most likely be an angle 
involved when using, for example, a vacuum cleaner or mop. 
•	 Functional efficiency
•	 Comfort
•	 Health and safety
•	 Ease of use
•	 Quality of working

still be able to be used by people with the shortest hand width.

biggest hands, although not as comfortably. Therefore, for the 
gripping circumference a specific length will have to be chosen, 
which might not be optimal for people with the smallest or biggest 
hands.

design, I want to try and take the extremes into account in terms 
of dimensions. Although this is more often than not, not feasible, 
as it might make the dimensions of your design way too big as 
catering to both the extremes of the smallest and the biggest. Or 
because certain features are more difficult to make adjustable, 
such as the gripping circumference. Nevertheless, if an attempt 
is made to include as many as possible, within reason, it will still 
have a better impact in being inclusive.

more reserved when brainstorming and therefore coming up with

of the ideas being useful and therefore less having to be discarded.

9. Concept Evaluation 
Evaluating the concepts is a crucial part of the design process, it 
is the point of no return, as you make the decision which concept 
should be chosen for further development and detailing. 
This chapter will take you through the process of converging from 
the five concepts, to one final chosen concept which will later be 
further developed and detailed. 
As a designer it is easy to get caught up in the concepts and have a 
favourite design which might inhibit the ability to make a rational 
choice, thus in order to make an effective decision it is important 
to be critical and keep the final users and their problems as 
the focus of the decision-making process.
In order to do this effectively, a combination of evaluation/ 
decision-making methods and personal expertise and 
intuition were used. Evaluation methods focus on how well 
the goal formulation and design objectives are satisfied 
(Andreasen et al., 2015, p. 180) while personal expertise and 
intuition see the concepts as a whole and recognises their 
advantages and disadvantages. Furthermore, to conduct 
a more dynamic decision-making process, the concepts were 
discussed and evaluated with multiple fellow design students with 
the additional help of mock-up models of the different grips and 
handles shown in the concept boards.
The following methodological evaluation methods were applied: 
The Weighted Objectives Method (Cross, 2008, p. 152). 
Furthermore, an evaluation of the concepts was conducted, 
inspired by the Product Concept Evaluation technique (van 
Boeijen, 2014. p. 135) with two experts in the field of products 
aimed at the elderly impaired and inclusive design, who were also 
interviewed in section 3.4 - Interviews.
Methodological evaluation methods give a clear overview of the 
concepts ranking in relation to the criteria or objectives its graded 
against, which is one of its main advantages. At the same time 
it is important to keep in mind that value aspects are always 
subjective as they are related to personal perception. This means 
it could point at a concept to be a technically good concept but 
might be difficult to grade its competitive advantage, value, and 
utility (Andreasen et al., 2015, p. 183).

9.1 Mock-up prototyping 
Mock-up prototyping or, sketch modelling (van Boeijen, 2014. p. 
163), is a technique used to visualise ideas or concepts with three-
dimensional models. They often make use of simple materials 
such as cardboard, foam, and other easy to manipulate materials. 
In this case my main objective was to bring some of the main 
features of my five concepts from 2D drawings to 3D models 
on a 1:1 scale, in order to get a better understanding of feeling, 
dimensions and possible textures. By being able to hold, grip 
and feel them, it provided me with more content to help assess 
and evaluate the different concepts. 
As the concepts that were developed are quite large and have many 
different functions and attributes to consider, it was important 
for me to focus on modelling only certain aspects of the 
concepts. As ergonomics and how the user grips and holds the 
floor-cleaning device was at the centre of my process, I made the 
decision to focus on the handles and grips for prototyping.
Although the mock-up models of the handles were a way of rapid 
prototyping, and thus are only representative of the concepts 
handles to a certain extent, it was still a vital iterative process 
in which I could hold and feel the different shapes and explore 
different size, shapes and curves to work my way towards a 
comfortable, ergonomic and functional handle.
Foam modelling 
I started out modelling and shaping low density polyurethane 
foam, while refrencing to each one of the handles visualised in my 
concept boards. The tools used in the process were: utility knife, 
measuring tape, sandpaper, pencil. 
In some cases, I decided to explore and make some adjustments 
and modifications to the positions and shapes of some of the handle, 
at an attempt to create more comfortable to hold variations and to 
gain additional insights on ways to detail or improve the chosen 
concept later on in the process.
Wood modelling
Wood was used to make more realistic feeling models, as it 
had more weight to it and overall stronger structure, which also 
allowed me to give them more surface textures such as rubber 
grips. 
Though, wood was a lot more difficult to manipulate than foam, 
thus giving me less freedom in creating complex shapes. 
3D printing
3D printing was used to create more complex shapes, such as the 
cross grip represented in Concept board 2. 3D printing required 
CAD-modelling and therefore a lot more time consuming compared 
to foam modelling and not the best rapid prototyping method. 
Also, the 3D printers (Fused Filament Fabrication – FFF) that 
were available to me were limited in the dimensions they could 
print, leading to the majority of the bigger sized handles and 
grips being difficult and very time consuming to print. It was soon 
discovered that 3D printing would be more beneficial if smaller 
objects were to be printed and also took place at the end of the 
prototyping process. 
During the modelling process in all mediums, the insights gained 
from the ergonomics data (section 7.3.2 Ergonomics) were kept 
at hand in order to create handles with suitable dimensions.

9.2 Weighted Objectives Method
The Weighted Objectives Method (WOM) is an evaluation 
method for comparing design concepts based on overall value of 
each design concept (van Boeijen, 2014. p. 151). It assigns scores 
to the degree to which a design alternative satisfies a criteria or 
objective. 
The method allows you to take into account the importance of a 
given objective by allowing it to account for more than others by 
giving it more weight. 
9.2.1 Procedure 
The total amount of ‘weight’ needs to add up to 100. The score 
given to each concept on how well it meets an objective range 
between 1 and 10. The total score per objective for each concept is 
calculated by multiplying the score by the corresponding weight. 
The accumulated score of each concept is calculated and the 
concept with the highest score is the preferred design. 
The objectives used for the Weighted Objectives Methods are those 
that were developed in section 6.1 of the Exploration Phase. The 
final representation will be a matrix, with the criteria in rows and 
the concepts in columns (see figure 31). 
9.2.2 Ranking the objectives 
The ranking of objectives was determined by using the insights 
gained from results obtained during the interviews with the 
targeted user-group (see the objectives in section 6.1). 
During the interview it was made clear by the respondents which 
criteria they consider most important when looking at floor cleaning 
products. These criteria were then re-worded to create design 
objectives, which can be found in section 6.1 Design objectives and 
functions. These design objectives were then directly used in the 
Weighted Objectives Method matrix.

9.3 Product Concept Evaluation 
Product Concept Evaluation helps to get a better understanding 
of how intended users as well as experts or even other designers 
value the generated concepts (van Boeijen, 2014. p. 135). It 
provides critical feedback on the different aspects of each concept, 
as well as the overall value the concept has. This can assist in 
determining which aspects should be optimised or even combined 
in order to reach a good concept. In this case the overall format 
was much more informal, and took the shape of a conversation or 
brief discussion about the concepts potential.
Although the potential user-group is the most important stakeholder 
to consider during this process, I also wanted to get feedback from 
an expert that works in the field of products designed for age-
related impaired people. Lastly, I made the decision to involve 
other design engineer students, to get their critical feedback on the 
different concepts as well as pick their brain on how to improve, 
modify or clarify some of the main aspects.
The main objective of evaluating the concepts with others is to 
determine which concepts have more potential as well as gain 
insights on how to further develop or detail a potential final 
concept. 
Procedure 
The overall procedure taken for the Product Concept Evaluation 
generally consisted of open ended discussions which were supported 
with a general line of questions or topics. With the only differences 
being that the discussions varied slightly, as the designers have 
more background in design and engineering opposed to the non-
designers. 
The concept representations used during the sessions were the 
visualised concept boards which include both visual drawings of 
the concepts with annotations and a description of the different 
features. 
9.3.1 Designers feedback
The feedback gained from the designers was very useful, mostly 
due to the fact that I could talk to them on a design level about the 
feasibility, aesthetics, and overall value of the concepts.
Generally, the feedback was quite steady in terms of them 
having similar opinions. Concept 1 and Concept 2 were too 
conventional. Concept 3 had too much of a ‘medical’ image as 
it resembled the shape of crutches too much, which was an aspect 
I wanted to avoid with the designs. 
Concept 4 was not as clear to understand, but with some 
explaining it soon became apparent that it was the most unique 
design. It was mentioned that it could be combined with certain 
features found in the other concepts, such as the handle design 
features of Concept 1 or Concept 3. Concept 4 was the overall 
most preferable choice as it had a lot of potential but needed some 
detailing to become stronger.
Concept 5 was the easiest concept to ‘sell’ as it was very straight 
forward in appearance and use. It was made clear that it would be 
the easiest to market, with the lowest risk of implementation. Its 
downfall was that it did not solve all the problems the user group 
was experiencing, and only focused the gripping obstacle.

10.2 Button layout and shape
The Hybrid Vacuum cleaner is the chosen concept, in part due to its 
high score in the Weighted Objectives Method, although it scored 
quite low for the Usability objective. The Usability objective is 
one of the most important objectives the concept should fulfil, 
therefore it is important to elevate this score to a higher level for 
the Hybrid Vacuum cleaner concept. 
In order to make it more intuitive to use for the user, it is important 
that the controls of the device are straight forward and simplified 
to ensure easy understanding. Therefore, the decision was 
made to have the following controls; 1 on/off button, 1 height 
adjustment button, and 1 ejector button. By minimising the 
amount of buttons/functions and only including the essentials, I 
expect the product to be easier to understand for the user.
Furthermore, the shape of the buttons can give the user ‘use 
clues’ and help clarify the function of the buttons. By having the 
ejector button in the shape of an arrow, it symbolises and 
indicates the ‘moving forward’ motion the detached base would 
make when pressed. The power button will be large and have a 
power icon on it which lights up when pressed. All buttons will 
be large, making locating and pressing them easy, even for 
people with limited motor skills. 
Lastly, the placement of the buttons is important to consider. 
Placing the most frequently used buttons closest to where the 
user grips the handle is the obvious layout. As well as having the 
least used buttons further away from the gripping area, 
to minimise the risk of pressing them by accident during use. 
Therefore, the ejector button should be placed closest to gripping 
areas while the power button and handle extension button can be 
placed further away.

11.Domain Theory
 The product synthesis phase is the third step in the Encapsulation 
Model, and in this project, it is the final stage. Its main focus is 
defining how the final concept design will be materialised while 
taking into account the composition of the design and its parts 
(Andreasen et al., 2015, p. 193). The goal of this phase is also to 
touch upon the products related activities and functions in order 
to clarify the different organs needed for the concept to be realised 
as well as clarifying the use-scenario and product interaction 
between user and product.
The main theory which will act as the backbone for the Product 
Synthesis phase is the Domain Theory. It consists of three 
interacting domains: activity domain, organ domain and part 
domain. In this case the part domain will not be included.  
In figure 39 a symbolic illustration of the relationship between 
the three domains can be seen using the Moka Pot as an example. 
Each of the domains can be seen as an independent description of 
the product.

(from Andreasen et al., 2015, p.199)

•	 Part Domain - how it is built

11.1 Activity Domain
The first element of the Domain Theory that will be addressed is 
the Activity Domain. It focusses on how the product is used 
by the user in practice, and what kind of processes take place. 
The main interest is therefore what the product can be used for 
and what the product can do (Andreasen et al., 2015, p. 200). 
The four main operators that should be considered while developing 
the Activity Domain are: Products, Humans, Methods, and Active 
surroundings. 
Use-activity model

leads to a visualisation of the service period of the product. 
The main operators which were involved in the activity model 
include: Products, Humans and Active surroundings. In this 
case they indicate, in the red boxes, who or what is involved in 
each of the activities in the model. 
Here are some examples, which are represented in the use-activity 
model on the next page (figure 40).
‘Dirt accumulates on the ground’, is an environmental influence 
therefore categorised as the Active surroundings operator. 
‘Handle height is adjusted’, is an operation done by the person 
using the device, therefore, categorising it as Humans operator.
By visualising the use-activity model it sheds light on each step 
of the service period of the floor-cleaning device and clarifies the 
intended use. It also indicates some of the important activities 
involved, allowing me to see which potentially need further 
detailing. A crucial part of the use process is, for example, the 
activity of the base detaching and cleaning underneath obstacles. 
Product is any kind of materialised and executable

The Organ Domain focusses on what the product is able to do,

12. The Hybrid Vacuum cleaner
12.1 Concept description
The Hybrid Vacuum cleaner is a unique floor-cleaning device that 
utilizes the convenience of a vacuum cleaner, with the technology 
of a robotic floor cleaner and optimised with the comfort of 
ergonomically designed handles and usability. By combining all 
these aspects into one floor-cleaning device, what you end up with 
is an inclusive, user-friendly concept design that can be used by a 
wide variety of people.
At first glance it seems like a conventional stand-up vacuum 
cleaner, but when looked at it closer, it becomes apparent it is 
much more than that. 
Its primary function being a vacuum cleaner with the convenience 
and reliability vacuum cleaners are known for. However, the 
base consists of two parts, allowing for easy cleaning underneath 
obstacles such as couches, beds, or chairs without the need to 
change position. By pressing the ejector button, located close to 
the gripping points on the handle, the base detaches and with 
the help of sensors, cleans underneath the obstacle and quickly 
returns to the vacuum’s base where the built-in dirt cannister is 
automatically emptied into the vacuum cleaner, allowing you to 
continue vacuuming the floor.
The ergonomic design combines adjustability with optimised, 
muli-grip handles, to allow the user to adjust the vacuum cleaner 
to an optimised height while the ergonomic handles allow for 
comfortable gripping positions, giving the user better control over 
the vacuum cleaner. This in turn also leads to less strain being 
put on the body, meaning that it is user friendly to people who 
live with age-related impairments such as arthritis, weakened 
muscles and limited agility and flexibility. 
12.2 Use storyboard
The steps taken while using the Hybrid Vacuum cleaner are very 
straight forward, as it closely resembles the use of a conventional 
vacuum cleaner but with some extras. This means that it is also 
easily adapted to by the user, therefore being innovative but at 
the same time familiar in many aspects. 
On the following pages you can see the use-scenarios in different 
steps. The first storyboard shows the overall steps during floor 
cleaning. The second storyboard demonstrates how the dirt is 
emptied after vacuum cleaning. Finally, the final storyboard shows 
the different gripping positions the user can have while using the 
Hybrid Vacuum cleaner.

13. Final concept discussion
 
Conceptual vulnerability can manifest itself in even the most 
well thought out designs and processes. The methods, models, and 
tools that I implemented throughout the start until the end of the 
design process were applied as thorough as possible to minimise 
conceptual vulnerability. Despite this, there are always areas 
which could be improved, reiterated, or changed in order to 
get a better result. Below I have compared the final design once 
more to some of the main design objectives formulated in section 
6.1. 
1. Ergonomics
Great attention was paid to designing the Hybrid Floor vacuum 
in such a way to make it as ergonomic as possible. I did some 
research into human anthropometry to get a rough estimation on 
the dimensions to use for both the overall height and the handle/
grips of the cleaner. Furthermore, foam modelling was done to get 
a real-life sense of the chosen dimensions of the handle and its 
overall shape. 
Where the Hybrid Vacuum cleaner could improve on in terms of 
ergonomics, is by having more adjustable features, such as the 
angle of the handle itself or interchangeable grips that are directly 
aimed at different age-related impairments. 
2. Usability 
Usability and the overall intuitiveness of the Hybrid Vacuum 
cleaner were also addressed during the design process, with special 
attention placed on button shape, size, and placement in order to 
make it as easy as possible for the user to use. Furthermore, only 
the necessary controls were included, meaning there is less room 
for confusion while not impacting the performance of the cleaner.
To get a deeper understanding and validation, if the design is 
actually as intuitive as expected, thorough user testing should 
take place with participants from the user group. This was not 
completed during the span of this project and is an important 
aspect that should be carried out in further development.
3. Manoeuvrability
In an attempt to make the Hybrid Vacuum more manoeuvrable, a 
set of light-weight materials were used in combination with a slick 
design and construction, to minimise the weight. Furthermore, the 
guiding wheels located under the base of the vacuum minimise 
the friction between the base and the floors surface. Lastly, the 
ergonomic grip allows the user to have more control over the device 
and bring their stronger/larger arm muscles into play, adding to 
the manoeuvrability.
Very limited material research was done, and the materials chosen 
were chosen based on previous knowledge. In order to make it as 
light as possible while still being durable, thorough construction 
and material research needs to be done. 
4. Performance
The performance of the Hybrid Vacuum cleaner is met in terms of 
the terms initially described, such as making cleaning easier and 
being able to clean under obstacles. Based on the research done 
on existing technologies, it is safe to say that these functions can 
without a doubt be met if a functional prototype were to be made, 
but due to not having a fully functional prototype it has not been 
proven by myself.  
For further development, a functional prototype needs to be made 
and its performance must be tested in a variety of different use 
contexts and floor surfaces to validate the concept design further.
107
==PAGE:113==
5. Price 
There was no price calculation carried out, as this required 
further detailing of production methods, packaging, transport, 
manufacturing, and materials. But given the overall material 
choice and technology that is needed for the Hybrid Vacuum 
cleaner, it should not exceed the cost of any other conventional 
vacuum cleaner.
To get a clearer estimation of the costs involved and final selling 
price of the Hybrid Vacuum cleaner, further development and 
detailing needs to take place as well as extensive research into 
various factors such as manufacturing. 
6. Aesthetics 
Although no extensive shape, colour or form study was done to 
ensure good aesthetics, I made sure to make it as minimalistic and 
smooth as possible, in order to give the Hybrid Vacuum cleaner a 
more neutral appearance. 
One aspect that was intentionally enforced, was to make sure it did 
not appear to look like a ‘medical’ device, or any other senior home 
care product. Although my main target group is that of elderly 
people living with age-related impairments, the intention was 
to make it attractive enough to be desired by a larger audience. 
Overall, I think that objective was met with the design, although 
there is still room for variation of colour and texture use.

14.1 Project plan
Creating a project plan before starting a long-term project, such 
as this master thesis project, is crucial as it helps to plan the 
activities that need to be completed by a certain date. However, it 
is also very difficult to know beforehand, all the tasks that need 
to be completed and how long they will take to complete, as there 
are always unexpected hurdles or setbacks that need to be dealt 
with, or on the other hand, some tasks might be completed faster 
than expected. 
The overall structure used to split the timeline up were the four 
main phases; Orientation phase, Exploration phase, Concept 
synthesis phase and the Product synthesis phase. Roughly 5-6 
weeks were planned for each phase (see Appendix 6 for plan). 
Overall the time allocated to each activity was quite accurately 
estimated, as well as the specific activities and tasks planned to 
be completed. This was mostly due to the fact that I dedicated 
enough time to actually making the planning scheme, with 
realistic expectations, and also kept it in mind throughout the 
projects duration. Despite my best efforts, some tasks ended up 
taking longer than expected.
The Exploration phase was quite time consuming, as getting 
primary data involved planning interviews and meetings with 
external people. Furthermore, response times of people was not 
always taken into account, often leading to time being lost waiting 
for responses. 
Another phase that was a lot more time consuming than originally 
anticipated, was the visualisation of ideas and concepts as well 
as the rapid prototyping. As the facilities that were used for the 
rapid prototyping were often occupied (3D printers and workshop) 
which was not taken into account prior to starting. 
Overall the planning for this project was quite realistic and well 
thought out beforehand, which thankfully led to most of the tasks 
being fully completed on time with the right standard of quality 
being maintained.

15. Conclusion  
With an ageing population across the world rising, primarily in 
Europe, due to multiple factors including longer life-spans, it is 
becoming more important than ever to put some of our focus on 
designing appropriate products to facilitate to this demographic 
group. With the physical degeneration, that start as early 65+ 
years of age, keeping up with and undertaking even the simplest 
tasks, such as household chores, can become problematic, tiring 
and even dangerous. 
This demographic change is giving way to a set of unmet needs that 
affect this user group, making living independently more difficult 
than it should be, as they lack the right tools and equipment to do 
so. This thesis focused its attention on one of the aspects of living 
independently, which is maintaining a hygienic and clean-living 
environment, more specifically keeping the floors of the home 
clean. Cleaning the floor can be dangerous and labour intensive 
as it often involves the person to bend over in uncomfortable 
positions or by using current floor-cleaning products that are often 
not ergonomically designed. 
In order to get a better understanding of the problem, a number 
of areas within this overall problem area were investigated. It 
became apparent that the old-age dependency ratio across Europe 
is expected to double by 2050, meaning the market for products 
that cater to seniors is ever expanding, exposing both a social need 
as well as a business opportunity. Furthermore, it also appeared 
that losing independence is among the top worries of people above 
the age of 50 years old, as elderly care is becoming more expensive, 
and they do not want to become a burden on friends or family, 
solidifying the fact that there is a need for products that help 
them be more independent. Both primary and secondary research 
coincided with these findings.
The next step was to understand the specific age-related 
impairments that are common among the aging population and 
how it affects them while maintaining their house hygienic and 
clean, such as cleaning the floors. Primary and secondary research 
found that the most common impairments were; reduced mobility, 
arthritis, weakness of strength and overall. Furthermore, 
technology exploration was conducted to investigate current 
solutions and products on the market, as well as alternate 
technologies that could be applied to solve the main issues. The 
market analysis verified the fact that there are very few products 
that actually solve the previously mentioned unmet needs, and the 
only products on the market that really focussed on people living 
with age-related impairments had very ‘medical’ appearances, 
making them very unattractive for people to use as well as giving 
them the sense that something is ‘wrong’ with them, these also did 
not include floor-cleaning products.
The insights gained during exploration lead to scoping the projects 
solution space to focus on developing a floor-cleaning product 
with competent usability and ergonomic features, primarily 
concentrating on grips, handles and adjustability. Based on 
user insights a list of requirements and design objectives were 
formulated, with emphasis on the ergonomics, usability, and 
manoeuvrability, but also keeping the performance, price range, 
and aesthetics in mind.
Using the foundation of knowledge built during the Exploration 
phase of the project, the Concept Synthesis phase was initiated 
with ideation and conceptualisation and eventually leading to 
the Product synthesis of the final concept design. The Hybrid 
Vacuum cleaner is the outcome of the design process, which is a 
vacuum cleaner that combines technology of a robotic floor cleaner 
with that of a conventional stand-up floor cleaner. It focusses on 
ergonomics by including comfortably designed multi-grip handle 
which is adjustable in height. Furthermore, its usability objectives 
are met by minimising the complexity of the user’s interaction 
with the product, and only including the necessary controls, which 
are located in an easy to reach location on the handle. Lastly, the 
detachable base allows the users to clean underneath obstacles 
they might find in their house, such as couches, beds, or tables, 
without having to bend over of reach down.

Appendix 2 - Rosemary Frazer – Senior Manager at 
GDI Hub
1. What is your role within the Global Disability 
Innovation Hub?
My role is managing stakeholder engagement and to build 
up our supporter base. I was also responsible for linking 
up disabled people with designers, technologists, engineers 
etc.
2. What is your personal motivation to work with an 
organization such as the GDI Hub?
I think there are wonderful possibilities for how inclusive 
design, AT and engineering can improve the lives of 
disabled people around the globe.
3. In your opinion, what role do product designers and 
engineers have when it comes to inclusive design? 
(including designing for elderly, people with disabilities, 
etc)
This is a crucial role for product designers and engineers. 
There are one billion disabled people around the globe 
and this is set to grow as we live longer and live with 
conditions which would not have been possible in the 
past. This is a huge market. My frustration is that too 
often disabled people are not included in the decisions on 
product designs, or if they are, it is too late in the process. 
Inclusive design is good for everyone and we must get 
better at working in partnership.
4. What are some of the current and future attempts the 
GDI Hub takes in order to reach their vision and goals?
We are launching an MSc on design and disability. There 
will be three scholarships available for disabled students, 
we want to encourage more disabled people into this area 
of study. We are also working a range of partners to look 
at we can highlight the fact that so many disabled people 
do not get the equipment needed to live independent lives 
and want to help reduce costs and improve availability of 
equipment by helping to develop a better market place for 
such equipment.
5. As far as you know, are there a lot of products or 
technologies currently out there that specifically aim at 
this market segment? If so, could you mention any?
Not that I am aware of and I have looked around. I believe 
this is due to people assuming that someone else will carry 
out these tasks for the disabled person, this is not the case 
in most instances. As a result homes tend to become less 
cared for and cleaned as regularly as they were.
6. Is there a need for products for elderly and disabled 
people that facilitate easier and more efficient cleaning 
around the house?
Definitely there is a market for such products, there is 
huge gap here.
121
==PAGE:127==
7. What are your thoughts on independent living and 
what kinds of positive effects would products that are 
aimed at helping elderly have on their mental and 
physical well-being?
Independence is so important to everyone and it is vital 
that when someone becomes disabled due to the ageing 
process that their lives are as unaffected as possible. They 
should be able to carry out the day to day tasks as much 
as possible. This is not just important from a physical 
standpoint but also better for good mental health.
8. What are some important aspects I should consider 
when tackling this problem area? Why?
Speak to people, get their views and think about what you 
yourself would want. Too often disabled and older people 
are seen as ‘other’. This is a huge mistake and they should 
be considered the same as everyone else just that they 
need certain adaptations and equipment to carry on living 
their lives.
9. What are some of the first thoughts and/or directions 
you see this project going in? What are some of the 
current vital problems that need to be addressed?
As a wheelchair user I find hoovering a problem, also 
cleaning higher places such as tiling in the bathroom, 
windows etc. Something should be available to make these 
tasks easier.
Any further remarks or insights related to this topic that 
could benefit this project can be written here:
I think it is a wonderful step in the right direction that 
these issues are being considered rather than product 
designs focused entirely on wheelchairs, prosthetics etc.

Appendix 3 - Caroline Jacobs – Head of Development 
at RIDC
1. What is your role at RICA? What does it involve?
Head of Business Development. Working with commercial 
clients and charitable funders to identify and deliver user-
centred research projects that that help inform the design 
of products and services through the involvement of older 
and disabled consumers.
2. What is your personal motivation to work with an 
organization such as RICA?
I am interested in accessible and inclusive design and like 
to make a practical difference to society.
3. What does the typical process look like when testing 
and evaluating the existing products and services?
Identify the research questions - what you want to find 
out. usually followed by identify the existing and or 
potential users. Identify where best to test i.e. in a lab, 
at home, out in the environment etc, duration of testing - 
sometimes one-off , other times a few months. All depends 
on the product/service and user! Testing is followed by 
data analysis and reporting.
4. How does RICA determine if a product is suitable for 
the disabled or elderly impaired? What kind of criteria 
are used?
Typical criteria are usability, accessibility and 
affordability plus is it something that people are likely to 
want / see a need for in their lives!
5. In your opinion, what is the importance of involving 
the elderly and disabled people in the evaluation process?
For mainstream products/service this helps you to develop 
inclusive products and services and ultimately reach a 
wider market. They are a more ‘critical’/’difficult’ user 
to design for. Something that is easy for disabled older 
people to use is easy for others. If it is specialist product 
targeted at this group of users it is simply sensible market 
research/testing to test with your potential customer base /
user group!
6. As far as you know, is there a need for products that 
facilitate easier floor cleaning for elderly impaired 
people?
light weight ‘wand’ vacuum cleaners
7. What are some of the problem areas that you know 
about regarding current cleaning products for elderly? 
Are there any unmet needs you know about? - (If there 
are not any specific to cleaning products, maybe mention 
some general ones that are common among other types of 
products)

Assembling and Dismantling: Assess ease of use and 
convenience of assembling and dismantling the appliances. 
• 
Grip Design: Consider the grip design (shape, design, and 
surface) & handle height for comfort. 
• 
Controls, Switches and Indicators: Assess the suitability
