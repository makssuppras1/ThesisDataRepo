Abstract
This thesis is part of the FLOW project, which stands for ”Flexible energy systems Leveraging
the Optimal integration of EVs deployment Wave”.
The rising market share of Electric Vehicles (EV) in Europe, driven by climate goals [1], man-
dates a shift from Internal Combustion Engine Vehicles (ICEV) to EVs for decarbonizing trans-
portation. To achieve massive adoption, EVs associated costs must be reduced to the extent
that users don’t see an economic problem compared to ICEVs. That is why the main goal of this
project is to make electric vehicles more attractive and convenient by showcasing the economic
incentives and environmental advantages Smart Charging (SC) can offer.
It must also be added that the surge in EV adoption poses challenges to the electric grid, par-
ticularly in public charging scenarios, where a high number of EVs can connect and cause
instabilities. So the study will also address a SC solution to this, offering a centralized control
considering grid connection limits.
The study sets out to achieve specific objectives:
1. Investigate the real impact of smart charging on reducing charging costs and equivalent
CO2 emissions in public parking.
2. Quantify the differences between decentralized and centralized SC approaches, i.e. con-
sidering each EV by itself, or as a fleet.
3. Explore the potential of V2G to enhance the economic benefits for EV drivers.
To achieve these goals, two SC algorithms were designed and tested in a workplace charger
cluster and demonstrated promising results. Despite their simplicity, decentralized and cen-
tralized approaches offered similar optimization outcomes, making centralized control a more
feasible strategy as it respects fuse limits.
The decentralized algorithm was subject to different conditions such as cost optimization using
spot prices, which showed a 15% reduction, which lowers to 5% with the real tariff structure,
highlighting its importance for the EV drivers. Carbon emission reductions of 12% can be ob-
tained as well, while Vehicle to Grid (V2G) is still not able to take over V1G as the preferred
choice, as the charging costs increase due to the charging/discharging efficiency.
The centralized control had similar results in terms of optimization but considered fuse limits,
which makes it an upgraded version of the decentralized one.
To conclude the document, possible improvements in the rigor of the work and future research
on the field are mentioned.

1
Introduction and objectives
In recent years, there has been a continuous upward trend in the market share of EV in Europe,
representing 21.6% of new car registrations in 2022 [2]. This fact is mainly driven by the Euro-
pean climate law, which states that EU countries must reduce greenhouse gas emissions by at
least 55% by 2030 (compared to 1990 levels), with the final goal to make the EU 2climate neu-
tral” by 2050 [3]. To attain this goal, one of the aspects to focus on is the decarbonization of the
transportation industry, which still accounted for 11% of Europe’s total Greenhouse Gas (GHG)
emissions by the 4th quarter of 2022 [4].
In order to reduce the carbon emissions in the transportation sector, it is essential to gradually
substitute the currently deployed ICEV with their electric counterparts. This measure will only
make sense if the energy mix used during the new lifetime of EVs is lower in GHG emissions
than their predecessors. The lifetime energy mix encompasses not only the energy needed to
charge the battery, but also the materials utilized to manufacture the vehicle itself, which must
be extracted using renewable energy sources [5]. Anyhow, given the increasing adoption of
EVs, ensuring the sustainability of charging has become a key short-term goal.
One of the technical difficulties that an increased EV adoption entails, is the impact they have
on the electric system. Battery Electric Vehicles (BEV)s have batteries of around 40 kilowatt-
hour (kWh) on average 1, which means that a considerable power input is needed to charge the
battery in a reasonable time, since the duration of charging is one of the key factors that keeps
users opting for gasoline-powered vehicles rather than moving to EVs. For those who can afford
to have a charger at home, extended charging times are not a concern, as they can charge their
vehicles overnight, although this becomes an issue for long journeys. Indeed, another limiting
factor for EVs compared to ICEVs is the capacity of the fuel tank. However, for those who need
to use public charging stations, time becomes a limiting factor that must be taken into account.
Various fast-charging techniques are being implemented to address this issue, but that is not the
focus of this project. The key point is that the way to increase the charging speed is to increase
the power of the charger (although it is not always necessary, since vehicles come with their
own Battery Management System (BMS) that determines the ideal power from a battery health
perspective).
As a result, EVs must be considered as important electrical loads, especially when considering
charger clusters that can aggregate high power demands at a single point. Therefore, the
implementation of a new charging infrastructure is restricted by the capacity of the local power
grid, as the deployment of a large number of Electric Vehicle Supply Equipment (EVSE) can
lead to grid instability [7].
Although Norway is the leading EU country in EV share, only 30.7% of the Norwegian fleet is
plug-in as of October 2023. More precisely, 23.6% accounts for BEV and the remaining 7.1%
for Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicles (PHEV) [8]. If the European Union (EU) follows, in the next
years, the same trend as Norway, the number of BEVs is expected to increase significantly,
making it necessary to address their impact on grid stability.
To smooth the effects of high-load charging processes on the grid, two main actions are possible:
1. Increase grid robustness: Upgrading and expanding the power grid infrastructure to ac-
commodate higher charging demands is crucial. This includes investing in smart grid

technologies that can improve electricity supply efficiency. Additionally, incorporating en-
ergy storage solutions can help to balance the intermittent nature of renewable energy
sources, and provide a buffer during peak charging times. This action is generally much
easier to put into practice than relying on storage, and sometimes less expensive. How-

2. Apply SC techniques: This action entails the utilization of real-time data and communi-

enables better coordination between Charge Points (CP)s and the general energy grid.
Incorporating V2G capabilities can further enhance grid flexibility, allowing EVs to con-

1.1
Problem presentation
In this project, the main focus will be on the second action: Smart charging. Also known as
V1G, when the power direction is unidirectional; or V2G when it is bidirectional. More precisely,
the study will evaluate the workplace charger cluster flexibility potential when SC techniques
are implemented under different scenarios.
In recent years, several studies have faced different possibilities around charging vehicles in the
private area. These studies have shown promising results and improvement in features such
as charging costs, CO2 emissions, grid support, etc. However, these studies do not reflect
the increasing need for Public Charge Points (PCP), as, with the increase of EV adoption,
people who cannot afford to have a charger at home due to space or cost, will need to rely on
public chargers, making the number of these to grow proportionally to the democratization of
EV ownership [9].
The main difference between charging at home and charging in a public place is the amount of
time that the user can connect to the power supply, which is a key factor when talking about the
flexibility potential. This thesis is aimed at understanding if EV charger clusters in parking lots
can offer flexibility while charging, not only to the user but also to the grid operators.
The main hindrance that SC methods find nowadays is the lack of transparency between EVs
and EVSEs. This makes battery-related information, such as State Of Charge (SOC), State of
Health (SOH), or efficiency ratings not available. Given this situation, the data used throughout
the project will consist of session recordings provided by the Charge Point Operator (CPO) of
the analyzed site: Spirii.
Spirii ® is a company that has been operating in the e-mobility sector for the past 12 years both
as CPO and a service/platform provider, not only operating the chargers but also acting as an
interface between the user and the machines [10]. The data provided will be explained in detail
in section 4.2.
One of the SC key conditions is that the user’s comfort must be maintained. The load shift should
therefore not reduce the access to cars when needed by workers [7]. That is why a historical
data set will be considered in this study, to do a perfect foresight of the charging schedule and
ensure user satisfaction.

State of the art
The integration of EVs in the power system has been extensively explored in several review
articles, each shedding light on distinct aspects. Papers such as [11] discuss control strate-
gies related to charging-discharging coordination. Mathematical modeling-based solutions for
operational management problems are explored in [12]. Furthermore, [13] evaluates EV grid
integration standards, [14] provides a comprehensive review of available EV chargers, and [15]
offers an overview of integration barriers and possible services provided by EVs. These re-
views collectively set the stage for understanding the broader context of EV integration within
the power system.
Analyzing the impact of EVs on the power grid requires differentiation between charging power
profiles, uncontrolled or controlled. The first involves vehicles charging at full capacity until the
required energy is input. In contrast, the second incorporates optimization and control strategies
to address different matters such as grid challenges, offer services to the power system, opti-
mize economic considerations, and improve renewable energy utilization. Other factors such
as user behavior, vehicle characteristics, and charger/grid features must also be considered.
In the realm of data sources for EV integration studies, [16] performs a comprehensive review
of information sources that span the last two decades. It categorizes the different types of
data sources into surveys, ICEV and EV trials, and charger trials, and determines the essential
parameters to implement SC. These parameters are the SOC, charging power limit, plug-in and
out time, charged energy, or charging time. However, due to constraints in the communication
protocols between the EVs and EVSEs, this information is in most cases not available, as it is
for the case of this project. In these situations, to date, different hypotheses and approximations
need to be implemented. However, there remains a lack of detailed overviews that specifically
address data sources related to EV driving and charging behavior.
Moving to studies focused on flexibility potential, [17] analyzes EV charging and flexibility based
on reports from public charging stations, specifically in the Netherlands. The research divides
the charging elasticity depending on the context of the EVSE into “park to charge”, “charge near
home” and “charge near work”, which offer different charging schedules, i.e., different load shift-
ing possibilities. Researchers found that 59% of the aggregated EV demand could be delayed
for more than 8 hours. Furthermore, they found that home chargers offer the largest flexibility
potential, as they show higher connection times, which enables power to be distributed through
a broader time range to fit grid needs, especially when it comes to moving the evening demand
peak to the night [18]. This information is crucial for understanding the potential flexibility in
workplace charging scenarios, as the charging hours are the exact opposite of the at-home
chargers.
With the increase in EV ownership, widespread concern has been raised regarding the potential
for EV charging demand to overload electricity grids. Even though SC seemed a proper solution,
local clustering of EVs can lead to unintended consequences as national incentives fail to align
with local pressures. In [19], Daneshzand investigates how the selection of charging tariffs
can influence grid stress and carbon reduction. The results show that smart charging is not
necessarily a better grid solution than on-demand charging. The latter is supported by [20],
where an EV study in the United Kingdom (UK) is described. Xydas conducts a cluster analysis
of charging sessions from public charging stations. The study investigates the charging impact
on the distribution network and emphasizes the potential for Demand-Side Management (DSM)
based on charging habits in specific areas. The main drawback of on-demand charging is the

Regarding user preferences, [21] assesses the willingness of potential adopters of SC solutions

In summary, within the context of studying the integration of EVs in the power system and
the flexibility potential of smart charging, the literature review underscores the importance of
understanding control strategies, user behavior, and the availability of data sources for accurate
modeling.
2.1
Contribution of this work
As seen in the previous section, several papers have reviewed different general characteristics
concerning EV integration and more specifically the flexibility potential of EVSEs. The purpose
of this study is to continue exploring the field of smart charging to better understand its po-
tential contributions to a greener future and a more Distributed Energy Resource (DER)-based
network. In other words, the document aims to discover the potential benefits offered by a
workplace EVSE cluster in terms of cost for the user and CO2 emissions.
The project will involve the implementation of a smart charging algorithms, coded in Python,
which will be tested on a parking lot located in Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark, under various scenarios
(more information in section 4.1). These scenarios will encompass different optimization stud-
ies, including decentralized cost and CO2 emission reduction, centralized emission curtailment
from the entire cluster perspective considering fuse limits, and exploring V2G capabilities. Real
charging profile data is crucial for the accuracy of the algorithm. This information will be pro-
vided by Spirii, the CPO at the specified location, and will include details such as connection
time, charging time, and energy delivered. However, a key source of information such as the
SOC will be missing, hence various hypotheses are formulated, adding a layer of complexity to
the study.
To ensure the relevance and applicability of the findings, the results are aimed to be tested in a
real-world demonstration as part of FLOW, a European project that explores user-centric V2X
smart charging solutions and their integration into energy grids. This demonstration will serve
as a practical verification of the feasibility and effectiveness of the Smart Charging algorithm
within the specified workplace charger cluster.

Methodology
3.1
Research design
This study adopts a mixed-methods approach to investigate the flexibility potential of smart
charging EVs in a workplace setting, specifically at the EV charging stations in front of building
325 at Technical University of Denmark (DTU) Lyngby Campus.
From a Distribution System Operator (DSO) perspective, technical challenges surrounding EV
integration are the main concern. Still, their solution cannot be detrimental to the quality of ser-
vice and the profitability expected by EV owners [5]. This study aims to showcase the flexibility
possibilities of SC in a workplace without affecting user needs. Thus, historical charging data
will be used to find the economic or environmental benefit of SC, and if SC is compatible with
user satisfaction. The primary data source for this research is the charging data provided by
Spirii, the CPO managing the chargers at the selected location; but DTU Campus Service also
helped clarifying other charger characteristics.
The project will encompass the following activities:
1. Collect and manage data from the parking lot chargers, extracting and sorting information
from the files provided by the CPO so that it can be used in an optimization script.
2. Analyze the preliminary information to extract information on flexibility potential, user be-
havior, average charging hours, uncontrolled profiles, etc; and sort the data to be com-
patible with the algorithm input requirements. Given the lack of some parameters, some
hypotheses will need to be formulated.
3. Extract and use other information such as electricity price or CO2 emissions through time,
in order to implement in different SC scenarios.
4. Develop, compile, and test Python-based linear SC algorithms that adapt the charging
power profile to different situations by shifting the load over time or modulating the mag-
nitude of the power to meet the constraints of the system.
5. SC methods can be customized to meet very different requirements. In this investiga-
tion, several scenarios will be considered and the flexibility potential of the parking EVSE
will be tested through computer simulations. These simulations will be iterative, allowing
modifications to the algorithm until the optimal and more realistic execution is achieved.
6. Assess the performance of the model under different scenarios and compare the out-
comes against the uncontrolled scenario.
7. In the final phase of the study, recommendations for future features and improvements to
the algorithm will be proposed.
After the thesis, a real-world demonstration is planned to be executed to validate the feasibility
of the algorithm in practical conditions. This demonstration is part of the FLOW project, insights
will be studied and the differences between theory and practice will be highlighted.
3.2
The algorithms
In this section, the logic and mathematical formulation behind the algorithms is explained. As
previously commented, two different algorithms are implemented in this project: the decentral-
ized algorithm, with individual charging control but no consideration of the cluster constraints;
6
“Quantifying the flexibility potential of electric vehicles smart charging”
==PAGE:21==
and the centralized algorithm, which executes a charging modulation respecting the cluster’s
fuse current limit without forgetting about the individual needs.
Both scripts are based on the ScyPy optimize Python package. SciPy’s optimization module of-
fers functions designed to minimize (or maximize) objective functions, potentially under specific
constraints. The module includes solvers to address non-linear problems, linear programming,
constrained and non-linear least squares, root finding, and curve fitting [22]. The problem is
formulated as a linear programming one, which aims to minimize a linear objective function
subject to linear equality and inequality constraints [23].
Two different approaches/algorithms are proposed, a ”decentralized” one, where each EV charg-
ing session is optimized separately, and a ”centralized” one, where all the EVs are optimized at
the same time, and the algorithm is run each time an EV connects or disconnects.
The two algorithms are pretty similar, the second one being a modification of the first, which
includes the fuse limit. Therefore, to understand the centralized algorithm developed in sub-
section 3.2.3, it is important to read subsection 3.2.2 before.

by modulating the power and modifying the time series. The optimal solution will be found by

3.2.3
The centralized algorithm
The purpose of this algorithm is to smart charge the EVs considering all the connected vehicles,
and adapting their charging needs to the cluster capacity.
Despite their similarity, two main features differentiate this algorithm from the previous one:
1. The algorithm is executed every time a vehicle is connected or disconnected from the
parking lots as long as the number of connected EVs is not 0. In other words, the algorithm
runs when there is a change in the number of vehicles connected to the grid. The idea
is that every time a vehicle connects or disconnects from the cluster, the script will run
the centralized optimization considering the energy already delivered to the EVs that were
connected before, so that the remainder Ep at each optimization run is a simple subtraction
of the energy delivered in the previous instants Ed from the total session energy E:

4
Case study
The project aims to shed light on several critical aspects:
• Cost Reduction: Evaluate how the implementation of smart charging strategies can lead
to cost savings for the user. Even though the chargers are managed by Spirii, the prices
are set by DTU Campus Service following a dynamic tariff which will be explained in detail
in subsubsection 4.7.1.
• CO2 Emission Reduction: Investigate the potential environmental benefits of SC, aiming
to reduce the equivalent carbon emissions associated with EV charging. The idea is that
the power input will be modulated and shifted in time to avoid high emission periods without
affecting the user’s convenience. For more information, check subsubsection 4.7.1.
• Centralized management: Considering the limits of the cluster’s fuse, the centralized
control will modulate all the vehicles together to prevent overloads. This will lead to a
decrease in the flexibility potential but is a more feasible application in the short term. The
basics of this scenario are explained in subsection 4.7.3.
• V2G: In subsection 4.7.2, the feasibility and advantages of vehicle-to-grid technology in
the workplace charging context will be explored.
To achieve these goals, the previously explained algorithms will be defined and tested under dif-
ferent scenarios on a chosen charging cluster. The algorithm will be run in computer simulations
to extract conclusions on the feasibility of the above services in a public parking lot.

4.2.1
The power time series
As explained in section 3.2, the algorithms are based on charging sessions divided in times-
tamps, so the previously defined data set needs to be converted into a time series of the deliv-
ered power in each EVSE outlet. To achieve this, the following has to be considered:
1. Set the resolution of the time series: the size of the time intervals is very important be-
cause a higher resolution results in more samples per session and therefore a higher
computational burden per algorithm execution. However, a smaller resolution reduces
the reliability and precision of the outcomes. As a consequence, a balance between ac-
curacy and computational power must be found. For this project, a resolution of 15 min
was decided as optimal [27].
2. Establish the time along the specified range: so the index of the time series will consist of
15 min timestamps between the 5th September 2022 and the 20th August 2023.
3. The power in the data set must be converted according to the new sampling. The energy
delivered in each charging session is distributed along the timestamps with a maximum
power of 11 kW as mentioned before. Since we are assuming uncontrolled charging as
the baseline, it is assumed that the power delivery is maximized until the required energy
is fulfilled in each session, so the charging will take place at the beginning, leaving some
room for optimization until the vehicle is finally disconnected.
NOTE: In the first timestamp and in some of the last ones (when the vehicle is still charging
when disconnected, which means the battery is not filled) a conversion of the power needs
to be applied, so that the power delivered along a less than 15-minute span is translated
into less power along the whole span.
4. One time series per outlet. There can be more than one vehicle connected at once so, to
differentiate among connected vehicles, twelve time series need to be created.
4.2.2
The algorithm dictionary
To run the algorithm properly, one more data conversion needs to take place. The algorithm
works with vectors and matrices and does not read dates; therefore, the different charging
sessions need to be ”packed” in a certain format that allows the algorithm to read the different
parameters.
First, the timestamps were converted into relative indices, i.e., 05/09/2022 00:00 becomes 0,
05/09/2022 00:15 becomes 1, etc. This way, the script knows the order of the sessions and is
capable of dealing with them individually.
Second, the recorded sessions were encapsulated in 12 data frames, one per charger outlet.
Inside those sets of data, each session represented a row that contained the following informa-
tion:
• Relative Connection Time (RCT)
• Relative Disconnection Time (RDT)
• energy delivered in kWh
All these data frames were merged in a Python dictionary that, with the specified 3 variables,
contains most of the needed information regarding the charging sessions for the algorithm to
optimize the charge.

4.3
Considerations and hypotheses
Due to the lack of transparency offered by the EV manufacturer, some key information is missing
and therefore some hypotheses need to be defined. The missing information and the consider-
ations made in this regard are listed.
1. There is no SOC available. The state of charge gives information on the amount of
energy to be charged and the urgency to charge, e.g., a vehicle with 5% SOC would
have priority to receive power over another with 30% if SC is applied. The initial level
of the battery is also important in V2G because both the upper and lower limits must be
respected, i.e., not surpass 100% SOC neither discharge below 0%.
As a consequence, the total energy delivered throughout the entire session must be equal
to that of the uncontrolled data set. As boundaries, it is determined that at no timestamp
the energy delivered can be higher than the total energy needed for the session, or can
lower below zero; that is, the EV cannot be discharged from the initial level nor charged
over the final level.
With this approach, a feasible outcome is ensured even though the SOC is not known.
2. There is no power information. The lack of knowledge regarding the delivered power
is another issue. In the initial data, we have information about the total energy delivered,
but we lack the power profile through which this energy has been delivered. This is crucial
because the vehicle’s BMS typically demands different power levels based on the state
of charge. After all, especially around the maximum and minimum SOC limits, power
magnitude affects the battery’s health and thus its lifetime. However, manufacturers do
not disclose how this BMS operates due to intense competition with other brands.
Hence, the decision is made to adopt the simplest approach: taking the energy delivered
and dividing it by the charging time. This way, a square wave is considered even though
in reality, the power is modulated by the BMS and smoother power up and downs would
take place. This especially makes a difference in some cases where we encounter profiles
that charge and stop due to unknown BMS behavior. For the reader to have a better
understanding of what is meant here, the real power input and the considered are drawn
in Figure 4.4.

4.4
Electricity price
In the specified time frame, which spans from September 5, 2022, to August 20, 2023, hourly
spot prices for energy were obtained from Energinet’s data repository [28]. The data, comprising
timestamps and associated energy costs measured in Danish krones (DKK) per MWh, is a
critical factor in determining the cost dynamics for charging EVs.
However, it is essential to underscore that EV users will not directly bear the spot prices for
their charging needs. Instead, a distinct tariff, established by DTU Campus service, governs
the charging costs. This tariff adheres to a specific formula: The charging price is set as the
maximum value between 2 DKK/kWh and the spot price plus an additional 1.35 DKK/kWh,
as shown in Equation 4.3. This tariff includes the DSO fees from using the electric network
infrastructure, so the campus tariff is to be applied directly over the spot market price.
DTU price = max(2, spot price + 1.35)[DKK/kWh]
(4.3)

4.5
CO2 emissions
For the emission-reduction oriented smart charging, it is necessary to know the equivalent CO2
emissions during the studied period. Hence, carbon dioxide emissions associated with energy
production were procured from Energinet’s recordings [29]. This dataset is measured in equiv-
alent grams of CO2 per kilowatt-hour (gCO2/kWh) with a timestamp resolution of 5 min, so an
average conversion to 15-minute timestamps was applied.
Even though EVs themselves are less polluting than internal combustion engine cars (even
though this is a highly debated topic [30]), the energy used to charge them often comes from
fuel oils or other non-green sources, which makes the whole e-mobility industry ineffective.
So, with the increasing adoption of EVs, it is mandatory to apply carbon reduction charging
techniques or the effects of e-mobility will be counterproductive.
The CO2 emission time series serves as a foundational input for the optimization algorithm
when oriented towards emission reduction. The integration of this data enables the algorithm
to prioritize charging during periods characterized by lower CO2 emissions, thus contributing to
the overarching goal of environmentally conscious and sustainable EV utilization.

4.6
Algorithm base configuration
Given the conditions and assumptions defined in this chapter, the following parameters can be
set as general configuration for the algorithm. These parameters do not have to be changed in
most of the different scenarios defined in section 4.7.
The parameters to be set are:
1. The boundaries between which the power can fluctuate, this is, the top and bottom limits
of the EVSE’s fuse, i.e., 0 and 11 kW. These boundaries must be modified during the V2G
examination, as in this case, the charger is set to be able to inject power into the grid. This
fact makes the power vary from -11 to 11 kW.
2. The power-to-energy conversion factor aP→E depends on the resolution of the time series,
which was established as 15 minutes, which makes aP→E = 15/60 = 0.25 for all cases.
3. In the centralized algorithm, there is a parameter that is common to every execution as
well, which is the parking’s fuse capability. However, this parameter will be subject to
study in future sections so as of now, no fixed value will be established.
The rest of the parameters depend on each charging session as the sizes of matrices and
vectors depend on the duration of the sessions or connected vehicles, so their value will be
dynamic, i.e., change in every algorithm execution.

Economic cost optimization
From the user perspective, the motivation that comes to mind when talking about smart charging
is the economic incentive: reducing the cost of charging. That leads to the first scenario, in
which, considering first the spot price and then the DTU Campus tariff, the costs associated with
charging the EV at DTU will be compared before and after applying the SC algorithm defined
in section 3.2.
The script will receive the dictionary defined in subsection 4.2.2 as input together with either
the spot or Campus price depending on the case. During its execution, it will scroll through the
dictionary, receiving an RCT, a RDT, and energy to be delivered for each charging session.
This, along with the electricity prices between the received relative times, will make the script

4.7.2
V2G Optimisation
As previously mentioned, in this scenario the idea is to apply the vehicle-to-grid functionality
in the public parking lot, which means that the vehicles will have the capability to inject power
back into the grid. It is worth noting that this is a theoretical experiment to study the possibilities
26
“Quantifying the flexibility potential of electric vehicles smart charging”
==PAGE:41==
that the V2G mechanism can offer in a public environment, as the vast majority of vehicles and
chargers currently do not provide V2G capability.
V2G’s viability remains in doubt to this day. Examples of Vehicle-to-Home (V2H) applications in
household settings or neighborhoods have been demonstrated [31]. However, some business
models around V2G have already been defined [32]. So, to keep it a simple approach and show
the optimal scenario from the technical perspective, the following considerations were made:
• No dependency between the efficiency and the input/output power is considered, even
though it is known that efficiency drops at low power levels. The losses are assumed to
be fixed at 10%, both for input and output. This does not impact previous V1G applica-
tions because charging data readings from the charger’s perspective are used, where the
energy conversion has not happened yet. However, when it comes to extracting energy
from the battery, not only the energy going available from the charging instants is less
than the theoretical one, but also more energy is needed when we discharge the battery.
• Normally, the energy would come in at DTU Campus price and is assumed to be sold
back at spot price. In addition, network fees must be factored in. Should the user pay
these fees again or be reimbursed? Ultimately, the user is reusing the infrastructure but
contributing clean energy to the system, providing flexibility to the grid and relieving the
burden on transmission lines when used in a local context. Due to these and many more
complexities, the decision is made to assume that, when V2G is applied, the buying and
selling prices are equal. Hence, the incentive for the user lies in charging the vehicle at
low prices and discharging it at high prices to profit from the difference.
• Again, the state of the grid must be taken into account. The mechanism is not as straight-
forward as injecting power into the grid and forgetting about it; grid codes establish that
generators must meet a series of conditions to avoid compromising the stability of the
grid. Additionally, other V2G applications may involve distributing energy within the local
context, such as charging other vehicles in the parking lot, thereby alleviating the load on
the cluster’s fuses.
For this scenario, it is assumed that vehicles inject energy into an infinite and invariable
pool of energy, i.e., grid codes are not considered.
4.7.3
Centralized control
In the previous sections, ideal optimization values will be achieved by considering each vehi-
cle in isolation, as would be done in a household setting. However, these cases are not very
realistic, as real-world applications must account for the limitations of the electrical grid. Since
the price is the same for all vehicles in the parking lot, if multiple vehicles are connected at the
same time, they will all shift their charging to the low-cost energy period, potentially resulting
in transformer overload. Therefore, it was deemed necessary to control the entire cluster. To
achieve this, while respecting the individual needs of each vehicle, a constraint will be added
to the optimization algorithm, setting the power limit determined by the parking lot fuse, as the
individual fuses for each charger are already included in the algorithm’s boundaries.
Algorithm execution
The centralized algorithm introduces a series of complexities, one of which is the algorithm’s
execution timing. Previously, the algorithm ran when a car connected and lasted until the car
disconnected. Now, it is the same, but with the potential for multiple vehicles. The algorithm will
run each time a vehicle connects or disconnects from the EVSEs, as long as the total number
of connected EVs is greater than 0, as running a SC algorithm makes no sense when there are
no vehicles to charge.
“Quantifying the flexibility potential of electric vehicles smart charging”

==PAGE:42==

ergy required by each EV in each execution (NOTE: the required energy is obtained from the
initial scenario data). In each execution of the algorithm, the energy needed by each vehicle
to meet the initial demand is calculated by subtracting the total energy required for the entire
session from the energy charged up to the execution point, as explained in Equation 3.13 from
subsection 3.2.3.

Fuse capability effects on optimization
Since the rating of the fuse of the parking transformer directly binds the number of vehicles
that can be charging at once, as it limits the total power that the EVSE cluster can extract from
the grid, the fuse acts as a bottleneck for the optimization pursued in this study. Therefore,
the effects that fuse size has on SC outcomes will be tested. To make it more graphic, if the
fuse capability decreases, the number of vehicles charging at the same time will decrease as
well, forcing some vehicles to shift their load to not-that-low periods, reducing the economic or
carbon emission savings.

5.1.3
V2G Optimisation
The vehicle-to-grid functionality is more complex than the scenarios presented previously be-
cause the EV can work as a load or as a generator. The latter needs to comply with more strict
grid regulations, and the market was not designed with this kind of actor in mind. To simplify
the case, some considerations were made in subsection 4.7.2.
These considerations can be summarized as follows:
1. The boundaries, or power limits, of the EVSEs are set to -11 and 11 kW as the lower and
upper limits, respectively.
2. A constant Round Trip Efficiency (RTE) of 81%, which is applied because energy is ex-
tracted from the battery.
3. The grid and environment are considered unalterable. This means the effects that the
injected power will have on the network are not considered.
Efficiency cannot be introduced into the algorithm due to the restrictions of this simplified ap-
proach, so it was applied after the optimization was executed, only to the V2G case, by multi-
plying the outgoing power by 0.81. This has to be done in order to avoid breaking the minimum
SOC constraint. Indeed, since 1 kWh of charged energy becomes 0.9 kWh in the battery and,
if that 1 kWh is asked back, the battery will be discharged by 1.1 kWh, so only 0.81 kWh can
be discharged.
The V2G optimization was performed considering both spot prices and campus prices, the first
being more realistic since that is the price at which users will sell the energy (otherwise the grid
would not buy it, as the tariff is set by the CPO to obtain revenue but is not interesting for the
market). Anyway, both price profiles were considered to check which would be more interesting
from the customer’s perspective.
Regarding CO2 emissions, it was ambiguous to establish how the energy injected back into the
grid would affect the CO2 emissions. When the EV is charged, there are equivalent emissions
for that energy input depending on when that happened, but when that energy, or a fraction of
it, is returned to the system, the quantification of the emissions is not straightforward. This can
be explained better with an example:
If the energy is input at, let’s say, 20 gCO2/kWh, and output at 120 gCO2/kWh, the load that
will consume that energy would have an equivalent emission of 20 instead of 120 gCO2/kWh,
reducing the overall emissions of the whole grid, not the cluster specifically. The reduction
magnitude is still unclear as grid size is not comparable to the cluster’s capacity and the input
and output numbers are not that simple in the real practice, as the emissions fluctuate while
charging and discharging.
Given this uncertainty, it was decided that V2G-CO2 optimization would not be tested in this
project as it is a topic yet to be studied more deeply.

5.2.1
Fuse size effects
The fuse limit is the maximum power that can flow through the parking transformer without
tripping. This quantity, even though it is a preset value, is susceptible to variation under certain
circumstances. Under the limit, it will of course work perfectly for the lifetime of the equipment;
but over the limit, the fuse can still conduct electricity for a certain amount of time. This time
depends on the magnitude of the overload, which can be observed in Appendix A.
The current power rating on the cluster fuse is 68.3 kW, which is equivalent to 99 A. Considering
that each EVSE has a maximum power capacity of 11 kW, this fuse allows 6 vehicles to charge
with full power at the same time. This means that:
• A lower fuse limit would reduce the number of EVs that can charge at the same time at
full power, and thus the optimization would give worse results.
• A higher fuse limit would increases the ability to leverage price or CO2 valleys. However,
a larger fuse is more expensive to maintain for the CPO, so the feasibility of this measure
needs to be assessed.
This is why the algorithm was executed with different fuse sizes to see how lower limits in-
crease the algorithm failed attempts 1, while a higher fuse size shows better optimization results.
Whether these improvements are worth the investment is to be judged by the CPO.

Conclusion
After designing the SC algorithm and implementing it in different scenarios in which different
objectives were set, the following conclusions can be extracted:
• The algorithm has offered a simple approach to Smart Charging. This simplicity helped to
establish an overview of the flexibility potential of a public charger cluster but also entailed
some limitations that hindered a more realistic development of the project. However, the
purpose of the conducted study was fulfilled.
• In the algorithm design phase, two different linear algorithms were designed:
– The decentralized SC algorithm, designed for independent charging sessions, pri-
oritizes a seamless user experience, maintains equal energy delivery, manages the
battery limits, and modulates power depending on input conditions.
– The centralized algorithm establishes a control approach from the cluster’s fuse per-
spective, respecting physical limits and covering the same requirements a the de-
centralized one.
• These algorithms were executed in the charger cluster from the case study, which con-
sisted of 12 EVSE outlets of 11 kW located in a workplace in Lyngby. The scripts ran
using historical charging data from the same location and were tested under certain sce-
narios. This was meant to assess the convenience of smart charging according to the
goals sought.
• In the charging cost optimization scenario, the best results were obtained using spot prices
to optimize, as the higher price volatility enables a higher degree of optimization which
gives a 14.65% cost reduction during the studied period. However, the most realistic situa-
tion would include campus prices. Under this scenario, due to an increased price stiffness,
the possible optimization is compromised in favor of the CPO’s economic margins, giv-
ing a cost reduction of 4.60%. Also, as the equivalent CO2 emissions are correlated with
price, despite trying to achieve a cost reduction, 5.06% less emissions are accomplished.
• In the carbon emission reduction scenario, the decentralized algorithm was executed to
achieve a 12.46% emission reduction. Similar to the previous point, this also led to a
reduction of 2.5% in costs for the EV drivers.
• In the V2G section, a very simple model was analyzed and, due to the discharging effi-
ciency and price structure, the results were not encouraging. This was due to:
1. The unavailability of efficiency inside the algorithm prevented the correct assessment
of the viability of selling energy based on available energy and output power.
2. The project’s linear algorithm, chosen for simplicity, faces limitations in handling non-
linear complexities, such as the distinct charging and discharging behaviors of EVs.
3. The structure of the V2G market lacks clarity, leading to an unclear business model
and financial deficits. Questions about the treatment of DSO tariffs during discharging
impact user motivation.
4. Insufficient information about key EV elements, like battery capacity and SOC, ham-
pers decision-making regarding charging, discharging, and power usage based on
the state of charge.

• The last scenario covered again carbon reduction but now from the whole cluster’s per-

vehicle conditions. The simulation was a success as very similar results to the decen-
tralized CO2 scenarios were accomplished but in this case, respecting the fuse power

In summary, the study aimed to assess the effectiveness of Smart Charging in a public park-
ing scenario, with specific objectives in mind. Firstly, the investigation confirmed that smart
charging can indeed lead to substantial reductions in both charging costs for users and equiva-
lent CO2 emissions. Secondly, after comparing the centralized and decentralized approaches, it
could be observed that, despite setting a power cap in the parking, the optimization results were
very similar, making the centralized control a more convenient and feasible strategy. Lastly, the
analysis of V2G scenarios indicated the need for further exploration, as the preliminary findings
suggested that, despite a simplistic model, V2G did not outperform V1G by a sizeable value.
This underscores the complexity and potential nuances associated with integrating V2G sys-
tems for enhanced economic and environmental benefits.
6.1
Possible improvements & future research
As possible future study lines or improvements following this thesis, some possibilities are re-
marked:
• In the initial phase of the master thesis, there was the intention of conducting a practi-
cal demonstration to exemplify the flexibility potential of EVs in a real scenario using the
designed algorithm with some modifications. However, the planned real-world demon-
stration could not be executed as originally anticipated due to time constraints and logis-
tical challenges. This adjustment in the research approach was necessitated by practical
considerations and did not diminish the rigor or scope of the subsequent analytical and
theoretical aspects of the study. Anyhow, as part of the European project FLOW [33], this
demonstration will take place in the future but cannot be part of this thesis due to time
limitations.
• Some points that would help obtain better results
– Improved information inputs: Obtaining more comprehensive data, such as SOC,
battery capacity, and BMS details, would enhance the accuracy and realism of the
results. This additional information could better capture the intricacies of electric
vehicle behavior and contribute to more nuanced algorithmic decisions.
– Integration of predictive analytics: Incorporating predictive analytics, such as ma-
chine learning algorithms, could enhance the algorithm’s ability to anticipate and
adapt to dynamic user behaviors and grid conditions. This could lead to more proac-
tive and responsive Smart Charging strategies.
• Enhancing the V2G model by transitioning from a simple approach to a more sophisti-
cated quadratically-constrained algorithm will lead to significant improvements. The intro-
duction of distinct conditions for charge and discharge power offers an improved control
mechanism, allowing for greater flexibility and responsiveness to varying operational re-
quirements.
• Improving the business model for Vehicle-to-Grid V2G integration necessitates the de-
velopment of a well-structured economic framework. Incentivizing users to adopt V2G
techniques requires careful consideration of various factors, including grid usage taxes,
42

pricing strategies for buying and selling energy, and overall economic viability. Crafting a
correct economic model involves striking a balance between offering attractive incentives
for users to participate in V2G interactions while ensuring the sustainable operation of
the electric grid. Decisions on pricing dynamics play a pivotal role in creating a business
model that encourages widespread adoption of V2G technologies.
• Again with V2G solutions, incorporating reactive power (Q) injection into vehicle-to-grid
operations holds significant potential for enhancing grid services. While traditional mod-
els primarily focus on active power exchange, introducing Q injection allows electric ve-
hicles to contribute to voltage regulation and power factor correction, thereby improving
the overall stability and efficiency of the electrical grid. This expanded functionality aligns
with the evolving needs of modern power systems, showcasing the potential for V2G to
offer comprehensive solutions beyond mere energy exchange.
• The charging cluster can offer more SC possibilities such as microgrids with minor re-
newable resources and Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS), V2H, and Vehicle-to-
Vehicle (V2V) communication within the Smart Charging framework. This approach would
provide a holistic exploration of electric vehicles’ interaction with energy grids, residential
energy management, and collaborative charging strategies, offering a better understand-
ing of the multifaceted applications of Smart Charging technologies.
