Abstact 
The importance of the CD8+ T-cell response is increasingly recognized for its role in 
alleviating infection, providing protective immunity, and determining the severity of 
SARS-CoV-2 infection. Differences in memory, exhaustion, and functional phenotypes 
are thought to impact the quality of the CD8+ T-cell response, leading to varying levels 
of disease severity. However, the factors driving the differentiation of functionality in 
mild versus severe patients remain unknown. To investigate this, endogenous TCRs in 
primary human T-cells were replaced with TCRs targeting immunodominant SARS-CoV-
2 epitopes from two different donors. By characterizing and comparing TCRs derived 
from patients with severe and mild symptoms in identical genetic backgrounds, we 
aimed to understand the role of the TCR in inducing different CD8+ T-cell responses. 
Two separate experiments were conducted to assess functionality and exhaustion 
during chronic antigen stimulation. We successfully generated three transgenic TCR T-
cell lines from two different donors: one with a TCR derived from a severe COVID-19 
patient and two from mild cases. All cell lines were specific to the HLA-A01:01 
restricted SARS-CoV-2 peptide TTDPSFLGRY, and their ability to become activated was 
validated. Distinct memory phenotypes were observed, although several results cast 
doubt on the characteristics of these subsets. Comparisons between the severe and 
mild transgenic cell lines revealed a general trend of higher levels of activation and 
effector functions in the severe cell lines compared to the mild. This could indicate that 
the TCR is a driver of dysfunctional exhaustion and excessive cytokine production. An 
attempt was made to develop a killing assay to assess the cytotoxicity of the transgenic 
T-cells targeting the TAP-deficient BM36.1 cell line, but this was unsuccessful.

1 Introduction 
The emergence of severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) at the 
end of 2019 led to a worldwide pandemic. The rapid spread of the disease overwhelmed 
healthcare systems, triggered global lockdowns, and had extensive negative economic 
impacts [1]. To date, more than 775 million cases have been confirmed, with an 
estimated 7 million deaths worldwide [2]. Additionally, the long-term effects of severe 
COVID-19, the disease caused by SARS-CoV-2, remain an active area of research [3]. 
COVID-19 can manifest as a broad range of symptoms and most commonly causes 
mild symptoms similar to the common cold [4]. In a minority of patients, the disease 
can progress to pneumonia and multi-organ failure [4,5]. A combination of risk factors, 
including age, sex, immunodeficiencies, and genetic disposition, likely influences the 
clinical outcome of the disease [5,6]. However, cases of life-threatening infection have 
also been observed in patients with no apparent health-related risk factors [7]. 
Therefore, there is a present need to improve our understanding of what causes 
variance in the clinical outcome of SARS-CoV-2 infections.  
In the past five years, several studies have suggested that CD8+ T-cells are important in 
determining the outcome of infection and contributing to the protective immune 
response against SARS-CoV-2 [7–10]. Our group recently identified immunodominant 
CD8+ T-cell antigens from patients with severe and mild symptoms of COVID-19, 
alongside the cognate T-cell receptor (TCR) repertoire. Several immunodominant 
epitopes were found to be shared between mild and severe patients. We and others 
have suggested that CD8+ T-cells targeting SARS-CoV-2 epitopes exhibit inferior 
functionality in severe patients compared to those with mild symptoms [10,11]. 
Additionally, phenotypical differences in terms of memory, activation, and exhaustion 
have also been identified.  It is currently largely unknown what drives this difference in 
phenotype and functionality.  
The TCR serves as a fingerprint of the T-cell and determines its specificity, functionality, 
and fate. It is therefore of interest to investigate how SARS-CoV-2-epitope-specific TCRs 
might shape and define the functionality of cytotoxic T-cells.  
The overall aim of this project is to investigate the functional differences of TCRs 
isolated from SARS-CoV-2-specific CD8+ T cells in individuals with severe and mild 
COVID-19. By inserting various TCRs into the same genetic background, we seek to 
elucidate the influence of SARS-CoV-2-epitope-specific TCRs on the clinical outcome 
of COVID-19 infection. The following sections provide background and motivation for 
the experiments conducted in this project. They describe the development, activation, 
and effector functions of cytotoxic T-cells, outline the current state-of-the-art 
knowledge about T-cell line engineering, and examine the impact of CD8+ T-cells on the 
clinical outcomes of COVID-19.

1.1 The immune system 
The mammalian immune system is a very efficient and complex system for defending 
against microbial and viral infection. The system can generally be divided into two 
sections, the innate and the adaptive immune system [12].  
The innate immune system is the body's first line of defense against pathogens, 
providing an immediate, non-specific response. It is comprised of various cell types, 
including macrophages, neutrophils, natural killer cells, and dendritic cells [13]. This 
system quickly recognizes and eliminates invaders, preventing the spread of infection. 
The innate immune system is also essential in recruiting and organizing the whole 
immune response through the production of a wide variety of cytokines that assist in 
activation, differentiation, and recruitment [14].  
However, when the innate system fails to contain a pathogen, the adaptive immune 
system is required to resolve the infection [15]. Unlike the innate immune system, the 
adaptive system functions through receptors that are highly selective toward specific 
antigens. In addition to this, the adaptive system has an immunological memory that 
can quickly act on pathogens previously encountered. It is mainly B and T cells that 
mediate the adaptive immune response. B-cells can recognize antigens through B-cell 
receptors (BCR) expressed on their surface [16]. Additionally, when B-cells encounter 
antigens, they can become activated and become plasma cells, which secrete 
antibodies. Both BCR and antibodies can recognize practically an infinite number of 
antigens. The secreted antibodies can specifically bind and neutralize pathogens by 
recruiting various effector functions [17].  
T-cells acts primarily through the TCR, which, unlike the BCR and antibodies, can only 
recognize peptides derived from foreign antigens. These peptides must be associated 
with the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I or class II molecule, which are 
presented on so-called antigen-presenting cells (APC) [18]. The human MHC is also 
called the human leukocyte antigen (HLA).  The most important APC are the dendritic 
cells, B-cells and macrophages [19]. Most T-cells can also be divided into two 
subpopulations: CD4+ and CD8+ T-cells, which recognize antigens presented on MHC 
class II and MHC class I, respectively [18]. CD4+ T-cells, also known as helper T-cells, 
play a pivotal role in orchestrating the immune response by activating and directing 
other immune cells through the release of cytokines [17]. In contrast, CD8+ T-cells, or 
cytotoxic T-cells, are responsible for directly attacking and destroying virus-infected 
cells, cancer cells, and other damaged cells [20].  
This work primarily focuses on CD8+ T-cells. The following sections will detail antigen 
presentation and their development, activation, differentiation, and effector functions.

1.1.2 MHC class I presentation and recognition  
For both self and nonself peptides to be presented to CD8+ T-cells, the peptide must 
first be processed through the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway [21]. Proteins are tagged 
for degradation by the attachment of ubiquitin to free primary amine groups. Following 
this, the targeted protein is transported to the proteasome, which is found in both the 
cytosol and nucleus [17]. The proteasome is a multimeric protein complex with a 
cylindrical shape with a catalytic and regulatory domain. The so-called catalytic core 
called the 20S proteasome, consists of four stacked rings that each contain enzymes 
with different cleavage specificities which includes cleavage after acid residues, 
cleavage after basic residues and cleavage after hydrophobic residues. Regulatory S19 
protein complexes can bind to each end of the 20S proteasome, facilitating the binding, 
deubiquitination, and transfer of proteins into the catalytic core. During infection, 
stimulation with type I and II IFNs can result in an alternative 20S catalytic core being 
formed. This proteasome, called an immunoproteasome, has a weaker ability to cleave 
peptides after acidic residues but a stronger ability to cleave after basic and 
hydrophobic residues. The binding cleft in MHC class I molecules can accommodate 
peptides with hydrophobic C-terminal anchors and as such the immunoproteasome 
can provide more and different peptides [22]. Additionally, the immunoproteasome is 
involved with processing transcription factors of the NF-κB family which is involved with 
activating proinflammatory pathways [23]. For many antigens, the peptide processing 
can be fully compensated by the standard proteasome [24].   
Once the ubiquitinated proteins reach the proteasome, the polyubiquitin chain is 
removed, and the proteins are cleaved into peptides ranging from 2 to 25 residues, 
which are released into the cytosol (see Figure 1.1). As mentioned above, the length and 
residue content can vary depending on the type of proteasome. Following this, peptides 
are transported from the cytosol into the ER by the transporter associated with antigen 
processing (TAP). This protein complex consists of two proteins, TAP1 and TAP2, which 
preferentially transport peptides of a certain length (8-12 residues) with hydrophobic or 
basic C terminals [21,25]. It is possible to transport longer peptides but with much less 
efficiency. These peptides can then be further processed in the ER or returned back to 
the cytosol. Inside the ER, peptides can be loaded onto MHC class I molecules. This 
process is assisted by four chaperones: calnexin, ERp57, calreticulin, and tapasin. 
Together with the MHC class I molecule and TAP, these proteins form the MHC class I 
loading complex [26–28]. When the MHC class I heavy chain is transported to the ER, it 
acquires a specific glycosylation pattern that serves as a recognition motif for calnexin 
and calreticulin. Once bound by these chaperones, the MHC class I heavy chain is 
folded with the assistance of ERp57, which allows for the binding of β2-microglobulin. 
This triggers the release of calnexin, which gives the MHC class I heavy chain/β2-
microglobulin dimer an open conformation that is stabilized by calreticulin and TAP. At 
this point, peptides with low binding affinity will continuously be replaced with higher 
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
affinity peptides. This process is called peptide editing and will continue until a peptide 
binds with sufficient strength to change the conformation of the MHC class I, which 
releases the chaperone proteins. The pMHC is then recognized by glucosidase, which 
deglucosylates the molecule and prevents further interaction with calreticulin. The 
peptide-MHC class I (pMHC) can then leave the ER through the Golgi apparatus and go 
to the cell surface.

1.1.3 T-cell development 
T cells are derived from hematopoietic stem cells in the bone marrow. However, the T-
cell progenitors migrate to the thymus via the blood, undergoing further differentiation 
and proliferation. The T-cell progenitors, often termed double-negative due to the lack of 
CD4 and CD8 markers, will initially lack most cell surface receptors that characterize 
mature T-cells [17]. A small subpopulation of the double-negative cells will contain the 
δγ T-cells and invariant natural killer T cells, but these will not be discussed further here. 
Once in the thymus, T-cell precursors undergo proliferation and a specific program of 
transcriptional and gene rearrangement, committing them to becoming T-cells. T-cells 
can express an incredibly large number of unique antigen-binding receptors, with a 
theoretical 10¹⁸ unique combinations being possible [17]. This is made possible by a 
mechanism that allows for the rearrangement and controlled modification of the genes 
encoding the TCR. T cell receptors (TCR) consist of two protein chains: The T-cell 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
receptor α (TCRα) chain and T-cell receptor β (TCRβ) chain. Both the α and β chains 
contain a variable (V) domain and a constant (C) domain. Each chain has a cysteine 
residue that forms a disulfide bridge between them. A transmembrane region anchors 
the TCR to the membrane. Similar to the Fab domain in antibodies, the variable regions 
of both chains fold together to form antigen-binding sites.  
The expression of the TCR is organized into two loci, TCRα and TCRβ. The TCRα locus 
contains genes for the Vα and Jα segments, while the TCRβ locus contains Dβ, Vβ, and 
Jβ gene segments. The human TCRα locus contains 70-80 Vα gene segments and 61 Jα 
gene segments[17], while the TCRβ locus contains 52 Vβ gene segments, two D 
fragments, and seven Jβ fragments. An overview of how the V, D, and J fragments are 
rearranged is provided in Figure 1.2. In brief, during somatic recombination in T-cell 
development in the thymus, single V, J, C, and D fragments are recombined to form a 
single mature TCR transcript. The TCR is assembled through so-called V(D)J 
recombination, where site-specific recombination occurs through a process mediated 
by the recombinase RAG1 and RAG2, in addition to the continuously expressed DNA 
repair machinery [29].   
The initial step in the maturation process is the rearrangement of the β chain of the T-
cell receptor. At this stage, the β chain will pair with a temporary α chain to form a pre-
TCR molecule. This complex will constitutively provide an activating signal without 
antigen stimulation. This signal will halt the rearrangement of the TCR β locus and 
initiate several rounds of proliferation followed by expression of CD4 and CD8. 
Following this, rearrangement of the TCR α locus and subsequent selection of a 
functional α chain will begin. Unlike the β locus, the rearrangement will occur 
continuously on both chromosomes. This significantly increases the chance that a T-
cell will pass the subsequent selection process. Cells that have successfully expressed 
both an α and β chain move on to positive selection within the thymus cortex. Here, 
self-antigens are presented on epithelial cells on both MHC class I and class II 
molecules. Double-positive T-cells that cannot bind self-pMHC within a few days will 
die. Positive selection ensures that only those T-cells with receptors capable of 
interacting with pMHC molecules survive and mature. 
At this stage, the phenotype of T-cells is determined as either CD4- or CD8-expressing 
cells while losing expression of the other. T-cells subsequently undergo negative 
selection when they encounter macrophages and dendritic cells expressing MHC class 
I and II with self-antigens. T-cells with a high affinity towards these self-derived peptides 
are killed by apoptosis.

During positive and negative selection, the vast majority of cells die within the thymus. 
However, those that pass both selections become mature CD4 or CD8 T-cells that are 
released into the blood circulation. At this stage, the cells will not have encountered 
their cognate antigens and are referred to as naive. This phenotype is very limited in its 
functionality. The naive T cells will eventually enter circulation and make their way to 
secondary lymphoid tissue.  
1.1.4 CD8 T-cell activation and effector functions 
For a naïve T-cell to become activated, it must interact with its cognate antigen 
presented on an MHC molecule through its TCR, and this is commonly referred to as 
signal 1. However, the TCR alone cannot function as a fully operational cell surface 
receptor without the co-expression of CD3. The CD3 complex is composed of six units: 
CD3γ, CD3δ, two copies of CD3ε, and two ζ chains. The three CD3 chains have large 
extracellular domains, whereas the ζ chains have small extracellular domains [17]. All 
proteins in the CD3 complex contain intracellular immunoreceptor tyrosine-based 
activation motifs (ITAMs), with one ITAM in each of the CD3 chains and three in each ζ 
chain. When pMHC binds directly to the TCR, it induces a conformational change in the 
TCR complex. This leads to the phosphorylation of the intracellular ITAM domains, 
initiating a signaling cascade that provides activating signals [30]. Additionally, the 
binding of the TCR to the MHC is assisted by the CD8 co-receptor which binds a stable 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
region of MHC class I that is separate from where the TCR binds. This allows for the 
formation of a three-part complex (TCR–pMHCI–CD8) [31].

An additional two signals are required for T-cell activation (see Figure 1.3): signal 2 and 
signal 3. Signal 2 involves the co-stimulation of CD28, which binds to CD80 (B7.1) or 
CD86 (B7.2) on the surface of antigen-presenting cells. CD28 thus serves as a way to 
amplify the signal from weak TCR binding while also providing protection against 
unwanted (anti-self) antigens. Signal 3 is provided by inflammatory cytokines, primarily 
IL-2, IL-7, IL15 and IL-15 and IFNα/β. Continues antigen stimulation can also induce a 
state of exhaustion in CD8+ T-cells, which results in impaired effector functions. 
Exhausted CD8+ T-cells exhibit an upregulation of inhibitory receptors such as PD-1, 
LAG3, CD39 and TIM-3 [32,33]. 
Killing of target cells by activated CD8+ T-cells can occur via several mechanisms, 
resulting in the induction of apoptosis in the target cells. The primary method of killing 
occurs via a calcium-dependent release of lytic granules. These granules are stored 
inside isolated lipid bilayer compartments in an inactive form, but when released from 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
the cell, they become active [17]. The process of releasing the granules involves the 
fusion of the storage compartments with the cytoplasmic membrane of the T-cell. This 
results in proteins associated with the lipid bilayer surrounding the lytic granules being 
expressed on the surface, such as CD107a. Thus, CD107a is an indirect indicator of 
degranulation [34]. Two main classes of cytotoxic proteins are released during this 
process: perforin, which forms pores in the target membrane, and granzymes, which 
trigger programmed cell death. Cells undergoing apoptosis due to the cytotoxic 
granules are quickly recognized and digested by phagocytic cells, in addition to direct 
killing mediated by perforin and granzymes. CD8+ T-cells help eliminate infection by 
releasing the cytokines interferon-gamma (IFN-γ), tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), 
and tumor necrosis factor-beta (TNF-β). Interferon-gamma can directly inhibit viral 
replication while upregulating the expression of MHC class I in nearby cells, thereby 
increasing the likelihood of detecting infected cells. TNFα and TNFβ also inhibits virus 
replication and can aid in triggering apoptosis. TNFα secretion is also partly responsible 
for the bystander effect where distal cells bearing TNF receptors is induced to undergo 
apoptosis [35].

During the initial priming, CD8+ T-cells differentiate into several subsets, each with 
distinct production of cytokines. The most prevalent subtype is the cytotoxic CD8+ T-
cell (Tc1), which primarily functions to release perforin, granzyme β, IFNγ, and TNF, as 
described above. Several other subsets with reduced cytotoxicity such as Tc2, Tc9 and 
Tc17 produce a wide array of cytokines similar to those of CD4 T-cells. These less 
cytotoxic subsets are important for inducing various cytokine-mediated effector 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
functions and immune regulation. The differentiation is primarily driven by the presence 
of specific cytokines. Cytotoxic CD8+ can be differentiated in the presence of IL-12. 
Following antigen clearance and resolution of inflammation, the majority of the 
activated CD8+ T-cells will undergo apoptosis. However, a small portion will persist in 
the body as memory T-cells. These cells provide long-lasting immunity as they can 
rapidly differentiate upon re-encountering their cognate antigen [36].  An overview of 
various surface markers used to distinguish the different memory subtypes is given in 
Figure 1.4. Memory T-cells are categorized into three distinct subtypes based on their 
phenotypical markers and effector functions: central memory T (TCM) cells, effector 
memory T (TEM) cells, and TEMRA cells. TCM cells facilitate reactive memory and 
express the chemokine receptor CD197 (CCR7), aiding their movement to secondary 
lymphoid organs. Though they have minimal effector function, they can quickly 
proliferate and differentiate into effector cells upon antigenic stimulation. TEM cells, on 
the other hand, provide protective memory, migrate to inflamed peripheral tissues, and 
exhibit rapid effector functions [37]. TEMRA cells are terminally differentiated cells that 
carry a higher level of cytotoxic and exhausted genes compared to TEM and TCM cells 
[38] 
1.2 SARS-CoV-2 
SARS-CoV-2 belongs to the large Coronaviridae family, named for the Spike(S) protein's 
resemblance to a halo or corona [39]. This family of viruses is commonly found in many 
mammalian species, including humans, cattle, cats, ferrets and bats[40] Specifically, 
SARS-CoV-2 belongs to the β-genus which also includes Middle East Respiratory 
Syndrome-Coronavirus (MERS-CoV), HKU1, and SARS-CoV-1. As such, SARS-CoV-2 has 
a high sequence similarity to these viruses, with the highest being with SARS-CoV-1[41]. 
There have been many cases of inter-species transmission of coronaviruses between 
mammals. It is currently believed that both SARS-CoV-1 and SARS-CoV-2 jumped from 
bats to humans either directly or through intermediate hosts such as pangolins or other 
animal species [42]. Like most RNA viruses, coronaviruses can evolve rapidly, with their 
evolution happening on a timescale of months[43]. SARS-CoV-2, first discovered in 
December 2019, spread rapidly due to a lack of immunity in the population. It took eight 
months from the emergence of SARS-Cov-2 before the first divergent lineages were 
identified. The virus continued to mutate, leading to the emergence of new variants 
classified as variants of concern (VOC) by the WHO.  The Alpha variant appeared in the 
UK in December 2020, followed by the Beta variant in South Africa and the Gamma 
variant in Japan. The Delta variant emerged in December 2020, eventually becoming 
dominant. By November 2021, the Omicron variant had outcompeted Delta [44]. As of 
May 2024, subvariants JN.1 and JN.1 of the Omicron variants have the highest 
prevalence in the United States and Europe [45,46] 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
Coronaviruses are spherical in shape, with a diameter ranging from 80 to 120 nm. They 
are covered with "spikes" composed of S protein trimers, which are essential for the 
virus's entry into its host [46]. The S protein contains two functional subunits, S1 and S2, 
which are necessary for binding to target receptors on the host's surface and for the 
fusion of the viral and host membranes, respectively. SARS-CoV-2 binds specifically to 
the ACE2 receptor. ACE2 is mainly found in the lung, intestine, heart, and kidney cells 
[47]. Inside the virus particle, a single-stranded RNA genome is structured in a helical 
manner by a nucleocapsid (N) protein. During viral replication, the N protein is localized 
to the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and Golgi apparatus and is critical for the assembly 
of new virus particles. It has also been demonstrated that N protein is important for 
regulating replication and transcription by promoting the association of the RNA with 
the nsp7-nsp8-nsp12 complex, which is thought to initiate replication and transcription 
[48] 
The virus envelope is made up of a lipid bilayer containing mainly membrane (M) protein 
and a smaller amount of envelope (E) protein. During infection, the M protein is the 
most abundant viral protein and is crucial for virus assembly and membrane budding 
[49]. It helps maintain the viral envelope and determines the shape and size of the viral 
capsid by interacting with other structural proteins. Its interaction with the S protein is 
important for retaining the S protein in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and Golgi 
apparatus and incorporating it into new virions. Furthermore, the binding of the M 
protein with the N protein stabilizes the nucleocapsid and the inner core of the virion, 
which is essential for completing virus assembly [46]. The E protein, which ranges from 
8-12 kDa, is a small integral protein anchored in the membrane by its transmembrane 
domain. The E protein assembles into homopentamers that function as ion channels, 
influencing virion release and playing a crucial role in cell infection. Notably, the E 
protein is overproduced in infected cells, with only a fraction incorporated into the virion 
envelope. At the same time, the majority is found at the sites where viral particles 
assemble and bud [50]. 
The SARS-CoV-2 genome consists of a single-stranded positive-sense RNA. Similar to 
mRNA in eukaryotic cells, it is capped at the 5' and 3' ends, which enables immediate 
translation inside the host. The genome ranges from 26 to 32 kilobases and encodes 
sixteen non-structural proteins (nsp1-16), four structural proteins (S, M, N, and E), and 
eleven accessory proteins, including ORF3a, ORF3b, ORF3c, ORF3d, ORF6, ORF7a, 
ORF7b, ORF8, ORF9b, ORF9c, and ORF10 [46,49].  
Once the virus particle releases its content into the host cells, the translation of non-
structural proteins begins immediately. This results in the formation of two large 
polyproteins that can be further processed into individual proteins, which make up the 
viral replication and transcription complex (RTC). After the expression of nsps, double-
membrane vesicles (DMVs) are formed. These are specialized organelles characterized 
by two lipid bilayers surrounding a central lumen. The DMVs are produced from the ER

membrane, mediated by the non-structural proteins nsp3, nsp4, and nsp6 [51]. The RTC 
is assembled inside the DMVs and facilitates the replication and transcription of part of 
the viral genome. These organelles are important because they enable the virus to 
evade the cell's natural immune defenses and gather all the necessary factors for viral 
replication in one location [52]. Following this, the RTC will convert the positive-sense 
viral RNA into its complementary form to subsequently synthesize more positive-sense 
RNA. During this process, subgenomic RNAs will also be formed through discontinuous 
transcription and serve as the template for the structural and accessory proteins. The 
genomic RNA will bind with the N protein to form nucleocapsid, which together with M, 
E and S protein will form a new virion. The matured virus particle is released from the 
cell through a process called exocytosis. The virus can then continue to spread the 
infection throughout the body [46].  
As previously stated, SARS-CoV 2 binds to the ACE2 receptor, which is expressed not 
only in humans but also in a wide range of mammals, likely accounting for the wide 
range of potential hosts. In addition to the ACE2 receptor, proteolytic processing of the 
S protein by host proteases is also required before the virus can enter a host cell. The 
proteases transmembrane protease serine protease 2 (TMPRSS2), cathepsin L, and 
furin have all been shown to be involved with this [52,53]. TMPRSS2, in particular, is 
highly expressed in the nose and lungs, which could explain why SARS-Cov 2 prefers 
infecting tissue at these sites.  
Infection with SARS-CoV-2 can result in a wide range of symptoms. At the beginning of 
infection the virus will bind to the epithelial cells in the respiratory tract before migrating 
further into the lungs. The replication at this point can occur very rapidly and result in a 
strong immune response resulting in a cytokine storm. A cytokine storm is the immune 
system's excessive and uncontrolled release of pro-inflammatory cytokines. This 
hyperactive immune response can lead to widespread inflammation, respiratory 
distress syndrome, and respiratory failure, which is the main cause of death in Covid-19 
patients [42].  
1.2.1 Role of the CD8+ T-cell response in SARS-CoV-2 infection 
Throughout the COVID-19 pandemic, the majority of research focused on the humoral 
response, i.e., the viral clearance and protective immunity provided by antibodies. 
However, since then several studies have highlighted the important function of CD8+ T-
cells in fighting SARS-CoV-2 infection and determining the severity of the disease [54–
56]. Equally importantly, the T-cell response is critical in its role in providing protective 
immunity against future infections [55,57].  
Several clinical reports have shown that a state lymphopenia is associated with 
moderate and severe COVID-19, where the T-cell count is considerably lower than that 
of mild and severe patients [58,59]. It has been shown that patients with asymptomatic 
or mild cases of COVID-19 fail to generate a robust B-cell response but instead develop 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
a strong and varied CD8+ T-cell response without inducing systemic inflammation. 
Conversely, patients with severe symptoms generate a strong B-cell response but a less 
pronounced CD8+ T-cell response [60–62]. It has been speculated that the low B-cell 
response observed in mild and asymptotic patients is due to a fast and effective 
clearance of infected cells by CD8+ T-cell in the early stages of engaging the adaptive 
immune system. This results in an inferior generation of SARS-CoV-2 specific B-cell 
germinal centers, which rely on the presence of antigen for a longer period of time [63].  
Several studies have also reported reduced functionality in CD8+ T-cells. Specifically, 
only 8-12% of SARS-CoV-2-specific CD8+ T-cells in patients with severe COVID-19 were 
able to express the cytolytic molecules granzyme B and perforin, compared to about 50% 
in patients with mild cases [63].  A low number of IFNγ, TNFα, CD107a and IL-4 
expressing CD8+ T-cells have also been correlated with severe disease [64,65]. 
Variation in functionality is further cooperated by mild patients exhibiting a greater 
frequency of activated phenotype markers, including CD38+, CD39+, HLA-DR+, Ki-67+, 
and CD69+ [10,65] 
Interestingly, the exhaustion state of SARS-CoV-2-reactive CD8+ T cells has also been 
shown to play an important role in the severity of the disease [66]. Severely ill patients 
display impaired exhaustion features in their CD8+ T cells, characterized by high levels 
of inhibitory receptors and reduced effector functions. This impaired exhaustion is 
associated with persistent type I interferon signaling and a lack of CD4+ T cell help, 
leading to increased expression of cytotoxic and pro-inflammatory genes. In contrast, 
patients with mild COVID-19 exhibit a higher proportion of exhausted CD8+ T cells, 
which may help mitigate tissue damage and reduce disease severity. The functional 
defects observed in exhausted CD8+ T cells from severe cases include impaired 
proliferation and anti-apoptotic signaling, contributing to lower numbers of SARS-CoV-
specific memory CD8+ T cells in circulation. This suggests that the exhaustion state of 
CD8+ T cells is a double-edged sword thatmay protect against excessive inflammation 
in mild cases, but its impairment in severe cases leads to heightened inflammation and 
tissue damage [66] .  
Recent research have also highlighted the importance of the TCR repertoire as a 
biomarker for disease severity. Normally, TCR repertoires are diverse and contain many 
different TCRs, but during an infection, they can become more focused because certain 
TCR clones that target the pathogen are selectively proliferated. A recent study analyzed 
14 million TCR sequences from COVID-19 patients and specific TCR clusters linked to 
protective immunity were identified. These clusters, found in recovered individuals, 
correlate with successful immune responses, suggesting that a diverse and highly 
clonal TCR repertoire is vital for effective immunity and recovery from COVID-19. 
Monitoring TCR repertoires could, therefore, serve as a valuable biomarker for 
predicting disease severity and informing therapeutic strategies. It has been 
demonstrated that a machine learning approach using TCR beta repertoire data could 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
accurately classify recovered COVID-19 patients with a high degree of accuracy, further 
supporting the use of TCR repertoire analysis in assessing immune responses antigen-
specific T cells are instrumental in the control of SARS-CoV-2 infection  
It is currently unknown what drives the variance of phenotype and functionality of CD8+ 
T-cells described above, and this is one of the questions we seek to answer in this work.   
1.3 T-cell line engineering 
The precise alteration of a cell's genome is an invaluable tool with diverse applications, 
from researching cellular functions to redesigning entire biosynthetic pathways. Over 
the past fifty years, various genome editing techniques have been developed, each with 
increasing efficiency and specificity. While genomic engineering originally relied on 
homologous recombination, which is spontaneous and occurs at low frequency in 
undesired locations[67], zinc finger nucleases (ZFNs) and transcription activator-like 
effector nucleases (TALENs) were developed to introduce double-stranded DNA breaks 
at specific sites. However, both ZFNs and TALENs require extensive cloning and protein 
engineering [68]. 
 
Figure 1.5 Mechanisms of DNA repair following CRISPR-Cas9 induced double-strand breaks. Non-homologous end 
joining (left) disrupts the gene of interest through nucleotide deletions or additions. Homology-directed repair (right) 
corrects the gene using donor DNA. Figure is from [69] 
Clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR)/Cas9 is the newest 
addition to the genome engineering toolbox and has revolutionized research in a wide 
range of fields, including immunology. CRISPR/Cas9 was originally discovered as a 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
bacterial immune system that allowed for the recognition and cleavage of foreign DNA 
[70]. The introduction of the double-stranded break in the DNA is mediated by the Cas9 
enzyme, which is guided to the target site by a guide RNA. The guide RNA consists of 
two parts: the trans-activating CRISPR RNA (tracrRNA) and CRISPR RNA (crRNA). The 
CRISPR RNA binds to the target site, while the tracrRNA binds together the Cas9 and 
crRNA. The tracrRNA and crRNA are most commonly combined to create a single gRNA 
(sgRNA), which can more easily be transferred to the cell's nucleus [71]. sgRNA bound 
to Cas9 will also recognize and bind to a genomic DNA sequence upstream of a 
protospacer adjacent motif (PAM). Thus, the genomic locations that can be targeted by 
CRISPR/Cas9 are limited by the presence of a PAM sequence nearby the target site. 
Different Cas9 proteins have been developed that recognize different PAM sites, which 
have helped overcome this limitation [71,72]. 
Once a genomic break has been introduced, the cell's own DNA repair machinery will 
attempt to repair it. This can occur using one of two pathways: nonhomologous end 
joining (NHEJ) or homology-directed repair (HDR), see Figure 1.5. NHEJ is an error-prone 
method that will result in frameshift mutations through the deletion or insertion of 
random nucleotides [70,73]. This pathway is utilized to create gene knockouts of 
targeted genes. HDR uses DNA sequences homologous to sequences found on the 
sister chromatid as a template to direct the repair. However, if an exogenous repair 
template is introduced together with the CRISPR/Cas9 protein, integration at the target 
site can occur. HDR-mediated repair is therefore more commonly used to facilitate 
knock-in (KI) of genes. An overview of the HDR and NHEJ pathways is given in Figure 1.6.

The use of the HDR pathway is complicated by the fact that it is only active during the S 
and G2 phases of the cell cycle, in contrast to NHEJ, which is active throughout the 
entire cell cycle [74]. CRISPR/Cas9 allows scientists to study the role of specific genes 
in a much more high-throughput manner compared to genetically modified animal 
models [72]. CRISPR/Cas9 can also be used to replace endogenous TCRs, which is 
notably being used for the construction of CAR T-cells, having enormous therapeutic 
potential. 
When studying antigen-specific T-cells, a major challenge is obtaining enough cells for 
use in in vitro assays. Additionally, some T-cell clones might be present in donor 
material at a very low frequency. CRISPR/Cas9-mediated insertion can help overcome 
this challenge by manufacturing the desired T-cell clones. This can be done by replacing 
the endogenous TCR in primary cells or by using an immortalized cell line. A 
comparison between traditional in vitro T-cell research and transgenic T-cell lines in 
primary and immortalized cell lines is summarized in Figure 1.6. 
2 Aim and objectives 
The CD8+ T-cell response is critical in resolving SARS-CoV-2 infection. Several studies 
have highlighted the vital role of CD8+ T-cells in combating SARS-CoV-2 and influencing 
disease severity. Patients with mild COVID-19 develop a robust CD8+ T-cell response, 
while severe cases exhibit reduced functionality. 
This project investigates the factors driving the differences in functionality and 
phenotype between T-cell clones from patients with severe and mild COVID-19. 
Specifically, we aim to shed light on the role of the TCR in inducing different functional 
avidities. The objectives are as follows: 
• Generate transgenic T-cell lines expressing TCRs from T-cell clones of severe and mild 
COVID-19 patients. 
• Validate the functionality of these transgenic T-cell lines and assess their value as a 
model for SARS-CoV-2 epitope-specific T-cell clones. 
• Characterize and compare the phenotype and functionality of the transgenic T-cell 
lines during repeated antigen stimulation

3.6 Production of fluorescently labeled pMHC tetramer 
MHC class I monomers were produced using published methods. In brief, the MHC 
class I heavy chain and β2m were both expressed as inclusion bodies in Escherichia 
coli using the pET28 expression system. The proteins were refolded and stabilized using 
UV-sensitive peptide ligands. The correctly folded pMHC molecules were biotinylated 
using the BirA biotin ligase kit (Avidity LLC, Aurora, CO) and purified using size exclusion 
chromatography (FPLC, Waters Corporation, USA) and stored at -80°C.  pMHC 
monomers were assembled by UV-mediated ligand exchange: 100µg/ml pMHC with 
photolabile peptide was mixed with 200µM peptide of interest in PBS (8 g/L of NaCl, 
1.44 g/L of Na2HPO4, 0.2 g/L of KCl, and 0.24 g/L of KH2PO4, pH 7.4) and incubated at 
355nm UV light for one hour. pMHC multimers were subsequently assembled by 
combining biotinylated pMHC with 0.092µl 0.2 mg/ml PE-conjugated streptavidin (BD 
Biosciences, Cat# 405204) for every 1 µl of pMHC followed by incubation for 30 minutes 
at 4 °C. To block unbound sites on the streptavidin, D-biotin (Sigma-Aldrich) was added 
to a final concentration of 25µM. A 10x tetramer freezing buffer (5% BSA, 50% glycerol in 
water ) were added prior to storage at -20 °C. Immediately before use, tetramers were 
centrifuged at 12000g for 5 minutes to remove precipitated protein.

4 Results and Discussion 
4.1 Design and validation of HDR templates for endogenous TCR 
replacement in primary human T-cells 
To investigate the functional differences between TCRs identified in antigen-specific 
CD8+ T-cells from mild and severe COVID-19 patients, several transgenic T-cell lines 
were created. TCRs of interest were selected based on previous work in our group, 
where single-cell sequencing determined the TCR repertoire in immunodominant 
antigen-specific T-cells. Five TCRs were selected from five different patients: three from 
patients with severe symptoms requiring hospitalization and two from patients with 
mild symptoms that did not require hospitalization. The patient ID is used to refer to the 
various TCRs and subsequent cell lines in the following sections (see Table 4.1). The 
TCRs AP-29, AP-42, AP-43, and AP-75 are all specific to the HLA-A01:01 restricted SARS-
CoV-2 epitope TTDPSFLGRY (TTD), while AP-70 is specific to the HLA-B07:02 restricted 
SPRWFYYL (SPR).

CRISPR/Cas9 mediated HDR was used to replace the endogenous TCRs in primary 
human T-cells. The sgRNA was designed to create double-stranded breaks within the 
TCRα loci, specifically inside the Cα gene fragment, which enables insertion of the HDR 
template. For every TCR, the corresponding Jα, Vα, Jβ, Dβ, and Vβ segments were 
combined, as illustrated in Figure 4.1B. Every HDR template was flanked by left and 
right homology arms which complements the cleavage site. Additionally, to ensure 
correct separation and bicistronic expression of both TCR chains, a P2A and T2A site 
was inserted before the TCRα and TCRβ chain, respectively. These are genetic elements 
that cause ribosomal "skipping," which results in two separately translated proteins 
from a single mRNA transcript. When genes are linked using P2A, approximately 18 
amino acids remain at the C-terminus of the first translated protein, and a single proline 
residue is left at the N-terminus of the second protein [75]. It is currently unknown what 
effect this has on the functionality of the TCR. As such, a furin cleavage site was 
inserted immediately upstream of the P2A site, which has been shown to remove 
residual residues [76].

It is unclear why the AP-70 and AP-75 TCRs could not be expressed. Interestingly, all cell 
lines subjected to nucleofection, including AP-70 and AP-75, show a significant 
increase in the CD3-negative population compared to the no-KI control. During the 
CRISPR/Cas9-mediated integration, double-stranded DNA breaks are generated until 
an insertion successfully occurs or the cleavage site is mutated such that the sgRNA 
can no longer recognize it. Additionally, the decrease in CD3+ T-cells suggests that 
background expression of endogenous antigen-specific TCRs is unlikely in the 
transgenic cell lines due to the disruption of the TCRα loci. It should be highlighted that 
the TCRβ loci are not affected by the CRISPR/Cas9-mediated insertion. Therefore, 
mispairing of transgenic TCRα and endogenous TCRβ is possible, but the presence of 
such TCRs is not assessed in this work. 
In summary, we successfully generated three transgenic T-cell lines from two different 
donors, all specific to the TTD epitope. The frequency of tetramer-positive T-cells in 
these transgenic lines is significantly higher than what would typically be observed in 
unmodified primary cells. These results suggest that our approach of endogenous TCR 
replacement is an effective method for generating a large number of antigen-specific T-
cells for in vitro assays. As we were unable to generate a TCR cell line targeting SPR, we 
were limited to a single epitope in the subsequent experiments.

for a period of 8 hours together with the protein transport inhibitor brefeldin A. 
Intracellular staining of the activation makers IFNγ, TNFα and CD107a was 
subsequently performed.

4.4 Comparison of PBMC-derived APCs and TAP deficient BM36.1 for 
T-cell activation 
The initial intracellular staining of activation markers IFNγ, TNFα, and CD107a revealed 
a significant discrepancy between the tetramer frequency and the double-positive 
IFNγ+ and TNFα+ population (Figures 4.2 and 4.4), which is presumed to indicate true 
activation. During the intracellular staining experiment described in Section 3.1, there 
was some uncertainty regarding the actual tetramer frequency since tetramer staining 
was not performed in parallel with intracellular staining. However, we speculated that 
the low activation level was a result of using A01:01-positive PBMCs as our model for 
antigen presentation. The use of PBMCs has the additional disadvantage of contributing 
a background of antigen-specific CD8+ T-cells. Therefore, we wanted to explore using a 
different in vitro antigen presentation model. It has previously been described that 
different antigen presentation models can result in differing views of T-cell avidity [84].

One such candidate is the EBV-transformed lymphoma cell line BM36.1 (A01:01), which 
is deficient in the enzyme TAP, essential for transporting peptides from the cytosol to 
the ER [85]. Due to the loss of function of this enzyme, very few endogenous peptides 
are presented on the surface, and empty MHC dimers are transported to the surface. 
Culturing the cells with exogenous peptides makes it possible to generate stable pMHC 
on the surface with the peptide of interest. We wanted to explore the viability of using 
this cell line for subsequent activation and killing assays. 
To assess this, intracellular staining was performed using different ratios of effector to 
target cells with peptide-pulsed BM36.1 and PBMCs (donor 3). These cells were used to 
stimulate the transgenic T-cell line from donor 1, AP43. The results, shown in Figure 4.6, 
revealed a significantly higher activation level in the cells interacting with BM36.1. 
Figure 4.6C compares the frequency of tetramer-positive CD8+ T-cells with that of the 
cytokine-secreting T-cells (IFNγ+, TNFα+). This comparison shows that activation 
induced by BM36.1 is significantly closer to the tetramer frequency. Additionally, the 
ratio of effector cells to target cells greatly influenced activation using PBMCs, with only 
half the frequency of activated cells observed when decreasing the ratio to 1:1. In 
contrast, when decreasing the effector-to-target ratio from 1:3 to 1:1 for BM36.1, no 
significant decrease was observed. A similar trend was seen with the granulation 
marker CD107a. Thus, BM36.1 was evaluated to be a significantly better model for 
antigen presentation for use in in vitro activation and was used in subsequent 
experiments.

breakthrough cases of the disease are still being observed [45]. In this landscape,

activation [90]. High levels of surface expression of HLA-DR and CD27-expressing cells 
were observed in all transgenic cell lines after both the second and third activations. No 
differences in HLA-DR expression were observed between the severe and mild TCRs, 
although AP-29 exhibited the lowest average expression of CD27, indicating an inferior 
activation level. Overall, our results indicate that the transgenic T-cell lines can undergo 
massive proliferation upon antigen stimulation, similar to what occurs during an acute 
viral infection [91]. However, repeated antigen stimulation eventually leads to a sharp 
decrease in the number of antigen-specific cells.  
To investigate the development of exhaustive phenotypical markers further, we 
conducted an additional repeated antigen stimulation experiment (Figure 4.8A). This 
experiment was only carried out with transgenic cell lines from donor 2. In addition to 
HLA-DR, CD27 and PD-1 the antibody panel also included the exhaustive markers Lag-3 
and Tim-3. Antigen stimulation was provided to the cells using peptide-pulsed BM36.1, 
and the cells were sampled 24 hours later. This assay was combined with multiplex 
quantification of cytokine and chemokine secretion and intracellular staining of IFN-γ, 
TNF-α, and CD107a (see section 4.6). Due to the observed decline in the antigen-
specific CD8 T-cell population in the previous experiment, the assay was limited to 
eight days, with a total of three consecutive stimulations.

4.6 Functional comparison of SARS-CoV-2 specific CD8 T-cells 
To characterize the functionality of the transgenic SARS-CoV-2 specific CD8 T-cells 
during repeated antigen stimulation, we assessed their ability to secrete various 
cytokines and chemokines using a multiplex immunoassay. The analysis was carried 
out on supernatant harvested 24 hours after antigen stimulation, as described in Figure 
4.8. Additionally, the production of IFNγ, TNFα, and CD107a was assessed with an 
intracellular staining assay. The assay was carried out using peptide-pulsed BM36.1, 
which was added to cells sampled before any antigen stimulation had occurred and 
after the third activation. 
Figure 4.11A summarizes the non-normalized percentage of double-positive IFNγ and 
TNFα CD8+ T-cell population. The cells sampled before antigen stimulation were 
observed to be induced to secrete IFNγ and TNFα, while no expression was observed in 
the DMSO control. Due to variance in the antigen-specific population between the 
different cell lines, direct comparison is difficult. However, the data does allow for a 
comparison of cytokine expression after being subjected to repeated antigen 
stimulation. It is observed that for all the transgenic cell lines cytokine expression was 
decreased following the third activation, in accordance with the increase in exhaustion 
markers in Figure 4.7 an 4.9. Interestingly, AP-29 exhibited the greatest capacity for 
cytokine expression after the third activation. A significant decrease was observed for 
AP-42, while a smaller decrease was observed for AP-43. This may indicate a 
dysfunctional exhaustion program induced by AP-29, compared to the milder cell lines 
AP-42 and AP-43. Given that inducing a state of exhaustion is important to prevent 
excessive host tissue damage, the failure to establish a proper exhaustion program may 
contribute to disease severity. 
It has been reported that patients with severe disease exhibit a large discrepancy 
between the number of cytokine-expressing CD8 T-cells and the tetramer-positive 
population compared to patients with mild disease [10]. To investigate if a similar trend 
occurs in transgenic cell lines, we plotted the double-positive IFN+, TNFα population 
from intracellular staining performed before repeated antigen stimulation against the 
frequency of tetramer-positive cells (see Figure 4.11B). However, the opposite trend was 
observed, with mild cell lines displaying the most significant difference between 
cytokine-secreting and tetramer-positive cells. Notably, the frequency of tetramer-
positive cells was lower than that of the cytokine-secreting population. This suggests 
some background expression of cytokines, which was also evident in the no KI control 
(supplemental Figure 4). These results contradict the results observed in section 4.2, 
where AP-29 had the lowest cytokine-producing capacity.

The notion that AP-29 exhibits a stronger cytokine-expressing capacity is further 
supported by the findings in the multiplex immunoassay (Figure 11C). The data is

measured before activation was used for this normalization as it is assumed that the

frequency will not change significantly during the 24-hour incubation. From these 
results, it can be seen that AP-29 has a stronger expression of IFNγ compared to the 
mild cell lines AP-42 and AP-43, both before and after repeated antigen stimulation. 
Expression of the inflammatory cytokines IL-6 and TNF-α during viral activation were 
surprisingly not observed, despite being some of the main antiviral cytokines secreted 
during infection [93,94].  
High levels of GM-CSF expression were observed in all the cell lines, with the highest 
being observed for AP-29 both at the first and third activation. GM-CSF plays an 
important role in inflammation, and its expression is highly upregulated by CD4+ and 
CD8+ T-cells following activation. The cytokine serves several important functions 
during viral infection, although the precise molecular mechanism underlying this 
function is not fully understood. It is speculated that GM-CSF may act through 
mechanisms similar to those of other pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-1β, TNF-α, 
and IL-6, potentially involving signaling molecules shared with type I and type III 
interferon pathways. Although the expression of CM-CSF is beneficial to the host when 
fighting infection, it can also be detrimental during chronic inflammation [95].  
The level of IL-3, produced mainly by activated T-cells, has been demonstrated to be a 
predictive marker for the severity and outcome of SARS-CoV-2 infections. IL-3 helps 
with increasing innate antiviral immunity by recruiting dendritic cells. Low levels of IL-3 
levels are associated with increased severity and viral load during infection [96]. From 
our experiment, the levels of IL-3 are observed to decrease following repeated antigen 
stimulation. The highest expression after the first activation is observed in the mild cell 
line AP-42 while high levels are also observed for AP-29. Following the third activation 
highest levels was observed for AP-29. 
The concentration of several chemokines was also determined for this assay. The 
importance of chemokines in COVID-19 has been demonstrated in several studies 
where over-expression of the genes encoding CCL2, CXCL10, CCL3, and CCL4 and 
observed for severe patients [97,98]. The chemokine CCL4 was observed to be highly 
expressed for the mild AP-42 after the first activation but expressed in relatively low 
amounts for AP-29 and AP-42. Following the third activation, the CCL4 was significantly 
upregulated for AP-29 and, to a lesser extent, AP-42. A similar trend was observed for 
the secretion of CCL3, CXCL9 and CXCL10. In other viral infections, CXCL10 was found 
to be elevated in the plasma and bronchial alveolar lavage fluid and is correlated with 
disease severity [99] 
Taken together, these results demonstrate that the functionality of transgenic antigen-
specific cells decreases after repeated antigen stimulation. However, the cytokine and 
chemokine-secreting capacity of AP-29 is greater than that of the mild cell lines AP-42 
and AP-43, both before and after chronic antigen exposure. This indicates that the 
severe cell line AP-29 induces inferior exhaustion programming, which may contribute 
==PAGE:50==
49 
 
to cell tissue damage observed in patients with severe disease, in line with other 
studies. The superior functionality of AP-29 does, however, also contradict several 
studies demonstrating that TTD-specific CD8+ cells exhibit lower functionality in 
patients with severe disease compared to those with mild disease [10,81,92]. This 
suggests that the observed difference is not a result of the TCR but rather a combination 
of other factors, including genetics, transcription factors, antigen concentration, and 
the overall cytokine milieu. 
4.7 Design and optimization of killing assay  
The ability of the transgenic T-cell lines to become activated upon antigen stimulation 
has been thoroughly demonstrated in the previous sections. However, to evaluate the 
cytotoxic capabilities of these transgenic T-cell lines, we aimed to develop and optimize 
an assay for monitoring the killing of TAP-deficient BM36.1 cells by T-cells using the 
Incucyte monitoring system. Although the surface expression of pMHC is transient, it 
could enable us to use a wide range of epitopes in future studies. 
To determine the optimal ratio of antigen-specific effector to target cells, we 
investigated several ratios using AP-43 and the no-KI control from donor 2. This 
consideration is crucial, as only a limited number of cells can be seeded in each well 
without impacting the image quality due to overcrowding. Given that the frequency of 
tetramer-positive cells ranges from 5-10% after cryopreservation, only a limited number 
of antigen-specific cells can be added to each well. BM36.1 cells were pulsed with TTD 
peptide and stained with an RFP proliferation dye (see figure 4.12). Wells only 
containing the target cell was also included. The percentage confluence of the target 
cells was calculated for each image and normalized to the confluence at the start of the 
assay. Due to a limitation of one scan every four hours, the resolution of the experiment 
was limited.

5 Conclusions  
We aimed to investigate the functional and phenotypical differences between SARS-
CoV-2-specific CD8+ T-cells in severe versus mild COVID-19 cases. To achieve this, we 
replaced the endogenous TCR in primary human T-cells with TCRs identified from 
patients experiencing severe and mild disease. 
We successfully integrated three different TTD-specific TCRs into two different donors: 
one from a patient with severe COVID-19 and two from different patients with mild 
COVID-19. The surface expression of the TCRs was confirmed by tetramer staining, 
which revealed varying knock-in efficiencies for each TCR. 
Characterization of the transgenic cell lines' capacity to secrete cytokines upon antigen 
stimulation revealed that all transgenic cell lines responded functionally to antigen 
stimulation in a concentration-dependent manner. Thus, the transgenic T-cell lines 
appear to be a valid approach for the in vitro assessment of SARS-CoV-2-specific clones. 
Distinct memory subtypes (Naïve, TCM, TEM, and TMRA ) were found for all the 
transgenic cell lines with a similar distribution of each subset. However, phenotypical 
markers were identified within the Naïve population, not typically associated with this 
subset. Therefore, there is currently doubt about the true nature of the memory subsets 
for the transgenic cell lines. 
The expression of phenotypical markers of exhaustion during repeated antigen 
stimulation was assessed in two different experiments. Upon repeated antigen 
stimulation, all T-cell lines showed upregulation of activation and exhaustion markers. 
The results did not reveal significant differences between TCRs from severe and mild 
cases in terms of exhaustion or activation. However, cells sampled within 24 hours of 
antigen stimulation revealed a presumed strong downregulation of TCRs expressed on 
the surface of the transgenic cells. The TCR from the patient with severe disease 
exhibited the strongest downregulation, even after three consecutive antigen 
stimulations. This, combined with a higher capacity for the secretion of various 
activation and effector cytokines and chemokines, suggests an inferior exhaustion 
programming induced by the TCR from the severe patient. This could suggest that the 
excessive cytokine secretion and tissue damage are at least partially dependent on the 
specific TCR. However, this hypothesis is not strongly supported by the phenotypical 
markers of exhaustion.
