Abstract
A recently renovated room in the Technical University of Denmark is planned to be used
for a ﬁeld study with the objective to study the performance of suspended ceiling panels
with phase change materials (PCM) for comfort cooling. The panel conceptual idea is
to ﬁll a metal case with PCM. Water can be used to discharge the PCM by circulating
it through copper pipes included in the panel.
In this thesis the panel concept was
further developed by suggesting how it could be incorporated in the mentioned room as
well as an operation and control strategy. Through numerical simulations using TRN-
SYS 17 the concept’s thermal comfort and energy use performance in the cooling season
were evaluated. The PCM ceiling’s performance was compared against an All-air and
a TABS system. Finally, a procedure to perform the ﬁeld test in the room was proposed.
The thermal environment created by the PCM was poorer than expected, 90% of oc-
cupied period was expected to be within prEN-16798 Category II, which corresponds
to a room operative temperature range from 23◦C to 26◦C. Only 73% of occupied time
fell within this range with the PCM system, while 82% and 84% was achieved with
the TABS and all-air systems respectively. The room’s operative temperature was 91%,
96%, and 94% of the occupied period in a range from 22◦C to 27◦with the PCM, TABS,
and all-air systems respectively. Energy savings of 15% compared with the all-air system
were achieved under these thermal comfort conditions, the energy use of the system was
almost the same as the TABS system.
Similarities were observed between the PCM and TABS systems. Similar energy savings
and peak power reductions were achieved when compared to the all-air system. A similar
fraction of the cooling demand was shifted into the nighttime by both systems.
The PCM concept can be potentially used to provide thermal comfort and improve
energy savings during the cooling season in oﬃce buildings and mild climates under the
right operation and design circumstances. Experimental work is needed to conﬁrm the
ﬁndings made in this study but the results initially suggest that PCM ceiling panels
can be used as an alternative to TABS in renovation projects, as similar behavior and
beneﬁts were observed.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Most of the scientiﬁc community agrees that global temperatures will likely increase
between 2 K to 4 K in this century. This increase in global temperatures is likely to
change Earth’s climate in a global scale. There exists some uncertainty on the exact
consequences of climate change especially in speciﬁc location context, but in general, it
is accepted that warmer summers, with more frequent and intense heatwaves are to be
expected (IPCC, 2013).
Global warming is caused by the release of gases which form a dense layer that al-
lows heat to enter Earth’s atmosphere but prevents it from leaving it into space. This
eﬀect is known as the green house eﬀect and is caused by green house gasses. (Crich-
ton et al., 2009).The United States Environmental Protection Agency, EPA (2018b)
states that the key green house gasses emitted by human activity are: Carbon Diox-
ide (CO2),Methane (CH4), Nitrous Oxide (N2O) and Fluorinated gases (F-gasses).
EPA (2018a) also states that carbon dioxide is the largest greenhouse gas emitted glob-
ally and accounts for 75% of global green house gas emissions.
The main source of
carbon dioxide released into the atmosphere is burning fossil fuels, such as oil and coal.
Building energy use varies somewhat from region but in China, the United States and
the European Union buildings account for about 40% of the total energy use. By 2010
ﬁgures, fossil fuel was the source of 50% of the energy used by these buildings. As a
consequence, the built environment is a big contributor to global emissions and climate
change (Cao et al., 2016).
Concerns over climate change and the inﬂuence of the built environment have caused
a big interest in improving building energy eﬃciency and reduction of global warming
emissions. In countries with mild climates, the focus is on preserving heat in the building
by improving the building envelope (Lomas et al., 2017) which increases overheating dur-
ing summer. In mild and cold climates, low winter temperatures have a greater impact
in public health than high summer temperatures. However, rising global temperatures
1
==PAGE:20==

are expected to increase public health negative impacts associated with high summer
conditions, even in mild climates (Lomas et al., 2017).In such climates, currently ther-
mal discomfort associated with overheating in buildings raises larger concerns than more
serious public health issues. As indicated in Alker et al. (2014) there is evidence on the
relationship between the indoor environment and productivity in oﬃce buildings. Up
to 6% decreases in productivity have been reported due to an inadequate thermal envi-
ronment. (Wargocki et al., 2006). In most oﬃce buildings in developed countries, staﬀ
costs can represent 90% of operating costs, so even a 1% improvement in productivity
can yield substantial beneﬁts.
Buildings can be cooled in a number of diﬀerent ways, one interesting relatively new
method of cooling buildings is by activating the thermal mass of multistory buildings by
circulating cold water in their slabs. This method has been deﬁned as ”Thermally Active
Building Systems” or TABS. One of the most important beneﬁts of these systems, is that
they can provide thermal comfort operating with water temperatures close to the desired
room temperatures. This characteristic causes that the systems can use renewable en-
ergy as sources, or increase the eﬃciency of heat pumps, thus, reducing the demand for
fossil fuels and the building’s green house gas emissions. (Olesen, 2012),Kazanci (2016).
A major drawback of TABS is that it has to be incorporated in the building from the
design stage. This makes TABS an unfeasible solution for existing buildings that were
designed with other climatic systems. In this context, a lot of attention has recently
been given into phase change materials or PCM’s. Signiﬁcant research has been done in
the use of these materials as a way to improve thermal comfort in buildings and achieve
energy savings. PCM’s are materials that are engineered to change phase (from solid to
liquid for example). In theory, a lot of thermal energy can be stored or released in the
material at a narrow temperature range as molecular bonds form or break during the
phase change. This means that a similar eﬀect than building with high thermal mass
can be achieved with considerably less material. Also, if this eﬀect is controlled in a
similar way as in a TABS system, the same comfort and environmental beneﬁts that
TABS have could be achieved in buildings which were not designed with this strategy.
1.2
Objectives
The main objective of this thesis is to evaluate the performance of a climatic system
concept, that consists of ceiling panels that contain phase change materials (PCM) for
cooling in renovated oﬃce buildings. A secondary objective is to propose a way of im-
plementing such panels in a ﬁeld study planned to be performed in a real room. The
main research questions that the thesis will try to answer are:
1. Is it possible to use phase change materials (PCM) as part of a suspended ceiling
to provide cooling and achieve acceptable thermal comfort conditions during the
Page 2

cooling season in a mild climate?
2. Can similar beneﬁts as systems with thermally active building structures (TABS)
be obtained in renovated buildings?
The work performed in this thesis can be divided in three parts. The ﬁrst part of the
work consisted of theoretical design on how could the PCM panels be created and how
could these panels be incorporated in a real room. Physical construction of the panels
was also attempted.
The second part of the thesis and the main work performed consists of numerical
simulations to asses the performance of the ceiling tiles and compare the system against
other developed cooling systems. The knowledge which is obtained from this part will
be used to answer the proposed research questions.
The third and last part consists on designing a ﬁeld test using the knowledge gained
from the ﬁrst two parts. The objective for part three is to design a ﬁeld test for the
PCM panels so that it can be performed at a later time.

Chapter 2
Background
2.1
Indoor environment in buildings
The indoor environment in buildings is usually divided into the thermal environment,
indoor air quality (IAQ), daylight and lighting, and the acoustical environment (Alker
et al., 2014). This thesis addresses mainly the thermal environment but a remark is
made that the other aspects of the indoor environment are at least equally important.
2.1.1
Assesment of the thermal environment
It is natural to ask the question what is an acceptable thermal environment and how
to measure it before attempting to develop methods to improve it. Currently the most
popular approaches into the characterization of the thermal environment are the pre-
dicted mean vote (PMV) and the adaptive thermal comfort model.
The PMV, as indicated in ISO (2005) is an index that predicts the mean value of the
votes of a group of people in relation to their overall thermal sensation. The votes are
based on a 7 step scale in which 0 is thermally neutral, -3 is a cold perception and +3
is a warm perception. This index uses environmental, metabolic, and personal clothing
parameters to perform a thermal balance over the human body in which 0 in the 7 step
scale would correspond to thermal equilibrium between the person and the environment.
The previously mentioned parameters that encompass the PMV are:
• Air temperature.
• Mean radiant temperature.
• Relative humidity.
• Air velocity.
• Physical activity, measured in ”MET” (1 MET = 58W/m2).
• Clothing, measured in ”clo” (1 clo = 0.155 W/m2/K).
The PMV is complemented by the predicted percentage of dissatisﬁed (PPD) occu-
pants. This second index, as its name suggests, expresses the amount of people that ﬁnd
4
==PAGE:23==
Chapter 2. Background
the thermal environment uncomfortable. PPD is obtained as a function of the PMV.
Besides overall thermal comfort, the thermal environment is also characterized in
terms of local comfort, which accounts for the eﬀects of:
• Draught.
• Floor surface temperature.
• Radiant temperature asymmetry.

2.2
Thermal environment control in buildings: Cooling
Buildings can be cooled both actively and passively. Examples of passive cooling in
buildings are controlling solar gains through shading, and using natural heat sinks to
dissipate heat such as outside air. Passive cooling techniques can provide good thermal
comfort and excellent energy savings, however, they are limited by the local climatic
conditions. Santamouris (2016). Oﬃce buildings are commonly cooled by active systems.
These systems can be classiﬁed as air-based systems or hydronic systems. Air systems
use air as the heat transfer medium, while hydronic systems use water as the heat transfer
medium used for cooling or heating. A cooling system can also be classiﬁed by the heat
transfer mechanism it uses to remove heat from the room. Air based systems rely on
convection to transfer heat away from the room while hydronic systems use a combination
between radiation and convection. Systems that rely in radiation are usually referred
to as radiant systems. In order for a system to be considered a radiant system, at least
50% of the heat in the room has to be removed by radiation (Ashrae, 2016).
2.2.1
All-air systems
In the context of this thesis, an all-air system should be understood as an HVAC sys-
tem that relies in tempered conditioned air to provide both thermal comfort and air
quality and ventilation in the room (McDowall, 2007). Various authors refer to these
systems as ventilation systems, mechanical ventilation, or air conditioned systems. As
Kazanci (2016) explains, these systems use air as the heat transport medium and and
rely almost entirely in convection as the heat transfer mechanism in the conditioned
space.
2.2.2
Thermally Active Building Systems (TABS)
TABS is a radiant system in which pipes are installed within a thick concrete slab. TABS
can be used both for heating and cooling. In the case of cooling, cold water is circulated
in the pipes to cool the slab, the cold slab will then exchange heat with the room. Figure
2.2.1 shows a schematic example of a TABS construction. (Olesen, 2012).

As Kim et al. (2015) explain, the large area of the ﬂoor and ceiling surfaces makes
possible to achieve the required cooling power for thermal comfort by circulating the
water at relative high temperatures.
This practice is deﬁned by REHVA (2009) as
high temperature cooling.
The high temperature required by the cooling system in-
creases the eﬃciency of heat pumps and enables the possibility to use renewable sources
Kazanci (2016).
An important diﬀerence between TABS and other radiant systems is that TABS have a
large thermal mass. This will cause the room’s operative temperatures to respond slower
to variations in outdoor conditions. This causes that temperatures in the room are con-
trolled over a range instead of at a constant temperature, as in the case of other cooling
systems. The high thermal mass of the system also enables the storage of heat in the
structure, the active cooling of the structure can be performed during the night, a pe-
riod when due to lower air temperatures heat pumps will be more eﬃcient Olesen (2012).
Olesen (2012) explains that TABS contribute indirectly to improving the indoor en-
vironment by reducing draft and improving the indoor air quality.The fact that part of
the cooling demand is handled by the radiant hydronic system allows the ventilation
system to be designed only to provide fresh air in the room. This will result in consider-
ably lower air ﬂow rates, which will reduce the risk of draft in the room and reduce the
noise caused by the ventilation system.
TABS systems have also some limitations.
Unlike all-air systems, TABS cannot re-
move latent loads from the room. The humidity control must be provided by either
natural or mechanical ventilation. In some climates, humidity might limit the cooling
capacity of TABS, due to the risk of condensation if the system operates below the dew
point temperature of the room. Other disadvantage of TABS is that in order to be most
eﬀective the concrete surfaces of the slab should be exposed to the room. This is often
not possible due to acoustic limitations(Kim et al., 2015).

3.1
Thermal Storage
Thermal storage systems have an important role in many energy systems, such as solar
heating systems, systems of waste heat applications and buildings.In buildings, they
are especially important in heating and cooling applications (Zhang et al., 2006). As
Sharma et al. (2009) states, thermal energy storage can improve the performance of
energy systems and are essential to conserve energy. There are two ways thermal energy
can be stored, sensible heat storage and latent heat storage.
3.1.1
Sensible heat storage
In sensible heat storage, thermal energy is stored as sensible heat by changing the tem-
perature of a material. The amount of energy stored can be quantiﬁed with Equation
(3.1.1) (Sharma et al., 2009).
Q = m ∗Cp ∗(Tf −Ti)

Where:

m: Material mass [kg].

Tf: Final temperature of the material [K].
Ti: Initial temperature of the material [K].
3.1.2

In latent heat storage, energy is absorbed or released during the phase change of a
material as it is used to break or form molecular bonds.The energy transfer during this

3.2
Phase change materials
A phase change material is able to change phase at a certain temperature and pressure.
The phase change can be solid-solid, solid-liquid, solid-gas, liquid-gas, and vice versa
(Akeiber et al., 2016). PCM’s work mainly as latent heat storage materials. Figure 3.2.1
illustrates the temperature and heat ﬂux of a liquid-solid PCM as it solidiﬁes due to a
temperature change (∆T).
Figure 3.2.1: Time evolution of the temperature and heat ﬂux during the solidiﬁcation
of a pure body submitted to a temperature step. T Kuznik et al. (2011).
The phase change process can be divided in three stages. During the ﬁrst stage, the
material releases sensible heat (hl) and the temperature of the material starts to drop.
Page 10
==PAGE:29==
Chapter 3. Phase change materials for building applications
At the end of stage 1 the freezing temperature (Tf) is reached and stage two begins
as the material transforms from liquid to solid. During this stage, the latent heat (hf)
stored in the PCM is released at a constant temperature in the material. Stage three
begins once the material is fully solidiﬁed, the temperature in the material decreases and
it continues to release sensible heat (hs) until equilibrium between the material temper-
ature and the ambient temperature is reached (Kuznik et al., 2011). A similar process
occurs when the material melts, the diﬀerence is that the temperature rises and heat is
absorbed instead of released.
The process shown in 3.2.1 represents a material that is able to change its phase
in an isothermal process, PCM’s used in building applications do not change phase in
this manner but rather over a temperature range. This is due to many factors, such as
impurities in the materials and the combination with other non-PCM materials when
integrated into building applications (Kosny, 2015).
Another characteristic of PCM’s used in building applications is that the latent
energy transfer in the material can be diﬀerent depending on the direction of the process.
In other words, the temperature at which the material changes from solid to liquid might
be diﬀerent than the temperature at which the material changes from liquid to solid.
This characteristic is caused by a phenomenon called sub-cooling (or super cooling) in
the PCM (Kosny, 2015). The solidiﬁcation of a PCM starts with the formation of small
crystals in the liquid phase. If the formation and growth rate of these crystals is low
the material can remain in its liquid phase even if the temperature decreases below the
fusion temperature. This will cause the solidiﬁcation to start later and the temperature
of the material will stabilize until this happens(Kuznik et al., 2011).
3.2.1
PCM containment
PCM materials are not used as construction materials, instead they are integrated into
conventional building materials. Kuznik et al. (2011) and Khudhair et al. (2004) list
impregnation, and encapsulation as diﬀerent methods to incorporate PCM in building
materials.
PCM can be directly impregnated into a porous material like concrete or gypsum. The
phase change can occur in the pores. Direct impregnation can cause the PCM to leak out
of the containing porous material during the phase change and the mechanical strength
of the containing material is reduced (Kuznik et al., 2011).
In encapsulation, the PCM is contained in a capsule of a diﬀerent material that acts
as a shell to prevent leakage. The capsule can have diﬀerent shapes and sizes. There are
two main methods of encapsulation, micro-encapsulation and macro-encapsulation. In
micro encapsulation circular or rod shaped capsules in the range from 1 µm to 1 mm are
incorporated in the material. The enclosing material can be used as a heat exchanger
between the PCM and the material but it has to be compatible both with the PCM and
Page 11
==PAGE:30==
Chapter 3. Phase change materials for building applications
the host material. This method also reduces the strength of the host material.
The second encapsulation method is macro-encapsulation, where the PCM is enclosed
in a larger package and incorporated directly in the building material. A disadvantage
observed in this method is that often, the PCM solidiﬁes around the edges and the re-
maining material does not change phase so not all of the latent heat storage potential is
used (Khudhair et al., 2004).
3.2.2
PCM Characteristics
There is a wide variety of PCM materials with diﬀerent characteristics. The selection of
the PCM material and its characteristics depends on the application in which the mate-
rial is going to be used. Kalnaes et al. (2015) discuss brieﬂy the desired characteristics
of PCM’s in building applications:
• A high heat of fusion per unit volume and unit weight as well as a high speciﬁc
heat is desired. This will allow to use less PCM to obtain the desired eﬀects.
• The phase change temperature has to match the application. In building applica-
tions ranges between 10◦C and 30 ◦C are used (Akeiber et al., 2016).
• Low vapor pressure at operative temperature is important to avoid danger of rup-
ture in the encapsulating material.
• Chemical stability and low corrosion rate will enable to extend the lifetime of the
system and lower costs as the risk of the PCM reacting with other materials it is
in contact with is reduced.
• The selected PCM should not be hazardous or toxic, to human beings as well as
to the environment. The PCM should be recyclable and should be ﬁre safe.
• Reproducible crystallization without degradation will also increase the PCM’s life,
as more melting/freezing cycles will be performed without the degradation of the
material.
• A small degree of super cooling and high rate of crystal growth are desired.
• The material should have a small volume change during solidiﬁcation. Large vol-
ume expansion results in more space needed needed for the expansion and results
in a lower heat of fusion per unit volume.
• High thermal conductivity of the PCM will allow it to to absorb or release heat at
higher rate.
• Economic considerations have to be considered as well, the ideal PCM should be
cheap and abundant.
3.2.3
Numerical modeling of phase change materials
Kosny (2015) gives a very complete summary on the overview of modeling phase change
materials. As the author states, the physical phenomena of a material undergoing a phase
change was mathematically described by Slovenian physicist Jozef Stefan with a set of
diﬀerential equations. The model consists on solving the heat and mass balances over a
Page 12
==PAGE:31==
Chapter 3. Phase change materials for building applications
domain that contains a liquid phase and a solid state separated by a moving boundary.
These set of equations are known as the ”Stefan Problem”. Analytic solutions exist to
solve the Stefan problem, however these solutions work only for materials that undergo
phase change isothermaly. For materials that change phase over a temperature range,
which is the case of PCM’s used in building applications, the Stefan problem has been
approximated with numerical methods. The most common methods used in most simu-
lation tools are the so called enthalpy method and heat capacity method. Comparative
studies of the methods showed that the heat capacity method has better accuracy when
compared against measurements. Most algorithms incorporated in commercial modeling
tools do not consider diﬀerences between the direction of the phase change, which can
lead to inaccuracies, particularly with non paraﬃn based materials.
3.2.4
PCM classiﬁcation
There is no single PCM that possesses all of the desired characteristics previously men-
tioned.
Kuznik et al. (2011) classiﬁes PCM’s into organic, inorganic, and eutectic.
Organic PCM are paraﬁns, fatty acids, and polyethylene glycol. Some of the desired
properties of organic PCM’s are that they are available in large range of phase change
temperatures and posses a high heat of fusion. These PCM’s freeze with little super cool-
ing, have the ability to melt congruently and posses self nucleating properties. Organic
PCM’s are compatible with conventional construction materials and do not segregate.
Additionally they are chemically stable, non toxic and recyclable. The use of Organic
PCM’s also has problems in building applications. Their thermal conductivity is low (0.2
W/mK) and they are ﬂammable depending on their containment (Kuznik et al., 2011).
The most common inorganic PCM’s used in building applications are salt hydrates. The
advantages of these materials are that they have a large volumetric latent heat storage,
lower cost compared to organic PCM’s, high thermal conductivity (0.5 W/mK) and they
are non ﬂammable. These materials however, have a large volume change in the phase
change, and show signiﬁcant super cooling and segregation (Kuznik et al., 2011).Eutectic
materials are a mix of two diﬀerent PCM, they can be organic-organic, inorganic-organic,
or inorganic-inorganic combinations(Zhang et al., 2006).
3.2.5
Phase change materials in building applications
Kalnaes et al. (2015) identify the most common applications of PCM’s in buildings. The
ﬁrst application discussed is peak load shifting, where the peak cooling or heating load
is divided during the day leading to a reduction and energy saving. ”Free cooling” is
identiﬁed as another application, the idea is to store the cooling load during the day in
the PCM’s latent heat storage capacity and discharge the energy during the night using
cold night air. A similar concept can be used for heating, in which thermal energy in
the form of solar gain can be stored and then released in periods when there is no sun.
PCM’s can also be used potentially for thermal comfort. The added thermal mass of the
PCM and the fact that the phase change process takes place in a narrow temperature

range can reduce temperature ﬂuctuations and reduce maximum temperature peaks.
This is especially true for light constructions.
3.2.6
Previous studies
Akeiber et al. (2016) made a review of recent studies of application of PCM for passive
cooling in buildings. Full scale testing and numerical modeling were the most popular
methods in the reviewed studies. Most studies focused in macro-encapsulating PCM in
room walls. The most popular PCM type studied in the reviewed articles are organic
paraﬃn materials. The authors suggest as relevant future studies the integration of PCM
with other passive cooling methods such as night ventilation. They also list a combina-
tion of PCM with active conventional cooling systems as ”a potentially important topic
for future studies”. Finally, the authors also suggest cost evaluations and assessment of
PCM in climates with high humidity.
Koschenz et al. (2004) developed a panel with micro-encapsulated paraﬃn PCM for
oﬃce buildings. The panels had a total thickness of 5 cm and 25% of PCM by weight.
Aluminum ﬁns were incorporated in the panel to achieve an overall thermal conductivity
of 1.2 W/mK, water can be circulated in the panel through capillary tubes. A single
panel performance was measured under laboratory conditions and later, a room was
simulated with a numerical model the authors developed and integrated into TRNSYS.
The study found that this panel construction with circulating 15 kg/hm2 of water at
18◦C from 22:00 to 06:00, was suﬃcient to keep the simulated room temperature at a
comfortable range. The authors suggest the pilot testing of the panels in a real building
as a future study.
Weinlader et al. (2016) installed and monitored two diﬀerent PCM cooling ceiling
types in two oﬃce rooms. The ceiling is connected to a water circuit that can be used
both for heating and cooling. The studied panels consist on a layered structure in which
one of the layers is a macro-encapsulated salt hydrate based PCM. The diﬀerence be-
tween the tested panels is the positioning of the water pipes. In one panel the PCM
layer was placed on top of the pipes and in the other the PCM layer was placed below
the pipes. Measurements were carried during several weekends from April to August of
2015. Users were replaced by thermal dummies to eliminate user behavior as a variable.
The study found that the ceiling types produced 8-17 W/m2 passive cooling power,
compared to the 80-100 W/m2 produced by conventional radiant panels measured at
globe temperature of 26◦C. The authors also found that despite the low cooling power,
amount of ceiling covered (less than 50%) and high internal gains (50 W/m2) the highly
insulated rooms took from 7-9 hours to reach temperatures of 27◦C.
Jamil et al. (2016) investigated the use of macro-encapsulated PCM in the ceiling as
a retroﬁtting option to reduce air temperature and improve user comfort for an existing
house in Melbourne Australia. In this study both numerical and experimental methods
Page 14
==PAGE:33==
Chapter 3. Phase change materials for building applications
were used. PCM was installed in the ceiling of a room in the house and air tempera-
tures were recorded. A similar room without PCM was used as a control. A model was
then created in Energy Plus and validated with the recorded data. Results of the study
showed 1.1◦C reduction in indoor temperature and 34% reduction of thermal discomfort
hours in the room with PCM.
Alam et al. (2017) compared the eﬀectiveness of diﬀerent application methods of
PCM in residential buildings, with a combination of numerical and physical experimen-
tation methods. The authors investigated the diﬀerences in air temperatures caused by a
macro encapsulated PCM installed in the ceilings of a residential building in Melbourne,
Australia. Two methods of operation of the PCM were studied, passive cooling and
free cooling. The authors deﬁned free cooling as the use of nigh time air to aid in the
discharge of the PCM, without an air handling unit. The comparison was carried out
through validated numerical models created in ANSYS and Energy Plus. The authors
found that free cooling application of PCM is more eﬀective than passive application.
During the 7 day study period, passive application resulted in 0.4 K reduction of peak
indoor zone temperature, compared with 2.6 K reduction observed with free cooling
application.
Tzivanidis et al. (2012) performed a parametric analysis for space cooling systems
based on cold water ﬂowing during the night in spaced pipes embedded in a PCM layer
in the ceiling slab combined with a night ventilation strategy. The authors made the
analysis with a numerical model solved with a transient three dimensional ﬁnite dif-
ference method.The study found that the main parameters that inﬂuence the system
performance are pipe spacing, PCM layer thickness, pipe depth within the ceiling, cool-
ing water inlet temperature, night cooling duration and PCM properties. The authors
make suggestions on the optimum values of some of these parameters to achieve low
energy use and high thermal comfort.
Nielsen (2016) used IDA ICE to simulate the indoor environment and energy use of
an all-air system, TABS, radiant panels, and PCM ceiling panels during the cooling
season in a room in Copenhagen and Rome. The study found that in Copenhagen, the
all-air system resulted in the lowest energy use. The use of TABS and PCM panels
increased the energy use by 126% and 101% respectively. In Rome, the all-air system
resulted in the highest energy use. In this location, energy savings of 35% and 39% were
achieved with TABS and PCM panels respectively.

Methodology
To achieve the project’s objectives and research questions an existing room was selected
for the study. The room is a close approximation to an oﬃce in it’s use and layout. A
ceiling panel which incorporates PCM and pipes through which water can be circulated
and is being developed in the university was proposed as a novel way to control the
room’s thermal environment. There are plans to conduct a ﬁeld test in this room with
the PCM panels after they are fully developed.
As a ﬁrst step, the required amount of ceiling tiles and the PCM material quantity
were determined, based on calculated cooling loads and on the constraints of the real
boundary conditions in the room and the panels. Next, with the aid of simulation tools,
the PCM panels were simulated under passive operation, which in the context of this
project means that no water was circulated in the panels. The objective of these set of
simulations was to observe if the circulation of water was needed. After this, a para-
metric study was performed to determine an operation strategy that would result in an
optimization between thermal environment and energy use.
The following step was to design the room’s thermal environment with two additional
climatic systems, An all-air system and a thermally active building system (TABS). A
comparison of the performance of the PCM system with these two systems was made,
mainly in terms of thermal comfort and energy use. To perform this comparison, the
room was simulated with the three diﬀerent systems.
Finally, the design of a ﬁeld test study was proposed. The design of the experiment
is proposed as part of this work so that it can be used as input when the ﬁeld test is
carried out in the future.

4.4
Simulation work
4.4.1
Software tools
All numerical simulations were performed using TRNSYS 17. TRNSYS is a simulation
environment suitable for transient simulations of systems, including multi-zone buildings.
(Wisconsin Madison, 2018). The program has a modular structure in which separate
numerical models represented in components are connected to each other to model a
certain system. The components are called ”Types”,followed by a number. Examples of
TRNSYS components can be pumps, solar collectors, multi-zone buildings, and weather
data.
This structure has the advantage of oﬀering a very ﬂexible environment com-
pared to other similar software packages. The software was also chosen because several
researchers have developed components (or ”Types”) of phase change materials.

4.5
System comparison
Three climatic systems were simulated in the room under the same occupancy conditions.
A comparison in terms of indoor environment and energy use was made to evaluate the
performance of the systems. The systems simulated were:
• A mechanical ventilation variable air volume system referred as the ”all-air sys-
tem”.
• A thermally active building structure (TABS) consisting in circulating water in
the building’s slabs.
• Ceiling panels with phase change material and the possibility of circulating water
in the panels (PCM)
4.5.1
Modiﬁcations to the room
The simulated room will not have the same boundary conditions as the actual room.
The room was simulated with a room on top of it and a room below it. The reason
for this decision is that this represents a more common situation of an oﬃce room.
Additionally, the TABS system will be able to perform in more favorable conditions, as
both the ceiling and ﬂoor surfaces will be activated to remove heat and the conclusions
obtained from the comparison against this system will be more general.
4.5.2
Indoor environment
The indoor environment was assessed with the categorization of operative temperature
suggested in prEN-16798 (EN-Standard, 2016). The indoor environment was studied to
determine the performance of each system to deliver comfort conditions. The initial aim
was to achieve 90% of occupied time within Category II of prEN-16798, which meant the
operative temperature had to be kept between 23◦C and 26◦C. The indoor environment
was also assessed to have a reference for the energy use comparison. This is a similar
methodology than the one suggested in the work of Fabrizio et al. (2012), where a com-
parison between convective and radiant climatic systems was made.
Additionally to the thermal environment, the relative humidity in the room was also
assessed. This was done to control the assumptions behind the categories of prEN-16798,
as well as to demonstrate that the humidity is also kept within recommended limits. Rel-
ative humidity should be kept between 30% and 70% according to prEN-16798.

use. Two energy intensive processes were considered, the ﬁrst process is the conditioning

5.2.3
System control
The fan of the system works during the occupied period, from 08:00 to 17:00. It will
provide the minimum ﬂow rate (3.7 ACH) when the room operative temperature is be-
low 23◦C. Based on the signal of a proportional controller based on the room’s operative
temperature, the ﬂow rate will be adjusted to the maximum ﬂow rate (8.5 ACH) when
the room temperature is greater or equal than 26◦C. During the unoccupied hours the
fan is turned oﬀand no climate control is performed. According to prEN-16798, 2 ACH
must be provided during unoccupied hours, however, the standard allows the inﬁltration
rate to be part of this ﬂow. An inﬁltration ﬂow of 0.2 ACH was simulated, and the un-
occupied period is greater than 10 hours, which is enough time to meet the requirement
of the standard.
The heat recovery can be bypassed with base in the logic shown in the diagrams in
Figure 5.2.3.
Basically the air will go through the heat recovery unit if any of the
following conditions are true:
• The outside temperature is lower than 18◦C and the exhaust temperature is larger
than the outside temperature.
• The room operative temperature is lower than 23◦C and the exhaust temperature
is larger than the outside temperature.

6.2
PCM Modelling
To model the PCM in the building, TRNSYS component ”Type 399” developed by
(Dentel et al., 2013) was used and connected with the building component (Type 56).
Type 399 models the phase change material with an enthalpy method, it calculates the
phase change with measured enthalpy data as a function of temperature. The algorithm
incorporated into the component is able to calculate the solid-liquid and the liquid-solid
transitions with their corresponding characteristics therefore, it is able to model the hys-
teresis of the peak melting and freezing temperatures.(Kosny, 2015). Dentel et al. (2013)
mention that the algorithm in the component to model the pipes in the panel is the re-
sult of the work of (Koschenz et al., 2004).
6.2.0.1
Type 399 main assumptions
The PCM panel was modelled as a single layer of PCM in the component. The steel
cover of the Panel was not included in the component. The density of the PCM used
was the average between the density of the liquid and the solid states (825 kg/m3).
The latent enthalpy was set to 15.8 kJ/kg. The melting and freezing curves provided
by the material manufacturer and shown in Figure 4.3.1 were used as inputs for the
enthalpy-temperature functions required as input for Type 399.
6.2.1
Connections between Type 399 and Type 56
The working principle of the connection between Type 399 walls and building Type
56 is shown in Figure 6.2.1. Dentel et al. (2013) suggest to model the elements that
contain PCM as ”a high conductivity dummy wall with known boundary conditions”.
The author’s recommendation for the deﬁnition of a dummy wall is a wall that has high
conductivity and a convective heat transfer coeﬃcient in the back side less than 0.001
kJ/hm2K. The boundary conditions are the surface temperatures calculated in Type 399

Field Test
In this section the preliminary design of a ﬁeld test for the studied room is proposed.
The panel placement, methods, and instruments are proposed, as well as suggestions for
the analysis of the results. This experimental design could be used as the starting point
for the future planned ﬁeld test.
8.1
Objectives
The ﬁeld test has two main objectives.
The ﬁrst objective is to assess the thermal
comfort in the room with the PCM panels. The second objective of the experiment is
to determine the performance of the panels and their overall role in the HVAC of the
room. Data generated from the ﬁeld test can also be used to validate simulations made.
8.2
Proposed set up of PCM panels in room
For practical reasons of installing the water pipes and leading them out of the room,
the setup with 40% of the ceiling covered shown in Figure 8.3.2 is suggested. It is likely
that about 4 kg is the practical PCM quantity that will be incorporated in the panels,
which is equivalent to 1.5 cm of PCM. According to Figure 4.3.2, the PCM could store
the equivalent of 25 W/m2 acting in the room for 9 consecutive hours.
8.3
Thermal Comfort assessment
The thermal environment in the room will be assessed in two categories.
The ﬁrst
category is the overall thermal comfort, which will be determined by measuring the pa-
rameters of the predicted mean vote (PMV). The second category will be speciﬁc comfort
conditions, in which the operative temperature distribution, surface temperatures, tem-
perature asymmetry, and temperature drift will be measured in the room.

capacity is being used.

8.5
Room HVAC measurements
The last set of suggested measurements consist on determining measurements on the
HVAC systems and room parameters that could inﬂuence the heat removal of the room.
The suggested systems to measure are the ventilation system, the hydronic conditions of
the overall PCM circuits, surface temperature imaging, and shading and window control
measurements.
As observed in the simulations, the ventilation system can inﬂuence greatly the heat
balance of the room, especially for high occupant densities. The purpose of this mea-
surement is to know the ﬂow rate and supply and return air temperatures to know the
system’s contribution to the heat balance of the room.
Hydronic measurements in the PCM are suggested to know how much heat is being
removed by the water circulation on the tiles. This can be known by using a ﬂow sensor,
such as a Kamstrup sensor and temperature sensors for the supply and return water of
the room boundary.
An important decision in the ﬁeld test is whether to limit the use of shading and
window opening to the occupants during the measurements. If shading and window
opening are not going to be controlled, the following actions are suggested.
A schedule can be created by logging in the time the window is opened or closed.
A movement sensor can be used to log two diﬀerent values, and the window could be
logged either as open, or closed. An evident problem is that the window has more than
only two positions. In the case of the room’s shading, a camera can be programmed to
take a photograph at certain interval (for example every 3 hours). This could help to
create a schedule that could be used in simulations.
Finally, it is proposed to measure the room’s natural ventilation air change. It may
not be possible to continuously log this value, but the air change can be determined
with tracer gas with windows opened and closed a number of times to get a closer
value to reality that can be used to improve the simulations and to have an idea on the
inﬂuence of the inﬁltration in the thermal balance of the room.

of over cooling, by counting the time below 23◦C and 22◦C.
The system performance could be evaluated by logging key temperature values. The
system behaviour and performance can be studied by plotting two graphs for a period

stored could be approximated with the heat ﬂux measurements in the PCM panels.

The expected result in these simulations was to observe an improvement in the room’s
thermal environment when PCM was added, as this seems to be a common conclusion
found in the reviewed literature. The results shown in Figure 7.1.1 show that adding

the thermal environment of the room than adding PCM only. The percentage of time
outside Category III was reduced from 66% to 22% from Case-1 to Case 3 and only to
64% from Case 1 to Case-2.
It was also expected that the thermal environment would improve by adding PCM
because the overheating hours would decrease, as the latent energy storage is able to
absorb the heat gains. A slight increase in fraction of time below 23◦C was also expected,
because of the overall reduction of inside temperatures, particularly during the colder
weeks of the cooling season. Figure 7.1.2 shows that this is generally the case with one
exception. When PCM is added in the room with ventilation (Case 4 compared with

below 23◦C and 24◦C decreases. This can be seen more clearly in Figure 7.1.3 during
the weeks from September 9 to September 28.There is not enough evidence to fully ex-
plain this, but a reasonable explanation is that the heat gains absorbed by the PCM
during the occupied period are being released during the unoccupied period, and acting
as passive preheat to the room. In the case with ventilation, the heat recovery could be
compensating for the passive heat gain absorption, but in the case without ventilation
this is not possible so the heat being absorbed by the PCM in the room could be causing
thermal discomfort by exceeding the lower temperature limit of the range.
The reason why adding PCM in the room is not aﬀecting the thermal environment
as much as it was expected might be because there is not enough PCM in the room,
however, as shown in chapter 4, theoretically there is enough PCM in the room to ab-

Eﬀect of selected parameters in the discharge of the PCM
The parametric study in a week on July showed that in general, a combination be-

to observe that not all of the studied parameters had the same eﬀect in the results.

Decreasing the temperature of the water supplied in the PCM panels had a signiﬁ-

increased the solidiﬁcation percentage the PCM achieved during the night. Decreasing
the supply temperature from 21◦C to 15◦C caused the PCM to solidify around 15% more
during the night in a 6 hour schedule and 20% more in a 12 hour schedule. In terms of
thermal environment the eﬀect of the temperature varies depending on the conditions of
the other parameters, but observing Figures 7.2.1 to 7.2.4 it can be seen that decreas-

decreases the time outside Category III.
The schedule in which water is circulated in the PCM ceiling also has a signiﬁcant
inﬂuence in the behaviour of the PCM and the consequent thermal environment in the

for 12 hours in the PCM panels in contrast with the 6 hour schedule of TABS.

9.4
Limitations
The results obtained from the simulations should be used with caution if a general con-
clusion is to be made as no experimental measurements were performed to validate the
models. A critical assumption that was made and remains to be tested is the thermal
conductivity increase considered in the panels. The considered value used was the one
that Koschenz et al. (2004) used due to similarities in the panel structure, however,
further work is needed to determine precisely this value.
Perhaps the most critical part of the simulations that needs to be validated against
experimental data is the PCM component used in simulations. Although the compo-
nent was not validated in this study, other authors have studied the component in a
detailed level. As Claros-Marﬁl et al. (2014) mentions, Type 399 was developed based
on previously experimentally validated models. Benoit et al. (2017) explain some of the
limitations of Type 399 with base in a numerical validation they performed. The authors
mention that Type 399 can fail to detect phase change events if large simulation time
steps are used. This is because of the implicit numerical method used in the component,
in which the heat capacity of time step tn+1 is assumed very similar to time step tn, if
the temperature of time step tn corresponds to one phase (for example solid phase) and
the temperature of time step tn+1 to the other phase (liquid phase), then the cumulative
energy in the PCM is miscalculated. The authors observed that under small time steps
of 1 minute, the issue was minimized, in this simulations this is the time step which
was used. Other issue Type 399 has is that it is not able to model super cooling in the
PCM, this could lead to an over estimation of the heat capacity during the solidiﬁcation
process of the PCM. These limitations were minimized by using 1 minute time steps
in the simulations, however, there are no other developed PCM components that are
able to simulate embedded pipes for water circulation in the structure. This situation

highlights the importance of performing a ﬁeld test or full scale experiment to have more
conﬁdence in the results and expand the use of the models.
9.5
Answers to the research questions
9.5.1
Is it possible to use phase change materials (PCM) as part of
a suspended ceiling to provide cooling and achieve acceptable
thermal comfort conditions during the cooling season in a mild
climate?
The answer to this question is not straightforward and is mostly inconclusive after per-
forming the study as there are a lot of variables involved. The simulations performed
represented one PCM material in a speciﬁc room, with speciﬁc loads and a speciﬁc cli-
mate context. The results obtained varied greatly depending on the operation mode of
the PCM. Using the PCM passively proved to be an unsatisfactory method to provide
adequate thermal comfort. Using water in the PCM resulted in much better comfort
conditions, however, these conditions corresponded to the lower comfort (Category III)
temperature ranges of standard prEN-16798. Even with the water circulation, accept-
able comfort conditions for making a comparison against the other systems were reached
by varying the thermal conductivity of the PCM. This might not be the most realistic
representation of the PCM panel in the room. Another possibility is that the PCM op-
eration is not being performed optimally, the focus of the operation mode was made in
basis to enhance the cooling capability of the PCM, the operation and control strategy
used might not be robust enough to consider the variability in outdoor temperatures
or in heating and cooling demand of the room. It is also important to remember that
the thermal comfort categories were used more as a metric to be able to compare the
systems than a prediction tool for comfort. Although it is a good indicator of comfort,
we should not forget the assumptions that are made to simplify thermal comfort only to
operative temperatures. The assumptions of air velocity, and constant clothing level of
occupants might vary considerably from a controlled test or a simulation test than in a
real application. This is again another reason why a ﬁeld test is so necessary to further
justify the use of these climatic concepts.
Finally, another important assumption that remains to be evaluated is that the panel
can be physically constructed, the ﬁrst iterations that were tried proved to be unsuc-
cessful in containing the PCM material inside the panel. Also, it is still unclear that
the PCM panel will be approved to be used in a real scenario for ﬁre safety reasons.
Although this might sound trivial, these are important components of this question, as
the climatic control concept should be able to be physically installed.

9.5.2
Can similar beneﬁts as systems with thermally active building
structures (TABS) be obtained in renovated buildings?
In principle, some of the beneﬁts of TABS systems could be achieved as well with PCM
systems. It was possible to use high water temperatures during the cooling season and
achieve a Category III thermal environment. The PCM system had a similar energy
performance when compared with the TABS. The energy savings compared against the
all-air system were practically the same for both systems. In terms of cooling demand
and peak cooling power, the cooling demand was eﬀectively shifted into the night by
both systems and the peak cooling power of the ventilation was considerably reduced.
This enables the possibility to match the cooling demand with renewable sources that
are present during the night, for example, take advantage of the cold night air or radiate
heat into the sky. Another clear beneﬁt observed, is that it was possible to reduce signif-
icantly the required ﬂow rate of the ventilation system, which resulted in energy savings.
Reducing the air ﬂow rate also reduces the risk of local discomfort due to draft. Finally,
a potential additional beneﬁt of PCM is that in order to be eﬀective, the concrete struc-
tures of the construction do not need to be exposed. This could overcome the acoustical
limitations that TABS have. However, the panels do not have any acoustic design prop-
erties so the fact that the concrete surfaces are not exposed do not guarantee there will
not be acoustical issues in the room. It was also found that the thermal conductivity
aﬀects the thermal environment considerably, so any modiﬁcations in the panels, such as
adding acoustical insulation layers will most likely aﬀect the results. These observations
can be limited by the fact that the building and weather combination played an impor-
tant role in the energy removed from the room. Also, the minimum ventilation rate was
signiﬁcant because of the high occupant density in the room. It would be interesting to
observe if the same results could be achieved at diﬀerent climates and with lower room
occupancy.
9.6
Future Work
To conﬁrm some of the claims made in this study there is further work that can be per-
formed. Work to validate the simulations and their assumptions should be prioritized.
One of the most interesting aspects to validate, is how realistically the enhanced thermal
conductivity (1.2 W/mK) was modelled, as it was shown that this parameter inﬂuenced
greatly the thermal comfort performance of the panels. To do this it is proposed to model
the panel in a program that is able to model heat transfer more realistically (like HEAT
2 or COMSOL) to determine a theoretical conductivity value to the PCM. Afterwards,
a single constructed panel can be experimentally tested in a similar way than Koschenz
et al. (2004) did. The single panel can be tested under a known load (with direct contact
to an electric blanket for example) and measure the heat ﬂux and temperatures in the
panel to estimate the energy stored and released by the PCM. Finally, this simpliﬁed
situation can be modelled in TRNSYS considering the value obtained from COMSOL

or similar and comparing the results against the measurements from the test.
Another action to validate the PCM model created in this thesis is to perform a test
in one of the climate chambers. Once the panels are constructed, they can be installed
in a climate chamber, by modifying the building component in the TRNSYS model to
match the chamber, comparison between simulated and measured values such as opera-
tive temperatures or cooling power from the PCM surfaces can be made, which will give
a general idea of the overall quality of the modelling assumptions. Finally the ﬁeld test
in the study room should be realized. The set up and methods proposed in this thesis
can be used as a starting point to perform the test. Fire testing the panels should be a
prioritized before the ﬁeld test is performed.
There is also further work worth performing with the models developed in the thesis
by extending the study.
It was shown that the building characteristics, climate and
cooling loads might be inﬂuencing the results.
It will be interesting to perform the
same study under diﬀerent loads, diﬀerent locations, and with diﬀerent building en-
velope properties. It will be interesting to reduce the occupant density to observe if
the energy savings results change and compare once again the results with the work of
Nielsen (2016) to see if the studies still diﬀer. The variation in climate and cooling loads
may provide information of the limitations of the PCM concept.
Other proposed future study is to perform a similar analysis to this study but extended
over the whole year. In the consulted literature, the eﬀects of PCM are evaluated only
during the cooling season. An interesting question to answer is if the PCM will have
adverse eﬀects during the heating season. In this thesis it was shown that a considerable
fraction of the thermal discomfort was caused by the low temperatures of the transition
periods, so even in warmer climates this is a relevant question, especially when evaluat-
ing energy use.
It was shown in this thesis that there is a possibility to shift part of the cooling de-
mand in the night. Another interesting aspect to study is the possible renewable energy
sources that could be coupled with the PCM system. Natural ventilation night cooling,
radiative night sky cooling, and ground loop heat exchangers are interesting coupling
possibilities that can be studied.
Finally, an economic evaluation of the ceiling panels can be made to have a better idea
on possible limitations of the material. The macro-encapsulated approach can reduce
the material cost. Additionally to the cost of the material, the life cycle of the panel can
be assessed to determine the economic potential or limitations of these concepts.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
The PCM concept investigated in this project can be potentially used to provide ther-
mal comfort and improve energy savings during the cooling season in oﬃce buildings
and mild climates under the right operation and design circumstances.The PCM system
tested proved to be not very eﬀective in passive operation mode. In order to fully take
advantage of the heat storage capacity of the material during the periods with higher
cooling demand some sort of method to aid in the discharge of the PCM during the night
is needed. It was also shown that operation and control strategy have very large inﬂuence
in the outcome of the thermal environment. In the case of PCM, the parameters that
aﬀect the most the thermal environment are the duration of the water circulation sched-
ule, the supply water temperature, and the conductivity of the PCM, the ﬂow rate will
not have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence of the PCM at least if it is within the turbulent regime.
A robust control strategy is needed to obtain adequate thermal comfort conditions and
the strategy required needs to be more complex than simple limiters in the room op-
erative temperature. The ﬁnal system operation and control strategy is most suitable
for a wider range in thermal comfort temperatures (Category III), the system could
not provide comfortable thermal environment requirements under the simulated condi-
tions and operation strategy if more strict thermal design category criteria were followed.
When compared against other cooling systems in buildings, the PCM system showed
promising results in terms of environmental beneﬁts. Energy use savings of 15% and
peak cooling power reduction of 30% were observed during the cooling season. The PCM
system also showed similarities in behaviour and operation possibilities as TABS sys-
tems. Similar energy savings and peak cooling power reduction were observed compared
to an all-air system. It was shown that it is possible to shift some of the cooling de-
mand into the night, which may lead to interesting renewable energy sources utilization
strategies, such as night ventilation cooling or night radiative cooling. Additionally, the
system could satisfy the cooling demand with the limitations mentioned in the above
paragraph in a similar way to the TABS system with temperatures considered adequate
for high temperature cooling.

Chapter 10. Conclusion
Further work has to be done to fully support the claims made in this project. It is
important to perform physical experimentation to conﬁrm the ﬁndings of this project.
The ﬁeld test proposed can be used for this purpose. Finally, to fully determine if the
results are being caused by the PCM and not other factors, a similar study performed
under diﬀerent climates, occupant loads, and building characteristics is recommended.
It is also important to study the indoor environment and energy use of the system during
the whole year.
