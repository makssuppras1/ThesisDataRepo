Abstract
This thesis has two primary objectives. The ﬁrst objective is to identify if using high fre-
quency, automatically recorded ship operations data can improve the prediction of propulsion
performance when compared to conventional manually collected data. The second objective
is to evaluate if switching from a semi-empirical wave added resistance estimation method
to a 2D linear strip theory based approach improves the overall quality of propulsion perfor-
mance prediction. This thesis is written in collaboration with Vessel Perfromance Solutions
APS (VPS) and Hammonia Reederei GmbH & Co. KG (Hammonia) in an attempt to ad-
dress these aims and objectives. VPS provide ship performance evaluation as a service to
their customers and have developed a propulsion performance analysis tool. Hammonia is
a ship management company, with the intent of further improving on current propulsion
monitoring tools available to them. For the purpose of this thesis Hammonia has given the
author access to their database of ship operation data. Data for three sister vessels be-
longing to the HHI 8200 TEU class is used to develop and test improvements to propulsion
performance analysis methods. The performance analysis tool by VPS is used to evaluate
the operational data. Furthermore, a 2D linear strip theory based interpolation tool is inte-
grated into the VPS analysis tool to evaluate potential improvements. The results presented
by this thesis are divided into two steps. First, the propulsion performance is evaluated as a
time series with linear trends indicating time dependent developments. Secondly, the data
is analyzed using a statistical approach to quantify the prediction quality of the diﬀerent
methods investigated. The results show strong indications that using automatically recorded
data improves prediction quality when compared to manually collected data. Furthermore,
the statistical analysis supports the hypothesis that using the new method to estimate wave
added resistance improves prediction quality. Finally, the thesis outlines future areas of work
to improve on the current ﬁndings and shortcomings.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Due to the fact that fuel consumption has a signiﬁcant impact on the operating expenses
(OPEX) of modern container vessels, it is a high priority for ship owners and operators to
be able to accurately quantify and monitor a vessel’s energy eﬃciency. Broadly speaking
the energy eﬃciency of ships can be broken down into three main areas of focus; opera-
tional performance, eﬃciency of machinery and hull % propeller eﬃciency. For the sake
of this thesis, operational eﬃciency refers to topics such as eﬀective voyage planning, trim
optimization and weather routing. Eﬃciency of machinery addresses the condition of fuel
consuming machinery. Lastly, the hull and propeller eﬃciency describe the state of the hull
and propeller fouling. In the context of this thesis fouling refers to the degradation surface
roughness as described by Townsin [23]. Throughout this thesis the hull and propeller eﬃ-
ciency is referred to as propulsion performance. While machinery and operational eﬃciency
are comparatively simple to measure, the propulsion performance of a vessel operating in
variable weather conditions is more diﬃcult to judge.
This thesis is written in cooperation with the ship management company Hammonia Reederei
GmbH & co. KG (Hammonia). Currently Hammonia is relying on data manually reported
on board vessels in order to monitor hull and propeller performance. Due to various reasons
this approach has been proven to be unsatisfactory. Additionally, an increasing amount of
automation of the propulsion and navigation systems has led to high frequency data becom-
ing available for analysis. Hammonia has set the target of identifying if an improved analysis
methodology, using high frequency d ata, may provide for a signiﬁcant improvement in hull
performance monitoring capabilities.
Vessel Performance Solutions APS (VPS) oﬀers performance analysis services to ship owners
and ship operators. By collecting and analyzing operational data, VPS benchmarks vessel
performance with respect to performance prediction models. Tracked over time this tech-
nique enables the vessel manager to identify any deterioration in the three main performance
categories outlined above. In order to derive meaningful decision support an accurate quan-
tiﬁcation of performance is required. To provide customers with decision support services,
VPS continuously tries to improve the prediction quality of their analysis tools.

1.2
Aims and objectives
This thesis has two primary objectives. The ﬁrst objective is to identify if using high fre-
quency, automatically recorded ship operations data can improve the prediction of propulsion
performance when compared to conventional manually collected data. The second objective
is to evaluate if switching from a semi-empirical wave added resistance estimation method
to a 2D linear strip theory based approach improves the overall quality of propulsion perfor-
mance prediction.
Accurately estimating wave added resistance is an important factor in order to evaluate the
propulsion performance of ships at sea. Currently, VPS has implemented a method based
on Kreitner’s [13] approach to estimate wave added resistance. The aim of this project is
to adapt the Design Tool developed by Nielsen [17] and Martinsen [16] to improve the wave
added resistance prediction. The alternative method is integrated into the VPS propulsion
performance analysis tool in order to evaluate its success. Finally, the thesis also aims apply
a statistical evaluation method of performance data scatter. In order to quantify the pre-
diction quality of the diﬀerent approaches tested, the evaluation method is used to provide
a comparison.

Methodology
2.1
Deﬁning the hydrodynamic performance of a ship
When considering a conventional commercial ship operating on the ocean at a given speed,
its resistance against forward motion, R can be categorized into two main components:
R = RT + ∆R
(2.1)
Where RT is the towing resistance and δR is the added resistance.
In general, a ship’s calm water resistance is inﬂuenced by its speed, displacement, and hull-
form. Calm water resistance refers to the resistance of the vessel in ideal environmental
conditions. Hence, no additional factors such as water waves, currents, wind, rudder action
and hull fouling are taken into account. Under these assumptions, the towing resistance RT
is constituted from three individual components. These components can be categorized into
frictional resistance RF, residual resistance RR, and air resistance RA [14]. The individual
components can be added to obtain the total towing resistance:
RT = RF + RR + RA
(2.2)
When considering ships operating in variable environmental conditions, the neglected addi-
tional resistance components need to be taken into account. The scope of this investigation
includes the following components:
- Ra : wind induced added resistance
- Rw : wave induced added resistance
- Ras : resistance increase due to deviation of water temperature and water density
- Rhf : resistance increase due to hull and propeller fouling
These component of added resistance are added in order to describe the total added resis-
tance, ∆R:
∆R = Ra + Rw + Ras + Rhf
(2.3)
The eﬀective towing power, PE, is determined as:
PE = R × Vs
(2.4)
where Vs is the vessel speed.
In order to utilize operational data collected on board of vessels to determine propulsion
performance, the correction method outlined in Figure 1, as well as a calm water resistance
model outlined in Section 2.2.2 are used.

2.2
Overview of VPS ship performance monitoring tool
2.2.1
General description of VPS tool
- Vessel Performance Solutions ApS (VPS) is a consultancy focusing on providing ship owners
and operators with ship performance evaluations.
Through their web-interface the VPS
performance tool allows access to the analysis of operational data gathered from a ﬂeet of
vessels. VPS provide performance analysis for several individual aspects of a ship’s operation,
such as:
• Propulsion performance
• Main engine performance
• Base load and boiler performance
• Data quality analysis
One of the key corner stones of the VPS analysis tool is the propulsion performance analysis
tool. This tool provides condition based monitoring of the hull and propeller fouling by an-
alyzing operational data received from the vessels. This tool is of main focus for this thesis
and is forthwith referred to as the VPS performance tool.
Figure 2 outlines the general approach, as well as the required data ﬂow, for propulsion
performance evaluation. Analogous to Figure 1, the schematic depicts sequential steps to
process operation input data. The resulting output data is the tracked over time in order to
determine if the propulsion performance indicator is deteriorating or improving. The trend
of the indicative propulsion performance provides the ship manager with decision support
on how to improve the performance of the vessel. Hull and propeller fouling can contribute
signiﬁcantly to increased fuel consumption and thereby operating expenses. Once a certain
threshold is crossed, potential rectifying actions such as hull and propeller cleanings or dry-
docking can be decided upon.

proach

The operational input data shown in Figure 2 may be manually collected noon report data or
automatically recorded operational data (further discussed in section 3.1.2). In general the
input data covers key navigational parameters such as speed over ground, heading, draughts,
wind speed and wave heights. Machine related operational input data covers main and aux-
iliary power and fuel consumption, fuel properties and electrical energy consumers.
VPS has developed a validation engine and ﬁlter criteria, which prevent implausible or in-
correct input data from progressing into the ﬁnal performance evaluation. The ﬁlter and
validation stage is shown as dashed lines in Figure 2.
The red ﬁelds shown in Figure 2 represent correction methods. The main correction meth-
ods implemented are due to changes in ship resistance caused by wind (Brix [2]), waves
and trim. Other correction factors include shallow water eﬀects (ITTC[8]), water density
(ITTC[8]) and fuel quality (ITTC[7]). The correction method due wave added resistance is
highlighted because this is the main focus of the thesis. Currently VPS has implemented a
correction method based on Kreitner’s approach to estimate wave added resistance (section
2.3.2). This thesis examines the results of implementing a correction method based on 2D
linear strip theory (section 2.3.3). The aim of improving the correction method is to increase
the overall prediction accuracy of the propulsion performance.
The ﬁnal input to the data processing schematic shown in Figure 2 is the ship model data.
In order to eﬀectively benchmark the performance of a vessel type, the ship model provides
target values. The model can be considered as a model of the best possible performance of
the vessel at ideal calm water conditions. The following section outlines the process used to
develop a calm water resistance model for the VPS propulsion performance tool.
2.2.2
VPS calm water model
The aim of the VPS calm water model is to establish a dense matrix of operating speeds and
draught combinations to accuratly predict the calm water resistance at any given combina-
tion of speed and draught. Later, the VPS algorithm uses linear interpolation to calculate
the predicted calm water power at any given speed - draught combination. Changes to the
calm water resistance model based on trim variations are neglected for this study. This is
done in order to minimize complexity and potential sources of uncertainty when analyzing
propulsion performance. It is however recognized that at high speeds and certain draught
ranges the potential inﬂuence of trim variation can be signiﬁcant.
The VPS calm water model is based on three sources of input; model test report, com-
putational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) report and sea trial results.
Furthermore, information
regarding hydrostatic particulars are taken from the trim and stability booklet. An extract
of the model test report is shown in Appendix B. The CFD report was commissioned by
Hammonia Reederei in 2014 to further study the resistance characteristics of the HHI 8200
TEU vessel class. The investigation is based on fully viscous RANSE based simulations [25]
and examines low speed resistance changes due to trim variations. An extract of the results
and a description of the computational methods are shown in Appendix C.

2.3
Added resistance due to waves
2.3.1
Summary of computational approach
As described in section 2.1, the wave added resistance Rw is deﬁned as the mean of the un-
steady force caused by wave interference with the ship. A visualization of the hydrodynamic
resistance components are shown in Figure 6 below. The total hydrodynamic resistance
consists of the mean unsteady wave resistance, ¯Rw, and the calm water resistance, RT. In
order to accurately predict the unsteady wave induced forces several methods have been
developed. Well-known methods include Maruo’s far-ﬁeld method, Salvesen’s radiated en-
ergy method as well as Gerritsma & Beukelman’s method [3]. Near-ﬁeld approaches include
Faltinsen et al.’s [11] asymptotic approach for short wavelengths and Choi et al.’s Green
function method.
Figure 6: Visualization of added wave resistance of a ship advancing in irregular seas with
constant speed and thrust
The two methods utilized throughout this thesis are Salvesen’s [20] method and Faltinsen’s
[11] asymptotic approach. Salvesen’s method is applied to long wavelengths, whereas Faltin-
sen’s is applied to deal with short wavelengths. Both methods are well established and have
been tested against model experiments with good results [22].
Faltinsen [10] shows that there is an inherent link between the motion of ships and the wave
added resistance. Hence, above mentioned methods all depend on accurate predictions of
ship motions. Well-known methods to calculate ship motions include; Boundary Element
Methods (BEM) using 3D Green functions, 2D linear strip theory, and Computational Fluid
Dynamics (CFD) codes [3]. For the purpose of this thesis, the 2D linear strip theory de-
veloped by Salvesen [21] is utilized to predict the required ship motions.

equations are taken from Salvesen papers published in 1970[21] and 1978[20].

2.4.1
Description of the Design Tool
The Design Tool was originally developed by Nielsen [17] and later expanded and further
developed by Martinsen [16]. The tool is intended to be used during the initial ship de-
sign phases by naval architects, as well as rule makers. The initially intended use-case has
been altered for this study. By adapting the MATLAB code of the Design Tool, the tool is
incorporated into the VPS performance analysis tool in order to correct for wave added resis-
tance. The Design Tool is chosen because it provides a quick and computationally eﬃcient
way to obtain an estimate of wave added resistance based on 2D linear strip theory. The
alternative of computing strip theory directly using Salvesen’s and Faltinsen’s method is not
feasible. Therefor the Design Tool provides a good trade-oﬀbetween accuracy and ease of
implementation. Furthermore, in cases where detailed information regarding hull geometry
is not available the Design Tool oﬀers the ability to estimate wave added resistance without
the detailed knowledge required for direct strip theory calculations.

tainer and RoRo vessel types. Based on their general dimensions, the reference geometries

Operational propulsion data utilized for the case study
In the context of this case study, operational propulsion data refers to the data collected
on board a vessel necessary to evaluate its propulsion performance. The propulsion perfor-
mance analysis follows the methodology previously outlined in sections 2.3.1 and 2.2.2. The
operational propulsion data is obtained through access to a database hosted by Hammonia
Reederei. The data is exported using a web-interface and stored in MS Excel ﬁles for pre
and post processing. Pre-processing of the data is necessary in order to ensure that all units
and data formatting is compliant with the VPS propulsion performance analysis tool. The
ship-board propulsion data used for this case study can be broken down into two categories;
manually reported noon data and automatically recorded data. In addition, one external
data source is considered: hindcasted weather information. Below listed descriptions outline
important characteristics of the utilized data:

Manually reported noon data is referred to as "noon reports" throughout this report. This
abbreviation is chosen because the reports are typically sent at 12:00 local time. A noon
report typically covers all relevant parameters concerned with navigation and operating on
board machinery. The information contained in the noon reports are collected manually by
oﬃcers and crew members. The data is therefore partially based on human observations
and on data copied from some form of analog or digital measurement device (power meter,
anemometer, etc.). Due to the nature of noon reports, individual reports typically cover
a period of about 24 hours. The time intervals are however variable and not restricted.
It is common practice to shorten or extend reporting intervals depending on Hammonia’s
reporting policy. In general, the operational data reported is an average value recorded over
the time period covered by the report. The noon reports used to evaluate the propulsion
performance in this study only include reports for time periods when the ship is operating
in open waters. Maneuvering and harbor reports are excluded from analysis.

6
Conclusions and future work
6.1
Summary of main conclusions
In order to achieve the aims and objectives outlined at the beginning of the thesis, the anal-
ysis of results is approached in two steps. Initially the results of the case study for HHI
8200 TEU class vessels are evaluated on the basis of time series and linear regression trends.
The analysis of these results has shown that the evaluation of propulsion performance varies
signiﬁcantly depending on data type and analysis method. Both the trend line gradients
and absolute values of propulsion performance KPIs show signiﬁcant diﬀerences, depending
on the type of input data being evaluated. This raises the question, which data set is more
reliable. More speciﬁcally, which method has a higher prediction quality.
In order to evaluate propulsion performance prediction quality, a statistical approach to
evaluate data scatter is applied. Comparing results from noon reports and autolog data
show that that an improvement in prediction quality of up 30 - 50% can be achieved when
using autolog instead of noon reports. Two out of the three vessels analyzed in the case
study support this hypothesis. In the third case, no improvement could be determined. The
results show that a more detailed investigation into the dominant factors contributing to
data scatter is required.
As part of the study, the currently implemented method to evaluate wave added resistance
is replaced by a newly developed approach. The new approach uses a Design Tool developed
by Nielsen [17] and Martinsen [16], which based on Salvesen’s 2D linear strip theory [20]
and Faltinsen’s asymptotic method [11]. The case study examining results from three sister
vessels of the HHI 8200 TEU container ship class shows that implementing the new method
improves prediction quality by about 25 - 35%. Again, results of two out of the three vessels
support this claim. The results of the third vessel show very similar levels of prediction qual-
ity. Although signiﬁcant improvements are possible, the overall degree of remaining scatter
is still signiﬁcant. This leaves plenty of room for future improvements.
This thesis has shown that accounting for wave induced vessel motions when evaluating
propulsion performance improves prediction quality. Given further reﬁnement of the method,
the analysis could generate even more accurate predictions of propulsion performance. Com-
parisons between the Design Tool interpolation results and direct calculations of wave added
resistance show that there is still room for improvement. The main area of inaccuracy is
identiﬁed to be at drafts below 12m. In such cases the limited Cb interpolation range cur-
rently implemented, causes the interpolation accuracy to break down. Furthermore, in its
current state, the Design Tool is limited to wave headings ranging between beam seas and
head seas. This limitation is relatively easy to overcome and could potentially lead to further
improvements in prediction quality. Other improvements or topics of future study identiﬁed
by this study are outlined in the following section.
49
==PAGE:54==
6.2
Future work
The following list of potential topics for further investigations was identiﬁed during the
various stages of this project. Many of the topics could not be included into this study due
to scope limitations. Each topic is accompanied by a brief remark:
• Vessel type-speciﬁc interpolation scheme: in cases where hull geometries are available,
the interpolation scheme could be ’personalized’ to a speciﬁc hull. This could lead to
signiﬁcant improvements in interpolation accuracy.
• Using weather hindcast data which includes Tz values: removing the need to estimate
the Tz could lead to more accurate calculation of wave energy spectra. Thereby wave
added resistance prediction could be improved.
• Using a bi-modal wave energy spectrum: replacing the Bretschneider wave energy
spectrum with a bi-modal wave energy spectrum could be used in order to account for
wind seas and swell. Frequently vessels are exposed to both wave systems, which is
not accounted for by the Bretschneider wave energy spectrum. Furthermore, including
diﬀerent headings to the swell and wind waves could also be worth investigating.
• Improvements to the C# DLL: the implementation achieved in this project is time
intensive. Further code optimization is required to reduce itteration speed from about
5 sec/ iteration to < 1 sec/iteration.
• Use a stable detection algorithm to determine intelligent ﬁlter criteria for autolog data:
using intelligent ﬁlters could signiﬁcantly reduce scatter in the autolog data.
• Use of artiﬁcial neural networks (ANN) or other form of machine learning to determine
causalities: this study was not able to examine factors which cause frequent under-
predictions of added resistance. Due to the large amounts of variable involved, ANNs
could be used to identify dependencies and sensitivities.
