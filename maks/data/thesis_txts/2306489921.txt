Preface
This thesis is the ﬁnal result of a ﬁve month study, containing the analysis and experimental
work within the ﬁeld of high voltage current- and voltage transformers broad band characteristics.
The paper is written in collaboration with DONG Energy A/S and the Department of Electrical
Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), as a master project during the spring
semester 2016. Responsible supervisor from DTU has been Joachim Holbøll, professor at the
Electrical Engineering Department. Responsible supervisors from DONG Energy has been Jesper
Hjerrild, Senior Power System Specialist at Wind Power and Georgios Rogdakis, Power System
Engineer at Wind Power.
The work performed for this master project consists of a theoretical study of current- and voltage
transformers characteristics, with a main focus on high frequency behavior. A suitable model of
the instrument transformers test setup have been created in the simulation tool Matlab SIMULINK,
prior to the experimental work. The main work load was experimental, and was performed in the
high voltage Power Lab at DTU. A current- and voltage transformer was tested for three different
scenarios, with a frequency range of 50 Hz to 5 kHz.

Abstract
The aim of this thesis is to identify the characteristic behavior of current- and voltage trans-
formers, when harmonic frequencies are present in the system. Current- and voltage harmonics are
known to be present in the power grid, but how they inﬂuence instrument transformers accuracy
performance is still unresolved. DONG Energy is one of many power companies relying on
accurate measurements from instrument transformers, as the data is a direct source for predicting
the customers electricity bill in the end of the distribution hierarchy.
In the ﬁrst part of this thesis, a short literature study is conducted, where the primary focus
has been on a transformers response to change in frequency. An examination of high frequency
measurement procedure and equipment was performed prior to the actual testing. Secondly, a
suitable model for both current- and voltage transformers was designed in Matlab SIMULINK. The
results gained from this model showed that instrument transformers are able to function accurately
under harmonic inﬂuence, but not with a change in operating frequency.
The largest part of this thesis consisted of the laboratory experiments, which where conducted at
The Technical University of Denmark, in the high voltage PowerLab. A physical test setup was
assembled and constructed, and high current and voltage measurement equipment was calibrated
and tested. Finally, three types of experimental procedures was carried out, where the behavior of
instrument transformers were tested at power frequency, increasing frequency and with composed
waveforms.
The results of the experiments show that instrument transformers are well suited to handle harmonic
presence in the power system. The replication error between primary- and secondary current or
voltage is consistent with the rated class of the transformer, both under and over nominal current- or
voltage amplitude. Results from the increasing frequency test showed, that instrument transformers
are unable to handle change of operating frequency and that voltage transformers are more sensitive
to a distorted signal than current transformers.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Instrument transformers are electrical devices used for scaling down a high, primary current
or voltage, to a low, measurable current or voltage, which can be read by measuring instruments
such as meters, relays etc. Instrument transformers are divided into two main categories: Current
Transformers (CT) and Voltage Transformers (VT), which also can be referred to as Potential Trans-
formers (PT). Their advantage compared to other measuring devices is among others robustness
and low cost.
When it comes to monitoring the current- and voltage level in the modern power systems, a
key task is to measure harmonic currents and voltages, as well as the harmonic power [1]. It is
important for evaluating power quality, detection of resonances, electromagnetic interference and
location of harmonic pollution sources. How much does in fact harmonic frequency effect actual
current- and voltage measurements from the instrument transformers? It is a new problem which
has presented itself in the later years when it comes to monitoring of power systems, as there is no
actual data from manufacturers on this subject.
1.2
Problem formulation
The main objective of the MSc-project is to investigate and analyze the characteristic behavior
of high voltage CTs and VTs based on theoretic/numeric models and laboratory investigations.
Especially the transformers characteristics at higher frequencies and composed waveforms are of
particular interest in this project.
Non-linear loads and others unstable devices are a source of disruption in the power grid, and
they make room for new questions that needs an answer. In this case, how does the instrument
transformers respond to badly distorted signals? Does it effect them at all, and if so, to what degree?
DONG Energy requires a better understanding and assessment of the characteristics of CTs and
VTs, when including frequencies higher than 50 Hz. They are interested to know if they should

1.3
Method and delimitation
To address the goals described in the problem formulation, some main tasks is to be conducted
as follows:
• Theoretical study regarding instrument transformers.
– The start of the thesis consists of reading literature concerning current- and voltage
transformers, with a priority regarding harmonic and high frequency behavior and
responses. Magnetization characteristics of transformers was also a part of this theoret-
ical study, with regards to losses connected to frequencies.
• Simulations of a suitable CT and VT model at frequencies up to 5 kHz.
– The properties and qualiﬁcation of several simulation programs is to be explored, as a
part of the start up of the thesis. Matlab SIMULINK was deemed to be the program
most suited for the requirements of this thesis, and previous knowledge of the program
played a part of the decision, as well as the programs ability to design non-linear
behavior.
– Two separate simulation models is to be created and tested, with the aim of being as
equal to the physical current- and voltage transformer test setups as possible.
– Short circuit and open circuit tests should be conducted, to characterize the physical
transformers, and the component values should be used as a base in the simulation
model design.
• Appropriate measurement procedure for evaluating behavior at high frequency.
– The equipment needed for measuring current, voltage, phase and frequency at fre-
quencies higher than 50 Hz, with a large broad band accuracy, should be decided and
calibrated before use, to ensure reliable experimental results.
• Laboratory assessment of the CTs and VTs characteristics at:
– Power frequency
– Increasing frequency
– Composed waveforms
As mentioned in the abstract of the thesis, the experimental process in the laboratory holds the
largest part of the work done for this project. An experimental test setup and method for proceeding
with measurements was decided upon before testing, but this proved to be a work in progress. Per-

1.3. METHOD AND DELIMITATION
3
forming high accurate measurement is a delicate process, and many new problems and challenges
uncovered themselves along the way. The current transformer was assumed the easiest type of
transformer to test before the project, as it does not require high voltage. A phase-gain analyzer was
applied to measure phase between primary- and secondary current, as well as primary current RMS
value. True RMS meter with high frequency range was judged to be accurate and well suited for
this type of measurement, and was used for the three types of transformer assessment mentioned
above.
The voltage transformer measurements was carried out secondly, when the current transformer
testing was concluded. VTs have a high internal impedance, and when subjecting it to higher
frequencies, some unexpected results presented itself. Resonance was reached fast, leading to an
unexpected high voltage peak, causing the phase sensitive multimeter and one of the ampliﬁers
phases to break down. Therefore, no further phase measurements were performed as a part of
this thesis. Fortunately, the ampliﬁer had three phases, and the voltage error measurements could
continue. A spectrum analyzer was added to the test setup at this point, to analyze the harmonic
content of the voltage in detail. The analyzer conﬁrmed with the frequency spectrum, together with
the waveforms seen on the oscilloscopes and values read of the True RMS meters, an inconsistency
of the voltage amplitude, with a drifting of nearly one volt in magnitude. When the input voltage
was kept constant, none of the measuring equipments was able to capture a stable reading.
This led to an extensive research, which concluded in that the fault lied within the hand held
multimeters. When changing them with digital multimeters at a higher decimal range and with a
higher accuracy, the problem of drifting voltage values was solved, and the voltages maintained a
constant value down to the fourth decimal at all times. The laboratory assessment for the voltage
transformer was redone, and the difference between the old and the new measurements proved
to be the deciding factor for the VTs ability to maintain class at harmonic inﬂuence or not. The
frequency spectrum was obtained at different composed waveforms with the spectrum analyzer
at nominal voltage, and the procedure was repeated for the current transformer test setup as well,
to get a better insight into the composed waveform harmonic distribution of CTs as well as VTs.
The idea was to redo all CT measurements as well, with the digital multimeters, but an external
problem occurred, and the ampliﬁers remaining phases broke down. The remaining project time
does not cover the ampliﬁers repair, but the CT measurements did not show any of the drifting
signs which caused problems for the VT, and the results is therefore still presumed to be accurate.
Measurement technique is a work in progress, and new knowledge is obtained continuously,
leading to new ideas and improvements along the way. By the end of the experimental laboratory
work, looking back to the beginning, there are some changes that could have been made. Unpre-
dicted challenges is a part of the learning experience in laboratory work, which this thesis can be
seen as an example on.

Delimitations
The delimitations set in the commencement of this report is as follows:
• Ampliﬁer power limitations makes it not possible to create a single, composed waveform for
the current transformer test setup at nominal current 1600/5 A.
• Phase-gain analyzer or phase sensitive multimeter broke down before VT measurements.
• The lack of repetition of measurements, regarding both CTs and VTs, have to be considered
when analyzing the results. Some measurements will only be repeated once, due to the
amount of different measurement procedures and time restriction of the thesis. All measured
results have been logged manually, by reading the measuring instruments, and one repetition
had a time span of 30 min.
1.4
Readability
The thesis paper is divided into 9 chapters and a set of appendices, and it is organized as follows:
• Chapter 2 describes the theory behind high voltage CTs and VTs, and explains their main

CHAPTER 2
Instrument transformer basics
Instrument transformers are one of the key components regarding monitoring and protection
of the power system today, and they are required to be both accurate and reliable. A introduction
into the basic concepts of current- and voltage transformers have been covered in chapter 2, and it
provides an insight into this special type of transformers operating principles, with a main focus
on high frequency behavior. As measurement accuracy is the essential task for an instrument
transformer, it is interesting to know how frequencies affect this ability, and if so, to what extent.
Harmonic inﬂuence of current- and voltage transformers is a newly risen question, and ﬁnding up
to date literature on the topic has been a challenge.
2.1
Transformer response to change in frequency
To get a complete and total overview over the internal proceedings in a transformer, when
exposed to a change or increase in frequency, its magnetic aspects have been investigated. Chapter
2.1 regards transformers in general, and the actions stated here will be valid for most transformers,
and is not restricted to current- and voltage transformers. The primary source of literature in section
2.1 has been the book Applied Electromagnetics by Martin A. Plonus [2].
2.1.1
Magnetic behavior of ferromagnetic core
The core of a power transformer is usually ferromagnetic, which means it consists of a material
with high magnetic properties [2]. These elements are mainly Fe, Ni and Co, as well as alloys
and compounds of these materials, and their common factor is a strong, internal molecular ﬁeld
with volumes of molecules in parallel based on the individual electron spins. The polycrystalline
structure in ferromagnetic material is called domains, and the domains spins spontaneously and
changes direction without the effect of an external ﬁeld or with regards to each other. The total
magnetic ﬁeld of the material is equal to zero. To use a ferromagnet for a purpose, it needs to
be magnetized by a magnetic ﬁeld, which will align all domains, resulting in a strong, combined
external ﬁeld. If the magnetic ﬁeld is removed after magnetizing the ferromagnetic material, some
of the domains will remain in their changed position, and some will return to the former random

Hysteresis
The ﬁrst time a magnetic ﬁeld H is applied to a ferromagnetic material, the core magnetization will
start from zero, and increase with the ﬁeld along the initial curve, as ﬁgure 2.1 outlines [2]. At the
same time, the induced ﬂux density B and the magnetization M will also start to increase. The
increase will continue along the initial curve magnetization curve, as it comes from a unmagnetized
state, until the point of saturation is reached, which is deﬁned as in equation (2.2):
Bs = µ0(H + Ms) ∼= µ0Ms
(2.2)
where Bs is the ﬂux density at saturation, µ0 is the permeability of free space (4π · 10−4Hm−1),
Ms is the magnetization saturation of the material and H is the applied magnetic ﬁeld strength. This
point is the materials maximum limit for current conducting. When the current I in the magnetizing
coils decreases, the magnetic ﬁeld H will decreases as a result, but the initial curve will not be
retracted back to its original state. Instead, B will decrease in the rate of delay caused by the
domains of the material. This is the effect of hysteresis, and it is a source of magnetic loss in a
transformer. When the current I is reduced to zero, which is when H is zero, a net magnetization in
the core remains, and can be indicated by Br, given by equation:
Br = µ0Mr
(2.3)
where Br is the remanence ﬂux of the core, µ0 is the permeability of free space and Mr is
the remanence magnetization. To de-magnetize the core to its original state again, a negative
ﬁeld/current has to be applied, to force the ﬂux in an opposite direction. This force is called the
coercive force Hc. Figure 2.1 shows the non-linear and multivalued nature of magnetization in
ferromagnetic materials. The shape of the hysteresis curve is determined by crystal imperfections,
inclusions and cavities, and they all effect the behavior of the domains of the material. The area
of a loop is a measure of the energy lost to heat per cycle, and therefore related to the energy that
must be dissipated by a magnetic material in an alternating ﬁeld.

Leakage ﬂux
Not all the ﬂux that enters the primary winding will transfer to the secondary winding [2]. Some is
lost as it passes along the core path of the circuit. This phenomenon is called ﬂux leakage, and it
occurs due to the relative permeabilities µr of ferromagnetic materials. The purpose is to conﬁne
the ﬂux, and the range of permeability is typically 102 to 105, compared to air, which has a relative
permeability equal to 1. Air is therefore unable to withhold the ﬂux in a good manner, and some
will escape to the surroundings outside the core.
Leakage inductance is caused by an imperfect coupling between primary and secondary winding of
a transformer. It is equivalent to a series inductance of the winding, and will in reality be in series
with the load. Higher frequencies will lead to an increased effect of inductive reactance added to
the main impedance load in the circuit, with the know formula (2.11):
XL = 2π · f · L
(2.11)
where XL is the reactance of the winding, f is the frequency and L is the inductance.
2.1.3
Magnetic behavior of a ferrite core
If the adjacent atoms in a material have opposite spins, that are not equal in magnitude, it
is a ferrite [2]. It is a semi-magnet, and its magnetism is called ferrimagnetism. It differs from
ferromagnets, which have adjacent atoms that spins in the same direction, creating a strong ﬁeld,
and anti-ferromagnetic materials, which have adjacent atom with opposite spins as well, but equal
in magnitude, creating a net magnetization equal to zero. Ferrites magnetic properties are therefore
lower than for ferromagnets, typically with a ﬂux density of 0.3 Wb/m2, compared to ﬂux density
of ferromagnets 2 Wb/m2. This low magnetizations makes ferromagnets the preferable material
when it comes to generators, motors and transformers in normal power operation, which need a
material with a large ﬂux density and saturation magnetization to be induced.
Ferrites differs from iron and other ferromagnetic materials due to their bonded-alloy powder struc-
tures, which makes them insulators. Ferrites typically have a resistivity range ρ from 1 −104Ωm,
much larger than the iron range 10−4Ωm. The advantage by having a high resistivity is that ferrites
are immune to eddy currents, making ferrites an ideal material when it comes to high frequency
conducting. They are however not used commonly at power equipment that operates at lower
frequencies, even though large precautions are made to prevent eddy current losses in this type of
equipment. The reason is ﬁrst of all the low saturation of a ferrite due to the non-magnetic part of
the material. Second, the cost of manufacturing ferrites are relatively high. In addition, ferrites are
brittle with a low mechanical strength, ruling them out in most power equipment where mechanical
strength is a consideration.
A ferrite core has a stable permeability over a high frequency range, and is often chosen for
equipment with a frequency higher than 1 MHz [4]. According to [5], the permeability is a function

Current transformers
Current transformers are commonly employed in the power grid to transform a high current
down to a low current [6]. Meters, relays and other equipment more suitable for lower current, can

reliable measurements, the demand for precision and accuracy is high when it comes to current
transformers.

directly to the grid, being a constant current source. The primary of a CT is the primary of the

2.2.1
Accuracy class and limits
Current transformers are divided into two sub-categories; measurement CTs and protection
CTs, where the demand of accuracy is higher for the ﬁrst category. Protection CTs are usually
marked with a P for protection, together with their class. They can tolerate a much higher over
current, as they are designed to detect a fault, rather than measure a current [7]. The saturation
level of protection CTs is higher, so the high primary current can reach the required level, which is
decided by current setting of the tripping/protective relays. The most important thing for a pro-
tection CT is that the reﬂection in the secondary is true in the case of a fault condition in the primary.
According to the IEC standard 60044-1 [8], current measuring transformers have certain lim-
its which they are required to obtained when it comes to accuracy [9]. The accuracy is dependent
on several factors, including burden, core saturation, external electromagnetic ﬁelds for disturbance,
temperature, rating factor and connected load to name a few.

Standard burden of a CT
To ensure optimal accuracy for the current transformers, standard burden impedance should
be assured at the low current side of the transformer [6]. For instrument transformers, this load
is referred to as "burden", to separate it from the main load of the total network. This burden is
usually given in VA from the transformer manufacturer. Standard burden includes all additional
loads connected to the secondary terminals, such as metering devices, copper conductors, relays
and other impedances. The total burden of a CT can be calculated by adding all external burdens,
and multiplying it with the rated secondary current of the CT, as in equation (2.14):
S = I2
2 · Zb
(2.14)
where S is the total apparent power deﬁned for a CT, I2 is the rated secondary current and Zb is
the impedance burden. Conductors should be sized appropriately, as both under-dimensioning and
additional wire length add resistance and leads to inaccuracy. The windings of the CT itself also
have a small internal burden, though it is very small compared to the other inﬂuences, and can
therefore be neglected in most cases.
2.2.3
Open-circuit conditions
A current transformer should never run with its secondary terminals open circuited, as this
lack of voltage control will cause the CT windings to create a huge peak voltage [10]. A no-load
==PAGE:27==

condition causes an enormous stress on the windings, eventually leading to CT failure. CT failure
can result in damage of equipment, as well as danger to any present personnel. For this reason, a
load should always be connected to the secondary terminals of a CT, and it should never be broken
as the primary line is installed. For safety purposes, a CT should always be shorted even though
the instrument is not in use.
2.2.4
Effects of increasing frequency on a CT
The voltage and current grid frequency is standardized at 50 Hz in Europe for normal operation.
When regarding the transformer, a change in frequency may have several effects on its behavior [11].

harmonic inﬂuence plays a larger part in the modern grid strategy. As the presence of harmonic
waveforms increases, the accuracy of the current transformer is believed to be decreasing. Equation

2.3
Voltage transformers
Voltage transformers (VT), or sometimes called potential transformers (PT), are used for
metering and protection purposes in the power system. VTs are connected in parallel with the
monitored circuit, and steps down the system voltage from the high voltage side, to a standardized
value for meters and relays on the low voltage side. The winding ratio on the low voltage side is
higher than the high voltage side, and the system voltage is connected to the VT across its primary
terminals to ground. In Europe and Asia, the general secondary voltage is 110 V phase-to-phase
[7]. By having standardized secondary values, equipment can be designed and connected to a high
voltage system, independently of what voltage level it should monitor. A VT operates by the same
principles as a power transformer, the main difference being size, power capability, operating ﬂux
levels, and compensation[10]. VTs have low power ratings, a high voltage VTs power range is 3-5
kVA, which makes the VTs physical size smaller. Figure 2.9 presents a very simpliﬁed model of

2.3.1
Accuracy class and limits
A VTs accuracy is dependent on its winding resistance and leakage reactance [7]. This
impedance determines the voltage error and phase errors of the transformer, and it varies with the
burden (VA) connected at the secondary side. To improve its accuracy and limit the error, cores
materials with higher permeability qualities and better heat dissipation can be chosen. This can
also limit the excitation current. A better core lamination will also improve heat dissipation, and
can reduce the core size.

2.3.2
Standard burden of a VT
The standard or rated burden is the maximum load the transformer may have to feed during
operation [10]. To not confuse it with the load of the total power system, the name burden is
commonly used when it comes to instrument transformers. The burden is deﬁned as everything
externally connected to the secondary terminals of the VT, including meters, relays and conducting
wires, and it is usually given in volt-ampere [VA]. The impedance of each component should be
added algebraically to determine the total load, and the impedance values can either be found in the
manufacturers data sheets if available, or be measured directly.
2.3.3
Short-circuit operation
A VT should under no circumstances be short-circuited on its secondary terminals [10]. A VT
is designed to be able to withstand mechanical and thermal stress for 1 second with full voltage
applied to its primary terminals without obtaining any damage. In under a second, normally, a
protection device will detect the fault and disconnect the overvoltage. If this safety system fails, the

prolonged fault would lead to a temperature rise that would exceed by far the insulation limits, and
axial and radial forces on the windings would cause severe damage to the VT.
2.3.4
Effects of increasing frequency on a VT
The voltage and current grid frequency is standardized at 50 Hz in Europe for normal operation.
When regarding the transformer, a change in frequency may have several effects on its behavior
[11]. As mention in section 2.2.4, the presence of non-linear loads in the power system is a cause
of harmonic currents. Harmonic voltages gets generated in the impedances in the system by these
harmonic load currents [14]. If the frequency from the supply side of the transformer increases,
or the voltage waveform is distorted due to harmonics, the overall loss will be inﬂuenced by the
means of:
• Core losses - increase of hysteresis and eddy current loss
• Winding losses - skin effect in the resistive part of the windings
• Resonance - High voltage peak caused by resonance in the transformer
As hysteresis and eddy current losses both are frequency dependent, this type of loss will increase
with frequency. Eddy currents are the most crucial cause of loss when it comes to losses caused
by a harmonic presence. The harmonics affecting the VTs in the power grid are primarily coming
from the supply side of the transformer. The overall transformer energy loss will go up, and due
to the resistive loss from the windings caused by the skin effect, the temperature will rise, and
strain the transformer coils. The output voltage might decrease as a cause of all the additional losses.
How the frequency inﬂuences a VTs behavior can be represented by its transfer function, equa-

3.3
Fundamental measurement principles
All measuring equipment and measuring setups will introduce their own uncertainties to the
ﬁnal measuring result [15]. Because of this, it is not possible to be absolutely sure that the value
read from the measuring equipment is in fact the true value. There are multiple reasons that leads to
additional of measuring uncertainty, with different effects on the ﬁnal measuring result. According
to [15], there are ﬁve main causes to inaccuracy of measurement:
• The person performing the measurements
• The measuring equipment
• The test object
• The measuring environment
• The method of measurement
The person performing the measurements in this project will be a cause of inaccuracy, mainly
because most of the measuring values were obtained manually. The measuring instruments are
usually sold with a maximum accuracy, speciﬁed in the instruments data sheet. Some of the mea-
suring equipment used in this project had to be calibrated before use. The accuracy of equipment
is degraded by age, and needs to be calibrated on a regular basis, and the calibration procedure
and results are given in appendix A. In appendix B, all the equipment used in this project is listed,
together with their maximum error, or accuracy. The test objects themselves, here being the current-
and voltage transformer from DONG Energy, introduce their own inaccuracy to the ﬁnal result.
The environment where the measuring took place is a contributor to inaccuracy, in this project
being the high voltage laboratory at DTU. Temperature is not something that has been given much
space during these experiments, though it is an important part of the real scenario. However, all
instrumentation were checked on a regular basis, to avoid overheating. The laboratory is dry and
clean, with a stable temperature and lighting scheme.
A set of measuring results will repeated over a number of times will have some differences,
which is called deviation, and is a part of all measurements. Deviations are unavoidable, and they
will distribute themselves around the true value. The errors of measurements are divided into
two categories: accidental or systematical. Accidental errors happens from time to time, and are
==PAGE:41==

unpredictable. If only one set of measurement is performed, it is not possible to decide weather
the error is accidental or not. Repetition and reproducibility is therefore important when it comes
to accurate measuring. Systematical errors can be either known or unknown, and can either be
avoided or they can be unavoidable. If a measuring error is known, it can be used to correct the
measured result. The corrected result will then be closer to the true value of the measurement. For
the current measurement, systematical measuring error was discovered and corrected accordingly,
and this is presented in appendix C. Equipment is often given with a maximum error ±a in their

4.2.3
Composed waveforms
The third simulation proﬁle was superimposed harmonics on the power frequency. In the
PowerLab, this meant connecting the large current generator to the variable transformer, hence
the grid, as the main source should be 50 Hz. The ampliﬁer was supplying the higher frequencies
through the ferrite core transformer. The different connection possibilities have been discussed and
simulated in the following section. Option 4 was deemed the best alternative, and was selected for
the experiments in the high voltage laboratory.
Connection points
Prior to the testing and modeling, the idea was to use the same type of transformer to superimpose
current at higher frequencies by connecting it to an ampliﬁer, see section 3.4.3 and 3.4.1 regarding
ampliﬁer and current generation. The ampliﬁer is able to generate a maximum output of 270 V,
maximum frequency of 5 kHz, handling current up to 30 A. The ﬁrst problem that was encountered
was the lack of output effect, as the ﬁrst idea was to supply high frequency through an identical large
current transformer. The transformers magnetic cores was consuming a large part of the power from
the ampliﬁer, making the ﬁnal current in the circuit barely measurable. Also, a sinusoidal waveform
could not be obtained, and the oscilloscope showed a heavily saturated waveform. Therefore, the
transformer used for connection between the ampliﬁer and high current circuit was changed, and
connected to the 2000/5 A ferrite core transformer, wounded and made for a special project in 2015
[16]. Ferrite core transformers are preferable as they are more suitable for handling high frequency
currents. The downside is that ferrites saturates quicker than iron when subjected to high currents.
==PAGE:54==
42

Option 1 - Series without protection impedance
The ﬁrst suggestion was to connect the ampliﬁer in series with the high current primary circuit.
The problem with this connection point was the high currents at the ampliﬁers secondary side. This
happens due to the low resistive path from the current source and ampliﬁer, resulting in a loop of
high current. As the ampliﬁer only can handle 30 A maximum at its outputs, the fuses activates and
shuts down, before the ampliﬁer heats up and goes into overload.
Option 2 - Parallel without protection impedance
In the second attempt, the ferrite core is connected across the primary connections of the DE-CTm,
and in parallel with the current generator. Here, the simulations showed a very large current in
the primary circuit of the DE-CTm. The current on the secondary side of the DONG Energy
transformer is however low, which means that only a small amount of the primary current is
transferred through to its secondary. This is due to the low impedance of the ferrite core, creating a
low impedance path for the current to ﬂow. The voltage at the secondary output is high compared to
the current. The current ﬂowing in the secondary of the ferrite core gets very high in this scenario,
larger than the maximum current, thus creating overheating and system failure.
Option 3 - Series with protection impedance
When the ferrite core is connected in series with the main circuit, protected by a resistive element
between ampliﬁer and main circuit, the current through to the DONG Energy transformer is
sufﬁcient and accurate. The current level at the ampliﬁer is very low, a few mA at the most. The
problem with this scenario is that the magnitude of the harmonic current will be too low, and it will
not affect the output signal, as it is overrun by the main current from the grid.
Option 4 - Parallel with protection impedance
By connecting a protective resistive component between the ferrite core transformer model and
the main, high current circuit, the total current at the primary side of the ferrite core is low, some
mA in current. The secondary side of the ferrite core current level is also decreased., and the
voltage level is low. The secondary current from the DE-CTm model is close to ideal, with a small
phase displacement and magnetic losses. When using the parallel connected ferrite core at a higher
frequency, a harmonic disturbance is measurable on the secondary side of the DE-CTm.

4.2.4
Limitations of the current transformer SIMULINK model
The simulation model of the current transformer test setup is able to produce waveforms,
similar to what is expected when changing the frequency, and can be used as a good aid when it
comes to test setup evaluation and prediction of the current transformers behavior at the various
scenarios. There are however certain limitations which needs to be considered when using the
model, presented in the bulletins:
• The correct core data for the DE-CTm is unknown. The non-linear inductors used to simulate
the magnetic behavior of the transformers contains core data copied from a another, known
==PAGE:57==
4.3. VOLTAGE TRANSFORMER SIMULINK MODEL
45
core, and used as a replacement.
• The core used to simulate the ferrite core is the same steel core, which was used for modeling
the DE-CTm core. The behavior for the ferrite will not be as for a real ferrite, as the ﬂux
density is higher in this steel/iron core. The geometry is measured from the reference
transformer core.
• Short circuit and open circuit tests was performed to characterize the components of the CTs.
Not all the component values were possible to measure in the laboratory, some were to small
for the instrumentation available.
• The model does not take external impurities and disturbances into account.
• The model does not include any capacitances.
• The model can not be used as an actual model to perform speciﬁed tests and receive exact
values, due to the problems speciﬁed above. It can be used as a way of understanding, and as
a prediction of transformer behavior.
With regards to what has been stated in the bulletins, the model showed good simulation results,
compatible with the results that have been measured in the high voltage laboratory. All the
differences and similarities have been commented thoroughly in chapter 6.

The phase displacement is dependent on the burden impedance and leakage reactance of the
transformer windings. As the frequency is increasing, the secondary voltage measured across the
burden impedance starts to lag the primary voltage. This difference is proportional to the increasing
frequency. The amplitude of the secondary output voltage is also decreasing, due to a voltage drop
across the series impedance. The leakage inductance should be kept as small as possible to avoid
large losses at higher frequencies. The burden should be ensured to be within the optimal range
speciﬁed by the transformer manufacturers.

4.3.4
Limitations of the voltage transformer SIMULINK model
The simulation model of the voltage transformer test setup turns can be used as a aid when it
comes to test setup evaluation and prediction of the voltage transformers behavior at the various
scenarios. There are however certain limitations which needs to be considered when using the
model, presented in the following bulletins:
• The core data is not the actual core data for the speciﬁc voltage transformer from DONG
Energy.
• The model does not take external impurities and disturbances into account.
• No capacitances is included in the VT SIMULINK model.
• The model can not be used for simulations of a resonance situation, and the VT is not as
responsive to a change in operating frequency in the simulations.
With regards to what has been stated in the bulletins, the model showed good simulation results,
compatible with the results that have been measured in the high voltage laboratory. All the
differences and similarities have been commented thoroughly in chapter 8.

CT standard burden test
The purpose behind the standard burden test is to ensure that the impedance value equals the
standard burden value given by the manufacturer to provide optimal accuracy. By measuring the
voltage drop directly across the secondary terminals of the CT, when applying rated secondary
current, the burden can be calculated by applying equation (5.1):
SV A = Vdrop · I2
(5.1)
where SV A is the standard burden in volt-ampere, Vdrop is the voltage drop across the secondary
terminals and I2 is the measured secondary current. The terminals should be isolated from other
loads, short circuited, as for the voltage drop across the injection points to be measured by a
voltmeter. To avoid over-voltage, the burden is slightly lower than the rated burden.

The purpose of this test was to analyze the amplitude and phase angle frequency response of a

was used to excite the CTs, and by ensuring a constant primary current, the behavior of the CTs
could be observed. The CTs selected for the measurements was the DONG Energy measuring-
and protection transformer. Figure 5.2 gives the schematic of the test setup made for increasing
frequencies, with the ampliﬁer used as the main power source. The ampliﬁers maximum output
voltage is 270V per phase, making a limitation for testing at nominal current. However, even at a
low current of 100 A primary, the phase angle displacement could be measured. The ampliﬁer was
connected to the two DE-CTs in parallel, creating a current sharing between the CTs and the copper
bar. A small current was ﬂowing in this circuit during the measurement, but it was considered

(5.2)

was measured for every odd frequency up to 5kHz. The starting idea was to create an increasing
operating frequency, by powering the system with the Spitzenberger ampliﬁer as the main source,
connected to the high current circuit through the ferrite core. This was not was not realizable, as it

CHAPTER 6
Current transformer -
Discussion and comparison
with SIMULINK model
Current transformers are according to the results presented in chapter 5, well suited for han-
dling harmonic inﬂuence. The CTs measuring abilities have been evaluated for a signal with
maximum 10 % harmonic contribution, and for the required current range, the class can be said
to be obtained. The CT was modeled in SIMULINK in chapter 4, and the experimental results
have been compared against the results from the model, to see if the simulations can describe the
actual transformer behavior. Current transformers behavior under harmonic conditions have been
evaluated more closely as an addition, based on results gained from a spectrum analyzer.
In this master project, current- and voltage transformers broad band characteristics have been
investigated. The two instrument transformers have been subject to the same three types of tests,
and their response have been both simulated and measured. How the transformers accuracy is
affected by a harmonic presence is the main objective for the project, both in magnitude and phase.
Does the transformers measure inaccurately when there is a small level of harmonic content in the
signal, or can their performance be deemed sufﬁcient?
The current transformer proved to be the easier test subject of the two instrument transform-
ers. This is most likely a combination of familiarity with the CTs due to [16], and the low voltage
level present, compared to the VT tests.
6.1
Measurement results and comments
At the end of the project period, one statement can summarize large parts of the laboratory
experience: Measuring equipment is sensitive, and should be used with care. All the required
equipment have to be tested before usage, compared and validated. This was a necessity in order to
trust the obtained results. Small, additional loads to the measuring circuit can create a differences in
73

measured result, critical to the total outcome. As an example; it became very clear when measuring
the secondary current, and the connection of the oscilloscope caused a current drop of about 10
mA. This small change was enough to make the current error ratio 1 % inaccurate, making the
CT unable to maintain class. Therefore, a thorough investigation should always be made when it
comes to accurate metering, especially regarding the sensitive measuring CTs. The protection CT
is able to keep within class at all times, and will only go out of boundary at frequencies higher than
5 kHz, according to measurements done in this project. The total measuring uncertainty calculated

capabilities is not according to the 0.5 class for currents below 1.5 A, according to the calibration,
and the results below 1.5 A can therefore be seen as a cause of both the CT and Rogowski coils

6.1.1
Power frequency
As performed in the special project High Voltage CTs and VTs, Principles and Broad Band
Testing [16], the CTs was investigated at normal operation, to conﬁrm their accuracy class as well
as the functionality of the test setup. From the measurements gained, it was reconﬁrmed that
current transformers are able to keep their given accuracy class well, both below and above rated
current value. The CTs exhibits the largest amount of instability at lower currents, below 10 %
nominal current. It is therefore advised to ensure high or nominal current at all times when the aim
is to perform accurate measurements.
The necessity of a correct burden on the secondary side, and its impact on the CTs have been tested,
and by comparing measurements done with and without an additional resistive burden attached,
it can be stated that transformers used for ﬁne measurements only, should have an impedance
conﬁrmation, so the accuracy is optimized. CTs used for protection purposes are not so sensitive,
and have a larger range before disregarding class boundary.
Regarding phase angle error when measuring a 50 Hz current, a phase shift of 0.003o, from
the beginning to the end of the measurement, was observed. These results shows that current
transformers are having a stable phase displacement between primary and secondary current, from
0 to 120 % times rated current.
6.1.2
Increasing frequency
The investigations of the current transformers at increasing frequency at a stable primary current
reveals how the current error is dependent on frequency. The measuring CT is responding rapidly
to a change in operating frequency, and at a frequency of 1 kHz, the CT is not able to maintain
class anymore. The protection CT shows the same response as the measuring CT, though due to the
fact that it is accepted to have an error of 5 %, it does not fall out of class at 5 kHz, where the test is

The phase error was measured only for the DE-CTm. An increase in operating frequency re-
sulted in a negative angle between the primary and secondary current, decreasing linearly with
frequency, as ﬁgure 5.11 in chapter 5 shows. The higher frequencies create a transformation delay,

reactance of the windings XL is equal to:
XL = 2πfL

where f is the frequency and L is the inductance of the coils. According to the standard IEC 60044-1
for current transformers, a 0.5 class CT should not exceed a phase shift δ of 0.9 centiradians at 1.0
times the nominal current IN. This converts to an angle of approximately 0.5 degrees. The increase
in frequency affects the phase angle immediately, and the CT passes the maximum allowed phase
angle error between 50 Hz and 150 Hz.
6.1.3
Composed waveforms
In the power grid, the main component of the grid current will be at 50 Hz frequency. Around
1-3 % will be at another frequency, and the larger part of this will be between the 3rd- and 19th-
harmonic, as they are the most dominant components [19]. This test is the closest to the actual
situation of the power grid, where among other non-linear loads are effecting the main current to a
small extent. A limitation was however, that the ampliﬁer is only able to create one frequency at the
time, meaning the input current to the DONG Energy current transformers would be a combination
of the grid current and a composed signal, not a mix of several harmonics. The percentage of
harmonic current compared to nominal current has also been exaggerated in some cases, as the test
where 10% of the nominal current with 150 Hz was superimposed on the grid current. The purpose
behind this was to see how much disturbance the CTs are actually able to handle, before falling out
of their accuracy class.
The measured values from the laboratory experiments were presented in chapter 5, and the result
has been summarized:
• The amount of waveform distortion is dependent on the amplitude of the dominant harmonic
current component.
• The number of harmonic frequency component is according to measurements, insigniﬁcant.
For instance, there is no difference between the 3rd-, 5th- and 7th-harmonic, when it comes
to current error.
• The measuring CT from DONG Energy can almost stay within class under harmonic inﬂu-
ence. The performance increases with the current, up to 120 % nominal current.
• The protection CT from DONG Energy can stay within class under harmonic inﬂuence.

6.2
Frequency analysis of composed current waveforms
A standard for regulating the THD limit in the power systems is not given by law, but IEEE
has provided a standard with recommended values for acceptable harmonic distortion. According
to IEEE Std 519,"RECOMMENDED PRACTICES AND REQUIREMENTS FOR HARMONIC
CONTROL IN ELECTRICAL POWER SYSTEMS":
"Computers and allied equipment, such as programmable controllers, frequently require AC
sources that have no more than 5% harmonic voltage distortion factor [THD], with the largest
single harmonic being no more than 3% of the fundamental voltage. Higher levels of harmonics
result in erratic, sometimes subtle, malfunctions of the equipment that can, in some cases, have
serious consequences.[20]"
The limits on voltage harmonics are thus set at 5% for THD and 3% for any single harmonic. It
is important to note that the suggestions and values given in this standard are purely voluntary.
However, keeping low THD values on a system will further ensure proper operation of equipment
and a longer equipment life span.
The frequency analysis of the current signals from the DE-CTm resulted in a positive outcome for

8.1
Measurement results and comments
The correct measuring equipment is of utmost importance when it comes to high voltage
measurements. A very small mistake can cause large impact on the total resulting error. An
example is a situation that occurred during HV measurement of the VTs in the PowerLab: The
resistive probe connected to reference VT during calibration is speciﬁed to function with a 10 MΩ
output impedance. When connected only to the oscilloscope, which has an internal impedance of 1
MΩ, the measuring result is wrong. Connected to the multimeter, which has an internal impedance
of 10 MΩ, the measuring result is correct. Connected to both the multimeter and the oscilloscope,
the input impedance is changed and the difference resulted in a nearly 10% measurement error.
It is important to ensure the correct measuring equipment, by knowing what is connected to the
secondary terminal and what is actually being measured.
In this project, the high voltage was measured with a HV-probe, suited for accurate, high fre-
quency measurement. The results from this probe was obtained through a highly accurate digital
multimeter, see section 3.5.2.

8.1.2
Increasing frequency
In the second test, the VTs performance at an increasing frequency was investigated. As the
resulting graph in ﬁgure 7.9 in chapter 7 clearly shows, the VT starts having a poor performance
even at operating frequency 150 Hz. Above 200 Hz, none of the DE-VT test results showed that
the transformer was able to keep within class. Voltage transformers are, in opposite to current
transformers, designed with many copper windings at its primary side. This is the reason for a large
impedance on the HV-side of the transformer, with a large inductance due to the multiple coils.
When the frequency increases from the fundamental frequency, the inductance of the windings
will lead to a higher impedance, as well as a fast rise in system voltage. At a certain frequency, a
resonance situation will occur, which is were the inductive reactance and the capacitive reactance of
the windings are equal in value, and cancel each other out. At this point, the system voltage is much
higher than rated transformer voltage, and there is a voltage peak. After reaching peak voltage, the
voltage level drops quickly again with the continuous increase in frequency. A resonance situation
can cause an extreme overload on the system, and should be avoided.
The error of the VT was measured while keeping the voltage at a controlled input level, lower than
the nominal voltage. Resonance is preferably to be avoided in this case, as a safety to the equipment
connected. The voltage at the high voltage side was kept constant at all frequencies, varied with
the ampliﬁers output amplitude. This resulted in a distinct lowering of voltage before resonance,
and heavy increase of voltage after resonance, to maintain the voltage level at a constant value.
The frequency was increased from the fundamental, with every odd up to 1250 Hz. According
to the measured results, the VT is unable to maintain its class after passing 150 Hz. The voltage

Composed waveforms
The third laboratory experiment performed on the voltage transformer was regarded as a more
valid test scenario, as it is closer to the actual situation of the power system. As the input signal

harmonic, the situation of harmonic inﬂuence in the grid was designed. As the voltage error
ratio results shows, the VT performance is effected by the presence of harmonics. The measured
values from the VT laboratory experiments was presented in chapter 7, and the results have been
summarized as follows:
• A voltage transformers accuracy is affected by a harmonic presence (tested up to 10%), but
not at a critical sense.
• A voltage transformer transforms harmonic waveforms accurately to the secondary side.
• A voltage transformer is able to maintain class under harmonic inﬂuence.
• The harmonic level is the same at both input and output side of a voltage transformer.
The experiments at frequencies higher than 50 Hz have demonstrated the voltage transformers
sensitivity to harmonic inﬂuence. When inﬂicting a composed waveform on the system, it does not
affect the voltage transformers accuracy in a critical sense. The DE-VT performed within class
at all tests conducted with a composed waveform, both for lower and higher frequencies. The
percentage of harmonic content compared to the fundamental frequency is important, but below 10
%, accuracy is not affected distinctively.
The composed waveform scenario is closest to the actual situation of the power system, which
makes the ﬁnal test result the most interesting ones. In the real system, there would not only be
one harmonic in addition to the power frequency, but many, depending on harmonic disturbance
sources in the grid.

Comparison with the SIMULINK model
The voltage transformer SIMULINK model is viable to a certain degree, with the fact that it is
able to predict the outcome of a voltage transformer behavior. It should be noted that it is not an
exact replica of the VT used in the PowerLab, as the transformers real core data was not used. Due
to this fact, the obtained values should not be used for verifying purposes or to ensure safety.
When the VT simulation model is simulated at 50 Hz, rated output voltage, the primary and
secondary voltage is almost in phase with each other, with a very small voltage drop between
the voltage across the windings, and the voltage across the burden. This drop is caused by the
magnetization current, and losses across the leakage impedances, and is very small at normal
operation.
A further increase in frequency causes the simulation model to become even more phase dis-
placed between the primary and secondary voltage. The secondary is lagging the primary with an
increase consistent with the frequency, and the voltage drop becomes larger. This causes a larger
magnitude difference between the voltage across the windings and the impedance, creating a larger
voltage error.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
The purpose of this project was to investigate the inﬂuence of a harmonic presence in grid
current and voltage on instrument transformers. Are current- and voltage transformers capability
for accurate measurements damaged by harmonic presence, or are DONG Energy in fact, chasing a
ghost? This is the essential question throughout this master thesis, and the aim is to decide weather
or not DONG Energy should continue with this investigation.
The project was based on two transformers, provided for laboratory experiments by DONG Energy,
equal to the transformers they use offshore at their own wind farms. First, a theoretical study was
carried out, to gather some basic information regarding the behavior of transformers, when being
subjected to a change of frequency. Secondly, a suitable simulation model for both the current- and
voltage transformer test setup was designed, to analyze and predict the behavior of experiments to
come. Finally, the actual experimental procedure was carried out in the high voltage laboratory
at DTU, where the DE-CTs and DE-VT was tested at power frequency, increasing frequency and
with composed waveforms. A frequency analysis of the two transformers was included in the test
procedure.
9.1
Results
The main work load of this project consisted of laboratory experiments, and based on the results
obtained, the following conclusions have been made:
• Current- and voltage transformers are designed for an operating frequency of 50 Hz, and
a change in this fundamental parameter leads to inaccuracy. This statement is supported
by measurement results obtained from testing both current- and voltage transformers in the
PowerLab, at an increasing frequency. The CTs are more adaptable to a change in frequency,
and are exhibiting good measuring performance to nearly 1 kHz, when the phase error
displacement is excluded. VTs however, are very sensitive to a frequency change, due to
their high primary impedance, and are not able to deliver accurate results when the frequency
increases to 150 Hz. As a contrast to the CT measurements, the VT will reach resonance
113
==PAGE:126==
114
CHAPTER 9. CONCLUSION
frequency at a lower frequency level, resulting in a dangerously high peak voltage, with a
fast rise time. A change in operating frequency of an instrument transformer is not a realistic
scenario in the power grid, nonetheless, the behavior of the VT especially, should be worth
noting.
• The results from the composed waveform test proved the DONG Energy current trans-
formers to be highly suited, for dealing with a harmonic signal. The current transformer
was subjected to a harmonic inﬂuence up to 22 % of the fundamental frequency, and at
all higher harmonics tested, the DE-CTm and DE-CTp kept their rated class at nominal
current. The class of 0.5 is kept within a current range of 30-120 % times nominal current IN.
• The DONG Energy voltage transformer undertook several tests with composed waveforms,
both at high and low frequency, and it has been veriﬁed that this type of transformer is able
to maintain its accuracy class when submitted to a harmonic waveform. The maximum test
consisted of 10 % harmonic inﬂuence, compared to the fundamental, resulting in an accuracy
between 30-120 % nominal voltage VN. As VTs are only required to operate within its
accuracy class between 80-120 %, this is recognized as a very good performance.
• The frequency analysis performed on both primary and secondary side of the CT and VT,
showed two things: One, there is no relevant difference between the harmonics in the com-
posed signal entering the transformer, to the signal leaving the transformer. In other words,
there is no generation of internal harmonics observed, when inﬂicting a current- or voltage
transformer with a composed waveform. Second, the 3rd-harmonic is the largest inﬂuence,
compared to the other, intended harmonic. When the 3rd-harmonic is the superimposed
harmonic, the amount of other frequencies is minimal, and the THD-level is as good as equal
to the harmonic content of the 3rd-harmonic compared to the fundamental.
• When inﬂicting a higher frequency (>1 kHz) on the transformers, the ratio between THD-
level and harmonic component percentage switches, and the 3rd-harmonic becomes a large
contribution to harmonic disturbance in the signal. This result has not been investigated
further, and is limited to the voltage transformer testing.
• From the comparison of frequency spectrum between the SIMULINK model of the CT and
VT, to the measured result from the high voltage laboratory, it was noticed a lack harmonic
disturbance in the simulated currents and voltages. The SIMULINK model does not contain
a signiﬁcant harmonic next to the composed harmonic signal created, especially there is a
distinct lack of 3rd-harmonic presence. In the measured waveforms, the harmonic distortion
from other frequencies are present, though small, and with a decreasing value from the
largest contributer, namely the 3rd-harmonic. An attempt to recreate the resonance situation
in the VT SIMULINK model was done, where parasitic capacitance was included in the
simulation model. The capacitance between the primary and secondary winding had the
==PAGE:127==

largest inﬂuence on the result, and by tuning this carefully, resonance frequency fr = 650
Hz could be found. The importance of capacitances for obtaining an accurate model were
therefore acknowledged, and should be included in any future modeling.
9.2
Recommendations
Besides the just stated conclusions regarding the measuring accuracy of instrument transform-
ers, a set of other conclusions regarding measurement technique including a harmonic frequency
has been drawn. As stated in the introduction, laboratory work is a learning by doing process, and
new knowledge is gained along the way, together with ideas of improvement. When it comes to
recommendation for instrument transformer measurement under harmonic conditions, the following
can be stated:
• It is important to ensure the correct loading on the secondary side of the current transformer.
It is sensitive to the equipment connected at its secondary terminals, and a change in loading
can disrupt the measurement value. If connecting additional measuring equipment to the
secondary, after assuring correct standard burden, it can lead to a change in secondary current
I2, leading to a measuring error, enough to put the transformer out of its accuracy class.
• Voltage transformers are very sensitive to equipment connected to its secondary terminals,
and as for the current transformers, it should be conﬁrmed that load is the recommended one.
• Using a suitable measuring equipment can be the difference between obtaining the correct
result or not. With respect to this project, where high accuracy is the key component, this
turned out to be very important. Especially in the case of harmonic measurements with
a composed waveform. The Agilent U1241B True RMS hand held multimeters, which
were used for current measurements, turned out to be unﬁtted for the task. According to
the multimeter’s data sheet, the accuracy between 500 Hz to 1 kHz is 1 % between 10 and
100 V. But, the instrument changes after 10 V, and reduces its resolution from 4 digits to 3
digits after 10 V. This made them unable to measure the VTs values, as a accuracy down
to the ﬁfth digit was required, as this was the shifting voltage decimal. For the current
measurements, the resolution is higher in the required measuring area. The solution for the
VT measurements was to change the hand held multimeters with digital multimeters, with a
6 digit resolution and a 0.04 % accuracy and frequency range 10 Hz - 20 kHz.
Regarding the original task of gaining the experience to be able to provide DONG Energy with
certain recommendations for using CTs and VTs in non-power frequency environment, there is still
some work that needs to be done. Based on the work performed in this thesis, it can be stated that
accurate measurements requires the correct equipment and loading of secondary, and their accuracy
should be veriﬁed for each new, connected component. The project is however not at a point yet, to
suggest a measuring procedure to be recommended, also for transformers at a higher voltage level,
==PAGE:128==

and speciﬁcs to be given. For that, further investigations will need to be conducted.
9.3
Future work
The projects experimental procedures have given answers regarding current- and voltage trans-
formers, but it has also been the reason for new questions when it comes to the effect of harmonics,
that could be worth looking into. A list of suggestions to how to proceed from this project has been
included as a ﬁnal comment to this master thesis:
• Regarding the Matlab SIMULINK modeling of current- and voltage transformers test setup,
there is improving possibilities in both simulation models, since the models can not be
viewed as an accurate representation of the systems. By obtaining the correct core values of
the current- and voltage transformer, a more accurate model can be designed. An accurate
model would be able to tell when the harmonic limit of the transformers is reached, when it
will have a resonance and when it starts to affect the accuracy to a damaging degree.
• A limitation of the project has been time. More measurements to back the results obtained
and the stated conclusions, would give the project report a higher validation. The test setups
have been created and measurement techniques has been tested during this project. DONG
Energy used a ELSPEC metering device, which is an expensive and highly accurate device.
A further study of current- and voltage transformers could be to include this device in the
test setups.
• The composed waveforms in this project was a composition of the fundamental frequency,
and one additional harmonic frequency. It could be interesting to created an input signal with
a closer relation to the actual harmonic composition found in the offshore grid, and perform
an even more realistic investigation of the current- and voltage transformers response to this
harmonic signal.
• The situation of resonance in the VT is interesting, and is deﬁnitely worth looking into more
closely, in a future project. The voltage transformer from DONG Energy proved to have a
resonance frequency of approximately 650 Hz. This situation was more crucial when 650 Hz
was used as the operating frequency, but even at the composed waveform tests, 650 Hz was a
source of more distortion than the other harmonics.
The ﬁnal conclusion to summarize the project is, based on the results gathered and presented in this
master thesis, that current- and voltage transformers are effected by harmonic inﬂuence, but not
to a class damaging extent. Regarding measuring accuracy and maintaining rated class, both the
CT and VT are able to deliver satisfactory performances, also under circumstances with a distinct
presence of harmonic frequencies.

A.2
Rogowski-coil calibration
The Rogowski coil was used for high current measurements in the CT testing, and as a reference
at higher frequencies, as it is frequency independent. To ensure measurement accuracy, a high
current shunt is placed in series with the transformers in the circuit, causing a path for all the current
to ﬂow through it. This leads to a small a voltage drop across the low resistance in the shunt, which
is proportional to the current ﬂowing though it. The current can be scaled directly by reading the
voltage measured across the shunt. A shunt is frequency independent, which is an advantage for
this projects measurements. Shunts are however temperature dependent, and has a derating factor
for continuous use. A thermal measurement was therefore taken to ensure that the thermal limits
where kept, which is considered to be 80oC. A fan was used to prevent the shunt from possible
heating.

APPENDIX E
Open circuit and short circuit tests
In appendix E, the procedure for short circuit and open circuit testing, used for identifying the
transformer component values is explained.
E.1
CT component identiﬁcation at rated frequency
To create as realistic a transformer model in SIMULINK as possible, the real values for the
series- and shunt impedances in the CTs had to be found, so the transformers could be characterized.
An open circuit and short circuit test was conducted, as the DONG Energy transformer is fully
embraced in a epoxy resin insulation, and the component values are unknown and unapproachable.
As the current transformer differs from a regular transformer, some obstacles occurred when trying
to perform a open circuit test. The high current side could not be powered sufﬁciently through the
secondary low current to measure any output signal, and therefore the test was conducted from low
current to high current in both cases.
E.1.1

calculated by equation (E.1):

E.1.2
Short-circuit test
A short circuit test was performed to determine the copper loss of the transformer windings,
modeled as the series-branch of the equivalent diagram. This test is performed at the rated load
current of each current transformer. Figure E.2 gives the test setup schematic.
Figure E.2: Current transformer short circuit test setup
To measure the values, an ammeter, voltmeter and wattmeter was connected as the diagram
shows. The low voltage side of the transformers are shorted, and the meters are measuring high
voltage values, at rated frequency. Copper losses are different from core or iron losses, they are
unstable, and current dependent. Therefore, measurements are always taken at full load, to give the
optimal loss. Current transformers are however designed with an over-dimensioned copper part, to
reduce losses and eliminate a source of inaccuracy.

E.1.3
Open-circuit test
The current transformer differs from a regular power transformer because it is a current device,
and therefore its most important task is to measure high current at a high precision. Due to this
task, the copper windings, which is a source of non-linearity as it becomes more lossy with current,
is over-dimensioned, so it will be able to handle higher current. The magnetic losses does not
change with current, as the power needed to magnetize the molecules in iron is constant. It effects
inaccuracy linearly and constantly. To identify the shunt elements of the current side, the high
current side was open circuited, while applying full load voltage to the low current side.
Figure E.4: Current transformer open circuit test setup

in parallel with the transformer impedance to compensate for inductance inﬂuence. When tuning
the capacitors carefully towards zero at rated voltage, the resistive current could be read from the
voltmeter. The series impedance is also present in this measured current, but can be excluded by

Broad band characteristics of high voltage CTs and VTs 
Master project 
 
Background: 
As the wind farms are getting bigger, the influences from the wind farm to the main onshore power grid are 
increasing. This means that the harmonic disturbances (HD) due to the connected wind farm also are 
changing. The impact can be increasing or decreasing the HD in the wind farm and in the onshore main grid. 
DONG Energy (DE) needs a better understanding and assessment of the characteristics of CTs and VTs, 
when including frequencies higher than 50Hz. Both amplitude and phase angle of the power frequency, 
harmonics and composed waveforms are of interest. 
 
Project description (see also tasks on next page): 
The main objective of the master project is to investigate and analyse the characteristic behaviour of high 
voltage CTs and VTs based on theoretic/numeric models and laboratory investigations. 
The transformer’s characteristics at frequencies over 50Hz and composed waveforms are of particular 
interest. 
Starting point for the student is the previous work done in the special project “High voltage CTs and VTs, 
principles and broad band testing”. The theory of instrument transformer operation at higher frequencies has 
to be studied and the behaviour and effect on the measuring equipment in a broad frequency range >=50Hz 
has to be investigated. A test setup has to be designed to determine the instrument transformers broad band 
characteristics. This includes measurement under composed waveforms as possible under the given 
laboratory conditions. 
Laboratory measurements have to be performed followed by comparison of the results with the expected 
values. 
Finally, suggestions and recommendations for use of CTs and VTs in non-power frequencies environment is 
to be made. 
The following aspects should be covered 
 
Suitable model of CTs and VTs at frequencies up to 5kHz 
 
Laboratory assessment of the transformers characteristics under 
o 
power frequency 
o 
higher frequencies  
o 
composed waveforms 
 
Investigations of the influence of load and measurement equipment 
 
Recommendations for use of CTs and VTs in non-power frequency environments

necessary information and feedback on project progress.
