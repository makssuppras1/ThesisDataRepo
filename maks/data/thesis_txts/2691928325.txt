Multiphysics, multiscale modelling of solid oxide cell
by Shangzhe Yu
To increase the lifetime of the solid oxide cell (SOC) stack, its mechanical behavior
should be studied through numerical modelling. It can be done by using a detailed
model which has ﬁne structures such as sealants and metal-window frames.
But
the detailed model usually requires a high computational cost to solve the model,
particularly on the stack level modelling. In this regard, we present a multiphysics and
multiscale model that contains the detailed geometry (e.g., sealants and metal window
frames) to evaluate mechanical performance of SOC stack components eﬃciently. The
Weibull distribution and J-integral are applied to calculate failure probabilities and
energy release rate respectively.
On the single cell stack level, the model shows there is little chance for components
to suﬀer failure when the SOC stack is under operation. In contrast, failure is more
likely to happen when the stack is cooled down to room temperature as both failure
probabilities and J-integral are considerably increased. A parametric study is given
to ﬁnd the optimized TEC of sealants and fuel-electrode support layer.
On the stack level, multiscale modelling is utilized to predict mechanical perfor-
mance eﬃciently. The key idea of the multiscale modelling is to input results from
a homogenized stack model to the detailed model as boundary conditions. The ho-
mogenized parameters, such as thermal conductivity and stiﬀness matrix, are deter-
mined through numerical methods and a 4-SOCs stack is modelled by a homogenized
model. After mapping temperature ﬁelds and displacements from the homogenized
stack model to the detailed model, failure probabilities and J-integral of three diﬀerent
cells in the stack are successfully obtained.
Deviation sources of the multiscale modelling are discussed. It is recommended
that only temperature results from the homogenized model should be input into the
SRU model.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and objective of the thesis
Currently, fossil fuels, such as oil, coal and natural gas, still supply major energy to
human society although the share of renewable energy keeps increasing. Human beings
heavily rely on fossil fuels due to the relatively cheap price and mature technology
for utilization. However, a future of 100% renewable energy supply is possible [1] and
more importantly, it is necessary due to the growing pollution and worsening climate.
One of the key issues in future energy system where solar and wind energy prevail is
the grid balancing since solar and wind have intermittent features that are subject
to locations and weather. This ﬂuctuation must be alleviated or oﬀset in order to
guarantee reliable electricity supply and the healthy state of the transmission grid.
One approach is to use electrolysis&fuel cell, as shown in Figure 1.1, to compensate
for the variation of renewable energy.
Figure 1.1: A 100% renewable energy system with electrolysis&fuel
cell as electricity compensation devices.
Electrolysis is a promising technology to convert electricity into hydrogen chemical
bonds energy.
Hydrogen is chosen as a carrier of excessive renewable energy not
only owing to its environmentally friendly nature, but also due to its high energy
density and elemental abundance [2]. Among the electrolysis technologies, solid oxide
cells (SOCs) shows relatively high electrical eﬃciency and fast kinetics [3], [4]. It is
also capable of electrolyzing CO2 which makes it beneﬁcial for carbon capture [3].
Nevertheless, it has issues in material degradation as a result of the high-temperature
operating conditions. Particularly, fractures in components may result in gas leakage
and cross-over of reactants. The former decreases the eﬃciency while the latter can
generate the hot-spot accelerating the degradation. To increase the lifetime of SOCs,
the fracture behavior in cells needs to be studied. This can be done by carrying out

Literature about Numerical models on diﬀerent scales can be found in the last
ﬁfteen years. On the single cell stack level, Jiang et al. [5] modelled a single cell with

the stack level, Peksen et al. [10] built a SOFC stack multiphysics model and studied
its mechanical performance. The max tensile stress was found at the gas inlet and it
was attributed to the high temperature gradient. Lin et al. [11] studied the stress of
glass-ceramic sealants on the stack level. It showed the most vulnerable area was at
the edges of sealants. Blum et al. [12] found the highest tensile stress was located
at the boundary of the manifold.
Compared to stress, literature about interfacial
cracks of sealants are fewer and most of them were pure solid mechanics models [7],
[8] where electrochemical reactions and heat exchange between ﬂuid and solid were not
considered. Hence, they had a uniform temperature ﬁeld which was not a plausible
assumption. Lin et al. [9] did give a multiphysics model analysing interfacial crack of
sealants, but it was on the single cell level.

more signiﬁcant. However, it requires a huge time cost even without including solid
mechanics. For example, Banerjee et al. [13] built up a 40-SOCs stack and it took
about one week to ﬁnish. In order to accelerate calculation, Li et al. [14] applied
symmetry boundary, homogenized domain and in-home developed current density on
30-SOCs stack modelling. However, it still needed twenty hours to ﬁnish. Recently,
Navasa et al. [15] came up with an attractive concept of fully homogenization for
SOC models that enormously decreased computational time as shown in Table 1.1.

Nevertheless, as a result of homogenization, the detailed geometry is ignored making
the results of mechanical performance less convincing. In brief, we are faced with this
problem: we want to model mechanical behaviors of SOCs on the stack level that
have detailed geometry while at the same time calculation should not be too long.
It then naturally leads to a thought of multiscale modelling, i.e., using the results
of the homogenized stack model as boundary conditions of the detailed model in order
to evaluate its mechanical performance.
To examine the mechanical behavior in the SOC stack, this thesis makes the
following contributions:
• Numerical modelling on a single cell stack of detailed geometry that has metal
window frames and sealants.
• Evaluate the mechanical performance of the stack components with a focus on
interfacial fractures of sealants
• Carrying out stack level modelling through the multiscale method. Coupling the
homogenized model (on the stack level) and the detailed model (on the single
cell stack level).
1.2
Methodology
The multiphysics model presented in this thesis includes electrochemical reaction,
heat and mass transfer, momentum transfer, and solid mechanics. The multiphysics
modelling was carried out through commercial ﬁnite element software COMSOL 5.6.
All physics except solid mechanics were calculated simultaneously. Then the results
were validated including mesh density independence, mass conservation and agreement
with experimental I-V curves. Later the result of the temperature ﬁeld was used to
calculate thermo-mechanical stresses. After ﬁnishing the computation of the detailed
model, multiscale modelling between the single cell stack and 4-cell stack (using the
homogenized approach to model stack level behaviors) was implemented. A ﬂow chart
summarizing the methodology is shown in Figure 1.2.

Chapter 2
A brief review on solid oxide cells
SOCs are electrochemical devices with a great development foreground as they
provide an approach for energy conversion between chemical energy and electricity in
an eﬃcient and environmentally friendly way. The SOC technology can consume fuels
to output electricity under fuel cell mode (known as SOFC). Or it is operated reversely
which is supplied with electricity in order to generate fuels such as hydrogen (known as
SOEC). In other words, materials and structure in both fuel cell mode and electrolysis
mode are the same. This section gives a short introduction to SOCs with a focus on
SOEC. The structure of the SOC and its components, as well as commonly used
materials, are introduced. Additionally, a comparison between diﬀerent electrolysis
technologies is made.
2.1
Physical structure
The typical physical structure of a planar SOC is composed of two channels and
three layers as shown in Figure 2.1. In electrolysis mode, water as "fuel" is sent to the
cell and later is converted into hydrogen and oxygen. Note that it is recommended
to accompany water with hydrogen in order to avoid Ni redox problem [16].
At
the oxygen electrode side, although air does not participate in the electrochemical
reaction, it is served as sweeping gas to carry product oxygen out of the cell. In fuel
cell mode, the electrochemical reactions are reversed. For example, the hydrogen ﬂuid,
as the fuel, is sent to the same fuel electrode where it is consumed to produce water.
Figure 2.1: The schematic of the geometry and reactions in a planar
SOC under electrolysis mode.

Chapter 2. A brief review on solid oxide cells
The electrochemical reactions in SOC involve three phases: gas phase, ionic con-
ductor phase and electronic conductor phase. This indicates the electrochemical reac-
tions happen on a triple-phase-boundary (TPB) where three phases meet with each
other. The more precise description is that reaction takes place on the eﬀective TPB
[17] where ionic and electronic conductors are not isolated as shown in Figure 2.2.
Ref [18] pointed out that volume fraction of electronic and ionic conductor should
be higher than percolation threshold in order to make TPB throughout the porous
electrode. If a mixed ionic and electronic conductor is applied, only solid and gas
phases are needed.
Cell conﬁguration can be classiﬁed based on the support layer (the thickest layer
that provides mechanical strength). Generally, it has four types: electrolyte-support,
fuel-electrode-support, oxygen-electrode-support and interconnect-support. For electrolyte-
support, the thick and dense electrolyte provides strong structural support, but ionic
resistance is high. To decrease the resistance, high operating temperature is required
which however decreases the lifetime of SOCs. Fuel/oxygen-electrode-support is ap-
plied to lower the operation temperature by using a thick fuel/oxygen support layer
instead of a thick electrolyte. The major problem of oxygen-electrode-support is its
low conductivity. For fuel-electrode-support design, Ni reoxidation can harm the cell
[19]. The strong support can be provided by the metallic interconnect. Nevertheless,
it also has drawbacks such as chromium poisoning and ﬂows ﬁeld design limitation
[20].
Cells are connected in series or parallel to form a stack. Usually, they are connected
in series to provide a relatively high output voltage [21]. For certain stack voltage and
power requirements, the connecting of cells needs to combine both series and parallel
[21]. It is necessary for each stack to have a gas manifold that sends reactant gas
from the supply pipe to the stack and remove the unreacted gas and product out of
the stack. It can be an external or integral manifold. Besides, ﬂow ﬁelds also play an
important role in the stack design. It is possible to arrange fuel and air ﬂow to be
co-ﬂow, counter-ﬂow or cross-ﬂow. [22] pointed out that co-ﬂow design shows more
uniform temperature distributions. The temperature distribution is also related to the
gas uniformity that can be increased by adjusting channels conﬁguration [23], [24].
2.2
Components and materials in a solid oxide cell
A SOC is composed of a fuel electrode, an oxygen electrode, an electrolyte, in-
terconnects and sealants.
It was found that no single metal could be a satisfying
material for fuel electrode and as a result, the common material for fuel electrode is
a cermet Ni/yttria-stabilized-zirconia (YSZ) [25]. Ni serves as catalyst and electronic
conductor while the function of YSZ is to transport oxygen ions, decrease thermal
expansion mismatch and prevent the Ni grains from growing to unaccepted level [25],
[26]. The oxygen electrode material is usually a combination of lanthanum strontium
cobaltite ferrite (LSCF) and gadolinia doped ceria (GDC). LSCF is a mixed ionic and
electronic conductor, but its oxygen ion diﬀusion is not high [27]. Consequently, GDC
is added for the purpose of increasing ionic conductivity [28]. The electrolyte is a
dense layer containing only YSZ. In the middle of electrolyte and oxygen electrode,
there is a thin barrier layer GDC that prevents reaction between strontium and zir-
conium to form SrZrO3 [28], [29]. This new phase is not desired because it is poor in
transferring oxygen ions [26], [29]. Between cells, there are interconnects to connect
them. Furthermore, it functions for separating fuel electrodes and oxygen electrodes.
The popular material of the interconnect is steel owing to its relatively cheaper cost.

high tensile stress can be found. Finally, sealants must exhibit much low electrical
conductivity that needs to be lower than 1×10−4 S cm−1 [30].
Sealants of SOCs can be divided into three types [29]: compliant sealants (e.g.,
metal-brazes), compressive sealants (e.g., mica-based composites) and rigid sealants
(e.g., glass-ceramic). The ﬁrst is heated to become liquid that can ﬁll the gap between
components owing to capillary action.
Good wetting is achieved with the help of
reactive metal that reduces the ceramic material. During reduction, a new phase is
formed which improves the wetting of compliant sealants on the ceramic surface. Its
advantage is the deformable ability that can release the stress but the reactive metal
can lead to degradation of the joints [29]. Compressive sealant is another deformable
material. The sealants work when an external force is applied. This type of sealants
is required to strictly match TEC to that of adjacent components. However, it brings
a diﬃculty in the stack design because it needs to maintain compressive force during
operation. The last sealant is the most common sealant used in SOCs because it is
insulating, directly bonded and easy to manufacture [31]. The rigid sealant usually
contains glass that becomes soften during fabrication through which it covers the
surface of joints. It then returns to be rigid after being cooled down to operation
temperature. Considering it is a brittle material, its TEC must match with the joints.
Plus, chemical stability is an issue for glass-ceramic. For instance, barium is usually
added to glass-ceramic sealants because it is beneﬁcial in decreasing glass transition
temperature Tg but in the meanwhile, it is reactive with other components [32].

2.3
Comparison with other electrolysis technology
There are in general three kinds of electrolysis technology: alkaline electrolysis
cell (AEC), proton exchange membrane electrolysis cell (PEMEC) and solid oxide
electrolysis cell (SOEC). Their operation conditions are summarized in Table 2.1
AEC is the most mature electrolysis. It is characterized by a liquid electrolyte and
a diaphragm between two electrodes that separate generated oxygen and hydrogen.
Despite its high level of commercialization, several problems exist that make it less
attractive compare to other electrolysis. First, the diaphragm can not completely sep-
arate product. In other words, hydrogen and oxygen have a chance to meet with each
other indicating a potential safety problem. Secondly, its current density is limited
by high ohmic resistance across the electrolyte and diaphragm. The liquid electrolyte
gives rise to another limitation that AEC can not be operated under high pressure [33].
In comparison, PEMEC has some improvement. It has a solid (polymer) electrolyte
and thus it can be operated under high pressure only on the hydrogen side. This
is helpful for both hydrogen storage purpose and safety [34]. Additionally, the thin
electrolyte membrane with high proton conductivity allows higher operation current
density compared to AEC [33]. However, PEMEC also has some drawbacks, such as
using rare elements (Ir, Pt) which makes it less cost-competitive in contrast to AEC
[2], [33], [35]. The last choice is SOEC which is the least mature electrolysis technol-
ogy. SOEC needs to be operated under high temperature (650 - 1000 ◦C [2]) that can
cause high thermally-induced stress, creep and other durability problems, but such
high temperature also leads to beneﬁts. For example, owing to no evaporation heat
being needed [36] the thermal neutral voltage of SOEC is lower than PEMEC. Plus,
the kinetics is faster in SOEC than PEMEC. In order to reach 1 A/cm2, PEMEC
usually needs to be operated around 1.6 V while for SOEC current density can be up
to 1.5 A/cm2 at thermal neutral voltage 1.29 V [37]. The high current density and
lower operation voltage give an economic advantage over other electrolysis technology.
Another power of SOEC is its chemical ﬂexibility [33], [38], which allows it to electrol-
ysis CO2 to CO or co-electrolysis H2O/CO2 to syngas H2/CO. Consequently, it can
be used to recycle CO2. If problems related to long-term degradation at high tem-
perature and high current density are solved [39], [40], it has remarkable advantages
in large-scale production of hydrogen [2], [4], [33].

SOC is a complex electrochemical device that contains diﬀerent physics processes
which are coupled with each other. This interplay strongly aﬀects the performance
of SOCs such as current density distribution, temperature gradient and thermally-
induced stress. Therefore, suitable mathematical models should be implemented oth-

details as possible, several plausible simpliﬁcations can be made to alleviate the dif-

material behaviors are isotropic, such as thermal conductivity and electrical conduc-
tivity. Additionally, provided by the computational cost and convergence issue, the
manifold is excluded from the model where turbulent ﬂows can happen. However,
some assumption, such as uniform temperature over the cell [43], is not reasonable
since temperature gradient is an evident source of thermally induced stress and can
inﬂuence electrical conductivity.

3.3
Heat transfer
All three heat transfer mechanisms exist inside SOC. First, there is heat convection
in gas channels. If local thermal non-equilibrium (LTNE) in a porous medium is taken
into consideration, heat convection also exists in porous electrodes. In the solid part,
heat conduction takes place and there are heat sources generated from electrochemical
reactions and electrical resistance. Besides, radiation can be also found between solid
particles and between channel surfaces.
SOC needs to work under high temperature indicating radiation should play an
important role. However, this work does not consider radiation eﬀects. Precise cal-
culation of radiation heat transfer requires detailed values of reﬂectivity, emissivity,
spectral scattering coeﬃcients and spectral adsorption coeﬃcients. But they are de-
pendent on wavelength and temperature which makes them hard to measure. In fact,
the lack of reliable properties is the biggest impediment for including radiation in SOC
modelling [56]. Secondly, the previous research [57] has pointed out the radiation in
planar anode-support SOC (this is the geometry used in this work) could be neglected.
Providing by the above consideration, radiation heat transfer is not involved in the
model.

literature [46], [50], [58], [59]. In the following, focus will be on heat transfer inside the
porous medium. For porous medium, LTNE model is used instead of local thermal

4.4
Method of multiscale modelling
The homogenized stack model provides an overview of distributions of physical
ﬁelds over the stack such as temperature, pressure, current density, stress, etc. They
are macroscopic physical quantities, and cannot reﬂect the distributions of physical
ﬁelds in cells locally as the active area of the stack is considered as a whole, i.e. a
porous medium, see [15]. To examine the speciﬁc response of cells e.g. mechanical
response, a model with detailed geometry of the cell is needed. However, a stack may
consists of multiple cells. Therefore, it is very computationally expensive if a fully
detailed stack model is used. To capture the physical ﬁelds in cells more accurately
while maintaining the computational eﬃciency of the model, a coupling scheme is
presented in this work. The physical quantities obtained in the homogenized stack
model can be applied as the boundary conditions on the detailed model as shown in
the following procedures:
1. Run the homogenized stack model.
2. Based on which cell in the stack needs to be analyzed, output the corresponding
data of displacement and temperature from the homogenized stack model.
3. Input the above data of displacement and temperature to the detailed model
in order to calculate the total strain and the thermally induced strain. The
mechanical strain can be determined according to Eq. 3.36.
4. Impose boundary load for the cell (e.g., the bottom cell should be applied an
external force due to gravity from all other cells above it). Stress can be obtained
through Hooke’s law.

4.6
Numerical settings
Models in this work are built up in commercialized software COMSOL Multi-
physics 5.6. Taking into account the fact that physical variables are strongly coupled
with each other, the fully coupled approach instead of the segregated approach is
adopted to solve models. A direct solver, PARDISO, is used to solve the linear system.
Newton iterations are applied to solve non-linear systems with a relative tolerance of
×10−4.
There are 1052920 and 380000 mesh elements for the SRU model and the ho-
mogenized stack model (4-SOCs stack) respectively. The number of mesh elements
are determined after mesh independence is achieved.
Higher mesh density in the
SRU model is due to J-integral calculation. Because path independence of J-integral
should be realized, extremely ﬁne mesh at sealants is necessary for the SRU model.
All models are solved on a workstation with dual Intel Xeon 5218 with 2x16 CPU
cores and 1 TB RAM. The whole calculation is divided into three steps:
1. Calculate all physics ﬁelds simultaneously except the solid mechanics.
2. Use the result of the temperature ﬁeld in the ﬁrst step and calculate solid me-
chanics without cracks.
3. Again use the result of the temperature ﬁeld in the ﬁrst step and calculate
variables in solid mechanics with cracks.
The above treatment is beneﬁcial to accelerating calculation as a consequence of sep-
arating calculation of solid mechanics and other physics.

5.3.1
Failure probability and J-integral at RT
Table 5.3 gives failure probabilities at RT. Compared to failure probabilities in the
OP case shown by Table 5.2, failure probabilities under RT are enormously increased.
This indicates SOC is vulnerable to the thermal cycling. Particularly, sealants exhibit
much higher failure probabilities than FELS, FEL and ELEC. J-integral of the RT
case, as given in Figure 5.6, is also evidently increased compared to the OP case.
Among the three positions, position 1 has the highest J-integral.
The increasing
failure probabilities and J-integral probably arise from a higher temperature diﬀerence
between the reference temperature (around 800 ◦C) and the operation temperature
(25 ◦C) in contrast to the OP case.
In conclusion, the failure is more likely to happen under RT compared to under
OP. To investigate the impact of material properties on failure under RT, three cases
are present in the following: case A) with diﬀerent initial crack length, case B) with
diﬀerent sealants TEC and case C) with diﬀerent FELS TEC.

increasing initial crack length, J-integral at position 1 increases at the beginning but

As a comparison, J-integral at position 3 shows little variation.

are close to each other, although H2O mole fraction in the SRU model is slightly
higher the homogenized model.
The diﬀerence of mole fraction is linked to the diﬀerence of current density as
given by Figure 6.1(g) and 6.1(h). In spite of the same average current density 1,
some diﬀerences are found locally. To begin with, the highest current density in the
SRU model is higher than the homogenized model which explains the corresponding
higher H2O mole fraction. Plus, current density in the SRU model has a plunge as a
consequence of the FEL area being larger than the OEL area (see Figure 4.1(b)). In
the homogenized mode, this detailed structure is ignored as illustrated in Figure 4.2
and thus current density after homogenization is more uniform. Despite the above
diﬀerence, current density distribution of both models in the active electrode area is
similar to each other.
In brief, the homogenized model shows good agreement in temperature, mole frac-
tion and current density with the SRU model, but there is distinguishment in me-
chanical performance between both because the homogenized model neglects the ﬁne
structures. This is one major driving force behind multiscale modelling.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
In this work, a multiphysics model of the SOC with detailed geometry is present
on the single cell level (SRU model). Owing to its detailed geometry, the model is
believed to give more accurate results compared to the model only containing a simple
sandwich structure. Furthermore, a homogenized model is used to study SOCs on the
stack level. However, due to missing ﬁne structures, it gives less reliable results of solid
mechanics compared to the SRU model. To solve this problem, multiscale modelling
is implemented whose core idea is to input results of the homogenized stack model
into the SRU model as boundary conditions.
First of all, the SRU model is carried out on the single cell stack level. A SOC stack
model with detailed geometry is built up and its mechanical performance is analyzed.
The notable discovery in this work is when the stack is under operation, FELS, FEL,
ELEC, AS and FS are almost impossible to suﬀer failure, as failure probability and
J-integral are quite low. In addition, The most fragile area for the sealant is located
at the ﬂuid inlet edge. This ﬁnding is consistent with experimental results, cf. [98].
In contrast to OP, it is more likely for the stack to go through failure when it is cooled
down to RT. A parametric study shows sealants TEC should be close to 12×10−6 1/K
in order to decrease their failure probability.
Secondly, a comparison is discussed between the SRU model and the homogenized
model on the single cell level.
It shows average values of physical variables, such
as temperature, mole fraction and stress tensor, are similar. This demonstrates the
homogenized idea works successfully.
Thirdly, the failure behavior is studied on the stack level through multiscale mod-
elling. It is found that MC shows a higher failure probability and J-integral compare
to BC and TC. The safest position of the stack is its bottom and the author at-
tribute it to the higher compressive load generated by the gravity of upward cells. To
increase the credibility of the multiscale modelling, mapping displacement from the
homogenized model is advised to be avoided.
