Title: Risk assessment of Automatic Train Control systems: HAZOP and semiformal

Summary: 
As the thesis title - Risk assessment of Automatic Train Control systems: HAZOP and 
semiformal analysis - reveals, the project deals with the risk assessment of ATC 
systems. Specifically, the main focus is on the hazard identification which is the first 
stage of the risk assessment process and is considered as the cornerstone for the 
efficiency of each risk analysis.  
The main objective is to capture the most suitable method for the hazard 
identification of ATC systems, in terms of completeness, comprehensiveness and 
accuracy. Having examined many relevant papers and applications as well as most 
of the developed hazard identification methods and all relevant standards, the 
HAZOP method was selected for study as it provides flexibility and usability in 
different engineering fields. 
While conventional HAZOP provides really good results in other engineering 
industries, its application on railways, and especially on advanced train control 
systems, is challenging. The main reason is the use of guidewords for the 
identification of deviations of the system, process, components etc. Towards that 
purpose, the use of both guideword and deviation checklists, as the basis of the 
analysis, is investigated in terms of completeness and accuracy in accident scenario 
description.  
Moreover, Cause Consequence Analysis is used to identify and evaluate the possible 
outcomes of the identified initiating events. Finally, a semiformal method is used to 
place the failure modes in the operational context. The results of this method are 
combined with the failure modes of the HAZOP. 
In order to test the methodology, the aforementioned technique was applied at a 
modern train control system. The system that was chosen for that purpose is the 
CBTC. Since, no detailed specification data were available, the architecture and 
configuration of the six main suppliers were examined and a reference system was 
generated by the most used components and functionalities. The functions and 
their allocation occurred by the IEEE 1474 std. 
The result of the project is a hazard identification method that fulfils all efficiency 
criteria and is able to be efficiently used for every ATC system.

Abstract 
 
Train accidents can cause catastrophic impacts to people, infrastructure and environment. To prevent 
these impacts, while also achieving a high level of robustness and operability, Automatic Train Control 
(ATC) systems have been introduced. Since these systems affect and deal with safety issues, they 
should be considered as safety-critical and developed under safety-related regulations. According to 
the standards that deal with the safety requirements of railways and all other safety-critical systems, 
ATC systems shall be assessed under risk analysis at all phases.  
Modern ATC systems introduced the use of high complexity functionalities based on hardware 
equipment and software as well as advanced communication technology. Although these 
technological advantages increased the operability and reliability of railways, they also increased the 
need for structured and systematic risk assessment, as well the complexity of such processes. The 
cornerstone and one of the biggest challenges of the overall process is the hazard identification, 
leading to the main objective of the present thesis: to capture the most suitable hazard identification 
method for ATC systems and optimize its process. 
In the present case, the term ‘’suitable’’ is interpreted as complete, comprehensive and accurate. 
Towards that aim, many relevant papers, applications, methods and variations have been examined. 
The result of this research led to the selection of HAZOP because of its proven flexibility and usability 
in other fields. In the present project, alongside the conventional guideword-based HAZOP, an 
additional, based on a new deviation checklist, was applied on a generated CBTC reference system. 
This new deviation check list was developed based on dimensional analysis of the ATC systems. 
Moreover, characteristic examples of human error and cause consequence analysis applications were 
provided. Finally, the HAZOP process was related to a formal representation of the rail system, as a 
set of state machines with guard conditions on state transitions. 
The results of this analysis identify and highlight the comparative benefits and limitations of each 
method while facilitate further improvements and applications. Finally, a refined HAZOP approach, 
specified for ATC systems, is proposed.

Chapter 1.  
Introduction 
 
Since the beginning of the railway revolution, in the early seventeenth century, people have realized 
the huge loss that derive from train accidents. Either they concern freight or, especially, passenger 
trains. Any accident from a derailment to a train-to-train collision, can potentially cause catastrophic 
results. Over the years, the intensification of rail operations combined with the heavy dependence of 
the whole network on every single train movement increased also the losses occurred not only by rail 
accidents but also by the inefficient rail operation.  
Towards that purpose, railway signalling systems and operating rules were introduced and applied. 
Railway signalling systems, consist of various mechanical, electrical and electronic equipment that, as 
a whole, control the movement of trains. Correspondingly, the operating rules define how these 
physical equipment should be used. By then, the safe operation of most railway networks depends 
heavily on the implemented signalling system and the correspondingly applied operating rules. 
The increasing demand for heavier traffic movements, at higher speeds, combined with increased 
safety, led to major developments in the signalling and train control technology [1]. Thus, the concept 
of Automatic Train Control (ATC) describes the architecture of automatically operated railway that 
reduces the human involvement while includes various train protection functions. So, implementing 
and using such systems, safety issues and staff costs are reduced while trains services are improved. 
Obviously, such systems are considered as safety-critical because of the high operating speed, the 
many on board passengers and the catastrophic consequences that their failure may lead to. 
Nowadays, thanks to the technological development in different engineering fields, various ATC 
systems have been developed and implemented in different railway networks around the world. 
According to the technical, operational and safety requirements of each specific application, the 
provided functionalities of these systems can be extended from simple Automatic Train Protection 
(ATP) functions to Automatic Train Operation (ATO) and Automatic Train Supervision (ATS) functions. 
Most advanced ATC technologies make use of telecommunications for traffic management and 
infrastructure control.  
Such traffic management systems can be divided according to the networks that are applied at. In 
Europe, Communications-Based Train Control (CBTC) systems are commonly used in mass transit 
networks while European Rail Traffic Management System (ERTMS) is the European Union proposal 
for mainlines.   
Likewise in other engineering fields that concern life and environmental threats as well as substantial 
financial losses, in modern train control and traffic management systems, a systematic and complete 
risk assessment process is necessary to ensure the predefined safety and reliability requirements. 
Obviously, the high complexity of such safety-critical systems increases also the complexity of the risk 
assessment process.  
The first step in this process is the hazard identification stage where all potential hazards are identified 
and ranked according to their severity and frequency of occurrence while they are also linked with 
their corresponding causes.

1.1. Problem statement 
As mentioned above, Automatic Train Control (ATC) is a system used to facilitate and ensure the safe 
and efficient operation of trains. Towards that goal, standardized and additional - case specific - 
system safety requirements are applied to all ATC systems. According to the international and most 
of the national standards, these safety requirements shall always be in accordance with the hazard 
control of the system for the overall lifecycle of the project. The cornerstone of the hazard control 
efficiency is the systematic hazard identification.  
During the years, various techniques have been used for the hazard identification of ATC and signalling 
systems. Checklists, Preliminary Hazard Analysis (PHA), Failure Mode Effect Analysis (FMEA) and 
Hazard and Operability study (HAZOP) are some - and probably the most applied - of them. Although 
these methods have been efficiently used in other engineering industries and recommended by 
international standards for railways (e.g. IEC), there are problems in their application to ATC systems. 
Checklists, a way of capturing the existing experience of hazard occurrence, may not cover all potential 
hazards and should be only used for preliminary hazard studies and as a final check, ensuring that 
there are no quality deficiencies in the results of the applied analysis. PHA which aims at hazard 
identification and is mainly carried out on an early stage of the project design - when information, 
details and operating procedures are not explicitly defined - does not lead to detailed hazard 
information. The results are not comprehensive and mitigation measures are not provided. FMEA and 
its extension FMECA (Failure Mode, Effects and Critically Analysis) techniques are widely used to 
identify how systems, processes and components can possibly fail to fulfil their design intent. Although 
these methods are largely applicable to system, human, hardware, software and procedure failure 
modes, while their causes and consequences on the overall system are sufficiently presented, they fail 
to provide combinations of failure modes. They can also be costly and time consuming as well as 
deficient to be used in multi-layered systems like ATC [2].  
Alongside FMEA, HAZOP seems to be the most used and recommended method for the hazard 
identification of railway and ATC systems. Although in principle the proper application of this 
technique seems to be able to fulfil all the risk assessment requirements, considerable issues occur in 
practice. The main issue derives from the use of guidewords that in some cases leads to incomplete 
and nonsensical results as well as overlaps in the identified deviations. This overlaps make the method 
even more time-consuming and therefore even more expensive while at the same time giving a result 
which can be confusing, and in which gaps may be difficult to identify.   
Finally, although some applications of formal methods for the specification and verification of 
signalling systems have been made, no development towards the combination with hazard 
identification methods could be found. Such combination would help to keep track of the large 
number of initial states of the system while also identifying the wide range of items that can go wrong.

1.2. Research objectives 
The origin of this work was the observation that existing risk analysis methods, which are standard in 
other engineering fields, could certainly be applied to railways. The HAZOP method was selected for 
study because of its proven flexibility and usability in other fields. 
The literature review, revealed the several applications of the HAZOP method to various railway 
systems, including interlocking, signalling and ATC systems (more details about these applications exist 
in the next chapter). However, this also showed the significant differences in the scenarios identified 
and the extent of the causes and consequences reported.  Thus, the research objectives of the present 
thesis are: 
• 
To investigate whether the guidewords and deviation checklists, used as a basis for a HAZOP 
analysis, result in significant differences in competence and accuracy in the accident scenario 
description.  
In order to provide answers to these questions, several experiments were carried out and a limited 
(comparative) validation study was performed. The HAZOP method, using both guidewords and 
deviation checklists was applied on a reference system in order to investigate the differences in the 
identified accident scenario description. The selected system was the CBTC. Thus, another objective 
that derived is: 
• 
To carry out a full scale HAZOP study for the safety-critical components of the CBTC system. 
According to the identified previous work, there is no such full scale, publicly available and accessible, 
study, not only using the HAZOP method but using any hazard identification technique. Such study, 
requires a high degree of comprehension of the operational procedures, functions and safety 
requirements of the investigated system.  
Moreover, as an expected finding from the studies, it was found that the HAZOP method used could 
be related to formal methods which have been used to prove safety properties and identify logical 
design errors in interlockings and other railway systems. Thus, a further objective is to: 
• 
Determine whether the HAZOP method (which is termed as an informal method) could be 
used as part of formal analysis to derive safety requirements; and 
• 
To determine whether HAZOP could be more intimately combined with formal methods. 
Summarizing, the general objective of this master thesis has been to optimize the ATC hazard 
identification process, particularly from the point of view of completeness and accuracy of analysis, 
and determine if the selected HAZOP method could be intimately combined with formal methods.

Specifically, the main focus is on the hazard identification of such systems. The report consists of 11 
chapters. Chapter 1 outlines the problem statement and the objectives of the current research.

HAZOP and human error worksheets and the cause-consequence diagrams.

Chapter 2.  
Background and previous work 
 
2.1. Automatic Train Control (ATC) 
Although the specific definition of Automatic Train Control1 (ATC) varies around the world and the 
existing applications, the term generally describes the concept where machines regulate the train 
movement in favour of safety, security and efficiency. Thus, ATC is an integrated signalling system that 
assures safety in the train operations and includes all corresponding automatic functions. In most 
cases, such systems encompass the ATP and ATO packages, while ATS is usually also considered as 
part of the whole.  ATP subsystem is responsible for the safety of the train movements, ATO for their 
automatic operation and ATS for their external supervision. Specifically: 
Automatic Train Protection (ATP) is considered as the system that constantly ensures safety in railway 
operations by preventing accident occurrences due to functional or operational failures. Towards that 
goal, they include elements and functions that enforce obedience to signals and speed restrictions by 
applying speed supervision measurements. Except of the train speed, its position, in relation to the 
end of the provided movement authority (MA) and other relative data are also taken into account. 
Such systems can be distinguished in continuous and intermittent ATP according to the way the 
information are transferred from track to train. 
Automatic Train Operation (ATO) refers to the system that works under the protection of the ATP 
system and executes the partial or full automatic operation of trains. Thus, this system is not 
considered as safety-critical while it includes functions for the starting, cruising and precisely stopping 
of trains. Additional functions related to the door operation and other controls can be also included. 
According to the required human involvement and the way of operation, five distinguished 
automation levels exist, ranging from on-sight to unattended train operation. In most cases, ATP is 
used on rapid transit networks while the highest grade of automation is avoided in order to reduce 
the risks of failures and handle emergency issues.  
Automatic Train Supervision (ATS) is the system responsible for monitoring the status of the system 
and providing the necessary controls needed to maintain the intended traffic schedule and patterns. 
The main aim of the system is to optimize the railway operations and the reliability of services.   
Since the early 20th century, many different systems have been referred as ‘’automatic train control’’. 
Initially these systems used intermittent communication while the provided features and 
functionalities were restricted. Nowadays, thanks to technological development in railway, 
telecommunication and other engineering fields, much more advanced train control as well as 
signalling systems have been introduced and implemented. One of the greatest achievements in the 
signalling field is the introduction of moving-block systems that in some cases replaced the fixed-block 
ones. Moreover, continuous railway communication has been also established. Two of the most 
advanced and implemented train control systems that make use of such developments is the ERTMS, 
standardized for the European mainlines, and the CBTC, which is widely used in urban rail lines.

2.2. Risk assessment for ATC 
Risk assessment is the iterative process which provides information that decisions about safety issues 
are based on. In principle, these information concern the assessment and comparison of the harmful 
operational risks that may occur by the realisation of the project against the ones that will occur if the 
project will not be realized. Thus, risk assessment concerns the systematic analysis of the possible 
single and aggregated losses derived from the analysed project as well as their likelihood, severity and 
mitigation measures. The results of this analysis are used as input to risk reduction and feedback on 
its efficiency throughout the lifecycle of the analysed system. Obviously, risk assessment is connected 
with hazard identification and risk reduction. According to EN 3010 [3], risk assessment can be divided 
into three major stages - risk identification, risk analysis and risk evaluation – that continuously 
contribute to the overall risk management process.  
 
Figure 1 Risk management process [3] 
 
Risk assessment can use qualitative, quantitative or hybrid approaches. Qualitative approaches are 
mainly used for non-harmful risks while quantitative for the significant ones. Although these 
approaches are based on the same fundamental principles, they differ in sense of the depth of 
analysis, the required inputs and outputs as well as the systems and phases that their application is 
required. Qualitative risk assessment is mainly based on the experience of the experts and does not 
require high level of system details or data. On the other hand, quantitative assessment relies upon 
more analytical processes, requires much more data and is more complex and expensive. Such 
assessment is usually applied on novel systems with a corresponding wide range of novel potential 
hazards. 
Likewise other systems where hazards may lead to harmful consequences to people, infrastructure 
and environment, railway systems, and especially their safety-critical parts like the ATC, the signaling 
and the interlocking systems, require complete and systematic risk assessment in order to ensure 
safety, reliability and operability.

Although various approaches, structures and methods are used for risk assessment within the railway 
industry, specific standards have been developed in order to provide guidance for the application of 
safety requirements and activities during the lifecycle of the system. One of the most popular 
standards that deals with risk assessment for railways is the EN 50126 [4]. EN 50126 defines risk 
assessment as the process that ‘’addresses the identification of hazards, evaluation of risks and a 
judgement on the tolerability of the risks where as risk management involves identification and 
implementation of cost effective risk control measures and assurance that resources are diligently 
applied to control and maintain risk at acceptable levels’’ [4]. The distinctive risk assessment steps are 
illustrated below. 
 
Figure 2 Risk Assessment process [4] 
 
The system definition concerns all the technical components of the analysed system, its functions, 
the interactions with all involved humans as well as its physical and logical boundaries and interfaces. 
Thus, the intended system functionalities are included alongside the interfaces with external systems 
and the environment that set the boundaries of the system. The environment includes all the 
components or systems that are connected or interface with the analysed one as well as the people 
and processes that affect or are affected by it.  Moreover, the operational modes and the external 
requirements are also included in the system definition and corresponding documentation.  
Hazard Identification concerns the identification and ranking of potential hazards that may occur in 
the analysed system. Obviously, this stage is very crucial for the risk assessment and the overall 
efficiency of safety assurance. Different systematic hazard identification techniques exist, all ensuring 
that all relevant functions, processes and operating modes are carefully analysed and documented. 
The results, at railway system level, define a set of credible “c-hazards” of different severity levels [4]. 
All identified hazards are linked with their corresponding causes and ranked according to their risk 
severity. 
Hazard Log is a fundamental risk management tool, used to record the hazards identified in the 
previous stage and the potential accidents and incidents while also describe the taken or 
recommended mitigation measures. This document is updated during the system’s lifecycle with 
further identified hazards. Many different writing forms exist for the hazard logs.

When hazards are identified, it is crucial to identify also the consequences that they may lead to. This 
is achieved by the consequence analysis where the progression of the pre-identified hazardous events 
is assessed and their possible effects are gathered and documented. In this stage, the intermediate 
events should be identified and quantified in a way that the individual sequences, actions, and possible 
effects of the hazards are represented in a clear, comprehensible and operationally relevant manner 
[4]. Thus, diagrammatic representations are commonly applied. The likelihood of occurrence of every 
incident that may follow the realization of each hazard is also estimated and provided in the final 
results. Consequence analysis may be made in a quantitative or qualitative manner. The qualitative 
analysis aims to identify the progression of events that may follow a hazard and may lead to an 
accident or a safe system state. The quantitative analysis focuses on the probability of occurrence of 
these intermediate states. Event Tree Analysis (ETA) and Cause Consequence Diagram are the widely 
used methods for such purpose.   
When the consequence analysis is completed, the identified risks are evaluated and assessed against 
predefined safety acceptance criteria in order to determine the implementation of the necessary 
mitigation measures and extra safeguards. These acceptance criteria are defined as Tolerable Hazard 
Rates (THRs) and are usually agreed by the corresponding responsible bodies. Thus, THRs define the 
rates of occurrence and tolerability of the losses of the hazards that would lead on acceptable risk 
levels for them [4]. The results of the hazard identification process and the THRs allocation are used 
as input for the hazard control.  
Although the aforementioned risk assessment steps as well as hazard control concern different 
aspects of the project lifecycle, they are and should be considered as inter-related.  
As mentioned above, the present thesis project focuses on hazard identification, so in the next 
sections this stage alongside the most used and recommended techniques are further analyzed. 
2.3. Hazard Identification 
A systematic and comprehensive Hazard Identification stage is considered as fundamental for the 
efficiency of the overall risk assessment process and safety assurance. With a low quality of hazard 
identification, the remaining stages of hazard assessment become meaningless. The hazard 
identification results shall ensure that all aspects of the analysed system are considered for all 
processes and operating modes during its overall lifecycle. When hazards are identified, their causes 
should be determined. Moreover, the severity and likelihood of occurrence for all of them should be 
assessed, leading to a systematic rank according to the numerical estimated risks associated with 
them.  
2.3.1. Hazard definition 
In order to identify all hazards, the definition of hazard should be first clearly provided. Different 
organisations provided different definitions for hazard. One of the most adapted ones is the one of 
European CENELEC norms EN 50126-1 where hazard is defined as ‘’a physical situation with a potential 
for human injury’’ [4]. Obviously, this definition only refers to hazards that may potentially lead to 
human injury and not to losses of material values or environmental damages. This limitation is 
overcome by the EN 50129 that defines as hazard ‘’a condition that could lead to an accident’’ [5]. 
Correspondingly, according to the European Railway Safety Directive, ‘’accident means an unwanted 
or unintended sudden event or a specific chain of such events which have harmful consequences’’ while 
‘’serious accident means any train collision or derailment of trains, resulting in the death of at least 
one person or serious injuries to five or more persons or extensive damage to rolling stock, the 
infrastructure or the environment, and any other similar accident with an obvious impact on railway 
safety regulation or the management of safety’’ [6].

2.3.2. Hazard Identification methods 
Over the years, a number of specific tools and methods for hazard identification has been developed. 
Although most of the have some common features (searching for causes and consequences etc.) there 
are considerable deviations that effect their efficiency. Moreover, different techniques are more 
suitable for specific applications, phases, provided inputs and required outcomes. 
Concerning the risk analysis of railway systems, the most used and widely adapted standards are IEC 
50126 [4] and Yellow Book [7]. Both of them set the hazard identification as the basic stage of the Risk 
Assessment phase. According to these standards, this stage shall include both empirical and creative 
hazard identification methods in order to ensure that all significant hazards are identified. The 
empirical hazard identification techniques include checklists, structure walkthroughs or Failure Mode 
and Effect Analysis (FMEA) tools. The creative techniques, include brainstorming and HAZOP proving 
a much more systematic approach. Moreover, in accordance with the Yellow Book, a task analysis 
shall be conducted and used for the identification of human error factors. Thus, a modified HAZOP, 
FMEA or FMECA method shall be also used to handle failures occurred by human errors.

Empirical hazard identification methods 
Hazard Identification Checklist is a structured approach that achieves to capture the previous 
knowledge and is widely used for preliminary hazard identification. They typically consist of a list of 
already known hazards or possible hazard causes derived from previous risk assessments or incidents 
that have occurred on similar systems or operations. This list can be applied to the overall system, a 
subsystem or single elements according to the volume and complexity of their functions. Obviously, 
no checklist can be regarded as totally exhaustive and its level of competence relies heavily on the 
experience and system knowledge of the analyst while their efficiency is very limited when it deals 
with novel systems.   
Task Analysis in not a particular method but a systematic methodology that includes different human 
factor techniques. The term is widely used for the processes that aim to identify the knowledge, skills 
and behavioural requirements that are necessary for each task [8]. Task Analysis methods, like 
Cognitive Task Analysis (CTA) or Hierarchical Task Analysis (HTA), are often used for risk assessment 
of human interactions in railway and other industries. Specifically for railways, task analysis can be 
really useful for the analysis of human-machine interfaces, human factors and procedural errors as 
well as the identification of hazards associated with them. The methods can be applied to working 
environments (e.g. CTC) or specific human task while the result can include hazardous human errors, 
procedure failures etc. Thus, such techniques are appropriate for dominant human errors or 
procedural activities that may be against safe operation [9].  
Creative hazard identification methods 
Hazard and Operability (HAZOP) study is a structured qualitative technique used to identify and 
evaluate risks to people, equipment, environment and/or organisational objectives of a planned or 
existing process or operation. It is based on the use of guide words that show how the design intention 
might not be achieved and lead to undesired effects that concern safety or operability issues. The 
output of the method is the identification of the failure modes of analysed system or process as well 
as their causes and consequences. It was initially developed for and applied to chemical systems but 
it has been also extended to other industries. Recently, studies have been carried out for its 
application to more complex operations and software systems like modern signalling and train control 
systems.   
10 | P a g e  
 
==PAGE:25==
Failure Mode and Effect Analysis (FMEA) is a structured analysis used to study the failures and their 
corresponding failure modes of the components or features of the analysed system, as well as the 
causes and consequences of these failures. Thus, in the beginning the failure modes of every element 
are identified and then their causes and consequences to a wider subsystem or the overall system are 
checked. The size of the analysed single elements can vary from a single physical component to a much 
larger system. Then, the identified failure effects repeatedly become failure modes at the higher 
functional hierarchy level leading the final failure modes of the overall system. The initial failure modes 
can be identified by HAZOP or other hazard identification methods. An extension of FMEA is the 
Failure Mode Effects and Critically Analysis (FMECA) where the criticality analysis is included and used 
to classify the probability of the failure modes against the severity of their consequences [10].   
Brainstorming is mostly combined with other methods at several phases of the life cycle of a system. 
Although the term is often used to generally describe the discussion between people of knowledge, 
there are specific techniques that should be followed in order to encourage imaginative thinking and 
free-flowing conversation. Such technique is useful for the hazard identification of novel systems 
when no data or past experience is available. According to the case and the output requirements, 
formal or informal approaches can be applied.  
Structured What If analysis (SWIFT) makes use of checklists of possible failures and other events (such 
as loss of power, bad weather etc.) to identify potential problems. Direct questions are asked about 
the occurrence of these failures or events and their consequences. The quality of a SWIFT analysis 
depends heavily on the quality of the checklist used. So, a high quality analysis requires that there 
have been earlier detailed assessments such as HAZOP or epidemiological studies. Its usefulness arises 
from its generally more limited use of resources - in terms of time and people – and the more limited 
reliance on credibility and imagination than other methods. It also has a useful property that 
extraneous conditions, not obviously related to the under study system, can be taken naturally into 
account (e.g. what are the possible effects of an earthquake on an ATC system)?. 
If an extensive what if checklist is used (as it is necessary for a complete analysis), it will be necessary 
to pre-process the checklist prior to an analysis in order to delete irrelevant questions.   
 
Other methods 
Formal methods are mathematically-based techniques mainly used for the specification, 
development and verification of software and hardware systems [11]. These techniques can 
substantially contribute to the robustness and reliability of a design while also provide confidence in 
the security of the system. IEC 50126 describes Formal Methods as a ‘’useful for analysing logics’’ 
technique for hazard control and fulfilment of safety targets [4]. For hazard identification, formal 
methods can be used to identify failures, malfunctions and human errors that can contribute to or 
form a hazard. It should be mentioned that formal methods cannot ensure that the specification of 
the system is correct but only verify that they are fulfilled. Formal methods require that ‘’safety 
requirement predicates’’ are specified at the outset, and which can be proved to be satisfied by the 
design. The methods therefore depend on at least some initial hazard identification.  
Fault Tree Analysis (FTA) requires that the analyst first identifies a ‘’top event’’ which is an undesired 
consequence of system erroneous function or of failure. The method then follows as a top down 
construction process where the causes the may lead to this failure are stated and connected with AND 
and OR gates. This process requires previous high understanding of the investigated system. The FTA 
is therefore not a complete approach for hazard identification. It requires an initial step to identify the 
11 | P a g e  
 
==PAGE:26==
important ‘’top events’’ and this can be done for example by using HAZOP. FTA can however be used 
to extend the initial hazard identification by tracing long or complex causal chains which result in a 
hazardous state or undesired consequence. This applies particularly if there is extensive use of 
redundancy in the examined system.  
Event Tree Analysis (ETA) is strictly a notation which can be used to diagram alternative outcomes of 
the triggering of a hazard. It is useful as a supportive tool for HAZOP is cases where there are many 
possible causes.  
Cause-Consequence Analysis (CCA) is a technique used to identify and analyze the potential 
consequence chains that derive from an undesired initiating event and estimate their probabilities. 
These probabilities are estimated according to the probabilities of occurrence of the conditions that 
follow the initiating event. A Cause-Consequence Diagram is developed from an initiation event (e.g. 
a failure) and fault trees that show the causes of this event. The output of the analysis is a 
diagrammatic representation of the failure logic of the analyzed system that includes both the causes 
and consequences. 
Rapid Ranking Analysis (RRA) is mainly used in a preliminary phase to provide a classification of the 
hazards associated to their significance. Although it is not efficiently applicable on complex systems, 
it can provide confidence that hazards with high estimated risk are captured. According to the risk 
severity and frequency of occurrence, a hazard-ranking matrix is often generated and used.  
In order to provide, in a formal way, an efficient system overview that includes all critical states, State 
Transition Diagram (STD) is used. By applying this method, these system states are represented in a 
graphical form, their transitions can be analysed and the sequence of events will occur if the necessary 
conditions are not fulfilled can be identified.   
There are many human reliability analysis methods which have been published and researched since 
the early 1970’s. Only a few of these however are focused on identification of human error 
possibilities. Of these Action Error Analysis [12]  [13] [14] is applicable in a team workshop in a similar 
way to HAZOP, and covers both the error modes and deviations from design intent, and also the error 
causality. Human HAZOP [15] corresponds to the error mode part of action error analysis.

2.4. HAZOP for ATC and signalling systems 
As stated above, the focus of the current thesis is to capture the most suitable, for ATC systems, hazard 
identification method. Due to the proved flexibility and applicability, the HAZOP technique is chosen 
to be studied further analysed. The confidence of its potentially efficient application is increased by 
the recommendations of the corresponding standards as well as the different variations, papers and 
applications that exist. 
HAZOP is characterized as a ‘’useful’’ method for hazard identification and a ‘’partially useful as 
supporting element’’ method for hazard control in IEC 50126 [4].  In IEC 62425 [5] and Yellow Book 
[7], it is categorized as highly recommended for railways systems’ hazard analysis. Although this 
method is widely and efficiently used for identification of risks in other industries, its exact application 
to railway signalling systems seems not equally efficient.  
One of the first attempts to apply the HAZOP method to a signalling system was made by J.B. Camargo 
et al, in 2006 [16]. Focusing particularly on the CBTC signalling system, a HAZOP method is applied 
over some of the most crucial signals of the system like the maximum-speed and the position of the 
train. The guide words applied to these signals obtained by the HAZOP to PES (Programmable 
Electronic System) approach. Thus, some initial conceptual hazards were identified and the 
corresponding recommendations were stated.     
J. B. Camargo attributed this lack of efficiency to the suggested parameters and guidewords that are 
not suitable for the railway systems [17].  Thus, maintaining the approach of the traditional HAZOP 
technique, the proposed HAZOP-R (HAZOP for Railways) method includes new parameters (e.g. 
interface, time, action etc.) and guidewords generated specifically for train control systems.  The 
defined deviations occur by the combination of these parameters with the corresponding guidewords 
applied to both software and hardware safety activities. This method is greatly based on previous 
studies of HAZOP methods generated by and applied to the train control system of the Korean 
Railways. HAZOP-KR (HAZOP for Korean Railway) is proposed by Jong-Gyu Hwang [18] as the most 
suitable method of Preliminary Hazard Identification during a safety activity procedure. 
In 2009, a new approach called ‘’Functional Safety Analysis Approach’’ was introduced [19]. According 
to the proposal, in the first step of the process, the functions are divided and the inputs and outputs 
of them are clearly stated. Then the implementation processes are described according to different 
sub-functions. For the hazard identification, the HAZOP method is used. Guidewords are adjusted to 
the output signals leading to the identification of the hazards. According to the input signals and using 
FTA and FMECA the causes of these hazards are traced, and the risk of the systematic hazards is 
assessed. Finally, a safety analysis of the identified hazards is carried out and a safety case is 
developed. The main strengths of this method is that according to the identified failure modes, the 
cause roots are easily traced, the causes, consequences and functions are clearly related while the 
probability of occurrence is derived by the use of FTA.  
A high promising attempt to deal with the drawbacks related to ambiguity, incompleteness, 
nonsensicality and redundancy of the application of conventional HAZOP to railway systems has been 
made by Deutsche Bahn AG (DB) [20]. The assessors of the company, aiming to provide a structured 
framework that can automatically generate questions and reduce the requested high amount of time 
and costs, use a modification of HAZOP and CHAZOP. The parameters are differentiated by being split 
into three categories: objects, actions and attributes. Objects and attributes are further separated into 
immaterial (e.g. program) and material (e.g. train), and numerical, descriptive and binary, respectively. 
Some objects can be further divided into sub-objects that all have attributes. These parameters are 
combined with guide words providing five different logical, mathematical and structural 
13 | P a g e  
 
==PAGE:28==
interpretations. Finally, missing interrelations between expressions are overcome by extending the 
guide word set by additional corresponding guide words. This method aims to omit the time 
consuming HAZOP meetings by avoiding the ambiguity of the provided expressions.  
The aforementioned method has been further developed by J. Schulze [21]. In his master thesis, the 
set of questions is extended to actions. According to the provided definition, these actions need an 
agent object that executes them while, in accordance with the outcome, other objects, like sources, 
destinations or instruments, may be necessary. Finally, time parameters, preconditions and post-
conditions should be fulfilled. Thus, the model for actions provide a much more structured approach 
addressing the issue of finding HAZOP deviations. Although this approach seems promising, especially 
if it will be automated, there are further steps that should be made to complete it. Functions and 
events as well as the corresponding guidewords should be added, while the lack of automation makes 
the method really time consuming when compared with other approaches.

Methodology 
 
In order to capture the best hazard identification practise for ATC systems, many relevant papers, 
approaches and applications were studied, most of the developed hazard identification methods were 
considered while the recommendations of all relevant standards were also taken into consideration.  
Although over the years empirical approaches were mainly used for the hazard and risk detection of 
the electrical and mechanical train control systems, the introduction of computerized signaling 
systems in railways highlighted the need for more structured and systematic approaches. Towards 
that aim, some of the aforementioned hazard identification methods have been tested or applied, 
leading to various results.  
A widely used hazard identification approach, for the most advanced ATC systems, is the hazard 
checklist. Many different authorities and organizations provide assorted checklists, varying from small 
and generic to extensive and specific ones. Although this method captures the longstanding 
experience on railway hazards and their causes while ensures that common problems will not be 
forgotten, they limit the analysts’ imagination and miss hazards that emerge from new technologies. 
PHA is also widely used for new railway projects, but it leads only to preliminary results. So, it should 
be only applied on the early project development, when design details and operational specifications 
are not clearly defined, and be followed by more extensive techniques. A technique that can be really 
efficient for the identification of hazards of train control systems is FMEA (and FMECA). Although 
currently there is little application on ATC, its extensive application in other systems makes it 
promising. The main drawbacks of the method is that it does not identify combinations of failure 
modes while it can be difficult to be applied on multi-layered systems like the most advanced ATC 
systems.   
On the other hand, HAZOP method seems to be able to overcome most of these limitations. Its proper 
application can trigger the people´s imagination while its wide usability and flexibility makes it able to 
be applied to new technologies. Moreover, it can be undertaken during the design stage as well as 
during all other stages, including the operation stage. 
The predominance of HAZOP against most of the other hazard identification methods for railway 
systems, is also confirmed by the most acknowledged standards concerning the risk analysis of 
railways. According to IEC 50126, HAZOP is the only ‘’useful’’ hazard identification technique (FMECA 
is the only one stated as ’’highly recommended’’), Yellow Book [7] highlights the efficiency of creative 
techniques (HAZOP and brainstorming) but does not provide any specific classification, while IEC 
62425 [5] states its application as ‘’highly recommended’’ for the hazard analysis of railway systems.  
Although HAZOP seems as the most (or at least one of the most) suitable technique for the hazard 
identification of railway and specifically ATC systems, doubts about its efficiency, in terms of 
completeness, comprehension and accuracy, arise. These doubts mainly derive by, but can be also 
dealt with, the way of identifying all possible deviations. Furthermore, the analysis can be very time-
consuming and requires a high detailed level of provided documentation.

3.1. HAZOP analysis Methodology 
3.1.1. Conventional HAZOP method 
As aforementioned, the conventional HAZOP analysis is a structured qualitative technique, widely 
used in chemical process plants and other industries, aiming on the hazard identification and 
operability concerns of the analysed system. This method is based on the use of guidewords and 
parameters over study nodes. The overall system is divided into a number of suitable units (study 
nodes) so that for each of them, a meaningful design intent can be specified. This design intent is 
reflected by the parameters while the guidewords show how this intention might not be achieved, 
leading to undesired effects that concern safety or operability issues. The combination of the 
guidewords and parameters reveal the undesired deviations of the under investigation node.   
According to the ISO 3101 [3], the normal steps of the HAZOP process within a facilitated workshop 
is:  
• 
Splitting the analysed process, system or procedure into smaller parts (sub-processes, 
subsystems etc.) in order to make the review clearly defined; 
• 
Definition of the design intent for each of the aforementioned sub-processes or subsystems 
and application of all relevant guidewords on each including component. When a guideword 
leads to an undesired event, it is regarded as deviation; 
• 
For each deviation, causes, consequences and existing safeguards are identified and the 
mitigation measures are proposed; 
• 
The overall discussion and proposed action for the identified risks treatment are documented. 
In order to carry out a complete, comprehensive and accurate HAZOP analysis for an ATC system, a 
representation of all parts including in the system as well as their interactions is necessary. This can 
include mechanical drawings, data flow diagrams or circuit schematics at design level according to the 
required analysis.

3.1.2. Applied HAZOP methods 
Among others, the objective of the present thesis is to capture the most efficient HAZOP method for 
ATC systems while investigating whether the use of a deviation checklist, instead of guidewords, result 
in significant differences in competence and accuracy in the accident scenario description. Thus the 
two developed HAZOP approaches were compared and the most suitable among them was applied 
alongside the deviations checklist based one.  
For the purposes of the existing approaches comparison, a safety-critical ATC component was defined 
as the under investigation node and one of its core vital functions as its design intent. The parameters 
and guidewords were used according to the approaches’ proposals. Then the identified lists of 
deviations were compared in terms of completeness, accuracy and level of overlaps. 
The results show that the Functional safety analysis approach, developed in the IEEE, identified more 
accurately the failure modes while there was no, or at least limited, overlap (the level of overlap will 
be discussed later when the approach will be fully applied). It is also noticed that there are some 
common deviations not existing in any of the identified failure modes. For example, the intermittent 
transmission of the target point, which is required by some ATC systems. Moreover, some other 
deviations on the deviation checklists are ambiguous (nonsensical deviations were excluded from the 
final list). 
This considerable limitation arises from the use of guidewords. Since guidewords were initially 
developed for process plants, their application in other industries may lead to limited results. 
Correspondingly, when applying them to an ATC system, it is possible to overlook some hazards not 
related to the provided guidewords while some deviations may be ambiguous or nonsensical.  
Towards that goal, the principles of dimensional analysis were applied in the present project. Thus, 
explicit and direct deviations, instead of guidewords, are selected. Obviously, in order to cover all 
possible failure modes while also providing an ‘’orthogonal’’ generic failure mode (i.e. one with no 
overlaps), these deviations should cover all hardware, software and human levels. Thus, the applied 
deviations concern the dimensions of time, of continuously operation of more than one components 
or subsystems, the degree of operation and parameterised for both continuous and discontinuous 
working state. Moreover, external causes have been also added in the list as they can lead to faulty 
functioning. The applied functional failure modes checklist was provided by [22] and is shown below.

3.2. Human Error analysis 
Although the modern ATC systems tend to minimize the human involvement, especially for the 
execution of safety-critical functions, the human element still exists and effects some processes. 
Operators’ inputs and verification checks are required for specific actions and therefore the safety 
analysis of the overall system cannot be separated from the analysis of human actions.  
Except of the operators’ errors, human errors can also derive during the maintenance, installation and 
of course the design stage. Many of these errors can be avoided by prediction and careful design. In 
the present thesis, only the operators’ errors are investigated, since the design, the maintenance and 
the other phases are outside of the current scope.

3.3. Consequence analysis 
Consequence analysis aims to identify the possible effects of the hazards identified in the hazard 
identification stage. Specifically, since these hazards are identified, this method is used to provide the 
critical path of successive intermediate events that their success or failure would lead to an incident 
or accident and estimate the likelihood of occurrence of each of them.  
The technique used for this purpose at the present project is the Cause Consequence analysis (or 
Cause Consequence Diagramming). This is a bottom-up diagrammatic representation where the under 
consideration hazard is set as initiating event at the bottom of the diagram. The corresponding factors 
that may affect the progression of the event are then related and assessed, leading to the possible 
outcomes as a result of their success or failure. These factors include both safeguards as well as 
necessary random factors that influence the escalation of the event. The outcomes are finally 
displayed at the top of the structure and can range from a safe state to a catastrophic accident. 
The notation of the diagram is very simple. The analysed initiating event is represented as a single 
block on the bottom of the diagram. All intermediate events are displayed as blocks where on the top 
of them there is a ‘’Yes’’ and a ‘’No’’ block notation. The event following the success of the each 
intermediate event is displayed as the next intermediate event connected to the ‘’Yes’’ box while the 
one following its failure is connected to the ‘’No’’, respectively. Finally, the outcomes (consequences) 
of the overall event escalation are stated mainly on the top of the diagram inside hexagon blocks.   
In order to estimate the likelihood of an accident occurrence after a specific failure, probabilities of 
success and failure should be assigned to all intermediate events. These probabilities as well as the 
necessary conditions or combination of conditions of the success and failure of these intermediate 
safeguards can be illustrated and estimated by the use of Fault Tree Analysis (FTA).

3.4. Semi-formal methods analysis 
The following description of the use of formal methods is taken from Taylor, 2016 [22]. 
“Formal methods involve the use of mathematical forms of expression such as set theory or 
mathematical logic (predicate calculus) in order to describe systems (such as railways) or software 
(such as interlocking circuits) [25] [26] [27] The descriptions can then be used in order to prove that 
safety requirements, also expressed mathematically, are satisfied by a specific design. The methods 
have been used to validate the logic underlying design of simple fixed block operation (section 9.1) 
and ATC under moving block operation (section 9.2). 
Graphical methods, such as finite state machines, state charts and Petri nets can be made to be just 
as formal as set theoretic or predicate calculus notations. In the present case, the grafcet notation 
[28] has been used to represent railway systems. The reasons for this are: 
• 
Grafcets can be made to be isomorphic to the structure of a railway network. 
• 
A marking of the grafcet can be made such that each token represents a train or a part of a 
train. 
• 
In contrast to finite state diagrams the grafcet can represent the parallel development of 
multiple state variables, such as multiple trains in the same rail network. 
• 
Petri nets need for “helper” place markings which serve to control the progress state 
transitions through the net. These “helper” place markings do not necessarily have any 
physical interpretation. The nets can become quite complex in order to be able to describe 
the processing of “helper” markings. By contrast, in grafcets (which were developed from Petri 
nets [29]), the no “helper” markings are needed, and each marking can be made to represent 
an actual physical state. 
• 
Grafcets do not allow for more than one token at a single place in the network. If this were 
necessary, then predicate/transition petri nets [30] or coloured petri nets [31] could be used, 
with little change in the following analysis. However this did not prove necessary since grafcets 
proved adequate to express the rail situations. “ 
Grafcets are graphs or networks consisting of place nodes and transition nodes interlinked by 
connections or arcs. For the present purpose, a simple graphic transition symbolism is used. The initial 
situation is represented by a double square. Place node represent possible states of a system or part 
of a system. Place nodes can be marked with a token, indicating that some part of the system is in the 
corresponding particular state. Each connection has a transition guard, which is a logical condition 
that indicates whether a state transition between the two sate nodes is possible. A transition is 
fireable if all previous steps are active while it is fired when its associated conditions are true. After 
the transition firing, the previous steps are deactivated and the next ones activated.  Branch lines can 
be shown at the exit from a state node, indicating that two or more parallel state transition processes 
are created in a transition. Join lines can be used to indicate that two parallel processes must complete 
before the next transition can take place. i.e. multiple parallel processes are replace by a single 
process.  
Using formal methods, the safety requirements of the system are stated precisely in terms of safety 
requirement predicates, but the sufficiency of these requirements should be validated by other 
methods. Thus, other methods are necessary for hazard identification and the associated risks. For 
that purpose, as stated before, the HAZOP technique is used in the current project.

Case Study – Communication-Based Train Control 
 
4.1. CBTC System Overview 
Communication-Based Train Control (CBTC) is an automatic, continuous train control railway signalling 
system that uses bidirectional train-to-wayside equipment telecommunication to utilize the traffic 
management and the infrastructure control. Although the system characteristics, performance and 
functional requirements depend on each application, it should primarily include: 
• 
High-resolution train location determination, independent of track circuits 
• 
Continuous, high capacity, bidirectional train-to-wayside data communications 
• 
Train-borne and wayside processors performing vital functions [32] 
According to IEEE 1474, a CBTC system is a "continuous, automatic train control system utilizing high-
resolution train location determination, independent from track circuits; continuous, high-capacity, 
bidirectional train-to-wayside data communications; and trainborne and wayside processors capable 
of implementing Automatic Train Protection (ATP) functions, as well as optional Automatic Train 
Operation (ATO) and Automatic Train Supervision (ATS) functions." 
Automatic Train Protection (ATP): ATP functions provide the basic protection level for passengers, 
personnel and equipment against operational hazards. They maintain fail-safe protection of trains 
against collisions, excessive speed and other hazards which may occur during train movements by a 
combination of train detection, train separation and interlocking. These functions shall be vital and 
mandatory at all CBTC applications. Bidirectional ATP shall be ensured by the system. 
Automatic Train Operation (ATO): ATO functions perform any or all of the functions otherwise 
assigned to the train operator. These functions are generally considered as optional and non-vital as 
they are not safety critical. They typically include automatic speed regulation, automatic station 
stopping, automatic door control and performance level regulation. Different functions are required 
for train operations with or without crews.  
Automatic Train Supervision (ATS): ATS functions provide the overall real time control and supervision 
of the traffic including all monitoring, control and automation needed to totally support and 
coordinate the train movements. These functions typically include the train tracking, the performance 
adjustments of individual trains to maintain schedule and the data provision to adjust service in order 
to minimize the inconvenience otherwise caused by irregularities. They should be fully integrated with 
other functions to support service management and provide for a single consistent user interface. 
Levels of automation 
Depending on the operational requirements and the provided CBTC technology, the system can allow 
different Grades of Automation (GoA). In principle, the available levels of automation are: 
• 
GoA 1: Manual operation. Train operator is present and controls the starting and stopping of 
the train, the door functions and emergency situations. 
• 
GoA 2: Semi-automated operation (STO). Almost similar to the manual operation, including 
automated train stopping.  
26 | P a g e  
 
==PAGE:41==
• 
GoA 3: Driverless Train Operation (DTO).  Both starting and stopping are automated. No driver 
is needed, but an attendant should be present to handle the doors operation and emergency 
situations. 
• 
GoA 4: Unattended Train Operation (UTO). All starting, stopping, door operations and 
emergency situations are fully automated with no onboard staff needed.  
In most applied cases, the GoA 1 is only available for fallback reasons while GoA 3 is not commonly 
applied. Obviously, the higher the GoA is, the higher the safety, functional and operational 
requirements are [33].  
 
4.2. CBTC System Architecture and Design 
In most applications, CBTC equipment is settled in the Control Centre, at the trackside and in station 
equipment rooms as well as on-board the vehicles. All the included electronic devices communicate 
to each other through the data communication network. Thus, the overall system can be 
geographically divided into four major subsystems and some additional ones. 
 
 
 
Figure 6 CBTC system 
 
Since the architecture of the system depends heavily on the supplier and the system requirements, 
the applied subsystems can have variations in the technical approach, the functions and the 
components.  
CBTC ATS Subsystem 
The Automatic Train Supervision (ATS) system provides the main interface between the operators and 
the CBTC system while it is responsible for the non-vital functionalities of monitoring and scheduling. 
Thus, functions like train displaying, identification, tracking, routing and automatic regulation are 
included. Moreover, tasks like stopping functions and acting as the interface with the passenger 
information systems are commonly provided. 
The ATS equipment is mainly installed at central locations (Control Centre) although additional local 
control subsystems are often used as fall-back.  
27 | P a g e  
 
==PAGE:42==
Depending on the system requirement and the supplier’s technical approach, the ATS subsystem 
consists of several different servers, workstations, printers, displays and other equipment 
ATS subsystem interfaces physically and functionally with the wayside and train-borne subsystems as 
well as with the interlocking via the CBTC data communication equipment.  
CBTC Wayside Subsystem 
The configuration of the Zone Control subsystem and the decision for distributed or centralized 
architecture, also depends on the supplier and the applied technical approach. Except of the Zone 
Controller (ZC) it typically includes the computer-based interlocking (CI) and other wayside equipment, 
providing the wayside ATP and ATO functionalities in its particular authority area. Such functions 
typically include the generation of movement authority and the communication with the trains.  
Zone Controller is responsible for the calculation of the wayside ATP and ATO functions. It uses data 
from the other subsystems in order to calculate movement authorities for the vehicles inside their 
particular zone. These movement authorities are sent to the ATS and on-board subsystems to ensure 
safety.   
According to the received route setting commands, the Computer Interlocking controls and supervises 
wayside equipment like signals, switch machines and level crossing equipment in order to ensure the 
safe train operation.  
Moreover, the subsystem also contains other wayside devices like balises, signals, point machines, 
track vacancy devices and platform doors.  
Balises are the electronic beacons that are installed between the rails and used to transmit (Uplink) or 
receive (Downlink) data from the passing trains. They are distinguished to active and passive balises 
as well as to Fixed Data Balise and Transparent Data Balise. Active balises are powered from the 
signalling power supply while the passive ones receive energy from the passing trains [34]. In CBTC 
systems only Fixed Data Balises are used for the position accuracy. These kind of balises transmit the 
same data to all passing trains. Balises are typically deployed in pairs in order the trains to distinguish 
the travel direction.   
Except of the radio based CBTC positioning system, other track vacancy detection systems are used as 
in order to enhance safety (fall-back) and for non-equipped train operations. In most cases these 
secondary track occupancy systems consist of track circuits while axle counters may also be used. 
Track circuits are simple electrical devices that work by running a circuit that uses the track rails to get 
connected to a power source at one end and a relay at the other. Axel counters are devices installed 
at both edges of a section counting and comparing the axles of the passing trains. That way, except of 
the train integrity, the direction and speed of passing trains can be detected.   
Vehicle On-board Subsystem 
The CBTC on-board subsystem contains one or more Vehicle On-Board Controllers (VOBC) and 
associated sensors responsible for speed and train location measurements as well as other devices.  
VOBC is a vital component of the overall system as it is cable for providing and supporting the on-
board ATP and ATO functions. The VOBC processes all the inputs/outputs for the control of the 
propulsion, the generation of service brake and emergency brake profiles and the door systems. 
Moreover, it enables different operation modes (if permitted) and station stopping while it also 
monitors and reports the speed, location, direction and configuration of the hosting train.  
28 | P a g e  
 
==PAGE:43==
The radio communication between the train and the wayside equipment as well as the ATS equipment 
is achieved by the Data Communication System (DCS). DCS is a separate or integrated - to the train 
unit (TU) - system that is composed by software and hardware, including radios and antennas on the 
front and the rare of the train.  
Train speed measurement and positioning are determined by speed sensors, accelerometers and 
position plate sensors. The last one determines the train location while it also receives different types 
of position information from the database by detecting transponders that are established in the bed 
of the tracks. These information include all the necessary data that are used to calculate the crucial 
factors for the safety of the train movement.  
Finally, the communication between the train operator and the VOBC is established by the cab display 
and the console.  
In most cases, high reliability and fail-safe is achieved by redundancy architecture configurations 
(VOBC, mobile radio units and antennas), notions and automatic switchover (for instance of CPU) in 
case of identified failures.  
CBTC Data Communications Subsystem (DCS)  
This subsystem concerns all the ground-based and onboard equipment used for the communication 
between the major CBTC subsystems that shall support bidirectional data transfer as well as sufficient 
bandwidth and low-latency for all CBTC functions. This subsystem is not considered as vital as it does 
not execute any functions autonomously, but is essential for the vital functions that take place in other 
subsystems.  
The typical DCS architecture consists of the trackside backbone network, the train-to-trackside radio 
network and the train onboard network.  
The wired trackside backbone network is usually fibre optic cables that have high capacity and specific 
characteristics depending on the sophistication of the system and the operating environment. This 
characteristics shall comply with the performance and safety requirements. Interconnect points and 
technical rooms are typically used to avoid interconnection failures.  
The radio network shall provide continuous, high availability and low-latency geographic coverage and 
bidirectional data transfer and support the CBTC functions. Thus, radio data communication 
components comprise the trackside radio access points (APs), used to provide communication to the 
wayside subsystem, and the trainborne radio and antenna equipment to communicate with the on-
board system. The APs are typically connected to the wayside by Ethernet using fibre optic cables and 
are deployed on a star-based or ring-based network topology. Continuous connectivity is provided by 
the overlapping of AP coverages. Depending on the applied architecture, the antennas may include 
additional switching and routing equipment. Furthermore, network switches are used to 
communicate with the ATS system. 
In most cases, CBTC equipped trains have two identical on-board setups, ensuring redundancy and 
two-ways operation. These setups are typically located in the front and rear driver’s cab and are 
connected in order to ensure that they continuously receive the same data from the trackside 
equipment. The communication between them is achieved by the train on-board network (Ethernet).

4.4. CBTC system safety requirements 
Likewise all ATC systems, the design and implementation of each CBTC system should be in accordance 
to specific system requirements. These requirements should meet the predefined business 
requirements and are commonly divided into functional, performance, technical, RAM and safety 
requirements (in different cases, different sub-categories exist e.g. assurance or environmental 
requirements). Although in most cases these requirements are case specific, a minimum set is 
provided and standardized in IEEE 1474.1 [32].  
Since the scope of the current project is focused on the safety of ATC systems, in the present section, 
we concentrate on the safety requirements. Generally, these safety requirements are defined during 
the tendering phase where the Functional Safety targets are stated alongside the essential measures 
to achieve the predefined, by the authority having jurisdiction, system safety levels.  
Focusing on the CBTC system, since it is a train control system widely applied in mass transit, its design 
shall provide safety to passengers, public, operators and maintenance staff by preventing as a 
minimum the accidents described above.  
Towards that goal, the applied ATP functions shall at least prevent the occurrence of the catastrophic 
accidents mentioned at section 2.4.1.Thus, the corresponding ATP functions should be designed in 
accordance with the required fail-safe principles and be considered as vital, while under normal 
operational conditions, the system safety should not be depended on human actions. 
Thus, the system should ensure that vital functions are performed safely under: 
• 
Normal operation and inputs, when no failures occur 
• 
Normal operation and inputs, when hardware failures occur (fail-safe) 
• 
Abnormal operation and/or input  
Furthermore, the system implementation should ensure that the THR is greater than 10-9 system 
operating hours. This number represents the required quantitative safety level which ensures that any 
hardware, software or communication failure or combination of failures, which may cause accident, 
will occur with a frequency lower than the aforementioned.     
Thus, the safety-critical hardware components should be fail-safe or checked redundant. That way, 
any safety-critical hardware failure or combination of failures should occur with a frequency lower 
than the provided THR, while if that may occur more often, they will not lead to conditions known to 
be safe. 
Similarly, vital software should meet the corresponding vital requirements, data-driven software 
should be protected against possible input errors and vital functions should be implemented in a fail-
safe manner. 
4.4.1. Functional Safety Requirements 
 
Since the main focus of the present project is on the safety-related hazards that may arise by the CBTC 
functional failures, in this section, the corresponding general functional safety issues are outlined.    
Functional safety is a part of the overall safety of a system or a component and reveals the protection 
against the hazardous consequences that are caused by a technical failure or an unintended human 
mistake in response to the inputs and commands it receives. In most cases, it concerns electronic 
devices or software. 
The main standard that deals with the issues of functional safety and governs functional safety of both 
heavy and light railways is the European Standard IEC 61508. According to this standard, the definition 
35 | P a g e  
 
==PAGE:50==
of the functional safety is: ‘’the part of the overall safety that depends on a system or equipment 
operating correctly in response to its inputs’’ or ‘’the detection of a potentially dangerous condition 
resulting in the activation of a protective or corrective device or mechanism to prevent hazardous 
events arising or providing mitigation to reduce the consequence of the hazardous event’’ [45]. 
Moreover, this standard adopts the Safety Integrity Level (SIL) in order to define the targets of the 
safety-critical functions and elements. So, when the SIL is determined, a risk can be controlled within 
an acceptable level. Obviously, the SIL is also depends on the THR level mentioned above.  
Correspondingly, an ATC system, and especially its safety-critical equipment and functions, shall 
ensure that it satisfies the defined functional safety requirements. Obviously some of these functional 
safety requirements are affected by local conditions, e.g. the presence of level crossings or platform 
screen doors. Most railway systems, require a SIL 4 (THR between 10-8 and 10-9). 
Aiming on the development of general requirements related to train control systems, the Institution 
of Railway Signal Engineers provided, among others, a non-specific set of functional safety 
requirements that are applicable to any train control system, including all ATC systems like the CBTC 
system [46]. These, requirements highlight as a minimum the assurance of safe train separation, train 
protection against unauthorised MA or exceeding speed limits, unambiguous and timely information 
to train operators and signallers, level crossings and engineering works protection and emergency 
brake capability. Specifically, according to the IRSE Fundamental Requirements for Train Control 
Systems, the core functional safety requirements of all train control applications should be:  
• 
Before a train is given authority to move the section of line should be proved to be clear and 
to be secure against derailment and conflicts.  
• 
After movement authority has been given, the security of the line should be maintained until: 
o the complete train has cleared the section of line, or 
o the authority has been rescinded and the train has come to a stand, or 
o the authority has been rescinded and the train has sufficient space to come to a stand 
safely. 
• 
The signaller and train driver, or automatic train operation sub-system [ATO], should be 
provided with unambiguous, consistent and timely information to perform their duties.  
• 
Sufficient space should be provided between following trains to allow each train to brake to a 
stand safely. 
• 
Controls should be in place to prevent and/or mitigate the consequences of: 
o trains passing the end point of their movement authority and 
o trains exceeding the maximum permitted speed and 
o trains (and individual vehicles) moving without authorisation. 
• 
Protection should be provided for level crossings, engineering work etc.  
• 
The system should provide for communication between signallers and others. 
• 
The means should be provided for preventing trains from being routed onto a line with which 
they are not compatible, and to instruct a train to stop in an emergency.  
Thus, at least these functional safety requirements shall be fulfilled also in a CBTC system application. 
These requirements are mainly ensured by the implementation of the vital ATP functions as well as 
the interlocking principles. Obviously, in order to ensure the SIL 4 levels, these functions shall be 
designed in accordance with checked-redundant principles.

It can be clearly seen that by using the HAZOP-KR method, the identified deviations have a high level 
of overlap. Almost all deviations occurred more than one time. For example, the deviations ‘’Target 
point is not transmitted (1)’’ and ‘’Target point is erroneously transmitted (2)’’ occurred two times. 
This high level of overlap causes too much more effort and cost and the risk that problems will be 
overlooked because ‘’they can be found with another guideword’’. Moreover, in terms of accuracy, 
the guideword other is not clear. While it represents an abnormal behavior for the corresponding 
parameters, the way that this abnormality is defined is not clear, leading to competence issues and 
problems to identify the potential consequences. For example the deviation ‘’Limit of movement 
protection and target point is erroneously determined’’ is not defined if the target point is lower, 
which is on the safe side, or higher, which may cause accidents. Finally, since the data parameter 
covers the creation, transmission and reception of data, it results in even more overlaps in a full HAZOP 
analysis. The reason is that, in constant data transmission processes, when some data are lately 
created, they will be also lately transmitted and received, respectively.  
The Functional Safety Analysis approach, produces much more efficient results. By adjusting the 
guidewords only to outputs, no overlap between the failure modes is produced while all deviations 
are very accurate. Only the ‘’wrong’’ guideword may be considered as indeterminate, as it is not clear 
if it describes a target point in another route or one in the same but located before or after the actual 
one. But this is also the value of this guideword, to be able to represent and cover a range of different 
abnormalities. Finally no deviations identified by the HAZOP-KR method which were not also identified 
by the IEEE method.  
Summing up, both approaches produced the same level of completeness. The only exception occurred 
in the HAZOP-KR where the ‘’target point before/after the actual one’’ is included under the 
guideword other and the parameter data. Since, this deviation is one of the most important ones, it is 
regarded as a significant accuracy issue. This issue in addition with the high level of overlap, make in 
overall, the Functional Safety analysis approach more suitable and efficient, but capable of 
improvement. Thus, in the next chapter, this method will be fully applied to the reference CBTC system 
and compared with the corresponding based on full deviations checklist HAZOP method.

5.2.1. Full deviations checklist

are outside of the study scope. Additionally, possible emergency brakes and delays are stated in some

stated (and many times their identification was also controversial), only the possible accidents exist in 
the consequence column. These consequences do not take into account the operation of the existing 
safeguards assumed for the reference system. Some of these safeguards, which are in accordance

The results of the hazard identification analysis on the transmission of the target point show that the 
most hazardous situation can occur when the provided target point is either after the actual one 
(higher) or in another abnormal position (wrong). In the second case, the failure can be possibly 
identified by the track circuits when the train will enter in a section not included in the expected route. 
Then the whole territory shall enter in a fail-safe state. Of course, in that case, there are still 
possibilities of overspeed (different permanent speed restrictions), derailment (wrong position of 
points) or collision (occupied section). 
In the case of higher target point, the failure is much more difficult to be identified. The only way is if 
the provided target point is located in a different track section (and track circuit) than the actual one. 
In that case, the whole territory shall also enter in a fail-safe state, but the likelihood of accident 
occurrence is lower. The permanent speed restrictions as well as the position of points are unlikely to 
be different while if the target point is not much higher than the actual one it will be included in the 
overlap protection. Of course this does not mean that the accident occurrence is totally eliminated.  
For these case, the redundant ZC (if existed) is proposed to be used in parallel with the actual one 
(mixture) and not only in cases of failure. Moreover, other secondary track occupancy systems are 
suggested for critical locations as well as smaller sections. Of course the system shall be tested 
thoroughly in advance and there should be a mechanism that identifies the source of failure when it 
appears.

Chapter 6.  
Comparison of the applied HAZOP approaches 
 
The HAZOP approach using the full deviation checklist identifies the failures that may arise by applying 
deviations to the design intent of each component, while the one using the guideword checklist, by 
applying the guidewords to the outputs of each component. Obviously, each component may have 
more than one design intents. Correspondingly, each function (in our case used as design intent) of 
each component may have more than one outputs.  
In total, the full deviation checklist based approach was applied on 10 nodes while the guidewords 
based one on 7. The difference occurred by the data communication subsystem which in the first 
approach was examined separately setting as the design intent the transmission of data from the 
source subsystem to the destination subsystem. In the second one, the DCS could not be examined 
separately since it does not produce any outputs. Thus, the communication failures are mentioned as 
causes of failures.    
Although it is not totally feasible to compare the identified failure modes of the applied approaches, 
since they deviations/guidewords are applied to different parameters, it is observed that as a general 
trend, the same failure modes were identified in most components. The fails to function, prolonged 
operation, delayed operation, part of operation and erroneous operation deviations lead to no, fast, 
slow, part and wrong output, respectively. Not direct correspondences exist for the high and low 
output that may be caused on more/less function, erroneous function, wrong location or too high/low 
velocity, depending on the under investigation function.  
A crucial gap in the identified deviations concerns the time dimension. Except of the fast and slow 
guidewords there are no other ones for this dimension. Thus, the cases of intermittent and interrupted 
operation (for continuous working components) as well as early and late starting (for discontinuous 
functioning components) are not covered. Further discussion about the value and consequences of 
these issues is provided in the next section of the report. 
Another issue concerns the interpretation of the guideword wrong that in some cases may include 
more than one deviations. For example the wrong target point output is normally interpreted as 
different than the actual one but that way the transmission of a correct target point to a wrong train 
is not considered. Similarly the wrong track occupancy status that does not investigate the case where 
the status is correct but it concerns another section. This interpretation issue can be minimized only 
by a systematic brainstorming and detailed review of all parameters of the corresponding outputs. In 
the deviations based approach, these gaps are covered by the use of wrong train, wrong section and 
wrong route deviations.   
In terms of overlap, both approaches have a very low overlap level, although it seems that a not 
careful deviations based HAZOP application can possibly lead to a few overlap issues. The provided 
deviations were not specifically generated for ATC systems and so there are cases where more than 
one of them have the same meaning. In the current case, the most suitable ones were used. 
Furthermore, the deviation based approach needs significantly more time to be carried out in a full 
scale since it firstly needs to identify the output of each failure in order to find the corresponding 
consequences. In the guidewords-based approach, these outputs are directly identified, since they are 
the basis of the analysis, and the consequences are much easier to be determined.  
61 | P a g e  
 
==PAGE:76==
Finally, in order to identify the hazards and the corresponding consequences of an ATC system, it 
seems more proper to have as a basis the failures of the outputs than of the functions. The reason is 
that, in contrast to other engineering systems, like the process plants, a failed function can only 
produce harmful consequences by the failed outputs it generates and not by other means (like high 
overheating or gas emission).  Thus, these functional failures should be regarded as causes of hazards 
and not the hazards themselves.

Chapter 7.  
Human Error analysis 
 
Since the scope of the present thesis is to investigate the safety issues associated to the operation of 
ATC systems, and not generally related to the railway operation, only the human factors directly 
related to the operation of the CBTC system were considered in this section. These factors mainly 
concern the CBTC human-machine interfaces (HMI). Thus, actions executed outside of the reference 
system boundaries, for instance during maintenance procedures, are not included in the analysis. 
In order to investigate the human errors that are related to the CBTC HMI functionalities, firstly some 
definitions should be provided. These definitions concern the users, the user interfaces and the 
control actions. Thus, according to the IEEE 1474.2 [50]: 
• 
CBTC user is any authority-authorized personnel who receive information from, provide 
information to, or perform repairs or maintenance on, a CBTC system. 
• 
CBTC user interface is the portion of the human computer interface in which the CBTC user 
interacts with the CBTC system to observe and/or perform functions implemented by the 
CBTC system. Includes, but is not limited to, displays, audible indicators, tactile entries, cursor 
positioning device implementations, and voice input devices. 
• 
Control action is a request by a CBTC user for the CBTC system to perform an operation. A 
control action may require a single user input, or a sequence of user inputs, and a user 
requested control action may be subject to verification checks prior to the CBTC system 
performing the requested operation. 
Thus, as human control actions related to the CBTC HMI are considered the user information inputs 
and the verification checks that are necessary for the performance of a system operation. These inputs 
and checks are mainly optional and depend heavily on the authority having jurisdiction. So, only the 
mandatory user information inputs and verification checks, as provided by the IEEE 1474.2, are 
investigated. These mandatory inputs and checks can be divided according to the subsystem they are 
executed at: 
Trainborne subsystems:  
• 
Operating mode selection 
• 
Overspeed alarm condition acknowledgement 
Non-trainborne subsystems: 
• 
Inputs to request and cancel routes, including ability to control and limit movement 
authorities of CBTC-equipped trains 
• 
Inputs to establish and remove work zones, block track sections/switches and establish 
temporary speed restrictions 
In order to identify the possible human errors of the mentioned above human actions, the provided 
in chapter 3.2 list of error modes was first applied to them. When the combination of an error mode 
with one of these actions was meaningful, it was regarded as a hazard.  
Thus, having identified all potential human error modes, the consequences for all of them were traced. 
Since the analysis is based on the generated reference system, the operating procedure was

Consequence analysis 
 
Having identified the failure modes that can occur in the safety-critical components of the system, the 
Cause Consequence analysis is used to identify, represent and estimate the critical paths that these 
failures can lead to incidents and accidents (according to the aforementioned definition provided by 
the European Railway Safety Directive) as well as the likelihood of occurrence of all of them. When 
one of these failures arise in a safety-critical element, specific safeguards shall exist to avoid, or at 
least mitigate the severity and likelihood of, the occurrence of harmful consequences (accidents).  
As it is impossible and not reasonable to construct a cause-consequence diagram for all identified 
failure modes within the time limit of a thesis project, some characteristic examples are provided 
(Appendix D). These examples concern the vital functions executed in safety-critical components that 
are necessary for the generation of a correct MA, one of the core functionalities of the CBTC system. 
These safety-critical components and the corresponding vital functions are: 
• 
Balise - Transmit absolute position data 
• 
ODO - Position measurement 
• 
VOBC - Train Location determination 
• 
Zone Controller - Limit of movement protection and target point determination 
 
The most significant failure modes, in terms of the severity of potential consequences, failure mode 
that can occur at each of these components is set as initiating event. In general, this severity should 
be determined by a risk matrix of RRA but in this project the necessary data for such estimations are 
not provided. The failure modes are identified by the HAZOP analysis using the full deviations checklist 
while the safeguards are in accordance with the reference system.

Semi-formal Methods analysis - Grafcets 
 
In this section, grafcets are used to represent, in a simple way, the behaviour of train control and 
signalling systems. In all these cases, the grafcets are isomorphic to the structure of the railway 
network or the investigated onboard system.  
The current analysis uses grafcets just for the highest level. Notations are abbreviated modified for 
convenience and understandability. Thus according to the method semantics, the initial situation is 
illustrated by a double square and the steps by single squares, but the outputs are stated inside the 
corresponding steps instead of being notated by a rectangle. 
The first two cases represent railway networks under fixed block operation while the last two, sub-
process of the moving block system. The provided cases combine the graphical representation of the 
grafcet method with deviations identified by the applied HAZOP. For that purpose, many different 
representation ways were tested, from excessive and complicated to limited, concluding to the 
simplest ones in order to provide an analysis that is convenient and understandable.  
Although the analysis is simplified and limited to specific examples, it still reveals the degree of 
complexity of railway safety issues. There are many possibilities for failure and a large number of rail 
network states in which those failures can arise. The graphical representation helps to keep track of 
the large number of initial states of the system, while the HAZOP and cause consequence analyses 
help to identify the range of items which can go wrong.

9.2.  Grafcet for Moving block signalling 
While, the fixed block system is based on physically separated blocks and therefore it is feasible to use an 
isomorphic grafcet to represent directly the physical states of the train operation, in moving block systems, 
the blocks are dynamically defined in real time, while data concerning the train location and the provided 
MA are continuously transmitted. Therefore, it is not possible to represent isomorphically the entire process 
(although many attempts were tried). However, dividing this process into sub-process that take place in 
specific physical locations (e.g. inside the train), allows to represent efficient grafcets.  
The main steps of the moving block process, in CBTC applications, are the following: 
1. Trains continuously compute their position  
2. Trains periodically send their position 
3. ZC computes MA 
4. Wayside radio continuously sends the MA to train 
5. VOBC computes speed profile and checks actual speed 
6. VOBC commands emergency brake if necessary 
From these steps, the train positioning determination (step 1) and the authorized speed supervision (step 6) 
were selected for further analysis. 
  
Train positioning determination 
The first step of the moving block process is the positioning computation which is executing entirely by 
onboard components. The BTM periodically receives absolute positioning references by the trackside balises 
while the odometer (ODO) continuously calculates the traveling distance. Both send the corresponding data 
information to the VOBC which using the necessary algorithms, calculates the actual position of the train. 
This position is then transmitted to the ZC in order to update the generated MA.

10.1. Results 
Most of the identified failure modes that can possibly lead to an accident, can, under specific circumstances, 
lead to collision as well as derailment. The main reason is that if a train moves outside of the correct 
Movement Authority Limit, there are possibilities of both track occupancy by another train (collision) as well 
as route points in wrong position (derailment). As collision, all head-to-head, head-to-rear, head-to-obstacle 
collisions are considered. 
The deviations that are not possible to lead to accidents are the ones that are on the safe side, for example 
when the generated target point is before the actual one or when the calculated ATP profile is lower than 
the actual one. Operational issues, like unnecessary braking or delays, will arise from these deviations, but 
since the project scope is only towards safety, they are not considered here.  
The bar chart below illustrates the total number of deviations as well as the number of deviations that may 
lead to accidents, collisions and derailments, for both approach applications. As accidents, both the collisions 
and derailments are considered.

derailments may occur by the latent hazards of a route request or cancelation while train-to-obstacle

because of human failures. As mentioned above, the analysis should not be considered complete since there 
are many other case specific human actions that were not analysed while actions during abnormal 
operational conditions (maintenance, failure management, recovery, etc.) should be also considered.

followed. So, the corresponding safeguards are mentioned and specific strategies for further mitigation 
measures can be carried out.

exist. Only specific characteristic example were analysed and therefore the results did not produce any new 
findings concerning safety issues. But as earlier the objective was to test the feasibility of the identified 
hazards linking with formal methods and not to produce full scale results.

10.2. Discussion 
In principle it is not totally proper and feasible to compare the results of the different HAZOP approaches 
since they are applied to different parameters. The provided deviations from the deviations checklist-based 
approach were applied to the design intent (vital functions) of each component, while the guidewords from 
the guidewords-based approach to the outputs of these vital functions. Thus, although most of these vital 
functions produce one single output, a specific deviation in the component functioning may have several 
deviations in the produced output. Analogously, a specific output deviation may be caused by several 
functional deviations.  
However, testing manually all identified hazards of both approaches, it was found that both of them are 
relatively complete covering almost the same deviations. Although the methodology based on the 
dimensional analysis was found to be logically complete as far as the dimensions identified are complete, this 
completeness cannot be fully guaranteed in high level since it was only possible to validate the results against 
a limited set of accident cases. So far, two approaches have been for determination of completeness: the 
performance of the analysis by two teams followed by comparison of the results and the comparison of 
analysis results with accident case historical data to determine the extent to which accidents were 
predictable. Both approaches were impossible to be covered within a master thesis since there were no 
human resources to carry out the analysis while the CBTC system is relatively new and has limited 
implementations, so only very limited accident data exist, or are publicly available. Thus, completeness can 
be only assumed by the comparison of two different methods. The only clear gaps are identified in the use 
of the guideword-based HAZOP approach that does not take into account the continuously operation that is 
required by most components and does not include the deviation of intermittent operation in the results. 
Moreover, the guideword wrong, if it is not used carefully, can also cause incompleteness issues. 
Correspondingly, the careless use of this guideword can cause issues of accuracy since it can possibly cover 
different deviations. Accuracy issues are also noticed in the deviation checklist, since it was not initially 
developed for railway systems and therefore it includes deviations that are totally irrelevant while others 
produce overlaps. These issues can be only overcome by high detailed knowledge of the system procedures, 
brainstorming and careful application by experienced engineers.  
This requirement of excessive system knowledge and brainstorming makes the method also more time 
consuming than the conventional HAZOP and therefore more costly. However, in terms of usability, the 
approach based on the full deviation checklist is more convenient if there are many outputs from each 
function since it decreases substantially the work load.  
Finally, the results revealed that applying guidewords or deviations to outputs rather than to functions is 
more efficient for the analysis of ATC systems. The reason is that, in contrast to other engineering systems, 
where HAZOP is traditionally applied to, a failed function can only produce harmful consequences by the 
failed outputs it generates and not by any other means. Thus, it is preferable to regard the output errors as 
failures and the functional errors as their causes.  
Despite these deficiencies, this method can be considered at least equally efficient to identify the main failure 
modes and used for further purposes, like the quantification of THR requirements. Moreover, the identified 
failure modes can be equally applied to any other Automatic Train Control system. 
Alongside this method, hazard analysis should be carried out to identify the consequences of possible human 
errors. Although modern ATC systems tend to minimize the human involvement, especially in safety-critical 
processes, the analysis showed that there are still possibilities of harmful consequences associated to human 
actions. When analyzing only the mandatory human actions during normal operation, such consequences 
87 | P a g e  
==PAGE:101==
are considerably limited, but in real life applications there are many other case specific ones that should be 
investigated. Moreover, such analysis should be carried out for all lifecycle phases. Especially for the design 
(where hazards can be significantly decreased but also increased) as well as for maintenance, test, 
commissioning and other phases where procedures are not standardized and safety issues rely much more 
on human actions. 
  
The way that such actions can lead to accidents can be identified and illustrated by the use of cause 
consequence analysis. While in the current project there were no available data for quantification of the 
results and therefore the likelihoods were not estimated, the analysis revealed the high flexibility of the 
method. The freedom it provides make the representation of very complex cases with many successive 
safeguards and consequences feasible. Moreover, the corresponding diagrams could be also drown as top-
down, depending on the adopted approach and the analysis targets. 
 
Finally, as stated before, HAZOP analysis is important as it allows for a rapid identification of a multitude of 
failure modes arising at the technological level while formal methods are important because they place the 
failure modes in the operational context. Thus, their potential combination can produce significant results. 
In this project, it was tested and proved that such combination is feasible, although for complicated process 
the graphical representation could be extremely large and difficult to be constructed. Much further 
investigations and applications are required in order to provide full scale analysis. 
 
10.3. A refined HAZOP approach for ATC systems 
As mentioned above, in contrast to other process systems, like chemical and process plants, a deviation that 
may arise in an ATC function can only effect the system by the erroneous output it will produce. No other 
consequences, related to safety issues, can occur. Thus, it is more appropriate to set as hazards to be 
identified the output failure modes instead of the functional failure modes. Correspondingly, the functional 
deviations should be regarded as the causes of failures. 
The detailed investigation of HAZOP, provided the basis for an improved approach using more consist 
deviations and an extension of the list of guide phrases. Having studied, for the purposes of the present 
project, most of the input and output signals of the vital CBTC functions, it observed that all of them contain 
specific information data. These information data can be divided into specific categories according to their 
content. For instance, a generated and transmitted MA contains information about the target point of a 
specific train. Moreover, this MA signal shall be transmitted within a specific time limit to the corresponding 
train. Correspondingly, the track section occupancy status data includes the vacancy status and the 
corresponding section id while the permanent speed parameters, the speed limits for a specific section. This 
signals shall be also transmitted within a predefined time.

Other issues derived from the present analysis and should be dealt are the continuous operation and data 
transmission of some safety-critical ATC components and signals. Although the transmission time is already 
defined as possibly being higher or lower than the design target values, these can also have the right value 
but being intermittent. The same applies also to measurements like the positioning by an odometer or speed 
by a tachometer. This intermittent operation may not cause immediate or direct consequences but should 
be examined in the analysis because the effects of the intermittent deviation may be cumulative. Depending 
on the rate of intermittency of functioning, this deviation could be also regarded as no. This intermission rate

should be predefined and the intermittent guideword should be added to the checklist concerning the 
continuous generated outputs. 
Finally, another guideword that should be further investigated and possibly added in the checklist is the 
intermediate (in between). This is mostly applicable to interlockings and not to ATC systems. Since there are 
functions with dual outputs that need some time to change state, the option of an intermediate state exists 
and may produce different consequences than the wrong state. Therefore it should be investigated.  This 
applies for cases where a parameter can take values in between to finite states (interlockings, point positions, 
level crossings and semaphore signals). 
That way, the only issues that are not directly face concern the communication failures since the 
communication subsystem does not produce any specific output. For that reason, its potential errors can be 
regarded as causes for output deviations.  
Summing up, this suggested HAZOP approach, seems able to capture the issues that derived by the traditional 
guideword approach. Since all parameters of each output are well defined, logical completeness is achieved. 
This also minimizes the level of overlaps between the identified deviations while makes them very accurate. 
Because of this, the consequences and corresponding safeguards can be systematically traced. The deviations 
identified and scenarios captured represent a more complete set than for the approach initially used in this 
study. The result appears to be more readily comprehensible. Finally, it could be more easily and efficiently 
automated.  
The discovery of the need for these additional guidewords was a perhaps surprising result of this study, since 
the list of guidewords for use in HAZOP has remained constant in published descriptions of HAZOP over the 
last 40 years.

Chapter 11.  
Conclusion 
 
While business, performance and safety requirements were traditionally considered to be in a constant 
conflict, in the railway sector, the evolvement of railway technology came to deal simultaneously with all 
these issues. Modern train control and signalling systems tend to minimize the human involvement while 
provide higher robustness and safety levels. However, there is still a long way in order to fulfil the constantly 
increasing demands for all these performances. 
Focusing on railway safety, a critical issue arises from the high functional complexity and novelty of the 
recently introduced systems. Although railway safety systems are very advanced in the way they prevent 
accidents, they also represent new technology and therefore it is important to investigate them for any 
accident potential. Thus, they shall be examined under correspondingly highly structured and systematic risk 
analysis and hazard control. And the cornerstone of their efficiency is the hazard identification. 
Having observed this demand, the general objective of the present thesis was to capture the most suitable 
hazard identification technique for Automatic Train Control systems and optimize its process. In this case, 
the term ‘’suitable’’ was interpreted as complete, accurate and cost efficient. Towards that aim, the HAZOP 
method was selected for further study, mainly because of its proven flexibility and usability in other fields.  
The literature review, revealed the existence and development of some HAZOP applications to railway 
systems, including ATC, as well as the considerable differences in the identified scenarios. Thus, the 
investigation and comparison of two different HAZOP approaches, one based on a guideword and one based 
on a deviation checklist, was selected. Moreover, in order to place the identified failure modes in the 
operational context, a combination of HAZOP with semi-formal methods was tested. This methodology, as 
well as some characteristic examples of human error and cause consequence analysis, were applied on a 
generated reference CBTC system, the most used ATC system for mass transit networks. 
The comparison of the two different HAZOP approaches produced some interesting results. First of all, the 
investigation of the safety-critical CBTC functions showed that the identified failure modes may cause 
harmful consequences concerning only the corresponding produced outputs. Moreover, since the safe 
operation of the system requires continuous functioning of most of the vital elements, the dimension of time 
is very critical. Thus, functional deviations like intermittent should be also taken into consideration. These 
observations combined with the fact that the methodology based on dimensional analysis is logically 
complete as far as the identified dimensions are complete, led to the proposal of a refined HAZOP approach. 
According to this approach, all safety-critical output signals are analysed, the included information data are 
parametrized, and specific, to the category of each parameter, guidewords are applied to all of them. That 
way, the overlaps are eliminated while the related, to each parameter, safeguards and consequences are 
systematically traced.  
According to the results of the present studies, these consequences, as well as the intermediate events that 
are necessary for their occurrence, can be efficiently and systematically identified and illustrated by the use 
of cause consequence analysis. Thus, except of the representation utility of this method, it is very useful to 
qualify and quantify, if the necessary data exist, the accident occurrences after the appearance of a specific 
failure mode.    
91 | P a g e  
==PAGE:105==
In these failure modes, the human errors, which may cause harmful consequences, shall be also considered, 
since, despite the limited and restricted human involvement in modern ATC systems, there are still cases that 
their actions can be the cause of serious accidents. So, their errors and their possible effects should be also 
investigated in accordance to the provided case.      
Finally, this large number of failure modes can be placed in the operational context by linking the hazard 
identification results with formal methods such as the grafcet representation. Although in cases of many 
parallel actions and possible deviations concerning them, like in complex functionalities of ATC systems, their 
right combination is complicated, the linking of similar consequences makes their representation feasible 
and useful. Furthermore, the ability of grafcets to be isomorphic to the railway network structure, makes 
them convenient and understandable.  
By these means, the ATC risk assessment process can become even more structured, solid and systematic 
while new potentials arise for further improvements and applications. Although completeness and total 
effectiveness would be never claimed, step by step we are approaching this utopian goal.  
 
11.1. Future work 
As it happens with all new methods, a real world test application for both the applied and refined methods 
is needed in order to validate the results and ensure its efficiency. But since a real application needs to be 
carried out in a high detail level, it will be very time and effort consuming, and therefore expensive, if it is 
executed manually. 
This highlights the need of automation. That way, the analysis efficiency will be increased substantially while 
a foundation for reuse of the previous safety experience will be provided. This, in turn, will contribute to 
improvements of the overall under investigation system or process. 
Moreover, significant improvements in the results and efficiency of the analysis will be achieved by 
considering the method as living document. When an accident or a near miss occurs, the analysis shall be 
updated while an investigation shall also take place in order not only incorporate new failure scenarios and 
accident possibilities but also identify why they were overlooked.   
The principles of the current method would be also applicable for other aspects or stages of the lifecycle of 
a project. For instance by investigating the ATO functions the operational hazard that may lead to delays and 
other operational issues would be identified and mitigating measures would be proposed. Similarly, by 
modifying the adopted scope, the method could be also used for the stages of maintenance, testing or 
upgrade.  
Although the purpose of this study was to investigate hazard identification methodology, it also served to 
provide information on the high level of safety to be expected from modern ATC systems, especially when 
the parallel improvements achieved by modern interlocking systems are taken into account. A large part of 
this safety though is dependent on the assurance of fail safety in the individual system components. 
A separate issue is that if a potential accident scenario is initiated, and the ATC system is than successful in 
preventing the accident to progress to fulfillment, there is still the problem of recovery, which may involve 
trains being rescued or shunted, as well as proceeding under caution at reduced speed, but without full 
protection, or with protection provided by human observation and manual control. Many accidents have 
earlier occurred during the recovery phase. Although this kind of problem was outside the scope of the 
92 | P a g e  
==PAGE:106==
present study, it seems clear (and was confirmed by limited trials) that the approaches investigated can be 
extended to the recovery stage.

Onboard Equipment 
Vehicle On-Board Controller (VOBC) 
Vehicle On-Board Controller (VOBC): VOBC is the vital on-board component responsible for the provision of the on-board ATP and ATO functions.  
Design intent: Train Location determination, ATP profile determination, Authorized speed determination, Actual train speed/train travel direction 
determination, Authorized speed and travel direction supervision/enforcement 
Train Location determination: CBTC train location determination shall safely and accurately establish the location of both the front and the rear of the train 
with sufficient resolution and accuracy to support the performance and safety requirements. 
 
ATP profile determination: Determination of the profile of authorized speed as a function of train location.´ 
 
Authorized speed determination: This function determines the current authorized speed for a CBTC train.  
 
Actual train speed and train travel direction determination: This function determines the train’s actual speed and travel direction. 
 
Authorized speed and travel direction supervision/enforcement: This function supervises and enforces the authorized speed and authorized travel direction.

Zone Controller 
Zone Controller (ZC): is a processor-based component that used data from the CI and other systems in order to execute the wayside ATP and ATO functions. 
Moreover, it communicates with the neighboring ZC in order to ensure the safe train control hand-over. 
Design intent: Track sections occupancy status, Limit of safe route determination, Limit of movement protection and target point determination 
Track sections occupancy status determination: establish whether a section of track is occupied by one or more trains, including trains not equipped with 
CBTC train-borne equipment and/or trains with inoperative CBTC train-borne equipment.  
Input: track circuits output: track sections occupancy status (ZC) 
Limit of safe route determination: determine the limit of safe route for a CBTC-equipped train.  
Input: ZC section occupancy, ZC end of track, route status info CI. Output: limit of safe rout (ZC), route status information (VOBC) 
Limit of movement protection and target point determination: Determining the limit of movement protection for safe train separation and travel through 
interlockings, and to respond to other conditions that may require a more restrictive limit of movement protection for a specific train and determining the 
target point location applicable to the limit of movement protection, with consideration of the associated safe braking model.  
Output: location of CBTC train ahead (VOBC), target point (VOBC)

Onboard Equipment 
Vehicle On-Board Controller (VOBC) 
Vehicle On-Board Controller (VOBC): VOBC is the vital on-board component responsible for the provision of the on-board ATP and ATO functions.  
Design intent: Train Location determination, ATP profile determination, Authorized speed determination, Actual train speed/train travel direction 
determination, Authorized speed and travel direction supervision/enforcement 
Train Location determination: CBTC train location determination shall safely and accurately establish the location of both the front and the rear of the train 
with sufficient resolution and accuracy to support the performance and safety requirements. 
Input: unique absolute position references (balise), route status input (ZC).  Output: train location (ZC, ATS) 
ATP profile determination: Determination of the profile of authorized speed as a function of train location. 
Input: permanent speed restrictions (ZC or VOBC), temporary speed restrictions (ATS), braking curves (brakes VOBC). Output: ATP profile (VOBC) 
Authorized speed determination: This function determines the current authorized speed for a CBTC train.  
Input: ATP profile (VOBC), train location (VOBC) Output: authorized speed (VOBC) 
Actual train speed and train travel direction determination: This function determines the train’s actual speed and travel direction. 
Input: actual speed (ODO, tachometer), travel direction (travel direction measurement device). Output: actual speed (VOBC, door opening interlocks), travel 
direction (VOBC) 
Authorized speed and travel direction supervision/enforcement: This function supervises and enforces the authorized speed and authorized travel direction. 
Input: authorized speed (VOBC), actual train speed (VOBC). Output: speed supervision (train brakes subsystem), travel direction supervision (train brakes 
subsystem)

international research environments and industrial and public partners. 
 
DTU Management Engineering has app. 400 employees consisting of academic staff, PhD students and technical and administrative staff.
