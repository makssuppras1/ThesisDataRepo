Abstract
Harvesting the potentials of power generation in offshore is fundamental for the energy tran-
sition. In Europe, building a high-voltage direct current (HVDC) grid in the North Sea can
be major progress in clean, efficient, and secure energy production. Installing large-scale fa-
cilities far offshore increases the efficiency, while security of supplied is achieved by creating
a well-connected power network. Energy hubs and interconnectors are important technologies
currently under discussion and planning among researchers, TSOs, and governments. Given
the high investment costs, regulatory challenges, and the novelty of DC grids, it is essential to
study the design and operational decision-making processes well in advance using appropriate
modeling and future projection tools.
The state of the art includes numerous studies on OPF solvers, topology optimization, and
methods for problem relaxation, as well as topology optimization based on load and generation
balancing. However, a research gap exists in combining a high-fidelity electrical model of a DC
grid with a comprehensive, customizable energy system model. This thesis addresses that gap
and develops a concept enabling reliable analysis of investment and operational decisions for
energy hubs and interconnectors.
This thesis investigates DC grid modeling and optimal power flow to achieve market-based
topology optimization for a DC network. A set of open-source tools is selected and integrated
to i) model a DC grid in steady-state and solve power flow optimization (OPF) problem; ii)
conduct multi-period transmission network expansion planning (TNEP) based on detailed fu-
ture energy system models and variable renewable energy production patterns. The proposed
concept provides both a suggestion for optimal investment decisions and frequent operational
power flow commands. The used topology optimization tool is designed to handle large input
datasets, including hourly generation and market-price patterns over a 25-year time horizon,
while keeping the computation time in small ranges. The solution of the transmission network
expansion planning (TNEP) ensures economical viability of the investments. To demonstrate
the proof of concept, a case study focused on the North Sea is conducted, and the robustness
of the results is evaluated through sensitivity analysis.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Offshore wind energy production is regarded as a key component in achieving international
decarbonization targets by 2050. However, the fluctuating nature of wind generation neces-
sitates a robust and interconnected power system infrastructure to ensure the security and
reliability of electricity supply, particularly in future networks with high wind energy pene-
tration. The trend in offshore systems is toward larger scales, greater distances from shore.
The capacities of turbines, wind farms, and transmission cables are continuously expanding,
as larger-scale systems offer higher efficiencies compared to smaller-scale alternatives. In the
European context, a concept that is being discussed by many power system stake holders such
as the national transmission system operators (TSO)s, European Network of Transmission Sys-
tem Operators for Electricity (ENTSO-e), politicians, and research institutes are the energy
hubs and interconnectors. Beside conventional radial connection of the offshore energy, hybrid
interconnected infrastructure are to be built to increase the reliability and efficiency of the
energy supply. Hybrid meaning that the connections are utilized for at least two functionali-
ties: energy transmission between onshore electricity markets as well as integration of offshore
generated electricity. The Esbjerg Cooperation [1] between four European countries and North
Sea Wind Power Hub (NSWPH) [2] are examples of actions emphasizing the importance of
interconnected energy hubs.
Since there are currently no existing energy hubs, and the first one is expected to be built
by 2030 and beyond, it is important to study the design and financial challenges of this in-
frastructure well in advance. Due to the high costs of energy hubs and uncertainties, it is a
difficult decision for investors and development companies to commit to such investments. For
example, recently, the Danish Energy Agency did not receive any bids for the wind farms in the
North Sea that are intended to be connected to the Danish energy island [3]. The reasons cited
include significant increases in prices and interest rates [4]. This highlights the importance of
thorough physical and economic analysis during the planning phase. The benefits of offshore
energy hubs increase when multiple hubs are interconnected to tackle the uneven distribution
of load and variable renewable energy production across different markets and locations. Thus,
it is crucial to plan energy hub and interconnection projects at an international level.
The technology enabling high-power transmission over long distances is high voltage direct
current (HVDC) technology, which offers many advantages over alternating current (AC) sys-
tems, such as lower losses and the ability to control power flow. The topology optimization of
an HVDC grid is the focus of this study, known as the transmission network expansion planning
(TNEP) problem. Since topology optimization is closely related to daily dispatch optimization,
1

power flow optimization and topology optimization should be considered together.
1.2
Research Questions
These questions are discussed in this thesis:
1. How can offshore energy hubs be connected with each other and the lands optimally?
With the increasing scale of offshore wind projects, the efficiency of energy harvesting
must improve to offset high investment costs. To maximize the benefits of offshore hubs,
the topology of the offshore DC grid should be optimally designed. Existing research
either employs simplified electrical modeling tools focusing on balancing demand and
generation or uses detailed electrical models with low-fidelity future power system projec-
tions, neglecting the affects of large offshore facilities in on the market price and tailored
generation patterns. The combination of a detailed electrical model and a customizable
power system mode. Given an existing offshore direct current (DC) grid infrastructure,
this study examines the optimal expansion of the grid.
2. What is the objective of the topology optimization?
Operating the DC grid in the North Sea requires an international cooperation involving
different stake holders. Increasing the connectivity of the demand and generation nodes
has many advantages such as better security and reliability of supply, market convergence
and more. In order to make the TNEP attractive to all the grid participants, the overall
economical social welfare should be optimized.
3. How to optimize the overall economical social welfare?
Having the ability of controlling the flows in a DC grid by voltage source converter (VSC)
converters, gives the freedom of transmitting power to the buses with the highest demand
besides respecting technical and regulatory constraints. Firstly, an electrical model should
be employed which can represent components of a DC grid such as converters and DC
branches and is able to optimize the system variables to reach a certain objective. This
objective is social welfare and a market-based approach is done to optimize the power
flow.
4. How to assess the economic viability of an investment decisions for grid expansion?
The highest costs of the a DC grid are for the converter stations and the submarine cables.
In this study, the placement of the converters and a connection to the home market of
the hub are assumed to be existing and the further inter-zonal connections are to be
optimally selected based on the benefit maximization of a time horizon. The assessment
of the expansion can be done by evaluating the contribution of the new branches in an
expanded grid scenario.

1.4
Structure of the Thesis
In Chap. 2, the state of the art for offshore grids and interconnections is explored. This includes
a review of relevant projects, such as the Esbjerg Cooperation and the North Sea Wind Power
Hub (NSWPH), which focus on the integration of offshore wind energy into the European grid.
Additionally, the literature on HVDC systems and topology optimization is reviewed, address-
ing various methodologies for offshore TNEP. This chapter provides a theoretical foundation
for understanding the challenges and opportunities of offshore grid development.
Chap. 3 extends this discussion by applying state-of-the-art methods to multi-terminal and
meshed HVDC grid modeling.
The existing literature on power flow modeling in AC/DC
systems is analyzed, including both nonlinear and linear formulations such as the ’dc’ approxi-
mation. Special emphasis is placed on converter modeling, DC grid optimal power flow (OPF),
and topology optimization problem. Additionally, the chapter introduces key simulation tools
such as CorRES and Balmorel, which generate energy hub production patterns and project
electricity market prices.
Chap. 4 focuses on the methodology for solving the TNEP problem explains the toolchain
for power flow and topology optimization. It introduces the mathematical formulation of the
objective function used in the optimization process.
In Chap. 5, the developed methodology is applied to a case study based on energy hubs and
interconnectors in the North Sea. Various input parameters and datasets are introduced such
as wind turbine and energy hubs specifications, converter station characteristics, and HVDC
cable properties. The results are evaluated through sensitivity analysis, which gives insights
into the robustness of the results against changes of the input parameters, for example, the
discount rate and candidate definition.
Chap. 6 concludes the thesis with discussion on the methodology, its limitations, and potential
future research directions. The assumptions made in the modeling process are assessed.

Chapter 2
State of the Art: Offshore Grids and
Interconnections
Many initiatives and studies have been undertaken by European governments and TSOs to
analyze the potential and design of energy hubs and dual-purpose interconnectors. These efforts
aim to support the efficient integration of offshore wind energy and enhance cross-border energy
exchange. In this chapter, the abstract concept, terminology, and technical literature of DC
grids are introduced, providing a foundation for understanding their role in future offshore
energy systems.
2.1
Background on Offshore Energy Hubs
Energy hubs, also known as energy islands, are offshore facilities that collect the power gen-
erated by multiple offshore wind power plants (OWPPs), integrate storage and conversion
technologies, and transmit the energy to the onshore grid. Additionally, they can act as nodes
to interconnect power systems and electricity markets [5]. Dual-purpose interconnectors, in
turn, are undersea cables that integrate wind energy into the public grid while also make power
trading between different bidding zones possible.
The Esbjerg Cooperation [1] exemplifies a collaborative initiative aimed at advancing energy
hubs and interconnectors in the North Sea. Formed by Denmark, Germany, the Netherlands,
and Belgium, this partnership targets the installation of 65 GW of offshore wind capacity by
2030 and 150 GW by 2050. The cooperation focuses on coordinated planning for energy hubs
within the countries’ exclusive economic zones (EEZs) and explores potential interconnections
between these hubs. By leveraging advanced HVDC technology, it aims to ensure the efficient
and reliable transmission of electricity across long distances and between markets.
The NSWPH [2] aligns with these goals, focusing on the integration of offshore wind energy into
the European grid through interconnected energy hubs. These hubs are designed to combine
the collection of offshore wind power, cross-border energy distribution, and the production of
green hydrogen. By optimizing the use of hybrid infrastructure, the NSWPH aims to enhance
efficiency and promote cross-market energy trade, contributing to a more integrated European
energy system.
The PROMOTioN (Progress on Meshed HVDC Offshore Transmission Networks) project [6]
addresses the technical, regulatory, and economic challenges of developing a meshed HVDC
grid.
This EU-funded project emphasizes standardization, control systems, and regulatory
frameworks to enable the efficient operation of interconnected hubs and interconnectors, further
4
==PAGE:14==
strengthening the North Sea’s role as a hub for renewable energy and cross-border cooperation.
InterOPERA [7] is another initiative dedicated to the HVDC technology that aims to en-
able interoperability of multi-terminal direct current (MTDC) systems. Ensuring that different
HVDC components and transmission systems can operate together stably is a crucial milestone
in realization of offshore DC grids. The project defines technical frameworks and standards to
achieve compatibility of the HVDC systems.
The offshore network developing plan (ONPD) report [8] provided by ENTSO-e in 2024 states
that 14% of the European offshore renewable energy sources will be connected via dual-purpose
hybrid infrastructure, which is 69.3 GW out of the total planned capacity of 495 GW. In the
case study of the thesis, the sum of the installed capacity of the OWPPs does not exceed this
14% portion, making the analysis more realistic. The benefits of connecting the markets are
named among others as following:
 better use of offshore renewable energy sources
 better European energy security due to increased network redundancy
 better usage of the maritime space
 price convergence between market areas.
In the context of energy hubs and interconnections, two offshore market setups are considered:
the home market (HM) setup and offshore bidding zone (OBZ) setup. In the HM setup, offshore
wind farms bid into their home market and receive its electricity price, which is the status quo.
The OBZ setup creates a separate bidding zone for offshore hubs and each energy hub will
compete with offers and bids in from other hubs and onshore bidding zones. Assuming trade
agreements between all the operators, a central algorithm will match those offers and bids and
dispatch the hubs to optimize the overall system [9]. The OBZ setup is generally preferred
by many European and British organizations such as [10], [9], and the European Commission
[1] because the OBZ model results in marginally higher socio-economic welfare and higher
efficiency. Furthermore, OBZ is more compliant with the 70% rule (Sec. A.2) than the HM
model [11]. Despite that, the HM setup is applied in this thesis since no market data for an
offshore bidding zone is given. Fig. 2.1 illustrates the representation of the two market setups.

2.2
Technical Literature Review
Reference [12] proposes a method to optimize a transmission system topology for multiple wind
farms. It consider both AC and DC systems to find an optimal combination and methodology
based on objectives such as electrical and unharvested losses, cable investment and maintenance
costs while not violating constrains like power balance, voltage, and current limits. As an out-
put it results an optimal network topology of wind farms and onshore point of connections.
The proposed algorithm can be also used to extend an existing network. The optimization
algorithm is based on the genetic algorithm (GA), which is meta-heuristic solution method to
find the global optimum but with not a certain guarantee.
Jat et al. [13] propose a MTDC demonstration grid linking the UK, Denmark, and Germany
directly without considering any OWPP. Through a cost-benefit analysis, they demonstrate
the project’s financial feasibility, with payback periods ranging from 10 to 23.5 years under
different scenarios. The methodology involves estimating annual benefits based on electricity
price differences between countries, incorporating investment and operational costs to calculate
the net present value (NPV), and performing sensitivity analyses on factors like line capacities
and pricing variability to assess economic viability. This study relies on historical day-ahead
market prices and uses the future scenarios given by ENTSO-e under TYNDP 2018.
Reference [14] extends the previous study to analyze different DC grid topologies including
hybrid offshore asset (or energy hubs in this thesis). It formulates an optimization problem
to maximize the benefit and minimize the costs. The objective function is made of i) hourly
benefit and ii) yearly investment costs.
The authors in [15] approach to solve a long-term TNEP problem based on the electricity
market. It uses historical market data to create a framework for the static TNEP for an HVDC
grid. The proposed framework determines the network topology, transmission capabilities, off-
shore power flow, and the resulting social welfare.
The study [16] examines optimal transmission development in the North Sea region and the
impact of the study time horizon. Other key aspects discussed include the comparison between
radial and complex offshore grid architectures. Using an investment optimization model that
aims to minimize generation and transmission investment costs as well as operational costs, the
study determines the optimal offshore grid topology. Compared to the current thesis, it covers
a larger case study (more nodes and transmission) and, to limit critical computation time, uses
fewer time stamps. The study considers a 40-year time horizon, represented by two years (2030
and 2050), with each year including 2-hourly time stamps from 8 weeks distributed throughout
the year.
In [17], detailed offshore wind generation simulations, including wake losses, and energy system
optimization are used to study the cost-effectiveness of offshore energy hubs in the Baltic Sea
towards 2050. The paper analysis range is above the electrical power systems and includes also
heating and mobility systems of Baltic Sea countries as well, when assessing different scenarios.
The results suggest location and size of both energy hubs and transmission infrastructure. The
related literature is further introduced and applied in the next chapter.

Chapter 3
Multi-Terminal and Meshed HVDC
Grids Modeling
In the recent decades, HVDC technology has attract attention both in the research and in-
dustry, and has been applied in several long-distance transmission projects, due to its higher
efficiency comparing to the counterpart AC system. One of the applications is offshore wind
energy transmission and its success has lead to considering building more than two terminals
as it is the case in a conventional point-to-point connection. With the technological advances
in the high-power VSC, MTDC systems has become a promising possibility for the current and
future power systems [18, 19].
One of the major advantages of the HVDC grids is the freedom determining the energy flows
by applying control strategies in the AC/DC converter systems.
In pure AC systems, the
power flows are mainly a function of the line impedances and can be only effected by additional
devices such as flexible AC transmission system (AC) and phase shifters [20]. Whereas in a
DC grid, the converters can accomplish this task beside translating AC to DC or reverse. To
be able to run power flow and optimal power flow (OPF) simulations, models of the relevant
components of the power system such as AC and DC, converters, and DC lines are required.
In the following section, these models are derived and explained. This chapter is considered as
an application of state of the art since the optimal power flow calculations are widely explored
in the literature. The essential models for the purpose of the study are covered here.
3.1
AC/DC Power Flow Modeling
The optimal power flow (OPF) problems in combined AC/DC systems are inherently nonlinear
and nonconvex due to factors such as the power flow equations in DC branches and the con-
verter loss models. However, to ensure global optimality, improve convergence, and enable more
efficient computation—particularly in large-scale systems—these models are often formulated
in relaxed forms using convex or linear approximations. In addition to the full nonlinear AC
formulation, alternative approaches include quadratically constrained quadratic programming
(QCQP), second-order cone programming (SOCP), semidefinite programming (SDP), and the
linear DC approximation (network flow (NF)) [21]. A hierarchical overview of these possible
formulations is provided in [22, 23].
In the literature, two power flow models for the branch like components can be found: branch
flow model (BFM) and bus injection model (BIM), which are equivalent models based on Kirch-
hoff’s laws [21]. These models can be relaxed using the mentioned formulations. In this thesis,
the basic nonlinear BIM is used to compute the optimal power flow solution for a single time
7
==PAGE:17==
step (i.e., one hour). The linear ’dc’ approximation is critically assessed for its potential inac-
curacy, which depends on the network characteristics [24]. The primary sources of inaccuracy
in the ’dc’ formulation include line impedance, transformers, and filters. However, since this
study focuses on topology optimization for a predominantly DC grid, without the mentioned
components, and involves a large input time series dataset, the linear ’dc’ approximation is used
for the TNEP. This approach is considered sufficiently accurate for the study’s purpose while
also significantly reducing computation time. Throughout this section, the ’ac’ formulations

Converter model
It is in this study assumed that the converters are VSC type since this model is more promising
for the use in energy hubs than its counterpart line commutated converters (LCC) due to its
higher controllability of active and reactive power. An HVDC converter station is made of
four main part: AC transformer, filter, phase reactor, and the converter. The transformer is
for matching the voltage of the converter AC side output with the grid voltage level. The AC
filter is for filtering the high-frequencies from the converter. This is usually connected between
the transformer and the phase reactor. Recent developments in VSC based on multi-modular
converter (MMC)s could even eliminate the need of filters [25] and therefore reduces the costs
of the stations. The phase reactor are installed to reduce the harmonic and fault currents. The
converter as the soul of a station is made of multiple insulated gate bipolar transistor (IGBT)s
which translates DC to AC (inverter mode) or reverse (rectifier mode). Fig. 3.1 shows the
different components and connections in a VSC-HVDC converter station.

DC Grid Optimal Power Flow
DC grids are typically controlled at three main hierarchical layers similar to an AC grid: pri-
mary, secondary and tertiary [31, 32, p. 402]. The primary level is for controlling the DC

value. The second level is power control which permits to achieve power set-points. These
set-points are provided by the tertiary control level that is operated by central dispatchers.
Other actions of tertiary control include tripping or connecting terminals and lines, and change
of droops characteristics. The tertiary calculations are based optimal operation of the grid with
objectives such as market profit, losses minimization, supply security, and so on. Fig 3.6 illus-
trates the hierarchical control structure of a DC grid. The time constant orders of the primary

3.4
Future Power System Modeling
Das et al. [35] study the balancing challenges of power systems, focusing on the North Sea due to
fluctuations in generation and forecasting errors, and propose a Balancing Tool Chain. This tool
chain includes market modeling, forecast simulations, intra-hour balancing, and area control
modeling. In [36], the tool chain is used to assess the value of future network development in
the North Sea for balancing forecast errors, and the impacts of forecast errors are analyzed.
A part of this tool chain can be utilized in this thesis to carry out optimal grid development.
First, the two main models of the tool chain, Balmorel and CorRES, are introduced, followed
by an explanation of their integration into the thesis objective.
3.4.1
CorRES
Correlations in Renewable Energy Sources (CorRES) is a simulation tool developed at the Tech-
nical University of Denmark (DTU), Department of Wind Energy, which simulates both wind
and solar generation [37]. It combines meteorological time series from the Weather Research
and Forecasting (WRF) model with stochastic simulations, providing consistent wind power
generation patterns and forecast errors for single wind turbines or aggregated wind farms as
time series data with minute-scale resolution.
The meteorological data enables modeling wind and solar PV generation for both current and
future scenarios, accounting for changes in the geographical distribution of installations. The
fluctuation modeling, based on [38], ensures realistic power spectral densities for wind speeds
at individual locations and maintains coherence functions between different locations. These
fluctuations are generated as stochastic time series and integrated with the WRF data. Since
the fluctuation simulations have an expected value of zero, their addition does not affect the
long-term mean wind speeds derived from WRF.

Balmorel is an energy system optimization model that operates on a bottom-up, partial equilib-

is specially used in this thesis to conduct optimal power flow. Balmorel uses receives CorRES

three years in the future 2030, 2040, and 2050. The full-year hourly day-ahead market data of

Chapter 6
Conclusion and Future Works
This work applies a tool capable of solving both OPF and multi-period TNEP problems to
achieve a market-based topology optimization of an offshore HVDC grid. The tool can han-
dle both radial and meshed grid structures. Since grid topology optimization is a long-term
investment decision, it is crucial to use accurate input data, specifically, generation patterns
of VRE and market prices. These inputs are obtained from two additional modeling tools:
one tailored to generate energy hub-specific patterns based on detailed properties and loca-
tions, and another providing day-ahead market data based on projected power system and
capacity developments over the next 25 years. The objective function of the OPF problem
is designed to maximize the social welfare of the entire system, which requires system-wide
coordination. This optimization aligns with the goal of connecting market zones to enhance
the efficient utilization of offshore wind energy and support price convergence between zones.
The OPF objective function specifically maximizes power extraction profits at the onshore
AC nodes, ensuring maximum power supply with the highest economic benefit for the system
operators. As a result, energy flows toward zones with the highest prices. Since high prices
are typically correlated with higher demand, the OPF indirectly maximizes demand fulfillment.
These three models are integrated to analyze a case study in the North Sea region from both
electrical and economic perspectives. It is assumed that an radially connected architecture of
energy hubs already exists and the question is how the topology can be expanded. This complies
to the sequential development nature of power systems. The results indicate that the TNEP
solution is highly dependent on candidate branch installations, discount rates, and prices. The
greater the price differences between zones, the higher the feasibility of new installations. The
findings suggest that meshed connections are less effective than radial configurations, where
hubs are first connected radially and then linked to neighboring hubs. In the case study in-
volving the Netherlands, Germany, and Denmark, offshore connections between NL-DE and
DE-DK yield higher profits than a direct NL-DK connection.
The topology optimization and power flow analysis can be particularly useful for stakehold-
ers looking to invest in new interconnectors between energy hubs, as well as for offshore power
system analysts. Given the high costs associated with installing wind farms, energy hubs, and
converters, evaluating interconnections at an early planning stage can cause long-term economic
efficiency. The methodology presented in this study can be extended to include AC grids and
larger energy systems.

6.1
Limitations
In this study, the operation and control of energy hubs, specifically focusing on power flow
optimization, are assumed to be centrally managed with the goal of maximizing overall social
welfare. However, in reality, future energy hubs will likely be operated by the respective coun-
tries that own them, each aiming to maximize its own energy production benefits.
The optimal power flow and topology optimization in this work are based on the assump-
tion that all energy trades take place in the day-ahead market. This implies that there are no
forecast errors for generation or load, meaning all generated power is traded at the day-ahead
market prices provided by the Balmorel model. Under this framework, analyzing trades in the
balancing market is not possible, as the OPF does not account for load profiles. The conse-
quent is, selling reserve capacity from energy hubs or transmission lines cannot be considered.
However, since trading in the balancing market generally yields higher benefits for system op-
erators, excluding it leads to a lower calculated NPV. For the feasibility study, this represents
a worst-case scenario.
6.2
Future Works
Additional operational constraints and objectives
With the flexibility of the open-source code of in PowerModelsACDC, it is possible to imple-
ment constraints than may have to be considered do national and international regulations.
For example the 70% rule, introduced in Sec.2.1, or selling priorities to home country can be
defined as the constraints on the branch power flow variable. Other objectives such as benefit
models from curtailment with a proper weighting can be added to the objective function of
TNEP.
Home market vs. offshore bidding zone model
As a future work, the OBZ mentioned in Sec. 2.1 can be incorporated through additional
market models to derive price patterns which comply more the future plans of the energy hubs.
Additionally, from the perspective of energy hub planners, integrating energy storage facilities
modeled as offshore loads could be explored, as these facilities would also influence market
dynamics and optimization strategies.
