Abstract
Bronchoscopy is a medical procedure that uses a bronchoscope (a thin, lighted view­
ing tube) with a camera at the end that allows doctors to observe the lungs through the
bronchi, diagnose and treat lung diseases. Compared with other lung diagnostic methods
(lung CT), it has the characteristics of low cost, fast diagnosis, direct operation (removal,
removal of foreign objects), and intuitive effect (endoscopic images are easy to diagnose).
This paper aims to build a three­dimensional reconstruction system to achieve the trans­
formation of endoscopic images into 3D bronchial shapes, to achieve navigation in the
bronchi and the development of bronchial robot control strategies.
Research in recent years has shown that deep learning techniques can efficiently com­
plete depth estimation and three­dimensional reconstruction, but due to the scarcity of
medical endoscope datasets and the inability of models to adapt to medical scenarios,
direct application to medical scenarios usually leads to suboptimal performance. For the
bronchial application scenario of this paper, data collection was carried out in a virtual
simulation environment and a real bronchial model to establish my dataset; two networks
with different characteristics were used to tune and train the generated dataset, and the
depth estimation and 3D reconstruction results from different networks were compared,
evaluated, and discussed.
After getting relatively accurate depth results, we used bronchoscope videos as input to
conduct experiments on depth estimation and 3D reconstruction, and evaluated the recon­
struction speed and quality. The experimental results show that this method significantly
improves the performance of the network in real­world captured endoscope images and
can be used to perform the 3D reconstruction.
Keywords: Endoscope tracking, 3D reconstruction, Bronchoscopy, Visual SLAM, Depth
estimation, Self­supervised learning

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Lung diseases, such as lung cancer, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and
pneumonia, pose a significant global health challenge, affecting millions of people annu­
ally [1]. Among these, lung and bronchial cancer remains the leading cause of cancer­
related deaths in the United States [2]. A 2018 survey by the American Cancer Society
estimated approximately 1.7 million new cancer cases involving lung or bronchial cancer,
with a mortality rate reaching 25.27%. Early detection and timely intervention are essen­
tial to mitigate the burden of these diseases [3], reduce morbidity, and improve patient
outcomes by identifying these cancers in their preliminary stages [4].
Figure 1.1: Medical bronchoscopy examination
During bronchoscopy (Figure 1.1 https://guysandstthomasspecialistcare.co.uk), a flexible
bronchoscope is inserted through the patient’s mouth or nose into the bronchus to ob­
serve the inner surface of the bronchial lumen. The bronchoscope camera transmits real­
time images to a monitor, allowing the doctor to navigate the airways [5]. However, the
complex tree­shaped bronchial structure and the narrow field of view make it difficult for
physicians to locate specific branches that require examination without additional navi­
gation aids [6]. Depth estimation plays a critical role in bronchoscopy, enabling accurate
surface reconstruction and position tracking for improved navigation. However, the in­
tricate structure, narrow diameter, poor lighting, and lack of distinctive texture features
pose significant challenges for accurate depth estimation and 3D reconstruction. Tradi­
tional multiview geometry methods, such as SfM [7] and SLAM [8], have demonstrated
limited performance in this context.
In recent years, deep learning methods have shown remarkable success in depth esti­
mation in various domains [9]. However, their application in the medical field is hindered
by issues like privacy concerns, the scarcity of large­scale datasets with accurate depth
ground truth, and limited computing resources. Consequently, self­supervised learning
(SSL) has emerged as a popular strategy, using geometric constraints between adjacent
video frames to estimate depth and pose without requiring extensive annotations [10].

The foundation model has garnered attention for its exceptional performance in various
tasks [11]. With a large number of parameters, extensive training data, and comprehen­
sive training strategies, these models excel in downstream tasks involving visual, tex­
tual, and multi­modal inputs. However, their performance often declines in specialized
domains like endoscopy [12]. Training a foundation model from scratch for medical ap­
plications faces challenges such as limited annotated data and insufficient computational
resources. To address this, researchers are increasingly focusing on optimizing existing
foundation models through parameter adjustments and fine­tuning for specific medical
scenarios [13].
Unlike traditional foundation model applications, which often focus on tasks such as seg­
mentation and detection. This thesis explores the application to surgical depth estimation,
with the aim to building a simple system that reconstructs bronchial shapes (point clouds)
directly from endoscopic image sequences.
1.2
Thesis Overview
In current bronchial examinations, surgeons manually navigate the bronchoscope to tar­
get nodules that requires diagnosis and treatment by relying on endoscopic images com­
bined with preoperative lung CT scans [14]. However, the limited information provided by
the bronchoscope and the manual nature of these operations result in a time­consuming
and costly process [15]. This highlights the need for effective bronchoscope position­
ing methods to assist in intervention. Advanced technologies, such as electromagnetic
sensors and 3D shape sensing, have shown promise in addressing these challenges.
This thesis investigates a visual navigation method (VNB) for bronchial examinations. The
approach involves equipping the bronchoscope with a monocular camera to capture im­
age data as it traverses the bronchus [16, 17]. Using a depth estimation network trained
on specialized datasets, the captured images are converted to 3D point clouds. By esti­
mating the relative camera positions from consecutive images, the camera’s trajectory can
be determined. These point clouds are aligned with the estimated camera coordinates to
reconstruct the 3D shape of the bronchial structure as the bronchoscope advances.
1.2.1
Problem Statement
To develop and validate the proposed VNB approach, two bronchial datasets with accu­
rate depth ground truth were obtained: one from simulations in a virtual environment and
the other from a 3D­printed rigid bronchial model. These datasets were used to fine­tune
and train two depth estimation neural networks, enabling the generation of depth images
from endoscopic images.
In the actual application scenario, an Ambu® bronchoscope with monocular camera (Fig­
ure 1.2 https://www.ambu.com) is utilized to collect images of the bronchial interior. The
trained depth estimation models are used to convert these images into depth maps. Sub­
sequently, reconstruct the 3D shape of the bronchus from the depth maps. The accuracy
of the reconstructed shape is evaluated by comparing it to the actual bronchial structure,
and the deviations are analyzed and discussed.

Related Work
Depth estimation and 3D reconstruction are fundamental tasks in computer vision, driving
applications such as robotic navigation, autonomous driving, and virtual reality [18, 19, 20,
21, 22]. In the medical domain, they are increasingly employed in areas such as surgical
navigation, image segmentation, and surgical robotics, providing improved spatial under­
standing. Although extensively studied in natural scenes, applying these techniques to
endoscopic images poses unique challenges due to the complex and constrained medical
environments.
This section introduces related work in depth estimation and 3D reconstruction, with a
focus on their applications in endoscopic scenarios.
2.1
Monocular Depth Estimation
Scene depth estimation plays a crucial role in computer vision, enabling the perception
and understanding of various environments. Earlier active depth estimation methods re­
lied primarily on lasers, structured light, and surface reflections to obtain point clouds,
perform surface reconstruction, and estimate depth information [23]. However, generat­
ing dense and accurate depth maps typically incurs significant labor and computational
resource costs [24]. As a result, image­based depth estimation has emerged as the dom­
inant research approach, offering broad applicability across a wide range of scenarios
[25].
The early methods of image­based depth estimation relied on depth signals, such as
vanishing points [26], focus/defocus [27], and shadows [28], but were mainly applicable to
constrained scenes. As computer vision advanced, researchers introduced hand­crafted
features and probabilistic models, including scale­invariant feature transform (SIFT) [29],
speeded up robust features (SURF) [30], pyramid histogram of oriented gradients (PHOG)
[31], conditional random fields (CRF) [32], and Markov random fields (MRF) [33]. These
methods employed both parametric and non­parametric learning to predict monocular
depth maps. The advent of deep learning brought significant improvements, particularly
in depth estimation.
Traditionally, image­based depth estimation methods have been based on stereo cam­
eras, where depth is derived from the disparity between two 2D images through stereo
matching and triangulation [34]. However, this approach requires at least two fixed cam­
eras [35] and struggles in scenes with insufficient texture for reliable matching [36]. Con­
sequently, the focus shifted to monocular depth estimation, which uses a single camera
to acquire images or video sequences without the need for additional complex equipment.
This method has become increasingly popular due to its practicality, especially in medical
applications like endoscopy, where a single camera is typically used to capture images of
the bronchial tree.
Monocular depth estimation faces challenges because monocular images lack reliable
stereoscopic relationships, making depth regression an ill­posed problem [37]. Monocular
images represent the three­dimensional world in two dimensions, losing depth information
during imaging. This loss of depth makes it difficult to assess the size, distance, and oc­
clusion of objects. To restore this depth information, various monocular depth estimation
techniques have been developed. In medical scenarios, such as endoscopic bronchial ex­
aminations, accurately estimating the depth of the bronchial structure is crucial the proper

structure of the scene, these methods can aid in understanding and navigating complex
anatomical structures.
2.2
Pose Estimation
Pose estimation for monocular endoscopic image sequences, similar to depth estima­
tion methods, can be categorized into geometry­based and learning­based approaches.
Geometry­based methods typically involve steps such as feature extraction, feature match­
ing, epipolar geometry, perspective­n­point (PnP) and iterative closest point (ICP). How­
ever, these methods heavily rely on the accuracy of feature matching, which is often hin­
dered in endoscopic scenarios due to soft tissue deformation and illumination changes.
Learning­based methods, on the other hand, leverage convolution­based feature en­
coders to extract features and convert them into pose matrices, offering improved ro­
bustness to photometric transformations in endoscopic environments. These methods
have shown significant promise in overcoming the limitations of traditional approaches.
Feature­based visual odometry (VO) is a common technique for pose estimation. The
process begins with identifying the salient features and matching them between frames,
followed by estimating camera ego­motion using principles of multiview geometry [38].
Comparative studies on local feature detectors in endoscopic datasets have highlighted
the limitations of traditional methods. For example, SIFT has shown superior performance
in some scenarios [39], such as colonoscopy datasets augmented with artificial transfor­
mations, but challenges remain in maintaining consistent motion tracking for extended
periods [40]. Techniques like chromo­endoscopy and color channel selection have been
explored to enhance feature detection by increasing saliency. Other methods, such as
spatial motion fields and contour detectors, have further improved feature matching and
density [41].
In contrast, learned feature extractors have shown better performance in terms of feature
density, quality, and pose estimation accuracy. Neural network architectures have been
developed to detect features directly [42] or compute their descriptors [43], with some
designs estimating features and descriptors simultaneously [44]. Vision transformer ar­
chitectures have also shown potential to combine feature detection and matching tasks
[45].
The learned features have been successfully applied to endoluminal VO. For example,
fine­tuning the SuperPoint detector with additional loss functions improved matching and
reconstruction accuracy. Self­supervised models trained on phantom datasets have also
enhanced robustness in challenging regions [46]. Siamese networks [47], convolutional
autoencoders [48], and graph neural networks [49] have been employed to detect and
match features, often outperforming traditional methods in both precision and matching
scores.
End­to­end learning­based pose estimation approaches are increasingly gaining traction.
Unlike feature­based algorithms, these methods consolidate multiple VO pipeline com­
ponents, such as feature extraction, matching, and pose estimation, into a single net­
work. Supervised models have been developed to directly learn camera poses from sim­
ulated endoscopic videos [50], with some incorporating uncertainty modeling to improve
predictions. Additionally, self­supervised approaches have emerged, jointly estimating
depth and camera pose in an end­to­end framework. For example, networks trained with
shared loss functions or integrated with photometric tracking [51] have shown substan­

2.3
3D Reconstruction and Navigation from Endoscopy
images
Depth estimation and 3D reconstruction are critical for tasks such as surgical visualization,
navigation, and recognition. These methods provide the foundation for enhancing visual
clarity, enabling navigation in non­rigid environments, and improving semantic recognition
of anatomical structures.
Various studies have explored mosaicting and stitching techniques to expand the visual
field in endoscopic procedures. For example, a comprehensive overview of endoscopic
image mosaics and surface reconstruction prior to 2014 is summarized in [52]. Hardware
designs for endoscopes with positioning sensors enable image stitching through accu­
rate pose estimation and constraints, addressing errors caused by stomach expansion
and non­rigid deformation [53]. Hybrid rigid registration methods that combine feature
matching and template matching have been used to create full views in probe­based en­
doscopy [54].
To address limitations in traditional stitching methods for non­rigid laparoscopic scenes,
non­rigid SLAM systems have been developed to generate dense deformation fields from
sparse feature matching, compensating for pixel deformations [55]. Multi­spectral en­
doscopy combined with video splicing technology enables the creation of panoramic views
and registration with optical biopsy targeting. Enhanced descriptors, such as improved
ORB descriptors with Gaussian pyramids, have been applied to improve image matching
accuracy for endoscopic mosaics [56].
Real­time stitching in laparoscopic images has also been addressed using non­rigid SLAM
systems that compensate for pixel deformations during stitching [55]. Approaches based
on SIFT feature extraction and K­nearest neighbor feature matching have been applied
to estimate transformation matrices for endoscopic mosaics.
3D reconstruction is a prerequisite for surgical navigation, particularly for registering en­
doscopic 3D reconstructions with preoperative models. Methods have been developed to
match preoperative models with surgical scenes using pose estimation and visual mark­
ers, allowing surgical guidance [143]. Reference point­based registration methods align
CT reconstructions with video images to visualize internal structures during surgery.
Markerless methods, such as Structure from Motion (SfM) and SLAM, have become
prominent for reconstructing 3D endoscopic scenes and enabling visualization through
non­rigid registration. Sparse mapping techniques have been developed for oral environ­
ments using SLAM. For high­risk sinus surgery, enhanced image­based navigation meth­
ods have been introduced. SfM and Multi­View Stereo (MVS) approaches reconstruct
endoscopic point clouds, which are then registered with CT models using Fast Point Fea­
ture Histograms (FPFH) [57]. Real­time registration methods have also been proposed to
assist in environments such as the human uterus and minimally invasive surgeries [58].
Deep learning­based reconstruction methods have gained traction in surgical navigation.
Improved homography models have been trained to achieve image stitching and visual
field expansion for endoscopic applications. Convolutional neural networks trained on or­
gan biomechanics enable non­rigid registration of organ surfaces. Joint training methods
for depth estimation and semantic segmentation have also been developed in arthro­
scopic images.

To address deformable registration challenges, two­stage methods have been proposed
to enhance registration accuracy. Particle filter approaches have been applied to trans­

reliability, confidence assessment methods have been used to eliminate unreliable depth
measurements, enhancing stereo accuracy, and ensuring robust navigation in surgical
environments.
2.4
Datasets
2.4.1
SCARED Dataset
The SCARED (Structured Cadaveric Anatomical Real­time Ego­motion Depth) dataset,
introduced by Allan et al.
(2021), is collected from fresh porcine cadaver abdominal
anatomy. It consists of 35 endoscopic videos paired with point cloud data and ego­motion
ground truth. The dataset provides high­quality depth maps derived from a structured light
projection technique, using a da Vinci Xi endoscope and a projector. This process involves
capturing stereo pairs from multiple camera positions (5­10 per dataset) to generate depth
maps for the left and right eye of the stereo camera pair. The depth values are expressed
in millimeters and invalid pixels are appropriately masked.
Figure 2.1: RGB images in SCARED Dataset
To enhance dataset size, the SCARED dataset (Figure 2.1) includes additional camera
motion and warped depth maps, with known camera poses recorded using the da Vinci
Xi kinematics system. These camera poses are provided as 4x4 matrices, allowing for
accurate spatial alignment of the depth maps. The dataset includes ground truth for each
frame, and the depth maps are accompanied by stereo camera calibration data. The
dataset is split into three parts for training, validation, and testing, with 15,351, 1,705, and
551 frames, respectively.
For depth estimation experiments, the SCARED dataset is used as the primary test set.
Models trained on the SCARED dataset are then applied to the SERV­CT and Hamlyn

2.4.2
EndoSLAM Dataset
The EndoSLAM dataset was specifically designed to support the evaluation of SLAM al­
gorithms in endoscopic environments, offering comprehensive data for endoscopic video,
accurate 6­DoF ground truth poses, organ geometry, and topographical structures. The
dataset consists of both real­world ex­vivo and synthetic data.
For the ex­vivo component, four types of cameras were employed: two capsule endo­
scopes, MiroCam® (320×320 resolution, 3 fps, 170° field of view) and PillCam® COLON2

Dataset Generation from Simulation
Existing research on depth estimation in medical imaging lacks sufficient applications in
bronchoscopic environments. Due to the flexible structure of the bronchi, there are limited
datasets with accurate ground truth are available for study. Neural networks for broncho­
scopic depth estimation require dedicated bronchoscopic image datasets for training, en­
abling the development of models better suited to this environment for 3D reconstruction
and validation. This chapter and the following present two datasets: a virtual broncho­
scopic dataset and a bronchial model dataset, which serve as the foundation for subse­
quent 3D reconstruction tasks.
3.1
Simulation Environment
In order to obtain the dataset used for depth estimation network training and 3D recon­
struction, a highly simulated virtual bronchial environment was constructed. The real
bronchoscope navigation scene was simulated and a high­quality experimental basis was
provided for related research. The construction of the virtual environment mainly includes
the segmentation of the bronchial model, the extraction of the centerline path, and the
rendering and interaction of the virtual bronchoscope.
3.1.1
Construction of the bronchial model
The bronchial model is the core part of the virtual environment (Figure 3.1a), and its con­
struction is based on the patient’s preoperative chest CT scan data. The chest CT image
is preprocessed using the 3D Slicer tool, and the anatomical structure of the bronchial tree
is segmented using the watershed algorithm or the region growth method. To ensure the
accuracy of the model, manual corrections are used during the segmentation process to
optimize the details of small branches and complex areas. The segmentation results are
triangulated to generate a three­dimensional surface model of the bronchus. The conti­
nuity and geometric accuracy of the model surface are ensured, which is convenient for
subsequent rendering and interactive operations.

EndoOmni
5.1.1
Network Overview
EndoOmni is the first foundation model designed for zero­shot cross­dataset depth es­
timation in endoscopy. It adopts a self­learning paradigm, where a teacher model gen­
erates pseudo­labels to guide the training of a student model using both labeled and
unlabeled data. To address the challenges posed by noisy depth labels, EndoOmni incor­
porates a robust training framework that estimates the confidence per­pixel to adaptively
weight learning, thus prioritizing cleaner labels and mitigating the influence of noisy ones.
Furthermore, a weighted scale­and­shift invariant loss (WSSI) is proposed to improve
robustness against domain shifts (Figure 5.1) [61].
Figure 5.1: EndoOmni Network Overview
Experimental evaluations demonstrate that EndoOmni achieves state­of­the­art perfor­
mance in zero­shot relative depth estimation (RDE), outperforming existing medical imag­
ing methods by 33% and general­purpose foundation models by 34%. In addition, when
fine­tuned for metric depth estimation (MDE), EndoOmni consistently surpasses domain­
specific models, maintaining superior accuracy in both in­domain and out­of­domain sce­
narios.

Dense Prediction Transformer (DPT) decoder for depth estimation. The model is trained
in two stages:
• Teacher Model Training: The teacher model is trained on labeled datasets using
the scale­and­shift invariant loss (SSI) to ensure robustness across datasets with
varying depth distributions. Depth labels are derived from stereo cameras, CT reg­
istration, and synthetic data to enhance training diversity.

DV­LoRA
Fine­tuning large foundation models for domain­specific tasks is costly due to their vast
parameters. Low­Rank Adaptation (LoRA) mitigates this by introducing trainable low­
rank matrices into frozen pre­trained weights. However, conventional LoRA has limited
adaptability, especially in complex tasks like endoscopic depth estimation.
To address this, we propose Dynamic Vector­Based Low­Rank Adaptation (DV­LoRA),
which dynamically adjusts fine­tuning during training. Unlike fixed­rank LoRA, DV­LoRA
introduces trainable diagonal matrices to regulate learning in a two­phase adaptation
mechanism.
Mathematical Formulation: Given a pre­trained weight matrix W0 ∈Rd×k, LoRA modifies
the transformation as:
h = W0x + BAx,
(5.23)
where B ∈Rd×r and A ∈Rr×k are trainable low­rank matrices with r ≪min(d, k). The
pre­trained weights W0 remain frozen.
DV­LoRA enhances this by introducing trainable scaling vectors Λu and Λv:
xout = W0xin + ΛvBΛuAxin.
(5.24)
Here, Λu and Λv are diagonal matrices that dynamically scale the LoRA components.
DV­LoRA operates in two phases adaptation strategy:
1. Warm­up Phase: Only A and B are trained, ensuring stable low­rank adaptation.
2. Dynamic Adaptation Phase: A and B are frozen, and Λu and Λv are trained, refining
adaptation.

This section presents the implementation of a real­time 3D point cloud visualization sys­

Initialization of Parameters and Visualization Window
The system initializes the necessary parameters and sets up an Open3D visualization
window using o3d.visualization.Visualizer(). The following configurations are ap­
plied:
• Camera parameters are defined, including the eye position, target point, and up
vector.
• The visualization window size, font settings, and other UI­related parameters are
configured.
Data Acquisition and Preprocessing
Data from multiple directories are read and processed:
• RGB Images: Loaded from the camera image folder.
• Depth Maps: Predicted by the EndoOmni network, normalized, converted to a grayscale
three­channel format, and resized.
• Point Clouds: Generated by projecting depth maps into 3D space using camera
intrinsic parameters.
• Camera Poses: Read from text files, storing transformation matrices that define the
position and orientation of each camera frame relative to the model origin.
All jpg and png image files are read and sorted by numerical order, along with the corre­
sponding .ply point cloud files and pose information files.
Frame­by­Frame Processing and Rendering
For each frame:
• The point cloud is placed at the corresponding camera position, and its color is set
to pink [1, 0.5, 1].
• The camera position is visualized as a point and assigned a black color [0, 0, 0].
• The point cloud and camera position are rendered and displayed before saving the
frame.
• Subsequent frames’ point clouds and camera positions are appended to the previ­
ous ones, maintaining the same coloring scheme.
• The rendered Open3D visualization is saved as a temporary image temp_screen.png
using vis.capture_screen_image() and displayed.

rating:

Conclusion and Future Work
7.1
Conclusion
In order to address the lack of research in the 3D reconstruction of bronchial medical
images, this thesis constructed two datasets: one based on a virtual environment and
the other based on a real bronchial model testing platform. The datasets contain xxx and
yyy bronchial images, corresponding depth maps, and camera pose information for each
frame. During the dataset construction process, several calibration tasks were carried out,
including monocular camera calibration, hand­eye calibration, and pivot calibration, with
multiple coordinate transformations performed. The depth maps from both datasets were
projected into 3D space to generate point clouds for the reconstruction of the bronchial
shape. The reconstruction results were then subjected to error calculation and evaluation.
In practical applications, an accurate and adaptable neural network model is required
to train a neural network tailored to the scenario. Real­time bronchial images are input
into the depth estimation model and pose estimation model to obtain depth maps and
camera pose information. After converting the depth maps into point clouds, the camera
pose is used to reconstruct the bronchial shape. After reviewing cutting­edge research,
two medical domain neural networks, EndoSLAM and EndoDAC, were selected. These
networks include both depth prediction and pose prediction components. The thesis at­
tempts to train these networks; however, as the datasets used for training these networks
were based on abdominal and intestinal data, which have significant differences from
bronchial data (including color richness and the number of features that can be captured),
these factors greatly affect the feature extraction quality of the neural networks, thereby
influencing the training results. As mentioned in Chapter 5, the training process on our
dataset failed, and accurate depth and pose predictions could not be obtained. There­
fore, a depth estimation network specifically designed for the bronchial environment, En­
doOmni, was chosen. The pre­trained model was used for depth estimation, resulting in
high­quality predicted depth maps, which were used for 3D reconstruction. Pose infor­
mation was obtained from the pose data in the model dataset and the pose estimation
network in EndoSLAM for 3D reconstruction experiments. However, due to inaccurate
positional information caused by sensor movement during the model dataset collection
and pivot calibration errors due to manufacturing deviations in the bronchial model, the
3D reconstruction based on the model dataset only revealed the general shape but was
not accurate. On the other hand, the pose network in EndoSLAM had not been trained
with bronchial images, so its fitting to bronchial images was not ideal, resulting in inaccu­
rate 3D reconstruction based on PoseNet pose information. Error calculation and quality
assessment were conducted for the 3D reconstruction of both approaches.
Finally, a simple system was built to read the point cloud file sequences and correspond­
ing camera pose information, enabling the rapid reconstruction and visualization of the
bronchial shape.
7.2
Future Work
Due to my lack of experience in debugging and tuning neural network training processes,
I encountered significant difficulties in attempting to fine­tune the neural network, which
resulted in unsatisfactory training model outcomes. Additionally, delays in the delivery
of components for the bronchial model testing platform, manufactured by the company,
Autonomous Visually Navigated Bronchoscopy
55
==PAGE:64==
prevented me from progressing according to the planned schedule. I only received the
bronchial model in early January, which meant that much of the work (such as the En­
doOmni section in Chapters 4 and 5, and Chapter 6) did not begin until early January.
These negative influences forced me to expedite the implementation of the entire pipeline,
leaving little time to focus on many details. More comprehensive experiments could be
conducted to improve this work. Below are some potential directions for further improve­
ment.
Measurement and Elimination of Errors in the Model Dataset Collection
When building the dataset collection platform, more reasonable methods should be used
to fix the sensor probe onto the bronchoscope to avoid movement during the collection
process. Regarding manufacturing errors in the bronchial model, more precise measure­
ments of the model platform should be performed to determine the extent of the errors
and consider optimization methods for coordinate calibration based on these errors. This
would make the pose information in the dataset more accurate and reliable.
Effective Deep Learning Training to Obtain Accurate Depth and Pose
Estimation Network Models
Further modifications to the network structure and adjustments to the loss function should
be made to ensure that the trained model achieves better validation results on the dataset.
These models could then be used for 3D reconstruction.
