Abstract 
The EU Water Framework Directive (2000) requires the achievement of good ecological and chemical status 
of European water bodies especially by the prevention of point and non-point source pollution. Since 
significant efforts have been achieved in the past decades to reduce impacts from waste water treatment 
plants (WWTP), the attention of municipalities in most urban areas focuses more and more on impacts of 
combined sewer overflows (CSO). Indeed, pollutant load discharged into the environment without a proper 
treatment can affect the quality of receiving water bodies.  
Different studies show the importance of an integrated view to understand the observed dynamics of the 
pollutants. Measurements carried out through on-line sensors allow suitable dynamic measurements of 
critical parameters like COD or NH4 (Langergraber 2003). 
In this perspective of increasing interest in water quality measurements, an on-line sensor was installed at 
CSO structure of the sewer system in Ålebækken, a catchment located in the northern part of Lyngby- 
Taarbæk municipality. A team of several students from DTU Environment developed their projects working 
on the data collected by these sensors, focusing on different aspects of the procedure of data collection, 
analysis and developing.  The collection of the quality data was challenging due to the arduous conditions in 
the sewer system and to some uncertainties relative to the measurement devices. 
The aim of the present Ms.C Thesis is to build up and to calibrate a WEST model of urban drainage which is 
representative of the catchment. The first step has been an analysis of the available data collected at the CSO 
structure between December 2016 and March 2017. Rain events were defined, and flow and ammonia 
concentration data analysed.  
By using the WEST model with Matlab as an interface it has been possible to do a calibration on the model 
parameters using both the data collected by the on-line sensor mentioned above and the ones coming from 
the Italian catchment Cascina Scala (Lombardy). It makes sense because the latter has different characteristic 
of extension and population, but the same layout. One of the main differences between them is that quality 
data from Cascina Scala catchment are collected with an autosampler, in a less continuative way. Two 
calibration series were performed: one on the water quantity module (flow) and another one on the water 
quality module (ammonia). Both of them were investigated through a traditional calibration method 
(fminsearch). The quantity calibration in Ålebækken case study has been performed on a long-term basis in 
order to obtain results representative not only in occasion of rain events, whereas the quality calibration had 
to be carried out on two shorter periods, because of the big amount of missing data. Concerning Cascina 
Scala catchment, only event-based data were available, hence an event-based calibration was the only 
possible solution. 
Finally, the load of pollutants during the rain events and during the overflows has been calculated in the two 
case studies.

1. Introduction 
Combined sewer networks are present in many large cities all over the world: in the United Kingdom about 
70% of the total length of the sewers is combined, in France and Germany the figure is also round 70%, and 
in Denmark the percentage is 45% (Butler and Davies 2011). 
These networks carry wastewater and storm water. During small rain events, the water is carried to 
wastewater treatment plants (WWTP), where it is treated before being released to the environment (a river 
or sea) , as schematically represented in Figure 1.1.  However, during heavy rain events, or due to design 
flaws, the network capacity can be easily exceeded. This situation is getting worse because of an increase in 
the peak flow and peak volumes generated by the gradual expansion of the urban areas, and corresponding 
increase in waterproof areas, in addition to the issues due to climate change. Thus, the urban surface flooding 
as well as untreated water is discharged directly to the enviroment, known as combined sewer overflow 
(CSO). 
 
 
FIGURE 1.1:  COMBINED SYSTEM (SCHEMATIC PLAN) (BUTLER AND DAVIES, 2011). 
CSOs could be very harmful for the environment and the health of the water body because of their heavy 
pollutant content. Wastewater contains untreated or partially treated human and industrial waste including 
suspended solids, bacteria, toxic pollutants and oxygen demanding compounds such as ammonia (NH3 and 
NH4+). A large quantity of degradable organic components can cause an oxygen deficit in the receiving 
waters, which would lead to an increase in the mortality of aquatic organisms. Generally, potential threats 
to a water receiver caused by discharges of untreated sewage from a combined sewage system may be short-
term, delayed, and long term, and the impact could be hydraulic, chemical, physical, sanitary, biochemical, 
and aesthetic (Brzezińska, Zawilski, and Sakson 2016). 
Since the 1970s, an increased awareness in European critical condition of water bodies had led to the 
issuance of European directives, to limit and control the quality characteristics of the water released in rivers. 
Some of the most relevant ones are: 
• 
The Bathing Water Directive (76/160/EEC), which required ‘bathing waters’ to be designated, and 
their quality to comply with bacterial standards;

• 
The Urban Waste Water Treatment Directive (91/271/EEC), that specified a minimum level of 
wastewater treatment, based on the urban population size and the receiving water type, to be 
achieved by 2005; 
• 
The Water Framework Directive (2000/60/EC), a European Union directive which commits European 
Union member states to achieve good qualitative and quantitative status of all water bodies 
(including marine waters up to one nautical mile from shore) by 2015. 
To reach the goal of a high performance level in hydraulic and environmental protection, both careful urban 
planning and water management of stormwater runoff in urbanized areas are essential. 
Two different types of solutions are possible. At first, retention tanks, which are usually built along the 
network to store the water during the peak rain intensity periods and later release it at lower flow rates. 
Nevertheless, these infrastructures are very expensive and difficult to locate in already built up areas. The 
other possible solution is to operate a monitoring of the water quality on the existing sewer system using 
high time resolution measurements, which is definitely less costly than the previous solution. Following this 
approach, a better understanding of the dynamic of the pollution phenomena is possible, and a strategy can 
be set up. 
A very efficient tool to manage the urban water systems, as well as to evaluate and mitigate the effects of 
the CSOs, is a water quality and flow model. Mathematical models allow to understand and explore the 
system in question: they enable to test various operating strategies and to see their effects on the long term. 
They allow as well to have a global vision of the system composed of sewer system, wastewater treatment 
plant and receiving water body. In this project, a holistic model was used to evaluate and forecast the 
pollutants’ distribution in relation with the flow and accordingly to develop strategies for reducing pollutants’ 
load discharged in the environment during CSOs. 
It is within this context that reliable monitoring data turn out to be indispensable in order to set up a realistic 
model and to calibrate its parameters. The progress conducted over the last decades has been made possible 
by advances in instrumentation technology (sensors and actuators). Most existing monitoring studies are 
based on autosampling and, therefore, they often focus on loads rather than dynamics while online sensors 
allow suitable dynamic measurements of critical parameters like COD or NH4 (Langergraber et al. 2003). 
Autosampling procedure involves several possible errors of measurement: sampling, sample transport, 
storage, and analytical errors.  
Nevertheless, the collection of high accuracy and high precision data is tough due to the subjected 
challenging condition of the on-line sensor (e.g. clogging, fouling), resulting in noise, errors and sensor 
failures. Note that their cleaning and maintenance is rarely performed, which can also affect the sensor 
accuracy. There are as well several gaps of missing data, connected to memory limitation in the sensor 
controller device, or procedures of maintenance and calibration of the sensor. When clearly observed, it is 
possible to run a calibration of the sensors, in order to evaluate and adjust these common systematic errors 
(Jézéquel 2014). The calibration requires a remarkable effort in terms of time and because sensors not always 
work as expected. Thus, sometimes the results appear not to be realistic.  
It should be pointed out that uncertainty is intrinsic in any modelling process, and can be classified, as 
suggested by Vicens et al. (1975), into three categories: model uncertainties, parameter uncertainties, and 
uncertainty inherent in natural processes. Also the uncertainties related to the data collection, mentioned 
above, must be considered. Uncertainties cannot be eliminated, but their amplitude should be estimated 
and, if possible, reduced. It is necessary to understand their sources and impact on model predictions. 
(Deletic et al. 2012).

1.1. Objectives 
This paper presents the set-up of a lumped conceptual model of Ålebækken catchment through the software 
WEST, using Matlab as an interface, where simulations on the hydraulic part and on the water quality part 
have been performed. WEST is a software for dynamic modelling and simulation designed for wastewater 
treatment plants (WWTP). Basically, any kind of process that can be described by ordinary differential 
equations (ODE) can be represented and simulated. 
The present model will be run with parameters and data from Ålebækken catchment and from Cascina Scala 
catchment. Thus, the first calibration will be based on data collected at an open CSO placed in Ålebækken, 
where an online sensor for water level and one for quality are installed. The second calibration will be made 
exploiting data from the Italian catchment placed in Cascina Scala (Pavia, Lombardia). Rain data from 
Ålebækken are collected with a 1 minute timestep through four rain gauges, located in Ermelundværket, 
Furesø park, Gladsaxe, and Mølleåværket, while continuous quality data are collected by an on-line sensor 
located at the CSO structure. Moreover, water level measurements, from which flow data are obtained, are 
collected at the CSO structure with a 10-minute timestep. For what concern Cascina Scala rain data, they are 
picked up through two rain gauges, one placed in the middle of the catchment, and the other one at the 
outlet. Finally, Cascina Scala quality measurements are sampled through an autosampler with a variable 
timestep, depending on the flow rate, whereas the water level is measured through a flowmeter. 
The aim of this thesis is to set up a model capable to simulate flow and pollutant concentrations and loads, 
and compare the model performances between calibration made with two different sets of parameters and 
data, collected by following different strategies, which are the traditional sampling and the use of online 
sensors. Therefore, the objectives of the thesis are the following: 
• 
To identify the best fitting values of the parameters on both hydraulic and quality modules by 
performing a traditional calibration method;  
• 
To detect if the data collected with an autosampler carry to a similar fit between measured and 
simulated data than data collected by an on-line sensor; 
• 
To define if the model achieves its primary goal of adequately representing both Ålebækken and 
Cascina Scala pollutants’ concentration and flow, by means of a pairwise comparison of measured 
data and model predictions.

2. Theoretical background 
 
Firstly, an overview about what water quality represents and the most important laws about this issue are 
presented. Finally, a focus on the pollutants that turned out to be relevant in the present project will be

2.1. Water quality in sewer systems 
The term ‘water quality’ refers to all the constituent of water, including dissolved substances and any other 
substances conveyed by the water. In this project a flow rate and water quality analysis at a CSO structure, 
(the outlet of a combined sewage system) will be carried out. Combined Sewage flow includes three main 
components: base sanitary flow (wastewater), rainfall derived inflow (stormwater) and infiltration.  
The definition of wastewater (according to EU Directive 91/27/ECC) is water that is either biological, 
chemical, physical or thermal polluted (EEC Council 1991).  
Wastewater can have a number of definitions, as “a combination” of: 
• 
Domestic effluent consisting of blackwater (excreta, urine and fecal sludge) and greywater (kitchen 
and bathing wastewater); 
• 
Water from commercial establishments and institutions including hospitals; 
• 
Industrial effluent, stormwater and other urban run-off; 
• 
Agricultural, horticultural and aquaculture effluent, either dissolved or as suspended matter” 
Wastewater from industry and residential areas contain different types of contaminants such as pathogenic 
micro-organism, solids (gross solids, grit, suspended solids, volatile solids), organic and inorganic compound, 
nitrogen, phosphorus, sulphur, hydrocarbons, FOG (Fats, oils and grease), heavy metals and synthetic 
compounds.  
As previously anticipated, water quality describes a board spectrum of items related to how we identify water 
concerns and how we collectively address them. However, water quality is generally defined as “the 
chemical, physical and biological characteristics of water, usually in respect to its suitability for a designated 
use”.  
An immediate indicator of water quality is the concentration of pollutant that is the strength of polluted 
liquid containing a constituent of mass M in water of volume V and it is given by c = M/V, usually expressed

2.1.1. Ammonia 
Total nitrogen (N) exists in nature in four main forms: organic nitrogen, ammonia, nitrite and nitrate. 
Nitrogen is present in water in the form of ammonia, nitrite and nitrate, which are inorganic forms of 
nitrogen. A reduction of the dissolved nitrogen concentration in water bodies is needed, since nitrogen 
together with phosphorus are responsible of the over enrichment of the receiving environment. This over 
enrichment can lead to explosive plant growth and algae blooms, resulting in eutrophication, fish kills etc. 
Ammonia exists in solutions in two forms: Ammonium ion 𝑁𝐻4
+ and Ammonia gas 𝑁𝐻3 , depending on the 
pH and temperature of the wastewater. In areas with hard water, like Denmark, since the pH of sewer 
system is between 6.5 and 7, the most of the ammonia is present in the form of ammonium ion (Butler and 
Davies 2011). "Ammonia nitrogen" (i.e. NH3+ NH4+) is reported to be as average 30 mg-N/l in moderate 
municipal wastewaters. However, it may vary in the range of 12 and 50 mg/l, representing very diluted to 
concentrated wastewater respectively (Henze et al., 2001). 
Human excreta are responsible for a large proportion of the organic nitrogen in wastewater (94%). Of this 
percentage 50% derives from urine (urea) which is rapidly converted to ammonia under both aerobic and 
anaerobic conditions (Butler and Davies 2011). 
In the light of this, the ammonium concentration of wastewater is a well-known indicator for the proportion 
of faeces in water. Therefore, ammonium is found to be the most important of the four available AN-ISE on-
line sensor parameters to focus on. 
 
2.1.2. BOD5 
An indirect indication of the organic material amount in a wastewater can be derived from one of two tests: 
the biochemical oxygen demand value (BOD) or the chemical oxygen demand (COD). BOD5 test is a laboratory 
simulation of the microbial processes occurring in water contaminated with organic compounds. It measures 
the DO consumed in a sample diluted in a 300 ml bottle during a specified incubation period (usually 5 days 
at a temperature of 20 °C in darkness).  The DO is used by micro-organisms as they break down organic 
material and certain inorganic compounds. 
BOD5 is a very common parameter used in the control of treated wastewater effluent quality through the 
setting and monitoring of discharge consent standards (e.g. 25 mg/l). Rivers are considered to be polluted if 
their BOD5 exceeds 5 mg/l, and 3 mg/l is required for salmonid rivers (EEC Council 1991).

2.1.4. TSS (Total Suspended Solids) 
Total Suspended Solids (TSS) characterize the unsolved substances in wastewater. They represent a common 
indicator of water quality, indeed, TSS constitute the main vector of certain contaminants in  combined sewer 
systems during wet weather conditions (Gasperi et al. 2008).  According to the analysis performed by 
(Larrarte and Pons 2011), the concentration of TSS in raw wastewater lies below 500 mg/l in Western 
countries, with an average value of about 250 mg/l. The predominant particles in suspension during both dry 
and wet weather flows are approximately 40μm in size, and primarily attributed to sanitary solids mixed with 
sediments entering from surface source. Inorganic compounds such as sand, gravel and stones can be 
dominating in cases where the wastewater consists of a large part of rainwater. Most of the suspended 
material in combined sewer flow (≅90%) is organic and is biochemically active with the capacity to absorb 
pollutants. Settling velocities are usually less than 10mm/s (Crabtree et al., 1991).

3. Modelling approach 
 
In Section 3, an overview about the general concepts of modelling which are significant in this context will 
be presented. In particular, a closer look on integrated urban wastewater models and the process of 
calibration, crucial for the present work will be assessed. After that, a focus on WEST software will be

3.1. General concepts of modelling 
In a computer model, mathematical relationships that represent the behaviour of a system are numerically 
solved. Each model comes with four essential elements: 
• 
Forcing function or external variables - the external inputs of the model; 
• 
state variables – describing the conditions of the system; 
• 
mathematical equations – describing the relationships between forcing functions and state variables; 
• 
parameters – coefficient in the mathematical representation of process which are the objective of 
calibration. 
Once the model is built, a sensitivity analysis, a calibration and a validation must be performed. A sensitivity 
analysis consists in detecting which are the parameters that mostly affect the behaviour of the model. The 
validation consists of an objective test to show how well the model output fits the data.  A more detailed 
focus on calibration will be made in the following paragraph, whereas no deepenings will be done on the two 
other phases of the model construction (sensitivity analysis and validation) because they lie outside the aim 
of the present work. The results from both of these phases will be taken from the work made by Olesen 
(2015). 
 There are several distinctions between the different type of models, and only the ones that are relevant for 
the present project will be presented below. If any of the variables in the model are regarded as random 
variables having a probability distribution, then the model is a stochastic model, otherwise the model is 
deterministic. Stochastic and deterministic models may be classified into either conceptual or empirical, 
depending on weather the model is based on physical laws or not. Distributed and lumped models are also 
used to classify models. These models describe how the model treats spatial variability. A lumped model does 
not take  into account the spatial distribution of the input, whereas distributed models include spatial 
variability (Zoppou 2001). 
As widely anticipated before, in this thesis a lumped conceptual model of flow and pollutant load is developed 
at the CSO structure, placed at the outlet of a combined sewer system.  
3.1.1. Integrated urban water modelling 
The construction of an integrated model is an essential step for the development of an approach which looks 
at the sewer system, at the WWTP and at the water body. Representing and understanding the urban system 
as a whole potentially allows better and more effective solutions to be engineered (Butler and Davies 2011). 
Indeed, it allows to monitor different global scenarios, to develop different hypothesis and observe the 
effects on the whole system on the long term. 
A complete urban storm water integrated model includes rainfall-runoff modelling and transport modelling. 
Rainfall-runoff modelling is based on the generation of surface and sub-surface runoff from precipitation 
excess, the washoff and build-up of pollutants from impervious surfaces. The transport modelling considers 
the routing of flow and pollutants through the storm water infrastructure (Zoppou 2001). Building a model 
is a challenging task as ruling physical processes must be properly taken into account, secondary processes

3.1.2. Calibration 
An integrated model, as any other model, need to be calibrated and validated to enhance its reliability and 
reduce the uncertainties in the model output to a sufficient level. To calibrate a model means to estimate 
the value of its parameters in order to obtain simulated data that are as close as possible to the measured 
data. Some of the parameters can be determined through direct measurements, but most of them are 
conceptual representation of abstract characteristics of the catchment and must be estimated through a 
trial-and-error process (i.e. modifying at every iteration the value of the parameter as long as you get the 
desired accuracy and precision). 
The evaluation of a model performance consists in quantifying the difference between the simulated and the 
measured output and is calculated by the equation called objective function. The choice of the objective 
function  is strongly dependent on the field of application and on the objectives of the modelling exercise 
(Bennett et al. 2013). Some of the most used are: Rout Mean Square Error (RMSE), Mean Error Absolute 
Value (ABSERR), Mean Error (BIAS), Peak Error (PDIFF), Nash Efficency (NS) and Number of Sign Changes 
(NSC). For the present work, the RMSE was chosen as goodness evaluation tool, and calculated as follow

3.2. WEST software 
In this project and case study, WEST (Wastewater treatment plant Engine for Simulation and Training) is used

WEST was developed for dynamic modelling and simulation of wastewater treatment plants. However, WEST 
can be seen as a general simulation environment for computing the dynamics in a network of interlinked 
elements so any kind of processes that can be described by ordinary differential equations (ODEs) can be 
represented in and simulated by WEST.  
For the current project, a subsection of WEST called WESTforOPTIMIZATION will be used, which provides a

In the “configuration environment” the user can graphically build the considered system under study, since 
models are described in MSL-User (Model Specific Language). The model base is aimed at maximum reuse of 
exiting knowledge and is therefore structured hierarchically. All reusable knowledge, such as mass balance, 
physical units, default parameter values and applicable ranges,  is centrally defined ( a-M. Solvi et al. 2005). 
Therefore, for the description of a highly detailed system with a short computational time, the Tornado

Infiltration of external water 
Figure 3.6 shows that there is always a certain volume of water inside the sewer system, even when the 
consumption is assumed to be the lowest, and this phenomenon is due to the intrusion of external water. 
Indeed, a vast volume of unwanted water intrudes into sewer systems through leaky joints or connected 
house drains, especially after rain events, when the soil is full. Infiltration inflow is originally non-polluted so 
that it is not necessary to direct it to any treatment. Even more than 2/3 of the water passing the plant is 
originally non- or less-polluted (Weiß, Brombach, and Haller 2002). The main consequences of the additional 
discharge of water is dilution of water carried to the WWTP, but also lessened sewer transport and storage 
capacity and flushing effects, besides increasing operational costs e.g. of pumping facilities. According to 
studies carried using different approaches (Weiß, Brombach, and Haller 2002) and based on timeseries of 
effluents to 34 different WWTP in South-West Germany, average parasite water flow volumes in sewer 
systems can be assumed as high as wastewater volumes.

3.2.2.1. 
Rain and evaporation  
Rain input data can be fed into WEST model in a simple time-rain vector format using a time interval that 
should be low enough (e.g. 5 minutes) to make sure to simulate peaks that activate combined sewer overflow 
( a-M. Solvi et al. 2005). Time in WEST is expressed in fractions of one day, then attention must be paid to

Evaporation is the vaporisation of water from plants and water bodies and therefore its removal from surface 
runoff. The amount of evaporation is significantly higher in summer than in winter, and the same is true for 
day and night. Its effect during short duration rainfall is negligible, and it was assumed that evaporation losses 
are low compared to other losses. The default value of 657 mm, given by WEST, represents the mean annual 
evaporation.

Losses 
Losses consist of vegetal interception, depression storage, infiltration and evapo-transpiration. All these 
contributes have to be estimated (or calibrated) and subtracted from the relative total bloc rain. The kind of 
loss to which the effective rain is subjected depends on the proportion of impervious and pervious area, 
expressed by the factor 𝑓𝑖/𝑝 .Indeed, whereas evaporation, wetting and depression filling are taking place for 
both surfaces, infiltration into the soil only happens on pervious surfaces. A detailed description of losses, 
with their analytical expressions, can be found in  Solvi et al. (2005)

3.2.4. Limitations 
A lumped model differs from a distributed model because the flow is calculated as a function of time alone 
at a particular location, while in a distributed system routing the flow is calculated as a function of space and 
time throughout the system (Chow, 1988). This is a simplification of the physical reality that is applied in 
order to build an easier model.  
Another approximation consists in considering the rain from one rain gauge as equally distributed on the 
whole catchment, not considering the flow routing processes. It is also a strong simplification, because the 
inhomogeneous spatial distribution of the rain could lead to different results. 
CSOs are not included in the basic WEST library. A way to recreate a CSO structure is to insert a tank, and 
then set the maximum flow to the flow where it is expected that no CSO occur. If this flow is exceeded, the 
CSO water can be taken out of the sewage system.

longer than 60 minutes between two tilts, the precipitation events are separated into two events. If there is 
only a tilt, no event is created. (..) A precipitation event always starts at the time of the first tilt minus 1 minute 
The intensity of the first minute is the amount of precipitation in this minute divided by the time difference 1 
minute. The intensity of a later stage in the event is defined as 0.2 mm precipitation (corresponding to a tilt, 
i.e. the spatial resolution of the meter) equally distributed to the previous tilt, while the rest is said to have 
fallen within the last minute.” (Danish Meterological Institute 1993). Measurements are presented in KM-2 
format, which consists of a status line and a number of fixed-intensity rain intensities. There are no empty 
lines in the format. An example is given in Figure 4.5:

4.4. Calibration 
The concept of calibration was explained in Paragraph 3.1.2. In the following section the practical calibration 
procedure will be presented. Calibration will be performed for both flow and ammonia parameters, according 
to the sensitivity analysis performed by Olsen (2015) and either for Ålebækken and Cascina Scala catchments. 
A separation must be done between dry weather periods and wet weather periods, in order to allow the 
simulation to represent the peaks properly. An overview about sensitivity analysis is also presented below. 
“A sensitivity analysis should be carried out to determine which parameters are most important to be known 
with high certainty: the estimation methods and the determination of the parameters by experiments should 
focus mainly on the most sensitive parameters” (Jørgensen, 1986). 
In the present work, the sensitivity analysis of the quantity and the quality parameters carried out by Olesen 
(2015) is adopted, in order to have a higher efficiency calibration. The analysis is based on a Monte Carlo 
approach, and consists in performing 1000 simulations, to detect which ones are the parameters with the 
biggest impact on the state variables of the model. The complete procedure can be found in Olesen (2015): 
here only the results are presented.  
The results of the sensitivity analysis show that for the flow module the most sensitive parameters are the 
linear reservoir constant [d-1] for the rainfall-runoff transformation model, indicated by the letter k, the 
pervious fraction of the catchment, indicated by the letter f and the infiltration of external water, expressed 
in l/s∙ha. For the quality module, the most sensitive parameters are NH4 sewer, which is the ammonia 
concentration in wastewater (mg/l) and NH4 runoff, which represents the ammonia concentration in runoff 
(mg/l). A summary of these parameters is contained in Table 4.4.

data, because as previously explained no flow data were available during the overflows because of a 
geometrical limit of the sensors. It should be pointed out that this calculation is going to be only an estimation 
of the real value of pollutant load discharged during rain events, because the several sources of uncertainty 
present both in the measured and in the simulated data affected the quality of the simulation. 
4.5.2. Cascina Scala load calculation 
The ammonia load is finally calculated for Cascina Scala case study, in relation to events 08c, 13c, 14c, 17c, 
19c, 20c. A load calculation on the measured data has been performed by multiplying the flow rate by the 
relative value of ammonia concentration. Considering that the timestep between two consecutive 
measurements is variable between two and twenty minutes, it is expected to find a value that strongly 
underestimates the real value of load. An estimate of the load is also performed for the simulated values, 
which are given with a fixed timestep of two minutes, in a continuous series, which is expected to allow to 
find a more reliable value of pollutant load.

6. Discussion and comparison 
 
A comparison between the results obtained in the two case studies, and the relative discussion, is given in 
the present Chapter. A division is made according to the topic faced. Firstly, a discussion about the different 
characteristics of the two catchments is presented. Secondly, a brief digression on the sources of uncertainty 
linked to the inputs and the measured data and thirdly a discussion on the flow calibration results and on the 
NH4 concentration results. Afterwards a discussion on the load calculations and lastly some observations on 
the performance of the software WEST in relation to the present project.  
6.1. Analysed periods and catchments 
First of all, it should be noticed that the two series of data were very different one from each other. Indeed, 
in the Ålebækken case study long time series of data were available, relative to the winter period between 
2016 and 2017. On the other hand, in Cascina Scala the provided data were focused on rain events, which 
occurred between 2000 and 2003, mostly during the spring or summer season. 
A further debate concerns the different dimensions of the two catchments: Ålebækken is a large catchment 
of about 1015 ha whose sewer system collects wastewater from 3 different municipalities, while Cascina 
Scala is a basin of 15 ha, that collects the sewage coming only from the relative residential location, and it’s 
separated from the surroundings from a hydrological point of view. 
6.2. Input and measurements uncertainty  
As already explained in the previous paragraph, the rain data chosen as an input for Ålebækken case study 
are the ones registered by a single rain gauge (Furesø park). This simplification turns out to have a quite 
strong impact on the results, indeed it can be noticed in several occasions that variations in the flow and in 
the ammonia concentration are not preceded by any rain event. Another possible reason of mismatching 
between rain data and flow data is the phenomenon of the snow melting, which produces an effect on the 
flow but is not inserted as an input. 
The second approximation carried out in the analysis of the rain series is to consider the rain block all equally 
distributed on the surface of the catchment, which is however an intrinsic simplification in the software 
WEST.  
The uncertainty of the model, besides the rain input, is due to the bare concept of lumped conceptual model 
but also to the measured data applied for the calibrations. By using the sensor data, the uncertainty in the 
pollutant measuring is approximately 5% (Olesen 2015). A certain level of uncertainty is also linked to the 
samples used for the correction of the sensors, performed by Skov (2017).  
For a conceptual model, the model only gets results as good as the calibrated data, and the uncertainty 
related to the data must be taken into consideration when using the model itself. Calibrating on unreliable 
data increases the risk of modelling the error in the data. For this reason, an accurate removal of the outliers 
in the concentration measurements was performed by Skov (2017). 
6.3. Flow predictions

the simulation of the flow rate. In Cascina Scala one, considering the small development and the high 
reactivity of the catchment, all the measured peaks are well reproduced by the simulation. From a 
mathematical point of view, the parameter which is representing the “reactivity” of the catchment and how 
much the output is blunt, is the constant reservoir k, expressed in d-1. A low value of k means that the reaction

6.4. NH4 concentration prediction 
Looking at the NH4 concentration measurements, in both the case studies, it is possible to see two different 
trends, particularly in relation to the first flush phenomenon. The most remarkable and repetitive 
phenomenon that can be underlined looking at Ålebækken concentration data is that during rain events, 
after a first short period of slight concentration increase, there is a strong decreasing due to the dilution of 
the wastewater. The trend is very different in Cascina Scala catchment: the values of concentration 
associated to the first part of the rain event are much higher than the average value of concentration visible 
in the analysed periods. This demonstrates that the first flush does not occur indistinctly in all the 
catchments, and for all the events. Generally, this phenomenon occurs more evidently in small catchments, 
in occasion of strong rainfalls, and mostly for pollutants linked to the TSS and bacterial pollutants (Papiri 
2011).

online sensor settled at the CSO structure, which measures the values of concentration with a resolution of 
1 minute, although quite long gaps of measurements were missing due to lack of memory in the controlling 
device, malfunctions or maintenance. On the other hand, in Cascina Scala the concentration data were 
collected through an autosampler, with a time resolution variable in accordance with the flow, but not lower

events, and specially the first flush phenomenon described above. The reason is that the model is built as a 
series of linear tanks, hence not able to model the first flush. As suggested by Yue and Hashino (2000), a 
possible way to simulate the first flush phenomenon is to add a parallel tank to the first tank of the series. 
Ultimately, such short timeseries and low values cause slowdowns in the numerical solution, hence a balance 
had to be found between the accuracy of the numerical solver and the execution time. This led to a more 
coarse simulation, which slightly varies from its mean value.  
 
6.5. Pollutant load calculations 
The pollutant load was calculated for both the catchments, in occasion of the rain events and of the overflow 
in  Ålebækken case study, and in occasion of the rain events in Cascina Scala case study. In the first scenario, 
it has been possible to execute a comparison between the measured and the simulated data in occasion of 
the rain events. It turned out that the simulation gave quite acceptable results when the flow simulation was 
close to the measures, while when the peaks of flow were not represented by the simulation, also the load 
simulation underestimated the measured values.  
Much higher values of pollutant load have been found in Ålebækken case study than in Cascina Scala one, as 
expected considering the different size of the two catchments. Moreover, the value of the pollutant load is 
of course linked to the values of mean concentration of NH4 in wastewater and in the runoff. In the light of 
this, much higher values of load have been found in Ålebækken than in Cascina Scala. These values are very 
different between Ålebækken catchment and Cascina Scala catchment.

6.6. WEST model performance 
Ultimately, a brief resume on the performance of the WEST software relatively to the present analysis is 
presented. The software was firstly designed to be used for wastewater treatment plant, but it turned out to 
be suitable for the urban drainage simulation. It has the advantage of a very short simulation time, allowing 
for an automatic calibration of the model and long-term simulation in a reasonable time. On the other hand, 
WEST does not simulate the days and dates correctly, and the numerical solver accumulates an error at every 
iteration. For this reason, a complete match between the dates of the measured and the simulated data 
cannot be achieved. 
In this study, WEST has been run using Matlab as an interface, but care has to be taken when following this 
approach. It must always be kept in mind that the optimizer is only a numerical tool, and is up to the user to 
set reasonable starting values and intervals for the parameters, based on the literature and on the 
experience. Indeed, the calibration keeps on running even if the software faces numerical errors or the 
parameters’ initial values are very far from the physical reality. Both these issues can be avoided performing 
an initial manual calibration directly in WEST. For example, it has been experienced in Cascina Scala case 
study that a value of infiltration lower than the unit, together with a too high value of initial water level in 
the tank, led to a numerical error. In the light of this, the most reasonable value of both the parameters has 
been found through a manual calibration, then the automatic calibration of the infiltration was performed 
with a reasonable starting point.  
In general, some troubles were found performing Cascina Scala flow calibration because the initial and ending 
values of the measured hydrograph were very close to zero, and the numerical solver was facing instability 
problems in performing the simulation. For this reason, an additional flow rate had to be added, then 
removed, to allow the simulation to run. 
Also in Ålebækken case study some instability was found few times, but it was easily fixed by setting a high 
enough lower bound for the infiltration rate.

7. Conclusions 
 
The aim of this project was to set up a lumped conceptual model in WEST and examine if a reliable water 
quality and quantity model could be made. Two different datasets were available: one coming from 
Ålebækken CSO structure and another one from Cascina Scala catchment. Once the model was calibrated, it 
was possible to simulate the pollutant load discharged by the CSO structure, during the rain events and the 
overflows in Ålebækken case study, and during the rain events in Cascina Scala one. 
It should be pointed out that ammonia concentration data were collected with two different strategies: with 
an on-line sensor positioned in correspondence of the CSO structure in Ålebækken case study, and through 
an autosampler installed at the outlet of the sewer system in Cascina Scala case study. One of the aim of the 
project is therefore to detect if this difference strategies of data collection could lead to different results in 
the two cases. 
The results obtained can be summarized in the following bullet points: 
o A total of 22 rain events and 14 overflows has been defined in the CSO structure at Ålebækken during 
the period of analysis; 
o The parameters’ values resulting from the fminsearch calibration on the water quality and quantity 
modules were identified for Ålebækken case study, together with their RMSE values. The calibration 
turned out to be quite accurate. Being the fminsearch a tool for finding the minimum of a scalar 
function, one value for each parameter has been identified for each investigated event. The values 
of the investigated parameters are in accordance with the measured ones. 
o The parameters’ values resulting from the fminsearch calibration on the water quantity and quality 
modules were identified for Cascina Scala catchment, together with their RMSE values. The 
calibration turned out to be very accurate with the flow, but less with the NH4 concentration. This 
could be due to the difficulty of the numerical solver to face such small numbers as the ones involved 
in Cascina Scala quality module. The parameters resulting from the calibrations look reasonable if 
compared with the corresponding ones in Ålebækken. 
o The load calculation during the rain events gives reasonable results. We can see that in 5 of the 20 
analysed rain event the simulation is deviating from the measured value of more than 30%. As 
explained in Chapter 6, this happens when the flow simulation is not accurate. Even the errors in the 
concentration measurements add a rate of uncertainty to the load calculation. We can however 
deduce that a very fitting flow simulation would minimize the error on load calculation. 
o The pollutant load calculation from simulated data in Cascina Scala can’t be matched with the 
measurements because they aren’t giving a reliable reference. But considering the high performance 
of the flow simulation we can suppose that the load calculation is giving reasonable results. 
o The pollutant load during the overflow was also calculated for simulated data in Ålebækken case 
study. Only 3 of the 14 overflows identified inside the real measurement were reproduced by the 
simulation. This happens for the same reason cited below: the flow simulation was not accurate 
enough to reproduce all the flow peaks. It is also likely that the overflows which took place in mid-
January were due to snow melting phenomenon, hence the simulation was not able to reproduce 
them.  
o The lumped conceptual model in WEST turned out to be overall adequate to simulate the analysed 
processes. Some problems were found in the simulation of the high peaks of flow in Ålebækken case 
study, and they were likely due to a too big uncertainty on the input. Some troubles were also found 
in the NH4 concentration simulation in Cascina Scala.

The automatic calibration of the model performed using Matlab as interface has been a very useful 
tool, which allows to perform a fast calibration of several parameters at the same time. Otherwise, 
the results must be critically analysed by the user, because the optimizer could lead to results very 
far from the physical reality, if the starting point and the bounds were not properly chosen.

8. Further outlooks 
 
The further work presented in this Chapter will be divided in four sections. Firstly, further work for the 
measuring of wastewater is presented, secondly recommendations for the model setup are listed, thirdly 
improving on the calibration strategy are proposed and lastly brief suggestions to improve the software WEST 
are presented. 
8.1. Measurement improvements 
Since it has been widely proven that it is important to have high quality data from the sensors, further works 
should focus on reducing the uncertainties linked to sensor measurements. An accurate work about the 
maintenance and the cleaning of the sensors at Ålebækken CSO structure was carried out in the project 
presented by Skov (2017). In accordance with it, the main goals are the ones listed below. 
o To prolong the duration of the measuring campaign and see the effects of different low-
maintenance scenarios; 
o to increase the number of grab samples, in order to give a statistical basis to evaluate the on-line 
sensors performance; 
o to improve the in-situ installation at the CSO structure so as avoid the phenomena such as clogging 
or fouling which could affect the performance of the sensor, but also update or replace the SC 200 
controller device installed together with the sensors. Thus, the current device has limited data 
memory, that imply a frequent download and back-up of the data from the memory card to avoid 
gaps of missing data.  
Regarding Cascina Scala catchment, the suggestion is to install an on-line sensor for water quality which 
would enable to obtain a continuous series of data. This would improve the quality of the calibration, since 
the optimizer would have a bigger number of point on which calculate the error. Moreover, it would enable 
to calculate the load of pollutants also from measured data so as test the reliability of the simulated one. 
8.2. Model structure uncertainties 
Further work could be put in the improvement of the model of the catchment in WEST. A model is a 
conceptual simplification of the physical reality, hence some uncertainty is linked to this representation.  The 
rain fall is one of the greatest uncertainties in the runoff generation (Schellart, Shepherd, and Saul 2012). In 
the light of this, less conceptualization of the catchment area would allow for the possibility of using several 
rain series in the model, coming from different rain gauge. Especially in Ålebækken case study, where the 
effect of the spatial variability is stronger, a locally measure of the rain and then an interpolation would be 
recommended. 
8.3. Calibration 
First of all, an uncertainty based calibration of the model is recommended to be performed. This is because 
uncertainty is intrinsic in any modelling process and originates from a wide range of sources (model 
parameters, input data, calibration data, initial conditions, model structure). It cannot be eliminated, but its 
amplitude can be estimated and reduced (Deletic et al. 2012). An example of uncertainty based calibration 
method is the Monte Carlo, which is ‘based on an informal likehood measure for the determination of a 
“behaviorability” threshold, because of the impossibility of creating a true model, due to the different 
intrinsic error sources of a model’ (McMillan and Clark 2009). 
Secondly, more calibrations could be performed on the model, by changing the starting points ant the bounds 
of the parameters, in particular on the ammonia concentration. Moreover, considering that the final aim of 
==PAGE:81==
DTU Environment 
M.Sc. Thesis 
August 2017 
 
Giulia Franchini 
 
78 
 
the calibration is to estimate the load of pollutants, a calibration could be performed directly on the loads, 
when relative measurements are available. Indeed, the range of the calibration depends on the aim of the 
project: if a good representation of the rain events is required, event based calibrations should be performed; 
otherwise, if the aim is to have a simulation that is representative of every atmospheric condition, a long 
term based calibration is a better solution. 
 
8.4. WEST improvements 
Finally, some suggestions are provided to fix some features in WEST software which turned out to be 
problematic for the scope of the present work. 
o To include a transport equation would enable to reproduce the first flush effect; 
o to add to the series of tank a tank in parallel, which would enable to reproduce the slow runoff; 
o to improve the numerical solver to face small numbers close to zero with an acceptable computation 
time; 
o to enable the user to insert the dates directly in the software and minimize the error on the 
timesteps during the iterations; 
o to fix the issue with the units of the output: it happened that even if m3/s was set as unit output, the 
values were expressed in m3/d; 
o to add an extension to simulate the process of snow melting, which turned out to be very relevant 
in the measured flow. 
On the basis of the work performed, it is supposed that an improvement of the measurements and of the 
rain input, together with a more physical-based model structure could lead to a better fitting simulation and 
more realistic parameters; it would also make the pollutant load calculation more realistic and  exploitable 
for real applications. Finally, some upgrades of the software WEST could make the development of a water 
quality model easier in the future.
