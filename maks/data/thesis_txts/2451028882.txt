Abstract 
 
Proton exchange membrane fuel cell (PEMFC) is a promising fuel cell as the next generation 
power source for transportation as well as portable applications since due to its high efficiency to pro-
duce electricity via electrochemically converting hydrogen and oxygen into water at low operating 
temperature with high-power density. However, a significant barrier preventing it from widespread 
used comes from the slow rate of oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) at the cathode. Pt is a current cata-
lyst for ORR. However, its low abundance and high cost prevent the commercialization of PEMFC. An 
encapsulated non-precious metal where the graphite layers encase the inner metal core has recently 
developed as a promising ORR catalyst owing to its high catalytic activity, low cost, and electrochem-
ical stability.  
The iron carbide (Fe3C) core encapsulated in the N-doped graphene (NG/Fe3C) has been vali-
dated as a novel ORR catalyst. Further studies to improve the ORR catalytic activity of NG/Fe3C based 
on Density functional theory are presented in this thesis. Two main approaches are studied. The first 
approach is to modify the Fe3C substrate in NG/Fe3C heterostructure. The second approach is based on 
doping new heteroatom into graphene. 
The ORR catalytic activity of NG/Fe3C heterostructure with the introduced new metal element 
at the interface is evaluation, including explicit water solvation. It has been found that the ORR catalytic 
activity of overlayer N-doped graphene can further enhance by introducing Ni and Co atoms in Fe3C 
substrate near the interface. The heterostructures with Ni and Co atoms at the interface are also stable 
against phase separation. It has been found that the introduction of metal atoms at the NG/Fe3C interface 
alter the work function of overlayer N-doped graphene, which is found to be correlated with ORR 
binding energy. 
The model catalytic activity of B-doped graphene and B-doped graphene supported on Fe3C is 
found superior to that of N-doped graphene and N-doped graphene supported on Fe3C. Doping boron 
atom into graphene cause the distinct active site and different mechanism to stabilize ORR intermediate, 
related to N-doped graphene system.  
The results propose a new possibility to enhance the ORR activity of NG/Fe3C heterostructure 
by manipulating the composite of Fe3C substrate as well as introducing new dopant atom on the gra-
phene sheet. 
Finally, the water solvation and adsorbate-free boron atom effect on ORR mechanism in B-
doped graphene are studied. It is found that the water solvation affects ORR mechanism on B-doped 
graphene and it essential to include water solvation in ORR model to correct adsorption free energy of 
ORR intermediate on B-doped graphene while the effect of adsorbate-free boron atom can be negligible 
at low doping concentration.

1.Introduction 
 
1.1 Motivation 
Due to the continuing increase in the world’s population, the global energy demand rises 
rapidly and profoundly fulfilled by fossil fuel source. However, it is notoriously known that fossil 
fuel causes significant environmental and social problems such as climate change and local air pol-
lution. Additionally, fossil fuel is limited reserved in nature. The fossil fuel sources are 
nonhomogeneous distribution across the world, the increased price due to political uncertainty or 
rapid consumption will cause the problem related to the security supply in the nations solely relying 
on the imported fossil fuel [1]. As a result, renewable energy seems to be a potential candidate to 
replace fossil fuel in the future.  
As one of the large energy-consuming sectors, a better energy conversion efficiency and a 
significant reduction in emission are expected from the transport sector. The transport sector con-
sumes about one-quarter of the total world energy supply [2], and it is one of the significant con-
tributors, releasing CO2 into the environment [3]. 
Among renewable technologies for transport application, a fuel cell has received increasing 
attention due to its low emission and high efficiency. The fuel cell produces electricity by a mean 
of electrochemical reaction converting of hydrogen and oxygen into water which is only a by-prod-
uct of the main reaction, so a fuel cell is nearly considered as a zero-emission technology. Addi-
tionally, since there is no fuel combustion, the fuel conversion efficiency is not limited by the Car-
not cycle law, which generally determines an efficiency limit for a heat engine. Theoretically, the 
thermodynamic efficiency of the fuel cell is about 83% at 25℃, which is 10-20% higher than the 
direct utilization of hydrogen in the internal combustion engine [4]. Moreover, unlike other 
electrochemical devices such as batteries, the reactants of fuel cells, hydrogen and oxygen, are not 
limited stored in the cell but are fed continuously from outside. Therefore, the fuel cell does not run 
down as long as the fuel can be supplied. On top of that, the external storage for the fuel cell can 
quickly be replenished compared to battery storage, which needs several hours for recharging [5]. 
However, the commercializing of the fuel cell technology are impeded due to the high cost 
related to material and components, especially catalyst, as well as the low durability and actual 
performance. More researches and developments in this technology are essential for widespread 
use in the transport sector. 
 
1.2 Proton exchange membrane fuel cell (PEMFC) 
Several types of fuel cell are classified, depending on employed electrolyte and working 
temperature. The proton exchange membrane fuel cells, also known as polymer electrolyte 
membrane fuel cells (PEMFC) is a suitable fuel cell for transport application since it is operated at 
low temperature, enable the PEMFC to fast start. Also, the thinness of the membrane electrode 
assembly (MEA) of the PEMFC allows compact fuel cells can be made, resulting in a high power-
to-weight ratio [6].  
The PEMFC works under the acid condition and is typically fueled with pure hydrogen. 
Hydrogen is feed at the anode side where the hydrogen oxidation reaction (HOR) occur. Hydrogen 
is oxidized and releases electrons at the anode. These electrons travel through an electrical circuit

The kinetics of the half-cell reaction at both electrodes is critical for fuel cell performance 
[11]. As shown in Figure 1-1, the black line represents the current-potential characteristics of half-

Since the kinetics of ORR is approximately six orders of magnitude slower than the HOR kinetics 
[12], the overpotential at the anode is much bigger than that of the cathode. Consequently, the over-
potential at cathode greatly outweigh the overpotential at the anode and mainly contributes to the 
significant loss of fuel cell performance. To achieve high energy conversion fuel cell, a primary 
target to overcome is the slow kinetics of ORR. 
Platinum (Pt) has been used commonly as the most efficient catalyst in fuel cell [13] and 
to overcome high overpotential of ORR, a high Pt loading is required at the anode side [8]. How-
ever, a high cost and low availability of Pt can dramatically raise the price of the PEMFC [14]. 
More than that, due to its substantial kinetic activation barrier, ORR is not facile even with 
Pt. In the experiment, a significant current density from the PEMFC with Pt or Pt-based catalyst

the poor stability of Pt under operating condition. Under the acid environment of the PEMFC, Pt

with outperformance and more durability than Pt is required.

To improve the catalyst, an understanding of the ORR mechanism is necessary. The overall

the overall process.

∆GORR = -4.29 eV

The ORR kinetics is related to the electrocatalytic current, which is determined by the rate 
of reaction, and it is turn out that the rate of the reaction depends on the potential-dependent acti-
vation energy barrier [17]. Study the ORR process at the atomic level can help to identify the po-
tential-dependent step and the original of the overpotential of the ORR [13].  
The ORR process occurs on the catalyst surface and has several elementary steps that in-

and forms an adsorbed oxygen molecule on the catalyst surface.

1.4 Linear Scaling Relation 
As previously discussed, the CHE model provides a method to understand the ORR mech-
anism and to identify the origin of the overpotential for ORR process. To find a better ORR catalyst, 
one may try a trial-and-error based method, randomly selecting new material and evaluating theo-
retical overpotential as a function of three intermediate Gibbs free adsorption energies. However, 
it was found that the Gibbs free adsorption energy of these three intermediates on (111) metal sur-
face are strongly correlated, as shown in Figure 1-3. The linear scaling relations among these three 
intermediates on the (111) metal surface can be written as the following equations. 
 
∆G(*O) = 2∆G(*OH) 
Equation 1-35 
∆G(*OOH) = ∆G(*OH) + 3.2 eV 
Equation 1-36 
 
Using the linear scaling relations, the oxygen reduction activity can be predicted as a func-
tion of Gibbs free adsorption energies of only one intermediate. In this way, the number of inde-
pendent variables used to describe the ORR activity can be reduced. However, it is undeniable that 
==PAGE:12==
7 
 
the ORR activity based on the scaling relation is an approximation, but it has been proved that these 
linear scaling relations successfully predict the ORR activity trend on Pt and Pt-alloy surface [18].  
Moreover, the ORR activity trend based on the scaling relation also provides useful information to 
improve the catalyst. It is suggested that a material which obeys the linear scaling relation with a 
lower oxygen binding energy about 0.2 eV than Pt should be the best ORR catalyst [18]. The ma-
terial binding oxygen with stronger or weaker than the best catalyst will have a poorer ORR activity. 
Nevertheless, being limited by the linear scaling relation between *OOH and *OH, a minimum 
overpotential that the catalyst can be possibly achieved is about 0.37 V [17]. Therefore, in some 
way, the catalyst which breaks the linear scaling relation between *OOH and *OH intermediate are 
required.

1.5 Non-precious metal catalysts (NMPC) 
Since Pt is a precious metal, using Pt as a catalyst make the PEMFC cost-prohibit. To over-
come the cost issue, a new type of ORR electrocatalysts, an encapsulated non-precious metal where 
the metal or metal carbide nanoparticle (core) is encased by graphite layers (shell), has recently 
developed. The outer graphitic layer protects the inner core against corrosion under the PEMF con-
dition while the inner particles active the graphitic carbon layer toward ORR. Since its low cost and 
prominently improved stability, the encapsulated non-precious metal catalyst gains much attention. 
For example, the Fe nanoparticle encapsulated in pod-like carbon nanotubes (CNTs) (Pod-
Fe) is synthesized, and it shows a significantly catalytic active toward ORR than CNTs in an acid 
electrolyte. The Pod-Fe also maintains its ORR activity well under the PEMFC operation condi-
tions. The catalyst exhibits a much higher ORR activity and more extended stability after the CNTs 
is doped by nitrogen, indicating that CNTs well shield the inner metal core against the acid envi-
ronment and doping N into carbon lattice can enhance ORR activity. The DFT calculations suggest 
that the catalytic activity could rise from the electron transfer from Fe atom to CNTs and increase 
a pz charge density on the C atoms as well as reduce the work function of the carbon surface [19]. 
Fe-cluster embedded in Fe3C nanoparticles (Fe3C(Fe)) encased in nitrogen-doped graphitic 
layers (NDGLs) is synthesized by directly pyrolyzing. The catalyst shows high ORR activity com-
parable to the Pt/C catalyst with better stability than Pt/C catalyst in alkaline solution [20].  
==PAGE:13==
8 
 
A similar high-pressure pyrolysis method is used to prepare the iron-based NPMCs by 
Zhong at el [21]. The catalysts are in the form of N-doped graphitic layer encapsulating iron-com-
ponents nanoparticle, which is identified to be Fe and Fe3C phase. The catalyst exhibits high ORR 
catalytic activities and excellent stability with only 25 mV shift after 10,00 cycles of potential scan 
in acid media. Besides, the catalyst prepared from a free-N precursor was prepared to compare the 
ORR activity. It has been found that the catalyst from a free-N precursor which consists only me-
tallic Fe inside graphene layer, has reduced catalytic activity. After doping nitrogen, the catalyst 
shows higher ORR activity again but still much lower than the catalyst prepared by the N-containing 
precursor. The results indicate the significant role of the surface N-functionality and Fe3C inner 
since the catalyst still has only metallic Fe inside after doping nitrogen.  
The graphite layer encapsulated iron-based nanoparticles were synthesized by a high-pres-
sure pyrolysis approach with uniform morphologies but varied compositions in the inner core [22] 
to validate the role of Fe and Fe3C inside the ion-component core, It has been found that the ORR 
activity positively correlates with the Fe3C content.  
Moreover, a hollow sphere of iron carbide nanoparticles encased in graphitic layer (Fe3C/C) 
is successfully prepared by mean of the high-pressure pyrolysis. The F3C/C catalyst exhibits excel-
lent ORR activity and stability in both acid and alkaline media. On top of that, after the Fe3C nano-
particles are removed from the catalyst, the catalytic toward ORR activity is lost, confirm a vital 
part in the catalysis of the Fe3C nanoparticle even not directly contact with the electrolyte [23].  
The DFT study by Reda et al. [24] investigating the ORR activity of graphene and N-doped 
graphene supported on both Fe3C(010) and Fe(110) surface indicates that Fe3C support can promote 
N-doped graphene toward ORR activity more than Fe. Also, this study shows the importance of N-
doping for achieving high ORR activity. The electron donating from Fe3C support to the overlayer 
N-doped graphene is suggested to be responsible for the high ORR activity of the Fe3C supported 
N- doped graphene. It has been suggested that a support layer with a stronger electron donating than 
Fe3C but weaker than Fe could possess the same or better ORR activity than Fe3C support and even 
higher than Pt(111). 
Recently, many studies have explored the synthesis of bimetallic carbide encapsulated in 
N-doped graphene and showed that it displayed good ORR activity and electrochemical stability. 
The cobalt iron carbide nanoparticles encapsulated by N-doped graphene nanosheet (CoFe car-
bide/NG) is synthesized through a simple refluxing strategy followed by a post-annealing process. 
Upon testing as electrocatalyst for ORR, the CoFe carbide/NG exhibits higher activity than those

1.6 Focus of project 
As high performance and durability catalyst than Pt is desirable for commercialization of 
the PEMFC. To do so, searching for new promising material and testing its catalytic activity toward 
ORR are required. Since there are many materials and different structures of which a suitable cata-
lyst can be. The synthesis and examination of the ORR catalytic performance of each material com-
bination experimentally must take time and resource. By using the DFT calculations and CHE 
model, the preliminary ORR activity and mechanism on various setup structure can be studied, and 
the results will potentially suggest a promising material for further experimental study.  
This project aims to study ORR activity on doped graphitic carbon overlayers on various 
substrates material by apply DFT calculation and CHE model to develop an understanding of the 
atomic scale properties, which influence the ORR activity. The possibility of using the substrate to 
enhance ORR activity will be assessed, as well as a single atom doped graphene.  
Based on the study by Reda et el, the Fe3C encapsulated by N-doped graphene (NG/Fe3C) 
is a promising structure toward ORR. The underlying ORR mechanism of the NG/Fe3C is proposed 
as well as a possible way to improve the ORR activity. In this project, the NG/Fe3C structure is 
used as a catalyst prototype model. The Fe3C substrate will be slightly modified by introducing new 
elements in the substrate layer, and then the influence of the new element in the substrate layer on 
ORR activity will be studied. The influence of new dopant atom in graphene layer on ORR activity 
also includes in this project. Additionally, to understand how the substrate layer and dopants affect 
the ORR catalyst performance, the electronic structure of the catalyst is investigated.

2.2 Density Functional Theory (DFT) 
2.2.1 
Hohenberg-Kohn theorem [31]    
In the density functional Theory (DFT), the many-body wave function is solved in term of 
electronic density (n(r)). In this way, solving the electronic structure problem for N-electron system 
deals with only 3 degrees of freedom, instead of 3N degrees of freedom. The DFT is based on the 
Hohenberg-Kohn theorem, which is divided into two parts. The first theorem state that the external 
potential (𝑣𝑒𝑥𝑡(r)) is univocally determined by the electronic density, besides a trivial additive con-
stant. Since the Hamiltonian can be written in term of the external potential, different electron den-
sity results in different Hamiltonian, which necessary gives different ground state function. This 
means, electronic density also determines the ground state function and all properties of the ground 
state. The second theorem state that the ground state energy can be obtained from the ground state 
electronic density, which minimizes the total energy and can be found by mean of the variable 
principle. 
According to the first theorem, the total energy can be represented as a function of the 
electronic density as the following form.

3.Material Screening for N-doped          
Graphene Supported on Metal-iron    
Carbide  
 
Due to low availability and high cost of Pt-based catalyst, seeking a new ORR catalyst to 
replace Pt-based catalyst is necessary for large-scale commercialization of fuel cell technology.  
The encapsulated non-precious metal is promising alternatives catalyst considering its low 
cost, high ORR catalytic activity, long term durability [19], [23]. It has been suggested that the 
outer carbon shell protects the inner metal particles against the corrosion in an acid or alkaline 
solution while the inner metal particles activate the outer carbon shell toward ORR activity [34]. 
The iron carbide (Fe3C) core encapsulated in the graphene has been validated as a novel ORR cat-
alyst by recent experiments and theoretical studies [22], [23], [24]. Recent studies have explored 
the synthesis of bimetallic carbide encapsulated in N-doped graphene, which displayed good ORR 
activity and electrochemical stability [25], [26].  
In this chapter, a set of metal-iron carbide supported N-doped graphene is studied to iden-
tify a promising catalyst for ORR. The N-doped graphene coated iron carbide (NG/Fe3C) is used as 
a prototype of the catalyst model. The new metal element is introduced at the interface between N-
doped graphene and the Fe3C support layer. The structure of the catalyst after introducing a new 
metal element is expected to be maintained, and the electronic structure of the overlayer N-doped 
graphene could be close to that of the NG/Fe3C catalysts. However, there must be some modifica-
tions due to the introduced metal element which change the interaction between the catalyst surface 
and the ORR intermediates and result in a deviated ORR catalytic activity from that of NG/Fe3C 
heterostructure. This chapter aims to identify the new promising heterostructure for ORR catalyst, 
which should show an improved ORR catalytic activity than the NG/Fe3C heterostructure and 
should be as stable as possible. 
 
3.1 Calculation details 
3.1.1 
Structure of the catalyst model 
The NG/Fe3C heterostructure under this investigation here has been prepared by Reda et 
al. [24]. The unit cell of the NG/Fe3C heterostructure consists of 7 atomic layers of Fe3C(010) slab 
(6.8 Angstrom) overlayered by N-doped graphene, as shown in Figure 3-1(a) and (d). The surface

3.1.4 
General calculations 
All density functional theory (DFT) calculations are performed using the Vienna Ab initio 
Simulation Package (VASP) [43], [44], [45], with atomic simulation environment (ASE) [46]. Core 
electrons are described with the projector augmented wave (PAW) method [47], [48], and the basis 
set for the electronic wave functions is a plane wave with 500 eV energy cutoff. A Fermi smearing 
of electronic occupations with a width of 0.1 eV, a Monkhorst-Pack [54] mesh for the Brillouin 
zone sampling of 5 x 5 x 1 and BEEF-vdW [49] functional in which vdW-DFT2 [50] non-local 
correlation is employed in the calculation. The spin-polarized calculation is performed, and the 
structure relaxation is continued until the maximum force on each atom in the structure less than 
0.01 eV/Angstrom. Dipole correction is used to decouple electrostatic potentials on the two sides 
of the two-dimensional structure. During the structure relaxation, the three bottom layers of the 
substrate are fixed in the bulk position in the case where the new metal atoms substitute Fe atoms 
in the first layer of Fe3C substrate from the interface. If the new metal atoms are introduced in the 
second layer of Fe3C substrate from the interface, two bottom layers of the substrate are fixed. 
In case of bulk structure, the DFT calculation is performed by using the same plane wave 
basis set with 500 eV energy cutoff, BEEF-vdW functional and a Fermi smearing of electronic 
occupations with the width 0.1 eV. However, a Monkhorst-Pack mesh for the Brillouin zone sam-
pling depends on the unit cell of the bulk structure. The (k1, k2, k3) sampling is chosen in such a 
way that kivi > 30 for vi being the corresponding lattice vector. The structure of bulk phases is 
obtained from Material project [53].

3.3 Summary 
 
In this chapter, material screening is performed to find the new promising candidate heter-
ostructure for ORR catalyst. The study focuses on the NG/Fe3C catalyst, which has been recom-
mended as a good ORR catalyst. The new heterostructures model is modified from the NG/Fe3C 
heterostructure by substituting one layer of Fe atoms in Fe3C substrate near the NG/Fe3C interface 
by new metal atoms. The ORR activity of the new heterostructure is approximated from the adsorp-
tion energy of *O and *OH intermediate. The solvation effect is taken into account in the calculation 
by including a single water layer. The stability of the new heterostructure is evaluated by calculating 
the formation energy of the heterostructure related to pure metal phase and metal carbide phase of 
the introduced metal element. The composite change of the introduced metal atoms between the 
first and second layer of the Fe3C support is considered for both stability and activity analysis. The 
results show that substituting Fe atoms in Fe3C substrate near the NG/Fe3C interface by Ni, Co, and 
Zn atoms could form a stable heterostructure with enhanced ORR catalytic activity related to that 
of NG/Fe3C heterostructure.

4.1 Calculation details 
The unit cell of NG/Fe3C heterostructure, as described in Chapter 3, is used in the calcula-
tions. However, it has been suggested that the presence of nearby unoccupied nitrogen atom where 
its neighboring carbon atoms and the nitrogen atom itself do not bond with any adsorbates, signif-
icantly stabilizes the adsorption of *O intermediate on N-doped graphene. The lack of free nitrogen 
atoms in the catalyst model could lead to artificially weak binding of *O intermediate while too 
many free nitrogen atoms cloud cause *O intermediate to bind so strong and the reduction reaction 
of *O to *OH becomes the potential-determining step [41]. To take the advantage of the free nitro-
gen effect and minimize the barrier along the ORR free energy diagram, free nitrogen atoms and 
*O adsorbate (as spectator which will be present on the catalyst surface throughout the entire oxy-

Chapter 3 is extended in the surface plane to obtain a triplet (√2 × √3) cell. In this way, the catalyst 
model contains 3 nitrogen atoms in the unit cell. The first nitrogen atom is occupied by oxygen 
atom serving as a spectator (the oxygen atom bond at ontop site of the carbon next to the nitrogen 
dopant atom). The second nitrogen atom serves as a free nitrogen atom where their three adjacent 
carbon atoms do not bind with any ORR intermediates.  The third nitrogen atom serves as an active 
site for the ORR where the ORR intermediates will bond with the carbon atoms next to this nitrogen 
atoms. The N-doped graphene supported on metal-iron carbide heterostructure is modeled in the 
same way as described in Chapter 3. The metal atoms of element M will replace a single layer of 
Fe atoms in the Fe3C substrate either at the first and second layer from the NG/Fe3C interface,

Zn, Mn, Mo, and W.  The sample of calculate structures are shown in Figure 4-1.

introduced metal element and the layer where Fe atoms are substituted. The work function and the

4.2.4 
Electronic properties of N-doped graphene supported by metal-iron carbide. 
 
It can be seen from previous results that the adsorption free energy of the ORR intermedi-
ates on NG/Fe3C heterostructure change as the presence of new metal atoms near the NG/Fe3C 
interface. In this section, Bader charge analysis and project density of state (PDOS) calculations are 
employed to gain insight into the underlying ORR mechanism of NG/Fe3C heterostructure with 
new metal atoms near the interface. 
 
Many experimental and theoretical studies show that the ORR activity of N-doped gra-
phene can be boosted by supported on metal carbide or a transition metal [19], [42], [64], [65], [58]. 
It has been suggested that a support layer modulates the electronic structure of overlayer N-doped 
graphene in favor for the ORR intermediates, such as a change in the work function [19], charge 
distribution and density of state especially pz orbital of carbon atom on N-doped graphene [58], 
[64]. 
 
Figure 4-8 shows the work function of overlayer N-doped graphene in each heterostructure 
as a function of the adsorption energy of the ORR intermediates. It can be seen that the introduction 
of new metal atoms in the Fe3C substrate near the interface can change the work function of the 
overlayer N-doped graphene, suggesting the modified electronic structure of the overlayer N-doped 
graphene by the substrate. 
In case of heterostructure with weak interfacial interaction between N-doped graphene and 
the substrate (interlayer distance > 3.4 Angstrom), such as NG/Cu_1st_Fe3C and NG/Zn_1st_Fe3C 
heterostructure, their work function and the ORR intermediate adsorption energies are close to those 
of unsupported N-doped graphene as shown in yellow and green circles in Figure 4-8.  
For NG/M_Fe3C heterostructure with strong interfacial interaction with overlayer N-doped 
graphene (interlayer distance < 2.3 Angstrom), the adsorption free energy of the ORR intermediates 
seems to be correlated with the work function of the overlayer N-doped graphene as demonstrated 
in Figure 4-8(b), (d) and (f). The binding strength between the ORR intermediates and the carbon 
atom on N-doped graphene in each heterostructure become stronger as the work function of the 
overlayer N-doped graphene decrease.  
The correlation between the work function of undoped (doped) graphene and the ORR ac-
tivity has been already reported for many graphene-based catalyst systems, i.e. Fe nanoparticle en-
capsulated in a pod-like carbon nanotube [19], Co nanoparticle encapsulated in graphene [66], het-
eroatom (N, S, and P) doped order mesoporous carbon [67]. The correlation between the reduced 
work function of overlayer N-doped graphene and increased the ORR intermediate binding energy 
could understand as the carbon atom on the N-doped graphene with lower work function will have 
lower energy barrier for donating electrons to the adsorbate, thereby enhance the bonding strength 
between the carbon atom and ORR adsorbate. 
 
The shift of work function of supported N-doped graphene depends on the work function 
difference between N-doped graphene and metal-iron carbide substrate [56]. When they are in con-
tact, the electron transfer will occur across the interface. The available charge on the overlayer N-
doped graphene is expected to stabilize the ORR intermediates [64]. It is worth noting that the *O 
intermediate adsorption energy is changed more than the others for difference substituted metal 
element at the interface. Figure 4-9(a) shows the average charge on supported N-doped graphene 
and the *O intermediate adsorption energy. The *O intermediate adsorption energy seems to in-
crease as the available charge on the overlayer N-doped graphene increase. However, there is a 
scatter around the linear fit, especially among the heterostructure with strong interaction between 
graphene and the substrate. Therefore, the average charge on the overlayer N-doped graphene may 
be too crude to explain the variation in *O binding energy [24].

limited range, the data points may not be enough to establish the correlation between Bader charge 
on oxygen and *O binding energy. 
In addition to the work function, it has been suggested that the electronic coupling between 
N-doped graphene and the substrate causing distinct ORR activity could be explained by the density 
of pz state of the active carbon atom.  Liang et al. propose that the N-doping structure with the 
higher density of pz state will bond with the ORR intermediate stronger [64], [68]. Additionally, the 
correlation between pz band center of active carbon atom in graphene sheet with the catalytic activ-
ity has been proposed, suggesting that the ORR intermediate binding strength decrease as the pz 
band center approaches the Fermi level [58]. 
Figure C-2 and Figure C-3 in Appendix C show the project density of state (PDOS) onto 
carbon atom at an active site of free-standing N-doped graphene and NG/M_Fe3C heterostructure. 
For all catalyst structure, the pz orbital of the carbon atom at the active site locates near the Fermi 
level whereas the px and py orbital locate far below the Fermi level. Therefore, it is understandable 
that the pz orbital is expected to be used to form a bond with the ORR intermediates [68].  
The PDOS onto carbon atom at active sites in NG/Cu_1st_Fe3C and NG/Zn_1st_Fe3C het-
erostructure are similar to that of unsupported N-doped graphene as in these two heterostructures, 
the overlayer N-doped graphene has weak interaction with the substrates. For other heterostructures 
with the strong interaction between N-doped graphene and the substrate, the pz state shift down and 
the density of pz state increase, suggesting an electron transfer from the substrate into pz orbital of 
carbon atom on the overlayer N-doped graphene.

4.2.5 
Stability of Ni and Co doping Fe3C bulk structure. 
As previously discussed, the presence of Ni or Co atoms in the Fe3C substrate near the 
interface could improve the ORR catalytic activity of the pristine NG/Fe3C heterostructure. In the 
final part of this chapter, the possibility of Ni and Co atom dissolving in Fe3C bulk structure and 
form FeNi and FeCo carbide is considered by calculating the formation energy of Ni and Co doping 
in Fe3C bulk structure at different concentration. In this way, the results will guide the possibility 
of synthesis FeNi and FeCo carbide and used them as an inner core for encapsulated non-precious 
metal ORR catalyst. 
The unit cell of Fe3C, Ni3C, and Co2C in bulk structure are obtained from Material project 
[53]. The DFT calculations related to the bulk structure in this section is done by employing the 
generalized-gradient approximation (GGA) functional in the represent Perdew, Burke and Ern-
zerhof (PBE) [70] as it has been shown yielding the best accuracy for lattice constants, cohesive 
energies, and bulk moduli for metal [71], [72]. The plane wave basis set with an energy cutoff of 
800 eV, the Fermi smearing electronic occupations with 0.1 eV and Monkhorst-Pack [54] mesh for 
the Brillouin zone sampling of (k1, k2, k3) where kivi > 30 for vi being the corresponding lattice 
vector are employed in the calculation. The doping structure is fully relaxed, allowing the volume 
and the shape of the unit cell change until the maximum force on each atom less than 0.01 eV/Ang-

Summary 
 
ORR activity of NG/Fe3C heterostructures with new metal atom substituted Fe atoms in the 
Fe3C substrate is evaluated computationally. The water solvation and free nitrogen atom are taken 
==PAGE:51==
46 
 
into account by including explicit water molecule and one free nitrogen atom (and one *O interme-
diate as a spectator) in each calculation. The most promising metal elements which show ORR 
activity related to NG/Fe3C heterostructure are Ni and Co atoms. 
 
The stability and ORR activity of the heterostructure with Ni and Co atom at a substituted 
concentration up to two layers of Fe3C substrate are further studied. As the substituted concentration 
increased the heterostructure with Ni atoms is more stable against the phase separation to pure metal 
Ni or Ni3C. Unlike Ni substitution, the stability of the heterostructure with Co atoms increases with 
the Co atoms concentration up to 50%Co. After this concentration, the stability against phase sep-
aration to form pure metal Co decrease. The ORR activity depends on Ni and Co substitution con-
centration as well as atomic arrange of these substituted atoms near the interface. The lowest ORR 
overpotential of Ni is 0.70 V at 100%Ni. The lowest ORR overpotential of Co is 0.72 V at 100%Co; 
however, due to stability, the lowest ORR overpotential could be 0.72V at 50% Co. 
 
Ni and Co atoms active NG/Fe3C heterostructure toward ORR differently, The presence of 
Ni atoms in the first two layers of the substrate stabilizes *OOH intermediate, result in lower ORR 
overpotential relative to NG/Fe3C heterostructure. Co substitution in the first two layers of Fe3C 
substrate destabilizes *O intermediate such that the reduction reaction of *O into *OH become 
potential determining step. The *O intermediate accumulates on the surface and destabilizes the 
next *O intermediate, the activity of the heterostructure increase. 
 
The correlation between the work function of the overlayer N-doped graphene, and the 
ORR intermediate binding energy is found, suggesting that the support layer can tune the work 
function of the overlayer N-doped graphene. The overlayer N-doped graphene with lower work 
function will bond the ORR intermediates stronger. 
 
Finally, the formation of FeNi carbide and FeCo carbide is considered. It has been found 
that Ni and Co atom destabilized Fe3C bulk structure, suggesting that FeNi carbide and FeCo car-
bide are unstable.

Chapter 5  
5.Heteroatom-doped Graphene Sup-
ported on Iron Carbide  
 
Carbon-based catalysts are considered as the most promising alternative to substitute Pt-
based catalyst owing to low cost and long-term durability [11]. Various heteroatoms (nitrogen, 
phosphorous, oxygen, sulfur or boron) doped on graphene has been synthesized and reported as a 
promising ORR catalyst. As the size and electronegativity of the heteroatoms are different from 
those of carbon atom, introducing heteroatoms in graphene sheet could cause electron modulation 
to change the charge distribution and electronic properties of graphene sheet which in turn could 
alter their interactions with the ORR intermediates and enhance the ORR catalytic activity. Nitro-
gen(N)-doped graphene as a potential ORR catalyst has been caught a great deal of attention due to 
its high catalytic activity. Also, recent work shows that N-doped graphene supported on transition 
metal or metal carbide exhibit high ORR activity and long-term durability. Beside the N-doped 
graphene, boron (B) doped graphene has been reported as an excellent ORR catalytic in alkaline 
solution with also high CO tolerance [28]. In fact, among heteroatom-doped graphene, B-doped 
graphene has been reported to have the highest ORR catalytic [29], making it a potential alternative 
to replace Pt-based catalyst in PEM fuel cells.  
As different doping element, the ORR mechanism on B-doped graphene could differ from 
that of N-doped graphene. Some details of the ORR reaction path of B-doped graphene has been 
proposed [80], but there are several key questions related to ORR activity on B-doped graphene 
that need to be answered. In this chapter, the ORR activity of the B-doped graphene and B-doped 
graphene supported Fe3C in acid solution is considered and compared to the N-doped graphene 
system. The ORR overpotential of each structure is evaluated by calculating the thermodynamic 
free energy diagram. Then, Bader charge analysis and projected density of states (PDOS) calcula-
tions are employed to gain insight into the underlying ORR activity of each structure. The effect of 
water solvation and adsorbate-free boron dopant atoms on the ORR catalytic mechanism of B-
doped graphene will be discussed in Chapter 6.

5.1.2.3 Electronic property  
 
To gain more understanding about ORR activity on each system, the Bader charge analy-
sis and projection density of state (PDOS) calculation are employed. 
Figure 5-5(a)-(B) show Bader charge on atoms in undoped, N- and B-doped graphene with-
out Fe3C support. A negative sign of Bader charge means an increase of charge on that atom and 
vice versa for a positive sign.  
On undoped graphene, there is no net charge on carbon atoms. Introduce dopant atom into 
graphene lattice cause non-uniformly charge distribution. In N-doped graphene, N atom has nega-
tive charge (-1.24e) while the neighboring carbon atoms become positively charged (+0.32e). This 
is because N atom (electronegativity = 3.04 [84]) has higher electronegativity than C atoms (elec-
tronegativity = 2.55 [84]). When N atom is doped in graphene lattice, electrons are attracted toward 
N atom, increasing charge around N atom and decreasing charge around adjacent C atoms. On the 
other hand, in B-doped graphene, B atom has a positive charge (+2.03e), and the neighbor carbon

atom has a negative charge (-0.58e). As B atom (electronegativity = 2.04 [84]) has less electroneg-

increases and decreases near B atom.

stabilize all ORR intermediates. The ORR intermediate stability possibly attributes to the non-uni-

non-uniformly charged distribution of B-doped graphene, the positive boron atom serves as the 
active site for *OH and *OOH intermediate where *OH and *OOH intermediate also adsorbs more 
strongly compared to those on undoped graphene. The incorporation of N and B atom into graphene 
lattice creates the charge site, in favor of the ORR intermediates [85]. The charge distribution could 
increase the attractive electrostatic force between unpair electrons of the oxygen atom in the ORR 
intermediates with an atom at an active site [40].

creased in the adsorption energy of *O and the increases in Bader charge on *O due to support layer 
has been reported on N-doped graphene with a Cu(111) support [42].

5.2 Summary 
 
In this chapter, the ORR activity of six graphene-based catalyst structures is evaluated. 
These structures graphene, N-doped graphene, B-doped graphene, G/Fe3C, NG/Fe3C, and BG/Fe3C. 
The results have been shown that doping nitrogen and boron atom or Fe3C support stabilize the 
ORR intermediates and activate graphene toward ORR. Incorporating both the dopant atom and 
Fe3C support further stabilize the ORR intermediates and enhance ORR catalytic activity. The most 
active heterostructure is BG/Fe3C where *OH and *OOH intermediate are most stabilized.  
 
The Bader charge analysis has been shown that doping nitrogen and boron atom into gra-
phene lattice cause non-uniform charge distribution on graphene which turns to be in favor of the 
ORR intermediates. Doping also causes a change in pz orbital of atom in the active site. In general, 
increase electron density near Fermi level of pz orbital of atom at the active site.  
 
Fe3C support also raises electron density on overlayer undoped (doped) graphene and in-
creases pz electron density of atom at the active site. The charge redistribution and the increase in 
pz orbital could be responsible for improving ORR catalytic activity. However, it is difficult to 
explain the variation of the ORR intermediate stabilities with only one quantity as the difference in 
an atom at the active site which interacts differently with the ORR intermediates.  
 
For undoped graphene, O(sp) state of *O intermediate is unfilled, resulting in unstable *O 
intermediate. The presence of nitrogen dopant atom or Fe3C stabilizes *O intermediate by filled 
O(sp) state. Unlike the others, *O intermediate adsorbs on C-B bridge site on B-doped graphene 
and bond with both carbon and boron atom, filling O(sp) state. The further stabilization due to Fe3C 
on *O intermediate in B-doped graphene does not seem to correlate with Bader Charge on *O 
intermediate like other two systems (undoped graphene, N-doped graphene). 
 
Finally, the stability calculation suggests that N-doped graphene and B-doped graphene are 
thermodynamically stable under PEMFC operation condition.

6.Adsorbate-free boron Atom and Water 
Solvation Effects on Oxygen Reduction 
Reaction in Boron-doped Graphene  
 
As previously discussed in Chapter 5, doping B atom into the graphene lattice stabilized all 
ORR intermediates and improved the ORR activity of graphene. In this chapter, the adsorbate-free 
boron atom and water solvation effect on the ORR intermediate in B-doped graphene are studied. 
The adsorbate-free boron atom refers to the boron atom in the graphene where its neigh-
boring carbon atoms and the boron dopant atom itself, does not bond with any adsorbate. It has 
been reported that the presence of an adsorbate-free boron atom nearby the active site stabilizes all 
ORR intermediates and results in a higher ORR catalytic activity, compared to the system without 
an adsorbate-free boron atom [83].  
An adsorbate-free nitrogen atom effect on ORR catalytic activity of N-doped graphene has 
been observed [39] and well-studied by DFT [41]. It has been found that the adsorbate-free nitrogen 
dopant atom selectively stabilized *O and *O2 adsorbates over *OH and *OOH adsorbate. The lack 
of the adsorbate-free nitrogen atom or too many adsorbate-free nitrogen atoms could result in arti-
ficially weak or strong the *O binding, directly affecting the correction of calculated ORR overpo-
tential [41].  
Moreover, since the ORR in PEMFC occurs at the interface between electrode and electro-
lyte, the ORR catalyst operates in a water environment where the interaction with surrounding water 
molecules could alter the stability of the ORR intermediates [42], [13]. On top of that, the water 
solvation effect could vary between different catalyst material.  
For the Pt(111) surface, previous studies have shown that it is necessary to include water 
interaction in order to accurately simulate the mechanism of ORR on Pt(111) in acid solution [90] 
and it has been found that the effect of water on the binding energies of *OH and *OOH in more 
significant than that of *O2 and *O [89]. A similar water solvation effect on ORR binding energies 
on Ag(111) has been observed [91].  
In the case of N-doped graphene, the water solvation effect on the ORR intermediate has 
already been investigated, and it has been found that the water solvation can shift the adsorption 
free energy of the ORR intermediates to a more negative value (stabilize the ORR intermediates), 
resulting in a lower theoretical ORR overpotential, compared to that in vacuum [37], [40]. The 
previous studies show that the water solvation stabilizes *O and O2 than more than *OH and *OOH 
on N-doped graphene and the explicit H2O molecule is essential to correct adsorption energy of the 
ORR intermediates [37], [41]. Additionally, the strong stability effect of water solvation on the *O 
intermediates appears not to be limited to the basal plane N-doped graphene. The significant *O 
stabilization via solvation is found on other type materials where the atomic oxygen is located at 
the atop adsorption sites such as N-doped graphene with Stone-Wales defects, Fe3C supported gra-
phene, hydrogen-terminated graphite step zigzag edge and hexagonal boron nitride [41].  
==PAGE:68==

Unlike N-doped graphene, it has been reported that *O intermediate is stably adsorbed at 
the C-B bridge site on the B-doped graphene, forming an epoxide-like structure [29], [83] which is 
also confirmed in the present study in the previous chapter.  This could lead to different stabilization 
effects due to the Adsorbate-free dopant atom and water solvation effect on the B-doped graphene.  
 
6.1 Calculation details 
All density functional theory (DFT) calculations are performed using the Vienna Ab initio 
Simulation Package (VASP) [43], [44], [45] with atomic simulation environment (ASE) [46]. Core 
electrons are described with the projector augmented wave (PAW) [47], [48] method, and the basis 
set for the electronic wave functions are plane wave with 500 eV energy cutoff. A Fermi smearing 
of electronic occupations with the width of 0.1 eV, a Monkhorst-Pack [54] mesh for the Brillouin 
zone sampling of k1 x k2 x k3 with kivi > 30 for vi being the corresponding lattice vector and the 
BEEF-vdW [49] functional in which vdW-DFT2 [50] non-local correlation are employed in the 
calculations. The spin-polarized calculation is performed, and the structure relaxation is continued 
until the maximum force on each atom in the structure is less than 0.01 eV/Angstrom. A dipole 
correction is used to decouple electrostatic potentials on the two sides of the two-dimensional struc-
ture 
Most of the considered B-doped graphene models consist of graphitic boron atoms uni-
formly distribute in a graphene sheet. The B-doped graphene models in this study are modified from 
the N-doped graphene structure obtained from Reda et al. [41]. For the catalyst model with explicit 
water molecules, most of the stable water molecule arrangement in the calculation showing hexag-
onal arrangement is also already determined by Reda et al. [41], using the minima-hopping algo-
rithm [59]. The vacuum layer region between the periodic image of the system is about 14 Ang-
strom. The adsorption energy of the ORR intermediates, which is defined relative to water, as Equa-
tion 1-17,  Equation 1-18 and Equation 1-19 in Chapter 1, is directly obtained from the DFT calcu-
lation and then converted to the Gibbs free energy as described in Chapter 2. The adsorption ener-
gies in the presence of water are calculated by replacing one water molecule with an adsorbate, and 
the energy of the water molecule is assumed to be the average energy of one water molecule in the 
water layer without any adsorbate as described in Chapter 4.

In case of N-doped graphene, the oxygen atom which prefers to form two bonds can only 
form a single bond with the carbon atom at the active site since the carbon atom can only provide 
one single bond for the *O intermediate. Consequently, the *O intermediate on N-doped graphene 
is quite unstable as it has one dangling bond left (the unfilled O(sp) state). It has been suggested 
that the additional electron density on the *O intermediate due to the adsorbate-free nitrogen atom 
selectively stabilizes the *O intermediate by filling the unfilled O(sp) state [41].  
Unlike N-doped graphene, the *O intermediate is stably adsorbed at the B-C bridge site on 
B-doped graphene, forming bonds with both the carbon and the boron atom at the active site. There-
fore, the sp state of the *O intermediate on the B-doped graphene is filled, as shown in the previous 
chapter. In this case, the additional charge on the *O intermediate possibly raises electrostatic in-
teraction between the atom at the active site (positive charge) and the adsorbate (negative charge). 
The more charge is transferred, the more attractive electrostatic between the *O intermediate and 
the atoms at the active site. 
 Unlike the *O intermediate, the *OH and *OOH intermediates are favorably adsorbed on

atom so it could not directly withdraw charge from the carbon atom. This could be explained why 
the adsorbate-free boron atom selectively stabilizes the *O intermediate over the *OH and *OOH 
intermediate. 
As shown in Figure 6-5, the closer adsorbate-free boron atom increases Bader charge on 
the *O intermediate by allowing the oxygen atom to withdraw more charge from the boron atom at 
the active site. The same situation is possible to occur with *OH and *OOH intermediate. In this 
way, it could be expected that the adsorbate-free boron atom may stabilize the *OH and *OOH 
intermediate at high concentration of boron in the graphene.

Stabilization by water solvation 
Here, the water solvation effect caused by explicit water molecules on the adsorption free

The destabilization at two water layers could be because the *O intermediate is quite stable 
at the C-B bridge site, so the *O intermediate seems to form weak hydrogen bonds with water 
molecules, compared to other ORR intermediates. To adsorb the ORR intermediates on the surface, 
one of the water molecules is removed, resulting in the loss of three hydrogen bonds and disruption 
of the hexagonal arrangement of water. The adsorption of the *OH or *OOH intermediate could 
restore the missing hydrogen bonds and the hexagonal arrangements by forming three hydrogen 
bonds with the water molecules.  Additionally, the shorter distance between the *OH and *OOH 
intermediate with the water molecule than those in water network has been found, suggesting that 
the *OH and *OOH intermediate not only restore the hydrogen bonds network but also strongly 
interact with water molecules. In the case of the *O intermediate, it could form the hydrogen bonds 
which are likely to be weaker, compared to other intermediates or possibly form only one hydrogen 
bond. As a result, the adsorption of the *O intermediate could not be enough to restore the missing 
hydrogen bonds in the water layer, resulting in destabilizing the hexagonal arrangement of water 
[89], [91] and hydrogen bonding between the first and second water layer. The destabilization is 
likely to be less after adding more water layers due to a denser structure of bulk water. The B-doped 
graphene structures with the double layers of water with and without adsorbate are shown in Figure 
E-2, Appendix E.

that on *O intermediate. With the single water layer, the stabilized energy for the *OH and *OOH 
adsorbate is 0.13-0.17 eV at 5H2O/*ads and increase to 0.44-0.47 eV at 11H2O/*ads. The solvation 
effect on the B-doped graphene also depends on the number of the water layer. The water solvation 
effect on the *OH and *OOH intermediate increase from 0.13-0.17 eV to about 0.40 eV as the 
number of water layer increase from 1 (5H2O/*ads) to 3 water layers (17H2O/*ads) in the case of 
no adsorbate-free boron atom.

The effect of adsorbate-free boron atom and water solvation on ORR overpotential 
 
In the final part of this chapter, the effect of adsorbate-free boron atom and solvation effect

ferent B/*ads and H2O/*ads ratio. For the first catalyst model, the pristine structure (Figure 6-11(a)) 
consists of the B-doped graphene with 16 atoms (15 carbon atoms and 1 boron atom) and four

*OOH formation on the surface is the potential determining step. Consider to the same model in a 
vacuum (black line in Figure 6-11(a)), the overpotential in a vacuum is 0.99 V with the formation 
of the *OOH intermediate on the catalyst surface being potential determining step. The calculated 
ORR overpotential with the presence of four water molecules is lowered about 0.25 V. The main 
effect which reduces the ORR overpotential is the stabilization effect on the *OOH adsorbate due 
to the surrounding water molecules.  
The first model catalyst is further expanded in order to include the adsorbate-free boron 
atom effect into account. The second catalyst model, which is a (√2 × √2) version of the first 
model, is shown in Figure 6-11(b), there are 2 boron atoms (one serves as an active site, and another 
one is an adsorbate-free boron atom) and 8 explicit water molecules. The calculated ORR overpo-
tential of the second model is found to be 0.53 V, lower that of the first model with the formation 
of the *OOH intermediate on the catalyst surface being the potential determining step. The presence

the stability of the *O intermediate increases with the presence of the adsorbate-free boron atom,

way around; it stabilizes the *OH and *OOH intermediate over the *O intermediate. In total, the

6.2.4 
Summary 
 
Two effects responsible for stabilizing ORR intermediate on B-doped graphene are com-
putationally studied. These two effects are adsorbate-free boron atom and water solvation effect. 
The presence of adsorbate-free boron atom stabilizes the *O intermediate over the *OH and *OOH 
intermediate at boron concentration in the graphene lower than 6.3% B. At higher boron concen-
tration, the adsorbate-free boron atom could stabilize *OH and *OOH around 0.15 eV (2B/*ads at 
8.3%B). At lower boron concentration the adsorbate-free boron atom effect on *OH and *OOH 
intermediate is negligible. The stabilization due to adsorbate-free boron atom on *O intermediate 
can reach 0.5 eV at 5B/*O (6.3%B).  
 
The water solvation stabilizes the *OH and *OOH intermediate while has a negligible ef-
fect on the *O intermediate. The water solvation effect depends on the H2O/*ads ratio in a single 
water layer as well as the number of the water layer. The stabilization via water layer on the *OH 
and *OOH can reach around 0.4 eV by 11H2O/*ads in the single water layer, or by three water 
layers (17H2O/*ads).  
 
The presence of the adsorbate-free boron allows *O intermediate withdraws more charge 
from the carbon atom at the active site and then enhance the binding energy. The situation seems 
not to occur with the *OH and *OOH intermediate at the ontop site of boron dopant atom. The 
adsorbate-free boron effect on *O intermediate is found to vary with distance but within short range 
compared to the adsorbate-free nitrogen effect. The solvation effect via hydrogen bond has a mar-
ginal effect on the *O intermediate on the B-doped graphene since the *O intermediate is quite 
stable on the C-B bridge site. 
 
As the adsorption free energy of the *OOH intermediate determines the ORR overpotential 
on the B-doped graphene, the results suggest that it is important to include water molecules in ORR 
model to correct the adsorption free energy of the ORR intermediates. The adsorbate-free boron 
atom effect can be negligible in ORR model at boron concentration in the graphene lower than 
6.3%.

7.Conclusions and outlook  
 
7.1 Conclusion 
 
In this thesis, the electrocatalytic oxygen reduction reaction on the carbon surface is studied 
using computational methods based on DFT. The studies can be classified into two main parts. 
 
The first part relates to the ORR activity of N-doped graphene on Fe3C substrate. The ORR 
activity of NG/Fe3C heterostructure with new metal atoms at the interface is evaluated by taking 
water solvation into account. By changing the composite of the substrate near the interface between 
N-doped graphene and Fe3C substrate, the ORR activity of overlayer N-doped graphene can be 
tunable. The introduce new metal atoms in Fe3C substrate change the work function of overlayer 
N-doped graphene, which is found to be correlated with ORR intermediate binding energy. It has 
been found that with the presence of Ni and Co atoms in Fe3C substrate near the interface, the 
electronic structure of N-doped graphene is modulated toward ORR. Moreover, the ORR activity 
of overlayer N-doped graphene varies depending on the concentration and configuration of Ni and 
Co atoms in the Fe3C substrate. Besides, the heterostructure with Ni and Co atom is found to be 
stable against phase separation. However, the dissolved Ni and Co atoms in Fe3C bulk structure 
destabilize Fe3C bulk structure, suggesting unstable of FeNi carbide and FeCo carbide. 
 
The second part relates to the ORR activity of B-doped graphene and B-doped graphene 
supported on Fe3C. The model catalytic activity, including water solvation, of B-doped graphene 
and B-doped graphene supported on Fe3C, is found superior to that of N-doped graphene and N-
doped graphene supported on Fe3C. Doping Boron atom into graphene lattice generates an active 
site and mechanism to stabilize ORR intermediate differently from N-doped graphene system. The 
water solvation and adsorbate-free boron effect on ORR mechanisms in B-doped graphene system 
are studied since these two effects are expected to exist during PEMFC operation. Even thought N-
doped graphene and B-doped graphene are both graphene-based material, the stabilization due to 
adsorbate-free dopant atom and water solvation in these two systems is found to be different. How-
ever, including solvation is essential for the correction of the ORR intermediate adsorption on B-
doped graphene, same as N-doped graphene. 
 
7.2 Outlook 
 
Based on the results of this studied, the following topics are worth studying in the future. 
• 
As the presence of Ni and Co atoms near the NG/Fe3C interface enhance the ORR catalytic 
activity of NG/Fe3C and the ORR catalytic activity is found to increase as the concentration of 
Ni and Co atoms near the interface increase. The further increased ORR activity could be fur-
ther enhanced by increase Ni and Co concentration as well as using nickel carbide or cobalt 
carbide as a substrate. 
• 
The simple model catalytic activity of B-doped graphene and B-doped graphene supported on 
Fe3C is found to be higher than that of N-dope graphene. The obvious next step is to include 
appropriate water solvation effect to correct the adsorption free energy of ORR intermediate. 
Additionally, supported B-doped graphene on different metal or metal carbide could further 
enhance ORR activity and gives more results to understand the ORR mechanism on B-doped 
graphene.
