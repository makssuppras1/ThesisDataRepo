Abstract
First, an analytical derivation of the relevant Generalized NonLinear Schrø” dinger
Equation is presented. The problem of the mode proﬁle dispersion is introduced through
the Constant Overlap formulation of the equation, and the proposed approximation of
the overlap integrals’ frequency dependence is deﬁned.
An eﬃcient numerical approach to nonlinear propagation in waveguides through this ap-
proximation is then studied in detail. Two main test cases are studied, a weakly-guiding
step-index ﬁbre and a weakly-guiding parabolic index ﬁbre, where the approximation
is compared to the standard approach of neglecting the frequency dependence and cal-
culating the overlaps at a center wavelength λ0, and benchmarked against a Gaussian
Quadrature approach, whose accuracy can be increased systematically. Consequently,
more test cases with stronger contrasts and a microstructured ﬁbre are reported. The
performance of the approximation is studied in a number of settings. The combined
accuracy and eﬃciency of the proposed approximation make it superior to the other two
approaches in few-mode simulations.

Introduction
The work described in this work has been carried in the spring semester of 2021 as
my Master Thesis in ”Photonics Engineering” in Danmarks Tekniske Universitet, and
”ICT For Internet and Multimedia - Curriculum Photonics” in Universit`a degli Studi
di Padova under the double degree programme ”TIME”. The workload is 30 ECTS;
in DTU’s system this is equivalent to 5 full months of work. The same thesis is also
counted as 18 ECTS of thesis + 9 ECTS of stage + 3 extra ECTS in Padua.
During these ﬁve months I have worked under the supervision of Jesper Lægsgaard
(DTU), extending his Python codes to prepare and run simulations with an approximate
extension of the well-known and used overlap integral formulation of the Generalized
NonLinear Schr¨odinger Equation. This equation is used to describe the propagation
of electromagnetic pulses in waveguides, described through the so called modes of the
waveguide. The overlap formulation of the GNLSE is only approximate, as it assumes
that the transverse integral of the modes’ transverse distributions is not dependent
on its frequency arguments, or rather that the dependence can be neglected. My code
interfaced with Jesper’s previous codebase and extended the available methods. Initially
it was planned to develop two approximations, and the preparation code and analysis
was carried for both, but one of the two (Gamma Functions Approximation) has been
later abandoned when it became clear that the complexity of its implementation would
greatly surpass the slight increase in accuracy.
This document then focuses on the
Eﬀective Area Approximation (EAA).
The interest in extending the overlap integral formulation lays in the crudeness of the
approximation in situations were the mode proﬁles change signiﬁcantly in the wavelength
range of interest. For example, this means that the constant overlap approximation is
not able to model cutoﬀs (i.e. when a mode starts/stops being guided at a wavelength
characteristic of the waveguide and mode).
Furthermore, ﬁbres like parabolic-index
ﬁbres or microstructured ﬁbres show signiﬁcant changes in the mode proﬁle even away
from cutoﬀs. The main focus of this work is to extend the constant overlaps to include a
degree of frequency dependence, while keeping the time complexity penalty reasonable.
The results of this work have shown that the proposed EEA is greatly superior to the
CO approach in the test cases. At the price of a small increase of complexity, the EEA
can produce results fairly accurate to a heavier Quadrature Gaussian approach. The
results of this work have also been submitted as a scientiﬁc paper to the journal ”Optical
Society of America B” on the 24th of May (2021), titled ”Approximation of mode proﬁle
dispersion in the Generalized Nonlinear Schr¨odinger Equation superior to the constant
overlap approach for weakly guiding ﬁbers”, which is, at the time of writing, waiting
for review. After that date, according to my project plan, I would have one month to
write this document. Since often I found myself waiting for simulation results, and I
had already written the paper, I used this time to characterize more in depth the EAA.
In a couple of chapters it is then mentioned that time was not available to run more
simulations; I could have just cut those parts out, but I believe it is more interesting to
report everything I found and studied during these months, even if it is not polished.

3
Approximations of the overlap integrals
In Eq. (43), the two frequency integrals can be evaluated numerically through a dou-
ble convolution, which using a Fourier transform has a complexity of O(NlogN). The
direct evaluation would be a O(N 3) process, as the double integral is performed for all
the ω values. We now observe that the constant overlaps assumption in practice means
that the mode proﬁles should not change signiﬁcantly in the frequency range where the
power is relevant. This assumption can become particularly wrong in ﬁbres where the
mode proﬁle is very dispersive, as for example parabolic index ﬁbres, or in any ﬁbre close
to modal cutoﬀs, where the modes expand and leak in the cladding, and regardless of
the ﬁbre type in processes like SuperContinuum generation (SC) and Dispersive Wave
Generation (DWG) where the power is spread over a large portion of the spectrum.
Two approximations are stated and studied in the following, labelled the Eﬀective
Area Approximation (EAA) and Gamma Functions Approximation (GFA). Their per-
formances appear to be comparable at ﬁrst glance, since they conserve the structure of
the CO GNLSE, but practically implementing the latter is incredibly numerically heavy.
Only the EEA has been studied in numerical simulations, while the analysis for the GFA
stopped before that but is still reported for reference. In any case, the average error in
the GFA’s integrals was found to be only slightly better than the EAA, suggesting that
the latter is more than adequate.
A crucial step in assessing the validity of the approximations is showing that they con-
serve the photon number, as the physical processes (Kerr, Raman) do. This is done

6
Weakly guiding ﬁbres
This section describes the ﬁbres used in section (7) in detail. In all the following, the
errors of the Finite Diﬀerence (FD) approach might be mentioned and discussed, but no
eﬀort has been done to remove or reduce them. This is outside the scope of this work,
which is comparing methods for solving the GNLSE, and as long as the used modes
are the same for the diﬀerent approaches, the FD errors have no eﬀect on such scope3.
This document focuses on the technical point of view, rather than producing simulations
accurate to the real physics. The GQ grid here used the following parameters: nr = 15,
nθ = 7 and rmax = 3a. This was found to give the best accuracy in representing the
eﬀective areas of the modes, with a normalized error at worst ≈0.015. These ﬁbres have
closed form solutions known for their modes, but these were not considered, as the FD
approach is considered good enough and more generic.
6.1
Finite-Diﬀerence Modes
Two silica-based ﬁbres with ∆n ≤0.01 have been tested, one step-index ﬁbre and one
parabolic-index ﬁbre, also referred to as ”SIF” and ”PIF” in the following.
The 6
highest neff modes are considered in the simulations; in LP terminology, this means
the LP0,1 and LP1,1 mode groups are included. The LP terminology will be used to
refer to the modes in a simpler way; table (1) reports the equivalence between diﬀerent
naming conventions and labellings for the 6 modes set. Later, modes set of 12 and 14
modes are also used; the convention is trivially expanded. The symmetries E and O
refer to the Hx component of the mode, along the axes x and y in the transverse plane,
so that for example EO means that the mode is even-symmetric along the x axis and
odd-symmetric along the y axis. Inside a symmetry group, the indexing is such that
the highest eﬀective index at the minimum wavelength λm = 0.5µm is labelled as XY0;
eventual index crossings (i.e. the dispersions of the neff curves cross each other) are
detected and handled so that the modes keep a consistent labelling. Lastly, the modes
are calculated with constant material indices (f.x. ncl = 1.44 at every frequency step),
with the material dispersion added later through a perturbation theory approach. The
source for the silica Sellmeier data used here is taken from [5].

considerations are valid for both. Lastly, these values are obviously aﬀected by the grid
size and sampling ﬁnesse, especially as the z component tends to be focused close to the

The soliton dynamics picture
The high power levels in the ﬁbres mean that soliton dynamics, like modulation in-
stability, can happen parallely to the modal coupling.
It is recalled that a soliton
can only exist6 when the dispersion is anomalous (D > 0). In the SIF, the LP01 has
D ≈23ps/km −nm and the LP11 has ≈8ps/km −nm, so that both mode groups can
display solitonic evolution. On the other hand, the PIF has respectively 20ps/km −nm
and −8.5ps/km −nm, so that only the fundamental LP01 can produce solitons. Ex-
ploring the solitonic interaction in detail is not the scope of this work, but debug data
was plotted at diﬀerent steps during propagation, and it can be interesting to better
understand the simulations. For this reason, the reported plots are also not particularly
polished - as they were debug information - and it has been deemed unnecessary to
recalculate them. It is anyway interesting to correlate the slowing down reported in the
execution time with the onset of the modulation instability in the modes.
Figures (22) and (23) report the power against time at two diﬀerent steps; at the ﬁrst
one the LP01 is starting to collapse, while at the second the LP11 is (and the LP01 has
fully collapsed). The diﬀerence in the soliton dynamics of the two mode groups is given
by the diﬀerence in input power, eﬀective area and dispersion coeﬃcient. The LP11
modes are four, while the LP01 only has two (the orthogonal polarizations, without
the orientation degeneracy). Typically, the soliton number is deﬁned in a single mode
regime, but here the degeneracy and coupling of the modes is non-trivial. Also, it has to
be reminded that the input pulse shape is a gaussian and not a soliton; furthermore, the
interaction between the diﬀerent mode groups aﬀects the evolution signiﬁcantly. The

7.4
Complexity and execution time
Due to the large eﬀective areas close to the modal cutoﬀat 1.65µm, the GQ grid needs
to be fairly large to have converged results. In ﬁg. (14) and ﬁg. (18), the GQ grid
had rmax = 3a.
Usual GQ grids can get away with rmax ≈1.5a, but in this case
the modal cutoﬀs are very close to the centre wavelength, so the modes will in general
expand considerably. The convergence check for the GQ grid was performed both with a
larger grid and by using the same grid without considering the mode proﬁle dispersion,
checking the deviation from the CO spectrum. The remaining error (comparable to
1% in the Aeff’s) is caused mainly by the reduced smoothness of the mode proﬁles
calculated on a ﬁnite grid; this is justiﬁed by tests where the error (optimized in terms
of GQ grid parameters) was increasing with a larger sampling δx.
Running a GQ simulation with the given parameters required approximately 2 hours,
hence making it impractical to average the execution time over many runs. Furthermore,
the use of a nonlinear integrator (dopri5 in scipy.integrate.ode) with variable stepsize
can inﬂuence the run time comparison between simulations with substantially diﬀerent
results, for example slowing simulations down when the peak power is higher. Therefore,
the time comparison is performed on a single call to the subroutine evaluating the
nonlinear operator, i.e. the left hand side of Eq. (21). The more thorough complexity
comparison in [6] for the GQ and CO remains valid for the EEA, as its complexity is
the same of the CO.
The current implementation of the CO and EAA is iterating through the non-null overlap
integrals, rather than four nested cycles on the modal indices, which in these test cases
more or less cuts the execution time in half, as a large number of overlap integrals are
symmetry forbidden. EEA and CO diﬀer only in the preparation of the ¯G functions from
the ˜G in the initialization. As a result of this, the call time of the nonlinear operator
function in the both cases is increased by only 16% averaged over 3000 calls. This value
is dependent on the exact simulation parameters, as for example the ratio of non-null
overlap integrals, but it is expected to always be a modest penalty. On the other hand,
the GQ call time is approximately 27 times longer than the CO (for both ﬁbres). Part
of the increased eﬃciency of the CO and EEA with respect to the GQ method is due
to the ability to know a priori which overlap integrals are zero, hence avoiding useless
calculations. Implementing this knowledge within the GQ method is impossible, because
the overlap integrals are never explicitly evaluated. The run times reported in ﬁgs. (17)
and (21) have been seen to be quite consistent with each other during this work. The
exact output values for the SIF are 194s for the CO, 203s for the EAA and 3870s for the
GQ, while for the PIF the respective values are 514, 580 and 6019s7. It is found that the
time ratios found for the nonlinear operator functions are lowered by other operations,
like for example saving data and plots at intermediate steps, which are the same for the
three methods. The GQ requires an increase in execution time of a factor 20 and 10.4
(SIF,PIF)8 while the equivalent factors are only 1.07 and 1.13 for the EEA.

run again on a more powerful desktop computer. In previous runs the times were comparable for the

The focus of the work reported was placed on weakly guiding ﬁbres. Increasing the
index contrast in a SIF (or in general in a waveguide) means that a larger number of
modes can be guided in the same wavelength range. Due to the unfavourable complexity
scaling of the overlap-based approaches with the number of modes (O(M 4)), considering
for example a silica rod with a large core is practically impossible. Even in the weakly
guiding ﬁbres reported earlier, at shorter wavelengths some guided modes were neglected.
Arguments can be made as to how those modes would likely not interact much, ﬁrst of
which the fact that they would start with no power at the input (they are not guided at
λ0). In any case, two examples of stronger contrasts are brieﬂy reported in this section.
It can be expected that the EEA could have a slightly harder time, as the overlaps might
have a more complicated dispersion curve. This reasoning is justiﬁed by the fact that
weakly guided modes are somehow ”close” to the bulk plane waves, and they mostly
change in size with wavelength, while strong conﬁnement can induce more complicated
modal structures, with especially stronger z components of the ﬁelds. This section also
explores the relevance of the distinction between the two diﬀerent ﬁeld conjugations in
the eﬀective areas Aeff and A′
eff, as it is expected that the stronger z components
might induce a relevant diﬀerence in their wavelength dependence.

evolution. Checking in detail, after 1mm of propagation the diﬀerent mode groups are
already not overlapping anymore. Clearly, if the LP02 and LP21 were fed any power at
the input, their interaction within their own modal groups would make the simulations

spike around the central wavelength, which is identiﬁed with the LP21 modes generated

Proposed extensions of the EAA
It is seen that using a constant factor plus the eﬀective areas of the involved modes
to represent the dispersion of the overlap integrals is a signiﬁcant improvement on the
accuracy of the output spectrum with respect to the constant overlap approach. At the
same time, it is also seen that the accuracy of this method is limited by its increasing
error moving away from the centre of the dataset. Some ideas to reduce this error are
proposed and analyzed in this section, to see if an Extended EAA can be developed.
The error for non-diagonal frequency arguments described in the previous section is not
considered directly here, as it is intrinsic to the structure of the approximated overlap
integral, and it is unlikely to be improvable considerably. This section considers test
cases where the error of the EAA is not already 2%, as it would be hard to improve an
already excellent ﬁgure. Rather, all the tests are performed on the PIF used previously;
it is possible that using a strong contrast ﬁbre or a microstructured ﬁbre could yield
diﬀerent results. This was not tested due to the time constraints of this work, but it
can be said that if an extension is worse in this case, it is (a) unlikely to be better in
another case and (b) impractical to use, as the method’s accuracy is not available without
running much longer simulations with the GQ method, so it cannot be guaranteed to be
an improvement beforehand. Nevertheless, it can also be that those geometries, which
in general show larger overlap deviations from the exact solution, might be the ones
beneﬁting the most from an extension of the EAA. This could be explored in future
work.

11.1
Multiple centre frequencies
The ﬁrst idea to enhance the accuracy of the EEA is to use two diﬀerent centre fre-
quencies. Since the EEA is exact at the centre frequency, this could allow to have two
53
==PAGE:55==
points in the frequency grid, for example the centre frequency of the input pulse and the
predicted one for the DWG peak, with near perfect accuracy. Trivially, the concept can
be extended in principle to any N number of centre frequencies. Implementing this in
the current nonlinear operator function is non trivial. The most eﬃcient way to evaluate
the integral in the nonlinear polarization is in time (Eq. (22)), and then convert the
total polarization back to frequency only once at the end of the function. To introduce
a frequency dependent fmnpq coeﬃcient, a number of additional FFTs (+IFFTs) pro-
portional to N 4
modes is needed. This is clearly a huge penalty in the workload needed at
every call of the function. Furthermore, the proof given for the photon number conserva-
tion is based on a symmetry argument between the four modal terms in the ∂zGm · G∗
m.
Introducing a single dependence on ω, and for example not ω1, breaks this symmetry,
most likely breaking also the conservation. It is not certain that this will happen be-
cause other symmetries could still arise or be already present, but the tests carried in
this chapter seem to indicate that this would not be the case. A second proposal, which
is easier to implement, is to calculate two separate PNL at every step, with the two
centre frequencies, and average them appropriately to obtain a new polarization as
PNL(ω) = PNL,1(ω) · W1(ω) + PNL,2(ω) · W2(ω)
(81)
where the two functions Wi(ω) represent generic weights to the average, and the indices
1 and 2 refer to the two diﬀerent centre frequencies. Note also that this scheme is easily
extended to N centre points, with an expected penalty in complexity linear to this N.
The reasoning is seen in ﬁgure (44): on the two sides of the centre, the sign of the error
of the approximated overlap is diﬀerent, so summing appropriately could compensate
it. For example, averaging between 1.4µm and 1.55µm could compensate the over and
underestimation given by the two approximations.

Linear averaging
Seeing in the previous section that there is little if nothing to gain from using an expo-

averaging, the fact that increasing the number of centre points increases the accuracy
is encouraging.
11.1.3
Constant averaging
Continuing on the idea of compensating the deviation as in ﬁg. (44), the next averag-
ing scheme considers constant weights in the regions between two centre points, with a
weight of 1 outside the ﬁrst and last points and 1/2 between points. Since the approxi-
mation is exact at the centre wavelengths, the value there will be 1 for the appropriate
wavelengths. The spectrum for this case is not reported for the sake of brevity. One
possible issue with this averaging is the discontinuity of the averaged polarization at the
centre points, but in the reported cases this has been checked to not produce disconti-
nuities in the spectrum. In any case, the numbers do not suggest this approach has any
advantage even compared to the other averaging schemes.

11.1.4
Note on weighing the input ﬁelds
Another idea, which could at ﬁrst seem interesting, is to average the input ﬁelds and then
sum their respective nonlinear responses. This is easily seen to be a wrong approach,
as it assumes that the nonlinear operator has the property of linearity. Another easy
argument is that the Raman response connects frequencies displaced by a certain amount
centred at ≈∆f = 13THz; consider for example two monochromatic peaks separated
by such ∆f, with two centre points of the approximation at the respective frequencies.
Averaging the input ﬁelds would completely cut away one ﬁeld from the other, and the
Raman scattering would be lost. Just for the sake of completeness, a trial with the 5
centre points used earlier has been performed, reporting an error of 135%12.

11.1.5
Final comments
All in all, it is found that averaging the diﬀerent PNL does not produce any reason-
able improvement on the accuracy of the EAA, while actually degrading the photon
conservation accuracy and increasing the required workload. The only improvement is
found using a linear averaging, but it is a slight improvement that does not justify the
mentioned drawbacks, and whose generality is in doubt: it would not be surprising if
diﬀerent test cases showed the EAA was slightly superior. It is also noticed that it
improves the accuracy on the single modes’ spectra slightly. To sum up, this technique
is not likely to have any worth going forward; furthermore, it would be impractical to
use as it is found to be unreliable: without having the GQ spectrum already simulated
it is not trivial to know which averaging combination (of points and weight functions)
is better, and with the GQ spectrum already simulated there is no need for it.
11.2
Common rescaling factor
A second proposal for an extension of the EEA is to introduce a common rescaling
factor to the overlap integrals, to account for the average deviation from the exact
values. One thing to keep in mind is that the overlaps of the kind Γmmmm are exact
in this approximation, and they are among the strongest contributors to the nonlinear
polarization. Rescaling them too would probably introduce an error larger than the
improvement. They then have to be kept away from this rescaling. Once it is accepted
that diﬀerent kinds of overlaps might need a diﬀerent rescaling, this is more generic than
only the mode-diagonal terms. If, for the sake of the argument, the overlaps Γmmnn
are overestimated while the Γmmnp are underestimated, the rescaling to average should
be performed to diﬀerent normalization functions to avoid increasing an error while
reducing another. At the same time, this is highly impractical and problem speciﬁc, with
would make it hard to use in a practical simulation. The following then focuses to the
rescaling of all terms, but the Γmmmm, to the average deviation. The average deviation
could already be seen for example in ﬁgures (8) and (12), but the rescaling is also
reported in detail in ﬁgure (49) in this section. Lastly, in terms of implementation, the
rescaling factor can be applied directly to the output PNL or to the input ¯G functions,
with an acceptable penalty in both cases.
The comparison setting is the same used for the previous section, and the spectra are
reported in ﬁgure (48). The simple EAA reports an error of 7.7% like before, while the
two versions of the EEAA report an error of 8.55% for the ﬁnal rescaling and 8.29% for
the initial rescaling.
The issue with this approach, as mentioned already, is that the average rescaling does
not take into consideration the diﬀerent kinds of overlap. Figure (49) reports the used
rescaling factor, comparing it to two relevant overlap integrals. The constant 1 factor
for long wavelengths is the regime where the ﬁbre is single moded and the EAA is
exact; at 1.25µm the crossing of all curves identiﬁes the centre wavelength.
While
the accuracy of the Γ0101 is increasing, the Γ1212’s is actually signiﬁcantly worsened.
Having just one exception to the rescaling (Γmmmm) is not enough to actually produce
an improvement in the accuracy. Alas, having diﬀerent rescaling factors would evidently
make preparing the simulation dataset a very tricky process: how many diﬀerent ”kinds”
of overlap rescalings are needed? How can this be automated? This could be a large
number, which would imply an increase in complexity, with an increase in accuracy
of the approximation which is likely not signiﬁcant. After these considerations, this
approach can be abandoned too.

Final comments on the extension of the EAA
To sum up, this chapter proposed some extensions of the EAA, meant to compensate
its loss of accuracy away from the centre wavelength.
Diﬀerent ideas are proposed,
but in the employed test case, none was found to be useful. In general, it is found
that tinkering with the approximation would break, at least slightly, its photon number
conservation property, and increase its workload. Nevertheless, stronger index contrasts
might actually be the context in which these extensions are useful; after all, the weakly
guiding ﬁbres are seen to be behaving well enough for the simple EAA. It has to be

12
Conclusions and future possibilities
This work explored the possibilities for expanding the common approach of numeri-
cal nonlinear propagation in multimode waveguides using Constant Overlap integrals.
It focused on the so called ”Eﬀective Area Approximation”, which showed promise
in modelling the mode proﬁle dispersion and cutoﬀwith a small complexity penalty.
The proposed approximation has been tested against a Gaussian Quadrature, a slower
method whose accuracy can be increased at the price of a slower simulation. Throughout
the work, ideas and possible further developments are mentioned whenever they came
up naturally.
In the few-mode regime, it is found that the total runtime is cut by a factor of at least 10
in a 6 modes simulation, while the accuracy loss is acceptable. The tests carried in this
work focused on step-index ﬁbres and parabolic-index ﬁbres. Due to time constraints,
exploring the behaviour of the approximation in microstructured ﬁbres has only been
done superﬁcially; in these more complex geometries, the mode proﬁles are also more
complex, and the Gaussian Quadrature needs to slow down even more to guarantee a
reasonable accuracy. There is hope that this overlap-based method can signiﬁcantly
speed up the simulation in this case, as it is of high practical interest, and the initial
tests seem promising. Even if the proper convergence of the GQ is a challenge in these
cases, it can be argued that having a result that is close to the GQ in a shorter time
is still of practical interest. Future work could be for sure imagined in exploring more
complex geometries.
Possible extensions of the approximation have been explored. Alas, it seems that in-
creasing the accuracy is not only impossible, but it would anyway come at the price of
a larger complexity, both in the sense of runtime and preparation of the data, and of
a loss of the photon number conservation. Although more possibilities are present in
this direction, it is not expected that more work would be done in this direction, as the
performance level of the EAA is already acceptable in most cases. The only chance for
this to be interesting is if it were useful in microstructured ﬁbres, where the accuracy
issues of the EAA become more important.
Another approximation, named ”Gamma Functions Approximation”, was proposed and
later discarded because of its unmanageable complexity. The discussion about it was
left for the purpose of reporting all the work that was carried in these months.
All in all, it can be said that the proposed EAA is superior to the CO approach in all
cases, with a modest penalty in runtime. This might make it an interesting practical
choice for nonlinear simulations in short pieces of few-mode ﬁbres.
