Abstract 
The climate is changing; this is the reality that we as humans have to deal with. The water 
levels are rising and the precipitation patterns are changing due to increased temperatures 
on earth. This is caused by human activities through the past 50 years, where the use of 
large quantities of fossil fuels have released all sorts of greenhouse gasses (GHG) into the 
atmosphere (WHO 2014). Currently the environment is on many countries and cities agenda, 
where policies have been introduced in order to reduce emission of GHGs. At the same time 
urbanization makes cities grow rapidly, which only stresses the need for smart and 
sustainable solutions within development and planning of cities. 
 
With an increased focus on sustainability city planners do what they can in order to right 
past mistakes. City planning and design of urban developments is a very complex matter as 
the amount of influencing parameters are almost countless. Due to the complexity within 
urban planning, design decisions are often based on discussions, experiences and 
aesthetics generated within the design team. Unfortunately this does not give any 
guarantees for development of sustainable designs. This thesis documents how a small-
scale urban development can be optimized in terms of energy consumption, accessibility to 
nature and solar exposure. Using a parametric approach, it was possible to generate 
solutions based on sustainability indicators, while accounting for various design-parameters 
and constraints. By analysing how dwellings in the neighbourhood was influenced by large 
vegetation, the surrounding nature, and geometries of the buildings, it was possible to 
connect different design-scales, which previously has not been attempted while using 
parametric modelling. In order to test these influences several scenarios were created and 
optimized solutions were found for each scenario. By comparing the findings with the initially 
planned geometry a great potential for parametric optimization in urban developments was 
discovered. Despite great potential, the use of parametric modelling within small-scale urban 
developments is challenging and complicated. Extensive discussion of the problems and 
uncertainties within both parametric modelling of city developments and a model developed 
as part of this study will be presented within this thesis.

1 // Introduction 
In cities around the world, new districts, neighbourhoods and public spaces are constantly 
developing in order to deal with the constant growth of population that occurs in larger cities 
all over the world. Naturally, Copenhagen is no exception, and large development projects, 
such as Ørestad, Nordhavn, and the Carlsberg are districts where residential, business, and 
retail developments are currently being build (Municipality of Copenhagen 2014a; 
Municipality of Copenhagen 2014b; Municipality of Copenhagen 2014c). 
 
Within Greater Copenhagen a new development is currently (2015) being brought to life. A 
new sustainable city named Vinge located near the city of Frederikssund. The location of the 
future Vinge is currently covered by fields and scattered farms, which allows city planners to 
develop Vinge as a sustainable city from day one. Being a greenfield site provides the 
planners with several benefits, for instance increased flexibility for the design of buildings, 
infrastructure and energy systems (Wiley 2011). Starting from empty fields provide the 
planners with a unique opportunity to develop a city district which will fulfil all the future 
demands for sustainable city developments in Copenhagen and contribute to the goal of a 
CO2 neutral Copenhagen in 2025 (Miljøforvaltningen 2012). 
 
The increasing demands within every aspect of sustainability challenge architects, planners, 
and engineers to develop urban areas, which combines wishes and demands from 
municipalities, planners, and end-users. The increased complexity within city development 
have increased the collaboration between architects and engineers as seen in the Integrated 
Energy Design [IED] framework and certification tools, such as the DGNB (Deutche 
Gesellschaft für Nachhaltiges Bauen) certification tool for Urban Districts which require 
knowledge from all parts and encourage collaboration. As a result of the increased 
complexity within urban developments, digital tools are being rapidly being developed by 
engineers and architects to aid and control the increasing amounts of data within each 
project.  
 
Simulation of energy consumption, daylight, indoor climate etc. are common on every new 
building project and the methods and tools used to document the design, energy efficiency 
and indoor comfort are highly developed. The disadvantages of most of these tools are that 
they are designed to singular buildings and will not work on a larger scale, such as a city 
district or a small neighbourhood. Zooming out from the building scale to the urban city 
scale, the use of simulation and design tools become less frequent and examples of such is 
not everyday practice. The primary reason is found within the complexity of cities, where 
multiple aspects influence the everyday life for the citizens (Beirão et al. 2011).  
 
Planning of urban developments is often based on the planners and landlord’s visions, rules 
of thumb, and personal experiences. The designer’s influence is hereby dominant to the final 
design and the level of sustainability incorporated in the project. Economic studies from 
construction projects show that the cost of change increases along with the design 
progresses through the design phases (Hendrickson 2000, chap. 2). Decisions about 
changes are often seen as consequences related to the early designs are often discovered 
later in the project. Work by Nikolai Steinø et al. shows that while the understanding of a 
project increases along with time, the possibility to influence the project decreases (Steinø et 
al. 2013), as illustrated in Figure 1-1.

The lack of knowledge in the early design phases can be a time consuming and costly affair. 
Frameworks such as IED seek to increase collaboration to feed the early design phases with 
as much knowledge and understanding as possible. PhD Tore Banke describes in his 
dissertation a typical design process from the architecture firm 3XN, which can be seen in 
Figure 1-2. Through questionnaires, answered by various international architecture firms,

in Figure 1-2 can be seen as a common design process.

Providing architects and other stakeholders with valuable information at the earliest stages 
can aid the architects in their discussion making and eliminate uncertainties as early as 
possible. Tore Bank concludes in his dissertation that parametric tools can aid the early 
design phases in terms of daylight and the understanding of the project. He describes how 
parametric tools can be used to communicate knowledge, which was previously hard to 
communicate for the stakeholders within the project.

Parametric modelling is rather new within architecture and planning, but despite its youth, it 
allows non-programmers to develop unique models suited for each project (Banke 2013, 
p.242). Using parametric modelling in the early design phases can provide the design team 
with required knowledge in order to make the “best possible” decision and thus achieve a 
higher degree of sustainability in terms of nature and economics. 
 
Sustainability have become one of those fluffy words that are mandatory in the description of 
most products, no matter if it is a sports shoe or a large office building and people often 
have a different view on sustainability and what it involves. One way to bring clarification and 
avoid misunderstandings is to create some clear measureable definitions that can be 
connected to the sustainability aspect. Such definitions are typically called sustainability 
indicators and allow various stakeholders to compare different cities, strategies, solutions, or 
what the indicators are supposed to provide knowledge about. 
1.1 Vinge 
The development of Vinge is currently (2015) the largest urban development in Denmark with 
an area of 350 ha (Frederikssunds Kommune et al. 2012). Vinge is located in the outskirts of 
Greater Copenhagen area near cities as Frederikssund and Ølstykke.  
 
Vinge will be a part of “Fingerplanen” (the Finger Plan) which for decades has laid ground for 
the large-scale urban development of Greater Copenhagen (Vejre & Brandt 2007; Andersen 
& Jørgensen 1995).  
 
The initial thoughts behind the Finger Plan was introduced in 1938/1939 (Figure 1-3), but due 
to the Second World War the actual implementation of the Finger Plan happened in 1947. 
Each finger in the plan represents a transportation vein where public transportation is 
covered by the trains and where development of cities was planned around the stops within 
each vein. Despite changes in the regional planning in 1972, which broke the finger structure, 
the principle was not forgotten and was again implemented in the Regional Plan from 1989 
and have been maintained since (Andersen & Jørgensen 1995).  
 
Vinge will be connected to the Frederikssund finger (the middle finger) with a brand new  
S-train station. Vinge Station will be the first new S-train station developed in 40 years and 
will write a new chapter to the story of the Finger Plan (Frederikssunds Kommune et al. 2012). 
 
Once Vinge is fully developed it will accommodate 10.000 citizens and 4.000 jobs making 
Vinge comparable to some of the semi-large cities in Denmark such as Vordingborg or 
Haslev (Frederikssunds Kommune et al. 2012). The city does not only seek to provide its 
citizens with its basic needs such as public transportation, school, day-care, sport facilities, 
and parks, but also create a sustainable city using Smart City strategies.   
 
Since Vinge is a completely new development they have a unique opportunity to be a frontier 
among sustainable developments where Smart City initiatives can be implemented from the 
very beginning.  
 
The visions behind Vinge is described with five key points (Frederikssunds Kommune et al. 
2012), all of which clarifies the wishes for Vinge.

1.3 Scope of study 
When talking about urban planning it is important to acknowledge that it exists in many 
scales, and should therefore be worked upon in different ways. What requires one focus in 
large scale planning might not require the same focus when designing small neighbourhoods. 
At this stage other things such as exact building locations, lot sizes and dimensioning of 
buildings will take focus. 
 
This study will focus on the small-scale urban planning of one pre-defined area in 
Deltakvarteret (Figure 1-12). The area R2, where row houses is going to be build, was 
chosen as case in agreement with project manager Rikke Grendal, as this area together with 
the area in V2 are the first areas to be build in Deltakvarteret. 
 
 
 
Figure 1-12: Location and naming of the areas in Deltakvarteret. Source: SLA 2014. 
 
This thesis investigates how greenfield urban developments can use sustainable indicators, 
and parametric modelling to generate a deeper understanding in the early design phases 
where the knowledge is required. Using an academic approach, based on case specific 
indicators the study seeks to assist the development of Deltakvarteret in finding alternative 
solutions fulfilling both the building regulations and the related quality criteria previously 
described. Working in small-scale urban planning, demands that the planners, and thus this 
study, to consider all relevant design aspects. In a district as Deltakvarteret, which is 
characterised by wild nature, and a place where nature will be allowed to prevail. With the 
wishes for Deltakvarteret it is very likely that large trees will be present in the future city 
district and their location should therefore be considered throughout the design phases.   
 
Unplanned elements can cause problems in terms of comfort and energy for buildings. If 
placed wrong, extra energy might be required for additional room heating and artificial 
lighting. In order to avoid future problems various locations of large vegetation will be 
investigated. Through the use of parametric modelling, evolutionary optimization algorithms, 
and sustainability indicators it will be tested if multi-objective optimization of a small-scale 
urban development is possible. The mentioned tools will be used upon different scenarios 
where the vegetation will be varied. The impact large vegetation and other obstructions will 
have on small-scale urban planning will be investigated through analysis of the solutions 
generated using evolutionary optimization algorithms and case specific sustainability 
indicators.

2 // Methodology 
The approach chosen in order to study the possibilities of using parametric models in small 
urban districts and design sustainable neighbourhoods based on sustainability indicators are 
described in this chapter. At first topics concerning parametric models are explained 
followed by a brief description of how cities and parametric models can be coupled and how 
these can influence the future design of sustainable cities. This topic leads to a description 
of various digital tools, both used within this thesis and for use elsewhere.  
 
Where the first half of this chapter primarily is characterized by theory and descriptions, the 
second half will focus on the actual case, Deltakvarteret in Vinge. First the sustainability 
indicators are defined and next the actual development of the parametric model is explained 
in detail. 
2.1 Parametric design 
Parametric design is a digital design method where different parameters, rules and 
constraints are connected and together shape the final design (Jabi 2013). Within this project 
a residential neighbourhood is designed using a parametric computer model, developed 
specifically for this project. 
2.1.1 Parametric modelling 
Parametric modelling has a different meaning depending on the field of study. The 
terminology of parametric originates from mathematics and statistics where a parametric 
model is defined as a group of distributions that can be described within a finite number of 
parameters. 
 
Within CAD (Computer Aided Design) modelling a parametric model has a different definition 
and will be described in the following. In parametric modelling different parameters is used 
to define geometry or other design output. Basically anything can be a parameter, as long as 
it is defined and properly implemented. Among others parameters can be distances, forces, 
data or patterns. Common for any parametric model is that when one parameter is changed 
the model will adapt to the changes and generate the geometry defined by the new 
parameters. 
 
A parametric model holds several advantages compared to traditional CAD when it comes to 
making changes, and especially larger models where different parts of the model might be 
interconnected. A simple example of the advantage of parametric modelling could be the 
visualization and design of a brick wall. If the designer decides to use a longer brick the two 
models (regular and parametric) would have to adapt. In the parametric model this could be 
done within seconds just by changing the input, which define the brick dimensions. In a 
regular model this would be more time consuming, as more action would be required.  
2.1.2 Parametric modelling in the building industry  
The use of parametric design has existed for decades. In the 1970’s parametric design was 
used by the car and aerospace industries where the large-scale production benefitted from a 
parametric design method (Banke 2013, p.136). In the building sector parametric design was 
introduced in the beginning of the 1990’s where Frank Gehry worked with the CATIA 
(Computer-Aided Three Dimensional Interactive Application) platform, which he used to 
construct a sculpture completed for the 1992 Olympics (Nagy 2009). CATIA was originally 
developed as a engineering tool for the aeroplane industry and was later used in the 
spacecraft, shipping, and car industry and now also in the building industry (Banke 2013, 
p.136). 
 
==PAGE:38==
 
14 
Since Frank Gehry introduced the parametric design method to architecture, the use of 
parametric design has spread widely and is now used for buildings in all scales. 
 
 
 
Figure 2-1: Parametric used in architecture, 1) Bejing National Stadium, 2) Beijing National Aquatics Center, 
3) Yas Hotel, Abu Dhabi. 
 
Zooming out from the building scale reveals a whole new field of parametric opportunities 
related to the urban design and planning. Within urban design, parametric design has 
steadily developed over the past 20 years and is commonly used in avant-garde architecture 
at all scales ranging from interior design to large urban planning projects. Along with the 
complexity of a project the superiority and benefits of parametric modelling can be seen, as 
such models can be developed to handle data and geometry with high complexity 
(Schumacher 2009). Zaha Hadid Architects is one of the architectural firms who use 
parametric approaches in many of their planning projects. A few examples of implemented 
parametric projects in urban design are One-North Masterplan, a mixed-use business park in 
Singapore for 138.000 people and Kartal-Pendik Masterplan a mixed-use urban area of 550 
ha (Schumacher 2009). 
 
 
 
Figure 2-2: Parametric design used in cities. Source: Left, Zaha Hadid Architects 2006. Right, (Fluxi_Z 2011). 
2.1.3 The influence of parametricism in future smart cities  
Parametricism is the next what experts have named the recent avant-garde architectural 
style of designing. The style is based on digital animation techniques and its use can be seen 
in architectural interior design and design of large urban developments (Schumacher 2009). 
In an urban setting parametric models and thus parametricism can be used to analyse a 
variety of aspects. A few examples are solar potential, planning of street layout, distance 
analysis, economics, and density studies. One example of this is a study from SHC 2013, 
International Conference on Solar Heating and Cooling for Buildings and Industry where the 
solar potential on roofs in a medium sized city was investigated using a parametric approach. 
From geographic information systems (GIS) the required data was extracted and used to 
generate a 3D model and collect information about building age, population, density, 
location, etc. In their study they accounted for roof area, inclination, orientation, and shading 
in order to map the locations with high solar potential. They argue that utilizing the locations 
==PAGE:39==
 
15 
with high solar potential would move the production closer to the consumer, which would be 
advantageous in terms of energy transportation and better integration in a future smart grid. 
The studies showed that 62% of the energy consumption could be covered by placing PV 
panels in these locations (Amado & Poggi 2014). Furthermore they highlight the possibility to 
store data related to the PV production and user consumption in the GIS model (Amado & 
Poggi 2014). Future smart cities would benefit from mapping this information as it would 
allow energy designers to see which districts have a positive, neutral or negative 
(consumption is higher than production) energy production and thus provide information 
related to positioning of future PV panels.  
 
Before the influence of parametricism is elaborated further the general concept a smart city 
will be explained briefly. The concept of smart city has emerged through the last decade 
where ideas about how communication technologies and information could improve the 
functionality, efficiency, competitiveness of cities and create new ways to address problems 
of poverty, social aspects, and environmental issues (Batty et al. 2012). In the Business 
Dictionary a smart city is defines as: 
 
 
“ A developed urban area that creates sustainable economic development 
and high quality of life by excelling in multiple key areas; economy, mobility, 
environment, people, living, and government. “ - (Business Dictionary n.d.) 
 
This definition is much alike the description Jesse Berst, Chairman of the Smart Cities 
Council gives. He describes a smart city as, a city who uses technology to strengthen the 
liveability, workability and sustainability and succeed in all three aspects (Vander Veen 2014). 
Other experts as Professor Michael Batty and colleagues, have analysed the paradigm shift 
many cities face in order to become a smart city, which they have defined as: 
 
 
“ A city in which ICT is merged with traditional infrastructures, coordinated 
and integrated using new digital technologies. “ - (Batty et al. 2012) 
 
The two definitions are related, yet different. Definition by Business Dictionary is a 
description of what a smart city does, where the definition by Batty et al. related to the actual 
dilemma. How to become a smart city?  
 
The interconnectivity of information tools and communication technologies developed over 
the past decade makes it easier for various stakeholders to see the connection between 
different city sectors. A stronger connection makes it possible to improve efficiency, equity 
and the quality of life for the citizens (Batty et al. 2012).  
 
Improving the quality of life is exactly what H. Taleb and M. Musleh attempts to do through 
parametric design. They investigate the potential in using parametric design optimization 
processes in urban planning in order to create a more sustainability style of planning. A hot 
climate city district was used as case and urban environmental factors such as wind speeds, 
solar radiation, and energy consumption was investigated. Through the use of Grasshopper 
(visual programming software) and Galapagos (evolutionary solver in Grasshopper) it was 
possible to improve the condition of wind, reduce the total energy consumption and thus 
designing a more sustainable city district. The optimized solution was gained by changing 
the building geometry where both the architectural footprint and the building height were 
adjustable (Taleb & Musleh 2015).  
 
From the two examples it is clear that the use of parametric modelling spans wide within the 
field of urban planning. In the first example the solar potential in an existing city was 
analysed. In the second example an urban district were optimized by altering the shape of 
the buildings. It seems that the use of parametric modelling is either used to either analyse 
existing urban districts and entire cities in order to gain knowledge or for development of 
advanced design of buildings or urban districts according to adjustable parameters.  
 
Using parametric modelling approaches in order to make the jump from “regular” to smart 
city is very likely to happen as it becomes easier and easier to create tool and access the

2.2 Existing software 
When it comes to the process of planning new urban city districts architects and planners 
tend to use digital tools to control various information such as site geometry and data from 
which the layout of the site is based upon. This section will describe a few examples of tools 
designed to assist planners.  
2.2.1 Urban Network Analysis Tool (UNA) 
UNA is an analysis tool developed by Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), which 
can be used to analyse the existing build environment by coupling data from various sources. 
By tracking data such as number of purchases in stores, tailing traffic, real estate patterns, 
and business location patterns, a broader knowledge of the city life can be achieved by 
coupling these data with the layout of any city. The tool subdivide the city into three 
geometric elements, 
 
§ 
Links  - Represented by paths along which traffic can occur. 
§ 
Nodes – Tracking intersections of paths and is where public spaces are formed. 
§ 
Buildings – That contains data and is where the movement of people begins or ends. 
 
By using this geometric setup the software can help designers explain social, economic, and 
environmental relations by using three simple network analysis methods, which will 
explained below (Sevtsuk & Mekonnen 2012). 
 
Reach metric – Find the number of reachable destinations within a given radius. By 
specifying the search it is possible to find the number of jobs, number of residents, amount 
of floor area etc. The radius can be changed based on transportation mode. 
 
Closeness metric – Within specified radius the closeness metric summarizes all the data 
within the area, for instance jobs, residents and floor area. 
 
Betweenness metric – Estimates the number of times a building is passed within a given 
timeframe. An example on the use of this metric could be an investor trying to find the 
optimal location for a new retail shop. By combining data from buildings and public 
transportation the betweenness index will find how many people pass by specific points of 
interest and in that way find the best location for a new business. 
 
Urban Network Analysis Tool is build upon a GIS based platform and is used within the 
computer program ArcGIS by Esri.  
2.2.2 CitySIM 
CitySIM is a tool that can be used to simulate solar radiation on façades and rooftops in 
large city models. The tool uses in 3D user interface, from where the result can be analysed 
directly. Besides the solar radiation module CitySIM include a node-based thermal energy 
module used to find the conduction of analysed façades in the model. This module accounts 
for wind speed, orientation and increased temperatures caused by solar radiation (Robinson 
et al. 2009). In regards to solar radiation a model called Simplified Radiosity Algorithm (SRA) 
by Robinson and Stone (2004) is used. The model is based upon the Tregenza sky that 
divides the sky into 145 sky patches. 
 
Furthermore CitySIM can be used to simulate the energy demand of buildings. The 
calculations uses an integrated occupants behaviour model which respects the stochastic 
aspects of occupant behaviour related to heating, cooling and ventilation (École 
Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne 2014). 
 
An article from 2009 mentions coming models to be added to CitySIM. Amongst others three 
aspects were very interesting. Besides the energy flows in urban developments during 
==PAGE:42==
 
18 
operation time it was planned to integrate calculations and databases which could provide 
information about embodied energy in materials and perform life cycle analysis (LCA).  
 
The third relates the energy used for transport of people and goods around the city. Using 
MATSim-T (Multi-Agent Transport Simulation Toolkit) from 1990, which simulates the 
transport of individual agents (people or goods) in a given urban scene. The scene is based 
on a map of the given city where each building is equipped with coordinates and other 
relevant information such as number of driving licences, population, and availability of cars. 
By coupling common activity patterns, activity locations (home, office or activity), and all 
possible routes connecting these locations would enable the software to harvest valuable 
information about the traffic flow in the city. Using an evolutionary solver the hourly activity 
of each agent would then be possible to find (Robinson et al. 2009). When implemented a 
much broader understanding of the traffic around the city would be achievable. Similar 
models for pedestrian behaviour could be the next step of further development.  
 
Please note that it has not been possible to find information that verifies if mentioned 
initiatives have been implemented yet.  
2.2.3 Flux Metro 
Flux Metro is a tool designed for use in the very beginning of a new city project. Flux Metro 
handles data from city codes, tax controllers and other third party sources and collects the 
data in one place making it easy for developers to access location specific information. 
Within the software it is possible to apply a filter so only the relevant codes and regulations 
are visible for the user. 
 
Loaded within Flux Metro is related references with direct access to city codes and 
regulations for detailed study.

user interface is easy to understand and city code restrictions such as heights and views are 
visualized in 3D (Figure 2-4). 
 
Note that Flux Metro is a project that is still under development and the only city in which it is 
implemented is Austin, Texas. 
2.2.4 CityCAD 
CityCAD is a holistic city-planning tool that couples the layout of city districts to the actual 
planning dilemmas. Within CityCAD it is possible to do city design analysis where 
accessibility, traffic and occupancy are just a few the elements included in the software. 
CityCAD makes it easy to visualize these data through figures and diagrams. Further more 
sustainability analysis, liveability analysis and street analysis can be done in CityCAD.

2.2.5 Grasshopper Environment 
Grasshopper is a plugin for the CAD software Rhinoceros 3D. Grasshopper allows architects 
to use parametric tools and simple programming techniques due to a visual programming 
interface where non-programmers can develop unique models at low cost (Taleb & Musleh 
2015; Banke 2013, p.4). 
 
Grasshopper is an open-source environment, which makes it possible for third person 
developers to create and share components and plugins with the rest of the world. 
Components related to urban planning, civil engineering and architecture are constantly 
being added and developed. Amongst others are the component package called Ladybug + 
Honeybee.  
 
Ladybug is used to create environmental cautious designs using standard EnergyPlus 
weather files and illustrating results in easily understandable way (Sadeghipour 2014). 
Ladybug can be used to analyse solar radiation and comfort conditions and allows the user 
to easily produce different visual outputs such as solar roses and psychometric charts. 
 
Honeybee connects the visual programmatic interface of Grasshopper to EnergyPlus, 
Radiance, OpenStudio and Daysim making these tools usable in a parametric way 
(Sadeghipour 2014).  
2.2.6 Summary of Existing Tools 
The difference between mentioned urban development tools span rather wide and the idea

developments these analysing programs lack a possibility to seek alternatives (Sevtsuk 
2014). The ability to generate alternative solutions and compare them are essential for 
architects, planners and other stakeholders in order to present and ensure that the planned 
changes are actually beneficial. Tools as CityCAD and CitySIM can be used to generate fast 
urban developments and harvest useful information for the planners and other stakeholders. 
The information attainable from CityCAD and CitySIM and similar software are only a small 
portion of the information required to design urban districts.  
 
In a study focusing on urban analysis tools, where UNA was the primary software, Andres 
Sevtsuk mentions, that since good urban planning requires thousands of variables, which a

2.3 Solver and Optimization Engine 
Optimization and problem solving can be handled using different methods. Due to software 
limitations the evolutionary solver have been used throughout this study and will be 
explained in the following. 
 
An evolutionary solver is a method used to find optimised solutions to problems where 
multiple parameters influence the result in on or the other way. Developing an evolutionary 
solver is quite complicated and the theory behind requires a great amount of knowledge to 
programming and mathematics. Luckily an integrated plugin can be found inside 
Grasshopper, making optimization and problem solving possible for non-programmers as 
myself. 
2.3.1 Principles used in evolutionary solvers 
An evolutionary solver is based on genes (variables) which when connected generate a 
fitness. The fitness can best be visualised as a fitness landscape as seen on Figure 2-6 
where we have two parameters, gene A on one of the horizontal axis and gene B on the 
other. Found in the vertical axis is the fitness – the result generated by gene A and B at 
different values. Note that this example only has two genes, which makes visualization of the 
landscape understandable.  
 
 
Figure 2-6: Fitness landscape (left) and populated fitness landscape (right). Source: David Rutten. 
 
When the solver starts it has no idea about the shape of the fitness landscape and can only 
guess for solutions by populating the fitness landscape with individual solutions, called 
genomes (Figure 2-6, right). A genome is a value holding information of each and every gene, 
much like a coordinate. The genomes are sorted by its fitness, ranging from fittest to the 
weakest. A defined part of the weakest solutions is then discarded. This is the end of 
Generation 0. Next the solver breeds the best performing genomes in order to make 
Generation 1 and maintain a proper population size. The new combinations of genomes 
called mutations or offspring will appear near their parents (genomes from Generation 0) in 
the fitness landscapes covering new ground in the search for a greater fitness, and this a 
better solution.  
 
 
 
Figure 2-7: Illustrated evolutionary solver process. Source: David Rutten. 
 
==PAGE:46==
 
22 
The generation of new mutation is essential for evolutionary solving since the chances that 
the optimal solution is found within Generation 0 (where the genomes were generated 
randomly) is very low, yet possible (Rutten 2010). This procedure is repeated until the solver 
terminates. This typically happens when some criteria are met or the maximum of generation 
is reached (Runarsson 2000). 
2.3.2 Benefits & limitations 
We live in a world where there rarely is one perfect solution and where everything has both 
pros and cons. Same goes for evolutionary solvers, which is listed in the following. 
 
Limitations of evolutionary solvers: 
§ 
Slow, evolutionary solvers are slow and it is not unseen that a single problem can 
take days to solve. The run time is highly connected with the number of parameters 
which generates the solution space (Rutten 2010). 
§ 
Solution is not guaranteed, the solver will however continue and attempt to find 
better solutions indefinitely. This can be avoided by making the fitness clarification 
clearer. 
 
Benefits of evolutionary solvers: 
§ 
Flexible, can tackle a variety of problems 
§ 
Forgiving, the solver will happily calculate its way through a problem, which have 
been over- or under-constrained or with a poorly formulated fitness function. 
§ 
Progressive, while the solver is running it will continue to spit out solutions of higher 
and higher quality. This makes it possible to see the solver progress and even 
unfinished run will yield something that might be useable as a result. 
 
It seems that most problems can be solved using evolutionary solvers, but the process can 
be very time consuming. One way to reduce the run time is the make a more “direct” fitness 
function that feeds Galapagos with the final fitness value.  
2.3.3 Fitness in general 
When the solver runs it will always try to find the fittest genome as it moves its way through 
the generations. Finding the best solution, no matter if it is a minimum or maximum is 
sometimes easier than other times as it depends on the shape of the fitness landscape. On 
the following page, figures illustrating different fitness landscapes will be illustrated. The 
advantages and disadvantages of the fitness landscapes are like wise described in the 
following. 
 
The fitness landscape illustrated in Figure 2-8 is an example of a great fitness landscape, 
where the search for maxima will always to progressive from generation to generation. As 
mentioned we do not live in a perfect world and there might exist solutions, which are 
equally good as illustrated in Figure 2-9 where multiple maxima or minima exists.

Illustrated on Figure 2-10 the fitness landscape can contain both local maxima and of course 
the actual maxima. If the solution space, which contains the maxima, is narrow chances are 
that the perfect solution is never found. This can happen if none of the genomes from 
Generation 0 happens to be located on the slope towards the maxima. If all genomes are 
located on a slope that leads to a local maximum, genomes with a chance to “hit the jackpot” 
might be terminated as the solver progresses. Illustrated on Figure 2-11 is an even worse 
fitness landscape where the solution spaces are small and hard to find by the randomly 
generated population from generation one. Drawbacks on this type of fitness landscapes 
occur when one of the genomes hit the lower peak. The solver will then repopulate the area 
near the lower peak and the genomes near the high peak might go extinct. 
 
Even worse is the discontinuous fitness landscapes illustrated on Figure 2-12 since the 
solver never knows if the actual solution is found. Say that the randomly generated genomes 
are all located at the lowest point of the graph. The solver will then keep populating that area 
trying to find new ground. Occasionally one of the genomes might hit higher ground and 
==PAGE:48==
 
24 
populate the newly discovered area with its offspring. The process of finding higher ground 
can again occur for an indefinitely amount of time until a lucky genome again find its way to 
higher ground. Genomes in a discontinuous landscape have no idea about which way to 
move, and the progression is more or less based on luck and chances are that the perfect 
solution will never be found. 
 
A fitness landscape shaped as illustrated on Figure 2-13 will also cause trouble for the solver 
since the genomes offspring can both be very similar and very different. This makes it hard 
for the population to find “stable ground”. 
2.3.4 Fitness Function 
Illustrating the fitness function will be impossible with the amount of variables required for a 
project of this size where the fitness function will be generated on a series of input such as 
view, energy use, solar potential, and location.  
 
If possible the drawbacks mentioned above should be avoided, but will often be impossible 
to avoid in models with many variables. Ways to handle this is to use a large start population 
and using settings where the solver does not exclude possible solutions to fast.  
 
Discontinuity can be avoided by ensuring that the fitness value does not come in steps. 
Examples of this could be the number of oddly shaped objects in a box with a given volume 
or fitness value defined by True/False statements – returning only ones or zeros. 
2.3.5 Fitness pressure 
Besides avoiding discontinuity the fitness function can in most situations be guided further in 
the right direction using fitness pressure. Fitness pressure is a method used to allow the 
solver to distinguish between two or more seemingly identical solutions. 
 
Fitness pressure is easily explained using an example. Imagine a situation where the goal is 
to minimize and distribute the distance equally between an area with evoked nature and 
three houses. In one evolutionary generation the two following solutions F1 and F2 have 
been found. A fitness value of 300 for both solutions is found by adding the distances 
together.  
 
𝐹1 = 20𝑚+ 130𝑚+ 150𝑚= 300𝑚 
𝐹2 = 100𝑚+ 50𝑚+ 150𝑚= 300𝑚 
 
The fitness value for both solutions are the same, the distances however are not. It is clear 
that solution F2 is better as these values are better distributed and closer to the actual goal 
of equal distance for every house. The solver can however not draw the same conclusion as 
it only reads the fitness values and nothing more. As the fitness value is equal the solver 
cannot tell which direction to search for better solutions. In the following, one way to add 
fitness pressure can be found. Note that the same distances are used as previously. 
 
𝐹1 = 20𝑚−𝑎𝑣𝑔! + 130𝑚−𝑎𝑣𝑔! + 150𝑚−𝑎𝑣𝑔! = 7300𝑚! 
𝐹2 = 100𝑚−𝑎𝑣𝑔! + 50𝑚−𝑎𝑣𝑔! + 150𝑚−𝑎𝑣𝑔! = 5000𝑚! 
 
With the applied fitness pressure, the solver will now be able to distinguish between the 
different solutions as solution F2 has a lower fitness than F1.  
 
Fitness pressure can be applied to all fitness functions and sub fitness functions, which 
provide inputs to the final fitness value. One should however be careful not to apply 
pressures, which will drive the solution in the wrong direction or exclude alternative solutions.

2.4 Sustainability indicators 
In these days sustainability have great influence on urban planning both on a political level 
and seen from the planners perspective as most cities have initiated various green projects 
in the pursuit of more sustainable cities (Siemens Global 2014). One of the difficult parts of 
these initiatives is to determine whether these initiatives are leading the cities towards 
sustainability or not. Virginia W. Maclaren discusses this issue and points out that one of the 
barriers that needs be passed in order to accomplish, this is the lack of clearly articulated 
methods to report urban sustainability (Maclaren 2007). A way to generate this clarity is 
using clearly defined indicators based on measureable values in order to make comparisons 
possible. In general an indicator can be related to density, pollution, traffic, energy 
consumptions or health. Basically anything that contributes with information related to the 
overall goal, in this case sustainability. Looking upon urban sustainability reporting Maclaren 
found that almost anyone who has been working with the development of urban 
sustainability goals and indicators agrees that introducing environmental consideration into 
the policy debate is a necessity. She also points out that there is not any “best” definition of 
urban sustainability as cities are likely to develop differently depending on their current 
environmental, economic and social circumstances (Maclaren 2007). The indicators and 
work done by Maclaren are focused on a much broader perspective than the perspective 
used within this thesis. Within this thesis indicators will be created, based on seven quality 
criteria provided by the municipality as mentioned in the introduction of this thesis.   
 
The following quality criteria have been target for the creation of indicators and thus the 
overall optimization of the neighbourhood. 
2.4.1 Best possible view for everyone – Indicator 1 
The angle of view towards nature from each window is measured and returned in degrees. 
This indicator distinguishes between 3 grades of nature, as some views generally are valued 
higher than others. Further explanation of used method can be found on page 40. 
2.4.2 The neighbourhood must be sustainable – Indicator 2, 3 and 4 
Being a sustainable neighbourhood can involve endless amounts of initiatives, regulations, 
rules, restrictions etc. In the early design phases the question of location and form of the 
dwellings will be one of the largest focus points. Buildings can be sensitive to location and 
orientation, and proper knowledge is required in order to keep the building from overheating 
and minimize the energy consumption in order to fulfil the building regulations.  
 
The following measureable subjects have been used as sustainability indicators. 
2.4.2.1 
Overheating 
Number of hours above 26°C per anno is found for each dwelling. According to standards a 
maximum of 100 hours above 26°C and 25 hours above 27°C is recommended (Dansk 
Standard 1995). The amount of overheating can function as an indicator for several things. It 
relates to the overall comfort inside the dwelling, need of solar shading, location and size of 
window, ventilation, etc., which makes it a great indicator as it has multiple purposes. 
2.4.2.2 
Energy consumption 
Using both overheating and energy consumption as indicators cause some unintended 
overlaps as both indicators are influenced by some of the same parameters. Examples of 
overlapping parameters are window sizes, window orientations, solar shading systems, and 
ventilation systems. Looking at the energy consumption is nevertheless important as this 
indicator is based on the thermal properties, surface areas and materials. The energy 
consumption is also fairly easy to compare with the building regulations and other residential 
reference dwellings. The municipality and developers of Vinge hopes that the coming buyers 
will build according to the low energy standards for 2020, however the 2015 standards 
==PAGE:50==
 
26 
should as a minimum be fulfilled. The low energy class standards for 2020 require a 
maximum annual energy consumption of 20 kWh/m2 and the standards for 2015 require a 
maximum consumption of (30 + 1000/A) kWh/m2 where A is the heated floor area.  
 
The energy consumption for each dwelling within this thesis is found using Termite, a plugin 
for Rhino/Grasshopper. Additional information can be found on page 33. 
2.4.2.3 
Solar radiation 
Knowledge about solar radiation is primarily for use in the earliest design phases as 
information related to positioning of windows and sizes can be helpful for the architects. In 
modern buildings solar radiation is a helpful source of heating and can minimize the overall 
heating demand. Solar radiation can however easily be the reason for overheating and thus 
an increased cooling demand.  
 
Solar radiation can also be used to estimate potential daylight, placement of solar panels 
and utilizing thermal mass in buildings. 
 
Within this thesis the solar radiation during the heating season is used as an indicator of 
potential solar radiation for every dwelling in every given scenario. The solar radiation have 
been found through pre-simulated scenarios and afterwards simplified to obtain faster 
optimization time. Explanation and further information can be found on page 42. 
2.4.3 Direct connection between transport, dwelling and nature 
Easy transportation around the neighbourhood requires a street layout that is simple and 
direct. SLA architects have in their proposal for Deltakvarteret area R2 made a central street, 
which connects R2 with the adjacent areas V2 and R1 (Figure 1-9), furthermore a road leads 
traffic from the dense neighbourhood in B4, see Figure 1-12, where each area is illustrated. 
Around the border of the R2 pathways for walking are planned which is connected with all 
others areas in the districts, allowing fast and direct pedestrian connection. The connection 
between transport and dwelling can be secured by locating the parcels close to the street. 
Placing parcels next to the street gives surrounding space for nature in the neighbourhood. 
The guidelines to this quality criterion are hereby drawn. Measuring the distance from the 
parcel to the street centre will give an indication about the closeness and connection 
between transport and dwelling. Location of the house on the parcel is still optional for the 
owner. This measure will not be categorized as an indicator but used as a valuable sub-
fitness value since this quality criterion naturally will have to be respected in the generated 
solutions.

2.5 Adjustable parameters 
Looking at the indicators they all has some relations to the location and the orientation of the 
buildings. The amount of view and solar radiation are directly connected to the orientation 
and somewhat also the location. Small location movements will however not influence these 
indicators as much as the orientation. Indicators as overheating and energy consumption are 
partially connected to the orientation and location. There are however many other 
parameters such as materials, WWR (Window to Wall Ratio) and systems used for heating, 
cooling and ventilation. With that said, the orientation can have huge influence on the overall 
energy consumption and should always be considered. The closeness between dwelling and 
transportation are mostly related to the location and the distance from the road. The rotation 
does to some extend matter, as the road cannot zigzag in order to minimize the distance.

A Voronoi diagram is created using a predefined component found in Grasshopper. A 
Voronoi diagram is the partitioning of a planar surface with any given number of points into 
polygons such that every polygon only contains one point. Furthermore the line separating 
every point must be closer to the two separated points than any other point (Weisstein 2015). 
An example is illustrated in Figure 3-8. In our case the four loosely defined nodes and the 
Voronoi component will divide the perimeter into four polygons with different areas. By using 
Galapagos the areas are optimized and made equally large by adjusting the location of the 
four nodes. Once the areas are of the same size, the centre point of each polygon is stored 
and used as centre point for each row of dwellings. Using Voronoi diagrams with equally 
large polygons will in most cases ensure a proper starting point for the location of the 
buildings. If this method will work in other situation has not been tested, it was only 
concluded that it worked for this specific case.

3.4 View 
Nature plays a central role in Deltakvarteret with green areas, easy access to the delta and 
small creeks surrounding the area. In order to utilize the nature in the best possible way, 
location and orientation of the dwellings should be considered. The importance of the 
available view for each dwelling is emphasized in the quality program where the view 
composes one of the seven quality criteria for Deltakvarteret.  
 
The quality criteria states: Best view for all. This means that the average amount of view 
available from all dwellings should be as large as possible. Naturally all dwellings will not be 
able to obtain equal greatness in terms of view and some dwellings will have less and some 
more. In order to provide best possible conditions for everyone, the gap between the best 
and worst should be as small as possible. 
 
The amount of available view can be measured in degree and is typically called the angle of 
view. The angle of view can be measured in multiple ways as it depends on the location from 
where the “eye” is located, number of “eyes”, and its field of vision.  
 
Modelling wise, the “eye” measuring the angle of view can be constructed using different 
approaches. One method is to locate an “eye” in the centre of the building with a field of 
view on 360° (Figure 3-12). Locating the “eye” in the centre of the house does have several 
disadvantages as this approach and the measured angle of view depends on the window 
size and the distance between the “eye” and window. By comparing the angle of view in 
Figure 3-12 and Figure 3-13 it can be seen how the window size influence the field of view. 
 
Another approach is to create one “eye” per window. Again several options about the “eye” 
location should be considered. If the “eye” were located inside the house, similar to a real 
person, the field of view would again be dependant on the window size and distance from 
the window, see Figure 3-14 where the differences in field of view between views in the 
left/right and up/down direction in illustrated.

An alternative method is to locate the “eyes” on the centre of the window surface. One of the 
advantages of this method is that the field of view is completely independent and does not 
rely on the geometry of the house. The field of view would however be 180° wide and would 
make a typical building able to obtain a total angle of view of 720°, which does not really 
make sense and could cause confusion. In reality a person would also have to stick the head 
outside in order to obtain a 180° field of view.  
 
In order to make a realistic evaluation of the view, one “eye” have been located on the 
windowpane, with a field of view on 90°, 45° in each direction from a line perpendicular to 
the windowpane. This allows a typical house with four façades to obtain a maximum of 360° 
angle of view, which seems realistic. 
 
In Grasshopper the “eye” is made using an isovist. An isovist is defined as the volume of 
space, which is visible from a specific point. Isovists can be used both in 2D and 3D, but it is 
worth keeping in mind that 3D isovists is much more data heavy, which is why 2D isovists 
are used within the model. In Grasshopper isovists cannot be placed directly on the window 
==PAGE:65==

surface since the point cannot be placed on a line. This small issue is solved by moving the 
isovist away from the line and generating obstructing lines which limits the “eyes” to a field 
of view on exactly 90°.  
3.4.1 Modelling of the view 
The angle of view is the accumulated angles of view from all windows for each house. The 
way the isovist component work in Grasshopper is very simple. It takes three inputs, the 
point, the obstacles, and the radius, which is helpful to exclude eventual lines of sights that 
exceed a certain distance. Final input is the number of rays that the isovist component 
should send out in order to detect eventual obstructions. Once again a high number will be 
more data and more time consuming. A total of 30 points per isovist have been used 
throughout the optimization. This amount was found to be a good balance between 
accuracy and used computational time. 
 
In Grasshopper all the obstructions is defined such as dwellings, roads, nature etc. and 
connected to the isovist component. The isovist component then generates a list with all the 
intersections between the rays from the “eye” and the obstructions previously defined. Since 
the only thing of interest is the nature (delta, water, creeks etc.) the intersections between 
anything besides nature is subtracted from the list. The length of the generated list can be 
used to calculate the angle of view by adjusting for the number of initial rays produced by 
the isovist using the formula (1).

3.5 Solar Radiation 
Knowledge about Solar Radiation can aid designers and provide valuable information, which 
can be used to push the design in different directions. Decisions about window location, size, 
shading, solar panels, thermal mass and so forth are examples on topics where knowledge 
about solar radiation is beneficial if not required. Solar radiation on a specific surface, also 
know insolation (derived from Incident Solar Radiation), is a measure for the amount of the 
energy that hit the surface and it is not affected by any kind of surface properties such as 
roughness and reflection (Marsh 2011a). 
 
The total amount of radiant energy from the sun can be divided into direct and diffuse energy. 
The amount of direct solar energy from the sky on any given surface is highly dependent on 
the angle of incidence and location on earth.  
 
When solar radiation is to be estimated for a surface a sky subdivision is required. One of the 
popular methods is the Tregenza sky algorithm. This algorithm divides the sky into 145 
subdivisions as illustrated on Figure 3-17. The 145 equally large subdivisions are placed on 
eight altitude bands which allows each division to be considered as a point source without 
noticeable error (Hu et al. 2012).  
  
 
Figure 3-17: Simulating the sky using 145 evenly distributed virtual energy sources. Source: Hu et al. 2012. 
 
The work by Tregenza was adopted by the Commision Internationale de I’Enclairage (CIE), 
who recommended the use of this 145 segments subdivision (Marsh 2011b). Other methods 
to subdivide the sky are illustrated in Figure 3-18 and other alternative methods have among 
others been published by White et al in 1998, Tregenza in 1995 and Wenninger in 1999 
(Marsh 2005). The method developed by Tregenza seems to be the most successful and is 
amongst others used by Robinson & Stone who have done significant progress in terms of 
irradiation analysis in an urban scale.  
 
 
==PAGE:68==
 
44 
Figure 3-18: Examples of sky illustrations. Source: Marsh 2005. 
 
Work by Robinson & Stone (2004) have made solar radiation analysis simpler and quicker, 
than traditional analysis methods, with their development of the GenCumulativeSky module. 
The module is fast and produces results with an RMSE (root-mean-square-error) of less than 
2% (Robinson & Stone 2004). The module use EnergyPlus climate files and pre-processes 
the cumulative diffuse / luminance distribution to sky patches based on theory by Tregenza. 
 
One of the applications of the GenCumulativeSky is found in Ladybug, which uses the 
module to calculate the radiation from different sky patches, also based on the Tregenza sky 
dome. 
 
Despite the use of an already simplified method (GenCumulativeSky) to estimate the solar 
radiation, such an analysis can be relatively time consuming. The simulation time depends 
on the analysis area, number of analysis points and the number of point sources. A 
preliminary simulation on the case area showed computational time of more than one minute. 
Reducing the number of analysis points could decrease the computational time, but it would 
most likely cause the results to become incorrect and misleading. 
 
As the solver typically will have to run more than a 1000 solutions a computational time of 
several minutes would cause the solver to run for days before reaching a satisfying solution. 
In order to reduce the computational time for each simulation an alternative and simplified 
method has been created. 
3.5.1 Solar radiation – development of alternative method 
The Tregenza sky is as mentioned generated from 145 point sources each contributing with 
a potential solar radiation depending on its location on the sky, e.g. point sources radiating 
from south have more potential energy than a point source located in the northern part of the 
sky dome (Figure 3-17). If an object, e.g. a tree, house, or mountain, comes between the 
point source and the analysis point the amount of radiation accumulated in the analysis point 
will be reduced. This reduction will naturally continue the more point sources are obstructed.  
 
Ideally a simulation for every possible obstruction scenario created by the 145 point sources, 
should be carried out and saved as data and then imported as needed to the actual model. 
Next, each window in the model could be matched to a similar scenario and results could be 
extracted from the data generated by pre-simulation. This would be much faster as no actual 
simulations would be required between iterations performed by the solver. This idea does 
==PAGE:69==
 
45 
however have one disadvantage as the number of possible combinations for 145 unique 
point is an enormous number, far to large to be handled by standard computers. This means 
that the method should be simplified and the number of point sources reduced.

As a typical window is vertically positioned only half of the point sources can potentially hit

would take several months of simulations (on a personal computer), which was neither 
acceptable nor possible. Based on these estimations it was clear that the number of point 
sources should be narrowed down to approximately ten point sources, which would provide 
fast results and a manageable number of simulations. The simplification and further 
reduction of the point sources have been described below. 
 
Looking at the properties for windows and glazing in particular it became clear that some of 
the non-contributing point sources (orientation dependant) could be discontinued, narrowing 
the number of point sources down even further. Published back in 1991 in the ASHRAE 
transactions the relation between the reflectance of the glazing and angle of incidence is 
described, and it was found that the angle of incidence below 40° caused almost no 
reflectance. Between angles of incidence of 40° to 90° it can be seen in Figure 3-19 that the 
reflectance grow from near zero to one (Furler 1991).

incidence between 55-80°. It is fair to assume that point sources hitting the window with an 
angle of incidence above 55° will not contribute with any significant direct solar radiation. 
This assumption is made while assuming that modern reflective glazing is used in the new 
city development.

3.6 Daylight potential 
Daylight is typically an important design factor when buildings are designed due to comfort 
and the use of artificial lighting. Daylight also plays a significant role for the wellbeing of us 
humans who everyday spend most of our time inside buildings. Daylight in general fulfil two 
of the basic human needs, the ability to see the space around us and the ability to 
experience the outdoor environment (International Energy Agency 2000). The importance of 
daylight is discussed and documented countless of times, which can be found all around the 
Internet. In a Realdania founded book by Henning Larsen Architects (who is acknowledged 
for their work with daylight in buildings), the importance of daylight is connected with health, 
welfare, economy, architecture, and energy (Henning Larsen Architects 2012). 
 
A typical daylight analysis performed with Velux Visualizer, RADIANCE or DaySIM will 
typically be a rather time consuming affair, which would cause the evolutionary solver to run 
for weeks before reaching a useable result. Even though daylight simulations have not been 
implemented, an initial evaluation of the daylight potential can be performed. The amount of 
daylight in buildings is influenced by the amount of direct, diffuse and reflected light, which 
enter a building through a window or other transparent material. Both the choice of window 
type and the surrounding materials (indoor and outdoor) will influence the amount of daylight. 
Materials with high reflectance distribute light much better where dark and coarse materials 
typically will absorb much of the energy. Before daylight enters a room, a part of the desired 
daylight is reflected away by the window, which was briefly discussed in Section 3.5 about 
solar radiation (Figure 3-19). Since the scope of this study have been limited to the 
happenings outside the thermal envelope, only the daylight availability have been considered 
and consideration of materials and their various properties (orientation, size, inclination, 
reflection, transmission, and absorption) have been avoided. 
 
The availability of daylight and thus the daylight potential depends heavily on the 
surrounding elements. Obstructions such as adjacent buildings, carport, and large 
vegetation will all decrease the daylight potential on a façade. By detecting the surroundings 
of each window for nearby obstructions it will be possible to detect locations with low 
daylight availability. 
 
Termite already performs an operation where nearby obstructions are detected. By 
projecting rays from each windowpane towards each point source nearby obstructions can 
be found. A total of 73 rays are projected from each windowpane, which corresponds to one 
half of the Tregenza sky. By extracting the results from Termite and counting the number of 
obstructed rays is it possible to generates a value, which describe the daylight potential from 
each window and thus the average for each façade. The daylight potential is defined as the 
percentage of visible sky accessible from the windowpanes. The daylight potential for a 
façade is found by calculating the average accessibility from the windows.

3.7 Fitness function 
Definition of the fitness function is crucial as this formula solely is responsible for the input 
given to the solver as described in Section 2.3. The fitness function, which is solved for each 
iteration, returns only one value. This means that if more than one parameter should 
influence the calculated fitness value, these values would have to be integrated into the 
function. How this is done is very dependant on the problem and can be defined, as it is 
most fitting for the current project. 
 
In this specific case several parts of the model will contribute and influence the final fitness 
value. This includes all the indicators and other important values related to the constraints in 
Deltakvarteret. One way to define the fitness value is to normalize all the sub-fitness values 
and add them together. With this strategy all sub-fitness values are potentially weighted 
equally, yet not guaranteed.  
 
As an example, say two sub-fitness values are used, one for the angle of view and one for 
the energy consumption. At first, they would have to be normalized as the angle of view 
might be 75° and the energy consumption 13 kWh/m2 annually, which would cause the view 
to have a much higher influence. In order to normalize each sub-fitness value some 
boundaries needs to be defined. Such boundaries could be seen as in Table 8.

The overall goal is to maximize the angle of view. Next the boundary is defined. In this case a

second sub-fitness value to be found is the energy consumption. In this case one would

graded with zero. Based on the boundary conditions for the two mentioned sub-fitness

way, however in most situations the view will not exceed 90° which means that the score will 
be below 0.5 and thus the energy consumption will have greater influence on the total fitness 
value.

As mentioned the alternative method is developed to decrease the computational time it 
takes the solver to make one iteration. This involved simplification of the method and it was 
required to make assumptions along the way. It is within these assumptions that the 
differences in results are found and why the two methods did not match perfectly. One of the 
obvious reasons is found within elimination of all point sources outside the 55° viewport. 
Shading objects outside this viewport such as façade displacements can provide shade, 
which is not included in the alternative method. 
 
Another reason can be found within the simplification of the 28 remaining point sources 
within the 55° viewport. The point nodes inside the viewport are as mentioned divided into 
nine larger subdivisions with three to four points in each. If one of the point sources inside a 
segment is obstructed the alternative method will evaluate the entire segment as shaded, 
and no direct or diffuse sunlight will be obtained from that area of the sky. This means that 
the constructed alternative method will think that a larger part of the sky is obstructed even 
though only one third of the segment is actually obstructing. As a result of these findings the 
alternative method will, in some situations, find a lower solar radiation (as seen in Figure 4-1 
and Figure 4-2) than using traditional simulation through Ladybug.

4.2 Galapagos - solver settings 
The evolutionary solver used within this project is as mentioned the implemented plugin for 
Grasshopper named Galapagos. Before the solver is started the settings should be adjusted 
as these settings have great influence on the way that the solver progresses through the 
iterations. In the settings window in Galapagos it is important to adjust the population size, 
the initial boost and inbreed factor, as these are crucial for the behaviour of the solver. 
 
The most important settings are the population size, initial boost to the first population, the 
amount of genomes to carry on to the next generation, and most importantly the inbreed 
factor. 
 
The inbreed factor indicates where the solver should seek for better solutions while moving 
from generation to generation. The inbreed factor span from fully zoo-chilic to fully inbreed 
indicated as -100% to 100%. The two extremities will be explained in the following. If fully 
inbreed, genomes will be mated with other genomes located very close to each other in the 
fitness landscape. In other words the genomes will be very similar. If the inbreed factors is 
set to zoo-chilic the genomes will mate with other genomes located very far from each other, 
which means that the genomes will be very different from each other. A huge disadvantage 
of fully zoo-chilic is that the newly generated genome (offspring) is very likely to be located 
between two or more optima or minima, and thus a very poor fitness as result. The inbreed 
factors should on the other hand not be 100% inbreed as this increases the chance of the 
solver getting stuck in a local maxima/minima and not finding best possible solution. 
 
Various settings have been tested in order to see the effect and possible differences they 
might cause. The tested settings were chosen in an attempt to keep the population number 
as low as possible while still getting proper solutions. The reason why the population number 
is kept to a minimum is due the computational time of each iteration, which takes between 
60-210 seconds. In Table 12 the tests, with corresponding settings, are listed.

Looking at the growth (Figure 4-14, Figure 4-17, Figure 4-20, and Figure 4-23) of the fitness 
value for all tests it is clear that the solver is approaching a fixed solution as the average 
fitness value is growing. This happens as the solver discontinues the poor solutions. 
Common for all tests is the general behaviour of the development of solutions. For the first 
200 iterations (generation zero) the solver populates the fitness landscape with genomes 
seeking solutions randomly. It can be seen that most solutions have a fitness of five or below, 
with a few exceptions. In all tests solutions with a fitness value around five or ten seem to 
cluster up as the solver moves from generation to generation. This jump is perfectly 
explainable as the fitness value is awarded when all dwellings are located inside the 
perimeter. It is very likely that solutions with a fitness value around five have dwellings 
located outside the area perimeter.  
 
In Test 1 (Figure 4-14), where the population was 50 and inbreed factors was 60%, it can be 
seen that the solver approaches a solution faster than the other test cases. After 
approximately 600 iterations the solver seems to stagnate around a fitness value of ten. 
Through further iterations better solutions was found as it can be seen in Figure 4-15 where 
the ten best solutions were found after 700 iterations.  
 
In Test 2 and 3 (Figure 4-17 and Figure 4-20) the inbreed factors were reduced to 30% and 
10% respectively. As a result of this reduction the solver does not approach a steady state 
as fast as in Test 1 (Figure 4-14), indicating that the solver search other areas of the fitness 
landscape for better solutions. A reduction of the inbreed factor did not seem to find any 
remarkably better solutions within the conducted number of iterations. With that observation 
and the words of David Rutten from the Advanced Conceptual Design Workshop in London 
2010, a inbreed factor of 60% was decided. In the lecture he mentions that a good inbreed 
factor is found around 75% (AA School of Architecture 2010), but due to the number of 
parameters influencing the fitness value, a slightly lower inbreed factor was chosen. 
 
In order to avoid the solver getting stuck in a local optima the population size was doubled 
to 100. Using a larger population will allow the solver to populate a larger area of the fitness 
landscape and thus minimizing the risk of getting stuck in a local maximum.  
 
Within the result from Test 4, where the population was 100, initial boost two and an inbreed 
factor of 60%, the highest fitness value so far was found. Interesting enough this solution 
was already found in iteration 675 as it can be seen in Figure 4-24. 
 
Settings used in test 4 was chosen for further use, as these provided the best results along 
with a steady development. With only limited genomes in each generation, naturally all 
combinations cannot be tested, which would also make the solver useless. The question is 
whether the number of genomes in each generation is enough? By looking at Figure 4-26-
Figure 4-30, the attempted locations and rotations throughout all iterations are visualized.  
 
From the visualization of the locations it can be seen that the solver has covered almost the 
entire area, in the search for better solutions. It can likewise be seen in which area the solver 
has found better locations and that this area is tested in depth.  
 
Please note that if just one dwelling is located outside, the solution is likely to be 
discontinued. This means that even though the rest of the dwellings are placed perfectly, the 
total fitness value will not be as high as a solution where all 16 dwellings situated poorly 
inside the perimeter. This almost “perfect” solution can however be retrieved again as the 
solver progresses.

almost all orientations have been attempted. It can be seen the most common orientation for

The evolution of the indicators and the sub-fitness values is especially interesting in 
situations where detailed designs and decisions should be taken. One might wish to 
optimize further or adjust due to a slight priority chance. If the designers wish to find 
alternative solutions with for instance, less overheating, looking for solutions within the ten 
fittest solutions is a great way to gain inspiration and knowledge for further refinement.  
 
In Scenario 2-4 the difference between the top ten fitness values range from 0.04-0.12, 
which is far less than the results seen in Scenario 1 and 5 where the differences are 0.44 and 
0.48 respectively. Low differences are typically found in situations where the solver keeps 
searching the same area of the fitness landscape for solutions. In such a situation the 
possibility of finding better solutions is always present but the chances are decreasing as a 
function of time. It should be underlined that there always is a chance that the solver gets 
stuck in a local maxima and that better solutions actually exist.

6 // Discussion 
In urban planning the final design is often based on decisions that extend the visible 
elements. Parameters such as density and other co-related parameters are often connected 
with large-scale urban planning (Beirão et al. 2012) and will typically not be used in small-
scale urban planning projects where the executive guidelines are already fixed from 
preliminary design phases. Such a case is Deltakvarteret, where the functionality and density 
for the area is already defined by rules and geometry restrictions found in the quality 
program by SLA (Stig L. Andersson Landskabsarkitekter) and Rambøll (SLA 2014). In this 
study the development of a small neighbourhood was attempted using sustainability 
indicators coupled with parametric modelling. An evolutionary solver within a parametric 
model was used to find optimized solutions within five different scenarios where the 
influence of the large vegetation was investigated.  
 
The best-found solution from each scenario was compared with the initial design proposal 
for each scenario, which was done in order to test the use of parametric modelling within 
small-scale urban planning. The general comparisons showed that three out of four 
indicators had been improved. The indicator evaluating the energy consumption was 
lowered by 2.7%, the indicator measuring the solar radiation was increased by 0.7% and the 
indicator related to the general view was increased by 8.1% on average for all analysed 
scenarios. The final indicator, which evaluated the amount of overheating, did not improve 
and the yearly amount of hours above 26°C, were increased by 84.8%. In reality the 
increased amount of overheating is not as bad as one should think, as the hours of 
overheating did not exceed 2 hours in any of the analysed models.  
 
Energy consumption and hours of overheating was found using Termite, a plugin for 
Rhino/Grasshopper. Termite is a free-to-use software developed by PhD student Kristoffer 
Negendahl and is based on the Danish software Be10. The use of Termite was initially 
chosen because results are provided through formulas and no computational time 
consuming simulations were required. This was beneficial in the beginning as the model 
provided almost instant results. Termite did however cause some complications on the 
modelling, as Termite is designed for singular buildings and not 16, which was the case in 
this project. Coupling multiple buildings to Termite caused the program to stumble on the 
solutions resulting in lost solutions. It was found that the program takes input from the model, 
then sends it through the Be10 program, where all the results are found and saved to a *.xml 
file. Next Termite reads the results from the *.xml file (extensible markup language), which 
then can be displayed directly in Grasshopper. The problem was that the computer jumped 
from one building to the next before the results from the previous building was finished. This 
happens as the program opens a *.xml file to which the results are written. If the computer 
starts the next calculations before the *.xml file from the previous calculation is closed again, 
the program cannot write results from the current building to this file. This issue was solved 
by pausing the model for 0.6 seconds between every calculation, which allowed the 
computer to close the *.xml file before continuing. Furthermore Termite did not accept 
branched data trees (ways to organize data in Grasshopper). Ideally the dwellings would be 
located in one data tree with 16 branches, which would be connected directly to Termite. As 
this was not possible the only solution was to use one Termite model for each dwelling. This 
resulted in a very “heavy” model, but at least Termite behaved as intended.  
 
As mentioned Termite caused a computational “heavy” model, which made editing of the 
model difficult and increased the used computational time for each generated solution 
significantly. The computational time used to calculate each solution varied between 60 and 
210 seconds, depending on if the perimeter constraints were respected or not. The 
complication within Termite can be avoided by using an alternative as Archsim 
(http://archsim.com/). Archsim is a plugin that allows users to access fully featured 
EnergyPlus energy simulations inside Grasshopper. Archsim is designed for the early design 
phases and can be coupled with Galapagos, which makes it for a great alternative – unless 
simulations are too time consuming, which have not been tested.  
==PAGE:124==
 
100 
 
One of the chosen indicators within this study was the amount of solar radiation, which 
traditionally can be rather time consuming. In order to keep the computational time down, a 
simplified and alternative method for measuring solar radiation was created. In Grasshopper, 
Ladybug (an environmental analysis plugin) can be used to measure solar radiation within a 
reasonable timeframe. The method used in Ladybug is already a simplified method 
introduced back in 2004 (Robinson & Stone 2004). Despite using an already simplified 
method the process was still to time consuming, which was the reason for further 
simplification of the method. In the new simplified method the original Ladybug method have 
been simplified further through use of pre-simulated results stored in datasets. These results 
were imported into the model of Deltakvarteret and used for quick evaluation of the solar 
radiation in each scenario. The new method turned out to be approximately 25 times faster 
than the traditional radiation analysis in Ladybug. The method showed partly reliable results, 
with an accuracy of 12-31% RMSE (root-mean-standard-error). Despite the relatively high 
margin of error the method was evaluated acceptable for further use in this study.  
 
The method was to some extend successful, but further testing and optimization of the 
method should be conducted before any final conclusions about success or failure can be 
drawn. In general it is a question of accuracy versus time, where both often is desired. One 
obvious way to reduce the computational time would be to reduce the number of analysis 
points populated on each analysed surface. With the currently used analysis grid size a total 
of 2240 analysis points are used to analyse the 32 façades (70 points per façade). Reducing 
the number of analysis points to 128 (four points per façade) would certainly decrease the 
computational time and possibly generate close to instant results. Almost instant results 
would most likely eliminate the need for such a rough simplification as attempted in this 
study. The question is; would the accuracy be better or worse than the simplified method? Is 
there such a thing as a perfect grid size, where the relation between accuracy and time 
becomes even? Questions like these has unfortunately not been answered, as it was not 
within the scope of this study. At current stage the method is designed with a focus on solar 
radiation through glazing panes, which was the initial reason why only the sky patches within 
a 55° field of view (vertical and horizontal) was included. This assumption has naturally 
caused differences and is assumingly the main reason why differences of 12-31% RMSE 
was found. One could argue that all visible sky patches should be able to contribute to the 
radiation results, which would also allow this method to be used for analysis of thermal mass, 
PV location etc. Another advantage would be that the model would suddenly be able to 
detect nearby obstructions, such as the façade displacements seen in the row house 
neighbourhood in Deltakvarteret, which previously was not observed. One way to do so, and 
still keep the amount of sky segments low, would be to consider the remaining point sources 
as two segments, one for left side and one for the right. The amount of solar radiation from 
these segments could then be estimated through a True/False count from the list already 
generated as part of the Termite model – same list which is used to determine the potential 
daylight.  
 
The interconnectivity between elements such as the indicators, quality criteria and 
constraints inside the model all influence the final design, characterized by one value – the 
fitness. The evolutionary solver, Galapagos, was used to optimize the locations of the 
dwellings according to four indicators and geometrical constrains, and proved successful. 
The use of Galapagos and evolutionary solvers in general does however create two 
complications, which should be considered. First of all the use of Galapagos for multi-
objective problem solving involve some limitations in terms of solution comparisons since 
Galapagos only attempts to maximize/minimize the fitness value. It would be interesting to 
enable Galapagos to optimize two or more values, for instance the view and solar radiation 
and still consider all other aspects of the model. Octopus for Grasshopper is another 
evolutionary solver, which allows the user to search for best trade-offs between multiple 
goals (Vierlinger 2014). As mentioned Galapagos operates with one value and it is with this 
value that all the objectives should be considered. This was done using a fitness function, 
which favoured the view and location constraints over the remaining indicators and sub-
fitness values. Using a fitness function is actually a great and simple way to express the 
overall score of a solution, but should be handled with care. In this study the Street sub-
==PAGE:125==
 
101 
fitness related to the distance between dwelling and street turned out to have greater 
influence on the solutions than expected. This sub-fitness could have been defined 
differently, where near perfect conditions would yield the same result. A similar method was 
used for evaluation of the energy consumption, where all consumptions below 20 kWh/m2 
per year would yield the same result. This would allow the solver to find areas in the fitness 
landscape where the conditions were fulfilled and further optimization of the actual indicators 
could happen. The influence of the Street sub-fitness was quite significant and the analysis 
revealed that if input were excluded from the fitness function, Scenario 3 would actually be 
the scenario with third highest average fitness score instead of the fifth highest. 
 
The indicators, which have been used for optimization, were the amount of view, energy 
consumption, hours of overheating, and solar radiation on the southern and northern 
façades.  The indicators were based on quality criteria created to ensure a living, healthy and 
sustainable city district. In the results the ten best-found solutions for each scenario were 
presented. Besides the previously raised concerns about the Street sub-fitness it was found 
that the ten best performing solutions would not necessarily contain a spectrum of “perfect” 
solutions with various designs and alternative solutions. The truth is that the solutions often 
will look similar and typically become more identical as a function of time. This was 
especially clear in Scenario 2 to Scenario 4 where the ten best-found solutions had almost 
the same fitness value, location, and orientation. Both indicators and sub-fitness values 
(except the View indicator and the Street sub-fitness) were normalized on a scale from zero 
to one where one is was the best possible. Despite normalization it was discovered that the 
indicators did not have the same influence. Where the indicators of energy and partly 
overheating were evaluated with an indicator score between 0.9 and 1.0 in the majority of 
the solutions, the indicator for solar radiance was typically evaluated with an indicator score 
between 0.6 and 0.8. Even in Scenario 1, where almost no obstructions beside the buildings 
themselves was present a maximum indicator score around 0.85 was observed. One could 
argue that the indicator for solar radiance should have the same influence as energy and 
overheating, even though there are both pros and cons for why the indicators should be 
equally influential. Cons, the solar radiance will in most buildings influence the energy 
consumption, making it a sub element of the energy consumption indicator. Pros, energy 
consumption are influenced by much more than just the environmental aspects, which 
makes it possible to create a low consuming building by improving on systems, thermal 
elements etc. However, solar radiance is a necessity in future residential houses where the 
primary part of the consumed energy will be used for space heating.  
 
While looking at the dwellings individually it was found that the eight dwellings south of the 
street were the only dwellings suffering from overheating and that the amount of overheating 
dropped to almost zero in the scenarios with lots of obstructing vegetation. In terms of 
energy consumption the results vary a lot and only vague conclusions could be drawn. It 
was found that dwelling 10 and 11, located north of the road, were the dwellings with the 
highest energy consumption seen a cross all five scenarios. Same two dwellings 
experienced the highest solar radiation in Scenario 5, which could indicate that the higher 
energy consumption was caused by solar radiation. However, a high solar radiation does not 
equal higher energy consumptions in all situations. In Table 15 all scenarios and their overall 
results have been listed and it can be seen that in Scenario 1, where the highest average 
solar radiation was found, the lowest energy consumption was found as well. Apparently 
there are some collisions between these indicators, which possibly is explained by the 
remaining inputs used in Termite that have great influence on this indicator.

The differences between the optimized model and initial proposal were primarily found in 
improved energy consumption, increased accessibility to desired views, and an improved 
solar radiation in all scenarios except Scenario 5. The increased energy consumption can be 
a result of an optimized location, but it is more likely that the differences are the 
consequences of an increased exterior wall area. The initial proposal includes façade 
displacements on up to 6 metres in certain locations where the initial boundaries were 
limited to a maximum displacement of 2 metres. In terms of view it should be taken into 
considerations that the author of this study have defined the desired areas with great views 
where the delta and the evoked nature are located. It is possible that the perception of the 
location for these areas might differ between the author and designers behind the initial 
proposal of this particular neighbourhood. On the other hand the differences is quite 
remarkable, which indicates that better solutions in terms of view definitely can be generated 
using parametric optimization. 
 
As mentioned many aspects influence the final result, where the adjustable parameters 
among others influenced the solutions as values related to geometry, indicators and 
constraints was changed. Results were furthermore dependant on the non-adjustable 
elements such as the obstructing vegetation in each scenario. This vegetation turned out to 
have direct influence on most of the studied indicators. It was observed that the number of 
obstructing trees in the area could not necessarily be coupled to the results, as the location 
of the trees were important as well. This was well illustrated by the daylight potential where 
façades with nearby obstructing trees, suffered as the trees blocked large parts of the sky. 
Furthermore both scenarios with trees in the southern part experienced a clear drop in hours 
of overheating and amount of solar radiation. Count wise, Scenario 2 and Scenario 4 hold 
almost the same amount of trees, but location wise the two scenarios are very different. This 
does however not seem to affect the total solar radiation as much as one would think. 
Scenario 2 experienced slightly less solar radiation due to the southern location of 
obstructing vegetation. Comparing with Scenario 1, where almost no vegetation was present, 
a small increase of solar radiation and overheating was observed for this scenario.  
 
Energy wise the influence of large vegetation varied a lot between the results from the 
optimized model and the initial proposal. Where the energy consumption increased in 
Scenario 2 and 4 for the optimized models, it was reduced in the initial proposal within 
mentioned scenarios. Again the results related to the energy consumption is rather blurry 
and in this case contradictory, which naturally raises the question, is the energy 
consumption a valid indicator for a study like this?  
 
The energy consumption is one of the most important elements seen in a global perspective, 
where we as consumers of the earths limited resources need to reduce the emission of 
green house gasses, which have reached alarming heights. The energy consumption of 
suburban dwellings (no matter if the energy source is coal or renewable energy) is highly 
influenced by the geometry and the choice of materials used in each individual house, 
neither of which is changed remarkably in this study. Even with different locations, 
orientations, and levels of surrounding obstructing elements the energy consumption did not 
exceed 25 kWh/m2 pa. in any of the found solutions. This is naturally a good and valuable 
observation, which indicates great sustainability potential, but also indicates that the 
==PAGE:127==
 
103 
adjustable parameters within this study did not have any major influence on this indicator. 
Then again, Be10 is a very simple piece of software and results should nevertheless be used 
with caution. The use of view, overheating and solar radiation as indicators provided great 
knowledge about how orientation, location and obstructing vegetation can influence the 
results even though overheating was calculated with the same software as energy 
consumption a strong connection between this and location of obstructing elements were 
found. 
 
There is no doubt that the use of parametric approaches within urban planning holds great 
potential as it can be used for multiple purposes, documented by among others Wassin Jabi, 
Nikolai Steinø, José Beirão and many others. Parametric approaches in the building industry 
are typically found in two scales. Either in a city scale where cities and city districts are 
planned using solar analysis (Amado & Poggi 2014), GIS (Beirão et al. 2008; Sevtsuk 2014), 
indicators (Beirão et al. 2012) or even a combination of similar parameters. Another place 
where parametricism is typically seen is within the early design phases of a specific building 
(Banke 2013). In between these two scales the use of parametric optimization approaches 
seems limited as neither documentation nor projects with such use was found. 
 
It is not unlikely that the use of parametric approaches in this scale is limited by the required 
amounts of inputs and information, which typically will consist of multiple assumptions, just 
as seen within this study. However, neglecting the need for better understanding of all 
sustainability parameters in cities is not possible as such information is needed in the 
development of future smart cities and sustainable neighbourhoods. International Energy 
Agency have with a recently started project “Implementation of Energy Strategies in 
Communities” (IEA EBC Annex 63) acknowledged this need and discovered that energy 
optimization of communities requires much more than a simple up-scaling of the available 
tools currently used within the building industry (Strasser 2013).  
 
In this study similar observations were made, where “slow” processes, caused a heavy 
model, with computational time of approximately three minutes per iteration. Ultimately this 
resulted in premature solutions where the solver was stopped after 24-48 hours of runtime 
even though the used method for evaluating solar radiation was already made 25 times 
faster. Expecting instant results is naturally a long shot in an urban model that evaluates 
energy consumption, view, geometry, solar radiation and overheating while respecting 
geographical constraints from the construction site and road geometry. However, 
simplifications of the model should be made as two days of wait time, will probably be 
considered unacceptable in most architectural studios where one should be able to test 
design alterations and receive solutions without having to wait for half or whole days. 
 
Keeping in mind that all the used tools never had been operated before the beginning of this 
study, it is very likely that the used method could be improved significantly and maybe reveal 
that a CO2 neutral Copenhagen might be closer than previously anticipated.  
 
Ideas for further development and simplifications of the model developed within this project 
can be found right after the conclusion.

7 // Conclusion 
This study investigated the design implication large vegetation would cause to a small-scale 
urban city development, as such considerations typically will not be issued by the designers 
before the early building design phases begins. A small neighbourhood consisting of row 
house dwellings in the future city of Vinge comprised the boundaries as case study for this 
project. Five different scenarios were created, in which the amount and location of 
obstructing vegetation varied. Through parametric modelling, optimized solutions for every 
scenario were found using an evolutionary solver and four sustainability indicators. The 
found solutions were compared with the initial design proposal for the area and individually. 
The results document the efficiency and potential of parametric modelling for small-scale 
urban developments as optimized solutions with lower energy consumption, improved solar 
radiation and increased quality of view was generated. Using obstructing vegetation as a 
design parameter clearly influenced the generated solutions and direct connections between 
dwelling locations, hours of overheating, daylight potential, and conditions for solar exposure 
was found. Especially obstructing vegetation located south of the analysed dwellings should 
be considered as these caused the largest impact on the results.  
 
The use of parametric optimization models for urban developments is however not without 
complications as multi-objective problem solving can be very time-consuming and requires a 
great deal of detailed knowledge about elements in all scales. A holistic approach is typically 
a necessity for sustainable city developments and sustainability indicators can help clarify 
the uncertainties often occurring in holistic design processes. Using indicators to generate 
optimized urban designs can be a powerful approach, but the indicators should be chosen 
carefully as many aspects of city development is interconnected and some requires 
unobtainable information, which would trigger unreliable solutions.  
 
Parametric approaches hold great potential in the quest for more sustainable city districts. In 
this study the energy consumption was reduced by 2.7% on average. With further model 
development and methodology improvements even greater reductions and improvement 
could possibly be achieved by optimizing other neighbourhoods in Vinge, Denmark or 
anywhere else.

7.1 Future work 
Several aspects of the conducted study holds potential for further development and research, 
where especially the presented model, parametric urbanism, urban solar analysis and the 
relation between energy consumption and communities are topics of particular interest.  
 
The use of parametric modelling optimized energy consumption, view and solar radiation on 
a small-scale urban development. This was achieved despite author being a first time user of 
Rhino/Grasshopper and parametric approaches in general. It was discovered that the 
generation of optimized solution could be rather time consuming due to the inclusion of 
multiple aspects such as indicators and various constraints. Instead of including all sorts of 
indicators in the solver, a few crucial indicators should be found. A study where these crucial 
indicators were found and investigated should be carried out. The found indicators should 
contain influencing information that could be used within different planning departments and 
useable for design at various scales. Furthermore the indicators should be easy to obtain 
and shouldn’t require computational heavy processes. 
 
Computational lightweight processes are the keywords for the research potential for urban 
solar analysis. The potential and demand for further simplification of methods used for solar 
radiation have already been briefly discussed, but other approaches for simplification also 
exist. A yet untested approach is to test if methods using agent based modelling could prove 
beneficial. Say for instance – instead of searching all 145 source points in the Tregenza sky, 
agents could be sent to a few relevant locations. If no obstructions were found in this point, 
the surrounding area (neighbouring source points) could be considered unobstructed and a 
solar radiation value from this part of the sky could be estimated. If the an obstruction was 
found, this would be reported back to the model and new agents would be sent to 
investigate the surrounding area to test which of the source points is contributing and which 
is not. The mentioned idea is currently nothing more than just an idea and further research 
testing by future MSc and PhD students would be required. 
 
The knowledge about energy consumption in urban planning is useful, but at the same time 
this measure is heavily influenced on parameters that typically isn’t relevant in urban 
planning strategies. Termite, which was used within this study, is a young promising tool 
with great potential. It does however require further development in order to handle urban 
scenarios where up to possibly 100 buildings should be investigated. Coding the entire Be10 
core into a Grasshopper component would eliminate the read/write issue previously found. 
Proper coding would even allow Termite to work with Grasshopper data trees and thus 
multiple dwellings at once.  
 
In regards to the model presented in this study a few things could have been done differently 
in order to accelerate the speed of which the solver would find optimized solutions. The 
number of parameters the solver has to adjust will naturally increase the number of possible 
combinations. Removing the façade displacements as a parameter (it could be partially 
random instead) would decrease the number of adjustable parameters from 24 to 12, which 
would reduce the number of combinations significantly.  
 
Another interesting aspect would be to analyse the view in 3D, this would make it possible to 
distinguish between view from ground floor and first floor. In areas with high density it is 
likely that view towards green areas are only available from the top floors and not from the 
ground level. Furthermore it would make modelling of the terrain possible, which would 
prove beneficial for planning in hilly landscapes.
