Abstract
Most people spend nearly all waking hours indoors and spend approximately one­third
of their lives asleep [1]. Nevertheless, little is known about how real­world light exposure
and bedroom climate combined to influence sleep, especially across a country like Norway
with an extensive latitudinal span. This study, therefore, followed ten healthy adults, five
residing in Tromsø (69°N) and five in Tønsberg/Kristiansand (58°N), for four consecutive
weeks from mid­March to mid­April 2025. Wearable LYS sensors recorded individual
light exposure, Fitbit Inspire 3 devices logged sleep quality and architecture, and bedside
loggers measured nocturnal temperature, relative humidity, illuminance, and CO2.
Questionnaires assessed subjective sleep quality, ventilation habits, and perceived bed­
room comfort. Results revealed consistent regional differences in both light exposure and
indoor climate: Southern participants received twice as much daytime melanopic light
and ventilated bedrooms more frequently, resulting in cooler, more humid, and better­
ventilated environments. Northern bedrooms were warmer and more thermally stable,
but often showed CO2 accumulation overnight.
Sleep outcomes, however, were only modestly affected by these environmental differ­
ences. Warmer bedrooms were associated with longer sleep duration, whereas daytime
light exposure showed only a weak positive link to REM sleep. Subjective and objective
sleep assessments diverged in several cases, highlighting the value of combining both
methods.
Overall, the findings suggested that latitude influenced sleep mainly through behaviour
and environmental conditions rather than directly. Although the small sample size and
estimation uncertainties limited generalisability, the study demonstrated the feasibility of
multimodal monitoring in real­world settings and identified temperature as a stronger sleep
correlate than light in early spring.

Resumé
De fleste mennesker tilbringer næsten alle deres vågne timer indendørs og sover omtrent
en tredjedel af livet[1]. Alligevel vides der kun lidt om, hvordan lyseksponering i virkelighe­
den og klimaet i soveværelset tilsammen påvirker søvnen, især i et land som Norge med
stor geografisk breddeudstrækning. Dette studie fulgte derfor ti raske voksne, hvoraf fem
boede i Tromsø (69°N) og fem i Tønsberg/Kristiansand (58°N), i fire på hinanden følgende
uger fra midten af marts til midten af april 2025. Bærbare LYS­sensorer registrerede in­
dividuel lyseksponering, Fitbit Inspire 3 enheder loggede søvnkvalitet og ­arkitektur, og
sensorer ved sengen målte natlig temperatur, relativ luftfugtighed, belysningsstyrke og
CO2.
Spørgeskemaer vurderede subjektiv søvnkvalitet, ventilationsvaner og oplevet komfort i
soveværelset. Resultaterne viste gennemgående regionale forskelle i både lyseksponer­
ing og indeklima: Deltagerne i syd modtog dobbelt så meget melanopisk dagslys og luft­
ede soveværelserne oftere, hvilket resulterede i køligere, mere fugtige og bedre ventil­
erede miljøer. Soveværelserne i nord var varmere og mere termisk stabile, men viste ofte
CO2 ophobning i løbet af natten.
Søvnudfald var dog kun beskedent påvirket af disse miljøforskelle. Varmere soveværelser
var forbundet med længere søvnlængde, mens dagslyseksponering kun havde en svag
positiv sammenhæng med REM­søvn. Subjektive og objektive søvnvurderinger afveg i
flere tilfælde, hvilket understreger værdien af at kombinere begge metoder.
Overordnet tydede fundene på, at breddegrad påvirkede søvnen primært gennem adfærd
og miljøforhold frem for direkte. Selvom den lille stikprøve og usikkerheder i estimaterne
begrænsede generaliserbarheden, demonstrerede studiet gennemførligheden af multi­
modal monitorering under virkelige forhold og identificerede temperatur som en stærkere
søvnkorrelat end lys i det tidlige forår.

Introduction
1.1
Background & Motivation
Sleep is a fundamental physiological process that is essential for cognitive performance,
emotional regulation, and somatic restoration [2]. Light is the principal zeitgeber for the
human circadian system, the spectral composition, intensity, and timing of light exposure
synchronise the suprachiasmatic nuclei and thereby modulate melatonin secretion, alert­
ness, and sleep propensity [3]. Contemporary indoor lifestyles mean that people spend
about 90% of their waking hours under electric lighting. The spectral distribution of this
light, and its limited diurnal variation, differs markedly from natural daylight [4]. In par­
allel, indoor climatic variables, including temperature, humidity, and ventilation, fluctuate
across dwellings and seasons, each of which has been associated with sleep continuity
and next­day performance [5].
Although laboratory experiments have precisely isolated the effects of either light [6] or
thermal and air­quality factors [7] on sleep, individuals in everyday environments en­
counter a combination of stimuli whose joint influence remains inadequately characterised [8].
Field investigations that concurrently monitor personal light exposure and bedroom cli­
mate are scarce [9], and even fewer compare settings that differ markedly in photo pe­
riod [10].
This knowledge gap is particularly pertinent to Norway, where pronounced
latitudinal variation in the photopic environment, may interact with indoor conditions to
modulate sleep [10].
This study aimed to contribute to this area by combining wearable sensors, in­room mon­
itoring, and participant­reported outcomes to examine how light exposure and bedroom
climate jointly relate to sleep during early spring in Northern (69° N) and Southern (58° N)
Norway.
1.2
Objectives and research questions
The main objective was to examine how real­world variations in light exposure and bed­
room environment were associated with both objective and subjective sleep quality, and
whether these relationships differed with latitude.
To achieve this aim, the study ad­
dressed the following research questions:
RQ1 What are the typical 24 h profiles of photopic and melanopic light exposure for resi­
dents in Northern versus Southern Norway during early spring?

Background
This chapter introduces the theoretical foundations for how light and indoor climate influ­
ence human sleep and circadian timing.
2.1
Sleep and circadian regulation
Sleep occupies nearly one–third of the human lifespan and is essential for memory con­
solidation, immune defence, metabolic regulation, and tissue repair [2]. Deep sleep en­
hances glymphatic clearance of neuro­toxic waste [11], whereas REM sleep supports
emotional processing [2]. In healthy adults, 7–9 hours of consolidated sleep per night
is recommended, typically distributed across four to six cycles of non­REM and REM
stages[1].
The sleep–wake rhythm is governed by the circadian clock, the internal biological clock
with a period of 24 hours. The master pacemaker resides in the suprachiasmatic nu­
cleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus and is synchronised to the solar day by external signals,
the most potent of which is light [12]. Photons are detected by rods and cones, as well
as by intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs). The ipRGCs express
melanopsin, which has a peak sensitivity near 480 nm, and they send direct input to the
suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) via the retinohypothalamic tract. Non­photic cues such as
physical activity and meal timing also modulate the SCN, but their phase­shifting capacity
is modest compared with that of light [3]. Figure 2.1 summarizes the core components.

2.2
Light as a circadian zeitgeber
Light acts through five circadian­relevant parameters, spectrum, intensity, timing, dura­
tion, and distribution [13]. Short­wavelength (460 nm to 480 nm) light is most effective for
suppressing nocturnal melatonin and shifting the clock [14]. Conversely, long­wavelength
(>600 nm) light has less melanopic potency and can be used in the evening without de­
laying sleep [14].

Natural daylight (400–700 nm) contains a broad spectrum of wavelengths that reinforce
circadian entrainment by delivering high­intensity, blue­enriched irradiance light during
daytime hours[15]. Morning light exposure advances the circadian clock and enhances
alertness, while consistent daytime exposure improves the amplitude and stability of cir­
cadian rhythms, reducing vulnerability to disruptive artificial light in the evening [16, 15].
Typical indoor lighting, by contrast, delivers less melanopic stimulation, especially in the
vertical plane at eye level. The mismatch can weaken entrainment and heighten sensi­
tivity to disruptive evening light[15].

Daylight Efficacy Ratio (M – DER). These metrics are all designed to quantify how effec­

The spatial distribution of light also differs in circadian impact. It is greatest when illu­
mination reaches the vertical plane at eye level, making light sources positioned in front
of the observer (e.g., windows or wall­mounted fixtures) more effective than overhead or
backlighting [17, 15]. Residential bedrooms are usually lit by low­intensity interior sources
(e.g., bedside lamps or ceiling fixtures), giving eye­level photopic illuminances around 10–
50 lx; however, in urban settings, obtrusive ‘light­trespass’ from street­lighting can raise
background levels well above this range [9, 24, 25].
2.3
Indoor climate as a sleep modulator
While light sets the circadian phase, the indoor environmental quality (IEQ) in the bed­
room, comprising temperature, air quality, humidity and noise, shapes the micro­environment
in which sleep unfolds [26, 27]. These factors modulate both physiological and psycho­
logical dimensions of sleep in subtle yet significant ways [26].
2.3.1
Thermal environment
Sleep onset is facilitated by a drop of approximately 1°C in core body temperature [28].
Most health authorities recommend bedroom air temperatures between 18–20°C for adults,
and surveys show a common preference for slightly cooler conditions [29, 30, 31]. How­
ever, real­world field studies consistently report poorer sleep when operative temperature
falls below 18°C or rises above 21°C [9, 7]. Mechanistically, low ambient temperature in­
creases vasoconstriction and impairs slow­wave onset, while moderate warmth (around
19°C) stabilizes the skin temperature gradient and supports consolidated sleep [5]. This
justifies treating 18–21°C as a target band in analysis and design [30, 31].
2.3.2
Air quality
Elevated indoor CO2 levels, typically a proxy for poor ventilation and bioeffluent build­up,
have been associated with fragmented sleep and impaired cognitive function the next day
[32, 33]. In residential bedrooms, CO2 frequently exceeds recommended thresholds, par­
ticularly when windows remain closed overnight [9, 29]. ASHRAE suggests maintaining
indoor CO2 below 1000 ppm, while levels above 1500 ppm may negatively affect sleep
[29, 31].
2.3.3
Relative humidity
Humidity modulates thermal perception and respiratory comfort during sleep. High relative
humidity (>60%), especially in warm rooms, impairs evaporative cooling and increases
restlessness [34, 27]. Conversely, low humidity (<40%) may cause mucosal dryness or
irritation, but evidence on its impact on sleep architecture remains inconclusive [34]. De­
spite this, a relative humidity range of 40–60% is widely considered optimal for temperate
climates, balancing comfort, allergen control and sleep stability [29].
2.3.4
Acoustic environment
The nocturnal sound environment plays a key role in sleep maintenance. Both continuous
background noise (e.g., traffic, ventilation) and transient sound events (e.g., neighbours,
slamming doors) can cause cortical arousals and reduce sleep efficiency [9, 35]. Ex­
posure to noise levels above 35 dB during sleep is known to increase awakenings and
disturb sleep stages, especially light and REM sleep [36, 37]. As natural ventilation often
compromises acoustic isolation, integrated solutions should balance airflow with sound
insulation [31].

Integrated biological pathways
Environmental factors converge on shared neuroen­
docrine and autonomic routes. Bright, short­wavelength light late in the evening sup­
presses melatonin and delays circadian phase [38], whereas elevated bedroom temper­
ature or CO2 raises core temperature, heightens sympathetic activity, and disrupts slow­
wave sleep [5, 32]. In contrast, a cool, well­ventilated room combined with very low light
supports melatonin secretion and deeper, more stable sleep [28, 15]. Approaching dawn,
a gradual rise in melanopic illuminance together with slightly warmer, fresher air stimu­
lates cortisol and serotonin release, enhancing morning alertness and mood [39].
2.4
Seasonal and geographic context
The influence of indoor conditions and biological rhythms cannot be fully understood with­
out accounting for the geographical and contextual settings that shape daily exposure
patterns and behaviours.
Latitude dictates how much daylight and darkness people receive across the year. Near
the Arctic Circle (66°33 N), day length swings from almost 24 h in midsummer to only a
few hours in mid­winter, whereas Oslo (59–60° N) experiences a milder 18 h / 6 h range,
and equatorial regions remain virtually constant [40, 41]. At high latitudes, the midnight
sun can shift the body clock earlier in the morning, but the drawn­out twilight and late
sunsets often delay bedtime as well [42]. Long winter darkness lets melatonin rise earlier
in the afternoon and for longer, which can slow falling asleep and raise insomnia risk in
some groups, yet people also tend to sleep a little longer overall [43, 10].
In Tromsø (69.7° N) the summer “midnight sun” removes dusk completely and pushes
the circadian phase forward and the winter “polar night” nudges it back six months later
[40]. When this rapid spring change coincides with the start of Daylight Saving Time,
the extra evening light can delay melatonin and fragment sleep for several weeks [44].
Further south, the same clock change has only brief effects because the daylight shift is
less extreme [44].
Local context matters, too. Urban areas add light pollution and poorer air quality, eroding
circadian contrast, whereas rural settings offer darker nights and cleaner air, linked to
deeper sleep, better mood and stronger alignment with solar time [25]. Limited daylight
and scarce restorative natural settings also raise vulnerability to mood disturbances [45].
The prevalence of Seasonal Affective Disorder, a depressive subtype that peaks in au­
tumn and winter and remits in spring, increases with latitude [46]. How well these needs
are met depends on how the built environment is shaped.
2.5
Architectural practices & building design
Walls, windows, and ceilings shape not just space, but also the conditions under which the
body rests and recovers. The way buildings are designed determines how we encounter
light, breathe air, and experience thermal comfort, especially during the most vulnerable
hours of sleep.
2.5.1
Daylight access & façade design
In regions with limited daylight during the winter months, modern buildings are often de­
signed to maximize natural light. This includes large south­facing windows, open floor
plans, and bright interior surfaces that reflect light. However, these strategies must be
balanced with thermal performance and glare control, especially in high­latitude regions
where the sun stays low in the sky [47, 48].

According to the Norwegian building regulations (TEK17), sufficient daylight is considered
achieved when the daylight factor is at least 2% in minimum half of the room’s floor area.
In practice, this is often approximated by ensuring that the window area corresponds to
at least 10% of the floor area [49]. This window­to­floor ratio aims to provide adequate
natural illumination while taking into account energy efficiency and thermal comfort.
2.5.2
Thermal Comfort and Insulation Strategies
Indoor temperature regulation is closely linked to sleep quality [50]. In colder climates,
such as Norway, buildings typically rely on high­performance insulation, airtight construc­
tion, and mechanical ventilation to maintain stable indoor temperatures [48]. Concepts
like Passive House design and low­energy standards are widespread in the Nordic region
[48]. In warmer climates, by contrast, architectural solutions often emphasize natural ven­
tilation, shaded facades, and cross­breezes to prevent overheating [48].
2.5.3
Ventilation & IAQ
Healthy sleep also depends on air quality, which is directly influenced by how a build­
ing is ventilated. Design choices such as whether to include mechanical ventilation with
heat recovery, operable windows, and the placement of air inlets and outlets all affect in­
door air quality. While building regulations in many countries now emphasize ventilation
performance, actual implementation still varies by building age and local practices [51].
It is recommended to supply at least 7–8.5 L/s of fresh air per person (approximately 0.35–
0.5 Air Changes per Hour) to maintain CO2 levels below 800–1000 ppm [31]. Relative
humidity should be kept between 30% and 60%, and mechanical ventilation with heat
recovery combined should be included with operable windows where feasible [52].
2.5.4
Acoustic environment
Equally important is the acoustic environment. To safeguard restorative sleep, design
and assessment frameworks increasingly specify indoor noise limits. For example, the
WELL Building Standard requires that average background noise in bedrooms, measured
over at least 12 hours, including from 22:00 to 07:00, does not exceed 35 dB (A) Leq
[53]. Acoustic insulation, mechanical system noise control, and strategic room layout are
therefore essential elements of healthy bedroom design.
Air­ and impact­sound insulation between flats should reach at least DnT,w 45 dB to satisfy
ISO 16283­1 and limit neighbour noise [54]. Façades and windows facing traffic need
double­ or triple­glazed units with laminated panes, giving Rw ≈30–35 dB [37]. Tight
sealing around frames and service penetrations is essential for these ratings to hold.
2.5.5
Architectural implications for circadian health
Elements such as building orientation, room layout, and surface materials all contribute
to how much daylight is received during waking hours and how well artificial light can
be controlled in the evening. These architectural choices have biological implications,
highlighting the importance of interdisciplinary thinking that brings together architecture,
building physics, and chronobiology [47].
For instance, Figueiro et al. (2017) demonstrated that increasing morning exposure to
circadian­effective light in office workers led to earlier circadian timing, improved sleep
quality, and reduced sleep onset latency. Although the study used controlled lighting
interventions rather than architectural modifications, the results highlight how increased
morning light exposure, achievable through façade design or daylight optimization, can
support circadian alignment in built environments [39].

2.6
Measuring Light, Environment and Sleep
A range of studies emphasize the importance of capturing both environmental exposures
and sleep outcomes through systematic measurements [26, 15, 7]. In research on sleep,
circadian biology, and indoor environmental quality, it is common to use a combination of
objective and subjective methods.
Objective approaches, such as measuring temperature, CO2 levels, light exposure, and
sleep patterns using wearable or environmental sensors, provide continuous and unbi­
ased data on both environmental conditions and physiological responses [16, 55]. Sub­
jective methods, such as questionnaires, sleep diaries, or comfort ratings, offer insight
into how individuals perceive and interpret their sleep and surroundings [56, 57]. These
tools typically assess perceived sleep quality, including aspects such as sleep latency,
sleep depth, nighttime awakenings, and daytime functioning [56].
Because sleep is influenced by multiple interacting factors, combining these two types
of data is considered best practice in field­based research. Objective data can reveal
underlying patterns or disruptions that participants themselves may not perceive, while
subjective data adds contextual richness and individual experience. This mixed­method
approach is increasingly common in real­world studies exploring how environmental ex­
posures affect human sleep and well­being [26, 58].
Building on this rationale, the following chapter outlines how these principles were applied
in the design and execution of the present study.

Study Design
Objective and subjective data were collected over a four­week period (mid­March to mid­

sleep and light exposure were captured through weekly questionnaires.

quality was examined. Two sites with contrasting springtime daylight profiles were se­
lected. Tromsø (above the Arctic Circle, 69° 40′ N) in the north, and two southern loca­
tions, Tønsberg and Kristiansand (≈59° N). Figure 3.1 illustrates the geographic distribu­

3.3.4
Sleep Data (Fitbit)
Sleep metrics were measured using Fitbit Inspire 3 devices that were worn nightly. The
devices recorded total sleep time, sleep quality, sleep­stage estimates (light, deep, REM),
resting heart rate, and skin temperature.
During sleep, the watch tracked beat­to­beat changes in heart rate, referred to as heart­
rate variability (HRV), which varied across different sleep stages. These physiological
signals were used to estimate transitions between light, deep, and REM sleep. Each
morning, sleep­stage estimates were generated from the previous night’s heart­rate and
movement data [65].
Skin temperature was estimated by built­in sensors that tracked changes at the skin sur­
face during sleep. Because skin temperature fluctuates more than core body tempera­
ture, it reflects thermoregulatory processes [66]. While the device was worn at night, it
measured skin temperature every minute and used these readings to calculate nightly
deviations from the participant’s personal baseline.

Statistical Models and Correlation Analyses
All analyses were conducted in Python. Basic descriptive statistics were calculated to
show average values and variability. Because the sample was small and most of the data
did not follow a normal distribution, non­parametric methods were applied to avoid strict
distributional assumptions.

The primary statistical tool was Spearman’s rank correlation (ρs), used to assess mono­
tonic relationships between variables. To further control for confounding effects, partial
Spearman correlations were computed for selected variable pairs. A significance thresh­

lationship between two variables of interest, partial Spearman correlations were therefore
calculated for the parameter pairs of interest. Each variable was first regressed on the
control covariate(s), and the ranked residuals were subsequently correlated, [80, 79, 78]:

3.5.3
OLS Regression
To quantify the relative influence of multiple environmental variables on sleep, ordinary
least squares (OLS) regression was applied. This method models the linear relationship
between a dependent variable and one or more independent predictors. The model esti­
mates a set of coefficients that best fit the observed data by minimizing the sum of squared
residuals between predicted and actual values.
OLS regression allows for the simultaneous inclusion of multiple predictors, enabling as­
sessment of their contributions while controlling for potential con­founders [81].

mEDI = lux × DER
(3.3)

Summary of Main Observations
Subjective Sleep Patterns
Southern participants consistently reported better perceived
sleep quality than those in the North. Weekly GSQS scores were lower (indicating bet­
ter sleep) in the South throughout the study, with a slight improvement over time in both
regions. A temporary dip in the North coincided with the daylight saving transition. Re­
ported sleep disturbances were mainly related to stress/illness and evening light expo­
sure. Evening screen use was only reported in the North, while moderate evening light
was more frequent in the South. Environmental complaints (e.g., noise or temperature)
were rare and only appeared in Northern bedrooms.
By week 16, most participants felt their sleep had improved or remained stable, though
the North showed more variability week to week. PSQI scores collected post­study con­
firmed overall poor sleep quality in 8 out of 10 participants. Scores were slightly worse in
the North (6.6 vs. 6.0), with the most common issues being difficulty falling asleep and
frequent awakenings. Despite some discrepancies with objective data, Southern partici­
pants consistently reported better sleep than those in the North.

5
Discussion
This chapter discusses how the measured light exposure and bedroom environment in­
fluenced sleep in Northern and Southern Norway. The findings are interpreted in relation
to the study’s four research questions and relevant literature.
5.1
RQ1: Light exposure profiles showed clear regional and
individual differences
The study revealed considerable variability in personal light exposure, both between indi­
viduals and across regions. Southern participants generally experienced higher melanopic
and photopic illuminance than their Northern counterparts, but individual behaviour may
have played a big role across the entire cohort.
This finding aligns with previous work suggesting that latitude alone is an insufficient pre­
dictor of circadian­effective light exposure in modern societies, where indoor lifestyles
and building design largely determine light access [17, 82]. For example, while the photo­
period was similar across regions in early spring the Northern region experienced about 2
hours more of daylight at the end of the four weeks, yet the Southern participants recorded
double the amount of Illuminance. Therefore, time spent outdoors, and personal routines
may have created divergent exposure patterns. Such behavioural and environmental me­
diation has been noted in other field studies [89, 45], highlighting the importance of context
over geography.
Importantly, no participants reached recommended thresholds for morning mEDI expo­
sure (≥250 lux for 4 hours) known to support circadian entrainment [82, 20]. This rein­
forces concerns that modern light environments may inadequately support healthy circa­
dian rhythms.
The implications are twofold: First, interventions aimed at improving circadian alignment
may need to focus more on behavioural change (e.g., morning outdoor time) and ar­
chitectural features (e.g., daylight access) than on latitude per se. Second, the large
inter­individual variation suggests that standard lighting recommendations may need per­
sonalization based on lifestyle and exposure patterns.
Future studies should consider combining wearable light sensors with detailed behavioural
diaries to disentangle the relative contributions of built, social, and climatic factors on cir­
cadian light exposure.
5.2
RQ2: Light–sleep associations were modest and most
evident for REM sleep
Associations between daytime light exposure and nocturnal sleep outcomes were gener­
ally modest in this dataset. No significant correlations emerged between mean daytime
mEDI and total sleep time, sleep score, or time spent in deep or light sleep. However, a
weak positive association was observed between mean daytime mEDI and REM sleep du­
ration (Spearman ρ ≈0.25–0.26), suggesting a potential link between circadian­effective
light and sleep architecture. Although the effect size was small and the data highly vari­
able, this pattern aligns with established circadian mechanisms, where higher daytime
melanopic exposure promotes REM consolidation through better circadian alignment [15,
90].

mEDI might influence sleep architecture, particularly REM regulation, more than sleep
quality alone. Southern participants tended to receive greater morning light exposure.
This may help explain why mEDI showed modest associations with REM sleep, even
though Northern participants generally slept longer. One hypothesis is that mEDI primar­
ily influences circadian phase and REM regulation, while environmental parameters like
temperature and ventilation exert stronger effects on sleep duration and continuity in this
dataset. These distinct pathways may act in parallel to shape overall sleep quality.
Importantly, since mEDI values were not directly recorded but rather an estimation, this
approximation may have introduced noise and may have obscured stronger associations.
The fact that a weak link to REM sleep still emerged despite this limitation suggests that
circadian­effective light could play a meaningful role, especially in shaping sleep architec­
ture rather than quantity.
Future work should investigate whether mEDI timing, particularly morning exposure, cor­
relates more closely with internal circadian phase markers such as dim­light melatonin on­
set (DLMO), REM latency, or sleep fragmentation indices. This may help clarify whether
the modest associations observed here reflect real physiological effects or are attenuated
by behavioural and methodological noise.
5.3
RQ3: Bedroom temperature emerged as the most
consistent environmental predictor of sleep quality
Among the measured environmental parameters, bedroom air temperature showed the
most consistent association with sleep outcomes. Higher nighttime temperatures were
linked to both longer total sleep time and higher composite Fitbit sleep scores. This sup­
ports previous research suggesting that thermal comfort strongly influences sleep conti­
nuity and depth [30].
Interestingly, relative humidity (RH) showed a strong negative correlation with sleep, but
this was likely mediated by its inverse relationship with temperature. Similarly, nighttime
illuminance exhibited weak negative associations with sleep outcomes, in line with prior
findings that even low levels of light can disrupt circadian physiology. However, CO2
concentrations were not significantly related to sleep duration or quality, suggesting that
within the observed range (600–1000 ppm), CO2 levels had less impact than thermal
conditions.
Participant­level variation adds further nuance. For example, S5 experienced both the
lowest bedroom temperatures and the highest estimated daytime light exposure, pointing
to the role of behaviour, such as window use or time spent outdoors, in shaping both the
thermal and circadian environment. On average, Northern bedrooms were 1–2 °C warmer
than those in the South, likely due to reduced window ventilation. This temperature dif­
ference may have conferred a modest sleep advantage in the North.
While temperature emerged as the strongest single predictor in this dataset, it could in­
teract with other factors, including light and ventilation, and may itself be shaped by sleep
behaviour.
Taken together, the findings highlight temperature as a key modifiable indoor parameter
for promoting sleep. Still, causality cannot be firmly established, and the contributions
of other environmental variables should not be discounted. Future studies could explore
these dynamics more precisely using real­time window­opening logs, thermal imaging, or
experimental manipulation of thermal conditions.

5.4
RQ4: Subjective and objective sleep assessments
showed partial convergence
Subjective and objective methods captured overlapping but distinct aspects of sleep. In
this study, these two approaches showed only partial agreement, consistent with prior
findings that perceived and measured sleep often diverge [91, 92].
Weekly GSQS scores did not correlate significantly with Fitbit sleep scores, suggesting
that objectively better sleep was not necessarily perceived as such. When narrowing the
focus to the last few nights of each week, a modest correlation emerged. This points to
a possible recency bias in self­evaluation, where recent nights influence weekly ratings
more than earlier ones.
Participants also reported poor sleep quality on the PSQI, with most exceeding the con­
ventional cut­off. However, as the PSQI was administered about one month after the ob­
jective tracking period, it reflects a different time frame and cannot be directly compared
with the wearable data. Despite elevated latency and disturbance scores, participants
rated their overall sleep quality as “fairly good,” and reported minimal daytime dysfunc­
tion or medication use. This also suggests that PSQI global scores may overestimate
perceived sleep problems when minor but regular disruptions are present or recall bias
affects monthly retrospection.
The lack of association between subjective sleep and estimated mEDI further suggests
that perceived sleep is influenced by more than environmental or physiological inputs.
Although Southern participants received more morning light and reported better sleep, the
overall relationship between mEDI and GSQS was weak and not statistically significant.
Implications: These findings reinforce that subjective and objective tools capture different
but complementary aspects of sleep. Wearables quantify sleep continuity and staging,
while questionnaires provide insight into perception, mood, and expectations. Using both
revealed a more nuanced picture, objectively average­to­good sleep, yet frequent subjec­
tive dissatisfaction, likely shaped by psychological, contextual, or environmental factors.
Future research should aim to better understand the causes of these mismatches. Com­
bining wearable devices with ecological momentary assessments (EMAs), daily diaries,
or stress/mood tracking could help identify why some individuals perceive their sleep as
poor despite normal physiology. Moreover, future studies could investigate whether inter­
ventions targeting perception (e.g., cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia) improve
subjective­objective agreement or overall sleep health.
5.5
RQ5: Latitude influenced behaviour and exposure more
than sleep outcomes directly
While the study involved participants living at substantially different latitudes (58°N and
69°N), the findings suggest that latitude shaped environmental exposure and behaviour
more than sleep itself. Participants in the North and South experienced different daylight
durations, temperatures, and ventilation practices, yet objective sleep outcomes, such as
total sleep time, efficiency, and staging, remained broadly similar. This indicates that be­
havioural adaptations and indoor environments can buffer natural geographic differences.
Rather than acting as a direct determinant of sleep quality, latitude influenced the con­
ditions participants had to manage: the North presented colder, darker mornings and
longer evenings, while the South allowed for greater daylight access and cooler, fresher
bedrooms. However, participants adjusted accordingly. Northern bedrooms were warmer

tilated their rooms and spent more time outdoors. These differing strategies resulted in

This supports previous research suggesting that environmental context and personal rou­
tines may override geographic predictors such as latitude alone [89, 45]. The implication
is that sleep health interventions should focus less on geographic location and more on
modifiable factors, such as morning light exposure, bedroom conditions, and behaviour.
To better understand how latitude and seasonal variation interact, future studies should
examine more extreme phases of the year, such as the polar night or midnight sun, when
natural light exposure differs dramatically. Extending the study period to include multi­
ple seasons would allow researchers to observe how sleep and behavioural adaptations
evolve over time, and whether compensatory strategies remain effective under more pro­
nounced environmental changes.
Practical Implications
Although the small sample size and technical limitations of this study restrict generalizabil­
ity, the results offer some tentative guidance for improving everyday sleep environments
in similar contexts. Most notably, bedroom temperature showed a consistent association
with sleep duration and quality. This suggests that maintaining thermal comfort, poten­
tially by reducing excessive overnight cooling or improving temperature stability, could be
a simple, actionable step to support better sleep in colder climates.
The data also highlighted that ventilation practices, while beneficial for air quality, were
linked to lower room temperatures and shorter sleep. This trade­off may be relevant for
building design and occupant behaviour. For example, solutions such as heat­recovery
ventilation or smart window strategies could help balance air exchange and thermal com­
fort without sacrificing sleep quality.
While light exposure showed only weak associations with sleep in this study, the consis­
tently low morning melanopic light levels recorded across participants point to a possi­
ble area for improvement. Design choices that increase morning daylight access, such
as window placement, curtain types, or morning light routines, may help align circadian
rhythms, particularly in darker seasons.
Overall, even with limited data, the findings reinforce that subtle adjustments to indoor
environments and daily routines may help individuals optimise sleep in Nordic settings,
especially during transitional seasons.

Innovation Potential
This project highlights an opportunity for a personalized sleep optimization system that
integrates environmental sensing, wearable sleep data, and subjective feedback. Building
on the observed relationship between bedroom conditions and sleep quality, a potential
innovation could be a smart bedside hub that:
• Tracks bedroom temperature, CO2, humidity, and light.
• Syncs with wearable sleep trackers (e.g., Fitbit, Garmin).
• Allows users to report subjective impressions (e.g., “felt too cold”).
• Learns individual patterns over time by linking environmental and physiological data.
• Provides real­time, personalized advice (e.g., “open your window for 30 minutes” or
“set the temperature to 18.2 °C tonight”).
Unlike existing systems, this solution would combine subjective, objective, and physio­
logical data to deliver actionable recommendations tailored to each user’s preferences
and patterns. It could also adapt seasonally and integrate with smart home devices to
automate ventilation, heating, or lighting.
While similar products exist (e.g., sleep trackers with general advice), few offer dynamic
learning or direct integration between indoor environment and personalized sleep support.
This represents an incremental but practical innovation with potential for commercial de­
velopment, particularly appealing to the growing “quantified self” and biohacking markets.
5.6
Limitations
This field study has several limitations that qualify the interpretation and generalisability
of its findings.
The initial comparative aim was testing latitude­related differences in sleep was con­
strained by incomplete/light­exposure data, forcing us to pool all participants into one
analytical cohort. With only ten non­randomly recruited participants and imperfect demo­
graphic matching between regions, statistical power was low and external validity was
limited. Any apparent regional contrasts may therefore reflect uncontrolled individual dif­
ferences rather than latitude in this study.
The study assumed shorter daylight at the Northern site, yet around the spring equinox,
Tromsø experienced more daylight than the Southern sites, reducing the intended light­
exposure contrast. No intra­individual comparison between early (weeks 1–2) and late
(weeks 3–4) phases was performed, leaving the effect of increasing day length within
participants unexplored.
Data were collected only during mid­March to mid­April. This narrow window omits the
pronounced seasonal extremes in day length typical of Norway and captures the daylight­
saving­time transition only partially. Consequently, the results cannot be extrapolated
to winter or summer conditions, when photo­period and behavioural adaptations differ
markedly.
All physiological and light­exposure data were obtained with consumer­grade wearables
(Fitbit Inspire 3; LYS Button). Although convenient, these devices have documented ac­
curacy limitations compared with laboratory standards (e.g., polysomnography for sleep,
spectroradiometer for light). A firmware error rendered the on­board mEDI values un­
reliable. Therefore, estimated melanopic EDI were calculated from RGB and lux chan­
A Comparative Study of Geographical Differences in Light Exposure and Sleep Quality in Northern and
Southern Norway
71
==PAGE:88==
nels using CIE S 026–based approximations, which introduce assumptions about spec­
tral composition. Differences between the iOS (“LYS Track”) and Android (“LYS Collect”)
apps, plus occasional battery depletion, signal loss, or sync failures, produced gaps and
variability in the dataset despite issuing backup sensors.
Weekly questionnaires and retrospective indices (e.g., PSQI, GSQS) provided valuable
context but are inherently vulnerable to recall bias, mood effects, and individual interpre­
tation. In particular, perceived disturbances (e.g., noise, light) may not align with objective
exposures.
Objective bedroom noise (SPL) was not instrumentally recorded due to logistical con­
straints; instead, results relied on participants’ subjective ratings, which may miss tran­
sient or low­level events. Likewise, potentially relevant room attributes, window orienta­
tion, surface colours and detailed photos were not systematically documented, limiting
environmental contextualisation.
The analyses are correlational and cannot establish causality. Unmeasured behavioural
or physiological factors (e.g., stress, diet, physical activity, circadian phase markers such
as dim­light melatonin onset) could confound observed associations between light, indoor
climate, and sleep.
Taken together, these constraints position the present work as exploratory. Future re­
search should expand the sample size, span multiple seasons, include objective acoustic
monitoring and fuller architectural documentation, apply validated spectral light measure­
ments, and incorporate biological markers of circadian timing to strengthen causal infer­
ence.

Conclusion
This study investigated how personal light exposure, bedroom environment, and latitude­
related context influenced sleep among participants in Northern and Southern Norway
during early spring. Across multiple data sources, wearable sensors, light measurements,
and self­report instruments, the results revealed clear regional and individual variation in
exposure conditions, but relatively modest effects on sleep outcomes.
The most consistent relationship emerged between bedroom temperature and sleep qual­
ity. Warmer rooms were associated with longer sleep duration and slightly better com­
posite sleep scores, while cooler and more humid environments tended to coincide with
shorter sleep. These patterns were supported by both objective logging and self­reported
ventilation behaviours, suggesting that bedroom climate plays a key role in shaping sleep
under everyday conditions.
Light exposure, by contrast, showed weak associations. While Southern participants re­
ceived higher daytime melanopic light and reported better subjective sleep quality, objec­
tive metrics such as sleep duration or efficiency did not differ substantially between re­
gions. The only light–sleep association observed was a modest correlation between total
mEDI and REM sleep minutes, which may suggest some influence of circadian­effective
light on sleep architecture, but the relationship was limited.
Subjective and objective sleep measures aligned only partially. While wearables cap­
tured relatively stable and average­to­good sleep, many participants reported poor sleep
in questionnaires, especially on the PSQI collected post­study. These discrepancies may
reflect how self­perceived sleep is shaped by recent experiences, expectations, or con­
textual factors not reflected in physiological measurements.
Latitude did not appear to have a direct effect on sleep outcomes in this sample. Despite
meaningful differences in daylight duration, indoor light levels, and ventilation behaviour,
objective sleep remained broadly similar across regions. This suggests that participants
adapted to their local conditions in ways that buffered environmental differences through
personal routines, building use, or sleep timing adjustments.
Overall, the results point to moderate links between thermal conditions and sleep, limited
associations between light and sleep, and evidence that latitude shaped environmental
exposure and behaviour more than sleep itself. The study was constrained by a small
sample size and some technical limitations in the light data, which likely reduced sensitivity
to smaller effects. Still, the combined dataset provides a nuanced picture of how indoor
and behavioural factors interact with seasonal and geographic context in shaping sleep
under real­life conditions in Northern and Southern Norway.

User Manual for Fitbit Inspire 3, HOBO Sensor, and LYS Button 
This kit includes three devices that together provide an overview of sleep and light conditions: 
Fitbit watch: The Fitbit Inspire 3 is a fitness tracker that monitors sleep, physical activity, and heart rate. 
For this project, the watch is primarily used to track sleep. Participants must wear it every night while 
sleeping.  
HOBO Sensor: The HOBOware Environmental Sensor is a device designed to monitor indoor air quality by 
measuring CO₂ levels and temperature. The device is pre-configured before delivery, so no additional 
setup is required. 
LYS Button: The LYS Button is a small, portable light sensor that measures light intensity, spectrum, and 
exposure over time. It is used to track daily light exposure and understand the relationship between light 
and sleep. The sensor attaches to clothing with a safety pin and syncs via Bluetooth to a mobile app.
