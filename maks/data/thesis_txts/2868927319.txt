Abstract 
Finding alternatives to fossil fuels is crucial to make the shipping sector more sustainable. Liquid 
ammonia is a promising alternative, but due to its high toxicity there are safety concerns. This thesis 
aims to analyse the risks associated with ammonia bunkering and strategies for risk mitigation. A 
probabilistic risk analysis (PRA) was conducted based on historic failure rates for components, which 
showed the probability per operation of various leak magnitudes. The event trees of the system were 
then modified to test the impact of two initiating events and two improvements to the system. To gain 
feedback on various risk mitigation proposals and insight into the preferences of stakeholders and 
experts, a multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) was conducted based on interviews. The results 
show that the probability of an accidental release of ammonia per operation is 6.3⋅10-5. During 
bunkering operations, leaks are most likely to originate from the hose connecting the bunker-vessel and 
the receiving ship. Stakeholders prefer more safe and expensive risk mitigation strategies. Both analyses 
showed that more personnel in the control room is an ineffective risk mitigation proposal. Only the 
PRA showed that double-walled piping along the transfer line is ineffective, while the results from the 
MCDA were inconclusive.

1.  Introduction 
In line with the “Polluter pays” principle, the European Union requires shipping companies to purchase 
carbon permits to cover their emissions. At the time of writing in 2025, companies operating within the 
EU are expected to cover 70% of their emissions, and in 2026 they will cover 100% of their emissions 
(Council of the European Union 2022). According to estimates by The International Maritime Organisation 
(IMO), about 64% of the total CO2 reduction in the shipping industry in 2050 will be due to the use of 
alternative fuels (IMO 2020). Since international shipping accounts for close to 3% of global emissions, 
making this sector more sustainable is important to the objective of reducing greenhouse gas emissions 
(IMO 2020). Thus, shipping companies are under increasing pressure from the EU and the IMO to switch 
from marine fuel oil (MFO) to more sustainable alternatives. 
In the pursuit of this objective, the potential of various low-carbon or carbon-free fuel alternatives is 
being researched, such as liquefied natural gas (LNG), liquid hydrogen, biogas, and liquid ammonia. 
Among these, ammonia is likely one of the best candidates (Valera-Medina et al. 2018;Al-Aboosi et al. 
2021), as it is relatively easy to store in a liquified state (-33°C, around atmospheric pressure) and is 
already produced on a large scale through the Harber-Bosch process, 70% of which is used for fertiliser 
(Ishaq and Crawford 2024). The experience with storing, producing ammonia, and transporting ammonia 
is a big advantage (Al-Aboosi et al. 2021). According to Inal, Zincir, and Deniz 2022, ammonia is safer, 
cheaper to produce and store, and more sustainable than hydrogen. It is estimated that ammonia 
production accounts for 2% of global energy consumption (Bora et al. 2024), but ammonia may be 
produced without emitting any CO2. So called “green ammonia” is not produced through the traditional 
Haber-Bosch process, as it uses renewable sources of energy. Hence it may play a significant role in 
reducing emissions from shipping if ammonia is adopted on a large scale. Due to the toxicity of 
ammonia, there are naturally concerns about the safety of using it as a fuel. This paper will focus on the 
safety aspect of ammonia bunkering. 
The field of ammonia-bunkering is changing quickly, and it can be difficult to keep up with the latest 
developments. Large reports have been released during the work on this thesis, and in December 2024 
the IMO approved interim guidelines for the use of ammonia as a fuel for ships (Lloyd’s Register 2024). 
While ammonia bunkering shares some characteristics with LNG bunkering, its toxicity requires that 
changes be made to bunkering operations, risk management, regulations, and guidelines (DNV 2023). In 
a study on the perceptions in the maritime community regarding ammonia fuelled vessels, it was found 
that 60% of respondents had safety concerns, while 12% said they would not work on such vessels 
(MMMCZCS 2024). Multiple experts interviewed during this thesis have stated that ammonia fuel should 
not just be statistically safe, but that it must also be perceived as safe by stakeholders, as an accident 
could stymie the implementation of ammonia fuel by ruining its public imagine, thereby slowing a green 
transition in the shipping industry. Since guidelines and regulation around ammonia bunkering are still 
being formulated, there is a need for stakeholders with different interests and perspectives to come 
together to define the best practices. Risk mitigation presents a challenge in that there are many factors 
to consider, such as costs, risk reduction potential, the diminishing returns of risk reduction, and more 
(Li et al. 2009). Balancing the relevant aspects of risk mitigation may be done using an MCDA (Multi-
Criteria Decision Analysis), as it provides a framework for the decision-making process. 
Probabilistic risk assessment (PRA) is commonly employed to address sources of risk in complex 
systems. Here risk is defined as probability times consequence. The use of PRA is associated with the 
nuclear industry where it has been used since the 1960s, but there is precedent for using PRA in other 
industries, such as the chemical industry, where hazards are of major concern to human safety (Aydin et 
al. 2021;Kennedy et al. 1980). To evaluate the probability of an undesired event (often called a top event), 
such as a subsystem failure, fault tree analysis (FTA) is often used (Kennedy et al. 1980). Fault trees show 
==PAGE:10==
2 
 
the interdependencies of components or elements (basic events) in a system, and how combinations of 
faults may lead to the undesired event. In this way fault tree analysis can be used to isolate sources of 
risk down to the component level in otherwise complicated systems, and “what-if” scenarios can be 
created to show how risk can be reduced or increased by changes to the system or initiating events (IE). 
PRA may be paired with Monte Carlo Simulations (EPA 2014), but this is outside the scope of this paper. 
Using ETA and FTA, probabilistic simulations can be run that can assist decision makers in finding the 
most likely points of failure of a system. 
The PRA in this paper builds on a concept for ammonia bunkering developed by Ming et al 2022 (see 
figure 1 below), as this simplified concept is a good starting point for understanding the basics of 
ammonia bunkering. However, based on early expert interviews, it has been modified by changing V1 
and V2 into ESD-valves, valves which close automatically in case of an emergency shutdown. There 
are eight main steps to ammonia bunkering, according to the same study. The bunkering process is 
automatic. 
1) The bunkering-vessel’s transfer line is precooled with cold ammonia while it moors up against the 
receiving ship.  
2) The ships are connected by a bunker-hose with a Quick Connect-Disconnect Coupling (QC/DC), 
which is designed to stop the flow and prevent a rupture if the ships drift apart. In this step a bunkering 
safety link (BSL) would also be established, which connects the emergency shutdown (ESD) systems 
of the two ships. 
3) The transfer-line is inerted with nitrogen to remove oxygen and moisture. 
4) The transfer-line is purged with ammonia vapour to remove the nitrogen. 
5) Liquid ammonia is transferred from the bunker-vessel to the receiving ship 
6) Both sides of the transfer-line are drained of ammonia. 
7) Inerting with nitrogen, similarly to step 3. 
8) Disconnecting the vessels. 
Since liquid ammonia is only transferred during step 5, it is assumed to pose the largest risk of an 
accidental release during bunkering. Hence this paper will focus on step 5. As the concept by Ming et al 
2022 concept for ammonia bunkering does not include a vapour return line, it is not considered in this 
paper.

For the analyses later in the paper, it is important to understand the ESD system. The ESD systems of 
both ships communicate via the BSL, which means that any ESD initiator will initiate a trip regardless 
of where it happens: i.e., if a leak occurs in the transfer line of the receiving ship, the ESD valves of the 
bunker vessel should close rapidly. Some examples of initiators may be fire, loss of power, failure of 
BSS, abnormal pressure, gas detection, or a manual trip of the ESD system (SGMF 2024). In this paper 
the latter three will be used and explained in the same order. 
First, the pressure detection system will initiate a trip of the ESD system if the pressure at the outlet 
varies unexpectedly from that of the inlet. This system is purposefully somewhat insensitive, since the 
pressure gradually changes when pumping begins, and as unpredictable fluctuations are expected during 
bunkering. Second, it may be activated by detectors that are placed strategically around the pump and 
pipe systems and can shut down the system if a certain concentration is reached by one or more detectors 
depending on whether a voting system is used (ABS 2023). The voting system is the number of detectors 
that must detect a concentration above the limit to initiate a trip of the ESD system, which is important 
as gas detectors may activate spuriously (SGMF 2024). The limit at which the ESD is activated may vary 
depending on where the detector is located, but a range of 150-300 ppm is normal (ABS 2023). Detectors 
are more sensitive in enclosed spaces where the airflow is controlled to increase detection. Under the 
right conditions small leaks may avert detection by either system if they happen on the outside (e.g. on 
the deck). From the moment that a leak is detected, the operator in the control room will be notified 
straight away by an alarm sound. If the automatic ESD system does not actuate correctly or shows an 
error the operator will initiate the ESD system manually by the push of a button. Operators or workers 
will have to initiate the ESD system manually if they are alerted by the odorous or visual signs of a leak 
(the operator in the control room has live camera feeds on the operation). 
This paper seeks to contribute the following: 
 
Simulate various initiating events that can occur during ammonia transfer. 
 
Simulate the risk-reduction potential of three safety measures using PRA. 
 
Simulate the probability of different leakage-magnitudes during ammonia transfer.

with different levels of complexity and safety.

2.   Literature Review 
The following section will describe the most important sources for key insights used and discussed in 
this paper and explain the research gap which the paper seeks to address. 
2.1   Safety recommendations for ammonia bunkering 
Equinor and DNV 2023 and DNV 2023 argued persuasively that fully refrigerated ammonia is by far safest 
option compared to partially refrigerated and pressurised ammonia. Hence, this paper will focus on fully 
refrigerated ammonia. MMMCZCS 2023 proposes various risk mitigation methods, and how the risk of 
fatalities can be increased or decreased by changing certain operational parameters and by 
implementing some risk mitigation proposals. American Bureau of Shipping 2024 provides advice on a 
broad range of topics within the scope of ammonia bunkering, such as equipment materials, security 
zones, and storage of hoses. SGMF 2024 contributes to the literature with the latest guidelines and 
recommendations on ammonia bunkering operations. The report advices on pumping, ESD valves, 
safety systems, bunkering checklists, and more. MMMCZCS (2) 2024 provides some of the most up-to-
date recommendations for tank design, detection, layout of bunkering facilities, and operational safety. 
Ming et al 2022 has focused on the properties of ammonia and how materials should be used or avoided 
for the tanks and transfer-lines, as well as calculations of pressure. This study is especially important to 
the thesis, as it provides an overview of the bunkering process accompanied by illustrations which form 
the basis for the PRA conducted during the thesis. Concerning the human element, Eriksen et al. 2023 
outlines the areas in which ammonia bunkering is expected to alter the common industry practices, and 
lists competence, occupational hazards, and procedures as the most impacted. 
2.2   Risk analysis data 
In the literature, there is a precedence for using quantitative risk assessment (QRA) based on ETA and 
historical failure rates to estimate the leak probabilities of maritime fuels during bunkering (Depken et 
al. 2024). QRA has also been used to analyse risks of a leak in the industrial synthesis of ammonia (Liu 
et al. 2021). Akyuz et al. 2018 showed how human error can be accurately estimated by combining the 
probabilities of Generic tasks with risk-increasing probabilities such as lack of experience or knowledge 
transfer in the context of bunkering operations. DNV 2021 was instrumental as it provided estimates for 
pipe leakages, and many parameters from the ‘bunker to vessel’ scenario were used as input data. This 
will be explained in Section 3. This study also provided some recommendations for increasing safety. 
Kletz 2001 provided numerous estimates of human error across various industries and case studies to 
showcase exactly what went wrong and how to learn from past mistakes. The full list of sources for all 
failure data is given in Section 3.2.1. The QRA conducted by DNV 2021 compares the risk of two 
bunkering scenarios, those being bunkering from a ship (Scenario 1A&B) and bunkering from a vessel 
(Scenario 2) finding only the risk of the latter acceptably low. The same study includes a consequence 
analysis of potential leak and how the two scenarios differ with regards to the leak duration and 
ammonia-cloud size. Yang and Lam 2024 conducted more detailed simulations to model the dispersion 
of ammonia clouds generated from leaks.  
2.3   MCDA and expert opinions 
In Inal, Zincir, and Deniz 2022 MCDA was used to investigate whether ammonia or hydrogen is the better 
sustainable fuel with respect to five criteria. The importance of the criteria was rated by experts before 
the analysis. Hansson et al. 2020 has demonstrated how MCDA can be used to evaluate the prospects of 
ammonia as a fuel. In the study, ten criteria across four categories (Economic, technical, environmental, 
social) were used to rate the viability of 11 alternative fuels. Each criterion was then rated during a 
workshop by stakeholders. S. Schwarz and K. S. Rossbach 2024; Andersson et al. 2020 provide a framework 
for choosing the overall criteria categories. Schwarz and Rossbach 2024 has also shown how data gathered 
==PAGE:14==
6 
 
from interviews with a broad selection of stakeholders can be used to measure industry preferences and 
perceptions of maritime fuels when the interviews are combined with an MCDA using REMBRANDT. 
2.4   Research gap 
No study has been identified that uses data on human and component reliability to construct fault trees 
and event trees to estimate and isolate sources of risk. A research gap as been identified as to the use of 
PRA in modelling and probability of a release of ammonia and which components and factors should 
be addressed with the highest priority in reducing the probability of a leak during bunkering. Risk 
mitigation proposals identified by studies such as DNV 2023 are rarely accompanied by estimates of 
risk-reduction potential or leak probabilities which take into account both components and human error. 
To estimate the magnitude of a leak, it is essential to approximate the isolation time, and no studies 
have been identified that use a mix of human error and component/system failure to estimate the 
duration of different types of leaks, and what consequences that will have for leak magnitudes. Nor 
have any studies been identified that measure how stakeholders perceive risk-reduction strategies for 
ammonia bunkering, or how the perceptions of risk or other factors may be influenced by changes to 
the strategy.

3.   Methodology 
In this section, the methodology will be explained. First, the early interview-process with experts will 
be introduced, as these are cited later. Second, the PRA modelling-process in the software SAPHIRE is 
summarised after which the research into component and human failure is explained thoroughly with 
references. Third, the event trees and scenarios explored in the PRA are outlined. Last, the MCDA and 
interview-procedure are explained.  
3.1   Early interviews with experts 
Early in the project, two experts on bunkering were consulted for clarifying questions and feedback. 
Since human error was thought to be important factor in bunker safety, a plant operator with experience 
working on ammonia carriers and other ships was consulted for their experience with human error in 
the control room. Below each expert who provided feedback on the thesis and answered questions is 
listed for reference. Not all these experts participated in the MCDA analysis, as some of them were 
consulted earlier in the project. The goal is to provide enough information about a given expert to give 
the reader enough context to gauge the basis and validity of a given expert opinion cited during the 
thesis, while still maintaining the experts’ anonymity. 
Expert 1: Engineer working for a large company that develops technology relevant to bunkering. This 
engineer has around 40 years of experience at the company and is currently working on developing 
technology and operations for ammonia in shipping. 
Expert 2: Engineer working at a company that develops technology and methods for ammonia 
bunkering and handling. Has over 30 years of experience with shipping and bunkering. 
Expert 3: Senior researcher at DTU working with sustainable supply chains and operations. 
Expert 4: Operator at waste-to-energy plant in Denmark. Experience with working on vessels carrying 
ammonia and limited experience with handling ammonia, both at the energy plant and on vessels. 
3.2   Probabilistic risk analysis (PRA) and modelling in SAPHIRE 
For the probabilistic risk analysis, the software SAPHIRE was used to construct fault and event trees. 
SAPHIRE stands for Systems Analysis Programs for Hands-on Integrated Reliability Evaluation and is 
used primarily in the nuclear energy industry to assist with PRA (Office of Nuclear Regulatory Research, 
n.d.). SAPHIRE has been used to assess the potential fire hazards to a nuclear reactor, and how they 
impact the core damage frequency per year (Safaei Arshi et al 2010). SAPHIRE has also been used to 
quantify the impact of risk mitigation measures using historical component failure rates, taking into 
account human errors and common cause failures (Kordalivand et al 2024). For this study, a Windows 7 
version of SAPHIRE 6.77 was run on a virtual machine. Part of the analysis (e.g. the positions and 
numbers of valves and flanges) was based on the design of the conceptual bunker operation by Ming et 
al 2022 as it was available to the public and provided a springboard for dialogues with experts and a 
starting point for the PRA. 
The model went through various iterations. First a preliminary model was developed in tandem with 
the latter part of the literature review, which was important for discovering missing information to be 
filled out by deeper research and conversations with experts. The scope was narrowed to focus on the 
ammonia transfer operation rather than the proceeding steps, as it posed the largest risk. Some important 
early feedback came from conversations with Experts 1 and 2 who clarified likely points of risk and the 
role of the operators in the bunkering process. Save pipe-ruptures, this analysis does not consider 
common cause failures and all probabilities in the SAPHIRE model are independent.

3.2.1   Failure rates of components 
In this subsection, the research behind the failure rates used in the PRA is explained thoroughly. The 
table at the end of the subsection provides an overview of the final numbers. 
The simulation builds on historic and calculated failure rates from several sources detailed below in 
table 1. The failure rates of the simulation were changed over time as newer and more relevant sources 
were discovered, but in all cases the failure rates were compared to other sources. It is important to 
mention that failure probabilities vary a lot depending on the source, from around 1.3x to a factor of 10. 
This may be due to sourcing methodology, the sample size, or the source data. For failure-rates that are 
in units per year, it was especially important that the source be as closely linked to bunkering, due to 
how much standards and use-time vary depending on context. All failure rates that are in units per year 
have been divided by 91, as the transfer frequency was assumed to be 91 times per year.1 Hence, the 
result was the probability of failure (PoF) per operation, and the simulation shows the probabilities of 
various leaks per operation. This assumption is taken from DNV 2021 and to keep the failure rates as 
closely aligned with the number of operations per year, many of the failure rates were sourced directly 
from the same study. 
3.2.1.1   Automatic emergency shutdown system 
An automatic ESD system (auto ESD system) is comprised of many parts, such as the BSL, the ESD 
valves (often actuated ball valves), flow or pressure sensors, and ammonia gas detectors. In this 
simulation, the auto ESD system is abstracted in such a way that the many parts and components of the 
system and their individual failure modes and failure rates are ignored. This was done for simplicity 
and due to the lack of data on the subcomponents of the system. Instead, a flat failure on demand rate 
is assumed for the system in its entirety. The failure rate varies depending on the source, generally from 
1‰ to 1% (DNV 2021;Arnet 2014;Barabadi et al 2013). Experts 1 and 2 confirmed that 1‰ would be a 
better estimate. 
3.2.1.2   Piping and hose 
The failure rates of the piping and hose taken directly from DNV 2021. The failure rates for the hose 
were derived from Concept 2 (Ship-to-ship bunkering), where the only source of a leak is the hose 
connecting the two ships. Aneziris et al. 2021 is a study on LNG storage and bunkering at ports, and 
assumes the probability of a rupture to be a fourth compared to the one in DNV 2021, however the data 
of the latter study was preferred due to the direct link to ammonia. Since this study also explores the 
possibility of other possible leaks, the failure rates of the internal piping are derived from Concept 1B, 
which analyses the risk of ship to tank transfer. In Concept 1B the pipeline is 20 m long, and the failure 
rates used in this study have not been modified to accommodate more accurate estimates of pipe-length. 
The failure rate of the double piping is the most uncertain and difficult to estimate. For reasons that will 
be explained in Section 3.3.1.1 neither the probability of a small leak nor a rupture was included. 
3.2.1.3   Valves and flanges 
The failure rates for valves and flanges are not linked to bunkering. The probability of a FTC error for 
the actuated valve was sourced from the American nuclear industry (Eide et al. 2007). Since the original 
data was in units per operation it was assumed to be applicable. The other failure rates came from 
International Association of Oil & Gas Producers 2019, as the data was new, complete, and detailed. The 
failure rates were largely corroborated by DNV 2013 and Lloyd’s Register 2018.

3.2.2   Human error aspect of simulation 
Expert 2 confirmed that most of the bunkering process is automatic. Although the steps leading up to 
the bunkering itself (maintenance of components, connecting flexible hose to manifold, etc.) are 
dependent on human action and may be causes for IEs if workers and operators make mistakes, humans 
play only a small role during bunkering. Yet, it will be the operators’ duty to monitor that the operation 
proceeds as planned and to intervene quickly in case there is a system failure. For this analysis, the 
failure of the operator to respond within 25 seconds was defined as a timing error and shown as a failure 
of the manual ESD system in the event tree. The precise response time was not simulated nor the 
significance of the exact delay. This is explained in the next subsection. If the auto ESD system fails, 
an operator will have to initiate the manual ESD system rapidly to stop the leak. In the extremely 
unlikely worst-case scenario where the manual ESD system fails, the leak will have to be stopped by 
operators and workers at the scene by stopping the flow physically or cutting the power to the pump 
(Expert 2). This was not simulated.

3.2.3   Emergency shutdown system detection time 
Below the research into the detection times of the ESD system is explained. As mentioned, this 
system may be activated automatically by sensors or manually by operators. 
3.2.3.1   Automatic system 
The actuation time of the automatic ESD system depends on magnitude of the leak and where it occurs. 
According to a review by Gerbec and Aneziris 2022, the response of an automatic ESD system varies 
from 10 to 180 seconds. SIGTTO 2021 sets the time of actuation at 25-30 seconds from the activation of 
the ESD system to the ESD-valves are fully closed, but this number does not include detection time. 
IGC code 18.10.2.1.3 sets the upper time limit at 30 seconds within actuation. Meanwhile, conversations 
with Expert 2 have made more specific estimates depending on the situation. Unless the leak is small, 
it will be detected instantly by the pressure detectors and the ESD valves will close after 5 seconds. A 
more rapid closure is not recommended due to the risk of a surge (SIGTTO 2021). For the simulation, 
the following was assumed: If the leak is small and happens in an enclosed space it will be detected 
after 25 seconds by detectors and the ESD valves will close after 5 seconds. If a small leak happens 
outside, then manual intervention by operators is assumed to be necessary in this simulation. The timing 
of this scenario will be explained below. 
3.2.3.2   Manual ESD system 
In the literature the response time of the manual activation of the ESD system varies significantly. 
According to Gerbec and Aneziris 2022 it varies from 45 s to 180 s. Kletz 2001 and Jeong et al. 2018 assume 
the time to be around 10 seconds, whereas Arnet 2014 defines the time of isolation as 120 seconds for 
small leaks and 15 seconds for medium and large leaks due to faster detection. According to Expert 2 it 
may take the operator 30 seconds to initiate the ESD system from the moment that it is detected. For 
this simulation it will be assumed that the operator will press the manual ESD button after 30 seconds 
of hearing the alarm but if he makes an error or his response is delayed this time will be increased by 
another 30 seconds. In the case of a small leak on the outside, 60 seconds was assumed as the combined 
time to detect the leak, react to it manually and for the ESD valve to close. In case of human error, the 
time was increased to 90 seconds. The same timings will be assumed if a small leak happens inside: 25 
seconds to detect the release and 30 seconds to react to it, giving a 60 second isolation time if the

3.2.5   Limitations of methodology 
The failure rates for components and operators have a large degree of uncertainty. Moreover, the 
analysis builds on a concept for ammonia bunkering which may differ in significant ways from 
bunkering procedures which are under development. For instance, the number of valves may vary, the 
length of the transfer line, and an ammonia vapour return system will change the system. To allow for 
the impact analysis based on leak magnitudes, it was necessary to differentiate the fault trees of the 
subsystems (hoses, valves, pipes etc.) into different top-events in the event tree. This has had the effect 
of changing the combined probability of a leak by -4.4% compared to an event tree where the fault trees 
==PAGE:21==
13 
 
are grouped together. This difference was judged insignificantly small in comparison with the larger 
uncertainties of the failure rates. It does not have an impact on the part of the analysis where the impacts 
of improvements are considered.

Below follows a summation of the various scenarios that were simulated and an explanation of the 
improvements which were added to test their impact. The first letter refers to the scenario, and the two 
numbers refer to the initiating event and the improvement, respectively. First the improvements of the 
base event trees starting with X will be explained. The miscellaneous W scenarios are explained last. 
3.3.1   Improvements to scenarios 
In this section, the three improvements whose risk-reduction potential was tested in the PRA will be 
explained. 
3.3.1.1   Improvement: Double-walled piping (X11 & X21) 
According to expert 1, the bunkering line from the bunker-vessel to the bunker station and tank on the 
receiving ship is not double-walled. However, on the receiving ship double-walled piping is used on 
the line connecting the ammonia tank to the fuel-preparation-room (Expert 1). In these scenarios, a pipe 
leak will only lead to an emission of ammonia if the outer layer of pipe fails simultaneously with the 
inner layer. For simplicity, the failure rate of the outer layer of piping is assumed to be the combined 
probability of a medium and large release from DNV 2021 without including the probability of a small 
leak, as this is assumed to constitute a significantly smaller release of ammonia given the extra layer of 
protection and the possibility of the ammonia to diffuse throughout the outer pipe. The hose between 
the ships is not double walled in this scenario. The double-walled piping is included in the fault trees 
of a small, medium, or large leaks, but not a rupture of the pipe as common cause failure is considered: 
Since a pipe rupture is assumed to be caused by an external, catastrophic event (e.g. dropping something 
heavy on the pipe), the double piping will not offer much protection in case of a rupture. The double-
walled pipe is included in two fault trees as an extra AND gate condition in the fault tree of a large 
release and an extra condition in the fault tree of a small release, see appendix A. 
3.3.1.2   Improvement: Extra operator (X12 & X22) 
There is an extra operator in the control room who can initiate a manual ESD, making the number of 
operators three (one on the bunker barge and two in the control room on the receiving vessel). All 
three have the same probability of human error.

3.3.2.1   Initiating event: Poorly maintained ESD system (X1, X11, X12) 
It is not obvious how often the automatic ESD system should be maintained, but it has been suggested 
that it be maintained preventatively or inspected every 35 days to minimise the risk of a failure on 
demand (Barabadi, Naseri, and Ratnayake 2013). In these scenarios, the PoF on demand of the ESD system 
has increased from 0.1% to 1% due to not having been maintained sufficiently, which is a realistic 
number based on other estimates for the failure rate of the auto ESD system and the model by Barabadi, 
Naseri, and Ratnayake 2013. This simulates an increased probability of a FTC, delayed operation, internal 
leakage, or other failure modes for the ESD valves, and problems with detecting a leak from the pressure 
detection system and the gas detectors. 
3.3.2.2   Initiating event: Defect pressure detection (X2, X21, X22) 
As mentioned earlier, pressure detectors measure the variance in the flow along the pipeline. In this 
scenario, the operators are unaware that the pressure detection system is defect and start bunkering as 
normal. Normally ruptures and medium and large leaks are detected almost instantly, but in this 
scenario, it first takes 5 seconds for the gas-detectors to detect the leak and then the usual 5 seconds for 
the ESD valves to close fully. This increases the magnitude of some leaks without changing the 
underlying probabilities in the fault trees.

3.4   MCDA and interviews 
An MCDA was conducted based on interviews with stakeholders, who were asked to compare three 
strategies against nine criteria. The purpose was to collect various risk mitigation proposals from the 
literature and the early interviews and receive feedback in a systematised way from a broad selection 
of stakeholders, such as engineers from different companies and university professors. The MCDA was 
conducted using REMBRANDT, which stands for Ratio Estimations in Magnitudes and/or deci-Bells 
to Rate Alternatives which are Non-DominaTed. REMBRANDT is similar to AHP (Analytic Hierarchy 
Process), but REMBRANDT seeks to solve some of the drawbacks of AHP, namely the rank-reversal 
problem (Maleki and Zadeh 2012). Although the rank-reversal would not have played a role, this was not 
certain while the MCDA was being conducted. 
3.4.1   Selection of criteria 
The criteria had to be collectively exhaustive and mutually exclusive, meaning that there had to be no 
overlap between them, while covering all relevant aspects of bunkering safety. Before the systematic 
interviews began, essential feedback on the criteria was first provided by a plant-operator (Expert 4) and 
senior researcher at DTU (Expert 3) and then sent to two highly experienced engineers (Experts 1 & 2) 
for further feedback and approval.

3.4.5   Limitations of methodology 
Since the panellists were not presented with data on which to base their opinion, they had to use their 
‘gut-feeling’. While some were confident in their answers, others would not be sure of the exact score 
to give a particular strategy. However, all panellists were always sure which strategy (if any) they 
preferred over the other with respect to a particular criterion. For instance, there was never any doubt 
which strategy would be cheaper operationally, and which had the lower probability of a leakage. There 
was one case in which the criteria were viewed differently, with most panellists viewing ‘C7 Training 
of personnel’ negatively similarly to C1 and C2 (hence viewing lower complexity and lower costs as a 
positive), panellists 6 and 8 saw more training as a positive thus preferring the more complex strategies 
rather than the simpler strategies with regards to training. Moreover, the analysis was limited by the 
==PAGE:28==
20 
 
ability of panellists to judge the impact of certain characteristics of the strategies. Since the strategies 
had to be consistent across interviews for the analysis to have any meaning, they could not be updated 
or improved as more research was made. The pool of panellists limits the application of the data, since 
it is quite small and is dominated by experts working in Denmark. More research with a larger and a 
more varied pool of panellists is necessary to judge the preferences of stakeholders with regards to risk 
mitigation proposals.

5.   Conclusions 
This study was conducted to analyse methods and strategies for risk mitigation in bunkering operations 
with fully refrigerated ammonia. Ammonia is a promising alternative to fossil fuels and may play a 
crucial role in making the maritime sector more sustainable. However, since ammonia is highly toxic 
to humans and the environment, changes will have to be made to bunkering operations, as even a small 
accident might damage its image and slow its implementation. 
A PRA was conducted to estimate the probability of an accidental leak of ammonia during ship-to-ship 
bunkering. The following findings were made: (1) Based on historical failure rates of components, the 
probability of a leak was found to be 6.3⋅10-5 per bunkering operation. (2) The analysis showed that 
double walled piping along the transfer line reduces the probability of particularly small and very large 
leaks but does not reduce the total probability of a leak sufficiently to warrant implementation. 
However, redundant valves should be implemented at particularly critical junctures. (3) Having more 
operators in the control room may reduce the probability of a delayed reaction, but simulations showed 
that only the probability of a leak larger than 1500 kg was reduced somewhat. (4) By far, the biggest 
source of risk is the hose connecting the two ships. Since the hose is exposed to wind, is vulnerable to 
falling objects, and human error may occur as bunkering is prepared, leak detection may be slower. 
Based on interviews with experts and findings from the literature review, three risk mitigation strategies 
were defined. Eight Panellists were asked to rate the relevance of nine criteria in five categories 
(Environmental, economic, technical, social, and regulatory). Their feedback used in an MCDA using 
the REMBRANDT method, a modification of the Analytic Hierarchy Process. The MCDA showed the 
following: (1) Panellists prefer more safe bunkering strategies despite the increased costs and 
complexity. (2) Experts and panellists tended to agree with the findings of the PRA regarding the 
redundancy of having two operators per control room. (3) Concerning the effectiveness of double-
walled piping, there was no consensus. (4) Although the average rating of each criterion did not vary 
much, the least relevant criteria according to panellists were the economic criteria, whereas the social 
criteria were rated most relevant. Of these, training of personnel was regarded most important. 
In conclusion, ammonia bunkering practices are rapidly being defined by regulators, engineers, and 
classification societies to enable safe operations when ships begin using ammonia as a fuel. This paper 
shows that leaks are rare and highly unlikely to exceed 150 kg. Due to the many precautions that are 
likely to be implemented (personnel breathing apparatuses, ventilation, water curtains, etc.), a leak may 
be isolated quickly without causing casualties. Future guidelines and regulation should be rigorous 
especially while ship operators are acquainted with the changes and procedures are refined. 
More research into the safety of ammonia bunkering based on more detailed simulations is needed to 
minimise and understand risks. It is important that future research analyses how initiating events can be 
precipitated by human error before and during bunkering, as lack of experience among workers and 
operators in the test phase of ammonia shipping may lead to errors. Relevant stakeholders should be 
included in this research, such that risk mitigation proposals are scrutinised from different perspectives.

C:   Strategies for MCDA 
C1: Strategy 1: 
 
The transfer-line is labelled in accordance with common regulation. 
 
Two workers can initiate manual ESD: An operator on the bunker vessel and an operator in 
the control room of the receiving ship. 
 
Gas sensors are set to 300 ppm and spread at regular interviews along the transfer line. There 
are no extra sensors above particularly risky points such as the FPR, TCS or the bunker 
station if it is inside. This could be a violation of regulation, as 2 or 3 detectors are commonly 
mandated by classification societies (MMMCZCS (2) 2024). The voting system is 2/2. 
According to panellist 8, the sensors should be much more sensitive in rooms with personnel, 
i.e. 2 ppm which is around the concentration at which humans can smell ammonia. According 
to ABS 2023 the gas sensors should be set to 300 ppm around the bunker station, but 150 ppm 
for the tank connection space. 
 
25 air changes/hour in enclosed spaces FPR, TCS, around pump and bunker station if it is not 
outside. 
 
Maintenance or inspection of some critical elements (detectors, ESD valves) of the auto ESD 
system takes place every six weeks. This period is somewhat arbitrary, as it is based on the 
assumption that it should take place every 35 days (Barabadi, Naseri, and Ratnayake 2013). In 
the strategies this has been rounded down to one month (∼4 weeks) as the baseline and 
multiplied by 50% to give six weeks. 
 
SIMOPs are not regulated. 
 
Manually activated water spray systems at entrances to TCS and FPR. 
 
Flow speed is 8 m/s which is around 500 m3/h. 
C2: Strategy 2: 
 
Risky points of the transfer line (around the manifold, pumps, bunker station) are painted 
purple in accordance with a recommendation by Expert 4. The entire transfer-line is labelled in 
accordance with regulation. 
 
Radio course for key personnel (i.e. operators) for fast response time and fewer 
communication errors. This recommendation comes from Expert 4 and according to panellist 2 
it is the norm. 
 
Two workers can initiate manual ESD: An operator on the bunker vessel and an operator in 
the control room of the receiving ship. 
 
Gas sensors are set to 300 ppm. There are gas sensors at regular intervals along the transfer-
line and 2 extra gas sensors at risky points (pumps, manifolds, bunker stations, FPR). The 
voting system is 2/2. 
 
25 air changes/hour in enclosed spaces FPR, TCS, around pump and bunker station if it is not 
outside. 
 
Maintenance or inspection of some critical elements (detectors, ESD valves) of the auto ESD 
system takes place every four weeks, as explained above in strategy 1. 
 
Part of the transfer-line is often exposed on the deck of the ship, although this can be 
minimised or avoided depending on how the ship is designed (MMMCZCS (2) 2024). To 
protect the transfer-line, it is prohibited to move anything heavy (such as containers) above 
the transfer-line. 
 
No SIMOPs in a pre-defined area around which bunkering takes place.  
 
Manually activated water spray systems at entrances to TCS, FPR and at the bunker station 
above the manifolds. 
 
Flow rate is 6 m/s which is around 380 m3/h. 
==PAGE:46==
38 
 
C3: Strategy 3. 
 
The entire transfer-line is painted in purple and labelled in accordance with regulation. 
 
At the outlet of the ammonia tank on the bunker-vessel there are usually ESD valves. In this 
scenario there are two ESD valves, so if one fails to close during an emergency, the other will 
hopefully close correctly. 
 
Double-walled piping along as much of the transfer line as is feasible. This may be 
complicated by intersections, valves, and flanges. 
 
Radio course for all personnel that is involved in the bunkering operations. 
 
There is an extra operator in the control room of the receiving ship. 
 
Yearly practice simulations for operators to engage the manual ESD system. According to 
Experts 2 & 4, operators may panic in an emergency scenario or not remember exactly what to 
do as accidents that require manual intervention by the operators are rare. 
 
There are three gas sensors set to 300 ppm instead of two at the points mentioned in strategy 
2. The voting system at these locations is 2/3, meaning the detection is more sensitive. There 
are gas sensors at regular intervals along the transfer line, but these have no voting system, 
meaning that any detection of ammonia gas at 300 ppm will activate the auto ESD 
 
45 air changes/hour in enclosed spaces such as FPR, TCS, around pump and bunker station if 
it is not outside. Panellist 8 commented that depending on how the ventilated air is collected 
and how sensitive the detectors are, 45 air changes may be too much to detect small leaks as 
ammonia vapour becomes diluted. 
 
On top of that there are 25 air changes/hour along the part of the transfer-line which is in an 
enclosed space. 
 
Maintenance or inspection of some critical elements (detectors, ESD valves) of the auto ESD 
system takes place every two weeks, as explained above in strategy 1. 
 
Difficult maintenance work is only done during the day as recommended by (expert 4). 
 
Any part of the transfer line that is exposed on the deck is protected by a physical barrier and 
it is prohibited to move anything heavy above it as in strategy 2. 
 
No SIMOPs anywhere on the ship while bunkering is taking place. 
 
Manually activated water spray systems at entrances to TCS, FPR, at the bunker station above 
the manifolds, and at ventilation outlets. 
 
Flow speed is 4 m/s which is around 250 m3/h. This is the flow rate in the simulation 
conducted in (DNV 2021).
