Abstract 
Brewers spent grain is a byproduct from the process of brewing beer. Each year approximately 40 
million tons of BSG are produced where most of it is used as animal feed. Every time 100 liter of beer 
is produced, 20 kilos of BSG is produced. It is a product that breweries either dispose of or it is used 
as animal feed. However, this byproduct is fit for human consumption and is also nutritious, making it 
possible for breweries to have a better use for it.  
This thesis researches the fractionation of BSG was pressed in the Dacofi® filter press, with 100 and 
300 micron filters, and the new filter press, with 500 and 1000 micron filters. The BSG was pressed in 
experiment 2 – 6 were pressed under settings, which were changed to investigate possible changes to 
the properties of the BSG. These settings were the RPM at the feed inlet, RPM of the rolls in the press, 
distance between the rolls and filter sizes. The fractions were analyzed for the different contents 
before and after pressing, which were the total protein content, soluble protein, sugar content, dry 
matter and ash content. Several analytical techniques were used to measure these levels.   
It was observed that after the pressing in the Dacofi® filter press that the filtercake would increase by 
1 % in total protein content and 10 % dry matter. After pressing in the new filter press with 500 and 
1000 micron filters, it was observed that the filtercake increased 1 % in total protein and 8 % in dry 
matter. Calculations show that if the BSG, filtercake and filtrate sediment were dried, the protein 
content would be approximately 20 %, 20 % and 34 % respectively.  
There was not found changes in the experiments that could be attributed to the parameters changed 
on the filter press. The significant changes that were observed, could mostly be contributed to the 
soluble protein and ash content.

Aim 
This report will focus on the use of the Dacofi® filter machine to fractionate brewers spent grain, from 
DTU pilot brew and Braunstein, into a liquid and solid fraction. There will be two fractions of liquid 
and solid each as two filters are used a 100 and a 300 micron filter in the machine. Later experiments 
will use a new rotary drum press, that is similar to the Dacofi® filter machine, but with 500 and 1000 
micron filters. On both machines the settings, be changed to optimize the pressing. 
The main aim of the thesis: 
• 
Investigate the effect of different settings during the press has on the fractions 
• 
Investigate how different types of brewers spent grain will perform in the pressing 
• 
To analyze the nutrients in the brewers spent grain and the four fractions.

Introduction 
 
Brewing 
The brewing of beer has been going on for many thousands of years, with many different ingredients. 
Since the year 1516 a law specifying which ingredients are allowed to be used in brewing beer has 
been applied in Germany and called Reinheitsgebot. Newer adoptions to the law have been applied 
in modern times, these ingredients are mainly malt, yeast, water and hops [Kunze 2010]. 
Malt is grain, usually barley, which has been germinated and then roasted. This purpose of this process 
is to produce enzymes that break down starch to sugar, to add flavor and color to the beer. To produce 
the enzymes needed, the grain undergoes a process called steeping, where moisture is added to the 
grain. This makes the grain enter its natural germination state where the grain develops α- and β-
amylases. After the enzymes have been developed, the germination terminates as the grain enters 
the kilning stage. This is where the grain is first dried at low temperatures to remove moisture and 
then it is dried at temperatures higher than 80°C. This last step in the kilning process is significant for 
the development of the color and the taste that the malt adds to the beer. [Eßlinger 2009; Lewis & 
Young 2002]. The dark color of the malt is due to a maillard reaction, which the chemical reaction that 
the amino acids and the reducing sugars undergo when heated [Coghe et al. 2006]. 
To extract the sugar from the malt, a process called mashing is initiated, where malt is added to water. 
The water’s temperature will start at around 50°C where the proteases will degrade the proteins to 
prevent haze/cloudiness in the beer. The temperature is then raised to around 60°C where β-amylases 
break down starch to maltose. The next temperature increase is to 70°C where the α-amylases break 
down starch to maltose, dextrin, etc. [Eßlinger 2009; Kunze 2010]. The last step of the process is to 
remove the malt and raise the temperature to 100°C, thereafter adding hops. The high temperature 
deactivates the enzymes [Eßlinger 2009; Kunze 2010]. At the boiling step, hops are used to give a bitter 
flavor and has traditionally been used to prolong the shelf life of beer as it has antibacterial an effect 
[Eßlinger 2009; Kunze 2010]. 
After the hops boiling, the wort is cooled down, and yeast is added to beer so that alcohol can be 
produced from the sugars [Kunze 2010]. 
The beer industry is ever growing larger and one of the most wasted byproducts are the Brewer Spent 
Grain(BSG), which is the leftover malt after the mashing step. This byproduct is suitable for human 
consumption, but most is used as animal feed. Each year approximately 40 million tons of BSG are 
produced where most of it is used as animal feed [Lynch et al. 2016a; Qiu et al. 2019]. Every time 100 
liter of beer is produced, 20 kilos of BSG is produced [Lynch et al. 2016b]. 
Brewers Spent Grain 
BSG is a byproduct which can cost money for the brewery to dispose of. If there is no farmer in the 
local area that can use it for animal feed. If a brewery has a local farmer with livestock a small fee can 
be paid to the brewery but usually it is free for the farmer [Bolwig et al. 2019]. 
BSG is highly nutritious, with a high amount of important nutrients, such as proteins and fiber, while 
having a low amount of carbohydrates. This is due to the carbohydrates being removed from the grain 
during the mashing. Studies has shown that BSG contains approximately 4% protein and 80% water 
after brewing [S. I. Mussatto et al. 2006]. The removal of water in the BSG would concentrate the 
nutrients and give the breweries a valuable byproduct [S. I. Mussatto et al. 2006]. The biggest obstacle

With regards to finances a company can get approximately 1 US dollar per kilo of dried BSG, but if the

Dried BSG could contain up to 30% protein, as seen in Figure 1, which would make BSG a valued 
appliance in other foods [Bolwig et al. 2019; S. I. Mussatto et al. 2006]. Dry BSG could be applied to 
protein bars as a “filler” or be used as a meat substitute. If milled, flour of BSG needs another type of 
flour to stick together if made into bread or cereal [S. I. Mussatto et al. 2006]. BSG has a huge potential 
in the baking industry as a substitute for 10 – 15% of the flour, as this would add nutrients and complex 
flavors [S. I. Mussatto et al. 2006]. BSG has also been applied as components in building bricks and for 
the manufacture of paper [S. I. Mussatto et al. 2006]. 
BSG pressed by use of the Dacofi® filter press fractions could all be used, the liquid fraction after 
centrifugation could be reintroduced to the wort or used instead of ordinary water in baking. The 
filtercake and the sediment after centrifugation could be used as flour or biogas. 
Protein in brewers spent grain 
Proteins are macromolecules that are composed of chains of amino acids and are vital for living 
organisms. Soluble proteins originate from the endosperm of the grain, whereas insoluble proteins 
originate from the husk of the grain [Lynch et al. 2016a]. The particle size of proteins varies from tens 
of nanometers to hundreds of microns [Ripple & Dimitrova n.d.]. If the amino acids in the protein have 
hydrophilic properties, the protein will be soluble in water, and vice versa if the amino acids have 
hydrophobic properties the protein will aggregate in the wort thus forming haze [Ripple & Dimitrova 
n.d.]. 
The protein from the barley grains before malting constitutes approximately 12 % and can be 
categorized into four groups: water-, alcohol-, salt- and alkali-soluble proteins. The water-soluble 
proteins are called albumins and will not be present in significant quantities in the BSG as it will 
dissolve in the wort. The salt-soluble proteins are called globulins and will also not be present in the 
BSG as they dissolve if salt is added to the brewing water. The albumin and globulin contain a high 
amount of the essential amino acids lysin, tryptophan and methionine [Arendt & Zannini 2013; Hansen 
2010]. As most breweries add brewing salts before the mashing step these will be in the wort. The 
alcohol-soluble proteins, prolamins, which are called hordein for barley. Hordein affects people with 
celiac disease or gluten intolerance. Hordein contains a high amount of the amino acids glutamine and 
proline [Shewry & Halford 2002]. The hordein will degrade during malting and will thus not be present 
in BSG [Ikram et al. 2017]. The last group of proteins in the barley is the alkali-soluble proteins, these 
are called glutenin. Glutenins from barley are called hordeum and will remain in the BSG. Hordeum 
contains a high amount of the essential amino acids phenylalanine, valine and leucine with the 
addition of proline and tyrosine [Arendt & Zannini 2013; EWART 1967].

Dacofi® Filter press 
The Dacofi® filter machine is a downstream processing machine (DSP), which fractionates solid and 
liquid parts of a medium. The medium is introduced through a hole in the top of the machine by an 
inlet feeding system which presses the medium into the “pressing chamber”. The “pressing chamber” 
consists of four rotating cylinders, two rubber rollers and two filter rollers. The rollers work in pairs of 
a rubber and a filter roller rotates in against each other, thereby pressing the medium between the 
two rollers. The machine fractionates the liquid fraction from the solid fraction by pressure generated 
from the feed inlet and the two rollers. Thereby letting the liquid fraction pass through the filter roller 
to an individual outlet in each filter roller. The solid fraction will be collected from the roller after it 
has exited the “pressing chamber”, as seen in Figure 4. The filter rollers used in the machine have 
different pore sizes one having 100 and the other 300 microns, which gives two liquid fraction and 
two different solid fractions [Meister 2016; Stubbe & Hansen 2013]. This gives a total of four fractions 
This machine gives the opportunity to concentrate nutrients in the medium, without the use of heat 
which could denature some of the nutrients and is energy intensive [Meister 2016].

Distance between rollers which changes the pressure of the rollers on the medium. 
•

•

Protein determination 
To evaluate the protein in the different fractions in the processed BSG, different analytical methods 
were utilized, these methods are described in detail below. 
Dumas combustion method – Total protein 
The Dumas method analyzes the protein percentage in a given sample by measuring the total nitrogen 
content and multiplying with a protein-nitrogen conversion factor, which for barley is 5.83 [DTU 
Foedevareinstituttet 2019; Liquide & Systems 2016]. 
The principle behind the Dumas protein determination method is detailed in three steps. The first step 
is combustion, where the sample is heated to 800°C, which releases nitrogen bound in the sample. 
The released nitrogen will react with pure oxygen and produce two nitrogen oxides. The second step 
is the reduction and separation step, where the nitrogen oxides are reduced to nitrogen gas and other 
gasses and water are separated from the nitrogen. The separated gases are absorbed in absorption 
column [DTU Foedevareinstituttet 2019; Liquide & Systems 2016]. 
Bradford assay – Soluble protein 
The Bradford assay was used to measure the amount of soluble protein in the liquid fraction of the 
BSG. The Bradford assay uses a colorimetric color change at 595 nm wavelength in the sample to 
measure soluble protein amount. The change occurs when the Coomassie dye and the proteins in the 
sample react, thus creating a color change. The higher soluble protein content present in the solution

Sugar content 
The sugar content in the sample was measured in degree Plato which describes the percentage of 
sucrose in a solution. Thus, a value of 14 °Bx is 14 grams of sucrose in a 100 gram suspension. 14 grams 
are usually enough for a beer fermentation.

Materials and Methods 
This section describes the materials, sample preparations and the analytical methods used in the 
thesis. 
Raw BSG 
BSG was primarily collected from the brewery Braunstein, with different recipes for each experiment. 
The BSG for the first experiment was collected from DTU Pilot Brew. The BSG from Braunstein was 
collected in buckets which could accommodated around 50 kg each. Braunstein brewery is located in 
Køge which is 50 km from DTU, which makes transport a factor. As the samples can lose temperature, 
which could effect the results of the press 
Dacofi® Filter press 
The settings which could be changed on the Dacofi® filter press was the distance between the filter 
roller and the rubber roller, roller RPM and Feed RPM as described in the section above. 
The distance between the filter roller and the rubber roller, which affects the pressure was estimated 
to a standard of 0.1 mm and to test the distance effect it was decreased by approximately 1 mm. 
The roller RPM affects the buildup of BSG and capacity of the pressing. The standard speed the roller 
was estimated to was 3.3 RPM, and was changed to an estimated 7.7 RPM to test the effect. 
The feed speed affects the pressure in the pressing chamber and standard setting was an estimated 
50 hertz and at testing it was estimated to 25 hertz. 
The Dacofi® filter press has filter pores of 100 and 300 microns. The new Larson filter press was 
programmed to have the same settings as the Dacofi® but has filter pores of 500 and 1000 microns. 
Sampling 
For each experiment setting, samples were taken from each step. One 100 g sample were taken of the 
malt used in the experiment in the right ration. Six samples were taken from each 50 kg bucket with 
BSG, two each from the top, middle and bottom to ensure sampling was equally distributed. Six 
samples were taken from the filtercake, one from the 100/500 micron filter and one from 300/1000 
micron filter, from the start, middle and end of each bucket that was pressed. Six samples were taken 
from the filtrated liquid, one from the 100/500 micron filtrated liquid and one from 300/1000 micron 
filtrated liquid, from start, middle and end of each bucket that was pressed. The two filtrated liquid 
fractions(100/500 and 300/1000 micron) was then centrifuged separately for 20 minutes at 8000 RPM, 
two samples were taken from both of the 100/500 and 300/1000 micron filters, one from eluent and 
one from the sediment. To see an illustration of the sampling see Figure 5.

Sample preparation 
After sampling the samples were cooled down to around 4°C and then frozen at -40°C. When the 
samples had to be analyzed they were thawed to 4°C for 1-2 days to ensure homogeneity in the 
sample. 
Drying 
Before the samples could be used in DUMAS combustion method, dry matter and ash determination, 
the samples needed to be dried. The sample size was approximately 5 g for solid samples and 25 g for 
liquid samples. To remove the moisture in the samples the samples where heated to 90 °C for an 
estimated 48 hours or until no weight difference was noted. This was done to decrease deuteriation 
of protein.

Analytical methods 
This section will focus on the analytical methods used to analyze the properties of the BSG and the 
fractions after pressing 
Dumas combustion method – Total protein 
The Dumas combustion method was used according to the analysis regulation number 202.01 from 
The Department of Food Technology at DTU National Food Institute dated 6th of May 2019. 
Combustion temperature was set to 800 °C and the method “Cereal” was used for measurement. 
Samples where dried before analysis and were approximately 0.5 grams of dry weight. Protein 
conversion factor of 5.83 was used according to the analysis regulation. 
Apparatus used for DUMAS protein determination was “rapid N exceed” from Elementar. 
Bradford assay – Soluble protein 
Soluble protein analysis was done after drying according to the Coomassie Plus (Bradford) Assay Kit 
from Thermo Scientific manual. The assay was done as a standard test tube protocol with bovine 
serum albumin. The colormetric color change was measured at a Genesys 10S UV-Vis from Thermo 
Scientific at 595 nm. 
Brix 
The sugar content was measure on a digital refractometer, refractometer 30PX from Mettler Toledo. 
A few drops of the thawed sample were placed in the measuring cell, and the reading was taken.  
EBC 
The method used was described in the Analytica-EBC 8.5 
Color of the liquid samples were measured on a Genesys 10S UV-Vis from Thermo Scientific at 430 nm 
in a 10 mm cell. The measurement was done after separation of the liquid and the solid fraction in the 
liquid samples. A blank sample of demineralize water was used. To determine the EBC the equation 
mentioned before was utilized. 
Free Amino Nitrogen 
The method used was described in the Analytica-EBC 8.10 
The sample was diluted 1:100 and 2 mL diluted sample was selected and mixed with 1 mL color 
mixture(100 g disodium hydrogen phosphate, 60 g potassium dihydrogen phosphate, 5 g ninhydrin 
and 3 g fructose diluted to 1 L in water) and then placed in a 100 °C water  bath for 16 minutes and 
cooled afterwards in a 20 °C water bath for 20 minutes. 2 mL diluting solution ( 2 g potassium ionate, 
600 mL water and 400 mL 96 % ethanol) was added to the mixture and the absorbance was measured 
at 595 nm. A blank cell was made with the same ration of components but with water instead of 
sample. A standard (0.1072 g glycine) was used in the measurement and was treated the same but 
without addition of sample. 
Ash 
To determine the amount of ash in the sample, the sample was combusted at 500 °C for and estimated 
8 hours in an oven. After this, the sample was cooled to approximately 35 °C, and hereafter placed in 
a desiccator until it was weighed.

Discussion 
The lossof product from the pressing was later discovered to be caused by the pumps as they were 
empty at the start of pressing and thus at the end of pressing had filled up with leftover liquid in it. 
This could be seen as the liquid fraction was not extracted from the pumps before a significant amount 
of BSG was pressed. This liquid was then not measured as pressed liquid. 
The majority of the pressed BSG goes through the 300 fraction. This could be reasoned with that the 
300 micron filter is the path of least resistance. It should also be noted that even though more is 
pressed through the 300 micron filter, there are no significant difference between the dry matter 
content for both filtrate and filtercake for 100 and 300 microns filters, as can be seen in Table 2. 
It can be observed that most of the total protein is concentrated in the solid fraction of the pressed 
BSG and that there is no real difference found in the different filters. This shows that most of the 
proteins are insoluble, as they are not majorly present in the filtrate. The total protein from un-pressed 
BSG to pressed filtercake increases the total protein content with about a 40 % increase, from 5.7 % 
to 7.5 %. If the pressed BSG is utilized as flour the total protein content would be an estimated 20 %. 
The protein in the filtrate will be found in the solid fraction after centrifugation in both filters and in 
approximately the same percentage as the filtercake. If the solid fraction of the centrifugated filtrate 
was dried it would have a total protein content of an estimated 35 %.

The soluble sugar is mostly present in the wort where it is wanted for the fermentation. Although the

Conclusion 
This thesis researched the fractionation of BSG from two different breweries. There were two fractions 
of solid material and two fractions of liquid. These were obtained using a Dacofi® filter machine, first 
with 100 and 300 micron filters and then with 500 and 1000 micron filters. An array of different 
settings were investigated to optimized the effectiveness of pressing of BSG. These settings were then 
utilized on 7 different types of BSG.  
There was not found changes in the experiments that could be attributed to the parameters changed 
on the filter press. The significant changes that were observed, could mostly be contributed to the 
soluble protein and ash content. However, these changes were not consistent. It was seen that the 
BSG from the different experiment were different from each other, but it was also observed that BSG 
from the same experiment differed from each other. They differed slightly in total protein and dry 
matter and significantly in the ash and soluble protein. These differences were attributed to the 
natural variances in the BSG. Some changes in the 500 and 1000 micron filters had been observed: 
they had less dry matter than the 100 and 300 micron filters. 
It was observed that after the pressing in the Dacofi® filter press that the filtercake would increase by 
1 % in total protein content and 10 % dry matter. After pressing in the new filter press with 500 and 
1000 micron filters, it was observed that the filtercake increased 1 % in total protein and 8 % in dry 
matter. 
Calculations show that if the BSG, filtercake and filtrate sediment were dried, the protein content 
would be approximately 20 %, 20 % and 34 % respectively. Therefore, it can be concluded that the 
pressing of the BSG does not increase the total protein content in the dried product but lowers the 
energy required to dry the product, which could be beneficial from an economic standpoint. 
After the experiments it was discovered that the scale used for weighing the samples was not 
calibrated correctly, this could have affected the results. When the BSG was pressed, the liquid came 
out later as it had to travel through the pipes and pumps. This made it hard to know when to take 
samples. If the sampling is done too quickly it might not be the representative for the trial, since the 
sample would belong to the previous trial. 
In the last experiment, experiment 7, it was observed that there were significant differences in the 
500 and 1000 micron filtrate that was pressed in the Dacofi® filter press, although these differences 
were not present in both the trials. To get a realistic depiction of the distribution in the double 
pressing, more trials are needed with a higher amount of product. 
The use of filtrate liquid added to the wort, to increase the yield of fermentation, was found to not be 
recommended due  to the low concentrations of free amino nitrogen and sugar, which would hinder 
the fermentation.
