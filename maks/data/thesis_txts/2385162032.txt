Abstract 
An important bottleneck to a fast drilling progress, is the use of oil-based mud (OBM) in 
shallow sections (17-½" and 12-¼"), as cuttings contaminated by OBM cannot be discharged 
to sea. This is causing large amounts of cuttings handling, which triggers higher cost and 
logistical issues.  
 
The main reason for using OBM in these shallow sections is due to its superior inhibitive 
properties regarding borehole stability issues with reactive clay. Some clay minerals swell 
when in contact with fresh water, and this can cause problems when drilling with water-based 
mud (WBM). These problems will often have a great impact on the rate of penetration. 
However, most WBMs are disposable, and no cuttings handling onshore is required. 
 
In this thesis it is discussed whether the technical disadvantages of WBM can be counteracted 
by lower cost and fewer cuttings handling issues. A cost analysis was done on a typical 1000 
m long 17 ½" section. The result shows that if the drilling progress is slower with WBM than 
OBM, requiring additional rig time, it would still be cost beneficial for up to 18 additional 
hours. 
 
Case studies were carried out to emphasize the practical differences between WBM and 
OBM, and address potential for improvements with WBM. The results show that friction can 
be an important one as well as the inhibitive properties.  
 
Another set of case studies were carried out to see if there is a connection between the 
inhibitive KCl concentration used in WBM and borehole stability issues with respect to the 
amount of smectite present in the lower part of the Hordaland Group in the North Sea. The 
results show that in general a good inhibition was achieved with KCl concentrations above 
150 kg/m3.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
The falling oil prices have forced today’s oil and gas companies to be more efficient and 
reduce operational costs. Drilling and well operations are very costly and connected to high 
rig rates. Thus, Statoil wants to investigate the main bottlenecks to a fast drilling progress. An 
important one is the use of oil-based mud (OBM) in shallow sections (17-½" and 12-¼"), as 
cuttings contaminated by OBM cannot be discharged to sea. This is causing large amounts of 
cuttings handling, which triggers higher cost and logistical issues.  
 
The main reason for using OBM in these shallow sections is due to its superior inhibitive 
properties regarding borehole stability issues with reactive clay. Some clay minerals swell 
when in contact with fresh water, and this can cause problems when drilling with water-based 
mud (WBM). These problems will often have a great impact on the rate of penetration (ROP), 
and in a worst case scenario it may lead to borehole instability and abandonment of the well. 
However, if OBM can be replaced successfully by WBM in more wells with the drilling 
progress maintained, or even increased, a lot of money can be saved and the environmental 
impact can be reduced. Most WBMs are disposable, and no cuttings handling onshore is 
required. 
 
1.2 Problem definition 
A part of Statoil’s dilemma with respect to the choice between WBM and OBM relates to the 
potential for ROP. For some rigs the ROP with OBM is limited by the cuttings handling 
capacity. Increasing the capacity is very costly, possibly in the 50-100 MNOK range, and is 
not physically possible on small rigs. This is not a good solution since many of the rigs are 
rented and have a short scope and time to pay back investments. 
 
This thesis will discuss whether the technical disadvantages of WBM can be counteracted by 
lower cost and fewer cuttings handling issues. It will also be discussed whether some of the 
most important technical differences can be reduced without compromising the major 
advantage it is to use a dischargeable WBM. 
 
The practical differences between OBM and WBM are investigated with respect to the 
borehole stability and other factors affecting the ROP. From experience it has been seen that 
friction may be an important one. The thesis looks into how WBM can be utilized to reduce 
the overall drilling cost and environmental impact. The goal is to achieve higher ROP with 
dischargeable WBM, while saving cuttings- and waste handling cost.

2 Literature review 
2.1 Shale formations 
Borehole instability is a major concern during drilling. The consequence might be mud loss, 
“tight hole”, “stuck pipe” or lost hole sections. A major reason for being stuck is mechanical 
collapse of the borehole. Most instabilities of practical importance occur in shale or 
mudstone, predominantly in the overburden. Mechanical hole collapse is often combined with 
insufficient hole cleaning. Such stability problems amount to typically 5-10% of drilling 
costs, incorporating loss of time and sometimes also of equipment. Based on these numbers, 
there is an estimated cost to the petroleum industry worldwide of hundreds of million dollars 
per year (Fjær et al., 2008). 
 
In recent years, there has been an increasing demand for highly deviated, multilateral and 
horizontal wells, since a single production platform then can drain a larger area. However, 
stable drilling is normally more difficult in deviated than in vertical boreholes. Borehole 
stability problems often occur in shales that are rich in swelling clay minerals, and are often 
associated with abnormally high pore pressure. The swelling clay can be treated by chemical 
additives (e.g. salt) in the drilling mud.  
 
In the subsequent chapters a literature study is done on shale to give a better understanding of 
how shale formations affect borehole stability during drilling. 
 
2.1.1 Mineralogy 
Shale is a fine-grained, sedimentary rock consisting of silt- and clay-size mineral particles. 
These particles are clastic sediments, formed by erosion of older rocks and transported by 
water, wind or ice to their place of deposition (e.g. lakes or coastal areas), where they become 
compacted and may eventually solidify into shale. Shales are often found with layers of 
sandstone or limestone, and most of the shale occur in extensive sheets several meters thick 
(Britannica Academic, 2017). 
 
Shales are characterized by having a laminated structure and by being fissile, i.e. they exhibit 
a tendency to split into thin layers that are usually parallel to the bedding-plane surface 
(Britannica Academic, 2017). The most important minerals in shaly rocks are clay minerals, 
which are composed of layers of sheet shaped crystals. Within a given sheet, ions different 
from the dominant ones may be present, giving rise to a possible charge deficiency. Usually, 
mainly because of oxygen and hydroxide at the surfaces, these have negative excess charge, 
and will therefore attract cations (Fjær et al., 2008). The cations are loosely held to the surface 
and may be exchanged with pore fluid or mineral inter-layers. The cation exchange capacity 
(CEC) of clay is a measure of the number of cations that are required to neutralize the clay 
particle as a whole (Farrokhrouz and Asef, 2013). 
 
Characteristically, shales consist of at least 30% clay minerals and substantial amounts of 
quartz. The most common groups of clay minerals are kaolinite, illite and smectite. The 
==PAGE:12==
 
4
molecular structure of clay consist of a silica tetrahedron and an alumina octahedron. 
(Britannica Academic, 2017; Fjær et al., 2008). 
 
Kaolinite is a two layer mineral (1:1 ratio), where successive layers are strongly bonded to 
each other with a hydrogen bonding, which leads to low cation adsorption. Therefore water is 
not permitted to enter in between the layers (Fjær et al., 2008), as kaolinite exhibit little or no 
swelling on hydration (Foster, 1954). 
 
 
Figure 2.1 - Kaolinite structure (Farrokhrouz and Asef, 2013) 
Smectite is a group of phyllosilicate clay minerals with high affinity for water. It has a three-
layered crystalline structure consisting of two tetrahedral silica layers with a central 
octahedral alumina sheet (2:1 layer ratio). The layers are continuous in the plane and make a 
big molecular surface, but the bonds between the layers are weak. The net negative charge of 
the lattice, allows water and other molecules to exchange and enter between the layers and 
cause large swelling or shrinking of the clay (Farrokhrouz and Asef, 2013). 
 
 
Figure 2.2 - Smectite structure (Farrokhrouz and Asef, 2013) 
Because of slow temperature-activated (diagenetic) geochemical changes, smectite is 
gradually changed to illite. This transition requires temperatures of 70-90 °C, corresponding 
to depths between 2500 and 4500 m in areas of average geothermal gradient. This is the 
reason for the presence of more reactive, ductile swelling shales at shallower depths and less 
reactive and more brittle shales at deeper locations with larger in-situ stresses (Farrokhrouz 
and Asef, 2013; Fjær et al., 2008). 
 
Illite has the same basic unit as smectite, but has a greater isomorphous substitution in the 
structural sheets, leading to greater charges on the structural units. However, these charges are 
to a large extent neutralized by cations of potassium, which is fixed and is not exchangeable. 
Some substitution of Al for Si in the tetrahedral layer leads to a permanent net negative 
charge and a medium cation adsorption. Thus, the swelling properties of illite fall between 
==PAGE:13==
 
5
kaolinite and smectite, but are in general much closer to kaolinite (Fjær et al., 2008; Foster, 
1954). 
 
Figure 2.3 - Illite structure (Farrokhrouz and Asef, 2013) 
Shale also contain smaller quantities of carbonates, feldspars, iron oxides, fossils, and organic 
matter. The composition of these small quantities will often determine the color of the rock 
(Britannica Academic, 2017; Geoscience, 2017). 
 
In general, with higher organic content in the shale, the color gets darker. The presence of 
iron hydroxide minerals such as hematite and limonite gives rise to reddish and purple 
colouring, while mineral components rich in ferrous iron impart blue, green, and black hues. 
Calcareous shales are light gray or yellowish (Britannica Academic, 2017). 
 
Physical properties such as permeability and plasticity are largely dependent on the grain 
sizes of the constituent minerals (Britannica Academic, 2017). Because of the abundance of 
clay minerals, pore sizes in shale are very small, and are typically between 5 and 25 nm. The 
nanometer size pores lead to laboratory measured permeabilities in the nano-Darcy range. The 
porosity of shale may vary from only a few percent to quite high up to 70 percent. However, 
the permeability remains very small even with the highest porosities. (Fjær et al., 2008)

2.1.2 Lithostratigraphy  
Sandstones and carbonates make up around 15% and 10%, respectively, of sedimentary rocks 
by volume. Nevertheless, most of the overburden which has to be drilled through to get down 
to the reservoirs in the North Sea consist of shale, which is the most abundant sedimentary 
rock (~75%). These shale sections are often associated with stability problems during drilling 
and are therefore of special interest (Fjær et al., 2008).  
 
Lithostratigraphy is based on the recognition of rock type, and the basic unit is a layer. A 
layer can be characterized by similarity in mineralogy, grain size, color or fossils. For 
geological mapping purposes, single layers commonly are too numerous or too difficult to 
correlate. Layers are henceforth grouped into formations, which represent reasonably uniform 
mappable units. A formation may be correlated over tens of kilometers and may be several 
tens of meters thick. For practical reasons a formation may be subdivided into members. A 
series of formations may constitute a larger unit, called a group (Fabricius, 2001). 
 
In Figure 2.5 and Figure 2.6 the lithostratigraphy of the North Sea and the Norwegian Sea are 
presented, showing how shale (clay) dominates in the overburden in the Nordland-, 
Hordaland- and Rogaland Group. The following descriptions of the groups are adapted from 
the Norwegian Petroleum Directorate fact pages (NPD, 2017), and with additional references 
specified. 
 
Nordland Group 
The group is distributed over most of the North Sea Tertiary Basin, and is dominated by 
marine claystones. These are grey, sometimes greenish-grey and grey-brown, soft, locally 
silty and micaceous. The sandy Utsira Formation occurs in the lower part of the group in the 
Viking Graben area. The Utsira Formation is the only formation presently defined in the 
Nordland Group in the North Sea, whereas in the Norwegian Sea the group consist of the Kai-
, Molo-, and Naust Formation.

2.1.3 Wellbore stability challenges 
The time associated with unexpected borehole stability problems is estimated to account for 
10-15% of the time required to drill a well. Since the rig time is the major cost factor in a 
drilling operation, the wellbore stability problems are very costly. The main challenges 
encountered may be unexpected circulation losses that take time to cure, and problems 
associated with borehole collapse (Aadnøy, 2010). The failure mechanisms are mechanical 
collapse (shear/compressive failure) and fracturing (tensile failure) (Aadnøy and Looyeh, 
2011), as illustrated in Figure 2.8. 
 
 
Figure 2.8 - Failure mechanisms in shale (Fjær et al., 2008) 
Borehole collapse happens when the formation near the borehole fails mechanically, due to 
underbalanced mud weight and too high stresses around the wall. When drilling in reactive 
shales with a water based mud with potassium chloride (KCl) additive (the reason for using 
KCl will be further discussed in Chapter 2.1.5), a primary response is dehydration and 
shrinkage of the clay structure. This will reduce the compressive stresses around the borehole 
and have a positive effect on the borehole stability. However, shrinkage may eventually result 
in generation of tensile stresses within the formation, especially when using very high KCl 
concentrations and/or if there is an extensive period of exposure time. Tensile stresses can 
induce tensile failure and lead to breakouts. Thus, very high KCl concentrations can 
contribute to destabilize the shale. An optimum KCl-concentration appears to exist for the 
stability of each individual case. It is very challenging to determine this concentration, as it 
will depend on a number of parameters (in situ stresses and pore pressure, shale minerology, 
permeability, diffusion coefficient, strength, stiffness, borehole pressure, other drilling fluid 
constituents, open hole time etc.) (Horsrud et al., 1998). 
 
Nearly 90% of the wellbore instability problems in the petroleum industry occur in shale. 
These problems include rock mechanics, chemical reactivity and swelling impact on 
mechanical behavior, and thermal stimulation (Farrokhrouz and Asef, 2013). When drilling 
with an aqueous drilling fluid in shale, water will tend to react with the formation and change 
the mechanical properties of the rock, and may cause formation damage and borehole 
instability. The failure can be seen as cavings or sloughing shale (washout), resulting in hole 
enlargement, bridges and fill (Ouadfeul and Leila, 2015). This can cause a tight hole if the 
==PAGE:20==
 
12
cuttings or cavings pack off around the drill string, as seen in Figure 2.9. In worst case the 
drill string might get stuck if the circulating fluid is not able to clean the hole.  
 
 
Figure 2.9 – Packing off while pulling out (Statoil ASA, 2017) 
As mentioned earlier, the laminated structure of shale leads to anisotropy. The strength 
anisotropy is likely to influence borehole failure, in particular for deviated holes since the 
bedding planes will represent possible planes of weakness (Fjær et al., 2008). Thus, shear 
failure will change with inclination.  
 
The most common and serious problem in shales is the swelling (clay hydration) that 
frequently happens in soft reactive shales and causes reduction in hole diameter (Farrokhrouz 
and Asef, 2013). This requires time consuming reaming and may give difficulties running 
casing. The chemical interaction between the drilling fluid and shale may also alter the stress 
state and enhance shale instability. This problem will be further discussed in Chapter 2.1.5. 
 
Gumbo is the name of very soft (plastic) shale, which is often sticky and contains 
considerable amounts of swelling minerals, and may cause problems like bit balling 
(accretion of clay on the bit), solids accumulation and drillstring sticking (Fjær et al., 2008). 
 
Whenever the driller experience high torque and drag, it is referred to as a tight spot. Also, 
non-ideal well trajectories and too sharp “dog legs” may contribute. Thus, it can be many 
reasons for failures causing tight holes. 
 
Lost circulation is another challenge that can be experienced while drilling. If the mud 
pressure exceeds the formation fracture pressure, it can lead to tensile failure and fracture of 
the formation rock, allowing mud to be lost into the formation. Mud loss can also occur when 
drilling with overbalance into weak permeable formations with high porosity (such as shallow 
sandstones), natural fractures and faults, or cavernous and vugular formations (Mitchell and 
Miska, 2011). The mud loss may also lead to a temporary pressure drop in the well, and pore 
==PAGE:21==
 
13
fluid may flow into the well from permeable layers higher up. This may lead to a rapid 
increase in well pressure (“kick”) and a risk of a blowout (Fjær et al., 2008).  
 
A high mud weight is dictated by pore pressure and shear failure gradient (collapse pressure). 
The shear failure gradient is controlled by well inclination, pore pressure and formation 
strength. Estimates of the unconfined compressive strength (UCS) obtained from sonic log is 
often poorly calibrated, and conservative (i.e. high shear failure prediction)(de Lesquen, 
2017). Nevertheless, the high mud weight must not exceed the fracture gradient. An example 
of the allowable mud weight range, called the mud weight window, is shown in Figure 2.10. 
 
 
Figure 2.10 - Mud weight window and common borehole problems (Aadnøy, 2010) 
However, even when the mud weight is far less than what is required to fracture the 
formation, lost circulation can still result from a high equivalent circulating density (ECD). 
The ECD equals the static mud weight plus a term proportional to the friction pressure drop in 
the annulus. Excessive wellbore pressures can be caused by high flow rates or surge effects 
when tripping too fast (Fjær et al., 2008; Mitchell and Miska, 2011). The mud’s rheological 
properties (viscosity, yield point, and gel strength) should be specified to maintain its desired 
cuttings suspension and transport properties, but at the same time it should enable the well to 
be circulated at an optimal pump pressure (Mitchell and Miska, 2011).

2.1.5 Chemical instability 
As previously discussed, the chemical-induced shale instability caused by the drilling-
fluid/shale interaction is a major concern regarding borehole stability and drilling progress. 
The flux of water and ions into or out of shale alters the stress state through pore pressure, and 
is considered to be the primary reason for shale instability (Farrokhrouz and Asef, 2013). 
Increasing pore pressure may destabilize a rock with respect to shear and tensile failure (Fjær 
et al., 2008). In this chapter the chemical mechanisms behind it will be discussed.  
 
Capillary pressure 
When the mud in the borehole contacts the pore fluid in the shale through the pore-throat 
interface, it will develop a capillary pressure. OBM usually gives better stability than WBM, 
which can be explained by the example of drilling with a fluid that is completely non-wetting 
to shale, such as pure oil. The capillary pressure for oil (or a non-wetting fluid) in contact 
with a water-wet shale is expressed as (Fjær et al., 2008):

OBMs are however not pure oils, but contain a water phase which may have an influence on

impermeable borehole wall conditions over time, such as sodium silicate or long-chained 
molecules (glycol, polymers, etc.) that are thought to build molecular filter cakes on shale 
surface (Fjær et al., 2008). 
 
Osmosis 
Experimental results by Mody & Hale (Chen et al., 2001) proved that initial shale pore 
pressure was altered due to chemical effects developing with time, caused by osmotic forces. 
Osmosis is described as the flow of water from a dilute solution to a more concentrated 
solution through a semi-permeable membrane. It is driven by chemical potential differences 
between the water on either side of the membrane (Cheryan, 1998). The presence of a semi-
permeable membrane is essential for osmosis to occur, since the membrane permits water 
(solvent) molecules to pass while restricting the solutes and ions to enter. An ideal semi-
permeable membrane is one that only allows the water to pass through and prevents ion 
movement. Shale does not act as an ideal semi-permeable membrane as it is leaky and does 
not fully reject ions. A measure of how well shales can prevent ion movement is called 
membrane efficiency (Farrokhrouz and Asef, 2013). The process of osmosis is illustrated in

pressure, overcomes in situ vertical and horizontal stresses, and any cementation bonds 
holding the mineral particles together (Wilson and Wilson, 2014).

the clay. One way to reduce the smectite from swelling is by adding chemicals such as KCl to

2013). KCl treatment is effective because the native interlayer sodium (Na) or calcium (Ca)

2014). The potassium ions goes in and "blocks" the water molecules from being attached to 
the crystalline structure. Also, shrinkage happens because of the size of the potassium ion, 
which fits more easily into the clay mineral crystal lattice than the native sodium ion.  
 
Apparently, KCl exposure enhances plasticity, which is beneficial for borehole stability. The 
shrinkage effect leads to improved stability with respect to the lower mud weight limit, but if 
the chemical strain is high, tensile or shear failure may be induced (Fjær et al., 2008).

2.2 Drilling fluids 
Drilling fluids, also called drilling mud, are multicomponent water- or oil based fluids that are 
used for drilling and completion of wells. Each type of fluid has different benefits and 
disadvantages, which are usually determined by cost, technical performance and 
environmental impact. More than one fluid system is normally used when drilling the same 
well, as it will face different formations, and challenges due to increasing pressure and 
temperatures downhole. Thus, some functions of the drilling fluids may vary, but the basic 
functions are the same for most oil-well drilling operations.  
 
2.2.1 Functions 
The drilling fluid is used to execute and facilitate vital functions in the drilling process, such 
as hole cleaning, formation pressure control, powering downhole tools, sealing permeable 
formations, and cooling, lubrication and support of the bit and drilling assembly. It also 
enables mud-pulse telemetry from logging instruments and cuttings analysis, and provides 
corrosion inhibition.  
 
In addition to its functions, drilling fluids should minimize formation damage and maintain 
wellbore stability. Damage, such as formation swelling and washouts, may be reduced by 
chemical additives and choosing the right mud weight. 
 
The following descriptions of the drilling fluid functions are adapted from a drilling fluid 
company presentation (M-I SWACO, 2016). 
 
 
Figure 2.12 - Key drilling fluid functions (M-I SWACO, 2016) 
 
Hole cleaning 
The viscosity, density and annular velocity of the drilling fluid are important parameters for 
removing cuttings from the wellbore. Cuttings removal is also a function of cuttings size, 
shape and density, ROP and drillstring rotation. In large hole sections the annular velocity can 
be low and insufficient to clean the hole. In these cases, high viscosities and sometimes high 
==PAGE:28==
 
20
weight must be used. The drilling fluid must suspend drill cuttings, weight materials and 
additives, and allow cuttings to be removed by the solids control equipment. 
 
Pressure control 
The density of the drilling fluid (mud weight) is balanced to control the formation pressures 
and stresses. The wellbore pressure should be greater than the pore pressure and the shear 
failure gradient (collapse pressure), and less than the fracture gradient to maintain wellbore 
stability. If not, problems such as influx of formation fluid (kick and blowout), lost 
circulation, hole instability or stuck pipe may occur. 
 
Seal permeable formations 
The differential pressure will try to force the drilling fluid into the formation. To limit the 
invasion of mud filtrate into the formation, the drilling fluids are designed to deposit a thin, 
low permeability filter cake (also known as mud cake) on the borehole wall. Special additives 
such as bridging and plugging material are added to improve the cake quality. 
 
Cooling, lubrication and support 
Circulation of the drilling fluid cools and lubricates the bit and drilling assembly, which 
reduces friction and heat generation. The drilling fluid also supports the drilling assembly 
through buoyancy, reducing the hook load at the derrick.  
 
Powering downhole tools 
The flow of the drilling fluid is used to power mud motors or electrical tools through power 
generation by downhole turbines.

and rotational viscometer. (Bourgoyne et al., 1986) 
 
The Marsh funnel viscometer is a rapid test of the consistency of a drilling fluid. The test 
consists essentially of filling the funnel with a mud sample and then measuring the time 
required for 1 quart (946 ml) of the sample to flow into a mud cup. The funnel viscosity is 
reported in units of seconds per quart, or in SI units as seconds per liter. (Bourgoyne et al., 
1986) 
 
Since the flow rate varies throughout this test, it cannot give a true viscosity for drilling fluids 
which are non-Newtonian. It will only serve as a quality check, indicating changes in the mud 
viscosity (Hossain and Al-Majed, 2015). 
 
The rotational viscometer gives a more meaningful measure of viscosity than the Marsh 
funnel. It measures the drag caused by relative motion of the fluid and a surface. The mud is 
sheared at a constant rate between a rotating outer sleeve and an inner bob. The test is carried 
out at different standard speeds, and sometimes only for 600 rpm and 300 rpm. The 
dimensions of the bob and rotor are chosen so that the dial reading is equal to the apparent 
Newtonian viscosity in centipoise at a rotor speed of 300 rpm. The apparent viscosity, &), in 
centipoise at any other rotor speeds is then given by:

A third non-Newtonian rheological parameter is called the gel strength, which is the shear

(Bourgoyne et al., 1986).  
 
Most drilling fluids are colloids, and tend to form gelled structures when stagnant and liquefy 
when sheared. Gel strength is a measure of the ability of the drilling fluid to develop and 
retain a gel form based on its resistance to shear. It determines the fluids ability to hold solids 
in suspension. (Hossain and Al-Majed, 2015) 
 
pH 
The pH of a drilling fluid is a measure of the acidity of the mixing water. The acidity of a 
drilling fluid can be measured by the concentration of hydrogen ions in the fluid, and is 
defined by: 
 
,p = −log [ps] 
 
where  [ps] is the hydrogen ion concentration in moles per liter. Since the product  
 {[ps][up_]} must remain constant for any aqueous solution, an increase in [ps] requires a 
corresponding decrease in [up_]. Therefore, a solution in which [up_] > [ps] is said to be 
alkaline, and a solution in which [ps] > [up_] is said to be acidic. The pH of the drilling 
fluid affects the rate of mud mixing, borehole stability, mud properties, corrosiveness, 
viscosity, gel development, and filtration control. The desired pH in drilling mud is generally 
8.5 to 9.5, which will keep the rate of corrosion at an acceptable level. A higher pH should be 
==PAGE:33==
 
25
avoided, because it is detrimental to most organic additives and causes flocculation of clay 
(Hossain and Al-Majed, 2015). 
 
Filtration 
The filtration properties of a fluid determine its ability to form a filter cake on the borehole 
wall. The filter cake is formed from the solid constituents in the drilling fluid and should be 
thin and smooth, while controlling the loss of liquid from the drilling fluid due to filtration 
(Hossain and Al-Majed, 2015). 
 
Sand content 
The sand content should be kept at a low level by the solids control equipment, due to its 
abrasiveness on pumps, pipe connections and tools.  
 
Liquid and solids content 
The liquid and solid content of a drilling fluid should be known for better control of the mud 
properties. The mud may require to be conditioned by the addition of liquid, chemicals or 
removal of contaminants. In OBM, the oil-water ratio is also measured to have proper control 
of the oil emulsion (Hossain and Al-Majed, 2015). Furthermore, the electrical stability is 
measured to describe the quality of the emulsion. 
 
Water activity 
The water activity changes with the salinity of the water in the mud, and is a way to control 
movement of water into or out of shales. 
 
Water Hardness 
Water hardness of drilling fluid is primarily due to calcium (Ca2+) and magnesium (Mg2+) 
ions present in the water. Calcium usually enters the water from either limestone and chalk, 
which are composed of calcium carbonate (CaCO3), or calcium sulfate (CaSO4). The primary 
source of magnesium is dolomite (CaMg(CO3)2). As the drilling fluid circulates, water 
dissolves the minerals when in contact with the formation (Hossain and Al-Majed, 2015). The 
water hardness can have a negative effect on the lubricity of the fluid.

2.2.3 Friction 
One of the most important functions of drilling fluids, is to lubricate the drill string and 
reduce the mechanical friction generated between the drill string and the casing or the open 
borehole. Drilling fluids with higher lubricity can reduce torque and drag problems and 
minimize stick and slip concerns. The requirement for drilling fluids with high lubricity 
increase in highly inclined wellbores such as extended reach drilling (ERD) wells. Reducing 
mechanical friction will improve drilling efficiency in general, and will enable longer reach 
for ERD wells (Taghipour et al., 2014). 
 
 
Figure 2.14 - Torque and drag (Bennetzen et al., 2010) 
When drilling inclined and horizontal wells the drillpipe is forced against the sides of the 
wellbore, generating friction. Torque and drag will develop as illustrated in Figure 2.14. 
Torque and drag is a measure of the resistance to rotation and upward or downward 
movement, respectively. It is a function of tension or compression and the area in contact with 
the borehole (Bennetzen et al., 2010). High friction will reduce the weight and surface torque 
transmitted to the bit, which may lead to low rates of penetration, poor tool face control, high 
well tortuosity, short runs, severe drill string and bit wear, and at the same time, could cause 
problems while running casing, liners and completions (Barton et al., 2011; Mitchell et al., 
2015). 
 
Compressional forces resulting from drag can cause the drillpipe to buckle in a lateral 
configuration. As the axial load increases, the pipe may transit to a helical shape, at which 
point downward movement is halted (Bennetzen et al., 2010). However, a full-scale study 
presented by Mitchell and Weltzin (2011) show that lateral buckling was the primary 
==PAGE:35==
 
27
buckling mode upon drillstring lockup. Thus, the contact load from both lateral and helical 
buckling may generate friction forces that eventually halter the downward movement.

Lubricity is the measure of the reduction in friction and wear by a lubricant. Important 
properties of a good lubricant are high viscosity, thermal stability, high boiling point and low 
==PAGE:36==
 
28
freezing point, corrosion retardation and resistance to oxidation. Friction is reduced by 
separating two moving parts by a thin slippery film. The lubricating fluid should be viscous 
enough to provide this separation without being so “sticky” that it requires a great deal of 
energy to shear the fluid and move the parts. If the fluid is too thin, the surfaces will be in 
contact and friction will increase (Petroleum, 2017).  
 
Viscosity of fluids decrease as temperature increase. The viscosity index is an indicator of the 
fluids resistance to change as temperature changes. A high index number is desirable, and it 
increases with the increase of molecular structure length, while branching will decrease it 
(Kania et al., 2015). 
 
It is well known that OBM provide better lubrication compared to WBM. This is because oil 
is non-polar, which means it does not have a positive or negative charge. Oil molecules can 
therefore slide past another easily without any particle attraction or repulsion. Another reason 
for oil being slippery is that oil molecules are planar and only form bonding forces within 
layers. Thus, two layers of oil don’t bond to one another to any great degree. Water however, 
is polar, having a partially negative and positive charge. This makes the water and other polar 
molecules stick to each other (Petroleum, 2017). 
 
The long hydrocarbon chain molecules in oil will give more effective separation between 
solid surfaces. The oil acts as a carrier for boundary lubricant additives which provide an 
active polar end group that will chemically bind to metal surfaces and produce a surface layer 
of oil. This provides boundary lubrication, which reduces the COF (Hirani, 2016). 
 
In WBM, lubricants with additives of fatty-acids, polymers and/or glycols can be added to 
reduce the friction, but may be very costly. These lubricants may have a moderate 
biodegradability and a Norwegian environmental rating of “yellow”. This requires evaluation 
of the product and a permit before it can be discharged to sea on the NCS (OSPAR, 2013).

2.3 WBM 
WBM is a drilling mud in which the continuous phase of the system is water. The 
composition of WBM can be made up of a combination of principal components and 
secondary components. The principal components of WBM are usually brine, fresh- or 
seawater, weighting materials, viscosifiers and fluid loss control. Secondary components can 
be shale inhibitors, lubricants, corrosion inhibitors, lost circulation material, detergents, 
deflocculants and defoamers. 
 
Accordingly, there are many types of water-based mud systems. They can be classified into 
spud mud, inhibitive polymer based WBM and reservoir drill in fluids. However, in order to 
use WBM to drill sections with shale, inhibitive fluids are required. An inhibitive mud is a 
mud with salt or calcium to reduce the hydration of shale. 
 
Inhibitive polymer muds can be classified into different systems such as KCl/glycol system, 
silicate system and high performance-inhibitive system (HPWBM). These muds are WBM 
where polymers are used to control properties such as viscosity, fluid loss control, 
flocculation or deflocculation (thinning), shale inhibition and lubricity. 
 
2.3.1 Composition of inhibitive WBM 
An inhibitive WBM can be made up of a combination of the following components. 
 
Brine 
The brine is typically a solution of potassium chloride (KCl), which among a large amount of 
research effort has been selected as an effective shale hydration inhibitor (Zhong et al., 2013). 
The potassium ion enters the exposed shale section and neutralizes the charge on it. This is 
reducing the water contact with the shale, which improves the shale stabilization (Hossain and 
Al-Majed, 2015). However, to minimize clay swelling and hydration, relatively high 
concentrations of KCl ranging from 2% (~20 kg/m3) to 37% (~424 kg/m3) are required. It is 
highly flocculating and its presence in the mud can adversely affect rheology and filtration 
control.” (Zhong et al., 2013) 
 
Weighting material 
Barite (BaSO4) is the most commonly used weighting agent. 
 
Viscosifiers 
Bentonite clay is the most common drilling fluid additive, and is a widely used viscosity 
builder. However, since inhibitive mud systems (KCl systems) inhibit the hydration of 
bentonite and diminish its effectiveness, polymers from cellulose are used instead. Polymers 
are multi-purpose additives that may simultaneously modify viscosity, control filtration 
properties, stabilize shales and create or prevent clay flocculation. 
 
==PAGE:38==
 
30
Fluid loss control 
Starch and polyanionic cellulose (PAC) are common polymer additives used to reduce fluid 
invasion into the formation. Starches form compressible particles that plug the small openings 
in the filter cake. PAC is a polymer that coats the surface of the clay, which restricts the 
surface fractures or pores, reducing or preventing the capillary movement into the shale. KCl 
enhances the rate of polymer absorption onto the clay (Fink, 2012; Hossain and Al-Majed, 
2015). 
 
Shale inhibitor 
Shale stabilization can be achieved when the additive captures the shale or when a specific 
ion such as potassium enters the shale. Common shale inhibitors are high molecular weight 
polymers, potassium and calcium salts (e.g. KCl) and glycols (Hossain and Al-Majed, 2015). 
The standard additives used by Statoil is KCl and glycol, while there are additional polymers 
on HPWBM. A paper done by Aston & Elliott (1994), based on experimental tests, states that 
high levels of shale inhibition are achievable with glycol muds. A general theory is that the 
mechanism behind the inhibition is that glycols can hydrogen-bond, which will disrupt the 
hydrogen bonding between water and shale. Their findings show that glycols that work 
together with KCl are causing hardening of soft shale, which is responsible for the high 
inhibition levels. The glycol displaces water from the clay without causing shrinkage (Aston 
and Elliott, 1994). 
 
When drilling shallow, soft and highly dispersive (gumbo) shales, the K+ ion is rapidly 
consumed. Thus, regular additions of potassium salt are necessary and can become expensive 
when drilling large holes. 
 
Lubricants 
As WBM’s usually have higher friction factors, lubricants are used if applicable. The 
reduction of friction between the wellbore and the drill string is an important issue as it will 
reduce the torque and drag. If there is an increase of the overall friction force caused by 
torque and drag, the force acting on the drill bit would decrease, which will reduce the ROP. 
Lubricants commonly used by Statoil include glycols (non-clouding), esters and rape seed oil. 
 
Corrosion inhibitors 
To prevent corrosion, it is important to control the pH, which for most muds lies between 9.5 
and 10.5. The most common additive used is caustic soda (NaOH) (Hossain and Al-Majed, 
2015). 
 
Lost circulation material (LCM) 
Loss of mud to the formation may happen in highly permeable sandstones, natural 
fractures, cavernous formations and induced fractures. Overbalanced drilling increase the risk 
of lost circulation. Bridging agents are added to the mud to prevent the loss of whole mud or 
excessive filtrate. Common products for reservoir applications include calcium carbonate or 
==PAGE:39==
 
31
suspended salt. For lost circulation treatments, any suitable sized products can be used, 
including mica, nutshells and fibers (Hossain and Al-Majed, 2015; SLB, 2017a). 
 
Detergents 
Detergents are used to prevent shale and clay from sticking to the drilling assembly and to 
prevent gumbo shale from agglomerating and plugging the annulus and flowlines (SLB, 
2017b). 
 
Deflocculants and defoamers 
Deflocculants are thinning agents used to reduce viscosity or prevent flocculation. Defoamers 
are surfactants used to lower interfacial tension so that gas will readily escape from the mud 
(SLB, 2017c, 2017d). 
 
 
 
==PAGE:40==

2.4 OBM 
OBM is a drilling mud in which oil is the continuous phase of the system. Water is normally 
added for economical reasons. Thus, OBM may be referred to as an invert emulsion drilling 
fluid, where oil is the external continuous phase of the system with emulsified droplets of 
water/brine as the internal phase. The oil-water ratio (OWR) is usually between 60:40 and 
90:10 for standard OBMs, but some muds are formulated with only oil as the liquid phase and 
water content is less than 5% (Hossain and Al-Majed, 2015). 
 
2.4.1  Composition of OBM 
An OBM can be made up of a combination of the following components. 
 
Base oil 
The base oil can be diesel oil, or more environmental friendly synthetic fluids and mineral oil. 
Synthetic fluids are biodegradable and include esters (derived from vegetable oils), ethers and 
olefin oligomer (Hossain and Al-Majed, 2015). 
 
Emulsifiers 
Emulsifiers/surfactants are added to emulsify the water phase and make oil the wetting phase. 
The invert emulsion helps to prevent shale swelling. 
 
Brine 
The water phase in OBM, is usually a saline solution referred to as brine. Salt is used to 
reduce the activity (aw) of the water phase and “match” the formation salinity to avoid 
osmosis effect. The brine is most commonly a solution of calcium chloride (CaCl2) (SLB, 
2017e). 
 
Weighting material 
Barite is the standard weighting material for OBM as for WBM. 
 
Viscosifiers 
In OBM, organophilic clays are used to build viscosity. These are clay minerals, typically 
bentonite, whose surfaces have been coated with oil-wetting agents to make them oil-
dispersible (SLB, 2017f). 
 
Insoluble additives 
Organic fluid loss additives and bridging material are used to reduce fluid invasion into 
formation. 
 
Lime (Calcium Hydroxide) 
Lime (Ca(OH)2) is added for alkalinity to combat acidic gases such as CO2 and H2S (Hossain 
and Al-Majed, 2015).

3.1.1 Formation stability 
WBM has less inhibitive properties and gives higher risk of wellbore stability issues, 
washouts and bit balling than OBM, as discussed in Chapter 2.1.3 and 2.1.5. All OBM 
systems are inhibitive (effectiveness lower with low OWR) and will generally not react with 
clay formations. The risk of stability issues increase in high inclination wells (>30 degrees), 
in long sections (>1500 m) and with operation time (> 8 days). High inclination wells are 
often drilled with high mudweight to avoid hole collapse. A large overbalance may enhance 
the invasion of WBM into shale formation. Furthermore, a high operation time means a high 
exposure time for the WBM and shale to react.  
 
3.1.2 Membrane efficiency 
WBM has a low membrane efficiency compared to OBM, leading to higher fluid loss to 
formation and potentially increasing the chemical effect and hole instability. 
 
3.1.3 Friction 
OBM is a lubricant by nature, and will reduce friction more than WBM. Drilling with OBM 
will minimize torque and drag, which may increase the ROP. Lubricant additives may be used 
in WBM to reduce friction, but these can be costly and may not be environmentally friendly.  
 
3.1.4 Fluid cost 
OBM can cost 4-5 times the cost of WBM. Also, WBM has no need for cuttings containment 
and waste handling. A 1200 m long 17 ½" section with WBM will typically be 5 MNOK 
cheaper than same section with OBM. However, drilling with WBM can be slower than 
drilling with OBM, and additional rig cost has to be accounted for. The effect of this will be 
analyzed in Chapter 3.2.

3.1.5 Cuttings handling 
Cuttings contaminated by OBM cannot be discharged to sea. Thus, cuttings handling and 
waste management is required. This triggers high cost and logistical issues when drilling 
shallow sections. 
 
One option to manage cuttings handling is by the process of cuttings re-injection (CRI). CRI 
is done by grinding the drill cuttings on the rig, to form a slurry which is then pumped 
downhole. It benefits from avoiding crane lifts, transportation and onshore processing. 
However, the solution requires a dedicated injection well and a good quality formation, and 
will only be feasible when there is one available. Other limitations of CRI are high CAPEX 
and OPEX, weight and space requirements on rig, technical requirements, reliability of CRI 
equipment and risk of leakage from the injection well. 
 
The other option is to transport the cuttings to shore, either by bulk tank solution or skip and 
ship solution. The bulk tank solution is generally more expensive and technically challenging. 
Thus, skip and ship is the recommended solution for transport to shore by Statoil. This 
solution can in principle be applied to any drilling operation from a fixed platform or a mobile 
unit. 
 
The process of transport to shore, known as skip and ship, starts with the cuttings being 
loaded into skips. Empty and filled skips are stored on the rig, and depending on the drilling 
program filled skips are loaded either onto a supply vessel or a dedicated vessel if required. 
This process increase the number of crane lifts and health, safety and environment (HSE) 
related risks.  
 
The amount and rate of cuttings generated increase with the length of the section, the hole 
size and the ROP. Drilling with OBM in 17 ½" sections may require extra dedicated vessel(s), 
which increase cost and environmental impact. Drilling ten meters of this section will 
generate 4 ton cuttings assuming a specific gravity of 2.6. Thus, the space available on the rig 
to store cuttings, availability of vessels and impact of bad weather are critical factors that may 
limit the drilling progress. Loading operations are suspended when wind speeds or wave 
heights are excessive, and the risk of costly down time increase during winter time. 
 
From what is described above, it is prominent to see the benefits of using an environmentally 
friendly WBM. This allows the mud and the cuttings to be discharged to sea. Consequently, 
there is less HSE issues and no need for skip and ship, CRI injectors or cuttings 
handling/waste management. 
 
3.1.6 Dilution rate 
WBM requires higher dilution rates to remove fine solids from the mud than OBM. When 
drilling green clay, the cuttings dissolve in the WBM and the mud has to be dumped and 
replaced by new mud in order to maintain required specs. Also, WBM requires higher 
chemical concentration and the chemical consumption is high due to the fluid/formation 
reaction. This requires new mud to be mixed. Thus, it can be challenging to maintain the mud 
==PAGE:43==
 
35
properties during operation. Some of the standard systems are dump and dilute systems, 
requiring high focus on logistics and chemical handling. Therefore, it can be more convenient 
to use OBM, since WBM needs more work and monitoring to maintain its properties. 
 
3.1.7 Environmental impact 
OBMs are an environmental contamination hazard. Potential spills/discharges will have 
environmental impact. Also, the transport and treatment of cuttings, generating polluting 
emissions will have a negative impact on the environment.  
 
The use of OBM can also be bad for the human HSE. The increased number of crane lifts 
when using skip and ship solution are increasing the risk of hazardous incidents. Personnel 
may be exposed to hot OBM and its vapor during operations requiring manual handling. 
Examples are changing shaker screens or removing metal shavings tangled in the shaker 
screens after milling operations.

4 Case studies: WBM/OBM 
Case studies are carried out to emphasize the practical differences between using WBM and 
OBM in shallow sections. To make a good comparison between wells drilled with WBM and 
OBM, they should be as similar as possible. Therefore, many wells have been investigated to 
find examples with comparable casing design, inclination, depth and length of section. An 
extract of the wells is found in Appendix B: Extract of investigated wells. 
 
4.1 Rimfaks 34/10-J-1H 
This well is chosen because it is a practical example of the difference in friction using WBM 
and OBM. The 12 ¼" section goes from 2135 to 3180 m MD through the Shetland formation, 
and was drilled with both WBM and OBM. From the final well report, it was found that the 
12 ¼" section was planned to be drilled with OBM. However, since there was no boat 
available to receive the WBM after drilling the 17 ½” section, it was decided to continue 
drilling with the WBM.  
 
It was experienced a very low ROP, and drilling parameters were varied without 
improvements. It was drilled from 2135 – 2206 m MD with an average ROP of 3.3 m/hr, and 
a high weight on bit (WOB) (18-30 ton) was necessary to make progress. The min/max torque 
readings from daily drilling report (DBR) are 8 and 12 kNm. 
 
When the boat was available, the WBM was replaced with the OBM. Then the ROP increased 
immediately, with drilling rates above 100 m/hr and a WOB between 2 – 6 ton. The following 
417 m were drilled with an average ROP above 70 m/hr. The min/max torque readings for 
this interval were 9 and 15 kNm. 
 
It is seen that the ROP achieved with OBM was significantly higher, even with a considerable 
lower WOB, compared to the WBM performance. The reason for this is most likely due to 
higher friction with the WBM, even though the torque readings from the intervals are almost 
the same. Resistance to downward movement resulting from drag could make the WOB 
surface measurements look higher than what the actual WOB was. Experiencing the same 
torque along with increased ROP with OBM, imply that more work is done on the bit, and 
less work is done as friction between the drillstring and the borehole wall. 
 
The same BHA and drill bit were used in both intervals. From DBR geology reports, it is 
found that the general lithology is the same for both the WBM and OBM interval mentioned 
above. The intervals are within the Shetland formation and consist of claystone with minor 
chalk stringers, which is supported by the completion log in Figure 4.1. The consistent high 
gamma ray (GR) readings and low resistivity, indicate claystone in both intervals. This 
supports the assumption that the poor performance in the WBM interval is due to high 
friction, and not because of a more challenging formation than in the OBM interval. 
Furthermore, the caliper log shows an enlarged hole in the interval drilled with WBM. Two 
possible reasons for this are the reaction between WBM and formation, and mechanical action 
of drillpipe because of the slow progress.

4.1.1 Discussion 
The performance of OBM are superior compared to WBM with respect to friction, and as 
seen from the case study of 34/10-J-1H it may have a significant impact on the ROP. 
 
When the actual WOB is reduced by friction it can reduce the effectiveness of the bit, 
especially when hitting hard stringers. If the progress is stationary for some time while 
circulating the well, it will increase the likelihood for washouts. Higher friction may also 
enhance stick-slip and vibration issues. 
 
Furthermore, a slow drilling progress will lead to a longer exposure time for the drilling fluid 
and the formation to react, which may enhance stability problems and halter the progress even 
more.

4.2 Fram/Byrding 
The wells 35/11-B-22H at Fram and 35/11-A33 Y1H at Byrding are chosen for the case 
studies since they were drilled with WBM and OBM respectively. The wells are located only 
a few km away from each other, and their 17 ½" sections are quite similar regarding depth, 
length and inclination. Also, both wells represent fluid related drawbacks which emphasize 
the practical differences. 
 
4.2.1 35/11-B-22H Fram (WBM) 
The 17 ½" section was drilled with WBM from 905 to 2116 m MD with a maximum 
inclination of 56 degrees. The 1211 m long section was drilled in June/July 2016, and was 
finished (shoe to shoe) in 8 days and 6 hours. 
 
This well was the first time drilling a 17 ½" section using WBM in a production well on 
Fram, where previously WBM had only been used for drilling vertical exploration wells. The 
WBM used was based on Troll fluid specs. Fram is located just a few km north from the Troll 
field where only WBM is used. The KCl concentration used will be further analyzed in 
Chapter 5.5. 
 
It is reported that the formation was stable for the entire period during both drilling and 
running casing. However, tight spots (15 ton overpull) were reported at top Lista formation 
(1503 m MD) and top of the green clay (1071 m MD) when POOH. These tight spots are 
most likely due to the BHA hitting ledges in the formation transitions, as illustrated in Figure 
2.9. From 969 m MD, packing off indications were observed as attempts were made to 
lubricate (wash/ream) out of hole due to further tight spots. An increasing drag trend (max 15 
ton) and slow progress was observed.  
 
When drilling the section, occasional hard stringers and stick slip issues were encountered 
from 1730-2113 m MD, resulting in a poor ROP. The average ROP for the section was 16.6 
m/hr. Furthermore, encountering hydrocarbons in the Lista sandstones added complexity to 
the cement job in order to have the required barrier. The section was finished (shoe to shoe) 
with a progress of ~147 m/day.

4.2.2 35/11-A33 Y1H Byrding (OBM) 
The 17 ½" section was drilled with OBM from 1141 to 2187 m MD with a maximum

Based on a risk and performance evaluation, the 17 ½" section for this well was decided to be 
drilled with OBM although the same section on Fram already had been drilled with WBM. 
The fluid selection decision making is based on technical requirements (TR) from Statoil’s 
governing documents. TR 3596 – “Standard well designs for NCS”, refers to the guideline 
(GL) document 3518 – “Drilling fluids, completion fluids and total fluid management” to be

for this well section due to the following risks:

finished (shoe to shoe) with a progress of ~131 m/day.

If the 17 ½" section at Byrding was drilled with WBM and a ROP of 16.4 m/hr (as achieved

more than it took with OBM. Assuming that completing the section after being drilled would 
take the same time for both scenarios, the additional 27 hours can be compared with the plot 
in Figure 3.1. It is seen that the scenario with WBM would not be cost beneficial, adding 
approximately 1.8 MNOK DWM cost compared to the OBM case.  
 
In chapter 3.2 it is seen that the WBM scenario is cost beneficial with respect to the OBM 
scenario, until it is break-even after 18 hours additional rig time. If drilling the section with 
WBM was 18 hours slower than with OBM, it would correspond to a ROP of 19 m/hr.

In recent years, the Grane field has started to use WBM in shallow sections where previously

4.3.1 25/11-G-18 AY1 (WBM) 
The 14 ¾" x 17 ½" section in G-18-AY1 was drilled with WBM from 1151 to 1758 m MD 
with a maximum inclination of 51 degrees. The 607 m long section was drilled in January

When drilling the section, it was planned to increase the mudweight to 1.55 SG before 
entering the green clay formation at 1505 m MD. At 1355 m MD it was experienced total 
losses to the sand formation. After curing losses, a decision was made to wait for supply of 
mud and LCM prior to continue drilling. They had to wait for more than a day due to bad 
weather. The section was then drilled to TD with a reduced mudweight of 1.40 SG.  
 
The estimated shear failure gradient in the green clay formation had been overestimated, 
which dictated a high mudweight. The high mudweight (1.50 g/cm3) triggered losses in the 
sand formation above. The total mud lost to formation during the section was 375 m3, and 115 
m3 was lost on shakers.  
 
It was not experienced challenges when drilling or running casing through the green clay. The 
KCl concentration used will be discussed in Chapter 5.7.  
 
The gross ROP for the section was 14.7 m/hr, mostly limited due to experiencing losses.

5 Case studies: KCl/smectite 
One of the most important technical differences between WBM and OBM is the shale 
inhibition, where OBM is superior. KCl is recognized as the best inhibitor when drilling with 
disposable WBM. The theory suggests that there exists an optimum concentration of KCl in 
the drilling mud for each individual case. If this optimum concentration could be determined, 
it may reduce the inhibition difference with respect to OBM and improve the drilling 
performance of WBM. In this chapter, case studies are carried out to see if there is a 
connection between the KCl concentration used and borehole stability issues with respect to 
the amount of smectite present in the formation. This is done by studying drilling and well 
reports, fluid test reports and X-ray diffraction (XRD) mineralogy data. 
 
One of the most challenging formations to drill in the North Sea with respect to borehole 
stability is the lower part of Hordaland due to its high content of highly reactive smectite. 
These shallow sections also have a large hole size (more cuttings to handle). The case studies 
will therefore only consider sections from the lower part of the Hordaland Group to the upper 
part of the Rogaland Group. 
 
To begin with, a comparison is done of the smectite content in different fields where the 
particular sections of interest were drilled with WBM. XRD data with analyses performed in 
1991 from the ex-Hydro database, has been collected from CoreDB, and is plotted against 
TVD with top Rogaland as datum, as shown in Figure 5.1. The available data of interest is 
limited due to the criteria mentioned above and comparable datasets. Data from analyses 
performed in Statoil’s research center more recently shows a significant higher smectite 
content, such as presented in Figure 2.7. Thus, only data from the ex-Hydro database (the 
most extensive one) are compared, assuming that the relative difference between these 
datasets are comparable, although the exact smectite content may be underestimated.  
 
In Figure 5.1 it is prominent to see how the smectite content increase towards the lower part 
of Hordaland and top Rogaland, and may be referred to as the green (or brown) clay 
formation. The plot also shows that the different wells have quite similar values from top 
Rogaland and 200 m above.

5.8 Discussion 
The uncertainties in XRD analysis are large. However, the abundance of smectite in the lower 
part of Hordaland seems to be quite similar for the wells investigated. In spite of that, the KCl 
spec used in the different wells range from 140 to 210 kg/m3. In general, a good inhibition 
when drilling the green clay was achieved in all the wells from the case studies except 30/6-E-
4 BT3. In this well they were not able to maintain the KCl concentration which fell as low as 
110 kg/m3. Combined with a long exposure time, it was most likely the reason for 
experiencing tight spots in the green clay. Nevertheless, the inhibition performance was 
improved compared to the previous failed well tracks E-4 B and E-4 BT2. The reason for the 
improvement was most likely due to switching from NaCl WBM to KCl WBM. 
 
Based on the results from the case studies, keeping the KCl level above 150 kg/m3 along with 
glycol seems to provide satisfactory inhibition. For all the wells investigated, the glycol 
content was in the range of 3-5%. This small variation of glycol content is considered to be 
insignificant for the variations in inhibition performance. Thus, the glycol content was not 
analyzed in the case studies. 
 
A good inhibition was achieved even for the long exposure time of 6.2 days at 30/6-E-14 A, 
as they did not experience any stability issues. An important factor for this achievement are 
probably good operational drilling practices. I.e. avoiding or limiting stationary time with 
circulation/pumping, especially in the green clay formation. Instead of exposing a limited spot 
with the fluid flow from the bit, the drillstring was reciprocated during unplanned stops. Other 
operational factors and mud properties may also be as important as the inhibitive properties of 
the mud for the borehole stability, such as pumping pressure, annular velocity, drillstring rpm, 
drill bit, WOB, mud weight, viscosity, etc.

6 Conclusion and recommendation 
Because of its better inhibition, OBM gives better performance than WBM when drilling 
reactive clays. However, it creates logistical and environmental challenges. Very costly 
modifications have to be done to most drilling units to handle cuttings generated from a 
higher ROP in shallow sections with OBM. In terms of cost-benefit, there is a higher potential 
for improvement using WBM than OBM. 
 
Improving the ROP with WBM requires satisfactory inhibitive properties. From the KCl case 
studies, it was found that the inhibition with WBM generally was satisfactory, as long as the 
KCl concentration was kept at a sufficiently high level. This applies to the green clay 
formations in the North Sea, which in general was found to have the same content of the 
primary swelling clay mineral smectite.  
 
On Oseberg Øst, a KCl concentration of 150 kg/m3 was found to give successful inhibition 
even after 6 days exposure time. Thus, it may be recommended to start off with a similar KCl 
spec when drilling the green clay sections, and to keep a high focus on the mud spec to be 
able to maintain its properties. 
 
To further improve the ROP with WBM and reduce the technical differences between OBM 
and WBM, it is seen from the Rimfaks case study that friction can be an important factor. 
Thus, it is recommended to investigate how to improve the friction properties of WBM 
without defying its major advantage of being environmentally friendly and dischargeable.
