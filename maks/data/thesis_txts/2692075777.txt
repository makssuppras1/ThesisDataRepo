Foreword
The master thesis investigates how non­destructive testing can aid the re­documentation
of reinforced concrete elements considered for reuse. The topic was chosen based on
the authors’ and supervisors’ interest in contributing to a more sustainable approach to
building design and the growing interest in the reuse of building materials. The process
has been highly educational and it has been exciting to work with such a relevant topic,
which has introduced the opportunity to meet and talk to committed and skilled industry
actors.
This thesis has been prepared during a period of five months at the Section for Materials
and Durability, Department of Civil Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark, in
partial fulfilment for the Master of Science in Engineering.
It is assumed that the reader has basic knowledge in the areas of statistics, physics and
material science.

Abstract
The reuse of building components is almost non­existent in the building industry today.
In order to reduce the emissions and waste associated with the production of concrete
elements, direct reuse should be a priority. To promote a safe and standardized approach
to reuse, non­destructive test methods are suggested as tools to evaluate the quality and
durability of concrete. Two well­known non­destructive test methods proposed for this ap­
proach are electrical resistivity and ultrasonic testing. This thesis investigates the possi­
bility of evaluating the quality and durability of prefabricated reinforced concrete elements
purposed for reuse using electrical resistivity and ultrasonic testing.
The investigation was based on an extensive literature review used to identify the influenc­
ing parameters on electrical resistivity and ultrasonic testing, their applicability in detecting
damage as well as the quality and durability parameters in cylindrical concrete specimens.
Experimental tests were performed on cast concrete specimens ranging from 5 ­ 65 MPa
to investigate the relationship between the ultrasonic pulse velocity (UPV) and concrete
compressive strength. Moreover, the ultrasonic pulse amplitude (UPA) was used to in­
vestigate the detection of artificially created voids of different sizes and location in cast
concrete specimens. Experimental bulk electrical resistivity testing combined with a non­
steady­state migration experiment was conducted to investigate the relationship between
concrete resistivity and the chloride migration coefficient, chloride content, and chloride
penetration depth in drilled concrete cores. Interviews were then conducted with industry
actors to explore the industry reluctances and the need for a standardized approach to
reuse.
An exponential correlation of 0.99 between the ultrasonic pulse velocity and the concrete
compressive strength was obtained from the experimental test. Moreover, the ultrasonic
pulse amplitude of concrete specimens containing voids showed significantly lower am­
plitude than a homogeneous reference specimen. The bulk electrical resistivity measure­
ments decreased non­linearly with the amount of chloride ions present after the migration
test. A clear linear correlation between concrete resistivity and the non­steady­state mi­
gration coefficient (R2 = 0.885) was obtained. Moreover, a strong correlation between de­
creasing concrete resistivity and chloride penetration depth (R2 = 0.93) was established.
The study found that it is possible to accurately estimate the compressive strength of cast
concrete specimens under 25 MPa using an exponential regression model while under­
estimating the strength for specimens above that value. Additionally, the ultrasonic pulse
amplitude is an applicable method for detecting artificially created voids in cast concrete
specimens. Lastly, it proved possible to evaluate the chloride penetration depth, chloride
concentration, non­steady­state migration coefficient and chloride penetrability level by
combining electrical resistivity with rapid chloride penetration in drilled core specimens.

Introduction
1.1
Background
In 2019, the Emission Gap report, prepared by the UN Climate Panel, states that too little is
being done to reverse climate change and that significant upheavals are necessary for the
goals set in the Paris Agreement to continue to be achievable [1]. The world’s resources
are consumed at a pace that puts tremendous pressure on ecosystems. Unfortunately,
environmental investments are often downgraded in today’s society because the effects
are only visible far into the future. However, if changes do not happen, high costs and
consequences will have to be paid for by future generations [2].
The construction industry, which is a significant consumer of resources and energy, plays
a vital role in creating change [3] . Consumption in the industry is high, but enormous
amounts of waste are also generated every year. The resources and efforts invested in
the production of building materials are in many cases not utilized sufficiently. Because
of this, the EU set a goal for 70% of all building materials to be reused by 2020 [4].
The reuse of building materials would help reach Sustainable Development Goal (SDG)
No.13, which concerns itself the fight against climate change. The pandemic has resulted
in greenhouse gas emissions falling by 6% in the year 2020. However, to meet the re­
quirements of limiting global warming to 1.5°C, greenhouse gas emissions need to be
reduced by an additional 7.6%. In addition, climate change affected more than 39 mil­
lion people in 2018, and it continues to contribute to more severe and frequent natural
disasters [5].
Today, the building industry is responsible for approximately 40% of all the materials used
on a global scale, while it produces around 35% of all waste in the world [6]. Therefore,
it is essential to reduce material consumption and generated waste in this industry to
aid in contributing to a more sustainable world. Given that many buildings are made
using prefabricated concrete elements, it is crucial to lower the consumption of these
materials and generate less construction waste. One way to do this is to start thinking
more circularly. It is estimated that a larger part of the prefabricated concrete elements
is suitable for reuse after the building’s end of life. For the case of concrete columns and
floor hollow core slabs, it is estimated that 90% of the elements can be reused while the
number is 80% for concrete beams and core walls [6]. Additionally, it is estimated that
the lifespan of the structural system of a building is anywhere 30 to 300 years [7]. The
potential for reusing concrete elements is therefore enormous.
When a material is recycled, it enters a process where the material is broken down. This
process is energy­consuming and should therefore be considered in a life cycle assess­
ment. However, when a material is reused, these energy­consuming processes associ­
ated with breaking the material down are not present. It is therefore recommended through
the hierarchy of waste management that reusing is of a higher priority than recycling [8].
It is reported that cement is the most important contributing factor to the environmental
impacts and that no evidence recycling concrete has a substantial reduction in emissions.
Although, it does contribute to reducing the waste that is sent to landfill [9].
The direct reuse of structural concrete elements has proven to increase overall savings
in CO2­emission for most elements. It is reported that the more times a component is
1

reused, the greater the savings in emission it will have. For example, concrete floor slabs
will have a saving of CO2­emission of 45% after merely a single use, while the saving
rises to 60% after reusing the element twice. The trend of increased savings in CO2­
emissions also applies to core walls, roof and beams. Whether or not reusing these
elements will have a lesser environmental impact is indisputable. Although the reuse
of concrete columns largely depends on whether or not the elements are designed for
disassembly. Therefore, designing for disassembly is an essential factor to consider when
reusing concrete elements regarding lowering CO2­emissions. However, for most cases,
reusing is found to be more beneficial in lowering CO2­emissions, and other environmental
impacts [6].
A large part of the circular economy model is developing systems and models that incen­
tivize businesses to adopt a more circular approach to the life cycle of building materials.
Many recommendations have been made on how to operate more circularly. This in­
cludes digitally managed material passports as documenting the properties of building
components will facilitate the reuse of building materials. Moreover, designing for disas­
sembly is also an essential aspect of reuse both for now but especially for any future reuse.
The driving factor within the circular economy is essentially working from a standardized
approach. This concerns the documentation of any properties of the components in a ma­
terial passport and designing elements in standardized sizes designed for disassembly
[10].
The many barriers to Circular Economy are hindering its implementation in the building
industry. These barriers are, amongst others, related to legal issues. These legal issues
are concerning the CE­marking of building components with the EU Member States. A
harmonized standard is a crucial element in the reuse of structural elements. Moreover,
creating a standardized approach to reuse can also solve the challenges of technical
requirements, where documenting the specifications of components is crucial. Lastly,
standards also provide guidance and knowledge to relevant actors within the industry,
which will encourage safe reuse [8].
To create a standardized approach to reuse, it is necessary to implement the proper doc­
umentation. Several papers suggest a material passport as the solution for this [8], [10],
[11]. Implementing a material passport in the building industry is a great tool to help close
the loop and adopt a more circular economy rather than the current linear model. It is sug­
gested that the properties included in a material passport should incorporate the physical,
chemical and biological properties as well as properties relating to the process, such as
ownership and material flows.
To document and evaluate the physical properties in a material passport, it is necessary
to investigate the current state of structural elements through field testing. It is, therefore,
necessary to evaluate components in a non­destructive manner. The challenge then be­
comes to evaluate the properties using non­destructive tests as accurately as when using
destructive tests. One of the strengths of non­destructive testing is its ability to be used to
evaluate the quality and durability without compromising the integrity of the element. Elec­
trical resistivity and ultrasonic testing are both recognized non­destructive tests method to
evaluate these parameters. However, these test methods have not yet been implemented
as standard methods to evaluate the reusability of concrete elements.

Scope and problem statement
This thesis aims to investigate the evaluation of quality and durability parameters for a

concrete elements. This is done through an extensive literature review, experimental

2.1
Electrical resistivity testing
Most electrical resistivity techniques are used to quantify the conductive properties of the
microstructure of concrete. When studying concrete subjected to an electric field, the ions
start to move in the pore network of the concrete. The concretes ability to withstand ionic
movement (flux) is often referred to as the real resistance of concrete.
The concrete electrical resistivity is a geometry­independent material property describing
the resistance of concrete in a unit cell geometry [12], [13]. Equation 2.1 describes the
relationship between electrical resistivity and electrical resistance for concrete.
ρ = k · R
where
R = V
I
(2.1)
Where ρ is the electrical resistivity of the concrete, R is the resistance of concrete given
by the ratio between Voltage V and resulting current I. Furthermore, k is a factor that
accounts for the specimen geometry. The geometry factor depends on the size and shape
of the specimen and the method of testing. The geometry factor k is described in more
detail for the application of the different measurements techniques in subsection 2.1.1,
subsection 2.1.2 and subsection 2.1.3.
In addition to the real resistance of concrete, there is a capacitance property of concrete
that tends to store the electrical charge. In measuring the electrical resistivity, the storage
of electrical charge should therefore always be considered [14]. Therefore, when studying
the electrical resistivity of concrete electric circuit modelling is typically applied, meaning
that the concrete acts as an electrical component with real resistance and capacitance
behaviour [14]. Generally, the connection of the concrete to the circuit is provided by
metal electrodes. The response of such a system is often determined with the aid of
an equivalent circuit model [13]. These models represent the electrical properties of the
concrete as well as the electrode­concrete interface.
Based on the proposed electric circuit models, different measurement techniques have
been developed. The most common electrical resistivity measurement techniques are
presented in the following sections.

Wenner four­point line array surface resistivity Test
The Wenner probe technique was first introduced in the Bulletin of the Bureau of Stan­
dards [15] in 1915 to determine soil strata for geologist field inspections. Later the method
has been modified for concrete applications [16]. The method uses the Wenner­array
setup, where four electrodes are positioned with equal spacing in a straight line as seen
in Figure 2.2. The two outer electrodes apply an alternating current (AC) I into the con­

outer electrodes [13].

it may result in inaccurate readings because of the polarization effect [16]. The geom­

k = γ · a
(2.3)

spacing of the electrodes is much smaller than the depth of the material being measured
(a << d, L). It should be noted that the geometry factor should be calculated based on the
specimen geometry for smaller cylinders or cubic specimens evaluated in the laboratory

2.2
Ultrasonic testing
The Ultrasonic test is a well recognized non­destructive test method used to evaluate the
quality of concrete for more than 60 years [20]. Ultrasonic testing is becoming prevalent
in the field of civil engineering, and its applications mostly include the evaluation of con­
crete structures and pavements [21]. A study conducted by Yeih and Huang et al.[22]
from 1998 showed the possibility of evaluating corrosion damage in reinforced concrete
by measuring the average amplitude attenuation and electrochemical parameters. The
study showed that the ultrasonic pulse velocity method was valid for estimating certain
parameters relating to the quality and durability of the reinforced concrete.
Ultrasonic pulses are mechanical waves with a frequency above 20kHz, which can, in
essence, behave similarly to audible sound waves. However, ultrasonic waves do not
transmit through the air or a vacuum. Because of this, couplants need to be applied
between transducers and the surface of the concrete, which is done to ensure full trans­
mission. When the ultrasonic waves enter the concrete, they will scatter. This is due to
inhomogeneity in the concrete caused by the structure of the material being made up of
different constituents. The interfacial transition zone (ITZ), which is the region of the ce­
ment paste around the aggregate particles, are shown to reflect the seismic waves ut of
the main beam, which is the direct path between the transducers.
In the field of ultrasonic testing to different methods can be applied; Ultrasonic Pulse
Velocity (UPV) and Ultrasonic Pulse Amplitude (UPA). Studies show that a combination
of the two methods shows a more accurate prediction in results compared to only using
only one of the methods [23]. However, UPV is the more recognized and used method.
2.2.1
Wave types
Ultrasonic waves can propagate in four modes in a solid medium. These types include
longitudinal waves, shear waves, plate waves and surface waves. Within the field of
ultrasonic testing, the two types of waves that are most commonly used are longitudinal
waves (P­waves) and shear waves (S­waves or transverse waves). An illustration of the
particle movement of these two wave types can be seen Figure 2.5.
Figure 2.5: Longitudinal and shear waves [24]
For the longitudinal waves (P­waves), oscillations happen in a longitudinal direction, which
is where the name stems from. These waves are also known as pressure or compression
waves due to the active forces in the waves being expansion and compression. Lastly,
the wave type is known as density waves due to the fluctuations of material density in the
8
==PAGE:19==
movement of the waves. Longitudinal waves are able to be generated in solids, liquids
and gasses due to wave energy being able to travel through atomic structures. For shear
waves, oscillations happen at a right angle or in the crossing of the direction of propa­
gation. Unlike longitudinal waves, shear waves are not effective at travelling in liquids or
gasses, and they only propagate effectively in materials that are considered acoustically
solid.
Each of the above­mentioned wave types propagates through a solid medium with its
characteristic velocity. From seismic theory, it is known that longitudinal waves propagate
through a solid with the highest velocities and that the surface waves travel with the lowest
velocities [20]. The velocity of a longitudinal wave mostly depends on the elastic properties
and density of the concrete and is given by the following expression:

2.2.2
Transducer arrangement
When conducting ultrasonic tests, the transducers can be arranged in three different trans­
mission configurations; direct transmission, semi­direct transmission and indirect trans­
mission. The direct transmission configuration is the most preferred of the three because
it allows for the maximum amount of energy to be received. The semi­direct transmission
configuration is also widespread as it is advantageous in situations where it is necessary
to avoid reinforcement. Although, if the transducers are too far from one another, the
ultrasonic wave will attenuate to a level not detected by the receiver. Lastly, there is the
indirect method of arranging the transducers. The challenge with this arrangement is that
the signal received is greatly weakened compared to the direct method. Moreover, it has
a higher probability of error, and it is, therefore, necessary to follow a different, and longer
procedure [20]. The three methods of arranging transducers can be seen in Figure 2.6

2.2.4
Wave interaction and interference
When evaluating ultrasonic waves, it is necessary to understand the concept of interac­
tion and interference. When two waves interact, they will superimpose each other. The
amplitude of a superimposed wave is then calculated by adding the sum of the amplitude
of the two waves. When two identical waves from the same point origin interact, their
combined pressure will double. When this happens, the waves are said to be in phase
(0° phase difference), meaning that the peaks and valleys of the waves are aligned in a
concept called constructive interference. When the waves are out of phase (180° phase
difference), one’s peaks is aligned with the other waves’ valley. These waves combine in
a concept called destructive difference, where the waves cancel out each other. When
waves are neither in nor out of phase, the amplitude is calculated by adding the sum of
the wave amplitude for the two, as mentioned previously. An illustration of this can be

3.3.2

3.3.2.1
Purpose
In order to ensure high structural safety and performance of reusable RC elements, it is
important to determine its compressive strength with a high degree of probability without
damaging the reusable element. Ultrasonic testing is most commonly used for quality
assurance during and after the construction of new concrete structures. However, in re­

structures [27], but has not yet been established as a standardized test procedure for
the evaluation of reusability. Therefore, the purpose of this experiment was to accurately
predict the compressive strength of concrete using UT and to evaluate the applicability of
this NDT method for the growing field of reuse. The applicability of the ultrasonic pulse
velocity test was compared to the Schmidt Hammer test, which is another NDT method
that has been shown to accurately predict the compressive strength of concrete [28].

3.3.3

Purpose

3.3.4
Chloride permeability of concrete using electrical resistivity testing
3.3.4.1
Purpose
Corrosion of reinforcing steel due to chloride ingress is known to be one of the most
common environmental attacks that leads to the deterioration of concrete structures [38].
Chloride ingress can harm reinforced concrete structures, as they break down the pas­
sive layer around the reinforcement, potentially leading to cracking and in the worst­case,
failure of the structure.
To prevent chloride­induced corrosion, it will be necessary to evaluate the chloride perme­
ability of the concrete and from there on estimate the desired service life of the structure.
Usually, this is done by determining chloride diffusion and migration coefficients which
makes it possible to estimate the necessary concrete coverage needed to ensure the de­
sired service life of the element. From a re­use perspective where the concrete cover is
fixed, these tests can provide engineers with valuable information about the remaining
service life of the element, utilizing the rate of chloride penetrability.
Agencies across the world have adopted standardized test methods for measuring con­
crete’s ability to resist chloride ion penetration [39]. The most common electrical indication
test for evaluating chloride permeability in concrete structures today is AASHTO T 277 and
ASTM C1202, and are commonly known as rapid chloride penetration test (RCPT). More­
over, NT built 492 [40] is commonly applied to rapidly assess the durability of the concrete
structure by means of the non­steady­state migration coefficient. The above­mentioned
tests are recognized as reliable and time­efficient but require destructive core drilling to
be conducted.
As described in subsubsection 4.1.1.2 research studies on both bulk and surface resistivity
testing have shown promising results as alternatives to the rapid chloride penetration tests
mentioned above. In comparison, the ER test does not require core specimens to be
conducted and can be performed in a time instant.
The purpose of this experiment was to evaluate if ER testing could be utilized to determine
the concretes ability to resist chloride ion penetration and if it can be used to estimate the
remaining service life of the concrete element. Moreover, this experiment aimed to in­
vestigate the correlations of the bulk electrical resistivity measurements with measured
chloride content, chloride penetration depth, and the estimated non­steady­state migra­
tion coefficient.
3.3.4.2

12504­1 [41] using diamond core drilling equipment. Before the core drilling, no informa­
tion about the concrete composition of the column was not obtained. However, utilizing
Schmidt Hammer testing at the building site, the compressive strength of the column was

For the preparation of the catholyte and anolyte reservoirs, a thin layer of silicone was
applied between the plastic components (ring and the endplate) to improve the tightness.
Moreover, foam tape was applied to the reservoir surface to prevent any leakage between
the concrete and reservoir interface.
500ml of catholyte and anolyte solutions were then pumped in the catholyte and anolyte
reservoirs respectively before connecting the cathode to the negative pole and the anode
to the positive pole of the power supply. The current for each migration setup was preset
to 15 mA, resulting in voltage ranging from 25­30 V. The current and temperature of the
anolyte solution was recorded at the start and end of the experiment.

Bulk resistivity measurements were performed uni­axially with a potential difference of
50V direct current maintained across the ends of the test specimen. The concrete speci­

contacts at their interfaces to ensure proper electrical connection. A direct current of 1mA
was then applied, and the drop in the potential between the electrodes was measured.
Five measurements were performed for each of the specimens with a 60­second interval
between each recording. Reference is made to section 2.1 and subsubsection 4.1.1.1 for
more explanatory theory on the test method and its influencing parameters.
Prior to titration, the water content of the 8 slices cut from the surface penetrated by the
catholyte solution was measured. This was done by weighing the sample immediately
after coarse crushing and then weighing them again after being exposure to 105°C for a
minimum of 24 hours. Furthermore, the coarse samples were pulverized into dust by a
crushing mill. Subsequently, 5 g of each sample were extracted for the titration.

dust samples were first mixed with 20 mL distilled water to achieve separation of the par­
ticles. Then 10 mL concentrated nitric acid (65%) was added to the mixtures. Moreover,
50 mL of boiling hot distilled water was added to each of the mixtures. After cooling, an
additional 1 mL concentrated nitric acid then was added to control that no gas evolution

4.1.1
Electrical resistivity testing
4.1.1.1
Influencing parameters on Electrical resistivity measurements
In the following section, parameters influencing the electrical resistivity measurement of
concrete are presented. Published literature on this topic has found multiple factors in­
fluencing both the intrinsic electrical resistivity of concrete as well as the resistivity mea­
surements.
Effect of water to cement Ratio
In general, the water to cement (w/c) ratio is one of the main factors contributing to the
permeability of concrete. A concrete mixture with a high w/c ratio will result in a more
porous hardened concrete which again will make the movement of ions easier and the
concrete more permeable. Thus the resistivity of concretes with higher w/c ratios will be
lowered. The inherent electrical resistivity of concrete is known to be affected by the pore
size distribution and interconnection and also the conductivity of pore fluid [13].
Due to this relationship, electrical resistivity testing has been used to monitor concrete
porosity during its early age evolution [46]. Moreover, ER has been utilized to monitor the
microstructure evolution in concrete during the hydration processes [47].
An experimental study on different concrete compositions with different wŗatios conducted
by Van Noort et al.[48] showed that the electrical conductivity in general increased with
the w/c ratio. This again shows that there is a decreasing relationship between resistivity
and the w/c ratio. By increasing the w/c ratio, the volume fraction of hydrated cement
paste in the concrete mix increases.
Effect of aggregate type and size
Generally, normal­weight aggregates (e.g. from crushed rocks and land marine sources)
have higher electrical resistivity than hardened cementitious paste because they lack in­
ternal pore structure. The dense nature of natural stone aggregates makes it difficult for
ions to move through them. Indeed, aggregates act as an obstacle in the electric current
path, hence increasing the electrical resistivity of concrete [49]. Several researchers have
attempted to investigate the effect of aggregates on the electrical resistivity of concrete.
One experimental study on the effects of aggregates on concrete resistivity conducted
by Sengul et al.[50] indicated that the increase in aggregate content for concrete mix­
tures resulted in higher measured resistivity. For all the aggregate contents studied in
the experiment, the mixtures containing larger aggregates were measured to have higher
resistivities compared to the mixtures with aggregates of smaller particle sizes. More­
over, it was observed that the increase of resistivity most significant for concrete mixtures
34
==PAGE:45==
containing more than 40% aggregates. Also, this experimental study highlighted that the
resistivities of the concrete produced with crushed lime­stone aggregate were higher than
those produced with gravel aggregates for the same aggregate volume concentrations.
This finding was taught to be a result of poor bonding between gravel and cement paste.
Overall, there are several studies concluding that the electrical resistivity of concrete in­
creased with the aggregate content for different mixture series investigated as they hinder
the electric current path [49]–[51].
Effect of Curing Conditions
As described previously electrical resistivity of concrete is highly correlated to porosity
and pore solution characteristics hence it can be related to the degree of hydration of the
cementitious material and the degree of saturation. Literature highlights that the degree
of hydration of cement­based materials and the pore saturation degree can significantly
affect the resistivity of the hardened concrete [52]–[54].
One study conducted by Li et al.[52] showed that the electrical resistivity of the tested
concrete was highly dependent on the pore saturation degree. Electrical resistivity mea­
surements at 100% pore saturation were measured in the range of 170 ­ 300 Ωm while it
increases rapidly to the range of 1100 ­ 20534 Ωm for specimens at 40% pore saturation.
Differences in resistivity can develop as a result of curing conditions as it influences the
hydration process of concrete. Also, sample storage conditions are shown to affect the
resistivity measurements because it can influence the degree of saturation. Therefore
wetting the specimens prior to the resistivity measurements is recommended [16].
Effect of temperature
Several researchers have reported that temperature variation in the concrete can sig­
nificantly influence the resistivity measurements. Moreover, there is an agreement that
increased concrete temperatures results in a decreased measured resistivity [55][13]
[56][57].
The electric current flow in concrete is the result of ionic movement within the pore solu­
tion, and ionic mobility is affected by temperature [13]. Variation in temperature affects
resistivity by changing the ion mobility, and the interactions between ion and ion as well
as between ion and solid [16]. Concrete pore solution contains Na+,K+, Ca2+, SO2−
4 , and
OH−[58]. These ions are transferring the current through the concrete and thus the vari­
ation in temperature plays an important part in the resistivity measurements. Additionally,
temperature variation can affect the moisture content of concrete which indirectly affects
the resistivity measurements [59].
Earlier experimental studies have described a linear relationship between resistivity and
temperature given by Equation 4.1 [60][61][56].
ρ = ρ0(1 + α · ∆T)
(4.1)
Where ρ is the resistivity measured at a temperature T°C, ρ0 is the resistivity at refer­
ence temperature T0 (e.g. 21°C ), ∆T is the temperature difference (T0 −T) and α is the
temperature coefficient which has been reported to be from 0.022­0.035/°C. It should be
noted that this linear relationship has been found to only be applicable when the temper­
ature T is in the interval of T0 ± 5°C [60][61]. Advancements are currently being made
35
==PAGE:46==
in this field and more recent literature presented by Spragg et al.[57] and Poyet et al.[59]
has used an exponential relationship for correcting resistivity to temperature variation.
Effect of electrode contact
The electrode­concrete interface may affect the electrical resistivity measurements both
for four­point Wenner configuration as well as for two­point bulk electrical measurements.
An experimental study on the sensitivity of electrode contact solutions and contact pres­
sure for two­point measurements, performed by Newlands et al.[62] found that the con­
tact solution applied to the electrode­sponge­concrete interface (moist sponge) could af­
fect the resistivity measurements because the connectivity in this interface depends on
the conductivity of the contact solution. The study demonstrated that both saturated cal­
cium hydroxide and sodium chloride solutions offered minimal resistance at the electrode­
sponge­concrete interface compared to mains water.
Moreover, the study evaluated the effect of contact pressure and found that an increase in
contact pressure reduced the effect of the contact artifacts at the interfaces, leading to a
lower resistance and hence a more reliable measure of concrete resistivity [62]. The study
highlighted that there is a need for a standardized approach for resistivity measurements
by using a contact solution of known conductivity and particular contact pressure. This
would provide the research on ER with more reliable data. Furthermore, mains water was
concluded to be satisfactory as a contact liquid. However, the variability of mains water
on a global scale can affect electrical resistivity research.
Another study researching the effects of electrode contact in four­point Wenner configu­
rations conducted by, Millard and Wenner et al.[63] found that probe diameter and contact
pressure had an influencing effect on the electrical resistivity measurements. The study
reported a maximum error of 6% for the resistivity measurements of electrode contact
points varying from 1 ­ 40 mm [63].
Effect of probe spacing
For electrical resistivity measurements using the Wenner probe configuration, a source
of error is often related to the size and placement of aggregates inside the concrete. As
previously presented aggregates are shown to have a greater resistivity than cementitious
material. The initial assumption that the concrete is homogeneous may therefore affect
resistivity measurements. In order to mitigate errors related to aggregates influence on
the resistivity in Wenner arranged resistivity testing, researchers recommend considering
a wide enough electrode spacing thus limiting the influence of non­homogeneity. This
spacing is usually recommended to be between 20 mm and 7 0mm [16][63].
A common suggestion is to take several measurements at various locations for then to
study the average of these. Also, it is suggested to vary the probe spacing so that the
effect of larger aggregates can be taken into consideration. Therefore, many commercial
instruments are equipped with variable probe spacing [16].
When knowing the maximum aggregate size it is recommended to consider an electrode
spacing that is 1.5 times greater than the aggregate size. This is shown to reduce variance
in Wenner arranged measurements [64]. In an experimental study conducted by Gowers
and Millard et al.[64] it was observed that a smaller electrode spacing than the maximum
aggregate size increased the standard deviation of approximately 10%. Moreover, the
spacing between the electrodes will influence the depth of the which current that can travel
through the concrete. Literature suggests that the electrical current may travel through the
concrete volume with approximately the same depth as that of the electrode spacing [55].
36
==PAGE:47==
Therefore an increased spacing will allow the current to flow further into the concrete.

that specimen geometry will affect the resistivity measurements for the four­point method
is common knowledge in the field of electrical resistivity research. However, information
on this topic is perceived as limited.

Effect of embedded reinforcement
Several researchers have studied the effect of rebar presence on electrical resistivity mea­
surements on concrete structures. From theory, it is known that electrical current fluxes
move through pathways with the least amount of resistivity. For RC structures the resis­
tivity measurements can be influenced by the embedded reinforcement which can distort
the homogenous current flow.
Polder et al.[55] studied the effect of embedded reinforcement on concrete resistivity mea­
surements using a four­point Wenner configuration. In this experimental research, it was
observed that placing the four electrodes directly over the embedded reinforcing bar with
a cover depth of 10 ­ 20 mm can result in significant erogenous resistivity measurements.
According to Polder, the resistivity measurements in such a case could be artificially high
or low dependent on which of the four electrodes were near the rebar.
A study conducted by Weydert et al.[66] concludes the same as Polder stating that four­
point measurements conducted directly over a reinforcing bar with only a cover depth

of 10 ­ 20 mm could result in inaccurate measurements with a factor ranging from 2­6.
Furthermore, Weydert concluded that Wenner probe four­point measurements should not
be performed parallel to embedded reinforcing bars with a cover debt below 30 mm.

Effect of signal frequency
As described in section 2.1 saturated concrete can store some of the electric charges and
can therefore behave as an Resistor­capacitor (RC) circuit. This effect introduces a phase
difference between the electrical current imposed and the measured potential referred to
as AC impedance [13].
Figure 4.2: Schematic representation of the AC Impedance response of concrete [13][16]
When studying the general representation of the AC impedance response of concrete it
is clear that there are two arcs, one in a high­frequency range and the other in the low­
frequency range (Figure 4.2). The characteristics in the higher frequency region are pri­
38
==PAGE:49==
marily influenced by the microstructure of concrete while the characteristics in the lower
frequency region are influenced by the electrode­concrete interface [13]. Moreover, it
can also be seen that there is a great difference in impedance compared to the real com­
ponent of the impedance. The real component, which is obtained at zero phase angle
corresponds to the true uniaxial resistance of the concrete [16].
Most electrical resistivity measurements are therefore prone to be recorded with a real
and imaginary component, as the phase angle is rarely zero [16]. The optimal frequency
for electrical resistivity measurements is dependent on the concrete mixture proportions
and moisture conditions and is therefore complex to determine.
According to Layssi and Alizadeh et al.[13] resistivity measurements on concrete speci­
mens using the two­point uniaxial method should be carried out at a frequency range of
0.5 ­ 10 kHz to obtain the best results. Layssi and Alizadeh studied the electrical resistiv­
ity of concrete carried out with frequencies in both the high­ and low­frequency domain.
They found that measurements at a low signal frequency of 40 Hz overestimated the re­
sistivity measurements by 9% relative to measurements taken at a high signal frequency
of 1 kHz [13]. Moreover, their experimental research concluded that uniaxial measure­
ments that were taken below 500 Hz generally overestimate the results due to the effect
of electrode­concrete interface effect.
Measurements performed with a Wenner probe configuration, on the other hand, are less
prone to overestimation because the electrode­concrete interface effect is not as domi­
nant. Therefore according to Layssi and Alizadeh, the Wenner probe measurements can
be performed in a wider frequency range (10 Hz ­ 10 k Hz) [13].
4.1.1.2

Diffusivity is the controlling parameter that determines the time it takes for chloride ions
to diffuse into concrete and is highly related to the corrosion of RC structures. Corrosion
of reinforcing steel due to chloride ingress is known to be one of the most common en­
vironmental attacks that leads to the deterioration of concrete structures [38]. To prevent
chloride­induced corrosion, it will be necessary to determine the chloride penetrability of
the concrete.
As previously presented, the chloride permeability is commonly measured through Rapid
Chloride Migration test, Rapid Chloride Permeability Test (RCPT), or Bulk Diffusion (BD)

concrete permeability properties and hence electrical resistivity has been studied as an
alternative method for application. Electrical resistivity testing has been found to be both
efficient and effective in determining concrete chloride permeability indirectly [13], [16],
[39], [50], [68].

reliable technique to estimate the diffusion coefficient of concrete making it a valuable
NDT method for service life estimation for reusable RC elements.
4.1.1.3
Correlation between concrete resistivity and corrosion of reinforcement
Corrosion is one of the main causes of deterioration of reinforced concrete (RC) struc­
tures. The corrosion process depends on several parameters such as oxygen availabil­
ity, the ratio of anodic/cathodic area, relative humidity (RH), and lastly concrete electrical
resistivity [16], [71]. Since concrete resistivity plays a part in the rate of corrosion several
researchers have studied the relationship between reinforcement corrosion and electrical
resistivity.

==PAGE:51==
Researchers generally agree that there is an inverse relationship between the corrosive
environment in reinforced concrete and the electrical resistivity [16]. Moreover, there is
an agreement that the electrical resistivity of concrete decreases with an increase in re­
inforcement corrosion [13], [16], [37], [68], [72], [73].
A comprehensive literature review on this topic has been conducted by Hornbostel et
al.[72]. The literature review looked at research concerning the relationship between cor­
rosion rate and concrete resistivity. The literature review concluded that even though
concrete resistivity measurements are promising for the Service Life Assessment of RC
structures, further investigations on the relationship between corrosion rate and electrical
resistivity need to be conducted. Furthermore, literature on this topic concludes that the
higher electrical resistivity of concrete lowers the risk and the rate of corrosion [13], [16],
[72].

4.1.1.4
Correlation between Electrical Resistivity and Compressive Strength
The compressive strength is one of the most important mechanical properties of concrete
and is mostly measured by destructive uniaxial compression testing utilizing the failure
load from the test to find the strength of the concrete. Several researchers have studied
the relationship between concrete compressive strength and electrical resistivity.
One of the key factors affecting the compressive strength of concrete is the inter­facial
transition zone (ITZ) which is the region of the cement paste around the aggregate par­
ticles [75]. Studies show that there is no significant impact from the ITZ on the electrical
resistivity of concrete [16].
A study conducted by Pilvar and Ramezanianpour et al.[76] found that there was a weak
correlation between concrete compressive strength from different concrete mixtures and
their respective surface resistivity. The strongest relationship between the two was ob­
tained by polynomial curve fitting which resulted in an R2 value of only 0.413 (Figure 4.3).

4.1.1.5
Correlation between Electrical Resistivity and crack detection
The presence of cracks can influence the electrical resistivity measurements of as it can
change the transport properties of the concrete. Several researchers have investigated
crack detection and characterization by means of electrical resistivity measurements. Ex­
perimental testing has been performed using a rectangular four­point arrangement instead
of the Wenner probe technique because it allowed for measurements in both orthogonal
directions without having to rotate the probe in between each measurement [77], [78].
A study conducted by Lataste et al.[77] found that electrical resistivity measurements
conducted by a rectangular four­point arrangement (5 x 10 cm) could be applied to RC
structures for the assessment of structural damage. The experimental study found that
crack depth and crack openings could affect the electrical resistivity of the concrete. It’s
worth noting that several assumptions that may not be applicable in reality were made.
Moreover, the study was based on simulations on concrete blocks limited in size which
could affect the impact of the crack [77].
Another study conducted by Goueygou et al.[78] also utilizing a rectangular probe setup
found that the electrical resistivity testing was able to characterize some cracks dependent
on the concrete quality and crack severity. Moreover, ER testing showed to be effective
in distinguishing between dry and wet cracks. However, further research on this topic
needs to be conducted before ER testing can be recommended as a method for crack
characterization.
4.1.1.6
Correlation between Concrete Resistivity and delamination
Researchers have studied how electrical resistivity testing can be used to detect delam­
ination of concrete. In general, electrical resistivity measurements are disturbed by de­
laminated concrete. However, a relationship between the two was not found conducting
this literature review.
42
==PAGE:53==
A study conducted by Chouteau and Beaulieu et al.[79] investigating the applicability of
electrical resistivity tomography for damage detection of concrete found that the presence
of delamination differentiated the electrical resistivity measurements compared to mea­
surements taken on the same concrete without delamination. This discovery gave rise
to the conclusion that delamination at early stages can be detected using resistivity mea­
surements. However, it should be noted that the measurements were conducted with 21
linearly aligned electrodes instead of the four­point Wenner method [79].
Similarly, a study conducted by Morales et al.[80] found that electrical resistivity mea­
surements can be used to identify delamination by observing the measured difference
between measured resistivity of in­tact and delaminated concrete covers.

Effect of moisture content
The moisture content of concrete has been shown to influence the ultrasonic test results.

significant difference in pulse velocity between a properly cured standard cube compared
to a structural element made from the same concrete with different curing conditions [27].
Prior to UPV testing, the effects of moisture content should therefore be considered in
order to establish an optimal correlation between pulse velocity and concrete strength.
Moreover, a study conducted by Güneyli et al.[82] on the effects of moisture content and
concrete temperature on UPV measurements found a strong linear relationship between
measured pulse velocity and water content. Based on correlations obtained in the ex­
perimental study an increase in water content of 1 % was concluded to increases the
measured pulse velocity of 160 m/s on average.

in pulse velocity only occurs when the temperature of the concrete falls below 5°C or
above 30°C. For temperatures outside of the aforementioned temperature range, correc­
tion factors should be applied [27]. These correction factors can be seen in Table 4.2,
[20].

ship between measured pulse velocity for five different concrete mixtures and their re­
spective temperature (R2 > 0.94). Moreover, Güneyli concluded that the increase in tem­

Effect of path length
The distance between the transducers, also known as the path length, is recommended to

Effect of embedded reinforcement
In general, the seismic waves will travel with a greater velocity when when the pulse is
sent close to embedded reinforcement. This is because waves travel at approximately
twice the velocity in steel compared to concrete. How much the ultrasonic pulse veloc­
ity increases, is based on how close the measurements are taken to the reinforcement.
Moreover, the number of rebars, their diameter, and their orientation in relation to the path
between the transducers all affect the pulse velocity [20], [27].
Fodil and Chemrouk et al.[83] researched the influence of embedded reinforcement on ul­
trasonic pulse velocity measurements in the concrete of different strength classes. They
found that the presence of embedded steel rebars parallel to the wave propagation di­
rection affected the UPV measurements. The influence of embedded reinforcement was
found to be more significant for lower­density concrete [83]. However, as discussed previ­
ously the pulse velocity measurements are found to decrease exponentially with decreas­
ing density which should have been taken into consideration.
Effect of specimen geometry
The pulse velocity measurements are recommended by various standards to be con­
ducted with transducer resonant frequencies between 20 to 100 kHz. These frequencies
generate long wavelengths and therefore imply limitations in the dimensions for the con­
crete specimens [84]. Exactly how much lower the pulse velocity is reduced primarily
depends on the ratio between the wavelength of the ultrasonic pulse and the smallest
lateral dimension of the measured specimen [27], [20].
The effect of the length­to­diameter ratio was investigated by Wiciak, Cascante And Polak
et al.[84]. Specimens with identical diameters but with different lengths were investigated.
The study concluded that the effects of the length­to­diameter ratio only had a minor in­
fluence on UPV results. Moreover, the authors recommended that the specimen length
should exceed more than one wavelength recommended by ASTM C597­16 [84].
Effect of calibration Calibration is an important step in the process of doing ultrasonic
pulse velocity measurements. If the equipment is not calibrated before and after conduct­
ing the measurements, then there is a risk of obtaining inaccurate results. The calibration
is done by measuring the velocity and transit time of a calibration included in the equip­
ment [27].

Correlation between UPV and Compressive Strength
Many papers state the applicability of ultrasonic testing in predicting the compressive
strength by pulse velocity measurements [20], [23], [37], [85]–[87]. Although some studies
have different approaches as to how to most accurately predict the compressive strength.
An experimental study conducted by Guy et al.[88] studied fifty different structural ele­
ments by means of ultrasonic testing. By comparing in­field pulse velocity measurements
to compressive strength measurements conducted in the lab strong correlations between
the two were made (R2 > 0.9) was obtained for all fifty elements. A table presented in the
study comparing measured compressive strength with measured ultrasonic pulse velocity
for five different element types can be seen in Figure 4.5.

A study conducted by Liang and Wu et al.[23] investigated the theoretical elucidation
on the empirical formulae for the ultrasonic testing method for concrete structures. The
study found that it was possible to estimate compressive strength using ultrasonic testing.
Moreover, the paper suggested a theoretical model using both the ultrasonic pulse velocity
and ultrasonic pulse amplitude. This model showed to increase the accuracy of predicted
compressive strength. However, the failure probability of the combined method was still
34.8%. Moreover, the study suggested further work on a regression model should be
conducted [23].
An experimental study conducted by Turk and Trtnik et al.[85] investigated the prediction
of concrete strength using ultrasonic pulse velocity and artificial neural networks. In this in­
vestigation, MatLab programming was used to create a model based on an artificial neural
network to more accurately predict the compressive strength of concrete using ultrasonic
testing. The strength of the proposed model is that it does not need a specific equation
that differs from traditional prediction models. Also, this model can continuously re­train
new data, so that it can conveniently adapt to new data [85]. The compressive strength
estimates obtained showed to be significantly more reliable than those from the regres­
sion modeling. The study was able to increase the R2 value from 0.62, 0.48 and 0.39 for
correlations found through regression modeling to 0.84, 0.90 and 0.80, respectively, for
correlations found using artificial neural networks [85].
Sabbag and Uyanık et al.[86] investigated how to most accurately predict the compres­
sive strength of reinforced concrete by measuring the velocities of both P and S waves.
The study considered variables such as concrete design, reinforcement size and curing
time. The results showed that the correlation between the compressive strength found
through unixial compression test and ultrasonic pulse velocities for both p and s waves
had an R2­value above 0.90. The study also found that combining results from ultrasonic
pulse velocities for p and s waves showed higher accuracy in predicting the compressive
strength compared to only using one of them [86].
Furthermore, a handbook on non­destructive testing also supports the applicability of ul­
trasonic testing in the predicting compressive strength. According to the handbook, it
possible to predict the compressive strength using the pulse velocity both for in situ and
precast concrete. Although, it is stated that no actual physical relationship between the
compressive strength and the pulse velocity exist. It presents a study that examined this

sive strength. However, some variation in results and methods exists. One study finds
a linear correlation between the parameters while others find it to be exponential. Addi­

regression whereas others simply state the applicability. The experiments also vary in
sample size, the geometry of concrete specimen and whether or not the specimens are
contained reinforcement. Although each article approaches these experiments differently,
the conclusion remains the same.
4.1.2.4
Correlation between UPV and crack detection
An important aspect of reusing concrete elements is being able to detect cracks, both
internally and on the surface. Moreover, measuring the depth of these cracks is equally
as important as being able to detect them. Several studies have conducted experiments
that through ultrasonic testing, can detect internal cracks [73] while some studies also can
measure the depth of surface cracks [35], [36].
An extensive literature review on nondestructive test methods for concrete bridges con­
ducted by Kashif, Ibrahim, and Jameel et al.[73] reported that ultrasonic pulse velocity
measurements will be noticeably lower in cracked areas compared to non­damaged con­
crete. Moreover, this paper reported that combining different NDT’s would be most reliable
in terms of crack detection.
Furthermore, surface crack measurements using the indirect transmission of ultrasonic
testing have shown to be reliable. If the pulse velocity in a certain concrete specimen is
already known, it is possible to calculate the crack depth. This is due to the fact, that a
pulse will have to bypass the air gap in the crack. Hence, the path length of the pulse will
become greater. Using this theory, it is possible to calculate the depth of the crack [36].

Correlation between UPV and reinforcement corrosion
An important aspect of damage detection in RC concrete elements is being able to detect
corrosion. Eventually, corroded reinforcement will affect the concrete element, which will
lead to several other damage types. Detection of corrosion is, therefore, a matter of safe
reuse.
An experimental study conducted by Yeih and Huang et al.[22] found a relation between
the attenuation of the ultrasonic pulse amplitude (UPA) and electrochemical parameters.
This was done through an accelerated corrosion experiment of concrete specimens. The
study found that specimens with a smaller rebar diameter had a greater attenuation at an
early stage of corrosion compared to specimens with larger rebars, which can be helpful
in the evaluation of corrosion damage.
Furthermore, the correlation between the instantaneous corrosion rate and amplitude at­
tenuation was investigated. Although no values describing the correlation is was provided
in their paper, it is clear that there is a correlation between the two parameters (Figure 4.8).

4.1.2.6
Correlation between UPV and detection of inhomogeneity
Ultrasonic testing has been shown to detect non­visible defects in concrete. A paper by
Lorenzo et al.[91] on the application of ultrasonic pulse velocity to detect concrete flaws
reported the effectiveness of the ultrasonic pulse velocity method in the determination of
concrete homogeneity. The paper stated that it is possible to estimate the uniformity of
concrete an detect any internal defects that may be present. According to the article, this
can be done by comparing the ultrasonic pulse velocity of a given specimen to that of a
reference specimen.
An extensive review on non­destructive test methods for the evaluation of concrete struc­
tures by Davis and Ansari et al.[81] highlighted that ultrasonic pulse velocity can be used
to make assumptions on the uniformity in concrete members. By determining the com­
pressional wave velocity and the path length, it is possible to make inferences about the
uniformity of the concrete. The principle of the procedure is to compare the pulse ve­
locity measurements to a reference measurement obtained from a homogeneous part
of the concrete. The homogeneous concrete will result in the fastest pulse velocity ob­
tained. Thus, any inhomogeneities can be identified by a considerable reduction in the
pulse velocity. The testing principle is illustrated in Figure 4.10 which depicts the paths of
ultrasonic pulses as they travel from one side of a concrete member to the other side.
Figure 4.10: Travel path of ultrasonic wave at different scenarios for inhomogenity [81]

The first travel path represents the reference measurements which have the shortest time
and travel path between the transducers. The second scenario represents an inhomo­
geneity that causes the pulse to travel at a lower velocity. The third scenario illustrates
a diffracted path which results in a longer travel path due to a small crack, while the last
scenario represents a larger crack or void which interrupts the ultrasonic wave [81].

Results from qualitative interviews
4.3.1
Documentation and procedure: Christian Gamst ­ Rambøll (Norway)
A large part of the thesis is related to the documentation and testing procedure of rein­
forced concrete elements. It was, therefore, essential to interview actors within the field
who had dealt topic. During the literature review, it was discovered that old hollow­core
slabs had been reused in a project at Kristian Augusts gate 13 (KA13) in Oslo, Norway.
The hollow­core slabs were originally used in Regjeringskvartalet in Oslo, Norway. An
interview with Rambøll, who were the consulting engineers on the project, was there­
fore conducted, as their knowledge and experience on the topic would be indispensable.
Christian Gamst, who was part of the team of consulting engineers with Rambøll, con­
tributed with a great amount of knowledge throughout the interview and provided this
study with a relevant report on the reuse part of the project at KA13 containing inspection
procedures for the documentation process. Firstly, it was interesting to know if the Ram­
bøll before the meeting had received any documentation on the hollow core slabs, and
he had the following to say:
”The existing drawings of the hollow core slabs were available from the
manufacturer. In Norway, they are obliged to keep it for five years, although
we were still able to receive project drawings from 1986.”
The importance of proper documentation in this context should not be neglected as it
was one less barrier to hinder the process of reusing the structural concrete elements. In
Norway, the rules dictate that such documentation should be saved for five years. How­
ever, it is implausible that concrete elements will be reused within the first five years of its
production. The report on the project at KA13 stated that they conducted a uniaxial com­
pression test and a carbonation test on drill core specimens. However, no tests measuring
the chloride content was conducted. Moreover, it was interesting to see if the company
conducted any non­destructive test on the hollow core slabs. According to Christian:
”For the project on Kristian Augusts gate 13, we extracted drilled cores for
the uniaxial compression test. A carbonation depth test was also conducted
to determine the remaining lifespan of the hollow core slabs. It was also
necessary to measure the chloride content according to our experts at
SINTEF.”
The reason for not doing any chloride test is most likely since the hollow core slabs weren’t
exposed to the outside during their lifetime. About non­destructive testing, they also had
the following to say:
”No, non­destructive testing was not considered in the project. Drilled cores
were extracted, while the hollow core slabs were cut in half to evaluate
whether or not it was according to the production core, which they were.
Also, the placement of the reinforcement and cover depth was investigated.”
This further highlights the importance of documentation, as it is a necessary step of
reusing in a non­destructive manner.
Testing non­destructively ensures that it is not
needed to extract drill cores and cut the components for all elements. However, this
does require the manufacturer to be liable for any information in the production drawings
for the component’s lifetime. Moreover, it was interesting to explore if the company had
performed any visual inspection and how they approached any external damages. They
70
==PAGE:81==
stated the following :
”Cracks were one of our main concerns. Before disassembling, we inspected
the hollow­core slabs and documented that there were no cracks by
photographing them. After disassembling and reworking the hollow core
slabs, an additional visual inspection was conducted and once again, it was
documented that no visible cracks were identified. We were concerned and
rigid about cracks. If the hollow core slabs had any visible cracks, then they
were discarded.
According to Rambøll, any cracked components should no be reused in any new con­
struction as they should immediately be disqualified elements based on this damage.
Moreover, they highlight the importance of inspecting a component multiple times in the
process and documenting the state of the hollow core slabs during the process. In the
process of disassembling, transporting and storing, the components can be damaged,
especially during the disassembling process. This one of the many reasons to design for
disassembling. After discussing external damages and visual inspection, the interview
shifted to the discussion of internal damages. To this, Christian was asked if they had
conducted any tests to examine the presence of voids, honeycombing and other inhomo­
geneities. He stated the following:
”We did not look into any internal damages. Although, it is important to know
if there are any internal cracks. The shear capacity was examined, and it
was nowhere near the full capacity, so that was not a concern. The tensile
strengths of the hollow core slab were also examined. Looking back at it,
testing for internal damages is something that should be done for future
work.”
Christian Gamst agrees that internal damage detection is an integral part of safe reuse,
which is a valuable statement from him as this thesis incorporates void detection as part
of the experimental testing. Additionally, in the process of reusing structural elements,
many challenges can arise. Concerning this matter, Christian had the following to say:
”The documentation part of the process was the most challenging aspect as
a consulting engineer. Finding out what exactly parameters needed to be
documented was problematic. Reusing structural elements is a new
approach, and not many structural engineering firms have the experience. It
there took a lot of time to figure out what to document and how to do it.”
The big take from this quote is that there is uncertainty surrounding the reuse of struc­
tural elements since there is no standardised approach to reusing structural elements.
Therefore, a standardised approach is necessary to help break down some of the bar­
riers relating to time, price, knowledge, and inconvenience. A lack of experience within

5.1
Strengths and weaknesses of the methods of data
collection
When conducting literature review, experimental testing, and qualitative interviews, it is
essential to be aware of their limitations. Although each data collection method has its
strengths, it is still constructive to reflect on the different weaknesses of the three. Addi­
tionally, combining the use of the methods can be highly beneficial in eliminating these
weaknesses.
5.1.1
Literature review
Doing a literature review is a suitable approach for gaining particular knowledge within
a subject and collecting data. A strength of the literature review is that one can explore
a large amount of relevant literature according to set criteria. Additionally, using several
databases is advantageous in finding a vast amount of valuable sources. Moreover, lit­
erature from these databases can be based on the number of citations and publications,
which indicate the reputability of these sources. Lastly, these databases can be based
on the number of citations and publications, indicating the reputability of these sources.
One of the weaknesses of the literature review is that search results greatly depend on the
generated keywords and how well they are defined, meaning the quality of the literature
review highly depends on ones prior knowledge on the topic. However, some of these
problems can be circumvented by doing multiple iterations in a literature review, as this
will contribute to better knowledge on the topic, which ultimately helps generate more
relevant keywords.
In addition, a search using generated strings created from the keywords can also be
considered a weakness because the number of possible strings created from keywords
is enormous, and it is therefore unlikely for an individual to search using each of these
strings. This means that there is a probability of relevant literature being omitted from the
review. However, having a systematic and intuitive approach to string creation should be
valuable in finding the most relevant strings.
Lastly, using different search databases can provide significantly different results. For
instance, when Google Scholar is used, an endless amount of literature can appear, which
can be hugely overwhelming and time­consuming. However, if sites such as Scopus or
Research Gate are used, a considerable less amount of results will appear. Therefore,
a weakness in the literature review can be having too many results with lower relevance
from a database compared to other databases, where one risks a too narrow search,
which can result in relevant literature being omitted.

5.1.2
Experimental testing
Conducting experimental testing can be highly beneficial to a study as it allows one to test
different theories based on information gained through a literature review, which can be
valuable for instances where the literature is by a lesser a reputable source.
In addition, it also allows for a recreation of specific experiments and the validation of
results obtained from these experiments. Doing experimental testing also allows one
to modify the execution of specific experiments by adding certain test parameters not
included in these experiments.
Moreover, experimental tests on specific theories allow a researcher to have full access to
the data obtained in the experiment, which means that they decide what data is relevant to
the specific study. When other studies do a similar experiment, they may have a different
approach to the subject and therefore choose to omit data useful in other contexts.
A weakness in doing experimental testing relates primarily to the practical aspects of this.
Time is a huge factor when doing experimental testing regarding how much time an indi­
vidual can spend on the experiment and how much time specific experiments take. How­
ever, some experiments do have accelerated variants of specific experiments to reduce
the time of the test.
Furthermore, an experiment can be limited by what equipment and materials are available.
If a researcher does not have access to equipment to perform specific tests, an alternative
experiment must be chosen.
5.1.3
Qualitative interviews
There are many advantages to doing qualitative interviews. First, by conducting inter­
views, one can gain current knowledge from individuals within the industry based on their
real­life experiences. This aspect is valuable as it can take some time for relevant knowl­
edge about specific topics to be published.
Additionally, compared to a literature review, doing qualitative interviews increase the
number of people that one can acquire knowledge from. Scientific researchers can be
specialists on specific topics with a tremendous amount of experience and knowledge.
However, only acquiring knowledge from a smaller share of individuals who share their
work is different from the possibility of interviewing most people within an industry. There­
fore, doing interviews makes it possible to gain knowledge from countless industry actors
who will likely never publish any scientific literature.
Conducting interviews can also give access to resources and additional literature that oth­
erwise would not be available and therefore accelerates the process of doing a literature
review. In addition, doing qualitative interviews also provided the study with access to old
concrete columns used for experimental testing.
One of the weaknesses is that some of the knowledge gained from individuals can be
somewhat anecdotal, suggesting that one should be critical of any information as it can
be based on personal accounts rather than absolute facts. Interviews can thereby be
somewhat subjective, and it is essential to research any claims made by individuals during
an interview.
One of the interviews was conducted with multiple interviewees present, which, to some
degree, can influence what is said in the interview, as having a superior present at an
interview can alter the dynamic of the interview. Some interviewees can therefore sub­
consciously filter themselves during interviews.

5.2
Discussion of literature review
The literature review has identified strong correlations between the two methods for quality
and durability evaluation of reinforced concrete elements. Moreover, several factors that
could influence the electrical resistivity and ultrasonic pulse velocity have been identified.
In this chapter the main findings from the literature are summarized and discussed.
5.2.1
Electrical resistivity
Electrical resistivity testing is gaining popularity as it is a simple, fast, and reliable NDT
method to evaluate quality and durability parameters. The most common measurement
techniques are the two­point uniaxial and four­point Wenner techniques, where the latter
is most suitable for field inspections. However, the electrical resistivity of concrete can be
affected by both concrete properties and the execution of the testing method. These fac­
tors need to be appropriately accounted for when conducting electrical resistivity testing
to obtain precise resistivity values.
It is strongly recommended taking resistivity measurements when concrete is in Saturated
Surface Dry (SSD) condition. Furthermore, aggregate size, content, and distribution have
been identified to have a significant influence on the concrete resistivity due to its hindering
influence on the ionic movement.
In addition, factors such as the presence of reinforcement, cracks, and specimen geom­
etry can have a significant influence on the resistivity measurements. Although, to avoid
measuring at locations with reinforcement, it is recommended that a rebar detector is used
to establish the location of any embedded reinforcement.
Delaminated areas have also been shown to disturb the electrical resistivity measure­
ments. However the detection of delamination is not quantifiable. Electrical resistivity
measurements are therefore primarily used to identify delaminated areas by observing
the measured difference in resistivity compared to intact concrete.
Moreover, electrode­concrete contact and signal frequency are shown to affect the resis­
tivity measurements. For the two­point uniaxial measurements, it has been identified that
electrode contact solution and electrode contact pressure can influence the measured
resistivity values while variation in probe spacing are known to influence the resistivity
measurements when using the four­point Wenner technique.
Strong correlations have been established between electrical resistivity and concrete dif­
fusivity. Studies have found that concrete resistivity is inversely correlated to chloride
ingress and that a lower resistivity indicates faster chloride diffusion. However, chloride
concentration is suggested to have a small influence on the concrete resistivity. In theory,
there is a linear relationship between the chloride diffusion coefficient and electrical resis­
tivity. However, due to temperature effects related to rapid chloride permeability testing,
this correlation tends to be non­linear.
Both ASTM C1202 and AASHTO TP 95 offer categorization for the degree of chloride
penetrability based on the charges passed and measured surface resistivity, respec­
tively. These standard can aid engineers in the decision­making and selection of po­
tential reusable elements when knowing their future exposure environment. However,
77
==PAGE:88==
these standards only provide information regarding the chloride penetrability level without
quantifying the current state of chloride contents present in the concrete. ER testing can
be used to estimate the chloride concentration and penetration depth, but only after a
reference measurement has been collected destructively.
Moreover, according to Table 4.1 the risk of corrosion can be determined using electrical
resistivity measurements. Additionally, a strong correlation can be found between the
electrical resistivity of concrete and the rate of corrosion in reinforcement. Surface elec­
trical resistivity testing can therefore be highly beneficial in the inspection phase as it can
quantify both the risk of corrosion and chloride penetrability level according to standard­
ized procedures.
5.2.2
Ultrasonic testing
Ultrasonic testing has become more popular in the field of structural health monitoring
(SMH) as it is a fast and reliable method for the evaluation of concrete quality parameters.
Research indicates that ultrasonic testing can be applied to evaluate the compressive
strength of existing concrete structures and is currently being used to evaluate and monitor
the quality of new concrete structures. However, the method has not yet been recognized
as method of evaluating and re­documenting the reusability of concrete elements.
The studied literature supports the applicability of ultrasonic testing in predicting the com­
pressive strength of concrete. However, the ultrasonic pulse velocity (UPV) is largely
dependent on the concrete material properties resulting in uncertainties in estimated re­
sults. Researchers are currently working on establishing acceptable recommendations
based on broad data collection from experimental testing on local construction projects.
Moreover, the ultrasonic pulse velocity method is found to be suitable for the determination
of the overall concrete quality. Re­documentation and evaluation of the concrete quality
can be performed according to the quality grading tables presented in Table 4.3 which
is found to be reliable in the many studies conducted in the last 20 years. Furthermore,
ultrasonic testing could be applied to evaluate the homogeneity of RC concrete elements
as a quality assurance step in combination with the concrete quality grading. The com­
bination of concrete grading and homogeneity evaluation could be crucial in a material
passport.
The literature review found that ultrasonic testing can detect the most common internal
defects (cracks, voids, honeycombing, etc.) in a quantifiable manner. The method, there­
fore, shows to be a valuable aid in the quality control and selection of reusable RC ele­
ments. Test test method can be used evaluate homogeneity in concrete, which could be
highly beneficial in the inspection phase.
Lastly, ultrasonic testing is well suited to field inspection due to its portable nature and
flexibility in transducer arrangement. By applying direct, semi­direct, and indirect trans­
ducer arrangements, the measurement of the ultrasonic pulse velocity can be adapted to
the desired testing location.

and chloride diffusivity as presented in subsubsection 4.1.1.2.

5.4
Discussion on the implementation of reuse in the
building industry
In this section, the implementation of direct reuse in the building industry will be discussed
based on the results obtained in the literature review, experimental testing, and qualitative
interviews. This includes discussing the need for direct reuse of concrete elements and
the reluctant forces hindering its implementation in the circular economy. Furthermore,
an inspection procedure to evaluate the reusability of concrete elements is proposed and
discussed.
5.4.1
Why is there a need for reuse in the building industry?
The world’s resources are consumed at a pace where it puts immense pressure on ecosys­
tems. If changes do not happen, high costs and consequences will have to be paid for by
future generations [2]. The construction industry, which is a major consumer of resources
and energy, plays a vital role in creating this change.
Among the most important driving forces for increasing the degree of reuse in the con­
struction industry are the environmental effects it could have. The reuse of building ma­
terials reduces the need to extract virgin resources and the production of new materials.
It has been identified that several studies conclude that the reuse of concrete elements
provides greenhouse gas savings compared to recycling.
However, there is a constant need for examples to showcase the benefits of reuse and
highlight how investing in reuse products can initiate environmental and socio­economic
value. The Kristian August gate 13 project discussed in the interview with Rambøll (sub­
section 4.3.1) is one of few projects in Scandinavia that has reported their experiences
and findings on the use of reuse of structural elements (steel and concrete) in a new
building project, providing the building industry valuable information on this matter.
To meet the requirements of limiting global warming, greenhouse gas emissions need to
be reduced by a significant amount. As the building industry is responsible for approx­
imately 40% of all the materials used on a global scale, while it produces around 35%
of all waste in the world [6] it owns a responsibility to contribute further to the reduction
of greenhouse gas emissions and other environmental impacts. The implementation of
direct reuse has proven to be a wise step to take in accomplishing these goals.
It is estimated that a larger part of the prefabricated concrete elements is suitable for reuse
after the building’s end of life. Additionally, it is estimated that the lifespan of the structural
system of a building can have a life span of up to 300 years [7]. The market for the reuse
of concrete elements, therefore, has untapped potential.
5.4.2
What are the reluctant forces hindering the reuse of concrete
elements in the building industry today?
Different barriers relucting the implementation of circular economy of concrete elements
in the building industry have been identified throughout studied literature and conducted
interviews. Both literature and interviews with the Lendager group, Danish standard, and
Rambøll pointed out barriers related to legal issues. The circular economy of concrete
elements today is hindered by the risk involved in the documentation process of technical
parameters and the fact that many buildings are not designed for disassembly. Moreover,
the documentation of technical parameters of concrete for reuse purposes has not yet
been standardized, inflating the risk involved in this procedure. As a whole, this creates
an uncertain atmosphere that discourages the stakeholders from taking action towards
the direct reuse of concrete elements.
82
==PAGE:93==
A vast majority of the existing building structures today are not built with disassembly in
mind making the reuse of structural elements a complex and challenging process. Pre­
fabricated concrete elements are most often connected to in­situ cast structures to obtain
their desired structural behaviour. The disassembling process in such cases can reduce
structural parameters while simultaneously creating concerns for building safety. The
interview with Rambøll disclosed that the magnitude of resources dedicated to the disas­
sembly process on KA13 was not economically sustainable.
As a direct effect of the above­mentioned barriers, the process of reusing concrete el­
ements can become costly. Since cost­efficiency is a governing factor in most building
projects, implementing reuse gets less attractive. Furthermore, as there is more uncer­
tainty involved in the documentation and disassembly procedures concerns in the bud­
geting.
The above­mentioned barriers clearly indicate that there is a need for a standardized
approach to direct reuse of concrete structures.

as the concrete cover depth, reinforcement diameter and reinforcement spacing. It may
be necessary to cut an element in such a scenario to ensure that critical is obtained. If
a large number of identical elements from the same building is considered for reuse, the
cost of destroying one element will be acceptable to enable further reuse of the residual
bulk of elements. Moreover, the cut element could provide vital information regarding
the carbonation and chloride ingress, enabling the engineers to estimate the remaining
service life of the element.
Furthermore, it is proposed to visually inspect all elements, following a detailed inspection
plan. Data collected from the literature review and the interview with Rambøll indicated
that visual inspection plays an important role in the initial phase of re­documentation.
The visual inspection covers a comprehensive examination of the concrete to identify and
define the current element condition. However, the visual inspection is often limited to
surface evaluation and should therefore be applied as the first evaluation method of the
procedure.
When conducting a visual inspection, it is imperative that the inspector documents any
observations deviating from normal and healthy­looking concrete. The inspector should
actively look for signs of exhaustion from physical loading, deformations, defects, im­
perfections, and distress. Moreover, the inspector should document any visible sign of
damage and assess the extent, nature, and severity of the observed damage. Cracks
are a common damage type that is highly influencing the assessment of the degree of
reusability. When cracks are identified, the cause of crack has must be considered to be
able to decide whether the element is still suited for reuse. Furthermore, the crack must
be measured, and an assessment of consequences affecting the load­bearing capacity,
integrity, and permeability must be performed.
It is highly recommended to plan the visual inspection before it is carried out so that the
inspector and the various stakeholders in advance have agreed upon the minimum criteria
that the element should fulfil. This may be that the element should not have any visual
defects, which was the case for Rambøll’s re­documentation of the HC­slabs reused in
the KA13 project.
If the elements fulfil the minimum criteria for the visual inspection, non­destructive testing
is recommended to evaluate the quality and durability parameters for the concrete ele­
ments as well as to evaluate any internal or accepted defects more carefully. According
to the procedure, three categories of investigation are proposed. These are the evaluation
of structural parameters, damage detection, and service life estimation.
Ultrasonic testing has been shown to accurately predict the compressive strength of the
concrete and is therefore recommended to be used to evaluate the compressive strength
of the element. Furthermore, ultrasonic testing can be applied to evaluate the quality
grade of the concrete according to Table 4.3 while simultaneously detect any internal
defects as these will result in significantly lower pulse velocities and signal amplitudes as
described in subsection 4.1.2 and subsection 4.2.2. Moreover, ultrasonic testing can be
used to evaluate any present crack.
85
==PAGE:96==
To perform reliable service­life estimates, it is considered necessary to extract one drilled
core in the evaluation of the carbonation development. If the element has been exposed
to external environmental stresses and is considered to be subject to chloride penetration,
the same core can be split in two, enabling rapid chloride analysis to be performed on the
other half. This destructive evaluation will provide the engineer with reference levels of
carbonation and chloride concentration required to perform the further non­destructive
evaluation of the residual elements using electrical resistivity testing.
As presented in subsubsection 4.1.1.2 and subsection 4.2.3, there is a strong correlation
between the electrical resistivity and parameters related to the ingress of chloride ions into
concrete. When the desired chloride migration coefficient has been obtained from a rapid
chloride experiment, e.g. NT Built 492 [40], surface electrical resistivity measurements
can be performed to evaluate if any potential changes in the residual elements migration
coefficients. However, as the chloride migration coefficient only has a small influence
on the concrete resistivity value, the accuracy of this prediction can be influenced by the
execution of the test as well as the various influencing parameters presented in subsub­
section 4.1.1.1. It is therefore recommended to conduct surface resistivity (SR) testing
according to AASTHO TP 95. Moreover, SR testing is recommended to assess the risk
for corrosion of embedded reinforcement according to Table 4.1 in scenarios where chlo­
ride exposure is considered to be unlikely, and a rapid chloride permeability test has not
been performed.
Furthermore, it is recommended to plan the disassembly of the elements in detail. A well­
thought­out disassembly plan will be resource­saving and facilitate a more efficient and
safe construction process. If the elements are only partially designed for disassembly,
a greater risk of damage is involved in the disassembly process. It could be wise to
perform a risk analysis in such a scenario to identify and quantify the probability of potential
issues arising. Furthermore, the potential problems identified in the risk analysis should
be evaluated based on the degree of consequence they can inflict on the element.
5.5
Further work
When approaching a topic as comprehensive as the reuse of concrete elements, it is
necessary to narrow the scope early on in the process and cover the topic as in­depth
as possible. However, doing this will leave many areas of the matter uncovered. It is,
therefore, necessary to take a step back, expand the scope and outlay what topics need
to be covered for further work and what the limitations of the study are.
For the experimental testing, a more extensive collection of data would be beneficial for
the study. A larger pool of data is expected to provide a better correlation between test­
ing parameters. This will ultimately create more accurate models in estimating parame­
ters concerning the quality and durability of concrete elements. Creating more accurate
models is an essential aspect of further work. More accurate models of the relationship
between the compressive strength and ultrasonic pulse velocity could also be created by
using artificial neural networks [85]. Therefore, it would be beneficial for the future work
of the study to take a multi­variable approach using models based on artificial neural net­
works.
In any further work, additional experimental tests would need to be conducted to further
explore the correlations between the ultrasonic pulse velocity, electrical resistivity and
other damage types such as corrosion of reinforcement from Table 5.1 as well as other
structural parameters.
86
==PAGE:97==
Moreover, including other non­destructive test methods would also be helpful for the study
as each method has its strength and capabilities. Impact echo testing and radiographic
testing, amongst other methods, have been described as applicable methods for evalu­
ating concrete in a non­destructive manner [101]. Additionally, using the different non­
destructive test methods in combinations with each other is reported to be beneficial for
some test [73].
Any further work should also explore the possibility of designing for disassembly as it is
reported that this aspect can be key in whether or not the reuse of some prefabricated
concrete elements can be considered to have a lower environmental impact [6]. Creating
a safe and efficient disassembly will further aid in this aspect.
Lastly, it should be noted that additional transportation can be expected to occur in the life
cycle of reused concrete elements compared to recycled concrete, which is due to dissem­
bled concrete elements having more significant logistical challenges. Reused elements
should be disassembled, loaded, transported, unloaded, stored, loaded and transported
to the building site. It is, therefore, necessary to consider the environmental impacts con­
sidered with these process in any future work. The only way to be utterly sure that reusing
an element is more sustainable is to conduct a life cycle assessment on the individual
cases to evaluate if reusing an element in the given situation have a lesser environmen­
tal impact than the alternative. However, this could be substituted by general guidelines
on what determines the degree of sustainability instead of doing a complete life cycle
assessment.

6
Conclusion
6.1
Literature review
This paper has identified several factors that might influence the electrical resistivity of
concrete through an extensive literature review. These include w/c ratio, aggregate type
and size, temperature, curing condition, electrode contact, probe spacing and embedded
reinforcement. The main findings from the literature review on the correlations between
electrical resistivity testing and quality and durability parameters are listed as following:
• Concrete resistivity and chloride ingress are inversely related, where lower resistivity
values indicate faster chloride diffusion.
• Experimental research has found a strong non­linear correlation between concrete
resistivity and the chloride diffusion coefficient, hence ER measurements can be
used as a reliable non­destructive technique to estimate the service life of existing
RC elements.
• A relationship between concrete resistivity and risk for reinforcement corrosion has
been established based on experimental testing. In general, it is agreed that there
is a high risk for the corrosion process to take place when the concrete electrical
resistivity is less than 10 kΩcm.
• Electrical resistivity testing has been shown to detect cracks and delamination in a
non­quantifiable manner.
A variety of different parameters can influence ultrasonic testing. These include the den­
sity and elasticity of the material; hence factors like w/c ratio, aggregate gradation, and
curing time are likely to affect the measurement. Likewise, factors relating to reinforce­
ment and specimen geometry are also shown to affect the UPV measurements. The
main findings from the literature review on the correlations between ultrasonic testing and
quality and durability parameters are listed as following:
• A strong correlation has been obtained between ultrasonic pulse velocity and con­
crete quality grading.
• A strong non­linear relationship between ultrasonic pulse velocity and compressive
strength has been obtained in several studies. However, the ultrasonic pulse veloc­
ity is largely dependent on the concrete material properties resulting in uncertainties
in estimated results.
• Ultrasonic testing shows to be efficient in crack detection and methods for crack
depth measurements have been developed.
• Various studies have concluded that ultrasonic testing is applicable to estimate the
corrosion of embedded reinforcement concrete elements.
• Studies show that ultrasonic testing can be applied to detect internal defects and
inhomogeneities as well as surface defects such as delamination.
6.2
Experimental testing
The conclusion drawn from the experimental test on the determination of concrete com­
pressive strength using ultrasonic testing can be summarized as followed:
• There is a correlation between the compressive strength of cast concrete specimens
and the ultrasonic pulse velocity achieving an R2 ­ value of 0.99 using exponential
regression.
88
==PAGE:99==
• The regression model could accurately predict the compressive strengths below 25
MPa and underestimated the values for stronger concrete, hence the regression
model is applicable in estimating the compressive strength in cast concrete speci­
mens.
• The ultrasonic pulse velocity method is more accurate and less destructive than the
Schmidt Hammer test.
• Concrete quality grading is not an accurate measure for the compressive strength
of concrete specimens.
From the experimental tests on void detection, the following conclusions can be made:
• It is possible to detect artificially created polystyrene voids in cast concrete speci­
mens using the ultrasonic pulse amplitude.
• The pulse velocity of concrete is reduced by artificially created voids bigger than 20
mm.
• It is not possible to detect simulated honeycombing or voids smaller than 20mm
The following conclusion can be made from the determination of chloride permeability
using electrical resistivity testing:
• An exponential relationship between the bulk electrical resistivity and chloride depth
measurements was obtained with an R2 ­ value of 0.94.
• A power correlation between the decrease in electrical resistivity and the increase
in chloride concentration was obtained with an R2 ­ value of 0.93.
• A linear correlation between the electrical resistivity and the non­steady­state mi­
gration coefficient was obtained with an R2 ­ value of 0.88.
• An inverse linear correlation between the electrical resistivity and charge passed
was obtained with an R2 ­ value of 0.99.
• Using penetrability levels from AASHTO TP 95 and ASTM C120, the specimens
were found to have high chloride­ion penetrability levels.
• It is possible to evaluate the chloride penetration depth, chloride concentration, non­
steady­state migration coefficient, and chloride penetrability level by combining elec­
trical resistivity with rapid chloride penetration tests such as NT Build 492 and NT
Built 443.
6.3
Qualitative interviews
The results from the qualitative interviews can be summarized as the following:
• There is a great demand for a standardized procedure for the reuse of reinforced
concrete elements.
• A significant challenge with reusing structural elements is the risks involved.
• Designing for disassembly will ease the reuse of concrete elements.
• Not having the proper documentation of an element is a barrier to reuse.
