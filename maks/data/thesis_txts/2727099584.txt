ABSTRACT
Contamination analysis within the pharmaceutical and food industry is of utmost importance to
ensure products are safe to use. To determine the degree of contamination, the heterogeneity of
a sample can be used.
The goal of this paper is to use deep learning models to estimate the
sample heterogeneity in images of microbes. The images are provided by IntuBio and are manually
annotated by the author of this thesis and approved by domain experts from IntuBio such that
deep learning models can be trained. The training data consist of five different types of microbes
and were selected based on their differences in morphology. The object detection model named
Faster R-CNN and the multi-class segmentation model named U-Net are the selected deep learning
models. These two models were trained to locate and classify the microbial colonies in the images.
The Faster R-CNN model performed below expectations so a synthetic, pseudo-synthetic and pixel
intensity experiment was performed to investigate what was challenging the model. It was found
that the 2D morphology of the microbial colonies is the main challenge in the images for the Faster
R-CNN model. The U-Net model performed as expected and could accurately locate the microbial
colonies. The estimation of sample heterogeneity based on the two models was quite similar where
the heterogeneity of only one of the five sample sets was estimated correctly. It was found that
estimating sample heterogeneity is a challenging task and that different types of microbes can
appear quite similar which can challenge the classification of microbes for deep-learning models.

INTRODUCTION
Contamination in a production line is a significant challenge in the food and pharmaceutical industry.
In a wide range of applications including, milk and dairy production [1], preparation of drinking
water [2], and manufacturing of sterile drugs and equipment [3] it is of high importance to ensure
products are not contaminated since contaminated products can harm the consumer.
Different
frameworks and strategies are currently used in the food and pharmaceutical industry to perform
risk analysis in regard to contamination in products. HACCP (Hazard Analysis and Critical Control
Points) [4] is recognized worldwide as one of the most effective methodical systems for ensuring the
quality and safety of products and is based on creating various critical control points (CCP) that are
checked at specific times to establish a risk assessment. Another methodical system named FMEA
(Failure Modes and Effects Analysis) was developed to identify error modes and prevent them from
occurring through a methodical examination of the production design [5]. Combining both HACCP
and FMEA can provide the most beneficial results [6].
This project will focus on microbiological contamination and more specifically bacterial and fungus
contamination.
The current guideline for examining non-sterile products in the pharmaceutical
industry is provided by [7] which states that the degree of contamination must be based on the
microbial count in a product. The methods presented in [7] have incubation times varying from
3 to 7 days depending on incubation temperature, method and microorganisms. The microbial
count is then obtained after a microbiologist has manually counted the bacteria.
Furthermore,
in the food industry, the detection of bacterial pathogens is a time-consuming process that can
take up to 2-3 days for preliminary identification and up to 7 days for confirmation of the specific
types of pathogens [8]. Therefore, to save time and resources and possibly increase accuracy in
microbial count there is great interest in developing Rapid Microbial Methods (RMMs) which can
give microbial counts quicker [9]. To quantify bacteria or fungi one can use the colony-forming unit
(CFU) metric. A colony is defined as a cluster of bacteria or fungi growing together [10].
In addition to determining the degree of contamination, it is also useful to know the microbial types
which contaminate a product. In both the food and pharmaceutical industry there are numerous
guidelines that dictate which types of microbes are allowed in certain products. For example, when
filtrating drinking water [11] states that there must be no traces of Escherichia coli bacteria in the
water. Thus, knowing the microbial types in a sample can provide assurance that a product is safe
to use and also help to prevent future contamination.
IntuBio is a company that addresses the long incubation times in [7] and [8] while still adhering to
the same qualitative standards in counting bacteria. Moreover, the IntuBio system also allows for
high throughput meaning a lot of samples can be analysed at the same time. Currently, IntuBio
excels in counting CFUs but has not developed any software that can classify microbes and estimate
sample heterogeneity. Here, the sample heterogeneity is the number of different types of microbes
present in a sample. If IntuBio could also provide a measure of sample heterogeneity that can help
the customers in their contamination analysis. That is what this project will be centred on, which

LITERATURE STUDY
Identification and classification of bacteria have been popular research topics in the food and phar-
maceutical industry. Identifying and counting the number of bacteria can provide a measure of how
contaminated a product is whereas classifying the types of bacteria in a product can help prevent
future contamination. [12] provides a review of numerous methods used to identify and classify
microbes in the past 30 years. In [12], classical image analysis methods for bacteria identification
relies on thresholding, edge detection and watershed methods. [13] presents a method where CFUs
are identified by binarizing images with a threshold followed by morphological operations to remove
noise. Across 3 different bacteria strains, [13] obtained an average error of 2.90% between the system
count and an expert manual count. [14] presents an edge detection method where a Gauss and a
Laplace filter of size 5x5 followed by a median filter of rank 3 are applied to images of size 512x512
pixels. That sequence of filters finds edges in the images which are used for CFU identification after
thresholding the images. The average error ranged from 1.9% to 2.7% for cell counting. [15] also
utilizes a threshold to segment CFUs combined with a watershed algorithm that can split merged
CFUs. An average CFU counting error of 6.8% was obtained. [12] also mentions [16] as a classical
method that can classify microbes. In [16], a support vector machine (SVM) is used to classify
microbes based on four features which are colony solidity, compactness, color mean, and color vari-
ance. Testing the SVM on two different types of bacteria and with different combinations of the
four features, a F4 score of 0.9 was obtained.
Multiple studies have also focused on using deep learning methods to identify and classify bacteria in
images such as [17][18][19][20]. [18] scans agar plates every 30 minutes to obtain lens-free holographic
images which are used in two different deep neural networks (DNNs). The first DNN detects the
CFUs based on bacterial growth, whereas the second DNN classifies the bacterial types based on the
spatiotemporal features obtained from the coherent images. Within 12 hours, [18] managed to obtain
more than 95% detection sensitivity and approximately 97.2%, 84.0%, and 98.5% classification
accuracy for E. coli, K. aerogenes, and K. pneumoniae, respectively. [17] also utilizes deep learning in
the form of coupling convolutional and recurrent neural networks with lens-free microscopy imaging.
After 8 hours this method reached an average bacterial detection of 96% along with a classification
precision and sensitivity around 93.1% and 94.0% for 7 different bacteria. [19] presents an object
detection method for detecting malaria parasites in blood cell images which are similar to images
of bacteria. The object detection method uses a Faster R-CNN model for detecting red blood cells
and non-red blood cells. Discarding red blood cells and other problematic cells, a classification
accuracy of 59% was obtained for the Faster R-CNN model alone. Using the output of the Faster
R-CNN model together with an AlexNet classifier, [19] obtained a 98% classification accuracy when
having removed red blood cells and other difficult cells. Similar to [19], [20] proposes a hybrid
method consisting of a U-Net and regional proposal network (RPN) to produce segmentations of
cells in microscopy images. The output mask from the U-Net is combined with the bounding box
predictions from the RPN and used as input to a watershed algorithm which outputs the final cell

The method in this thesis will use a Faster R-CNN model to detect and classify microbes since it

MICROBIOLOGY
Overview
This section will provide an introduction to the microbiology regarding CFUs and bacterial
growth. The characteristics of the chosen microbes for this project are described.
Microorganisms, also known as microbes, are living organisms of microscopic size. Bacteria and
fungi are examples of microbes. Microbes play a vital role in every environment on earth and can
be both beneficial or threatening to human health [22]. Some microbes are widely used in food
production as part of fermentation processes whereas other microbes, such as pathogenic bacteria,
can cause diseases such as tuberculosis.
Therefore, it is important to have clean environments
without dangerous microbes when producing food or medicine that is consumed by humans. To
count CFUs in a sample it is necessary to provide an optimal environment for the bacteria or fungi
to grow in. Such an environment much be rich in nutrients and must have a specific temperature
based on which type of bacteria or fungi that is cultivated [23].
Typically, samples are placed
in an incubator which is an enclosed device that controls the temperature, humidity and other
environmental conditions. Placing bacteria in an optimal environment will allow growth by binary
fission which is the primary method of reproduction for bacteria [24]. This process is illustrated in
Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Illustration of binary fission, the primary method of reproduction for prokaryotic or-
ganisms. The illustration shows how a bacteria cell is split into two identical bacteria cells. It must
be noted that the different colouring is for visualization purposes. The DNA and the nucleus are
identical. Image is from [25].

The reproduction time varies for different types of bacteria. Bacteria from the species of Escherichia
have a reproduction time of approximately 20 minutes whereas the tuberculosis-causing bacteria,
Mycobacterium tuberculosis, has a reproduction time of 15-20 hours [23]. After a number of repro-
ductions, the bacteria or fungi will cluster together and form CFUs. These CFUs will continue to
grow and become large enough for visual inspection and automatic detection.
As mentioned earlier CFUs grow by utilizing the nutrients located close to them. However, after
some time, they will have depleted most of the nutrients in their local area. To ensure survival,
the CFUs will need to spread away from their local area to find new nutrients. A typical way for
bacteria to spread is by performing swarming motility. Swarming motility is a type of bacterial
behaviour where bacteria within a CFU migrate away from the CFU and spread across the surface
of the medium they are located in. During this spread the bacteria moves in coherent flows to
form densely packed clusters [26]. Visually, these newly formed clusters will appear very similar to
low-contrast CFUs without actually being CFUs. Thus, swarming causes issues when identifying
CFUs as one cannot be sure whether a specific object is a low-contrast CFU or swarming.

There exist a vast number of different types of microbes. Therefore, a traditional classification
system has been created by microbiologists which can subdivide microbes into different classes.
The major groups of microbes, according to [27], are bacteria, archaea, fungi, algae, protozoa,
and viruses. This project will focus on bacteria and fungi which also can be subdivided based on
different characteristics such as phylogeny, metabolism, environment and morphology [28]. Since
the data for this project consists of 2D images, morphology will be the characteristic that is used
to differentiate between CFUs. An overview of how to subdivide bacteria based on 3D morphology
is given in Figure 3.2.

Overview
This section describes the IntuBio method and how a liquid sample of microbes must be
prepared to initialize an analysis. The oCelloScope, which is the imaging component in the
setup, is also described along with the post-processing of the images which aims to remove
noise in the images. Finally, the annotation process of the images is described.

• 4.2 Imaging

4.1
Preparing a sample
The IntuBio method is a type of membrane filtration technique where a viscous liquid is filtrated
and microbes are collected on a filter. The filtration device is designed by IntuBio and consists of a
sample inlet, a filter ring, a filter ejector and a piston with a sample outlet. An illustration of the
device can be seen in Figure 4.1. The liquid is injected through the sample inlet and the CFUs are
collected on top of the filter ring. The top piece of the device is removed and the filter is then placed
in a microtiter plate which consists of a number of wells. Separating the filter from the bottom
piece is done by pushing the filter ejector. When the filter is in a well then a liquid medium can be
added on top and on the sides of the filter. After a short period of time, the medium will solidify
which fixates the microbes on the filter. The whole procedure can be seen in Figure 4.1

4.2
Imaging
The oCelloScope is the component in the IntuBio set-up which scans the wells in the microtiter plate

4.4
Annotation
IntuBio has developed a semi-automatic detection algorithm to find CFUs in normalized images.
The input to the algorithm consists of a reference image typically captured after an hour of in-
cubation and a normalized image. The algorithm performs a thresholding to find CFUs followed
by morphological operations and area filtration to remove noise. The output of the algorithm is a
binary mask where the foreground consists of detected CFUs. Manual classification has been per-
formed by the author where each detected CFU has been given a class label corresponding to the
described classes in section 3. Three of the eight sample sets consist of mixed samples, where it can
be difficult to classify the CFUs since they can look quite similar. Therefore, all the detected CFUs
have been inspected along with the entire time stack of each CFU to accurately classify the CFUs.
Moreover, it must be noted that the detection algorithm is not bulletproof and might detect objects
which are not CFUs. Especially in samples with swarming, it is hard both for a human expert and
for the detection algorithm to decide which objects are CFUs and which are swarming. That is
another reason for manual inspection of the CFUs to ensure swarming is detected and excluded.

Challenges

Merging CFUs

one another and sometimes will grow into each other and merge into one CFU. This, in particular,

data-set. The merging of CFUs is a challenge with regard to the change in morphology. When two
CFUs merge together the resulting CFU are usually much larger and not very circular as seen in
Figure 5.1. The resulting mask of a merged CFU will not resemble the typical morphology of the
individual CFUs and therefore challenge the classification model.

with a smoother contour. The reason for a smaller erosion is to be sure not to cut off any branches.

5.2
Patches
The high-resolution images captured by IntuBios oCelloScope are of size 6119x5319 pixels. Images
of that size take up a lot of computer memory which makes any type of image analysis quite slow.
Therefore, images are split into smaller patches. Two sets of patches with different sizes have been
created.
One set consists of patches with size 200x200 pixels whereas the other set consists of
patches with size 660x660 pixels. Patches of size 200x200 pixels typically contain one or two CFUs
whereas patches of size 660x660 pixels can contain up to 10 CFUs. The idea behind using two
different sets with different patch sizes is to compare model performance and investigate if multiple
CFUs in one patch will challenge the model.
There are different approaches to take when creating patches. The simplest approach is simply to
create a grid where each region in the grid equals the desired patch size. A patch size of 660x660
pixels equals an 8x9 grid and a patch size of 200x200 pixels that equals a 26x30 grid. As one might
notice the grids do not capture all of the 6119x5319 pixels in the Intubio images. Therefore, the
grids are centralized in the Intubio images which means the outer borders are not included in the
patches. Since most CFUs grow in the middle of the images only a very small fraction of all CFUs
are potentially not included in the patches following this approach. An example of a patch grid
can be seen in Figure 5.5. A disadvantage of subdividing an image into patches is that the CFUs
located on the borders of the grid are also subdivided into different patches. Thus, only fractions
of the CFUs at the grid borders are represented in some patches. A fraction of a CFU can look
quite different compared to how the whole CFU looks like. That is an issue for a model trying to
learn the morphology of CFUs. However, the issue can be partially solved by giving the CFUs at
the border a distinct class label. In this way, the border CFUs do not disturb the signal of the
well-represented CFUs.
Another approach could be to create patches in a "sliding window" fashion. That means patches
will overlap each other. The objective of using a sliding-window approach is to increase the number
of patches. More patches results in a larger training set which potentially can improve the model.
Moreover, the number of well-represented CFUs will likely increase since a CFU located at the
border of one patch will maybe not be located at the border of the neighbouring patch since the
patches overlap. Despite increasing the number of patches, the overlapping of patches creates the
challenge of dividing the patches into a train, test and validation set. To avoid data leakage between
the train, test and validation set, neighbouring patches must be placed in the same set. It requires a
sophisticated book-keeping system of the patches to avoid this data leakage problem. Based on this
need for a book-keeping system it has been decided not to implement the sliding window approach
but instead implement the regular grid approach.

characteristics of other types of microbes. To obtain good and clean patches a tradeoff has been
made with the number of patches which has been decreased. A reduction in the number of patches

6.1
Dimensionality reduction methods
Dimensionality reduction methods (DRMs) are unsupervised machine-learning methods that reduce
high-dimensional data sets into low-dimensional data sets. A good DRM is a method which can
convert most of the variation in the high-dimensional data set into low-dimensional data. The chosen
DRMs for this analysis are PCA and t-SNE because they use linear and non-linear transformations,
respectively. By applying a linear and a non-linear method the goal is that at least one of the
methods can successfully capture the relationships among the data points. The output of the DRMs
must be used in combination with a clustering algorithm to determine the sample heterogeneity.
The number of distinct clusters in the 2D space provides the number of different microbes in the
sample. In this analysis, a patch around each CFU is used as input to the DRMs. The patches
are selected based on annotated masks which means the analysis of the DRMs proposes only a half
solution to the objective of the thesis since the detection of CFUs has been skipped. Thus, the
analysis can be seen as a proof-of-concept and if the results are promising a segmentation of CFUs
can be implemented beforehand.
The patch size is fixed at 300x300 pixels to ensure that the largest CFUs can be captured within
a patch. Moreover, patches of a fixed size are required to use PCA and t-SNE since each patch
must be represented in the same high-dimensional space. It is important to ensure that only one
CFU is captured within each patch. Therefore patches containing multiple CFUs or parts of other
CFUs are discarded. Choosing a smaller patch will likely increase the percentage of CFU pixels in

the CFU. Moreover, smaller patches might increase the number of patches since fewer patches will

PCA is regarded as one of the most popular DRMs and is widely used and well-documented. For
those reasons and based on the fact that PCA linearly transforms data into a coordinate system

principal components and are the eigenvectors of the covariance matrix of the high dimensional data
set. The eigenvectors are ranked according to their eigenvalues such that the eigenvector with the
largest eigenvalue accounts for the largest variation in the data set. In this analysis, the principal
components have been computed by using singular value decomposition (SVD) which can factorize

6.2
Faster R-CNN
Faster R-CNN is a state-of-the-art object detection model which in [35] showed great performance
for the PASCAL VOC [36] and MS COCO [37] data sets. As the name indicates, it is faster than its
two predecessors, R-CNN and Fast R-CNN, because Faster R-CNN uses a regional proposal network
(RPN) instead of a selective search or edge box detection. Moreover, training can be done end-to-
end due to shared convolutional layers between the different parts of the model which increases the
training speed. Faster R-CNN has been chosen based on the above abilities. In this project, Faster
R-CNN presents a more complete solution to the project thesis compared to the DRMs since sample
heterogeneity is determined using only the normalized image and not on ground truth masks which
is the case for the DRMs. However, the sample heterogeneity is not directly determined by Faster
R-CNN. Since Faster R-CNN is a supervised model it is trained to learn a specific target which is
finding objects in an image and classifying those objects. Therefore, the sample heterogeneity will
be determined by the number of different predicted class labels in each image. The model is trained
on microbes with different types of morphology to ensure that if a new unseen microbe is presented

morphology. The model is composed of two stages where the first stage generates bounding boxes

6.2.1
Stage 1
As illustrated in Figure 6.2 the first component in stage 1 of the model is the backbone convolutional
neural network (CNN). The backbone CNN takes an image as input and outputs a feature map.
During the convolutions, the image is downsampled along the spatial dimension.
The next component in stage 1 is the regional proposal network (RPN) which utilizes the feature
map from the backbone CNN. Each point in the feature map is used to create a specified number
of anchors. Anchors are bounding boxes and are defined to have a specified number of different
rectangular sizes and aspect ratios.
During training, all anchors are sorted into three different
categories. The sorting is done by computing the intersection over union (IoU) between each ground
truth bounding box and each anchor. In object detection, IoU is used as an accuracy measure of
how well a prediction fits the ground truth. Figure 6.3 shows the idea behind IoU where the red
box functions as a prediction and the blue box is the ground truth.
Figure 6.3: Illustration of intersection over union (IoU). The red box can be seen as a prediction
and the blue box can be seen as ground truth. The IoU is computed by dividing the area of overlap
area between two boxes by the area of the union of the two boxes. The IoU is used as a measure of
accuracy with object detection.
The best IoU value is 1 which is obtained when a prediction is precisely equal to the ground truth.
An IoU value of 0 indicates that the prediction does not overlap at all with the ground truth box.
Two user-specified IoU thresholds sort the anchors into a foreground category containing anchor
boxes with large IoU to the ground truth bounding boxes and a background category containing
anchors with low IoU to the ground truth bounding boxes. The anchors which have IoU scores
between the two thresholds are discarded.
After sorting the anchors, the RPN must learn the
object characteristics based on the anchors and the feature map.
This is done by passing the
feature map through a single 2D convolution that downscales the number of filters in the feature
map. The output of that convolution is used as input in two separate 1x1 convolutions which are
named the regression head and the classification head. The reason for using 1x1 convolutions is to
maintain the same receptive field and reduce the number of filters. In the classification head, the
number of filters is reduced to one filter which provides a confidence score that determines if the
27
==PAGE:34==
Jens Christian Bang Gribsvad
MSc Thesis
June 14, 2023
anchor contains an object or background. In the regression head, the number of filters is reduced
to four filters which provide the coordinate offsets between the positively labelled anchors and the
ground truth boxes. During training, the RPN utilizes the positive and negative labelled anchor
boxes to update the regression and classification head. The RPN learns through two different loss
functions: binary cross-entropy (BCE) loss for the classification head and smooth L1 loss for the
regression head. Both loss functions will be described in further detail in section 6.4. The final step
of training the RPN is to adjust the positively labelled anchors by adding the calculated offsets to
the anchor box coordinates. The anchors are then named proposals and used as input for stage 2.
During inference, the RPN also utilises an algorithm called non-maximum suppression (NMS).
Since there do not exist any ground truth boxes in the inference step, anchors cannot be labelled
positive or negative. Therefore, all anchors are inputted into the proposal module which outputs
a confidence score and offset predictions for all the anchors. However, anchors may overlap each
other quite significantly which is not desirable in terms of computational speed. Therefore, NMS
is applied which is a technique that aims to find the best bounding box out of a set of overlapping
boxes. NMS works by choosing the anchor with the largest confidence score. Every anchor is then
compared to the chosen anchor and if the IoU between the chosen anchor and the other anchor are
larger than a specified threshold the other anchor is removed. The number of anchors is hereby
reduced and only the anchors with the largest confidence of containing an object are kept.
6.2.2
Stage 2
The objective of stage 2 is to predict the class of each proposal. Therefore, a classification network
is created. However, since proposals are of different sizes they must be resized to a fixed size so the
input is consistent with the two networks. Region of interest (RoI) pooling is used to do the resizing
since it is a differentiable operation and thus enables backpropagation. RoI pooling works by taking
each proposal and subdividing them into a specified size. The values in the subdivided images are
computed by max pooling. Figure 6.4 shows an example of RoI pooling where a feature map with
a red proposal box is subdivided into a 2x2 grid where the values of that grid are determined by
max pooling.

6.2.3
Implementation details
The main code structure of the model has been based on the implementation of Faster R-CNN
in [38] but has been modified to fit this project. In [35] a pre-trained VGG16 network is used as
backbone CNN. In this project, a pre-trained ResNet50 model has been chosen as backbone CNN
because it takes up less memory than a VGG16 model and showcases similar performance on the
ImageNet data set on which the two models are trained. The architecture of ResNet consists of
residual blocks that are connected with skip connections. The skip connections work by passing
the output of a layer as the input to the two subsequent layers. Features learned in the first layers
are passed down through the network which can prevent the network from learning too abstract
features. The skip connections are a good feature for the backbone CNN to possess in this project
since the CFUs have simple characteristics.
Moreover, it must also be decided where in the ResNet50 model to extract the feature map. For
each ResNet50 block, the input image is halved along the spatial dimension. Therefore the number
of ResNet50 blocks is controlled by the spatial dimension of the input image and desired spatial
dimension of the feature map. If the spatial dimensions of the feature map are large, many an-
chors will be generated and processed which will increase the computational time. If the spatial
dimensions of the feature map are small, the computational time will be small but the accuracy
might decrease. In [35], the spatial dimension of the input image and feature map is (1000,600)
and (60,40), respectively, which means the input image is downscaled approximately by a factor of
16. However, in this project, the first five blocks of ResNet50 are used which downscales the input
image by a factor of 32. It has been chosen to use five blocks as the best performance was found
with this number of blocks. Moreover, it is difficult to directly compare the downscaling between a

When training the RPN, the anchor boxes must be given a positive or negative label based on user-
specified IoU thresholds. In [35] the positive IoU threshold is set to 0.7 and the negative threshold is
set to 0.3. The exact same thresholds are used in this project. A larger positive IoU threshold will
result in fewer anchor boxes being used to train the RPN. This can possibly decrease the number of
proposed boxes during inference since fewer anchor boxes will be similar to the positively labelled
anchor boxes used in training. Contrary, a smaller positive IoU threshold would reverse the scenario
resulting in a model that proposes more boxes during inference but these boxes might not overlap
a lot with the ground truth boxes.
During inference, an IoU threshold for NMS must also be specified. In [35], an IoU threshold of
0.7 is chosen for NMS which reduces the number of proposals substantially without harming the
detection accuracy. However, in this project, a much lower IoU threshold of 0.01 is used because
it is desired to obtain only a single bounding box for each ground truth box. In this way, the box
with the highest confidence score is always chosen and boxes that overlap this box are discarded.
The disadvantage of setting the IoU threshold this low is missing out on boxes with slightly lower
confidence scores that might fit the ground truth boxes better. However, with many overlapping
predictions, it can also be difficult to determine which predictions fit the ground truth boxes the
best. Thus it is easier to evaluate the performance if the predictions do not overlap each other.
It must also be noted that in [35], the second stage of the model also consists of a regression network
that produces offsets from the RPN proposals. This network is not part of the [38] implementation
and has not been added to the model in this project. Other experiments have been prioritized higher
than implementing the additional regression network since the model can run without it. However,
there is a possibility that it can hurt the performance since boxes are not adjusted a second time
in stage 2.
6.3
U-Net
Another supervised model named U-Net has been chosen as an alternative approach to the Faster
R-CNN model. One of the strengths of a U-Net model is that it requires only a few training images
to obtain good results and that it works well on large images because of the tiling strategy developed
in [39]. Moreover, U-Net is a widely popular segmentation method within the field of biology which
fits this project perfectly. It is a well-documented method which makes it easier to implement. A
U-Net model also consists of skip connections which stabilizes the convergence and also recovers
spatial information that might be lost during down-sampling. Based on those reasons, U-Net was
chosen as the alternative approach to the Faster R-CNN model.
A U-Net model is a fully convolutional neural network that takes an image as input and outputs a
segmentation mask. In this project, the output is a semantic segmentation mask which is a mask
where each pixel in the image is given a class label. A semantic segmentation will then contain
segmentations of objects with class-specific pixel values. Semantic segmentation can in this project
be used to determine sample heterogeneity by counting the number of different class labels in each
output mask. Moreover, bounding boxes are computed for every object in the segmentation mask in
a post-processing step where noise also is removed. This allows for a direct comparison between the
Faster R-CNN and U-Net model. The U-Net model will be trained on the same images of microbes
31

MSc Thesis
June 14, 2023
presented in section 3.1 to enable a direct comparison to Faster R-CNN.
The U-Net architecture consists of two main parts: an encoder and a decoder. The encoder takes
the input image and sends it through four encoder blocks. Each encoder block consists of two 3x3
convolutions followed by a rectified linear unit (ReLU) activation function. A copy of the output
of each encoder block is saved and used in skip connections which connect encoder and decoder
blocks in the same depth in the architecture. Between each encoder block is a 2x2 max pooling
layer which reduces the spatial dimensions of the image in half. Moreover, the number of channels is
doubled for each encoder block. After the image has been through the encoder part and converted
to a feature-rich map it is sent through a block called the bridge which connects the encoder and
decoder. The bridge consists of two 3x3 convolutions followed by a ReLU activation function. The
output map from the bridge is then up-sampled with a 2x2 transpose convolution where it reaches
the decoder.
Note that 2x2 transpose convolutions are the opposite of 2x2 max pooling which
means the spatial dimensions are doubled after each 2x2 transpose convolution. In the four decoder
blocks, the feature maps from the skip connections are concatenated with the output from the
2x2 transpose convolutions located between each decoder block. Each block consists of two 3x3
convolutions followed by a ReLU activation. The number of channels is reduced in half in each
decoder block. After the final decoder block, the feature map passes through a 1x1 convolution to
convert the number of channels to the desired number of output classes. Figure 6.6 illustrates the
architecture of the U-Net model.

6.4
Loss functions
To provide the best possible results the models must be trained on loss functions that accurately
penalize and reward the model for bad and good predictions. In the Faster R-CNN model, there are
3 different losses: a classification and regression loss in the RPN and a classification loss in stage
2. In the U-Net model, there is one loss function that evaluates the segmentation mask with the
ground truth mask.
6.4.1
Faster R-CNN loss functions
Starting with the classification loss in the RPN, the objective is to find a loss function that can
measure the difference between the predicted confidence scores and the ground truth bounding
boxes. Since it is one of the most commonly used loss functions for binary classification, BCE loss
will be used. The BCE loss is defined as follows
ln = −[yn · log (xn) + (1 −yn) · log (1 −xn)]
(6.5)
where xn denotes the predictions and yn denotes the targets. BCE loss penalizes predictions with
the negative log function which is a function that increases exponentially for values approaching
the value of zero. That means that predictions far off the target value are penalized more than
predictions close to the target value. Thus, the model is more inclined to improve the predictions
33

Jens Christian Bang Gribsvad
MSc Thesis
June 14, 2023
which are far off the target value. An example of this can be seen in the scenario where the target
value is y = 1 and the prediction is x = 0.1. The cross entropy will in this case become L = 2.303.
If the prediction were better with a value of x = 0.9 the cross entropy will be L = 0.105 which
is a significantly lower value than 2.303. One of the disadvantages of BCE loss is the sensitivity
towards outliers and imbalanced data. Fortunately, the amount of positive and negative anchors
are sampled equally so there is no imbalance in the data which justifies the choice of the BCE loss.
It must also be mentioned that a sigmoid function is used to convert the BCE loss to a [0,1] range.

6.6
Data augmentation
To improve the Faster R-CNN model, a number of different data augmentations are applied to
the training set. The idea behind using data augmentation is to present more variation and di-
versity to the model. This can prevent the model from overfitting and improve performance. The
37
==PAGE:44==
Jens Christian Bang Gribsvad
MSc Thesis
June 14, 2023
augmentation techniques used are described below:
• Horizontal and vertical flips are geometric data augmentations that flip the images. Flip-
ping the images increases the number of different CFU locations in the images.
• Random brightness contrast is a pixel-level type of augmentation which changes both
the brightness and contrast in the image. This augmentation is useful since the brightness
and contrast can vary among different images. Therefore, applying this augmentation should
increase the robustness of the model towards the image quality.
• Rotation is a geometric augmentation which rotates the image around the image centre by a
degree between [-90,90]. Applying rotation will introduce empty spaces in the corners. These
spaces are interpolated by a border replication. In rare cases, some artefacts may be introduced
if a CFU is located very close to the image border. The rotation augmentation increases the
variation in the training set since the number of different CFU locations is increased.
• Random crop is a geometric augmentation which crops a region around the CFUs and
rescales the crop to the original image size. A linear interpolation is used to do the rescaling.
This type of augmentation is useful to increase the variation of CFU sizes since the crops can
make CFUs appear larger. This mimics the growth rate variation where some CFU starts
to grow sooner than other CFUs and therefore appears larger at hour 12 where the image is
captured.
It must be mentioned that the probability of applying one of the techniques is 50%. Thus, during
training, an image first has a 50% chance of being horizontally flipped, then it has a 50% chance of
being vertically flipped, then it has a 50% chance of being adjusted with random brightness contrast
and so on.

As illustrated in Figure 6.9, the first component in the pipeline is the pre-processing of the sample

instead of a single class label for each CFU. Using the probabilities for the chosen microbes, a
multiclass support vector machine (SVM) classifier is trained. A multiclass SVM is a popular and

separates each microbial class the most. Figure 6.10 shows a 2D t-SNE representation of the 5D
probability space.

hyperplane that can separate the microbial classes.
When the SVM receives the probabilities of a new microbe the SVM will output a new set of
probabilities of the microbe belonging to one of the five microbes. If the probabilities are equally
low the microbe is likely too different from the chosen microbes and must be considered a new type
of microbe. Therefore, the following logic will be used:
1. If the largest probability is above 0.475 the CFU will be assigned the class label belonging to
the largest probability.
2. If the largest probability is below 0.475 the second largest probability is computed. If the
largest probability is above 0.3 and the second largest probability is above 0.2 the CFU will
be assigned a split label of the two classes with the two largest probabilities.
3. For all other cases that do not fulfil 1. and 2. the CFU probability distribution is discarded.
The threshold values in the above logic have been found through trial and error where the class
probabilities were compared with ground truth labels. For ground truth images where the predic-
tions are correct the largest probability is usually around 0.6. For ground truth images where the
predictions are wrong the largest probabilities are usually around 0.3.
The sample heterogeneity is estimated based on the distribution of the class and split class labels.
If a class or split class label is within 30% of the label with the largest occurrence then the number
for the sample heterogeneity is increased by one. Thus a distribution with just one peak will result
in a sample heterogeneity of one whereas a distribution with three equally sized peaks will result
in a sample heterogeneity of three. The threshold of 30% is found by trial and error such that the
sample heterogeneity number equals one for the five chosen microbes.
6.8
Training parameters
The Faster R-CNN and U-Net model are implemented in PyTorch which is a Python library for
deep learning. The initialization of the model weights follows the default PyTorch initialization
which is a Kaiming uniform distribution. An analysis regarding different initialization distributions
has not been conducted.
The optimizer used in this project is the Adaptive Moment Estimation optimizer (Adam) since it
was the one used in the Faster R-CNN implementation [38]. Through trial and error, the learning
rate is set to 0.0001. However, a more optimal approach to finding a good learning rate would be to
carry out a hyperparameter search. The PyTorch default weight decay value of 1e−8 is also used.
An analysis of using different optimizers has not been conducted.
The network has been trained on DTUs GPU cluster where either Nvidia Tesla A100 PCIE 80GB
or Nvidia Tesla V100 32 GB have been used. To fit the images into memory, the batch size is set
to 64 and 8 for the 200x200 patch and 660x660 patch data sets, respectively.

DISCUSSION
Overview
This section will discuss the obtained results from the previous section together with the
major decisions that have been made in this project. First, the decisions that have been
made regarding the data are discussed along with a discussion of how temporal data could
potentially help the model. Then the results obtained for the Faster R-CNN are discussed
together along with a discussion about other approaches that may improve the Faster R-CNN
performance. This section is concluded with a discussion of the results obtained by the U-Net
model and the sample heterogeneity results.
• 8.1 Data
• 8.2 Results
8.1
Data
An important decision in creating a feasible project was to decide how many and what types of
microbes should be selected for training the models. Five different types of microbes were selected.
However, a larger number of different types of microbes will likely increase the robustness of the
models towards unseen microbes as the models are exposed to a larger amount of microbial variation.
One can argue that this will also increase the accuracy of the heterogeneity estimation since the
probability space of the predicted CFUs is larger which means each CFU prediction will have a more
unique location in this space. The reason for choosing only five different types of microbes is to
demonstrate the concept that microbes can be classified and used for accurate sample heterogeneity
estimation before scaling up. The choice regarding which five types of microbes to use was based on
the available samples IntuBio could provide. The objective in the selection of microbes was to use
five types of microbes with different morphological characteristics. However, as mentioned in section
5.1 the variation of CFU appearance within each microbial type is very large due to different start
times for microbial reproduction. Ultimately, the five types of microbes have a significant overlap
in the CFU morphology for the selected time point at hour 12 of incubation as illustrated in Figure
5.3. This challenges the classification of the microbes which was shown in Figure 7.2 and 7.10. The
model performance would likely be improved if other types microbes with bigger differences were
selected.
When selecting the number of samples for each type of microbe it was difficult to know if the
selected number of samples would be sufficient to train a deep-learning model. Of course, more
data results in more variation presented to the models which can improve model performance.
After more than a week of annotating and labelling CFUs, it was decided that the amount of data
was sufficient. However, since the data have been filtered based on the criteria described in section
5.2 the resulting number of patches is likely a bit too small. 324 patches of 600x600 pixels and 1271
patches of 200x200 pixels are probably too few patches, especially when the objective is to learn
59

the morphology of five different types of microbes. Moreover, only 2.9% and 5.6% of all patches in
the 660x660 and 200x200 patches, respectively, are of CFU type 4 which is probably not enough
patches to learn the morphology of CFU type 4. Data augmentation can of course be beneficial
when the data size is too small but as seen in section 7.2.1, it did not manage to improve the Faster
R-CNN model performance. Another strategy could be to include merged CFUs and CFU located
at the border of the patches which would increase the data size significantly. However, the class
distribution would be heavily skewed towards the class containing border CFUs which can challenge
the classification since the model will be motivated to only learn the majority class.

8.2
Results
8.2.1
Faster R-CNN
The results obtained by the Faster R-CNN model were a bit unexpected. The low mAP and IoU
scores in Table 4 and 5 show that the model struggled a lot with locating CFUs in the images.
The backbone CNN is the component in the Faster R-CNN model which extracts features from the
input image from which the localization of CFUs is made. Therefore, it might be the backbone
CNN that is responsible for the poor model performance.
The ResNet50 model is used as the
backbone CNN and is pre-trained on the ImageNet data set which consists of complex objects like
animals, vehicles, tools, etc. Since the ResNet50 model is not trained to classify different microbes
the features extracted by the ResNet50 model might not be useful to locate and classify CFUs.
As [46] state, if the backbone CNN has not been trained on the objects in the dataset the Faster
R-CNN may not achieve good results. Therefore, it can be argued that a different backbone CNN,
which is trained to classify microbes, is better suited to extract useful features that can be used to
identify and classify CFUs.
An alternative approach presented by [47] is to fine-tune the last or all of the layers of a ResNet50
model as backbone CNN. By doing so, [47] obtained an mAP score of around 0.3 for lesion detection
in tomography images. Thus, [47] shows that the Faster R-CNN can work on objects which are
not included in the ImageNet object set. However, it can be discussed if the same mAP scores
can be achieved in this project if all of the last layers of the ResNet50 model were finetuned. The
lesion data set is different from the data used in this project so an mAP score of around 0.3 is not
guaranteed. However, it will likely improve the performance of the Faster R-CNN model since the
features in the ResNet50 model are trained towards detecting CFUs.
Another approach presented in [48] is to create another version of the IoU metric to optimize the
regression loss of the Faster R-CNN model. The new version of the IoU is defined as:
IIoU = IoU −C −(A ∪B)

patches since the CFUs are smaller in those patches than they are in the 200 × 200 patches.
The classification ability of the Faster R-CNN model did work somewhat as expected. The clas-
sification accuracy of approximately 60% across the different microbes present in Figure 7.2(a) is
similar to the 59% obtained in [19] where seven different cell types were predicted. That is a decent
classification accuracy keeping in mind how the t-SNE plot in Figure 7.9(a) showed that features for
the different microbes overlap quite significantly. Another interesting result regarding classification
accuracy is the average IoU of the predictions across the microbial types. In Table 5 it was shown
that the predictions on type 1 microbes had the largest IoU values of 0.46 and 0.5 despite having the
lowest classification accuracy of 56% and 76% for all predictions and best prediction, respectively.
This indicates that type 1 microbes are difficult to predict despite having localised the microbes
well. The main reason for this could be that the number of type 1 samples is quite low and thus the
type 1 microbe is underrepresented. The model cannot learn the morphology of type 1 microbes on
such a small number of samples so when a type 1 microbe is presented to the model it will predict
the microbe as a different type. Furthermore, type 1 and type 2 microbes can look similar which
is shown in Figure 8.1. That can explain why the Faster R-CNN predicts 23% and 24% of type 1
microbes as type 2 microbes for all predictions and best prediction, respectively.
The learning curves of the Faster R-CNN model resemble quite well the poor model performance.
Figure 7.3 shows that the validation curve barely decreases and fluctuates a lot. This indicates
there is a difference between the training and validation set. The low number of 127 samples in the
validation set is probably causing the fluctuations since they cannot generalize the 1017 samples in
the training set. Thus the model is trained in the direction of the training set which is not similar
to the direction of the validation set. Increasing the number of samples in both sets will probably
make the validation set generalize the training set better which ultimately will make the validation
curve decrease together with the training curve.
The data augmentation results were quite surprising since they were not better than the initial
results. Despite using a number of different types of augmentations it did not improve the perfor-
mance metrics. Multiple reasons can explain this behaviour. First off, the types of augmentation
might not resemble the variation in the data. If that is the case the augmentation will not improve
the performance and maybe even make it worse. However, the variation among the microbes in the
data is mostly based on different pixel intensities, blurriness, location, orientation and size. It is
therefore hard to argue against the selected augmentations since they address most of the variation
in the data. Secondly, the number of epochs might have been too low. Since data augmentations
introduce more variance to the training set it could have been beneficial to train the model for
more epochs. Perhaps combining that with a smaller learning rate could potentially improve the
augmentation results.
Generally, it is a good practice to do a hyperparameter search across the number of epochs, learning
rate values and different optimizers. It is difficult to know if other hyperparameters can improve the

model performance significantly but nonetheless, it can narrow down the list of potential elements
that are responsible for performance. However, it was prioritized to do a synthetic, pseudo-synthetic

Based on the synthetic experiment it was shown that the model can locate and classify simple
objects like triangles and circles. The good performance is probably based on that the objects in
the training, test and validation set all come from the same statistical distribution. Thus, the model
is asked to predict almost exactly the same objects as it has been trained on. That is probably the
biggest difference from the IntuBio data where there is a large difference in the morphology between
each individual CFU and therefore also a lot of variation between the training, test and validation
sets.
The pseudo-synthetic experiment showed that the main challenge for the model is the CFUs and not
the background. With a background of Gaussian noise, the model could not detect and classify the
CFUs. One could argue that the background should just have been coloured white to better diagnose
the effect of the background. However, given the poor mAP score with the noisy background, it
probably would not have improved the performance significantly.
8.2.2
U-Net
The U-Net model performed quite as expected. U-Net is a biomedical segmentation model, so it
was expected that the U-Net would be able to detect CFUs quite well since CFUs have a similar
appearance to the cells presented in [39]. The accurate detection is what is responsible for the large
mAP scores in Table 10 since an mAP score above zero requires an IoU that is larger than 0.5. As
mentioned in section 6.3, the performance can probably be improved if the kernel size used in the
morphological closing in the post-processing of the segmentation mask is reduced. Moreover, it can
also be argued that the training of the U-Net model was stopped too soon. Both the validation and
training curve was decreasing at epoch 50 where the training stopped. The classification abilities
of the U-Net model proved to be similar to the Faster R-CNN model based on the classification
accuracy matrices in Figure 7.2(b) and 7.10. That is a bit odd since the U-Net model predictions
have a larger IoU with the ground truth boxes than the Faster R-CNN prediction has. However,
this supports the argument of the microbial types overlapping each other since better predictions
with larger IoU do not result in larger classification accuracy.
8.2.3
Sample heterogeneity
The proposed method for estimating sample heterogeneity performed decently. The heterogeneity
estimation was correct in out of five samples for each of the model probabilities. On the other hand,
the estimation deviated from the true value with a value of one for the other samples. The proposed
method has multiple shortcomings which might explain the decent performance. When filtering the
patches, a threshold is applied to binarize the patch such that CFUs located at the boundary can be
removed. However, CFUs with less dark pixel intensities might not be captured by this threshold
which results in including CFUs located at the boundary. This will ultimately confuse the models
since they are trained to identify and classify entire CFUs. Moreover, some merged CFUs have
64
==PAGE:71==
Jens Christian Bang Gribsvad
MSc Thesis
June 14, 2023
a pixel percentage below 15%. Those CFUs can potentially also confuse the model since merged
CFUs look very different from individual CFUs.
Another shortcoming of the proposed method is the filtering of patches. For high-concentration
samples, the resulting number of patches might be very low since most patches are discarded because
they contain border CFUs or merged CFUs. This is problematic since the resulting patches do not
represent all of the variations in the entire sample. For example, there might be two different types
of microbes in a sample but because the patches are filtered only three patches are not discarded and
they all contain just one of the two microbial types. The heterogeneity estimation in this scenario
will likely be inaccurate since one of the two microbial types is never included in the estimation. The
scenario might not be very common. Nevertheless, it is still a weakness of the proposed method.
Probably the most difficult aspect of heterogeneity estimation is deciding when a new microbe is
sufficiently different from the other microbes that it must be considered an independent type of
microbe. The proposed method relies on logic based on a few threshold values. The thresholds can
be thought of as setting restrictions in the 5D probability space. If a new microbe is too far away
from any of the point clusters in Figure 6.10 then it must be considered a new microbial type. Thus,
the thresholds are attempting to define what "too far away" is. These threshold values have been
manually found and are not optimized. Changing these values will likely influence the heterogeneity
estimation significantly. Therefore, the heterogeneity will likely be more accurate if an optimization
of these thresholds were performed.

FUTURE WORK
9.1
Data expansion
As discussed in section 8, it would be interesting to investigate how the Faster R-CNN and U-Net
model would perform given temporal data. The hypothesis is that it potentially could improve the
performance of both models since key features like growth rate and more distinct CFU morphology
which appears in the later hours of incubation would be included. For this to work properly, the
amount of data must be increased. Including temporal data introduces even more variation to the
data set. Therefore, the data set should be a lot larger than it currently is such that the models can
learn as much of the variation as possible. Even without the inclusion of temporal data, it would
be a good idea to expand the data set. Again, it is difficult to know exactly how much additional
data is needed and if the performance actually would improve significantly. However, it will be an
interesting experiment to conduct.
Furthermore, it could also be interesting to select some other types of microbes to see if that
impacts the model performance. There definitely exist microbes that are morphologically much
more different than the ones selected for this project.
Moreover, different experiments with a
varying number of different microbes would also be interesting to perform. Could that improve
the performance of both models? and how does the number of different microbes used to train the
models correlate to the precision of the sample heterogeneity estimation?
9.2
Other modelling approaches
Inspired by the setup in [19], both the U-Net and Faster R-CNN model could be expanded to also
use an additional classification network after each model. In [19] a Faster R-CNN model predicts cell
or not cell objects which are then used as input to a classification network that more accurately can
predict the class of the objects. Exactly the same could be done for the Faster R-CNN and U-Net
model in this project, such that their objective is to predict CFU or not CFU. Those predictions
would then be used as input to another classification network which hopefully can classify them
accurately. A self-constructed CNN or any untrained ResNet model could be suitable classification
models.
Another approach which can incorporate the temporal dimension is a recurrent neural network
(RNN) [49]. It is a type of network where the connections between the nodes can create a cycle.
Thus, the output of nodes can affect subsequent input which is why it is named a recurrent neural
network. An RNN is learning temporal patterns and can for example be used to predict the next
word in a sentence. On the other hand, an CNN is learning spatial patterns and can locate CFUs in
images. Creating a framework that combines a CNN followed by an RNN could prove to be quite
powerful since both spatial and temporal patterns are used to locate and classify CFUs. Moreover,
the sample heterogeneity can be estimated similarly to how it has been done in this project.

CONCLUSION
This thesis proposes a method of determining sample heterogeneity based on a Faster R-CNN and
U-Net model. Both models were trained to locate and classify five different types of microbes. The
performance of the Faster R-CNN model was not as expected with a low mAP score of 0.0372.
The inference examples showed that the size and placement of the prediction boxes did not match
the ground truth boxes. Moreover, using different types of data augmentation did not improve
the model performance. However, the Faster R-CNN model performed significantly better with an
mAP score of 0.92 on synthetic data consisting of simple objects (circles and triangles). Through
a pseudo-synthetic experiment, it was concluded that it is the 2D morphology of the CFUs that
are the main challenge to the model and not the background. A pixel intensity experiment could
also reveal that the pixel intensities of the CFUs did not challenge the model since the obtained
mAP scores were around 0.038. An investigation of the feature map from the backbone CNN in
the Faster R-CNN model showed a significant overlap between the features from the different types
of microbes. This overlap can explain why the classification accuracy is around 60% across the five
different microbes for the Faster R-CNN model.
The U-Net model showed a better performance than the Faster R-CNN model with an mAP score
of around 0.574. Despite a larger mAP score, the classification accuracy was not significantly better
for the U-Net model. Including temporal data can perhaps improve the classification accuracy since
the growth rate and change in CFU morphology in the later stages of incubation can be used for
a stronger differentiation between the different types of microbes. The inference examples showed
that the U-Net model could accurately locate the CFUs in the images.
Finally, the two models were used to estimate the sample heterogeneity for five different sample
sets of which the sample sets of Yarrowia l., Escherichia c. and Saccharomyces c. are unknown
to the models. Each model managed to estimate the correct sample heterogeneity in one of those
five sample sets. Some of the images in the Yarrowia l., Escherichia coli and Saccharomyces c
sample sets were out of focus which could have decreased the model classification since the CFU
characteristics might not be captured well when the CFUs are slightly out of focus.
