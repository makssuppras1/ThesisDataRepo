Abstract
Optical coherence tomography is an non-invasive imaging modality providing high-
resolution cross-sectional images of biological tissues.
The project focuses on the design
and characterization of a Swept-Source Spatial-Offset OCT system promising high imaging
rate and high imaging depth. The characterization showed a sensitivity of 60.4dB, with a
roll-off of 12.7db/mm. The axial resolution of the system is measured to be 9.2µm, ap-
proaching the theoretical limit of 6µm. The system signal-to-noise ratio has been improved
by 16dB through polarization management. This project is setting a baseline for the sys-
tem’s performance and is identifying possible improvements, such as: increasing the power in
the reference arm in order to achieve a shot-noise limited system, developing a more robust
and time-efficient processing scheme, as well as correcting for dispersion mismatch with a
dispersion compensator.

Motivation
Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) is an imaging technique based on light inter-
ference. When light interacts with the sample, back-scattered light is collected and depth
information is extracted from the phase. Such a system can produce images of a scattering
media up to a few millimeters deep, with a resolution of a few micrometers [1].
This technology has found important applications in ophthalmology [2]. The retina, for
example, is highly sensitive and difficult to access. In this case, OCT with a light source
in the near infrared (NIR) region can be used to examine the retina without damaging or
discomforting the patient. OCT is therefore considered as a non-invasive technique. The
images obtained allow structural information about the retina to be recovered, which can be
used to diagnose diseases such as diabetes-related retinopathy or ocular cancer [3][4].
Applications of OCT are now expanding into other medical fields, such as dermatology
and proctology [5][6]. In these areas, OCT can be used as a tool to identify cancerous tissues
and assist medical personnel in locating sick regions [7]. Another emerging applications is in
histopathology, which is the microscopic examination of diseased tissues [8]. A combination
between OCT data and machine learning could eventually be better at detecting diseases in
their early stages compared to conventional histopathological analysis.
It is also important to note that the use of OCT extends beyond the medical field. In
fact OCT is also used in the industry for non-destructive testing, including the analysis of
composite materials and in-line inspections [9].
Optical Coherence Tomography is attractive to many fields due to its ability to generate
high-resolution, depth-resolved images without damaging tissues or materials. In order to
make this technology even more appealing, key parameters such as resolution, imaging depth
and imaging rate must to be improved. In the medical field, such improvements would en-
able imaging of a larger portion of the human body, visualization of finer structural details,
and reduce sensitivity to motion artifacts. For these reasons, numerous OCT designs have
emerged over the past two decades, each addressing specific limitations.
The first design is Time-Domain OCT (TD-OCT) [1], followed by Spectral-Domain OCT
(SD-OCT), and Swept-Source OCT (SS-OCT) [10][11]. The present project is based on the
Swept-Source design as it enables higher imaging rates than the other two approaches, reach-
ing up to megahertz scanning range [12]. Like SD-OCT, SS-OCT provides greater stability
compared to the original TD-OCT, while offering a deeper imaging range than SD-OCT.
In the current system, SS-OCT operates at a central wavelength of 1040nm, which pro-
vides several advantages. Longer wavelengths scatter less than shorter wavelengths, enabling
deeper penetration into biological tissues. Additionally, a 1040nm central wavelength lies
within the biological window, see Figure25 in the Appendix. The biological window imposes

Basic Concepts of Optical Coherence Tomography
OCT has become a key imaging modality in biomedical optics.
It performs high-
resolution cross-sectional and 3-dimensional imaging. The cross-sectional and 3-dimensional
images are generated by detecting the echo-time delay of the backscattered light coming
from the imaged tissue. A single echo-time delay measurement is known as an A-scan.
An A-scan is obtained when a broadband laser beam interacts with the sample at a fixed
lateral position. Since the sample reflectivity varies with depth, the measurement encodes
depth-dependent information. Performing a transversal scan and collecting sequential axial
measurements results in the reconstruction of a cross-sectional image, also known as B-scan.
Finally, performing multiple B-scans along the orthogonal scanning direction results in the
reconstruction of a C-scan image, see Figure 1.
Figure 1: Illustration of three OCT imaging modalities: A-scan on the left, B-scan in the
middle, C-scan on the left [14].
Having established how OCT echo-time delay measurements can be organized into A-
scans, B-scans and 3-dimensional images, it is important to note that the way data is acquired
depends strongly on the system’s architecture. As introduced in the previous section, three
main OCT designs exist, relying on distinct methods to collect interferometric signals. These
architectures come with their own advantages and limitations, which in terms influence imag-
ing depth, sensitivity and acquisition speed.

2.1
Three OCT Designs
2.1
Three OCT Designs
All three designs rely on the powerful technique of interferometry, which enables the
measurement of both the magnitude and the echo-time delay of the backscattered light.
More specifically, OCT is based on low-coherence interferometry, which relies on light with
short coherence length. This means that the light loses its fixed phase relationship over
a short distance. This property is what gives OCT its high axial-resolution and since the
coherence length is inversely proportional to the source bandwidth, broadband light sources
are used in OCT to achieve fine depth resolution.
OCT can be understood as an advanced interferometer, similar to the Michelson con-
figuration shown in Figure 2. Like all interferometers, an OCT consists of a light source, a
reference arm, a sample arm and a detector. The emitted light is divided into a reference
and a sample arm: in the reference arm the light travels a known optical path, while in the
sample arm the light interacts with the tissue or material under investigation. The reflected
light from the reference arm and the backscattered light from the sample arm are then re-
combined. When the optical path lengths difference between the two arms is within the
coherence length of the light source, interference occurs. The interference signal is finally
recorded by the detector.
Figure 2: Schematic of a simple interferometer, presenting a light source, a beam splitter, a
reference arm, a sample arm and a detector [14].

These two important observations form the basis of two types of OCT designs: the
TD-OCT and the Fourier-Domain OCT (FD-OCT). TD-OCT has a movable reference arm

narrow spectral line while sweeping over a broad spectrum. SD-OCT uses the first principle,
and SS-OCT uses the second principle.
The SS-OCT design offers several advantages over TD-OCT and SD-OCT. First, the
imaging rate is directly proportional to the sweeping rate of the laser source, which ranges
from the kilohertz (kHz) to the megahertz (MHz) regime. Such a high imaging rate reduces
motion artifacts, enables real-time imaging and allows the acquisition of a larger field of
view. In addition, SS-OCT achieves greater imaging depth compared to SD-OCT because of
its lower sensitivity roll-off. Sensitivity roll-off describes the system sensitivity as a function
of depth.

From Equation 4, the photocurrent can be divided into three contributions: the "DC
terms", the "Cross-correlation terms" and the "Auto-correlation terms". The DC terms
represent a constant background arising from the reflectivity of surfaces in the reference and
sample arm. The cross-correlation terms come from the interference between the reference
field and each backscattered field. Finally, the auto-correlation terms originate from the
interference between the different scatterers within the sample. Among these contributions,
two contain depth information, the cross-correlation terms and the auto-correlation terms.
The depth information is encoded in the phase, to retrieve this information the signal must
be Fourier transformed, producing an A-scan, see Figure 5.
Figure 5: Illustration of an idealized A-scan, representing the three contributions to the
signal: DC term, Auto-correlation terms an Cross-correlation terms [14].
After Fourier transforming the interferogram, the DC term, cross-correlation terms and
auto-correlation terms can be identified. The DC-term appears as a strong signal localized at
the origin (zero-delay) and can be minimized by performing background subtraction on the
interferogram. The cross-correlation terms generate depth-resolved peaks at ±2(zR −zSn),
corresponding to the positions of the scatterers relative to the reference arm optical path
length.
In contrast, the auto-correlation terms produce peaks at optical path difference
between pairs of scatterers, rather that at the actual depths of the individual interfaces,
their signal is usually closer to the zero-delay. Ideally, an A-scan would only retain the cross-
correlation terms, with the DC and auto-correlation terms removed, in order to provide an
depth-resolved signal without any artifacts.

System Design
The SS-SO-OCT built here is based on a Mach-Zehnder interferometer configuration,
see Figure 6. Compared to its Michelson counterpart, this type of design allows to decouple
the reference and the sample arm. As a result, the spatial offset feature can be implemented
exclusively in the sample arm without affecting the reference arm. In addition, the Mach-
Zehnder configuration offers a great control over the optical power in each arm.
Figure 6: Experimental setup of the SS-SO-OCT. HWP, half-wave plate; BS, beam splitter;
PBS, Polarizin beam splitter; QWP, quarter-wave plate; GM, galvo mirrors.
A Thorlabs swept-source laser (SL1050-P16) was used in this setup, operating at a cen-
tral wavelength of 1040nm with a 3-dB tuning range of 80nm. The laser is sweeping at a
rate of 15.9 kHz with an average output power of 7.5 mW. The swept-source is based on a
cat-Eye external cavity design, see Figure 26 in the Appendix. The laser selects a a spectral-
line using a grating, and sweeps over the full bandwidth by rotating the grating. The light
is finally coupled to free space through an optical fiber followed by a collimator, giving a 1/e
radius of approximately 1.25mm, see Figure 27 in the Appendix.
After collimation, the beam passes through a half-wave plate (HWP), which can rotate
the polarization state of the light. This becomes relevant later when the beam interacts
with a polarizing beam splitter (PBS) located in the sample arm. At this stage, the beam
is divided by a beam splitter. Initially, a 90:10 beamsplitter (BS) was used, transmitting
90% of the light and reflecting 10%. In the current configuration a 50:50 beam splitter is
implemented, splitting the light evenly between the sample and reference arm.
In the sample arm, the beam first passes through a PBS, which transmits p-polarized
light and reflects s-polarized light. The previously mentioned HWP has to be rotated so

that most of the light from the laser source is in the p-polarized state. Under this con-
figuration, the majority of the light transmitted through the 50:50 BS passes through the
PBS. The transmitted beam, now fully p-polarized, propagates through a quarter-wave plate
(QWP) which transforms linearly polarized light to elliptically polarized light. The QWP is
placed at a specific angle, that transforms linearly polarized light to circularly polarized light.
There are two main reasons for converting the polarization state to circularly polarized.
First, it has been demonstrated that circularly polarized light penetrates deeper into biologi-
cal tissues [16]. Second, on its return path from the sample, the light beam must be reflected
by the PBS, which only reflects s-polarized light into the fiber coupler. This implies that
the backscattered light needs to be circularly polarized in order to be fully converted into
s-polarized light, upon the second encounter with the QWP, see Figure 7.

After passing through the QWP, the beam is directed onto a galvo mirror system, which
performs lateral scanning in the x- and y-directions, thereby enabling the acquisition of B-
scans and volumetric images. The scanned beam is then focused onto the sample by the
objective. After interacting with the sample, part of the backscattered light is collected by
the objective and retraces its path through the sample arm before being reflected by the
PBS onto a collimator connected to a fiber coupler. The fiber collimator is mounted on a
translation stage, enabling the implementation of a spatial-offset in the sample arm.
In the reference arm, the beam is first directed toward a second 50:50 BS. The reflected
portion of the light is focused onto a mirror while passing through a dispersion compen-
sator, which corrects for the dispersion mismatched between the reference and sample arm
introduced by the objective. The lens, dispersion compensator and mirror are mounted on
a translation stage, which can change the length of the reference arm. After the beam is
reflected by the mirror and collimated by the lens, the light passes back through the BS.

3.1
Polarization Management
A thorough understanding of the polarization state of light within the system is es-
sential, as proper control of polarization directly impacts the system performance. In an
attempt to understand polarization in the system, a simple model of light polarization was
implemented. The model in question relies on Jones calculus, where the polarization of light
is represented by a Jones vector and each optical elements are represented by Jones matrices
acting on the polarization vector. With such a model, the following assumptions are made:
the light is fully polarized, and the light can be described as a transverse coherent wave. At
first glance, these assumptions seem reasonable given that a laser is being used as a light
source.
Polarization management is important for a number of reasons. The first relates to the
power budget of the system. In the sample arm, the optical power depends directly on the
polarization state of the light. Therefore the polarization-controlling components must be
placed in a way that maximize the sample arm power. On the other hand, in the reference
arm the polarization state does not influence the power budget.

While the optical setup defines the physical properties of the system, the quality of
the final image depends strongly on the image processing.
In reality, the raw interfero-
gram recorded by the detector shows a distorted picture of reality, caused by the sweeping
characteristic of the laser and the dispersive properties of the system. In order to obtain a
high-resolution, artifact-free image, the raw data must undergo some signal processing steps
before Fourier transformation.
The two key processing steps are k-linearization and dispersion compensation.
K-
linearization corrects for the fact that swept-source lasers do not scan uniformly in wavenum-
ber, leading to non-linear sampling of the interference fringes. Without correcting for this,
the Fourier transformed signal will decrease in amplitude, present blurred and distorted
depth profiles, as illustrated in Figure 10.

Dispersion compensation accounts for dispersion mismatch between the reference and
sample arm. Dispersion mismatch happens when different optical components are present in
the OCT arms, leading to a change in the group velocity. When not corrected for, this leads
to a broadening of the axial point spread function (PSF) and reduces the image resolution,
see Figure 28 in the Appendix. Dispersion introduces an additional phase, Φdisp, to the
OCT signal. The cross-correlation phase terms can be rewritten, incorporating the effect of
dispersion, see Equation 9.
Φn = 2k(zR −zSn) + Φdisp
(9)
And the dispersion phase can be expanded in a Taylor series around the central wavenum-
ber, k0.
Φdisp(k) = β0 + β1(k −k0) + 1
2β2(k −k0)2 + 1
6β3(k −k0)3...
(10)
The first term β0, is a constant phase offset, it doesn’t have any effect on the measured
intensity profile. For that reason the A-scan is unchanged whether or not there is a constant
phase offset. This term is therefore set to zero. The second term, β1(k −k0), is also set to
zero since it only represents a shift relative to the zero time delay. It has therefore no real
physical meaning.
4.1
Three k-linearization and Dispersion Compensation Algorithms
Fortunately, there are ways to correct for non-linear sampling and dispersion mismatch.
Three methods have been investigated throughout this project. All of them build on the
same principle: raw interferogram is resampled to correct for the non-linear sampling in
k-space. Afterwards, the dispersion mismatch is compensated by removing the dispersion
phase Φdisp, up to to an arbitrary order.
4.1.1
Conventional Width-Optimization Algorithm
The conventional algorithm builds on peak-amplitude or peak-width optimization. These
kind of algorithms require multiple sets of measurements in order to perform optimization
across all depths. The k-linearization and dispersion compensation increase in efficiency as
a function of the number of depth measurements.
In this project, peak-width optimization is used to correct for non-linear resampling
and dispersion mismatch. A total amount of seven peak measurements at different depths
were used. The algorithm first removes the background and DC signal. Next, the phase
of the interferogram is fitted by a polynomial phase function Φpoly, with its lowest term
being quadratic and its highest being chosen arbitrarily, see Equation 11. The signal is then
multiplied by the complex conjugate of the phase function. The second moment of the peak
is calculated and minimized by guessing a new polynomial phase function. After a number
of iterations, the peaks become transform-limited and should approach the theoretical axial

resolution of the system.
Φpoly = a2k2 + a3k3 + a4k4 + a5k5 + a6k6... + aNkN
(11)
The polynomial phase function compensates for peak broadening caused by dispersion
mismatch. However, the signal still requires correction for the non-linear sampling in k-space.
To achieve this, the mapping function between the sampling points and the wavenumber is
generated. This is done by calculating the linear phase term Φlin, which is found by com-
puting the first moment of each PSF.
The k-linearization mapping function can be calculated by computing the difference in
the total phase between each successive peaks, where the total phase is given by: Φlin+Φpoly.
The nonlinear behavior remaining from the subtraction is caused by the non-linear k-
sampling. From all the mapping functions generated, the best looking mapping functions
are chosen and averaged to generate the final k-linearization mapping function.
Finally, the k-linearization mapping function can be used to resample the signal, and
the dispersion compensation can be applied.
4.1.2
Attendu et al. Algorithm
While the previous scheme relies on lengthy and more complex measurement protocols,
the Attendu et al. algorithm was developed to provide a robust calibration method capable
of simultaneously k-linearize and correct for dispersion mismatch by only using two mirror
measurements [17].
The algorithm relies on the fact that measuring a mirror on each side of the zero-delay
produces interferogram whose phases contain a dispersion term of the same magnitude but
opposite sign, as shown in Equations 12 and 13.
Φ+ = Φlin(∆L+) + Φdisp
(12)
Φ−= Φlin(∆L−) −Φdisp
(13)
Where Φ+ is the phase from the signal on the positive side of zero-delay, while Φ−is the
phase from the negative side of the zero-delay. Adding those two phases to each other will
remove the dispersion term Φdisp and isolate the linear terms, Φlin(∆L+) and Φlin(∆L−).
Φ+ + Φ−= Φlin(∆L+) + Φlin(∆L−)
(14)
Dividing Equation 14 by two is effectively the same as finding the linear phase term
for a PSF positioned in between the two measured peaks. Any nonlinearity coming from
this expression can be attributed to improper sampling. A mapping vector can be generated
from this and the measurements can be resampled through interpolation.

System Sensitivity
In addition to the sensitivity roll-off and axial-resolution, the overall sensitivity of the
OCT system is a critical performance metric, as it defines the weakest detectable signal.
Sensitivity depends on multiple factors, such as the source power, system losses, multiple
kind of noises, such as the shot noise, the excess noise and the receiver noise [15]. In this

The section is divided into to subsection, the first one is showing the sensitivity of the
system still using a 90:10 BS and the second subsection is showing the last in date sensitivity

Discussion
The current SS-SO-OCT system, equipped with a HWP in the reference arm and a
50:50 BS, has demonstrated a sensitivity of 60.4dB with a sensitivity roll-off estimated at
12.7dB/mm, measured using the most effective processing scheme available. Throughout the
project the signal has been improved by at least 16dB, through the control of the polarization
inside the system. Some additional signal increase, which has not been properly measured,
could come from an improvement of the systems alignment and from the increased power in
the reference arm. Those two additional contributions to the signal could be the subject of
some further investigations.
Even though the sensitivity of the system has been greatly improved by correcting for
the polarization orthogonality between the two arms, it remains far below the 100dB sensi-
tivity typically required for imaging biological tissues. Consequently, further enhancements
are necessary, either by increasing the source power or by further optimizing the power dis-
tribution with the OCT system.
Increasing the power of the laser source offers two main advantages. First, the light
intensity at the sample plane will be increased, making the system better suited for imaging
highly scattering media, such as biological tissues. Second, the power in the reference arm
is also enhanced, allowing the sensitivity to further increase and potentially reach the shot-
noise-limited regime.
Modifying the power budget also offers several advantages. First, it avoids the need for
an expensive new laser source. Second, the system design presents some substantial power
loss in the reference arm due to the reference arm BS. Currently, the reference arm loses 75%
of the power due to a 50% energy loss every time the light beam travels through the BS.
Replacing the BS with a PBS would preserve most of the reference arm power. This way,
most of the light entering the reference arm could be collected by the detector. Additionally,
incorporating a PBS would provide greater control over the polarization in the reference
arm, as a PBS effectively functions as two orthogonal linear polarizers.
Regarding the polarization state of the system, several aspects remain to be investigated.
In section 5.2 discrepancies between the theoretical model and the experimental data were
observed, but these were not the only deviations identified during the polarization study.
The theoretical model was also used to predict the modulation amplitude as a function of
the first HWP for two configurations: with the HWP in the reference arm positioned at 45◦
and 0◦. When the HWP in the reference arm is set to 45◦, the overall behavior does once
again look similar to the theory, but some differences in the shape appear, see Figure 23.
Specifically, the experimental data resembles a cosine squared function where the theoretical
prediction follows a smoother cosine shape with gradual transitions at the minima.

Conclusion
The aim of this project was to design and characterize a newly built SS-SO-OCT sys-
tem. The characterization analysis showed a sensitivity of 60.4dB with a sensitivity roll-off
of 12.7dB/mm. The axial-resolution was measured to be 9.2µm which is close to the theo-
retical resolution of 6µm.
The design was improved in two ways: first, a HWP was added in the reference arm cor-
recting for misalignment between the sample and reference arm polarization states, resulting
in a 16dB signal increase. Second, the reference arm power was increased by implementing
a 50:50 BS instead of a 90:10 BS. This modification was intended to bring the system closer
to the shot-noise limit regime. Unfortunately, the system is still far from that regime.
The work carried out in this project brings the system a few steps closer to imaging bio-
logical tissues as well as competing with state-of-the-art OCT systems. The characterization
provides an instant picture of the system’s performances, and highlights the remaining work
required to achieve a fully operational SS-SO-OCT system. Changes in the design, such as
polarization management, have already enhanced performance, getting the system closer to
the required 100dB sensitivity needed for tissue imaging.
The first logical next steps are: reaching the shot-noise-limited regime. This is a critical
requirement, since FD-OCT systems are mainly superior to TD-OCT because of their higher
SNR when the system is shot-noise limited. Second, the dispersion mismatch between the
two arms could be partially corrected by adding a dispersion compensator in the sample
arm. A third step could be to implement a more robust processing scheme that can simul-
taneously linearize and dispersion compensate the data in a user friendly and time efficient
manner. Having a robust processing algorithm provides high resolution images, close the
theoretical resolution, and increases trust in the results. Finally, the polarization state of
the system could be further investigated as it can provide a greater understanding of the
system polarization state, and could theoretically lead to a small increase in SNR. Further
investigation of the polarization state would mainly be for curiosity purposes.
As a conclusion, this work provides a full description of the system performances and
sets the standard for future improvements.
