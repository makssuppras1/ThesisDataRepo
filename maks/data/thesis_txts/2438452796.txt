Abstract
In this project, a method for detecting man-made targets in heterogeneous environment, using
polarimetric synthetic aperture radar (POLSAR) data is developed. There are several reasons as
to why this is of interest. Detection of man-made targets is crucial for the military for tactical
reasons. The detection can however also be carried out at sea to nd shing vessels, monitor ship
trac and aid in immigration control.
Two datasets are used in this project. The rst is from the Foulum test site. The resolution in
this dataset is very poor, but the methods can be tested at low computational costs. The second
dataset is from Therma, it contains a few vehicles of military origin, the vehicles are placed near a
road. This dataset has excellent resolution, but computationally this lowers the eectiveness.
Firstly, an introduction to SAR and previous usage of SAR data is given. The introduction does
also hold a problem statement naming the CFAR-technique as the method of choice for detection
with POLSAR data. A section containing the current CFAR-usages and the overall reason why
POLSAR data is superior to other data-types is also presented.
Secondly, the theory used is described.
In this section, the basics of CFAR is introduced, as
well as the truncated Clutter-statistics-based Two-parameter CFAR detector, on which the trun-
cation idea is based. The complex normal distribution and the complex Wishart distribution and
derived equality of two complex Wishart matrices is also described herein. Equality of gamma-
parameters, azimuthal symmetry, diagonal and block diagonal cases are also described, although
only azimuthal symmetry has been used for testing once. Furthermore, Edge detection using the
Wishart-distribution is reviewed and nally the equivalent number of looks for dierent CFAR
congurations is worked out.
Thirdly the data is described and the initial tests for equality of matrices, using both edge detection
and change detection is carried out.
Finally, the analysis and results are shown. A basic CFAR detector was developed, with no modica-
tions, but it had major issues around edges and when targets were present, as targets contaminated
the training data. To account for this, the edges of the segments were found using the edge de-
tector. With the edges, the result became signicantly better, however, targets still contaminated
the training data. Therefore a truncated CFAR detector using information about the edges was
developed. It excludes major outliers and makes the training data homogeneous. This detector was
used on both the Foulum and the Therma dataset and was able to detect vehicles. It did, however,
suer near edges of segments, as the edges were only compared to each other. Lastly, a conclusion
sums up the project and suggestions for applications and further improvements are discussed.

Introduction
Back in the early 1940s, the military was in grave need of an all-weather aerial remote surveillance
device [Engineering and Wiki, 2015]. Radar was a logical choice given the ability to penetrate clouds
while being unaected by the wavelengths of visible light. High-resolution radar did, however,
require a massive antenna, which, by the time, was unable to get carried by aircraft. In the early
1950s, Carl Wiley suggested that each object would have its own doppler-shift as the antenna
has a dierent speed relative to the objects.
Frequency analysis of radar reection could thus
construct a detailed image, without the need of the previously required large antenna. The idea was,
independently, developed at the University of Illinois around one year later and the development
of the rst practical Synthetic Aperture Radar, SAR, began in the summer of 1953.
In 1974 The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration and engineers from Jet Propulsion
Laboratories started the exploration for alternative uses of the SAR technology. In 1978 the rst
civilian SAR application, Seasat, was launched with the purpose of studying the oceans. Since then
SARs have been applied to numerous dierent elds in the studying of the earth.
While being able to collect data on the face of the earth is one thing, data treatment is another.
Multiple ideas have come up, each relating to the respective topic of interest. Very famous methods
are classication, neural networks, and segmentation, all of which have functionality in broad
aspects. While broad functionality is good, narrow and specic problems using dierent approaches
are often needed.
Detection of ships is interesting as immigration control, ship trac monitoring and shing vessel
detection are problems of today. As with most man-made objects, ships are fairly easy to recognize
in a SAR image as the ship structure reects the electromagnetic waves transmitted by the SAR
instrument. The sea will also appear dark, due to the absorption of the waves herein. Furthermore,
the increase in resolution of SAR instruments makes it even easier to detect ships today.
On land, an object made by a man still tends to stick out. Compared to the ocean, however, the
heterogeneous background, that is the nature of land, complicates the automatic target recognition
(ATR). It is of great importance for the military, to be able to nd targets in heterogeneous
backgrounds on land, thus methods for optimizing the accuracy and eciency of the ATR are
needed.
One of the most obvious ways of optimizing the accuracy is by increasing the amount of information
that is available in the data. Polarimetric SAR data holds information about the polarimetric
properties of the surface as well as the intensity of the returned signal, whereas original SAR or
single-pol SAR are limited in information.
An optimization of the accuracy will however also
increase the data-processing-time as the data now contains both complex parts as well as several
extra measurements.
Throughout the following project, it is of interest to map objects in polarimetric SAR images.
Finding objects in a SAR image is fairly dicult, thus methods of optimizing the computational
burden as well as the precision are of great interest today.

State Of The Art
Constant false alarm rate (CFAR) is a well-known method for detecting objects. The concept is
to create a detector which adaptively determines a threshold based on statistical modeling of the
background. CFAR uses a moving reference window, which is divided into a test window, a guard
window and a background window. The pixel under test (PUT), in the moving reference window,
is compared with the local threshold.
Modeling of the background-clutter is the backbone of the CFAR technique. Log-normal [GOLD-
STEI.GB, 1973], Weibull, generalized Gamma [Li, Hong, Wu, and Fan, 2010] , K, alpha-stable
and G [Frery, Muller, Yanasse, and SantAnna, 1997] are the most commonly used models. Only
Log-normal and Weibull perform at a fast pace, whereas the rest suer computationally in terms
of parameter estimation. Cell averaging CFAR (CA-CFAR), and two-parameter CFAR are used
extensively, as parameter estimation is ecient. CA-CFAR estimates the mean value, while two-
parameter CFAR estimates mean and standard deviation. CA performs well in a homogeneous
state and two-parameter performs well in heterogeneous state [Ai, Yang, Song, Dong, Jia, and
Zhou, 2017].
While the above mentioned CFAR techniques are used extensively, numerous alternative CFAR
techniques are developed and used for dierent purposes. Smallest-of CFAR (SO-CFAR) [TRUNK,
1978] and greatest-of CFAR (GO-CFAR) [Hansen and Sawyers, 1980] use, respectively, the smallest
or largest mean value of the background windows as detection threshold. This implies an increased
false alarm rate (FAR) or a larger detection loss. Both do, however, have their appliances, for
instance, the GO-CFAR is used when it is vital to avoid false alarms at the edge of the clutter,
whereas the SO-CFAR is used when the targets are closely located. Order statistic (OS-CFAR)
is another way of doing the CA-CFAR. In the OS-CFAR outliers are, to an extent, eliminated,
and the result is superior to that of a CA-CFAR. Unfortunately, the OS-CFAR require a manual
operator for good results and the computational eciency is low. [ROHLING, 1983].
Applying the CFAR technique to nding objects in a SAR image has been done before [Ai, Yang,
Song, Dong, Jia, and Zhou, 2017; Gan and Wang, 2005; Pappas, Achim, and Bull, 2017; Huang and
Liu, 2016]. In [Ai, Yang, Song, Dong, Jia, and Zhou, 2017] a two-parameter CFAR ship-detector,
based on adaptively truncated clutter statistics, is proposed. The truncation in this detector refers
to a reduction of the dataset by eliminating values that exceed a threshold based on the trunca-
tion. This detector is needed due to the degradation in the probability of detection (PD) caused
by interfering ships, i.e closely gathered targets, side lobes, and ghosts in busy shipping lines. It is
argued, that the outliers lead to parameter overestimation, which again causes inaccurate statistical
modeling as the FAR drops while PD degrades.
The two-parameter-truncated-CFAR detector described above uses the log-normal statistical model,
as the Kullback-Leibler distance yields lower values for this model than for the alternatives meaning
that the other probability functions diverge from it. It is further noted, that the detector does not
use a guard window, as it is argued, that the window is ineective in multiple-target scenarios.
Furthermore, the truncation depth is set based on a priori knowledge or study of the area, meaning
that an expert is needed to use the method to avoid excessive clutter elimination. The method
does, however, yield promising results in the detection of ships in multiple target areas. The two-
parameter-truncated-CFAR detector works well in busy harbors, but is still a computationally hard

3.1.1.
CA-CFAR
CA-CFAR, or Cell-Averaging CFAR, detectors are useful in the maintaining of constant false alarm
rates in exponentially distributed noise and clutter [GANDHI and KASSAM, 1994]. Furthermore
the CA-CFAR is the baseline of the CFAR techniques. In CA-CFAR The average of N background
cells, Yi, is estimated, the background cells surrounds the PUT, Xi, and the guard pixels. The local
average of the background of the moving reference window is multiplied by a threshold, scalar of
size τ, and compared with Y . When testing in exponentially distributed clutter, a target is declared
present in the case that Y > τ ¯X where τ = N(α−1/N −1), where α is the desired signicance level
α ∈{0, 1}. This is derived in [GANDHI and KASSAM, 1994], and has no further use in the thesis,
and is therefore not discussed further.
This means that a moving threshold based on the local mean can be evaluated at low computational
costs. A big drawback for the CA-CFAR method, is that adjacent targets can 'cover' for each other
in the sense, that the average due to a stronger target can increase the local average enough,
that the threshold will surpass that of the signal of the weaker target. The described CA-CFAR
technique considers a single dimension of data i.e leading and trailing cells are the training data,
and the center-most pixel is the PUT.
The CA-CFAR detector can be converted to nding targets in other distributions, by generalizing
the concept i.e. a set of hypotheses are set up;
H0 : PUT = ⟨Background⟩
H1 : PUT ̸= ⟨Background⟩
A threshold is found based on the given distribution. If said PUT is lower than the threshold, the
PUT is no dierent from the background, if it exceeds the threshold, a target is declared present.

in terms of the pre-set probability of false alarm, i.e α. The GO-CFAR is supposedly better than
CA-CFAR in non-homogeneous clutter environment, as choosing the larger mean should decrease
the amount of outliers detected, it does however still suer from the 'adjacent target problem'
described in CA-CFAR. SO-CFAR is also better in non-homogeneous clutter environment, but
will be slightly better at nding adjacent target than GO- and CA-CFAR, unfortunately this will
increase the false alarm rate [Wol].
3.1.3.
OS-CFAR
OS-CFAR, Order statistic CFAR, is a method, where the background cells are sorted in ascending
order in numeric value [MathWorks]. The k'th pixel is chosen for comparison with the PUT. The
comparison is obviously still in terms of hypothesis tests, where a parameter τ is multiplied with
the k'th pixel to get a threshold value, i.e if the dataset is exponentially distributed, the case is as
described in CA-CFAR. This method is superior to both CA-CFAR and the two subsets of it, GO-
and SO-CFAR, but lacks in terms of computational eectiveness, as the sorting of the background-
cells is timely. Doing OS-CFAR should however not cause problems in terms of multiple targets
adjacent to each-other. If one for instance chooses the k'th pixel corresponding to the median for
comparison, the presence of a stronger target will not have eected the training data. On the other
hand, if a large amount of targets are present, a lower quantile should be chosen to account for it.
3.1.4.
LN-CFAR and NM-CFAR
LN- and NM-CFAR does a two parameters estimation, i.e mean and standard deviation, but are
only made for the intensity and log-intensity domain, respectively [Ai, Yang, Song, Dong, Jia, and
Zhou, 2017]. The parameter-estimation of these methods are simple, and therefore these are widely
used. This corresponds to doing the above, but on normally distributed data.

4.1.
Foulum dataset
Dataset number one consists of two subsets, the rst being the L-band EMISAR data from April
1998 the second the L-band EMISAR data from may 1998. The test site of the Foulum data is lo-
cated at The Research Center Foulum of the Danish Institute Of Agricultural Sciences. It contains
a large number of agricultural elds with dierent crops. The Foulum datasets are acquired via
the EMISAR system, which is a fully polarimetric airborne SAR system. The system operates at
two frequencies, C-band (5.3 GHz/5.7 cm wavelength) and L-band (1.25 GHz/24 cm wavelength).
The spatial resolution is 2m × 2m (one-look) and the ground range swath is approximately 12 km
with incidence angles ranging from 35◦to 60◦. The data from this system is calibrated using an
internal calibration system. The original one-look scattering matrix data have been transformed
to covariance matrix data and these data are averaged to reduce speckle through a cosine-squared
weighted 9 by 9 lter. The data used in this study is, therefore, 13-look covariance matrix data
with a 5 m × 5 m pixel spacing.
The test site is not ideal for target detecting using CFAR, as no targets of interest are to be found

This section will cover the structure as well as discuss the performance of the product. Below,
three dierent approaches are covered. The rst is an unmodied CFAR on the Foulum data. The

5.2.
Approach and result of CFAR with knowledge about the location of edges
applied to the Foulum dataset
The CFAR with no modications had signicant performance issues near the edges of the segments.
To regard this issue, a new approach is made. Using the theory about edge-detection from Section
3.4.4, the edges of the dataset is extracted, and used as an input for the CFAR detector so as to
make sure, that only homogeneous background is compared to the PUT. The concept has a few
problems, that needs to be handled. The biggest problem is that the edges detected through the
edge-detector are very wide, which means, that edges will span several pixels and create the same
problem as it did with the basic CFAR. Dividing the entire image into segments through region
growth is a way of getting around this problem. In this case pixels near the center of a region is
compared to the surrounding pixels. If a pixel could belong to the same distribution, it is absorbed
by the segment, and a new mean is found for the pixels in the group. This is an iterative process
and will be very time-consuming, as each pixel in the image needs to be compared to the local
segment. Another way of handling the problem is thinning the edges, and only comparing pixels
to the respective segment, in which they belong. As the edges are already found, this process is
relatively straightforward. With each orientation, the local maxima of the edge strength are found,
i.e the largest value of the test-value −2ρln(Q) is found and declared an edge. Throughout this
project, four orientations are used, i.e the lter conguration is Kf = {lf, wf, df, 4}. The thinning
therefore needs to be done in four steps; the rst step looks for maxima in the horizontal edge
image, the second looks for maxima in the 45◦-edge image and so forth. A detailed description and
illustration of these four steps can be seen in Figure 20.

5.3.
Approach and result of CFAR using truncation and knowledge about edges
applied to the Foulum dataset and the Therma dataset
The modied CFAR, that uses information about the edges made the CFAR detector signicantly
better. The problem is now to avoid strong outliers or targets in the training/background data, as
they may signicantly reduce the performance of the detector. The problem is however twofold,
as the CFAR-detector is coupled to the edge detector. If a strong outlier is found on either side
of the PUT in the edge-detector, the PUT may be ruled edge. If the edge detector is awed by
the presence of targets, the idea about using the thinned edges as input to the CFAR-detector is
awed as well. One of the largest errors, that will be encountered is that the PUT is surrounded
by an edge, such as in 21(c). A large portion of the training data will, in this case, be ignored, and
the PUT may ultimately be compared to a wrong background as opposed to where it resides.
The adaptively truncated clutter-statistics-based two-parameter CFAR detector is a good starting
point. The downside of this method is the computational time, as the adaptively truncated CFAR
method not only estimates the parameters of the distribution but also eliminates outliers and up-
dates the parameters again. The upside is the apparent ability to perform in very inhomogeneous
and target heavy environment.
To integrate this method, two modications need to be applied; the edge detector needs to truncate
either side of the PUT, to make sure, that the edges, that are found, actually are edges. The second
modication applies to the CFAR detector. It needs to make sure, that the background around the
PUT and guard zone should be target/outlier-free.
An apparent problem that also needed to be implemented, was the adjustment of the conguration
of the edge detector. It is crucial, that the edges are as thin as possible, it is however also crucial,
that the 'hedges', Figure 26, have both edges registered. An edge will through the detector be weak,
then strong then weak again, as shown in Figure 20. Is the situation, that a hedge is present, the sit-
uation will be weak, strong, weak and then strong and weak again. To catch both edges, and reduce
the thickness, the conguration is changed from {9, 3, 1, π/4}, corresponding to 45 by 15 meters in
the Foulum dataset, at a 1% signicance level to {6, 2, 1, π/4}, corresponding to 40 by 10 meters
in the Foulum dataset, at a 0.01% signicance level. The thinning of the edge will be changed as
well. It has been defaulted to nding the max of 20 continuous values, this shall be reduced to 5.
On to of this, the truncation will be applied, in hopes of receiving a more usable edge-input for the
CFAR detector. The CFAR detector will only be changed in terms of the truncation, which have
already been included in the codeow of the CFAR_Window_Thin function as shown in Figure 25.
5.3.1.
Codeow Full_edge
To include the truncation parameter, the handle, called 'type_handle' in the Edge_detector, Fig-
ure 22, is changed from Basic_edge to Full_edge. The basic edge handle accepted the two input
parameters consisting of the data on each side of the PUT, it averaged both sides and compared
them to each other. The Full edge handle accepts both input parameters and averages them indi-

5 times for each input. Finally, when all iterations have been carried out, the mean value from
each side are compared to each other. In the rare case, that no pixels fulll the hypothesis of being
equal to the mean of all the pixels, the mean of all the pixels are used instead. The codeow of the

6.
Conclusion
In the previous project, a CFAR detector using polarimetric synthetic aperture radar (POLSAR)
data is developed. The initial detector used only the very fundamentals of the CFAR method, i.e
a pixel under test (PUT), a guard window and a background window. The basic CFAR detector is
incapable of detecting targets in the POLSAR image. The reason for this is that the background
data is not coming from one segment alone. Using a basic CFAR detector will often not be su-
cient due to this problem alone, furthermore, the presence of both outliers but also the presence of
targets will contaminate the training data in the surrounding pixels causing an enormous amount
of targets being found.
To account for this problem, the edges of the segments were found. These were then implemented
into the CFAR detector, so as to ensure, that a homogeneous background was compared to the
PUT. The CFAR detector using information about the edges is very superior to the basic detector
but still suers from the problem with the presence of outliers or targets. The precision of this
type of detector is however degraded around the edges, where pixels are compared to other edges.
Finally, a detector using information about the edges while simultaneously truncating the back-
ground or training data was developed. The truncation was implemented to regard the outliers and
presence of targets in the training data. The truncation was added to the edge detector as well, so
as to ensure, that the outliers or targets did not create edges around the targets. The truncation
works by comparing the training pixels to the mean of the training data, and exclude pixels that do
not belong to the training data. The product became capable of detecting targets after this change,
but still had problems near edges, where the training data only consists of other edge-pixels. The
truncated CFAR detector with information about the edges does also suer computationally, as
the many iterations and comparisons to exclude the outliers in the training data is ineective.
The result of the two rst detectors is assumed incapable of detecting. The basic CFAR detector
is completely unable because of the presence of segments, i.e the background is not homogeneous.
The second will suer around edges, and especially around thin segments dened herein as hedges.
This is primarily because the edge detector does not nd both edges of the hedge-segment, resulting
in a mix of two segments being used for comparison. The last attempt at creating a good detector
is certainly capable of detecting targets. It does, however, require a long time to carry out this
detection which obviously is a problem in terms of real-time target detection, which is the ultimate
goal of the project.
The equivalent number of looks (ENL), as well as the parameters ρ and ln(Q) are used in the
determination of the threshold as well as the test-size. Especially the ENL is a dicult parameter
to estimate correctly. This is primarily due to the fact, that the conguration of both the CFAR
but also the edge detector changes with the truncation. Eectively what this means, is that the
estimate should be updated after each iteration, as the conguration is changed when truncation
excludes pixels. The truncation process is slow with xed values of ρ, while the ENL is being
updated based on a linear regression model of the conguration. The process will be many times
slower if a correct estimate of the ENL and ρ-parameter were to be used. It is therefore very
important, that the ENL is correctly estimated for the conguration, that is used if it is based on
the linear regression. As the nal CFAR detector was used on the dataset granted from Therma,
the conguration was not correctly estimated. This meant that the test-size were too small as
compared to the threshold. The reason why the ENL were not correctly estimated was that the
conguration used needed to be much larger than initially assumed when the estimate was carried
out. The result did however still show the vehicles as signicant, which emphasize, that the ve-
hicles will continue to be signicant to an extremely high threshold. Some of the larger vehicles
were however not detected. The reason for this was that the guard-window was too small, and the
vehicles in the POLSAR data were larger than expected due to the resolution, the pixel-size and
the averaging carried out to reduce speckle. This is however easily xed by changing the size of the
guard window so that the vehicles can t into the guard without any of the vehicle-pixels sticking

6.1.
Applications and further work
The product is able to detect targets, and will if the coding is optimized further, be able to detect
fairly fast. One of the major implications with the code structure, as it is now, is that the guard-
window-pixels are loaded into the memory, only to be excluded again. This process is rough, but
can be worked around easily in the hands of a skilled programmer.
Real-time detection is however still a long shot, as the data needs to be processed rst. On top of
that, the detector may have issues around small ponds and the like, if the size of these, are the size
of the vehicles of interest. A good way to use the product is, however, to carry out the detection
and compare it with the edges detected. This way, the targets of interest will show up quickly. On
top of this, it is very recommended, that the signicance level is changed to obscure levels, as the
vehicles will continue to show to a ridiculous threshold. It is however clear, that the detector will
struggle in town-areas, where the background will not be homogeneous regardless of what is done.
To further increase the accuracy of the product, a segmentation could be used instead of the edge
detection. The segmentation should better the problem around the edges, it is clear, however, that
the edge pixels may still dier from the background, as the pixels may indeed be a mixel rather
than a pixel from the given segment.
Another suggestion is to only use the truncation method and even exclude the guard window. By
selecting a suciently large training area, the outliers and targets will be excluded from the training
data. This method does, however, require an enormous window, and it will probably struggle near
hedges, as well, as small segments will be compared to other segments. This method furthermore
has the computational factor, as the many comparisons will take forever.
It could also be of great interest to try testing the method on forced azimuthally symmetric co-
variance matrix data as well as the diagonal covariance matrix data only.
Furthermore using
single-channel SAR data could also be interesting. It has been argued that the forced azimuthal
symmetric covariance matrix data yields better results than full covariance matrix data when deal-
ing with segmentation in POLSAR data [Skriver, Nielsen, and Conradsen, 2003].
