Abstract
Urban transportation networks are subject to significant demand and supply-side uncertainty
leading to a large degree of day-to-day variability in journey travel times.
This variability
in travel times has been found to play a key role in the route choices of travelers. Modeling
route choices of travelers and understanding route choice behavior is a key step for both trans-
portation planning and management. The goal of this Master’s thesis is to investigate how
travel time reliability (TTR), i.e. the uncertainty in travel time, affects route choices through
the use of machine learning techniques for classification. To do so, Global Positioning System
(GPS) data of the greater Copenhagen Area, which is matched to the network of Copenhagen,
is used. Link travel times estimated from multiple observed trajectories at the same time of
day were used to model distributions of travel times. Next, choice sets of routes are gener-
ated for the different origin-destination pairs along with route-level attributes of interest like
distance, mean, and variance of travel time or the share of specific street types of the whole
trip. Followed by this, the route choice behavior is modeled using state-of-the-art techniques
from machine learning, namely Logistic Regression, Random Forests, K-Nearest-Neighbours,
and Neural Networks. This is done to predict the route choice for the same origin-destination
pair and to evaluate how people respond to uncertainty regarding travel time.

1
Introduction
Transportation networks are subject to significant demand and supply-side uncertainty leading
to a large degree of day-to-day variability in journey travel times. This variability in travel
times has been found to play a key role in the route choices of travelers [1] and stems from
varying traveler habits, variation in demand, and particular events on the demand side like
concerts or football games as well as "physical bottlenecks and incidents, capacity variability,
accidents, inclement weather, signal controls, etc" [2] on the supply side. Quantifying travel
time reliability (TTR) and modeling route choices of travelers to understand route choice be-
havior are important steps for both transportation planning and management. The latter forms
a key component in traffic assignment, which is the last step in the widely used four-step trans-
portation planning model [3]. Historically, predominantly stated preference (SP) studies have
been used for these tasks. But the growing availability of data gives rise to revealed preference
methods (RP) to quantify TTR and travel time distributions (TTDs) [4]. The main difference
between RP and SP studies is that the former is "based on actual, observed behavior" like
the GPS data in this study, while the latter is "based on hypothetical, stated behavior" [5].
Examples of this kind of study could be questionnaires.
The aim of this thesis is to exploit a large dataset with 2.3 million car trips supplied by the
company "Connected cars A/S" [6] to achieve two objectives. First, TTDs as well as TTR
measures on the link level shall be estimated. Links are the edges, of which the street network
consists and they can be identified by a unique identifier. Secondly, route choice behavior shall
be modeled with machine learning methods, including Logistic Regression, Random Forests,
K-Nearest-Neighbours, and Neural Networks. The contribution of this study to the body of
knowledge is, therefore, twofold: it estimates central measures of transportation planning like
the TTR profoundly by using a great number of observations and second models route choices
with machine learning and compares the solutions to a classical approach. Traditionally, route
choice behavior has been studied using discrete choice theory based on the Random Utility Max-
imization framework, and thus only very few studies "applied data-driven methods to identify
route choice set for each origin-destination pair" [7]. The results of this thesis are, therefore,
of high relevance in this field of study today.
The remainder of this thesis is structured as follows: in section 2, a brief literature review of the
central articles about travel time reliability and route choice modeling is presented. Section 3
describes the provided data and contains an exploratory data analysis and preprocessing steps.
Section 4 is concerned with the process of transforming the provided GPS data to link-level
travel times. Section 5 introduces the Machine Learning algorithms and route choice methods
used in this study. In section 6, the results are presented followed by a discussion and conclusion
in sections 7 respectively 8. An overview of the written code can be found in Appendix A.

2
Literature Review
This section is concerned with a brief review of the literature on travel time reliability and
route choice modeling, beginning with the former field of study.
2.1
Travel Time Reliability
Travel time reliability is defined as the "temporal uncertainty experienced by travelers in their
movement between any two nodes in a network" [4] and its importance can be derived from
the penalties travelers face for late arrival. In their paper, Carrion et al. [4] review the state of
research on the value of travel time reliability (VOR) and perform a meta-analysis to analyze
the cause of the differences between reliability estimates. The VOR, therefore, expresses the
monetary value travelers give to an improvement of travel time predictability but can also be
interpreted as the "marginal rate of substitution between travel cost, and travel reliability" [4].
According to the authors, most of its estimates were gained with the use of stated preference
experiments rather than revealed preference experiments. Furthermore, they describe a lack of
RP studies and the reasons for their shortage, namely few demonstrations of RP experiment
settings, unavailability of travel time data, and costs related to the execution of RP experiments.
Generally, travel time can be separated into three components: the free-flow-time (minimum
time which is needed to travel the distance while driving the maximum allowed speed), the
systematic delay, and the unexplained delay. The systematic delay can be anticipated and
it causes, therefore, no uncertainty (e.g. peak vs off-peak variability) while the unexplained
can not be foreseen. Travel time variability includes both uncertainty dimensions, the system-
atic and unexplained delay [8] and is related to the width of the travel time distribution; the
most common definition is the difference between the 90th and the 50th percentile, but also the
standard deviation σ or the coefficient of variation (CV) are commonly used to quantify the
TTR. Different distributions are used in literature to model travel time. Srinivasan et al. [2]
use shifted log-normal distributions (SLN) but also the Burr Type 3 or 12, Gamma or Cauchy
distribution are often used [9].
To quantify the value of congestion and the value of reliability, Prato et al. proposed a revealed
preference approach in order to predict travel behavior.
They used GPS data from probe
cars from which they could obtain about 14.000 observations. A doubly stochastic generation
method was used to create the choice set of 100 routes for each of the observed origin-destination
pairs. Finally, route choice behavior is estimated with a mixed path size correction logit model.
The paper adds evidence about the value of congestion and the value of reliability for peak and
off-peak hours, describes the need to include a measure of reliability and shows how data can
be used in RP studies to "obtain estimates of the monetary value of travel time" [10].

2.2
Route Choice Modeling
Modeling route choice behavior is a key task to understand travelers’ perceptions of routes and
their characteristics, "to forecast travelers’ behavior under hypothetical scenarios, to predict
future traffic conditions on transportation networks and to understand travelers’ reaction and
adaptation to sources of information" [11]. Route choice modeling is, however, a non-trivial
task given the complexity of human decisions, the insufficient knowledge of travelers about
the network, or the lack of information about travelers’ preferences [11]. Historically, choice
modeling has been dominated by theory-driven approaches which are based on Random Utility
Maximisation (RUM). The RUM theory "postulates that an individual is a rational decision-
maker, who aims at maximizing the utility relative to their choice" [12]. The most common route
choice model types are logit and probit [13]. The utility can be divided into two components,
the deterministic utility Vi,n and a random component ϵi,n which describes the uncertainty of
the model. The utility of alternative i for individual n can therefore be denoted as: [12]

Lai et al., therefore, investigated whether machine learning is suitable to model drivers’ route
choice model. They propose different algorithms, such as support vector machines, decision
trees, and neural networks, and address issues such as finding an expressive way to label route
choices as well as interpreting the results for behavior analysis. It was shown that ML al-
gorithms, particularly Random Forests, can yield better results with respect to performance
metrics than classical econometrical approaches like the Multinomial logit. They expound on
the problems of limited data and believe that the precision of their models could be improved
by using a larger amount of data [13].

Further, they compare insights that can be gained from interpreting the Random Forest classifier
in comparison to classical approaches and conclude that "ML methods can also prioritise the
affecting variables that affect travellers’ decision and thus obtain behavioural insights" [13].
However, the importance of the used features differs and, therefore, also different interpretations
will be yielded when using ML instead of classical approaches.
2.3
Conclusion
In summary, quantifying travel time reliability measures and modeling travelers’ route choices
are key tasks in transportation planning and management. Traditionally, route choices have
been modeled using discrete choice theory and even though ML techniques have proven their
superiority in predicting within various fields over the last decades, they are still underexplored
in this context [13, 14]. This thesis adds value by addressing this gap. It seems promising to
leverage their strength in prediction choices, while interpretability should be retained as much
as possible.

This chapter is concerned with the description and exploratory analysis of the GPS data that

The last subsection shows the

3.1
Description of the Data
Connected Cars installs tracking devices in vehicles that log real-time data of the GPS points
of trips and store them with a unique identifier in their cloud. The company wishes to provide
"intelligent and effective monitoring of vehicles for workshops and fleet managers, as well as a
better driving and service experience for car owners" [6]. The data used in this study has been
collected by the company in the time window of September - November 2019 in the greater
metropolitan area of Copenhagen and is, therefore, still unaffected by the COVID-19 pandemic.
The trips have been anonymized by cutting between 50 and 500 meters of the start and end
points off.
The dataset was generated by 40,649 cars and contains 2,351,514 different trips with 18 features
as can be seen in Table 3.1. Each trip can be identified with a unique tripId and consists of
multiple rows, one for each GPS measurement which results in a total number of 212,685,546
GPS points. The time period between these measurements is irregular. Generally, there are
three groups of features in the dataset: the first seven concern the car itself (carId, brand,
model, engine, fuel, year, and car segment) followed by five features about the GPS (longitude,
latitude, speed, direction, time of the record) and finally six features about the trip (tripId,
start and end time, duration, estimated fuel use and idle time).

3.3
Preprocessing
The dataset was preprocessed in multiple steps before it was ready to use. The fact that more
data was available than could be processed gave the opportunity to select a subset of data
that will turn out to be most useful to the remainder of the study. The bottlenecks of data
processing will be described in subsection 4.1. For the purpose of this thesis, it is beneficial to
have trips that overlap, so that multiple observations of travel time can be obtained for each
link. Therefore, data outside a certain range of latitude and longitude was disregarded (since
trips out of Copenhagen will less likely overlap).

4
Travel Time Calculation
In this chapter, the process of transforming the raw GPS data into travel time distributions will
be described in three steps, starting with a section about the task of "map-matching", followed
by timestamp matching, which is concerned with calculating the travel times on a link level.
The third subsection deals with the fitting of travel time distributions to the travel times.
4.1
Map-matching
In order to calculate travel times with the provided GPS data, it is necessary to match them
onto the underlying street network, a process called "map-matching". For this task, the open-
source map-matching algorithm of Milliard-Ball et al. called "pgMapMatch" [16] is used. While
there are multiple algorithms for this task, the authors introduce a useful "match score" that
represents the "likelihood that the match for an individual trace is correct" [16]. This metric
is based on three components: a geometric likelihood based on the distance of every vertex
to the candidate edges, a topological likelihood that compares the length of the GPS trace to
the length of the candidate trajectory, and a temporal likelihood. For the latter, the algorithm
calculates the speed which would be required for traveling between two vertices and compares
it to the speed limit of the street. This procedure addresses one main shortcoming the authors
have found while reviewing previous algorithms, namely that they assume that drivers always
take the fastest possible route.
The main caveat of the pgMapMatch algorithm is the time needed to map-match traces with
an average time of 14 seconds [16]. The authors state that precision is prioritized over speed.
Matching all of the 1.6 million trips in Copenhagen would therefore take up to 9 months with
a single CPU thread. Even running the algorithm on a server with multiple threads leads to
too extensive runtime. Because of this, map-matching is the bottleneck regarding more data
usage. This study will, therefore, use a subset of the Copenhagen dataset. For each of the 92
days, 4000 trips have been randomly sampled, leading to a subset with 368,000 trips.
Still, obtaining this subset of map-matched data required a lot of computational resources. On
average, each trip needed more than 20 seconds as opposed to 14 in the study of Milliard-ball
et al. This can be explained by the length of the trips in this study, as the time required for
map-matching correlates with the length of the trip. Also, there are outliers that require up to
30 minutes for a single trip. This resulted in a total run time of nearly three months. Therefore,
it was necessary to run multiple map-matching processes in parallel. Also, the total amount of
368,000 trips was split up into three subsets, so that it was possible to start working with the
first subset while the others were still in progress.
11 of 60
==PAGE:21==
The map-matching delivers multiple new pieces of information for each trip. First, a list of
links the car has traveled. "Links" are sub-parts of the streets in the street network and can
be identified with a unique id. By obtaining the link ids, it is possible to state which specific
streets in Copenhagen the car has driven. Second, the map-matching score is provided. In
their paper, Milliard-Ball et al. suggest a threshold of 0.7 for the score, as "a lower threshold
probability will yield more false positives, i.e. matches that are predicted to be successful but
are not in reality, while a higher threshold probability will yield more false negatives" [16].
Third, a linestring object is gained, which contains and connects the GPS points of the links
which have been matched to the trip, and last, the origin and destination nodes are stored.
To make this study easier to understand, the following terminology is introduced: at this point,
three different kinds of datasets are used. The first is the "Sampled dataset" of Table 3.2
with the GPS points the study started with, which from now on this text will refer to as the
"GPS data". Second, there is the data gained by the map-matching process, namely the "Map-
matching data" with the properties described above. Last, there is the "Streetstable" which
contains information about the underlying street network of Copenhagen. An overview of the
features of the new two datasets can be seen in Table 4.1 and 4.2.

4.2.1
Naive Approach
In the naive approach, the travel times for each link were obtained by finding the entry- and exit
times for each link. To do so, it was necessary to find the GPS points closest to the start and
end of each street. This was achieved by minimizing the distance between the link coordinates
and the coordinates of the original data. In order to get a realistic matching for the whole
trip, each matched timestamp had to be at the same time or later than the time stamp before.
Additionally, a penalty factor was introduced to minimize the gaps between the matched GPS
data. The motivation behind this was as follows: if the car crosses the same streets multiple
times, the algorithm should not simply prefer the time stamp which is closest to the link but
should also take the temporal dimension into consideration. Therefore, the GPS point which

4.2.2
Dimensionality-reduction-approach
The first, naive approach for obtaining the travel times on a link level has three major caveats:
a large amount of data is discarded, it yields imprecise results and it is down to chance that the
GPS points fall on the beginning and end of the street. While developing the next approach,
all these three problems shall be addressed.
The first thought behind the second approach is that any street link is surrounded by the
respective GPS data and, therefore, one can obtain the travel time of a link by finding out how
much of the distance between two GPS points is overlapping with the link. The general idea
is illustrated in Figure 4.5. If the time delta between the GPS recordings is for example ten
seconds and the link overlaps with 40 % of the distance between two GPS points, four seconds
should be associated with the link (as highlighted with the thick line in the figure below). 13
seconds will be distributed in total to the link in the middle.
Figure 4.5: General idea of the second approach. Note that the same trip is shown as in
figure 4.2. By comparing the color of the GPS points, one can find the exact segment which
was zoomed in on
Having this in mind, it comes down to finding the overlaps and quantifying the percentage of
overlap. The first idea to obtain these metrics was the following: starting from a GPS point, an
algorithm should find the first GPS point, with which it fully includes a link. Visually speaking,
this would mean that in Figure 4.5, the algorithm would start with the leftmost GPS point and
stop only at the rightmost GPS point, as the two GPS points before do not fully cover the link
in the middle. The algorithm should find this point by comparing the latitude and longitudes
of the GPS points and links and find those GPS points, which surround the link. From there,
the illustrated calculations could be executed in order to get the travel times.
17 of 60
==PAGE:27==
However, this idea was quickly dropped as it is prone to errors. Especially, when the GPS trace
is going vertically up- or downwards, this algorithm got stuck and could not finish matching
the trip. It was tried to include a tolerance factor, which would make it easier for the algorithm
to find the next GPS point, but still, this approach seemed not to yield reliable results. The
problem with this approach is that it tries to find the overlaps with the coordinates being in
their original, 2-dimensional representation. Realizing this led to the final method. In order to
find the overlaps between the links and the GPS points, both will from now on be projected
onto a lower dimension, meaning that the longitudinal coordinates (or x-coordinates) will
be compared against each other as well as the latitudinal (or y) coordinates. For a better
understanding, this idea is illustrated in Figure 4.6.
Figure 4.6: Illustration of how overlaps are detected and quantified. Again, the same trip is
shown as in Figure 4.2.

Referring to Figure 4.6, the matching would work as follows: the beginning of the street (marked
with "Start") falls onto a GPS point, so no further work needs to be done. The following three
GPS points are fully included in the link, so their full time deltas will be assigned to the link.
The next point lies outside, but as the point before is within the street, some of the passed
time should be assigned to the link. The distance between two GPS points shall from now on
be referred to as "edge". This makes it easier to refer to the time, which passed between two
consecutive GPS points. The algorithm now calculates the length of the edge and then
the overlap of this edge with the street in the x and y dimension. The actual overlap is
then calculated with Pythagoras’ Theorem. This approach assumes all distances to be straight
lines, which is a good approximation when GPS data is recorded frequently, as in this case,
the distances are shorter and therefore more similar to straight lines. For infrequent recording,
the approach gets assumably less accurate. However, during this study, many trips have been
visually inspected and it was found that the GPS recordings are of high enough resolution to
justify this assumption. By dividing the length of the overlap by the length of the edge, it is
possible to know exactly how much percent of the time has to be assigned to the link. The
remaining time of the edge will then be distributed to the following link in the next step.
A second metric that is calculated, is the amount of the link which is covered by over-
laps. This will be used as a quality measure. If, for example, the algorithm can not find all
the overlaps of the GPS points with the street and it is covered by less than 80 %, it will be
discarded. Even though the threshold of 80 % seems rather low, it was found that this is a
good compromise between throwing out too many observations and achieving reliable results.
Especially in curves and after street corners, the amount of street coverage might be below
90 %, while the algorithm still delivers a sufficient match to the GPS points and hence reli-
able travel times. This is the case for Figure 4.7b, where only 82 % of the street is covered
by the two edges as the street spans a bit further in the x-dimension than the GPS points,
which will always be the case when the devices are not tracking precisely in the middle of an
intersect. Still, if one imagines the movement of the car from one GPS to another (plotted in
green), it will pass by the whole street segment, which is indicating that the match is good.
While Figure 4.7a is arguably also a mediocre match, it will be discarded as the link is cov-
ered by 77 %. One argument for why this matching in (a) is qualitatively worse than in (b)
is that a car starting from the first GPS point to the last will not cover the whole street segment.
On the other hand, a matching also has poor quality, if the link is associated with too many
edges. This could lead to the mistake of distributing too much time to that part of the street.
Therefore, an upper limit of 1.2 street coverage was consequently set to ensure high-quality
results. Still, the algorithm is not working perfectly in every single case. We can see an example
in Figure 4.8a where the match was discarded as the coverage is above the threshold, while the
matching is perfectly fine. The reason for this mistake is in this case the slightly inaccurate

Overlap definition and quantification of the ratios
Multiple sets of different rules have been tried and evaluated to yield the best possible result,
including a rule, which
1. detects if a street lies completely between two GPS points (as in this case the whole
spread of the street is explained and the algorithm could be stopped early),
2. detects if two GPS points lie completely on a street to accurately account for 100 % of
their time delta to the respective street,
3. compares the distances of the start and end of the edge and street to detect if the overlap
is not legit and in fact lies outside the street,
4. neglects edges, which have a slope with a different orientation than the slope of the street
in order to detect corners.
While the rules above worked sufficiently in combination with the rules which have finally been
used and will be explained below, they made the algorithm complicated and non-intuitive. It
turned out that a simple set of few rules which have been carefully adjusted, perform better
while also increasing the explainability. Additionally, the more specific rules above (including
all the hyperparameters which came with them) often led to wanted behavior in some parts

Matched GPS points need to be consecutive
Finally, a rule is introduced that requires the GPS points matched to a link to be consecutive.
So if for example, the car trip is indeed a circle, then a link might have overlapped with GPS
points 2, 3, and 4, but also 12, 13, and 14. But as 4 and 12 are no consecutive numbers, the
second chain of numbers will be disregarded for that link. Additionally, the following link can
not be matched to any GPS points prior to the last matched point (as the edge of the last
matched point and the point after will be the first edge that overlaps with the next link. This
can be visually be retraced with Figure 4.5).
This strict assumption was relaxed later on. The GPS data does have outliers from time to
time and to make the algorithm more robust, it is allowed to have a matching gap of a
maximum of one GPS point for a link if the algorithm detects overlaps for the
following points. The time of the gap will then afterwards be associated with that link. An
example, of where this feature leads to an improvement is shown in Figure 4.9. It can be seen
that the slope of the street is quite steep and therefore, a matching is accepted while having
overlap only in the y-dimension. However, as elaborated earlier, it is required in that case that
both, the edge and link need to have steep slopes, which is not the case for one of the points
in the bulk. After a matching gap of a maximum of one GPS point is allowed, the algorithm
yields the wanted results which can be seen in Figure 4.9b. Generally, the algorithm should
find the GPS points, which fully embed the link.

5.1
Choice Set Generation
In this chapter, the generation of the choice set, i.e. the generation of alternative routes for
every observed trip, will be described. These will be used with the observed trips to train the
machine-learning model, which will learn to distinguish the observed route from the choice set
alternatives and learn the importance of the features along the way.
In order to get reliable results from the model, it is necessary that the choice set consists of
realistic alternatives, i.e. routes, which the driver could have actually chosen. Also, the choice
set should reflect a large variety of routes, since multiple factors may influence car drivers’
decisions, including that [24]
1. people have limited knowledge about the traffic network, which means they can only
choose rationally based on their perception.
2. there might be considerable day-to-day variation along different routes.
3. differing routes are often chosen for the sake of variation.
4. different persons have different preferences and driving habits.
Therefore, a choice set should have a larger variety than the "x" shortest paths by distance
or time. In fact, observational studies have shown that less than 50 % of drivers choose the
shortest path by distance or time [25]. Another reason, to not only include shortest paths is
the fact, that they are often quite similar.
A way to generate additional routes, which substantially differ from the shortest paths is to
sample a "cost" from a uniform distribution for each link. Then, a "shortest path" will be
calculated based on this metric. Calculating multiple shortest paths based on this "cost" will
again result in similar routes and it is, therefore, necessary to assign a new random cost for
each route that shall be generated. Due to its randomness, the choice set will have a large
variety of alternatives, but since the algorithm still searches for the most efficient alternative
based on this random cost, it will not calculate arbitrarily long paths. While this approach
might appear unorthodox, the literature suggests that choice sets are better if they are properly
disjointed [26]. In addition, the fact that the choice set will contain multiple shortest paths and

==PAGE:42==
an observed trip justifies the unorthodox completion of the choice set. A choice set of size 100
for a sample origin-destination pair (OD) can be seen in Figure 5.1 to get a feeling of how this
way of generating routes is behaving.

is computationally unfeasible for this study, the aim was to get an expressive choice set of

shortest paths based on length are added followed by the five shortest paths based on
travel time. The number of the latter group can be lower than five when one path appears
in both groups. Finally, the choice set was completed with the shortest paths based on

cost, when all shortest paths by length and time are exactly equal. It is not always possible

5.2
Route Choice Modeling with Machine Learning
Now that a choice set of size 16 with 15 features (mean travel time, variance of travel time,
length, 11 street type features, and peak/offpeak information) is accessible for 140,000 OD pairs,
one can start building prediction models in order to understand and model car drivers behavior.
In the center of implementing route choices with machine learning lies the issue, that labels are
needed in order to train a supervised ML model. A route choice set, however, has no natural
set of labels that comes with them.
Therefore, three different sets of labels are generated
for performing ML. For the first, random labels from 0-15 are attached to each alternative of
the choice set. The second alternative was proposed by Lai et al. [13]. In their paper, the
choice set of each OD pair is ordered by length and labeled accordingly, i.e. that the shortest
alternative is labeled with "0" and the longest with "15". It should be noted, that they still
use the length of the trip as a feature for prediction. While this seems problematic at first
glance, it is presumably justifiable as the length features are on different scales for different OD
pairs. However, in this thesis, the feature "length" is dropped when predicting labels based
on distance due to the risk that this has a bad influence on the meaningfulness of the model.
The third way to label the data is to reformulate it as a binary classification problem, i.e. that
no longer 16 classes need to be correctly labeled, but only two. The way how this is achieved
will be described below. Based on these three ways of labeling the data, four approaches of
modeling choice behavior with machine learning were developed:
1. Multi-class-classification with random labels
In this case, the data with 2.240.000 rows (140,000 OD pars with 16 alternatives) and 15
features are rearranged as follows. Each choice set for one OD pair is put into one row.
The position of the true observations among the artificial ones is random. The target is
then the position of the true observation (number from 0 to 15). Since the information,
whether a trip is during peak or off-peak is always the same for an OD set, it will be
added as one feature for the whole row.
37 of 60
==PAGE:47==
2. Multi-class-classification with labels ordered by length
The only difference to 1. is, that each choice set is ordered by length and the label is set
accordingly.
3. Binary classification using a one-versus-all scheme
In this approach, each choice set for one OD pair is again put into one row. The difference
to 1. is that now each alternative will be put into the first position of the row one by one.
If the alternative is the observed one, the row is labeled with 1 and 0 otherwise. This
way, each alternative is compared against all the others once.
4. Multi-class-classification with a convolutional neural network (CNN) depict-
ing an MNL model
In this case, the same dataset as in 1. is used. The difference is, that now a CNN is used
to model a Multinomial logit (MNL) model. The exact way, how this will be implemented
will be discussed in subsubsection 5.3.2.
Except for 4., for each approach three models are implemented, namely Logistic Regression,
Random Forests, and K-Nearest neighbors, which will be trained with four-fold cross-validation,
and parameters are optimized with Grid Search. All ML-algorithms will be discussed in the
next section. The models will be assessed using the accuracy (percentage of correct predictions)

are, to the best of the authors’ knowledge, the most profound TTR estimates for the city of
Copenhagen. When comparing the average values of around 0.5 with the literature [32], it
can be noted, that the variability of the travel time is rather high for the data we have. This
means, that we can expect, that the variability will play an important role in how travelers
choose routes in the machine learning experiments.
The CV varies heavily among street types. It is lowest for motorways with a mean coefficient
of variation of 0.31 and a peak around 0.25 and highest for primary links with a mean and
peak of around 0.78. Primary links are "the link roads (sliproads/ramps) leading to/from a
primary road from/to a primary road or lower class highway" [28]. Recalling that a CV value
of 0 indicates no variability and a higher value greater variability, this finding seems intuitive;
while cars drive uniformly on a highway, people might accelerate or brake differently on ramps
leading to primary streets, which makes the travel time on these links more uncertain. We
can see the second-highest average of CV values for tertiary links (0.76), i.e. "the link roads
(sliproads/ramps) leading to/from a tertiary road from/to a tertiary road or lower class high-

of their low number of samples of 54. The average value for motorway links is with a value
of 0.47 not as large as for primary, secondary, and, tertiary links but still way larger than the
values for the motorways themselves (0.31).

7
Discussion
This section is concerned with a critical revision of the used methods, a discussion of how
machine learning methods can be used in route choice modeling, and an outline of future work.
7.1
Limitations
A major motivation for this thesis was adding further evidence about travel time reliability
measures and route choice modeling by using a dataset, which is larger than those of most
comparable studies in the literature.
It was possible to create a dataset with 140,000 OD
pairs. However, with some decisions, the dataset could have been even larger. First, it would
have been useful, to only map-match weekday trips. Weekend trips were not the subject of
this study and as the map-matching procedure is the main bottleneck for the use of more
data, it was wasteful to map-match and not use them. However, even though more weekday
trips could have been gained by sorting the original data beforehand, it would not have been
possible to process more data during the following steps. Especially the generation of the choice
set is computationally demanding, too. 15 alternative routes were generated for each origin-
destination pair of the 140,000 trips, which is only a fraction of the 100 alternatives Prato et
al. used in their study [10]. But this is not necessarily a problem since it is not obvious, which
size the choice set should have:
"On one hand, a small choice set may not be able to reproduce the chosen route,
and consequently may not capture the individual’s behavior and preference.
On
the other hand, a very large choice set might cover the chosen route, but possibly
lead to misinterpretation of the estimated route choice coefficients, as relatively few
alternatives are actually perceived by individuals." [7]
Also, the performance of the models does not increase further beyond a size of 20 [13]. There-
fore, further steps for this study include generating a larger choice set, but it should still be
pointed out that the size of 16 is enough to draw expressive conclusions.
Travel time distributions have been fitted for all links, for which ten or more observations were
available. For modeling the TTDs and calculating their mean and variance, the Lognormal
distribution has been used. However, parts of the literature and own experiments suggest the
use of the Burr Type 12 distribution [9]. This kind of distribution was not used, as its ex-
pected value is not defined for some configurations of its parameters [9] which was the case for
a subset of links. In addition, it is easier to aggregate multiple Lognormal distributions. In a
larger study, it could nevertheless be value-adding to use the Burr distribution instead of the
Lognormal and to find ways of approximating the mean value.
55 of 60
==PAGE:65==
This study used two different street-network databases, namely the one of OSMnx and the
Streettable (osm2po). The reason for this is, that the map-matching algorithm uses the Street-
table, while the rest of the methods like the choice set generation relied on the built-in features
of the library OSMnx which uses its own system. By developing a recursive algorithm, it was
possible to connect 95 % of the links between the dataset. The remaining 5 % have been as-
sumed to have similar values as links in the proximity. Working with two different datasets is
therefore introducing additional impreciseness. This problem can be avoided by relying on one
street network by using other ways to generate the choice set. However, due to the fact, that
the databases contain the same information in the vast majority of the time and since a level
of translation of over 95 % was achieved, is it unlikely that this caused any problems. Despite
the extra effort, the use of two systems causes, are the remaining disadvantages neglectable.
7.2
Machine Learning in Route Choice Modeling
Historically, route choices have been modeled using discrete choice theory and even though ML
techniques have proven their strength in predicting within various fields over the last decades,
it was shown that they are still underexplored in the context of route choice modeling [13, 14].
This thesis aimed to address this gap in the literature. Four machine learning approaches, of
which the last one (the CNN) depicts a classical discrete choice model, were developed. In
this subsection, the learned coefficients of the first approach, particularly those of the Logistic
Regression, shall be compared against the CNN in order to find out, whether one can derive
the same conclusions when using ML or discrete choice theory in route choice modeling.
In Table 7.1, two sets of coefficients can be found. First, those of the CNN shown in the chapter
before (approach 4). The second set of weights stems from the Logistic Regression with random
labels of approach 1. The reason, why these two approaches are compared against each other
is, that they use the same set of random labels. In addition, the LR is most similar to the linear
CNN. Note, however, that the coefficients of the LR using l2 regularization from Appendix
C are used, not those of Table 6.3. This was done as the l1 regularization shrinks coefficients
to 0. To make the comparison easier, the average values over all classes of the coefficients in
Appendix C were computed. First and most importantly one can see in Table 7.1, that the signs
of all learned features are the same except for 11 (motorway) and 21 (secondary). Importantly,
the weights for these two features are at least for one of the models just above or below zero,
and therefore, one can say that both models are not entirely discordant.
The largest negative features for the CNN are 41 (residential), mu_per_m ( µ

be regarded as a utility or as a disutility, while some notable differences remain. Particularly,

7.3
Future work
In this thesis, the map-matching library was used to obtain the links car drivers traveled from
GPS data. To calculate link-level travel times, a timestamp-matching algorithm was developed
which connects the temporal information of the GPS points to the links. In future work, it
would make sense to combine this algorithm with the map-matching library. The map-matching
algorithm finds the most likely route among others based on the GPS points, that have the
timestamps as a feature. Therefore, one could add the quantification of the travel times to the
map-matching algorithm so that it gives the travel times as an additional output. This would
improve the quality of these measures even further and decrease the number of steps that are
necessary to transform GPS data to link-level travel times.
By labeling the alternatives based on their length, it was possible to achieve an f1-score of 68 %
using a Random Forest. This is not surprising, as the RF algorithm performed best for route
choice modeling in multiple studies [13, 7]. A naive classifier would score 19 % in this approach
since 19 % of the cases are labeled with "10". Therefore, it was possible to train a model which
performed over 3.5 times better than a naive classifier on the whole dataset. At this moment,
this study uses 15 features, while in a comparable study, Bekhor et al. used 50 features [7]. It
is likely, that the performance of the model in this study can be further improved if additional
information like the number of left and right turns in a trip or the number of street lights would
be included. Future work with this dataset thus includes the generation of even more features
to improve the prediction accuracy.

8
Conclusion
This thesis used a rich dataset provided by the company “Connected Cars A/S” to firstly obtain
expressive estimates for travel time reliability and secondly address the gap in the literature,
how machine learning can be used to model route choices. 368,000 trips were map-matched
and around 10,6 million link level travel times were calculated with a novel, self-developed algo-
rithm. Coefficients of variation were calculated for over 16,000 links in the Copenhagen street
network, for which ten or more travel time observations were available. These CVs represent
important information for transportation planners and it was found, that this coefficient varies
significantly between street types.
A dataset with 16 alternatives for 140,000 origin-destination pairs and 15 features was de-
veloped, which represents one of the largest datasets to model route choice compared to the
literature. By interpreting four different machine learning approaches for route choice modeling
in-depth, this thesis adds new evidence of how data-driven approaches can be used for modeling
human behavior. First, randomly generated labels were used and an accuracy and f1-score of
48.8 percent were achieved with Logistic Regression. Then, targets based on trip length were
used and a Random Forest scored 69.4 and 68.0 percent for accuracy and f1-score. Third, a
binary RF-classifier using a one-vs-all scheme was trained and yielded an accuracy of 94.9 and
an f1-score of 93.2 %.
Random Forests thus performed best in two of three experiments while Logistic Regression
was superior in only one approach. The LR, however, is most similar to the models in RUM
theory and its interpretation and comparison to classic approaches is therefore more straightfor-
ward. Interpretability is an important property when not only predictions, but also behavioral
insights are demanded and the Logistic Regression offers a good balance between these two
requirements. KNNs scored significantly worse or did not converge at all. It was shown, that
labeling the alternatives by their length can increase the performance by up to 20 % despite
dropping the of route-length feature. Reformulating route choice as a binary problem yielded
no improvement in prediction performance and is computationally demanding and thus less
usable than the other approaches.
Last, a convolutional neural network was implemented to mimic a Multinomial logit model.
Due to low capacity, the performance of the CNN was significantly below those of the other
approaches with an accuracy and f1-score of 43.5 and 44.2 %. By analyzing the trained weights,
it was found, that drivers in this dataset perceive primary and tertiary streets as well as trunks
as a better alternative to the remaining street types. The distance, mean travel time, and
variance per meter are sought to be minimized by car drivers, which is in great agreement with
the existing literature [10].
59 of 60
==PAGE:69==
It can be stated, that the ML methods often yielded results that were similar to the Multino-
mial logit and that interpreting the parameters of the machine learning models, particularly
Logistic Regression, generally leads to the same conclusions as for the CNN depicting an MNL
model. Thus, this study suggests that data-driven approaches can be used to model route
choices and that their interpretation yields behavioural insights. However, it was seen that the
Logistic Regression in approach 1 ignored the variance of travel time when using l1 instead of l2
regularization which shows that one must be aware of the exact specifications of a model when
using it for choice modeling. The importance of the used features differs between the CNN and
the LR while they both select predominantly the same features as the most important features.
This study, therefore, concludes that machine learning models have great potential in modeling
route choice and human behavior.
