Introduction
This project is accomplished in collaboration with Danfoss. The company is well known
in cooling and heating systems. The project gets advantages of the designed model from
”System identification of an evaporator using Gaussian Process Modeling” project [1].
The presented work in this project is heavily inspired by the project proposal provided by
Danfoss, as can be seen in appendix A .
The project keeps focus on designing control system for the industrial refrigeration units,
specifically the evaporator and the temperature of the superhear zone. Industrial refriger­
ation is imperative for advanced society and therefore the request for high quality control
of these system is noteworthy. Refrigeration frameworks can change incredibly, as frame­
works may be custom­built or modified after establishment. Due to these varieties, it is
frequently the case that there’s no point by point show portraying the particular framework
accessible. A demonstrate can be established, but this could be exceptionally expensive
and time­consuming. The essential advantage of having a good control system is ability
to diminish costs through expanded effectiveness.
These industrial refrigeration systems are ordinarily for retail food­ or industrial cooling.
A common application is cooling of foodstuffs in a general store setting. Essentially, the
cooler works by cooling down air interior a box, which at that point absorbs heat energy
from the foodstuff. Cooling systems of this sort come in numerous variations, changing
both in shape and measure.
Commonly, there are the “closet­coolers” which are either continuously open, or have a
huge door on the front. Another common sort is the “basket­cooler”, which may be a wide
box, which can have a sliding glass outline within the top or have an open top. These
sorts of coolers can be seen in figure

Problem Analysis
This chapter presents the scope of the project and the objectives that ought to be reached.
Different strategies for coming to said objectives are displayed, taken after by the selecting
of the strategies which serve as the bases for framework advancement. This chapter
discuses with the project issue explanation, which serves as a overarching goal for the
project.
As Danfoss mentions in the project proposal the main objective of the project is to design
a reliable and robust control system which is satiable for a kinda of refrigerators and at
the same time it must be plug­and­play. It means that it ought to require the slightest sum
of setup conceivable, and ought to self­adjust most if all parameters are not available.
2.1
Danfoss Requirements
As this project does not concern that of a particular refrigeration framework, there are no
difficult prerequisites. Typically due to diverse refrigeration frameworks having diverse
requirements, depending on the environment in which they operate.
Taking after the extend proposition displayed by Danfoss, some soft requirements are
presented:
• Flexibility: The designed controller must has ability to adapt its self to a new set­point
• Robustness: for this venture characterized as being able to handle outside distur­
bances or obscure inner non­linearities. This moreover covers dealing with different
refrigeration frameworks with diverse inner dynamics.
• Plug­and­Play: The system must:
1. Optimize its own controller.
2. In worst case provides safe control. (probably not the most optimal one)
• Keep the superheat zone in the evaporator above the critical set point.
• Reach and maintain a set temperature of the cooling box (air temperature inside of
the refrigerator).
The primary point guarantees that evaporator does not get overflowed, while the second
point guarantees that the refrigeration unit can cool the products.

Now we take a look into how the refrigerant moves around the system. We start with
the compressor it because it is the driving force of the refrigerant, pushing it around the
system. The refrigerant is entering into the compressor as a saturated vapor and it’s
low temperature and low pressure. The compressor pulls the refrigerant in and rapidly
compresses it. This forces the molecules together so the same amount of molecules fit
into a smaller space. This increases the collision rate of the molecules because the are
all constantly bouncing around as the collide the convert their kinetic energy into heat. At
the same time all the energy is put in by the compressor is converted into internal energy
within the refrigerant, this results in the refrigerant increasing in internal energy, enthalpy,
temperature and pressure.
Now the refrigerant comes from the compressor into condenser. The condenser is where
all the unwanted heat is rejected out into the atmosphere. This will include all of the heat
from the building as well as the heat from the compressor. When the refrigerant enters
the condenser it need to be at a higher temperature than the air around it in order for the
heat to transfer. The grater the temperature difference, The easier the heat transfer will
be. The refrigerant enters as a super heated vapor at high pressure and high temperature
and it passes along the tubes of the condenser. Fans will blow air across the condenser
to remove the heat to cool in down. As the air blows across the tubes it removes the
thermal energy from the refrigerant. As the refrigerant gives up this heat, it will condense
into a liquid. So by the time the refrigerant leaves the condenser, it will be a completely
saturated liquid, still at the higher pressure but also slightly cooler, although it is enthalpy
and entropy will have decreased.
Next the refrigerant makes it’s way from the condenser around and into the expansion
valve. The expansion valve meters the flow of the refrigerant into the evaporator. The
valve is like a spray nozzle. So as the refrigerant passes through the expansion valve, it
will expand because the pipework after it going into the evaporator, where is at a lower
pressure so the refrigerant will be able to expand to fill this. As it expand it reduces in
pressure and temperature. So the refrigerant will leave the expansion valve at a low
pressure and temperature. In figure 2.1 we illustrate a thermal expansion valve which
uses a capillary bolt to control the flow through the valve, depending on the temperature
at the outlet of the evaporator. But it is worth to notice that there are many different type
of the expansion valves.

Subcooling and superheat
When the low­temperature and low­pressure vapor comes into the compressor a high­
temperature and high­pressure vapor comes out at it hottest point and the vapor enters
the condenser coil where it start to reject heat until it becomes a saturated state or vapor

turns into a saturated state approximately in the middle of the evaporator coil. After this
stage the refrigerant will be vapor. So superheat is the temperature increase in vapor.

2.4
Research
Many industrial systems are controlled by a type of classical PI(D) controllers where the
installer typically uses an experienced­based set of controller parameters due to the lack
of information about the dynamical behavior of these systems (i.e. no detailed dynamical
models exists for these systems.) The idea in this project is to investigate the possibility
of utilizing a control strategy that uses a simple and robust controller as a baseline and
through appropriate parameterization optimizes the controller performance correspond­
ing to current operating conditions. The application in this project is a given classical
display case, used in a supermarket refrigeration system (as case study). An expected
configuration of the used sensor instrumentation for the unit is illustrated in (2.2)
Figure 2.2: Sensor configuration of a controller instrumentation in an evaporator
There are two general methods for data­driven controller design, one of them is that the
transformation function of the controller system is predetermined and fixed, and their nu­
merical parameters are variable, and it directly uses the measured information of the
system. In this method, the controller structure is obtained through certain principles
or empirical knowledge of the system, and then the controller design problem becomes
the identification of the controller parameters, which are mostly data­driven control mod­
els such as repetitive feedback adjustment based on this method. The second method
of controller design is based on the estimation of a certain function or some equivalent
descriptions of the system such as neural networks, Taylor estimation or equivalent trans­
formations to nonlinear systems, etc., and then the controller parameters are used to
minimize that function based on a specific goal using data Input­output of the system in­
cluding online and offline data are determined. Among the models based on this method,
we can mention data­based adaptive control [4] [5] [6] [7] [8] [9] [10]
When information from the system model is not available or the dynamic model has large
uncertainties, it becomes very difficult to apply model­based methods for implementa­
tion and analysis. Creating a state space model or input­output model in practice is not
easy and can be almost impossible. Even if the system model can be created, the ex­
istence of unmodeled dynamics is inevitable. In this way, the design of the closed loop
control system based on the system model with uncertainties, under mathematical as­
sumptions, may cause unpredictable problems in practical applications or cause a lack
of safety. Although a large number of articles have been published on the study of the
8

==PAGE:15==
resistance (robustness) of a control system, only a few resistant (robust) control methods
can be considered successful in the face of practical problems; Therefore, the develop­
ment of data­based adaptive control, which is a data­driven method, is very important for
nonlinear systems, not only in theory but also in practical applications. Although fuzzy
adaptive control and neural network­based adaptive control can recognize nonlinear sys­
tems without the need for a detailed dynamic model of the system, each of them requires
a comprehensive understanding of the system in order to create fuzzy rules or extensive
operational data for neural training. . In general, their controller design depends on fuzzy
rules and neural network model; Therefore, the fundamental problems of model­based
control still exist [11].
Data­driven adaptive control is an attractive method that has shown great interest in re­
cent years. In non­linear systems, the working points of the system are determined first.
Since the performance of the system is only valid for a small domain around the work
point, it is necessary to repeat the quasi­partial derivative estimation algorithm for each
work point. Since the emergence of data­based adaptive control, there have been many
studies on it in both theoretical and practical fields, including Zhang in 2006 [12] Leonardo
and Antonio in 2009 [13], and Leonardo et al. in 2010 [14] can be mentioned.
Data­based adaptive control can be used when the system has many dimensions and
details and its modeling is difficult or time­consuming, such as airplanes, drones, ships,
power plants, etc. In fact, the mentioned method shows a high ability to stabilize com­
plex nonlinear systems with high working accuracy [15]. To be more precise, data­based
adaptive control is one of the data­driven control methods. This means that without the
need for system equations and only by using input and output data, the desired behavior
of a system can be achieved [16]. The first step of this research is to estimate the out­
put of an unknown process using CFDL dynamic linearization compact form technique.
This method is based on a new pseudo partial derivative vector [17]. In the second step
of the research and after the estimation of the system, an attempt will be made to im­
prove the characteristics of the transient state response (range, settling time, etc.) and
the characteristics of the steady state response (steady state error) by presenting a new
control method. The proposed method is optimized sliding model control with intelligent
algorithms. Similar to this proposed method is found in references [18] and [19]. The
results of the conducted researches show that the sliding model control method in order
to stabilize and control disturbed systems does not perform well for a group of non­linear
systems. The three major weaknesses of this control method are chattering phenomenon,
low stability margin and unfavorable transient state parameters. Lack of sufficient flexi­
bility against nonlinear behaviors can be introduced as the main reason for the ineffec­
tiveness of sliding model control in disturbed systems. The optimal sliding model control
method shows high flexibility because the control signals are not limited to absolute math­
ematical values. Therefore, the idea of combining sliding model control and optimization
algorithms can be an efficient way to improve the weaknesses of sliding model controller
against nonlinear behaviors in disturbed systems.

2.5.1
Comparison of PID control MPC control
In predictive control, the control signal model is always obtained by solving a constrained
optimization problem. The cost function of the optimization problem can be energy con­
sumption, fuel consumption, tracking error energy, etc., depending on the type of plant. To
better understand the performance of model predictive control, it is better to have a com­
parison between the common proportional­integral­derivative PID controller and model
predictive control to control the speed of a 12V direct current motor. When designing PI
controller parameters, we only consider frequency or time domain characteristics (such
as rise percentage, settling time, bandwidth, etc.) and have nothing to do with the energy
consumption of the control system. But because the motor is 12 volts, we should not ap­
ply more voltage to the motor, because the motor may burn. Therefore, the control signal
must be smaller than 12 volts, which cannot be considered in setting the parameters of
the PI controller. On the other hand, the issues of energy consumption and constraints
related to control signals and even the states of the system itself can be considered in the
predictive control of the model. For example, it is possible to design a controller where
the control signal is less than 12 volts, the motor speed is never greater than 1000 rpm,
and at the same time as these constraints are met, the energy consumption of the motor
is also minimized.
The outstanding advantage of model predictive control is online optimization along with
taking into account the physical constraints governing the system.

Background 
Many industrial systems are controlled by a type of classical PI(D) controllers where the installer typically 
uses an experienced-based set of controller parameters due to the lack of information about the dynamical

robust controller as a baseline and through appropriate parameterization optimizes the controller 
performance corresponding to current operating conditions.

(as case study). An expected configuration of the used sensor instrumentation for the unit is illustrated in

Classified as Business 
The hypothesis that we would like to investigate and confirm is the following: 
It is possible to develop a robust and adaptive control strategy for the evaporation process that optimize the 
controller performance during different operating conditions.   
The solution should function in a plug&play framework.  
 
Purpose 
In a nut shell: we would like to develop a control strategy that can be implemented on any refrigerated unit 
in a given supermarket refrigeration system, i.e. in a plug&play framework. 
As a baseline the controller should use a simple but very robust control strategy in order to guarantee the 
overall requirements that are: 1 – food safety and 2- operational safety (not causing the compressor failure 
by ensuring that the refrigerant at the outlet of the evaporator is superheated). The controller should, 
furthermore,  optimize its performance under different operational conditions.  
The considered system (a refrigeration system) is chosen to maintain overview and clarity, while at the 
same time exhibiting nonlinearities that are common in industrial systems. 
Scope 
The focus will be on the evaporation unit (or the refrigerator) of the complete refrigeration cycle. The 
developed algorithms will primarily be implemented and tested on a (nonlinear model) in Matlab/Simulink 
environment. However, the resulting solution shall be tested and verified on a real system in the Danfoss 
Lab. In Nordborg. 
Solution strategy 
The main aim of this project is to develop a robust and reliable control strategy that can optimize its 
performance during the operation. The candidate is free use the strategy of their choice (in agreement with 
the supervision group). 
 
Any developed method is expected to meet the following requirements: 
• 
Robustness: The solution strategy should be robust against external disturbances (or unknown 
internal nonlinearities) 
• 
Fast adaption and learning period: information on system dynamics can be obtained through 
implementation of appropriate system identification techniques. The required time for carrying out 
the system identification periods should be limited (to avoid problems with maintaining food safety 
requirements)  
• 
Plug & Play feature: As the detailed dynamics of the underlying systems (i.e. evaporators) are not 
known in advance. It is required that the controller 1- provide safe control (albeit non-optimal 
performance) and then 2 – optimize its own performance while maintaining its core functionality 
during the operation.
