Abstract
On-line water quality monitoring is increasingly recognized as a key element in strategies
for integrated urban wastewater management. This is notably due to promising appli-
cations enabled with on-line sensors like: Water quality based real-time control (RTC),
evaluation of impact on receiving water bodies from combined sewer overows (CSO's),
and a better overall understanding of in-sewer processes. However, because of the harsh
wastewater environment, the on-line sensors are subject to, they typically have high main-
tenance requirements limiting their application in practice.
The aim of this master thesis project is to assess the performance of two on-line sen-
sors (HACH Solitax and HACH AN-ISE) when operating under non-ideal conditions by
quantifying the uncertainties related to high-time resolution ammonium and turbidity mea-
surements. The sensors are installed in situ, i.e. directly in the raw wastewater ow, with a
low-budget construction at a CSO structure in the area of Ålebækken in Virum, Denmark.
A monitoring campaign subdivided into three phases has been conducted in the period
November 2016 to March 2017. In Phase A, the on-line sensors were maintained on a daily
basis to establish a baseline (best achievable accuracy). In Phase B, the Standard Operat-
ing Procedure (SOP) proposed by Alferes et al. (2014) for collecting "good quality data"
was followed. This SOP requires weekly maintenance of the on-line sensors. In Phase C,
a low-maintenance scenario was simulated with bi-weekly cleaning of the on-line sensors.
In parallel with the monitoring campaign, grab samples were collected on a daily basis
at the CSO structure and analysed afterwards in the laboratory. The found laboratory
measurements are used to evaluate the on-line sensor data.
The project results indicate that on-line monitoring of wastewater in the sewer system is
feasible. However, a number of challenges related to the installation of the on-line sensors
must be taken into account. Thus, a test period of 2-3 weeks is suggested as a preliminary
step before a monitoring campaign is initiated.
In addition, the results from Phase A
show that under optimal maintenance conditions, the on-line AN-ISE sensor measures
ammonium in wastewater with good accuracy. Furthermore, a comparison of the deviation
between laboratory measurements and the AN-ISE data for Phase B and Phase C suggests
that a less stringent maintenance routine still can provide ammonium measurements of
acceptable quality. Thus, the deviation is found to be approximately 40% for both phases.
The turbidity measurements for all project phases give rise to major deviation between
the on-line sensor data and the laboratory measurements, mainly because of disturbances
as an eect of clogging.
Based on the results obtained in this project, a maintenance
routine with bi-weekly cleaning of the on-line sensors at the Ålebækken CSO structure
is therefore suggested. In addition, an improvement of the in situ on-line sensor set-up
is recommended to prevent major disturbances of the turbidity measurements. Although
the results of this project should be considered as preliminary, they potentially allow for
relaxed on-line sensor maintenance requirements in general and thereby favour a future
and wider deployment of on-line water quality sensors in drainage systems.

Sammenfatning
Online monitorering af spildevandskvalitet anses i stigende grad for at være et vigtigt
element inden for håndtering af spildevand i urbane områder. Med online sensorer mulig-
gøres realtids styring af aøbssystemer baseret på vandkvalitet fremfor kvantitet, hvor
sidstnævnte typisk er tilfældet i eksisterende styringssystemer. Det bliver således med on-
line sensorer således muligt at evaluere eekten af overløb på økosystemet i recipienterne.
Dertil vil online monitorering kunne give en bedre forståelse af de processer, der nder
sted i aøbssystemet. Erfaringer fra tidligere forskningsprojekter viser dog, at online sen-
sorer kræver en høj grad af regelmæssig vedligeholdelse, primært pga. de barske forhold,
sensorerne er udsat for i aøbssystemet.
I dette speciale er det blevet undersøgt, hvordan hyppigheden af vedligeholdelse påvirker
kvaliteten af målinger fra online sensorer. To forskellige online sensorer (HACH Solitax og
HACH AN-ISE) er installeret ved et overløbsbygværk i området Ålebækken i Virum, Sjæl-
land. Sensorerne er installeret in situ, dvs. installeret direkte i spildevandsstrømmen, ved
hjælp af en simpel konstruktion bygget til formålet. I perioden november 2016 til marts
2017 blev ammonium koncentrationen i spildevandet monitoreret med AN-ISE sensoren,
og ligeledes blev turbiditet målt med Solitax sensoren. Samtidig blev der taget stikprøver
ved overløbsbygværket på alle hverdage. I laboratoriet blev prøverne efterfølgende testet
for henholdsvis ammonium og turbiditet. De indsamlede data er herefter anvendt til at
evaluere online sensorernes målinger.
Projektet omfatter tre testfaser. I Fase A renses online sensorerne dagligt med det formål
at denere en såkaldt baseline, dvs. hvor godt måler sensorerne under optimale betingelser.
I Fase B testes anvendeligheden af en vedligholdelsesprocedure (på engelsk Standard Oper-
ational Procedure (SOP)) for indsamling af kmålinger af god kvalitet" udviklet af Alferes.
et al. (2014) i det såkaldte danEAU projekt. Denne procedure blev oprindeligt udviklet
for online sensorer installeret på Lynetten Renseanlæg i København og foreskriver ugentlig
rensning af sensorerne. I Fase C vedligeholdes online sensorerne kun én gang hver fjortende
dag for at undersøge eekten af mindre hyppig rensning.
En af de største udfordringer i projektet har vist sig at være forstyrrelser af sensor-
målingerne pga. tilstopning med papir og andre større partikler fra spildevandet. Det
kan derfor understreges, at en indledede test af online sensorerne er vigtig. Resultaterne
fra projektet viser endvidere, at under optimale betingelser, kan der opnås gode målinger
af ammonium koncentrationer med AN-ISE online sensoren. Dertil indikerer resultaterne
fra Fase B og C, at mindre hyppig rensning af online sensorerne ikke reducerer kvaliteten
af ammonium målingerne, idet at afvigelsen imellem laboratorieresultaterne og sensor-
målingerne ligger på omkring 40 % for begge faser. Resultaterne fra projektet viser også,
at turbiditetsmålinger fra Solitax sensoren ofte forstyrres af tilstopning. Dette medfører, at
turbiditetsmålingerne uktuerer i høj grad, hvilket gør det svært at identicere en eventuel
sammenhæng imellem ammonium koncentrationen og turbiditet i spildevand.

Baseret på resultaterne fra dette projekt kan det overordnet konkluderes, at online mon-
itorering af spildevand ved Ålebækken overløbsbygværk er muligt, herunder, at måling
af ammonium koncentration giver anvendelige resultater. Det anbefales, at online sen-
sorerne fremover renses mindst hver 14. dag. Dertil anbefales det, at installationen af
online sensorerne forbedres med f.eks. yderligere afskærmning eller indbygget rensning,
således at turbiditetssensoren bliver yderligere beskyttet fra det rå miljø i spildevandet.
Afslutningsvis anbefales det, at man i fremtidige projekter undersøger eekten af længere
perioder uden online sensor vedligeholdelse.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The European Water Framework Directive (WFD-EC 2000) has set the goal of achieving
"good ecological status" in all European water bodies by year 2015 (European Commis-
sion, 2009). To achieve this goal, knowledge of the quantitative and more importantly
the qualitative dynamics in combined sewer systems, i.e. pipes that hold both rain- and
wastewater, is required. Untreated sewage from Combined Sewer Overows (CSO), acti-
vated by rain events, can have a negative impact on the ecosystem in the receiving water
body.
Wastewater contains untreated or partially treated human and industrial waste
including suspended solids, bacteria, toxic pollutants and oxygen demanding compounds
such as ammonia (NH3 and NH+
4 ). Thus, a heavy load of wastewater can cause oxygen
decit in the receiving water body, which in severe cases can lead to formation of toxic
by-products, and as a consequence of this, issues with environmental pollution including
mortality of aquatic organisms can arise (Winther et al. (2009), Brzezi«ska et al. (2016)).
Existing methods for sampling and subsequent analysis of wastewater are often inadequate
at capturing the dynamics in the sewer system. For instance, the number of samples is typ-
ically restricted because available automatic samplers generally contain 24 bottles, which
is making it dicult to capture the dynamics of a rain event. In addition, monitoring
programmes are costly and require a high level of maintenance and handling (Bertrand-
Krajewski (2004), Brzezi«ska et al. (2016)). Consequently, on-line water quality monitor-
ing has, during the past two decades, been increasingly recognised as a key component
for integrated urban water management. Many promising applications are enabled due to
the availability of data from on-line sensors. This includes dynamic on-line prediction of
water quality parameters and Data Quality Control (DQC), i.e. detection of anomalies
in the sewer system through comparison of predicted and measured data. Additionally,
valuable knowledge about in-sewer processes such as erosion, accumulation and transfer of
sediments can be gained from on-line monitoring. Finally, on-line measurement provides
the basis for pollution based Real Time Control (RTC) strategies to control storm-water
run-o (Hoppe et al. (2011), Lacour et al. (2009a), Fricke et al. (2016)).
2
==PAGE:17==
Despite the availability of a growing number of on-line sensors, real-time water quality
monitoring is not yet commonly performed in sewer systems. The primary cause is the
absence of accessible locations enabling easy installation and regularly maintenance of the
sensors. So far, several projects have been conducted with on-line sensors installed at the
inlet and/or the outlet of a Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP), mainly by research
groups and universities in Europe and the United States. Experience gained from such
projects shows that because of the dicult conditions, the sensors are subject to, frequent
manual sensor maintenance is required to ensure reliable data (Alferes et al. (2014), Brzez-
i«ska et al. (2016), Lacour et al. (2009a), Bertrand-Krajewski (2004)).
In this master thesis project, two frequently applied types of on-line sensors (HACH Solitax
and AN-ISE) were installed at the Ålebækken CSO structure in the Lyngby-Taarbæk
municipality area in Denmark. At this location, a main pipeline is lead into a 10 meter
long open wastewater channel, ideal for in situ installation and test of on-line sensors.
The term in situ implies that the on-line sensors are installed directly into the wastewater
stream. Compared with previous projects carried out in Denmark, this thesis is dierent
because the on-line sensors are installed upstream in the sewer system and not at the inlet
to a WWTP thereby enabling more direct wastewater quality measurements.

2.1
Characterization of wastewater
The denition of wastewater (according to EU Directive 91/27/ECC) is water that is either
biological, chemical, physical or thermal polluted (Council of the European Communities,
1991). Wastewater from industry and residential areas contains dierent types of contami-
nants e.g. pathogenic micro-organisms, suspended solids, organic and inorganic compound,
heavy metals. This is briey described in the following paragraphs.
2.1.1
Total suspended soils
Total Suspended Solids (TSS) characterize the unsolved substances in wastewater. Accord-
ing to Henze and Comeau (2008), the concentration of TSS in raw wastewater varies from
250-600 mg/l. Organic substances constitute around 70% of the total suspended solids.
However, inorganic compounds such as sand, gravel etc. can be dominating in cases where
the wastewater consists of a large part of rainwater. TSS function as a vehicle for sediment
associated contaminants such as heavy metals, which means that TSS represent the degree
of runo pollution to a certain extent (Némery et al. (2012), Hoppe et al. (2011)). At the
WWTP, various methods (grids, lters etc.) are used to remove suspended solids (Winther
et al., 2009). According to EU legislation, the concentration of TSS in water discharged

from the WWTP after treatment must be less than 35 mg/l (Council of the European
Communities, 1991).

2.1.5
Diurnal variations in wastewater
The wastewater ow from residential and industry areas varies signicantly with time.
The term diurnal variations is commonly used to describe the uctuations in wastewater
during 24 hours. Figure 2.1 illustrates a typical ow pattern in domestic wastewater with
two peaks during 24 hours. The rst peak occurs around noon as a delayed eect of the
consumers morning routines with showers and toilet use. The second peak occurs in the
evening around 20 p.m., where the consumers have returned from work and are cooking
dinner etc. The minimum ow is generally observed during the early morning hours as an
eect of lower consumer activity during the night. The described tendency for wastewater
ow is only present during dry-weather periods. Rainwater- and snow disturb this pattern
(Winther et al. (2013), Gray (2004)).

2.2.1
Water Sampling Campaigns
Water sampling campaigns are commonly used to monitor pollutant concentrations in wa-
ter bodies. A water sampling campaign consists of two parts: Sampling in the eld and
analysis of the samples. In the eld, the samples are collected manually or with an au-
tomatic sampler. The samples are either analysed immediately in the eld with simple
test methods and/or afterwards in the laboratory according to recognised standard meth-
ods (Eaton et al., 1995). Aside from uncertainties emerging from sample conservation,
transportation and preparation, several challenges are in particular related to wastewater
sampling campaigns. One challenge is variation in particle size and settling-time, which
means that some particles will oat at the bottom of the wastewater pipe/channel whereas
other particles will oat at the water surface. The resulting heterogeneity makes it di-
cult to collect representative samples. Another challenge is the quantitative variations in
wastewater induced by rain events, diurnal uctuation etc. (Winther et al., 2009).
Time- and ow proportional sampling

pling. In a time proportional sampling campaign, small equal batches of wastewater are
collected with a pre-determined frequency. All batches together constitute the total sample
volume. With this approach the sampling frequency is adjusted to the wastewater ow and
not arranged according to pre-determined time intervals. If the purpose of the wastewater

2.2.2
Water Quality Parameters
Monitoring of water quality is traditionally based on physico-chemical parameters such
as temperature, conductivity, turbidity, pH and concentration of dissolved oxygen (DO).
Conductivity is a measure of water's capability to conduct an electrical ow and is used
to determine the concentration of ions in the water. A change in conductivity gives an
indication of changed conditions in a recipient. Turbidity is a measure for the haziness of
the water due to colloids and is determined based on the waters ability to dim light. The
international units for turbidity are Jackson Turbidity Units (JTU) and Nephelometric
Turbidity Units (NTU). The pH is a measure of water acidity or alkalinity. The concen-
tration of DO gives an indication of the ecological state in the water body (Winther et al.
(2009), Appelo and Postma (2005)).
2.3
On-line monitoring of sewer networks
Several attempts have been made within the latest decades to install on-line sensors in
sewer systems (Brzezi«ska et al. (2016), Fricke et al. (2016), Bertrand-Krajewski (2004),
Aarhusvand et al. (2015), Alferes and Chad (2013), Lacour et al. (2009a), Lacour et al.
(2009b)). On-line sensors placed at strategic locations in the sewer network can deliver
high-time resolution data series recorded continuously over selected time-intervals. Most
Real-time control systems are based only on hydraulic data and do not account for water
quality control (Lacour and Schütze, 2011). Data measured by on-line sensors can give
a new important insight into the water quality characteristics of a sewer system. When
considering monitoring of wastewater quality, Vezzaro and Mikkelsen (2015) identify the
various applications for software sensors;
 Dynamic on-line prediction of variables: On-line sensor data enables prediction of
water quality parameters that normally would be monitored with a sampling cam-
paign and lab analysis. Simple software sensors can also be used to predict variables
that would otherwise require resource-demanding sensors. For instance the ammo-
nium concentration could potentially be predicted based on data from a turbidity
sensor.
 Monitoring and fault detection: Any deviations from normal sensor behaviour can
be detected when measured and predicted data are compared. This can for instance
highlight a need for sensor maintenance.
 Back-up software sensors; If any measured data is missing from a sensor, the data
can be reconstructed with the predicted data and used as a substitute.

Winkler et al. (2008) argue that a consistent monitoring strategy is an important criteria
for success in monitoring of sewer networks.
Large amounts of data will be generated
rapidly and it will be infeasible to interpret the data without automated tools.
With
an intelligent monitoring station that processes the data properly, it could be possible to
provide the user with 'near real-time' information about the sewer network. This is further
elaborated on in Chapter 6.
2.3.1
On-line sensor installations
Two main methods for installation of on-line sensors exist; In situ and Ex situ. On-line
sensors installed in situ are submerged directly in the wastewater, e.g. in a manhole or
at a CSO structure.
Ex situ installation means the reverse, i.e.
the on-line sensor is
not installed directly in the sewer. Instead, wastewater is pumped from the sewer into
a container or something similar where the on-line sensor are located. Alternatively, the
sensor resides in a dry environment above the wastewater. The latter is the case for for
instance laser-based sensors.

O-line analysis encompass validation of the on-line sensor data. It is generally recognized

faeces in the water (Winther et al. (2013), Winther et al. (2009)). Also, reduction of total
nitrogen (Ammonium, Nitrate and Nitrite) is of high priority at WWTP's (Ministry of

important of the four available AN-ISE on-line sensor parameters to focus on.

Calibration of Solitax turbidity sensor with calibration standards
The Solitax on-line turbidity sensor is calibrated according to HACH Company (2009) with

for a so-called zero-point calibration is as follows:
1. The sensor is removed from its mounting and cleaned with tap water. The body of
the probe is wiped o with paper. Then, the head of the probe is cleaned carefully
with soft tissue and a Q-tip dipped in a 3% HCL solution.
2. The sensor is placed in a calibration cylinder with deionized water so the tip of the
probe is 5 cm under the water surface. The measured turbidity is recorded from
the sensor measure display. The measured value is multiplied and entered as oset
value. Normally, the oset value is around 1.0. The sensor is then taken out of the
deionized water and is dried with tissue. Finally, the calibration cylinder is cleaned.
3. The 200 NTU calibration standard is gently inverted 50 times and slowly poured into
the calibration cylinder. The Solitax probe is then placed in the solution so the tip
is 5 cm under the surface. The measured value is recorded and the probe is cleaned
again. The measured value shall preferably be very close to 200 NTU.
4. The procedure described for the 200 NTU standard is repeated with the 800 NTU
standard. The measured value is recorded and shall preferably be very close to 800
NTU. Based on the recorded value, a new factor is calculated with the following
equation:

a lab measurement and the on-line sensor measurement at the given time where the grab
sample is collected. The deviation D between the lab and the on-line sensor measurement
is calculated with the following formula:
D[%] = Sensorv −Labv

Labv is the lab measurement value, which is considered as being the "true" ammonium con-

ments from the specic time where the grab sample is collected. For both on-line sensors,

4.4
Validation of on-line sensor data
An o-line correction of the on-line sensor data sets is performed to detect the most com-
mon assumed measurement errors. In Matlab (Version R2012a, MathWorks Inc), a number
of simple tests are applied simultaneously to the on-line sensor data, and based on the out-
come of these tests, a measured value is declared valid or not valid. The basic principle
for these tests are adopted from the french software tool EVOHÈ developed by the LGCIE
(Laboratory of Civil and Environmental Engineering) at INSA Lyon (National Institute of
Applied Sciences Lyon). EVOHE enables evaluation of ow rates, volumes and pollutant
loads in the sewer system. In addition, EVOHE works on the basis of several tests cate-
gorized either as "basic tests" or "expert tests" in the user manual (Becouze-Lareure and
Invernon, 2012). In the present project, four of the simple tests have been selected and are
deployed on the on-line sensor data-series. These four tests have been selected based on
the ndings in Jézéquel (2014), which concludes that they are typically sucient and the
most essential ones to include. Each test is described below:
 Status of the sensor: Periods in the data-series where the on-line sensors are
out of operation or in maintenance mode are manually discarded. As a part of the
maintenance routine, a picture is taken of the sc 200 controller before and after the
cleaning procedure is carried out. The two picture time-stamps are then used to
dene the sequence of data that is discarded.
 Physical range: This test detects physically aberrant values and discards them.
The criteria for this test is dened initially. According to Henze and Comeau (2008)
the normal concentration of ammonium in wastewater varies between 20-100 mg/l.
In Jézéquel (2014) a range from 1-80 mg/l ammonium is used as limit for wet-weather
periods. Thus, the interval 1-100 mg/l is dened as the physical range for the valida-
tion tests in the present project. Likewise, the physical range for wastewater turbidity
is set at 100-500 NTU (based on the results presented in Chapter 5). All values not
belonging to these intervals are considered as being outliers and are discarded.
 Variation range (compared to previous measurement): In this test, the mea-
sured value is compared to the previous measurement. A maximum allowed dierence
is initially dened. For the AN-ISE measurements, the maximum allowed dierence
is xed at 20 mg/l. For the Solitax measurement, a maximum dierence of 50 NTU
is set. If the dierence is above the maximum, the value is discarded. The aim of
this test is to detect unrealistic deviations in the data-series.
 Variation range (compared to moving average): The concept of this test is
similar to the previous one. However, the measured value is compared to the moving
average instead of the previous measurement. The number of values considered in
the moving average is dened initially. Likewise, the maximum allowed dierence
between the measurement and moving average is set initially.

5.2
Experience from preliminary test period
The rst objective of this project was to identify the practical challenges related to instal-
lation of on-line sensors at Ålebækken CSO structure. It can be stated based on experience
that a preliminary test period is required when installing on-line sensors in drainage sys-
tems. Thus, it takes both considerable time and man-power to complete the installation
and make the on-line sensors operate correctly. It is therefore important to devote sucient
time to the following tasks before starting a monitoring campaign:
 Location: Finding a suitable location for installation of on-line sensors is a key
prerequisite. Ålebækken CSO structure oers a unique access to the raw wastewater
ow, which makes it "straightforward" to install on-line sensors. Though in drainage
systems, similar locations are dicult to nd, so the alternative course of action
36
==PAGE:51==
is installation of on-line sensors in manholes.
However, Figure A.1 in Appendix
A shows how the access to on-line sensors is limited when installed in a manhole.
This makes maintenance comprehensive because it requires professional manpower.
At Ålebækken CSO structure maintenance can be performed by non-professionals
(students, DTU eld technicians). However, several strict safety instructions must
still be followed when the on-line sensors are maintained.
 Construction: At a location like Ålebækken CSO structure, where the on-line
sensors are installed in situ, a well-designed and robust construction keeping the
on-line sensors submerged in wastewater is essential. In the present project, the on-
line sensors are set-up by a low-budget construction, giving rise to some challenges,
e.g. much paper is caught by the construction pushing the on-line sensors out of
the water. In addition, maintenance of the on-line sensors is hampered by the large
amounts of paper attached to the sensors. For more information see Appendix A
and the Logbook in Appendix J. Another aspect, which must also be taken into
account, is the large variation in water height (from 35 to 122 cm at Ålebækken
CSO structure in 2016). This makes it dicult to build a simple construction that
constantly keeps the on-line sensors below the waterline of the wastewater. Taking
the above into account, it can be stated that a well-designed and robust construction
is key to obtain good on-line sensor measurement performance.
 On-line sensors: It takes considerable time and man-power to adjust the on-line
sensors to a new environment (2-3 weeks). For instance, performing matrix or regular
calibrations is comprehensive. More information in the Log Book in Appendix J.
Additionally, one must be aware that the AN-ISE on-line sensor cartridge must be
replaced every 6-12 months according to the manual (HACH Company, 2013a). In
addition to delivery time of the cartridge, it takes one week for a new cartridge to
adjust to its environment. The aforementioned issues stresses the importance of a
preliminary test period.

6.1.1
Longer duration monitoring campaigns

In particular, the eects of dierent low-

good performance of the sensors and resource availability of sewer operators.
6.1.2
Better sensor performance evaluation
A better statistical basis for evaluation of on-line sensor performance could be obtained
with an increased number of laboratory measurements. For instance, duplicate or triplicate
testing of all grab samples would be a valuable improvement. Likewise, the number of
collected grab samples could be increased. With this kind of data, it would be possible

Other suggestions for improvement include validation of laboratory test results (HACH

6.1.3
Improved in-situ installation
The physical in-situ installation of on-line sensors at Ålebækken CSO structure could be
improved in several ways. On way to improve the installation would be to extend the
shield of plastic around the on-line sensors so it does not only cover the probes, but also
covers the entire metal pipe. This could potentially prevent paper from getting attached
to the pipe in periods with high ow in the channel. However, one have to be careful
in relation to such arrangements since projects like AMOK has proven that for instance
sensor calibration can become inconvenient in such cases (see Chapter 2 for more details).
Implementation of cleaning (e.g. compressed air cleaning) of the on-line sensors is a more
advanced and more expensive potential improvement. Thus, this kind of set-up would
probably mitigate the large number of disturbances seen in the turbidity data-series. It is
likely that more ideas and design concepts for improving the physical sensor set-up could be
the outcome of such follow-up projects. Another way to improve the quality of data from
the on-line sensors, is to update or replace the sc 200 controller device installed together
with the sensors. Thus, the current device has limited data memory, which entails that
data must be retrieved frequently to avoid gaps in the data-series.
6.1.4
Additional parameters with same on-line sensors
The HACH AN-ISE on-line sensor has four ion-selective electrodes, which individually
measures ammonium, nitrate, potassium and chloride. In the present project, only the
recorded ammonium data have been examined. Therefore, a obvious follow-up project
could include processing of the remaining water quality data already obtained from the
present project. In addition, a new project could also be initiated where all the above
four water quality parameters are monitored and validated continuously. This type of data
would enable examination of correlations between the dierent water quality parameters,
for instance in a similar way as described in Winkler et al. (2008) where four parameters
are measured and correlated.

6.2.1
Real-time Control (RTC) strategies
As described in Chapter 2, classic real-time control (RTC) strategies in drainage systems
facilitate the activation of retention volumes based on hydraulic data not taking the quality
of discharges into account. However, with continuous measurements from on-line sensors
installed in sewer networks, pollution-based RTC could be enabled. On-line water qual-
ity measurements facilitate control of pollutant loads transported in sewer systems, thus
making it possible to divert less polluted storm-water run-o into receiving water bodies
and more polluted run-o to the WWTP. With continuous on-line measurements, a better
characterization of wastewater can be achieved, i.e. diurnal variations, in-sewer processes,
etc. Moreover, better acquaintance of wastewater in the sewers could facilitate optimiza-
tion of the treatment processes at the WWTP (Fricke et al. (2016), Lacour and Schütze
(2011), Hoppe et al. (2011), Brzezi«ska et al. (2016)).
6.2.2
Improved water quality models
Water quality data is very useful as background data for estimation and calibration of
dynamic water quality models. Water quality models are used to determine the inuence
of CSO's on receiving water bodies, simulate in-sewer processes and evaluate dierent
RTC strategies. High resolution data-series from on-line sensors could provide the basis
for uncertainty-based calibration of water quality models (as in Vezzaro et al. (2013) or
Vezzaro and Mikkelsen (2015)) or to quantify the accuracy of estimated CSO pollutants
loads (as in Bertrand-Krajewski and Bardin (2002)). Likewise, on-line sensor data can be
used for validation of grey-box models, i.e. a state-space model where the state constitute
the amount of sedimentation in the sewer, and the model output is the pollutant mass ow
to the WWTP (Bechmann et al., 1999).
6.2.3
Intelligent monitoring networks
In integrated urban water management, intelligent data acquisition systems that present
and archive on-line sensor data in a logical and user-friendly way is a key element (Alferes
et al., 2014). Thus, monitoring stations connected to several on-line sensors generate huge
amounts of data, which must be processed and interpreted quickly in a intelligent way
to avoid so-called data graveyards. Winkler et al. (2008) argue that monitoring stations
should not be designed as "data collectors" but as "data communicators". Thus, an intel-
ligent monitoring network has to provide the end-user with automatically quality checked
data, useful as decision support in the day-to-day operation at the utility or WWTP. For
instance, the intelligent monitoring station could inform the end-user about need for sen-
sor maintenance or provide information about a registered combined sewer overow. This
could potentially enable the utility operators to pro-actively mitigate the eects of an over-
ow, if the measured concentrations are considered harmful to the ecological status of the
receiving water body.

just one out of many possible applications within the eld of environmental engineering

6.2.5
Compact in situ microchip sensors
A well-known fundamental challenge for the use of on-line sensors for wastewater moni-
toring is the negative impact of the harsh environment. In the light of this, researchers
have recently been working on inventing more robust sensor devices specically aimed at
wastewater monitoring. An interesting example of this is the so-called MicroChem project
sponsored by the European Community and including partners from the Danish company
Danfoss and from DTU Nanotech (Bowden et al., 2002). The aim of this research project
was to develop micro chips capable of operating autonomously in the eld for more than
one year. As regards maintenance, the goal was that these micro devices had to be able
to operate in maintenance-free mode for up to 12 months. This is enabled by low-powered
micro device operation combined with excellent reagent stability. More specically, the
parameters of these micro chip sensors were ammonia and phosphate, and the ultimate vi-
sion of the project was to enable realization of an extended environmental digital nervous
system, as described in (Bowden et al., 2002). If such micro devices become commercially
available, it would be possible to base future projects on this type of technology. If this
could in turn be combined with IoT technology, a really strong new basis would be available
for future projects.

measuring ammonium at one point in a ow tells you about that point, but measured at

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The presented study shows that in situ on-line monitoring of wastewater running upstream
in the sewer system is feasible. As expected, a certain degree of regularly maintenance is
required due to the harsh environment the on-line sensors are exposed to. Thus, several
challenges were experienced in connection with the in situ installation of the on-line sensors,
e.g. clogging, incorrect mounting of sensors etc., which emphasized the importance of a
preliminary test period before starting a monitoring campaign.
The results from Phase A of the monitoring campaign show that under optimal mainte-
nance conditions, the on-line AN-ISE sensor measures ammonium in wastewater with good
accuracy when compared to laboratory measurements. In addition, a comparison of the
deviation between laboratory measurements and the AN-ISE data for Phase B and Phase
C suggests that the weekly maintenance routine dened in the existing danEAU SOP can
be relaxed without an unacceptable decrease in the quality of the ammonium measure-
ment data. Thus, the deviation was found to be approximately 40% in both phases, with
exception of a number of outliers. All in all, the results suggest that less stringent on-line
sensor maintenance routines can still provide ammonium measurement results useful for
operational purposes.
As regards turbidity, it can be stated that (based on the results from all three monitoring
campaign phases) the Solitax on-line sensor turbidity measurements are characterized by
considerable uctuations. Thus, a major deviation between the on-line sensor data and
the Portable Turb® 43 turbidity meter measurements is found. The primary reason for
this deviation is disturbances caused by paper blocking the sensor signal.
Based on the results found in the present project, the suggested maintenance routine for
the on-line sensors installed at the Ålebækken CSO structure is biweekly cleaning of the
AN-ISE on-line sensor. Moreover, an improvement of the in situ set-up is suggested to
enable collection of data with higher quality from the on-line turbidity sensor. Thus, the
current set-up does not protect the this on-line sensor suciently, resulting in major dis-
turbances of the turbidity measurements. Although the results of this project should be
60
==PAGE:75==
considered as being preliminary, these potentially allow for relaxed on-line sensor main-
tenance requirements in general. In turn, this favours a wider spreading of on-line water
quality sensors in sewer systems, which enables a wide range of environmentally promising
future applications, including: Pollution based Real-Time Control, pro-active management
of in-sewer processes and optimization of key processes at wastewater treatment plants.

Alteration of construction
The rst set-up existed only of a metal pipe holding the sensors and a wooden plank holding
the pipe. During the preliminary test period this construction failed at keeping the sensors
in the wastewater stream. Due to heavy weight from paper the metal pipe moved from
its vertical position. The consequence hereof was that the sensors were sticking out of the
water. The installation was therefore improved with a plastic shield around the sensors to
reduce the amount of paper getting attached. Moreover, a robe was tied around the top
part of the metal pipe and connected to the fence to keep the pipe in place.
Change of Cartridge
Early in the testing period an error message was observed for the AN-ISE sensor saying
Cartridge Old. According to the manual from HACH the sensor cartridge must be replaced
after a period of 12 months (HACH Company, 2013a). A new cartridge was ordered and
the cartridge was replaced on the 22/11-16.
Re-installation of AN-ISE sensor
After the cartridge replacement, a period of two weeks was spend on adjusting the new
cartridge to the harsh environment at the Ålebækken CSO structure. At rst the measured
wastewater ammonium concentration was too low. After several Matrix 1 and 2 correc-
tions based on ammonium concentrations measured with the test-kit from Merck had been
performed, experts at HACH was contacted. The recommendation from the HACH expert
was to change the angle of the AN-ISE sensor to avoid air bubbles from disturbing the
signal in the probe. The AN-ISE sensor was reinstalled with a 45 ◦C degree in the stream.

1 INTRODUCTION 
This document summarizes the protocol for operation and maintenance of the monEAU stations 
within the framework of the danEAU project. The procedure here explained could be adapted to other 
applications. 
 
1.1 Problem statement 
The main goal of monEAU stations is to collect on-line measurements of different water quality 
parameters in different water system (rivers, WWTP, lakes, etc.). The on-line measurements can be 
used to describe and identify spatial and temporal variations in water quality, trends and variation of 
the polluting sources. 
As a SWI-subproject, the danEAU project is aimed to test and evaluate monEAU stations to on-line 
monitor water quality at the inlet of WWTPs, sewers and CSO. Data collected can be used for different 
purposes such as modelling and forecasting of water quality and real-time control. 
 
Table 1 summarizes the set of sensors currently involved within the daEAU project, and it include 
conventional sensors for physical-chemical parameters (pH, conductivity, turbidity, temperature), an 
UV spectrometer (TSS, CODt, CODs), and ion selective electrodes (NH4, Cl-, K+). To achieve a good 
quality of the on-line data a systematic maintenance routine is critical. Checking, cleaning and 
calibration of the sensors need to be properly carried out to guaranty a good behaviour of all the 
system and to provide useful data.  It is the purpose of this document to make sure that future operators 
will follow a standard procedure to avoid/reduce any problem on the field and in the collection of 
data.

2 OPERATIONAL PROCEDURE 
A quality assurance program is needed to guaranty a proper collection and posterior use of the data. 
Such a program includes two main components: quality control and quality assessment of the 
measurements.  
The quality control is carried out by applying a proper maintenance routine that includes: checking of 
the system, cleaning of sensors and fittings, testing/validation and calibration of sensors and finally 
sampling. The quality assessment of the data is carried out by applying different software tools that 
include: offline methods based on control charts and online methods for detection/replacement of 
outliers and filtering.  
Next section summarizes the operational procedure that has been agreed within the danEAU project 
concerning the maintenance routine and offline methods.

At the monEAU stations a systematic maintenance routine need to be followed. Figure 1 summarizes

Sensors will be cleaned once per week

• 
Normally sensors will be treated one by one (cleaning--»testing/validation--»calibration 
when it is needed). However, if two persons are available some tasks could be done in parallel.  
• 
Sensors are tested/validated by using comparative reference measurements. Control charts 
are then built with appropriate acceptability limits. Three types of control charts will be used: 
1- Standard solutions (solitax, pH, conductivity). Done in situ. 
2- results from the laboratory (TSS, CODt, CODs, NH4, K+, Cl-). Done afterwards. 
3- Portable sensors (conductivity, pH and turbidity). Done afterwards. 
• 
Results from the control charts using standard solutions are used to decide when a calibration 
is needed (when the acceptability limits are broken). 
• 
Two samples will be taken at each station twice per week, one at the beginning of the visit 
before any maintenance, and the other one after the maintenance tasks have been done. In 
that way the effect of the maintenance routine can be evaluated.

PRINCIPLE 
 
The automated procedure for the determination of Ammonia is based on the modified Berthelot reaction; 
ammonia is chlorinated to monochloramine which reacts with salicylate to 5-aminosalicylate. After 
oxidation and oxidative coupling a green colored complex is formed. The absorption of the formed complex 
is measured at 660 nm. 
 
 
LABORATORY FACILITIES 
 
1. Maximum power consumption depending on the analyzer configuration, 2000 VA. Check voltage at the 
back of the instrument before installation. 
2. Facilities for chemical wastes. Check environmental regulations for proper disposal of waste. 
 
 
PROCEDURE SAMPLE PREPARATION 
 
Water no. 1.1.3 
 
Field of application 
 
Sample preparation for the determination of total alkalinity, aluminum, amino acids, ammonia, anionic 
surfactants (MBAS), bromide, calcium, C.O.D., chromium, conductivity, D.O.C., ethanol, hardness, 
magnesium, manganese, methanol, nitrate + nitrite, nitrite, nonionics, ortho-phosphate, potassium 
permanganate, potassium, total-N (UV digestion), protein, silicate, sodium, volatile acids and urea in water. 
 
Principle 
 
The sample is not preserved and analyzed as soon as possible, at least within 24 hours after collection. 
Store the sample at 4°C till analysis. 
 
Note: The sample preparation in this method is only meant to be an example procedure. It requires 
validation on its correct procedure for all types of samples to be analyzed, which is the responsibility of the 
user.

12. Degassing procedures; 
a) Good: degas the water with a helium gas flow of approximately 20 liter/hour with aid of a sparer. Degas 
5 liter water for 15 minutes. This procedure removes all dissolved air or, degas the water by boiling the 
water for 10 minutes. Cool down to room temperature before usage. 
b) Medium: degas the water with a nitrogen gas flow of approximately 20 liter/hour with aid of sparer. 
Degas 5 liter water for 15 minutes. This procedure will remove most of the dissolved gasses. 
c) Bad: degas the water by ultrasonic waves. This procedure will remove a small part of the dissolved 
gasses.
