Abstract
This project entails the development of a Building Performance Simulation (BPS) tool, targeted at
architects and usage in the early design stage, when evaluating volume massing designs. This tool
is relevant for architects in order to be able to design buildings with better indoor thermal comfort,
operational energy eﬃciency and daylight accessibility.
The BPS tools is documented and developed based on feedback from Schmidt Hammer Lassen
architects (SHL). It is found that the requirements put forward by SHL are in alignment with those
found in the literature.
The developed BPS tool excels compared to other open-source tools, by being fast, running the
validated Accelerad GPU based raytracing engine. Furthermore, the developed BPS tool is targeted
at the volume massing activity, hence making it easier to use, by requiring a minimum of user inputs,
compared to other more general BPS tools.
The developed BPS tool is tested and compared to another open-source validated BPS tool. The
comparison shows alignment between results and diﬀerences are elaborated.
Other tests are also
conducted, highlighting interesting aspects and considerations of the implementation.
Lastly, ideas for future development are presented.

A brief review on the history of the built environment
Before diving deeper into the topic of this thesis it is relevant to understand the context of this
project, which is the built environment and its history.
Starting with the why, homo sapiens have throughout history sought to shelter themselves
from changing weather conditions, arguably to reach a better thermal equilibrium with the
surroundings and not to expend more energy than needed on neither heating or cooling their
bodies. Moreover, the built environment have also served as a shelter for more extreme weather
phenomena, such as storms, cold winters and hot summers, hence increasing survivability. Dur-
ing more modern times, P. O. Fanger [Fan70] pioneered the search for quantifying the eﬀect of
climate on human thermal comfort. During his research this was achieved by subjecting individ-
uals to a variety of condition with diﬀerences in: Air temperature, mean radiant temperature,
relative humidity, air speed, metabolic rate and clothing insulation. Based on the diﬀerent con-
ditions, test subjects was then asked to identify how comfortable they were feeling. With this
empirical data, the Predicted Mean Vote/Percentage People Dissatisﬁed (PMV/PPD) model
was developed and have been adapted in standards and served as an inspiration for other
quantifying metrics on human comfort.
Having looked at the motivation behind the built environment and a model for quantifying
to which extend the built environment delivers conditions for thermal comfort, this leads to the
questions of what to do to achieve this goal. Historically humans have manipulated natural
materials within their vicinity to do this. Without any access to advanced building technologies,
what is now known as vernacular architecture, is characterized by being very responsive to the
given climate in the fundamental design of a building. An example of this approach, as seen
from ﬁgure 1, is that buildings from the colder Nordic regions have a high level of insulation
and a minimum of openings such as doors and windows. On the opposite hand vernacular
architecture from the Mediterranean warmer climates, display lower levels of insulation and
many openings to allow natural ventilation throughout the building. Given that vernacular
architecture are hypothesised to have been created through a process of trial and error, it is
very diverse all around the world. Furthermore, it is not only responding to climate, but also
other factors such as available natural materials and cultural diﬀerences.
(a) Norwegian vernacular architecture
(b) Greek vernacular architecture
Figure 1: Examples of vernacular architecture from diﬀerent climate regions. Source: wiki
Given the rich history of vernacular architecture all around the world, interestingly with
globalization and advancements in building technologies, styles of architecture have become ever
more mixed. Advancements in Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning (HVAC) systems as
well as global access to more structurally advanced building materials have given unprecedented
degrees of freedom when designing buildings. In a lot of ways, skyscrapers, as can be seen in
ﬁgure 2, are a testament to humans unprecedented ability to manipulate natural materials to
create buildings.

Figure 2: Examples of skyscrapers from around the world. Notice that despite the diverse climates

At a glance it seems great that humans are becoming better at meeting their desire of
thermal comfort and having more freedom than ever when designing how to do so. But it is
worth noting that there has been some unintended consequences following the evolution of the
built environment. In order to fulﬁl the motivation of meeting human thermal comfort, building
envelopes are designed and produced with an increasing degree of air tightness and thermal
insulation. This makes sense when it is desired to achieve comfortable thermal indoor climate
while minimizing the energy usage during the operation of the building, however this also
makes buildings more susceptible to problems. An examples of this are the skyscrapes shown
by ﬁgure 2. If they are not properly serviced by HVAC systems they become inhabitable due
to: Temperatures being too hot or too cold, since the largely glazed facades has similar eﬀects
to that of a greenhouse, levels of carbon dioxide from occupants and other indoor pollutants,
such as volatile organic compounds, being too high. Moreover, given that humans spend an
increasing amount of time in buildings, up to 87 % of their time found in a study on average
Americans [Kle+01], negative impacts on physical and mental health are becoming pronounced.
Factors contributing to this include but are not limited to: Access to daylight, views to the
outdoor, limited physical activity and the manipulation of our circadian rhythm due to the
exposure of artiﬁcial lighting. Lastly it should be noted that the built environment, during
its life cycle of manufacturing of materials, assembly/construction, operation and demolition,
is accounting for up to 40 % of energy related CO2 emissions [Env17]. Hence in summary,
the creation of the built environment have become a complex multi-objective design problem
balancing: Thermal indoor comfort, physical and mental comfort, energy usage, other pollution
factors and artistic endeavours.
These are all aspects to keep in mind when designing and constructing buildings which leads
to the who. Today it is custom to segregate the diﬀerent aspects of design and construction
into specialized disciplines. This is so due to the large complexity of modern buildings, where
historically the same persons could fulﬁl multiple roles. Today, generally speaking, building
disciplines can be categorized into contractors and consultants. Contractors covering all things
related to craftsmanship, manufacturing and construction such as carpentry, bricklaying, met-
alworking, painting, etc. And consultants covering engineers and architects. Given the scope
of this project the focus will be on the role of the consultants in the building process i.e. engi-
3
==PAGE:13==
neers and architects. The collaboration between engineers and architects during the design of
a building can be organized in indeﬁnitely many ways. Each discipline have their strength and
weaknesses and a great outcome happens when architects and engineers achieve a good collab-
oration [Nat67]. Moreover, the way the two disciplines choose to collaborate often depends on
the type of project. E.g. if the project is of very technical nature, such as a bridge or hospital
with many technical installations, the engineers tends to play a larger role. On the ﬂip side,
given projects of more artistic nature or any project with fewer technical requirements, the ar-
chitects tends to play a larger role. Another factor is the timeline of the design process which is
an important aspect of how the disciplines collaborate. From the experience of SHL it is found
that a large part of their building projects are organized by the architectural oﬃce submitting
their proposal for an architectural competition, drawing solely on their own in-house experience
and their own previous experience from working with engineers. This is done to keep cost low,
since a competition design proposal can be expensive to create and there is no guarantee that
the oﬃce will be selected for the project. In the case of a competition win, engineers will be
involved and help ﬁnalize the design with all technical aspects included. Another option is
that a client directly contacts SHL and asks for their help without conducting a competition.
However even in this case there is still a strong emphasize to keep cost low and hence only
involving engineers in the later stages of design.
A great example of a successful outcome from a collaboration process, following a com-
petition, is the creation of the Sydney Opera House. Danish architect Jørn Utzon won the

2.1
Building Performance Simulation
Building Performance Simulation (BPS) enables the prediction of the performance of a building
before it is built. Predictions can be made in terms of: Operational energy eﬃciency, daylight
accessibility, thermal comfort, etc. It is very relevant to have this type of information at hand
throughout all stages of design, in order to be able to navigate the multi-objective nature of
todays building design processes. However as described in [Hen10] by ”...the uptake of building
performance simulation in current building design practice is surprisingly limited. The actual
application is generally restricted to the ﬁnal phases in building design...”, there is a limited
uptake in the use of BPS tools. Moreover, the potential of using BPS to elevate the energy
eﬃciency of building is in [Cla01] described as ”... an untapped, clean energy resource of vast
potential”, given the notion that the energy that is not used, does not need to be produced. In
[Cla01] it is also noted that: ”It is a strange paradox that in the age of IT information is never
in the hands of those who need it to make informed decisions”. Given this untapped potential
it is sought to develop a useful BPS tool, to be used in the early design stages.
2.2
Barriers
To achieve this goal of developing BPS tool, to be used in the early design stages, it is important
to acknowledge that there exists barriers for the uptake of BPS tools in the early design stage.
Some of these barriers have been identiﬁed in [PP20b] as:
 Engineering performance issues regarding indoor climate is basically considered irrelevant
in relation to form-giving in early design phases.
 A concern that (measurable) engineering performance issues may overrule (“nonmeasur-
able”) architectural design issues.
 Mindset and workﬂow mismatch between architects and engineers when designing.
 A desire to use simulation tools for design but current ones are insuﬃcient and inappro-
priate.
 Limited economy allocated to the early design phase (e.g. in competitions) is a barrier to
close collaboration between architects and engineers.

discussed previously in section 1 and as can be extrapolated from the outlined barriers, the

such as the architects being constrained in their design by the engineers feedback on how to

BPS tools, this is also a diﬀerentiator of this project.

this is possible given the constraints of the selected validated engines.

its core built on the ISO 13790 standard hourly method [13708], with additional functional-

calculated using Accelerad. To limit the development time it is chosen to leave out the more
advanced features of modelling HVAC systems, hence the features of ICEbear implemented in
this project is equivalent to those described in ISO13790.

pled and fast solution for this. However in order to comply with the requirement of a BPS tool
to have a front-end which can operated without any expert knowledge about the underlying
engines, front-end prototypes will also be developed in this project. These front-end prototypes
will be developed for the CAD program Rhino-Grasshopper, which is the most commonly used
CAD program by architects all around the world, for the early design stage, based on the
experience of SHL.

 Validated, by using validated engines and methods.
 Fast, using hardware acceleration (GPU raytracing) and the simpliﬁed ISO13790 hourly

Lighting simulation using Radiance
In this chapter it is sought to investigate how Radiance fundamentally conducts lighting simula-
tion. This is motivated by the fact that Accelerad is modelled after Radiance, so understanding
how Radiance works, translates to understanding how Accelerad works. Lastly there is a large
body of research and technical documentation regarding the workings of Radiance which is not
currently matched by Accelerad.
As described in [LS98] some of the main capabilities with regards to the accuracy of Radiance
is:
 Physically based reﬂectance models with the possibility to model specular highlights.
 Accurately modelling indirect illumination using irradiance caching, instead of a constant
ambient term.
It is worth noting that at the time of initial development these were non trivial tasks, which
gave rise to Radiance popularity in the building industry. Furthermore for better usability and
higher uptake among professionals the following requirements in the development of Radiance
have been meet:
 The ability to model both electric light and daylight.
 Mitigating memory requirements when rendering scenes, by using raytracing together with
a spatial tree data structure - octree.
 Being able to take inputs from a variety of CAD programs.
At the end of the chapter a small comparison is made to an commonly used alternative
method, which is also used for lighting simulation - the radiosity method.

5.2
Direct and specular light
The use of deterministic sampling in the Monte Carlo integration becomes relevant for very
signiﬁcant/bright light sources, particularly when they are small, such as the sun when observed
from earth. For instance using uniform stochastic sampling, in a sunlit scene, will require a very
high number of samples to reduce the variance of the Monte Carlo integration to a suﬃcient
tolerance. Or to put it diﬀerently, when rays are spawned in random directions, it will require
a lot of rays to ﬁnd a small light source. To overcome this Radiance uses deterministically
sampled shadow rays toward user speciﬁed ”signiﬁcant” light sources in a scene. This is done
by specifying the light source as a ”light” primitive. A direct light contribution can be viewed
as a peak in the Li (θi, φi) factor, in terms of the rendering equation.
Specular/directional light reﬂections are also handled separately in Radiance, since they also
introduce a high variance if sampled solely stochastically. These contribution can be viewed as
peaks in the ρbd (θi, φi; θr, φr) factor, in terms of the rendering equation. Many materials deﬁ-
nitions exists in Radiance that display specular components (e.g ”plastic”, ”metal”, ”mirror”,
depending on the parameter settings of the material deﬁnition), all of which are sampled using a
hybrid deterministic/stochastic process as described in [War92]. That is deterministically sam-
pled rays at the peak specular contribution and stochastically sampled rays for semi-specular
contribution around the peak in order to sample the entire highlight.
5.3
Indirect light
Some of the main innovation during the development of Radiance, is the handling of global illu-
mination/diﬀuse interreﬂections that stands apart from the standard methods at the time. Con-
ventional tools made use of a constant ”ambient” term to replace the missing energy [War94].
This is an computationally eﬃcient approach, however unable to produce accurate results in

5.5
Comparison to the radiosity method
For comparison, another approach which is commonly used to conduct lighting simulations
is the radiosity method. As described in [TSE19], the radiosity method is used in software
packages such as: Relux, DIALux and AGi32. Fundamentally radiosity can be viewed as a
discretization approach to solve the rendering equation, similar to the Finite Element Method

6
Daylight simulation using Radiance
Based on the learnings of how Radiance fundamentally conducts lighting simulations, it is in
this chapter is sought to investigate how this is applied to the case of daylight simulations in
buildings.
6.1
Climate Based Daylight Modelling
Pioneered by John Mardaljevic during his PhD work [Mar00], Climate Based Daylight Mod-
elling (CBDM) seeks to accurately predict daylight accessibility within the built environment.
For his PhD work John Mardaljevic chose Radiance to conduct the daylight simulations, lever-
aging the accuracy of the indirect lighting calculation in Radiance.
The underlying premise of CBDM is that the daylight accessibility within a room can be
subdivided into the light intensity of the sky (treating the sky as a light source) and the
geometry plus materials of the scene.
Similarly to energy modelling within the built environment, CBDM usually calculates hourly
values of daylight through an entire year. This is done to account for the eﬀects of all four
meteorological seasons as well as daily cycles.
While it is possible create renderings using CBDM, in this project CBDM will be used for
quantifying illuminance (and irradiance) values in sensor points. The senspor points will be
deﬁned by a location (x,y,z) and a direction vector. This behaviour mimics a light sensor.
6.2
Sky models
As the name Climate Based Daylight Modelling suggests, the daylight modelling is based on
the given climate of the scene location. This is achieved by using statistical or synthetic climate
data for the given location which is then used as a basis to formalize the radiance or luminance
of the sky using ”sky models”.
Several approach exists for collection such data of varying
degrees of ﬁdelity [LS98]:
 Basic: Hourly measurements of global horizontal irradiance and diﬀuse horizontal irradi-
ance.
 Intermidiate: Hourly measurements of illuminance both horizontal and normal to the
direction of the sun.
 Advanced: Measurements of the actual sky luminance distribution using a sky-scanning
device.
The predominant method used for create a representation of the sky distribution in CBDM
is to use the Perez all weather sky model based on 8760 hourly measurements (one whole year)
of direct normal irradiance and diﬀuse horizontal irradiance. However in order to get a historic
perspective and understand the context, it is worth mentioning some of the CIE Standard skies.
Also the CIE standard skies are still usefull if high ﬁdelity data, such as actual sky luminance
from a sky-scanning device, exists. It is acknowledges that other sky models have since been
developed, but the focus of this report will remain on the ones implemented in Radiance.

In order to be able to make prediction about a volume massing designs operational energy
performance, it is important to understand the mechanics of heat transfer. There exists three
primary mechanisms of heat transfer: Conduction, convection and radiation. As a starting
point, these phenomena will be considered steady state conditions.

7.2
Convection
Convective heat transfer is the transfer of energy at the interface between a solid and a ﬂuid,
governed by the combined eﬀects of conduction and ﬂuid motion [CCT17]. It can be categorized
as either forced convection, when the ﬂuid motion is driven by external forces such as a fan,
pump or the wind, and natural convection when the ﬂuid motion is driven by buoyancy forces.
Buoyancy forces occur when a part of the ﬂuid is heated, resulting in its rise due to lower
density (or fall of adjacent, colder, higher density ﬂuid).

8.1.3
Determining Heating-Cooling need
At this stage, the only part of the ISO 13790 method that has not been discussed is how to
determine the heating-cooling need φHC,nd. It is determined by ﬁrst computing the given air
temperature (θair) in a scenario where there is no heating nor cooling (φHC,nd = 0). If the
resulting air temperature is within the user deﬁned setpoints of desired indoor air temperature,
then no heating nor cooling will be needed during the given hour, also called free ﬂoating
conditions. However if the air temperature is above the cooling setpoint or the air temperature
is below the heating setpoint, then the needed heating or cooling will be interpolated to the
given setpoint boundary.

than the ISO 13790 method. In general EnergyPlus can be used in a variety of diﬀerent ways
based on the user inputs.

surface resistances (Rsi, Rse), but computes the eﬀects of convection and thermal radiation of
the surface of opaque building construction on an hourly basis.

9.1
Daylight
As described in [Sub17] there is a variety of diﬀerent ways to use Accelerad to quantify daylight
accessibility within a building. In this project, both the daylight coeﬃcient method (in the
literature sometimes referred to as the two-phase method) and the three-phase method are
used.
9.1.1
Sky model
Both the daylight coeﬃcient method and the three phase method requires a sky matrix (denoted
S in section 6), with values of sky radiance. This is achieved by generating a Tregenza dicretized
Perez all weather sky model using the following command-line argument:
1 c:\ radiance\bin\gendaymtx -O0 weather_data .wea > perez_sky_matrix .smx
In this way gendaymtx is used to generate three matrices of size 146-by-8760. Three matrices
to account for each RGB color channel, 146 rows as the 145 patches in the Tregenza discretized
sky plus one patch to account for the ground-plane and 8760 columns as all hours in a year.
The content of the matrices are luminance values weighted by the gendaymtx default sky color,
which is ”-c 0.960 1.004 1.118”.
This is so since the ”-O0” argument determines that the
radiance should only be in the visible spectrum, where ”-O1” can be used to output radiance
in the entire solar spectrum. ”weather data.wea” contains diﬀuse horizontal and direct normal
irradiance for all 8760 hours of the year, used as input for the Perez all weather sky model.
This weather data is typically extracted from an EnergyPlus weather ﬁle and a large database
of such formatted weather ﬁles can be found online EnergyPlus weather ﬁle database.
During the development of this project it was realized that since only illuminance and
irradiance values are needed, it is suﬃcient to use one channel instead of three color channels.
This simpliﬁcation is achieved by using the input argument ”-c 1 1 1”, which enables the use of
only one of the three matrices. This reduce the size of both S and Cdc to a third and eliminates
the need for rmtxop. rmtxop is used for performing matrix transformations, such as combining
three RGB matrices into a single illuminance matrix.
9.1.2

Final version
Based on the feedback during the workshop, when presenting the minimum viable product, this
information was used as a driver for the development of the ﬁnal version. The major takeaway
from the workshop was to disregard any notion of inserting rooms and work with ﬂoor plates
spanning the entire volume massing interior.
Since the three phase method is developed for evaluating rooms with only one window, there
exists no computationally eﬀecient implementation of the three phase method on rooms with
multiple windows. It is possible to iterate all windows one by one, but this is computationally
ineﬃcient as will be shown in section 15.
For this reason the daylight coeﬃcient method is selected in the ﬁnal version implementation.
The daylight coeﬃcient method is then used both for the daylight study, with sensorpoints 0.85
meters above the ﬂoor plate. And to quantify solar radiation entering the room by placing
sensor points right behind each window. The incoming solar radiation is then piped to the
whole building energy simulation implementation of ISO 13790.
The entire ﬁnal version is implemented as a Python-C++ Visual Studio solution which can
be found here: github link
Moreover it is worth noting that only one color channel is used and matrix multiplication
is implemented in numpy and cupy.

Convergence analysis of Radiance parameters
When using Radiance or Accelerad it is up to the user to establish the appropriate settings for
the ambient calculation. For instance what should the number of ambient bounces and ambient
division be, to reduce the variance of the solution to the rendering equation to a tolerable
level. Andy McNeil [McN+13] [ML12] highlights in the following blog post an approach he has
used to perform convergence analysis of the three-phase method on the BRE-IDMP dataset
[Mar01]. The BRE-IDMP was the dataset also used in John Mardaljevic research [Mar00]. In
the blogpost it is highlighted that the most important parameters in a rﬂuxmtx calculated is
the parameters:
 ”-ab”, ambient bounces, accounting for how many interreﬂections to account for, for each
rays
 ”-ad”, ambient division, setting the number of hemispherically sampled rays at each in-
tersection.
 ”-lw”, limit weight, deﬁning a lower limit for the weight/payload of the ray. If the weight/-
payload of a ray is lower than ”-lw” the ray is terminated.
As established here Radiance workshop, rcontrib introduction, Greg Ward on page 29, the
limit weight parameter should be set as
1
ad or lower. Furthermore, based on his convergence
testing on the BRE-IDMP dataset Andy McNeil suggest testing the following settings of the
three parameters:
 ”-ab” of, 6, 8, 10, 12
 ”-ad” of, 45, 46, 47, 48, 49
 ”lw power” of, 1, 1.5, 2, 2.5, 3, meaning -lw =
1
-adlw power
These setting are used for a convergence testing of the VMT. The testing is conducted
by computing two runs with rﬂuxmtx on the same scene with the same settings and then
looking at the variance between the two identical runs. Since the ray, scene-traversal is longer
for the daylight analysis, due to the rays starting at the ﬂoor plane and not just behind the
window pane these points are used. The rendering variance is quantiﬁed based on the daylight
coeﬃcients using the r2 score as can be found in table 7. Since there is 4096 sensor points in
the scene, the variance calculation is based on a comparison of 4096 x 146 = 598,016 daylight
coeﬃcients. Together with computing r2 scores, rﬂuxmtx wall times are also tracked in order
to be able to balance accuracy with speed. The average wall times are computed as an average
of the two runs with the same ambient parameter setting and can be found in table 8.

A crucial part of developing a new BPS tool is ensuring its accuracy. Even though VMT runs
on the validated engine Accelerad and uses the well established ISO13790 method, there is
always the possibility that data is piped or formatted wrongly. For this reason it is desired to
perform a comparison between VMT versus EnergyPlus and Radiance. Some diﬀerences are
to be expected, since there is diﬀerence in the algorithms used by the engines. However these
diﬀerences should be understood and elaborated. Ladybug Tools is used for convenience as a in-
terface for EnergyPlus and Radiance. For comparison a simple room geometry is implemented,
in order to minimize the parameters in the modelling. This geometry model is orientated as

18
Discussion
Having presented the results generated during this project, next it is relevant to discuss some
of the aspects involved with the development of VMT.
Covid19 has presented some challenges for making the user involvement aspect a larger part
of the development process. It is hard to estimate the eﬀects of having the end user feel like they
are a part of the development process. Moreover, casual talks at the coﬀee machine, where ideas
and feedback might spontaneously arise, has been limited. That being said the feedback that
was provided during workshops and through questionnaires has been very helpful. Moreover,
the feedback that was provided from the workshop questionnaire also showed good alignment
with the barrier identiﬁed in section 2.2.
The attempt of making a BPS tool that relies only on one quantiﬁcation of the ﬂux from
the sky model to the windows/facades for both the illuminance and irradiance calculations was
unsuccessful. This is so since it was chosen to pursue the daylight coeﬃcient method over the
three phase method, due to its vast speed diﬀerence as showed in section 15. The daylight
coeﬃcient method needs to perform two separate calculations for illuminance and irradiance
since the average transmittance of the window is diﬀerent in the two cases. It is still possible
achieve this single estimation of ﬂux, for both illuminance and irradiance estimation, using the
three phase method. But since the main motivation behind during so is speed, it does not
make sence to pursue this option further, given the current implementation of the three phase
method in Radiance/Accelerad.
One upside from using the three phase method is the possibility of using BSDF ﬁles of the
fenestration system, with accurate optical angular properties for the given window system. As
showed in section 13 this could help with Accelerad providing more accurate predictions of the
solar heat entering the building used by ISO 13790.
In terms of the desired maximum computation time of the VMT, on larger models such as
the one shown in section 17, there is still room for improvement on the computational speed of
the back-end. Another aspect of computational speed which is not benchmarked in this report
is the geometry, window and sensor point preparation time by the front-end. For smaller models
this time is negligible, but for larger models it can become signiﬁcant.
19
Conclusion
In this project there has been a successful documentation and development of a BPS tool
targeted at the volume massing design stage. The BPS tool has been developed in collaboration
with Schmidt Hammer Lassen Architects and it was found that their requirements to such a
tool align well with the ones found in the literature.
The tool overcomes the barriers in the uptake of such a tool, identiﬁed in the literature by
being: Open-source free to use, easy to use by having a prototype front-end that requires a
minimum user input, fast by running GPU based raytracing and reliable producing validated
results.
Several tests of the developed BPS tool has been conducted, including a comparison to
another validated commonly used BPS tools - Ladybug Tools. Good alignment between results
has been shown and sources contributing to diﬀerences has been identiﬁed. It has been shown
it is possible to outperform the Radiance functions dctimestep and rmtxop, by using the BLAS
and cuBLAS matrix multiplication libraries.
Lastly it has been shown how the BPS tool can be applied to a case study from Schmidt
Hammer Lassen Architects.

20
Future work
The development process has shed light on several ideas for future work on the development
for BPS tool targeted at the volume massing design activity.
First and foremost it is recommended to look into developing a GPU based raytracing engine
targeted at the volume massing design activity. The speciﬁcation for such an engine is:
 Keep all data on GPU memory, e.g. don’t pipe raytracing output to standard output, but
keep it in GPU memory for subsequent matrix multiplication.
 GPU based (like Accelerad) progressively updated (like ClimateStudio) raytracing.
 The output should be based on the three phase method, to allow BSDF ﬁles to be evaluated
and only to perform the ﬂux calculation from sky to windows/fa¸cades once.
 Only lambertian surfaces needed.
 Based on Optix-CUDA. OpenGL also an option.
Given that only lambertian surfaces is needed for this BPS tool it could also be interesting
to investigate the possibility of using a radiosity based formulation. Moreover, since irradiance
caching is not currently used in the Radiance/Accelerad based matrix methods it could be
interesting to investigate if there is a potential speed improvement from using ”irradiance
caching” for ray-contribution coeﬃcients values. This has previously been done in DAYSIM.
With this raytracing/radiosity based engine in hand the following things should be consid-
ered:
 The use of accurate solar position and investigate what eﬀect this has on accuracy vs
speed.
 Consider a sparse matrix format for very specular BSDF ﬁles. Also avoid computing any
night hours, i.e. where the Perez all weather sky model is zero for all patches.
 Speed of the front-end matters, so consider making a C++ Rhino-Grasshopper front-end,
which would minimize the need for data transfer between front-end and back-end.
Given the continuous development of hardware and that graphics cards excel at parallelizable
tasks, such as ray-tracing and matrix multiplication, with the right hardware and software it
should be possible at some point to conduct real-time BPS of volume massing designs.
