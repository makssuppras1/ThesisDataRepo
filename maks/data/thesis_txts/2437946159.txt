Abstract 
 
In this project, a feedback system that motivates seniors to stay physically active by measuring 
their activity levels is developed through Co-Creation and tested with end-users. Projections from 
studies show a rapid growing proportion of seniors in developed countries. These changes create 
a challenge for society, since it becomes more difficult to allocate sufficient resources to support 
the growing amount of seniors. One solution to mitigate the growing strain on society is to focus 
on healthy aging and facilitate independent living. Current solutions do not focus on seniors’ 
needs and many of them do not have a preventive approach to the underlying problem. This is 
where the development of a new feedback system for seniors rises to the occasion. A great focus 
for this project will be to involve seniors in the development and to utilize Co-Creation as the 
main theory to identify user needs and evaluate the solution. A draft implementation plan is 
developed in collaboration with Tryg as a case study in order to explore business possibilities to 
implement the idea. Eventually, a testing of the functional prototype is conducted to evaluate its 
effectiveness and there are significant indications that the developed solution has a potential to 
be successful. Finally, empirical reflections about involving seniors in the design process are 
discussed.

1. Introduction 
 
The purpose of this project is to apply and evaluate Participatory Design to develop a 
feedback system for older people that effectively motivates them to be more physically 
active. The project is an adjunct to and supported by an EU project called REACH [1], which 
is an initiative to improve senior citizens’ general health state and reduce the risk of loss of 
function and associated illnesses. 
 
Developed countries, including Denmark, are experiencing a rapid growth in the proportion 
of older people. According to World Population Ageing report from UN, one of eight persons 
worldwide were aged 60 or above in 2017. Their projections show that in 2030, the 
proportion of seniors will increase to one out of six persons (United Nations, 2015). 
 
As seniors’ proportion in relation to younger people increases, there will be a challenge for 
society to allocate sufficient resources to support seniors. These rapid changes can translate 
into implications for nearly all sectors of society, one of them being the health sector. 
Specifically, hospitalization and healthcare expenses are continuously growing [4]. 
 
An important approach to meet this challenge is supporting seniors in maintaining 
independent living, by improving their ability, mentally and physically. In this way, they can 
support themselves in their own homes with as little assistance as possible. Physical activity 
is one of the most scientifically proven approaches to achieve independent living. Studies 
show that seniors who do regular physical activity have reduced loss of many physiological 
functions and a lower risk of developing chronic diseases (Taylor, 2014), which are closely 
connected to the ability to remain independent. Governments and policy-makers already 
focus on the need for innovative solutions targeting seniors in order to ensure healthy aging 
through physical activity and lower expenses of costly dependent living. However, current 
technological solutions promoting healthy living fail to address seniors’ needs, because they 
are designed for younger active people. Thus, they cannot motivate them adequately to be 
physically active. Since seniors are usually overlooked when designing this type of solutions, 
this project will focus on involving them in the design process of a new feedback system 
through Participatory Design.  
 
Here is an overview of the report structure. In Chapter 2, we will present this project’s 
Problem Background and in Chapter 3, there will be a research on the current solutions for 
seniors’ independent living and physical activity promotion. Chapter 4 will summarize the first 
two chapters in scoping a problem statement and will conclude by documenting the 
Research Questions of this project. In Chapter 5 there will be a literature review for the main 
theories used in the project and how they were applied. Chapter 6 contains the whole 
Research Design part, which presents the overall strategy of conducting this study in a 
coherent and logical way. It is divided in Design Approaches and the actual Design Process. 
In Chapter 7, a sketch of a possible implementation for the designed solution will be reported 
through the use of a case study. Chapter 8 will summarize how the Research Questions are 
answered and Chapter 9 is a discussion that suggests promising subjects for further 
exploration based on the findings and experiences of this project. Finally, Chapter 10 will 
contain the report’s Conclusion.

2. Problem Background 
 
World Health Organization (WHO) reports that about 3.2 million deaths per year are 
attributed to physical inactivity. This makes physical inactivity the fourth leading risk factor for 
deaths globally (6% of deaths). Looking into the top five risk factors for mortality, physical 
inactivity is closely connected to three other leading risk factors on the list: high blood 
pressure, blood glucose levels and obesity (Who, 2010). 
 
Many studies show strong scientific evidence that people, including older adults, who do 
regular physical activity have lower rates of all-cause mortality and reduced risk of 
developing chronic diseases (Taylor, 2014). For example, a large comprehensive study of 
older American women of at least 65 years old showed that women with high levels of 
physical activity had a 40-50% lower mortality rate from all causes, including cardiovascular 
disease and cancer, than sedentary women. The study also revealed that increased physical 
activity appears to provide fewer benefits for those older than 75, suggesting that early 
lifestyle changes can be of significant matter (Gregg, 2003). 
 
The importance of regular physical activity for older adults is not only about reducing 
mortality, but also about improving their quality of life. Many physiological functions, such as 
balance, endurance, muscle strength and cognitive performance decline with age (Taylor, 
2014). Functional loss decreases the ability to perform daily functions such as washing 
oneself, eating and drinking. All of those impact independent living, which is a key factor for 
quality of life. Independent living is defined as the ability to direct one’s own life with the 
same productivity and freedom of choice as a non-disabled person [2]. 
 
 
The shift from being independent to being dependent on others is a big deal to most seniors 
and maintaining functional independency is among the highest priorities for them [Appendix 
A]. Keeping seniors independent is critical, both to maintain the quality of life and to reduce 
health resources. A collection of 66 studies has shown that muscle strength and aerobic 
activities have positive impact on functional independence for older adults. The findings 
indicate that regular physical activity with sufficient intensity reduce the risk of functional 
limitations and disability of older adults by 50% (Paterson & Warburton, 2010). 
 
The increased awareness from research has led governments to recognize the benefits of 
physical activity and a production of global and national guidelines for physical activity. The 
guidelines suggest that older adults should do at least 30 minutes of moderate activity or 15 
minutes with vigorous intensity for five days a week. Additionally, it is recommended to do 
two sessions of muscle-strengthening activity per week (Ministry of Health, 2013). 
 
Although physical activity is one of the most important factors for independent living and 
longevity, the majority of older adults doesn’t get the recommended activity levels of 
moderate-vigorous activity. A survey in England shows that only 20% of men and 17% of 
women aged 65-74 is achieving the recommended physical activity levels set by WHO 
(Townsend et al., 2012). 
 
The number of older adults has been growing in a rapid rate the last decades, due to 
medical science and more focus on healthy living. According to the World Population Ageing 
report from UN, the world population of seniors aged 60+ has increased by 48% since year 
2000, and is projected to have doubled by 2025, while continuing to raise the next couple of 
decades, as seen in Figure 1 below.

3. Current Solutions 
 
Since physical activity is a cornerstone of healthy aging, a lot of healthcare providers are 
continuously looking for solutions and tools to help seniors become more physically active. 
There is a variety of existing solutions that already focus on supporting elders, such as EU 
funded project initiatives, nursing homes and activity centers. This section introduces some 
of the solutions and approaches that currently exist for healthy aging. 
3.1. Healthy Aging Projects  
 
There are several projects from an EU, national and organizational plan, that deal with active 
and healthy ageing for senior citizens. Following projects can be mentioned: 
3.1.1. EIP on AHA project 
The European Innovation Partnership on Active and Healthy Ageing (EIP on AHA) initiative 
aims to bring all relevant actors from a European, national and regional level across different 
policy areas to focus on a specific societal challenge. This initiative has a goal to improve the 
health and quality of life of European seniors, supporting the long-term sustainability and 
efficiency of healthcare systems. They also promote scaling up of innovative digital solutions 
for active and healthy aging, as well as monitoring their impact on healthcare [5]. 
3.1.2. The REACH project 
 
REACH is an EU project with 18 partners, including universities and companies, that aims to 
develop a service system that will turn clinical and care environments into personalized 
modular sensing systems that encourage the elderlies to become healthy via activity. The 
Danish partners are Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality, DTU and the University of Copenhagen. 
REACH stands for Responsive Engagement of the Elderly Promoting Activity and 
Customized Healthcare. REACH is an initiative to improve senior citizens’ general health 
state and reduce illnesses that cause loss of function. The improved health state can be 
achieved through physical activity, healthier food and regular fluid intake. REACH targets to 
develop personalized health solutions through wearable devices and sensor technology that 
are able to monitor seniors’ general health. The collection of data can be used as feedback 
to motivate seniors and make professionals identify health problems before they become 
severe. A healthier lifestyle and an early intervention by REACH solutions can lead to less 
time spent in long-term care facilities and ultimately reduce hospitalization expenses 
(REACH2020.eu). 
3.1.3. National Institute on Aging 
 
National Institute on Aging (NIA) is an American federal agency under the U.S. Department 
of Health and Human Services. It leads a broad scientific effort to understand the nature of 
aging and to extend the active and healthy years of life. Moreover, it supports and conducts 
Alzheimer's disease research [6]. One of their campaigns is Go4Life, which is designed to 
help older adults fit exercise and physical activity into their daily life. It offers exercises, 
motivational tips, and free resources to motivate senior citizens to prepare for, start or 
continue doing physical activity [7]. 
 
 
==PAGE:14==
6 
 
3.1.4. Royal Phillips 
 
Royal Phillips is a company focusing on improving people’s lives through innovations in the 
healthcare sector. They have created more than 450 products and services having a goal to 
create solutions that deliver better care to more people at lower cost [8]. They are also a part 
of the REACH project described earlier, to help co-create new innovations through 
stakeholders and end users. One of their research-based platforms is the Future Health 
Index (FHI). It is a platform which addresses global health challenge because of the 
combination of growing and aging populations with the rise of chronic diseases and 
healthcare costs. One of their projects is the implementation of connected home care in 
order to monitor aging populations at home and not in hospitals [9]. 
3.2. Danish elderly healthcare sector 
 
In Denmark, municipalities are responsible for the care of seniors. They support seniors to 
manage their daily life by providing a Social and Healthcare Assistant (SOSU) to assist them 
with daily tasks at home, and when seniors become too weak to live alone, a care center 
(plejecenter) is provided. One of the most used tools by SOSUs is the wheel toolkit Virker 
Hverdagen (Figure 3), developed by Region Hovedstaden E-learning. Virker Hverdagen is a 
concept, consisting of a digital learning material with real-life cases. The concept provides 
healthcare assistants and students with concrete tools and common understanding on 
observation, documentation and action in relation to the prevention and detection of early 
illness. The concept provides a “wheel tool” that contains subjects from the seniors’ daily life. 
It helps the staff to see changes in seniors and what are their patterns of movement. It is 
developed for the region of Zealand [10].  
 
Figure 3: Wheel tool from Virker Hverdagen 
 
The staff follows and observes how the seniors handle their own health, how they manage 
their level of functioning and what they usually do. Subsequently, the SOSUs and nurses 
meet 2-3 times per week to share knowledge and involve the seniors if a change has been 
addressed. The purpose of the meeting is to catch symptoms based on senior’s changed 
behavior before they become ill. An experienced SOSU in Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality, 
Miguel Opstrup, emphasizes that technological systems can be an essential player in their 
day of work: "Technology helps to support. It cannot take over. It cannot stand alone".  
==PAGE:15==
7 
 
 
Social and healthcare staff would like to see if the senior is getting down with a bad heart 
rate and how  the movement patterns look like as it can make their work more efficient 
[Appendix A].  
 
The healthcare system is not working in a preventive way, since it often provides help when 
it is too late and the patient’s health is already deteriorated. Furthermore, the evaluation tool 
does not reliably register patient’s physical activity in real time, as the wheel toolkit is 
designed for manual observation of behavior changes. 
3.3. Organizations & Activity centers 
 
Many organizations, like Ældresagen, offer consultation and organize events for seniors. 
Ældresagen is Denmark's second largest voluntary organization. They help seniors to stay 
active as they age, by offering a variety of activities including outdoors activities in natural 
areas, such as swimming and cycling [11]. In addition to that, there are activity centers, such 
as Rustenborghuset in Lyngby, that provide a wide variety of activities and courses [12]. 
Most of them are subscription-based and facilitate seniors that are already motivated to stay 
physically active. 
3.4. Technological solutions 
 
In recent years, there has been an increasing demand on developing seniors healthcare 
services utilizing novel technologies by helping them to be more physically active and 
providing independent living (Rasche et al., 2015). 
  
In this paragraph, we will distinguish the technological solutions in two categories and look 
into different examples for those. The systems that aim to help seniors be healthier can be 
either monitoring-based, feedback-based or a combination of those two. Monitoring systems 
aim to gather user data in order for third parties, such as SOSU assistance or family 
members, to keep users safe if a risk is detected. In the other category, the feedback 
systems provide an overview of data for users, in order for them to collect insights and have 
more control of their situation.  
3.4.1. Monitoring Systems 
 
One commonly used technology for monitoring systems is Internet of Things (IoT). IoT refers 
to a network of objects that can sense and exchange data in an internet infrastructure 
(Azimi, Rahmani, Liljeberg, & Tenhunen, 2017). The technology can provide a promising 
paradigm for monitoring in general and can include important services to detect older adult’s 
behavior patterns (Dohr, Modre-Osprian, Drobics, Hayn, & Schreier, 2010). 
 
One example of a product utilizing IoT technology is BeClose [13]. It is a wireless home 
monitoring system that connects caregivers to care receivers. It combines a various set of 
sensors at home, as well as a pendant that stays on user with an emergency button. It 
monitors senior’s movements and location patterns and sends alerts and notifications to 
senior’s family. For example, it can show when the senior wakes up, opens a door or leaves 
the house premises. There is no focus on physical activity and it is mostly designed for 
family members that want to check on their parents’ safety. 
==PAGE:16==
8 
 
3.4.2. Feedback Systems 
 
Wearable trackers are often used in feedback systems for self-monitoring. These trackers 
enable seniors to monitor their activity and the monitored data should be structured and 
representable for the user to control their activity and to motivate a healthier lifestyle. Current 
wearable trackers are mostly designed for younger users and it is unclear if they are suitable 
for older users (Clemes, Matchett, & Wane, 2008). 
 
One example is Fitbit. Fitbit has different versions of wearable watches that help the user to 
measure daily activity from an activity meter that can measure heart rate, sleep rhythm, 
steps and distance. This is done wirelessly and all data is stored to access it [14]. The watch 
is connected with a phone application, which is the main user interface for the user to 
receive feedback of stored data. Despite of the various wearable devices and applications of 
Fitbit, none of them focus on seniors needs. This fact is supported by a Fitbit employer, as 
he answers on the Fitbit forum about involvement of seniors: “Fitbit products are designed 
for how most people want to track their activity and exercise. This means that some use 
cases are not well served and we understand this tradeoff” [15]. This indicates that Fitbit are 
designed for younger generations, and thus neglect seniors’ needs.  
 
Figure 4: Fitbit HR Charge watches [16] 
 
An example of combination of monitoring and feedback system is the company GreatCall 
[17]. They offer a variety of different devices designed for elders such as wearable devices 
and easy-to-use cellphones, as well as offering various health and safety services. Their 
wearable device can track activity and detect falls. Other services include 24/7 personal 
support and medication reminders and consultation. They also provide remote monitoring 
technology to identify emerging health issues, here again the focus is on the senior’s family 
member to monitor them, neglecting the independence notion. One of the main 
disadvantages of the platform is that you cannot have the all the services with one device. In 
order to get the full benefits of it, the user needs to buy all different products, since every 
device only is designed for a single function.

3.5. Summing up 
 
As mentioned, there are various ways the healthcare system tries to help seniors being 
functional. Activity centers, care homes and assistance from SOSUs are some of them. 
These solutions do not focus enough on physical activity and its benefits. Furthermore, the 
healthcare system does not function as a preventive solution and uses immense amount of 
resources on frail persons whose quality of life has already started deteriorating.  
 
Despite the high demand for healthcare solutions that improve the senior’s quality of life, a 
lack of user-centered solutions to target the elderly segment still exists. As far as 
technological solutions are concerned, there are solutions orienting to physical activity, but 
not targeting seniors’ needs. Motivational elements are missing as well. The GreatCall 
company that have the seniors in mind, develop products that are not focusing on preventing 
health issues, but rather responding to emergencies by notifying family members, which also 
limit the seniors’ independence notion. Those reasons make current solutions less holistic 
and the technology difficult to be accepted and adopted by seniors.

4. Problem Statement 
 
As introduced in Chapter 2, Problem Background, there is a disproportionate growth of 
senior citizens in relation to younger citizens. This creates a challenge for society, as there 
will be a lack of supportive healthcare personnel in the future and the expenditures of social 
and health services will increase significantly. Physical activity is a solution that can prevent 
this situation. A person that is physically active have lower risks of a wide range of diseases 
and improve physiological functions that support independent living. Thus, improvements in 
physical activity can result in higher quality of life and less risks to be hospitalized, 
contributing to a healthier ageing society. 
 
The existing solutions that were explored in Chapter 3 seem to have various limitations to 
tackle the problem. The majority of existing solutions are working reactively, meaning that a 
person has to become weak or experience an emergency before they take effect. As far as 
technological solutions working as feedback systems are concerned, they mostly do not 
target senior needs. For this reason, the focus will be on developing a new feedback system 
that motivates seniors to be more physically active and is tailored around their needs.  
 
In order to achieve that, seniors will not just be the central subject of this project, but rather a 
collaborator in a partnership designing role. Also, there is a focus on the behavioral design 
and habit adoption aspect in order to achieve increased user motivation. Those two 
elements come in alignment with contemporary research trends (see Chapter 5). 
 
The target user group is defined as senior citizens of 65 years old and above. The users 
could be of any gender, educational background or social status. Basic technological literacy 
is required. We are targeting people who do not have mental or cognitive disabilities. In 
general, seniors that are too frail or weak to support themselves are excluded from the target 
group. We will focus within the Danish context, as the Danish healthcare sector is close 
integrated and its structure offers a fertile ground for implementing innovative digital 
solutions. Moreover, the trend of healthcare innovation in Denmark contributes as well in this 
opportunity.

5. Theoretical Background 
 
This section introduces the three main theoretical topics that will be the focus of the project. 
There is a short literature review and an elaboration on how and why they could be relevant 
to the project. The theories that are going to be examined are Participatory Design, 
Behavioral Design and the Technology Acceptance Model. 
5.1. Participatory Design 
 
Participatory Design puts the user as a collaborator in the center of the design process and 
focus on discovering tacit insights, as well as producing effective and user-driven design 
solutions. In this project, it will be one of the driving research factors, as the main 
methodology is based on co-creation workshops with potential users.  
 
Participatory Design (PD) has its origins in Scandinavia in the 1970’s, as a movement 
bringing together workers and academia in order to design new working environments 
(Spinuzzi, 2005). Although it had a political drive back then, the goal to democratize the 
design process by incorporating the relevant users in it remains until today. Actually, the 
user moves from just a “subject” status to a “partner” (E. B. N. Sanders & Stappers, 2008). 
The empowerment and the involvement of users provides benefits, such as a thorough, 
critical reflection that can turn up tacit knowledge (Spinuzzi, 2005). 
 
In the mid of 2000’s, two new terms emerged to describe what PD is - Co-Creation and Co-
Design. Although those two are usually used as synonyms from design practitioners, there is 
a distinction in literature (Mattelmäki & Sleeswijk Visser, 2011). Co-design refers to the 
collective creativity in the design process (E. B. N. Sanders & Stappers, 2008) by 
empowering future users and stakeholders to explore, envision and develop a design 
solution via specific tools and methods. On the other hand, Co-Creation has been identified 
in two ways from researchers. One would be the general notion of collective creativity, which 
can be applied outside a design process as well, such as product naming (E. B. N. Sanders 
& Stappers, 2008). The second approach puts Co-Creation inside Co-Design as a method 
where the users are involved specifically for creating solutions (Mattelmäki & Sleeswijk 
Visser, 2011), for example when users sketch or prototype solutions in collaboration with 
designers. 
 
There is a variety of ways for the user to participate and collaborate with the 
designer/researcher. The most well-known are workshops, focus groups, interviews, 
organizational games and prototyping sessions (Spinuzzi, 2005).  
 
Workshops are one of the main tools in PD. They are used for co-design sessions and other 
collaborative events where participants work to explore a topic and create common 
suggestions (Binder & Brandt, 2008). The aim of workshops is to bring together relevant 
stakeholders of the subject under study, to collaborate on designing solutions for a specific 
challenge. 
 
There are plenty of methods, tools and approaches for how to structure and facilitate a 
workshop. The general approach is to divide the workshop into three stages (CIPTEC, 2016) 
with different types of exercises for each stage. The first stage is about creating comfort 
among the participants in order to loosen up the atmosphere and make everyone involved 
and comfortable sharing their thoughts. The second stage is about staging discussion to 
==PAGE:20==
12 
 
make participants more immersive and knowledgeable about the subject before entering the 
third stage, which is about co-developing solutions. An initial opening stage, where the 
workshop and its goals are introduced, and a closing stage, where the workshop is 
concluded and evaluated, should be included. In order to build a workshop different 
alternatives exist. Major research has been made for probes and generative tools 
(Mattelmäki, 2008; E. B. N.Sanders, 2000; E. B. N. Sanders & Stappers, 2014) to elicit 
insights and explore user needs. Prototypes and tangible mock-ups bring something 
physical on the discussion table, thus, facilitating collaboration between participants (Brandt, 
2007; Foverskov & Binder, 2011; E. B. N. Sanders & Stappers, 2014). Another relevant 
activity, that could create design dialogues and reveal tacit knowledge and relationships, is 
design games (Brandt, 2006; Brandt, Messeter, & Binder, 2008). 
 
PD can be implemented to different sectors, especially where different stakeholders take 
part in the operation of this sector. Organizations and business innovation is usually a driver 
(Buur et al., 2013), but also public sector and specifically healthcare system (Nishimura et 
al., 2013) have been in the center of interest for PD in the last years, since various actors 
exchange value in those networks (patients, doctors, hospitals, society, government etc.).  
 
However, when it comes to seniors in specific, co-creation has lately started to generate new 
studies. One of the large-scale projects, REACH Horizon2020 (REACH2020, 2016a; Valk et 
al., 2018), deals with the acceptance of new technologies from senior citizens and provide 
interesting insights. Moreover, smaller projects conclusions (Massimi & Baecker, 2006; 
Scandurra & Sjölinder, 2013) can contribute in this project’s co-creation approach with 
seniors, such as that meeting with the seniors continuously over time and engaging them in 
different activities helped them articulate their needs better. Because of that, the design 
team believes that co-creation can be a helpful tool to collect user insights from seniors. 
 
5.2. Behavioral Design 
 
Physical activity and exercises, such as walking or weight lifting, have benefits that manifest 
when they are done usually. This means that they need to become adopted habits. 
Behavioral Design is a field with great relevance when Designers want to enhance or 
introduce a new behavior. In this project, it is used as a practical analytical tool for designing 
solutions and analyzing research findings.  
 
Behavioral Design is a design research field and application that aims to change human 
behavior. It draws its notions from sciences, such as psychology, economics and cognitive 
neuroscience, in order to produce solutions that successfully and ethically change a specific 
behavior. The human behavior usually consists of habits that happen without intention and 
are difficult to break (Neal, Wood, & Quinn, 2006). This is why observations in the user’s 
environment are suggested to be a better method to study a design problem, in comparison 
to an interview where the interviewee will try to consciously answer questions about things 
that are done unconsciously. 
 
In general, behavioral design literature describes behavior in terms of the trigger that 
provokes the behavior under study, then the actual behavior and finally, the consequence or 
the result of the behavior (Miltenberger, 2011). Cash (Cash et al., 2017) explains that 
Behavioral Design incorporates design strategies aiming to create triggers that alter a 
behavior having a desired result in mind.  
==PAGE:21==
13 
 
 
Behavioral Design has been applied in several branches during the years. Wherever habit 
formation and behavior adoption are required, behavioral Design is relevant. Some 
examples could be consumer behavior and marketing, organizational changes for employee 
behaviors and the public sector for citizen guiding 
 
Several theoretical and practical frameworks have been developed. Probably nudging is the 
most common and well-known. Nudges are small, indirect environment/artifact features that 
aim to alter people's behavior and decision-making in a predictable way without forbidding 
any options or significantly changing their economic incentives (Thaler & Sunstein, 2008). 
Thaler and Sunstein have elaborated on the benefits that nudging can have in important 
sociotechnical sectors, such as health, wealth and happiness. Governments already 
implement nudging in their policy-making strategies in order to make public services more 
cost-effective and easier for citizens to use. For example, UK Government devotes 
resources to achieve that through the behavioral Insights Team (BIT), whose goal is to 
introduce a more realistic model of human behavior in policy [19]. 
 
Other researchers have tried to develop theoretical frameworks that try to explore and 
analyze behavior in order to control it. Fogg’s behavioral Model (B. Fogg, 2009) breaks down 
a specific behavior in three distinct factors: Motivation, Ability, and Trigger. Basically, Fogg 
explains that for a behavior to take place by a user, the user needs to be motivated and able 
to do it. If there is a combination of those two, then a suitable trigger could make the 
behavior possible.  
 
Eyal (Eyal, 2014) developed the Hook model, claiming that a habit is created in a loop of four 
parts (Trigger, Action, Reward and Investment). The designer can design for each one of 
these parts in order to successfully put the user through the loop many times to create a 
habit. Wendel (Wendel, 2013) has developed practical tools addressed to designers with the 
purpose of studying and changing user behaviors. 
 
Although, the theoretical and practical approach of behavioral Design varies, one common 
aspect could be identified. Researchers agree that the evaluation part of a design is of great 
importance, not only to identify if the design is successful in changing a specific behavior, 
but also if it does this in an ethical way. Morality and the positive user experience should 
drive any effort of behavioral change (Wendel, 2013). 
 
Persuasive Technology is a branch of behavioral Design which refers to the technological 
artifacts that are designed to change attitudes or behaviors of the users. Fogg (B. J. Fogg, 
2003) investigated how people are motivated or persuaded when interacting with computing 
products and coined the term “Captology” to describe this field. Although, the most frequent 
application of of persuasive technology is the use of computers to sell products and services, 
there is great beneficial potential to in using it to improve human health and well-being 
(IJsselsteijn et al., 2006). Even more specifically, Persuasive GERONtechnology has been 
developed which focuses on vitality and independence up to the highest possible age (van 
Bronswijk, 2006). Although, Persuasive Technology is still a young research field, several 
challenges have been thoroughly identified, when it comes to healthy ageing (Intille, 2004), 
such as developing and introducing adaptive interventions at the right time (Klasnja et al., 
2015). However, Persuasive Technology has a set of principles (mobile simplicity, mobile 
loyalty, kairos) that can tackle the potential challenges (Spikol & Zapico, 2009).

5.3. Technology Acceptance Model 
 
Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) is a theory that tries to model how users get to accept 
and use a specific information system technology (Davis, 1989). It is relevant to the present 
project, since the target group is considered to have difficulties getting used to new 
technologies and IT systems. In the present project it is going to be used in a practical way 
when designing the solution and also when testing it with users. 
 
TAM focuses on the factors that influence an individual user decision to accept the 
technology. According to TAM, perceived ease of use and perceived usefulness are the 
most important determinants of the actual system use. These two factors are influenced by 
external variables, such as social, cultural and political factors (Surendran, 2012). Perceived 
usefulness describes the degree to which a user believes that using a particular system would 
enhance his or her job performance, while perceived ease of use is about the degree to which a 
person believes that using a particular system would be free from effort (Davis, 1989). Several 
studies have been made to elaborate TAM to more complex models that attempt to explain the 
perceived usefulness and ease of use through different factors and sociotechnical aspects, such 
as subjective norms and computer self-efficacy. TAM2 (Venkatesh & Davis, 2000), TAM3 
(Venkatesh & Bala, 2008) and Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology 
(UTAUT) (Venkatesh & Davis, 2003) are mainly the models that try to do that. Lai (Lai, 2017) 
performs an extensive literature review on technology acceptance models and concludes 
that researchers, designers and organizations ought to deeply understand the theoretical 
and practical aspects of the models in order to effectively apply them. 
 
Although TAM has been widely researched, only recently, it has become an important 
theoretical tool for healthcare and health IT research (Holden & Karsh, 2011). When it 
comes to seniors’ health, the IT solutions are few, since technology acceptance tends to 
decrease with age (Valk et al., 2018). However, this is not the case for daily use of internet 
via smartphones and tablets. In only two years, this number went up by 20% for seniors 
aged 65+ (Danmarks Statistik, 2015). Valk (Valk et al., 2018) concludes that the wearable 
technology has potential to be successful in getting adopted by seniors and being useful to 
promote their health, but several considerations need to be made. Findings point out that 
self-efficacy and customization play an important role in supporting the senior user group’s 
acceptance towards new technologies (Valk & Lu, 2018).

6.1.1. Hasso-Plattner Design Thinking 
 
The Hasso-Plattner design thinking model (Stanford d.school, 2010) was used for structuring 
the project’s process. It is divided in 5 phases (Empathize - Define - Ideate - Prototype - 
Test). Empathize is about investigating the users in order to build empathy for who they are 
and what is important to them. Define aims to analyze the collected findings to scope a 
specific and meaningful challenge. Ideate applies divergent thinking to develop ideas and 
generate design alternatives. Prototype is about getting ideas into the physical world by 
developing and designing prototypes. Test refers to getting feedback on the solutions, 
improving them and getting new insights about the users to validate the concept (Stanford 
d.school, 2010). The project team has followed the design thinking process throughout to 
structure and organize the entire project. As it can be observed in Figure 6, this process is 
not linear but rather iterative, like any design process. The arrows in the figure showcase the 
potential iterations that might take place. This model will also be used as a template for 
section 6.2 to document the design process.

6.1.2. Double-Diamond Model 
 
Another traditional design process, the double-diamond (UK Design Council, 2005), has 
been used to organize, structure and design the aim of the workshops. The double-diamond 
process is divided in four parts (Discover, Define, Develop, Deliver) and for each part there 
could be a potential for a workshop with a corresponding goal (see Figure 7). The first part, 
Discover, is a divergent phase where inspiration and insights are gathered and user needs 
and values are identified. The second part, Define, is a convergent phase where the findings 
from Discover phase are analyzed and the problem space is concretized. The third part, 
Develop, is another divergent phase, where solutions are created, prototyped, tested and 
iterated. Finally, the fourth part, Deliver, is the last converging phase where the resulting 
solution is finalized and launched (Davies & Wilson, 2013).

Results 
 
Initially, they were introduced to the problem in which the project works. The participants 
believe that physical activity is beneficial for their health and were aware of the 
consequences of not being active enough. According to the participants, the transition from 
work to retirement has been a difficult change in their lives. They had to adapt to this newly 
acquired role. 
 
Most participants reported that they were relatively active and practiced some physical 
activity, such as yoga, shopping, visiting museums and cycling, 2-4 times per week. They 
would like to be even more active but they also have the fear of overloading themselves and 
losing functional ability. They don’t feel dependent on others and want to maintain their 
physical activity to continue to be independent in the coming years. 
 
The social aspect was a very interesting topic to discuss; most of them thought that social 
relations may have an influence on someone’s behavior and habits, when it comes to 
physical activity. A participant mentioned: “It inspires me if I am with people who have the 
same interests with me”, while another one said: “I think being social is mostly positive, 
otherwise it might be boring. It is nice to have a good social circle”. They were familiar with 
the use of technology to some extent, they use smartphones and some of them are familiar 
with tablets and computer. It was surprising that most of the respondents knew or had an 
idea about wearable trackers. They could see the potential of using it in connection to 
becoming more physically active. They were asked about a future solution that would use 
wearable trackers to motivate seniors to become more physically active. Some of them could 
see the idea’s prosperity; others thought it was not necessary. Nevertheless, they believed 
that it could be motivating for other seniors who are not as active. They were open about 
sharing personal data in terms of physical activity, especially to doctors and other 
professionals, as long as it is beneficial to be physically active. The participants mentioned 
that they would not like to share their data with their family for monitoring purposes. There 
must be an option for the users to customize to whom they want to share. Some were 
interested in seeing their activity through the day; others thought it was not motivating 
enough by numbers. Others believed that suggestions from their own doctors could motivate 
them. 
 
Summary 
 
Seniors are in general aware of the need and benefits of being physically active. One of their 
main reasons to stay physically active is that they want to be healthy and see their 
grandchildren grow old being able to help and interact with them.

6.2.2.1. Fogg Behavior Model  
 
Fogg (B. Fogg, 2009) developed a model (FBM) that is trying to explain specific behaviors. 
He distinguished three main elements that influence a behavior. There needs to be the 
appropriate amount of Motivation and Ability by the user and if this activation threshold is 
satisfied, then a Trigger can be introduced to the user that will make him/her to do the 
desired behavior (Figure 22). Each FBM element will be analyzed in relation to the findings 
of Interviews, Workshop 1 and Observation sessions. Fogg (B. J. Fogg et al., 2010) sets up 
a framework on how to approach this. 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:45==
37 
 
 
Figure 22: Fogg's Behavior Model [20] 
Trigger 
 
Here, some of the potential triggers that could be incorporated with the right timing are 
presented. It was identified that the users would listen to their doctor’s suggestions. This is 
an opportunity to incorporate the public healthcare system in the solution. Some triggers 
could be that the system is suggested by doctors and SOSUs monitor users and give 
comments via the system. 
 
Another issue that could be used as a trigger is the need of the users to see their results and 
their progress. To do that, suitable color visualizations and structure should be designed to 
represent users’ status and significant changes in their data. 
 
The importance of routine and scheduling of the target group and their need to learn new 
things can be helpful triggers for action. These could be incorporated by suggesting 
appropriate times to be active based on their personal calendar and recommendations from 
weather and location. Also, their need to learn new things can be exploited by providing 
famous quotes and interesting facts about physical activity and their situation. Having a pet, 
like a dog, can be of great motivation, which could be used as a trigger. All those triggers 
could take the form of notifications in the system. 
 
Ability 
 
If triggers alone do not succeed in the adoption of the wanted behavior, then the other two 
elements should be manipulated. The second element of FBM is Ability. Fogg explains that 
in order for a behavior to be adopted by someone, it needs to be easy to do by them. He 
distinguishes 6 Ability Factors (time, money, physical effort, brain cycles, social deviance 
and non-routine). By analyzing each one of these factors, a designer can identify where the 
user finds it difficult to do a behavior. Then, the designer can take design decisions to 
minimize this difficulty.  
==PAGE:46==
38 
 
 
Time is about how much time the wanted behavior takes. The recommended amount of 
physical activity for elders is 30 minutes of moderate intensity five days per week (Ministry of 
Health, 2013). This is an achievable goal, especially because seniors tend to have more 
available time in their day. Finally, exercising, like weightlifting, may take more time, but 
again 30-60 minutes is the standard amount and it does not need to happen every day. Only 
twice a week could be enough, according to World Health Organization (Ministry of Health, 
2013). 
 
Money is also not a crucial factor for the wanted behavior of being physically active. There is 
no need of a lot of money to stay physically active. Aerobic exercise, such as walking and 
running, can happen literally with no extra cost. Although, weightlifting may demand a gym 
subscription, there are alternatives like bodyweight exercises and cheap equipment, such as 
elastic bands. 
 
Physical effort is the main purpose of the behavior. Especially for some users, this might be 
felt even more. This could move some users away from the behavior and lean towards 
inactivity. The system should incorporate features that mitigate this factor and make the 
users think less of the physical effort. Rewards that make the user feel reimbursed for their 
efforts could be one solution. 
 
Brain cycles is about the mental effort that is needed for the wanted behavior. Being 
physically active usually does not demand significant mental effort. It actually works in an 
opposite direction by relieving stress. It is possible that the user undertakes mental effort 
when they need to schedule one activity that they are not used to. To mitigate that pain, the 
system could have less options on when, where and how to do it and also, give concrete 
activity suggestions depending on weather, location and personal calendar events. 
 
Social deviance is in general something that is rare when talking about physical activity. 
Walking or doing exercises are nothing outside the norm. Of course, there might be cases of 
social discomfort, when people are overweight or feel that they do not fit in specific 
environments, such as gyms. The system could provide the feature of doing activities in 
groups and suggest alternatives to locations and gyms when needed, in order to alleviate 
this pain. 
 
Non-routine is the last Ability Factor and it examines if the user is used to doing the wanted 
behavior. If not, there is a risk of the Ability being low and not having an adoption of the 
behavior. The target group might be used to walking, but running and strength training are 
more possible to be less done by them. In order to mitigate that, the system should connect 
those habits to another one that is well-known to exist in their routine. 
 
In general, the Ability for the target group is considered high. Solutions for any difficulty are 
suggested above, and maybe, physical effort and non-routine are the factors that are 
identified to have the most risk to affect the adoption of the wanted behavior. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:47==
39 
 
Motivation 
 
According to FBM, the third element of motivation is divided in three Core Motivators and 
each one of them has two facets: 
● Sensation (Pleasure | Pain) 
● Anticipation (Hope | Fear) 
● Belonging (Acceptance | Rejection) 
 
By manipulating those motivators, the project team has the ability to make users adopt a 
behavior, since they will have more motivation to do so. Specifically, the behavior of being 
physically active and walking more during the day could be adopted by increasing motivation 
in this way. 
 
The user’s sensation of pleasure could be increased by seeing results, like better health and 
psychology. Learning something new and enjoying nature are some things that were found 
to be beneficial in that aspect from Workshop 1. Being physically active can eliminate the 
pains of weakness or boredom. As far as anticipation is concerned, our research showed 
that people hope to live longer and meet new people. The system should be able to promote 
those hopes. On the other hand the fear on missing on their grandchildren can be tackled by 
the longevity that physical activity provides. Doing something wrong or getting used to 
something new is another fear that can be coped with system tutorials and explanations, as 
well as doctor suggestions. Finally, offering the possibility to do things with other people and 
friends can be a great Belonging motivator, as the users feels that they are accepted in a 
social group. 
 
6.2.2.2. Hook Model 
 
Although, FBM was considered an efficient and practical analytical tool for the research 
findings, in order to have a more holistic approach to the behavioral design aspect of the 
project, another model was used. The Hook Model by Nir Eyal (Eyal, 2014) was a good 
opportunity to combine practicality and novel elements to contribute in the system design. 
 
Eyal developed the Hook Canvas (Figure 23), where he suggests that to create a habit-
forming product, a designer needs to put the user through a loop of four parts - Trigger, 
Action, Reward and Investment.     
 
Figure 23: Eyal's Hook Canvas [21] 
==PAGE:48==
40 
 
 
Trigger is an element that was already described in FBM. An important distinction here, 
made by Eyal, is the difference between external and internal triggers. While the external 
triggers are about physical system features that will prompt the user to a specific action, the 
internal ones will have a crucial contribution to that as well. They are feelings and mental 
associations that are related to a specific behavior. For example, friends and doing things 
with them could be a trigger for people to be physically active, because the sentiment of 
boredom and loneliness is decreased. In this way, activities, such as walking and exercising, 
can be associated with fun and the feeling of social belonging. 
 
Action is the behavior that the project team expects after the introduction of the triggers. In 
order to analyze that element, Eyal uses Fogg’s model of trigger-ability-motivation, which 
was analyzed above. 
 
Reward is the third element of the model. It is the prize that the user is expecting to have 
after doing the action. The important thing here is to have rewards that are not static, but 
variable, depending on the action. In this way, the user does not know what to anticipate. 
Eyal distinguishes three different types of rewards - rewards of tribe, hunt and self. Rewards 
of the tribe are social rewards and derive from competition and partnership. Hunt rewards 
are about making the user putting effort to find their prize and this prize in the system might 
be new knowledge, experience points, money through shopping offers or even the natural 
reward of endorphins when exercising. Finally, the self-rewards are about self-achievement 
and the feeling of completeness and mastery. This can be achieved by the various 
visualizations of the user’s improvement. 
 
Investment is the last element that will drive the user back to the start of the loop. Basically, 
investment is about putting value in the system overtime, as it is used. By having aspects 
like leveling up and streak rewards, it is more possible that people will come back and in that 
way create a habit.  
 
Although the Hook model has a lot of common aspects with FBM, it brings important novelty 
in the analysis of the “Empathize” findings. The last two elements of Reward and Investment 
are the parts that complement the FBM and further validate the use of gamification features 
as a motivation tool in the system.

System Description 
 
Although, the design requirements give a general overview of what the solution system 
should do, here, a summary of its main features will be presented. Figure 24 illustrates the 
relationships between the system and different relevant stakeholders, showing the value 
exchange among them. Some of the connections in this figure are noted with a yellow 
asterisk to showcase that further validation needs to be made. Those considerations are 
described below, as well as the rest of the system. 
 
The system consists of an activity tracker and an application. It was decided to introduce a 
technological solution, since the target group was identified to be familiar with technologies 
such as smartphones, tablets and application interfaces. The use of smartphone from 
seniors over 65 is increasing (Danmarks Statistik, 2015a) and as the generations are aging 
there will be more and more technology literacy. 
 
The system should visualize and present the user’s data from the activity tracker in an 
understandable and clear way with distinct colors and graphs. The history of the user’s data 
should be presented as well. The main set of data to be presented is consisting of steps, 
distance, calories burnt and time that the user heart rate stayed above a threshold. 
Furthermore, the system should have gamified challenges as a feature and reward the user 
for achievements digitally and physically. 
 
An important element of the solution is the social aspect. The users should be able to 
connect with other users and share/present their progress among each other. There should 
be a feature of creating group activity events. Finally, there should be a possibility to 
collaborate or compete with other users.  
 
The system should be able to provide suggestions and nudges in the form of app 
notifications. The notifications could take the form of activity suggestions depending on 
weather forecasts. The system should synchronize with the user’s calendar in order to 
identify schedule gaps and propose activities. Suggestions could also be made depending 
on user’s location or interests. Alternative ways of notifications are the interesting facts, 
famous quotes and personal achievements in order to motivate users to be more active.

Another feature that we included to be tested is the Daily Challenge (Figure 41), which 
provides an easy achievable challenge for users to complete every day. If they complete the 
challenge, their streak increases by one and resets to 0 if they don’t manage to complete it 
before midnight. The purpose of the daily challenge is to make users come back to the app 
to not lose their streak. It can feel discouraging to reach the recommended amount of steps, 
so giving a manageable challenge can give them the motivation to initiate activity. The daily 
challenge represents the habit-forming feature in our system and has similarities with some 
of the gamification features that we want to implement in the “Campaign” tab, as illustrated in 
the Marvel Prototype. In addition, a new motivational fact about physical activity is displayed 
every day with the daily challenge to further arouse their interest and lure the users to form a 
habit of checking the webpage.  
 
Technical description 
 
The functional prototype is based on the Fitbit trackers and utilizes the Fitbit API to collect 
health information from the users. In order to access the data, the user needs to authorize 
that their personal health data is accessible from the API. This is an one-time authorization. 
Fitbit uses OAuth 2.0 for user authorization and API authentication. The OAuth 2.0 
framework requires your application to obtain an Access Token when the Fitbit user 
authorizes your app to access their data. The Access Token is used for making HTTP 
request to the Fitbit API [22]. The connection with the Fitbit API was outsourced to a special 
advisor of DTU Management Engineering. The health data is presented in a JSON file 
(online text file in matrix form) and can be accessible with an HTTP request. The website is 
located on a DTU server and coded in HTML, CSS and JavaScript by the project team. See 
Appendix J for full code.

6.2.5.3. Design Iterations 
 
Several changes were made in the screen designs, since new insights were taken into 
account from Workshop 2.  
 
An important modification that altered the app’s structure and feature was the elimination of 
the Calendar. It was found that users were getting confused with the existence of a new 
calendar in the app and they preferred the use of their standard phone calendar and the 
synchronization with the app’s events. 
 
Another point where the users could not follow up was the Community side. Although, they 
understood the social aspect of it, there was confusion with its structure. It was decided to 
have some modifications (Figure 49). Friends and Events were separated and each one of 
them got a place in the main menu. The Events side was divided in Events that the user is 
going to and Other events that are suggested to them. This created another benefit, since 
the buttons to “Create an event” and “Add a friend” could take more space now, thus they 
are more visible.

7. Implementation 
 
This section describes how MOSYS can be implemented as a viable business solution, 
based on a case study made in collaboration with Tryg and analysis of the system’s value for 
different stakeholders. 
 
7.1. Open Innovation X 
 
Open Innovation X (Oi-X) is a programme, where different companies provide a challenge 
for innovative students and put their employees and advisors to students’ disposal for a short 
period of time. The project team attended in the Oi-X Health 2018 (6th-17th of April), since 
one of the challenges, provided by Tryg, was about developing a new insurance model that 
targets seniors by promoting a health and well-being services [24]. In Oi-X Finals, the team 
presented a 4-minute pitch of the developed business idea in front of investors and 
healthcare sector experts. 
 
During the event, two more members were recruited for the development of the idea. The 
two new participants brought diverse skills in the team. One of them was a software 
developer and the other one being a psychologist, who worked with seniors and monitoring 
their activity with activity trackers. 
 
The goal for the team’s participation in the event, was to explore opportunities to develop the 
business idea around the designed solution. Having experts and companies providing 
feedback and insights on how it could be implemented can provide real-world validation.  
 
7.2. Case study: Tryg 
 
Health insurance is often part of a salary package in relation to the private labor market, 
meaning that people are covered by the health insurance through their job. Their insurance 
stops, when people leave the labor market and retire, which results in Tryg having a very low 
senior customer base. Other than that, Tryg wants to focus on the senior customer segment 
because the market size of seniors will grow dramatically in the coming years (ONU, 2015). 
  
The new generation of seniors has grown up with a lot of opportunities and nowadays, 
seniors have high expectations for the years after retirement, including absence of physical 
and mental illnesses [24]. Tryg wants to support healthy living and make seniors more self-
reliant in order to attract and be appealing for the new generation of seniors. 
 
In Oi-X, the project team had close collaboration with Tryg about how to further develop 
Mosys to make it fit into a new business model. For this case study, the design team will 
develop this new business model, as being part of Tryg. Tryg validated the value proposed 
by Mosys, but their main consideration was to narrow down the target group in order to focus 
the communication and make the product more accessible for potential customers. In order 
for Mosys to be commercialized and implemented through Tryg, it was re-conceptualized to 
target elderlies with osteoarthritis.  
==PAGE:82==
74 
 
7.2.1. Problem 
 
 
In a rapidly ageing society (Suzman & Beard, 2011), there is a need for independent living. 
However, one of the most common diseases for seniors is arthritis (Conte et al., 2008). 
Specifically, osteoarthritis is one of the most common and disabling diseases for this 
population.  When these people retire, they are less likely to stay physically active because 
they lose their routine. Osteoarthritis can be relieved by physical exercise (Bennell & 
Hinman, 2011; Halilaj et al., 2018), yet people often stay inactive due to the pain involved at 
the onset of physical activity. Physical inactivity may lead to deterioration of the condition 
and overall well-being.  
 
7.2.2. Solution 
 
Although there is a transition targeting users with osteoarthritis, the need to motivate them to 
stay physically active remains. Mosys, the solution that was described in section 6.2.2.3, 
6.2.3.4 and 6.2.5.3 can be used in this context. Tryg can offer a platform that aims on 
relieving user’s pain. It is composed of an application, an activity tracker and other added 
services. Motivation is provided through social and community engagement, gamified 
challenges and connection with doctors and other healthcare professionals.  
 
A distinguishing factor for the Tryg Mosys system is the organization of events about 
osteoarthritis in collaboration with Gigtforeningen, the Danish arthritis association. Moreover, 
exercises made to relieve osteoarthritis are introduced in the application’s challenges. 
Finally, Tryg provides special services, such as the transfer of user’s to its events location, 
as well as consultation with arthritis specialists. Figure 53 represents the set of products and

7.2.3. Value Proposition 
 
Tryg Mosys aims to relieve osteoarthritis pain for its users and make them more functional 
and independent in their daily life. Subsequently, that translates to an overall better physical 
and mental health, improving their quality of life. 
 
In an upper level, this could incorporate a positive societal impact in time. Since physical 
activity could alleviate osteoarthritis symptoms, without the use of pharmaceutical 
interventions or surgery, the hospitalization and healthcare expenses would decrease and 
there would be a healthier ageing society.

8. Answering Research Questions 
 
This section summarizes how the research questions are answered from the design 
process, which is described in previous sections. 
 
Research Question 1: What would a system look like, which effectively 
motivates seniors to be more physically active by incorporating activity trackers? 
 
As argued earlier, physical activity in the daily life of seniors is important, both for the 
individuals and in terms of reducing resources required to take care of the elders. The 
project team has developed a solution, that can help seniors be motivated to stay physically 
active and help professionals to notice activity patterns in order to prevent physiological 
function loss and well-being deterioration.  
 
The solution incorporates an activity tracker, measuring user’s steps, distance and calories, 
in connection with an application. The application has four tabs, which represent the most 
important functionalities: Home tab, Event tab, Friend tab and Challenge tab. The Home tab 
gives an overview of user’s activity in relation to their daily goal. This is the main page, which 
also includes buttons that refer to basic user information such as their activity history, 
achievements and health indicators. 
 
The Event tab gives the senior the opportunity to create and join activity events. Events bring 
in a social aspect that through our research was found to motivate seniors more than a 
quantified goal. The events can be made for friends or publicly. In future versions, 
collaboration with various stakeholders (Ældresagen, Gigtforeningen) could be established 
in order to book a spot in their exercise-related events. The Friends page is mainly to show 
friend profiles and add new contacts, in case the user wants to challenge or invite a specific 
person. The last page, Challenge, is to motivate competitive minded seniors. There are 
gamified challenges that can be played in a solo mode or with friends. Also, an easy 
achievable Daily Challenge and a daily motivational quote are available. This feature is 
mainly to make users come back, since they are building up a streak that can be shown on 
their

The second aspect of this Research Question is that the solution motivates effectively 
seniors to be physically active. In order to validate that, we conducted an experiment by

prototype of Mosys in order to identify if they get motivated to walk more.

Research Question 2: How could the system be developed through co-

Participatory design has been a common theme for this project. The designer team has 
continuously attempted to bring potential users from the target segment and healthcare

insights, which were collected and recontextualized into design requirements for the system. 
Co-creation has been part in finalizing the design through user testing of prototype and A/B 
testing to collect further ideas/validation from the target segment and to enhance the user 
experience.

Research Question 3: How could this system be implemented? 
 
The purpose of the collaboration with Tryg through the participation in Open Innovation X 
was to develop a case study that could be a possible implementation plan for the developed 
system, Mosys.  
 
In collaboration with Tryg, a new business model was developed in order to implement 
Mosys in their business needs. One of the main changes was narrowing down the target 
group, in order to focus the communication. This makes it easier to capture the attention and 
set up a specific marketing strategy. Osteoarthritis senior patients were selected, as they are 
a specific target, but also compose a big market. Furthermore, physical activity is considered 
a solution for relieving their symptoms. 
 
Various business development elements were explored to develop the business plan. 
Identifying the problem, developing a value proposition for different stakeholders, competitor 
analysis and draft calculation of margin profit are some of the aspects that were analyzed. 
For example, it was found that different stakeholders need to come together in order to make 
the system work. An important partner for this business model is Gigtforeningen (Danish 
Arthritis Association), since they can provide their expertise through consultation and special 
event organization. In order to sustain and grow the business, a subscription model was 
selected in combination with in-app advertisements.

9. Discussion 
 
In this chapter, interesting insights and findings that emerged from the research and design 
process will be presented and discussed. Some of the points that are made in this chapter 
relate to selection of methods, empirical evaluation of co-creation with seniors and ideas for 
future research. 
 
We believe that the breadth in methods selection provided various and useful insights. The 
goal of this breadth was to explore all the aspects of a development process. Moreover, the 
breadth was necessary, since the selected research methods would complement each other. 
For example, trying to identify behaviors and habits that often happen unconsciously brought 
up the need to have observation sessions with the users, rather than only conducting 
interviews with them. Although the breadth is apparent, depth was not neglected. It was 
necessary in specific research methods, such as the co-creation workshops and behavioral 
design approaches, since they were the driving factors of this project. 
 
Some practical new knowledge was also gained by the project team on what works and what 
challenges might occur when organizing and conducting workshops with seniors. For 
example, being playful can be efficient in involving seniors in the workshop environment. 
Despite an initial uncertainty, seniors participated actively in the playful aspects of exercises 
(Name Game, Kahoot quiz) and in this way the creating comfort goal was achieved.   
 
We found that precise time allocation and thorough explanations are of great importance. 
Because of lack of time, some exercise explanations were fast and not concrete, which 
resulted in confusion among participants. Seniors are in general practical and want to have 
an explicit reason of why something happens. This means that, a clear goal of every 
exercise needs to be outlined and it cannot be too abstract. We observed this kind of 
confusion in some of the more complex exercises of Workshop 1, such as the Empathy Map. 
These findings were considered in order to improve Workshop 2, which resulted in more 
useful insights. 
 
Finally, recruiting workshop participants can be a main challenge in co-creation processes. 
Although Brandt (Brandt et al., 2010) suggests that a well-defined project, which is promoted 
by strong stakeholders (Lyngby-TaarbæK Municipality),  can address the participation of 
potential users. There was a challenge to recruit seniors from care homes, because they 
would already participate in another project’s workshops. A delay of 1-2 weeks in the original 
project planning was observed just to find relevant and enough seniors at a common time 
and place to participate in our workshops. Having a wide network of colleagues or friends 
can bring a solution to that, as well as inviting groups of people that share some kind of 
everyday practices (Brandt et al., 2010).  
 
When recruiting workshop participants, the project’s team was afraid to include seniors that 
are too technologically experienced, which would affect the usability testing results. At the 
same time, we wanted participants that represent the “future user” (basic experience with 
technology), as in co-design processes, designers and future users are carrying out central 
part of the design activities together (Brandt et al., 2010). We believe that the final selection 
of participants succeeded in solving this trade-off, because they had adequate technological 
knowledge to represent the future users, but also showcase any usability issues for the app 
design, that should be resolved in design iterations. 
 
==PAGE:92==
84 
 
One of the findings of Workshop 2, which is validated by insights from interviews and 
Workshop 1, is the technological literacy of senior users. In general, technology acceptance 
seems to go down with age (Valk et al., 2018), but this is a case that can start to slowly 
diminish due to seniors’ increasing knowledge of interactions and their ease of using 
standardized usability principles. Specifically, it was found that the majority of the project 
participants (65+), use smartphones and internet today and that percentage will only 
increase in the next years. Furthermore, through Workshop 2, it was obvious that seniors are 
used to advanced screen interactions such as swipe functions and standardized icon 
recognition. These facts showcase and justify the project team’s decision to develop a 
system integrating an application and an activity tracker. 
 
On the other hand, in this project, there is a research limitation that should be translated into 
potential future work for the project team. The solution was developed with a holistic 
approach, thinking of it as a system, rather than just a product. A crucial component of the 
system was the connection of the healthcare system and its professionals with the designed 
solution. We strongly believe that engagement with professionals was not adequate in this 
project in order to validate this systemic connection. Although, the interaction with a SOSU in 
an interview and in Workshop 1 provided interesting and helpful insights, there is a need to 
plan engagement with different stakeholders in the healthcare system, such as doctors, 
nurses, SOSUs and directors. A new set of workshops, involving this aspect of the system, 
is crucial. This could provide insights on what data could be useful for doctors and how they 
could be implemented in their system without interfering with their processes. 
 
Eventually, an important point for future work is the need for further user involvement. The 
involved participants might not represent the typical senior citizen, because their technical 
literacy and their physical activity levels are higher than the average Danish senior. 
Furthermore, the small number of people engaged in testing is not enough for adequate 
concept validation. Moreover, since we are dealing with behavior change it is important to 
test the solution on a long-term basis, in order to check that it motivates users effectively and 
in a sensible and ethically responsible way. Also, further development via a robust 
implementation plan with quantified value propositions and economics can make the solution 
emerge in the real world.

10. Conclusion 
 
In this project, a feedback system, Mosys, measuring seniors’ physical activity levels to 
motivate them to stay active, was developed and tested. Co-Creation was the main theory 
used to identify user needs and evaluate the solution. A draft implementation plan explored 
the business possibilities of this idea. Eventually, a testing of the functional prototype was 
conducted and there were significant indications that Mosys has a potential to be successful.  
 
A wide variety of challenges on seniors’ healthy living were examined during this project. In a 
rapidly ageing society, seniors need to stay physically and mentally healthy in order to stay 
independent and avoid functional loss. Inactivity and weakness may occur especially after 
retirement, because of missing social life from work and not having things to do. Weakness 
and inactivity lead to deterioration of well-being. Subsequently, hospitalization and 
healthcare expenses will only go up. Although physical activity could contribute to a solution 
by offering a general preventive measure against loss of function, current technological 
solutions that monitor physical activity and provide feedback are not designed for elders. 
This report tried to act on challenges mentioned above. An important finding in this research 
is seniors’ awareness. Seniors are aware of the challenges to independent living and know 
about the connection of physical activity and general health. This finding was important as 
awareness makes them susceptible to motivating efforts. When someone knows of the 
impacts of one habit, usually there is willingness to change it. 
 
Participatory design and co-creation workshops are found to be efficient in researching the 
seniors’ challenges and needs, as well as designing solutions with them. Participatory 
Design theory would lead one to expect that the continuous engagement with activities and 
discussions with seniors are able to involve them as end users and offer important findings 
(Massimi & Baecker, 2006). Through our process and results, we confirmed that the above 
expectation is true. Although age and lack of understanding of the workshops purpose could 
be limiting factors in a design research of that kind, with the appropriate involvement and 
workshop designs, it is possible to collaborate effectively with seniors in a co-creation 
environment. In Chapter 9, there is a wide description of insights out of the conducted 
workshops. Thorough explanations, time allocation, concrete goals and playfulness are 
some of the main points that the project team takes from its experience with involving 
seniors in the design process.
