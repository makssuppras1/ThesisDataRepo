Abstract
The coupling concept of photovoltaic-thermal collectors (PV/T) and phase change
material ceiling panels (PCM) is researched in this thesis. For PV/Ts’, the average
specific cooling power and total cooling energy was examined for the entire cooling
period, 1st of May - 30th of September, for different climates and collector slopes
through simulations in the TRNSYS17 simulation studio. An experimental compari-
son between radiant cooling panels (RCP) and PCM ceiling panels was used to assess
their behaviour and ability to provide an acceptable indoor thermal environment in
a two-person climate chamber.
First of all, by running a parametric analysis for the PV/T supply temperature,
a 21oC value was found as representative. For it, the highest total cooling energy for
the period analyzed was 165 kWh/m2 with an average specific cooling power of 70
W/m2 for Copenhagen, Denmark and the least, 67 kWh/m2 total cooling energy and
35 W/m2 average specific cooling power, for Athens, Greece respectively. Moreover,
it was determined that the cooling potential varies with the latitude, decreasing from
North to South, and it is dependent on the climate. By comparing the cooling de-
mand with the PV/T cooling energy production for the entire cooling season for the
cities analyzed, it was found that storage tanks and additional chillers are required.
For northern European cities the PV/Ts cover 100% or more of the cooling demand.
Nonetheless, for central and southern European cities, the PV/T production is in-
sufficient, but still higher than 50%, which leads to high energy savings potential.
Furthermore, a deeper analysis for Copenhagen, Denmark, showed the collector slope
to have a different effect on the cooling energy than on the electrical output. A verti-
cal mounting ensures the highest cooling energy production while an optimum slope
for electricity production performs worse in terms of cooling.
The indoor thermal environment for both the RCPs and the PCM ones was com-
pared according to the categories presented in EN 15251 [1]. The standard recom-
mends a temperature range for offices between 23.5oC and 25.5oC for category I,
23oC and 26oC for category II and from 22oC to 27oC for category III. The RCPs
outperformed the PCMs, the operative temperature being within CAT III for 91% of
the time in the RCP case, while for only 48% for the PCM panels. On the other hand,
the PCM tiles’ ability to shift the demand to a later time was observed, but due to
the insufficient thermal capacity an adequate indoor thermal environment could not
be kept. Nevertheless, the PCM tiles represented a commercially available solution,
making the combination of the two, PV/Ts and PCM, not optimal.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Nature and Scope
More and more stress has been put on our planet in latest years due to increasing
population and economic growth. As the two above contribute to climate change,
countries need to act and develop sustainable solutions in order to diminish its effects.
One possible way which scientists have already been looking into is improving energy
efficiency, thus reducing energy use.
A report with respect to energy certification of buildings from the International
Energy Agency states that the building sector accounts for more than 40% of the
primary energy use [2]. As population continues to increase, this share will keep the
same trend. Moreover, statistics show that for the European Union, the residential
sector accounts for more than 25% of final energy use [3], this energy being mainly
used for space heating and cooling among others. Of this 25%, only 15.7% of the
final energy use in buildings is covered by renewables, thus leaving room for higher
penetration. As the European Union brings a more ambitious strategy for 2030, 40%
cut in greenhouse gas emissions and a 27% share of renewable energy use [4], coupling
of photovoltaic-thermal collectors with phase change material ceiling panels could be
the cornerstone for renewables integration in space cooling.
With this goal in mind, this study investigates the cooling potential of PV/Ts
and the behaviour of phase change material panels for the development of a coupled
system which provides high-temperature cooling for buildings. This thesis will employ
the use of a photovoltaic-thermal panel as a radiator, that cools the water required
for discharging the PCM through night radiative cooling. First, the potential of the
collector is analyzed through simulations in TRNSYS17 for different collector tilts
in Copenhagen, Denmark. The average cooling power and total cooling energy for
the entire cooling period is examined for multiple cities across Europe with different
climates with which broad maps of PV/T cooling potential are developed.
The PCM panels are investigated as they could account for the lost thermal mass
of new lightweight buildings. Also, the PCM panels have the ability to store heat and
shift it to night time when PV/Ts produce cold water which presents an edge when
compared to other technologies. Thus, the behaviour of such panels is investigated
through experiments. On top of that, radiant cooling panels, an already existing
technology on the market, are also employed in experiments in order to compare
the two technologies with respect to their ability to provide an acceptable indoor

and necessary improvements to PCM panels in order to become a feasible technology.
Lastly, this master thesis comes with a solution for further implementation of

Objectives
The main objectives of this master thesis are:
• PV/T cooling potential for different European climates and collector slopes
• PV/T dimensioning for cooling purposes
• Benefits and limitations of phase change material usage
• Experimental comparison of market present radiant cooling panels and PCM
tiles
• Establishment of necessary improvements in PCM ceiling panels
All building up as a starting point for coupling PV/T collectors and PCM ceiling
panels as a cooling system for buildings.

CHAPTER 2
Introduction to Night
Radiative Cooling
Night radiative cooling is a passive process in which a heat transfer medium, such as
the surface of a photovoltaic-thermal panel, can be cooled down at night by releasing
heat to the sky. This occurs due to the fact that the effective sky temperature is
lower at night than the air.
Most of the heat is released through the radiative heat transfer, thus the name
”Night radiative cooling” [5]. Nevertheless, the convective heat transfer can also play
a part in the cooling process if the air temperature is lower than the one of the heat
transfer medium. However, if the air temperature is higher than the agent’s, the
convective heat transfer counters the radiative cooling process by heating it up.
Throughout the years, many applications of night radiative cooling have been
experimented. For instance, Eicker & Dalibard mention that one of the first, tested
almost 60 years ago, was the movable insulation [6]. This application takes advantage
of the radiative heat exchange directly between the interior of the building and the
sky [7]. This method however has its downsides, such as the motorized system for
removing and reassembling the roof or inability to withstand harsh meteorological
conditions.
Another application used in early years, described by Erell & Etzion [8], was the
use of roof ponds. Here, the water in the pond, absorbs the extra heat from the
building. Then, through processes such as radiation, convection and evaporation, it
releases the previously absorbed heat.
Nevertheless, as technology advances, new methods appear for employing night
radiative cooling. This thesis focuses on the use of closed-water based systems such
as photovoltaic/thermal panels (PV/T) for cooling, therefore the next sections will
be focused on previous research in that area.

In this section, a short description of a photovoltaic-thermal panel (PV/T) will be
presented. A representation of the photovoltaic-thermal panels is shown in 2.1.

The panels are composed of photovoltaic cells on top, which create electrical power
by converting the incident solar radiation. Then, the cells provide heat to the fluid
circulated in the embedded pipes through the absorber plate situated between the two.
Moreover, PV/T modules should establish a strong and uniform contact between the
pipes and the absorber plate in order to provide good heat transfer.
It is first worth mentioning that for night radiative cooling, unglazed solar collec-
tors would present a better choice for some applications. This is because PV/Ts have
lower emissivity and glazing which reduce the heat transfer. Nevertheless, PV/Ts
have been chosen for this study as these panels can offer, on top of heating and
cooling, electrical energy with little difference in cooling energy [10].
2.2
Advantages and disadvantages for using PV/Ts
Using PV/Ts for employing night radiative cooling brings benefits as well as draw-
backs, some of them being mentioned in this section. A number of these advantages
and disadvantages have also been identified by Grossule [11]. The following benefits
occur when using PV/Ts:
• higher utilization factor for PV/Ts (day and night)
• higher radiative exchange during summer (more clear sky-nights)
• energy savings - as night radiative cooling does not employ the use of a refrig-
eration cycle
==PAGE:23==
2.3 Overview of night radiative cooling applications potential
5
• extra cooling provided by convective heat exchange if outside temperature is
lower than the collector’s surface temperature
• can be coupled with high thermal storage materials
The disadvantages of PV/Ts when providing cooling energy through radiative
heat exchange during night are:
• installation costs
• maintenance (cleaning or replacing damaged parts)
• prevention of the freezing of the agent circulating through the loop
• phase difference between demand (day) and production (night)
• waste disposal at the end of lifetime
• intermittent nature of renewable energy
Nevertheless, in some cases it may be beneficial to use other forms of applications
and locations. Some papers discussed in the following section exhibit novel radiators
designed specifically for cooling purposes by taking advantage of night radiative cool-
ing. Therefore, these could have a higher potential for cooling and thus would be
better for cooling purposes specifically.

To start with, in 1999 Erell & Etzion adapted a model for heating to cooling
applications for desert areas and validated it with experimental data [8]. Their ex-
periment consisted of an insulated roof pond in order to reduce heat gains from the
surrounding air. The water in the pond was then circulated at night through flat-
plate collectors with the purpose of cooling the water by radiation and convection.
Their first important finding was that convection plays a bigger role when the radia-
tor is unglazed. This enhanced the cooling performance of the radiator as explained
previously. Moreover, multiple flow rates were tested during the experiments as well
as different environmental conditions. This led to the conclusion that if the air has
a lower temperature than the radiator’s surface, the cooling output of the radiator
increases for higher flow rates, due to the convective heat transfer, whilst having a
higher air temperature than the one of the radiator leads to a decrease in the cooling
resulting from radiation. Lastly, an average cooling power of 80 W/m2 was found for
desert areas.
Secondly, Meir et al.
present a study of a radiative cooling system consisting
of unglazed flat plate collectors, whose walls consist of a polymer [13]. Moreover,
the backside of the radiator was insulated in order to minimize the heat loss. The
radiator, which used water as heat carrier was then coupled with a tank. The purpose
of the study was to investigate the cooling power through experiments (Oslo, Norway
between May-June 1999) which were later on used to validate a model. The model was
then utilized for investigating the impact of the tilt angle, aperture area and reservoir
volume on the cooling power.
Results showed that the heat transfer occurs from
both radiation and convection, radiation representing the larger part as expected. If
the surface temperature of the panels was higher than the ambient air, heat transfer
through convection increased the cooling power. Regarding the tilt, 10o resulted in
an average cooling power of 60 W/m2, a horizontal placement (0o) increased it by 9%
whilst a tilt angle of 32o showed a reduction by 7%. Finally, the system was able to
cover the demand of a one-family house of 150 m2 under clear skies and low humidity,
but employed the use of an auxiliary cooler otherwise.
In another paper published in 2011 by Eicker et al., the electricity and cooling
output of a PV/T panel and PCM system present in a building has been analyzed [6].
The PV/Ts were uncovered and used in experiments conducted in Stuttgart, Germany.
A simulation model developed in TRNSYS was then validated using the results from
the experiment.
The main purpose was finding the radiative heat exchange with
the sky and the resulting cooling power. The cooling power from the experiment in
Stuttgart varied between 100 and 120 W/m2 for different water inlet temperatures.
The same radiator was employed in the European Solar Decathlon competition in June

2010 in Madrid, Spain, where the cooling power varied between 60 and 65 W/m2 when
a storage tank was part of the system. When the radiators were directly connected
to the PCM the cooling power output reduced to 40-45 W/m2. Moreover, the model
was used to simulate annually the behaviour of the system in Madrid, Spain and
Shanghai, China. The choices for the location were based on the climatic differences
between them: humidity and solar radiation level. Even though the solar radiation
level is higher in Madrid, the system proved to be less efficient in humid scenarios
such as Shanghai. The total cooling energy delivered by the PV/T is 16.5 kWh/m2
in Madrid, covering 27% of the cooling demand. In Shanghai only 11% of the cooling

operating for a higher number of hours for the former.
Anderson et al. assessed the heating and cooling performance of a building inte-
grated collector [14]. The main focus of the paper is on cooling production through
night radiative cooling of an unglazed solar collector but the heat production during
the day is taken into consideration as well. The system is different than usual as it is
designed for the integration in a troughed sheet metal roof. Nevertheless, two tanks
are used, one for storing hot water while the other one for cooling purposes. Both the
experiment which took place in New Zealand and the validated model resulted in an
average cooling power of 50 W/m2 which shows that this technology has significant
potential.
Other authors, Hosseinzadeh & Taherian also looked at the cooling potential of
unglazed flat plate collectors [15]. The experiment, which took place in Babol, Iran
had the purpose of cooling water using cheap unglazed collectors which was then
used during the day for cooling the building. Different flow rates and meteorological
conditions were studied. The most important findings were that glazed covers are
required for cooling the radiator surface below the ambient air and that the output of
the radiator is directly influenced by the flow rate. The average cooling power under
clear sky with humid conditions was 45 W/m2.
Zhang et al. looked at the potential of coupling microencapsulated phase change
material with a nocturnal radiative cooling system [16]. However, the paper presents
an interesting and different approach to the system than the others. It involves the
usage of two closed loops, one for the sky radiator and the other for the cooled ceiling.
The two exchange heat with the PCM stored in a tank through two heat exchangers.
The system described was simulated in different cities in China: Hong Kong, Shanghai,
Beijing, Lanzhou and Urumqi. The simulation results show an average cooling power
of 30, 42, 45, 46 and 46 W/m2 for the cities analyzed respectively.
A more developed model of heating and cooling unglazed panel (SHCP) oriented
at 90o was analyzed by Yong et al. [17]. The SHCP is designed to be easily integrated
in a building due to its design. In order to find the cooling power, experiments were
made in Tianjin, China. For them, the panel was connected to two tanks, one for

Xu et al. performed experiments and numerical assessments for using a flat plate

though the optimum tilt for the location was 10o. Still, for the experiment which took
place in September in Beijing, China, the average cooling power registered a value of

Three different designs of unglazed radioconvective panels were investigated for

black coated panel proved unreliable, the study continued on the first two, the former
providing on average a cooling power of 60 W/m2 whilst the latter 45 W/m2.

cooling power. However, the SH-RC produced on average a cooling power between

The most influencial parameter was the air temperature as it affected both radiation
and convective heat losses. Other parameters, such as high wind speeds or low rel-

simulation. For the same nights, the PV/Ts generated 50 W/m2 in the experiment
and 42 W/m2 in the simulation. The differences between experiment and simulation
occurred mostly due to the inability of TRNSYS to account for rain.
An entirely passive system for night radiative cooling is presented by Jourbet et
al. [24]. The radiator described is different than a conventional solar panel as it has
no fins and glazing. In addition, for the experiment in Stellenbosch, South Africa,
the radiator was tilted at 5o. Finally, under both clear and cloudy sky conditions the
radiator’s average cooling power output was 55 W/m2.

has great potential. Nevertheless, some important notes are presented. For instance,

Change Materials
(PCM)
In the interest of prefabrication of buildings, thus reducing the time of construction
or the time for interventions in case of damage, the trend moves towards lightweight
buildings.
As opposed to heavy weight buildings, which have high thermal mass,
lightweight buildings require components to replace the lacking thermal storage ca-
pacity.
One way for storing large amounts of heat is Phase Change Materials (PCM).
These materials have the ability to store thermal energy as latent heat, having a
higher thermal energy per unit mass than conventional building materials [25].
3.1
PCM classification
There are multiple types of phase changing materials. In order to understand them
and their use better, PCMs can be classified according to transition form and com-
ponent.
When talking about transition form, PCMs can transition from and in: solid-
solid, solid-liquid, gas-solid and liquid-gas. Both PCMs which have gas as one of the
transition forms are generally avoided in applications due to large volume change [26].
On the other hand, solid-solid PCMs are advantageous compared to others but have
also high production costs. Lastly, solid-liquid PCMs are cheap and thus a viable
option. Nevertheless, problems arise in their case as well due to phase separation
and supercooling phenomenon (in order for some PCMs to cool and thus solidify a
temperature below the melting one is required).
PCMs can also be differentiated based on the material. There are three types [27]:
• Inorganic (salt hydrate & metallics)
• Organic (paraffin compounds, ester-acids, etc. [26])
==PAGE:32==
14
3 Introduction to Phase Change Materials (PCM)
• Eutectic - mixture of organic and inorganic PCM (organic-organic, inorganic-
inorganic, inorganic-organic)
When choosing the correct phase change material for the desired application the
advantages must be put in balance with disadvantages. For example, inorganic PCMs
are cheap and have high thermal conductivity but are subject to subcooling, phase
separation and can corrode metals or gypsum materials. However, the organic ones
are subject to flammability issues and low thermal conductivity. Nevertheless, one
important aspect is represented by the operating temperature range of the PCM
which should match the temperature range of the application for an efficient use of
thermal mass [25].

(Figure 3.1). This type was analyzed in more detail as it is the most spread PCM
type in buildings. Moreover, the materials used in the experiments and simulations
described in this thesis are solid-liquid PCMs.

Figure 3.1 presents the behaviour of a panel which contains the PCM in capsules
inside the panel which can be made out of gypsum. It is worth mentioning that other

When considering an office building, in the morning, the PCM is in solid state
(top circle). As temperature increases in the room due to occupants, equipment and
solar gains, the material starts to absorb the heat. When the temperature reaches
the melting point, the PCM turns to liquid, absorbing larger amounts of heat with
little temperature increase in the process (red circle). Thus, the PCM cools the room.
During night, the PCM can release the absorbed extra heat to the environment or to
an agent with a lower temperature, returning back to solid state (discharge phase).
As previously stated, the extra heat can be released directly to the environment,
passively or by employing an agent, such as water, which is circulated through pipes
embedded or in contact with the PCM panel for discharging. The latter option can
then be coupled with an external radiator which releases the heat through radiation
to the sky or a mechanical chiller.
3.3
Positive and negative aspects of PCM usage
In this study, PCM panels were used as ceiling panels for cooling purposes. Therefore,
this section presents the strengths and weaknesses of this application.
First of all and one of the most important advantages for the combination between
PCM and photovoltaic/thermal panels is the peak-shaving of cooling load during the
day and its shift to nights [29]. A representation of it can be observed in Figure
3.2. This makes it possible for the PV/Ts to discharge the PCM as they produce
cooling at night. Moreover, peak-shaving leads to improved indoor climate as the
indoor air temperature has less fluctuations within the occupation hours. The shift
of cooling load to night also brings an advantage with or without the coupling of
PCM with PV/Ts. This is because electricity prices are lower at night, leading thus
to cost-effective consumption of electricity even in the case of utilizing mechanical
chillers for discharge.
As the PCM panels reduce the peak cooling loads, it reduces also the capacity of
the HVAC systems. This reduction translates into lower initial costs and lower oper-
ation & maintenance costs. In addition, PCM panels can be discharged using higher
inlet temperatures than conventional systems, increasing thus the energy efficiency
of the chiller.
Lastly, the negative aspects of PCM panels are related to corrosion, leakage and
flammability. These three imply higher production costs than conventional building
materials because of the necessary technologies required in the production stages
(such as encapsulation), in order to avoid the above mentioned issues [26]. Table 3.1
gives a summary of the advantages and disadvantages of phase change materials.

3.4
Research concerning PCM in buildings
This section is a literature review regarding the usage of PCM in buildings. First of
all, there are multiple methods to integrate PCM in buildings. Therefore, the papers
reviewed start with reports which have a broader approach to PCM, while the latter
present experimental studies with focus in PCM ceiling integration. Lastly, articles
analyzing the integration of radiators such as unglazed solar collectors or PV/Ts used
for discharging PCM ceiling panels were presented.
The search was conducted using the search engine available from DTU, ”DTU
Findit”. Multiple keywords were used but the ones with the most relevant results
were: ”PCM”, ”PCM in buildings”, ”PCM ceiling panels”, ”PCM cooling”.
Pomianowski et al. presented a literature with respect to technologies using PCM
for energy storage in [31]. The paper gives an overview of multiple studies ordered by
PCM usage: passive - in construction materials (gypsum, wall boards, concrete, bricks,

etc.), active (thermally active panels containing PCM), in glazing/shading/blinds and
in HVAC components. With respect to materials, gypsum, wall board and concrete
can be combined with 45%, 60% and 6% of PCM weight respectively. Thus, light
weight building can be easily improved from a thermal storage point of view as op-
posed to heavy ones with the use of phase changing materials. On the other hand,
thermally active building components present problems due to the drop in thermal
conductivity when increasing the PCM content. One solution proposed is using high
conductive meshes in-between the layers. Nevertheless, in areas with less research
such as glazed envelopes with PCM, the authors mention that the use of PCM would
be beneficial as it would minimize solar heating loads but also problematic as it could
lead to increased heat losses.
The paper also enforces the fact that the addition
of PCM in buildings would lead to lower energy use and improved indoor comfort.
Lastly, it is concluded that the potential of PCM should be compared to conventional
systems. In addition, it is stressed that combination of PCM technologies with night
cooling is recommended.
The usage of night cooling ventilation along with PCM in buildings is presented
by Álvarez et al.
[32].
The paper presents two solutions which have been both
numerically and experimentally tested in the MECLIDE project. The first one uses
fins filled with PCM material placed to the exterior of the wall protected by an exterior
cover. Tangential fans are used to circulate the air in the bottom-up direction to
discharge the PCM. The second integration option is represented by cylinders placed
in the hollow core slab. In this case as well, air is circulated through the hollow core
to discharge the PCM. The two options provide a cooling power of 227 and 632 W/m2
respectively. Finally, this study was chosen as the authors portray the two solutions
as significantly better than the others presented in literature. Thus, a comparison
can be made with the results of the application presented in this paper.
Another approach to PCM integration and passive cooling can be observed in
the work of Arce et al. [33]. The authors wanted to observe the effect of awnings
with the hope of improving the use of PCM in concrete, as high solar radiation and
high outdoor temperatures were preventing the discharging process.
Experiments
were used to compare to their previous study where no awnings were installed on two
identical cubicles, one without and one with microencapsulated PCM, for a typical
continental weather.
Two scenarios were analyzed, one with free cooling (closed
windows) and another where windows were always opened. The paper concludes that

conductivity of the PCM has a strong effect on the system performance and thus

4.1
TRNSYS 17
For this study, the TRNSYS 17 simulation studio was used for modeling the PV/T-
PCM system and the office room. This program is a user-friendly, graphical software
environment used to simulate the behaviour of transient systems [39].
The software employs the use of two parts. The first one, called the kernel, solves
the model through iterations and plots variables. The second part is a library made
out of components called ”Types”, representing parts of a system.
These can be
controllers, weather data processors, pumps, HVAC equipment or even multizone
buildings. In each of the component, the user can set parameters to desired values in
order to model them as close as possible to the desired product. In addition, TRNSYS
has an interface and other tools which can be used to simplify the user’s experience
or to create complex features.
The Simulation Studio serves as a graphical interface where the user can create
the system and simulate it. Here, components are added, connected and designed to
suit the needs of the user. Then, with the use of the studio, the user can visualize
results after simulating. Two other features, TRNSYS3D and TRNBuild, help with
the creation of multizone buildings. The first one is a SketchUp addon which allows
the user to visualize the geometry while drawing, which can later be imported into a
component within the Simulation Studio. The second one allows editing the already
created building by modifying walls, material properties, internal gains etc. Another
==PAGE:40==

feature, TRNEdit allows the user to enter in the manual editing of TRNSYS where
parametric analyses can be made. Lastly, TRNSED allows users to develop executable
files which contain systems created in TRNSYS, that can be distributed along with

Thus, this thesis employs the use of TRNSYS for the creation of the PV/T and

the optimum flow determined and used in the simulations presented by Pean et al. in
[10]. For the second one, the flow required for achieving turbulent flow was selected
(Re>3000). However, according to the PV/T data sheet, the panels cannot withstand
such a high flow [40]. Moreover, due to the division of the flow into 15 pipes per
module, a very high flow rate is required to achieve turbulence. Still, for the sake
of discussion, the required flow was calculated and found to be 11000 kg/h with the
corresponding convective heat transfer coefficient from tube wall to collector fluid,
4188 W/m2.K, determined according to Appendix A.

30th of September, which is considered the cooling period. The outputs investigated
are the cooling power and the electrical energy output with respect to different col-
lector tilts. Three values were chosen for the angle at which the collectors can be
mounted: 0o, 45o and 90o. The value of 45o was chosen as it is the optimum collector
slope for Copenhagen. However, the other two, even though they will result in less

5.4
Conclusion - PV/T
A PV/T model was created in TRNSYS17 based on a previous study by Pean et al. of
a ”PV-therm polycristalline” WIOSUN product. Some improvements were brought
to the model in order to avoid pipe freezing during winter. Thus, changes were made
because of the replacement of water with a 40%/60% propylene-glycol/water mixture.
The new model was compared to the base one and afterwards employed for a para-
metric analysis involving two flow rates, a laminar one of 121 kg/h and a turbulent
one having a value of 11000 kg/h and three collector slopes, horizontal, vertical and
the optimum one for electricity production (45o) for Copenhagen, Denmark. More-
over, the supply temperature was varied between 7oC and 25oC in order to cover the
supply temperature ranges for air based cooling coils and high temperature cooling
systems.
From the comparison between the two models, lower values were found for the
specific cooling power and the cooling energy. It was also determined that TRNSYS17
underestimates the cooling power production when compared to reality, and a bigger
difference would occur in rainy days because of evaporation than the one stated in
the literature. In addition, the parametric analysis for Copenhagen, Denmark showed
that a turbulent flow would increase the cooling potential by approximately 36% but
it is not feasible due to the PV/T construction. Nevertheless, different collector slopes
lead to variations in both cooling and electricity production in the cooling season (1st
of May - 30th of September). As expected, the electricity production decreases by 7%
between the optimum and a horizontal orientation and by 21% when switching to a
vertical orientation from the optimum one. On the other hand, the cooling production
registers a reduction of 7%, this time from a vertical orientation to the optimum for
electricity production, and 13% from 45o to 0o. This leads to a maximum reduction
in electrical energy by 20 kWh/m2 and a maximum of 31 kWh/m2 for cooling for
the entire cooling season mainly due to incident solar radiation, amount which varies
between slopes.
The PV/T model was also employed after for the simulation of different climates
and with the results, cooling potential maps of Europe were developed. It was found
that cooling potential from night radiative cooling exists for all climates mentioned
but it is less for humid Mediterranean climates than for a humid continental one.
Moreover, a variation of cooling power with latitude was found, an increase being
registered from South to North. For a supply temperature of 21oC, the Pcool,spec
ranges from 35 W/m2 to 70 W/m2 while the cooling energy from 67 W/m2 to 165
W/m2 from Athens to Copenhagen for the entire cooling season.
By modelling the cooling demand for the cities where the PV/Ts were simulated,
it was determined that for cities such as Copenhagen, Denmark; Oslo, Norway and
Frankfurt, Germany, the cooling production of the PV/Ts is equal or even higher
than the entire cooling demand. On the other hand, for cities like Athens, Greece;
Bucharest, Romania; Madrid, Spain and Milan, Italy the PV/T cooling production
does not match the cooling demand.
Thus, water tanks are required because of
variation in cooling production due to the renewable nature of the technology for one,

but also for storing the additional cold water. An additional cooler is still necessary

Methodology
From January to May 2018, several experiments were made in order to study the
behaviour of radiant cooling panels and panels containing phase change material.
These experiments were conducted at the Technical University of Denmark in Kongens
Lyngby.
For both panel types, an experimental chamber located in the ICIEE Laboratory
was employed. Chamber 6, was thus designed as a two person office where cold water
was supplied in the ceiling with the purpose of analyzing their behaviour and to
compare their capability of providing an acceptable indoor thermal environment.
6.1
Experimental system
The experimental system consisted of three main parts: the climatic chamber rep-
resenting a two person office, the suspended ceiling which was comprised of either
radiant cooling panels or phase change material panels and the ceiling water supply
system.
The purpose of the experiment was to study the behaviour of the two panels with
the aim of providing an adequate thermal environment. The comparison between
the two was chosen as the radiant cooling panel is an already existing system on the
market while the phase change material panels are not yet widely spread. Moreover,
the radiant cooling panels cannot be coupled with the PV/Ts as they require wa-
ter circulation during the day in order to provide cooling while the PV/Ts produce
cold water during night. On the other hand, the PCM panels can transfer the cold
water demand to night, thus allowing the system to take advantage of the night ra-
diative cooling. Nevertheless, as the experiment was strictly aiming at following the
behaviour of the panels with respect to thermal comfort, no ventilation system was
employed.

7.4
Conclusion - RCP & PCM
The results from the experiments made on both the RCP and Tiles between January
and May 2018 were presented in the sections above. Both systems were analyzed in
terms of behaviour and with respect to their capability of providing an acceptable
indoor thermal environment for the occupants.
The study showed that the RCPs are able to provide a better thermal environment,
as the room is in CAT III of EN 15251 for 91% of the occupancy, while for only 48% in
the case of Tiles. This is definitely due to the maturity of the former technology, while
the latter requires further studies and improvements before reaching its optimum. In
addition, also with respect to the indoor thermal environment, both the RCP panels
and the PCM tiles managed to attain a category A temperature difference between
the ankles and abdomen of a seated person according to ISO 7730, showing a good
temperature distribution within the climate chamber.

The main differences found in the behaviour of the RCP and Tiles was the heat
flux distribution over the day. As the radiant cooling panels do not have the ability of
storing the heat, the heat gains and the water circulation must be done in the same
time span. However, in the case of Tiles, the phase change material stores the heat
during the day which can later be extracted during the night by the water circulation
through the piping system.
Still, investigations are required with respect to the
necessary quantity of PCM in order to provide an acceptable thermal environment.
Increasing the flow rate or running multiple water circulations during the day could
bring improvements with respect to this problem, but that is not the aim of this

PCM panel if discharging would be made through water circulated in the pipes. For
a better discharge, the pipes should be embedded in the panels allowing for an even
distribution of heat on the axis parallel to the thickness of the panel.
An important aspect of the study is that the experiments were made while ignoring
parameters such as ventilation which would be employed in reality.

the system would also help with the distribution of the heat within the room, thus
dividing the heat gains over the area of the ceiling as equally as possible. The addition
of ventilation would also provide benefits in the Tiles scenarios, as the manufacturer
suggests that air should be supplied in the plenum, which would then pass through
the orifices in the panel, supplying air into the room while at the same time providing
benefits in the discharge process.
Lastly, the chamber integrity must be verified.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
The aim of this thesis was to study the behaviour and the ability of providing an
acceptable indoor thermal environment with a system of photovoltaic-thermal col-
lectors coupled with indoor ceiling panels containing phase change material. Such a
system would lead to a higher utilization factor for the PV/Ts and would increase the
amount of energy use coverage by renewable sources. Nevertheless, the integration of
PCM is vital for the system as it increases the thermal mass of lightweight buildings
and it can facilitate the usage of cold water production from the PV/Ts by shifting
the cooling load at night.
Unfortunately, due to time and logistic issues, the coupling of the two compo-
nents was not possible. Thus, the study provides an extensive literature review of
both PV/Ts and PCM along with separate simulations and experiments of the two.
Consequently, Chapters 4 and 5 constitute a thorough description of the methodol-
ogy behind a simulation study of PV/Ts and the results and discussion with respect
to their cooling potential. Nevertheless, the PCM panels were analyzed in compar-
ison to an already existing system on the market, radiant cooling panels, within an
experimental study.
The first part showed that the PV/Ts have considerable potential with respect to
cooling energy production for different types of climates. Still, the main drawbacks
are related to the supply temperature provided to the PV/Ts and the changes made to
the validated model. Because the system was not coupled, a parametric analysis with
respect to the supply temperature was made which resulted in ranges for cooling power
and energy for the cooling season. Here, the maps provided are limited to cities and
no information is given with respect to larger areas. Nonetheless, an educated guess
based on specialized literature provided an approximation for the supply temperature
which led to more realistic values for the cooling power and energy respectively. With
this, an estimated coverage capability of the PV/Ts for the cooling demand of the
simulated office was determined.
Although it provides a categorization based on
climate which provides the reader with an idea about the technologies capabilities
and limitations, the implementation of different cooling seasons based on climate
would provide the reader with more realistic values. Moreover, the dimensioning part
also brings limitations to the coverage capability, as the demand was computed by
employing an ideal cooler, which does not represent a realistic cooling system. With
respect to the ideal cooler, an impact can be seen also in the dimensioning of the
PV/Ts, as it represents an estimation of the cooling demand for ideal conditions.
==PAGE:102==
84
8 Discussion
Nevertheless, the dimensioning was made for the worse night with regard to cooling
energy production of the PV/Ts, thus the overdimensioning of the system could
account for the differences that would occur due to realistic conditions.
A big impact on the study is related to the changes to which the model was
subject to. Although the changes were not extensive, and only due to the addition
of glycol in the water to prevent freezing of pipes, the model was not validated again
with experiments. Still, the results presented reasonable values when compared to
the specialized literature.
In the experiments part related to the RCP and PCM, the behaviour and the
capability of each component for providing an acceptable thermal environment was
analyzed. Still, some problems arise due to the unexpected behaviour of the entire
system. To start with, the chilling system which supplied cold water to the suspended
ceiling was not working within parameters. The supply temperature set-point was
set to 18oC while the system registered 20oC on average. The high accuracy of the
Kamstrup sensor which was measuring the inlet temperature led to the assumption
that either errors are occurring in measurements within the cooling machine or the
water within the pipes gains heat between the chilling machine and the point of
entry in the room. Also related to the control, within the experiments made for the
Tiles containing the PCM, a time delay occurred for water circulation start and stop.
Nevertheless, this was due to the PLC which did not account for the time change in
March, which is believed to have no influence over the system behaviour as it is only
a visual error.
Additionally, the measurements between the surface temperatures measured by
the PT1000 sensors and the ones measured by the heat flux sensors differed con-
siderably. While the PT1000 class A provided accurate measurements, the surface
temperature registered by the heat flux sensor was inaccurate but with little influ-
ence over the results due to the considerable number of different sensors employed.
However, an extra surface temperature sensor placed on the top side of some panels
would have further corroborated the heat flux measurements.
As the PCM content was not enough in the tiles or due to problems related to
the construction of the cooling system, the Tiles did not prove reliable in providing
an acceptable indoor environment. This could be nonetheless also because of the
cooling system employed and the tiles chosen, as this component is dedicated more to
increasing the thermal capacity of the building while requiring continuous ventilation
through the plenum than for load shift to the night, thus limiting their coupling
capability with the PV/Ts.
Finally, if the experiments were compared to the results of the simulation, the
cooling production of the PV/Ts could be sufficient for the discharge of the PCM
tiles. As presented, the average specific cooling power has a value of 70 W/m2, while
the specific cooling power supplied to the PCM tiles is always less than 60 W/m2 for
the reference case.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
This study shows that coupling PCM and PV/Ts into a system could be one way
for fighting climate change. Moreover, the combination between the two technologies
could help increase the share of renewables in buildings energy use.
As research shows, cooling potential exists, but as the collector slope plays a
role in both cooling and electricity for PV/Ts, a decision needs to be made with
respect to the application’s main purpose. However, cooling energy is registered even
for humid subtropical and hot-summer Mediterranean climates such as Milan, Italy
and Athens, Greece where the average cooling power is 47 W/m2 and 35 W/m2
respectively. Nevertheless, these values are not enough to cover the cooling demand,
thus, for these locations, an auxiliary chiller is mandatory. Still, the usage of PV/Ts
for cooling purposes would reduce the capacity of the required chillers. On the other
hand, for colder climates such as Northern Europe, where the average specific cooling
power reaches 66 to 70 W/m2, the PV/Ts could prove sufficient for covering the
cooling purposes, reducing considerably the required capacity of auxiliary chillers.
Even though this study showed that in some cases the cooling energy generated by
the PV/Ts can cover up to 20% more than the energy demand, auxiliary chillers could
still be required due to the renewable energy nature.
The experiments made showed that the RCPs outperformed the PCM tiles. A
CAT III (EN 15251) acceptable indoor environment was kept for 91% in the case
of RCPs compared to 48% in the case of PCMs during occupancy. Moreover, the
temperature difference between the ankles and abdomen of a seated person was below
2oC in both cases, being in accordance with category A of ISO 7730.
The capacity of the PCM tile proved insufficient for maintaining an adequate in-
door thermal environment and thus an improvement is required in its construction.
For a better distribution of heat in the panels, the pipes should be embedded. Nev-
ertheless, a higher thermal conductivity should be achieved in order to increase the
discharge efficiency. Ventilation should also be taken into consideration as it could
prove beneficial, especially for the PCM tiles presented.

CHAPTER 10
Further Studies
Within this study, the cooling potential of PV/Ts was analyzed for different locations
around Europe. With the aim of using their cold water production from night radia-
tive cooling, the possibility of coupling them to ceiling panels containing PCM for
achieving an acceptable indoor thermal environment was analyzed. The results from
the PV/Ts investigation showed promising results and their veracity was corroborated
with specialized literature. The PCM tiles were compared with an already existent
technology, radiant cooling panels, in order to study their behaviour. However, the
Tiles were not able to achieve the goal, as the same issues rose as in specialized
literature such as insufficient capacity, construction and heat conductivity.
Nevertheless, some improvements are required for the study at hand. First of all,
the chamber integrity should be investigated as high influence of the external tem-
perature was observed. Further investigations should be made with respect to both
PV/Ts and PCM panel construction, system coupling and time-span of experimental
and simulation studies expansion.
The system should be coupled and corroborated with experiments and simulations.
This would allow the investigation of PV/T cooling potential based on realistic and
precise values for the inlet water temperature. Furthermore, the study should expand
and analyze a realistic usage of a PV/T where cold water, hot water and electricity
production are employed with dedicated water tanks and water draw-offs schedules
for an entire year. Nevertheless, the reason for the difference between experiment
and simulation in cold water production for rainy days should be further examined.
On top of that, for the coupled system, an optimum control with respect to energy
savings and indoor thermal environment needs to be created as in early nights and
late mornings the PV/Ts cooling production is limited and employing the PV/Ts
during those time periods would result in an inefficient usage of the pumps.
A benchmark experiment should be made for the employed chamber with a sus-
pended ceiling implemented in order to keep the same air volume separation between
room and plenum. Then, with the cooling deactivated and only the heat gains active
with the same office schedule, a representative set of data would be found. Thus,
any improvement, small or big to the indoor thermal environment can be quantified
relative to the benchmark experiment. With respect to improvements in previous
investigations, ventilation should be added for the experiments involving the Ther-
macool product as their construction and data sheet suggest that the panels were
designed for plenum ventilation and no water circulation. Also, surface temperature
==PAGE:106==
88
10 Further Studies
sensors should be added on the back side of the panel (facing the plenum) and inside
for clearer results.
A new panel construction should also be pursued. For economic reasons, microen-
capsulation should be disregarded and a macroencapsulation technique undertaken
instead. To avoid heat conductivity issues and the partly charging/discharging of
the phase change material, investigations should be made with respect to optimum
thickness, improved conductivity with heat conductive meshes and embedded pipes
for a more uniform heat transfer. A cost analysis of the final product is required
along with reliability and safety tests to ensure that no problems related to corrosion
and fire arise.
For both the Thermacool Tiles and the new PCM panels developed, a CFD anal-
ysis should be developed upon the addition of ventilation systems. A comparison
between normal ventilation systems and the employment of the Coandă Effect on the
PCM panels should also be made.
Finally, with the system composed of PV/Ts and the new PCM panels up and
running, other scenarios are to be investigated. A study with no cold water storage
tanks, thus with the direct circulation of the cold water produced by the PV/Ts should
be established. In order to determine the system’s limits, extreme conditions with
respect to solar heat gains and flow rates cases must be analyzed. Nonetheless, yearly
simulations and field tests for different locations would further benefit the technology
and speed up its implementation.

This appendix comes as a supplement to the difference in cooling energy between
collector tilts for the investigation in Copenhagen, Denmark. In Figures B.4 and B.3
the trend over the entire week between the 6th and 14th of July can be observed.
Figure B.1 shows however the incident solar radiation for the 10th of July, one day of
the respective week.
Even though the peak is highest for a 45o tilt around midday, which explains the
higher electricity production than for the other tilts, the solar radiation incident on the
collector within the morning and evening is influencing the cooling energy production.
As the sun starts rising, less incident solar radiation lands on the collector at a vertical
orientation than for 45o and 0o, trend which continues until dusk except for a small
peak at noon. Moreover, the solar radiation incident on the collector is also higher
for 45oC than for 0o until 06:00, the same applying for the evening after 16:00. This
translates into lower heat gains from solar radiation, thus resulting in cooling energy
generation. This can be observed in Figure B.2 as the cooling energy production is
highest for a vertical orientation, while higher for 45o than for 0o for the same time
intervals respectively.
The pattern distinguished for this day can also be seen over the entire week and
it is most likely viable for the entire cooling period, 1st of May - 30th of September.
Because of this, the cooling energy over the cooling period is thus highest for a vertical
tilt, higher for 45o - optimum for electricity production and lowest for a 0o placement.

APPENDIX D
Parametric analysis in
TRNSYS 17
In this Appendix, a quick guide for doing parametric analysis is presented.
The
following guide is based on experience and the already TRNSYS guide present in the
documentation [52].This is a powerful tool, as multiple scenarios for the same system
can be simulated by only creating a base case. This gives the user the opportunity of
working faster and analyze the influence of different parameters without much effort.
The first step is for the user to create a simulation with all the desired components
in the Simulation Studio. The user friendly interface of the studio allows for a much
faster creation of the system without requiring any programming skills. Within the
simulation, an equation component needs to be created under the desired name (e.g.
”Simulation Parameters”) and in it, the parameters required for the analysis need to
be defined. Then, after the desired system is created and running without any errors
the input file needs to be created by selecting ”Create input file” under the drop-down
menu ”Calculate”. This will create the simulation ”.dck” file in the same folder, which
is needed for the next steps. It is important that the user solves any errors before
creating the ”.dck” file as error solving becomes harder when already in the further
described TRNEdit.
With the ”.dck” file created, the user opens it with TRNEdit. In TRNEdit, the
simulation is presented as code lines. Thus, the user must find the line - EQUATIONS
”Simulation Parameters”, and under a line with the word EQUATIONS followed by
the number of parameters defined in the ”Simulation Parameter” component previ-
ously created in the studio. A more experienced user could make other changes in
the ”.dck” file, without returning to the studio. This involves changing the simula-
tion start/stop time, tolerances etc., or even rewrite the code by changing values and
connections between components.
After making the desired changes, the user must save the file. Afterwards, by
clicking the ”New Table” under the ”Parametrics” drop-down menu, a selection menu
appears which allows the user to select the parameters which should be involved in
the respective run along with the number of runs. After clicking ok, a table appears
with the number of lines equal to the number of runs, and the column number equal
to the number of selected parameters. Here, the user can write the values by hand or
import an already made table in Microsoft Excel by pasting it in.
==PAGE:120==
102
D Parametric analysis in TRNSYS 17
Finally, with the use of the ”TRNSYS” drop-down menu the user selects the
option ”Run Table” which will run the desired scenarios. Then, the user can make
use of the new created output files, or of the already built-in function ”Plot” for fast
visualization of results.
