Abstract
This thesis investigates the interplay between app usage patterns and urban environments.
Using the comprehensive NetMob23 dataset of smartphone app usage recorded in 100x100m2
city tiles, we identified temporal activity patterns associated with land use classes such as
Nature, Residential, and Work. Self­Organizing Maps are applied to uncover these pat­
terns, which we clustered and analyzed to reveal the main temporal usage patterns. Then,
XGBoost was employed to validate these findings by predicting land use classifications
based on temporal app usage data.
The results highlight distinct digital patterns tied to urban functionality. Navigation apps
peak during commuting hours, aligning with Nature and Residential zones, while Business
apps dominate standard work hours in Work tiles. Leisure­oriented app categories such
as Entertainment and Social Media exhibit more scattered usage patterns. A compara­
tive analysis across 20 French metropolitan areas reveals a degree of universality in these
temporal patterns, indicating shared digital behaviors across various city contexts.
This research contributes to urban studies by integrating temporal app usage with land use
classification, offering a new perspective on the interplay between digital activity and urban
form.

Introduction
The digital age has transformed urban landscapes into dynamic hubs of activity. Mobile ap­
plications are increasingly central in modern life, shaping how individuals navigate, commu­
nicate, and interact with their environments [1, 2]. The data generated through app usage
presents an opportunity to uncover patterns of human behavior that reflect the functionality
and character of urban areas.
Despite the potential of app usage data to provide insight into urban dynamics, understand­
ing how digital activity aligns with physical environments remains a complex challenge [3].
App usage patterns, shaped by spatial and temporal factors, vary significantly across urban
areas and are influenced by land use, population density, and socioeconomic conditions [4].
To explore these dynamics, we utilize the NetMob23 dataset [5], a rich source of spatiotem­
poral app usage data, to uncover how digital activity interacts with urban environments.
This thesis investigates the relationship between mobile app usage patterns and urban en­
vironments, focusing on individual cities and comparisons between multiple metropolitan
areas. At its core, this research aims to uncover temporal trends in app usage that charac­
terize different urban areas, such as residential neighborhoods, work districts, and green
spaces, and to understand how these patterns vary over time. Using Lyon as a case study,
we use Self­Organizing Maps, an unsupervised machine learning technique, to identify app
usage patterns. These patterns are then clustered based on their distance to neurons on
the map, which allows us to connect app usage behavior to urban contexts.
In addition, XGBoost is used to evaluate the ability of app usage data to predict land use
classifications, such as distinguishing between residential and work areas. By integrating
predictive modeling, this research validates the clustering results and assesses the extent
to which app usage data can inform land use predictions.
Beyond Lyon, this study broadens its scope to explore app usage patterns across 20 French
metropolitan areas. This comparative analysis aims to identify recurring relationships be­
tween app usage and urban land use across diverse contexts. By examining whether pat­
terns observed in a single city extend to other urban areas, the study also seeks to uncover
broader insights into how app usage reflects urban dynamics.
This research bridges the gap between digital behavior and physical space. Uncovering
how app usage reflects and interacts with urban land use deepens our understanding of
the digital dynamics that shape modern cities. These findings offer valuable insights for
future work, including applications in urban planning and the analysis of human­environment
interactions.

1.1
Literature Review
This section reviews studies that use mobile app data for urban analysis, focusing on spa­
tiotemporal datasets for human behavior, clustering techniques to identify activity patterns,
and predictive models for land use classification. These studies help position this thesis
within mobile app usage and urban environments.
Integrating mobile phone data into urban studies has transformed the understanding of
urban dynamics, offering a dynamic, data­driven alternative to traditional methods. Foun­
dational research in this field demonstrates the value of spatiotemporal data in uncovering
patterns of human behavior and land use within urban environments.
Toole et al. [6] used Call Detail Records and supervised machine learning to analyze pop­
ulation distribution and activity across urban areas. Their approach identified clusters of
locations with similar land use and phone activity, highlighting how mobile data can reveal
actual land use beyond zoning regulations.
Lenormand et al. [7] combined network theory with mobile phone data to analyze land use
across Spanish cities. Using community detection methods, they classified urban zones into
functional areas like residential, business, and nightlife districts. Schelling­inspired models
were introduced to explore land use dynamics, uncovering links between urban structure
and transport systems. Their findings uncovered consistent spatial patterns across cities in
Spain.
De Nadai et al. [2] explored urban vibrancy in six Italian cities by correlating mobile phone
data with Points of Interest (POIs) and urban morphology. Their research demonstrated

how human mobility interacts with land use diversity, highlighting the role of urban structure
in shaping vibrant city life.
Li et al. [8] analyzed mobile app usage patterns by applying multi­way clustering to a large­
scale dataset, uncovering 508 spatiotemporal patterns that revealed the interplay of app

Further analyses have explored the spatiotemporal distribution of mobile applications across
cities, finding that some applications exhibit high spatial clustering while others, mainly
widely used services, show a more dispersed geographical footprint [12]. Correlations be­
tween land use indicators, such as residential and commercial areas, and temporal mobility
patterns have also been assessed, emphasizing the role of commuting flows in shaping app
usage [13].
Present Study
Building on prior studies that analyze urban dynamics using mobile data, this thesis com­
bines Self­Organizing Maps for temporal clustering with XGBoost for predictive modeling.
This approach enables the identification of fine­grained app usage patterns while validating
their link to urban land use classifications. SOM offers scalability for large datasets like
NetMob23 while providing interpretable visualizations of high­dimensional data, making it
a valuable alternative to traditional clustering methods like k­means [14].
XGBoost, a robust gradient­boosted decision tree model, validates the clustering results
by predicting land use classifications labeled as Nature, Residential, and Work. Recent
studies highlight XGBoost’s superior accuracy in urban analysis, with XGBoost outperform­
ing traditional models like Random Forest and Support Vector Machines in high­resolution
land use classification tasks [15]. Additionally, it has demonstrated exceptional accuracy in
urban land use change modeling, achieving 93% accuracy while uncovering critical trends
such as built­up area expansion and agricultural land decline [16].

Dataset
This chapter presents an overview of the datasets applied in this thesis. We describe the
characteristics of each dataset and the preprocessing steps undertaken. These datasets
form the basis for exploring the interaction between app usage patterns and urban en­
vironments, enabling insights into urban behavior’s spatial, temporal, and socioeconomic
dimensions.
The primary dataset is the NetMob23 app­usage dataset [5], presented in section 2.1, which
offers granular temporal and spatial information about how people interact with various
applications across urban landscapes. It provides detailed usage patterns for various apps
distributed across city tiles. Orange, a major mobile network operator in France with roughly
30% of the market penetration, provides the dataset. The data is captured by monitoring
4G network traffic through specialized probes, which track the uplink and downlink flows of
68 popular apps, associating them with specific base stations. These traffic volumes are
mapped onto a high­resolution 100×100m2 grid, providing anonymized and precise insights
into app usage patterns across urban and rural areas.
Several datasets, presented in section 2.2, were explored to obtain land use classifications
for city tiles. These include OpenStreetMap (OSM) data, the Copernicus Land Cover Data,
and the Corine Land Cover data. Among these datasets, we chose the Corine Land Cover
data for its accuracy and alignment with the spatial granularity of the NetMob23 dataset.
Additional datasets, described in section 2.3, were incorporated to examine the character­
istics of the city tiles and enhance the interpretability of app­usage patterns. These include
the OSM road network data, which offers metrics related to urban infrastructure, includ­
ing the density of road intersections, and the INSEE demographic dataset, which provides
socioeconomic indicators such as income levels and poverty rates.

1. NetMob23: The core dataset for app­usage analysis details its structure and prepro­

2. Classification Datasets: A comparative exploration of datasets used to classify tiles
by land use. It concludes with the selection of the Corine Land Cover data.
3. Analysis Datasets: Used to explore infrastructure and socioeconomic patterns in

2.1
NetMob23
The NetMob23 dataset [5] serves as the core dataset for this thesis. It is an extensive
collection of anonymized smartphone app­usage records combining 15­minute intervals
with 100 × 100m2 tiles. It allows a detailed exploration of urban app­usage patterns across
20 major metropolitan areas in France.
The key features of the dataset include:
• Apps: Usage data for 68 different smartphone applications that cover a variety of
types, such as social media, navigation, and gaming. Together, they are responsi­
ble for about 70% of France’s total network traffic, as observed by Orange telecom.
Please refer to appendix A for a comprehensive listing of these applications.
• Temporal Scope: The dataset monitors 77 days, from 16th March 2019 – 31th May
2019, with data captured every 15 minutes.
• Spatial Resolution: The dataset is aggregated at the city tiles level, each represent­
ing a 100×100m2 area. Each tile is identified by a unique tile_id with its corresponding
geographic coordinates.
• Coverage: The dataset covers 20 of France’s largest metropolitan areas. For the full
city list, please refer to appendix B.
2.1.1
Preprocessing
Although the dataset was cleaned before analysis by the data providers in [5], we applied
further preprocessing steps to ensure consistency for the analysis. They are summarized
below.
Selection of Full Weeks
To maintain consistent weekday patterns, only complete weeks
from Monday to Friday were kept. Specifically, the period is from March 18th to May 24th,
as this is the first Monday and the last Friday, respectively. This period selection ensured
uniform weekday coverage without partial weeks.
Exclusion of Anomaly Weeks
Some weeks were excluded due to known anomalies
highlighted in the NetMob23 article:
• Week 13 (March 25th–31st): Excluded due to the Daylight Saving Time (DST) change
on March 31st caused a one­hour inconsistency.
• Week 19 (May 6th–12th): Excluded due to a nationwide network outage on May 12th.

• Week 21 (May 20th–26th): Excluded due to significant traffic reduction in southeast­
ern France between May 22nd and 25th.
Granularity:
The data was kept at its original 15­minute intervals to preserve the highest
level of temporal detail. This approach allows flexibility in later stages.
Daily Aggregation:
We aggregated the data daily, producing 96 timestamps per app,
each representing a 15­minute interval throughout the day. The usage downlink values
were summed across all days for each tile and app to create a representative daily usage
pattern.
We decided to keep the 15­minute intervals as it allows flexible aggregation and detailed
time series analyses, depending on the use case.

Copernicus Land Cover Data
The Copernicus Land Monitoring Service (CLMS) [19] is part of the European Union’s Earth

The Land Cover Data provides detailed classifications for natural areas, agricultural zones,

visualized in fig. 2.1 (middle), where residential and urban tiles are underrepresented.
Urban areas were underrepresented in the dataset, which primarily focused on natural fea­

This dataset was ultimately deemed unsuitable for this thesis due to its limited coverage of
urban features.

category. Thanks to the granularity of this data set, we can group the data into three cat­

dataset into the 20 metropolitan areas covered in the NetMob23 data.

100×100m2 tile. In cases where a tile intersected multiple land cover polygons, we assigned
the classification that covered the most significant area within the tile.

2.3.1
OSM Road Intersection Data
We used OSM data to analyze road networks by exploring the relationship between app­
usage patterns and walkability, as discussed in section section 4.5. Then, we extracted

the data using the osmnx library [22], which provides tools for retrieving and analyzing OSM
data. We specifically focused on the walking layer networks, including only pedestrian­
accessible streets and paths. Additionally, we analyzed urban walkability by examining
the density of intersections within city tiles, exploring the relationship between app­usage
patterns and the density of walkable networks.
The density of intersections in an area is a widely used metric for assessing walkability [23,
2]. Higher intersection densities naturally correlate with:
• Increased Connectivity: More intersections mean shorter block lengths and more
pedestrian route options.
• Pedestrian­Friendly Environments: Areas with dense street networks tend to be
more navigable on foot and may encourage walking over vehicle use.
• Urban Activity: High intersection densities are typically found in urban centers with
mixed land use and higher population densities.
By analyzing the intersection density within city tiles, we aim to understand how app usage
patterns relate to different areas’ walkability and urban characteristics.
2.3.1.1
Preprocessing
To extract the network of a city, we first obtained the city boundary as a polygon. Then,
we used the polygon as shape to extract pedestrian­accessible streets and paths using
osmnx. The key components from the network were nodes and edges, where edges are
road segments connected by the nodes. The focus was on identifying intersections based
on the degree of nodes. All nodes with a degree less than three were removed, as a degree
of 2 is a regular road without any intersection, and a degree of 1 is a dead­end.
Then, we performed a spatial join between the nodes and the 100 × 100m2 NetMob23 city
tiles. This process grouped nodes by their respective tiles, counting the intersections within
each tile.
2.3.1.2
Addressing Discrepancies
While preprocessing, some tiles showed anomalous behavior by showing over 90 intersec­
tions within a single tile. When inspecting these anomalies, we noticed they were associated
with public buildings such as libraries and hospitals, where internal paths were included in
the extracted network. To resolve this, we excluded all tiles with intersections above 90 and
determined the threshold through manual observation of outliers.
An example of this discrepancy is illustrated in Figure fig. 2.4, which shows a side­by­side
comparison of the OSM terrain view and the extracted network for a tile with an anomaly.
This figure shows how all the intersections come from within the Building.

3.3
Clustering of App Usage Patterns
To explore app usage patterns across urban landscapes, we utilized an unsupervised clus­
tering approach using the Self­Organized Map (SOM). This method aims to uncover hidden
patterns in app usage that naturally align with different types of urban areas, such as Na­
ture, Residential, or Work zones. We aim to reveal app usage patterns related to specific
urban features by clustering time­series data.
3.3.1
Theory
The Self­Organizing Map (SOM) was developed by Kohonen in 1982 [25] and is a machine
learning technique for unsupervised learning that uses Competitive Learning, where nodes
compete for the right to respond to a given input data, instead of error­correction learning,
which updates all neurons based on an error signal to reduce prediction errors. SOM is
particularly useful for visualizing high­dimensional data by projecting them onto a lower­
dimensional grid while preserving the topological structure in the data.
The Self­Organizing map (SOM) is a map of neurons, each identified by a unique coordinate
(i, j), typically on a two­dimensional grid. Each neuron is associated with a weight vector
with the same dimensionality as the input data. The SOM uses a distance metric, usually
the Euclidean distance, to measure the similarity between neurons and the input data and
neighboring neurons on the grid. During training, the weight of the best­matching neuron
and its neighboring neurons are updated to better align with the input data. The SOM
algorithm can be summarized in the following steps:
1. Initialization: The weight vectors of the neurons are initialized, typically by using
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to improve the training speed.

Discussion
This chapter reviews the findings and places them in a broader context. We will address

5.1
Limitations of the Data
NetMob Data
The NetMob dataset is extensive and provides a rich spatiotemporal app
usage data source. However, the temporal scope is limited to weeks 12 to 21, covering
the Spring season. While this period offers insights into typical app usage patterns, it does
not account for seasonal variations that may influence the spatial distribution of app us­
age. Specifically, during warmer months, Social_Media may be used more frequently in
Nature tiles, while in colder months, the usage can shift more dominantly to Residential
tiles. Therefore, the observed patterns in this thesis reflect app usage in spring.
Corine Land Cover Data
The Corine Land Cover dataset provides a valuable reference
yet has shortcomings. While the classification is detailed, we treat it as an absolute truth. In
reality, many areas labeled as Residential, Work, or Nature are more nuanced. Specifically,
Work combines various industrial and commercial land uses (see table 2.2) rather than a
uniform, job­centric area.
Moreover, the classification system can struggle with mixed or transitional areas, where
residential neighborhoods border commercial and industrial zones. Such overlaps chal­
lenge the assumption that each tile aligns neatly within a single land use class. We also
struggled with this when attempting to classify the city tiles extracted from OSM data (see
section 2.2.1). In these cases, most of the borders between Nature and Building areas were
mixed because of the overlap of the labels.

Changes in App Usage Over Time in Relation to Urban Areas
The predictive model, trained on SOM­derived clusters, quantifies how temporal app us­
age patterns correlate with urban land use classes. With an average accuracy of 74%, the
model significantly outperforms random guessing. F1­scores of 0.77 for Nature, 0.74 for
Residential, and 0.69 for Work tiles confirm that temporal app usage variations are infor­
mative enough to distinguish between urban contexts.
The confusion matrix reveals symmetrical misclassifications between Residential and Na­
ture tiles, with 4,178 Residential tiles predicted as Nature and 3,953 Nature tiles predicted
as Residential. This symmetry suggests that the model sometimes confuses these two
classes, likely due to overlapping temporal patterns.
For the Work class, misclassifications are evenly distributed between Nature (1,497) and
Residential (1,565), indicating that the model does not perceive Work as being closer to one
specific class. These patterns highlight that while the model captures class distinctions well
overall, it struggles with subtle differences, particularly between Nature and Residential.
SHAP analyses further support these findings by revealing how key clusters strongly influ­
ence predictions for each class. For instance, Navigation clusters strongly impact Nature
and Residential tiles. The impact of Cluster 1 from Navigation on Nature aligns with our
analysis in section 5.2.1, where we observed that the majority of city tiles in Cluster 1 be­
long to the Nature class. For Residential prediction, the impact of Cluster 0 from Navigation

5.2.3
Universal Patterns Across Cities in Terms of App Usage
Navigation–Nature
In the Navigation–Nature case (fig. 4.7), the positive clusters exhibit
distinct peaks in the early morning (around 07:00) and late afternoon (around 16:00), re­
flecting commuting activity through Nature tiles. These tiles mainly represent areas in the
city’s periphery, where Residential zones are often surrounded by Nature tiles. As dis­
cussed in 5.2.1, Cluster 1 in the Navigation category is primarily associated with Nature
tiles in peripheral areas. This spatial distribution, combined with its residual time­series
peak at 07:00, strongly suggests a connection to morning commutes. This pattern, con­
sistently observed across city clusters, highlights the role of Navigation apps in supporting
daily mobility through Nature tiles.
The negative clusters display an inverse trend during the morning hours, characterized by
a sharp drop in activity around 07:00. After midday, these clusters stabilize with residual
values close to zero, indicating that their primary shared characteristic is the absence of
significant Navigation activity during the early morning commute. This lack of early morning
usage differentiates these tiles from those classified as Nature, supporting the notion that
commuting activity is a key temporal marker for this category. These findings align well with
the anticipated temporal dynamics of Nature tiles.
Navigation–Residential
In the Navigation–Residential case (fig. 4.8), the positive clus­
ters exhibit a trough around 07:00, followed by a gradual increase in activity from midday
into the evening. This reflects residential areas becoming more active as residents return
home, with a pronounced increase in activity after 16:00, coinciding with the end of the typ­
ical workday. The consistency of this evening­oriented curve across cities underscores its
role as a distinctive marker of urban residential life.
The negative clusters display an inverse trend, with a pronounced peak around 07:00, re­
flecting higher Navigation activity in the morning, when residential areas are quieter as
residents leave for work or other activities. Later in the day, the negative clusters show a

sharp decline in activity after 18:00, contrasting with the increase in evening activity in the
positive clusters. This inverse relationship suggests a universal pattern across cities: Res­
idential tiles are likely to exhibit lower Navigation activity in the early morning and higher
activity in the late evening.
Additionally, the pronounced deviation of negative clusters from zero indicates their strong
contribution to the classification. Specifically, a Residential tile is less likely to be classified
as Navigation if it demonstrates higher activity in the morning and lower in the evening,
further reinforcing the distinct temporal dynamics observed in residential app usage.
Business–Work
In the Business–Work case (fig. 4.9), the positive clusters consistently
exhibit a bell­shaped activity curve, peaking during standard working hours (08:00 to 17:00).
This pattern reflects an apparent and predictable rhythm of app usage, strongly associating
Work tiles with working activities across cities. The consistency of this trend underscores
its universality in identifying work­related contexts.
In contrast, the negative clusters demonstrate substantial variability. While some resemble
positive clusters with a bell­shaped distribution, others follow an inverse pattern, character­
ized by lower activity during typical working hours and peaks in the early morning and late
evening. This inconsistency among the negative clusters makes it challenging to establish
a cohesive trend. Nevertheless, the prevalence of the positive clusters underscores their
critical function in capturing the temporal patterns that define Work tiles.
Summary
Across the three cases, Navigation–Nature, Navigation–Residential, and Business–
Work, the results demonstrate consistent universal patterns in app usage. The positive
clusters consistently reflect distinct temporal behaviors: morning and afternoon peaks for
Nature tiles, evening­oriented activity for Residential tiles, and a bell­shaped workday curve
for Work tiles. These trends align with human routines such as commuting, working hours,
and leisure periods.
While more variable, the negative clusters also provide insights into deviations from these
patterns. In the Business–Work case, their inconsistent structure contrasts sharply with the
well­defined positive clusters, highlighting the dominance of workday app usage patterns
in identifying Work tiles. Similarly, in the Navigation–Nature and Navigation–Residential
cases, the inverse patterns in negative clusters complement the positive clusters by further
distinguishing tiles that deviate from the expected class.
These findings strongly indicate the existence of universal app usage patterns across cities.
While local variations and inconsistencies in negative clusters are present, the recurring
trends in positive clusters suggest that temporal app usage patterns are shaped by widely
shared urban routines, supporting the hypothesis of partially universal app usage behaviors.

5.3
Future Work
This thesis opens several opportunities for further exploration and refinement. Incorporating
demographic or socioeconomic data could help clarify the influence of age and income on
app usage patterns. For instance, specific app categories may exhibit distinct temporal
trends in neighborhoods with varying income levels or demographic structures, providing
richer explanations for the observed patterns.
Methodologically, exploring models that directly incorporate spatial relationships is a logical
next step. While XGBoost proved effective for classification tasks, it treats each tile inde­
pendently, ignoring potential spatial autocorrelation. Utilizing spatial statistics, graph­based
models, or geospatial deep learning techniques could better capture the physical structure
of cities. Potential approaches include graph neural networks (GNN) or hierarchical clus­
tering frameworks that assess the connectivity of tile arrangements, potentially improving
predictive accuracy and interpretability.
A deeper exploration of edge cases—like mixed­use zones or rapidly evolving urban dis­
tricts could contribute to our understanding of app usage dynamics. This might involve
incorporating finer­grained land use data or higher­resolution temporal windows to track
short­term trends and anomalies.
Lastly, a limitation of this thesis is that we focused exclusively on Monday–Friday data, ex­
cluding weekends. Extending the analysis to Saturdays and Sundays could reveal distinct
usage patterns, potentially reflecting leisure activities or other weekend­specific behaviors.
This could provide a more comprehensive understanding of app usage dynamics through­
out the week, including how Navigation or Social Media might shift during non­work periods.
Exploring Socioeconomic Factors in App Usage
As an extension of this research, we
investigated how app usage patterns and predictive performance could differ in socioe­
conomically disadvantaged areas. Using 200x200m2 grid­level socioeconomic data from
INSEE [35], we calculated poverty ratios for individual city tiles and applied HDBSCAN [36]
to cluster tiles into neighborhoods in Lyon based on their poverty levels.
We computed weighted F1­scores for each neighborhood to evaluate the model perfor­
mance across these clusters and compared them to the citywide F1­score. This analysis
aimed to reveal spatial variations in predictive accuracy, particularly within lower­income
neighborhoods. A detailed explanation of the results is provided in appendix I.
Although this preliminary analysis was not directly incorporated into the thesis objectives,
it offers a direction for future research. Investigating app usage behavior in disadvantaged
neighborhoods could offer insights into socioeconomic patterns.

Conclusion
In this thesis, we aimed to understand how app usage patterns correlate with diverse urban
areas. Using spatiotemporal data aligned with categorized land use labels, we analyzed
how patterns emerged across various app categories and examined whether these patterns
were consistent nationally rather than representing local idiosyncrasies.
We performed a comprehensive analysis using the NetMob23 dataset, which contains tem­
porally granular data of mobile app usage grouped by application. First, we aggregated
weekday data into hourly features. Then, we utilized the Self­Organizing Map to identify
distinct temporal time­series patterns for each app category and link them to specific land
use areas. This clustering step was followed by predictive modeling using XGBoost, which
validated the clustering results by demonstrating that the temporal patterns showed clear
usage behaviors and held predictive value. Finally, we broadened our perspective by per­
forming a comparative analysis across 20 French metropolitan areas to assess the degree
of universality in these observed patterns.
The Self­Organizing Map revealed distinct temporal usage signatures associated with spe­
cific land use classes. Specifically, Navigation­related apps consistently peaked during
morning and evening commuting hours in Nature and Residential areas, and Business­
related apps peaked throughout the conventional workday hours (08:00–16:00). Similar sig­
natures were identified for other app categories, confirming that usage patterns frequently
matched intuitive real­world urban functions. We used the XGBoost classification to vali­
date these observations further, showing that the identified temporal patterns can predict
land use classifications with notably higher accuracy than a simple baseline model. Finally,
our analysis of multiple French cities indicated that these temporal usage patterns exhibited
a degree of universality.
We also acknowledge some limitations, as we assumed each city tile corresponds predom­
inantly to a single land use type, which is a simplification given the density, complexity, and
mixed nature of many urban landscapes. Although the cross­city comparison offered valu­
able insights into the generalization of these patterns, the selection of app category–land
use cases was necessarily constrained, and some temporal trends showed variation rather
than perfect uniformity.
The findings of this research offer practical significance for future exploration by providing
tools and insights for future studies in urban analysis.

KMeans Clustering for Class
Determination
To address the limitations of regression, we considered transforming the problem into a
classification task by categorizing the intersection counts into discrete classes. To deter­
mine the optimal number of classes and their boundaries, we utilized KMeans clustering,
where we tested cluster sizes ranging from 2 to 9. The assessment was determined by the
Silhouette Score of the clusters [37], which measures how similar a data point is to its own
cluster compared to other clusters.
The optimal number of clusters was found to be 2 based on the highest silhouette score.
We then plotted the distribution of intersection counts across the KMeans­derived clusters,
as seen in fig. G.1.
Figure G.1: Distribution of intersections across clusters. The left cluster has tiles with inter­
sections ranging from 0 to around 10, while the right cluster has intersections ranging from
around 10+.
We then executed the Classification Model on this data, where the target variables were
set to the corresponding cluster values. This gave us the results provided in table G.1 and
fig. G.2. Here we observe how the precision of Class 0 is 0.98, which is incredibly high, while
Class 1 has a precision of 0.18, and a F1­score of 0.30. This makes the model insensible,
as it seems to be determined entirely around Class 0’s performance.
Using KMeans clustering to determine class boundaries did not produce interpretable or
useful classes.

Justification of SOM over Raw Data
The raw data models, even with significantly fewer features, achieve higher accuracy per
feature compared to the SOM data model. This suggests that statistical features extracted
from raw data may retain more predictive power per feature than SOM cluster labels. Al­
though SOM clustering reduces dimensionality, it does not necessarily provide better ac­
curacy per feature compared to raw data models that use statistical features.
The primary aim of this thesis is to uncover and understand patterns and trends in app usage
across different urban landscapes. To address this, we used Self­Organizing Maps (SOM)
to analyze and cluster app usage data. The SOM effectively revealed meaningful patterns
by grouping similar time­series app usage profiles, allowing us to interpret the underlying
structures in the data.
Building upon these insights, we used the SOM clusters as input features to predict land­
use classifications (Nature, Residential, Work). To evaluate the effectiveness of the SOM
approach, we compared it with models using raw time­series data and statistical features.

Work on Poverty areas
Here we describe the analysis of socioeconomic patterns in Lyon’s city tiles and their rela­
tionship with predictive model performance. We used the INSEE dataset containing 200x200m2
grid­level city tiles and clustered them into socioeconomic neighborhoods based on poverty
ratios.
The poverty ratio for each tile was computed as follows:
PovertyRatio = men_pau
men
(I.1)
where men_pauv represents the number of poor households and men represents the total
number of households within the city tile. A spatial join was performed to merge Lyon’s city
tiles with the poverty­level grid.
Then, to identify neighborhoods with similar socioeconomic characteristics, we applied
HDBSCAN, which is a density­based clustering algorithm, using both the spatial coordi­
nates of the tiles to account for spatial proximity, and their corresponding poverty ratios.
The resulting clusters of the HDBSCAN are shown in fig. I.1. In total, there were 2139
different poverty ratios, and 2207 different clusters.
To assess the predictive performance of the XGBoost model across these poverty­defined
neighborhoods, we computed the weighted F1­score for each cluster. The F1­score were
compared to the city overall F1­score to evaluate spatial variations in prediction accuracy.
The comparison of F1­scores is shown in fig. I.2.
