Abstract
Wave energy is one of the possible solutions for meeting the future energy demand in a clean and sus-
tainable way. Extracting large amounts of energy, a wave energy device would be subjected to extreme
and fatigue loads from the waves. Designing such a device, a trade oﬀneeds to be done between making
a device that is strong enough to withstand the loads and on the same time not too heavy making it
ineﬃcient and too costly. Having good estimations of extreme and fatigue loads are therefore critical
when designing an eﬃcient wave energy device. This thesis has aimed to create a tool that can be used
between the already existing hydrodynamic and solid mechanic models available at CorPower Oceean.
The goal has been that the tool shall extract the extreme and fatigue loads from the hydrodynamic model
and format them in a way so that they can be used in the solid mechanical model.
Four diﬀerent tools have been created and compared for calculating fatigue using amplitude and spectral
methods, where the amplitude methods also are able to estimate extreme loads. The fatigue tools have
been evaluated against each other in a simple example showing that the estimated accumulated fatigue
damage can be decreased by using several variables. An application of the tools has been done on a
critical sub system of the wave energy device developed by CorPower Ocean. Where in this application
critical points against extreme loading and fatigue have been localized. A new design has been suggested
based on the strength analysis from the ﬁrst one. Increasing the number of variables and using the tools
developed in this thesis can signiﬁcantly improve the fatigue damage estimations of the system. What
fatigue method to use depends on the details for each case.
Keywords: Renewable energy, wave power, wave energy device, wave energy converter, wave scatter,
oﬀshore structures, design load case, hydro dynamics, solid mechanics, extreme loading, fatigue, am-
plitude methods, spectral methods, Rainﬂow Cycles, Rainﬂow Matrix, accumulated damage, statistical
analysis, Rainﬂow Projection, ﬁnite element analysis.

Chapter 1
Background
1.1
Wave energy
With a steadily growing energy demand and an increasing concern over diﬀerent climate issues, consum-
ing clean energy has become increasingly important. Where the sector of renewable energy has grown
rapidly during the last decades. Large amount of research have been put into solar cells increasing their
eﬃciency. Wind turbines have been able to reduce their cost of producing energy and prototypes of
oﬀshore ﬂoating wind turbines are being tested which opens up for large possibilities of harvesting energy
at deeper waters [1],[2]. Other renewable energy concepts such as tidal turbines and wave energy devices
are also being developed to use the large potential of ocean energy, [3].
It is the sun that creates the wind and the wind that creates the waves. In the classical theory of entropy
this would mean that the sun has higher entropy than wind, which has higher entropy than the waves.
This could perhaps explain the complexity of taking energy from the diﬀerent sources. Solar energy has
been used for millenniums by the plants on the earth. Windmills and wind turbines have been used
for centuries in the modern world. While taking out the energy from ocean waves has until today not
been made economically feasible. The idea of entropy can of course not explain all of this but it is an
interesting aspect to keep in mind when developing wave energy. Harvesting the energy in waves has a
large potential. Waves are relatively stable, contains a large amount of energy and are often located far
away from land. The mean energy ﬂux in a wave can be calculated by,
¯E = 1
2ρgA2Vg.
Where ρ is the density, g the gravitational constant, A the wave amplitude, and Vg is the wave group
velocity [4]. Having a 1 km2 of deep water ocean with A=3m, Vg=3.5 m/s the waves on this area contains
∼11 MWh. This is approximately what 161 Swedish households uses daily,[5]. With a total world ocean
area on approximately 361.900.000 km2 it is clear that the potential for wave energy is large, [6].
The machine catching energy of waves is often called a wave energy device (WED) or wave energy con-
verter (WEC). There are and have been many prototypes of diﬀerent devices. These prototypes can
broadly be sorted into three types as displayed in Figure(1.1.1), 1) overtopping devices, 2) oscillating
water column, 3) point absorbers,[7]. This thesis have been done in collaboration with CorPower Ocean
(CPO) where the WED they are designing is a mechanical point absorber.

1.2
System overview
The WED developed by CPO is a complex system containing several diﬀerent modules. Some of the main
modules of the WED have been illustrated in Figure(1.2.1). The WED is a deep water buoy oscillating
at the surface, where this wave induced motion is transformed to electricity. The centre of the WED
consist of the PTO system which takes the translational motion from the buoy to rotational motion for
the generators and the wave springs that make the buoy oscillate out of phase with the waves. The
buoy and ﬂoating systems are connected through the piston rod to the mooring line which in return are
connected to the anchor keeping the buoy in place during operation.

1.3
CorPower Oceans design process
The WED developed by CPO has not yet been produced in full scale, but a 1:12 and 1:16 scale models
have been created and tested. At the moment a 1:2 scale model is being designed. In this design phase
a large amount of work have been done to create a hydrodynamic simulation model. Where this model
describes the behaviour and forces on the system in irregular waves. The hydrodynamical model has
been veriﬁed by experimental tank testing on the 1:12 and 1:16 models. The hydro mechanical model
is used as basis for the design, it gives motions and forces as input for the solid mechanical strength
analysis of the system. The design process at CPO can be seen as an iterative process, where results
from the hydrodynamic model will give input for the solid mechanical model. Where the result from
the solid mechanical model might led to a new iteration of the design. This design process have been
illustrated in Figure(1.3.1). Where depending on the evaluation/redesign a new design can either lead
to a large iteration where a new hydrodynamic simulation needs to be done or to a small iteration using
the solid mechanic models. The large iteration is done if the redesign have an eﬀect that might change
the behaviour and forces on the WED in the hydrodynamic model.
Figure 1.3.1: A schematic picture of the design process used at CPO.
During its life time the WED is going to be exposed to a number of diﬀerent load cases. Where some of
these will lead to extreme loads on the WED, and others will lead to fatigue loads. The system therefore
needs to be designed to withstand and survive both extreme and fatigue loads. As of today CPO does
not have an eﬃcient process of extracting extreme loads and does not have a speciﬁc method of analysing
fatigue loads obtained from the hydrodynamic model.
Today the fatigue of the WED is calculated by taking the worst load case scenario and applying this load
with a constant amplitude for 106 times (inﬁnite lifetime). This is a conservative way of dimensioning for
fatigue where the average load most likely is far from the maximum load. Designing the structure like this
probably means that the structure is over dimensioned, meaning extra weight and cost. A more accurate
way of calculating the fatigue damage of the WED would be to take into account the irregularities from
the waves and by so also more irregularities in the loads on the WED.

For the WED developed by CPO it is important to have a low inertia for the system to be able to eﬃ-
ciently extract energy from the waves. Reducing or limiting weight of the structure is thus an important
factor when designing the WED. Reducing weight could also lead to a more cost eﬃcient system, with
less material, easier handling etc.. Something that is crucial when trying to prove that the concept of
wave energy works and can become cost eﬃcient.
1.4
Purpose & objectives
The Purpose of this thesis have been to develop a tool that takes the varying forces from irregular waves
that the WED might encounter and creates extreme and fatigue loads from these that can be used in
the strength analysis. This in practice have meant using the large amount of data produced by the CPO
hydrodynamic model developed in Matlab/Simulink, processing it and export extreme and fatigue loads
that can be used in the solid mechanical model SolidWorks-Simulation. As described by Figure(1.4.1),
this is a tool between the hydrodynamic and solid mechanical model. A tool to simplify and improve the
current design process.
Figure 1.4.1: The purpose of this thesis is to create a tool that takes the data from the hydrodynamic model
processes and creates extreme and fatigue loads that are usable in the solid mechanical model.
To reach the purpose of the thesis a number of objectives have been set that are listed below.
1. Create a tool for a general load case that can be used with small modiﬁcations for other Matlab
users. The tool should take the data from the hydrodynamic model, reduce it and format extreme
and fatigue loads in the way that it can be used for strength analysis in SolidWorks. (Chapter 4)
2. Develop and use fatigue tools based on diﬀerent methods for a simpliﬁed example. Compare and
evaluate the methods against each other and current methods. (Chapter 4)
3. Do a load and strength analysis on some subsystem of the WED with results from the developed
tool. Compare this results with current methods used by CPO. Based on the outcome of the analysis
do a redesign of the part to improve the system. (Chapter 5)
1.5
Disposition of report
This report consist of seven separate parts Background, Theory, CPO models, Fatigue methods & tools,
Application , Conclusion and Appendices. The theory chapter ﬁrst handles the background theory that
have been used throughout the thesis, but also important theory for extreme loading and fatigue. Some
of the more detailed theory of fatigue for multiple variables and in the frequency domain is described in
the Appendices(B)-(C). In the CPO models some details about the models and system used by CPO is
described, this is since the models have had a large inﬂuence on how the tools in the thesis have been
formed. The fatigue methods & tools chapter presents and explains the methodology of four diﬀerent
fatigue tools that have been created for estimating fatigue loads, 1) Single variable method 2) RP direct
method 3) RP two step method 4) Spectral method. The four diﬀerent fatigue tools have been evaluated
on a simple geometry with some loads from the hydrodynamic model developed by CPO. With this simple
example the fatigue methods have been compared to each other.

Chapter 2
Theory
2.1
Deﬁnitions and background theory
This section goes through some deﬁnitions and general naval architecture theory such as scaling laws
and wave scatters that have been central through out the thesis. It also goes through more background
theory speciﬁc for wave energy and how design load cases can be deﬁned for this. It also presents theory
about reduction factors that are used in strength analysis for both extreme loads and fatigue.
2.1.1
Coordinate system
The coordinate system used to throughout the project contains three translational degrees of freedom
(Heave, Sway and Surge) and three rotational (Roll, Pitch, and Yaw) as described in Figure(2.1.1).
Figure 2.1.1: The coordinate system used for the WED. Picture from [8].
The WED developed by CPO is axially symmetric on the outside meaning that the the two rotational
motions roll and pitch and the two translational motions surge and sway would be the same. This is
true for the outer hydro mechanical loads, however for the inner loads the WED is not axi-symmetric.
Meaning that some simpliﬁcations have to be done when calculating the structural mechanical loads in
the unsymmetrical inner part.

2.1.4
Design load cases
The WED will be exposed to several diﬀerent design load cases (DLC), where each load case can be
deﬁned by two main factors, 1) Operational mode of the WED, including factors such as the power pro-
duction, faults and emergency modes. 2) Environmental mode including factors such as wave height and
ice in the water. Essentially combining these two factors one can obtain almost an inﬁnite amount of load
cases. Where analysing all these fast becomes unpractical if not impossible. To limit the number of load
cases and make this practically possible regulatory bodies suggest a number of operational modes for the
DLC that needs to be calculated. Where these load cases typically handles cases as, power production,
start up, emergency shut down, survival, damage stability, transportation etc., [11]. The environmental
factors would typically be determined by variables such as wave height(Hs), wave period (Tp) and the
direction of the waves(θ).
Guidelines for oﬀshore designs [12][13], typically divide the loads into ultimate limit states (ULS) which
can be seen as states limited by extreme loads, loads that might break the WED after one impact. Fatigue
limit states (FLS) which are the many loads occurring during a longer time leading to fatigue damage.
Accidental limit states (ALS) and serviceability limit states (SLS). Only ULS and FLS have been treated
in this project. In this thesis the extreme loads have included simulations from the worst case scenario, a
50 year’s storm. But also the loads that might occur during the more common operational modes. The
fatigue loads have been based on simulation data from 25-108 sea states including normal, detuned and
survival modes for a fully functional WED as operational modes.
The operational proﬁle of a WED, depending on wave height and period could typically look like in Fig-
ure(2.1.4). Where three operational modes are displayed in colours, all modes assuming that the WED
will be fully functional.

2.1.5
Reduction factors
Depending on certain properties and ﬁeld of application of the part there might be need to add a re-
duction factor (Kf) in the strength analysis. These correction factors could be applied in both the ULS
and FLS cases, where the allowed stress then would be reduced. These reduction factors can typically be
added for weaker parts such as welds, diﬀerent geometries, or environment of operation where a corrosive
environment would reduce the strength of the material gradually. The correction factors are often based
on semi-empirical equations or experience and are deﬁned in diﬀerent rules and regulations by third party
classiﬁcation agencies such as ABS, [14]. For fatigue these reduction factors can sometimes lead to new
SN-curves that are reduced compared to the original one. These reduced SN curves can be found for
speciﬁc materials in classiﬁcation rules, [14].
For welding the correction factors typically depend on a number of diﬀerent factors, such as the thickness
of plates, direction of load contra direction of weld etc.. Based on these measurements a reduction factor
can typically be estimated. A weld that is perpendicular to the force could typically be a problem while
a weld that is parallel to the loading is usually not, this is because the parallel weld will not be critical
in the way that it does not need to carry any load.

2.2
Extreme loading
The extreme loads are the highest load that occur during the whole life time of the WED. Depending
on what force that is considered the extreme loads can occur in diﬀerent sea states, it might be during
a 50 year storm but it might also be during a normal operational sea state. The goal of this WED is to
withstand a 50 year storm and the loads that might occur on it during this storm, but also the extreme
loads that might occur in other sea states. A 50 year storm implies the worst weather that the WED will
encounter during a 50 operational period, at the speciﬁc location. These states that are limited by one
extreme loads is also refereed to as ultimate limiting states.
When designing against extreme loads there are usually a few diﬀerent criteria that needs to be fulﬁlled
in the design. Where typical criteria are maximum tensile (σallowed) and shear (τallowed) stress, maximum
allowed displacement of the component (δallowed), safety against buckling etc. These criteria change de-
pending on material and component that is analysed. Whatever the criteria are they need to be fulﬁlled,
where often only a few of the many criteria are dimensioning. Since wave energy is a relatively new area
complete standards are not jet fully developed, typical guidelines can be found in texts like Guidelines
on design and operation of wave energy converters by DNV GL, [12]. Where in these document many of
the guidelines are based on experience from ﬂoating oﬀshore wind turbines and other oﬀshore structures
where there are more experience.

2.3.1
Fatigue and how to estimate it
The most probable cause of fracture in a construction is in many applications especially metal not the
once in a lifetime extreme load but the many time occurring lower load causing the phenomena called
fatigue. Fatigue can thus be deﬁned as a failure that occurs due to repeated loads, where non of the
loads gives higher stresses than the ultimate stress for the material. The process of fatigue can be said
to contain of three stages [18],
1. Crack initiation, a crack is created in the material.
2. Crack propagation, the already existing crack already continuous to grow from the stress cycles
it is subjected to.
3. Final fracture, the crack has grown so big that there will be a ﬁnal fracture in the component.
When estimating the fatigue life or damage of a component there are three main approaches to calculate
the fatigue. Stress life (SN), Strain life (EN) and Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics (LEFM), [18]. These
approaches are shortly described below. The one that have been used in this thesis is the SN approach.
1 SN approach. This approach is based on how many stress cycles that the material can withstand
before failure. Where the fatigue properties of the material, usually are obtained from experimental
testing and varies from material to material. The material data are usually presented in so called
SN-curves (also called Whöler curves) describing how many cycles the material can withstand be-
fore fracture. An example of a SN-curve can be seen in Figure(2.3.1). The SN method is the most
frequently used method for high cycle fatigue, [18].
2 EN approach. This approach calculates the plastic stresses and it possible to use for both crack
prediction and total life estimations. It is a common method for low cycle fatigue. This method is
not as common as the SN method and thus there are far less material information in this method,
[18].
3 LEFM approach. This approach assumes that there already is a crack in the material and then
tries to calculate the growth rate of the crack. Crack growth is estimated by the stress intensity at
the tip of the crack. This is a fracture mechanics approach, it is often used in applications where
cracks already exist and needs to be monitored and the growth rate can be estimated,[18].
Fatigue can be deﬁned into high and low cycle fatigue, where low cycle is less than 103 cycles and high
cycle is more than 103 cycles. Having a average wave period on 5s the number of wave encounters on
a year would be ∼6.3 millions and on 20 years ∼126 millions. If every wave encounter would lead to a
stress cycle this would lead to that the dimensioning would be against high cycle fatigue. However if only
a few of this wave encounters would lead to signiﬁcantly high stress in the material this would lead to a
low cycle fatigue.

the peaks and valleys. Thus reducing the data to only contain the information of peaks and valleys of
the stress are an important part of calculating the fatigue. Where by having a data set only containing
the information about of peaks and valleys in the stress fatigue can be calculated relatively easy.

Fatigue in the frequency domain
An other approach for calculating fatigue is the spectral method, which in some literature is also refereed
to as the frequency based method. In this method the accumulated fatigue damage is calculate in the
frequency domain instead of the time domain as is done in the amplitude methods. This method was
ﬁrst developed in the 1980’s for simplifying the calculations on oil rigs. It is a frequently used method
for system exposed to random loads such as in the oﬀshore industry, [20]. It is also recommended for
analysis where the loading frequencies is close to the systems eigenfrequencies, meaning that the reso-
nant fatigue is of importance, [21]. This method calculates the fatigue using a power spectral density
(PSD) of the load signal and the stress transfer function of the analysed part. The reasons for using
the frequency domain for calculating fatigue are mainly two points 1) The load on the structure might
be random in nature and then the best approach for calculating the loads is a statistical method which
this method is by using the PSD. 2) Calculating the fatigue with the frequency domain might reduce the
calculation time compared to a long time history of data that is used when in amplitude methods [20].
Using the frequency methods there are a few underlying assumptions [22], some of which are listed below.
• Load analysis and associated structural analysis is assumed to be linear.
• The loading vector is randomly distributed according to the PSD.
• The load sequence is time independent.
For calculating fatigue in the frequency domain there are as mentioned above two things that are initially
needed, 1) PSD of the load signal (Sη), 2) the stress transfer function (Hσ(w)). Where these two can be

transfer function is used when calculating the number of stress cycles at the diﬀerent amplitudes, and
ﬁnally for calculating the fatigue in diﬀerent structures. The accumulated fatigue damage is calculated
by using the SN-curve of the material and adding the separate load cycles.

3.1
Hydrodynamic model
As the hydrodynamic model is the basis for the rest of the system the fundamentals of the model are
described in this section. To ﬁrst understand the fundamentals of the hydrodynamics, the wave loads of
ﬂoating oﬀshore structures should be explained. Wave loads as illustrated in Figure(3.1.1), can be seen
as two parts ﬁrst excitation forces and then the restoring loads from the added mass, restoring forces,
put these together and you will get the motions and behaviour of the body.
Figure 3.1.1: Wave loads can be seen as two parts, the exicitation forces from waves and internal systems and
the responses of the body with properties like added mass, hydrostatic forces. Picture from [23]
The hydrodynamic model at CPO is developed in Matlab/Simulink. The model created by CPO can
analyse the forces and motions of the system in both regular and irregular waves, the irregular waves
are deﬁned by two parameters, signiﬁcant wave height Hs and peak period Tp of the waves. These two
parameters can then be combined by the JONSWAP (Joint north sea wave project) wave spectrum to
create the wave spectrum of the waves. The hydrodynamic model has been validated by comparing the
results from simulations with results from experimental tank testing of scale models.
The Simulink model developed by CPO is formulated assuming linear wave theory. Linear wave theory
assumes as non linear wave theory ideal ﬂuid (no viscosity) and that there is no surface tension at the free
surface. The linear theory also neglects the higher order terms of the boundary conditions allowing for a

simpler wave equation and practical relationships, as e.g. the dispersion relation (k = ω2/gtan(kh)), that
for deep water waves becomes k = ω2/g. The linearisation is usually a good approximation for waves
where the wave height(H) is much lower than the wave length(λ), H/λ << 1. Linear wave theory allows
for summing up the diﬀerent waves by superposition,[4]. The linearised theory also allows for analysing
the properties of the buoy in frequency domain, as is done in WAMIT [24].
The current Simulink model is restricted to two degrees of freedom (DOF), surge and heave. These two
motions are coupled to each other, meaning that the motions in heave direction does have an aﬀect on
motion in the surge direction. However this coupling eﬀect is usually much smaller than the direct eﬀect.
The model is based on the Newtons second law formulated as,
(I(mb + mP T O) + m∞)¨xbuoy = FP T O(x, ˙x) + Fhydro(x, ˙x)
(3.1.1)
Where I is the Identity matrix ¨x is the acceleration vector, mb is the mass of the buoy, mP T O is the mass
of the PTO system and m∞is the inﬁnity frequency added mass matrix. Fhydro is the hydrodynamical
force vector coming from waves, currents and other body ﬂuid interactions. FP T O is the machinery force
vector coming from the power take oﬀ(PTO) system. These equations can when solved give the total
motion of the WED as described by the most right part of Figure(3.1.1). The forces and what they
represent are presented in the proceeding sections. A more thorough description of how the loads are
calculated can be found in Appendix(A), however a complete derivation of the loads is not given in this
thesis because it considered out of scope.
3.1.1
Hydrodynamic forces
The Simulink model developed by CPO uses WAMIT, a wave interaction analysis program for a some of
their calculation. WAMIT uses linear and second-order potential theory for calculating forces on ﬂoating
or submerged bodies in waves. It uses the so called panel method to solve for the velocity potential of
the ﬂuid and by so also the pressure distribution on the bodies, [24]. WAMIT is run as a preprocessor to
the Simulink model to get the hydrodynamic coeﬃcients of the model in advance. The results are then
combined with particular environmental conditions and a model of the PTO/mooring system to simulate
the complete behaviour of the WED. The hydrodynamical or outer forces (Fhydro) on the buoy can be
divided into four diﬀerent parts,
Fhydro = Fexe(x, ˙x) + FRad( ˙x) + Fhyst(x) + FDrag( ˙x).
(3.1.2)
Where Fexe are the excitation forces from the wave, Frad are the radiation forces from the buoy, Fhyst
are the hydrostatic forces and Fdrag are the drag forces on the buoy. Frad can physically be explained as
the force that are generated on the buoy when it is moving up and down creating a wave. To calculate
this WAMIT calculates a number of radiation state vectors for the geometry that then can be used in the
CPO Simulink model to calculate the radiation force in an eﬃcient way. Fexe are the forces on the buoy
coming from the wave and is also calculated with the help of WAMIT data and depends on the angular
frequency of the incoming waves.
Fhyst is calculated by multiplying the submerged volume and multiplying it with the water density and
gravity. Fdrag is calculated by estimating the drag coeﬃcient CD of the body in diﬀerent directions
and then multiplying it with the projected area and the velocity of body or surrounding ﬂuid. A more
detailed description of how the hydrodynamical forces are calculated in the Simulink model can be found
in Modeling, Simulation and Dynamic control of a wave energy converter, by Maria Bånkestad [25]. It
follows the classical hydrodynamical theory that also can be found in books like Marine Hydrodynamics
of J.N. Newman,[4].

3.2
Solid mechanical model
The second model is the solid mechanical model that calculates the stresses and fatigue damage of the
structure and PTO system. These stresses have been calculated by so called Finite Element Analysis
(FEA) where the geometric and material properties are combined to calculate the stress and fatigue
damage of the components. The program used in this thesis is SolidWorks but any other FEA program
such as Ansys, SolidEdge or Abaqus could probably been used.
One of the large advantages with working in this kind of programs are that the interface is user friendly
where the stress and accumulated fatigue damage of the diﬀerent parts can be seen directly in the graph-
ical interface of the program. Also applying forces and boundary conditions to the model can be done
easily, and the results can be seen in a easy way. However SolidWorks have some limitations and to do
some of the more developed fatigue studies such as RP two step as have been developed in this thesis, the
data from the SolidWorks FEA analysis needs to be exported and used in Matlab where larger freedom
of use is possible compared to in SolidWorks.

3.2.2
Fatigue in SolidWorks
SolidWorks uses the so called SN-curve of the material to estimate the accumulated fatigue damage. The
material properties are stored in SolidWorks as standard SN-curves (also called Whöler curves) for a
number of diﬀerent materials. If the SN-curve is not available it can be derived from the material elastic
modulus based on the ASME austentic steel curve, [30]. It is also possible to ad own SN-curves into the
program if needed for a speciﬁc material. SolidWorks uses a theory of cumulative damage to calculate
damage of the structure over alternating stress cycles. It calculates damage by using the linear damage
rule (same as the Palmgren-miner’s rule) meaning that the fatigue is time independent, and the sequence
of the stress does not matter for the accumulated damage. This implies that the fatigue analysis carried
out in this way can only be done with linear stress analysis. Importing load cycles into SolidWorks are a
very time consuming process. Therefore a goal in this thesis have been to reduce the equivalent fatigue
load that is to be imported in SolidWorks down to 25 000 points or 12 500 RFC. 1 point means one
force at one time, 25 000 points then means one varying force signal at 25 000 time steps. This is an
important detail since this means that the simulated data with 1 800 000 points for each sea state needs
to be reduced down to 25 000 points. This reduced series should then represent all the diﬀerent loads
from diﬀerent sea states to be used for one fatigue analysis in SolidWorks. SolidWorks can calculate the
fatigue for a random variable load and multiple variable loads. For the fatigue using one variable the
von Mises or ﬁrst principal stress can be used for calculating the stress and fatigue. For multiple fatigue
loads it can only use the von Mises stress when calculating accumulated fatigue.
Solid work can also calculate the fatigue in the frequency domain based on a PSD on the force. It does
this by deriving the Stress transfer function for the diﬀerent nodes in the model. How to calculate the
stress transfer function can be found in Appendix(C.2). Using this method the fatigue can be calculated
by three diﬀerent approximations Narrow band, Steinberg or Wirshing [31]. How fatigue is calculated as
a function of time can be found in Appendix(C.4).
3.2.3
Validation & accuracy
FEA analysis is a well established method for calculating stresses and displacement of diﬀerent struc-
tures. Errors in the FEA model are typically modelling or discretization errors, this would probably be
relative small errors,[28]. Larger error would probably come from the boundary conditions that might
not be as exactly reﬂected by reality, or the simpliﬁcations in the geometry of the model that might be
necessary to keep the number of DOF at a feasible level. In the fatigue case one factor that is disregarded
is the time variable, a large load in the beginning of the load history might lead to a large crack that
dramatically decreases the fatigue life of the component. However the method of accumulated damage
and using a SN curve and Palmgren Miner’s rule is a well known method and is widely use. In the
design phase the order of the real sea loads are unknown and thus it does not help to have an option
of taking the order account in the model. A fracture mechanics approach, based on an already existing
crack might be a better option but requires more information and is not a possible analysis in SolidWorks.

This section explains the single variable tool and its methodology. The amplitude methods are based on
having a signal where e.g. force is measured as a function of time, F(t). What is important in case of
fatigue is the RFC of F(t) which is extracted in this tool and can then be used in SolidWorks. Each sea
state can be seen as separate design load case, where when the waves are to high the control system of the
WED is assumed to be fully functional meaning that the WED can use its system to control PTO system,
breaks etc.. The single variable tool includes several diﬀerent load cases and adds these together to one
new equivalent load series. Input data for using this method is the load/variable data, the wave spec-
trum that it is going to be subjected to, and some user/methodology speciﬁc inputs. The ﬂowchart of the
tool is illustrated graphically by Figure(4.1.1). Where an example of inputs and a output signal are given.
Using the amplitude method for fatigue one of the largest problems is the amount of data. Simulating
30 minutes for each sea state with a sampling frequency of 0.001Hz the number of data points or values
for one sea state and parameter are 30x60x1000=1 800 000, this then needs to be multiplied with the
number of sea states analysed (25-108), to get the total number of points or values used in the fatigue
analyses 25 · 1800000 = 45000000. With limitations in the FEA software these number of points needs
to be reduced to do an eﬃcient and practically possible fatigue analysis. The goal have been to reduce
this data set to 25 000 points, which this tool can do. The number of points can be reduced signiﬁcantly
by ﬁltering and sorting the load signals so it only contains the load cycles in form of RFCs. These can
then be gathered in one RM from the diﬀerent sea states, that can be regenerated to reduce the number
of points further.

4.2
Multiple variable - Amplitude method
As described in the theory of multiple variable fatigue it is not as easy to select the peaks and valleys
in the stress that creates the RFC since these positions are initially unknown for multiple loads. Also
when selecting the loads these needs to corresponds to the same time in all load series. This implication
means that a RM cannot just be created and scaled in the same way as for the single variable method.
Creating a RM the RFC would lose its time perspective, which makes it impossible to track the diﬀerent
event happening in the two diﬀerent load signals to each other. Because of this complication a diﬀerent
method is used based on random selection of RFC, where the method can be found in Appendix(B).
Both the RP direct and the RP two step method which are explained in the following sections use this
random selection of RFC method.
4.2.1
RP direct
The RP direct method is based on selecting linear combination of several loads or variables and then
taking the peaks and valleys important for fatigue from these linear combinations. Where one of these
linear combinations are assumed to contain the worst loads for the analysed part. Except the random
selection of RFC, RP direct is built on the same principles as the single variable method. Where this
method is done one every separate linear combination. An overview of how the RP direct method ﬂow

4.5
Discussion - Fatigue methods & tools
It is clear from the simple test that the fatigue damage on the simple example is changed signiﬁcantly
depending on what method that is used. One should remember though that how much the fatigue dam-
age will change between the diﬀerent methods are case speciﬁc. If for example in the simple test the
angle α was small or even negligible there would be no diﬀerence between the RP methods and the single
variable. If the variations in (F) also was negligible there would not have been any diﬀerence between
the single variable case and constant amplitude. If any of this would have been the case using single
or multiple variable would have been a waste of time since it would not give any more accurate fatigue
analysis and using single or multiple variables does require some extra work compared to the constant
amplitude case.
The Steinberg method for calculating the fatigue in frequency domain shows similar results that are
obtained from the amplitude methods, however Narrow band and Wirsching shows large deviations from
both the Steinberg method and amplitude methods. Why the spectral methods in general gives a higher
damage/h than the amplitude methods using single and multiple variables is unknown. In other studies
these methods have been proven to be quite a lot larger than the experimental results, [33]. But since
no experimental result are available here it is diﬃcult to know if the spectral methods or the amplitude
methods are further away from what the experimental result would have been. One reason might be that
the eigenfrequency of the tested structure was relatively far from the frequency of the load, where the
ﬁrst mode was at 10Hz and the second at 66Hz, and the load frequency was typically around ∼0.17Hz.
Where the spectral methods should typically be used for structures that have similar eigenfrequency as
the loading frequency, this have not been the case for this simple example. Also the damping should have
mattered but for this case a modal damping was set to 0.1 which would be considered relatively high for
this kind of structure,[32]. Where a high damping should give a lower damage.
What fatigue method to use is case speciﬁc where this needs to be chosen speciﬁcally for each case, taking
into account the circumstances for the case. Circumstances could for example be how large inﬂuence the
diﬀerent loads will have on the stress in the structure, what linear combination of loads that could be
the most damaging or the eigenfrequencies of the diﬀerent structure.

Application
The idea with this application have been to try one/multiple of the tools that have been created during
the thesis on a real applicable example. Also to do one loop of the design process that is used by CPO
in their work described in the background with Figure(1.3.1). The application have been on the 1:2 scale
model, assuming that it will be exposed to the loads based on a wave scatter from Yue outside of France.
Two sets of extreme load cases are tested, fatigue loads are based on 25 sea states and three diﬀerent
operational modes. The fatigue loads will be calculated using the RP two step but also single variable and
constant amplitude to compare the fatigue damage. Using the RP two step method also the positions of
the piston rod (see Figure(5.1.2)), have been included in the analysis. Four hotspots against extreme and
fatigue loads have been detected, and the safety factor of these diﬀerent hotspots have been determined.
A redesign have been implemented and evaluated based on results from the strength analysis. Since the
design of this subsystem is intellectual property of CPO, there are only very simpliﬁed illustrations of
the analysed subsystem showed.
RP direct is not used because the most damaging linear combination of the loads are unknown or there
are no clear correlation between the two loads. Spectral methods are not used because this is a relatively
complex structure including many diﬀerent parts such as bearings, rubber sealing and high pressure air.
This implies that it would be diﬃcult to estimate the damping that would have a large inﬂuence on
the accumulated fatigue damage. Also the eigenfrequency of the subsystem is probably higher than the
frequency of the waves, where a typical eigenfrequency of machinery is between 10-100Hz, [32]. Therefore
the subsystem will probably not be in resonance with the loads from the waves and thus the spectral
method is not considered suitable.

5.5
Discussion application
The results show that the dimensioning factor are both ULS from extreme loads and FLS. Where the
safety factor on the original design against extreme loads was γULS =1.3 and the safety factor against
fatigue γF LS=0.43. This is some what surprising results since it was expected that the system would
be over dimensioned against extreme loads and fatigue, since CPO are using the constant amplitude
method for calculating fatigue damage. However this surprising results comes from the estimation of the
horizontal forces, where CPO have claimed that with the not yet designed mooring system they will be
able to reduce the horizontal extreme forces to 40KN and the fatigue ones to 20KN. Since no design of
this exist, it have not been used in this application.
Implementing the redesign was not easy, with many limitations from the surrounding system only a few
parameters could be adjusted with out interfering with the surrounding systems. However a redesign was
implemented and the weight of subsystem was reduced with 218 Kg. The result show that at point 2-4
the redesign was successful and the aimed safety factor was reached. However at point 1 the safety factor
was reduced to 0.25, which is lower than the initial safety factor on 0.43 using the RP two step method.
This reduction is most likely due to some change in the geometry in the connection between two parts
where the stress concentration occurs. A change that was implemented when trying to reduce weight of
the subsystem. Therefore a update in the design should be made putting this material back and by so
increasing the safety factor and utilizing the increased diameter of the outer cylinder. This mistake of
removing to much material at the wrong place does illustrate the iterative design process explained in
Figure(1.3.1) well, where now a new iteration needs to be done for increasing the safety factor of the design.
The biggest uncertainty in the analysis is probably the mooring system and connection between mooring
line and the piston rod. Since this is not designed yet. The design of this would have a large inﬂuence
on the loads in the analysis, by so also the outcome. The stresses caused by the horizontal force Fx are
∼10 time larger than the stresses caused from the vertical force Fz, even tough Fz is much larger. To
increase the lifetime of the subsystem it is therefore critical to reduce the horizontal forces on the piston
rod. Including more variables to the fatigue analysis could most likely increase the fatigue life of the
system analysed signiﬁcantly. For example a variable as the wave direction would imply that the same
side or angle of the analysed structure is not loaded as frequently. Thus the accumulated damage would
be spread around the whole part increasing the fatigue life signiﬁcantly. The hydrodynamical model does
in this analysis only include 2 DOF, a pitch angle would probably give a better estimation of the real
forces in the mooring line, since this angle would reduce the mooring angle α and thus the horizontal force.
The SN-curve that have been used is taken for the materials from SolidWorks, where SolidWorks estimates
the SN-curve from elastic modulus of the material and ASME austenitic steel curve. This is probably
a very rough estimation of the SN-curve and the validity of this curve can be heavily questioned. For
making a better fatigue damage estimation this SN-curve should be speciﬁc for the material used instead.
For applications like this a new SN-curve should have reduced strength properties due to corrosion. These

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Four diﬀerent fatigue calculations methods have been used, leading to four diﬀerent tools. The diﬀerent
methods have diﬀerent level of complexity, where a higher complexity might provide more accurate fatigue
estimations but also implies more time and work. What method that is most suitable is case speciﬁc.
For some analysis, one variable might be suﬃcient to calculate the fatigue damage if the inﬂuence of the
other loads are small or negelectable. In these cases the Single variable tool would be suﬃcient. For
others load cases a more detailed multi variable method where there are several loads giving a inﬂuence
on the fatigue might be needed to give a accurate fatigue analysis. If there are a clear correlation between
the loads, or one of the loads are dominating the stress RP direct tool can be used because the linear
combination giving the largest damage could be found. Otherwise the RP 2 step tool can be used to
ensure that the correct stress cycles are found. The Spectral tool can be used using a derived PSD for
structures that are in resonance with the loads. Using this method it is important to have good control
of the damping in the structure since this will have a large inﬂuence on the accumulated damage. The
process of deriving the correct damping could be problematic or diﬃcult with out experience in this. The
damping has a large inﬂuence on the stress and it is thus critical when calculating fatigue. All four tools
have been developed using a probabilistic approach to reduce the amount of input data to SolidWorks.
The selection of number of RFC and settings such as thresholds, output points, and ﬁlters thus needs
to be handle with knowledge of the consequences of the choices. The tools have been developed to work
for the more general case but are not 100% reliable, therefore for each case the user needs to ensure that
the tool works correctly. As much plausibility as possible should be made from the diﬀerent ﬁgures of
loads, RFC, RM etc. displayed during the process and the diﬀerent control values that are calculated. It
should also be remembered that the tools have not yet been veriﬁed with any experimental results.
Including position as a variable in the fatigue analysis could have a large impact on the results. The
theory is relatively easy but demands a bit more of work compared to the RP two step analysis only
containing varying forces and moments. The theory and method including the position factor is simple,
however including the position in fatigue analysis is only possible outside SolidWorks. In using the posi-
tions factor it is important to have similar or preferably the exact same mesh at the diﬀerent positions. It
is important because the stress in every node are used, changing the mesh and positions of nodes between
the simulations this means that the results needs to be projected to another mesh. This projection have
been implemented in the tool but could give some loss of information since the exact same nodes does
not exist when using this. The projection of meshes have been implemented in the code but should be
handled with caution and knowledge of eventual losses or extreme events that could occur.
The fatigue tools have been written in Matlab and can with small adjustments bee used for any geometry
and load wanted. The multiple load have been used for two (three including position) variables, this
can easily be extended to several more variables using the same theory. The tools have been custom
made to ﬁt the purposes of CPO where large focus have been to see the end result in FEA software and
where the tools are only a middle step in the process. Nevertheless for the RP two step the fatigue can
also be calculated in Matlab to save time instead of importing it to SolidWorks, setting up a fatigue
analysis, importing fatigue loads and so on. A good thing with SolidWorks is that the graphical interface
is very user friendly compared to the a 3D plot in Matlab. SoldidWorks is as other FEA software good
when it comes to calculating the stiﬀness matrix or stress and displacement of a structure. However
SolidWorks is relatively limited in fatigue analysis, doing the work in Matlab or any similar software

would lead to possibilities for a more accurate and detailed analysis, including variables such as prin-
cipal stress, correction factors for certain exposed areas etc.. For doing a accurate fatigue analysis the
recommendation is therefore to use each software for what it is good at. This means using the FEA
programs for calculating the response in the structure due to certain loads. While using the numerical
software for the large data sets from the hydrodynamical model and FEA software to calculate the fatigue.
Calculating the fatigue can be done by using the Palmgren Miner’s rule in SolidWorks or Matlab. For
fatigue cases with low stress but still high enough to be important for high cycle fatigue the accumulated
damage is some times problematic to calculate in SolidWorks. Where this cycles are sometimes excluded.
For these cases (e.g. application of the piston rod system) the fatigue have been calculated in Matlab
instead. This is seen as a large weakness in SolidWorks, since loads on the wave energy device typically
comes from accumulated damage in the many smaller cycles.
This thesis have created a tool that can increase the precision of the fatigue analysis. For this to be a
useful it is also important to look on the data put in to this model and other data used in the analysis.
The strength analysis of the structure can never be better than the information that are used as input
for the analysis. If load estimations on the structure are poor, important details such as wave direction
are missing, SN-curve is of poor quality, reduction factors for corrosion or welds are not set properly all
this will have a eﬀect on the outcome of the strength analysis. If these factors are poorly estimated or of
low quality it does not matter how good fatigue analysis you make, it will still be uncertain because of
the poor quality of the input data. Therefore it is important when doing a detailed fatigue analysis that
the right information are available and of good quality, otherwise there is no point in doing a detailed
fatigue analysis. It is also important to ensure that the analysed part is the critical part. If a very
detailed strength analysis is done of the structure, but this is not the critical part it is useless because
something else might break before. Therefore one needs to ensure that the part analysed is the critical
part or analyse several parts.
6.1
Fulﬁlment of purpose and objectives
Several tools have been developed during this thesis to create a link between the hydrodynamic model
developed in Simulink by CPO and the solid mechanical model. Where these tools are taking the load
series from the CPO Simulink model and exports them in a way that it can be used for the solid
mechanical model used by CPO. For the RP two step tool a intermediate step needs to be done doing
some pre calculations in the FEA program. The RP two step tool also allows for calculating the fatigue
directly in Matlab which could improve the analysis further. The tools can probably be used with small
modiﬁcations for other models than the ones used by CPO. How the objectives listed in the background
have been fulﬁlled can be seen below.
1. Four tools have been created that can be used for ﬁnding fatigue and extreme loads, for single and
multiple variables. The tools are written in Matlab and are not totally generic but can with small
adjustments be used for most cases.
2. Four tools have been developed using diﬀerent methods for estimating fatigue. The methods have
been compared to each other on a simple example. Where it is clear that increasing the number of
variables can increase accuracy of the fatigue calculations signiﬁcantly. However this depends on
the properties for each separate case.
3. The bottom part of the WED connecting the mooring line to the PTO system have been selected
as an application. A load and strength analysis have been done on this part, where some hot spots
in the structure have been identiﬁed and the safety factors against extreme loads and fatigue have
been determined. A redesign have been implemented and evaluated.

The tools could be implemented in a future structural optimisation program. Where depending on the

When knowing response of the structure as Cm, this allows for a very eﬃcient way of calculating the

iments or operations on the WED could allow for real time surveillance of stress in the structure. By
so the accumulated damage in the structure can be estimated on the same time as operating the WED.
Including this system in a future WED-park could give a real time update on the fatigue damage on the
structure. With knowledge about current damage/hour the WED could be controlled to reduce damage
on it. Knowing the accumulated damage this would also allow for less maintenance/inspection on the
buoy, which might reduce the levelized cost of energy produced.
The tools are written to handle 1-3 variables, where these tools easily could be developed for further more
variables using the same theory. The tools could be further developed to be more generic and having
more options in the sense of input variables. The tools could also be developed for more complex load
structures such as pressure on the hull, where detailed strength analysis then could be made also on
these kind of structures. Instead of signed von Mises stress the principal stresses could be used when
calculating the stress since this is a more relevant measure of stress in cases of fatigue where a certain
sensitive directions is knows as for example in welds. Also consideration should be taken into account
if the accumulated damage in the long term should be calculated in SolidWorks or in Matlab. Where
larger possibilities are given in Matlab, especially for eventual monitoring systems.

B
Rainﬂow Projection
One way of dealing with fatigue for multiple variables are the Rainﬂow Projection (RP) methods. The
RP methods are a continual on the Rainﬂow counting method, which as for the single variable method
is based on calculating the Rainﬂow cycles (RFC) of the stress. The RP methods are based on creating
linear combinations of the diﬀerent load vectors and calculating which one that creates the largest stress.
Two methods of selecting the linear load combinations that gives the highest stress cycles are theoreti-
cally explained in this appendix and have been used in this thesis, 1) RP direct method 2) RP two step
method. Where in the RP direct method the linear combinations (LC) are chosen directly from the load
signals and can then be used in a FEA program. For the RP two step method some pre-processing is
needed from a FEA program to then be able to extract the correct load series. The RP direct method
is partly described in courses by P. Johanesson [36], while the RP two step mostly have been developed
independently but is suggested for detailed fatigue analysis in Guide to load analysis for durability in
vehicle engineering, [19].

D.3
Rainﬂow ﬁlter & Rainﬂow Cycles
When the data have been inspected and corrected the number of values in the load signal needs to be
reduced for the data to be used in SolidWorks. When reducing the large amount of values/points the
key is to keep the important information and discard the unimportant information. In the case of fatigue
what is important are the peaks and valleys of the load signal. Therefore these values need to be kept
while the others are discarded. To take out the turning points a ﬁlter have been used. The ﬁlter used in
the tool is a Rainﬂow ﬁlter. What is deﬁned as a turning point or not is decided by a threshold value. A
standard threshold is to use about 5% of the global range of the load signal, [19].
The turning points are used to calculate the so called Rainﬂow cycles (RFC) with so called Rainﬂow
counting. The Rainﬂow counting is a way to calculate the hysteresis loops of the corrected and ﬁltered
load signal. It is a well known counting method for fatigue cycles that was ﬁrst developed by professor
Murakami, [19]. Where the Rainﬂow counting includes the larger stress cycles as well as the smaller ones.

D.5
Merging RM from diﬀerent sea states
The method described above for extracting the relevant load cycles from a complete load signal can be
done for each sea state individually. But since the WED also will be exposed to a number of various
sea states leading to diﬀerent forces, the loads from every sea state needs to be considered. This can be
done for RM in a few diﬀerent ways. The ﬁrst way would be to simply add the diﬀerent load signals by
adding them to one large load signal and then from this load signal go through the steps described above
to get the RM of all sea states. Another way would be by ﬁrst calculating the separate RM for each
sea state and then adding them all together. Doing the later it is important that the RM does have the
same discretisation (bins) so that they match each other. The second method have been implemented in
the tool, because it makes the process including the probability of the diﬀerent sea states easier. Using
the method of adding RM the probability factor of time scaling can be implemented by multiplying the
whole RM with a probability factor. By doing so a gathered RM matrix can be calculated by adding the
diﬀerent sea states together. This methods have been described in more detail below.
As described in previous sections the RFC can be discretized into diﬀerent bins and by so creating a
RM showing the number of diﬀerent cycles in a matrix as seen in Figure(D.5a). This RM can be seen
as a 2D histogram where the likely hood of a load cycle occurring is proportional to the value in that
discretized interval. Having the same discretization for the load cycles from all the diﬀerent loads the
RM matrices from the diﬀerent sea states can be added together as described by equation(D.1) where
the index number of each RM represents the sea state.

The number of data points from the LCD-method could be reduced by decreasing the number of bins

to broader bins. The LCD method could be made more exact than the probability distribution, but it is
a play-oﬀbetween number of data points and the accuracy of the load cycles in the bins. The probability
method is therefore used instead of the LCD method, this could also be motivated by a large set of data

D.8
Random selection of RFC - Multiple variables
For multiple loads the number of load cycles can be calculated for any load signals with Rainﬂow ﬁlter-
ing and Rainﬂow counting in the same way as is done in the single variable method described above.
However merging and regeneration of load cycles cannot be done in the same way for multiple variables
as for single variables. When working with multiple variables, the peak of the speciﬁc load series and its
position in time (or in the vector) needs to be saved. Because this position is needed when locating the
corresponding values in the other load signals that are used in the analysis. Therefore the cycles cannot
75
==PAGE:86==
Appendix D - Amplitude methods detailed methodology
be binned in and sorted into a RM, doing this it would loose its time/position information and thus the
connection to the corresponding load in the other load signals would be lost.
So to be able to reduce the number of RFC for multiple variables, a random selection of RFC are im-
plemented instead. This is a simple method where based on the probability of each sea state a number
of RFC are randomly chosen, where the same positions or times are chosen in both/all load signals.
By doing so the load cycles keep their time perspective to each other and can then be applied to the
structure on the same time to mimic that speciﬁc loading event. The number of RFC to be chosen from
each sea state are based on the probability of that sea state. As for example if a sea state occurs 10% of
the total time and a total of 12 500 RFC are wanted as output. 1 250 RFC will be taken from this sea state.
This method of selecting random RFC from each sea state and then merging them can ﬁrst seem a bit
arbitrary and not as stable as the merging and regeneration of RM described in the section(D.6). Where
that can be seen as a histogram and just re sampling the histogram which is a traditional statistical
method. But when considering the method of choosing random load cycles from the remade load signal
this is actually the same as re sampling the histogram of the RM except no bins is required for random
selection of RFC. To control that the damage is kept the method described in section(D.8) is applied,
visual inspection and pseudo damage of the load cycles.
D.9
Results from FEA studies - Multiple variables
In the RP two step tool some results from a FEA analysis are needed to run the fatigue analysis. For each
load Fm that are going to be implemented in the fatigue analysis, this needs to be used in a separate load
analysis in the FEA software to get the response of the structure (Cm) for each node in the structure.
This needs to be exported from the FEA software as .txt ﬁle so that it can be imported in Matlab by
the RP two step tool. Also the geometry of the analysed structure and the position of each node needs
to be known and exported from the FEA software. If the fatigue analysis in Matlab wants to be done
on only a certain area of the structure analysed in the FEA software the nodes and their position at this
area needs to be exported from the FEA software. By doing that the RP two step tool can analyse only
the stress and fatigue in these nodes, by so saving some computational time.
D.10
Finding extreme nodes - Multiple variables
In the RP two step method the RFC for σ(t), should be calculated for each node. Calculating these load
series for each node is a very time consuming process and for a geometry with many nodes it becomes
problematic because of the large amounts of data. Therefore a small algorithm have been implemented to
ﬁnd the most loaded nodes and ﬁnding the RFC based on the values in these nodes. The algorithm goes
through a number of random time positions (ti,random) and calculates the most damaging nodes of the
structure for each of the random time steps. By so a number of extreme nodes are found. Where these
extreme nodes are the critical areas of the structure which then would give the highest damage, and are
thus the interesting areas. Based on the extreme nodes the RFC used for calculating the accumulative
damage can be identiﬁed. The number of time steps that are checked can be adjusted as an input of the
tool, but is a default set to about ∼6000.
