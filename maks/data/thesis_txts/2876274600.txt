Master’s Thesis
Abstract
Abstract
Quartz ultra-stable oscillators (USOs) are the time base of many Low Earth Orbit satellites and they are criti-
cal for precise orbit determination. In the South Atlantic Anomaly (SAA), satellites are exposed to an intense
radiation environment, dominated by trapped protons, which can perturb onboard clocks and degrade the
performance of tracking systems such as DORIS and GNSS. This thesis quantifies how the trapped-proton
environment imprints itself on the USO of ESA’s Sentinel-3A satellite and it derives an empirical link between
local proton flux and clock drift, with a view toward future missions such as Genesis.
Daily SP3 orbit/clock files are used to reconstruct a relativistically corrected clock-residual series at 1 min ca-
dence. A dedicated processing chain is developed to stabilise the baseline (moving-average and high-pass
filtering are applied), compute local clock drift, and automatically detect negative drift peaks associated with
SAA passages. Each peak is geo-located and classified as ascending or descending. The radiation environ-
ment is described using time-resolved trapped-proton flux from SPENVIS and directional proton measure-
ments from NOAA/EUMETSAT POES–MetOp satellites. A Gaussian-kernel co-location scheme provides, at
every drift sample, a local flux estimate and its standard error.
Within the SAA, the absolute local drift correlates strongly with co-located proton flux. These relationships
define a first-order transfer function from trapped-proton flux to clock drift that can be propagated to a repre-
sentative orbit to obtain order-of-magnitude predictions of radiation-induced clock excursions and to inform
future timing-error budgets.

Introduction (2-3 pp)
1.1
Motivation
Ultra-stable oscillators (USOs) are at the heart of satellite timekeeping systems, providing the onboard fre-
quency reference required for precise orbit determination, high-accuracy geophysical measurements and
stable instrument timing. Satellites that carry geodetic payloads such as DORIS (Doppler Orbitography and
Radiopositioning Integrated by Satellite) rely on highly stable onboard clocks to achieve centimeter-level orbit
accuracy over the mission lifetime. Achieving such stability is challenging because the space environment
exposes oscillators to various perturbations, such as temperature extremes, magnetic fields from Earth or
from the spacecraft and radiation, that can degrade their frequency stability [3]. This work addresses the
last factor: space radiation, which has emerged as an element with a big impact on USOs performance in
orbit. Space radiation comprises high-energy particles from multiple sources. For the USO’s stability, the
most relevant components are galactic cosmic rays (GCR), mainly protons with energies from tens to hun-
dreds MeV [4], trapped protons and electrons in the Van Allen belts (protons up to several hundred MeV
and electrons up to a few MeV), and solar energetic protons ejected from the Sun during solar activity [3].
Energetic particles can induce measurable frequency shifts in quartz crystal oscillators. In brief, when ion-
izing particles penetrate a quartz resonator, they deposit energy and displace crystal lattice impurities or
defects, leading to slight changes in the resonant frequency [3]. Over time, the cumulative effect of these dis-
placements (total ionizing dose and low dose rate effects) and occasional events (single event upsets during
intense solar flares) can perturb the frequency of an oscillator. These radiation-induced clock perturbations
were first identified on satellites carrying the DORIS payload like Jason-1 [3], where the quartz USO experi-
enced discontinuous frequency excursions linked to passages through regions of enhanced trapped proton
flux, such as the South Atlantic Anomaly (SAA). These excursions produced stations and orbit positioning
errors at the decimeter level and they forced the introduction of dedicated clock correction models, as well as
pre-irradiation “hardening” of the quartz resonator to reduce its sensitivity. Despite these mitigation efforts,
preventing radiation-driven drift in flight is still difficult, as each USO behaves differently. In addition, shielding
can stop low-energy particles, but high-energy protons in the tens of MeV range can still go through typical
spacecraft shielding with little attenuation, and adding enough additional mass to make a difference is usually
unacceptable for the spacecraft. Alternatively, more radiation-tolerant clock technologies (e.g. rubidium or
hydrogen masers) are less sensitive to the particle environment but come with higher complexity and cost.
For many geodetic missions, a quartz USO therefore remains the practical solution, which makes it necessary
to characterize and model its response to radiation instead of simply trying to eliminate the disturbance. This
challenge is amplified by the fact that the radiation environment itself is highly variable in time and location.
In light of these challenges, the present work aims to quantify the relationship between radiation and USO fre-
quency drift, using real in-orbit data. This could potentially enable more timely clock correction and improved
orbit post-processing, and it may be directly relevant to forthcoming missions such as ESA’s (European
Space Agency) Genesis, where a radiation environment unlike common LEO (Low Earth Orbit) conditions
could make timing robustness even more critical. To carry out this analysis, I rely primarily on real in-orbit data
from the satellite Sentinel-3A, whose long operational lifetime and precise orbit and clock products provide
dense time series of USO behaviour in a LEO environment.

Sentinel-3A
Sentinel-3A is part of ESA’s Copernicus programme and, together with Sentinel-3B, it provides global Earth
observation data with a primary focus on ocean and climate monitoring. The mission objectives include
measuring sea surface topography, as well as sea and land surface temperature and colour with high ac-

Sentinel-3A was launched on 16 February 2016 into a near-polar, Sun-synchronous orbit at approximately
815 km altitude. The satellite carries a payload tailored to these objectives, including the Ocean and Land
Colour Instrument (OLCI), the Sea and Land Surface Temperature Radiometer (SLSTR), the SAR Radar

DORIS Ultra-Stable Oscillator (USO), so that GPS and DORIS observations share a common clock. This
architecture allows GPS-derived receiver clock corrections to directly trace the behaviour of the DORIS USO
in orbit [5, 6]. Previous analyses of these data have already demonstrated the strong impact of the SAA on
the Sentinel-3A USO and on DORIS geodetic solutions [5, 6]. In the present work, Sentinel-3A is used as
a testbed to study radiation-clock coupling in LEO, and as a proxy for future missions operating in harsher
radiation environments.

2.1.1
Ultra-Stable Oscillators (USOs)
History
Quartz-based ultra-stable oscillators have been standard spaceborne frequency references for decades be-
cause they are compact, power-efficient and sufficiently stable to serve as the spacecraft “timebase” for many
geodetic and Earth-observation payloads (e.g., DORIS). The first precision quartz oscillator flown in space
was on the Transit navigation satellite in 1958, achieving a frequency stability on the order of 5 × 10−11
over 100 s [8]. Over the following decades, continuous development improved the stability of space-qualified
quartz oscillators by several orders of magnitude. By the 1990s, state-of-the-art USOs achieved stability as
high as 7 × 10−14 (100 s) [8], about a thousand times better than the 1950s designs. The remarkable track
record made USOs a trusted instrument for satellites, enabling new clock designs in low-mass missions.
Early navigation constellations also relied on USOs for onboard timing, before atomic clock technology was
space-qualified. Planetary probes and deep-space missions also adopted USOs for critical experiments: for
example, NASA’s Voyager (1977) and Galileo (1989) missions carried ultra-stable quartz oscillators for radio
science downlinks [9]. Today, the term USO generally refers to a high-stability quartz oscillator, whereas
modern navigation satellites have largely adopted atomic frequency standards for superior long-term stabil-
ity. Nevertheless, quartz USOs remain essential on many geodetic missions thanks to their compactness,
proven reliability, and excellent short-term stability.
Principles and Frequency Stability
USO’s performance is built on the fundamental properties of quartz crystal resonators. A quartz resonator is
a thin plate or disk of piezoelectric quartz cut at a precise angle and size; when an electric field is applied via
plated electrodes, the crystal mechanically vibrates at a well-defined resonant frequency [8]. The cut angle
and dimensions determine the resonance frequency and temperature behavior of the crystal. High-quality
quartz crystals exhibit extremely sharp resonance (high Q factor), meaning they store oscillation energy with
minimal loss each cycle. For instance, a simple wristwatch crystal might have Q ∼3 × 104, whereas a
precision USO’s crystal can exceed Q = 3 × 106 [8]. This high Q underpins the “ultra-stable” frequency:
the intrinsic frequency stability of a quartz resonator can reach the 10−13 level over 100 s in the best de-
signs, although achieving this in practice requires careful engineering of the supporting oscillator circuitry
and environment [8]. In essence, a quartz oscillator consists of the resonator plus sustaining electronics and
environmental control systems that ensure the crystal operates under stable conditions. While a low-end
oscillator might only need a simple transistor loop and a battery, a state-of-the-art USO incorporates a suite
of design features to minimize frequency perturbations. These include temperature control, isolation from
vibration and shock, magnetic shielding and regulation of the power supply and load impedance.
The result of these measures is an oscillator output of exceptionally high stability. Key performance metrics
include short-term stability, often quantified by the Allan deviation σy(τ) over an averaging interval τ. An-
other metric is phase noise, the power spectral density of phase fluctuations in the oscillator’s signal, which is
crucial for systems requiring low-jitter reference signals [8]. Over longer periods, oscillators exhibit frequency
drift (aging), a slow monotonic change in frequency. Quartz oscillators typically age on the order of 10−12
per day after an initial burn-in [8]. Notably, the aging rate of premium quartz oscillators is comparable to that

USOs in Satellite Navigation, Timing and Geodesy
Beyond their internal design, USOs play a pivotal systems role in spacecraft. Essentially, an USO provides
the master clock for a satellite’s onboard timing and frequency needs. Many satellite subsystems (telemetry,
signal generation, data time-stamping, etc.) draw on the stable reference frequency distributed from the USO
unit. In communications satellites, for instance, the USO ensures carrier frequencies remain tightly controlled
and digital data streams are correctly synchronized. In Earth observation and geodesy missions, USOs are
indispensable for precision tracking systems. One example is the DORIS system, which uses a network of
ground beacons and an onboard USO-driven receiver to measure Doppler shifts for orbit determination as
well as geodetic results. DORIS receiver’s local oscillator is disciplined by an internal 10 MHz USO, serving
as the frequency reference for all Doppler tracking measurements. Any frequency instability in the USO would
directly degrade the Doppler data and thus the accuracy of the results. Indeed, the requirement for DORIS
oscillators is extremely stringent stability (on the order of 10−12 over 10–100 s) to enable millimeter-level
ranging precision [10] [11].
Similarly, GNSS rely on ultra-stable oscillators to timestamp signals for positioning, even if modern GNSS like
GPS and Galileo have moved to atomic clocks (rubidium and cesium, or even passive hydrogen masers) for
improved long-term stability. Still, even atomic-clock-based systems often incorporate quartz oscillators too
for short-term stability and as backups. USOs are also crucial in VLBI applications. In VLBI, widely sepa-
rated radio telescopes (or, in some cases, a spaceborne radio antenna) must record signals with precise time
tags derived from stable clocks. Typically, hydrogen maser clocks are used on Earth, but any space VLBI
transmitter must carry an USO or atomic clock to maintain coherence with Earth-based time references [12].
Finally, an upcoming example is the ESA Genesis mission, which plans to co-locate GNSS, DORIS, SLR
and a VLBI transmitter on one satellite to realize a next-generation Terrestrial Reference Frame (TRF). In
Genesis, a single USO will serve as the central timing reference to which all these techniques are synchro-
nized. This underscores how, whether for navigation, communication or science, the USO often constitutes
a mission-critical subsystem in satellite payloads and platforms.
Environmental Limitations and Radiation Effects
Despite their excellent stability under controlled conditions, USOs are subject to various environmental per-
turbations, especially in the harsh conditions of space.
Radiation exposure has emerged as one of the
most significant limitations to quartz oscillator performance in orbit. Quartz crystal oscillators can experience
frequency shifts when exposed to ionizing radiation (high-energy protons, electrons, gamma rays) due to
radiation-induced defects or charge trapping in the crystal and electronic components. The effect depends
on total dose and dose rate: higher radiation doses generally cause a larger cumulative frequency offset or
drift, while intense short bursts (such as crossing a radiation belt region) can induce abrupt frequency jumps.
In LEO, the prime example is the South Atlantic Anomaly (SAA), a region of enhanced trapped proton flux
that LEO satellites frequently pass through. Several missions (e.g. Jason-1 [13], SPOT-5 [14]) have reported
sudden frequency deviations and an increase in oscillator drift rate immediately following SAA passages, for
which corrective models were made. Jason-1 improved its corrective model in 2016 with Characterization and
Modeling of Environment (CARMEN); its positioning bias with respect to ITRF2000 was reduced from several
decimeters to 6-11 cm [13]. Besides radiation, other environmental factors impose limitations. Temperature

2.1.2
Difference from atomic clocks (RAFS/PHM)
As previously mentioned, geodetic satellites (like Sentinel or Jason series) typically carry high-quality USOs
rather than full atomic clocks, whereas navigation satellites (like Galileo) are also equipped with atomic clock
systems. For example, each Galileo satellite hosts two Rubidium Atomic Frequency Standards (RAFS) and
two Passive Hydrogen Masers (PHM), both atomic clocks that together provide excellent stability and redun-
dancy [15]. The PHM offers exceptional long-term stability (time drift is <1 ns per day, losing on the order of
only one second over 3 million years) [16]. Rubidium clocks, while slightly less stable (they are expected to
lose on the order of 3 seconds over 1 million years [16]), are very reliable and serve as backups and com-
plements to the hydrogen maser [15]. Unlike USOs (which are crystal oscillators), atomic clocks use atomic
energy transitions as the frequency reference, making them far less sensitive to environmental factors and
drift [16]. Instead of having an oscillating mass as the time-base, these clocks follow the properties of atoms
when transitioning between different energy states: when an atom gets excited by an external energy source,
it goes to a higher energy state and then to a lower energy state. During this transition it releases energy at a
very specific frequency, which becomes its reference. To make a good clock, the frequency needs to be de-
tected and used as an input to a counter. In the RAFS, they use rubidium atoms, in the PHM hydrogen ones.
In reality, the atomic clock output is often coupled with a quartz oscillator for signal generation: this combi-
nation leverages the low phase-noise of the quartz and the long-term stability of the atomic reference [17].
Geodetic LEO satellites typically avoid onboard atomic clocks to save size, weight, power, and cost; instead
they fly a quartz USO and use external GNSS timing to estimate and correct its drift, achieving the required
timing for precise orbit determination [17]. In summary, RAFS and PHM are atomic clocks providing superior
stability (sub-nanosecond/day level) compared to USOs, which, while extremely stable for quartz devices,
exhibit higher drifts and require frequent calibration or correction by ground or GNSS-based timing.
2.1.3
Clock estimation from DORIS and GNSS tracking
The behavior of a satellite’s onboard clock (whether an atomic clock or a USO) can be estimated using
both GNSS and DORIS tracking data. In the case of GNSS tracking, the satellite effectively acts as a re-
ceiver of GPS/Galileo signals, and its clock bias relative to the GNSS system time is treated as an unknown
parameter in the orbit determination or navigation solution. This receiver clock bias must be solved for at
every measurement epoch [18], yielding a time series of clock offsets (usually one estimate per observation

the South Atlantic Anomaly) may require specialized modeling. Because both GNSS and DORIS are sen-

satellites use a single USO for both their DORIS and GPS receivers, which allowed engineers to replace
the DORIS polynomial clock model with the high-rate GPS-derived clock solution [5]. In theory, the GNSS
pseudorange measurements observe the clock as a series of epoch-wise time bias corrections, while the
DORIS Doppler measurements observe the clock as a frequency offset; when integrated over time (or con-
verted to an equivalent range error), both methods reveal the same underlying clock error evolution. Thus,
by leveraging either GNSS timing biases or DORIS Doppler shifts, or a combination of both, one can retrieve
the spacecraft clock drift and offsets with high precision, ensuring that clock errors are properly accounted
for in orbit determination.
2.2
Near-Earth radiation environment
2.2.1
Van-Allen Belts and South Atlantic Anomaly
The Earth is surrounded by two main zones of trapped charged particles known as the Van Allen radiation
belts. The inner belt, located roughly from about 0.2 to 2 Earth radii above the equator (or 1000 km to 12000
km), contains predominantly high-energy protons (tens to hundreds of MeV) along with some electrons. The
outer belt, extending roughly from 4 to 9-10 Earth radii (or 13000 to 60000 km), is dominated by energetic
electrons (up to several MeV) and it is more variable in response to solar activity [20]. Between them lies a
“slot” region of relatively lower particle flux, cleared by wave-particle interactions. These belts are toroidal in
shape and constrained by Earth’s magnetic field, which guides the particles as they gyrate, bounce between
mirror points, and drift around the planet.

is largely shielded from inner belt protons by the Earth’s magnetic field and atmospheric cutoff, so its USO
experiences much lower radiation exposure most of the time. Thus, the SAA represents a localized hazard
in LEO, as a short-term, high-intensity radiation encounter on each orbit. This situation is in contrast with the
environment in higher orbits, where exposure to the radiation belts can be more continuous, as discussed
next.
2.2.2
Low Earth Orbits and Medium Earth Orbits
Radiation exposure in space varies strongly with orbital altitude. As previously discussed, except for passages
through regions like the SAA or occasional high-latitude encounters with energetic electrons, the ambient
radiation in LEO is relatively low. In terms of impact on an USO, this means the device might undergo short
bursts of radiation-induced frequency perturbation when in the SAA, but then it has time to recover or remain
stable in between. The total dose accumulated in LEO builds up slowly over time, predominantly coming from
those intermittent spikes on each orbit.

concern, as continuous irradiation can lead to cumulative frequency offset and potentially increased noise
or aging rates, with low opportunity for the oscillator’s performance to recover between exposures. This
represents a new challenge for a quartz USO, since historically most high-precision oscillators in space
have either been in LEO (experiencing only transient SAA effects) or have been atomic clocks in MEO/GEO
which are intrinsically more radiation-hard. It is evident that these situations require precise characterization
and modeling of the effects, as well as new and careful design strategies. For example, pre-irradiating the
resonators with a gamma source (a form of aging the crystal with a high dose, e.g. 20 krad) has been shown
to make subsequent in-orbit shifts more predictable and smaller [8].

solar wind and magnetic field act as a shield, deflecting many cosmic rays (a phenomenon known as the

higher. Cosmic rays play a key role in generating inner belt protons through a process called Cosmic Ray

of which decay into protons that become trapped in the inner radiation belt. Thus, during solar minimum, this
source of new trapped protons is enhanced, whereas at solar maximum it is suppressed [28].
Secondly, the solar cycle influences Earth’s upper atmosphere density through EUV (extreme-ultraviolet)
heating [29]. During solar maximum, increased EUV radiation causes the thermosphere to heat and expand.

of the radiation belts, an expanded atmosphere at solar max causes greater loss of trapped particles at low
altitudes: the edges of the inner belt (especially the SAA region which dips to low altitudes) are eroded by
the denser atmosphere [29]. On the contrary at solar minimum, the cooler and contracted atmosphere offers
less collisional loss to the trapped particles, allowing the belts, and particularly the inner proton belt, to grow
more intense at lower altitude [29]. Empirical radiation belt models reflect this difference: for example, the

and electrons. However, most SEP particles have difficulty reaching the inner regions of the magnetosphere
because the Earth’s magnetic field largely shields low-altitude orbits from all but the most energetic solar
protons. Only extreme events (often during solar maximum years) can penetrate down to MEO or LEO al-

experience single-event upsets or radiation damage from SEPs, especially if they are in higher orbits or polar
trajectories where magnetic shielding is the weakest [30].

modulation of the belt. The combined storm/SEP forcing may temporarily deepen the radiation-driven dips
observed in the clock residuals, but it would need to be analysed further.
The 11-year solar cycle is thus a driving factor behind the background radiation levels that a spacecraft will
encounter over its mission lifetime. For the Genesis mission timeframe, for instance, the transition from Solar

weak, but by its end (a solar minimum around 2019-2020) the inner belt proton flux, especially in the SAA,

proton flux at low altitudes. These solar-driven variations prompt the question of whether the USO onboard
can exhibit any measurable response to changes in the radiation environment tied to solar activity. In theory,

2.3
SPENVIS
SPENVIS (Space Environment Information System) is an integrated platform developed by the European
Space Agency (ESA) to model the space environment and its effects on spacecraft. It provides a set of stan-
dard models for various space environments. Through SPENVIS, mission designers and researchers can
simulate conditions such as the Earth’s radiation belts (trapped high-energy particles), solar energetic parti-
cle events, galactic cosmic rays, plasmas in the magnetosphere/ionosphere, atmospheric gases, micromete-
oroids and orbital debris, and even geomagnetic field configurations [32]. By combining these environment
models with orbit trajectory generators and shielding analysis tools, SPENVIS allows rapid evaluation of ra-
diation exposure and other environmental factors for a given spacecraft mission scenario [32].
One of SPENVIS’s key features is access to well-established radiation belt models, which is particularly rele-
vant for this work. In our context, SPENVIS will be used to estimate the radiation environment along the con-
sidered mission’s orbit. Specifically, SPENVIS offers both the classic NASA AE8/AP8 models and the newer
AE9/AP9 models for trapped electron (AE) and proton (AP) fluxes. The AE8/AP8 models are static, empirical
models based on satellite data from the 1960s–1980s, providing long-term average fluxes in the Van Allen
belts, and they can be tuned for solar maximum or minimum. In contrast, the AE9/AP9 models (developed
by AFRL/NASA) incorporate more recent measurements and statistical variability, yielding probabilistic flux
estimates with uncertainty bounds and allowing for dynamic conditions, but they are computationally heavier.
By running these models in SPENVIS for the relevant orbital parameters (altitude, inclination, right ascension
of the ascending node, mission duration, etc.), one can obtain estimates of particle fluxes and dose rates that
the onboard oscillator will encounter. The output from SPENVIS is obtained in graphical or .txt form and it will
later be used in the Methodology (Chapter 1) to correlate with the oscillator performance.
2.4
Previous evidence and starting point of this thesis
Over roughly the last two decades, several geodetic missions have shown that radiation effects on USOs are
not only detectable but also operationally relevant. Empirical corrections have been derived for SAA-related
perturbations on Jason-1 and later refined for Jason-2/T2L2 and other missions, where DORIS frequency off-
sets were mapped over the anomaly and modelled in order to restore precise orbit determination performance
(e.g. [24], [4]). More recently, Sentinel-3A has provided an opportunity to study these effects with co-located
GPS clock estimates, confirming that trapped protons in the SAA produce transient frequency excursions that
can be modelled and corrected [5,14,33]. So far, however, most of this work remains mission-specific and it
is largely limited to LEO.

3.1
Sentinel-3A SP3 data
I, for this work, used daily SP3 orbit/clock files for Sentinel-3A provided by TUM. Each file spans one UTC day
and contains time-tagged satellite state vectors and the satellite clock correction with 1-minute cadence (1441
epochs from 00:00 to 24:00). The files follow the IGS SP3-c specification: satellite positions (ITRF frame)
and velocities are given per epoch together with a clock correction with respect to the file’s timescale (GPS
Time) [34]. In SP3 the clock field is expressed in microseconds; in the pipeline it is converted to nanoseconds
for convenience (the variable name is clk_ns). The initial analysis window focuses on December 2024
(solar-maximum conditions), and it later expands to additional months for contrast. From each SP3 day I
extract:
• r(t), v(t): Cartesian position and velocity (ECEF, Earth-Centered Earth-Fixed).
• ∆tobs(t): observed satellite clock correction.
Here, ∆tobs(t) is the satellite clock offset with respect to the GNSS system time, as estimated in the precise
orbit determination and written in the SP3 file. To extract them, daily SP3 files are parsed with the provided
readprev.m function, which reads the SP3-c records and returns the satellite state vector time series and
the epochs in GPS Time. Specifically, readprev.m outputs xv(t) with rows [x, y, z, ∆tobs, ˙x, ˙y, ˙z] (SI
units; clock in seconds), and a vector of epochs (converted from MJD to hours in the pipeline). For other

Time alignment over multi-day windows
When stacking more than a few orbits and repeating the previous steps for 30 days, I observe that the simu-
lated trapped-proton series from SPENVIS gradually loses phase with respect to the clock residuals: in the

first hours the proton spikes precede the negative clock peaks (as expected), whereas over a month some
spikes appear after the peaks. This is not a change in causality but a time-alignment artifact.
In these runs SPENVIS is fed with a single circular orbit built from the first-epoch osculating elements and
sampled at ∆t = 60 s, without updating the orbital geometry on subsequent days. The real Sentinel-3A or-
bit is Sun-synchronous: its node precesses (J2) in inertial space at about 1◦/day, while the ground track and
local-time constraints follow a repeat pattern. If the nodal evolution and repeat-cycle phasing are not reflected
when generating the radiation series, the simulated SPENVIS ground track effectively repeats the first-day
geometry, while the real satellite advances according to its repeat pattern. The mismatch accumulates as a
phase error in the SAA crossings, producing an artificial time shift between the simulated flux series and the
clock data that becomes noticeable after ∼12–15 days within a 30-day dataset.
To correct this:
• SPENVIS could be run separately for each day using the osculating elements at 00:00 UTC and gen-
erate a 24 h time series. This captures the day-to-day RAAN evolution and it keeps the time base
locally consistent.
• A daily physical lag τ between Φp(t) and δtres(t) could be estimated via cross-correlation within
±30 min (typical τ ≈3–15 min), then shift the proton series by τ before overlay and fitting.
• In the longer term, the SPENVIS tool itself could be extended to correct this.
In this chapter I only flag the issue and keep the pipeline as it is, because for the purposes here (SAA passage
identification, energy-wise comparisons, and mean trend fitting) minute-level phase errors do not change the
conclusions. The corrective options above will be adopted in a dedicated refinement pass if tighter timing is
required.
3.2.2
POES/MetOp data
Although several months across different years were inspected during the exploration phase, for the final
validation of the algorithm and for selecting model parameters I will later focus on two reference months:
October 2019 and October 2024. The reasoning is that this pair provides a comparison between a month in
(near) solar minimum and one in (near) solar maximum, so that both the USO behaviour and the radiation
environment can be contrasted under different solar conditions. To cross-check the reliability of the empirical
SPENVIS outputs used elsewhere, for these two months I also process in-situ measurements from the NOAA
POES/MetOp mission family; these measurements show a consistent morphology and order of magnitude
with the SPENVIS simulations, especially over the SAA.

3.3
Pre-processing and filtering
3.3.1
Preliminary filtering
Problem statement and filtering requirements
Clock residuals from Sentinel-3A are sampled at one-minute cadence and they show two kinds of beaviour:
first, the narrow and intermittent negative spikes associated with high radiation areas (the features of interest),
and secondly, the background fluctuations and slow components that make the spikes harder to isolate, and
that later later amplify noise when differentiating to compute the drift of the clock. The goal of this exploratory
phase is to test several simple filters and decide which properties are necessary for the final pipeline, in order
to:
• preserve spike morphology (depth, width, timing) as much as possible;
• reduce high-frequency jitter so that the time derivative (drift) is interpretable;
• avoid introducing spurious ringing or overshoot around spikes;
• keep the method simple and reproducible across many days.
These requirements lead to short-window smoothing for noise suppression and, when needed, very light
detrending for slow components, while explicitly avoiding any operation that would flatten or distort radiation-
induced peaks.

3.3.2
Baseline removal with High-Pass filtering
High–Pass filtering (HPF)
After the exploratory tests, the residual time series still exhibits slow baseline wander that obscures spatial
patterns in the drift map. A high-pass filter is therefore introduced to stabilise the baseline before numerical
differentiation, while preserving the short-lived structures of interest.
A high-pass filter attenuates low-frequency components (slow trends) and it lets higher–frequency changes
pass [38]. It is specified by a cutoff frequency fc (the −3 dB point), or equivalently a cutoff period Tc = 1/fc:
variations slower than Tc are suppressed, while faster variations are largely preserved. In this work a zero-
phase Butterworth IIR (Infinite Impulse Response) is used so that no timing shift is introduced. A Butterworth
IIR filter is a recursive high/low-pass with a maximally flat passband (no ripples). From this point on, filtering
tests are first performed on single-day data sets, so that the effect on the features of interest can be clearly
inspected without them being masked. The same processing steps are then applied to the full 9-day (or
eventually longer) timeline to produce global drift maps and verify whether the new filtering method actually
improves the results.
MATLAB implementation
HPFs in MATLAB are designed as Butterworth IIR filters using designfilt(’highpassiir’, ...
’FilterOrder’, ’HalfPowerFrequency’, fc, ’SampleRate’, fs). The design specifies the
half-power frequency (the conventional −3 dB point) via HalfPowerFrequency, epo is the epoch vector
(days) and the sampling rate is fs = 1/∆t with ∆t in seconds; the cutoff is selected by a period (e.g. 6 h,
2 h, 102 min) and converted to frequency fc = 1/Tc [39].
Variables:
• fs [Hz]: sampling rate computed from the median cadence.
• fc [Hz]: half-power cutoff frequency; Tc = 1/fc.
• residual [ns]: raw clock residual time series.

3.3.3
Refinement of High-Pass Filter
Rationale
After the cutoff sweep analyzed in the previous subsection, I adopt a zero-phase Butterworth HPF with a
102 min half-power period to stabilise the residual baseline before differentiation. However, when the HPF is
applied to the entire series, it still reduces the amplitude of the radiation-driven features that I want to quantify
later. I therefore refine the pipeline so that the HPF acts only on the slow baseline while leaving those short
features completely untouched.
MATLAB implementation
From this point on, I use peaks to denote short-lived, spike-like excursions in the clock residual. I do not
describe the peak-finding logic here, as it will be carried out in 3.4. To isolate the peaks, I only assume a
conservative boolean vector mask_peaks(t) ∈{0, 1} that flags those intervals. Before building the mask
and filtering, I bridge missing samples by linear interpolation with the nearest end values and I recentre the
series by removing its median so that the raw residual oscillates around zero.

Refinement of Moving-Average
Now, for completeness, I mirror the same masking strategy to the moving-average (MA) filter. The two ap-

Rationale

removing orbit-scale curvature.

3.4
Peak detection algorithms
The goal of this section is to detect radiation-correlated clock excursions in Sentinel-3A residuals and to ex-
tract a consistent set of peak metrics for later correlation with trapped-proton flux. Two MATLAB routines
are implemented: a base single–day detection algorithm (detectpeaks.m) and a multi–day detection al-
gorithm (multidetectpeaks.m). Both share the same physics-aware pre-processing and allow the user
to choose, via an input flag, between two baseline-control strategies: the high–pass filter (HPF) applied to
the baseline only, and the centred moving-average (MA) computed while avoiding the peak samples, as they
were both presented in 3.3. Events are then validated against two acceptance criteria, whose definitions and
thresholds are detailed later, and they are characterised by depth, prominence, area, flux, geolocation and
orbit direction. The quantitative comparison of HPF vs. MA and the month-to-month results are presented in
the Results Chapter (4).

4.2
Solar-cycle and solar activity influence
4.2
Solar-cycle and solar activity influence
As introduced in 2.2.3, trapped-proton fluxes in the SAA are modulated by the solar cycle through two coun-
teracting mechanisms. First, the CRAND source (Cosmic Ray Albedo Neutron Decay) feeds the inner belt
from galactic cosmic rays (GCRs): GCR intensity is higher at solar minimum, so the source term for trapped
protons is enhanced; conversely, at solar maximum frequent solar disturbances (Forbush decreases) sup-
press the GCR flux and reduce the CRAND injection. Secondly, enhanced EUV heating at solar maximum
expands the upper atmosphere, increasing collisional losses for low–altitude protons and thereby depleting
the low–L–shell inner belt; at solar minimum the atmosphere contracts and these losses are reduced. Both
effects imply that, particularly at low L (i.e., over the SAA core), trapped-proton fluxes tend to be lower at
solar maximum than at solar minimum.
Monthly maps reported in [22] show that the SAA’s northern/low-L sector exhibits lower fluxes during solar
maximum (e.g., 2014) than during solar minimum (e.g., 2019), while at higher L the response can differ in
sign, reflecting the balance between atmospheric loss and magnetospheric source terms. This motivates a
direct, month-to-month comparison of the clock response across different solar-cycle phases.
Expectation for the clock
A stronger trapped-proton environment should produce more frequent and/or deeper negative excusions in
the filtered residual δtres(t) via a more negative instantaneous drift ˙δt(t) while crossing the SAA.
4.2.1

To probe the solar-cycle imprint on the clock, I process two representative months with the previously vali-
dated algorithms: October 2019 (near solar minimum) and October 2024 (near solar maximum). The peak-
detection pipeline from before is applied unchanged: baseline removal uses the baseline-only centred Moving
Average (window = 80 samples); candidate events are validated by a combined SAA geolocation mask and
a proton-flux threshold, and peak depth is measured on the detrended baseline. As trapped-proton proxies
I use SPENVIS AP8 (with solar-min/solar-max settings matched to the month), with MetOp real data as a
statistical cross-check. For each month I produce world maps of the clock drift, world maps of the residu-

Solar activity and geomagnetic storm
Building on the framework sketched in 2.2.3 (SEPs and storm-time geomagnetic forcing), I hypothesise a

and preliminary and it requires a controlled analysis to extract a robust relationship. As a concrete future

and, within an SAA mask, a correlation between the daily count of validated peaks, their median depth, and
the drift amplitude with Dst/AE could be carried out.

Towards Genesis
5
Towards Genesis
The empirical models derived in this work establish a quantitative link between local trapped-proton flux and
short-term drift of the on-board Ultra-Stable Oscillator. In particular, the linear model in the SAA-wide region
and the saturation model in the SAA-core describe how the absolute local drift increases with flux over two
different spatial scales. Although these models are calibrated on Sentinel-3A data, they can be interpreted as
a first approximation of a generic "radiation-to-drift transfer function" for USO-based timing systems operating
in a proton-rich environment.
A natural next step is to use this transfer function to obtain first order estimates of the clock behaviour for
future missions like Genesis, whose Medium Earth Orbit will cross regions of the inner radiation belt that
have not hosted a USO yet. The idea would be that for a given Genesis orbit, one can generate a representa-
tive trajectory and compute the expected trapped-proton environment along it using tools such as SPENVIS.
The resulting time series of local flux F(t) can then be fed into the best-performing flux-drift model from this
thesis. This would yield predicted distributions of additional clock drift ∆D(t) along the Genesis orbit, from
which one can derive metrics such as typical and extreme drifts per orbit, expected number of large events
per day or cumulative degradation over mission-relevant time scales.
In practice, the Genesis USO will not be identical to the Sentinel-3A clock in terms of shielding, frequency,
electronics and thermal environment. Any prediction should therefore include a scaling factor accounting for
the different hardware and shielding configuration. Even with this limitation, the approach could already pro-
vide an order-of-magnitude estimate of how often the Genesis clock might experience "uncomfortable" levels
of drift in orbit and whether these events are rare excursions or a systematic contribution to the timing error
budget. Such information would be valuable, for instance, to size the ground calibration strategy, to define
safe operating margins for time transfer links (GNSS, DORIS) or to evaluate the benefit of additional shielding
mass.

Conclusions and Future Work
6
Conclusions and Future Work
The analysis carried out in this thesis shows that radiation-induced perturbations on the Sentinel-3A USO are
not isolated anomalies, but they follow a systematic and locally predictable trend with trapped-proton flux in
the belt. The combination of a linear model in the SAA-wide region and a saturation model in the SAA-core
provides a compact description of how the absolute local drift responds to increasing flux over two different
spatial scales. In this sense, the fitted relations can be interpreted as a first empirical "radiation-to-drift trans-
fer function" for USO-based timing systems operating in a proton-rich environment.
Fitting the models to two opposite phases of the solar cycle, such as October 2019 (near solar minimum) and
October 2024 (near solar maximum), provides a first empirical range for the corresponding model param-
eters and it suggests that the underlying flux-drift relationship remains broadly stable across very different
radiation regimes. This supports the idea that the models derived here can serve as a starting point for
extrapolations to other epochs, orbits and missions, at least at the level of order-of-magnitude estimates for
radiation-induced drift.
More generally, the flux-drift models developed in this thesis should be seen as a first-order description rather
than a final answer. They capture a robust trend between local trapped-proton flux and additional USO drift,
but the underlying transfer function can and should be further refined and enriched, for example by:
• including energy-resolved flux information instead of a single integral channel;
• adding geomagnetic parameters (e.g. L-shell, magnetic latitude) to account for the structure of the
inner belt;
• incorporating proxies for shielding and temperature, which are known to affect clock behaviour;
• allowing for more flexible non-linear response terms, beyond the simple linear and saturation forms
tested here.
A natural next step would be to extend the methodology developed here to other days, months and years, and
to other satellites already operating. Repeating the same analysis on those missions would make it possible
to validate the functional form of the flux-drift relationship over different radiation regimes, to quantify how
mission-specific design choices (clock technology, shielding, orbit geometry) modify the model coefficients,
and to bound the range of plausible Genesis behaviours by analogy with existing spacecraft.
A very preliminary test of this transfer-function idea has already been performed within this work by applying
the model calibrated on October 2019 data to the October 2024 dataset; the corresponding plots can be
found in Appendix 6. Although the implementation is still exploratory, it illustrates how the Sentinel-3A results
can be propagated to a different radiation regime. The same workflow, combined with dedicated Genesis
environment runs (like SPENVIS ones for trapped-proton conditions at the planned MEO) and hardware
characterisation of the Genesis USO and its shielding, would provide a concrete starting point for a future
study of radiation-induced drift on satellites’ USOs.

Appendix 1: How to run the code
This appendix describes the MATLAB material delivered together with the thesis and it explains how it can
be used to reproduce the main results or to analyse new datasets.
The scripts are organised around three main blocks:
• core utilities for reading Sentinel-3A data and preparing orbital parameters for SPENVIS;
• a peak-detection toolbox to identify radiation-induced events in the on-board clock;
• a set of case-study and model-fitting scripts that apply the toolbox to specific months or days and
generate the figures used in the thesis.
All files are contained in the ZIP archive submitted with the thesis. Before running any script, the root folder
of the archive should be added to the MATLAB path.
Code structure
Core data-handling utilities
• clockrel.m, gpstime.m, readprev.m: functions provided by the supervisor to read Sentinel-3
SP3 files, they convert the time tags and compute the basic clock residuals with respect to a relativistic
reference model. These routines are used in essentially all analysis scripts and they should not need
to be modified.
• spenvis_orbital_parameters.m: a function written for this thesis that takes as input the epoch,
position and velocity of the satellite and outputs the orbital parameters required by SPENVIS (semi-
major axis, eccentricity, inclination, argument of perigee, right ascension of the ascending node and
mean anomaly). It is mainly used to generate the SPENVIS runs that provide trapped-proton fluxes
along the Sentinel-3A orbit.
Peak-detection toolbox
• detectpeaks.m: core routine that analyses one day of clock residuals together with the corre-
sponding proton-flux time series. It applies the chosen detrending method (high-pass filter or moving
average, with peaks masked), it identifies negative peaks in the residuals associated with SAA cross-
ings and high radiation environment, and it returns a MATLAB structure containing the list of events
and their main metrics (time, depth, prominence, area, local flux, latitude/longitude, orbit direction, to
which in the scripts one can add the uncertainty on the depth.).
• multidetectpeaks.m: wrapper around detectpeaks.m that loops over several consecutive
days. It is used mainly for month-long analyses and when called appropriately it returns aggregated
arrays of peak properties, together with summary statistics and optional plots.
• flux_peaks_saa.m: short helper function, built in the same spirit as detectpeaks.m, that ex-
tracts the largest proton-flux peaks from MetOp/MEPED data. It is used in the October 2019 and
October 2024 analyses to compare clock behaviour with real flux measurements.

All analysis scripts rely on three main types of input data:
• daily Sentinel-3A SP3 orbit and clock files, providing satellite position and clock offset;

• auxiliary products such as geomagnetic coordinates or solar-activity indices (ACE), used only in a few

The SP3 and flux data are interpolated to a common 1-minute grid. The main outputs produced by the scripts

• time series of clock residuals and their filtered baseline;
• lists of detected peaks with associated metrics (time, depth, local flux, uncertainty, ascending/descend-

• co-located datasets of local drift and proton flux, later used by the model-fitting scripts;
• diagnostic figures: daily time series with highlighted peaks, world maps of drift and flux, and scatter

The exact filenames of the .mat outputs are usually documented in the header comments of each script and
they can be adapted if needed.
SPENVIS trapped-proton flux
SPENVIS is used to generate trapped-proton fluxes along the Sentinel-3A orbit (AP-8/AE-8 models). Each
run is stored as an ASCII table, with one line per time step, containing at least:
• time tag (seconds from start of the run);
• geographic latitude, longitude and altitude of the spacecraft;
• additional geomagnetic quantities (e.g. L–shell, B/B0 etc.);
• integral proton flux above a given energy threshold.
The case-study scripts read these tables and store the result as MATLAB vectors that are then passed
to detectpeaks.m or multidetectpeaks.m. The exact filename of the SPENVIS file to be used is
defined near the top of each driver script.

• lat, lon, alt: satellite latitude, longitude and altitude;
• p_flux_tel0(1:6) and p_flux_tel90(1:6): directional proton fluxes for channels P1-P6 in
the two telescopes (units cm−2 s−1 sr−1).
In the thesis we mainly use the P6 channel of telescope 0 (highest energy protons aligned with the magnetic
field), but the other channels are kept available for future work.
Running a standard analysis
The general workflow is similar for all case studies and it can be summarised as follows:
1. Prepare the environment Extract the ZIP archive, open MATLAB in the root folder and add it (and its
subfolders) to the path using addpath or the MATLAB interface.
2. Check input paths and settings Open the script corresponding to the period of interest (for instance
Sentinel3A_02jan2024.m, S3A_2019_OCT.m or S3A_2024_OCT_DLR.m) and adjust, if nec-
essary, the variables that point to the SP3 files, SPENVIS outputs and MetOp data.
3. Run the driver script Execute the script from the MATLAB editor (Run or Run section button). The
script, or each section singularly, will:
(a) call clockrel.m, gpstime.m and readprev.m to build the daily clock-residual series;
(b) read the corresponding proton-flux time series from SPENVIS;
(c) pass the data to detectpeaks.m or multidetectpeaks.m to identify SAA-related events
and save the resulting structures to .mat files;
(d) compute local drift;
(e) generate the plots associated with that particular case study.
Then any different file may have different specific sections that can be run, as mentioned above.
4. Fit the radiation-drift models Once the co-located flux-drift datasets have been generated for a given
month (in this case it has only been done for October 2019 and 2024), run ModelFit_SAA_wide.m
and ModelFit_SAA_core.m. These scripts perform the fits of the three models and output both the
fitted parameters and the figures. Finally, Comparison_Models.m can be used to summarise and
compare the performance of the models.

Using the toolbox on new data
To apply the analysis to a different period or to another satellite with similar data products, a typical approach
is:
• adapt one of the existing case-study scripts (for instance Sentinel3A_02jan2024.m for a single
day or S3A_2019_OCT.m for a full month) by changing the list of SP3 files and the corresponding
proton-flux inputs;
• ensure that the time grid and units are consistent with the assumptions in detectpeaks.m and
multidetectpeaks.m (1-minute sampling, clock residuals in ns, flux in the same units as used for
SPENVIS/MetOp);
• run the adapted script to generate a new set of peak lists and co-located flux-drift datasets, which can
then be used directly by the existing model-fitting scripts.
This modular structure is intended to make it easy to reproduce the figures shown in the thesis and, at the
same time, to extend the analysis to future datasets or to other missions.
