1. ABSTRACT 
 
European Fisheries management is currently in the process of investigating additional methods 
that would further enhance the credibility of stock assessments. However, due to various reasons 
there has been a reservation regarding the utilization of fishing data provided by the fishermen as 
a supplementing management tool.   
 
This thesis deals with the application of Fully Documented Fisheries (FDF) data as an additional 
tool for fisheries management. Based on the FDF data, various spatial distribution maps for the 
time period 2010-2015 were plotted against spatial distribution maps based on the International 
Bottom Trawl Survey (IBTS) data for the same period. Through the comparison between the 
maps of the 2 datasets it was shown that the FDF data can be used as an enhancing or a 
supplementary tool to the surveys.  
 
In addition, GAM models were utilized in order to model the proportional distribution of the 
small and large size cod in the catches in both datasets. In addition the GAM models were used 
in order to compare whether or not an additional scientific value exist in the collection of fishing 
information haul by haul instead of trip by trip. 
 
 
Abstrakt 
 
Europa Kommissionens fiskeriforvaltning er i gang med at undersøge metoder, der yderligere vil 
styrke troværdigheden af bestandsvurderingerne. Grundet forskellige årsager har der været et 
forbehold med hensyn til udnyttelsen af fiskernes data som grundlæggende forvaltningsværktøj. 
Denne afhandling omhandler anvendelsen af fuldt dokumenteret fiskeri (FDF) data som et 
supplerende værktøj for fiskeriforvaltningen. Baseret på FDF-data blev forskellige rumlige 
fordelingskort for tidsperioden 2010-2015 plottet, mod rumlige fordelingskort baseret på den 
internationale bundtrawl survey’s (IBTS) data for samme periode. Ved sammenligningen af 
kortene over de 2 datasæt, blev det vist at FDF data kan bruges som et forøgende eller et 
supplerende værktøj til undersøgelserne. 
Derudover blev GAM modeller benyttet til at modellere den forholdsmæssige fordeling af små 
og store torsk i fangsterne i begge datasæt. Desuden blev GAM modeller brugt til at vurdere 
hvorvidt indsamling af fiskeriinformationer på træk enkeltvis kunne give mere viden end 
informationer indsamlet for ture enkeltvis.

3. INTRODUCTION 
 
From ancient times, fishing has been one of the major tools in providing food resources for 
humanity. However, as the fisheries were being developed (and along with them our knowledge 
regarding their influence in the ecosystem and more importantly on the fish stocks), the scientific 
community eventually recognize the finite of living aquatic resources (fish stocks), although 
renewable, and their need for proper management (FAO, 2003).  
 
Effective fisheries management is depending on accurate accounts of the removed amounts of 
fish from the seas (discards and landings) (Faunce, 2011). Until the beginning of 21st century 
almost all fishery data available from both scientific surveys and commercial sources had a 
spatial component that was largely overlooked (Booth 2000). This might have been one of the 
reasons that in some cases led to inaccurate abundance estimations, which in turn could have led 
to ambiguous perceptions regarding different aspects of a species’ biology such as distribution, 
growth and reproduction (Booth 2000). Partially due to the early overlook of the spatial 
distribution, the European fisheries were caught in a vicious circle, where the introduction of low 
Total Allowable Catches (TAC) for cod led to catches above the permitted and substantial 
amounts of cod were being discarded or landed on the black market. Due to poor regulation and 
monitoring, the resulting reduction in the quality of the catch data undermined the quality and 
reliability of the stock assessments, which led to an even lower TAC policy (Ulrich et al., 2015).  
 
In order to counter this deficiency, the International Council for the Exploration of the Sea 
(ICES) introduced the International Bottom Trawl Survey (IBTS) in the North Sea, in order to 
provide with a constant, dependable and standardized data any changes in the spatial distribution 
and abundance of the fish stocks and the biological parameters of the stocks. In order for the 
provided data to be as reliable as possible, the survey is taking place during the 1st and the 3rd 
quarter of each year (ICES, 2012) (ICES, 2013). 
 
Furthermore, in order to increase the quality of the stock assessments, while at the same time the 
likelihood of a stock collapse would be reduced and the vulnerability of the stocks would be 
decreased, a variety of different management approaches were introduced (Watson et al., 2004). 
These approaches are either direct or indirect and are means that have been introduced in order to 
control the inflow and outflow of the fisheries. As inputs can be classified the vessel licensing 
(indirect) or the limitation in the entry of new participants in the fishery sector (direct), while as 
outputs can be considered the numerous regulations and laws concerning the catching techniques 
(indirect) and various technical regulation as well as the constitution of a catch quota system 
(direct). 
 
==PAGE:6==
6 
 
In addition, the control requirements of a restrictive TAC led to the introduction of new 
monitoring strategies that require almost real time catch data for quota debiting at fleet or even 
fisherman level (Faunce et al., 2015). 
 
As the landing statistics provided by the fishermen suffer from a variety of deficiencies such as 
missing and/or incomplete data, one of the major steps that have to be implemented in order to 
ensure an improvement of the stock assessments is the enhancement of the information provided 
by them (Watson et al., 2004). The introduction and improvement of Vessel Monitoring Systems 
(VMS) has allowed fisheries scientists to take into account the spatial dimension of fisheries 
data. The incorporation of the spatial dimension is a crucial achievement in fisheries research 
and management as the new ecosystem management approach of fisheries render the traditional 
datasets of landings – effort inadequate for many purposes. Nonetheless, an integration of 
fishermen logbook catches data (even if their estimation might be biased) with scientific data 
might be necessary in order to create dependable distribution maps of catches and estimations 
(Bastardie et al., 2010) (Gerritsen and Lordan, 2010). 
 
Fully Documented Fisheries (FDF) might be an additional tool in order to further enhance the 
credibility of the stock assessments. It was recently introduced in trials as a supplement to the 
already implemented TACs and Common Fisheries Policy (CFP). The main interests of the FDF 
data compared to the usual catch data, are the information haul by haul that provides instead of 
trip by trip, and the inclusion of cod discards in contrast to the traditional fisheries that provide 
only the amount of landings. The idea behind the new management tool is to allow the fishermen 
to freely optimize their catches through optimization of catch selectivity during their fishing 
activities, while at the same time they will be responsible for the impact of their activities on the 
stocks as a whole. This can be achieved by the documentation of the entire activity from the 
fishermen themselves, meaning that in contrast to the previous situation (where only the landings 
were documented) the fishermen will have to monitor, report and document all the catches 
(Kindt - Larsen et al., 2011). 
 
However, very few steps have been taken in order to achieve a better stock management through 
the integration of FDF and scientific data. Even though there is an ever increasing demand for 
better and more representative assessments, very few studies have been conducted since 2008 
regarding the scientific value of FDF data. Reasons on to why there is little research on the 
additional value of the FDF data can be attributed to the reservation of the scientific community 
towards the data provided by the fishermen. This reservation is mainly centering on a) the 
technical challenges associated with fisheries and b) issues associated with imbalance between 
fisheries and surveys (Murray et al., 2008). 
 
The main aim of this report is to conduct an (initial and superficial) investigation on the 
additional scientific value of the haul by haul data and the additional information on small fish 
==PAGE:7==
7 
 
distribution provided by the logbook data filled by the fishermen , in order to fill (up to a point) 
the lack of knowledge on the stated issue. For that investigation, the report will try to answer 
three (3) main questions that aim to provide a better understanding on the issue: 
 
1. Is there any coexistence between small and large cod and which are the areas where the 
fishermen encounter small cod in the North Sea? 
2. Is there any correlation between the FDF patterns and the scientific observations provided 
by the International Bottom Trawling System (IBTS)? 
3. Is there any additional value in collecting the fishing information haul by haul instead of 
trip by trip? 
 
Furthermore, through the comparison of FDF and IBTS datasets, we’ll try to review the quality 
and accuracy of the FDF data, through a 3-step procedure: 
 
 by applying statistical methods for mapping and analyzing spatially explicit data, 
 by analyzing the patterns of cod aggregation in the North Sea and  
 by evaluating the added value of high resolution fisheries data through a comparison 
between the haul by haul and trip by trip results. 
 
The overall objectives of this thesis are as follows: 
 
 Review of the quality, coverage and accuracy of the FDF dataset, 
 Apply statistical methods for mapping and analyzing spatially-explicit data, 
 Analyze patterns of cod aggregations in the North Sea, 
 Evaluate the added value of high resolution fisheries data.

4. MATERIAL AND METHODS 
 
A total number of 2 different datasets were used in the data analysis of this Master thesis, namely 
Fully Documented Fisheries (FDF) and International Bottom Trawl Survey (IBTS) data. The 
FDF Danish data were obtained from the DTU Aqua database while the IBTS datasets were 
acquired from the ICES database (www.ices.dk). FDF datasets contained information 
exclusively for Danish fishing vessels participating in the FDF trials and their activities in the 
North Sea (both European and Norwegian water), while IBTS data contained data from scientific 
research that took place in the European waters of the North Sea. 
 
As both datasets corresponded to fisheries activity taking place in the North Sea, the acquisition 
of North Sea maps was necessary in order to achieve a better description of the activities. For 
this reason two maps were used, one based on the shipping forecast zones and one geographical, 
as their use would make the description easier if the activity was being described based on the 
map areas.

4.5. VMS SPATIAL DISTRIBUTION 
 
The VMS data from fifteen (15) fishing vessels (trawlers) was analyzed in order to probe the 
fishing activity in the different areas of North Sea. The analyses were conducted through the use 
of the VMStools package for R (Hintzen et al., 2012) as it was considered at the moment that it 
would provide fishing activity maps in a faster way than a more conventional method. A time 
period of six (6) years (2010-2015) was analyzed and the activity of the vessels was discerned 
based on three different speed profiles. For activities with speed profile was less than 2 knots 
they were categorized as “No fishing”. Activities with speed more than 2 and less than 3 they 
were categorized as “Fishing”, whereas those with speed over 3 were categorized as “Steaming”. 
After this, only the data connected to fishing activity was kept. We then gridded the VMS fishing 
positions in cell of x by x nm to produce a Figureof fishing effort, either for whole time period 
and for each year separately.

4.7. DATA ANALYSIS 
 
We first created the Figurewith the catches distribution i) based on the fishermen data for the 
whole 6 year period (2010-2015) and ii) for the same time period based on the scientific data. 
The distinction of the large and the smaller fish was one of the most important parameters and 
major issues of the thesis. The logbook data provided by the fishermen was consisted of the 
weights per size per haul per trip, in direct contrast to the IBTS data, which contained either the 
length per haul per trip or the age per haul per trip. In order for the two data sets to become 
comparable, the length per haul in the IBTS data was converted into weight through the use of 
the length – weight relationship: 
 
W = a*L^b 
Where: 
W is the weight, 
a is the scaling coefficient for the weight at length for cod, 
b is the shape parameter for the body form of the fish species. 
 
After the data handling of both datasets had reached the desired level and their format was 
similar, they were merged based on the haul number per trip. 
 
We aimed to separate cod catches between juvenile (referred to as “small” throughout) and adult 
(referred to as “large” throughout). “Small size” would include discards, but it was also looked 
which landings would include juvenile fish. In order to achieve that, the easiest way was to 
allocate the fish into the two categories (small and large) based on their age - based maturity 
stage. The age is not available in commercial catches, so it was necessary to translate the market 
categories into age. The first step into that direction was creation of six figures (one for each 
year) linking the various market sizes and their respective weights with their attributed age. The 
various market sizes and their respective weight range were calculated based on the 
categorization provided by the Hanstholm Fiskeauktion. Length at maturity is provided by 
various sources, but there is no consistency between them (e.g. (Morris and Green 2002), 
(Knickle and Rose 2013) and ICES), so it was decided to allocate the corresponding age for each 
market size based on information from the ICES WGNSSK report 2015 and independent of the 
first maturity. Thus, based on figure 1 (ICES WGNSSK report 2015), the various age ranges for 
each size group were estimated, as shown in tables 5 and 6, with the categorization of the fish in 
one of the two groups being conducted based on their age. Cod is partly mature at age 2 and 3 
and fully matures at age 4, so it was decided to only include age groups 1 & 2 in the “small fish” 
category, with the other ages (>3) to be included in the “large fish” category. The ages per 
weight groups that were created were the same for the years 2010-2013 and 2014-2015 (table 5 
and 6), due to the fact that according to figure 1, the weight per age remains unchanged for the 
periods 2010-2013 and 2014-2015.

4.8. STATISTICAL ANALYSIS 
 
The contents of the FDF and IBTS datasets contain information of fish catches and discards, 
collected from the North Sea and the Skagerrak. However, all that information is not continuous; 
therefore a linear model is inadequate. In addition, the datasets are consisting of independent 
observations representing one of two possible outcomes with the same probability of “success” 
for each outcome, making them ideal for binomial distribution. For these reasons the statistical 
analysis was implemented with a GAM model with a binomial distribution. 
 
For the statistical analysis of the project, three GAM models were created. The first model was 
created in order to investigate the influence of the fishing activity and the survey on the amount 
of landings. For that reason, the landings were plotted as a logarithmic function of the survey and 
the fish size type. In this model, the intercept was the logarithmic average of the annual landings 
from the fishing activity. 
 
The first GAM model had a logarithmic formula log(Land)~Survey*factor(type), based on the 
formula log(y) ~ (x * z). The logarithmic values of landings were modeled as a function of the 
interaction of the 2 different kinds of survey (fishing and IBTS) and the two types of fish size 
(small and large).  
 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
The other two GAM model were a multiple regression and was created in order to examine the 
probability of co-existence between small and large cod in haul by haul and trip by trip 
respectively. Both formulas were cbind(large, small) ~ Survey + s(br_pos,lg_pos) + s(year, 
bs="cc", k=5) + s(month, bs="cc", k=5) and both were based in the formula f(y) ~ x + s(z). The 
size categories (large, small) were plotted as a function of the survey type, the year and the 
month in order to model the effect of these parameters on the size proportion of the catches.

6. DISCUSSION  
6.1. SPATIAL DISTRIBUTION OF FISHING EFFORT (VMS) 
 
As it is evident by the VMS map (Figure 3a-f), the area of fishing activity In the North Sea 
remains almost the same for the entire time-period.  The areas where the bulk of operations take 
place are the Skagerrak, Fisher, N. & S. Utsire and remain unchanged  throughout the period. 
Small changes can be observed in the activity taking place in the areas closer to the central North 
Sea (Forties, Dogger),  as well as in the areas closer to the British shores and the areas closer to 
western Europe (Thames, Humber and German bigth). These results come into agreement with 
those from Plet-Hansen 2016 and in contrast with the results of van Helmond et al., 2016, who 
came to the conclusion that the fishermen changed behaviour after their participation in the FDF. 
This can be mainly attributed to the fact that the study performed by van Helmond et al., 2016 
included vessels from different country which in addition targeted a variety of species (although 
the main target was cod), while the fleet in this study targeted only cod. Furthermore the study 
fleet from van Helmond et al., 2016 was operating mainly in the southern part of the North Sea 
(Thames, Humber and German bight) where the distribution of large size cod is not as dense 
(maps 4 & 7) as in the northern part where the Danish fleets are mainly operating, a fact also 
pointed in Engelhard et al., 2014 and in van Helmond et al., 2016. 
6.2. SPATIAL DISTRIBUTION OF COD CATCHES 
 
The spatial distribution of cod was plotted based on the FDF data either for the entire period 
(figures 4 and 5) or for selected years (figures 6 and 7). In addition maps of spatial distribution 
were produced based on the IBTS data (figures 8-10). The maps that were produced from both 
datasets were compared (figure 12). The reason for that comparison is because demersal trawl 
surveys, as well as in many place around the world, are the most common dataset used for 
conducting assessments of stock status independently from fisheries activities, like IBTS in the 
North Sea (WGNSSK report 2015). In addition, scientific trawl surveys have a higher validity 
and reliability than the fishing-dependent information, since they are subjected to a series of 
standardizations which will further minimize the error in the projections (Kotwicki et al., 2011).  
Based on these reasons, the IBTS data are considered to be the only independent dataset 
appropriate to be used as a comparison tool for the FDF data. When comparing the FDF and 
IBTS distribution maps, we observe that the distribution of the two maps created from logbook 
data – Figure 4 (distribution of the entire period), figure 5 (display of the entire season’s 
distribution with the small and large sizes being depicted separately) – is being allocated in the 
same areas as the IBTS distribution, although in the northern part of the North Sea the frequency 
of the activity is higher, a fact easily observed in every figure as the fishing activity covers 
entirely the areas of activity. Whereas in contrast, we can see that in IBTS (figure 8) the vessel 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
operations are distributed in a less condense way than fisheries. However, that is not surprising, 
since the sampling number is limited in the survey when compared to fishery (ICES 2015).  
 
The pattern of overlapping distributions of both datasets is also apparent in the distribution of 
large sizes for the years 2012 and 2014. However, even though the distribution of larger cod 
more or less covers the same areas for the IBTS and the FDF data, there are some differences 
between the two datasets with regards to the distribution of smaller cod. Figures 9 and 10 are 
based on the survey data and depict a scarce distribution of small size cod throughout the North 
Sea and especially in the northern areas. In contrast, the fishery data shows a fairly bigger 
allocation in the northern parts of the North Sea. These variations cannot be attributed to the fact 
that the catches of the IBTS survey are mainly limited to the winter quarter of the survey 
(February-March) as in Engelhard et al., 2014, due to the fact that the FDF dataset was also 
limited to the same time period. Possible reasons to these variations may be a) the fact that the 
duration of each sampling procedure is limited to 30 minutes in daylight (in contrast to the 
fishing activity which is not strictly limited by time or daylight) (ICES, 2012) or b) the fact that 
the survey samplings are conducted only for a few days in every sampling period (ICES, 2013), 
contrary to fishing activity.  In addition to these reasons, the low stock biomass for the ages 1 & 
2 during the years 2012 and 2014 (ICES, 2015) make the encountering of small size cod by IBTS 
less likely.  
 
Furthermore, changes in the distribution of catches’ biomass can be seen when the years 2012 
and 2014 from the FDF data are compared with each other.  The fishing activity for the year 
2014 was restricted to the northern parts of the North Sea, in contrast to 2012, when the 
fishermen operated in a small area in the center of the North Sea.  In addition, as we can see 
clearly in figure 11, small sized fish was caught in less fishing spots in the area south - west of 
Norway for the year 2014. However even though the encounter sites were less than 2012, the 
amount of bigger individuals in the group ages (age 1 and 2) that consists the category (figure 2) 
of small fish was more common. The reason for this change might be attributed to the shifts in 
the stock biomass for group ages 1 & 2, with the small sizes less abundant during the year 2012 
(second lowest abundance since 1970) compared to 2014 (ICES, 2015).  
 
In addition, as it is illustrated in figures 6 - 11, there are clear differences in the proportion of the 
small sized cod distribution fished in the two datasets for the entirety of the period and the period 
2012 and 2014. The first difference is the location of the hauls containing an amount of small 
sized fish. Whereas the proportions related to FDF data are mainly located in the northern areas 
of North Sea, the small sizes in the IBTS data are found mainly in the southern areas of North 
Sea. Although one possible explanation might be attributed in the spawning stock biomass shift 
in the North Sea (Engelhard et al., 2014), another justifiable reason for the difference in the two 
datasets might be the fact that the Danish fishing fleet operated throughout the period mainly in 
the Northern part and rarely in the southern, while the survey fleets had difficulties in conducting 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
efficient survey during that time period and especially during these two years, mainly due to bad 
weather, which prohibit them from surveying the northern areas (ICES, 2015). 
6.3. STATISTICAL ANALYSIS 
 
The comparison with the IBTS survey showed an extremely small deviation, with an 
insignificant difference. At the same time, the influence of the type and the influence of the IBTS 
survey on the fish size type according to the GAM model, were significant, their estimations 
weren’t very different than the intercept. In general the results produced by the model didn’t 
provide any further insight; they just portrayed an already known fact, that the amount of small 
sized cod caught during fishing activity is higher than the amount caught during a survey. 
 
The second GAM model that was created, aimed into comparing the haul by haul proportional 
distribution of small and large size into the two datasets. It was a parametric model with the two 
sizes (large and small) expressed as a function of the survey and the smoothed position 
(coordinates), the smoothed year and the smoothed month. The regression splines (smoothers) 
used in the model belonged to the thin plate regression category. The reasons for using this 
category of splines are that it prevents the knot placement problem that is very common when 
conventional regression splines are used and it is very useful splines in comparing model based 
only on the regression splines. In addition, the use of thin plate regression splines is convenient 
when it is used in a zero inflated data, an important issue since it might cause traceability issues 
in the model (Crawley, 2007). According to the GAM model, the proportion of small fish 
retained by the fishermen were statistically significant more than those caught in the survey, 
although the deviance explained in the model was extremely low (9,34%) mainly due to the high 
amount of zeros in the data - sets. 
 
The third model was almost the same with the second one, with only one difference, instead of 
comparing haul by haul proportions, the various hauls were merged in their respective trips in 
order for the proportions to be compared the as trip by trip. It was set up in this way in order to 
compare the results with those of the second model, in order to compare when we get better 
information regarding the proportion of sizes caught by fishing activity. The result provided by 
the model were slightly different than those from the haul by haul model (higher estimations), 
however the differences were insignificant. These results lead us into two assumptions. The first 
one is that there is no significant difference in estimating the size proportions either haul by haul 
or trip by trip, which although it cannot be ruled out it seems unlikely. The second assumption is 
that the construction of the model was not the appropriate one in order to investigate the 
question. 
 
During the setting up of the model, many issues emerged. The provided datasets, especially the 
FDF dataset, were inflated with zeros, making the model setting and its projections’ 
==PAGE:36==
36 
 
interpretations challenging. In addition, there have been a lot of issues with the way the datasets 
were set up, especially the FDF data. The different formats in the two datasets made it even more 
challenging, since in order for an appropriate comparison between the two, they had to be in a 
similar format. Apart from the obvious difference of format in the two datasets, a frustrating 
issue was the absence from one or the other of elements necessary for the data handling, such as 
the amount of discards in the FDF data was located in a different file and it had to be retrieved 
from that, or the absence of a weight category from the IBTS data which had to be created from 
scratch. Although the various issues were solved, the amount of time spent in order to solve it 
was considerable and it could have been spent elsewhere. An additional problem was that the 
FDF data contained a lot of wrong information, such as wrong coordinates (resulting in fishing 
points positions on land), trip identifiers - which ought to be unique for each trip – being the 
same for two or more fishing trips, an extremely large amount of zeros and the missing of the 
discards column from the dataset (it was available originally in a different dataset).

7. SUMMARY 
 
In overall, every question was answered, with only two however providing a clear answer. 
Through the plotting of the distribution and especially the proportion maps it was proven that 
small and large cod coexist in various proportions throughout the northern part of the North Sea, 
where the Danish fleets are operating. Furthermore, it was shown that while in most of the 
northern part fishermen caught small and large size cod in various proportions in each haul, the 
area between Norway and Fair Isle (N. & S. Utsire, Viking and Fair Isle) is the geographical 
region where the fishermen encounter exclusively small sized cod.  
 
In addition it was shown that there is a correlation between FDF patterns and IBTS observations 
when projected on the same areas, as the information provided by the two datasets illustrates. 
Moreover, it showed that the FDF data can act as supplementary dataset to scientific surveys, 
especially important in cases where the surveys are unable to be conducted due to various 
reasons, as was the case in some years during this time period. 
 
Regarding the third question, no definite answer can be provided. Based on these results, there is 
difference in the two methods with a small fraction of information being lost when the 
information is being collected trip by trip. However, although there are differences in the results 
provided by the haul by haul and trip by trip, the differences were very small and thus no clear 
answer can be given.

8. CONCLUSION 
 
Accurate estimations of spatial distribution of the various sizes of cod are extremely important in 
the formulation of effective management plans and reliable stock assessments. In this thesis we 
conducted an investigation regarding of the additional scientific value of the FDF data. During 
this we managed to see that there is an actual value in the data provided by the fishermen as in 
many cases it can be used either as an enhancing or a supplementary tool to the surveys. This 
became especially apparent during the years 2012 and 2014, when the IBTS survey was unable 
to survey the northern areas of the North Sea due to bad weather. 
 
In addition we managed to review various aspects of the FDF data, such as the accuracy, the area 
of coverage and the quality of the data set. Although the majority of the covered areas were the 
northern areas of North Sea, spanning from Fair Isle to Skagerrak, the quality of the provided 
data was in general very good. Disregarding the fact that the data set was zero inflated, the 
quality was good enough to provide insight to the size distribution of cod in the North Sea, as 
well as the size distribution in the catches and the areas where the small size cod was dominant. 
 
In the evaluation of the value of the high resolution fisheries data, the results provided by the 
FDF data were ambiguous. The result from the GAM models used in the statistical analysis 
provided results that were hardly different from those of the trip by trip, however the FDF 
dataset was zero inflated (a fact that influenced the outcome of the model) and there are valid 
doubts whether the way the model was set up was the appropriate way in order to perform this 
kind of comparison. 
 
Lastly, regarding future studies, further evaluation regarding the added value of the high 
resolution fisheries is recommended. The results produced from this project were inconclusive 
making a further research in the subject necessary in order to acquire a better answer on the 
subject.
