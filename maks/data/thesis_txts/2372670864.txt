Abstract
The present study concerns a PVT study of a real reservoir sample, in HPHT (High Pressure
High Temperature) conditions. An introduction to this specific type of reservoirs is provided,
and relevant studies found in open literature are reported as well. All the experiments
conducted, were performed using the HPHT PVT apparatus provided by Sanchez
Technologies®. The PVT experiments executed were: Atmospheric flash test, Visual
observation of the saturation pressure (T: 25-200 °C), Constant Mass Expansion (CME) for
the temperatures 85 °C, 100 °C, 150 °C and 159 °C, Differential Liberation test (DL) in 7 steps
at the reservoir temperature. A compact description of the aforementioned techniques is
available, introducing the concept of the methods applied. The presented results involve the
gas oil ratio (GOR), the Formation Volume Factors (FVF), the API gravity, as well as the
calculation of the initial sample’s composition. The phase envelope of the mixture has been
determined for the temperature range T: 25-200 °C. Moreover, the results are presented
along with reference values provided from an external laboratory. The comparison with the
reference values showed a general good agreement, considering the laboratory
technicalities, as well as differences in the detail of the analysis of the two laboratories. A
discussion is also included along with the graphs presented in the results’ section, targeting
to give the reader an insight concerning the real laboratory conditions. This discussion is
crucial for the correctness of the interpretation.
Keywords: PVT study, HPHT, real reservoir fluid.

1. INTRODUCTION
It has been predicted that by 2030, the global energy demand will increase by 50-60 % [1].
With hydrocarbons still being the main global energy supplier, the conventional reservoirs’
stocks are getting increasingly diminished. Consequently, investigation focuses lately in
deeper formations, the so called HPHT (High Pressure High Temperature) reservoirs [2]. In
order to comprehend the extremeness of such environments it should be noticed that
pressures are higher than 690 bar, whereas temperatures exceed 150 ˚C [3]. Challenging
environments as such, have inevitably forced the whole oil and gas industry to expand in
terms of expertise. The risk, as well as the cost of drilling and completion are significantly
enhanced [4], logging tools currently used had to be reformed to withstand the HPHT
conditions [5], and of course technical challenges in a laboratory level could not escape this
remodeling demand.
Core laboratories have raised the bar in order to meet the HPHT challenges [6], and thus
continue providing the industry with all the crucial experimental data. The thermodynamic
properties as well as the PVT behavior of the hydrocarbon mixtures is the key information
needed for the development of a reservoir. The measured data can be used from the
petroleum engineers in order to generate or tune their models. The models are used to
predict the relevant properties, aiming to optimize the exploitation planning along all steps,
from midstream to downstream, and from primary to enhanced oil recovery.
Using sophisticated experimental setups like HPHT PVT cells, the representative samples of
the reservoir fluids can be exposed to the exact HPHT reservoir conditions. In that fashion,
these laboratory assemblies meet the requirements to perform the standardized PVT
experiments, both with safety and with high measuring accuracy. Hence, the classic
experiments such as the visual observation of the saturation pressure, the CME (Constant
Mass Expansion), the DL (Differential Liberation), the separator test or the single stage flash
test can be successfully performed. Even though thermodynamic models could predict the
properties of engineers’ interest, the uniqueness of a reservoir fluid necessitates
experimental data. Regardless of their significance though, measurements of physical
properties as well as phase equilibrium data of real reservoir fluids are scarce in the open
literature. Purpose of the present study is to report the PVT results of a real reservoir fluid,
and provide the reader with the conceptuality of the applied experimental techniques. The
special interest of the project is to perform the PVT study of a real reservoir sample in HPHT
conditions, using a HPHT PVT experimental assembly. The assembly has been successfully
used in the past, for relevant studies on synthetic hydrocarbon mixtures.

2. LITERATURE REVIEW
The present literature review concerns published PVT and density data of reservoir fluids
and synthetic mixtures, found in the open literature. As it was ascertained during this
investigation the available HPHT PVT data of naturally occurring reservoir fluids or even
synthetic multicomponent hydrocarbon mixtures is really scarce, mainly due to the high cost
of such laboratory workshops. Nevertheless accurate and precise data are crucial for the
improvement of models concerning the development of HPHT reservoirs. As a result,
numerous computer PVT packages based on EoS have been developed and used to
overcome the lack of real laboratory data. Regardless of the rareness of the aforementioned
laboratory data, there are some published works presented in the following paragraphs.
The works presented in this part concern PVT studies of reservoir fluids or synthetic
mixtures. The experimental part of these works has been conducted in PVT assemblies
which are either the same or differ slightly from project to project. For example, in the
project of Pan et al. (1995), the measurements were conducted in a high pressure mercury
free PVT system, and concern the bubble and dew point near the critical region, as well as
the compressed fluid densities of the ternary system n-butane, carbon dioxide and methane.
The obtained data were also compared to model predictions [7]. Two years later, the
experimental study of a naturally occurring reservoir fluid is presented by Yang et al. (1997).
The laboratory equipment of this study was a high pressure PVT assembly (ROP France)
accompanied with a monitor camera recorder, and an on line fluid densimeter (Anton Paar).
The provided data include bubble and dew point near the critical region, the critical point of
the mixture, the compressed fluid’s compressibility factor, the fraction of the liquid volume
in the two-phase region, as well as the liquid vapor phase density [8].
A project concerning five synthetic reservoir fluid mixtures with a ranging
content is that
of Yan et al. (2001). Using the ROP (France) high pressure PVT system, the phase envelope,
the compressibility factor (single phase region) and the liquid volume fraction in the two
phase region were obtained for the temperature range from (303 to 433) K. Moreover the
critical point of three out of the five synthetic mixtures is also reported [9]. In the work of
Stenby et al. (1993) the investigation of a reservoir fluid is presented. The study provides
PVT, viscosity and density measurements obtained by the mercury free high pressure
apparatus designed again by the company ROP (France) with a maximum working pressure
of 700 bar and temperature range (-253.15 to 523.15) K. Sapphire parts of the apparatus
cells allow the visual observation of the phases in equilibrium, whereas the determination of
the sample’s volume was conducted with mechanically driven pistons [10].
In the work of Khan and Hatamian (1991) the experimental results of constant composition
expansion (CCE) and constant volume depletion (CVD) for a synthetic fluid of gas condensate
are reported. The used fluids were a binary methane butane mixture and a ternary mixture

reported [17].
Although density measurements were also provided along with PVT data in some of the
aforementioned projects, the following works consider only density measurements of
reservoir fluids and synthetic hydrocarbon mixtures.
The work of Al-Meshari et al. (2007) provides density, as well as viscosity data, for Saudi
Arabian near critical fluid, volatile oil, and gas condensate in the reservoir conditions. The
density measurements were conducted using an Anton-Paar HPHT densimeter (DMA 512 P).

are also provided for binary, ternary and synthetic hydrocarbon mixtures in the work of Le

3. PVT EXPERIMENTS
The present section aims to introduce to the reader the general description of the PVT
experiments conducted in the present study. More technical details are provided in the
Methods’ section.
PVT experiments of separator test, CME (constant mass expansion) and DL (differential
liberation) are presented in the following paragraphs. These tests constitute classic
standardized experiments that are conducted for real reservoir samples. Purpose of these
experiments is to provide all the crucial fluid properties needed for the development of the
reservoir. Regardless of all the available theoretical models, PVT experiments are essential
due to the uniqueness of every reservoir fluid. The procedure of the experiments includes
representative fluid samples exposed in the P,T conditions of our interest, using a PVT
apparatus.  After the completion of these tests, the obtained fluid properties are available
for the reservoir engineers to construct the fluid models and implement them to the
reservoir simulators.
3.1
Constant Mass Expansion
Purpose of the CME test is the determination of the sample’s bubble point, namely, the
pressure at which the fluid enters the two phase region, for a specific temperature. This is
the pressure at which the first bubble occurs. CME test is also called CCE (constant
composition expansion), due to the fact that no discharging of the sample takes place during
the experimental procedure. The figure below demonstrates the steps of a CME test:
Figure 1: CME test representation.
The high pressure single phase sample is subjected in a high pressure cell, which is bathed in
a constant predetermined temperature. Step by step the cell pressure is reduced by
expanding the sample’s volume using a piston. After every stage of decreasing pressure, a
satisfactory time is given to the system in order for the equilibrium to be ensured. In every
step, the cell’s pressure and volume are recorded and then plotted, in order to provide the
==PAGE:16==
16
information about the bubble point. Considering the different compressibility of the oil and
gas phase, the aforementioned plot (pressure versus volume) will show a breakpoint (in
terms of trend) at the sample’s saturation pressure. Moreover, a visual determination can
be also provided depending on the PVT apparatus accessories (sapphire window, camera
recording [7]) [27].
It should be mentioned at this point, that the experiment of CME cannot exactly simulate
the case of production process in a reservoir. This is because in real reservoir conditions,
with P
< P , (P
= the reservoir pressure) the more mobile gas phase will escape from
the reservoir in a different ratio from the oil phase. This is not taken into consideration
throughout a CME experiment.
3.2
Differential Liberation
The fluid parameters that can be determined from a DL test are: R , B and B .
These are the formation volume factors (FVF). The next figure illustrates the DL
experimental procedure:
Figure 2: DL test representation.
The high pressure single phase sample is again (like in CME test) loaded in the PVT high
pressure cell, and maintained in constant temperature. The steps of DL test include
expansion of the cell’s content by decreasing pressure. The equilibrium is reached in every
stage after a small period of time. When pressures smaller than the saturation pressure are
reached, all the liberated gas phase is discharged. This results to a different composition of
the remained sample. The remaining liquid phase is again expanded, and the liberated gas
phase is also removed from the cell. The procedure is repeated until atmospheric pressure is
reached. The volumes of oil and gas phase are recorded in every pressure step. Moreover
==PAGE:17==
17
with the gas composition of every stage, the remaining oil’s density can be back calculated
using the mass balance [27].
In DL test an extreme assumption is made. This is the hypothesis that during the production
phase of a reservoir, for P
< P , only the gas phase is leaving the reservoir.
3.3
Separator Test
Separator test is a simulation of the separation process, in which the reservoir fluid is
exposed when it reaches the surface. Purpose of this test is the maximization of
liquid oil production at the surface conditions. This goal is achieved when the
optimum P,T combinations are determined. The steps of a separator test (in terms of
P,T conditions) can be two or more depending on the experimental design. The last
stage is always conducted in atmospheric conditions, aiming to simulate the stock
tank. Throughout the test the GOR (Gas Oil Ratio) can be determined. Separator test
data may be also used for the optimization of separators’ design [27].

Methods
In this section the HPHT PVT apparatus used for the present study is described. Routine
processes are explained (cleaning, pressure calibration), and all the experimental procedures
conducted (PVT tests) are also described. As concerns the PVT tests, the reader could always

4.2.1 PVT Apparatus Description
All the PVT experiments presented in this work were conducted using a HPHT PVT 240/1500
provided by Sanchez Technologies ®. The main part of the apparatus is the HPHT stainless
steel PVT cell, equipped with a moving piston. A transparent sapphire window on one side of
the cell permits the visual contact with the inner part of the cell. The cell is positioned in a
thermal isolation jacket so that it can operate in different temperatures. For safety reasons,
the assembly is positioned inside a cabinet. A control software named Falcon, provides the
administrator with automated control of the apparatus. In the following figure an illustration
of the HPHT PVT is provided.
==PAGE:19==
19
Figure 3: HPHT PVT Apparatus provided by Sanchez ®[26]
PVT cell
The cell of the apparatus is a stainless steel cylinder, with a total volume of 240 cm . One
end of the cylinder is immobile and consists of a transparent sapphire window. On the other
end of the cell, a stainless steel piston is packed, making that part of the cell mobile. Thus,
volume and pressure in the cell can be regulated by the piston’s position. The piston is also
equipped with a magnetic stirrer. The stirrer’s performance can be adjusted in several
speeds providing the cell’s content with homogeneity. The operating limits of the cell are up
to 1500 bar and up to 200 °C. Sapphire’s presence though, presupposes some more safety
limitations as concerns the temperature and pressure ramps. More specifically the
maximum pressure ramp allowable is 1 bar/s, whereas the temperature’s one, is 5 °C/min.
The cell is cased in a heating jacket in order to maintain a constant temperature provided by
the thermostat. Two pneumatic valves (V3 and V4) are diametrically opposed to the cell’s
periphery, and close to the cell window, allowing the charging or discharging of each fluid
phase. These two valves can be operated either automatically (using Falcon) or manually.
Each of the pneumatic valves branches to 4 more valves that are only manually controlled,
and provide the operator with several input and output options to and from the cell. The cell
can also be rotated to a specified angle by using the oscillation procedure from Falcon.
These different inclinations provide the right position for several different operations,
depending both on the planned experimental procedure, and the type of the contained
fluid. A camera is positioned in front of the sapphire window, following the cell’s movement
in case it rotates. Thus, an en face view of the cell is any time available to the operator. The
PVT cell is also equipped with lighting, so that visibility is enhanced. The lighting sources are
two bulbs which transmit the lighting through four optical cables positioned in the four
corners of the cell.
==PAGE:20==

Figure 4: Falcon operator software illustration
Cell Pressure
The pressure inside the cell is controlled by the motion of the motorized piston, as long as
the valves are closed and no leaking is taking place from any of the potential leaking paths
(moving parts, failed o rings from the packing, or not tightly closed valves). There are two
different ways for the operator to achieve a set-point pressure in the cell: constant pressure
or constant flow rate. Both options are provided by the Falcon software. The constant
pressure runs the piston with a set pressure ramp (1 bar/s), until the set-point pressure is
reached. The constant flow rate forces the piston to move with respect to ml/min, and
depending on the operator’s goal (pressure increase or decrease) the options ‘fill’ or ‘inject’
should be also selected. A Dynisco PT35A pressure transducer measures the cell’s pressure
with an accuracy of +-0.6 bar [26]. The pressure value is continuously displayed by Falcon
software, which can also provide a pressure versus time diagram to the operator. The
software also applies a correction of the measured pressure, with respect to different
temperatures. Before conducting any experiment, a pressure calibration against another
reference pressure transducer is performed.
Cell Volume
The volume of the cell is controlled by the piston’s position. The cell can reach the maximum
volume of 240 cm , whereas the minimum volume of 8 cm is reached, when the piston is
at the lower end of stroke. Regardless of the number of phases in the cell, or any leakage’s
presence, the volume value displayed by Falcon concerns only the piston’s position. The
volume is measured according to the number of the piston’s motor revolutions. When the
cell’s content is in the two phase region, the volumes of each phase can be determined with
the usage of Euclid software as long as the camera and lighting are on, and so the interphase
can be set by the operator. Thus, the volumes of the different phases can be easily
computed at any time, since the cell volume and the cell’s inclination are known. Using
cylinder’s geometry, stereometry can be easily applied from the Euclid’s software in order
for each phase’s volume to be calculated.
==PAGE:21==

Cell Temperature
The temperature in the cell is regulated by an electrical heating system, which is employed
inside a heating jacket. In addition to that system, a circulating bath controlled by a separate
thermostat, is also employed in order to support a constant temperature surrounding for
the cell. The bath usually maintains an environment 5 degrees lower than the set-point
temperature, also protecting the main electrical heating system from overworking. The
operator can achieve a set-point temperature using the temperature regulation option by
Falcon software. The cell temperature is constantly displayed, and is measured by a
resistance thermometer which is located on the cell’s wall. The provided accuracy of the
thermometer is +-0.02 °C [26]. A temperature versus time graph is also available by Falcon,
allowing the administrator to check how the heating or cooling proceeds. The maximum
temperature ramp allowed is 5 °C/min, ensuring safety for the sapphire window.
4.2.2 Cleaning
The cleaning procedure is conducted before any new fluid is inserted to the PVT cell.
Purpose of the process is to exclude the presence of traces from previous fluids. Thus, the
purity of the new fluid is ensured. The cleaning technique applied is repeated twice, using
toluene and ethanol. Toluene is used first, as it allows the dissolution of all hydrocarbons,
whereas ethanol is the final cleaning substance used. The key purpose of the technique is to
ensure that every part of the internal surface (cell, dead volumes, valves and stirring’s
blades) will come in contact with the cleaning substance. Using a funnel, toluene is inserted
and allowed to flow through the dead volumes, while the piston is at the lower end of
stroke. Then, the operator can close the V4 (down valve) and move the piston backwards
expanding the cell’s volume to its maximum, while imbibing the flowing toluene. Using the
stirring at the same time (when the volume of the cell is greater than 30 ml), both the cell
and the blades come in contact with the toluene. The cell is left drenched with the toluene
for 24 hours. The same procedure is again repeated with ethanol. After emptying the cell
from ethanol, the piston is moved back again, and all the internal volume (cell and dead
volumes) is exposed to vacuum for 24 hours, ensuring ethanol’s complete vaporization.

dead volume was filled with ethanol (valve V3 is open see fig 4). In one of the four outlets of
this dead volume a SIKA gauge was connected, with the corresponding valve being open as
well. Thus, the pressure applied in the cell, was actually applied in the dead volume as well.

4.2.4
Transfer of the sample
The sample of fluid A has been conditioning in a stainless steel cylinder for 24 hours at 93 °C
and at P=485 bar. The aforementioned P,T conditions ensured the single phase of the
sample. Rocking of the cylinder had also been performed in order to provide the sample
with homogeneity. In order to transfer the sample into the PVT cell an ISCO pump was used.
This is because the single phase state of the sample should be guaranteed during the
transfer process. The ISCO pump is actually acting as a high pressure syringe pump. The
pressure in the pump is generated by compressed water, and the maximum pressure it can
operate is up to 515 bar. In order to perform the transfer from the sample cell to the HPHT
PVT cell the following configuration was set: the outlet of the sample cell was connected to
one of the manually controlled inlets of the PVT cell (while all the PVT valves were still
closed), whereas the inlet of the sample cell was connected with the ISCO pump. Of course
the PVT cell as well as the dead volumes branching to the outer valves were all vacuumed.
By opening the inlet of the cell to the ISCO pump a constant pressure of 485 bar was applied
to the sample. Afterwards, the outlet of the sample’s cell was opened to the dead volume
until V3 (see figure 4). Then V3 was slowly opened in order for the sample to fill the dead
volume of the PVT cell. Moving the PVT’s piston backwards with a constant flow rate of 200
cm /h the temperature was at the same time set at 85 °C. When the cell volume had
reached the 65 cm the stirring was on and sufficient time was provided to the system in
order for the temperature to stabilize. After the temperature was finally stabilized, the fluid
from the PVT cell was pumped back again to the sample cylinder, while the ISCO pump was
running in constant pressure at P=485 bar. This final process was repeated 3 times, ensuring
the homogeneous remixing of the fluid A. When the final amount of fluid A was inserted to
the PVT cell, valves V3 and V4 were closed, and the PVT cell was left for 1 day at 85 °C and
481 bar.
4.2.5 Atmospheric flash test
The atmospheric flash test performed for the fluid A, was conducted in one stage. The
sample collected was exposed to a single stage flash from the initial P,T conditions of 500
bar and 85 °C respectively, to the ambient conditions (P=1,013 bar and T=24,5 °C). In order
to collect the sample for the separator test, a cylinder of 75 ml capacity was used. The
cylinder was first evacuated and its mass was measured. The evacuated cylinder was then
connected to the bottom valve of the HPHT PVT cell and vacuum was again applied for the
connection as well as for the dead volume of the cell. The bottom valve was then opened to
the dead volume, whereas the cylinder’s valve was still closed. The opening of the cylinder’s
valve had to be performed very carefully for two reasons. Primarily a possibility of a great
pressure drop would be precarious for the sample’s single phase condition, and thus would
lead to the samples loss. Furthermore, the goal at this stage was the collection of around 10
ml of the single phase fluid A. The cell’s conditions were recorded both before and after the
transfer. During the transfer the cell’s piston was running in constant pressure, while the
fluid A was allowed to slowly flow from the PVT cell to the slightly opened evacuated
cylinder. After the successful transfer of the desired amount the cylinder’s mass was
measured again. Afterwards, the cylinder was connected to the gasometer, which had been
evacuated for 24 hours. After opening the cylinder’s valve, all the contained gas was allowed
==PAGE:23==
23
to be released in the gasometer. Finally, the gasometer was set to run in constant pressure
at P=1.013 bar, and the system (gasometer and the connected cylinder) was left to stabilize
for 48 hours. The P,V,T conditions of the gasometer were recorded. Both the released gas
and the dead oil were collected and sent for chromatographic analysis.
4.2.6 Visual determination of saturation pressure
The saturation pressure measurement was repeated for 10 different temperatures. The
procedure applied was the same for every temperature. The procedure involved pressure
decreasing in constant temperature, until the first bubble appeared. At the beginning a rapid
depletion performance was conducted in order for the operator to get a first glance of the
pressure under which the sample was entering the two phase region. Then, a set-point
pressure 3 bar above the pressure noticed was selected, and the pressure decrease was
repeated again with a constant flow rate of 1 cm /h. With this smaller rate of pressure
decrease the accuracy would be better. During the aforementioned procedures the stirring
was on (30 %). The measurement was repeated 3 times for every different temperature.
4.2.7 CME
The CME procedure was performed by Falcon software, meaning that once the parameters
were set by the operator the experiment could run independently, and all the acquired data
were stored automatically. Concerning the present project the CME test was performed for
the fluid A. The concept of the technique has already been described in the PVT experiments
section. So in this section the parameters selected by the operator are provided. Before
demonstrating the parameters it should be noticed that the software performs the
experiment in 3 different stages. The first one concerns the expansion in the single phase
region, the second one focuses on the detection of the saturation pressure, whereas the
third stage concerns the expansion pattern applied when the sample is diphasic. So during
the first stage a set-point pressure (usually around 510 bar in the present case) was selected
and then with a flow rate of Q= 20 (cm /h), and a ΔP of 20 bar, an ending pressure few bars
above the saturation pressure (determined by the visual observation) was approached,
leading to the end of the 1st stage. A stirring time of 300 s was provided to the system for
every step, in order for the equilibrium to be easier approached. Moreover, 300 s of waiting
time were again provided to the system, after every step. Afterwards, during the 2nd stage,
the saturation pressure is detected. In order for the software to move towards this direction,
a specific pressure range is set by the operator. Applying a step by step pressure decrease of
0,5 bar with a Q= 10 (cm /h), Falcon determines the saturation pressure according to a
specific criterion. This criterion is a change in the slope of the P-V plot, which will be larger
than 30%. Finally in the 3rd and last step of CME, the examined fluid is already in the two
phase region. The depletion pattern applied in this stage is described by the following table:

4.2.8 DL (Differential Liberation)
The differential liberation experiment for the fluid A was conducted at the reservoir
temperature (T=159 °C), in 7 steps with the first step being at P=207.8 bar and the final one
at P=1.201 bar. The set-point pressures of every step were selected according to the ones
used from the external laboratory, and so the results could be compared with the reference
source. In order to perform the experiment the HPHT PVT cell, containing the fluid A, was
set to stabilize at the reservoir temperature (T=159 °C) for 24 hours, with the piston running
at constant pressure P=310 bar. For every step of the differential liberation the same routine
was followed. Before starting any performance in the PVT cell, an evacuated cylinder of 75
ml capacity was connected to the dead volume of the cell, in order for the released gas of
every step to be collected. Afterwards vacuum was again applied for the dead volume as
well as for the connections. Using the Falcon software, the pressure ramp of 0.1 bar/s was
selected. Thus the set-point pressure of each step would be approached with a slow rate. In
that way enough time would be provided to the gas components of the fluid A to smoothly
degas in the emerging gas phase, as soon as the fluid A was entering its two phase region. All
this pressure decrease was conducted without the stirring. Once the set-point pressure was
reached, the piston was set to run in constant pressure, and the stirring was initiated in the
PVT cell for 15 min. Hence the homogeneity of the system provided a faster approach to the
equilibrium state. Since the equilibrium was achieved the stirring was stopped and the P,V,T
conditions in the cell were recorded. Moreover using the Euclide software, the volume of
each phase was also measured. Afterwards the gas phase was removed. In order to remove
the gas phase from the PVT cell to the cylinder, the piston was set running in constant
pressure and V3 was first opened to the dead volume. At that point the cylinder’s valve was
still close. It should be mentioned at this point that the opening of the cylinder’s valve
should be executed really carefully, since a great pressure drop would disturb both the
equilibrium as well as the set-point pressure. Finally the removed gas was collected in the
==PAGE:25==
25
cylinder whose weight was again measured. Thus the mass of the gas in every step could be
calculated, and be used for further calculations as it can be demonstrated in the results’
section. It should be mentioned at this point that as concerns the final step of the DL, a
different procedure was followed for the gas transfer. The total amount of sample left at the
7th step of the DL was more than the needed (or the PVT cell total volume was smaller than
the needed), in order for the operator to successfully target for the last set-point pressure.
That means that even when the cell was in full expansion, the cell pressure was larger than
the atmospheric. Hence, for the transfer of the final gas, the PVT cell was connected to the
gasometer, and the final discharging of the gas took place there. So the volume of the final
gas at the atmospheric pressure was measured from the gasometer.

lead to a larger deviation per component. Last but not least the technicality already

6. CONCLUSIONS
In this final part, all the interpretation of the present project is summarized, aiming to
describe briefly the key concepts of the results, with respect to ‘real life’ laboratory
conditions.
In a general manner, it is important to be mentioned that the details concerning the
methods applied by the external laboratory, were not available in full extend. This forces the
present study’s comparison in a restricted framework in advance. The most crucial point of
the methods’ differences would start with the conditioning of the initial sample, then
continue with the transferring of the sample, and would finally end up with all these
operating details that could affect the measuring (stirring’s duration, waiting times for
temperature stabilization or for equilibrium restoration etc.). In addition to that, the detail in
the chromatographic analysis was not the same for the two laboratories in the case of the
‘dead’ oil. The external laboratory provided also the molar fractions of the C1-C4 group,
whereas the analysis used for the present work started from the C5. So technically, all these
components (C1-C4) were lost in the analysis for the case of the present study.
The HPHT PVT apparatus (Sanchez Technologies®) has been previously tested in well-defined
mixtures, and its performance has been validated successfully. Moreover, during the
execution of the experiments no leaking was observed, in the P,T range of the study’s
interest. Thus, the comparison of the experimental results with the reference values, can be
fairly conducted.
When looking at the results of the visual determination of the saturation pressure, it could
be said that a generally good agreement stands, with respect to the reported results
provided by the external laboratory. Deviations concerning the real pressures reported can
always occur because of the different pressure calibration procedures applied. The
deviations could be compared for only two temperatures. Even for those two points though,
the comparison seems satisfactory, meaning that the saturation pressure values reported
were really close (see phase envelop of fluid A).
In the case of the atmospheric flash test, the back calculation of the sample’s composition
seems to be in a significantly good agreement for the majority of the components. The
reported GOR values though, showed a deviation of 6,8 %. More specifically the GOR
reported from the external laboratory was larger than the experimental. In this case, with
the final compositions being in good agreement, laboratory technicalities might justify the
deviation. For example for how long was the sample exposed to the ambient conditions,
before the relevant measurements were recorded. Moreover the fashion in which GOR was
calculated might not be the same (molecular weight of the released gas was included in the
GOR calculation of the present project for example).
The CME experiment along with the composition of the initial sample ensure that fluid A is a
volatile oil. The P-V curves obtained from CME correspond to a volatile oil’s behavior with
the characteristic smooth transition of the slope, for pressures below the saturation
==PAGE:44==
44
pressure. The back calculated composition also indicates that the fluid A is a volatile oil (see
methane’s fraction).
Considering the DL results, it has already be mentioned at the results’ section that the
imperfect execution from the first step, lead to all these deviations in the FVFs as well as in
the GOR in every pressure. Nevertheless all the volumetric properties follow ‘normal’ trends
with respect to the expected. More specifically, GOR and B decrease with decreasing
pressure, whereas B decreases with increasing pressure.
As concerns the back calculation of the initial samples with respect to the different
experiments (atmospheric flash and DL), the compositions differ. This should not happen
considering that the final computation of the composition is based on the mass balance.
Regardless of that though, this comparison illustrates the imperfect execution of DL.
Finally, when looking at the calculated API gravities, two points have to be highlighted. First
point concerns the API comparison with respect to the reference values. The deviations for
both tests (flash and DL) are reasonable when considering the density of the ‘dead’ oils
which differed as well. The second point concerns a comparison with respect to the different
tests. In both laboratories the API calculated from the flash test’s results is different from
that calculated from the DL tests. This is something expected and illustrates the procedure
dependence of the results.
