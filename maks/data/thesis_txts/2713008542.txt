Abstract
Underwater vehicles are broadly used in execution of many subsea operations. To get all
beneﬁts from the under water vehicles, it is vital to be able to accurately control the po-
sition and orientation of the vehicle and also make a reliable motion control of them. In
order to enable the vehicle to operate properly the source of energy should be easily ac-
cessible while robot searching around the subsea. An under water docking station can be
implemented where robot can automatically connect and recharge its battery as well as
transferring data. To reach the docking station, navigation to the station is needed but in
an underwater environment, GPS signals will be shielded, and the vehicle cannot navigate
to docking station through GPS. Therefore using the fusion of multiple sensors (cameras,
pressure sensors, etc.) can help robot to estimate the location of the docking station so a
docking action can be achieved by accurate control algorithms.
In this Project, a motion control system (path planning, maneuvering and estimation)
for a remotely operated vehicle (ROV) is going to realized and the goal is the robot au-
tonomously drive towards a docking station through a predeﬁned path.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
This chapter introduces the MSc thesis project "Motion Control of ROV for Autonomous
Docking"and provides a general overview of the project. The project background and mo-
tivation will be given, followed by the project objectives. Finally, the thesis outline is pre-
sented.
1.1
Background and Motivation
In recent years, the leaning of offshore power generation, both in oil and gas (O&G) and in
offshore renewable energy (MRE), is to shift production platforms to offshore where there
is signiﬁcant energy potential. Although higher energy potential and cost savings have
resulted in deep-water sites become feasible, the costs for operations, maintenance, and
repair are inevitably increasing with the move into deeper offshore regions.Therefor the
needs for Inspection, Maintenance and Repair(IMR), results in a high tendency in using
robotics technology to autonomously perform tasks in order to reduce dangers and cost.
To take a beneﬁt of the autonomous underwater vehicles for performing these tasks,robot
must have the ability to plan the homing path after doing its mission and can come back
to the surface station or an underwater docking station for transferring data or recharging
battery . In this regards, a motion control system is required to control the robot to follow a
path and bring it back to the station. Motion control is a fundamental enabling technology
for such a quality, and every motion control system requires a guidance component [6].
There have been conducted some projects to develop a control system which provides
the ability of autonomously follow the path toward the desired position [3]. beside that,several
works have also done about the methods of docking, e.g. vision-based autonomous dock-
ing [11]using a camera as feedback, or vision-based docking by introducing a visual percep-
tion algorithm combined with decision algorithms for image processing [14] . But in order
to reach to the docking station,ﬁrst the robot should be able to ﬁnd the way to the docking
module,in this regard the path following method can be a way to run the robot toward the
position of the docking station.
However,the highly dynamic and three-dimensional under-water environments make
autonomous navigation of underwater robots one of the interesting but challenging tasks
in the ﬁeld of study. For the majority of the applications navigation,path planning and
tracking are essential for a successful performance. In this project path following and nav-
igate to the docking station is investigated. Generally speaking in most application of un-

1.2
Objectives
This thesis aims at developing a motion control system to autonomously drive ROV towards
a docking station and perform the docking/undocking operation. Path planing, maneuver-
ing and estimation for ROV motion control is going to investigate. It is assumed that the
visual and inertial measurements are available for estimating/measuring the position of
the docking module as well as that one of the ROV. And also a-priori map of the environ-
ment where the vehicle operates is assumed to be available.
The following objectives are achieved at this project:
• Development of a guidance system that provides set points in heading, speed and
possibly altitude/water depth to the motion controller of the ROV.
• Development of a robust motion control system that enable the ROV to maneuver
in 2D/3D environment in the presence of external disturbances (i.e. constant ocean
currents) and follow a given path.
• implementation the developed algorithms in the BlueROV robotic platform, and per-
form experimental campaigns to test and verify the results obtained through simula-
tion.

Motion Control System
The interest in marine control systems has grown in great extend in recent times, and the
efforts are put on to make marine crafts perform a larger specter of tasks such as path-
following, dynamical positioning and automatic execution of small missions, e.g., seabed
mapping, without using human action. One of the important subsection of marine crafts
control system is motion control system. Motion control system is deﬁned as three inde-
pendent subsystems named guidance, navigation and control. These systems interact with
each other through data and signal transmission in order to satisfy control objectives.

• Environmental data:Information about wind, waves and ocean currents can be used

• Feasibility:Each waypoint must be feasible, in that it must be possible to maneuver

4.3
Guidance System Design
Guidance can be deﬁned as (Shneydor, 1998):"The process for guiding the path of an object
towards a given point, which in general may be moving." Breivik and Fossen (2009) state:
Guidance represents a basic methodology concerned with the transient motion behavior
associated with the achievement of motion control objectives.
There are different motion control scenarios that can be classiﬁed as:
• Set-point regulation: The desired position and attitude are considered constant.
• Trajectory tracking: The goal is to force the system output to track a desired output.
• Path following: Following a predeﬁned path independent of time.
Here in this project, The path following based on straight line path generating is consid-
ered.
4.3.1
Path Following
In the path following guidance, the control objective is to converge and follow a predeﬁned
path independent of time. In order to achieve this, several guidance laws have been stud-
ied and implemented such as the Line-of-Sight (LOS) guidance, the Pure Pursuit (PP) guid-
ance and the Constant Bearing (CB) guidance While our focus in this project is on the LOS
guidance law. Frequently, the LOS guidance is considered in connection with the heading
autopilot due to the fact that the two systems form together a cascade structure that needs
to be stabilized [10]. The LOS guidance algorithm has simple structure and is efﬁcient but
the downside is that the vehicle is not possible (in the general case) to succeed in accom-
plishing the motion control task, i.e. to converge to the desired path in the presence of an
external disturbances (the wind or sea currents, for example). A simple solution for this
can be adding a integral action in order to eliminate any constant offsets induced by the
constant, or slow time-varying, environmental forces.
4.3.2

This Project is focused on designing the LOS guidance for the movement on horizontal
plane while it has been considered that the vehicle will keep a desired depth in all mission.
Therefore, vertical and horizontal plane can be decoupled.

5.1
Analysis
The controller designed to run the robot to dive toward the desired depth and keep the
depth in all mission of following the deﬁned path. Furthermore in maneuver in horizontal
plane keeping the desired heading and speed is also aimed. Hence, ﬁrst consideration is
that the controller response time be fast enough and run the vehicle to the set point, and
this can be achieved by introducing the the proportional(P) controller.However, using only
the proportional controller has an shortcome, it does not always bring the system to the
desired set point. To overcome this problem an integral action introduce to the controller.
The Integral control(I) removes the any deviations that may exist. Thus, the system returns
to both steady state and its original setting. In order to reach our goal these two controller
looks enough as it seems that can provide the desired behaviour of the robot. Because
of having the noise command and feedback signal, introducing a Derivation(D) may adds
complexity, increase noise susceptibility and erode gain margin of the system. Therefore
the decision was that the system doesn’t need derivation term.
The designing method of the controller gains that has been shown in previous chapter
in equations (4.25, 4.26 and 4.28) require the natural frequency and damping ratio. To ﬁnd
the natural frequency and the damping ration of the system pole location was considered.

7.1
Conclusion
Using the provided 4-DOF manoeuvring and diving model with surge, sway, heave and yaw
and also with the aim of the designed lookahead-based LOS guidance, the path following
methodology was adopted for the ROV.
The simulation (Simulink) was done ﬁrst to verify and validate the designed LOS guid-
ance algorithm as well as the controller. Each of the controllers were tested in the sim-
ulation environment. Controller synthesis and simulation was carried out based on the
identiﬁed model previously have found by "Andreas Baldur Nørregård Hansen"[9]. Several
waypoint was tested to conﬁrm the functionality of the designed algorithm for LOS and all
controllers ( depth, heading, velocity).
The sensors available on-board the BlueROV is not able to measure velocity or position
in the horizontal plane. Therefore, an external measurement system was needed for en-
abling any forms of closed-loop velocity and position control. A camera tracking system
was used with 16 cameras that could track the position and orientation of the vehicle.
All designed controllers as well as LOS guidance was implemented in python and ROS
to verify the theoretical design of path following algorithm in real robot and make a experi-
mental campaigns.
In the experimental part of the project, the codes to send the required command to
robot was created. The basis for position and velocity estimation by using the camera sig-
nals was implemented. Several test was conducted and some tuning in parameters was ap-
plied to make the robot follow the desired mission. Control signals shows somehow accept-
able performance. Although the path following performance was not in its perfect level,
but validated the practicality.
7.2
Future works
The ﬁnal goal is to perform docking operation. Expanding the guidance system to reach
the last waypoint where that is located close to the docking module can be the next step

toward making the autonomous docking. The following is a list of suggestions to improve
the control system to complete the docking task.
• The last waypoint can be deﬁned in front of the docking station but with a short dis-
tance from that.
• In the last leg of the path where robot drives to the last waypoint, position controller
is required which control the robot toward the desired position P = [x, y,z], and stop
at the last waypoint.
• In the last step start moving to the docking module activate the magnets, and ﬁnish
the docking.
In the last steps the good position estimation is required. Position controller which read
the position of the robot from estimator and have the position of the docking station as a
setpoint can control the robot to the last desired station and ﬁnish the task.
