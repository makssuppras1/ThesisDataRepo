ATRACT: The objective of this study was to investigate the potential trade-off associated with in-
duced defence mechanisms in the diatom Pseudo-nitzschia seriata. My research hypothesis posited 
that the allocation of resources towards defence mechanisms may lead to reduced growth rates or 
increased nutrient requirements, as energy and nutrients are diverted from essential processes. Ad-
ditionally, this study aimed to explore the intraspecific physiological variation within Pseudo-
nitzschia seriata, contributing to our understanding of The paradox of the plankton at a species-
level. Pseudo-nitzschia seriata is known for its production of domoic acid (DA), a toxin with impli-
cations for marine mammals and humans alike. Batch culture experiments were conducted using 
three different strains of Pseudo-nitzschia seriata at various temperatures to determine their strain-
specific optimal growth rates and temperature ranges. To induce toxin production, chemical cues 
from copepods were used, and the resulting rejection frequency was recorded using high-speed cam-
era technology. The findings demonstrated strain-specific optimal growth rates, with each strain 
exhibiting superior performance at different temperature conditions. Moreover, significant effects 
of the treatment (induced cells vs. non-induced cells) on the copepod rejection probability were 
revealed.     However, the cost associated with toxin production proved difficult to document. 
This study contributes to the existing knowledge on diatom defence mechanisms and the influence of 
temperature on species interactions. By investigating the trade-off between induced defence mecha-
nisms and growth rates in Pseudo-nitzschia seriata, we enhance our understanding of the intricate 
dynamics within diatom populations. Furthermore, our findings provide insights into the paradox of 
the plankton at an intraspecific level, shedding light on the factors that shape species diversity and 
coexistence. Additionally, considering the implications of climate change, this study contributes to 
our understanding of how temperature variations may impact diatom defence strategies and their 
ecological interactions.

1 Introduction 
Unicellular organisms form the foundation 
of food webs within the oceans. 71 % of the 
surface of Earth is covered by oceans and 
roughly one-half of the global net primary 
production (NPP) occurs within them 
(Behrenfeld, 2014). Since the 1880s, ocean 
and land temperatures have increased with an 
average rate of 0.08 ◦C per decade according 
to NOAA’s 2020 Annual Climate Report 
(NOAA, 2021). Changes in the climate can 
alter currents, salinity, stratification, up- and 
downwelling, nutrient supply and the compo-
sition of plankton communities. The ongoing 
climate warming is expected to enhance ver-
tical and thermal stratification within the 
oceans and will thereby reduce mixing and, 
thus, the supply of nutrients from the deep 
ocean into the upper mixed layer. Many phy-
toplankton species - such as diatoms - heavily 
rely on inputs of nutrients to thrive and out-
compete other autotrophic plankton species. 
The predicted reduced mixing is expected to 
limit the competitive abilities of diatoms 
(Barnett et al., 2005), and thereby reduce the 
diversity within communities. Rapid fluctua-
tions in plankton abundance and community 
structure can have significant impacts on 
food webs and ecological processes via cas-
cading bottom-up and top-down effects 
(Suikkanen et al., 2013). This can lead to 
consequential changes since aquatic herbi-
vores remove on average 51% of the annual 
primary production (Cyr and Face, 1993). 
Compared to the terrestrial systems, this is a 
fraction of up to three times higher (Cyr and 
Face, 1993). Though it is worth noting that 
terrestrial and aquatic systems are very dif-
ferent from one another. 
Phytoplankton play a crucial role in the 
global carbon cycle, and the consequences of 
global warming can have significant implica-
tions for their functioning (Behrenfeld, 2014 
Hays et al., 2005). Pelagic organisms may 
migrate or adapt to cope with changing envi-
ronmental conditions. The competition for re-
sources might be harsh or the herbivory pres-
sure intense. Intense predation pressure con-
tributes to the high diversity observed in    
phytoplankton communities (Leibold et al., 
2017) as well as the genetic diversity within 
species (Sjöqvist and Kremp, 2016). The     
understanding of how factors like tempera-
ture, nutrient limitation, and predation pres-
sure influence the composition of plankton 
communities is an important and relevant 
question since genetic diversity is considered 
an important factor for the stability of ecolog-
ical functions such as productivity, nutrient        
cycling and resource use. For example, the 
study Sjöqvist and Kremp et al.,2016 exposed 
six distinct strains of the diatom Skeletonema

grown in monocultures and in a mix. They 
found the primary production within the 
mixed cultures to be significantly higher 
compared to the monocultures under salinity 
stress (Sjöqvist and Kremp, 2016). Genet-

more efficiently than genetically uniform

Given the well-documented importance 
of phytoplankton diversity in annual primary 
production (Cyr et al., 1993), system stability 
(Suikkanen et al., 2013) climate adaptations 
(Hughes et al., 2004; Huey et al., 2012), and 
their central role at the base of food webs, it 
becomes essential to study and comprehend 
the maintenance of diversity. Not only at the 
interspecific level but also at an intraspecific 
level, since intraspecific diversity is gaining 
increasing recognition as an ecological aspect 
of biodiversity (Raffard et al., 2019). There-
fore, the objective of this study was to exam-
ine intraspecific diversity in the cold-water 
diatom Pseudo-nitzschia seriata, as previous 
reports have indicated high clonal diversity 
within this species (Evans et al., 2005, Caste-
leyn et al., 2009, Casteleyn et al., 2010). 
Clonal diversity is the number of unique 
clonal lineages present within a single sample 
and the measurement of how many distinct 
clones are collected and how they are distrib-
uted (Ellstrand and Roose, 1987). This study 
focused on different strains exposed to vary-
ing temperatures and examined the trade-offs 
associated with survival, specifically investi-
gating the costs and benefits involved. 
1.1 Paradox of the Plankton and       
intraspecific variation 
The Paradox of the Plankton (Hutchinson, 
1961) is a well-known ecological phenome-
non that challenges the principle of com-
petitive exclusion in stable environments. 
This paradox refers to the coexistence of nu-
merous phytoplankton species that compete 
for the same few resources, despite theory 
predicting that only one species per limiting 
resource should survive (Hardin, 1960). This 
phenomenon has not been observed in other 
systems at the same level as in phytoplank-
ton, hence the paradox of the plankton. The 
paradox has important implications for        
understanding community dynamics and bio-
diversity, as it suggests that coexistence may 
be maintained through complex ecological 
interactions, such as niche differentiation, 
tradeoffs, neutral mutations, or variable envi-
ronmental conditions (Roger et al., 2012, 
Barrett and Schluter, 2008, Tilman 1982 ). 
For a long time, phytoplankton blooms were

believed to be composed of monoclonal pop-
ulations dominated by a few well-adapted 
genotypes. However, many studies investi-
gating genetic diversity within blooming 
phytoplankton species report high levels of 
intraspecific variation (Tesson et al., 2014, 
Godhe and Härnström, 2010, Weber et 
al.,2021, Dia et al.; 2014 ). Genetic diversity 
within species may allow for adaptation to 
changing environmental conditions and     
promote the coexistence of different geno-
types with different physiological traits 
(Sjöqvist and Kremp, 2016, Huey et al., 
2012). In terms of evolution, the level of     
genetic diversity within individuals can shape 
a species’ capacity to adapt and impact its 
long-term survival (Barrett and Schluter, 
2008). The level of intraspecific diversity can 
furthermore have an impact on primary 
productivity as well as a functional response 
to changing environments (Roger et al., 2012, 
Sjöqvist and Kremp, 2016). Intraspecific     
genetic diversity plays a role in modulating 
interspecific interactions that, in turn, influ-
ence biodiversity. Biodiversity determines 
the productivity and stability of a system and 
is thus influenced by intraspecific genetic    
diversity, ultimately affecting primary pro-
duction (Schob 2015). 
Using sensitive DNA methods such as 
fingerprinting techniques to determine the 
nucleotide sequence at a certain part of the 
DNA, recent studies of intraspecific variation 
have identified high genetic variation within 
phytoplankton, both across different loca-
tions (Ajani et al., 2021) and within the same 
location (Weber, 2021). 
In one study that used micro-satellites and 
investigated the diatom Ditylum brightwellii, 
at least 2400 genetically distinct clonal line-
ages were estimated in one bloom population 
utilizing capture-recapture statistics based on 
the frequency of re-sampled clonal lineages 
(Rynearson 2005). Another study utilizing 
microsatellites detected 296 isolates with 
unique multi-locus genotypes out of 310 iso-
lates of the diatom Pseudo-nitzschia pungens 
(Casteleyn et al., 2009). Samples were taken 
at ten locations in four different water entities 
in the Southern Bight of the North Sea (see 
left map in figure 1). They found 95 %          
genetic diversity (Casteleyn et al., 2009), 
which is comparable with the clonal diversity 
found by Evans et al., 2005 investigating      
the same species (i.e., Pseudo-nitzschia 
pungens). Here they used six microsatellite 
markers and genotyped 464 isolates of which 
453 were different resulting in a clonal diver-
sity of 98%. These samples were collected on 
42 separate occasions, over three sampling 
periods during spring (2002 and 2003) and 
autumn (2003) in the North Sea (see the right 
map in figure 1).

Sexual reproduction is not thought to oc-
cur every year. In fact, the estimated length of 
time between sexual reproduction events for 
Pseudo-nitzschia multiseries is three years 
(Davidovich and Bates, 1998). However, lit-
tle is known about the exact timing and mag-
nitude of sexual reproduction events in field 
populations of phytoplankton species. In 
many diatom species, sexual reproduction is 
essential for population survival since the cell 
size tends to decrease over time through asex-
ual reproduction (Davidovich and Bates, 
1998). As the study of Evans et al., 2005 took 
place over a limited time frame of two years, 
sexual reproduction might have been limited. 
The high genetic diversity within popula-
tions suggests that many diatom species are 
able to react quickly to environmental 
changes (i.e. climate change) through selec-
tion, and that evaluating the genetics or phys-
iology of one single individual is insufficient 
for the description of the capabilities of the 
entire species

intraspecific diversity within plankton 
species. Like the paradox of the plankton, the 
best adapted clone of a species should in     
theory out-compete other genotypes. But nu-
merous studies show otherwise, not just at

the role of geographic isolation in relation to

differentiated populations of the same species

1.2.1 Possible reasons for high genetic      
diversity 
There have been several suggestions for 
possible explanations of the coexistence of 
different genotypes within phytoplankton 
species. One of them is based on environ-
mental fluctuations, as the environment in 
aquatic habitats changes daily, and a steady 
state may be difficult to reach. Phytoplankton 
communities are seldom - if ever - in a steady 
state due to constant variation at all scales 
(e.g. nutrients, temperature, light etc) thereby 
preventing competitive excluding and a 
steady state (Huisman J. and Weissing J., 
1999)(Pančić and Kiørboe, 2018). 
Different genotypes may be the best 
adapted at different times of the day or year, 
but not long enough to out-compete others 
due to constantly changing environmental 
conditions. In other words, this variation 
within populations could correspond to or-
ganisms’ optimal fitness in several temporary 
micro-habitats during the day, season or year 
within the water column (Richerson et al., 
1970 Roger et al., 2012). The constantly 
changing environment within the oceans cre-
ates several temporary micro-habitats in the 
water column and thus contributes to a signif-
icant variation in diversity. This dynamic     
nature leads to fluctuations in the composi-
tion and abundance of marine organisms, re-
flecting their ability to adapt and respond to 
ongoing environmental changes. The study of 
Roger et al., 2012 tried to document this the-
ory by growing four strains of the marine di-
atom Skeletonema marinoi in monocultures 
and in a mixture. They found that clones pos-
sess different phenotype characteristics and 
that they may dominate under a specific set

of conditions, but the clonal dominance will 
change as the environmental conditions 
change (Roger et al., 2012), thus leading to a 
high level of genetic variability. Another 
study from Rynearson and Armbrust, 2004 
showed clones of D. brightwellii from two es-
tuaries separated by less than 40 km to be     
genetically distinct from one another and dis-
played different population-wide phenotype 
responses to high and low light intensity 
(Rynearson and Armbrust, 2004). 
These differences in phenotype character-
istics can occur via selection of new muta-
tions or the selection of pre-existing genetic 
variation. A beneficial adaptation may allow 
the species to survive at higher temperatures 
or survive at lower nutrient concentrations. 
Adaptation based on pre-existing genetic var-
iation is likely to be the faster adaptation      
towards changing environment since benefi-
cial genes are usually at a higher frequency 
(Barrett and Schluter, 2008). 
In addition, when environmental condi-
tions are favourable, the release of genotypes 
from the resting stages in the sediment could 
add to the genetic pool (Godhe and 
Härnström, 2010). 
Another theory for the coexistence of differ-
ent genotypes is neutral mutations as they 
may also contribute to the high level of intra-
specific diversity. Mutations with no effect 
on the fitness of the organism are not subject 
to natural selection and may be influenced by 
genetic drift, which can lead to the loss or fix-
ation of neutral alleles (Hedgecock, 1994). 
A third theory is based on the trade-off con-
cept. Phytoplankton species have evolved 
several key traits to cope with a given envi-
ronment such as cellular chlorophyll concen-
tration (Litchman and Klausmeier, 2008), 
cell size variation (Li2022) or defensive 
mechanisms (Pančić and Kiørboe, 2018).   
Defence mechanisms enable the organisms to 
counter grazing pressure and can be physio-
logical (e.g. bioluminescence, toxicity),    
morphological (e.g. colony formation, silica 
shell), or behavioural traits (e.g. escape        
response) (Pančić and Kiørboe, 2018).     
However, each trait mechanism incurs a cost, 
which creates a trade-off. If there were no 
cost to a trait such as a defence mechanism, 
all species would be in an equal defence state, 
and diversity would no longer be promoted 
by grazing. (Ryderheim et al., 2021, Olesen 
et al., 2022). Experimental manipulations 
have demonstrated that predation of phyto-
plankton enhances genetic variability. 
If all predators were to be removed, the ge-
netic diversity would decrease significantly 
(Leibold et al., 2017). A defence mechanism 
can be inducible (induced only when exposed 
to grazers or pathogens) or constitutive

(Pančić and Kiørboe, 2018). 
1.2.2 Trade-offs 
For many defence mechanisms, the costs 
remain unquantified or undocumented. 
The cost associated with defensive traits is an 
important aspect to consider. Defensive 
mechanisms often require energy and            
resources to produce and maintain, which can 
come at the expense of other vital biological 
processes such as growth. The defence mech-
anism triggers the costs, but many mecha-
nisms may only be relevant when the             
resources are limited or predation pressure is 
high (Wang et al., 2015, Chakraborty et al., 
2019). The interactions and dynamic pro-
cesses occurring in natural environments are 
often difficult to fully capture and reproduce 
in controlled laboratory settings. Therefore, 
defensive mechanisms may entail hidden 
ecological costs that may not manifest in       
laboratory settings (Ryderheim et al., 2021). 
The costs for the defensive mechanism of 
toxin production in Pseudo-nitzschia remain 
debatable (Lundholm et al., 2005). The study 
of Tammilehto et al., 2015 showed that this 
defensive trait can be induced via chemical 
cues from copepods. Here, they exposed the 
diatom Pseudo-nitzscgia seriata directly and 
indirectly (separated by a membrane) to two 
different copepods. Two polyester tissue     
culture flasks were connected by two holes 
covered by a 2.0 µm polycarbonate          
membranes to allow water exchange but pre-
vent the exchange of copepods and algae 
cells. One chamber was filled with copepods 
and algae, and the other chamber was only 
filled with algae. The study found toxicity of 
the algae to be induced by the presence of    
copepods suggesting this response to be 
chemically mediated by waterborne cues 
from the copepods as it is upregulated in the 
presence of grazer cues for some toxic strains 
of Pseudo-nitzschia (Lundholm et al., 2005, 
Selander et al., 2019, Tammilehto et al., 
2015). The study of Ryderheim et al., 2021 
found a clear benefit of the toxin production 
using the dinoflagellate Alexandrium minu-
tum when they induced toxin production     
under different nutrient limitation levels of 
nitrogen. Induced cells had an up to three 
times higher chance of being rejected by a   
copepod compared to the non-induced cells. 
The chance for rejection showed to be posi-
tively correlated to the toxin content within 
the cells. No evidence of the direct cost in 
terms of reduced growth rate was demon-
strated. Neither the study of Tammilehto et 
al., 2015 showed any cost related to toxin 
production as they found no effect on the net 
growth between controls and induced cells

However, in one study of the diatom

Given that defensive traits have been 
proven to be beneficial (Olesen et al., 2022; 
Tammilehto et al., 2012), and the costs re-

managing the responses of phytoplankton

1.2.3 Cost for toxin production 
The toxin content and growth have been 
proven to be negatively correlated in            
P. seriata (Olesen et al., 2022), hence the 
cost, in this case, is reduced growth. Other 
potential ecological costs for defensive 
mechanisms in phytoplankton could be 
nutrient concentrations. A higher limit of 
nutrients may be required for some species 
with high growth and vice versa. 
The minimum resource concentration at 
which a population can survive and the 
growth rate equals zero is referred to as R* 
(Tilman 1982). When several genotypes of 
the same species, or several species are lim-
ited by the same resource, the species or gen-
otype with the lowest R* will win the compe-
tition. Depending on the system, species      
involved, consumption requirements and in-
flux of nutrients, the system can have several 
oscillations. But if we consider a very simple

system, with only one strain of a species, and 
no influx of nutrients, we can determine R* 
graphically (Fig. 3).  
If a strain were to inhabit a habitat, it would 
start to utilize the  resource, R. As the popu-

would decrease. At a point in time, the 
growth of the population would reach zero 
due to a lack of resources. At this point, R* 
can be determined. At R* the mortality rate 
would be in exact balance with the growth 
rate, hence the population growth equals 
zero. If the supply rate of the resource is ex-

this would be an equilibrium. (Fig. 3). R* is 
thus the concentration of a resource a popu-

maintain a stable equilibrium population in a 
laboratory setting without any external 
grazing mortality. The ’mortality’ is the rate 
at which the limiting nutrient is metabolised 
and lost by the cells (Tilman 1982). 
A beneficial trait could be the ability to 
survive at very low concentrations of nutri-

could be reduced growth. Or with a high de-
fence capacity, the cost could be equally high 
requirements for nutrient concentrations 
(high R*). Nutrient requirements, growth and 
defence capacity might be positively corre-
lated.

1.3 Pseudo-nitzschia 
The diatom genus of Pseudo-nitzschia is 
globally distributed and some species are 
known to produce domoic acid (DA). DA is 
a neurotoxin that causes amnesic shellfish 
poisoning (ASP) 
and 
is 
transferred 
throughout the marine food web via inges-
tion by pelagic and benthic organisms, 
leading to sickness and death in marine 
mammals and seabirds. Consumption of 
contaminated seafood can cause serious 
health effects in humans, including coma, 
short-term memory loss, mild gastrointestinal 
distress, seizures, and even death (McCabe et 
al., 2016, Gary et al., 1990). The first incident 
of amnesic shellfish poisoning happened in 
1986 and was caused by human consumption 
of blue mussels containing DA. At least three 
people died, and over 100 became sick (Bates 
et al., 1998, Sim et al., 1989), thus invoking 
an increasing interest in the Pseudo-nitzschia 
genus. The origin of the toxin was linked to a 
Pseudo-nitzschia multiseries bloom, which 
served as a food source for the mussels. Since 
then, other incidents of poisoning and death 
in seabirds (e.g. Mathematics, 2016) and ma-
rine mammals (e.g.Scholin et al., 2000) have 
been documented. Nowadays, the concentra-
tion of Pseudo-nitzschia species and the pres-
ence of DA in the flesh of seafood for human 
consumption is monitored by surveillance 
programs worldwide. 
Pseudo-nitzschia spp. are pennate diatoms 
with bilateral symmetry. All species display 
the same general morphology, namely a cell 
wall (frustule) made of silicic acid [Si(OH)4]. 
The frustule is composed of two valves (Epi-
theca and Hypotheca) that encloses the cell. 
At the edge between them, a slit (raphe) runs 
and is reinforced at the inside of the cell by 
small silica "bridges" (fibulae). However, the 
morphometrics may vary from one another 
between the strains depending on the sur-
rounding environment (Lelong et al., 2012). 
All but one Pseudonitzschia species (Pseudo-
nitzschia americana) possess the ability to 
form chains. The chain length is dependent 
on the species (Lelong et al., 2012).

1.3.1 Reproduction 
Pseudo-nitzschia species exhibit asexual 
and sexual reproduction. But in the asex-
ual reproduction cell size decreases gradu-
ally each time they divide. In asexual repro-
duction, the hypotheca becomes epitheca of 
the daughter cell. A new hypotheca will then 
be formed within the epitheca. Sexual repro-
duction is required to regain the largest cell 
size possible. Sexual reproduction takes 
place when the daughter cells reach a lower 
size threshold as a result of multiple asexual 
reproduction events (Bates et al., 1998).     
Below this size threshold, cells can no longer 
reproduce sexually and will therefore eventu-
ally die. The threshold, however, is species 
dependent (Lelong et al., 2012, Bates et al., 
2018). Most species are heterothallic, and re-
quire two parental cells of the opposite type, 
a "−" and a "+". "+" and "−" gametangia par-
ent cells align in a parallel formation with no 
physical link between them, and sexual        
reproduction occurs within 5-7 days (Lelong 
et al., 2012).   
1.3.2 Blooms 
Diatoms often bloom in upwelling zones 
where favourable currents enable them to 
remain and thrive in the upper sunlit wa-
ter column (Anderson et al., 2006,Lelong 
et al., 2012). In addition, these zones often 
facilitate nutrient-rich waters coming from 
the ocean depths, which are beneficial for     
diatom blooms. Many Pseudo-nitzschia spe-
cies are described as being cosmopolitan, in-
dicating their global distribution (Hasle, 
2002, Lundholm and Moestrup, 2002). When 
Pseudo-nitzschia forms a bloom, it can be 
both dense and toxic. During a bloom event 
of P. australis in May 2003 in Santa Barbara 
Channel, California, the abundance ranged 
from 0.4∗105 to 2∗106 cells∗l−1 and with a DA 
concentration between 32 and 1684ng∗l−1     
resulting in over 1500 pinniped deaths (An-
derson et al., 2006). DA-producing P. seriata 
has been confirmed in Scotland, Canada,     
Japan and Denmark (Lundholm et al., 
2005,Stonik et al., 2011).

1.4 The aim of the project 
Explaining the paradox of the plankton at 
an interspecific level is already challeng-
ing, and equally puzzling is the coexistence 
of different genotypes within a species. Ac-
cording to the competitive exclusion princi-
ple, at steady-state, only one strain should be 
able to exist, while other strains competing 
for the same resources should be out-com-
peted. The existence may be explained by one 
or several reasons as discussed above: (1) A 
steady state within a natural ecosystem in a 
given habitat is difficult to reach due to con-
stantly changing environmental conditions. 
Thereby, no adaptation is the best suited for 
the ever-changing conditions during the day, 
week, season or even on a yearly basis. (2) 
Mutations within the genetically different 
strains are neutral, making the strains physi-
ologically identical, (3) there are trade-offs 
among key traits. Some traits provide bene-
fits that increase survival rates but also come 
with a cost. 
In this study, I investigated trade-offs for 
three different strains of P. seriata at 5 or 6 
different temperatures. Three parameters 
were examined through experiments: (1) Dif-
ferences in temperature optima for growth. 
(2) Different grazer protection, including dif-
ferent capacities to up-regulate toxin produc-
tion (negative correlation between growth 
rate and grazer protection) at different tem-
peratures and (3) Different R* (positive rela-
tion between growth rate and R*, and positive 
correlation 
between 
R* 
and 
rejection            
frequency). 
Here I have explored how different geno-
types of the same species with known physi-
ological differences (e.g, growth rate and de-
fensive traits) can co-exist. I included three 
different strains and repeated the same exper-
iment at 5 or 6 different temperatures. To de-
termine the effectiveness of toxin production, 
I used direct video observations for individ-
ual copepod-prey interactions and demon-
strate a clear temperature optimum for differ-
ent strains, and a clear benefit of toxin pro-
duction for two out of three strains.

1.5 
Hypotheses 
My hypothesis is twofold. First, I propose 
that activating defence mechanisms, specifi-
cally through toxin production, carries a cost 
in terms of decreased growth and/or               
increased R*. I hypothesise a negative corre-
lation between defence efficiency and 
growth. This is because allocating resources 
towards defence can divert energy and nutri-
ents away from essential processes like cell 
division, metabolism, and reproduction, ulti-
mately leading to a decrease in growth rates. 
In addition, I hypothesise a positive correla-
tion between growth and R* and a positive 
correlation between defence efficiency and 
R* With high growth, the cost might be a high 
R* and vice versa. With low growth, the        
organism might be able to survive at very low 
nutrient concentrations meaning a low R*. 
Second, I anticipate that as temperatures in-
crease, the growth rate will initially rise until 
reaching a peak, after which it will decline, 
and different strains will have different tem-
perature optimums for growth. Finally, I      
expect to observe a distinct characteristic of 
toxin production through a significantly 
higher rejection frequency for induced cells 
compared to control cells. In sum, I will in-
vestigate the following five hypothesises. 
1. A negative correlation between defence 
efficiency and growth. 
2. A positive correlation between growth 
and R*. 
3. A positive correlation between defence 
efficiency and R*. 
4. As temperatures increase, the growth rate 
will initially rise until reaching a peak, af-
ter which it will decline, and different 
strains will have different temperature 
optimums for growth. 
5. Strains will have characteristics of toxin 
production through a significantly higher 
rejection frequency for induced cells 
compared to control cells.

3 Data analyses 
For all statistical tests, the statistical software 
environment R version 4.1.2 20211 was used. 
All data were checked for normal distribution 
by QQ-plots supported by supported by the 
Shapiro-Wilk tests. 
QQ plot draws the correlation between a 
given data and the normal distribution. If all 
points fall approximately along the reference 
line, we assume normality. 
The Shapiro–Wilk test is a test of normality. . 
If the p-value given by the Shapiro-Wilk test 
is greater than the significance level of 0.05 
the distribution of the data is not significantly 
different from normal distribution. In other 
words, we can assume the normality. 
3.1 Growth Rate dependency on tem-
perature 
The relationship between strain and growth 
was tested with a one-way analysis of vari-
ance (ANOVA), after checking data for      
normal distribution for every temperature 
(appx. 8.2.1). One-way ANOVA is the com-
monly used statistical approach for similar 
studies (Weber, 2021, Roger et al., 2012, 
Tammilehto et al., 2015, Leibold et al., 2017). 
Strains were set as the independent variable, 
and growth was set as the dependent variable. 
The analysis was conducted on the subset for 
every 
temperature 
separately 
before 
including and comparing temperatures. Post 
hoc analysis with Tukey’s Honestly Signifi-
cant Difference (HSD) was performed using 
the R package (emmeans). Post hoc tests 
(also known as pairwise comparison test), is 
performed after a significant result is            
obtained in tests that compare multiple 
groups like the ANOVA test. The purpose of 
a post-hoc test is to identify which specific 
groups differ from each other when the over-
all analysis (ANOVA) indicates a significant 
difference between groups. 
This information enhances our under-
standing of the relationships and patterns 
within the data set and helps to draw more    
accurate conclusions from the study. 
In addition, I applied a reparametrized ver-

3.3 Induction of copepodamides and 
defence capacity 
For the statistical analysis of the rejection 
frequency, I used the R package (lme4) and  
applied a generalized linear mixed effects    
regression model with a binomial distribution 
and logit link function, taking into account 
the random effects of replicates (i.e., the      
copepods). Data was found to be normal dis-
tributed.  
The generalized linear mixed effects re-
gression model approach is commonly used 
by similar studies (Ryderheim et al., 2021, 
Olesen et al., 2022). This statistical method is 
well-suited since both fixed effects and ran-
dom effects are specified via the model. Here, 
’Treatment’ is specified as the fixed effect 
variable and I am investigating its effect on 
the response variable "Rejections/Captures". 
The model investigates the impact of treat-
ment on the ratio of "Rejections" to "Cap-
tures". The random effect allows for account-
ing for variability in the response variable 
due to grouping or clustering within the lev-
els of the "Replicate" variable. 
After fitting the model for every strain and 
checking the normal distribution, I per-
formed an ANOVA to test the significance of 
the fixed effect of treatment. 
To calculate the odds ratios and their con-
fidence intervals based on the estimated fixed 
effects, I utilized the "confint" function in R 
for every strain and every temperature. The 
function was applied to the fitted generalized 
linear mixed-effects model using the argu-
ment parm="beta". By obtaining the odds    
ratios, it is possible to assess the impact of 
treatment on the odds of rejections relative to 
captures.

5 Discussion 
5.1 Growth rates 
This thesis aimed to investigate different 
trade-offs in the diatom Pseudo-nitzschia se-
riata, by growing three different strains under 
5-6 different temperatures. While doing so, I 
(1) measured the growth rates, (2) induced 
defenses (toxin production) by exposure to 
copepodamides and (3) measured the R*. 
Growth rates and R* measurement was per-
formed for all temperatures, while toxin in-
ducement experiments were carried out at 
4◦C, 6◦C, 8◦C and 10◦C. 
With this data, I aimed to document variations 
between the strains such as variations in opti-
mal growth rates and defence capacity. In addi-
tion, I aimed to define a cost for the ability to 
produce toxins. 
5.1.1 Growth rates 
Three strains of P. seriata were cultivated at 
multiple temperatures, but strain C3 showed 
no growth after the initial experiment at 4◦C 
and was replaced with strain D3. Interest-
ingly, strains H1 and G2 also exhibited abnor-
mal growth patterns at 4◦C. Possible reasons 
for this abnormality include a short acclima-
tization period or reawakening after strain 
storage. Although cultures were acclimatized 
for 2-6 days, confirmation of acclimatization 
was not obtained. To assess acclimatization, 
one could have considered it complete when 
the growth slopes of triplicate cultures were 
not significantly different from each other. 
However, the results of this study align 
with a well-documented principle indicating 
that moderate temperature increases lead to 
higher growth rates in smaller-sized phyto-
plankton species, followed by a decline (Hare 
et al., 2007). For example, previous studies 
on polar diatom strains and various species 
grown at different temperatures have shown 
a general increase in growth rates with           
increasing temperatures. The three strains of 
the polar diatom isolated from Greenland 
Fragilariopsis cylindrus showed an increase 
in growth rates with increasing temperature 
(1◦C, 5◦C and 8◦C) (Pančić et al., 2015). Like-
wise, eight different species grown at the 
temperatures (9◦C, 12◦C, 16◦C, 20◦C and 
25◦C) showed a general increase in their 
growth rates with increasing temperatures 
(Montagnes and Franklin, 2002). However, 
there are contrasting findings as well. One 
study demonstrated that the mean community 
growth rate in an arctic plankton community 
decreased with increased temperature in the 
open ocean community but increased in fjord 
communities until 6◦C, after which it de-
creased (Coello-Camba et al., 2015). Thus, 
when examining some species and size 
groups, the growth rate increased with 
==PAGE:43==
Physiological within-species variation in a diatom, Pseudo- nitzschia, and co-existence of strains with different traits 
 
 
Page 42 of 58 
 
temperature, while others decreased. In gen-
eral, smaller size species had increasing 
growth rates while larger species had a         
declining growth rate. This supports previous 
studies showing that global warming favours 
smaller-sized 
species 
of 
phytoplankton 
(Montagnes and Franklin, 2001, Morán et al., 
2010). 
The observed trend in my study is compa-
rable to a Canadian arctic strain of P. seriata 
cultivated at similar temperatures ranging 
from -1.6 to 15◦C. The growth rate of the 
strain increased until 6◦C and then levelled 
off (Stapleford and Smith, 1996). In our 
study, the mean growth rates across all tem-
peratures started to plateau at 10◦C, which fits 
when considering the strains in this study 
were collected in a warmer climate than the 
Canadian Arctic. It is most likely an adapta-
tion towards the temperatures in a given cli-
mate. Stapleford and Smith, 1996 found the 
mean growth rate to be 4 ◦C (0.35 day−1), 6 ◦C 
(0.49 day−1) and 10 ◦C (0.46 day−1), whereas 
the mean growth rates across all three strains 
at the same temperatures in this study were 
4◦C (0.27 day−1), 6◦C (0.31 day−1), 8◦C (0.38 
day−1), 10 ◦C (0.44 day−1), 12◦C (0.42 day−1) 
and 16◦C (0.13 day−1). The optimal growth 
rate at 10 ◦C (0.44 day−1), is close to the opti-
mal growth of the Canadian Arctic strain 
(0.49 day−1), which is also the temperature 
where both growth rates began to level out. 
Hence, the strains within a given environ-
ment are indeed adapted to the temperatures 
in that environment. 
The strains used in this study were collected 
in Denmark in April. Fig. 20 shows the 
monthly minimum and maximum sea surface 
temperature in Copenhagen which is close to 
the collecting site of the strains of this study. 
Data is based on years of historical data 
(NOAA). In April, the minimum temperature 
is 2◦C while the maximum temperature is 
8◦C. This is within the range of growth for the 
strains used in this present study, and indi-
cates, that these specific strains will most 
likely thrive until reaching a max temperature 
in June -mid-July off 16-17 ◦C (Fig. 20).

In this study, strains G2 and D3 did not 
exhibit any growth at 16◦C. This is most 
likely above temperatures at which the strains 
can maintain growth. Though it is worth not-

have enabled the strains to survive and grow 
at 16◦C. While previous studies on P. seriata 
from Danish waters did not show improved 
survival with longer acclimation periods 
(Fehling et al., 2004), strains isolated from 
Scottish waters have been reported to grow at 
15◦C with similar growth rates to strains iso-

4◦C (Hansen et al., 2011). The temperature 
optima cover a broad range of temperatures, 
but strains are likely to adapt to the given en-
vironment. 
 
5.1.2 Strain-specific growth rates 
In all cases, I observed a strain-specific 
pattern of increasing growth rate with rising 
temperatures, followed by a decline in 
growth rate between 10◦C and 16◦C after 
reaching a strainspecific maximum (Fig. 8 
and Fig. 9). 
This indicates that each strain has a different 
temperature range for optimal growth, which 
is supported by applying a Norberg curve 
(Norberg et al., 2001; Thomas et al., 2012) 
(Fig 10). However, it is important to 
acknowledge that obtaining more precise es-
timates would require additional measure-
ments in the lower end, as suggested by the 
high Twidth value of 80 for strain H1. 
My findings support the notion that 
strains of P. seriata taken from on sample at 
temperate climates exhibit variations in their 
optimal growth conditions, as reported in pre-
vious studies (Stapleford and Smith, 1996, 
Hansen et al., 2011). In addition, this study 
goes a step further by demonstrating that 
even within the same environmental condi-
tions, strains of P. seriata can differ from one 
another in terms of their growth characteris-
tics, as shown by the results (Fig. 8, Fig.  9, 
Fig 10). This highlights the inherent diversity 
and variability within phytoplankton popula-
tions, even in seemingly homogeneous envi-
ronments. 
Notably, strain D3 exhibited poor growth 
performance at higher temperatures (Fig. 9, 
Fig. 10). The Norberg curve suggested a Tmax 
at 16◦C (Table 2) for growth and is skewed 
towards lower temperatures, implying that 
this particular strain may thrive better at 
lower temperature ranges, or is able to main-
tain higher growth at lower temperatures than 
the strains H1 and G2. It is worth considering 
that including lower temperatures in the ex-
periment could have yielded better results for 
lower limits. D3 might be thriving or more 
==PAGE:45==

Page 44 of 58 
 
dense earlier in the year (Fig. 20) than strains 
H1 and G2 given the lower growth at higher 
degrees. 
In addition, since one strain had growth at 
16◦C (H1), the upper limit for growth was not 
defined for this strain by experiments. The 
Norberg curve suggested a temperature of 
17◦C (Table 2), but further experiments are 
needed to confirm this temperature. In addi-
tion, the Norberg curve estimated a Twidth at 
80, indicating a clear lack of more measure-
ments. Twidth is the range of temperatures at 
which the organisms can grow. To achieve 
optimal overall results, strain H1 should have 
been cultivated at temperatures ranging from 
approximately 0◦C to 20◦C, and all strains 
should have been tested at lower tempera-
tures, such as 0◦C or even negative tempera-
tures like in the study of Stapleford and 
Smith, 1996. 
The observed differences in growth rates 
among the strains might correspond to their

5.2 Cell volume 
Cell size is a key trait and is related to many 
physiological rates in phytoplankton (e.g. 
growth rates and nutrient uptake), biotic in-
teractions (e.g. grazing by zooplankton) and 
behaviour within the environment (e.g. verti-
cal migration and sinking speed) (Violle et al. 
2007, Barton et al., 2013). The study of Mon-
tagnes and Franklin, 2002 found a significant 
decrease in cell volume with increasing tem-
peratures for five of eight diatom spices and 
two flagellate spices. For one species, they 
found the cell size to increase with increasing 
temperatures, but this trend was driven by a 
few large cells (Montagnes and Franklin, 
2002). 
For this study, equation 2 by Lundholm et 
al., 2004 was applied. This equation is a mod-
ification of the equation given by Hillebrand 
et al. (1999). The modifications are made to 
fit better on Pseudo-nitzschia spp. It is as-
sumed that the linear part accounts for 60% 
of the cell length for Pseudo-nitzschia spp. 
and thus modifications are made accordingly. 
However, I found no clear pattern in cell vol-
ume in this study, but a minor tendency to a 
decreased cell volume at lower temperatures 
for two strains (Fig. 12). 
For future investigations, it would be interest-

5.3 Defence capacity 
This study provide strong evidence for an 
enhanced survival advantage in P. seriata 
with induced toxin production when they are 
fed upon by copepods. Thus this study prove 
a benefit of a trade-off 
5.3.1 Inducement of toxin production and 
rejection frequency 
The results of this study provide compelling 
evidence for the benefits of toxin production 
in P. seriata, particularly at temperatures of 
8◦C and 10◦C. I observed significantly higher 
rejection frequencies of induced cells by co-
pepod cues compared to the controls, indicat-
ing a clear advantage of toxin production in 
deterring predation (Fig. 14 and Table 16). 
Among the three tested strains, two exhibited 
stronger physiological defence mechanisms 
in terms of toxin production, indicating 
strain-specific variation in defence capacity. 
The third strain had a tendency to higher re-
jection of induced cells but was not signifi-
cantly supported. This could be reasoned by 
the low capture amount, and additional test-
ing is required to determine whether this is a 
lower defence capability for this specific 
strain, or if the non-significant result is due to 
lower capture amount. 
Nevertheless, these findings align with previ-
ous research by Ryderheim et al. (2021), sup-
porting the notion that toxin production con-
fers a defence mechanism in phytolankton, 
and enhances the survival odds. In addition, 
my results are consistent with the findings of 
Olesen et al. (2022), who reported rejection 
fractions of 0.75-0.80 for induced cells and 
0.10 - 0.25 for controls. Similarly, this study 
demonstrated comparable results, with rejec-
tion frequencies ranging from 0.20 to 0.29 for 
controls and 0.4 to 0.8 for induced cells (Fig. 
16 b and c, and Fig. 14). These findings fur-
ther support the notion that induced toxin 
production enhances defence efficiency 
against copepod predation. 
Interestingly, my analysis indicated that 
temperature did not significantly affect the 
defence capacity (Fig. 16 a). This suggests 
that P. seriata is capable of maintaining its 
defence capacity across a range of tempera-
tures as long as conditions are favourable for 
growth. However the study of Weber, 2021 
showed increasing toxin content with in-
creasing temperatures in 12 out of 17 strains. 
This suggests, that the efficiency is not de-
pendent on temperature, but the toxin content 
is. I did not measure the toxin content in this

5.4 Trade-offs 
In order for predation to promote diversity 
among phytoplankton, defensive traits must 
come with a cost. Otherwise, species or gen-
otypes with stronger defences would outcom-
pete those with weaker defences, leading to a 
homogenization of defence levels across the 
population. The production of toxins in P. se-
riata is inducible, which suggests a signifi-
cant cost associated with this defence 
mechanism. If there were no associated costs, 
one would expect consistent production of 
toxins. 
To explore this trade-off, I hypothesized a 
negative correlation between growth and de-
fence efficiency, suggesting that high defence 
efficiency would result in reduced growth 
rates. Additionally, I hypothesized that with a 
high maximum growth rate, the cost could be 
a high R*. And lastly, we hypothesized a pos-
itive correlation between defence efficiency 
and the minimum resource requirement for 
zero growth (R*), suggesting that higher de-
fence efficiency would be associated with an 
increased R*. By comparing R* values with 
optimal growth rates and defence efficiency, 
we aimed to gain insights into this trade-off 
and its implications. 
 
5.4.1 Growth and induced toxin production 
To investigate the potential cost of toxin pro-
duction in terms of reduced growth rate, I ex-
amined the growth rates of P. seriata exposed 
to copepodamides over a 48-hour period, in-
cluding both induced and control groups. 
However, the analysis did not reveal any sig-
nificant decrease in growth rate in the major-
ity of the experiments (Fig. 17). 
Trade-offs in defence mechanisms in phy-
toplankton are challenging to demonstrate in

laboratory setups (Pančić and Kiørboe, 
2018), and previous studies have suggested 
that the levels of toxin produced may be too 
low to result in a significant difference in 
growth rates (Lundholm et al., 2018). Inter-
estingly, Lundholm et al. (2018) found that an 
increased presence of herbivorous copepods 
led to higher toxin production, indicating a 
potential relationship between copepod pres-
sure and the cost of toxin production. The 
study by Olesen et al. (2022) documented a 
clear cost associated with toxin production, 
showing a decrease in growth rate with in-
creasing toxin levels. The discrepancy be-
tween my findings and those of Olesen et al. 
may be attributed to differences in experi-
mental duration and sampling strategies. 
Olesen et al. employed a longer incubation 
time of 72 hours and measured toxin levels 
within the cultures. The extended incubation 
period and direct measurement of toxin con-
tent may be necessary to detect the cost of in-
duced defence mechanisms in terms of re-
duced growth. Additionally, in this study, not 
all samples were collected exactly during the 
exponential growth phase. For instance, in 
the 8◦C experiment, the cells were induced a 
few days before reaching the exponential 
phase, resulting in limited growth for both the 
control and induced cells in that particular ex-
periment (Fig. 17). 
To further investigate this matter, it would 
be beneficial to repeat the experiment while 
ensuring that all samples for inducement are 
collected within the exponential growth 
phase. Extending the incubation time to 72 
hours instead of 48 hours, and a continuous 
inducement of copepodamides could provide 
reliable results since the copepodamides 
quickly decrease (Selander et al., 2019). It 
would also be interesting to induce the cells 
throughout the entire experiment regardless 
of the growth phase. These experimental set-
ups may provide additional insights into the 
potential cost associated with induced toxin 
production mechanisms. Moreover, measur-
ing the toxin content within the cultures could 
prove beneficial in highlighting the cost of 
toxin production.

The measurement of R* can indeed be chal-

5.5 Variation within species               
enhances the diversity of plankton 
communities 
This study provides valuable insights into the 
variations between strains in terms of growth, 
temperature optima and defence effective-
ness. The presence of different optimal tem-
peratures for each strain suggests that the 
diversity within a phytoplankton bloom can 
contribute to a prolonged duration of a 
bloom. Different genotypes can dominate at 
different temperatures, potentially increasing 
the overall temperature tolerance and ensur-
ing the persistence of the bloom across a 
range of conditions. This variation within 
strains is most likely due to a mixture of en-
vironmental fluctuations and trad-offs. 
Furthermore, the observation that some 
strains exhibit a slightly higher defence ca-
pacity in terms of toxin production, suggests 
that physiological defensive mechanisms can 
enhance the survival probability of these 
strains when faced with predation. This high-
lights the importance of considering the di-
versity of defence mechanisms within a phy-
toplankton community. 
While the costs of defensive trade-offs 
were difficult to document in this study, pre-
vious research has demonstrated such costs 
(Olesen et al., 2022). This suggests that there 
may be trade-offs between defence mecha-
nisms and other physiological traits, such as 
growth rates or resource allocation. Future 
studies focusing on a larger number of strains 
and employing more precise measurements 
may provide further insights into the costs as-
sociated with defensive trade-offs. 
Regarding the relationship between de-
fence capacity and temperature, the findings 
of this study differ from those of a previous

Different genotypes may have varying

5.6 Suggestions for future research 
Here, I have provided valuable insights 
into the challenges and potential approaches 
for further investigating key traits such as de-
fensive mechanisms in phytoplankton. It is 
indeed important to acknowledge that docu-
menting defensive trade-offs under labora-
tory conditions can be challenging, but with 
careful experimental design and additional 
measurements, more conclusive results can 
be obtained. 
To gain a better understanding of the costs 
associated with defensive mechanisms, it 
would be beneficial to increase the number of 
strains studied, as the variation between 
strains has been found to be substantial (We-
ber2021). Additionally, conducting experi-
ments with copepod feeding on induced cells 
for a wider range of strains and extending the 
incubation period could provide more in-
sights into the potential costs and effects of 
induced defence mechanisms across temper-
atures. 
In terms of investigating the regulation of 
defence capacity with temperature, including 
a broader range of strains and measuring the 
DA content would be advantageous. Further-
more, closely monitoring the growth rate 
would be crucial for assessing the potential 
trade-offs between growth and defence effi-
ciency. 
Overall, incorporating these suggestions 
and addressing the challenges associated with 
measuring costs and trade-offs in phyto-
plankton would contribute to a more compre-
hensive understanding of the dynamics be-
tween defensive mechanisms, growth, and 
environmental factors.

6 Conclusion 
This study set out to investigate the following 
hypothesis: 
 
1) Strains will have characteristics of 
toxin production through a signifi-
cantly higher rejection frequency for 
induced cells compared to control 
cells. 
2) A negative correlation between de-
fence efficiency and growth. 
3) A 
positive 
correlation 
between 
growth and R*. 
4) A positive correlation between de-
fence efficiency and R*. 
5) As temperatures increase, the growth 
rate will initially rise until reaching a 
peak, after which it will decline, and 
different strains will have different 
temperature optimums for growth. 
In conclusion, this study demonstrates the en-
hanced survival of diatoms through induced 
toxin production. Among the three tested 
strains, two exhibited stronger physiological 
defence mechanisms in terms of toxin pro-
duction, indicating strain-specific variation in 
defence capacity. However, no significant 
correlation was found between rejection fre-
quency and growth, although there was a ten-
dency towards decreased growth at higher 
rejection frequencies. The analysis did not re-
veal a clear correlation between R* and rejec-
tion frequency or R* and growth.  
 
Notably, the study identified different tem-
perature optima for optimal growth among 
the three strains. This finding sheds light on 
the paradox of plankton, where different gen-
otypes with different temperature preferences 
can coexist and contribute to the overall di-
versity of phytoplankton communities. 
While this study contributes to our under-
standing of diatom defence mechanisms and 
the role of temperature in shaping species in-
teractions, further investigations are needed 
to explore the trade-offs and costs associated 
with different defence strategies. Future re-
search should focus on analysing the varia-
tion within species and elucidating the under-
lying mechanisms of trade-offs in order to 
gain a more comprehensive understanding of 
the paradox of the plankton.
