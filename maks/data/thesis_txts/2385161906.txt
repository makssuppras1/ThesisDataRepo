Summary
The goal of the thesis is to study regional and global sea level budgets, trying to
assess where closure is or is not achieved and why. As dened in the literature,
the sea level budget is closed when the sum in the variation of the two individual
components (steric and mass induced) is equal to the total sea level variation
observed using altimetry. The motivation behind choosing this particular sub-
ject lies in the tremendous importance of the latest climate changes. When the
degree of understanding of the past phenomena rises, our capacity to assess the
damages and to predict what is about to come also improves considerably.
In this thesis, two types of multivariate statistical methods (regression and
canonical correlation) are applied on three dierent input sets: sea level anoma-
lies, steric heights and mass induced heights, the analysis being carried out for
a period of approximately 13 years (from September 2002 to December 2015).
For this specic time span, the sea level budget is not closed. Even if simi-
lar patterns can be observed between the dierent datasets and some of the
discrepancies can be explained, the overall assessment is not satisfactory. For
a deeper understanding of the process, more details should be included in the
investigations, some of them being mentioned in the last chapter.

Chapter 1
Introduction
During the past couple of decades, more and more evidence concerning the
climate change problem has been gathered. The average global temperature is
rapidly rising, the Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets are shrinking, extreme
weather events become more present in some regions of the world and the sea
level is notably rising [5]. Confronted with this crucial information, scientists
and authorities in charge try to continuously improve the overall understanding
of the ongoing changes.
Studying the sea level variations (SLV) and determining as accurately as pos-
sible the sea surface height (SSH) are thus some of the most pregnant research
directions nowadays. The SSH remains one of the most important indicators
of global climate change and can be used in various applications (short term
weather change prediction, long term climate pattern approximation, ice and
ocean dynamics, coastal phenomena etc.). However, the main goal remains en-
suring that human life is not threatened in any way by the latest developments.
Sea level variations can be classied in two main categories, based on the cause
of their occurrence: steric (density related) sea level variations are due to the
thermal expansion of sea water, whereas mass induced variations are due to the
exchange carried out between dierent water sources. The steric eect is depen-
dent on temperature and salinity variations in the sea water; by measuring the
temperature and the salinity variations as a function of depth, it is now possible

to isolate the steric eect and see how much of the total water level variation
originates from this category [6, 7]. On the other hand, the mass induced varia-
tions can be identied as direct additions or subtractions of water mass, simple
examples being the ice sheets', ice caps' or glaciers' melting contribution.
In [8] the authors emphasize the importance of closely studying the sea level
rise, both as an indicator of the impact that society had on climate change
during the last decades, as well as an indicator of what is about to follow. It is
denitely crucial to anticipate the changes that will emerge and to assess how
fast these changes will manifest themselves.
Clearly, the advancement in sea observation systems had great merit in the
latest discoveries. With data from satellite altimetry (TOPEX/Poseidon, Jason-
1, Jason-2), space gravimetry observations (GRACE - Gravity Recovery and
Climate Experiment) and hydrographic oats (Argo mission), more compelling
investigations can be carried out. The sea level budget can be dened based
on Equation 1.1. It is important to mention that each of the equation's terms
indicate the rate of change for each component. Several studies ([6], [8]) describe
the sea level budget as being closed when the sum of the two independent
components - steric and mass induced - is equal to the total sea level variations
observed by altimetry.
SLVtotal = SLVsteric + SLVmass
(1.1)
A closed level variation budget over a certain period of time indicates that the
human degree of understanding of the phenomenon is high. If the two individual
causes account for the total sea level rise, the observations are complete and
accurate [8]. In practice, however, dierent studies show that the closure of
the sea level budget is highly dependent on the period in question, on extreme
weather phenomena and on the mathematical models and corrections applied
to the measured input data.
The following analysis will try to identify and
explain some of the discrepancies observed. The aim is to spot regional and
global patterns, by applying multivariate statistical methods on three datasets
corresponding to the main terms from Equation 1.1.

Measurements and technology
The present thesis will consider and analyze three dierent datasets, correspond-
ing to the three main factors discussed above. The total sea level variation will
be studied based on a satellite altimetry set. The mass induced variations will be
determined using the GRACE mission measurements, while the steric variation
will be analyzed using the Argo mission data.
2.1.1
Satellite altimetry
Satellite altimetry has been the main technology used for determining sea surface
heights during the last past decades.
The basic principle of these kinds of
measurements is the precise determination of the distance between the altimeter
and the target - in this case the distance between the satellite and the ocean
surface (Figure 2.1). By measuring the time delay of the radar signal, the height
h can be calculated as h = ctd
2 , where td is the time delay and c is the speed of
light.

km distance (Figure 2.2). Based on the accurately measured distances between
the two satellites, gravity anomalies can be determined and subsequently the
mass induced sea level variations can also be calculated.

Chapter 4
Conclusions and further
developments
The main goal of the present master thesis was to study regional and global
sea level budgets and to asses where exactly an why closure is achieved. As
explained in the previous chapters, all the statistical methods used are part
of an exploratory data analysis that tries to oer more insight into the three
studied entities [global sea level variations, steric (density related) variations
and mass induced variations] as well as into the closed sea level budget concept.
Regression analysis using the monthly global averages proved itself to be a strong
tool in the present study: this type of analysis provides information about the
overall behavior of the three separate components over the 13 years period. The
emphasis lies in the velocity and acceleration parameters from each model, as
they are indicators of how much the sea level is decreasing or increasing and
how fast this process occurs. It has been shown that adding more parameters
to the regression model does not necessarily provide a better t for the phe-
nomenon. Over parameterization can aect the statistical signicance of some
of the parameters, in the end the 2-parameters model remaining the most useful.
Another interesting aspect discussed refers to the spatial distribution of trends
obtained using regression analysis. Based on the spatial patterns observed in
the reported trends, disrupting natural phenomena can be identied and used to
==PAGE:88==
70
Conclusions and further developments
explain variations in the sea level. Canonical Correlation Analysis oers similar
results, being most useful in the identication and interpretation of the El Niño
and El Niña events.
The main conclusion is related, of course, to the budget closure. For the 2002 -
2015 period, the sea level budget seems to not be closed. However, some small
regions exhibit closure to some extent. Considering the 2002 - 2013 time series
and obtaining favorable results prove that the concept of a closed sea level budget
is highly dependent on the time period in focus. Other changes in the model
parameters or in the initial data may also cause considerable modications in
the results.
Extensions can be made to the analysis presented in the previous chapters: one
approach could be related to the pre-processing of the initial data. It would
be interesting to see how the budget closure is aected when the GIA correc-
tion is also applied to the altimetry dataset (here it was applied only to the
GRACE Tellus dataset), when outliers are eliminated aprori or when the miss-
ing GRACE monthly data is generated through a more accurate procedure than
simple averaging.
Another interesting course of action would be applying multiset canonical corre-
lation analysis simultaneously on the three datasets. This would provide much
better insight into the linear relation between the three sets of initial data.

Three plot functions were created and used in the above mentioned scripts
in order to obtain the desired gures. Function PlotCoastline.m overlays the
coast lines onto a previously created gure. Function PolarColorMapAndPlot.m
creates a polar color map and plot, where the distinction between negative and
positive values is easy to spot. The last used function PlotCCInitialData.m is
connected to the canonical correlation analysis and creates a plot containing
the correlation between an estimated canonical variable and one of the initial
datasets.
