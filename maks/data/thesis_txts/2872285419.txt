Abstract
While recent studies have primarily focused on quantifying the fuel mix required to meet
the EU ETS and FuelEU Maritime targets, this thesis provides a long­term roadmap to
2050, assessing how these regulatory schemes will influence the cost of conventional
maritime fuels. The core objective is to evaluate the evolving cost gap between fossil
fuels and e­methanol, based on a detailed LCoM analysis, adjusted for carbon pricing.
The study integrates projections for CAPEX, electricity prices, and CO2 costs to assess
the economic competitiveness of e­methanol as a primary decarbonization option. Addi­
tionally, the roadmap incorporates biofuels and other e­fuels as complementary transition
pathways, outlining their potential role in bridging the economic and technological gap
during the shift from fossil­based to carbon­neutral shipping fuels.

Assumptions
• The price of fossil fuels used in shipping is assumed to be constant, while the total
cost for shipping companies includes additional and varying costs due to carbon
pricing mechanisms applied to their emissions.
• Engine efficiencies are assumed to be identical across all engines and ship types,
regardless of the fuel used.
• The e­methanol plant is assumed to operate during the cheapest hours of the year.
For example, if the optimal operating time is 6.000 hours/year, those would be the
6,000 lowest­priced hours.
• The total CAPEX of the e­methanol plant is split into two components: the hydrogen
plant and the methanol plant. The hydrogen plant CAPEX is assumed to follow a
cost of 1,349.7 €/kW in 2025 and to decline over time according to the average of
conservative and ambitious scenarios from NOASR [1­2]. The methanol synthesis
unit CAPEX is scaled from a reference value of 28.86 M€ for a 100 TPD plant and
adjusted for a 274 TPD facility. A 10% cost reduction is assumed for every doubling
of cumulative installed capacity, reflecting technological learning [3].
• The electricity demand to produce one tonne of methanol is assumed to be 12.3
MWh/tMeOH for both hydrogen plant consumption, its degradation, and methanol
plant consumption as explained in Section 2.5.
• The amount of CO2 required to produce one tonne of e­methanol is assumed to be
1,4 tonnes, as detailed in Section 2.5.
• The LCoM for a given year is assumed to correspond to a plant constructed in that
same year and it includes an IRR of 8%.
• O&M costs are assumed to be 2% of the CAPEX [4].
• Electricity spot prices in Spain are taken from the study “Flexibility Solutions for High­
Renewable Energy Systems” and are adjusted to real2024 values and assumed to
follow a compound annual decrease [5].
• The prices paid by the e­methanol plant to consume electricity are assumed to be
the electricity spot prices as explained in Section 2.4.
• EU ETS prices are assumed to lie between the conservative and ambitious scenar­
ios of the study “Tightening EU ETS targets in line with the European Green Deal:
Impacts on the decarbonisation of the EU power sector” [6]. In the base case, EU
ETS prices are set at 71% of those in the ambitious scenario, as explained in Sec­
tion 3.2. In the conservative case, EU ETS prices are set at 50% of those in the
ambitious scenario, as explained in Section 1.5
• Biogenic CO2 prices are assumed to follow the market equilibrium formula described
in Section 2.3 following an 8% profit margin for biogas plants.
• CCU and CCS technologies are assumed to be equally available and viable across
locations. The distance from the biogas plants to potential PtX plants and potential
storage facilities is assumed to be 50 kilometres.

Introduction
Greenhouse gas emissions continue to rise and the transport sector plays a crucial role
in this increase. As shown in Figure 1, between 1990 and 2023, transport­related CO2
emissions grew by approximately 78%, outpacing the 48% increase in overall global emis­
sions or the 64% rise in overall global emissions excluding land use, which are hard to
measure. Within this sector, maritime transport alone accounted for approximately 12%
of CO2 emissions in 2023, corresponding to around 3% of global GHG emissions [10­11].
The growing demand for international trade, driven by globalisation, has further acceler­
ated this trend and, despite improvements in fuel efficiency and carbon intensity, the total
amount of emissions from shipping is still increasing.
Figure 1: Evolution of global and transportation sector CO2 emissions [12­13].
Therefore, the maritime industry is under increasing regulatory pressure to decarbonise.
First, IMO­2020, then the EU ETS and now the RED II and the FuelEU Maritime regula­
tions, are reshaping the industry’s economics, imposing higher costs on traditional fossil
fuels. At the same time, alternative fuels offer a promising pathway to comply with the
targets, although their feasibility depends on a combination of several factors.
While recent studies estimate the fuel mix needed to meet regulations, this thesis analyses
how such policies affect fossil fuel prices and their cost gap with e­methanol over time.
The study is structured into four key sections:
1. From the off­taker’s perspective, upcoming regulations such as the EU ETS are
analysed to determine how they will impact the cost of traditional fuels over time.
The purpose of this section is to estimate the costs that shipping companies will
incur for each type of fuel, which is assumed to reflect their WTP for alternative
fuels.
2. From the producer’s perspective, a business case of an e­methanol plant is devel­
oped to estimate the LCoM, which includes an IRR and is assumed to reflect the

==PAGE:14==
WTA of producers over time.
3. Once both sides (supply and demand) are measured, the comparison between WTP
and WTA is analysed. This section aims to estimate the evolution of the price gap
between traditional fuels and e­methanol, as well as the subsidies required to bridge
it.
4. The last section focusses on identifying bottlenecks in e­methanol production, analysing
potential improvements to reduce the transition cost from traditional fuels.
By integrating economic, regulatory, and technological factors, this thesis aims to pro­
vide a comprehensive understanding of the elements shaping the viability of e­methanol
adoption in the maritime sector.
According to the IMO, the barrier to uptake alternative fuels for the shipping industry relies
on its current uncertainty of when and by how much fuel prices could change. This is what
this thesis is aiming to solve [14].

1
Demand Perspective: Regulatory
impact on fossil fuel prices over time
The maritime industry is undergoing a rapid transformation, particularly in relation to fuel
choices. Regulatory measures such as IMO 2020 have already shifted the sector toward
low sulphur fuels, and recent policies, most notably the EU Emissions Trading System
(EU ETS) and FuelEU Maritime, are further encouraging a reassessment of fuel strategies
among shipping companies. As illustrated in Figure 1.1, there is a clear trend toward the
adoption of alternative fuels. This shift is driven not only by environmental considerations
but also by the potential for long term economic benefits.
Figure 1.1: Growth of alternative fuel uptake by number of ships [15].
These developments highlight the critical role of regulation in shaping the maritime fuel
landscape. By increasing the relative cost of conventional fossil fuels, such policies en­
hance the competitiveness of cleaner alternatives. At the same time, the evolving cost
structure of traditional fuels, including both base prices and associated emission costs,
plays a key role in determining the economic feasibility of switching to low or zero carbon
options.
1.1
Fossil fuels used in shipping
Historically, HFO has been the predominant marine fuel, particularly in the form of IFO
380. However, it faced challenges after the IMO reduced the maximum sulphur content
in marine fuels from 3.5% to 0.50% [16]. In response, companies began transitioning to
VLSFO and MGO while others chose to install scrubbers on vessels to continue using
HFO [17].
In contrast, LNG and its associated engine technologies are expected to play a cru­
cial role in the transition to cleaner shipping practices, as shown in Figure 1.1. When

1.2
Price of fossil fuels used in shipping
The fuel costs taken for this study are based on bunker prices in Rotterdam, a key bench­
mark port for fuel prices in Europe [19].
These are 450€ per tonne of IFO­360, 550€ per tonne for VLSFO, and 700€ per tonne
for MGO and LNG, which are considered stable over the years for the purpose of this
analysis. This approach is supported by the Mærsk Mc­Kinney Møller Center for Zero
Carbon Shipping, which shows mostly stable prices for LNG after 2030 [20].
Long­term price forecasting for fossil fuels is challenging due to complex geopolitical,
economic, and technological factors. Using stable prices in the calculations avoids in­
troducing additional uncertainty and allows for a clearer assessment of regulatory and
technological impacts. It is the price gap between fuels that makes this comparative anal­
ysis relevant, and that tends to remain consistent over time, as external factors typically
affect the price of all fuels simultaneously.
1.3
Upcoming regulations
1.3.1
EU ETS
The inclusion of maritime transport in EU ETS is expected to have a significant impact
on the shipping industry. This regulation applies to a wide range of vessels, including all
ships over 5,000 gross tonnage, as well as cargo and offshore ships between 400 and
5,000 gross tonnage. However, certain vessels, such as warships, naval auxiliaries, fish­
related ships, and government ships used for non­commercial purposes, are exempt from
these rules. As a result, more than 55% of all ships, accounting for over 90% of the CO2
emissions from the maritime sector, are covered by this regulation [21].
For voyages between two EU ports, 100% of emissions are covered under the EU ETS,
while trips starting or ending outside the EU are subject to 50% coverage. This approach
ensures that international shipping is also considered for its environmental impact. The
system introduces a gradual phase­in: companies must surrender allowances for 70% of
reported emissions in 2025 (payable in 2026), increasing to 100% from 2026 onwards.
Initially, only CO2 emissions are covered, but from 2026 the scope will expand to include
CH4 and N2O. These changes, along with the rising share of payable emissions, will
directly impact fuel cost projections [22].
1.3.2
FuelEU Maritime
The EU has introduced another regulation under the FuelEU Maritime initiative to reduce
GHG emissions from shipping, accounting for both TTW and WTT emissions to capture
the full environmental impact of marine fuels. It applies to all ships over 5,000 gross ton­
nage that transport passengers or cargo for commercial purposes, regardless of their flag,
and follows the geographical scope of the EU ETS. Under FuelEU Maritime, companies
must reduce their annual CO2, CH4, and N2O emissions per ship, based on a reference
==PAGE:17==
value of 91.16 gCO2eq/MJ, with the target share of this value changing every five years
[21].
The reduction targets are as follows:
• 2% from 1 January 2025 = 89.336 g CO2eq/MJ
• 6% from 1 January 2030 = 85.690 g CO2eq/MJ
• 14.5% from 1 January 2035 = 77.942 g CO2eq/MJ
• 31% from 1 January 2040 = 62.900 g CO2eq/MJ
• 62% from 1 January 2045 = 34.61 g CO2eq/MJ
• 80% from 1 January 2050 = 18.232 g CO2eq/MJ
A key feature of this regulation is its allowance for fleet­wide averaging, meaning the
over­performance of one ship can offset the under­performance of others within the same
company. As a result, companies are expected to begin blending or substituting traditional
fuels with low­emission alternatives, which has led to several studies exploring potential
fuel combinations to meet the requirements. Although the regulation does not directly
affect fuel prices, it is expected to influence the WTP of companies for alternative fuels to
comply with the mandated targets.

1.5
EU ETS allowance price projection
A study by PICIR outlines EU ETS allowance price projections under two decarbonisation
pathways: a conservative scenario, targeting a 43% emission reduction by 2030 (relative
to 2005 levels), and an ambitious scenario aligned with the EU Fit­for­55 target of a 55%
reduction by 2030 (relative to 1990 levels) [6]. By 2025, the study forecasts CO2 prices
of 27.5 €/t under the conservative pathway and 100 €/t under the ambitious one, rising to
100 €/t and 300 €/t, respectively, by 2050.
For the purpose of this calculation, the EU ETS reference projection is set as 71% of the
ambitious trajectory, reflecting the ratio of the average EU ETS allowance prices of 2024
(65 €/t) to the projected ambitious value (91,5 €/t).

2
Supply Perspective: Analysis of
e­methanol costs over time
As the shipping industry seeks viable pathways for decarbonisation, methanol emerges
as a promising alternative fuel, at least in the short and mid term. While ammonia remains
in the early stages of development, methanol benefits from a more mature technological
landscape, with simpler bunkering demonstrated and existing refuelling infrastructure.
As the demand for alternative fuels grows, the shipbuilding industry is expected to follow a
learning curve similar to that of LNG, leading to accelerated capacity expansion. Despite
an estimated 16% increase in capital costs for methanol­fuelled vessels compared to
conventional ships, this premium is not substantially different from that for LNG vessels,
which already account for a significant portion of new orders [27].
However, the long­term competitiveness of e­methanol will depend on infrastructure ex­
pansion, supply chain efficiencies, biogenic CO2 availability, and regulatory frameworks
that support its integration into the maritime sector. All these factors suggest that the
future of e­methanol relies on its price, subsequently determined by its production costs.
2.1
Procedure to calculate LCoM
The aim of this section is to evaluate how the LCoM will develop over time. Therefore, it is
assumed that the LCoM varies year­on­year as the CAPEX, OPEX, CO2, and electricity
prices vary over the years. For this reason, the analysis is based on a plant that would be
constructed every year with its respective changing costs.
As fuel production tends to concentrate where it is most cost­effective, and given that
Spain received 53% of the total subsidies awarded by the European Hydrogen Bank in
April 2024, the plant is planned to be located in Spain. This decision leverages not only
the country’s abundant renewable resources but also its strong institutional support for
hydrogen projects. Moreover, with a capacity of 150 MW, the plant strikes a balance
between achieving economies of scale and maintaining manageable infrastructure and
operational complexity [28].
2.2
CAPEX
The total CAPEX of the e­methanol plant is divided into two main components: the hy­
drogen plant and the methanol plant. Each is assessed separately based on established
methodologies from existing literature, allowing for a more transparent and robust cost
estimation. This split enables a more accurate representation of the techno­economic dy­
namics of the plant, as both units are subject to different scaling factors and cost­reduction
trends over time.
The CAPEX of the hydrogen plant is estimated using the same methodology as in DTU
studies, where investment costs are expressed per unit of installed capacity with a refer­
ence value of 1349.7 €/kW [1]. As this study is for a 150 MW plant, the CAPEX for the
initial year of the hydrogen plant is calculated as:

2.3
Biogenic CO2
Biogas plants that upgrade their biogas have the option to either release, sell or store their
captured CO2. Their willingness to store it depends on the economic incentives provided
by governments, which effectively determine the minimum price at which they are willing
to sell their CO2.
Assuming that such incentives are set to cover the costs associated with doing CCS, the
price at which biogas plants sell their CO2 to external parties, such as PtX facilities or
fossil­based emitters, follows these pricing dynamics:
==PAGE:23==
From the perspective of these emitters, who do not use CO2 as a feedstock but may
purchase and store it to offset their emissions, two alternatives arise:
1. Pay the EU ETS carbon price to emit one tonne of CO2.
2. Buy CO2 from a biogas plant and store it, incurring a cost of P + CS, where P is the
price paid to the biogas plant for their CO2, and CS is the cost of storing it.
To be indifferent between these two options, emitters would be willing to pay up to:
EU ETS = P + CS
⇒
P = EU ETS −CS
This price ensures that their total cost of buying and storing CO2 equals the cost of emitting
CO2 and paying the carbon price.
Therefore, the maximum market price the biogas plant can receive for its CO2 is:
Pmax = EU ETS −CS
This becomes the price that PtX plants using CO2 as a feedstock would need to pay to
access it, as it is the maximum amount that their competitors (emitters) would be willing
to pay.
However, for the biogas plant to consider this sale economically viable, the revenue from
selling CO2 must at least cover its costs and provide a reasonable profit. If government
incentives for CCS go beyond cost coverage and include a margin, this margin would set
the minimum profit for the biogas plant when selling its CO2.
However, under the assumption that these incentives only cover the full cost of CCS
without generating any margin, the minimum acceptable price P for selling CO2 should
simply equal the cost of capturing and the profit decided by the biogas plant:
P ≥CCC
This condition occurs when:
P ≥CCC
⇒
EU ETS −CS ≥CCC
⇒
EU ETS ≥CCC + CS
This means that the biogas plant would sell its CO2 to the market at a price P = EU ETS−
CS when this value exceeds its total cost and desired profit, or more specifically, when
EU ETS ≥CCC + CS.
If the EU ETS allowance price does not exceed this threshold, the plant would instead
sell its CO2 at a price of P = CCC, which covers its cost of capturing the CO2 plus the
intended profit margin.
This framework not only highlights how the EU ETS impacts the price of biogenic CO2,
but also reveals how government intervention can hinder the deployment of e­methanol
by subsidising alternative pathways such as CCS.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to explore the economic feasibility of e­fuel adoption for sustainable
shipping, with a specific focus on e­methanol. Through an assessment of CO2 price
dynamics, regulatory developments, and cost trajectories, it provides a forward­looking
picture of the evolving marine fuel landscape.
Today, with growing regulatory pressure and rising CO2 prices, the cost advantage of fossil
fuels is gradually diminishing, with costs projected to range from 41.6 €/GJ in a high­cost
scenario to 22.8 €/GJ in a low­cost scenario by 2050. In this context, LNG is expected to
become the most cost­effective option after 2040, supporting its current growth in demand
for the transition.
This transition from fossil fuels to e­fuels will be a gradual process. In the interim, biofuels
can serve as a bridging solution, facilitating emissions reductions while utilising existing
infrastructure and avoiding electrolyser investment costs. Bio­LNG presents a competi­
tive, near­term option by allowing blending with fossil LNG, although its long­term scala­
bility is constrained by biomass availability and increasingly emission caps. In contrast,
bio­methanol offers a more adaptable pathway, enabling vessels to transition from fos­
sil to bio­methanol and ultimately to e­methanol over their operational lifetimes, thereby
ensuring regulatory compliance and operational continuity throughout each phase of the
transition.
E­methanol prices in 2050 are projected to range from 59.2 €/GJ in the high­cost scenario
to 26.6 €/GJ in the low­cost scenario, reflecting significant uncertainty in future produc­
tion costs, technology development, and market conditions. In the optimistic scenario,
e­methanol could become one of the most cost­effective shipping fuels. In contrast, the
base scenario that offers a more realistic and attainable outlook projects a cost of 41.37
€/GJ by 2050. Under these conditions, e­methanol remains the most expensive fuel,
costing 15% more than fossil methanol, 27% more than VLSFO, and 36% more than
LNG DFDSS, which would be the cheapest option.
In contrast, under more pessimistic scenarios where CAPEX and electricity costs do not
decrease sufficiently, e­methanol remains entirely uncompetitive. This underscores that
the viability of e­methanol is closely tied to the ambition of regulatory frameworks. Without
a strong commitment from the EU and targeted investment in electrolyser deployment and
grid integration, the potential of e­methanol may not be realised.
Beyond CAPEX and electricity costs, the price of CO2 plays a key role in shaping the cost
of e­methanol. Rising EU ETS allowance prices and increased CCS subsidies can raise
the market value of biogenic CO2, unintentionally driving up e­methanol production costs.
As fossil fuels continue to dominate global energy systems, e­methanol produced using
biogenic CO2 provides a net­zero alternative that replaces fossil­based CO2 emissions
on a one­to­one basis. In this context, restricting access to biogenic CO2 for e­methanol
production may hinder short­ and medium­term deployment, ultimately limiting the long­
term scalability and competitiveness of this fuel.
Building on this, biomass is expected to become increasingly limited, forcing a shift toward

Outlook
Looking ahead, this study explores how the role of e­methanol in maritime decarboni­
sation could vary depending on how real­world conditions evolve. Several uncertainties
remain, which could significantly influence the outcome or help narrow the range of pos­
sible trajectories as more information becomes available.
For example, fuel price stability for currently used fossil alternatives was assumed, but
geopolitical dynamics and market fluctuations could introduce volatility, either reinforcing
or weakening e­methanol’s long­term competitiveness. Similarly, CO2 pricing under the
EU ETS was modelled using a broad range, with the base scenario derived as 71% of
the ambitious trajectory. Future work could refine this input using a more detailed and
context­specific methodology.
On the technology front, the economic feasibility of e­methanol relies on rapid cost re­
ductions for electrolysers and methanol synthesis plants. The anticipated cost declines
are tied to global deployment rates, but any delay in scaling up these technologies could
postpone or compromise cost parity.
This study relies on Spanish electricity price projections from 2018, which are shaped by
national energy strategies, grid modernisation efforts, and market design. As the electric­
ity market continues to evolve, future feasibility studies could further refine this input to
reflect updated market conditions.
Regarding carbon sourcing, rising demand for biogenic CO2 across sectors could acceler­
ate the shift toward DAC, reshaping the LCoM trajectory. Additionally, if DAC technologies
do not reach projected cost declines, e­methanol production at scale could face significant
challenges.
Alternative fuels, such as e­ammonia, warrant further investigation to determine when
infrastructure and safety barriers might be overcome.
Overall, the outlook for e­methanol is conditional. Ongoing monitoring of cost develop­
ments, carbon policy evolution, and infrastructure deployment will be essential to refine
feasibility assessments and support strategic decision­making in maritime decarbonisa­
tion.
