Abstract
In recent years mental health issues are rising and more people are affected than
ever before. This study aims to investigate the association between mental health
issues and the spatial, socioeconomic, and environmental pollution data of 628 zones
in the Greater Copenhagen area. As mental health issues are a vast area with many
different influencing variables this study will focus solely on the social aspect of the
variables affecting mental health. The study was comprised of many aspects of the
data science pipeline, starting with the creation of the zones, data collection from
various sources, data cleaning, spatial analysis of the data, and lastly several machine
learning models were developed to predict the number of mental health diagnoses
within each zone. The results of the spatial analysis revealed interesting patterns
across the zones especially civil status and citizenship showing a high correlation with
the number of mental health diagnoses. Two different regression models were created
for each of the years from 2012 to 2017 and both attained r2 scores above 70% for
each year. Feature importances were calculated from the models to discover the most
important features when predicting the mental health diagnoses which revealed a
strong tendency towards the socioeconomic and demographic variables in the dataset.
Based on the findings, this study concludes that greater attention should be given to
individuals not in relationships, as well as those with foreign citizenship, to address
mental health concerns effectively.

SECTION 1
Introduction
Mental health issues are rising and it affects many people in Danish society(Psykiatrifonden,
2023)[1]. This study aims to examine the correlation between mental health and a
wide range of factors at a zone level within the greater Copenhagen area. By focusing
on a specific geographic region, this research seeks to gain a deeper understanding of
how various factors interact and influence mental health outcomes in a local context.
The subject of mental health issues consists of a wide amount of variables and factors
making it diﬀicult to comprehend.
The American psychiatric association defines mental health issues as ”Mental ill-
nesses are health conditions involving changes in emotion, thinking or behavior (or a
combination of these)”
In general mental health issues have many different influencing factors.
The
Biopsychosocial Model (G L Engel, 1977)[2] divides the factors into three main cate-
gories, biological, psycho, and social, the latter of which will be investigated.
While numerous studies have explored the relationships between various individ-
ual social variables and mental health, this project aims to consolidate multiple social
variables within the same study. This study also seeks to examine the patterns over a
zoning scheme centered around the 20 municipalities of the Greater Copenhagen area.
The zones will consist of both highly urban zones but also have rural and suburban
areas. This factor is important to question the current belief of the major difference
between urban, suburban, and rural areas in regard to mental health issues (Paykel
et al. 2020)[3].
Using spatial analysis and various machine learning methods, the aim is to develop
both an understanding of how the mental health status within the Greater Copen-
hagen area currently and try to predict the overall number of diagnoses within the
specific zones.

The method for this thesis will follow the data science pipeline meaning it can be

• The second task was to find all relevant data and connect it to the zones
• The third task was to clean and preprocess all the acquired data

This project changed over the course of the five months, as data from DST turned
out to be very diﬀicult to obtain. The original goal was to create a Graph Neural
Network using the zones as nodes. This was not possible as the data was delayed

hexagonal zoning scheme was not feasible, and a population-based zoning scheme was
needed. Furthermore, the DST data was only obtainable for 531 of the 626 zones and
therefore some of the variables are not linked to every zone.

conducted in the field of spatial analysis and mental health, as well as those employ-
ing machine learning techniques within the domain of mental health. Most of the
referenced studies primarily emphasize the impact of non-rural environments on men-
tal health outcomes. Additionally, numerous investigations adopt the methodology
of employing questionnaires or surveys to collect data pertaining to the prevailing
state of mental health within a given geographical area, which will not be used in
this project.

• Ethnicity of the area
• Socioeconomic status within the area
• Access to mental health facilities
• Environmental exposures such as air and noise pollution
• Access to greeneries and rural areas
• Build environment and surroundings
• Sentiment of geotagged social media
• Relevant modeling for spatial data

In (McLaughlin, et al. 2012)[6] researchers aimed to understand the relationship
between socioeconomic status (SES) and mental disorders in adolescents. They ex-

deprivation, subjective social status, and community-level income variation. The find-
ings revealed that subjective social status, which refers to how individuals perceive
their social standing, consistently showed the strongest association with mental dis-
orders among adolescents. Higher subjective social status was linked to lower risks of
mood, anxiety, substance, and behavior disorders.
It is also important to look at how SES indirectly affects mental health.(Oanh
L. Meyer et al. 2014)[7] shows that individuals with lower SES had greater concerns
about neighborhood safety, which in turn had a negative impact on their levels of
physical activity. This lack of physical activity was then associated with poorer men-
tal health. Although the overall model was similar across different racial/ethnic and
gender groups, the average levels of the constructs varied among these groups. Fur-
thermore, it is interesting to investigate how Work-life conflict correlates with SES
and its resulting effect on mental health. (Kim, Young-Mee and Cho, Sung-il. 2020)
[8] found that individuals who are more educated or have higher income levels may
face increased challenges in managing work-life conflict, and this, in turn, has a greater
impact on their mental well-being. This suggests that socioeconomic circumstances
can influence the way in which Work-life conflict affects mental health outcomes.

A crucial part of the project is examining the spatial layout of the zones and the
access to greeneries within the zones. Previous studies reveal that three measures of
green space access show associations with anxiety and mood disorder treatment counts

access to green space within a 3 km radius, as well as proximity to the nearest
useable green space, were associated with lower treatment counts. The proportion

treatment counts. However, access to green space within 300 meters did not exhibit
significant associations. (Thomas Astell-Burt and Xiaoqi Feng and Gregory S. Kolt.

individuals in greener neighborhoods were less sedentary. An interaction effect was
observed between physical activity and green space, indicating that the relationship
between green space and mental health is influenced by physical activity and another
stimulus of the body. Specifically, among individuals who were less physically active,
having more green space did not significantly benefit mental health. However, among
those who were more physically active, there was a protective association between
green space and mental health.
An important factor is also the built environment of the zones. For this study,
built environment refers to the land use of the zone and not the specific buildings
within the zone. (Kan et al, 2022)[17] showed that the impact of built-environment
quality on mental health is weak, with housing characteristics having a more direct
effect on mental health in communities with poorer housing conditions and an in-
direct effect in communities with better housing conditions. Furthermore, the built
environment can influence social interactions and community engagement, which are
also important factors when considering the mental health of a zone (Kostas Moura-
tidis, 2018)[18].

Overall, the paper concludes that each of the four ML methods has its own unique
strengths and limitations and that the choice of method depends on the specific prob-
lem being addressed and the characteristics of the data. The authors emphasize the
importance of careful data preprocessing, model selection, and validation to ensure
accurate and reliable results. They also suggest future research directions, such as the
integration of multiple ML methods, the development of hybrid models, and the incor-
poration of uncertainty analysis, of which the latter has a big impact when analysing

3.1 Zones
11
- LTM1. The hexagonal zoning scheme was preferred in the Cambridge and Lisbon
reports as it possessed several advantages such as uniformity in unit areas, reduc-
tion in sampling bias, and availability of data for each zone. Accordingly, hexagonal
zones were also created for the greater Copenhagen area with a distance of 500 me-
ters between the center of each zone 2, and with a flat top and six corners specified
using latitude and longitude coordinates. It should be noted that the hexagons in
the Copenhagen area were slightly modified by the script to ensure that the hexagons
shared borders, thereby enabling the merging of hexagons.
The criterion for each hexagon in this scheme was to contain at least 50 house-
holds3, thus necessitating the merging of hexagons to meet the required household
count. The data on the number of households in each zone was acquired through
the public data supply available at the Danish Datasupply API[25]. Furthermore,
the municipality and address ID for each address was also extracted, to exclude all
hexagons that are not part of the Greater Copenhagen Area. This approach enables
the exclusion of hexagons that do not meet the specified criteria and facilitates a
more accurate analysis of the data. Though if a hexagon had overlapping municipal-

3.5
Twitter data
Sentiment data in a zone analysis of mental health is relevant as it can provide insights
into the emotional and psychological well-being of individuals residing in a particular
area. This can provide valuable information about the prevalence of mental health
issues. Furthermore, the inclusion of sentiment data in a zone analysis of mental
health can help to identify patterns and trends that may be useful for targeted inter-
ventions aimed at improving mental health outcomes in affected populations.
The Twitter data in this study was collected using both the academic API and the
standard API by the DTU transport division. The primary objective of the Twitter
data is to conduct sentiment analysis and generate a score for each zone. All the
Twitter data used had a specific geo-location tagged in the tweet with latitude and
longitude coordinates. Each tweet underwent a series of cleaning steps that involved
the removal of links, non-character symbols such as emojis, and other irrelevant in-
formation.
The cleaned tweets were then assigned to their respective zones, and
sentiment analysis was applied to each tweet. Subsequently, each zone was assigned
a sentiment score using various libraries such as Textblob[28], the Vader lexicon[29],
and the AFINN lexicon[30]. Textblob employs machine learning techniques trained on
extensive data to classify text as positive, negative, or neutral in terms of sentiment.
Vader lexicon utilizes a predefined lexicon to assign polarity, positive or negative, and
intensity values, allowing for nuanced analysis. The AFINN lexicon contains a list
of words along with their corresponding sentiment scores. It is important to note
that the data is sparse for many of the zones, meaning that most of the zones have a
sentiment of zero and thereby likely decreasing the importance of this variable in the
study.

Modelling
The models will be developed for each year, with the zones being divided into separate
train and test sets using an 80-20 split. Specifically, 80% of the data will be allocated
for training, while the remaining 20% will be reserved for testing. This process will
be repeated for each year, considering the temporal variables, while the stationary
variables will be reused across all six models.
Following the data collection, processing, and interpretation stages, a comprehen-
sive statistical analysis was conducted to gain insights into the relationships between
the examined variables. To explore and identify the influential factors impacting the
target variable, regression analysis was employed. This analytical approach offers
a robust and dependable method for investigating the impact of multiple variables
simultaneously. Specifically two different regression techniques were used. The first
is LassoLarsCV (LL) (Eric Iturbide and Jaime Cerda and Mario Graff, 2019)[31], a
regression algorithm that combines the benefits of Lasso regression and Least Angle
Regression (LARS). Lasso regression introduces a penalty term to the linear regression
objective function, encouraging sparsity in the coeﬀicient estimates and performing
feature selection. LARS is a stepwise regression algorithm that adds predictors to
the model based on their correlations with the target variable.

SECTION 5
Results and Discussion
The results of the models discussed in section 3.10 are presented in this chapter. Fig-
ures and tables will be the foundation of the presentation of the models. To link the
results with the variables the most important variables for each model are also pre-
sented. The models were created using the sckit-learn package for python[32]. Prior
to training the models the unnecessary and redundant variables were removed, first
by removing all columns for which each row value was 0, and then by removing val-
ues such as the geometry of the zone and the text generated from the Twitter data.
The total number of columns went from 202 to 162, and thereby the models had 162
features used for the prediction.
5.1
Results
Before starting the modeling process, the predictor variables were checked for mul-
ticollinearity.
This was done to make sure that there weren’t strong correlations
between two or more independent variables. High correlations can make it diﬀicult
to determine which variables have an impact on the mental illness variable. (Aiken
et al. 1991)[40] emphasized the importance of this step in their research on multiple
regression analysis. Only variables within the same category showed multicollinear-
ity. That is, zones with many cars also tend to have more mopeds and vans. The
correlations can be seen in figure 4.17, 4.19 and 4.19
To optimize the model several tuning loops were applied, each iterating through
different values of the hyperparameters that can be tuned when training a RF model.
Figure 5.1 shows the plot of different models and their performance and the optimal
hyperparameters is marked by the red dot.

5.2
Discussion
As mental health issues and the underlying reasons behind it is a very complicated
topic, this study has many limitations associated with it. Even though the research
was restricted to the social factor of the Biopsychosocial Model, there are still many
variables within the social factor which was not considered. This includes variables
such as exercise, social engagement, unemployment, and many others. Accurately
predicting the number of mental health diagnoses is an extremely challenging task
due to the many variables involved, along with the presence of other influential fac-
tors. Achieving a prediction with 100% accuracy is not feasible.
The importance of both the land use variables and the POI and their relation with
mental health was not found to be significant in this study. This could be because
of the quality of the OSM data or because of the importance of some of the other
variables that are just more dominant. Specifically, it was not found that green areas,
such as parks, forests, and grass areas had a negative correlation with the mental
health variable. This was surprising as many studies indicate that populations living
near green areas usually have better mental health, as described in chapter 2.
Most of the data was either calculated as means across the zones or normalized
by the population counts across the zones. Another approach could have been to
normalize the data with the area of the zones instead. The trends and patterns could
potentially be significantly different. This approach would be especially important
to get a diverse view of the land use and POI variables.
The Twitter data turned out to have a very insignificant role, both regarding the
outcome of the models and when examining the correlation with the mental health
variable. As the data was very sparse it is hard to know if this is representative. To
expand, additional data could be added such as Instagram and Facebook data, as
both platforms also have the option to upload images with the post. Having images
added to the post would make it easier to specifically know if the sentiment in the
post is connected to the characteristics of the area in which the post was made from.

The mobility variables did not play a big role in the feature importance of either

SECTION 6
Conclusion
This study explored the association between different spatial and socioeconomic vari-
ables and mental health on a zone level in the Greater Copenhagen area. Several ma-
chine learning models were built to try and predict the number of diagnoses within a
zone. The correlation of the different variables was examined the results showed that
not all variables had significant importance in predicting the mental health variable.
While the socioeconomic variables, such as marital status and citizenship, had
the most significant impact on the models, the land use and points of interest (POI)
within each zone did not contribute significantly to the prediction outcome of the
models. The noise and air pollution had a higher correlation than most of the land
use and POI variables, but neither were factors in the modeling.
Patterns were observed across the zones with variables such as income and car
ownership showing clear trends across the zones. It is harder to spot patterns be-
tween the mental health variable and the other variables from the thematic maps,
but in general both the physical health diagnoses and the mental health seemed to
have an increase in the proximity of the city. As for the temporal aspect, there was
no significant increase in the zones over time, regarding mental health diagnoses.
The models showed good results with all of them reaching r2 scores above 75%
and the RMSE scores generally below 80 meaning that the model on average was
only 80 diagnoses off the actual count. Fine-tuning the models was crucial as the re-
sults were much worse when the model training was done with the default parameters.
To conclude, the results suggest that a specific emphasis on solving problems with
mental health should be focused on people not engaging in relationships and also a
focus on citizens born outside of Denmark. From the model result it was also possible
to estimate how many diagnoses there will be in a certain zone, with a slight error
margin.

SECTION 7
Future work
The main problem with the study was the lack of time due to the lack of data. There-
fore, many further models and explorations can be done with the data. Modeling
types such as Graph neural networks could be a promising way to further explore
mental health diagnoses and a way to explore the relationships between the zones.
Including the other factors from the Biopsychosocial Model would make it far more
complex, but also give a better estimate of what variables are the most important.
The RF models could be expanded as they work even with non-linear data. Fur-
thermore, other research techniques such as questionnaires or polls could be utilized.
This would perhaps be beneficial for areas such as the greeneries and POI, as the
questionnaires might provide more reliable data on the importance of those factors.
Another option to improve the land use and POI variables could be to attain them
from a nonpublic source, such as the google maps API. This would eliminate the
problem with the quality of the tags and the correctness of the currently obtained
data.
Other urban variables to consider could be the surrounding built environment
or further analysis of how mental health is affected by social media. As this study
only looked at sentiment data of questionable quality, a deeper dive could be done by
examining habits and content viewed on social media.
Lastly, as data were obtained from different years, the temporal aspect of analyzing
the change over the years could also be an interesting topic. In addition, the mental
and physical health categories had many subcategories as outlined in section ??.
These categories could be further explored to try and find patterns within specific
deceases and the zones
