Abstract
Retinoic acid (RA) plays an important role in a variety of immune functions. In the
thymus, it regulates the homeostasis of thymic epithelial cells and negative selection
of thymocytes. It is essential for secondary lymphoid organ development, and plays
an important role in inducing oral tolerance. It has been described to promote home-
ostasis and aid in inflammatory responses by mediating differentiation and function
of T cells. RA has also shown to play a suppressive role in RA is acquired through the
diet and binds to its receptor, which functions as a transcriptional regulator that is able
to activate and repress hundreds of genes. In this project the role of RA in develop-
ment of T cells and in type-3 infections was studied using a transgenic mouse model
that induces an inactivation of the retinoic acid receptor in all RORγt-expressing cells.
In the absence of RA signalling, the development of Vγ4- γδT17 cells in the thymus
was altered. Vγ4- γδT17 increased in numbers and persisted longer in the transgenic
mice than in the control mice. In this regard, RA seems to have a role in regulating
the development of Vγ4- γδT17 cells. Furthermore, an accumulation of single positive
CD8+ and CD4+ cells in the thymus was observed. At the same time, there was an
accumulation of IL-17 producing cells in that were mostly TCRβlow/ intermediate. A C.
rodentium experiment was performed, which showed an increased bacterial burden in
the absence of RA signalling in type 3 lymphocytes. However the analysis of tissue
samples was inconclusive.

Introduction
1.1
Retinoic acid
1.1.1
Source and signalling
Retinoic acid (RA) is a metabolite of vitamin A, named after its essential function in
producing pigments in the eye retina [1]. RA is acquired through the diet in the form
of retinyl esters from meat or β-carotene from plants [2], [3]. β-carotene is taken up
directly by enterocytes, where one molecule can be converted to two all-trans retinal
molecules by cleavage at the central C15-C15’ double bond [4]. Thereafter it undergoes
a reduction reaction to retinol and a subsequent esterification to be incorporated into
chylomicrons. β-carotene can also be packed into chylomicrons unmodified by the
enterocyte [5]. When retinyl esters are ingested, they are first hydrolyzed to retinol in
the lumen of the intestine by pancreatic lipase or at the microvilli of the enterocytes by
phospholipase B [6]. Retinol is then taken up by enterocytes, where it is reconverted
to retinyl ester by the enzyme lecithin retinol acyltransferase (LRAT) [5]. Retinyl es-
ters are subsequently packed into chylomicrons with triglycerides and cholesterol and
released into the lymphatic system [3].
In the circulation, chylomicrons undergo hydrolysis of triglyceride by the action of
lipoprotein lipase, and apolipoprotein E, converting them into chylomicron remnants
that are able to be captured by hepatocytes in the liver [5]. Most of the retinol in the
body is stored in hepatic stellate cells in the liver and is the reason why liver is the
most abundant animal food source of vitamin A [7], [8]. Retinyl esters are hydrolysed
back to retinol by lipid hydrolases and carboxyl esterases in hepatocytes before being
released into the circulation. [3].
Dendritic cells (DCs), macrophages, epithelial cells and stromal cells can synthesize
enzymes that degrade retinol to RA intracellularly [2], [9], [10]. When retinol enters
these cells, it is first oxidised to retinaldehyde by the cytosolic enzyme retinol de-
hydrogenase (RDH). In a subsequent step, retinaldehyde is oxidised to retinoic acid
by retinal dehydrogenase (RALDH) (Figure 1). The synthesis of the latter enzyme is
tightly regulated as the reaction is irreversible and the concentration of RA needs to
be controlled to maintain homeostasis [11]. The different metabolites of Vitamin A
are collectively called retinoids, and while retinaldehyde is the active chromophore of
vision, RA acts as a transcription factor binding ligand [12]. RA comes in different
isoforms, however all-trans RA is the physiologically most abundant form and is the
natural ligand for the retinoic acid receptor (RAR) [2], [3].
Due to the hydrophobic nature of retinoids, binding proteins are needed to carry them
across extracellular and intracellular compartments. Retinol binding protein (RBP)
and the serum protein transthyretin (TTR) transports retinol from the liver into the
circulation as well as the small intestine through bile [5], [13]. Retinol can enter cells

by diffusing through cell membranes or through a surface receptor called Stra6 that
enhances the transfer in cells that require large amounts [3]. Inside the cells, retinol
is carried by cellular retinol binding protein 1 and 2 (CRBP1 and CRBP2). CRBP2
is found exclusively in mucosal cells of the small intestine. It prevents oxidation of
retinol and promotes esterification by LRAT. CRBP1 is expressed in a variety of cells
and is found in all tissues where retinol oxidation takes place [3], [14]. CRBP1 can
also bind the oxidised form of retinol, namely retinaldehyde. When retinaldehyde is
further oxidised to RA, it will bind to the cellular retinoic acid binding proteins 1 or 2
(CRABP1 or CRABP2) (Figure 1). Although free RA can bind the RAR-RXR complex,
CRABP2 can enhance the binding by facilitating the nuclear transfer [3].

RA has the ability to initiate transcription of hundreds of genes by binding to retinoic
acid receptors (RAR), which can function as nuclear transcription factors. Three RAR
paralogs exist, namely RARα, RARβ and RARγ [15]. These form heterodimers with
the retinoid X receptors (RXR), RXRα, RXRβ and RXRγ, forming a complex that func-
tions as a ligand-activated transcriptional regulator that recognizes and binds RA re-
sponsive elements (RARE) found in the promoter region of target genes [3], [15] (Fig-
ure 1). The tree RAR heterodimerize in different combinations with the three RXR,
but their ligand binding properties do not change [3]. The N-terminal domain of
RARs, which is variable, affects gene regulation, and includes phosphorylation sites
for cyclin-dependent kinases and mitogen-activated protein kinases [3], [15]. The lig-
and binding domain and the DNA-binding domain, which interacts with the RARE,
2
==PAGE:9==
are highly conserved among RARs [15], [16]. The ligand binding domain is also the
site for RXR heterodimerization [17]. The RARE are motifs composed of two hexam-
eric repeats separated by a number of nucleotides. The number of nucleotides affects
recognition by the different RAR/RXR heterodimer combinations [3]. The RAR can
bind either upstream or downstream of the target gene and can therefore either re-
press or activate genes [3], [15].
In the absence of RA, the RAR-RXR complex recruits the corepressors, nuclear receptor
corepressor (NCOR) and silencing mediator of retinoic acid and thyroid hormone re-
ceptor (SMRT) [3], [18]. These corepressors then promote the recruitment of other fac-
tors, such as histone deacetylases (HDAC) that induce chromatin condensation in the
region, preventing transcription [15] (Figure 2). Upon RA binding, the ligand-binding
domain of RAR undergoes a conformational change allowing release of corepressor
complexes and the binding of coactivators, such as histone acetyl transferases (HAT)
and methyltransferases. This will ultimately lead to chromatin decondensation and
ability to transcribe target genes [3], [15], [18]. Degradation of RA inside the cells is
catalysed by Cyp26 enzymes, mediated by CBRP1, which are upregulated by excess
RA levels to keep the concentration within a narrow range [15], [18] (Figure 2).

1.1.2
Retinoic acid in the immune system
In addition to its numerous roles in embryonic development, hormone function, re-
production and vision, RA is an important molecule for the immune system. Vitamin
3
==PAGE:10==
A deficiency, especially in childhood, is associated with increased morbidity and mor-
tality from infectious diseases [2].
Thymocyte development
The interactions between thymic epithelial cells (TEC) and developing thymocytes are
required for generating functional T cells in the thymus [19]. Cortical TEC (cTEC)
drive the development of DN to DP thymocytes and the positive selection of peptide-
MHC recognition by the TCR [20]. Medullary TEC (mTEC) express self-antigens and
are responsible for negative selection of self-reactive T cells and induction of regula-
tory T cells (Treg) [21]. RA has shown to regulate the homeostasis of TEC in vivo.
In the absence of RA signalling, cTEC proliferation increases while mTEC drecreases.
This, in turn, impacts T cell development by reducing generation of SP thymocytes
[22]. Activation of the RARγ-RXR complex has been shown to induce apoptosis due
to neglect, in DP thymocytes [23].RA can also have the opposite effect, by rescuing
self-reactive thymocytes, mediated by RARα [24], [25]. RA signalling has therefore
shown to play a role in negative selection of developing DP thymocytes [12].
SLO formation
RA is essential for the initial phase of secondary lymphoid organ (SLO) formation [12],
[26]. During embryonic development, RA signalling through RARβ induces stromal
cells to produce chemokine CXCL13 to attract lymphoid tissue inducer cells (LTi). This
localisation of LTi cells in the lymph node anlagen allows them to form clusters from
which lymph nodes develop [27]. RA also induces expression of RORγt in Lti precur-
sors which allows their differentiation into mature Lti cells that can colonise the LN
and Peyer’s patch anlagen [28]. Consequently, RORγt knockout mice do not develop
LNs or Peyer’s Patches [29]. The offspring of pregnant mice that were put on a vitamin
A deficient (VAD) diet, have smaller and fewer SLOs [28]. RA is therefore important
for normal SLO development.
Homing molecule induction
RA mainly produced by migratory DCs from the GI tract and GALT induce the ex-
pression of the gut homing molecules, C-C chemokine receptor 9 (CCR9) and α4β7
integrin, on T cells, B cells, as well as type 1 and 3 innate lymphoid cells (ILC1 and
ILC3) [30]–[32]. Stromal cells in the mesenteric lymph nodes (mLN) also produce
RA, contributing to the gut homing molecule induction [33], [34]. The α4β7 ligand,
mucosal addressin cell adhesion molecule-1 (MAdCAM-1), is expressed on large and
small intestinal edothelial cells, while the CCR9 ligand, CCL25, is only expressed in
the small intestine within the gut environment [35], [36]. In the bone barrow, RA
signalling also induces expression of α4β7 in DC precursors for gut-tropism [37]. In
the intestine, RA itself induces DCs to upregulate the RALDH enzymes necessary for
retinoid conversion, generating a positive feedback loop that allows them to influence
T cell differentiation and imprint gut homing molecules on lymphocytes [38]. In VAD
mice, Th17 that are present in the intestine at homeostasis are lost [39].
T and B cell differentiation and function
RA has a pleiotropic role in maintaining the balance between inflammatory and reg-
4
==PAGE:11==
ulatory immune responses. Its role in inducing effector funtions in T cells is largely
dependent on the cytokines and signals in the microenvironment. [18], [40].
RA is known as an essential molecule for maintaining and promoting oral tolerance,
by driving the development of inducible regulatory T cells (Treg) at steady state [18].
Intestinal DCs induce conversion of naïve CD4+ T cells into Treg in the presence of RA
and transforming growth factor (TGF)-β in vitro [41]. T helper 17 (Th17) differentiation
is also dependent on TGF-β but requires the presence of proinflammatory cytokines
such as interleukin (IL) 6 [42]. Th17 differentiation is negatively regulated by RA,
even in the presence of TGF-β and IL-6, by inhibiting IL-6 receptor (IL-6R) expression
in naïve T cells in vitro, thereby shifting the balance to Treg differentiation [43]–[45].
Furthermore, RA signalling through RARα suppresses production of the cytokines
IL-4, IL-21 and interferon-γ (IFN-γ) in effector and memory T cells, that inhibit Treg
conversion, thereby ensuring a tolerogenic T cell response [46]. In the presence of a
RAR antagonist, mLN DCs improve their Th17 differentiation capacity and reduce
Treg differentiation in vitro [44]. In a murine modedl of Crohns disease, vitamin A
supplementation promotes Treg development and improves intestinal inflammation
[47]. Vitamin A deficient (VAD) mice have impaired oral tolerance, which underlines
its importance in maintenance of homeostasis [48].
Under inflammatory conditions, RA is required for effective immune responses. In
VAD mice, the differentiation of effector T helper cells is impaired, as well as their
production of IL-17 and IFN-γ [39]. In vitro cultures have shown that RA can enhance
IFN-γ production in T cells when co-cultured with IL-12, IL4 or IFN-γ, but reduced
production when cultured without these cytokines, which indicates that RA can act
as an adjuvant for expression of certain cytokines [49]. RA in combination with IL-15,
can also induce the production of proinflammatory cytokines IL-12 and IL-23 by DCs,
promoting the differentiation of effector Th1 cells, or Th17 if in the presence of IL-6,
and reducing Treg induction [50]. RA has also shown to stabilise Th1 commitment and
prevent transition to a Th17 fate [51].Furthermore, RA has also been shown to enhance
Th2 development while supressing Th1 differentiation in vitro and in vivo [52], [53].
The effect that RA has on the Th1/Th2 balance is mainly induced by regulation of cy-
tokines produced by APCs, and may explain why studies show contrasting functions
of promoting and suppressing Th1 and Th2 differentiation under different conditions
[18].
In conclusion, RA has an important role in inducing T cell responses both at steady-
state and under inflammatory conditions. However, its effect is entirely dependent on
cytokines and other microenvironmental factors [40].
RA has also proven to be important for B cell class-switching. DC-derived RA in the
gut, in combination with IL-15 and IL-16, can induce IgA class switching in B cells,
which is important for effective humoral responses to control the microbiota and main-
tain homeostasis [54].

1.2
Type 3 lymphocytes
Lymphocytes can be classified into three types based on the type of pathogen they
exhibit an immune response against. Type 1 lymphocytes specialize in fighting viral
and intracellular bacterial infections, while type 2 are associated with parasitic infec-
tions. Type 3 lymphocytes, which are the focus of this project, are important in battling
extracellular bacterial and fungal infections [55]. They are characterized by their pro-
duction of cytokines interleukin (IL)-17A, IL-17F and IL-22. IL-17 and IL-22 expression
is directly linked to the transcription factor (TF), retinoic acid related orphan receptor
γt (RORγt), which is a key TF used to identify these cells [56]. The main lymphocytes
in this category are T helper 17 (Th17) cells, γδT17 cells, mucosal-associated invariant
T17 (MAIT17) cells, natural killer T17 (NKT17) cells and innate lymphoid cells 3 (ILC3)
[57], [58].
1.2.1
Th17
T cells originate from hematopoietic stem cells (HSC) from the fetal liver before birth
or from the bone marrow after birth. HSCs give rise to lymphoid progenitors, that en-
ter the thymus and gradually become committed to the T cell lineage [59]. Recruitment
of progenitors into the thymus, relies mainly on P-selectin and chemokine receptors 9
and 7 (CCR9 and CCR7) [60]. In the thymus, T cell development is divided into three
types of stages based on their expression of CD4 and CD8: the double negative (DN)
stages, DN1, DN2, DN3 and DN4, where they are not expressing CD4 nor CD8; the
double positive (DP) stage, where they are CD4+CD8+ ;and single positive (SP) stage,
where they express either CD4 or CD8 [59]. RORγt is first expressed in DP thymocytes
and is required for their survival while its downregulation is required for maturation
into SP thymocytes [61]. SP thymocytes exit the thymus as naïve T cells and circulate
continuously through the lymphatic system until they become activated by antigen
presenting cells (APC) [62]. Cytokines and other microenvironmental factors deter-
mine their differentiation into specific subsets.
Cytokines that promote T helper 17 (Th17) differentiation are IL-6, IL-21, IL-23, and
TGF-β, while other cytokines such as IL-2, IL-4, IFN-γ, and IL-27, inhibit their dif-
ferentiation. RORγt is an essential transcription factor in the differentiation of these
cells [63]. TGF-β and IL-6 are required at the time of priming. This is followed by
IL-21–driven amplification, where Th17 cells will produce IL-21 that signals in an au-
tocrine manner, amplifying its own expression and upregulating IL23R. A final step
is IL-23–driven stabilization [64]. The combination of TCR stimulation and IL-6/TGF-
β exposure induces IL-23 receptor expression and a STAT3-dependent expression of
RORγt. This enables them to acquire an effector function of producing of IL-17A, IL-
17F, IL-21, and IL-22 [63]. At the same time, they will express chemokine receptors,
such as CCR4 and CCR6 that facilitate their migration to inflammatory sites in the
mucosa, specifically the intestines, lung, and skin [64].

1.2.2
γδT17 cells
γδ T cells are a small subset of self-renewing T cells generated in the thymus that have
an innate-like function [65], [66]. As opposed to the conventional αβ T cells, they do
not require major histocompatibility complex (MHC) presentation of peptide epitopes
for activation and they leave the thymus as functional effector cells [67]. When they
leave the thymus, they take up residence in different tissues, particularly at barrier
sites where they release cytokines to provide protection against invading pathogens
[68]. Unlike αβ T cells that require peptide presentation on MHC molecules, they can
recognize antigen directly through the T cell receptor (TCR). However, they are able
to bind ligands presented by the non-classical MHC Class Ib on infected or damaged
cells. Many of the ligands recognized by γδ T cells, that have been identified, are asso-
ciated with cellular stress or damage. Additional ligands include heat-shock proteins,
nucleotides, phospholipids and mycobacterial lipids [55].
As thymocytes progress to the DN2 stage, the β, γ and δ TCR starts to undergo rear-
rangement and by DN3, in the case of a successful rearrangement, the cells are either
expressing TCRγδ, or TCRβ associated with the surrogate α chain (pTα), termed pre-
TCR [68]. At DN3, the point of divergence between the two T cell types, the TCRβ
selected cells move on to the DP stage, undergo further selections to become SP and
finally leave the the thymus as naïve CD4+ or CD8+ T cells [55]. On the other hand,
the successfully rearranged γδ T cells, remain CD4-CD8- and mature into effector γδ
T cells that leave the thymus and home to different tissues [68].
In the mouse, following successful TCRγδ rearrangement, development of γδ T cells
occurs in a series of time-restricted waves defined by the γ-chain variable regions
(Vγ), which correlate with a specific tissue homing pattern as well as cytokine pro-
duction [68] (Table 1). In the first developmental wave, beginning at embryonic day
13.5 (E13.5) and ending at E16, Vγ5δ1 T cells develop by positive selection following
interactions with the thymic expressed Skint-1 molecule [69], [70] (nomenclature by
Heilig and Tonegawa [71]). Vγ5δ1 T cells exclusively home to the epidermis of the
skin to become tissue-resident IFN-γ producing dendritic epidermal T cells (DETCs)
[72]. The current understanding is that γδ T cells that are not selected on Skint-1 have
two fates: 1) to become IL-17-producing (γδT17) or IFN-γ-producing (γδT1). This
depends on the strength of the TCR signal and successful expression of certain tran-
scription factors [68]. Thus, a strong TCR signal will induce γδT1 cells, whereas weak
TCR signals promote γδT17 development [73]–[75]. This process occurs in two major
waves, one that gives rise to Vγ6 γδT17 cells and one that gives rise to Vγ4 γδT17
cells. Vγ6 development lasts approximately from E16 until birth, producing Vγ6δ1
cells that home to mucosal epithelial sites. The next wave, from E18, includes the
emergence of Vγ4+ and Vγ1+ cells, which continue developing after birth and into
adulthood. These γ-chains can pair up with different δ-chains, increasing their TCR
variability [68].

molecules [68]. While a complete picture of the transcriptional programs that govern

[84].
The transcription factor c-Maf, has been identified to be required by all γδT17 cells.
c-Maf activates chromatin accessibility to promote expression of Rorc, Blk and Il17a
(encoding IL-17) while at the same time antagonizing negative regulators such as TCF1
and Lef1 [75]. Deletion of cMAF resulted in complete loss of γδT17 cells and did
not affect other type 3 lymphocytes [75]. Moreover, cMAF expression is reduced by
strong TCR signalling, which provides a link between weak TCR signals and γδT17
commitment factors [75].
Although the two main γδ T cell subtypes are Vγ4+ and Vγ6+, a small portion of
Vγ1+ cells have the capacity of IL-17 production, but they are mostly associated with
the type 1 IFN-γ producing subtype [85]–[87]. In addition to expressing RORγt, γδT17
cells can be identified by the expression of chemokine receptor CCR6, high levels of
adhesion molecule CD44 and a lack of CD27 [88], [89]. IL-1β and IL-23 stimulate pro-
duction of IL-17, IL-21, and IL-22 in γδT17 cells [90]. γδT17 cells have also been shown
to expand and proliferate in response to IL-7 signalling in vitro and in vivo [79]. γδT17
cells are primarily found in lymphoid tissues and mucosal sites. In the GI tract, γδT17
cells are distributed along the intestinal lamina propria, and are also found in innate
lymphoid follicles and mesenteric lymph nodes [91], [92]. After acute intestinal injury,
8
==PAGE:15==
γδT17 cells are the primary producers of IL-17 and protect the intestinal epithelium by
limiting permeability and maintaining barrier integrity [93].
Several mouse models have demonstrated that γδT17 cells can dominate IL-17 produc-
tion during the initial stages of bacterial and fungal infections [85]. In mice infected
with Mycobacterium tuberculosis, γδT17 cells, stimulated by IL-23-producing infected
DCs, provided a first-line of defense through secretion of IL-17 [94]. Similar effects
were found in Escherichia coli infected mice [95]. Oral infection with Listeria monocyto-
genes elicited the development of Vγ6 γδT17 cells, that were simultaneously produc-
ing IL-17 and IFN-γ in the intestinal mucosa. Upon reinfection, these cells expanded
in a memory-like manner, and provided enhanced protection against the pathogen,
showing an adaptive response [92]. High levels of γδT17 have been found in the cen-
tral nervous system of mice with the inducible multiple sclerosis model, experimental
autoimmune encephalomyelitis (EAE). Their IL-17 production amplifies the Th17 re-
sponse, and consequently TCRδ-knockout mice have less severe EAE pathology [90].
γδT17 cells are therefore an important component of mounting type 3 responses as
well as being inflammatory in autoimmune conditions.
1.2.3
Other type 3 lymphocytes
In the thymus there are two other small T cell subsets, besides Th17 and γδT17, that
can develop an IL-17 producing phenotype, namely MAIT17 and NKT17. MAIT17
and NKT17 cells are described as innate-like unconventional αβ T cells that have
a limited TCR variability[96]. NKT17 recognize glycolipids presented by CD1d, an
MHC class I-like molecule, through their semi-invariant TCR [97]. They can be po-
tent producers of IL-17 and are most abundant in the lung and lymph nodes [98], [99].
They have been found to contribute to IL-17 production for neutrophil recruitment
in bacterial pulmonary infection with Streptococcus pneumoniae, and to play a role in
certain asthma and liver failure models[100]. MAIT17 cells participate in antibacte-
rial immunity by recognizing antigens, such as riboflavin intermediates, presented by
major-histocompatibility-complex-class-I-related protein MR1. They have been found
in barrier sites, lymphoid tissues and liver [96]. MAIT cells are are mainly CD4CD8 or
CD8+, while some are CD4+ [101].
ILC3 are bone marrow derived cells that exhibit similar functions to type 3 T cells.
They produce IL-22, IL-17 and granulocyte macrophage-colony stimulating factor (GM-
CSF) [102]. They are important for homeostatic maintenance of epithelial barrier sites,
particularly in the intestine. They can be divided into two types, NCR+, which are pre-
dominantly IL-22 producers and NCR-, which generate more IL-17 [103]. Although,
ILC3 have a protective effect at steady state, they have been implicated in inflamma-
tory bowel diseases when inappropriate activation triggers excessive cytokine produc-
tion, leading to an inflammatory state [103].

1.3
Retinoic acid in type 3 immunity
RA plays an important role in mediating type 3 immune responses. Under inflam-
matory conditions, the RA-driven Treg induction is counteracted by IL-1, shifting the
Treg/Th17 balance to induce Th17 differentiation instead [18]. IL-1 signalling can in-
hibit SOCS3 expression, which acts as a feedback inhibitor of STAT3, a transcription
factor essential for Th17 differentiation. This results in amplified STAT3 phosphory-
lation induced by Th17-promoting cytokines[104]. The concentration of RA has also
been shown to be an important factor for Th17 differentiation in vitro, where physio-
logical levels promoted its development while high concentrations inhibited it [105].
Interestingly, a study on VAD mice observed a significant decrease in Th17 differenti-
ation. However the same study also observed an altered gut microbiota and morpho-
logical changes in intestinal epithelial cells, which would indicate that these factors
contribute to T cell differentiation as well [106].
Just as for LTi cells, which are a type of embryonic ILC3, RA signalling is needed for
maturation and expression of RORγt in ILC3. At steady state, GM-CSF produced by
ILC3s promotes RA and IL-10 secretion by DCs and macrophages, further enhancing
Treg induction [107]. Inhibition of RA signalling by treatment with RA inhibitors dur-
ing a C. rodentium infection in Rag-/- mice, that rely on cytokine-production by ILC3 as
they lack of T cells, resulted in impaired IL-22 production and consequently impaired
infection clearance [108].
In vitro, RA supplemented with IL-1 or IL-18 and IL-23, has shown to significantly
enhance IL-22 production by γδ T cells. Chromatin immunoprecipitation assays have
shown that RARα binds directly to the IL-22 gene promoter in γδ T cells [108]. In
vivo, RA-induced IL-22 also increased antimicrobial peptide production in the intes-
tine [108] RA in vivo suppresses pathogenic γδ T cell responses in the context of EAE
[109]. In summary, RA can have both suppressive and inductive effects for type 3
lymphocyte generation and function.
1.3.1
Citrobacter rodentium as a tool to study type 3 immune responses
Citrobacter rodentium is a gram-negative bacterium that acts as a murine intestinal
pathogen. It mimics enteropathogenic Escherichia coli (EPEC) and enterohemorragic
Escherichia coli (EHEC) infection in humans and is therefore a widely used murine
model to study these in in vivo [110]. C. rodentium is a pathogen that affects the large
intestine. When mice are infected orally, the bacterial colonization begins in the ce-
cum, descending toward the distal colon [111]. During the initial stages of infection,
IL-22 and IL-17 produced mainly by ILC3, are important for inducing an appropriate
immune response. When the adaptive immune response develops, CD4+ T cells be-
come the major IL-17 and IL-22 producers, and also produce IFN-γ as the infection
progresses [110], [111].
Snyder, McDaniel, Tian, et al. [112] performed a C. rodentium study on Vitamin A de-
ficient (VAD) mice as well as on on mice with a negative dominant mutation on T
cells (T-dnRAR). The dn mutation rendered the RAR inactive in T cells, preventing
10
==PAGE:17==
RA signalling. The VAD were not able to clear the pathogen and developed a chronic
infection. Upon receiving a single dose of RA, the infection was cleared, and an induc-
tion of IL-17 expression was observed. The T-dnRAR mice had high IL-17 expression
in T cells but only 40% of mice were able to clear the infection. This study concluded
that T cells, macrophages and neutrophils are required for C. rodentium clearance [112].
1.4
Project aim
The first aim of this master thesis project is to study the effects of RA in thymic devel-
opment of RORγt+ cells, with a particular focus on γδ T cells. This project is a contin-
uation of the investigation of the phenotype observed by Agerholm, Rizk, Kadekar, et
al. [113] in the RARdn mouse model described below. This study found a change in
the development of the Vγ4- γδT17 cells. Vγ4- cells increased significantly in num-
bers, while Vγ4+ cells did not, indicating a Vγ-chain specific regulation by RA [113].
The RARdn trasngene was also expressed in SP CD4+ thymocytes, but at lower levels
than in the γδT17 cells.
This projects’ goal was to study the development of γδT17 cells in the neonatal time-
line at three time points, day 1, week 3 and 5, both in the developmental stage in the
thymus and after their exit into the periphery. Other RORγt+ cell subsets were also
studied to see if lack of RA signalling can affect these cells.
The second aim in this project is to evaluate how the lack of RA signalling in type
3 lymphocytes affects the ability to clear an enteropathogenic infection, using the C.
rodentium model. Due to the importance of RA in the differentiation and function
of type 3 lymphocytes, as well as its role in inducing gut homing molecules, it was
hypothesized that the RARdn mice will have an impaired ability to clear the infection.
1.4.1
Murine model
To study the effect of RAR signalling in type 3 lymphocytes, a transgenic mouse model
generated by Cre-Lox recombination was used in the experimental part of this project.
This model has been achieved by crossing two types of genetically modified mice,
RORγtCRE with an insertion of Cre recombinase in the promoter region of the gene
coding for RORγt, and RARdnF/F with an insertion/ flanked by of two LoxP sites on
both sides of the stop codon of a dominant negative(dn) RAR transgene which has
been inserted into the ROSA26 locus Figure 3.
The ROSA26 locus is an easy to manipulate genetic segment that has not been found
to code for genes that play a role in any function. It opens up during embryogenesis
and is expressed on all cells. When a stop codon is placed in front of the inserted
gene, the gene will only be transcribed when cre recombinase is expressed and cleaves
the DNA at the LoxP sites of the stop codon. Once the stop codon is deleted the
transgene will be constitutively transcribed in the cell. A dn mutation is a mutation
that prevents the expressed protein from functioning properly. In this case, the RARdn
11
==PAGE:18==
is the human RARα gene that is trunkated at the ligand-dependent activation domain
at the N-terminal [114].
When these mice were crossed, RORγtCRE-RARdnF/F mice were generated, where all
RORγt expressing cells will concomitantly express Cre recombinase. The Cre recombi-
nase will cleave the off the floxed STOP codon of the RARdn gene, allowing constitu-
tive expression. The RARdn will bind to the RXR ligand, preventing the endogenous
RAR to bind and function as a binding site for RA. This results in an inactive RAR in
all RORγt expressing cells (Figure 3).
As RORγt is expressed not only in effector type 3 lymphocytes but also in DP thymo-
cytes during thymic development, all conventional αβT cells, both naïve and mature,
will have blocked RA-dependent RAR signalling.

2.3
Citrobacter rodentium infection
A cryovial of C. rodentium glycerol stock solution (company, conc) was thawed at room
temperature for 30 seconds and the surface was scraped with a sterile loop. A vial of
4ml sterile Luria Broth (LB) was inoculated with the loop and incubated overnight
at 37◦C at 400rpm. The next day, the enriched culture was transferred to 100ml of
sterile LB and incubated for 3 hours until the solution reached an OD of 1.417. The
cell suspension was then centrifuged at 3500rpm for 12 minutes and resuspended in
sterile LB, to reach a concentration of 2x1010 CFU/ml. The following formula was
used to calculate the concentration: CFU/ml=(5x108)(OD)-3x107.
4 cre+ and 4 cre- mice were infected with 2x109 CFU C. rodentium in a volume of 100µL.
2 cre+ and 2 cre- litter-mate controls were inoculated with 100µL sterile LB. The weight
of the mice was monitored daily. At day 11 of the experiment, the mice were sacrificed
and the mLNs and colon were harvested. The faeces were collected in eppendorf tubes
and weighed. 1ml of sterile DPBS was added to the tubes and they were incubated at
37◦C, with intermittent vortexing, until homogenized. A 96-well plate was prepared
with 180µL of sterile DPBS in each well. 20µL of the homogenized fecal solutions was
added to wells and 10x serial dilutions were prepared, from 10−1 to 10−6. 100µL of
each dilution was plated on oxoid medium plates and incubated overnight at 37◦C.
Coliform CFUs were counted the following day and the final CFU in the samples was
calculated based on the dilution and the weight of the fecal matter.

Discussion
The role of RA in the immune system can be either immunomodulating or inflamma-
tory depending on the condition, RA concentration, cytokine production by different
cells and other factors that are present in the environment [18]. RA’s role in promot-
ing and repressing differentiation and function in conventional T cells has been re-
searched extensively [40]. Furthermore, the function of γδT17 cells has been shown
to be affected by RA [109], [121]. However little is know about the role of RA in the
development of γδT17 cells in the thymus, which was addressed in this project. As the
emergence of this IL-17 producing subset of γδ T cells is restricted to the embryonic
and neonatal timeframe, the number of γδT17 cells was highest at the first analysed
timepoint, day 1 after birth, which was followed by a steep decrease in the following
two timepoints, at week 3 and 5. At day 1, >90% of γδT17 cells were Vγ4-.
In the RORγtCRE-RARdnF/F mouse model, where RAR signalling is impaired once
the cell starts expressing RORγt, at which point the commitment to the γδT17 lineage
has started, several differences were detected when compared to Cre- controls. In the
thymus, the number of Vγ4- γδT17 cells was significantly higher than in Cre- controls.
This observation could be explained by two possible reasons: 1) the differentiation
into a Vγ4- γδT17 may be regulated by RA signals in the thymus, 2) that the thymic
egress mechanism is impaired in these cells and they accumulate. RA may act as an in-
hibitory factor that puts a halt on the Vγ4- developmental wave early in neonatal life,
allowing the subsequent wave of Vγ4+ cells to develop normally. In the absence of RA
signalling, the inhibitory mechanisms that stop Vγ4- differentiation could be delayed
until a time point between week 3 and 5. The simultaneous decrease in number of Vγ4
γδT17 cells in both the thymus and pLNs, could be the result of the prolongation or
delay of the Vγ6 wave. The observation that Vγ4- cells numbers are not higher in Cre+
mice in the pLN could be brought by a defect caused by lack of RA signalling that in-
duces cell death in the periphery or that the homing capacity to peripheral lymphoid
tissues is impaired.
DP thymocytes express RORγt transiently [61] and are therefore expressing the trans-
gene, although it may not be as efficient as for γδT17 cells [113].In the thymus of
RORγtCRE-RARdnF/F mice, a significantly increased number of SP thymocytes was
detected, along with a decrease in the pLN, and it was most significant in CD8+ SP
cells. For this observation, there could be several explanations. One is based on that
RA plays a role in inducing negative selection of DP thymocytes with non-reactive
TCRs [23] and that this ability could be impaired by the expression of the RARdn
gene, leading to an increase in thymocytes that escape negative selection. Another
viewpoint is that the increased number of SP thymocytes and of γδT17 cells in the
transgenic mice may be caused by the same mechanism. Whether this mechanism
has to do with cell migration or simply to control normal development, remains to be
investigated.
The increased expression of IL-17 in SP and DN thymocytes of RORγtCRE-RARdnF/F
36
==PAGE:43==
mice was interesting to see. Because these cells were not further characterized in this
project, several theories can be developed to explain the observation. The IL-17 pro-
ducers may be effector T cells that have either become activated in the thymus or have
migrated from the periphery. However, most of the TCRβ+ IL-17+ cells did fall within
the TCRβlow/ intermediate region, which would indicate that they could be unconven-
tional αβ T cells such as MAIT and NKT [119], [120]. Nevertheless, further research
needs to be done to identify this IL-17 producing subset, or subsets, and to investigate
the role that impaired RA signalling has on their accumulation in the thymus.
Finally, to investigate how RORγtCRE-RARdnF/F mice respond to an infection that
requires a robust type 3 immune response, the C. rodentium model was used. The
transgenic mice had a substantially higher bacterial burden than Cre- infected mice,
although they did not show any weight loss that would indicate impaired pathogen
clearance. In the samples taken from the colon, the resulting data was difficult to anal-
yse due to ineffective staining and low detectable cell numbers. In the mLN however,
the number of Th17 cells, γδT17 cells and ILC3s were higher in the transgenic mice.
Moreover, Th17 cells, but not ILC3s had lower expression of CCR9. The only differ-
ences detected in cytokine expression in the mLN was a reduced IL-17 expression in
Th17 cells, and an increased IL-22 production by ILC3s. Seeing the accumulation of
type 3 lymphocytes in the mLN, but not the colon, and the higher bacterial burden
it could be hypothesized that lack of infectino clearance could be impaired to some
extent. However, this experiment needs to be repeated before any conclusions can be
made.
