Abstract
In an era marked by the escalating concern over climate change and environmental
degradation, corporations worldwide are increasingly acknowledging their important
role in mitigating negative environmental impacts. The transport sector, responsible
for a significant share of global CO2 emissions, has emerged as a critical focus area
for sustainability efforts. This thesis delves into the realm of green maritime corridors,
expanding the conventional understanding to encompass not only ships but also the
surrounding infrastructure. As a case study, the Port of Aarhus takes center stage,
with particular attention given to the transportation company, Dania Connect A/S, a key
component of the portãs infrastructure.
The study evaluates two alternatives within the context of Dania Connect A/S, conôÙ
sidering the replacement of their current internal combustion engine trucks with batôÙ
tery electric trucks. The assessment employs a Total Cost of Ownership analysis and
a MultiôÙCriteria Decision Analysis framework, incorporating four overarching criteriaëƒ
Economical, Technological, Environmental, and Social along with seven correspondôÙ
ing subôÙcriteria.
The Total Cost of Ownership analysis reveals that battery electric trucks outperform
their internal combustion engine counterparts over a sixôÙyear period, potentially yielding
savings of 0.7 million DKK per vehicle. Moreover, the MultiôÙCriteria Decision Analysis
underscores the viability of battery electric trucks when aggregating benefits across
the specified criteria, including a potential reduction of 63% in CO2e emissions over the
lifeôÙcycle.
Nevertheless, the study also acknowledges the challenges associated with the adopôÙ
tion of battery electric trucks, particularly concerning current technological limitations.
It raises questions about the feasibility of Dania Connect A/S electrifying their entire
fleet at present, highlighting the need for continued advancements in electric vehicle
technology and infrastructure.

Introduction
In todayãs interconnected world, the movement of goods across the globe is essenôÙ
tial to sustaining our global economy. This intricate web of transportation networks,
employing trucks, planes, ships, and trains, facilitates the delivery of billions of tons
of cargo annually which supports global growth. However, the transport sector has a
large contribution to the global emission of carbon dioxide (CO2).
The freight sectorãs carbon footprint is a matter of global concern, accounting for apôÙ
proximately 8% of the worldãs total greenhouse gas emissions. If the perspective is
broadened to include the emissions stemming from warehouses and ports, the figure
climbs to as much as 11%. The pressing nature of this problem is further underscored
by the projected tripling of global demand for freight transportation by 2050, particuôÙ
larly in growing economies across Asia, Africa, and Latin America. Unfortunately, this
growth is expected to double the greenhouse gas emissions attributed to freight transôÙ
portation [1].
One of the main challenges is the heavy reliance that the sector has on oil and gas,
despite other energy sectors making strides in reducing fossil fuel consumption. BarôÙ
ring transformative action, business as usual will lead to an alarming outcome: freight
transportation may well become the highestôÙemitting sector by 2050.
To fully comprehend the issue, it is imperative to dissect the sources of emissions
within the realm of freight transportation. While oceanôÙgoing ships handle nearly threeôÙ
quarters of the worldãs cargo, it is road vehicles, including trucks and vans, that conôÙ
tributed the majority of 74% of emissions within the freight sector [2]. Ships, although
they often burn fossil fuels and emit carbon, are relatively efficient in carrying large
volumes of cargo simultaneously [1]. In stark contrast, road freight can emit over 15
times more CO2 than ships while transporting the same amount of cargo over the same
distance [3]. Notably, road transport is experiencing rapid growth, with approximately
80% of the global increase in diesel consumption attributed to trucks. This growth is
partly fueled by trends like eôÙcommerce and home delivery [1].
However, greenhouse gasses are not the sole climate concern associated with freight

1.1
Green Maritime Corridors
This paper will focus on the operations and stakeholder linked to the shipping area of
the transport sector as it is estimated that 80% of global trade by volume and 70% by

The following section will provide a brief overview of the operations and challenges that

1.1.1
Quayside operations
The main operations at the quayside are related to the loading and unloading of the
ships, which is often associated with the quay cranes carrying cargo from the vessel at
the quay to distribution vehicles. This operation has been heavily researched as it is
found to be an area where there are possibilities for both increased energy efficiency
and decreased emissions. Study shows that vessels account for 27% of the total port
emissions [11]. Another study developed a mathematical formula that determines the
optimal number of quay cranes for the unloading of the vessels in order to minimize the
CO2ôÙemissions [12].
Another wellôÙknown and studied quay side challenge is the berth assignment problem,
which seeks to minimize the time that an arriving vessel is waiting before it is being
unloaded. Hence the berth assignment problem is mainly related to reducing the seaôÙ
side emissions. Numerous studies have developed different formulations of the berth
assignment problem [13] and [14]. Another study finds that on top of the berth asôÙ
signment, portôÙship communication where the ship is informed of its berth assignment
before reaching the port has potential to reduce the fuel consumption by 7.26ôÙ19% as
the ships can reduce their speed and arrive just in time [15].
1.1.2
Yardside operations
The yardside operations consist of transporting and storing the cargo through wareôÙ
house management and yard crane scheduling. Especially the yard crane scheduling

1.1.3
Landside operations
The landside operations of a port consist of the interterminal transportation being the
transportation of containers between locations at the port. But it is also the intermodal
transportation to the hinterland, meaning the trucking and railing of goods to locations
outside of the portãs area. Sustainability challenges in landside operations have become
increasingly prominent. One major challenge is the environmental impact of transportaôÙ
tion, with emissions from trucks and trains contributing to air pollution and greenhouse
gas emissions. Ports are also often located in ecologically sensitive areas, posing risks
to local ecosystems and biodiversity. Additionally, the expansion of port facilities can
lead to land use conflicts, as urban development and port expansion compete for limôÙ

reducing emissions and protecting natural resources, is a complex task that requires
innovative solutions and collaborative efforts between port authorities, transportation
providers, and environmental organizations.
Quayside and yardside operations will not be the main focus of this study, but it is
considered relevant to include it as the entire port and its operations need to be taken
into account when working with green maritime corridors.

Discussion
The impact of the two alternatives has been evaluated over a sixôÙyear horizon, based
on data provided by Dania Connect. This data is both historical for the ICEs but also
includes expectations for the future of the BEVs. Therefore, the analysis does not
account for the possibility of drastic developments in factors such as GHG emissions
or the cost of ICEs, nor does it consider the potential for significant decreases in the
cost of BEVs, although such changes are not anticipated. This issue applies to both
the TCO analysis and the MCDA. To address this, the analysis relies on actual data
available from Dania Connect regarding their current ICEs and ordered BEVs.
In the TCO analysis, data such as purchase price and service agreements that the
company has already signed were used as parameters. However, these parameters
are subject to change in the future. For example, the purchase price of BEVs is currently
higher than that of ICEs, primarily due to the cost of the battery pack, which accounts for
51% of the total cost. Studies indicate that battery pack prices are expected to decline
by 40% from 2022 to 2025 [42], potentially making BEVs an even more costôÙeffective
option in terms of TCO. It is also assumed in the analysis that the cost of diesel and
electricity will remain constant over the sixôÙyear period, although these prices fluctuate
daily. To test the robustness of this assumption, sensitivity analyses were performed
on both values, resulting in nearly equal TCOs when the electricity price was increased
by 100% and the fuel price was decreased by 20%.
In the MCDA, the current performance of the two alternatives within the criteria was asôÙ
sessed. However, as previously mentioned, the driving range, charging infrastructure,
and charging time of BEVs are expected to improve in the future, potentially leading
to higher scores for this alternative. These expectations were taken into account in
the sensitivity analysis, but since they are based on predictions, there is inherent unôÙ
certainty. Additionally, as shown in Figure 5.5, GHG emissions over the life cycle are
expected to decline for all fuel types by 2030 compared to 2021.
Regarding the MCDA approach SMART used in this thesis, it must be noted that one of
its main advantages is the ability to determine the total value through a weighted sum of
impact scores [29]. The direct rating method in SMART is also relatively easy to underôÙ
PreôÙFeasibility Assessment of Port Operations in the Context of Green Maritime Corridors
44
==PAGE:49==
stand, which is important given that Dania Connect is not familiar with MCDA. In contrast
to the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP), SMART provides weights for attributes based
on the scales used to measure them. AHP, on the other hand, determines attribute imôÙ
portance without considering measurement scales, leading to less precise questions.
Another drawback of AHP is that the introduction of new alternatives might reverse the
order of existing alternatives. Given the rapid technological changes in Dania Connectãs
sector, new feasible alternatives could emerge in the near future. Therefore, SMART
was considered a preferable choice if Dania Connect intends to use MCDA for future
decisionôÙmaking.
The process of weighting criteria in the MCDA relies solely on input from Dania ConôÙ
nectãs COO. While he represents the companyãs leadership and has a mandate in the
decisionôÙmaking process, there is a risk of subjectivity in the weighting process. The
Chief Financial Officer (CFO) could have a different opinion and adjust the weights for
criteria such as Revenue and GHGôÙemissions. There is also a potential bias in the
weighting process, as the COO might favor the BEV alternative since four BEVs have
already been allocated to a route where driving range is not a significant concern. It
would have been beneficial to conduct workshops with company employees to gather
their perspectives on what is most important. This would have ensured a more robust
weighting process. To obtain a broader assessment, input from different companies in
the sector could also be sought to identify additional criteria and weigh them according
to importance in their business models.
Using the global scale for scoring the alternatives was necessary since only two alterôÙ
natives were considered. This approach introduces some risks, as the two endpoints
are determined based on publicly available information and may not necessarily repreôÙ
sent the true endpoints. However, using the global scale allows for easier introduction
of new alternatives, as the endpoints are already defined. This flexibility is advantaôÙ
geous for Dania Connect, as it is possible that they may consider other alternatives in
the future, even though they are currently focused solely on battery electric trucks.
For future analyses, it would be relevant to explore other alternatives, such as hydrogenôÙ
powered trucks. Volvo Trucks, a Swedish truck manufacturer, expects to launch a
hydrogenôÙpowered truck with a driving range of 1,000 kilometers and a refueling time
of less than 15 minutes within this decade [43]. This could be a potential alternative
for Dania Connect to consider, although it is essential that the hydrogen used origiôÙ
PreôÙFeasibility Assessment of Port Operations in the Context of Green Maritime Corridors

==PAGE:50==
nates from renewable sources to significantly reduce lifeôÙcycle emissions, as shown
in Figure 5.5. Additionally, a combination of different alternatives could be explored.
Gathering more quantitative and qualitative data, including input from other companies
in the sector, would provide insights into the components of Total Cost of Ownership and
result in more accurate aggregated weights for the MultiôÙCriteria Decision Analysis.

Conclusion
To address Dania Connectãs options in their ambition to become a more sustainable
company, two alternatives were evaluated using both a Total Cost of Ownership analyôÙ
sis and a MultiôÙCriteria Decision Analysis with seven different subôÙcriteria. These analôÙ
yses were chosen to assess the costs and benefits associated with each alternative.
The first alternative is to maintain the current business model, utilizing a fleet of ICEs.
The second alternative is to invest further in BEVs with the aim of achieving a fully
electrified fleet by the end of the decade.
The parameters and values used in the analysis were identified in collaboration with
Dania Connectãs COO, Lars Sû¡rensen. The TCO analysis indicated that over a sixôÙ
year holding period, BEVs would be the preferable choice, with a cost of 2.5 million
DKK, compared to 3.2 million DKK for ICE vehicles, resulting in a difference of 0.7
million DKK per vehicle. This cost advantage is achieved despite the significantly higher
initial purchase price of BEVs. Given that the company currently operates 46 vehicles,
adopting BEVs for the entire fleet could lead to savings of 32.5 million DKK over a sixôÙ
year period. The primary factor driving these savings is the difference in electricity and
fuel prices. To assess the robustness of the result, a sensitivity analysis was conducted,
which involved increasing the electricity price by 100% and reducing the fuel price by
20%. Under these updated parameters, the TCO for both alternatives equaled 2.9
million DKK over the sixôÙyear period.
The MCDA approach, specifically the SMART method, proved to be a favorable choice
for constructing a decision framework for Dania Connect. It allowed for the integration of
quantitative assessments with the creation of value functions. During the SMART analôÙ
ysis, the identification of relevant criteria for evaluation was based on existing studies
and publications, with the final criteria selection made in close collaboration with Lars
Sû¡rensen. This process resulted in the selection of seven criteria: Revenue, DrivôÙ
ing range, Charging infrastructure, Charging time, GHGôÙemissions, Driver satisfaction,
and Capacity of the vehicle. Based on these criteria, it was determined that the battery
electric truck is the preferred alternative due to its superior performance in the Revenue
and GHGôÙemissions criteria, resulting in better overall performance in both analyses.
PreôÙFeasibility Assessment of Port Operations in the Context of Green Maritime Corridors
47
==PAGE:52==
However, the difference in aggregated benefits in the MCDA was not substantial. A
sensitivity analysis was therefore conducted, indicating that when the Driving range
criterion is highly weighted, the ICE alternative performs better due to the current limôÙ
itations in the BEVãs driving range. However, it is expected that this limitation will be
addressed in the future with advancements in battery technology.
In conclusion, the BEV emerged as the bestôÙperforming alternative in both the TCO
analysis and the MCDA, influenced by the fact that the already ordered BEVs are asôÙ
signed to a route where the current driving range is not a significant concern. This alterôÙ
native has the potential to yield savings of 0.7 million DKK per vehicle over the sixôÙyear
period and reduce CO2 emissions by 63% over the vehicleãs lifeôÙcycle. Nonetheless,
transitioning the entire fleet to electric vehicles overnight presents considerable risks
and uncertainties. Future investments could be made to meet client demands in areas
where the BEVãs driving range is adequate.
For future work, it is recommended to explore additional alternatives and gather more
data to refine the criteria weighting. This would provide insights into whether the reôÙ
sults would differ from those presented in this thesis and allow for an investigation of a
combination of alternatives.
