Abstract
Magnified TopoôÙTomography is a novel synchrotron technique that has the potential to
increase the acquisition speed of threeôÙdimensional grain maps as well as their orienôÙ
tation. This technique is a modality of darkôÙfield xôÙray microscopy which up until now
has been used mainly to inspect grain volumes by performing layered scans and stackôÙ
ing them to retrieve a threeôÙdimensional map of a grain. This process is lengthy and
can limit the DFXM users if fastôÙprocesses are to be explored during inôÙoperando experiôÙ
ments. LayerôÙbyôÙlayer 3D reconstructions have a resolution limitation since the voxel size
is anisotropic in the x, y and z directions, due to stacking different projections. This issue
can be improved by using magnified topoôÙtomography since the illumination of the whole
grain allows for a homogeneous pixel size with high resolution.
Data acquired at the ID03 beamline at ESRF taken from an aluminum 1050 poly crystal
is analyzed using iterative tomographic algorithms which show the spatial and angular
resolution capabilities of magnified topoôÙtomography.
New artifacts in the data have been described and hints to their solutions are presented.
This artifacts seem to limit the resolution of the reconstruction algorithms. Their origins are
hypothesized to be from neighboring grains or edge effects that seem to darken stripedôÙ
regions of the sinograms. For the vector tomography modality of MTT, an additional type
of artifact is seen that seems to come from a shift of the projections, causing to unsmooth
the sinusoidalôÙlike shapes of the sinogram. These might come from the limitation of the
rotation stage causing misalignments.
The analysis of resolution and segmentation proved that the use of 1441 projections does
not improve drastically the final shape determination of a downôÙsampled scan using only
21 which leads to think that the fast acquisition of magnifiedôÙtopoôÙtomography is possible.
Additionally, reconstruction using vector tomography datasets proved to give insights into
the grains internal features and orientations.
Finally, an attempt was made to visualize the grainãs weakôÙbeam contrast, revealing loôÙ
calized features that may be associated with dislocations. This could be highly beneficial
for magnified topoôÙtomography, as it would enable the simultaneous acquisition of both
weak and strong beam contrasts within a single measurement.

Introduction
The different properties of materials can be roughly divided into categories such as their
electrical and magnetic capabilities, their mechanical properties, their thermal resistances
and dependence, their chemical or corrosive properties and their optical properties. All of
these are in some way linked to the atomic structure of the materials. [1]
By choosing a particular class of materials, crystals, we simplify the analysis in a way
since crystals are by definition materials with periodic atomic structures. This periodicity
can give them exceptional performances for conducting electricity or heat, for instance,
since the order of the atoms is arranged into patterns called lattices that are responsible
for the rapid and ordered propagation of electricity for example. It is therefore intuitive that
the structure of these materials is an important subject to understand, since it can and it
has opened so many doors for the advancement of technology.
The study of crystalline materials covers a wide range of disciplines and impacts an even
larger number of industries. Its history and evolution begins in each primitive civilization,
where, by different methods, ancient humans discovered some of the crystalline materials
properties such as the reflectivity of metals to create mirrors or the amorphous structure of
obsidian that allows to create extremely sharp instruments when being cut. These instruôÙ
ments and discoveries have projected humans into big leaps in understanding crystalline
materials. A bit more recently, in the beginning of the 20th century, Sir William Henry
Bragg linked the propagation of xôÙray radiation to the atomic structure of such materials.
This created a strong tool that allowed us to analyze and understand crystals and their
properties in a deeper way.
Parallel to Braggãs endeavors, tomography was birthed by Johan Radon. This discipline
was developed due to the discovery of a transformation that allows to model the propôÙ
agation of xôÙray light through a sample and into a detector. This discovery allowed to
back project this acquisition which means to retrieve the body traversed by light and in
this way to acquire a threeôÙdimensional map of all kinds of samples. It is of widespread
use in hospitals when performing tomographies and it is also used to study samples using
laboratory based xôÙray machines. [2]
Linking the diffraction of light that arises from crystalline structures and the tomographic
reconstruction methods, as well as the synchrotronãs higher energy, higher flux and coôÙ
herence xôÙrays, new techniques have been developed to answer the questions of metôÙ
allurgical science. Techniques combining diffraction contrast and tomographic principles
came to light such as 3DXRD and DCT.[3][4] Techniques that could map the grains of
a metal with high precision. To add to this arsenal of techniques, TopoôÙTomography, a
technique that focuses on imaging the 3D structure of a single grain by aligning a rotation
axis to the diffraction vector was introduced.[5]
In addition, the creation of xôÙray lenses allowing to magnify the images taken with xôÙrays,
open the door to higher resolution studies. To go further in resolution, DarkôÙField XôÙray
microscopy was introduced.[6] DFXM is a technique that magnifies the diffracted light from
a grain inside a sample and can acquire layers and volumes with a high resolution. This
technique has been used primarily to scan layers of these grains and then stack them to
create a 3D volume, but the use of topographical and tomographic methods can be of
great value to increase speed and resolution of the images. This is why the technique

2.1
3D Diffraction Contrast Imaging
Crystals are solids with periodic structures, meaning that the atomic structure of a given
crystal can be described by a certain pattern that is repeated in space. [10]
This pattern is called the lattice and it is described using the following geometric convenôÙ
tions. A given lattice structure can be represented by having the knowledge of the three

4.1
Binary Tomography
The premise of the binary tomography is bringing down the 6 Dimensional problem into
a 3D problem like classical tomography. In this case we do the alignment for MTT as
described previously, aligning our tomographic rotation motor with the diffraction vector
and we proceed to do a scan in which, at every tomoôÙstep we perform an integrated scan
to acquire the full spread of the diffracted beam at each given position. We can refer as
the scan done at each tomoôÙstep as the internal loop and for binary tomography, we will
refer to it as the internal binary loop. During the experiment, our internal binary loop scan
was made by rotating diffrz motor of ID03 which would correspond to a rocking curve in
the horizontal configuration at the initial tomoôÙstep. In other words, this scan is equivalent
to taking a rocking curve when the sample is not rotated. This precision is important since
we will see in the next sections that, as expected, the angular spread is rotating with
the sample and performing an integrated scan over a single direction is not allowing to
capture the whole diffracted signal which leads to a nonôÙhomogeneous intensity across
the sinogram. Discontinuities in the sinogram can only be explained by nonlinearities like
the reciprocal space problem described previously.
Additionally to these discontinuities, all MTT sinograms taken so far show some dark
stripes that are tilted with different angles. The current hypothesis for the presence of
these artifacts is that the signal could be dimmed by a satellite grain nearby or an edge
effect. This is furthermore discussed in the final section. For this section, we address this
problem by considering these stripes as nonlinearities of the data and they are our main
justification for the use of iterative algorithms.
Since the premise of binary tomography is to integrate the intensity of each step of the
given scan, we donãt have any additional information from the grain than the classical
tomography gives. We will see how for the vector tomography we can obtain more inforôÙ
mation with a similar scan. The information we can extract from the binary tomography is
therefore purely morphological. For instance, we can extract geometrical information of a
grain, starting by the surface area of the grain, its volume, orientation and centroid. AddiôÙ
tionally, some morphological information like the sphericity, the curvature and more can
be extracted by the use of simple filters. This has been developed and the techniques
to extract these features are wellôÙsettled into the scientific literature. In theory, this will
consist in the best way to obtain the complete information of a grain boundary since the
layerôÙbyôÙlayer technique has a vertical resolution limit which corresponds to the chosen
vertical step taken between each layer.
We will dive deeper into the reconstruction and the data analysis in the following and
for each step, brief discussions on the accuracy and quality of the showcased results is
presented. Most of the methodology developed for 3D MTT reconstruction was developed
initially for the binary tomography and we then extrapolated it to the vector tomography
which is detailed next.

4.4
Image Analysis
As we can see for binary tomography, the reconstructions even though they are improved
using regularization such as Total Variation are still populated with noise and streak artiôÙ
facts that make it hard to segment the grain from the background and acquire some more
quantitative data like the true volume or the curvature of the edges. We suggest here
a sequence of filters and morphological operators to achieve a segmentation of a grain
using the binary tomography modality of MTT. Furthermore, this segmentation algorithm
was also used for vector tomography to segment the volume of the subôÙsamples at difôÙ
ferent rocking steps. To create this segmentation algorithm, the SciôÙKit image library was
used. [27]
The segmentation algorithm starts with the raw reconstruction seen from the sagittal view.
This is important since it represents the crossôÙsection of the stack of all the reconstrucôÙ
tions and has often less visible streak artifacts that can make segmentation harder. The
first step is Otsu thresholding. The Otsu method minimizes the variance of two intensity
domains by selecting a threshold in the intensity value corresponding to its minimum. This
sets a good start to extract the grain from the background but as we can see in figure 4.8,
step 2, the thresholded image shows a grain with a porous texture. This comes from the
error induced by the reconstruction algorithms. [28]
To fill in the voids we use a gaussian blur with a large variance of around 10 to 20 pixels.
In image analysis, the variance of the gaussian filter corresponds to the span of the kernel
over the image. This morphological operator will make the segmentation lose accuracy
but it is a robust way to obtain a first mask.
Following the Gaussian blur, we run a Sobel edge detector. The Sobel operator consists
of taking the gradient of the image along the vertical and horizontal axis. The gradient
in image analysis is a finite difference operator represented by a square matrix. The
resulting of the gradient along each direction shows where the image has the biggest
sudden changes, which is translated by the edges. Combining both directions allows us
to detect edges of differently shaped images. [28]
In step 4 of figure 4.8 we see that the Sobel kernel fails to detect the outer edge of the
kernel and additionally it detects internal edges for remaining features from the previous
operations. To solve this, an immediate solution is to increase the gaussian blur but by
doing so the accuracy of the segmentation is decreased. Therefore, we rather perform
another Otsu thresholding over the edge detected image and in this way the strongest
edge which is the outer one is selected by the algorithm.
Finally, an algorithm called FloodFill is used to fill the contour of the grain. [29]
This type of segmentation can be used in this specific case where there is prior knowledge
of having only one object in the image with a somewhat homogeneous filling and sharp
edges.
Results and discussion of the performance of the algorithm are presented in the next
chapter.

6
Conclusion and Future Perspectives
After working with data from different MTT modalities, some conclusions and recommenôÙ
dations for the next steps arise. This first try described in this work reveals the potential
for single grain imaging that MTT has as it is fast and has a high resolution, both spatial
and angular. The conclusions have been separated into the different modalities since
the datasets seem different for each of them and therefore, different elements have to be
taken into consideration for each.
6.1
MTT Generalities
6.1.1
Experiment
MTT relies on the fine alignment to keep the grain in a diffraction condition over a 360ãÎ
rotation around its diffraction vector. The alignment can be difficult and timeôÙconsuming
since, in addition, it is difficult to know exactly which grain is being observed when doing
the alignment. During the experiments, a scan was done to observe the different grains
and make a map but this is not accurate and gave us only some morphological informaôÙ
tion to identify the grains. It is therefore recommended to perform a 3D grain mapping
technique such as DCT before the MTT acquisition to have additional information about
the grains, their spreads and their orientation. This information can be useful when alignôÙ
ing but also during the reconstruction since it can help to optimize the acquisition and
reconstruction parameters.
6.1.2
Normalization
An additional generality is that we recommend the use of the wavelet method to remove
the detectorãs errors while preôÙprocessing MTT data. This is important since the bright
images usually taken for tomography, that consist of images taken when the beam is
shining on the detector with no sample, can correct some artifacts especially ring artifacts
in reconstructions. We see that this method seems to be efficient for MTT data.
6.2
Sinogram Discontinuities
As described in chapter 4, the dark stripes in the sinogram induce errors that propagate
in the reconstruction step, lowering the final quality of the reconstruction. The current
interpretation of these stripes is the presence of neighboring grains or edge effects that
come from some elements not belonging to the grain.
Tackling this artifact is important to reduce the noise when reconstructing. Firstly, to unôÙ
derstand exactly where these features are coming from, a digital twin of a perfect sample
with no strain should be used and a light propagation modeled combined with the resoôÙ
lution function should be studied. After setting this numerical model, more imperfections
can be added to the sample which can be deformation, strain, edge effects and neighborôÙ
ing grains. Hopefully from these numerical experiments, a similar resulting sinogram can
be acquired that will confirm the hypothesis and give hints on how to solve the problem.
Additionally, another idea is to develop a reconstruction algorithm using a masked reôÙ
construction method that is briefly introduced. The premise of this algorithm is to find by
using a machine learning method, the pixels that correspond to the dark lines and then
only perform optimization over the ones that do not seem to be streaked by this effect.
This idea can be further developed if itãs deemed to be appropriate.

We also see on the sinograms that the intensity is not homogeneous, with strong variations
across the tomographic rotation. This is caused by the spread of the grain not being
homogeneous over the ü and ü orientations which creates an additional issue for the
reconstruction.
For a future experiment, the sample chosen has to have an angular spread of the order
of the numerical aperture, allowing the CRL to integrate the whole diffracted intensity,
allowing a more homogeneous illumination of the grain. To tackle the darkôÙstripes in the
sinogram, a sample with less grains has to be scanned. This can be achieved by electro
polishing a sample such as the one studied in detail in this work. Since it is not confirmed
yet that the darkôÙstripes arise from neighboring grains, next experiments should avoid
having dynamical diffraction coming from the grain of interest. This can be achieved by
deforming the sample or selecting a sample with smaller grains, which gives another
reason to perform DCT to characterize the MTT samples prior to the MTT experiment.
6.3
Binary Tomography
6.3.1
Resolution analysis
For binary tomography, we see firstly that different reconstruction algorithms were sucôÙ
cessfully applied to reconstruct a 3D volume of the grain. Additionally, the resolution was
evaluated by changing different parameters, proving that the alignment as well as the
number of iterations are of great importance.
6.3.2
Segmentation
A segmentation method is presented, this is an example using classical image analysis
tools and eventually some deep learning model could be made to segment the images in
a better way but the data is deemed to be too noisy at the moment which will difficult the
correct training.
This segmentation technique still needs to be evaluated when comparing the segmentaôÙ
tion to a ground truth. We propose for example in an upcoming experiment to perform
similar measurements as the one made for this work and in addition, to do a layerôÙbyôÙlayer
scan that can then be used as a ground truth.
6.3.3
Down sampling ü
Probably the most exciting result for binary tomography in this work is the similarity not
of resolution but of the segmentation when down sampling the tomographic steps. As
we have seen, the segmentation using a reconstruction with 1441 projections is 95.9 %
similar to the one using only 21. This means that the scans can be very fast and give
a correct indication of the grainãs shape. This has great potential to see for instance the
evolution of the grainãs shape during a mechanical deformation or when annealed.

6.4
Vector Tomography
6.4.1
Acquisition instability
As seen for the weak beam sinogram, we can see that the sinograms have some perôÙ
turbation that might arise from the fine movements that are required to be done for the
rocking scans. These artifacts have been seen in XôÙray and electron nanotomography
and a joint iterative algorithm exists to correct these misalignments. Due to the time limôÙ
itations of this project, this was not implemented, but it is recommended to be used for
later jobs using MTT if this artifact is prevalent.
6.4.2
MultiôÙdimensional representation
Two different methods were presented to label different regions of the grain with their
corresponding ö¡ value. The segmentation one seems to underestimate the regions corôÙ
responding to each ö¡ value, whereas the max finder technique is spaceôÙfilling and does
not seem to have this problem. We suggest once again to compare these methods with
a ground truth by taking 2D topographs and comparing them to sections of the 3D reconôÙ
structions. More tessellation methods can be made, for example, by taking the fitted peak
of the pixel distribution or its FWHM.
The analysis of each labeled region can then be quantified using image analysis techôÙ
niques. We showed here an example to take the gradientãs orientation of each subôÙslice,
but depending on the goal of the experiment, different parameters can be calculated such
as their size, curvature, volume. etc.
6.4.3
WeakôÙbeam
As seen in the results from the max finder tessellation methods, the values that correôÙ
spond to the maximum of each pixel only correspond to 7 out of 45 ö¡ values. We consider
that the strong beam corresponds to these values. A reconstruction for a subôÙvolume
taken at 0.1 degrees from the rocking curve center was made and this seemed to show
punctual round features instead of regions of the grain. These features might be disloôÙ
cations and this could open a possibility of acquiring a fast 3D volume of dislocations in
the same time as the grainãs morphology is acquired. DFXM has already proved its caôÙ
pabilities for acquiring extensive dislocations in 3D by performing layerôÙbyôÙlayer scans.
[8] A faster acquisition time with MTT could deepen the understanding of the dislocation
networks and their evolution in 3D. As mentioned, the limitation during this work for this
reconstruction was the perturbed sinogram which is expected to be corrected using joint
iterative methods. [31]
6.4.4
PinkôÙbeam DFXM
Recently, a technological advance, pinkôÙbeam DFXM (pDFXM), has proved that the use
of a pink beam with an increased bandwidth can increase the diffracted intensity of DFXM
experiments up to 27ôÙfold while maintaining a high spatial resolution and keeping sufficient
angular resolution to map the grainãs misorientation as in the case of the monochromatic
beam. This can lead to faster acquisition of up to 20ôÙfold. [32]
We have seen that MTT can be a fast technique, needing only 20 projections to obtain a
good segmentation of the grain. This leads to conclude that combining pink beam DFXM
with MTT could lead to even faster acquisition times and that the MTT reconstruction
could also benefit from the increased contrast of pDFXM. This can allow for the 3D imagôÙ
ing of faster processes inside the grain to improve the understanding of their complex
mechanisms.

6.4.5
Reciprocal space sampling
A recent thesis on the topics of DCT reconstruction paired with topo tomography was pubôÙ
lished to have a robust mathematical description of TT as well as refining the 6 dimensional
reconstruction algorithm of DCT and presenting a joint reconstruction algorithm. [33]
It is clear that this type of description adds robustness and allows for optimization for TT.
It is interesting to mention in the scope of this work that one of the findings of this thesis
is the ability to map the orientation of the grain using TT. With a single TT scan only a
position map of the grain can be acquired, but performing multiple TT scans by aligning
the grain of interest at different scattering vectors or changing the detector to sample
distance can allow to probe more Fourier elements of the orientation space. Since each
TT scan requires alignment and acquisition time, the experiments are limited to acquire
only a few different TT scans which can be used to provide the average orientation at
each voxel.
In MTT, the spatial and angular resolution is enhanced by the presence of a magnifying
lens and the long detector to sample distance. This means that the Fourier space can be
sampled directly by one MTT scan, which is why this joint approach was not considered.
Although, the sampling of reciprocal space is still to be studied since the lens introduces
a resolution function that is anisotropic in reciprocal space. A further mathematical deôÙ
scription following the style of the thesis discussed here would be relevant to be able to
understand how to optimize the reciprocal space probing using MTT.
6.5
Summary
In conclusion, in this work we have studied the capabilities of MTT by reconstructing and
analyzing data from its different modalities. For the binary tomography case, we find that
fast acquisitions are possible, showing that an acquisition with only a few projections leads
to equivalent results in terms of the grain shape as one with more than 1000 tomographic
steps. The relevance of using a reconstruction algorithm with a regularizing term is shown
by quantifying the evolution of the LSF resolution, and a segmentation algorithm is preôÙ
sented as well as a description of MTTãs artifacts. For the vector tomography modality,
two different reconstruction methods are presented using segmentation or a tessellation
by finding the maximum value of each pixel over the rocking curve. An example of what
can be quantified by using image analysis on each labeled component is shown for the
gradient orientation, and lastly, an attempt of a reconstruction with weak beam contrast
is shown. Next steps and suggestions for the future study of MTT and future experiments
are given.
