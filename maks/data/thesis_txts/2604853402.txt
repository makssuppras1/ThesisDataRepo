Abstract
In the modern power system, the electricity providers must have enough installed power
capacity to meet real life demands. Many inefficiencies are caused in the power system
due to the perishable nature of the grid. Due to the lack of energy storage devices within
the grid system, the energy must be immediately delivered to and used by the consumer.
There are now are large number of cases where batteries are being integrated for energy
storage within the grid and Lithium ion batteries are the most commonly used under this
category. This reduces the inefficiencies and leads to better usage of the energy produced.
The growth is due to their versatility, high energy density and efficiency Divya and Øster-
gaard (2009).
Batteries are able to react to the grid demands nearly instantaneously, but also have the
capacity to function over longer durations and have a wide range of storage and power
capacities. Significant advancement in newer battery chemistries has allowed for a wide
range of battery options for new storage applications and has increased the robustness
and functionality of the batteries within the electric grid. More grid applications have
become suitable for BESS as battery costs have decreased while performance and life have
continued to increase Mears et al. (2003).
The possibility to make the batteries last longer by predicting their health is one of the
needs of the hour. If their lifetime is known then it also becomes easier to use them ef-
ficiently and replace them before their lifetime expires. This thesis tries to do that with
certain limitations.

Introduction
The current storage scenario uses energy for a variety of applications and with different
techniques. Most applications include black start and voltage support but could also in-
clude storage of excess energy generated. These technologies range from mechanical and
electrical to electrochemical. The different types of storage technologies are given below
in Figure 1.1
Figure 1.1: Current storage technologies
As electrochemical storage is quickly growing and its flexibility which is better than other
storage systems is being realized, it becomes important to look the reason for this. And
more importantly it would be necessary to contribute in this growth if possible.
1.1
Motivation and objective of the thesis
Research in Li-ion cell technologies and chemistries of the present systems has increased
by more than 1660% from 1990 to 2014 Müller et al. (2017). In addition to their broad capa-
bility of providing ancillary services with high efficiency compared to other technologies,
Li-ion systems are not problematic system technology in terms of geographical require-
ments. As an example, unlike pumped hydro storage or compressed air energy systems,
Li-ion systems are independent of geographical constraints and are therefore easily scal-
able. With so much increase in Li-ion chemistries, it is important to understand how they
work to utilize their full potential.

1.2. State of the art
The objective of this thesis is to understand and analyze the working of batteries by mod-
eling them and then use their data in machine learning techniques to predict about their
health for their better performance in the future. With a framework in mind that could be
used by others who need to analyze their battery data.
1.2
State of the art
The work done by Widodo and Shim done in 2011 given in Widodo et al. (2011) describes
the use of Support Vector Machine (SVM) and Regression Vector Machine (RVM) as ma-
chine learning algorithms to find the State of Health (SOH) of a Li-ion battery. A sample
entropy method was developed and an estimated SOH were used to train and as input
data and target vectors respectively. The data to validate the system was taken from the
data repository of NASA Ames Prognostics Center of Excellence (PCoE). The data was
from a custom built battery test bed of Li-ion 1850 sized rechargable batteries Saha and
Goebel (2007). This work concluded that both SVM and RVM can be used in SOH pre-
diction and that RVM performed better than SVM in the prediction.
The paper Eddahech et al. (2012) uses Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) to predict the
deterioration in battery performance. One of objectives in this research was to model
the behavior of a high-energy-density lithium ion-cell on the basis of Electro-chemical
Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS). A model was made to simulate Li-ion batteries. Aging
tests and power cycling tests were performed on the model. In the end temperature, cur-
rent magnitude, and SOC variations were used as additional input network for RNN. Two
predictors, capacity and estimated resistance were used for battery performance evalua-
tion. The results revealed that neural predictors were able to accurately predict variations
in performance with aging.
In Nuhic et al. (2013) an estimation of SOH and remaining useful life (RUL) has been
done using SVR for batteries in hybrid and electrical vehicles. The data for Li-ion batter-
ies is collected from terminal behavior like voltage, current and temperature. This work
introduces a new method for composing training data by processing them with load col-
lectives. Load collectives provide information about the occurrence frequency of a certain
combination of two signals like current or temperature. Another type of load cycle count-
ing was used known as rainflow counting. It counts the number of times the SOC jumps
from one value to another. For the features in SVM, a matrix of load collectives of type,
temperature over current and SOC over temperature was generated. In the end the vali-
dation showed satisfactory results.
In Andre et al. (2013) two methods, a standard Kalman filter and Unscented Kalman filter
were used to estimate SOC and SOH of a Li-ion battery. Here also SVM was implemented
coupled with the filters. Some of the parameters used for SVM were relative cell capacity
at a particular time, minimal and maximal temperature, time span, mean current rate,
maximal SOC, etc. The SOH was not calculated directly but from an estimated actual
cell capacity and ohmic resistance defining end-of-life criteria. For the results, the dual
Kalman filters showed SOC estimation error below 1%.
Unlike state-of-charge, state-of-energy (SOE) has been used to estimate degradation in
Dong et al. (2015). A wavelet-neural-network-based (WNN) battery model and filter es-
timator is used to estimate SOE. To acquire voltage, current and temperature a test bench
was established. The test results were used to analyze some factors like open circuit volt-
age (OCV), discharge current and temperature. For the SOE estimation a particle filter
(PF) was employed and then verified by using different operating temperatures and dis-
charge currents. The results show that this method can be used to estimate SOE with an

State of health has also been estimated using group method of data handling Wu et al. (2016a).
Group method of handling data (GMHD) is a polynomial neural network. The terminal
voltage curve art constant charge current is set as a feature to reflect SOH. A differential
geometry approach (DGA) was used to acquire featured parameters from thousands of
sample points from the experimental data. Then GMDH neural network was used to
establish the relation between voltage curves and SOH. This paper also uses the NASA
battery data set to validate the accuracy of DGA and GMDH. Also, experiment results
from another battery developed by a power company from China was also used for val-
idation. In summary this dual method resulted in accurate SOH estimation results for
Li-ion batteries.
In Wu et al. (2016b) also, the remaining useful life (RUL) was estimated using importance
sampling (IS) and feed forward neural network (FFNN). The difference in voltage curves
of battery constant current (CC) charge under different cycle numbers is used for RUL
definition. Then a FFNN with 40 hidden neurons is employed for estimating RUL online.
Then IS is utilized to select FFNN input from amount of voltage points for accurate li-ion
charge curve reconstitution. The approach predicted the actual RUL with an error less
than 5% for practical operations.
In Lin et al. (2017) a single particle model (SPM) has been used to model LiFePO4 battery
for electrical vehicles (EVs). Then a sliding mode observer (SMO) is used to identify the
model parameters. The SMO is offline based parameter estimation identification method.
One SMO was used for SOC estimation while another for cell contact resistance. The
SMO showed good performance in accurate prediction of terminal voltage and model
parameters. This method showed less than 1% of error and was a good method for SOC
prediction.
Unlike most of the estimation techniques, the method for SOC/SOH estimation in Chaoui
et al. (2017) does not require prior knowledge of battery’s model or parameters. Instead it
uses ambient temperature variations and battery’s voltage and current data for the predic-
tion. An input time-delayed neural network (ITDNN) is used for the estimation. Before
the data is given to the neural network it is normalized, meaning each data like voltage
measurement is divided by the maximum possible measurement. This normalization im-
proves the performance of ITDNN. This method estimated SOC and SOH with low error.
Another advantage of this method was that despite the effects of hysteresis, temperature
and aging, it estimated the required quantities with minimal error.
D. Yang et al. (2017) describes a parameter extraction method called hybrid pulse power
characterization (HPPC) to identify the battery parameters of first order equivalent cir-
cuit model. Then a three layer neural network called back propagation neural network
(BPNN) is used to train the input data. The method has low computational cost and low
memory requirement. The training rate could be increased by increasing the number of
layers of the neural network.
Another data driven identification of SOH is done using Nonlinear Frequency Response
Analysis (NFRA) in Harting et al. (2018). Here an aging test of a battery is done and a
correlation measure is used to identify the most sensitive frequency range for ageing tests.
The mid-frequency range of 1 Hz to 100 Hz was found to show the strongest correlation.
The Spearman rank algorithm was used to find the correlation. SVR was used to build
a regression model using parameters like initial capacity, initial resistance, anode and
cathode thickness, etc.

1.2. State of the art
In Tang et al. (2018) also uses the NASA battery data to propose an estimation algorithm
for battery SOH. Here SOH has been used as a linear function of the regional capacity of a
battery. For this capacity calculation Kalman filter has been used. The estimator does not
need SOC or impedance information therefore can be used for large number of battery
cells. Also this method is not sensitive to noise and filtering algorithms and is effective as
large as 1C current rate. The estimation errors from the results were less than 2.5%.
Li et al. (2019) also uses the NASA battery data set to study battery aging. Here Gaussian
process regression (GPR) is used to forecast the battery health conditions. First, the initial
incremental capacity curves are filtered by using advanced signal processing. Then im-
portant features are extracted from partial incremental capacity curves using correlation
analysis method. Thirdly, Gaussian process regression (GPR) is applied to model the
short term SOH. Lastly, an autoregressive long-term battery remaining useful lifetime
(RUL) is established using the results of battery SOH values and previous output. The
results show that short-term SOH estimations and long-term RUL prediction are accurate
and reliable. Of the four tested batteries the root mean square error (RMSE) are less than
23 cycles.
Song et al. (2020) presents a SOH estimation method using XGBoost algorithm with accu-
racy correction by using Markov chain. The features taken include average voltage, volt-
age difference, current difference and temperature difference to describe the aging pro-
cess of the Li-ion batteries. This paper also uses the data from the NASA Ames Reseach
Center. This method of prediction was compared with the predicted results of linear re-
gression, support vector machine and k-nearest neighbour. Among all these methods
XGBoost was closest in prediction to the real values. The improvement in prediction was
around 10-20%.
Liu et al. (2020) also uses SVR to estimate the SOH values. Three features have been
extracted here to represent the dynamic changes in a Li-ion battery during charging and
discharging. They are constant current charging time (CCCT), constant voltage charging
time (CVCT) and time duration of the fixed segment (FST). To determine the correlation
between the health indicators and the SOH, grey correlation analysis (GCA) method is
used. The model developed is validated using NASA’s battery data. Differential evolution
(DE) technique is applied to the SVR to optimize the parameters of SVR. The model was
able to estimate a high precision for SOH.

1.3
Methodology and Data
The methodology of this thesis would be to first model the basic of cell of a battery. Then
moving further to predict their states like state of charge and state of health. In the end
the battery data would be used to predict their lifetime.
The data used in this thesis are of two different kinds. The first one is from the researchers
of University of Colorado - Colorado Springs Colorado Springs (n.d.) This data is from
Panasonic batteries was collected in their own laboratory and it has been used for the first
4 chapters of this thesis. The second one is from Severson et al. (2019) manufactured by
A123 Systems. This data has been used for the last chapter for machine learning tech-
niques.
So why two types of data has been used instead of one? There are several reasons:
• The first dataset has different temperatures while the second set has one set of data
for one temperature.
• The first set have open circuit voltage data necessary for the static part of modeling
and also the dynamic data required for the dynamic modeling. The second data set
has only the voltage data.
• The first dataset because of the previous reason has capability for parameter estima-
tion required for modeling while the second dataset has not.
• Size of first set is (1 X 201)*3 + (1 X 178)*3, so 1137 sets of data at 9 different temper-
atures. The second one has the size of (124000 X 99) at a single temperature which
is 30°C.
Due to these reasons the first dataset is perfect for state estimation and filters and the
second one due to its less complexity is perfect for machine learning algorithms. It would
be really difficult to use either of these for each other’s purposes.

Chapter 2
Cell Circuit Modelling
To study the working of batteries it is important to understand the concept of its building
blocks which are the cells of a battery. This chapter is to explain the fundamental work-
ing behind a cell’s operation and the building of its dynamics. We use electrical-circuit
analogy to explain the behavior of cell’s voltage to different input-current stimuli. Some
simulations will be shown to back the theory. These simulations have been taken from
the data set of researchers from University of Colorado Spring called the MATLAB ESC
toolbox.
2.1
Open circuit voltage representation
The observed behavior of a cell can be explained from a very basic circuit model repre-
sented by the open circuit voltage (OCV) on the output side of an electric circuit. The
model is given in the figure fig. 2.1
Figure 2.1: OCV Model
This is a equivalent circuit model. The elements in this model don’t describe the con-
struction of the cell but rather its fundamental response. The output side of the model
registers some measured voltage. So this basic model is simply an ideal voltage source.
In the model, cell terminal voltage v(t) is not a function of load current i(t), nor a function
of past cell usage. The voltage is constant throughout. This is not a good model of a bat-
tery cell as the real voltage of a cell is dependent on load current, past usage, temperature,
etc. Later the model will be improved but this volatage source will remain an important
part of the final equivalent circuit model.

Chapter 3
State Estimation
Whenever batteries are used, one of the most important things to measure about it are
the currents, the voltages and the temperatures. It is necessary to know the state of the
battery for it’s optimum working and these measurements help to understand the battery
state. This state estimation includes the estimation of SOC and later on the health of the
cell. Different cells may have different SOCs. Unequal SOCs may need to be balanced and
cell energy and power be calculated which is transmitted to the load. We will consider
the health of the cell called State of Health (SOH) in the next chapter. For this chapter the
main concentration is on the estimation of SOC of a cell.
3.1
SOC estimation
When using cells or batteries over long periods of time, cell SOC, diffusion currents and
hysteresis states change very quickly, over the course of minutes or hours. Other tend to
change very slowly, like cell capacities and resistances. These could change to only a few
percent of their original over years of use.
The quickly changing quantities will be referred to as ”states” of the cell, while the slowly
changing quantities will be referred to as ”parameters” of the cell. The cells SOC will be
represented by a vector zk. A good SOC estimate is required to balance both energy and
power in a cell. SOC was defined in the previous chapter as a quantity between 0% and
100%. SOC is not a quantity that can be measured by a device or read by a meter. For its
estimation current, voltage, temperature must be combined to form an estimate.
There are many simple as well as complex methods that give good estimates of SOC Piller
et al. (2001). An accurate measurement of SOC can have certain benefits like:
• Performance: Without good SOC estimation, one must be conservative to avoid over-
charge or overdischarge. With good estimation there is reliable confidence and the
cell can be used judiciously.
• Density: Exact state of charge and battery information allows the battery to be used
within its design limits, so there is no need for complex engineering. This can lead
to lighter and smaller battery packs.
• Longevity: The probability of overcharging or overdischarging a cell is reduced with
a decent SOC estimation. Overcharge or overdischarge may cause permanent dam-
age and reduced lifetime of a cell. A good SOC estimate is need to make sure there
is no overcharge or overdischarge.

3.2.3
A mixed approach
Another approach to estimate SOC would be to have a mixed approach that combines
the above mentioned two approaches. This can be done by using a model that has the
cell input (current) and output (voltage) behaviors. The model would input the current
and process noises, and use the true system of the cell to output the voltage which would
also contain the sensor noise which would be included in the model as well. The true
system means the true SOC with a set of true diffusion currents and hysteresis voltages.
The process noise is due to the current sensor and voltage sensor measurement tools. As
these quantities are unknown it makes the SOC estimation unpredictable. The sensor
noise means is due to the variation in true and measured current. As the true values are
not known, its effect in SOC estimation is unpredictable as well.
So we know that model based estimations mean that we cannot measure the system’s
true state. So we try to measure the input which is the current and use that same input
throughout a model of the system. We predict the system output (voltage) based on
the state estimate and measured input. The predicted output is then compared with the
measured output. If the two are identical then the model’s estimation is good. If they
are different then the estimate is poor. This approach till now is the same as coulomb
counting. Till now this approach has been similar to coulomb counting, but when we take
the difference between true and measured values and use this difference as a feedback
mechanism to update the model’s state estimation then we make a better state estimation.
This inclusion of feedback makes a good estimation which was missing from the coulomb
counting method.
The feedback needs to be handled carefully. There could be a lot of voltage prediction
errors. These could be due to state estimation error, modeling errors and measurement
errors. Therefore the state estimate feedback must be taken into careful consideration.
One of the algorithms to do this could be Kalman filters that computes the state estimate
in spite of the uncertainties mentioned. There are many improved versions of this filter
like the Extended Kalman filter (EKF) and the Sigma point Kalman filter (SPKF). These
would be looked in depth in the upcoming section.

able process noise random input signal. yk is the output of the system. The function f(.)
computes the state equation of the system while h(.) computes the output equation of the
system. They are generally time varying but this dependency will be removed for ease of
calculations.
The sequential probabilistic inference finds a recursive estimate of the state xk of the sys-
tem using all inputs and measurements of all outputs till time k. Here Uk is the set of
inputs while Yk is the set of outputs. These sets can be written as:
Uk = {u0, u1, ..., uk}
(3.12)
Yk = {y0, y1, ..., yk}
(3.13)
The solution of these equations would be sequential, which means that its estimates would
be based on prior estimates for every time step. Therefore the result would be a sequence
of estimates. The solution is also probabilistic as it takes into account the randomness of
the sensor noise and process noise while computing the estimates. The true state of the
system can be represented by a state vector whose value changes over time. These changes
are due to the known input uk and the also due to the unknown process noise input wk.
Therefore the state must be modeled at a particular time as a vector random variable. The
state sequence must be modeled as a vector random process. The previous input state can
defined as xk−1 while the current state as xk. The uncertainty when going from xk−1 to xk
due to the process noise wk−1 can be represented by the conditional probability density
function fX|X(xk|xk−1).
The state cannot be measured but the noisy system outputs yk can be observed. These
observations can help us know about what is happening inside. Using the observations
and the model, the state can be estimated. As these measurements are not deterministic,
the sensor noise vk uncertainty can be modeled by conditional probability density func-
tion fY |X(yk|xk). The uncertainty due to process noise and sensor noise randomness, will
always cause error in the estimation of the state. Thus we need to have a state estimator
confidence interval in the estimation itself. For the measure of the randomness in the prob-
ability and process, we need random variables. These random variables will be explained
in the next section.

Extended Kalman filter
The Kalman filer is a good minimum mean sqaure error (MMSE) estimator for linear
systems. In linear systems the noises are white and Gaussian, which means the noise has
a probability density function of a normal distribution. However the real life noise and
also in the ESC cell model are nonlinear, so the linear Kalman filter is not very effective.
The sequential probabilistic inference solution can be used for nonlinear systems but the
calculation of Expectation E is not same. One of the following three strategies can be used
for a state estimator of nonlinear systems:
• The extended Kalman filter(EKF): This method does the work of analytic linearization
of the model at each point of time. This method can still work if the system nonlin-
earities are mild.
• The sigma point Kalman filter (SPKF): Also known as the unscented Kalman filter,
this method does the work of performing statistical linearization of the model at
each point of time. It gives out better results than EKF.
• Particle filters: This method is the best but requires a lot of computational power.
It uses the Monte Carlo to find probabilities and does not assume Gaussian distri-
butions. Particle filters are necessary when the system has severe nonlinearities or
when the pdfs have multiple peaks which could result in different state trajectories.
Before we use EKF there are certain assumptions that need to be taken:
• When computing the output of a linear function, the EKF assumes that the expected
value of a nonlinear function at an unknown state is the same as when the function
is evaluated at an expected state. E[fn(x)] ≈fn(E[x])
• When the covariance estimates are computed, the EKF uses the Taylor-series expan-
sion to linearize the system equations. Higher order terms are discarded.

Health Estimation
4.1
Need for health estimation
With time and usage, the cells in a battery would age and have a decrement in their per-
formance. They will finally reach a point where they won’t be able to provide adequate
power or energy to the battery, which is called as the cell’s end of life. So it’s important
that between a cell’s beginning of life and it’s end of life we have a thorough knowledge of
the present degradation of the cell so that accurate calculations can be of state of charge,
available power and available energy.
For health estimation we need to understand how different parameters in the cell model
change over time. Which of them are more significant and how to account for them in
the cell model. One of the causes of cell failure is normal aging. But failures can also
happen because of poorly controlled manufacturing process, cell design faults, impurities
in the materials used for manufacturing or uncontrolled operations. Cells with design
or manufacturing faults would appear normal for a time period but would failed very
quickly.
This chapter would deal with those quantities that cause a change in the performance of
the cell. We need to understand and track the normal aging process as well as uncon-
trolled operations like overvoltage and overtemperature and so on accelerate the normal
degradation mechanisms. These indicators are indicators of battery state of health (SOH).
The most commonly used definition of SOH are ones involving total capacity and the
series resistance of a cell. Accurate estimation of these two indicators will allow us to
compute reliable total power and energy estimates of a battery pack.
4.1.1
Total capacity
The total cell capacity Q of a battery cell decreases as it ages. In lithium ion cell it is mainly
due to unwanted side reactions that consume lithium which could have been used for
charging and discharging of the cell. Another cause for loss of total capacity is the struc-
tural deterioration of the electrode active materials in the cell. Figure 4.1 shows the ideal
cell behavior. There are nine sites where lithium could be held. Presently four negative
and four positive electrode sites are occupied. When the cell is fully charged the negative
electrode would be fully filled while the positive electrode would be empty. Similarly
when the cell is fully discharged the positive electrode would have all nine sites filled
while the all sites in the negative electrode would be empty.

4.1.2
Equivalent series resistance
When the cell ages, its equivalent series resistance (ESR) increases. This is also due to
structural deterioration and side reactions. This side reactions form resistive films on the
active material particle which impedes ionic conductivity. While structural deterioration
disrupts electronic pathways between particles which decreases the electronic conductiv-
ity.
As resistance and power are so closely connected, a rise in the resistance in a cell is called
a power fade. So ESR is a major contributing factor and having an up to date knowledge
about it becomes important especially regarding power calculations. It can be a major
factor in contributing to SOC estimation using voltage based methods but it does not affect
coulomb counting methods.

• It computes for measurement errors in both x and y.
• It doesn’t allow σ2
x1 and σ2

Out of all the three solutions AWTLS gives the best estimate:
• They also give a closed from solution.
• The solution can be computed recursively.
• Fading memory can be easily added.
• Allows individual weighing for xi and yi data points.

Theory
Machine learning is a method of extracting knowledge from data. It includes the fields

Machine learning outside of commercial applications has influence in the way the data-

5.1.2
Types of supervised learning
There are mainly two types of supervised machine learning problems called classification
and regression.
In classification the aim is to predict something from a given list of options. Classification
is sometimes divided into binary and multiclass classification which is a classification for
more than two classes. Binary classification can thought of as yes/no answer. Classifying
email as spam would come under this category as there are only two options ”spam” and
”not spam”.
For regression, the aim is to predict a continuous number or number of objects. Prediction
prices of houses rising or falling through the years is a regression classification. When
predicting house prices the amount could be in any given range. Another example could
be predicting a person’s annual income from their given age, education and place of their
living.
An easy way to distinguish between classification and regression would be to see if there
is some continuity in the output. If there is a continuity like when predicting the annual
income then the given problems is that of regression.

The second type of machine learning technique is called unsupervised leaning technique.
Unsupervised learning is used when there is no known output and the algorithm cannot

5.1.4
Types of unsupervised learning
There are two types of unsupervised learning called transformations and clustering.
In unsupervised transformation, the algorithm creates a new representation of a dataset
which might be easier for humans or other learning algorithms to use. A common use is
called dimensionality reduction, which converts a high dimensional representation of the
data into a lower dimensional data with fewer features. This application is usually seen
in reduction to two dimensions for visualization purposes.
Clustering on the other hand divide given data into distinct groups of similar items. An
example would be clustering of photos on a social media site in different groups like home,
vacation, work, etc. The algorithm uses location when these photos were clicked and uses
that information to divide them.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and further work
6.1
Conclusion and some learnings
The work done here was to analyze the lithium ion batteries for their correct prediction
if required for data driven procedure. And in the end to try and use these data driven
techniques to see how well they actually work.
The first step was to analyze the basic building block which is a cell. Starting from the basic
model of just using open circuit voltage to predict the SOC. Then the modeling went on
to add certain parameters like series resistance, hysteresis voltage and diffusion voltage
to make the model more realistic. In the end the final model called the enhanced self
correcting model was made. We saw certain graphs from the model and compared them
with the actual data. Some plots like OCV vs SOC at different temperatures helped us to
understand the role the internal temperature plays.
Then we saw the voltage based and the current based methods to calculate the SOC. As
neither of them tell the whole story, we needed a method that combines them together. To
monitor the dynamic part of the cell model we needed to delve on the probability density
function as the state of a cell is not a given specific quantity but can be found as there is
a probability to find it. These Gaussian processes lead us to a linear Kalman filter but as
they cannot be used for non linear models like a battery cell they had to be improved and
we got an extended Kalman filter. The extended Kalman filter worked well but could be
further improved.
For health estimation we need a method to find the total capacity of the battery. If we can
predict how the total capacity decreases over time then we can comment on the health of
the battery. There was a comparison of three methods, weighted least squares, weighted
total least squares and approximate weighted total least squares. It was shown that the
approximate weighted total least squares works the best.
In the final chapter we saw that trees are good machine learning algorithms to use regres-
sion techniques on battery data. The only problem was that voltage arrays were estimated
instead of total capacity which was hard to calculate due to the size of the arrays. More
computational power would be ideal for that kind of calculation.
