Abstract 
Xylitol is a five-carbon sugar polyol that has gained recent attention for its use as an artificial 
sweetener, as it possesses various advantageous characteristics. Currently, xylitol is produced 
industrially in an unsustainable process and thus biological xylitol production is an attractive 
alternative. Biological xylitol production has been done with various organisms, including the 
extremeophilic, CTG clade yeast Debaryomyces nepalensis. Approaches that focused on the 
improvement of D. nepalensis xylitol yields have so far been limited to fermentation optimisation 
and modelling. In an attempt to increase xylitol production in D. nepalensis, a CRISPR/Cas9 system 
from Saccharomyces cerevisiae was employed. However, transformation of D. nepalensis proved to 
be a major bottleneck. Nevertheless, a foundation for genetic engineering in D. nepalensis has been 
laid, and future efforts might overcome the transformation bottleneck. In turn, this could allow for 
improved xylitol yields in D. nepalensis using genetic engineering strategies, and ultimately lead to 
the establishment of a viable, biological xylitol production process.

1 Introduction 
Xylitol is a five-carbon sugar polyol that is as sweet as sucrose but has a 33% lower calorie rate. As it 
furthermore possesses anti-caries characteristics, it has been used as an artificial sweetener in a 
wide variety of food products such as chewing gums, candies, and toothpaste (Mäkinen, 2011). 
Recently, xylitol has attracted considerable attention, as it was listed as one of the twelve most 
important value-added chemicals from biomass by the US Department of Energy (Werpy et al., 
2004). The current annual worldwide xylitol market is estimated at 125,000 tons at a price of 4.5-5.5 
US dollars per kg (Albuquerque et al., 2014). Although xylitol naturally occurs in some fruits and 
vegetables, it does so only in small amounts (Granström et al., 2007a). Hence, xylitol is produced at 
an industrial scale in a process that involves the reduction of xylose using a metal catalyst under high 
hydrogen pressure and high temperature (Yadav et al., 2012). Xylose is the second most abundant 
sugar in nature (Jeffries, 2006), and the xylose that is used for industrial scale xylitol production is 
derived from lignocellulosic hydrolysates (Albuquerque et al., 2014). However, only pure xylose can 
be used for this process, resulting in a relatively poor total xylose to xylitol conversion yield of 50-
60% (Granstom et al., 2007b). Furthermore, this industrial process is unsustainable, as it is very 
energy intensive (Yadav et al., 2012). Consequently, many efforts have focused on biological xylitol 
production, which offers a more sustainable alternative to the industrial process (Albuquerque et al., 
2014). An overview of the structural formula of xylitol is provided in Figure 1. 
 
 
Figure 1. Structural formula of xylitol. Reproduced from Tong et al., 2007. 
 
Xylitol production has been observed in a wide variety of organisms, such as in the bacterium 
Escherichia coli (Su et al., 2015), the filamentous fungus Trichoderma reesei (Wang et al., 2005), and 
in yeasts (see below). Many of these species have been utilised in attempts to establish a cost-
effective biological xylitol production process, and even a microbial consortium of Bacillus subtilis 
and Candida maltosa has been proven successful (Cheng et al., 2011). However, yeasts possess 
certain advantageous characteristics concerning the biological production of fine chemicals that are 
not observed in bacteria and filamentous fungi. For example, their unicellular form is easy to 
cultivate, scale-up, and harvest (Jeffries, 2006; Riley et al., 2016). In addition, their thick cell walls 
help them survive harsh process conditions. Furthermore, unlike bacteria, yeasts are resistant to 
viruses (Jeffries, 2006; Riley et al., 2016). Consequently, yeasts can be applied for a wide variety of 
biotechnological processes, from traditional sugar fermentation to environmental applications such 
as bioremediation (Johnson, 2013). The scope of biological xylitol production in this study will thus 
==PAGE:7==
7 
 
be limited to yeasts; the reader is kindly referred to reviews about xylitol production in other 
organisms (Sasaki et al., 2012; Pal et al., 2015; Dasgupta et al., 2017). 
Among yeasts, xylitol production has been observed in native xylitol-producing yeasts such as 
Debaryomyces species (Nobre et al., 1999) and Candida species (Granström et al., 2007a), as well as 
in recombinant Saccharomyces cerevisiae (Oh et al., 2013; Wei et al., 2013; Jo et al., 2015). 
Traditionally, S. cerevisiae has been the best-studied yeast, and it is considered as a conventional 
yeast species. On the other hand, most native xylitol-producing yeasts can be safely classified as 
non-conventional yeasts. Non-conventional yeasts have remained underexplored largely due the 
lack of elaborate genetic engineering tools (Riley et al., 2016; Wagner et al., 2016). Although 
progress has been made in this field recently, for example for Candida guilliermondii (Papon et al., 
2013), Candida tropicalis (Zhang et al., 2016), and Pichia pastoris (Kang et al., 2017), the genetic 
engineering toolbox of most non-conventional yeasts is still not as well-developed as that of a model 
yeast such as S. cerevisiae. Thus, in order to unlock the potential of non-conventional yeasts, genetic 
engineering tools must first be developed (Riley et al., 2016). 
 
1.1 Project aim 
The overall purpose of this study is to establish an efficient biological xylitol production method 
using the yeast Debaryomyces nepalensis. Since there are no elaborate genetic engineering tools 
available for this species, these tools will be developed. Consequently, these tools will be applied for 
metabolic engineering of D. nepalensis in order to increase its xylitol production.

2 Background 
2.1 The Debaryomyces genus 
2.1.1 Phylogeny 
The ascomycetous Debaryomyces genus belongs to the family of Debaryomycesyomycetaceae in the 
order of Saccharomycetales (Lopandic et al., 2013). It is a highly diverse genus, and Debaryomyces 
species have been isolated from a wide variety of environments, such as from Arctic glaciers (Jaques 
et al., 2015), coal mines (Saluja and Prasad, 2007), tobacco fermentations (Vigliotta et al., 2007), and 
from a wide variety of food items (Breuer and Harms, 2006). As it is such a diverse genus, the 
classification of individual Debaryomyces species has been the subject of discussion for some time. 
For example, Debaryomyces hansenii and Debaryomyces fabryi were initially classified as two 
distinct species (Kreger van Rij and Veenhuis, 1975). Later, they were reclassified to a single species, 
although two sub-varieties were installed: D. hansenii var. hansenii and D. hansenii var. fabryi 
(Nakase and Suzuki, 1985). However, now they are considered as distinct species again (Groenewald 
et al., 2008; Jaques et al., 2009; Nguyen et al., 2009). The entire process was further complicated by 
the existence of anamorphs for both species, Candida famata var. famata for D. hansenii var. 
hansenii and Candida famata var. flarerii for D. hansenii var. fabryi (Groenewald et al., 2008). 
Traditionally, classification of Debaryomyces species was performed using techniques such as 
ribosomal DNA (rDNA) analysis, maximum growth temperature analysis, or determination of the 
activity of glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PDH) (Lopandic et al., 2013). Recent advances in 
molecular techniques have allowed for a higher resolution in the identification of individual 
Debaryomyces species, for example through DNA dissociation analysis (Groenewald et al., 2008), 
intron sequence analysis (Jaques et al., 2009; Feng et al., 2014), and PCR fingerprinting (Nguyen et 
al., 2009; Cornet et al., 2011). These studies have also shown that there is a large exchange of 
genetic material in the Debaryomyces genus, providing a possible explanation for its diversity 
(Nguyen et al., 2009). Even more recently, genome sequencing studies and comparative genomics 
have established a strong foundation for phylogenetic research and classification (Dujon et al., 2004; 
Butler et al., 2009; Riley et al., 2016), although only four Debaryomyces genomes have been 
published (see section 2.1.3). Consequently, there is still some controversy about intergenus (Jaques 
et al., 2015) and interspecies classification (Gumá-Cintrón et al., 2015). 
As mentioned previously, the Debaryomyces genus is situated in the order of Saccharomycetales 
(Lopandic et al., 2013), in which three major subdivisions can be observed (Souciet et al., 2009; 
Dujon, 2010). One of these subdivisions contains the oleaginous yeast species Yarrowia lipolytica 
and other Dipodascaceae yeasts. Y. lipolytica has a GC-rich genome that is roughly twice as large as 
that of other hemiascomycetous yeasts (Dujon et al., 2004). Another subdivision contains the 
Saccharomycetaceae yeasts (Souciet et al., 2009; Dujon, 2010). This subdivision is the best 
characterised of the three, as it contains the model organism S. cerevisiae. The last subdivision is 
that of the CTG clade (Souciet et al., 2009; Dujon, 2010), in which the CUG codon is decoded into 
serine instead of into the standard leucine (Massey et al., 2003) (see section 2.1.3). The 
Debaryomyces genus belongs to this CTG clade, as do most Candida species such as Candida 
albicans, Candida tropicalis, and Candida guilliermondii (Dujon, 2010). An overview of the 
phylogenetic relation of yeast species that are relevant for this study is provided in Figure 2.

From all Debaryomyces species, D. hansenii is the only species that has been characterised relatively 
well (Prista et al., 2016), although there still exists a large knowledge gap with for example S. 
cerevisiae. Therefore, in this study, knowledge about D. hansenii (and also from the closely related 
Candida genus) will be extrapolated to Debaryomyces nepalensis. Although this should be done with 
caution, as there is a large interspecies (Jaques et al., 2015) and even intraspecies (Gumá-Cintrón et 
al., 2015) variety in the Debaryomyces genus, no adequate alternative exists. 
 
2.1.2 Biology, morphology, and physiology 
Most Debaryomyces species are haploid (Breuer and Harms, 2006), although diploid and hybrid 
species have also been characterised recently (Lopandic et al., 2013; Jaques et al., 2015). D. hansenii 
is a homothallic yeast, and the haploid variant reproduces vegetatively by multilateral budding, 
whereas sexual reproduction occurs through meiosis and ascospore formation (Dujon et al., 2004; 
Breuer and Harms, 2006). C. famata was previously considered to be the anamorph of D. hansenii 
(Groenewald et al., 2008; Jaques et al., 2009; Nguyen et al., 2009), although more recent research 
has indicated that C. famata and D. hansenii are actually separate species (Dmytruk and Sibirny, 
2012). Whereas some Candida species are dimorphic, as they can form either true hyphae like C. 
albicans does (Sánchez-Martinez and Pérez-Martín, 2001) or pseudohyphae, which are for example 
observed in C. guilliermondii (Papon et al., 2013), hyphae of any sort are mostly absent in the 
Debaryomyces genus. D. hansenii has only been reported to form pseudohyphae in one occasion 
under fairly selective conditions (Cruz et al., 2000). Figure 3 provides an overview of the morphology 
of D. hansenii.

As mentioned previously, the Debaryomyces genus is part of the CTG clade (Dujon, 2010). This clade 
is highly diverse, as there are large differences among its members in for example ploidy, genome 
size, and pathogenicity (Butler et al., 2009). On the other hand, a common feature of CTG clade 
yeasts is their natural ability for xylitol production, which is for example observed in C. tropicalis 
(Granström et al., 2001) and C. guilliermondii (Granström and Leisola, 2002). Indeed, D. hansenii and 
D. nepalensis also naturally produce xylitol (see section 2.2). Another common feature of D. hansenii 
and certain Candida species is their Crabtree-negative nature (Sánchez et al., 2006; Diaz-Ruiz et al., 
2011) and oleaginicity (Breuer and Harms, 2006; Lamers et al., 2016). However, D. hansenii is in 
many aspects an oddity among CTG clade yeasts. For example, most Candida species have been 
shown to display at least some pathogenic effects (Butler et al., 2009), the most pathogenic species 
being C. albicans (Noble and Johnson, 2007). In contrast, the Debaryomyces genus is mostly 
regarded as non-pathogenic (Desnos-Ollivier et al., 2008; Butler et al., 2009), although some recent 
reports have emerged concerning infections caused by Debaryomyces species (Moretti et al., 2007; 
Tafer et al., 2016). However, as the classification and nomenclature of yeasts is still under revision 
(Hittinger et al., 2015) and as Debaryomyces species and Candida species have traditionally been 
hard to distinguish, it is still unclear if these infections are truly caused by Debaryomyces species 
(Prista et al., 2016). 
D. hansenii furthermore stands out in the CTG clade because of its extremeophilic characteristics. 
For example, it displays xerophilic and osmotolerant behaviour (Breuer and Harms, 2006), as well as 
tolerance to high concentrations of chlorine dioxide (Ramírez-Orozco et al., 2001) and other 
inhibitory compounds such as penconazole, benomyl, and cycloheximide (Breuer and Harms, 2006), 
and resistance to heavy metals (Gumá-Cintrón et al., 2015). Furthermore, D. hansenii can tolerate a 
wide range of pH (3.0-10; Breuer and Harms, 2006) and temperature (0-35 °C; Yaguchi et al., 2017). 
Although some Candida species are also considered as halotolerant (Gunde-Cimerman et al., 2009), 
halotolerance is a key characteristic of D. hansenii (Adler et al., 1985; André et al., 1988). D. hansenii 
can tolerate concentrations of up to 4 M NaCl, whereas growth of S. cerevisiae is for example 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
restricted to 1.7 M NaCl (Gunde-Cimerman et al., 2009). The growth of D. hansenii is actually 
enhanced on media containing 1 M NaCl as compared to media without NaCl (Breuer and Harms, 
2006).  
Because of its halotolerance, D. hansenii has served as a model organism for studying the 
mechanistic and molecular basis for halotolerance in yeast (Breuer and Harms, 2006; Prista et al., 
2016). Under salt stress, yeasts accumulate osmolytes, particularly glycerol (Gunde-Cimerman et al., 
2009). Glycerol was indeed found to be the major osmolyte in D. hansenii, and its production 
increased with increasing NaCl concentrations (Adler et al., 1985; André et al., 1988, Thomé, 2007). 
Recently, the enzymes responsible for glycerol production in D. hansenii, glycerol 3-phosphate 
dehydrogenase 1 (GPD1), and glycerol 3-phosphatase (GPP2), have been isolated and characterised 
(Thomé, 2004; Gori et al., 2005; Thomé, 2005). Besides glycerol accumulation, a variety of other 
mechanisms are available to D. hansenii to deal with salt stress. For example, a wide variety of 
transporters exist that contribute to ion homeostasis (Prista et al., 2016), and D. hansenii also has 
the ability to store sodium in dedicated vacuoles (Herrera et al., 2017) and to alter the composition 
of its plasma membrane (Turk et al., 2007). Moreover, D. hansenii displays an unusual tolerance to 
intracellular sodium, which is why it has been described as a sodium includer yeast (Prista et al., 
2005). 
Debaryomyces species are also extremeophilic in their utilization of carbon sources (Nakase and 
Suzuki, 1985). For example, D. hansenii readily assimilates xylose, glycerol, galactose, as well as more 
unconventional carbon sources such as cellobiose, soluble starch, and n-alkanes (Yadav and Loper, 
1999). In addition, D. hansenii is able to assimilate inorganic ammonium and nitrite (Nakase and 
Suzuki, 1985). It should be noted that there is a considerable variation in substrate utilization range 
among individual Debaryomyces species (Nakase and Suzuki, 1985; Jaques et al., 2015). 
Because of its extremeophilic characteristics, D. hansenii is able to adapt to a wide range of 
environments. This implies that it can serve as a starting reservoir for the development of other 
extremeophilic species, as various local selective pressures then drive the development of new 
species in order to adapt to specific niches (Jaques et al., 2015). Indeed, the extremeophilic nature of 
the Debaryomyces genus, combined with its rapid exchange of genetic material at the genus level, 
might provide an explanation for the its diversity that is observed between species (Jaques et al., 
2015) between strains (Gumá-Cintrón et al., 2015). The molecular basis for the extremeophilic 
characteristics of D. hansenii will be further explored in the next section. 
 
2.1.3 Systems biology 
Knowledge about the molecular biology of the Debaryomyces genus was poorly developed until D. 
hansenii was included in the Génolevures project (Feldmann, 2000). In this project, thirteen 
hemiascomycetous yeast species were subjected to genomic exploration. Consequently, the first 
sequenced genome in the Debaryomyces genus was that of D. hansenii CBS 767 (Dujon et al., 2004). 
Recently, three other Debaryomyces genome sequences have become available: that of the salt 
tolerant strain D. hansenii MTCC 234 (Kumar et al., 2012), the heavy metal tolerant strain D. hansenii 
J6 (Berrocal et al., 2016), and of the species Debaryomyces fabryi (CBS789; Tafer et al., 2016). In 
addition, the genome of the closely related species Scheffersomyces stipitis (Pichia stipitis) has been 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
published (Jeffries et al., 2007), as well that of various Candida species such as C. albicans, C. 
tropicalis, and C. guilliermondii (Butler et al., 2009). 
The availability of various yeast genome sequences has led to comparative genomics studies (Dujon 
et al., 2004; Butler et al., 2009; Souciet et al., 2009; Dujon, 2010; Riley et al., 2016), which has 
resulted in an increase of knowledge about the systems biology of D. hansenii. Several interesting 
properties have been observed that all contribute to D. hansenii’s ability for rapid genetic alteration 
and thus might form an explanation for its extremeophilic characteristics (Prista et al., 2016). An 
illustration of some of these properties is provided in Figure 4. For example, of all the yeasts that 
have been explored in the Génolevures project, D. hansenii has the highest coding capacity (79.2 % 
of the entire genome) and the highest number of putative coding sequences (6,397) (Dujon, 2010). 
Furthermore, the D. hansenii genome contains a large gene redundancy of around 50% and thus a 
large number of paralogues, although no wholegenome duplication (WGD) event has taken place, as 
is the case for S. cerevisiae (Souciet et al., 2009; Dujon, 2010). Many of the paralogues are 
furthermore organised in tandem gene arrays (TGAs), an aspect that occurs much more frequently in 
D. hansenii than in other hemiascomycetous yeasts (Dujon et al., 2004). This organization likely 
provides an advantage for organisms when it comes to rapidly adapting to a new environment, as 
these TGAs are fertile breeding grounds for novel gene functions (Despons et al., 2011).

Large genomic redundancy results in the existence of paralogous proteins. (B) Ambiguous decoding 
of the CUG codon results in a varied proteome. (C) A high frequency of mitochrondial DNA 
incorporation into nuclear DNA causes ‘de novo’ gene formation. Adapted from Prista et al., 2016.

As mentioned previously, the Debaryomyces genus is part of the CTG clade of yeasts (Dujon, 2010). 
In this clade, the CUG codon is not decoded into the standard leucine (Massey et al., 2003). Instead, 
it is translated into serine because of the existence of a serine-tRNA containing a leucine 5’-CAG-3’ 
anticodon (Ser-tRNACAG) (Massey, et al., 2003). The existence of this Ser-tRNACAG is specific for the 
CTG clade, and thus a total number of 43 tRNA genes are present in D. hansenii, as opposed to the 
total of 42 tRNA genes in S. cerevisiae (Dujon et al., 2004). However, the CUG codon can also still be 
decoded into the canonical leucine, although with low efficiency (3%), because the Ser-tRNACAG can 
also be charged with leucine (Miranda et al., 2007; Santos et al., 2011). Because of this unambiguous 
CUG decoding, a distinctly varied proteome is observed in D. hansenii (Santos et al., 2011). This in 
turn might also contribute to the extremeophilic characteristics of D. hansenii. It should be noted 
here though that unlike the aforementioned properties this phenomenon is observed in the entire 
CTG clade and is thus not specific for D. hansenii. 
Another feature of the genomic organization in D. hansenii that might contribute to its 
extremeophilic nature is the ability to rapidly evolve de novo gene functions (Dujon, 2010). D. 
hansenii contains a large number of genes that do not have direct homologues in other species, even 
in the most closely related species (Souciet et al., 2009). Consequently, these genes may have been 
obtained either through horizontal gene transfer or through mutational changes (Dujon, 2010). 
Indeed, in D. hansenii it has been observed that the frequency of mitochrondial DNA (mtDNA) 
transfer to the nucleus is very high, significantly higher than in other hemiascomycetous yeasts 
(Sacerdot et al., 2008; Dujon, 2010).  
 
2.1.4 Genetic engineering 
Although some D. hansenii genome sequences have been published (see section 2.1.3) and a large 
number of genes have been annotated, only a handful of genes and proteins from the 
Debaryomyces genus have been thoroughly characterised. This can be explained by the absence of a 
fully developed genetic engineering toolbox for D. hansenii and the Debaryomyces genus in general 
(Prista et al., 2016). Indeed, most of the genes that have been characterised up to this point have 
been done so through complementation studies using S. cerevisiae. For example, this is case for 
several D. hansenii genes that contribute to its halotolerance (Thomé, 2004; Gori et al., 2005; 
Thomé, 2005). However, these complementation studies could for example be hindered by the 
differential CUG decoding in D. hansenii as opposed to that in S. cerevisiae (see section 2.1.3). There 
is thus a clear requirement to establish a genetic engineering toolbox for the Debaryomyces genus 
(Prista et al., 2016; Yaguchi et al., 2017). A toolbox like this has for example recently been developed 
for other non-conventional yeasts such as C. guilliermondii (Papon et al., 2013), C. tropicalis (Zhang 
et al., 2016), or P. pastoris (Kang et al., 2017). Although some attempts have been made in D. 
hansenii, this has not yet resulted in for example well-characterised promoter libraries, reporter 
genes, or standardised integration sites. 
Ricaurte and Govind made initial attempts to establish a genetic engineering system for D. hansenii 
(Ricaurte and Govind, 1999). It was observed that D. hansenii was sensitive to hygromycin B, an 
antibiotic commonly used in yeast as a dominant selection marker (Kaster et al., 1984). This 
observation was thus exploited by the transformation of a plasmid that confers hygromycin 
resistance in S. cerevisiae into D. hansenii using electroporation (Ricaurte and Govind, 1999). 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
However, very low transformation efficiencies were obtained, even when native D. hansenii 
autonomously replicating sequences (ARS) that had been isolated previously were included in this 
plasmid. However, this might be explained by the fact that a portion of the promoter driving the 
expression of the hygromycin resistance gene had been deleted (Ricaurte and Govind, 1999). Higher 
transformation efficiencies were obtained when the S. cerevisiae URA3 gene was used for 
transformation in a D. hansenii URA3- strain (Ricaurte and Govind, 1999). This D. hansenii URA3- 
strain was also used for the characterization of D. hansenii’s potential for heterologous protein 
expression using GFP under control of several S. cerevisiae inducible promoters and the endogenous 
GPD1 promoter (Maggi and Govind, 2004). Although the endogenous GPD1 promoter caused the 
strongest GFP expression in D. hansenii, this was still lower than the GFP expression that was 
observed in S. cerevisiae, possibly because the URA3 marker and terminator that were used in D. 
hansenii originated from S. cerevisiae.  
Other attempts to establish a genetic engineering system were made using a HIS4 auxotrophic 
marker system in D. hansenii (Minhas et al., 2009). The HIS4- mutant strain was isolated following 
ultraviolet (UV) irradiation. The HIS4 gene was also isolated from D. hansenii genomic DNA, as well 
as several ARS, resulting in a dedicated D. hansenii vector. This vector was subsequently transformed 
in D. hansenii using electroporation, which led to relatively high transformation efficiencies (4*104 
transformants/μg DNA). Consequently, this vector was used for a successful disruption of an 
endogenous gene using HR, of which it was the first case in the Debaryomyces genus (Minhas et al., 
2009). 
As mentioned in section 2.1.2, C. famata was long considered to be the anamorph of D. hansenii, but 
it has recently been reclassified as a separate species (Dmytruk and Sibirny, 2012). Nonetheless, 
attempts that have been made to establish a genetic engineering for C. famata can probably be 
safely extrapolated to D. hansenii, as ARS from C. famata have been shown to function properly in D. 
hansenii (Minhas et al., 2009). A genetic engineering system was established in C. famata based on a 
leucine auxotrophic strain and complementation using the S. cerevisiae LEU2 gene (Voronovsky et 
al., 2002). Moreover, native ARS were isolated, and high transformation efficiencies were observed 
using either spheroplast transformation (6.3*104 transformants/μg DNA) and electroporation (1*105 
transformants/μg DNA). Another common yeast transformation method, lithium acetate 
transformation (Gietz and Schiestl, 2007), was successfully applied in C. guilliermondii (Boretsky et 
al., 2007), although not in D. hansenii. 
 
2.1.5 Biotechnology 
The extremeophilic characteristics of D. hansenii are highly advantageous in biotechnological 
applications (Breuer and Harms, 2006; Yaguchi et al., 2017). For example, the combination of its 
halotolerance, wide range of pH, and resistance to chlorine dioxide could allow for semi-sterile 
production conditions, as D. hansenii would likely be the only organism that is capable of growing 
under these conditions. This is further strengthened by the fact that D. hansenii produces killer 
toxins that are capable of limiting the growth of for example S. cerevisiae (Gunge et al., 1993) and 
Candida species (Banjara et al., 2016). Its halotolerance is furthermore useful because it allows for 
the utilisation of cheap, salt-containing wastestreams, such as that of rapeseed oil production 
(Breuer and Harms, 2006) and that of desalination effluents (Yaguchi et al., 2017) as a substrate. 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
Furthermore, its osmotolerance allows for high product concentrations (Breuer and Harms, 2006). 
All these characteristics of D. hansenii would aid significantly in reducing production costs and 
process development costs (Breuer and Harms, 2006; Yaguchi et al., 2017). Another beneficial 
feature of D. hansenii when it comes to establishing a bioprocess is its generally recognised as safe 
(GRAS) status (Dasgupta et al., 2017). 
As the genetic engineering toolbox for D. hansenii has remained underexplored, not that many 
studies have been aimed at the production or expression of heterologous products. Heterologous 
expression in D. hansenii is further complicated by its alternative CUG decoding, as explained in 
section 2.1.2. Instead, most studies have thus focused on natural products of D. hansenii (Yaguchi et 
al., 2017). For example, its oleaginous nature has been exploited for the production of lipases 
(Papagora et al., 2003), and it could provide a basis for fatty acid production (Breuer and Harms, 
2006) or that of single-cell oils (Johnson, 2013). Other enzymes have been produced in D. hansenii, 
such as β-glucosidases (Saha and Bothast, 1996) and esterases (Besancon et al., 1985). In addition, 
under iron starvation, D. hansenii produces moderate amounts of riboflavin (vitamin B2) (Seda-Miró 
et al., 2007). However, the most famous fine chemical originating from the Debaryomyces genus is 
xylitol, whose production will be further explored in section 2.2. 
D. hansenii is one of the most important yeasts in the food industry (Breuer and Harms, 2006). For 
example, it is the most prevalent yeast in cheese (Fleet, 1990) and in a wide variety of sausages 
(Dalton et al., 1984). Its halotolerance, osmotolerance, and xerotolerance makes it ideally suited for 
these types of environments (Breuer and Harms, 2006). Moreover, D. hansenii contributes to the 
flavour of cheese by synthesizing for example volatile acids and sulphurous compounds (Arfi et al., 
2007), and it can indeed be used as a starter culture (Gori et al., 2012). In meat fermentation, D. 
hansenii fulfils a similar function (Flores et al., 2004), as well as being involved in the ripening 
process (Cano-García et al., 2014). In addition to directly contributing to the product quality, D. 
hansenii can control the production microenvironment, as it can produce killer toxins that target 
unwanted spoilage organisms, as well as secrete vitamins and amino acids that can be utilised by 
fermenting bacteria (Breuer and Harms, 2006). Although it should be noted that D. hansenii can also 
be involved in the food spoilage, it nevertheless is a beneficial yeast for the food industry (Breuer 
and Harms, 2006). 
 
2.1.6 Debaryomyces nepalensis 
Debaryomyces nepalensis was initially isolated from soil in Nepal (Goto and Sugiyama, 1968). This 
first strain (CBS 5921) has, however, not been characterised. Another D. nepalensis strain (NCYC 
3413) was isolated from a rotten apple in a fruit market in India (Gummadi and Kumar, 2006). Strain 
D. nepalensis NCYC 3413 was classified based on 26S rDNA D1/D2 sequence analysis with a 99.8% 
sequence identity to D. nepalensis CBS 5921 (Gummadi and Kumar, 2006). Strain D. nepalensis NCYC 
3413 (hereafter: D. nepalensis) has subsequently been fairly well characterised, as will be explained 
below. Figure 5 provides an illustration of D. nepalensis morphology, and a standard D. nepalensis 
growth curve is depicted in Figure 6.

2.2 Xylose and xylitol metabolism 
2.2.1 Transport and pathway 
As mentioned in section 1.1, xylose is the second most abundant sugar in nature (Jeffries, 2006). 
Consequently, plenty of yeasts have evolved the ability to assimilate xylose (Pal et al., 2015; 
Dasgupta et al., 2017). However, it has been widely established that most yeasts preferentially utilise 
glucose as a carbon source, and thus most studies on sugar transporters have focused on glucose 
transporters (Ferreira et al., 2013). Some of the glucose transporters are able to also transport 
xylose into the cell through facilitated diffusion (Jeon et al., 2011). However, in a given transporter,  
the rate of glucose transport is often much higher than the rate of xylose transport, as has been 
observed for a variety of native xylose consuming yeast (Nobre et al, 1999; Ferreira et al., 2013). In 
addition, the transport of xylose is repressed in the presence of glucose, a phenomenon known as 
carbon catabolite repression (CCR) (Gancedo, 1998). Another pattern in native xylose-consuming 
yeasts is that an alternative, dedicated xylose/H+ symporter exists (Nobre et al., 1999; Ferreira et al., 
2013). For its expression, both the presence of xylose and the absence of glucose are required. This 
transporter was shown to be active for example in D. hansenii and D. fabryi (Nobre et al, 1999; 
Ferreira et al., 2013), and xylose transport even increased in the presence of NaCl (Nobre et al., 
1999). An explanation for this effect was provided: Na+ flux generates an ion potential that 
consequently allows an increase of xylose/H+ symport (Ferreira et al., 2013). 
Once xylose has entered the cell, it is reduced by xylose reductase (XR, EC: 1.1.1.307, encoded by the 
XYL1 gene) to xylitol, using either NADPH or NADH as a cofactor (Hahn-Hägerdal et al., 1994). Xylitol 
then either crosses the cell membrane or it is oxidised by xylitol dehydrogenase (XDH, EC: 1.1.1.9, 
encoded by the XYL2 gene) to xylulose. Almost all XDHs are specific for NAD+ (Hahn-Hägerdal et al., 
1994). Although the passage of xylitol through the cell membrane is still under discussion (Sasaki et 
al., 2012, Su 2013), it is perhaps exported through FPS1 (Wei et al., 2013), a protein channel involved 
in glycerol export in S. cerevisiae. Xylulose is phosphorylated by xylulose kinase (XK, EC: 2.7.1.17, 
encoded by the XYL3 gene), resulting in the formation of xylulose-5-phosphate (Hahn-Hägerdal et 
al., 1994). Xylulose-5-phosphate then enters into the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP). 
Alternatively, when XK is absent, xylulose can be converted into arabitol and then into ribulose-5-
phosphate, which then enters the PPP (Jin et al., 2003; Jin et al., 2005). However, this alternative 
pathway for xylose assimilation has a much lower efficiency (Jin et al., 2005). An overview of xylose 
metabolism is provided in Figure 7.

2.2.2 Importance of cofactor balance 
In most native xylitol-producing yeasts such as D. hansenii (Nobre et al., 1999) and C. guilliermondii 
(Granström and Leisola, 2002), XR is exclusively specific for NADPH, and XDH is exclusively specific 
for NAD+. This results in a cofactor imbalance under oxygen-limited conditions, as NADH 
accumulates as it cannot be reoxidised by the respiratory chain (Hahn-Hägerdal et al., 1994), and 
there is most likely no direct transhydrogenase activity in yeast (Bruinenberg et al., 1985). Excess 
NADH inhibits the XDH-mediated oxidation of xylitol into xylulose (Granström et al., 2007a), and thus 
xylitol accumulates. Xylitol does not accumulate under aerobic conditions, as there is no cofactor 
imbalance. 
It has been widely established that yeasts can oxidise excess NADH through the formation of ethanol 
and glycerol. This phenomenon has also been observed in native xylitol-producing yeasts, although 
unlike in S. cerevisiae, it is induced by oxygen limitation and not by excess glucose availability (Nobre 
et al., 2002). However, the reoxidation of NADH under anaerobic conditions is only possible when 
glucose is used as a carbon source, not when xylose is used. This is because more NADH is generated 
in the catabolism of xylose because of the cofactor imbalance in XR and XDH than the amount of 
NADH that is generated in the catabolism of glucose (Bruinenberg et al., 1983). A fitting redox 
balance would require the formation of one mol of glycerol per mol of consumed xylose. 
Consequently, not enough xylose can be directed to ethanol, resulting in a shortage of ATP 
(Bruinenberg et al., 1983).  
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
As mentioned previously, the cofactor specificities of XR and XDH and the availability of oxygen are 
critical parameters in the production of xylitol (Hahn-Hägerdal et al., 1994). This is the case for both 
S. cerevisiae and native xylitol-producing yeasts. Some species possess XRs with dual cofactor 
specificity, so XR can for example also use NADH as a cofactor to reduce xylose. When dual cofactor 
specificity is observed, in most cases a single enzyme is responsible for this, and there still exists a 
certain preference for either NADPH or NADH as a cofactor (Lee et al., 2003). However, as an 
exception to this rule, two XR-coding genes were found in Pachysolen tannophilus, both having a 
different cofactor specificity (Verduyn et al., 1985).  
The XR of S. stipitis possesses dual cofactor specificity for NADPH and NADH, whereas its XDH is 
specific for NAD+ (Cho and Jeffries, 1998). During xylose fermentation under oxygen-limited 
conditions, S. stipitis does not produce xylitol. Instead, it produces ethanol. This can be explained by 
the fact that its XR also utilizes NADH as a cofactor. The excess NADH that arises under oxygen-
limited conditions can thus more easily be reoxidised, alleviating the cofactor imbalance (Cho and 
Jeffries, 1998; Bruinenberg et al., 1984). In turn, this causes an increase in xylose assimilation and 
ethanol production. Ethanol production has also been observed in C. tropicalis, which has the same 
XR and XDH cofactor specificities as S. stipitis has (Granström and Leisola, 2002). Furthermore, C. 
maltosa also produces ethanol from xylose, although its XR is specific for NADPH (Lin et al., 2010). 
However, its XDH can use NADP+ as a cofactor. This combination of XR and XDH cofactor specificities 
thus also alleviates the cofactor imbalance, which explains why C. maltosa produces ethanol. In S. 
stipitis, the ethanol production is increased when oxygen is present, suggesting that despite the fact 
that S. stipitis’ XR is partly NADH dependent, it is still more NADPH favoured (Cho and Jeffries 1998). 
Indeed, the extent to which ethanol is produced from xylose by yeasts is probably also related to the 
enzymatic activities of XR and XDH.  
 
2.2.3 Strategies to improve xylitol production 
A wide variety of strategies has been applied in order to improve xylitol production, both for native 
xylitol-producing yeasts and for S. cerevisiae (Pal et al., 2015; Park et al., 2016; Dasgupta et al., 
2017). S. cerevisiae is unable to assimilate xylose as its native xylose utilization genes are poorly 
expressed (Toivari et al., 2004). Therefore, S. cerevisiae has been engineered to express these genes 
for xylose consumption and xylitol formation (Oh et al., 2013; Wei et al., 2013; Jo et al., 2015). 
Although good yields have been obtained, S. cerevisiae might not be the ideal organism for xylitol 
production, as it for example lacks the halotolerance of D. hansenii (Adler et al., 1985; André et al., 
1988) or D. nepalensis (Gummadi et al., 2007). This study thus focuses on the strategies that have 
been applied in native xylitol-producing yeasts in order to increase the xylitol production. An 
overview of all applied strategies is provided in Table 1.

2.2.3.1 Metabolic engineering 
Traditionally, the tools that are required for metabolic engineering have been better developed for 
conventional yeasts such as S. cerevisiae. In addition, because there is a wealth of genomic, 
transcriptomic, and metabolomic data available for S. cerevisiae, models can be constructed that 
facilitate the rational design of metabolic engineering strategies (Sánchez and Nielsen, 2015). These 
models can range in size from detailed kinetic models to models for metabolic flux analysis (MFA) 
and to genome scale metabolic models (GSMMs), all of which can be used effectively in order to 
design viable metabolic engineering strategies (Santos et al., 2011; Sánchez and Nielsen, 2015). 
Because of a lack of genetic engineering tools for non-conventional yeasts, metabolic engineering 
has only started fairly recently in these species (Granström et al., 2007a). Although there still is a gap 
to conventional yeasts such as S. cerevisiae, genome editing techniques such as CRISPR/Cas9 (see 
section 2.3) can be employed to close such a gap. In turn, an increase in fundamental knowledge and 
functional genomics studies should lead to an increase in adequate models for non-conventional 
yeasts, too. Nevertheless, the attempts to improve xylitol production in non-conventional yeasts 
have so far focused on traditional literature research and hypothesis-driven research, as will be 
discussed below. 
 
2.2.3.1.1 Heterologous expression 
As mentioned in section 2.2.2, the cofactor balance of XR and XDH is very important in xylitol 
production. Indeed, there have been attempts to modify the native cofactor balance of certain 
yeasts through heterologous expression. Most of these efforts have focused on XR, and not so much 
on XDH (Zhang et al., 2015). Lee et al. overexpressed a XR from Candida parapsilosis in C. tropicalis 
(Lee et al., 2003). This XR has a cofactor preference for NADH and demonstrated high activity. 
Consequently, xylitol yield and productivity increased, up to a final productivity of 5.09 g/l/h and a 
final concentration of 275 g/l. Furthermore, the use of this XR with dual specificity also decreased 
the formation of by-products such as ethanol and glycerol because the excess NADH is reoxidised 
through the reduction of xylose by XR. Glycerol and ethanol cause problems in downstream 
processing of xylitol, as further purification steps are required for its isolation (Lee et al., 2003). 
Another attempt was made to overexpress an XR in C. tropicalis, although here the XR was isolated 
from the fungus Neurospora crassa (Jeon et al., 2012). This XR was selected for high activity. The 
XYL1 gene was codon-optimised and the XR was overexpressed, resulting in the generation of strain 
LXU1. This led to an increase of xylitol yield (62%) and productivity (73%), as compared to the parent 
strain. It should be noted however, that the XYL2 in this strain had also been disrupted (Jeon et al., 
2012) (see section 2.2.3.1.2). Strain LXU1 was then further modified through the heterologous 
expression of a xylose transporter from Arabidopsis thaliana (Jeon et al., 2013). This transporter was 
selected for its high affinity for xylose. Like the XR from N. crassa, it was overexpressed, resulting in 
an increase of xylose uptake (29%) and xylitol productivity (25%). Xylitol yield had already 
approached the theoretical maximum, as the XYL2 had already been disrupted in this strain (Jeon et 
al., 2013). 
 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
2.2.3.1.2 Gene disruption and inhibition 
Since xylitol is converted by XDH into xylulose, XYL2 has been the primary target for gene disruption 
attempts. If no xylitol is utilised for cell growth, a theoretical yield of 100% conversion of xylose into 
xylitol could be achieved (Ko et al., 2006). Indeed, XDH activity was disrupted in C. tropicalis (Ko et 
al., 2006), D. hansenii (Biswas et al., 2013; Pal et al., 2015), and Kluyveromyces marxianus (Zhang et 
al., 2014), which resulted in high yields of xylitol in all cases (see Table 1). In these XYL2-disrupted 
strains, the xylitol production is independent of oxygen concentration to establish a cofactor 
imbalance (Ko et al., 2006). Consequently, these strains can be grown under aerobic conditions, 
which results in high growth rates and thus in high xylitol productivities. However, XYL2-disrupted 
strains require a co-substrate for cell growth and cofactor regeneration (see section 2.2.4). 
Therefore, Ko et al. disrupted either only one copy of XYL2 in the diploid yeast C. tropicalis and also 
mutated the XYL2 through site-directed mutagenesis (Ko et al., 2011). This strain still displayed a 
high xylitol yield and productivity, but no longer required a co-substrate for cell growth. 
 
2.2.3.1.3 Increasing cofactor supply 
Other attempts were made to improve xylitol production through increasing the cofactor supply for 
XR. In yeasts, most NADPH is generated in the oxidative PPP (Bruinenberg et al., 1983), and the 
availability of NADPH is rate limiting in xylitol production in for example C. guilliermondii, whose XR 
is specific for NADPH, as acetate accumulates to regenerate NADPH (Granström and Leisola, 2002). 
Thus, a strategy was proposed to increase NADPH regeneration in C. tropicalis (Ahmad et al., 2012). 
The XYL2-disrupted strain (Ko et al., 2006) was engineered to contain the PPP genes ZWF and GND, 
coding for glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PDH) and 6-phosphogluconate dehydrogenase 
(6-PGDH), respectively. This led to an increase of 21% in volumetric xylitol production as compared 
to the parent strain (Ahmad et al., 2012). The same parent strain was used for another attempt to 
increase xylitol production through enhanced NADPH regeneration (Ahmad et al., 2013). Here, the 
GK gene, encoding for glycerol kinase, was disrupted. Simultaneously, the genes GCY1,2,3 from S. 
stipitis, encoding for NADP+-dependent glycerol dehydrogenase, were included. This led to an 
increase in NADPH regeneration from glycerol catabolism, and consequently the volumetric xylitol 
productivity was enhanced by 30% (Ahmad et al., 2013).  
 
2.2.3.2 Strain mutagenesis and adaptation 
All metabolic engineering strategies described above can be considered as rational engineering 
strategies. Improvements in strain performance can also be obtained through more data-driven 
methods. An advantage of these latter methods is that often less resources and/or prior knowledge 
is required to perform them. On the other hand, a possible drawback is the untargeted nature of this 
approach; during random mutagenesis, mutations can arise that for example decrease strain stress 
tolerance.  
Kim et al., 2001 treated S. stipitis with ethyl methanesulfonate (EMS) and screened the generated 
mutants for xylitol production (Kim et al., 2001). An overproducing strain was obtained that lacked 
XDH activity. Consequently, xylose was converted to xylitol with a yield of 100%, although the 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
productivity was not that high. A C. tropicalis strain in which XDH activity was absent was also 
obtained following chemical mutagenesis with nitrosoguanidine (NTG) (Jeon et al., 2011). This strain 
required a co-substrate for cell growth and NADPH regeneration. Fed-batch fermentation under fully 
aerobic conditions with this strain resulted in a high xylitol volumetric productivity and yield (Jeon et 
al., 2011). Interestingly, these results are comparable with that of a C. tropicalis XYL2-disrupted 
strain that was obtained using a rational engineering approach (Ko et al., 2006).  
A different strain of C. tropicalis was also mutagenised with UV and N-methyl-N’-nitro-N-
nitrosoguanidine (MNNG) treatments (Rao et al., 2006). This strain was screened for xylitol 
production, and an increase of 13% in xylitol yield was observed, as compared to the parent strain. 
This increase was caused by an increase in enzymatic activity of XR (Rao et al., 2006). An increase in 
XR activity was also observed in K. marxianus following EMS treatment (Kim et al., 2015). The 
obtained strain was then subjected to directed evolution. Finally, this resulted in an increase of 39% 
in xylitol yield and a 120% increase in xylitol concentration. These increases were caused by an 
increase in total XR activity of 2-fold. Interestingly, NADPH-dependent XR activity as well as NADH-
dependent XR activity increased (Kim et al., 2015). 
The studies mentioned above have all been aimed at direct increases in xylitol yield or productivity. 
However, an important aspect of strain adaptation and mutagenesis is that a large number of 
mutations are generated simultaneously, so a trait can evolve that is not directly attributable to a 
single gene. This was indeed the case as observed in C. maltosa when it was cultivated in corncob 
hemicellulosic hydrolysate (CHH) (Jiang et al., 2016). Lignocellulosic hydrolysates contain inhibitors 
that affect for example cell growth (Albuquerque et al., 2014). A directed evolution approach over 
65 generations resulted in a strain that was tolerant to these inhibitors (Jiang et al., 2016). In turn, 
this strain was able to produce xylitol with a higher yield (56%) and productivity (55%) than its 
parent strain. Genome sequencing of the evolved strain is in process in order to identify the 
mutations that are providing tolerance against the inhibitors that present in CHH (Jiang et al., 2016).  
 
2.2.3.3 Miscellaneous  
As seen in the previous sections, a wide variety of strategies has been applied to increase xylitol 
production in native xylitol-producing yeasts. However, even more studies have been conducted. For 
example, enzyme engineering efforts have focused on XR to increase its NADH cofactor specificity 
(Pal et al., 2015) or on XR to improve its specificity towards xylose (Nair and Zhao, 2008). Another 
approach was to use sodium benzoate in order to increase the xylitol yield in Candida mogii 
(Wannawilai et al., 2014). Sodium benzoate inhibits phosphofructokinase, leading to an increase in 
NADPH regeneration through the PPP (Wannawilai et al., 2014). In addition, process parameters 
such as pH, temperature, or the fermentation method can be modified in order to increase xylitol 
yield and productivity (Pal et al., 2015; Dasgupta et al., 2017). Finally, the use of immobilised cultures 
has also been attempted to stimulate xylitol production in C. guilliermondii (Cortez et al., 2016) and 
in C. tropicalis (Dalli et al., 2017).  
All of the above mentioned strategies have been successfully applied to some extent in order to 
increase xylitol production and/or yield. However, they are beyond the scope of this study, and thus 
==PAGE:25==
25 
 
the reader is kindly referred to other reviews about these subjects (Sasaki et al., 2012; Pal et al., 
2015; Pérez-Bibbins et al., 2016; Dasgupta et al., 2017). 
 
2.2.4 Effect of substrate on xylitol production 
It has been widely established that the choice of substrate affects cell growth, product formation, 
and by-product formation. The growth rate of D. hansenii is for example higher on glucose than on 
xylose, although the cell yield is the same (Nobre et al., 1999). This can for example be explained 
through the observation that the expression of XR and XDH is induced in the presence of xylose 
(Nobre et al., 2002). As mentioned in section 2.2.1, CCR is an important phenomenon, because the 
expression of XR, XDH, and XK is repressed in the presence of glucose, and CCR has been observed in 
most native xylitol-producing yeasts (Guo et al., 2006). Since lignocellulosic hydrolysates are a 
mixture of largely glucose and xylose (Albuquerque et al., 2014), overcoming CCR is important for 
utilising them to the fullest extent. Overcoming CCR can for example be done through the 
heterologous expression of XR (Lee et al., 2003) or through fed-batch approaches (Oh et al., 2013). 
On the other hand, CCR can also be used as an advantage when a heterologous XR is used (Zhang et 
al., 2016). The heterologous XR is unaffected by CCR, whereas the native XDH is affected. 
Consequently, xylose was converted to xylitol with near theoretical yields (98%) in K. marxianus 
while cell growth and cofactor regeneration were supported by glucose catabolism (Zhang et al., 
2016), allowing for efficient use of lignocellulosic hydrolysates. 
As seen in section 2.2.3.1.2, near theoretical yields of xylitol can also be obtained when XYL2 is 
deleted. XYL2-disrupted strains are unable to grow on a medium containing xylose as a single carbon 
source, and require a co-substrate to support cell growth and cofactor regeneration (Kim et al., 
2001; Ko et al., 2006). In D. hansenii, glycerol was found to be the most efficient co-substrate 
(Biswas et al., 2013), although gluconic acid was the most effective in S. stipitis (Kim et al, 2001). 
Glycerol supplementation was also found to be an effective co-substrate in strains with undisrupted 
XYL2 genes to increase xylitol production, as it allows for increased NADPH generation (Arruda and 
Felipe, 2009; Arruda et al., 2015). 
The cost and availability of a substrate is furthermore important in establishing a biobased process 
(Granström et al., 2007b). In this regard, glycerol is moreover a good candidate because it is the 
principle by-product from biodiesel production, amounting up to a total of 10% (w/w) (Yang et al., 
2013). Another cost-effective co-substrate for xylitol production might be molasses, which has been 
shown to increase xylitol production in C. guilliermondii (Hernández-Pérez et al., 2016). 
 
2.2.5 Xylitol production in Debaryomyces nepalensis 
D. nepalensis was originally isolated for the production of pectin lyases and pectate lyases (Gummadi 
and Kumar, 2006; Gummadi and Kumar, 2008).  Subsequently, it has been applied for xylitol 
production from xylose (Kumar and Gummadi, 2010) and from various lignocellulosic hydrolysates 
(Paidimuddala and Gummadi, 2014).  
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
The XR from D. nepalensis has been purified and characterised (Kumar and Gummadi, 2011). It was 
observed to be absolutely specific for NADPH. As mentioned previously, D. nepalensis is a 
halotolerant species (Gummadi et al., 2007). Interestingly, the its also displayed moderate 
halotolerance, as it retained 90% of its activity in the presence of either 100 mM NaCl or 100 mM 
KCl. The enzyme was further used for studying the mechanism of inhibition from inhibitors present 
in lignocellulosic hydrolysates on aldose reductases in general (Paidimuddala et al., 2017).  
Since its XR is strictly specific for NADPH (Kumar and Gummadi, 2011) and its XDH is strictly specific 
for NAD+ (Kumar and Gummadi, 2010), D. nepalensis behaves similarly to most natural xylitol-
producing yeast species in terms of cofactor imbalance induced by oxygen limitation and in its 
subsequent xylitol production. The growth rate of D. nepalensis is higher on glucose than it is on 
xylose, although the cell yield is the same (Kumar and Gummadi, 2010). Utilizing glucose as the sole 
carbon source results in the formation of ethanol, glycerol, and arabitol as the major metabolites. 
On the other hand, D. nepalensis produces xylitol and glycerol from xylose (Kumar and Gummadi, 
2010), as well as small amounts of acetate (Sharon Mano Pappu, personal communication). When 
grown on a mixture of glucose and xylose, D. nepalensis exhibits a diauxic growth pattern, indicating 
that CCR also occurs in this yeast (Kumar and Gummadi, 2010).  
Initial studies of xylitol production in D. nepalensis led to a xylitol yield of 0.51 g/g and a productivity 
of 0.72 g/l/h when pure xylose was used as a substrate (Kumar and Gummadi, 2010). Instead, when 
various lignocellulosic hydrolysates were used, the xylitol yield and productivity decreased to 
approximately 0.30 g/g and 0.20 g/l/h, respectively, depending on the type of hydrolysate that was 
used (Paidimuddala and Gummadi, 2014). In order to improve the xylitol yield and productivity, 
various strategies were employed. For example, optimization of the oxygen concentration, 
combined with a fed-batch approach, resulted in a maximal xylitol yield of 0.64 g/g and a 
productivity of 0.43 g/l/h (Himabindu and Gummadi, 2015). Optimisation of other process 
parameters through statistical approaches resulted in a xylitol yield of 0.58 g/g, a productivity 0.76 
g/l/h, and a final concentration of 59.4 g/l (Pappu and Gummadi, 2016). Machine learning methods 
were then applied to further optimise process parameters, resulting in an increase of the final xylitol 
concentration by another 11% (Pappu and Gummadi, 2017).  
The current xylitol production capacities of D. nepalensis might be less impressive than that of 
various Candida species (see Table 1). However, it should be noted that D. nepalensis was not 
originally isolated for xylitol production. In contrast, a C. tropicalis strain that is used for xylitol 
production was isolated from the sludge of a xylose-manufacturing factory (Oh and Kim, 1998), 
providing a starting advantage over D. nepalensis. Nonetheless, D. nepalensis possesses other 
advantageous characteristics. For example, its halotolerance makes it suitable for harsh process 
conditions and semi-sterile conditions (Breuer and Harms, 2006). In addition, D. hansenii has a GRAS 
status, whereas most Candida species are classified as opportunistic pathogens (Dasgupta et al., 
2017). Finally, genetic engineering strategies to improve xylitol production have already been 
applied to Candida species but not to Debaryomyces species, making it a slightly unfair comparison.

2.3 Genome Editing 
The ability to make accurate genetic modifications to the DNA of an organism is invaluable in 
fundamental as well as applied research. Traditionally, targeted genetic modifications were 
constructed using marker genes that were integrated into the genome using homologous 
recombination (HR) (Capecchi, 1985). However, the efficiency of spontaneous HR is not sufficient in 
a large variety of organisms, as in mammalian cells and plants for example only an efficiency of one 
in a million can be obtained (Durai et al., 2005). This efficiency is dramatically increased when a 
targeted double-strand break (DSB) is generated (Jasin, 1996). The recent advent of genome editing 
techniques has greatly improved the ability to generate targeted DSBs and thus the ability to 
generate targeted genetic modifications. Initial genome editing techniques such as zinc-finger 
nucleases (ZFNs) (Durai et al., 2005) and transcription activator-like effector nucleases (TALENs) 
(Boch et al., 2009) consist of proteins that have both a DNA-binding domain and a nuclease domain. 
Although these techniques have been proven effective, new proteins have to be constructed for 
every DNA sequence that is targeted (Durai et al., 2005; Boch et al., 2009), causing a major 
bottleneck in high-throughput genome editing. Consequently, RNA-guided nucleases such as Cas9 
and Cpf1 and DNA-guided endonucleases such as NgAgo have gained recent attention, as described 
below.  
 
2.3.1 CRISPR/Cas9 
The Clustered Regulatory Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats (CRISPR) and CRISPR associated 
protein 9 (Cas9) system was originally discovered in bacteria as a defence mechanism against foreign 
DNA, for example originating from viruses (Deltcheva et al., 2011; Jinek et al., 2012). Adaptive 
immunity is provided in a three-step process. First, foreign DNA is spliced into short fragments, 
termed protospacers, by Cas proteins. These protospacers are inserted into the host genome, and 
are then referred to as spacers. This insertion proceeds in a clustered manner, resulting in a CRISPR 
array of spacers, with individual spacers being separated by short palindromic repeats. Secondly, the 
entire CRISPR array is transcribed, and a pre-CRISPR targeting RNA transcript (pre-crRNA) is 
obtained. This transcript matures through the functioning of a trans-activating crRNA (tracrRNA), 
which contains a complementary nucleotide sequence to the short palindromic repeats in the pre-
crRNA transcript. This latter hybridization results in the recruitment of RNase III, and individual 
hybrid crRNA-tracrRNAs are created from the pre-crRNA. Finally, these hybrid RNA complexes guide 
Cas9 to the DNA sequence that is complementary to the crRNA sequence (which is complementary 
to the spacer sequence). Cas9 then induces a DSB in the targeted sequence, and thus provides 
immunity. Figure 8 provides an overview of this entire process. 
 
==PAGE:28==
28

spliced and inserted into the host genome in a CRISPR locus. Following insertion, this locus is 
transcribed, resulting in a pre-crRNA transcript. A pre-crRNA-tracrRNA hybrid is then formed through 
base-pairing at the palindromic repeat sections. This hybrid is then processed to form single crRNA-
tracrRNA complexes. Consequently, Cas9 is guided to its target by these individual crRNA-tracrRNA 
complexes, and DNA degradation ensues. Red circle indicates a single crRNA-tracrRNA complex. 
Reproduced from Yamamoto, 2015.  
 
A key discovery was that Cas9 can be reprogrammed by a hybrid ‘single guide’ (sgRNA) that 
combines both the crRNA and the tracrRNA into a single RNA (Jinek et al., 2012). This sgRNA is thus 
composed of a scaffold region that binds Cas9 and a targeting region that defines the nature of the 
genomic target through hybridisation. The targeting region is only 20 bp in length, and it is the only 
region that has to be substituted in the sgRNA to select a new target for Cas9 cleavage. Cas9 is 
guided by the sgRNA to induce a DSB in the targeted region. The induced DSB can then be repaired 
by one out of two repair pathways (Iyama and Wilson, 2013): high-fidelity repair through the 
homology-directed repair (HDR) pathway, or through error-prone repair through the non-
homologous end joining (NHEJ) pathway (see Figure 9). Although for example in S. cerevisiae, HDR is 
the most common repair pathway choice, in most organisms such as humans, plants, and fungi, 
NHEJ predominates (Mao et al., 2008). The error-prone NHEJ frequently introduces insertions, 
==PAGE:29==
29 
 
deletions, or frameshifts, and this can indeed be exploited for gene disruption. More precise 
genomic edits can be made through the HDR pathway using a donor template that contains the 
desired nucleotide sequence (DiCarlo et al., 2013). 
 
 
 
Figure 9. Overview of CRISPR/Cas9-induced DSB repair. Repair can occur through one out of two 
repair pathways: error-prone through the NHEJ pathway; or high-fidelity repair through the HDR 
pathway. In the NHEJ pathway, both strands are rejoined directly, resulting in frequent, random indel 
mutations at the junction site. This often results in frameshifts and/or the formation of premature 
stop codons, ultimately leading to gene disruption. In the HDR pathway, a repair template must be 
supplied, which allows for more precise edits. Reproduced from Ran et al., 2013. 
 
As mentioned above, the nature of the genomic target can be altered by changing the 20 bp 
targeting region of the sgRNA. However, efficient Cas9 cleavage requires the presence of a 
protospacer adjacent motif (PAM), which should be located directly downstream of the 20 bp 
sequence of the genomic target that is complementary to the targeting region of the sgRNA (Jinek et 
al., 2012). This PAM varies among Cas9 proteins, but for the most commonly used Cas9, from 
Streptococcus pyogenes (SpCas9), the PAM is NGG (Jinek et al., 2012). Other PAMs are also accepted 
for SpCas9, although with lower efficiencies (Hsu et al., 2013). Although the NGG sequence occurs 
frequently in the genome of most organisms, other Cas9 proteins with different PAM-specificities 
can be used if the NGG sequence is not present in the desired target region (Ran et al., 2015). 
Moreover, the PAM requirement of SpCas9 has been altered through protein engineering and 
several variants exist, with PAM specificities such as NGCG or NGAG (Kleinstiver et al., 2015). 
The CRISPR/Cas9 system has been applied for genome editing successfully in a wide variety of 
species such as yeast (DiCarlo et al., 2013), humans (Mali et al., 2013), and plants (Belhaj et al., 
2013). Besides genome editing, it can for example be used for other applications such as 
constructing epigenetic modifications (Hilton et al., 2015), targeted genomic imaging (Chen et al., 
2013), and the construction of gene drives (Hammond et al., 2016). Furthermore, the modular 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
nature of the CRISPR/Cas9 systems allows for multiplex engineering, resulting in an increased 
genetic engineering speed (Cong et al., 2013; Horwitz et al., 2015); Jessop-Fabre et al., 2016). 
 
2.3.2 CRISPR/Cpf1 
In many aspects, the CRISPR/Cpf1 system is similar to the CRISPR/Cas9 system (Zetsche et al., 2015). 
Cpf1 (CRISPR from Prevotella and Francisella) is, like Cas9, an endonuclease that cleaves DNA in an 
RNA dependent manner. Cpf1 is thus also guided to its genomic target by a crRNA. However, the 
PAM requirement for Cpf1 is TTN, as opposed to the NGG of SpCas9. Thus, the use of Cpf1 expands 
the range of genomic regions that can be targeted with RNA guided endonucleases. The PAM of Cpf1 
is located upstream of the target site, whereas the PAM of Cas9 is located downstream of the target 
site. Furthermore, Cpf1 generates a staggered DSB with an overhang of five nucleotides which is 
located at a distance of eighteen bp from the PAM site. In contrast, Cas9 generates a blunt DSB 
which is only three bp away from the PAM site. The staggered DSB generated by Cpf1 might be 
useful in directional gene transfer, as the efficiency of the HDR pathway can for example be limited 
in non-dividing cells (Iyama and Wilson, 2013). Finally, Cpf1 does not need the help of a tracrRNA to 
process the crRNA from the pre-crRNA transcript (Zetsche et al., 2015). Consequently, its 
crRNA/sgRNA only has a length of 42 bp, which for example facilitates its de novo synthesis. In 
addition, the fact that Cpf1 does not require a tracrRNA simplifies multiplex engineering approaches, 
as a single polymerase III promoter can drive the expression of a customised crRNA array (Zetsche et 
al., 2017). In contrast, multiplex engineering using CRISPR/Cas9 often involves the use of large DNA 
constructs or the transformation of multiple sgRNA plasmids (Zetsche et al., 2017). 
 
2.3.3 NgAgo 
As mentioned previously, Cas9 and Cpf1 are both RNA-guided endonucleases. Recently however, 
DNA-guided endonucleases have been discovered in the prokaryotes Thermus thermophilus (Swarts 
et al., 2014) and Pyrococcus furiosus (Swarts et al., 2015). These Argonaute (Ago) proteins are, like 
Cas9 and Cpf1, involved in the defence against foreign DNA. Prokaryotic Ago proteins are guided by 
5’-phosphorylated DNA guides 13-25 bp in length, and cleave ssDNA as well as dsDNA. More recently 
however, the Ago from the eubacterium Marinitoga piezophila (MpAgo) was shown to be guided by 
a 21 bp RNA molecule (Doxzen and Doudna, 2017). Ago proteins also exist in eukaryotes, where they 
are involved in RNA interference (RNAi). In contrast to prokaryotic Agos, eukaryotic Agos are 
considered to be guided exclusively by 5’-phosphorylated RNA guides 20-30 bp in length (Swarts et 
al., 2015).  
In an analogous manner to the CRISPR systems, an Ago from Natronobacterium gregoryi (NgAgo) 
was adapted for genome editing (Gao et al., 2016). This Ago was shown to efficiently create genome 
edits in human cells with high efficiency and specificity, using a single 5’-phosphorylated guide DNA 
(gDNA) of 24 bp in length. Such a short DNA guide would indeed be very useful in genome editing 
experiments, as it can be easily synthesised de novo and its 5’-phosphorylation enhances its stability 
in vivo. Furthermore, NgAgo does not have a PAM requirement, removing the limitation on 
sequences that are amenable to editing. Another advantage of NgAgo over SpCas9 and Cpf1 is its 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
smaller size (887, 1368, and 1228 amino acids, respectively), simplifying its delivery to targeted cells 
through viral vector packaging (Gao et al., 2016).  
NgAgo thus seems to have a considerable advantage over the CRISPR/Cas9 and CRISPR/Cpf1 
systems. However, multiple independent research groups (Burgess et al., 2016; Khin et al., 2016; Lee 
et al., 2016) have failed to reproduce the originally observed effect of genome editing in human cells 
by NgAgo. It is thus still unclear if NgAgo is a true genome editer like Cas9 and Cpf1. It has also been 
proposed that NgAgo is merely involved in gene silencing through RNA nuclease activity (Burgess et 
al., 2016; Ye et al., 2017).

3 Experimental strategy 
As explained in section 2, no previous attempts have been undertaken in the genetic engineering of 
D. nepalensis. Furthermore, there is a considerable lack of general biological knowledge about D. 
nepalensis, and there is no genome sequence available. Especially the latter presents a considerable 
hurdle for establishing a genetic engineering system, as conventional gene targeting strategies rely 
on HR. However, as mentioned in section 2.3.1, the CRISPR/Cas9 genome editing system requires 
sequence knowledge of a coding region of a gene of interest of a mere 20 bp (sgRNA) (Jinek et al., 
2012). Furthermore, the CRISPR/Cas9 system is very efficient at disrupting native genes through 
inducing error-prone repair by the NHEJ DNA repair pathway. This system will thus be used for 
genetic engineering in D. nepalensis.  
In order to identify genetic engineering targets for the enhancement of xylitol production, genome 
scale metabolic modelling will be attempted, in combination with literature studies. The identified 
genes of interest will then be amplified from D. nepalensis genomic DNA and their sequence will be 
determined. Consequently, based on this sequence, sgRNAs will be designed. Next, a transformation 
system will be developed for D. nepalensis. Finally, genomic edits will be made and transformants 
will be screened for xylitol production.

4 Materials and methods 
4.1 Strains and plasmids 
D. nepalensis NCYC 3413 was the only yeast strain that was used in this study. The CRISPR/Cas9 
plasmids pML104 and pML104-HygMx4 were obtained from John Wyrick and Patricia Wittkopp, 
respectively, through Addgene (http://www.addgene.org/). These plasmids were purified from a 
dam- E. coli strain (JM110, kindly supplied by Professor U. Varshney, Indian Institute of Science) to 
allow for efficient BclI cutting (see section 4.9). Long-term plasmid storage was performed using E. 
coli DH5α.  
 
4.2 Media and growth conditions 
D. nepalensis NCYC 3413 was maintained on solid YEPP medium containing 10 g/l yeast extract, 20 
g/l peptone, 5 g/l pectin, and 20 g/l agarose. YPD medium (10 g/l yeast extract, 20 g/l peptone, 20 
g/l dextrose) was used for liquid cultivation of D. nepalensis NCYC 3413. When required, hygromycin 
B was supplemented at a concentration of 250 μg/ml. All yeast cultivations were performed at 30 °C. 
The E. coli strains were cultivated in solid and liquid Luria-Bertani (LB) media and supplemented with 
100 μg/ml ampicillin and/or streptomycin. E. coli JM110 was continuously subjected to streptomycin 
selection. Cell growth was monitored through optical density (OD) measurements at 600 nm using a 
Shimadzu UV-1800 spectrophotometer. 
 
4.3 E. coli competent cells preparation 
A standard calcium chloride protocol was employed for competent E. coli cells preparation (Dagert 
and Ehrlich, 1979). Briefly, cells were grown to an OD600 of 0.35-0.40 in 200 ml liquid LB medium and 
directly chilled on ice. Cells were harvested at 3,000*g for 15 minutes, resuspended in 100 ml ice-
cold 100 mM magnesium chloride, pelleted at 2,000*g for 15 minutes, and resuspended in 100 ml of 
ice-cold 100 mM calcium chloride. Following incubation on ice for 30 minutes, cells were pelleted at 
1,000*g for 15 minutes and resuspended in 3 ml of 85 mM calcium chloride containing 15% (v/v) 
glycerol. Aliquots of 50 μl were then directly stored in -80 °C. 
 
4.4 Primer design  
Primers to amplify native D. nepalensis NCYC 3413 genes were designed based on the four available 
genome sequences from the Debaryomyces genus (see section 2.1.3). The respective sequences 
were identified using BLAST (Altschul et al., 1990) and a multiple sequence alignment (MSA) was 
generated using Clustal Omega (McWilliam et al., 2013). Primer sequence design was guided by 
NetPrimer (http://www.premierbiosoft.com/netprimer/) and the resulting primers were checked for 
specificity using Primer-Blast (Ye et al., 2012). 
 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
4.5 Colony PCR 
Overnight grown D. nepalensis colonies were resuspended in 20 μl of 20 mM NaOH, boiled for 10 
minutes at 98 °C, and pelleted at 10,000*g for 2 minutes. Subsequently, 1 μl of the supernatant was 
used as a template in a standard Taq polymerase PCR reaction in an Eppendorf Mastercycler. The 
resulting PCR products were purified through gel-electrophoresis and their nucleotide sequence was 
determined using Sanger sequencing. 
 
4.6 SgRNA design 
SgRNAs targeting native D. nepalensis genes were designed based on the nucleotide sequence that 
was obtained through colony PCR and subsequent Sanger sequencing (see section 4.5). SgRNA 
sequences were selected for on target efficiency using Benchling (https://benchling.com/) and 
selected for 5’ proximity. A specificity check was performed using BLAST (Altschul et al., 1990) 
against all available Debaryomyces genome sequences (see section 2.1.3). Oligonucleotides 
containing the sgRNA sequence were then designed using dedicated software for the pML104 and 
pML104-based plasmids (Laughery et al., 2015). For direct cloning into pML104-HygMx4 (see section 
4.8), these oligonucleotides do not need to be phosphorylated. 
 
4.7 Transformation 
4.7.1 D. nepalensis electroporation 
D. nepalensis electroporation was performed based on previously published protocols for D. 
hansenii (Minhas et al., 2009), C. famata (Voronovsky et al., 2002) and C. guilliermondii (Boretsky et 
al., 2007). Briefly, 1 ml of a saturated culture was reionculated into 30 ml of fresh YPD and grown 
until the OD600 reached 1. Cells were harvested at 4,500*g for 10 minutes and resuspended in 6 ml 
of 50 mM sodium phosphate buffer, pH 7.5, containing 25 mM DTT. Following incubation for 15 
minutes at 30 °C, cells were washed once with ice-cold sterile water and once with ice-cold 1 M 
sorbitol, and resuspended in 3 ml of ice-cold 1 M sorbitol. Aliquots of 200 μl were then made, and 
0.5 μg of plasmid DNA was added. This mixture was transferred to a pre-chilled 2-mm 
electroporation cuvette, and electroporation was performed using a Bio-Rad Gene Pulser with the 
following settings: 11.5 kV/cm, 50 μFd, and 100Ω. Cells were washed out of the cuvettes through the 
addition of 1 ml of YPD, and incubated for 1 h at 30 °C. Finally, cells were pelleted, resuspended in 
0.2 ml of 1 M sorbitol, plated on selective media, and incubated at 30 °C for 2-3 days. 
 
4.7.2 D. nepalensis lithium acetate 
D. nepalensis lithium acetate transformation was performed based on previously published 
protocols for C. tropicalis (Ko et al., 2006) and C. guilliermondii (Boretsky et al., 2007). Briefly, 1 ml of 
a saturated culture was reionculated into 30 ml of fresh YPD and grown until the OD600 reached 1. 
Cells were harvested at 4,500*g for 10 minutes, washed with sterile water, and resuspended in 10 
ml of 0.1 M lithium acetate, 10 mM Tris-HCl, 1 mM EDTA buffer (pH 7.5). Following incubation at 30 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
°C for 15 minutes, cells were pelleted and resuspended in 3 ml of fresh 0.1 M lithium acetate, 10 mM 
Tris-HCl, 1 mM EDTA buffer (pH 7.5). Aliquots of 100 μl were then made, and 1 μg of plasmid DNA 
was added, as well as 250 μl of freshly prepared 50% PEG. This suspension was mixed thoroughly 
and incubated for 30 minutes at 30 °C. Next, cells were heat-shocked for 15 minutes at 42 °C, chilled 
on ice for 1 minute, harvested (4,500*g for 10 minutes), resuspended in 1 ml of YPD, and incubated 
for 1 h at 30 °C. Finally, cells were pelleted, resuspended in 0.2 ml of sterile water, plated on 
selective media, and incubated at 30 °C for 2-3 days. 
 
4.7.3 E. coli calcium chloride  
For transformation of plasmids into E. coli a standard calcium chloride heat-shock protocol was 
employed (Dagert and Ehrlich, 1979). 
 
4.8 SgRNA cloning into pML104-HygMx4 
Cloning of sgRNAs into pML104-HygMx4 was essentially done exactly as previously described 
(Laughery et al, 2015). Briefly, pML104-HygMx4 was digested overnight with SwaI (New England 
Biolabs) at 25 °C. After heat inactivation for 20 minutes at 65 °C, BclI (New England Biolabs) was 
added to the reaction mixture and it was incubated overnight at 50 °C. The digested plasmids were 
then purified by gel-electrophoresis. Oligonucleotides containing the sgRNA sequence were 
hybridised at a concentration of 3 μM in 1x T4 ligase buffer (New England Biolabs). Hybridisation was 
performed in a thermocycler (Eppendorf Mastercycler), with initial incubation at 95 °C for 6 minutes 
and then gradual cooling to 25 °C with 1 minute cycles and 2 °C increments. The hybridised sgRNA-
oligonucleotides were then directly ligated into cut pML104-HygMx4 at a molar ratio of 50-100:1, 
respectively. Ligation was performed overnight at 16 °C, and subsequently 2 μl of the ligation 
reaction was transformed into E. coli.

5 Results 
5.1 Selection of a CRISPR/Cas9 system 
As mentioned in section 2.3.1, the CRISPR/Cas9 system has been applied successfully for genetic 
engineering in a wide variety of species such as yeast (DiCarlo et al., 2013), humans (Mali et al., 
2013), and plants (Belhaj et al., 2013). Hence, a large variety of plasmid based CRISPR/Cas9 systems 
are available (http://www.addgene.org/). For example, dedicated yeast plasmids exist that contain a 
wide range of selection markers, auxotrophic as well as dominant, as well as different codon-
optimised Cas9 proteins. In yeast, most genome editing efforts have focused on S. cerevisiae (DiCarlo 
et al., 2013; Laughery et al., 2015; Apel et al., 2017), and most available CRISPR/Cas9 systems are 
indeed S. cerevisiae based. However, the URA3 gene from S. cerevisiae has previously been used to 
restore a URA3- D. hansenii strain to prototrophy (Ricaurte and Govind, 1999), so there might not be 
a compatibility issue for the use of a S. cerevisiae CRISPR/Cas9 system in D. nepalensis.

5.2 Identification of metabolic engineering targets 
As mentioned in section 2.2.3, genome scale metabolic models (GSMMs) can be used in order to 
identify viable metabolic engineering strategies. A GSMM has recently become available for D. 
hansenii (BioModels #1302010023), as part of an automated model construction study for 49 
important fungal species based on comparative genomics (Pitkänen et al., 2014). Hence, this model 
was used as a proxy model for D. nepalensis metabolism. However, even a simple wild type 
simulation of this model in OptFlux (Rocha et al., 2010) did not result in any biomass formation (data 
not shown). Moreover, the biomass equation of this model seems to be broken. As other models 
that originate from the same publication are also generally not functional (Mikael Rørdam Andersen, 
personal communication), the GSMM strategy was discontinued. Instead, the focus was shifted to 
the identification of viable metabolic engineering strategies through literature research. As 
mentioned in section 3, the CRISPR/Cas9 system is ideally suited for disrupting native genes, 
whereas it is less suited for heterologous expression studies, given the knowledge limitations 
concerning D. nepalensis. Thus, metabolic engineering strategies for increasing xylitol production in 
D. nepalensis were restricted to disrupting native genes, as will be exemplified below. 
 
5.2.1 Disruption of XYL2 
The native D. nepalensis XDH, encoded by the XYL2 gene, is responsible for the conversion of xylitol 
into xylulose (see Figure 7). As seen in section 2.2.3.1.2, XYL2 disruption has been applied to a range 
of native xylitol-producing yeasts in order to improve the xylitol production (Ko et al., 2006; Pal et 
al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2014). In all these organisms, this strategy proved to be successful, so it will 
likely also be successful in D. nepalensis. In addition, the XYL2 disruption strategy is useful because it 
aids in the validation of the implemented CRISPR/Cas9 system. This is because XYL2-disrupted cells 
are unable to grow on a medium that contains xylose as the single carbon source, as the pathway for 
xylose metabolism is blocked (Kim et al., 2001; Ko et al., 2006). Thus, this facilitates screening for 
successful XYL2 disruptions through replica plating. 
 
5.2.2 Disruption of GPD2 
The native D. nepalensis glycerol 3-phosphate dehydrogenase 2 (GPD2), encoded by the GPD2 gene, 
is responsible for the production of glycerol under anaerobic conditions (see Figure 7). As mentioned 
previously, glycerol is a by-product of xylose fermentation in D. nepalensis (Kumar and Gummadi, 
2010). Not only does glycerol production channel the carbon flux away from xylitol production, it 
also complicates the downstream processing of xylitol (Lee et al., 2003). Therefore, a strategy was 
employed to reduce the production of glycerol in D. nepalensis.  
The glycerol metabolism in yeast is fairly well characterised (Ahmad et al., 2013). Glycerol 
production can either be induced by salt stress as it serves as an osmolyte (André et al., 1998) or by 
an intracellular redox imbalance, where it can be used for NAD+ regeneration (Bruinenberg et al., 
1983). In S. cerevisiae, there are two isozymes for glycerol production: glycerol 3-phosphate 
dehydrogenase 1 (GPD1) is salt stress-induced, whereas glycerol 3-phosphate dehydrogenase 2 
(GPD2) is induced by a redox imbalance (Ansell et al., 1997). It was first proposed that D. hansenii 
==PAGE:40==
40 
 
only contains a single GPD gene (Thomé, 2004; Gori et al., 2005; Thomé, 2005). However, it is now 
thought that D. hansenii also contains 2 isogenes (Patricia Thomé, personal communication). 
Consequently, in this study, the GPD2 gene was selected for disruption, as glycerol is produced 
during xylose fermentation in order to regenerate NAD+ (Granström and Leisola, 2002). 
Although the GPD1 of D. hansenii has been fairly well-characterised (Thomé, 2005), there is not so 
much information available about GPD2, especially not in relation to xylose metabolism. To the best 
of my knowledge, the only report about GPD disruption in this context was from Zhang et al., who 
disrupted the native GPD gene of K. marxianus, which resulted in an increase of xylitol production 
(Zhang et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2016). However, this was done in strains with heavily modified 
genetic backgrounds, and no valid conclusions can be drawn from these experiments that could be 
used in this study. The possible effect of a GPD2 disruption in D. nepalensis can also be inferred from 
indirect experiments, such as the addition of formate to the growth medium (Bruinenberg et al., 
1985, Granström and Leisola, 2002). Formate can be utilised in yeasts as an energy source but not as 
a carbon source (Bruinenberg et al., 1985). Its oxidation, catalysed by NAD+-linked formate 
dehydrogenase, results in an excess of NADH. Thus, formate addition can serve as a proxy for a 
GPD2 disruption, as such a disruption would also result in an excess of NADH. 
The addition of formate to the growth medium was studied in C. tropicalis and C. guilliermondii 
(Granström and Leisola, 2002; Granström et al., 2007a). The XR of C. tropicalis has a dual cofactor 
specificity, whereas the XR of C. guilliermondii is specific for NADPH (Granström and Leisola, 2002). 
In C. tropicalis, formate feeding resulted in increased xylitol production, as well as in increased 
glycerol and ethanol production. This can be explained by the fact that its XR can use NADH as a 
cofactor, resulting in increased xylose consumption and xylitol production (see section 2.2.2). On the 
other hand, C. guilliermondii could not utilize the excess NADH for xylose reduction because its XR 
cannot use NADH as a cofactor. No increase in xylitol production was thus observed, only the 
production of glycerol was increased. Nevertheless, C. guilliermondii was able to cope with the 
excess redox stress, so GPD2 disruption in D. nepalensis should not be a problem in that regard. 
Based on the above, GPD2 disruption might lead to an increase in xylitol production, or at least 
facilitate the downstream processing of xylitol.  
 
5.3 Primer design, colony PCR, and sgRNA design for XYL2 and GPD2 
In order to design sgRNAs targeting the native D. nepalensis XYL2 and GPD2 genes, their nucleotide 
sequences had to be determined. Thus, primers were designed based on a multiple sequence 
alignment (MSA) of XYL2 and GPD2 sequences taken from the available Debaryomyces genomes (see 
section 2.1.3). These MSAs can be found in the Appendix, supplementary Figures 1 and 2, 
respectively. These primers were then used to amplify both XYL2 and GPD2 from D. nepalensis 
genomic DNA using colony PCR, and correctly-sized amplicons were obtained for both genes (see 
Figure 12). Next, the nucleotide sequence of the amplicons was determined using Sanger 
sequencing, and the partial XYL2 and GPD2 sequences are displayed in the Appendix, supplementary 
Figures 3 and 4, respectively. Consequently, sgRNAs targeting XYL2 were designed based on this 
obtained sequence. Due to lack of time, no sgRNAs targeting GPD2 were designed.

5.5 Transformation of pML104-HygMx4 into D. nepalensis 
As mentioned in section 2.1.5, genetic engineering has not been attempted previously in D. 
nepalensis. Thus, before the CRISPR/Cas9 system could be used, a transformation system had to be 
established. Based on successful D. hansenii transformations through electroporation (Ricaurte and 
Govind, 1999; Minhas et al., 2009), electroporation was attempted in D. nepalensis with pML104-
HygMx4. However, no transformants were obtained on selective media (data not shown). In 
contrast, growth was observed on non-selective media, indicating that the cells had survived the 
electroporation procedure (data not shown). Next, an attempt was made to transform D. nepalensis 
with pML104-HygMx4 using a lithium acetate protocol. Unfortunately, again no transformants were 
obtained (data not shown). 
 
5.6 Cloning of sgRNAs targeting XYL2 into pML104-HygMx4 
An initial attempt was made to clone the sgRNAs targeting XYL2 into pML104-HygMx4, although this 
was unsuccessful (data not shown). As the transformation of D. nepalensis proved to be more of a 
bottleneck and as it had a higher priority, the sgRNA cloning efforts were discontinued. However, 
this sgRNA cloning into pML104-HygMx4 is based on a standardised protocol (Laughery et al., 2015) 
and should not form a major hurdle.

6 Discussion 
The aim of this study was to establish a genetic engineering system for D. nepalensis, as genetic 
engineering had previously not been attempted in this yeast. The fact that no transformants were 
obtained presents a major bottleneck to any genetic engineering attempts. Both the electroporation 
and the lithium acetate transformation protocols that were used in this study were based on 
protocols that had been successful for D. hansenii (Minhas et al., 2009) and various Candida species 
(Voronovsky et al., 2002; Ko et al., 2006; Boretsky et al., 2007). For lithium acetate transformation, 
usually a carrier DNA molecule is added to the transformation mixture in order to enhance the 
passage of the DNA of interest over the cell membrane (Gietz and Schiestl, 2007). In this study, 
lithium acetate transformation was performed without carrier DNA. Nonetheless, lithium acetate 
transformation without carrier DNA had been successful in C. guilliermondii (Boretsky et al., 2007), 
so this might not have been a limiting factor. No attempt was made at spheroplast transformation 
(Gietz and Woods, 2001) as there was a lack of required reagents and time. Since transformation of 
D. nepalensis had not previously been attempted, there was no positive control available to be used 
as a reference. Thus, it is not certain if the transformation procedure causes the bottleneck or that 
pML104-HygMx4 itself is non-functional in D. nepalensis. 
To the best of my knowledge, only two research groups have been successful in Debaryomyces 
transformation (Ricaurte and Govind, 1999; Minhas et al., 2009). For one research group, only 4 out 
of their 42 D. hansenii strains are transformable at all, and electroporation is the only viable method 
(Govind S. Nadathur, personal communication). In addition, it was observed that the use of the 
URA3 auxotrophic marker resulted in more D. hansenii transformants than when a hygromycin 
dominant marker was used (Ricaurte and Govind, 1999). Furthermore, auxotrophic markers are well-
established in yeast genetic engineering, and the use of dominant markers can be expensive and 
they can cause background noise (Pronk, 2002), although this latter phenomenon is also the case for 
auxotrophic markers (Alam et al., 2016). Indeed, the initial strategy for this study was to use 
pML104-HygMx4 in order to disrupt the native D. nepalensis URA3 gene. Consequently, the 
CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid pML104, which is identical to pML104-HygMx4 except for the fact that it 
contains a URA3 marker instead of a hygromycin marker, could be used to complement the 
generated D. nepalensis URA3- strain. A D. nepalensis URA3- strain can still be generated using for 
example UV and 5-fluoro-orotic acid (Boeke et al., 1984; Hashimoto et al., 2005; Hamedi et al., 
2013). With such a strain, pML104 could perhaps be successfully used for the establishment of a 
genetic engineering system in D. nepalensis.  
As mentioned in section 2.1.1, Debaryomyces species are part of the CTG clade of yeasts, in which 
the CUG codon is decoded into serine, as opposed to the standard leucine. This mistranslation could 
form a possible explanation for the unsuccessful transformation procedures in this study. This is 
because the hygromycin resistance marker for example was perhaps not properly translated, as the 
ORF contains nine CUG codons, of which some are also located in conserved regions (data not 
shown). However, the hygromycin marker (Gritz and Davies, 1983) that had been used previously for 
D. hansenii hygromycin based transformation (Ricaurte and Govind, 1999) also contained nine CUG 
codons. Nonetheless, higher transformation frequencies results were obtained when the URA3 
marker from S. cerevisiae was used (Ricaurte and Govind, 1999). This latter marker contains only a 
single CUG codon. This URA3 gene was also used to restore C. guilliermondii, another CTG clade 
yeast, to prototrophy (Boretsky et al., 2007). When the single CUG codon was substituted by a 
==PAGE:44==
44 
 
different leucine codon, CUC, the transformation efficiency increased 100 fold. Thus, site-directed 
mutagenesis could be applied to pML104, as its URA3 marker also only contains a single CUG codon 
(data not shown). However, the Cas9 contains another 61 CUG codons (data not shown), so perhaps 
de novo gene synthesis might provide a more efficient solution. In addition, pML104 could be further 
adapted for use in D. nepalensis through the isolation of the native D. nepalensis URA3 gene and the 
isolation of ARS sequences, both of which has been proven successful in increasing transformation 
efficiency in D. hansenii (Ricaurte and Govind, 1999; Minhas et al., 2009) and C. guilliermondii 
(Boretsky et al., 2007). 
The fact that the genome sequence of D. nepalensis is not available was relatively easily overcome in 
this study because only the coding sequence was required to design sgRNAs. However, this lack of a 
genome sequence is a bigger hurdle when it comes to functional genomics and other genetic 
engineering approaches. For example, the integration of heterologous DNA constructs relies on HR, 
for non-coding DNA sequence knowledge is required. Non-coding DNA sequences are more prone to 
mutations, and can therefore not be as easily amplified as coding sequences can. Genome editing 
through the NHEJ pathway is also not true editing, and as George Church has put it: “burning a page 
of the book is not editing the book” (Ledford, 2016). Indeed, a truly well-equipped genetic 
engineering toolbox also contains for example promoter libraries, reporter genes, and standardised 
integration sites. Furthermore, a NHEJ-deficient strain could be developed to enhance the efficiency 
of CRISPR/Cas9 mediated HR, as NHEJ is the predominant DNA repair pathway in most fungi, and 
most likely also in D. hansenii (Prista et al., 2016). Elaborate genetic engineering toolboxes have 
recently been developed for other non-conventional yeasts such as C. guilliermondii (Papon et al., 
2013), C. tropicalis (Zhang et al., 2016), or P. pastoris (Kang et al., 2017). In addition, CRISPR/Cas9 
has been successfully employed in C. albicans (Nguyen et al., 2017) and in D. hansenii (Govind S. 
Nadathur, personal communication). If the adaptation of the CRISPR/Cas9 system from S. cerevisiae 
as described in this study remains unsuccessful, these systems might be more reliable as fewer 
problems would arise from differential codon usage. On a more general note, the alternative codon 
usage in CTG clade yeasts might form a barrier in heterologous expression studies (Papon et al., 
2016), although this should be manageable with modern DNA synthesis capacities (Yaguchi et al., 
2017). 
Although the use of GSMMs has been limited in this study, as the model for D. hansenii proved to be 
dysfunctional, they nonetheless provide a powerful option for the identification of metabolic 
engineering strategies and for data-driven discoveries. The GSMM that was used in this study was 
generated based on comparative genomics (Pitkänen et al., 2014) and proved to be dysfunctional. As 
GSMM construction is a time-consuming process, automated constructed is a viable strategy, as long 
as it is performed correctly (King et al., 2016). Improvements in automated model construction are 
being made (Dias et al., 2015), as well as in the validation of automated reconstructions (Biggs and 
Papin, 2016; Steffensen et al., 2016). In addition, a unified standard for GSMMs (Ravikrishnan and 
Raman, 2015; Sánchez and Nielsen, 2015), for example provided by the BiGG Models database (King 
et al., 2016), will contribute to the further use of GSMMs. In this study, only a D. hansenii GSMM was 
used, although GSMMs from closely related species are also available, for example from Candida 
glabrata (Xu et al., 2013), S. stipitis (Damiani et al., 2015), Candida shehatae (Bideaux et al., 2016), 
and C. tropicalis (Mishra et al., 2016). Nevertheless, these models are not specific for D. nepalensis, 
and, as there is a lot of intergenus and interspecies variation in the Debaryomyces genus (see section 
2.1.1), they might not provide sufficient accuracy.

7 Conclusion 
The aim of this study was to create an efficient xylitol-producing D. nepalensis strain through a 
genetic engineering approach. However, the development and implementation of such an approach 
was hindered by unsuccessful transformation attempts, presenting a major bottleneck. Nonetheless, 
the foundation for the establishment of a genetic engineering system for D. nepalensis has been laid, 
and future directions are provided. For example, the pML104 CRISPR/Cas9, if adapted for use in D. 
nepalensis, might be useful in establishing a genetic engineering system, although a URA3- strain has 
to be generated first. Also, an efficient colony PCR protocol was successfully applied to D. nepalensis, 
which should prove useful in the screening of possible transformants and for the sequence 
determination of novel genetic engineering targets. In addition, promising targets for genetic 
engineering in order to increase the xylitol yield in D. nepalensis have already been identified, their 
nucleotide sequences have been determined, and sgRNAs have been designed. Moreover, the 
targeting of the XYL2 gene will allow for rapid determination of CRISPR/Cas9 activity, as XYL2 
mutants are unable to grow on a medium containing xylose as the single carbon source, facilitating 
transformant screening through for example replica plating. All of the above work will ease another 
attempt in the establishment of a genetic engineering system for D. nepalensis. Eventually, with such 
a system in place, an efficient xylitol-producing D. nepalensis strain could be obtained. Combined 
with its natural extremeophilic characteristics, such a D. nepalensis strain would be an ideal 
candidate for establishing an efficient biological process for xylitol production.

8 Perspectives 
As outlined in section 2.3, genome editing tools such as CRISPR/Cas9 are powerful for generating 
targeted genetic modifications. Novel nucleases are still being discovered (Sakade et al., 2017), such 
as the RNA-nuclease C2c2 (Abudayyeh et al., 2016), thus continuing to expand the genome editing 
toolbox. Nonetheless, genome editing has intrinsic limitations, for example in the scope of the edits 
that can be achieved and the fact that is limited to the DNA that is already in place. An important 
hallmark of synthetic biology however, is that the use of DNA is not limited to that of a single 
organism. For example, metagenomic studies (Thomas et al., 2012) will continue to increase the 
DNA content of databases, and adaptive laboratory evolution methods will continue to for example 
expand biological diversity (Cobb et al., 2013). Next, improvements in de novo DNA synthesis, for 
example in enzymatic DNA synthesis (Ud-Dean, 2008), will further facilitate organism design. 
Moreover, the expansion of low-cost biological hardware such as microfluidic devices (Yehezkel et 
al., 2016) will bring automation to biology. Such advances in automation will lead to more 
reproducible biology, which will in turn facilitate the true programming and engineering of biology 
using computational tools (Del Vecchio et al., 2016; He et al., 2016; Sadowski et al., 2016). In 
addition, reproducibility means that biological data will become more accessible to deep learning 
methods, for example resulting in improved omics data analysis (Ching et al., 2016).  This could lead 
to tailored chassis organisms, with for example minimal genomes (Vickers et al., 2016), that could be 
further engineered for diverse biotechnological products (Jouhten et al., 2016). Eventually, 
organisms might be designed from scratch using computer aided design and be built in an 
automated manner (Boles et al., 2017). Furthermore, the genetic code of such organisms could be 
expanded in order to include unnatural amino acids, allowing for the production of an extremely 
varied proteome (Wang et al., 2006; Pánek et al., 2017). In addition, this would also allow for the 
expansion of the substrate range that is useful for biological processes. All in all, the rapid 
developments in synthetic biology and metabolic engineering will thus directly contribute to a more 
sustainable future.
