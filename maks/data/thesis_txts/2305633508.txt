Abstract
In recent years, the use of Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) for scientiﬁc, commercial and military
purposes have increased. The goal of this master’s thesis is to derive a 6 degrees-of-freedom non-linear
model of a ﬁxed wing UAV and design a ﬂight control system for it. This model derivation is presented, as
well as the linearization of this model at a speciﬁc operating point. The longitudinal and lateral dynamics are
decoupled and a linear analysis is presented to showcase the dominance of the natural oscillatory modes in
different variables.
Three stability augmentation systems (SAS) are designed using state feedback, state feedback with feed-
forward action and state feedback with integral action. The three stability augmentation systems are designed
to make the UAV complacent with level 1 ﬂight as determined by the MIL-1797 handling qualities, as well
as to eliminate steady state error. The design method which lead to the best overall results on both the linear
and non-linear simulation was found to be full state feedback with feed-forward action.
Furthermore, a control augmentation system (CAS) is designed in order to achieve command tracking for
the normal load factor. The system is augmented with a proportional integral compensator in order to
ensure zero steady state error, after which the control augmentation system is designed using two different
approaches. The ﬁrst is linear quadratic regulation with output feedback, and the second is linear quadratic
command tracking with output feedback. It was found that the linear quadratic tracker yields the best results
on the linear and non-linear simulation. A sensitivity analysis was then performed on the linear quadratic
tracker for changes in parameters which would occur in the event of ice accumulation on the aircraft. It was
found that performance decreases with increased parameter changes up to a certain point, after the controller
performance is no longer acceptable.
In order to ensure continued acceptable performance in the event of unknown parameter changes, direct
model reference adaptive control (DMRAC) was designed on the SAS. The linear quadratic tracker was then
implemented and through simulations it was found that the DMRAC model succeeds in forcing the system
to track a reference model and that the linear quadratic tracker successfully forces the normal load factor to
track the command.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In this chapter, an introduction into the development of ﬂight control systems and unmanned aerial
vehicles is given in section 1.1. The Aerosonde aircraft, upon which ﬂight control systems will be designed
and implemented throughout the project, is also described, followed by a brief literature survey. The problem
statement will be presented in section 1.2, and an overview explaining the thesis layout is described in
section 1.3. Finally, the software tools which will be utilized for simulation and design are described in
section 1.4.
1.1
Background
In this section, a history of ﬂight control systems is described alongside the major developments of
control theory, after which a short description of unmanned aerial vehicles is given. The Aerosonde model is
introduced after which a brief literature survey, covering the main topics of this thesis, is presented.
1.1.1
Flight Control
The Wright brothers success in ﬂying the ﬁrst aeroplane in the early 1900’s can highly be attributed
to their realization that instead of needing to make the aircraft inherently stable, they needed to make
sure the pilot could control it, using his perceived height from the ground to draw feedback, so he could
make a decision[10]. They then made sure that the plane could be controlled. This can be considered as
a ﬂight control system; the modern day controller was replaced by the pilot and the measurements from
the current aircraft sensors were replaced by his perception of the aircraft’s position and speed. By 1914,
the Sperry Gyroscope Company had implemented an autopilot, and a demonstration of the pilot raising his
hands from the controls during ﬂight, while the mechanic walked along the wings, was seen by the public.
Afterwards, during World War I, great advances were made in aircraft design, through the then developed
small perturbation theory of aircraft, as well as the successful measurement of stability derivatives[23]. Up
until World War II, classical control theory was continuously developed, mainly Nyquist’s stability criterion
in the frequency domain and Bode’s frequency theory. The expansion of the aircraft size, as well as the speed
and altitude envelopes, during the Second World War motivated further advances in classical control theory,
which lead to the successful take-off, transatlantic ﬂight, and landing of the US Air Force C-53 in 1947[23].
Due to the combination of the availability of the ﬁrst analogue computers, as well as the publication of
the root-locus technique in 1948, automatic control and the corresponding stability analysis became more
widespread by aircraft companies. Further advances continued to happen, and control surfaces became
fully powered. The emergence of supersonic ﬂight lead to the development of adaptive control in the 1950s.
The adaptive controllers were designed due to attempts to design autopilots for the supersonic ﬂights, and

"The Brave Era". Due to the lack of theoretical understanding and hurried implementation, the "Brave Era"

1.1.2
Unmanned Aerial Vehicles
An Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) is an aircraft which operates without the presence of a pilot on-
board. It can be remotely piloted, or autonomous. The modern Unmanned Aerial Vehicle was used in World
War I in the form of aerial torpedoes, where the Hewitt-Sperry Automatic Airplane made its ﬁrst ﬂight in
1916. The development of UAVs throughout the years has mainly been for military purposes, ranging from
aerial battle purposes to reconnaissance and radar jamming. Today, UAVs are also being used for scientiﬁc
missions, recreational purposes, as well commercial (for example wildﬁre mapping) and professional aerial
surveying. The continuous expansion of application areas has lead to a high need for advances in UAV
technology.
1.1.3
Aerosonde Aircraft
The Aerosonde UAV was designed in the early 1990’s for speciﬁc scientiﬁc applications, mainly
meteorological. As such, it was designed to endure over long ranges, perform reliably in varied and
sometimes extreme terrains and climates, as well as to collect weather data[17]. In 1997, the aircraft made a
fully robotic ﬂight, operating autonomously during take-off, ﬂight, and landing. In 1998, the Aerosonde
"Lamia" crossed the north Atlantic. At the time, it was the ﬁrst UAV to successfully cross the north Atlantic,

Literature Survey
A longitudinal command stability augmentation system for an unstable combat aircraft is designed by
Mansour et al. through pole placement techniques in [18]. Military handling qualities are used to provide
the control system requirements. They show that the handling qualities are met through the augmentation of
the system up to certain speeds, after which the level 1 ﬂight requirements are no longer met.
Stevens et al. develop a method for designing aircraft control systems through linear quadratic output
feedback techniques in [22]. They prove that speciﬁc compensator structures can be used to shape closed
loop responses. They also show that modifying the performance index can lead to the elimination of steady
state errors without the need for integrators in the forward path.
A direct model reference adaptive control algorithm is proposed by Sobel for the design of a stability

1.2
Problem Statement
The main objective of this project is to model a ﬁxed-wing unmanned aerial vehicle in order to reach a
decoupled linearized state space model, and then design a ﬂight control system for the linearized decoupled
longitudinal dynamics. An analysis is to be performed on the decoupled linear longitudinal dynamics in
order to derive the ﬂight control system requirements to reach stability and high manoeuvrability. After this
has been achieved, a different approach will be taken to further develop a more robust system.
The ﬂight control is divided into two tasks. The ﬁrst is to design a stability augmentation system in order
to ensure the stability of the longitudinal oscillatory behaviour. The second task is to ﬁnd a control design
method to obtain a control augmentation system, which will allow any command to be tracked through a
speciﬁc control variable, in order to allow the manoeuvring of the aircraft to its performance limits.
The ﬁnal complete control system (consisting of both stability and control augmentation system) should
also be able to withstand aircraft parameter variations. The methods used in the design of the ﬂight control
system should include modern control theory. The application of these modern control approaches to the
aircraft should be tested on the aircraft model in a simulation environment in order to analyze the stability
and performance of the controlled aircraft.
1.3
Thesis Overview
In chapter 2, a 6 DOF non-linear aircraft model is derived from basic concepts of motion. The model is
then linearized at speciﬁed operating points corresponding to a typical ﬂight condition for the Aerosonde
UAV, and presented in state-space form. The model is also decoupled into longitudinal and lateral motion.
The eigenvalues are analysed and a modal analysis is presented. The linear longitudinal modes are described
and used to derive a model approximation which allows the design of a controller to stabilize the natural
mode. This control system is known as the stability augmentation system, which is introduced in chapter 3.
The stability augmentation system is designed using full state feedback methods in order to augment the
system and ensure compliance with military handling requirements. In the ﬁrst design method, only full state
feedback is implemented. In the second approach, full state feedback along with reference feed-forward is
implemented. Integral action is then introduced and state feedback is used to design the ﬁnal state feedback
controller with integral action. The three approaches are simulated, and the most satisfactory controller
is selected as the stability augmentation system on which the control augmentation system is designed.
Motivation for this choice is also discussed. The control augmentation system, presented in chapter 4, is
designed in order to reach reference input tracking, in order to achieve manoeuvrability along the aircraft
pitch axis. The control augmentation system is ﬁrst designed using a linear quadratic regulator. A second
approach, linear quadratic tracking, is then designed and implemented. These designs are both implemented
using output feedback since modern linear quadratic design techniques based on state feedback are not
desirable for aircraft applications. The two control augmentation systems are then simulated and the design
which leads to the best reference tracking is selected for the continuation of the project. Motivation for the
choice is also presented. A sensitivity analysis is performed by simulating the effect of ice accumulation on
the aircraft. In order to ensure the ability of the aircraft performance to remain satisfactory under parameter
variations, model reference adaptive control is applied on the stability augmentation system in chapter 5.

CHAPTER 3
Stability Augmentation System
In this chapter, the control system for the longitudinal ﬂight dynamics will be designed and implemented
on both the non-linear and linear aircraft models. This control system is known as the Stability Augmentation
System (SAS) since it is responsible for improving the stability of the short mode oscillations. In section 3.1,
the SAS is deﬁned in more detail. The design methods used are intended to make the aircraft eigenmodes
comply with certain standards and requirements. These requirements are also presented. Three different
control system architectures will be designed and tested in section 3.2; the ﬁrst using state feedback and
eigenvalue placement only, to obtain the desired closed loop characteristics. The second architecture consists
of full state feedback, with added feed-forward action, in order to obtain both the desired closed loop
characteristics, as well as reference tracking. The third architecture is state feedback with integral action, in
order to also have the desired closed loop characteristics, but with zero steady state error. The ﬁnal choice of
preferred architecture for the SAS, to be used in the remaining project, will then be presented and argued for.
3.1
SAS Deﬁnition, Angle of Attack Similarity Transformation and Handling
Qualities
A stability augmentation system or SAS is a control system responsible for making sure that the aircraft
eigenmodes are stable and damped, independently of the pilot or command inputs. In this case, the SAS
is designed using the angle of attack and pitch rate for feedback. A similarity transformation is derived
in section 3.1.1 to obtain the angle of attack as a state. Certain guidelines have been put in place over the
years for the design of the SAS. One such guideline, known as MIL-1797, uses damping ratio and natural
frequency limits in order to set the requirements for different classes of aircraft, as well as different categories
of ﬂight. This speciﬁcation was used for the Longitudinal short-period SAS and is explained in more detail
in section 3.1.2.

Control Augmentation System
In this chapter, the control augmentation system (CAS) is designed for the closed loop state feedback
SAS with feed-forward action. In section 4.1, the CAS is deﬁned, after which motivation for the choice
of control variable is explained. In order to have the control variable as an output, equations relating the
control variable to the current states of the system are derived in section 4.1.1, after which the equations
are validated through simulations in Matlab and Simulink. Compensator dynamics are added to the system
for the CAS, and the complete architecture is explained in section 4.1.2. In section 4.2, the two different
design methods to be implemented and tested, which are both optimal control methods, are explained as
well as their solution approach. Algorithms for solving the problems are presented and implementation
methods for the current SAS are also shown. The results obtained through simulation for both these control
architectures are presented and discussed in section 4.3. In order to see how the CAS would fare under

4.1
Control Variable and CAS Architecture
Under manual control of an aircraft, in most cases, a stability augmentation system is all that is needed.
However, in the case of high-performance military aircraft, where certain tasks require the manoeuvring of
the aircraft to its performance limits, more speciﬁc control augmentation systems (CAS) are needed[23].
One such tasks is the "Dogﬁght", a classical aerial battle between ﬁghter aircraft which is conducted at close
range[4]. During a Dogﬁght, high manoeuvrability and ability to change manoeuvres quickly are important
and a control system must be in place, to allow for taking advantage of this. In this case, a good control
variable for the pitch axis is the normal acceleration of the aircraft, Az, which is deﬁned as the acceleration
along the negative z-axis of the aircraft, and it is directly relevant in order to perform a maximum-rate
turn[23]. For control purposes, the normal acceleration can be replaced by the normal load factor, Nz, which
is given by −Nz/g. Another useful characteristic of a normal acceleration CAS is that the accelerometer
(measuring Az) output has components proportional to the angle of attack, and thus the normal acceleration
CAS can inherently stabilize the short period mode[23]. In this section, the equations needed to obtain
the normal load factor as an output of the current system are derived, and the architecture of the CAS is
presented.

4.2
Optimal Control with Output Feedback
In this section, two design methods for the CAS are introduced, both in the form of Linear Quadratic
control problems. In the ﬁrst method, a quadratic cost function is used in order to stabilize the states and
achieve the desired time response. This is done by ﬁnding a control law which will minimize this cost
function. In the second method, a reference input tracking problem is solved, also through the deﬁnition
of a cost function, in order to minimize error deviation. Both design methods utilize Linear Quadratic
(LQ) algorithms which select the optimal gain (the gain at which the speciﬁed cost function is minimized)
values simultaneously. This also means the algorithm will automatically place the compensator zero in
section 4.1.2 at the optimal location. The design methods utilized in this chapter can be used for multiple
loops at a time. Also, once the performance index is selected, the gains are computed via the algorithm,
which is another advantage in modern control; the choice of the performance index is where the engineering
judgement enters[23]. In classical control, multiple loops are tuned individually and usually the tuning
requires much more engineering judgement or intuition. Output feedback allows selecting speciﬁc outputs
for the controller gains, meaning that the system can be augmented to incorporate any structure, which in
this case allows the choice of augmenting the system with the compensator dynamics in equation 4.10. In
aircraft control, the more common modern control design methods which depend on state-feedback, are not
suitable. This is mainly due to the need for selecting a large number of design parameters. This usually
requires trial-and-error selection, which should be avoided for aircraft controls[23]. Therefore, the design
methods used in this project are based on selecting nonstandard performance indices, along with output
feedback instead of state feedback[23].

4.5
Conclusion
Two design methods were used to implement an optimal control augmentation system on the aircraft
models through load factor feedback. The LQR method lead to an increased settling time, as well as a large
steady state error on the longitudinal linear and complete non-linear model. The LQ tracker method lead to
desirable time responses for all three models, as well as a damping of the phugoid dynamics, and reference
tracking. Therefore a sensitivity analysis was performed on the LQ tracker CAS. The sensitivity analysis
showed performance changes, but very small, for lift and drag changes up to 10%, after which the non-linear
model fuel limits had been reached at 20% parameter variation. Since the LQ tracker CAS design method
lead to a system where the load factor followed the reference signal with a shorter settling time then for the
LQR design, as well as the small error signal obtained when using the LQ tracker CAS, this was selected as
the CAS design method for the remainder of the project.

CHAPTER 5
Adaptive Control
In the previous chapter, a robust control method was used to design a CAS which was able to withstand
bounded uncertainties with minimal performance deterioration up to 20% changes in parameters. In this
chapter, an adaptive control method is used in order to design a system which can withstand uncertainties
by adapting the control law by performing an online uncertainty estimation and produce a control input
which can anticipate, overcome or minimize deviations from the desired closed-loop behaviour[8]. The
approach used is Direct Model Reference Adaptive Control (DMRAC), which is deﬁned in section 5.1.
The full state feedback SAS with feed-forward action is redesigned in section 5.2. The DMRAC design
equations are derived in section 5.3. The implementation of the SAS is explained in section 5.4 and the
results are presented and discussed. In section 5.5 the LQ tracker CAS is tested on the new SAS. A summary
is presented in section 5.6.
5.1

The basic architecture of DMRAC is shown in Figure 5.1. In model reference adaptive control, a

law, as opposed to indirect model reference adaptive control where the parameters are estimated at each time
step by solving a certain algebraic equation that relates the parameters with their on-line estimates[13]. The
aircraft SAS was re-designed using the DMRAC approach, after which the LQ tracker CAS designed in the

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion and Future Work
6.1
Conclusion
Starting from the equations of motion, the 6 DOF non-linear aircraft model was derived in section 2.1 in
the form of 12 non-linear equations. The non-linear model was linearized under speciﬁc operating points
corresponding to typical ﬂight conditions, in section 2.2. Decoupled explicit longitudinal and lateral state
space models were obtained. The numerical state space longitudinal model of the Aerosonde aircraft was
presented in section 2.3. Linear analysis was performed and the natural modes of the longitudinal model
were evaluated. The short period approximation was obtained, representing the dynamics of the short period
longitudinal natural mode dynamics in section 2.3.4. The actuator model for the elevator control surface was
introduced in section 2.3.5, since control designs were to be made on, and implemented with consideration
to, the actuator. The complete linear longitudinal and short period approximation model were validated
through simulation alongside the 6 DOF non-linear model in section 2.3.6.
In section 3.1.1, a similarity transformation was made on the longitudinal model in order to obtain a
measurable variable for the design of a stability augmentation system. The transformation was validated
through simulation. The design requirements for the SAS were obtained by comparing the characteristics of
the short period approximation to the military handling requirements in section 3.1.2. Full state feedback
was employed as a design method for the SAS in section 3.2. The design was made on the short period
approximation in three separate ways; the ﬁrst only using full state feedback, the second using full state
feedback and reference feed-forward and the ﬁnal using full state feedback and integral action. Using
only full state feedback, the short period mode damping was improved and the handling requirements
were fulﬁlled. Adding reference feed-forward action improved the time response of the system. Adding
integral action also fulﬁlled the handling requirements on the short period model, but replaced the phugoid
dynamics of the complete longitudinal model. An attempt to keep the short period mode characteristics
within the requirements while improving the damping, without eliminating the phugoid dynamics, lead to an
asymptotically unstable system. Due to its superior results over the two other design approaches, the full
state feedback with reference feed-forward action design method was selected for the stability augmentation
system on which the project would continue.
A control augmentation system was designed in chapter 4. The normal load factor was chosen as a
control variable, and motivation of this choice as well as measurement derivations were presented. The
proportional-integral compensator architecture of the CAS was presented in section 4.1.2. Optimal control
in the form of linear quadratic control was chosen to design the CAS, and both a linear quadratic regulator
and linear quadratic tracking CAS were designed in section 4.2. Algorithms for solving the design equations
were successfully written and implemented in Matlab for both design approaches. The LQR simulations
65
==PAGE:86==
66
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
resulted in successful reference tracking for the short period approximation, but yielded undesirable results
for the longitudinal and complete aircraft models. As for the LQ tracker design, the simulation yielded
satisfactory results on all three models with respect to the time response. This design method was therefore
chosen as the preferred CAS design for the continuation of the project. It was shown that compensator
dynamics could be added to the system through the deﬁnition of unconventional performance indices and
through the use of output feedback. To evaluate the robustness of the LQ tracker, a sensitivity analysis was
performed in section 4.4. The accumulation of ice on the aircraft was simulated by increasing the drag of
the aircraft model, as well as decreasing the lift. Simulations were then made using the same exact LQ
tracker controller gains calculated for the unchanged aircraft model. The results showed that the selected
performance indicators (settling time, rise time and percentage overshoot) for increased parameter change
(up to 20%) proved that the performance decreased. At a 20% variation, the non-linear 6 DOF Aerosonde
model fuel limits were reached.
In order to increase the robustness of the ﬂight control system, model reference adaptive control was
chosen as a method to augment the SAS. The baseline SAS (full state feedback with reference feed-forward
action) was redesigned on the short period approximation without actuator dynamics in section 5.2. This
was due to the inability of the MRAC to estimate the actuator dynamics, unless the input reference signal
was a step response. The MRAC was designed in section 5.3 and implemented on the SAS in section 5.4.
The results proved that the new SAS augmented with adaptive control was able to follow a reference model
with parameter variations, as well as to provide bounded estimates of the true parameter variations. The
CAS was then tested on the adaptive SAS, and it was found that along with model reference adaptive control
the LQ tracker CAS gave the overall desired results: closed loop characteristics within the limits speciﬁed
by the military handling requirements, as well as desirable time response with respect to reference input
tracking, despite parameter variations.
6.2
Future Work
The LQ tracker should be designed and tested without added compensator dynamics, to verify that steady
state error can be eliminated through the correct cost function alone. This could be more practical since the
compensator dynamics increase the complexity of the system.
In order to fully take advantage of the robustness properties in practical applications, methods should be
investigated in order to extend the MRAC design so it can estimate the actuator dynamics by estimating the
uncertain input gain.
Since MRAC assumes perfect cancellation of uncertainties, a different approach such as L1 Adaptive
Control could be implemented as a substitute, since it assumes only partial uncertainty cancellation.
Fault detection and isolation techniques could also be implemented, in order to make the aircraft perform
better under actuator and sensor faults, not only changes in the model parameters.
