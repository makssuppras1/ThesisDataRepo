Abstract 
 
Urban lakes such as Utterslev Mose in Copenhagen are exposed both to legacy internal 
loading and to episodic combined sewer overflows (CSOs). Constructed wetlands 
(CWs) are increasingly used to buffer these impacts, yet their long-term performance 
and operational status near end-of-life are rarely documented, and traditional 
monitoring is costly. This study uses the over-25-year-old Utterslev Mose CW as a case 
to (i) review its initial treatment performance, (ii) characterize recent influent quality 
and variability, and (iii) evaluate the practical usefulness of low-cost sensors for 
monitoring an aging CW. 
 
Historical performance was reconstructed from commissioning reports (1998–2000), 
focusing on total phosphorus (TP), total nitrogen (TN), chemical oxygen demand (COD) 
and total suspended solids (TSS) removal for lake water and CSO. Recent CSO influent 
(2024–2025) was analyzed from four hydraulically connected overflow structures, and 
nearby structures of the catchment. Dilution factors were derived from WWTP influent 
statistics to interpret within-event dynamics. Two low-cost sensor units were deployed 
and compared with utility water-level and flow data via z-score-based time-series 
analysis. An online visualization dashboard was developed to help field work and data 
visualizing. A short sampling campaign in October 2025 provided additional data. 
 
The historical review suggested high removal efficiencies for TP, TN, COD and TSS 
during the early years of operation. Recent measurements suggest that CSO inflows 
now may carry lower average concentrations of organic matter and nutrients than 
reported two decades ago, while also displaying spatial, event-to-event and within-
event variability. Dilution-factor analysis indicated coupled behavior of TN–TP and of 
COD–TSS, with evidence of first-flush patterns for particulate-associated pollutants. 
Depth readings from both low-cost sensors closely followed reference hydraulic data 
and, together with the dashboard, helped identify hydraulic activities in the CW. 
 
Overall, the study provides a documented example of an end-of-life CW, explores how 
low-cost sensors can help with hydraulic monitoring and field planning, and highlights 
the need for more systematic long-term monitoring of the CW. The conclusions remain 
preliminary due to the limited monitoring period and data, incomplete influent/effluent 
records, and future work should combine extended sampling campaigns, sensor 
calibration against laboratory analyses, and replicated case studies in other aging CW 
systems.

1. Introduction 
Urban receiving waters face pressures from wet-weather discharges—especially 
combined sewer overflows (CSOs) that release mixtures of sewage and stormwater 
during heavy rainfall. These events convey deliver nutrients, organic matter, suspended 
solids, heavy metals, pathogens and organic micropollutants (e.g., PAHs), degrading 
ecological/chemical status and creating public-health risks[1], [2].  
 
At the same time, many European lakes remain affected by legacy eutrophication, total 
phosphorus (TP) concentrations have declined over past decades but have plateaued in 
recent years[3]. Syntheses from Denmark/Europe show that recovery can be slow, and 
that internal phosphorus loading may delay improvement despite reductions in external 
loads[4]. Long-term effects (>8–10 years) are less consistent unless restoration 
measures are repeated[5].  
 
Nature-based solutions (NBS) use ecosystem processes for urban water management. 
Within this framework, constructed wetlands (CWs) are increasingly applied to manage 
stormwater and treat CSO discharges by combining filtration/sedimentation, sorption–
precipitation, microbial degradation, and plant uptake, thereby removing TSS, organic 
matter and nutrients during intermittent overflow events[1], [6]. Certain configurations 
can also receive surface waters (rivers/lakes) for polishing, complementing urban lake 
management[7]. Evidence syntheses further show wetlands reduce TN/TP transport 
across treated wastewater and urban/agricultural runoff, and CWs are widely employed 
in urban-lake restoration[8], [9]. 
 
Constructed wetlands, like any engineered system, can experience performance decline 
as they age: sediments accumulate, pore spaces clog, and reactive media may become 
exhausted, all of which can reduce treatment efficiency over time[10]. Despite 
widespread adoption of CWs for CSO control in Germany, France, Italy and elsewhere, 
systematic long-term (multi-year to decadal) monitoring remains uncommon, and 
results beyond the early years are rarely documented in the international literature; post-
construction monitoring is generally not mandated, though stricter data requirements 
are now being considered in some countries[11]. This critical lack of data has left a 
substantial knowledge gap regarding the long-term performance and eventual 
operational status of systems nearing or reaching their end-of-life. 
 
Existing evidence on the long-term performance of CW is scarce, and traditional 
monitoring methods are expensive and labor-intensive. In recent years, low-cost 
sensors and open-source Internet of Things (IoT) technologies have increased the 
potential for more intensive and distributed monitoring, offering a practical way to 
densify coverage, capture events in near real time, and support more responsive urban 
operations and management[12]. Their metrology maturity, reliability, and maintenance 
==PAGE:6==
 
2
requirements remain major obstacles, but can be mitigated with fit-for-purpose quality 
assurance/quality control (QA/QC), phased (lab→ field) testing, and hybrid 
deployment using reference instruments[13]. 
 
In light of these gaps and practical constraints, a case study of a long-operating 
constructed wetland is conducted to address three objectives: (i) to review the influent 
and effluent characteristics and removal efficiencies of the constructed wetland under 
study during its initial period of operation, and to assess possible long-term changes in 
influent quality using limited recent data available; (ii) evaluate the feasibility and 
practical value of low-cost sensors for monitoring an end-of-life constructed wetland; 
(iii) document and validate the current operational status of the system, incorporating 
the practical insights and challenges learned from field sampling activities, to inform 
monitoring strategies for aging constructed wetlands. In this study, the over-25-year 
Utterslev Mose (Copenhagen) constructed wetland is used as a case to share practical 
experience and insights for end-of-life constructed wetlands. Early studies reported 
high treatment efficiencies for both lake water and CSO; however, it remains unclear 
whether the system still performs at those levels or has experienced substantial 
functional decline after more than two decades of operation. In interpreting current 
performance, potential changes in influent quality must also be considered. For example, 
phosphorus equivalents in Danish domestic wastewater have significantly declined 
since the 1990s, reflecting the effects of detergent regulations and other control 
measures[14].

2.1 Location and construction 
Utterslev Mose (Utterslev Marsh) in Copenhagen, Denmark, is an urban wetland 
system that has long suffered from eutrophication and poor water quality, caused by 
internal phosphorus release from lake sediments together with periodic inputs from 
combined-sewer overflows. To mitigate these impacts while maintaining the 
hydrological balance of the marsh, the municipality constructed a large vertical-flow 
constructed wetland (CW) system on the western part of the Utterslev Mose system 
between March 1997 and October 1998. The facility consists of two coupled units: an 
upstream overflow structure (overløbsbygværk) connected to the combined sewer 
retention pipe (bassinledning), and a downstream constructed wetland.

the CW is indicated by the red hatched area and marked by a black arrow. (Source: Google Maps) 
2.2 Structure and treatment principles of the CW 
According to the facility's technical manual[15]，the downstream constructed wetland 
is based on vertical-flow root-zone technology and consists of synergistic vegetation, 
microbial, and substrate compartments. The vegetation layer, dominated by common 
reed (Phragmites australis), develops a dense root network extending up to 60 cm, 
which provides vast surface areas for microbial attachment and the physical adsorption 
of metals and phosphorus, while also contributing to nutrient removal through direct 
plant uptake. The substrate profile has a total depth of approximately 2.0 m and is 
==PAGE:8==
 
4
composed of three distinct layers: an upper 0.7 m layer of gravel mixed with wood 
chips to support vegetation; an intermediate 0.3 m layer of crushed marble specifically 
designed to bind phosphorus through precipitation with calcium; and a bottom 1.0 m 
gravel drainage layer. This configuration ensures physical filtration, chemical sorption, 
and pH buffering while maintaining hydraulic conductivity. 
 
Figure 2.2 Schematic cross-section of the constructed wetland structure. The profile illustrates the three synergistic 
layers: the root zone layer (gravel and wood chips), the reactive filter layer (crushed marble for phosphorus 
precipitation), and the drainage layer. The system is lined with an impermeable HDPE membrane to prevent seepage. 
(Source: Adapted from the facility’s technical drawing[16].) 
The constructed wetland, which could also be referred to as the root zone hereafter, 
operates through intermittent vertical-flow cycles involving inflow, retention, and 
drainage phases. The influent undergoes active pre-aeration as it is pumped over a stone 
aeration cone (Figure 2.3 and Figure 2.4) to increase dissolved oxygen levels before 
distribution, while the subsequent drainage phase ensures passive oxygenation of the 
porous media[15]. This fluctuation creates the alternating aerobic and anoxic conditions 
essential for nutrient transformation.

Figure 2.4 The stone aeration cone. Historical photo

According to monitoring data from the facility's testing phase, nitrogen enters the 
wetland primarily as organic nitrogen and ammonium, with minimal concentrations of 
nitrate and nitrite[17], [18]. Nitrogen removal is achieved through a sequential process: 
==PAGE:9==
 
5
organic nitrogen is first transformed into ammonia via ammonification. This is followed 
by nitrification, where ammonium is oxidized (which requires aerobic conditions 
provided by the drainage phase). Ultimately, the nitrate is removed as nitrogen gas 
through denitrification. According to the manual[15], denitrification occurs in anoxic 
zones that form locally due to degradation processes, creating a "mosaic" of co-existing 
aerobic and anoxic micro-zones within the root zone. 
 
Phosphorus removal is primarily driven by precipitation with calcium within the 
reactive crushed marble layer and adsorption to substrate minerals. The periodic re-
oxygenation is vital, as prolonged water saturation can trigger the release of phosphorus 
bound to iron compounds. The Key treatment objectives include the reduction of total 
phosphorus (TP) and total nitrogen (TN), as well as chemical oxygen demand (COD) 
and total suspended solids (TSS). 
2.3 Operational procedure and water cycle 
The CW system operates under two primary functional modes, according to HOFOR’s 
report in 2023[19]:  
2.3.1 Dry-period operation (lake-water treatment) 
During dry weather, the system continuously treats nutrient-rich lake water recirculated 
from Utterslev Mose. Lake water is introduced through valve MV82 (by gravity or 
pumping) into the root zone until the water level reaches approximately 16.4 m. The 
water is then retained for about three days to allow biological and physico-chemical 
purification within the planted basins. In winter the retention period can be extended 
longer because of lower temperatures and reduced microbial activity or special weather 
conditions, but the treated volume per batch remains similar.  
 
After the treatment period, the effluent is discharged back to the marsh by pump P80 
until the level drops to approx. 14.55 m. Each full treatment cycle treats roughly around 
2300 m³ of lake water.

2.3.2 Rain-period operation (CSO treatment) 
During wet weather, the CW switches to a storage and overflow-treatment function. 
Assuming a typical initial water level of 16.4 m (maintained for lake water treatment), 
the effective storage capacity available for stormwater is approximately 3,700 m³.  
 
The system response is triggered when the water level in the upstream overflow 
structure rises to 16.54 m. At this threshold, two mechanisms are engaged[17]: 
 
Pumped CSO: The pump P70 connected to the CSO retention pipe is set to start at this 
level. Its purpose is to handle rain events that are not large enough to cause massive 
overflow, lifting the influent into the root-zone basin via the aeration cone to ensure 
oxygenation. However, the pump is programmed to stop if gravity overflow is detected 
or predicted, if the root-zone basin is full, or if freezing conditions occur. 
 
Gravity CSO: Gravity overflow would also occur at this water level. This happens 
specifically when the inflow volume exceeds the capacity of pump P70, or when P70 
is deactivated by the control system during high-intensity rain events. 
 
If the water level continues to rise and exceeds 17.14 m in the rootzone, excess water 
bypasses the wetland and discharges directly to Utterslev Mose to prevent overtopping. 
 
After a rainfall event, stored stormwater is prioritized for return to the sewer network. 
Valve V83 opens to drain the water back by gravity, to the extent possible where 
hydraulic capacity in the sewer network permits. However, this drainage is physically 
limited to the 16.4 m level. Consequently, any residual combined sewage remaining 
==PAGE:11==
 
7
below this level is retained in the root-zone for treatment (typically with a 3-day 
retention period) before being discharged to the marsh by pump P80. In this mode, CSO 
mitigation takes operational priority over routine lake-water treatment. 
 
However, the current operation procedures and schedule of the CW system remain 
unclear.  
 
2.4 Mechanical equipment and control system 
Structural components described herein are based on the technical manual[15], while 
operational parameters refer to the hydraulic analysis report[19] to reflect the latest 
documented configuration: 
2.4.1 Pumping units 
Inlet pump P70: This pump lifts influent from the upstream retention pipe or lake 
water from Utterslev Mose into the root zone via the aeration cone. It has a capacity of 
approximately 570–575 m³ h⁻¹ and equipped with dry-run protection. 
 
Outlet pump P80: A submersible pump situated in the outlet shaft that returns treated 
effluent from the root zone to Utterslev Mose.  
2.4.2 Valve and control network 
Flow routing within the plant is regulated by a set of automatic and manual valves. 
 
MV82: An electrical valve that admits lake water to the system when required, with a 
capacity of approximately 1125 m³ h⁻¹. 
 
V72: The valve remains open during gravity filling of lake water but must be closed 
during maintenance or when the system is pumping lake water to prevent backflow. 
 
V83: Act as a sewer connection or bypass valve. During lake water treatment, it is kept 
closed to prevent incoming lake water from draining directly into the sewer network. 
During emptying stage after rain events (mentioned in section Error! Reference source 
not found. ), it is opened to allow stored water to drain back to the sewer system by 
gravity. 
 
V73-V75: V74 and V75 are located between the drainage network and the outlet pump 
well (P80), allowing for the isolation of the well and enabling load balancing between 
the two drainage systems if significant flow differences arise. V73 functions as a "short-
circuit valve" connecting the surface ring channel directly to the P80 well. It remains 
closed during normal operation to prevent mixing untreated water with treated effluent, 
==PAGE:12==
 
8
and is opened only under special conditions, such as during sludge removal 
maintenance. 
2.4.3 Flow- and level-measurement instruments 
Hydraulic conditions are tracked mainly through the following electromagnetic 
flowmeters and level transmitters connected to the SRO system: 
 
Flowmeters: FT70 monitors pumped inflow (from P70). FT71 measures gravity inflow 
(both gravity CSO and gravity lake water), and FT80 records the volume of treated 
effluent discharged to the marsh. 
 
Water level sensors: water levels are continuously measured by LT70 in the upstream 
retention pipe and by dual sensors LT71/LT72 in the root-zone basins to ensure 
redundancy. 
2.4.4 Water-quality monitoring probes 
The CW is equipped with online sensors measuring dissolved oxygen (DO), pH, 
temperature, and oxidation–reduction potential (ORP) at several points—typically at 
the inlet, within the root-zone, and at the outlet. These sensors provide continuous data 
to evaluate biological conditions and detect operational anomalies such as oxygen 
depletion or pH drift. 
2.4.5 Core hydraulic and structural components 
Aeration cone: A stone structure located at the center of the basin. It is designed to 
oxygenate pumped influent from P70, increasing water–air contact before entering the 
distribution channels and promoting aerobic conditions. Gravity inflow (via FT71) 
typically bypasses this cone and enters the distribution ring directly. 
 
Distribution system: An annular channel surrounding the aeration cone that conveys 
flow to submerged distribution pipes delivering water evenly into the planted root-zone. 
 
Drainage network: The bottom of the CW features a 1.0 m drainage layer containing 
perforated pipes laid at 1.5 m intervals. These pipes collect treated water and discharge 
it through pump P80. 
 
Emergency Overflow: A physical weir structure designed to bypass excess water 
directly to the marsh if the water level in the wetland exceeds the safety threshold to 
prevent overtopping of the embankments.

2.4.6 Central control and operator interface 
Local operation is managed through a user interface integrated with the municipality’s 
SRO system (centralized control, regulation and monitoring system). The panel offers 
nine display menus covering overall plant overview, water levels, DO, temperature, pH, 
counters, timers, and parameter settings. The system supports both automatic 
unattended operation and manual or remote override from HOFOR’s control center.  
2.5 Connection to the CSO Network 
Utterslev Mose is one of the receiving water bodies within Copenhagen’s combined 
sewer system and receives stormwater and wastewater discharges from several 
upstream overflow structures. In total, seven CSO outlets are hydraulically connected 
to the Utterslev Mose system: UM21, UM19b, UM18, UM17b, UM16, UM14, and 
UM26 (see Figure 2.6 below).  
 
The constructed wetland system (UM19b) primarily receives inflow from the UM21 
branch, which collects sewage and stormwater from the adjacent catchment. It treats 
and temporarily stores overflow water before releasing it downstream into UM18, thus 
maintaining a controlled base flow through the Utterslev Mose system.

3. Methods 
3.1 Historical performance review 
3.1.1 Data acquisition 
Historical water quality data were retrieved from the facility's commissioning 
reports[17], [18] and the study by Gervin and Brix[20]. The dataset covers the initial 
operational period from October 1998 to January 2000. Data extraction focused on 
reported concentrations and removal efficiencies for key parameters: TP, TN, COD, and 
TSS. Data were categorized seasonally: Summer is defined as May through October 
(inclusive) due to persistent high phosphorus levels, with the remaining period 
classified as Winter. The monitoring campaign was divided into two phases: 
 
Lake-Water Treatment Phase: From October 1998 until June 1999, the system treated 
low-nutrient lake water exclusively as no sewer overflows occurred. While 
approximately 50 lake-water loading events occurred during the entire study period 
(with 42 recorded in 1999), performance was evaluated based on 24 valid inlet and 
outlet analysis sets. 
 
CSO Treatment Phase: Following the first sewer overflow events in June 1999, the 
monitoring focus shifted to combined sewage handling. A total of 16 sewer loadings 
were evaluated during this phase. Due to occasional sampling failures at the inlet during 
specific events, the final dataset for performance assessment consists of 12 inlet 
samples and 16 outlet samples. 
 
3.1.2 Monitoring configuration and sampling   
Flow-proportional sampling was conducted using automatic samplers. Based on the 
manual commission reports, inlet sampling locations varied depending on the water 
source type (CSO or lake water) and were adjusted during actual operations. The 
samples were sent to a specialized institution for analysis after being collected from the 
field. 
 
For CSO events, samples were primarily collected from the retention pipe located 
upstream of the CW or the P70 pump well: Gravity overflow was sampled by the ISCO-
1 automatic sampler (triggered by signals from FT71), with the intake tubing placed 
just behind the overflow edge of the structure. ISCO-1 is not activated when lake water 
is introduced through MV82. Pumped overflow to the CW was sampled by ISCO-2 
(triggered by signals from FT70) from within the P70 pump well. The ring channel 
inside of the CW served as a backup location when designed sampling method is 
==PAGE:15==
 
11
unavailable (e.g., sampler failure). In these cases, the inlet water concentration is 
converted by a factor calculated from the average of three relevant values for the 
retention pipe and ring channel. 
 
For lake water, influent samples were typically collected manually at the intake grating 
located on the north side of the access dam, but in many cases the reports used water 
quality data from VMP Station 1706 (Utterslev Mose West Basin) as the representative 
influent concentration. Sampling at this station was conducted every 14 days during the 
summer period (May–September) and monthly during the winter. To estimate the inlet 
concentration for specific inflow events, the analysis used either the value from the 
sample closest in time to the event or the average of the two nearest samples.

3.2 Recent influent quality profiling  
3.2.1 Data sources and acquisition 
Recent water quality data for the period 2024–2025 were provided by HOFOR from 
their internal monitoring records[21]. However, only CSO influent data are available, 
while all other influent and effluent data have not been measured. To characterize the 
CSO pollutant level entering the Utterslev Mose system, water samples were analyzed 
from four hydraulically connected overflow outlets: the overflow structure of the CW 
system (UM19b) and its downstream CSO outlets UM18, UM17, and UM16. The 
sampling location for UM19b corresponds to the same CSO sampling point used in the 
historical dataset. 
 
3.2.2 Monitoring configuration and sampling    
Four CSO events were analyzed, which were observed on February 6, August 4, 
October 10 in 2024, and on January 6 in 2025. However, data availability varied across 
these events as not all outlets successfully collected samples or provided complete data

13
for each date. Sampling was conducted using automatic samplers with different 
configurations: 
UM19b: For the events in October 2024 and January 2025, the carousel autosampler 
collected 24-bottle sequential samples, programmed to take a 200 mL sub-sample every 
2 minutes and switch bottles every 10 minutes, allowing for the analysis of event 
dynamics. For the August and February events, single-bottle composite samples were

Downstream outlets: Samples from these outlets were generally collected as single-

3.3 Low-cost sensors evaluation 
3.3.1 Sensor information and deployment 
The study utilized low-cost, IoT water quality monitoring units developed by the BoSL. 
According to the sensor manuals[22], [23], each unit consists of a 3D-printed logger 
box housing an Arduino-compatible motherboard (MiniCore v0.5.6) and a 3.7V 
rechargeable Li-ion battery. Data transmission is managed via an integrated SIM-7000 
module supporting 4G/NB-IoT networks, enabling remote data upload to a web server. 
Two specific sensor probes were applied for this study: 
DTEC Probe (Depth, Temperature, Electrical Conductivity): An in-line multi-
parameter sensor measuring water depth via a vented pressure transducer, temperature, 
and electrical conductivity (EC) via voltage drop across electrodes (0–1023 integer 
output). 
TURB Probe (Turbidity): An in-line optical sensor measuring turbidity based on light 
reflectivity (0–1023 integer output). 
Two sensor units were deployed to monitor critical points within the Utterslev Mose 
treatment system on 13 June 2025 in the morning. On 13 August 2025, maintenance 
was conducted for both sensor units (probe cleaning and possible battery replacement). 
For both units, the data logging boxes were secured in elevated positions to prevent 
contact with water during operation. 
CSO Unit ("Denmark 1"): This unit was installed on the interior wall of the CSO 
structure (located at the terminus of the detention basin pipeline). The probe was fixed 
near HOFOR automatic sampler and operates continuously. However, valid water 
quality readings are obtained only when the water level in the structure rises sufficiently 
to reach the probes during inflow events.

3.3.2 Data Acquisition and Management Architecture 
The sensors utilized a dual-logging strategy. High-frequency raw measurements were 
recorded locally to an onboard SD card every 2 minutes for both units. Aggregated 
mean values were transmitted to the cloud server at specific intervals: 10 minutes for 
the CSO unit (Denmark 1) and 30 minutes for the wetland unit (Denmark 2). 
Consequently, server timestamps reflect the upload time, with each data point 
representing the average of the preceding measurement period. All data were 
timestamped in Coordinated Universal Time (UTC+0). 
 
Remote access to the uploaded cloud data was managed via the BoSL IoT web 
interface[24]. Raw data showing time-series, depth, temperature, EC and turbidity were 
retrieved directly as CSV files from the database. Local SD cards provided a high-
resolution backup for data recovery. To assess whether fluctuations in the sensor 
readings were consistent with the hydraulic dynamics of the constructed wetland 
system—and thereby evaluate the sensor’s monitoring performance—supplementary 
hydraulic records were obtained from HOFOR’s SRO system[25]. These records 
included time-resolved water levels at the key control points (the retention pipe and 
root-zone basin), as well as data measured at the system’s flow meters (FT70, FT71 and 
FT80). The dataset also contained pump-status and valve-activation signals and covered 
the period 1 July–14 August 2025.

3.3.3 Online visualizing dashboard 
To facilitate real-time monitoring of the hydraulic status within the CSO structure and 
the wetland, a custom web-based dashboard was developed. The interface was built 
using HTML5 and CSS3 for structure and styling, with JavaScript serving as the core 
processing engine. The visualization component utilizes the Plotly.js library to render 
dynamic time-series plots directly in a web browser, allowing for immediate inspection 
of sensor data without the need for manual file retrieval. The dashboard is programmed 
to execute automated data fetch cycles (default interval: 5 minutes) via HTTP requests 
to the cloud server.

3.4.2 Sample preparation and analysis 
Sample storage 
Samples were placed in a refrigerator (4–6 °C) immediately after retrieval from the 
field. Sample processing and the analysis of TSS and COD were completed within 24 
h. Samples requiring storage for more than one week were frozen to prevent 
degradation. 
Total suspended solids  
TSS was measured using 0.7 µm glass-fiber filters (ADVANTEC). A single replicate 
was prepared for each water sample. Filters were first rinsed with deionized water and 
dried in an oven with labelled aluminum weighing dishes at 105 °C for at least 30 min. 
After drying, the filters (along with aluminum weighing dishes) were cooled for 20 min 
in a desiccator containing silica gel, and were only taken out before filtering the samples.  
To ensure homogeneity, each autosampler bottle was shaken before withdrawing a 30–
50 mL aliquot. Each aliquot was filtered through a pre-weighed filter (weighed together 
with the dish). The filter was then returned to its aluminum weighing dish and dried 
again at 105 °C for 1.5 h, followed by a 20-min cooling period in the desiccator. The 
final dry weight was then recorded. 
TSS concentration was calculated from the increase in filter mass divided by the filtered 
volume:

where 𝑚dry SS (g) is the mass of solids retained on the filter and 𝑉sample (mL) is the

Chemical oxygen demand (COD) 
COD was analyzed using the HACH LCK514 test kit（range: 100–2000 mg L⁻¹）
together with a HACH LANGE DR3900 spectrophotometer. Each test vial contains 
pre-dosed reagents, and a QR code on the vial surface allows for automatic recognition 
by the spectrophotometer. 
To obtain a representative average, a composite sample was prepared by combining 
equal aliquots from each individual sample. Before addition, each test vial was shaken 
gently to disperse any solids settled at the bottom. Then, 2 mL of the composite sample 
was added to the vial, which was shaken again and placed in the COD digester. 
Digestion was performed under the “HT” program (high-temperature digestion) for 15 
min. After digestion, the vial was shaken gently, cooled to room temperature, allowed 
to settle, and then measured directly with the spectrophotometer. 
Ammonia and NOx 
An aliquot of the well-mixed sample was drawn into a syringe and filtered through a 
0.2 µm syringe filter into a labelled plastic vial. Filters were replaced as needed to 
prevent pressure build-up, and new syringes were used for each sample to eliminate 
cross-contamination. Vials were filled to capacity to minimize headspace, thereby 
reducing the potential for ammonia volatilization. A procedural blank containing 
reverse osmosis (RO) water was prepared for quality control. Samples were submitted 
to the university laboratory technicians for analysis. 
Total nitrogen and total phosphorus  
For TN and TP analysis, an unfiltered aliquot of the homogenized sample was 
transferred into a glass vial, filled to approximately 80% capacity. Vials were 
immediately capped and labelled. A RO-water blank was included for quality control. 
Samples were submitted to the university laboratory technicians for analysis.

Hydraulic Reference (SRO data) Interpretation 
Reference hydraulic data (available dataset from July 1 to August 14) contained mixed 
signals that required logical filtering to isolate specific flow events. The raw dataset 
included the following key hydraulic variables measured at 5-minute intervals from 
July 1 to August 14, 2025: pumped inflow (FT70, m³/h), gravity overflow/lake intake 
(FT71, m³/h), outlet pumping (FT80, m³/h), and valve status indicators (V82, 
dimensionless). A logic-based filter was applied to distinguish Gravity CSO events 
from gravity lake water inflows, as both are measured by the same flowmeter. The status 
of the lake water intake valve (V82) served as the discriminator: 
 
When V82 is activated, the flow recorded by FT71 was classified as lake water and 
excluded from the CSO dataset. When V82 is deactivated, the flow recorded by FT71 
was classified as potential gravity CSO entering the root zone. However, analysis 
revealed that on several dates where the valve was closed (July 7, 14, 21, 30 and August 
11), the flowmeter recorded still minor flows (see Appendix 4). These readings occurred 
only within a short time after the valve was closed, suggesting a possible delayed effect 
rather than real overflow events. Consequently, only the events on August 3rd (peak: 
1,323.7 m³/h) and August 6th were retained as “corrected gravity CSO”, and all other 
non-zero values were corrected to zero for evaluating sensor response. 
 
Time Alignment 
Since the low-cost sensors (10- and 30-min intervals, respectively) and the SRO system 
operated on different logging frequencies, a temporal alignment step was performed 
prior to statistical comparison. Using the sensor timestamps as the reference index, a 
nearest-neighbor matching algorithm was applied to identify the corresponding SRO 
data point within a varying tolerance window (± 5 minutes for Denmark 1; ± 15 minutes 
for Denmark 2). Pairs falling outside this window were excluded. Assessment of the 
aligned dataset showed a mean time difference of 1.8 minutes for Denmark 1 and 3.2 
minutes for Denmark 2. 
Outlier Removal 
Periods where sensor probe were physically removed from the water or the original 
position for maintenance (probe cleaning on August 13, 2025) were identified by abrupt 
disturbance in readings. Consequently, all data recorded from this point onwards were 
excluded from the analysis. 
For lake water treatment-related datasets (specifically LT71, LT72, and Denmark 2 
parameters), outlier removal was targeted at specific suspicious periods identified 
through visual inspection. Within these defined windows, transient spikes were detected 
and removed using a Z-score threshold (e.g., ∣𝑍∣> 3 or ∣𝑍∣> 2.5).

Performance Assessment 
To evaluate sensor performance despite differences in measurement units (e.g., meters 
vs. m3/h), a Z-score normalization was applied: 𝑍= (𝑥−𝜇)/𝜎, where x is the original 
value, 𝜇  is mean of the dataset and 𝜎  is standard deviation of the dataset

across two dimensions: trend consistency was assessed using Pearson’s correlation 
coefficient (r) with P-value significance testing, while relative measurement accuracy 
was quantified by the Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of the Z-scores against the corrected 
SRO reference data.

their closely aligned DF curves—indicates a clear first-flush pattern, where particulate 
and organic fractions are mobilized at the onset of the overflow and subsequently 
diminish as the event progresses. Because this decline was not accompanied by 
corresponding increases in DF, the reduction is unlikely to be driven solely by dilution 
and instead reflects a genuine decrease in pollutant concentrations during the event.

4.4.2 Sensor turbidity-based comparison 
Turbidity was examined as an indicator of short-term water quality changes, given its 
sensitivity to suspended particles and disturbance within the flow path. 
 
Figure 4.17 presents the normalized comparison between DK2 turbidity and the root-
zone water-level signals. The two-reference water-level meters were nearly identical, 
but their correlation with DK2 turbidity was very weak and statistically insignificant (r 
= – 0.0115 to – 0.0117, p ≈ 0.60; MAE ≈ 1.16). The turbidity datasets showed higher-
frequency fluctuations and several spikes that did not correspond to the fill–drain cycles 
of the basin. These results suggest that DK2 turbidity was largely influenced by local 
disturbances around the probe—such as minor sediment resuspension, water movement 
across the submerged platform, or probe motion—rather than by the overall hydraulic 
stages of the wetland. 
 
Figure 4.17 
However, a noticeable turbidity spike was observed in DK2 around 3 August, 
coinciding with the large CSO event previously discussed. Although the overall 
correlation with water-level signals remained low, this obvious response suggests that 
DK2 may still have the potential to indicate extreme pollution events.  
 
==PAGE:43==
 
39
 
Figure 4.18 
A similar pattern was seen at DK1 (Figure 4.18): despite the very weak association and 
generally high difference between turbidity and the reconstructed gravity-flow signal (r 
= – 0.0230, p ≈ 0.082; MAE ≈ 0.82; n = 5754), the sensor recorded a pronounced rise 
in turbidity during the CSO event. The turbidity values at DK1 did not return to their 
earlier baseline afterwards, possibly due to residual CSO water remaining in the 
overflow structure or particulate and colloidal matter adhering to the probe surface. In 
contrast, DK2 did not exhibit prolonged high levels, which may reflect the stronger 
dilution within the root-zone basin and its more dynamic hydraulic conditions that 
likely helped flush away residuals on the probe. 
 
Overall, the turbidity signals from both DK1 and DK2 did not show meaningful 
correlation with hydraulic dynamics at their respective locations. Although both sensors 
were able to indicate the occurrence of extreme pollution events to some extent, DK2 
did not demonstrate evidence-based potential for tracking routine lake-water quality 
variations, and the position of DK1 makes frequent maintenance difficult while offering 
limited practical value for continuous water-quality monitoring. Importantly, these 
observations remain preliminary. More data type rather than only hydraulic data—such 
as time-resolved turbidity measurements obtained during dedicated CSO sampling 
campaigns—would be required before any firm conclusions can be drawn.

The visualization dashboard provides practical value for planning and coordinating 
field work, particularly under the uncertain hydraulic procedure of an end-of-life 
constructed wetland. For example, by reviewing the DK2 depth data (see Figure 4.21), 
the general pattern of root-zone hydraulic cycles becomes apparent: during dry-season 
operation, the basin typically undergoes a fill–hold–drain sequence roughly once per 
week. This information can be used to schedule sampling activities more flexibly. 
 
On 5 October, DK2 displayed a sharp rise in water level, accompanied by a sudden 
turbidity increase and a pronounced drop in electrical conductivity—signals that 
collectively indicate a large inflow into the root zone (see Figure 4.20). Around the same 
time, DK1 showed similarly abrupt changes across its parameters (see Figure 4.19), 
allowing the event to be identified as a CSO inflow reaching the constructed wetland 
via gravity overflow. Following this event, the root-zone water level remained 
unchanged for nearly two weeks, even in the absence of further intensive rainfall. This 
unusual stagnation suggested a possible interruption of normal operation, which was 
later confirmed through communication with HOFOR: the wetland’s effluent pump 
(P80) had malfunctioned due to flooding. 
 
This example illustrates how the dashboard can support real-time decision-making

operation of the wetland is uncertain. 
 
4.4.4 Maintenance 
As discussed earlier, the turbidity signal of DK1, positioned at the overflow structure, 
showed a clear drift after the first major CSO event and did not return to its initial 
baseline. For DK2, located in the root-zone basin, the drift was smaller, but seasonal 
growth of macrophytes and algae during summer likely introduced additional 
interference. Both sensors were installed on 13 June, and the first maintenance—battery 
replacement and probe cleaning—was carried out two months later on 13 August. As 
shown in Figure 4.22 and Figure 4.23, both probes of DK2 were partly wrapped by 
vegetation, although the turbidity probe remained relatively clean on the inner glass 
surface due to the protective cap. However, the turbidity probe of DK1 (Figure 4.24) 
was comparatively clean, indicating that the previously observed drift was unlikely to 
have been caused by probe pollution. 
 
Although the batteries were operating at relatively low voltages—particularly for DK1, 
which uploaded data more frequently—both sensors remained functional throughout 
the two-month period without interruption. Based on this maintenance experience, a 
two-month servicing interval appears feasible and reasonably safe under the observed 
operating conditions.

4.5 Sampling activity in 2025 
4.5.1 Sample analysis 
During the 2025 sampling campaign, four bottles were successfully collected before 
the autosampler stopped due to battery failure. As a result, the dataset covers 
approximately 7:00–10:36 on the morning of 13 October. Total nitrogen (TN) and total 
phosphorus (TP) were not analyzed because of instrument malfunction at the laboratory. 
 
For TSS, the measured concentrations (including duplicates) ranged from 8 to 20 mg 
L⁻¹, with no clear temporal pattern across the four bottles. The overall average was 13.6 
mg L⁻¹. As the influent water was relatively clear, the filtration resistance during 
analysis was low, meaning that the 30–50 mL sample volumes used may have produced 
slightly underestimated TSS values. 
 
COD analysis was performed on a composite of the four samples. The resulting 
concentration was approximately 90 mg L⁻¹. However, this value lies below the 
recommended measurement range of the test kit (100–2000 mg L⁻¹), indicating that the 
result may carry additional uncertainty. 
 
Dissolved inorganic nitrogen species were analyzed in two of the samples. Ammonium 
concentrations were 1.11–1.20 mg N L⁻¹, while nitrite and nitrate were below the 
quantification limit in all cases. Phosphate ranged from 0.73 to 0.75 mg P L⁻¹. TN and 
TP were not analyzed due to instrument malfunction at the laboratory. 
 
4.5.2 Current operational procedure in the constructed wetland 
At the beginning of this study, the routine operational procedure was confirmed with 
HOFOR to be the following: under dry-weather conditions, lake water from Utterslev 
==PAGE:48==
 
44
Mose was introduced into the root-zone basin on Monday mornings, retained and 
treated within the root-zone system, and subsequently pumped back to the lake on 
Thursday mornings. The exact timing and the hydraulic triggers for starting or stopping 
each step were not fully specified. 
 
However, due to delays related to site-access permits and early battery failures in the 
monitoring equipment, the preparatory work for sampling could not be completed until 
late October. By the time the sampling activity took place on 13 October, the CW 
system was no longer operating as intended. A CSO event on 5 October had resulted in 
flooding of the pumping well and the subsequent failure of pump P80, which prevented 
the scheduled emptying of the root zone. Since then, the system has not returned to 
normal operation, meaning that no stable or reliable operational procedure could be 
confirmed during the remainder of the study period.

5. Conclusion 
 
The present study examined an over-25-year-old constructed wetland in Utterslev Mose 
to (i) conduct a reevaluation to its early performance, (ii) characterize recent recorded 
CSO influent quality, (iii) explore the use of low-cost sensors for monitoring an end-
of-life constructed wetland (CW), and (iv) document the current operational status of 
the system. Using historical reports, four recent CSO event sampling records, a small 
lake-water sampling campaign, and an exploratory low-cost sensor deployment, the 
work provides an indicative picture of how the system and its influent conditions may 
have evolved over time. 
 
This study contributes to three main ways. First, it brings together fragmented historical 
records and recent monitoring to provide one of the few documented case studies of a 
CW that has treated both CSO and lake water over more than two decades, highlighting 
how influent quality and CSO dynamics may change over time at the approach to “end-
of-life”. Second, it demonstrates a practical workflow for combining traditional 
sampling, hydraulic data from “Control, Regulation and Monitoring” system and low-
cost IoT sensors, including time-alignment, simple interface development, and basic 
QA/QC steps, in a complex, intermittently operated constructed wetland. Third, it offers 
concrete, field-tested insights on the deployment, maintenance and interpretation of 
low-cost sensors in CSO and natural environments—where strong hydraulic gradients, 
vegetation growth and limited access are typical. 
 
Several limitations must be acknowledged. Recent CSO quality at the CW is 
represented by only four events with limited datasets, and TSS and nutrient data are 
incomplete in some seasons and events. Historical data were extracted from reports 
rather than raw time-series, limiting the ability to reanalyze performance or fully 
harmonies methods across decades. Differences in sampling strategies (volume-
proportional vs. time-proportional) and analytical coverage introduce uncertainty. The 
low-cost sensor evaluation covers only a short period in a single summer, relies mainly 
on hydraulic reference data, and focuses on two parameters (depth and turbidity). Much 
of the material is in Danish and is quite old; some information may be lost in translation, 
and some data and information may no longer be usable nowadays. 
 
These limitations point directly to future research needs. First, sustained, long-term 
monitoring of both influent and effluent—ideally with flow-proportional sampling and 
joint use of reference instruments and low-cost sensors—would be required to quantify 
how treatment performance evolves in aging CWs and to separate the effects of 
changing influent quality from internal degradation. Second, a broader set of CSO 
events across several structures in the Utterslev Mose network could help clarify 
whether the spatial and temporal heterogeneity observed here is typical or event-
==PAGE:50==
 
46
specific, and how it influences wetland loading. Third, targeted experiments on low-
cost sensors (e.g. controlled calibration campaigns, alternative mounting strategies, 
redundancy between probes) could better define when and where such devices are 
reliable enough for operational decision-making in CSO and wetland applications. 
Finally, comparative studies on other long-operated CWs—both within and beyond 
Denmark—would help build a more general understanding of “end-of-life” behavior 
and inform guidelines for retrofitting, repurposing or decommissioning aging systems.
