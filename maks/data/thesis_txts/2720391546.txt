Abstract
Mould is a large problem as it is found in the building where we live, and for many years
has been known as a health problem. It is often found in wood and other organic building
materials, and more frequently on plywood than on massive wood boards, for which the
reasons are not quite known.
In this master thesis the capability of moisturising and the behaviour of water in plywood
compared to massive wood, exposed to water in different fibre directions, is investigated.
The analysed plywood and massive radiate pine samples are made of wood imported
from the same sawmill and grown in the same area and climate. By that only method of
treatment and mix of sapwood and heartwood differ.
Furthermore, the two types of pine material are tested for mould resistance in the mould
chamber along with spruce samples in plywood and massive pieces, as well as samples
of OSB material. Also here the water adsorption is observed to find if there is a relation
between the moisture content and the mould resistance.
As the last part of the experiments simulations are made to analyse the adsorption capa­
city of the tested materials in the Danish outdoor climate. An increasing problem is the
more effective insulation of our houses, non­insulated attics above insulated grown floors,
gets less and less escaped heat from the heated part of the house. This leads to lower
temperatures, higher relative humidity and thereby higher risk of mould growth. Models of
a cold­ventilated attics are made in WUFI Pro (software for one­dimensional hygrothermal
calculations on buildings) , with boards of plywood, massive spruce or OSB as a base for
roofing felt on the outer surface of the roof structure.
The test of moisturising and drying of the pine samples were, in a preliminary test, tested
with a floating test and moisture content was calculated by the change in mass. After­
wards, nails and a moisture meter were used to measure the local moisture content in
different spots in the samples. The results showed a higher absorption capacity in the
longitudinal direction compared to the tangential and radial, where the radial had the low­
est capacity of absorption. But looking at the measured local moisture content the results
showed that water travelled faster in the tangential compared to the longitudinal direction,
which from the theory was not expected and the results are therefore questioned and dis­
cussed in the thesis. The results also showed a fast achievement of the fibre saturation
point (FSP) in the outer layers of plywood compared to the massive wood.
The results of the mould test showed a lower resistance in the plywood than massive
samples of the same sort, but in general, a higher resistance was seen for the spruce
samples than for the pine. The OSB performed better against mould than the tested
plywood and massive pine but also the largest variation in mould resistance along the
surface were found. The pine samples were all 100% covered of mould after 32 days,
spruce plywood and OSB after 38 days, and spruce massive were at the end of the test
after 49 days not fully covered.
The results found with the simulations were similar to the ones found in the absorption
tests when comparing the plywood to the massive spruce. For the OSB the lowest max­
imum moisture contents were found at 22%, while the plywood and massive spruce both
had maximum moisture contents at 29­31%.
From the performed experiments in this thesis it is concluded that a connection between
the moisture content on the surface and resistance to mould can be drawn. A fast achieve­
ment of fibre saturation point (FSP), as seen in plywood, results in a lower resistance to
mould. But it is also concluded that mould growth is not only depending on moisture con­
tent, but may also depend on among others type of wood, location and direction in the
trunk, time since cut, and surface structure, which are not investigated in this project.

Sammenfatning
Skimmelsvamp er et stort problem, da det findes i huse, som vi opholder os i, og har været
et kendt sundhedsmæssigt problem i mange år. Det er ofte set på træ og andre organiske
byggematerialer, og mere hyppigt på krydsfiner end på massive træbrædder, hvilket der
ikke umiddelbart findes en forklaring på.
I dette kandidatspeciale undersøges kapaciteten for opfugtning af krydsfiner sammenlig­
net med massivt træ, udsat for vand i forskellige retninger til fibrene. Krydsfineren og det
massive radiata fyr , som undersøges, er lavet af træ importeret fra samme savværk og
er groet i samme område og klima. Derved er kun behandlingsmetoden og variationen af
splint og kerneved forskellig i prøverne.
Derudover er modstandsdygtigheden mod skimmelsvamp for de to typer fyrmaterialer
testet i skimmelsvampkammer, sammen med krydsfiner og massivt træ i gran og OSB
prøver. I denne forbindelse observeres opsugningen af vand i prøverne også, for at un­
dersøge en sammenhæng mellem fugtindhold og skimmelmodstand.
Som den sidste del af de eksperimentelle test, er der blevet lavet simuleringer for at ana­
lysere opsugningsevnen for de testede materialer i et udendørs dansk klima. Et stigende
problem er her set, hvor isoleringen i bygninger effektiviseres, som medfører et ikke iso­
leret tagrum får mindre undsluppen varme fra den opvarmede underetage. Dette medfø­
rer lavere temperaturer, højere relativ luftfugtighed og højere risiko for skimmelsvamp i
loftrummet. I programmet WUFI er der lavet modeller af et uopvarmet, ventileret hulrum
med krydsfiner­, massiv­, eller OSB­plader som underlag for tagpap i en loftkonstruktion.
Testene for opfugtning og udtørring af fyrprøver blev i et indledende forsøg testet ved
flydemetoden, hvor fugtindholdet blev bestemt ud fra ændring af masse. Derefter blev
søm og en fugtmåler brugt til at måle det lokale fugtindhold i forskellige punkter i prøven.
Resultaterne viste en høj absoberingsevne i den langsgående retning sammenlignet med
den tangentielle og radiale, hvor den dårligste evne til at opsuge vand blev fundet i den ra­
diale retning. Det blev dog fundet, at vandet flyttede sig hurtigst i den tangentielle retning,
hvilket baseret på teorien ikke var forventet, og der stilles derfor spørgsmål ved dette,
som diskuteres i projektet. Resultaterne viste også, at fibermætningspunktet blev opnået
hurtigere i de yderste lag i krydsfineren sammenlignet med samme afstand til overfladen
i de massive prøver.
Resultaterne for skimmelforsøgene viste en lavere modstandsdygtighed hos krydsfiner
end hos de massive prøver indenfor samme træsort, men generelt var en højere mod­
standsdygtighed observeret hos gran sammenlignet med fyr. OSB prøverne viste en hø­
jere modstandsdygtighed end de testede krydsfiner og den massive fyr, men også den
største variation langs overfladen. Fyrprøverne var alle 100% dækket af skimmel efter 32
dage, grankrydsfineren og OSB efter 38 dage, og gran massive var ved forsøgets sidste
måling efter 49 dage ikke fuldt dækket endnu.
Resultaterne fundet i simuleringerne var meget lig resultaterne i absoberingstestene, når
man kigger på krydsfiner og massiv fyr. Det laveste maksimale fugtindhold blev fundet for
OSB på 22%, mens det maximale fugtindhold for krydsfiner og massiv lå omkring 29­30%.
Det er konkluderet ud fra de udførte eksperimenter i dette kandidatspeciale, at der kan
findes en sammenhæng mellem fugtindhold og skimmelmodstandsdygtighed. Træmateri­
ale som på kort tid rammer fibermætningspunktet i overfalden når træet udsættes for fugt,
som det ses ved krydsfiner, viser en lavere modstandsdygtighed overfor skimmelsvamp.
Det bliver også konkluderet, at mængden af skimmelsvamp ikke kun afhænger af fugtind­
hold, men kan også afhænge af bl.a. træsort, placering og retning i træstammen, tid siden
udskæring og overfladens struktur, hvilket ikke er undersøgt i dette projekt.

1
Introduction
For many years mould has been an issue for buildings all over the world and is still an
increasing problem. The problem is both material damage from where the fungi grow and
the health issues arising from spending time in an indoor environment affected by the
poisonous gasses mould brings into the buildings [17]. The material damage from mould
is mainly discolouration, and therefore the most critical and discussed effect of mould is
the health problems it leads to.
The mould is found in different materials used for construction purpose, but the growth
depends among others on the amount of organic compounds in the material [14]. There­
fore wood materials are especially vulnerable to mould growth, a material that is widely
used for both load­bearing structures and secondary construction indoor and outdoor.
Many types of treatments and coverings are used today to prevent mould growth on
wooden materials. Chemical treatments that are often used may be expensive and causes
difficulties in the recycling process. An attractive alternative to these methods is investig­
ating the potential of different types of wood to find a type of wood that is more resistant
to mould by nature [22].
1.1
Motivation
For the past years in the modern building industry, there has been a focus on optimizing
buildings to lower the energy used for heating. In Denmark almost 40% of the energy is
used primarily for heating, ventilation and lighting in buildings. Therefore with the aim to
increase the the use of energy for heating, better insulation to keep the warm air inside
the house or office building is an important factor.
Many buildings build before the 21st century consist of an insulated part and an unin­
sulated ventilated attic. As the houses are renovated and more insulation is added to
save energy and comply with updated requirements the attic stays unheated. The bet­
ter insulation means less escaped head coming from the living area of the house to the
attic, where the temperature now is lower especially in the winter, than before the renov­
ation. By that, more and more cases with growing of mould in unheated attics are seen [4].
In the Danish Standard [5] buildings are divided into three service classes, which set the
framework of requirements and calculations of the individual structure.
For buildings in service class 2 among other, the requirements state:
Ventilated constructions protected against precipitation, for example, ventil­
ated roof constructions.
For buildings in service class 3 another requirement is formulated as follows:
Base for asphalt roofing, if these are not designed so that they can be attributed
to service class 2.

service class 2 or 3 only depends on whether it is ventilated or not. This implies that a

rooms belong in service class 2, even though damp rooms belong to class 3. This results

1.2
Purpose and method
The purpose of this master thesis is to investigate how the production of wood material
affects the mould growth. It is known that mould acts differently under the same climate
conditions depending on the wood. Especially cases with mould growing faster on ply­
wood compared to other wood materials are often seen. The reason for this tendency
remains unclear, and could be due to the effect of glue on the moisture content, the choice
of material, or due to the treatment the plywood undergoes in production.
In this project, different wood materials are investigated; how the water travels through the
wood and the connection to mould growth behaviour. This is done by laboratory testing of
water adsorption and desorption of plywood, and massive wood pieces to investigate the
water transport in different fibre directions. The two materials have been imported from
the same sawmill and thereby produced from the same trees grown next to each other,
but going through different types of production methods. By that, the only variable factor
is the production method of producing either massive samples or plywood samples.
To investigate the relation between the moisture absorption and mould resistance, a test
for mould growth is made on the samples as well, where also samples of another sort and
material are investigated. As a supplement to the experimental work 1D simulations of a
roof structure is made to analyze the moist behaviour of wood material in the Danish out­
door climate. With the simulations, the experiential results are compared to the situation
of mould problems we see in buildings all around the world.
1.2.1
Limitations of project
This project focuses on answering the purpose by a simple experiential work with the
available equipment at the Danish Technological Institute and has be completed within
the five months allowed.
The contact with a sawmill in Chile has made it possible to work with two different wood
materials produced by the same supplier. This is rarely seen and contributes to an ana­
lysis with a low level of uncertainty. For the other samples used for the mould test, this has
not been possible, and therefore comes with more uncertainties when comparing those.
The simulations are an alternative to an actual experiment. Reaching the results that are
found from the simulations experimentally requires more time and funds than available in
this project.
1.2.2
Structure of report
This master thesis starts with a literature study made prior to and concurrently with the
execution of the experiments as a background knowledge on the subject. Then a chapter
on the relevant theory regarding wood as a material and its relation to moisture is found. In
the following parts the materials and the method of the performed experiments are presen­
ted, as the methods of the tests not completely follow any standard, the test method is
described for the results to be sufficiently understood, which are presented in the following
chapter. Based on the material presented in the report, the discussion of the results are
performed in the following chapter. At the end of the report, a conclusion is made, and
relevant options for future work within this subject is presented. At the end of the report,
raw data and all results obtained during the experiments are found.

2.1
Effect of relative humidity and temperature on mould
growth
Wood is one of the most mould­affected materials when viewing building materials that
we use for houses. Earlier studies show that a high content of organic compounds in a
material results in a less water is required for mould to grow. Therefore when compar­
ing wood to materials such as gypsum or concrete, which has a lower amount of organic
content, less of water is needed to provoke mould in wood [11] [15].
In a laboratory study made to test the critical moisture level of mould growth [15], ten dif­
ferent building materials were tested by spraying a solution with six different mould types
on the samples and rating them from 0­4, where 2 is denoted as the critical mould level.
Among these were plywood, chipboard and pine sapwood together with the other build­
ing materials exposed to a mixture of spores from six types of fungal typically observed
in affected damp houses. The fungal was sprayed on one surface of each test piece and
placed in different climate chambers where the temperature and relative humidity were
controlled. From the test it was then possible to see the mould growth behaviour depend­
ing on material type, temperature and relative humidity (RH).
The results showed that for none of the wood pieces mould was observed at a relative hu­
midity of 75% after 12 weeks, but after 16 and 32 weeks mould was found on plywood and
sapwood respectively at 75% RH. To reach a faster growth of mould 80% RH or higher
was needed.

Theory
3.1
Wood
The construction wood that we use today for load­bearing structures, floors and wood
cladding is made from trunk which purpose has been to take the load from the crown of
the tree and transport water from the roots up to the leaves in the top. Therefore wood is
an unseen material for construction, but also a challenge because of its living behaviour
[20].
3.1.1
The physical structure of wood
When sawing a trunk the capacities of the material differ from the outer parts to parts
closer to the centre. Therefore the physical and mechanical properties of a piece of wood
depend on their locations in the trunk. The tree trunk consists of different elements which
are illustrated in fig. 3.3.
Figure 3.1: Cross section of trunk [8]
The outer layer of the trunk is the Outer bark which consists of dead cells to protect the
trunk from external harm. The Inner bark is the living cells of the bark, which transports
food to the places in the Cambium layer where the trunk grows. On the outside of the
cambium layer, new bark cells are created and on the inside new wood cells. On the
inside of the cambium layer, the wood of the trunk is situated and can be divided into two
types, the Sapwood (outer part) and the Heartwood (inner part). The sapwood consists
of active cells that transport water through the trunk by a negative pressure created by
the leaves in the crown of the tree. The heartwood consists of dead cells which no longer
contribute to water transport. The sapwood and heartwood consist of long thin tubular
cells which are mainly orientated in the direction of the trunk direction. A small part of the
cells are orientated radial to the trunk direction and are called medullary rays. These cells
are found from the cambium layer into the Pith (the center of the trunk) and transport and
store food between the layers. The number of rays depend on the type of wood. All the
cells in the tree have cell walls consisting of cellulose and are bound together by lignin
[18][20].

Microstructure of softwood
Softwood trees are often known for their cone shape, naked seeds and needle­like leaves.
The wood has a simple construction compared to hardwood as approximately 90% of soft­

the lumen and can be seen in fig. 3.2 (right). The purpose of the cell lumen is to transport
liquids through the wood while the cell wall in the tracheids brings strength to the wood.
In these cells, the pores are placed to transport water from one cell to another in radial
direction of the tree trunk.
Approximately 10% of the wood is parenchymal cells which are used for storage of nutri­

3.2.1
Measurement of moisture in wood
One of the purposes of the tree trunk is to transport water. Therefore when used as
building material, water will still move through the wood as in a living tree. The water
content affects, among other things, the density, dimensions, strength and deformation
ratio and biological persistence.
The water content of wood, u, is measured in mass percentage and calculated as in
equation 3.1:

where mwater is the mass of evaporable water, mdry is the dry mass of the wood piece,
and mwet is the mass of the wet wood piece.
Freshly cut wood, also named green wood, has a higher content of moisture compared
to the same type in dried condition. A newly cut tree often contains the same amount of
water as the level in a dry matter, and most of the water will typically be placed in the
active sapwood and a smaller amount in the less active heartwood [16][20].
3.2.2
Moisture transport
When water is moved from wood there are two types of water evaporation; the molecularly
bound water and the molecularly unbound water. The first water type to leave the wood in
a drying process is the unbound water, placed in the cell lumen of the wood. When there
is no more unbound water in the cell and the drying process continues, the bound water
starts to evaporate. The bound water is placed in the cell walls and is fixed by hydrogen
bond. When unbound water leaves a cell lumen it happens as an aspiration of the pore
connecting the tracheids by capillary forces. These forces result in hydrostatic tensile
stress in the remaining bound water. The water in the cell lumen often contains small air
bubbles, where the water in the cell lumen with a large amount of air will dry first, typically
in or close to the surface. Therefore, there can be water starting to leave the cell walls
(bound water) when there is still water left in the cell lumen (unbound water) deeper into
the wood sample.
The water content corresponding to all unbound water being removed and only the level
of water that can be present as bound water is left is called the Fibre Saturation Point
(FSP). The value for the FSP depends on the tree type, but is often between 25­30 M­%.
When the water content in the wood decreases to be smaller than the FSP, the wood piece
will release water to its surroundings and shrink into a smaller volume. By increasing the
water content from the FSP to a higher value, water will be absorbed and the volume
increase.
The hydrostatic tensile stresses in the cells coming from the capillary forces, which are
present in the cells when the moisture content is below FSP result in a diffusion of water
molecules that diffuse through the lumen and cell walls. This process below the FSP is
slow and takes more energy than the process of water transport through the pores when
the moisture content is above the FSP. Therefore the drying process will take longer time
when the moisture content is below the FSP, than if it is above.

Adsorption of water in dry wood happens due to capillary forces in the different cells and
pores. As mentioned earlier, the main task of the trunk is to transport water from the roots
to the crown. Therefore when a dry piece of wood adsorbs water adsorption capacity will
be distributed as 1:2:40 for tangential, radial and longitudinal direction respectively.
The FSP also marks a boundary for the properties of the wood, as when the water content
is below FSP, the physical and mechanical properties depend greatly on the water content,
while most of the properties are independent of the water content when it is above FSP
[16][20].
3.2.3
Sorption Hysteresis
When a piece of wood is neither gaining or losing moisture to its surroundings at a con­
stant temperature and rh, the wood has achieved an equilibrium moisture content (EMC).
Sorption isotherm describes the relationship between RH and EMC. An example of this
relationship is shown as a sorption hysteresis in fig. 3.4. The relationship varies for each
type of tree, and the figure is thereby a an example of one situation. A sorption hysteresis
shows the effect of a wood specimen’s history on the EMC. The desorption process is
measured when the wood is going from wet or FSP to dry condition by lowering the RH in
the surroundings. The black line in the figure shows the initial desorption which is the first
drying process after a newly cut down tree in green condition. After the first desorption,
a repetition of the same process will result in a desorption curve being below the initial
desorption curve. The blue line in the figure shows the opposite process, where the wood
is going from a dry state to FSP by increasing the RH called adsorption, which is placed
even further down the diagram [16].

3.2.4
Drying of wood
When wood is drying, the moisture content at the surface is lower than it is in the centre.
Thereby the water moves from the centre and out and evaporates into the surrounding
air. The lower the level of water in the wood piece, the lower the difference in moisture

content between the surface and the centre will be.
When the surface is moisturised it will begin to shrink and tension will occur in the surface.
The tension in the surface is opposite action as the dry center in the wood, where a
pressure is acting. If the tension in the outer wood gets too large, bending, twisting or
even external cracks will be arise. The external cracks will be visible in the surface and will
typically occur in the end grain which often dries 10­20 times faster than perpendicularly
to the fibres.
After the outer part of the wood dries and the centre part begins crossing the FSP the
tension in the outer turns to pressure and tension begins in the centre. In this situation,
internal cracks can occur, and might not be visible on the surface.
When the outer part shrinks the tension in the centre can create a collapse in the cell
lumen, when the free water is leaving the wood. The chances of collabs increase as the
temperature increase when the temperature is more than 60◦C. At this point, the cell walls
have become softened and thereby lost their strength [19].

Discussion
In the present chapter, the results presented in chapter 6 will be discussed and evaluated.
7.1
Water transportation in massive Radiate Pine
For all tested wood samples it is relevant to investigate how the massive wood material
behaves as all test samples consist of massive pieces of wood. It has been tested how
the water is adsorbed in a longitudinal, radial and tangential direction.
In fig. 7.1 the results from all tested massive wood samples tested on surface A, B and
C have been collected to be presented as moisture gradients. Each of the four diagrams
shows the moisture gradient at one point in the time of the test period. The results presen­
ted in fig. 6.4 for surface A, fig. 6.7 for surface B and 6.6 for surface C have been meas­
ured at different distances to the water, therefore the most suitable way to compare those
are by the moisture gradients, even though only two have been made for surface A, as
the test period for these were shorter than for the rest.
7.1.1
Longitudinal and tangential direction
Looking at fig. 6.4 the water took 7 hours in tangential direction to reach the 10 mm sensor
and then anotheer 17 hours to reach the 20 mm sensor. This shows that the water travels
more and more slowly through the piece of wood the longer into the wood it gets.
From fig. 7.1a and 7.1b it is clear that a very high moisture content in the tangential direc­
tion is achieved compared to the other directions. After three days the moisture content
in the tangential direction is 35.9% 20 mm into the piece of wood. Looking at fig. 7.1c
approximately the same moisture content for longitudinal direction was achieved 40 mm
into the wood after 17 days i.e., 5.5 times longer time than the half­length in tangential
direction.
7.1.2
Tangential and radial direction
In fig. 6.4 it can also be seen that after 28 hours, a moisture content of 21,5% was meas­
ured 20 mm into the wood in tangential direction. In fig. 7.1d a similar moisture content
was achieved 20 mm in, in radial direction after 28 days. Thereby the water travelled
approximately 24 times faster in the tangential direction than in the radial direction.
7.1.3
Radial and longitudinal direction
The movement of water in the two directions is compared in fig. 7.1, where it is seen that
40 mm into the wood, a moisture content of 14% is measured in radial direction after 28
days, while for longitudinal direction the same moisture content is achieved after 13 days,
more than two times faster.
Looking at the development in moisture contents a general tendency is seen for all three
directions in the massive test samples: When the 5 mm sensor reaches a moisture con­
tent of approximately 15%, the 10 mm sensor measure the first increase in moisture con­
tent. Also for all directions, none of the mean values of the moisture content above 40%
have been registered, even though the moisture meter can register moisture contents
up to 47.1%. The measurements become very uncertain when the moisture content has
exceeded the FSP, and therefore there will be no further discussion of this.

Comparison of absorbed level of water
While the sensors are used for measuring the moisture content at a specific point, the

longitudinal direction. According to the theory for moisture transportation described in
section 3.2.2 a wood sample adsorbs 40 times more water in the longitudinal direction
than in the tangential direction. The same relationship is not seen in these results, where
the level of adsorbed water in the longitudinal direction is less than 2.5 times larger level
of compared to the tangential direction. Also, the velocity of the water mentioned earlier,

which was 5.5 times the half­length, meaning 11 times larger in the tangential direction,
is even less in accordance with the theory. The different test set­up of the samples tested
on surface A (tangential direction) compared to the other two tests can be an explanation
for the very large difference in the results and the theory. For the surface A test the water
could travel a maximum of 2.5 cm, because of the height of the tested board. It is seen

moving towards a constant value. The tendency seen from the massive wood is that the
water is spread into the piece, therefore if the water had the possibility to travel more than

content in general in the sample. Another difference in the test set­up was that surface

the bottom of the water container. Surface A was fully covered with water and the self­

can also be the explanation for the water travelling 24 times faster in tangential direction
compared to the radial, which is a large difference when the water was expected to travel
two times the distance in radial direction compared to the tangential. This large difference
also casts doubt on whether the sealing used for covering the untested surface of the
samples has been dysfunctional. This would make the biggest falls of the samples tested
in tangential direction by the floating test since parts of the ends (the ability for water to
travel in longitudinal direction) were below water level.

larger than in the radial direction (MB) as stated in the theory, but after 31 days (last

were only shortened in the long direction, so while surface A and B were cut in the sawmill
in Chile, surface B was cut at the Danish Technological Institute right before the paint was
applied. Looking at the results, this might have affected the ability to adsorb water.

Potential of the program
The software WUFI is normally used by engineers in the Danish building industry to make
a hygrothermal performance of buildings. It is used in this project to reach results in a
real­life situation outside the lab, and this is comparable to the results achieved in the
experiments. Working in WUFI was decided because of the 1D set­up, which makes the
program simple but still sufficient for the purpose of this project. Another advantage of
this program has been the technique found in Vapour Barrier in Cold Ventilated Attics ­
The use of vapour barriers in ceilings against cold ventilated attics, with different amounts
and types of insulation materials [10] for a ventilated attic which is not directly accessible
in many programs as this.
The disadvantages of the program have been the way to model of the wood materials
have been analysed in this project. The materials are homogeneous layers with thickness
and material parameters such as heat capacity, dry density and water vapor diffusion
resistance factor. In that way when drawing moisture gradients as in fig. 7.12, the layers
of e.g. plywood consisting of alternate glue and tangential cut wood are not taken into
account. Thereby the conditions in the surroundings are relatively correct, but how the
water is distributed in the individual material, is more questionable. With that, the results
from this part should be taken with caution.

8
Conclusion
Based on the discussion of the results from the performed experiments in this project, a
conclusion of the wood materials’ behaviour when affected by water and mould can be
drawn.
From the adsorption tests of the radiate pine, it can be concluded that most water is
adsorbed in the longitudinal direction of the massive wood piece, as stated in theory.
The study showed a larger adsorption capacity in the tangential direction and a smaller
longitudinal than expected, concluding that the different tests performed have affected the
result, or that the time from the side of the sample was cut has an influence on the water
absorption.
It is concluded that the fastest achievements of FSP are found in plywood in the outer lay­
ers. The glue layer has an impact on the water spreading, as the moisture content reaches
FSP in the second layer before the water moves into the third layer. In the massive wood,
the water is more evenly distributed into the wood piece, but at one point they reach the
same moisture contents in the surface as plywood. The same tendency was seen in the
simulations when the wood material is used for roofing felt in a roof structure. The OSB
material did not achieve as high moisture contents as the massive and plywood material,
and here an even larger difference in moisture content is seen from the moist gradient in
the board than found in plywood. From that it is concluded that the high content of glue
in the OSB reduces the capacity of water adsorption.
This study showed a higher mould growth on the tested plywood samples compared to
the massive sample of the same sort. The radiate pine was found to be less resistant to
mould growth compared to the spruce and OSB samples, due to either the massive pine
being cut tangentially while the spruce was cut radially, or the spruce as the sort being
more resistant to mould.
A relation was found between the moisture content on the surface of the radiate pine and
mould growth. From the experiments performed in this project, it is found that high mois­
ture content, achieved quickly in plywood, results in low resistance to mould growth.
The OSB showed higher resistance to the mould than the plywood samples and the
massive pine, and also the lowest moisture content in the mould chamber. Along with
that a large difference was seen in the OSB samples where some veneers were affected
by mould after 2 weeks, while others with the same surface took 4 weeks incubation time
before mould was observed. By that, it is concluded that the location in the sapwood has
an effect on the mould resistance. Also, the glue in OSB is found to improve the res­
istance to mould when compared to plywood despite thicker layers it still showed lower
resistance.

Future work
In this master thesis, a relation between moisture on the surface and mould growth has
been found, from the conducted experiments it was also seen that mould depends on
other factors as well. This leads to a possibility of new research areas to analyse mould
growth on untreated wood materials.
When the results from the water absorption of the massive and plywood samples were
processed, large deviations were found, and some outcomes were unexpected due to the
knowledge of the theory. Therefore, new execution of these tests with a higher number
of test samples, as well as more accurate sensors with a higher number of measurement
points are relevant to this topic. Within the same test, a focus on different cutting methods
of massive wood is interesting, as the pine and spruce acted differently, but due to several
uncertainties the effect of the tangential versus the radial was unknown.
As the wood is found to react differently to mould due to types, location in the trunk and
treatment method, it leaves a question of what would happen if the material was a mix of
the different factors mentioned. How would the plywood board with outer layers consisting
of heartwood or outer layers having twice the thickness as the middle layers resist to
mould?Products like these would be more expensive to produce, but research within the
subject could verify whether it will extend the lifetime of the product and also cause lesser
health damage than the products available at the moment.
