Abstract 
The idea of using hydrogen as an energy carrier to provide flexibility in the energy system has been articulated 
and debated extensively throughout the 20th and into the 21st century. This thesis investigates the potential 
role of hydrogen technologies (water electrolysis, hydrogen storage, fuel cells and hydrogenation of biogas) in 
the Danish heat- and electricity system, which faces an increased deployment of variable renewable energy 
sources, especially wind power. The role of hydrogen is analyzed and compared to other flexibility options 
(transmission, demand response and dispatchable generation) in order to determine the optimal level of 
hydrogen production, storage and electricity generation based on hydrogen. This is done with the Balmorel 
Energy System Model, in a long-term investment optimization where electricity and heat generation is 
balanced to meet demand on an hourly level. The Balmorel add-ons COMBTECH and HYDROGEN are 
modified to adequately represent electricity generation based on hydrogen. The research conducted in this 
thesis reveals, that hydrogen technologies could optimally constitute 4.8 % of total electricity generation by 
2050, corresponding to 8.7 % of total fuel consumption for electricity and heat. Exact levels of hydrogen 
storage capacity are difficult to determine, but could be within the range 0.4 TWh to 1.0 TWh. Hydrogen 
becomes more attractive if the transmission network is not expanded beyond what is currently planned and 
if dispatchable generation from biomass and waste is restricted. In contrast, hydrogen becomes less attractive 
if demand response measures (load shifting) becomes available. Results are sensitive to modelling methods 
and data assumptions: Firstly, testing different time aggregation methods indicate that both time resolution 
and time-step selection have a large influence on the consumption and storage level of hydrogen. The thesis 
is not able to quantify the quality of different aggregation methods, but further energy system modelling 
research into energy storage must pay detailed attention to this issue. Secondly, the assumptions on techno-
economic data for hydrogen technologies and the prices of biogas influences the optimal investment pathway. 
Nevertheless, hydrogen could have a potential across all price- and efficiency sensitivities.

1. 
Introduction 
The latest report from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) describes pathways for 
limiting global warming to 1.5C above preindustrial levels, which require a rapid, far-reaching and 
unprecedented transition in energy, land, urban, infrastructure and industrial systems (IPCC, 2018). On a 
global scale, this means that the energy sector should reach net zero CO2 emissions as fast as possible; possibly 
with economically developed nations leading the way, including Denmark. 
As stated in the current Danish Energy Agreement, Denmark aims to become a net zero emitter of CO2, latest 
by 2050 (EKFM, 2018). Although Denmark has already reached a large renewable energy (RE) share of 29 % 
of total primary consumption, a large deployment of renewable energy sources is yet to be undertaken 
(Eurostat, 2019). Faced with the challenge of decarbonizing energy systems; energy planners, researchers and 
policy makers are continuously searching for and developing a transition pathway that balances economic 
competitiveness, security of supply and the urgent need to reduce CO2 emissions (Giannakidis et al., 2015).   
With an increasing share of variable renewable energy (VRE) production from wind and solar, a key element 
for long-term energy planning is to ensure the right amount of system flexibility to sufficiently balance energy 
supply and energy demand (DEA, 2015). Broadly speaking, flexibility is provided by a combination of: flexible 
production, flexible consumption, transmission connections to surrounding countries and energy storage. The 
relative importance of each component depends crucially on future energy markets, energy policies and 
technological improvements (IEA, 2014).  
The Danish decarbonizing strategy for the energy sector has, until now, been dominated by integration of 
large amounts of on- and offshore wind power along with the substitution of oil and coal by natural gas, biogas 
and biomass (Further details in Section 2.3). While a transition pathway based on dispatchable biomass plants 
does not cause the same flexibility challenges as with large amounts of intermittent wind and solar power, the 
consumption of biomass is problematic with regards to suitability, CO2-neutrality and resource scarcity 
(Klimarådet, 2018). 
The idea of using hydrogen as an energy carrier and as a storage media has been discussed for more than 200 
years and interest in the potential for hydrogen production, storage and consumption has been particularly 
strong in Denmark, due to its historically large deployment of wind power (Further details in Section 1.2). 
Despite optimism in the early 00’s, hydrogen technologies have not yet experienced a commercial 
breakthrough. However, future price reductions, technological advancements, the imperative of 
decarbonization and the growing need for energy system flexibility could bring hydrogen technologies back as 
a key technology in the energy transition.   
Energy system modelling provides important insights into promising and cost-efficient technologies, thereby 
qualifying the much-debated question about hydrogens potential. In this light, the objective of this thesis is 
to investigate the potential role of hydrogen by simulating hydrogen technologies in the energy system model 
(ESM) Balmorel. The objective is to analyze the role of hydrogen both as a mean to absorb electricity in 
periods of high production and to act as back up capacity in periods with low VRE production. The thesis 
focuses specifically on the Danish energy system, and includes important surrounding countries, in order to 
adequately simulate the interdependencies of energy systems though transmission.

1.1 
Hydrogen terminology and scope 
The following section introduces the terminology used when discussing different hydrogen technologies. The 
options for producing and consuming hydrogen are plenty, and each of the processes contribute with unique 
benefits for energy system flexibility. The suggested terminology might simplify the complex array of available 
technologies, but serves to make a clear and comprehensible thesis. 
Today almost 50 % of the global hydrogen production comes from steam reforming of natural gas. Hydrogen 
is both used for non-energy purposes (production of fertilizers) and in the energy industry for converting heavy 
hydrocarbons into gasoline and diesel fuel. Only about 4 % is currently produced by water electrolysis, a 
process where electricity is used to split water into hydrogen (H2) and oxygen (O2) (Navarro et al., 2015). 
After production, hydrogen must be stored.  
For energy purposes, hydrogen can used in (at least) five different ways:  
1) Direct consumption for high-heat purposes in industries.  
2) Direct use in transportation with fuel cell electric vehicles.  
3) Production of synthetic drop-in fuels for transportation (hydrogen to fuel, H2F),  
4) Hydrogen upgrade of biogas (hydrogenation), where the CO2 content of biogas is reacted with 
hydrogen to form methane (hydrogen to gas, H2G). This gas can be injected directly into the existing 
natural gas grid and consumed interchangeable with natural gas.  
5) Direct consumption of hydrogen in fuels cells for electricity production (hydrogen to power, H2P) 
This process also generates heat, which can be utilized in the district heating network. All production 
and consumption processes are illustrated in Figure 1.1.  
Hydrogen electrolysis can provide flexibility on the demand side, as production can planned to absorb low-
price electricity in periods of high VRE production. Theoretically, this means that a stimulation of all six 
consumption processes indirectly could provide system wide flexibility, if hydrogen is produced from 
electrolysis. In contrast, only H2G and H2P contributes with supply side flexibility, because they generate 
electricity.  
  
Figure 1.1. Scope of thesis and terminology. 
This thesis focuses on the potential of hydrogen when providing both demand and supply side flexibility. 
Referring to Figure 1.1, this means that electrolysis, hydrogen storage, H2G and H2P is included in the 
research. These technologies will be referred to as H2 technologies. Since both H2G and H2P consume hydrogen 
and generates electricity, these technologies are characterized as technologies for electricity storage.

1.2 
Background 
For a long time, hydrogen has been envisioned as the fuel of the future. In a famous Jules Verne quote from 
1874, a progressive engineer assures his audience, which worries for the expiration of coal resources, that 
hydrogen eventually will replace coal: 
 
“Yes, my friends, I believe that water will one day be employed as fuel, that hydrogen and oxygen which constitute it, used 
singly or together, will furnish an inexhaustible source of heat and light, of an intensity of which coal is not capable. 
Someday the coal rooms of steamers and the tenders of locomotives will, instead of coal, be stored with these two condensed 
gases, which will burn in the furnaces with enormous calorific power. There is, therefore, nothing to fear” (Verne, 2004). 
Since the end of the 18th century, alkaline electrolysis has been used to produce hydrogen (DEA, 2018a). In a 
Danish context, the scientist Poul la Cour from Askov Højskole was carrying out experiments in 1891, that 
connected a wind turbine with a simple electrode and electrolyte set up in order to save wind energy for later 
illumination needs (Nissen, 2009).  
Currently, academics have been debating the prospects of a “hydrogen-society”; Bossel et al. (2003) claims 
that “… the vision of a hydrogen fuel cell economy will remain a dream forever” due to high conversion losses 
and better alternatives. Amory B. Lovins (2005) presents the argument that most of the criticism surrounding 
hydrogen is based on basis errors and common misconception.  
For more than 40 years the Danish Energy Agency (DEA) has supported research in fuel cell technologies 
(Sørensen and Sørensen, 2015) and since 2004 Denmark has invested 288 mio. euro in Research, Development 
and Deployment (RD&D) of hydrogen and fuel cells technologies. This accounts for almost 17 % of total 
national spending on energy RD&D. In comparison, the United States invested 3152 mio. euro in hydrogen 
and fuels cells RD&D in the same period, equivalent to approximately  4 % of the national energy RD&D 
budget (IEA, 2019). 
In the early 00’s a hydrogen scenario analysis carried out at Roskilde University concluded that “…it is likely, 
but not certain, that hydrogen technologies within 10-20 years will obtain an economically favorable position 
at the energy markets…” (Sørensen et al., 2001).  This conclusion was based on the observation, that scarcity 
of oil resources would stimulate market demand for hydrogen, and that electric battery vehicles (EBV) would 
not be able to compete with electric fuel cells vehicles (EFCV). In hindsight, none of these observations hold 
true today, where oil prices do not show compelling signs of scarcity and where the size of the current EFCV 
fleet has been largely outnumbered by EBV (EAFO, 2019; OECD, 2019).  
To stimulate hydrogen research, a white paper produced in 2004 by the DTU community, presented a 
suggestion for a research strategy, which could bring Denmark in the forefront of international hydrogen 
research, with possible economic and environmental benefits (Nørskov, 2004). 
Moreover both the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA), Hydrogen Europe1 (HE) and Dansk 
Energi (DE) have published reports on the future prospects for hydrogen. IRENA and HE highlight the large 
potential for transport, industry (especially for high heat processes) and for either pure or direct blending of

hydrogen into the gas grid or for hydrogen based biogas upgrade. Large scale electricity production by 
hydrogen fuel cells are deemed lees probable, due to high efficiency loses, but could be relevant for niche 
application, like electricity generation in remote areas with poor or zero connection to the electricity grid 
(Hydrogen Europe, 2019; IRENA, 2018). DE’s analysis for the period until 2035 is less optimistic and 
concludes, that hydrogen technologies, both for transport and in connection with the gas grid, are too 
expensive compared to alternatives such as electric battery cars and biogas. However, this conclusion should 
be revised, if a growing demand for biomass based fuels in combination with ambitious CO2 mitigating policies, 
causes biomass prices to rise (DE, 2016). 
In parallel with these reports, multiple energy system modelling research has been conducted on the possible 
role of hydrogen (Further details in Section 4.1). The thrust of this research points in the same direction as 
IRENA and HE, and highlights the importance of hydrogen production for transport, industries and as a 
flexible consumption technology for absorbing low price electricity. As a storage media for later electricity 
production (either with fuel cells, hydrogen gas turbines or in combination with the natural gas grid) hydrogen 
is a less obvious component.  
1.3 
Research question 
Although parts of previous research only deems hydrogen a marginal role for H2P and H2G, at least four 
reasons justify further research into the potential role of exactly these technologies: 
Firstly, some studies do not adequately include H2G technologies in their simulations. An exception is 
provided by Jensen et al. (2019), but the specific implementation (formulated in the Balmorel add-on 
COMBTECH and HYDROGEN) could be adjusted (see Chapter 6) in a way that improves economics of both 
H2P and H2G. 
Secondly, the importance of hydrogen storage, H2P and H2G is not always analyzed in direct relation to other 
flexibility elements. Hydrogen storage could become more relevant if transmissions lines to surrounding 
countries are not further extended or if dispatchable generation from waste or biomass is reduced, while 
electricity demand in the future is expected to grow. With all options considered, the large deployment of 
VRE does not demand storage pr. se. Rather, what is needed is energy system flexibility, and storage options 
should be investigated in this light.  
Thirdly, many previous Energy Systems Models (ESM) studies on hydrogen have a multinational scope, where 
a specific focus on Denmark is not provided. Larger attention to nation-specific technology potentials could 
be important, especially in light of the present energy strategy of biogas and the high wind power potential 
in Denmark an (see section 2.2.2 and 2.2.3). In addition, the storage option, relative to other flexibility 
elements, could be uniquely sensitive to exactly Danish characteristics.  
Fourthly, not much attention is drawn towards the complex task of choosing a representative time aggregation 
technique when modelling long-term energy and electricity storage. As described in Chapter 7, time 
aggregation can potentially have a large influence on particularly VRE and storage investments.  
With these observations in mind, the research question for this thesis is as follows:  
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
What is the future Danish potential for electricity storage with hydrogen, both including electricity generation 
from fuel cells and hydrogen upgraded biogas? How is this potential related to and affected by other flexibility 
elements?  
Considering the challenges of modelling energy storage, attention towards time aggregation and model 
sensitivity of different time aggregations will also be considered, although not formally as a specific research 
question.  
The evaluation of hydrogen storage as opposed to other potential storage technologies will not be given much 
attention. Rather, the objective to find out, if electricity storage, represented by H2 technologies, will have a 
potential in the future energy system with increasing need of flexibility. 
1.4 
Methodology and thesis structure 
The research question is investigated by linear optimization modelling performed with the ESM Balmorel. 
The investigation is structured around a baseline scenario with four alternatives. The main simulations are 
performed as a long-term investment optimization from 2020 to 2050 in steps of 10 years and with limited 
foresight of 10 years. The time resolution is 225 steps pr. year (15 hours x 15 weeks). Before the optimization 
is performed, a few preliminary steps are necessary: 
First, a short introduction to the Danish energy system will provide essential information on current Danish 
energy characteristics, historic development, energy policies and resource potentials (Chapter 2). This chapter 
serves to frame the thesis in a Danish context and to briefly show how electricity storage has historically been 
part of energy planning. 
Next, a characterization and description of energy system flexibility serves to understand the concept of energy 
storage in relation to the integration of VRE and other flexibility elements (Chapter 3). This is followed by a 
literature review on previous attempts to uncover the potential of hydrogen storage and a description of 
different types H2 technologies, including the their data and some LCOE calculations validation (Chapter 4). 
Then an introduction of the Balmorel model broadly describes how the model works, which alternative models 
exist and why exactly Balmorel was chosen for this thesis (Chapter 5). This is followed by a description of 
how hydrogen technologies are modeled and the modifications that have been implemented and corrected, 
compared to the standard core model and add-ons (Chapter 6).  
Since time aggregation is important when modelling storage, the entire Chapter 7 is devoted to presenting 
key time aggregation issues, which leads to a specific aggregation method for later use in the simulation.  
The next two chapters describe, validate and calibrate the model input data (Chapter 8) and the idea and 
setup of the five main scenarios (Chapter 9). 
The optimization is performed in four steps:  
1) Simulation of main scenarios. 
2) Simulation of different time aggregations. 
3) Simulation of price sensitivities. 
4) Simulation of efficiency and performance sensitivities.

1.5 
Limitations 
The task of investigating certain aspect of H2 technologies involves a natural inclusion and exclusion of some 
energy system processes and technologies. In this thesis, the transport sector is only represented indirectly by 
a growing demand for electricity. Also, aviation, shipping, process heat from industry, and energy flows to 
and from refineries are excluded. Further, alternative storage options to H2 technologies are not included. As 
mentioned in Section 1.3, the objective is not to analyze the H2 storage options in opposition to other storage 
alternatives, but rather to illuminate the potential in electricity storage in relation to other flexibility types. 
Referring to the specific setup of Balmorel, it should be mentioned, that curtailment of VRE is not allowed 
and energy demand is assumed inelastic. Balmorel is used in the economic dispatch mode and does not include 
unit commitment and ramping. Electricity and heat demand must be met in all time steps, and balancing 
markets and ancillary services are not included.  
The thesis has a specific focus on Denmark. This mean that the neighboring countries are included to ensure 
realistic input and export dynamics, but the simulation results from these countries are not presented. Since 
the objective is to analyze electricity storage, there is a limited focus on heat and heat storage. These 
technologies are an integral part of the model, but not presented in the results.

2. 
The Danish Energy System 
The chapter will provide essential information on current Danish energy characteristics, historic development, 
energy policies and resource potentials. This chapter serves to place the thesis in an actual Danish context 
and to briefly show how electricity storage and hydrogen has previously been part of energy planning. 
The section distinguishes between total primary energy consumption and energy consumption for heat and 
electricity. The latter category is included because it roughly reflects the heat and electricity production 
included in Balmorel. It is constructed with the Eurostat (2019) energy balance by including the 
transformation input of the “Conventional Thermal Power Stations” and  “District Heating Plants ” 
categories, while also including primary production from wind and solar PV and net electricity imports. In 
this way, biomass consumption in households is not included which also not the case for the Balmorel 
representation.

by five major fuels, coal (26 %), biomass (22 %), wind3 (16 %) and gas (12 %) and waste (in total 13 %).

2.2 
Current Characteristics and Future Potentials 
2.2.1 
Combined heat and power 
The Danish energy system is characterized by a large integration of heat and electricity, facilitated by an 
extensive district heating network. In 2017, district heating accounted for 53 % of all heating measured by 
heated area, whereas the second largest heating source, a condensing gas boiler, accounted for only 18 % 
(Danmarks Statistik, 2017). The well established district heating network enhances the overall system 
efficiency and provides the necessary conditions for seasonal heat storage, which could be an important element 
in future energy systems with a large amount of VRE (Klimakommissionen, 2010).  
Basically all thermal plant that produces electricity, also produce heat (Eurostat, 2019). As described above, 
these plants mainly consume waste, coal, biomass and gas. A large part of electricity and heat decarbonization 
has been based on a substitution of coal and oil toward biomass (see section 2.3). Analysis shows that this 
such a pathway is problematic with regards to sustainability issues, CO2-neutrality and resource scarcity 
(Klimarådet, 2018). The total consumption of solid biomass in Denmark amounts to 117 PJ pr. year, of which 
43 % is imported (Eurostat, 2019).  
Reports shows that the total national resource potential of biomass in the future could be in the range 205-
245 PJ, excluding maritime biomass but including both straw and manure (DEA, 2014a). These estimates are 
uncertain and follows from assumption-based scenarios.  
2.2.2 
Wind power 
Since the oil crisis in the 1970’s, Denmark has pursued policies aiming at larger energy independence. Initial 
focus was on substitution of oil towards coal, natural gas and eventually nuclear power, combined with a large 
effort to reduce energy consumption and to support RD&D in renewable technologies (DEA, 2010; IRENA, 
2013). In the early 1980’s support of wind power gave rise to local wind turbine cooperatives, and later, in the 
1990’s, a fixed feed-in tarif model resulted in larger deployment of wind power (Meyer, 2003). In 2018, the 
total wind danish capcity reached a level of 6.1 GW, divided into 4.4 GW onshore and 1.7 GW offshore (DEA, 
2019a). Estimates shows that onshore wind could be further expanded up until 12 GW, while still being 
economically preferable compared to offshore wind, which suffers from larger investment and maintenance

2.2.3 
Biogas 
Biogas is produced by anaerobic digestion of biodegradable organic material and organic waste. If not treated 
further, the gas is consumed in small scale gas engines for heat and power generation or in a small gas boiler 
for heat production (DEA, 2018a). In 2016, Denmark produced 9 PJ of biogas (Eurostat, 2019). 
Current estimates of the biogas potential in Denmark ranges from 20 PJ (Birkmose et al., 2013) up to 50 PJ 
(DEA, 2018c) depending on what resources are assumed available for inclusion. Especially the assumption for 
straw has a significant impact.  
Instead of direct consumption, biogas can be upgraded to natural gas quality, making it possible to inject the 
upgraded gas into the existing natural gas grid network and consume it interchangeably with natural gas. 
Broadly speaking, there are two ways of upgrading biogas; Either by CO2 removal or by reacting it with 
hydrogen (hydrogenation). A benefit from hydrogenation is that it boosts the energy content of the biogas 
which could be important in a future with limited biogas resources. Further, hydrogenation could provide 
flexibility benefits by linking the gas consumption with VRE (DEA, 2018a). This described further in 
section 4.5. 
2.2.4 
Transmission lines 
Denmark is currently connected to the surrounding countries Norway, Sweden and Germany by transmission 
lines with a total capacity around 6 GW (DEA, 2018d). Further extensions of the transmission grid has 
already been decided, and will link Denmark and the Netherlands in 2020 by a 700 MW cable (Cobra) and 
Denmark and the UK by a 1400 MW cable (Viking-Link) in 2023 (Figure 2.2, left).  
In 2017, Denmark was a net importer of electricity with a net import of 5.0 TWh (18.1 PJ). Generally 
speaking, Denmark can be considered an “transit country” for electricity, that links the low-price Swedish and 
Norwegian electricity market with Germany and Central Europe (DEA, 2016a). In 2017, Denmark had a net 
import of electricity of 11.1 PJ from Norway and 10.9 PJ from Sweden, whereas the net export to Germany 
accounted for 4.0 PJ (Nordpool, 2019). 
Linking electricity markets provides flexibility, because aggregation of geographical areas can level out location 
specific variations in VRE. Generally speaking, linking electricity markets causes electricity prices in 
neighboring areas to level out, which, disregarding the transmission investment cost, raises the total system 
welfare. In Figure 2.2 (right), price duration curves (PDCs) for DK1 and the neighboring regions are showed 
for 2018. As expected, the PDC for Norway (NO2) has generally less high-price and low-price hours than the 
rest of, due to large hydro storage facilities. The integration of large amounts of intermittent renewable energy 
can be observed in the +6000 hours regime for DK1 and Germany (DE), where prices are lower and even 
negative (DK1 prices are negative in 0.6 % of all hours and 1.5 % for DE). Low prices occur because VRE

produces electricity at zero marginal cost and therefore enters the merit order curve in a way that lowers the 
market-clearing price. This is also known as the merit order effect. 
Future expansion of the transmission capacity beyond what has already been planned is technically possible, 
but necessitates that energy authorities, and ultimately national governments in the involved regions can 
agree on investment and maintenance conditions. The final decision on further expansion of the transmission 
network should weight the investment cost against the relative welfare gains, while also considering issue like 
security of supply and energy independence.

Renewable energy development since 1990 
Since 1990, the amount of renewable energy in Denmark has been gradually increasing, starting from a level 
of 6 % in 1990 (measured in proportion to primary consumption) and reaching 29 % in 2016. This is mainly

Another important development in the energy sector is rise in energy efficiency, both in the industrial and 
residential sector. However, improved efficiencies often comes along with increased consumption, which partly

in 2016 (Figure 2.3, right) . As mentioned in section 2.1, coal is still the single most used fuel for heat and

around 80 % of total consumption. According to the current Danish government, coal fired power plant will 
be banned from 2030 (EFKM, 2017).

2.4 
What will the future energy system look like? 
For more than a decade, energy planners and authorities has been demonstrating different decarbonization 
pathways for the Danish energy system, leading the way to an energy system solely based on renewable energy. 
These pathways should not be interpreted as a forecast of what the future energy system will look like. Rather, 
they serve to present the operational space for policy making guided by normative imperatives in times of 
global warming (DEA, 2014b; Smil, 2005). 
The following contains a short presentation of a few decarbonization pathways for Denmark. They are included 
to provide examples of possible pathways and to show that electricity storage is not always included. A brief 
review of the scenarios will examine them on their assumptions of demand response, transmission capacity

of primary energy consumption. All scenarios reach zero emissions in 2050 and are formulated based on 
multiple types of energy system modelling.

3. 
Flexibility in the Energy System 
This chapter describes different types of energy system flexibility and relates these to Danish characteristics 
and potentials.  
Flexibility in the power system can be defined as “… the extent to which a power system can adapt the patterns 
of electricity generation and consumption in order to maintain the balance between supply and demand in a 
cost-effective manner” (IEA, 2014). In addition to this definition, a temporal distinction between long-term 
and short flexibility is useful, because it underlines that flexibility both is an issue on very small time scales 
(a generator trip should be handled within seconds) and longer time scales (like dealing a high share of VRE 
in hours of either high or low production) (DEA, 2015). Another useful categorization of flexibility is to 
distinguish between demand side flexibility (targets the consumption of energy) and supply side flexibility 
(targets the production of energy). 
Broadly speaking, flexibility in power systems can delivered in a combination of four different flexibility 
elements: dispatchable generation, demand-side management, storage capacity and gird infrastructure (IEA, 
2014). These are briefly described in the following sections 3.1-3.4. 
However, flexibility does not just concern the electricity sector. Especially in Denmark, where an extensive 
district heating network allows for large scale heat storage, the heat sector is essential in providing flexibility. 
Likewise, the transport sector will become important in the future, when BEV can act as storage capacity or 
when production of synthetic fuels can provide flexible demand. This thesis only indirectly includes the 
transport sector (represented by a growing electricity demand), while the heat sector and the options for 
thermal storage is included, but not devoted particular interest.  
One could argue that curtailment of VRE should also be included as an option for providing flexibility. 
Research has shown that if we are less concerned with balancing non-curtailed supply with demand, and 
rather focus on covering demand all the time, it might be economically favorable to greatly oversize VRE-
generation capacity and curtail overproduction instead of investing in storage (Budischak et al., 2013). 
3.1 
Dispatchable generation 
Dispatchable generation technologies has historically been the dominant source of flexibility. However grouped 
in one category, dispatchable technologies vary significantly on their level of flexibility, with traditional coal 
and nuclear power plant having ramping times of several hours or more, while gas fired turbines are 
considerably faster (IEA, 2014). As mentioned in section 2.1, Denmark has currently a dispatchable generation 
capacity of approx. 8 GW. 
3.2 
Demand-side management 
Demand-side management includes a large variety of different processes, which can be divided into load 
shifting and load shedding approaches. Load shifting processes are characterized by a transfer in time of power 
demand, which could take place in many households processes, like electric heating of water or charging of 
electric vehicles. In contrast, load shedding reduces the overall consumption. This could be in high demand 
industrial processes with very high utilization rates (IEA, 2014). 
==PAGE:23==
22 
 
A recent study of the German electricity demand estimates, that the total flexible demand potential from 
both load shedding and load shifting is about 11 % of the total electricity demand, both now (2013 data) and 
in the future (Müller and Möst, 2018). This result is based on an hourly calculation, and would change if 
longer time periods were considered. When estimating the potential, not only power demand is important, 
but also the potential storage capacity and duration of the demand transfer.  
Another study conducted in Denmark reaches a slightly larger potential when estimating the so called 
“technical resource potential of non-disruptive residential demand response”. The potential is in the order of 
GW for power and GWh for storage, and is expected to increase with increased electrification. In 2030 the 
potential from only washing machines, clothes dryers, dishwashers and electric vehicles is estimated to be 
around 4 GW and 9 GWh. However, this power potential is defined as the “maximum coincident power”, 
which is the maximum aggregated potential load, assuming that the timing of the mentioned appliances is 
totally flexible over a period of one day (Mathieu et al., 2014). In order words, these appliances will not be 
able to quickly consume or deliver 4 GW.  As a point of reference, the current Danish peak load consumption 
is about 6.5 GW (as mentioned in section 2.1).  
In an energy system modelling exercise on the integration of the Nordic and German Electricity systems, 
flexible demand is modelled as virtual electrical storage without costs and losses, and with a capacity of 4 
hours of loading of 10 percent of peak demand (Hethey, et al., 2015). The overall price effect of the flexible 
demand on the PDC is found to be limited.   
3.3 
Grid infrastructure 
Both the transmission grid (high-voltage lines) and the distribution grid (lower voltages for local distribution) 
are important for flexibility. Due to the geographical representation in Balmorel, where the electricity demand 
equation should only be fulfilled within a region and not within an area (see section 5.3), this thesis only deals 
with the transmission grid.  
Generally speaking, transmission lines have a smoothening effect on the electricity system, because location 
specific VRE fluctuations can cancel out to certain level when aggregated over large geographical areas (IEA, 
2014). Further, in combination with storage facilities, wind resource potential in one region can be combined 
with cheap storage potential in other regions, which is the case for Denmark and Norway.  
As mentioned, Denmark is well connected through transmission lines to the surrounding countries (Norway, 
Sweden and Germany) with around 6 GW capacity. Further extensions of the transmission grid has already 
been decided, and will link Denmark and Netherlands in 2020 by a 700 MW cable (Cobra) and Denmark and 
the UK by a 1400 MW cable (Vinking-Link) in 2023 (DEA, 2018d). 
A recent analysis from (Energinet, 2018) shows that transmission lines add crucial flexibility to the Danish 
energy system. Following a scenario where the transmission capacity is lowered from todays 50 % of total 
generation capacity (including both VRE and dispatchable units), to only 10 % of total generation capacity, 
the annual system cost are raised by 170 mio. euros. This cost is mainly caused by the lack of electricity 
export possibilities in times of high electricity production from VRE 
==PAGE:24==
23 
 
3.4 
Storage 
Storage technologies can be characterized according to their physical storage principle, being either mechanical 
(pumped hydro, flywheels and compressed air energy storage), chemical (batteries and hydrogen storage), 
electrical (high power capacitors) or thermal (heat pit storage). Some technologies are able to operate at very 
long time scales (pumped hydro), while some will be discharged within seconds (like super capacitors). On a 
worldwide scale, pumped hydro accounts by far for the largest form of electricity storage (IEA, 2014). 
Theoretically speaking, storage technologies would be able to solve almost all flexibility issues that comes 
along with a high integration of VRE. In reality, storage facilities are often either very costly or restricted to 
specific geographical locations.  
Denmark produces practically no electricity from hydro power (DEA, 2018b) while a few seasonal heat storage 
pits are currently under test and demonstration (PlanEnergi, 2019). However, Denmark is well connected to 
Norway where large pumped hydro reserves are used to store excess electricity production from Denmark. 
3.4.1 
How to quantify storage? 
When talking about storage and when comparing different storage studies, it is important to use clearly 
defined concepts to avoid misunderstandings. Storage can be defined in many ways, and the following contains 
a suggestion for useful concepts. 
At least four absolute terms are used in the literature: 
1) Storage size or storage capacity: The size of the storage reservoir (in TWh) 
2) Storage consumption: Annual amount of energy which has been stored and then consumed (in TWh). 
3) Storage generation: Annual amount of electricity produced from stored energy (in TWh) 
4) Storage power: The generation capacity of the plant consuming or producing stored energy (in MW) 
All absolute terms can then be related to some other absolute term which allows for cross-comparison of 
studies. A few examples could be: 
5) Storage capacity pr. annual energy consumption. 
6) Storage consumption pr. annual energy consumption. 
7) Storage power pr. total power generation capacity  
3.4.2 
Storage needs 
A few attempts have been made to estimate the future electricity storage need or potential in Denmark, but 
results are strongly sensitive to assumptions regarding electricity markets, available technologies and their 
characteristics (dispatchable/variable), transmission capacities, modeling methods etc. In addition, it should 
be noted that there might not exits any actual “need” for storage. As argued in section 1.3, what is needed is 
flexibility. As an example, the current Danish transmission capacity to neighboring countries approximately 
equals the peak load consumption and the maximum capacity of the VRE (see section 2.2.4). From a technical 
point of view, this means that balancing of supply and demand could approximately be ensured by import 
and export of electricity. However, it could be economically favorable to store electricity or electricity storage 
could serve other purposes such as ensuring a certain degree of self-sufficiency and independence. 
==PAGE:25==
24 
 
An investigation of the Danish economical potential is provided by Krog Ekman and Jensen (2010) who finds 
that revenues from arbitrage on the current spot market does not provide a positive business case. In contrast, 
underground pumped hydro system and compressed air energy storage (CAES) might soon be able to operate 
at the balancing market, but further research is needed to validate this result. The article concludes, that in 
a scenario where biomass resources are prioritized for the transport sector, “… large scale electricity storage, 
both for balancing and for energy managements, is inevitable”. However, the study does not provide any 
quantitative estimation of this.   
The EU storRE project has found a storage capacity need of 500 GWh for West Denmark (Sorknæs et al., 
2013). This result is purely based on technical and not economical considerations, at it assumes an energy 
system with 80 % wind energy which includes the district heat sector with heat storage. Further, it assumes 
a 1500 MW transmission line to Germany, also having a 80 % wind based energy system which is strongly 
time correlated with Denmark. Since there are no transmission lines to Norway (and the UK) such a result 
serves rather as a theoretical upper limit, than as an educated guess of the real potential for storage.  
Analyzing the pan-European power system as a whole, another study finds a need for no-loss storage capacity 
of 10-15 % of annual demand, in a system that does not contain any form of dispatchable generation and only 
relies on sun and wind power (Rasmussen et al., 2012). This figure is reduced drastically if dispatchable 
generation or oversizing of VRE capacity (with an option of curtailment) is allowed. The figure increases if 
storage technologies like hydrogen, are not 100 % efficient. The study underlines that these figures should 
only be used as a theoretical upper benchmark,  since they rest on severe simplifications. Among these is the 
lack of integration with the heat and transport sector, the lack of demand response technologies and the 
assumption of a bottle-neck free transmission system, equivalent to unlimited and free electricity transmission 
across Europe.  
By reviewing more than 60 studies Blanco and Faaij (2018) finds that electricity storage capacity is usually 
less than 1.5 % of annual demand for energy systems (mostly only electricity) with up to 95 % VRE 
penetration. For 100 % renewable energy systems (but not necessarily 100 % VRE) the storage capacity is 
often below 6 % of annual demand. Some of these system also include transport, while the majority only 
considers heat and electricity. Even though the review seeks to systemize different types of studies, the overall 
result is difficult to interpret, knowing that the studies differ on sector inclusion, wind and solar share, the 
geographical scope and thereby also the transmission infrastructure. A take away point could be that there 
exists a potential for storage, that this potential generally increases with the amount of VRE and that the 
potential is highly sensitive to the set-up, methods and scope of investigation.

4.1 
Short literature review on hydrogen and flexibility 
In the last decade multiple research articles on the potential for hydrogen energy technologies have  been 
published. This activity demonstrates the ongoing and current academic interest in the possible role of 
hydrogen. The studies includes both H2G, H2P, H2F and FCEV. 
In one of the early studies Karlsson and Meibom (2008) investigate a possible long-term investment path for 
the Nordic (Denmark, Norway, Sweden and Finland) energy system, ending with a 95 % RE in 2050. The 
Balmorel optimization study, includes both P2G for electricity generation and direct consumption in FCEV, 
the latter being exogenously defined. The model finds it economically unattractive to utilize hydrogen P2G, 
but choses to produces most hydrogen for transport by electrolysis instead of steam reforming. In 2035, the 
hydrogen storage capacity in Denmark West amount to almost 180 MWh.  
In a later, but similar Balmorel study Meibom and Karlsson (2010) investigates the possible role of hydrogen 
in 2060 in Scandinavia and Germany. In a scenario where hydrogen can only be consumed for electricity 
generation (no hydrogen demand from transport) it is optimal to invest in approx. 13 GW hydrogen consuming 
combined circle (CC) turbines, and a hydrogen storage capacity corresponding to 1.2 % of annual wind power 
production. Nearly all hydrogen investments takes places in Germany.   
Another study applies the energy system model TIMES, where hydrogen penetrates the market already before 
2040 and accounts for 5 % of the total energy consumption in 2050, primarily used in the industrial and 
transport sector (Sgobbi et al., 2016).  For H2P, less than 1 % of electricity generation is based on hydrogen 
in 2050, which is only marginally increased even with a 25 % cost reduction of hydrogen fuel cells. These 
results apply to a simulation of all EU28 member countries, under a long-term decarbonization scenario.  
Other studies based on less complex optimization models draw similar conclusions. Eichman et al. (2016) finds 
that hydrogen systems already now could enter the California wholesale electricity market, aiming at 
production and selling of pure hydrogen. This finding is verified by Wang et al. (2018), who concludes that 
electrolyzers in the California 2025 energy system can contribute with considerable benefits in terms of 
flexibility and decarbonization of the transport sector. Likewise, a Danish case study finds an attractive 
business case in combining offshore wind farms with electrolytic hydrogen production for direct sale to end-
users, and note that H2P is less attractive (Hou et al., 2017). 
==PAGE:27==
26 
 
Latest, attention has been draw towards modelling the integration of hydrogen into the national gas grid. 
This is studied in Clegg and Mancarella (2016), which shows that the introduction of power-to-gas technologies 
in the British energy system can lead to 4 % price reduction of natural gas. Also, Ida Grædsted Jensen et al., 
(2019) shows that under heavy biomass restrictions hydrogenated biogas could become relevant. This study 
also shows that pure hydrogen in fuel cells is more attractive than both hydrogenated biogas and thermal gas, 
but generally less attractive than upgraded biogas (without hydrogen).   
4.2 
Electrolyzers 
Hydrogen can be produced electrochemically in an electrolyzer cell, where electricity is used to split water 
into hydrogen and oxygen6. Currently, at least three types of electrolyzers could potentially be used for 
hydrogen production, each of them having individual advantages and disadvantages. 
The Alkaline Electrolyzer Cell (AEC) is a proven technology (TDP 4), which have been used for hydrogen 
production for more than 100 years and is currently the most used commercial electrolyzer. It benefits from 
its relatively high durability (long lifetime). Unfortunately, AECs are rather inefficient (approx. 65 % 
electricity-to-H2 efficiency) without any major improvements within sight. Moreover, under the current 
performance properties, ACEs are not optimal for providing flexibility in systems with a high amount of 
fluctuating energy because of slow ramping7 time (minutes) and start-up time (from minutes to hours). This 
could potentially be changed by a reasonable market demand for such types of electrolyzers. In the future, 
the execs heat from AECs could be used for district heating (DEA, 2018a) 
The Proton Exchange Membrane Electrolyzer Cell (PEMEC) is operated at low temperatures (below 100 ˚C) 
and shares efficiency levels with the AEC. However, it is better suited for accommodation of VRE, because of 
its fast ramping rates (around one minute from minimum to maximum power). For small capacities (< 200 
kW) the PEMEC is in TDP 3, while larger capacities (>200 kW) are only in TDP 2. As the AEC, the execs 
heat from the PEMEC could be used for district heating (DEA, 2018a) 
The Solid Oxide Electrolyzer Cell (SOEC) is not yet a proven technology at large scale (TDP phase 1), but 
possible performance and material characteristics suggest that the SOEC has a large potential. The cell 
materials are cheap and the expectations for the efficiency is high (above 90 % for the eletricity-to-H2

seconds. The cell is operated at high temperatures (700 - 800˚C), but does only requires a heat net-input 
corresponding to heat loss in the system when operated at thermoneutral temperature. This means that 
SOECs both consume and produce heat, the former accounting for the largest part (DEA, 2018a). 
The SOEC also has a potential for energy flexibility purposes because of its reversibility, which means that it 
can both be utilized as an electrolyzer (electricity-to-H2) and as fuel cell (H2-to-electricity). The would allow 
for a significant higher capacity factor. Research shows advancements has been made for reversible SOEC/FC,

4.3 
Hydrogen storage 
For energy storage purposes, hydrogen must be stored after production. Generally speaking, hydrogen can 
stored in five different ways: 1) as pressurized gas, 2) as liquid hydrogen, 3) via absorption in metal hydrides, 
4) in chemical compounds and 5) via adsorption at special surfaces (DEA, 2018e). In this thesis it is assumed 
that hydrogen can be stored as pressured gas in three different ways: underground storage, a steel tank and 
decentralized steel tank (Sgobbi et al., 2016). 
4.4 
Fuel cells 
A fuel cell is the opposite of an electrolyzer: hydrogen reacts electrochemically with oxygen and releases energy 
(heat and electricity) and form water. In this thesis, two different types of fuel cells are included: 
The Solid Oxide Fuel Cell (SOFC) which, like the SOEC, operates at very high temperatures (600 - 700 ˚C), 
which allows for cogeneration of heat and electricity. This ensures a relatively high efficiency (around 60 % 
H2-to-electricity and a Cb-value around 1.6). The SOEF can be operated with various fuels as input, here 
among hydrogen. The SOFC is not yet a mature technology and is classified between TPD  1 and 2. No real-
life examples of large scale (>250 kW) SOFC plants exists (DEA, 2016c) 
The Proton Exchange Membrane Cell (PEMFC) operates a low temperatures (< 100 ˚C) and is expected to 
have a lower H2-to-electricity efficiency than SOEC, but produces more heat (a Cb-value around 1.25). It 
benefits from its short ramping time (from cold start to full capacity within seconds), but the technology is 
still relatively immature, and classified in TDP  2. Only a few large (1 MW) units are in operation today 
(DEA, 2016c). 
4.5 
Biogas and upgraded biogas 
Biogas is produced by anaerobic digestion of biodegradable organic material, primarily pig and cattle slurry, 
which accounts for approximately 80 % of the input material, but also industrial organic waste is used. After 
digestion, the gas contains 60-70 % methane and 30-40 % CO2, and the digestrate can normally be used as 
fertilizer. The biogas can be used directly is small scale gas engines for heat and power generation or in a 
small gas boiler for heat production (DEA, 2018a, 2014a). 
Biogas can be upgrade to natural gas quality (primarily methane) by further clearing of the gas and removal 
of CO2. The gas, which is this thesis is termed upgraded biogas, can be injected directly into the natural gas 
grid and is interchangeable with natural gas in CHP plants.  
Instead of removing the CO2, biogas can also be upgraded to natural gas quality by hydrogenation, a process 
which reacts CO2 with H2 to form methane. As mentioned, hydrogenation can provide important energy 
system flexibilities by linking the natural gas grid with fluctuating renewable energy production. Further, the 
hydrogenation process doubles the energy content in the original gas, which could be important in a future 
with limited biogas (and biomass) resources. In this thesis, this gas will be termed hydrogenated biogas. 
==PAGE:29==
28 
 
An alternative to biogas is thermal gas, which is produced by thermal gasification of solid biomass such as 
wood chips, wood pellets, straw and other solid organic residues from industrial processes and agriculture. 
The biomass is heated in a low-oxygen environment whereby a syngas, consisting of H2, CO, CO2, methane 
and water is produced. After a cleaning process, the gas can now, just like the biogas, be used in gas boilers 
and gas engines or upgrade to natural gas quality (DEA, 2018a). The thesis does not cover the option for 
producing and consuming thermal gas. Firstly, because a newly published report concludes that biogas in 2050 
will be economically superior to thermal gas (EAE, 2017). Secondly, although large uncertainties applies to 
this result, considering the research question, there are no important benefit of adding a technology that 
exhibits the same functionalities as an already exiting technology.

5. 
The Balmorel Energy System Model 
This chapter broadly describes the discipline of energy system modelling and characterizes Balmorel among 
other ESMs. The chapter also briefly describes why Balmorel was chosen for this thesis and it presents the 
model framework and a few core Balmorel concepts.   
5.1 
Energy system models 
Determining the optimal low-cost investment path for generation capacities that meets forecasted demands 
has been of interest for decades. The so called “capacity expansion models” were some of the first applications 
of linear programming and dates back to the 1950’s (Kagiannas et al., 2004). Starting with the oil crises in 
1970, which was followed by increasing interest in climate change, environmental issues and sustainable 
development, energy system modeling gained renewed interest in integrating these issues (Wei et al., 2006). 
Today, ESMs are an integral part of energy planning, as is used by policy makers, energy companies and 
research institutions. Important issues under investigation includes the deployment of VRE, the ambition of 
reducing CO2 emissions and the complex sector coupling of heat, energy and transport (Wiese et al., 2018a).  
A large variety of computer models are available for analyzing RE integration. In Connolly et al. (2010) not 
less than 37 models are revived, here among Balmorel, and classified according the model type. Some important 
distinctions between the models are: (1) Bottom up vs. top down models: Bottom up models are generally 
technology rich and seeks to identify and select specific technologies. In contrast, top down models are 
macroeconomic tool that links macroeconomic data with energy data and energy demand. (2) Scope of 
optimization: Model can either optimize the operation of the investment of different technologies (or both). 
(3) Geographical area: Models ranges from being local and community based to having a state, regional, 
international or even global focus. (4). Sector inclusion: Models vary on the inclusion of  the electricity sector, 
the heat sector and the transport sector. (5) Timeframe and time resolution: The scenario timeframe ranges 
from years to decades and the time resolution spans from seconds to years. As is the case for Balmorel, model 
are not permanently fixed in one categorization, but can be continuously renewed and expanded. 
5.2 
Balmorel since 2001 and motivation for choosing Balmorel 
The ESM Balmorel (BALtic Model Of Regional Energy Liberalization) is an important part of this field of 
energy system modelling. The model was originally developed for analysis of the power and heat sector in the 
Baltic Sea (Ravn et al., 2001). Later is has gradually been developed and is now widely used in both research 
institutions, industry and for governmental energy planning. In 2016, Balmorel was used in the Nordic Energy 
Technology Perspective 2016, which is an extensive modelling exercise, offering a detailed analysis of how the 
Nordic countries can achieve a near carbon-neutral energy system (OECD/IEA, 2016). 
Using the terminology from section 5.1, Balmorel is a bottom up model, seeking a partial equilibrium between 
supply and demand in the energy sector. Balmorel can both optimize operation and investments of 
technologies, and is currently set up with the ability to cover almost all countries in northern Europe and the 
Baltics. Inclusion of more countries generally only requires time series of consumption and VRE generation, 
and information on installed generation and transmission capacity. Balmorel covers both the electricity sector 
and heat sector, and allows for a detailed geographical representation of the latter. The transport sector is 
==PAGE:34==
33 
 
not directly included, but can be modelled with an exogenously defined energy demand. Time handling in 
Balmorel is very flexible and optimization horizons spans from weeks to decades, with a user-defined level of 
foresight (see section 5.3.3). Maximal time resolution is hours, and time selection and aggregation is flexible 
and offer a variety of different combinations (Wiese et al., 2018a). 
The options for detailed time selection and aggregation is the principal motivation for using Balmorel in this 
thesis. Modelling of flexibility options like long-term and short-term storage in combination with weather 
dependent VRE technologies, requires a high temporal resolution (Wiese et al., 2018b). Further, with a specific 
interest in the potential for H2 technologies in Denmark, a detailed description of the Danish energy system 
is necessary, including a good representation of the district heating network. Both fuel cells, H2P and H2G 
either consumes or produces heat, and in a cost competitive energy market where marginal cost differences 
could have a high impact on the optimal solution, the integration of hydrogen with the heat sector is 
important.  
5.3 
Further description of Balmorel 
For an exhaustive description of Balmorel, the reader is referred to either Ravn (2016) or Wiese et al. (2018a). 
Here only the most crucial parts for the thesis will be described.  
Balmorel is deterministic linear optimization model written in GAMS. The source code has been provided 
online since 2001. Balmorel exist in four different base models, balbase1, balbase2, balbase3 and balbase4, 
with different temporal characteristics. In this thesis, balbase 4 is used. The model assumes perfect competition 
and seeks to maximize welfare. When applied without price elasticities, this is equivalent to minimizing costs.

Equation 5 connects fuel consumption and electricity generation, while equation 6 connects fuel consumption 
and heat production. Equation 7-10 ensures that generation, transmission and storage does not exceed the 
capacity, while equation 11-12 ensures that the capacity equals the exogenous defined level plus investments. 
Equation 13 represents the electricity storage balance.  
A special parameter CHRONOHOUR act as a multiplier to the net storage load. This parameter will be 
described and discussed later. Basically, it seeks to represent the fact, that one time step often represents 
more than just one hour. It important to notice that only the storage capacity is changed by this formulation 
and not the hourly net inflow to the reservoir. Hydrogen storage will be modelled in this way.   
As nice feature with linear optimization is that the marginal values from the electricity balance equation 
corresponds to the electricity price: The marginal values can be interpreted as the price of producing one 
additional energy unit, which is equivalent to the marginal cost of the last dispatched generation technology 
at the merit order curve. At market clearance (when the demand curve intersects the supply curve or the 
merit order curve) this marginal cost is the electricity price.  
5.3.2 
Spatial resolution 
Balmorel operates with three different geographical levels which are subordinated and decreasing in size: 
Countries (C), Regions (R) and Areas (A). Some equations must be satisfied at area level (like the capacity 
constraint (7) and the heat balance (3)) while other must be satisfied at region level (like the electricity 
balance (2)). Electricity can be transmitted between regions via transmission lines, but within regions and 
==PAGE:36==
35 
 
across area borders, electricity is assumed to flow freely. Some countries are quite detailed like Denmark (2 
regions, 10 areas) while others countries has a lower spatial resolution, like the UK (1 region, 2 areas)  
5.3.3 
Temporal resolution and foresight 
Time is also handled in three subordinated levels: Years , Seasons and Time steps. Seasons subdivide the year 
into a user-defined amount of periods, while time steps further subdivide each season into a user-defined 
amount of time segment. In this thesis, seasons are chosen to represent weeks, while time steps are chosen to 
represent hours. However, other types of subdivisions are also possible.  
Often, not all years, seasons and time steps are included in a simulation, and some sort of time aggregation is 
used. In the Balmorel code, we distinguish between YYY, SSS and TTT which represent the full data 
structure, while Y, S and T represent the years, seasons and time steps selected for the simulation.  
Balmorel BB4 has the feature of allowing the user to manually choose the level of foresight used for investment 
decisions. Originally, this feature is implanted under the terminology of “rolling horizons” (Ravn, 2016).  The 
basic idea is that investments decisions are made with a defined level of foresight, which allows the model to 
include future changes in the forthcoming years (emissions policies, available technology, energy consumption, 
fuel prices etc.). into the investment decision of the actual year. Investment decisions with zero or limited 
foresight can be termed “myopic”, in contrast to a full foresight model, which optimizes investment by taking 
all the simulated years into account at once.  
The degree of foresight has a large influence on the computational demand, and is shown also to affect the 
final results of the model. Fuso Nerini et al. (2017) shows that a 10-years limited foresight for the UK energy 
system, in the period 2015-2050, during which emissions are gradually forced to decrease, can result in a 500 
billon £ extra cumulative cost, compared to full foresight (no reference cost level provided). In Heuberger et 
al. (2018), the extra cost of not having full foresight is estimated to increase the cumulative system cost with 
14 %. And (Bramstoft and Ravn, 2019) shows, that for a North European heat and electricity system, a 10 
years limited forecast can reduce the total system cost with 18 %, compared to the zero foresight.  
Real life decisions are seldom taken with full information. In this thesis, a limited foresight of 10 years is used 
to reflect uncertainty of future price developments, technological improvements etc.   
5.3.4 
Add-ons  
Balmorel is built in a modular structure, with the before mentioned base model acting as a core to which 
different add-ons can be attached, depending on the research interest. This thesis will apply the HYDROGEN 
add-on and the COMBTECH add-on, with modifications presented in section 6.2. The HYDROGEN add-on 
allows for simulation of hydrogen technologies (electrolyzers, fuel cells and hydrogenated biogas), while the 
COMBTECH add-on allows for a combination of fuels to be used in one technology.

7. 
Time Aggregation 
This chapter describes the concept of time aggregation and its relation to modelling VRE and storage. Further, 
the aggregation method used in this thesis is developed and described. 
The variability of renewable energy sources implies a careful handling of time aggregation in energy system 
modelling (IRENA, 2017). Ideally, the most accurate results would be provided by full-resolution modelling, 
but this often requires much computational power. Therefore, modelers needs to aggregate time, that is, 
selecting a representative amount of time slices which capture important characteristics of the energy system, 
like periods with high electricity demand and low energy production from VRE and vice versa. This is 
particularly important, when investigating system flexibility and storage because the need for flexibility could 
depend on relatively few, but important time periods during the year.  
That necessity of finding the correct balance between time resolution and computational demand is highlighted 
in Pfenninger et al. (2014) who frames the task of “… resolving details in time and space” as one out of four 
main challenges for energy system modelling in the twenty-first century. An example of time aggregation 
being important when modeling VRE, is found in Haydt et al. (2011) where a low resolution model, compared 
to a high resolution model, causes a significant overestimation of the demand meet by VRE. Other examples 
are provided in (Buchholz et al., 2019).  
A well known measure for selecting representative time slices which takes both VRE generation and demand 
into account, is to validate the selection against the residual load12 duration curve (RLDC) (IRENA, 2017).  
Another factor that could be important is to respect the chronology of the original time series (an example of 
why this could be important is provided later). Here, chronology refers to the temporal order of the selected 
time slices or time steps.   
7.1 
Time aggregation applied in the thesis 
As mentioned in section 1.3 the object of this thesis is not to fully uncover the topic of time aggregation. 
However, since the quality of time aggregation can affect investment decisions and impact the estimated need 
for storage and flexibility, it considered non-negligible. 
The thesis aggregation (TA) method, seeks to include the residual load and to respect chronology. The method 
is developed with a experimental mindset and does not guarantee optimal results. The reason for moving 
beyond a standard heuristic selection approach13, is a (naive) belief in the benefits of quantitate and 
optimization based approaches. The hard truth is that a very simple approach might be just as good. The 
even harder truth is that we might never get to know which approach is best, if we do not a have a robust 
way of validating different types of aggregations. 
Aggregations can be pre-validated before the actual simulation starts (this is what is done in this thesis) and 
they can be post-validated, a process in which the model outcome is validated against a full-resolution model

7.1.1 
A small general example of storage and chronology 
The objective of this section is to provide a small example of why chronology is important when modelling 
storage: 
Suppose that a simple isolated energy system is powered by only one variable electricity source, which supplies 
a constant demand in combination with a storage facility. The energy source generates electricity as a binary 
step function (the source is either on or off) and the capacity is scaled such that the total annual electricity 
generation exactly matches the total annual electricity demand. In one extreme case, in which the energy 
source is periodically on and off in a pattern that changes every time step, the needed storage capacity would 
be equivalent to the consumption of only one time step. In another extreme case, in which the variable energy 
source is continuously active in the first half part of the year and inactive in the last part, the needed storage 
capacity would be equivalent to the total consumption of half a year.  
If these examples represents two full-resolution time series, it evident that any time aggregation would need 
to take into account the chronology if the resulting storage level should approximate the full-resolution level. 
It is not enough to ensure that the aggregation contains the correct distribution of RL-values, which is ensued 
by only considering proximity to the RLDC.

7.1.3 
Describing the method 
The aggregation method used for the main simulations rests on three observations or objectives:  
1) As mentioned above, an attempt to approximate the full-resolution RLDC can be used as a measure 
to take into consideration both the variability of supply from VRE and demand. Here, this is done 
by comparing the distribution of aggregated RL values to the non-aggregated distribution. Ideally, 
this would ensure that most possible combinations of high/low production from VRE and high/low 
demand is included. 
2) A good approximation of the RLDC should naturally lead to low Cend-value. However, to further 
stress this condition, a low Cend-value could be demanded as an independent criteria for a good 
aggregation.  
3) As described in the small examples in the sections 7.1.1 and 7.1.2, the right shape (few or many 
fluctuations) of the C-profile and a representative V-value is preferable (it will be described why V 
as metric for a good time aggregation is hard to use). Such an approach seeks to ensure, that what 
seems to be a correct distribution of RL (ensured by step 1) and step 2) does also have a representative 
chronology. 
The time domain of the model is set up so that a non-aggregated model is divided into 52 weeks, each with 
168 hours. The final aggregation is chosen to contain 225 time steps. Experience from different simulations  
shows that this amount of time steps is reasonable in terms of computation time.  
Now, an algorithm performs 10.000 random selections of 225 elements from RL. To ensure a broad 
representation of both intra- and inter seasonal dynamics, the algorithm selects exactly 15 (out of 52) seasons 
and 15 hours (out of 168) with 6 hours interval14.

probability density function. The same is done for the non-aggregated RL series. Each bin has a certain value

Geography 
The Danish energy system is closely linked to the neighboring countries, and investigations on energy system

8.1 
CO2 emission policy 
CO2 emissions are naturally reduced by the rising price on CO2 quota (see section 8.3). Additionally, the 
model is constrained to linearly reduce Danish CO2 emissions from 7.9 Mt CO2 in 2020 (this is the optimal 
level without any restriction) towards zero emission in 2040.  
The political ambition of the current Danish government is to reach net zero emissions in 2050. As the 
decarbonization of electricity and heat can be considered relatively easy compared to the challenges for 
agriculture, industry and heavy transport, is could be fair to aim for zero emissions from electricity and heat 
already in 2040. Such a constraint will apply for Denmark in this thesis. The rest of the countries are restricted 
to reach zero emissions in 2050, but have no limitation in the period before 2050.  The CO2 emission reduction 
also applies to the other scenarios.

not only depend on the fuel price, but also on the optimal emission level under CO2 restrictions. 
Some articles (Jensen et al., 2019) includes a negative CO2 emission from biogas, because anaerobic digestion 
of manure before spreading, reduces CH4 emissions to the atmosphere. Estimates show that this effect could 
contribute with approx.  8.7 kg CO2-equivalents pr. GJ biogas (DEA, 2016d). This negative contribution is 
not included in the model, since the uncertainties that applies to this contribution are fairly large, both in 
terms or the net CO2 effect and the regulatory implementation.  
As mentioned in section 2.3, Danish coal consumption for electricity and heat will be banned from 2030. This 
ban is also included in the model.   
As mentioned in section 2.1, 13 % of the primary fuel consumption for electricity and heat is waste. In the 
model, this amount (38 PJ in 2050) is fixed, because it is assumed that society will not stop producing waste, 
and that this waste needs treatment. The model assumes waste consumption CO2 neutral.

8.5 
Calibration 
Ideally, the model should be able to reproduce energy production patterns in the base year (2020) that roughly 
corresponds to actual energy statistics. However, if policy driven taxes on energy commodities or technology 
subsidies are not reflected in the model, or if real life behavior contradicts the assumption of rational behavior 
under full competition, models outcomes might differ from the real world.  
An example is the large real-life Danish consumption of biomass, which does not appear in an un-calibrated 
model. This could be caused by the fact that biomass for heat production, is subject to tax exemption unlike 
other non-CO2 neutral alternatives (Klimarådet, 2016). To correct for this, the model is constrained to have 
a minimum use of biomass (wood pellets, woodchips and straw) corresponding to the biomass share for 
electricity and heat consumption in 2016. Likewise, there is also a constraint on maximal coal consumption. 
Both restriction only apply in 2020, while coal is banned from 2030 and onwards

9. 
Scenario Formulation 
This chapter presents the idea and setup of the five main scenarios. These are constructed in line with the 
research question, which aims at estimating the role of hydrogen for electricity storage in the relation to other 
types of flexibility. As mentioned in section 1.3, the economic importance of each flexibility element 
(interconnection, flexible generation, storage and demand response) is determined in relation to the other 
elements.  
In order to evaluate the need for hydrogen storage and hydrogen technologies a base scenario is constructed 
as reference point. Additionally, one scenario dedicated to each of the remaining three flexibility elements is 
constructed, in order to evaluate the importance of this specific element in relation to hydrogen storage. 
Finally, the base scenario is tested against a scenario, where no hydrogen storage is available. All scenarios 
are summed up in Table 9.1 and described in detail in the following.

The base scenario serves as reference point for the other scenarios. Important characteristics of the scenario

9.2 
Low transmission scenario (LOW TRANS) 
Denmark is currently well connected to surrounding countries and, as described in section 2.2.4, future 
transmission investments have already been decided. With an increasing share of VRE generation, it is likely 
that further expansions of the grid infrastructure will be economically feasible. This scenarios is constructed 
such that new investments, beyond what has already been decided, are not allowed. In Balmorel, low 
transmission is modeled by adding an equation which constrains new transmission capacity (variable VXKN) 
to be equal or less than the exogenously defined level (parameter XKINI).  
9.3 
Low dispatchable generation scenario (LOW DIS) 
As mentioned in section 2.2, a large part of the energy system decarbonization has been achieved by a 
substitution of coal and oil towards biomass. This does not cause the same issues with flexibility as wind, 
since biomass is dispatchable. However, as biomass could become a scarce resource in the future, and because 
==PAGE:51==

biomass is not inherently sustainable, is it relevant to consider an energy system where biomass is not 
consumed for heat and electricity generation. In this scenario, consumption of biomass (straw, wood, wood 
waste and woodchips) is banned from 2030. In addition, to further reduce the dispatchable generation capacity, 
consumption of waste is restricted to 15 PJ in 2030 and to zero in 2040 and onwards. In Balmorel, this is done 
by setting the annual maximum fuel consumption (parameter GMAXF) to zero in 2030 and forward for straw, 
wood, wood waste and woodchips, and by setting the exact annual waste consumption (parameter GEQF) to 
15 PJ in 2030 and to zero in 2040 and 2050.  
9.4 
Demand response scenario (DR) 
As described in section 3.2, demand side management could contribute with considerable flexibility, especially 
on shorter time scales. In this scenario, demand side measures are implemented as a load-shifting management, 
modelled as a large electric battery with an intra-seasonal storage option only. This means that the battery 
cannot be used for seasonal energy storage. Two important model formulations, that determine the how 
demand response is modelled must be mentioned:   
First, given a CHRONOHOUR parameter of 6, the relationship between maximal DR storage capacity (in 
GWh) and maximal DR power (in GW) will be 6-to-1. Second, since ramping is not considered, the total 
storage volume can be discharged or charged within one time step. This mean, that a totally charged battery 
of 6 GWh will be totally discharged if it delivers 1 GW in a time step.  
For the implementation of DR, one must choose to let either power or storage capacity be the defining 
parameter. In this thesis, power will be the defining parameter.  
In section 3.2, a study suggests the DR-potential could be around 11 % of peak demand while a modelling 
exercise assumes 10 % of peak demand. To really challenge the flexibility provided by hydrogen technologies, 
this thesis would like to use a higher maximum power capacity, and thus assumes a gradually increasing 
power capacity of 1 GW in 2020, 2 GW in 2030, 3 GW in 2040 and 4 GW in 2050. In the BASE simulation, 
peak electricity demand is almost 6 GW in 2020 and 12 GW in 2050. This mean that demand response power 
capacity will constitute 17 % of peak demand in 2020 and 33 % of peak demand in 2050. Although a bit high 
compared to studies16, this could reflect increasing electrification and technological advancement.  
Since batteries in Balmorel are defined pr. storage volume, the total installed capacity must be 6 GWh, 12 
GWh, 18 GWh and 24 GWh in 2020, 2030, 2040 and 2050 respectively. This is defined by the GKFX 
parameter. To avoid endogenous investment, one easy option is to define very high (> 109 EUR/MWh) 
investment cost. The price of utilizing the storage capacity is set equivalent to the VOM cost of a large scale 
lithium-ion battery (DEA, 2018e). This is a low estimate, but still useful, because it will challenge the hydrogen 
storage option for short-term storage even more. This demand response technology is only applied to Denmark.

10. Main Scenario Results 
This chapter presents the most important results of the main simulation, where the baseline scenario is 
compared against the four alternative scenarios. Included in this section is a presentation of electricity 
generation and fuel consumption, installed capacities for both power, hydrogen technologies and transmission, 
hourly generation and storage profiles, electricity prices and total system cost.  
When describing electricity generation, the abbreviation FCH2 refers to electricity from fuel cells, while 
GTBGH2 refers to generation based on hydrogenated biogas (often in form of a gas turbine). BG refers to 
generation based on either upgraded biogas or raw biogas.  
When describing fuel consumption, H2 refers to the total level of consumed hydrogen, both including H2 for 
fuel cells and H2 for hydrogenation. Following this notation, BGH2 refers to the level of consumed biogas for 
hydrogenation, not including the energy content of H2.  
For exact generation and consumption levels, the reader is referred to appendix A, which contains data tables 
for 2040 and 2050.  
The computational demand when combining: storage options, investment decisions with foresight, a large 
COMTECH and HYDORGEN add-on, five different countries, and a relatively fine time resolution (225 time 
steps pr. year) is large. Therefore, the simulation has been performed at the High Performance Computing 
(HPC) clusters, provided by the DTU Computing Center. This allows for a computational time of 
approximately 3-7 hours for each of the standard scenarios.

technologies will provide flexibility, if another flexibility element is restricted. The restriction also increases

transmission lines in the BASE scenario is used to export electricity from wind.  
Reduced generation from dispatchable sources (waste and biomass) raises the generation from H2 to 1.5 TWh 
and from BGH2 to 3.0 TWh. The total raise (1.3 TWh) does not fully cover the generation from DIS in the

on waste (38.3 PJ), which has very high investment and variable costs17 compared to natural gas plants. When 
waste consumption - apart from the LOW DIS scenario - is fixed at 38.3 PJ (see section 8.3), the optimal 
generation profile is to scale waste capacity just large enough to consume the fixed consumption at a constant 
hourly level throughout the whole year. This effect can be observed in section 10.3, and suggests that even 
though waste consumption is dispatchable it should only be treated as “flexible”  in technical terms, but not 
according to economics.  
Demand response (DR) lowers generation from both FCH2 and GTBGH2, but does not eliminate it. This 
indicates, that hydrogen in the BASE scenario is both used to cover imbalances in supply and demand in the 
short-term (within weeks: DR is restricted to intra-seasonal storage) and in the long-term. The option for DR 
also reduces the net import of electricity. 
The absence of H2 technologies creates a demand for electricity generation from raw and upgraded biogas 
(2.5 TWh) and changes the role of Denmark from being a net importer of electricity to a net exporter. Both 
Germany and the UK, who are also subject to the no-hydrogen restriction, likewise exploit the benefit of 
hydrogen storage in the BASE scenario, where FCH2 and GTBGH2 accounts for 10 % (Germany) and 8 % 
(UK) of total electricity generation. When these options are not available, a raised electricity import from 
Denmark could be a least-cost alternative. The biogas generation in Denmark in this scenario is based on 52 
% pure biogas and 48 % upgraded biogas.

10.7 Summary of main scenario results 
The main results show that hydrogen technologies could optimally constitute 4.8 % of total electricity 
generation in 2050, corresponding to 8.7 % of total fuel consumption. In absolute numbers, this equals an 
electricity generation of 3.2 TWh (0.9 TWh from fuel cells and 2 TWh from gas turbines) and a fuel 
consumption of 24.6 PJ (9.3 PJ biogas for hydrogenation and 15.3 PJ hydrogen). These results apply only to 
2050 and hydrogen does only play a marginal role in the preceding years. A major reason for the entrance of 
hydrogen technologies in 2050, is the steep slope of the PDC (most likely caused by a large VRE deployment) 
in combination with decreasing investment cost of SOEC and SOFC. 
The consumption and production pattern change in the alternative scenarios. In the low transmission scenario, 
the total consumption of hydrogen (H2) and hydrogenated biogas (BGH2) grows by 29 %, which is 
accompanied by larger investments in electrolyzers, fuel cells and storage capacity. The optimal storage 
capacity is 0.4 GWh, but, as will become evident from the next chapter, this level should be interpreted with 
care.  
In the low dispatch scenario, the total consumption of H2 and BGH2 grows by 38 %, but H2 technologies 
does not fully substitute the generation from dispatchable waste and biomass. Instead, electricity import 
increases, but without investing in new transmission capacity.  
In the demand response scenario, the total consumption of H2 and BGH2 decreases by 32 %. It is surprising 
that the introduction of demand response technologies does not have large effect on the PDC.  
The absence of H2 technologies stimulates a demand of approximately 20 PJ biogas (52 % pure biogas and 
48 % upgraded biogas) in 2050 and further results in a large expansion of transmission lines. The transmission 
capacity is increased with 72 % compared to the base scenario and with 144 % compared to the low 
transmission scenario, corresponding to exogenous capacity. The total system cost in 2050 are 6 % higher

11. Sensitivity Analysis Results 
The following chapter contains results from the sensitivity analysis. As mentioned in section 1.4 one of the 
objectives of this thesis, is to investigate the importance of time aggregation when modelling storage.  
The first part is devoted purely to different types of time aggregations. Overall, the results show that the 
model is very sensitive to time aggregation, but it is difficult to understand the causal effect. The analysis of 
the results seeks to provide an explanation of the model behavior but does not reach a solid conclusion. 
In the second and third part, a more traditional sensitivity analysis is carried out, focusing on the cost of 
hydrogen technologies and biogas (price sensitivity) and of the efficiency and the possible reversibility of 
SOEC/SOEF (performance sensitivity). Price sensitivity and efficiency sensitivity analysis for H2 technologies 
is carried out according to the upper and lower bound of prices and efficiencies reported in DEA technology 
catalogues (DEA, 2018a, 2016c). These levels can be interpreted as a representation of the 90 % confidence 
interval and are considered the most valid choice of sensitivity levels.

decades. As an investment decision in 2050 only, the costs of fuel cells have decreased sufficiently to let FCH2

11.1.2 Hydrogen storage profiles and the C-profile 
One approach to seek an understanding of different time aggregations and their effect on the final result, is 
to examine the C-profiles (which can be determined before simulation) and compare them to the H2 storage 
profiles. As mentioned in Chapter 7, the C-profile reflects the needed storage capacity at a specific time step 
in an isolated 100 % VRE (sun and wind) system. Adding transmission lines and dispatchable generation to 
this system could cause both large and small changes in the C-profile – we do not really know. There are no 
arguments for why the C-curves and the H2 storage profiles need to be connected; but by intuition, they could 
be connected. If this is the case, we have found a way to explain model behavior of different types of 
aggregations. If it is not the case, we must look for other explanations. 
Figure 11.3 shows the storage profile of the BASE, AL 15X15 and RA 15X15 aggregation (left axis) and their 
C-profile (right axis). While all H2 storage profiles share low-volume behavior around hour 3000 and after, 
this is not the case for the C-profile. In the BASE aggregation, there could be a tendency of a relatively steady 
volume increase from hour 2000 to hour 6000 both for the H2 profile and the C-profile, but this is not the 
case for the other aggregations and the tendency can therefore not be generalized. 
 
Figure 11.3: H2 Storage profiles for 3 aggregation and their associated C-profile (abbreviated CL). C-curve values are displayed at the 
right axis and are dimensionless due to normalization. They have been displaced such that their minimum value is zero. 
It is hard to determine a direct relationship between lines of the same color. The lack of similarity between 
the C-profile and the storage profile suggests that the shape and the maximum and minimum of the C-profile 
does not have a great say in the final H2 storage profile.   
However, this method of visual comparison is problematic as it includes the risk of overseeing or over-
interpreting relationships. A thorough investigation should be carried out on a quantitative basis, and should 
include more aggregation types. The scope of this thesis does not allow for a further investigation, but many 
interesting hypothesis and aggregations methods are still to be tested.

11.4 Summary of sensitivity analysis results 
A short investigation into different types of time aggregation reveals that the model is sensitive to aggregation 
types. The chapter seeks to uncover the causal effects of different time aggregation on the model output, 
especially focusing on storage capacity and investments and operation of hydrogen technologies. More 
specifically, the chapter investigates the effects of the number of time steps, the interval between the time 
steps and the C-profile. Without a stronger methodology and more simulation samples, it is difficult to 
determine any kind of relationship. Especially due to the fact, that no proper post-validation procedure is 
available. 
The base scenario results are sensitive to prices on H2 technologies; the total H2 consumption is reduced with 
44 % at high prices and increased with 74 % at low prices. In contrast, the model results are fairly robust to 
prices on biogas and efficiencies of electrolyzers and fuel cells. The result should be interpreted in the light of 
the upper and lower bounds being relatively higher and lower for H2 prices than the other variables.  
Should the SOEC/SOFC technology become reversible under the given assumptions, hydrogen consumption 
would increase with 93 %. Further, such a technological advancement would push forward the breakthrough 
year of H2 technologies to 2040 instead of 2050.

12. Discussion 
The discussion is structured around four key themes. First, the model results will be compared to previous 
studies and ESM simulations of hydrogen technologies. This seeks to validate the final results and to frame 
the thesis within the existing literature. Next, the model setup will be discussed. This includes a reflection of 
the technological representation of the energy system, the scenario formulation and the choice of analyzing 
only Denmark. Third, the much-mentioned issue about time aggregation will be further discussed and possible 
new investigation paths will be suggested. Finally, the generalizability and implications of the results will be 
discussed.  
12.1 Comparison with other studies and resource potentials 
The result from the main simulation (consumption of 9.3 PJ biogas for hydrogenation and 15.3 PJ hydrogen 
in 2050) is not far away from some of the previous decarbonization pathways for Denmark. IDA 2050 finds a 
total H2 consumption for heat and power at approximately 20.6 PJ while the DEA Wind scenario finds 38.8 
PJ H2 for both biodiesel and SNG. The share between biodiesel and SNG is not stated but if we assume 50 
% and also consider SNG represent hydrogenated biogas, this pathway ends at 19.4 PJ H2 for heat and power. 
However, where this this thesis also points toward utilization of fuel cells, the IDA 2050 and DEA Wind 
scenario primarily use hydrogen for hydrogenation.   
The KK scenarios is without any production or consumption of hydrogen for heat and power, since it is 
considered too costly. This thesis suggests that it could be more costly not to include H2 technologies  - or 
other storage alternatives (see further discussion below). 
Previous studies such as Meibom and Karlsson (2010) finds that the optimal H2 storage capacity is 1.2 % of 
annual wind production (all countries in the study included). As mentioned, it is it difficult to determine the 
exact level, but it could well be between 0.7 % and 1.7 % (The BASE and the HR1H scenario storage capacity 
in relation to annual wind production) – similar to 1.2 %.  
Another more sceptic article authored by Sgobbi et al. (2016) reveals that less than 1 % of total electricity 
generation will be based on hydrogen, while this thesis finds 4.8 %. The reason for the very low share could 
be that the study assumes very low fuel cell efficiencies (46 %) and no option for cogeneration of heat. As 
already mentioned in section 6.1, the assumption that high heat flow (> 600 ˚C) from SOEC and SOFC is 
directly compatible with the district-heating network, is a simplification. Especially the heat input to SOEC, 
which could need additional energy input to deliver high heat, should maybe be implemented with a lower 
efficiency or a larger heat demand. This could challenge the feasibility of the H2 production. However, results 
from the sensitivity analysis shows the H2 consumption is relatively robust to changes in electrolyzer and fuel 
cell efficiency. Further, the fast ramping rates, does only apply is the cell is kept at operating temperature 
(> 600 ˚C) in periods of no production. Considering the capacity-factor of 43.5 %, this would either require 
a large heat loss during the 56.5 % of the year without production, or it would imply that ramping rates 
would have to be considered.   
Analyzing the need for storage in general, Blanco and Faaij (2018) finds the maximum needed storage capacity 
for systems with up to 95 % VRE to be below 1.5 % of annual demand. Using the higher estimate for needed 
storage capacity in this thesis (1 TWh), results in a storage need of exactly 1.5 % of annual demand. 
==PAGE:73==
72 
 
Notwithstanding skepticism concerning the 1 TWh level and the lack of post-validation, this thesis is within 
and reproduces the general finding.  
A final question is now, if the biogas consumption and the needed storage capacity for H2 storage is within 
the range of the Danish potential? The consumption of 9.3 PJ biogas is well within the capacity range of 20-
50 PJ, and almost equal to the current 2016 production of 9.0 PJ. Thus, according to the model we do not 
need to expand the current biogas facilities, but should just ensure that the gas is not consumed as raw biogas 
in gas engines and heat boilers, an instead used for hydrogenation and lead intro the gas grid. This would 
require investment in H2 upgrading facilities.  
The current underground gas storage capacity in caverns is 11 TWh and could technically be used for H2 or 
for hydrogenated biogas instead (Pedersen et al., 2014). Whatever option is the most feasible, the needed 
storage capacity would be well within the limit.   
12.2 Model set up and important assumptions 
The thesis has a specific focus on Denmark, while four other countries are also included to ensure realistic 
import and export dynamics. This means that the optimal solution might be a sub-optimal solution for 
Denmark, since the model optimizes the system as a whole. Such effects are important to illuminate if the 
model results will be used to support national energy planning and to formulate national energy policy. One 
way of looking into such issues, is to compare relative changes in national system costs with relative changes 
in the total system cost (the objective function).  
Another issue that could have large impact for the estimated potential in H2 technologies is the presence of 
alternative and competing technologies for energy storage. An interesting example is CAES, which can also 
be utilized for long-term electricity storage. It is evident from the sensitivity analysis, that there is potential 
for even more electricity storage and that this potential depends on the storage cost; in 2050 the consumption 
of H2 would increase with 74 % if H2 technology costs drop to the DEA lower bound. Likewise, the 
consumption of H2 would increase with 93 % if reversible SOEC/FC becomes available. (The assumption of 
SOEC/FC reversibility is, however, highly optimistic and does not reflect any research-based technology 
assessment). More research and different set ups could shed light on and evaluate H2 technologies as opposed 
to alternative options. This could also include more types of batteries to challenge the use of hydrogen for 
short-term storage. Batteries will never be able to compete pr. storage volume, but they could easily compete 
in efficiency and in fast power response to stabilize short-term system imbalances. 
If not just considering other electricity storage alternatives, the H2 technology potential could also be 
evaluated against more types of flexibility that done in this thesis. This could include an exogenous flexible 
demand for hydrogen (demand should be meet on a seasonal or yearly basis), representing a growing interest 
in production of synthetic fuels. Such a demand would contribute with absorbing low price electricity, and 
this could cause hydrogen production for heat and electricity to be less favorable. Generally, the inclusion and 
linkage of energy production and consumption patterns, both across geography and across sectors, is expected 
to reduce the need for storage (Blanco and Faaij, 2018). 
==PAGE:74==
73 
 
On the supply side, as mentioned in Chapter 3, the assumption of not allowing for VRE curtailment favors 
H2 technologies. Without curtailment the model is forced to consume VRE production at all time steps, even 
though this may be sub-optimal.   
The introduction of the mentioned technologies or functionalities could have been either integrated in the 
base scenario or they could have been analyzed in separate scenarios. Many interesting alternative scenarios 
exists and it could have been valuable to combine the already existing scenario (like LOW TRANS and LOW 
DIS) to simulate more extreme situations. However, the logic behind the current scenario formulation is to 
measure the effect one step at the time, and to only include technologies in the BASE scenario, which already 
exits (which is why demand response is not included in BASE). Formulations of more scenarios is always an 
option, but considerations regarding simplicity and communication are also important, along with 
acknowledging the scope of this thesis. 
As mentioned before, heat storage (both intra- and inter-seasonal) is part of the model, although not devoted 
much interest. In 2050, in the base scenario, the inter-seasonal heat storage (heat pit) capacity amount to 0.6 
TWh corresponding to approximately 2 % of total heat consumption (see Appendix A for more information). 
The intra-seasonal heat storage capacity amount to 5 GWh (heat tank). It is presumed, that both types of 
storage adds considerably flexibility to the system and reduces the potential for H2 technologies. The issue of 
the CHRONOHOUR parameter does also apply to heat storage.  
Finally, the validity of the optimization does not only depend on the inclusion or exclusion of certain 
technologies, specific input data and model set ups. The general optimization result should also be interpreted 
in the light of our treatment of stochasticity. For instance, real life stochastic variations in weather patterns 
are treated as deterministic, meaning that the model optimizes yearly production, consumption and storage 
level with full information of all time steps. This approach is rather unrealistic, and the optimal dimensioning 
of storage capacity and storage power in the real world, would probably include some buffer capacity to handle 
unforeseen events. It is possible to do stochastic energy system modelling, but this would require more 
computational power.  
In line with this argument, it is important to note, that the model is not an energy system forecast but a 
suggestion of how a least-cost energy system could be constituted under the given assumptions. Real life 
behavior or taxes and tariffs, as mentioned in section 8.5, might cause a deviation from this suggestion.  
12.3 Time aggregation 
Although not part of the research question, particular interest has been devoted to the issue of time 
aggregation.  
12.3.1 Storage capacity scaling 
One dimension of time aggregation is concerned with the storage capacity and the parameter 
CHRONOHOUR. The fact that storage capacity varies with almost 1000 % can partly be attributed to the 
size of the CHRONOHOUR parameter. The thesis is not able to determine an appropriate size of 
CHORNOHOUR, since such a determination would have to be based on knowing the “true” or full-resolution 
storage capacity. However, if the true storage capacity was known, one could start to speculate in how to 
==PAGE:75==
74 
 
determine CHRNOHOUR for a specific time aggregation that approximately would lead to results close to 
the “true” capacity.  
By experience form the model sensitivity analysis, three factors seems to have great effect on the final storage 
capacity. These are: 
1) The interval between the time steps within a season 
2) The total number of time steps  
3) The specific selection of time steps, that is, the seasonal representation, the residual demand etc. 
If we were to determine a relationship between the most appropriate scaling factor and the interval of time 
steps, the number of time steps and the selection of time steps, one could imagine treating the appropriate 
scaling factor as a function of these three (almost independent) variables. The selection of time steps could 
be quantified with inspiration from the methods suggested in Chapter 7. By an exhaustive construction of 
multiple aggregations, a quantifiable relationship would maybe appear. If this is the case, this relationship 
could be used as rule of thumb for determining the CHRONOHOUR in models with alternative setups.  
It might be the case that the most appropriate scaling factor is also highly dependent on the model set up. 
Further, the scaling factor could also be dependent on input time series of VRE production or electricity 
demand. This, of course, complicates things even further.  
If we do not succeed in determining the “true” scaling factor, what would then be the consequences? If the 
cost of storage is mainly attributed to the cost pr. power capacity (MW) and not the storage capacity (MWh), 
which is the case for H2 technologies as implemented in this thesis, we do not need to worry much. The 
investment in storage capacity might be over- or underestimated but since it does not have a large influence 
on the objective function, it is of less importance. An implication of this is that the best way of reporting the 
potential/need for storage would be to highlight the storage power or storage consumption, and not the storage 
capacity.    
However, if the cost of storage capacity is high, which is the case for electric batteries, the CHORONOHOUR 
parameter could have a large influence on the results. In these cases, full-resolution or near full-resolution 
optimizations would be recommended.   
12.3.2 Generation capacity and operation 
The other dimension of time aggregation is concerned with the effect of time aggregation on investments and 
operation of what in section 3.4.1 is called “storage power”. The electrolyzer capacity, as an example, increases 
with 162 % when using a high-resolution aggregation but decreases drastically when using a very low 
resolution.   
This thesis investigates this issue by creating a few alternative time aggregations for validation of the 
aggregation used in the main scenarios. It is evident from the ambiguous results, that a better and more 
systematic methodology for investing this issue is needed. Not least because the specific outcome of a certain 
aggregations could be attributed to stochastic variations in the time series.  
In the end, we do not really know if the methods described in Chapter 7 (proximity to RLDC, low Cend and 
similarity between full-resolution and aggregation C-profiles) is a better aggregation method than simple 
==PAGE:76==
75 
 
heuristic selection. This is a severe problem, but the scope of this thesis does not allow for a detailed and 
systematic investigation of this issue. Future research into this should be done with a smaller model that 
would be able to perform full resolution optimization, which would allow for a proper post-validation 
procedure. However, it would be important that the smaller model include critical characteristics such as long-
term and short-term energy storage, investment options and cogeneration of heat and power.  
Specifically for Balmorel modeling, it is worth to consider if handling of multiple fuel inputs could be done 
more efficient than with the current COMBTECH add-on. This add-on greatly raises both the number of 
technologies and constraint, which is computationally challenging when modelling high-resolution.   
As a final note, it is important to state that the results from this thesis, do not uniquely define the problem 
of time aggregation as an issue only connected to energy storage, but is also important for VRE. Variations 
of wind production are also observed across different aggregations, but the relative deviation is not as large 
as for hydrogen technologies. The fact that deviations of hydrogen production is quite large in relative terms 
but quite small in absolute terms, is another important observation. Since electricity storage is only a marginal 
technology (the relative share of total consumption is quite small) this leads to a question about whether 
energy storage is more sensitive to time aggregation per se, or if it is just sensitive because it is a marginal 
technology. This question could be investigated by further research.  
12.4 Generalizability & Implications 
As an open ending for this thesis, it is relevant to ask if results are generalizable to other ESMs and other 
countries and what the implications of the results might be. The question both concerns academia as well as 
other institutions interested in energy planning and policy.  
12.4.1 Academia 
Internally in academia, the results enhance the importance of considering the presence of H2 technologies in 
energy system modeling which pursue long-term optimal investment path with a high share of VRE. Although 
only analyzed for Denmark, there are reasons to believe that similar results would apply to other energy 
systems with a high amount of VRE and poor access to cheap pumped hydro storage. 
When reviewing previous literature, very little attention is drawn to the issue of time aggregation when 
modelling hydrogen and energy storage. Possibly because energy modelers implicitly know, that their models 
are sufficiently robust to alternative sets up. Another possibility is that researchers are predominantly focused 
on presenting a result rather than presenting a problem to which they do not have a solid answer. 
As already mentioned above, the thesis suggest that time aggregation methods should be handled with care, 
and that interpretation of storage capacities cannot be done without reference to the storage scaling parameter 
(in Balmorel CHRONOHOUR). Since the challenges of time aggregation are not caused by a unique time 
handling in Balmorel, the effects observed in this thesis are likely to also apply to other ESMs, which faces a 
need for time aggregation.  
12.4.2 Energy planning and policy 
Referring back to the introduction, an important task for energy planning in the 21st century is to support 
and ensure a continuous decarbonization of the energy systems using least-cost solutions. In this light, this 
==PAGE:77==
76 
 
thesis shows that it is important to consider the flexibility benefits by adding H2 technologies to the energy 
system. At least three elements should be considered for energy planning and policy: 
First, economic incentives and support: The current level of wind power in Denmark has a long history of 
governmental financed support schemes. Some would argue that this support has been a prerequisite for the 
high amount of installed wind power today. The argument is that subsidies like feed-in tariffs or premiums 
has helped maturing the wind power industry and provided valuable expertise in planning, construction and 
controlling energy systems with a high VRE share. Following this, energy planning and policy should consider 
if H2 technologies need financial support (beyond the current RD&D level) or other types of framework 
conditions to foster an eventual breakthrough.  
Second, investment risks: Results from this thesis shows, that electrolysis has a positive effect in absorbing 
low-price electricity. Stating with investing in or supporting just this type of technology seems less risky that 
also investing in or supporting fuel cells, since hydrogen from electrolysis can be used for multiple purposes 
(see Figure 1.1). This means that market penetration, is not dependent on only one type of down-stream 
consumption. Likewise, investing or supporting electricity generation based on hydrogenated biogas does also 
reduce risk compared to fuel cells. Since hydrogenated biogas is interchangeable with natural gas and upgraded 
biogas, the investment in CHP gas turbines is not dependent on only one type of fuel. Further, it can be 
utilized as transition-technology, both suitable of a full or a partly renewable energy system. In other words, 
CHP gas turbines can be utilized both now and in the future, and in an energy system relying on wind or on 
biomass (via hydrogenation or upgrade of thermal gas). 
And finally, market effects: due to the effect on the PDC, hydrogen technologies could restrain the 
“cannibalizing effect” (wind power tends to drive down spot market prices) of increasing wind power 
(Hvelplund et al., 2017). This could provide better market conditions for a continued expansion of VRE.   
12.5 A final remark 
Apart from providing insight intro low-cost solution, this thesis also serves to demonstrate, that a Danish 
carbon-neutral energy system, as depicted in Balmorel, is not technically infeasible and could potentially even 
be cheaper than the system we have today. Figure 12.1 shows an update of Figure 2.3 from Chapter 2, which 
illustrated the development in the electricity and heat sector since 1990. The new figure has been extended 
with results from the base scenario, and now provides both a hind cast of previous development and a

13. Conclusion 
This objective of this thesis is to estimate the future Danish potential for electricity storage with hydrogen, 
both including electricity generation from fuel cells and hydrogenated biogas. Further, the thesis seeks to 
analyze how this is potential is related to and affected by other flexibility elements. The analysis is performed 
as a long-term investment optimization where electricity and heat generation is balanced to meet demand at 
an hourly level. The model assumes a steadily growing electricity demand towards 2050 and is restricted to 
reach a zero CO2 emissions by 2040.  
Results show that hydrogen technologies could optimally constitute 4.8 % of total electricity generation in 
2050, corresponding to 8.7 % of total fuel consumption. In absolute numbers, this equals an electricity 
generation of 3.2 TWh (0.9 TWh from fuel cells and 2 TWh from gas turbines) and a fuel consumption of 
24.6 PJ (9.3 PJ biogas for hydrogenation and 15.3 PJ hydrogen). These results apply only to 2050 and 
hydrogen does only play a marginal role in the preceding years. A major reason for the entrance of hydrogen 
technologies in 2050 is the steep slope of the PDC, most likely caused by a large VRE deployment.   
The consumption and production pattern change in the alternative scenarios, each of which is constructed to 
illuminate the effect of other flexibility elements; transmission, dispatchable generation and demand response. 
In the low transmission scenario, the total consumption of hydrogen (H2) and hydrogenated biogas (BGH2) 
grows by 29 %. In the low dispatch scenario, the total consumption of H2 and BGH2 grows by 38 %. In the 
demand response scenario, the total consumption of H2 and BGH2 decreases by 32 %.  
The effect of adding hydrogen technologies to the energy system can be observed on the electricity price 
duration curve, which is leveled out compared with a system without hydrogen flexibility. According to the 
model, it is economically favorable to produce hydrogen in the low-price regime (below 30 EUR/MWh), and 
generate electricity based on hydrogen in the high price regime (above 40 EUR/MWh). 
The optimal storage capacity is difficult to determine due to issues of time aggregations. An educated guess 
would place the storage level between 0.4 TWh (the base scenario) and 1.0 TWh (the 1-hour high-resolution 
scenario). 
The time aggregation methods developed for the main scenarios seeks to include residual load validation while 
also respecting chronology. The size and complexity of the model do not allow for a proper post-validation 
procedure, and the quality of the aggregation method remains uncertain. A short investigation into different 
types of time aggregation reveals that the model is sensitive to aggregation types. However, a deeper 
understanding of the causal effects of time aggregation is out of scope for this thesis. The results suggest that 
modelers generally should pay careful attention to time aggregation methods when modelling storage. 
The base scenario results are sensitive to prices on H2 technologies; the total H2 consumption is reduced with 
44 % at high prices and increased with 74 % at low prices. In contrast, the model results are fairly robust to 
prices on biogas and efficiencies of electrolyzers and fuel cells. Should the SOEC/SOFC technology become 
reversible under the given assumptions, hydrogen consumption would increase with 93 %. Further, such a 
technological advancement would push forward the breakthrough year of H2 technologies to 2040 instead of 
2050.   
==PAGE:80==
79 
 
Summing up, there could be a potential for electricity storage with hydrogen when approaching 2040 and 
2050, but this conclusion should be revised if alternative cheap electricity storage solutions becomes available. 
A certain level of electricity storage in general, as one out of four flexibility elements, seems economically 
feasible for a Danish energy system with large amounts of wind power.
