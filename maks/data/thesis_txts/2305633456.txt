Abstract 
     
During the last years, the there has been an increase in the installations of the subsea production 
systems that use seabed pumps. The subsea long cables which are used to connect the seabed 
pumps with the coast can be many kilometers increasing the total capacitance of the cable. 
Switching of vacuum circuit breakers (VCB’s) can generate severe transient recovery voltages 
(TRV’s) which need to be evaluated in order to size the breakers,protect the equipment and avoid 
economic losses associated with power quality problems. The analysis of the TRV’s classified the 
disturbances that occurred to a category of power quality problem which helped the presentation 
of the results and identifying the cause that induced the TRV’s. 
 
Data for the subsea electric power system were collected and a radial network model was 
developed in RSCAD. Two main configurations were studied, the first in scenarios 1 to 5 and the 
second in scenario 6. Six scenarios were it total simulated assuming combinations of load/no load 
operation and ideal/no ideal connection between the subsea switchgear busbar and the load. 
Sensitivity analysis was performed in the cable length to examine the possible impact of the length 
on the TRV. The transient events studied the transients associated with breaker switching in six 
cases where in each case only one breaker was switching on or off. 
 
This thesis focused mainly on the TRV at the load endings and secondary on the current at the 
receiving endings of the subsea cable whereas the TRV on other nods of the distribution network 
were not studied. The VCB’s were energized in various points of the voltage wave to investigate 
the impact of the point of the wave energization to the TRV. Detailed tables and selected graphs 
presented all the disturbances observed.

1. Introduction    
  
 
1.1 
Subsea Production Systems 
 
Subsea production systems refer to wells lying on the seabed of shallow or deep water and their 
operation is to extract oil or gas out of them. A movable rig is used to drill the well and subsea 
pipelines transfer the extracted oil or gas to a processing facility [1]. As offshore field development 
increases, advanced technologies such as seabed pumps are becoming more and more common in 
the current energy outlook. The cumulative and annual installations of subsea trees (assembly of 
valves, spools, and fittings used for oil and gas extraction) until 2013 and prediction until 2018 is 
shown in Figure 1.1. 
 
 
Figure 1.1 Cumulative and annual installations of subsea tree installations by 2013 and prediction 
until 2018 [2] 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
4 
 
Seabed pumps can vary in size from hundreds kW up to a few MW and are often located tens of 
kilometers away from the platform or from the onshore power system. It is considered already a 
mature technology that increases oil recovery from subsea tie-backs. Seabed pumps reduce 
production cost by reducing backpressure on the reservoir that increases flow rates and total 
recoverable reserves [3]. Among their many advantages, it has been credited to them faster 
production, improved recovery and extension of field life, easier extraction from low energy 
reservoirs, reduced operating expenses, better flow assurance performance and faster and safer 
start-up of low energy wells. 
The concept of power supply to seabed pumps used herein, makes a use of a radial network, with 
a topside transformer, a submarine cable, a subsea power conversion, and a distribution system. 
The radial network, on which are based all the scenarios investigated is shown in Figure 1.2. 
 
 
Figure 1.2 Radial-type subsea power network 
 
 
1.2 
Importance of Transients 
 
The scope of this project is to investigate the transients that occur during the energization and de-
energization of the subsea switchgear. The analysis is focused mainly in Transient Recovery 
Voltage (TRV) measured at the terminals of the load. TRV is defined as “the voltage transient that 
occurs across the terminals of a pole of a switching device upon interruption of the current” [4]. In 
other words TRV is a switching transient associated with circuit breakers whose terminals are 
exposed to this voltage. 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
5 
 
Transient overvoltages (TOV) are one of the main problems that electronic equipment designers 
have to deal with. Transients are formed in a power network from the sudden release of 
previously stored energy and their characteristics depend on the combination of the system 
configurations and type of circuit breaker operations. The TOV’s formed can develop a significant 
magnitude and frequency capable of causing failures to the equipment. A proper evaluation of 
them should be done to size the circuit breakers and the protective equipment [5][6][7]. 
The characteristics of a voltage transient induced by switching of circuit breakers are the stored 
energy, the oscillatory ring wave and the DC offset. The stored energy is responsible for the 
magnitude of the TOV and the oscillatory ring wave is determined by the capacitance and the 
inductance of the cable and the transformer. DC offset is due to the X/R ratio of the cable and the 
transformer [8]. 
Switching transients are one of the causes of power quality problems which have gained attention 
after the widespread of power electronics. Before the introduction of microprocessors for 
industrial control, and automation in factories and offices conventional equipment was not 
seriously affected by minor variations of the power. For example in case of a fault the supply 
voltage dipped, the lights just dimmed and the motors produced a lower output. Nowadays an 
Adjustable Speed Driver (ASD) contributes to the harmonic burden that the power system can 
accommodate and switching devices affect the quality of the power delivered [9]. 
The reasons that for the last years there has been an increased emphasis on the quality of the 
power delivered can be summarized as follows. 
 Poor power quality can  have impact on the accuracy of the utility metering 
 Poor power quality can lead the protective equipment to malfunction 
 Poor power quality can cause equipment downtime or damage 
 Poor power quality can increase the cost because of the preceding effects 
 Poor power quality can induce problems associated with electromagnetic compatibility and 
noise [10] 
The majority of the equipment that is in use today is susceptible to damage or service interruption 
during poor power quality events [10]. Transients have been found to be responsible for 
deterioration and failure of the equipment insulation [7][8][11][12][13]. In specific, almost one 
third of all motor failures occur because of fast switching surges [14]. Moreover, the spikes caused 
by switching operation are not linearly distributed across the transformer winding causing failure 
of the transformer [15].

1.4 
Classification of the Disturbances 
 
Before presenting the results it would be prudent to classify the disturbances that can cause 
power quality problems. “A power quality problem is any occurrence manifested in voltage, 
current, or frequency deviation that results in failure or disoperation of customer equipment” [9]. 
Many different kinds of disturbances were spotted through the procession of the results and the 
classification of them will help their presentation and the perception of their cause. 
The common types of disturbances in a power system are 
i. 
transients 
ii. 
RMS variations 
iii. 
voltage fluctuations 
iv. 
voltage imbalance 
v. 
waveform distortion 
vi. 
power frequency variations 
 
A transient is the response of an electric network to a sudden and undesirable change in network 
conditions. A transient is the state that leads the power system from its previous steady state to 
the new. Transients are momentary and their duration is in the range of microseconds or 
milliseconds.  
 
Transients can be classified into two broad categories 
I. 
Impulsive 
II. 
Oscillatory 
 
“An impulsive transient is a sudden, non power frequency change in the steady state condition of 
the voltage and/or current waveform that is essentially in one direction, either positive or 
negative, with respect to those waveforms”[17]. Impulsive transients can be classified further in 
subcategories according to their rise time. Hence, there are nanosecond, microsecond, and 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
11 
 
millisecond impulsive transients. Their duration is typically less than 50 nsec for the nanosecond 
impulsive transients, less than 1 msec for the microsecond and bigger than 1 msec for the 
millisecond category. Lightning strikes are usually responsible for impulsive transients because of a 
direct strike to a power line, or magnetic induction or capacitive coupling from strikes on adjacent 
lines. Impulsive transients are also called surges or spikes. 
 
“An oscillatory transient is a sudden, non-power frequency change in the steady state condition of 
the voltage and/or current waveforms that swings (oscillates) both positively and negatively with 
respect to the normal voltage waveform.” Oscillatory transients are described by their magnitude, 
ring frequency, point-on-wave and multiple zero crossings [18] and they can be subdivided with 
respect to their frequency. 
 
According to [16] an oscillatory transient with primary frequency component less than 5 kHz and 
duration between 0.3 to 50 msec is classified as low frequency. Low frequency transients are 
usually encountered on sub- transmission and distribution systems and they can occur due to 
resonances during switching [10]. The most typical example is capacitor bank energization that 
results in a low frequency oscillatory transient with typical spectral content in the range of 300 to 
900Hz and a peak magnitude of 1.3 to 1.5 times the crest voltage of the steady state of the 50 Hz 
waveform [9]. Ferroresonce and transformer energization oscillatory transients also appertain to 
the low frequency category [17]. 
 
The next subcategory is the medium frequency and oscillatory transients with frequency varying in 
the range of 5 to 500 kHz fall in this category. Typical duration of the oscillatory transients in this 
category is 20 μsec and they are the most dangerous with a magnitude that can reach even 8 pu of 
the crest voltage of the 50 Hz waveform. Medium frequency oscillatory transients can be triggered 
by back-to-back capacitor energization and cable switching [9]. Back-to-back capacitor switching is 
the energization of a capacitor bank when other adjacent capacitor banks are already energized. 
 
The final subcategory is the high frequency oscillatory transients with predominant frequency 
component larger than 500 kHz and typical duration 5 μsec. Hence, these transients require 
according to Nyquist theorem a time step of 1 μsec but RSCAD cannot operate in real time with 
simulation step smaller than 10 μsec. Examples of this category are switching transients, 
commutation transients in power electronic devices and excitation of the power system frequency 
by an impulsive transient [9][17]. 
 
RMS variations refer to a decrease or an increase in the root-mean-square voltage. These 
variations can be classified into three categories in respect to their duration. Instantaneous are the 
variations which last less than 30 cycles, momentary those which do not exceed 3 sec, temporary 
those up to 1 min and long duration the variations longer than 1 min [16]. 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
12 
 
The event at which the rms voltage decreases at the power frequency of the network is called 
voltage sag or dip. Voltage sag can occur if a large load experiences a line-to- ground fault or a 
large motor start up. The converse event is called voltage swell whereas the event of decreasing 
the voltage below 0.1 pu is called interruption and it can occur by faults, control malfunctions, or 
equipment failures [9][10]. 
 
Voltage fluctuations can be triggered when the load exhibits continuous, rapid variations in load 
current. They are described by their magnitude expressed as a percentage of the fundamental 
magnitude. The result of voltage fluctuation in a light system is called flicker and it can be 
observed by the human eye [9]. 
 
Voltage imbalance is the event when the three phases have different amplitudes relative to one 
another. Voltage imbalance occurs when different loads are connected to each phase resulting in 
different voltage drops through the line impedances [10]. 
 
Waveform distortion refers to phenomena such as harmonics, interharmonics, dc offset, notching 
and noise. Harmonics are the integer multiple of the fundamental frequency. Fourier analysis of 
the distorted waveform reveals the fundamental component and the harmonics. RTDS can 
conduct Fourier analysis in the Multiplot program. The harmonic distortion is usually measured 
with the total harmonic distortion (THD) which is the ratio of the geometric sum of all the 
harmonics to the fundamental. Harmonic distortion is caused by the operation of non linear loads 
and devices of the power system. Power electronics are the major source together with 
overheating of transformers and overheating of neutral wires in wye-connected circuits. 
 
Interharmonics are frequency components that are not integer multiple of the fundamental. DC 
offset refers to the presence of a DC voltage or current in an AC power system whereas nothing to 
repetitive disturbances in the line voltage. Finally noise is undesirable electrical signals in the 
voltage or current waveform [9]. 
 
Frequency variation describes the event when the voltage deviates from the nominal value. At any 
instant, the frequency of the power system is decided by the balance between the load and the 
capacity of the generators. Hence, when this balance changes small deviations in the frequency 
occur [9]. 
 
 
1.5 
About RTDS 
 
RTDS is a fully digital power system simulator that solves ElectroMagnetic Transients (EMT) in real 
time. The RTDS Simulator was the very first fully digital real time simulator, and still continues to 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
13 
 
be used for surveying among engineers, electrical utilities, power system equipment 
manufacturers, research institutes and universities for performing real time power system 
simulations. Historically, the RTDS Simulator was the outcome of a research project at the 
Manitoba HVDC Research Centre in 1989. 
Real time simulation means that a system’s response over 1 second is computed in exactly 1 
second. For the typical time step of 50 microseconds, all calculations that are required to 
determine the state of the power system are solved within this time step. PSCAD which is a non 
real time simulator for the calculation of the solution for each time step requires more than the 
real time which can be even 10 times bigger than the simulation step. PSCAD typically is equipped 
with only one CPU whereas RSCAD uses parallel processing. 
The RTDS Simulator operates without interruption in real time and provides accurate results over 
a frequency range from DC to 3 kHz. This range is a big advantage of the RSCAD Simulator 
compared to traditional stability or load flow programs because these programs’ analysis is 
restricted in a limited frequency range. The hardware is bundled into modular units which are 
called racks that permit easy expansion of the Simulator’s computing capability according to the 
needs of a simulation. 
 
Figure 1.8 The RTDS Simulator with two racks [19] 
The parallel processing is carried out by RISC Processor Cards (GPC) that are placed in each rack. 
The RTDS Simulator can assign each component and network solution to a different processor 
reducing the computation time achieving its real time operation. 
RSCAD is patented software by RTDS Technologies developed to interface with the RTDS 
simulator. RSCAD is an all-in-one package including all the features needed for conducting a 
simulation and monitoring the results of it without the user needing a third party products. The 
ability of the RSCAD Simulator running continuously in real time allows the user to view the results 
of a simulation like operating a real power system modifying parameters or applying contingencies 
[19],[20].

1.6 
Motivation and goals of the project 
 
During the last decades, there has been an increasing interest to the transients associated with 
circuit breaker operation. A radial network system like the one investigated which consists of a 
long submarine cable is even more susceptible because it has more capacitance than a 
conventional. A proper evaluation of the induced transients should be considered for the proper 
sizing of the circuit breakers and the insulation coordination. Although, there are many studies 
already available in the literature concerning transients induced by circuit breakers each network 
should be examined individually because the characteristics and the risk of failure depends on the 
parameters of the examined power network. 
The project goals can be summarized as: 
 Modeling the system components in RTDS 
 Performance of switching events for various scenarios. 
 Recording of the TOV that occurs at the input of the load and the current running through 
the receiving end of the subsea cable. 
 Analysis and identification of possible risks introduced by the TOV’s. 
 Recommendations for proper sizing through sensitivity analysis.  
 
 
1.7 
Project Limitations 
 
The simulation time step is limited by the complexity of the simulated network. RSCAD can 
operate in real time with a minimum time step of 10 μsec although only the scenario referring to 
the cable energization achieved a minimum time step of 15 μsec. RSCAD can operate in a smaller 
time step than the minimum but it works in non real time using only one CPU increasing the 
calculation time by many times the simulation step. The frequency of the switching transients is 
expected to be within the range of 3 – 25 kHz. [8] A minimum sample rate according to Nyquist 
theorem demands double of the frequency. For example an oscillatory transient of 20 kHz needs a 
simulation time step of 1/(2*20 kHz) or 25 μsec to be properly sampled and this time step is not 
achieved by many scenarios investigated in this project. 
 
The project meant to include an ASD connected with an Induction Motor (IM). RSCAD Support 
Center provided an ASD model and some references that it was based on upon request. The 
references did not explain how all the parameters had been calculated in the control circuit of the 
ASD and RSCAD was unable to explain, too. Hence, the circuit could not be modified to control the 
size of an IM that it was needed and resistive and inductive loads were used instead, to represent 
the seabed pumps (IM’s).  
==PAGE:24==
 
 
15 
 
The fact of not using power electronics can result in less harmonic distortion added to the TRV 
since ASD’s and power electronics in general induce harmonics to the feeding network. Another 
phenomenon that is sacrificed by using resistive and inductive loads is the motor starting. The 
energization of the motor is operated by means of a circuit breaker that interconnects the feeding 
network and distribution network. When an IM is energized, the motor current causes a voltage 
drop to the other loads of the power system at the point of common coupling (PCC). A starting of a 
large motor such as 4 MW seabed pump, it can draw high currents until the machine reaches its 
nominal speed resulting in a voltage sag which can last more than 5 cycles (100 msec). [10]. 
Although that the model does not include the complex high-frequency response model of an ASD 
and IM according to [12] it is still able to illustrate qualitatively the phenomenon of interest. 
The most important limitation triggers the modeling of vacuum circuit breakers (VCB). RSCAD 
model all types of circuit breakers with a big resistance that opens and closes. Therefore, 
important concepts such as current chopping and re-ignition cannot be modeled in RSCAD. 
When a VCB opens, an arc burns in the metal vapor from the contacts which requires a high 
temperature at the arc roots [21]. The current flow supplies with heat and while the current 
reaches zero, the metal vapor production is decreased. At the instant that the metal vapor cannot 
support anymore the arc, the arc suddenly stops or “chops out”- This is the action that is called 
“current chop” and stores energy in the system. Should the breaker opens at a zero current no 
energy is stored in the system but if the breaker opens at another point of the current waveform 
energy is stored. For modern breakers, current chop depends on the contact material, the load 
surge impedance and design and it ranges from 3 to 21 A [7][12]. 
If the very small current chop is coupled with the system capacitance and transformer inductance 
can generate a high frequency TRV on the breaker contacts and re-ignition occurs if the TRV 
exceeds the rated TRV of the breaker. Re-ignition can be dangerous because on each successive 
re-ignition the voltage escalates [8]. 
A third characteristic of a VCB is the high frequency current quenching capability that is defined by 
the slope of the high frequency reignited current when the current is zero. The importance of this 
parameter lies in the occurrence of re-ignitions when a TRV exceeds the dielectric strength of the 
breaker contacts. This results in high frequency current components superimposed to the power 
frequency current [12].

2.1 
The Simulation Time Step (Δt) 
 
The state of the power system is only computed at integral instants of time, therefore a mention 
to the simulation time step is useful. The selection of the simulation step was based on the 
anticipated frequency and the limitations of the software. Selection of a too large simulation step 
in relation to the anticipated frequency will cost in the accuracy of the results since the time steps 
will “miss” the frequency effects. On the other hand if the selected simulation time step is too 
small there will be excessive simulation times. 
 
In switching transients, the anticipated frequency varies in the range of 3 – 25kHz[8]. According to 
Nyquist criteria the minimum sample rate has to be twice the anticipated frequency, i.e. 20 - 167 
μsec.  
 
 The typical time step that RSCAD simulates is 50 μsec and the absolute minimum that can be run 
on RSCAD is 10 μsec. This absolute minimum can only be achieved in very small networks. RTDS 
has to solve all the equations within the given time step using the limited number of racks and 
processors that are available in order to achieve real time operation. That means that the time 
step cannot be randomly small without sacrificing the real time operation. The complexity of the 
networks that were used herein allowed a minimum time step of 15 μsec [22].

2.4.2 RSCAD option for cable models 
 
Travelling wave transmission line models of distributed parameters and PI section models are both 
available to model transmission lines in RTDS. Usually traveling wave models are used for all the 
cases and PI section model must be an option only if the length line is short. There are several 
reasons for this option. 
==PAGE:33==
 
 
24 
 
Travelling wave models are distributed parameter representations of transmission lines and they 
simulate with accuracy long lines than the lumped parameter representation inherent in PI section 
models. Lines up to 80km are considered short and lines longer than 240km are long. All the lines 
used in this project are less than 100km and for a steady state analysis a PI model can represent 
with accuracy. The short model ignores the shunt capacitance of the model because it is very 
small. On the other hand, the analysis of transient phenomena where higher frequencies are 
involved the wave characteristics of the voltage and the current on the line should be taken into 
account [28]. 
 
Travelling wave models are able to simulate the whole line using one instance of the model. PI 
section models tend to use more simulator hardware due to the requirement to cascade several PI 
sections to represent the line and to model the network nodes between the individual sections. 
On this point, travelling wave models require only one or two processors to model a complete line 
containing as many as 12 mutually coupled conductors [29]. 
 
Furthermore, travelling wave line models make available a tool for separating the power system 
into mathematically isolated sub−systems which can be run on separate RTDS Racks if required. 
 
The CABLE program that is another member of the RSCAD family of tools is used to define the 
geometry and parameters of the core and layers of a single phase cable. 
 
In general, transmission lines comprise of several mutually coupled phases or conductors. The 
extent of coupling is dependent on the geometry of the line and the proximity of the individual 
phase conductors to one another and to the ground. The CABLE program in RSCAD, is used to 
define the cable, its geometrical configuration and other parameter data for travelling wave line 
models. Both the Frequency Dependent and Bergeron lines are defined in this manner. PI section 
models are also available which can use the Bergeron output from the Cable program [29]. 
 
In the nominal pi representation the shunt admittance is divided into two equal parts placed at 
the two ends of the line whereas the series impedance is placed in the middle.

Energization of a transformer or energization of a nearby transformer can cause significant 
distortion of the wounding currents. Saturation, can also occur when a transformer is energized 
during a restoration of the power system. Therefore, the saturation of the core and the hysteresis 
characteristic had to be included in the transformer models. RSCAD provides the user with two 
options about the magnetizing branch. The first which is simpler is the linear model in which the 
magnetizing brunch is included in the model as an inductive brunch which is automatically 
calculated by RSCAD based on data entered for magnetizing current. The calculated value is fixed 
so the magnetizing current increases linearly with increase in applied voltage. Hence, effects of 
core saturation are not represented like iron losses and non linear nature of the iron core when it 
becomes saturated. Core saturation is responsible for the ferroresonance phenomenon which 
causes overvoltages and irregular wave shapes [22][34]. 
 
==PAGE:39==
 
 
30 
 
The second option that it was used in all the transformers herein included both saturation and 
hysteresis and the magnetizing current is included as part of the saturation/hysteresis calculations. 
A special tab popped up when the saturation and hysteresis option was chosen and the 
parameters were left to their default values that RSCAD suggests. RSCAD suggests that the 
saturation should be placed to the winding of the lower voltage. 
 
Magnetizing current was assigned 1% and stands for the magnetizing current that should run in a 
specific winding under 1 pu voltage if the other winding is open circuit. The proportion of the 
magnetizing current defines the proportion of the leakage reactance placed on each side of the 
magnetizing branch. The magnetizing current on the second winding was set to zero so all the 
leakage reactance was put on the first winding. 
 
The component is equipped with a dynamic tap changer which was used for scenario 6 to mitigate 
the increased voltage in the load bus. 10 tap positions were used and the initial tap position was 
set below 5 to decrease the voltage. RSCAD does not suggest extreme tap positions as they may 
cause numerical instabilities. 
 
Leakage reactance was set 0.10 pu for both transformers used in scenarios 1 to 5 and the topside 
step up transformer used in scenario 6. In scenario 6 a subsea transformer was introduced with 
0.085 pu leakage inductance. In power systems the leakage inductance provides a mechanism to 
reduce fault currents fault currents without having to add resistance. [34] 
 
No Load Losses were assigned their default value of 0.01pu and they are realized by introducing 
resistances between the nodes to which the windings are connected [22]. Copper losses stand for 
the windings losses because of a possible resistance in the windings and a zero value was entered. 
Copper losses were modeled with a fixed resistance.  
 
 
2.6 
Vacuum Circuit Breakers 
 
The investigated circuit is equipped with Vacuum VCB’s. As observed, every time a switching 
device is opened or closed, interactions between the circuit breaker with the other power system 
components can occur. These interactions frequently induce high frequency TOV’s in the power 
system and depending on the specifications of the VCB and the power system parameters can fail 
distribution equipment. These high frequency TOVs take place when a VCB exhibits abnormal 
behavior like current chop (when the breaker opens), restrike (excessive magnitude of TRV), re-
ignition (excessive frequency of TRV) and prestrike (when the VCB closes) [7][8][13]. 
 
==PAGE:40==
 
 
31 
 
RSCAD has only one model for circuit breakers and it is modeled as a large resistance when it is 
open and a small resistance when it is closed. RSCAD does not model the arcs associated with the 
breaker operation which are responsible for current chop and re-ignition. These arcs have the 
characteristic of a high frequency and very short time duration (they are cleared before the next 
zero crossing of the current). Therefore a much smaller time step is required to achieve the 
resolution to model these phenomena than the 15-30 microseconds which was the lowest time 
step that the complexity of the network allowed RSCAD to simulate.  
 
 
Figure 2.7 lf_rtds_ sharc_sld_BREAKER component with closed initial status 
 
The component “lf_rtds_sharc_sld_BREAKER” that can be found in the Power System Master 
Library at the Fault Models sector was used. Editing of the component included a unique name per 
phase and phase breaker closed resistance at its default value of 0.1 ohm. The entry “Extinguish 
Arc for abs(I) at or below” was set to zero and it means that if an open command is given to the 
breaker while current is flowing the absolute value of the current will be compared to this 
parameter and only if the absolute value is lower than this parameter the current will be 
interrupted. The reason behind this is that when inductive loads are switched lead to voltage 
spikes. Therefore, by setting “Extinguish Arc for abs(I) at or below” at zero means that the 
breakers open only when current crossing the breaker is at its zero point [22]. 
 
The circuit breakers in RSCAD are controlled by a switch signal (integer number) passed on the 
backplane. Value “0” opens the breaker whereas value “1” causes the breaker to close. The 
control signal was created in a separate hierarchy box based on digital functions. The Hierarchy 
component is used to schematically separate the user’s circuit diagram into a number of blocks. 
 
The severity of the TOVs depends on the point of the voltage waveform that the breaker is closed. 
Therefore the voltage waveform has to pass through a zero crossing detector in the positive going 
direction and delayed as many degrees as needed to energize the breaker. This was controlled 
with a slider but the degrees need to be converted in time. The point on wave energization is 
entered in degrees and this value is multiplied by the inverse value of 360 times the period. The 
frequency is 50Hz, hence the gain block was assigned the value 0.0000555. That means that if the 
user wants to energize the breaker when the voltage waveform is at 60o the voltage waveform has 
to be delayed for 60*0.0000555 sec. The breaker operation circuit that was designed for all the 
scenarios herein is displayed in Figure 2.9. 
 
==PAGE:41==

The zero crossing detector signal of the voltage of the bus N7 located right behind the circuit 
breaker and the control pushbutton BRKACLOSE are sent to an AND gate. The user has pushed the 
BRKACLOSE button in the Runtime wishing to close the breaker but he has to wait until the next 
zero crossing. When this happens the AND gate’s signal logic output becomes 1. Then the two 
pulse generators delay the signal for the point on wave control and the signal is lead to the ‘Set’ 
input of the S-R flip flop. The flip flop is turned to logic one when the Set parameter is on logic one 
and finally the breaker closes. The de-energization of the breaker does not need point on wave 
control because the breakers are set to open when the current is crossing zero. 
 
 
2.7 
Load 
 
The seabed pumps represent the load in the electric power system. A seabed pump consists of an 
IM and a Variable Speed Drive (VSD). A VSD is an ASD that is used to control the speed of the IM 
and the torque by means of varying the frequency and the voltage supplied to the IM. There are 
many advantages using a VSD [35] with the most important being the ability of an IM equipped 
with a VSD not be run at full speed, when the load requirements are so. Therefore there is 
reduced energy consumption and energy cost. 
 
A VSD comprises of two stages. The first is an AC-DC converter of six diodes that rectify the 
voltage. The second is a DC-AC converter which has six semiconductor switches and its control is 
based on Sinusoidal Pulse Width Modulation (SPWM). Unfortunately, RSCAD was not able to 
provide a reliable functional model that could drive a bigger machine than the one that it was 
originally designed and the whole analysis was not conducted with an IM model driven by a VSD.

In order to explain the theory behind the cable energization it should be taken into account that 
electrically wise a cable is mainly a capacitive element. Therefore, the energization of a cable is 
similar to the energization of a capacitor bank. The magnitude of the TOV depends on the moment 
that the cable is energized. Should the circuit breaker closes when the voltage at its terminals is 
zero the TOV is ideally zero while if the circuit breaker is energized on 90o the TOV reaches its 
maximum. This happens because depending on the point of wave energization the capacitor bank 
is charged differently and different amount of energy oscillates between the capacitance of the 
cable and the inductance of the step-up transformer. 
 
Initially the capacitance of the cable and the inductance of the step down transformer are not 
energized whereas the inductance of the step-up transformer is energized since the circuit breaker 
is placed between the subsea cable and the topside transformer. The voltage in the capacitor has 
to be continuous; therefore if the cable is energized on a point that the voltage wave is not zero 
there is a discontinuity in the voltage of the capacitor bank. To avoid this, the capacitor bank has 
to be charged through the inductor of the step up transformer resulting in the initiation of a 
transient. After a while when the voltage input becomes equal to the voltage in the capacitor 
bank, the current running through the circuit breaker is at its maximum. Due to the energy 
conservation this current cannot go straight to zero, so the voltage at the terminals of the cable 
continues to increase exceeding the amplitude of the voltage input leading to a TOV. As the 
current of the circuit breaker decreases and when it reaches the zero the voltage of the capacitor 
bank reaches a peak value and it begins to discharge [36]. 
The subsea cable is simulated with distributed parameters so it can be considered as an infinite 
group of RLC circuits connected in series. The ohmic content of the cable helps to damp the 
transient so the oscillation does not continue forever. The waves of the cable energization are not 
purely sinusoidal because the infinite group of the RLC circuits in series of the cable introduces 
distortion and apart from the cable the step down transformer is being energized, too. The step 
down transformer is considered as an inductive load and when an inductive load is switched TOVs 
are introduced [36]. 
==PAGE:56==
 
 
47 
 
It can be also observed that the steady state voltage increases while moving from the source to 
the receiving edge of the cable. The capacitive line charging current running through the line 
inductance causes an increase in voltage of the line which is becoming bigger as we move from the 
sending to receiving edge when the line is in no load operation. Assuming a line with negligible 
losses the load is the shunt capacitance of the cable therefore the current is purely capacitive. 
When capacitive current runs through the inductance of the line the produced voltage drop is in 
phase with voltage of the sending edge increasing additively the voltage. The phenomenon is 
called Ferranti effect and it applies in all medium or large lines that operate in load conditions [28].

The TOV measured is 34.34kV (rms phase to neutral) or 1.27 pu. According to [37] ferroresonance 
is “a phenomenon usually characterized by overvoltages and irregular wave shapes and associated 
with the excitation of one or more saturable inductors through a capacitance in series with the 
inductor.”  
The subsea cable and the stray capacitance of the step down transformer play the role of the 
capacitance whereas the inductor is the core of the transformer which saturates when the voltage 
increases beyond a specific point. All the transformers herein have included the saturation effect 
and they operate according to a saturation curve. The saturation curve illustrates the flux as a 
function to the applied voltage and it has a “knee” voltage set at 1.25pu. The value of the 
inductance of the transformer is higher when the core is not saturated and this change in the 
value happens at the “knee” voltage. When the transformer operates under nominal voltage levels 
the capacitance is smaller than the inductance [13]. 
The de-energization caused a TOV higher than the “knee” resulting in the saturation of the 
transformer core and decreasing the value of the inductance. When the value of the capacitance 
becomes equal with the value of the lowered inductance, ferroeresonance starts [13]. Figure 3.6 
was plotted with the saturation effect disabled on the step down transformer by changing the 
“Type” of the transformer component. Figure 3.6 stresses the importance of including the 
saturation effect in the study of TOVs. The model with the non saturation effect had a lowered 
TOV at 29.94 kV (line to neutral) or 1.11 pu and no irregular waveform.

able to catch oscillatory transients up to that high frequencies. These phenomena cause 
deterioration, poor power quality and are damaging the equipment installation [6][11].

Figure 3.26 reveals that right after the energization of the second subsea breaker the phase to 
phase voltage of ASD1 plummets to 0.4 pu. This undervoltage is a power quality problem and the 
cable is responsible for it. Scenario 2 which examined the same circuit without the presence of the 
cables, does not perform undervoltages and restrikes. The cable, as mentioned before, electrically 
is a capacitive bank. If a discharged capacitor is energized when the voltage is near its peak, the 
voltage across the capacitor cannot change immediately. Then the capacitor pulls the voltage of 
the busbar to which is attached down while the current reaches the capacitor to charge it. 
Afterwards, the system rebounds, the voltage of the busbar overshoots to 1.29kV (1.32 pu) and 
this is followed by an oscillatory recovery of 6.6 kHz and then restrikes occur. All the cables are 
connected to the same busbar, hence switching of the second circuit breaker affects the voltage of 
the first ASD [17][38].

4. Conclusions & 
future work       
 
 
 
4.1 
Conclusions & recommendations 
 
Scenario 1 studied the energization of the subsea cable in a series with a step down transformer 
and the measurements are based on the rms phase to phase voltage at both ends of the subsea 
cable. Scenario 1 performed the biggest TOV of 88% and showed the impact that can have in the 
voltage the switching of a capacitor bank. In specific scenario 1 concludes that when the breaker is 
energized at a point where the voltage wave is on 0o the TOV is the smallest possible whereas if it 
closes close at its peak the TOV reaches its maximum. It also concludes that the discontinuity in 
the voltage across the capacitor bank being energized is responsible for the inauguration of the 
impulsive transient.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Scenario 1 verifies the Ferranti effect and stresses the importance of the saturation in the 
transformer. Scenario 1 proved the presence of ferroresonance during the de-energization of the 
subsea cable and concluded that the saturation of the core transformer should be taken into 
account in a transient analysis study.  
Scenario 2 concludes that the energization of a transformer induces an impulsive positive 
transient due to sudden change in the current running through the transformer inductance. On 
the other hand, back-to-back energization of a transformer assuming ideal connection between 
the subsea switchgear and the step down transformer induces no TOV’s but harmonics. 
The objective of scenario 3 is to study the impact of the load conditions to the induced transients 
by the circuit switching.  Scenario 3 draws the conclusion that the operation under load conditions 
induced transients of reduced TOV, much slower rate of rise, without oscillatory transients 
following the spike and less harmonic content than operating with open endings which reflect the 
whole wave back. 
Scenario 4 took into account non ideal connection between the subsea switchgear and the step 
down transformer. The cable found to induce transients of bigger TOV and caused the circuit 
==PAGE:102==
 
 
93 
 
breaker to restrike after the first spike. On the other hand, the TRV performed slower spikes and 
had lower harmonic content than scenario 2 which assumed ideal connection. Back-to-back 
energization of the second and third cable induced a negative spike to the voltage of the first load. 
A sensitivity analysis was conducted with 3 different cable lengths of 1, 2 and 5 km and it ended up 
that the longest cable caused more severe power quality problems. 
Scenario 5 which was simulated with load conditions and non ideal connection between the circuit 
breakers and the step down transformers concluded similar results to scenario 3 and the negative 
spikes during the back-to-back energization induced under no load conditions were not observed 
here. With load conditions less power quality problems are induced than running with no load 
conditions. The sensitivity analysis recommends the smallest cable among the 1, 2 and 5 km for 
less harmful TRV.  On top of this sensitivity analysis, the configuration of 10, 20 and 50 m long 
cables induced transients of bigger TOV than the 2 and 5 km with higher rate of rise. The latter 
suggests that not necessarily the shortest cable is the ideal option. Moreover the breakers did not 
restrike and no negative spikes were observed during the back-to-back energization. 
In scenario 6 where the load is energized only through a cable without step down transformer the 
results vary a lot compared to scenario 5. TRV measured at the input of the of the load was found 
to induce bigger TOV’s in scenario 6 but no restrikes of the circuit breaker were observed. Back-to-
back energization in scenario 6 caused negative spikes. The sensitivity analysis with short cable 
configuration concluded that lower TOV’s and negative spikes were induced but the short 
configuration induced an oscillatory transient that lasted for a few cycles. In general the step down 
transformer was found to mitigate the transients at its secondary winding and the configuration of 
scenario 6 should be avoided. 
The investigation on the point wave of energization of the circuit breakers was studied to illustrate 
how the point of the voltage wave can affect the TRV and not to suggest an angle at which the 
breaker should close to cause a lower TOV. The investigation was done on the point of the voltage 
of phase A and the measured voltage was the phase to phase voltage AB. When the breaker is 
energized on 0o of the voltage wave of phase A no severe TOV was noticed to the phase to phase 
voltage AB but the impact on phase C is not captured. An example of this follows in Figures 4.1 and 
4.2 taken from scenario 6 with configuration of long cables and energization at 0o of phase voltage 
A. Figure 4.1 shows the phase voltages A, B and C where phases A and B perform no TOV whereas 
C does. Figure 4.2 illustrates the phase to phase voltage of AB where no TOV occur. 
==PAGE:103==
 
 
94 
 
Figure 4.1 3Φ voltage waves during energization on 0o of phase A (Node 19) 
 
Figure 4.1 Phase to phase voltage AB wave during energization at 0o of phase A 
The de-energization of the circuit breakers was found to cause small TOV’s in scenario 4 with 5 km 
cable connected between the breakers and the load and small oscillatory transients in scenario 5 
with the short cables. The reason for not observing severe power quality problems during the de-
energization of the circuit breakers is because RSCAD cannot model the current chop and the 
circuit breakers are set to open when the current running through them is zero. The cause of 
TOV’s is the stored energy in the system the instant that the breakers open and since the breakers 
open at current zero there is no stored energy in the power system, hence no TOV’s occur. 
==PAGE:104==
 
 
95 
 
To sum up the results should be interpreted carefully because the limitations of RSCAD can have 
affected the results presented. The lack of ability from RSCAD to model a VCB cannot produce 
realistic results during the de-energization of the breakers, the time step which was not able to go 
lower than 15 μsec cannot catch oscillatory transients of higher frequencies and the combination 
of the two previous points cannot represent re-ignition that often occurs in circuit breakers when 
high frequency transients are imposed on the VCB contacts. On top of that the shortage of the 
software to supply with a reliable model of an ASD that would add accuracy in the results renders 
RTDS not the suitable software for TRV studies. 
 
4.2 
Future work 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
After the end of this thesis that studied the TRV induced in the load of a subsea power system the 
following topics can be further investigated. 
 A conduction of the same study in another suitable platform that can support smaller time 
step than RSCAD can and is equipped with a VCB model. A choice could be EMTP software. 
 Further investigation of the transients in other nodes of the subsea power system 
especially to the side of transformers which are connected with short cables to examine 
possible failure. Previous studies like [7] and [8] have concluded that cables of 200 feet 
(circa 60 m) connected between the switchgear and the transformer can catastrophically 
fail the transformer. 
 Collection of data about the basic impulse level (BIL) of the transformers and 
implementation of techniques and equipment that can mitigate the switching transients. 
Arresters, surge capacitors and snubbers have successfully proven to address to transients 
associated with breaker switching.
