Abstract 
 
The scope of this thesis is to examine the functional split concept, as well as, the 
implementation and the impact of this in the 5G fronthaul. The architecture of each split 
option and potential pros and cons will be analyzed. Furthermore, through a theoretical 
case study, the impact of a functional split is examined, in terms of required bitrates on 
the fronthaul link. Getting an overall image of the required bitrates will allow us to 
evaluate the feasibility or viability of each split option. Also, the aggregation concept will 
be presented, as dimensioning of the backhaul is as important as the fronthaul. The 
factors that affect the backhaul dimensioning, as well as, the main aggregation methods 
will be presented. Also, a capacity planning and dimensioning scenarios will be 
presented.  Finally, an overview of the proposed 5G deployment architecture takes place, 
as well as, an introduction to the most important characteristics and technologies of the 
5G mobile networks.

1. Introduction 
 
Over the last decade, mobile communications have become an indispensable part of our 
everyday life and they have clearly contributed in changing modern society. The effects 
of mobile communications from social and economic perspectives are obvious, as they’re 
being used for much more than simple communication. A case in point is the use of 
mobile communications in autonomous vehicles, virtual reality, augmented reality, 
Internet-of-Things (IoT) and many more services, which aim to improve our everyday 
life. As more and more mobile devices acquire internet connectivity, it’s anticipated that 
mobile data traffic will keep growing rapidly. According to forecasts from Ericsson, the 
total mobile traffic will increase by a factor of 5 by 2024. Global mobile data traffic will 
reach 136 exabytes / month by 2024. [1] 
 
Figure 1: Traffic predictions from 2014 to 2024. [1] 
As Mobile Network Operators (MNOs) strive to cope with the rapidly growing 
data traffic and the incremental demands for new services, a redesign in the architecture 
of todays’ mobile networks seems emerging, in order to facilitate the latest trends. 
Deployment of next generation networks, the 5G mobile networks seems essential, so 
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
growing requirements such as higher performance, increased coverage and capacity 
could be fulfilled. Todays’ mobile networks are undergoing a revolutionary 
transformation and optimization, from 4G to 5G era. When transition from 4G to 5G will 
be completed, the 5G ecosystem will be a superior platform, offering real-time services 
characterized by low latency times and high Quality-of-Service (QoS), even in the most 
remote regions. 
The 5G system will be redesigned in both tiers, Core and Radio Access Network 
(RAN), so as to efficiently and effectively cope with the new requirements. The upcoming 
5G Core and RAN with the New Radio (NR) air interface will offer, support for a wide 
range of frequency bands, efficient spectrum utilization, lower power consumption and 
connectivity with a massive number of end devices.  All of these new features would 
require new and redesigned 5G radio access technologies, such as Multiple-Input-
Multiple-Output (MIMO) with beamforming, millimeter waves (mmWaves) and Non-
Orthogonal Multiple Access (NOMA). Furthermore, in order to support various use 
cases, different deployment architectures are expected, so as to combine both LTE and 
5G connectivity. Finally, as mobile data traffic is skyrocketing, an emerging need is born 
to evaluate the upcoming 5G networks architectures and examine potential pros and cons 
of each case. Last but not least, an assessment is needed on the 5G RAN and the fronthaul 
transport networks. That is to say, how could the RAN efficiently cope with the demands, 
leveraging concepts such as the functional split in order to optimize the transmission 
requirements in terms of bitrates and capacity, on the fronthaul links. 
 
1.1 The Evolution of Mobile Cellular Technologies 
Over the last few decades, mobile communications have been evolving with a remarkable 
fast pace. The rapidly growing number of mobile subscribers made mobile operators and 
companies to develop new technologies, so the rising demands of users can be fulfilled. 
Back to the early 1980s, it was then when the 1st generation (1G) mobile networks were 
introduced, offering analog voice services but no data services. Later in 1980s, the 2nd 
generation (2G) of cellular networks was introduced, being the milestone of digital 
cellular networks. 2G technology could seamlessly provide digital voice services and 
Short Message Service (SMS) text messages. In 2G era, Global Systems for Mobile 
Communications (GSM) was the most widely used technology. General Packet Radio 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
Service (GPRS) and Enhanced Data rates for GSM Evolution (EDGE) were introduced as 
on top technologies of 2G mobile networks, allowing for packet-based services with data 
rates of up to 384kbps. 2G mobile networks were making use of Frequency Division 
Multiple Access (FDMA) and Time Division Multiple Access (TDMA) schemes. [2] 
The 3rd Generation (3G) of cellular networks was introduced in 2000. 3G cellular 
technology was considered a superior platform, offering increased data rates and a wide 
variety of services such as e-mail, video-streaming and Global Position System (GPS) 
navigation. With achievable data rates of up to 2Mbps, 3G was the ideal candidate of 
being a multimedia platform. In 3rd generation networks, the widely used GSM 
technology was replaced by Universal Mobile Telecommunication Systems (UMTS), 
which was based on Wide Code Division Multiple Access Scheme (WCDMA). High 
Speed Downlink Packet Access (HSDPA) and High-Speed Uplink Packet Access 
(HSUPA) were introduced later. Both technologies were based on UMTS and the goal 
was to provide even higher data rates at that moment, up to 14.4Mbps for HSDPA and 
5.6Mbps for HSUPA. [2] 
As data traffic was growing rapidly, the successor of 3G networks was introduced, 
the 4th (4G) cellular networks or otherwise called Long-Term Evolution (LTE) networks. 
The 4G cellular technology is an all IP based communication platform, being released for 
the first time in 2009. The incentive towards 4G networks design was to provide superior 
experience to the end user, by providing internet access, video-streaming and other data 
services at data rates of up to 150Mbps. LTE networks were making use of a more 
advanced multiplexing scheme, the Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiple Access 
(OFDMA) scheme. [3] Later, based on existing LTE infrastructure, LTE advanced was 
introduced to fulfill the prerequisites specified by ITU for International Mobile 
Telecommunications (IMT) - Advanced. LTE-Advanced can provide increased data rates 
of up to 3Gbps, higher spectral efficiency, increased number of active users and improved 
coverage using MIMO technology. [4] Finally, LTE-Advanced Pro will be the successor 
of LTE-Advanced. LTE-Advanced Pro is still under development and as “Pro” implies, it 
will be an ever more advanced communication platform, delivering a wide variety of 
new services like public safety services, IoT, automotive communications, all of which 
will be integrated in 5G.  LTE-A Pro will be the foundation stone of the upcoming 5G and 
that is why, it will facilitate the transition from 4th to 5th generation. [5]

1.2 The emergence of 5G 
It’s been almost 10 years since the first deployment of 4G networks and each cellular 
technology has an estimated life cycle of 10 years. Having said that, 4G networks can still 
provide a wide variety of services with satisfactory QoS, fulfilling most of consumers 
service requirements. However, it’s anticipated that mobile data traffic will rise 
significantly, as the number of connected devices will explode over the next few years. 
As a result, 4G networks may not be able to handle such rapid expansion in the future. 
The design of a new platform is vital to accommodate the growing needs for enhanced 
connectivity and improved performance. 5G is envisioned as a superior digital platform, 
accomplishing the goal of a fully connected society. 
An important incentive towards 5G development is the debut of IoT. IoT 
applications are becoming increasingly popular and it’s expected that the number of 
connected cellular IoT devices will account for 4.1 billion of the total connected devices, 
by 2024. [1] The rapid proliferation of IoT will lead the industry to a digital era, 
skyrocketing the productivity, sustainability and innovation. IoT devices will be 
widespread used in several fields such as public safety, healthcare, manufacturing, 
entertainment and automotive.

The key requirements of 5G for the IMT-2020 networks have been specified by the 
ITU and as a result, the following have been identified as the most critical: [6] 
➢ High data rates - 100+ Mbps user data rates. 
➢ High capacity - 10Tbps per km2. 
➢ Low energy consumption – 10+ years of battery life. 
➢ Ultra-high density – 1 million nodes per km2 
➢ Low latency – 1 ms end-to-end latency. 
5G will support a wide variety of use cases, each use case with different requirements. 
However, most of the popular use cases have been grouped into three main categories, 
based on IMT-2020: 
• Extreme Mobile BroadBand (xMBB) 
The target of the xMBB service is to provide increased data rates, low latency and 
improved coverage, so connectivity could be available anywhere. Mobile data traffic is 
growing rapidly and day by day, more and more devices are connected to a cellular 
network. xMBB service will offer a unique enhanced mobile broadband experience, 
fulfilling the rising consumer demands for increased performance. 
• Massive Machine – Type Communications (mMTC) 
This scenario is identified by the loose requirements, in contrast to the other two 
use cases. The exchange of a low volume of traffic with non-stringent delay requirements, 
from a massive number of connected sensors and devices, is what characterizes the 
mMTC scenario. 
• Ultra – Reliable and Low – Latency Communication (URLLC) 
Low latency, high availability and reliability are some of the key characteristics, 
which represent this scenario. Mission critical applications, self-driving cars and industry 
automation are examples under the URLLC use case. [7]

The development of key requirements and standardizations are under the liability

is to develop concepts that will set the foundation stones of 5G ecosystem. Their proposed

IMT-2020. [7]

2. 5G Mobile Networks 
 
Based on the latest versions [9][10] from the 3GPP, there will be two different paths 
towards 5G development and deployment, the Non-Stand-Alone (NSA) and the Stand-
Alone (SA). The NSA option refers to the early stage architecture that the NR will be used 
in combination with LTE and it’s based on the existing EPC, while in the SA, the NR will 
be based on the all-new 5G Core. Yet, it should be noted that the development of 5G 
system is still under extensive research, which is expected to be completed by the end of 
2019 with release 16. The 5G NR is expected to smoothly cooperate and co-exist with 
existing LTE architecture, as well as, it’s been characterized as a future-proof technology 
that would be able to cope with future demands. Finally, not only does 5G provide 
compatibility with 4G Radio (E-UTRA) and services, but it will facilitate interoperability 
with other non-3GPP technologies. [9][10] 
This chapter is mainly focused on the description and evaluation of the 5G 
architectural proposals introduced by the 3GPP. To be more specific, the introduced 
deployment options will be analyzed and examined for potential pros and cons of each 
one. Furthermore, the building blocks of the 5G ecosystem will be introduced, such as 
transmission scheme and frame structure. Finally, the last subsection is dedicated to the 
overall summary of the chapter. 
 
2.1 The Next Generation Core 
The 5th Generation Core Network is anticipated to fulfill all the requirements for all the 
new upcoming services and use cases. Based on the capabilities and the reference design 
of the 5G core network as defined from the 3GPP, it has been characterized as a Service 
Based Architecture (SBA). This is justified by the fact that network elements have been 
split into several new smaller instances called Network Functions (NFs). The outcome of 
this split is a more flexible and less costly deployment of network equipment, as 
complexity of NFs becomes lower. NFs can be distinguished among those dedicated for 
User Plane (UP) and Control Plane (CP) purposes, facilitating the creation of logical 
architectures with minimum dependence on the physical infrastructure. The separation 
between control and user plane allows flexible operation and split among core network 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
and RAN. [11] Furthermore, another important key aspect of this architecture is that, it 
should be multi-vendor compatible, allowing for interoperability and cooperation among 
core networks from different operators. An overall representation of the 5GC network 
with the individual network functions can be seen in figure 4.

The 5G Core network is comprised of several NFs, each one serving a different purpose. 
The NFs and their functionalities are the following: [11][12] 
• Network Repository Function (NRF): Maintains a database of the existing NFs and 
their supported services, in the network. 
• Network Exposure Function (NEF): Maintains a database about NFs supported 
services and provides information to others about them. 
• Network Slice Selection Function (NSSF): Selection of the Network Slice that 
fulfills users’ requirements. 
• Policy Control Function (PCF): Management of network performance, control of 
policies and charging, based on UE subscription information. 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
• Unified Data Management (UDM): Management of user information and 
credentials, subscriptions management. 
• Authentication Server Function (AUSF): Authentication and authorization 
functions. 
• Access and Mobility management Function (AMF): Access control of UEs, 
registration and mobility management, security and location services. 
• Session Management Function (SMF): Assignment of IP addresses to UEs and 
management, roaming services, session establishment and termination. 
• User Plane Function (UPF): Management of QoS, user traffic forwarding, traffic 
inspection, monitoring and reporting. 
The operation principle of the proposed architecture that consists of several NFs, is that 
the user who requests a specific service, performs discovery to check for available 
instances in the network and acquires access to the appropriate NF, based on the type of 
requested function. Discovery is achieved using NRF instance, which receives the request 
and grants access to the proper NF, taking into consideration users’ request. [11] An 
overall example of the discovery process in a single mobile network can be seen in figure 
5. The procedure can be more complicated when a request traverses NRFs of multiple 
mobile networks.

2.2 The Next Generation RAN 
The technical specifications of the Next Generation RAN (NG-RAN) along with the NR 
system, are under study by the 3GPP. The NG-RAN is expected to provide compatibility 
with both NR and E-UTRA, while supporting connectivity with the 5GC. The proposed 
architecture consists of next generation Node Bs (gNBs) interconnected with each other 
via Xn interface, while communication between the NG-RAN and 5GC is achieved 
through NG interface. The gNBs are terminated to specific 5GC NFs, the AMF and UPF. 
[10] While the RAN deployment scenario in figure 6 represents a 5G SA NR case, LTE 
base stations or otherwise called eLTE eNBs might be deployed in an NSA E-UTRA 
scenario. eLTE eNBs are the next generation LTE eNBs, which can communicate with 
both EPC and NGC. Therefore, interoperability among NG-RAN and E-UTRA is essential 
to achieve multi-connectivity, especially in mixed deployment scenarios.

Flexible allocation of processing resources, depending on the requirements of each use 
case, is an important characteristic of the newly designed RAN. A case in point is that the 
functionality of a gNB can be split among two different locations, the Centralized Unit 
(CU) and the Distributed Unit (DU), while communication between them is achieved via 
Fs interface. Depending on the functional split, a subset of functions can be allocated to 
either CU or DU. The operation principle of a functional split is the placement of higher 
protocol layer NR processes to the CU, while assigning the lower protocol layer functions 
to a DU, close to gNB. [9] A functional split can offer several gains that will further 
examined in chapter 3. 
 
2.2.1 NR – The Next Generation Air Interface 
One of the building blocks of 5G networks is the NR air interface introduced by the 3GPP. 
The NR will leverage higher untapped frequency bands, while both licensed and 
unlicensed spectrum are being considered, depending on the use case. [10] Exploitation 
of high frequencies will allow for wider spectrum, which will boost capacity and 
performance, compared to LTE. The NR will provide solid interworking with both 5G 
and 4G, offering compatibility with both SA and NSA deployments.

In contrary to LTE, which is mainly deployed in the sub-3GHz frequency range, 
deployment of NR will take place in three different frequency ranges, the low (<2GHz) 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
with maximum channel bandwidth of 20MHz, the mid (2-6GHz) with maximum channel 
bandwidth of up to 100MHz and lastly, the high (>24GHz) with support for up to 
400MHz of channel bandwidth. [14] The increased channel bandwidths are expected to 
bring substantial gains in spectrum efficiency, which is one of the key characteristics of 
the 5G system. The 3GPP in [10] has grouped, the potential for initial deployment 
frequencies, into two frequency range groups. The 1st group is the Frequency Range 1 
(FR1) that includes frequencies from 450MHz to 6GHz with supported subcarrier spacing 
options of 15/30/60KHz. The 2nd group is the Frequency Range 2 (FR2) for deployments 
in the mmWaves, 24.25GHz to 52.6GHz, supporting 60/120KHz subcarrier spacing 
options. The specified bands support both Time Division Duplexing (TDD) and 
Frequency Division Duplexing (FDD). [10] Finally, it should be noted that re-farming is 
also being considered, from LTE to NR, as most frequencies in the low range are already 
occupied by LTE. 
Among the introduced primary frequency bands, supplementary UL (SUL) and 
supplementary DL (SDL) frequencies have been included that can be assigned either on 
the DL or UL. The aim of supplementary frequencies is to increase the overall coverage 
and capacity, when needed. A case in point is that, an SDL can increase the downlink

systems, offering high throughput in a wide range of deployment scenarios. CP-OFDM 
offers flexible subcarrier spacing options, from 15KHz to 120KHz, compared to LTE 
where only 15KHz was the only option. Furthermore, normal CP and extended CP can 
be implemented. 5G NR supports both TDD and FDD transmission, using the same frame 
structure. [14] An overview of the NR frame structure in the time domain can be seen in 
figure 9.

shorter duration than this of a normal slot. This type of transmission can be useful in use

2.2.3 5G Key Technologies for Radio Access 
The 5G radio access will leverage some of the already existing technologies, as well as, of 
newer ones. The advanced technologies in the 5G RAN will facilitate the efficient 
utilization of the wider spectrum, they will boost coverage and significantly increase 
performance. The most important technologies that will contribute into the establishment 
of the 5G system are massive MIMO with beamforming, and NOMA. 
 
2.2.3.1 Massive MIMO and Beamforming 
MIMO has been identified as a key technology that leads to multiple benefits, such as 
improved spectral efficiency, reduced power consumption and higher performance. The 
main idea behind MIMO concept is that the base station is configured with multiple 
antennas, hence being able to send or receive multiple data streams, simultaneously. The 
more antennas, the more data streams can be transmitted or received simultaneously, 
thus higher data rates. [19] 
MIMO architectures were firstly introduced in the 3GPP Release 8, with the 
earliest ones being Single-User MIMO (SU-MIMO) and Multi-User MIMO (MIMO). In 
SU-MIMO, both base station and UE were equipped with multiple antennas, yet the 
transmitted data streams were received exclusively by one UE. In MU-MIMO, both base 
station and UEs were equipped with multiple antennas, while the transmitted data 
streams were received by several UEs, using spatial multiplexing.[18][19] When the first 
version of MIMO appeared in Release 8, MIMO became available only for the downlink, 
while the maximum configuration was 4x4. Later in Release 10, MIMO became available 
for the uplink, while the maximum configuration came up to 8x8. MIMO was 
considerably enhanced with the 3-Dimensional MIMO (3D MIMO) architecture, 
otherwise known as Full Dimensional MIMO (FD-MIMO), which became available in 
Release 13. [18] 3D MIMO enabled the exploitation and control of both the elevation angle 
(vertical) and the azimuth (horizontal); hence a beam could be steered towards a specific 
area or user in order to ensure the required coverage. The evolution of traditional MIMO 
is the massive MIMO, where gNBs will be configured with a few hundred antenna 
elements, providing to simultaneously serve high number of users by exploiting the same 
==PAGE:26==
25 
 
time-frequency resources. [20] Exploitation of spatial multiplexing will allow for 
independent data transmission per spatial channel. 
 
Figure 11: Massive MIMO concept with spatial multiplexing. [19] 
Beamforming will be another key component of massive MIMO that will play a 
fundamental role in the establishment of mmWaves. A case in point, is that beamforming 
allows a massive number of antennas to transmit, using narrow beams in order to 
concentrate a certain level of power in a specific direction. [19] Such a technique can 
effectively eliminate interference, as each beam targets only the intended device. An 
overview of the massive MIMO with beamforming concept can be seen in figure 12.

Massive MIMO with beamforming will contribute significantly in the 
implementation of mmWaves. As mmWaves operate at high frequencies, they suffer 
from poor propagation characteristics. The signal is susceptible to blockages and as a 
result, the signal strength gets low. A case in point is that beamforming can eliminate 
propagation losses, by using high power directional beams. All in all, massive MIMO and 
beamforming are expected to be the foundation building blocks of 5G radio access, 
increasing the overall coverage and capacity of the network, while providing significant 
performance gains. 
 
2.2.3.2 Non-Orthogonal Multiple Access  
Multiple access (MA) schemes allow for efficient utilization and distribution of available 
radio resources among several users. That is to say, as spectrum in mobile networks is in 
most cases scarce, a MA scheme facilitates the effective share of available bandwidth 
among multiple UEs, hence improving the overall spectral efficiency. MA schemes can 
be distinguished among the orthogonal (OMA) and non-orthogonal (NOMA) ones. [22] 
 
While OFDMA and Single-Carrier FDMA (SC-FDMA) were the principle MA 
schemes in LTE, a need for a new MA scheme seems to emerge due to the high 
requirements of 5G networks. NOMA seems to be a candidate for 5G networks and that’s 
why is under extensive study from the 3GPP, as a MA scheme that could offer 
considerable improved performance and spectral efficiency. [22] In figure 13, we can 
observe the main differences between OMA and NOMA schemes. As can be seen in 
OMA, each user can exploit the available resources in an orthogonal manner, within a 
specific frequency band or time slot. In NOMA, several users exploit the available 
resources simultaneously in a non-orthogonal manner. Multiplexing in the power-
domain is the unique characteristic of NOMA, as other MA schemes allow multiplexing 
either on the frequency or time domain. [22] 
NOMA can be distinguished into two main categories, based on how the 
multiplexed signals are being separated, the code-domain and the power-domain 
NOMA. In the code-domain NOMA, each user is assigned a unique code while the signal 
is being separated with the use of spreading codes. In the power-domain NOMA, based 
==PAGE:28==
27 
 
on the channel conditions, each user is assigned different power level and the signal is 
being separated in the power domain. [22] 
 
Figure 13: Multiplexing with OMA and NOMA. [22] 
Overall, the operation principle of NOMA is based on the allocation of power, depending 
on the radio conditions. To be more specific, NOMA selects a number of UEs with 
variable radio conditions and multiplex them into the same radio resources, using 
variable transmission power. The goal of this technique is to enable UEs with strong 
channel conditions to use the same radio resources assigned to UEs with poor channel 
==PAGE:29==
28 
 
conditions. [22] As a result, interference can be eliminated, leading to improved spectral 
efficiency. In addition, as multiple users with different radio conditions can be served 
simultaneously, user fairness can be improved. 
 
2.3 The Non-Stand-Alone Architecture 
In the NSA configuration or otherwise called “E-UTRA-NR Dual Connectivity (EN-DC), 
the NR is implemented based on the existing LTE RAN and core network (EPC), thus not 
providing the full set of 5G native services to the users. Yet, consumers will experience 
all the other gains of 5G NR such as lower latency, higher data rates, and increased 
capacity. It should be noted that in the NSA scenario, MNOs might need to upgrade the 
EPC in order to cope with the increased capacity requirements. In any case, the NSA 
migration path will allow MNOs to partly provide the 5G experience considerably faster 
while keeping the cost low, as minor changes and upgrades will be needed in the already 
deployed mobile networks. Thus, MNOs will have the opportunity for a smoother 
gradually transition to a 5G SA solution. 
The 5G NR base stations (en-gNB) are deployed within the existing LTE 
infrastructure and acquire a tight cooperation with the LTE base stations (eNB) through 
the X2 interface. X2 interface will support user and control plane separation, allowing the 
exchange of signaling information and the forwarding of user data. In this scenario, the 
Master Node (MN) is the eNB and the Secondary Node (SN) is the en-gNB. Both LTE and 
5G NR base stations are interconnected to Mobility Management Entities – Service 
Gateways (MME/S-GW), through the S1 interface. [9]  
A User Equipment (UE) will be able to leverage all the features and benefits of 5G 
NR, if the device is compatible with the dual connectivity feature. If this is the case, when 
the UE sends an Attach Request to the network, it will be identified that the device 
supports dual connectivity. That is to say, the UE supports communication with en-gNB 
5G NR base stations. As a result, MME will handle the situation by limiting the 
communication only with the SN / en-gNB, so the user can be benefited from the 5G 
services. Figure 14 represents the overall NSA NR network configuration. Finally, it 
should be noted that both co-sited and non-co-sited E-UTRAN deployments are possible.

2.4 The Stand-Alone Architecture 
The 5G SA architecture refers to the newly designed 5G platform, consisting of the 5GC,

of a critical importance, as 4G networks are not expected to be phased out soon.

2.6 5G/LTE Interworking 
Nowadays, as a huge transition from 4G to 5G networks is underway, major changes are 
expected to take place in the overall architecture of mobile networks. The deployment 
architecture options, as proposed by the 3GPP are multiple and diverse, combining access 
to both RATs, 4G and 5G. Providing mixed solutions, which allow access to both 4G and 
5G seems the logical solution to go for, as LTE devices are not to be phased out soon and 
LTE technology will be indispensable for many years to come. The mixed technology 
nature of deployment cases, two different RATs based on the EPC or 5GC and the diverse 
capabilities of commercial devices, make interoperability a complex situation. A case in 
point is the different Access Stratum (AS) and Non-Access Stratum (NAS) protocols for 
LTE and 5G devices, leading to complicated interworking. 
Regarding the AS protocol, it handles communication among the RAN and the 
UE, exchanging information related to the radio channel, while NAS is responsible for 
the communication between the UE and the core network, containing information related 
to mobility management. [23] Depending on the type of the core, EPC or NGC, a UE can 
make use of the corresponding NAS protocol to communicate with the core. As todays’ 
LTE devices are backwards compatible with UMTS, similarly next generation 5G devices 
==PAGE:38==
37 
 
are expected to support previous technologies, thus being able to support both NGC and 
EPC NAS protocols. However, LTE devices might not be forward compatible, supporting 
5GC NAS protocol. Yet, solution to this problem may be given by upgrading the software 
or hardware on the 5GC, so an LTE device could establish a communication link with the 
core. Nonetheless, an LTE device with DC feature, will be able to make use of the NR as 
a secondary RAT, yet the core network should be 4G based, like in option 3 / 3A. In other 
words, if option 2 is to be deployed, the existing LTE devices might not be able to 
communicate with the NGC due to incompatible NAS protocol. 
A legacy LTE device can support connectivity with the EPC (EPC NAS) with EN-
DC feature, while a 5G device can support connectivity with both EPC and 5GC 
(EPC/5GC NAS). An LTE EN-DC enabled UE always establishes initial communication 
with the EPC, using E-UTRA air interface. A 5G device always establishes initial 
communication with the 5GC, using either E-UTRA or NR air interface. Yet, a 5G device 
can acquire initial communication with EPC, if 5GC is not available. In any case, a UE can 
initiate the appropriate NAS procedure, depending on the capabilities of the device and 
the core configuration. An essential characteristic of 5G devices that will facilitate the 
interoperability in cases of mixed EPC and 5GC deployment scenarios, is the registration 
mode feature. [24] A 5G UE can either operate in single or dual registration mode, 
depending on the network architecture. In single-registration mode, a device can initiate 
either EPC or 5GC NAS procedure, depending on the core configuration. The device can 
only keep one active registration at a time, either at the EPC or the 5GC. In dual-
registration mode, a device can initiate both EPC and NAS procedures at the same time, 
being able to keep both connections active. However, in this case, a device can certainly 
be registered to only one of the two available core networks. As dual-registration mode 
requires interworking among the EPC and 5GC and exchange of information among the 
Home Subscriber Server (HSS) and Unified Data Management (UDM) is essential, a 
commonly used database is installed on the backend of the network, providing a 
communication path between the EPC and the 5GC. [24] An overall representation of a 
single or dual-registration mode scenario, can be seen in figure 21. [24]

3. 5G Split Architecture 
 
5G mobile networks will have considerably high requirements, thus imposing an 
important traffic burden on the transport networks. The functional split concept, which 
has already been known from C-RAN, is being considered as a technique to split the 
functions of a gNB into a CU and a DU, allowing for centralization gains and indeed 
alleviating the traffic burden on the fronthaul transport links.  
The purpose of this chapter is to provide an overview of the 5G NR protocol stack 
along with the proposed functional splits from the 3GPP. Also, an assessment of the 
potential benefits of each split option will be made. [9][25]. It should be noted that the

writing and according to [10], which is the latest release from the 3GPP, 5G protocol stack 
seems to be identical to the LTE protocol stack, yet this might be revised in next releases. 
Also, presented functional splits are subject to changes until 5G protocol stack is finally 
agreed on the next releases. Finally, the chapter will be summarized in the last subsection.

The physical layer receives transport blocks from the MAC layer and each frame 
is attached a Cyclic Redundancy Check (CRC) code. After that, the transport blocks are 
being segmented into smaller block segments and encoded. Next, the encoded blocks are 
being forwarded to rate matching, where the coded bits from each block are rate matched 
for transmission within a specific Time Transmission Interval (TTI). [27] The code words 
are going through scrambling, where each code word has its coded bits inverted. In this 
way, signals can be separated so each one can be received only from the intended device. 
Then, the coded bits are modulated into symbols. The implemented modulation scheme 
affects the amount of reduction on the signal, as modulation order determines the 
number of bits mapped to each symbol. The next processing block receives the modulated 
symbols and maps them into one or more transmission layers, while the symbols on each 
layer are encoded in order to be transmitted on the available antenna ports. After that, 
symbols are mapped into resource elements, thus transforming symbols to subcarriers. 
[26] Resource elements can be further aggregated into resource blocks, while the number 
of available resource blocks for transmission, is being determined by the MAC scheduler. 
==PAGE:42==

[27] Finally, subcarriers are going through beamforming and port expansion processes; 
they’re transformed from frequency domain into IQ symbols in the time domain and a 
Cyclic Prefix (CyP) is attached to prevent Inter Symbol Interference (ISI). [26]

3. The Network Layer 
The network layer is the top layer of the protocol stack where Radio Resource Control 
(RRC) is located. The RRC is responsible for the management of data from the CP, as well 
as the UP. The connection among network layer and the data link layer is based on the 
exchange of radio bearers. [26] The functions that reside in the RRC are related to mobility 
and handovers, CA and Dual Connectivity management, as well as security functions. 
[10] Furthermore, RRC controls the establishment and release of the RRC connection 
between the gNB and the UE. Finally, Non-Access Stratum (NAS) protocol, which 
==PAGE:44==

handles the exchange of signaling data between the AMF and UE, is transferred by the 
RRC. That is to say, RRC transfers NAS related information among NAS and UE and vice 
versa, as well as it broadcasts information into the network about NAS. [28]

3.2.1 Option 1: RRC-PDCP 
Option 1 refers to the split where CU accommodates the RRC, while most of the functions 
such as PDCP, RLC, MAC, PHY and RF are handled by the DU. As, this option allows 
for separation of CP and UP, it can be identified as a potential CP/UP split. This is justified 
by the fact that the RRC located in the CU is responsible for the CP, while PDCP located 
in the DU is responsible for UP.[26] Placing the UP in the DU can be proven beneficial, 
especially for delay-sensitive applications and use cases requiring low latencies, where 
processing and storage of user data should take place at the edge of the network. As most 
functions are executed locally in the DU, it’s been anticipated that the traffic burden on 
the fronthaul link will be relatively low and variable, depending on the user load. On the 
downside, by splitting the PDCP and RRC, a challenge might arise on how to effectively 
secure the interface without affecting the overall performance of this split option.[9] This 
split is similar to LTE DC option 1A [12]. 
 
3.2.2 Option 2: PDCP-RLC 
Option 2 refers to the split where RRC and PDPC functions have been assigned to the 
CU, while placing RLC, MAC, PHY and RF in the DU. As a result, the exchanged data in 
the fronthaul link are in the form of PDCP PDUs. This split scenario facilitates traffic 
aggregation and management from eNBs and gNBs. [9] As this split option has already 
been standardized for LTE DC, the 3GPP has already chosen option 2 for further study 
in Release 15, identifying the fronthaul link among CU and DU, as F1 interface in NR. 
==PAGE:46==

Furthermore, separation of CP and UP can be achieved, using the E1 interface. As a result, 
the separation of CP and UP can bring additional benefits by assigning CP/UP operations 
to the most optimized location, while configuration of UP and CP capacity becomes more 
flexible, depending on the use case. The deployment scenarios being identified by the 
3GPP, are the following: [10] 
1. Centralized CP and UP: Can be deployed as one common entity or separate ones. 
CP handles one or several DUs, while UP handles the user traffic in DC scenarios. 
2. Distributed CP and centralized UP: CP can be deployed close to a DU and it’s 
capable of handling one or multiple DUs in a local level. UP handles the user 
traffic in DC scenarios. 
3. Centralized CP and distributed UP: CP is being deployed centrally, so operation 
of several DUs can be achieved. UP is deployed close to a DU.

3.2.3 Option 3: Intra RLC 
This split option separates the RLC functional block into two separate ones, the High-
RLC and the Low-RLC sub-block. In this scenario, RRC, PDCP and High-RLC are 
allocated in the CU. Furthermore, ARQ operations are assigned to the CU. Low-RLC 
operations such as aggregation and segmentation, as well as MAC, PHY and RF are 
allocated at the DU. In other words, RLC functions can be executed at the High-RLC block 
in the CU, while segmentation can be performed at the Low-RLC block in the DU. In such 
a scenario, the benefits are multiple such as centralization of aggregated traffic from eNBs 
and gNBs, higher centralization gains due to centralized ARQ, enhanced protection over 
CP signaling data as well as support for higher number of devices in connected mode at 
the DU. [9] Centralization gains of option 3 are higher compared to option 1 and 2 and 
the traffic burden on the fronthaul link is low to modest, depending on the use.  
Option 3 has a variation referred as 3-2 or split based on Tx RLC and Rx RLC, where the 
High-RLC is the receiving side, thus consisting of receiving entities, while the Low-RLC 
is the transmitting side, consisting of transmission entities. The Tx RLC receives RLC 
SDUs from PDCP and forward the packets to the MAC block, while RLC assembles the 
MAC SDUs based on the requested format from the MAC block. The Rx RLC receives the 
RLC PDUs from the MAC block and submits the packets to Rx RLC. Similar to option 3, 
option 3-2 facilitates traffic aggregation from NR and E-UTRA, as well as, this option 
imposes more relaxed requirements on the transport network, as MAC forwards RLC 
PDUs as whole packet to RLC instead of having RLC to send RLC SDUs to PDCP. 
However, the downside of this option is that flow control is required in the CU and Rx 
RLC as well as, in the DU and Tx RLC, hence the implementation of two buffers is a 
prerequisite. [9] 
 
3.2.4 Option 4: RLC-MAC 
In this split scenario, separation of RLC and MAC is achieved by placing the RRC, PDCP 
and RLC functions in the CU, while assigning the MAC, PHY and RF to the DU. In this 
split option, the data exchanged through the fronthaul interface are in the form of RLC 
PDUs. As, no benefits are expected from this split option, there are no further studies 
associated with option 4. Yet, option 4 might be further investigated in later release. [9] 
==PAGE:48==
47 
 
3.2.5 Option 5: Intra MAC 
Option 5 refers to the split, where RRC, PDCP and the High-MAC sublayer are located 
in the CU, while Low-MAC sublayer, PHY and RF are located in DU. Therefore, MAC 
related operations are either executed in the CU or in the DU, or in both locations. In this 
option, centralized scheduling decisions can be performed by the High-MAC sublayer, 
while this sublayer keeps track and controls several Low-MAC sublayers. In addition, 
ICIC and CoMP are performed in the High-MAC sublayer. Time sensitive operations 
such as HARQ are performed in the Low-MAC sublayer. [10] The benefits of this split 
option are multiple such as support for traffic aggregation among gNBs and eNBs, well-
balanced traffic exchange on the fronthaul transport network, relaxed latency 
requirements and optimized scheduling of interference mitigation techniques. However, 
this option requires a more complex interface among CU and DU, as well as, additional 
delays on the fronthaul might be the case due to scheduling decisions among CU and 
DU. [9] 
 
3.2.6 Option 6: MAC-PHY 
Split option 6 places the MAC, RLC, PDCP and RRC to the CU, while keeping the physical 
layer and the RF in the DU. The exchanged data through the fronthaul link are in the 
form of transport blocks, thus not putting a huge bandwidth burden on the fronthaul, as 
the signal has not gone through heavy processing yet. [26] When the payload is 
forwarded to the physical layer, the signal is going through heavy processing such as 
modulation, layer mapping, RE mapper and iFFT, thus increasing the overall size of the 
signal.  This option allows for multiple gains such as traffic aggregation and management 
from eNBs and gNBs, centralization of scheduling operations, as well as Joint 
Transmission (JT) capabilities. However, it should be noted that this split option requires 
tight synchronization among the operations of the MAC and PHY layers. The information 
exchanged among the CU and the DU are related to system configuration and scheduling 
such as modulation and coding, antenna configuration, layers mapping, beamforming, 
resource block allocation, etc. [9]

3.2.8 Option 8: RF-PHY 
Split option 8 refers to the traditional split also known as CPRI, separating the PHY from 
the RF. As almost all functions are allocated to the CU, the centralization gains are 
multiple, facilitating operations such as CoMP and MIMO. In this split option, the 
exchanged data between CU and DU are in the form of IQ samples transferred by CPRI 
protocol, which is the most commonly used. [26] Also, this option allows for aggregation 
and management of traffic among eNBs and gNBs. Furthermore, separation of PHY from 
RF allows for reusability of RF parts to be leveraged from different RATs. However, this 
split option puts a huge burden on the fronthaul transport link, leading to high costs, 
high resource utilization, etc. [9] A case in point, is that the fronthaul bitrate in this option 
is not depended on the cell / users load, thus the load on the fronthaul network is constant 
==PAGE:52==
51 
 
and considerably high. [26] All in all, it could be said that this split option seems an 
unfeasible choice for 5G, as the requirements on the fronthaul interface scale with the 
number of antennas, thus requiring a huge capacity fronthaul network, especially when 
massive MIMO antenna systems are used.[26] 
 
3.3 Chapter summary 
Overall, choosing the ideal split option for every use case might be proven a complicated 
process. This is justified by the fact that the split options with the highest centralization 
gains, they impose the highest bitrates on the fronthaul links. As a result, it can be clearly 
seen that there is no ‘golden’ split option, providing everything at once, both high 
centralization benefits and low fronthaul requirements. Of course, it should be mentioned 
that all functional splits may be feasible in theory, but when it comes to implementing 
one of these, MNOs have to consider several factors. Such factors might be the use case 
and the capabilities of the already deployed network infrastructure (e.g. capacity of 
optical fibers) to ensure the feasibility of the chosen split option. Taking into 
consideration the latest work from the 3GPP at [9] and [25], option 2 is mostly considered 
for a higher layer split, while for a lower layer split, options 6 and 7 are mainly 
considered. Nevertheless, the standardization process is still ongoing and split options 
might be revised in next releases.

4. Split Option Impact on the Fronthaul Transport Network 
 
A functional split can significantly affect the bitrate requirements on the fronthaul 
transport network, as it determines the amount of functions executed either on CU or 
DU. Indeed, it should be taken for granted that 5G networks are expected to have 
considerable higher requirements compared to LTE. 
The goal of this chapter is to provide an insight on the fronthaul bitrate 
requirements, considering worst case scenarios for both LTE and 5G networks. In 
addition, a comparison will be made among LTE and 5G results, so as to acquire a better 
understanding, in what extent 5G will impose higher requirements on the fronthaul 
transport networks compared to LTE. Furthermore, an overview of the aggregation 
concept will be given, as well as, a capacity planning and dimensioning case will be 
examined. The functional splits considered in this chapter are: options 2, 6, 7-1, 7-2 and 
7-3. The focus will be only kept on these 5 functional splits, as these are the mostly 
considered from the 3GPP, according to [10] and [25], thus they’re most likely to be 
standardized or implemented. 
Finally, it should be stated that functional splits are under extensive research and 
nothing has been standardized by the time of writing, thus everything is still under 
discussion. That is to say, that the formulas used for calculations and the results presented 
in this chapter might have a small or huge deviation from the finally agreed ones in future 
works, as well as, they might differ from actual implementations. The work on this 
chapter follows the 3GPP work on the functional splits area. 
 
4.1 Motivation 
After 3 semesters and as I had gone through several courses related to mobile networks, 
it’s been crystal clear to me that coverage and capacity are the two most important 
prerequisites that every mobile network should comply with. In order to ensure the 
required coverage and capacity in a specific area, careful planning and dimensioning is 
required, so as to consider all the necessary factors before deploying or upgrading a 
network infrastructure. Yet, this work is mostly focused on the capacity aspect and not 
the coverage.  
==PAGE:54==
53 
 
Deployment of new network equipment can be considerably cost effective and 
time-consuming for any MNO; hence optimal operation of the existing infrastructure 
should be ensured. An example of a costly network upgrade that could contribute in the 
overall capacity, is the deployment of optical fiber. Deployment of optical fibers would 
require an enormous cost, including the purchase of optical fibers, labor, civil work, 
material costs and many other possible expenses. As a result, such a scenario might not 
be feasible or even viable for an MNO. In this scenario, the MNO should consider what 
can be done in any aspect, prior to making any additional expenses.  
The implementation of a functional split on the fronthaul transport network, can 
affect the required capacity of the links, hence it seems an interesting area for research in 
order to acquire what would be the required bitrates under worst circumstances, so as a 
MNO could know, if its network is capable of coping with these requirements. Indeed, 
this work is theoretical based, while the data used are mostly from work under study 
from the 3GPP. As a result, the estimations might have a deviation from reality, though 
an examination of the results is worth it anyway.

4.4 Fronthaul Aggregation 
In a real deployment scenario, a CU is expected to support multiple DUs, hence multiple 
fronthaul data streams will be aggregated into a single CU. In other words, the traffic of 
all the fronthaul links will be aggregated into a single backhaul link. The required 
capacity on the fronthaul links directly affects the capacity of the backhaul link. 
Therefore, it could be said that, it’s not enough to only consider the fronthaul links in the 
dimensioning process, but the backhaul should be taken into consideration as well. The 
required capacity should be ensured in both fronthaul and backhaul, but especially in the 
backhaul, as it should never be bottleneck. 
 
Backhaul capacity can be costly, thus using statistical multiplexing seems a way to 
go, as a countermeasure to reduce the required capacity of the link. The implementation 
of a statistical multiplexing or aggregation method would mean that the required 
capacity on the backhaul link would be less than the sum of bitrates from each one 
fronthaul link.[34] As a result, under or over dimensioning can be avoided in some extent,

All in all, the main concept is to aggregate two areas where the requirements are 
almost equal to the needs of a single area, hence imposing almost the same burden on the 
aggregation link. Otherwise, if we would aggregate two areas with both having high 
needs and assigning the 3rd area to another aggregation point, then the split of traffic 
would not have been well-balanced. That is to say, the requirements for capacity in one 
aggregation point would be considerably higher, while the requirements on the other 
point would be low. As a result, one point would require huge capacity, compared to the 
other. In our case above, as the residential area would be aggregated in the 2nd point, it 
could be combined with DUs from another area nearby (e.g. nightclubs district) where 
this area has high traffic from 1:00 to 6:00, while almost no traffic during the other hours 
of the day. In this way, the areas aggregated in the 2nd point would have different peaking 
times, as well as, different traffic patterns, hence achieving maximum aggregations gains, 
similarly to the 1st aggregation point.  
The overall conclusion to be drawn throughout the above results is that the choice of cells 
to be aggregated, as well as, the used aggregation method has a huge impact on the 
capacity of the aggregation link. We noticed that having multiple cells peaking at the 
same time in a single aggregation point creates a considerable growth in the bitrates on 
the aggregation link. On the other hand, having cells with high peak-offset can 
significantly relax the requirements on the aggregation link. Another measurement in 
order to relax the capacity needs is to aggregate cells in multiple aggregation points, so 
as to achieve maximum aggregation point. This would happen if the cells to be 
aggregated were following different traffic patterns. As mobile traffic and the number of 
connected devices grow rapidly, it becomes apparent that all of the above-mentioned 
parameters should be taken into consideration in the planning and dimensioning process, 
so as to efficiently and effectively cope with the growing traffic and needs. 
 
4.5 Chapter summary 
This chapter is focused on the impact of the chosen functional split on the fronthaul 
transport network. Through a case study, the required bitrates on the fronthaul are 
examined, assuming worst-case scenarios. The goal of this case study was to present the 
requirements of the 5G in the fronthaul and compare them with the 4G ones. In addition, 
==PAGE:77==

through a case study, through a case study, the aggregation of different traffic patterns 
and the impact on the aggregation link is examined. 
Going through the results of this chapter it can be concluded that 5G requirements will 
significantly exceed those of 4G. Higher layer options such as 2 and 6 seem to have 
relaxed requirements compared to those on lower layers such as 7-2 and 7-3. Therefore, 
a high difference on the fronthaul bitrate is expected, when implementing different 
functional split options. Furthermore, when aggregating multiple DUs deployed in areas 
with different traffic patterns, the bitrates on the aggregation link depends on the traffic 
patterns, the peaking times, as well as, the number of aggregation points. In general, the 
peak-offset, or the peaking times among the different patterns can considerably affect the 
total achieved bitrate on the aggregation link. It’s been realized that for some peak-offsets, 
high gains were achieved while for others, the gains were lowered.

5. Conclusion 
 
The purpose of this thesis was to provide a well-rounded overview about the upcoming 
5G system, through presenting the SA and NSA proposed architectures, as well as, the 
main technologies that will accompany the 5G system. In addition, another point of this 
report was to examine the impact of a functional split into the 5G fronthaul. 
From a logical point of view, deployment of 5G will begin with NSA architectures, so as 
to achieve a smoother transition to a fully 5G SA architecture later, while keeping LTE 
connectivity. Depending on the already deployed infrastructure, MNOs will possibly go 
for the more feasible and viable one, hence all the architectures presented in this report 
might not be finally considered. The deployment of 5G will be accompanied with a bunch 
of new technologies that will fulfill the 5G vision. 
The functional split concept seems to be tightly connected with the 5G deployment, as it 
will allow for centralization, while ensuring the required bitrates on the fronthaul 
transport network. A functional split can affect the required capacity of the fronthaul. As 
proposed from the 3GPP and through the examination on this report, options 2 and 6 
seem to demand the least resources while options 7-2 and 7-3 have higher requirements. 
Indeed, this outcome is based on a theoretical study, as well as, many of the input data 
used for this study is still under discussion from the 3GPP. 
Finally, it’s been realized that backhaul dimensioning is so important as the fronthaul 
dimensioning. The fronthaul directly affects the required capacity of the backhaul, 
depending on the number of cells to be aggregated, the traffic of the cells and the 
implemented functional split. When it comes to a real deployment scenario where several 
DUs with different traffic patterns will have to be aggregated, the optimal solution should 
be found in order to ensure the necessary backhaul capacity while keeping the costs in a 
viable level.

5.1 Future Work 
As this theoretical study mostly follows the work by the 3GPP, it would be interesting to 
examine what other alliances such as NGMN and Small Cell Forum have proposed, so as 
to compare the architectures of the functional splits from the 3 alliances, as well as, 
investigate potential deviations in the estimated fronthaul bitrates. Also, instead of 
considering worst case scenarios, average case scenarios might be considered in order to 
present more realistic results, as the presented bitrates might never be seen in a real 
network. Finally, as the estimations on this work is based on information that is under 
study and nothing has been standardized, it would be interesting in the future to compare 
the finally agreed splits and the corresponding bitrates with these on this work.
