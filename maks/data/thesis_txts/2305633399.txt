 Preface
The project set out to investigate the possibility and performance of an 
embedded pipe heating system in a window bay spandrel in combination with a 
ventilation window. The solution should be an alternative to mechanical 
balanced ventilation system and a conventional convector. The ventilation 
window was/is intended to be physically combined with a heating unit below, 
which allows for additional preheating of ventilation air when the window itself 
does not provide sufficient preheating.  
Morten Ørsager had the idea for this project, which arose in the context of the 
refurbishment project of Højstrupparken. This project was supposed to 
investigate the development and performance of a decentralized, combined, 
heating and ventilation unit based on preliminary studies performed for a study 
case of Højstrupparken. 
During the course of this thesis, some shortcomings in the preliminary project 
were discovered. More specifically the ventilation windows were implemented 
incorrectly in IESVE (the dynamic building simulation tool used for this 
project). Implementing the ventilation window in a dynamic simulation turned 
out to be a more complicated task than expected, due to the main literature 
describing the implementation in IESVE not being very precise in their 
description. With assistance from people involved in that same project (Laustsen 
et al. 2014), it is the believe that this thesis has managed to implement the 
ventilation window an accurate and effective way to determine the potential 
energy savings caused by the ventilation window. The limitations of the 
fundamental and further developed method used in this project is thoroughly 
analysed and discussed in relation to further improvements.  
As a result, the systems’ separate performance is evaluated in order to assess the 
possibilities of a combined prefab unit without dealing with the actual 
connection between the two systems.

 Abstract
Refurbishment project will reduce the energy consumption for operation of 
buildings and help clear the path towards a fossil fuel free society. But extensive 
energy renovations might not always be the only answer from a holistic point of 
view. The indoor climate is equally important and must be investigated as part of 
the performance requirements set out for a renovation. If the indoor climate is 
neglected, overheating or bad indoor air quality can easily become an issue. With 
architecture considered as part of the holistic refurbishment approach, the 
flexibility for certain refurbishment solutions are limited considering the 
buildings architectural preservability.  
The case study is based on a 1940’s brick building located in Odense, Denmark; 
considered due to its preservability and possibilities for integrated technical 
solutions in the main renovation components.  
The performance of ventilation windows and embedded pipe heating systems 
(EPHS), as alternatives to conventional convectors and mechanically balanced 
ventilation systems, has been investigated to explore the possibility of a 
combined refurbishment solution with ventilation and heating, possibly as a 
prefabricated unit.  
The investigations are performed and analysed in regard to energy performance, 
indoor climate, and architectural impact, to assess the solutions from a holistic 
point of as possible within the time frame of the project.  
Using the intended refurbishment proposal with ventilation window, the case 
study can be energy renovated to Energy Class 2, as defined in the Danish 
Building Regulations, with a total energy consumption of 101 kWh/m2. That 
means an energy saving of 54.1 % compared to the existing conditions of 220 
kWh/m2 without renovating the east and west facing brick facades, the major 
part of the energy savings is caused just by replacing the existing windows. 
The ventilation windows reduce energy performance for heating with 4.3 % for 
the case study, compared to normal windows with similar properties. They also 
include the preheating feature, which potentially will improve the thermal indoor 
environment in vicinity to the air supply valves compared to standard fresh air 
valves with no preheating. The cooling aspect of the ventilation window has not 
been assessed within the time frame of the project. 
Integrating EPHS in a combined unit while still allowing for prefabrication 
seems unlikely given the results presented in this report. The EPHS relies on 
large surface heating areas, even with additional internal insulation and 
maximum allowable capacities for EPHS, the heated surfaces cannot be 
arranged within the boundaries of the prefabricated unit.  
The alternative is a convector integrated in the spandrel (brystning) below the 
window. The convector has sufficient heating capacity for the refurbished 
conditions, but might be a compromise of the aesthetics.

 INTRODUCTION
This chapter includes a description of the project 
scope, focused on refurbishment potential an energy 
savings. The introduction leads up to the problem 
statement for this project. 
 Project scope
Part of the ambitious climate strategy proposed by the previous Danish 
government, was to completely out-phase the use of fossil fuels by 2050; this 
includes buildings energy supply, industry, and transportation. A milestone was 
to out-phase all fossil fuel energy consumed within the building sector for 
heating before 2030. (Energistyrelsen 2012) 
To meet the goals, a conversion to energy sources that relies on sustainable 
needs must be prioritised e.g. wind, thermal, hydro and solar power supplied 
with incineration of renewable fuels, in the beginning, to cover peak heat loads. 
In addition, the energy demand for existing buildings need to be reduced 
through refurbishment.  
The awareness of energy consumption and CO2 emissions has increased during 
recent years. Newer buildings are subjects to dynamic building simulations to 
ensure good energy performance and indoor climate, including thermal comfort 
(Trafik- og Byggestyrelsen 2016); this was not the case half a century ago. In 
addition, new buildings are often demanded to meet specific requirements from 
EN standards by clients. 
Older buildings in general have a high heating energy consumption since thermal 
insulation was not prioritised at the same level as it is today; this can be reflected 
in the architectural expressions. These buildings symbolize a part of our past and 
culture, which is worth maintaining to preserve our architectural history. So how 
can these buildings be refurbished for future standards of energy performance 
while preserving the architectural values? 
Improved energy performance and certain styles of architecture can be 
challenging to combine. Some architectural features are hard to replicate using 
modern components to improve energy performance and thermal indoor 
environment. Especially thin walls and large glass facades are factors that can 
influence the building’s robustness to external conditions like solar gains and 
transmission. When performing refurbishment projects, some buildings will face 
new problems with air quality and overheating if energy savings are the only goal 
of the renovation. The economic aspect of energy renovations are the main 
concern of house owners, with improved thermal comfort and indoor climate as 
2nd and 3rd place in importance (Knudsen & Foldbjerg 2014).

Older buildings frequently have problems with thermal discomfort caused by 
technical issues e.g. lack of insulation, non-ideal positioning of heating units, 
large infiltration rate and moisture problems.  
How these problems are dealt with, varies depending on the building’s 
preservability, as usually effective renovation methods tend to alter the 
expression more, unless the refurbishment strategy is thoroughly investigated. At 
this point project economy will be the all-determining factor of whether the 
architecture is preserved respectfully to the original state.  
When research and development recourses are available and dedicated towards 
refurbishment, the quality of historical and architectural building refurbishment 
could potentially reach new levels; architectural values can be preserved and the 
option of more extensive energy renovation will contribute to lowering CO2 
emissions and energy consumption, easing the transition to fossil free fuel 
society.  
 Target
The main target is to develop and research alternative refurbishment solutions 
with Højstrupparken as a case study. Proposals must respect architectural values, 
while providing a better energy performance for the building. Alternative heating 
and ventilation systems are investigated and compared to conventional solutions. 
The refurbishment of Højstrupparken is desired to fulfil renovation class 1 or 2 
according to Danish Building Regulations of 2015.  
The project is carried out with a holistic approach, considering energy 
consumption, indoor climate and architecture as main parameters to evaluate the 
refurbishment solutions.

 Problem statement
Can refurbishment design solutions be improved by decentralised heating 
and ventilation systems, taking into account architectural values are 
important, given a buildings preservability, while improving the thermal 
indoor environment and energy performance? 
Can the refurbishment of Højstrupparken be improved to accommodate the 
original refurbishment proposal by Erik Møller Arkitekter, by implementing an

Will an EPHS integrated in the spandrel (brystning) below the window, in the

environment?  


properties?  
 
Can the ventilation air be preheated sufficiently to improve thermal indoor 
environment the air supply zone?  
What are the potential energy savings in indoor climate for the refurbishment

account the decentralised solutions?

 Tools
A short introduction of the tools used for investigations 2.2.1
 IESVE, dynamic building simulation tool
IESVE is a dynamic building simulation tools that allows for accurate modelling 
of building services constructions to assess indoor climate and energy 
consumption, based on weather data for specific locations. It is developed by 
Integrated Environmental Solutions. 
IESVE was chosen over other dynamic simulation tools like IDA-ICE and Bsim 
since energy performance of the ventilation windows were previously 
accomplished in IES-VE in the main literature used for this thesis.  2.2.2
 Be15, energy calculation tool
Be15 is the newest version of Be10 released in the beginning of 2016. It is the 
most commonly used energy calculation tool in Denmark and is developed by 
SBi (Statens Byggeforskningsinstitut). The tool has a spreadsheet based interface, 
which gives you instant feedback whenever input data is changed. Be15 is used 
to calculate the energy consumption for new building as documentation that 
current energy requirements in Danish Building Regulations can be fulfilled. The 
calculations are performed mainly in relation to DS 418, with SBi 213 as 
recommended input data.

 Architectural preservability
Buildings in Højstrupparken symbolize, among other buildings from the 1940’s 
and 1950’s, the transition from traditional building methods to industrialised 
building elements. After 2nd world war ended in 1945, the political tendencies led 
towards establishing a welfare society by, among other things, promoting quality

workers using the traditional building methods (Almennet & Realdania 2015).

Højstrupparken is a combination of traditional Danish architecture built in 
bricks, combined with the core of Danish functionalism: simple geometry 
dictated by function. By adding balconies, window bays and recreational areas 
outside, a synergy between indoor and outdoor was established. It was desired to

history. Led by great architects, who combined the best from traditional building 
styles with functionalism as one of the strongest trades in Danish design.  
Some of these buildings are considered architectural symbols of a highly refined 
building method driven by ideals for a better standard of living for everyone by 
promoting qualities that were not available during the time of 2nd world war.  
Many of these buildings are facing problems like significant thermal bridges, and 
poorly insulated constructions in risk of mould problems, which could 
eventually make them uninhabitable. A refurbishment is in order.

4. The ventilation window 
 
 
Master Thesis – DTU

 THE VENTILATION WINDOW
The ventilation window offers a ventilation solution as 
well as improved energy performance compared to a 
conventional window. The concept of the ventilation 
window is presented. Literature about the ventilation 
window is discussed thermal properties are 
investigated. 
 Concept and operation
The ventilation window system is designed to deliver preheated ventilation air 
through the window. The preheating is caused by heat loss through the inner 
glazing and solar radiation when available. The system could be considered an 
alternative to conventional ventilation systems in situations where an air supply 
method is required, but space for installation is limited.  
By eliminating the need of supply ducts, ventilation windows can save 
installation cost for of the air supply system. The producers of the ventilation 
window, Ventilationsvinduet, claims a 25 % energy reduction for heating and 
cooling, as well as improved indoor climate by providing fresh air supply at all 
times (Ventilationsvinduet ApS 2015). The window is passively operated by 
thermostatic valves that open and close depending on the outdoor and supply air 
temperature. By minimizing or redirecting the airflow the ventilation window 
allows for three different modes of operation explained in 4.1.2.

 4.1.2
 Passive operation bythermostatic wax valves
The passive version of the ventilation windows from ClimaWin Techniq are 
operated using thermostatic valves for adjusting the airflow. The thermostatic 
valves are regulated by pistons filled with a wax composition that expands or 
contracts depending on the surrounding temperature. 4.1.3
 Modes of operation
 
Figure 4-2: Modes of operation for the ventilation window (Ventilationsvinduet ApS 2015) 4.1.3.1
 Normal mode
The normal mode is the main feature of the ventilation window; the preheating 
function. Outdoor air is supplied from the bottom of the window frame and 
into the ventilated gap for preheating; then supplied into the room from the top 
of the window frame by air supply valves. One air supply valve is dimensioned 
for 4 l/s air flow corresponding to 14.4 m3/h; more valves can be installed in 
one window (Ventilationsvinduet ApS 2015).  4.1.3.2
 Minimum flow mode
The minimum flow mode is active when the preheating temperature is so low 
that the supplied air could cause thermal discomfort. To prevent that, the air 
flow rate is reduced by a thermostatic wax valve. The minimum ventilation flow 
rate is reduced to 40 % of the normal mode i.e. 1.6 l/s, allowing extended time 
for preheating. The natural ventilation flow rate required by Building 
Regulations should be calculated in consideration to the minimum flow, to 
ensure sufficient air flow rates at all times.

 4.1.3.3
 Self-cooling mode (by-pass)
The self-cooling mode is active when the outdoor temperature is above 23 oC. 
By allowing outdoor air to circulate the preheating gap without supplying the 
heated air to the room the glazing can be cooled; reducing heat transmission to 
the building. Meanwhile air is supplied directly from the outside, bypassing the 
preheating gap through a valve in the upper part of the window.  4.1.4
 Air supply requirements and flow rate
As described, the airflow through the ventilation window is regulated by 
thermostatic wax valves. The airflow will be reduced if the outdoor temperature 
is too cold for sufficient preheating of the ventilation air. This will generally 
occur in winter, when the outdoor temperature is at its lowest. This will reduce 
the ventilation rate to the zones, thereby increasing CO2 concentration over 
time. To accommodate the Building Regulations, air supply valves must be 
installed in the ventilation windows in relation to the minimum air flow mode 
(see Figure 4-2). In minimum flow the windows must be able to supply 0.3 l/(s 
m2) altogether. 4.1.5
 Exhaust ventilation to maintain negative pressure
For the ventilation window to operate sufficiently, it is required that exhaust 
ventilation is installed. The exhaust ventilation is responsible for providing a 
negative pressure in rooms with ventilation windows, which will draw the 
outdoor air through the ventilated gap for preheating. If the negative pressure is 
not maintained indoor air might find its way to the ventilated gap with risk of 
condensation. To guarantee a variable flow rate between minimum and normal 
flow mode, the exhaust ventilation rate must be increased as well.  4.1.6

 Thermal properties of the ventilation window
Assuming a constant air flow through the ventilated gap can be achieved, the 
convective heat loss can be reduced significantly, leaving mostly the radiation 
heat loss between the inner and outer glazing of the ventilated gap.  
The reason is found in the air temperature throughout the ventilated gap; the air 
is heated more significantly along the inner glazing and only little along the outer 
glazing. This makes the convective heat loss from the gap to the outer glazing 
less significant due to a smaller temperature difference, see Figure 4-5. 
This statement is based on the findings in ‘ Thermal Performance of Air Flow 
Windows’ (Raffnsøe 2007), which describes the temperature profile and 
convective heat flux for the ventilated gap depending on the flow rate and solar 
irradiation.  4.2.1
 Definition of the Effective U-value (Ueff)
The effective U-value (Ueff) is describing the heat loss of a ventilation 
window in consideration to a reduced convective heat loss and impairing 
of the actual U-value, caused by air flow through the ventilated gap.  
By placing a boundary in the outer glazing, the heat loss from this boundary can 
be described as Ueff (See Figure 4-5). The effective U-value represents the U-
value for the ventilation window on equal terms with a traditional U-value, but it 
it’s properties are depending flow rate through the ventilated gap, solar 
irradiance and heat transfer to the ventilated gap (at a significantly higher level 
that a normal window)

 4.2.2
 Heat recovery
The ventilation window offers sort of a heat recovery for ventilation air. The 
main difference between the ‘heat recovery’ for the ventilation window, and for 
heat exchanger in an air handling unit (AHU), is that preheating in the 
ventilation window is taking place because of intentional heat loss through the 
inner glazing to the ventilated gap, the heat exchanger is transferring energy 
from exhaust air and transferring it to the supply air, which otherwise would be 
lost. 
The fact that the ventilation window has a ventilated gap, will increase the heat 
transfer through the inner glazing. However, due to the air flow through the gap, 
some of this heat loss will be recovered as preheating in the supply air.  4.2.3
 Calculation of performance with dynamic properties
ClimaWin has developed their own tool for to calculate energy savings for 
cooling and heating. ClimaWin’s tool however, is not designed to evaluate 
indoor climate dynamically. Due to the dynamic properties of the ventilation 
window it is complicated to implement in standard dynamic building simulation 
tools.   
In this project, a method to implement the ventilation window in IESVE is 
further developed. The method is based on expressions from WIS, WinVent and 
guarded hotbox measurements (Laustsen et al. 2014). The method is used to 
estimate the air supply temperature of the ventilation window, depending on 
solar irradiation and flow rate through the ventilated gap. The procedure for 
generating the temperature profile is explained in chapter 7. 
 Literature study: Energy performance of ventilation windows
The investigations performed in this project is based on the 1+2 composition 
due to its performance being more well documented and available. The 
uncertainties about condensation on the interior side of the 2+1 window during 
cold outdoor conditions, remains the main reason why more the 1+2 
composition has been more investigated.  
Investigations show that energy performance is improved slightly; according to 
(Laustsen et al. 2014), comparing ventilation windows with 3-layer windows with 
fresh air valves, both with glazing U-value of 0.8 W/(m2K) and exhaust 
ventilation, is additional 7 %. Using the 3-layer windows and a balanced 
mechanical ventilation system with a heat exchanger efficiency of 75 %, the 
energy savings are 19 % compared to exhaust ventilation and 3-layer windows 
with fresh air valves. This means a decent energy saving potential for the 
ventilation windows, but the heat exchanger is still superior. The investigation is 
based on a building case with only south facing windows.

The gt( ) function is a fuzzy ‘greater than’ used in IESVE to avoid ON/OFF 
behaviour, i.e. constant opening and closing of windows. It defines a 
proportional band variating linearly between 0 and 1, the bandwidth is 5 oC. Dry 
resultant temperature (tdr) also known as the operative temperature is the sensed 
variable. The profile allows window opening to begin at 23 oC and full opening 
at 28 oC. Night ventilation is assumed to be an option whenever the outdoor 
temperature (to) is above 15 oC and indoor temperature is above 23 oC.  
The ramp( ) function allows specific return values, in this case 0.2 and 1, as 
opposed to the gt( ) function only allowing variation between 0 and 1. Since a 
complete window closing based on the wind speed solely is not desired, a ramp 
function is chosen for this purpose.  If the wind speed exceeds 5 m/s a gradual 
reduction will be multiplied to the temperature based window opening, gt( ). At 
10 m/s the window opening will be reduced to 20 % of the temperature based 
opening. If reduced window opening degree causes the indoor temperature to 
increase the gt( ) part will compensate by returning a higher opening value. At 28

Domestic hot water consumption is 250 l/(m2 year) for an average residential

 Discussion ofpart 66.6.1
 Definition of model boundary conditionsfor average apartment
The boundary conditions for apartment Type 1 and 2, will not completely 
represent any of the apartments in the case building. They are an average 
representation including the heat losses from roof and ground slab distributed 
between 8 similar sections of the multi-storey building.  
The model is setup in a way that energy consumption will be fairly accurate for 
the whole building; it is important to recognise that orientation influences solar 
gains since the case building and apartment is facing South. To consider this 
observation, North facing apartments will be taken into by switching the 
orientation of the South facing windows in Be15.  
Heat load calculations for dimensioning the heating system capacity in IESVE 
do not include solar gains, and will therefore NOT be orientation specific. 
However, due to the assumption of an average heat loss for the case apartments, 
average heating system capacities have been found. This must be addressed 
before choosing an actual heating system for critical apartments additional 
externally facing boundaries.  
It is estimated that using a South facing apartment represents a more critical 
indoor climate in terms of overheating; indoor climate simulations will be 
performed using south facing conditions.  6.6.2
 Natural ventilation
Natural ventilation (including infiltration) for refurbished conditions are 
assumed to be provided by fresh air valves. If the same assumption is used for 
ventilations windows (that both natural ventilation and infiltration are provided 
by the supply air valves in the ventilation windows) the benefits of preheating 
would be included in a way that assumes complete air tightening. This 
assumption would be too ideal. Therefore, a basic infiltration rate of 0.13 l/(s 
m2) will be included as non-preheated infiltration; as it is the case for 
mechanically balanced ventilation systems (Energistyrelsen 2015). The remaining 
0.17 l/(s m2) will be supplied through the ventilation windows to achieve a total 
fresh air supply of 0.3 l/(s m2) in accordance with the Building Regulations.

 6.6.3
 Window opening inMacroFlo
Assumptions regarding window opening can easily be ideal due fact that it 
requires occupants to react based on thermal sensation.  
The biggest concern is one specific scenario; the windows have been closed 
during a summer day, and the occupants arrive home in the afternoon to find 
the apartment overheated; first thing occupants do is to open the windows. If 
the outdoor temperature is low enough and the current wind speed can provide 
sufficient natural ventilation, the air temperature can quickly be reduced; 
operative temperature will take longer since it depends on the radiant 
temperature from surfaces, which will take longer to cool, especially if the 
apartment has been overheated by a significant solar gain.  
This scenario could result in more hours of overheating while occupants are 
present compared to continuous window opening, preventively avoiding 
overheating. Justifying window opening in any case is complicated unless the 
control is mechanical and based on sensors.  
To compensate for an ideal window opening pattern, the windows will not be 
fully opened until the operative temperature exceeds 28 oC; this is outside the 
acceptable range since Building Regulations allow only indoor temperature in 
residential buildings to exceed 28 oC for 25 hours per year and 27 oC for 100 
hours, 6.2 stk. 1 (Trafik- og Byggestyrelsen 2016). 
To reduce high natural ventilation flow rates and reduce the risk of draught, two 
measures was taken.  
 
Only one-sided ventilation is performed, not allowing cross-flow 
ventilation. 
 
A wind speed based window opening reduction has been introduced to 
the window, to avoid peak flow rates caused by high wind speeds. 6.6.3.1
 Side note on occupants’ behaviour and window opening
Occupant presence and behaviour is a complicated topic in the subject of 
building simulation. The biggest challenge is unpredictability of occupant 
behaviour, which tends to be oversimplified in the context of dynamic building 
simulation. Even the presence of occupants varies greatly between buildings of 
same type, and can have a significant impact on the energy consumption. 
Altogether, making standardisation of deterministic schedules for dynamic 
building simulations complicated (Kirk & Knudsen 2015).

 PROVENT: Implementing the ventilation window in IESVE
The following method has been used in PROVENT. The project was partially 
funded by Dansk Energi and conducted by Elforsk, Niras, SBi and DTU BYG 
(Laustsen et al. 2014). The method is limited to produce accurate results under 
the following conditions: 
 
The effects of window frames are not included  
 
Expressions are based on a window of standard size (1230x1480 mm) 
 
The window composition must have a double glazing on the inside and a 
single layer glass on the outside (chosen to avoid condensation) 
The expressions are developed using WinVent to perform parametric variations 
depending on the: external temperature (Te), incident solar radiation (Is) and the 
air flow through the window (Ga). From the parametric variation, a stepwise 
linear regression method is used to determine two mathematical expressions for:  
 
Tgap 
The preheated air temperature, expressed by (Ga), (Te) and (Is),  
 
Ueff 
The effective U-value, expressed by (Ga) and (Te) 
These expressions are implemented in IESVE to evaluate the dynamic 
performance of ventilation windows.

 Zone specific parameters
The method involves parameters specific to each thermal zone. By including 
specific parameters, an air supply temperature profile must be derived for 
every thermal zone. The general method is used in PROVENT investigation 
(Laustsen et al. 2014). 
Zone parameters which determine supply temperature are:  
 
Zone dimensions (floor area and height) 
 
Air flow rate through the ventilated gap (ventilation rate to the zone) 
 
Area of window glazing  
 
Solar gain to the thermal zone The supply temperature profile is defined 
for an ‘average ventilation window’ for each in each thermal zone. 
The air supply temperature profile will represent the average supplied air 
temperature if more windows are included. Defining an average ventilation 
window will be sufficient for a dynamic simulation tool like IESVE, since zone 
temperature is calculated as uniform. 
An average ventilation window, more precisely, is defined by: 
 
An average solar irradiation based on the total solar gain to the room.  
 
Average air flow through the ventilation window, assuming all air supply 
valves are distributed evenly.

 Assumptions forIESVEventilation window expression
To include heat loss correction for ventilation windows in IESVE some 
simplifications are made.  
Assumption from (Laustsen et al. 2014) 
 
Glazing g-values are assumed to constant as set in IESVE. In reality, the 
g-value will change depending on the solar irradiation (Is) and air flow 
(Ga). However, Eq. (7.4) is assuming a constant g-values in the expression 
for (Is). The g-value however, varies only slightly within the range of the 
air flow rates (Raffnsøe 2007). 
 
Windows in same zone are not specified by orientation, the solar gain to 
the zone is divided equally between all windows in the zone.  
 
The properties of the window frame for potential preheating is not 
considered.  7.8.1
 Key differences for current investigation
In this project the method of implementation is different in some ways 
compared to (Laustsen et al. 2014): 
 
Based on specific parameters for every zone, the expressions for Ueff and 
Tgap are calculated for an average ventilation window specifically for that 
zone; this means if one window has more valves and a higher flow rate, 
resulting in less preheating, the average (Tgap) will take this into account. In 
the previous investigation all windows are the same size as the standard 
window for which the original expressions have been developed Eq. (7.5) 
and Eq. (7.6).  
 
The whole heat loss correction is implemented in one temperature profile 
for natural ventilation. This was assessed to be more practical because 
IESVE allows absolute profiles for temperature. Internal gains are 
expressed by a maximum sensible load and a modulating profile; 
predicting the maximum sensible heat load in each simulated zone would 
further complicate the implementation process. 
 
The air density and specific heat capacity are assumed to be constant 
based on the average outdoor temperature of the heating season 3 oC 
(DenmarkDRY_2013.fwt) when converting the heat loss correction to an 
absolute temperature profile. 
 
The ventilation air bypassing the ventilated gap in the window at outdoor 
temperatures above 23 oC. At this stage, further preheating of ventilation 
air will only contribute to overheating.

 Discussion of part 77.9.1
 Evaluation ofpreheatingtemperature profile for IESVE
The method described in previous sections will allow the ventilation window to 
be implemented in IESVE to dynamically calculate supply temperature from the 
ventilated gap. The method considers: 
 
Preheating by heat losses from indoor to the ventilated gap in the window 
(included in ‘dark’ Ueff) Eq. (7.6) 
 
Additional heat losses caused by impaired U-values when supplying air 
through the ventilated gap (included in ‘dark’ Ueff) Eq. (7.6) 
 
Additional preheating caused by solar irradiation on the glazing by 
combining the ‘dark’ effective U-value (Ueff) with the potential preheating 
caused by solar irritation (Ueff,Is) Eq. (7.10) 
 
By-pass module transition over a 5 oC bandwidth from 18 to 23 oC, Eq. 
(7.14) 
The method does NOT consider: 
 
Transition between minimum flow mode to normal flow mode by variable 
supply air flow rate.  
 
Cooling potential by air circulation in the ventilated gap when the bypass 
module is active.  
The temperature profiles used in this project are based on the minimum natural 
ventilation rate for residential buildings is 0.3 l/(s m2) (Trafik- og Byggestyrelsen 
2016). The amount of air supply valves was determined the minimum flow mode 
to ensure that sufficient natural ventilation can always be supplied. This also 
means that the potential higher flow rate in normal flow mode is not considered.  
To include the full potential of the ventilation window in IESVE a combination 
a modulating profile determining the flow rate and an elaborated temperature 
profile would be required. Theoretically the profiles could be set up as in Eq. 
(7.16) and Eq. (7.17).

The modulation profile (MOD) will determine the flow reduction of the air 
supply valves based on outdoor temperature (to) Eq. (7.16). It describes a 
variation between maximum and minimum flow rate with outdoor temperature 
(to) as sensed variable. Assuming that outdoor temperature is 0 oC the flow rate 
will be reduced to 40 % of the normal flow rate, according to Table 4-2. The 
flow rate will rise linearly to 100 % at 5 oC outdoor temperature.  
The absolute profile (ABS) controls the supplied temperature by linear variation 
between 3 different temperature profiles. It considers variable flow rate between 
0 and 5 oC outdoor temperature, by linear variation between two different 
temperature profiles (Tfinal,MIN and Tfinal,NOR), this will allow for different amount 
of preheating as the air flow through the ventilated gap varies. Additionally, it 
considers the bypass function described as a linear variation between 18-23 oC as 
described in Eq. (7.14). The linear variation will vary between Tfinal,NOR and Tocool. 
Tocool is an expression similar to Tfinal,Is (Eq. (7.13)) but considers the cooling 
potential caused by circulation of outdoor air in the ventilated gap to increase 
transmission from indoor to the ventilated gap. This would represent the self-
cooling mode in Figure 4-2, in combination with the by-pass function.  
Due to IESVE having a limit for characters in profiles it is not possible to 
implement this elaborated expression (Eq. (7.17)) and consider all flow modes 
described in Figure 4-2.  
It is important to remember that the negative pressure in the apartments 
provided by the extraction ventilation system is the main driver for ensuring 
natural ventilation. Unless the extraction rate is increased, which could be done 
by turning on the extractor hood in the kitchen, no additional natural ventilation 
can be guaranteed though air supply valves allow for it.

 Energy performance
The refurbishment proposal by Erik Møller Arkitekter can achieve Renovation 
Class 2; the energy consumption was found to 105 kWh/m2 per year including 
electrical energy for extraction ventilation and heating for domestic hot water. 
Compared to the Renovation Class 2 limit of 113 kWh/m2 for this building.  
The specific fan power (SPF) for the extraction ventilation system is assumed to 
be 1 kJ/m3 compared to 2 kJ/m3 for existing conditions in relation to ‘Håndbog 
for Energikonsulenter’ as described in Table 6-7 (Energistyrelsen 2015). This 
will require the current air handling unit (AHU) to be replaced. Since the 
extraction rate is very low, the potential energy savings by replacing the AHU are 
minor at only 2.6 kWh/m2; other energy savings strategies should be considered, 
unless the current AHU is in need of replacement.   9.3.2
 Thermal indoor environment
As for the existing conditions, the low air speeds achieved at the external 
opening justifies the window opening strategy. The overheating requirements of 
100 and 25 hours above 27 oC and 28 oC respectively, can be fulfilled if the 
natural ventilation is supplied with one-sided window opening without causing 
discomfort by draught and high air speeds in the occupied zone.   9.3.3
 Indoor air quality
The CO2 concentration exceeds 1100 ppm whenever windows remain closed. 
The air tightening of the building can lead to challenge with high CO2 
concentration. If the extraction ventilation is increased in relation with the 
installing a new AHU, the electrical energy used for fan operation would be less 
significant. The additional ventilation heat loss however, would contribute to a 
higher energy consumption, but ensure a better indoor air quality, which should 
be considered.

 Thermal indoor environment
According to the simulations, no additional overheating problems are detected 
during the assessment of the thermal indoor environment compared to 
refurbished conditions in chapter 9. The MacroFlo flow rates by window 
opening increases 0.1 ACH or 1.3 l/s additionally during summer. However, the

The ventilation windows ability to preheat the air can improve the local thermal 
indoor environment if compared to regular fresh air valves in the window frame. 
The preheating is significant, but at times still very cold. Keep in mind that the 
window composition for is 1+2; the thermo glazing on the inside reduces the 
preheating temperature due to a smaller heat loss to the ventilated gap, but will 
also have a higher surface temperature on the inside, both of these factors 
should be considered when deciding on the window composition.  
The 1+2 composition was chosen due to previous investigations and preliminary 
mathematical expressions being available, making it possible to implement in 
IESVE.  10.3.3
 Performance of ventilation windows
It is certain that the temperature profiles have been correctly implemented in 
IESVE (it has been checked several times), what happens during the simulations 
however seems to have an effect on the preheating temperature of the 
ventilation window. During peak solar gains the calculated preheating 
temperature can be up to 58 % (4 oC) higher than the simulated.  
It was investigated whether the assumption about air density made in Chapter 7 
could influence the final preheating temperature; however, it was found that the 
density difference based on a 40 K difference at current flow rates only caused 
0.5 oC difference which could explain slight deviation during hours with no solar 
gain. 
The fact that solar gains causes a very different preheating temperature suggest 
that IESVE could be reducing some parts of the temperature profile expression, 
it is uncertain what exactly happens.

 CONCLUSION
The problem statement will be answered based on the 
investigations performed during the thesis.  
 Decentralised combined system
Development of a decentralised combined system that provides a solution for 
ventilation and heating, is motivated by the fact that other buildings are facing 
the issues as Højstrupparken in context of refurbishment. To provide a flexible, 
well defined and sufficient system which solves to the challenges in this case 
study would have been an ideal conclusion.  
When developing a combined system which has to consider aesthetics, energy 
performance and indoor climate, at the same time as having limited flexibility for 
refurbishment solutions, defining the compromises and choosing one solution 
over the other the can be a challenge, and will to some extent be subjective. 
The heating unit capacities were thoroughly investigated for different 
refurbishment proposals, and visualised in the context of the study case. It was 
made clear that conventional convectors, compared to embedded pipe heating 
systems (EPHS), will be more practical to integrate in a combined decentralised 
system, due to maximum capacity limitations and space requirements for EPHS; 
even assuming optimal operation conditions. Choosing EPHS will require more 
extensive and costly renovation, compared to the integrated convector which 
allows for easier integration in a prefabricated system, due the its compactness 
and higher heating capacity.  
The architectural values are a subjective thing in this matter. It is the author’s 
opinion that the refurbishment proposal no matter the heating system will 
improve the window bay area by freeing up space. Whether an EPHS system or 
an integrated convector is chosen will mean about a 50 mm difference to the 
spandrel construction; if the convector is well integrated. It is suggested that 
increasing the spandrel thickness will add to the window frame dimensions in 
the corner of the window joints (Figure 12-13), which aesthetically speaking is 
not an ideal compromise.  
It is important that the architectural values remain but a compromise seems to 
be in order in relation to the integration of the heating system, especially since 
the original window bay has already been improved in relation to the hollow 
spandrel.  
 Performance of ventilation windows
The energy performance of ventilation windows was found to provide a 4.3 % 
heating reduction compared to conventional windows with similar properties. 
The maximum simulated preheating temperature was found to 9.7 oC and 14.7 
oC when calculated based on simulation output. At hours with a substantial solar 
gain, the difference is at its highest. Why the difference tends to be that

The preheating as calculated will not prevent cold supply air temperatures, as the

This values determines the minimum supply flow rate through the ventilated 
gap, allowing maximum time for preheating. However, any preheating is an 
improvement to supplying outdoor temperatures during heating season and will 
reduce the risk of thermal discomfort.  
As savings for heating is not very significant, this should not be the main 
incentive for choosing ventilation windows over conventional ones. An in depth 
investigation of the local thermal indoor environment would have been in order, 
to assess the full potential of ventilation windows, by including their cooling 
potential and variable air flow rates to allow for improved indoor air quality.  14.2.1
 Ventilation window expression
The expressions developed for the preheating temperature profiles in IESVE, 
allow for a heat loss correction based on the dynamic properties of ventilation. 
The heat loss correction is implemented using IESVE variables as a temperature 
profile, to simulate the dynamic influence on the thermal indoor environment 
and energy consumption.  
The expressions are based on a fair amount of assumptions to make the 
implementation possible. Assuming uniform solar irradiation on the windows in 
combination with the possibility for different window sizes and additionally 
differentiating normal windows from ventilation windows, could influence the 
results significantly. The most accurate results will be achieved by using only 
ventilation windows with a standard size (1230x1480 mm), as the fundamental 
mathematical expressions, used for method in this project, was developed based 
on such a window. In addition, the window must have the thermo glazing on the 
inner side, also described as a (1+2) composition.  14.2.2
 Embedded pipe heating systemsin spandrel
Embedded pipe heating systems (EPHS) will not be sufficient as a stand-alone 
heating systems in the spandrel alone. The main reason is that EPHS are 
designed for heating with low temperatures using a large surface area. In 
addition, the refurbishment options are limited due to the preservability of the 
brick facades; the option of external insulations is excluded, meaning the peak 
heat loads remain too high.

As the old windows are replaced in any of the refurbishment solution, the risk of 
cold draught or radiant asymmetry around the external façade is less likely; the 
heating system are not necessary around at the external walls, i.e. a floor or wall 
heating system (EPHS) can be considered as a potential solution.  
The peak heat load/or heating unit capacities estimated are greatly influenced by 
the renovation. By adding 30 mm internal high insulating material, such as PUR, 
both the energy demand and heating unit capacities will decrease further. 
Beyond 50 mm internal PUR the significance of adding additional insulation is 
harder to justify, it could be discussed whether the advantages are outweighed by 
reducing the internal volume. Exact internal insulation thickness should be 
further investigated in relation to other energy saving strategies like heat 
recovery of waste water or exhaust air.  14.2.3
 Thermal indoor environmentand window opening 
The thermal indoor environment was found to be improved by refurbishment, 
having less hours with overheating than the existing conditions. The main reason 
was the lower g-value by adding new windows, which significantly reduces the 
solar gains in the simulation results. In addition, the size of the balcony is 
increased, a larger overhang is acquired, reducing the potential for solar gains. 
To avoid overheating in IESVE simulations, a predictive window opening 
profile was implemented based on indoor temperature and wind speed. The 
opening profile allows for continuous opening of windows at all times when 
indoor temperatures are above 23 oC, and is fully opened at 28 oC if outdoor 
wind speed is less than 5 m/s; an opening reduction is assumed between 5-10 
m/s wind speed. The opening profile was used assuming that only one-sided 
(window opening) ventilation is possible, resulting in peak air speeds of 0.16 
m/s at the external openings, only exceeding the Danish Building Regulation 
requirement of 0.15 m/s in the occupied zones slightly.  
It is important to clarify, that using a predictive window opening profile does 
not guarantee overheating requirements can be fulfilled. However, it is assessed 
to provide a critical estimate, not taking into account the option of cross-flow 
ventilation, which is possible by opening doors between thermal zones. 
This method was used to justify that the thermal zoned can be occupied without 
disturbance from draught at all times while performing sufficient natural 
ventilation to prevent overheating.

 Further work14.3.1
 Full dynamic evaluation of ventilation window performance
As discussed, the expression implemented in IES-VE allows for further 
improvements to consider all aspects of the ventilation window. This includes 
variables flow rates and the cooling potential of the self-cooling mode. The main 
issue is that IES-VE does not allow for the amount of characters required for 
the proposed method in one temperature profile. The evaluation could be 
performed in Excel, and give an accurate estimate of energy savings for heating, 
and possibly cooling as well. Ideally other simulation tools should be 
investigated, with the goal to implement the method proposed in this project.  14.3.2
 Computational Fluid Dynamics assessment of ventilation windows
As the preheating performed by ventilation windows can be assessed at any 
given time step throughout an annual simulation in IES-VE, it would be 
interesting to investigate the local thermal comfort in the vicinity of the air 
supply valves in different scenarios.  
By performing a series of stationary CFD assessments (possibly in FloVENT) 
with different supply temperatures, the simulation data from IESVE could be 
assessed in relation to the results of stationary conditions found by CFD. The 
results could be evaluated using EN 7730 and EN 7726 for local thermal 
discomfort assessment by calculating draught rating and PMV based on local air 
speed and temperature in the vicinity of the air supply valves. The actual 
improvement in thermal comfort should be assessed in relation to standard fresh 
air valves with no preheating.  
The investigation would have to be aware of air disturbance caused by the 
supply valve opening, to model an accurate air flow pattern, also a slightly lower 
inner surface temperature of the window, compared to a standard 3-layer 
window, caused by preheating in the ventilated gap, would be essential to the 
investigation.

Investigation of window opening with MacroFlo 
During the thesis it is made clear that the assumptions regarding the MacroFlo 
window opening profile might be ideal by allowing long periods with high 
ventilation flow rates, potentially causing discomfort by draught.  
The flow rates achieved during initial investigations were are plausible; however, 
the duration of high flow rates was a greater cause of concern since the 
ventilation profile will allow constant ventilation if the indoor air temperature is 
too high.  
Based on investigations of exiting conditions for Højstrupparken study case, a 
test whether the MacroFlo peak ventilation flow rates can be reduced by limiting 
window opening in relation to the outdoor wind speed was proposed. The 
findings are described in this Appendix.  
As made clear in the report, any assumptions regarding occupant behaviour can 
be complicated to justify, due to occupants responding differently to thermal 
sensations or in some cases, not responding at all. Of course this include 
assumptions regarding window opening based on outdoor wind speed.   
The investigation includes assumptions below: 
• 
Window opening is performed actively by occupants (no mechanical 
opening) 
• 
No internal shading is used to prevent overheating, i.e. no internal shading 
e.g. venetian blinds or curtains. 
• 
One-sided window opening i.e. no cross flow ventilation and only one 
openable window in each thermal zone.

As an addition to the original expression the gt( ) is multiplied with a ramp 
function. The ramp( ) function works the same way as gt( ) by defining a linear 
variation based on a sensed variable.  
The ramp( ) allows specific return values, in this case 0.2 and 1, as opposed to 
the gt( ) function only allowing variation between 0 and 1. Since a complete 
window closing based on the wind speed is not desired, a ramp function is 
chosen for this purpose.   
If the wind speed exceeds 5 m/s a gradual reduction will be multiplied to the 
temperature based window opening, gt( ). At 10 m/s the window opening will 
be reduced to 20 %. If reduced window opening degree causes the indoor 
temperature to increase the gt( ) part will compensate by returning a higher 
value, at 28 oC the window will be fully opened, if the wind speed is less than 5 
m/s and 20 % open if the wind speed is 10 m/s.   
Example: If the wind speed is 10 m/s and the dry resultant temperature is 28 oC, 
the opening degree will be 0.2 based on the wind speed, and 1 based on the 
temperature, multiplied the opening degree will be 0.2 i.e. 20 %.

C.7 
MacroFlo setup for window opening 
All thermal zones are assigned with one MacroFlo to only utilize one sided 
ventilation. This is considered to a conservative approach since cross flow 
ventilation will be possible considering the layout of the apartment. Internal 
doors are assumed to never be opened as opening would enable the option for 
cross flow ventilation; a crack opening however is assumed since this is also a 
requirement in building regulations. The idea is that evaluating the thermal 
indoor environment without the option of cross flow ventilation, will present a 
more critical situation to compensate for ideal window opening profile as 
described in the report.

I.2 
Instructions 
About the workbook 
This evaluation spreadsheet is developed specifically for the paste table format 
directly in from IESVE 2015, earlier or later version might treat data differently. 
The purpose of this excel file is to generate an indoor climate evaluation report 
for multiple zones, considering the usage hours. The evaluation is performed in 
relation to DS/EN 15251 (showing distribution in categories). For non-
mechanically cooled buildings, an adaptive thermal comfort approach is 
implemented following CISBE TM52 guidelines and comfort criteria. 
Instructions 
0. Example 
When opening up the workbook a report is displayed. This example can be 
tested with the parameters included in the workbook to get a feel of its usability. 
The settings are changed in the INPUT sheet. 
1. Sheets 
The workbook includes several sheet; only sheets marked in GREEN are 
necessary in order to perform the evaluations. These are DATA, INPUT and 
REPORT. Others are used for calculations that are not represented visually as 
in the report. These sheets can be accessed for documentation of calculation methods, however 
the workbook depends on a lot of references and named ranges which might not be ideal for 
transparency of the calculations.  
Important: Do not open any other sheets than INPUT and DATA until they 
contain zone and input data, this can sometimes bug up the chart references 
(for some reason). 
 
2. DATA sheet 
2.1. Copy paste 
The data sheet is for pasting data directly from IESVE tabular data in 
Vista Pro (
). The data should be pasted in as yearly data, including 
8760 data points for each parameter. All data for each thermal zone shall 
be placed in clusters with no gaps in between in the DATA sheet. It 
is recommended to paste data for one zone at the time.  
2.2. Recommended data 
To evaluate zones in relation to DS/EN 15251 or the CISBE TM52, the 
occupied hours need to be included as part of the zone data. Assuming 
all zones will have internal gains from occupants when they are present, 
the IESVE parameter 'People gain' is used as parameter of occupants 
being present in the zone. ‘Operative temperature’ is used for 
temperature evaluation and can be enables in Vista Pro by clicking (
) 
and enabling TM52 adaptive comfort.

can be chosen.  
The yearly analysis showing MIN and MAX values and dates, and the 
yearly plot. The peak analyses will also show a graph of the MAX day 
(therefore not recommended for negative peak, so far).
