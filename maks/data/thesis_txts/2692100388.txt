Abstract 
 
It is beyond any doubt that the environmental awareness has been drastically risen compared to previous 
decades, both on an individual basis as well as on a societal level. The urgent need for countries 
worldwide to restrain the GHG emissions and achieve their environmental goals led them to resort to 
more environmentally friendly initiatives in a wide range of sectors including among else the 
transportation industry. Last year, the Danish government announced that Denmark is ambitiously 
aiming at reaching 775.000 electric or hybrid cars on the country’s roads by 2030. Similarly to 
Denmark, many countries have also resolved towards this direction. Therefore, it is getting constantly 
more crucial to scientifically forecast how the future demand of electric vehicles is expected to be 
developed, which is of course a challenging and multifaceted task.  
This thesis seeks to predict the potential demand of electric vehicles in Denmark by integrating the 
frameworks of two different modelling approaches. The first one entails the implementation of a widely 
used discrete choice analysis on a disaggregate level in order to estimate how several attributes affect 
the customers in terms of what type of vehicle they intend to purchase. Despite this approach’s 
straightforward and simple structure though, these models fail to capture the crucial role of innovation 
and its impact on electric vehicles’ penetration into the market. In order to tackle this discrete choice 
analysis’ shortage, a diffusion model was applied additionally given as input the past sales of electric 
vehicles in Denmark. Since though conventional forms of diffusion models such as the Bass’ and 
Gompertz’s models fail to encapsulate the dynamic effects in terms of substitutions among different 
products (in this case conventional and electric vehicles), an updated version of diffusion modeling was 
implemented as proposed by the existing literature. Using this expanded model, the two approaches are 
combined by engulfing the diffusion effect in the utility function for electric vehicles in order to forecast 
future trends for electric vehicles.

1. Introduction 
 
During the last decade, there has been a rising tendency towards the direction of mitigating the 
Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions in Europe. For this purpose, several governments and public 
authorities have given emphasis on the transport sector, considering that 29% of the total global GHG 
emissions originates from the transportation activities, whereas almost 45% of the latter stems from 
passenger cars (Fevang et al., 2021).  Under this perspective, Electric Vehicles (EVs) have been 
regarded as a mean of actualizing a decarbonizing transportation society and responding to the challenge 
of diminishing the GHG emissions (Skjølsvold and Ryghaug, 2020). In order to strengthen the 
competitiveness and the popularity of EVs among potential consumers, a wide range of policies have 
been gradually applied by various countries. Examples of such policies involve the implementation of 
subsidies, tax reductions, free parking, exemptions from road pricing etc. (Maciulis et al., 2018). A 
typical example of how this kind of policies can eventually lead to increasing demand of EVs can be 
illustrated in the case of Norway. In fact, the Norwegian government legislated tax exemptions for 
owners of vehicles with an internal combustion engine, contributing to a great degree to a share of 
52.2% and a 20.4% of new passenger cars registrations for Battery Electric Vehicles (BEVs) and 20.4% 
for Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicles (PHEVs) respectively (Fevang et al., 2021). 
Taking the EVs’ rising popularity into consideration, the need to accurately forecast their future demand 
has inevitably emerged. Nevertheless, certain difficulties arose during this attempt. Discrete Choice 
Modelling has been quickly established as a popular approach of analyzing the demand for new products 
and EVs were no exception (Glerium et al., 2021). In fact, Discrete Choice Models (DCM)  are 
commonly applied in occasions in which an agent, i.e. a decision maker is being given a set of options 
(alternatives) and is then asked to make either a choice or a series of choices among them (Train, 2009). 
Hence, DCMs are hence useful under circumstances when aiming at evaluating the potential impact of 
distinct policies on customers´ choice behavior, since it is possible to estimate how likely it is for a 
customer to select a certain alternative, considering a series of relative variables (Oppewal and 
Timmermans, 2001). The core of applying a DCM can be summarized into two main components: 1) 
calculating the aggregate probabilities for the base year by replicating the existing market shares and 2) 
implementing an iterative recalibration algorithm for readjusting the Alternative Specific Constants 
(ASCs) and possibly the scale as well, in order to ensure that the choice shares won´t deviate from the 
base year´s observed shares (Jensen et al., 2017). 
Nevertheless, applying solely a DCM in some instances isn´t considered to be sufficient for forecasting 
prospective market shares.  Although EVs are experiencing a significantly rapid growth throughout the 
last years, much still remains to be done until they become a mass marketed product and a dominating 
==PAGE:10==
2 
 
product. According to relevant statistics obtained from IEA´s Global EV Outlook (2021), over the last 
decade, there has been a constant annual growth of EVs surpassing 50%, except for the years 2019 and 
2020, in which the annual EVs´ registrations increased rose by nearly 40%. Despite this upturn, in 2020 
EVs obtained an annual market share equal to 4.6%. These findings reveal the necessity for taking 
account of the role of innovation and how it affects the EVs´ penetration rates into the new market over 
time (Jensen et al., 2017). 
A very popular approach which incorporates the mechanism of innovation in the into the forecasting 
procedure is the Bass Diffusion Model. This model is mainly built upon two distinct components: 1) 
the innovators who adopt an innovative product or technology early on and 2) the imitators who are 
influenced after getting in contact with the formers (Massiani & Andreas, 2015). It basically comforts 
to the principle of the famous S-curve, which describes the three main stages of an innovative product´s 
technology life cycle. However, typical diffusion models like Bass’ and Gombertz’s were criticized 
among others for not being able to illustrate the substitution effects between a new product and its 
predecessor, i.e. the replacement of an older technology by a newer version.  
The purpose of this thesis is to suggest a framework combining both the DCM and the diffusion 
modelling principles, in order to compensate for DCM´s weakness in terms of its forecasting accuracy. 
After executing a discrete choice analysis, diffusion model was applied as a separate estimation.For the 
DCM part, a randomly drawn sample of the Danish population from a previous stated preference choice 
experiment will be exploited, whereas for the diffusion part, statistical data were mined which illustrate 
the annual registrations and ownership of EVs over the last years in Denmark. 
This thesis´s structure is organized as follows: the second chapter covers the literature review and is 
divided into three distinct sections with respect to discrete choice analysis review, diffusion review and 
finally a review of the most commonly used attributes. Next, the third and the fourth chapter illustrate 
the methodology followed for the estimation of the DCM and the diffusion discussing the input data 
and their estimation procedure. The fifth chapter provides the obtained results separately for each of the 
two models, followed by the sixth chapter which entails a discussion and criticism around the credibility 
of the results, combined with some conclusions.

2. Literature Review 
This section of the thesis aims at presenting a retrospect of the methodological frameworks that have 
been exploited throughout the last decades for the purpose of performing EV preference analysis. 
Furthermore, a review on the various attributes that have been taken into consideration in the context 
of the applied modelling techniques.  
  
2.1 DCM Review 
In this sub-section, there will be a description of the most salient sub-categories of DCM and some of 
the most representable milestones of DCM techniques applied within the cars’ adoption.  
The most widespread modelling technique has initially considered to be the discrete choice analysis in 
the form of Discrete Choice Model (DCM), which addresses the EVs’ adoption as a buyer’s preference 
or choice among a set of vehicles’ alternatives subject to a series of attributes (Liao et. al., 2016). This 
concept is based on the assumption that customers tend to make decisions after they weigh the trade-
offs from one attribute to another (Liao et. al., 2016). DCMs are prominent tools in the fields of 
economics, as they are capable of providing useful measurements such price elasticities and 
substitutions effects (Liu & Cirillo, 2017).  The first distinction of DCM classifies them into Logit and 
Probit. Probit models atone for the Logit models’ weaknesses which according to Train (2003)  fail to 
properly: 1) express random taste variation, 2) produce substitution patterns and 3) incorporate panel 
data with temporally correlated errors as input.  
Mc Fadden in 1974 was the first scientist who developed a theoretical framework of econometrics 
models in order to analyze the discrete choice principles, setting this way the theoretical basis of the 
DCMs’ applications. He was actually the one who outlined the individual choice behavior as a structure 
of three distinct components: 1) the set of alternatives among which the individuals have to decide, 2) 
the attributes observed by the decision-makers and 3) the individual choice and behavior model (Mc 
Fadden, 1974). The outcome of his research was the so-called Multinomial Logit (MNL) model, which 
covers for a logistic regression in which more than two alternatives/outcomes are available to the 
decision-maker. Nevertheless, MNL was criticized for strictly requiring the assumption of 
Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives (IIA). According to the IIA property, the ratio between two 
alternatives is not affected by the remaining alternatives, i.e. the person’s choice among two alternatives 
does not depend on the other available choices (Cheng  & Long , 2007). Although in some real-life 
situations this assumption can be verified, it is quite often proven to be unrealistic. In order to tackle 
this MNL weakness, the Nested Logit (NL) model was developed as an extension to the conventional 
MNL. According to Train (2009), the NL model is a suitable approach in occasions in which the 
available alternatives can be clustered into subsets, named nests. The NL approach has been evinced to 
==PAGE:12==
4 
 
be more realistic than the MNL, under the perspective of the former still holding the IIA property, but 
in this case, it holds within the nest rather than the full set of alternatives. The chronologically next 
important advancement in DCM research was the Mixed Multinomial Logit (MMNL) model, which is 
regarded as a flexible and computationally practical method for estimating more complicated data 
structures, while not enforcing the IIA property (Mc Fadden & Train, 2003).  
Naturally, the primordial DCM applications which were developed in the mid-80s, did not emphasize 
neither EVs nor vehicles’ adoption, but they were applied on other fields such as the medical sciences 
for the purpose of  examining Fertility and Child Mortality (Wolpin, 1984) and the economics of labor 
mobility in terms of job matching and occupational choice (Miller, 1984). Train (1983)  was one of the 
pathfinders of exploiting DCM’s principles towards an automobile demand research. In specific, Train 
formulated a model which was used to forecast the number of vehicles owned by the examined 
households as well the annual expected travel distance, while taking into account the vehicles’ 
characteristics such as their purchase price and annual operation cost. Later on,  Brownstown (1996) 
developed a vehicle choice model within the wider geographic area of California by using a stated 
preference (SP) dataset and by implementing a micro-simulation demand forecasting model. The 
principal upgrade compared to previous similar applications was that Brownstown did not focus on the 
vehicle ownership, but he emphasized on the vehicle transactions in order to compute the potential 
demand of vehicles with alternative fuels. Another popular modelling technique involves the latent 
variable model (Ben-Akiva et.al., 2002 and Ghasri et.al., 2019). The purpose of this kind of technique 
is to account for variables which cannot be directly observed (Bierlaire, 2018). These are referred to 
variables of psychological nature (among other) and are considered to be influenced by exogenous 
variables such socio-economic ones (Liao et. al., 2016).  
 
2.2 Diffusion Review 
The typical disaggregate DCMs as described in the previous section are appropriate for predicting future 
market shares for EVs for stable markets, however, they lack in their capability to take the concept of 
innovation into account (Fjendbo et.al., 2017). In order to tackle this weakness of DCMs, several studies 
have been conducted in order to describe how new technologies diffuse and how they affect the potential 
market shares of the examined product. Prior to the diffusion of the technology, one of the first 
foundations of the diffusion concept is considered to be the Gombertz diffusion model. Gobertz (1825) 
tried to practically explore the conceptual assumption that an increase in the age of an individual causes 
an exponentially increased mortality rate, by providing a function which uses the individual’s age as an 
independent variable and the mortality rate as the dependent variable.  
At the early 60s the importance of diffusion in aid of the socio-economic development had already been 
realized and thus several diffusion investigations were conducted in Latin America, Africa and Asia 
(Rogers, 1963). Some of the most common fields in which the diffusion was applied were agriculture, 
==PAGE:13==
5 
 
public health, family planning etc. Rogers defined the diffusion of innovation as a process which takes 
place the individuals in a social system via various communication channels such as “word of mouth”, 
advertisement etc. Rogers (1963) also argued that there five distinct adopter classes on the basis of a 
normal distribution : 1) innovators, 2) early adopters, 3) early majority, 4) late majority and 5) laggards. 
The way these five categories are distributed is illustrated in the following graph: 
 
 
Figure 1: Categorization of adopters in a diffusion model according to Rogers (1963) 
 
Another milestone of the diffusion of technology is the Bass equation, which was developed by Frank 
Bass, and which is one of the most popular diffusion modeling techniques nowadays. Bass (1969) 
classified the potential users of a new product into the into innovators, who embrace a new technology 
regardless of other people’s decisions, and the imitators, who imitate the formers. These two concepts 
are mathematically interpreted as the factor of innovation p and the factor of imitation q, respectively. 
Similarly to Rogers’ diffusion model, the Bass model also assumes that the new technology when 
launched in the market is expected to follow the so-called S-shaped curve and its three distinct growth 
phases, i.e. rapid growth, maturity and decline.  
Since then, many diffusion applications have been conducted being inspired by the research of the 
aforementioned predecessors. Jun and Park (1999) developed a choice-based diffusion model which 
encapsulates both the diffusion and the substitution effects for multiples generations of products. In 
order to do so, they incorporated the diffusion effect as a separate factor (compared to the innovation 
and imitation factors in Bass equation) within the utility function in order to capture the diffusion 
impact. Massiani and Gohs (2015) exploited the results of a research regarding the impact of several 
policy measures in Germany for boosting the diffusion of EVs, so that they conclude on appropriate 
values for p and q  within the automotive technologies’ diffusion and especially for EVs. In 2017, Jensen 
et. al. proposed an upgrade for Jun’s and Park’s model (1999) by re-formulating their diffusion equation. 
Compared to Jun & Park, Jensen et.al. replaced the probabilities’ formulas from the formers’  with 
models approximated at a disaggregate level Jensen’s et al. proposed formula results to obtaining the 
betas of the examined parameters, a scale parameter as well as the Alternative Specific Constant (ASC) 
for EVs. Furthermore, instead of p and q (Bass parameters), their utility function specification involves 
==PAGE:14==
6 
 
only one respective parameter, i.e. the diffusion effect (similarly to Jun and Park). Later on, in order to 
examine the diffusion of electric mobility in 18 EU countries and five benchmark studies worldwide, 
Fluchs (2020) incorporated for each examined country two invariant parameters a and b which stretch 
the diffusion curve horizontally and stretch or compress it respectively. Nevertheless, it should be noted 
that these two parameters cannot be interpreted in a direct way, i.e. they don’t involve an actual market-
oriented interpretation. He also embodied a diffusion speed factor in the diffusion formula, which 
reflects how the growth rate is related to share of vehicles not yet being adopted (Fluchs, 2020).  
 
2.3 Attributes’ Review 
Reviewing the existing literature and previous relevant studies revealed that the customers’ EV 
purchase behavior is influenced by a wide variety of factors. One of the most popular approaches in 
order to examine the magnitude and the significance of  various attributes from the customers’ 
perspective is the Stated-Preference (SP) Choice Experiment.  The main categories of attributes can be 
categorized as follows: 
 
2.3.1 Monetary/Financial Attributes 
The first monetary attribute which customers tend to consider before proceeding with buying an EV is 
its purchase price. Several studies have implemented a pivoted design in order to account for this 
attribute, i.e. potential buyers were presented an EV purchase price which was modified and pivoted 
with respect to a reference vehicle price which was declared by the survey’s respondents themselves. 
All examined studies have concluded a negative and statistically significant effect of purchase price on 
the perceived EV utility. Some interesting findings regarding the interaction between the purchase price 
and the household income emerged from Potoglou & Kanaroglou (2007), who examined how different 
income groups are affected by the purchase price during their decision. In specific, they concluded that 
the car’s purchase price is a more significant and determining factor for medium-income individuals 
rather than for individuals with high income. This finding was found to be confirmed by Achtnicht 
(2011) as well, who further reported that the Willingness-To-Pay (WTP) of low- or medium- income 
customers is lower regarding improvements in other car attributes, since their purchase price range is 
narrower due to budget constraints.  
Another commonly appeared cost attribute across studies is the operational cost, which was observed 
to be incorporated into reasonably different ways. Jensen et. al. (2020) assumed separately the yearly 
cost, which concerns a fixed annual expenses for taxation and insurances, and the operation cost which 
concerns the expenses related to the vehicle’s propulsion. From another point of view, other studies 
ranked the vehicles’ operational cost reversed relative to their purchase price by assuming that EVs 
were the cheapest in terms of operational costs, followed by PHEVs and by ICVs (Bockarjova et. al., 
2013). Finally, some studies considered operational expenses as a variable cost subject to the vehicle’s 
==PAGE:15==
7 
 
driving range, e.g. Glerium et.al (2013) assumed operation cost for ICVs approximately equal to 11 
€/km and for EVs no more than 5.4 €/km. In either case many studies lay emphasis on the difficulty of 
approximating the energy costs for EVs depending on the possibility of EVs’ owners being able to 
charge their private cars at home or not, which may deduce different findingσ (Jensen et.al., 2020).   
 
2.3.2 Technical Attributes 
This attribute category describes the car characteristics related to the vehicle’s capabilities, whose low 
performance is commonly considered to be a crucial barrier towards the path EVs becoming a mass 
product to a greater extent. Naturally, the quality of these attributes has endured dramatic improvements 
making EVs an evidently more preferable choice than it was in the past.  
Hess et.al. (2009) found that a longer driving range positively affects the perceived customer’s utility 
and that the WTP for this attribute is high as well, but while being statistically insignificant. The reason 
behind range not being statistically significant is most probably due to Hess et.al  assuming that the EV 
range’s upper and lower bound to be fairly low (48 to 97 km). This assumption is naturally strongly 
dependent on the existing technology of the research time period. Subsequent studies which expanded 
the EV driving range to higher values concluded that it had a positive impact on the EV utility while 
being statistically significant. A car’s performance is also measured as a function of its maximum speed 
and acceleration. Valeri & Danielis (2015) revealed that acceleration was proven to be insignificant in 
the context of the obtained substitution patterns, which they interpreted as a lack of prior experience 
with EVs from the respondents, while they also observed that the interest of individuals in attributes 
like speed and acceleration is also related to their social profile. For instance, Potoglou and Kanaroglou 
(2007) examined the interaction effects within the acceleration ~ gender and acceleration ~ household 
type (single/engaged/family). Their results demonstrated that females value acceleration to a lower 
degree compared to males and that for single people acceleration weighs more than for engaged 
individuals. Regarding the boot size, Jensen’s et.al. (2020) research concluded that customers are 
willing to pay more for acquiring a boot size of very large size than acquiring large or medium boot 
size with respect to a small boot.  
 
2.3.3 Infrastructure Attributes 
Infrastructure attributes are concerned with the availability of charging infrastructure, and they mainly 
focus on EV users. A commonly evaluated distinction across the infrastructure regards the EV users 
who already have access to charging infrastructure (i.e. charger) at home or who can potentially have a 
charger installed at home, and those who do not and may not have in the future neither. Apparently, the 
formers are more concerned about attributes such as the distance to the nearest charger from home and 
their availability degree. Ito et al. (2012) investigated the potential demand and the customers’ WTP for 
==PAGE:16==
8 
 
improved infrastructure services for alternative fuel vehicles by applied an NMNL model. Their 
findings showed that the infrastructural development is related to the EVs’ annual market shares and 
specifically that the former can be taken as successful only as long as the EVs’ annual registration 
surpass a 5.6% of the total yearly car sales. Furthermore, Jensen et. al. (2020) indicated a connection 
between the driving range and the customers’ perceived importance of the available infrastructure, i.e. 
they estimated the infrastructure attributes only as long as the EV’s driving range was less than 200 km, 
since otherwise customers weren’t found to be concerning about attributes such as the charging 
infrastructure availability that much. In almost every case, the improvement of the existing charging 
infrastructure (e.g. extending fast charging station infrastructure) was supported as a positive driver of 
achieving a higher EV adoption (e.g. Bockarjova et.al., 2013).  
 
 
2.3.4 Environmental Attributes 
Although the CO2 emissions are related to the respective car’s characteristics, there will be a separate 
reference to it since it is also linked to individuals’ environmental awareness. Although EVs don’t have 
cause any direct emissions, they still generate some indirect emissions generated from the battery 
manufacturing and the electricity generation, but either way the EV emissions are considerably inferior 
to the ICVs’ emissions. Bockarjova et.al. (2013) defined the total amount of CO2 emissions in tons per 
year as a function of expected annual number of kilometers driven. On the other hand, Achtninht (2010) 
doubted the emissions per km relevance within car buyonce decisions and stated that although it is a 
substantial matter, its magnitude and consideration may widely vary across the sampled population.  
 
2.3.5 Socio-economic and Demographic Attributes 
Socio-economic attributes may not be directly connected to the car attributes or the infrastructure, but 
they still may affect an individual’s choice to buy an EV. Rasouli’s and Timmerman’s study (2013) 
showed that there is a statistically significant relationship between certain demographic attributes and 
the adoption of an electric vehicle. In particular, it was shown that there is a higher inclination towards 
the buoyance of an EV by individuals below 35 years old, by households with young children, by people 
who achieved a higher vocational or a middle-specialized education, and the results also indicated a 
slightly larger preference for EV by males compared to females. Similar results well obtained by other 
studies as well such from Shin et. al. (2019) 
 
2.3.6 Policy Attributes 
The legislation of policies which motivate citizens to purchase an EV and thus promote the EV market 
expansion is also an important driver which has been applied by many governments.  An interesting 
finding is derived from Hess (2009) who presented a 1000$ subsidy to the respondents with two 
==PAGE:17==
9 
 
different ways, one presented as tax reduction and one as purchase price reduction. The comparison 
showed that the tax reduction is valued more compared to the price reductions, which suggests to EVs 
being considered as products with a high social statue value. On the contrary, the provision of 
facilitations such as free parking or access to High-Occupancy Vehicle (HOV) lanes was tested to be 
insignificant (Qian. & Soorpramanien, 2011).

4. Diffusion for EVs Estimation 
As aforementioned, although the DCM provides an efficient framework for obtaining the likelihood 
that an individual chooses a certain alternative among a set of others, it still lacks the capability of 
actually forecasting future situations and scenarios. To put it simply, diffusion can be defined as the 
process by which individuals or firms either adopt a new technology or replace the older technology 
with a new one (Hall B., 2004). EVs are considered to be a newly launched product and thus the impact 
of innovation through the trajectory of their market penetration should be taken into account as well. 
This can be achieved supplementing the initial DCM with a diffusion model, which can represent the 
concepts and the results applicable to technology innovation, adoption, and implementation studies 
(Dhakal T. and Lim D., 2019).  
 
4.1 Bass Equation 
The Bass model is considered as an efficient tool for the purpose of illustrating the diffusion of new 
products and technologies, i.e. predicting their long-term market penetration pattern. At least one out 
of two conditions should be fulfilled in order for the Bass model’s application to be valid: 1) The new 
product has been recently launched by a company and few periods of past sales are already known or 
2) the new product has not been introduced yet, but there are available sales data  for a product of similar 
nature  (Lilien and Rangaswamy, 2000). The Bass model classifies the potential consumers into two 
groups: 1) the innovators, who are the first to acquire the new technology and are influenced by external 
mechanisms such as mass advertisement and 2) the imitators who acquire the new product after being 
convinced by the innovators via “internal communication” processes such as the “word of mouth” 
(Massiani H. & Andreas G., 2015). 
The Bass equation in its simplest form can be expressed as follows (Jensen A. et al., 2017):

4.2 Diffusion Data and Estimation 
During the last years EVs have been standing on a path of increased popularity and development both 
on a worldwide and European level. The transition towards EVs rising dominance within the vehicle 
market is being significantly enhanced by the increased emphasis given on factors such as the EVs’ 
production and operation cost decrease, the growth of the charging infrastructure and the existing 
urgency to achieve the CO2 mitigation goals (Hall D. et.al., 2020). In 2020, about 3 million EVs were 
registered all over the world which led to around 10 million EVs going around on the roads globally by 
the end of 2021 (IEA, 2021). Meanwhile in Europe, although in 2020 the car market was shrank by 
around 22%, EVs’ registration were doubled and reached a 10% market share (IEA, 2021).  
Denmark is also one of the backbones of the EVs’ market development, since it was one of the first 
European countries to set aspirations towards diminishing the GHG emissions released from the 
transportation primarily, but from other sectors as well (Skytte K. et. al., 2014). Since the transport 
sector is the most dominant one in terms of GHG emissions, it sounds reasonable that the sustainability 
initiative should focus on it. In fact, transportation CO2 emissions have significantly increased from 12 
million tones in 1990 to 13.9 million tones in 2018, which is interpreted as the 25% of the emissions of 
that year (DEA, 2020). Furthermore, according to the Danish Energy Agency’s predictions for 2030 
(2020), emission from road transport are anticipated to account for more than 90% of the total 
transportation emissions in 2030, whereas passenger cars are expected to correspond to more than half 
(58%) of the total road transport emissions of 2030.

At first, the Bass equation formula given above was implemented applying a Nonlinear Least Squares 
(NLS) regression, setting the observed shares for each time previous time-period as the dependent 
variable and the right component of the Bass equation as the independent variable in order to compute 
the values of p, q and M. Before applying the NLS though, it is required to decide on some reasonable 
and initial values for all three parameters. In the case of p and q it’s easier, since previous studies showed 
that their values mainly range between 0 and 1. However, defining a starting value for the potential 
market m is not that straightforward, since it intensively can vary within a very extensive range and 
strongly depends on the nature of the product itself as well as the market in which the product is 
launched. Thus, considering that the parameters’ estimates may differ a lot given different EVs’ market 
potential values, a more thorough research should be conducted. Jensen et.al. (2017) approximated M 
==PAGE:27==
19 
 
by setting it equal to half of the car owning families in Denmark of that period and suggested that an 
external dataset should be exploited rather than trying to compute M based on the results of NLS due 
do the existing uncertainty.   
In this part of the estimation, in order to avoid assuming market potential as an absolute number it was 
decided to define it as a percentage. Another argument for not implementing M as part of the estimation 
comes from Jensen et.al. (2017) who stressed the risk of M being insignificant due to market data 
insufficiency. Furthermore, Lilien et.al. (2020) pointed out that NLS tends to underestimate the value 
of M. Thus, it was assumed that M is equal to 100% and thus instead of using period sales in the Bass 
equation, the reported market shares were used instead.  
As already mentioned in the literature review, although the Bass diffusion provided a simple framework 
for illustrating the adoption process and the market penetration of innovative products by the 
population, it still comes with several uncertainties. Specifically speaking, Bass diffusion comes in short 
when the need is to incorporate products’ dynamics, i.e. dynamic substitution dynamics effects and 
patterns. One of the most popular approaches which aims at partially filling this gap is the diffusion 
model suggested by Jun and Park (1999), who suggested a diffusion model combining both diffusion 
and substitution patterns among different products or technologies by integrating the former into the 
alternatives’ utility function. According to them, three assumptions should be fulfilled in order for the 
diffusion effects’ incorporation to hold: 
1. Newer generation products will eventually replace older version completely. 
2. Each transaction corresponds to the buoyance of one unit of product. 
3. Previous choices of similar products don’t affect later choices.

5.1.3 Elasticities 
One of the most useful applications of DCMs is that they enable the analyst to inspect how the demand 
for a certain alternative may change given a change in an attribute of the alternative itself or even of 
another alternative (Rich and Mabit, 2018). Since DCM involves the demand of an alternative being 
measured as a choice probability, it is obvious that a change of one alternative’s attributes will affect 
the shares (choice probabilities) not only of the alternative itself, but the shares of the other alternatives 
as well. The above-mentioned property can be explained via the concept of Elasticity. According to 
Train (2019), the elasticity can be simply defined as the percentage change in the shares of a specific 
alternative given a percentage change in one of the attributes belonging either to the same (direct 
elasticity) or a different alternative (cross elasticity). Apparently, this indicated is of great importance 
within the context of the model’s sensitivity analysis, since it allows the researcher to examine how 
susceptible the model to potential changes is, i.e. how the customers’ choice probabilities (i.e. also their 
decisions) are affected in response to change in some unobserved factors. (Train, 2019).

6. Discussion and Conclusion 
This research has attempted to exploit the principles of discrete choice analysis and diffusion modelling 
in order to investigate how the future demand of EVs in Denmark will be shaped. Since the beginning 
of the current research it was soon realized that the multiple assumptions that had to be made as well as 
the wide range of the different estimation phases would jeopardize the final result.  
The first step in relation to discrete choice analysis was to inspect how different attributes affect the 
customers’ car purchase decision. A wide range of attributes from a past survey were taken into account 
which mainly regarded the technical characteristics of the car types and segments as well as attributes 
related to the existing infrastructure in Denmark. On the contrary, it was decided to leave the socio-
economic and socio-demographic variables (e.g. income, marital status etc.) out of the research, since 
previous studies’ findings indicated that their effect on customers’ preferences were contradictory and 
ambiguous, and they are strongly dependent on the region where the study takes place. One possible 
proposed direction for future work regarding the attributes selection could be to also consider 
geographic attributes in order to distinguish different behaviors and priorities of individuals depending 
e.g. on the city or even the district they live in. Hypothetically, this could be done by using a software 
of Geographical Information Systems (GIS).  
In terms of the executed DCM’s results, the results can be evaluated as plausibly, since all the obtained 
betas had the expected signs. Furthermore, the elasticities for two different scenarios of interest were 
calculated by running two simple simulations, one for each scenario. This was done in order to observe 
how a percentage change in the purchase price of a car type would re-shape the market shares. It would 
also be interesting to investigate the direct and cross elasticities not only across car types but for car 
segments as well with respect to different attributes. Finally, the WTP was also calculated as a mean to 
examine the trade-off between the marginal utility of the attributes and of the purchase price, whereas 
the WTP for boot size wasn’t computed due to suspecting that it’s not easily interpretable.   
The most ambitious goal of the research was to analyze the effect of diffusion in the EVs’ forecasting 
process, since although EVs were typically launched into the market several decades ago, they are still 
seen as an innovative technology which hasn’t yet achieved a rapid growth trend. First, the typical Bass 
diffusion model was applied, using the annual EVs ownership data in Denmark, since after 
consideration it was considered as a more appropriate input compared to the annual sales whose trend 
was fluctuating. The findings of Bass diffusion showed that not much later than 2040 the EVs in 
Denmark will have reached their full market potential and will have achieved a 100% share.  
Since this scenario seems as an extremely optimistic one, it was decided to implement a more extended 
form of diffusion model, which comes from Jun and Park (1999) and was later upgraded to an even 
greater extent by Jensen et.al. (2017). The dominant differences between the simple structure of Bass 
diffusion model and its extended versions is that the innovation and imitation factors of Bass diffusion 
are replaced by a unique factor named diffusion effect and that the mathematical formulation of 
==PAGE:40==
32 
 
diffusion is involved in the utility function. Similarly to Bas diffusion, it was required to set a certain 
value for the EVs’ market potential. In this case, it was decided to specify the market potential 
considering the Denmark’s goal to accomplish the existence of 775,000 EVs by the end of 2030.  
However, the results showed that by the end of 2030 the number of EVs owned by Danish citizens 
won’t significantly exceed the 300,000.  
Overall, due to no prior experience with diffusion models and due the complicated nature of diffusion 
estimation and its many technical details that have to be included, it is highly possible that the current 
research’s results may have not been totally correctly calculated. In either case, some thought and 
consideration about the diffusion implementation will be discussed. First of all, a matter of discussion 
is the time period basis that should be used (months, quarters, years etc.). This diffusion research was 
conducted on a yearly basis; thus, it would be a good idea to study other time intervals as well. It would 
also be interesting to examine if it is more suitable to exploit as input the EVs’ registrations or the EVs’ 
ownership. One other upgrade would be to examine the separation of buyers into first-time buyers and 
not first-time buyers, as indicated by Park and Jun, and calculate the respective probabilities for the two 
categories separately. However, this data weren’t to be found for the Case of Denmark in the first place. 
Furthermore, a direction for further research could involve the examination of how different policies 
and legislations would impact the preferences for EVs by the customers.
