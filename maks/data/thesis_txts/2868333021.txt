Abstract
An accurate prediction of the wave run-up forces on offshore structures are important for a
safe and secure design of the structure and the secondary structures such as boat landings,
ladders, platforms and access doors. The wave run-up forces cause significant damage to
secondary structures, as the design force and associated wave run-up is often greatly under-
estimated.
A new method called the Direct Surface Description (DSD) method in OpenFOAM [16]
has recently been developed by Qwist and Christensen (2022) [19]. The DSD method has
demonstrated significant advantages over the existing official release OpenFOAM solvers
interFoam and interIsoFoam in predicting wave kinematics and surface elevation.
In this thesis, the recently developed DSD solution algorithm in OpenFOAM is utilized to
simulate wave run-up on circular monopiles. The purpose is to validate the use of the DSD
method for regular and extreme waves focusing on a 1-in-50 year occurrence.
The DSD method is validated for regular waves using a selection of experimental results
from Kriebel (1992) [12] and comparing it to interIsoFoam/waveIsoFoam and results from
Christensen and Hansen (2005) [4]. An attempt to validate the DSD method for extreme
wave cases is performed by comparison with Andersen et al. (2010) [2].
The DSD method proved to be effective in the determination of the wave run-up on monopiles.
It outperformed the existing OpenFOAM solver waveIsoFoam in terms of accuracy and com-
putational time for regular waves. The DSD method also proved to be effective in the simu-
lation of irregular waves in a two-dimensional domain, but an irregular wave series was not
simulated in 3D.

Introduction
Construction of offshore wind farms includes the building of large structures such as foun-
dation, pile, and turbine. Smaller structures, called secondary structures, are important
for serviceability. They include boat landings, ladders, platforms, and access doors and are
placed above the water surface. The distance between the water surface end the secondary
structures is determined based on a combination of factors including safe access from ships
and forces from wave run-up. Wave run-up describes how the waves interact with a struc-
ture. An example of this interaction is seen in Figure 1. The pictures are from the Horns
Reef 1 wind farm, with a significant wave height of Hs = 2.5 m, and a platform level 9 m
above still water level (SWL).
Figure 1: Wave run-up at Horns Reef 1 for Hs = 2.5 m, where the platform level is 9.0 m above
SWL.[2]
The forces from the wave run-up cause significant damage to the secondary structures, as
the design force, and associated wave run-up, is often greatly underestimated. Elevating the
platform excessively to mitigate wave impact is not a viable solution, as it compromises the
safe and efficient transfer of personnel or equipment between the ship and the platform. This
solution would most likely require an intermediate platform to access the elevated platform,
leading to the initial problem.
The maximum run-up levels determine the design of the platforms. Additionally, the wave
run-up distribution around the pile is important for the location of entry points. Entry points
are often situated where wave impact is minimal, to ensure operational safety and accessibil-
ity. The entry points are also determined based on the dominant wave direction. Tools like
wave roses, which provide statistical distributions of wave directions, are valuable for iden-
tifying the most frequent wave approach directions. By analyzing these patterns, it may be
possible to determine the optimal location for entry points, balancing safety and practicality.
Determination of the wave run-up is often done using Computational Fluid Dynamic (CFD)
simulations. This project aims to simulate the wave run-up on circular monopiles, using the
newly developed method called the Direct Surface Description (DSD) in OpenFOAM[19]. It
is expected that the DSD method can deliver better, faster, and more accurate results when
compared to the existing official OpenFOAM solver, waveIsoFoam. This is a step towards

An overview of relevant theories, such as diffraction theory and the velocity stagnation head

in section 3.

test conditions for simulations for regular waves based on Kriebel (1992) [12] in 2D and 3D
without a monopile, as well as 3D with a monopile. The test conditions for simulations
for irregular waves with a piston time series based on Andersen et al. (2010) [2] in 2D are
provided, along with test conditions for regular waves with a 1-in-50 year wave height, also

The project aims to validate the DSD method by replicating the wave run-up results from
Kriebel (1992) [12] and Andersen et al. (2010) [2] papers. The project seeks to provide
the results of wave run-up from the validation, which are easy to replicate for future use.
With the validation of the DSD method, the project aims to provide valuable insights and
improvements, in the modeling and prediction of wave forces on secondary structures, for
offshore wind turbines.

Background
This section explores the principles of diffraction and its implications, focusing on linear
and nonlinear diffraction theories, and their applicability to represent the wave run-up on a
cylindrical cylinder.
When an incident wave impinges on the cylinder it will generate reflected and diffracted
waves. The diffracted waves are called scattered waves, which influences the pressure around
the cylinder, thereby influencing the forces on the cylinder. This interaction modifies the
wave profile near the structure, creating a localized increase in wave height called wave run-
up. The forces on the cylinder may be calculated using potential flow theory within the
diffraction regime.[20].
The maximum wave run-up, Rmax, occurs when the incident wave reaches the front of the
cylinder. As the wave propagates around the cylinder, a wave run-up envelope, R(θ), is
formed along the exterior of the cylinder, representing the run-up at all angular positions,
see Figure 2.
Figure 2: Sketch illustrating the interaction of an incident wave with a cylinder, leading to a wave
run-up envelope R(θ) around the cylinder.
In Figure 2 the parameters wave height H, wave period T, water depth h and surface eleva-
tion η are introduced.
As the wave energy redistributes, the diffraction effects strongly depend on the ratio of the
cylinder’s diameter D to the wave length L. For small D/L ratios, the waves largely flow
around the cylinder with minimal disturbance. However, as D/L increases, diffraction ef-
fects become more pronounced, altering the pressure fields and resulting in oscillatory forces.
Inertia forces are decreased as D/L increases for linear diffraction theory.

Linear diffraction theory is generally considered applicable for smaller wave heights, as noted
by De Vos et al. (2007) [6]. However, there are significant limitations when using linear the-
ory to predict wave run-up. De Vos et al. (2007) emphasizes that second-order theories
are necessary to accurately account for run-up, especially for steep waves. Attempts to ex-
trapolate from linear theory often fall short, with measured run-up consistently exceeding
the predictions of linear models. This discrepancy becomes especially pronounced for waves
with high steepness, where the nonlinear effects dominate. Overall the threshold for linear
diffraction theory is D/L > 0.2, meaning that in this range, the scattered wave energy is
negligibly small [6].
The work of Chakrabarti and Tam (1975) [3], as reviewed by Kriebel (1992) [12], provides
one of the most comprehensive data sets on wave run-up in the diffraction regime. Their
experiments demonstrated that for smaller cylinders, the scattering of incident waves was
minimal. As the relative size of the cylinder increased, the run-up distributions changed
markedly, with larger cylinders generally experiencing higher wave run-up. However, these
results were not consistent and in some cases, the measured run-up was significantly lower
than predicted by linear diffraction theory for certain wave numbers.
Studies by Christensen and Hansen (2005) [4] have shown that breaking waves introduces
a non-linearity, which contributes to a larger wave run-up. The analytical expression does
not include the diameter of the cylinder, though diffraction theory states that the diameter
influences the run-up. When the wave steepness approaches the breaking limit, the run-up
can manifest as a vertical jet. This phenomenon occurs because energy and momentum are
highly concentrated at the wave crest, causing the run-up to form as a thin film of water
over the leading half of the cylinder.
2.1
Second-order run-up theory
Kriebel (1992) [12] investigated the nonlinear interaction of Stokes’ waves, with a vertical
circular cylinder on a plane bed, with a primary focus on the phenomenon of wave run-up.
The goal of the study was to provide a more accurate model for predicting wave run-up,
particularly in cases where nonlinear effects are significant, due to the limitations of the
existing theories. The linear diffraction theories tend to underestimate the effects of non-
linear waves, particularly for steep wave conditions. The wave run-up in Kriebel (1992) is
based on a second-order diffraction theory, derived from Stokes’ second-order theory. Many
existing second-order diffraction theories have been proven to have limitations due to failure
to satisfy the second-order boundary conditions.
The theoretical results for second-order wave run-up was compared to 22 laboratory ex-
periments, with monochromatic long-crested waves, at a depth-to-radius ratio h/r = 2.77.
Within the 22 laboratory experiments only 8 of the experiments were within Stokes’ second-
order theory.
The wave run-up envelope around the cylinder was recorded using video
equipment, and crest run-up heights were analyzed at 15◦intervals. Parameters such as
relative cylinder size kr, relative depth kh, where k is the wave number, and wave steepness
H/L were varied across the 22 test cases.
The tests within the validity of Stokes’ second-order theory showed that, non-linear diffrac-

For kH = 0.402 the run-up envelope was smaller than the observed run-up, but could be
improved with a finer grid resolution. Furthermore, it was seen that a sloped bed produced
significantly larger run-ups on the cylinder, due to breaking waves.

at the wave crest are forced to convert their kinetic energy into potential energy, traveling
the distance u2/(2g) up the cylinder. The predicted run-up is seen in Equation 1.

De Vos et al. (2007) [6] investigated the factor m for a cylindrical and cone shaped foundation,
exposed to regular and irregular waves. For the irregular waves, the 2% excess run-up height,
R2%, was used, as it allows for safe design of coastal structures against wave overtopping. For
regular waves, De Vos et al. (2007) found that the theoretical run-up using linear diffraction

the run-up for low wave steepness was overestimated, and the high wave steepness run-

Numerical approach
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is used as analytical solutions are not feasible for all
problems. Instead, numerical methods are employed to simulate complex physical processes.
While CFD results are not always 100% accurate, it is possible to adjust many parameters
to improve precision.
Various methods exist within CFD, and this study uses the open source software Open-
FOAM [16]. Within the OpenFOAM framework two solvers are utilized. The first one is
waveIsoFoam, a well-established solver included in the waves2Foam (W2F) extension [22].
However, waveIsoFoam has certain limitations, particularly in terms of computational speed,
and the accuracy of velocity predictions at the surface.
As a result, there is an ongoing development in solver technologies. The second solver, using
the Direct Surface Description method (DSD) [19], is a newly developed tool specifically

waves2Foam
The toolbox addresses limitations in OpenFOAM’s native wave modeling, including the in-
ability to generate and absorb surface water waves flexibly. The toolbox is open-source and
available through the OpenFOAM-Extend Community, enabling broader use and future en-
hancements [11].
The (W2F) toolbox extends the Reynolds Averaged Navier–Stokes (RANS) equations, cou-
pled with the continuity equation for incompressible flows, and incorporates the Volume of
fluid (VoF) method with ”wave relaxation zones” to improve wave generation and absorption
[11].

3.2
Direct Surface Description
The DSD method removes the air phase, treating it as a void phase with constant pressure.
This leads to reduced computational cost and improved surface velocity accuracy [19].
The DSD method is a VoF approach designed to solve the Navier-Stokes equations for a single
phase, as expressed in Equation 8, alongside the continuity equation previously defined in
Equation 5.
∂u
∂t + ∇· (uu) −∇· (ν∇u) = −1
ρw
∇(p −ρwg · x)
(8)
Where ν is the kinematic viscosity, ρw is the density of water and x is the point coordinate
vector in space. This approach reduces the degrees of freedom in the simulation, resulting
in faster computational times compared to traditional two-phase solvers such as interFoam
and interIsoFoam. Boundary conditions are imposed directly on the free surface [19].
The relative pressure at the free surface is described based on Equation 9. As an approxi-
mation, the atmospheric pressure at the free surface is set to a constant value of p = 0, since
the pressure gradients in air are negligible compared to those in water. Furthermore, the
surface tension is expected to have minimal impact, and is therefore neglected. The dynamic
boundary condition is satisfied through a Dirichlet condition for the total pressure, meaning
that the solution to the boundary is fixed.
prgh,fs = −ρwg · xfs
(9)
Where prgh,fs is the relative pressure field in cell centers on the free surface and xfs is the
distance from a specified point or level to the free surface.
When only the water phase is considered, deriving a free surface boundary condition for ve-
locity is not feasible. While the motion of the free surface should correspond to the velocity
at the free surface, this does not provide a definitive boundary condition. Instead, linear
spatial extrapolation from internal cell centers, is used to compute velocities at face and cell
centers outside the water domain, as required for discretization [19].
Two variants of the DSD method are implemented using explicit Runge-Kutta schemes [19]
to solve differential equations. The variants are the DSD-RK2 and DSD-RK4, which are using
second-order explicit Runge–Kutta and fourth order explicit Runge–Kutta time integration
schemes, respectively. According to Qwist and Christensen (2022), DSD-RK4 demonstrates
superior accuracy in capturing free surface dynamics and velocity profiles.
The removal
of the air phase significantly reduces the computational domain and associated computa-
tional cost. Relative simulation times for different methods highlight the efficiency of DSD
methods. DSD-RK2 serves as the baseline, and is the fastest among tested solvers. DSD-RK4 is
approximately 1.5 times slower than DSD-RK2. In comparison, interFoam and interIsoFoam
are 2 to 3 times slower than DSD methods, due to the inclusion of the air phase and more
complex interface handling. Additionally, the DSD method significantly reduces velocity
oscillations near the free surface compared to interFoam and interIsoFoam.

Furthermore, the DSD method achieves stable convergence at higher Courant-Friedrichs-
Lewy (CFL) numbers, approximately C =∼0.1, compared to interFoam and interIsoFoam,
which require C < 0.05. This indicates that the DSD method performs better with larger
time steps [19].
Overall based on Qwist and Christensen (2022) the DSD method is computationally faster
than interFoam and interIsoFoam, due to the exclusion of the air phase and optimized
discretization schemes. It also exhibits smoother and more accurate wave surface elevation
profiles.

This ensures that

case, a single force probe is utilized to encompass the entire platform.
The forces function object computes and outputs the forces and moments acting on the
defined patches, specifically the platform. These calculations are based on the pressures and
viscous stress’ acting on the patches. However, in this scenario, there are no viscous stress
affecting the platform. The forces and moments are resolved along the x, y and z directions.
4.4
Gridding strategy
A grid is made on the basis of the function blockMesh, which can be used in 2D and
3D. The 3D computational mesh was constructed using the snappyHexMesh approach. This
approach identifies the intersections between a structures’ geometry and the hexahedral cells,
and removes the cells enclosed by the geometry. The structures’ geometry is defined from a
triangulated surface geometry in Stereolithography format (STL).

Future perspectives
This section will cover subjects which future projects of a similar nature could incorporate.
The DSD method and the solver DSD-RK2 has been investigated for regular wave cases. Fur-
ther work with the method could provide more insights and improvements in the modeling
and prediction of wave forces on secondary structures for offshore wind turbines.
Firstly, the representation of the waves could be finer, at least for the base mesh. This could
improve the results for both DSD-RK2 and waveIsoFoam. This will of course require longer
computational times, but it could serve as a check of the results presented in section 6.
Alternatively, adding wave gauges on the side of the flume in the range x = [6.5 m, 8.5 m],
could contribute to undisturbed wave height measurements, which might improve the results.
As mentioned, the goal of running pistonTimeSeries in 3D with a monopile was not ac-
complished due to limited time, and late discoveries. This is a goal worth pursuing, as it
could provide more accurate results for the design run-up height, R2%. Firstly, it is rec-
ommended to use a better sampling frequency for the piston signal, as 20 Hz turned out
to be insufficient. Secondly, it is recommended to test how the simulations run in 3D with
pistonTimeSeries for an irregular wave time series. Lastly, it is recommended to expand
the domain to an actual sea state. This can be done using a typical North Sea case using
publicly available metocean data from the Dutch projects.
Further work and processing of force data from a platform is needed, to better estimate the
impact force from the associated run-up. This requires a look into how the DSD method
calculates the pressure on the platform. Adding porosity to the platform is a step towards
making the simulation represent a real platform better. This is also a step towards making
the platforms on offshore wind turbines safer.

Conclusion
The aim of the project was to provide valuable insights and improvements, in the modeling
and prediction of wave forces on secondary structures for offshore wind turbines.
The DSD method was validated by replicating the wave run-up results from Kriebel (1992)
using the DSD method and waveIsoFoam. The wave run-up on the monopile results from the
simulations were compared to the experimental and theoretical results from 5 cases presented
in Kriebel (1992). Case 1-3 have the same wave length, and increasing wave height. Case
4 and 5 have short wave length, and an increase in wave height. In general waveIsoFoam
overestimated the run-up compared to both the experimental data and second-order run-up
theory provided by Kriebel (1992), expect for case 5. The wave run-up from DSD-RK2 was
closer to that of Kriebel (1992).
The difference in wave lengths were evident, as case 1-3 overestimated the run-up compared
to the experimental data from Kriebel (1992). Whereas, case 4-5 underestimated the wave
run-up. In general case 3-5 came within a 10% deviation of the experimental results, while
case 1-2 were close to this limit. When comparing the wave run-up from DSD-RK2 with the
second-order run-up theory provided by Kriebel (1992), these were within the 10% deviation
range, for all cases but case 2. It was also noted that the results from Kriebel (1992) may
be inaccurate, as the wave generation might be based on first-order wave generation. This
means that the wave kinematics may not be accurately described, potentially leading to
lower run-up values than that predicted by second-order run-up theory for longer waves.
In general, the DSD-RK2 solver provided better results than waveIsoFoam. The discrepan-
cies have been attributed to spurious velocities that occur at the wave crest with the use
of waveIsoFoam. The spurious velocities come from the interaction with air, which is the
phase that is removed in the DSD method. It was expected that the DSD method provided
better, more accurate and faster results compared to waveIsoFoam. This was proven to be
correct. The DSD-RK2 proved to be 1.2-4 times faster than waveIsoFoam, which is also due
to the air phase being removed in the DSD method.
One of the aims of the project was to validate the DSD method for irregular waves. This val-
idation was not completed, as the pistonTimeSeries gave some issues during the project.
This was due to a wrong use of boundary conditions. The pistonTimeSeries was vali-
dated in 2D for irregular waves, and a validation in 3D is next in line. A validation of
pistonTimeSeries with regular waves in 3D was done.
In an attempt to provide some validation of the DSD method for irregular waves, a selection
of the Andersen et al. (2010) experiments were chosen focusing on a 1-in-50-year occurrence.
The validation was done using regular waves with a wave height, H2%, for 4 cases, namely
case 6-9. The shape of the wave run-up envelope on the monopile compared well with that
of Andersen et al. (2010).
A comparison of the run-up measured at the front of the monopile with a calculated run-up,
using the velocity stagnation head theory, provided good results for case 6 and 7. However,
as the wave height increased, the measured wave run-up became more underestimated. The

deviations were attributed to the method of using H2%, which does not necessarily corre-
spond to the 2% excess run-up, R2%. In addition to this, no slope was introduced in the
simulated domain, which was the case in Andersen et al. (2010).
A platform was added, to provide insight into the forces affecting the platform from the as-
sociated wave run-up. These impact forces are often difficult to estimate. The DSD method
does not conserve the volume in cells near the surface, leading to significant non-physical
spikes in force measurements and necessitating the smoothing of the obtained data. Using
the momentum equation, it was possible to relate the velocities derived from the measured
impact forces to the velocity stagnation head theory, to verify the order of magnitude of the
velocities. With the assumption that the impact force only affects 20% of the platform, the
measured velocities corresponded well with the stagnation head velocities for case 8 and 9.
For case 7, the measured velocity was underestimated, which was due to the smaller wave
height, which hardly reached the platform.
The location of the smallest wave run-up was also found to be in the range 45◦-75◦(300◦-
315◦). These locations would serve as an optimal place for the entry points on the platform
in terms of safety.
In conclusion, the DSD method has proven to be effective in the determination of the wave
run-up on monopiles. It out performs the existing OpenFOAM solver waveIsoFoam in terms
of accuracy and computational time for regular waves. It has proven to be versatile in terms
of mesh fineness and the stability of simulations. The DSD method also proved to be effective
in the simulation of irregular waves in a 2D domain, but an irregular wave series was not
simulated in 3D

Investigation of parameters for case setup
An investigation of the parameters used in the case setup has been made. The investigation
included boundary conditions, two different solvers, DSD-RK2 and waveIsoFoam, aspect ratios
(AR), the CFL number and the influence of the setting adjustTimeStep. The investigations
have been made in 2D.
B.1
Wave flume case set-up
The simulations were conducted in a flume with a total length of 29.17 m and a height of
0.8 m, featuring a relaxation zone at each end. A wave height of H = 0.15 m, period of

C.1.2
Dimensionless run-up using ηmax
In this section the measured run-up is determined based on ηmax instead of H/2, as the crest
height is significantly larger than trough, due to non-linearity of the waves. The Kriebel
(1992) and Christensen and Hansen (2005) results are still based on H/2. Additionally, no
surface elevation data is provided for these. Kriebel (1992) based his experiments on Stoke’s
second-order theory, and as mentioned, only 8 out of 22 experiments fell within the theory’s
applicability. Based on Figure 6, only one of the five cases in this study is expected to fall
within this regime.
The results in Figure 61 and 62 for DSD-RK2 and waveIsoFoam, are not directly compara-
ble between Kriebel’s experimental data, second-order diffraction theory or Christensen and
Hansen. The comparison is however done in an attempt to see if the data would fit better,
using ηmax. This would be the best way to compare run-up envelopes, as using ηmax to
normalize the run-up, is more accurate for higher order waves.
In Figure 61, the measured dimensionless wave run-up envelope using ηmax for case 1-3 is
presented. It can be observed that the run-up envelope for DSD-RK2 is reduced and aligns
well with Kriebel’s experimental data. Whereas, for waveIsoFoam, the run-up envelope for
case 1 remains over predicted, but for case 2 and 3, the envelope is more similar to Kriebel’s
experimental data and second-order run-up theory.
