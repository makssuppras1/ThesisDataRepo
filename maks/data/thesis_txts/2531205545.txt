Abstract 
Turning an idea into a business concept requires contemplating how and when the product 
or service should be marketed, the value of the product or service, the distinction between it 
and similar goods and distribution methods. The main stimulus of this thesis is to examine 
the business feasibility of a new informal value transfer (IVTS) system as a fintech 
entrepreneur. The proposed IVTS is named FinMatch in this study and is presented as an 
alternative system or mechanism for the transfer of remittances from immigrants. The 
implementation of a matchmaking system is a core idea and an innovative part of FinMatch. 
The Iranian diaspora in Denmark is selected as the key case in the research and data was 
collected using both qualitative and quantitative research methods. The findings show that 
FinMatch provides an efficient flow of remittances based on market, product, financial and 
legislative analysis. 
Key Words: Remittance, Entrepreneurship, Fintech, Business model, feasibility analysis, IVTS

CHAPTER 1. 
INTRODUCTION 
1.1. Background and Motivation 
Money transfers from people working 
abroad are a developmental lifeline for 
many countries. If migrants take their 
earnings home in the form of either cash 
or goods to benefit their families, these 
payments are known as workers or 
migrant remittances. Over the past few 
years, remittances have developed steadily and now constitute the growing source of foreign 
income for plenty of developing countries. 
Financial remittances were identified as a significant migration-related growth mechanism. 
Economic remittance flows rose gradually from the 1990s to the present day. Nonetheless, 
aside from financial remittances, transnational societies often contribute by 'social 
remittances' -the exchange of expertise, information, ideas and values that migrants send 
home.  
Taken together, financial and social remittances play a significant role in achieving individual 
family aspirations, regional and national growth agendas, and achieving SDGs1 more broadly. 
Nevertheless, the relatively high cost of remittances must be reduced in order to benefit from 
the actual growth gains of financial and Social remittances. (Swing, 2018) 
Many immigrants have to use informal value transfer systems (IVTS) because of the high cost 
of remittances through their conventional banking system. In addition, there are further set

1.2. Research Questions 
Informal value transferring is generally an alternative or a substitute for conventional banking 
system. Despite existing traditional IVTS like hawala, in some circumstances, the need for a 
new digital IVTS is evident. The new system must work independent or parallel from formal 
value transferring system and at the same time has ability to integrate with digital multimedia 
devices. Combination of these two capability may create a faster and cheaper system 
compare to formal financial institutions in value transferring. Based on this background 
(problem statement), the following research objective arises: 
Explore feasibility of the new IVTS with matchmaking by developing a suitable business 
model. 
This main research objective was divided into the following sub-questions: 
1. For whom does the new IVTS create value and who are the main customers?  
2. What bundle of values does the new IVTS deliver to customers and which one of the 
customer’s problem it going to solve?  
3. Is employing matchmaking algorithms for the new IVTS feasible?  
4. How does the IVTS with matchmaking systems can take place outside of the 
conventional banking system in international market? 
5. What regulatory restrictions are existing for IVTS? 
1.3. Purpose of the Research  
The overall aim of this thesis is to investigate the feasibility of a new IVTS in format of a 
financial technology (fintech). Basically, fintech combines finance and technology to provide 
a variety of new services to businesses and consumers. Traditional financial institutions like 
banks have provided most of the services fintech firms offer to date. 
==PAGE:8==
  
 
 
 
The new IVTS is named FinMatch for this thesis. The main value of FinMatch for users is to 
transfer funds between countries without changing currency, and to provide ways of 
improving remittance flow. The matching algorithms connect users from various countries 
and currencies that need to send the same amount of funds but to the opposite destination. 
FinMatch will at all times demonstrate its commitment to SDGs by addressing two of the 17 
goals in the united nation’s 2030 agenda for Sustainable Development as below: 
• 
SDG indicator 17.3.2: Increasing the volume of remittances as a percentage of gross 
domestic product (GDP); 
• 
SDG indicator 10.c.1: Reducing the cost of remittances. 
The genesis of this thesis can be traced back to the time when I struggled to transfer money 
to my homeland, Iran, like many other Iranian students abroad and immigrants in the post-
sanction era (The United States economic, trade, scientific and military sanctions against Iran 
in 2017). In this unusual situation, finding a legal, cheap and swift solution to remittances 
would improve and make life easier for many of my people.

2.2. Informal value transfer system (IVTS) 
Money or value transfer system (MVTS) under the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) refers 
to financial services, involving acceptance of cash, check, other monetary instruments or 
other stores and, through communication, message, transfer or clearance networks to which 
the MVTS provider provides a recipient, in cash or other form. The transactions carried out in 
==PAGE:10==
  
 
 
 
these areas may include, and include new methods of payment, one or more intermediaries 
and final payments to a third party. (FATF, 2016) 
The transfer of value or money may be made via various channels. Channel selection depends 
on various factors such as value amount, geography, domestic / international, transfer 
destination, currency of value, official / informal and so on. 
Formal value transfer system (FVTS) refers to mechanisms or services provided for the 
transfer of funds or equal value to a different geographical location by a financial institution 
known as bank institution. (Siklos, 2001) In the framework of a formal value transfer system, 
a global network is set up for financial institutions to send and receive information from each 
financial transaction. This network system is SWIFT (Society for Worldwide Interbank 
Financial Telecommunications) which provides a secure, standard and reliable platform for 
more than 11,000 institutions and more than 200 countries. (SWIFT, 2019) 
In contrast to FVTS, Informal value transferring system (IVTS) refers to any system, 
mechanism or network that could replace formal value transferring. Several definitions of 
IVTS have been proposed. Carroll (1999) called this system an “alternative banking” and 
defines it a “system which is subject to no external auditing, control or supervision, whereby 
money or value can be transferred from one country to another”. This definition is close to 
those of Schaap & Mul (1995) who define IVTS as “a commercial activity involving the transfer 
of money across national borders through a non-bank institution or organization”. This 
definition is verified by Akse (1996) but he argues that underground banking is not necessarily 
come out in order to involve criminal money transferring. He then adds important additions 
to Schaap & Mul’s definition as the following: 
• The transaction cannot be based on any commercial operation (i.e. money sent to a 
relative who studies or visits foreign countries).   
• Sometimes it is not cash, it is products or other things, like money, electric equipment 
or computer. Some people in the US believe that in the Netherlands diamonds will play 
an important role.  
• Transactions can also be domestic; they are not necessarily international.  
• Often, no agency or entity is involved in the move. It can be as simple as two people, a 
family or a small network of individuals. 
==PAGE:11==
  
 
 
 
But bureau of the U.S. Department of the Treasury (FinCEN, 2003) have provided a 
precise and comprehensive definition of IVTS :  
An ‘Informal value transfer system’ refers to any method, process or network of people 
receiving money for the aim of making the resources or equivalent value payable to a third 
party in another geographic area, whether in the same form or otherwise. Transfers are 
typically carried out outside the traditional banking system through non-bank financial 
institutions or other business entities whose primary business activity may not be money 
transfers. The IVTS transactions interconnect periodically with formal banking structures. 
IVTS, like so-called alternate remittance and underground banking networks, operated as far 
back as 5800 BC and pre-dates Western banking systems. The systems were originally 
established in Asia as a means of settling accounts with in villages on the Indian subcontinent. 
As expatriates from the original countries settled abroad, their use as global networks for 
financial transactions spread as. Today, operations with IVTS are found in most countries. 
Depending on the ethnic group, IVTS is called by a variety of names, such as 'hawala' (Middle 
East, Afghanistan, Pakistan); 'hundi' (India); 'fei ch'ien' (China); 'phoe kuan' (Thailand); and 
'Black Market Peso Exchange' (South America) 
In a following up the investigation, FinCEN Advisory (2003) describes that, there are two types 
of IVTS – a conventional system with similar features and procedures to those used centuries 
ago; and a new or contemporary system that often intersects with clients and accounts in 
formal financial institutions. The procedure can be very simple or complex depending on the 
form of IVTS. 
2.2.1. 
Traditional IVTS 
In Four participants are needed in a simple or conventional IVTS transaction (such as hawala): 
a sender of the funds, a recipient of the funds, and IVTS operators in the sender and recipient 
countries. Figure 1 provides an example of a simple IVTS transfer where person one in 
Country A wants to give money to person two in Country B. 
Step #1: Person one gives IVTS operator currency in Country A. 
Step #2: In Country A, IVTS provides a code or other identification mechanism to Person one.  
Step #3: Country A IVTS operator notifies their Country B equivalent by telephone, fax or e-
mil of the amount of the transaction to be paid to Person two and the code. 
Step #4: Person one will contact the recipient, Person two, in Country B, and will provide that 
==PAGE:12==
  
 
 
 
individual with the code. 
Step #5: Person two goes to Country B IVTS, provides the correct code, and collects the funds 
it gets. 
 
Figure 1: A traditional and basic IVTS operation is illustrated. Figure inspired by: FinCEN Advisory (2003) 
The IVTS provider charges a fixed fee, commission or benefit from the difference in exchange 
rates between the official and the black market price in Country B.  
In reality, no money is being transferred. Country A's IVTS operator uses senders ' money to 
"stock" its cash supply for use in potential payments for incoming requests. The opposite 
happens in Country B, where the cash collected by the IVTS operator is generated by the 
sender in his country. Account settlement can be achieved using other methods, such as: 
•  The physical transfer of currency by courier between the operators across borders. 
• Usage of the accrued currency to buy quickly moving goods, which are then exported, 
then sold. 
• Cash obtained from the sale shall be made available to the second operator of the 
IVTS; as payment for products to be traded; through the smuggling or selling of gold 
and precious stones, i.e. diamonds or tanzanite; or through the handling of invoices 
(overcharging or undercharging of goods or services).

2.2.2. 
Modernised IVTS 
A modernized or modern IVTS activity usually requires using conventional financial 
institutions including banks to complete the process. As part of a cash intensive business, a 
company owner may offer IVTS services such as a convenience store, supermarket, cafe, 
import / export firm, travel agency, etc. The owners accept money from their "clients" to pass 
to individuals in other countries in addition to conducting their daily business. The owner of 
the company may not be allowed by the government to act as a money transmitter and/or 
may not be licensed with FinCEN as a money services business (MSB). 
The initial method in modernised IVTS is identical to a conventional IVTS transition in steps 1 
to 5. The operator's earnings are close. The account settling process is therefore different. 
The money earned by the IVTS is combined with the funds created by the daily company and 
deposited on the local bank's commercial or personal account. The company owner then 
directly sends a wire transference from his account to the IVTS operator in Country B for 
money owing for the various IVTS transactions after a period over which several IVTS 
transactions collect funds (see figure 2). Alternatively, the funds could be first transferred to 
financial institutions accounts in IVTS hub regions (for example, United Arab Emirates 
(U.A.E.), London, New York or Hong Kong). Ultimately, funds are provided to the IVTS 
operator in Country B either through a subsequent wire transfer to an account on the bank 
or through withdrawal and delivery to the operator of the money. 
 
Figure 2 :Modernised IVTS operation. Figure inspired by: FinCEN Advisory (2003) 
==PAGE:14==
  
 
 
 
Passas (1999) identifies the reasons for the use of IVTS. He highlights that the purpose is more 
traditional and cultural even if there is not any emergence and development in using IVTS.  
Passas (1999) lists the main reasons for using IVTS as the following: 
• Inability to trust traditional banks. 
• Inability to enter the modern banking network. 
• Local bank has no way of sending money abroad. 
• Ineffective, inefficient, cumbersome, unfriendly financial system.  
• Lack of tax system integrity  
• Actual or alleged desire to circumvent the government's regulations. 
• Cultural reasons.   
• Lower prices. 
• Quicker delivery. 
• To prevent monitoring and to defend properties from nationalization. 
• Compensation for surveillance agents overseas.  
• Money regulation bypass. 
• Illegal motives. 
Passas (1999) also identifies four characteristics of IVTS; 
• There may be no actual commercial practice (i.e. the money sent to a friend who 
is studying or traveling abroad). 
• Often it is not currency, but goods or other transactions such as jewellery, 
electronics or computer equipment. 
• Transactions can not only be international; they may also be regional. 
• Often, no agency or entity moves it. This can be as easy as two people, a family or 
a small people's network. 
FinCEN Advisory (2003) identifies user of IVTS as Individuals, businesses, and even 
governments to transfer money at home and abroad. Expatriates and immigrants also use 
IVTS in their home countries to transfer money back to their families and friends. IVTS 
operations are often used by legal businesses, merchants and government departments, who 
have little or insufficient structured financial system operations in countries. 
This advisory then lists a more comprehensive reasons for using IVTS as the following: 
1) Political instability, ineffective payment structures and/or a dysfunctional financial sector 
within a country;  
==PAGE:15==
  
 
 
 
2) A lack of formal financial institutions which are easily accessible in remote areas of some 
countries;  
3) Transfers which are more effective, reliable and cheaper than formal banking institutions. 
(For instance, bank wire transfers include fees charged to the sender and recipient, may 
take few days to complete, and may be delayed or lost. Funds transferred through IVTS 
are accessible within 24 hours, with minimum or no fees charged to the participants.);  
4) Avoid paying higher currency exchange rates. (Funds sent through conventional 
transactions shall be exchanged to the currency of the recipient country; the fee paid for 
the conversion of the exchange rate shall be determined by the institution. IVTS operators, 
who trade on currency exchange rates, shall charge lower fees.);  
5) To escape controls on currency documentation. (Many governments, has set monetary 
reporting requirements for financial institutions. Some emigrants and people mistrust 
governments and seek to avoid some monitoring of their financial activities.); 
6) To avoid tax payment;  
7) To guarantee anonymity because there may be limited or no documents maintained; in 
other words, there is no paper trail.  
2.3. Digital Business Model  
The business model is described at the most basic level solely in terms of the economic model. 
The problem is the definition of income generation. Source income, pricing strategies, cost 
ratios, margins and expected volumes are main decision variables. 
Hence, Stewart & Zhao (2000) approach the model as “a statement of how a firm will make 
money and sustain its profit stream over time.” The model represents an architectural 
configuration at the operational level. The focus is on internal processes and infrastructure 
design that allow the company to generate value. Decision factors include ways of generating 
or providing programs, operating procedures, distribution of resources, control of 
information and strategic streams. 
==PAGE:16==
  
 
 
 
Mayo & Brown (1999) refer to “the design of key interdependent systems that create and 
sustain a competitive business.” Strategic concepts stress the general trajectory of the 
business positioning of the product, relationships around corporate boundaries, and 
opportunities for growth. Economic benefit and profitability are of interest. Decision 
components include selection of partners, meaning development, alignment, vision, 
principles, networks and partnerships. 
Slywotzky (1996) refers to “the totality of how a company selects its customers, defines and 
differentiates its offerings, defines the tasks it will perform itself and those it will outsource, 
configures its resources, goes to market, creates utility for customers and captures profits.” 
Osterwalder & Pigneur (2010) offer a more comprehensive definition of business model. They 
define a business model as “the rationale of how an organization creates, delivers, and 
capture value”. They also believe that a business model works as a template for implementing 
strategy through organizational structures, processes, and systems.  
Gantz & Reinsel (2012) report that data are massively transferred from a large number of 
transactions made by millions of clients and organisations across the ecosystems of the 
organisation every month, on a daily and hourly basis. 
And, Aagaard (2019) believes that these data can be used more efficiently by businesses to 
attract consumers, make smarter pricing choices and predict competition, as well as to 
improve assortments, production and logistics.. 
The term ‘second economy’ is used by Arthur (2011) to refer to a broad, automatic and 
invisible economy that created by digitalization and bringing about the biggest social change 
since the Industrial Revolution. In this type of economy, digitization affects whole 
ecosystems, their business models (BMs) and the basic business functions of a supply chain 
company. (Aagaard 2019).  
The U.S. Mobile App Report (ComScore, 2016) exposes that total digital media time spent has 
exploded since 2013. The report reveals 53% grown in time spent and 111% increase in 
mobile app usage in the three years. The share of mobile apps in total mobile usage, based 
on the same report, was 87% in 2016.  
By taking that fact into account, Weill & Woerner (2018) state that businesses need to 
improve their digital business models in the way they digitally reach their consumers and 
==PAGE:17==
  
 
 
 
generate interest through platforms like blogs and mobile apps. They also argue that if your 
company does not have a strong digital experience, many customers, especially young 
people, will move to industry competitors or do more business with companies that provide 
excellent digital customer experience, operate in adjacent industries and start offering similar 
services. 
For a traditional physical BM that relies on place and people, it’s critical and challenging to 
change to a great digital business model (DBM). The challenge, according to Weill & Woerner 
(2018), appears in three areas: internal power, business process and customer data. Given 
that challenge, Weill & Vitale (2001) define three components for a DBM: content, customer 
experience and platform. They also argue that for creating a convincing customer value 
proposition, all these components must work together.  
2.4. Digital Business Model Framework 
Different business model frameworks are presented by several authors to explore and 
visualize what a DBM contains. Hulme  (2011) answers why it’s important to visualise our BM:  
“The first is that you need to think about your BM. In reality, this is what makes you learn 
about and express different facets. The second is that it allows one to take full account of BM. 
It is also a challenge to dive straight into the marketing channels and forget about anything 
else. However, all facets of the BM are interrelated and we need to be able to take the entire 
cycle into account. Finally, if we build a common language around BM, it will make it possible 
for everyone to participate in debate and creativity.” 
The most popular and commonly used BM framework is the Business Model Canvas (BMC) 
by Osterwalder & Pigneur (2010). By means of this graphical description of the BM content, 
the BMC provides a summary, contrast and growth outlook. It is an industry practice and is 
recognized as a simple path to creativity in the business model. The canvas consists of nine 
blocks arranged in a rectangular layer and the blocks are individually and linearly analysed. 
Osterwalder & Pigneur (2010) argue that The costumer segment is a BM core and the 
organisation will therefore be designed to meet the needs of a single customer segment. The 
whole right side of the canvas is for value, where efficiency is the left side. 
==PAGE:18==
  
 
 
 
However, BMC is a user friendly framework and contents some lean start-up elements, but 
Aagaard (2018) points out that the model does not cover the technological aspect of BM and 
neither look at data as a specific value. In addition, BMC has provided a basis on the 
assumption that the customer needs are recognised which may not be an appropriate case 
for a new DBM.  
Another major criticism of BMC, made by Maurya (2012), is that Osterwalder & Pigneur 
(2010) fail to make BMC actionable and unable to guide and navigate an entrepreneur to find 
the way from an idea to build a successful start-up. Maurya (2012) because of this, attempted 
to present Lean Canvas (LC) which was designed for entrepreneurs. He substituted four blocks 
of the original template BMC to cover some issues he missed in BMC: 
1. Problem: Maurya would first want entrepreneurs to discover the real challenge 
and create the right product or service and solve it. He says people generally try 
to build what they're trying to build, but it's just the wrong thing.  
2.  Solution: Entrepreneurs should consider the best solution to the problem as a 
simple counterpart. The approach also motivates the entrepreneur, because it's 
his baby. 
3. Key Metrics: It's very similar to key actions / activities. The author needs to guide 
the entrepreneur on the few KPIs he has to keep in mind to deal with. 
4. Unfair Advantage: Maurya also proposes an unfair / competitive advantage box. 
This means the one thing that your company cannot easily replicate. This point is 
definitely not at the very beginning. 
Gierej (2017) compared different aspects of BMC and LC. He reports that LC focuses strongly 
on the accuracy of the customer's needs. It begins with the description of the question or 
need (1) and then identifies possible consumer segments (2) to test the theories presumed. 
The next step is to define a new value proposition on the basis of knowledge or to redefine 
the problem. After consumer value assessment, research on the development of solutions 
begins. Gierej (2017) justifies this proposed workflow which most digital businesses collapse 
because before producing the product they do not verify the needs of their customers. The

2.5. Feasibility Analyses 
The goals of a feasibility analysis explored by Georgakellos & Macris (2009) as develop and 
implement the ideas of an entrepreneur into actual business processes, products or services; 
define the strengths and weaknesses of an organization and its competitors. They determine 
other purposes of a feasibility analysis as the following: 
• Develop a strategy to further business growth;  
• Create guidelines for the operation of a business;  
• Helping the entrepreneur to raise funds from lenders or investors. 
Therefore, Georgakellos & Macris (2009) Offer a written summary of the company and the 
related financial details which can be separated into four different sections:  
• Marketing Action Plan,  
• Plan of Operations,  
• Project of execution of the project, and  
• Financing strategy. 
The approach suggested by Georgakellos & Macris (2009) is similar to that found by Barringer 
& Ireland (2018). Both studies share a number of key components and dimensions of a 
business viability for a successful entrepreneurship. 
According to a definition provided by Barringer (2015) , the feasibility analysis is the method 
of assessing whether a business idea is viable. They provide a framework for carrying out a 
feasibility study by identifying the four key areas: product/service feasibility, industry/target 
market feasibility, organizational feasibility, and financial feasibility. If one or more of the four 
elements of the feasibility study fall short of a business idea, it should be dropped or 
reconsidered. 
Barringer (2015) agrees that many entrepreneurs make the mistake of recognizing a business 
idea and then leap immediately to build a company to illustrate and obtain support for the 
idea. This sequence often omits or provides little time for an important step in assessing the 
==PAGE:20==
  
 
 
 
viability of a business idea. He suggests, Therefore, for a complete feasibility analysis, both 
primary and secondary studies are required. Primary research is a study conducted by the 
person or persons who complete the analysis. This usually involves talking to prospective 
customers, receiving input from industry experts, conducting focus groups, and conducting 
surveys. The secondary analysis tests the data that has already been collected. The data 
usually includes market studies, data from the Census Bureau, analyst forecasts, and other 
related information collected through library and Internet research. 
Comparison of the methods imply us to follow Barringer (2015)’s approach as an outline to 
assess the business model.  
Following the literature review, an appropriate feasibility assessment method was selected 
to evaluate whether the business model make sense from product, market, regulation and 
financial standpoint. In this section, the four components of feasibility analysis are described 
and subsequently argued how each component could be determining as follows: 
2.5.1. 
Product/Service Feasibility Analysis  
The feasibility study of the product and service is an evaluation of the overall appeal of the 
proposed product or service. While there are many important things to consider when 
starting a new enterprise, nothing else matters if the product or service itself does not sell. 
There are two elements to the feasibility study of the product/service: desirability and 
demand.  
Desirability and demand of a product or service will be evaluated by talking to industry expert 
and potential customer to declare that the business idea is desirable or the service solves any 
problem and serve any need in marketplace. The chief idea is to make sure product/service 
is what the potential customers desire before rushing to development.  
This approach is associated with “get out of the building” approach that used by Lean Start-
up and called customer development Barringer (2015) (Ries, 2011). Following this section 
lean start-up method was investigated. 
A tool that suggested by Barringer (2015) to analysis desirability and demand is concept 
statement which include one-page initial description of the product/service idea to solicit the 
industry experts and potential customer’s feedback.  
==PAGE:21==
  
 
 
 
Rather than developing a concept statement, in this feasibility analysis, a “minimum viable 
product” (MVP) is created to present the preliminary product/service idea. Besides that, a 
combination of quantitative and qualitative case study approaches was used to improve 
customer development evaluation. 
2.5.2. 
Industry/Target Market Feasibility  
Industry / target market feasibility is an evaluation of the potential attractiveness of the 
industry and of the target market for the proposed product or service. Most companies, and 
definitely ambitious start-ups, usually do not seek to represent the entire industry. 
Alternatively, they pick or carve out a limited target audience and aim to represent that 
community of consumers especially well. There are two components to the industry / target 
market feasibility analysis: the attractiveness of the sector and the target market 
attractiveness. 
This analysis includes recording the attractiveness factors of the industry / target market, 
such as profit margin, life cycle, growth rather than shrinkage, operating margin, age of the 
industry, etc. The response will be if there is a demand for the business concept or whether 
it can be sold at fair rates. 
2.5.3. 
Organizational Feasibility Analysis  
Organisational feasibility analysis shall be performed to assess if the proposed company has 
adequate management experience, operational competence and capital to be successfully 
launched. There are two primary issues to be addressed in this area: management skills and 
resource adequacy. 
Organizational feasibility was tested by proving management prowess by exposing that the 
solo entrepreneur or the management team has passion to the start-up. In addition, showing 
managerial and social skill of the manager and the potential new-venture team (founders, 
key employees and advisors) should prove organizational feasibility. 
2.5.4. 
Financial Feasibility Analysis  
Financial feasibility analysis is the final aspect of a systematic feasibility study. Preliminary 
financial evaluation is generally adequate for a feasibility analysis; however, further rigour is 
normally not needed at this level, as the details of the market will eventually change, making 
==PAGE:22==
  
 
 
 
it impractical to invest a lot of time in the early stages of preparing detailed financial forecasts.  
The most critical things to consider at this point are the total cash start-up required, the 
financial performance of comparable businesses and the overall financial feasibility of the 
proposed company.  
2.5.5. 
Legislation Feasibility Analysis 
A feasibility analysis is carried out by the company to assess if a specific action makes sense 
from an economic and organizational point of view. Such a report is typically intended to 
provide a summary of the main issues relevant to the course of action being considered. The 
aim is to test the viability of the proposed course of action and to locate any "make or break" 
problems that would argue against the action being taken or imply that a positive result will 
be impossible. 
According to APMG (2016), in order to determine the legal viability of the project, the legal 
due diligence process should involve at least three essential steps, as listed below: 
The first step is to evaluate the legal system in place. This involves the identification and 
review of applicable legislation and regulations that may have an impact on the project. The 
second step is to determine the legal preparation of the contracting authority. The third 
phase is an in-depth legal review of the key issues of the project.

3.1. Lean Start-up Method 
The Lean Startup describes a scientific approach for setting up and releasing new products. 
Eric Ries (2011) believes that start-up exist is not just build a product and make money. 
Therefore, he describes the fundamental activities of a start-up as turning ideas into 
products, measuring how customers respond and then learning whether to pivot or 
persevere. 
Blank (2013) explained three key principles of lean start-up as follows: 
First, summary of all untested hypotheses on the business idea by the founder in a business 
model framework such as BMC instead of having been involved in planning and research for 
months.  
Second, using the “get out of the building” approach which called customer development. 
This principle tests all unexploded assumptions by asking potential users, buyers and partners 
for feedback on all aspects of the business model, including product characteristics, pricing, 
distribution channels and affordable customer acquisition strategies. Creating rapidly 
“minimum viable product” (MVP) and eliciting immediately customer feedback is an 
emphasis in this step. The customer feedback would revise the assumptions and cause 
iteration in the cycle of testing, redesigning and offering. 
Third, the agile development approach works alongside customer development to eliminate 
wasted time and resources through iterative and incremental product development.

3.2. Research Design  
It is essential for a new start-up to ensure that the business model hits the ground, reaches 
its target market and becomes a valuable alternative to competition. Following this 
exploratory study, it is necessary to outline the business research design by focusing on the 
lean start-up methodology for data collection and analysis. 
As mentioned in the lean start-up overview, customer development is one of the three parts 
of a lean start-up. In this concept, customer feedback is collected through empirical research 
methods and analysed quantitatively and qualitatively. 
Yin (2011) defines research design as a logical plan for getting from initial research question 
to set of conclusion. The logical plan contains the process of collecting, analysing and 
interpreting data. (continue with validity and reliability)  
Greener (2008) argues that the result of a quantitative data collection and analyses method, 
such as web and paper survey, is used to map out a comprehensive view of research 
questions. He also contends that with qualitative data collection and analysis methods e.g. 
interview, the researcher provides themes or area of investigation in more depth.  
Maurya (2012) suggests the principle of two phase validation: first qualitative, then 
quantitative. He believes that the initial goal of a customer development is to uncover strong 
signals for or against hypotheses.  The strong signal, either positive or negative,  may be able 
to get through a reasonably small sample size as five customer interview (Nielsen, 2000). When 
the initial validation on the hypotheses done, now it’s rational to use the results to craft a 
survey and verify the findings quantitatively.  
Brandenburg (2013) presents similar approach for a market research. He claims that before 
quantitative market analysis, qualitative market research will be conducted if the business 
definition has not already been studied. In this scenario the researchers should consider the 
original and impartial reaction and views of the customer on the new idea without external 
influencers including previous experience of related goods. For a new idea, it is important to 
first understand changes and modifications before continuing with a quantitative market 
analysis to verify the final idea. 
Brandenburg (2013) also mentions the importance of carrying out qualitative and 
==PAGE:25==
  
 
 
 
quantitative market research before beginning a new business, launch a new product or 
service as follows: 
• Quantitative analysis offers reliable statistics and details that highlight market value 
and can help safe buyers, since the loss of their investment is minimized in the light 
of potential demand. It also helps administrators to collect inventory and revenue 
estimates. It can be done by using the target market response rates and any price 
details or expectations indicated by respondents during the research. 
• Qualitative market analysis reflects the unbiased opinion of the industry on the 
company concept, product or service. By recognising these viewpoints, a 
management will modify and adapt their approach to ensure client loyalty and 
profitability on the market; the product or company requires and needs the customer 
to be competitive. 
As mentioned earlier, research design provides a plan or framework for collecting and 
analysing data. It also suggested that for an appropriate product and market feasibility 
analysis we must written down what kind of data do we need, how we are going to get them 
and what are the futures and limitations of the chosen approach.  
Different data collection techniques are suggested in format of research method which are 
often related with two approaches, inductive and deductive. (Wilson, 2014). 
Hyde (2000) defined inductive as “a theory-building process, starting with observations of 
specific instances, and seeking to establish generalisation about the phenomenon under 
investigation”. In contrast, “deductive approach starts first with developing hypotheses base 
on theory and then design a research strategy to test the hypotheses.” (Wilson, 2014) 
In the deductive approach hypotheses are pre-selected based on existing literatures and 
theories or identified research questions; whereas in the inductive approach, hypotheses are 
brought out from analysing of data.

some highly publicized cases of people unintentionally breaking the law are in effect. 
Therefore, there is no money transfer services or fintech company available to transfer funds 
from Denmark to Iran at this moment. This also includes popular money transfer providers 
such as Western Union and MoneyGram, who do not offer their services to Iran because of 
the sanctions and complications that have surrounded Iran in the last few years.  
While sanctions against Iran have loosened globally, many banks may resist transferring 
money or savings to Iran. This is because a lot of international banks and other financial 
institutions are either based in the US or are using the U.S. dollar. Any organization that trades 
in U.S. dollars risks prosecution by the Justice Department. The civil or criminal penalties for 
such offences are severe. For banks that don't hold such a large US stake, transferring money 
online to Iran will be easier. Traditional bank transfers take a bit of time and are slightly 
complex. However, they aren't too hard to complete.  
As an example, Reuters reported on May 2018, after the U.S. had withdrawn from an 
international nuclear deal with Iran that had been sealed in 2015, “Denmark’s Danske Bank 
said it decided earlier this year to phase out and exit all activities to and from Iran, due to 
increasing operational and reputational risks associated with doing business in Iran…Danske 
Bank said it also decided to suspend existing relations with Iran that allowed it to support 
certain customers in doing business in Iran.” (Reuters, 2018) 
Sending money to Iran electronically through cryptocurrency transfers can be a quick way. 
However, the cryptocurrency market is not well regulated in contrast with conventional 
money-sending methods at all. On the positive side, on a national level Iran has welcomed 
==PAGE:39==
  
 
 
 
cryptocurrencies. Very recently the Central Bank also released its own national 
cryptocurrency. The advantage of using cryptocurrencies is that they usually send out very 
fast. A crypto-currency trade is also often very easy to carry out. When your chosen 
cryptocurrency has arrived in Iran, the receiver can go about turning it into Iranian Rial. The 
disadvantage to cryptocurrencies is that they are extremely loosely regulated, and you are at 
more serious risk when using them without adequate knowledge. 
The another channel to send funds to Iran is through wire transfer with agreeable banks. Few 
such banks are; Future Bank (based in Manama, Bahrain), Iran-Venezuela Bi-National Bank, 
Iranian-European Bank (headquartered in Hamburg, Germany), and Islamic Cooperation 
Investment Bank (a private bank in Iraq).  
The Iran sanctions situation is myriad and complex. In this case, using the simplest and oldest 
methods of money transferring such as IVTSs and Hawala will probably pay off, even though 
they are slower and costlier. (Merry, 2020)

4.2.1. 
FinMatch in Action 
The example below reflects a better understanding of how FinMatch functions: 
Sender A, an Iranian employed in Copenhagen, wants to send DKK 1000 back to his mother 
(Recipient A) in Tehran. Sender B, an Iranian citizen in Tehran at the same time, needs to send 
the same amount of money from Tehran to his student son in Copenhagen (Recipient B) in 
Riyal. Sender A and sender B, instead of transferring funds through the existing remittance 
schemes and paying high fees and a long time for cross-border transfer, only need to find 
each other and solve their transfer needs through a simple domestic transaction. In fact, 
sender A transfer 1000 DKK to recipient B in Denmark and sender B in Tehran transfer the 
same sum to recipient A in Riyal. (see figure 12) 
==PAGE:44==
  
 
 
 
 
Figure 12: FinMatch’s mechanism in action 
FinMatch benefits from matchmaking algorithms that create stable user pairs with certain 
criteria. The criteria are the opposite transfer destination and the similarity of the volume of 
the fund to be transferred. 
To order to increase the safety and trust of customers, FinMatch shall open the bank accounts 
in each county in which the service is provided. Bank accounts serve as deposit accounts for 
freezing funds before ensuring that both parties have transferred funds first to FinMatch to 
release funds to the second party. In the example above, sender A and sender B pass funds 
to the FinMatch account in the first step. FinMatch therefore verified the transfer of funds to 
recipients A and B. 
The platform integrates seamlessly with local mobile payments and digital wallets such as 
Mobile Pay, Swish and Vipps in the Nordic countries. These solutions provide customers with 
a secure and easy transaction through the use of a Touch ID or a Face ID identifier on their 
device. On the other hand, the companies providing the payments will charge 1-3 % of the 
fees for each transaction that may have an effect on the FinMatch fee rate.

impossible and unusual to set up a digital money transfer system in the form of IVTS. The 
digital business model could provide an appropriate framework for documenting and 
developing the business idea for an entrepreneur who wants to create and improve IVTS. 
Before reaching that stage, a feasibility analysis must be carried out to test the assumptions 
about the business idea. 
This study was intended to assess feasibility by conducting four different analyses. Apart from 
the results achieved, some important points are being considered:  
The result of industry and market analysis has shown that remittances are a very profitable 
business, particularly for large operators in this sector. On the other hand, increasing 
competition was identified in the remittances markets as a means to reduce transaction costs 
and improve market efficiency. In this case, only start-ups with innovative and creative ideas 
can succeed in the global marketplace. Even if FinMatch is able to provide services worldwide, 
it must necessarily be examined first in a smaller market. The lack of a large remittance 
operator to Iran gives FinMatch an opportunity to grow and gain experience in Iran’s market. 
The product and service analysis recognised FinMatch as an innovative product capable of 
maintaining and gaining market share, but there are still some limitations. As mentioned 
earlier, it required two senders and two recipients to complete a transaction. Maintaining 
balance between senders is crucial to any match-making system. This issue for FinMatch will 
be resolved by increasing users to a certain level. Until then, one side of the waiting time may 
be longer than the other. 
Financial projections have shown that FinMatch can reach a break-even point after two years, 
==PAGE:59==
  
 
 
 
even under the most pessimistic scenario. By reducing costs and developing alternative 
sources of revenue, this can be achieved earlier. However, the impact of the recent global 
pandemic on the remittance market remained unclear. 
The main limitation of this study is the lack of specific information on the regulation of the 
FinMatch mechanism. The way in which it operates is not similar to any other fintech models 
and is not even within the existing legal frameworks. It therefore requires further 
investigation and legal advice in order to avoid possible future risks. 
Further research and work needs to be done prior to the start of the development phase are 
listed below:  
• 
Research on technical feasibility and the proposed technology. 
• 
Risk and uncertainty analysis with consideration of different aspects (financial, 
political, technology, etc.). 
• 
More research on policy, standards and legislative aspects for EU and non-EU 
countries.  
• 
Predicting uncontrolled factors such as sanctions, political crises, etc.
