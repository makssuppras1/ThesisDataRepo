ABSTRACT 
The purpose of this project is to develop a new atom-economical way of the dehydrogenation of alcohols 
with the use of unsupported cobalt nanoparticles. There have not been paid much attention towards 
unsupported heterogeneous 
earth-abundant 
metals 
for 
dehydrogenation 
of alcohols. The 
dehydrogenation of alcohols can liberate molecular hydrogen as a byproduct, which is a valuable gas that 
can be used for other purposes. The only other byproduct is water. A preliminary screening of prepared 
Co-NPs followed by an optimization for the synthesis of imines from benzyl alcohol and 
cyclohexylamine were carried out. An optimization of Co2(CO)8 have also been carried out. The 
reactions can be applied to different benzyl alcohols and amines. We have discovered that Co2(CO)8 has 
the ability to form benzimidazoles and quinolines from amines and alcohols. Quinolines and amines are 
both important organic compounds in the pharmaceutical industry. The mechanisms of the formation of 
imines and heterocycles have not been investigated yet. The system has not been optimized completely 
for all the products but shows good activity to form imines and benzimidazoles and little activity in the 
formation of quinolines. In all cases, we believe that this can be a good starting point for using 
unsupported heterogeneous catalysts.

1. INTRODUCTION 
The growing environmental worries urge chemists to find alternative solutions towards greener 
chemistry. Methods for dehydrogenation of alcohols can include sacrificial waste e.g. by hydrogen 
transfer and/or toxic waste using a hydrogen acceptor or strong oxidants/metal salts respectively. These 
methods can be necessary since removing a hydrogen atom from adjacent atomic centers of a 
hydrogenrich organic molecule are highly unfavorable. To avoid any waste, the ideal dehydrogenation 
reactions are the acceptorless dehydrogenations. In the acceptorless dehydrogenation, the only byproduct 
that develops is molecular hydrogen and water. Molecular hydrogen is non-polluting and is valuable in 
the chemical industry. Not only do we want acceptorless dehydrogenation reactions, but also a catalyst 
that has the ability to run these reactions under mild conditions and at the same time being 
environmentally benign, easily accessible and non-toxic. The acceptorless dehydrogenation reaction has 
been limited to the precious metal to be the most efficient such as Ru, Rh and Ir.1,2 Recently great 
attention have been devoted to explore more earth abundant metals. 
Co is an earth abundant metal, which is a promising candidate for the acceptorless dehydrogenation of 
alcohols. Only a few examples of homogeneous Co catalysts have been reported for the acceptorless 
dehydrogenation to form imines3–5, however, heterogeneous unsupported Co catalyst have major 
advantages over the homogeneous catalysts e.g. the reusability and price of the heterogeneous catalyst.6 
Since research on unsupported Co catalyst is very limited, we will also touch the research done on 
homogeneous catalysis. The unsupported heterogeneous catalyst might be less attractive to investigate, 
since the selectivity of homogeneous catalysts is higher.6 Heterogeneous catalysts still have an advantage, 
since they can be recycled. Furthermore, the reported homogeneous Co catalysts have been pincer-like 
Co complexes. A pincer ligand is a chelating agent that binds to three adjacent coplanar sites on the metal 
center.7 Pincer ligands can be expensive and hard to synthesize, but pincer complexes have the advantage 
of being stable at high temperatures, which is a result from the strong interaction between the ligands 
and the metal center. Furthermore, pincer complexes can form very active species. An Ir(PCP) pincer 
complex introduced by Aoki and Crabtree8, which was able to dehydrogenate cyclooctane to cyclooctene 
under reflux condition, gave rise to further investigation of this complex. Kaska and co developed an 
Ir(PCP) complex that was more efficient in the acceptorless dehydrogenation of cyclooctane.9 Later, Liu 
and Goldman developed an Ir(PCP) complex that is less sterically crowded, which provided close to

1.1. 
Cobalt in general 
In group 9 of the periodic table we find Co alongside with the more rare metals Rh and Ir. The main 
source of Co is being a byproduct from Cu and Ni mining, thus Co being a considerable economic 
importance. Co is extensively being used in magnets and in coloring of glasses and ceramics. From a 
bioinorganic point of view, Co is essential in trace amounts. The heart of vitamin B12 (also known as 
cobalamin) contains Co. It is however, only required in trace amounts, but cobalamin is essential to all 
animal life. The most common oxidation states of Co are the Co(III) and Co(II) species with a d6 and d7 
electron configuration respectively. Geometrically Co complexes form more tetrahedral complexes than 
any other of the d metals. This is because the difference in the ligand-field stabilization energies (LFSEs) 
of tetrahedral and octahedral geometries are at a minimum6.  
The simplest neutral carbonyls known for Co is the dimeric [Co2(CO)8] complex and for Rh and Ir are 
the tetrameric [Rh4(CO)12] and [Ir4(CO)8] complexes. The carbonyl complexes are often used as 
precursors that turns into active catalytically active species. 
1.2. 
CATALYSIS 
A catalyst is a substance that can increase the rate of a reaction. A catalytic substance can either be 
metallic (usually as a complex) or non-metallic. A catalyzed reaction versus a non-catalyzed version of 
the same reaction is faster since the catalyst lowers the activation energy by providing a different reaction 
pathway. However, one or more of the steps in the catalytic pathway(s) can be blocked by a substance 
called a catalyst poison. 
==PAGE:9==
 
INTRODUCTION 
 
 
 
6 
As mentioned, a catalyst increases the rate of a reaction and in addition, a catalyst decreases the Gibbs 
energies of activation. Furthermore, a catalyst will not affect the Gibbs energy of the overall reaction. 
The mechanism of the reaction pathway will also be very different from that of non-catalyzed reaction, 
thus resulting in different shapes and lower maxima. The product has to be released in a 
thermodynamically favorable step or the catalytic cycle will terminate. If one of the intermediates have 
a lower Gibbs energy than the final product itself, the intermediate will then be the final product. 
The essential thing about a catalyst is, it has the ability to undergo a cycle, where the reactants are 
consumed, the products are formed by the catalyst and the active catalyst is regenerated. An example of 
a catalytic cycle involving a Pd(II)-complex is the well-known cross-coupling reaction, where at least 
three mechanistic steps take place, an oxidative addition, transmetalation and reductive elimination to 
give the product and regenerated catalyst. Other catalytic mechanisms will be described later, and we 
will see how versatile metal-catalysts are when it comes to reaction pathways. The lifetime and efficiency 
of a catalyst are crucial from an economical perspective. The efficiency of a catalyst is commonly 
expressed as being the turnover frequency. Low concentrations of catalyst that is highly active have a 
large turnover frequency. The turnover frequency can be expressed using the following equation, where 
the conversion of a reactant A to give product B by a catalyst with a reaction rate 𝜈:

The lifetime of a catalyst is commonly expressed as the turnover number, which is the number of cycles 
the catalyst survives. If a catalyst is to be of use, it has to have a high turnover number. Impurities in the 
starting material or side reactions can result in a lower turnover number. Furthermore, the selectivity of 
a catalyst has to be taken into account, which means the proportion of the desired product should be much 
higher than side products. In many cases, catalytic reactions form CO2 and H2O, which are both 
thermodynamically favored but undesirable products. Thus, chemists are searching for more atom 
economic ways for catalytic reactions. Atom economy is the ratio between the molecular mass of the 
product and the molecular mass of all reactants. Ideally, all atoms from the reactants are present in the

final product, which means the reaction has a high atom economy and viewed as environmentally 
sustainable. If the goal is to increase the atom economy of reactions, catalysts are a very common tool. 
Metal catalysis is divided into two classes, which can either be homogeneous or heterogeneous. 
In general, the mechanisms that usually take place at metal centers of a catalyst are ligand substitution, 
redox reactions, oxidative addition, reductive elimination, migratory insertion reaction, 1,2-insertion and 
β-hydride elimination. The discovery of a mechanism of a certain catalyzed reaction can be challenging 
since catalytic reactions contain many steps, which can be hard to control. In general, it is harder to 
explore the mechanism of a heterogeneously catalyzed reaction since it is more challenging to 
characterize species on a surface than those in a solution. 
1.2.1. Homogenous and heterogeneous catalysis 
Homogeneous catalysis is defined as when the catalyst is in the same phase as the reagents. 
Homogeneous catalysis is normally in solutions, where the catalyst is present as solutes in liquid reaction 
mixtures. Heterogeneous catalysis is defined as when the catalyst is in another phase of the reagents. The 
catalyst is normally in a solid phase while the reagents are present either in a solution or gaseous phase. 
Homogeneous catalysis is an attractive option of doing catalysis because it is often very selective towards 
the formation of the desired product. The mechanisms of homogeneous catalytic activity are more 
accessible to the very detailed investigation in comparison of a heterogeneous catalyst, as species in 
solution are easier to characterize. One major disadvantage of homogeneous catalysis is that a separation 
step is required, which is one reason homogeneous catalysis is avoided in the industry. In large-scale 
production, homogeneous catalysis is used for exothermic reactions since heat is easier to disperse from 
a solution than a solid heterogeneous catalyst.  
Examples of some of the well known homogeneous catalysts are Grubb’s catalyst and Wilkinson’s 
catalyst which are used for alkene metathesis and hydrogenation of alkenes respectively.6  
Heterogeneous catalysis is widely used in the industry since it is easier to separate the solid catalyst from 
the product and tolerates higher temperatures. Heterogeneous catalysts are high surface-area materials, 
which are typically fine divided particles. The active site of a heterogeneous catalyst can either be on the 
surface of the catalyst or in the internal surface of the catalyst. Zeolites can be used as heterogeneous 
catalysts which have pore-like structures where the reaction occurs. 
==PAGE:11==

A support is often used for metal particles to provide a catalyst. Examples of reactions that are catalyzed 
by supported metal catalysts are interconversion of hydrocarbons, reduction of the poisonous nitrogen 
oxides to N2 an O2 and formation of CO2 from O2 and CO, which is used in the catalytic converters of 
vehicles.11 
An important feature of heterogeneous catalyst is the adsorption process. This is necessary for the 
catalysis to occur, however, the adsorption must not be too strong since it can block the catalytic sites. 
Heterogeneous catalysts have two different ways to adsorb various molecules. One way is physisorption, 
where no new chemical bond is formed, and the molecule sticks to the surface of the metal. 
Chemisorption is another way of adsorption, where new bonds are formed. An example of chemisorption 
is the bond breaking of molecular hydrogen, where each hydrogen atom binds to the metal surface.6 Our 
main focus will mainly be on Co catalysis, both homogeneous and heterogeneous and their application. 
However, we will also touch other transition metal catalysts, since the more recent studies with Co is 
highly inspired by the function of other transition metals. 
1.2.2. Mechanisms of catalysts 
Before we dig deeper into the area of different catalysts, one must understand the different catalytic 
pathways that can take place during a catalytic cycle. One should note there are many kinds of 
mechanisms that metals can do, but we will only describe the mechanisms that are relevant to the 
following sections. That is an oxidative addition, reductive elimination, ligand substitution, 1,2-insertion 
and β-hydride elimination. Oxidative addition and reductive elimination are precise opposite reaction as 
goes for 1,2-insertion and β-hydride elimination. The mechanisms can occur in a different succession 
and multiple times. With such different mechanisms implies that transition metals are able to catalyze 
different reactions in different ways.  
1.2.2.1. 
Oxidative addition 
The oxidative addition is, as the name applies, an oxidation step of the metal center by forming two new 
bonds. An R-X molecule can be added to a metal center M, by breaking the R-X bond to form an X-M-
R complex. Normally, the R-X bond is a C-Halogen bond. Oxidative addition normally requires an 
unsaturated metal center, which typically has 16 electrons. The oxidative addition is, however, not 
restricted to transition metals only, but also metals like Mg when forming Grignard reagents. The

can be EtI, where M-Et is formed together with the I- anion, which binds to the metal subsequently. 
Another possibility is the concerted pathway mechanism, where bonds are formed and broken at the same 
time. However, the true mechanism of the oxidative addition is not known. Radical oxidative additions

1.2.2.2. 
Reductive elimination 
Earlier we mentioned that the reductive elimination is a reverse reaction of the SN2-like oxidative 
addition. The metal center will break two bonds to regenerate the unsaturated metal center and forming 
two new chemical bonds R-R’. The two bonds that need to break in the R-M-R’ complex has to be right 
next to each other (cis in square-planar complexes) to be broken. If not, the bonds of the two ligands will 
be too far away to interact. In square-planar complexes, the metal can be able to switch between the cis 
and trans isomers, since the isomers are in equilibrium. Both the oxidative addition and reductive 
elimination are important, almost essential, reactions in catalytic processes.13

1.2.2.3. 
1,2-Insertion 
1,2-Insertions are commonly observed with ligands with a hapticity of 2 (η2) such as alkenes or alkynes. 
A 1,2-insertion is, for instance, where a metal center that binds to an alkene and another ligand X, where 
X usually is a hydride but can also be bigger systems such as aryls, breaks the M-X and the double bond 
of the alkene to form the alkylated product. The ligand X now appears on the alkane chain on the 2 
position. It is called 1,2-insertion because the ligand X that was 1 bond from the metal center ends up

1.2.2.4. 
β-hydride elimination 
β-hydride elimination is the opposite of a 1,2-insertion. The metal center eliminates a hydrogen on the 
β-position of the ligand, which can be an alkane chain. Both β-hydride elimination and 1,2-insertion 
proceed through a syn intermediate, where bonds are either broken or formed on the same side of an 
alkene. The alkene will bind to the metal with η2 and to a hydride. 1,2-insertion combined with β-hydride 
elimination can isomerize alkenes through a low-energy pathway.15

1.2.2.5. 
Ligand substitution 
Ligand substitution is where a metal complex replaces one ligand by another. Ligand substitution can 
take place in different steps depending on which ligand is replacing another ligand. Ligand substitution 
can be categorized into three categories: association, dissociation and interchange. We can draw 
analogues from SN2 and SN1 reactions to the associative and dissociative mechanisms respectively. An 
associative mechanism involves an intermediate where the metal has a higher coordination number. 
Another ligand is then discarded to regenerate the same coordination number as before.  The associative 
mechanism plays a role in many d8 square-planar complexes because they can be stable having only 16 
==PAGE:14==
 
INTRODUCTION 
 
 
 
11 
electrons, such as Pd(II). The dissociative mechanism is where a ligand leaves before a new ligand is 
introduced. This results in a lower coordination number of the complex and suggests that this is more 
favorable for the coordinatively saturated complexes having 18 electrons.  
The last type of ligand substitution is the interchange mechanism. The interchange mechanism has two 
types of mechanisms. That is the associative and dissociative activated interchange, which we will denote 
IA and ID respectively. The interchange mechanism is more common than the associative and dissociative 
mechanisms described in six-coordinated complexes. It is hard to distinguish between IA and ID since the 
intermediate, if there is one, is harder to detect. In the interchange mechanism, the complex and the 
ligands form a transition state, thus suggesting that the coordination number of the complex do not 
increase or decrease.6

1.3. 
Acceptorless dehydrogenation 
Different strategies of the acceptorless dehydrogenation of alcohols have been proposed presented by 
Milstein and co.16 The four strategies that will be described are the following: a successive acceptorless 
dehydrogenation, an acceptorless dehydrogenation with molecular hydrogen and water release, 
borrowing hydrogen and coupling of redox pairs. All reactions are summed up in Figure 6. 
In the successive acceptorless dehydrogenation the catalyst liberates molecular hydrogen as the only 
byproduct from both the starting material and intermediate generated by a nucleophilic substrate. As an 
example, the catalyst will dehydrogenate a primary alcohol to yield the corresponding aldehyde. An 
amine can be introduced to react with the aldehyde to give the hemiaminal. The final product is the 
amide, from dehydrogenation of the hemiaminal, and the catalyst will liberate molecular hydrogen. The 
catalyst is regained to catalyze another cycle. 
Instead of having the hemiaminal as the intermediate, the release of water will give an imine. The imine 
can either be the product or the intermediate, where it can further undergo a cyclization reaction to give 
an aromatic product. The cyclization step does not require further liberation of molecular hydrogen. 
==PAGE:15==
 
INTRODUCTION 
 
 
 
12 
The borrowing hydrogen strategy does not evolve hydrogen since the hydrogen atoms obtained from the 
dehydrogenation of the alcohol are transferred for a hydrogenation step. After the nucleophilic substrate 
is introduced, an imine is formed, which then reacts with the hydrogen atoms to yield the amine and the 
active catalyst. We will give a specific example for the borrowing hydrogen strategy later, when we 
introduce Guerbet condensations. 
For the coupling of redox pairs, the catalyst dehydrogenates the alcohol to form the aldehyde and a metal-
hydride. By adding an unsaturated substrate forms a nucleophilic metal alkyl that reacts with the aldehyde 
to form a new C-C bond. This form of reaction does not lead to any production of molecular hydrogen 
or water. Atom economically this type of reaction is ideal since all atoms in the starting materials end up 
being in the final product.

The magnetic properties of Co will make it easy to collect and wash for recycling reaction termination.

Furthermore, the Re -NPs are not as easy to recover compared to Co-NPs.

1.4. 
CARBON-HYDROGEN BOND ACTIVATION 
A carbon-hydrogen bond activation (or C-H functionalization) is a type of reaction where a C-H bond is 
cleaved and is replaced by a C-X bond, where X is usually C, O or N. A transition metal is usually 
involved in a C-H bond activation reaction, where the hydrocarbon is coordinated to the metal center. 
Aromatic C-H bond activation is a useful way of making hydrocarbons to useful organic compounds. 
One of the proposed mechanisms of the C-H bond functionalization, both oxidative addition and 
reductive elimination is involved. The second possibility is an electrophilic activation where a substrate 
that is generally electronrich undergoes an aromatic electrophilic substitution-type mechanism. A third 
possibility is a sigma-bond metathesis, which goes through a four-centered transition state. The overall 
mechanism understandably depends on which transition metal is used. The very first C-H bond 
activations were carried out with transition metals. In 1965, Joseph Chatt reported af Ru catalyzed C-H 
bond functionalization of naphthalene26, and in 1955 Murahashi reported chelation assisted C-H bond 
functionalization of azobenzenes or aldimines to indazolones with Co2(CO)8.27 His research indicated a 
potential of Co complexes for C-H bond functionalization, but the application was very limited by the 
forcing conditions with a pressure of 150 atm and a temperature of 190°C.

1.5. 
Aromatic heterocycle chemistry 
Heterocycles are important organic compounds and are very often encountered in medicine. The history 
of medicine can almost be defined by heterocycles only. For instance, quinine was used to prevent 
malaria in the 1500s, although the structure was not known. The first synthetic drug was antipyrine, 
which was used for reduction of fevers (1887). The first effective antibiotic was sulfapyridine synthesized 
in 1938. Other drugs that contain aromatic heterocycles are Tagament (Cimetidine) and the well known 
Viagra for the treatment of heartburn and male impotence respectively.35  
1.5.1. Friedländer synthesis of quinolines 
In the late 1800s, the synthesis of quinolines became very attractive among chemists. Amongst those 
chemists are Skraup, Döbner, Miller, Niementowski and Friedländer.  
The Friedländer synthesis is a reaction where quinoline deriviatives are formed by using 2-
aminobenzylaldehydes and ketones.36,37 The reaction needs a catalyst such as trifluoroacetic acid, 
toluenesulfonic acid, iodine or Lewis acids.38–41 There are two viable reaction mechanisms for the 
Friedländer synthesis, which can be seen in Figure 9. In the first step, the 2-amino substituted carbonyl 
and the carbonyl from the ketone reacts together to give an aldol product. The second step is the

The other mechanism is a formation of a Schiff base, which undergoes aldol reaction and the last step is 
the elimination to form the quinoline.42 Variations of the Friedländer synthesis have also been described. 
One of them is the Niementowski quinoline synthesis, which discovered another way to synthesize 
quinolines 10 years later.43 Instead of using the 2-aminobenzaldehyde, Niementowski used anthranillic 
acid instead. The mechanism for the Niementowski synthesis is very similar to the one of Friedländer. 
The modern synthesis of quinolines can be achieved using a metal catalyst under milder conditions. 
Verpoort and co reported a Ru-catalyzed approach to the Friedländer synthesis in 2008.44 Instead of using 
the 2-aminobenzaldehyde, the group used the corresponding primary alcohol instead. The reactions were 
carried out in 80°C for 1 hour with 1% of catalyst loading. One equivalent of a strong base (KOH or 
tBuOK) was needed to carry out the reaction. Different Ru-complexes were used where the yields varied 
from 14% to 100%. Various bases have also been tested for the quinoline synthesis but KOH and tBuOK 
came out as the best bases for the Ru-catalyzed quinoline synthesis. The first step of the mechanism is a 
dehydrogenation of the alcohol to give the aldehyde. The second step is the elimination of the α-proton 
of the ketone to react with the aldehyde to give the cross aldol product in the third step. Elimination of

1.5.2. Benzimidazoles 
Benzimidazoles are also important building blocks in the pharmaceutical industry because of their 
biological activity. The benzimidazole motif is found in many drugs that exhibit activity against viruses 
such as HIV, herpes and influenza.45 
Benzimidazoles are usually synthesized by the reaction of 1,2-diaminobenzene with carboxylic acid 
derivatives under strongly acidic conditions, which Wagner and Millet presented in 1939.46 They heated 
a mixture of 1,2-diaminobenzene and concentrated formic acid to form the benzimidazole. The method 
suffers from stoichiometric amounts of waste but recently the use of aldehydes as substrate and iodine 
as catalyst in presence of peroxide improved the reaction conditions.47 In 2017, Milstein and co reported 
a direct synthesis of benzimidazoles by acceptorless dehydrogenation of aromatic diamines and alcohols 
with a homogenous Co catalyst and without the use of base for the first time.45 Other groups have reported 
the same kind of reaction with Ir, Ru or Cu catalysts48–50, however, the reactions presented need a 
stoichiometric amount of hydrogen acceptor or strong base. Milstein and co also tried with a 
stoichiometric amount of base but the absence of base gave yields that were great. However, a hydride 
source was needed to carry out the reaction in form of sodium triethylborohydride. The catalyst used is 
a Co-pincer complex (PNN) as a precatalyst to generate the active Co(I) species. The reactions were 
carried out in toluene at 150°C with a catalyst and hydride source of 5 mol % each, 4Å molecular sieves 
with a reaction time of 24h in a closed system. A substrate scope showed great results with aliphatic 
chains and aromatic substrates with yields mostly above 90%. A simple mechanism has also been 
suggested. The catalyst dehydrogenates the alcohol to give the aldehyde. The aldehyde forms an imine 
by the elimination of water followed by ring closure to form two primary amines. The Co catalyst 
dehydrogenates one of the amines to form an imine bond and the benzimidazole.45

1.6. 
SIDE REACTIONS 
When doing research in chemistry, there are chances that researchers will encounter side reactions and/or 
products. In this section, a number of side reactions will be covered, which can occur in the acceptorless 
dehydrogenation reactions.  
1.6.1. Guerbet condensation  
Guerbet condensations have been known since the late 1800s. For a Guerbet condensation to take place 
the reaction often needs a catalyst and high temperatures. The first step is the dehydrogenation of a 
primary alcohol to give the aldehyde. The second step is the aldol self-condensation to give the 
corresponding vinyl aldehyde by the elimination of water. The last step is a hydrogenation step, to give 
the Guerbet product.16 Using a catalyst for the acceptorless dehydrogenation can also yield the Guerbet 
product. However, it is not the desired product when the target molecule is a coupling between two 
substrates such as an alcohol and an amine. This can indeed occur after the first dehydrogenation step of 
an alcohol. 
If we consider the Friedländer synthesis only using alcohols such as 2-aminobenzyl alcohol and 1-
phenylethanol with a catalyst able to dehydrogenate the secondary alcohol, it can undergo a Guerbet 
reaction. A mixture between the two enantiomers can be used since the product does not have a chiral 
center (the quinoline). Possible products from a Guerbet condensation with 1-phenylethanol are shown 
in Figure 10, where the middle product is due to a dehydration. A hydrogenation of the alkene can occur 
on the middle product to give the right most product.

1.6.2. Dehalogenation 
Dehalogenation is a reaction where a C-Halogen bond is cleaved to form a C-H bond. The 
electronegativity of the halogens can tell us about the bond strength between the halogen and the carbon 
atom. Furthermore, the bond strength can tell us about the rate of dehalogenation. Fluorine is the most 
electronegative halogen and will form the strongest bond with carbon compared to other halogens. The 
bond dissociation energies of C-Halogen bonds are described as CH3-I (234 kJ mol-1), CH3-Br (293 kJ 
mol-1), CH3-Cl (349 kJ mol-1) and CH3-F (452 kJ mol-1). We can compare these dissociation energies to 
CH3-H (435 kJ mol-1)54, which gives us a better picture of C-Halogen bond strengths. Thus, the rate of 
dehalogenation can be described as I > Br > Cl > F, where C-I has a higher rate and C-F has a lower rate 
of dehalogenation. 
By using starting materials or synthesizing products that contain a C-Halogen bond, one must be aware 
of dehalogenation. Aryl halides can undergo elimination-addition reaction under strongly basic 
conditions (such as tBuOK). As formerly mentioned, the weaker the C-Halogen bond the more likely the 
aryl halide can undergo elimination-addition. 
1.6.3. Homocoupling reactions 
Heterocoupling reactions are often more interesting to investigate than homocoupling reactions since it 
can give rise to a broader perspective within the field. Heterocoupling reactions are often competing with 
homocoupling reactions. Acceptorless dehydrogenation of alcohols and aliphatic amines to form imines 
can lead to a mixture of both the heterocoupling imine and the homocoupling imine. The selectivity of 
the catalyst plays a crucial role in this strategy. Catalysts with lower selectivity can avoid the 
homocoupling product by having a primary amine next to a tertiary carbon. In 1996 Bailey and James 
reported an aerobic oxidative dehydrogenation of amines to give imines by using a Ru-complex in the 
synthesis of nitriles, alcohols, aldehydes and ketones.55 Another report by Albrecht and co reported 
various Ru-complexes, which were tested for the oxidative homocoupling of amines to give imines.56 
The reactions were carried out in toluene and heated up to 150°C in a closed system. One of the Ru-
complexes managed to finish a reaction with benzylamine after 12 hours to give more than 95% of the 
homocoupled imine with a catalyst loading of 5 mol % (Figure 11).56 However, they have not tested the 
catalyst for two different amines that can give the heterocoupling, which can be interesting to test the

of, which is why one has to aware of the competing homocoupling product when optimizing reactions. 
1.6.4. Decarbonylation of primary alcohols 
Decarbonylation of primary alcohols is where H2 gas and CO gas are liberated from dehydrogenating 
primary alcohols and decarbonlyate aldehydes. A recent study by Mazziotta and Madsen62 described 
dehydrogenative decarbonylation of primary alcohols using a Ru-catalyst. The transformation in this 
fashion is more challenging since the catalyst have to be able to both release molecular hydrogen and 
CO gas. Both molecular hydrogen and CO are known ligands for a variety of metal complexes.62 Before 
this research, only two systems were shown to perform dehydrogenative decarbonylation, which were 
[Ir(COD)Cl]2BINAP and [ToMRh(CO)2]. The study that was mentioned used Ru(COD)Cl2 in presence 
of phosphine ligands. The displayed yields were mostly great ranging from 13% to 95% also using 
aliphatic primary alcohols. 
If a coupling between two substrates is the goal for a reaction, such as the imine formation between 
primary alcohols and an amine, decarbonylation of the formed aldehyde after dehydrogenation can be a 
major problem. However, as we mentioned, no earth-abundant metals are able to perform 
dehydrogenative decarbonylation.

1.6.5. Formation of carboxylic acids 
Heterogeneous and homogeneous catalysts are able to form carboxylic acids from primary alcohols. In 
2013 it was presented for the first time by Milstein and co, that a Ru catalyst can dehydrogenate primary 
alcohols to form carboxylic acids.63 Ghalehshahi and Madsen64 have also recently reported a simple 
homogeneous Ag-catalyst that catalyze primary alcohols to form carboxylic acids in very high yields. 
The reactions were carried out with 2.5 mol % Ag2CO3, 2.5 mol % MnBr2 (as a co-catalyst), 2.5 
equivalent of KOH and PVP as an additive, which is able to dehydrogenate benzyl alcohol to form the 
corresponding carboxylic acid. In a substrate scope, the yields of various carboxylic acids were excellent 
ranging from 58% to more than 90% and great when aliphatic alcohols were used (mostly >70%). Thus, 
not only precious metals have the ability to convert primary alcohols to carboxylic acids but also simple 
metals and with great yields. From the results described, one has to be aware of the formation of 
carboxylic acids since, as described, very high yields can be obtained.  
A couple of mechanistic studies have been investigated for heterogeneous Ag-catalyst on alumina65 or 
silica66 support for the dehydrogenation of primary alcohols. What both studies revealed was the alcohol 
was being absorbed on the surface of the support, which is responsible for abstracting the proton from 
the hydroxy group where silver then cleaves a hydride from the alkoxide to form the carbonyl 
compound.65,66

heated to 164°C and stopped after 20 hours. We did not observe any conversion of the benzyl alcohol, 
probably due to the poor decomposition of Co2(CO)8 in a closed system. A tube was also heated by 
microwave with same catalyst loading. 80 mg TOPO, 8 mL mesitylene, 4 mmol benzyl alcohol and 4.4 
mmol cyclohexylamine were reacted in a Biotage microwave vial sealed with a cap. The reaction was 
carried out at 164°C for 48 hours. Again, we did not observe any conversion of the benzyl alcohol. The 
reaction might have been running at a low temperature or that mesitylene might be a poor microwave 
solvent. 
We also considered the formation of homocoupling product. A reaction with 2 mmol benzylamine, 2 mol 
% Co2(CO)8 as catalyst, 5 mg TOPO and 2 mL mesitylene was refluxed for 50 hours. 92% of the 
benzylamine was converted into the homocoupling product. The important thing, however, is that a 
homocoupling can indeed be formed from the primary amine. With this result, we set up another reaction 
to understand the competition of homocoupling product and heterocoupling further. With the same 
conditions, we also added 1 equivalent cyclohexylamine. From this reaction, we expected to see a mixture 
between heterocoupling and homocoupling. After 50 hours we observed the conversion of the 
benzylamine to be 80% and the main product was the homocoupled imine. The yield of the 
heterocoupling was only at 16%. Thus, the catalyst is, to some extent, selective towards the 
homocoupling product, which the driving force can be due to the higher reactivity of the primary amine 
to the dehydrogenation but also higher stability of the product.

C-H activation and other dehydrogenation reactions 
All our attempts to explore the possibility for our catalyst to produce C-H activations have been carried 
out in closed systems. We have tried an example of C-H activation reaction described by Kim and co28 
where we alkylate 2-phenylpyridine at the ortho position. Only two reactions have been carried out where 
one of them was with prepared Co-NPs (459) and the other with Co2(CO)8 (5 mol % each). The reaction 
with Co2(CO)8, we added 20 mg TOPO. As the alkene which had to be added on the 2-phenylpyridine, 
we chose neohexene (3,3-dimethyl-1-butene) with 6 equivalents in respect to 2-phenylpyridine. The 
reactions were carried out with 2 mL mesitylene for 48h at 164°C in a closed system. However, our 
heterogeneous Co catalyst was not able to either convert the starting materials or produce any product.

3. CONCLUSION 
Cobalt is an earth abundant metal and the magnetic properties of our catalyst make it easy to be removed, 
washed and recycled. The acceptorless dehydrogenation is an atom economical transformation and by 
recycling the catalyst, it gives the opportunity to have a cheaper and more environmentally benign 
approach for the synthesis of organic compounds. 
In this work, an optimization of the reaction between benzyl alcohols and amines to form imines and 
hydrogen gas was attempted, leading to a very simple catalytic system, based on Co2(CO)8 and TOPO, 
in the absence of bases and other additives.  
The precatalyst is believed to gives rise to Co-NPs as the active species for the catalytic process. The 
nature of the catalyst is currently under investigation. 
The substrate scope and limitation was then studied, obtaining imines in moderate yields. However, the 
catalyst is not totally selective since homocoupling products from the amines were observed. 
As future work we intend to extend the substrate scope for the imine synthesis from benzyl alcohols and 
amines so far performed and also investigate the possibility to obtain cross-coupling imines reacting 
different amines. 
We tried to extend the application of the new catalyst to different systems, and in particular we tried to 
obtain quinolines and benzimidazoles. 
These substrates are important compounds in the pharmaceutical industry and their synthesis with a 
cheap catalyst can have an influence on the large-scale production of drugs. We showed that Co2(CO)8 
has the ability to catalyze the formation of both compounds in low to moderate yields. However, the 
systems still need further optimization also to increase selectivity for the desired products. 
We plan to investigate the mechanism of those transformations and because of the high reactivity the 
catalyst, we also intend to explore the possibility to have C-H activation with other experiments.

5. EXPERIMENTAL SECTION 
5.1. 
General procedure 
All chemicals used all chemical reactions are obtained from Sigma-Aldrich without further purification. 
The conversions and yields were monitored by GCMS spectrometry on a Shimadzu GCMS-QP2010S 
instrument equipped with an Equity-5 column with the dimensions 30m x 0.25mm x 0.25µm. 
Tetradecane was used as an internal standard for calculating the yields displayed on the GCMS. The 
equation used for calculating the yields of the products is as follows: 
𝑦(%) = 𝑎∗𝐴𝑝𝑟𝑜𝑑𝑢𝑐𝑡
𝐴𝑠𝑡𝑎𝑛𝑑𝑎𝑟𝑑
∗𝑚𝑠𝑡𝑎𝑛𝑑𝑎𝑟𝑑
𝑀𝑊𝑠𝑡𝑎𝑛𝑑𝑎𝑟𝑑
∗𝑀𝑊𝑠𝑡𝑎𝑟𝑡
𝑚𝑠𝑡𝑎𝑟𝑡
∗100 
Where 𝑎 is the slope of the calibration curve, 𝐴𝑝𝑟𝑜𝑑𝑢𝑐𝑡 and 𝐴𝑠𝑡𝑎𝑛𝑑𝑎𝑟𝑑 are the area peaks of the product 
and internal standard respectively. 𝑀𝑊𝑠𝑡𝑎𝑛𝑑𝑎𝑟𝑑 and 𝑀𝑊𝑠𝑡𝑎𝑟𝑡 are the molecular weights of the internal 
standard and the starting material respectively. 𝑚𝑠𝑡𝑎𝑛𝑑𝑎𝑟𝑑 and 𝑚𝑠𝑡𝑎𝑟𝑡 are the masses of the internal 
standard and the starting material respectively used in a reaction. By multiplying with 100 we will acquire 
the yield, 𝑦(%), in percent. 
Products were purified by wet flash chromatogtaphy using Geduran® Si 60- silica gel 60 (40-63 µm). 
The silica gel was neutralized with pure Et3N and flushed through the column with the eluent (2 % Et3N 
in n-hexane). The columns with a diameter of about 5 cm were filled 10-15 cm above the filter. The 
fractions that contained the product was visualized using Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC) on 
aluminum sheets precoated with silica gel (Merck 25, 20 x 20 cm, 60 F254). The eluent used for TLC was 
the same as the eluent used for the column (2 % Et3N in n-hexane). Ultraviolet (UV) light was used to 
visualize the spots from the fractions. 
Identification of products 1H and 13C-NMR were recorded with a 400MHz Bruker Ascend NMR. The 
solvent used for both types of NMR was deuterated chloroform (CDCl3), which is visible at 7.26 ppm 
(residual CHCl3) on the 1H-NMR spectrum and at 77.16 ppm on the 13C-NMR spectrum. All NMR 
spectra are found in the Appendix section. Multiplicities are reported as s=singlet, d=doublet, dd=doublet 
of doublets and m=multiplet and coupling constants are shown in Hz.
