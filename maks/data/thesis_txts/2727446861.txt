ABSTRACT 
 
Lignocellulosic feedstocks are a sustainable substrate for manufacturing value-added biobased 
products. However, during lignocellulosic biomass pretreatment, which is needed to release 
fermentable sugars, inhibitory compounds such as weak acids, furan aldehydes and aromatic 
compounds are either released or produced. Additionally, most physical and chemical detoxification 
methods are either too expensive or non-environmentally friendly. In this context, microbial 
degradation of inhibitory compounds stands out as a promising detoxification strategy. 
In this study, the establishment of a bacterial cell factory tolerant to common inhibitors present in 
hemicellulosic hydrolysates was investigated. The selected host microorganism was a xylose-utilising 
and acetic auxotroph E. coli strain (AFR-1). The tolerance of the host to a synthetic hydrolysate 
containing 286 g/L xylose and the following inhibitors: HMF (1.14 g/L), furfural (0.06 g/L), vanillic 
acid (0.07 g/L), vanillin (0.12 g/L), benzoic acid (0.15 g/L) and syringaldehyde (0.63 g/L), was 
assessed. It was found that the host could tolerate up to 56% of the aforementioned inhibitory 
compound concentrations. Single-compound toxicity experiments revealed that syringaldehyde was 
the most toxic inhibitor for E. coli AFR-1, followed by vanillin, furfural, benzoic acid and HMF. The 
toxicity of some degradation products, such as furfuryl alcohol, 2-furoic acid, vanillic acid, syringic 
acid and protocatechuic acid (PCA) was also assessed, showing lower toxicity levels than the 
inhibitors present in the hydrolysate. 
Three plasmid-based degradation pathways for vanillin and syringaldehyde that involve a total of 8 
genes from Sphingobium sp. SYK6 (ligV, desV, desA, ligM, ligA, ligB, ligC and ligI) and 4 genes 
from Pseudomonas putida KT2440 (glllA, gllB, gllC and gllD) were tested in E. coli AFR-1. 
Complete degradation of vanillin to vanillic acid and syringaldehyde to syringic acid catalysed by the 
combined action of DesV and LigV enzymes was observed after 24 h. However, a bottleneck was 
found in the second degradation step to convert vanillic acid to PCA by LigM and syringic acid to 
3MGA by DesA. Both enzymes are tetrahydrofolate-dependent and it is argued that a cofactor 
limitation could be an explanation for the observed limited activity. Full degradation of PCA by the 
action of LigAB, and 33% degradation of gallic acid catalysed by GlllA were also observed after 
24 h.  
It was concluded that the tested degradation pathways in E. coli AFR-1 would only be able to degrade 
vanillin to vanillic acid and syringaldehyde to syringic acid if they were to be used to detoxify a 
lignocellulosic hydrolysate. Hardly any toxicity alleviation would be expected from vanillin 
conversion to vanillate and a maximum 14% toxicity alleviation could be expected due to 
syringaldehyde conversion to syringate. Overall, different metabolic bottlenecks preventing complete 
degradation were identified and several optimization strategies were proposed for further 
investigation, aiming to fully utilise lignocellulosic hydrolysates as viable feedstock for the 
production of bio-based products at industrial scale.

1. INTRODUCTION 
The impending depletion of fossil-based resources and the increasing awareness of sustainability 
within society has for the past decades led to the research on new and sustainable resources that can 
replace them. Among them lie lignocellulosic feedstocks (2nd generation biomass), a sustainable 
alternative for the production of advanced biofuels and a wide range of chemical products [1].  
Lignocellulosic feedstocks, such as wood or agricultural waste, provide abundant and renewable 
carbon sources. These feedstocks contain polymeric sugars, cellulose and hemicellulose, which can 
be released by hydrolysis and subsequently be fermented to a wide range of value-added products 
[2]. However, efficient fermentation of lignocellulosic hydrolysates is limited because, in addition to 
fermentable sugars, a range of toxic compounds is generated during the pretreatment and hydrolysis 
of lignocellulosic biomass [3]. These inhibitory compounds can be divided into three main groups: 
weak acids, furan derivatives, and aromatic compounds [4]. 
This project is framed within a broader project that corresponds to a PhD thesis carried out by Adrian 
Frey (co-supervisor of this thesis). The main project intends to make use of different lignocellulosic 
hydrolysates to produce value-added chemicals, such as aromatic amino acids and isobutyric acid. 
The selected host microorganism for hemicellulosic valorization is Escherichia coli K12 MG1655. 
However, in order to be able to use the hydrolysate in relevant concentrations as a carbon source, it 
first needs to be detoxified, since it contains significant concentrations of well-known growth 
inhibitors.  
In this context, the present Master Thesis project has focused on establishing recombinant E. coli 
strains that would be more tolerant to inhibitors present in the hemicellulosic fraction of 
lignocellulosic hydrolysates, such as furfural, 5-hydroxymethylfurfural (HMF), vanillin, 
syringaldehyde, benzoic acid and acetic acid. In order to achieve this goal, different plasmid-based 
degradation pathways were tested in an E. coli xylose-utilizing strain (AFR-1), aiming to establish 
cell factories that are able to uptake different inhibitory compounds and metabolize them. Namely, a 
degradation pathway for furfural and HMF, as well as a vanillin and syringaldehyde degradation 
pathway were addressed in this project.  
The purpose of this project is embedded in the wider goal of building a bioeconomy, transitioning 
from the current economy that still depends on the exploitation of fossil resources. Among the reasons 
that are mostly driving this shift it is worth noting the fluctuation of oil prices, an overwhelming 
increase of the world population, and climate change, all requiring a more sustainable way of 
managing resources [5]. If successful, the establishment of cell factories tolerant to lignocellulosic 
hydrolysates would result in a milestone within the broader goal of reaching a green, sustainable, and 
environmentally-friendly society.

2. BACKGROUND 
2.1. 
Lignocellulosic biomass 
Biomass and biomass-derived materials have been suggested to be among the most promising 
alternatives to fossil resources [1], [5]. They are produced by biological photosynthesis using 
atmospheric carbon dioxide, water, and sunlight. Therefore, biomass is considered the only 
sustainable source of organic carbon on Earth and a viable alternative to petroleum for the production 
of carbon-neutral fuels and chemicals. In this context, lignocellulosic biomass, which is the most 
abundant and renewable source of biomass in the planet, is crucial [6]. 
Nonetheless, the sustainability of producing fuels and chemicals from biomass has been the subject 
of intense discussion. In particular, the sustainability of bio-based manufacturing processes that rely 
on first generation biomass, such as starch and sugar crops, is a concern, since limited availability of 
these crops can lead to competition with food production [1], [5]. In this regard, lignocellulosic 
feedstocks have unique benefits over other sources of biomass because they consist of non-edible 
plant portions and have no impact on food supplies. Additionally, considerable amounts of 
lignocellulosic waste from forestry and agriculture are produced annually, which could be used to 
produce a wide range of value-added products [6]. 
From an economic point of view, lignocellulosic biomass can be produced at low cost compared to 
first generation biomass and especially crude oil [2]. However, the conversion of lignocellulosic 
biomass into fine products remains a difficult task. Lignocellulose has evolved to be resistant to 
degradation, making enzymatic and chemical breakdown problematic [7]. To modify the physical 
and chemical properties of the lignocellulosic matrix, costly and energy-intensive pretreatment of the 
biomass is required [1]. Despite the abundance and low cost of lignocellulosic materials, the greatest 
challenge is to efficiently produce compounds with high selectivities and yields while maintaining 
economic viability. Extensive research is currently being conducted to address this problem, with the 
development of biorefinery technologies seeking to refine biomass in a similar manner to 
petrochemistry in order to produce renewable fuels and environmentally friendly chemicals [5]. 
2.1.1. Structure and composition of lignocellulosic biomass 
Lignocellulosic biomass consists primarily of three biopolymers: cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin 
(Figure 2.1). Depending on the type of lignocellulosic biomass, these polymers are organized into 
complex three-dimensional structures with variable relative composition. The remarkable resistance 
to degradation of lignocellulose is mainly due to the crystallinity of cellulose, the hydrophobicity of 
lignin, and the encapsulation of cellulose by the lignin–hemicellulose matrix [7]. 
The major component of lignocellulosic biomass is cellulose. The repeating unit of the cellulose chain 
is the disaccharide cellobiose, composed of two glucose units. Cellulose structure is crystalline and 
consists of extensive intramolecular and intermolecular hydrogen bonding networks, which tightly 
binds the glucose units [1], [5]. Since it is estimated that about half of the organic carbon in the 
biosphere is present in the form of cellulose, its conversion into fuels and value-added chemicals is 
of great importance [8].

Hemicellulose is the second most abundant biopolymer. Unlike cellulose, hemicellulose has a random 
and amorphous structure, which is composed of several heteropolymers including galactomannan, 
glucuronoxylan, arabinoxylan, glucomannan and xyloglucan [1], [5]. Hemicelluloses differ in 
composition. For example, hardwood hemicelluloses contain mostly xylans, whereas softwood 
hemicelluloses contain mostly (galacto)glucomannans [2]. The heteropolymers of hemicellulose are 
composed of different monosaccharide units that include: pentoses (C5 sugars) like xylose and 
arabinose, hexoses (C6 sugars) like mannose, glucose or galactose, and some acids like acetic acid 
and uronic acids [1], [5]. Hemicelluloses are embedded in the plant cell walls to form a complex 
network of bonds that provide structural strength by linking cellulose fibres into microfibrils and 
cross-linking with lignin [7].

Lastly, lignin is a three-dimensional polymer of phenylpropanoid units. It works as the cellular glue 
which provides compressive strength to the plant tissue and the individual fibres, strength to the cell 
wall and resistance against insects and pathogens [2]. The structure of lignin is formed by the coupling 
of three different phenylpropane building blocks (monolignols), namely p-coumaryl alcohol,

coniferyl alcohol, and sinapyl alcohol. The corresponding phenylpropanoid monomeric units in lignin 
polymers are identified as p-hydroxyphenyl, guaiacyl, and syringyl units, respectively [1], [5]. 
It is important to note that cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin are not uniformly distributed within the 
cell walls. The structure and the quantity of these plant cell wall components vary according to 
species, tissues and maturity of the plant cell wall [6], [7]. In general, lignocellulosic biomass consists 
of 35–50% cellulose, 20–35% hemicellulose, and 10–25% lignin. Proteins, lipids, and minerals 
comprise the remaining fraction [8].  
2.2. 
Pretreatment of lignocellulosic biomass  
One of the most important goals of lignocellulosic biomass refining is to fractionate lignocellulose 
into its three major components: cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin. As described in Section 2.1, 
lignocellulosic biomass is an attractive feedstock for the production of fuels and value-added 
chemicals due to its wide abundance, low cost, and high content of sugars [5]. However, the resistance 
of these materials to degradation makes their utilization a challenge. The cellulose present in 
lignocellulosic biomass cannot be efficiently hydrolysed by enzymes (cellulases) in its native form 
and hydrolysis yields lower than 20% are usually obtained [9], [10]. The digestibility of cellulose is 
hindered by factors such as its crystallinity, the protection provided by hemicellulose and lignin 
fractions, its accessible surface area, and the degree of hemicellulose acetylation [9], [11]. Therefore, 
pretreatment of lignocellulosic biomass is essential to overcome its inherent recalcitrance. 
Diverse pretreatment methods have been investigated and developed in order to modify the physical 
and chemical structure of lignocellulosic biomass in order to efficiently separate hemicellulose and 
lignin fractions from cellulose without promoting sugar degradation. However, pretreatment remains 
one of the most expensive stages in the overall process of converting lignocellulosic biomass to bio-
based products [5]. In the case of bioethanol production, for instance, economic analyses indicate that 
the greatest cost (nearly 40%) is associated with the stages related to sugar release from hemicellulose 
and cellulose, including the steps of pretreatment, enzyme production, and enzymatic hydrolysis, with 
pretreatment accounting for nearly half of this total (about 18%) [12]. Due to the significant impact 
on process economics, one of the biggest challenges for biomass utilization is to develop efficient 
and cheaper pretreatment methods. 
The following requirements should be met in order to develop effective and economical pretreatment 
processes: deconstructing its three-dimensional structure, modifying its polymerization degree and 
decreasing the cellulose crystallinity; increasing the surface area and porosity of the material; 
generating highly digestible pretreated pulp that lead to high sugar yields after hydrolysis; avoiding 
the formation of inhibitory compounds; allowing hemicellulose and lignin recovery for subsequent 
use for the production of valuable coproducts; requiring a low demand of further operations such as 
washing and neutralization; and requiring minimal energy, chemicals, and water inputs [5], [9], [11]. 
Over the past decades, many technologies have been proposed for biomass pretreatment, which can 
be classified into four categories: physical, chemical, physicochemical, and biological. Some 
examples include, among others: milling, extrusion, microwave or ultrasound irradiation, steam 
explosion, ammonia fibre explosion, supercritical CO2 explosion, ionic liquids, alkaline hydrolysis, 
liquid hot water pretreatment, organosolv processes, wet oxidation, ozonolysis, dilute and

concentrated acid hydrolysis, and biological pretreatments using either enzymes or microorganisms 
[5], [9], [11]. 
Considering that each pretreatment method has a different impact on biomass structure, it is typically 
necessary to combine some of these techniques in order to achieve better results. Physical 
pretreatments, for instance, seek to reduce the particle size and crystallinity of cellulose and increase 
the surface area of the biomass, whereas chemical pretreatments typically target the solubilization 
and removal of lignin and hemicellulose fractions [12], [13]. Furthermore, as the structural and 
physicochemical properties of lignocellulosic biomass vary from one feedstock to another, so does 
the complexity of the lignocellulosic biomass matrix for each feedstock [9]. As a result, not all 
feedstocks respond the same way to pretreatment, which makes it essential to identify the most 
efficient pretreatment technology for each biomass. 
In this project, a lignocellulosic hydrolysate (UO5) produced from wood chips as feedstock is used 
as a reference regarding its composition. The employed novel process to fractionate this particular 
lignocellulosic feedstock is a good example of how the aforementioned requirements to design an 
efficient and economical pretreatment are tackled, and how different pretreatment operations can be 
combined to optimize the fractionation result. The main operations of the so-called Fabiola process 
are depicted in Figure 2.2. As can be seen, a hydrothermal pretreatment using steam is done to soften 
wood and make it more porous. Then, a first fractionation step consisting in dilute acid hydrolysis of 
the hemicellulosic fraction is done using sulfuric acid, followed by an organosolv process using 
acetone as solvent to dissolve and remove the lignin fraction. The remaining solid phase (cellulose 
pulp) is washed with water and physically pretreated via milling and extrusion to optimize the sugar 
yield when it is further enzymatically hydrolysed. From the liquor, lignin can be efficiently 
precipitated and removed, and the remaining hemicellulosic hydrolysate can be further concentrated.

2.3. 
Inhibitors present in lignocellulosic hydrolysates 
As described in the previous section, lignocellulosic feedstocks are not readily accessible for 
bioconversion by microorganisms, and thus require pretreatment steps to decrease the biomass 
recalcitrance [16]. However, as a result of biomass pretreatment, different inhibitory compounds (ICs) 
are formed due to the chemical degradation of sugars and lignin (see Figure 2.3). The amount and 
nature of the formed ICs is directly related to the pretreatment method and feedstock used [17]. The 
toxicity of these compounds to fermentative microorganisms is a limiting factor in the production of 
valuable products from lignocellulosic biomass [18]. Inhibitory compounds can be classified in three 
main groups: weak aliphatic acids such as acetic, formic and levulinic acid; furan derivatives (mainly 
furfural and hydroxymethylfurfural); and aromatic compounds [17].  
 
Figure 2.3. Inhibitory compounds formed during pretreatment of lignocellulose (extracted from [16]). 
==PAGE:17==

As illustrated in Figure 2.3, when hemicellulose is degraded, C5 sugars (e.g. xylose and arabinose), 
C6 sugars (e.g. glucose, mannose and galactose), and acetic acid are released. Cellulose is hydrolysed 
to glucose. At high temperature and pressure, pentoses can be degraded to furfural. Similarly, 5-
hydroxymethylfurfural (HMF) is formed from the degradation of hexoses. Formic acid is formed 
when furfural and HMF are broken down. Levulinic acid is formed by HMF degradation. Phenolic 
compounds are liberated from partial breakdown of lignin [4], [16]. 
The mechanism of inhibition of weak aliphatic acids depends on the extracellular pH [19]. At pH 
values lower than the pKa of the corresponding acid, the acid is in its undissociated form. Under these 
conditions, the growth-inhibiting effect on microorganisms has been proposed to be due to the inflow 
of undissociated acid into the cytosol. Once in the cytosol, dissociation of the acid occurs due to the 
neutral intracellular pH, thus decreasing the cytosolic pH by accumulation of protons [4]. The drop 
in intracellular pH resulting from inflow of weak acids is neutralised by the action of the plasma 
membrane ATPase, which pumps protons out of the cell at the expense of ATP hydrolysis, reducing 
ATP availability for cell growth [4], [19].  
However, the most common situation would be to perform the fermentation at pH values higher than 
the pKa of these weak acids. For example, the optimal pH for E. coli is between 6.5-7.5 [20] and the 
pKa value of acetic, levulinic and formic acid is 4.76, 4.64 and 3.75 respectively [19]. Thus, when 
adjusting the pH of the fermentation feed and media to 6.5-7.5, those acids would be neutralized and 
no intracellular proton accumulation would take place. In this situation, the observed growth 
inhibition could be due to osmotic stress caused by a concentration gradient of, for instance, formate 
or acetate in the media. Under those circumstances, the cells would spend energy in active transport 
of those compounds inside the cell in order to achieve osmotic regulation [19]. 
Furan aldehydes like furfural and HMF are known to damage cell walls and membranes, inhibit cell 
growth, reduce enzymatic activities, break down DNA, inhibit protein and RNA synthesis, and reduce 
ethanol production [16], [21]. Similar mechanisms of inhibition have been reported for aromatic 
compounds. A large number of toxic phenolic compounds have been identified as lignin degradation 
products, derived from 4-hydroxybenzyl, guaiacyl, and syringyl units, including: vanillin, coniferyl 
aldehyde, syringaldehyde, 4-hydroxybenzaldehyde, catechol, 4-hydroxybenzoic acid, syringic acid 
and dihydroconiferyl alcohol [22]. It is important to note that phenolic compounds can also originate 
from extractive components such as terpenes and tannins rather than lignin [17]. 
Small aromatic molecules (monomers) can be transported across bacterial cell membranes [23]. Still, 
it is not currently established if higher molecular weight species (e.g., dimers) can cross cell 
membranes [24]. Therefore, there are several indications that lower molecular weight phenolics are 
more toxic to microorganisms [25]. These molecules have been reported to cause biological 
membrane integrity loss, DNA damage via intracellular reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation, 
and inhibition of central metabolism enzymes [19]. 
In this context, removal of inhibitors prior to fermentation is required for optimal microbial 
performance. Several methods for detoxification of lignocellulosic hydrolysates have been 
investigated over the past decades. These methods, which are discussed in detail in Section 2.4, aim 
to minimize the harmful ICs through their removal, neutralization, absorption, or metabolism.

2.4. 
Detoxification of lignocellulosic hydrolysates  
To alleviate the toxic effects of inhibitory compounds produced as a consequence of lignocellulosic 
biomass pretreatment, many detoxification methods have been investigated. The focus has been 
placed on removing the inhibitory compounds from the hydrolysates, degrading the inhibitory 
compounds, or improving the resistance of the fermenting microorganisms to the toxic effects of the 
inhibitory compounds [26]. It should be noted that when different lignocellulosic hydrolysates and 
microorganisms are used, it is not possible to directly compare various detoxification strategies. The 
composition and degree of inhibition of lignocellulosic hydrolysates vary depending on the feedstock 
and the employed pretreatment, and distinct microorganisms have different inhibitor tolerances [3]. 
The detoxification methods can be generally divided into three categories: physical, chemical, and 
biological methods. 
Physical methods include technologies such as membrane filtration, adsorption, or ion exchange, 
among others. Membrane separation includes processes like microfiltration, ultrafiltration, 
nanofiltration and reverse osmosis, but only the last two are adequate to separate sugars from 
inhibitors. Usually, inhibitors have small molecular weight and are allowed to pass through the 
membrane while molecules with high molecular weight like sugars remain in the retentate fraction. 
Membrane technology has some advantages like low energy consumption and high flexibility of 
operation. However, membranes are prone to get fouled and are expensive [27]. The use of anion 
exchange resins has been reported to be efficient for removal of phenolics, furan aldehydes and 
aliphatic acids. However, even if resins can be regenerated and reused, they are also expensive [28]–
[30]. Lastly, activated charcoal is a well-known and low-cost detoxification method. Inhibitors like 
furans and phenolics were reported to be successfully removed using activated charcoal. The removal 
of inhibitors is suggested to be related to its strong hydrophobic nature, which allows to retain 
hydrophobic compounds. The main drawback of this method is the significant generation of waste 
[31], [32]. 
Some good examples of chemical detoxification methods are overliming and liquid-liquid extraction. 
Alkali treatments, particularly the treatment with calcium hydroxide (overliming), have been widely 
used for improving the fermentability of lignocellulosic hydrolysates [33]. In an overliming process, 
lime is added to the hydrolysate, resulting in the formation of insoluble salts. Total furans were 
reported to be reduced by 51% and soluble phenolic compounds were reduced by 41%. One drawback 
of overliming is the formation of calcium sulphate precipitate (gypsum) as waste [26]. Liquid–liquid 
extraction is a separation method used to separate two miscible compounds using a solvent that 
specially dissolves one of them based on the polarity of the solvent and the compound to extract. 
Some carboxylic acids and phenolic compounds can be removed from lignocellulosic hydrolysates 
using this method. However, as main disadvantage, it requires using non environmentally friendly 
organic solvents [34]. 
Biological detoxification methods can be divided into enzymatic or microbial treatments. The 
majority of the published studies about enzymatic treatment of lignocellulosic hydrolysates focus on 
the use of laccases and peroxidases to degrade lignin derivatives [16]. For instance, laccase 
detoxification tests using a diluted sugarcane bagasse hydrolysate (30 g/L total sugars) containing 
various fermentation inhibitors such as furans, phenolics, and acetic acid showed a total reduction of 
==PAGE:19==
Establishment of Microbial Cell Factories Tolerant to Lignocellulosic Hydrolysate 
9 
 
phenolics by 77.5% [26], [35]. However, this method has no effect on furan aldehydes and weak 
aliphatic acids. Furthermore, incubating hydrolysates with enzymes takes a long time and, if 
purchased, they significantly affect the operation costs due to their high price [16]. 
Microbial detoxification methods can be either based on the use of novel isolated microbial strains 
with the inherent capacity to degrade some types of inhibitors, adaptation of microorganisms to 
enhance their tolerance to certain inhibitors, or metabolic engineering strategies. Numerous novel 
microorganisms have been isolated from the soil and identified for biological treatment of 
lignocellulosic hydrolysates based on their potential for depletion of toxic compounds [26]. For 
instance, in a study carried out by Lopez et al. (2004), six isolates including five bacteria related to 
Methylobacterium extorquens, Pseudomonas sp., Flavobacterium indologenes, Acinetobacter sp., 
Arthrobacter aurescens, and one fungus, Coniochaeta ligniaria, were chosen based on stable growth 
on ferulic acid, furfural and HMF. All six isolates depleted toxic compounds from the medium after 
6 days, but only C. ligniaria C8 (NRRL 30616) was effective at metabolizing furfural and HMF as 
well as the aromatic compounds and aliphatic acids. This strain removed 78% of HMF and 97% of 
furfural from a 10% v/v corn stover hydrolysate after 30 h [36]. As main drawback, this method 
usually involves a two-stage fermentation strategy where these novel isolates are first used for 
detoxification of the hydrolysate (also consuming some sugars if they are not mutated to grow only 
on inhibitors) and then an established cell factory is used in a second fermentation for the production 
of a target product from the detoxified media [37]. Thus, as a negative effect, the overall production 
time can be significantly increased and the available sugars for the production stage can be decreased. 
The adaptation of a microorganism to the hydrolysate is another biological method for improving the 
fermentation of lignocellulosic hydrolysates. As an example, Martín et al. (2007) adapted a xylose-
utilizing genetically engineered strain of Saccharomyces cerevisiae with sugarcane bagasse 
hydrolysates by 353 h cultivation using a medium with increasing concentrations of phenols (from 
1.5 to 2.3 g/L), furfural (from 0.7 to 3.4 g/L), and aliphatic acids (from 2.5 to 8.7 g/L). The 
performance of the adapted strain was compared with the parental strain: the ethanol yield after 24 h 
of fermentation of the bagasse hydrolysate with inhibitors (phenols: 1.4 g/L, furfural: 2.2 g/L, 
aliphatic acids: 5.0 g/L) increased from 0.18 g/g of total sugar with the non-adapted strain to 0.38 g/g 
with the adapted strain [38]. The main disadvantage of this method is that, even if the tolerance can 
be increased, the inhibitors are not removed from the media, which is desired to achieve more efficient 
and cost-effective downstream processing to separate and purify the product. 
Finally, metabolic engineering of microbial cell factories to enhance their tolerance to inhibitors is 
considered to have a great potential. Some examples of metabolic engineering of established 
industrial cell factories such as S. cerevisiae, E. coli or Corynebacterium glutamicum include 
overexpression of alcohol dehydrogenases to promote furfural and HMF reduction to furfuryl alcohol 
and HMF alcohol, which are less toxic, or recombinant expression of laccases to promote degradation 
of aromatic compounds [39]. Also, some novel isolates that can degrade inhibitors have been 
engineered. Zou et al. (2021) engineered P. putida KT2440, which cannot naturally utilize xylose, to 
also prevent glucose consumption so it can only utilize the inhibitors as sole carbon source, while 
using another cell factory (Bacillus coagulans) in a co-culture to utilize all the sugars from the 
hydrolysate and produce lactic acid [40]. The authors reported 100% removal of furanic aldehydes 
and 90% of the lignin derived aromatic inhibitors from the corn stover hydrolysate. However, it is

important to highlight that 30% (v/v) corn stover hydrolysate was used for that study (which 
corresponds to 36.6 g/L xylose, 6.7 g/L glucose and 4.8 g/L arabinose) and it took 24 h to detoxify 
the inhibitors present in the hydrolysate [40]. While this is a positive outcome, reducing the duration 
of the detoxification phase and using more concentrated hydrolysates will further enhance product 
yield and ultimately improve the economics of the process. 
2.5. 
Biological degradation pathways  
As discussed in the previous section, detoxification of lignocellulosic hydrolysates is essential prior 
to their utilization as feedstock for bio-based manufacturing. However, most physical and chemical 
detoxification methods are either too expensive or generate large amounts of waste. As for the 
biological methods, adaptive laboratory evolution (ALE) of microorganisms to enhance their 
tolerance to inhibitors or metabolic engineering strategies to partially degrade inhibitors to less toxic 
compounds are considered to be limited, since they do not allow full detoxification. In this context, 
using novel isolates with the inherent capacity of utilizing inhibitors as carbon source through native 
metabolic degradation pathways seems to have a great potential. 
However, even if some novel isolates were reported to successfully degrade inhibitors, they usually 
require a long time even when using diluted hydrolysates and, more importantly, they are only used 
for biodetoxification purpose but not for production of bio-based products [39], [41], [42]. As 
discussed in Section 2.4, co-culturing a strain for detoxification along with a strain for production can 
be inefficient and usually two-stage fermentation strategies show better results. Therefore, the main 
disadvantage of this method is long detoxification times that can affect the overall process economics. 
Engineering novel isolates to utilize all the sugars present in lignocellulosic hydrolysates and at the 
same time overexpress the production of a target product could be a potential solution. Nevertheless, 
the feasibility of that approach is unclear since the genetic and metabolic knowledge about these novel 
isolates is limited and the potential success or failure when using otherwise established genetic 
engineering tools to modify them is uncertain. 
Within this context, a novel and ambitious detoxification strategy is proposed in this project. The aim 
of this study is to recombinantly express plasmid-based degradation pathways identified in other 
microorganisms in an established and deeply investigated cell factory (E. coli) that could also be used 
for the production of bio-based chemicals in a single-stage fermentation. In particular, the selected 
host microorganism is a xylose-utilizing E. coli strain, named AFR-1, that has already been modified 
to be an acetate auxotroph, which allows it to utilize acetic acid from the hydrolysate. Thus, the 
capacity to degrade this common inhibitor present in hemicellulosic hydrolysates is already achieved, 
and the recombinant degradation pathways to be transformed and tested in the host tackle the other 
two groups of inhibitors: furan aldehydes (HMF and furfural) and aromatic compounds, such as 
vanillin and syringaldehyde. 
2.5.1. Furfural and HMF degradation pathway 
In both aerobic and anaerobic microbes, the most commonly observed native mechanism of 
detoxifying furfural and HMF employs reducing equivalents (e.g., NADH or NADPH) to convert the 
aldehydes into the corresponding alcohols, furfuryl alcohol and 5-(hydroxymethyl) furfuryl alcohol 
==PAGE:21==

(also referred as HMF alcohol), which are less toxic [43]. However, this approach is not optimal as it 
consumes reducing equivalents and leads to dead-end compounds that persist as impurities. 
Alternatively, microbial conversion of these compounds to metabolites that can enter, for instance, 
the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle and be used as carbon and energy sources is much more desirable 
[43], [44].

To that end, Wierckx et. al (2010) reported the relevant discovery of a metabolic pathway in the soil 
isolate Cupriavidus basilensis HMF14 for the complete catabolism of furfural and HMF into the TCA 
cycle via a common intermediate, 2-furoic acid [46]. In silico and physiological analyses identified a 
series of microbes with putative homologous gene clusters, including Burkholderia phytofirmans 
[45]. As can be seen in Figure 2.4, in both cases, both aldehydes are converted to the corresponding 
acids (2-furoic acid from furfural and 2,5-furandicarboxylic acid from HMF) by oxidoreductases. A 
2,5-furan dicarboxylic acid decarboxylase generates 2-furoic acid as a common intermediate, which 
is then converted to 2-furoyl-CoA that is ultimately incorporated into the TCA cycle via 2-oxo-
glutarate.  
In the same study, the authors constructed a plasmid containing the identified gene cluster from C. 
basilensis HMF14 and expressed it in a heterogeneous host (P. putida S12) that lacks the capacity to 
grow on HMF or furfural [45]. The resulting strain, (P. putida S12_HMF), was shown to be able to 
utilize either furfural or HMF as sole carbon source, at a growth rate of 0.23 h−1 and a yield of 40% 
(C-mol biomass/C-mol substrate). This growth rate was comparable with the growth rate of C. 
basilensis HMF14 on furfural and HMF (0.22 and 0.25 h−1, respectively) [45]. However, no literature 
was found regarding the transformation of this pathway in E. coli. In a separate study, the same 
authors also developed a strain capable of producing high yields and titers of 2,5-furandicarboxylic 
acid, a highly sought replacement for terephthalic acid, via the use of this pathway [47]. Overall, this 
discovery led the way for aerobic conversion of furfural and HMF into a usable carbon source for 
both biomass growth and for manufacturing value-added products [43], [45]. 
2.5.2. Vanillin and syringaldehyde degradation pathway 
During the past few decades, some novel isolates were shown to have the ability to degrade lignin 
and catabolize lignin derivatives such as vanillin and syringaldehyde.  Among them, Sphingobium 
sp. strain SYK-6 possess the best characterized catabolic systems for lignin-derived aromatic 
compounds [48]. This bacterium is capable of utilizing various types of lignin-derived monoaryls 
such as ferulic acid, vanillin, and syringaldehyde as its sole carbon and energy sources [48].  
As can be seen in Figure 2.5, these aromatic compounds, derived from guaiacyl and syringyl units, 
are degraded through vanillate and syringate, respectively. Both are common intermediate 
metabolites in the biodegradation of lignin. Vanillate is then converted to protocatechuate (PCA) by 
the action of vanillate/3MGA O-demethylase (LigM). The resulting PCA is further metabolized via 
the PCA 4,5-cleavage pathway that includes a step catalyzed by PCA 4,5-dioxygenase (LigAB). 
Syringate is converted to 3MGA by syringate O-demethylase (DesA), whose sequence is 49% similar 
to that of LigM in Sphingobium sp. SYK-6 [48]. The resulting 3MGA can be metabolized by 
branching into three pathways. Among them, the gallate cleavage pathway is crucially involved in 
the process since it allows further degradation from gallic acid to pyruvate and oxaloacetate as central 
cell metabolites. 
The bacterial degradation of vanillin through the PCA 3,4-cleavage pathway enzymes has also been 
investigated in Pseudomonas strains [49], [50], Acinetobacter baylyi ADP1 [51], Caulobacter 
crescentus [52], C. glutamicum ATCC 13032 [53], and Rhodococcus jostii RHA1 [54]. Additionally, 
the degradation of syringaldehyde by other bacteria than Sphingobium sp. SYK-6 has also been

3.7. 
Preparation of E. coli AFR-1 electrocompetent cells 
Some colonies of E. coli strain AFR-1 were streaked from an LB agar plate and grown overnight in 
5 mL LB media, supplemented with 1 g/L NaOAc at 37 ºC and 250 rpm. Cultures were placed on ice

centrifuged again at 6500 g at 4 ºC for 7 minutes. The supernatant was discarded, and the cells were 
resuspended in 1 mL ice-cold MQ water, transferred to 1.5 mL pre-cooled Eppendorf tubes on ice, 
and centrifuged 2 minutes at 4000 g at 4 ºC. The supernatant was removed by pipetting and the pellets 
were gently resuspended in 200 µL MQ water and kept on ice until used. 
3.8. 
Plasmid transformation 
Aliquots of 45 µL of freshly made E. coli AFR-1 electrocompetent cells were added to pre-cooled 
1.5 mL Eppendorf tubes and mixed with 5 µL plasmid on ice, gently stirring with the pipette tip. The 
mixture was transferred to pre-cooled electro-transformation cuvettes (0.1 cm gap, Bio-Rad) and kept 
on ice. The cuvettes were electroporated at 1.8 kV, and 950 µL LB media were added immediately 
after electroporation. The cells were incubated for 1 hour at 37 ºC and 250 rpm for recovery. A total 
of 50 µL of the culture was plated on LB agar selection plates containing 10 µg/mL gentamicin and 
incubated overnight at 37 ºC. 
3.9. 
Degradation pathways assessment 
E. coli AFR-1 was grown overnight at 37 ºC and 250 rpm and made electrocompetent (see Section 
3.5). The strain was transformed by electroporation with the corresponding plasmid (see Section 3.6). 
A single colony of each of the transformed strains was grown over day in 1 mL LB media supplanted 
with 1 g/L NaOAc and 10 µg/mL gentamicin at 37 ºC and 250 rpm. E. coli AFR-1 was also grown 
as negative control under the same conditions but without antibiotic. After over-day cultivation, 50 
µL of the culture were transferred to 5 mL M9 extra media supplemented with 20 g/L xylose, 5 g/L 
yeast extract, 1 g/L NaOAc and 10 µg/mL gentamicin (for the plasmid bearing strains only) and 
grown overnight at 37 ºC and 250 rpm.  
The overnight cultures of both plasmid bearing and no-plasmid bearing strains were diluted to an 
initial OD600 of 0.1 in fresh media supplemented with 20 g/L xylose, 5 g/L yeast extract, 1 g/L NaOAc 
and 10 µg/mL gentamicin (for the plasmid bearing strains only) and incubated for 2-3 h until OD600 
doubled to 0.2. Blanks were also prepared for each experiment. When the cultures reached an OD600 
of 0.2, the pTet promoter was induced by the addition of anhydrotetracycline to get a final 
concentration in the media of 0.5 µg/mL. The inducer was also added in the same concentration to 
the negative control and to the blanks to ensure the same conditions. One hour after induction, the 
corresponding inhibitory compound was added to the plasmid-bearing culture, the negative control

samples and calibration standards were added to a HPLC plate. As a control, 1 mL MQ water was

4. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
In this section, the results of the toxicity assessment of different inhibitors commonly present in 
lignocellulosic hydrolysates, as well as some of their biological degradation products, and a synthetic 
hydrolysate combining them all, are presented and discussed. The tolerance of the selected host 
microorganism for this study, E. coli AFR-1, is evaluated and discussed in an industrially-relevant 
context.  Additionally, the results from testing different plasmid-based degradation pathways 
transformed in E. coli AFR-1 are shown and discussed, identifying successful degradation steps and 
bottlenecks that would become targets for optimization. 
4.1. 
Toxicity assessment 
The first part of this project focused on assessing the tolerance of the host microorganism (E. coli 
AFR-1) to different inhibitory compounds that are usually present at different concentrations in the 
hemicellulosic fraction of lignocellulosic hydrolysates. Both single compound experiments and 
synthetic hydrolysate experiments were done. The composition of a real raw lignocellulosic

However, even if E. coli AFR-1 was shown to be able to grow well in UO5-C5 synthetic hydrolysate 
at 20 g/L xylose, it must be noted that, from an industrial perspective, it is too diluted. In the context 
of industrial biomanufacturing, to produce a low-medium value-added product, such as a bulk 
chemical, as a rule of thumb a minimum product titer of 100-200 g/L must be achieved in order to 
make the process economically feasible. Assuming an average product yield on substrate of 0.5 g/g, 
a substrate consumption in the range of 200-400 g/L would be required. However, to achieve that 
substrate uptake from a lignocellulosic hydrolysate, this needs to be very concentrated, which also 
means accumulation of inhibitory compounds during the course of the fermentation. This is one of 
the main challenges of using lignocellulosic hydrolysates as feedstock for biomanufacturing at 
industrial scale and the drive of this project. 
In order to investigate the tolerance limit of E. coli AFR-1 to the synthetic hydrolysate UO5-C5 and 
the corresponding maximum xylose consumption before reaching total inhibition, a new toxicity 
experiment was designed. The purpose of this experiment was to simulate a fed-batch fermentation 
using concentrated UO5-C5 hydrolysate as continuous feed to ensure a constant xylose concentration 
of 20 g/L. In that way, xylose concentration would be kept low enough to avoid substrate inhibition 
and the initial ICs concentration would be low as well. However, when adding more hydrolysate, ICs 
would accumulate assuming no biological degradation from E. coli AFR-1. 
A synthetic UO5-C5 hydrolysate was prepared in M9 “extra” media supplemented with 20 g/L xylose 
and the respective concentration of the other sugars shown in Table 4.1, 5 g/L yeast extract, 1 g/L 
sodium acetate and ICs (furfural, HMF, vanillin, vanillic acid, syringaldehyde and benzoic acid) in 
the concentrations shown in Table 4.1. It should be noted that sodium acetate, which comes from 
neutralization of acetic acid at pH 6.8, is not considered an IC when using E. coli AFR-1, since this 
strain is an acetate auxotroph, so it gradually consumes acetate and it would not accumulate. The 
tolerance of E. coli AFR-1 to acetate was tested and it was shown to be able to grow at 10 g/L acetate 
(see Appendix B). The solution containing 100% the ICs concentration from UO5-C5 was serially 
diluted in M9 “extra” media supplemented with 20 g/L xylose and the respective concentration of the 
other sugars, 5 g/L yeast extract and 1 g/L sodium acetate. The methodology and experimental 
conditions of the toxicity experiment were as described in Section 3.4. The obtained results are shown 
in Figure 4.3. 
As can be observed in Figure 4.3, the growth rate of E. coli AFR-1 decreased from 0.5 h-1 at 10% 
hydrolysate (28.6 g/L xylose) to 0.2 h-1 at 56% hydrolysate (160 g/L xylose). Higher hydrolysate 
concentrations (75%) led to total inhibition. Regarding the lag phase duration, it was delayed 2 h 
compared to the control at 10% hydrolysate, but more than 20 h at 56% hydrolysate. It should be 
noted that each growth curve from Figure 4.3-A would be representative in terms of maximum growth 
rate and lag phase duration of a batch fermentation using the corresponding hydrolysate concentration 
(assuming no substrate inhibition). So, if a batch fermentation were to be done using around 50% 
hydrolysate as feed, a lag phase of 25 h and a poor growth rate would be expected, leading to high 
operating costs and low productivity. However, during a fed-batch fermentation, starting with a low 
hydrolysate concentration (e.g., 10%) would allow to get a short lag phase and high cell density during 
the first hours of operation, leading to lower operating costs and higher productivity, even if the 
growth rate would gradually decrease until getting to a 50% hydrolysate concentration.

4.1.2. Single compound toxicity assessment 
The toxicity of a total of 10 single compounds was assessed in this study: 5 inhibitors present in 
hydrolysate UO5-C5, namely furfural, HMF, vanillin, syringaldehyde, and benzoic acid, and 5 
biological degradation intermediates that can be formed from them, such as vanillic acid and PCA 
from vanillin, syringic acid from syringaldehyde, furfuryl alcohol from furfural and 2-furoic acid 
from both furfural and HMF. These experiments were performed as described in Section 3.4. 
4.1.2.1. 
Main inhibitors 
The aim of these experiments was to assess the tolerance of the host microorganism to each inhibitor 
within a concentration range that could be relevant when using UO5-C5 hydrolysate as feedstock, 
and to identify which compounds are the most toxic. The resulting growth curves from the toxicity 
assessment of the inhibitory compounds present in hydrolysate UO5-C5 are shown in Figure 4.4. 
Different types of growth inhibition can be observed by inspecting the growth curves. Some 
inhibitors, like furfural and vanillin, can significantly delay cell growth, especially in the range of 
0.4-1 g/L. Total inhibition of cell growth was observed with vanillin at 1 g/L. Other inhibitors, like 
HMF and benzoic acid, did not seem to have a strong impact in the lag phase duration even at 1 g/L. 
However, all the inhibitors showed a clear impact in the growth rate and the maximum OD within the 
tested concentration ranges. In order to be able to quantify and compare the obtained results, the 
corresponding growth rates (Figure 4.5) and lag times (Figure 4.6) for each inhibitor at different 
concentrations were calculated. 
Furfural and 5-hydroxymethylfurfural (HMF), both furanic aldehydes, showed different toxicity 
levels. 1 g/L, HMF had almost no effect on the lag phase duration and the inhibition of the growth 
rate was around 30% compared to the control. That concentration is similar to the one in concentrated 
UO5-C5 hydrolysate (1.1 g/L HMF). Similar studies using E. coli K12 DH5α showed 50% growth 
inhibition at 1 g/L HMF and total inhibition at 2 g/L [58]. Furfural showed a long lag phase of 20 h 
and a growth inhibition of 35% at 1 g/L. Furfural results are in agreement with what is observed in 
existing literature, since 33% inhibition was observed using E. coli K12 MG1655 at 1.15 g/L furfural 
(12 mM) [59].  However, it should be noted that even if furfural seems to be more toxic to E. coli 
AFR-1 than HMF, its concentration in UO5-C5 hydrolysate is only 0.06 g/L, and at that 
concentration, its toxicity is very small (< 4% inhibition). 
Vanillin and syringaldehyde, both aromatic aldehydes, showed the highest toxicity. A 40% growth 
inhibition was observed with syringaldehyde at 0.5 g/L. That concentration is similar to the one in 
concentrated UO5-C5 hydrolysate (0.6 g/L). To get the same level of inhibition, 0.42 g/L vanillin 
were required. 0.5 g/L of both compounds caused a 3 h longer lag phase compared to the control, but 
much longer lag times were observed with vanillin at higher concentrations (15 h at 0.75 g/L). 
However, at lower concentrations syringaldehyde was more toxic than vanillin. For example, 0.12 
g/L syringaldehyde caused an inhibition of 17% ± 9%, while vanillin at 0.13 g/L caused an inhibition 
of only 3% ± 3%. Additionally, vanillin was shown to cause total growth inhibition of E. coli AFR-1 
at 1 g/L. These results show higher toxicity of vanillin and syringaldehyde on E. coli than what was 
observed from literature. Pereira et al. (2016), tested these compounds in E. coli K12 DH5α and found

Degradation intermediates 
The aim of these experiments was to assess the tolerance of the host microorganism to some of the 
potential biological degradation products that could be formed from the main inhibitors within a 
concentration range that could be met when using UO5-C5 hydrolysate as feedstock, and to compare 
the toxicity of the main inhibitors with their degradation products. The resulting growth curves from 
these toxicity assessment experiments are shown in Figure 4.8. 
As it can be observed in Figure 4.8, furfuryl alcohol has a strong impact on the lag phase duration in 
the same way as furfural. However, the other tested compounds showed no effect (PCA) or a very 
slight effect (2-furoic acid, vanillic acid, and syringic acid) on the lag time within the tested 
concentrations. It can also be seen that 2-furoic acid and syringic acid significantly affected the 
maximum OD600. In order to be able to quantify and compare the obtained results, the corresponding 
growth rates (Figure 4.9) and lag times (Figure 4.10) for each biological degradation product at 
different concentrations were calculated. 
Furfuryl alcohol can be produced by reducing furfural, while 2-furoic acid is the direct oxidation 
product of furfural and an intermediate product of HMF catabolism. On one hand, according to 
literature, most microorganisms possess general oxidoreductases (e.g., alcohol dehydrogenases) that 
are able to degrade furfural to furfuryl alcohol [45]. However, that leads to a dead-end product that is 
still toxic. On the other hand, the oxidative degradation pathways of furfural and HMF require very 
specific gene clusters that can lead to full catabolism of both inhibitors [44], [45].

4.2. 
Test of plasmid-based degradation pathways 
A total of four plasmid-based degradation pathways, designed and constructed before the start of this 
project, were planned to be transformed into E. coli AFR-1 and tested during this study. Particularly, 
one plasmid containing the complete furfural and HMF degradation pathway (pSIJ862), and three 
plasmids containing different steps of the vanillin and syringaldehyde degradation pathway (pJD22, 
pJD23 and pJD25) were planned to be tested. The genotype of the four plasmids is shown in Section 
3.3 and the corresponding plasmid maps are shown in Appendix C. 
The first step before testing the performance of the plasmids was to sequence them in order to verify 
that they were constructed as designed and look for potential mutations. To do so, the four plasmids 
were extracted and purified as described in Section 3.7 and samples were prepared and sent to an 
external company (UNVEIL) to obtain full plasmid sequences as described in Section 3.8. The 
corresponding nucleotide sequences were aligned and compared on Benchling with the ones that were 
designed. As a result, it was found out that the sequences of plasmids pJD22, pJD23 and pJD25 were 
correct, but point mutations were found in plasmid pSIJ862. It was therefore decided to start testing 
the verified plasmids and put pSIJ862 on hold, as it would need to be reconstructed. However, due to 
time limitations it was not possible to do that, so eventually pSIJ862 could not be tested in this project. 
4.2.1. Vanillin & syringaldehyde degradation pathways 
Vanillin and syringaldehyde degradation pathways are complex and different routes are possible (see 
Figure 2.5, Section 2.5). To cope with that complexity, three different plasmids were designed and 
constructed, each containing 4 genes responsible for 3-4 degradation steps, in a way that the final 
degradation product of the first plasmid-based degradation pathway would be the initial substrate for 
the next one. It should be noted that in order to achieve full catabolism of vanillin, all the degradation 
steps from plasmids pJD22, pJD23 and pJD25 would be required, but to fully catabolize 
syringaldehyde, only plasmids pJD22 and pJD25 would be necessary.  
In order to test the plasmids, the first step was to prepare electrocompetent E. coli AFR-1 cells and 
then transform the plasmids as described in Sections 3.5 and 3.6 respectively. The ability of the new 
plasmid-bearing strains to degrade the corresponding inhibitors was tested as described in Section 
3.9, using E. coli AFR-1 was used as negative control and media blanks were used to inspect any 
potential inhibitor degradation in the media without the action of any microorganism. Samples for 
OD600 measurement and HPLC analysis were taken at time 0 h (when the inhibitor was added) and 
after 24 h. The measured ODs from these experiments are shown in Appendix D. The methodology 
to prepare HPLC samples and to carry out HPLC analysis is described in Sections 3.10 and 3.11 
respectively. 
4.2.1.1. 
Test of plasmid pJD22 
Plasmid pJD22 contains 4 genes, namely desV, ligV, desA and ligM, all of them from Sphingobium 
sp. strain SYK-6, that can act on both vanillin and syringaldehyde and degrade them to PCA and 
gallic acid respectively. The ability of bacterium Sphingobium sp. SYK-6 to degrade various lignin-
derived compounds including vanillin and syringaldehyde has been thoroughly investigated over the 
past two decades. 
==PAGE:44==
Establishment of Microbial Cell Factories Tolerant to Lignocellulosic Hydrolysate 
34 
 
Kanimura et al. (2017) carried out a study of Sphingobium sp. SYK-6 enzymes involved in 
syringaldehyde oxidation and identified nine gene products that exhibited oxidation activities for 
syringaldehyde to produce syringate. Among these, the desV and ligV genes were most highly and 
constitutively transcribed in cultures grown with syringaldehyde. Disruption of the desV gene resulted 
in a significant reduction in syringaldehyde oxidation activity and growth. A double mutant in both 
desV and ligV was not able to grow on syringaldehyde [62]. In the same study, the desV gene was 
cloned and expressed in E. coli, and the recombinant protein was purified and characterized. The 
enzyme was categorized as a broad range aromatic aldehyde dehydrogenase since it could act on 
many different substrates. It showed similar activity against syringaldehyde and vanillin. The enzyme 
could also use NADP+, but activity with NAD+ was 7.3-fold higher [62]. 
In another study performed by Masai et al. (2007), the ligV gene of Sphingomonas sp. SYK-6, 
encoding vanillin dehydrogenase, was identified by functional complementation of the vanillin-
degradation deficient strain Sphingomonas paucimobilis. The gene was cloned and expressed in E. 
coli and cell extracts were assayed [63]. Its activity with NAD+ was approximately 90 times higher 
than that achieved with NADP. LigV also has a wide substrate range. It exhibited the highest 
dehydrogenase activity toward vanillin, but showed relatively high levels of activity toward 
benzaldehyde, 4-hydroxybenzaldehyde, 3,4-dihydroxybenzaldehyde, and 3-methoxybenzaldehyde 
[63]. A ligV mutant of Sphingomonas sp. SYK-6 abolished normal growth on vanillin [63]. In a later 
study, the ligV gene was cloned and expressed in E. coli, and the recombinant protein was purified 
and characterized. The enzyme was also found to be active against syringaldehyde but had a clear 
preference for vanillin [62]. 
The desA gene, encoding syringate O-demethylase, was first identified by Masai et al. (2004) by the 
complementation of a Sphingomonas sp. SYK-6 mutant defective in degradation of vanillate and 
syringate [64]. Cell extract of E. coli carrying desA converted syringate to 3-O-methylgallate (3MGA) 
only when tetrahydrofolate was added to the reaction mixture. The enzyme catalyzes the transfer of 
the methyl moiety of syringate to tetrahydrofolate, forming 5-methyltetrahydropholate [64]. 
Disruption of desA in Sphingomonas sp. SYK-6 resulted in a growth defect on syringate but did not 
affect growth on vanillate, indicating that desA is essential to syringate degradation [64]. The ligM 
gene of Sphingomonas sp. SYK-6 was discovered by functional complementation of E. coli by Abe 
et al. (2005). Crude cell extracts of the recombinant E. coli host containing LigM converted vanillate 
and 3-O-methylgallate (3MGA) to protocatechuate and gallate, respectively, with similar specific 
activities, and only in the presence of tetrahydrofolate [65]. 
In this study, the performance of the plasmid pJD22 transformed in E. coli AFR-1 was tested using 
vanillin and syringaldehyde individually as substrates. The corresponding degradation pathways and 
HPLC results are shown in Figure 4.11 (vanillin as substrate) and Figure 4.12 (syringaldehyde as 
substrate). When using 0.5 g/L vanillin as substrate, it was observed that, after 24 h of growth, the 
plasmid-bearing strain was able to completely degrade vanillin (Figure 4.11-B). A high peak (160 
mAU at 280 nm) at a retention time of 5.94 min that corresponds to vanillic acid and a very small 
peak (2 mAU at 280 nm) at a retention time of 3.9 min that corresponds to PCA were also detected 
after 24 h in the plasmid-bearing strain. These results suggest that almost all the vanillin was degraded 
to vanillic acid, meaning that the ligV and desV genes had a very good performance. However, the

The results of testing the recombinant E. coli strain AFR-1 bearing pJD22 should be put in context 
regarding the potential toxicity alleviation of UO5-C5 hydrolysate that could be achieved thanks to 
the successful degradation steps. From the toxicity experiments with synthetic hydrolysate, it was 
observed that E. coli AFR-1 could tolerate up to 56% hydrolysate (v/v). That value corresponds to a 
maximum xylose consumption of 160 g/L and an accumulation of 0.07 g/L vanillin, 0.04 g/L vanillic 
acid and 0.35 g/L syringaldehyde. Assuming that E. coli strain AFR-1 bearing pJD22 can fully 
degrade vanillin to vanillic acid and syringaldehyde to syringic acid, the potential toxicity alleviation 
can be estimated.  
From the single compound toxicity assessment, it is known that, on one hand, 0.07 g/L vanillin 
inhibits growth by a 3%. If that concentration is converted to vanillic acid and summing up the 0.04 
g/L vanillic acid already present at 56% UO5-C5 hydrolysate, the resulting total vanillic acid 
concentration would be 0.11 g/L. At that concentration, vanillic acid could inhibit growth by a 10%, 
so it is suggested that this one-step degradation would not be that beneficial. It is clear that further 
degradation is required to mitigate the toxicity of vanillin and vanillic acid. For instance, considering 
only one more step, 0.11 g/L PCA is known to have no toxic effect to the host microorganism. On 
the other hand, 0.35 g/L syringaldehyde is known to inhibit growth by a 32%, whereas 0.3 g/L 
syringic acid inhibited growth by 18%. In this case, it can be argued that successful detoxification 
step could be achieved if degrading syringaldehyde to syringic acid. Thus, the 160 g/L maximum 
xylose consumption from UO5-C5 could be potentially increased to 180 g/L. However, that would 
still not be enough to obtain industrially relevant product titers (100 – 200 g/L), even if syringic acid 
was the only toxic compound to accumulate during the fermentation.  
It can be concluded that further degradation of vanillin and syringaldehyde is required to significantly 
detoxify UO5-C5 hydrolysate. From an industrial point of view, full catabolism of the inhibitors all 
the way to the TCA cycle should be targeted, instead of only a number of degradation steps that lead 
==PAGE:48==

38 
 
to less toxic compounds, in order to facilitate future downstream processing operations to recover and 
purify the product. Usually, downstream processing accounts for 70-90% of the total operational costs 
in bio-based manufacturing [67], so minimizing the number of chemical compounds that are present 
in the fermentation broth is desired, especially when manufacturing low molecular weight 
extracellular products like most bulk chemicals.  
In order to optimize the performance of pJD22 expressed in E. coli AFR-1, the observed bottlenecks 
must be solved. From the obtained results, two bottlenecks were identified: the genes desA and ligM, 
responsible for the conversion of vanillic acid to PCA (ligM), syringic acid to 3MGA (desA) and 
3MGA to gallic acid (ligM). It should be noted that the corresponding enzymes (DesA and LigM) are 
both tetrahydrofolate-dependent O-demethylases. The O-methylated aromatic compounds are the 
donors of the methyl group that is transferred to tetrahydrofolate (THF), forming 5-methyl-THF. In 
the studies where these two genes were cloned and expressed in E. coli and the enzymes were 
characterized [64], [65], it was reported that the enzymes were active only when the cell lysate was 
supplemented with THF. Unfortunately, no literature was found of in vivo assays in E. coli. 
Before testing pJD22, it was known that the enzymes DesA and LigM required THF as cofactor and, 
by inspecting E. coli online databases (EcoCyc), it was found out that E. coli K-12 MG1655 (ancestor 
of E. coli AFR-1) possess a pathway to biosynthesize THF [68] and that it can also regenerate THF 
from 5-methyl-THF via the methionine synthesis by the native gene metH [69]. However, the 
obtained results showed almost no activity of both THF-dependent enzymes, suggesting that the THF 
availability in E. coli AFR-1 might be insufficient for the introduced O-demethylation steps. 
Considering that it was previously shown that both enzymes can be successfully expressed in E. coli 
folded in their active conformation and some activity was detected in this study, it is hypothesised 
here that the main cause of the bottleneck lies in a cofactor limitation. 
It should be noted that E. coli has been reported to be unable to uptake folates [70], so supplementing 
the media with folate would not be a solution. However, E. coli, like many other bacteria, can 
transport the folate breakdown product, para-aminobenzoate-glutamate (PABA-glu), via the AbgT 
transporter and use it for bacterial folate synthesis [70]. Nevertheless, it is unclear if the bottleneck 
lies in the THF biosynthesis or in its regeneration from 5-methyl-THF. In a recent study conducted 
by Cai et al. (2021), a plasmid containing desA and ligM genes from Sphingomonas sp. SYK-6 was 
transformed into Rhodococcus opacus to convert syringate into gallate. In that study, similar problems 
were found: a maximum 10% conversion of syringate to gallate was obtained with and without 
supplementing the media with 0.5 mM THF [71]. Only when constructing an in vivo THF recycling 
system bearing metF and ligH genes from Sphingomonas sp. SYK-6 along with desA and ligM, a 
35% conversion of syringate to gallate was obtained after 50 h with an initial OD600 of 0.8. When 
increasing the initial OD600 to 5, a 100% conversion was obtained after 36 h. Therefore, it is proposed 
as future work to further investigate the native E. coli THF biosynthesis and regeneration pathways 
and engineer the host to either upregulate the native pathways or introduce a recombinant pathway 
that enhance THF synthesis and regeneration. As an alternative, homologue genes that do not depend 
on THF as a cofactor could be tested and, if successful, replace ligM and desA genes in pJD22.

4.2.1.2. 
Test of plasmid pJD23 
Plasmid pJD23 contains 4 genes, namely ligA, ligB, ligC and ligI, all of them from Sphingobium sp. 
strain SYK-6. As shown in Figure 4.13-A, the encoded enzymes in this degradation pathway can 
degrade protocatechuate (PCA), the end product of the pathway enclosed in pJD22 when using 
vanillin as substrate, to 4-oxalomesaconate (OMA). 
The genes encoding the protocatechuate 4,5-dioxygenase of Sphingomonas sp. SYK6 were first 
identified by Noda et al. (1990) by functional complementation of P. putida. A DNA fragment that 
conferred the ability to grow on vanillate and protocatechuate was sequenced and found to contain 
two genes, ligA and ligB, which encode the two subunits of the enzyme [72]. The two genes were 
initially cloned and overexpressed in E. coli using the lac promoter, but the highly expressed proteins 
formed inclusion bodies [72]. His tag-fused ligAB genes were expressed again in E. coli in a later 
study under the control of the T7 promoter and produced in large quantities in soluble form [73]. 
The ligC gene of Sphingomonas sp. SYK6 was expressed in E. coli by Masai et al. (2000) and the 
recombinant protein was purified to near homogeneity [74]. Production of 2-pyrone-4,6-
dicarboxylate (PDC) from 2-hydroxy-4-carboxymuconate semialdehyde hemiacetal (CHMS) 
catalysed by LigC was confirmed in the presence of NADP+ by electrospray ionization-mass 
spectrometry (EI-MS) and gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) [74]. The ligI gene of 
Sphingomonas sp. SYK6 was also found by Masai et al. (1999) in the region of the ligAB genes [75]. 
The gene was cloned, expressed in E. coli, and purified to near-homogeneity. The protein, which is a 
monomer, was found to be inhibited by the addition of thiol reagents, suggesting a cysteine residue 
in the catalytic site [75]. The insertional inactivation of the gene in Sphingomonas sp. SYK6 led to 
the complete loss of 2-pyrone-4,6-dicarboxylate hydrolase activity and to a growth defect on vanillate 
[75]. 
In this study, the performance of pJD23 transformed into E. coli AFR-1 was tested using 0.3 g/L PCA 
as substrate. The corresponding degradation pathway and HPLC results are shown in Figure 4.13. It 
was observed that, after 24 h of growth, the plasmid-bearing strain was able to completely degrade 
PCA (Figure 4.13-B). This means that all the PCA was degraded at least to CHMS, the first 
degradation product, meaning that the ligAB genes (which encode protocatechuate 4,5-dioxygenase) 
had a very good performance. 
However, there is no visibility of the performance of the next steps (genes ligC and ligI) due to the 
lack of commercially available HPLC standards for CHMS, PDC and OMA. It is suggested as future 
work to employ different analytical methods rather than HPLC, such as GC-MS, to be able to assess 
the performance of the ligC and ligI genes. In any case, it must be noted that no unidentified peaks 
that could correspond to the degradation products were observed in the chromatogram of the plasmid 
bearing strain after 24 h (Figure 4.13-B), which suggests that they are not visible within the employed 
detection range (230-300 nm).  From the negative control results (Figure 4.13-C), it can be observed 
that E. coli AFR-1 was not able to degrade PCA to something else. Finally, the results from the blank 
(Figure 4.13 D) showed that PCA did not degrade by itself in the media.

4.2.1.3. 
Test of plasmid pJD25 
Plasmid pJD25 contains 4 genes, namely glllA, gllB, gllC and gllD, all of them from Pseudomonas 
putida strain KT2440. As shown in Figure 4.14-A, the encoded enzymes in this degradation pathway 
can degrade gallic acid (the end product of the pathway encoded in pJD22 when using syringaldehyde 
as substrate) or OMA (the end product of the pathway encoded in pJD23), to pyruvate and 
oxaloacetate. These metabolites are able to enter the TCA cycle. Thus, pJD25 contains the gallic acid 
catabolic pathway from P. putida KT2440.  
Gallate dioxygenase (GlllA) is a ring-cleavage dioxygenase that acts specifically on gallate to produce 
the keto-tautomer of 4-oxalomesaconate [76], [77]. The protein is a homotrimer and uses Fe2+ as the 
main cofactor. The enzyme was found to share a common ancestor with protocatechuate 4,5-

dioxygenase (LigAB) and has a two-domain architecture that might have evolved from the fusion of 
the large and small subunits of the latter [77]. Nogales et al. (2005) found that expression of the glllA 
gene was induced when P. putida KT2440 cells grew in the presence of gallate [77]. In that study, 
the gene was cloned and expressed in E. coli, and the recombinant protein was purified to 
homogeneity to characterize the enzyme activity [77]. 
4-oxalomesaconate tautomerase (GllD) is an enzyme that interconverts two of the tautomers of 4-
oxalomesaconate (OMA), namely (1Z,3Z)-4-hydroxybuta-1,3-diene-1,2,4-tricarboxylate (enol form) 
and (1E)-4-oxobut-1-ene-1,2,4-tricarboxylate (keto form), a reaction that was believed to be 
spontaneous prior to the discovery of the enzyme by Nogales et al. (2011). In that study, the gene was 
cloned and expressed in E. coli, and the cell-free extract was used to characterize the enzyme activity 
[76]. The authors also found out that disruption of the gllD gene did not prevent growth of the 
organism in 5 mM gallate, but the mutant strain grew poorly (OD600 < 0.1) at gallate concentrations 
lower than 2 mM, suggesting that a functional gllD gene is essential for efficient gallate degradation 
at low concentrations. 
The enzyme encoded by gllB is a new type of 4-oxalomesaconate hydratases that shares no sequence 
similarity to other enzymes with the same functionality, such as those encoded by ligJ in 
Sphingomonas sp. SYK6 [76]. The enzyme forms a homohexamer and contains one Zn2+ atom per 
monomer [76], although the enzyme is capable of using Fe2+ and Co2+ with similar efficiency [78]. 
The substrate for the enzyme is only the enol-form of 4-oxalomesaconate, (1Z,3Z)-4-hydroxybuta-
1,3-diene-1,2,4-tricarboxylate [76]. The gllB gene of P. putida KT2440 was cloned and 
overexpressed recombinantly in E. coli, and the cell-free extract was used to characterize the enzyme 
activity [76]. 
The gllC gene, which encodes a metal-dependent aldolase (requires Mn2+ or Mg2+ for activity) is 
located in the gallate degradation gene cluster of P. putida KT2440. Nogales et al. (2011) showed 
that a mutant with disrupted gllC gene was not able to grow on gallate [76]. In that study, when the 
gene was cloned and overexpressed in E. coli, extracts from the recombinant host exhibited 
significant 4-hydroxy-4-methyl-2-oxoglutarate aldolase activity. It was shown that the gllC gene is 
essential for gallate degradation and encodes a 4-hydroxy-4-methyl-2-oxoglutarate aldolase whose 
function cannot be replaced by that of other aldolases present in the organism [76]. 
In this study, the performance of pJD25 transformed in E. coli AFR-1 was tested using 0.5 g/L gallic 
acid as substrate. It should be noted that the results of the initial qualitative screening were unclear, 
since the apparent gallic acid degradation in the plasmid-bearing strain after 24 h could be within the 
error range. Therefore, the experiment to test pJD25 was repeated with biological triplicates, which

5. CONCLUSION 
The tolerance of the host microorganism, a xylose-utilizing and acetate auxotroph E. coli strain, to a 
synthetic lignocellulosic hydrolysate that contains 286 g/L xylose and the following inhibitors: HMF 
(1.14 g/L), furfural (0.06 g/L), vanillic acid (0.07 g/L), vanillin (0.12 g/L), benzoic acid (0.15 g/L) 
and syringaldehyde (0.63 g/L), was assessed in this project. According to the obtained results, it was 
determined that the host microorganism can tolerate up to 56% of the aforementioned inhibitory 
compound concentrations. Thus, the maximum amount of xylose that this cell factory could consume 
from that hydrolysate and convert into a product of interest would be around 160 g/L. Assuming an 
average product yield on substrate of 0.5 g/g, that would lead to a product titer of around 80 g/L.  
Even if these results may seem promising, product titers of at least 100-200 g/L are usually required 
at industrial scale for bulk chemicals production in order to make the process economically feasible. 
It should be noted as well that the observed growth rates at lab scale could be lower at industrial scale 
due to more heterogeneous conditions (gradients). Additionally, the construction and expression of a 
production pathway along with the degradation pathways in the host is expected to increase the 
metabolic burden and further affect growth. Therefore, it was concluded that the tolerance of the host 
microorganism to the hydrolysate should be improved in order to be able to achieve industrially-
relevant product titers from it in a future. 
The single-compound toxicity experiments revealed firstly that, among the identified inhibitors 
present in UO5-C5 hydrolysate, syringaldehyde was the most toxic for E. coli AFR-1, followed by 
vanillin, furfural, benzoic acid and HMF. It is suggested that aromatic aldehydes are the most toxic 
group of compounds for the host. Secondly, the tested degradation products of the main inhibitors 
were shown to be gradually less toxic along the degradation pathway. For example, at the same 
concentration (0.3 g/L), 30% inhibition was observed with vanillin, but only 10% with vanillic acid 
(first degradation product) and 0% with PCA (second degradation product). 
The test of plasmid-based degradation pathways for vanillin and syringaldehyde in E. coli AFR-1 
allowed to identify some successful degradation steps as well as bottlenecks that should be solved in 
the future to make them work completely. On one side, the test of pJD22, containing 4 genes (ligV, 
desV, desA and ligM) from Sphingobium sp. SYK6, showed only good results for the first degradation 
step. Complete degradation of vanillin to vanillic acid and syringaldehyde to syringic acid catalysed 
by the combined action of DesV and LigV enzymes was observed after 24 h. However, the other two 
enzymes from pJD22 showed very limited activity to convert vanillic acid to PCA (LigM), syringic 
acid to 3MGA (DesA) and 3MGA to gallic acid (LigM). It should be noted that both enzymes, LigM 
and DesA, are tetrahydrofolate-dependent and it is argued that a cofactor limitation could be a likely 
explanation for the observed low activity, which should be further investigated.  
The test of plasmid pJD23, containing 4 different genes (ligA, ligB, ligC and ligI) from Sphingobium 
sp. SYK6, showed good performance of the ligAB genes (encoding the two subunits of a 
protocatechuate 4,5-dioxygenase), since full degradation of PCA was observed after 24 h. However, 
due to the lack of commercially available chromatography standards for CHMS, PDC and OMA, 
there was no visibility of the performance of the next steps (catalysed by LigC and LigI). Lastly, from 
the test of pJD25, containing 4 genes (glllA, gllB, gllC and gllD) from P. putida KT2440, only partial

degradation of gallic acid (33%) was observed after 24 h, which suggests a bottleneck already in the 
first step of the degradation pathway catalysed by the gallate dioxygenase GlllA. It is argued that 
gallic acid transport through the cell membrane could be a limiting factor that should be further 
investigated. The performance of the next degradation steps (catalysed by GllD, GllB and GllC) could 
not be properly assessed due to the lack of commercially available chromatography standards for 
OMA-keto, OMA-enol and 4-carboxy-4-hydroxy-2-oxoadipic acid (CHA). Pyruvate and 
oxaloacetate, if produced, would have been consumed in the TCA cycle, so the only way to determine 
if all the steps worked would be to perform a growth experiment in minimal media using gallic acid 
as sole carbon and energy source. 
It was concluded that, if a cell factory based on E. coli AFR-1 harbouring the tested degradation 
pathways were to be used to detoxify UO5-C5 hydrolysate, it would only be able to degrade vanillin 
to vanillic acid and syringaldehyde to syringic acid. When combining the plasmid performance results 
with the observed current tolerance of E. coli AFR-1 to UO5-C5 hydrolysate and the single-
compound toxicity assessment results, no toxicity alleviation would be expected from vanillin 
conversion to vanillate at the concentrations that both are present in the hydrolysate and a maximum 
14% toxicity alleviation could be expected due to syringaldehyde conversion to syringate. All in all, 
even if the goal of this project has only been met partially, this is considered a promising first step 
towards establishing bacterial cell factories that are able to both detoxify lignocellulosic hydrolysates 
and produce industrially relevant product titers.

6. FUTURE WORK 
In order to verify the estimated toxicity alleviation of UO5-C5 hydrolysate that could be achieved by 
converting vanillin to vanillic acid and syringaldehyde to syringic acid, which was based only on the 
single compound toxicity results, it would be necessary as future work to perform a growth 
experiment of E. coli AFR-1 bearing pJD22 in synthetic hydrolysate UO5-C5 and compare it with 
the results obtained in this project without the plasmid. 
The HMF and furfural plasmid-based degradation pathway could not be tested during this project due 
to time constrictions. It is proposed as future work that pSIJ862 is constructed again and tested in E. 
coli AFR-1. UO5-C5 hydrolysate has a relevant concentration of HMF (1.14 g/L) that can inhibit 
growth by a 30% just by itself. Thus, it is considered that testing and optimizing the expression of the 
degradation pathway would be a milestone in the detoxification capacity of the cell factory. 
Regarding the vanillin and syringaldehyde degradation pathways, further research is required to make 
all the steps work. A bottleneck involving desA and ligM genes was identified in the test of plasmid 
pJD22. It is known that both enzymes (DesA and LigM) are tetrahydrofolate-dependent and a cofactor 
limitation could be the cause of the observed limited activity. It is proposed that the native THF 
biosynthetic pathway of E. coli is further investigated and engineered to enhance its production and 
recycling capacities. 
With regard to the analytical limitations found when testing pJD23, it is proposed as future work that 
alternative analytical methods, such as GC-MS instead of HPLC, are employed to assess the 
performance of every degradation step contained in pJD23. If it is found out that all the PCA was 
degraded to OMA, that would mean that all the steps worked. Otherwise, new bottlenecks would be 
identified and become targets for optimization. 
Finally, as a strategy to optimize the observed limited gallic acid degradation when testing the plasmid 
pJD25, it is proposed to further investigate the gallic acid transport in the host. It is suggested as 
future work to include in pJD25 the putative gallic acid transport system encoded by galT and galP 
genes from P. putida KTGAL and assess if the resulting strain is able to grow on gallic acid as sole 
carbon source. This approach was shown to be successful in other E. coli strains, so it is thought to 
be a promising solution. Nevertheless, it should be noted that, if in the future the whole vanillin and 
syringaldehyde degradation pathway were to be constructed in a single vector, gallic acid would be 
produced only as an intermediate (it is not present in the hydrolysate) and therefore, in that case, gallic 
acid transport from the outside may not be necessary.
