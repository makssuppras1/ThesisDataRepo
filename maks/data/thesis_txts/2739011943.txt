Sea ice ridges make up an estimated 40-50% of the total area of Arctic sea ice
cover, however there is a gap in the data and analysis of their characteristics on a
large scale. NASA’s ICESat-2 laser altimeter, ATLAS, provides photon elevation
data for the Arctic at a high resolution. The purpose of this paper is to develop a
ridge detection algorithm which will create a surface profile from ICESat-2 data
and identify ridges and their features. The algorithm developed, the DTU-Aalto
Ridge Detection Algorithm (DA-RDA), is successful at creating a surface profile
for full satellite tracks of data, spanning several thousand kilometers. DA-RDA is
able to identify ridges and quantify their characteristics along smaller tracks. The
GUI developed for this algorithm makes the data and surface profile more easily
accessible.
Keywords: remote sensing, sea ice, laser altimetry

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Sea ice is vital resource for the world. Sea ice has been used by humans in
the Arctic for millennia for hunting, transportation, and daily life. It’s a habitat in
and of itself, supporting the lives of thousands of species. Walrus, seals, and polar
bears use sea ice as a hunting and breeding ground, while reindeer and Arctic fox
use it for transportation and foraging [3]. Sea ice and ice leads form a platform for
plankton and algae to grow, feeding generations of migratory birds[3]. Sea ice in the
Arctic plays a vital role in the Arctic, but also for the rest of the world. With climate
change destroying much of the Arctic habitat, the role of sea ice becomes even more
vital. Summer sea ice is shrinking at a rate of 12.2% per decade [4]. According to
the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) Sixth Assessment Report,
there is a high confidence the Arctic Ocean will be ice free after the summer melt
season before 2050[5]. This is important for every aspect of life on Earth, because
sea ice is vital for maintaining the heat exchange balance of the planet.
Figure 1: Sea ice ridges [6]
Sea ice ridges play a vital role in the life cycle of sea ice. According to the
National Snow and Ice Data Center (NSIDC), a sea ice pressure ridge is defined
as a deformation that pushes sea ice into piles that form peaks above surrounding
level sea ice, with sails above the ice and keels below [7]. Sea ice ridges are formed
primarily from young sea ice, which is becoming more prevalent as multi-year ice
melts during increasingly warmer summers[8]. They make up an estimated 40-50%
of the total Arctic sea area, but are severly understudied compared to the more
==PAGE:14==
2
accessible level sea ice [8]. According to Gradinger et. al. (2010), pressure ridges
are crucial for sea ice fauna during periods of extreme summer melt[9]. Additionally,
ridges impact the rate at which sea ice melts - larger keels melt faster than thin
level ice, however the rate between ridges and thicker, stronger multi-year ice is not
very well studied[8]. Ridge sails consolidate and harden if they survive multiple melt
seasons, which would mean they melt slower[8].
Another more commercial impact of sea ice ridges is on the development
of ice breakers for the shipping industry and military uses. Ridges are important
factors to determine ice hull strength and mobility in the Arctic.
To examine riding in the Arctic, photon elevation data from the Ice, Cloud,
and Land Elevation Satellite 2 (ICESat-2) will be analyzed to determine local sea
ice surface level and calculate anomalies above this to determine ridge frequency and
characteristics.
1.2
Objectives and Research Questions
The objectives and goals of this thesis can be summarized as follows:
1. To determine sea ice profile from satellite altimetry data.
2. To create an algorithm to detect sea ice ridges from satellite altimetry data.
3. To validate the algorithm using airborne altimetry data.
4. To develop a graphical user interface (GUI) to improve the accessibility of this
analysis.
5. To compare ridge heights and locations with temporal and geographic variations.
These objectives will be accomplished by considering work done by previous
researchers and using ICESat-2 photon elevation data to complete a new analysis of
sections of sea ice in the Arctic.

2.1
Laser Altimetry
Laser altimetry is a method of active remote sensing that uses principles of
Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) to measure the range between the surface
and the instrument and records time of flight (TOF) of the photon get surface
topography[10]. The basics of laser altimetry is as follows: a laser altimeter emits
laser pulses which are reflected from the first surface the beam interacts with. Part
of this reflected radiation returns to the detector and the laser pulses two-way-travel-
time is recorded. The distance above the ellipsoid can then be calculated considering
the speed of light, the height of the satellite, and atmospheric interference [11]. There
are multiple types of laser altimetry, including airborne and spaceborne. Airborne
laser altimetry includes Airborne Laser Scanning (ALS) and Airborne Laser Profiling
(ALP)[10]. The advantage of airborne scanners is that they are able to collect a
large swatch of densely distributed data with high accuracy[10]. Another example of
an airborne altimeter is the Airborne Topographic Mapper (ATM) from Operation
IceBridge, which will be used for validation data later in the paper. Figure 2 shows
the basics of how a space-borne laser altimeter works and the signals received from
different surfaces, in particular the example shows transmission over canopy[11].
Figure 2: Principles of Laser Altimetry, courtesy of [11].
Different materials reflect light differently at different wavelengths. There
are three elements of reflection that are important to understand from here, shown
in Figure 3: albedo, backscatter, and reflectance. Albedo is defined as the fraction of
sunlight that is reflected back from a surface. The more light that is reflected, the
higher the albedo. Albedo values vary from approximately 0.04 for deep, calm water
to greater than 0.8 for fresh snow or thick clouds [12]. Backscatter is defined as the
energy that is reflected back towards its source by the terrain or particles in the
==PAGE:16==
4
atmosphere [13]. Reflectance is defined as the fraction of total radiant flux incident
upon a surface that is reflected relative to a perfect Lambertian (diffuse) surface. A
Lambertian surface is an ideal surface that reflects light diffusely or isotropically in all
directions. Specular surfaces are the opposite of Lambertian or diffuse surfaces, which
reflect light at the same angle they hit in the surface in one direction. The reflectance
of a surface describes its deviation from a pure Lambertian process. Reflectance
values are determined experimentally and vary with the wavelength, polarization,
and angle of incidence of incoming light. Reflectance of a surface varies with surface
roughness, which is relevant when discussing sea ice and ice ridges which vary in
surface roughness. Surface roughness is defined as the standard deviation of surface
elevation over a given distance. An example of a spectral surface is a calm lake, while
fresh undisturbed snow (low surface roughness) is an almost Lambertian surface [14].
[12]
Figure 3: Reflection processes and elements: reflectance, backscatter, and albedo.[12]
Space-borne laser altimeters are highly susceptible to noise and backscat-
ter from clouds and rough surface topography. Additionally, all lidar signals and
reflectance values in the Near Infra-Red (NIR) are highly sensitive to changes in
surface media, meaning the wavelength chosen for the laser depends on the intended
application of the instrument [14]. The ICESat-2 laser altimeter, ATLAS, operates
at 532nm. At this wavelength, the following reflectance values for elements of the
cryosphere are listed below in Table 1. Additional values, adapted from Tedesco
(2015) are shown in Figure 4.
Applications of laser altimetry are in use everyday - validating research and
models, providing digital maps and Digital Elevation Maps (DEM), and tracking
subtle topographic changes[10].

2.2.1
Orbit Specifications
ICESat-2 flies in a near-circular near-polar orbit with a repeat cycle of 91
days, covering latitudes up to 88◦North and South. The inclination of the orbit
is 92◦. The satellite operates at a nominal altitude of ∼500km at a velocity of
7km/s.[15]
The Reference Ground Track (RGT) is defined as an "imaginary track on
Earth at which a specified unit vector within the observatory is pointed". The RGT
is between the 2nd laser pulse’s left and right tracks, along "Pair Track 2", or the
middle beam pair, which can be seen in Figure 8 [19]. There are in total 1387 RGTs
defined[20].
The spacecraft changes orientation approximately twice a year to maximize
solar illumination of the solar panels.
When the spacecraft is travelling in the
"forward" orientation, it travels along the +x coordinate of the ATLAS instrument
reference frame. When going in a backward orientation, it travels along the -x
coordinate. The orientation impacts the strong and weak beams of the ATLAS
instrument and will be discussed in later sections [19].

2.2.2
ATLAS
The ATLAS instrument is the sole instrument on ICESat-2 and is com-
prised of three main subsystems: the transmitter, the receiver, and the alignment
monitoring and control system. Figure 6 shows an overview of the ATLAS instrument.
Figure 6: ATLAS Instrument Overview [21]
The transmitter subsystem is comprised of two lasers, primary and redundant,
with only one active at a time. The lasers can be seen in Figure 6 on the bottom
right side of the right image. The transmitter outputs a single laser pulse split into
six beams arranged into pairs of a strong and weak beam at 532nm, in the low-pulse
green energy spectrum. ATLAS has a pulse repetition frequency (PRF) of 10kHz,
sending 10000 pulses per second, and emits approximately 20 trillion photons per
pulse. The pulses are narrow, less than 1.5ns . The strong beams contain ˜21%
of the transmitted energy each, while the weak beams have ∼5.4% each. The
combination of strong and weak beams is designed to enhance the radiometric range.
The strong beams are the primary lasers for low-reflectivity targets, such as open
water and oceans. The redundancy of lasers is required to complete the scientific
mission of monitoring the cryosphere in rapidly changing regions with necessary
accuracy and precision, primarily along the edges of Greenland and Antarctica. The
scientific objective to retrieve sea-ice freeboard and calculate sea-ice thickness is a
driving factor for increased vertical precision requirements from the original ICESat
==PAGE:20==
8
data products. The arrangement of the three beam pairs allows for along-track
and across-track direction surface slope measurement in a single-pass, allowing for
determination of height change with two passes. The footprint of the laser on the
ground is ∼17m, spaced ∼0.7m along track, creating a large overlap between shots.
This greatly increasing along-track resolution. The vertical precision of sea surface
height measurements within sea-ice leads is 3 cm[18]. Figure 7 shows what the beam
looks like on the ground, and the three pairs of beams separated approximately
3.3km apart with a footprint of 17m. With the pulse repetition frequency of 10KHz,
the instrument takes a measurement every 70cm. The across-track measurement
distance per pulse is therefore approximately 6.69km, from the left-most beam to
the right-most.
Figure 7: ICESat-2 ATLAS Beam Patterns and Ground Track Distancing [22], [23]
Each pair of beams is assigned a Pair Track, which is a line between the
actual location of the left and right beams which can be seen in Figure 8.
The main element of the receiver subsystem is the telescope, which collects
light and focuses it on the receiver optics. The telescope can be seen in Figure 6 in the
bottom left corner of the left image. Light from each beam is focused onto a fiber optic
cable, passed through a pass band coarse filters to remove background light, then
fiber-coupled to single-photon sensitive photo-cathode array photomultiplier tubes
(PMTs), which convert optical energy into electrical pulses. The overall efficiency of
the ATLAS receiver is ∼6%.
ATLAS uses single-photon sensitive detection to measure time of flight (TOF)
with a precision of 800ps (8 · 10−10s). Transmitted pulses are separated by ∼15 km
in flight. The ATLAS instrument uses photon-counting electronics (PCE) to record
precise timing of photon events. The GPS is coupled with an Ultra Stable Oscillator
(USO), which allows the internal of timing of ATLAS to match to the GPS time.

The accuracy and drift of the USO directly impact height measurements and the
ability to precisely measure photon TOF. The received photons are filtered with
on-board software by comparing photon events to a digital-elevation model (DEM)
to determine the photons that are most likely reflected off the Earth’s surface. The
Range Window is defined as the window of time-tagged photons over Earth’s surface.
To further filter out background noise, a histogram of photon time tags is formed
within the range window to statistically determine photon events most likely coming
from Earth’s surface.
The ICESat-2 has different accuracy’s for different surface types. Laser can
penetrate through open water with minimal loss but has significant errors in snowpack
due to volumetric scattering that can effect the accuracy of height measurements
and impact the calculated ridge sail height[14].

vector.
2.2.3
ATL03 Data Product
The ATLAS instrument provides multiple data products, which can be
visualized in Figure 9. The primary data product used is the ATL03 Global Geolocated

Photon Heights product, provided by the National Snow and Ice Data Center (NSIDC)

2.4.1
Determining Variability in Arctic Sea Ice Pressure Ridge Topogra-
phy With ICESat-2 [1]
This paper derives frequency and height of individual pressure ridges and
maps surface roughness in the winters (primarily April) of 2019, 2020, and 2021.
The authors used the ATLAS instrument on ICESat-2 and the ATL03 and ATL07
data products for comparison. They further present the University of Maryland
- Ridge Detection Algorithm (UMD-RDA) used on ATL03 data to detect ridges.
UMD-RA extracts sea ice surface height and filters the data for creation of a surface
profile. Data is considered background noise if it is far from the photon height (hph)
distribution. Photons that are close to the hph distribution indicate surface return
and are retained, the rest are discarded as background noise through the following:
surface mode (hm) +10m ≥photon height (hph) ≥hm −2m
A second layering of filtering retains only photon within the 15th - 85th percentiles of
hph distribution. Corrected heights relative to the mean sea surface (MSS) are used by
applying atmospheric and tidal corrections and DTU18 MSS model. Individual ridges
are determined by computing the local level ice surface along-track for converting
corrected heights to height anomalies relative to the local level ice surface height,
and using an a optimal cutoff height of H0 = 0.6m above the surface height to define
the minimum ridge height for the anomalies. The algorithm is validated with ATM
lidar collected from under-flights of ICESat-2 in April 2019. The results show that
ATM and ATL07 surface heights are highly correlated, but had large differences
in areas of rough sea ice. It was found that ATL07 underestimates the height of
individual ridge sails, and that UMD-RDA was able to better catch the indiviual sails
and their magnitude. The sails extracted using this algorithm show that sails are
frequently thicker than 3m in older ice but much smaller in younger ice. Additionally,
sea ice is minimally crushed when the pack is compressed, and smaller ridges are
more common across the ice cover than very large pressure ridge complexes. The
paper concludes that while yearly variability of morphology in pressure rides is low,
regional variations are significant and ice deformation is more prevalent in multiyear
ice than in seasonal ice.

paper validates this data with two independent data sets - the Operation IceBridge

3.1
Introduction to Sea Ice
Sea ice begins to form when seawater reaches approximately −1.8oC. As the
temperature decreases, small spicules of ice form called frazil ice - this is the first
stage of sea ice formation[28]. It is important to note that salt does not freeze, so
as salt water begins to freeze the salt is expelled. Ice crystals themselves are pure
freshwater[29]. However, sea ice is a combination of ice crystals and brine pockets.
From this point there are two possible paths that ice formation can take, depending
on how stable the surrounding conditions are. If the ocean is rough, the process
will lead to the formation of pancake ice, but if it is calm it undergoes congelation
growth[29]. From here, cementing and consolidation of ice can form ice sheets and
floes[29]. Rough-bottomed ice originates from the pancake cycle[29]. These two
paths are visualized below in Figure 14. Definitions of the terms used can be found
in Table 2, taken directly from the NSIDC cryosphere glossary definitions. [29]

3.2
Mechanics of Ice Ridges
Ice ridges are defined as pressure ridges which is an elongated accumulation
of broken ice resulting in deformation. [31]. Ridges are formed by a consolidation of
rafted layers or broken pieces of ice flows. Rafting is a deformation process in which
==PAGE:31==
19
one piece of ice overrides another[31]. Figure 17 shows how ice ridges and multi-year
ice is formed. Young ice is formed and then begins rafting, where it elements slide
over each other. As the ice thickens, rafting continues and leads to bending of the ice
and compression ridges. As the ice ridge ages, it begins to degrade and consolidate,
smoothing over time.
Figure 17: Development of sea ice cover. [32].
Figure 18 shows a diagram of a cross-section of a ridge. The main components
of a pressure ridge are the sail, the consolidated layer, and the keel. The sail is
the upper part of the ridge that is measured from the local level ice surface to the
peak and the keel is the lower part, measured from the bottom of the ice surface
down. The keel is much larger than the sail. The consolidated layer is the section in
between that sits at the local level ice.[31] The consolidated region of ice is generally
a layer of rubble that has been bonded by refrozen slush or water to fill in the voids
==PAGE:32==
20
from the ridging process[33]. It indicates the state and strength of the ridge, as
well as its age, and can contain the remains of multiple rafter layers[31]. There are
several types of ice deformation that can be mistaken for pressure ridges, such as
sastrugi or blown snow. Shown in Figure 18 is a sastrugi, which are wind-driven snow
shapes, although they are typically lower than ridge sails[33]. However, depending
on the threshold or cutoff value for ridge height, these features may be included from
detecting deformations and obstacles on the sea surface. Therefore, it is important
to consider the height of not only ice ridges but also other ice and snow features.

4.2
A Variation on Previous Studies: UMD-RDA
4.2.1
Surface Finding Method and Variations
The next steps in processing the data parallel the work done in the University
of Maryland Ridge Detection Algorithm (UMD-RDA) from Duncan & Farrell, 2022
[1]. For comparison purposes the work in Duncan & Farrell (2022) is split into two
==PAGE:43==
31
main sections: surface finding and ridge detection. The steps used in the UMD-RDA
are listed below. Data is only processed where ATL07 data is available, eliminating
cloud contaminated shots. There are multiple elements of the UMD-RDA that have
the possibility to be further optimized or varied slightly to increase accuracy and
resolution. These elements will be investigated below.
1. Construct photon height (hph) distribution from 5-shot aggregates of photons.
2. Determine the mode of the 5-shot aggregate with 0.5m bin width. If the data
is bimodal, take the lower mode.
3. Retain photons with the range (hm+10m)≥hph ≥(hm-2m) of the 5-shot
aggregate.
4. Retain photons within the 15th-85th percentile of the hph distribution of the
5-shot aggregate.
5. Define sea ice surface height (h) as the 99th percentile of the remaining hph
distribution, per shot.
6. Remove MSS from photon height and apply tidal and atmospheric corrections
to get corrected heights, hc, per shot.
7. Construct 25km along-track segments to determine local surface levels. σh is
the standard deviation of each segment and is the surface roughness. HL is the
mode of the segment and is defined as the local level ice surface. In case of
bimodality, take the highest mode.
8. Remove HL from hc to obtain height anomalies (ha) which is the height of the
sea ice above the local surface level.
From this processing, the end result is the corrected sea ice surface height, hc, which
is defined as the retrieval arising from the air/snow interface - the first surface
encountered by the laser pulse. [1]
§1

The first step in the UMD-
RDA is to create a 5-shot aggregate of photon heights. This was investigated to
determine if a different number of shots created a better height profile. Figure 28
shows the output of the UMD-RDA if all other elements are held constant and only
the shot aggregate is varied. For now, only the red line (hP99) should be considered,
the other profile will be discussed later (see paragraph §4). Figure 28 shows the result

The result of this study is that the number of shots in the aggregate has

The next step in the filtering

this study the possibility of keep the mode of the remaining data was also analyzed

percentile to calculate h was used. This is because when the mode was used there

interacts with. Additionally, it has no smoothing effect as it does not consider the

Determining Local Level Ice Height
The final step in the surface finding
process in Duncan & Farrell (2022) is to find the local surface height (HL) and
subtract it from the correction photon heights (hc) to calculate the prominence of
the ice above the local surface level, here called height anomalies (ha). In Duncan
& Farrell (2022) this is accomplished by assigning the mode of a 25km along-track
segment of photons as HL. However, ice is incredibly variable and conditions can
change quickly. In Johnson et. al (2022), 25km is classified as a coarse resolution
when calculating sea ice roughness[40].
Figure 33 shows resolutions of 10km and 25km compared, using the mode as
the method to determine local surface level. The height anomalies (ha) are provided
in blue with the local surface (HL) in black. It is important to point out that the
height anomalies have already been corrected for the local surface level. The red line
shows the surface roughness. The surface roughness is highly susceptible to outliers
in this method. As seen in the pop-out in Figure 33, using this method there are
small segments of ha that are abruptly removed from the surrounding area due to a
change in the local surface height. This could produce issues during ridge detection if
there is a ridge on the edge of these sections. For this reason, the method of finding
the local surface level is changed.
Figure 34 shows the effect of varying both the resolution and the method
used to find HL. In this plot, the mode, median, and mean are all calculated for
both 10km and 25km resolutions. The median method provides the smoothest local
surface level with the lowest standard deviation over the whole track, reducing the
impact of abrupt surface level variations on ridge-finding capabilities. The purpose of
ridge-detecting algorithms is to identify ridges which could otherwise be categorized
as outliers. The smaller the variability of local surface level, the more likely ridges
will be properly identified. In situations with many outliers and skewed data (which
can be seen in Figure 30), the best method to determine the central tendency of data

4.3
DTU-Aalto Ridge Detection Algorithm
Following the study of the UMD-RDA, the DTU-Aalto Ridge Detection
Algorithm (DA-RDA) was created, which is a variation on the UMD-RDA. The
steps are listed below and explained in detail. There are two main portions. The
first is surface finding, which filters the photon heights from ICESat-2 and creates a
profile at a per-shot basis. This retains the original resolution of the data, at 0.7m[1].
The second is ridge detection, which identifies ridges and calculates various statistics.

Calculating Mode and Determining Bimodality
Relevant Function: ManualMode.m - subsection A.7
While seemingly straight-forward, further investigation revealed the MATLAB handles
the mode of data is not ideal for this application. Figure 37 show data from a 7m
segment of RGT371 from 22 April 2019 and a histogram of the data. Five different
ways to calculate the mode were investigated.
• MATLAB’s built in mode function
• A manual mode calculation which involves calculating the highest bin of the
histogram and matching it with a list of possible modes
• Calculating a manual probability density function (PDF) and calculating the
mode as the value with the highest probability
• Using MATLAB’s fitdist function to automatically determine the normal
distribution, calculating the mode as the value with the highest probability
• Using MATLAB’s fitdist function to automatically determine the kernel distri-
bution, calculating the mode as the value with the highest probability
By visual inspection of Figure 37 the mode is between 0.3 and 0.4m. The
closest values to the real mode are from the manual mode calculation and the kernel
fit. Multiple windows were analyzed in the same manner to determine the best fit
mode value. The manual mode was determined to be the best option to calculate the

mode of photon elevations moving forward. Additional plots showing mode analysis
can be seen in Appendix B.

There are several methods to determine if a distribution is bimodal. For this
paper, the data is sorted into bins of width 0.1m, as previously mentioned. The
mode is taken from the highest bin. However, the data is determined to be bimodal

§2
Part 2: Tidal Corrections
7. Apply tidal corrections (geoid_free2mean and tide_earth_free2mean) to get
corrected heights, hc (per shot). [24]
One factor that needs to be considered when comparing different types of
data is the tidal system it’s provided in. A tidal system refers to how the permanent
tide is applied to the geoid model of Earth, shown in Figure 13 [24]. The permanent
tide is a non-periodic, time-averaged deformation caused by the solar and lunar
tidal attraction that deform the shape of the geoid the Earth’s crust[24]. There
are two tidal systems that will be considered here: mean tide and tide free. A
mean tide system provides data that could be measured in the real world where
the deformation caused by permanent tide is present[24]. For example, measuring
instantaneous sea level relative to the geoid implicitly includes deformation caused by
permanent tides[24]. The tide free system is a system where the effect of permanent
tide deformation is eliminated from the shape of the Earth, essentially using the
geoid as a reference[24]. An exaggerated model comparing the two tide systems can
be seen in Figure 39.
==PAGE:54==

The tidal system is relevant because MSS data is provided in a mean tide

Ridge Detection
Relevant Function: RidgeDetection.m - subsection A.9
The second section of the DA-RDA is ridge detection, which will identify
ridges meeting a certain set of criteria and determine sail width and height, frequency
of ridges, and ridge spacing. Additionally, the sea ice region where each data point
and ridge peak resides will be identified and saved. The main steps are listed below.
Figure 40 shows a flowchart of how the code accomplishes steps 1-3. The Rayleigh
Criterion is explained in further detail below.
==PAGE:56==
44
1. Set the optimal cutoff height for ridge detection (H0) to 0.6m.
2. Apply the Rayleigh Criterion to identify ridges.
3. Calculate ridge sail height (Hs), ridge width (WR), ridge spacing, (DR), and
ridge frequency (fR).
Figure 40: Algorithm visualization: DA-RDA Ridge Detection.
Ridge Identification and Rayleigh Criterion There are several steps
involved in ridge identification. First is peak identification by finding a local maxima
(sail) and then trough identification of local minima (troughs). In order to classify
as a potential ridge sail the peak must be greater than or equal to a threshold height
(hthreshold), which is a given height above the local surface level (HL). This number is
typically defined as between 0.4-0.6m [42], [1]. Here the value 0.6m was chosen. Next,
the Rayleigh Criterion is applied. The Rayleigh Criterion for ridge identification
determines which of these peaks are identified as pressure ridges relative to the
surrounding troughs. To be classified as a ridge sail, a peak must satisfy the Rayleigh
Criterion, which states that the trough on either side of the peak must be less than or
equal to half the peak height. This can be visualized in Equation 10. Where haT,high
is the height anomaly of the upper trough, haP is the height anomaly of the peak
(which will later be classified as a sail if it meets the Rayleigh Criterion), and haT,low
is the height anomaly of the lower trough. A reminder that the height anomaly, ha,
is the height of the ice above the local surface level.
2 · haT,high ≤haP & 2 · haT,low ≤haP
(10)
For each ridge this criteria is evaluated. If the peak passes the criteria then
it is classified as a ridge with a single sail. If the peak fails then the upper or lower
==PAGE:57==
45
trough moves to the right or left, respectively, and the criteria is tested again until a
trough with a height low enough relative to the peak is found on both sides. This
is then classified as a ridge with multiple sails. For a ridge with multiple sails, the
highest peak is used as the sail height. An example of the application of the Rayleigh
Criterion can be seen in Figure 42, showing possible scenarios when trying to classify
the ridge around Peak 1:
• Sub-figure (a) shows an ideal scenario, where Peak 1 is immediately classifiable
as a ridge with one sail. The peak and the immediate surrounding troughs fit
the criterion.
• Sub-figure (b) shows a scenario where the trough to the left does not meet
the criterion and must be moved negatively in the along-track distance until a
trough low enough is identified.
• Sub-figure (c) shows a scenario where the trough to the right does not meet
the criterion and must be moved positively in the along-track distance until a
trough low enough is identified.
• Sub-figure (d) shows a scenario where the troughs on either sides of the peak
are not low enough and the search for a trough low enough must be spread
outwards from the peak.
Figure 41 shows the results this process, with ridges identified with multiple
or single sails marked separately, before they are combined for the final output of
the function.

objective of OIB was to bridge the gap between the end of ICESat in 2010 and

a satellite to provide validation data[45]. The mission operated out of multiple
aircraft but was primarily based on a Lockheed P-3B Orion and Douglas DC-8.
OIB has multiple instruments that measure snow depth, ice elevation and thickness,
surface temperature, bed topography and other characteristics of sea ice, ice sheets
and glaciers[45]. The primary instrument that will be used for data validation is
the Airborne Topographic Mapper (ATM), which is an airborne laser altimeter,
previously mentioned in subsection 2.1[46]. The altimeter operates at 532nm, the
same at ICESat-2[47]. The ATM has a horiztonal resolution of 0.2m at an elevation
of 800m [47]. Figure 44 is a picture of the ATM from 2009.
The data used is from the dataset IceBridge ATM L1B Elevation and Return
Strength, which is a Level 1B (L1B) Standard Data Product (SDP)[48]. L1B data are
reconstructed, unprocessed instrument data at full resolution that has been processed

where the ICESat-2 data is also retrieved from, in a HDF5 file format. The data

Discussion
6.1
Results from Ridge Analysis
Table 5 shows the results from using the ridge algorithm over multiple RGTs
between 2019 and 2024. The area with the most frequency ridges was along RGT163,
which went through the Central Arctic, Beaufort Sea, and Barents Sea (see Figure 58
for the location). This aligns with current data from Sudom & Timco, et. al. (2011),
where the most and largest ice ridges are found. The largest ridges were found along
RGT371, which covered the majority of the central Arctic.
However, it is important to take into consideration that not all ridges have
been properly identified, as shown in Figure 60. The ridge detection algorithm as it
currently stands does not output a matrix with all ridges. The characteristics it is
able to identify appear to be accurate, when comparing the output of the algorithm
with the plots of the sea ice surface. Therefore, the frequency of ridges in Table 5
and the number of ridges found in the output files can unfortunately not be trusted
at this point in time. The best guess is there is a small error in the code to assign
indices of ridges that is removing ridges. The identification element of the algorithm
appears to be working accurately, but not the output. Forward work will include
fixing this error in order to calculate more accurate ridge statistics.
When comparing the output of the surface elevation finding portion of the
algorithm with the validation data from OIB, there is a high degree of correlation,
with a correlation coefficient, r, between 0.78 and 0.92. The higher the peak, the
worse the correlation. This is to be expected. The filtering done by the DA-RDA
has taken as many precautions as possible to retain the highest portion of the sea
ice surface, but unfortunately ridge data is easy to classify as an outlier, because
technically is when compared to the local surface level. Ridge sails may be off by as
much as a factor of 2, according to the OIB data. This is clearly a systemic error.
The same result was also found in Duncan & Farrell (2022), so it is to be expected
on variations of the algorithm[1].
Because the sail height is consistently lower than the OIB data, the sail height
can still be used with that consideration in mind. Ridge width data is still accurate
as well. The primary statistic impacted is the frequency of ridging. Further analysis
will need to be done to determine if the number of sails identified is impacted.

6.5
Limitations
While the algorithm appeared successful, the output was only moderately so.
There are limitation to this work, both inherent or systemic, random, and human
error. The primary human error is a bug in the code for the outputs of the ridge
detection. The primary systemic error is the difference in height between the OIB
and processed ICESat-2 data. There are multiple points of random error, which are
most likely caused by atmospheric interference. The DA-RDA does not filter for
clouds, but this should be included. Therefore, there are currently limitations for
cloud covered areas.
One of the inherent difficulties in satellite altimetry of ice ridges is that the
ideal situation is the satellite travels perpendicular to the ice ridge. However, this
is frequently not the case, the satellite may travel along an ice ridge for kilometers
before moving off of it. Because of this, ridge algorithms may return with a ridge
width of several hundred kilometers. In this case, the local surface level is also effect,
so it is unlikely as there will be peaks and troughs relative to the local level. But there
is still the possibility. This limitation is inherent to the nature of measurements, but
future work might include evaluating optical images to determine ridge orientation.
6.6
Forward Work
6.6.1
Alterations to the Algorithm
One element that should be added to the code is to remove clouds and other
atmospheric influences. In Duncan & Farrell (2022), they only use ATL03 data that
has corresponding ATL07 data, which filters for clouds. However, the ATL07 data
product has a much coarser resolution than ATL03, so another method should be
==PAGE:78==
66
used. There is the possibility of removing high values without data surrounding
them, but the goal is to make the algorithm as flexible as possible so this will be a
challenge while simultaneously ensuring that no ridge data is remove.
One desire for forward work is to translate this code into Python so it is
more readily available outside of an academic setting. The possibility of turning
the algorithm and GUI into an application is desirable as well, but would require
significantly more coding experience.
The primary forward work is, of course, to fix the current error in the code
for the output of the ridge matrix. The error is most likely in how the ridge sails are
grouped following the application of the Rayleigh Criterion, but unfortunately due
to time constraints the exact error was not found.
6.6.2
Desired Further Analysis
For future work with the ridge detection algorithm, it would be ideal to work
with larger datasets to compare more areas over the same time period. Additionally,
to work with the same RGTs over the course of multiple years. This will take
several hundred hours of processing time for all this data, but would provide valuable
information to ridging, and more widespread datasets.
One ICEsat-2 HDF5 file, for RGT813, had no surface types identified. A
possible point of inquiry would be to determine why and how many datasets are
impact by the same issue.
Due to the large datasets and ridge detection algorithm, which only has one
true criterion, the Rayleigh Criterion, this work would be an excellent application
of machine learning tools. Becuase there is a large variation in data, it is difficult
to code the algorithm and filtering to not remove ridges, as the are frequently seen
as outliers. With machine learning, ridge detection may become more accurate and
faster.
Ideally, optical OIB images would be included and geolocated to validate
the OIB and ICESat-2 data. Geolocated optimal images are readily available from
EarthData for OIB.
Additional analysis would ideally include multiple beams from the 6-beams
ICESat-2 has, as currently only the strong central beam that most closely follows
the RGT is used. If more beams are used, it would provide a larger swath of data
and better resolve ridge characteristics.

Conclusion
The purpose of this research was to develop an algorithm to detect sea ice
ridges from ICESat-2 photon elevation data using principles of satellite altimetry
and knowledge of ridge mechanics. ICESat-2 is a novel instrument that provides the
highest resolution altimetry data of the Arctic using the ATLAS instrument, which
is able to transmit beams of lasers and detect single photons at their return.
Sea ice is an important part of our world and sea ice ridges, while they
comprise a large volume and area of sea ice, are not widely studied or understood.
This research set out several objectives and goals, all of which were met to varying
degrees of success.
1. To determine sea ice profile from satellite altimetry data.
2. To create an algorithm to detect sea ice ridges from satellite altimetry data.
3. To validate the algorithm using airborne altimetry data.
4. To develop a graphical user interface (GUI) to improve the accessibility of this
analysis.
5. To compare ridge heights and locations with temporal and geographic variations.
The ridge detection algorithm closely follows the method of the UMD-RDA, however
in this thesis multiple parameters were varied to optimize the output of the algorithm.
Multiple studies were conducted on the variations to confirm the highest resolution
and quality of data was retained.
Figure 65: Operation IceBridge L1B Optical Image from 19 April 2019 showing ice ridges.
==PAGE:80==
68
The ridge detection algorithm developed here, DA-RDA, is able to calculate
local surface level and create a sea ice surface profile, maintaining the resolution of
the original ICESat-2 data, 0.7m along-track. With this algorithm and ICESat-2
data, high resolution sea ice profiles are more accessible. The GUI created requires
minimal user input and is able to automate the sea ice surface retrieval process from
photon elevations. The surface profiles were confirmed with validation data taken
from the airborne altimetry mission, Operation IceBridge, with a high degree of
correlation. The algorithm is able to at least partially detect ridges, but omits some
of them from the output. Ridge detection was successful along smaller tracks of
ICESat-2 data and analyses of ridges for multiple tracks spanning 2019-2024 were
completed.
Several analyses were conducted on the temporal and geographic variations
of sea ice ridges across the Arctic basin, resulting in confirmation and increased data
surrounding the higher and wider ridges in the Beaufort Sea.
The development of this algorithm and the analyses of multiple datasets will
help further the understanding of a large portion of our sea ice cover in the Arctic.
By making datasets more accessible, more people will have a greater understanding
of the mechanics of our world and our climate. With the continuation of rapidly
disappearing sea ice, understanding the formations of the Arctic becomes even more
vital.
