Abstract 
γδ T cells along with natural killer T (NKT) cells and mucosal-associated invariant T (MAIT) cells 
compose the unconvenƟonal T cell group. Although they occupy only a small fracƟon of blood 
circulaƟon, they are abundant in Ɵssues. In contrast with their αβ counterparts, γδ T cells tread 
between both innate and adapƟve immune systems, and their recogniƟon capabiliƟes are not 
limited to pepƟde anƟgens presented through major histocompaƟbility complex (MHC) 
molecules. A more interesƟng subset for this study is the blood circulaƟng Vγ9Vδ2 T cells which 
have the ability to sense phosphoanƟgens through butyrophylin interacƟons. These 
phosphoanƟgens are present during infecƟons and in the tumor microenvironment (TME).  
Immune response can be so severe that it could potenƟally cause damage to the body if leŌ 
unchecked. For this reason, the immune system employs immune checkpoint receptors (ICRs) 
which are responsible for immune regulaƟon and act as breaks to the immune response. Most 
known ICRs are cytotoxic T lymphocyte anƟgen 4 (CTLA-4) and programmed cell death 1 (PD-1). 
Another lesser-known family of ICRs are the killer immunoglobulin-like receptors (KIRs) which are 
mostly expressed by NK cells but can be found in some T cell subtypes, like γδ T cells. Most ICRs 
can outcompete cosƟmulatory molecules required for immune cell acƟvaƟon and/or carry 
intracellular inhibitory moƟfs like immunoreceptor tyrosine-based inhibitory moƟf (ITIM) which 
send inhibitory signals to immune cells prevenƟng acƟvaƟon. Another acƟon of CTLA-4 is the 
ability to rip oﬀ cosƟmulatory CD80/86 from APCs through CTLA-4 mediated transendocytosis. 
In cancer, ICRs mechanisms can be exploited by tumor cells to evade cytotoxic acƟvity of T 
lymphocytes. Immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) therapy aims to prevent interacƟons between 
ICRs and their ligands with the use of anƟbodies that bind directly to the ICR or its ligand 
prevenƟng their inhibitory acƟon and rescuing T cell acƟvity. ICB therapy is mostly focused on 
convenƟonal T cell subsets, however in some cases tumor cells can mutate and reduce the 
expression of MHC class I molecules, making anƟgen recogniƟon harder for convenƟonal T cell 
that relies on this interacƟon to act. On the other hand, γδ T cells are not restricted to MHC 
presentaƟon to idenƟfy malignant cells thus making them ideal for MHC deﬁcient cancers. 
In this study we aim to explore the mechanisms behind CTLA-4 acƟon in Vδ1 and Vδ2 T cells, 
using isolated peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) from healthy donors. Here, we 
idenƟfy that CTLA-4 and KIR expression can be induced in Vδ1 and Vδ2 T cells though cytokine 
and TCR sƟmulaƟon. AddiƟonally, we showed that CTLA-4 inhibiƟon by therapeuƟc anƟ-CTLA-4 
anƟbodies did not aﬀect proliferaƟon or cytokine expression of key cytokines in Vδ1 and Vδ2 T 
cells. Finaly, we worked on a model to evaluate potenƟal CTLA-4 mediated transendocytosis of 
CD80/86 molecules by Vδ1 and Vδ2 T cells.

Introduction 
Immune system 
Immunity is divided into innate and adapƟve, and it is tasked with defending the body against 
harmful pathogens or cancer. The vanguard of immunity is the innate immune system, acƟng 
swiŌly within a maƩer of hours and having a broad range of targets. The innate immune system 
is not comprised only by cellular components, it includes mulƟple external barriers like the skin 
and the gut. Moreover, there are humoral agents like the compliment system, which is a set of 
serum proteins that aƩack pathogens and increase inﬂammatory responses (Riera Romo, Pérez-
Marơnez, & CasƟllo Ferrer, 2016). The other part of the innate immune system is its cells. Innate 
immune cells can be split into two categories: myeloid and lymphoid.  
Myeloid cells include monocytes, neutrophils, macrophages, dendriƟc cells (DCs), mast cells, 
eosinophils, and basophils. These cells are equipped with paƩern recogniƟon receptors (PRRs), 
which recognize a broad range of molecules that are found on the surface of pathogenic 
organisms or are products of apoptoƟc and damaged cells. These molecules are called pathogen-
associated molecular paƩerns (PAMPs) and damage-associated molecular paƩerns (DAMPs) 
respecƟvely (Li & Wu, 2021). One of the main acƟons of the innate cells is to produce cytokines 
and chemokines that will recruit other immune cells to the site of inﬂammaƟon and infecƟon. 
Another important acƟvity is the direct aƩack of the pathogens. For example, some innate cells 
like macrophages can phagocytose, while granulocytes like neutrophils can produce molecules 
that are toxic for pathogens (Marshall, Warrington, Watson, & Kim, 2018).  
Innate lymphoid cells (ILCs) are separated into 4 major groups. Cytotoxic natural killer (NK) cells 
which can produce perforin and granzyme to kill infected or cancer cells and are producers of 
interferon-γ ( IFN-γ) and tumor necroƟc factor-α (TNF-α) (Spits & Di Santo, 2011). The other 3 
groups are non-cytotoxic and are called group 1 ILCs (ILC1s), group 2 ILCs (ILC2s) and group 3 ILCs 
(ILC3s) each group capable of producing diﬀerent cytokines, similar to CD4+ T cells of the adapƟve 
immune system which will be explained in a later part (Table 1) (ArƟs & Spits, 2015).

The adapƟve immune system on the other hand, provides a slower but highly eﬀecƟve and 
speciﬁc defense. The cells that comprise the adapƟve system are T cells and B cells, and in contrast 
to the innate cells they can recognize highly speciﬁc anƟgens through T cell receptor (TCR) and B 
cell receptor (BCR) (Marshall et al., 2018). TCRs are able to recognize fragments of anƟgens 
(pepƟdes) while BCRs recognize epitopes on anƟgens (Marshall et al., 2018). TCRs are comprised 
of a combinaƟon of two TCR chains and CD3 molecules. There are 4 potenƟal TCR chain families 
α-chains, β-chains, γ-chains and δ-chains (TCRα, TCRβ, TCRγ, and TCRδ). These chains can be 
paired as TCRα/TCRβ in αβ T cells or TCRγ/TCRδ in γδ T cells (Shah, Al-Haidari, Sun, & Kazi, 2021).  
Before the adapƟve immune system acts, it needs to be primed. An important funcƟon that some 
innate cells possess is that they can present anƟgens to acƟvate the adapƟve system. 
Macrophages and DCs are considered to be the classic innate professional anƟgen presenƟng 
cells (APCs), while B cells can also act as professional APCs (Kambayashi & Laufer, 2014). When a 
professional APC encounters PAMPs or DAMPs with its PRRs, it will act by internalizing the 
pathogen or the damaged cell through phagocytosis, pinocytosis or clathrin-mediated 
endocytosis. AŌerwards, the APC will present its target anƟgens through major histocompaƟbility 
complex (MHC) class II molecules in its surface (Gaudino & Kumar, 2019). AlternaƟvely, an 
infected cell will present anƟgens through MHC class I. MHC class I is expressed in all nucleated 
cells and presents endogenous pepƟdes to cytotoxic CD8+ T cells. With the excepƟon of DCs which 
are able to present both exogenous and endogenous pepƟdes through MHC class I to CD8 T cells, 
through a mechanism called cross-presentaƟon (Ackerman & Cresswell, 2004; Gaudino & Kumar, 
2019). MHC class II, however, is unique to professional APCs and presents exogenous pepƟdes to 
CD4 T cells (Gaudino & Kumar, 2019). 
ConvenƟonal and unconvenƟonal T cells 
T cells can be categorized as convenƟonal or unconvenƟonal. ConvenƟonal cells include CD4 and 
CD8 αβ T cells, while NK T cells, mucosal-associated invariant T (MAIT) cells and γδ T cells are 
categorized as unconvenƟonal T lymphocytes. UnconvenƟonal cells compose approximately 10% 
of T lymphocytes in the blood, but are abundant in Ɵssues like the liver and gut (Pellicci, Koay, & 
Berzins, 2020). ConvenƟonal T cells have highly variable TCRs that recognize pepƟde loaded MHC 
molecules. On the other hand, unconvenƟonal T cells are able to recognize anƟgens without the 
help of MHC molecules and their recogniƟon capacity expands to non-pepƟde targets (Godfrey, 
Uldrich, Mccluskey, Rossjohn, & Moody, 2015).  
ConvenƟonal T cells 
CD4 T cells have mulƟple roles during the immune response. Through the producƟon of cytokines, 
they act as helper cells (Th) to supplement the acƟvity of both innate and B cells, while they also 
suppress and regulate unwanted immune responses as regulatory T cells (Tregs). The diﬀerent 
phenotype of Th cells is guided by the cytokines they receive during their acƟvaƟon (Table 2) 
(Geginat et al., 2014; Sun, Su, Jiao, Wang, & Zhang, 2023).

Development 
γδ T cells comprise approximately 1-6% of CD3 lymphocytes in the adult human blood and can be 
found in Ɵssues in high concentraƟon (Papadopoulou, Sanchez Sanchez, & Vermijlen, 2020).  For 
the development of T cells, the journey starts as thymic seeding progenitors (TSPs), which migrate 
to the thymus from the bone marrow and become early T cell precursors (ETPs). During their early 
development in the thymic cortex thymocytes are idenƟﬁed as double negaƟve (CD4 and CD8 , 
DN) and are separated into four main stages DN1, DN2, DN3 and DN4 (Sun et al., 2023). The ﬁrst 
signal in the development process  derives from Notch receptors, which drive ETPs to upregulate 
T cell factor 1 (TCF-1), a transcripƟon factor essenƟal factors for the survival and of ETPs (Sun et 
al., 2023). In turn, TCF-1 drives expression of the transcripƟonal factors GATA-3 and Bcl11b, of 
which GATA-3 restricts diﬀerenƟaƟon into the B and myeloid cell lineage, while Bcl11b blocks the 
path to NK cell diﬀerenƟaƟon and further promotes development to mature T cells (Garcia-Perez 
et al., 2020).

strength model, which suggests that T cells that receive a strong TCR signal are skewed to γδ T 
cells expressing γδTCR. On the other hand, when the signal is weak they are shiŌed more to αβ T 
cells expressing surface pre-TCR (Ciofani & Zúñiga-Pﬂücker, 2010; Hayes, Li, & Love, 2005; Parker 
& Ciofani, 2020).

γδ T cells in cancer  
Mouse models show that γδ T cells have both pro- and anƟ- tumor acƟvity. They can directly kill 
cancer cells through the producƟon of perforins and granzymes or through tumor necrosis factor-
related apoptosis-inducing ligand (TRAIL) and FAS ligand (FASL) (Silva-Santos et al., 2019). 
Indirectly, γδ T cells also suppress tumor growth through producƟon of cytokines like IFN-γ or 
TNF-α and acƟvate and recruit other cytotoxic cells like CD8 T and NK cells (Silva-Santos et al.,

producer of IL-17 and has been suggested to have pro-tumor funcƟon (Silva-Santos et al., 2019).   
In humans, the anƟ-tumor acƟvity of γδ T cells is similar to mice (Mensurado, Blanco-Domínguez, 
& Silva-Santos, 2023), although, their pro-tumor role remains elusive due to the rarity of γδT17 
cells in healthy human circulaƟon and Ɵssues. However, IL-17 producing γδ T cells can be found 
during infecƟons and cancer in humans (Agerholm & Bekiaris, 2021; Mensurado et al., 2023).

Immunotherapy 
Cancer treatment can be summarized in four main categories: surgery, chemotherapy, 
radiotherapy and immunotherapy. Although, the ﬁrst three can be very eﬀecƟve, they come with 
a great toll in the paƟent’s body and quality of life due to the high collateral damage they cause 
(García-Fernández, Saz, Fornaguera, & Borrós, 2021). New hope arises with advances in 
immunotherapy treatment. Immunotherapy compels the body’s own immune system to combat 
cancer growth instead of relying on pharmacological agents to eliminate the malignant cells 
(American Cancer Society, 2019).  
There are mulƟple types of immunotherapy, including immune checkpoint blockade (ICB), 
cytokines that promote the acƟvity of lymphocytes, and adopƟve cell therapy (ACT)  (Riley, June, 
Langer, & Mitchell, 2019). Early immunotherapies focused on lymphocyte-promoƟng cytokines 
such as interferons and interleukins which promote the immune eﬀector cell’s anƟ-tumor abiliƟes 
(S. Lee & Margolin, 2011). IFN-α and IL-2 were among the ﬁrst approved immunotherapies against 
hairy cell leukemia and metastaƟc renal cancer respecƟvely (Bezwoda, Hesdorﬀer, Dansey, & 
Lewis, 1986; Rosenberg, 2014). AdopƟve cell therapy is a highly personalized therapy that uƟlizes 
the paƟent’s own cells, which are collected, expanded and modiﬁed ex vivo and then re-
introduced to the paƟent (Rosenberg & ResƟfo, 2015). The usual candidate cell for ACT are 
convenƟonal T cells, which can be modiﬁed into chimeric anƟgen receptor (CAR) T cells, however, 
γδ T cells have also been speculated as a candidate for potenƟal treatments. PotenƟal therapies 
using γδ T cells are: expansion of γδ T cell subsets, CAR-transduced γδ T cells and γδTCR-
engineered T cells which are currently in clinical tesƟng (Mensurado et al., 2023). For this study 
we will focus on immune checkpoint receptors and ICB therapy.  
Immune checkpoint receptors and ICB therapy 
The immune system is a powerful weapon against diseases and tumors, nevertheless if leŌ 
unchecked it can cause unwanted collateral damage to the body. Immune checkpoint receptors 
(ICRs) are an important tool for the immune system to regulate itself. Most known and well-
studied  ICRs are cytotoxic T lymphocyte anƟgen 4 (CTLA-4) and programmed cell death 1 (PD-1) 
among others (Table 3) (Waldman, Fritz, & Lenardo, 2020). ICRs regulate the acƟon of immune 
cells through 3 mechanisms. First, ICRs can outcompete ligands of cosƟmulatory molecules, thus 
depriving the cell of acƟvaƟng signals (Odorizzi & Wherry, 2012). Second, ICRs in their majority 
carry intracellular inhibitory moƟfs like immunoreceptor tyrosine-based inhibitory moƟf (ITIM) 
and immunoreceptor tyrosine-based switch moƟf (ITSM). When the inhibitory ICR engages with 
its ligand these moƟfs send inhibitory signals to the cell that restrict its acƟvaƟon (Odorizzi & 
Wherry, 2012).  Lastly, inhibitory ICRs can promote the expression of genes that inhibit the 
funcƟon or proliferaƟon of the cell (Odorizzi & Wherry, 2012). In this study the focus will be on 
CTLA-4 and killer cell immunoglobulin-like receptors (KIR), although PD-1 will be discussed due 
to its clinical importance.

CTLA-4 
As menƟoned above, T cells require three disƟnct signals to become acƟvated, CTLA-4 interferes 
with the second signal of acƟvaƟon, the cosƟmulatory signal. CTLA-4 also called CD154 has a 
much higher aﬃnity towards CD80/86 than CD28, outcompeƟng therefore CD28 binding with 
CD80/86, thus prevenƟng cosƟmulatory signals and subsequently T cell acƟvaƟon and 
proliferaƟon (Buchbinder & Desai, 2016). During CTLA-4 – CD80/86 engagement  the intracellular 
moƟf of CTLA-4 YVKM becomes phosphorylated and recruits src homology-2 protein tyrosine 
phosphatase (SHP2) molecules, which inhibit the acƟvaƟon signal from the TCR (Figure 3) (He & 
Xu, 2020; Hossen et al., 2023).  
A characterisƟc of CTLA-4 is that it is constantly cycling between the cytoplasm and cell 
membrane. As such, upon high aﬃnity interacƟons with CD80 or CD86, CTLA-4 can physically “rip 
them oﬀ” from the APC on its way back to the cytoplasm, through a processes called 
transendocytosis (Qureshi et al., 2011). Treg cells that express high levels of CTLA-4 may be 
reducing the overall acƟvaƟon properƟes of the APC and thus suppress immune responses by 
depleƟng CD80/86 molecules (Buchbinder & Desai, 2016; Qureshi et al., 2011). Further study into 
the transendocytosis mechanism suggested that there is a diﬀerent mechanism behind CD80 or 
CD86 transendocytosis. Hence, aŌer entering the cell, CTLA-4/CD80 is transferred to endosomes 
or lysosomes where both CD80 and CTLA-4 are degraded. On the other hand, CTLA-4/CD86 is 
able to separate before degradaƟon and CTLA-4 can cycle back to the cell surface (Kennedy et al., 
2022). 
Due to its high regulatory acƟon CTLA-4 possesses an important target for ICB treatment. Already 
there are approved therapeuƟc CTLA-4 inhibitors like Ipilimumab (commercial name Yervoy) an 
approved melanoma therapy, which is a human IgG1 monoclonal anƟbody (mAb) that can target 
and bind to CTLA-4, thus blocking its regulatory acƟon during T cell acƟvaƟon (Figure 4) and 
increasing the immune system response (Shiravand et al., 2022).  
In this study we aim to further explore the transendocytosis mechanism in γδ T cells, since CTLA-
4 expression in γδ T cells can be upregulated by phosphoanƟgens or TCR sƟmulaƟon (Tardos et 
al., 2025). γδ T cells could have further potenƟal and provide insight into CTLA-4 mediated 
transendocytosis.

Another important checkpoint receptor is PD-1 also known as CD279, which is expressed by T

with PD-L1/2 then its intracellular moƟf (in this case ITIM) becomes phosphorylated. This aƩracts

Under normal physiological condiƟons, PD-L1 is expressed in a wide range of cells including 
hematopoieƟc and non-hematopoieƟc. On the other hand, PD-L2 expression is more restricted

interacƟon is responsible for preserving self-tolerance and prevenƟng autoimmunity (Sharpe & 
Pauken, 2018). In many types of cancer, however, PD-L1 and PD-L2 expression is increased in the 
TME and it has been linked with poor disease prognosis (Bagchi, Yuan, & Engleman, 2021). In this 
case, the PD-1 – PD-L1/2 interacƟon leads to inhibiƟon of CTL-mediated cytotoxicity, reducing 
thus the potency of the immune system to combat cancer (Sharpe & Pauken, 2018).

γδ T cells in immune checkpoint blockade therapy 
As menƟoned above, ICB therapy uses anƟbodies that block the eﬀects of ICRs thus increasing 
the immune response against cancer. Although ICB therapies are a very prominent treatments 
against cancer they fail to produce a lasƟng result in many paƟents and some types of cancer 
(Bagchi et al., 2021). The reason why these therapies underperform remains poorly understood 
but it is probably aƩributed to resistance, either primary or acquired. Primary resistance means 
that there is no response to ICB from the start of therapy, while acquired resistance refers to cases 
that despite a good iniƟal response fail to sustain it (Bagchi et al., 2021). The mechanism behind 
the resistance is also an elusive subject, with one of the plausible reasons being the lack of tumor 
anƟgens that can be recognized by CD8 CTLs (Sharma, Hu-Lieskovan, Wargo, & Ribas, 2017). 
Another possibility is that tumor cells fail to present anƟgens through MHC class I due to 
implicaƟon and mutaƟon in the MHC presentaƟon pathway (Sharma et al., 2017). 
Based on the hypothesis that resistance to ICB therapy is related to poor or lack of MHC anƟgen 
presentaƟon by tumor cells. UnconvenƟonal T cell subsets, which have anƟtumor and cytotoxic 
capabiliƟes and are not restricted by MHC presentaƟon, will be superior at responding to cancer 
resistant to ICB (Catafal-Tardos, Baglioni, & Bekiaris, 2021). However, taking into consideraƟon 
the IL-17 producƟon capabiliƟes of some γδ T cells subsets, which leads to tumor growth 
(Schönefeldt et al., 2021). It is crucial to further study how ICB therapy might aﬀect and promote 
growth of IL-17 producing  γδ T cells in order to avoid unwanted side eﬀects of ICB therapy 
(Catafal-Tardos et al., 2021).

that this treatment has a posiƟve eﬀect on the paƟent’s γδ T cells response.  AŌer treatment, γδ

shown that Vδ1 have increase proliferaƟon and acƟvity, both in the tumor site and in blood 
circulaƟon, in paƟents that respond to the therapy compared with non-responders (Lien et al.,

Discussion 
This study was aimed at exploring the potenƟal mechanism behind CTLA-4, a prominent ICR that 
has been approved as targeted for ICB therapy with anƟ-CTLA-4 anƟbody (Ipilimumab) for 
metastaƟc melanoma paƟents (Bagchi et al., 2021). In the past years, most studies in ICB therapy 
were focused on convenƟonal T cells subset, however with resent studies it has been shown that 
γδ T cells have an impact in ICB therapy with anƟ-PD-1 and anƟ-CTLA-4 anƟbodies (Davies et al., 
2024; de Vries et al., 2023; Lien et al., 2024; Rancan et al., 2023; Tardos et al., 2025; Wistuba-
Hamprecht et al., 2016). Herein, we focus our aƩenƟon on CTLA-4 expression and funcƟon in 
healthy blood circulaƟng γδ T cells.  
We evaluated various acƟvaƟon models for γδ T cells, with a combinaƟon of cytokines and TCR 
sƟmulaƟons. In our models we included HMBPP which is a phosphoanƟgen that has been shown 
to acƟvate TCRγδ in Vγ9Vδ2 T cells (Hsiao et al., 2022; Willcox et al., 2023). In this study we 
observe no CTLA-4 expression in Vδ2 subset when we acƟvate them using HMBPP. However, with 
TCR sƟmulaƟon through CD3, we found that CTLA-4 expression is highly inducible in both Vδ1 
and Vδ2 T cell subset with and it mostly expressed intracellularly CTLA-4 rather than on the 
surface. 
CTLA-4 presents inhibitory acƟon by intervening in the cosƟmulatory signaling during T cell 
acƟvaƟon by outcompeƟng CD28 – CD80/86 binding and also by sending inhibitory signals to T 
cell (He & Xu, 2020; Hossen et al., 2023). By tesƟng CTLA-4 blockade using Ipilimumab as CTLA-4 
ligand we observed no inhibiƟon of Vδ1 and Vδ2 T cell regarding there proliferaƟon or cytokine 
producƟon. We evaluated IFN-γ, TNF-α and granzyme B which can be found in γδ T cell (Hu et al., 
2023; Vantourout & Hayday, 2013). However, there was no IFN-γ producƟon in either cell type 
and condiƟon we tested, this could be due to variaƟon between donors or an error of the set up 
that needs to be addressed with further tesƟng. Furthermore, the condiƟons tested here were 
not opƟmal. As shown in other experiments in this study, HMBPP did not induce CTLA-4 
expression. An addiƟonal hinderance in this set up is that the method we used to evaluate 
proliferaƟon (CFSE proliferaƟon assay) did not provide enough evidence regarding the rapid 
proliferaƟon of Vδ2 T cell in our set up. As future perspecƟves we would suggest aCD3 to promote 
CTLA-4 expression in Vδ2 T cell before evaluaƟng its inhibiƟon and a diﬀerent method to evaluate 
proliferaƟon but due to the lack of Ɵme it was not possible in this thesis. 
As shown, apart from inhibitory signals CTLA-4 presents the ability to transendocytos CD80/86 
molecules from the surface of APCs, this acƟon is more prominent in Treg cells (Kennedy et al., 
2022; Qureshi et al., 2011) In this thesis we created a potenƟal model to evaluate CTLA-4 
mediated transendocytosis by γδ T cells. This model provided us with evidence that Vδ1 cells 
exhibit high levels of CD80/CD86 uptake upon acƟvaƟon. However, this uptake is not Ɵme-
depended this could indicate that this incident is could be a case of non-speciﬁc trogocytosis 
which is performed by T and B cells (Daubeuf et al., 2010). On the other hand, Vδ2 T cells a more 
Ɵme dependent internalizaƟon that could indicate potenƟal transendocytosis. Although in the 
==PAGE:37==

case of Vδ2 the internalizaƟon of both CD80 and CD86 was not clearly aﬀected when comparing 
acƟvated (CTLA-4 expressing) vs control (non-CTLA-4 expressing) Vδ2 T cells, thus we cannot 
conclude that this is CTLA-4 mediated transendocytosis. Another interesƟng ﬁnding is that Vδ2 
have a diﬀerent uptake CD80 with a diﬀerent proﬁle than CD86. We detect lower levels of CD86 
in the later Ɵmepoints of our model indicaƟng potenƟal degradaƟon, as was ﬁrst described by 
(Kennedy et al., 2022).  
KIRs are an ICR family that is mostly expressed in NK cell but have also been idenƟﬁed in γδ T cells 
like Vδ1 and Vδ2 subsets  and have been linked with HLA class I defecƟve cancers (de Vries et al., 
2023; Fisch et al., 1997) Here, we idenƟfy expressions of 5 diﬀerent inhibitory KIRs (KIR2DL1, 
KIR2DL3, KIR2DL4, KIR3DL1 and KIR3DL2) in healthy peripheral blood Vδ1 and Vδ2 T cells in vitro. 
We showed that KIR2DL1 and KIR2DL3 are expressed in both Vδ1 and Vδ2 subsets and this 
expression can be further induced by TCR sƟmulaƟon and/or IL-15. KIR2DL4 has been found to 
be highly restricted to a small subset of NK cells (CD56high NK cells) (Goodridge, Campbell, WiƩ, 
ChrisƟansen, & Warren, 2003) and more recently, through transcriptomic analysis a Vδ1 cluster 
which was not present in healthy individuals (Tardos et al., 2025). Herine, we also observe no 
evidence that KIR2DL4 is expressed neither induced in any T cell subtype isolated from healthy 
blood. KIR3DL2 had the highest expression in Vδ1 T cells and shown clear inducƟon in the 
presence of IL-15 in Vδ2 T cells. Due to limitaƟons in the materials, KIR3DL1 expression could only 
be quanƟﬁed in the populaƟon KIR3DL1/2 which is comprised by that can only express KIR3DL1 
and cells that express both KIR3DL1 and KIR3DL2 simultaneously. However, we found that this 
KIR3DL1/2 populaƟon is restricted in Vδ2. Here, we also observed no expression of any of the 
KIRs we tested in either CD4 or CD8 convenƟonal T cells. These ﬁndings conclude that KIRs can 
be expressed and induced in vitro by TCR sƟmulaƟon and/or cytokines in Vδ1 and Vδ2 subsets. 
In the future we would like to test addiƟonal cytokines (like IL-7 and IL-2) and TCRγδ sƟmulaƟon 
HMBPP to further study KIR expression in γδ T cells.

Methods 
PBMC isolation 
PBMC that were used in the experiments were isolated from blood donors from Central Blood 
Bank at Rigshospitalet (Copenhagen, DK). Donor blood is diluted 1:1 in Dulbecco’s phosphate-
buﬀered saline (PBS, Gibco), then strained with 40μm mesh to remove clots. AŌer, the diluted 
blood is loaded in Lymphoprep tubes with 15ml Lymphoprep™ soluƟon (STEMCELL technologies) 
and is centrifuged at 800g for 25 minutes at room temperature unƟl the plasma and cells are 
separated, for this step it is important that the deacceleraƟon of the centrifuge is set to minimum 
to allow for cell separaƟon. The plasma is removed and then the cells are washed with cold PBS. 
To remove the red blood cells (RBC) lysis buﬀer (BioLegend) and then wash them three Ɵmes with 
cold PBS. Finaly, the cells are resuspended in heat-inacƟvated fetal bovine serum (FBS, Gibco) 
with 10% DMSO (Sigma) and transferred to cryotubes for storage in liquid nitrogen. 
Cell culture 
DG75 cell lines: Was provided to us by David Sansom from the University College London (London, 
UK) 
Thawing PBMCs and DG75:  
The cryovials are heated up in a water bath at 37oC for a short amount of Ɵme unƟl the ice is 
melted. Then the content is quickly transferred into culture media (RPMI 1640 (Invitrogen) 
supplemented with 10% heat-inacƟvated FBS (Gibco), 20 mM HEPES (pH 7.4; Gibco), 2 mM L-
glutamine (Gibco), 50 μM 2-mercaptoethanol, and 100 U/mL penicillin-streptomycin (Gibco). 
Media change is performed every 2-3 days replenishing half the culture media. 
In some cases, it is stated that PBMCs were acƟvated using plate-bound aCD3, HMBPP and/or 
cytokines (IL-2 and IL-15).  
For TCR sƟmulaƟon we used plate-bound aCD3 anƟbodies supplemented with IL-15 for 48h. 
CoaƟng plate-bound aCD3 is done by covering the plate with a soluƟon of aCD3 (1 µg/ml; OKT-3) 
in PBS for 3 hours in the incubator and then wash two Ɵmes using warm PBS. For inducƟon of 
Vγ9Vδ2 cells, we used 26.209 ng/ml (≈ 100mM) of (E)-4-hydroxy-3-methylbut-2-enyl 
pyrophosphate (HMBPP, Cayman 524 Chemical) at the start of the culture. In some cases, as 
stated in the result secƟon, HMBPP was removed oﬀ the cultures by harvesƟng the cells 
centrifuge them at 400g for 5min at 4oC before resuspending them again in fresh culture media. 
When stated, we added in the media IL-15 (Biotechne) at 10 ng/ml and IL-2 (Biolegend) at 5 ng/ml 
and replenish them every 2 days for longer cultures.  
To measure the level of excreƟon cytokines like IFN-γ, TNF-α and GzmB. We used BD GolgiStop (1 
µl/ml; containing monensin) for 3.5 hours prior to harvesƟng the cells. To stop these cytokines 
from been released from inside the cell. 
All cell cultures were incubated at 37oC and 5% CO2.

Flow cytometry staining 
For ﬂow cytometry analysis the cells are stained using ﬂuorescent conjugated marker speciﬁc 
anƟbodies. For live/dead stains we used Fixable Viability Stain 700 (1:1000, BD Horizons) and stain 
the cells for 10min in PBS and on ice and dark. Staining for surface anƟgens was done in 
ﬂuorescent-acƟvated cell sorƟng (FACS) buﬀer prepared with PBS 3% FBS. TCRγδ was stained 
prior to other markers with a staining Ɵme of 20min on ice.  The rest (unless otherwise stated) of

we needed to evaluate both intracellular and surface. All intermediate washes during staining

so we used streptavidin-FITC (BioLegend) for detecƟon. 
As menƟoned, CTLA-4 was evaluated both in total (surface + intracellular) and only surface

menƟoned above. The second half was ﬁxed and permeabilized using BD Cytoﬁx/Cytoperm™ 
FixaƟon/PermeabilizaƟon kit. Here, we add BD FixaƟon/PermeabilizaƟon soluƟon and incubated 
for 20min at room temperature and dark. AŌerwards the anƟbodies were diluted in n BD 
Perm/Wash buﬀer instead of FACS buﬀer and we stained the samples for 30min on ice and dark. 
The same method was used to stain cells aŌer BD GolgiStop treatment so we could stain cytokines 
inside the cell.  
CFSE proliferation 
To measure the proliferaƟon rate of cells we used CFSE (BD Horizons). Here, the cells are washed 
twice with PBS to remove any serum from them. Then they are resuspended in CFSE staining 
soluƟon containing 1μM CFSE in PBS and are placed in a water bath at 37oC for 10 min. 
AŌerwards, we add culture media to quench the CFSE stain and centrifuge at 400g for 5min at 
room temperature, remove the supernatant and ﬁnally wash again culture media before seeding 
them again. 
Transendocytosis assay 
To evaluate the potenƟal transendocytosis of CD80/86 by T cells, we cocultured DG75 cells along 
with PBMCs at 1:2 raƟo. To disƟnguish the diﬀerent cells during ﬂow cytometry we used CFSE 
following the same prosses as in “CFSE proliferaƟon” above.  
Staining during this assay follows diﬀerent steps. Upon reaching its Ɵmepoint, we harvested each 
coculture and immediately stained for live/dead using Fixable Viability Stain 700 (1:1000, BD 
Horizon) and stain the cells for 10min in PBS and on ice and dark. AŌerwards, in order to stop the 
cell from conƟnuing to uptake CD80/86 we ﬁxed them using eBioscience™ Foxp3 / TranscripƟon

(made using 1 part FixaƟon/PermeabilizaƟon Concentrate and 3 parts FixaƟon/PermeabilizaƟon 
Dilute) and incubate in ice and dark for 30min. AŌer ﬁxaƟon, we stained all Ɵmepoints 
simultaneously for surface anƟgen staining as menƟoned in “Flow cytometry staining” above.
