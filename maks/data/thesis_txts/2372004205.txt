Abstract
The competitive scene in online video games is becoming more and more prominent. As such it is
important to have a proper skill rating and matchmaking system in order to provide a proper game
and matching experience. We will investigate how such a skill rating and matchmaking system can be
made from a theoretical standpoint as well as investigating how the ELO and TrueSkill rating systems
work.
The purpose of this thesis is to provide a framework for skill rating and matchmaking systems as well
as investigate ways of making entertaining and mathematically fair matches. Diﬀerent mathematical
models and techniques will be employed in order to achieve this and we will investigate how each of
these fare in comparison to one another.
We have acquired data by using Bungie.net’s API for the game Destiny. Here we have requested for
data and acquired 1200 unique Destiny player’s data. This data will be used for some of our simula-
tions. Other data used in simulations will be based on randomly sampled data.
All of the data processing has been done in Python by either using our own code, built-in functions
and Python packages. Our own code has primarily been used to make the simulations and our own
custom update functions.

1
Introduction
Since the beginning of recorded history people have played games. Games are present in every human
culture and thus serve as a universal constant of the human experience. The precise deﬁnition of what
a game is, is still being debated, but Bernard Suit’s deﬁnition of the term is currently the most widely
accepted: "To play a game is to engage in activity directed toward bringing about a speciﬁc state of
aﬀairs, using only means permitted by speciﬁc rules, where the means permitted by the rules are more
limited in scope than they would be in the absence of the rules, and where the sole reason for accepting
such limitation is to make possible such activity" [1]. This is a very broad deﬁnition, but it highlights
the diverseness of what a game can actually be.
A game is usually distinguished from work, since work is performed for remuneration, and art, since
art is an expression of aesthetics or ideological elements, but even this distinction can be challenged;
professional football players play the game of football as their work and some puzzle games such as
Mahjong or jigsaws could be classiﬁed as art.
Since the invention of the internet, a new type of game has emerged. The online gaming community
has grown steadily since the commercialisation of the computer and today, the most popular online
game, League of Legends, has 100 million people playing every month with peak times having 7.5
million players playing at once [2].
The large amount of players who want to play a game for fun or even to become good enough to
compete in e-sports – the professional side of online gaming – has given rise to the idea of matchmak-
ing; since diﬀerent players have diﬀerent skills, a game between two players of widely diﬀerent skills is
not considered interesting. It is thus the primary goal of matchmaking to ensure an entertaining match.
In competitor-versus-competitor games, this translates to ﬁnding two players of equal or close-to-equal
skill, making the match both mathematically fair and also entertaining. However, in teams, these two
aspects of fairness can sometimes clash. Finding a team that is equal in skill to another does not nec-
essarily mean that the match is mathematically fair nor even entertaining. Finding a mathematically
fair match does not mean it is entertaining. An entertaining match is not necessarily mathematically
fair. In this report, we will focus on the entertainment aspect, rather than mathematical fairness.
We will separate a matchmaker into two parts: A skill rating part and a matchmaking part. The skill
rating part must satisfy the conditions of being able to represent a player’s skill, updating the skill
as it changes, and placing this skill on a leaderboard that determines which player is the best. The
matchmaking part must be able to match players to provide entertaining matches.
2
Games
The following section is intended for readers who have limited knowledge about video games. It can
be seen as supplemental to the more concise glossary.
We will in this report focus on games with FPS (First Person Shooter) elements. A game seen from
the ﬁrst person perspective is a game seen through the avatar’s eyes; the avatar is the character that
the player controls. A shooter is a game in which the avatar possesses a weapon and can thus ’shoot’
the enemies; in very rare cases, it may not be a ﬁrearm, but simply a close combat weapon also known
in gaming as a melee weapon. Despite not being able to shoot, the game is still called a shooter then.

The game that we have chosen as an example of this type of game is Bungie’s game Destiny from
2014. Destiny is an MMO (Massively Multiplayer Online) FPS game with millions of players. The
designation MMO simply indicates that millions of players play the game online with and against each
other. In the fourth quarter of 2014, the game already boasted 16 million players [3]. Destiny can be
played both cooperatively, where players play together against a computer-controlled opponent, and
competitively, where players play against each other. We will focus on the competitive side of things
with a focus on people who solo queue, that is, a person who enters the matchmaking queue alone.
Destiny’s PvP has two diﬀerent game sizes, 3v3 and 6v6. PvP is an abbreviation for Player versus
Player and is synonymous with competitive play. The abbreviation can also be used with the number
of players on each team such that 3v3 means 3 players versus 3 players and 6v6 means 6 players versus 6
players. In a match, the player controls their avatar in a simulated three dimensional world attempting
to accomplish the goal of the game mode that has been selected. A game mode determines which set
of rules apply to a particular game. This will be described with an example later in this section.
The general rule set that transcends game modes is that players kill each other to prevent the other
team from accomplishing the goal of the game mode. A kill, used as a noun, is not as serious in a video
game as in real life; the player who is killed respawns (is reintroduced on the game map somewhere
else) after a few seconds and the player who landed the ﬁnal hit, reducing the enemy player’s HP
to 0 (Health Points or Hit Points) is awarded 100 points. If someone damages another player, even
just 1 HP, but someone else gets the ﬁnal hit, that player is awarded an assist, which is worth 50 points.
Kills can be done in a variety of ways, through both the equipped weapon or an avatar’s special abili-
ties – an ability is a special combat move that the player’s avatar is capable of. The player’s melee is
usually categorised as an ability and some games, Destiny included, employ grenades as an ability. A
recent trend in games such as Destiny, Overwatch and Call of Duty is to introduce a so-called ’super’
ability. Unlike melee and grenades, which are abilities that every avatar can perform, a ’super’ abil-
ity is unique to the class of the avatar. A class determines which set of abilities the player’s avatar
is capable of – the speciﬁc type of melee ability, grenade and super ability that the avatar has access to.
Finally, we look into some more specialised rules by picking a game mode. One of the most popular
game modes in Destiny is Control. Control places three marked locations on the game map; stepping
into these for a small period of time places the point under the control of your team. If an enemy steps
into one of the zones that you control, they start to take control of that zone. The enemy team then
has 45 seconds to kill the player attempting to control their point or they will lose it. The more zones
you control when you kill an opponent, the more bonus points you get. For each zone you get 50 points
per kill as a bonus on top of the 100 points you get for the kill itself. When one team reaches 20000
points or more, the game ends. Should the game reach 12 minutes of time and no team has reached
20000 points, the team with the most points win. It is impossible to lose points once gained and most
maps in Destiny are small enough that the game rarely ends due to the time limit. For an example of
a Control match, see [4].

Motivation
We have been playing video games online for at least 10 years and believe that today’s standard ap-
proach of solving the problem of matchmaking is not at the level it could be. As we have experienced
many poor quality matches throughout the years, we felt the need to gain a deeper understanding of
how matchmakers work in video games and see if any improvements could be made since AAA games
(a term for games that before they are released are highly anticipated with big budgets) these days

inevitably have some kind of online component and in each of these, the experience ends up being
similarly poor. The matchmaking algorithm is a vital aspect of such games as it needs to provide
entertaining matches in order to provide a good experience to the player. If the player gets repeatedly
thrown into matches that they experience as poor matches they may, at the worst case, simply uninstall
the game and move on to a diﬀerent game.
We have on multiple occasions stopped playing a speciﬁc game because of a general poor match quality
and switched to another game only for the same thing to happen there. People today are more busy
than ever so the limited time people have to play video games they want to enjoy and not spend it by
being frustrated; we are not outliers in feeling this way [5] [6].
We will describe one of the cases that can happen when we play online to illustrate our point. Say we
are playing a 6v6 team game. The matchmaker usually tries to ﬁnd teams where the average skill of
the two teams are nearly the same. In some cases, this is accomplished by ﬁnding 12 players of equal
or near-equal skill. The problem is that this is not the most likely scenario. What usually happens is
that the matchmaker places one good player and ﬁve poor players on one team and six decent players
on the other. This essentially creates a pseudo 1v6 scenario as the good player cannot weigh up the
ﬁve poor players as much as the matchmaker likes to think he can.
To sum up the experience, the one good player gets the short end of the stick as that player is left to
ﬁght a losing battle. While the player is capable of killing any one of the six players on the oppos-
ing team, the player is unable to win the game and since most players skills are measured based on
who wins, the player loses some rank for playing this game. The rank, also called the Matchmaking
Rank (MMR), determines a player’s position on the leaderboard, so losing rank is bad for this player.
However the experience is also poor for the rest of the players. The six decent players might not ﬁnd
enjoyment in dominating ﬁve parts of the opposing team and being dominated by the last player,
especially if they have been in this situation before. The ﬁve poor players probably won’t ﬁnd any
enjoyment in losing all the time either. The amount of times we have experienced the above type of
match reveals that some matchmakers today only matches on the mean value of the two teams and
does not incorporate the variation in skill between individual players.
While it is possible to disregard the variation and still get entertaining matches, we believe that
by integrating the variation intelligently, we could get more entertaining matches. To support this
belief we now intend to investigate the problems in current matchmaking by looking at two common
matchmakers from a generalised framework. With this investigation we can then look into ways of
improving the matchmakers to give a more stable and entertaining experience. Finally, we will also
look into some player data to identify pitfalls in the practical implementations of the algorithms.

4
Theory
In this section we will lay the groundwork for the theory behind the ELO rating system and the
TrueSkill algorithm. We will begin by deﬁning some overarching concepts that are basic for most skill
rating and matchmaking algorithms. From this framework, we can identify the important choices that
we will attempt to improve.
4.1
Framework
If we start at modelling a player’s skill, we need to ﬁrst assume that we can reduce a player’s skill to
some kind of quantitative representation. This reduction gives rise to a separation between a player’s
actual skill and the scoring system’s estimate of a player’s skill. If the scoring system is great, the
diﬀerence between the actual skill and the estimate will be small. This can also be considered as
having a small noise term. If this is the case, it becomes simpler to calculate winning probabilities
accurately. When it is simple to calculate who wins, it becomes easier to matchmake players as a direct
result. However, the opposite is also true; a poor scoring system makes it nearly impossible to properly
calculate winning probabilities, which in turn makes the matchmaking poor. This indicates that the
focus of an investigation into matchmaking and game quality should be on the scoring system.
We now deﬁne a framework for a scoring system based around a single match. The scoring system will
represent a player’s skill, update a player’s skill and place the player on a leaderboard. We start at
the representation of a player’s skill. Here, we deﬁne an individual’s skill parameter(s) S as a random
vector; the current estimate, typically in the form of parameters of the posterior distribution, is then
denoted s. The posterior distribution mentioned would then be a conditional distribution where the
player’s individual skill parameter estimate would be based on game outcomes. Here, we have not
made any choices regarding the initial prior, the distribution of the population f0(s), but have rather
chosen to focus on how the skill rating system would work when a player has already played some
matches. Therefore, we will use the posterior distribution of the previous match as the prior for the
coming match.

now let player i be involved in the upcoming match with k being the total number of players involved

4.2
ELO Ratings
The ELO rating system is one of the most well-known skill rating systems. It was invented by Arpad
Elo and implemented as early as 1960 in games of chess. An ELO rating is a number usually between
0 and 3000 [7] – although theoretically it could be higher – that describes a player’s estimated skill.
Note that we do not call the ELO rating system a matchmaker, since it does not directly involve
matchmaking; it only represents a player’s skill.
The ELO rating system builds on the assumption that a player’s skill is a normally distributed random
variable [7]. In our framework, that means S is a normally distributed random vector of length 1. For
notation, we will use X ∼N(µ, σ2), where X is the normally distributed variable and µ and σ2 are
parameters, the mean value and the variance respectively. We can then say that S ∼N(µ, σ2). The
particular realisation s is then the ELO skill rating. However, the fact that it is normally distributed
with mean µ and variance σ2, with µ representing the player’s true skill parameter and σ2 being our
uncertainty in µ, is not used anywhere.
The individual skill parameters then gives rise to that individual player’s game skill, G – that player’s
performance in one particular game – as another normally distributed random vector of length 1. We
can express that G has the distribution G ∼N(S = s, β2). Here, the mean value is the particular
realisation of that player’s individual skill parameter s. β2 is a variance parameter that is unique to
the game and determines the length of the so-called ’skill chain’, that is, how much of a change in skill
is necessary for a better player to have a much higher chance of winning a particular game. Going
forward we will abbreviate the name as the ’skill chain variance’.
In our framework, the next step would be to ﬁnd the mapping that moves the individual game skills of
all players on a team Gi into the team game skills T, however, for the ELO rating system, our frame-
work is too complicated. In fact, since ELO is designed only for competitor-versus-competitor matches
[8], there would only be two teams with one player on each team. This would make the mapping h as
the simple identity-function. Further, the ranking would be one of three possible outcomes; player 1
defeats player 2 (denote this event R = 1), player 2 defeats player 1 (denote this event R = −1) or the
two players draw (denote this event R = 0) [10]. These three events – vectors of length 1 – replace the
team game skills and the random ranking vector in our framework.

the individual skill parameters, where a draw is treated as half a win and half a loss [9]. This treatment
means we can simply calculate the conditional probability that given player 1 and 2’s skill parameters

4.3
TrueSkill
We now turn to the TrueSkill algorithm [10]. TrueSkill is a system of algorithms – a skill rating and a
matchmaking algorithm – designed by Microsoft. We will start by describing the skill rating algorithm.
Let n be the number of unique players in the set U = {1, ..., n}. Let k be the number of unique teams
in the set M = {1, ..., k}. Any team Aj is of arbitrary size with players taken as Aj ⊂U such that
Aj ∩Ai = ∅when i ̸= j and ∪i∈MAi = U [10].
The individual skill parameters are represented nearly the same as with ELO, with S ∼N(µ, σ2 + τ 2).
The particular realisation s is then the estimated skill rating. It is evident that a new term τ 2 has
been added to the variance of the player’s skill. This is necessary because TrueSkill does not save s as
a TrueSkill rating, rather it saves the estimated mean and variance to represent the player’s skill and
uses the expected value of the distribution generated by the estimated mean and variance as s. As the
player plays more and more matches, the system becomes more conﬁdent in the estimated true skill
µ and thus reduces σ2. To avoid convergence – where a player would erroneously converge to a single
skill level and stay there for any future time – a dynamics term τ 2 is added to the variance to ensure
that a player can grow or decrease in skill after the system has become certain of a player’s skill [10].
The dynamics term is not part of the framework, but rather a practical implementation that works
with the way TrueSkill updates the skill rating.
On the individual game skills G, there is no diﬀerence from the ELO rating system. G is assumed to
follow a normal distribution G ∼N(S = s, β2) with mean value s and variance β2. With this we are
now ready to map the individual game skills of some players into a team to get the team game skills
T.

the fact that we cannot accurately measure the diﬀerence between two players performance, is known
as the draw margin. The draw margin for racing is ﬁve-thousandths of a second as anything below
this limit will be considered equally fast [12].

5
Model
In this section, we will analyse some ways to improve matchmaking.
The very ﬁrst thing that can be analysed is the assumption that a player’s skill is normally distributed.
While this assumption provides a simple framework to be built on and is easy to understand, it is not
a certainty that a player’s skill follows a normal distribution.
There are diﬀerent ways to approach this issue. The ﬁrst – as made by the ELO rating system and
TrueSkill – is to accept the assumption. On top of this acceptance or even in conjunction with other
solutions, we can then look at the ﬁrst place we noticed we could improve the current matchmaking,
mentioned back in the motivation, see section 3; the variability of current teams. We will discuss the
variability issue in section 5.1.1.
The second approach is to exchange the normal distribution by some other distribution. We are open
to this idea as ELO is an example of a matchmaker that beneﬁted from exchanging the normal distri-
bution with a logistic distribution. As such, if we had more data, it might have been possible to use
goodness-of-ﬁt measures to attempt to replace the normal distribution with another distribution. We
will leave this solution as further work.
The third approach is to expand the concept of ’skill’ as a more complex term with multiple parameters
to be updated. We will discuss this in section 5.1.2.

Skill decay
We can add an additional layer to the individual skill parameters if we include a player’s ’activeness’.
Say that a player takes a year-long break from a game. Many skill rating algorithms – including ELO
and TrueSkill – would preserve this player’s skill parameters as unchanged. However, by introducing
an ’activity’ parameter, we could acknowledge that this player has likely forgotten the controls of the
game, some of the nuances of the game or may simply be rusty in the beginning.

game Overwatch has skill decay implemented where every player must play 7 games every 7 days; if

5.3
Leaderboard
We have now analysed some of the choices that go into creating a skill rating algorithm and proposed
some improvements that could be made. Referring back to the three conditions of what a skill rating
algorithm must satisfy, we have looked into the representation of the individual skill parameters and
we have analysed the choices made by ELO and TrueSkill in order to determine some alternatives
for updating these parameters. We now only need to create a leaderboard from the individual skill
parameters to ﬁnalise a skill rating algorithm of our own.
We have previously mentioned how ELO and TrueSkill tackles their leaderboards. To reiterate brieﬂy,
ELO simply ranks players according to their mean value, µ. TrueSkill ranks players according to the
mean value minus three standard deviations, that is µ −3σ.
If we have more than one parameter, as in the case of the multivariate normal distribution, we can
reduce the vector of mean values to a single number by ﬁrst subtracting a single standard deviation
and then multiplying with a weighting vector. We subtract one standard deviation to account for
new players where the estimate is still uncertain. The weighting vector should be custom-made for the
individual game mode since changing the rules means changing the victory conditions and being skilled
in one game mode does not necessarily guarantee being skilled in another. To generate a leaderboard
for the entire game – not just the individual game modes – an average position over all the individual
leaderboards could be taken.
With the three diﬀerent ways of handling the leaderboard, we believe we now have the components
that make up a good skill rating system. As some of these parts will work better than others, we will
now set up a proposed model from elements previously discussed in this chapter.
5.4
Proposed model
The solution with the individual skill parameters from section 5.1.2 is a viable alternative to TrueSkill.
However, although we are quite partial to the idea of being able to gain skill even if one loses – this
could be accomplished by playing a really good match, but with a poor team – this representation
of a player’s skill pattern is unquestionably complex. If a simpler method can accomplish our goal of
making matchmaking more interesting, the simple method is the one preferred.
Our proposed model initially resembles ELO in that it has a single individual skill parameter, the skill
rating µ. This parameter is assumed normally distributed as is the case with the ELO rank. Using
the value µ as the estimate s, we form the conditional distribution for the individual game skills as
another normal distribution with mean value s and variance β2, the skill chain variance.
If we ﬁnd the expected value of this distribution, we get the particular realisation of player i’s individual
game performance gi. Forming this value together with other players we get the particular realisation
of the vector g. This vector then represents the players involved in the game and their performance
estimate for the coming match.
We now use the mapping from section 5.1.1, speciﬁcally equations (23) and (24) inserted into equation
(22). This equalises the variance between the teams such that the matchmaking algorithm only needs
to pair players that are of equal skill across the teams. With this, these pairings of equally matched
players can ﬁll up the teams at the same time, which thus also minimises the diﬀerence in variance
and produces an interesting match.

6.2
k-means clustering
One of the ﬁrst things we intend to investigate in our data set, is to get an overview of the player
base. We expect that one can pair various styles of playing the game. When we put together players of
diﬀerent playing styles, the team would be better equipped to deal with opposing teams. We hope to
identify these styles as diﬀerent clusters in a 58-dimensional space encompassing all of our parameters.
To identify these clusters, we employ k-means clustering.
k-means clustering is one of the simplest clustering methods. By choosing k in advance, the algorithm
partitions input data into sets Z1, ..., Zk by minimising the total sum of squared distances from each
data point to the mean of its assigned cluster. Given that there are a lot of ways to partition n data
points into k clusters, ﬁnding an optimal clustering is a hard problem, but an acceptable solution can
usually be found by assigning k random cluster centers and then iteratively recomputing new cluster
centers until a point when the centers do not change [28].
The problem we had when we used the k-means clustering can be illustrated in some 3D plots of what
happened when we used k = 2. We used the Euclidean norm to separate our clusters and used the
k-means clustering algorithm in Python on our data. We then projected the two clusters into three-
dimensional space by picking out three parameters that we believed would produce a good separation
by playstyle; a player’s average lifespan, their average score per game and the so-called Kills Deaths
Assists ratio (KDA), which is basically the sum of kills and assists a player has made, divided by the
number of deaths that a player has incurred. Figure 7 below shows the two clusters.

6.4
Data issues
We will now discuss some of the issues of the data set. The ﬁrst and biggest issue is the way Bungie has
designed their API and how Destiny tracks player activity. The statistics of many of our parameters
are ﬂawed due to this design; we will illustrate this with an example.
In a game mode type called Mayhem all the points are multiplied by ten. Instead of getting 50 points
for an assist you instead gain 50 · 10 points = 500 points. The issue lies with the fact that Bungie
decided to collect all the scores from all the game modes together into one average score per game
parameter. This means that you could inﬂate your ’score per game’ parameter heavily by playing
Mayhem modes on top of your normal PvP modes and this makes the parameter less representative
than it could have been. We will in our case assume that the players really are that good (high score
per game average) when in fact it could be a really terrible player who simply inﬂated this parameter.
The same is the case with "average score per life" parameter.
There are many other cases where the player tracking can be seen as ﬂawed. Another example lies with
the parameter "objectives completed per game". This parameter is equal to the number of objectives
completed divided by the number of matches entered. The issue here is that not every game mode has
speciﬁc objectives tied to them. ’Objectives’ denote objectives like capturing a ﬂag in a "Capture the
Flag" mode or physical locations you need to hold in "Control" modes. In modes that focus only on
players killing other players and nothing else, there are no objectives. We would have preferred the
game tracking the number of objectives completed divided by the number of game modes entered with
objectives as this more accurately describes whether or not the player ignores the objectives or focuses
on them. This also applies to various other parameters such as "relics captured", "zones captured",
"zones neutralized", "resurrections performed", "resurrections received", and so on. In the latter case,
only a rare few game modes allow players to resurrect each other and the parameter is thus very
skewed. A resurrection is when a player revives another dead player before the dead player respawns.
Any of these parameters essentially gets artiﬁcially lowered if you play a wide range of game modes,
which is bad, as a good player can have a low ’average objectives completed per game’-value but in
reality focus only on objectives when he decides to play the modes that make this option available.
Again, we have to assume that low average objectives completed per game values make you a bad player.
The last issue are the parameters that are not tracked. An interesting parameter we would really have
appreciated is "Matches Exited". This parameter would increment by one if you manage to ﬁnish a
match, but not if you quit before the match has properly ended. The ratio between "Matches Entered"
and "Matches Exited" should show whether a player consistently quits matches too early or ﬁnishes
them. This relates to a big issue in online gaming; the aforementioned rage quitters. Detecting these
players is really important as the players who actually play the game and ﬁnish their matches should
not meet the rage quitters and have their experience ruined.
Second, there are other issues with the data set that are not related to the way Bungie has tracked

7.1
Testing the models
We will start by testing diﬀerent ways of matching teams. We intend to test how much time it takes
for a particular matching method to ﬁnd a 6v6 match. Further, we will measure the diﬀerence in mean
value and the diﬀerence in standard deviation of the two teams. For each matching method we will run
the simulation 100 times and take an average time, mean diﬀerence and standard deviation diﬀerence.
This simulation does not need our data set from Destiny and we do feel that 1200 players may be a
bit too small; we have therefore generated a data set of 10000 players from a normal distribution with
mean 2500 and standard deviation of 850. With this normal distribution, we have approximated the
skill range of the current game Overwatch [24], which has a range between 0 and 5000. However, we
have not enforced the upper limit of this skill range to make it more like TrueSkill.
In order to simplify matters we assume that the pool of available players – the players in the queue
waiting for a game – consists of the entire player base, all 10000 players. For each simulation we will
brieﬂy explain how it matches players and then report the ﬁndings. The following two simulations are
intended to be analogue the current matchmaking experience with one being slightly exaggerated, see
section 3. The ﬁrst simulation works as follows
1. Find a player and assign that player to team 1
2. Find four players equal to or close to player 1 within a set skill band of 50 points. If no players can
be found within a 50 point band, we widen the skill band up to 75 points; if it is still impossible
to ﬁnd players, we start from step 1 again. When we have found four players, we put these
players on team 1
3. Find a player with the skill of player 1 + 500 and assign this player to team 1; again, if no such
player can be found within the skill band, it is expanded and should it still prove impossible, we
start over from step 1
4. We now ﬁnd the average skill of team 1
5. Find 6 players who are within 50 points of team 1’s average and assign them all to team 2
This is the exaggerated simulation; we do not believe any real matchmaker would work like this, but
we do believe that given latency issues, a matchmaker could prioritise a player with good connections,
but who is much more skilled than the rest of team over a player with bad connection and the same
skill rating as the rest of the team.
The exaggerated simulation uses 0.0023 seconds on average to set up a 6v6 match. The average diﬀer-
ence in mean between the two teams is 17.290 and average diﬀerence in standard deviation is 390.25.
We see that this setup time is incredibly low but with a big cost in the diﬀerence in standard deviation.
One could argue the diﬀerence in mean is rather small when we talk about teams where players’ skill
lies in the hundreds or thousands but the standard deviation diﬀerence is very large, as expected for the
exaggerated matchmaker. It should be evident that this matchmaker does not take the within-group
variance into account at all.

Next we will try a more realistic matchmaker that still does not take the variance into account. This
simulation will work as follows
1. Find a player and assign that player to team 1
2. Find eleven players equal to or close to player 1’s skill within a skill band of 50 skill points. If
no players can be found within a 50 point skill band, the band is widened to 75 points. If it is
still impossible, we start over from step 1
This is what we imagine a current matchmaker does. Going forward this matchmaker i denoted the
TrueSkill matchmaker, abbreviated as the TS matchmaker. The time to ﬁnd a match here is practically
the same as the method above as it uses 0.0026 seconds on average to set up a match. The average
diﬀerence in mean here is 3.9501 and the average diﬀerence in standard deviation is 145.80. This is
signiﬁcantly lower than before. Both the diﬀerence in mean and standard deviation between teams is
reduced to a much more acceptable amount. However, the standard deviation diﬀerence is still rather
high given that the matchmaker does not focus on variance at all.
We thus turn our attention to our proposed model, where we now focus on equalising the standard
deviation between the two teams and thus minimise the diﬀerence in standard deviation between teams.
We chose to do two diﬀerent simulations with this model. The ﬁrst works as follows
1. Find a player and assign that player to team 1
2. Search through the entire list of players in the queue and ﬁnd the player that is closest to the
skill of player 1. If no such player can be found within a skill band of 50, we go back to step 1
and try again. When the player is found, we assign that player to team 2
3. We repeat steps 1 and 2 to ﬁnd more pairs of players until the teams are ﬁlled, but we assign the
lowest rated player of the pair to the team where the highest rated player went for the previous
pair
This simulation took 4.46 seconds on average, gave an average diﬀerence in mean of 0.347 and an
average diﬀerence in standard deviation of 48.465. From this we see that at the cost of time we can
improve both the diﬀerence in mean value and the standard deviation even more. We now repeat this
simulation, but instead of ﬁnding the best player pair we now ﬁnd the ﬁrst player who is within the
skill band of 50.
The average run time of this version of our proposed model was 0.021 seconds per match. The average
diﬀerence in mean values were 11.29 and the average diﬀerence in standard deviation between teams
was 53.118. As can be seen, this version increased the speed signiﬁcantly and barely changed the
diﬀerence in standard deviation. The cost of this change lies in the diﬀerence in mean value that is
now larger than it was before, even larger than the TS matchmaker.
We also attempted a ﬁnal matchmaking method that instead of minimising the diﬀerence in standard

as the FL matchmaker. The matchmaking algorithm works as follows
1. We ﬁnd a player and assign that player to team 1
2. We ﬁnd another player equal to or close to player 1 within a skill band of 50 points. If we cannot
ﬁnd such a player we ﬁrst widen the skill band to 75 and if we fail again, we start over from step
1. When a player has been found, we assign that player to team 2

5. We assign the two players to team 1 and team 2 respectively such that the ratio of skill rating

We add some extra steps to simulate the match happening rather than just putting together the team

After creating the two teams, we use the quality function from the TrueSkill
module to ensure that the match quality is high enough. Recall that it calculates the probability of
the involved teams drawing, even in games that have no draw option. A quality of 1 in a game where
draws are not possible means that the TrueSkill algorithm calculates these two teams as having an
equal chance to win. Given the amount of players involved and our uncertainty in their skill, we have
produced a switch case that allows for lower quality matches to be put through at ﬁrst, but as our
certainty in players’ skill increases, so do we require matches of higher quality. The switch case works
as follows
1. If there is a player with an uncertainty of σ = 8 or above, the quality is allowed to be as low as
0.4
2. If there is a player with an uncertainty of σ = 4 or above, the quality is allowed to be as low as
0.5
3. If there is a player with an uncertainty of σ = 2 or above, the quality is allowed to be as low as
0.75
4. If all players have an uncertainty of σ < 2, the quality must be 0.9 or above
If a pair of teams fail to get past the quality check, we start over and ﬁnd two entirely new teams. We

7.3
Understanding the TrueSkill skill rating system
The following sections are included only for the interested reader as none of the results are breaking
new ground with regards to TrueSkill. The results listed are, however, part of our initial understanding
of the TrueSkill skill rating system. In the following, we use the last matchmaker as described in section
7.1, but we calculate the winning probability based on the estimated skill rather than the player’s real
skill.
This results in the players performing random walks as they go up and down in skill as the estimate
dictates. This also means that the focus in the following results should be on the practical workings
of TrueSkill; we can discuss some of these choices in the discussion section.

8
Discussion
We will now discuss the choices of the framework and modelling sections, see sections 4.1 and 5. We
will also follow up on some of the simulations, commenting on some of the pitfalls that can be avoided.
Finally we will discuss a few generalisations of our matchmaker that can be made to other games. We
will name some current game titles for this purpose, but will discuss some terminology to keep it on a
general level.
8.1
Framework
We will begin the discussion with the framework. We intended to create this framework to create
a generalised method of setting up new matchmakers within a similar conceptual device where each
choice could then be argued individually, rather than start from previous work and build on some
of the mistakes of the predecessor. As such, we will discuss some of the choices that we made when
creating the concept.
The ﬁrst choice we will discuss is the choice of prior for the individual skill parameters f0(s). We have
left this choice vague on purpose as we do not know which distribution would ﬁt the data of a speciﬁc
game the best. Clearly, if the prior is normally distributed, the TrueSkill algorithm would ﬁt in well,
as seen from the simulations. But for chess, it was found to be the logistic distribution that ﬁt the
data the best. Thus, this should be a choice made by the individual game.
Further, the parameters of this distribution should also be identiﬁed uniquely. They should be chosen
in such a way that the design of the framework ﬁts with the initial value(s). For instance, TrueSkill
has chosen their default initial values as µ = 25 and σ = µ
3 = 8.33, such that the initial ranking of
this player will be µ −3σ = 0. The many diﬀerent versions of the ELO rating system likely also has
a number of diﬀerent initial values for ﬁrst ELO rating; Glickman [7] talks about a provisional rating
system for the ﬁrst twenty games – this relates to the TrueSkill recommendation of a certain number
of qualifying matches that a player should go through before entering the true ranked playlist. But
some ELO rating systems also simply start at 1500 and then continue from there. The choice is left
to the game developer.
The second choice relates to restricting the conditional distribution of the individual game skills to
only being conditioned on the individual skill parameters. Many other factors could aﬀect a player’s
performance. These could be divided into things the player has control over and things outside of the
player’s control. For examples of the ﬁrst, take ’how is the player’s mental state during the match?’
or ’What time of day is the player playing?’ For an example of the latter, we could ask ’What if the
player gets a phone call?’ or ’What happens if the door bell rings?’. In this latter category, we also
have a big choice in our framework, a topic that we have thus far disregarded: latency issues.
When we set up the conditions for a skill rating system and a matchmaking, we mentioned that the
matchmaking system needed only fulﬁl the condition of creating entertaining matches. However, to
be entertaining a match must also be playable. With bad connections, an online game becomes un-
playable in a literal sense. Players are unable to interact with each other or against each other since
the connections are not aligned. This is known as latency issues.
Destiny has had many latency issues in its lifetime and as such Bungie came out and explained their
matchmaking process to appease the players. Brieﬂy explained, say that player a wants to ﬁnd a
match. Bungie ﬁlls their pool of available players with people who have a good connection to player a.
Then from that pool, they ﬁnd other players with a skill rating close or equal to the rating of player
page 58 of 160
==PAGE:60==
Anders Harbøll Christiansen, Emil Gensby
Spring 2017
a. If they cannot ﬁll all the spots on both teams, they widen the gap in variance for the skill. Then,
if the teams are still missing players, they widen the variance in connection quality. This process is
repeated until both teams are ﬁlled [? ].
This is a hybrid between skill based matchmaking and connection based matchmaking, where a quality
function is applied for both variables. From the above explanation it can be seen that Bungie favors
a good connection over an equal and entertaining match. This is a choice made from necessity since
Bungie does not have dedicated servers for playing PvP. If a game has dedicated servers, the connection
issues are drastically lessened to the point where a matchmaker can favor the skill ratings rather than
the connections, which is why we have chosen to disregard the connection issue.
Getting back to the conditional distribution, by choosing to focus on the skill ratings, we are assuming
that the other conditions, whether within the player’s control or outside the player’s control, can be
considered noise. In most cases, this seems like a reasonable assumption and for the latency issues, we
have now stated our standpoint.
The third choice we make is the mapping h from individual game skills to team game skills. We have
outlined some diﬀerent possibilities for this choice in this thesis, but we will discuss this choice in depth
in its own section below, see section 8.2.
The fourth choice relates to keeping the team game skills as a two-dimensional vector, where they
could be expanded into n dimensions. This is a simplifying choice made for this thesis, but expanding
upon this could be considered further work.
Finally, the ﬁfth choice that we have made is the conditional distribution, where the ranking of a
match is determined by the team game skills. A choice here could be an ordering or it could be a
more soﬁsticated winning probability calculation – as done by the ELO rating system in equations
(2) and (9) or the TrueSkill rating system by JeﬀMoser’s winning probability calculation [16]. By
choosing this conditional distribution for the ranking, where the only condition is the team game skills,
we do not take the synergy eﬀect that some players may have on each other into account. Some teams
might even perform more poorly than they would have apart from each other, a reverse synergy eﬀect.
By focusing on the team game skills as the only condition, we control the skill rating system from
beginning to end as dictated by our framework.
The challenge is now to take these choices in a way that allows for smooth updates. This involves equa-
tion (1), where – if we consider likelihood function ﬁxed – we need only consider a prior distribution
that lets us easily calculate the posterior. One such prior is the conjugate prior. If the posterior and
the prior distribution has the same distribution, they are called conjugate prior. We have mentioned
the use of such a prior in the model section, see sections 5.1.2 and 5.2. The reason is that it is an
answer to this challenge; by choosing a conjugate prior as the prior, we get easy update equations for
the posterior distribution.
In a more complicated system, such as the one mentioned in section 5.1.2, this is an ideal choice. It
would not be easy to calculate the integral in Bayes equation, the estimate of the winning probability
from equation (1), without choosing a conjugate prior. With the application of the normal-inverse-
Wishart distribution, it would make a complicated skill update much easier. However, this choice
would also require the other choices to be equally soﬁsticated – the matchmaking system would also
become complex and as mentioned before, a simple solution is preferred to a complex one if they can
accomplish the same thing.

As such, we chose in the proposed model to go with the simpler solution in the form of the ELO
update. With just one skill rating parameter, the framework simpliﬁes as we saw in the case with the
ELO rating system. This gives us time to focus on the matchmaking aspect, which we have done in the
simulations. First, we will discuss some more details about the third choice; the matchmaking aspect.
8.2
Matchmaking
As mentioned in the theory, a poor skill rating system could have the best winning probability calcula-
tion and the best matchmaking method ever created and the matches would still be poor. A good skill
rating system could have the worst winning probability calculation and still be capable of matching
players well. But the choice of h is still important.
If a player is accurately and reliably represented, the winning probability calculation may be considered
trivial since even simple versions would produce very accurate results. However, matching players to
make an entertaining match would still be something to consider.
Within the matchmaking consideration lies a subjective experience of what makes an entertaining
match. Most would argue that we need mathematical fairness to have an entertaining match and we
are not disputing that. We have been subject to a lot of one-sided matches due to a lack of considera-
tion for the within-group variance and as such, the many choices that one can make here has been a
focus in the model section, see section 5.1.1. However, as mentioned in that section, we also believe
that a more entertaining match can be constructed by equalising rather than minimising the standard
deviation.
When we reached out to companies for data, we came into contact with Hi-Rez Studios, makers of
the smaller games Smite and Paladins, who mentioned in an email correspondence that they attempt
to minimise the standard deviation of their matches. Both of us have played Paladins, but it did
not feel much diﬀerent from any other current online game; there were still many one-sided matches
sprinkled throughout. This may be due to the latency issues, where the matchmaker could prioritise
good connections, but we believe that, as is the case with the FL matchmaker, in the average case, a
better matchmaker can be found that takes the standard deviation into account in another way. This
is the pairing matchmaker from our proposed model; we believe this performs better since each player
only needs to ﬁnd one other player that is that player’s equal. We will discuss the results of this more
in the following discussion section on simulations.
8.3
Simulations
Recall the time simulations from section 7.1 and the best and average case results therein, see tables
?? and ??. Choosing the best matchmaker based on these results is a tough choice. It is clear from
the exaggerated and TS matchmaker, that these are by far the fastest methods for matching players.
However, if we make players wait longer, we can create much more mathematically fair matches as
seen on the proposed model simulation and FL method. What is unknown to us, however, is how the
run time of the matchmaking scales up in a real game scenario. The reason why the TS matchmaker
is a seemingly popular choice in today’s video games could be that the run time of more complicated
methods take much longer than players are willing to wait for a mathematically fair match.
If we look at the mathematical fairness between the diﬀerent methods, it can be seen that diﬀerent
models suits diﬀerent needs. In the ’Best Case’ simulation, we see that the FL matchmaker by far
provides the best team compositions in the various match-ups. When we then look at the ’Average

Case’, we see that our proposed model outperforms FL in both speed and team composition. Why
is this the case? FL is a method that heavily relies on ﬁnding all of the players within a small skill
band while the proposed model is a more lenient and inclusive method due to rule that players of
any skill can participate as long as a pair of similarly skilled players can be found. It would require
extensive testing in some real-world scenarios, but we believe the proposed model would function well
for providing entertaining matches.
Next, we look at the correlation between estimated skill ratings and the real skill ratings from the
sample TrueSkill data set and the correlation between the estimated skill ratings and the Destiny data

these results could argue that the inherent issue with TrueSkill might lie in the matchmaking method
itself and not so much the skill rating update and winning probability calculation. This also shows
us that TrueSkill in itself might be a good solution after all if the matchmaking part could be improved.
Looking next at the uncertainty in TrueSkill, we see that the uncertainty converges towards approxi-
mately 1, be it by random walk or by the underlying skill rating. This could be a sign of robustness
or it could be a sign that TrueSkill is forcing the uncertainty down simply by the number of matches
played and not as a result of an inaccurate skill rating estimate as should be. The addition of τ 2 to
the uncertainty ensures that the uncertainty never truly converges to 0, however, it is not a solution of
statistical satisfaction. It is diﬃcult to comment on the exact behaviour of τ 2 and its interaction with
the uncertainty, as we have not done a deeper investigation of this. It could however be further work
to determine whether or not τ 2 is even needed or if it interferes with the model.
Finally when we look at the tracked win/loss ratio, it is clear from the random walk simulation that
one should only look at a snapshot of a player’s matches. Adding one more point to a large sample
and taking the mean or a ratio will hardly change the mean/ratio. This does not only apply to the
win/loss ratio but for all parameters if we take our multivariate model into account. As such we stand
by our choice in looking at the 100 latest matches played by a player, as this more clearly represents
performance, but at the same time allows for a few outlier performances. This cutoﬀwould then have
the same function as α in the ELO rating system.
8.4
Additions
We now turn our focus to small additions that do not necessarily involve our framework.
These
additions are intended to address other issues that are also involved in the current gaming experience.
8.4.1
Skill decay
We will start with the addition of skill decay. Recall that skill decay looks at a player’s ’activeness’
and decides whether an inactive should lose some skill rating, due to decaying skills over time.
Some additional choices arise with the implementation of skill decay. The ﬁrst choice has already
been made – that being ’inactive’ should lower your skill rating and increase the uncertainty in your
skill – but this choice may have unintended consequences. After all, in a zero-sum matchmaker where
gaining skill is done by taking skill from other players, when a player loses their skill to a fake team,
that skill is lost forever. As such, the sum of total skill rating points of all players will decrease over
page 61 of 160
==PAGE:63==
Anders Harbøll Christiansen, Emil Gensby
Spring 2017
time. However, the other choice of only increasing the uncertainty in a player’s skill rating may not
be conducive to a good gaming experience either. A skilled player who returns to a game would be
matched as if that player still has a high skill and would most likely lose. This loss would then cost
the player a lot of skill due to the increased uncertainty. This could frustrate the returning player to
the point that the player would again leave the game.
A solution to this problem could be to add the lost skill to the player base again in the form of bonuses.
As an example, these bonuses could be applied to any winning team and although the bonus would be
very small compared to the other skill gains of winning a match, they would preserve the total amount
of skill present in the game. In the end, the choice is left to the game developers as to what the game
is intended to be. If the game is intended to run for an extended period of time, the preservation of
the total skill points should be prioritised.
8.4.2
Trolls & rage quitting
As with any group of people, there are good and bad players. However, in any online game there are
also a section of players that are known as trolls. These are the players that intentionally play to ruin
the game for other players and are deﬁnitely considered toxic players. An example of this behaviour
could be a player that feeds the enemy team. Feeding is a gaming term for players who either in-
tentionally or unintentionally ’feeds’ the enemy team points, contributing nothing to their own team.
More sophisticated trolls that use elaborate techniques to kill their own teammates also exist and it is
diﬃcult to identify these players.
Another group of players that fall into the ’toxic player’-category are the so-called rage quitters. As
the name implies, these are players that have problems with their anger. If even the tiniest things go
wrong, they yell at their screen, blame their teammates and become very angry. Often times this rage
means that they quit the match early, which leaves the match suddenly uneven; likely, this results in
the team’s loss, even if they were winning before.
Since both of these groups of people are considered toxic players, another addition to an optimal
matchmaker would thus identify these trolls and rage quitters and other toxic players. The problem
now lies in dealing with them. An initial solution would be to give them their own lobby – their own
queue – where they could ruin the game for each other. The only way to return to the regular queue
would be to stop trolling or rage quitting for some matches, even as the other players still continue
to do so. An example of dealing with trolls and rage quitters in this way can be found in Defense of
the Ancients 2, a game where players can report each other among other detection methods [21]. This
circumvents some of the problems of having to objectively identify the harder-to-ﬁnd trolls, however
it also gives the trolls the power to falsely report other players.
However, while this solution may initially seem like a good idea, it has the same inherent problems as
putting a person in prison. If the player is a troll, they will only become a better troll by putting them
with other trolls. If the player is not a troll, they might feel enough resentment towards the system
that they become a troll. Further, it is easier to ’escape’ from the troll queue than prison; if a player
is dedicated enough they can create an entirely new online account and continue as they have in the
past, only smarter and even harder to detect.
There seems to be no perfect way of dealing with toxic players; having them as a part of the normal
matchmaker isolates them so they do not learn each others tricks, but also ruins the game for the
other players in the game. Putting them in a ’prison’ server together only makes them more eﬃcient
page 62 of 160
==PAGE:64==
Anders Harbøll Christiansen, Emil Gensby
Spring 2017
when they inevitably come back, either legitimately – for the purposes of trolling more in the normal
matchmaking queue – or on a new account. As such, we propose a solution in conjunction with the
equalisation of variance across teams, see section 5.1.1. Thus when we ﬁnd one troll, we pair that
player with another troll such that both teams has a troll. This would in theory make the game equal
– a twisted sort of equal, but nonetheless equal.
For the problem of identifying these toxic players, this is not a part of this thesis. We will leave this
process to the game developers, but if it were possible to look at a player’s individual skill parameters
and objectively determine that a player was a troll or a rage quitter, we believe this would be an ideal
solution. However, as mentioned, the intricate techniques that some trolls use to ruin the game for
their teammates could be invisible even in cases where every skill parameter is logged.
8.4.3
Rubberbanding exploit
In the game Overwatch there is an extra incentive to play the ranked competitive mode, as opposed
to the unranked modes. In the ranked mode the player gains a special in-game currency (GC) for
participating. The player earns 0 GC for losing, 3 GC for a draw and 10 GC for a win. Further, at the
end of each competitive season – which lasts approx. 3 months – the player earns bonus GC and the
amount earned scales to your highest achieved rank. This currency is used to buy cosmetic items and
these have no eﬀect on gameplay. Although this addition seems harmless, another concept will expose
the ﬂaw of this incentive.
This concept is that of win/loss streaks. A win/loss streak is deﬁned as a series of wins or losses in a
row. When such a streak occurs the player will begin to gain or lose extra rank points for each win or
loss in the streak. This bonus usually caps at some value in order to balance the system. The bonus
points are put in place to make players converge towards their actual skill faster, if they have been
misplaced in the skill ranking [22].
The inherent issue with win/loss streaks is that players can abuse the system pretty easily - all that
the player has to do is to intentionally lose games to drop to an estimated skill rating much lower
than their actual skill rating. At this point, they get matched against players of much lower skill
than themselves and thus have a much higher chance of winning. This produces a winning streak and
through this, they earn a lot of these GC through little eﬀort plus they earn their old rank back at the
same time, perhaps even ending up at a higher rank than they were at before they started. This is a
clear abuse of the win streak bonus system, but it is also incentivised by the acquisition of guaranteed
GC. The particular abuse of the streaks is also known as rubberbanding; the player slingshots down
to a low skill level in the hopes of being bounced back stronger than before.
Why would they do this? First, it is much more likely to win against players of a lower skill than one-
self instead of attempting to win against players of ones own skill level. The Overwatch team recently
changed the win/loss streak to counter numerous amounts of issues, such as the win/loss streak abuse
and GC farming [22]. Farming is a gaming term for performing a speciﬁc set of actions repeatedly
to optimise player gain, in this case optimising GC-gain. There is an example of an Overwatch player
here [23], who had a 16-win streak who rose from rank 2700 to 3928 (recall that the ranks in Overwatch
range from 1-5000 [24]). In this context it means that this particular player went from being a skilled
player – top 15 % – to being in the top 1 % in those 16 games [25]. In reality, it is very unlikely that
a player suddenly has an epiphany and becomes an extremely skilled player over the course of just 16
matches.
As players’ win/loss streak now need more wins and losses in a row, another big issue emerges as this
page 63 of 160
==PAGE:65==
Anders Harbøll Christiansen, Emil Gensby
Spring 2017
could mean that the skilled players dropping to a lower rank now might get stuck there to some extent.
This would be crippling to the lower skilled players, who now constantly get hounded by players much
better than them. As such we propose another method of implementing win/loss streaks. While we
cannot deal with players intentionally losing games directly without some sort of detection system in
place, we can deter players from losing on purpose and win streaking back to a higher rank than they
came from.
This would require a rule that states that a player can only get bonus win streak points until they
reach their highest recorded rank or within some small skill band thereof. Once they hit their highest
recorded rank the win streak resets and another win streak must be earned ’legitimately’ in order to
rise faster in ranks. Losing streaks are a much harder matter to approach as one should be careful
not to make intentionally losing games extraneous or even rewarding. It should however be a simple
matter to identify the type of players that attempt to rubberband. If the game developers track a
player’s rank according to time played (actual time spent in-game in a match) it should be easy to
see if a player has dropped from say top 15 % to top 50% in a matter of hours. If we only look at
time played in actual matches we can circumvent the players who might want the drop in skill to seem
natural by doing it over several days or weeks. When such a player has been detected it should be up
to the game developers to ﬁnd a suitable punishment. Repeatedly detected oﬀenders could receive a
temporary ban for the competitive queues or reduced participation rewards if these exist.
8.5
Generalisation to other games
We have in this thesis made some choices to the proposed model where we had Destiny in mind.
But other games have some other considerations as well and we will brieﬂy go over some of these
considerations. We will start this section with a generalisation of the diﬀerent types of online games,
divided into two categories with two sub-categories.
We will denote the ﬁrst category as a "pre-
choose" game. This is the type of game where the player chooses their character before they enter the
matchmaking queue. Within this denotation there are two sub-categories:
• One character only. The player has a single account, goes into the game and queues up for a
match. Games like this include ’Destiny’, ’Call of Duty’ and ’Counter-Strike’.
• The game has a list of characters/heroes/classes you can choose from. The player chooses which
character to play as and then enters the matchmaking queue. Example games here are ’Heroes
of the Storm’ and ’Hearthstone’.
The second category will be denoted a "post-choose" game. In these games the players choose who
they will play as after a match has been found. Within this denotation there are also two cases:
• The game has a list of characters/heroes/classes you can choose from, but you cannot pick a
new character once the match begins. Games here include ’Paladins’, ’League of Legends’ and
’DoTA’
• The game has a list of characters/heroes/classes you can choose from, but you can change
character during the match if the player wishes to do so.
Games here include ’Battleﬁeld’,
’Overwatch’ and ’Team Fortress’.
Using our proposed model in the "pre-choose" category, "one character only" sub-category is what we
have been discussing so far. As these games only revolve around the player’s account we only have one
skill rating. Destiny might be a special case in this section, as you can have up to three characters on
your account, however, in Destiny you have to log out in order to switch between them. As such in
page 64 of 160
==PAGE:66==
Anders Harbøll Christiansen, Emil Gensby
Spring 2017
Destiny, we will consider each character it’s own "account" since players can have wildly diﬀerent skill
ratings for each character.
We now move on to the sub-categories that we have yet to discuss. The ﬁrst of these is in the "pre-
choose" category; the "list of heroes" sub-category.
In the game ’Heroes of the Storm’, shortened HotS, the players have a wide variety of characters they
can select (where each character is part of a class) before they enter the matchmaking queue. This
means that we know what class the player will be playing as and as such utilize this knowledge in order
to ﬁnd appropriate teammates and opponents. The issue here is the comparison between classes as each
class has a speciﬁc set of stats with some stats being unique to each class as they each have their own
role in the game. One way to overcome this issue is to create specialised comparison functions. These
would compare the way a warrior class would match up against another warrior class; since we are
pairing players, we could easily match up players. For the edge-cases, we could also have comparison
functions across classes. This way we can compare players across classes and still be able to match
players of equal skill. On top of this we can match players based on three criteria:
• Character skill/uncertainty: The skill rating and uncertainty for that given character
• Class skill/uncertainty: The skill rating and uncertainty for that given class. That is, the average
skill and uncertainty across all characters in that class that the player has played - perhaps with
some weighting as deﬁned by how much you have been playing each of the diﬀerent characters
in that given class.
• General skill/uncertainty: The skill rating and uncertainty across all characters the player has
played
The multiple levels of skill ratings and uncertainties would already challenge some of our framework,
but the above consideration could be useful in the case of HotS for generating a provisional rating for
each new character played. Clearly a study could be made for this sub-category on its own – but such
a study’s starting point would be on the one-character case, which is the focus of this thesis.
Moving on to the "post-choose" category, the ﬁrst sub-category here being "choose a character after
having found a match, but no character change during the match". In games such as ’League of Leg-
ends’ the player queues up, gets into a match and then selects the character they wish to play as. In
this case we are limited as we do not know what character the player is going to pick. As such we
must make a choice and expect the player to perform on the skill level of their best character. Even
if the player does not pick this character the player might still have a good understanding of how the
game works and if the player is of high skill said player might be able to quickly adapt to this new
character. In order to achieve this we must therefore look at the player’s highest character skill rating,
but combine this with the character with the highest uncertainty for any active character.
For active characters, we are must be careful not to leave exploits in such a system. Say a player is good
at three characters, but would like to be matched against some players with lower skill. This player
knows how the matchmaking works and starts a new character and plays this character for a single
match. This would give that player an easy way to have an active character with a high uncertainty
and would make it possible to be matched against players with lower skill, because the system would
need to consider the possibility that this player would play the new character. To avoid this issue, we
repeat the solution from above and let the new provisional ratings for new characters be based on how
good a player is at the game on the other characters. This would again need a two-level skill rating,
a general skill/uncertainty and a character skill/uncertainty. This type of system could also greatly

Spring 2017
beneﬁt from the ’activeness’ parameter of the skill decay system, where unused characters would be
returned to a status of ’unplayed’ if left untouched for a while.
Finally, the second "post-choose" sub-category is fairly similar to the above sub-category. The only real
diﬀerence between these two sub-categories would be that the player can in this case switch character
at any given moment. The above solution on matching people would still be the same, however, the
update of the skill ratings for each character must be done with some weighting, perhaps based on how
much time that character was played in the match. Precise investigations of the diﬀerent sub-categories
would require further work.
8.6
Retention
Improving a matchmaker also gives rise to a potential bonus - improved user retention time [? ]. This is
an important factor in businesses today, especially if they are dealing with video games or applications
(apps). Today it is extremely easy for a user to simply switch to a diﬀerent product if the service of
the initial product is too poor.
Let us say that a user plays a game with a matchmaker that provides poor / low quality matches. After
getting one poor match after another the user’s frustration is likely to have increased. At some point
the user decides to stop playing the game altogether, because the player no longer ﬁnds enjoyment in
the game. The same applies for apps. If an app’s design is messy and doesn’t function properly, the
user simply stops using it.
Now if said user was playing a game with a matchmaker that provides good / high quality matches,
that player is more likely to come back again to play more at a later time, as the game provides the
user with some enjoyment instead of frustration.
We care about retention time as game companies today earn more money on DLC (downloadable con-
tent, such as game expansions) or micro-transaction purchases (could be in-game currency, cosmetic
items, weapons, etc.) than they do on the actual game purchase [? ] [? ]. While many users claim
that they will never purchase DLC or spend money on micro-transactions, it says a lot that League of
Legends (a free-to-play game) earned 630 million dollars by in-game purchases alone. While there has
been no study of this, it could make sense that the longer you play a speciﬁc game, the more likely
you are to ﬁnd it in yourself to support the developers.
Despite this there will always be the people who will refuse to purchase anything or the whales who
are borderline gambling addicts and spend a fortune simply because they have to own everything the
game has to oﬀer [? ]. Long story short, in order for the companies to increase their potential revenue
they keep ﬁnding new methods in which they can keep their users happy and have them come back on
a regular basis. Having a good matchmaker and skill rating system in online games should hopefully
provide a strong foundation for user retention.

Further work
Skill rating and matchmaking are big subjects that have a lot of diﬀerent factors interacting due to
the complexity of what a game can be. As such, we have also considered a multitude of other ways
that this thesis could be expanded with further work.
While we have done a number of simulations we would still have liked to do some more complex ones.
The next step would be a simulation in which we attempt to simulate the inﬂux and outﬂux of players

in a game queue. The purpose of this simulation is to get a better idea of how the diﬀerent matchmakers
would work in a more realistic environment. In the simulation we would matchmake players and put
them into matches for 15-25 minutes (if we went by the general length of a competitive Overwatch
match). We do not want to put every single player into a match from the beginning but rather slowly

• If the results seems adequate one could ﬁne tune the matchmaker or perhaps do a live test in an
actual game.
Another interesting investigation is the psychological impact it would have to introduce our proposed
model (or other potential models) in an actual game. Psychological experiments are really important if
one wants to introduce new matchmaking and skill rating systems. If we have a game where teamwork
is in the core game design but the skill rating system favours selﬁsh play styles the game becomes a
completely diﬀerent game than the one the designers had intended. It is not ideal for the designers,
nor the consumer, as the players buy the game expecting one experience but gets another.
If we look at our proposed model and allow the teams to accept any pair found we run the risk of
having a match where some of the best players in the world are on the same team with some of the
worst in the world. This of course is a worst case scenario, but having above average players be on
the same team as below average players can potentially be frustrating for both parts, even if the team
does have a 1:1 relationship between the players on each team. The psychological impact would then
have to be investigated.
A number of other psychological experiments could also be employed. If we look at our multivariate
normal distributed model, an interesting psychological investigation could be setting the weight of the
win and losses to zero. This way the game outcome does not matter, but only on how you perform.
The experiment is having one group players play the game as normal – we would ask them to play the
game as they would normally do – and another that only their personal performance matters. From
this we could hopefully be able to see if this removal would cause players to behave in a more selﬁsh
manner or not. In this experiment the weighting of the diﬀerent parameters would be kept secret
except for the known weighting of wins and losses.
There are a few diﬀerent variations to the experiment above. One could also test the impact of let-
ting the players know what the weighting of the diﬀerent parameters are. The intended impact in a
game scenario would be to encourage certain play styles and encourage players to focus on speciﬁc
objectives. By knowing the weighting players might attempt to focus on maximizing the gains from
the highest weighted parameters, but this may only be achievable if they, again, have a selﬁsh play style.
Finally, another variation of the above two experiments could be the last combination of known and
unknown parameters; where winning and losing mattered, but players did not know by how much, but
they did know how the other parameters aﬀected their skill rating. These are the various psychological
experiments that we could think of. These would important to determine due to the eﬀects on retention
time.
One of the next steps would also be to extend the matchmaking and skill rating systems to accomodate

Because of the way Destiny has designed their API and how Bungie (the company behind
Destiny) tracks player data is ﬂawed it clutters our data. It is ﬂawed in the sense that in a game
mode type called Mayhem all the points are multiplied by 10.
Instead of getting 50 points for an assist you instead gain 10 x 50 = 500 but Bungie decided to
lob all the scores from the all the game modes together into one average score per game stat. This
means that you could inﬂate your score per game heavily by only playing Mayhem modes on top
of your normal PvP modes and makes the stat sort of useless. However we will just in our case
assume that the players really are that good when in fact they could be a really terrible player.
This also applies to the stat “score per life” and somewhat to KDA and KD
There are also players who suck on purpose leading to a (extremely) low score per game / life.
This means that they keep suiciding as soon as they respawn. This sounds odd... how does this
contribute their team? It doesn’t. It simply means that either a. they are internet-trolls or b. there
exists players who are striving to become #1 in suicides in the world on the online leaderboards.
We will however treat these players accordingly as horrible players as they are harming the team
they are on. If we had a much larger database of players, we would ideally match these suicide
players with each other given our vector similarity matchmaking, thus leaving “normal players”
alone and providing these suicide players with a “suicide match”. This also affects KDA / KD.
Another way Bungie skews the data is with the “average objectives completed per game” stat
as the game count also counts game mode types that has no objectives at all. This number gets
artiﬁcally lowered if you play a wide range of game modes which is bad as a good player can have
a low AOCPG value but in reality focus only on objectives when he decides to play that mode.
Again we must just assume that low AOCPG values makes one a bad player.

def compute_bic(kmeans,X):
"""
Computes the BIC metric for a given clusters
Parameters:
-----------------------------------------
kmeans:
List of clustering object from scikit learn
X
:
multidimension np array of data points
Returns:
-----------------------------------------
BIC value
"""
# assign centers and labels
centers = [kmeans.cluster_centers_]
labels
= kmeans.labels_
#number of clusters
m = kmeans.n_clusters
# size of the clusters
n = np.bincount(labels)
#size of data set
N, d = X.shape
#compute variance for all clusters beforehand
cl_var = (1.0 / (N - m) / d) * sum([sum(distance.cdist(
X[np.where(labels == i)], [centers[0][i]],
'euclidean')**2) for i in range(m)])
const_term = 0.5 * m * np.log(N) * (d+1)
BIC = np.sum([n[i] * np.log(n[i]) -
n[i] * np.log(N) -
((n[i] * d) / 2) * np.log(2*np.pi*cl_var) -
((n[i] - 1) * d/ 2) for i in range(m)]) - const_term
return(BIC)
