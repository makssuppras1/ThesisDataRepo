Abstract
Traditionally, room acoustics is introduced in the late design stages and requires technical
knowledge. Performance-based architecture relies on rapid feedback on environmental parameters
in the early stages. In this context, architects lack an integrated acoustic plugin and general know-
how, in order to design alternative acoustics solutions. By interviewing practitioners, this thesis
seeks to identify the prerequisites for workﬂow integration and user needs, in order to develop
a prototype for a new Grasshopper plugin. This plugin has a simple GUI, high functionality,
and can be used directly in a geometric model. It requires minimum inputs, while providing a
multitude of acoustic outputs. It uses a ray tracing algorithm, which is veriﬁed against Odeon
Acoustic Software for three diﬀerent cases of a medium sized rectangular room (wall panels,
concave geometry, and free-hanging panels). Furthermore, modelling accuracy vs computation
speed is also investigated, as this is especially important when developing a simpliﬁed tool for
acoustic calculations. The ﬁndings suggest that the optimum use of the tool, as presented in
this thesis, is in the schematic design phase prior to design detailing and consulting of acoustic
experts. The results indicate that the underlying algorithm requires improvements to reach an
acceptable level of accuracy, but the tool has potential to improve the design collaboration between
architects and acousticians. The plugin enables acoustically optimized rooms and buildings, by
contributing to acoustical architecture rather than solving issues in a ﬁxed design, when options
are limited.

Preface
This report is the ﬁnal requirement for a master’s degree in Architectural Engineering at the
Technical University of Denmark. The project has been conducted at the Department of Acoustic
Technology February to June 2017, in collaboration with acoustic product manufacturer Saint
Gobain Ecophon of Sweden, and the Polytechnic University of Turin in Italy.
The project is a continuation of similar research projects undertaken at the Department of
Acoustic Technology, investigating the introduction of room acoustic considerations in early
design phases. In 2013, Finnur Pind’s thesis on Broadband Hybrid Room Acoustic Simulations
saw the development of an advanced hybrid room acoustic simulation algorithm. In 2016, Olafur
Pjetursson’s thesis The Integration of Acoustic Simulations into the Parametric Design Process
linked the developed ray tracing algorithm to an architectural modelling tool.
This project uses the algorithm to create an integrated room acoustic plugin for the graphic
algorithm editor Rhino-Grasshopper. The tool aims to provide architects with the possibility to
evaluate and generate design based on room acoustics.

Introduction
1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In most construction projects, room acoustic design is not included until late in the design process,
and as a result, often ends in standard solutions due to a ﬁxed design. As change becomes
increasingly expensive and the design space narrows as the project progresses, evaluations of
design choices are required early on. Parametric modelling and more organic architecture, made
possible through modern construction techniques, also means relying on rules of thumb is not
always an option.
Focus on sustainability as a socio-economic issue, means there is a desire to document the
performance in all aspects of building design. This can be in the form of spatial, cultural,
ﬁnancial, and social, or the more technical areas of structural, thermal and acoustical etc.
Additionally, sustainable architecture means being able to optimize and avoid unnecessary use
of materials. As engineering tools are detailed and requires technical knowledge, low-resolution
tools usable in conceptual design development, are needed. Room acoustic modelling integrated
into CAD software of architects is yet to be done in a successful manner. This would require
user-friendly and unobtrusive feedback, corresponding to the technical abilities of architects. By
having an advice tool, architects knowledge on sound behaviour can increase and this might
strengthen the collaboration between acousticians and architects. In the end, buildings will have
better comfort, higher sustainability and lower cost.
1.2
Research questions
The main research questions are as follows:
• How can you design an analysis tool for parametric modelling that encourages
the incorporation of acoustic considerations in the early design phase?
• How can the tool create additional value for the architects?
A series of sub questions are generated: general properties, user levels, graphical user interface
(GUI), inputs-outputs, level of accuracy, how and when to use it, how to convey consequences of
design choices, and necessary technical knowledge.
1.3
Research objectives
The project aims to integrate the existing ray-tracing algorithm into the graphical algorithm editor
Grasshopper in the 3D modelling software Rhinoceros. The plugin must provide the opportunity
to simulate and visualize the acoustics of an existing room, and add value to the project and the
design team. With focus on Scandinavian architecture ﬁrms, the work ﬂow integration and user
needs are investigated.

Performance-based design
Development in software and digital design tools has given designers a bigger toolbox in the ﬁeld
of parametric design and evaluation of building performance. The new digital approaches to
architectural design are based on aspects such as topological space, parametric design, generative
algorithms, and performance-based design [1]. The need for informed design has brought on the
need to analyze environmental parameters in an early design stage in guidance of the design.
The possibility to create components in Grasshopper, has meant that analytical simulation tools
have been the focus of much development in recent years. Continuous feedback from the user
community and an encouragement to create custom plugins, gives it new opportunities not seen
prior in building simulation tools [2].
2.1
Parametric modelling
Parametric design allows a high degree of creativity from its unique forms, relative to other CAD
tools, which mean it has attracted much attention from practitioners and students in recent years
[3]. Using visual scripting languages, designers have shifted from form-ﬁnding to form-making,
with the main objective to "avoid repetition" and "avoid straight lines". The most popular tool
based on visual programming is Grasshopper. A similar program is Dynamo, which is integrated
into Autodesk Revit. Grasshopper is open-source and allows big ﬂexibility in geometry creation
at all levels of architectural design.
Figure 2.1: Geometric model of a bench in Rhino (left) and the visual programming language
Grasshopper (right). Source: https://christevcreative.com/.
These tools tries to combine design skills with scripting skills and allows designers to better
explore design in the conceptual phase by alternating design parameters, topological relationships
and rule algorithms [3].
3
==PAGE:16==
2
Performance-based design
Parametric design can be both generative and evolutionary, as you can establish a set of de-
pendencies and co-dependencies of the elements and control the behaviour of objects under
transformation, while maintaining topological characteristics [4]. By using genetic algorithms,
the geometry can be optimized based on preset constraints. Even though constraints are viewed
as detrimental to the architectural process, the contrary seems to be true, as it can encourage
innovative solutions [5]. Genetic algorithms are inspired by natural selection, where a ﬁtness
function continuously determines the "best" solution at each iteration [6].
The new ﬂuidity of design is a move away from euclidean geometry in the Cartesian space, and
towards topological geometry with continuous curves and surfaces, also known as Non-Uniform
Rational B-Splines [1]. NURBS are appealing as you can easily control the shapes by manipulating
the control points, weights and knots.
Furthermore, they make construction by computer
numerically controlled machinery (CNC) possible. To that end, advances in manufacturing
technologies with production of complex forms, together with generative possibilities in digital
tools, can open new dimensions in architectural design [1].
2.2
Building performance simulation
Performance-based design (PBD) is transforming the industry from prescriptive methods (regula-
tions, standards, design briefs) to performance requirements [7]. Building Information Modelling
(BIM) is a precondition for performance-based design, and requires model and data transparency
between stakeholders. Even though the complexity of PBD will be greater, require more time,
and cost more than prescriptive methods, it is a necessary mean for designing optimum solutions.
The main steps in PBD are: (1) Identifying and formulating the user needs, (2) Transforming
user needs into performance requirements and quantitative performance criteria, and (3) using
design and evaluation tools to assess whether or not a criteria is satisfactory [7].
User needs (e.g. functionality, safety, comfort, sustainability) will often be expressed in non-
engineering terms, and afterwards transformed into physical factors that are measurable (threshold
values). Simulation tool are essential to the integration of building performance simulation (BPS),
as they allow investigations into the performance buildings. Initial investigations does still,
however, rely more on previous knowledge and physical principles rather than performance values
[7].
Predicting behaviour early on can be diﬃcult, but have tremendous eﬀect on the ﬁnal building
design and performance. The design space is minimized as the project progresses, which make
changes at a later stage harder and more expensive. Many BPS tools available today are more
evaluative than proactive, but surveys have showed that accuracy and interoperability, which
usually ranks high with engineers are proceeded by intelligence and usability for architects [8].
This means tools that can guide and inform design, and investigate multiple alternatives, are
more important attributes to architects [9]. With demands on energy, sustainability and comfort,
building simulation used to aid decision-making in the early stages still faces a multitude of
challenges in academia and the industry [8].

Figure 2.2: Challenges and research areas in academia and the industry,
regarding early stage building simulations. From [8].
The connected research areas include:
• Proactive building simulations that guide rather than evaluate the design. In early
stages changes happen fast, which means the design can have evolved when a response is
received from the engineer. The general question from designers is "What if..?", and as it
can be diﬃcult to give immediate response on design alternatives, the architect is left in a
limbo state.
• Statistical methods that investigates a large number of alternatives and allows the
designer to explore a large design space. This introduces investigation into sensitivity,
uncertainty of inputs and design robustness.
• Holistic design that considers all relevant and often opposing performance objectives, and
considers their optimum integration into the process. Sustainability certiﬁcation schemes
tries to collect and rate these objectives.
• Optimization that can guide the design towards high performance. As building design is
extremely complex a "best" solution does not exist. Rather its important to investigate
alternative solutions where most requirements are fulﬁlled and not contradicting. The
development is towards multi-objective optimization, which quantiﬁes trade-oﬀcurves of
solutions (Pareto fronts). Adaptation in practice is still very limited despite growing interest
in academia.
• CAD-BPS interoperability to ensure fast and consistent modelling. BPS tools must be
integrated in the preferred modelling tools of architect. There exist four types of CAD-BPS
interoperability:

1. Integrated: Numerical calculations integrated into CAD environment
2. Run-time interoperable: Link between CAD and analytical models by add-on or API.
3. File exchange: Common ﬁle exchange formats (BIM, IFC)
4. Standalone: User interprets data.

drawings and presents results orally or in reports.
• Knowledge based methods that aim to reuse and share knowledge, and deﬁne default
values when input resolution is low. Early stage simulations may in large be dependent
on databases for the multitude of unknown inputs. The user should be aware of certain
risks: (1) Conﬁguration may present local "optimum" results, which causes the designer to
stop exploring the design space, (2) Default conﬁgurations may lead to misleading baseline
models, (3) Initial conﬁguration may lead the architect in the wrong direction, if these are
not aligned with engineers, who are responsible for code compliance in the late stages.
Eﬀective use of BPS possess some considerable diﬃculties attributed to the cultural diﬀerences
between the two ﬁelds of architecture and engineering. A survey in energy simulation programs
[9] found that even though much software is available, there is limited uptake in practice. The
reasons for this are: (1) Skepticism towards the potential to provide decision support, (2) Lack
of simulation know-how and unfamiliar working methods, (3) A perceived disconnect between
the simulation process and the architectural design process, (4) Programs used mainly for load
calculations and code compliance. Furthermore, the authors note there is limited guidance
available to architects for better understanding and integrating simulation as a design tool.
For architects, intuition, past experience, guidebooks and rules of thumb, rank highest in the
decision-making process, followed only then by simulation models [9]. The lack of technical
background will often be the biggest obstacle in building simulations models. Where an architect
will prefer a simpliﬁed simulation program, the engineer prefers sophisticated software with a
larger amount of input data and degree of control.
An investigation into the criteria for BPS tools, based on user needs for capabilities and speciﬁca-
tions, found a wide gap between the priorities of architects and engineers [10]. As an engineer
is mainly concerned about accuracy and ability to simulate detailed components, the architect
is more interested in doing quick analysis to assist decision-making in holistic design and the
integration of an intelligent design knowledge base. In terms of accuracy, the engineer will favor
accurate results and validated performance measures, while the architect is more concerned
with having conﬁdence in creating real sustainable design. The architect will also be more
interested in having comparative studies with diﬀerent alternatives and having simple input
options, whereas the engineer is interested in the quality control of inputs. Furthermore, the
architects lists the following additional important attributes: Allow design debugging, graphical
representation of design parameters (in the language of architects), conform to codes and rating
system, alternatives-based approach, and provide best practice examples. Architects also suggest
that the degree of tolerable error in results should be indicated, along with descriptions of the
uncertainties with the data model and algorithms.

As a tool developer rarely states its capabilities and limitation, the user is left with the diﬃcult
task of choosing between the vast amounts of diﬀerent option with very little background
knowledge. Five tool selection criteria has been formulated to address the issue of not having a
clear methodology to compare future BPS tools [10].
Figure 2.3: BPS tools selection criteria. From [10].
• Usability and information management. Functional operation of a tool i.e. input,
presentation of output, navigation and control. Learning curve, documentation, online help,
tables and error diagnostics. Incorporation of assumptions, using default values and choosing
templates. Including input quality control, comparative reports creation, performance bench-
marking, data storage, user customization, input review and modiﬁcation.
• Integration of intelligent design knowledge-base. Providing quantitative and qualita-
tive advice regarding the eﬀect of design decisions, i.e. templates, components, prescriptive
rules or procedural methods in the form of guidelines, case studies and strategies. Performing
context speciﬁc analysis, evaluate complex strategies, optimize design solutions, engage in
"what if" scenarios, verify compliance and analyze life cycle and economical aspects.
• Accuracy and ability to simulate detailed and complex building components.
The quality and validity of simulation models and their resolution. Can be based on analytic
veriﬁcation, empirical validation and comparative testing.
• Interoperability of building modelling.
The ability to manage and communicate
building data between collaborating ﬁrms and diﬀerent software. CAD integrated BPS tools,
Building information Modelling, Industry Foundation Classes, allows multidisciplinary data
exchange in all design phases, while reducing errors and unintended expenses.
• Integration with building design process. Adaptive and integrated into the design
process. Use in various purposes, by diﬀerent users and at diﬀerent design stages.

Performance-based design
Analysis of the design in terms of performance has long been an open-loop system with model
exchange or model sharing. Programs that are coupled in run-time and automatically generate
analysis models, makes rapid feedback possible and thus a closed-loop system in necessary [11].
To not over-complicate the input parameters the user must be able to "tag" surfaces and thus
assign relevant numerical values when the analysis begins [11].
In a conceptual design phase, BPS tools must be assisting rather than automating of the design
while facilitating quick generation of integrated solutions. Tools should reduce the analysis
evaluation cycle and support exploration of the design space to ﬁnd the most suitable alternative
[12]. Fluid tools that can provide preliminary initial results to guide the design decision in the
early phase, while also being able to analyze detailed building components at a later stage, are
needed [10]. It has been suggested that the best simulation tools does not oﬀer solutions, but
merely hints at possibilities. It is up to the architect to ensure coherence and quality [13].
2.3
Integrated performance modelling
It is not without issues to have designers (non-experts) interpret BPS results. Some proposals
to overcome this has been creating simpliﬁed tools or reducing the interfaces of expert tools
[14], and by coupling BPS tools to parametric scripting, the needed geometric details to run
simulations in the early stages can be created. But, the more parameters being considered, the
more complex a singular solutions seems. In the end it will come down to a weighting of the
aesthetic, environmental and economic trade-oﬀ, which requires an intelligent and critical designer
[15].
The two processes of building performance simulation can be called post-optimization and pre-
rationalization. Post-optimization is ﬁnding a solution to a form derived from architectural
aesthetics, which is the current practice in nearly all architectural design. Here the eﬃciency of a
ﬁxed geometry is optimized. Pre-rationalization aims to incorporate a series of geometric rules
and methods to generate a solution. The starting point is hence the rules of parameters based on
performance, material and fabrication [6]. So instead of analyzing the performance and updating
geometry afterwards, the designer has the capabilities to directly generate and modify design
based on performance [4].
As focus is traditionally on accuracy of simulations in early stage tools, it shifts towards highlighting
the performance inﬂuencing parameters instead [14]. By using integrated dynamic models the
architect can become a "hybrid practitioner", working in a design tool, visual programming
language and BPS environment at the same time [2].

3
Room acoustic modelling
The purpose of room acoustic modelling is to predict the acoustic performance and solve compro-
mising design choices. Computational modelling is an important tool for assisting acousticians
with numerical data and listening tests of the sound ﬁeld. The usability of detailed simulation
tools depends greatly on its accuracy and ability to produce reliable results [17].
In computational acoustics two methods exist: geometrical acoustics and wave-based methods.
Wave-based methods models the exact sound wave behaviour, where as geometrical acoustics
consider the wave as an energy-carrying ray. In practice, the use of hybrid geometrical models,
which combine diﬀerent modelling techniques, has been the standard model for design and con-
sultancy [18]. The accuracy of modelling tools depend on the skills of the operator in creating a
good geometrical polygon model, proper input data of surface properties and the numerical solver
[19]. Reliability of the simulations are important to such a large degree that many acousticians
completely disregard computer simulations [18].
Figure 3.1: Energy time response (echogram)
The room’s impulse response is used to evaluate the acoustics. By generating a source signal, a
receiver detects the decay in energy, based on the temporal response. The response is determined
by the space characteristics and surface properties, and consists of three parts: direct sound,
early reﬂections and late reverberation. The result is compiled in an echogram, which is used to
determine energy-based parameters and reverberation time.

3.1
Modelling techniques
The main room acoustic modelling techniques are presented with a focus on ray tracing and
geometrical methods.

3.1.1
Geometrical methods
In geometrical acoustics (GA) waves are regarded as energy-based rays, travelling in straight
paths. The method of ray tracing is the process of stochastic reﬂections in particle radiation
and detection [18]. Rays are reﬂected randomly and the energy decay is modelled, as they loose
energy by bouncing of surfaces and traveling trough the air. Boundaries are approximated to be
locally reacting, meaning the incident wave only inﬂuence the surface in the point of the incident
ray. GA requires a diﬀuse sound ﬁeld, which is found to be true in reality, especially at mid and
high frequencies [20].
The requirement of a diﬀuse sound ﬁeld is closely connected to the amount of rays used. If too
low, some reﬂection paths are missed. However, the amount of rays are closely connected to the
computational time as a compromise between eﬀort and accuracy must be found [18]. It has been
shown that the necessary number of rays depend on the volume and absorption area [21].
Early reﬂection are regarded as being important to determine accurate acoustic evaluation of
a space, by means other than reverberation time [22]. In the image source method, rays are
reﬂected specular manner relative to the receiver. This means more realistic representation of
early reﬂections, but for higher order reﬂections the number of image sources grows exponentially,
drastically increasing the computation time [17]. In real rooms after reﬂection order 3 or 4 the
main energy propagation interferes with scattered sound [18]. Other GA methods relies on the
same concepts of stochastic or deterministic tracing of the ray.

Geometry
Contrary to other applications, a highly detailed model will not improve the accuracy in GA.
Acoustic simulation requires models consisting of polygons of a certain size (large compared to
wavelengths). It has been proven that the dimensions should be minimum 0.5 m [26]. The fewer
polygons the less computational time is used for testing intersections. Even though speciﬁc tools
for model reduction have been developed [27], the process of remodelling geometric models to
an analytical models is still prevalent. As rays are traced and modelled, having a watertight
geometry is also important for a realistic response [28].
Curved surfaces are not applicable in geometrical acoustics, but as these can produce important
acoustic features, it is important to consider the eﬃciency of a plane approximation, relative to
the original curved surface [18]. For concave geometry, the sound ﬁeld outside the focal point can
be calculated with geometrical acoustics, while wave-based models are necessary for describing
the sound pressure in the focal point [29].
Surface characteristics
When a ray hits a surface it will be reﬂected and absorbed. The reﬂection depends on boundary
conditions, i.e. roughness of the surface. If there is no scattering, the ray will be reﬂected
specularly (the angle of reﬂection equals the angle of incidence, according to Snells law). In case
of scattering the incident wave can be reﬂected according to Lamberts cosine law.

The surface will absorb energy, based on the absorption coeﬃcient α. In GA, the ray will only be
reﬂected in one direction, determined by the scattering coeﬃcient as deﬁned by the deviation
from a specular reﬂection.
Figure 3.5: Characteristics of acoustic treatment. From [20].
Round Robin tests for acoustic simulations have shown high sensitivity of input data, including
the importance of correct scattering, as well as a tendency to underestimate absorption and
therefore overestimate reverberation time [19].
Absorption
The random incidence coeﬃcient is most commonly used for sound absorption. This measure
is regarded as suﬃcient for diﬀuse conditions, but uncertainty is linked to this coeﬃcient, as
measurement techniques in general provide poor reproducibility [30]. The methods for measuring
absorption coeﬃcients have a great deal of pitfalls that might cause inaccurate results [20]. In
room acoustic modelling, the user is often forced to guess surface coeﬃcients, and even small
inaccuracies in absorption coeﬃcients, can mean quite signiﬁcant discrepancies in reverberation
time. Estimating the absorption coeﬃcient has proven to be one of the most important parameters
of accuracy in room acoustic modelling.
Due to inaccuracies regarding phase change and
measurement methods, it is often up the acoustician to estimate the absorption coeﬃcient, which
means unwanted subjectivity interferes with output data [20]. Using surface impedance and
ﬂow resistivity, instead of the absorption coeﬃcient, would result in higher accuracy, as phase
information is maintained [30], but when considering wide band spectra and assuming low phase
change on reﬂection, the random incidence absorption coeﬃcient yield reasonable accurate results
[31].
Scattering
The scattering coeﬃcient is the most commonly used parameter for describing surface roughness.
It is deﬁned at the ratio of non-specular reﬂected energy, to the total reﬂected energy, where
s = 1 corresponds to a fully diﬀuse surface [21]. Note, the scattering coeﬃcient is separate
from the diﬀusion coeﬃcient, which describes how irregular the reﬂection pattern is (a diﬀusion
coeﬃcient of 0 corresponds to all the energy being emitted in a single direction). Like absorption,
the random incidence coeﬃcient has been found to be the most appropriate for room acoustic
simulations [20].

Determination of the scattering coeﬃcient in GA has been proven to be less straight forward,
as there exist no architectural software that are able to export this data [28]. This leads to
scattering coeﬃcients being dependent on the simulation software. Object scattering are generally
determined by assigning a high scattering coeﬃcient to a ﬂat surface or an impenetrable object.
As an approximation, it has been suggested that multiple scattering objects can be modelled
as volume with a bulk scattering coeﬃcient, which is appropriate for early stage simulation
[32].
Scattering is frequency dependent, and increases proportionally to the frequency due to the wave
length. A model for determining scattering coeﬃcients resulting in less guesswork has been
suggested [33]. Is is based on S-curves across octave bands, derived from a single number value at
mid frequency. Note that the most critical error of poor modelling of diﬀusive reﬂections, is an
overestimation of the reverberation time [20].
3.1.2
Wave-based methods
Even though GA is the most used method in commercial modelling software, its inaccuracy a
low frequencies is well documented. Due to wavelengths becoming larger than objects, wave
phenomena such as diﬀraction and interference, will become increasingly important as the
frequency drops [17]. Diﬀraction is the behaviour of waves, when passing through a slit or at the
edge of obstacles. This is typically overcome by adding separate diﬀraction models that imitate
wave behaviour. Interference is the interaction between waves and the subsequent change in
phase information. Two interfering waves might have similar displacements, which results in a
higher amplitude, or opposite displacements, which results in a reduced amplitude.
Wave-based modelling are continuum methods (describes objects by ﬁeld and distributions)
and solves the wave equation numerically by using approximations for a surface or volume.
The boundary or volume is discretized (meshing) and interactions between elements (nodes)
are examined. This causes longer computation time than GA methods [17]. With diﬀerential
equations, the rate of change between elements are estimated and transformed into algebraic
equations that can be solved step-wise [28]. In wave-based methods input data needs to contain
phase information in the form of impedance, which can be derived from the absorption coeﬃcient
providing some approximations [30].
Wave-based methods include: Finite Element Method (FEM), Boundary Element Method (BEM),
Finite-diﬀerence-time-domain (FDTD), and Finite-volume-time-domain (FVTD).

Where geometrical methods compute sound energies only, wave-based methods will estimates
sound pressure or particle velocity and model correct wave behaviour. Furthermore, the method
results in more precise modelling of curved surfaces, providing the discretization is detailed enough
[34].
3.1.3
Hybrid methods
As acoustic modelling is an ongoing research area with many diﬀerent branches, no optimum
solution exist. Accuracy and low computation time is the main focus areas, and to accomplish
this, many tools use hybrid methods. The most common method is to combine some deterministic
method for early reﬂections with a stochastic method for late reﬂections. The issue with the
image source method is that the computational demand, and in practice the sound ﬁeld, becomes
quite diﬀuse after the ﬁrst few reﬂections. Sound tends to scatter and reﬂection density increases.
Almost all commercial acoustic software use the ray-trace and image source method [18], with a
predeﬁned transition order of 1-4 reﬂections. Models are usually added to take care of diﬀraction
at edges or due to the surface length [35]. A possible method for diﬀraction, is to use secondary
edge sources with energy emitted from the edges of polygonal surfaces in the model [21].
Other hybrid models involve combining geometrical techniques at mid and high frequencies, with
wave-based methods for low frequencies. This limiting cross-over frequency is regarded as being
very important task for future research to be determined [18], as it is closely connected to accuracy
and speed.
3.2
Objective parameters
The aim of room acoustics design in standard rooms is to minimize noise and increase speech
intelligibility.
When the room is used for speech, three guidelines exist: low reverberation
time, low sound level, and high speech clarity [22]. These corresponds to the three parameters:
Reverberation time (T30), Clarity (C50), and Sound Strength (G). The reverberation is considered
to be important for determining the noise level, while Clarity provides a spatial impression of the
sound ﬁeld [36]. Clarity and reverberation time have their strength in being independent of the
sound power level of the source.
Striving simply for low reverberation time might decrease speech intelligibility, as Clarity has
shown great importance in terms of speech intelligibility. An experiment measuring two rooms
with identical Reverberation time, but diﬀerent Clarity has shown that the perceived quality of
the space can be signiﬁcantly diﬀerent [22].
Reverberation time is traditionally deﬁned as the time of sound level decay by 60 dB, due to
interaction with the room. Deriving the reverberation equation, requires an even distribution of
absorption and a diﬀuse sound ﬁeld [23]. For these conditions the famous Sabine equation are
valid.

3.3
Auralization and Virtual Reality
Auralization is the technique of creating an audible sound ﬁle from a simulation [23]. The function
is provided by many room acoustic modelling tools, but interpreting results can be diﬃcult and
require a background in acoustics [36]. The feature requires an additional phase of processing
and calculating the response besides the initial acoustic evaluation. To truly observe, understand
and control the acoustics during the design process, auralization can be a valuable supplement to
the traditional visual sensation [36].
Figure 3.8: Principle of auralization. From [23].
The energy histogram must be transformed into an impulse response and convolved with an
anechoic signal. The response is combined with a Head Related Transfer Function (HRTF) that
takes into account, for each ear, the diﬀraction of head and torso and the sound transmission from
various incident angles [38]. The result is called the Binaural Room Impulse Response (BRIR),
which can be convolved with a anechoic sound recording (e.g. a voice or an instrument).
Virtual Reality (VR) is making its way forward in architectural acoustics, but it is not without
diﬃculties. VR renderings still mainly focuses of the visual perception, and sound, if added,
are just eﬀects with no physical accuracy [38]. Both building (structure-borne sound) and room
acoustic modelling techniques are required, and it must be simulated for the entire audible
spectrum, which is not necessary in room evaluation. Auralization has long been a viable tool,
but producing real-time auralization with moving sources and/or receivers, requires continuous
re-computation. In order to realize this, the simulation complexity has to be greatly reduced.
Furthermore, user guidelines for input parameters must clearly be speciﬁed (number of particles,
transition order, model resolutions, surface coeﬃcients, etc.), as these have a great inﬂuence on
the results [38].

3.4
Early stage room acoustic modelling
At the moment there is no fully featured acoustic design software for architects [28]. It has been
estimated that room acoustics is not integrated into the workﬂow of architects, because there
simply exists a lack of tools [36]. The most important thing for an integrated acoustic simulation
tool, is that it works seamlessly into the users existing workﬂow and software [36]. Moreover, in
order to be useful, output data needs to be simpliﬁed and visualized in a way that beneﬁts the
architectural process. The number of parameters needs to be reduced, compared to traditional
acoustic modelling software, and recommended ranges speciﬁed to provide judgment references
[39].
It has been argued that a "reverse" strategy of acoustic modelling is necessary, in order to allow
the architects to generate design from the program, as opposed to using it as an evaluation tool
later in the process [40]. The issue is that acoustic simulation tools are complex,requires a rather
large technical knowledge, and is usually a stand-alone program with poor geometric modelling
capabilities, resulting in the fact that it can only be handled by acoustic experts [36]. Early stage
simulations does not exclude the need for an acoustic consultant in the late stages, but should
provide a rough understanding of the acoustic performance for designers [39]. Three groups of
users for early stage tools exist [40]: (1) The architect who is acoustic illiterate and does not
know how geometry and materials aﬀect acoustic quality, (2) The architect who possesses basic
knowledge about how geometry and materials aﬀect acoustic quality, and (3), The architect who
applies advanced acoustic modelling software as a tool.
Peters [41] presents diﬀerent methods on how acoustics could be integrated into the workﬂow of
architects in parametric design. The six strategies are:
• Material and geometry mapping
• Deﬁnition of acoustic zones
• Output of data suitable for acoustic performance simulation (space, subspace, surface)
• Parametric acoustic surface (designing scattering surfaces)
• Sound design strategy as part of the generating strategy (functions and spatial layout)
• Direct integration of acoustic performance calculation (genetic algorithms)
Peters propose four strategies for digital workﬂow integration in connection to the design of a
FabPod [28]. The goal is to get rapid feedback both at room and surface level, as two strategies
investigate room performance, and two investigate surface performance. For surface evaluation,
wave-based methods is used to calculate and visualize sound scattering. For rooms, the ﬁrst
method involves the traditional workﬂow, using parallel modelling and simulation, which often
requires re-modelling geometry. The last workﬂow is based on integrated modelling and simulation,
where simulation runs internally in the architectural modelling software. Diﬀerent workﬂows
must be ﬂexible to accommodate diﬀerent phases of the project, and ﬂexible in terms of input
and output [28].

A method of integrating acoustic simulation into parametric design of auditoriums has also been

Method
The project aims to develop a plugin prototype, to stand as a framework for further detailed
development. An important aspect has been to determine the user needs and tool requirements by
gaining inputs from practitioners. During the process the following steps has been taken:

3. Investigate workﬂow integration and user needs by conducting interviews with practitioners.

7. Reﬁne the prototype

4.2
Software development
An alpha version of a Grasshopper plugin has been developed. The main task was to create
custom Python plugins connected to the ray tracing algorithm. The code was cleaned for any
irrelevant parts and ﬁles, and the computation ﬂow of the algorithm determined. The online
Grasshopper community and an existing internal work group at DTU Civil Engineering has been
used for assistance in the plugin development. Components was created by deﬁning inputs and
output in the component front, while the underlying core can be scripted.
Python is one of the simplest and easiest programming language to learn and use by non-
programmers. It was chosen to continue in Python as the original algorithm was written in that
language. Components in Grasshopper can be created using Python, C# or Vb.Net. Python is a
general-purpose and object-based language. It is open source, and its strength is having a clean
syntax compared to similar languages. Its disadvantages is that debugging can be cumbersome,
and due to its high ﬂexibility, it is slower than other programming languages.

owner, and the project manager. The architect’s interests lies in creating holistic and aesthetic
architecture. The acoustician is focused on creating a good indoor environment, in relation to
regulatory demands and occupants well-being. The clients main concern is costs and in some
cases innovative architecture, which is related to smaller aspects such as rent prices, productivity,
etc. The project manager is interested in construction cost and can end up changing existing
design or product solutions.
Figure 5.1: Design stages in construction projects
In the programming phase, the client formulates the user needs and requirements incl. price,
areas, functions and site. The architect will formulate a conceptual design with room function
layouts, building mass, and preliminary technical considerations, in a competition or together with
the client. Next, the design gets increasingly detailed in the design development, and external
consultants will be included if they have not been already. The team then outlines the ﬁnal design
in detailed construction drawings, which will act as the base for tendering before construction
begins.
It is crucial to remember that even though there exist some guidelines and overall stages to
architectural design, it is a ﬂuid process that changes dependent on the project. In practice
there will be overlap between phases. The conditions of the process can have diﬀerent characters
depending on whether its a competition or not. A competition might last a few months and only
deal with conceptual design, or it can last several years and require detailed information and BIM
models.

5.2
Summary
Making early stage acoustic design a driving factor in the architectural process, depends on the
architects desire to create non-standard and optimized solutions. A precondition is that they
have the tools, which enables them to "own" the solution. Its use has to be evaluated against the
speciﬁc needs of the client and available information. It will not be applicable in all projects, and
designers must choose their cases with care in order to avoid waste. Moreover, architects will
have diﬃculties seeing the beneﬁts of increased acoustic considerations, until they are presented
with real solutions, where the gains are well documented.
Even though an embedded room acoustic modelling tool ideally is applicable at all stages, it is
admissible to consider the optimum use, which is when focus shifts from conceptual design, and
solutions starts becoming more detailed. A preliminary tool can assist in shaping the architectural
design in terms of room shape, embedded objects and material selection in the schematic design
phase.

6.1
The ray tracing algorithm
This section brieﬂy outlines the main steps of the ray-tracing algorithm. For more detailed
descriptions see [46] and [47], who are the developers. The algorithm is based on Kulowski [25],
with various added models for improved speed and accuracy.
The room consist of i number of surfaces. Each surface is deﬁned as an inﬁnite plane with an
inwards facing normal vector Ni. All surfaces has an absorption coeﬃcient α [0,1] and a scattering
coeﬃcient s [0,1]. The method will be explained for one ray, but in reality this is multiplied by
the predeﬁned number of rays.
A ray (vector) is shot from the source in a random direction with initial energy of 1 (0 dB). In
order to determine which surface the ray hits, the angle between the ray and the surface, normal
for every plane, is calculated. The plane with an obtuse angle and the shortest distance, is the
plane that is hit.
Figure 6.2: The cross-section of the concave room. v: the vector parallel with the ray, Ni: the vector
directed inwards to the room, normal with the ith plane, βi: the angles between the vectors v, N1 and v,
N2, respectively, P: the sound source, lk: the length of the line sector between the sound source and the
puncture point on the kth plane. From [25]
The reﬂection point becomes a new source position, and the direction of the new ray is determined
by the scattering coeﬃcient and a randomly generated number [0,1]. If the random number is
larger than the average scattering coeﬃcient (splitting coeﬃcient), the ray is reﬂected specularly
(Snell’s law). Otherwise it is reﬂected diﬀusely (Lambart’s cosine law). The loss in energy is
determined by the absorption coeﬃcient, and energy lost when traveling through the air according
to the air attenuation factor.
34
==PAGE:47==

Because absorption and scattering are frequency dependent, a model which runs all frequency
bands in a single pass is implemented to reduce computation time.
The receiver is modelled using the secondary source method, with a new source at every reﬂection
point. The secondary sources are deﬁned by the amount of energy the ray has lost since it left
the source. When the energy of the ray is decreased by a certain amount (-60 dB) it stops, and
contributions from the secondary sources are calculated. Only if a source is in the line of sight
from the receiver, is the secondary source valid. This is determined by a visibility check.
In order to detect which surface the ray hits, the "point in polygon" test is used. This is necessary
as planes can have multiple surfaces. In short, the methods checks whether or not the reﬂection
point is within a certain polygonal surface. As all the rays are traced an energy histogram is
created by grouping reﬂections into bins, based on the time elapsed from when the ray left the
source, till it hits a reﬂection point (see Figure 3.1).

6.4
Installation
The Wave components are saved as individual User Objects in a existing Grasshopper folder for
the purpose. To install on a diﬀerent computer, simply drag the ﬁles into the Grasshopper folder.
Objects can still be opened and modiﬁed.
The executable script containing the simulation algorithm is compiled using a Python module
called cxfreeze. This enables the ray trace to run without an integrated development environment
(IDE), as it saves all necessary toolboxes and packages. Only the main script needs to be compiled,
as it can still import regular scripts. The script is compiled in the Command Prompt. First set
the directory to the ray trace folder ("cd directory). Next input "python setup.py build". The
script is compiled in approximately 10 seconds. The "setup.py" ﬁle is a short script, specifying
the name of the compiled and special packages that need to be included. In this case the scientiﬁc
computing package "numpy" was added. The reason the tool is scripted directly in Grasshopper,
is because the Grasshopper component ghPython currently runs on an older version of Python
than the original algorithm. Furthermore, this method is ideal for further development of the
algorithm. A ﬁnal room acoustic algorithm could be a single compiled ﬁle.
6.5
Summary
The Wave plugin allows fast calculation and the reuse of data and comparative studies. It
has an easy and simple GUI, design related results, and saves results for further processing.
Reverberation time and Clarity are chosen as acoustic parameters, as they will determine the
initial acoustic quality, without overwhelming the user with output options. The use of General
and Absorbent material lists will educate the user, who is not familiar with acoustic boundary
conditions. Furniture is added to the geometric model by assigning a surface or box with a
high scattering coeﬃcient. The size and corresponding scattering coeﬃcient should be related to
the expected amount and type of furniture. Unfortunately, lists in Grasshopper are text-based,
which means imagery and detailed speciﬁcations of materials (thickness, price, manufacturer,
measurement method, mounting, etc.) must be added to the user manual instead.

8.5
Summary
Results show the limitations of the ray trace algorithm for smaller non-diﬀuse rooms. Reverberation
is severely overestimated (i.e. absorption not correctly modelled) at low and mid frequencies,
while appropriate results are found for high frequencies. Clarity results are similar to Odeon for
Case 1 and 2, at mid and high frequencies, while Case 3 show incorrect modelling of free-hanging
absorbers. In general, Clarity is underestimated in Wave for all cases.
The accuracy test has shown large uncertainty in Reverberation time calculation at mid frequencies,
but precise modelling for Clarity below JND, even with a low number of rays. 16000 rays appear
to be appropriate for achieving stable results of T30. Based on the test, the following ray settings
are chosen for the plugin: 1000 ("fast"), 4000 ("Standard"), and 16000 ("Best"), corresponding
to 1 min., 4 min., and 17 min., respectively. Note that it must be expected that a user will always
choose the fast option for all design iterations until the ﬁnal design is reached. Therefore, it is
important that this option provides reliable results. Accuracy tests based on real measurements
are needed to fully estimate the quality of the algorithms.
Test of free-hanging elements highlight the importance of modelling scattering due to diﬀraction.
Furthermore, the chosen method of curved surface splitting, appear appropriate compared to
Odeon. Modelling smaller rooms with low scattering and uneven distribution of absorption causes
ﬂuctuations in the results, as rays can bounce between non-absorbent walls in a specular manner.
In general, computation time increases with the number of surfaces and number of, but drops
with regard to the amount of absorbent materials and scattering.

9
Discussion
9
Discussion
It is relevant to consider who will beneﬁt from an early stage acoustic tool. The technical abilities
of the users, inaccuracy of the algorithm and detail level of inputs, mean that its application is
not without issues. Improving the comfort and indoor climate of occupants must be the end goal,
while also considering cost and sustainability. If the tool is used as documentation and the user
is not aware of its underlying limitations, it could mean exclusion of an acoustic consultant. In
that case, the acoustic conditions will most likely not be improved for the occupants. It might
even lead to additional spending when the project is completed, which is unfortunate for the
client. On the other hand, its eﬀect will be beneﬁcial if the user sees it as a preliminary advice
tool, used to shape the design and gain insight into room acoustics. When creating an early
stage simulation tool there are two possible pitfalls: (1) The user ﬁnds it too complicated and
the interface is not functional, or it is in general too technically demanding. (2) The tool can
give the user a sense of "false comfort", which mean they disregard the uncertainties and avoid
consulting an acoustician.
The simpliﬁcation of simulating a complex physical eﬀect, which already has its issues even in
advanced acoustic modelling tools, could mean unreliable results. If the tool is too inaccurate,
spending time on simulations in the early stage can be time waste full for architects. Moreover,
the suﬃciency of current solutions could mean architects simply do not agree on its usability.
But, as experience grows with its use, it might encourage users to look beyond the results of
the tool, and educate themselves on the eﬀects of geometry and materials. Room acoustics is
somewhat contradictory to the architectural design process, as it requires detailed construction
elements. Timing its use in the design process is therefore important. As the project progresses
most architects will shift to software that is more BIM compatible and has a higher level of detail.
It is interesting to consider whether a simulation tool for architects would be more beneﬁcial
in this context. On the contrary, at this stage it might be easier and more accurate to simply
use stand-alone acoustic software. In any case, detailed BIM software does not have the same
ﬂexibility for exploring design as Rhino does.
Leaving acoustic assumptions to non-acousticians enforces the importance of proper software
design to reduce uncertainties. Sensitivity analysis and similar tools could be a valuable step
in evaluating results. Furthermore, the large inﬂuence of scattering has been well documented,
and as regulatory recommendations are based on furnished rooms, prediction of objects and how
they are modelled is important. On the other hand, this might prove an extra burden, which
could discourage users. The handling of a material library in general is tricky, as the absorption
curve of materials diﬀer greatly even within the same category. Forming simpliﬁed libraries with
general materials (concrete, wood, etc.) is doable with average values, but this will only further
reduce accuracy. On the other hand, having a detailed library is a considerable task, but it
might be advantageous. This task could be solved by creating a online platform, where users
and manufacturers can add materials to a global library, which can be downloaded. In that case,
oversight would be important, to protect the data integrity.

==PAGE:62==
9
Discussion
Furthermore it must be considered whether the subjective parameters used by acousticians are
sympathetic to architects. Could they beneﬁt from an update that takes into consideration
users who are not familiar with acoustic terms? For instance, decibel in Clarity is not the same
measure as decibel in sound pressure level, which can create confusion. It will be the architects
responsibility to gain this knowledge, but it is not an easy demand as the tool can quickly be
disregarded if the gains are not visible. To that end, new and more abstract parameters related
to real-world noise complaints and spatial layout could be beneﬁcial.

Conclusion
10
Conclusion
The overall object of this project has been to investigate the implementation and development of
a preliminary embedded room acoustic tool for architects. Besides developing the conceptual
frame of a tool, the purpose of the project has been to illuminate the many challenges associated
with early stage acoustic simulations. For the plugin to create additional value to the project
and its members, it has been designed with high functionality, a simple user-interface, direct
use of geometric models, and a multitude of diﬀerent outputs. It is expected that the tool
will be most usable in special cases, where the designer or client want particular attention to
noise levels and speech intelligibility, or if geometry and material choices undermines the use of
heuristic techniques and standard solutions. The actual usability of the tool remains unseen until
a beta-version is tested in practice.
The room acoustic algorithm of ray tracing has issues with regards to accuracy, but it is estimated
that in spite of this, the implementation of the tool would still improve acoustic design in an
indirect sense, as users gain knowledge and experience. The preliminary tool will improve the
creative dialogue between architects and acousticians and force them to reconcile their opposed
interests in terms of short computation time and high accuracy, among other conﬂicting demands.
An embedded simulation tool should have the sophistication of being usable in all design stages,
but it is estimated that the created tool will be most valuable during the formulation of the
schematic design, where it can evaluate and reﬁne the design. The user is required to have basic
room acoustic knowledge, otherwise time and work might be wasted. Results might then be
disregarded by the acoustician - or worse - the project continues based in inaccuracies without
expert consultation.
Veriﬁcation tests of the ray trace algorithm shows that improvements are needed for better
performance and lower computation time. Reverberation time is unacceptably inaccurate for
medium sized rooms, while Clarity is more stable at mid and high frequencies. Clarity ﬂuctuates
less than reverberation time with regards to the simulation quality. It is estimated that additional
improvements and user selection models should be implemented in order to properly model
internal objects and curved surfaces.
The use of Auralization and Virtual Reality in early stage acoustic design remains to be investigated
in depth, in relation to the technology and its application. VR must have high accuracy and
functionality that makes it possible to alter materials directly in the environment. This will make
it a tool that truly assists design generation in an unobtrusive manner. Architectural practice
is developing towards performance-based design, parametric topological modelling and genetic
algorithms for optimization and generative design. Additionally, computer-aided-manufacturing
and VR sensory design systems will likely play a major role in the future. An embedded room
acoustic modelling tool touch upon all these topics, and can contribute to acoustic design reaching
previously unseen signiﬁcance in building design.

11
Future research
11
Future research
In order to determine the full potential of the tool in practice, additional investigations into its
usability and functionality are necessary. New components can improve the ease of comparative
simulations and multivariate analysis along with more design related objective parameters can
also be introduced. The amount of rays used in simulations should be dynamic and based
on, for instance, volume, number of surfaces and level of detail. There is continuous progress
from Grasshopper developers to ease the creation of custom components. A component wizard
(Grasshopper Assembly) for Visual Studio exist (C# and Vb.Net component) and the next version
of Rhino (v.6) will likely include new functions for creating components directly in the GhPython
component.
A pilot project in an architectural ﬁrm will reveal the possibilities and limitations of early
stage acoustics and embedded modelling. Gains in terms of cost, sustainability, client and user
satisfaction should be quantiﬁed. Testing the use of auralization and virtual reality in early stage
design would likewise be interesting.
There is deﬁnitely a desire for additional modelling tools. Being able to estimate sound insulation
of facades could be an extremely useful tool in the conceptual design phase. It would then
be a supplement to the already established analysis tools for wind, solar and shade conditions.
Furthermore, tools that allow architects to generate and optimize surfaces based on acoustic
properties would be beneﬁcial. The link to future customized manufacturing processes and genetic
algorithms should be considered in that same context. Implementing an embedded tool in Revit
Dynamo could be interesting. As the current algorithm runs externally from Grasshopper, it
should be possible to implement in a diﬀerent software. This would show the use of embedded
modelling at late design stages, when there is a higher level of detail. In that sense, a room
acoustic tool would see more use in optimizing a predeﬁned solution rather that generating
it.
Research is needed to fully understand the determination of furniture scattering and how it
connects to user inputs. Additionally, coupled rooms are becoming increasingly important in
architectural design, as functions become more ﬂuid. This is a special case in acoustics, and how
a preliminary tool handles this will be important to its accuracy. To improve accuracy and speed
of the algorithm, the image source method could be implemented for better determination of
early reﬂections. Meanwhile, diﬀraction models for scattering will improve the general quality.
Estimating the implications of using a splitting coeﬃcient for scattering, as the ray trace does
now, would likewise be interesting. Ideally, a wave-based method could be coupled with the
geometrical techniques for correct modelling of the full spectrum. To improve the computation
time, the algorithm should be implemented in a diﬀerent programming language (e.g. C#),
as Python is relatively slow and not eﬀective for multi-threading and memory intensive tasks.
Finally, as the algorithm is improved, comparison with real measurements would be necessary to
provide a true preview of its accuracy.

B.1
Ewa Bryzek, Architect, Henning Larsen Architects
Q1.1 Describe your design process.
We usually start looking at the context, the location, the neighborhood and the function of
the building. Then, how is the scale of the context, are there any special features? We will
always look at the climate as well. After this we start looking for the main idea. Sometimes it
can be a few diﬀerent ideas where we settle on the best. I usually work with both the volume
and how to organize the program and functions inside, at the same time. Some people just
work with the volume, and some people just work with the functions, so that can diﬀer.
When the competition starts, we get a program, which is basically a list of all the rooms in
an excel sheet. Then I make a script in Grasshopper that transforms the sheet into objects
in Rhino with exact square meters and names. You can then play with the blocks of the
functions. This is basically how you start working with every competition. You get all the
functions and areas of what the client wants. You can organize functions in a hundred diﬀerent
ways so together with that, you start your volume studies. The area study together with the
function study always leads you to the concept. When the team settles on one concept, then
you can start detailing it. But the concept you settle on can be done in many ways, so we
test diﬀerent alternatives again.
With a current concert hall competition, it was really important that we also started detailing
the main hall and its interior in the competition. When we started doing that, we stepped
away from Grasshopper, even though we still used it for decorating or designing elements,
for instance, wooden lamellas. It’s so easy to do that in Grasshopper [compared to Rhino].
Doing it in Rhino would be really time consuming. When you have Grasshopper, you can
divide the surface in so many diﬀerent ways so fast. Since we are architects we don’t know
anything [about acoustics] so we will send our Rhino model to our consultants, who then
checks acoustics. Then he comes back with some comments, for instance “Its maybe better
to have those balconies tilted” or “use some other material”. This always takes a lot of time,
especially when the consultant is in another time zone. We often get an answer on something
completely outdated, because you moved on to something else, so it’s deﬁnitely a waste of
time in some cases. The same can happen with the structure or MEP. Sometimes we just
have to make decisions and that might be annoying for everybody, but we as architects have
to make sure everything works according to the program.
The degree of Grasshopper used in projects depends on the people involved in the project.
Younger architects like to work with Grasshopper. This is why the team is always consisting of
an experienced architect, someone like me [younger architect] and two interns. The workload
is actually quite nice.

==PAGE:73==
In the beginning, ﬁnding the concept and main shape, we work together, but once we settle
on a concept, we start splitting the tasks. One intern will be making the physical models,
one can do test on the shapes in Grasshopper, and I will be sitting with the plan and the
program. At some point, we settle on the concept, and then we start testing the facade, the
structure, or the interior. How can it look? How can it work? We are also looking for things
that can support the concept, like references, and we now start tying everything together.
We always have to make a site plan [presenting ﬁnal competition A1 posters] with perhaps
some holes in the roof or however we want to do it. Then there’s always a set of diagrams
showing how it works. What does it relate to? How does, for instance, the wind work? We
always get our Sustainability department to show that it actually works. Then you have all
the plans with all the rooms listed in the competition brief to show they are within the square
meters. We do [exploded] axonometric drawings to show the client how the building works.
Then we show all the rooms and the relations. Even though you design this very cool shape,
you really have to make sure it actually works and that you can ﬁt all the things that the
client wants.
Determining the materials can be dangerous. If you put something expensive the client can

After a competition is won, a new team takes over the project. Usually one person from the

do have one team who has worked on a competition for two years, and they have to end
the competition in Revit. In that case, it’s all about the cost so everything has to be quite
detailed.
Q1.2 How and when do you include building simulations?
We include the building simulation and investigations in the competition material. We are

Sometimes they can be really involved [in the competition]. For every competition, we have

process, and usually they give some really good advice. It’s a very big support we can get
from them. These things can be really important to the concept.

Q1.3 In competitions, what is most important to you? and to the client?
Depends on the competition, which can be very diﬀerent. There are open competitions and
invited competitions. In the open competitions, you have 30 or 100 architects submitting
only boards and a booklet. You don’t even speak to the client, and everything is anonymous.
In invited competition, which is often the case for us, you are maybe 5 companies in total.

Q1.7 What would motivate you to include more acoustic considerations?
It depends on the project. For concert halls, its necessary to include acoustics, because you
want to make sure your design is good for the music and overall experience. For schools,
or big atriums, it should also be nice in terms of acoustics. Its shouldn’t be loud and you
shouldn’t feel intimidated by the room. It would be really nice to include the sound in the

Usually engineering drawings are super cool and easy to read, but sometimes people tend
to get very speciﬁc so it has to actually mean something, and it has to add something to
the project. A diagram with the quiet and loud function would be nice. For instance, in the
quiet are you have a speciﬁc type of materials and in the louder sections you have some other
materials. A diagram supporting this idea would be good. It always depends on the concept
as sometimes it’s really important to show a quiet area.
I could also see a beneﬁt in having some guides on how to design for acoustics in a practical
way. In terms of daylight for example you have some limits to number of desks you can have.
Q1.8 How do you use parametric modelling in the design process?
It’s diﬃcult to start doing the geometry in Grasshopper because you have so many parameters
to consider [in the competition] - for instance the modular grid. Already here you set some
dimensions. You can change the grid but then it messes up other parameters. You just have
to choose which parameters is the most important. Its less with Grasshopper and more with
common sense and logic.
I use mostly Grasshopper to calculate the areas from the project brief, which you can do very
easily in a way so you always know your total square meters. Then you can optimize and
such, but I don’t do that very much in Grasshopper. Just making a script can take a long
time, so if you have to optimize the structure, daylight, acoustics, and program, you have too
many diﬀerent parameters. You would rather go on previous knowledge. Architecture is a
combination of so many disciplines so you can’t have a very big knowledge in the engineering
ﬁelds. Our in-house engineers use the simulation plugins in Grasshopper.

Q1.13 Do you see a development in the industry towards performance-based design

wants. Sometimes he wants to be really groundbreaking and have something new and cool.
Q1.15 Do you see the need for my tool?

Q1.1 Describe your design process.
Personally, I am more skilled in mid and late stage design. I am not that conceptually fast
footed so to say. I usually end up with the results of a competition, which means I will see
the things that were not considered early on. We have a lot of diﬀerent projects. For instance,
when it comes to a block of ﬂats you develop a scheme together with the client or developer.
Here you talk about all sort of things, but their main concern is square meters. How many
square meters can we ﬁt into this lot and sell. The geometry and facade materials are part of
the early stages, but the geometry is not considered “complicated” - it’s not the driving factor.
It is plan wise, but ceiling heights and other things regarding acoustics isn’t really an issue
in the early stages. Also, there’s a bit of naive negligence of light, energy, and sometimes
structure, that will hit you in the back of the head later when it’s too late to change things.
If you develop something together with a developer and they ﬁnd out we have a new potential
client, you change your layout to ﬁt that. But it’s mainly plane based, 2D ﬂoor plan. On the
contrary, if you win a competition then you have a strong concept and its agreed with the
developer. When you have a competition, the developer has really thought things through in
the beginning and they make a list of demands. The proposal that best ﬁt the list of demands
wins the competition. Therefore, you already have solved a lot of problems and most things
are decided in the competition. There are rarely acoustic demands in the project. You have
a special case then maybe yes, but it’s mostly like: “Consider acoustics. Period.”. It’s very
seldom mentioned in the early stages.
There are three diﬀerent types of projects: A competition, a tender, and when a client comes
directly to you. The competition and when the client comes to you, are least common. Also,
there are competitions you win and competitions you lose. We have a hit rate of about 40%,
which is rather high, but the majority of the competitions we do, we don’t win. The actual
commission we get are primarily tenders, after that clients coming directly to us, and lastly
for competitions. In tenders, you mainly present your credentials, your team, and previous
projects. It will still be on a competitive level, and there are often multiple stages in the
tender process.
The duration on the projects diﬀer very much. In a competition, which can be a few months
depending on the size, things can move very fast after you’ve won, because the client is
prepared and knows what he wants. In non-competitions things move a bit slower. If you
have won a competition you are fairly conﬁdent that you concept are strong enough to survive
the whole project. But you have to sometimes over-conceptualize in order to tell a story and
to convey a sense of wonder. The client actually doesn’t choose the best project from rational
calculation, they choose the project they love by heart and then they rationalize afterwards.
That’s of course not the full truth but there’s something to it. Sometimes you design things
you know probably won’t hold up but you only have a limited amount of time so you can’t
solve everything - then you have to work out the details later.

Q1.3 In competitions, what is most important to you? and to the client?
You can’t rely on the decision makers to know the subject [acoustics] but they have to know
enough to make it a priority at an early stage. You have to tell the decision makers that,
for instance, a standard ceiling solutions looks bad, because they don’t care. Meanwhile,
architects are not going to use the building so they think that’s a minor problem. Therefore,
all oﬃce buildings have these ceilings. That’s what we are up against. It would be up to
the architect to stress the importance to decision makers I suppose. In our everyday work,
we try as best we can, but we often say “Let’s not bother them about that, we have bigger
problems”. Usually that’s true, there are bigger problems so it’s hard to get it [acoustics] to
the forefront. But, if we could own that, and do it on the ﬂy, and they don’t have to make a
decision because we already do it as a part of the normal process, then you can skip all the
politics and prioritizing. But then we need the tools.
Q1.4 How is your relationship and collaboration with engineers/acousticians?
We have had early corporation with structural, energy, sustainability experts in the early
stages but that’s rare. That mainly for high proﬁle projects. The results are oﬀcourse always
much better because you get a competition proposal that have incorporated these aspects.
Your normal workday is doing the ﬂoor plan scale 1:200, and then you start thinking about
the technical aspects. Then the external experts come in.

They have a diﬀerent mind-set, and in this crossroad, I think there is much potential. As
an architect, you get educated in looking at the world in a certain way, and what you are
trying to achieve is holistic solutions to all issues in one concept. If you’re an engineer you are
educated in breaking a project up in pieces and isolate every problem to solve them discretely.
If we talk about acousticians, developers and project leader think acoustics is just a small
chunk of this and it doesn’t aﬀect overall economy very much. Therefore, they can put that

It’s very diﬃcult to build and there are enormous risks. There is much money on the line and

Instead of sitting in our own corner, we try to go to the decision makers and say “Let’s do this
together. We can educate you and you can educate us. We can beneﬁt from each other”. In
my experience that is rather unique and can mostly happen with smaller clients that are more
driven by values, and have a strong personal passion invested. With big projects, you don’t
get that because stakeholder is usually hired hands, so they are not very interested in having
this dialogue. The actual client has no competence because everything is outsourced and the

have their own agenda, and that’s a really big problem. Ideally, everybody work together in

are completely diﬀerent ways of working, and of course there’s a clash, but I would like the
consultant to be able to play badminton with us in the early stages.
[When asked about being challenged more by acousticians] In terms of acousticians I don’t
really need to be challenged. I perceive the challenge myself on how could we do it. The
acoustician should be my team mate and we should do this together with our diﬀerent
competences. We together take on the challenge, it’s not one challenging the other.

Q1.6 What would motivate you to include more acoustic considerations?

Q1.8 How do you best use results from simulations?
When it comes to daylight and wind it’s usually very colourful renderings, but otherwise if

a no on the design, it’s always “more mineral wool absorbers”. It’s diﬃcult to have a creative
dialogue as it is usually late in the process and the acoustician is not so interested in having a
dialogue. They are more interested in teaching me what is possible and not possible, and not

perhaps they look at a folder from a manufacturer as well.

Jesper Bo Andersen, Aoustician, Rambøll
Q2.1 How is the collaborative process between you, as acoustician, and the archi-
tect?
There exist two diﬀerent types of project. In the ﬁrst you can have a cultural building, which
could be a multi-purpose hall, and you look at the room as being used for speech or music.
Here you are included in the initial phase as acoustician, and you discuss, perhaps not room
volume and geometry, but surfaces and placement of absorbents and materials. This often
in the competition stage as well, where nothing is really ﬁxed yet in the design. This is the
most exciting part for the acoustician. The second type of projects could be a school project.
These room types are usually similar throughout the project. Most architect have an idea
of what to do [in terms of acoustics], but they need an acoustician to validate the design.
Here you [as acoustician] check the design. There are fewer possibilities of design changes,
because the project is already though as being ﬁnished. You can change some areas, materials
and wall absorbents. The two extremes are therefore: being part of the design process, and
checking that the design fulﬁlls demands.
Typically, the architect will present an idea of how they want a room to be visually and
architecturally. Then I like to support their idea and make sure their vision is kept, while
it works acoustically as well. Sometimes the architect will ask for suggestions from the
acoustician. This can be diﬃcult, as you can end up suggesting something so far from the
architect’s vision that they have a hard time taking your idea and coming up with other
alternatives. So, if you suggest an idea that are far from the architect’s taste and desires,
the architect can get defensive. Then it becomes diﬃcult to explain that you can really do
anything you want in terms of materials. The challenge of the acoustician is to start the
creative process surrounding acoustics. But there is also the budget to think of as it can easily
become very expensive. What motivates me as acoustician is to be part of designing something
interesting with the architect. You [as acoustician] should not just have three options of an
acoustic ceiling to present the architect. You should see the possibility of combining materials
and do diﬀerent things, that works acoustically in line with the original vision of the architect.
This is the task of the acoustician. Not to sell standard solutions but to combine diﬀerent
elements.
Often you would have a meeting with the architect after looking at the design drawings, trying
to ﬁgure out what is important in this speciﬁc project, for instance, is it just price? At the
meeting, you pin down where the project is material wise. Then you can go back and do
calculations, which in most cases are necessary. Often its ODEON calculations since it is quite
easy to work with Sketch Up. But ODEON and computer simulations has its limitations,
especially in smaller rooms. The more experienced you get, the less calculations you do. After
this, you do the next meeting and adjust. Often you can be quite far from each other in
materials, and you can be met with skepticism of the acoustician’s choices as in “Is this really
necessary?”. When you think something is ﬁxed, the architect can come back to you with new
ideas. For instance, a ﬁrm partner has dictated a certain design style in the project. As an
acoustician, you hope that you get information on these changes, but it’s not always the case.

The process depends much on the architect’s skills in considering all aspects of the process
and their knowledge regarding sound.
Q2.2 What is important for architects in terms of acoustics?
In the early phases the architects cares mostly about the ﬂoor area and whether or not the
building owner gets as big usable area as possible. Most architects have a good idea of their
needs and possible solutions [with regards to acoustics]. They know absorption is needed
if the rooms should be usable for occupation. Often you merely conﬁrm their ideas, and
it’s mostly a conversation regarding materials. The geometry is ﬁxed at the point when the
acoustician is included.
If you want to argue the productivity beneﬁts of acoustics you wouldn’t do it to the architect,
but the building owner.
Q2.3 Which challenges exist, in getting architect to include more acoustic in the
early design phase?
Architects don’t necessarily have to know so much about acoustics. By being a generalist
trying to know a little about everything you will inevitable miss something. It is very diﬀerent
how architects approach acoustics. For some its very simple, and you just need an acoustic
ceiling, while for others it’s almost a magical aspect, and they are not even aware that you
can calculate the acoustic conditions. But in acoustics, many aspect has an impact and many
things can go wrong. They should not know so much about acoustics that they feel they can
do it [acoustic performance considerations] themselves. You can’t be an acoustician when
you are also an architect. Some can, but typically you are either architect or acoustician.
Typically, you end up with treating the ceiling because it’s often an unused surface.
It’s diﬃcult as an acoustician to say that architects should just do this [design acoustics
solutions] in the early phase. I don’t want to stop them, but if it’s a critical room being used
for speech or music, my opinion is that having an acoustic consultant pays oﬀ. The value will
be far greater than the small fee. On the other hand, some projects don’t need an acoustician.
For instance, in a classroom rooms, here such tool [authors simulation tool] could be useful in
the early phase. In that case, I think architect could build some experience with such a tool.
The main challenge is to change the architect’s idea of seeing regulation demands and
traditional standard solutions as limitations and get them to see new opportunities. Getting
architects to think of sustainability in terms acoustics is also important. For instance, the
eﬀect on health that poor acoustics can have if you are sitting in the same oﬃce for many
years. If you can see the acoustics as a value to the experience of a room, you can give it
an extra dimension. They don’t have to be able to design it themselves, but if they have
ambitions with regards to the complete experience of a room and not just the visual image
you use to sell the project, you’ve come a long way. Many architects already see these things,
but still many only sees architecture as a pure art form.

Q2.4 How is acoustic results is best presented to architects?
Surely, results in a color grid is useful and easy to understand, but what I think what makes

investigations on the sound ﬁeld. That could create value.

Q2.1 How is the collaborative process between you, as acoustician, and the archi-

Most often I’m included the late stages when the situation can get critical. At this point, it’s

example, from diﬀerent functions placed next to each other, or if rooms are open towards
each other. At this early stage, we can give advice to the potential consequences and how
it can be resolved. Here, no calculations are performed, so it’s mainly based on experience.
Along with the other acoustic aspects, such as dimensioning construction elements, we would

acoustic ceilings happens in the very end of the design projects.
In room acoustics, the most important thing is the materials and surfaces, which is something
that gets decided very late in the design process, even though architects would like to discuss

wall absorbent area is. In the more normal cases like oﬃces, acoustics is considered very late
and you usually go with experience and select acoustic ceilings. In some cases, architects
and consultants haven’t even considered it during the project. It’s diﬃcult to work around
having an acoustic ceiling, because you rarely have enough wall area to cover the amount of
absorption needed. Ceilings are attractive in the case that you can make them soft, which is
diﬃcult on walls.
The architect comes to me knowing they have to cover the ceiling, and knowing they have to
cover some of the walls. They then ask me how much of the walls they should cover, where I
can say “you need 8 times as much as you originally thought, and you should also change the
ceiling to fulﬁll the regulations” [laughing]. It’s not always like that, but in some cases they
severely underestimate the absorption area.
Quite often you met with a wish to create a “soul” of a room with regards to acoustics, while
us acousticians is regarded as only thinking of sound dampening. Not all of us are like that,
but it’s extremely important where you place your functions. If you have an atrium, which
can have a speciﬁc “soul” where you would like to be able to look up, you don’t want to put a
cafeteria in that space. Then it doesn’t make sense to have a reverberant room.
Since acousticians and architects have a lot of common targets like surfaces, both external
and internal, and construction elements, I ﬁnd acousticians are much more closely connected
to architects than other engineers. We have very little in common with the other technical
ﬁelds whose work is inside the walls, so our communication often goes around other engineers.

What is important is that developers also want to do the acoustic calculation for the architect,

I think many acousticians would say we should be included much earlier, but I don’t necessarily

and how relevant is this expert knowledge at this stage. This is relevant in all ﬁelds but the
acoustician is interesting as it’s a specialist that are often in the very periphery of the project
but somehow touch on almost all aspect of the project.
79
==PAGE:92==
My fear is that those that would normally contact an acoustician in an early phase would
skip that if they had such a tool. My fear is also that architects and smaller engineering ﬁrms
would use it as a documentation tool. There can a big gap between the user of the tool to the
decision-maker, whether it’s about fulﬁlling regulations or design decisions. It’s an important
thing to consider, whether this tool will create better or worse acoustics in buildings. Those
that never thinks about acoustics might think a little more, and those that already think
about it might consider it as documentation and then take a change on the accuracy.
