Abstract
This thesis focuses on the design and optimization of a shell­and­tube heat exchanger.
The primary objective was to investigate the internal processes within the tube and iden­
tify methods for enhancing heat transfer efficiency by incorporating various elements.
Initially, an exploration was conducted to examine different approaches for optimizing heat
transfer and evaluate their respective advantages and disadvantages. Subsequently, uti­
lizing the ANSYS Fluent program, a series of simulations were performed to validate the
findings from previous research and determine the most optimal configuration.
The optimization process considered multiple objectives, encompassing not only maxi­
mizing heat transfer efficiency but also accounting for factors such as pressure differen­
tials and the uniformity of flow temperature at the pipe outlet.
In addition, the optimization of the pipe’s shape has been an integral part of this project,
resulting in various pipe shapes designed to meet different specified requirements.

1
Introduction
Heat exchangers employed in heavy industrial machinery, such as ovens and similar
equipment, often suffer from sub­optimal efficiency and oversized dimensions. Shape
optimization presents a promising approach to address these challenges. Various meth­
ods can be employed to achieve this objective. One approach involves modifying the
shape of the tubes. Instead of employing straight tubes, altering their configuration can
facilitate enhanced heat exchange between the internal flow and the tube walls.
Another commonly employed technique is the incorporation of objects within the pipeline.
These objects possess different shapes and sizes but share a common objective of induc­
ing greater turbulence in the flow within the pipe. This turbulence enhancement promotes
improved heat transfer between the flowing medium and the pipe walls. Notably, many
of these inserted objects focus on imparting angular velocity, thereby generating swirl,
which further augments heat transfer efficiency.
For the purpose of this thesis, the focus is on an air­air heat exchanger, where the flowing
medium within the pipes is air. The primary aim of this thesis revolves around optimizing
the shape of the inserts through extensive simulations and analysis of the obtained results.
The project will be conducted at DTU as an integral part of my master’s thesis. This en­
deavor serves as not only my thesis for the master’s degree in mechanical engineering at
DTU but also for the master’s degree in industrial technologies at UPC. This thesis pro­
vides an opportunity to apply the knowledge I have gained throughout my years of study
at both institutions, enabling me to consolidate and enhance my skills and expertise in
engineering disciplines such as fluid mechanics and heat transfer.
Furthermore, this project offers a valuable chance for me to delve into the realm of Com­
putational Fluid Dynamics (CFD), an area that I have had limited exposure to during my
academic journey but find particularly intriguing for my future career prospects.
1.1
Problem Statement
As mentioned earlier, the project aims to enhance the heat transfer efficiency of a shell­
tube heat exchanger. Specifically, the focus will primarily be on optimizing the interior of
the tube while maintaining its straight shape. While striving for the most optimal solution,
factors such as the manufacturing process, design complexity, and other practical con­
siderations have been taken into account.
Hence, the objective is to achieve optimization while adhering to a simple and easily
installable geometry. Although, several shape optimizations on the pipe will be carried
out in order to improve the heat transfer performance. Initially, the project will be divided
into the following sections:
1. Literature Research.
2. Exploration of the swirl effect in the heat transfer on a pipe.
3. Conceptualization and design of different inserts.
4. Shape­Optimization of the pipe.

Literature Research
This section of the project encompasses the research conducted to facilitate the entire
process. The study encompasses several aspects, including an exploration of current
methods employed to enhance heat transfer. It delves into their designs and examines
the benefits each approach offers in terms of increasing heat transfer efficiency.
The research undertaken in this section aims to provide a comprehensive understanding
of the subject matter and equip the project with the necessary knowledge and tools to
tackle the complexities.
2.1
Current Techniques
This section presents an overview of various techniques employed to enhance heat trans­
fer. Among these techniques, a commonly utilized approach involves the insertion of ob­
jects within the pipe, this is also known as passive techniques. These objects induce
a swirling effect on the flow, leading to improved heat transfer. In the study [1], some
experiments were carried out regarding the swirl effect on the flux in water pipes, as a
conclusion, it can be seen how the heat transfer increases due to this swirl effect com­
pared to a laminar flow.
Furthermore, the article reaches the conclusion that heat transfer also escalates in propor­
tion to the twisted angle. In other words, as the angle of twisting increases, the amount of
heat transferred also increases. However, it is important to consider the potential draw­
back of increased pressure drop across the pipe, resulting from the presence of these
objects and the friction that the swirl effect on the flow generates due to the increase of
tangential velocity.
Additionally, both numerical and experimental evidence presented in the article [2] con­
firms that the swirled effect directly leads to an increase in heat transfer.
The other type of techniques used to increase heat transfer are known as the active tech­
niques. These techniques consist of complex procedures applied on the design system,
with external power required. The thermal performance and the efficiency of these tech­
niques are really high due to the use of external power. Some of the most common active
techniques currently used are [3]:
1. Mechanical Aids
2. Fluid vibration
3. Surface vibration
4. Electrostatic fields
5. Jet impingement
6. Injection
7. Suction
In this project, only the modification of the inflow of the pipes by adding different types
of inserts (passive techniques) will be investigated, which means techniques used to im­
prove the efficiency of the heat transfer in the outer part of the pipe, like ribs or other

2.1.1
Twisted tape inserts
In order to achieve this swirl effect on the pipe flow, twisted inserts are one of the common
ways to do it. The design involves a metal sheet wrapped around a central axis, creating a
twisted configuration. This twisting generates a swirling effect within the flow, enhancing
heat transfer. These inserts can cover the entire diameter of the pipe or only a portion of
it. However, if they do not span the entire diameter, a suitable mechanism for securing
them in place needs to be designed. It is worth noting that these inserts can be easily
removed for pipe cleaning purposes [4].
Figure 2.1: Twisted Taped Inserts with different twisted ratios [5]
[6] and [7] conclude, with some experiments carried out, these inserts increase the heat
transfer compared to a plain tube. Furthermore, the study also draws the conclusion that
a lower twisted ratio corresponds to a higher increase in heat transfer. This implies that
as the number of turns increases, generating a greater swirl effect, the heat transfer rate
intensifies accordingly. Subsequently, also the pressure drop is increased, as the friction
increases due to the increment of the tangential velocity given by the twists.
The twisted ratio of the inserts is not the only variable to consider; other factors such
as the width, placement, and length of the inserts must also be taken into account during
the design process.
In [8] the effect of twisted inserts placed separately from the wall is investigated. In this
particular case, it has been determined that the effect of the clearance ratio on the in­
crease in heat transfer is less significant compared to the twisted ratio. This finding was
taken into consideration during the simulation phase of the project. As a result, twisted
inserts that cover the entire diameter of the pipe were chosen for their ease of placement.
Another variable taken into account is the placement of the inserts. In this case, only
the inserts which do not span the whole diameter of the pipe should be considered. In [9],
some studies were conducted in order to see the influence of the placement of the twisted
insert. In this case, the working fluid was helium gas, and the results of the experiment
set that the inserts attached to the wall give a better performance in heat transfer than the
center placed inserts in a Reynolds number range of 2600 to 8760.

2.1.2
Multiple twisted inserts
Another method to improve the heat transfer on a pipe flow is the combination of more
than one twisted insert.
In [12], the investigation of the effect of 2 inserts in the pipe was carried out. In this case,
2 different configurations for the multiple twisted inserts were considered, co­swirl and
counter­swirl configurations.
The working fluid for those experiments was water, and the article concludes that insert­
ing 2 inserts instead of a single insert improves the heat transfer on the pipe. Moreover,
the counter­swirl configuration gave better results than the co­swirl configuration in heat
transfer, but also a higher value of friction.

2.1.3
Helical Screw Inserts
This consists of an insert with a helical shape introduced inside the pipe. These inserts
can be with or without a center rod. This technique is also a commonly used passive
technique to increase the overall flux performance on heat exchangers. As the twisted
inserts, these increase the turbulence on the flux buy generating the swirl on the flux,
which as mentioned before, increases the heat transfer performance.[14]
Some experiments have been carried out such as [15], where different types of helical
inserts were tested. The work flow used was air with a Reynolds number range between
2300 and 8800. In this case 3 different inserts were tested, a full­length helical tape with
centered rod, a full­length helical tape without rod and a shorter helical tape.
The findings indicate that the full­length insert with rod demonstrates the highest heat
transfer efficiency, whereas shorter inserts exhibit lower heat transfer performance. Ad­
ditionally, the experiments investigating the distance between the inserts and the walls
revealed that reducing the gap between the walls and the insert leads to increased heat
transfer.
Based on the findings presented in this section, it can be deduced that helical inserts
and twisted inserts are comparable techniques. Both methods induce a swirling effect in
the flow by twisting the inserts, resulting in similar heat transfer performance
2.1.4
Wire Coil Insert
Another technique is the use of a wire coil. This coil can give an improvement in heat
transfer due to interrupting the boundary layer near the wall, which increases the temper­
ature in the radial direction of the flow. In addition, this coil can lead to a swirl effect on the
flow, which as mentioned before, leads to a higher turbulence and as a consequence, a
higher heat transfer on the pipe. The material of the coil also can be relevant to the heat
transfer performance.
As stated in [16], the use of coils has different advantages such as easy installation, sim­
ple manufacturing and low costs, the possibility of installation in existing tubes, and no
change of mechanical strength compared to a plain tube.
In [16], the study conducted various experiments comparing the effects of aluminum and
copper coils with a plain pipe on heat transfer and pressure drop. Air was used as the flow­
ing medium for these experiments. The findings indicate that the use of coils enhances
heat transfer, with the extent depending on the number of turns employed. Essentially,
increasing the number of turns leads to higher heat transfer rates. Consistent with pre­
vious experiments, it was observed that the friction factor also increases, resulting in an

elevated pressure drop between the inlet and outlet. Furthermore, the use of copper coils
yielded superior heat transfer performance compared to aluminum coils.
The article, supported by [17] concludes that the impact of wire coils diminishes as the
Reynolds number increases, indicating that they are less effective for turbulent flows with
high Reynolds number.
2.1.5
Inner ribs effect
Inner ribs are another commonly employed passive technique in current practice. These
ribs are positioned inside the pipe inlet, introducing alterations to the internal flow. They
can vary in shape, angular orientation, length, and width. Some different experiments
have been carried out in order to see the effect of the ribs on the pipe flow. The ribs, gen­
erally create a flow disturbance which increases the turbulent kinetic energy, and there­
fore, an increase in heat transfer [18].
In [19], a series of experiments were conducted to investigate the impact of various rib
shapes on heat transfer efficiency in circular pipes. The experiments utilized airflow and
maintained a constant heat source on the pipe walls. A total of 16 different rib shapes were
tested. The results showed that all 16 rib shapes improved heat transfer compared to the
plain pipe. Among the tested rib shapes, the boot­shaped rib( Figure 2.4a) demonstrated
the highest heat transfer performance, while the reverse pentagonal rib (Figure 2.4b) ex­
hibited the lowest friction value.
(a) Boot shaped rib
(b) Reverse pentagonal shape

This results coincide with the results obtained in [18], where 3 different angles of ribs were
tested (60◦, 90◦and 120◦), and the first one gave the highest heat transfer while the last
one gave the lowest friction.
These results lead to the conclusion that rib shapes with a counter flow configuration
exhibit superior heat transfer rates, due to the creation of turbulence behind the rib which
increase the overall thermal performance.

3.1
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD)
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is the branch of fluid mechanics that uses algo­
rithms and numerical methods to analyze and solve fluid flows. The method used consists
in divide the space in control volume cells also known as finite volume control, which are
fixed in the space and solve each of the different equations for each cell in an iterative way.
Figure 3.1: Finite Volume Control
The 3 main principles used in this numerical method state the mass, the momentum and
the energy must be conserved over the time in each control volume cell [20]. These
principles consider also the following assumptions:
1. Fluid is continuum, which means the density is high enough so the motion and the
molecular structure can be ignored.
2. All the fluid properties such as location in space and time are averages of a large
number of particles within the control volume cell.
Those 3 principles applied are described below:

3.2
Shape Optimization
Shape optimization involves modifying the shape of various components to discover the
most efficient configuration, which entails defining a functional and aiming to maximize or
minimize it. Several parameters are taken into account in shape optimization, including
the component’s shape and the applied boundary conditions.
There are various approaches to design parameterization for shape optimization, such as
employing splines, Bezier curves, and other smooth interpolant methods. Frequently, the
functional being addressed relies on solving a specific partial differential equation within
the variable domain. [26]
As previously mentioned, in this particular study case, the software employed is ANSYS
Fluent. ANSYS Fluent utilizes the adjoint solver to tackle the mathematical challenges
associated with shape optimization.[27].
3.2.1
Adjoint Solver
The Adjoint Method is a specialized mathematical technique that expands the capabilities
of a Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) solution by offering comprehensive sensitivity
information regarding the performance of a fluid system under specific boundary condi­
tions. The adjoint solver is employed to calculate the derivatives of engineering quantities
with respect to system inputs, enabling the design of optimized geometries in the realm
16
Optimization of a shell­and­tube heat exchanger
==PAGE:25==
of computational design.
The adjoint optimization finds an optimal shape for a given operating condition, by derive
the optimal shape from a baseline CFD flow calculation. This method have advantages
such as the no need of parametrization of the geometry, or the low computation time.[28]
To achieve the solution, the adjoint method offers two distinct approaches: the discrete
adjoint and the continuous adjoint. These methods differ primarily in their order of op­
erations. The continuous adjoint first derives the adjoint equation and subsequently dis­
cretizes it, whereas the discrete adjoint begins with the discretization of the flow solver
and then determines the exact discretized adjoint equation. ANSYS Fluent employs the
discrete adjoint method.
The discrete adjoint method holds certain advantages over the continuous adjoint. Firstly,
it offers higher accuracy. Additionally, the differentiation can be verified using the finite dif­
ference method at both the unit function level and the system level. However, the discrete
approach has less efficency in speed and memory than the continuous approach[28].

5.1.6
Conclusions
After conducting tests with various full­span inserts, it can be concluded that they provide
a simple and effective method to enhance heat transfer in a pipe. These inserts are easy
to install as they span the entire diameter and do not require any specific design features
to hold them in place within the pipe. Additionally, as mentioned in the literature research,
they can be easily removed if the pipes need to be cleaned. Moreover, these inserts are
relatively easy to manufacture, typically consisting of a metal sheet twisted along a central
axis, making them a cost­effective solution.
Another advantage of using these inserts is that they can be employed in existing heat
exchangers, eliminating the need to install entirely new systems. Additionally, these in­

Shape optimization of the pipe
In this section of the report, various shape optimizations will be conducted using different
observables. These optimizations will be performed utilizing the adjoint solver provided
by ANSYS (subsection 3.2.1). To initiate the process, the observable needs to be defined,
following which the adjoint solver can be executed. Once the adjoint solver converges,
ANSYS’s design tool enables modifications to the pipe’s shape to maximize or minimize
the selected observable.
In the Ansys design tool, multiple parameters must be configured. The initial parame­
ter determines which surface can be modified in shape. In this specific scenario, only the
shape of the pipe wall is permitted to change, while the inlet and outlet diameters must
remain constant at a value of 28 mm.
Before modifying the geometry, the morphing method must be selected. In this instance,
Ansys provides three different morphing methods, as outlined in subsection 3.2.2. For the
pipe’s shape optimizations, only direct interpolation and radial basis functions were taken
into account. As mentioned earlier, the polynomial method yielded inconsistent results,
resulting in meshes with negative elements that could not be remeshed with the new ge­
ometry.
In addition, the boundary region must be specified, which represents the volume within
the selected surfaces can undergo surface changes. Ansys Fluent offers an option called
”get bounds” to define this boundary region, which provides a somewhat flexible region
around the chosen surfaces. In this study, the boundary region has been determined
using this tool, resulting in the following values:

6.4
Multi­objective shape optimization
The main objective of this section is to optimize the shape of the pipe to achieve a dual
outcome: enhancing heat transfer while simultaneously reducing pressure drop. As in­
dicated in the preceding section, the optimized pipes obtained exhibited either improved
heat transfer or decreased pressure drop, but none were able to achieve an overall en­
hancement in both aspects.
Nonetheless, these optimized pipes yielded valuable results and provided insights into
altering certain parameters by modifying the shape of a plain pipe.
To pursue this objective, two distinct methodologies have been employed, which are elu­
cidated in the subsequent sections.
6.4.1
Multi­objective shape optimization maximizing the ratio HT/P
In the initial phase of the multi­objective optimization, a novel observable was introduced,
known as ”ratio”. The creation of this observable involved defining two separate observ­
ables: the pressure drop and the mass­average integral of the outlet temperature. ANSYS
provides the capability to perform various mathematical operations with observables, and
in this case, a function was created by dividing the outlet temperature by the pressure
drop, resulting in the new observable named ”ratio.”
The main goal was to maximize the ratio between outlet temperature and pressure drop to
develop a pipe shape that simultaneously enhances heat transfer while minimizing pres­
sure drop.
Similar to previous cases, only the walls were permitted to be modified during this op­
timization, while the inlet and outlet remained constant. The boundary region was as­
signed the same values used in the majority of the previous optimizations, as depicted in

Conclusions
The thesis has presented various methods for enhancing heat transfer in a plain pipe.
Through testing different geometries, each approach exhibited a significant improvement
in heat transfer and temperature compared to the reference models.
The initial research focused on current methods utilized, examining various types of in­
serts, their geometries, and their respective qualities. From this investigation, it can be
deduced that full­span twisted inserts are among the most effective ways to enhance
heat transfer using passive techniques. These inserts are characterized by their ease of
manufacturing, cost­effectiveness, and simple installation in both new and existing pipes.
Additionally, they can be easily removed for cleaning purposes.
Furthermore, although not explored in this project, these inserts have the potential for fur­
ther optimization by incorporating wings on the edges or adding holes, potentially leading
to even greater enhancements. The helical screws also show promise as a viable option
with similar qualities to the twisted inserts among the explored passive techniques.
Overall, the research has provided valuable insights into improving heat transfer in plain
pipes and highlighted the potential of specific methods to be adopted for various applica­
tion.
Ansys Fluent served as the primary tool for developing the various models, resulting in
findings that align with the literature research conducted, and it has helped to validate the
effectiveness of the currently used techniques for heat transfer improvement.
Shape optimization has proven to be an effective tool for enhancing heat transfer in pipes,
as demonstrated in this report. While it can be challenging and occasionally lead to com­
plex geometries that are difficult to manufacture, using shape optimization appropriately
can yield significant improvements in performance. By employing this technique, favor­
able outcomes can be attained, making it a valuable approach for optimizing heat transfer
in practical engineering applications.
In conclusion, this master’s thesis has provided valuable insights into various heat trans­
fer and pressure drop optimization techniques for pipes. The results and findings can
serve as a foundation for the development of innovative and efficient solutions for diverse
engineering applications.
For future work, it is recommended to define additional constraints regarding the desired
heat transfer rate or the maximum allowable pressure drop. This will enable more mean­
ingful comparisons between different results, as having clear limitations is crucial.
Additionally, exploring other current passive techniques, such as helical screws or shape­
optimizing the insert, could provide valuable insights and potential improvements to the
system’s performance.
Moreover, investigating the combination of different techniques could offer a deeper un­
derstanding of how the system behaves and potentially lead to synergistic effects that
enhance overall performance. This approach could pave the way for innovative and op­
timized solutions for specific heat transfer and pressure drop requirements.
