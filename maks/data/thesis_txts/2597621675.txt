Abstract
The purpose of this study is to investigate the potential for applying Deep Learning models
to Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 satellite images for urban development. We investigate the
stability of the models, if there is a seasonal variation, and explore how the model can be
used to gain insights into urban development. Method-wise, the project is split into two
parts. The first part deals with training a model on the So2Sat dataset, and the second
part deal with acquiring new data, applying the trained models, and analyzing the results.
The focus areas in the second part were Tete and Maputo in Mozambique. In the first
part, it was seen that the models generally are not able to get very high accuracy, with the
best models achieving below 60 % accuracy. Sentinel-2 based classifiers achieved much
better accuracy than Sentinel-1 classifiers, which were around 40 %. It was found out, that
there was quite some instability and seasonal effect, especially when using only Sentinel-
2 data. Here it became clear, that choosing in which month the data was acquired, had
a big impact on the results. It was shown, that using Sentinel-2 generally resulted in
the highest classification quality. For the focus areas, data were acquired from 2016-
2019, and the results from these years were compared. It was shown, that the models
suggested that both Tete and Maputo were growing in size. A problem here was, that
since the models were quite unstable, it was hard to evaluate if differences from year to
year were reflections of true developments or just classification noise.

Introduction
In many places around the world, particularly in developing countries, there is very lit-
tle knowledge about how cities are developing, such as where the city starts and stops,
which areas are changing, and where newly built areas are popping up. Having access to
knowledge about city boundaries could be beneficial in many ways: It can provide infor-
mation about population growth and can assist in city planning, which is crucial in many
developing countries where cities are developing quickly. This work will explore the pos-
sibility of using Machine Learning (ML) models, particularly Deep Learning (DL) models,
applied on satellite imagery for urban development studies.
To do this, DL classification models will be trained exclusively on the So2Sat dataset,
which contains satellite images that are labeled using the Local Climate Zones (LCZ) sys-
tem. The LCZ system is a framework for characterizing urban surface cover. A model
that is capable of automatically classifying urban regions according to the LCZ system can
thus provide significant information for various applications, such as infrastructure plan-
ning and population assessment [1]. It is intended, that the models should be globally
applicable.
The focus areas in this work are two cities in Mozambique: Tete, a smaller city, and Ma-
puto, the capital of the country. Applying the DL models, trained on the So2Sat dataset,
to each city yields a map where all pixels are classified into one of the LCZ classes. By
creating maps for each year, we can make timelines, which describe certain aspects of
the city’s development, such as built-up area, compactness, etc. It will be investigated if
the results display a seasonal variation, and how stable the classification results are.
The TAgged Structures In Mozambique (TASIM) dataset contains geolocations of tagged
structures in Tete. Each tag is annotated with a label, where the categories function as
a proxy for income level. The dataset was tagged in 2016, so it can be used for partially
validating the classification results in 2016. If the LCZ classes display the ability to sep-
arate the TASIM categories, it could also mean that the LCZ classes can function as a
proxy for income level in Mozambique.
This project will also investigate models that are trained on both Sentinel-1 data and
Sentinel-2 data. The intention here is to ascertain what pros and cons each model has,
and what trade-offs there can be when choosing a model, and if they can potentially be
used in conjunction.
In [2] and [3], studies are presented, where they use the LCZ system to classify Colombo
in Sri Lanka and Guangzhou in China, and explore how it can be used to study urban
development and planning. However, both papers rely on manual classification, wherein
this project, we are exploring the potential for DL based automatic approaches. In [4],
a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) is trained on a multi-temporal set of labeled images.
Here a relatively good accuracy is achieved. However, the dataset is only from European
cities, so it does not show if the model will generalize well to cities across the world. Cre-
ating large-scale LCZ maps remains a challenge because of large intra-class variability
in spectral signatures resulting from regional differences in materials and vegetation etc.,
according to [4]. In [1], DL models are applied to the So2Sat dataset to achieve state of
the art performance. The best achieved performance is less than 60 %, which indicates
that it is quite difficult for the models to generalize well. After training the models, they are
used to create LCZ maps of Munich in Germany, Nairobi in Kenya, and Henan in China.
The paper does not investigate, how the results can be affected by seasonal variation, or

Datasets
This work is based on data from the two ESA satellites, Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2. Imagery
of cities with class labels has been collected in the So2Sat Dataset, where Sentinel-1 and

S1 has 4 different data acquisition modes:
1. Stripmap (SM)
2. Interferometric Wide swath mode (IW)
3. Extra Wide swath mode (EW)
4. Wave (WV)
The main mode over land is IW and the one that we are concerned with in this work.
In this mode, the instrument acquires data at a 250 km swath at a 5 m by 20 m spatial

parts of the signal. SLC products can include dual polarizations, in this project VV and
VH polarizations were used. VV means transmitting in vertical and receiving in vertical
polarization. VH means transmitting in vertical and receiving in horizontal polarization.
Dual polarization can be useful because some materials will scatter differently in different
polarizations, and therefore encode more information than only using a single polarization.
SLC products are not very easy to interpret with the naked eye, so it is interesting to see
if a DL model can extract something meaningful from this data product.

So2Sat Dataset
The So2Sat dataset is a dataset consisting of patches of satellite imagery from various
cities around the world, with each patch being 32x32 pixels, corresponding to a physical
size of 320 m by 320 m. Each patch is labeled into one of the 17 LCZ classes. The satel-
lites used are Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2. For S1 the data type is Single Look Complex
(SLC), with VV and VH polarizations. Both real and imaginary components, Lee filtered
Intensity, for both polarization, and two additional bands 1 are included. This results in 8
S1 bands. For S2, bands 2,3,4,5,6,7,8,8a, 11, and 12 are included which results in 10 S2
bands. The data type is Level 1C (L1C), which is Top-Of-Atmosphere (TOA), orthorec-
tified, but with no atmospheric correction. The bands that have a resolution larger than
10 m Ground Sampling Distance (GSD), are upsampled to 10 m. The data format for S2
is not specified in the So2Sat article [13], but was found out by email correspondence
with the So2Sat team. According to them, it should not matter whether one uses L1C or
L2A data, because the models are learning the non-linear relationship between S2 data
and LCZ classes. It might be that the presence of different atmospheric conditions in
different geographic regions, can lead to an increase in dataset bias. However, maybe
atmospheric correction is not good enough to really resolve this issue. Another reason for
not using L2A data is that there was little availability of this data type when they started the
So2Sat project, so they would have to produce it themselves, using ESA’s SNAP toolbox.
Given that they wanted to use Google Earth Engine, this was not viable.
With both S1 and S2 data, this results in a total of 18 bands for each patch.
The So2Sat paper [13] provides this quantitative description of the classes shown in figure
2.13. Many of these classes are on a spectrum, rather than being completely different.
For example, an area of open low-rise can transition into compact low-rise, simply by
increasing the density of buildings slightly. In fact, it could be possible for two areas that
are both classified as open low-rise to be more different, than two areas where one is
classified as open low-rise and the other is classified as compact low-rise.

The full So2Sat dataset includes data from 52 cities distributed across the entire world.
It is split into training, validation, and test sets. The training set includes all patches from
42 cities, and the validation and test set consists of different areas of the remaining 10
cities. This means that the training set is geographically separated from the test and
the validation set. This is quite important because it might be difficult for a DL model
to generalize to data from regions that it has not seen any data from, which will most
likely reduce the test set accuracy. An experiment will be carried out, where the sets are
shuffled, to reveal how large the effect of the separated sets is. It should also be noted,
that only 10 cities in the test set is actually not that many, and it is, therefore, difficult to
say, that the performance achieved on this test set is valid for all regions in the entire
world.
Finally, it should be noted that all the patches are homogeneous. This means, that each
patch contains only one LCZ class. However, when applying a DL model to real-world
data, it will necessarily have to see patches that are not homogeneous. For example,
there might be a patch that contains a bit of compact low-rise and a bit of low plants. It is
then not clear what a model that is only trained on homogeneous patches will do.

Deep Learning theory
Computer Vision is a field where algorithms and models are developed for a computer to
be able to extract meaningful information from images. It’s a field that encompasses many
types of algorithms, but a very powerful one and one that this report focuses solely on is
Deep Learning (DL). Deep Learning is the application of Deep Artificial Neural Networks
(D-ANN’s).
3.1
Fully Connected network
The Fully Connected (FC) network is the simplest type of Neural Networks. It consists of
an input layer, several hidden layers, and finally an output layer. The idea with the neural
network is to approximate a function that maps the input data to a desirable output. For
example, the input data could be the height and weight of an animal, and the output could
be whether the animal is a cat or a dog.

With Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN’s) we use weight sharing and take advantage
of spatial features in an image. In CNN’s, each layer consists of a set of spatial filters,
that we convolve with the output from the previous layer. The CNN can be viewed as a
series of filters for feature extraction where the filter elements are learnable parameters,
optimized via the backpropagation algorithm [16].

Figure 3.2 illustrates a new 2D convolution operation. The kernel position is restricted to
be fully within the input. This means that the output will be smaller than the input, for any
kernel larger than (1x1). In this case, the output will be (2x3). We can increase the output
size by padding the input. We can also use stride making the output smaller.
In a CNN, in each layer, there can be any number of filters, with each filter yielding one
output channel. After convolution, the outputs from each filter are stacked. So for exam-
ple, a layer with 20 filters will yield an output with 20 channels. If the input to the layer
has more than one channel, the depth dimension of each filter has to match, such that the
dimension of the filter is (HxWxC), where C is the number of input channels. So for ex-
ample, if the input has 20 channels, each filter needs to have a dimension of (HxWx20).
After the convolution operation, the values of the channels are mapped through a non-
linear activation function.
Another very common operator in CNN’s is the pooling layer. The pooling layer replaces
a region of a channel with some kind of summary statistic of this region, reducing the
channel in size. An example of a pooling operation is the maxpool, which replaces a
rectangular region of the channel with the max value of that region.
Figure 3.3 shows an example of a layer in a CNN. This particular layer includes a convolu-
tion operation, then a mapping through an activation function, and finally a pooling stage.
The output from this layer becomes the input for the next layer. Not all layers need to
have the same components. We can have many layers after each other all with different
components, kernel sizes, pooling types, etc. A CNN usually ends with a fully connected
network, to the output layer.

As explained the layers in the CNN can be different and there can be many layers. This
leads to different architectures. The architecture of a network describes the number of

layers and the components in each layer. A particularly successful and famous architec-
ture is the Residual Network (ResNet) [18]. A ResNet is built up of several blocks, with
the input to the network passing sequentially through the blocks. Each block can contain
several operations, such as convolutions, poolings, etc. A block may also have several
convolutional layers. For each block in the ResNet, there is also a skip connection, also
called identity mapping. The skip connection allows the input to the block to bypass that
block. The output from the block and the skip connection are rejoined after the block by
a summation. The network may contain several blocks, where some blocks might have
the same parameters (not weight sharing).
Figure 3.4 shows a block from a ResNet architecture. The branch that is not the skip
connection is called the residual branch. The idea here is that we try to make the model
fit the mapping F(x) = H(x) −x, where H(x) is the desired mapping. We add x to the
output such that if the model is fitting the mapping F(x) the output from the layer will be
the desired mapping F(x) + x = H(x). According to [18] this makes the model easier to
optimize because it alleviates the vanishing gradient problem.

4.1.6
Implementation
The PyTorch framework for Python was used. This framework allows the user to easily
create DL models with different architectures, and train them. The framework contains a
standard library of models, from where the ResNet and ResNeXt models were sourced.
A downside to these models is that they do not allow image input sizes smaller than
(224x224). Given that the So2Sat dataset contains images that are only (32x32), this
resulted in a lot of upsampling, which introduces massive computational cost, with no
performance gains. A better way would be to rewrite the models to work with smaller
input sizes, but that was not done. These models also only take inputs with 3 channels.
To use more input channels, the filters in the input layer was simply increased to match
the number of channels in the input image. If one wishes to use pre-trained weights,
the weights from the original filter can be copied to the new larger filter. However, since
the model is pre-trained on RGB images from the ImageNet dataset, it is questionable
whether it would have any benefit when using non-RGB bands or radar imagery.
For SE-ResNet and SS-ResNet, an additional library, written in PyTorch was used. This
is called ”pytorchcv Image classification and segmentation models for PyTorch” and is
available through PyPi [22] or from Github. Besides implementing a host of useful mod-
els, it also allows for smaller input sizes. The models can be loaded using PyTorchCV,
with the library’s get_model module. With this module, the user can choose which of
the available models he wants to load. Here the name for the SE-ResNeXt model is
”seresnext101_32x4d” and ”shakeshakeresnet20_2x16d_cifar10” for SS-ResNet. While
the name for SS-ResNet implies that it is pre-trained on the CIFAR-10 dataset, the pre-
trained weights are not used, so it doesn’t matter which pretraining is selected.
For loss function, Cross-Entropy Loss was used. For optimizer Adam was used. The
Adam optimizer is a method for Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD), which has an adap-
tive way of setting the learning rate. Both of these are implemented in the standard Py-
Torch framework.
The models are trained on DTU’s HPC system. Here the user has access to computation
on CPU’s and GPU’s.

Dataset balancing
The So2Sat dataset is highly unbalanced, in both training, validation, and test splits, which
can be seen in figure 4.1. When the training set has some very strong biases like this, the
model will learn those biases, which is generally not desirable. In this case, the validation
set is actually somewhat similarly balanced, so if we were mainly interested in getting
a high overall accuracy score, keeping the training set unbalanced might actually help.
However, we want the model to do well in all classes and for that reason, we need the
training set to be balanced. We also choose to use a balanced accuracy score, rather
than an overall accuracy score, such that we get a fair score of the model’s performance
on all classes.
The training set can be balanced in several ways. The underrepresented classes can be
oversampled, the overrepresented classes can be undersampled and both oversampling

4.3
Shuffling training and validation splits
An important feature of the So2Sat data set is that training data and test data are geo-
graphically separated. That means that the training set contains data from a set of cities
T and the test and validation comes from another set of cities V , with T and V being
disjoint. This asks the question of how much this separation affects the model’s ability
to generalize to the test set. To investigate this, new training and validation splits were
made. The new splits are made, such that the training contains a part of the original train-
ing set and a part of the original validation set. The same goes for the new test set. The
new sets are made, such that they have the same size as the original sets. The new sets
both contain 94% from the original training set, and 6% from the original validation set,
which corresponds to the ratio of training to validation samples in the original dataset.

4.4
Data normalization
Optimizing DL models can be easier if the data is normalized, especially if different vari-
ables have different means and variances. This is the case in this project, where S1 and
S2 data, as well as different S2 bands, have different means and variances. In this work,
the mean and variance of each variable have been calculated across the entire dataset.
When a data example is fed to the model, we first subtract the mean and divide by the
standard deviation.
4.5
Data augmentation
Data augmentation is a technique where we increase the training set size, by creating new
data examples from already existing ones. A few ways of doing this is to rotate, flip, or crop
the images. Augmentation functions are already implemented in the PyTorch framework,
however, they don’t work for images with more than 3 channels. For that reason, I had to
implement my own augmentations. Only rotation and flipping were implemented. Rotation
is implemented by rotating the image by a random number of degrees. Rotation was only
applied to S2 data. Unfortunately, there was not enough time to analyze the effects of
data augmentation, and therefore the results from training using data augmentation are
not included in this report.
4.6

For most of the classifiers based on S2 data, we used all 10 bands. In some experiments,
only a subset of the bands was used, to see if that could increase the performance of the
model. Selecting the optimal subset of bands is not an easy task. Preferably, one could
try all combinations, but this would lead to lengthy training time. Another strategy is to
use forward or backward feature selection. This, however, is also very time-consuming.
Instead, some hypothesis is needed to choose a subset of the bands. For example, it is
well known that blue light is strongly scattered by water vapor, and thus might be more
affected by atmospheric changes. So a hypothesis here could be, that excluding the blue
band could increase the model performance because it reduces the effect of atmospheric
changes. However, it might just be that the blue band is important for classification and
that we might see no increase or even a decrease in model performance. In total 6 models

5
Experimental setup
This chapter describes the experimental setup. This includes strategies for acquiring S1
and S2 data, methods for applying DL models to this data, using the TASIM dataset, and
metrics defined for model stability assessment.
In this project, the trained models had to be applied to the focus areas. For this, we had
to acquire Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 data and preprocess these in the same way as the
So2Sat dataset has been processed. Both S1 and S2 have several different data formats.
For S1, Single Look Complex (SLC) was used and for S2 Level-1C (L1C) was used. L1C
refers to Top-Of-Atmosphere (TOA) data, meaning that there has not been performed any
atmospheric correction. The reason for this is discussed in chapter 2.5.
After data has been acquired, the trained DL models have to be applied to said data. To
do this, the data has to be split into patches, that are the same size as the patches in the
So2Sat dataset. Then each patch has to be fed to the model and the output saved for
further analysis.
The TASIM dataset needs to rasterized. This is because it is simply a CSV file containing
geolocation of tags. This CSV file needs to be converted to an array, which can be directly
compared to results from the DL models.
Finally, some metrics need to be defined. These metrics are needed to evaluate how the
results change during the season, and assess the stability of the model.
5.1
Acquisition and preprocessing of Sentinel-2 data
During this project, four different methods for acquiring and processing Sentinel-2 data
were developed. They were developed iteratively, with each iteration attempting to alle-
viate problems with the prior methods. The first method used the SentinelSat API [24] to
download raw L1C images, and then ESA SNAP toolbox [25] to preprocess the images.
However, this method was more complicated, tedious, and very slow, with many products
either being offline or throwing internal server errors. For that reason, this method was
scrapped and only three methods were used. In these methods, Google Earth Engine
(GEE) was used. In GEE it is possible to collect, preprocess, and download S2 images,
with all the processing happening on Google’s servers. GEE has an online code editor,
where it is possible to write code in JavaScript [26]. In GEE three data collection methods
were implemented.
5.1.1
Method 1: Monthly cloudfree mosaics
The first one creates a single mosaic with the least possible amount of clouds for each
month. This method was developed in [27], where they also made their code available,
and thus no development had to be done. The algorithm collects all the available data
for the given month and computes a quality score for each pixel. This quality score is
an indicator of the likelihood that this pixel contains clouds. Afterward, a mosaic is made
which uses the highest quality pixels available and the image is resampled to 10m Ground
Sampling Distance (GSD). A more detailed description of this algorithm can be found in
[27]. This method was used to create 1 cloud-free image for each month of every year
from 2016 to 2019. This was sufficient for evaluating the monthly variation. A problem
with this method is that it only gives one image for each month. It was hypothesized that
having multiple images and averaging the results from each image would result in better
Applying Machine Learning on Satellite Data for Urban Development Studies
34
==PAGE:46==
and more stable results. Since this method only creates one image for each month, it
might be discarding a lot of valuable data.
5.1.2
Method 2: All monthly data
With the second method, we wanted to be able to acquire several images for the same
month. The reason for this is twofold: Firstly, we wanted to evaluate the intra-monthly
variation and secondly, we wanted to average several images for the same month, to get
a more robust classification. Therefore, a second method was devised, which downloads
all images for a month and Region Of Interest (ROI) with a cloud coverage less than some
threshold, which was set to 5%. The high-level workflow of this method is as follows:
1. Collect S2 L1C images
2. Filter on dates, TileID and ROI
3. Compute the cloud cover fraction within the ROI
4. Filter on cloud cover fraction
All data was as a final step resampled to 10 meter GSD.
The problem with this method is that we won’t always be able to get images from the
same months for each year because cloud cover changes from year to year. This can
make it difficult to evaluate the changes from year to year because one might be forced to
compare data from different months. Another problem is that the method for computing
the cloud coverage seems to not always work, and therefore some images have a lot of
clouds in them.
5.1.3
Method 3: Monthly cloudfree mosaics trisplit
The third method is similar to method one, with the only difference being, that we create
three mosaics per month instead of one. This method was devised to try and alleviate
some of the problems that occurred with method two. The problem with method 2 is, that
the availability of low cloud data differs from year to year. For example, in 2016 there
might be data from April, but in 2017 there is no data from April. If you then want to
compare the two years, you have to look at data from different months. Given that there
can be seasonal variation, this makes it hard to make the comparison. With method three,
we ensure that there are 3 images from each month. Of course, in some cases, there is
simply not enough data to create 3 cloud-free mosaics, so this does not solve the problem
entirely. The three mosaics are created by splitting each month into 3 parts that are each
10 days long (one part will be 11 days in months with 31 days). As with the other two
methods, the final images are resampled to 10 m GSD. This method was only used for
acquiring data for Maputo, because of time constraints.

4. Polarimetric Speckle Reduction

Step 4 was only applied to the intensity images, and given that they were not used for
training the models, this step was omitted. The processing is very memory intensive, so it
was not possible to process an entire image at once. SNAP has a function for subsetting

area. In the collocation, an S2 image of the desired region from GEE was used as master
and the S1 image as slave. The result of the entire processing is a file containing 4
S1 bands (Real and Complex bands for VH and VV polarizations), and all 13 S2 bands
collocated to the same region.

5.3
Creating LCZ maps
This section will describe the method for creating LCZ maps. An LCZ map is the resulting
array of applying the DL model to a satellite image. Each pixel will hold a value that
represents which LCZ class a patch around the pixel is classified as. When referring to
LCZ maps or simply maps, this is what is being referred to.
Maps are created by applying the trained DL models to the acquired S1 and S2 images.
When referring to S1 classifier or S2 classifier, it refers to a classifier that only uses S1
data and a classifier that only uses S2 data, respectively. Only one S1 classifier and one
S2 classifier has been used. The performance of these two classifiers on the So2Sat data
will be described in section 6.6.
A sliding window is implemented and the patch within the window is fed to the classifier.
For each patch, the model outputs the probabilities for that patch belonging to any of the 17
LCZ classes and the argmax of the probabilities which is the prediction. The classification
result is assigned to the center pixel of the patch. The resulting map is then saved in
2 arrays: one (HxW) array, Cpreds containing the predictions for each pixel, and one
(HxWx17) array, Cprobs containing the posterior probability of each class for each pixel.

5.4
TASIM rasterization method
To utilize the TASIM dataset, we need to rasterize it. This means taking the CSV file which
contains latitude, longitude, and category for each tag, and putting it into a 3-dimensional
array. The height and width of the array will correspond to the spatial extent of the maps
covering Tete. The third dimension will correspond to the number of TASIM categories,
which is 7. In the resulting TASIM raster, each pixel Si,j,c will contain the number of tags
in category c, in a (32x32) patch around the position (i, j). The reason for using a patch
size of (32x32) is that this is the size of the patches fed to the DL model, and the TASIM
raster can then be directly compared to the LCZ maps.
To do this rasterization, we first need to convert the coordinates. The TASIM tags are
in latitude and longitude, but we want to convert this into row and column indices in the
Coordinate Reference System (CRS) of the map covering Tete. To do this, we utilize the
Python PyProj library and the Rasterio library. PyProj converts the coordinates from the
TASIM CRS to the map’s local CRS. The rasterio.transform module then transforms these
local CRS coordinates into row and column indices. After transforming the coordinates we
can start the rasterization. To do this we initialize a 3-dimensional array as described. For
each pixel in this array, we search for tags in a (32x32) neighborhood around this pixel.
We then sum the number of tags in each category in each class, and assign the sums
to the array, such that Si,j,c contains the number of tags in category c in a patch around
(i, j). Since the TASIM tags are transformed into the coordinate system of the LCZ map,
the row and column indices (i, j) corresponds to the same physical location in both the
TASIM raster and the LCZ map. This rasterization process in itself is quite slow because
for each pixel we are searching through the entire CSV file. However, it only needs to be
done once, and afterward, we can analyze the results quickly using array indexing and
slicing.

7.1.4.1
Synthetic data experiment
An experiment was made, where some semi-synthetic data was generated. A (32x32)
patch of natural data and an (8x8) patch of compact low-rise was cropped out. The com-
pact low-rise patch was then inserted into the natural patch in different locations until the
whole patch was filled up with compact low-rise. At each step, the patch was fed to the
model, and the posterior probabilities saved.

Conclusion
In this project, we have explored the potential for using DL models for urban development
studies. Several experiments have been carried out, and the results analyzed.
It is a difficult task for a DL model to perform well on the So2Sat dataset. Using an S2
classifier gives validation accuracies below 60 % and using an S1 classifier gives valida-
tion accuracy of about 40 %, which shows that it is significantly more difficult to model
S1 data. For both S1 and S2, it is also shown that there is more confusion among built
classes than natural classes. Given that distinguishing between built classes is more valu-
able than natural classes, effectively the accuracy is even lower. There are also some
built classes which are strongly confused. Potentially, grouping classes into larger super-
classes might be a more useful approach. In one experiment, new training and validation
splits were made, such that they are no longer geographically separated. When a model
was trained and tested on these new splits, it was seen that the validation accuracy signif-
icantly increased, which supports the hypothesis, that it is hard for the model to generalize
to regions it has not seen before.
An S1 and an S2 model were trained on the So2Sat dataset and applied to data from Tete.
Here it was seen, that the results could vary depending on when the data was acquired.
For the S2 classifier, there was a large variation between months, in the area classified as
built. When plotted against the relative humidity, it could be seen that there was a trend
that data acquired in the more humid months resulted in more area classified as built.
When compared to the TASIM dataset, it was seen that results from the S2 classifier
fared better. The number of tags per patch generally corresponded to the Surface Frac-
tion Cover of the LCZ classes. This correspondence was much worse when using the S1
classifier. It was also shown, that the S1 classifier was worse at classifying the entire city
as built, covering only 76.1 % of the TASIM tags, versus 94.7 % for the S2 classifier. Both
S1 and S2 showed a similar trend in the development of the city from 2016-2019, that the
built area was increasing. However, the S2 classifier had higher uncertainty than the S1
classifier. These results could suggest, that using the S2 classifier is better for getting a
more detailed and meaningful overview of a city and for developments over many years,
while the S1 classifier is better when we wish to assess development over shorter periods
of a few years.
A single S2 model, trained on the So2Sat data, was applied to data from Maputo. Here
we were able to assess the stability of results for each month, and it was shown that
some months were indeed more stable than others. As discussed, this high stability is
important when we are comparing results for different years, and knowing which seasons
yield stable results is therefore beneficial. A trend was seen that the built area was in-
creasing from 2016-2019, similar to what was seen in Tete. However, there was again a
large uncertainty. The difference in LCZ class distributions for the total area and the CBD
area was assessed. Here it was seen that the CBD area contained significantly larger
proportions of mid-rise and high-rise, which are structures commonly associated with a
CBD.
