This thesis covers the analysis, design and test of a system for fast electrochemical 
impedance spectroscopy (EIS). The fast EIS system is designed for measurement of 
impedance in Li-ion cells in the frequency range 10 𝑚Hz −10 𝑘Hz with a relative 
uncertainty of 100 𝜇Ω.  
Related work is investigated with the purpose of establishing the current state of 
research for fast EIS and to gain insight into existing solutions. Additionally, the 
literature is reviewed to find common evaluation metric types and typical evaluation 
metric values for existing fast EIS measurement systems.  
Different methods used for EIS are described and establishes a theoretical basis for 
calculating the required noise levels in EIS measurement circuitry for obtaining 
measurements with a given impedance uncertainty. Practical considerations are 
made, explaining some of the explored system topologies and presenting arguments 
for the chosen circuit topologies. Considerations are made to accommodate on-line 
measurement of impedance. 
The implemented system described in this thesis is tested on an NCR18650 Li-ion 
cell. The tests yield performance metrics with values close to those of existing 
solutions. The system is scalable and is designed for the concurrent measurement of 
up to four cells, although the system has only been tested for the measurement of one 
cell.  
Although the implemented system achieves a repeatable 3𝜎 impedance uncertainty of 
12,3 𝜇Ω in some frequency ranges, systematical errors in the measurements mean 
that the impedance uncertainty in absolute terms is much higher. Based on 
electrochemical cell simulations, it is shown that the obtained impedance uncertainty 
is a systematical error which arises from windowing effects. Using the simulation, it is 
shown that measurements can be improved by using a longer measurement duration.

Electric vehicle (EV) sales are rapidly growing globally. Three million EV’s were sold 
globally in 2020 and 13 million EV’s were sold globally in 2023 [1]. With the dramatic 
increase in the sales and usage of EV’s in recent years, many EV’s are on the road. 
With a great number of EV’s on the road, there is potential for small improvements to 
make great impact.  
Important environmental considerations for the current battery technology are ethics 
and resource usage concerns involved in the manufacture of Li-ion batteries. It is 
estimated that the manufacture of Li-ion batteries for EV’s results in emissions ranging

Proper usage and state-of-health estimation of Li-ion batteries helps in reducing the 
wear on batteries and improving on energy efficiency in EV-applications and many 
other applications. This consequently leads to a reduction in Li-ion battery 
consumption due to extended battery life cycles and reduces CO2 emissions from their 
use. 
A popular and proven method for battery state estimation is electrochemical 
impedance spectroscopy (EIS) [4]. EIS can be used for non-intrusive estimation of 
state-of-charge (SOC), state-of-health (SOH) and physical parameters in 
electrochemical cells. EIS involves exciting a battery/cell using either a voltage or a 
current and measuring the consequent current or voltage response. From this 
response, SOC or SOH may be estimated.

Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) is a technique used to study the 
dynamic electrical characteristics of electrochemical processes [5, Ch. 1]. EIS is used, 
as it can provide insights into the physical and chemical properties of an 
electrochemical process in a non-intrusive way. EIS has a wide range of applications, 
including the study of interfacial processes, determination of a chemical solution 
resistance, the study of geometric effects in porous electrodes, detailed 
characterization of electrochemical cells / batteries and much more [5, Ch. 1]. 
The electrochemical processes studied with EIS are dynamic in time. For example, 
when an electric battery is charged, a redox reaction takes place inside the battery 
which converts the electrical energy and stores the as chemical energy [6, Ch. 3]. The 
process of charging takes time, and when adding more power, the charging process 
happens faster. How exactly a given battery responds to an electrical stimulus 
depends on the electrochemical processes which take place inside it. Conversely, the 
electrochemical processes inside a battery can, to some extent, be characterised 
based on its response to an electrical stimulus. This is the premise of EIS. 
The specifics of the response of an electrochemical cell to an electric stimulus can be 
described by means of analysis of the electrochemical reactions and physical 
phenomena in the cell. In this section, a brief overview of electrochemical processes 
and cell dynamics for Li-ion cells are covered. It is assumed that the reader has basic 
knowledge about electrochemical cells. 
The electrochemical processes in an electrochemical cell can be described with 
nonlinear differential equations based on physical cell parameters like electrode 
surface area, electrode geometry, current density, oxidation and reduction species 
surface concentrations and many other [5, Ch. 4].  The derivation and explanations of 
these differential equations are left out of this work as they are not at the core of the 
learning objectives. In EIS literature, it is common to model the impedance spectrum 
of an electrochemical cell using an equivalent small-signal linear circuit model. This 
circuit model contains resistors and capacitors which are used to imitate the behaviour 
of the dynamics associated with electrochemical processes [5, Ch. 4]. By linearising 
the differential equations governing the electrochemical processes, these linear circuit 
model equivalents can be obtained. It follows that the linear circuit models approximate 
the real system and can be inaccurate for large perturbations or time scales. 
Five phenomena are considered in this description of equivalent linear circuit models 
a) Inductance 
b) Electrolytic resistance 
c) Double layer capacitance 
d) Charge transfer resistance 
e) Mass transfer resistance 
The inductance is the inductance of the cell resulting from its size or geometry. The 
electrolytic resistance is the resistance of the electrolyte. The double layer capacitance

the electrolyte solution to the electrode surface is hindered by diffusion rates through

•

The properties of the type of excitation source and current sense method depends on 
the application and is explained in further detail in the coming sections. 
 
There exist many ways to measure impedance [5, Ch. 3]. For an application like for 
example Li-ion cells in EV’s, it is desirable to make the impedance measurement 
system able to perform its measurement while not removing the DUT from the 
application during “normal” use. Such a measurement system is called an online EIS 
system. Online EIS systems also exist in different forms and their measurement 
topology can be divided into two major categories [5, Ch. 3]

The feasibility of these methods for online EIS depends on the application. 
Traditionally, laboratory equipment for EIS of electrochemical cells uses potentiostats 
[11], however, different approaches exist which use both potentiostatic and 
galvanostatic methods for online EIS. These different approaches are covered further 
in the literature review in “Related work and common methods”. 
 
The choice of excitation signals in EIS influences the measurements. Different types 
of excitation signals have different properties and is chosen based on the application. 
They can be grouped into two overall types 
• 
Narrow bandwidth 
• 
Broadband 
Narrow bandwidth excitation signals generally provide the most accurate impedance 
measurement of the three types [9, Ch. 1]. It usually involves a stepped-frequency sine 
wave and measures the impedance of the cell at one frequency at a time [9, Ch. 2]. 
Chirps are also used. This method is also the slowest of the three.  
For the EIS system shown in Figure 4, the DUT can be excited with a sinusoidal 
voltage or current. Using the ADC’s, the DUT impedance may be found by dividing the 
phasors of the resulting measured cell voltage and current

where a shorter measurement time is desirable. By using a broadband signal, more 
than one frequency is measured at a time [9, Ch. 1]. However, the larger bandwidth 
gives rise to a larger RMS excitation magnitude or, if the magnitude is kept low, a 
poorer signal to noise ratio (SNR) [5, Ch. 3]. Standard broadband excitation signals 
are step functions, multisine, pseudorandom binary sequences (PRBS) and different

The AC cell voltage in an EIS application is often in the order of millivolts [5, Ch. 3]. 
This small amplitude (compared to the cell DC voltage) is deliberately chosen due to 
fact that the impedance of the DUT is a nonlinear function of current and state of 
charge (SOC). EIS theory is based on linear dynamical systems [5, Ch. 1], and 
therefore the excitation current must be kept low, resulting in a small AC cell voltage.  
For obtaining measurements with high accuracy, it follows that the voltage sense 
amplifier must have a low noise level. For getting the best performance out of the 
ADC’s, a high amplifier gain is also required to take advantage of the entire input range 
of the ADC. 
A low noise current sense amplifier is also required for high accuracy impedance 
measurements since current measurement uncertainty also affects the uncertainty of 
the calculated impedance. 
 
Digital processing aids are used for the acquisition of the analog voltage and current 
signals and consequent data processing. Methods involving broadband excitation 
signals typically require more data processing, using for example the FFT or wavelet 
transform [5, Ch. 3], [9, Ch. 2], making an MCU or PC necessary. 
 
When using broadband methods, evaluating impedance is more involved than for 
single-sine / narrowband methods. Typical methods involve sampling voltages and 
currents and transforming them to the frequency domain using wavelet transforms or 
the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) [12] [9, Ch. 2]. In the frequency domain, the 
impedance may be calculated.

Impedance 
The different frequency-domain metrics used for comparison with a reference 
impedance identified in the articles are 
1. NRMSE. Normalised Root Mean Square Error 
2. RMSE. Root mean square error 
3. Average relative error of the absolute value. 
4. Maximum relative error

NRMSE provides a useful relative indication of impedance accuracy. Compared to 
RMSE, NRMSE has its strength in comparing impedance accuracy across different 
measurement scenarios due to its normalization. As an example: An impedance 
measurement system obtains an RMSE of 1 𝑚Ω. This result can be accurate or 
inaccurate depending on what the absolute impedance is.  If the absolute impedance 
is 1 Ω, this means an accuracy of 0,1 %, whereas an absolute impedance of 10 𝑚Ω 
means an accuracy of 10 %. If no further information about the impedance is given, 
RMSE is not as useful. The weakness of NRMSE is that the normalization is not 
consistently defined across literature. It is common to see normalizations made with

In Figure 6, the top-level diagram of the implemented system is shown. In applications 
such as electric vehicles, many smaller cells are connected in series, also called a 
string, to generate a high voltage. With this construction, it is possible to use one 
excitation source to excite many cells simultaneously due to the cell current being the 
same for all cells in a string. 
A galvanostatic source in this application adds no electronic components between the 
cells and their application load. Online operation is possible with this approach, 
provided that the galvanostatic source gets its power from a different power source. 
Potentiostatic operation is also possible like in [14], where each cell is excited by its 
own separate DC/DC converter. The galvanostatic approach is chosen for this project 
due to its ability to excite multiple cells using one excitation source. Galvanostatic 
impedance measurement in an online application requires the application load current 
to be considered, as it also influences the total cell current and resulting cell voltage. 
It also requires considerations of application load impedance, and whether this will 
have a significant influence on the total impedance 𝑍𝑐𝑒𝑙𝑙||𝑍𝑙𝑜𝑎𝑑. 
 
For the determination of impedance, several different types of excitation signals exist. 
This was described in the section “Excitation signals and broadband / fast EIS”. In the 
context of online EIS, it is relevant to perform measurements in a short amount of time. 
EIS relies on some assumptions of linearity and time invariance. The measurement 
resulting from a very long EIS measurement duration can be less representative of a 
time invariant system, since the SOC and electrochemical equilibrium of the DUT has 
more time to change. This is relevant in applications where the DUT is subject to a 
load – as it could be the case in online EIS. For this reason, the system is designed 
primarily for use with broadband excitation signals, as they have the shortest 
measurement duration.

A pseudorandom binary sequence is a signal that is binary-valued and pseudorandom 
in its state. It usually refers to a discrete-time signal, where the duration of each 
consecutive binary state is quantised ie. has a unit length or a multiple thereof. In 
Figure 7, an example of a 3-bit PRBS sequence is shown. In this work, the PRBS 
signal is defined to be zero-centered, which means that the instantaneous amplitude 
of the PRBS signal is either +𝑎 or −𝑎.  
A PRBS sequence can be generated using a linear shift feedback register (LSFR). 
Using an LSFR shift register, certain bits are XOR’ed together and fed back to the 
input of the shift register. By using one of the memory cells or the XOR gate(s) as an 
output, a PRBS is obtained 
The specific bits chosen for XOR’ing and consequent feedback in the shift register 
affect the sequence. The longest obtainable sequence 𝑁 given a specific number of 
bits 𝑛 is 2𝑛−1 [9, Ch. 2]. This is also referred to as a maximum length sequence.

The design of the first stage in the voltage sensing amplifier was an involved process. 
Many parameters were determined in a parallel workflow. The design process is 
therefore not presented in a linear chronological way. Properties of the input filter 
affects the noise, and the choice of amplifier also affects the noise. In the following 
sections, the input filter design and noise design calculations are considered together. 
Slew rate 
When measuring impedance with high accuracy and a broadband excitation signal, 
distortion should be kept to a minimum. Distortion and intermodulation cause 
disturbances and crosstalk between different frequencies, which can affect the 
measured impedance at those frequencies. 
 
The DUT is excited with a PRBS signal. The PRBS signal switches discontinuously 
between its two states. The resistive and inductive part of the DUT combined with the 
discontinuous changes in the PRBS signal gives rise to large slew rates. An input RC 
low pass filter is fitted before the voltage sensing amplifier to reduce this maximum 
==PAGE:55==
Design of a system for online electrochemical 
impedance spectroscopy of Li-Ion batteries 
Page 55 of 153 
 
slew rate and thereby preventing distortion. Additionally, the input filter comprises part 
of the anti-aliasing filter for the ADC’s. 
In the interest of keeping noise levels low, the first voltage sensing amplifier stage is 
chosen to have a large gain [20]. The DUT is expected to have a DC voltage of about 
4 V. A large DC gain will result in amplifier saturation if the DC voltage is not subtracted 
or removed. A differential high-pass filter is added before the voltage sensing amplifier 
to remove this offset. The chosen differential input high pass filter is a first-order RC 
filter. An LC filter was considered, but if a lower cutoff frequency of 10 𝑚Hz is desired, 
the inductor or capacitor value would have to be very large.

The output stage is comprised of the collectors of two power transistors, Q2 and Q1.

Current measurement dynamic range in an online application 
The cell in online use in an application will be subject to dynamic loads from the 
application. This load will add to the excitation current used for measurement, altering 
the spectral content of the cell current and the resulting cell voltage. The load current 
from the application must therefore also be measured. Although the load current alone 
might be used to characterize the impedance of the cell, its spectral properties may 
not cover the entire desired frequency range of the impedance measurement. This 
could for example be the case for an EV motor, where a constant motor speed makes 
for a current spectrum with only few frequency components.  The current sensing 
amplifier must therefore be able to resolve the maximum load current signal 𝐼𝑚𝑎𝑥  while

For the fast EIS system, an STM32G474 development board is used as the MCU. The 
STM32G474 is chosen since it has a high clock frequency of 170 𝑀Hz, a floating-point 
unit, and several SPI interfaces. Originally, it was intended to sample the ADC’s at 
500 𝑘sps. Unfortunately, the round-trip time of the serial data through the Serial 
Peripheral Interface (SPI) devices and digital isolators is so long the SPI rate had to 
be lowered, setting an upper bound of 50 𝑘sps. 
Generative AI (Microsoft’s Copilot, https://copilot.microsoft.com/) has been used in the

As described in the section “FFT methods and impedance from broadband signals”, 
covering a large frequency range when using FFT’s can require a large number of 
samples. Due to my lack of experience in making fast real-time programs on a PC, it 
is chosen to implement the ADC sample storage on the MCU, rather than streaming 
data over the serial connection to the PC. On this MCU 128 KB of RAM is available. 
The implemented C-code stores the following data when performing a PRBS 
measurement 
• 
16-bit arrays of samples from ADC channels 0-8 
• 
16-bit array of samples the DAC / controlled current source uses for excitation 
The length of all arrays was set to 2047, being a maximum-length sequence of an 11-
bit PRBS, while not using all the available RAM. The frequency range from 10 𝑚Hz to 
10 𝑘Hz is not possible to cover with this number of samples using one measurement 
period, as covered in the “FFT methods and impedance from broadband signals” 
section. Using downsampling of the 50 𝑘sps ADC signals, the effective sample rate 
can be lowered, which allows for impedance measurement in the full frequency range. 
 
When downsampling a signal in the digital domain, it is also relevant to avoid aliasing. 
The downsampling is implemented with a real-time 8th order Butterworth decimation 
filter. Some of the design rules which are used to design the analog anti-aliasing filter 
can also be used in this context.

decimation factor of 25 and a sample rate 𝑓𝑠 of 50 𝑘sps, the resulting output sample 
rate is 2 𝑘sps. With the 8th order implementation, a stop band / aliasing attenuation of 
90 dB is achieved. This means that spectral content lower than

The impedance deviation at 50, 150, 250 and 350 Hz is attributable to the mains 
frequency which is causing conducted or wirelessly induced disturbances. The 
disturbances appear at 50 Hz and odd-order harmonics of the 50 Hz fundamental. 
The noise in the high-frequency region is higher than in the low-mid-frequency region. 
This high-frequency noise comes from the data from the PRBS mode 3 measurement. 
It is speculated that the noise is generated by aliasing from higher frequencies. This 
aliasing can have its origin in the analog anti-aliasing filter, which did not have its cutoff 
frequency adjusted to the changed sample rate requirement as described earlier. 
Originally, the system was designed for an 500 𝑘sps sample rate, but this sample rate 
was later reduced to 50 𝑘sps due to data processing and SPI -overhead. The analog 
anti-aliasing filter was however not changed from its original 100 𝑘Hz cutoff frequency 
due to a lack of time for ordering new components. 
This is not deemed a significant issue in low-frequency ranges / PRBS mode 1 & 2 
due to the much lower PRBS rate having a comparatively low energy near the system’s 
Nyquist frequency of 25 𝑘Hz. Also, the 8th order digital decimation filters make aliasing 
insignificant below the native Nyquist frequency for PRBS modes 1 & 2. The 
significance of aliasing was tested by fitting a 10 Hz 1st order LPF before the ADC’s. 
This did not change the noise performance in a measurable way for PRBS modes 1 & 
2.

After the measurements and lab work had been completed, it was discovered that the 
cause of this correlated noise is windowing effects. The Fourier transform assumes 
periodic signals. A PRBS signal is periodic, however, the implemented fast EIS system 
only performs one PRBS excitation period. The consequence of this approach is that 
the DUT has not reached a periodic steady state solution with just one PRBS excitation 
period. This hypothesis is confirmed by using the 3-RC state space simulation to 
simulate the cell using a representative cell model. A simulation is set up where the 3-
RC model is excited with two PRBS periods over a total simulated time of 
approximately 160 s. The 3-RC model’s initial conditions are all set to 0.

The reason for the apparent noise in the first PRBS period can be explained from the 
spectral properties of PRBS. The apparent noise is the interference of the transient 
event from the DUT with the PRBS spectrum. A PRBS spectrum has uniform amplitude 
but a uniformly distributed pseudorandom phase. The transient event interferes 
constructively and destructively with the PRBS spectrum, due to the PRBS’s random 
phase, resulting in this bounded amplitude noise.

measurements with more than one PRBS excitation period, so this windowing 
phenomenon has only been tested with simulation.

on how quickly a measurement can be carried out. Given that electrochemical cells of 
a similar type have some common spectral characteristics, it would be interesting to 
investigate whether using model-fitting or machine-learning approaches can estimate 
an approximate impedance spectrum based on a shorter measurement duration than 
traditional methods 
During the thesis work, it was discovered that the transient event during the start of an 
EIS measurement using PRBS cause systematical errors in the measurements. 
However, the obtained measurements are reasonably accurate to make an 
approximate impedance model fit. Given the properties of linear systems, it could be 
investigated whether the impedance model simulation can be used to estimate what 
the initial conditions should have been at the beginning of the PRBS measurement. 
The purpose of this is to augment the measurement with the ‘proper’ initial conditions 
as found with the simulation, such that the premise of periodic steady-state is fulfilled. 
This could perhaps help in improving the systematical errors caused by the transient 
event.

During this master’s thesis project, I have experienced that my priorities for a project 
are important to very clearly define in the beginning of the project period. For the first 
part of my project period, I reviewed literature and found out how existing systems for 
EIS were implemented. In hindsight, it has become clear that I went into too many too 
specific subjects, trying to find a completely new approach to EIS. This meant that, 
rather than approaching the project with an ambition of “designing a system for online 
EIS”, I tried to find a new way of doing it and failed at it. Eventually, I had to 
acknowledge that I would have to ‘just’ design and implement a system using existing 
techniques and focus my work on that. 
Additionally, my ambitions for how general and scalable the EIS system should be 
were too high, meaning I set some unrealistic expectations for what the system should 
be able to do. As a result, the development of the electronics took ten weeks rather 
than five, delaying the whole project. The hardware worked after a few minor 
corrections and produced usable results. However, data analysis showed that the 
system did not quite meet the specifications in terms of impedance accuracy. It was 
hypothesised that a change in the measurement procedure could improve the results, 
but due to the significant delays in the project, these improved measurements were 
never made.  
The original project plan had allocated almost two months for report writing. Due to the
