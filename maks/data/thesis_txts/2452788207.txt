Abstract
This report presents a non-linear dynamic aero-servo-structural optimization frame-
work applied to wind turbine blades under unsteady loads. The performances of the
optimization with the Nested Analysis and Design (NAND) approach and the Si-
multaneous Analysis and Design (SAND) approach are investigated and compared.
The optimal blade design is described with a focus on the impact of the coupling
between control and structure introduced in the analysis model.
The aerodynamic model for the loads is based on the Blade Element Momentum
theory. The structural model for the blade is based on the ﬁnite element method
and on a simpliﬁed cross section analysis of the internal blade structure. The op-
timization problem aims at reducing the mass of the blade within constraints on
the power, the tip displacement and the pitch angle, by varying the chord and the
control parameters. The optimization with the NAND approach is run using a Se-
quential Quadratic Programming Algorithm whereas the Interior-point algorithm is
used for the SAND approach.
This study shows that adding the control strategy as a design variable allows a relax-
ation of the structural constraints and further mass reduction. The SAND approach
was found to be less robust and less eﬃcient than the NAND approach.

Introduction
In a context of global warming and climate change, the need for clean and renewable
energy for our societies is more and more necessary. Among the diﬀerent renewable
energy sources, wind energy has proven to be a reliable and mature technology, with
a low cost of energy. In order to be sustainable in the long run, wind energy projects
should be feasible without subsidies, and as such there is a high pressure towards
further decreasing of the cost of energy, and increase in the turbine eﬃciency. The
wind turbines are made more eﬃcient by increasing the size of the rotor, using
lighter blades and increasing the energy production per turbine. Oﬀshore installa-
tion access better wind resources, and improve further the eﬃciency of the turbines.
However, with larger rotors and oﬀshore wind conditions, the structure of the blades
is subjected to complex loading and additional risks of failure. Structural optimiza-
tion allows engineers to ﬁnd optimal designs that can sustain these constraints while
decreasing the overall cost of energy.
The state of the art optimization for wind turbine blades are based on high ﬁdelity
and multidisciplinary models. Ning et al [2] presented a typical optimization of
a wind turbine blade with a focus on the diﬀerent possible choices for the objec-
tive function. The aerodynamic model is based on the Blade Element Momentum
method, and the structure is modeled using ﬁnite elements and cross sectional anal-
ysis. In addition, aeroelasticity is a key phenomenon in wind turbine technology.
It couples aerodynamics and structure, and as such introduce non-linearity in the
problem and addition diﬃculties for the optimization. Botasso et al [3] studied the
inclusion of aeroelasticity for wind turbine optimization using diﬀerent algorithms.
This required communication between the structural analysis and the aerodynamic
analysis to model the coupling. Zahle et al [4] have integrated the aeroelasticity
to the analysis using the aero-servo-elastic simulation tool HAWC2 [5]. The opti-
mization revealed that introducing bend-twist coupling improves the lifetime of the
blade and the annual energy production. Overall, high-ﬁdelity optimization in the
ﬁeld of wind energy is necessarily time-dependent and non-linear.
In the three studies mentioned above, the design sensitivity is based on ﬁnite-
diﬀerence gradient, obtained with existing analysis softwares. The existing optimiza-
tion frameworks in the wind energy industry do not allow to use diﬀerent methods
for sensitivity analysis. However, the ﬁnite-diﬀerence method is the most expensive
method for design sensitivity. Kang et al [6] presented a review of structural op-
timization of problem with transient loads, including analytic methods for design
sensitivity. The importance of considering dynamic loads is clearly underlined in
2
==PAGE:18==
this paper. The author suggested to update the existing optimization and analysis
tools in order to permit analytic design sensitivity and thus reduce the overall design
cost.
In addition, in the studies mentioned above, the classic or nested approach for opti-
mization is commonly used, where an external analysis software is called to solve the
governing equations. However, in the ﬁeld of multidisciplinary optimization, diﬀer-
ent optimization approaches exist. Balling and Sobieszczanski-Sobieski [7] compared
the classic approach with other approaches and suggested to investigate further the
performances of the simultaneous approach (or all-at-once approach). Orozco and
Ghattas [8] showed that the simultaneous approach, in a reduced form, was more
eﬃcient than the nested approach for the design of non-linear structures. Frank and
Shubin [9] compared diﬀerent optimization approaches applied to aerodynamics.
They concluded that the simultaneous approach was less robust but more eﬃcient
compared to methods based on ﬁnite-diﬀerence sensitivity analysis.
This work has several objectives. It aims at presenting a nonlinear dynamic struc-
tural optimization framework applied to wind turbine blades including aeroelasticity,
using analytic design sensitivity. In particular, this framework is built such that the
control strategy of the wind turbine is included and its impact on the optimal design
can be investigated. A second objective is to implement the Simultaneous Analy-
sis and Design (SAND) approach using the optimization framework, and investigate
whether it is better-suited to solve the problem at hand than the standard approach,
the Nested Analysis and Design (NAND) approach.
The analysis model used in this report focuses on the blade of the turbine. The blade
is adapted from the DTU 10MW reference wind turbine [1]. The aerodynamic loads
are computed using a classic Blade Element Momentum method. Aeroelasticity is
included in the model by considering the deformation of the blade in translation
and torsion. The structural properties of the blade are computed using cross sec-
tion analysis on a simpliﬁed model for the internal structure of the blade.
The
governing equations are solved using the Newmark-Beta implicit time integration
method.

Chapter 1
Analysis and Model
The present chapter introduces the model used for the analysis of the blade. The
assumptions simplifying the behavior of the blade, the equations used in the analysis
and the sensitivity analysis are reported here.
The ﬁrst section focuses on the
mechanical model, or how the structural properties of the blade are calculated. The
second section focuses on the aerodynamic model, or how the aerodynamic loads on
the blade are calculated. The third section reports the behavior of the controller
of the wind turbine. The fourth section explain how the equations of motion are
solved to obtain the dynamics of the blade. Finally, the ﬁfth and last section present
the default blade that will be used for the optimization as the reference and initial
design. Using the previous four sections, the structural properties and dynamics of
the blade under diﬀerent wind inputs are reported.
1.1
Mechanical Model
The objective of the mechanical model for the blade is to be able to calculate the
total mass, the mass matrix and the stiﬀness matrix of the blade for the equations
of motion. A real blade is a complex assembly of shell plates, bars made of various
materials (composite and steel among others). A high ﬁdelity model can be obtained
by modeling each element of the blade with a ﬁnite element based approach. The
software BECAS (BEam Cross section Analysis Software) developed by DTU Wind
Energy is an example of a medium ﬁdelity mechanical model [10].
However, a model taking in account every detail of the blade would be too complex
considering the scope of this work. It is possible to calculate with a good accuracy
the structural properties of the blade using a simpler model. Here, it will be assumed
the structural properties of the blade can be calculated from two spar caps assembled
with an ellipsoid envelop, and the behaviour of the blade is that of a cantilever beam.
The ﬁnite element method is used to calculate the global stiﬀness and mass matrices
from the distribution of cross-section mechanical properties.

1.1.2
Beam Finite Element Analysis
Building the global stiﬀness and mass matrices and solving the equation of motion
analytically can be challenging. Instead, the problem is solved numerically with
Finite Element analysis. The theory presented in this section is based on [12]. The
blade is divided into N beam elements, where the division of the blade is indicated
by ri, i = 0...N. The length of the ith element is ∆li = ri −ri−1, for i = 1...N.
Fig. 1.3: Discretization of the blade with nodes and elements for N=4
In the work presented here, we use the software FRANS, a linear ﬁnite element model
using Timoschenko beam elements, developed at DTU Wind Energy. FRANS uses
beam elements that have 4 nodes in each element, two of them being at the start
and end of the element. Each node has 6 degrees of freedom, 3 in rotation, and 3 in
translation. The total number of degree of freedom for the blade is m = 18(n + 6).
The displacement of each node is stored in a vector z = [z1, ..., zm] ∈Rm. This nodal
displacement refers to the displacement of the node compared to the non-deformed
blade, set as the reference state. Fig. 1.3 illustrates the discretization of the blade for
N = 4 as well as the nodal displacement for a simple deformation in one dimension.

The induced wind models the impact of the wake on the rotor aerodynamics. The
model chosen is based on the Blade Element Momentum (BEM) method presented

Equation of motion
Applying Newton’s second law on the blade gives the equation of motion of the
blade
M · ¨z(t) + C · ˙z(t) + K · z(t) = f(t)
(1.36)
where f refers to the aerodynamic loads applied on the blade. The vector f is
constructed using the equations reported in section 1.2. The forces fx, fy and mz
are evaluated at the radius position ri, i = 1...N, and allocated in the vector f at
the corresponding node and degree of freedom.
f = [0, ..., 0, fx,1, fy,1, 0, 0, 0, mz,1, 0, ..., 0, fx,N, fy,N, 0, 0, 0, mz,N]T
Note that there is no force on the degrees of freedom corresponding to translation
in the z direction, or rotation in the x and y directions. Only the aerodynamic force
is taken in account here. The loads due to the gravity or to the inertial loads due
to the rotation of the blade are not taken in account, as they have a lower impact
on the dynamics of the blade in operation. In addition, the aerodynamic loads are
included in the model as point loads. In reality, the aerodynamic load behave as a
pressure load. This is a modiﬁcation that could be interesting in the development of
this model for a higher ﬁdelity. Further improvement could include a more precise
account of the loads on the blade.
The mass, stiﬀness and damping matrices depend on the value of the chord. The
aerodynamic force depends on the position, velocity, acceleration of the blade, the
rotational speed, the pitch angle and the chord. The equation of motion can be

The two approaches have beneﬁts and disadvantages [8] [9] [7]. The NAND approach
is the most direct and intuitive approach as it can call the solver as a ”black box”. It
can be implemented on existing software with little implementation eﬀort. As such,
the size of the optimization problem is relatively small since the optimizer only
manipulates the design variables and delegates the tasks to ﬁnd the equilibrium
state to the solver. Thus, the NAND approach requires two levels of iterations, one
at the optimization level and one at the solver level. One of the major drawbacks of
the NAND approach is its potentially high computational cost. For gradient-based
optimization, which are commonly used, the gradient of the state variables with
regards to the design variables is required. When the solver is a black box, the
ﬁnite-diﬀerence method is used to compute the gradient, and it requires to call the
solver a number of time proportional to the number of design variables. Otherwise, a
solution is to implement inside the solver the computation of the analytic gradients.
This allows to decrease the time needed to compute the gradients, but it requires
an intense implementation eﬀort and to be able to modify the solver. The SAND
approach on the other hand has only one level of iteration, where the state equation
is satisﬁed only by the optimum. This avoids solving computationally expensive
state equations during the optimization. Instead, it is only required to evaluate the
residual of the state equation at each iteration and its jacobian with regards to the
design variables and the state variables. However, the size of the problem increases

the optimization problem studied for this work.
2.2
Optimization algorithms
Choosing the right algorithm to solve an optimization problem is essential. The
choice of the algorithm is oriented by the properties of the objective functions
and constraint functions, the number of variables, and the complexity of the prob-
lem.
The analysis model described in chapter 1 contains non-linearity. In addition, the
two methods we want to consider diﬀer by their sparsity. The NAND formulation
is generally a dense problem, meaning that the gradient of the objective function
and of the constraints are dense. In the SAND formulation, the gradients are large
and sparse, because most constraints depend on a few variables and not on the
whole set. The two algorithms chosen to solve the NAND and SAND approach
are the Sequential Quadratic Programming (SQP) approach and the Interior-Point
method.

2.4
Formulation of the optimization problem
This section reports the characteristics of the optimization problem chosen in terms
of design variables, objective function and constraints. From the analysis model
described in chapter 1, it is possible to create various diﬀerent optimization prob-
lems. We have chosen an optimization problem that allows to include the three main
phenomenon in the analysis model : unsteadiness of the response of the structure,
non-linearity in the aerodynamics and inﬂuence of the control. These phenomenon
were taken in account while reducing as much as possible the size of the problem (by
reducing the number of variables and constraints) in order to have the possibility to
run the optimization formulated in the SAND formulation on a standard portable
computer.

The design variables chosen are the control parameters used for the pitch control
and the distribution of chord along the blade :
• KK, reduction gain for pitch control [scalar]

• kI, integral gain for pitch control [scalar]

The chord is used in the evaluation of the mechanical properties of the blade through

of the lift and drag coeﬃcients regarding the relative thickness, and this simpliﬁes

inﬂuences directly the amplitude of the aerodynamic force, its evolution in time
inﬂuences the whole system.

Inequality constraints
Two sub-problems are considered corresponding to two diﬀerent type of input: gust

• the tip displacement in the ﬂapwise direction, δ
• the aerodynamic power P
• the pitch angle θP
The constraint on the tip displacement is necessary to prevent the blade from hitting
the tower in operation. It is connected to the stiﬀness of the blade: the stiﬀer the

Chapter 3
Design Sensitivity Analysis
The design sensitivity analysis consists in calculating the derivatives of the governing
equations used in the analysis model in order to obtain the gradient of the objective
and constraint functions with respect to the design variables. The gradients indicate
to the optimizer how the design changes the structural response and whether a
change in a given design variable can improve the design considering the optimization
problem at hand.
3.1
Gradient based optimization
The two optimization algorithms described in chapter 2, the Sequential Quadratic
Programming and Interior Point algorithms, are gradient-based algorithms and as
such require the derivatives of the objective function and constraints with regards
to the design variables.
There are two methods to compute the derivatives: the numerical methods and the
analytical method. The numerical methods are approximated whereas the analytical
method are exact but require an additional eﬀort in the implementation. In addition,
it is possible to use the semi-analytical method, where the analytical method and
numerical method are used together, in a way that optimizes the implementation
eﬀort.

Chapter 4
Implementation
This chapter presents information on how the analysis model and the optimization
problem are implemented. The relative simplicity of the analysis model permits to
built a solver from scratch, and to code all the functions used in the optimization,
for both SAND and NAND approaches. The ﬁrst section of this chapter outlines
the challenges speciﬁc to the implementation.
The second section describes the
modular software architecture chosen. The third section reports precisely how the
SAND and NAND formulations where implemented. Finally, the fourth and last
section elaborates on diﬃculties encountered during the implementation.
The entire work was implemented in MATLAB. Overall, the code contains 43 dif-
ferent routines, including 2 for checking the gradients, representing a total of 4551
lines of code for the ﬁnal version.
4.1
Challenges for the implementation
The objective of the implementation is to have three diﬀerent codes:
• A solver, that computes the state of the blade for given control parameters and
chord distribution. This solver uses the analysis model described in chapter 1.
A solver is necessary to predict what the constraints on the system should be
in the optimization.
• The objective function and constraints functions for the optimization in the
NAND formulation.
• The objective function and constraints functions for the optimization in the
SAND formulation.
Having three independent codes demands a large implementation eﬀort. Since the
three diﬀerent codes are based on the same analysis problem, it is more eﬃcient to
build the three codes such that they share some functions. In addition, a modiﬁca-
tion or a correction needs to be done only once in the common function, and not
three times for each code, when the functions are shared.
58
==PAGE:74==
4. Implementation
Another challenge in the implementation is to have a code that can be implemented
progressively and adapted easily. The progressive implementation is important for
checking the robustness of the solver and the optimization during the implementa-
tion process, in order to solve programming issues as soon as possible. The adapt-
ability is interesting if some parts of the analysis model needs to be changed. For
example, if only the control strategy in the model needs a correction, the code ar-
chitecture should enable that only the part taking care of the control has to be
modiﬁed.
Finally, the most important challenge for the implementation is to be able to check
the gradients easily. The method chosen for the sensitivity analysis is the analytical
method. As such, the analytical formulas for every gradients is reported in the code.
During the implementation, mistakes are possible while coding these formulas and
they can impact greatly the optimization, since the gradients need to be correct for
the optimizer to ﬁnd the optimum. As such, having the possibility to check every
gradients computed by the code is essential to ensure the correctness of the gradients
used by the optimizer.
In order to tackle these challenges, the code was built using modular program-
ming.
4.2
Modular programming
4.2.1
Principle
The IEEE Standard Glossary of Software Engineering Terminology deﬁnes modular
programming as “A technique for developing a system or program as a collection of
modules”, and a module is deﬁned as “(1) A program unit that is discrete and iden-
tiﬁable with respect to compiling, combining with other units, and loading [...] (2)
A logically separable part of a program” [27]. Developing a software using modular
programming consists of dividing the process in independent units. Each unit, or
module, takes care of a speciﬁc part of the process done by the software.
For the implementation of the code, the analysis model is divided into diﬀerent tasks.
Examples of tasks are “computing the relative wind speed” or “updating the pitch
angle”. For each task, a module is created. Each module is independent from the
others, meaning that it only needs its input to conduct its tasks. The modules are
used for the simple solver, the optimization in the NAND approach and the SAND
approach, but in a diﬀerent architecture for each code.

4.4
Problems encountered during the implemen-
tation
4.4.1
Module for matrix calculation
Most of the quantities manipulated by the solver and by the optimization are vectors
or scalar and the modular approach is appropriate. The only exception is the mass,
stiﬀness and damping matrices necessary for solving the governing equation.
In
order to use the module format here, the formulation of the matrices is changed.
For this problem, the cross section matrices are diagonal, and the global stiﬀness
and mass matrices are sparse. The sparse formulation is then used instead of the
matrix form. The module then takes as an input the chord and output the vector
containing the elements of the mass, damping and stiﬀness matrices.
Since the
sparse formulation is only valid when the indexes are also known, the indexes are
stored as global parameter of the problem. This is only valid if the numbering in
the extraction of the elements of the matrix into sparse formulation doesn’t change.
This fact is not necessarily true for the function sparse used in MATLAB to extract
the sparse formulation of a matrix. In order to solve this problem, the indexes of
the sparse formulation of the mass and stiﬀness matrices are always compared with
the indexes stored as global parameters of the problem.
For a problem with N = 10 sections, there are 1510 non-zeros elements (out of 34596
elements) in the mass matrix and the stiﬀness matrix. This poses the question of
whether the elements of the mass and stiﬀness matrix should be taken as input of
a module. Indeed, the size of the input of a module dictates how easy is it to check
its gradient. The analytic gradient needs to be compared with the ﬁnite-diﬀerence
gradient, which requires two calls to the module for each element of the input vector.
In addition, the size of the jacobian manipulated is very high.
During the entire time-integration, the mass and stiﬀness matrix are constant. It
would then be intuitive to compute the mass and stiﬀness matrices outside the time-
loop and propagate the corresponding jacobian during the integration. However, in
practice, this method is not adequate. The module solving the governing equation,
get newton raphson, is the only module using the mass and stiﬀness matrix. It is
a very sensitive module, and as such, it is essential to be able to check its jacobian
thoroughly. Adding thousands of input variables would require much more time
for checking. Instead, the chord is taken as an input of the module, and the mass
and stiﬀness matrices are computed again at each time step.
Even though this
method requires more computational time, it makes the code much more robust
and easier to check. In addition, it reduces the use of the sparse formulation, and
thus the potential mistakes caused by the assembly of the matrix from its sparse
formulation.

4.4.2
Identiﬁcation of gradient errors
During the implementation, a lot of time was spend on gradient checking.
The
general method for checking the gradients in the code is the following
• Run the gradient checker routine on every module at a low level,
• Solve the analytic gradients if error appear between analytic gradient and ﬁnite
diﬀerence gradient,
• Run the gradient checker routine on module of a higher level,
• If mistakes are still found, double check the modules at the low level
A special attention was given to the gradient of the time dependent variables, such
as the power. During the implementation, an error was found on the gradient of P
with regard to the control parameters2. The gradient of P with regards to kP, for a
simulation of 100 time steps is illustrated on Fig. 4.8

On this ﬁgure, it is clearly visible that the analytic gradient do not correspond to
the ﬁnite-diﬀerence gradient. Since all the modules used for the computation of P
were double checked for errors, it was assumed that the error would come (i) from
a badly chosen parameter h in the ﬁnite diﬀerence gradient calculation, or (ii) from

The ﬁrst assumption was not validated, as a change in the parameter h did not

The section reports the settings of the optimization problem, for both the NAND

Scaling is used in applied optimization to facilitate the optimization process. The
numerical algorithms have stopping condition that depend on the feasibility measure
and the ﬁrst-order optimality measure (see section 2.3). These measures depend on
the gradient of the objective function and the jacobian of the constraints. If the

software have a limited relative accuracy. For MATLAB, the ﬂoating point relative

of magnitude of 107 W, is at most 10−9 W. If the constraint violation tolerance on

Inﬂuence of the bounds on the design variables
The optimal design for the gust input and for the turbulent input have variables at
the upper and lower bounds, meaning that loosening the bounds can results in a
greater reduction in the mass of the blade.
In order to estimate the potential reduction in mass for a change in the upper and
lower bounds on the design variables, the Lagrange multipliers, noted µ are extracted
from the optimizer [28]. The Lagrange multipliers correspond to the coeﬃcients
used in the KKT conditions. If a constraint is not active, the multiplier is null. The
Lagrange multipliers indicate the improvement of the objective function ∆f for a
change in the constraints limits ∆g:
∆f = µ · ∆g

5.3.3
Computational time
In terms of computational time, the optimization problem run with the NAND
approach is overall faster than for the SAND approach. This is particularly evident
for the case with a turbulent input for a time window of 30 time steps, where the
SAND approach takes close to one day to ﬁnish, and does not reach the optimality
tolerance. This section aims at reporting more precisely the computational time
and how the time is spent in both method.
Elapsed time will be used here to
measure the performance of the optimizer. This is an imperfect measure, but it
has the beneﬁt to be easily accessible. Other performance measure exists such as
the number of matrix multiplication, but extracting this type of data would require
an unreasonable implementation eﬀort. The elapsed time presented here should be
understood not as an absolute measure, but a way to compare diﬀerent techniques
with each other.
Fig. 5.21 shows the elapsed time required to compute the nonlinear constraints
and their jacobians for the SAND and NAND formulations. It is around 10 times
faster in the SAND approach than the NAND approach, even for a large number of
time steps. Considering that the elapsed time of the optimizer is bounded by the
time taken to compute the constraints and their jacobians, this indicates that the
SAND approach has a good potential to reduce the computational resources used

5.3.4
Challenges for optimization in the SAND approach
The optimization problem in the SAND formulation has a much larger size than in
the NAND formulation. All the state variables are considered as design variables,
for every time steps, the number of design variables is a few thousands, depending
on the number of time steps. In addition to that, the optimizer has to deal with
nonlinear equality constraints. All of this together make the optimization problem
potentially diﬃcult to solve, and as such it is important to tune the parameters of
the Interior Point algorithm. This section investigates the challenges encountered
to have the SAND approach running and converging.
First, the problem with the turbulent input is studied, as it could not be solved by
the SAND approach for the given constraint violation tolerance. Fig. 5.23 reports the
evolution of the feasibility and ﬁrst-order optimality measures during the NAND and
SAND optimization with a turbulent input. The NAND optimization has a classic
evolution where the feasibility and the ﬁrst-order optimality decrease together until
the tolerance are reached. In the SAND approach however, the feasibility drops
below the tolerance after few iterations, and it takes much more time for the ﬁrst-
order optimality to decrease.

Conclusion
This reports presented two methods for nonlinear dynamic optimization problem
applied to wind turbine blades: the Nested Analysis and Design (NAND) approach
and the Simultaneous Analysis and Design (SAND) approach.. After presenting the
model used for the analysis, a simple aeroelastic model based on the Blade Element
Momentum method, the background theory used for time dependent nonlinear op-
timization was outlined and applied to formulate the optimization problem and to
describe the sensitivity analysis. The algorithms used by each approach were de-
scribed and the optimization problem was reformulated in the NAND and SAND
formulations. The implementation using modular programming was then described
and several challenges linked to the code were described. In particular, a routine
to apply automatically the chain-rule on the jacobian provided by the gradient was
described. Finally, the results of the optimization problems were presented for the
two approaches, and compared.
The design and control of a wind turbine blade under a gust and under turbulent
input was successfully optimized. Including control variables into the optimization
variables proved to improve the design by relaxing structural constraints. The opti-
mizations using the NAND and SAND approaches were implemented in MATLAB,
and proved to compute the same solution for a given optimization problem. For the
problem and analysis model studied in this report, the NAND approach used fewer
computational resources and was more robust than the SAND approach.
Several concluding remarks can be drawn from this study:
• From the design perspective, using a time-dependent problem helps to model
more precisely the blade, and the optimizer can take advantage of coupled
phenomenons, such as aeroelasticity, to improve the design.
• From the numerical and mathematical perspective, the main diﬃculty in the
problem was the nonlinearity of the governing equation.
A method based
on the residual formulation of the governing equation was necessary for the
sensitivity analysis in the NAND approach. In addition, this study showed that
the NAND approach is more robust than the SAND approach numerically.
• From the implementation perspective, the SAND approach required a lower
implementation eﬀort than the NAND approach. However, for the two ap-
proaches, the largest part of the work was implementing the analytic design
sensitivity. As such, the implementation beneﬁts of the SAND approach is low
100
==PAGE:116==
compared to the total coding eﬀort.
There are several points that can be studied further for the future of this work. First,
the SAND approach can be investigated to detail why it is less eﬃcient than the
NAND approach, and if it can be improved. In particular, this work suggests that
a special attention needs to be put on the formulation of the nonlinear constraints.
Considering that optimization of time-dependent problems is going to be more and
more interesting in the future, having a clear overview of the methods possible is
necessary in order to direct the implementation of high ﬁdelity software towards the
most eﬃcient choice.
The analysis model used for the optimization can be made more complex in order
to capture more sensitive phenomenon of wind turbines. First, realistic constraints
on pitch actuation and complex control strategy should be implemented in order to
estimate if the mass reduction observed in this study are realistic. In addition, the
following aspects can be included: the dynamics of the tower, a three-dimensional
turbulence ﬁeld, diﬀerent wind speeds. Furthermore, the design variables and con-
straints chosen in the optimization problem can be extended. Adding the dimensions
of the spar caps and a strain and stress constraint along the blade can include failure
and fatigue in the problem. The eigen-frequencies could be added as constraints, in
order to avoid resonance.
Finally, the performances of the optimization using a time-dependent problem should
be compared to commonly used optimization methods based on static loads, in or-
der to document precisely the beneﬁt of using unsteady loads.
A few physical
phenomenon can potentially be better predicted with a time-dependent optimiza-
tion. Fatigue damage is usually calculated with the time-series of the applied loads,
and can thus be included in a time-dependent optimization problem and better pre-
dicted. For oﬀshore ﬂoating wind turbines, the control strategy can have a major
impact on stability, and thus it is particularly relevant to optimize such turbines
with structural optimization using unsteady loads.
