Abstract
This thesis presents a comprehensive analysis of the potential replacement of the current
district energy system with a new CO2┬¡based district energy system (DES). The study
begins with an exploration of the different generations of district energy systems and
their characteristics. The current system is examined, which comprises separate district
cooling and district heating systems serving 55 and 99 buildings, respectively. The total
cooling and heating demands of the campus are evaluated to understand the systemÔÇÖs
requirements for accurate simulation and comparison.
The design and simulation of the new theoretical CO2 system should prioritize its ability
to fulfill the existing systemÔÇÖs requirements. This serves as the primary starting point to
ensure a comprehensive and equitable comparison. By considering the current systemÔÇÖs
functionalities and incorporating them into the design and simulation of the new CO2 sys┬¡
tem, a valid and accurate comparison can be made.
Multiple alternatives of the CO2┬¡based DES were analyzed, focusing on pressure and end┬¡
user substations. The objective was to determine if a replacement or investment in a new
system is logical. The results confirm that the different types and variations of the CO2
DES offer a more cost┬¡effective approach to cooling and heating the campus buildings,
with potential operational cost reductions of up to one┬¡third. The thermodynamic laws and
logical results of thermal cycles support the authenticity of the findings.
Future steps and areas of further research are suggested, including optimization of end┬¡
user systems based on individual building needs, the development of discharge stations,
and research on central thermal energy exchange.
In conclusion, this thesis provides a deep understanding of the current system and its po┬¡
tential replacement. It offers a strong rationale for initiating an investigation into replacing
the existing system with a CO2┬¡based DES. The research findings demonstrate significant
cost┬¡saving potential and are supported by thermodynamic principles.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Climate change is undoubtedly one of the most pressing issues of our time. To address
this challenge, Europe has adopted a number of strategies aimed at reducing the con┬¡
sumption of fossil fuels, which are one of the main contributors to greenhouse gas emis┬¡
sions. One such strategy involves implementing more efficient energy┬¡saving systems, in
particular regarding the heating and cooling sector which is responsible for up to 40% of
energy consumption [1].
One promising solution is the use of CO2 as a refrigerant in district energy systems (DES)
[2]. These systems have the potential to significantly reduce greenhouse gas emissions,
as well as offer other benefits such as increased energy efficiency and improved air quality.
However, there is still a need for more research to fully understand the viability of CO2
based DES [2].
By conducting a thermodynamic and economic evaluation of this type of system at DTUÔÇÖs
Lyngby Campus, this thesis aims to contribute to the development of sustainable and
efficient heating and cooling systems. The results of this study could contribute to make
future decisions on the implementation of CO2 based district heating and cooling systems,
both at DTU and elsewhere, and ultimately help to reduce our reliance on fossil fuels and
mitigate climate change.
1.2
Scope
In this thesis, an analysis will be conducted on a theoretical fifth┬¡generation CO2 district
energy system, which will be applied to the DTU campus in place of the current system.
The thesis aims to evaluate the systemÔÇÖs thermodynamic benefits and its economic feasi┬¡
bility as an improvement over the existing system. To achieve this some minor questions
need to be answered. The system that is currently in use will be classified and placed in
one of the District Energy System (DES) generations that exist. To simulate the new sys┬¡
tem, it will be necessary to follow the strategy used for digitizing thermal systems, which
has been taught in the framework of the course ÔÇØDigitalization of Thermal Energy Tech┬¡
nologies Modelling and SimulationÔÇØ [3]. The methodology taught in this course provides
an effective approach to simulate the proposed system in this thesis. As a final step, the
new system will be analyzed to evaluate its economic and thermal capabilities and to de┬¡
termine whether a future investment in switching the system at DTU Campus in Kongens
Lyngby to the new system with CO2 as a refrigerant is justified. The focus of the thesis
will be on the transportation of thermal energy to different buildings using a pipeline sys┬¡
tem with CO2 as a refrigerant. Additionally, the thesis will utilize an averaging method to
model the end┬¡user thermal cycles by considering the different demands of the buildings.
1.3
Objectives
This thesis aims to explore opportunities for improving the District Energy System (DES)
of DTU Campus in Lyngby by evaluating the feasibility of implementing a 5th generation
DES using CO2 as a refrigerant. The main goal of this thesis is to provide insights for future
investment in this system. Even if it is found that the proposed system is not suitable for the
specific case of DTU Campus, the thesis will have achieved its main goal by contributing
to the research on this type of DES.

To collect relevant data, it is crucial to consider factors such as the current systemÔÇÖs tem┬¡

for a full year, based on the same demand as the previous system for a specific year.
Throughout the simulation and data gathering processes, the supervisors will provide
their opinions and guidance to ensure the reliability of the simulation. The results of the
simulation will be analyzed and compared with the current system to determine if the
proposed CO2 DES is worth investing in for future operation at DTU campus.

Background
The use of fossil fuels for heating and cooling has been a major contributor to greenhouse
gas emissions, highlighting the need for sustainable alternatives. One promising solution
is the use of carbon dioxide (CO2) as a working fluid in district heating and cooling systems.
This study aims to evaluate the thermodynamic and economic performance of a CO2┬¡
based district heating and cooling system at the Technical University of DenmarkÔÇÖs (DTU)
Lyngby Campus 23.
To accomplish this, the research will first analyze the thermodynamic performance of the
CO2┬¡based system using simulations and experiments, including an investigation of its
coefficient of performance (COP) and primary energy consumption. The economic per┬¡
formance of the system will also be evaluated, with a cost┬¡benefit analysis and a life┬¡cycle
assessment. By shedding light on the potential of CO2┬¡based systems for district heating
and cooling, this research will also contribute to the development of sustainable energy
solutions for university campuses.
The findings of this study will be pertinent to other organizations and institutions consider┬¡
ing the implementation of CO2┬¡based systems in their heating and cooling infrastructure.
Moreover, the utilization of the same system for both heating and cooling operations can
also lead to space savings. Ultimately, this thesis will present a comprehensive evaluation
of the thermodynamic and economic aspects of a CO2┬¡based district heating and cooling
system at DTUÔÇÖs Lyngby Campus 23, providing an in┬¡depth analysis of its performance
and potential for adoption in similar systems[2].

2.2
5th generation district energy systems
The term ÔÇØfifth generationÔÇØ emerged in 2015 as a result of the evolution of district energy
systems. This evolution led to the development of newer and more efficient systems,
which were categorized as fifth generation DES [6]. According to [6], a fifth generation
district energy system is best defined as a near┬¡ground temperature district heating (DH)
network that exchanges heat and cold between connected buildings using seasonal stor┬¡
age. This system also requires the use of heat pumps in the connected buildings to reg┬¡
ulate the temperature for hot water supply. Although the fifth generation DES are already
being used around the world, they may not operate in the same manner. One of the most
common features of these systems is the utilization of renewable sources as a heat or
cold source [1]. These sources could include a nearby lake or river, a solar power plant,

or any other renewable source that is located in close proximity to the district system.
Additionally, the fifth generation systems strive to distribute temperatures that are close
to the ground temperatures [1] by extensively using heat pumps at the end┬¡user part of
the system [6]. Furthermore, the most crucial characteristic of fifth generation systems
is their ability to operate for both heating and cooling [1] [6] [2], which are also known as
bidirectional energy flow systems [7]. To visually understand the different generations of
district energy systems, an informative graph can be found in 2.1.

The unique characteristics of fifth generation systems are the key factors that contribute
to the significant improvements that they offer to district energy system technology. The
near┬¡ground temperatures of the refrigerant used in these systems ensure a reduction
of energy losses when the refrigerant is transported through pipelines to buildings that
require either cooling or heating. Additionally, integrating renewable energy sources can
lead to a significant reduction in CO2 emissions [1]. Furthermore, using a bidirectional
energy system provides great flexibility in its use, as the same system can be used for
cooling purposes during periods of higher cooling demand, eliminating the need for a
separate energy system [1]. By combining the bidirectional nature of the system with the
use of local or decentralized available energy sources, fifth generation systems provide
a more economically viable option for meeting the energy demands of a region, city, or
neighborhood. It should be noted that many consider fifth generation district heating to
be simply an improvement of fourth generation systems [6]. For simplicity, this separation
will be used in this thesis.

2.2.1
4th and 5th generation on district energy systems
Understanding the differences between fourth and fifth┬¡generation systems is crucial for
making an informed decision when selecting which system to use. While both genera┬¡
tions share common characteristics such as low temperatures and the use of local energy
sources, there are significant differences between them. This subsection will list and dis┬¡
cuss these differences in detail, building on the information presented in sections 2.1 and
2.2.
ÔÇó The 5th generation offers a even lower operation temperature when compared to the
previous generation and these temperatures are equal to the ground temperatures,
ensuring almost zero heat losses.
ÔÇó The new system can provide both cooling and heating at the same time.
While the use of low temperatures and local energy sources are the primary ways to
distinguish fifth generation systems from fourth generation systems, it is important to note
that due to the lack of standardization, there are some systems in use that offer additional
differences to the fourth generation, yet are still considered fifth generation systems.
2.3
CO2 based systems
To be classified as a 5th generation District Energy System (DES), a system must meet
specific requirements, which are outlined in reference 2.2. One of the distinguishing char┬¡
acteristics of all 5th generation systems is their ability to provide both heating and cooling
at low temperatures. CO2┬¡based systems are particularly effective at meeting these re┬¡
quirements because CO2 can function as a reliable refrigerant for both heating and cooling
applications. For an in┬¡depth explanation of why CO2 is the preferred refrigerant for 5th
generation DES, refer to section 2.3.2 for further information.
Water is currently the most widely used refrigerant for district heating and cooling systems,
including most DES. This is primarily due to the numerous benefits that water offers as
a refrigerant. Understanding these benefits is crucial to appreciating why water is widely
used in this context. However, there are several reasons why water should be replaced
by CO2. These reasons will be explained later in this section. Additionally, a new CO2
DES will be introduced in the subsection 2.3.3.
2.3.1
Why do they use water ?
Water is the most commonly used refrigerant for district heating and cooling systems due
to its favorable properties. These properties include:
ÔÇó Non┬¡toxicity
ÔÇó Non┬¡flammability
ÔÇó Availability
ÔÇó Stability
ÔÇó Good thermodynamic characteristics
These properties make water suitable for older generations of district heating systems,
where high temperatures allowed its use. The characteristics mentioned above played
an important role in making water the preferred refrigerant in such systems. Additionally,
water is classified as an A1 refrigerant A.1, which means it is safe for use in large systems.

2.3.2
Why CO2?
This subsection addresses the advantages of using CO2 as a refrigerant for the latest
and most advanced DES, as opposed to water. Water has limitations that become appar┬¡
ent when operation temperatures fall below 60 ÔùªC, as it becomes ineffective. Using high
temperatures for water┬¡based systems leads to high exergy losses, which is counterpro┬¡
ductive to the goals of the fifth┬¡generation DES [2]. In contrast, the fifth generation aims
to provide both heating and cooling at low and high temperatures, respectively. CO2 is
an ideal refrigerant for these systems due to its unique thermodynamic properties[2].
Another disadvantage of using water to achieve both cooling and heating with one system
is the need for a 4┬¡pipe system, instead of a 2┬¡pipe system. This requirement leads to
a larger underground space and more pipes, making the system less efficient and more
expensive. In contrast, CO2┬¡based systems can achieve both heating and cooling with
just two pipes, resulting in a more compact and cost┬¡effective system c[2].
2.3.3
Proposed system
The CO2┬¡based district energy system being developed at DTU campus will provide both
heating and cooling without utilizing any local or decentralized energy storage, such as
lakes or rivers, due to their unavailability. The design limits of the system, including tem┬¡
peratures, pressure differences, and velocities, will be based on sources from [2] and
[9].
The system will consist of two pipelines, with one carrying liquid CO2 and the other carry┬¡

the only factor of importance is whether it can provide the necessary amount of energy
for the simulation.

3.3.3
Steady pressure choice
To determine the appropriate pressures for the two pipelines, the pressures at various
ambient temperatures will be calculated first. ItÔÇÖs important to choose the worst┬¡case
pressures to avoid the risk of saturation in either of the pipelines. Using parametric tables,
the highest pressure for the liquid pipeline and the lowest pressure for the vapor pipeline
were calculated for each depth of operation.
The pressure of the liquid needs to be set to the maximum value calculated, since this
corresponds to the highest ambient temperatures. For the vapor, the opposite is true, and
the minimum pressure corresponds to the lowest ambient temperatures. Choosing the
highest or lowest pressures of the liquid and vapor pipelines, respectively, is necessary
as a worst┬¡case scenario, since a lower pressure for the liquid pipeline might lead to
saturation at higher temperatures, or for higher pressure of operation for the vapor pipeline
and lower temperatures might also lead to saturation. This can be better explained by the
P┬¡h diagram for CO2 (R744) shown in Figure 3.10.

In Figure 3.10, the blue area represents the liquid phase, while the orange area repre┬¡

Based on the arrows, it is evident that the liquid phase will not reach saturation as long
as the pressure corresponds to the highest temperature within the system. This indicates
that the pressure should be at its highest point to ensure that the liquid phase remains in
a non┬¡saturated state for all other temperature conditions.
Conversely, the vapor phase allows for a greater change in temperature, indicating that
the pressure should correspond to the lowest temperature, which is equivalent to the
lowest pressure. By maintaining this pressure┬¡temperature relationship, the vapor phase
remains non┬¡saturated throughout the system.
These temperature and pressure considerations help ensure that neither the liquid nor
the vapor phases undergo undesired phase transitions, allowing the pipelines to operate
safely and effectively.

Discussion
5.1
Points of interests from the results
The results obtained from the simulations offer valuable insights into the proposed CO2
system and contribute to the broader research on CO2 systems. Additionally, they reveal
significant findings and unexpected results that emerged during the course of this thesis.
Analyzing and discussing these additional findings is crucial for developing a comprehen┬¡
sive understanding of the implications and potential applications of the proposed system.
This discussion adds depth and context to the research, facilitating a more comprehensive
evaluation of the feasibility and advantages of adopting the CO2 system.
Furthermore, it is beneficial to categorize the two systems based on their generation in or┬¡
der to provide a broader understanding of their characteristics and capabilities. The new
CO2 system can be classified as a fifth┬¡generation District Energy System (DES) due to its
ability to simultaneously provide heating and cooling, operate at ambient temperatures,
and require end┬¡user sub┬¡stations for full functionality. Although the exploration of alter┬¡
native energy sources, such as lakes, is not within the scope of this thesis, the new CO2
system aligns with other aspects of fifth┬¡generation DESs (as discussed in 2).
Conversely, the current system falls under the category of a third┬¡generation DES based
on the definitions utilized in this analysis. It does not fully capitalize on the synergies
between heating and cooling that characterize fourth┬¡generation DESs. Additionally, while
the systemÔÇÖs temperatures generally remain below 100 ÔùªC, they do not consistently stay
below 70 ÔùªC (as mentioned in 3.2). These factors contribute to classifying the current
system as a third┬¡generation DES.
Understanding the generational categorization helps in comparing the characteristics and
capabilities of the two systems, providing additional insights beyond cost and efficiency
evaluations. These terms can be valuable for future research to comprehend the tech┬¡
nological superiority of one system over the other and to gain a quick overview of their
expected operational performance.
5.1.1
Worth of the CO2 system
The operational cost analysis consistently demonstrates that the CO2 system offers a
more cost┬¡effective option for DTU in terms of cooling its server rooms and heating the
required buildings, regardless of the chosen combination of end┬¡user substations. How┬¡
ever, it is important to note a discrepancy with the claim made by Danfoss [24], which
suggests that 5th generation DES systems are less cost┬¡effective.
It should be clarified that DanfossÔÇÖ statement may specifically pertain to a comparison
between 5th and 4th generation DES systems within the context of district heating. Their
comparison overlooks a crucial factor: the direct energy transfer between buildings in
need of cooling and those in need of heating. This direct transaction of energy significantly
contributes to the reduction of operational costs.
Moreover, it is worth noting that the 5th generation DES systems, as demonstrated in this
thesis, utilize end┬¡user substations that require additional electricity. This aspect may sup┬¡
port DanfossÔÇÖ claim if the systems are used solely for district heating. However, this thesis
provides evidence that harnessing the capabilities of the 5th generation DES system for
both cooling and heating purposes maximizes its potential and overall cost┬¡effectiveness.

5.1.2
Examining the Influence of Data Collection Errors on Outcome
Analysis
In accordance with the preceding chapters, certain errors were identified during the data
collection process for the current system and the calculation of potential heat losses.
These errors have an impact on the actual energy consumption of the buildings and are
presently being addressed by the responsible engineers. However, it is crucial to assess
how these errors might affect the findings of this thesis.
The error arises from the fact that some buildings exhibit higher measured energy con┬¡
sumption than their actual consumption [23]. Fortunately, this error should not have a
negative impact on the findings of the new system. The new system, characterized by
negligible heat losses due to the equal temperature of CO2 and the ambient environment,
may even require less thermal energy than initially calculated, resulting in additional sav┬¡
ings.
It is important to note that the cost of the current system is determined by the purchased
electricity and thermal energy from the power plant (without errors), while the cost of the
new system is influenced by the consumption values (with errors).
While the logical expectations suggest that the new system would be unaffected or po┬¡
tentially even more efficient due to the absence of significant heat losses, conducting a
new simulation would indeed be advisable to confirm these assumptions. By simulating
the new system with the updated and corrected data, it would provide a more accurate
assessment of its performance and validate the anticipated savings.
5.1.3
High pressures for the open loop heating sub station
During the simulations of the open┬¡loop heating end┬¡user system, significant findings were
observed regarding the pressure requirements after the compressor. While the overall re┬¡
sults of the system did not immediately raise any concerns, a closer examination of the
pressures revealed a potential issue. The pressure of CO2 after the compressor could
reach levels as high as 180 to 190 bars, necessitating the use of more specialized equip┬¡
ment [25].
This high┬¡pressure requirement might present challenges in terms of compliance with
safety regulations or the procurement of suitable equipment. However, it is important to
note that despite this issue, the system still offers a more cost┬¡effective option compared
to the current system.
5.1.4
Investigating High Coefficient of Performance (COP) Scenarios

such as instances where the COP reaches zero (indicating the absence of a compressor)
or exceptionally high values. These observations suggest that the compressor becomes
nearly obsolete in certain cases, resulting in significant disparities between the graphs
of electricity consumption and thermal energy. As a potential alternative, implementing a
new end┬¡user substation that eliminates the need for a compressor altogether could be
considered.
It is important to note that incorporating this change does not significantly impact the exist┬¡
ing analysis of operational costs, as the electricity usage associated with the compressor
was negligible. However, this information is valuable for future investigations to optimize

5.1.6
Depth average temperatures
The analysis of operational costs reveals a trend where higher depths result in increased
operational costs. This can be attributed to the methodology employed, which utilizes
average ambient temperatures for each depth. It is important to note that as the system
approaches the surface, temperature fluctuations become more pronounced.
In the case of dynamic pressure systems, maintaining a stable pressure requires signif┬¡
icant hourly adjustments to compensate for the fluctuating temperature. This poses a
challenge to prevent the saturation of CO2 in both pipelines. Alternatively, if the decision
is made to maintain stable pressures, the actual liquid pressure (maximum) and vapor
pressure (minimum) will be further apart.
Therefore, it is crucial to emphasize that the ideal depth selection should prioritize sta┬¡
bility in temperature throughout the day and across seasons. By identifying a depth with
the most consistent and extended temperature range, the system can operate optimally,
minimizing pressure fluctuations, and ensuring efficient and cost┬¡effective performance.
5.1.7
The Viability of CO2 System Without External Energy Storage
The analysis of operational costs provides evidence that the CO2 system can be utilized
without the need for external or natural energy storage. In the case of DTU buildings
requiring both cooling and heating, the energy demand equilibrium is sufficiently low to
achieve cost reduction without additional energy storage, such as a lake or river, to further
minimize costs.
While the integration of more extensive energy storage systems could potentially lead to
even greater cost reductions, the findings of this thesis demonstrate that for the specific
scenario of DTU, it is not necessary. This finding contrasts with many references in the
literature that emphasize the importance of natural energy storage for efficient operation
[2, 1].
The thesis highlights that by accurately determining the appropriate amount of cooling
and heating required across multiple buildings, the system can achieve economic viability
without relying on energy storage solutions. This serves as an important insight, show┬¡

Addressing the data errors
The issue of incorrect data measurements for gathering energy consumption information

will be even lower than the current calculations suggest.
While this thesis has followed a specific methodology based on the available data, future
investigations and analysis, incorporating the corrected consumption data, can further

consumption values, the thesis findings can be confirmed, providing more robust evidence

simulations, primarily due to high pressures and instability, leading to its infeasibility. How┬¡
ever, the addition of other components may mitigate these issues and make the system
a viable option in the future.
To conduct this future work, a focus on the end┬¡user systems is required, which means a

5.2.4
Discharging substations
The net energy fluctuating around zero in some cases highlights the need for appropriate
discharging stations to ensure the proper operation of the system. Further research on
these substations is highly recommended to develop effective methods for discharging
energy when needed.
The implementation of discharging stations would enable the system to handle situations
where the net energy is either surplus or deficit. During periods of excess energy, the
surplus can be stored in the discharging stations for later use when the system requires
additional energy. Conversely, when the system experiences a deficit in energy, the dis┬¡
charging stations can provide the required energy to maintain the systemÔÇÖs operation.
Investigating and optimizing the design and functionality of these discharging stations will
contribute to the overall efficiency and reliability of the CO2 system. It will ensure that
energy is properly managed, stored, and utilized, allowing for smooth and uninterrupted
operation throughout varying energy demands and supply conditions.
5.2.5
Heat exchange station
In the current system, a simple centralized heat exchanger is used to transfer thermal
energy from the power plant to the district heating system of the campus. However, in the
proposed CO2 system, additional components are required to ensure that the tempera┬¡
tures of the two pipelines match the ambient temperature. This introduces the need for
careful consideration and research in designing these components.
One potential approach could involve utilizing a decompression process for the liquid
CO2 in the cold pipeline, reducing its temperature to lower levels. Subsequently, the CO2
can be warmed up to the desired pressure and temperature before being distributed for
heating purposes. This aspect of the system requires thorough research and optimization
to ensure efficient and reliable operation.
By investigating different approaches and components for achieving the desired temper┬¡
ature balance in the CO2 system, researchers can develop innovative solutions that max┬¡
imize energy efficiency and overall system performance. This research will contribute to
the successful implementation of the proposed system and its integration into the existing
infrastructure.

Additionally, the economic evaluation should include a thorough analysis of the cost sav┬¡

researchers can determine the payback period for the investment.

Conclusions
In conclusion, this thesis presents a comprehensive understanding of the existing district
energy system and explores the potential replacement with a new CO2 District Energy
System (DES). Through a thorough analysis of the current system and an examination
of different generations of district energy systems, valuable insights have been gained to
guide the development and simulation of the new CO2 DES.
All the sub┬¡questions that were established as guidelines for achieving the final goal have
been answered.
By gaining a clear understanding of the current system, including its energy demands,
capabilities, and temperatures, it can be categorized as a 3rd generation District Energy
System (DES). The new system, based on the background information found, should be
considered a 5th generation system, incorporating advanced features.
The current system comprises two types: district cooling and district heating. There are
55 buildings connected to the cooling system and 99 buildings connected to the heating
system. Assessing the total cooling and heating demands of the campus has facilitated
a comprehensive understanding of the energy needs, enabling accurate simulations and
valid comparisons with the new system.
Significant errors were identified during the calculation of heat losses in the current sys┬¡
tem, primarily due to inaccurate measurements of energy consumption in the buildings.
This discovery not only contributes to future research on the actual efficiency of the system
but also provides crucial information for the engineers currently involved in its operation.
The new CO2 DES was analyzed with various pressure and end┬¡user substation con┬¡
figurations. The objective of determining the logical rationale for system replacement or
investment in a new system has been successfully achieved, as the results consistently
demonstrate the cost┬¡effectiveness of different types and variations of the CO2 DES in
providing efficient cooling and heating solutions for the campus buildings.
The operational costs of the two systems (the current system and the CO2 system) were
compared under the same requirements. All variations of the new system have proven to
be a more economical choice for meeting the heating and cooling needs of DTU campus.
The findings strongly support the need to investigate the replacement of the current dis┬¡
trict energy system with a new CO2 system. The new system has the potential to reduce
annual operational costs by up to one┬¡third compared to the current system. The au┬¡
thenticity of the results is reinforced by their alignment with thermodynamic laws and the
logical outcomes of thermal cycles.
Several future steps have been recommended for the next phase of implementing the
new CO2 DES. Key areas for further research include optimizing the end┬¡user systems
based on the specific needs of each building, exploring discharge stations, and conducting
research on central thermal energy exchange. Addressing these aspects will enhance the
overall performance of the system and contribute to its successful implementation.
In summary, this thesis establishes a solid foundation for considering the replacement of
the current district energy system with a new CO2 DES. The insights gained from this
research emphasize the need for further investigation and investment in a more cost┬¡
effective and efficient system. By addressing the suggested future steps, the successful
