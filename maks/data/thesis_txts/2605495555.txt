Abstract
The transition of marine propulsion system towards alternative fuels is mandatory to offset carbon
emission. Among the alternative fuels, ammonia is carbon free and can be produced in sustainable ways.
Ammonia has 17.8% hydrogen (wt %) and is easily liquiﬁed at 25°C and 8 bar pressure.
The two stroke
internal combustion engines currently used in the marine sector reach efﬁciencies about 50%. Solid oxide
fuel cells generate electricity with efﬁciencies greater than 50 % and can use ammonia as fuel. In this work,
simulation of ammonia decomposition kinetics revealed that greater than 99.99% decomposition of
ammonia is expected with average fuel distribution of 2.5
Nml
cm2 at 750 °C in the Ni-YSZ anode having
dimensions 10cm ×10cm ×300µm. The experimental analysis of anode supported single cell solid oxide fuel
cell (SOFC - Cell A) with 28.27 cm2 Ni-YSZ anode surface area and effective cell area of 12.56 cm2, conﬁrmed
that less than 15 PPM of ammonia concentration was detected at average ammonia ﬂow distribution of 16
Nml
cm2 . This dropped to less than 1 PPM at average ammonia ﬂow distribution of 6.4 Nml
cm2 corresponding to a
ﬂowrate of 80 Nml
min . The J-V characteristics of Cell A was studied for a pressure difference (∆P) of 40-100 mbar
between cathode and anode. The mapping of the pressure difference with the current density revealed that
hydrogen inhibition effects were signiﬁcant at average ammonia ﬂow distribution greater than 8
Nml
cm2 .
Further, another SOFC (Cell B) was characterised using in-situ ammonia decomposition reaction (Int-ADR)
and compared with ex-situ ammonia decomposition reaction (Ext - ADR; composition - N2:H2 = 1:3), and
pure hydrogen (H2 100%; composition - H2 = 100%), between temperatures of 750 °C to 850°C. The efﬁciency
test performed on Cell B with Int ADR composition revealed that ammonia fuel utilisation of 83 % was
achieved with a LHV efﬁciency of 56% at 750°C. The J-V characteristics showed similar performances for the
three compositions at different temperatures and ﬂowrates. The open circuit voltage (OCV) of Int-ADR was
similar to that of Ext- ADR conﬁrming that usage of ammonia as fuel in the Ni-YSZ anode involved two steps,
(i) decomposition of ammonia into nitrogen and hydrogen, followed by (ii) electrochemical conversion of the
hydrogen into steam. The electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) results showed that the ohmic
resistance decreased by 49% with increasing temperature while the polarisation resistance decreased by 15%.
The quality of the EIS spectra was tested using the Kramer’s Kronig (KK) tests which had residuals between ±
1%. The total ﬂowrate of 80 Nml
min of ammonia for Int-ADR, 160 Nml
min for Ext ADR and 120 Nml
min for H2 100%
were chosen after performing a distribution of relaxation time (DRT) analysis on changing total ﬂowrates at
775 °C. The DRT coupled with peak analysis for the temperature range of 750-850°C revealed that the charge
transfer resistance reduced by 66% due to improvement in ionic and electronic conductivity, while the gas
conversion resistance increased by 12% mainly due to better evacuation of steam. The Arrhenius plot of DRT
peak resistances revealed that the polarisation activation energy was same for the three compositions, at 16
kJ
mol , while the Ohmic activation energy was least for Ext ADR with 48
kJ
mol followed by Int ADR at 55
kJ
mol and
highest for H2 100% at 65
kJ
mol .

1
Introduction
As climate change takes the centre stage in this decade, alternatives for fossil fuel is needed to mitigate
emissions. Shipping accounts for 2.4% of the world’s emission as heavy fuel oil (HFO) is the commonly used,
which has high sulphur and carbon contents [1]. This calls for alternatives to replace HFO and it’s current
alternative the marine diesel oil (MDO). With no carbon content and already well established infrastructure
and safety standards ammonia (NH3) could be a possible alternative fuel for marine applications [2].
The concept of using ammonia as fuel in vehicles is not new. Belgium experimented with buses using
ammonia fuel in 1945. A mixture of liquid kerosene and ammonia was used in a gas turbine in 2015. Due to
regulations on NOx emissions, an after treatment of the exhaust gases is needed to reduce NOx emissions.
This renders the system to be bulky, making it ideal for stationary or marine applications [3]. As combustion
is limited by the Carnot efﬁciency, alternate methods of using ammonia as fuel could be investigated. One
such method is to use Ammonia as fuel in solid oxide fuel cells (SOFC) that are not limited by the Carnot
efﬁciency. SOFCs can reach more than 60% electrical efﬁciencies under nominal operating conditions [4].
Hydrogen through SOFC has the potential to replace the existing power generation unit in ships. Liquid
hydrogen has energy density of 9.1 M JL−1. The challenge with hydrogen liquid is the need of 700 bar and
cryogenic temperature. As hydrogen storage system is expensive, hydrogen carriers are preferred. Ammonia
liqueﬁes at 9-10 bars, and 20 °C. Liquid ammonia has high volumetric energy density of 15.6 M JL−1.
Therefore, liquid ammonia has energy density of 1.7 times that of liquid hydrogen [5].
Ammonia is currently used by different sectors, primarily as fertiliser, refrigerant, dyes and in
pharmaceuticals. It is also used as a precursor in the preparation of nitric acid for plastics, ﬁbres, explosives,
and hydrazine. Overall, the demand for ammonia was over 200 million tonnes in 2019. The conventional
production of ammonia is through the Haber Bosch process, which uses hydrogen produced from steam
reforming of natural gas and nitrogen cryogenically separated from air. The reaction occurs at high pressure
(≈100 - 300 bar) and temperature (≈300 - 500 °C) over iron and/or nickel based catalyst.
This energy
intensive process is responsible for 1% of global energy consumption and 1% of energy related green house
gas emission.
Although such extreme conditions are necessary for ammonia production, it has 17.8%
hydrogen by weight making it a good hydrogen carrier [6][7].
The source of hydrogen and the energy input determines the ‘green-ness’ of ammonia synthesis process.
If the hydrogen is produced from water electrolysis, and the electricity input to the Haber-Bosch process has
been sourced in a sustainable manner, the process would be emission free.
This is known as the green
Haber-Bosch process.
Biocatalysis, photocatalysis and electrocatalysis are the well known alternative
methods of ammonia synthesis. Electrochemical methods for synthesis of ammonia can offer a carbon free
process with lower energy consumption. Due to the invention of intermediate temperature solid state proton
conductors, lot of research is being carried out in the ﬁeld of solid state ammonia synthesis (SSAS) with an
aim of minimising material and energy usage [8].
With the possibility of ammonia to be produced in a
sustainable way, it is an attractive option as a probable hydrogen energy vector.

A signiﬁcant proportion of marine engines are

operates in with medium speed and have higher
power density, which results in a compacted engine,
but the 2 stroke engines have better fuel economy.
Advanced Marine engines are controlled electronically by the electronic control unit (ECU) which can vary
inlet and exhaust valve timings, injection pressure and timing. An advantage of the ECU is decoupling of
injection pressure from the RPM of the engine, as high pressure injection can be achieved at any rpm. This
results in low soot formation during the combustion process, therefore reducing PM2.5 emissions.
The major factors affecting efﬁciency of two stroke marine engines are the compression ratio, cylinder
bore size and stroke to bore ratio. Achieving high compression ratio results in faster expansion of gases after
combustion. This results in higher exhaust gas temperature within the cylinder volume during the expansion
process which results in higher efﬁciency of combustion. The size of the cylinder bore decides the rate of heat
dissipation from the cylinder to the surrounding, which is equivalent to heat loss. For a given stroke to bore
ratio, larger cylinder bore results in higher efﬁciency as the ratio of combustion volume to available surface for
heat loss would be high. This minimises heat loss in the cylinder. The third parameter affecting efﬁciency is the
stroke to bore ratio. Higher the stroke to bore ratio, larger is the efﬁciency as the efﬁciency of the scavenging
processes improved. Overall, these are some of the design parameters decided by the engine manufacturer
that affects marine engine efﬁciency [22][23].
Considering all the above factors, it has been found that the thermal efﬁciency of 2 stroke marine diesel
engines are around 50% with MDO as fuel. The efﬁciency of the 2-stroke ammonia engine is also expected
to be around 50% with an expectation that 2 stroke engines remain a dominant form of propulsion system in
marine application. The development time estimated for marine engines running on ammonia is expected to

1.3.1
Shipping Emission regulation
Since last few decades, regulations limiting SOx and NOx have been placed by the International Maritime
Organization (IMO). The emission levels are further tightened in emission control areas (ECA) which are
parts of North America and Europe. Sulphur present in the fuel is responsible for SOx and particulate matter
(PM2.5) emissions. The limits set by IMO on SOx emissions is shown in ﬁgure (10a). From 2020, operators
need to comply with new sulphur content level in fuel of 0.5%. The level is further reduced down to 0.01% in
ECA. As the lubrication of the engines is affected with low sulphur content, the IMO regulation has permitted
use of high sulphur content fuel provided there is a mechanism for reducing SOx emissions. This criteria has
pushed forward the usage of scrubbers.
(a)
(b)
Figure 10 – (a) SOx emission regulation change as a function of years. (b) NOx emission regulation as a
function of engine RPM. Adapted from [22].
The regulation to restrict NOx emissions have been in place since 2000. The emission level is a function
of engine speed and is divided into Tier I, Tier II and Tier III categories as seen in ﬁgure 10b. Tier I was
implemented in 2000 followed by Tier II for ships built after from 2011. Tier III is in place for ECAs in North
America and would be implemented in Europe in 2021. NOx generation is a function of engine temperature,
speed, injection timing, injection pressure, exhaust valve timing, type of fuel used and the charge ratio (Air to
Fuel ratio). Tier II conditions could be met by just varying the engine operating conditions while Tier III levels
require special NOx reducing technologies.
Certain technologies such as exhaust gas re-circulation, low
pressure and/or high pressure selective catalytic reduction and water in fuel (for methanol) can limit NOx
emissions at the exhaust. Each of the technologies have their set of challenges which penalise efﬁciency of
the engine [22][23].

with sea water in an open loop conﬁguration or fresh water with additives in a closed loop system. The
alkalinity of seawater helps in converting SOx to sulphates, which are then discharged back into the sea.
Closed loop system is generally used when alkalinity of seawater is very low to abate SOx emissions. The

The peak temperature and duration of combustion determines the quantity of NOx formed at the
exhaust of the engine. NOx formation is proportional to the amount of oxygen present in the engine during
combustion. Treated and cleaned exhaust gases can be recirculated into the engine by a method known as
exhaust gas recirculation (EGR). This method substitutes partly the volume of intake air with exhaust gases
therefore successfully reducing the amount of NOx generated during combustion. Due to limited availability
of oxygen in the recirculated exhaust gas, the combustion ﬂame needs to be distributed as the concentration
of the oxygen is reduced. Moreover, exhaust gases contain CO2 and H2O which have higher heat capacities
than air (O2 and N2). Therefore, the quantity of heat required to heat the gases increases. This results in
reduced peak combustion temperature due to increase in the combustion zone and loss of efﬁciency [22][23].
Another method to curtail NOx emission without affecting combustion is to use selective catalytic
reduction (SCR) system, which is ﬁt to the exhaust gas line.
SCR technology is compliant with Tier III
emission levels. In a SCR NOx is reduced to N2 and O2 catalytically as shown in ﬁgure 11b. Due to the large
surface area of the catalyst, faster kinetic reduction of NOx can be made in tune with the exhaust gas velocity.

in the fuel effects the levels of SOx in the downstream exhaust and particulate emission as well. All these
factors have led researchers explore alternative fuels for combustion.

1.4
Alternative fuels for ships
Since the shipping is responsible for 80% of the trade by volume, the fuel consumption rates are high. As
the regulation to cut emissions were put in place since the year 2000 by IMO, many investigations have been
conducted to study alternative fuels for shipping. With stricter regulation norms in the future, as discussed in
section (1.3.1), alternative fuels for ships have gained relevance and prominence. The main factors that affect
the choice of fuels for international shipping are energy density, cost per tonne of fuel, emission regulation
standards, quality of fuel, speed of delivery, geographical availability of fuel, fuel options, availability of bunker
space and safety of bunkering. All these factors determine the strategy of the shipping companies towards
shipping fuel for the conventional internal combustion engine powertrain system [26].
The challenges that exist with alternative fuels in ICE technology include combustion parameters such
as combustion temperature, fuel viscosity, inlet and exhaust valve timing, and exhaust gas treatment. The
biggest challenge also lies in different densities and volumetric energy density of the available alternative fuels

To be in line with achieving carbon neutrality, the alternative fuels used must be to a large extent derived

and transport sectors are gradually increasing their share due to policy support. The growthrates are in line
with the sustainable development scenario in accordance to the Paris climate agreement [50].
The possible routes for converting biomass to ethanol, biogas (CH4) and biodiesel is through
thermochemical and biological conversion methods.
The sustainability of biofuel largely depends on
biomass source.
The most common method of converting biomass to biofuels is through a bioreﬁnery.
Bioreﬁneries can produce various fuels based on the type of processes involved. Bioethanol can be produced

Biodiesel made up of fatty acid methyl esters (FAME) is produced from oil seeds by extracting the oil ﬁrst

Anaerobic digestor can produce biogas from mechanically processed grass. The biogass can be increased

1.5.2
Ammonia in Fuel Cells
A fuel cell is an electrochemical device which is a sandwich of anode | electrolyte | cathode, generates
electricity through electrochemical reaction of gases yielding very high efﬁciency. As the process does not
involve combustion, the overall efﬁciency is not limited by the Carnot efﬁciency.
Depending on the
temperature of operation and type of materials used fuel cells are mainly categorised into a) Polymer
electrolyte fuel cell (PEFC) b) alkaline fuel cells (AFC) c) molten carbonate fuel cell (MCFC) d) phosphoric
acid fuel cell (PAFC) e) solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC). The details of the same can be found in chapter 24 of
Future Energy (second edition) [59]. Among the types of fuel cells SOFC systems are characterised with low
cost, all solid materials, no ﬂooding issues and the highest operating efﬁciency. In the previous sections I
have reinforced the use of ammonia as a possible hydrogen carrier. In this section a investigation will be
made on analysing the use of ammonia in the upcoming direct ammonia fuel cell technology and SOFC
Systems.
1.5.3
Direct Ammonia Fuel Cell (DAFC) and Ammonia fed Solid Oxide Fuel Cell (SOFC) systems
A direct ammonia fuel cell system (DAFC) uses an alkaline membrane which conducts hydroxide (OH−)
ions. The operating temperature would ideally be around 100 °C to facilitate faster ramping up during a cold
start. The electrochemical reaction involves ammonia oxidation reaction (AOR) at the anode and the oxygen
reduction reaction at the cathode. The alkaline membrane electrolyte transfers the hydroxide ion through the

needs to be operated with a voltage of less than 0.5V versus standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) as higher
voltages favour NOx formations. This results is lower current density of operation. The membranes are still
under development and do not have extended life time. As an alternate, using ammonia directly in a PEFC
system is also a challenge as even ppm levels of ammonia gas deactivate the electrolyte material [60].

1.6
Problem Statement
The usage of ammonia in SOFC system is in it’s infancy. Before implementing the concept of ammonia as
a fuel in SOFC stacks, it is necessary to understand the behaviour of ammonia on a single cell SOFC. The
challenge of using ammonia in a fuel cell lies in understanding the actual behaviour of the cell.
As the
ammonia cracking is endothermic and the fuel cell reaction is exothermic, it is interesting to characterise the
20
==PAGE:32==
cell at different temperatures. The ammonia cracking is also a function of temperature and diffusion into the
nickel catalyst layer which is affected by the ﬂowrate of the gas.
By performing an J-V characterisation at different ﬂowrates, it is possible to determine optimal ﬂowrate
of ammonia for maximum fuel utilisation which minimises the quantity of fuel that needs to be recirculated.
This allows smaller balance of plant (BOP) size in ships as liquid ammonia already occupies a large volume.
The benchmark of ammonia as fuel could be with the reformed ammonia composition which is the ratio
of N2 : H2 = 1 : 3 and the pure hydrogen composition. The performance of ammonia in SOFC varies with
changing fuel partial pressures and could also be tested based on the partial pressure difference between the
anode and the cathode side. This would give an overview of optimal operating conditions.
Therefore, this thesis aims at exploring ammonia as a probable fuel for marine transport applications
with solid oxide fuel cells as the power generating unit. The effect of using ammonia in SOFCs have been
evaluated by studying single cell current voltage characteristics, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy
(EIS), the corresponding distribution of relaxation time (DRT) analysis and evolution of ohmic and
polarization resistances of the cell. By de-convolution of the EIS by DRT, an attempt is made to understand
the elementary processes of an ammonia fed SOFC.
Therefore this thesis will focus on
• Temperature characterisation of the single cell SOFC to understand the behaviour of cell overpotentials
with ammonia as fuel.
• Comparison of changing fuel composition in the single cell SOFC between hydrogen, externally
reformed ammonia and in-situ reforming of ammonia.
• Determination of efﬁciency by reducing the ﬂowrates in order to reach high fuel utilisation.
• Behaviour of the cell at different current densities in order to determine optimal current density of
operation.
• Ammonia conversion levels at different temperatures and ﬂowrates.

the rest could be occupied by gas (generally air). The anode has higher solid phase at 72% with nickel as the
electronic conductor at 28% and the ionic conducting phase YSZ at 44%. The gas phase is the remaining part
of the anode phase, constituting 28% of it [64].
Different layers of the single cell SOFC require different functionalities. The role of the anode is to oxidise
the fuel, transfer the ions to the electrolyte while also transferring the electrons to the external circuit.
Transition metal elements have the catalytic behaviour for oxidising the fuel and good ionic conductivity. To
avoid mechanical failures, thermal expansion mismatch between the layer must be avoided. This is achieved
by doping the transition metal element in a ceramic. Ni-YSZ cermet satisﬁes this criteria to a reasonable
extent, is cheap, porous, tolerant to impurities and stronger than the other layers making it feasible to be
used as a support.
The electrolyte of the SOFC, separates the anode and the cathode layers which prevents short circuit.
Hence, it should have low electron conductivity while having high anionic conductivity in an oxide ion
conducting SOFC. The electrolyte must also be free of impurities to avoid additional resistance to anionic
transfer.
The material 8YSZ has stable anionic conductivity while maintaining very low electronic
conductivity even at low oxygen partial pressures.
The active cathode layer is the crucial part of the SOFC cell as the losses associated with this layer is
predominant for a various temperature range than other layers. The cathode material should have properties
similar to that of the anode, while having high concentration of oxygen.
The cathode layer made up of
LSCF-GDC is a state of the art as it is a mixed ionic and electronic conductor (MIEC) offering high ionic and
electronic conductivities. The drawback of using LSCF-GDC is that the LSCF reacts with YSZ resulting in the
formation of an insulating layer Sr ZrO3. The GDC barrier layer effectively prevents the diffusion of LSFC to
the electrolyte thereby preventing the formation of Sr ZrO3 [64] [65].
2.2
Thermodynamics of Ammonia in SOFC
Every chemical reaction is a characteristic of the Gibbs free energy (G) and the entropy of the reaction
(S). The maximum possible energy that is available in a compound is a function of heat of formation. This
corresponds to a total thermal energy of ∆Hr for a reaction. The entropy of the overall reaction ∆Sr is the
function of the temperature. As the Gibbs free energy is the function of entropy and the total thermal energy,
the change in Gibbs free energy is given by equation (12).
∆Gr = ∆Hr +T ∆Sr
(12)
The change in the Gibbs free energy of the reaction gives the value of the available heat that can be
converted to useful work. The value of the Gibbs free energy determines the behaviour of the reaction. If ∆Gr
is greater than zero the reaction is not spontaneous.
This would necessitate the supply of energy for a
23
==PAGE:35==
reaction to occur. This is generally the case during water electrolysis. If ∆Gr is lesser than zero, the reaction is
spontaneous and there is a possibility to extract work.
The decomposition of ammonia is a thermodynamic function of temperature and pressure.
The
thermodynamic equilibrium of the ammonia cracking reaction (equation 7) reaches almost 99% at
temperatures around 600 °C as shown in ﬁgure 20. The resulting gas composition is 1:3 of N2 : H2. As the
pressure is increased, the equilibrium is shifted to the left following Le Chatelier’s principle, as the gas
pressure of the products is higher.
This causes slower decomposition of ammonia into nitrogen and
hydrogen at higher pressures. Overall, a SOFC operating between the range of 700-900°C thermodynamically
should be in contact with reformed ammonia.

Ammonia Decomposition Kinetics
In reality the kinetics of the reaction dominate over the thermodynamic equilibrium state. The kinetics of
a reaction is generally deﬁned by the reaction rate. The anode of the cell is made of Ni-YSZ. The nickel
present in the anode acts as a catalyst for ammonia decomposition into nitrogen and hydrogen.
The
decomposition reaction is affected by the composition of the inlet gases. For instance, the increase in partial
pressure of hydrogen could inhibit the further ammonia decomposition in the temperature range of 500-700
°C. The inhibition effect is due to adsorption of hydrogen on the catalyst layer. The rate of decomposition also
depends on the geometric dimensions of the anode of the SOFC and the loading of nickel in YSZ [63].

3.3.2
Fuel ﬂow subsystem
After the junction where the dry fuel mixed with the steam, a valve system is provided to control the inlet
fuel ﬂow. These valves are mechanically operated and subjected to temperatures above 100 °C by recording
the temperatures at T_dir1 and T_rev1 as shown in ﬁgure (25). In the direct ﬂow conﬁguration, which is the
standard to the setup, the fuel ﬂows radially outwards in the cell. A new way of fuel ﬂow is tested using this
setup. A provision is made to send the fuel in a reverse ﬂow conﬁguration, which has fuel ﬂowing radially
inwards.
The direct ﬂow and reverse ﬂow vent valves help to switch the ﬂow directions. The valves used in the setup
are bellow sealed valve. It is evident from the ﬁgure (25) that when FCV-1 is open, the reverse ﬂow vent valve
must be closed to avoid bleeding of the inlet fuel to the exhaust. This automatically corresponds to FCV-2
being closed and the direct ﬂow vent kept open. The reverse ﬂow conﬁguration can be achieved by doing
exactly the opposite, i.e. by keeping FCV-1 and direct ﬂow vent closed and FCV-2 and reverse ﬂow vent open.
The exhaust of the fuel ﬂow subsystem is connected to the exhaust gas treatment subsystem, further explained
in section (3.3.4).
3.3.3
Air ﬂow subsystem
The air ﬂow subsystem is similar to that of the fuel ﬂow subsystem. The air is not preheated as sufﬁcient
air channel exists within the furnace to increase the air temperature to that of the furnace and cell before
entering the cell test chamber. The exhaust air temperature is measured to observe any ﬂuctuations based on
operating conditions of the single cell SOFC at T_dir2 and T_rev2. The pressure difference is also measured
between the fuel and air inlets and fuel and air outlets. The inlet and outlet pressure difference measurements
switch based on the ﬂow conﬁguration. The same pressure sensors measure the pressure difference but the
recorded reading would switch between inlet and outlet. The ﬂowrate of air is ﬁxed at 500 Nml
min . This maintains
the air to fuel ratio greater than 3. The exhaust air is sent directly to the fumehood extractor.
3.3.4
Exhaust gas treatment system
The exhaust of both direct and reverse ﬂow conﬁgurations from the anode are tapped through a common
port and exits through the extractor of the fumehood, under normal conditions. To test the presence of
ammonia in the exhaust gases a gas wash system is installed. This system consists of a gas wash beaker and a
pH meter. The pH meter used here is Orion Star A221 by Thermoﬁsher, as shown in ﬁgure (26d). The pH
meter has a built in automatic temperature compensation (ATC) to correct for temperature related pH drift.
Exhaust gases pass through the gas wash system which generate small bubbles through a porous opening.
The volume of the de-ionised water stored is greater than 500 Nml. As the solubility of ammonia is very
high in de-ionised water, it can be assumed that all of the ammonia from the exhaust gas ﬂow is dissolved.
By measuring the rate of pH change over time, the concentration of ammonia dissolved can be veriﬁed and
normalised to the inlet ﬂowrate. The value of the pH change is data logged via a USB port and the software

3.4
Design Considerations
3.4.1
Single cell SOFC subsystem
The weldments on the stainless steel setup is ensured to be gas tight by performing a gas leak detection
test. The nickel mesh is rolled using a hydraulic bench press and veriﬁed to have uniform thickness using
a vernier caliper. The tolerance for thickness is ± 0.02 mm. The gold leads are ﬂattened and are welded to
the nickel mesh using an arc welder. The thermocouple on the anode side (T_dir1) is placed such that it just
touches the nickel mesh through the direct ﬂow conﬁguration tube and doesn’t apply any pressure. Mica and
thermiculite are used as they provide superior insulation at elevated temperatures.
The ﬁrst layer of mica is cut using surgical blades to ensure accuracy of ﬁnish. The layer of mica is wider
than the nickel mesh to ensure room for the glass seal. The glass seal is injected in this gap of 2 mm. The
thermiculite layer being delicate is easier to cut using the surgical blade but is delicate enough to break. The
thermiculite layer has a larger inner material cut out than the ﬁrst layer of mica. This is to ensure proper
glass seal around the cell. The SOFC is gently placed on the existing glass seal. Utmost care must be taken in
handling the cell in this condition as the cathode layer is not sintered. The gold mesh is gently placed on top
of the cathode. Gold is used as it is the least reactive material at high temperatures in air.

3.4.2
Exterior subsystems
Standardised components were used for rest of the subsystems. The components used on this setup is
salvaged from previously proven setups. The valve system and gas lines are built using Swagelok components.
All the components comprised of stainless steel 316, as ammonia is highly reactive with brass. Ammonia gas
also reacts with silicone and any contact with the same must be avoided. This necessitates using Teﬂon coated
O-rings in valves or using Kalrez Perﬂuroelastomer (FFKM) O-rings which withstand temperature between -
50°C to 330 °C and is non corrosive towards aggressive gases like ammonia.
The bill of material is shown in annex table (13). The gas lines and the valves cannot be grounded as they
are electrically in contact with the bottom plate. As the node of the cell is the negative electrode, the gas lines
in contact with the bottom plate do not cause short circuit. The gas lines also cannot be made leak proof
completely for hydrogen due to it’s low gas density and there are ppm levels of hydrogen leaking through the
connectors and valves. The ideal approach is to minimise number of connectors and valves along the fuel line.
The leak detection is done using a forming gas composition of 95% nitrogen and 5% hydrogen and Peaker
3000 Flex gas leak detector. Telﬂon tubes are used to supply ammonia and it is observed to not react or cause
any leaks for the duration of the experiment. Externally, ammonia leaks are also veriﬁed using the MicroIV
ammonia gas sensor in addition to a swab dipped in hydrochloric acid. It is conﬁrmed that there is no
ammonia leakage of greater than 1 ppm through any of the connectors.
The mass ﬂow controllers are calibrated using external ﬂow meter Deﬁner 220 by MesaLabs and a
calibration factor is introduced to correct for any ﬂow ﬂuctuations. The JUMO pressure sensors are calibrated
using PeakTech P5150 pressure sensor by changing the potentiostatic screw position. The syringe pump is
calibrated by measuring the quantity of water at different ﬂowrates. The bypass for the mass ﬂow controllers
are used to verify pressure difference between the fuel and the air side inlets. The pH meter is calibrated
using 7.00 pH buffer solution. The calibration is done automatically by the device as the buffer solution is
registered in the NIST database.
The exhaust gas treatment causes back pressure on the fuel side of the cell. Every 100 Nml of water column
contributes 3 mbar of backpressure in the fuel exhaust line. About 17 mbar of backpressure is contributed by
the gas wash channel mainly related to it’s porosity. A coarse gas wash channel is chosen as smaller pores
contribute to larger back pressures. The ∆P on the single cell must be maintained around 20 mbar for safe
operations.

3.5.1

understand these phenomenon better, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) can be used on a
single cell SOFC as a characterisation tool [70]. An Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) spectra
can be used for deconvolution of all the intrinsic mechanisms occurring in a single cell SOFC system. The

4.3.4
Temperature change test
The temperature change characterisation test aims at determining the activation energy of the elementary
processes. The activation of the ohmic related processes are of value compared to the rest of the process.
This is due to the fact that ionic and electronic conductivity of different layers in the single cell SOFC must be
activated for the SOFC to function. The temperature range of 750 °C to 850 °C was chosen to perform analyse
the behaviour of Cell B with Int ADR. The ammonia ﬂowrate was ﬁxed at 80 Nml
min .
With rising temperatures, the OCV of Cell B reduced while the maximum current drawn from the cell
increased as shown in ﬁgure (43a).
This is due to the fact that the activation losses associated with the
cathode, anode and the electrolyte reduces with rising temperatures.
This results in reduction of the
overpotentials therefore improving performance. The Ext ADR has comparable performance with that of Int
ADR while the H2 100% bettered the two in terms of reaching maximum current density (annex ﬁgures (59)
and (60)).
To better understand the overall behaviour of the cell, area speciﬁc resistance (ASR) was calculated from
the J- V curves at the current density of 0.4
A
cm2 for all the three compositions as shown in ﬁgure (43b). It was
observed that the ASR for all the compositions ﬂuctuated slightly at a particular temperature while also having
a linear reduction trend. The Ext ADR had a greater ASR value compared to Int ADR at all the temperatures. The
ASR behaviour at 800 °C is slightly different as it was the beginning of Cell B operation. The ASR for H2 100%
was slightly lower compared to Ext ADR at all temperatures except 775°C. The change in ASR relative to 750 °C
values revealed that the ASR reduction between the three composition had linear trend across temperatures.
The decrease of ASR trend was similar between Int ADR and Ext ADR compositions.
The Nyquist plot corresponding to the EIS spectra revealed that both the Ohmic and the polarisation
resistances decreased with increasing temperatures for Int ADR as shown in ﬁgures (44a) and (44b). The
original and the inductance and capacitance corrected Bode plots revealed that the charge transfer frequency
region decreased in Resistance while the gas conversion peaks raised as shown in ﬁgures (44c) and (44d). The
DRT of Int ADR showed that all the peaks were affected due to temperature change as shown in ﬁgure (44e).
The peak P1 for Int ADR had the largest change from 11 mΩcm2 at 750 °C to 22 mΩcm2 at 850 °C shown
in ﬁgure (44f). The gas conversion peak was also observed to rise in resistance by 12 % from 382 to 429
mΩcm2. These rise in trends are contrary to what was to be the expected behaviour from peaks P1 and P2.
This trend is observed both for Ext ADR and H2 100% compositions (annex ﬁgures (62) and (61)). This could
be due to improper evacuation of generated steam at lower temperatures of 750°C, which improves with
rising temperatures, therefore reducing the steam partial pressure and increasing the peak resistances [70].
The change in steam partial pressures might affect diffusion of the fuel, thereby affecting P1 in the same
manner as P2. Therefore it is not possible to concretely conclude that P1 is associated with decomposition of
ammonia. Moreover, the Int ADR is faster than steam methane reforming (SMR), which is observed as a
characteristic of P1.
The presence of nickel mesh as the current collector on the anode side makes the
possibility of ammonia cracking even earlier than in the anode of Cell B. Therefore, it is likely that no
ammonia decomposition reaction peaks can be observed in the DRT.
The remaining peaks P3 - P6 were observed to drop in resistance with rising temperatures. The largest drop
in resistance was observed with the charge transfer peak of P5 which reduced in resistance by 66% from 196 to

5
Conclusion and future outlook
Various tests were performed to analyse the potential usage of ammonia in solid oxide fuel cells for heavy
duty transport application.
The simulation of ammonia decomposition kinetics was validated using pH
method for ammonia detection. The simulation and the experimental data showed that less than 1 PPM of
Ammonia concentration existed for ﬂowrates between 40-80 Nml
min , and less than 15 PPM for the ammonia
ﬂowrate of 200 Nml
min . After validation of the test results, the simulation of ammonia decomposition kinetics
was extrapolated for a temperature range of 550-850 °C for a cell of dimensions 10cm × 10cm × 300µm, for
ammonia ﬂowrate of 250 Nml
min .
This showed that greater than 99.99 % decomposition of ammonia was
achieved at 750 °C, with 2.5 Nml
cm2 of average ammonia distribution.
The pressure difference test between the cathode and the anode was carried out on Cell A at 750°C with
in-situ ammonia decomposition reaction (Int ADR). The ﬂowrates of ammonia was changed between 25-200
Nml
min . The air ﬂowrates where changed between 500-1000 Nml
min to verify the effect of changing air ﬂowrates.
This test revealed that hydrogen inhibition effects were predominant at average ammonia ﬂow distribution of
greater than 8 Nml
cm2 .
The characterisation of the anode supported single cell SOFC was performed on Cell B between
temperatures of 750-850 °C. The characterisation was done with three different fuel conﬁgurations of Int
ADR, ex-situ ammonia decomposition reaction (Ext ADR; composition - N2:H2=1:3) and pure hydrogen (H2
100%). The efﬁciency test on Cell B was performed at 750 °C with Int ADR composition. The ﬂowrates were
changed between 25-80 Nml
min of ammonia. The overall efﬁciency of the cell was highest at 56% having fuel
utilisation of 83% for the ammonia ﬂowrates of 25 Nml
min corresponding to 2.0 Nml
cm2 of average ammonia
distribution in the Cell B. It was found that, improving fuel utilisation was the main factor involved in
improvement of efﬁciency.
The J-V characterisation of the Cell B for different fuel compositions revealed that the performance of Int
ADR was similar to that of Ext ADR. The OCV of the two were similar conﬁrming that ammonia
decomposition reaction was a two step process as mentioned in section(2.3). The OCV of Cell B dropped with
rising temperatures while having improvement in current densities suggesting improvement in ionic and
electronic conductivity which reduced activation overpotentials.
The EIS analysis coupled with DRT
suggested the use of higher ﬂowrates due to better fuel distribution. The brief sensitivity analysis of DRT
peaks for Int ADR composition revealed that
(i) The peak P1 may not be associated with ammonia decomposition reaction while using Int ADR fuel
composition but with convective diffusion of fuel in the Ni-YSZ anode. Therefore, P1 could be related to
the partial pressure of the fuel.
(ii) The rise of gas conversion resistance (P2) by 12% while increasing temperatures suggested better
evacuation of generated steam.
(iii) The solid state gas diffusion peak resistance (P3) increased with increasing ﬂowrates but decreased in
resistance by 16% with increasing temperatures. This could be due to it’s convolution with peak P4 at
lower temperatures.
(iv) The charge transfer peak (P5) reduced by 66% for changing temperatures, for both Int ADR and Ext ADR
suggesting high levels of ammonia decomposition while using Int ADR composition.
(v) The temperature activated peak (P6) changed by 45% each for all the three composition. No consensus
exists in literature on association of the peak with a physical process [70].
61
==PAGE:73==
The analysis was further extended to calculate the activation energies of different temperature effected
processes. It was found that the Ohmic activation energy for Int ADR was 55
kJ
mol slightly higher compared to
Ext ADR (48
kJ
mol ) and highest for H2 100% (65
kJ
mol ). However, the highest activation energy of 65
kJ
mol was
associated with the gas conversion peak P2 for Int ADR. This was 14% higher compared to Ext ADR and 51%
lower compared to H2 100%. This could indicate the endothermic effect of ammonia decomposition on gas
conversion, although further studies are necessary. The levels of activation energies for H2 100% are lower
than that reported in Caliandro P. et.al [70], mainly due to larger cell size. The larger cell size leads to a lower
peak resistances in the DRT peaks because of lower contact resistances.
For future outlook, to further understand the impact of using ammonia in SOFC, the temperature range
could be reduced until 650 °C and veriﬁed for ammonia decomposition levels. As pH detection method of
detecting ammonia could be affected by impartial dissolution of ammonia, detecting ammonia in the
exhaust gases can be done using Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR) which has the capacity to
differentiate both ammonia and steam [74].
Another alternate is to use a gas chromatography mass spectrometer (GC-MS) which would give
information on the exhaust gas composition, which will directly be related to ammonia conversion while
using anhydrous ammonia as fuel [75]. Further, ammonia conversion testing with only the experimental
setup (blank test) could provide information on ammonia conversion behaviour across temperatures due the
metallic components of the setup.
Segmented tests on short-stack SOFC running on ammonia has the
possibility to identify the conversion related peaks using DRT. The thermal effect of Int-ADR could be
visualised in addition to providing information on conversion of ammonia in an Ni-YSZ anode supported
cell.
The existence of nickel nitriding effect can be validated using scanning electron microscopy (SEM) coupled
with energy dispersive X-ray analysis (EDX) and angle resolved X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) to
identify bulk and surface compositions as a post test analysis on Cell B [74]. Long term tests on stacks running
with ammonia at high utilisation levels (greater than 60%) will help analyse the lifetime and degradation effects
of using ammonia as fuel. The endothermal effect of ammonia decomposition reaction could be quantiﬁed
using the enthalpy of reaction which helps in determining the temperature drop during operation of a SOFC
stack. By implementing this correction, it could be further be compared with Ext ADR composition accurately.
The overall LHV efﬁciency of 65% would be necessary for SOFC systems to compete with existing marine
2-stroke engines. The exact value of efﬁciency needed to offset the rise of costs due to implementation of SOFC
in ships is being researched.
