Abstract
In a time and age where digitisation is growing, associated cybersecurity risks are in­
evitably following along. The level of cybersecurity awareness, is at a race with the con­
stantly new surfacing threats of which the average employee is facing. This thesis aims
to investigate to what extent, the anecdotal story of dropping maliciously prepared USB
drives in private and public common areas, is beneficial for adversaries in 2024. Fur­
thermore, the extent of which users indiscriminately scan publicly available QR codes is
investigated. The general cybersecurity practice among employees of a university, two
governmental bodies and an NGO is examined, to gain insight in the response and be­
havior of risks in plain sight. Lastly, a brief overview of Safari browsers and associated
vulnerabilities is presented, before a theoretical comparison, exploring the advantages
and disadvantages of both attack types is discussed. The study shows novelty in the
USB drive experiments, as used honey tokens include file types of the Office Open XML
format. Additionally, novelty is shown as experiment locations include non­educational fa­
cilities. The thesis finds, that despite of a decrease in the number of USB drives plugged
in, and a reasonable amount of cybersecurity awareness among employees, USB pe­
ripherals still pose a threat to information security. Additionally, the results reflect the
dissemination of QR codes, and reveal that a significant amount of iOS mobile devices
using the Safari browser are affected by known vulnerabilities. The results also suggest
that QR code­based attacks are not matching USB­based attacks in terms of cybersecu­
rity awareness and preparedness across the different workplaces. Employees generally
show a high motivation for uncritically scanning QR codes as long as it is presented in a
convincing and trustworthy visual matter.

Introduction
In a time where we are increasingly dependent on digital systems, and the usage and
distribution of these only seem to grow, the threats and dangers that target these are
rapidly following the evolution. Cybersecurity is an immense field of research and encom­
passes a wide variety of subcategories. One of these is social engineering: A category
that investigates the process of deceiving a human being through manipulation, often ex­
ploiting a persons good will and kindness in order to gain access to restricted areas or
information. A well known method of social engineering and often displayed in movies is
the placement of USB drives in public places, that are found, collected and plugged in to
a restricted computer. It is not clear though, whether this increasing public awareness of
this attack vector through media depiction and other sources also leads to a higher degree
of operational security rendering this attack ineffective.
To some degree, the USB dropping attack has roots in the highly controversial AutoRun
functionality, that in Windows 95 through Vista was enabled by default, allowing remov­
able media such as CD/DVD­ROMs, USB drives and hard disks, to automatically execute
programs when inserted into a Windows system. It wasn’t until the release of Windows
7 in 2009 before the AutoRun feature was disabled for generic mass storage devices. In
addition, patches were later released for Windows XP and Vista, disabling the AutoRun
functionality for all non optical drives [1]. In 2011, Microsoft released a security intelli­
gence report with insights from 600 million individual computers worldwide, revealing that
43% of all malware originated from AutoRun attacks and 26% originated entirely from
USB­based AutoRun attacks [2].
In 2010, the highly sophisticated and powerful worm Stuxnet, was found at no less than
14 industrial sites in Iran. Stuxnet’s level of sophistication was a rare sight, and it exploited
four zero­day vulnerabilities in the process of self­replicating, detecting vulnerable soft­
ware, and compromising the programmable logic controllers (PLC) of the centrifuges pro­
cessing nuclear fuel, allowing them to spin out of control and become destroyed. Stuxnet
was designed to spread through USB drives and it was a real world example that cyber
warfare could seriously impact critical infrastructure [3].
Threat intelligence company Mandiant reports that espionage­motivated USB­based at­
tacks have seen a threefold increase in the first half of 2023, targeting victims in Europe,
the U.S. and Asia [4] [5]. USB­based worms have also been observed in the war between
Russia and Ukraine and has recently spread to USA, Vietnam, Chile, Poland and Ger­
many [6]. Evidence show that USB­based worms pose a great risk to critical infrastructure
secured behind air­gapped sites and structures. In a perfect world, critical infrastructure
is always properly segregated and secured, but in reality this is far from the case. This
was seen in 2017, when the devastating ransomware attack known as WannaCry, paral­
ysed numerous hospitals and organisations globally [7]. Another example of an incident
at healthcare facilities, was when an employee of an European hospital shared a presen­
tation with other participants at a conference in Asia. The transfer took place on a USB
drive, which at some point was plugged into an already infected computer, causing the
employee to bring back the malware and infecting the European hospital. [8].
Similarly to the threat of USB­based attacks, in 2022, FBI issued a warning against mali­
cious QR codes stating that cybercriminals were tampering with QR codes to steal infor­
mation [9]. This warning was published just 15 days after a series of QR code stickers

were discovered on parking meters in Austin, Texas. The QR codes linked to a fraud­
ulent website with the intent to steal money from citizens [10]. Another example of QR
codes used as an attack vector is from 2021, where customers of the German financial
institutions Sparkasse and Volksbanken Raiffeisenbanken were targeted with attempts of
phishing. Some of the well constructed phishing mails, that pretended to be from the re­

This thesis aims to investigate and assess the human behavior and level of cybersecurity
awareness in relation to risks in plain sight. The study will comprise experiments of placing
USB drives and QR codes containing honey tokens, in order to collect quantitative data
of the collector’s actions. These data, along with observations collected throughout the
individual experiments, will help answer the following research questions of interest.
• RQ1 ­ To what extent is the anecdotal story of dropping maliciously prepared USB
drives in private and public common spaces, beneficial for adversaries in 2024?
And to what extent do end users indiscriminately scan publicly posted QR codes?
• RQ2 ­ How is the general behavior and cyber security practice in regards to dropped
USB drives and publicly posted QR codes in a university campus, an NGO and
public government bodies respectively?
• RQ3 ­ How does one attack compare to the other in terms of risk, impact, awareness
and response?

Background
2.1
USB Peripherals
There are numerous attack vectors to consider when plugging in a USB drive. The at­
tack methods have evolved over the years, utilising both weak spots and different attack
techniques. USB­based attacks has previously been divided into categories by Nissim et
al. [12]. The categories comprise programmable micro controllers, USB peripherals and
electrical attacks, and can be seen in figure 2.1. Programmable micro controllers cover
attacks that takes advantage of the trust an operating system puts in human interface
devices (HID), such as external mice and keyboards [12]. These types of attacks have
seen new formats over the years, with one of the most characteristic being the Rubber
Ducky1. The Rubber Ducky is commercially sold and designed to be a HID disguised as
regular USB mass storage device. It can execute arbitrary keystrokes pre­programmed
and stored within the memory. Devices that alter in execution, but with a similar function­
ality is the USB Ninja2 and the trending Flipper Zero 3. The USB Ninja is disguised as a
regular charging and data transfer USB cable, but allows an attacker to execute a series
of keystrokes, triggering it from a remote control. The controversial Flipper Zero, how­
ever, is a multi­tool that supports both regular and Bluetooth­based keyboard emulation
functionality [13][14].
The severity of USB­based HID attacks can be comprehended by imagining an adver­
sary with physical access to a given device, and thus has the opportunity to input arbi­
trary keystrokes and mouse clicks, only in a much smaller time span and without actually
pressing a single key or button. In a fraction of the time, a well prepared payload could
disable security precautions, deploy malware or extract credentials. This underlines the
importance of locking devices when leaving them under no supervision.
Figure 2.1: Taxonomy of USB­based attack categories from Nissim et al. [12]
The USB peripherals category covers USB­based attacks that take advantage of regular
USB peripherals, fabricated merely with ordinary usage in mind. This category can be di­
vided into two subcategories: Reprogrammed peripherals and not reprogrammed periph­
erals [12]. The first subcategory comprises attacks where regular USB peripherals have
had their controlling software, such as the firmware or driver, maliciously reprogrammed.
As the novel BadUSB, originally presented by Nohl et al. [15] in 2014 worked by mod­
ifying the stock firmware, it belongs under this subcategory. The impact of USB­based

attacks using maliciously reprogrammed firmware is at least as dangerous as USB­based
attacks using programmable micro controllers. A certain level of expertise is required to
identify and develop firmware­based exploits on USB peripherals from scratch, but this
category isn’t without advantages for an adversary. It will, for example, be much harder
for a non­technical user, to detect malicious USB peripherals with modified firmware, as
they more or less are impossible to distinguish from trustworthy brand name peripherals.
Thus the knowledge of the physical presence of products and hardware used for USB­
based attacks, such as the Rubber Ducky, is worthless. While it hasn’t been possible
to examine the direct source, Nissim et al. [12] mentions examples of USB peripherals
with modified firmware that, similarly to the USB­based HID attacks, are able to compro­
mise a users computer in several ways. For example, a brand name gaming mouse with
modified firmware that will act as a keyboard in order to download or transfer malware
persistently across reboots, a USB peripheral presenting itself as an Ethernet adapter,
acting as a DHCP server in order to assign rogue DNS servers to the host machine, as
well as a mass storage USB drive that automatically mounts a hidden partition, even after
it is ejected by the user [12].
Attacks based on not reprogrammed USB peripherals is a branch that exploits USB pe­
ripherals as is, and without any modifications to the embedded firmware. These kind of
attacks are typically host based infections that can detect insertions of USB peripherals
and take advantage of these in order to spread to other machines [12]. Attacks like these
are highly reliant on functionality, either through integrated auto run features or unlisted
zero­day vulnerabilities, that allow executing code after insertions of USB drives. This ren­
ders the attack complexity higher than other USB­based attacks, but drastically enhances
the capability in terms of penetrating air­gapped infrastructures and stealthily spreading
in segregated networks. An example of a not reprogrammed USB peripheral attack is the
Stuxnet worm that spread to roughly 100,000 hosts worldwide [16]. The Stuxnet worm
exploited an improper handling of .LNK files used to display custom icons in the Microsoft
File Explorer. The exploit, that became known as the Stuxnet LNK exploit and registered
as CVE­2010­2568 [17], was also used in the similar Fanny worm from 2008. In fact,
Fanny also used the same exploit as the Stuxnet worm, identified as MS09­025 [18], in
order elevate privileges from user mode to kernel mode [19].
The third category that Nissim et al. [12] describe is the electrical­based attacks, using
electrical components to drain or overload the components of a computer. The most
noticeable attack of this kind is the USB Killer introduced in 2015 by a Russian security
researcher [12]. The later iteration, USB Killer 2.0, works by charging a capacitor to ­220
V and releasing the power to the USB interface, resulting in a broken device [20].
2.2
QR Codes
Uncritically scanning QR codes may seem harmless, but research and historical attacks
show that consequences can be of high severity. By analysing 14 million unique web
pages, Kharraz et al. [21] showed that malicious QR codes were used to conduct phishing
and spread malware already in 2014. QR codes can be used as an attack vector for
multiple types of attacks, spanning from simple phishing attacks to advanced browser
exploits, however the approach to attack a target remains the same: The victim needs to
be lured to scan a QR code. Using QR codes do have its advantages in luring victims
to visit phishing sites. As the two­dimensional bar codes are unreadable to humans, the
verification of the domain being linked to is entirely up the the users own discipline in
cases where the reader doesn’t automatically let the user preview the link. This makes
it an evident method of launching phishing attacks. The luring can be accomplished in
4

multiple ways. For example, deceiving posters imitating reliable companies, institutions or
governmental bodies, or by modifying the QR code placed on actual posters. Adversaries
can modify a QR code by entirely replacing it, or replacing only parts of it [22]. Deception
is also possible by producing nested QR codes that will return different results, based on
which of the embedded QR codes the reader detects. This could problematise detection
of fraud as the fraudulent link may only be returned in a fraction of cases [23].
Another attack technique using QR codes is the use of SQL or command injections. This
can be employed in cases where the data read from a QR code is to be stored in a
relational database. In case the reader lacks proper input sanitation, injections included
in the QR code data can alter, drop or manipulate database [24]. Command injection
is also an attack derived from improper input sanitation in a reader, but would affect the
device from which the QR code is scanned, instead of a database.
As QR codes often contain links that open in a browser, it is inevitable to mention com­
mon browser based exploits such as cross site scripting (XSS), cross­site request forgery
(CSRF) or session hijacking, that also poses a risk when scanning unknown QR codes.
While these well known web­based exploits can be protected against, more complex
browser based attacks, with the intent to stealthily propagate malware, has taken place
before. In 2016, human rights defender, Ahmed Mansoor, was close to being a victim of
Israeli NSO Groups 4 sophisticated spyware, Pegasus. By chaining zero­day vulnerabil­
ities, Ahmed Mansoor’s iPhone 6 would have been the subject of a complete take over,
merely by visiting a link from a text message [25]. The chain of exploits, later named
”Trident” by The Citizen Lab, got initial foothold by exploiting CVE­2016­4657[26], a vul­
nerability in the Apple iOS Webkit, allowing an adversary to execute arbitrary code [27].
QR code­based attacks are not limited to browser based exploits. Many mobile appli­
cations support custom URL schemes allowing app­to­app communication [28][29]. This
expands the attack surface drastically, as it allows crafting malicious links for other in­
stalled mobile applications.

Related Work
This chapter will cover previous work related to the research conducted in this thesis. The
referenced work will cover physical experiments with USB drives and QR codes, novel QR
code­ and USB­based attacks as well as proposals to mitigate the risk of these. Further­
more, the chapter includes related work focusing on the security awareness, knowledge
and behavior of employees and students.
Related research has been sought out through a research resource hub provided by DTU.
To gather relevant articles of interest, search parameters such as ”usb security”, ”usb
attack”, ”malicious usb”, ”usb dropping”, ”qr code phishing”, ”qr code security”, ”malicious
qr code”, ”qr code attack” as well as combinations of these have been used to query DTU
Findit [30].
In addition to DTU Findit search queries, snowballing have been used. Snowballing is
an information gathering method that helps in the process of discovering relevant articles
and work. Snowballing is accomplished by reading citations and references of articles,
to discover related research. While snowballing initially is meant as an alternative to
database searches when gathering articles for systematic literature reviews, it is also
a very effective method when discovering related work, as related work and literature
reviews, to some degree, share core contents [31].
Claes Wohlin [31] describes both backward and forward snowballing, meaning the pro­
cess of reading references in a given article as well as finding new articles citing the given
article.
Backwards snowballing have been used extensively, however forward snow­
balling have only been used to the extent that DTU Findit [30] provided links to third party
websites providing an overview of other articles, citing the article in examination.
3.1
USB Peripheral­based Research
3.1.1
Physical USB­based Attacks
In 2015, CompTIA [32] published a white paper reporting of workers’ habits in regards
to cybersecurity habits. In conjunction with an online survey collecting information about
1,200 full­time U.S employees, CompTIA conducted a social experiment where 200 un­
branded flash drives were placed in e.g. airports, coffee shops and public squares of
major cities. The files on the flash drives were text files containing a link and an e­mail.
CompTIA found that 17% of the flash drives were plugged in and had the stored files
opened as well as the link visited or the e­mail contacted [32].
Jeffrey Jacobs [33] and Matthew Tischer [34] have both written their master theses revolv­
ing around the anecdotal USB dropping attack. While it hasn’t been possible to access
the thesis of Jeffrey Jacobs, an article based on Matthew Tischers work was published in
2016. The paper by Tischer et al. [35] reported of an experiment where 297 flash drives
were dropped on the Urbana­Champaign campus of the University of Illinois. The flash
drives that were dropped, contained HTML files disguised as PDF, DOCX and JPG­files.
Their experiment showed that 290 (98%) of the flash drives were removed from their orig­
inal location, while 135 (45%) were plugged in and had one or more files were opened.
Furthermore, Tischer et al. did not find that the appearance of the flash drives, ie. attach­
ments such as keys or labels, increased the success of the attacks. In connection with
the experiment, the finders of the flash drives were prompted to answer a survey regard­

primarily motivated by altruism and curiosity [35].
In 2023, Hockenbarger et al. [36] published the article Lost and found: testing cyberse­

Tischer et al. [35] in 2016. The study deployed flash drives with similar naming semantics,

3.1.2
USB­based Attacks
Several articles have been published regarding the security of the Universal Serial Bus
standard. For example, in 2014, Noel et al. [15] presented how a regular flash drive could
be modified to present itself as a HID such as a keyboard. The HID could then input a
series of arbitrary keystrokes to extract data or place malware on the device [15]. This kind
of attack was to be known as BadUSB and has been the foundation for hacking tools such
as the USB Rubber Ducky1, which Cannoles and Ghafarian [37] used in combination with
mimikatz2 to automatically extract user credentials from the Windows 7 and 10 operating
systems [37].
Aranha and Lopes [38] propose a novel idea for data extraction of air­gapped machines,
using a maliciously constructed peripheral that, when connected to a device, transmits
data through blinking infrared LEDs [38]. Other research suggests using the Arduino Mi­
cro3 to run a script that automatically downloads and executes malware on a device [39][40].
Nissim et al. [12] published a survey in 2017 where they proposed a taxonomy of three
major categories in which 29 USB­based were classified under [12]. In 2018, Tian et
al. [41] published a survey on both USB­based attacks and defenses and categorises
these. Futhermore, they developed a formal verification of the USB Type­C authentication
specification and find that the specification design has fundamental flaws. Lastly Mam­
chenko and Sabanov [42] investigated the current state of the taxonomy studies when it
comes to USB­based attacks.
3.1.3
USB­based Security Enhancements
Many proposals of mitigating malicious USB peripherals has been presented over the
years. In 2015, Tian et al. [43] proposed GoodUSB, an architecture that allocates per­
missions to the connected device, based on the users expectations of functionality. In
2016, Angel et. at. [44] proposed Cinch, a technique using virtualisation to separate the
host machine and USB peripherals. In 2017, Kang and Saiedian [45] proposed USB­
Wall, which instead of utilising virtualisation, uses a low cost open source computer to
analyse the USB peripherals on behalf of the host machine. Similarly, Loe et al. [46] pro­
posed SandUSB, an hardware­based mediation between USB peripherals and the user’s
device, permitting the user to scan and analyse the USB to identify malicious USB periph­
erals. Also in 2016, Tian et al. [47] presented USBFILTER, a USB packet filtering method,
allowing to control permissions in a firewall­like manner, managing the access on a per
application level.
In 2017, Hernandez et al. [48] suggested FirmUSB, a firmware analysis framework that
analyses firmware images of USB drives on a binary level. This enables the detection
of malicious activity in the 8051 micro­controller firmware. Erdin et al. [49] suggested
a threat detection and prevention framework that with the use of a FPGA board, detects

3.2
QR Code­based Research
3.2.1
Physical QR Code Attacks
In 2013, Vidas et al. [55] posted QR code posters on 139 different locations (104 loca­
tions on campus, 35 off campus) and over four weeks, the posters were scanned by 225
individuals, whom after scanning the QR code, also visited the accompanying website.
In 2022, Sharevski et al. [56] carried out an experiment similar to the one conducted by
Vidas et al. [55], but instead of posting QR code posters in public places, 173 partici­
pants were prompted to select a method of signing up for a COVID­19 digital passport
trial. The prompting was done through a malicious QR code placed on a fake Centers of
Disease Control (CDC) poster. The study showed that 67% used either their Facebook
or Google accounts to sign up, 18,5% chose to create a new account and 14,5% chose to
skip sign up. The willingness to use non related credentials shows incautiousness among
experiment subjects.
3.2.2
QR Code­based Attacks
The security of QR codes have also gained attention over the years. In 2010, Kieseberg et
al. [57] published ”QR code security”, raising concerns of ways in which QR codes poses
a security risk. In their work, they explain attacks such as buffer under­ and overflow
by manipulating the character count indicator, SQL­ and command injections on reader
software, cross site scripting (XSS) attacks, and the risk of social engineering attacks,
stating that ”Leaving a poster of a QR Code on the parking lot of a company (instead of
the traditional attack with an USB drive) offering discount in a nearby restaurant is a new
attack vector which is likely to be successful” [57]. In 2014, Dabrowski et al. [23] presented
a novel QR code attack that worked by intentionally constructing QR codes embedding
another QR code. The attack is referred to as barcode­in­barcode attacks and combines
nested QR codes that either comply with the same two­dimensional barcode standard or
two different ones.
3.2.3
QR Code­based Security Enhancements
Several security enhancing frameworks and applications aiming to secure the use of QR
codes have been proposed. Yao and Shin. [58] suggested SafeQR, that with the use of
security APIs, allows the user to detect phishing and malware attacks. Mavroeidis and
Nicho [59] proposed QRCS (quick response code secure), a client­server architecture
using cryptographically secure hashing functions and digital signatures, to ensure the
authenticity and integrity.

Superior Architecture
The conducted experiments took departure in previous research by Tischer et al. [35],

JSON Web Tokens are a standardised and popular format, used to transfer claims be­
tween parties. JWTs were chosen as a transport medium to eliminate the risk of unau­
thorized database manipulation and pollution. By using JSON Web Signatures (JWS)

authenticity. JWSs permits signing and verifying JWTs both symmetrically and asymmet­
rically, and to keep the size of the URLs to a minimum, a keyed­hash message authenti­
cation code (HMAC) using SHA256 was chosen to secure the JWT [67] [68].

4.4
Server Application
A server was put at our disposal by DTU in order to receive and store notifications invoked
from honey tokens. To minimise the risk of the traffic getting dropped by local or internal
firewalls, it was decided to avoid using non standard ports for notifying the server. The
server was placed on the internal DTU network and had ports 80/tcp, and 443/tcp opened
to incoming external traffic. Furthermore, the server was assigned the IP 130.225.69.132
while SSH connections from the internal network was allowed on port 22/tcp.
The fully qualified domain name (FQDN), ”usb­honeypot.compute.dtu.dk”, was set to point
to the servers IP. In addition, the domain name, ”pid.dk”, was purchased and set to
point to the server IP as well.
An anonymous and short domain with a .dk top level
domain (TLD) was considered an advantageous approach in comparison to using ”usb­
honeypot.compute.dtu.dk”, as the appearance of ”usb” and ”honeypot” might alert users
scanning QR codes or following links.
The web server receiving the data was written in the Python3 6 programming language
using the Flask 7 framework for building web applications. The web application itself was
powered with the Green Unicorn (Gunicorn) Python WSGI HTTP server listening on port
5000 for incoming connections. The traffic was encrypted over TLS to minimise the risks
and complications in cases of organisational strict HTTPS only policies. Nginx was used
as a TLS termination proxy, listening on port 80 and 443 and proxy­passing incoming
connections to the Gunicorn server. In case there were incoming connections using port
80, they would be redirected to port 443 with a prefixed HTTPS scheme. A certificate

5
Results
This chapter outlines the concrete experiment implementation followed by a presenta­
tion of the experiment results. The quantitative data, collected through monitored exper­
iments, is analysed to gain a fundamental statistical overview of tendencies and corre­
lations and will have been scrutinised to remove duplicates and false positives. For the
USB drive experiments, data entries that are a result of someone clicking a link in DOCX
or XLSX file that has already notified the server have been removed. Cases where links
in DOCX or XLSX files are clicked, the accompanying URL scans from Microsoft Coop­
eration have also been removed.
Similarly in the QR code scan experiments, scan registrations that are conducted within
the same second (one occasion of 1.05 seconds difference between registrations as well
as one occasion where a MacBook had repeatedly refreshed the URL) and where as­
sociated metadata matches, i.e. User-Agent,ISP, ID etc., have also been reduced to a
single registration entry. Multiple registrations can be caused by the QR code scanning
application visiting the URL individually from the user or if a user decides to share a URL
over e.g. WhatsApp or Facebook. In a few occasions, the same QR code link was vis­
ited within the same second, and where the API data showed the same ISP, however
the User­Agents were different (iPhone and MacBook). As IPs were not saved during the
experiment, it is impossible to conclude that these scans originate from the same user
and thus they are left in the dataset as separate entries. Several occasions exist, where
a QR code link is visited with a few seconds interval from what looks like the same user.
These are considered re­scans of QR codes or refreshes of the pages and are also left
in.

5.5
User­Agent Analysis
This section examines the different User­Agents registered from the QR code experi­
ments. In particular, the analysis briefly covers the distribution of operation systems and
browsers, before presenting a vulnerability assessment, listing the count of security flaws
affecting the Safari browser versions on Apple iPhones (iOS).
Data of Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures (CVE) affecting Safari versions are gath­
ered through the National Vulnerability Database (NVD), managed by the National Insti­
tute of Standards and Technology (NIST). To ensure an accurate presentation, all CVEs
registered after the day where the last scan was conducted in the respective experiments,
have been discarded. To query the API, the Common Platform Enumeration (CPE) 2.3
naming scheme have been used as well as a freely obtained API key. CPE is described
by NIST as ”a structured naming scheme for information technology systems, software,
and packages” [75]. Only CPEs specifying the Safari application (cpe:2.3:a:apple:safari)
have been used and thus this section does not investigate the total vulnerabilities affecting
the various operating systems. To evaluate the severity of each vulnerability, the Com­
mon Vulnerability Scoring System 3.1 (CVSS) has been used. The CVSS assesses the
severity of a vulnerability by comparing parameters such as the attack vector, attack com­
plexity as well as the impact on confidentiality, integrity and availability. The CVSS uses
a scale of 0 to 10, where 0.1­3.9 is considered low, 4.0­6.9 is considered medium, 7.0­8.9
is considered high and 9.0­10.0 is considered critical [76].

6
Discussion
Cybersecurity awareness in the public domain is a complex field of research. Nonetheless
it is an important one. Considerations of multiple parameters have been made in order
to collect useful data in a responsible and meaningful way. From designing the overall
architecture, including visual representation, context, file types and names, to a ensuring
a responsible, proper and respectful enlightenment of the experiment subject. For this
study, ethical and moral considerations have been a substantial part of conducting the
experiments. In addition to the permissions to conduct experiments, it was important not
to harm experiment subjects, as they essentially have been participating in the experi­
ments without consenting. Therefore, sustaining the privacy of the individuals has been
prioritised along with providing thorough information of the experiments.
Inevitably, revealing the execution of experiments to experiment subjects can impact the
accuracy of data collected, as it can become a topic of conversation in social interactions.
This was seen in the experiment at UFM, however it is believed to be the responsible
way of conducting such experiments. In the USB drive experiments, the accuracy of data
collected can be influenced in multiple ways, as there are several scenarios where the
USB drives could have been plugged in without notifying the server. Cases where the file
contents are examined in text editors, the Windows File Explorer preview or opened in al­
ternative word processors such as LibreOffice Writer or Apache OpenOffice Writer, would
render no way of knowing if a USB drive had been plugged in. Methods to circumvent
these cases, such as using HID­based USB peripherals, could have been implemented
to get notified regardless of operating system and software. This however, was neither
possible nor desired. Conducting larger scale experiments using HID­based USB drives
requires a suitable budget, and even if the funding was raised, it contradicts the ethical
ambitions for the study, as unauthorised execution of keystrokes on individuals’ comput­
ers, would be too invasive. It is also important to mention the risk of adversaries taking
advantage of the experiment execution, by planting actual malicious USB drives and QR
code posters at the experiment locations. The knowledge of the authorised and harmless
experiments among employees, may temporarily reduce the natural state of alertness,
enhancing the attack effectiveness for an adversary.
Considering the easiness of preparing a malicious HID­based USB drives and the fact that
they are widely available and commercially sold, it could seem at first, as if USB­based at­
tacks are substantially more dangerous than QR code­based attacks. An HID­based USB
drive attack allows an adversary to effortlessly run a series of predefined keystrokes on a
target machine, to e.g. download and install ransomware or spyware, or steal confiden­
tial data by extracting or uploading it online. It doesn’t require a comprehensive technical
insight to carry out such attack. The complexity of infecting mobile devices through QR
codes, and with a similar severity as USB­based attacks are capable of, could seem as
if it requires resources in the scale of nation state threat actors. However when putting
context into perspective, results show that QR code­based attacks can be highly effective,
and that they do not necessarily require finding novel zero­day vulnerabilities. The pop­
ularisation of QR codes after the Covid­19 pandemic, propagating the every day usage,
enhances the effectiveness of QR code­based attacks. Entire societies quickly became
accustomed to scanning QR codes in their daily doings[56] and the widespread adop­
tion was particularly helpful in normalising and comforting the usage. As of 2023, QR
codes are even a part of the NemID mobile application used as for two factor authenticat­

==PAGE:59==
ing when accessing governmental IT services, legitimising the usage even further. The
newly accepted way of sharing, interacting and authenticating however, is not entirely
without disadvantages.
For many years employees have been trained to not plug in unknown USB drives as they
pose security risks, but in the time and age of increased digitisation and the accompanying
transition to cloud environments, USB drives are becoming less and less used. Just as
USB drives have been the subject of security research for more than a decade, malicious
use of QR codes are a rising field of research, where the awareness and knowledge of
security risks does not seem to match the cybersecurity awareness level of USB drives.
The broad application of QR codes enables several attack vectors, where a simple attack
using QR codes linking to a phishing page promising some kind of reward, could be imag­
ined to render an attack rather effective. The lack of cybersecurity awareness in regards
to QR codes could also be partially attributable to the lack media covered catastrophic
cybersecurity incidents like Stuxnet was.
It is expected that the most common method of awareness training employees, is by con­
ducting planned and authorised phishing campaigns. Some of the experiment locations
even reported that only phishing mails have been used to awareness train the employees
and that the alternative experiments with physical artefacts have been valuable to the cy­
bersecurity awareness level of employees. Results show that individuals, as opposed to
USB drives, are not necessarily trained to keep a critical distance to publicly displayed QR
codes. While phishing campaigns are a simple and effective way to educate employees
on cybersecurity awareness, it is believed to be beneficial, to train employees on multiple
types cybersecurity threats, including physical threats. QR codes are easily mass pro­
duced on stickers and posters, embodied in phishing mails and modified or replaced on
locations already present. Along with the tendency to not keep software up to date, as
the results reveal, the natural employee trust and altruism in conjunction with the societal
acceptance of QR code usage, renders these kind of attacks a serious threat. The results
underline the necessity to keep a scepticism when handling digital media; something that
many forget, as digital systems such a large part of our every day. It also underlines the
importance of keeping software updated, patching vulnerabilities of both mobile applica­
tions and operating systems.
While QR codes could be considered to pose a serious threat to information security, it
is difficult to compare QR code­based attacks to USB­based attacks directly. The effec­
tiveness and threat of each type of attack has multiple variables that play a significant
role in assessing the severity. From the adversary’s point of view, it can be crucial what
the objective of the attack is, as well as the amount of resources available to the adver­
sary. Gathering intelligence information such as device types, IP addresses and browser
fingerprints, inevitably requires less resources than chaining multiple zero­day vulnerabil­
ities together in order to infect users with sophisticated spyware. Considering the same
objectives, but by utilising USB drives as an attack mechanism, it could be argued that
the reconnaissance would be less efficient, but that it in terms of resources, would be
within easier reach to infect user devices. Subsequently do multiple parameters play a
role when considering the target location of a potential attack. Defense mechanisms such
as disabled USB ports, software based virus scan of peripherals, company wide managed
device solutions, stateful firewalls, no mobile device policies, atypical operating systems
and physical access restrictions could be instituted to hinder and complicate such attacks.
Threats and risks of USB­ and QR code­based attacks really are a unique assessment
based on environmental factors.

6.1
Future Work
As a continuation of this study, it would be interesting to investigate the various iOS ver­
sions used to scan the QR codes, and identify associated vulnerabilities. In continuation,
the various Android devices and browser versions could be investigated and mapped to
known vulnerabilities, in order to acquire a more accurate depiction of the effectiveness of
QR code­based attacks. From a techno­anthropological point of view, it would be interest­
ing to conduct qualitative interviews with IT departments and directions, to examine what
kind of, if any, preparations and defense mechanisms have already been deployed, to
counter real world attacks of both USB drives and QR codes. For example, an employee
from UFM informed that during the experiments, they realised that they had no official
procedure for handling lost and found USB drives. Furthermore, an IT security employee
at TS used a specific peripheral malware scanner on an air­gapped machine, to scan one
of the USB drives, before the experiment was initiated. Additionally, it would be inter­
esting to ask the experiment subjects about their motivation for scanning QR codes and
plugging in USB drives, as is seen done in previous related studies.
The data collected throughout this study has also piqued an interest in the privacy aspect
of both QR code scanners and communication solutions offered by big tech companies.
Multiple times, HTTP GET requests were sent by User­Agents shortly before or after a
legitimate user visited a link. Among the User­Agents were ”facebookexternalhit/1.1”: A
web crawler used to gather, cache and display information of websites shared on Face­
book 1. Multiple requests were also received from Microsoft IPs, revealing a peculiar
request pattern around the time where a link in a DOCX or XLSX file was opened. In a
short time span, Microsoft IPs would at first request the link from a ”null” User­Agent in
one country, and then request it again from an IP in a different country. Only this time
with a ”Mozilla/5.0 (Windows NT 10.0; Win64; x64) AppleWebKit/537.36 (KHTML, like
Gecko) Chrome/102.0.5005.63 Safari/537.36” User­Agent, suggesting that the link was
opened in a Chrome browser on a Windows 10 desktop computer. Other occasions of
this strange request pattern shows inconsistency in the Chrome browser version and the
amount of delay between the first and the second request. The relatively small time delay
between the legitimate user­invoked request and the the odd requests from Microsoft IPs,
indicates that the Microsoft requests were sent automatically. It would be interesting to
dig further in to why the request is sent several times from different IPs and with differ­
ent User­Agents. A qualified guess is that Microsoft attempts to circumvent User­Agent
restrictions by imitating a legitimate user. Based on the data, it is clear that keeping or
sharing secret links in both documents edited with the Microsoft Office suite as well as in
Facebook Messenger chats is not favourable.
6.2
Limitations
In the design and implementation phase of the study, it was considered whether PDF files
should be a part of the honey tokens placed on USB drives. Thinkst do include the source
code to generate PDF honey tokens in their repository. In the testing phase it was found
that the wide variety of PDF readers would be disadvantageous for the collected data, as
many of these did not notify the server. And when the PDF honey token did notify the
server, e.g. in Adobe Acrobat Reader, a warning dialog box would appear, informing the
user, that the document was trying to connect to an external source. In comparison to
honey tokens using the Office Open XML format, PDF files were found to be too much of
an uncertainty in terms of successfully registering data.

As mentioned, limitations are also present in the design and implementation of the ex­
periments, as scenarios where USB drives are plugged in and even have files opened,
without notying the server, is a possibility. This could happen if a user decided to open a
DOCX or XLSX file with protected view enabled, but without enabling editing or following
the link inside the document. Subsequently, the preview feature in the Windows File Ex­
plorer may have caused experiment subjects to become aware of the experiment without
the server being notified, as highlighting an HTML file would in some cases preview the file
contents within the Windows File Explorer. This would not notify the server. Furthermore,
due to limited resources and access restrictions, it hasn’t been possible to follow up on
the amount of USB drives and QR code posters removed from their original placements.
Finding participants has also been a difficult challenge of this study. In general, compa­
nies show an interest in the experiments, but are reluctant when discussing the publication
of the results. One company, specialising in cybersecurity, had initially agreed to partic­
ipate, but withdrew as results could implicitly be connected to their name, because they
wanted to stay anonymous but at the same time wanted to be mentioned in the thesis.
Conducting experiments at a company specialising in cybersecurity was interesting to us,
and therefore a similar company was also contacted, but did not respond. Furthermore,
we’ve been in dialogue with two major banks in Denmark. The head of security awareness
in one of the banks informed us that they already had red teaming in place and that they
could not participate. The other replied that the USB drive experiment would not work on
their company computers, but that the QR code experiment sounded interesting to them.
For some of the experiment locations, clearances were needed from several divisions,
and thus the preparation time included not only meetings orchestrating and planning the
experiments, but also filling in applications requesting permission from various depart­
ments. This turned out to be time consuming and thus the experiments were delayed.
6.3
Reflections
The design and implementation of the generation of USB drive files, QR code links and
the server side application, was changed after the experiments were concluded at UFM.
In the original design, the generation of files would construct a JSON file, that the server
would use to verify each of the requests received, ensuring that the ID, group and file name
were valid parameters and thus exclude false positives. The utilisation of JSON files for
looking up valid requests meant that the Gunicorn workers, i.e. number of OS processes
for handling requests, was set to only 1, to avoid file corruptions in simultaneous requests.
This also meant that the server could be brought down with a denial of service attack
rather easily. With the scale of the DTU experiments and possible curious minds taken
in to consideration, precautionary measures against such attacks, both intentionally and
unintentionally, were implemented with the JWT URL parameter. Additionally, the Flask­
Limiter2 library was included to rate limit each IP to 5 requests per minute. This allowed
the worker count to be raised, handling a larger amount of traffic.
In hindsight, the USB drive experiments at UFM could have been conducted differently.
Under different circumstances, where the possibility and resources from both parties were
available, it is believed that the experiment would have benefited from being conducted
over a longer period. While the size of the office allowed 40 USB drives to be distributed
without creating a stir, the placement of these could have been spread over several days or
weeks as was done in the USB drive experiment at TS. This too is applicable for the USB
drive experiment at DTU and would most likely have limited the amount of attention the
USB drives gained from being placed over night. As seen in the internal message sent to

7
Conclusion
This study has aimed to assess the cybersecurity awareness in the public domain. In
particular it has been sought out to what extend, the anecdotal story of dropping mali­
ciously prepared USB drives, as well as the placement of QR code posters in private and
public common spaces, is beneficial for adversaries in 2024. The study concludes that
both attack types poses a significant threat, but also that each attack type thrive in diverse
attack scenarios. For objectives aiming to download and install malware, or extract data
from a user machine, maliciously programmed USB peripherals are a viable method. QR
codes, on the other hand, are limited to the specifications of the Hypertext Transfer Pro­
tocol (RFC 2616), meaning that similar attacks would require some kind of vulnerability in
the software used to scan QR codes and access the link. The study also shows that a sig­
nificant amount of mobile devices used to scan QR codes are vulnerable to one or more
security flaws, rendering these kind of attacks possible. Additionally, the study shows
that the usage of USB drives are on a decline and therefore the likelihood of using these
as successful attack vectors is becoming less attractive. Additionally, the study shows
that the increasing popularity of QR codes renders these an effective approach of getting
the attention of passer­by. This makes it a prosperous approach for conducting phishing
attacks, reconnoitre or exploiting known mobile device vulnerabilities.
The USB usage decline, as well as the increased attentiveness of these, are reflected
in the experiment results. 10% of the USB drives were plugged in at the Copenhagen
office of TS, while 2.5% were plugged in at UFM and 11.18% were plugged in at DTU.
From a security perspective, this is still an alarming result. However, for some of the
experiment locations, the employees and IT authority responses show a reasonable level
of cybersecurity awareness. The USB drive experiment at MS showed that employees
demonstrated a high level of altruism, but with few or no concerns of a potential attack
happening. The decrease in the usage of USB drives, as well as the assumed increase
in awareness hereof, transpires when comparing the results to the studies conducted by
Tischer et al. [35] and Hockenbarger et al. [36], where 48%­98% and 23%­33% of placed
USB drives were plugged in, respectively. With this kept in mind, the data collected may
also bear a mark of the time span differences, in which the USB drives were distributed.
Placing all USB drives at the same time for both UFM and DTU may have caused an
adverse amount of attention. In contrast to the cybersecurity awareness level of USB
drives, very few, if any, reports of the QR code poster were, to the best of our knowledge,
brought to a level of concern. A desire to uncritically scan QR codes was seen across all
experiment locations, and the amount of vulnerable devices show that QR codes in could
in fact cause severe security incidents at larger corporations. In comparison the more than
10 year old study by Vidas et al. [55], where QR code posters at 139 different locations
were scanned by 225 individuals, the results show, except for the QR code experiment
UFM, a larger poster­to­scan ratio. This reflects the increasing QR code popularity and
acceptance, and thus also the enlarged attack surface.
In conclusion, attacks utilising both USB peripherals and QR codes, poses a threat in
2024. The study shows, that USB­based attacks can be more effective in workplaces
where there is less or no social activity, and more effective in large scale workplaces,
where departments are physically separated. It is expected that the decrease in amount
USB drives plugged in, is due to several factors, where one of them is the ascending
usage of cloud storage. While the level of cybersecurity awareness in regards to USB
Assessing Cybersecurity Awareness in the Public Domain
55
==PAGE:64==
drives is found to be reasonable for several of the experiment locations, the cybersecurity
awareness of malicious QR codes are close to non existing. Attacks using USB­ and QR
code­based attacks are difficult to compare directly, but it is concluded that each suitable
for different attack objectives and usage of neither are free of risks. The availability and
commercialisation of programmable micro controllers drastically simplifies USB­based at­
tacks while the common occurrence of QR codes opens a significant large attack surface.
The high utilisation of mobile browsers vulnerable to high or critical security flaws, shows
that the willingness to interact with foreign media is likely to be exploitable, and underlines
the importance of vigilance and alertness when interacting with foreign digital media, as
well as the significance of keeping software up to date.

A
QR Code and USB Dropping Instruction
A.1
Introduktion
Mit navn er Johannes Nordskov og jeg er i gang med at skrive mit speciale på DTU.
Først of fremmest vil jeg gerne takke for din og Trafikstyrelsens samarbejdsvillighed og
engagement i de eksperimenter, som jeg skal lave i forbindelse med mit speciale.
Du er måske allerede er informeret om, at jeg skriver jeg om awareness af cyber­ og infor­
mationssikkerhed i det offentlige rum og i den forbindelse laver en række eksperimenter.
Eksperimenterne består af, at “tabe” USB­stik i offentlige såvel som private fællesom­
råder, for indsamle data om, hvor mange af disse der indsættes i medarbejderes comput­
ere. Ydermere laver jeg et eksperiment, hvor der opsættes plakater med QR­koder, for
at indsamle data om, om domænerne som QR­koderne linker til besøges.
På USB­stikkene vil der kunne findes Word­, Excel­ og HTML­dokumenter, som underret­
ter mig hvis de åbnes. Der vil ikke være nogen former for ondsindet kode på USB­stikkene
og de indeholder samtidig information om eksperimentet, som finderen kan læse når en fil
åbnes. Ligeledes vil scanning af QR­koderne lede til en webside med samme information.
Data der indsamles ifm. eksperimentet vil udelukkende være til rådighed for mine ve­
jledere og jeg selv. De anonymiseres/censureres i specialet og slettes efter aflevering.
Det er frivilligt om Trafiksytrelsen ønsker at blive nævnt i specialet.
Eksperimentet har til formål at indsamle vigtig data til forskning, som der ikke findes megen
af i forvejen. Samtidig er det en oplagt mulighed for Trafikstyrelsen til, at få indsigt i dens
medarbejderes awareness­niveau inden for cybersikkerhed og fysiske sikkerhedstrusler.
Det er desuden også en god lejlighed til at højne awareness­niveauet samt uddanne
medarbejdere i at håndtere sikkerhedsbrister.
A.2
Plakater med QR­koder
Du vil modtage to plakater med QR­koder. Plakaterne informerer om, at der er en lodtrækn­
ing om 5.000 kr. til årets julefrokost og at man kan tilmelde sig ved at scanne en QR­kode.
QR­koden linker til et en side på et domæne (pid.dk) som ikke er forbundet med Trafik­
styrelsen. Hvis man besøger siden som QR­koden linker til, vil man kunne læse en tekst
om eksperimentet der udføres, at det ikke er farligt, hvordan man skal forholde sig samt
kontaktinformation på mine vejledere og mig.
Det er op til dig om du hænger en eller to plakater op, men jeg vil naturligvis gerne in­
formeres herom, da det kan have indflydelse på dataene der indsamles. Jeg vil også
gerne, hvis muligt, modtage billeder af ophængte plakater som jeg kan bruge i min rap­
port.
Eksperimentet med QR­koder skal løbe over en uge, med afsæt i hvornår jeres julefrokost
afholdes. Lisbeth vil undersøge datoen nærmere og vi vender tilbage med et starttid­
spunkt.

A.3
Efterladte USB­Stik
Udover de to plakater modtager du også en pose med 10 USB­stik. 5 af USB­stikkene er
med vedhæng i form af nøgler og labels og de resterende 5 er uden vedhæng.
Indholdet på alle USB­stik vil være ens for det blottede øje, men har forskellige iden­
titeter, så jeg kan indsamle data om, hvor mange af USB­stikkene der åbnes filer fra. Der
forefindes to mapper med en række filer i hver og når disse åbnes vil jeg blive underrettet.
Eksperimentet løber over 1 uge med planlagt start d. 6. december.
• Tidligt d. 6. december (inden medarbejdere møder ind) placeres nogle af USB­
stikkene tilfældige steder på kontoret. F.eks. parkeringspladsen, kopirummet, kan­
tinen, håndvaske, gangen eller gulvet er oplagte stedet at “glemme” eller “tabe”
dem. Der er ikke en fast plan for mængde og placering over de forskellige dage,
men det må gerne se så naturligt ud som muligt. Med dette menes, at de 10 USB­
stik f.eks. ikke placeres i samme rum og/eller på samme tid. Det er også muligt
at placere dem sent på dagen tirsdag d. 5., såfremt at rengøringen ikke kommer i
løbet af aftenen/natten. Hovedpointen er, at ingen skal se dem blive placeret :­)
• Resten af USB­stikkene placeres i takt med at de andre samles op, så der hele
tiden er nogle USB stik placeret, uden at det bliver unaturligt og de hober sig op.
Eksperimentet slutter onsdag d. 13. december om eftermiddagen.
• Med forbehold for at du også har et arbejde at passe, ville det være enormt hjælp­
somt, hvis du kunne tælle sammen hvor mange af de 10 USB­stik der er blevet
samlet op og hvor mange der er efterladt.
• Der står som information i filerne, at man gerne må aflevere USB­stikkene til IT­
afdelingen. Derfor kan det være en god idé at informere en enkelt person i IT­
afdelingen eller receptionen på forhånd.
• Det er vigtigt at USB­stikkene ikke placeres steder hvor folk der ikke arbejder i Trafik­
styrelsen kan finde dem. F.eks. receptionen i kontorfællesskab eller på delte park­
eringspladser.
• Du er velkommen til at kigge på filerne på USB­stikkene inden eksperimentet går
i gang. Dog håber jeg at du vil kopiere filerne over på din egen computer før de
åbnes, da det ændrer datoen for hvornår filen sidst er tilgået, hvis de åbnes direkte
på USB­stikket og dermed vil ændre på eksperimentet.
