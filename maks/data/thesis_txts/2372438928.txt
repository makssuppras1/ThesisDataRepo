Abstract 
 
Hydrocarbons are ubiquitous in the environment. Crude oil and petroleum products, which consists 
mainly of hydrocarbons, are known for causing environmental problems in the environment. Biological 
degradation, known as biodegradation, is one of the most important removal processes of hydrocarbons 
in the environment. Hydrocarbons are known for entering surface waters, and their susceptibility towards 
biodegradation, in these media, is therefore of great importance. A new biodegradation test, done at The 
Technical University of Denmark (DTU), has tested 53 hydrocarbons, covering the chemical spectrum oil 
and petroleum products. The hydrocarbons were tested at environmentally realistic conditions, at ng-
µg/L levels, well below their solubility. Aqueous solutions with a mixture of the hydrocarbons were diluted 
in three different surface water inocula: lake water, seawater and activated sludge filtrate. The inocula 
differentiates in concentration of nutrients and level of pre-exposure to the hydrocarbons. Half-lives of 
the hydrocarbons were generated using first order biodegradation rates. The median half-lives in lake 
water, seawater and activated sludge filtrate, separated by inoculum, were 17, 3.7 and 1.3 days, 
respectively. The linear alkanes were found to biodegrade fastest, whereas the high-molecular-weight 
(HMW) aromatics were most recalcitrant, with branched alkanes, cyclic alkanes and low-molecular weight 
(LMW) aromatics in-between, depending on the statistical method. The biodegradability of the 
hydrocarbons in the DTU study were compared to estimated half-lives by BioHCwin, a widely-used 
estimation program by the United States Environmental Protection Agency. The half-lives were mostly 
within a factor of 10, or overestimated by BioHCwin. Several hydrocarbons, especially in lake water, had 
undetermined half-lives, i.e. longer than the duration of the DTU study. 6 hydrocarbons, either belonging 
to the cyclic alkanes or the LMW aromatics, had half-lives underestimated by a factor of 10 or more, in at 
least one inoculum. BioHCwin seemed mostly to overestimate the half-lives of hydrocarbons with alkyl 
groups longer than methyl (-CH3). However, also HMW aromatics, such as Benzo(a)pyrene and 
Benzo(a)anthracene, had overestimated half-lives. The half-lives from the DTU study were also compared 
to a previous meta-study by Prosser et al., 2016, where 34 out of 37 compounds, were within a factor of 
10. The European Chemical Agency (ECHA), has made guidelines for evaluating the persistence of 
chemicals in the environment. About 2 out of 3 hydrocarbons in lake water were classified as ‘non-
persistent’, according to the ECHA guidelines. This was the case for 95% of the hydrocarbons in seawater. 
The general difference in half-lives for the hydrocarbons across inoculum in the DTU study, shows the 
importance the inoculum has, when comparing biodegradation data between different studies.

1. Introduction 
 
Petroleum based products from the distillation of crude oil plays a major role in modern civilization with 
an average daily supply of more than 96 million barrels of oil in 2016 (International Energy Agency, 2017). 
We use it as fuels for heating and transportation, such as gasoline and diesel, as well as in the production 
of plastics, pharmaceuticals etc. (Xue, et al., 2015; Das & Chandran, 2011; U.S. Department of Energy, 
2017). It consists mainly of hydrocarbons (HCs), but organic compounds containing sulphur, oxygen and 
nitrogen are also present in smaller amounts (Radovic, et al., 2012; Helmenstine, 2017; Prosser, et al., 
2016; Howard, et al., 2005). It is estimated that hydrocarbons from crude oil and petroleum products are 
the biggest cause of contamination in the environment, including water and soil pollution, by the 
unintentional release of millions of litres each year, through anthropogenic sources as well as natural 
seepage (Das & Chandran, 2011; Leahy & Colwell, 1990; Wang, et al., 2016; Head, et al., 2006; Wang & 
Fingas, 2003). 
 
1.1 Hydrocarbon composition and grouping 
Crude oil and petroleum products consists of complex mixtures, varying depending on origin, with more 
than 17,000 different compounds identified (Head, et al., 2006; Howard, et al., 2005). Due to the complex 
composition, crude oil is considered to be substances of Unknown or Variable composition, Complex 
reaction products or Biological materials, UVBCs, (ECHA, 2016). There are several different ways used 
when dividing crude oil into subgroups, but the most common classified subgroups are aliphatic/paraffinic 
(which can both be linear, n, and branched, i), cyclic aliphatic (known as naphthenes), aromatic and 
asphaltene /resin groups (Howard, et al., 2005; Helmenstine, 2017; Wang, et al., 2016). Great variation in 
the chemical composition of crude oil is seen across the world (Radovic, et al., 2012), but an approximate 
average composition is listed to be around 57% aliphatic/saturated HCs, 29% aromatic HCs and 14% 
asphaltenes/resins (Prince & Walters, 2007). 
 
Some compounds may contain both a naphthenic and aromatic group, and an aromatic or naphthene 
compound may contain one or more linear or branched aliphatic groups. This of course further 
complicates a clear separation between the different subgroups. 
 
1.2 Environmental effects of hydrocarbon contamination 
Major oil spills due to human errors, such as the Exxon Valdez and Deepwater Horizon accidents, caused 
millions of litres of oil to leak into the marine environments (The Ocean Portal Team , 2016; Xue, et al., 
2015). These accidents, have gathered great attention due to the major negative impact that these 
releases can cause on the marine environment, as well as human health (Xue, et al., 2015; Wang, et al., 
2016; Wang & Fingas, 2003). Large oil spills are known to create anaerobic zones with the ability to destroy 
affected flora and fauna, as well as causing hypothermia to marine mammals and birds by damaging their 
fur (Xue, et al., 2015; Wang, et al., 2016). Longer lasting effects on the ecosystem, as well as human beings, 
can also be caused by the toxicity of the crude oil compounds, many known to be cardiotoxic, teratogenic, 
mutagenic and carcinogenic (Block & Scholz, 2014; Zhou, et al., 2013).

1.3 This report 
Due to the toxicity of hydrocarbons mentioned, information on their fate in the environment, including 
degradation, is therefore of great importance in proper risk assessment when accidents occur (Birch, et 
al., 2017a; Aronson, et al., 2006; Wang & Fingas, 2003). Screening tests of the biodegradability of 
compounds by OECD, have been developed, and are widely used, for determining the biodegradability of 
compounds (OECD, 1992; Aronson, et al., 2006; European Chemicals Agency, 2014). Guidelines on the 
classification on the persistence of compounds in the environment, using the half-life of a compound, is 
set by the European Chemical Agency, ECHA (European Chemicals Agency, 2014).  As data on 
biodegradability do not exists for all compounds, models have been developed, and for hydrocarbons, 
BioHCwin is the prevailing one (Howard, et al., 2005; European Commission, 2003; Prosser, et al., 2016). 
Actual biodegradation tests are however always preferable. A new study by DTU (Birch, et al., In prep.), 
not yet published, tested the biodegradability of 53 hydrocarbons in three different surface water inocula 
at environmentally low concentrations, well under solubility. This data is relevant for assessing the 
biodegradation, and thereby the risk assessment, of hydrocarbons, many of which are found in petroleum 
products (CONCAWE, 2012). 
 
The focus of this report is to analyse the biodegradation data found in the DTU study as well as compare 
it to the classifications by OECD and ECHA. Furthermore, a comparison to the half-lives estimated in 
BioHCwin and their half-life ratio in respect to chemical parameters will be done. A short comparison 
between the DTU study and a previous meta-analysis by Prosser, et al. 2016, will also be done. The analysis 
done is this report could highlight possible disagreements between the DTU study and BioHCwin and 
thereby aid in the fine-tuning of BioHCwin. 
 
1.4 Hypotheses 
The work in this report is based on the following hypotheses, based on previously mentioned information, 
unless noted: 
 
• 
The hydrocarbons will degrade fastest, i.e. have the shortest half-life, in the surface water with 
the greatest microbial population as well as previous exposure (Atlas, 1981). For the surface 
waters used in the DTU study, this will mean that the compounds are degraded fastest in the act. 
sludge filtrate, followed by seawater and then lake water. 
 
• 
The susceptibility of the hydrocarbons to biodegradation is occurring in the following order 
(Olajire & Essien, 2014): n-alkanes > branched alkanes > low-molecular-weight (LMW) aromatics 
> cyclic alkanes > high-molecular-weight (HMW) aromatics. The hydrocarbons in the DTU study 
has therefore been divided into these groups as well as possible by this author (see Table 2 and 
3). 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
 
• 
BioHCwin will, for most compounds, overestimate the half-lives of the hydrocarbons compared 
to the new DTU study. This is based on the concentrations, well below solubility used in the DTU 
study, which might not have been the case for all tests used to make BioHCwin. Some of the 
biodegradation data used for BioHCwin is also based on studies in soil and sediment, were 
biodegradation is known to occur at a slower rate (European Chemicals Agency, 2014). 
 
• 
The classification on biodegradability in surface waters, according to ECHA guidelines, will, for 
most compounds, be correct or have a conservative estimate when comparing to BioHCwin, as 
seen in Prosser, et al., 2016.

2. Theory 
 
2.1 Biodegradation of hydrocarbons 
The main way hydrocarbons are removed in the environment is by biological degradation, known as 
biodegradation (Das & Chandran, 2011). Photooxidation is another important degradation way, whereas 
volatilization and dissolution are important distribution ways for low-molecular-weight hydrocarbons 
(Howard, et al., 2005; Birch, et al., 2017a). It is estimated that hydrocarbons have been ever-present on 
earth, plenty of time for some microorganisms to develop, that utilizes hydrocarbons as a source of carbon 
and energy (Prince & Walters, 2007; Prince, et al., 2010). Microorganisms capable of degrading 
hydrocarbons are ever-present in the marine environment, even in the Arctic region, and more than 175 
genera of bacteria, over 100 genera of fungi as well as several genera of cyanobacteria, yeast and algae, 
have been shown to degrade hydrocarbons (Prince & Walters, 2007; Head, et al., 2006; Prince, et al., 2010; 
McFarlin, et al., 2014; Kostka, et al., 2011; Olajire & Essien, 2014). Bacteria are however estimated to be 
the primary degraders of hydrocarbons in the environment (Das & Chandran, 2011; Varjani & Upasani, 
2013; Leahy & Colwell, 1990). 
  
The fastest way hydrocarbons are biodegraded in the environment is under aerobic conditions, which is 
the focus of this report, where oxygen works as an electron acceptor, providing the highest biochemically 
amount of energy (Das & Chandran, 2011; Widdel & Musat, 2010; Prince & Walters, 2007). The uptake of 
hydrocarbons can occur in several ways: directly into the cell if soluble, through adhesion to hydrocarbon 
droplet, emulsification of the hydrocarbon through a biosurfactant process or by extracellular breakdown 
of hydrocarbons through excreted enzymes (Wentzel, et al., 2007; Rojo, 2009). The degradation of the 
hydrocarbon inside the cell is done by monooxygenases and dioxygenases enzymes, where, respectively, 
one or two oxygen atoms are incorporated into the hydrocarbon, thereby rendering it easier for further 
metabolism (Prince & Walters, 2007; Widdel & Musat, 2010). The alcohol, epoxide or peroxide generated, 
is then further oxidised in the citric acid cycle/tricarboxylic acid cycle (TCA) proving energy and cell 
biomass for the microorganism (Olajire & Essien, 2014; Prince & Walters, 2007). 
 
An important way to clean hydrocarbon pollution in surface waters is by bioremediation, using the 
capabilities of microorganisms to degrade hydrocarbons (Ghosal, et al., 2016; Xue, et al., 2015). The two 
main ways to bioremediate is by bioaugmentation, where hydrocarbon-degrading bacteria are added, and 
biostimulation, where the conditions of the microorganisms already present, are stimulated/improved 
(Das & Chandran, 2011; Adams, et al., 2015). The efficacy of bioremediation relies on several factors: 
 
 
• 
Nutrients 
Nitrogen and phosphorous are the two main nutrients often limiting the biodegradation of hydrocarbons 
in the environments, as they are necessary for biomass growth (Atlas, 1981; Atlas, 1984; Das & Chandran, 
2011). In the years following the Exxon Valdez accident of the coast of Alaska in 1989, where 
approximately 41 million litres of oil were spilled, 50 ton nitrogen and 5 ton phosphorous where released 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
as fertilizer in a bioremediation effort (Bragg, et al., 1994). The ratio between carbon, nitrogen and 
phosphorous is of great importance in regards to the effect of biodegradation and inhibition has been 
reported with excessive nutrient use (Leahy & Colwell, 1990; Smith, et al., 1998; Das & Chandran, 2011; 
Head, et al., 2006). It is estimated that when hydrocarbons are solubilized, as opposed to oil slicks, 
nutrients probably won’t be the rate-limiting factor in the biodegradation due to adequate access for the 
microorganisms to the hydrocarbons (Atlas, 1981). 
 
• 
Temperature 
Temperature has a mayor influence on the biodegradation of hydrocarbons, as it influences the viscosity, 
volatility, solubility of the hydrocarbons as well as the microbial community present and their metabolism 
(Leahy & Colwell, 1990). The degradation of hydrocarbons is found to occur slower in Arctic regions 
compared to temperate and tropic regions, with the maximum degradation rate at 15-20°C and 20-30°C 
in sea- and freshwater, respectively (Atlas, 1981; Das & Chandran, 2011; Leahy & Colwell, 1990). 
 
• 
Physical state 
The physical state of hydrocarbons, soluble, emulsified or solid, is a very important factor in the 
biodegradation rate, since it controls the availability to microorganisms, as they act at the hydrocarbon-
water interface (Atlas, 1981; Prince & Walters, 2007). It is therefore a common assumption in 
environmental models, that only the dissolved part of compound is available for biodegradation 
(European Commission, 2003). This is problematic, since most hydrocarbons have very low solubility and 
accidental spills of oil and petroleum products in surface waters, often results in the formation of slicks 
and tar balls, with limited access for microorganisms (Olajire & Essien, 2014; Atlas, 1981). The majority of 
the hydrocarbon-degrading bacteria known, produce biosurfactants which emulsifies the hydrocarbons 
into droplets, increasing the surface area and thereby the biodegradation rate (Abbasian, et al., 2015; 
Leahy & Colwell, 1990). Dispersants, a mixture of surfactants and solvents, have been synthesized, tested 
and used in large scale in bioremediation efforts, although with mixed results (Kleindienst, et al., 2015; 
Atlas & Hazen, 2011; Makkar & Rockne, 2003). 
 
• 
Composition 
The degradation of hydrocarbons is depending on its chemical composition, in regards to its content of 
subgroups/classes (Atlas, 1981; Leahy & Colwell, 1990). This is of importance, since the composition of 
subgroups in hydrocarbon mixtures, as in oil and petroleum products, varies greatly (Radovic, et al., 2012; 
Atlas, 1984). The biodegradation rate of the subgroups/classes of hydrocarbons is believed to be occurring 
the fastest in the following order (Olajire & Essien, 2014; Das & Chandran, 2011): n-alkanes > branched 
alkanes > low-molecular-weight (LMW) aromatics > cyclic alkanes > high-molecular-weight (HMW) 
aromatics. There seem to be no clear line when an aromatic hydrocarbon is LMW or HMW but the 
distinction made in this report, is that an aromatic with a molecular weight > 202 it is HMW, based on 
Crompton, 1999. Some reports make the distinction in regards to number of rings, two or three is LMW 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
and ≥4 is HMW, but do not account for the weight by the addition of alkylated groups (Ghosal, et al., 2016; 
Peng, et al., 2008). 
 
The degradation rate of hydrocarbon mixtures, such as crude oil, changes with the compositional changes, 
both for the mixture but also for the individual components (Leahy & Colwell, 1990). Light crude oil has 
shown to be faster degraded than heavier oil, due to the microbial preference of the easier, more 
abundant, degradable hydrocarbons over the more complex ones, as well as its lower content of toxic 
aromatics and resins (Bacosa, et al., 2010; Leahy & Colwell, 1990; Shrivastava & Phale, 2012; Prince & 
Walters, 2007). 
 
• 
Other factors 
Factors such as pressure, pH, salinity, redox conditions and humidity are important and variable 
parameters when discussing biodegradation in soils, sediments and deep-sea environments (Leahy & 
Colwell, 1990; Ghosal, et al., 2016; Prince & Walters, 2007; Atlas, 1981), but will not be discussed here, 
since the focus of this report is degradation in aerobic surface waters where differences are determined 
to be negligible. 
 
Previous exposure of hydrocarbons in an environment as well as a high microbial population, have a 
positive effect on biodegradation, i.e. reducing the half-life of the hydrocarbons (CONCAWE, 1999; Leahy 
& Colwell, 1990; Atlas, 1981; Painter, 1995). There are three known ways adaptation works: 1) changes in 
the genetics, creating new capabilities of the metabolism, 2) stimulation and/or suppression of enzymes 
and 3) through improvement of the environment for hydrocarbon-degrading organisms (Leahy & Colwell, 
1990). 
 
An important but complex degradation process of hydrocarbons, is known as co-metabolism/co-
oxidation, defined by Horvath, 1972 as “oxidation of substances without utilization of the energy derived 
from the oxidation to support microbial growth”. Several species of fungi are known to oxidize 
hydrocarbons without complete mineralization (to CO2), known to create by-products both easier and 
more difficult for further degradation (Prince & Walters, 2007; Atlas, 1981; Ghosal, et al., 2016). 
 
The microbial community and their interactions are of great complexity and importance to the 
biodegradation of hydrocarbons (Leahy & Colwell, 1990; Wang, et al., 2016). The primary consumers 
compete for hydrocarbons but also nutrients, and are affected by the physical conditions as previously 
mentioned, but also negatively by protozoan predation and phage-mediated lysis (Head, et al., 2006). This 
along with many of the factors mentioned above, highlights the uncertainty with extrapolating data from 
laboratory studies into being applicable under field conditions.

2.1.1 Linear alkanes/n-alkanes 
Linear alkanes, also known as n-alkanes, are considered the sub-group/class/component most easily 
biodegraded (Atlas, 1981; Leahy & Colwell, 1990). The biodegradability of linear alkanes decreases with 
length/molecular weight (Setti, et al., 1993). Linear alkanes containing less than 4 carbons (C4) are very 
volatile and therefore negligible in surface waters under normal circumstances (Rojo, 2009). The half-lives 
of linear alkanes in surface waters is very short, mostly < 3 days, for compounds < 20 carbon atoms (Prince, 
et al., 2008). The degradability decreases with length, and n-alkanes with > C25 have been reported to be 
recalcitrant (Olajire & Essien, 2014), although several bacterial strains are known to degrade n-alkanes up 
to C44 (Wentzel, et al., 2007; Abbasian, et al., 2015). 
 
The initial step of n-alkanes is predominantly by monoterminal attack, generating a primary alcohol, which 
is then further oxidized to an aldehyde and then monocarboxylic/fatty acid, as seen in Figure 1 (Atlas, 
1981; Olajire & Essien, 2014). The fatty acid can then either be further oxidized by ω-oxidation 
(hydroxylation at the other end) or by β-oxidation through conjugation to coenzyme A, CoA (Rojo, 2009; 
Atlas, 1984). Subterminal oxidation of n-alkanes can also occur (Figure 1), generating firstly a secondary 
alcohol, then a ketone and an ester, which is then hydrolysed to form the alcohol and carboxylic acid 
products (Wentzel, et al., 2007; Olajire & Essien, 2014).

done using ChemSketch. 
 
2.1.2 Branches alkanes 
Branched alkanes, also known as iso-alkanes or i-alkanes, are n-alkanes with one or more alkyl group 
substituents. They are generally found being more recalcitrant to biodegradation than n-alkanes, and their 
recalcitrance increases the more branched they are (Rojo, 2009; Watkinson, 1991; Xue, et al., 2015). 
Branched alkanes with tertiary and quaternary carbon atoms as well as methyl groups at the anteiso/β-
position (Figure 2), has proven to be highly resistant to degradation due to steric hindrance to the oxidizing 
enzymes (Prince & Walters, 2007; Atlas, 1981; Watkinson, 1991).

2.1.3 Cyclic alkanes 
The biodegradability of cyclic alkanes, also known as alicyclic hydrocarbons or naphthenes, can be difficult, 
especially ones containing multiple rings (Olajire & Essien, 2014; Atlas, 1981). Some such as hopane, are, 
despite proven biodegradability, estimated so recalcitrant that it is used as a biomarker in biodegradation 
tests (Prince & Walters, 2007). The resistance of cyclic alkanes towards biodegradability, is estimated to 
be due to the lack of a terminal methyl group, where initial attack would occur, as seen in alkyl substituted 
cyclic alkanes (Olajire & Essien, 2014; Dutta & Harayama, 2001). Co-metabolism seems to play an 
important role in the primary biodegradation of cyclic alkanes (Abbasian, et al., 2015; Kirkwood, et al., 
2008; Atlas, 1981). The primary biodegradation of cyclohexane is seen in Figure 3, as reported in Xue, et 
al., 2015 and Olajire & Essien, 2014.

2.1.4 Aromatic hydrocarbons 
The biodegradation of aromatic hydrocarbons can be difficult, especially for the more branched 
compounds and the HMW PAHs, due to their low solubility and chemical stability (Churchill, et al., 1999; 
Prince & Walters, 2007; Ghosal, et al., 2016). PAHs can be, as previously mentioned, carcinogenic, which 
is problematic due to their bioaccumulative properties and 16 PAHs are listed as priority organic pollutants 
by the US EPA (Ghosal, et al., 2016). The LMW aromatics, including the BTEX group (benzene, toluene, 
ethylbenzene and xylene) have high solubility, compared to HMW PAHs, and are often more readily 
biodegradable (Shrivastava & Phale, 2012; Ghosal, et al., 2016). As previously mentioned, alkyl 
substituents, reduce the biodegradability of hydrocarbons, including aromatics, but the position also has 
a significant effect. Wammer & Peters, 2005, showed that the placement of methyl group(s) on 
naphthalene in the α position biodegraded faster than in the β position. The opposite however seems to 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
be the case for phenanthrene, pyrene and chrysene (Malmquist, et al., 2015). The initial step in the 
biodegradation of PAHs by bacteria, usually involves dioxygenase enzymes, generating a cis-Dihydrodiol 
in its initial step, whereas fungi mostly use monooxygenase, generating an arene oxide (Ghosal, et al., 
2016; Das & Chandran, 2011). For some bacteria, the initial step of some aromatics (toluene, xylene and 
cymene) involves the oxidation of a methyl group to an alcohol (Pérez-Pantoja, et al., 2010). The primary 
biodegradation of benzene is shown in Figure 4 as example and polyaromatic hydrocarbons follow a 
similar pathway, often generating catechol (Ghosal, et al., 2016; Husain, 2008). Catechol are further 
degraded by ortho or meta pathways by, respectively, intradiol or extradiol dioxygenases, before entering 
the TCA cycle (Husain, 2008).

Graphics were done using ChemSketch. 
 
2.2 Biodegradation modelling 
Due to the vast number of chemicals in the environment and the time and costs involved with laboratory 
and field tests, models are a useful tool (Mamy, et al., 2015). The BioHCwin model is a biodegradation 
model for predicting the half-lives of individual petroleum hydrocarbons developed by Howard, et al., 
2005. The individual hydrocarbons, present in the complex mixture of petroleum were identified and 
biodegradation data for the compounds were attained from the Syracuse Research Corporation’s 
Environmental Fate DataBase (Howard, et al., 2005). Mostly data used, was for field studies with 
environmentally realistic conditions (mixed-culture, petroleum mixture used, aerobic conditions, realistic 
concentrations). However, some test data were from activated sludge studies and other studies used open 
test systems where volatilization could occur (Howard, et al., 2005). Between the different studies, the 
parameters varied, i.e. difference in duration, inoculum type (water, soil, sediment), nutrient availability, 
temperature, microbial culture etc. This leads to high variance in the half-lives for individual compounds, 
easily an order of magnitude (Prosser, et al., 2016). The median was chosen for 121 structurally diverse 
compounds, which ended up as the training set, for the multiple-linear regression analysis used to make 
BioHCwin. Successive regressions were run to determine the most significant fragments (methyl, 
methylene etc.) in regards to their impact on the biodegradation (Howard, et al., 2005). BioHCwin ended

up with different 31 fragment coefficients used in determining the half-lives of the 121 individual 
petroleum hydrocarbons. The formula for the BioHCwin model is (Howard, et al., 2005):

3.1 New DTU study 
 
A new study was done at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), which tested the primary 
biodegradation of 53 hydrocarbons in inocula from Danish surface waters. It was done by Birch, et al., In 
prep, and is not yet published. In this report, it will be referred to as ‘the DTU study’ and ‘DTU’ in figures 
and tables. 
 
3.1.1 Test substances 
The chemicals used in the new DTU study was chosen with the aim of representing the variety of 
hydrocarbons (HC’s) present in petroleum. Compounds with boiling points below 110 °C, logKow below 3 
and water solubility below 1 µg/L (except for Benzo(k)fluoranthene) were discarded for practical 
purposes.  An effort to use compounds with available and non-available data from biodegradation tests 
were made. Hydrocarbons were chosen, so that all relevant subclasses/groups (paraffinic, aromatic, 
naphthenic etc.) was evenly represented, and a total of 53 HC’s was used.

solid at 20°C. However, a few compounds with melting points > 20 °C were added to the ‘volatile’ group, 
due to significant higher Henry’s law constants (KH) compared to the other chemicals in the group. This 
was deemed unproblematic due to the since the few ‘solids’ would become soluble/liquid in the mixture.

3.1.2 Test method 
A silicone polymer was loaded with the aforementioned hydrocarbons. Then a stock solution was 
generated by way of passive dosing, putting the silicone rod into demineralized water and given time to 
partitioning to equilibrium. The concentrations of the compounds in the stock solution were in the ng-
µg/L range, approximately a factor of 102 – 103 below their aqueous solubility. This was to test the 
compounds at environmentally relevant concentrations and to ensure that the HC’s were soluble. 1.5 mL 
of the stock solution was added to 13.5 mL of surface water in 20 mL vials. The vials were then closed and 
later analysed by Automated Headspace Solid Phase Microextraction coupled to GC-MS. The decrease in 
concentration of the HC’s were determined in respect to two abiotic controls, using demineralized water. 
The first-order degradation rate constant and half-lives (HL), were then determined. 
 
The test volume is an important parameter in biodegradation tests (Thouand, et al., 2011; Ingerslev, et 
al., 2000), and the 13.5 mL of surface water used per vial in the DTU tests might result in longer half-lives 
than what would be experienced in the environment. 
 
Half-lives of the hydrocarbons were generated, based on their first-order degradation curves.

3.1.3 Surface water inocula 
There were used three different types of surface water inocula in the study: lake water, activated sludge 
filtrate from a wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) and sea water. All samples were used for the 
biodegradation tests within 24 hours of sampling. The inocula used was not expected to contain any 
contaminants or any of the hydrocarbons used, in any concentrations that could affect the study. No 
nutrients or microbial populations were added. 
 
The samples of lake water were from Maglesø (Zealand, Denmark), 6-7 meters from the shore. The 
samples for the biodegradation testing of Group 1 HC’s were taken in April, and the samples for Group 2 
HC’s in October. The lake is located in rural settings, with minimum pre-exposure to petroleum 
hydrocarbons. 
 
The activated sludge samples are from the WWTP Lynetten, located in Copenhagen. The samples for the 
testing of the liquid and solid HCs, where taken in April (liquid HC’s) and October (solid HC’s). The sludge 
was filtered before use, through a 20 µm filter. The inoculum from act. sludge filtrate is considered to 
highly pre-exposed to hydrocarbons (Birch, et al., 2017b; ECHA, 2001). 
 
The seawater inoculum originated from the North Sea, 5-8 km off the coast of Esbjerg, Denmark. The 
harbour of Esbjerg has been the hub of the Danish offshore activities for the last 50 years (Esbjerg Havn, 
2017). The heavy marine traffic of the harbour has resulted in the release of hydrocarbons (Falck, 2012), 
and organisms used in this test, could therefore have been pre-exposed. The samples for Group 1 and 
Group 2 HC’s, were taken in May and September, respectively.

3.2 Data handling in this report 
 
For some of the hydrocarbons in the DTU study, their half-lives were undeterminable due to varies reasons 
(large variation between replicates, dubious values, non first-order decay curves etc.) These compounds 
have been abandoned and do not occur in this report. 
 
The degradation of several compounds was not fast enough for their half-lives to be determined during 
the duration the test period. The last day of the test period is therefore used as half-life value, even though 
it is clearly not the compounds actual half-life. Wherever this occurs in the report, it is mentioned and 
labelled. They are sometimes mentioned as having ‘undeterminable’ half-lives. Some of these 
compounds, have a higher predicted half-life than observed in the DTU study, which would falsely label 
them as having a ‘overestimated’ half-life. These compounds are not included in graphs and calculations  
when not singled out/mentioned. 
 
The partitioning between the inoculum and the headspace were not accounted for, even though it can 
underestimate the degradation rate of the more volatile compounds (Birch, et al., 2017a). Any correction 
of this problem has, to this authors knowledge, not been done in any of the tests, this report uses for 
comparison.

The n-P/n-alkane group, only containing n-Decane and n-Dodecane, are, as seen on Figure 7, clearly the 
chemical group degraded the quickest in all inocula. The maximum half-life was 2 days (n-Dodecane in 
lake water), with the actual half-life being even lower, due to no measuring before this point. It is in line 
with the literature, and the hypothesis of this report, that n-alkanes are the chemical subgroup degraded 
fastest. 
 
When looking at Figure 7, it can be seen that the i-P’s/branched alkanes, in general have low half-life 
values, except for one compound. That compound is 2,2,4,4,6,8,8-Heptamethylnonane, which has much 
higher half-lives than the other branched alkanes, in all inocula. The asterisk for 2,2,4,4,6,8,8-
Heptamethylnonane, denotes that a half-life was not reached in any of the three inocula. 2,2,4,4,6,8,8-
Heptamethylnonane, also known as Isocetane, is highly branched, much more than the other any 
inoculum. It contains one tertiary and three quaternary carbon atoms, all known to inhibit biodegradation 
(Prince & Walters, 2007). 2,6,10-Trimethyldodecane, has a similar carbon length to n-Dodecane, but 
containing three methyl groups (-CH3) at various positions. One of the methyl groups is at the anteiso 
position, known to impede biodegradation (Atlas, 1981). The branching, incl. the methyl group at the 
anteiso position, should therefore result in a higher half-live for 2,6,10-Trimethyldodecane compared to 
n-Dodecane, according to the theory. This is however not the case, as the average half-life for 2,6,10-
Trimethyldodecane is 0.6 days, compared to 0.8 days for n-Dodecane. Both values are however so low 
that any difference should be neglected due to the uncertainties of biodegradation studies. The similarity 
of half-lives for 2,6,10-Trimethyldodecane and n-Dodecane is however surprising.

than test periods. This is especially the case in lake water inoculum, where the majority (5 out of 9) cyclic

In lake water inoculum, 4 out of 10 low-molecular weight aromatics (LMW Ar) had half-lives longer than 
test periods of 98 days (p-Xylene, 1,3,5-Triethylbenzene, Tetralin and 1,4,6,7-Tetramethylnaphthalene). 
However, no LMW Ar had undetermined half-lives in the other inocula. The LMW Ar, hardest to degrade 
in seawater and act. sludge filtrate, was 2,2,5,7-Tetramethyltetralin (no data for lake water), with half-
lives of 19 and 37 days, respectively. It features a quaternary carbon, which is known to hinder 
degradation, as previously mentioned. Most of the LMW Ar have relatively low half-lives, exemplified by 
m-Cymene, Isopentylbenzene and Naphthalene, which all have half-lives < 10 days in all inocula. 
 
High-molecular weight aromatics (HMW Ar) are, as seen in Figure 7, generally degraded slower in lake 
water and seawater, compared to act. sludge filtrate. 1,2,3,6,7,8-Hexahydropyrene is the HMW Ar most 
resistant to degradation in the study, as its half-lives could not be determined in lake water (> 104 days) 
and seawater (> 111 days) but was determined in act. sludge filtrate at 70 days. Benzo(b)fluoranthene is 
the other HMW aromatic with a half-life > 104 days in lake water, but was much easier degraded in act. 
sludge filtrate with a half-life of 18 days (no data in seawater). In seawater, Pyrene was the other 
compound with a half-life > 111 days, and was also much easier degraded in act. sludge filtrate with a 
half-life of 0.5 days (no data in lake water). The most easily degraded HMW aromatics were Decylbenzene 
and Dodecylbenzene with half-lives < 1 day in all three inocula (no data for Dodecylbenzene in lake water). 
These two compounds are included in the HMW group due to their molecular weight (> 202). It could be 
argued that they should belong to the LMW Ar group due to their lone aromatic ring. The very fast 
degradation of Decylbenzene and Dodecylbenzene, could indicate initial attack on the alkyl group instead 
of the benzene ring. This is similar to that seen for n-Octylcyclohexane and seen by a previous study by 
Dutta & Harayama, 2001. The concentrations of the hydrocarbons used in the DTU study, well below 
solubility, indicate that the low solubility of HMW Ar’s, compared to other hydrocarbons, is not the reason 
for their slower biodegradation. The chemical stability, due to their stabilisation by conjugation of π-
electrons, could therefore be the main reason for recalcitrance towards biodegradation.

The overall picture, from the data in the DTU study, is that the n-alkanes are quickest to biodegrade and 
the HMW aromatics the slowest. The branched alkanes have 3rd highest average half-life, but are a factor 
of 2 lower in the other categories (except n-alkanes). The LMW aromatics have the 4th highest average, 
the 3rd highest median but the 2nd highest average* and median*. The average half-lives of the cyclic 
alkanes are almost as high as that of the HMW aromatics, but have much lower values in the other 
categories. It also, ‘only’ has the 3rd highest average* and median* value. 
 
It should be clear, that different methods generate different outcomes, when comparing susceptibility to 
biodegradation between the different groups. An overall ranking of the different groups susceptibility 
towards biodegradation, using the data in Table 7, will however be attempted by this author. The general 
susceptibility of the groups towards biodegradation, is deemed to occur in the following order: n-alkanes, 
branched alkanes, LMW aromatics, cyclic alkanes and HMW aromatics. The reason for labelling branched 
alkanes more susceptible than LMW aromatics, despite an (slightly) higher average half-life, is due to their 
values in the other categories, being lower by a factor of 2 or more.

4.4.1 ECHA classification 
The predicted and observed half-lives is compared to the classification of persistent compounds by the 
European Chemicals Agency (ECHA). ECHA classifies compounds as ‘persistent’ when the half-life exceeds 
40 and 60 days, for fresh- and seawater respectively. Compounds are classified as ‘very persistent’ when 
half-lives are longer than 60 days, for both freshwater/lake water and marine water/seawater (European 
Chemicals Agency, 2014). 
 
According to the ECHA classification, 64% of the compounds in the new DTU study can be classified as 
non-persistent in lake water, as seen in Table 10. Only 3% is persistent, but 33% can be classified as very 
persistent with half-lives over 60 days. When comparing the half-lives of the HC’s in the DTU study to the 
ones predicted by BioHCwin, 12% are conservatively predicted, i.e. the predicted half-lives are > 40 days, 
and the observed half-lives are ≤ 40 days. More problematic is it the disagreement of half-lives between 
the DTU study and BioHCwin, where 15% of the compounds have having half-lives > 40 days, when they 
are estimated to have half-lives ≤ 40 days. This underestimation of the 15% (7 HC’s) by BioHCwin, results 
in a ‘non-persistent’ classification, when they, according to the DTU study, should classified persistent. 5 
of 7 would even fall under the ‘very persistent’ classification. 
 
For seawater, approx. 95% of the HC’s can be classified as non-persistent, with half-lives ≤ 60 days, and 
the remaining HC’s being persistent/very persistent. For about 14% of the HC’s, their half-lives are 
conservatively predicted by BioHCwin. None of the compounds in seawater are misclassified in regards to 
under predicted half-lives.

5. Conclusion 
 
The hydrocarbons in the DTU study were generally found to degrade fastest in act. sludge filtrate, then 
seawater and lastly lake water, with average half-lives of 10, 17 and 41 days, respectively. The median 
values were 1.3, 3.7 and 17 days, respectively. This is consistent with one of the hypothesis made of the 
report. 15 out of 30 hydrocarbons with data in all inocula, follows this degradation pattern between 
inocula. The half-lives were undeterminable, i.e. longer than the test period, for 3, 4 and 12 hydrocarbons 
in act. sludge filtrate, seawater and lake water, respectively. 
 
When grouping the hydrocarbons by chemical group, the largest average susceptibility towards 
biodegradation were found to be: n-alkanes, low-molecular-weight aromatics (LMW Ar), branched 
alkanes, cyclic alkanes, high-molecular-weight aromatics (HMW Ar). The average half-lives were measured 
at 0.7, 13.6, 16.3, 28.4 and 29.7 days, respectively. If measured by median half-lives, the order between 
the LMW aromatics and branched alkanes would switch. The median half-lives, in the new order, were 
determined to be 0.4, 0.9, 3.2, 5.9 and 13.5 days, respectively. This, by this author, represent the best 
view of the groups susceptibility towards biodegradation. However, most cyclic alkanes (5) had half-lives 
longer than the test period, followed by LMW aromatics (4), HMW aromatics (3), branched alkanes (1) 
and n-alkanes (0). The susceptibility of the groups towards biodegradation, is therefore based on how the 
hydrocarbons are grouped and the way the results are used. This complicates the validation of the 
hypothesis, regarding the different groups resistance towards biodegradation. No correlation between 
the concentration of the hydrocarbons used in the DTU study, and their half-life was seen. 
 
Comparisons of the predicted half-lives of the hydrocarbons by BioHCwin, to the observed half-lives in the 
DTU study were made. When separated by inoculum, the average half-lives were overestimated 10, 13 
and 88 times for lake water, seawater and act. sludge, respectively. The medians were overestimated 4, 
8 and 29 times, using the same order. Hydrocarbons, with half-lives longer than the test durations, were 
not included, due to false overestimation labels. 6 hydrocarbons had underestimated half-lives by more 
than a factor of 10, in at least one inoculum; all belonging to the cyclic alkanes and LMW aromatics. 15 
hydrocarbons had overestimated half-lives by more than a factor of 10, in at least one inoculum, 
represented by all 5 groups. 9 of them had an alkyl group, longer than methyl (-CH3). In general, BioHCwin 
overestimated the half-lives as hypothesized in this report. 
 
When comparing half-lives to the study by Prosser et at., 2016, 34 out of 37 hydrocarbons were within a 
factor of 10. However, 6 of the compounds had undetermined half-lives in the DTU study. Using the ECHA 
guidelines, about two thirds of the hydrocarbons, would be classified as ‘non-persistent’, and the rest 
‘persistent’ or ‘very persistent’, in lake water, according their half-lives. In seawater, 95% would be 
classified ‘non-persistent’. OECD ready biodegradability screening test, were only available for 7 
hydrocarbons. Only 2,6,10-Trimethyldodecane tested as ‘Inherently biodegradable’, but had a half-life < 
1 day in all inocula. The rest are labelled as ‘Readily biodegradable’, with some compounds having 
undetermined half-lives in the DTU study.

A. Description of biodegradation tests in Prosser, et al., 2016 
 
The supplemental data used in Prosser, et al., 2016, is previously unpublished data done by ExxonMobil 
for 54 individual hydrocarbons. The compounds were added to a silicone tube and released by passing 
dosing. 
 
In Prince, et al., 2007, unleaded, oxygenate-free gasoline, was tested at ~70 ppm in rain- and seawater 
from New Jersey, USA, as well as in activated sludge. 131 hydrocarbons (C6-C12), were distinguished. 
 
In the study from Prince, el at., 2008, B20 biodiesel (20% soybean fatty acid methyl esters and 80% 
petroleum diesel) was tested for aerobic degradation in freshwater from New Jersey, USA, at ~21 °C. Two 
separate ways were used to analyse the degradation. In one, the water mixture was added to a vial 
containing the biodiesel and then sealed. For the less volatile compounds, the water mixture was added 
to an Erlenmeyer flask containing the B20 biodiesel and then loosely closed. 76 individual hydrocarbons 
were identified. 
 
The primary degradation of physically-dispersed, lightly-weathered crude oil was studied in Prince, et al., 
2013. The oil was dispersed using Corexit 9500 and the weathering was done by evaporating the crude oil 
at room temperature until 80% of the weight was left. More than 30 hydrocarbons were identified. 
 
In the study from CONCAWE, 2012, the half-lives of 74 hydrocarbons, representative of ‘higher molecular 
weight aliphatic and aromatic hydrocarbon classes found in heavier petroleum products and refinery 
streams’, were determined. There were three studies, grouped by the characteristics of the hydrocarbons 
(mostly liquid, mostly solid and two- and three-ring cycloalkanes). To secure that the hydrocarbons were 
present at concentrations below the aqueous solubility, a passive dosing system using a silicone tube was 
used. 
 
The primary biodegradation of Alaskan crude oil, was tested in -1°C local seawater in the study by 
McFarlin, et al., 2014. The tests were done under dispersed and non-dispersed, fresh and lightly 
weathered conditions. The weathering is similar to that of Prince, et al. 2013, and the dispersion medium 
was Corexit 9500 applied at 15 and 20%. The concentration of oil was 2.5 ppm tested in open top 
mesocosms and at 15 ppm amended with 16 ppm Bushnell Haas Broth medium. The half-lives of over 34 
hydrocarbons were calculated in regards to their loss relative to 17α (H),21β (H)-hopane.

dispersed Macondo oil in Norwegian seawater at 5°C. The oil was heated to 50°C for 30 minutes, then 
cooled to room temperature and mixed with Corexit 9500 dispersant.
