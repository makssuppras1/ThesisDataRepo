Abstract
Reducing the green house gasses concentration in the atmosphere is one main topic discussed
worldwide. For this, Pyrolysis is proposed as a technology for recovering waste into green fuels,
satisfying a new demand for methanol, and sourcing a carbon capture solution, all three topics
simultaneously.
The present work optimizes the conversion from feedstocks to methanol using pyrolysis and
anaerobic digestion, and simulates the process in Aspen Plus under the scope of high carbon
eﬀiciency.
Furthermore, four scenarios with different process connections are proposed, to
understand the main variables when carbon is converted to different products. Finally, the
variability in feedstocks compositions and load to anaerobic digestion is studied to predict the
behaviour of the downstream pyrolysis and synthesis process.
After this study, a carbon eﬀiciency of 93% is obtained under the chosen process conditions.
Moreover, the main variables affected by feedstocks’ volatility and scenarios’ flexibility are heat
and power, but carbon eﬀiciency is maintained constant.
The content of this project is proposed to be complemented with a feasibility study, including
different electrolyzer capacities and by-products (carbon dioxide or oxygen) selling solutions.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
For the past fifty years, global warming has been presented, discussed, rejected, challenged,
and so on. Today, yet not fully understood, is a reality with daily consequences worldwide. Its
main driver is proven to be the greenhouse gasses (GHG) emitted constantly and increasingly
since the Industrial Revolution. Today, reducing those emissions is a key economic element, but
still not enough to reach the goals set in the Paris Agreement (2015) Sustainable Development
Scenario (SDS), reviewed yearly by the United Nations[1].
Under these circumstances, technologies that remove carbon from the atmosphere are being
developed, one of which, Pyrolysis, the core of this project. Pyrolysis is a thermal treatment of
biomasses that obtains different fuels as product. Depending on the process conditions, those
products can be gas, liquids, or solids. Under the scenario of energy transition and reduction of
GHG concentration in the atmosphere, Pyrolysis is the technology selected in this project for
three reasons: the availability of resources, the opportunity to fulfill the energy demand, and
naturally, the removal of CO2 from the atmosphere. Following, these are better explained.
1.1.1
Resources
One strength that pyrolysis has is that the feedstocks needed are mostly waste resources1. Its
main components are manure, straw, food waste, and even urban waste and sewage. It is an
important aspect to consider because costs from disposal are prevented, and even more, given
a high value. The low price/disposal cost savings is not the only benefit of using waste: plenty
of emissions, from its disposal and form the green energy products obtained, are avoided.
The second life of waste products is an interesting scope, as humans produce 1.6 billion tons of
food waste per year. Moreover, related to livestock manure, there is the potential to generate
from 2,600 to 3,800 TWh of energy globally; and from waste crops, from 3,080 to 3,920 TWh.
In other words, there is a big potential for driving change[2].
Later, not only is is more convenient to reuse the residues and its’ avoided emissions but also,
by using anaerobic digestion and/or pyrolysis rich elements that are contained such as carbon,
nitrogen, etc, are captured for optimal usage. With pyrolysis, half2 of the carbon is stored,
and the other half is used instead of non-biogenic carbon for fuel production.
1.1.2
Energy demand
Biogas and Bio-methane
Gas is the fossil fuel with lowest emissions per MJ compared to the other two most consumed
fossil fuels worldwide, oil and coal. [3] In the proposed scenarios to reach the agreed goal of
2°C increase scenario by 2050 set in the Paris Agreement (2015), an increase in gas demand
in order to supplement oil and coal consumption is strongly recommended. [4] Consequently,
the present growing demand for gas is assured to continue in the future years.
Moreover, biogas and bio-methane are types of gas that come from alternative sources, such
as agricultural waste, municipal solid residues, or wastewater, among others. Some of these
sources include the capture of carbon when being produced, for example, crop waste. By using
some of the others, GHG emissions are avoided, as with manure and wastewater. Overall,
the process of biogas and/or bio-methane obtention generally reduces the equivalent carbon

Furthermore, biogas is presently mainly used for Power and Heat, and its usage does not seem

Methanol
Transport is the second largest cause of GHG emissions in the world [5], and presents an

fuels are hydrogen, bio-diesel, bio-oil, ammonia, and methanol, among others. Depending on
the type of transportation, some are more feasible and faster to implement than others.
This project proposes the production of methanol from biogenic carbon sources, or in other
words, green methanol. Methanol is stable and easy to store, non-hazardous, well-known, and
very importantly, compatible with present navigation motors. This fuel presents itself as a
perfect match for cutting down shipping emissions worldwide and has the support of leading
companies. The first cargo powered with green methanol has already been shipped, now there
is only time for the others to follow.
Currently, there are three main ways for obtaining green methanol, and all of them offer a life
cycle of GHG reductions compared to fossil fuels of at least 65%. [6] Even though there are
many different pathways for its obtention, the scarcity of bio-gen CO2 is a concern among the
major players in the Power-to-X industry.[7].
1.1.3
Carbon capture and storage
Carbon Capture is a field split into three main technology groups: Direct Air Capture (DAC),
Carbon Capture, Utilization and Storage (CCUS), and Bioenergy with Carbon Capture and
Storage (BECCS). [8]
DAC facilities are expensive and not very eﬀicient, and, do not prevent emissions either. CCUS
neither prevent emissions, but have a higher eﬀiciency and lower cost. On the other hand,
pyrolysis, a BECCS technology, is not costly, has stable storage with unlimited capacity, and
prevents further emissions because 50% of the carbon that is not stored is used for fuels, being
always its precedence avoided emissions.
As commented before, there are many reasons why pyrolysis is interesting for studying its
implementation. The following is presented how it is studied in this report in order to obtain
rich conclusions.

Process description
This study includes many steps for transforming feedstocks into green methanol and some other
by-products. There are six overall transformations that the feedstock suffers along the way
of converting to methanol. Starting with anaerobic digestion (AD), it passes through drying,
pyrolysis, partial oxidation (POX), hydrogenation (Quench), and finally, methanol synthesis.
The overall process is presented in Figure 2.1 and the justification for the selected technologies
and process conditions is presented below. It is important to clarify that many process variables
are defined based on Agri Energy Biogas plant operation.[9]
Figure 2.1: Process from feedstock to methanol via Pyrolysis diagram.
2.1
Anaerobic digestion
Anaerobic digestion (AD) is a mature technology, widely spread since the 1980s for biogas
production from agricultural residues.[10] It consists of a series of microorganisms breaking
down organic compounds in the absence of oxygen. It might be considered a simple process,
but actually consists of a large set of reactions under the influence of both microorganisms
and thermodynamics at the same time affecting each other.[11]
As a result of AD, biogas is obtained, as well as heat and solid/liquid waste called digestate.
Biogas is a product with a molar composition similarly split between carbon dioxide (CO2)
and methane (CH4). In the beginning, it was burned for electricity and/or used in on-site
fleet vehicles.[12]. Today, there is also a percentage of it being cleaned and upgraded to bio-
methane.[13] On the other hand, the liquid-solid output from the reactor, digestate, is usually
spread on the fields as fertilizer. There are new alternatives being introduced in the market for
adding value to this by-product, as in this report, a pyrolysis reactor.
As mentioned in the introduction (section 1.2), anaerobic digestion mass balances are studied
in depth in chapter 5. How the variability of feedstocks affects the digestate composition is in
fact under the scope of the project. On the contrary, the process of anaerobic digestion is not
simulated in Aspen Plus in this work.
For this project, the digestate from the anaerobic digestion before entering pyrolysis contains
15% of dry matter and a composition shown in Table 2.1.

In this project, a combination of press screw dryers and a decanter is proposed. First, multiple

2.2.2
Steam drying
To optimize the energy consumption of the dryer, heat from the downstream process is used in
this step to heat the digestate from the 40°C that they come from the screw press, until 102°C
temperature that water vaporizes at 1 bar set to the model. This is done with vapor at 150°C.
The ∆temperature of 50°C is set to overcome poor heat transmission conditions within the
solids. Later, the outcome vapor is condensed and cooled down to discharge conditions (40
°C, 1 bar) to maximize energy utilization. The solids on the other hand are also cooled down,
but this is in order to introduce them to the pelletizer.
2.2.3
Pelletizing
After drying the solids, an intermediate step between the dryers and the reactor is required,
pelletizing. This is because the texture and shape of the solids coming out of the dryer are
not correct for a proper flow of streams within the reactor (solids flowing from top to bottom
and gas flowing vice versa). The solids are too light and easy to be carried by the gas, so they
would not input the reactor correctly. This is solved then by compressing the solids to small
pellet shape.
Furthermore, this process is not modeled in the project but only considers the temperature
changes that the solids suffer from the steam dryer to the reactor.

2.3
Pyrolysis
Pyrolysis is a thermal process where carbonaceous material of high molecular weight is broken
down into a higher carbon content product and volatile products. It is an endothermic anaerobic
process where the temperature of the reactions, heat rate, residence time, and pressure, are
the main parameters. These affect the distribution of products: charcoal, pyrolytic oil, and
gas.[14] For the purpose of optimizing char production, slow pyrolysis is the optimal technology,
with conditions of <20°C/min heat rate, atmospheric/low pressure and >15 minutes retention
time.[15]
This project is based on Stiesdal’s SkyClean pyrolysis process and gets as an outcome 53% of
carbon (C) from the input biomass to the reactor as biochar, and the other 47% as a gas with
high carbon monoxide (CO) and carbon dioxide composition (CO2). This gas also contains
methane (CH4), and a diversity of heavy hydrocarbons (tars) which shall be treated before
conversion to methanol. Otherwise, their presence could interfere with compressing and other
processes in which they might precipitate.
The reactor is bed fixed, working at low pressure (≈1 atm) and 550°C at the bottom of the
pyrolysis reactor and 350°C at the top. The heat for the reaction is provided by hot gas flowing
from bottom to top, as shown in Figure 2.2.
2.4
Partial Oxidation
After pyrolysis, some heavy organic compounds called tars can be found in the vapor product.
It is proven that in fix-bed gasifiers the concentration of tars is higher than 10 g/nm3. These
can cause problems when compressing the stream up to synthesis conditions. Consequently,
they shall be removed. Partial Oxidation is an effective proposal, having the capacity to convert
up to 99% of tars into lighter compounds. This technique consists of two mechanisms: thermal
decomposition and oxidation. [16]
The process consists of O2 injection in a specific ratio to the vapor stream, obtaining con-
sequently different process temperatures.
The conversion of tars depends directly on the
temperature of the reaction and the stoichiometric air ratio. Based on studies, at 1300 °C
there is left in the vapor less than 100 mg/nm∧3, dry tars.[16]

2.5
Quench
After the removal of tars, the final changes in composition to the inlet stream are done by
introducing hydrogen. This affects the water-shift equilibrium (Equation 2.2) so more water is
obtained for its removal before the methanol synthesis. It is recommendable to extract as much
water as possible, as it is a co-product of the methanol production reaction (Equation 2.6)
and it can limit the conversion rate.
The H2 injection occurs in the Quench reactor, and the removal of water occurs in the com-
pression process, after every heat exchanger. Stage 1 occurs before the first compressor at ≈
1 bar (low-pressure process operational condition), Stage 2 before the second compressor at
3.9 bar and Stage 3 before the final compressor at 15.4 bar. After the last compression step,
there is no water removal proposed because the amount of water available to condense does
not justify a cooling process considering that before the methanol reactor, the stream is to be
heated. In Figure 2.3 is shown the response of the system (Quench + Compression) to the
variation of H2.

2.6
Char bed
After Pyrolysis, 41.1% of the solids introduced in the reactor leave in the form of biochar.
Before cooling them down and storing them, it is utilized in a Char-bed reactor for two purposes.
Firstly, biochar is used for further cleaning of the treated gas due to its good adsorption
properties. Consequently, large impurities and non-destroyed tars leave with the char, getting
the gas a higher than 99.99% purity [16]. Second, the high temperatures the char faces when
in contact with the gas make the char react and get even more deployed of carbons in the for
of CO and CO2.
The char bed process is not included in the modeling nor is its heat integrated into the process.
The only aspect being considered is that the syngas to methanol synthesis does not contain
any tars or other large impurities.

Electrolyzer
The process mentioned above requires feeds of O2 and H2, injected in the POX and Quench

a mature technology, with long-term stability and lifetime, and does not involve scarce or

The reaction is presented in

As with every reaction, even though thermodynamics could be optimized at low temperatures

2.10
Product separation
2.10.1
Condenser
As an outcome of the methanol reactor, a vapor phase composed of methanol, water, and
light gases is obtained. There is a property that distinguishes clearly water and methanol from
the rest of the stream which is the normal boiling point (NBP). In Table A.3 the different
NBP of the compounds are presented, justifying then an economical separation via conden-
sation using cooling water. After this first separation, 95.86% (molar) of the condensate is a
water/methanol mixture and the rest are light compounds (mainly CO2) that are valuable in
the process. In order to recover them, a second separation is proposed, distillation.
2.10.2
Distillation, CO2 recovery from condensate
As mentioned before, carbon conversion is one of the key parameters to optimize.
Con-
sequently, an intermediate separation stage is proposed: a pressure drop and a distillation
column at medium pressure to extract light compounds before the final separation of water
and methanol.
A distillation column is economically reasonable not only because heat consumption is very
low, but also because there is plenty of extra heat left1 for usage in other stages of the overall
process. The pressure is a compromise between a better separation, costs of re-compression,
and building costs. As re-compression power is lower than 1% of the overall requirement,
and the losses of methanol as condensate in recycling are almost null when the column is set
between 30 and 8 bar, the column is fixed at 8 bar to minimize construction cost and facilitate
its operation.
After the first distillation, 99.99% of the product is a methanol-water mixture. Methanol has
a concentration of 85% molar. For getting the desired purity (around 97%), a final separation
step is proposed, distillation.
2.10.3
Distillation, CH3OH-H2O separation
The high aﬀiliation or high solubility of methanol in water makes the separation not feasible
by only one liquid-vapor equilibrium. However, their relative volatility permits the obtention
of both compounds in high purity when using multiple consecutive liquid-vapor equilibrium.
Consequently, the aid of a distillation column is required. Methanol is the light key and the
purity needed only affects designing conditions as the number of theoretical stages and reflux.
It is important to mention that this is not the only possible technique to separate those
components but it is the cheapest. Absorption is also a well-known technique but involves
chemicals and second treatments that become too complicated and expensive for a small-scale
plant as the one presented in this report.
Moreover, low-pressure conditions are selected not only because the separation is less feasible
and more expensive under pressure, but also because the product is not being stored at much
higher pressures than atmospheric.

Model description
A model is always a subjective approach to represent real processes. In this section hypotheses
and decisions taken in order to have the most accurate approach are proposed. Aspen Plus
v11 is the process simulator chosen to represent the overall process, and the overview of it is
presented in Figure 3.1
Figure 3.1: Overview of the Aspen Simulation model of the upgrading process of digestate to
Bio-char and Methanol.
3.1
Properties package
A properties package defines which thermodynamic equations are prioritized when the simula-
tion runs and the component’s properties are estimated. There are many sets depending on the
nature of the components involved and which characteristics are prioritized (polarity, symme-
try, activity, among others ). Equally important is to estimate the properties of a component
itself and its behavior within others. The conditions under which the components react affect
as much as the nature of compounds, as not all thermodynamic estimations are accurate for
any conditions.
In the process of transforming biomass to biofuels, the main components are organic light
compounds with relatively low polarity and no high interactions between each other in the
gaseous phase, and biochar, which also has no interaction with the gases in the proposed
simulation. The operative pressure is low (except for the methanol synthesis reactor), and
the temperature conditions are not extreme. Then, Peng-Robinson is an accurate package for
simulating the processes.
On the other hand, a key different element introduced is water, which affects all vapor equi-
librium, as it has a very strong polarity. Then, the modified Peng-Robinson package is better,
as it is very accurate for light non-polar gasses in the presence of water.
As mentioned, later in the process, methanol appears as the main product in a mixture with
water.
Both compounds are very polar and consequently have strong interactions.
More-
over, methanol is synthesized at a pressure higher than 10 bar. For simplicity of the model,
the properties package is kept as modified Peng-Robinson. In a more detailed version, it is
recommendable to insert external interaction parameters of the methanol synthesis[23].
Separately, an electrolyzer is simulated, which not only is based on water (high polar compound)
but also puts a special focus on these chemical intermediate compounds called electrolytes. In
this special process, an NRTL-Electrolytes property package is chosen.

3.2
Non-Conventional products
Biomass and Bio-char are not considered “conventional” compounds. This is so because they
can have a wide range of composition and properties. It is then important to characterize
them properly to obtain a model that provides reliable results. In this study, information for
both components is provided by Stiesdal®. For further details please read the compositions in
subsection A.1.1.
There are some main properties that provide the information for the proper characterization
of non-conventional biomasses.
In this project, higher heating value (HHV), specific heat
capacity (Cp), fixed carbons(FC) and volatile matter (VM) are defined. The first one is a
thermodynamic property that describes the energy outcome the component gives when being
burned (including the energy from condensing its water); the second characterizes the energy
necessary to elevate the mass temperature by one degree Celsius, and the last two correctly
define the separation of the elements in solids and gas.

3.4
Drying
After the Anaerobic Digester, the digestate is passed by two treatments in order to extract as
much water as possible with as little energy as possible: mechanical de-watering and steam
dryer.
Mechanical de-watering
Mechanical de-watering is not simulated in Aspen nor the power utilized is considered in the
project, as it does not affect the energy content of the streams and therefore, the energy
conversion eﬀiciency analyzed among the results. It is also a minor power requirement in com-
parison to the electrolyzer for example, and so its avoidance does not challenge the legitimacy
of the results.
Steam dryer
The steam dryer is modeled as two heat exchangers, one for the Non-Conventional (NC)
compounds and the other one for water boiling. The reason for this is that when mixing NC
with conventional compounds, the simulator often has errors in closing the mass balance. In
Figure 3.2, D01 represents the solids and D03 its water content. HE-SD-09 and HE-SD-10
are aids for representing the drying of the solids. While the NCs are ”only heated up” to the
final temperature of 102°C, water is heated and vaporized in the other exchanger, in the exact
proportion so when liquid water (01-MOIST(OUT)) and solids (01(OUT)) are mixed again,
they correspond to the 12 kg moisture/ kg of DM as previously set.

3.6
Partial Oxidation
As explained in section 2.4, the operational temperature has a key role in the downstream gas
composition. This parameter is conditioned by how much O2 is involved in the combustion.
Consequently, with the aid of a design spec, the feed of O2 is set so that the temperature after
combustion is 1300°C. This last one was chosen for the almost complete tars conversion ratio,
and because methane presents no deviation from equilibrium at that temperature[16].
Tars are not obtained in the model after the pyrolysis reactors even though they are in the gas
output in reality. As the temperature chosen for partial oxidation plus the usage of a char-bed
lead to the total conversion of tars, their absence in the model is irrelevant. This hypothesis
assumes that the HHV of the gas with tars is similar to the HHV of the gas in the model,
without tars.
From the statement that methane is in equilibrium at 1300°C, and considering there is another
equilibrium reaction left to model, the water-gas shift reaction Equation 2.2, then a Gibbs
reactor is suitable for modeling the POX process. It is fixed as an adiabatic reactor with an
outlet temperature of 1300°C with only inlets of both feed streams: gas and oxygen.
3.7
Quench
After studying the effect of different hydrogen concentrations at the entrance of the methanol
conversion reactor, the optimal value of S=2 is fixed by using a design spec. This flow is
preheated to 550°C conditions and added to the gas stream in a Gibbs reactor, where the
water-gas shift reaction occurs.
The temperature condition for preheating is restricted both by the available hot streams and
the cost of materials for high-temperature integration. 550°C is a safe temperature for shell
materials in the heat exchangers. There are many hot streams available for energy integration,
so the latter explains the constrain for modeling.
The reactor is a REquil reactor, as the water-gas shift is an equilibrium reaction. Methane
reaction with hydrogen is not considered in this reactor. This though does not imply any
inconvenience because after the large temperatures of the POX there is no considerable amount
of methane left.

Product separation
The condenser is modeled as a heat exchanger followed by a separator. The liquid stream
leaves to a valve that corrects pressure to distillation conditions, and a RadFrac distillation
column is proposed for CO2 recovery. For the final methanol-water separation, pressure is
again corrected with a valve and then a RadFrac distillation column is also used.
For the first column, CO2 recovery, the condenser chosen is partial, due to the presence of
very light components (non-condensates) such as CO or CO2. The condenser temperature is
set to 40°C and that limits the amount of methanol in the distillate. Reflux, distillate rate,
and number of stages are set to optimize light component extraction on top. From the last
plate, a mixture of water and methanol is obtained, and separated in the second column.
In the second distillation column, methanol is the main light key component. Here stages,
distillation rate, and reflux are fixed to have a methanol recovery higher than 99.99%. The

5
Mass balance in Anaerobic Digestors
5.1
Introduction
As mentioned before, anaerobic digestion is a mature technique mainly owned by farmers, that
has gained relevance in the energy sector in the past years. Until recent times, the digestate
was considered a secondary by-product, useful for fertilization, but not valuable enough to be
studied in depth. When the opportunity to convert it to a successful Carbon-Capture and
green-fuels option appears, as with pyrolysis, the question is then raised; how do the variables
of the AD process affect the downstream processes?
5.2
Scope
In this section, how anaerobic digestion interacts with the downstream pyrolysis upgrade is
studied. For this, there are two main variables under the scope, the variability of the type of
feedstocks, and their load. With those in mind, feedstock load numbers provided by DBC are
treated, categorized, and combined with literature information from Phyllis. Then, calculations
are made, in which aspects such as the amount of water of the inputs and digestate, elements
composition of digestate, and energy content of the feedstocks are studied. Finally, a comment
regarding ashes content and elements fed to the pyrolysis downstream is made.
5.3
Selection of plants
Every biogas plant has its own characteristics in terms of type of feedstocks and amount of
feed variation. In this section, how these are studied is described.
Data for mass balances is provided by Danish Bio Commodities (DBC) with the complement
of some literature feedstock characterizations. Because of internal processes between DBC
and the plants, the amount of time under study for each plant differs, depending on how much
reliable data was collected.
There are three selected plants under study in this project, Agri Energy, Outrup, and Sindal
[9][26][27]. The characteristics that differentiate them are the reasons for the selection. The
first one, Agri Energy, has a dry matter content in the load considerably higher than regular
biogas plants (DMAgri 27.4%). The last two, even though they do not differ much in dry
matter percentage between each other (DMOutrup 22.8% and DMSindal 20.0%), each has
different behaviors. The former represents a plant with stable feedstock provision (both in
type and quantity), and the latter, has high variability in both aspects.
5.4
Hypothesis
To facilitate the balances, some assumptions are taken. All the nitrogen remains in the di-
gestate. All the sulfur leaves in the gas (it is considered that there is no detectable sulfur in
Stiesdal’s digestate composition). CO2, H2, and O2, are not considered to interact with water
(solubilize). This is due to a lack of wastewater composition data.

finally, a measurement of the amount of gas produced in one month (m3/month). All numbers

7
Conclusions
7.1
Conclusions
The process of upgrading feedstocks to green fuels involves many variables, in other words,
there are plenty of opportunities for optimization. The base case scenario is the grounding base
for implementing pyrolysis and synthesis technologies in existing biogas plants. It increases the
carbon conversion from 27% to 77% with the usage of a 3 MW Electrolyzer.
The other three scenarios presented rely much on the electrolyzer and energy cost, being
recommended higher detail in this technology before applying the proposed connections. Any-
way, the biogas usage scenario presents the most interesting results when methanol is the main
product. It presents a carbon conversion of 93% and an energy eﬀiciency of 87.3%.
It is not recommended to have flexibility between scenarios that have very different requirements
such as the base case and biogas utilization, without considering a parallel usage of hydrogen
and oxygen, or storage facilities (for minimizing the size of the electrolyzer). In any case,
however, it is recommended to continue this project with a feasibility analysis considering
heat, oxygen, hydrogen, and carbon dioxide as products.
One additional project outcome concerns the uncertainty associated with the composition of
the digestate, primarily due to variations in the feedstock input. It is proved in this project that
changes in the composition of the biomass affect the ratio and composition of biochar and
pyrolysis gas, consequently affecting methanol synthesis conditions. Even though this topic is
tackled by different approaches it is not possible with the current information to make precise
predictions about the extent of this impact.
Then, when the production of methanol from feedstocks is studied as an industrial case, is
recommended to treat the anaerobic digestion process also as one; contrary to many anaerobic
digestion plants’ operations nowadays. Prevision in the operation and accountability of streams
are beneficial when preventing carbon loss, optimizing energy utilization, and avoiding produc-
tion stops or larger inconveniences as when exceeding the reactor’s capacity for additional
ashes.
