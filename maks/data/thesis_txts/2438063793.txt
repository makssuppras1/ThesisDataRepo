Abstract
Many renowned buildings have suered serious issues with their marble cladding which include:
• Bowing and expansion of panels
• Surface deterioration and inferior appearance
• Safety problems including strength loss and cracking and spalling around xing points
The façade panels on the Finlandia Hall started to bow a few years after the building was
completed.
In 1998 the panels were replaced, but the new panels started to bow only six
months after the renovation.
Therefore the main goal of this thesis was to investigate the eect of temperature and moisture
variations on the durability of marble cladding for outdoor application. To get a better under-
standing of the behaviour of marble during temperature and moisture exposure, the following
experiments were carried out:
• Bowing test according to DS/EN 16306 [1] with impregnated and unimpregnated marble
• Ultrasonic propagation speed measured on specimens in bowing test
• Permanent expansion of impregnated and unimpregnated marble specimens in wet and
dry conditions
• 3-point bending test of marble specimens exposed to 50 thermal cycles in the bowing test
and of marble specimens exposed to 300 ◦C
• Test of the breaking load at dowel hole of marble specimens exposed to 50 thermal cycles
in the bowing test and of marble specimens exposed to 300 ◦C
In total seven dierent marble types were tested: four types from Italy (Canaloni, Gioia,
Cattani, Venai), one from Norway (Porsgrunn) and one from Switzerland (Peccia).
For the bowing test and 3-point bending a series of specimens with a dimension of 400 mm x
100 mm x 30 mm was prepared. Some Gioia and Porsgrunn specimens were impregnated and a
minimum of ve specimens of each type was used as reference specimens. The specimens used
in the permanent expansion test either had the dimensions 200 mm x 30 mm x 30 mm or 180
mm x 30 mm x 30 mm. Another series of 200 mm x 200 mm specimens was used for the test of
breaking load at dowel hole together with the half specimens previously tested in the 3-point
bending test. In total just over 490 specimens were prepared and tested during this project.
None of the marble types bowed more than 0.4 mm/m as prescribed by DS/EN 16306 [1]. The
Gioia marble was known have severe bowing problems but bowed only 0.003 mm/m. The Gioia
marble was proved to have a xenoblastic grain structure and an AGA result of 8.
The permanent expansion was measured after exposure to thermal cycles from 20-80-20 ◦C in
both wet and dry conditions. The permanent expansion in wet conditions became constant after

3-4 cycles except for Venai, Cattani and Canaloni. The maximum permanent expansion ranged
between 0.19 mm/m and 1.49 mm/m. The permanent expansion measured in dry conditions
was constant after only 3 to 5 cycles with a maximum expansion of 0.10-0.17 mm/m.
The thermal expansion coecient decreased from thermal cycle 1 to 10 in both wet and dry
conditions, as the permanent expansion reached a constant level.
The thermal expansion had a higher contribution to the total expansion than the permanent
expansion, when the marble was dry. The opposite applied to the wet marble.
The dierential expansion of the top and bottom side was used to estimate the bowing magni-
tude. The bowing magnitude and bowing direction (concave/convex) of some marble types was
estimated correctly, but other specimens bowed less than estimated and in the other direction.
The dierential expansion between the top and bottom can not explain the bowing alone.
The ultrasonic propagation speed of all marble types was reduced after being exposed to tem-
perature and moisture cycles in the bowing test. The increase of propagation speed from dry
to wet conditions was more pronounced after 50 cycles.
The exural strength of the specimens tested in the bowing test was reduced with 9 % (Gioia)
to 41 % (Cattani/Venai) compared to the exural strength of the reference specimens.
The breaking load of all marble types using Hilti epoxy mortar to x the dowels was between
4.0 and 4.3 kN. There was a signicant dierence between the breaking load of marbles with
Hilti epoxy mortar and marbles with cement mortar. The breaking load was reduced with 7 to
16 % when exposed to 50 thermal cycles.
There was no signicant dierence between the bowing magnitude of the impregnated and
unimpregnated marble specimens. No dierence was observed between the exural strength
and breaking load of the specimens with and without impregnation either.
The moisture
content of the impregnated Porsgrunn marble was slightly reduced compared to that of the
unimpregnated marble. It was observed that the eect of the impregnation was still intact
after 50 thermal cycles, albeit the eect was greatly reduced.
There was no distinct relation between the results obtained by exposure to 50 thermal cycles
up to 80 ◦C and a single thermal cycle up to 300 ◦C and it was not possible to distinguish
between the marble quality based on the tests.
The exural strength was reduced between 1.2 and 3.0 times the reduction of the ultrasonic
propagation speed. The breaking load was reduced by 0.4-1.0 times the reduction of the prop-
agation speed, however the breaking load had a very large standard deviation.
There was no immediate relation between the reduction of exural strength and reduction of
breaking load at dowel hole.
The exural strength reduction correlated strongly with the bowing. Also the breaking load
correlated well with the bowing.
There was a ne correlation between the maximum bowing and the maximum permanent
expansion in wet conditions. The dry permanent expansion and bowing did not correlate well.
There was no relation between the maximum permanent expansion in wet and dry conditions.

Preface
This Master's thesis is written by Michael Ryding and Nikolai Hammershøj Nymand in collab-
oration with the Department of Civil Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark as
the nal project for the Master of Science degree in Civil Engineering in 2018.
The project was prepared in the period from the 29th of January until the 29th of June 2018
and has a work load equivalent of 30 ECTS points.
The knowledge gained from this project is expected to contribute to the understanding of the
mechanism leading to bowing, degradation and strength loss of marble and hereby promote the
use of more durable marble as cladding for outdoor applications.
A CD with all data collected in present project is attached to the report. A directory of all
attached les can be seen in Appendix Q.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Marble has been used as a construction element for façades for centuries, especially due to
the aesthetic value of the material.
Originally marble was used as a construction element
with thicknesses of over 1000 mm resulting in very robust and durable façades and buildings.
However, as the need for lighter construction elements increased throughout the 19th century,
new cutting techniques and equipments were developed. This allowed for a signicant reduction
in thickness from 1000 mm to 20-50 mm enabling the use of thin marble slabs as façade cladding.
However, the usage of thin marble cladding has not been problem-free. Many renowned build-
ings including the Finlandia Hall in Helsinki, La Grande Arch de la Defénse in Paris, IBM
Tower in Brussels, the Amoco building in Chicago and Lincoln First Tower in Rochester have
suered serious issues with their marble cladding [13] [14] [15]. The main issues include:
• Bowing and expansion of panels
• Surface deterioration
• Safety problems including strength loss and cracking and spalling around xing points
• Inferior appearance
One of the most prominent structures suering from bowing of its marble façade cladding is
the Finlandia Hall designed by the Finnish architect Alvar Aalto.
Finlandia Hall
Finlandia Hall is a multipurpose venue in Helsinki designed by Alvar Aalto, and it was completed
in 1971. The entire façade consists of white marble panels from the Carrara region. After a
few years it was observed that the marble panels started to bow. Several tests were made on
the marble and new possible replacement candidates were selected. The tests did not include
a bowing test.
In 1998 the municipality of Helsinki replaced the old panels with new ones. Only six months
after the renovation, the new plates started to bow, cf.
Figure 1.1.
Today, the plates are
damaged to such a degree, that replacement presumably will be necessary within a few years.
As the Finlandia Hall is an iconic building serving as a cultural attraction, the bowing of
the marble panels causes great impairment of the structure's aesthetics. However, other more
critical problems follow with the bowing.

Bowing and Expansion
Numerous tests have shown that severe bowing and expansion only occur when water is present
and the marble is exposed to thermal cycles resembling the diurnal atmospheric heating-cooling
cycles. If the marble is dry, both the bowing and permanent expansion will be limited and it
is very unlikely that damage will be inicted on the structure [9] [16]. If moisture is available,
however, the permanent expansion can lead to cracking and spalling around xing points due
to insucient joint widths.
Strength Loss
When making static calculations with marble façade claddings it is important to consider the
strength loss due to deterioration.
Typically two parameters are governing when designing
façade claddings: the exural strength and the breaking load at dowel hole.
There are many reports on signicant loss of both exural strength and of breaking load at
dowel hole. The old façade panels of the Finlandia Hall lost 85 % of their strength in only 21
years and 40 % strength of the cladding used on the Danish National Bank was lost after 35
years of use. On the contrary, the strength loss of the panels on the Malmö City Hall, Sweden
is only 10 % after 25 years [14].
Some buildings have even experienced complete detachment of the marble panels from the
anchoring system due to strength loss and failure at the dowel holes, including the house of
"Sydsvenska Dagbladet" in Malmö, Sweden and Zagrepcanka in Zagreb, Croatia [14].
The cladding used at the Finlandia Hall and at the City Hall of Malmö origin from quarries
in the Carrara region in Italy and the marble used at the Danish National Bank is of the
Norwegian type, Porsgrunn. The loss of strength is therefore very dependent on the type of
marble used and even on the quarry from which the marble is extracted.
Laboratory tests have shown that the strength of some types of marble tends towards zero with
increasing bowing [8] [9]. The strength loss is typically connected to a visual bowing of the
façade panels as on the façade of the Finlandia Hall, however the marble can still lose strength
even though no bowing is evident as reported by Grelk et al. (2007) [14]. The 60-year old
façade panels on Lyngby City Hall, Denmark show no signicant bowing even though tests
have shown strength losses of up to 45 % [16].
Siegesmund et al. [8] and Koch and Siegesmund [9] showed that both the exural strength and
the breaking load at dowel hole decreases with increasing bowing of the marble panels, with
the loss of exural strength being the most pronounced. As of today there exists no guideline
==PAGE:29==
3
to estimation of the reduction of exural strength and breaking load at dowel hole. Therefore
it is necessary to investigate the relation between the loss in exural strength and the loss in
breaking load at dowel hole to be used in static calculations.
Ultrasonic Propagation Speed
Bowing of marble panels is not the only parameter that indicates strength loss. E Molina et
al. (2013) [17] showed that the ultrasonic propagation speed for marble is reduced when the
marble is deteriorating causing an increase in the mean pore size and introducing microcracks.
The ultrasonic propagation speed can thus be a tool to determine the deterioration and the
strength loss as the strength depends on especially microcracks and the pore system.
Extreme Heating
Typically it takes years for the marble to deteriorate when exposed to outdoor climate. Labo-
ratory tests can simulate the deterioration in 50 days. However, sometimes it can be necessary
to get an estimate of the marble quality in a matter of days or even hours.
Lord Rayleigh tested the strength of marbles after baking them in an oven at high temperature
and showed that the bending strength reduced signicantly with increased baking temperature.
An increase in porosity, permanent bowing and exibility was also observed [18].
It is therefore interesting whether baking marble at high temperatures will make it possible to
quickly distinguish between high quality and low quality marble types.

3.1
Geology
Marble is a metamorphic rock that is formed by subjecting limestone to high temperatures and
pressure during the process called metamorphism, cf. Figure 3.1. Marble consists primarily of
calcite (CaCO3) which is a carbonate mineral, but can also contain various mineral impurities
including silt, sand and clay. The impurities originate from other layers in the limestone and
can typically be seen in the marble by characteristically coloured veins. Marble is not foliated
as many other metamorphic rocks.
Dolomitic marble is another type of marble dierent from the calcite marble. The dolomitic
marble is formed by metamorphism of dolomite (CaMg(CO3)2), a sedimentary rock very similar
to limestone.
Figure 3.1: Limestone with shell fragments to the left and marble with calcite crystals to the
right. Adapted from [3].
3.2
Microstructure of Marble
The microstructure of a marble depends on its geological history, especially whether the marble
has been recrystallized statically or dynamically during metamorphism. Static recrystallization
occurs after deformation of the rock, whereas dynamic recrystallization happens during defor-
mation [19].
==PAGE:34==
8
Theory
When a static recrystallization occurs, the surface energy of the rock phases is sought minimized
leading to the formation of a granoblastic structure. The granoblastic structure is characterized
by an equigranular texture with either smoothly curved or almost straight grain boundaries,
cf. Figure 3.2 [20] [13].
Figure 3.2: Granoblastic texture of marble (2,000 x 2,800 µm). Reproduced from [4].
The calcite crystals are part of the hexagonal crystal family. In a marble with a perfect gra-
noblastic polygonal microstructure, the grain triple junctions are approximately 120 degrees.
Every grain in a marble with this ideal granoblastic structure will share its boundaries with six
other grains, which will be referred to as adjacent grains (cf. Figure 3.3).
Figure 3.3: Principle for counting number of adjacent grains (AG) around median sized grain
(G). Ideal granoblastic structure to the left and a more seriate, interlobate struc-
ture to the right. Reproduced from [1].
A marble with an ideal granoblastic structure will have a perfectly homogeneous distribution of
grain sizes. The more irregular the grain boundaries become, the larger the number of adjacent
grains. The number of adjacent grains also increases when the grain size distribution becomes
more heterogeneous as illustrated in Figure 3.3. It is also evident that the smaller grains can
have less than six adjacent grains while the larger grains can have many more than six adjacent
grains depending on the heterogeneity of the grain size distribution. This type of more complex
structure is referred to as serial interlobate structure or xenoblastic structure and is the typical

outcome of a dynamic recrystallization.
Pure calcite marbles are very anisotropic if the crystal matrix has a lattice preferred orientation
due to the the anisotropic thermal behaviour of the calcite crystal as explained in Section 3.4.
3.2.1
Petrographic analysis
The microstructure of the marble is very important in relation to the marble's suitability for
outdoor applications.
Marbles with more complex structures and hereby larger number of
grains have proved to be more suitable for thin façade cladding than marbles with perfect
or near-perfect granoblastic structures [13] [4] [21]. The microstructure of the marble can be
evaluated quantitatively be use of the Adjacent Grain Analysis (AGA), which is applicable for
calcitic marbles with a hexagonal crystal structure.
Figure 3.4: Example of four dierent calcitic marble types with AGA results from 6 to 9.
Reproduced from [1].
In the beginning of the AGA, the linear traverse method is applied to determine the grain
size distribution as described in the ASTM Specication C457-08.
For all the grains lying
completely or partly between the traverses, the Feret diameter along the longest axis of the
grains is measured and the median grain size is determined. Next the number of adjacent grains
for at least 50 median sized grains are counted. The AGA result will be the median value of all
the median sized grains. According to DS/EN 16306 [1] an AGA result of 6, meaning an ideal
granoblastic structure, is too low and the marble is unsuited for outdoor applications. The
most suitable marbles have 8 or more adjacent grains while marbles with 7 adjacent grains are
only semi-suitable, cf. Figure 3.4.
Tests of cladding on existing buildings have shown that the quality of the marble is related to
the number of adjacent grains, as the granoblastic marbles bowed to a larger degree than the
xenoblastic marbles [21] [16]. The high quality of marbles with a high number of adjacent grains
may be due to a better interlocking and hereby less dislocation of the grains, which decreases

Typically a heterogeneous grain size distribution will also be favourable for the durability of the

3.3
Ultrasonic Propagation Speed
Ultrasound is high frequency sound waves with frequencies beyond the human hearing range.
Depending on the use, ultrasonic waves can have frequencies in the range of 20 kHz up to more
than 1 GHz. Generally ultrasound can be used to measure distances using the principle of
echolocation. Ultrasound has been used for non-destructive testing since the 1960's to measure
the thickness of an object and for inspection of the microstructure of a material [5].
Ultrasonic testing is based on propagation of ultrasonic waves which are transmitted into an
object or material by a probe. The energy from the initially transmitted pulse is measured by
a receiver. The pulse is then travelling towards the back wall of the object and echoed back
towards the transducer losing some energy on the way. The remaining energy and the travel
time is detected by the receiver. The principle is illustrated in Figure 3.5.
Figure 3.5: Principle of ultrasonic testing. Reproduced from [5].
There exist two methods of receiving the ultrasonic pulse-waves: reection and attenuation. In
the reection method the sound waves are reected back towards the measuring device when
hitting an interface such as a wall or a material aw like a crack. Therefore the transducer must
be able to both emit and receive the pulses. The attenuation method is also called through-
transmission or direct transmission as a separate receiver detects the pulse from the transmitter

another impulse from the back wall of the void. As energy is lost when the pulse travels through
the void, the energy of the impulse stemming from the back wall will be reduced as illustrated
in Figure 3.5. In attenuation mode, the receiver only detects voids by a reduction in the energy

the sound of speed through the wet marble.

3.4
Permanent Expansion
On many buildings, a permanent expansion of the façade panels has been observed [16]. The
expansion can lead to large forces if the width of the joint between the panels is sucient to
cope with the expansion. These forces may lead to failure of the xings or the panels.
Multiple tests have shown that the permanent expansion of marbles is limited when the marble
is exposed to thermal cycles in dry conditions [4] [8]. However, when moisture is present, the
permanent expansion can grow almost innitely for some types of marble, cf. Figure 3.8a. Even
when the marble has reached a constant permanent expansion in dry conditions, the expansion
will continue to grow if the marble is subjected to wet cycles subsequently, cf. Figure 3.8b.
(a) Norwegian marble (Porsgrunn) [4].
(b) Swiss marble (Peccia) [8].

It is still not completely clear which mechanics cause the permanent expansion of marble,
however it is most likely due to the anisotropic thermal behaviour of the calcite crystal.
The thermal expansion coecient of calcite is 26 · 10−6 K−1 parallel to the crystallographic
c-axis but −6·10−6 K−1 perpendicular to it [30]. When calcite crystals are heated, the crystals
will expand in one direction and contract in the other direction, cf. Figure 3.9. As the crystals
cool down, the adjacent crystals will not return to their initial position, but rather be dislocated
and introduce microcracks between the crystals. The crystal matrix works almost as a jigsaw
puzzle in which the individual crystals are interlocked. If some of the crystals are skewed due
to heating-cooling cycles, the crystal will not t into the matrix causing the grain boundaries
to be pushed and hereby introducing a permanent expansion [18] [4].
The dilation of the marble will resemble that of calcite crystals, if the bulk crystal matrix has
a strong lattice and grain shape preferred orientation.
The maximum permanent expansion is typically seen parallel to the calcite crystal c-axes [4].

marble causing expansion and opening of grain boundaries which can be seen by micrography of
marbles after heat exposure. Figure 3.10 illustrates the increase of space between the boundaries

magnitude as explained in Section 3.6.

Bowing
As stated in the introduction, there have been many incidents where thin marble cladding
are bowing when used as façade panels [16] [9]. There is no exact reason to why the marble
is bowing and no certain pattern in how the panels are bowing. Some panels bow concavely
(inwards) and some convexly (outwards) on the same façade which is exposed to the same
weather conditions.
As showed in the TEAM project [16] the bowing occur a bit randomly and the geographic
location of the building does not explain why the marble is bowing at a certain rate or in a
certain direction, since both concave and convex bowing has occurred in dierent locations all
over the world.

the marble is wet and dried afterwards, no additional bowing is observed after the marble is
dried. Therefore it has been concluded that moisture and temperature cycles combined cause
the bowing of marble panels.

3.8
Surface Impregnation
Impregnating a material means to treat the material substrate with a special liquid or agent
in order to ensure watertightness or resistance to ingress of other materials. Typically impreg-
nating agents are used to waterproof porous building materials like concrete, brick and even
natural stone hereby preventing e.g. chloride ingress by water absorption.
The degree of wetting of a surface is determined by the force balance between the cohesive and
adhesive forces in which the surface tension plays a signicant role. If the surface tension is less
than the surface energy of the substrate, the uid will show a high degree of wetting. In this
occasion the contact angle of the water droplet will be smaller than 90◦as illustrated in Figure
3.17 and the surface material is termed hydrophilic. When the contact angle becomes larger
than 90◦, the surface material is said to be hydrophobic or non-wettable. The uid seeks to
minimize contact with the surface which is seen by formation of a liquid droplet (Figure 3.17).
The surface tension and surface energy of the substrate is directly related to the contact angle
through Young's equation, which will not be replicated here. As the surface tension of water
is constant, a reduction of the surface energy of the substrate will lead to an increased contact
angle and thus a lower wettability of the surface [34] [35] [36].
This feature can be achieved by an impregnating agent, whose purpose is to lower the surface
tension of the substrate hereby reducing the wettability. This will cause the water to keep its
droplet shape and run o the surface of the material rather than being absorbed by the material.

deterioration and bowing of the marble, it can explain why the bowing is reduced when the
marble is impregnated.

3.9
Extreme Temperatures
Lord Rayleigh conducted a series of experiments with marble baked in an oven at temperatures
up to 400 ◦C [18]. The experiments showed that the porosity of the marble increased with
increasing baking temperature. E Molina et al. (2013) [17] showed that increase of deterioration
and porosity walk hand in hand, and therefore it is obvious to assume that baking of the
marble will cause it to deteriorate. The ultimate strength is strongly reduced at high baking
temperatures, which again is a clear sign of deterioration. Lord Rayleigh also showed that
the exibility was increased with baking temperature and the marble experienced increasing
irreversible bowing when subjected to a load.
Not much information is given about the dierent marble types used in the experiments. It
is therefore of great interest to investigate a possible link between the marble quality and the
baking temperature's inuence on the material parameters, especially the bending strength.
3.10
Absorption, Desorption and Durability
J. Rodríguez-Gordillo and M.P. Sáez-Pérez [27] tested the absorption and desorption on marble
exposed to thermal cycles in dry conditions. The tests showed that both the absorption and
desorption increases with the number of thermal cycles, typically due to an increase in pore size.
As the absorption capacity increases, the material also becomes more sensitive. The ability to
absorb more water or other substances could potentially lead to damage by freezing or salt
ingress hereby reducing the durability of the marble.

The thermal expansion coecient was measured when the oven or water reached 78 ◦C.
In wet conditions the specimens were taken out of the container one at a time. As the specimens
were very hot, they were dicult to hold and place correctly in the measuring device. The
temperature may therefore have dropped by a few degrees before the specimens were placed
correctly.
When the specimens were measured in dry conditions, three specimens were taken out of the
oven at a time to minimize the number of times, the oven door had to be opened. Each time
the oven was opened, the temperature in the oven dropped 5-8 ◦C. To compensate, the oven
was allowed to heat up to 78 ◦C again before taking the next batch of specimens out. The
marble probably has a high enough heat capacity to keep the temperature at approximately 78
◦C even though the oven door was opened for a few seconds.
The temperature of the marble itself has not been measured, but as the temperature in the
oven/water container increased from 75 ◦C to 78 ◦C in a period of one hour, the marble had
most likely reached 78 ◦C when the measurement started.

The bowing data in Figure 5.2 suggested that the Gioia marble was not a Gioia type after all,

degree of grain interlocking. As described in Section 4.1.1, a Gioia marble will be granoblastic
with very smooth grain boundaries and a low number of adjacent grains.

6.1

6.1.1
Permanent Expansion
The maximum permanent expansion in wet conditions ranged between 0.2 and 1.5 mm/m. All
types except Venai, Canaloni and Cattani reached a steady level of permanent expansion after
3 or 4 cycles. The maximum permanent expansion was measured after either 22 or 36 thermal
cycles (20-80-20 ◦C).
It was expected that the expansion of all marble types would reach a steady level at some point.
However, there is no immediate explanation to the expansive tendency of the Venai, Cattani
and Canaloni marble types, as the expansion still increased linearly.
The Venai and Canaloni specimens had a coecient of variation of 0.34 and 0.60 respectively
compared to 0.07-0.27 of the remaining types. This indicated that the two types of marble are
very inhomogeneous, and it may have been expected that their standard deviation was high in
the other types of tests too.
The maximum permanent expansion in dry conditions ranged from only 0.10 to 0.17 mm/m,
which was much less than the permanent expansion in wet conditions, conrming previous test
results. All marble types reached a constant level of permanent expansion after only 3-5 cycles,
including Venai, Canaloni and Cattani.
The coecient of variation of Venai and Canaloni was also high in the tests in dry condition.
6.1.2
X60 Glue and Inserts
The rst batch of specimens was prepared with inserts. The inserts were xed with an X60 glue.
Based on results from previous tests [16] it was expected that the permanent expansion would
reach a steady level after 10 cycles when tested in wet conditions. However, the specimens kept
expanding and it was suspected that the glue also expanded. Tests with a bar made of glue
veried that the glue expanded permanently due to temperature and moisture cycles. The glue
==PAGE:110==
84
Discussion
contracted when exposed to temperature cycles in dry conditions.
It was decided to use specimens without inserts as the glue inuenced the results. However,
without the inserts, the measurement uncertainty increased more than expected.
It would therefore be advisable to use inserts when measuring the expansion to minimize the
contact area and measurement uncertainties. The inserts have to be made of stainless steel to
prevent them from corroding and they have be xed mechanically and not by glue.
6.1.3
Thermal expansion coecient
The thermal expansion coecient (TEC) was higher in wet conditions than in dry conditions
when measured in the rst cycle. As expected, the TEC decreased from cycle 1 to cycle 10,
as the TEC in cycle 1 also included the permanent expansion of cycle 1. The reduction of
the TEC from cycle 1 to 10 almost matches the magnitude of the permanent expansion of the
corresponding marble type.
However, it was unexpected that the TEC was reduced up to 40 % from cycle 10 to cycle 30
in wet conditions as the permanent expansion had reached a constant level for all specimens
except Venai, Cattani and Canaloni. The permanent expansion of the three types mentioned
increased almost linearly and it would therefore also be expected that the TEC measured in
cycle 10 and 30 would be equal.
The large dierence between the TEC of cycle 10 and the last cycle in wet conditions is most
likely due to measuring uncertainties. The measurement uncertainties due to the large contact
area between the specimen and the measuring device, may have caused some dierence, but
the measurement uncertainties alone can not explain why the TEC also decreased from cycle
10 to 30.
A decohesion of the marble grains can not explain the decrease in thermal expansion either, as
it would be expected that a looser grain structure would lead to a greater expansion. No other
immediate explanation of the behaviour can be reasoned.
The TEC of some types was almost twice as high in wet conditions as in dry conditions when
measured in cycle 1. In the last cycle, however, some marble types had a higher TEC in dry
conditions than in wet conditions. This may help explain why some of the marbles rst bow in
one direction and then in the other direction in the bowing test.
6.1.4
Permanent Expansion in Wet and Dry Conditions
There seemed to be no direct relation between the permanent expansion in wet and dry con-
ditions. Peccia had the highest dry expansion but lowest wet expansion. Venai expanded the
most in wet conditions but the least in dry conditions, which is unexpected as the marble was
very expansive in wet conditions.
The ratio between the wet and dry expansion ranged from 1 (Peccia) to 16 (Venai), but it
highly depended on the number of thermal cycles for Venai, Canaloni and Cattani as all types
kept expanding.

marbles with int-rolled surfaces.

The Gioia marble only reached a maximum bowing magnitude of less than 0.1 mm/m. As it

performed. The analysis showed that the Gioia marble had a very distinct xenoblastic structure
with a median of 8 adjacent grains opposed to the expected granoblastic structure with a lower
number of adjacent grains. Based on the actual microstructure, the marble was expected to be
of high quality and have a low bowing tendency. The results related well to the microstructure,
and it was concluded that the marble was not a Gioia marble.
6.3

It seems reasonable that the bowing may be caused by a dierential expansion between the dry
top side and wet bottom side of the marble specimens as sketched in Figure 3.14. Based on
the permanent expansion in dry and wet conditions (Section 5.4), the thermal expansion in dry
and wet conditions (Section 5.5) and the maximum temperature at the top and bottom side of
the specimens (Section 5.6), a curvature and a bowing of each type was estimated, cf. Table
5.7. The bowing estimation was then compared to the observed bowing (Section 5.2).
Peccia, Canaloni (÷) and Cattani all bowed the same way as estimated by the calculations,
but only the observed bowing magnitude of Canaloni (÷) was close to the calculated bowing
magnitude.
The bowing of Porsgrunn was opposite of what was estimated, but the bowing was in the same
order of magnitude.
It was unexpected that Venai bowed convexly, as the total expansion of the wet bottom side
was much larger than the expansion of the dry top side. Based on the calculated curvature,
Venai was expected to show severe bowing in the concave direction.
Some of the calculations estimated the bowing direction and magnitude direction correctly, but
some calculations were also way o. The dierential expansion between the top and bottom can
not explain the bowing alone, as the bowing mechanism is much more complex and depends
on more parameters than just the permanent expansion and thermal expansion.
Also the
variation of temperature and moisture content with time in each cycle has to be taken into
account. As explained previously, the surface treatment may also have an inuence due to
possible eigenstresses.

The ultrasonic propagation speed through the marble will probably depend on the temperature

The propagation speed was measured on two sides as shown in Figure A.4 in Appendix A.
The measurements through the thickness side were taken in order to have an estimate of the
propagation speed of the wet marble. It is not certain that the absorbed water had risen more
than 15 mm, so the marble may have been dry throughout the middle where the UPS was
measured, which also could explain why the UPS did not increase from dry to wet conditions
when measured on the "long side". With increasing number of cycles, the marble deteriorates
causing an increase in porosity and hereby absorption. As a consequence, the water would be
able to soak up to a higher level, which was seen by a greater increase in the UPS from wet to
dry conditions in cycle 50.
As explained in Section 3.3, the smallest dimension has to be larger than the wave length. With
a propagation speed of 5000-7000 m/s the smallest dimension has to be more than 100-140 mm.
As the thickness was only 30 mm and the width was only 100 mm, the measurements may have
been inuenced.
However, as almost all specimens had the same thickness and width, the
measurement "error" would probably be the same for all measurements.
The moisture content also inuences the propagation speed. As the moisture content could vary

from cycle to cycle, especially due to increased porosity, it was decided to use the propagation
speed of the dry marble when comparing the reduction of propagation speed with the exural

The exural strength of the reference specimens ranged between 15 and 23 MPa, which was in
the expected range of 5-25 MPa [8] [9] [39].
All types except Canaloni and Venai showed very little variation in exural strength. It was
expected that both Canaloni and Venai would behave this way, as the permanent expansion
tests also showed that both marbles are inhomogeneous.
The exural strength of the marble specimens did decrease when exposed to thermal cycles as
expected. It is worth noting that Gioia and Porsgrunn, which were assumed to be the lowest
quality marbles, had the lowest strength reduction. When looking at the microstructure of
the Gioia marble, it made more sense that it lost the least strength as it has a xenoblastic
structure (Appendix H). Canaloni and Cattani are classied as high quality marbles.
It is
therefore unexpected that these marbles had the highest strength reductions of approximately
40 %. However, the strength loss relates well to the bowing magnitude, which also was high for
both marble types as discussed in Section 6.5.2. The exural strength of the exposed specimens
also compared well with the strength measured by Siegesmund et al. (2008) [8] and Koch and
Siegesmund (2004) [9]. In their studies, the exural strength of marble panels with a bowing
magnitude of approximately 0.2 mm/m was between 10-14 MPa compared to 11-19 MPa in the
present project.
When determining the strength loss, it is also important to know how the strength has decreased
in time. A marble with a strength loss of 25 % measured in cycle 50 can be better than a marble
with a strength loss of 15 %, if the strength of the latter keeps reducing even after 50 cycles as
depicted in Figure 6.2. The strength was only measured after 50 thermal cycles in the present
project, however in future projects it would be interesting to investigate how the strength
develops with the number of thermal cycles. Also the relation between the number of cycles
in the bowing test and the corresponding years of exposure on a real structure would be of
interest to investigate.

6.5.1
Comparison with Ultrasonic Propagation Speed
It was desired to investigate the relation between the reduction of ultrasonic propagation speed
and the loss of exural strength. Figure 5.31 shows that the two parameters correlate well as
the R2-value is 0.31. The mean loss of exural strength was between 1.2 and 3.0 times the

By measuring the ultrasonic propagation speed on intact marble panels and exposed marble
panels of the same type on an existing building, the reduction can be calculated and used
together with Figure 5.31 to estimate the strength loss. It can provide valuable information on
the condition of the marble cladding almost instantly.
As the measurement of ultrasonic propagation speed is a fast and non-destructive method, it
will be both easier and cheaper to inspect and maintain marble claddings on façades.
6.5.2
Comparison with Bowing
Figure 5.33 shows that bowing and exural strength reduction correlated well as the R2-value
was 0.69. The mean loss of exural strength in percent was between 100 and 190 times the
bowing magnitude in mm/m with a mean slope of 132.
DS/EN 16306 [1] prescribes a bowing limit of 0.4 mm/m, corresponding to approximately a
50 % loss of exural strength according to Figure 5.33. It was deemed reasonable to assume
a maximum allowable strength loss of 30 % corresponding to a maximum bowing of 0.16-0.30
mm/m with a mean of 0.23 mm/m.
The TEAM project [16] even recommends a limit of 15 % strength loss for resistant marbles
which would correspond to a maximum bowing of only 0.11 mm/m.
Only the Gioia and
Estremoz VO marble were bowing less than 0.11 mm/m (the exural strength of Estremoz
VO was not measured). As explained previously, it is also very important to know whether
the measured strength loss is the maximum achievable strength loss or whether the strength
will keep decreasing when exposed to further thermal cycles as shown in Figure 6.2.
As a
consequence, the design parameter will not always be the strength loss measured after 50
cycles, but other parameters have to be taken into account too.

6.6
Breaking Load at Dowel Hole
The breaking loads of the reference specimens were all between 4.0 and 4.3 kN. Venai, which
was not tested in present project, had a breaking load of 1.6 kN measured in a previous project.
It was expected that there would be a larger variation between the breaking load of the refer-
ence specimens as seen for the exural strength. Furthermore the low breaking load of Venai
questioned whether the Hilti epoxy mortar somehow enhanced the strength of the marble.
The breaking load of the exposed specimens was reduced by 7-16 % which was far less than
the reduction of the exural strength (9-41 %). It therefore seemed as if the breaking load was
more dependent on the strength of the epoxy mortar than on the strength of the marble itself.

may prevent it from entering the pores.

6.6.2
Fracture Diameter vs. Breaking Load
It was observed that the fracture diameter decreased from 8-9 cm to 5-6 cm when using cement
mortar instead of Hilti epoxy mortar. An analysis of the relation between the fracture diameter
and the breaking load was therefore made. The "surface area" of the fracture was calculated
as a half ellipse, and the breaking load was then divided by the area to get a "stress".
All types except Cattani and Venai had a mean stress of 1.8-2.2 N/mm2. The stress of the
Porsgrunn specimens with cement mortar was 2.1 N/mm2, which was the same as for the
specimens with Hilti epoxy mortar.
It therefore seems like the Hilti epoxy mortar enhanced the strength of the marble by spreading
out the load on a larger area.
It was observed that the dowels still had a rm grip in the hole when xed with Hilti epoxy
mortar, and the dowels xed with cement mortar fell out after the test. The purpose of the
cement mortar is to ensure contact and grip between the dowel and the walls of the hole. The
strength and grip of the Hilti epoxy mortar, on the other hand, was so high, that it may have
simulated a Ø10 mm dowel instead of a Ø6 mm dowel. This might explain why the pressure is
spread out further hereby increasing the fracture diameter, and why the breaking load is higher
when the dowels are xed with Hilti epoxy mortar.
The coecient of variation of the breaking load of Venai and Canaloni was low compared to the
other test results. It was expected that Venai and Canaloni would have the highest coecient of
variation. However, as the breaking load of the marble may have depended more on the epoxy
mortar than on the strength of the marble itself, it could explain the low variation coecient.
6.6.3
Breaking Load of Dierently Sized Specimens
It was tested whether the breaking load of the virgin 200 mm x 200 mm Porsgrunn specimens
was the same as the breaking load of the ∼200 mm x 100 mm specimens that were previously
tested in the 3-point bending test. Based on a t-test there was a signicant dierence with 75
% condence.
A possible explanation of the dierence may be that the 3-point bending test initiates growth of
microcracks, which lowers the strength of the previously tested marble compared to the virgin
marble. The dowel holes are drilled at a distance of 10 cm from the center of the specimen,
where the stresses are half the maximum stress. Just like concrete, these large stresses may
cause microfractures and microcracks which in turn cause the initiation of new cracks when
loaded [43].

Extreme Temperature
As the bowing test typically has to run for at least 30 days before the marble quality can be
determined, it was investigated whether baking marble at high temperatures of 300 ◦C can
provide any indication of the quality in a faster way.
The exural strength of the specimens exposed to 50 thermal cycles from 20-80-20 ◦C was
compared to the exural strength of specimens exposed to a single thermal cycle from 20-300-
20 ◦C in an oven.
Only a single specimen was tested of all types except Porsgrunn of which four were tested,
thus not much data is available. However, there was no clear relation between the strength loss
measured in the two types of tests.
Also the loss of breaking load was compared between specimens exposed to the two dierent
temperature cycles. There seemed to be a good correlation between the reduction of breaking
load in the two tests. The breaking load of Peccia was reduced approximately twice as much
as the breaking load of the remaining types in both tests. However, the strength reduction
was almost equal for all types making it dicult to distinguish between high and low quality
marbles.
The Gioia marble showed no bowing tendency when tested in the bowing test. However, when
baked at 300 ◦C, the bowing magnitudes of all marble types were almost equal. Canaloni only
bowed 0.03 mm/m when heated to 300 ◦C but bowed 0.28 mm/m when heated to 80 ◦C in 50
cycles.
There was no correlation between the bowing measured after one thermal cycle up to 300 ◦C
and one thermal cycle up to 80 ◦C either.
It is therefore very questionable whether exposure to extreme temperatures can help determine
the quality of a marble. The accelerated bowing test has proved to be a good way to estimate
the marble quality. But the tests can not be accelerated unlimitedly as it probably would lead
to an incorrect conclusion.

6.10
Future work
6.10.1
Microstructural Analysis
As explained previously, the microstructure of the marble is very governing for the quality
and behaviour of the marble. A microstructural analysis - including e.g. an AG-analysis and
porosimetry - could give an indication of how and why the microstructure is changing due to
temperature and moisture cycles. It would also be interesting to see how the grain boundaries
change during exposure and also how the porosity and pore size are aected by the exposure.
6.10.2
Dierent Temperature Cycles
Battaglia et al. [28] showed that some calcite marbles may expand when exposed to even small
temperature changes. It would therefore be interesting to measure the permanent expansion
of marbles exposed to e.g. temperature cycles from 20-40 ◦C and 20-60 ◦C simulating the
temperature variation of autumn, winter and spring. The tests could help determine how the
expansion and bowing depends on the maximum exposure temperature. This could possibly also
help explain why panels on southeast- or southwest-facing façades (on the northern hemisphere)
typically bow to a larger extent than panels on north-facing façades [16].
6.10.3
Impregnation and DS/EN 16306 Bowing Test
As shown in Figure 5.17 the eect of the impregnation was reduced drastically after exposure
to 50 cycles in the bowing test. The bowing test was probably too harsh on the impregnation.
It would therefore be preferable to make a test, which simulates the environmental temperature
and heating cycles more realistically in order to test the eect that the impregnation would
have on an existing structure.
6.10.4
Mortar for Dowel Holes
There was a signicant dierence between the breaking load of specimens which used Hilti
epoxy mortar to x the dowels and specimens with cement mortar. It is interesting that the
breaking load can be so dependent on the type of mortar used. As a consequence, care must
be taken when testing the dowels as the strength may be over-/underestimated depending on
the mortar used. A future project could therefore test how the breaking load is inuenced by
the mortar used to x the dowels.
6.10.5
Strength Development
As discussed in Section 6.5, the tests provided no information about the development of strength
with the number of thermal cycles. It would therefore be interesting to perform the bowing
test and extract specimens in e.g. cycle 1, 5, 10, 15 etc. as depicted in Figure 6.2 and then
determine the exural strength or the breaking load at dowel hole of the specimens. This could
lead to valuable information about the strength development and hereby provide a better basis
for designing façade panels.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
Even though marble has been used for thin façade claddings for a century, there is still need
for knowledge about the use of marble. As marble is very popular on prestigious buildings, it
is necessary to use durable marbles to maintain the aesthetic appearance.
The main goal of this thesis was to investigate the eect of temperature and moisture variations
on the durability of marble cladding for outdoor application.
A total of 490 tests have been performed on seven dierent types of marble and with six dierent
test set-ups. The tests included:
• Bowing test according to DS/EN 16306 [1] with impregnated and unimpregnated marble
• Ultrasonic propagation speed measured on specimens in bowing test
• Permanent expansion of impregnated and unimpregnated marble specimens in wet and
dry conditions
• 3-point bending test of marble specimens exposed to 50 thermal cycles in the bowing test
and of marble specimens exposed to 300 ◦C
• Test of the breaking load at dowel hole of marble specimens exposed to 50 thermal cycles
in the bowing test and of marble specimens exposed to 300 ◦C
Bowing
Marble specimens were exposed to moisture and thermal cycles from 20-80-20 ◦C. None of the
tested marble types exceeded the limit of 0.4 mm/m prescribed by DS/EN 16306 [1]. The
absolute bowing magnitude was between 0.003 mm (Gioia) and 0.29 mm (Venai). The Gioia
was known to be unsuitable for outdoor application due to its severe bowing problems. However,
the Gioia marble tested in present project was proved to have a xenoblastic grain structure. An
adjacent grains analysis yielded an AGA result of 8, which explained the low bowing magnitude.
Permanent Expansion
The permanent expansion was measured after exposure to thermal cycles from 20-80-20 ◦C in
both wet and dry conditions.
The permanent expansion in wet conditions became constant after 3-4 cycles except for Venai,
Cattani and Canaloni, which had a maximum expansion of 1.49 mm/m, 0.74 mm/m and 0.65
==PAGE:126==
100
Conclusion
mm/m after 27 cycles and kept expanding at a constant rate. The permanent expansion of the
remaining types ranged from 0.19 mm/m (Peccia) to 0.29 mm/m (Porsgrunn).
The permanent expansion measured in dry conditions was constant after only 3 to 5 cycles with
a maximum expansion between 0.10 mm/m (Venai) and 0.17 mm/m (Peccia/Canaloni).
Thermal Expansion Coecient
The thermal expansion coecient was measured in cycle 1, 10 and the last cycle in the tests
with permanent expansion. The thermal expansion coecient decreased from cycle 1 to 10
in both wet and dry conditions, as the permanent expansion reached a constant level. The
thermal expansion coecient was reduced even further from cycle 10 to the last cycle in wet
conditions.
The thermal expansion had a higher contribution to the total expansion than the permanent
expansion, when the marble was dry.
The opposite applied to the wet marble, where the
permanent expansion of most types was more than 1.2 times the thermal expansion.
Moisture and Temperature Gradient
The permanent and thermal expansion of the wet bottom side and dry top side was calculated
using the temperature measured in the bowing test. The dierential expansion of the top and
bottom side was then used to estimate the bowing magnitude.
The bowing magnitude and bowing direction (concave/convex) of some marble types was esti-
mated correctly, but other specimens bowed less than estimated and in the other direction.
The dierential expansion between the top and bottom can not explain the bowing alone, as
the bowing mechanism is much more complex and depends on more parameters than just the
permanent expansion and thermal expansion.
Ultrasonic Propagation Speed
The ultrasonic propagation speed of all marble types was reduced after being exposed to tem-
perature and moisture cycles in the bowing test. The propagation speed measured on the dry
marble was reduced with 7-26 %. The increase of propagation speed from dry to wet conditions
was more pronounced after 50 cycles indicating an increased porosity and deterioration of the
marble.
Flexural Strength
The exural strength of the specimens tested in the bowing test was reduced with 9 % (Gioia)
to 41 % (Cattani/Venai) compared to the exural strength of the reference specimens. The
Canaloni and Venai marbles had large variations of exural strength indicating that both mar-
bles are inhomogeneous.
Breaking Load at Dowel Hole
The breaking load of all marble types using Hilti epoxy mortar to x the dowels was between
4.0 and 4.3 kN, which was approximately twice the breaking load measured in previous tests.
There was a signicant dierence between the breaking load of marbles with Hilti epoxy mortar
and marbles with cement mortar. The Hilti epoxy mortar increased the breaking load from
2.2 kN to 4.3 kN. It was also observed that there was a relation between the diameter of the
fracture caused by the test and the measured breaking load. Specimens with Hilti epoxy mortar
had a more widespread fracture than specimens with cement mortar.
The breaking load was reduced with 7 to 16 % when exposed to 50 thermal cycles.
==PAGE:127==
101
Impregnation
There was no signicant dierence between the bowing magnitude of the impregnated and
unimpregnated Porsgrunn and Gioia marble specimens. No dierence was observed between
the exural strength and breaking load of the specimens with and without impregnation either.
The moisture content of the impregnated Porsgrunn marble was slightly reduced compared to
that of the unimpregnated marble. The maximum temperature of the impregnated specimens
in the bowing test was signicantly lower than the temperature of the specimens without
impregnation.
It was observed that the eect of the impregnation was still intact after 50 thermal cycles,
albeit the eect was greatly reduced.
Extreme Temperature
Marble specimens were heated to 300 ◦C for 24 hours and the bowing, exural strength and
breaking load at dowel here were measured subsequently. There was no distinct relation between
the results obtained by exposure to 50 thermal cycles up to 80 ◦C and a single thermal cycle
up to 300 ◦C and it was not possible to distinguish between the marble quality based on the
tests. The accelerated bowing test has proved to be a good way to estimate the marble quality.
But the tests can not be accelerated unlimitedly.
Comparison Between Test Results
The exural strength was reduced between 1.2 and 3.0 times the reduction of the ultrasonic
propagation speed. The breaking load was reduced by 0.4-1.0 times the reduction of the propa-
gation speed, however the breaking load had a very large standard deviation, which questioned
the reliability of the model.
There was no immediate relation between the reduction of exural strength and reduction of
breaking load at dowel hole.
The exural strength reduction correlated strongly with the bowing. The strength reduction
in percent was between 100 and 190 times the bowing magnitude in mm/m.
The breaking load was reduced between 30 and 70 times the bowing magnitude.
There was a ne correlation between the maximum bowing magnitude and the maximum per-
manent expansion measured in wet conditions. The dry permanent expansion and bowing did
not correlate well. This may indicate that the wet permanent expansion is more inuential on
the bowing than the dry permanent expansion.
There was no relation between the maximum permanent expansion in wet and dry conditions.

Adjacent Grain Analysis of Gioia
Marble
The Bianco Carrara Gioia marble is known to be unsuitable for outdoor application as stated
in Section 4.1.1. However, when exposed to temperature and moisture cycles, the marble didn't
show the expected results. The bowing was only 0.003 mm/m (Figure 5.2) compared to more
than 5 mm/m from previous tests. The permanent expansion reached a constant level of 0.37
mm/m after only 3-4 cycles (Figure 5.5), but it was expected to keep increasing even after 20
cycles. It was therefore questioned whether it was a Gioia marble or not, and a thin section of
the marble was made.
Four pictures were taken in a polarization microscope in plane-polarized light. Figure H.1 and
Figure H.2 show thin sections of the Gioia marble with dierent magnications. The exact size
of the pictures is not known as it wasn't possible to measure by the equipment at DTU.
From the thin sections it is clear, that the marble has a distinct xenoblastic structure with a high
degree of grain interlocking. As described in Section 4.1.1, a Gioia marble will be granoblastic
with very smooth grain boundaries and a low number of adjacent grains.
It is therefore safe to conclude that the "Gioia" marble is not the same marble, which in previous
projects has been referred to as Gioia. In the remaining project, however, it will still be referred
to as "Gioia" as the origin hasn't been determined.

Expansion of Glue
All specimens with had an almost constant increase in permanent expansion in the rst 10
cycles when tested in wet conditions. It was suspected that the glue and not the marble was
expanding, as previous studies showed that weakly expanding marbles reached a constant level
of permanent expansion after approximately 5-10 cycles [16].
Two of the Canaloni specimens were therefore extracted from the water container after cycle 9
and placed in another container in which the temperature was kept constant at room temper-
ature. A third Canaloni specimen was put in an oven and dried at 80 ◦C. The specimens were
allowed to cool down to 20 ◦C before measurement and were then put in the oven again. All
three specimens were measured six consecutive day. The results are plotted in Figure K.1 and
compared with the expansion of the remaining Canaloni specimens.
Both specimens in the 20 ◦C water showed no further expansion. The specimen dried in the
oven (CAN-04) contracted almost 0.4 mm/m from cycle 9 to 10, but showed no signicant
expansion afterwards. It therefore seemed that the glue had no signicant inuence on the
permanent expansion of the marble if it was tested either in 20 ◦C water or in an 80 ◦C oven.
