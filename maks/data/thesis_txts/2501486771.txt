Abstract
Cross Laminated Timber, CLT has become more popular over the last decade as tall buildings up to 10 storeys
are build from this material outside of Denmark. CLT has proven a sustainable material and can be used as
load bearing ﬂoor and wall panels because of its in-plane and out-of-plane carrying capacities.
This thesis deals with the structural design of the main parts of a six storey student housing on Østerbro-
gade, constructed in CLT. Architechtural drawings by Vilhelm Lauritzen Architects made in collaboration with
MOE A/S forms the basis for the project. The structure is designed as a classic shearwall construction with
wall and slabs build from CLT elements. As fewer load bearing walls are present at the ground ﬂoor steel beams
are introduced in order to distribute the vertical loading. The total ﬂoor height might be increased if the beams
should not be visible, since the largest of the beam elements is a HEB-320 proﬁle.
The most critical elements has been identiﬁed from a vertical and horizontal load distribution, which forms
the basis for the design together with relevant theory on CLT. The ﬁndings show that the cross section of a
ﬁve layer CLT200 element is suﬃcient for the internal walls and a ﬁve layer CLT160 element is suﬃcient for
the external walls. For the complete design further investigations should be made to verify shearing in the ﬁre
situation for the walls with openings.
A ﬁve layer CLT150 cross section is suﬃcient for the roof slabs and a ﬁve layer CLT200 for the ﬂoor slabs.
The span of the ﬂoor slabs are relatively short which means that vibrations is kept at a minimal level for this
structure. Longer spans might be possible for some of the slabs but this can be critical for the deﬂection and
will increase the vibrations.
Most of the walls can be fastened with brackets and plates in order to prevent sliding and overturning. However
one wall was more critical regarding overturning and should be further investigated since anchoring with tensile
rods is not possible due to the capacity of the concrete foundation.
The horizontal loads are distributed under the assumption that the slabs work as an inﬁnite stiﬀdiaphragm.
This is ensured by designing slab-slab and slab-wall connections with screws in order to distribute the internal
forces in the diaphragm. Suction connections are designed with screws between the facade and slabs and brac-
kets between the roof and walls. The CLT members are designed for a 120 min standard ﬁre, whereby it was
found that the slabs and walls should be covered with two layers of plasterboard type F.
All in all the ﬁndings of this project shows that the geometry of the structure can be build from CLT ele-
ments, which is enabled by keeping the span of the slabs short and by introduction of load bearing steel beams.

Sammenfatning
Krydslamineret tømmer, CLT er gennem det seneste årti blevet mere populært idet høje bygninger med op mod
10 etager er blevet bygget med dette materiale udenfor Danmark. CLT er godkendt som bæredygtigt materiale
og kan anvendes som bærende gulv og væg paneler på grund af materialets lastfordelings egenskaber i planen
og ud af planen.
Dette speciale omhandler det strukturelle design af de primære dele af et seksetagers byggeri til studenter-
boliger på Østerbrogade, konstrueret i CLT. Arkitektegninger af Vilhelm Lauritzen Arkitekter udarbejdet i
samarbejde med MOE A/S udgør grundlaget for dette projekt. Konstruktionen er designet som et klassisk
tværvægs system med vægge og dæk fremstillet i CLT. Stueetagen er opbygget af færre bærende vægge end
de øvrige etager hvorfor stålbjælker er nødvendige til at fordele de lodrette laster. Den største bjælke er et
HEB-320 proﬁl og den total gulvhøjde bør derfor forøges hvis bjælkerne ikke skal være synlige.
De mest kritiske elementer er identiﬁceret gennem en lodret og vandret lastnedføring, hvilket sammen med
relevant teori om CLT danner grundlaget for designet. Beregninger viser at et værsnit bestående af et femlags
CLT200 element er tilstrækkeligt for indervæggene og at et femlags CLT160 element er tilstrækkeligt for yder-
væggene. Videre undersøgelser skal dog udføres for forskydning af vægelementerne med åbninger i brandtilstand.
Et femlags CLT150 element er tilstrækkeligt for tagelementerne og et femlags CLT200 element for gulvele-
menterne. Spændet for gulvelementerne er holdt relativt kort hvorved vibrationer er holdt på et lavt niveau for
denne konstruktion. Længere spænd vil være muligt for nogle af dækkene, men dette kan give kritiske nedbøj-
ninger samtidig med at det vil øge vibrationerne.
De ﬂeste af vægelementerne kan fastholdes mod glidning og væltning med vinkelbeslag og sømplader. Èn væg
er dog mere udsat i forhold til væltning og bør derfor undersøges nærmere, idet det ikke er muligt at fastholde
den med stålstænger da dette stiller for høje krav til kapaciteten af fundamentet som udføres i beton.
De vandrette laster er fordelt under antagelsen at dækelementerne virker som en uendelig stiv plade. Dette
opnås ved at designe dæk-dæk og dæk-væg samlingerne med skruer der fordeler snitkræfterne i pladen. Sam-
linger for sug udføres med skruer mellem facade og dæk og med beslag mellem tag og vægge. CLT elementerne
er designet for en 120 min standard brand, hvor det blev eftervist at elementerne skal beskyttes med to lag
gipsplader klasse F.
Alt i alt viser dette projekt at geometrien for konstruktionen er mulig at udføre i CLT, hvilket er sikret gennem
korte spænd for dækelementerne og indførelsen af bærende stålbjælker.

Introduction
This thesis covers the structural design of the main structural parts of a multi storey student housing on
Østerbrogade, constructed in Cross Laminated Timber. The project basis consists of architectural drawings
by Vilhelm Lauritzen Architects made in collaboration with MOE A/S. The structure consists of a basement,
a ground ﬂoor housing a café and ﬁve storeys above ground ﬂoor for residental usage. The main goal for the
project is to assess the optimal design solution for the structure by performing an analysis of the static sys-
tem including vertical load distributions, horizontal load distributions and joint distributions. The ﬁnal design
includes the dimensions of the load bearing parts and the design of selected joints above the ground ﬂoor.
Eurocodes and theory provided by [Brandner et al., 2010], [Wallner-Novak et al., 2014] and [Borgström and
Fröbel, 2017] forms the basis for the design calculations.
The overall objective of this project is to discuss the design solution of the structure in order to reveal some of
the advantages and disadvantages of the usage of timber as a load bearing material in tall buildings. CLT has
proven a sustainable material when extracted from well managed forests [Borgström and Fröbel, 2017] and it
provides such in-plane and out-of-plane carrying capacities that it can be used as wall and ﬂoor panels which
makes it a competetive alternative to concrete. CLT has become more popular over the last decades as tall
buildings up to more than 10 storeys are made from CLT outside of Denmark.
Firstly the report gives an introduction to timber as a construction material, and a short description of CLT
are given. Thereafter a Project basis describes the basis of design and construction criteria. The main load
bearing elements for ﬂoors and walls are chosen as CLT elements and steel are chosen for the load bearing beams.
A deﬁnition of the load bearing and stabilizing system are presented in Load distribution, where an intro-
duction of load bearing beams are suggested. Stability deals with the internal forces of the shear walls, which
shows the need for shear and tensile anchorage of the wall-wall and wall-slab joints.
Design of structural elements follows with a detailed description of the theory used for the design of the CLT
elements and the design solution for selected slab and wall members are presented. Walls with openings are
accompanied with an analysis made in FEMdesign. The following chapter Connections describes the design
procedure for selected stability connections and connections for wind suction. Most of the tensile and shear
connections can be designed with angle brackets and plates but one of the stabilizing walls need to be anchored
with steel rods. Screws are used to distribute the horizontal loading between slabs and between walls and slabs.
The load bearing members are veriﬁed for a standard ﬁre curve, and the resulting charring depths are presented
in chapter 8.
Chapter 9 serves as a discussion of the presented design solution, after which a conclusion serves to sum-
marize ﬁndings and ﬁnal design og the structure.
Drawings of the ﬁnal design solution are presented in appendix B.

Timber as a construction material
Timber is a well known building material and has beeen used for thousand of years. The material is one of the
most sustainable material if it is estracted from well managed forests. It has a high strength to weight ratio, it
is capable capable of transferring both tension and compression forces, it has good insulating properties against
heat and sound and its durability is relatively high. Many diﬀerent species can make a good structural timber
material, and it is therefore easily accessible all aorund the world. Furthermore the material can be easily
shaped and modiﬁed, and it can easily be connected using nails, screws, dowels, bolts and connectors [Porteous
and Kermani, 2013].
The limitations due to natural defects, available log sizes and maximum cross sectional dimensions and length
are overcome by developments in composite and engineered wood products. A uniform and high quality is
achieved by ﬁnger jointing and various lamination techniques which means that structural timber now is avail-
able in all sorts of shapes and with even higher strength properties than before [Porteous and Kermani, 2013].
Though timber forms a great construction material there is some characteristics of the wood which gives rise to
a certain variation in the material properties. The presence of strength reduction characteristics such as knots,
wood grain and the moisture content are some of the main issues regarding structural timber. Knots can have
a great inﬂuence on the strength properties since they distort the ﬁbres around them, and they are especially
a problem for sawn timber since the ﬁbers sorrunding the knot is no longer continuous, whereby the stress
becomes nonuniform around the knot. In general the size, shape, frequency and location of the knots inﬂuence
the quality of the timber. Wood grain is the longitudinal arrangement of wood ﬁbers or the pattern resulting
from this. In general the direction of the ﬁbres does not lie fully parallel to the longitudinal axis of the timber.
These grain deviations reduces the strength of timber, and rules has been made in order to classify the degree of
grain deviations. Other strength reduction characteristics are ﬁssures or cracks which can indicate some begin-
ning decomposition. The moisture content inﬂuence the stiﬀness and strength of the wood. Wood is in general
dimensionally stable when its moisture content is greater than the ﬁber sauration point, and there is an almost
linear loss in strength and stiﬀness as moisture content increases to about 27 % which corresponds to the ﬁber
saturation point. Most timber is dried to a moisture content between 17 and 23 % [Porteous and Kermani, 2013].
It can be quite diﬃcult to assess the strength capability of timber since it is a function of several parame-
ters including the species type, density, size and forms of members, moisture content, duration of applied load
and various strength reducing characteristics. To overcome this diﬃculty a strength grading method of strength
classiﬁcation has been devised. The design properties are determined non destructively through visual strength
grading or by machine strength grading, and the speciﬁc timber can then be classiﬁed according to its particular
strength, stiﬀness and density [Porteous and Kermani, 2013].
2.1
Engineered wood products
Engineered wood products, EWPs are developed in order to overcome the limtations of sawn timber. Many
diﬀerent kinds of EWPs are devloped in order to meet the demands of the building industry, but two of the
most popular EWPs are glulam and cross laminated timber. Some of the advantages of the EWPs compared
to structural sawn timber are according to Porteous and Kermani [2013] that
• They can be designed to meet speciﬁc requirements from the building industry
• Large sections or long panels can be manufactored from small logs where defects easily are removed
• They are often stronger and less prone to humidity-induced warping
The disadvantage to EWP’s is that it is more expensive to produce than solid timber, but this is often coun-
teracted by its ﬂexibility in use and its homogenity which makes it a much more reliable construction material

2.1 Engineered wood products
for the building industry.
2.1.1
Glulam
Glued laminated timber, also called glulam is constituted by several thin layers of timber boards bonded together
with durable structural adhesives. The layers are laid up so that the grain of all laminates is parrallel to the
longitudinal axis. The thickness of the individual laminates are typically 19-50 mm and the boards are connected
using ﬁnger jointing. The layering of the element makes it possible to match the lamination quality to the level
of design stresses, so that the higher grade laminates are placed in the outer more highly stressed parts and a
lower grade laminate is placed at the inner parts. Glued laminated members can function as beams or columns
subjected to combined bending and axial loading, and it oﬀers a great strength and stiﬀness to weight ratio.
2.1.2
Cross Laminated Timber
Cross laminated timber, also known as CLT and X-lam are solid panels build from at least three layers of timber
boards. CLT can have three, ﬁve, seven or more odd numbers of layers. The layers are stacked perpendicular
to one another, glued together and then pressed together over the entire surface area mechanically or by means
of a vaccum bag. The multi layered structure provides such in-plane and out-of-plane carrying capacity, so it
can be used as wall and ﬂoor panels. The panel thickness range between 50 to 500 mm and various panel sizes
can be made up till 4.8 m wide by 20 m long or even longer. Often the limiting factor governing their size
is the ability to transport, shipping or crane the panels. The panels are created so that the outer layers are
oriented in the direction of their major span. The CLT panels has improved stability when compared to that of
solid timber and it provide relatively high strength and stiﬀness properties in the longitudinal and transverse
directions, which enables two way spanning capability [Porteous and Kermani, 2013].

4.1
Summary
Load distribution deals with the vertical and horizontal load distribution of the structure. Vertical load distri-
bution deﬁnes the load bearing walls and it is clariﬁed that steel beams and a column are needed in order to
distribute the loading. The vertical load transfer in the walls and beams are calculated through Excel sheets in
appendix and the steel elements are designed in Mathcad. The ﬁnal design of the steel elements is listed in the
end of the section. Horizontal load distribution deﬁnes the stabilizing walls and the most critical load case was
found to be the wind load. The wind load is distributed to the shear walls by theory assuming an inﬁnite stiﬀ
diaphragm for the ﬂoor structure and the results are listed. Finally the internal forces in the slab elements are
statically distributed and listed. The internal forces in the slab elements will be used to design the connections
between the slab elements in order to ensure that the ﬂoor structure acts as a stiﬀdiaphragm.

B01 and S01

but this was not possible, so the adding of a column seems to be a good solution. It could be avoided to add a
steel column if beams are added spanning in the other direction as done on the left side of the building, but the
cross section of the beams will become quite big beacuse there is no supporting wall or column in the middle of
the room. It is therefore concluded that the most reasonable solution is to add a steel column S01.

5.3.1
Overturning
Brandner et al. [2010] presents four selected methods for calculating the resulting tensile force Ft. These methods
mainly consists of equilibirum equations primarily based on external loading and wall geometry. In this report
it was chosen to compare two of the methods, method A elaborated by Wallner-Novak and B elaborated by
Tomasi. The resulting tensile force from the two methods is compared in section 5.4. The full calculation can
be found in appendix J1 and J2.
Method A - Wallner-Novak
Wallner-Novak presents a stress block model, where the nonlinear stress distribution for the wood in compression
is substituted by a rectangular stress block. This stress block is assumed to have the constant width x = 1/4 · b
as presented in Figure 5.4. The inner lever arm for the overturning moment can then be found from the stress
distribution, and by dividing the overturning moment with the innner lever arm the tensile force is found.
There is no need for anchorage if the tensile force Zd is negative since the dead load in that case cancels the
overturning moment out. The equilibrium equation is written below [Brandner et al., 2010].

The CLT element is cabable of distributing loads in three directions, which is referred to as the global axes. The
direction of the ﬁbers of the individual boards are referred to as the local axes. The global axes are illustrated
in Figure 6.1 (a) and the local axes are illustrated in Figure 6.1 (b).
– x-axis is the global axis that runs parrallel with the ﬁber direction of the outer board layers. This is the
main direction of the load bearing capacity.
– y-axis is the global axis that runs parrallel with the ﬁber direction of the inner board layers. This is
the secondary direction for the load bearing capacity and it has a lower stiﬀness than the main direction
[Wallner-Novak et al., 2014].
– z-axis is the global axis that runs perpendicular to the ﬁbers of the CLT boards.
– 0-axis refers to the local axis for the boards that runs parrallel to the ﬁber direction.
– 90-axis refers to the local axis for the boards that runs perpendicular to the ﬁber direction.

8.2 General remarks
8.2
General remarks
In this project only the load bearing parts have been analysed in the ﬁre situation. As mentioned in section
3.4.3 it is required for the all elements except the rooﬁng area to endure a 120 min ﬁre without loss of load
bearing capacity according to [Traﬁk-, og Byggestyrelsen, 2016]. In order to fulﬁll this requirement all timber
is covered with a minimum of 2x15 mm gypsum, see drawing 6.1-6.3 in appendix B.
The load carryring parts are designed for a standard ﬁre situation. It should be noted that a standard ﬁre
resistance time can only be applied to ensure appropriate evacuation time, and can therefore not be used to
ensure the safety of valuable property of the building. It could therefore be considered to make a paramteric
ﬁre design. A parametric ﬁre design uses fully developed ﬁre curves, where the whole ﬁre process is taken
into account and more parameters, which have an eﬀect on the ﬁre cycle, is taken into account [Hertz, 2015].
However this method is more complex and the building is therefore only designed by the use of the standard
ﬁre method.
Timber in general can be considered a reasonable material regarding ﬁre safety, because it preserve its stiﬀness
and has a steady progress of charring Hertz [2015]. CLT elements are combustible and ignites when exposed
to ﬁre, the outer layer of the timber is turned into char which has no strength but acts as insulation because
of its lower heat conductivity whereby it protects the inner core of the wood. The formation of char is also
the reason why timber in general burns slowly. Underneath the char layer is placed the pyrolysis zone where
the temperature reaches 250 ◦C - 350 ◦C. The unburned core underneath the pyrolysis zone is unaﬀected since
the ignition temperature is not reached. Wide cracks, outer corners and metallic fasteners such as nails, srews,
dowels ect. will increase the temperature and thereby give rise to an increased burning rate of the timber
[Borgström and Fröbel, 2017].
According to [DS/EN 1995-1-2, 2007] higher strength and stiﬀness properties can be used for timber in the
ﬁre situation. The 20 % fractile is used for the strength properties as stated so the ﬁre strength in the design
situation becomes.

8.3
Standard ﬁre
Two methods can be used when designing a timber structure for a standard ﬁre a) The reduced cross section
method and b) The reduced strength method. For both methods a design charring depth dchar is calculated
based on the required time frame for the ﬁre duration. The cross section is hereby reduced initially with the
distance dchar, which depends on the charring rate for the speciﬁc type of timber and the type of protective
cladding. However the temperature distribution in remaining cross section needs to be taken into account as
well. Here the reduced cross section method assumes a pyrolysis zone k0 · d0, which is a layer without strength
and stiﬀness. The cross section is thereby reduced with an eﬀective charring depth def = dchar + k0 · d0 as
illustrated in Figure 8.1. For the reduced strength method no pyrolysis zone is assumed. Instead the strength
and stiﬀness properties are further reduced with the factor kmod.fi for the remaining cross section [DS/EN
1995-1-2, 2007] [Wallner-Novak et al., 2014].

The main goal of this thesis is to suggest a best ﬁtted design solution for the structural design of the building
student housing on Østerbrogade based on architectural drawings from Vilhelm Laurizen Architects and MOE
A/S. The overall design solution shows that it is possible to construct the whole structure in CLT, however
steel beams are needed in order to transfer the vertical loading between the load bearing walls because there
are fever load bearing lines at ﬁrst ﬂoor and ground ﬂoor.
The steel beams introduced on ground ﬂoor and ﬁrst ﬂoor has rather large dimensions.
However drawing
6.2 in appendix B of the ﬂoor structure reveals that if the ﬂoor structure above the CLT element is included in
the total ﬂoor height only 40 mm are left for insulation and gypsum plates if the total height of the ﬂoor struc-
ture should be 400 mm as planned according to the architechtural drawings in appendix A. It must therefore
be concluded that the suspended ceiling height should be increased under all circumstances in order to make
space for installations and insulation.
Two solutions are presented for beam B01 because the ﬁnal dimensions of the beam is a HEB-400 proﬁle
would require a total height of 760 mm for the ﬂoor build up. Here it was found that the HEB-400 can be
replaced by two HEB-300 beams as long as the cross section of the wall L07 walls doesn’t exceed 160 mm.
This is a reasonable solution since the walls in line L07 are not as heavily loaded as the two walls L03 and L06
designed in this project.
Another solution for transferring the vertical loads to the walls of the ﬁrst ﬂoor and the ground ﬂoor could
be to increase the span length of the slabs and avoid using the walls in line L07 and L11 as load bearing lines
by replacing these with lightweight walls. This might work for wall L11 since the span is only increased to 4.1
meter. However it should be checked if the wall is needed as a stabilizing wall which will require that the wall
is a CLT element, whereby the supporting beam B07 would still be needed. To avoid using line L07 as load
bearing will require a span of 7.8 meter of the slabs. It was found according to [Pedersen and Thomsen, 2019]
that spans up till 8 meter are possible for 7 layer CLT slabs, but it was problematic to meet the requirements
regarding deﬂection and vibration criteria. Another problem arises from the point that the walls contributes for
a great part to the overall stability, and it would therefore not be reasonable to avoid using CLT elements for
these walls. Furthermore it should be noted that if the slabs are continous sound will more easily be transported
to the neighbouring room, and the original solution is therefore better for the acoustics.
The veriﬁcations of the selected slab elements show that the deﬂection criteria is exceeded with 17 %. However
this is regarded as neglible, and since the frequency criteria is met the thickness of the slab elements of 200 mm
is suﬃcient. The spans of the slab elements are generally relatively small which is why ﬁve layer cross sections
can be used without compromising too much on the vibration criteria. The longest spanning elements, on 4.6
meter exceeds the acceleration with 83 %. This is much lesser than for the elements investigated by [Pedersen
and Thomsen, 2019], where the acceleration is exceeded by 613 % for elements spanning 8 meter. As shown in
detail 6.2 a layer of concrete is added to the top of the slabs. This reduces vibrations of the slab and makes a
good soundprooﬁng and should not be avoided, however it could be analyzed if less concrete could be used.
The stability calculations shows that most of the walls are not critical regarding sliding, tension and defor-
mation, and the walls can therefore be fastened by the use of brackets and plates. However one wall is critical
because it takes a large amount of the horizontal load which is only distributed over the length of 1 meter. This
means that the wall has to be fastened by the use of steel rods going from ﬁrst ﬂoor and all the way down to the
foundation, which places some demands on the basement concrete wall and foundation. Calculations shows that
the concrete cannot provide suﬃcient force to widthstand the tensile force and a new solution should therefore
be provided. One solution could be to treat the short wall as a column and not as a stabilizing wall. This will
leave the forces to be distributed to the longer walls of the system that are more capable of widthstanding the
overturning moment from the wind loading.

The vertically loaded walls are locally exposed to relatively high point loads coming from the load bearing
beams B03. The thickness of the internal wall L06 is therefore set to 200 mm, but the wall does not need to
be that thick if the wall is replaced with columns at these spots. This solution could be feasible if it can be
veriﬁed that the wall still can widthstand the horizontal forces when it becomes discontinous.
The walls are designed for a standard ﬁre situation which is on the safe side.
A parametric ﬁre analysis
could be conducted in order to ﬁnd out if some of the gypsum used to cover up the CLT elements could be
avoided, and thereby reducing the environmental impact even more.
In general this thesis shows that the structure can be constructed mainly from CLT elements if the ﬂoor
height is increased and steel beams are added to the load bearing structure. Another solution could be to use
the hybrid system suggested by Cree by Rhomberg. This solution presents a load bearing system where the
inner core is made from concrete to maintain stability and the vertical loads are distribued by the use of hybrid
slabs connected to wooden columns. The hybrid slabs is made from glulam beams supporting a reinforced con-
crete layer, and the hybrid slabs are connected to timber columns which together makes a frame structure. The
facade coverings can then freely be chosen as it does not contribute to the load bearing structure. Installations
can be build in between the ribs of the slabs, whereby suspended ceilings are not needed and the ﬁnal ﬂoor to
ﬂoor height can therefore be reduced. The Cree system oﬀers a lot of advantages and possibilities so it would
be interesting to look more into a structural design by the use of this.

Conclusion
The objective of this thesis is to propose a design solution for a multi storey student housing on Østerbrogade,
constructed in Cross Laminated Timber. The design is based on architechtural drawings by Vilhelm Lauritzen
Architechts made in collaboration with MOE A/S. Results based on the design solution evaluates on the capa-
bility of the model as a CLT structure and reveals some of the advantages and disadvantages of the usage of
CLT in tall buildings.
The structure is designed as a classic shearwall construction with the walls and slabs build from CLT ele-
ments. The load bearing walls are not continous for all of the levels. As fever load bearing walls are present on
the ﬁrst ﬂoor and ground ﬂoor steel beams are needed in order to distribute the loading.
All loads acting on the structure has been deﬁned and analyzed for every critical load combination.
The
vertical snow load and live load are distributed as a surface load on the slabs which is transferred to the load
bearing walls and beams. The horizontal loads arising from geometric imperfections, seismic load and wind load
has been deﬁned and it was found that the most critical horizontal load is the wind. The wind load is distributed
to the shear walls by the use of the theory of an inﬁnite diaphragm. Not all of the stabilizing walls continues
all the way down to the ground ﬂoor which means that three diﬀerent wall arrangement are analysed, one for
the ground ﬂoor, one for the ﬁrst ﬂoor and one for the second ﬂoor and up. The slabs need to work as a stiﬀ
diaphragm in order to distribute the loading to the shear walls. This requirement is met by designing the con-
nections between slab-slab and slab-walls for the moment and shear forces in the slabs arising from the wind load.
The shear walls of the building are constructed from CLT elements working as a diaphragm, whereby the
horizontal loading is transferred between the walls and down to the foundation. The horizontal loading is there-
fore accumulated for each level so that the ground ﬂoor has to carry the total horizontal loading. The most
critical walls are selected and checked for overturning, sliding and deformation. The overturning and sliding are
veriﬁed for ULS5 (dom. wind load) which is the most critical load case for horizontal loading and deformation
is checked in SLS3 (characteristic leading wind load). Only wall L12 and L06 are in the risk of overturning. The
risk of overturning for wall L06 is signiﬁcantly higher than for wall L12 which is because wall L06 is short, only
1 m when compared to the load acting on it. Most of the load bearing walls are not in the risk of overturning
or sliding because the vertical loading from the slabs are suﬃcient for holding the walls in place. However the
walls still need to be anchored regarding tensile and shear forces in order to meet the deformation criteria. The
individual and total deformation is checked for each of the critical load bearing lines and it was found that none
of the cases are critical when a reasonable amount of shear and tensile brackets are used.
The most critical structural walls and slabs are designed according to ultimate limit state (ULS), serviceability
limit state (SLS) and the ﬁre scenario (ALS). As CLT is not yet included in Eurocode 5, verﬁcations has been
made according to theory described in [Wallner-Novak et al., 2014], [Borgström and Fröbel, 2017] and [Brandner
et al., 2010]. A 150 mm slab is required for the roof structure in order to span 4.1 meter and a 200 mm slab is
required for the ﬂoor structure in order to endure the live loading on 5 kN/m2 for the common access routes.
The slabs exceed the deﬂection criteria with 17 % which is considered to be neglible since the calculations are
on the safe side. If longer spans was needed there might be some diﬃculties meeting the deﬂection an vibration
criteria, whereby it can be concluded that the design solution for the slabs are acceptable. The slabs need to
sustain the loading from the wind when it is distributed between the shear walls however this has proven not
to be critical. Furthermore the slabs are veriﬁed locally for pressing transverse to the element plane for all of
the places where the walls meet the slabs, and none of the cases was critical.
The walls are categorized into A: bearing and stabilizing facade walls, B: bearing and stabilizing inner walls and
C: walls that are only stabilizing. All of the walls has been veriﬁed for the vertical and horizontal load impact,
and those walls with openings are supplementet by an analysis made in FEMdesign. Wall L02 is the most
critical wall of type A because it has the largest openings. Buckling in ALS1 (ﬁre, dom. dead load) was found
to be the most critical load case with an utilization degree of 78 %. But also compression transverse to the
ﬁber was found critical as it is exceeded with 19 % for ULS5 (dom. wind load). A cross section of 160 mm are

needed for walls of type A. The two most critical walls for type B are wall L03 and wall L06. Wall L06 is critical
regarding shearing with an utilization degree of 61 % and the wall L03 is more utilized in the bukling state, 47
%. Both the wall cases shows that a cross section of 200 mm are suﬃcient. Wall L12 is the most critical for
wall type B. The wall is highly exposed to horizontal loading whereby compression perpendicular to the ﬁber
becomes critical with an exceeding 39 %. However it is still assessed that a cross section of 160 mm is suﬃcient,
since compression perpendicular to the ﬁber in general isn’t critical for the total load bearing capacity of the wall.
Stability connections are designed in order to ensure the load transfer between the diaphragms. A solution
for tensile anchoring are suggested for wall L12 and L06. Brackets and plates are used for tensile anchoring
of wall L12, but wall L06 needs to be anchored with steel rods embedded in the foundation. However the
foundation will most likely not be able to sustain the tensile force so this solution should be reconsidered. One
solution could be to make the wall non-stabilizing leaving the horizontal impact for longer walls. However this
will require a new analysis of the stabilizing system, and due to time limits this was not done in this project.
Wall L12 is designed for sliding where it was found that plates and brackets are able to sustain the shear force.
It is checked that the stabilizing walls complies with the deﬂection criteria which places some demands on the
shear and tensile joints. Here a high slip modulus is preferred, which is provided by shear and tensile brackets.
The slab-slab connections and wall-slab connections need to transfer the internal forces due to horizontal load-
ing. This is ensured by the use of screws. The connections to transfer wind suction are done by the use of
screws for wall-slab connections and brackets for roof-wall connnections.
Connections between walls has not been assessed in this project, but it is assumed that continous elements can
be used whereby there is only need for connections in wall corners.
The CLT members has been designed for a standard ﬁre by the use of the reduced cross section method under
the assumptions that all CLT surfaces are covered with 2x15 mm plasterboard type F, the distance between
the boards are less than 2 mm, and that the boards are glued with delamination. The design charring depth is
calculated in accordance with theory described in [Borgström and Fröbel, 2017], and the total eﬀective charring
depth was found to 79 mm for the slabs and 81 mm for the walls.
All in all this report can conclude that the structure can be build from CLT elements, which is ensured by
keeping the slab span short and by the introduction of load bearing steel beams.
