ABSTRACT
This thesis examines The Circularity Assessment Tool (CAT) in order to develop a redesign of the tool to 
improve the results as well as the process of using the tool for the potential companies. The CAT is a concept 
level circularity indicator and it has been developed as a part of the CircIt project. This thesis therefore 
contributes to this overall project.
By comparing the CAT to other tools and existing ways of evaluating product circularity, it is apparent that 
some inspiration can be drawn from these in order to make improvements in the redesign. Especially, in the 
area of the usefulness of the tool, it is found that other circularity indicators have incorporated, what is being 
designed for in the assessment. Additionally, in terms of usability some of the other tools are performing 
better in tests of clarity and simplicity of the way the assessment is performed.
Based on these comparisons, tests of the initial tool and interview with the developer, Sasha Shahbazi, the 
first version of the redesigned tool was developed. The important changes include alterations in the detail 
level of the assessment tool, to address the fact, that this tool, as opposed to most other tools, is assessing the 
circularity potential in a concept and not a finished or somewhat finished product. The tool is also improved 
in terms of guiding the user towards what strategies are important to design for, and thereby pointing towards 
improvement potentials.
The first version of the redesigned CAT is tested and improved in certain areas as part of this thesis, especially 
in terms of the usability of the tool, some improvements were necessary. But further testing with more 
companies working with different product types will need to be done in order to ensure, that the tool is 
capable of providing the necessary guidance.

1 INTRODUCTION
This thesis is written as a part of the CIRCit project, 
a project created to help companies with tools and 
guidance on transitioning their business to become 
more circular by looking at different aspects of the 
circular economy (CIRCit, n.d.). Figure 1 shows 
the six different work packages within the project, 
and this thesis will be contributing to work package 
three, circular product design. Circular design is 
important as the choices made for the product 
design are essential for the impacts of the product 
during its lifetime. UN environment estimates that, 
the design specification decides around 80% of the 
environmental impacts for a product (Nasr et al., 
2018). 
Particularly, this thesis will be examining a specific 
tool, to see how it performs in the given context, and 
attempt to improve possible downfalls. The project 
takes starting point in the Circularity Assessment 
Tool, which is being developed for the CIRCit 
project work package three. The purpose of the 
tool is to assist the user in evaluating and choosing 
between concepts and to find areas of improvement.
Linked with the current circularity assessment 
tool, is a set of guidelines (Guidelines for Circular 
Product Development), which should act together 
with the tool to guide users towards creating more 
circular products. Therefore, this project is also 
In recent years, environmental issues such as global warming, environmental 
pollution and plastic in the oceans, have raised the awareness that the take, 
make, dispose linear economy cannot continue indefinitely. In order to avoid 
these issues, a more circular economy and a rethinking of the way resources 
are used, is essential (Nasr et al., 2018). This awareness is also arising in many 
companies, that wants to transition into a more circular economy (Geissdoerfer, 
Savaget, Bocken, & Hultink, 2017).

1.1 The goal and problem statement
Evaluating product circularity is a tricky thing as it 
depends on multiple aspects of the product lifecycle 
and the product design. It is much easier to measure 
circularity in retrospect than evaluating what effect 
the design decisions have on the circularity. When 
the product is detailed there is, however, few things 
that can be changed. Saidani, Yannou, Leroy, & 
Cluzel, (2017a) argue that “operational guidance for 
engineers, designers or managers to improve their 
products in a circular economy context are missing”. 
Therefore, a tool for evaluating circularity potential 
at an early product stage could be called for, and the 
Circularity Assessment Tool (CAT) is an attempt to 
target this area. The performance of the tool should 
therefore be evaluated, in order to improve areas 
which are less optimal.  The goal is to design a tool 
which can be used broadly in product development 
companies and guide them in the early development 
towards creating more sustainable products. 
The process of reaching this goal can be defined 
through the following problem statement:
•	
What are the criteria for a good concept level 
circularity assessment tool?
•	
How does the initial assessment tool perform 
in this context?
•	
How can the circularity assessment tool 
be redesigned in order to better fulfill the 
requirements and criteria for good concept 
level circularity assessment tool?

2.1.1 Research clarification
The main activities in of the Research Clarification 
is to investigate how different researchers have 
described the circular product development and 
the ways circularity can be calculated. This included 
finding other tools which does this, understand 
these. For this purpose, it is also deemed important 
to investigate the concept of circular economy and 
get a more general understanding of approaches for 
implementation of circular economy. The questions 
to be answered in the Research Clarification are: 
How is Circular Economy defined through literature? 
What are the proposed strategies for implementing CE? 
What frameworks and guidelines exist for designing 
products for the circular economy? How is circular 
economy measured at a product level, and what types of 
indices, metrics and tools exist for measurement?
Based on the research, the aim is to find a clear goal 
and focus for the further project. The research should 
give an initial indication of the ways of calculating 
circularity during development and what purpose 
such a tool should fulfill. 
The procedure for finding literature included using 
different combinations of search words. For the 
main areas of the review, circular indicators, a more 
systematic approach was used, with inspiration 
from (Biolchini et al., 2005). A detailed description 
of the process of finding literature can be found in 
appendix 1. 
2.1.2 Descriptive study I
In the descriptive study, a better understanding of 
the problem and the desired state is investigated to 
give a basis for creating a redesigned solution. In 
this phase, a deeper understanding of the process of 
using the circularity assessment tool is investigated. 
This includes an interview with the developer (Sasha 
Shahbazi) which gives input in regard to how the tool 
should be used as well as which elements will work 
well in practice and which could need improvement 
or alterations. Also, the context in which the tool 
should be used is investigated further to evaluate 
whether this is optimal. To gather additional input 
in regard to use of the tool it is tested using cases. 
This can help to identify breakdowns in relation to 
using the tool.
Additionally, an evaluation and comparison 
of different tools that are comparable with the 
Circularity Assessment Tool within the CIRCit 
project, is performed. The tools are compared based 
on different parameters which can be used to evaluate 
the tools fit for the specific purpose. The evaluation 
is therefore not a general evaluation of which tools 
are best, but an evaluation to illuminate which tools 
include good solutions that can be incorporated into 
a redesigned assessment tool. 
The analysis amount to a design specification which 
defines the requirement and criteria for a desired 
state for a redesigned solution. 
2.1.3 Prescriptive Study
Based on the findings from the first two phases, 
there is a clear understanding of what needs to 
be improved in the tool and a clear definition 
of the desired state. Based on the structure of the 
Circularity Assessment Tool, the tool is divided into 
sub-function. For the development of sub-solution, 
existing tools are used as inspiration for finding new 
ideas. This acts as the basis for an ideation process, 
where different concepts for redesigning the tool 
are generated through a mind map. Based on the

concepts are evaluated, and the best performing

2.1.4 Descriptive Study II
In the second descriptive study, a verification of the 
improved solution is performed. The verification 
consists of two parts. The first is interviews with 
companies involved in the CIRCit project, who 
can evaluate on the idea behind the redesigned tool 
and give feedback on what can be improved. The 
second part is test with relevant companies. These 
tests are performed to assess both the usefulness of 
the tool and its result and the usability of the tool. 
Though the usefulness of the tool is prioritized 
which means that in order to get the best possible 
feedback on usefulness, the user is guided through 
the test and can ask questions throughout. In this 
way the usability becomes less of an obstacle for the 
evaluation.

3 LITERATURE STUDY
3.1 Circular economy as a concept
Circular Economy is a concept which have gained 
a lot of attention, especially during the past decade, 
both by academics and by practitioners (Geissdoerfer, 
Savaget, Bocken, & Hultink, 2017). This is likely 
due to an increased recognition that the linear model 
of consumption, where we take, make and dispose 
resources and products cannot continue indefinitely. 
It is simply not sustainable, neither for businesses 
nor for the environment. Many companies are 
beginning to see the risks of continuing the linear 
economy, like prices increasing on resources they 
are depending on (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 
2013) or regulations in terms of pollution. A 
change in the way we produce and consume is an 
extremely relevant topic now, with increased focus 
on sustainability due to climate issues, plastic in the 
ocean etc. 
Circular economy can be seen as an opposition 
to the linear economy. The circular economy is 
described as a restorative economy where the end-
of-life concept is exchanged with regeneration (Ellen 
MacArthur Foundation, 2013). Figure 3 shows a 
graphical representation of the circular economy as 
defined by the Ellen MacArthur Foundation. The 
figure shows the regenerative nature of the circular 
economy, the many different loops of recirculation 
and the different levels of technical and biological 
cycles within the circular economy. The technical 
side, on the right, shows the different levels for 
recirculation of technical nutrients, to avoid loss of 
material and value. The left side shows the biological 
cycle, in which biological nutrients are repurposed 
or returned safely to nature by anaerobic digestion 
or composting.  Within a perfect CE, leakages to 
the surrounding system is to be avoided completely 
but in practice the CE aims at minimizing leakages 
of resources and pollution from the system (Ellen 
MacArthur Foundation, 2013).
 
Circular Economy is a very popular concept and a 
concept with multiple definitions. As the amount of 
literature on the subject increases, the concept seem 
to broaden (Kirchherr, Reike, & Hekkert, 2017). 
Blomsma and Brennan (2017) explains CE as an 
==PAGE:22==

umbrella concept, which covers multiple different 
concepts or frameworks under one umbrella. 
They also explain that umbrella concepts arise 
when there is no existing development paradigm 
(Blomsma & Brennan, 2017). This seems to be 
the case for circular economy where the amount of 
frameworks and definitions within are continuing to 
increase (Kirchherr et al., 2017). Circular Economy 
have links to industrial ecology, cradle to cradle, 
performance economy, blue economy and others 
(Homrich, Galvão, Abadia, & Carvalho, 2018). 
The many different frameworks also contribute to 
different approaches to implementation of circular 
economy and different aims for the implementation. 
For example, cradle to cradle focuses on recirculation 
of materials and resources in a closed loop supply 
chain, while performance economy focuses on 
delivering value in a different way (Geisendorf & 
Pietrulla, 2018). For CE, the most prominent aims 
described through literature is economic prosperity 
and 
environmental 
quality 
with 
economic 
prosperity being the most important for private 
sector practitioners (Kirchherr et al., 2017). That 
CE is a means for economic prosperity could be a 
reason why many companies are finding the concept 
increasingly interesting.
Some literature on circular economy focuses mostly 
on recycling to implement CE, which is a very 
narrow approach to the concept, as it is not in all 
cases the most sustainable solution, especially since 
recycling is often times equal downcycling (recycling 
to a lower quality of material) (Kirchherr et al., 
2017). In the next sections, larger focus will be on 
strategies for implementation of CE and how these 
correlates with a sustainable development.

3.2.1 Circularity and sustainability
Circular Economy is in many ways related to the 
concept of sustainability. The two concepts have 
many similarities although the overall aim of the 
two concepts are not the same. The concept of 
sustainability is targeting a triple-bottom-line, 
which means it accommodates both environmental, 
economic and social aspects (Geissdoerfer et al., 
2017). Within the Circular Economy, the economic 
system is prioritized and mainly the economic 
and environmental concerns are benefitted, while 
the social aspects are only affected indirectly 
(Geissdoerfer et al., 2017). The two concepts still 
have overlaps in which “CE has the potential to 
understand and implement radically new patterns and 
help society reach increased sustainability and wellbeing 
at low or no material, energy and environmental costs” 
(Geisendorf & Pietrulla, 2018). Also in terms of 
their approach towards reaching their aim, there 
are some similarities, as described by Geissdoerfer 
et al. (2017): “both concepts frequently employ multi- 
or interdisciplinary approaches to better integrate 
non-economic aspects into development, which often 
conclude that system design and innovations are the 
main drivers for reaching their ambitions”. 
Even though circular economy, aims at a more 
sustainable way of consuming and producing, there 
are cases where the improved circularity actually 
increases the environmental impacts, due to the 
additional activities and changes in supply and 
demand (Zink & Geyer, 2017). According to Zink 
& Geyer, a central question is whether the secondary 
production actually replaces primary production or 
creates a new market and increases demand (Zink & 
Geyer, 2017). This can be the case for both products 
and resources. In some cases, recycling materials 
require added activities, which minimizes or even 
eliminates the environmental benefits from avoiding 
new production (Andersen, 2007). This is a possible 
direct rebound effect of the recycling of products. 
Also, in terms of the use stage, there are possible 
rebound effects. An often occurring rebound effect 
from products getting increasingly energy efficient, 
is that this can lead to an increased use, hence 
minimizing the environmental benefit from the 
product efficiency (Zink & Geyer, 2017). There will 
often be risks of burden-shifting where decisions, 
which are beneficial from one point of view, has 
a negative impact on another. An example of this 
is to incorporate recycled plastic in production, 
minimizing virgin material use from production, 
which might in turn make the material un-recyclable 
at the end of the products life (Kravchenko, 
McAloone, & Pigosso, 2019). Another example is 
making cars in lightweight carbon-fiber to maximize 
fuel efficiency, but this choice makes the material 
difficult to recycle when the car is no longer in 
service. In many areas there exist trade-offs and 
these are some of the challenges when designing for 
circular economy and it highlights the importance 
of approaching CE development holistically.  
3.3 Circular product development
When working towards a more circular model 
of production and consumption, the product 
design highly influences the product lifetime, the 
resources used for production and whether it can 
be upgraded, remanufactured, recycled etc. Circular 
product design is therefore an important element to 
a circular economy transition. In the development 
of frameworks for circular product design, many 
researchers have found inspiration within existing 
frameworks like eco-design and Design for X (DfX).
Eco-design is a widely used concept for improved 
==PAGE:25==
Literature study | 13 
environmental performance of products. Eco-design 
focuses on improving products’ environmental 
performance throughout the life cycle by minimizing 
material and energy use, choosing less impactful 
materials and promoting long-lasting products 
through durability, upgrade, repair etc. (Hauschild, 
Rosenbaum, & Olsen, 2017 (chapter 23)). Pigosso 
and colleagues (2015) describe that “Eco-design, a 
proactive approach to environmental management, 
involves the consideration of environmental issues in 
the product development process in order to minimize 
environmental impacts throughout the product’s life 
cycle, without compromising other essential criteria 
such as performance, functionality, aesthetics, quality 
and cost.”
Eco-design implementation is included in standards, 
guiding companies on how to use eco-design to 
improve products (European Commission, 2009). 
Many other guidelines for implementation and 
methods for evaluating products exist but there seem 
to be a confusion on how to approach eco-design 
and use the tools that exist (Pigosso, McAloone, & 
Rozenfeld, 2015). An important element in eco-
design is assessment of potential impacts, which can 
be done in a more or less quantitative way. One tool 
for measuring this is Life Cycle Assessment (LCA), 
but less extensive methods were introduced to enable 
environmental evaluation already at an early point 
in the product development (Pigosso et al., 2015). 
Recently, some researchers within circular economy, 
argue that eco-design is a concept within the linear 
economy and does not fit the mindset of a circular 
economy (den Hollander, Bakker, & Hultink, 2017; 
Moreno, 2016). Eco-design is criticized for focusing 
on producing less worse or better products instead 
of focusing on more innovative solutions.
Den Hollander and colleagues (2017) point out 
that circular product development and eco-design 
are fundamentally different, and therefore a new or 
adapted approach should be used for circular product 
development. Recent studies in eco-design show 
that the concept is continuously developing and that 
considerations such as circularity and increased life 
cycle thinking is tried implemented into the concept 
(Pigosso et al., 2015). This could indicate that the 
two concepts are implementing elements from each 
other, to create frameworks even more suited for a 
new sustainable and circular economy. 
Another 
principle, 
which 
circular 
product 
development finds inspiration from is Design for 
X (DfX). Design for X, focuses on different aspects 
of product design to create products with specific 
qualities in mind (Go, Wahab, & Hishamuddin, 
2015). Within this, Design for Sustainability (DfS) 
is related to eco-design and contains strategies on 
how to create more sustainable products (Mendoza, 
Sharmina, Gallego-Schmid, Heyes, & Azapagic, 
2017). These include; design for upgradability, 
maintenance, repair, refurbishment etc. According 
to Moreno and colleagues (2016), DfX is applicable 
to circular design, since it both contains preventive 
and closed loop design. This means that the DfS both 
focuses on product characteristics for preventing 
obsolescence; like upgrade, repair, refurbish etc. and 
design for closing loops; like design for recycling.
Some different frameworks for circular product 
design are proposed from different researchers. 
Den Hollander and collogues argue that the most 
important elements of circular product design is 
design for product integrity and design for recycling 
(den Hollander et al., 2017). Design for product 
integrity includes a range of physical properties 
related to keeping products in the loop (durable 
design, upgradability, maintainability, repairability, 
refurbishment and remanufacture). The framework 
seems to have reduced complexity of the approaches 
towards circular design. The framework does not 
target how to reduce or avoid use of materials 
and resources in production. Den Hollander and

In this project, the Circular Strategy Scanner, 
presented by Blomsma et al. (2019) is used as a top-
down approach defining product design guidelines 
within these strategies.

3.5 Product circularity indicators
Within product development, being able to access 
a concept or products performance is essential for 
improving or choosing between different concepts. 
Researchers within CE state the importance of 
using circularity indicators or metrics for measuring 
circularity as a way of evaluating performance 
(Gabrielsen & Bosch, 2003).

proposed, there is no general way of measuring 
product level circularity (Saidani, Yannou, Leroy, 
Cluzel, & Kendall, 2019). The tools that exist, differs 
extensively when it comes to level of analysis, type of 
data, number of indicators etc. Some tools are based 
on quantitative data like the flow of materials and 
energy while others are based on the user’s personal 
evaluation in a more subjective manor. Many CE 
indicating tools focus on a single aspect like recycling 
rate or resource use. This gives limited information 
about the circularity, since many other aspects of

Within Eco-design, LCA or other impact assessment 
methods are often used for evaluating product 
sustainability performance, which represents a 
well-defined method for measuring environmental 
impact. Such an approach does not exist for CE, 
but many approaches similar to LCA are used to 
analyzing circularity (Saidani, Yannou, et al., 2019). 
The benefit from assessments like LCA is that the 
analysis will be relative reproducible, as it is based on 
quantitative data, but it also sets high requirements 
for data availability and resources for execution. 
For this project, the scope is product circularity at 
a concept level. This means that extensive amount 
of data will likely not be available. According 
to Saidani, Yannou, Leroy, & Cluzel (2017b) 
circularity indicators should also provide additional 
information than what can be given from LCA like 
different mechanisms for closing the loops. LCA 
does not give direct indication of circularity but 
focuses on the environmental impacts during the life 
cycle. This is also pointed out by Franklin-Johnson, 
Figge, & Canning (2016), who describes that “If 
current practices focus on the reduction of negative 
burdens and not the comprehensive vision of material 
and product use (independent of their burden), then 
they are perhaps inadequate for guiding decisions that 
are at the very heart of Circular Economy (…)”. They 
indicate that using impact-based methods cannot 
fully assess aspects of CE.  
Linder, Sarasini, & van Loon (2017) have proposed a 
product circularity indicator which looks at material 
or product reuse. They “(…) define circularity at the 
product level as the fraction of a product that comes from 
used products (i.e., from closed- or open-loop cycles). 
A well-designed product-level metric may arguably be 
aggregated as a measure for the entire economy, though, 
in practice, a product metric may not encompass all of 
the features linked to a circular economy.” Measuring 
product circularity this way has the benefit of being 
reproducible and comparable between products. On 
the other hand, it cannot differentiate what type 
of loop the material, product or component cycle 
through, which means that important aspects of CE 
are excluded. 
Parchomenko, Nelen, Gillabel, & Rechberger (2019) 
have categorized existing indicators in regard to their 
method of calculating circularity. They describe that 
the indicators can be divided into three clusters; 
resource efficiency cluster, material stock and flow 
cluster and product centric cluster. Generally, they 
argue that product and system-dynamic perspectives 
are less represented compared to the others. 
Another aspect which is important in regards to 
circularity indicators, is that they should give the 
developers guidance in where to improve (Saidani, 
Yannou, Leroy, et al., 2017a). This is an essential 
element for circularity indicators which are to be 
used during product development. This is another 
area, in which single output indicators (single 
number, percentage etc.) are limited.

3.5.1 .1 Circular Economy Indicator Prototype
The Circular Economy Indicator Prototype (CEIP) 
is developed by Griffiths & Cayzer (2016). The 
indicator is based on the product and the product 
life cycle and is build up as a questionnaire. The 
questions relate to different phases of the product 
life cycle and does target many different circularity 
loops for circular economy, though they are not 
systematically targeted. For example, no questions 
relate to upgrading of the product and many areas 
are only covered minimally. The user answers 
the questions and is given different points for the 
answers, indicating that some initiatives give a 
higher circularity potential than others. From each 
stage of the life cycle, a certain amount of points is 
available. The questions are, however, often yes or no 
questions, in which grey areas disappear. An example 
of this is the question: “Does the product reduce 
waste through its use?”. When the user answers yes 
to this question, it will still not be clear whether the 
reduction is large or small, which has a significant 
impact on the circularity. In some questions, it is 
possible give an indication of in which range of the 
grey are the product is placed.
As an output, the user gets a product rating and a 
spider web, which indicate product score in relation 
to available score in different phases. This can give 
an indication of where to improve but does not 
indicate directly what product characteristics should 
be improved. Figure 9 shown the layout of the results 
of the evaluation. 
3.5.2 .2 Circular Economy Toolkit (CET)
The Circular Economy Toolkit (CET) is an online 
tool based on a questionnaire and is created by 
Bocken & Evans (n.d.). The user of the tool answers

and the life cycle of the product. The tool is relatively

3.5.3 .3 Circularity Potential Indicator (CPI)
The Circularity Potential Indicator (CPI) is 
developed by Saidani et al. (2017b). The basis for the 
development of the tool is that the researchers saw 
the need for a product circularity tool with a top-
down approach to cover CE. The tool is based on 
four building blocks related to CE. The four building 
blocks of the tool is based on the four aspects of 
CE from the Ellen MacArthur foundation; circular 
product design, new business model, reverse cycles 
and favorable system conditions. This approach 
seems to target CE at all levels, which is also one of 
the points from the developers; that circularity at a 
product level needs to be accessed holistically. 
The product circularity is evaluated on 20 attributes, 
which there are five of under each building block. The 
20 attributes are assessed through multiple choice 
questions; one to seven per attribute. Each attribute 
can get up to five points, each building block can 
get a score of up to 25 and the total circularity score 
goes up to 100. The output of the assessment is 
an aggregated circularity score, but as can be seen 
in figure 11 the part scores are also visible, which 
can give more information on where the product 
perform well and what should be improved. The 
tool is accessible as an online tool (Saidani, Yannou, 
& Leroy, 2017).   
3.5.4 .4 Circular Pathfinder (CP)
The circular pathfinder is developed by ResCoM 
(n.d.). The pathfinder is not actually an indicator 
but a guide to be used during product development 
to find product strategies for improving circularity. 
The tool is online based and ask questions through a 
chat window. The questions relate to product type, 
the relation costumers have to the product, the 
lifetime and the user needs. Based on the answers 
given, the pathfinder suggests strategies suitable 
to focus on for the specific product. The tool does 
include many circularity levels but are missing 
strategies to improve circularity in production and 
rethinking and reinventing strategies. One thing 
the tool does very well is to seek information about 
the product type and user dynamics, which are 
important to understand in order to find the most 
suitable strategies.  A screenshot from the circular 
pathfinder is shown in figure 12.

3.5.8 .8 Material circularity indicator (MCI) 
The Material Circularity Indicator (MCI) was created 
by the Ellen MacArthur Foundation & Granta 
Design (2015). The indicator evaluates material and 
component flows, just like the Resource Duration 
Indicator. The Material Circularity Indicator 
accounts for use of virgin material in production, 
amount of unrecoverable material contributed to 
the product and a utility factor, which describes the 
intensity and duration of use. The first two of these, 
virgin material in production and unrecoverable 
material, two indexes are created. These are used to 
calculate the linear flow index. The utility factor and 
the linear flow index are the basis for the material 
circularity index. The tool is created as an excel 
based tool, where the user has to put in a range of 
information about the recycled content, recycling of 
the product and the use duration and efficiency. The 
layout of the tool can be seen in figure 16. 
3.6 Conclusion 
Circular economy is a broad concept within the 
concept, the focuses and definition vary a great deal. 
Generally, CE is seen as an opposite to the linear 
economy and replaces waste with regeneration of 
materials, products, etc. In some definitions, CE 
is closely related to recycling, since this is seen as a 
direct way of keeping materials in the loop. Others 
find that this to be a narrow approach, and prompt 
for strategies that also prolongs the products life and 
rethinks the delivery of value. Within the CIRCit 
project a broad understanding of CE exist, which 
targets multiple strategies of CE, all the way from 
complete dematerialization to recovery of materials. 
Figure 16 The material circularity indicator (MCI) (Ellen MacArthur Foundation & 
Granta Design, 2015)

This is the understanding of CE which will be built 
upon for this project.  
In regard to circularity indicators, multiple different 
exist. It seems there is a disagreement in what is 
essential for measuring circularity. Some find it 
important that indicators are built on quantitative 
data and is reproducible and comparable through 
few indicators. Others argue that CE indicators 
should target broadly and should provide elaborate 
information on improvement potential.  This 
depends highly on which role such an indicator or 
tool should have
For this project, the product development phase is 
the focus. More specifically the the concept stage. 
Therefore, it is deemed important that the indicator 
can give elaborate information about the product 
performance and give a hotspot analysis of where 
to improve. Since the project take starting point 
in the understanding of CE strategies provided by 
the Circular Strategies Scanner, it is defined that a 
broader assessment of CE potential is beneficial in 
a tool. 
The tools or indicators described in the literature 
review base their analysis on different aspects in 
regard to the product. For a concept level tool, it 
is important that the tool has a large focus on the 
product design but still relates this to the products 
life cycle. For many of the tools, the actual product 
design is not very much included in the assessment, 
which means that they can only limitedly be used 
for looking at different concepts compared. The 
circularity assessment tool is the closest to a concept 
level tool and it will be looked further into how the 
tool performs for the purpose.

in the tool. Otherwise the users might just guess, 
which can create large uncertainties.

have similar products on the market. These can for 
example be used to anticipate customer use patterns 
that are essential to circularity. The company might 
also have done costumer or market analysis which 
can be used in the assessment.

4.4 Discussion around the use of the tool
When using the tool there are a few different steps the 
user has to go through which leads to the generating 
of the circularity potential scores, which is the result 
of the assessment. These steps were described earlier 
but a detailed discussion of the different aspects of 
the assessment is presented below. 
4.4.1 Gathering team and understanding the 
guidelines
Before using the assessment tool, the team should 
be gathered, and the guidelines should be read 
through and understood. Preferably, the members 
of the assessment team have a deep knowledge 
about the guidelines and have applied them during 
development of the concept(s). As earlier described, 
the assessment team largely influences the results of 
the tool, since it is very subjective. 
The next step of using the tool is to understand the 
guidelines. This requires the Guidelines for Circular 
Product Development (Shahbazi, 2019b), which 
works as a reference book for the tool. The ease of 
understanding the guidelines in the tool depends 
largely on the familiarity to the guidelines and 
whether it has been used in the product development 
or just read through. If the company have used 
the design guidelines throughout the product 
development, the understanding of the guidelines is 
much higher. If the assessment team have only read 
the guidelines (perhaps just one team member) they 
will have limited knowledge of the guidelines, which 
can make it difficult to know what each guideline 
includes. For example, operational stage of product/
service includes eight different sub-guidelines, some 
of these even have additional specification point 
under them, which makes it difficult to aggregate 
one number. Also, the link between the guidelines 
and the tool is problematized by the fact that the 
user should take into account the guidelines from 
different strategies. As can be seen in figure 21, a 
guideline like “durability and robustness” requires the 
user to look nine different places in the guidelines, 
to find out what the guideline contains. 
During the test of the tool, it was found that some of 
the guidelines in the tool are not overall guidelines 
but rather categories, in which guidelines are ordered. 
For example, “use renewable energy” is a guideline, 
which the user can evaluate directly, while “laws and 
regulation” and “joints and connectors” are categories 
in which specific guidelines are applied. During the 
testing of the tool, it was found that guidelines such 
as “use renewable energy” was easier to understand, 
as it presents an overall guideline to be followed. 
Therefore, some guidelines could be elaborated in 
==PAGE:44==
32 | Analysis of the Circularity assessment tool 
the tool to improve understanding, since consistency 
in the way guidelines are formulated, would likely 
make the assessment easier. Another aspect is the 
fact that the guidelines in many cases overlap and 
that it is not always necessary to fulfill all guidelines 
related to a strategy. 
4.4.2 Doing the evaluation
The process of inputting values to the tool consists of 
evaluating two different aspects for each guideline; 
the importance and the fulfillment. When these are 
to be assessed, it is essential that the users have a 
clear understanding of what the guidelines include. 
The scores given to each guideline in terms for 
importance and fulfillment are multiplied. This 
gives indication of which areas are important to 
take a closer look at. The approach is similar to the 
weighted objectives method (Cross, 2008), which 
is a method for evaluating product performance in 
regards to different objectives. Within this method 
it is stated that the evaluation of importance 
is difficult, without having a reference. When 
evaluating the objectives, it is recommended to 
compare importance between two objectives; is one 
more important than the other, to be able to define 
the importance of the different objectives. 
According to the description in the tool, the 
importance “should be evaluated subjectively based on 
product brief, product development goals, environmental 
and circularity goals, previous experience from existing 
products in the market or previous generations” 
(Shahbazi, 2019a). The description states that the 
evaluation should be a subjective evaluation from 
the assessment team but might be difficult to access 
in the first place, since there is a broad definition 
of the basis for the evaluation. It is hard to evaluate 
whether a guideline is important without having 
a reference point or something to trade-off with. 
When comparing different concepts, the importance 
will often be the same for the concepts unless they 
are focusing on very different circularity strategies 
and the concepts are significantly different. 
During the test using the Fairphone case, it was 
found that the evaluation of the importance 
could be problematic. This is due to the fact that 
the guidelines are very product related and the 
importance does not only depend on the product 
design, but is a holistic evaluation of the company’s

compared to the other concepts, which can be a 
good thing for the comparative analysis, but it might 
point out the wrong areas of improvement for the 
hotspot analysis. 
4.4.3 Assessment results
The results given in the assessment consist of 
individual circularity potential scores for each of 
the guidelines, for each strategy and a total score 
for the circularity potential. All these scores should 
be as low as possible to have a high circularity. For 
the individual guidelines, the scores give direct 
indications of what the product is not doing well, 
in relation to what is found important, which can 
be used as a direct pointer towards areas to improve 
the product design.  The total score is the sum of the 
potential indicators for each of the guidelines. The 
total of these can only be used to compare and can 
therefore only be used for evaluating more than one 
concept. This is because the total score will depend 
on how many areas are important, which can vary 
between concepts. Therefore, it cannot be defined 
that a score in a certain range correlates to a specific 
performance. 
Another result of the assessment is the strategy 
scores. These only make sense, when comparing 
concepts, and actually only concepts which have 
the same scores for importance. Otherwise a better 
score within a certain strategy, can be contributed 
to having lower importance. Lower importance 
scores can be contributed to the company not being 
very ambitious in pursuing different strategies. This 
also questions the way of calculating the circularity 
(lowest score is better). In the tool, the concepts are 
credited for what it is not doing bad instead of what 
it is doing good. 
4.5 Key findings and conclusion to chapter 
The analysis of the tool shows that there are some 
aspects of the tool which could be improved in order 
to provide a better experience in using the tool and a 
greater reliability in the assessment of the concept(s). 
First of all, the assessment tool is very sensitive 
to which stakeholders do the assessment, since 
the tool is very subjective. When working with a 
concept level indicator, some aspect will likely not 
be known for the product, which requires additional 
information from existing products or projects as 
well as knowledge about the relevant market. This 
information is processed by the assessment team and 
the participants therefore have great influence on the 
result. 
When going through the architecture of the tool, 
there are some elements that can potentially create 
breakdowns in the assessment. The understanding 
of guidelines can sometimes be difficult, since they 
are represented under different strategies and a 
==PAGE:46==
34 | Analysis of the Circularity assessment tool 
long range of sub-guidelines are to be considered. 
Also, in terms of assessing the importance of each 
guidelines, there are some uncertainty as to the basis 
for the evaluation and it can be difficult to evaluate 
importance without comparing to other guidelines. 
Secondly, the importance is based on many aspects, 
that the assessment team should consider themselves 
and aggregate into one number. This can create large 
uncertainty in regard to the results.  
In the result of the assessment, there are two different 
usages for the results. The tool can be used to identify 
guidelines which have been found important but is 
not properly fulfilled. For this, the tool gives a great 
feedback, as it clearly indicates where the design 
should be improved to fulfill the companies own 
expectations. The total score and the strategy scores 
have a more limited use, since these can only be used 
for comparing different concepts in regard to which 
is performing best.

5.1 Evaluation parameters
The following section will present the different 
parameters and characteristics, which are used to 
compare the tools. Some are just characteristics, 
which are used to identify what type of tool they are, 
while some are evaluation parameters, which can be 
used to evaluate their performance in relation to each 
other. 
5.1.1 Data input
The data input parameter describes what type of 
data is used in the tool to calculate the circularity. 
This can be based on the user’s own evaluation of 
performance in regard to specific areas. They can also 
be based on formerly known data about the product 
and the surrounding system, which could for 
instance be the weight of the product, the amount 
of recycled content or the assumed product lifetime. 
The type of data used has a big influence on the 
reproducibility and subjectivity of the evaluation. It 
is also important in relation to use of data input, that 
the data is actually available. If an indicator is based 
on quantitative data about the product/concept and 
this information is not available, the user might make 
their own personal estimate, which again makes the 
evaluation subjective.
5.1.2 Output
The indicator output is the results given to the user 
after the calculation/evaluation. This can be either 
quantitative or qualitative. The quantitative output 
can be a single indicator (percentage, time unit, score 
etc.) or it can be given as multiple indicators which 
shows performance in more than one area. It can also 
be an illustrative output, which does not necessarily 
give a quantitative output at all but provides 
qualitative information about where to improve.

context the tool is intended to take part in. Since

5.1.4 Target user
Most of the tools do not specify who the target user 
is, but generally states that they are for companies 
who intend to improve their circularity. Most tools 
state though, what kind of background knowledge 
the user should have. This means that some tools are 
mainly focused at the development team. Some tools 
seem to target higher strategic level stakeholders, 
who have more influence on decision making. 
5.1.5 Loops and strategies included
The loops included in the assessment refers to how 
well the tool capture the entirety of CE by including 
different loops (upgrade, repair, reuse, recycle, etc.) 
and different strategies, directly or indirectly. In 
the research clarification it was found that many 
tools only focus on single aspects of circularity (like 
recycling rates). None of the selected tools does 
this, but only few of them account for all circularity 
loops. The tools might have different understandings 
of what loops/strategies exist, which can be a part of 
the reason.   
5.1.6 Hierarchy of strategies
As was identified in the research clarification, not 
all strategies contribute equally to circularity. Most 
often, recycling or energy recovery are the least 
preferred strategies, while repairing and upgrading 
products is often more beneficial. Some strategies 
can be hard to assess the circularity potential 
from, at least without more information about 
the product and the use (like alternative business 
models). Some tools simply separate different loops 
to avoid weighing their circularity in relation to 
each other. This parameter should evaluate how 
the tool accounts for the difference in circularity 
contribution and whether it is done in a good way.  
5.1.7 Relation to product design
In the research clarification, it was found that 
the product design features have a large influence 
on their circularity. It is therefore essential that a 
concept level circularity tool has some connection 
to the product design and does not only focus on 
material flows. This is also an important factor since 
it is desired, that the tool should be able to point out 
what to improve in terms of product design. 
5.1.8 Dept of evaluation
The level of detail of the evaluation is an important 
parameter to look at. There are some benefits by 
having a superficial tool; the tool is often easier 
to use and understand. Also, more companies can 
potentially find time to use the tool. On the other 
hand, superficial tools might miss essential aspect of 
CE, which make them less trustworthy. It is difficult 
to say in general whether a tool should be superficial 
or comprehensive as it depends on what they should 
be used for. In the context of this project, it is desired 
to have a tool which lies in-between, as it should 
include as detailed information about the concepts 
as possible but there will be limited information 
available this early in the development. 
5.1.9 Ease of use
The ease of use of the tools depends on two different 
things; how easy it is to understand how the tool 
works and how easy it is to transform the available 
data into the needed input. The first part depends 
on how well it guides the user in what to do, how the 
user interface is build up. The second depends on the 
way questions are asked, and how much information 
should be put into one input field/score. Ease of use

and whether the user know what to do with it. 
5.1.10 Feedback
One of the goals for the tool is that it should 
effectively guide the user towards improving their 
product to be more circular. The level of information 
provided for the user to improve the product/
system varies a lot between the different indicators. 
Most indicators only give an overall indication of 
performance and a low level of detail on areas to 
improve. It is important that the user knows how to 
use the results and understand what can be done to 
improve this in the future.

For indicators which should be used for 
communicative

This is easiest to have if the indicator is built only 
on quantitative data as opposed to qualitative,

assignment, reproducibility is not critical, but it is 
still an important element for the indicator to be 
trustworthy. If the result and conclusion changes 
radically when different users perform the analysis, 
based on same product/system, it is difficult to trust 
that the indicator gives correct conclusions. The 
evaluation of this parameter should be based the on 
the information available at the concept level of the 
product development. This means that indicators 
which are built on quantifiable data, only gets a high 
score, if it is based on knowledge which is available. 
Otherwise the user might make subjective estimates. 
5.1.12 Inclusion of life cycle perspective 
In the research clarification it was pointed out that 
circular design should be looked at holistically. This 
means, that there should be an understanding of the 
context products are designed for. Creating products 
which can be upgraded and repaired will never reach 
its true circularity potential if the product system 
does not support upgrade and repair. Many tools 
include elements from the product system which 
is also an important element for assessing the 
circularity.

by testing some of them using the Fairphone case, 
which was presented and used on the Circularity

be performed using the case, as the knowledge 
neccesary is not available.

5.3 The desired state
In the comparative analysis, the desired state is also 
included. This is based on the context of the tool and 
the fact that it should be used at a concept level for 
comparing quite similar concepts. From the research 
clarification, it was found that circular economy can 
in some cases be equalized to recycling. This is not 
the preferred case for the redesigned tool, and it is 
important that the tool can differentiate the different 
types of loops. This is also linked to the hierarchy, 
which is an important element to include.
The relation to product design and the depth of 
evaluation should both be as high as possible, at the 
concept level, but since the amount of knowledge 
present is not very high at the concept level, these are 
evaluated to be lower for the desired state.  The ease 
of use should be as high as possible, as this can help 
to eliminate confusion and mistakes for the user. 
The usability of the tool is secondary for this project, 
but it is still worth considering when finding new 
solutions. The indication of improvement should 
also be as high as possible, as it is a key goal for 
the tool to be able to point towards improvement 
potentials. 
Reproducibility is a difficult area as a concept 
level tool will inevitably be subjective. This should 
though be minimized as much as possible by making 
the basis for the assessment as clear as possible. Last, 
the life cycle indication should be included as it 
is essential to know what purpose the product is 
developed for, but the life cycle is not the main part 
to be assessed, which is why the desired state is not 
the highest possible. 
5.4 Discussion of results 
The main reason for doing the comparative 
analysis is to identify areas in which the Circularity 
Assessment Tool could be improved and to see if the 
compared tools give good solutions for these areas. 
Another reason is to identify the areas in which the 
tool performs well to make sure that these aspects 
are kept and not changed in a way that makes the 
tool perform worse in those areas. 
5.4.1 Beneficial aspects
5.4.1 .1 Coverage of CE loops
In the comparative analysis, it can be seen that the 
CAT has a high performance regarding the coverage 
of CE loops and different strategies for CE. This is 
due to the fact that it is developed with reference to 
the Circular Strategies Scanner and the Guidelines 
for Circular Product Development (GCPD).

5.4.2 .2 Relation to product design
The close link to the GCPD also results in a close 
relation to product features which makes indication 
of product improvement easier. The link to product 
features might actually be a bit too high, since a high 
level of detail is not present at a concept level. The 
depth of the evaluation is relatively high since it goes 
into much detail with the product design features, 
which might also have a detail level which is too 
high for a concept level indicator. 
5.4.3 .3 Areas of improvement
From the evaluation in table 2, it can be seen that 
there are especially three areas in which the tool 
could be improved. 
5.4.4 .4 Ease of use
The ease of use is linked to both the difficulty in 
understanding the guidelines, which means the 
assessment takes a longer time. Secondly, the users 
might have a difficult time evaluating the importance 
of each guideline as it is based on many different 
aspects. 
5.4.5 .5 Hierarchy
Another area which could be improved, is the 
inclusion of a hierarchy of guidelines or strategies. 
A hierarchy can, to some extent, be included by the 
user through the importance, but it is difficult for 
the user to process another aspect into the evaluation 
of importance. If not included in the importance, 
the tool does not account for differences in the 
contribution to circularity for the different strategies. 
5.4.6 .6 Reproducibility
The tool has, from the beginning, been defined as a 
subjective tool. Still, it is preferable that the variation 
in results are minimized as much as possible. The tool 
seems to have a very high subjectivity, which means 
that the assessment will have a low reproducibility. If 
users have different understandings of the meaning 
of each guideline and their opinions about their 
importance vary significantly, this will create very 
different results. A study made by Saidani, Cluzel, 
Leroy, & Yannou (2019) analyses the robustness of 
three different indicators (MCI, CET, CEIP). This 
study shows that the qualitatively based indicators, 
CET and CEIP, has quite large variations between 
different test groups, even though the questions are 
relating to very specific aspects. This means that 
variations in the Circularity Assessment Tool (CAT) 
could potentially be even higher. 
5.4.7 .7 Indication of improvement
As discussed in chapter 4, the usefullness of the tool 
is to some extend very good. The hotspots, showing 
which guidelines are important to look at are helpful 
to guide the user in improving the product. The 
CAT is probably the tool which best shows specific 
areas of improvement, but it shows this based on 
the assessment team’s own evaluation of importance, 
which means that it is highly subjective. 
5.4.8 .8 Life cycle indication
The last aspect to mention is the inclusion of life 
cycle aspects, which is done to some extend in the 
CAT. It includes the production and use stage, but 
it does not account for the product lifetime and how 
the product is to be handled in the end-of-life. These 
are aspect which should be taken into consideration 
when evaluating the importance. This means that 
there are many parameters, which should be taken 
into account when the user evaluates importance. 
Figure 22 have summed up the aspects that should 
be included in the assessment team’s evaluation of 
importance. The figure illustrates how complex it is 
to evaluate all these aspects and generate it into one 
number.
5.5 Inspiration from other tools 
The existing tools show areas, where they can 
be used as a source of inspiration. For example, 
many of the tools (CET, CEIP, CP and CPI) are 
evaluating the products based on a questionnaire, 
==PAGE:54==
42 | Comparative analysis of tools
which gives a list of answers to the questions. These 
tools are generally easier to use, as they ask the 
questions more specifically. This could be used as 
a source of inspiration, especially in the evaluation 
of importance, where it is difficult for the user to 
know what to base this evaluation on. The Circular 
Pathfinder uses characteristics about the product 
and its life cycle to evaluate which strategies are 
most suiting. This principle could also be applicable 
for evaluation of important strategies to follow, and 
perhaps help to define a hierarchy of strategies.
The Circular Economy Toolkit asks questions in 
relation to two different aspects; the opportunity 
based on system and the feasibility based on 
product design. This idea can be used as a source of 
inspiration, since the opportunity is not based on 
what the company think is important but is based 
on what opportunities exist in the system. Also, 
some of the more quantitative tools can be used 
as a source of inspiration, as some data about the 
concepts and the planned lifecycle could perhaps be 
included. For example, the longevity of a product, 
can be estimated based on current products on the 
market. 
5.6 Conclusion to analysis 
The evaluation of the different assessment tools 
shows that the CAT has a close connection with 
the development of circular products and that it 
includes all the different loops and accounts for all 
the strategies proposed in the Circular Strategies 
Scanner. This gives it a great starting point for 
evaluating product features to improve design for 
circular economy. The level of detail in the guideline 
might be a bit too high for a concept level indicator 
though. Therefore, correlation between detail level 
needed in assessment and the knowledge available 
at the concept level of evaluation should be ensured. 
Additionally, it was found that especially the 
evaluation of importance can cause high variation, 
due to the large amount of information to be put 
into just one score. Also, the understanding of 
the guidelines can be difficult since it requires the 
user to evaluate many different sub-guidelines to 
understand the overall guideline.

7.5.2 .2 Importance
During the workshop, the idea of separating the 
different aspects of importance was widely discussed. 
The idea is that there will be specific strategies (and 
guidelines), which are most suitable, based on 
the product type due to its typical life cycle and 
design. The other part of the importance is what 
the company finds essential for its strategic goals. 
If the company has a goal of making the products 
upgradable, having a design that supports this will 
add to the circularity of the product. These areas will 
likely be the ones that the company believe to be 
economically viable for them. The idea of separating 
these areas of importance is an idea to look further 
into, based on the workshop.
7.5.3 .3 Fulfillment 
The sub-solutions were only discussed briefly 
during the workshop. This might be due to the sub-
solutions being relatively easy to understand, and 
closely related to the existing tools.
7.5.4 .4 Result calculation
During the workshop, the way the result is calculated 
was discussed. It was discussed whether the results 
from the assessment should be as low as possible or 
as high as possible, which was also briefly discussed 
in the Descriptive Study I. Here, it was pointed 
out that having the scores be as low as possible is 
a bit counterintuitive. It is more logical that a 
high fulfillment in many highly important areas, 
contribute to a high circularity score. In that way, 
the concept is rewarded for good circular design 
aspects instead of punished for what it does not do 
well enough. It was also discussed in what way the 
calculation of circularity could look. The circularity 
should not only be based on the company’s 
evaluation of what is important but should include 
an evaluation of what is objectively more favorable 
to design for.
Another thing that was discussed was the 
normalization of the scores. If the score is normalized 
to the importance score, the result will then be the 
relative circularity for that specific product type or 
the specific goals. If the scores are not normalized, it 
will be difficult to know if a score is good or not as 
it will depend on how many guidelines are deemed 
important. The highest score therefore only equals 
the best circularity when comparing concepts with 
same importance. This might be a bit confusing for 
the user.
7.5.5 .5 Communication
Just as the fulfillment, the communication of the 
result was not very heavily discussed. This was 
likely due to the fact that for most concepts, the 
communication of results, can be added without a 
profound effect on the rest of the concept.

8.1 Updating of morphology 
In the updated morphology, the considerations 
discussed in chapter 7 have been incorporated into 
some of the sub-solutions. In some cases, new sub-
solutions have been added. The format for guidelines 
has been renamed as the title, “format for guidelines” 
might be too narrow in its definition. One of the 
discussions during the workshop was that the basis 
for the evaluation should not necessarily be the 
guidelines. The guidelines can be seen as a means for 
fulfilling the strategic goals. Therefore, following only 
few guidelines can lead to a just as circular concept 
as following all guidelines related to a strategy. Also, 
some guidelines are closer linked to the detailed 
design stage rather than the concept level design. 
Therefore, “format for guidelines” was updated to 
be “basis for evaluation” and the sub-solutions were 
updated as well. Two sub-solutions based on the 
guidelines are kept, but the detail level should be 
much more overall than they are in the initial tool. 
Some sub-solutions in the result calculation were also 
updated. This is due to the discussion about dividing 
the different areas of importance, which should be 
reflected in the calculation of results as well. Also, 
the result calculations included a few more detailed 
calculations on how to calculate the circularity 
potential. The updated morphology can be seen in 
figure 34 and in appendix 12, where updated sub-
solutions are marked blue.

9.2 Strategic goals
The next step of the assessment is the company’s own 
goals. These are used to identify the aspects which 
the company would like to focus on. These are the 
strategies the company find to be most suitable for 
their product. This can be based on their existing 
production, supply chain and logistics system, or 
just what they think fits the product or the company 
image. Each of the strategies are evaluated by the 
user. They should define which strategies are essential 
to them, which ones are secondary and which ones 
have no importance to them. This evaluation assigns 
importance scores for the company’s goals. For this, 
descriptions of the strategies from the circularity 
assessment tool V.3 (see appendix 5) are used. Figure 
46 shows the layout of the sheet in the redesigned 
tool. The reason the company’s goal is important 
is that they are the best to evaluate what strategies 
are the most beneficial to them, and they are the 
ones who are most capable of identifying which 
strategies are easiest to design for. The product 
type questionnaire cannot evaluate, whether an 
improvement in material sourcing is an easy task or 
not. 
9.3 Criteria
The actual evaluation of the different concepts 
is based on different criteria. These are linked to 
the circular strategies and is a grouping of circular

is used together with the guidelines for circular 
product development (Shahbazi, 2019b). 
The goal is to formulate criteria which are generic, 
and therefore are relevant if the strategy is relevant, 
and they should target the detail level related to the 
concept level. The guidelines in the list are narrowed 
down to two criteria per strategy. Whether this is the

10.1 Types of tests and procedures
The verification of the tool is performed in two 
different stages. The first is qualitative feedback on 
the tool through interviews with companies involved 
in the CIRCit project. The second is through test of 
the tool with a new company to test usefulness and 
usability. 
The interviews cannot be used for testing neither 
the usability or the usefulness of the tool, since the 
interviews are based on a presentation of the tool 
and not a test. The interviews can still be useful for 
evaluating companies’ first impression and attitude 
towards the idea around the tool. Therefore, these 
cannot stand alone, but can provide additional 
feedback. 
In testing the prototype, there are two main aspects 
to be tested; the usefulness of the tool and assessment 
results and the usability of the tool. The usefulness 
is the key aspect, since it is essential that the results 
provided can be used by the company and makes 
sense. The usability is of course also important since 
it can cause a lot of breakdowns if the usability of the 
tool is not optimal. 
10.2 Interviews
For the work package three (WP3) in the CIRCit 
project, a range of companies have used the 
circularity assessment tool. These interviews are done 
by Skype due to the companies’ locations scattered 
over the Nordic countries. The primary purpose 
of the interviews were to wrap up the companies 
attachment to the WP3 of the CIRCit project, 
an therefore, Sasha Shahbazi leaded the meeting. 
The companies have used the initial tool and can 
comment on the changes that are made to the tool 
and give and overall impression whether they find 
the tool understandable and could see themselves 
using it. They can therefore not answer to whether 
the results generated by the tool are useful or not. The

Generally, there was a positive attitude around the

are relevant. The users trust in the tool seems much 
higher if they generally agree with the strategies 
which are suggested. Therefore, it is highly 
important that the questions are asked in the right

which means that some of the questions should be 
rewritten. The trust in the assignment of scores can 
be further improved by indicating where the strategy 
scores come from.

assessment, since it gives the users an idea that their 
selected strategies influence the assessment. There 
should though be changed some definitions of

understand them, and to avoid confusion. Also, 
the levels of importance should likely be called 
something different so that the user have and option 
which does not mean that it is irrelevant but that it 
is not a part of their strategic goals.

be beneficial to exclude irrelevant strategies from 
the assessment. In this way the assessment is less 
extensive to go through. Throughout the assessment, 
the user should be more informed of where the 
results come from and be provided with information 
before doing each step of what this is meant for, and 
how it is done.

12.1 Discussion of redesigned tool 
The redesigned tool, in many ways, targets the needs 
there is for an assistance tool in the early product 
development. The tool is specifically made for the 
detail level which is present for concepts and can 
be used as a supportive tool in the early product 
development. One very important aspect is the 
usefulness of the tool, which is improved due to the 
introduction of the product type questionnaire. This 
links the tool and the Guideline for Circular Product 
Development even more than they were before. 
In the guidelines, it is stated that before using the 
guidelines, it should be chosen which strategies to 
focus on. This process can be assisted by using the 
product type questionnaire and therefore the two 
tools work well together. 
12.1.1 Limitations
The main change made to the circularity assessment 
tool is the evaluation of importance. By changing the 
way importance is evaluated, some of the subjective 
aspects are eliminated from the tool. The product 
type questionnaire does, however, in some ways 
depend on the user’s ability to se possibilities. The 
user is, for example, asked whether the product or 
components COULD be used for something else 
or if there COULD be a market for secondhand 
products. This includes a level of subjectivity in the 
product type evaluation. 
Secondly, can it be discussed if it is possible to 
capture the nuances in different product types, and 
whether this is a realistic goal for a screening tool. 
It is certain that the questionnaire should be further 
investigated, but this does not necessarily mean that 
all nuances can be included. For this purpose, it could 
be relevant to make a risk analysis which evaluates 
the possible mistakes and define how critical these 
are. The most critical aspect is if relevant strategies 
are not captured, as this will mean that the company 
might be misguided in relation to which strategies 
are relevant. It is less critical if a strategy is deemed 
relevant even though it is not, as the user can define 
that they do not focus on it. 
Based on the tests performed on the tool, it is not 
possible to conclude whether the level of detail is 
right for the criteria. In the test with DBI, it was 
indicated that the detail level was suiting, but this 
cannot be concluded based on this one test.

12.2.1 Redesign or new tool
The balance between doing a redesign and doing a 
new tool, is an area which can be further discussed. 
The project is defined as a redesign of the initial tool. 
During the process of developing the redesigned 
tool, this balance has been a bit fluent. The initial 
idea was that the performance/fulfillment should 
be evaluated based on the guidelines, as this was 
a key aspect of the initial tool. The arguments for 
evaluating fulfillment at a strategic level are, however, 
strong enough to abandon this initial idea. 
For the concept development, the structure of the 
Circularity Assessment Tool (CAT) was used, due 
to the project being a redesign. This decision limits 
the solution space to some extent. Especially, the 
result calculation was a bit locked by this structure, 
as it should depend on the importance and the 
fulfillment, which means that the variations in these 
sub-solutions were limited. Whether the choice of 
focusing on this specific structure was beneficial or 
not is difficult to say. It is evaluated though, that 
the structure of the CAT is very well-suited for 
evaluating concepts. It has many similarities with 
the Weighted Objectives Method (Cross, 2008), 
which is used for evaluating concepts.
12.2.2 Challenges in testing
When performing the usefulness tests, it is difficult 
to find companies that are at a concept stage of 
development. Therefore, test persons were asked 
to find old concepts to evaluate, which means that 
their knowledge about each concept might be larger 
than it was in retrospective, when they developed 
the concepts. It is therefore difficult to say exactly 
how the tool performs as a part of the concept 
development process.
12.3 Next step

the tool can still be further tested. The usefulness of 
the tool in its complete format should also be tested 
further. It has only been tested in one session, and 
not used during the concept development process. It

as an assistance tool in that process. Also, for each of

Conclusion | 105 
The process started by establishing how circularity 
can be measured, based on relevant literature on 
the subject. This also included review of different 
tools and indicators. It was found in this study, that 
tools for evaluating product circularity is important 
to enable development of more circular products. 
Based on the indicators found, only few tools can be 
used in the early product development, due to the 
level of detail required. The Circularity Assessment 
Tool is one of these, but based on analyzing the tool, 
it was found that there are areas which could be 
improved. The most critical of these is to guide the 
use towards what is important to design for in their 
product, the level of detail in the assessment and the 
understandability of the tool. 
The Circularity Assessment Tool was used as a 
structure for the redesigned tool and the sub-functions 
of the tool could be used to build up a morphology. 
The sub-solutions for the morphology was developed 
with inspiration from other existing tools. One of the 
main discussions around the developed sub-solutions 
was whether the tool should rather be based on 
strategies than on guidelines. This is one of the areas 
in which the balance between doing a redesign and 
doing a new tool was a bit blurry. It was, however, 
assessed that having the evaluation at a strategy level 
had some strong arguments backing it up. 
With basis in an updated morphology, development 
of concepts was possible. This process was iterative 
and included finding sub-solutions which works well 
together and continuously improving the possible 
combinations. The three concepts developed in this 
process had different ways of evaluating fulfillment 
of strategies and presented different levels of 
transparency. The concept evaluated to be most 
suiting for the context is the one which evaluated 
strategy performance through different criteria. The 
tool concept also contains an evaluation of which 
strategies are most suiting for the product type, which 
sets the basis for the evaluation of product concepts. 
In the detailing of the concept it was found that the 
development of a questionnaire for finding relevant 
strategies is a complicated task, which requires 
multiple iteration for defining the solutions. 
In order to verify the performance of the redesigned 
tool, two different types of verification were 
performed; interviews with companies and tests with 
companies and students. The interviews showed 
good feedback on the idea around the tool, as most 
of the interviewees had a positive attitude around 
the changes made to the tool. The interviews can, 
however, not be used for evaluating how the tool 
performs, and therefore test with a relevant company 
was performed. This showed that the tool was quite
