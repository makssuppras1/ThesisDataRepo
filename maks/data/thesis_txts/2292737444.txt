2 Abstract 
This research project aims to enlighten impacts regarding vault insulation in Danish medieval churches. 
Simplified theoretical calculations suggest a heat loss reduction of 40 % by vault insulation in churches of 
continuous  heating. A newly developed lime based insulation plaster with aggregate of perlite, and expanded 
volcanic sand, is investigated. The project revolves around three main topics; 
1. Laboratory experiments for the determination of material properties in the lime based insulation plaster. 
2. Simulations of moisture and thermal conditions in church vaults without insulation as well as vaults 
insulated with mineral wool and the lime based insulation plaster. 
3. In situ climate measurements made in Annisse Church, that contains a test field on one vault of the lime 
based insulation plaster. 
Recipes and mixing ratios of the lime based insulation plaster have been made, based on texture, appearance, and 
consistency. Laboratory experiments reveal good thermal properties, and a thermal conductivity of 
approximately twice the one of mineral wool. The lime based insulation plaster shows good moisture transport 
properties; it has a higher capillary suction than brick, is capable of relatively quick drying and is vapour 
permeable. The lime based plaster shows good characteristics in regards to fracture energy, and an overlying 
layer of fiber reinforced lime mortar increases the durability and strength of the insulation, enabling the insulated 
vault to withstand certain loads. 
Simulations in Delphin 5 indicate good moisture properties in the lime based insulation plaster, as additional 
water loads of considerable amount do not affect the original underlying brick vault. The simulations also show 
relatively quick drying of the insulation plaster. Simulations of a mineral wool insulated vault show the same 
tendencies of a virtually unaffected underlying brick vault. Simulations of an uninsulated vault show a deep 
water penetration in the brick and a significant impact from external thermal conditions. 
In cases where the in situ measurements show high relative humidity in the insulation plaster, it is seen that the 
underlying brick remains unaffected by the liquid’s presence, and the insulation plaster is dried in the course of 
two weeks. The measurements performed do not indicate any condensation in the construction. Furthermore, 
measurements verify the excellent vapour permeability through the construction with lime based insulation 
plaster, and the thermal properties and impacts of insulating the vault are confirmed.  
 
Keywords: 
Thermal insulation, lime, lime based insulation plaster, material properties, moisture 
properties, medieval church vaults, simulations, in situ climate measurements

3 Resumé (Danish) 
Projektet har til formål at undersøge og belyse hvilke effekter isoleringen af hvælv i Danske 
middelalderkirker kan medføre. Teoretiske beregninger foreslår et 40 % reduceret varmetab for hele 
kirken ved hvælvisolering, i kirker med vedvarende opvarmning. En nyudviklet kalkbaseret 
isoleringspuds med tilslag af ekspanderet vulkansk sand, perlite, undersøges. Denne opgave inkluderer 
tre hovedformål; 
1. Laboratorieundersøgelse til klargørelse af materiale egenskaber for den kalkbaserede isolerings 
puds. 
2. Simuleringer af fugt og termiske forhold i kirkehvælv uden isolering samt med isolering af mineral 
uld og isolering af kalkbaseret isoleringspuds.  
3. In situ klima målinger er taget i Annisse Kirke, som har et prøvefelt af kalkbaseret isoleringspuds 
på ¼ hvælv. 
Udvikling af blandingsforhold og recepter er udført på baggrund af masse og konsistens. Laboratorie 
undersøgelser har påvist gode isolerings egenskaber, og varmeledningsevnen er fundet til at være 
omtrent dobbelt så stor som mineral uld. Den kalkbaserede isoleringspuds viser gode egenskaber i 
forbindelse med fugthåndtering; kapillarsugningsevnen i pudsen er højere end i tegl, pudsen har gode 
evner i forbindelse med relativ hurtig udtørring, og har derudover vist sig som værende diffusionsåben. I 
forhold til brudenergi, viser pudsen gode egenskaber i forbindelse med at optage energien, og et 
overliggende mørtellag af fiberarmeret kalk mørtel øger styrken, slidstyrken og holdbarheden af 
isoleringspudsen, hvilket og gør det muligt for det isolerede hvælv at optage visse fysiske belastninger. 
Simuleringer i Delphin 5 har påvist gode fugtforhold i isoleringspudsen. Vandbelastninger påført 
overfladen viser sig ikke at påvirke det underliggende tegl hvælv. Simuleringerne påviser yderligere 
relativ hurtig udtørring af isoleringspudsen. Simuleringer af et hvælv med mineraluld påviser ligeledes 
ingen betydelig påvirkning af den underliggende tegl. Simuleringer foretaget af et uisoleret hvælv viser 
dyb vandindtrængen i teglen, og væsentlig indflydelse af eksterne termiske forhold. 
I tilfælde hvor en høj relativ luftfugtighed måles i isoleringspudsen, fremgår det at tegllaget er upåvirket af 
denne øgede fugtighed, og pudsen er udtørret i løbet af et par uger. Målingerne indikerer desuden ingen 
forekomst af kondensering i konstruktionen. Målingerne tyder på en god vanddampdiffusion gennem 
konstruktionen, og de forbedrede termiske forhold ved isolering af hvælvet bekræftes. 
 
Nøgleord:

middelalderkirker, simuleringer, in situ klimamålinger

5 Introduction 
The purpose of vault insulation is reduction of heat loss through the vaults in churches with primarily 
with constant, or almost constant, heating. In connection with researching alternative insulation 
possibilities for vaults in Danish medieval churches, a lime based insulation plaster with perlite aggregate 
is investigated. The medieval churches in Denmark are not protected for conservation as long as they are 
still actively used by the congregation. They are definitely cared for by other authorities, and renovation 
propositions must go through several levels of authority before approval. The churches are subjects of a 
special provision ensuring the cultural values are not subjected to deterioration. It is therefore imperative 
that any method of insulating the vaults does not in any way cause harm or damage to any part of the 
church construction or interior. Traditional materials and methods are preferred for insulation of the 
vaults, making the lime based insulation plaster interesting, as lime has been used for mortar and frescoes 
for centuries. Additionally, the aggregate of perlite is completely natural and inorganic. Several other 
properties are vital to consider in regard to insulation of church vaults. They include moisture transport 
(liquid water from condensation, drift snow or rain, and diffusion of water vapour), thermal properties 
(insulation capability) and strength (in regards to durability and loads e.g. in connection with roof 
maintenance) of the utilized insulation material.  In this project several material parameters are 
established by means of laboratory experiments in regards to the aforementioned important parameters. 
Approximately 1700-1800 churches in Denmark originated in the Middle Ages, corresponding to well 
over 75 % of all churches in Denmark. The construction of medieval stone churches in Denmark dates 
back to approximately 1100-1450. In the time between the establishment of Christianity in Denmark 
(965) and the construction of stone churches, wooden churches were built. The life-span of these, 
however, was relatively short. Cross rib vaults are commonly found in medieval churches from the Gothic 
Era, but were also built into existing Roman-style churches. The vaults in smaller churches were usually 
composed of half stone thick brick and lime mortar, making the thermal loss through the vault significant. 
These types of churches are well represented in the countryside as well as in the cities of Denmark. The 
actual use of these churches varies to a great extent, so when considering the effect of a reduction in heat 
loss a certain distinction must be made between churches in an active community that are continuously 
heated and less active churches with periodical heating. In general churches in the cities are more 
frequently and actively utilized, but some village churches are used for many events and activities, and are 
also constantly heated.  
Annisse Church is an example of an actively used medieval village church. It is first mentioned in Danish 
historic records in 1175. The church consists of a Romantic choir and nave, although the corbie-step gable, 
cross rib vaults, and porch suggest alterations made to the construction in the Gothic period. Annisse 
Church has been subjected to a test area with the lime based insulation plaster on ¼ of one vault. The 
plaster is applied in a 100 mm thick layer with an overlying layer of fiber reinforced lime mortar. Annisse 
Church is electronically and continuously heated, which gives cause for significant energy, and thus 
financial, savings by reducing the heat loss.

Lemvig Church is a city church from the 12th century. Initially constructed as a Roman church, but 
remodelled in the Gothic period. The Gothic style is reflected in the corbie-step gables and external 
buttresses, providing support for the cross rib vaults. The church has been renovated on several different 
occasions, most recently in 2011-2012, where insulation of the vaults, among other things, was 
implemented. The vaults were insulated with 100 mm mineral wool (Rockwool) and an overlying layer of 
cement mortar was then applied. 
Both Annisse and Lemvig Churches are represented in the project. Simulations of moisture and 
temperature conditions through the vault construction with lime based insulation plaster, representing 
Annisse Church, and a vault with insulation of mineral wool, representing Lemvig Church. 
As mentioned, part of a vault in Annisse Church was insulated with the lime based insulation plaster and 
climate data has been accumulated during in the period of September 2014 – February 2015. Results from 
the measurements are presented and analyzed in this project.  
5.1 Thesis Statement 
The scope of this project involves finding, analyzing and comparing different methods of insulating vaults 
in Danish medieval churches. The principal consideration is energy optimization, while bearing in mind 
the conservation perspective of the cultural heritage. Calculations have resulted in significant estimated 
energy savings of 40 % by insulation of the vaults.  Materials considered and compared in the project 
include mineral wool and lime based insulation plaster.  
Through laboratory experiments, certain properties of lime based insulation plaster are determined and 
simulations of moisture and heat transfer through vaults with the two insulation materials will elucidate 
the suitability of the aforementioned materials, when considering the purpose and criteria regarding 
moisture, thermal, and strength parameters. For laboratory experiments, the lime based plaster has been 
tested using two different types of lime, and to examine the possibility of further reduction of thermal 
conductivity, Scotchlite S22 is added to some specimens.

6 Literature studies 
6.1 A Test of Four Different Experimental Methods to Determine Sorption 
Isotherms 
The sorption isotherm is not determined for the insulation plaster, but literature makes way for 
understanding experimental methods for determination of sorption isotherms.  
Title:   
A Test of Four Different Experimental Methods to Determine Sorption Isotherms 
Authors: 
 Björn Johannesson and Mårten Janz 
Published by:  Lund Institute of Technology, Division of Building Materials 
Published in:  Transport and Sorption Phenomena in Concrete and Other Porous Media, Doctoral 
Thesis, Björn Johannesen, Lund, 2000. 
Four completely different and independent experimental methods for determination of sorption 
isotherms are carried out on specimens of sandstone and porous glass. The sorption isotherm for a given 
material, gives information about the material’s ability to hold moisture at different relative humidities 
and water vapour absorption. The sorption isotherm is a common material parameter used in e.g. 
simulation contexts or for definition and description of moisture properties of a given material. The four 
methods are, in addition to  experimentally performed, evaluated and discussed relative to each other. 
The four different experimental methods are: 1) The climate box method: sample equilibration over 
saturated salt solutions, 2) The sorption micro-balance method: sample weighings after exposure to 
different relative humidities, 3) The pressure plate method: over-pressure drains saturated specimens 
and 4) The sorption micro-calorimetric method: Microcalorimetric measurements. 
The climate box method is a simple method for determination of the sorption isotherm. Samples are 
placed in separate climate boxes containing different salt solutions generating different relative 
humidities. By means of a fan, it is ensured that the relative humidity is kept uniform in the entire box. The 
specimens are weighed regularly until a constant mass is reached and equilibrium is achieved. In this 
experiment, 8 samples have been measured in 8 different relative humidities ranging from 11 % (LiCl) to 
97.6 % (K2SO4). Both absorption and desorption conditions were tested. 
The sorption micro-balance method consists of sample weighings with a symmetric micro-balance during 
the course of controlled variations in relative humidity surrounding the sample in a controlled and closed 
environment. The desired relative humidity is obtained by the mixing of dry and saturated nitrogen. Only 
small specimens (50-150 mg) can be used in this method, this decreases the surface resistance and 
equilibrium can be reached relatively quickly. Diffusion is neglected as a minor source of error in the small 
specimen. In this experiment, the relative humidity has been increased (and decreased for desorption) in 
11 intervals of 9.5 % relative humidity in the range 0-95 %.

The pressure plate method involves desorption of a saturated sample, and for high relative humidities 
only (93-99.9993 %), as the only method that can reach these high levels of relative humidity. An external 
pressure is applied in a sealed pressure tank containing the saturated specimen, and by means of 
calculations the corresponding relative humidity is estimated. The pores of the specimen are therefore 
drained by the added external pressure, and the mass is determined at the different pressure loads. The 
pressure difference between either side of the specimen is controlled by an underlying saturated ceramic 
plate that is placed on top of atmospheric pressure. The capillary forces of the ceramic plate are large 
enough to withstand the external pressure given in the vessel, preventing the passing of air through the 
pore system. Different ceramic plates are used at different pressures, and at high pressure levels a 
cellulose membrane can be beneficial. The maximum pore size of the ceramic plate, however should 
always be less than the maximum pore size of the sample, thus liquid transport within the plate is possible 
without conflicting with the pressure in the tank. In order to achieve perfect adhesion between the 
specimen and the ceramic plate, saturated kaolin clay is placed in between, a cloth preventing the clay 
from penetrating the specimen. As the applied pressure forces water out of the saturated specimen, the 
water is forced through the underlying materials and collected. Equilibrium is considered reached when 
the amount of collected water reaches a constant, and at this point the specimen is weighed. 
The last method, the sorption micro-calorimetric method is relatively new, and consists of two chambers, 
a sorption chamber and a vaporization chamber, which are connected by a tube. The dry sample is placed 
in the sorption chamber, and liquid water in the vaporization chamber; vaporized water travels to the 
sorption chamber through the interconnecting tube. The thermal power produced in the vaporizing 
chamber is used in calculations of the sorption isotherm. Assumptions in regard to the samples’ mass gain 
and surrounding relative humidities are established by means of calculations based on estimations of the 
water vapour flow through the diffusion tube and measured thermal power.

6.2 Measurement of the moisture storage capacity using sorption balance and pressure 
extractors 
As described in section 11, water retention curves were not found by means of laboratory experiments for 
Delphin simulations. Through studied literature, however the means of determination can be understood. 
The paper concerns the determination of moisture storage capacity in several porous materials. Results 
are presented in both water retention curves, as used in Delphin simulations, seen in section 11.1.1, and as 
sorption isotherms by determination of storage capacity in the hygroscopic range (sorption balance) and 
super-hygroscopic range (pressure plate). 
Title:  
Measurement of the moisture storage capacity using sorption balance and pressure 
extractors 
Authors: 
 Mårten Janz and Björn Johannesson 
Published by:  Lund Institute of Technology, Division of Building Materials 
Published in:  Transport and Sorption Phenomena in Concrete and Other Porous Media, Doctoral 
Thesis, Björn Johannesen, Lund, 2000. 
Moisture can have various effects on porous building materials, in regard to damage, degradation, 
durability, properties, and alterations in material properties. This represents the importance of knowing 
the moisture transportation properties of building materials. This is especially true for constructions of 
multiple layers, where the moisture conditions at interfaces are interesting. Water can be bound in the 
material both physically and chemically, in this case the emphasis is on physically bound water, which 
occurs when water molecules are bound to the surfaces of pores, until equilibrium with ambient 
circumstances takes place.  There is distinguished between capillary condensation and capillary water 
uptake, occurring in the hygroscopic and super-hygroscopic ranges respectively. For the hygroscopic 
range, the moisture storage content can be expressed by sorption isotherms determined by the relation 
between the moisture content in ambient air and in the given material. For the super-hygroscopic range, 
the moisture storage content is expressed by water retention curves, defined by the suction (pressure 
difference between ambient air pressure and pore water pressure) relative to the moisture content in the 
material. Hysteresis is commonly found in many materials between absorption and desorption for 
unknown reasons. The article covers brief explanations of measurements for the determination of 
sorption isotherms, as described in section 6.1, regarding saturated salt solutions, micro-calorimeter and 
sorption balance. The article states that given certain assumptions (a pore system consisting of cylindrical 
tubes with varying radii), it is possible to establish the pore size distribution given the moisture storage 
capacity, based on the contact angle and either sorption isotherm or water retention curves. Thus the 
opposite calculations are also possible, and the water retention curve can be determined by pore size 
distribution, which in turn can be determined by e.g. mercury intrusion porosimetry or freezing point 
depression.

The article describes experiments performed on cement mortar, cement-lime mortar, lime silica brick, 
two types of clay brick and a sandstone. Initially, general material properties such as volume, density, 
porosity and capillary number were determined by means described in (LBM-Prøvemetode 1) and (LBM-
Prøvemetode 2).  
For determination of moisture storage capacity in the hygroscopic range, the sorption isotherm was 
determined by means of the sorption micro-balance method, as described in section 6.1. Sample portions 
of approximately 50 mg crushed material were tested in relative humidities ranging from 0-95 % in 
intervals of 9.5 %. The equilibrium was considered reached when a mass change of less than 0.0001 % per 
minute was achieved. Additionally, a desorption curve for the cement mortar was made, initially with a 
saturated specimen, and the relative humidity decreasing in 15 steps from 92-0 %. 
For determining the moisture storage capacity in the super-hygroscopic range, the pressure plate method, 
as described in section 6.1, is utilized. For further information than given in section 6.1, it is mentioned 
that it is an advantage for the ceramic plate to have as large pores as possible, for more rapid tests, and the 
cellulose membrane is used at pressures of 15-100 bar. The overpressure applied to the tank is by means 
of nitrogen. The kaolin clay is not used on the cellulose membrane, as the good hydraulic contact can be 
established without. Before carrying out experiments, all specimens were water saturated by means of 
capillary suction (sandstone) and vacuum saturation (cement mortar, lime-cement mortar and bricks). 
Equilibrium was considered achieved when the water flow out of the pressure tank was less than 0.05 ml 
in 48 hours. After mass determination, the specimens were placed back in the pressure tank, and the 
pressure increased until the next equilibrium.  
The results from the experiments show densities and porosities in the range of 1800-2100 kg/m3 and 23-
30 % respectively, except aerated concrete with values of 594 kg/m3 and 79 % porosity. The capillary 
number is highest for samples of brick and sandstone, and lowest in cement mortar. Figure 6.2-1 shows an 
example of an achieved water retention curve – in this case for autoclaved aerated concrete, found by the 
combination of absorption (sorption balance method) and desorption (pressure plate method).

6.3 British Standard and RILEM water absorption tests: A critical evaluation 
Liquid transport properties have been determined in the project by means of capillary suction, vacuum 
saturation and Karsten tube. Some of the methods resemble ones described and criticized in the literature, 
as described below. 
Title:  
British Standard and RILEM water absorption tests: A critical evaluation 
Authors: 
 M. A. Wilson, M. A. Carter and W. D. Hoff 
Published by:  Department of Building Engineering, University of Manchester Institute of Science 
and Technology 
Published in:  Materials and Structures /Matériaux et Constructions, Vol. 32, 1999. 
The article describes experiments of water absorption performed on brick, stone and concrete with a 
critical approach. In regards to water absorption properties of porous construction materials, the total 
porosity and capillary suction are vital parameters in regard to strength, permeability and prediction of 
frost resistance. In masonry, the capillary suction can cause a weakening of the mortar if the suction 
occurs too rapidly, although preventing/reducing measures can be implemented, by wetting of the bricks 
or using additives to retain the water in the mortar. Also, the capillary suction is vital in regards to water 
loads in form of rain, as a too low capillary suction leads to the water being transported directly to micro-
cracks in joints or defects in the surface. 
Neither the British Standard nor RILEM, contain a description of sorptivity measurements (at publishing 
time). However a method is described by Hall and Tse, that resembles the method described in (LBM-
Prøvemetode 1). A specimen is oven-dried, cooled to room temperature and immersed in 3-5 mm water. 
The sample is weighed at certain time intervals, and the gradient of the slope of cumulative absorbed 
water volume per area plotted against the square root of time, represents sorptivity. The article states 
that sources of error in this experimental setup include accuracy in the timing procedure and water being 
sucked by the specimen sides as well as the bottom area. These sources of error can be directly 
transposed to the experimental setup of the (LBM-Prøvemetode 1) in determination of capillary suction.  
In regards to the initial rate of suction, the article describes a test method from the Bristish Standard, BS 
3921, and variations of the method for different materials. In short, the method consists of the immersion 
of a dry brick in 3 mm water, and weighing after 1 minute. No considerations are made in regards to 
timing errors or temperature variations, making the method erroneous, as sorptivity is somewhat 
dependent on temperature. In the version for natural stone, the dried specimen is immersed in 2 mm 
water, but with only one absorbing surface. The change of mass is measured regularly, until the upper 
surface is wetted. The method is more accurate to some extent, as it continues for a longer period, 
however no corrections are made in regards to timing errors or deviations from the linearity. A second 
variation, a dry concrete sample is immersed in 5 mm water and the mass change registered in large scale 
time steps. This method fails to consider issues regarding long-term absorption into concrete.

There is no vacuum absorption test in the British Standard, but a few variations are found in RILEM. For 
determination of water absorption porosity, a method resembling the one of (LBM-Prøvemetode 2), is 
used for determination of total porosity. The method consists of drying and cooling a specimen, placing it 
in a sealed chamber and initially applying vacuum pressure, followed by a water supply forcing 
penetration of all pores in the material. The variations in RILEM consist of different pressures and soaking 
times for the specimens of masonry, natural stones and concrete respectively. Investigations of vacuum 
saturation have shown different optimal procedures for saturation of given materials.  
BS 3921 contains a description of water absorption tests of masonry by boiling. A dry specimen is placed 
in a water tank at room temperature, and the temperature is, in the course of 1 hour, increased to 100 °C 
and maintained there for 5 hours. After cooling for 16-19 hours, the specimens are weighed and the water 
absorption porosity calculated. For natural stone, the method consists of boiling a previously air-dried 
specimen for 3 hours, followed by 24 hours of soaking and subsequent oven drying. For both methods, the 
results are presented in a percentage of dry mass. It is generally believed that the boiling procedures give 
the true total water porosity, as the water within the pores becomes water vapour and expands, the 
pressure increases and the possibility of penetrating all pores is present. With cooling, the phase-change 
from vapour to water will occur, thus achieving a fully saturated specimen. However, damage to the 
ceramic within the brick should be considered. 
By determination of the saturation coefficient, tests of water absorption by soaking are used. The 
saturation coefficient is defined as the percentage of volume filled pores by a 24 hour soak, specified in 
percentage water content by either mass or volume of an initially dry specimen. As air is trapped under 
the meniscuses in pores, a full saturation will not comply within 24 hours. However, in time the air will 
dissolve in the water and diffuse through the pore system, but including the evaporation from exposed 
surfaces, the duration in which a brick will achieve full 
saturation is 2 years. The soaking method described, is from 
BS 7263. For masonry, the specimens are soaked for 24 hours, 
and for natural stone, mass changes are registered every 24 
hours until constant mass is obtained. Concrete specimens are 
soaked and weighed every 24 hours until the change in mass 
between successive weighings is < 1 %. In none of the 
methods (brick, stone and concrete) are the specimens dried 
at the  initiation of the experiment, causing a reduced

7 Materials 
There are several complications involved in the insulation of vaults in Danish medieval churches, thus the 
material used must be carefully selected. Challenges found in connection with the insulation include the 
double curved irregular surface, the moisture found in the church attic, the ability to carry loads of a 
certain amount and the importance of building preservation. The material used therefore must be able to 
be distributed evenly on the surface, bear certain weight loads, absorb and give off moisture before 
reaching the original vault, and in some way provide a link to traditional building practices. The vault 
itself in medieval churches in Denmark is usually built of monk bricks in a single layer, bound together by 
lime mortar. In this section, materials investigated and/or in other ways used in connection with this 
project, will be presented. 
7.1 Lime 
Lime has been used in the building industry for thousands of years, and some even argue that the 
Egyptians used lime in the construction of the pyramids (Hansen, 2008). In Denmark lime has been 
produced for the building industry since the beginning of the Middle Ages. Lime has good building 
properties and durability, which provides the basis for its wide and long-term use. Lime is extracted from 
rocks containing calcium carbonate. The calcium carbonate undergoes certain procedures in order to 
achieve a desired workability, and after processing the product, it re-gains its desired natural properties. 
This process is illustrated in Figure 7.1-1 and described below in [1], [2] and [3].

There are basically two types of lime; air lime and hydraulic lime. The differences between the two lime 
types, are described below, and both types have been studied during the course of this project. Rødvig 
slaked lime is an air lime, and NHL3.5 is a hydraulic lime. 
Air lime is dependent on the presence of air and water for the carbonization process, as explained above. 
The carbonation occurs as a surface process and works into the material in time. The CO2 content in the 
air is mandatory, and water on the surface of the crystals makes it easier for the carbon dioxide to react in 
the carbonization process. An optimal carbonization in air lime occurs at a relative humidity of 50-60 % 
leaving the material neither too dry nor too wet. 
When the lime contains powdered oxides of Ca, Si, Al or Fe it can be categorized as hydraulic. The binders 
are hydraulic and make it possible for the lime to harden when in contact with water, and the presence of 
air is redundant for the reactions to occur. When no air is present, the carbonation process occurs from 
the inside as a volume process. If both air and water are present, both surface and volume processes 
occur. 
Lime has been used in traditional masonry, as a binding agent and mortar, in many medieval churches in 
Denmark (Andersen, Christiansen & Jakobsen, 2012). The use of lime in an insulating material for 
churches therefore seems reasonable when considering the preservation value of the church.

7.2 Perlite 
Perlite is a naturally occurring type of volcanic rock, which by rapid heating expands up to 20 times its 
original volume (Nordisk Perlite) Clusters of microscopic glass bubbles are formed as the formerly 
trapped water vaporizes (www.perlite.net). The expanded perlite does not contain any additives or 
chemical products, is inorganic, chemically inactive and still considered a natural product after expansion. 
The type of expanded perlite used in this project, is Europerl Perlite from Nordisk Perlite. The used grain 
size is 1-6 mm.

repellent, and the dust generation subdued. Perlite can also be used as an aggregate to concrete, mortar,

7.7 Bricks 
The first brick buildings in Denmark were churches and castles built in the 12th century. Bricks are made 
from burnt clay, and have various beneficial properties depending on the clay content, the processing 
method and particularly the firing temperature. Dry density, porosity, compressive strength, capillary 
number, and hygroscopicity are all essential properties of the brick, and all depend on the aforementioned 
factors. A lower dry density is the result of a higher porosity. The porosity of brick usually lies in the 
interval of 20-30 %, allowing a certain air exchange through the material. The color of bricks depends on 
the content of oxides and lime, e.g. red bricks usually have a maximum of 3 % lime, whereas the lime 
content in yellow bricks is at least 3 times the iron content (Hansen, 2008). 
7.7.1 Falkenløwe brick manufacturer 
Falkenløwe produces historic brick in the original manner. The clay is hand molded, air dried, and burned 
in a coal fired ring kiln for 6-7 days. The clay is only treated by the addition of sand in order to emaciate, 
and no chemicals are added. The old production method ensures a low compressive strength and a high 
water absorption, as historic brick is known for (Falkenløwe A/S).

9.3 Density and porosity 
Basic material parameters such as density and porosity are to be determined. Density and porosity are 
vital parameters for any given materials. The density of a material can be described in several ways 
relative to the state of the given materials and considering the porosity as well. Almost all materials are 
porous to a greater or lesser extent. The porosity effects parameters such as strength, thermal conduction, 
permeability of air and water, frost resistance, and water absorption This is not only dependent on the 
magnitude of the porosity, but also the pore size distribution, the shape of the pores and the quantity of 
open and closed pores (Nielsen A. , 1992).  
In the first instance, LBM – Test Method 2 is sought to be used (LBM-Prøvemetode 2) for determination of 
density and porosity parameters. Three different densities can be found by means of the given test 
method:  solid density, ρf, dry density, ρd, and density of the specimen in a vacuum water saturated surface 
dry condition, ρssd. The open porosity and the volume of open pores are determined by means of the test 
method, but the total porosity can also be found by the results. The density and porosity found for 
insulating lime plaster is to be used in Delphin simulations of a church vault with the given insulation 
plaster.  
Results from LBM Test Method 2 revealed, however, that the method was not conclusive as to results 
involving density and porosity, due to the water not being able to penetrate the perlite bubbles, even 
when under pressure. The perlite was thus air-filled to a certain extent and the true porosity not found. 
The results, revealed the open porosity corresponding to the effective saturation moisture content, used 
in Delphin simulations. The dry density and porosity were found by a simpler method.

9.4 Moisture transport 
Water can be transported through a material in either vapour or liquid form, through either vapour 
diffusion or capillary suction. Vapour diffusion is generally caused by differences in temperature or 
vapour pressure between the surfaces of the material. Capillary suction occurs as liquid is transported in 
porous mediums as a result of cohesive pores. The rate of the moisture transport is always dependent on 
the pore structure, whether they are open or closed pores, and the pore size distribution.  
The different forms of water transport will be further explained in the next paragraphs, and experiments 
regarding both vapour diffusion and capillarity have been conducted. Results of the experiments will also 
be included in the following sections.

vital to recognize and investigate the possibilities of moisture transfer in the material. The risk of dripping

9.4.1 Capillary suction 
Capillary suction occurs in porous materials when the material comes in contact with water. The suction 
happens as a result of a hydrostatic vacuum and surface tension in a moist pore. The moisture creates a 
concave water surface, a meniscus, under which a hydrostatic vacuum is created and the surface tension 
occurs. In an attempt to level out the pressure, a force stronger than gravity and friction combined sucks 
up water which is in contact with the material. As the water takes up more space in the pores, the 
resistance caused by friction is increased, the pull of gravity also steps in, and the process will slow down 
(Nielsen A. , 1992), (BYG DTU). The hydrostatic pressure increases as the meniscus radius decreases, and 
for larger pores capillary suction is therefore not possible. For very small pores that are filled quickly, a 
significant resistance from friction occurs although the hydrostatic vacuum is larger. The capillarity 
number, k, indicates the rate of the capillary suction, and a high capillary number means that the material 
has a fast suction property. 
Two experiments regarding capillary suction are conducted: LBM Test Method 1 and Karsten tube. Both 
methods result in values of sorptivity/capillary number, and the Karsten tube also results in the capillary 
saturated moisture content.

9.4.1.2 Karsten tube method 
The non-destructive Karsten tube method is used in order to estimate water absorption properties of 
porous materials. The method was first described by Rudolph Karsten in 1963 and became a quasi-
standardized test method in the 1970’s (Hendrickx, 2012). Several modifications of the method have been 
made, and a standard is currently (assumed to be currently in production – was being drafted at the time 
of Roel Hendrickx’ article, 2012) under drafting. It includes information regarding tube size, filling-
method, etc. A major benefit of the Karsten tube method is the rapid and simple way of assessing a water 
repelling treatment and the water transport effect of different surfaces. Results from Karsten tube 
measurements were previously used solely comparatively, but Roel Hendrickx has theoretically described 
analytical and numerical methods for evaluating results obtained by the use of the Karsten tube 
(Hendrickx, 2012), thus estimating water transport parameters such as capillary number, k, and capillary 
saturated volume moisture content, θcap.  The calculation methods described by Hendrickx are used in the 
processing of obtained test results in these Karsten tube experiments. 
The Karsten tube experiments are carried out on test specimens of Rødvig slaked lime and Natural

9.4.2 Drying 
In connection with the existing exposure to moisture in the church loft, the uptake and discharge 
possibilities of moisture are equally important factors to prevent moisture from doing damage to the 
construction. The drying out of a material involves the transportation of moisture to the surface of the 
material for evaporation. This transport occurs as a consequence of different moisture contents and the 
attempt of achieving equilibrium. The drying of a material is also dependent on external factors, such as 
==PAGE:60==
Tessa Kvist Hansen, 2015 
11573: Thermal Insulation of Vaults in Danish Medieval Churches 
DTU·BYG 
 
 
 
 
Page 58 of 138 
 
temperature, relative humidity, and air velocity. The drying of a material corresponds to the moisture 
uptake of the given material, and the drying mechanisms are by means of both capillary forces and 
diffusion. When liquid water is present in the material, the pores are saturated, the relative humidity is 
high, and capillary forces suck the moisture toward the surface. At a lower relative humidity, and when 
the pores are not saturated, diffusion is the main method of moisture transportation. 
The drying out of a material is not a constant, stationary mechanism but rather divided into three phases, 
as described by Krischer (Nielsen A. F., 1974), as seen in Figure 9.4-12. The drying rate is defined by g. 
 
Figure 9.4-12: Rate of drying as a variation of time (Nielsen A. F., 1974). 
The initial phase has a constant drying rate. The material is capillary saturated, and the drying is caused 
by the capillary forces being able to keep a moist surface by suction through the pore system. The rate is 
thus kept constant until the capillary forces are no longer able to maintain a moist surface. The drying rate 
is dependent on the temperature, relative humidity, and air velocity of the surroundings, as different 
conditions cause variations in vapour pressure and the convective moisture transfer coefficient. At the 
end of phase one, the material near the surface will contain maximum hygroscopic moisture. In the second 
phase, the rate of drying decreases, and the drying occurs as a combination of capillary transport in the 
depth of the material and vapour diffusion near the surface. In the third phase, the moisture content of the 
entire material reaches hygroscopic levels and only vapour diffusion occurs, causing the drying rate to 
decrease further. The drying will cease when moisture equilibrium between the material and the 
surrounding environment is achieved.

9.4.3 Water vapour diffusion 
Water vapour permeability is a vital parameter in regards to moisture transportation through a material. 
The parameter states the material’s ability to let water vapour penetrate. The water vapour transport 
happens in connection with uneven vapour pressure on either side of the vault, and the water vapour 
seeks equilibrium by penetrating the construction. The vapour pressure inside the church will exceed the 
attic vapour pressure as the temperature or relative humidity inside the church increases, or the loft 
temperature decreases. Condensation can occur if the vapour reaches a surface that is colder than the dew 
point temperature. To avoid condensation an implemented insulation material should be open to vapour 
diffusion, preventing the moist air to be trapped at the interface between materials or elsewhere within 
the construction. Should the water vapour condensate in the insulation material, a material with high 
capillary suction could transport the liquid moisture to the surface for evaporation (Brandt & et.al., 2013). 
The church loft also has natural ventilation, which would aid in the evaporation process of possible liquid 
moisture.  
Several parameters in regard to water vapour permeability are found by conducting experiments 
according to EN ISO 12572:2001 (Dansk Standard, 2001) – The Cup Test. The vitals of the investigated 
parameters are stated below. The water vapour permeance, W,  indicates the rate at which water vapour 
moves through the material with respect to the partial vapour pressure. The water vapour resistance, Z, 
describes the material’s resistance towards moisture transport. The water vapour permeability, δ, 
represents the material’s capability of allowing water vapour to penetrate. Also, the water vapour 
resistance factor, μ, a factor describing the material’s resistance of vapour penetration and flow relative to 
an equivalent air layer, is found.

9.5 Thermal conductivity 
Heat transfer is a vital component in regard to heat loss in any given building. There are three main types 
of heat transfer, namely convection, radiation and conduction. Convection is caused by the motion of a 
fluid/gas surrounding an object, and the heat transfer occurs between the two mediums. Radiation is the 
only heat transfer mechanism that does not require a medium, as radiation occurs to and from any given 
surface, even in a vacuum. Conduction is basically the direct transfer of heat between two objects. The 
thermal conductivity of a material, λ, is a material parameter describing the material’s ability to transfer 
heat. Thus for materials with insulation purposes, a low λ-value is sought. Two separate experimental 
setups are used to determine the thermal conductivity of the lime based insulation plaster; the simple 
apparatus called Isomet Heat Transfer Analyzer and the more complex setup of the Guarded Hot Plate. 
Both methods are described in the following paragraphs. 
The thermal conductivity in a composite material can be assessed by composite models of parallel and 
serial connections, described in (Gottfredsen & Nielsen, 1997). The models basically express upper and 
lower limits for the thermal conductivity of the composite material. Calculations are based on the ratio 
between the two compounds and the difference in thermal conductivity. In equations [36] and [37], V 
expresses the content of material 1 and 2 and λ represents the two thermal conductivities.

9.5.1 ISOMET Heat Transfer Analyzer 
The ISOMET Heat Transfer Analyzer is a simple method of gaining information about a material’s heat 
transfer parameters. For the experiment, an ISOMET Heat Transfer Analyzer model 2104 is used. In 
general terms, the ISOMET is a portable piece of equipment used to determine thermo-physical properties 
in a given material; mainly thermal conductivity, λ, heat capacity, cp, and thermal diffusivity, a. The 
dynamic measurements made by the ISOMET are based on temperature responses in the material. The 
probe sends a flow of heat into the material, and periodical temperature readings are made in order to 
determine thermal properties, provided it is assumed that the heat transport occurs in an infinite 
material. 
The experiment is carried out three times on each of the specimens specified in Table 9.5-2. There is no 
technical standard used, but specifications are given in the user manual of the equipment. A surface probe 
for thermal conductivities in the range of 0.04-0.30 W/(m·K) is used. The probe is specified for use on 
smooth, planar surfaces, with a diameter of at least 60 mm and a thickness of at least 10-15 mm. In order 
to avoid additional heat loss to the table surface, the specimens are placed on 30 mm of rigid insulation, as 
seen in Figure 9.5-2.

9.5.2 Guarded hot plate method 
The guarded hot plate is to determine the thermal conductivity of a given material by means of Fourier’s 
law. Fourier’s law is utilized by gaining information about the temperature difference between the 
surfaces of the test specimen, the temperature at the bottom of the specimen and the effect given from the 
hot plate. The above mentioned information is gathered in the experiment. The experiment and results 
are managed according to DS/EN 12664:2001 (Dansk Standard, 2001).  
The aforementioned dimensions of the test specimens are made in order to cover the heating unit in the 
guarded hot plate experiment, and the thickness of 30 mm corresponds to 5 times the nominal grain size 
of the perlite (1-6 mm), which is considered acceptable. Furthermore, Table A.1 (Dansk Standard, 2001) 
prescribes maximum and minimum thickness of specimens. It is vital to obtain as close contact as possible 
between the specimen and apparatus, which implies the need for plane surfaces. A planarity deviation of 2 
% of the specimen thickness is allowed for rigid materials. This cannot be obtained within the given 
material, so a compressible material is placed between the specimen and plates in order to establish good 
thermal contact. The experimental setup is illustrated in Figure 9.5-4.

9.6 Strength parameters

11 Delphin simulations 
The reasons for simulations in general are many. It might not always be possible to conduct correct 
experiments, they may be costly and time consuming, the presence of materials may be insufficient etc. 
Simulations are made in order to determine different responses to different situations given different 
materials and constructions. Simulations can determine how a given system performs under certain 
conditions. 
The software program Delphin 5 (version 5.6.8) is used to create 1D simulations of planar vertical 
transport of heat and moisture through the vault of a church. The simulations are made as an effort to 
show the moisture uptake and evaporation from the recently developed lime plaster with perlite. As a 
reference, simulations are made with conventional insulation of mineral wool as well.  
Delphin is basically a simulation program that can generate accurate simulations of coupled heat, vapour, 
mass (e.g. salt), air and pollution transfer through porous building materials. The software is used in 
various applications of planning, design, evaluation and indication of problem areas in both new 
constructions as well as existing structures. By means of Delphin simulations, yearly heating demands, 
mold growth criteria and thermal bridges with risk of condensation can also be determined. Furthermore, 
Delphin is used in several fields of research and development. 
The construction in Delphin is built in homogenous layers of certain thickness, and boundary as well as 
climate conditions, on either side of the construction is added. The desired output is determined before 
running the simulation.

11.1.4 Additional water load 
Water representing different moisture load situations connected to the occurrence of dripping 
condensation, melting drift snow or rain through leakages, are simulated by an additional water load 
added  to the upper vault surface. Thus the rain can be water gathered on other occasions than actual rain, 
but for simulation purposes the function of rain fall has been used. The rain programs are described in 
Table 11.1-4 and Figure 11.1-4. In the depicted program, it is seen that the duration of the rain fall is 
increased by 1 hour at every interval, and the drying period between rain falls is increased. The

The relative humidity in the insulation increases rapidly and excessively in the middle of October. There 
are no definite explanations for this, as no dripping condensation or roof leakage has been detected. 
However, as seen in Figure 7.1-1 and equations [1], [2] and [3], water is released during the carbonization 
process of lime. It is possible that the carbonization process reaches this point in October 2014. As 
described in section 9.1, full carbonization of the test specimens (the center) occurs 25.5 weeks after 
molding, and 8 weeks in the climate chamber with a supply of CO2. The insulation of Annisse Church took 
place in the spring of 2014. With an unknown of CO2 concentration and an overlying layer of lime mortar, 
it is plausible that the carbonization process took 6-7 months to reach the center of the insulation layer. 
The relative humidity only exceeds 100 % for a period of a few weeks, and furthermore, it is seen that the 
original brick vault is unaffected by the moisture.  
In Figure 11.3-6 the calculated vapour pressure on the construction elements is shown. Initially the 
saturated vapour pressure is calculated based on the temperature, and the vapour pressure is based on 
the relative humidity and the saturated vapour pressure. As can be seen in the figure, the vapour 
pressures in the insulation and in both vaults lies between the vapour pressures found in the church nave 
and the attic respectively. This suggests that the conditions are stable and a balance for water vapour is 
found. Hence, these results suggest that the water vapour permeability does not cause unwanted 
resistance for the vapour transfer through the vault. Calculations of vapour pressure are described in 
equations [48] and [49]. 
 
The increased relative humidity in October 2014 is also visible in terms of vapour pressure, and in that 
period the vapour pressure in the insulation exceeds the vapour pressure in the church nave. As in the 
relative humidity measurements, these conditions pass within a few weeks.

14 Discussion 
14.1 Recipe development 
The simplicity of the processing and workability of the lime based insulation plaster is reflected in the recipe 
development. The insulation plaster can be mixed by hand power, and consists merely of 3 basic 
ingredients - lime, water, and perlite. In Annisse Church, the insulation plaster is applied by hand, and the 
most difficult part of the work includes the assuming of awkward body positions in the church attic to 
work around the vault. The plaster is laid in a 10 cm thick layer, working upwards from the bottom by 
means of simple masonry hand tools.  
14.2 Material parameters 
Carbonization of 10 cm thick specimens is a process that requires presence of CO2 and takes time. It is 
seen that specimens which are 3 months old and stored without a CO2 supply have carbonized 
approximately 1 cm. With subsequent CO2 supply, the specimens are fully carbonized after 2 months. 
From the test field in Annisse Church, measurements indicate that the carbonization of the middle of the 
layer occurs after 6-7 months.  
Table 14.2-1 shows selected material properties of the lime based insulation plaster found by laboratory 
experiments as well as reference values for bricks and mineral wool. As it can be seen, the open porosity 
found by saturation, as also described in section 6.3, and equivalent to the effective water saturation, is 
relatively close to that of mineral wool. Mineral wool consists primarily of air, resulting in the very high 
open porosity. The dry density found in the insulation plaster is also relatively close to the largest value 
found for mineral wool – again, the air content is vital for this parameter. The porosity estimated by 
physical measurements is close, yet of lesser value, than that of mineral wool, and substantially larger 
than that of brick. As mineral wool is non-hygroscopic, there are no reference values regarding the 
retaining and suction of liquid moisture. The capillary numbers are found to be in the same range as the 
maximum for brick. There is a significant difference, however, in the results from the two experimental 
methods for the determination of the capillary number. In both cases though, the capillary number is 
found to be higher in specimens of Rødvig slaked lime as opposed to Natural Hydraulic Lime. The critical 
literature in section 6.3 doesn’t include the Karsten tube measurements, however critic of a method 
similar to (LBM-Prøvemetode 1) includes errors in the timing procedure and the possibility of absorption 
through the sides and variations in immersion depth. The errors in regard to the timing procedure can be 
sought to apply to the Karsten tube measurements as well, and also the decrease in water pressure as the 
water level drops can perhaps be regarded a source of error. It should also be noted, in regard to the 
Karsten tube measurements in situ and on specimens with cattle hair mortar, that the conditions at the 
interface are unknown. Although not confirmed, it is assumed that the water travels to the layer of 
insulation beneath the mortar. The ultrasound measurements on specimens subjected to water loads in 
Karsten tube were unfortunately found to be inconclusive. Experiments able to determine the liquid water 
flow and accumulation within the plaster, by means of either X-ray visualization or relative humidity 
sensors should be conducted. Hereby it could be established how the water spreads in the insulation layer 
and under the mortar.

lime mortar, the insulation plaster has shows capabilities of withstanding people loads (in Annisse 
Church) and a rupture energy of more than 8.72 J.

Experiments concerning the drying of specimens at two different air velocities show, not surprisingly, 
faster initial drying rates occurs for specimens subjected to an air velocity of 3 m/s relative to specimens 
exposed to zero air velocity. The experiments furthermore show a tendency of faster drying in specimens 
of Rødvig slaked lime compared to Natural Hydraulic Lime, and is more pronounced in specimens 
subjected to an air velocity of 3 m/s, but applicable in both cases. The air velocity in the attic of the church 
is unknown, however expected to be closer to 0 m/s than 3 m/s. It is not completely still due to leakages 
and impacts from the external air velocity. 
In referring to the different lime types, specimens of Rødvig slaked lime generally seem to be better in 
regard to the moisture properties of vapour diffusion and capillary suction. In association with vault 
insulation, the moisture properties are vital, as water vapour must be able to diffuse between the church 
==PAGE:121==

nave and the attic without getting trapped and condensing. In regard to capillary suction, it is best for the 
insulation plaster to have better properties than brick. In this case, when liquid loads occur from the top 
in the form of e.g. condensation drops from the roof or rain/drift snow through leakages, it can spread 
within the insulation layer. This prevents, or limits, the liquid transport to the brick vault due to gravity 
and capillary suction. 
14.3 Cattle hair mortar 
The lime mortar above the insulation plaster might create a sort of lime membrane on the surface. This 
membrane was discussed by Anders Nielsen at the Nordic Lime Forum’s annual meeting in October 2014. 
The membrane is formed as a tight layer of CaCO3 when the surface of the mortar carbonizes quickly in 
reaction to the air. The membrane supposedly delays/reduces liquid transport into the mortar, while the 
surface is open for diffusion from the inside so the possibility of liquid transport to the surface still exists. 
This capability is useful to a certain extent in the vault insulation. A possible liquid load should not, 
however, run off to the vault pockets by the external walls. Experiments, both in situ and the laboratory do 
not suggest that the effect of the lime membrane is problematic. 
14.4 Delphin simulations 
Delphin simulations show temperature variations in the construction similar to the ones measured in 
Annisse Church, and the results seem realistic, in relation to temperature and relative humidity variations 
throughout the vault construction. Deviations can be caused by inadequate information about the 
different existing material layers where several assumptions have been made based on accessible 
information on similar materials. Another source of error, can be found in the fact that the brick vault, in 
simulations, merely consists of brick, and the lime mortar between the bricks is neglected. The lime 
mortar may cause changes the moisture transport properties, principally in regard to water vapour, as the 
liquid does not reach the brick layer. The vault, after all, consists of ≈14 vol% mortar. Also, no suction 
experiment is conducted, and the water retention curve for the insulation plaster is assumed. Delphin 
generally gives the impression of the insulation plaster being able to manage the additional water loads. It 
is seen that the water loads are most critical in the summer months, though the water does not seem to 
penetrate to the brick layer, even with a substantial water load of 50 mm in both February and August. 
The moisture seems to be held and spread in the insulation layer, and dries out relatively quickly. Delphin 
simulations performed are 1-dimensional, but aforementioned experiments concerning accumulation and 
internal transportation of water could verify or invalidate the thesis of dissemination in the insulation 
plaster. It is possible that saturation prevents further absorption in the Delphin simulations, causing the 
results for all the rain programs in Lemvig Church to resemble each other tremendously. Furthermore, 
Delphin simulations show no signs of condensation within the construction layers or at interfaces 
between layers.  
14.5 Climate data 
The climate measurements in Annisse Church show that condensation in neither the insulation nor brick 
occurs, except the last two weeks of October 2014. At this time it is assumed that the liquid presence is 
caused by the carbonization of the middle of the lime based insulation layer. The measurements also show 
that in the period of 100 % relative humidity, the brick layer is unaffected, and the moisture evaporated

14.6 Possible consequences 
The consequences of insulating a church vault are may not only be the reduced heat loss and the 
subsequent energy savings.  
Insulation with granulate of paper or wood fiber not only complicates the roof maintenance, but moisture 
transportation properties are also limited. Furthermore, if the wooden roof construction is partially 
covered by insulation, the ventilation around the wood is reduced, and the possibility of drying if 
subjected to moisture, thus becomes limited. In turn, this can be the cause of rotting and/or mold 
formation. Insulation with the lime based insulation plaster prevents rotting and molding, in part because 
it is inorganic in itself and does not allow biological decomposition, but also by covering the vault 
geometrically, contact with the wood is avoided.  
The consequences regarding salt damage in insulation of vault are not known to the full extent. By 
insulating the vault, it becomes warmer, causing further evaporation of the moisture in the bricks, leaving 
the possibility of salt crystallization on the surface of the bricks. Any form of insulation of vaults with 
previous salt damage or priceless frescoes is not recommended. 
By insulation of the church vault, inevitably the self-weight of the vault is increased. This causes the 
outward forces to the external walls to be increased as well. The outward forces the vaults create on the 
external walls can be the cause of a gap emerging between the wall and the pointed arc bounding the vault 
externally, or even the cause of crack formation or deformation of the vault itself or the façade walls. In 
the Gothic era, when vaults were implemented in many roman style churches, the outward forces were 
taken into account by construction of internal and/or external pillars for the purpose of absorbing the 
forces gained from the transverse arches and cross ribs. In Annisse Church the presence of these pillars 
are seen internally. It is assumed that these pillars are capable of handling the additional load, as the 
density of the material is so low, and the additional load insignificant relative to the load from the weight 
of the bricks used. A illustration of a vault is seen in Figure 14.6-1.

The overall consequences of church vault insulation are not known to the full extent, but several scenarios 
are possible besides the ones mentioned above; e.g. the temperature in the church during summer 
becoming too cold, as the solar heated air from the attic is prevented from descending into the church and 
heating the air. In the case of required church heating in the summer period to obtain thermal comfort, the 
insulation of the vaults should be reconsidered. If vault insulation is the cause of a reduced relative 
humidity in the church nave, historical and other inventory may be damaged, although this seems unlikely 
to be the case. Measurements from the mineral wool insulated church in Lemvig do not show signs of 
significantly reduced relative humidity in winter, nor notably temperature reductions in the church 
during summer. To avoid the low relative humidity in the church during the winter, the inside 
temperature should be reduced in cold periods when not in use. Both scenarios described above could be 
investigated and clarified by simulations regarding indoor climate in e.g. BSim.  
14.7 Energy optimization in the medieval church 
Many actions can be taken in connection with reducing the energy consumption in the Danish medieval 
churches. The insulation of vaults is merely an initiative to reduce heat loss. A further reduction of heat 
loss can be achieved e.g. by the implementation of double glazing or the installation of low energy panes of 
glass. The heating system should be proportionally dimensioned for the specific church and 
heating/cooling time should be taken into consideration. For optimal energy efficiency, rapid heating and 
cooling of the church is preferred. Other suggested improvements for energy efficiency include LED 
lighting, reduced outdoor lighting, and implementation of church forest. One acre of church forest is said 
to bind 10 tons of CO2 annually.

14.8 Profitability 
As previously mentioned, the theoretical annual energy savings are estimated to be 40 %, and thus 18,500 
kWh using simplified calculations. Simple calculations of financial savings, based on an energy price of 
1.82 kr. pr. kWh (Dong Energy), means that the annual savings for the church could be almost 34,000 kr. 
The price of insulation of one dome is informed by the mason, Tore Bredtoft, to be approximately 25,000 
kr., including craftsmanship and materials. Given the fact that Annisse Church has 12 domes distributed in 
4 vaults, the total price of insulating these would be 305,000 kr. This is without taking into account bulk 
discounts, surplus materials, time savings as the crew becomes more experienced and effective with the 
material, and the fact that 4 domes are smaller than the rest. Disregarding increasing energy prices, the 
investment of thermal insulation with the lime based insulation plaster on the 12 domes would be 
financially met in less than 10 years. 
14.9 Conservation aspects 
As already mentioned, lime was a traditional material used in original medieval church construction, and 
the mixing and applying with simple hand tools must be considered in accordance with traditional style. 
In addition, perlite is a natural material, thus the insulation plaster can be regarded natural and at least 
partially traditional.  By means of the geometric possibilities in the plaster and the adhesion to the vault, 
the attic also maintains the architectural expression of the domes as seen in Figure 14.9-1.  
 
 
Figure 14.9-1: Pictures of vault in Annisse Church before (left) and after insulation with the lime based insulation 
plaster. 
Medieval churches are an important and attractive part of Denmark’s cultural heritage. It is therefore vital 
to their existence to maintain “life” in them. Despite the relatively small alterations implemented in the 
form of heating systems, insulation etc., the cultural heritage and history will be kept alive by those who 
use them. It is therefore necessary to develop and maintain a certain level of comfort in these all 
important Danish medieval churches – for the purpose of keeping them “alive”.

15 Conclusion 
Considering the thermal insulation capabilities of lime based insulation plaster with perlite aggregate, the 
thermal conductivity was found to be approximately twice as high as the value for mineral wool. However, 
theoretical calculations based on a 10 cm layer of the insulation plaster indicate substantial energy savings to be 
gained by the insulation of church vaults with the insulation plaster. Experiments have shown that a further 
reduction of thermal conductivity in the insulation plaster can be achieved by addition of Scotchlite S22 to the 
binding agent of lime.  
The plaster showed good qualities with regard to moisture management, in relation to both liquid and vapour. 
Simulations with liquid loads showed, that water was not transported to the brick layer, but likely distributed in 
the insulation plaster, as the capillary suction is higher in the plaster relative to brick. The insulation plaster was 
also found to be very vapour permeable – it has approximately the same water vapour permeability as brick, 
though with a lower water vapour resistance factor. This ensures vapour diffusion from the high vapour pressure 
in the church itself to the attic. Measurements in Annisse Church suggest a fine balance between the vapour 
pressures inside the church nave and in the attic, and the vapour pressure in the insulation plaster lies well 
between the two. Mineral wool is non-hygroscopic and is unable to transport liquid water, it is however very 
vapour permeable. Simulations of a vault with insulated with mineral wool and additional liquid loads did not 
reveal catastrophic wetness in the brick vault under the mineral wool. The measurements in Annisse Church 
furthermore do not suggest any condensation in the construction of the insulated vault. The experimental drying 
process of saturated specimens progressed well, and the drying phases described by Krischer (Nielsen A. F., 
1974) could be somewhat established.  
A higher capillary number and water vapour permeability, as well as a lower thermal conductivity was found in 
specimens of Rødvig slaked lime when compared to those of Natural Hydraulic Lime. The Natural Hydraulic 
Lime showed slightly better results with regard to compressive strength in previously executed experiments, but 
this was to be expected from the hydraulic lime. The fact that Rødvig slaked lime showed better results for most 
investigated parameters corresponds well to the use of the material in historical buildings, although the 
differences between the types is not prominent. The experiment regarding rupture energy showed good results 
compared to both Bateig limestone and aerated concrete. In addition, the test field of insulation on part of a vault 
in Annisse Church has been subjected to people loads without fracturing. Cases of mineral wool insulation, have 
previously showed damage to the mortar layer above the soft mineral wool, damaging the insulation.  
In regard to church vault insulation, the lime based plaster, which consists of only natural, inorganic products, is 
malleable, provides the possibility for the insulation to distribute evenly on the double curvature of the domes 
with good adhesion. The plaster can be mixed and applied by hand, which is definitely in keeping with 
traditional methods. And the binding agent of lime is certainly traditional, as it has been used in the construction 
of churches in Denmark since medieval times.

Reset writer: 
 
The writer is turned on by the wheel marked 
by the yellow surface. When on, “chart” is 
displayed. 
 
The wheel marked with the blue circle is 
adjusted so the writer reaches 50%. Turn up 
the speed by the wheel in the red circle, and 
the writer should launch to the right. Turn the 
speed back down to 0.1 mm/min. 
 
The reference value is set to 0 V by turning 
the wheel marked by the green circle. 
 
The writer is set to 0% by means of the 
wheel in the blue circle. 
 
Lastly the reference value is set to an 
appropriate value. 
 
 
The signal in the thermopile should be  approximately (3.86 ∙ t) / λ [mV]1, where t is the specimen thickness [m] 
and λ is the expected range of the thermal conductivity [W/m⋅K]. The thickness of the test specimen is 30mm, 
and previous studies of the thermal conductivity leads to believe that the range of voltage over the 
thermocouple is approximately 0-2mV. Thus in this case, the reference value is set to 2 mV. 
Insertion of a test specimen 
 
On the hot plate on the bottom of the equipment, a  foam layer is laid out.  
 
The five thermocouples and the temperature sensor are taped to the bottom surface of the specimen, 
within the middle area of 200x200mm. 
 
The specimen is placed on the hot plate. 
 
The other side of the thermocouples and the temperature sensor are taped to the top surface (Picture A) 
 
A foam layer is put on top of the specimens. (Picture B) 
 
The sides of the specimen are insulated as good as possible. (Picture C) 
 
The cooling plate is put on top of the specimen and also (Picture C and D)
