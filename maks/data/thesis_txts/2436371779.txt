1. Introduction 
1.1 
Background  
1.1.1 Nearly Zero-Energy targets  
Within the building sector, improving the energy performance and reducing the energy consumption 
have been a priority sector in the European Union (EU) energy policy as a part of the strategy for 
reducing the use of fossil fuels. Through a recast of the Energy Performance of Building Directive 
(EPBD) in 2010 issued by the European Parliament and the Council of the European Union, it is stated 
that by the end of 2020 all new buildings are to be designed and constructed to consume “nearly zero” 
energy (EU, 2010). The recast of the EPBD should be accomplished without compromising the essential 
human needs for a healthy, comfortable indoor environment.  
 
In Denmark, the government has established a green growth economy policy, presenting their goal of 
producing all their electricity and heat supply by using only renewable energy sources by 2035. By 
2050 the goal is to have a 100 percent fossil-free energy and transport system (Danish Ministry of 
Climate, Energy and Building, 2011). This shall be achieved by improving and combining a cleaner 
energy supply and reduction in energy consumption. Knowing that the building energy consumption 
within the European Union represents 40 percent of the total energy consumption, the development of 
buildings to consume very low energy use is highly prioritized (Buildings, u.d.). As a tool to reach these 
targets, the Danish Building Regulations have implemented the “Energy class 2020”, which contains 
specific requirements for maximum energy consumption in nearly zero-energy buildings (NZEB). All 
new buildings in Denmark are to comply with these requirements from year 2020.  For residential 
buildings to meet the low-energy class, the annual primary energy usage shall not exceed 20 kWh/m2 
(BR, 2018). This shall cover for domestic hot water, space heating and cooling, as well as electricity for 
lightning, pumps and ventilation fans. 
 
The design and construction of new buildings, including the energy consumption of 20 kWh/m2, should 
have high quality solutions for windows and daylight, and provide for a healthy and comfortable indoor 
environment. These requirements will require a strong need for research in cost-effective energy 
saving solutions, which follow the future energy prices, discount rates and local energy production 
systems. The implementation of a cost-optimal NZEB building should achieve a very low energy use 
building with indoor conditions meeting the human needs and promote well-being.  
 
1.1.2 Development of a Design Guideline   
The design of a building is a complicated process, involving a large number of parameters where large 
number of these parameters having strong interfaces. Implementation of a NZEB will not only involve 
==PAGE:11==
2 
 
reduction of the overall energy consumption of the house and introduction of renewable energy to 
cover the energy use of the household. The design process will increase the complexity, where the 
balance between a large number of design parameters and their interfaces will have to be established 
and controlled. A sustainable building should include the users’ quality household parameters, i.e. good 
daylight conditions, spatial experiences, functionality, and comfortable indoor environment regarding 
light, air, temperatures and acoustics. Providing the right settings to its users, the building will need to 
follow additional strict requirements regarding energy and indoor environment, without compromising 
aspect of quality. For future design of a nearly zero energy house, it will be important to focus on 
holistic solutions. 
 
1.2 
Problem statement   
Analyzing existing guidelines for designing optimized nearly zero energy building (ONZEB) and passive 
houses are presenting a clear and specific pathway on how to achieve the goals for improvement of 
energy performance and reducing the energy consumption. These design guidelines are developed to 
follow the current standards, and as a consequence thereof they may not be suitable for and fit for 
future improvements within the building sector. In the future, there may be concern that these 
guidelines can cause design of buildings being made on elder, outdated building requirements, even 
though improvements of the building standards and requirements may have been established. 
Developing a guideline with possibilities to implement newly updated strategies and standards should 
therefore be developed. There is a need for an open guideline, where also economical optimizations are 
implemented. 
 
1.3 
Aim  
The aim of this master thesis is to provide architects, engineers and builders with a guideline for 
designing nearly zero-energy type-house buildings (NZEB). The achievement will be consisting of 
development of a design guideline showing how to design a cost-optimal, low energy single family 
house with good indoor environment. The guideline will focus on how to explore all the available 
options and find an optimal combination of the needed elements to build a single-family house.  
 
1.4 
Scope 
The scope of the development of the guideline will consist of designing a new nearly zero energy 
building, limited to low-rise single-family buildings. All the research and investigations will be 
evaluated from a Danish perspective, using Danish climate and following the Danish building 
regulations. Although, the result of the guideline is considered to be feasible for all countries, using 
Denmark as reference. The investigations will be implemented on selected crucial design parameters

not be considered in the research, such as acoustic environment.

2. State of the art  
This chapter is containing a review of the state of the art, existing guidelines on how to design NZEB and 
passive houses. The purpose for the review is to highlight any setbacks of using the existing guidelines, 
which form the base for developing the new guideline.   
 
2.1 
Guideline for Nearly zero energy house  
The NZEB goal for a new construction building following the requirements from year 2020, can be 
achieved by a fully integrated process involving several specialists such as engineers, building 
constructors, architects etc.  The composition of the design team should be in interaction from the start 
until the end of the project though all iterative steps. Implementation of NZEBs is involving multiple 
aspects requiring efficient use of resources for determination of the relationships between the building 
and its systems, surroundings and occupants. Working toward designing a NZEB construction project, 
guidelines are being made to assist the design teams to follow the right path. New Building Institute 
(NBI) List of ZE Buildings is including a wide-range of mainstream building and ownership types that 
reflect a universal trend toward ZE adoption. 
 
NZEB Parameter Considerations  
The NBI guide is presenting a number of parameters to be considered for a NZEB during the design and 
construction phases. Evaluation of the climate is required, with reference to utilization of existing 
resources, taking advantage of free daylighting, cooling and other appropriate passive strategies. In 
addition to this, taking the advantages of the building orientation is an important parameter needed to 
be set during the early phases of the project. If possible, extending the building along an East-West axis 
is a good strategy for improvement energy loads, such as lighting and heat gain.  
 
Typical elements to be used for a NZEB will be ultra-low energy use, energy demand stability and 
renewable energy. Increasing of energy efficiency or generation of energy on site is case-specific and 
will vary with the capability of a specific building. This is conducted by reducing the energy 
consumption in connection with the availability of renewable energy resources at a specific site.  
Potential internal energy storage, electric equipment efficiency and enable reduction of grid impacts 
should also be considered.  
 
According to the NBI guideline, an important aspect for designing a NZEB is the building envelope 
composition in order to achieve a compact building with efficient thermal protection, and to manage 
the infiltration loads. The level of building envelope performances for NZEB has increased considering 
the given requirements.  
==PAGE:15==
6 
 
 
Further developments will be to evaluate the building services. There should be investigations 
conducted on the occupants and use patterns in order to ensure the electrical and mechanical 
equipment are correctly sized for the building and the intended use. Representing 30-50% of the 
energy load, these aspects should be carefully evaluated and properly managed. By reducing the loads, 
smaller mechanical systems can be designed and installed, which again will reduce capital and 
operation costs. Through provision of high-performance equipment in the building together with 
reduction of the loads, the remaining energy load may be covered by renewable energy (NBI, 2017).  
 
Evaluation of Guideline 
The guideline provided by the NBI is containing a broad presentation of how to design a NZEB. NBI is 
mostly focusing on the different team and groups being involved in the building project and is 
encouraging good communication and close cooperation throughout the project. There is limited 
information on how the process should be conducted in each phases of the project, mainly focusing on 
of what should be considered during the design phases. The guideline has a significant focus on the 
building management. 
 
2.2 
Guideline for Passive House  
In order to comply with the European Union goals of NZEB by reducing the energy consumption, 
passive design strategies will contribute to improvement of the interior comfort conditions, increasing 
the energy efficiency in buildings, and reducing overall energy consumption (NBI, 2017). The Passive 
House Institute (PHI) has played a crucial role in the development of the Passive House Concept and has 
actively worked through the International Passive House Association (iPHA) promoting the Passive 
House Standard worldwide, and presenting specified Passive House Guidelines that shall be followed to 
be considered as a Passive House, such as:   
 
Building envelope  
All edges, corners, connections and penetrations shall be identified, planned and designed with the 
intention that thermal bridges shall be avoided or reduced. Thermal bridges considered to be inevitable 
shall be minimized as far as possible. All opaque building components of the exterior envelope of the 
house shall be subject to high quality insulation. For most cool-temperate climates, this means a heat 
transfer coefficient (U-value) of 0.15 W/m²K (i.e. 0.15 watt per degree of temperature difference per 
square meter of exterior surface) shall be taken as the maximum accepted heat transfer. For single 
family homes, the U-value should be considered to be kept below 0.10 W/m²K (iPHA, u.d.). 
 
==PAGE:16==
7 
 
In terms of airtightness, a maximum of 0.6 air changes per hour with a pressure differential at 50 Pascal 
pressure limit shall be verified through an on-site pressure test. This is to ensure favorable ventilation 
and temperatures whilst preventing moisture damages (iPHA, u.d.).  
 
Window Performance  
Passive house windows shall be well insulated with a U-value of 0.80 W/m²K or less of the entire 
window, i.e. glazing and frame. Glazing of the window should have a high solar transmittance (g-value), 
minimum 50% to ensure net heat gain during the cold period of the year.  The windows shall be 
designed as airtight, and the spacers in the glass seal edge shall be thermically separated (iPHA, u.d.).  
 
The orientation and shading of passive house windows will be essential for this type of buildings. The 
windows shall be designed to have the largest sizes facing south providing the maximum amount of 
solar energy into the house. The frame of the windows should be minimized to ensure maximum 
glazing area and heat transfer. To avoid or reduce possible overheating in the house, the windows 
facing East and West are recommended to be equipped with shades to avoid this matter (iPHA, u.d.).  
 
Energy and Indoor Environment  
In terms of the Space Heat Energy Demand, the passive house guidelines suggest this to be no more 
than 15 kWh per square meter for the net living space per year or 10 W per square meter peak demand. 
Considering the Space Cooling Energy Demand for climate where active cooling is needed, the 
requirements will be almost similar to the heat demand requirements (iPHA, u.d.).  
 
Regarding the thermal comfort, specified temperature requirements will have to be met for all living 
areas during winter as well as in summer periods.  The temperature shall not exceed above 25 °C with 
not more than 10 % of the hours in a given year for a complete overview of general quality 
requirements (iPHA, u.d.).  
 
Ventilation system for the Passive house shall have an efficient heat recovery ventilation to provide 
good indoor air quality and saving energy. In Passive House, at least 75% of the heat from the exhaust 
air shall be recovered and transferred to the fresh air through a heat exchanger (iPHA, u.d.). 
 
The Renewable Primary Energy Demand (PER, according to PHI method), is requiring the total energy 
to be used for all domestic applications, such as heating, hot water and domestic electricity, shall not 
exceed 60 kWh per square meter of treated floor area per year for Passive House Classic (iPHA, u.d.).

Evaluation of Guideline 
The guideline provided by the PHI has shown to have some differences compared to the NZEB 
approach. The given requirements are also not updated to the current requirements for Building Class 
2020, such as the heat recovery from ventilation system now being updated to be at least at 85 %, and 
not 75% energy recovery. Compared with the NZEB, there are differences in the window design 
requirements related to both size and orientation. Passive house design for windows is requiring large 
windows located in the south facades for solar heat gain and small windows facing north. Considering 
NZEB, it is recommended a glazing-to-floor ratio to design the window sizes to ensure good thermal 
comfort in all of the rooms. It should be noted that Passive House is using the sun primarily for heating 
the building.

3. Methodology  
This chapter is describing the different methods, performance parameters and building regulations 
being used for the design of the NZEB.  The methods applied is used throughout the Thesis as tools for 
ease the decision making. Performance parameters and criteria is applied for the evaluation of energy 
and indoor environment. This chapter will act as reference points for the further process.  
 
3.1 
Method for Evaluating and Decision-Making for Optimal Design  
The design process can be decomposed into four general phases:  
 
 Identification and clarification of main parameters and demands 
 Conceptual design 
 Embodiment design 
 Detail design  
 
As a part of this design guideline, a decision-making method is implemented in the conceptual design 
phase, assisting the selection of the optimal design solution. This part of the study will present 
discussions and evaluations on the basis of essential evaluation factors, using the decision-making 
method, Choosing by Advantage (CBA).   
 
Establishment of the floorplan for the actual building, the process can be further proceed through 
investigations and selection of the available options of materials and elements needed to achieve a 
NZEB. At this stage of the process, a structured method of evaluating and selection would be beneficial. 
A specific decision-making method is introduced, being adapted in this guideline. In the research for 
selecting the most optimal design for the type-house, two structure decision-making methods is 
implemented for three essential building components:  
 
 Constructional Design  
 Design of Windows 
 
3.1.1 Choosing by Advantages  
The structured decision-making method for selecting an optimal design solution is the use of Choosing 
by Advantages (CBA), an approach created by Jim Suhr (Suhr, 1999). The principle of the method will be 
to assist people to reach objective decisions when having to compare mutually exclusive solutions 
(Domelen, 2009). This is considered as an important tool, focusing on the relative advantages of the 
alternatives and do not include the disadvantages, with is common with other methods. In addition, 
==PAGE:21==
12 
 
CBA will improve the building project by compromising between decision makers and presenting 
alternatives in the context of the decision.  The method is based on introducing the subjective part of 
the decision followed by differentiating the alternatives by summarizing their advantages and at the 
end process a weighting of the importance of the advantages.  
 
Building projects are complex systems that shall be evaluated based on multiple performance criteria 
including, but not limited to:  
 
 Energy consumption 
 Indoor environment performance  
 Construction processes 
 Building installations 
 Cost  
 
These criteria are often competing, in the mean of aiming for an optimal design within a particular 
performance criterion may result in evaluations in sub-optimal performance with respect to other 
criteria. Evaluating the building system with respect to determination of how the building shall be built 
will be assessed through several evaluation factors, such as construction process, quality etc. 
Identifying an optimal design solution will be a difficult decision, knowing the number of possible 
alternatives and the complex interactions existing between many designs variables, for example for the 
building envelope all the different compositions of materials, insulation types etc.  
 
As a clarification to the methods used in this analysis, definition of three important words will be as 
follows:  
 
 Alterative, is a possible decision for the building project. The CBA analysis will assist in deciding 
between numbers of mutually choices, which are considered as alternatives. 
 Factor is a container for criteria, advantages, importance and other types of data. Different 
factors playing a role to in the decisions, will have to be selected by the decision-maker. 
Deciding on which factors will be involved in the evaluation needs to be carefully be thought 
through. Failure in doing so may cause in a non-optimal decision.  
 Criterion is the decision rule or guideline established by the decision-maker. It will be 
established a target for each factor.

3.2 
Requirements to Energy Use and Indoor Environment  
The main variables defining the energy consumption during the development of a nearly zero energy 
house in Denmark will be the composition of the building envelope and the requirements for energy 
consumption. Parameters involving the comfort and indoor environment are considered to be 
separately performance parameters, being evaluated in accordance with their own design criteria. The 
target for energy savings will be achieved when these parameters meet the requirements provided by 
the regulatory bodies.  
 
3.2.1 Energy Use  
For maximum energy usage for residential buildings, The Danish Building Regulations has specified a 
fixed criterion which will apply for new houses of any size, which will come into force from 2020. The 
requirement is stating that the annual primary energy consumption in a building shall not exceed 20 
kWh per m2 gross floor area. The primary energy consumption shall cover for space heating, cooling, 
domestic hot water, and power consumption for pumps and ventilation fans. The total energy 
consumption in a defined building will be the sum of all energy consumptions on a yearly basis, 
multiplied by a fixed primary energy factor of 0.6 for District Heating, and a factor of 1.8 for electric 
consumers such as ventilation system and additional cooling devices (BR, 2018).  
 
Individual contributions can be calculated as follows (BR, 2018):  
 
Hot Water: By definition, hot water will have a yearly energy consumption of 13 kWh/m2 before 
converted to primary energy. Hence, the primary energy use will be: 13kWh/m2 x 0.6 
 
Space Heating: The heating demand will be calculated through IDA ICE simulation, as this simulation 
tool will be used throughout the thesis. The primary energy will be multiplied by the primary energy 
factor of 0.6, as for hot water. 
There will be different versions of criterions to be used for heating depending on where the research is 
targeting, the whole house, a section of a house or a specific room. For this design development 
research, the investigation will cover for the total building.  
3.2.2 Indoor Environment  
As quantitative design criteria for the comfortable indoor environment for a NZEB, the Danish building 
regulations have issued a statement for a specific energy framework in order to achieve best possible 
condition at the lowest possible energy use. This will involve contribution from heating, cooling and 
ventilation. Evaluating the individual contributions, the measure of indoor environmental energy 
==PAGE:23==
14 
 
consumption in the design guideline will be depending on temperature and air quality. The design 
guideline will ensure the building being designed with a healthy indoor environment taking both 
summer and winter conditions into account. This is accomplished by following the requirements for 
thermal indoor environment for future residential building.  
 
Thermal Indoor Environment  
To achieve reduction of the energy consumption, the thermal comfort will need to be monitored in 
accordance with the new building regulations.  Between spring and autumn, the indoor temperature 
normally will increase in Denmark, this due to the increased heat from solar radiation. During this 
season, venting through fresh air may be necessary to meet a desirable indoor temperature. During the 
colder period in Denmark there is an increase of heating demand, which the internal heat loads are 
unable to cover for. The indoor temperature request will vary with the different seasons as well as 
individual human desires, but normally additional heating have to be introduced.  
 
With reference to the Danish Building Regulations, it is stated that the excess temperature should be 
verified according energy class 2020. The design criterion for indoor temperature should be between 
20 to 27°C, with possibilities for temperature reduction in bedrooms. The temperature should not 
exceed 27°C for more than 100 hours per year and undercooling below 20°C for more than 40 hours 
per year following the requirement from DS EN 15251, Class II (Standard, 2007). 
 
Atmospheric Indoor Environment  
For NZEB, the quality performance of the ventilation system is an important part, both regarding the 
building energy demand and measurement of the indoor environment. Therefore, the choice of 
ventilation rate and control strategy is crucial when determining comfort and a favorable indoor 
environment. Minimum requirements for the ventilation rate are stated in the Danish Building 
Regulations. Adequate air quality shall be provided for all individual rooms, and it is assumed that 
average air quality shall be monitored in some rooms to verify the performance of the ventilation 
system.  The quality of the indoor air should be adapted for the number of hours the individual rooms 
are to be occupied.  The Danish Building Regulations have set up the following requirements for 
ventilation in houses, as seen in Table 2 below:

3.3 
Window Analysis 
To provide good and varied daylight conditions in buildings it is essential to create an optimal condition 
in term of comfort and energy by controlling daylight, direct sunlight, and passive heat achievements. 
The building regulations are defining the minimum requirements for daylight openings in buildings. 
 
3.3.1 Daylight  
The challenge with implementation of large daylight openings is that they may introduce substantial 
overheating and glare. In order to control these undesired factors, the design process should formulate 
a daylight strategy to be executed in the early design phase, taking both qualitative and quantitative 
aspects of daylight into account. The energy regulating framework from “Class 2020” for designing 
residential buildings are predicting primary rooms to have a minimum glazing-to-floor ratio of 15% in 
order to ensure optimal daylighting, this to ensure the inhabitable rooms and kitchen being provided 
with sufficient daylighting. Calculations of the daylight factor (DF), different conditions are to be 
considered, such as the design of the windows, the light transmittance of the pane, the room layout and 
the surroundings. The requirements for daylight have to be viewed in the context of the general health 
aspects of daylight. The quantity of daylight also affects the energy consumption for electric lighting 
(BR, 2018). Given the requirement of glazing-to-floor ratio of minimum 15% will not provide sufficient 
daylight factor of 2%, the room will need a glazing-to-floor ratio of 20%, in order to meet the DF 
requirement. 
 
3.3.2 Glazing Properties 
Description of glazing properties for window design can be found through the use of Pilkington 
Spectrum program, which giving the possibilities for combining a large number of different glass and 
cavities of their Pilkington glass products. The program will calculate the U-value of the product being 
combined together, following the European Standards: EN673 concerning the calculation of U-value 
and EN410 concerning the solar and optical performance.  
 
3.3.3 Cost of Conserved Energy, CCE

Conserved Energy. The approach is including a calculation of the marginal energy saving price for floor, 
exterior wall and roof, providing an economical overview when adding more thickness of a given 
insulation in an envelope component.  This way, it will be possible to choose a final solution that are 
balanced between each envelope component in terms of insulation thickness within a given boundary,

3.4 
Simulation Calculations of Energy use and Indoor Environment  
IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA ICE) is a tool for simulation of energy consumption in a specific 
building, indoor air quality and thermal comfort. In IDA ICE, fenestration systems may be included in 
the different envelope parts, such as opening, leaks, doors and windows. I addition to this, different 
heating, cooling, ventilation and lighting system can be attached to the rooms or zones. Heat sources of 
the building will involve several parameters depending on what is included in the building. These 
sources may involve:  
 
 Heating/cooling devices  
 Air handling unit(s)  
 Envelope 
 Direct sun through windows  
 Leaks and thermal bridges  
 Internal thermal mass, occupants, lights and equipment.  
 
In order to ensure a realistic result from the simulations of the building, all input data specified above 
should be implemented in the tool.  
 
IDA ICE simulation tool will be used for research and evaluation of the designed building performance 
throughout the design guideline to ensure an optimized result.  For this research, the simulation tool is 
used to simulate all the main parameters influencing the building performance and energy 
consumption. To be able to provide more realistic parameters, a weather file has been imported into 
IDA ICE for the location of Copenhagen, as the Danish perspective being used as reference.

4. Architectural Design  
This chapter will provide a general guideline for the process of the architectural design of the building, 
with evaluations of the environment and certain human purposes. It will focus on meeting the different 
needs from all the involved parties, as well as looking into the site opportunities such as energy 
availability, microclimate and orientation of the rooms. It shall conclude with a final example floorplan, 
using this Architectural design guideline. 
 
4.1 
Design Overview 
To achieve a holistic zero energy building, it is essential to establish a good overview of the construction 
project. As a commencement of the design process, a framework is established. This section will present 
actual elements which should be investigated and analyzed prior to initializing the design of the type-
houses. 
 
4.1.1 Microclimate  
Through investigation of the area where the NZEB will be erected, qualities and challenges related to 
the microclimate shall be identified. Analyzing the microclimate will be an important parameter 
through the design phases of the project.

This is ensuring the building to be well integrated in its local context, providing optimal use of passive 
means such as sun and wind (Anne Kirkegaard Bejder, 2014). Assuming the building will have low 
heating demands due to highly insulated building envelope, the need of passive solar heat will decrease. 
Hence, when working toward a NZEB there will be an important aspect to reach a balance between 
provisions of optimal daylighting and minimize the risk of overheating caused by passive solar heat. 
Here is the position of the sun during daylight hours through the different seasons emphasized. These 
==PAGE:28==

parameters are playing a major role in the decision-making. They will have impact on positioning the 
building at the actual site, the design process of the building itself, arrangement of internal functions, 
daylight conditions as well as utilization and limitation of the passive solar heat. 
 
4.1.2 Available Energy Sources 
Working toward a NZEB, the project group will have to consider the possibilities for energy supply 
depending on location of the site and also specific demands from the owner. It will be beneficial for the 
design of the building to establish an overview of the energy supply options before deciding which 
energy supply will be most suited for the building and the location.  
 
Evaluations of potential district energy supplies will not affect the exterior design of the building 
significantly, compared with implementation of solar energy. The latter will cause large demands to the 
overall geometry of the building, such as the roof surface and inclination, as well as the orientation and 
area of the façade. Other demands would be the position of the building at the site with regard to 
potential shade from other buildings, surrounding vegetation, etc. Selection of the energy supply for the 
building will need a number of considerations and it is also considered essential to investigate the 
premises in the view from the future residents. Furthermore, connection to the collective heat supply 
systems is considered to be mandatory, and specific framework regulations may also apply for the 
use/prohibition of energy supplies, for example for solar cells or solar panels (Anne Kirkegaard Bejder, 
2014). 
 
The most cost-effective solution can be achieved by bringing down the energy needed for the building 
through the design. Other economical parameters like subsidy schemes are politically determined, and 
district energy supply will be influenced by fluctuating energy prices. The in-building production of 
energy for internal consumption may be a safe choice in terms of energy price but will give increase of 
construction costs. Moreover, it will greatly influence the building design, and will require unpredicted 
maintenance over the operation period (Anne Kirkegaard Bejder, 2014). Hence, it is considered using 
district energy supply being the favorable solution cost-wise in most occasions. 
 
4.1.3 Energy Targets  
The energy targets will have to be clearly defined when starting a NZEB project. Specific energy goals 
for the building will provide information and be a guide for how the process should be handled 
throughout the project. The energy targets may differ, depending on which country the building shall be 
constructed for, and what kind of type-house that shall be designed regarding number of inhabitants.  
 
==PAGE:29==

The design of the NZEB type-house for this Thesis is based on location in Denmark and shall thereby 
follow the Danish Building Regulations “Energy class 2020” and fulfill the applicable Eurocode 
standards. The Danish Strategic Research Centre for Zero Energy Buildings is performing their 
activities based on the following definition of zero energy buildings, which will be the target for the 
NZEB type-houses (Anne Kirkegaard Bejder, 2014): 
 
Zero Energy Buildings (ZEB) are buildings designed with a low energy demand and that energy demand is 
covered by fossil free energy sources. It is thus based on an optimal combination of energy savings and 
supply of renewable energy from electrical, thermal and/or biogas networks or from on-site renewable 
energy systems.  
 
Zero Energy Buildings also have a very good indoor environment with respect to temperature, air quality, 
daylight and acoustics, as well as a high architectural quality and designed with respect for the user. 
 
The goal is to eliminate the problems of using fossil energy by changing to a fully fossil free energy system. 
Solutions should primarily be designed considering the long-term perspective, but solutions should also 
consider the transition of the existing energy system to the future energy system. The optimal solution will 
depend on the given context. 
 
4.1.4 Stakeholder Awareness  
To ensure a successfully development of a NZEB project, involvements of key participants being fully 
committed to a ZE vision and its goals are essential. This may be staff members, city manager, 
superintendents, architects, engineers or committed individuals. Participants with the correct 
commitments will generally provide awareness, and discipline other stakeholders and decision-makers 
in gaining the full support for the NZEB project (NBI, 2017).  
 
The process of a NZEB type-house project will have a large number of interested parties or 
stakeholders. The most highly influenced parties are considered to be the inhabitants, which will be the 
future users of the buildings, and the contractors, which will build and make profit of their work. 
Having several stakeholders involved in the project may create a number of different and sometime 
contradicting interests, and it is important to identify these early in the project to ensure their 
requirements can be taken into consideration in order to comfort as many stakeholders as possible. It 
should be noted that another group of mandatory stakeholders will have to be considered in addition to 
the users and contractors, these are the municipalities and other authorities, both locally and 
nationally.

4.2 
Design Development  
After investigating the overall view of the project defined framework and key parameters, the team 
should start with the overall building design. The design of the building is decisive in the development 
of a NZEB through establishment of good living conditions, with focus of physical and psychological 
well-being of its users and fulfilling the intended zero energy requirements. 
 
4.2.1 Users  
As a part of developing the design of the NZEB type-houses, it is essential to investigate the future 
users of the building. This guideline is to develop low energy type-house, which are standardized 
design of a residential building to manufacture in large numbers. Investigations should be performed 
on the actual common household composition in order to be identify the demands.  The three most 
common household compositions in single-family building and farmhouses in Denmark are couples 
without children, couples with one and couples with two children or more (Danmarks Statistik, 
2017).

The composition of the future household of the NZEB is decisive for the floorplan and disposition of the 
overall design building to ensure the users physical and psychological well-being. Disposition of the 
building is including spatial organizing, as well as the design and dimensioning of the technical systems.   
 
The final design of the NZEB should be fully optimized with regard to the functional floor plan, 
proportioning and orientation of the rooms, utilization of daylight, as well as choice of materials. The

implementation of a simulation tool for investigation and evaluation of the total building performance

A NZEB will have highly insulated and airtight envelope, thus reducing the energy demand due to its

reduced as well. One of the challenges with low-energy buildings is the potential discomfort caused by 
overheating, hence due attention will have to be paid to this particular parameter during the design 
phase. By commencement of the design of the floorplan for the NZEB, analysis of the rooms, their size, 
purpose and interfaces is important for the design process and be beneficial for the physical and 
psychological welfare of the users.  
 
Conceptual Design   
By analyzing the different rooms and their purpose, and dividing them into public, private and semi-
private area is advantageous for organizing the floorplan and provide a successful and comfortable 
design space. Public rooms are usually defined as the kitchen, dining- and living rooms. These rooms 
are often situated in the less private part of the building, which in this case is in the southern part. The 
public rooms, such as the living room, are designed to be open, inviting and host as a social space for the 
users where they can enjoy the afternoon sunlight. As for the kitchen, this is also preferred open, social 
and is often interconnected with the living spaces, as being more frequently used compared to the 
private rooms. Semi-private rooms are assumed to be the bathrooms and technical room, as being 
considered to be more private than the usual public rooms. The semi-private spaces will also be used by 
visitors but are secluded from the living spaces providing more privacy. Private rooms, such as the 
bedrooms, are sleeping or resting areas where potential guests are normally not intended to be present.

Orientation  
The two children bedrooms and the master bedroom have all been located in the North. The master

has been established for separation of the children bedrooms from the public areas to provide 
increased privacy.

 Building Envelope, evaluation of building structure and materials   
 
When evaluating the building system, deciding on how the building shall be built and the building

CBA method, which can be different for the two evaluation analyses in the construction design. To 
provide an overview over the selected factors and alternatives being evaluated, a scheme is established. 
At the end of each analysis, a discussion is carried out, resulting in an optimal decision of a design.  
 
It should be noted that the two analyses are dependent on each other. When the project is deciding on

5.1.1 Construction Process   
Number of material suppliers  
An important factor related to location and transportation will be the project suppliers. Increased 
number of suppliers and vendors will increase the complexity of the project related to arrival of the 
material at the worksite at the correct time (Legmpelos, 2013). Reliability of suppliers as well as 
transport distance is important factors affecting the progress and work efficiency on-site. Through the 
use of prefabricated components or volumes the number of suppliers is reduced, and the logistics and 
interfaces significantly reduced at the site.  Assuming a proper planning, coordination and less people 
involved at site, this should result in significant time saving. A such solution may probably result in a 
cost increase, but the evaluation of overall cost contra time saving will differ from site to site and 
difficult to predict.  
 
Location of material suppliers  
For on-site construction, the location of the suppliers can be seen as an important factor. Problems 
might be avoided by having suppliers close to the worksite, rather than having supplier far away where 
procurement and logistic may be more challenging. It can be assumed that delivery time and cost will 
increase with distance.  Considering prefabricated components or volumes, the distance is less likely to 
affect these factors. However, the longer the delivery distance, cost increases still apply for factory-built 
housing.  
 
Delivery time  
The delivery time and delivery reliability will significantly affect the on-site building system. Delays of 
delivery of components may as affect the overall progress due to interfacing activities.  Selection of 
suppliers should therefore be carefully evaluated up front, taking into account reliability and transport 
distances. Accurate planning with forecasting of deliveries will be important, especially for on-site 
building with numerous suppliers. For component/volume-built housing, these systems are not that 
vulnerable, but planning and delivery notifications should include a certain “float” to counter for 
potential delays at the prefabrication site.

A general issue to be considered is the accessibility at the worksite.  For on-site building, this is not 
considered to create significant challenges, as the components normally will be trucked in smaller 
volumes not requiring large space at the site surroundings. However, suitable space for unloading and 
storage should be considered and established.

5.1.2 Quality  
Evaluation of the overall quality has shown that prefabrication is the preferred solution. The fact that 
the manufacturing of components and volumes will be under controlled factory working conditions at 
the prefabrication plants, should in general significantly increase the quality of the product. During the 
construction of the product, the building contractor, architects and/or owner will have the possibility to 
inspect and approve the ordered products. This should enable errors and misalignments to be spotted 
and corrected prior to delivery to the building site.  It may also give possibilities for implementation of 
potential minor changes at an early stage without major consequences on delivery and progress. 
 
5.1.3 Weather/Environment Conditions  
The weather/environment condition may affect the project, depending on the location and time of the 
year. On-site building system might be significantly affected by these factors. Winter periods may result 
in requirements for temporary heating in cold areas. During the warm season the temperature impact 
may be more difficult to handle, resulting in certain construction activities might be postponed until 
more favorable weather is obtained.  Prefabrication options will not be that vulnerable to the weather 
conditions, there may be some concern during the installation processes related to surface ice at the 
foundation, and lifting restrictions during periods with strong wind (Legmpelos, 2013).  Prefabrication 
during controlled indoor conditions will give a preference to these options. However, all outdoor

5.1.4 Efficiency  
An uncertain, yet important factor will be the efficiency associated with on-site construction. Effeciency 
of the process is hard to measure, considering the workmanship at the worksite. For the prefabrication, 
it can be assumed that the factory is following a manufacturing system technique, resulting in a fairy 
high degree of efficiency.  
 
5.1.5 Design Flexibility  
There will always be a desire or even a need for flexibility during the design phase and also at a certain 
degree during the construction phase. The on-site construction option will allow design changes almost 
throughout the whole construction process due to the small components being assembled at the site, 
although this may result in minor delays. During the building project it is assumed that architects and 
owners would prefer some flexibility throughout the process. On the other hand, for the prefabrication 
option there will be a requirement for a complete design at the early stage prior to commencement of 
the manufacturing process. Any relevant changes should then be made in advance to avoid significant 
cost end schedule impact. Prefabrication will in general have no flexibility after the order has been set 
and materials ordered.  Minor modifications may be incorporated, pending of contractual and schedule 
conditions. Prefabricated components may however have more flexibility for design options during the 
early stages compared with prefabricated volumes, considering their fixed volume and size. 
 
An optional tool to ensure the design of the building is complete, which is newly presented to the

through the building project, using a specific Building Information Modeling (BIM) software. This 
software provides a 3D visual illustration of the building project before the construction is complete, 
with insight from virtual walkthroughs. This technology provide the building project an insight of the 
finishing project before the construction begins, ensure the design is fully understood (Ringland-
Johonson Construction, 2018).

5.2.2 Maintenance  
 The last evaluation factor will be related to the overall material maintenance, where the optimal design 
building should use materials and construction methods which will generate easy and cost-effective 
maintenance of the building over the operational life-time, without compromising the energy 
consumption and living comfort.  Generally, materials requiring high level of maintenance are likely to 
have lower initial capital costs, but may also have higher maintenance over the lifetime and some 
unpredicted environmental costs. The level of maintenance of building elements may also be influenced 
by the performance requirements of the Building Code, particularly with regard to durability and 
weather tightness.  
 
Wood in building construction has been used for centuries, especially in Scandinavia, and been proven 
to be a strong and durable material if designed and constructed in an efficient way. However, timber 
framing has several factors that will need to be taken into consideration.  Timber buildings are 
vulnerable against decay, mold, cracking and all types of termites and other insects. A disadvantage for 
this type of construction is that the material initially is not fire resistant and may need special treatment 
for fire-proofing.  
 
There is also a risk for condensation damages in timber frame constructions, this may occur at the 
surfaces or between the layers in the wall/roof constructions. This can be solved by a cavity wall 
construction, which allows water to run down the inner face of the exterior cladding. The walls should 
also incorporate a vapor barrier between the lining in the inner wall and the insulation in order to 
prevent vapor passing through. 
 
Evaluation is made on thermalite blocks, which is the most common residential construction material in 
Denmark today. Thermalite blocks can be produced with different densities, thus giving the building 
material different characteristics. It can be casted in any form, and in combination with the low density 
it will be possible to be assembled by hand and / or assisted by smaller lifting equipment. The 
aluminum powder will act like a kind of baking soda and causes the mixture to expand and filled with 
air hollows. Thermalite blocks will provide a good insulation due to the air hollows in the concrete, and 
will absorb and emit moisture and heat. The material is inorganic, making thermalite blocks allergy 
friendly material which will contribute to a comfortable indoor climate (Sørensen, 2008).

6. Design of Windows  
Selecting the appropriate window design is an important building element, contributing for both space-
heating/cooling and thermal comfort for a NZEB. This chapter is focusing on introducing an optional 
window design approach, explaining an iteration process of finding the right window size regarding the 
selected glazing properties for the building. This window design approach for this specific building 
project, is conducted through evaluation of the relationship between the room areas, window sizes, 
orientation and glazing properties of façade and the use of roof windows.  
 
The window properties will have a significant influence on the building performance related to space-
heating/cooling and thermal comfort. The design will have impact on the amount and distribution of 
incoming natural daylight into the rooms. Some windows will also have influence on the building’s 
energy consumption and the indoor environment. In order to achieve a high quality of the indoor 
environment for a NZEB, the windows is optimized to meet the building regulation requirements for 
energy, daylight and thermal comfort. This is executed by closely managing three main glazing 
parameters directing the window performance, as follows:  
 
 Heat loss Coefficient, U-value  
 Solar energy transmittance, g-value  
 Light Transmittance, LT  
 
The effect of these parameters on the Space Heating/Cooling Demand, Thermal Comfort and Cost of 
Conserved Energy, will be evaluated as the factors by implementation of IDA-ICE, using the designed 
floor plan with the final selected building envelope compositions.  
 
6.1 
Glazing Properties  
This thesis will focus on the three main window parameters. To be able to design the most optimal 
windows for the building project, it is important to have a clear understanding of the three main 
window parameters and their contribution to the window glazing. Only small changes in these 
parameters may provide significant contribution for achieving a NZEB, considering energy and indoor 
environment.  
 
6.1.1 Solar energy transmittance, g-value  
Solar energy transmittance, g-value, is measuring the amount of solar heat (infrared radiation) from the 
sunlight which can be permitted passing through the window and into the building. A theoretical range 
for the permitted g-value is set to between 0.0 - 1.0, given that a low g-value indicates a low percentage 
of the solar heat and a g-value of 1.0 representing no resistance, which means all the heat from the 
==PAGE:50==
41 
 
incoming sunlight passes through. Selection of the optimal g-value will also be dependent on the 
orientation of the building and the actual climate (Glazing Properties , u.d.).  
 
6.1.2 Heat Loss Coefficient, U-value  
Heat Loss Coefficient is measuring the amount of heat diffusion through the window. This parameter 
will also be affected by the floor, wall and roof design properties, but only the window properties will 
be considered in this chapter. The U-value measured will incorporate the whole window structure with 
the combination of glass and frame, although this parameter may also be calculated separately for each 
the window part. A low U-value indicates better insulating capacity of the window. As a guidance, a U-
value of 1.0 will provide a heat transfer of 1 Watt/square meter surface area for every degree difference 
between the outdoor temperature and the indoor temperature.  
 
6.1.3 Light Transmittance, LT  
The percentage of visible light that passing through the window or the glazing unit is named Visible 
Light Transmittance or Light Transmittance (LT). A closed wall without any fenestrations will have an 
LT of 0% and a full opening a LT of 100%. A consequence of allowing large percentage of light, may 
cause glare and overheating in the room. LT may be related to only the glazing unit or relative to the 
total window area, the glass and frame (U-value & g-value, u.d.).  
 
6.2 
Window Positioning relative to the Building Orientation  
The location of the windows in the NZEB is distributed as evenly as possible along all the façades in 
order to achieve optimal thermal comfort and daylight in all rooms. This will also include the use of roof 
windows to ensure all of the rooms are covered, following the requirement for using glazing-to-floor 
ratio of 20%, this will ensure all of the rooms to be provided with a DF of 2%. By evaluating different 
locations of the windows, the window final location at the building may contribute to achieve an overall 
positive net energy gain.  
 
This can be achieved by complex investigation and calculations of various combinations of window U-
value and g-value distributed on all the facades - and the roof if considered beneficial. The windows is 
investigated and evaluated with respect to the need for the rooms in the building, having different 
orientations. The approach is consisting of studying the window performance in each room in detail, 
considering the thermal comfort and evaluating the energy consumption for the space heating/cooling 
of the overall building.

6.2.1 Façade Window  
Façade openings in the building envelope is providing light and visual contact with the outdoor 
surroundings. These openings will, together with roof windows, be the main source for heat slipping 
out of the building, and the amount of light and heat passing into the building. The amount of light and 
heat passing into the different rooms of the building will be dependent on their location and orientation 
as well as the different room dimensions. The requirements for thermal comfort will differ between the 
different rooms, related to their different functions. Some of the rooms should be more prioritized as 
they will normally have more occupant hours. Typically, the bedrooms, requesting a cooler 
temperature during nighttime, or the kitchen and living room, where higher room temperatures and 
maximum natural daylight possibilities is desirable.  Evaluation of the energy performance of the 
windows by variations of the three main parameters will enable façade windows with optimal 
daylighting and thermal comfort, with minor compromises in space-heating and cooling demand.  
 
6.2.2 Roof Window  
For future NZEB, the use of roof windows will increase due to its beneficial properties. Roof windows 
can provide the building envelope twice as much daylighting compared to façade windows with the 
same window areas. The use of windows at the roof will provide more flexibility in the location, 
enabling provision of daylight to almost any room in the building. Roof windows are an efficient source 
for natural daylighting, which will be beneficial for future NZEB designed with living spaces in the 
center of the building. Design of various compact houses and buildings with large floor areas will be 
dependent on roof windows to follow the requirements for sufficient daylighting. Roof windows may 
provide improved performance in energy and thermal comfort for the NZEB by evaluation and 
balancing between the performance parameters such as the U-value, g-value and light transmittance. 
Implementation of roof windows will efficiently contribute the NZEB to achieve optimal daylighting for 
all rooms and provide a pleasant indoor environment.  
 
6.3 
Selection of the Optimal Windows  
During development of a NZEB, selection of the windows will be an essential part of the building design, 
especially for meeting specific building requirements, requiring precise evaluations.  
The current guidelines for applying large size window in south-orientated rooms and small size 
windows in north-orientated room may not be the optimal solution for future NZEB.  Consequently, this 
could cause inadequate daylight distribution for the north-oriented rooms and potential overheating in 
the south-orientated rooms. There is a need of an up-to-date guideline for window selections being 
more appropriate for new building conditions.

7. Design of Building Services  
The design of the building services is performed after the conceptual design of the building envelope, 
windows and essential design components and requirements have been settled. The building services 
for NZEB project may consist of many different sources and components. There are many new products 
on the marked subjected to continuous developments, resulting in more energy efficient systems and 
components. The target for selecting the energy supply system shall be an annual primary energy 
consumption in a building not exceeding 20 kWh per m2 (BR, 2018). This requirement can be analyzed 
through an evaluation process in IDA ICE.  
 
The previous chapters have been focusing on achieving the optimal building design and optimal indoor 
environment performance within a framework of very low energy demands. The design of the building 
system will determine how these energy demands may be covered and accommodated through 
different renewable energy sources.  
 
For the design of the building service system it is important to work out the best possible balance 
between energy saving and renewable energy production within the building and interaction with the 
external energy supply system in order to ensure the resources are applied and combined in an optimal 
way. The design of the building shall contribute to a reduction of the electricity demand, such as good 
daylighting, laundry spaces for drying, heat recovery from ventilation etc. Additional factors to ensure 
achievement of the annual primary energy usage will be the use of electricity saving appliances as well 
as the user behavior in the building.  
 
In general, there will be difficult to determine and obtain an optimal solution for this balance. 
Installations of the building service system will be depending on the available and appropriate energy 
sources, and be influenced by user demands, site location and not at least the budget for the project. 
Hereunder descriptions and evaluations of different building service systems available is presented.   
 
7.1 
Supply of Energy 
The energy demand will need to cover for the following consumers:  
 Heating and Cooling  
 Domestic Hot Water (DHW) 
 Electricity for Operation of Building (Ventilation, pumps, etc.) 
 Electricity for Appliances (lighting, household appliances, etc.) 
 
There is several options to obtain the necessary energy demand for the NZEB, such as: 
==PAGE:56==
47 
 
 
 Heated water from a district grid, in combination with electrical supply for appliances and 
lighting 
 Heated water from individual or group ground loops (geothermal heating), in combination with 
electrical supply for appliances and lighting 
 Electrical supply from a district grid 
 Electrical supply from internal source like solar panels in combination with battery package for 
storage of surplus energy, and with electrical supply from a grid as additional supply 
 
7.1.1 Water energy supply 
The water energy can by supplied from a district water supply grid, or through individually installed 
geothermal ground loop system.  The supply from a district water grid is considered as a simple and 
stable provider of heat energy with reasonable capital costs for installation. 
The supply of heated water from a ground loop system will require larger capital investments 
compared with supply from domestic grids. An advantage with ground loop system is that the water 
flow can be turned in warm periods and provide direct cooling and balancing of overheated spaces of 
the building through the lower ground temperature. Individual ground loops will also provide a stable 
delivery of energy, without fluctuations in market prices. Water energy supply as primary energy 
provider in Denmark will have a primary energy factor of 0.6 (BR, 2018). 
 
7.1.2 Electric energy supply 
Electric energy is commonly provided through a district grid system, which also can be combined with 
solar panels in combination with a battery system that can store surplus energy, but in cold and semi-
cold climates solar panels is not considered as a stable provider, there is a need for additional supply of 
electric energy from the grid. Such combined system is considered complicated and will need large 
capital investments. Electric energy supply is also accepted with a primary energy factor of 1.8 
according to the Danish Building Regulations (BR, 2018). 
 
7.1.3 Supply system Evaluation 
The use of water as primary system from a district grid will normally have the advantage due to simple 
supply and reasonable capital costs. Additional electric power will only be needed for lighting and 
pump/compressors.  
 
Where water supply from grid will not be possible, there will be 2 options: 
 
 Water from an individual ground loop, used as for grid supplied water. 
==PAGE:57==
48 
 
 Electric energy supply from a common district grid, where the energy can be transferred to heat 
through air-to-water heat pump or air-to-air heat pump. 
Both systems will have a similar primary energy factor as for water supplied form a grid system, but the 
capital investments will be higher. Water supply will use less energy to maintain a given temperature 
over a longer period, which will reduce the buildings carbon footprint relative to the use of electric 
systems. Consequently, water-based systems from a district grid is considered to be the preferable 
solution.  
 
7.2 
Heating and Cooling System Evaluation 
7.2.1 Energy Supply 
The primary energy supply for heating and cooling of the building can be made from the following main 
sources: 
 Water supply, either from district grids, or from individual/group ground loops 
 Electric supply, from district grids, or as combination of solar panels and district grids 
 
Heated water systems are considered as the most cost effective but will need additional supply of 
electric energy for auxiliaries like pumps and converters. Electric supply for heating and will require 
extensive converters for effective domestic distribution of heating/cooling, through either air or water. 
This is considered requiring higher capital costs, thus water supply is considered preferable also for 
domestic in-house heat distribution. 
 
7.2.2 Heating and Cooling Distribution  
After selecting the heating energy source, the design of the heating system for the building is initiated, 
with selection of heat distribution methods including a supply method for each building space. The type 
of heating system being selected may have impact on the design and construction of the room. The 
selection of internal heating system is important for the architecture, comfort, energy consumption and 
the capital and operational costs. Consequently, there is advantageous to evaluate the different options, 
their potentials and challenges in the early phases of the project. 
 
Floor Heating 
Floor heating, compared to conventional radiator systems, are active on larger surfaces of the building. 
The system is integrated into the constructed floor, such as the concrete slab foundation. A network of 
water tubing is installed a few centimeters under the concrete slab’s surface. The slabs temperature will 
increase through the heated water circling in the tubes, thus warming up the air in contact with the 
==PAGE:58==
49 
 
floor surface, giving a more comfortable temperature change in the room as the hot air and surrounding 
heated material will gradually blend into the air through convection as the warmer air ascend.  
 
Heat pumps 
When considering the heating system for the NZEB, there will be a need to determine the heating 
supply and the hot water distribution system through evaluations of the needed elements to enabling a 
functional distribution system. A heat pump may be implemented to provide a more effective use of the 
energy. 
 
There may be a need for heat pumps for: 
 Hot water heating, where the primary heat medium will need to further heated through an 
electric driven heat/compressor pump to lift the temperature for hot potable water and room 
heating (floor tubing) 
 Air heating where the outside air are heated through an electric driven converter pump for 
distribution of heated air into the room 
 
Both these systems may also be designed for cooling of the medium, acting for cooling down the 
indoor environment in the warmer periods. 
 
Floor Cooling   
Some of these heating distribution systems may also be designed for cooling of the medium, being able 
to change between heating and cooling. This can be provided by air heater/cooler converters, or 
through a water tube floor heating, which may also be used for cooling.  The latter is the most 
commonly known and applied heating distribution system in Denmark.  
 
 
7.3 
Ventilation  
Generally, there are two air flow strategies, natural venting or mechanical ventilation that will be 
implemented in a NZEB project. Provision of fresh air into the building is important to ensure a good 
indoor environment through high quality of indoor air and comfortable temperature. 
 
7.3.1 Venting 
The venting system in NZEB should ensure a good thermal indoor environment without large changes 
in temperature and uncomfortable airflow. There may be more use of venting outside the heating 
season compared to the heating seasons in order to provide good air quality. General supply of fresh air 
==PAGE:59==
50 
 
will need to have heat recovery according to the building regulations, and consequently have to be 
functioning together with a mechanical ventilation system. This can ensure provision of a balanced inlet 
and outlet flow through a heat exchanged venting for natural cooling in order to avoid overheating.  
 
The building will use the windows to ventilate the rooms to avoid overheating, indicating that the 
design of the ZEB windows will be essential. This is calculated through an iteration process, which is 
conducted with the determination of the optimal window and investigating the venting by observing 
the airflow passing in and out of the windows. Each room will have their individual maximum air flow, 
specified to 4 air changes per hour (4 h-1). The air flow will need to be calculated for each individual 
room, in order to adapt the partial opening of the different windows in the room through IDA ICE.  
 
The iterating process has confirmed the importance of the window design. A change in size of the 
window will have a significant influence on the amount of air flowing in and out. Consequently, 
monitoring of the maximum air flow through the window are important in case of the size will need to 
be modified to meet other design requirements, and new adjustments of the window openings will have 
to be established. Window design and the airflow will have to be adjusted to each other until reaching 
the limit of 100 hours of overheating (Standard, 2007).  
 
Mechanical ventilation 
Buildings that shall use mechanical ventilation will need detailed evaluation of locations, layout and 
sizing of ducts, silencers, aggregates etc. in the early phases. Efficiency of the ventilation system is 
dependent on different factors, such as: 
 
 Size and dimensioning of the system 
 Length of air ducts 
 Insulation of air ducts 
 Control system 
 Maintenance 
 User behavior  
 
 The efficiency and positions of fans and heat exchanger will also need to be evaluated. Selection of the 
mechanical ventilation system should include a heat recovery system, where the exhaust air can be 
used for heating the cold supply air through a heat exchanger.  
 
There are two types of vent duct layouts, common distribution ducts or individual ducts. Selection 
between the two different layouts will have influence on size of the ducts and location of the air 
==PAGE:60==
51 
 
silencers.  By evaluating the building service systems together with the room organization during the in 
floor-plan design, an optimum building service system layout can be achieved (Anne Kirkegaard Bejder, 
2014).  
 
Several options for mechanical systems are listed below (Mechanical ventilation of buildings, 2018):  
 
 A circulation system such as a ceiling fan, which will give internal air movement, but not 
provision of any fresh air. 
 A pressure system, where fresh outside air will be blown into the building by inlet fans, 
providing a higher internal pressure than the outside air. 
 A vacuum system, where consumed internal air is extracted from the building by an exhaust fan, 
providing a small under-pressure inside the building compared with the outside air. 
 A balanced system where both inlet and extract fans will be used, thus maintaining the internal 
air pressure at a level similar to the outside air, resulting in a reduction of air infiltration and 
draughts. 
 A local exhaust system where local sources of heat or contaminants will be extracted at their 
source, such as cooker hoods, fume cupboards and similar.

9. Analysis on Energy and Indoor Performance of Windows  
The analysis is working through an iteration process to find the optimal window for the building 
project. Throughout the process, glazing alternatives is applied, working with different glazing 
properties and glass units. Energy and thermal comfort regarding overheating hours is recorded, where 
the iteration is redirected to further investigations for new window proposals. The analysis will check 
the window performance by using different design alternatives.  
 
First part of this iteration process is analyzing the optimal windows with triple glazing including 
window frame. The reference building is used for this analysis, with the selected building envelope 
constructed material method, concrete slab for floors and CLT panels for wall and roof.  As noted in 
Section 6.3.1, the desired optimal window shall have the combination of a 20% glazing-to-floor area 
based on glazing with a LT of 75%. For this analysis, the iteration process is redesigning the glazing due 
to LT will be less than 75%.  For each change in LT, the glazing-to-floor area will change to ensure 
provision of sufficient daylighting for the building. The analysis is investigating and evaluating the 
energy and indoor environment performance of the house, and a comparison between triple- and 
quadruple glazing windows made. Further analysis is investigating the difference of having no window 
frame of the quadruple glazing, placing the glazing direct into the wall and only have a few windows 
which can be opened. Lastly a Cost Conserved Energy analysis (CCE) is performed to optimize the 
insulation thickness with the use of the three different window cases.  
 
Referring to the methodology, the choice of glass for glazing is found through the use of Pilkington 
Spectrum program, giving the possibility to combine a large number of different glass and cavities of 
their Pilkington glass products.

9.2 
Case 2 – Quadruple Window Glazing  
Window performance in the context of energy efficiency in buildings has shown to be an increasingly 
important component. Insulation glazing are still in continuous development to ensure achievement of 
future demands, such as development from double insulation glazing to triple, and now quadruple 
window glazing being the next step. A quadruple glazing window will consist of four glass panes, three 
spacer systems and three cavities. Application of quadruple glazing will provide a further decrease in 
heat transfer coefficient (U-value) of the glazing (Chmurny, 2016).  
 
As observed in the previous analysis, the triple glazing window will still give many hours of overheating 
in the south oriented spaces. For this iteration process, quadruple window glazing will be implemented 
for decreasing the U-value, in order to investigate any positive changes in the thermal comfort in the 
specific rooms. The quadruple glazing profile will have the same composition as the triple glazing 
profile from the first iteration. To be able to compare the results, the profiles will need to have the same 
glass functions.  
 
The quadruple glazing window with anti-condensation, illustrated in Table 29, will replace the anti-
condensation triple glazing window in the reference building. The LT value for the anti-condensation 
quadruple glazing has decreased with 10%, compared to the anti-condensation triple glazing, causing 
the required glazing area to increase. This will lead to resizing the total window size of all windows. The 
g-value for the new glazing is decreased to 0.47 and the U-vale to 0.4.

9.4 Discussion  
This chapter is discussing the four different cases of the Window Analysis, based on the results from the 
investigations related to thermal comfort, energy consumption and CCE-analysis. Discussing the 3 
different window design cases, where the effect of following 3 governing parameters are presented. 
 
The case studies were as follows:  
 
 Triple Glazing Window with combination of Anti-Condensation Solar Control Coating  
 Comparison of using Triple Glazing Window to Quadruple Glazing Window 
 Comparison of using Quadruple Glazing Window to Frameless Quadruple Glazing Window 
 
9.4.1 Triple Glazing with Solar Control Coating  
When investigating the triple glazing window, the anti-condensation window to be used in the northern 
rooms provided good thermal comfort for all of the rooms through the glass having a high LT value of 
71%, accomplishing the glazing-to-floor related to LT requirement, but the southern room showed 
many hours of overheating beyond the limit of 100 hours. Consequently, a window with solar control 
coating was selected. The solar control coated windows will have lower g-value leading to a lower 
overall LT value. Investigating the window performance for the living room and kitchen when both g-
value and LT had decreased, showed request for larger window size, provided a decrease in number of 
overheating hours. The hours decreased almost by 200 hours in the living room and 360 hours in the 
kitchen. This was caused by the difference of the g-value from 0.53 to a value of 0.32, also indicating 
that sufficient glazing was obtained through a decrease of LT of only 10%.  
 
Reviewing the CCE analysis of the Triple Glazing Window case, the target of obtaining a primarily 
supplied energy of 17 kWh/m2 was obtained by a decrease in the insulation thicknesses for all of the 
envelope components, this compared to the reference thickness of 0.6m. The different insulation 
thicknesses were balanced on a CCE-value of 1.15.  
 
9.4.2 Triple Glazing Window to Quadruple Glazing Window 
This section discusses the differences in window performance influencing the building between Triple 
and Quadruple Glazing window with frame. Initially, switching from triple glazed window to quadruple 
window would be expected to provide a more insulated building, due to having lower U-value and 
lower g-value. The results will therefore expect to show a lower energy consumption for the overall 
building.  
 
==PAGE:99==
90 
 
Referring to the quadruple glazing window used building, the window locations and number of 
windows being used will change when switching from triple to quadrupole glazing. Upgrading the triple 
glazing to quadruple glazing was performed with the same glass composition, ensuring correct 
evaluation of the analysis when comparing the two cases. By adding an extra glass, the LT percentage 
was reduced in both anti-condensation window and solar control coated window, this is reasonable 
when considering the extra layer would decrease the vision of the glazing. Additionally, the results in 
the analysis had to accomplish the required ratio of a 20% glazing-to-floor area based on glazing with 
LT property of 75%. Reviewing the differences of the glazing properties by upgrading the triple glazing 
to quadruple glazing having the same composition, these differences are found to be insignificant. As 
example, comparing the two solar control coated windows for the south oriented rooms, the U-value 
decreased by 0.2 whilst g-value decreased only by 0.02, and LT by 5%.   
 
The iteration process of using quadruple glazing window with solar control coating was expected to 
improve the thermal comfort of the rooms. The added glass layer consequently decreased the LT value, 
which lead to a larger required glazing area. The southern oriented rooms initially had a LT of 61%, 
indicating to having a large required glazing area, were reduced from 61% to 56%, requiring more of 
the wall areas to be used for window space. Evaluating the southern oriented room using manually 
controlled windows, the requirement for venting in the cooling season resulted in an increase in 
number of hours above 27 degrees, indicating the quadruple solar control coated window were not as 
efficient compared the triple glazed solar control coated window.  This may be due to the upgrade to 
quadruple glazing having the same composition only by adding an additional glass of the same type, 
resulting in a minor change in the g-value with a difference of 0.02, while a larger change in LT value of 
5%. A consequence of this were larger window size with inefficient g-value, causing more solar heat 
passing through an increased glazing size providing an increase in overheating hours in kitchen and 
living room. The increase in required glazing area for. Looking at the northern oriented rooms, the 
hours of overheating showed a minor increase, but still within the required limits.  Due to the 
inefficiency in the solar control coated quadruple glazing properties, increasing the number of 
overheating hours in south oriented rooms, caused the need of applying an Ideal cooler for quadruple 
glazing case. 
 
Evaluation of the energy demand after the ideal cooler being implemented is showing only insignificant 
increase in energy consumption, this indicating that the use of the large window sizes for venting is 
sufficient to meet the requirements. For the heating season, the overall building analysis results is 
presenting a large energy consumption for space heating, which were not expected. This may be due to 
the increase of glazing area covering most of the wall area, related to the reference thickness, has a U-
value of approximately 0.06 W/m2K. The used glazing’s covering most of the wall area, have a U-value 
==PAGE:100==

between 0.35-0.6 W/m2K, which indicates the insulation for the overall building have not decreased 
significally. During the heating season, the building has a high heat loss due to the large sized windows, 
which do not have sufficient insulation. Although, evaluating the low energy consumption in the cooling 
season, this will equalize for the large consumption during the heating season. The conclusion will be, 
looking at the difference of the two glazing types, that quadruple glazing will have lower energy 
consumption compared with triple glazing. For future investigation, this will have potential for 
providing improvements in the window development of window performance, but this will require 
comprehensive and detailed analysis. The main target will be to find a balance of the window 
performances during all seasons.  
 
The CCE analysis for the quadruple glazing showed a minor difference compared to the triple glazing

quadruple glazing window, also having a lower CCE value. Looking at the difference in thickness from 
the triple glazing case, the decrease in energy consumption will reduce the insulation thicknesses with 
approximately 50cm.  
 
9.4.3 Quadruple Glazing Window to Frameless Quadruple Glazing Window  
This section is discussing the difference in window performance influencing the building between 
Quadruple glazing window and Frameless Quadruple Glazing window. 
 
The objective of the third case was to investigate the window performance through implementing a 
frameless quadruple window in the building to in order to investigate the influence the new window 
will have on the overall building. It was assumed that the reduced U-value of only the spacer would 
provide some differences in both energy consumption and thermal comfort.  
 
The new windows, using the modified U-value of the frame were decreased due to only use of spacers, 
were implemented for the new analysis. The southern oriented rooms having larger areas with reduced 
number of windows, this to provide panorama vision and a more constant provision of light, which may 
be preferred in the southerly direction. The large window areas are assumed to be acceptable, knowing 
these windows will be closed at all time. The kitchen, originally using roof window for the quadruple 
glazing window case, were modified for the frameless case. They were relocated to the south facade to 
provide a larger window area similar to the windows in the living room. With the use of large window 
areas in the southerly direction may cause negative results regarding thermal comfort. For the northern 
oriented window, the sized remains the same.

Using the reduced U-value will result in a decrease in hours of overheating compares to the frame-used 
case. Comparing the frameless quadruple glazing window with the frame-used quadruple glazing 
window, the difference in the hours were minor when investigating only the northern oriented room 
were anti-condensation window is used. This seems reasonable due to the small change in the U-value 
of the frame, from a value of 2.040 W/m2K with frame to 1.996 W/m2K without frame. For the southern 
oriented rooms, the U-value of the frame changed from 2.04 W/m2K to a U-value of 1.879 W/m2K for 
only the spacer, resulting in a larger decrease in overheating hours for the kitchen, whilst for the living 
room the number of overheating hours increased. The reason may be due to the west located windows 
that are used for venting the living room during cooling season having a percentage opening of 49.3. 
This indicates that the large openings allow direct sunlight passing into the living room. The g-value 
during the cooling season for the west located glazing units can be assumed to be 1.0.  
 
The CCE analysis of the frameless case resulted in a reduced energy consumption, which were expected 
assuming the reduced U-value should influence positively on the overall insulation of the building. This 
indicates in a decrease in the insulation thickness for the individual envelope components, and 
therefore a lower CCE-value when the aim of supplied energy is 17 kWh/m2.

Conclusions 
The aim of the work presented in this Thesis will be how to develop a guideline for design of a Nearly 
Zero Energy building, taking the overall performance of the building into account. The aim will be to 
enable classifying the building as building class 2020, using the guidelines following the Building 
Requirement.  
 
The overall guideline is presenting a broad overview over the main building components involved, 
providing approaches for designing a Nearly Zero Energy Building, which were conducted throughout 
the thesis to present a proposal of an optimal design solution.   
 
Introduction of Choosing by Advantage, CBA¸ decision-making approach resulted in effective discussions 
followed by evaluations for selection of an optimal design. Further work will be to break down the 
selected factors, for each building part, in smaller sub-factors to investigate and evaluate at a more 
detailed and complex level to ensure making the correct decisions. Although, the number of factors and 
evaluation levels may vary when being implemented in the different building projects. The method will 
give the possibilities for including more people to cooperate in the decision-making process.  
 
Demonstrating the design guideline through an IDA ICE simulation, and implementing BR18 
requirements for energy use, thermal indoor environment and daylight conditions based on applicable 
building codes, standards and recommendations, resulted in being classified as building class 2020. For 
future work could be an analysis of the guideline to implement in other building regulations to ensure 
the openness of the guideline, enabling implementation of any requirements without any need for 
upgrading the guideline.  
 
A case study is performed to as a part of the guideline, providing an approach for selection of an optimal 
window design, providing a step by step guideline of an iteration process. The approach is being 
conducted to investigate the window performance in realistic design cases, using the reference building 
designed in chapter 5. Three cases were analyzed: 
 
 Triple glazing window,  
 Quadruple glazing window  
 Frameless quadruple glazing window  
 
Through investigations, the window size, type of glazing and window frame are influenced by the 
requirements for thermal indoor environment and daylight conditions. The optimization of windows 
==PAGE:103==
94 
 
using this method is showing that quite complex design challenges can be solved, where the uncertainty 
of not knowing how the selected design solution will influence the performance of the building.  
 
The optimization of the building design will be depending of a clear problem definition, which were in 
this thesis to design an optimal glazing window for the reference building. A conclusion for the overall 
thesis, of evaluating the guideline and the window analysis, it may be recommended to use the 
guideline for designing an optimized building design as a starting point having fewer relevant 
parameter variations, which will initiate to obtain a final solution.
