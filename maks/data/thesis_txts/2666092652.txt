Summary
The MrGurk illusion itself is a wierd illusion that you cant stop even if you
know it happens. Watching a recording of a someone speaking out the word
/VA/ but the sound of the recording is saying /BA/ the observer hears what
he sees. namely /VA/. Only by changing the visual perception the sound
changes. This is what is called the MrGurk eﬀect.
This happens because the mouth gesture of the talker, have a inﬂuence on
the phonetic percept. Speech perception is easier when the mouth gesture is
seen. This is especially good in a noisy environment. The Mrgurk eﬀect oc-
cures when the speech sound is presented simultaneously with a incongruent
articulatory mouth gesture.
The MrGurk eﬀect was discovered by Harry McGurk and John MacDonald
in 1976 and have sins been extremely studied to ﬁgure out how the brain inte-
grated the audiovisual sensory information. There are still the fundamental
question, why some stimuli creates single consonant percepts whereas others
creates consonant clusters.
Juan Camilo performed an experiment at Technical university of Denmark
(DTU) that created this illusion, He had 20 subjects watching a recording
of female speaking out ﬁve diﬀerent sounds. The subject saw her speak and
heard her utterance of the words and recorded what consonant they thought
she said.
Juan Camilo results supported the sequential speech cue hypothesis. This
discovery laid the foundation for this thesis. Because if the integration hap-
pens separately then the two integrations must be independent. A probability
model was created to investigate this discovery.

Chapter 1
Introduction
General introduction
When we see and hear someone talk, the brain receives signals from both
our visual and auditory senses. The brain then integrates this multi sensory
information to obtain the best perception of the spoken language. When we
are speaking with another person in a face-to-face situation, we often focus
on the other person’s face when they speak. This gesture is not only polite,
it is also a reaction that helps us to receive the correct message, especially
in a noisy environment. However, even after decades of research with more
than 2.800 citations of the article (McGurk and MacDonald, 1976), it is still
unclear which and how auditory and visual speech features are integrated.
This thesis focuses on the so-called McGurk eﬀect and follows Gil-Carvajal
(2020)’s results on the auditory and visual speech cues, aiming to shed new
light on the topic.
Advantage of audiovisual speech perception
Sumby and Pollack (1954) as well as O’Neill (1954) showed the beautiful
advantage of how visual speech gestures impacted the speech perception.
They showed that comprehensibility increased when both audio and visual
gestures were present compared to when you only had the audio. It makes it
easier for people with hearing loss to understand the person who is speaking
when they see the congruent articular mouth gesture corresponding to the
utterances. This can also provide a signiﬁcant beneﬁt to speech recognition
in hearing impaired as well as cochlear implant listeners. furthermore it has

MrGurk eﬀect
The McGurk eﬀect it is an example of an auditory illusion. it’s a great way to
show that accurate perception of the information involved the participation
of more than one Sensory system, for example vision and sound. The McGurk
eﬀect is an example that show that sensors did not evolve in isolation but
work together to help us perceive our world.
The McGurk eﬀect occurs when a speech sound is represented simultaneously
with incongruent articular mouth movement, corresponding to another word.
for instance in a noisy environment. (McGurk and MacDonald, 1976) In short
the McGurk eﬀect changes the sound we hear due the perception of the visual
gesture and it’s not something we can control.
The illusion can be divided into to parts.
a fusion - and a combination
- illusion. In fusion illusion the percept of the single consonant, has been
explained as the combination of the place information which is found in
the auditory and visual speech signals. The McGurk eﬀect shows that the
perceived place of articulation of an bilabial auditory consonant (i.e. /b/) can
be eﬀected by a simultaneous presentation of a conﬂicting visual non-labial
consonant (i.e /g/). They usually fuse into a third consonant observed as /d/
(Green and Norrix, 1997; Colin et al., 2002; Tiippana, 2014; van Wassenhove,
2013)
In the combination illusion, the percept of the two consonants are due to
their large perceptual prominent character. the bilabial consonant is typically
visually prominent, while the non-labial consonant contains a strong release
burst (Colin et al., 2002). In combination illusion the audiotory non-labial
consonant (i.e /g/) and a visual bilabial consonant (i.e. /b/) merge together
to a paring of the to consonants into a cluster of them /bg/.
Sequential speech cues hypothesis
The idea that, the speech features occurring in the initial consonant segment
are integrated separately from the audiovisual speech features in the subse-
quent consonant segment, is here referred to as the sequential speech cues
hypothesis (Gil-Carvajal, 2020).

This study investigates if the audiovisual integration happens separately in
the opening (initial consonant segment) and closing (subsequent consonant
segment) of the mouth then they can be considered independent when the
consonants are uttered in a medial position of two vowels such that they are
on the form VCV - vowel-consonant-vowel. It is not obvious that the initial
speech feature VC and the subsequent speech features CV are independent.
Assume that if they were dependent of each other, and utterance /aB/ is
heard in the initial state, then it would be more likely that the utterance
/Ba/ is heard in the subsequent state then for example /Ga/. This is because
the consonant /B/ would be the dependent part of both segments. It is not
trivial that those two should be independent.
the consonant /b/ , /g/ and /d/ were chosen because (Wassenhove et al.,
2007; Schwartz, 2010); showed that they elicit strong McGurk fusions and
(Massaro and Cohen, 1993; Green and Norrix, 1997) showed they had strong
elicit Mcgurk combinations.

Theoretical background
Experiment
The data used in the current study was collected by Juan Gil-Carvajal (Gil-
Carvajal, 2020). In this study, twenty subjects participated in the experiment
where they observed 5 visual, 5 auditory and 25 audiovisual speech stimuli
in two blocks for 10 diﬀerent trials each. In each trial, all audiovisual, visual,
and auditory stimuli appeared, and the order of presentation was randomized.
As we assume there is no diﬀerence between the two individual blocks, they
were joined to yield a total of 20 trials for each subject for each of the ﬁve
stimuli.
Subjects
The stimuli was presented by a Swedish native speaking female who was
placed in a sound-proof room with a background where she stated the ut-
terances /aba/ , /aga/ , /ada/, /abga/ and /abda/. To make sure they had
the same duration and she kept the same speaking rate, a metronome was
used which Munhall et al. (1996) suggested to give stronger MrGurk eﬀects.
the 25 audiovisual speech stimuli consist of all possible combinations of the
audio and visual stimuli. we let the index {A, V, AV } describe Audio, visual
and audiovisual stimuli respectively.

Chapter 3
Method
The proposed model of this thesis follows the results of (Gil-Carvajal, 2020)
which support his sequential speech cues hypothesis, assuming that the au-
diovisual speech features in the initial and subsequent segments of a single
consonants and consonant clusters are integrated separately. Therefore, they
can be said to be independent of each other and this is the basis of our model.
The idea for the model is that given a random guess of its parameters, it will
be able calculate the prediction of the observed values. The model will have
to work it’s way back from the guess to the predicted values.
Model
Looking at the ﬁve utterances /aBa/ , /aga/ , /ada/, /abga/ and /abda/ the
subjects was presented, we notice that two of them are combinations of two
others. namely /aba/ and /aga/ can be combined into /abga/ and /aba/ ,
/ada/ can be combined into /abda/. from this we can rearrange the response
categories into a 2-dimentional response matrix that represent the possible
responses for initial and subsequent segments. The rows describes the initial
segment of the consonants and the column descries the subsequent segment
of the consonants.
If the opening (initial) of the word is /ab/ and the closing (subsequent) is
/ga/ the initial segment is in the column "b", and the subsequent segment
is in the row "g". this gives the respond for the word "abga" (BG)
The diagonal (g,d,b) are the response with a single consonant and the others
is the response with a combination of two consonants.

Chapter 5
Discussion
This thesis investigates if the audiovisual integration happens separately in
the opening (initial consonant segment) and closing (subsequent consonant
segment) of the mouth then they can be considered independent.
A probability model was created with a 2-dimensional response matrix where
the rows and the column represented the initial and subsequent segment of
the mouth gesture. each segment consist of the consonants /G/, /B/ and /D/
such that all possible combinations of them created the 9 response categories.
This is because if the speech cue segments are independent, the syllables in
the stimulus will be independent and thereby the marginal probabilities will
be independent. Because of this independence, each consonant segment can
be explained with only two free parameters and it makes it possible to create
the 2-dimensional response matrix, with only 4 parameters for each stimuli
for each person. This divide the number of parameters in half compared to
the saturated model.
I compared the results of the model with the data collected in Juan’s re-
search by creating the mean proportional responses across subjects for the
model. This was done to see if the models results deviated from the mean
proportional response across subjects for the data.
The model captured the McGurk eﬀects in the initial and subsequent seg-
ments of the stimuli which supports the assumption that the speech cue
segments are independent or that there exists sequential audiovisual features
that are independent. The fusion illusion and combination illusions in the
perception of the audiovisual congruent and incongruent pairings of the di-
syllables was also captured by the model..
Example, for auditory /aBa/
dubbed onto the visual /aGa/ resulted in an increase of the response /D/ in
the model, signiﬁcantly. Moreover it captured the partial fusion illusion when

Conclusion
The results of the current study shows that the model was to complex to
create a general model for the sequential speech cues hypothesis.
Because it was possible to recreate the data through the 4 parameters for
each stimuli for each person, one could argue that the result of the model,
supported the assumption that the sequential speech cue hypothesis was true.
Then the initial and subsequent segments of single consonants and consonant
clusters can be seen as independent of each other, such that the audiovisual
speech processing was reﬂected as either and audiovisual enhancement of the
bilabial consonant or illusory percepts.
This thesis is a part of the big research of the McGurk eﬀect and can be used
to create a more simple model as the model did indicate that the speech
cue segments was independent. using the parameters from my model in a
multinomial distribution, a smaller test sample could be created and used to
train a more suitable model that could capture the McGurks illusions more
generalized.
