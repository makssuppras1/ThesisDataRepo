Abstract
Background and Motivation: Tissue culture substrate serves as an important tool to
study the effectiveness of drug on human tissue. Previous techniques to fabricate sub-
strates include micromoulding gelatine with PDMS stamps and bioprinting, but both tech-
niques suffer from drawbacks.
Method: In a proof-of-concept study, a custom micromoulding set-up was developed
including a microfabricated silicon wafer to microstructure the polypropylene nozzle tips
utilized for bioprinting. The microstructured nozzles were then used to extrude substrates
for culture of C2C12 cells.
Results: A 25-25 µm ”bottom-barrier” patterned silicon wafer with close to 25 µm deep
structures was fabricated. With optimized moulding temperature, force, and time of 95°C,
125 g of force, and 10 s, respectively, the wafer was used to transfer ridge structures with
a depth in the range of 20-25 µm to standard bioprinting polypropylene. The patterned
nozzles extruded ridge patterned PDMS substrates when extruding at most at 110 kPa
and a height of 50 µm from printing bed to nozzle tip. When plasma treating, incubating
the patterned PDMS extrudes with fibronectin, matrigel, geltrex at 50, 83, and 100
µg
mL,
respectively, and using C2C12 cells for culture, the cells delaminated after at most 3 days
of differentiation or 5 days of culture.
Conclusion: In the thesis it was demonstrated that standard bioprinting nozzles can be
patterned and used during a bioprinting sequence to form a ridge pattern in an extrude.
Further study is necessary to investigate the concept scalability to other inks such as
hydrogels for easier cell attachment during cell culture.

Introduction
In-vitro human tissue models are a novel tool that have the potential to redefine the pre-
clinical stages for biomedical and pharmaceutical research. Models for cardiac tissue are
of particular interest to further develop and improve. Cardiovascular diseases cause an
estimate 17.9 million deaths a year which is roughly 32% of all deaths worldwide [1].

The major issue of current research is twofold: in-vivo experi-
ments have a disproportionate high risk for human involvement
in clinical trails, and non-human animal models differ too much to
be able to capture human physiology [2]. Additionally, when the
drug finally has been introduced in the market, cardiotoxic effects
after chronic drug usage cause market withdrawal. The issue is
especially severe for clinically important drugs, and 10% of all
therapeutic drugs have been recalled over the last four decades
due to their cardiotoxicity [3].
The human cardiac tissue models have been developed in the
past decade to tackle the issues [4][5][6]. Cardiac tissue models
are required to be uniaxial, aligned and extended as well as force
generating to mimic to structure and function of native tissue. The
structure and function requirements are similar to skeletal mus-
cle tissue and have to be imposed during tissue maturation. Pre-
vious tools to create tissue culture substrates include using the
micromoulding technique. The technique uses a polydimethyl-
siloxane (PDMS) stamp that is fabricated by pouring PDMS on
a patterned silicon wafer (SW). The stamp is then pressed onto
a hydrogel to form a pattern in the hydrogel. The usage of the
micromoulding technique is shown in Figure 1.1. The patterned
hydrogel is called a tissue culture substrate and the pattern for
cardiac and muscle tissue consists of ridge structures commonly
referred to ”bottom-barrier” (BB) structures.
After fabricating the patterned tissue culture substrate, it can be
used to culture cardiomyocytes inducing alignment during mat-
uration. The hydrogel and cells in combination form the cardiac
human tissue models and are further combined with microelec-
trode arrays (MEA)s or other components to make heart-on-a-
chip system as seen in Figure 1.2 and Figure 1.3. The heart-on-
a-chip systems are utilized to test the electrophysiological safety

2.1
Background
Microfabrication of patterns for stamps is done in a cleanroom (CR) which is a room with
air filtration to contain contamination, temperature and humidity. The air flow is regulated
to prevent microparticles from potentially destroying a microdevice during fabrication.
The background to understand what happens during microfabrication of a master SW, the
initial part of the thesis, is explained here. The microfabrication relevant to the thesis can
be divided into four steps: thin film, lithography, dry etch, and wafer coating.
2.1.1
Thin Film Deposition
Thin films can be added on top of a SW, which is a typical substrate of microfabrication,
in order to create a bulk material [9]. The bulk material changes the surface properties of
the substrate which is important in later steps of the microfabrication.
A film can be either grown or deposited. If silicon is the material of the wafer, then a thin film
can be grown by thermal oxidation in a furnace. Such a furnace is heated to a temperature
of 1100 to 1200 °C. The high temperature allows for increased reactivity between oxygen
and the silicon of the wafer. The oxygen comes from either water vapor, which is used in
wet oxidation, or oxygen gas inflow, which is used in dry oxidation. Thermal oxidation is
a very reproducible process with good uniformity and can be used as a batch process.
Importantly, due to the oxidants having to diffuse through the grown layer to arrive at
the silicon, growth rate follows an exponential trend. Depending on dry or wet oxidation
and the oxidation temperature, the oxidation can take a large part of the microfabrication
time-wise. The thickness of the growth is an important consideration for the dry etching
step depending on the etch selectivity between the growth and subsequent layer. Etch
selectivity is explained in the dry etch section.
2.1.2
Lithography
Lithography in microfabrication is the process of adding a photoresist, which is a light-
sensitive polymer, to a wafer and creating a pattern in the polymer. A baking step is
added for the Lithography which is a three step process that can be divided into: spin
coating, exposure, and development [10][11][12]. A post-exposure baking step is also
very important for a photoresist with negative tone to induce cross-linking of the exposed
polymer chains.
Spin coating is the first step of the lithography. The photoresist is deposited onto a wafer
as seen on Figure 2.1.a.1. The deposition is seen on Figure 2.1.a.2.
The photoresist consists of a resin with varying length of monomers or polymer chains,
a photo-active component that changes the properties of the resist when exposed with
ultraviolet (UV)-light, and the solvent that the resin is dissolved in, which makes it a viscous
material and allows for coating. A photoresist is chosen based on the properties needed
and could be the thickness of the spun layer, thermal stability, chemical resistance, and
polarity, which is if the resin either decomposes or crosslinks with the incoming light.
Priming the SW might be necessary to increase adhesiveness of the coating step. Silicon
reacts naturally with the oxygen in air to form silicon oxide over time and water residues
in the oxide may form, which prevents direct deposition and even spreading of the resist
onto the wafer. Other impurities also have a similar effect.

During deposition, the wafer is held on a motorized chuck and the resist is deposited on
the wafer. The resist is then spread along the wafer by rotating the chuck at a low rpm.
Lastly, the chuck is rotated at high rpm at around 2000-5000 rpm to vary the resist height

Soft-bake follows deposition. A temperature increase to 90 to 120°C removes the solvent
and improves mechanical stability of the resist as seen on Figure 2.1.a.4. Allowing the

Exposure of the deposited resist by UV-light activates the photo-sensitive element and,
depending on polarity, causes dissociation of cross-linked polymer chains. To prevent
certain areas from being exposed, a photomask made of glass with chrome patterning
is placed on the resist, which is shown on Figure 2.1.b.1. Alternatively in order to create
a pattern without a mask, a maskless aligner (MLA) can be used that only exposes the
resist sequentially with UV-light.
A notable difference between the two is that the masked method is very fast, which allows
for batch production, but the photomask cannot be changed rapidly. In comparison, the
MLA is dynamic, but exposure takes much longer.

An important aspect to consider during exposure is alignment. If there is misalignment
during the exposure, the resist will be exposed and developed improperly which can affect
future microfabrication steps. The alignment is shown on Figure 2.1.b.2.
An optimal exposure dose of UV-light is needed during the process, and the dose is de-
pendent on resist, exposure light, developer, and mask material. It is important to predict
the theoretical resolution limit as it is critical for a design. The theoretical resolution limit
is effectively how small a pattern can be exposed. The limit is dependent on multiple
parameters such as gap between mask and resist, which is only relevant for the masked
method, resist thickness, and wavelength of exposure light.
Exposing the resist is shown in Figure 2.1.b.3 and the exposed pattern is shown in Fig-
ure 2.1.b.4.
Development is caused by a chemical agent acting on the exposed areas of the resist,
which is shown in Figure 2.1.c.1. As mentioned previously, the resist can be categorized
by polarity. If the tone of the resist is positive, the pattern is removed by the developer. If
the tone of the resist is negative, then the exposed resist remains after development and
the rest of the resist is removed by the developer.
Lastly to remove the developer and dissolved resist, the resist is rinsed with water and
spin dried at a high rpm. The development steps are illustrated in Figure 2.1.c.2 and
Figure 2.1.c.3.
Multiple types of mistakes can occur. Under-development can happen if the resist is not
given enough time to hydrate. Dark erosion happens if there is still solvent left, which
reacts with the developer and acts on the resist to make it thinner and pattern structures
smaller.
Good practice is to investigate the pattern of the resist after development before moving
on to etching any layer below. If the lithography step succeeds, a pattern of appropriate
scale should be evident in a microscope.
2.1.3
Dry etching
The dry etching technique used in the thesis is an anisotropic process that works se-
lectively in one direction at a rate to create depth in a layer by combining physical and
chemical interactions [13]. The mechanisms of dry etching are illustrated in Figure 2.2.
The physical interaction on the substrate of dry etching is due to a dissociated gas particles
becoming an ion when hit by an electron that is generated by two coils on a respective
side of the etching chamber. The ion gets bombarded onto the substrate by attraction to
a platen electrode. The chemical interaction is due to the gas reacting with an activated
substrate atom. The activation happens due to the bombardment, and the reaction forms
a product that can be pumped out by the ventilation system. Using a specific low pressure
plasma gas allows for both interactions to happen at once.
Furthermore, the reaction product needs to become a gas to be pumped out, therefore,
its volatility under a given temperature and pressure must match the chamber’s temper-
ature and pressure during the process. Knowledge about which gases interact with the
substrate at useful temperatures and pressures is important for the etching process to
choose an appropriate gas.
Sometimes also other gases are used as they improve the etching process if pumped into
the equipment together. If one type of gas is used, it would form a passivation layer on the
surface first. The passivation layer protects against chemical reactions and is removed

data analysis in MATLAB. However, one of the function of the script is to have an option

Micromoulding
3.1
Background
Micromoulding is a subset of microprocessing and is a technique used to form microma-
terials of thermoplastic materials with complex 3D geometry and high surface accuracy
[14]. In modern times, micromoulding has undergone substantial development compared
to when it was first introduced with microinjection moulding in the 1980s. Micromoulding
is currently linked to excellent geometry shape, size adaptability, stable product quality,
high efficiency and continuous and automatic production.
Micromoulding at large scale for thermoplastic materials can be characterized by three
main processes: injection moulding, hot embossing (HE), and thermoforming [22].
3.1.1
Injection moulding
For injection moulding, a mould consisting of two inserts and an injection channel is utilized
as shown in the sketch in Figure 3.1.a.1. The inserts are closed, evacuated, and heated
to above the glass transition temperature of the polymer. The polymer is then injected
into the mould shown as Figure 3.1.a.2, and cooled down causing the polymer to be
demoulded shown as Figure 3.1.a.3 The temperature is controlled in a cyclically way
called variotherm. Injection moulding is a technique that is well-suited for an industrial
series production where it is possible to make cycle times to change the temperature to
be as short as possible. One drawback is that fabricating the microstructured moulds
is only cost-effective at high production counts. Another drawback is that keeping the
polymer viscous enough to fill out the microstructures completely is hard as the polymer
cools down fast.
3.1.2
Hot Embossing
In HE, the machine consists of mould inserts as sketched in Figure 3.1.b.1. The moulds
transfer a shape to a thin thermoplastic material. The shape transfer happens as the
moulds are lowered to interface with each other, and a vacuum seal is created. The
thermoplastic film is heated above its softening temperature while a high force is applied
in a vacuum chamber, which is sketched as Figure 3.1.b.2. After replication of the mi-
crostructure on the film, the machine is then cooled, and the film is removed sketched as
Figure 3.1.b.3. A residual layer is found if the tool is not closed completely. A smaller
temperature cycle is possible for HE compared to injection moulding due to it not having
to transport a viscous polymer into the moulding cavity. For HE less material shrinkage
occurs as well as a lower friction force during demoulding allowing for a high-aspect-ratio
of the design; a high horizontal compared to vertical length. Normally, the film cannot
handle high stress from pressing over a larger vertical length. Additionally, setup is easy
for HE and modification can be done swiftly if necessary. Different types of films can also
be used without modifying the setup. Ultimately, HE is well suited for laboratory setups
with small and medium-scale production.

3.1.3
Thermoforming
Thermoforming is a technique applied to form thin films. A polymer film is placed into the
moulding tool with one structured side as sketched in Figure 3.1.c.1. Evacuation of the
tool and clamping of the film is the next step. Heat is applied to soften the film, and gas
presses the microstructure into the film as shown in Figure 3.1.c.3. Compared to HE, the
technique is not appropriate for laboratory processing of high-aspect ratio materials. The
thin film cannot be stressed enough to achieve the pattern.

3.1.4
Micromoulding of the PP Nozzle
Micromoulding of a PP nozzles is conventionally done by using an injection moulding
process [23]. Injection moulding is suitable for making polymer tubular structures which is
a relatively complex and intricate structure. As the nozzles are designed to be discarded
after use, the cost effectiveness is an important criteria of the moulding process. However,
injection moulding only becomes cost effective at very high production quantities, and it
is not appropriate for small scale fabrication needed for transferring the BB pattern to the
nozzle tip as a proof-of-concept.
HE, on the other hand, is considered to be the relevant technique to imitate to an extent
and mould a ridge structures into a nozzle tip. Some additional consideration is, however,
needed to ensure improvement of the moulding [24]. The exact negative replica should
not always be expected after demoulding. Successful moulding and demoulding is de-
pendent on embossing parameters such as pressure, temperature, and time. Increasing
them reduces polymer viscosity and improves the moulding step. However, in turn the de-
moulding step will suffer due to increased thermal shrinkage and adhesion to the mould.
Some detracting factors for using HE include: moulding structures to the lower end of the
microscale is very rare in literature, large-scale pattern recreation is usually not done, and
the type of material is usually not a commodity polymer such as PP.

Methods
For the thesis, it was important to find a simple way to micromould the PP nozzle tips.
Most of the above described techniques were not suitable as proof-of-concept technique
due to either requiring expensive moulds for the setup or only being cost-effective on the

3D Printed Holder
The holder is shown assembled in Figure 3.6. A benefit of
the final iteration of the 3D printed holder is alignment. Align-
ment of nozzle with the SW is an important consideration of
the custom micromoulding technique. The alignment of the
nozzle with SW is ensured largely by four interfaces within
the 3D printed holder and between the 3D printer holder and
the TA. The first of the three interfaces is the screw of the
holder that is screwed into the screw hole on the TA arm as is
shown in the custom setup overview in Figure 3.3. The sec-
ond interface is ensured by the leveling taking place when the
M4 hexagonal nut is screwed all the way to the short cylinder
of the 3D printed piece. The last interface comes from the
tight fit between the long cylinder of the 3D printed piece and
the 5 cc syringe. The fourth interface is between the thread
of the 5 cc syringe and the nozzle. The latter interface is
exactly the setup during extrusion of hydrogels with the bioprinter and is also the nozzle

threaded onto the syringe. When the nozzle is threaded tightly onto the syringe, it also
ends up being turned same amount each time ensuring consistency in pattern transfer. A
major benefit of the final iteration of the 3D printed holder design is that the nozzle can be
changed momentarily. The ability to quickly change the nozzle is achieved by screwing
the nozzle on-and-off the 5 cc syringe, and makes it possible to mould multiple samples

The reason for changing the 3D printing method from the Prusa to the SLA printer for the

sembly. The SLA printer was used subsequently and resulted in better quality holders.
The change to the SLA printer also made the printing procedure faster and more effi-
cient. This is due to the SLA printer using UV-light to cure a liquid solution and make it
solid. Using 3D printing methodology allows printing time to depend only on the model
height and not on the model width. Therefore, multiples of the same print can be printed
simultaneously without increasing printing time.
Still, the holder size could be optimised slightly to better fit the syringe, which would be
done simply by trying various scaling sizes of the model during SLA printing.

Extrusion-based Printing
4.1
Background
Extrusion-based 3D printing is a method to extrude a printable material automatically. The
extrusion material can be a variety of materials and is selected to fit the printing purpose.
Bioprinting, which is a subset of 3D printing, uses a material referred to as a bioink [27].
The bioink can contain biological cells, polymers, chemical factors, and biomolecules
based on the application of the bioink. Hydrogels is a material consisting of polymer
chains that can hold large amounts of water and mimic the physical properties of the
body [28]. Tissue scaffolds of hydrogels can be used for many things such as recon-
structive surgery, drug delivery, and as previously mentioned drug screening. Another
type of printing material used to create printing substrates is PDMS. PDMS is a poly-
meric organosilicon and features C-Si bonds[29]. Using PDMS as an ink for a substrate
is appropriate in a large number of applications. PDMS is biocompatible, transparent gas
permeable and cost-effective. However, due to its hydrophobicity, the material has poor
cell attachment without modifying the surface [30]. When printing, if gas is not removed
from the an ink, surface roughness, imperfections and inferior mechanical properties can
result as the gas aggregates.
The automation comes from using software that instructs the printer to print with either ad-
ditive manufacturing. The manufacturing technique adds layer-upon-layer of material until
a shape has been created, and sometimes each layer can be varied to make complex ma-
terials.

To understand the concepts of Figure 4.2, a very brief definition of the concepts of material
mechanics is given in the following text. Stress (σ) occurs when a force is applied over
an area of a material. The stress causes a deformation in the material and is referred to
as strain (γ). γ can change over time ( ˙γ) with changes in σ which in turn also impacts the
viscosity (η) of viscoelastic materials. ˙γ can be due to an oscillatory stress, which then
has an angular frequency of ω.
Shear stress, happens between the bioink and surfaces of the nozzle interface due to
the normal force by downwards motion caused by the plunger. Shear-thinning materials
reduce the shear forces as it begins to flow, which for the material can be observed as a
more gel or solid-like behavior initially and, afterwards, a more liquid like behavior. This
property is important when printing with cells as low shear increases cell survival when
printing.
Viscosity, η, is the ability of the bioink to resist deforming forces. A wide range of viscosity
is possible for bioinks and can be modulated by adding temperature, polymer concentra-
tion, molecular interactions with potential additives, and molecular weight. Bioinks with
a gel-like behavior maintain their shape after extrusion. Bioinks with a more liquid-like
behavior allow for cells to printed as well during extrusion. A good bioink has a mixture
of both and is shear-thinning when a force is applied. After removal of the applied force,
the ink facilitates shape retention by increasing viscosity. The former property for an ink
is the point the storage modulus (G’), which is a measure of the elastic portion of the en-
ergy stored during deformation, changes from its constant trend when plotting it versus
applied stress. The point can be seen in Figure 4.2.a. The latter property is known as
thixotropy and needs to happen as fast as possible as the ink exits the nozzle and falls
on the printing substrate. Thixotropy occurs in a time-dependent manner as can be seen
in Figure 4.2.c where G’, loss modulus (G”), which is a measure of the energy dissipated
as heat during deformation, and η. A material’s shear-thinning property depends on the
molecular structure of the material and can be quantified by fitting steady-state viscosity
at various shear rates, γ. with the power-law equation η = Kγ. (n−1) . Consistency index,
K, and flow behavior index, n, are both predictive properties for the shear thinning window
seen in Figure 4.2.b.
Yield stress of the ink decides the force needed for continuous and smooth flow. Yield
stress is an important property as low yields stress inks will just leak out of the nozzle.
Printing with high yields stress inks will result in shape-retaining structures. On a viscosity
versus stress plot, it is possible to determine the yields stress as the stress at the viscosity
maximum as seen in Figure 4.2.d.
The complex modulus is a property of a viscoelastic material and is a two-component
property. The ability of the material to store its energy in the elastic region is the first
component, while the second is a measure of the energy dissipated after storage. These
are denoted by G’ and G” respectively. Importantly, when G’ > G” it results in a gel-like
behavior of the bioink, whereas if G’< G”, then the result is a unstable system resulting in
poor definition post extrusion as seen in Figure 4.2.e. Additionally, the point where G’ is
equal to G” after certain cross-linking time (tcrosslinking) is called the gelation point, which
can be seen in Figure 4.2.f.
The loss factor is the ratio of G” to G’. The factor gives information about the crosslinking
of the material system as well as its stability. The loss factor can be used to predict the
printability of the material as values greater than 1, between 0.6 and 1, and between
0.4 and 0.6, and below 0.4 correspond, respectively, to no shape retention, poor shape
retention, good extrudability and shape retention, and not extrudable.

4.1.3
Extrusion Parameters
Secondly, the extrusion parameters need to be considered. These parameters include
printing pressure, printing speed, printing temperature, nozzle design, and cross-
linking parameter.
The printing pressure of the extrusion printer is caused by compressed air. The parame-
ter needs to be optimized when printing to allow for continuous flow of the extrusion. Yield
stress needs to be overcome by the pressure of the compressed air to begin extrusion
of the ink. If the compressed air pressure is too large or too low for a particular ink, the
resulting extrude is either too wide or results in a sporadic filament.
Printing speed is speed of the nozzle movement in x- and y-directions as well as z-
direction for 2D and 3D structures, respectively. An even match in speed needs to be
created between the nozzle speed and the extrusion pressure. If not, then the extrusion
might be elongated or thickened.
Printing temperature changes the rheological features of the bioink. Optimizing the tem-
perature of the print head and bed allows for slick extrusion and deposition. The temper-
ature also needs to be considered when printing with cells.
The nozzle design influences the shape of the extrusion. Shear stress, in particular,
is influenced by the nozzle cross-section, slope, length, and inlet/outlet diameter. The
shear then changes the previously mentioned rheological properties. Increased shear at
the nozzle tip can cause elongated cells if part of the bioink [34].
Crosslinking is used to increase the strength of the ink by creating new bonds be-
tween the polymer chains.
Multiple strategies are used to increase the crosslinking:
photocrosslinking started by a photoinitiator when irradiated with light, thermal crosslink-
ing started when the ink is exposed to heat, and chemical crosslinking using a chemical
reagent to start crosslinking [27].
4.1.4
Previous Methods to Create Tissue Cultures Substrates
As mentioned previously in the introduction section, prior designs to create tissue culture
substrates include using soft lithography to create ridge structures in a substrate material.
The technique of the designs is listed in Table 4.1 and will be generally explained in the
section to give an overview.
Previous designs can be divided into techniques creating flat substrates or substrates
with ridges. To create flat substrates, casting as is performed by pouring the PDMS onto
a surface. Micromoulding of soft-lithography and 3D printing techniques in the listed ar-
ticles are used to create surfaces with ridges. Soft lithography contains two categories:
micromoulding and microcontact printing.
Micromoulding happens when a stamp, typically made of PDMS, is used to mechanically
introduce a pattern into a substrate material. PDMS stamps are created by pouring PDMS
over a SW with a pattern. These stamps are then used to mould gelatine directly by
inverting the stamps onto the hydrogel [4]. Multiple others studies also use the previous
method to create substrates [5][35][36].
Microcontact printing, on the other hand, covers the top of the barriers of the PDMS stamp
with a material such as fibronectin. Placing the stamp into a thin, flat substrate then trans-
fers only the fibronectin but with the stamp pattern. Transferring fibronectin in a pattern
creates adhesion points for cells to material allowing them to grow aligned [37][4][38].
However, microcontact printing has been found to be less effective than micro-moulding
[4].

4.3.2
Heat Curing Experiment
Results for before and after heat curing of PDMS extrudes with varying temperatures and
duration can be seen in Figure 4.8. It is very noticeable that there is a loss of BB pattern
in the heat cured PDMS at 90 °C compared to the heat cured PDMS at both RT and 60
°C. Increasing the temperature makes it easier for the PDMS material to flow as well as
allowing for some thermal expansion. Thermal expansion for PDMS is not large enough
to destroy the structures. The removal of the structures must then be due to the flow.
Increasing curing time of PDMS or increasing the temperature, reduces the time it takes
to get the same stiffness [43]. To make a stamp, a certain stiffness is adequate to transfer
a pattern into a hydrogel. But curing for longer will increase the stiffness of the PDMS, and
when PDMS is the culturing substrate, it yields a higher mechanical load for alignment of
muscle cells which can potentially be beneficial for cell alignment.
With both considerations, the PDMS should definitely be cured at something close to 60
°C to reduce the possibility of pattern removal. The duration could be kept at the standard
24 hours as it gives aligned structures, but it could also be elongated to see if it impacts
the cell culture of C2C12 cells.

5.1.1
Anatomy and Physiology of the Skeletal Muscle
The muscle system is one of the largest organ systems in the body. Of that system,
the skeletal muscle compromises a majority [45]. Dependent on its function in the body,
skeletal muscle ranges in terms of size, shape and arrangement of fibres. The struc-
ture of skeletal muscle consists roughly of organised sub-units of fibres surrounded by
connective tissue as can be seen in Figure 5.1. At the highest level of organisation, the
muscle is surrounded by epimysium. Here, the muscle consists of multiple muscle fibres
bundled together. A bundle is sheathed by perimysium while a muscle fibre is sheathed
by endomysium.
Figure 5.1: The subdivision of muscle tissue is shown. The muscle is divided into sub-
units called fascicles surrounded by connective tissue, and those sub-units are divided
into smaller sub-units surrounded by connective tissue. Blood vessels provide nutrients,
and satellite cells serve to regenerate damaged tissue. Image is from [46].
A muscle fibre is a muscle cell, but contain multiple nuclei due to fusion during maturation.
Fibres consist of myofibrils and each myofibrils consists of multiple myofilaments. Two
important myofilaments function are the actin and myosin filaments. They make up the
contractile unit of the muscle called sarcomeres, which also gives the tissue its signature
striated look. Another type of cells called satellite cells are found in the muscle tissue
between the basement membrane and the sarcolemma, which is a cell membrane that
adjoins multiple myofibrils. Satellite cells can be stimulated by growth factors and act as
a reserve for when new muscle fibre cells are needed.

A skeletal muscle is subdivided in such a way to generate force on the microscale that
accumulates to a much larger force on the macroscale. It acts on the bones via tendons
to provide motion. In addition to generating force, the muscle tissue acts isometrically for
structural support, as a glucose and protein storage, and to regulate body temperature.
Other types of tissues are also present in the skeletal muscle organ system [47]. Nerve
cells, lymph and blood vessels as well as the already mentioned connective tissue are
present. These other tissue types are of great importance for normal function and play a
role during tissue maturation in an induction interaction .
5.1.2
Maturation of C2C12 cells
As can be seen in Table 4.1, the C2C12 cell line is popular for cell culturing on substrates.
To investigate tissue alignment on substrates with ridges, the C2C12 cells go through two
culture steps: an initial proliferation step and a subsequent differentiation step. In the
differentiation step, the myoblasts start to fuse into nascent myotubes creating a singular
cell with many nuclei. The fusion is regulated by many cell adhesion proteins and adaptor
proteins. Certain types of these two types of protein activate cell signalling pathways
which are essential for fusion. Other signalling pathways are activated as a result of the
myogenic differentiation programme but also help towards fusion [48].
Figure 5.2: Overview of C2C12 differentiation during culturing and differentiation on sub-
strate. Illustration is from [48].
An extensive deep-dive in each signalling pathway is too extensive for the thesis, but an
overview with the maturation progression and the role of multiple signalling molecules can
be seen in Figure 5.2. The C2C12 cells are at the myoblast stage of myogenic differenti-
ation and are induced to fusion after initial proliferation in growth media by changing to a
low serum media, otherwise known as differentiation media.
Myoblast-myoblast fusion occurs initially by ”primary fusion” and then by ”secondary fu-
sion” where nascent myotubes form from the myoblasts and subsequently more my-
oblasts fuse with nascent myotubes, respectively. Many aspects are involved in regu-
lating both types of fusion and include cell adhesion proteins, transmembrane lipids, and
intracellular domain-associated signalling, which are also called adaptor proteins. Two no-
table pathways involve cellular attachment to the extracellular environment via fibronectin
prominent during primary fusion as well as cell-to-cell attachment dominated by cell ad-
hesive molecules prominent during secondary fusion.

5.1.3
Previous Cell Culture Protocols
In the following section, an overview of protocols that use myoblast or cardiomyoblast for
cell culture is given. The section is a succession of the studies displayed in Section 4.1.4
as the following step to investigate cell maturation after making the tissue culture substrate
is to culture cells on it.
Important considerations for patterned tissue culture substrate are the myogenic cell type.
Myocytes and cardiomyocytes express different adhesion molecules resulting in a varia-
tion in compatibility with a substrate.
The cell type used in cell culture protocols varies with regards to how well it is suited to
give information about the cell model and its functional properties [49]. For laboratory use
it can be categorised as either primary, transformed, or self-renewing cell. The neonatal
rat ventricular myocytes (NRVM)s, which is a high-quality primary myocyte, are ready for
culture after extraction from the animal followed by isolation [4]. Human induced pluripo-
tent stem cell-derived cardiomyocytes, a self-renewing cell type, are another option, which
are derived from minimally invasive means [5]. C2C12 cells are a third option, and they
are a type of transformed cell that has been immortalized via genetic manipulation. The
C2C12 cells are known to be proficient in rapid proliferation under high serum conditions
and differentiation into myotubes under low serum conditions.
Some additional aspects of maturation are relevant to cell culture on tissue culture sub-
strates. These aspects are the cell adherence to the substrate, the type of medium, and
the cell density on the tissue culture substrate at the start of the proliferation phase.
To allow good adherence of cell to the substrate, adherence proteins are applied to the
substrate. Common among most cell culture protocols is fibronectin. Fibronectin is gly-
coprotein and has two regions called extra domain A and extra domain B that mediate
the cell-ECM interactions [50]. The typical concentration applied to PDMS substrates to
increase adherence to cells is 50 µg
mL. Other proteins that can be used for cell adherence
include matrigel and geltrex.
The medium used during culture is either growth or differentiation medium. Both types of
medium are composed mostly of a solution that is a combination of necessary nutrients
such as amino acids, vitamins, and inorganic salts. The solution is usually referred to
as high-glucose DMEM. Additionally, the growth medium contains some kind of solution
that allows for the cells to proliferate. 10% fetal bovine serum is a common choice [4].
Differentiation medium contains a solution that allows cells to differentiate. As previously
mentioned, a low-serum solution induces differentiation, and 2% fetal bovine serum is an
option for differentiation medium [4]. Added to both growth and differentiation medium is
penicillin-streptomycin which serves as an anti-microbial solution.
When seeding cells, the density needs to be high enough at both initial proliferation and
subsequent differentiation steps. 5.0·103viable cells
cm2 is a typical seeding density for C2C12
cells [51], which for a 6-well plate with an area of 8.96 cm2 and a height of 1.9 cm trans-
lates to 85,100 cells. Some studies report using similar amount for seeding C2C12 cells
[37][36], which is significantly less than the seeding density necessary for a primary cell
such as NRVM [8].

Preparing Medium
The preparation of growth medium was done by adding 89% DMEM, 10% fetal bovine

was done by adding 97% DMEM, 2% horse serum, and 1% P/S.
5.2.2
Thawing Cells
The cell laboratory work was initiated by thawing C2C12 cells from the cell laboratory
freezer in the basement. The procedure for thawing cells was as follows: before starting,
the culture medium was preheated, and the workbench cleaned. The cells were pulled
out of the freezer and placed in dry ice. The cells were then thawed quickly in 37 °C water
bath without submerging the vial with the cells fully. The vial was removed from the water
bath while a small chunk of ice remained. In the LAF bench, contents of the vial were
transferred to a 15 mL centrifuge tube. The transfer was done drop-by-drop at increasing
rate to not damage the cells. The tube was then centrifuged at 300 rpm for 4 minutes.
The medium was removed, and new medium was added while mixing with a pipette. Cell
density was found. After growth medium was added to a cell culture flask, 150k C2C12
cells were added as well to begin passaging.
5.2.3
Passaging Cells
The passaging of cells was done during maintenance of cells and just before the seeding
of cells on the tissue culture substrates for experiments [52].
Vials of trypsin/EDTA (T/E), growth medium, and phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) were
heated in the water bath to not shock the cells when added. Cell confluency should reach

70-80% before initiating passaging. The vacuum pump was turned on in preparation for
use.
The medium was removed from the cell culture flask with the vacuum pump. The attached
cells were washed with 10 mL of PBS by using a pipette four to five. This was done twice.
3 mL of TE was added to the flask, and the cell culture flask was incubated for 4 minutes.
Adherence was checked in the microscope, if still sticky the cell culture flask was hit
gently on the side. 12 mL of growth medium was added to stop the TE from working. The
solution in the cell culture flask was then transferred to a new 15 mL centrifuge tube, and
the contents of the tube were mixed gently. 0.5 mL of the tube was removed by pipetting
and placed into a vial. The contents of the vial were transferred for cell counting. Cell
density was found. After growth medium was added to a cell culture flask, 150k C2C12
cells were added as well. Adding the cells to the culture flask began a new passage. The
number of the passage was marked on the new cell culture flask.
5.2.4
Cell Alignment Experiments
After incubating the extrudes with a cell adhesive coating solution and preparing a vial
of cells after passaging, a cell alignment experiment was initiated. Firstly, removing the
coating solution from the 6-well plates with the extrude was done. Each well was then
washed twice with PBS or DMEM was done. 2 mL of growth medium was initially added
to each 6-well plate, and 100k C2C12 cells were seeded gently into the liquid. For two
days afterwards, the cells were allowed to growth and reach 90 % confluency. When 90
% confluency was reached, the growth medium was removed. The cells were washed
with PBS or DMEM, and the differentiation medium was added. Every subsequent 2
days, the differentiation medium was changed. The cells were imaged with a bright field
microscope each day throughout the growth period as well as the differentiation period to
collect information about the changes in cell state.

Considerations
It is clear from the results that cell culture was the least successful part of the thesis. After
creating the patterned PDMS, the surfaces were made hydrophillic to allow for cell adhe-
sive proteins to stick. The plasma treatment machine used to make the PDMS hydrophillic
was heavily used and unreliable with high variation as mentioned by the machine’s in-
structor. The PDMS became hydrophyllic as evidently by not repelling DMEM during
cell adhesive coating. A reduced application of the coating proteins results in poorer cell
attachment to PDMS. Poorer cell attachment means both increased likelihood of detach-
ment from the PDMS due to too forceful media change, and increased likelihood of cells
aggregating due to cell-cell attachments.
Most of the studied mentioned in Section 4.1.4 and Section 5.1.3 used fibronectin 50 µg
mL
without plasma treatment to induce C2C12 to stick to the substrate during microcontact
printing. Also, the incubation period was 1 hour. Cell density during seeding was 100k/6-
well plate well for C2C12 cells. The aforementioned preparation yielded aligned structures
and were similarly done when preparing for cell culture of the C2C12 cells on PDMS
in the thesis. This indicates that something else is the problem, potentially relating to
confluency or handling of the C2C12 cells during proliferation or differentiation media
change. Too many cells at the point of adding differentiation medium might be the cause of
the aggregates seen in Figure 5.6 and Figure 5.5 would be indicative of this. Investigating
which of the two mechanism cause cell detachment would be the first step. It could be
done fairly simply by ensuring that the media is removed and added very carefully and
trying to initiate differentiation at varying confluencies.

Conclusion and Outlook
The basis of the thesis was a proof-of-concept idea that was successfully explored, and
the aims, therefore, completed. A custom moulding setup including a BB SW to mould BB
onto a PP nozzle was created and refined. To evaluate the structures on the nozzle tip,
a MATLAB script was created. A PDMS bioprinter ink was formulated and extruded with
the BB patterned nozzle tip. The result was the pattern being transferred to the PDMS
extrude, creating tissue culture substrates. Subsequent maturation of C2C12 cells on
tissue culture substrates was then evaluated.
The four parts of the thesis were each sequentially important for the exploration and ac-
complishment of the aims. Each part had successful elements but some unsuccessful
elements emerged. The unsuccessful elements emerged mainly in the two latter parts of
the thesis. A gelatin ink formulation was made and printed with the BB patterned nozzle
tips. The transfer of the pattern to the extrude was unsuccessful. C2C12 culture on the
PDMS extrudes all detached from the substrate before completing maturation.
6.1
Future Perspective of the Concept
As the patterning concept has been proven with the results of the thesis with one type of
ink, the next step would be to scale the concept to be able to pattern other inks. Patterning
other inks is a question about having similar shear-thinning and thixotropic properties to
PDMS. The pattern needs to be able to reduce in viscocity to flow nicely when pressure is
applied and then quickly become more viscous after deposition on the printing substrate.
For gelatine, the viscosity could be improved by having a heating element during extru-
sion, and the thixotropic property could be improved by using a freezing element during
deposition. When the concept is scaled to for example gelatine as was attempted in the
thesis, the cell detachment problem will solve itself.
As mentioned in the EB discussion, the printing speed parameter should be investigated
when in comes to how the BB pattern can become more distinct in the extrude.
The custom moulding setup be off continued use in the future, but a modification should
be done to the 3D printed holder to achieve quicker sample production. Such a change
could be hard to do to current iteration of the 3D printed holder. The change should be
allow for batch production of nozzles and still allow for good alignment of the nozzle tip
with the SW.
The next step after aforementioned improvements would be to scale the concept to in-
dustrial size. The method to fabricate patterned PP nozzles on a large scale is to use to
injection compression moulding [23]. Many new consideration would have to be made
when converting the setup to industrial scale. New micropatterns on the mould for injec-
tion moulding could be the first consideration. By moulding a pillars positioned in different
geometric patterns, the new moulds could potentially allow for extrude patterning in mul-
tiple directions. The extrude would be patterned in some direction via free flow through
the pillars, but the stopped in others directions.

Group Information Extraction - Local Fuction 7
The seventh local funciton is used to extract sample grouping information from the folder to be used in the 
fifth local function. If samplle groupings exist for silicon wafer and extrude samples and bar charts need to be 
created for those, then the function can be added to those respectivie function in a manner similar to the the fifth 
local function.
Input is the table with information about what the file names are in the folder.
Output is information about the samples in the folder such as what the name of the groups (groups), at what 
index the group begins (idxs), the size of each group (size of groups), and a cummulative summation of the 
group size (A). All this is stored in order as a column vecter in the output matrix.
function outputg = groups(T)
The file extension is reduced to the first three characters.
    tmp=char(table2cell(T(3:end,1)));
    tmp=tmp(:,1:3);
Information about which samples are unique is gathered
    [groups,idxs]=unique(string(tmp));
The size of the groups is calculated
    sizeofgroups=zeros(length(groups),1);
    sizeofgroups(end)=length(tmp)-idxs(end)+1;
    for i=2:length(groups)
        sizeofgroups(i-1)=(idxs(i)-idxs(i-1));
    end
Cummulative sum of the size of groups and collection of the information into the output matrix
    A=cumsum(sizeofgroups);
    outputg = [idxs,groups,sizeofgroups,A];
end

Circularity Parameter Calculation - Local Function 9
The local function uses image analysis tools to calculate circularity of the extracted images of local funciton 8 
and feeds it back to 8.
Input is an image of the nozzle tip hole as mentioned in local function 8.
Output is the circularity value of the specific image.
function circularity = findcirc(Ic)
gray_image = rgb2gray(Ic);
Kmedian = medfilt2(gray_image);
BW = imbinarize(Kmedian,0);
stats = regionprops('table', BW, 'Centroid', 'Area','Eccentricity', ...
    'EquivDiameter','MajorAxisLength','MinorAxisLength');
cc = bwconncomp(BW); 
idx = find([stats.Area] > 400); 
BW2 = ismember(labelmatrix(cc),idx);  
invertBW2=~BW2;
stats2 = regionprops('table', invertBW2, 'Centroid', 'Area','Eccentricity', ...
    'EquivDiameter','MajorAxisLength','MinorAxisLength');
cc2 = bwconncomp(invertBW2); 
idx2 = find([stats2.Area] > 8000 & [stats2.Area] < 50000,1); 
BW3 = ismember(labelmatrix(cc2),idx2);  
stats3 = regionprops('table', BW3,'Perimeter','Solidity', 'Centroid', 'Area', ...
    'Eccentricity', 'EquivDiameter','MajorAxisLength','MinorAxisLength');
area=stats3.Area;
perimeter=stats3.Perimeter;
circularity=(4*area*pi)/(perimeter^2);
end
