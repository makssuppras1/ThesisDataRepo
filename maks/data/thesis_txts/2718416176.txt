Abstract
Challenges like increased city population, climate change and aging infrastructure control the
work of urban planners. Existing modelling tools are reaching their limitations when assessing
these challenges. Thus, in the recent years the usage of machine learning (ML) models has
been explored in the urban drainage setting. An important advantage of ML models is their
short simulation time. It allows numerous simulations with different settings in a short time pe­
riod which enables for example parameter calibration.
The following research investigates an approach that allows parameter calibration for an ML
model. The ML model developed by Palmitessa et al. (2022) has been used as base model.
The goal of this research is to calibrate catchment properties based on real­life measurements
and simulated series by the ML model. The data needed for this research originates mainly
from the publicly available ”Bellinge” data set.
In order that Palmitessa et al.’s model can be calibrated, four consecutive steps are carried
out in this research. Firstly, the ML model gets extended by a surface rainfall­runoff model.
The applied surface rainfall­runoff model is a Nash instantaneous unit hydrograph model. This
extension to the ML model includes catchment parameters as inputs to the ML model. Hence,
they can be calibration later on. The second step is adapting the training approach of the ML
model. A new training approach, namely the randomized training approach, is proposed. This
training approach ensures that the ML can handle a big variety of different inflow scenarios and
ratios. The third step and the last needed requirement before calibration is to set the ML model
into the framework of the real Bellinge drainage system. The ML should include the same wa­
ter sources as the real Bellinge drainage system. This is important as a simulated series gets
compared to a measured series during calibration. These three steps outline requirements a
ML model needs to fulfill to allow calibration. In this research project, several experiments are
carried out to ensure that these prerequisite are met. Overall, the analysed experiments lead
all to adequate and sound results.
The last step is the actual catchment parameter calibration. The effective areas connected to a
node are calibrated by a global heuristic search algorithm. The calibration leads to interesting
findings and adapts the simulation results towards the measurements. On the other side, it
shows potential and limitations of the applied calibration method DDS.
This project carries out fundamental work for calibration of ML models. It outlines which and
how requirements need to be met to enable calibration of ML models. Finally, it shows that
parameter calibration is possible and promising. The best way to calibrate such ML models
needs to be evaluated in further work.

Introduction
About 50 % of the worlds population lives in cities with a projected increase of up to 58 % in
the next five decades. As a direct result ”effective urban and territorial planning is critical to
mitigate the negative social, economic and environmental [aspects] associated with future ur­
ban growth” according to the United Nations (“World Cities Report 2022 ­ Envisaging the Future
of Cities”, n.d., p.18). Urban water engineers are an important stakeholder when it comes to
the mentioned urban and territorial planning. A well­planned drainage system is a key asset
in sustainable cities. However, planning a sound urban drainage system (UDS) is a challenge,
as many uncertain factors need to be considered. Two important factors in the 21st century
are urbanization and climate change (Zhou, 2014). Urbanization is a strong influence, because
more and more people need access to clean drinking water, so, consequently, a higher amount
of water needs to be treated. Climate change causes uncertainty as it changes the amount,
frequency and duration of water in urban areas. Precipitation in many regions has shifted to
more extreme highs and lows throughout seasons. The increase in heavy rain events can be
clearly seen in Europe and North America (Caretta et al., 2022). However, not only rainfall im­
pacts the water in cities, but also and rivers, sea water and glacial water need to be taken into
consideration. Cities can experience ”river (fluvial) floods, pluvial (precipitation­driven) floods,
sewer floods, coastal floods” and more (Dodman et al., 2022, p.925). All these potential risk
factors need to be considered when urban water engineers plan an extension or renovation of
UDSs.
A tool many urban water engineers use to account for these uncertainties is, computational
modelling. Through digitally modelling a scenario, numerous impacts can be evaluated and a
reliable solution can be designed (Sarni et al., 2019). Over the last 50 years computer simula­
tions have become an integral part of urban planning (Fu et al., 2022). This physically­based
modelling approach solves the Saint Venant’s equations for each time step (Abbott and Ionescu,
1967). Consequently, these model outcomes have a high fidelity which gives them the name
”high­fidelity models” (Hifi model) (Palmitessa et al., 2022). Commonly used Hifi model soft­
ware are Mike+ by DHI (DHI, 2021) or SWMM by EPA (EPA, 2020). However, Hifi models have
reached their limits when facing future challenges. The interactions between UDSs, nature and
humans are constantly becoming more interlinked and complex. This increases the computa­
tional costs for Hifi models significantly which is not desirable in times where real­time control is
a much discussed feature. Furthermore, Hifi models are dependent on many modelling param­
eters and assumptions which cannot always be calibrated due to data scarcity. Furthermore,
knowledge transfer between UDSs is hardly possible as insights are strongly based on the phys­
ical structure of UDS (Fu et al., 2022).
A promising alternative to Hifi models is machine learning (ML) in the urban water context.
In ML, systems learn from large data sets and can then identify patterns between various UDSs
parameters (Fu et al., 2022). The benefits seen in the usage of ML is that it can capture complex
data relations and needs less computational time and fewer parameter estimates as it is based
on data (Kwon and Kim, 2021). Whereas these points present the limits of Hifi models, they
do present strengths of ML approaches. In engineering, especially the usage of deep learning
which is a subdomain of ML has got lot of focus lately (Kwon and Kim, 2021). As one example,
the research done by Palmitessa et al. (2022) can be mentioned. The research shows that
through deep learning, pipe hydraulics can be well simulated when compared to Hifi results.
Additionally, the computation times for longer rain simulations can be reduced by up to two or­

ders of magnitude (Palmitessa et al., 2022).
When using deep learning for challenges of the 21st century like climate change and urban­
ization, existing modelling problems can be reevaluated. One frequent problem is the proper
runoff estimation from catchments in Hifi models. It is hard to properly include catchments as
numerous descriptive properties are needed, but often these parameters are unknown (Behrouz
et al., 2020). Due to changes in imperviousness through urbanization and in rainfall intensities
due to climate change, the total runoff volume and peak runoff is even more difficult to assess
(Barco et al., 2008). The standard approach to evaluate the catchment properties is by using
model calibration (e.g. Krebs et al., 2013,Behrouz et al., 2020, Barco et al., 2008). Calibrating
existing Hifi models is highly time consuming. However, calibration of ML models is feasible as
it is by far quicker. This allows to use various standard calibration approaches or specific ML
approaches for model calibration.
The motivation of this research is to implement catchment property calibration in a deep learning
setting. It is analysed with requirements a ML model needs to fulfill in order to be calibrated. The
focus is on defining a proper test case with all relevant water sources and training a stable ML
model which can handle different inflow scenarios. Additionally, the benefits of calibrating a ML
model are explored. The deep learning algorithm, gResNet model, developed by Palmitessa
et al. (2022) constitutes as ML foundation of this project. In the existing research, hydrological
modeling has been carried out by a Hifi tool, namely Mike+. Only the hydraulic modeling part
has been with the gResNet model. Thus, the research from Palmitessa et al. combines ML
and Hifi modeling. The following project works only with ML modelling. Therefore, hydrological
modeling is incorporated into the ML model which create a new coupled gResNet model.

Outline of research project
In order to be able to calibrate catchment properties, they need to be included in the input pa­

Afterwards, the designed ML algorithm needs to be set in context to real­life scenarios. Thus,

no longer on how well rain events can be simulated, but the overall system states considering all
potential inflows. This is relevant in order to be able to calibrate the simulation results towards

Finally, the calibration of catchment parameters can be incorporated. This is the fourth and
last part in this project. It is evaluated how well calibrating a ML model works. The applied

tool. In the last decades, physically­based models have mainly been used, but in recent years

The theory chapter firstly describes the basics of rainfall­runoff modelling. The chosen rainfall­
runoff modelling approach in this project is the Nash instantaneous unit hydrograph (IUH) (see
section 3.1). This is a widely used surface rainfall to runoff modelling approach. Then a short
introduction to neural networks (NN), a type of machine learning, follows (see section 3.2). As
this thesis focuses on an existing NN algorithm, this subsection targets the understanding of
common words used within NN modelling and presents Palmitessa et al.’s NN model set­up

3.1
Surface Rainfall­Runoff Modelling
A key aspect in hydrology is the evaluation of how water distributes on the Earth’s surface.
Hence, it is important to know how rainfall spreads as soon as it reaches the surface. A lot
research has been carried out which tries to model the correlation between rainfall and sur­
face runoff. Overall, it is a standard procedure to split the rainfall­runoff model into two parts
(Baymani­Nezhad and Han, 2013). The first step is to evaluate the effective rainfall towards a
catchment and the second step is to translate the effective rainfall to a runoff flow.
The subsequent theory section is structured in the same way. Firstly, it is described how the
effective rainfall for a catchment can be determined (see section 3.1.1). Afterwards, section
3.1.2 outlines how effective rainfall is transformed to a runoff flow. Several approaches exit
which could converse the rainfall to a flow. The unit hydrograph method is a commonly used
approach and therefore explained in section 3.1.2. As the unit hydrograph method is widely
applied, various rainfall­runoff methods developed from it (Agirre et al., 2005). Thus, section
3.1.2 presents also the Nash instantaneous unit hydrograph in more detail as this approach has
important benefits.
3.1.1
Determination of Effective Rainfall
This subsection outlines how the rainfall is converted to effective rainfall connected to a catch­
ment.
Firstly based on the measured rainfall intensity r, the total rainfall rtotal for a catchment can be
calculated by the direct runoff formula which is shown in equation 3.1. The calculated total
rainfall equals the total water amount rained on a catchment area AC.
rtotal = AC ∗r
(3.1)
However, not all the water reaching the surface gets converted to runoff. It is important to con­
sider additional hydrological processes such as irrigation IR, infiltration IF, evapotranspiration
EP, deep percolation DP and so on (Patwardhan et al., 1990). It is up to the modellers choice
4

which hydrological processes should be included in evaluating the effective rainfall. The more
processes are included, the more accurate are the obtained runoff estimates. The relation be­
tween rainfall r, effective rainfall reff(later translates to runoff) and the hydrological processes is
the following (Patwardhan et al., 1990):
reff = r −(IR + IF + EP + DP)
(3.2)
Inserting the effective rainfall into the direct runoff formal instead of the measured rainfall would
lead to a more reasonable rainfall estimate. However, the more hydrological processes are
included in the model, the more parameters need to be known or assumed.
In urban settings, often another approach is chosen to assess the adequate effective rainfall
for a catchment. This approach is based on adapting the catchment area AC of an catchment.
Instead of considering the whole catchment area often only parts of a catchment are consid­
ered. A common assumption is that only rainfall on the impervious area converts to runoff and
rainfall on the pervious area infiltrates (Ebrahimian et al., 2016). This assumption changes the
catchment area AC to an effective catchment area AC, eff in the following way with an impervious
factor IC:
AC,eff = AC ∗IC
(3.3)
Hence, the area parameter in equation 3.1 can be replaced by the determined effective catch­
ment area AC, eff to obtain a reasonable amount of rainfall which gets converted to runoff.
This assumption is possible, because catchments in urban drainage modelling are mostly anal­
ysed as lumped entity. The geological properties of a catchment are averaged and their spatial
variations not taken into account (Agirre et al., 2005). The same applies to the rainfall. The
measured rainfall at a certain point is often assumed to be constant over a whole catchment
and even test case. This is done as it simplifies hydrological simulations and parameter esti­
mation. Other approaches would be a fully­distributed model or semi­distributed model (Agirre
et al., 2005).
3.1.2
Translation from Rainfall to Runoff
The runoff behaviour for catchments in a drainage system vary greatly. The differences are
mainly caused by different catchment properties and rainfall characteristics (Agirre et al., 2005).
Therefore, it is important to transform the total rainfall adequately for the catchment to a runoff
flow. The simplest and most used rainfall­runoff conversion method is the unit hydrograph (UH)
theory defined by Sherman in 1932 (Magar and Jothiprakash, 2014). The ”unit hydrograph is
defined as a direct runoff hydrograph resulting from 1 mm [] of effective rainfall falling uniformly
over the drainage area at a constant rate for an effective duration ”(Chow, 1988). Unit rain
events with different lengths lead to differently shaped UHs even though the total rainfall vol­
ume is always 1mm for the UH approach. When the UH for a catchment is determined, it is
know how the catchment response to a unit of rainfall. Consequently, the evaluated effective
rainfall can be convoluted with the UH to generate a direct runoff hydrograph considering more
than one unit rainfall.
However, a limitation of the UH method is that the rainfall intensity needs to be uniform over the
duration of a rain event. This is extremely unlikely for real rain events especially if rain events
have a longer duration (Hossain, 2011).
If the applied unit rainfall to the UH approach approximates to a duration of zero, the resulting
UH turns into an instantaneous unit hydrograph (IUH). This IUH is defined ”as the direct runoff

3.2
Machine Learning Background
Using Machine learning approaches within hydrology has gotten a lot of attention in the recent
years. Especially hydrological or hydraulic processes which follow a simple input­output setting
can good be mimicked by NN models (Palmitessa et al., 2022). NN are a subset of machine
learning. An example for an input­output process in hydrology is the urban drainage infrastruc­
ture. A sewer system in connected to certain inflows and redirects them to a outlet at a certain
location and/ or time. Modelling drainage systems through different ML approaches has been
tried. ML models for the sewer system can predict full hydraulics of the system in a significantly
lower simulation time than Hifi models as the findings by Palmitessa et al. (2022) show. Re­
ducing simulation time is the goal of so­called ’surrogate models’. Typically, surrogate models
represent a second level abstraction of the original simulation model to reduce the computation
time (Razavi et al., 2012). Hence, the developed ML model by Palmitessa et al. (2022) is a
special type of surrogate model.
The following section presents relevant knowledge to understand the ML background of the
following research. Firstly, a brief insight into NN is given (see section 3.2.1). Afterwards, the
developed NN model by Palmitessa et al. (2022) is described in section 3.2.2. The devel­
oped NN model is of the type generalized residue network. Lastly, section 3.2.3 presents a
key aspect and benefit of NN models, namely Backpropagation. The subsection explains what
backpropagation is and why it is such a strong tool.

Principle Concepts
The developed surrogate model aims at predicting the water level h in [m] for each node, the
flow Q in [m3/s] for each pipe and the overflow over weirs Qw in [m3/s] for each weir. Thus,
three hydraulic states are considered are system states in this model. Figure 3.3 illustrates the
system states and their interconnection.
Figure 3.3: Figure a) hydraulic states of interest in the urban drainage model are the catchments

node, and the excess flow (Qw). Figure b) in the proposed workflow, the runoff and the initial

Figure 3.3 also outlines the workflow of Palmitessa et al.’s surrogate model. It takes runoff R
as an input and then predicts the three system states level h, flow Q and overflow Qw. The
prediction happens in an autoregressive manner which means that the prediction is made time
step by time step. Thus, the surrogate model gets the runoff for a time step t as input and
evaluates the changes in system states based on this inflow between time step t to t+1. It
learns to predict system state changes between two time steps for a certain inflow. The time
step size is 1 minute for the surrogate model. Mathematically, it can be expressed as change
of state vector x over time:

gResNet set­up
A residue is defined as difference between input and output data (Qin et al., 2019). In the
context of residue networks, a residue to the difference between predicted output data and prior
predicted output data made by another model (also known as labels). A standard feed forward
neural network is used to model the generalized residue and to construct the final prediction
(Chen and Xiu, 2021). Palmitessa et al. propose the following gResNet structure for assessing
the change of state vector x from time t to time t+Δt:
xt+∆t = L(xt, ΘL) + N(xt, ΘN)
(3.10)
with L and N being a deep feed forward neural networks with parameters Θ.
The L is typically defined by a single hidden while the N model formulation depends on the
analysed drainage system complexity.

The state vector x is defined by all relevant system states in the assess drainage model. If

xt = [h1, ..., hN, Q1, ..., QM, Qw,1, ..., Qw,N]
(3.11)
However, the work from Palmitessa et al. shows that learning the overflow behavior of weirs is
difficult for NN. Weir overflows only happen when the water level in the respective node reaches
ground level and the connectes pipes are full. Thus, not enough overflow scenarios happen in
order for the NN model to learn the dynamics. Therefore, Palmitessa et al. removed the overflow
states Qw from the state vector x and calculated them over the mass balance at the respective
node. The updated state vector x is defined the following:
xt = [h1, ..., hN, Q1, ..., QM]
(3.12)
As already illustrated in figure 3.3, the state vector changes depend on the runoff R entering

xt+∆t = L(xt, ΘL) + N(xt, Rt, ΘN)

3.2.3
Backpropagation
Backpropagation is the heart of neural network training. It is the approach how NN find the most
suitable weight parameters Θ. It is the most used training algorithm and widely applied since
the work from Rumelhart et al. in 1986 (Baydin et al., 2018). Also the work done by Palmitessa
et al. (2022) uses backpropagation.
NN training based on backpropagation is an iterative processes build up on in three steps.
At the beginning of the NN training, random weights for the weight parameters Θare created.
Then, an input is passed through a NN defined by the initial weight parameters. This generates
an output. This output is compared to a desired output and an error evaluated. This error is
then back­propagated through the NN to determine a better set of weight parameters Θ. The
aim of the training is to reduce the error between obtained and desired output as much as pos­
sible by changing the weight parameters Θ. This is usually done throughout several iterations.
Consequently, the iterative process has a structure of forward passing, backpropagation and
parameter Θadaptation (Vogl et al., 1988). Knowing the approach, explains the term ’backprop­
agation’. Backpropagation is the short form for backward propagation of errors.
Back­propagating the results through the NN is done by applying the chain rule to compute
partial derivatives. This computes a gradient of the error term in respect to the weights in the
NN. Hence, it is a gradient descent method which searches the NN weight space to find a min­
imum for the error term (Baydin et al., 2018).
Backpropagation is the dominant approach in NN training, as it has strong benefits with are
listed below (Vogl et al., 1988):
• Does not require any knowledge on NN
• Standard process which works well
• Contains no parameter except the number of inputs
• Is a way to directly connect input and output and see how they are interconnected
However, using backpropagation for training is not the only advantage it has. Applying back­
propagation for the calibration of models also works well (see section 3.3).
3.3
Calibration
Computational models for hydrological models consist of numerous input and model parame­
ters. Depending on how complex the analysed process, more or less parameter have to be
included. These parameters have to be either known through experiments and measurements
or they can be indirectly assessed or in the worse case just guessed (Shoarinezhad et al., 2020).
In order to have a useful model, it is important to have parameters which fit to the process they
should describe. Hence, terms like parameter estimation, parameter calibration and parameter
optimization are used to describe methods which should help to make a model more precise.
However, the approaches behind those terms vary greatly.
Parameter estimation is mainly linked to finding the most adequate model structure or dimen­
sions. Hence, parameter estimation happens during the creation of a model. When referring
back to section 3.2.1, finding the suitable weights for a NN, can be seen as parameter estima­
tion.
On the other hand, parameter calibration is the process of model adaption by adjusting inde­
pendent variables to make the model fit a known output better (Shoarinezhad et al., 2020).
Parameter optimization is often linked to either parameter calibration or parameter estimation.
The meaning of parameter optimization is explained later on. The previous two theory section

focused on setting models up and therefore included parameter estimation. The following sub­
section focuses on parameter calibration.
Until a few year ago, parameter calibration in hydrology has been extremely tedious. Hifi mod­
els are mainly calibrated manually. Manual calibration is an iterative trail and error approach to
find improved parameter values (Shoarinezhad et al., 2020). To manually calibration a process
a good understanding and much knowledge about the process is needed. Therefore, it is less
likely to get unrealistic calibrated parameters. Nevertheless, manual calibration is extremely
time and labour intensive (Cornaro et al., 2009).
With an increase of ML models in hydrology and the technical developments in the last years,
automatic calibration has gained more traction. Automatic calibration is much easier and faster
(Cornaro et al., 2009). It relies on mathematical/ statistical methods to find the best parame­
ter value. Mostly within automatic calibration, it is coupled to an optimization algorithm which
tries to minimize the difference between the current model outcome and the desired outcome
(Shoarinezhad et al., 2020). So, an automated parameter calibration uses parameter optimiza­
tion to find the ideal parameter value.
The following subsection 3.3.1 describes different automatic calibration algorithms. The sub­
section outlines the algorithm’s key aspects, advantages and benefits. Afterwards, subsection
3.3.2 goes more in detail on calibrating urban drainage models. The focus is on how catchment
calibration is carried out in literature.
3.3.1
Optimization Algorithms
Typically, automated calibration consists of three parts (Shoarinezhad et al., 2020):
1. Objective function which assesses difference between model outputs and observations
2. Optimization algorithm for adjustment of the to be calibrated model parameters
3. Termination criterion
The choice of objective functions is numerous. Overall, it has to be decided if the calibration
should try to optimize one objective functions or several. Thus, the calibration method can either
be a single­objective calibration method and a multiple­objective one. Examples for objective
functions are the weighted least squares function, the maximum likelihood or the Nash–Sutcliffe
efficiency (Kan et al., 2017).
As calibration methods usually are iterative approaches, it is important to define a termination
criterion which stops the iterations if an optimal value is found. Standard termination criteria
are convergence of objective function, convergence of calibrated parameter or simply setting
maximum iteration steps (Kan et al., 2017).
The choice of optimization algorithm is often case depended on the to be optimized model.

The testing and validation rain series are artificially created rain time series consisting of extreme

4.3.2
Dry Weather Flow Series
As Bellinge is a combined system, DWF is an important water source when considering the full
water amount transported by the Bellinge sewer. DWF varies on an annual, seasonal, daily and
hourly cycle (Butler et al., 1995). Hence, it affects the water amount in a sewer system more or
less depending on the point in time.
For the digital Bellinge UDS, load points are added in Mike+ to the model in order to mimic the
DWF. Thus, DWF is added to nodes as load points are added to nodes. Those load points have
an assigned cyclic behaviour which repeats each day. The DWF of a load point for a day is
determined by an average flow value multiplied by a changing factor throughout the day. The
factor changes every hour in the Mike+ model. Based on the cyclic properties per load point
and the connected load points per node, a dry weather time series for each node is calculated.
The DWF added at node G73F000 during one week is presented as an example in figure 4.7.
The temporal range of the DWF series are matched to fit the applied rainfall series.

5.2.2
Technical Configuration of coupled gResNet Model
As this research is based on work done by Palmitessa et al. (2022), the gResNet is structured
according to the findings from that paper. The NN training happens with a window size of 10
min, whereas the validation uses a window size of 1000 min. The gResNet model gets trained
over a maximum of 20000 epochs. However, an early stopping criteria is implemented which
stops the training after 500 epochs if the validation loss did not improve.
Overall the applied NN has 6 hidden layers with 100 neurons per layer. The widely used Adam
algorithm is utilized as optimizer with a learning rate between 3e­3 and 1e­4. The code is run in
Python 3.8. For the deep learning set­up, Tensorflow version 2.10.0 and tensorflow­probability
version 0.18.0 is used.
5.3
Randomized Training Approach for coupled gResNet Model
As mentioned previously, this research aims to calibrate catchment parameters in surrogate
models, here a gResNet model. This means that catchment parameters get calibrated based on
trained gResNet outcomes and corresponding measurements. Calibrating catchment param­
eters will change the inflow series to the trained gResNet model. However, a gResNet model
is only able to provide sound results for inflow scenarios it has seen during testing. Therefore,
the inflow series during training have to cover a wide variety of values and interlinked runoff

ratios. With the current training approach from Palmitessa et al. (2022), this is not feasible.
Consequently, a new way of training needs to get incorporated (see figure 5.1).
The new training approach needs to ensure that the gResNet model sees sufficient runoff vari­
ation during training. This should be achieved through random variation of catchment areas for
each rain event. Altering the area will impact the effective rainfall respectively runoff, so the
gResNet model can see different combinations of inflows. Runoff through rainfall is the only
water source considered in this model set­up. Hence, the inflow equation for a node i looks like:
Qin,i = QRR,i

presented in section 5.3.2.
The Hifi labels used for the gResNet training and evaluation are also based on the manipu­

simulated to generate labels for the gResNet model as described in section 5.2.1.
5.3.1

As described above, the gResNet model takes modified node inflow series as input data during
training and validation. Those node inflow series base on RR modelling. The approach is

the factor gets changed 203 and 59 times, respectively for these series.
The factors z are sampled independently for each subcatchment, considering a uniform distri­

7
Discussion
The subsequent section discusses the data, methods, and results applied and obtained through­
out this research. This discussion section is structured according to the overall working steps of
this thesis. Hence firstly, RR modelling insights are presented (see section 7.1). These insights
are critically examined and analysed for strengths and weaknesses. Afterwards, the same as­
sessment is repeated for findings connected to the new randomized training approach (see
section 7.2). Then, also the learnings connected to fitting the surrogate model to the Bellinge
framework are examined (see section 7.3). Lastly, the insights gained during calibration are
assessed (see section 7.4).
Each of these subsections emphasises on key findings together with their strengths and weak­
nesses. Firstly, they are listed to give an overview and afterwards explained in detail. The
connected limitations are also outlined.
This chapter is rounded of by giving an outlook for future work based on the gained insights
from this research (see section 7.5).
7.1
RR Modelling
The first step in this research has been to incorporate a RR model to the existing surrogate
model. The chosen model to be implemented is a Nash IUH model. The runoff from catchments
is determined by assessing the lateral inflow towards its respective node. According to the
outcomes presented in section 6.1, the developed RR model performs as good as the Hifi model.
The main insights found by appending the RR model to the surrogate model have been the
following:
• Nash IUH model performs as good as Model A from Mike+
• The bigger the areas of connected catchments to node, the higher the SSE value of that
node
• Lumping of the test case through shifting pipe dynamics into runoff behavior leads to
sufficiently good results
The applied IUH model is a very simple rainfall­runoff conversion approach. It allows to get a
good rough estimate of effective rainfall/ runoff, but neglects many relevant parameters such as
temperature, soil moisture and so on. These parameters all impact processes like initial losses,
infiltration and evapotranspiration which impacts again the runoff to a node. Thus, for a pre­
cise representation of generated runoff, other RR modelling approaches should be explored.
However, the provided digital model of the Bellinge UDS has ’Model A’ as runoff approach im­
plemented. This model is nearly as simple as the applied RR approach and neglects the same
parameters. As the main objective is to generate a surrogate model which is as good in simu­
lations as existing Hifi models, but can be calibrated, it is sufficient if the surrogate RR model is
as good as the Hifi RR model. Nevertheless, when the goal is to calibrate parameters in order
to make the simulated outcomes more accurate in ranges of a few cm or l/s, the potential error
caused by a too simple RR approach cannot be ignored.
Another benefit of incorporating the RR model to the gResNet model is the lumping of the
test case which it enables. The original fine test case of the digital Bellinge UDS is too big for
Palmitessa et al. (2022) surrogate model set­up. So, the test case gets lumped in order to be
an adequate sized test case. The applied lumping approach allows the test case to keep all
relevant catchments and only replace the surplus pipes by incorporating their dynamics into the
52
Calibrating catchment properties in physics­guided machine learning surrogates
==PAGE:65==
runoff behaviour. The gained insights show that lumping a test case in this way to keep some
pipe dynamics included is feasible.
One downside of this approach is that the bigger the connected area to a node, the higher the
SSE scores as found in this research. Hence, the RR model fits the Hifi runoff results less good.
Lumping catchments together additionally increases the connected area to single nodes signif­
icantly. Thus, the applied RR model for these nodes is even less accurate. The importance of
the right size of analysed catchment is much discussed in literature (f.e. Singh, 1998).
The main limitation, which also leads to bigger catchment sizes, is the applied RR model set­
up. The runoff is defined for each node depending on the respective connected areas. The
connected areas to a node are combined and the total runoff to the respective node evaluated.
Instead of evaluating the runoff for each catchment alone and then adding the runoffs up. The
second set­up would increase the numbers of catchments and therefore decrease the catch­
ment sizes. Additionally, it would allow to calibrate each catchment’s properties and not the
average properties belonging to a node. Nevertheless, it is to mention that this approach would
not be compatible with the lumping advantage.
7.2
Randomized Training Approach
The second step has been to apply a new training approach to the coupled gResNet model.
This randomized training approach alters the inflow series and ratios to the gResNet model
throughout the test case randomly in order to train the surrogate model for as many inflow
scenarios as possible. This should enable the surrogate model to predict good and stable
results for nearly all inflow scenarios. The evaluated experiments (see section 6.2) confirm
this hypothesis. Thus, the following findings can be concluded for the new training approach
together with the coupled surrogate model:
• The randomized training approach leads for all tested inflow scenarios to stable and good
results (node and link R2 scores of nearly always above 0.95)
• The randomized trained surrogate model is not as good in handling systematic changes
in connected areas as random changes
• Current randomized training approach works with multiplication factor between 0 and 2
• The coupled gResNet model has to be trained on around 10000 epochs, not 2500
As evaluated in section 6.2 and indicated by the key insights, there are several strengths, weak­
nesses and limitations connected to the applied randomized training approach.
Overall, the randomized training approach leads to good and stable simulation results for all
tested inflow scenarios. This means that a surrogate model trained in such a way can be used
for calibration as it should be able to handle all runoff scenarios applied during calibration.
Nevertheless, the randomized training approach can still be improved. One weakness is that
it cannot handle systematic area changes extremely good. Therefore, it would make sense to
not only train the surrogate model with randomly changing inflow scenarios, but also with sys­
tematical changing inflow scenarios.
The biggest limitation of the proposed method is the range of the applied multiplication factor
to effective areas during training. The author has chosen the limits between 0 and 2 arbitrarily.
The minimum value of 0 is set, because catchments in the countryside could have an imper­
viousness of zero and consequently no connected runoff. The Bellinge test case is located in
a rural area which made 0 as minimum value a plausible factor. The maximum multiplication
factor of 2 could be further analysed. Connected effective areas are typically determined by
Calibrating catchment properties in physics­guided machine learning surrogates
53
==PAGE:66==
maps and other knowledge of an area. Therefore, it is hard to find the correct effective area,
but it is possible to assess a good approximation. A multiplication factor of 2 equals an area
increase of 100%. Thus, the author’s mindset has been that the effective areas after calibration
would not be more than double the assumed size.
However, it could be argued that a higher multiplication factor would allow to train the surrogate
model for even more potential inflow scenarios. Yet, the trained inflow scenarios still need to be
within reasonable ranges for the simulated test case. The uniform distribution of the multiplica­
tion factors should be enough to experience all relevant inflow scenarios during training.
Lastly, in previous research connected to gResNet modelling, the training process consists of
maximum 2500 epochs with the same test case size. All trainings carried out with the coupled
gResNet model need at least around 10000 epochs to find the best set of NN weights. There­
fore, it would be recommended for future work on the gResNet model to work with significantly
more than 2500 epochs.
7.3
Setting Surrogate into Bellinge Framework
Before calibrating the surrogate model, the newly developed coupled gResNet model needs
to be set in context to the Bellinge UDS. As described in section 5.4, the surrogate model
needs to include boundary inflows and dry weather flows besides the rain runoff. Three different
coupled gResNet set­ups adapted to the Bellinge test case have been analysed. Section 6.3
evaluates that the surrogate set­up with an artificial boundary inflow and a training rain series
with prolonged dry periods, leads to the best simulated results considering all water sources.
The analysis of these results leads to following key aspects:
• Better to use artificial boundary time series as Hifi series as the range and distribution of
covered inflow values can be ensured
• Including longer dry weather periods between rain events leads to better simulation results
• DWF implemented in the digital model can be included into the surrogate model, but if
the DWF is badly represented in the Hifi model the mistakes get incorporated into the
surrogate model (f.e: seasonal variation)
• The different inflow series depending on the water source applied for training are strongly
depending on the test case
• For calibration a node with a long measurement series is needed, but through the limitation
caused by the current gResNet model abilities, the number of potential test cases are small
All key insights are again connected to different strengths, weaknesses and limitations.
Firstly, sound simulation results for the coupled gResNet model have been achieved when the
upstream inflow series has been incorporated through an artificial inflow series during surrogate
training. Using an artificial series ensures that the model can handle all possible flow dynamics.
For the Bellinge test case, inflow during rain events, no inflow or inflow during dry periods could
potentially happen. Thus, a time series including these flow scenarios is created (see section
4.3.3). For example, the dry period training inflow series bases on Hifi simulation outcomes for
dry weather periods and the amplitude of the sinus curve for the training inflow series during rain
depends on the maximum observed inflow for the strongest rain event. Hence, the generated
boundary series is test case depended and not in general applicable. However, the approach to
build a boundary series based on inflow series during rain events, zero inflow series and inflow
series during dry periods can be generalised. Overall, this way of training an upstream inflow
performs well for the analysed test case.

The second considered water source is rainfall.
The applied rainfall series during training
and validation for FluxesArt, dry experiment, which leads to the best results, has prolonged dry
weather periods between rain events. With the previous training and validation rain series, the
dry weather periods have been too short to empty out the drainage system up to the dry weather
water amount between events. However, the calibration rain series contains many periods with
dry weather flow and therefore the surrogate model needs to learn how the drainage system
behaves when it only contains DWF. That the drainage system has not enough time to empty
itself between events, can overall impact the baseline flows and levels predictions. Therefore,
it would make sense to increase the dry weather periods between rain events for all training
series used to generate a gResNet model.
The third and final water source is the DWF. The DWF is unique for each node as the DWF
depends on connected households and industries to a node. The DWF typically shows daily,
seasonal and annual variation which is also noticeable in the measured level at node G73F010.
However, the provided digital Hifi model only includes a daily variation. The surrogate model is
always only has good as the provided digital model in simulating DWF. Therefore, developing
another method to better predict the DWF in the surrogate model could be interesting.
It can be concluded that considering other water sources than runoff into a test case is ex­
tremely case based and hard to generalise. Hence, assessing additional inflow fluxes besides
rainfall to a coupled gResNet model needs to be carried out depending on the test case.
Additionally, the aim to calibrate a test case based on its surrogate simulation results limits
the potential test cases at the moment significantly. Firstly, a detailed digital model including
all water bodies impacting the sewer needs to exist. Then, a stable measurement series for a
node or link within the digital model over at least a few months is needed. Furthermore, the test
case should have not more than 100 links and 100 nodes as the applied gResNet model from
Palmitessa et al. (2022) is developed for drainage systems in this scale. It also cannot include
any special structures like pumps and storage units as these have not been evaluated by the
gResNet model yet.
7.4
Catchment Properties Calibration
After successfully setting up approaches which fulfill the requirements for calibration, the de­
veloped surrogate model for the Bellinge test case can be calibrated regarding its catchment
properties. The results obtained through calibrating the effective areas are presented in section
6.4. The calibration process and the results lead to following key findings:
• Calibrating the effective areas brings the simulated level results in node G73F010 closer
to the measurements
• One calibration iteration takes around 90 seconds which is mainly caused by the gResNet
simulation time (70 seconds)
• Number of calibration iterations currently set to an arbitrary guess of 750
• Limitations for calibrated parameters are currently set to ±50% based on their original
value
Also these key observations are assessed regarding their strengths, weaknesses and limita­
tions.
The applied heuristic calibration method simulates the coupled gResNet for each effective area

guess. One calibration iteration takes around 90 seconds where around 70 seconds are spend
on simulating the gResNet model. A simulation time of around 70 seconds for the coupled
gResNet model fits with the literature value (see Palmitessa et al., 2022). Hence, running a

Another limitation of the applied calibration approach is the random decision on the number
of calibration iterations. The chosen number could either be too high or too low. The chosen
750 iterations seem adequate for the calibration in this research. However, implementing an
early stopping criteria based on the SSE value development over iterations could be easily in­
cluded to improve the calibration set­up.
The most important limitation of the applied calibration is the set range of maximum ±50% ef­
fective area change during calibration. This means that the original effective areas connected
to a node can be only in­ or decreased by 50 % throughout the calibration. As 20 % of the
nodes reach this limit, the chosen limit range can be questioned. The ranges for potential ef­
fective areas would need to be increased to allow the model to find the optimum set of effective
areas. However, effective areas are limited due to their geographic properties and catchment
sizes. Therefore, it is questionable how much in­ and decrease is reasonable for effective areas.
Changing the effective areas strongly during calibration could also indicate that the surrogate
model tries to balance out another model error. Nevertheless, assessing the impact of changing
the limits could be analysed.
7.5
Research outlook
This final subsection focuses on further research which can be carried out building up on this
project. Section 7.1 to 7.4 already outline the key findings from this research. The sections also
discuss how weaknesses and limitations corresponding to the key findings could be improved.
Thus, these points all present starting points for future work on this topic. However, the pro­
posed improvements or further analysis are connected to tuning the coupled gResNet model
which works already well.
The most interesting starting point for future work on this research project is not along the work­
ing steps to create a surrogate model which can be calibrated, it is the calibration step itself.
This research shows that calibration of a surrogate model is possible and leads to reasonable
results. However, the project does not go further into how calibrating surrogate models could
best work.
Numerous different calibration approaches exist. Section 3.3 gives a short overview. All these
and many more calibration methods could be analysed. However, the most interesting calibra­
tion approach is probably using backpropagation.
The ability to use backpropagation is one of the major advantages of the developed gResNet
model by Palmitessa et al. (2022). Therefore, it makes sense to exploit this benefit for cali­
bration as well. Through backpropagation, gradients between the simulated results and input
parameters can be determined. Afterwards, these gradients could be optimized based on, f.e:
an Adam optimizer to find the calibrated input parameters. This would remove simulating the
surrogate model from the calibration approach and therefore, accelerate the calibration process
significantly.
Besides changing the overall calibration approach, there are also several other interesting pos­

objective function is one of these possibilities. For now a single­objective optimization based

Another interesting aspect would be to change the test case. This new test case should include

Conclusion
For important urban planning challenges of the 21st century, existing Hifi models are reaching
their limitations. The long simulation time does hardly allow testing numerous set­ups, parame­
ter calibration or real­time decision making. Thus, ML simulation models have been explored as
alternative. Research leads to ML models which show a significant speed­up in simulation time
without loosing accuracy and details in their simulation results compared to Hifi models. One
example is the gResNet model developed by Palmitessa et al. (2022). The main purpose of this
study was to calibrate parameters based on the surrogate model to showcase one important
strength of such ML models.
In more detail, the aim of this study was to calibrate catchment properties based on an de­
veloped coupled gResNet model. The work shows that calibrating surrogate parameters is
feasible when certain prerequisites are fulfilled by the surrogate model. The overall workflow
starting from the existing gResNet model to parameter calibration contains four main steps. The
following is a brief review of the four steps and thus, summarizes the work carried out in this
research:
1. Appending a Nash IUH RR model to the gResNet model which enables the surrogate
model to perform rainfall­runoff assessment (coupled gResNet model)
2. Operate a randomized training approach during surrogate training to ensure that the sur­
rogate model can handle a big variety of different inflow scenarios
3. Setting the newly developed coupled gResNet model into the Bellinge framework to as­
sure that all relevant water sources like inflows and DWF are correctly considered in the
surrogate model
4. Calibration of effective areas from catchments through a global heuristic approach, DDS
Carried out these four steps leads to various results and insights, the key findings are presented
below:
• A simple Nash IUH model is as good as a simple Hifi RR model
© Hifi RR model can be replaced by the surrogate RR model
• The developed randomized training approach leads for all tested inflow scenarios to stable
and good results
© It enables the surrogate model to handle all potential inflow scenarios it may experi­
ence (especially during calibration)
• Including all potential water sources from a test case is highly case dependent
© The proposed way to include boundary inflow series and DWF only works for Bellinge
• Replacing other water sources during surrogate training to artificial series gives better
control over trained inflow range
© Artificial inflow series during training can more easily be generalised
• Calibrating the effective areas brings the simulated level results in the sensor node closer
to the measurements
© Calibrating catchment properties leads to expected outcomes and is reasonably fast
58

© DDS is a safe first calibration approach, but relatively slow; calibration through back­
propagation should work better
Overall, the newly developed gResNet model for the Bellinge test case creates good and stable
simulation results. This work indicates that calibrating catchments properties leads to surrogate
simulated results which fit the reality better. With a relatively small expenditure of time, it is
possible to calibrate the coupled gResNet model. Hence, this research is a basis towards
calibration of NN models. Assessing a suitable calibration approach for such ML models would
be an interesting next research step.
