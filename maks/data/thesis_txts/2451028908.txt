Abstract 
Production of fish in aquaculture could be a solution to produce food for the growing population. 
However, there are challenges such as diseases, in producing fish in aquaculture. Rainbow trout 
fry syndrome is a disease that affects rainbow trout fry. The causative agent of this disease is 
Flavobacterium psychrophilum. Treatment of rainbow trout fry syndrome with antibiotics has 
been used for many years, but gradually there have been problems with resistant strains of F. 
psychrophilum and new treatment methods must be found. Bacteriophages or phages are viruses, 
that only infect bacteria, after which the phage multiply in the bacterium until the bacterium 
burst. Phages are therefore an opportunity for treatment of infections with F. psychrophilum. The 
aim of this project was to see if it was possible to treat F. psychrophilum with phages in rainbow 
trout egg and fry and to examined if phages could be found in organs from fry fed with phage-
coated feed and phage-sprayed feed. Fry was either fed phage-coated, phage-sprayed feed or 
control feed. There might be phages in the fry fed with phage-coated feed and phage-sprayed 
feed, however it looked like there were phages in fry fed with control feed. When fry were 
infected with F. psychrophilum, the mortality was very low in all groups and a definitive answer 
could not be given. It was also investigated, whether it was possible to increase survival for eggs 
by treating them with phages. Here it turned out that the concentration of F. psychrophilum on 
eggs did not diminish when treated with phage. However, it could not be ruled out that this was 
due to egg contamination, so a definitive conclusion could not be made.

1. Introduction  
1.1 Background and aim  
The world population is increasing fast and is estimated to reach 9.6 billion by 2050. Hence, due to 
the increasing population, the amount of food needed is increasing as well. As of now, It is not 
enough just to produce food on land, since this cannot support the increasing demand from the  
worlds growing population ( (Maribus, 2013); (FAO, 2016); (Kobayashi, et al., 2015)). Since the 
beginning of the 1950s, there have been an increase in global fisheries production until the late 
1980s where it began to level off and stagnating and has been more or less stable ever since (FAO, 
2016).  
 
Figure 1 : World capture fisheries and aquaculture production. The world catch has over the last two decades leveled off and 
stagnated, while production in aquaculture has increased (FAO, 2016).  
In 2016, 90.9 million tonnes of fish were caught in the oceans and inland waters. This is a decrease 
compared to the previous two years. A five-year plan has been drawn up to reduce catches from 
the oceans, which states that a reduction of 5 million tonnes of fish on a global level is expected to 
be reached in 2050 (FAO, 2016). 
Production of fish in aquaculture is one of the ways to potentially solve the problem of 
overexploitation of the oceans and in inland waters, whilst providing food for the growing 
population. During the 1980s and 1990s, world aquaculture production increased by 10.8 percent 
and 9.5 percent respectively, however, this decreased again. Between 2001 and 2016 aquaculture 
increased by 5.8 percent (Figure 1). In 2016, global aquaculture production was 110.2 million 
==PAGE:7==

tonnes, of which 80 million tonnes are used as food for fish. Of the total production of fish, 
including both catches and aquaculture, aquaculture was accountable for 46.8 percent in 2016, 
whilst in 37 countries more fish were produced in aquaculture than caught. In total, 51.4 million 
tonnes of fish were produced by aquaculture in inland waters, which corresponds to 64.2 percent 
of total aquaculture production (FAO, 2016).  
Denmark has a long tradition of producing rainbow trout. Production of rainbow trout in both 
seawater and freshwater, is between 40,000 and 50,000 tons per year. This makes rainbow trout 
the species that is the most produced in Denmark. Approximately 60% of all fish farmed are 
rainbow trout ( (Eurofish, 2019), (Musholm, 2019)). 
Aquaculture has many advantages, but there are also problems with this way of producing fish. 
The problems include; environmental impacts and diseases. Although, aquaculture uses nutrient-
rich effluents, wastage is still occurring in nature, which can have some negative effects on the 
environment (Maribus, 2013). Diseases in aquaculture is also a major problem, because there is a 
high transmission of pathogens leading to disease outbreaks and reduced survival, which leads to 
economical loses (Kobayashi, et al., 2015).  
This project is part of a larger project, which is financed by BONUS. BONUS is an organization that 
was formed by BONUS member states together with the EU. One of the many projects that 
BONUS is currently involved in, is whether bacteriophages can be used to treat Flavobacterium 
psychrophilum, which among others, affects the species rainbow trout (Onchorhynchus mykiss) 
(Middelboe M. , 2017).  
The aim of this project is:  
Flavobacterium psychrophilum in rainbow trout eggs and fry. First, it aims to examined whether it 
is possible to treat infected eggs with bacteriophages to reduce the mortality of eggs and larvae. 
Subsequently, it will be examined whether it is possible to find bacteriophages in fry where 
bacteriophages have been given by either being coated on the feed or sprayed on the feed and 
finally, an infection experiment will be performed to see if phages can be used to treat F. 
psychrophilum.

1.2 Rainbow trout fry syndrome (RTFS)  
When an outbreak of a disease strikes a fish farm, this can cause major economic losses. One of 
the diseases that can affect fish farms is rainbow trout fry syndrome (RTFS).  
Borg (1948) was the first to discover, that there was a 
bacterium that caused disease in salmonid fish when the 
temperature was 15 degrees or below (Borg, 1948). It was later 
found, that it was Flavobacterium psychrophilum which caused 
the disease in juvenile rainbow trout. Thus, the disease was 
named; rainbow trout fry syndrome (RTFS) ( (Lorenzen, et al., 
1991); (Madsen & Dalsgaard, 1998)). It is usually only fry that 
are affected by F. psychrophilum, while larger fish, around 5 
month and older are less affected by F. psychrophilum. 
Nevertheless, in fry, mortality as high as 90% have been 
observed in early studies. The immune system of fry is not fully 
developed during the first 2 months of feeding and therefore 
many outbreaks occur in that period ( (Nielsen, et al., 2011); 
(Decostere, D'Haese, Lammens, Nelis, & Haesebrouck, 2001)). F. psychrophilum have been 
detected in rainbow trout eggs and it is suspected that F. psychrophilum can be transmitted with 
vertical transmission (Kumagai & Nawata, 2010). Some of the typical clinical signs of RTFS is 
lethargy, loss of appetite, dark coloration of the skin, accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity, 
protrusion of the eyes and anemia manifested in pale gills and organs. In larger fish, skin lesions 
might appear ( (Lorenzen, et al., 1991); (Dalsgaard, 1993) and Figure 2).  
 
1.3 Flavobacterium psychrophilum  
The bacterium that causes Rainbow trout fry syndrome was first categorized as Cytophaga 
psychrophila. This was based on the biochemical criteria (Borg, 1948). C. psychrophila was later re-
categorized as Flexibacter psychrophilus based on DNA homology (Bernardet & Grimont, 1989). In 
1996, F. psychrophilus was transferred to the family Flavobacteriaceae and named Flavobacterium 
psychrophilum based on DNArRNA hybridization (Bernardet, et al., 1996).

1.3.1 Morphology and biochemical characteristics 
F. psychrophilum is Gram-negative, nonmotile rods that is 1 to 5 µm long and 0.3 to 0.5 µm wide in 
48-h liquid cultures. In some cases, some of the cells can grow even wider, up to 12 µm. The 
colonies are circular and smooth; however, most strains are also able to produce colonies with 
narrow and unevenly spreading edges. Colonies are bright yellow and non-diffusible flexirubin 
type pigments are present. The optimum growth temperature is between 15-18 °C. The bacterium 
is strictly aerobic and catalase and cytochrome oxidase positive (Bernardet & Grimont, 1989). and 
Madsen and Dalsgaard (1998) tested 299 different isolates of F. psychrophilum and found that the 
strains could degrade albumin, casein, collagen, fibrinogen, gelatin and hemoglobin. It was also 
found that only 9 of the isolates couldn’t degrade elastin (Madsen & Dalsgaard, 1998).  
1.3.2 Serotype 
Lorenzen and Olsen (1997) described three serotypes of F. psychrophilum: Th, Fd and FpT. Th was a 
major serotype and was divided into two serotypes: Th- 1, a major serotype and Th-2, a minor 
subtype. The serotypes Fd and FpT were both found to be minor serotypes (Lorenzen & Olesen, 
1997). Wakabayashi et al. (1994) had previously found that there were to serotypes O-1 and O-2. 
O-1 belonged to coho salmon (Oncorhynchus kisutch) in Japan and USA and O-2 occurred only in 
Japan in rainbow trout and ayu (Plecoglossus altivelis) (Wakabayashi, Toyama, & Iida, 1994). Later, 
Izumi et al. (1999) found an additional serotype O-3. They found, that serotype O-2 was the 
serotype specific for strains from ayu and serotype O-3 were specific for rainbow trout (Izumi & 
Wakabayashi, 1999). Dalsgaard and Madsen investigated this further and found, that the serotype 
Th was the dominant serotype. They also found, that in disease outbreaks in fry and larger fish 
both serotypes Th and Fd were involved. Furthermore, all isolates of F. psychrophilum showed 
proteolytic activities. However, some isolates with serotype FpT did not degrade elastin and were 
not associated with mortality (Dalsgaard & Madsen, Bacterial pathogens in rainbow trout, 
Oncorhynchus mykiss (Walbaum), reared at Danish freshwater farms, 2000).  
1.3.3 Virulence 
Eric Duchaud et al. (2007) was the first to make a complete genome sequence of a virulent strain 
of F. psychrophilum. It was found, that the genome consists of a 2,861,988–base pair (bp) and 
predicted that there are 2432 protein-coding genes. Some of these predicted genes are stress 
response mediators, adhesins and many putative secreted proteases, which are probably involved 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
in colonization, invasion and destruction of the host tissues (Duchaud, et al., 2007). In 2016, Daniel 
Castillo et al. made a comparative genome analyses of 11 F. psychrophilum to examine, among 
others, virulence factors. The results of the comparison showed that all F. psychrophilum isolates 
showed similar content and distribution of adhesion and metalloproteases proteins (Castillo, et al., 
2016).  Elastinolytic enzymes have also been proven to be a virulence factor for F. psychrophilum. 
The encoded protein is predicted to be a cell-surface-exposed lipoprotein with no homology to 
previously reported elastases (Rochat, et al., 2019). 
1.4 Methods used for detection, isolation and characterization of fish pathogens  
Microorganisms can be both beneficial and harmful. During a disease outbreak, it is important to 
find the pathogenic bacteria that caused the outbreak, so that a treatment can be initiated for the 
animals affected, to stop it from spreading further and ultimately stopping the outbreak of the 
disease. There can be used a polyphasic approach to identify bacteria. Polyphasic approach consist 
of phenotypic and genotypic methods. Some of the phenotypic methods are: morphology, 
motility, metabolism, physiology, cell lipid chemistry etc. Some of the genotypic methods are: 
Multilocus sequence typing, genome fingerprinting, genome GC contrast, DNA-DNA-hybridizing 
amongst other methods (Madigan, Martinko, Bender, Buckley, & Stahl, 2015).  
1.5. Prevention and treatment of RTFS 
As mentioned earlier, RTFS is caused by F. psychrophilum, a disease that cause mortality in 
rainbow trout fry, which leads to economic loses for fish farmers. Prevention and treatment of 
RTFS is therefore necessary.  
The first step in preventing RTFS is to disinfect rainbow trout eggs for F. psychrophilum (Lapatra, 
2000). It has been found, that iodophor, Betadine or Wescodyne was able to disinfect rainbow 
trout eggs for F. psychrophilum ( (Ross & Smith, 1972); (Brown, Cox, & Levinel, 1997)). However, 
the disinfection is not always sufficient enough to prevent the disease entirely, so outbreaks may 
still occur. 
1.5.1 Antibiotics 
For many years, antibiotics was used to control RTFS. Some of the first antibiotics for oral 
administration, for fish, to be investigated was sulfonamides, specifically sulfisoxazole and 
sulfamethazine ( (Amend, Fryer, & Pilcher, 1965); (Amend, 1970)). Some of the antibiotics that 
have been used through the years is Nifurpirinol, Sulfonamides, Oxytetracycline (OTC), Amoxycillin 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
(AMX), Oxolinic acid (OXA), Sulfadiazin/trimethoprim (S/T) and Florfenicol (FLO) (Barnes & Brown, 
2011); (Bruun, Schmidt, Madsen, & Dalsgaard, 2000)).  
Bruun et al. (2000) Isolated 387 strains of F. psychrophilum over a period from 1994 to 1998. The 
strains were tested for their susceptibility towards the antibiotics OTC, S/T, FLO, OXA. The results 
indicated that F. psychrophilum were able to acquire resistance to OXA, OTC and AMX. All isolates 
were shown to be susceptible to FLO (Bruun, Schmidt, Madsen, & Dalsgaard, 2000). Boyacioglu 
and Akar (2012) tested in vitro 20 different isolates from a large fish hatchery located in the west 
Aegean region of Turkey against ampicillin (AMP), amoxicillin-clavulanic acid (AMC), enrofloxacin 
(ENR), erythromycin (E), florfenicol (FFC), gentamicin (CN), oxytetracycline (OT), penicillin G (P), 
ciprofloxacin (CIP), and sulfamethoxazole-trimethoprim (SXT) by disk diffusion method test. By this 
test F. psychrophilum was susceptible to oxytetracycline, enrofloxacin, ciprofloxacin and 
florfenicol. These was then tested in vivo, where oxytetracycline, enrofloxacin and ciprofloxacin 
could not display enough efficacy because of resistance. Florfenicol showed much higher efficacy 
and in the end of the experiment the death rate caused by the infection was decreased 
significantly (Boyacioglu & Akar, 2012). In Denmark, only S/T and OXA are approved for the 
treatment of fish in aquaculture (Aarestrup, Olesen, Dalsgaard, & Madsen, 2019). In Denmark, 
only FLO can be used to treat RTFS in fry (Dalsgaard, Personlig meddelse). 
1.5.2 Vaccine 
 For many years it was not possible to produce a vaccine against F. psychrophilum. One of the 
reasons for this, is that the vaccine should be given early in the fry stage as possible, which is not 
possible because the immune system has not yet been developed, which compromises the 
usefulness of the vaccine (Gómez, Méndez, Cascales, & Guijarro, 2013). Furthermore, it is also 
important to think about how to administrate a possible vaccine. Because of the small size of fry, it 
may only be economic and practical if a possible vaccine were given by immersion or in-feed 
(Gómez, Méndez, Cascales, & Guijarro, 2013). Over the years, there have been several attempts to 
produce a vaccine, that can provide protection against F. psychrophilum. However there hasn’t 
been sufficient evidence that the vaccines has provided a sufficient result,  ( (Makesh, Sudheesh, & 
Cain, 2015), (Sudheesh, Zimmerman, & Cain, 2016), (Hoare, Ngo, Bartie, & Adams, 2017)). 
However, it appears that a vaccine against F. psychrophilum has been successfully produced for 
Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar L.) and it is the hope that this vaccine can be used against RTFS in 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
rainbow trout fry also. The vaccine is a polyvalent, whole cell vaccine containing formalin-inactivated F. 
psychrophilum with an adjuvant containing squalene and aluminium hydroxide (Hoare, et al., 2017).  
1.5.3 Bacteriophages 
In 1915 William Twort discovered bacteriophages. However, it was Felix d´Herelle in 1917 who 
discovered the properties of the bacteriophages, namely to be able to kill bacteria (Sulakvelidze, 
Alavidze, Glenn, & Jr, 2001). Bacteriophages, or phages, are viruses that are capable of infecting 
bacteria. Bacteriophages can either have doublestranded DNA (dsDNA) which most 
bacteriophages have, but they can also have single-stranded DNA (ssDNA), single-stranded RNA 
(ssRNA), and double-stranded RNA (dsRNA). Phages can vary widely in DNA content and 
composition, genome structure, proteins and biological properties. Bacteriophages are found in 
various sizes and structures and most phages are tailed. Tailed phages are divided into three 
families: Myoviridae, Siphoviridae and Podoviridae and they are considered virulent (Clokie & 
Kropinski, 2009). Phages have different life cycles. In the lytic life cycle, phages infect bacteria, 
after which they replicate themselves inside their host. When there are enough new phages, the 
cell is burst open and new phages are released so they can further infect bacteria. This is what 
makes phages natural killers of bacteria. For phages to replicate, they must use the host 
biosynthetic machinery (Koskella & Brockhurst, 2014), (Weinbauer, 2004).  
Due to the increasing antibiotic resistance, the development of bacteriophage therapy is 
increasing in a number of purposes such as control of infections in high density poultry production 
and for the treatment of fish pathogens in aquaculture ( (Nakai & Park, 2002), (Oliveira J. , 
Castilho, Cunha, & Pereira, 2012); (Park & Nakai, 2003), (Park, Shimamura, Fukunaga, Mori, & 
Naka, 2000), (Huff, Huff, Rath, Balog, & Donoghue, 2002)). These studies all show promising uses 
of phages for both treatment and prevention of pathogenic bacteria. For phage therapy to work, it 
is important to; choose the right phages, give the optimal dose of phage and give the treatment at 
the right time, otherwise phage therapy treatment will not be successful. Bacteriophages have 
very specific properties, which make them ideal for use in therapy for pathogenic bacteria. 
Properties which phages uses to fight against pathogenic bacteria are bactericidal, low intrinsic 
toxicity, minimal disruption of normal flora, and lack of cross-resistance to antibiotics (Ryan, 
Gorman, Donnelly, & Gilmore, 2011). It is important to constantly develop and find new phages. 
This can be done from the natural environment where its host is found and is self-proliferating.  
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
There are different ways to deliver phages to fish in aquaculture. In experimental animal studies 
parenteral delivery is the most popular and successful of all delivery methods, because of the 
delivery of phages directly in the circulatory system. Other delivery methods are intramuscular 
(IM), subcutaneous (SC), intraperitoneal (IP) and oral. Oral delivery has been proven successful in 
treatment of gastrointestinal infections and in some cases systemic diseases. However, it can be 
problematic to deliver phages orally through feed, as it cannot be ensured that all fish get enough 
feed to rise the desired concentration of phages in the fry/fish. (Ryan, Gorman, Donnelly, & 
Gilmore, 2011).  
Although bacteriophages contain many possibilities, there are still some challenges. One of the 
major challenges, is that pathogenic bacteria develop resistance towards phages. It is therefore 
important to have an understanding of which mechanisms the bacteria use to combat 
bacteriophages, in order to overcome these challenges. Bacteria, that are sensitive to phages, can 
become resistant when exposed to selection pressure from lytic phages ( (Middelboe M. , 2000), 
(Middelboe, Holmfeldt, Riemann, Nybroe, & Haaber, 2009)). Some of the resistant mechanism are; 
prevention of phage adsorption, degrade phage DNA inside the cell and/or block replication and 
mechanisms that lead to cell death, thereby protecting neighboring cells from further infection 
(Christiansen, 2014).

2. Materials and methods 
Two different experiments were conducted. The first experiment examined whether phages could 
be used to treat infection with F. psychrophilum. Rainbow trout was fed with phage-coated feed, 
phage-sprayed feed and with control feed to investigate whether it was possible to find phages in 
the following organs: brain, spleen, kidney and intestines. Afterwards, rainbow trout fry were 
infected with F. psychrophilum to examine mortality in fry in the various groups. In the second 
experiment, eggs were infected with F. psychrophilum to examine whether it is possible for F. 
psychrophilum to infect rainbow trout eggs. Rainbow trout eggs were exposed to the bath 
challenge with F. psychrophilum, after which they were incubated until they hatched. During this 
time samples were taken every 24 hours. In the last part of experiment with eggs, a group of eggs 
were exposed to phages, to see if F. psychrophilum infection in the eggs could be controlled by 
phage. 
In Denmark legislation in relation to animal tests that must be followed. This legislation helps to 
ensure; that animals do not suffer from the trials and that the trials are conducted properly 
(Schubart, 2019).  All relevant laws and regulations were upheld during the duration and 
completion of the trials. 
2.1 Fry fed with phage FPSV-D22 
Fry fed with phage-coated feed, spay-coated feed and control feed: Fry with a mean weight of 
1.5 g were divided into 3 groups in 12 aquariums by Ph. D. student Valentina Donati. Rainbow 
trout eyed eggs were provided by Trouex APS. The eggs were 200 day degrees, when they were 
delivered to DTU were they hatched and grew until the fry weighted about 1.5 g. The phage there 
were used in all experiment in this project were FPSV-D22, which is a Danish phage recently 
isolated. FPSV-D22 belongs to the Siphoviridae family and is a lytic phage. The feed was produced 
by Fixed phages and Fry were fed with 2 % of their body weight. Fry in aquarium 1 and 22-24 were 
fed control feed, fry in aquariums 2 and 4-6 were fed phage-coated feed and fry in 3 and 19-21 
were fed phage-sprayed feed. 
Sampled fish: Fry were sampled on day 3, 10, 24 and 31 after feeds were introduced. 5 fish were 
sampled from aquarium 4, 19 and 22. The fry were killed with an overdose of anesthetic 3-
aminobenzoic acid ethyl ester (MS-222). Fry was cut opened along the belly, just before the anus, 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
using a small scissors. Carefully, samples were taken for the biological examination of the spleen. 
Subsequently, the intestine was removed from the abdomen gently and its content was pressed 
out. The swim bladder was removed, so that a sample could be taken for biological examination of 
the kidney. The kidney was sampled for further study. The head was cut off and a sample was 
taken for biological examination. The brain was sampled for further investigation. All organs were 
stored in a pre-weighted Eppendorf tube with 150-300 µl SM-buffer (appendix 1). The Eppendorf 
were weighted before 5 µl chloroform was added and spun down in a mini centrifuge for 10 
seconds. The organs were stored at 5 °C until used.  
Experimental fry examined for pathogens: Samples were taken from the organs and plated on 
Tryptone Yeast Extract Salts (TYES) agar (appendix 1) and blood agar (appendix 1), in order to 
examine whether there were F. psychrophilum and other pathogens present in fry. The plates 
were incubated for 5-10 days at 15 °C.  
Injection challenge: Culture for infection trial was made by Ph. D. student Valentina Donati. F. 
psychrophilum strain 950106-1/1 was used for challenge of rainbow trout fry. The strain was pre-
cultivated in 5 ml TYES broth (appendix 1) and incubated at 15°C and 115 rpm for 72 h. After 72 h 
of incubation, 0.5 ml of the F. psychrophilum culture was inoculated into 100 ml TYES broth for 
48h at 15°C and 115 rpm. The culture was adjusted to 1X 105. Fry were infected with F. 
psychrophilum strain 950106-1/1 on day 33. Before injection, all fry were anaesthetized with MS-
222. Afterwards, fry were injected intraperitoneal with either 0.1 ml 1x105 whole cell F. 
psychrophilum culture (aquarium 4-6 and 19-24) or 0.1 ml sterile TYES (control) (aquarium 1-3) 
(Madsen & Dalsgaard, 1999). It was my supervisor Lone Madsen and Ph. D. student Valentina 
Donati who injected the cultures into fry. I helped catch and anaesthetize the fry.  
Double layer plaque assay for phages detection:  
Plates: Top agar was melted and cooled to a temperature around 45 °C. Hereafter, 4 ml top agar 
were poured up in sterile tubes, which were placed in water bath at 45 °C. Then, 300 µl 48h F. 
psychrophilum culture were mixed with top agar in a tube, vortexed and poured directly in TYES 
agar. The plates solidified before they were stored in an incubator at 15 °C until used later that day 
(Madsen, Bertelsen, Dalsgaard, & Middelboe, 2013). 
==PAGE:16==

Feed: Feed were tested to see if phage FPSV-D22 were present; 0.1 g of feed was sprinkled over 
the plates. A positive and negative control was added. The plates were incubated at 15 °C for 5-10

4. Discussion  
Rainbow trout fry were divided in 3 different groups; phage-coated feed, phage-sprayed feed and 
control feed. Length and weight were examined by fry to see if there was a correlation between 
which feed the fry got and how much they grew. In Figure 7, it can be seen, that fry fed with 
control feed grow faster than fry fed with phage-coated feed. This may be due to the fact that the 
fry sampled in the control feed group were some of the largest in the aquarium compared to fry 
sampled in the phage-coated feed group, where it may have been the smallest fry in the aquarium 
there was sampled. Another possibility may be that the phage on the phage-coated feed can have 
a nutritional effect on the feed, so that the energy density in the feed is reduced.  
Double layer assay was performed to detect phages from brain, kidney, spleen and intestine in fry. 
Double layer assay from day 10 and day 31 after start of feeding with different feed were 
performed twice. The first double layer assay from day 10 didn’t provide any clear spot, however 
second double layer assay from day 10 showed clear spot in phage-coated fed fry (aquarium 4), 
phage-sprayed fed fry (aquarium 19) and control fed fry (aquarium 22) in intestine, spleen, kidney 
and brain in various fry. Double layer assay performed on day 31 phages were detected in 
intestine for all groups. The second double layer assay showed phages in intestine for all the 
groups and in brain, spleen and kidney as well for fry fed phage-coated feed. 
Many studies have been performed on phage therapy. Both for the treatment of Vibrio and other 
pathogens in fish. Christiansen et al. (2014) examined phage-coated feed in rainbow trout to see 
for how long time it was possible to detect phages in the organs. There were three different 
groups, phages-coated feed through the experiment, phages-coated feed for 24 h and a control 
group. The fish were sampled for 384 h (16 days). It was found that there were phages in the 
intestine in all samplings from hour 1 and throughout the experiment for fish fed continuously 
with phage-coated feed. Phages were detected in kidney and spleen also, but through the 
samplings less was detected in the spleen and kidney. For fish fed with phage-coated feed, phages 
were only found within the feeding period and a sample taken at 48h for one of the fishes 
sampled (Christiansen, Dalsgaard, Middelboe, Lauritsen, & Madsen, 2014 ).  
There were found phages in most samples for the experiment that Christiansen et al.  (2014) 
performed compared to this trial. A reason for this could be that the phages-coated feed was 
==PAGE:32==
32 
 
stored at lower temperature than in this trial while it was fed to fry, which could have a 
significance on the survival of phages. In both studies, phages were found in kidneys and spleen, 
however, it must be said that phages were found more often in Christiansen et al. (2014) trial than 
in this trial. Christiansen et al. (2014) experiment only lasted for 16 days whereas this trial lasted 
for 31 days. In this trial, there were found clear areas in samples from fry fed with control feed in 
the intestine and in 1 sample in the spleen, this weren’t the case for Christiansen et al. (2014) trial. 
This could be because there were bacteria present in the intestine, which might have produced 
toxins that kill other bacteria. Because of clearance in the intestine for fry fed control feed, it 
cannot be concluded that it is phage, which creates clearance in intestine for fry fed with phage-
coated feed and phage-sprayed feed.  
The feed was given to rainbow trout fry for 32 days before fry were infected with F. psychrophilum 
on day 33 after starting with feed. Of fry fed phage-coated feed, the biological average mortality 
was 15.3 %. By contrast, the biological average mortality was 8.59 % for fry fed with phage-
sprayed feed. Fry fed with control feed, had a biological average mortality of 11.86 %. From this, it 
can be seen, that the group of phage-coated feeds have the highest mortality, then the group with 
the control feed and the phage-sprayed feed has the lowest mortality.  
Laanto et al. (2015) examined if phage FCL-2 could be a possible treatment against Flavobacterium 
columnare in rainbow trout fry. By bath challenge, fry was exposed to F. columnare for 2 h at 24.4 
°C at the concentration at 3 × 106 CFU mL−1. The phage was added to experimental aquaria after 
fish transfer with phage- to-bacterium ratios of 1:1 (three aquaria) and 10:1 (three aquaria) 
compared to infection dose. Fish were monitored for 7 days in 12-hour intervals and samples were 
taken from two aquaria from the following treatments: phage-to-bacterium ratios 1:1 and 10:1, 
phage control, and bacterial control. There were observed a significant increase in survival of fish 
following experimental phage treatments. The mean survival of fish infected only with F. 
columnare was 8.3%, whereas phage treatment resulted in a 41.7% (ratio 10:1) and 50% (ratio 1:1) 
(Laanto, Bamford, Ravantti, & Sundberg, 2015).  
In this trial, a biological average survival for phages-coated feed were 84.7 %, for phage-sprayed 
feed the biological average survival was 91.41 % and for control feed the biological average 
survival were 88.14 %. This is much higher than Laanto et. al (2015). This could mean that FPSV-
==PAGE:33==

D22 is a much better phage to F. psychrophilum than FCL-2 is to F. columnare or that the different 
method for which the fish were challenged, have a different effect on how infection is obtained.  
Kim and Park (2018) evaluated the control efficiency of phage PFpW-3 against F. psychrophilum 
infection in ayu with two different infection methods. First fish were challenged with a 0.05-mL 
suspension of live F. psychrophilum with a concentration of 6.2×105 CFU by IM injection. 1 group 
were treated immediately with a phage suspension with a concentration at 3.4 × 106 PFU/mL by 
immersion in a bath for 30 min. The other group was treated with PBS, only as a control. The fish 
were kept for 14 days at 18 °C. However, this were insufficient and the net-scratch (NS) method, 
was used to enhance infectivity. Fish were then bath challenged with suspensions of live F. 
psychrophilum with a concentration at 6.2 × 105 CFU/mL for 30 min. Afterwards, 1 group were 
bath challenged with phage suspension with a concentration of 3.4 × 106 PFU/mL for 30 min. A 
control group were exposed to bath challenge with PBS for only 30 min. The control group of fish 
there were challenged with F. psychrophilum by IM injection, had an average mortality rate at 45.5 
%. In contrast fish there were challenged with F. psychrophilum by IM injection and phages had an 
average mortality of 12.7%. IM administration failed to induce high mortality and enough 
infectivity, so systemic bacterial challenge was needed. In the trial with the method NS the control 
group had a mortality average of 90.0 % and for the group with phages the mortality average was 
40.0 %. Mortality in the phage-administered groups for both IM and NS was lower than that in the 
control groups (Kim & Park, 2018).  
Kim and Park (2018) showed that when IM injection is used, proper infection could not be 
obtained. In this trial, a low mortality rate is obtained in all groups, which means that it cannot be 
concluded that phage FPSV-D22 is a possible treatment of F. psychrophilum. With the low 
mortality rate in the control feed group, it is not possible to conclude whether the phage can be 
used to treat F. psychrophilum and whether it is best to use phage-coated feed or phage-sprayed 
feed. It might be interesting to do the experiment again and instead of injecting F. psychrophilum 
into fry then use the NS method instead.  
Egg trial were performed three times. The first time, the eggs died within 48 h.  The concentration 
reached 3,0*104 CFU/mg. In the second egg trial most eggs were hatched within 120 h after bath 
challenge. For bath challenge with F. psychrophilum 13 eggs/larvae were alive, 17 were dead and it

sterile TYES broth. There were 4 groups; With F. psychrophilum and without FPSV-D22(P1),

only three were dead. One in each group, except for the group P2.   
In this project it was examined whether phages could help lower the concentration of F.

psychrophilum. Contamination of the eggs may have been the reason why the phages have not 
been able to infect F. psychrophilum and thus the concentration of F. psychrophilum on the eggs 
has not decreased.

Phage therapy in relation to the treatment of pathogens on eggs has not been studied very much. 
However, Rørbo et al. has studied subject therapy in relation to pathogens on eggs of Atlantic cod 
(Gadus morhua L.) and turbot (Scophthalmus maximus L.). They examined whether phage KVP40 
was able to prevent mortality of eggs and larvae. When infected with Vibrio anguillarum. They 
found that eggs treated with phage KVP40 reduced mortality in larvae, even though eggs have not 
been disinfected before exposed to V. anguillarum, so eggs were contaminated with naturel 
pathogens (Rørbo, et al., 2018).  
Compared to egg trials 1 and 2, the survival of larvae was much higher in egg trial 3. This also 
applies to eggs treated only with F. psychrophilum and without phages. In egg trial 3 the 
concentration of F. psychrophilum was lower than for eggs treated without phages and with 
phages (figure 14). When double layer assay was performed in trial 3, it was found that there were 
phages present in 3 eggs treated only with phages and 3 eggs treated with both F. psychrophilum 
and phages. This may be because the eggs were highly contaminated, and this may have resulted 
in F. psychrophilum not having the right conditions to grow and for phages to infect F. 
psychrophilum.  
Rangdale et al. (1997) examined whether or not F. psychrophilum had an effect on egg hatching 
and survival of rainbow trout egg and larvae. Rainbow trout eggs were incubated with 1x1010 
cells/ml for either 30 min or 60 min. Eggs were monitored daily until hatching. They found, that 
colonization of F. psychrophilum on eggs did not have a significant effect on egg survival or 
hatching success (Rangdale, H., & Alderman, 1997). In another study by Cipriano et al. (1995) 
examined eggs from different hatcheries and found that only eggs who showed poorly survival 
was infected with F. psychrophilum. They concluded that eggs infected with bacteria could die, but 
that there must be different circumstances for this to be possible (Cipriano, Ford, & Teska, 1995). 
From Rangdale et al. (1997) and Cipriano et al. (1995) experiments, a possible explanation for why 
eggs/larvae have died in trials 1 and 2 may be found. In experiments 1 and 2, the eggs were first 
disinfected with iodophor and then infected with F. psychrophilum between 12 and 24 hours after 
arrival at the laboratory, whereas eggs from trial 3 were disinfected with iodophor and then 
infected with F. psychrophilum within 2 hours of arrival. This may have contributed to the eggs

dying in trial 1 and 2 even before infection with F. psychrophilum, whereas this was not the case in 
trial 3.

5. Conclusion 
The purpose of this project was to see if phages could be used to treat F. psychrophilum in 
rainbow trout and eggs and whether it is possible to find bacteriophages in fry where 
bacteriophages have been given either as phage-coated feed and phage-sprayed feed.  
To examine whether phages could treat infection with F. psychrophilum, fry were fed 3 different 
kinds of feed; phage-coated feed, phage-sprayed feed and control feed. Double layer assay was 
made to see if it was possible to find phages in brain, kidney, spleen and intestine in fry. Phages 
may have been found in both fry fed with phage-coated feed and fry fed the phage-sprayed feed. 
However, there was found some evidence of phages in the intestine of fry fed with control feed. 
This might be due to toxins produced by other bacteria in the gut. Fry was afterwards infected 
with F. psychrophilum to see if phages given orally via feed could prevent mortality. The biological 
mortality rate was very low for fry fed with phage-coated feed, fry fed with phage-sprayed feed 
and for fry fed with control feed. It was therefore not possible to say whether phages had a 
positive effect on the mortality of fry infected with F. psychrophilum.  
 Three experiments were done to examine whether phages could prevent high mortalities in 
rainbow trout eggs. In the first 2 experiments, eggs were infected with F. psychrophilum via bath 
challenge. In the third experiment, eggs were infected with F. psychrophilum via bath challenge 
and afterwards treated with phages. It was found, that the concentration of F. psychrophilum was 
not lower when eggs were treated with phage. However, this could be because the concentration 
of F. psychrophilum was very low due to contamination of the eggs. In trials 1 and 2, nearly all eggs 
/ larvae died, whereas only 3 dead larvae were found in trial 3. This could, again, be because the 
eggs were contaminated so that F. psychrophilum did not have the right growth conditions to 
create a sufficient infection.
