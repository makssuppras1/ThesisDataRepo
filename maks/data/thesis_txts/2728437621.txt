Abstract
Offshore structures are gaining popularity day by day resulting in the constant develop-
ment of offshore engineering. These structures are exposed to significant loads, thus lead-
ing to a need for higher design requirements both for the superstructure and the substruc-
ture.
This project investigates the response of pile foundations of jackets used for the founda-
tion of offshore structures with 3D Finite Element Modelling. Specifically, the subject of
interest is the effect of pile inclination, as it has been proven that it acts in favor of the
pile’s capacity.
More than 25 models in total have been simulated with the 3D FEM software ABAQUS/-
CAE in homogeneous soil, either sand or clay, with different soil parameters and batter
angles. Moreover, a case study on layered stratigraphy has also been conducted, as the
presence of multiple layers of various soils reflects common in-situ conditions.
The results are analyzed in terms of deflection, moment and shear forces development,
while p-y curves are also back-calculated to examine the soil’s response and the effect of
pile inclination on it. The results have also been compared to similar 2D models simulated
with D-Pile Group.
Keywords: soil-structure interaction, lateral loading, finite element modeling, battered
piles, p-y curves

Introduction
Offshore structures have become very popular in recent years, not only for oil and gas ex-
traction but also for the production of renewable energy through the utilization of offshore
wind farms. The constant development of offshore engineering and the need to exploit
deep waters has led to various forms of foundations. Among them, jacket foundations
have become significantly popular, as they can be installed in water depths up to 80m.
Jackets are designed to withstand various types of loads, such as wind, waves and vessel
impacts. The design of these structures takes into consideration the soil properties of the
seabed, environmental conditions and the installation process, which, in combination with
the existence of lateral loads, lead to increased complexity and thus the need to model the
structure’s response with the highest possible accuracy. The goal of the design is to ensure
the long-term stability and safety of the structure, while also optimizing its efficiency and
cost-effectiveness.
Jackets are secured to the seabed with piles, which are driven through the legs and then
become connected to them either at the pile top by welding or along the legs’ length by
grouting. In cases of significant loads, there can be multiple piles per leg, either vertical
or inclined. Batter piles are preferred when the lateral loads are significant, due to the
increased lateral resistance of the ground and thus the increased bearing capacity of the
foundation. However, the spacing between the piles plays a significant role in the behavior
of the foundation and should be taken into account, along with the piles’ efficiency.
A well-known theoretical approach to predict the soil-pile response of piles subjected to
lateral loads is p - y curves, which reflect the relationship between the lateral displacement
of the pile and the lateral resistance of the soil. The curve is based on Winkler’s beam
theory, according to which the soil-pile system can be simplified as a beam on an elastic
foundation, and the assumption that the soil can be modeled as a series of elastic springs.
A commonly used method to accurately predict the behavior of pile foundations under
various loading conditions is numerical modeling, as it provides valuable and detailed
insights into the structural behavior and helps in design optimization for improved perfor-
mance and reduced costs. There are various finite element method software that can be
used for 1D, 2D or 3D modeling. With 3D FEM modeling, the most detailed representa-
tion of both the soil surrounding the piles and its reaction around the piles can be achieved,
while also taking into account the detailed geometry of the piles’ cross sections and the
interface between the piles and the soil.
This project will investigate the performance of pile foundations for jackets of offshore
structures under extreme loading with 3D FEM modeling. The main goals of the project
are the analysis and definition of a single flexible pile’s capacity under lateral loading and
the estimation of the effect of the pile’s inclination on the lateral soil resistance in sandy
and clayey soils. Finally, a layered soil profile will also be investigated as a case study, in
order to look into the effect in multilayered stratigraphies.

2.1
Piles Under Lateral Loads
A laterally loaded pile is a typical case where the interaction between soil and structure
should be taken into account throughout the design, as the pile’s deflection and the soil’s
resistance are codependent. Their application in bridges, buildings and offshore structures
have led to extensive research to understand and predict their behavior. The basis of the-
oretical approaches is Winkler’s beam theory, according to which the pile is considered
as an elastic beam and soil resistance is simulated using a number of independent linear
elastic springs. In agreement with this theoretical approach, the displacement of a point, y,
is a linear function of the soil pressure, p, at the given point and a factor ko, which reflects
the stiffness of the soil in the direction of the displacement and is known as subgrade mod-
ulus or coefficient of subgrade reaction. The Winkler model cannot predict the response
accurately for the continuum material that soil is, as its behavior is defined by more than
one constants, such as Young’s modulus, E, Poisson’s ratio, v, shear modulus, G, bulk
modulus, K. Additionally, it doesn’t take into account the coupling effect between the
vertical and horizontal response in 2D and 3D models. Based on Winkler’s model:

2.2
Battered Piles
Throughout the years a lot of research has been conducted regarding single piles and pile
groups. The existing literature for the theoretical background of this project can be divided
in three main categories, that are not necessarily independent: Analytical, Numerical and
Experimental studies. Before moving any further it is important to explain how a batter
angle is defined as negative or positive. The following figure is useful in better under-
standing the concept.
Figure 2.3: Batter Angle Definition(Murthy, 2002
As shown in figure 2.3, a positive batter angle is considered to be in the loading direction,
while piles battered against the loading direction are considered negative.
2.2.1
Single Piles
Regarding single piles, the focus area is the response of piles under lateral loading, as the
lateral capacity of piles is significantly lower compared to their axial capacity. As already
mentioned an approach that is widely used is the ”p-y” method, where the pile’s behavior
is considered elastic and the soil is represented by non-linear springs that indicate the pile
resistance and its deflection y. p symbolizes soil resistance. The p −y curve reflects
the soil’s behavior and can be divided into three sections, elastic (linear), elastoplastic
(non-linear) and plastic behavior of soil.
Kubo(1964), Awoshika (1971) and Zhang et al. (1999a) have experimented on the p −y
method for batter piles, proving that the resulting p −y curves show similar patterns to
vertical piles, while the soil resistance is higher. Zhang et al. (1999a) suggested that the
ultimate resistance of soil, Pu, in the case of vertical piles can be multiplied by a factor ψ,
which is equal to the ratio of the coefficient of passive earth pressure Kpb for the battered
pile to the coefficient of passive earth pressure Kp for vertical pile as indicated in the
following equations 2.12 and 2.13.

More specifically, Zhang et al. (1999) Zhang, McVay, and Lai, 1999 performed centrifuge
model tests in medium to dense (Dr=55%) and loose (Dr=36%) sands for five pile incli-
nations (± 14.04, 7.13, 0) at 45g to simulate 13.7m long square 430mm x 430mm piles.
Results showed that as the batter angle decreased, the resistance increased up to 50% in
dense sands. On the contrary, the resistance decreased up to 35% in the case of posi-
tive batter. In loose and very loose sands the effect of batter was negligible compared to
medium-dense and dense sands.
Murthy (1965) performed lateral load tests both on vertical and batter piles to determine
the effect of different batter angles on the pile’s lateral resistance. Aluminum alloy tubes
of 19mm outer diameter and 0.88mm wall thickness were used in a 1080mm x 635mm
x 762mm testing tank, which was filled with sand of relative density Dr = 67%. The
batter angles varied between 0° and ±45°. For negative values down to -30° the lateral
resistance increases and decreases afterwards. For positive batter angles the capacity de-
creases, proving that negative batter has a positive effect on the pile’s lateral resistance.
Rao and Veeresh(1994) conducted model tests using aluminum piles of 12mm diameter
and three different lengths in soft marine clay. Their test results revealed that the lateral
capacity of the pile increased when the pile batter angle decreased from +30° to -30°.

Kim, Yun and Kang(2021) performed a model test on the behavior of single batter piles
under vertical loading in sand. They used a steel 500mm x 1400mm x 1000mm chamber
and a copper pipe pile of 940mm length and 28.6mm diameter with a wall thickness of
1.27mm. The batter angle varied between 10° and 40°. They also tested the effect of
sand’s relative density for values of Dr = 31%, 53% and 72%. The direction of the batter
doesn’t play a significant role here, as the enforced load is vertical. In the case of medium
to very dense sand, an increase in the batter angle from 0° to 20° acted positively on the
bearing capacity, whereas for bigger angles the bearing capacity gradually decreased. In
loose sand, the bearing capacity was higher only in the case of 20° batter.
Finite Element Method programs are very useful when it comes to numerical analysis of
soil-pile interaction, provided that the model is constructed correctly. Several numerical
studies have tested the compatibility of FEM models’ analysis results with experimental
results.
Kumar, Pandit and Uniyal(2017) presented a 3D model implemented in PLAXIS3D that
calculated the bearing capacity and pile top displacement of both vertical and batter sin-
gle piles in sand. The results were compared to static load test results of a model rigid
pile. In order to investigate the effect of batter angle, various 3D models with the same
material properties and geometry were generated. The conclusions were in compliance
with the experimental results. In detail, negative raked piles have greater bearing capacity
than vertical and positive raked piles under lateral loading. Accordingly, bigger pile head
horizontal displacements were noticed for positive batters than for negative and vertical
batters. Finally, increasing the negative batter angle leads to lower bearing capacity.
Lassaad, Mahmoud and Mourad (2017) constructed a 3D model in the finite differences
program FLAC3D and validated their model through Zhang et al.(1999) centrifuge exper-
iment and Ranjan et al. (1980) test data. They run the model both for sandy and clayey
soils, vertical and lateral loading cases. It was concluded that both batter angle and sand
density affected the pile’s lateral response in sand, as in dense soils the effect is more sig-
nificant. For positive batters up to 18° the lateral capacity decreased, whereas for bigger
angles it increased slightly in loose sand and decreased in dense sand. However, in the
case of a negative batter, the lateral capacity increased significantly for all densities as the
negative batter increased. Specifically, for a batter angle equal to -25.5° the lateral capac-
ity increased from 39% to 61% depending on the relative density. Moreover, a vertical
load has a great influence on battered piles in sand, as the lateral capacity increases with a
positive batter angle, whereas it decreases with a negative batter angle. In the case of clay,
the lateral response depends only on the batter angle, with the undrained shear strength
not having any effect. Lateral capacity increases both for positive and negative batter, but
with negative batter leading to higher percentages of improvement. The effect of vertical
loads is minimal.

Pile Groups
Zhang, McVay and Lai (1998) run vertical load centrifuge tests on 3x3 and 4x4 battered
pile groups in loose and dense sand. They later proceeded into running a numerical anal-

model piles of 304mm length and 9.5mm width were used to simulate 0.43m wide piles
of 13.7m length, fixed to the pile cap. Two tests were conducted for each pile group, one
for Dr=36% and one for Dr = 55%. The results showed that the axial bearing capacities

groups fail after the skin friction mobilization. The axial forces of the batter piles differ,

ments, the vertical capacity of a battered pile group is significantly higher than that of a
plumb pile group.
Singh and Arora (2017) run 66 tests on different batter pile groups under lateral loading
for batter angles of ± 20°, 25°, 30° and 35°. The experiments took place in a rectangle
1m x 2m x 1m steel tank to avoid the boundary effect. The tank was filled with poorly
graded river sand. The used piles were aluminum pipes with 20mm outer diameter and
1mm wall thickness. The total length of the pile was 0.90m, with an embedded length of
0.80m. The pile caps were mild steel plates of 2mm thickness. The results showed that
pile groups consisting of both vertical piles and batters show more resistance to deflection,
especially groups containing negative battered piles. Finally, changes in batter angle had
greater effect in cases of negative angles. As the angle increased from 20° to 25°, so did
the pile capacity, whereas for angles above 25° the capacity decreases.
Abu-Farsakh, Yu, Pathak, Alshibli and Zhang (2011) performed a numerical analysis using
FB-MultiPier program to validate the design method that was followed on the I-10 Twin
Span Bridge in Louisiana. The foundations of the bridge consist of driven raked piles.
The Louisiana Department of Transportation and Development performed a lateral load
test by pulling together the M19 east and west piers using steel tendons that were placed
through the pile caps. Some piles were instrumented with IPIs and some others with strain
gauges. After high-order polynomial fitting was applied to the resulting rotation and with
the implementation of elastic beam theory, the bending moment and soil reaction were
estimated. Comparing the results of the field testing with FB-MultiPier’s, they noticed
that the software overpredicted the pile cap’s lateral movements. Simulations based on
CPT data had a better estimation than soil borings. Moments that were back-calculated
through the curve fitting process were in compliance with the moments derived from the
strain gauge data. FB-MultiPier estimated larger positive and negative moments than the
IPI measurements. Finally, regarding the pile group’s response, inner pile rows bore higher
axial loads and the back-calculation of p-y curves showed that the effect of group effect
was negligible.
Souri, Abu-Farsakh and Voyiadjis (2019) evaluated the effect of the pile group configu-
ration and spacing on the lateral capacity of pile groups with the use of ABAQUS. They
constructed 3D FEM models of 4x4 pile groups consisting of only vertical piles, only bat-
tered piles and mixed groups. They run the models for three different spacings (3D, 5D
and 7D) and for soft, medium and stiff clay soils. The piles are 0.9m wide and 33.5m
long. The geometry, dimensions and pile group configuration are selected according to
the I-10 Twin Span Bridge in Louisiana. The authors concluded that larger pile spacing led
to increased group efficiency with the pile group of only battered piles having the highest
efficiency (70-90%). For bigger pile spacings (above 5D) the group effect is insignificant.
The resulting p-multipliers, regarding the modified p-y curves for pile groups, indicated
that the group effect is lower on piles in exterior columns and an adjustment should be
made based on the group configuration. Finally, the p-multiplier’s value depends on the
soil stiffness.

2.2.3
Single Piles & Pile Groups
Reese & Van Impe (2001) ”Single Piles and Pile Groups Under Lateral Loading” contains,
among others, theories about pile design and models of soil response. Although there is
an analysis of Awoshika’s (1971) experiment on single piles and groups of piles under
inclined loading, there isn’t any information about experiments on the response under
either vertical or lateral loading. However, some insight is given regarding the effect of
pile inclination on the behavior of piles under lateral loading. Specifically, it is suggested
that a multiplier is used on the p-y curves of a vertical pile to reflect the effect of the batter.
An empirical curve is compared to the results of Awoshika’s and Reese’s (1971) test tank
experiment and Kubo’s(1964) full-scale tests, showing that the use of the modifier leads
to questionable results.
Ranjan et al. (1980)Ranjan, Ramasamy, and Tyagi, 1971 performed an experimental test
on the lateral response of single batter piles and pile bents in clay. A 350mm x 350mm x
500mm wooden tank was filled with high compressibility clay. The aluminum pipe piles
used had a 9.5mm outer diameter and 360mm length. As far as it comes to single piles,
negative raked piles showed higher resistance than vertical piles, while positive batters
showed lower lateral resistance. Pile groups with battered piles, regardless of their angle
direction offered more resistance than pile groups consisting of only plumb piles. Groups
with negative batter piles, especially, offered more resistance than those with positive
batters.
Juvekar and Pise (2008) also conducted lateral load tests on single batter piles and groups
of two piles in sand of Dr=70%. They used stainless steel piles of 19mm outer diameter
and 0.75mm wall thickness. In the case of pile groups, various pile spacings were tested
(3D, 4.5D, 6D). Single negative raked piles in angles between -10° and -30° showed 15-
25% more resistance compared to single vertical piles. Positive raked piles in angles
between +10° to +30° showed 20-30% less resistance compared to single vertical piles.
Pile groups consisting of a vertical pile and a battered pile with an angle of 10° to +30°
offer 25-35% less resistance than that of the vertical pile group. Pile groups consisting of
a vertical pile and a battered pile with an angle of -10° to -30° or a combination of positive
and negative battered piles with angles varying between ± 10° and ± 30 ° have 15-35%
increased resistance than the vertical pile group.
It can safely be said that there is still room for further studies on pile groups, as the existing
literature on single piles is extensive. It can also be concluded that there haven’t been
enough studies on the lateral response of battered piles neither in clayey soils nor in layered
stratigraphies. There is still a need for guidelines on the estimation of group response and
the corresponding p −y curves. Finally, the p −y curves that are back-calculated from
the 3D model can reflect a higher level of the actual conditions, as more details are taken
into account than the existing models.

3.1
ABAQUS Model
The 3D Finite Element software ABAQUS/CAE was used to simulate various models of
single piles, both vertical and raked, to examine the effect of inclination and the varying
response of the foundation in different homogeneous soil conditions. The models were
run in two types of sand, medium and dense, and two types of clay, soft and medium
to stiff. The same models were also tested with the software D-Pile Group as a cross-
reference for the results’ validation. Finally, a case study in multilayered stratigraphy was
also conducted, in order to investigate the behavior of a single vertical pile and a single
batter pile. It should be noted that the installation of the pile is not modeled, thus the
installation effects on the ground conditions are not taken into account. In order to create
the 3D ABAQUS models, the same steps were followed, both for the vertical and the batter
piles, as described below:
3.1.1

The model consists of three parts, the pile, the inner soil acting as a plug, and the sur-

3.1.3
Interactions
It should be noted that there is a difference in the soil-pile interaction between the models
in sand and the models in clay, due to the different chosen material behavior. The Mohr-
Coulomb model was used for sand and the simple plastic model was used for clay, as
the rest of the suitable options for soils required data that weren’t available. The Mohr-
Coulomb model was also tested for clay but it was proven that it overestimated the soil’s
strength parameters (see Appendix B).
For piles in sand, the soil-pile interaction is represented by a surface-to-surface contact
with a friction coefficient, both between the pile and the inner soil and the pile and the
surrounding soil. The pile’s inner and outer surfaces are defined as the master surfaces
due to the pile’s higher stiffness, while the inner/surrounding soil’s surface in contact with
the pile is the secondary surface. The contact is assigned tangential behavior with penalty

Before performing the pushover analysis in order to obtain the loads that lead to the pile’s
plastic behavior, the first step is to apply the initial (vertical) stresses to the soil parts. The
stress increases linearly with depth and can be calculated with the following formula:
σv = γ · z = ρ · g · z
(3.2)

equilibrium without deformations. The required input data are two levels along with the
stresses at these levels, as well as a lateral coefficient, needed for the automatic calculation
of horizontal stresses, according to the well-known relation:
σh = K0σv
(3.3)
K0 can be estimated through Poisson’s ratio (v) as shown below:

The ultimate load can then be estimated by applying a high lateral load parallel to the x-

the pile reaches its yield stress.
3.1.5
Boundary Conditions
For all models, four boundary conditions were assigned, out of which three remain un-
changed throughout the different steps, while one changes at the last step of the lateral
load application.

Symmetry on the xz plane is ensured with the boundary condition type ”Symmetry/An-
tisymmetry/Ecnastre” and ”YSYMM”, which restrains displacement on the y-axis and
rotation around the x and z-axis and is applied to surfaces and sets on the xz-plane.
Regarding the surrounding soil, displacements on all axes are restrained at the bottom sur-

both horizontal directions, allowing for vertical displacements (settlements), while rota-

boundary condition is assigned to the reference point to restrain all displacements and
rotations. However, in the final step, displacement in the x-axis and rotation around the
y-axis are freed.
3.1.6
Steps
All the individual steps of the models are run as general static analysis, except the ”Geo-
static stresses” step, where geostatic analysis is performed to ensure equilibrium between
the assigned geostatic stresses of the initial step and the applied gravity loads on the soil
parts and the boundary conditions. The steps created in the models are summarized below.
Results and changes are propagated from each step to the next one.
• Initial step: Definition of boundary conditions and interface
• ”Geostatic” step: Gravity is applied to all the soil parts
• ”Reduced interface” step: The initial interface is reduced to the final values calcu-
lated according to eq.3.1
• ”Load” step: Lateral load application and change of the boundary condition on the
Reference point to allow deflection.
The time needed for each step to be completed depends on the chosen time-incrementation.
The default settings were chosen, apart from the ”Load” step where the minimum and
maximum increments were reduced in order for the program to converge, as for big load
increments the program would abort the calculations.

7
Conclusions
This project was focused on investigating the effect of battered piles on the structural
capacity of the pile and also examining the soil-structure interaction through 3D Finite
Element Modelling using ABAQUS/CAE.
Several models were set up in sand and clay, but also in layered stratigraphy to check
various values of batter angle in different soils of different strengths and stiffness. The
same models were also analyzed with the 2D pile design software D-Pile Group in order
to examine the differences between 2D and 3D models and test the validity of the 3D
results.
The effect of batter depends on the type of foundation soil, cohesive or granular, and
the parameters of the soil. In sand the direction of the batter affects the capacity, either
increasing it when it is opposite to the direction of loading (negative batter) or decreasing
it when it is in the same direction with the applied load (positive batter). As the batter
increases, the effect is more prominent. The same applies to the soil parameters, especially
the friction angle and the relative density. In clay, the direction of the batter doesn’t play a
role, as the capacity increases in either case, as the angle increases. The magnitude of the
effect is not affected by the undrained shear strength. Regarding the p-y curves, it seems
that the negative batter affects the soil’s response for the first 1-3m of depth, increasing
the soil’s strength and stiffness. In case of positive direction, the bigger the batter angle
the lower the soil’s stiffness and strength.
Between different programs, the input parameters differ, thus leading to mismatches be-
tween the results. In this case, D-Pile calculates automatically the soil’s stiffness, which is
quite higher than the value used in the ABAQUS models but also higher than the suggested
values corresponding to the given angle of friction. In conclusion, 3D modeling leads to
a more detailed design but the parameters used should be chosen based on engineering
judgement and expertise.

Future Work
The present project could be expanded or act as a base for future projects.
• Pile Group Modelling: Pile groups are also very common in jacket foundations
and the pile group effect could also be a topic of interest.
• Using another clay soil model: As it wasn’t possible to model the interface be-
tween the pile and the soil in the clay models, it would be interesting to use another
soil model, like Cam-clay, to also take into account the effects of the interface.
Moreover, the stress-strain relation can be taken into account. The results could
also be compared with this project to evaluate the accuracy of the various soil mod-
els.
• Modelling installation effects: In order to increase the precision of the results, it
could be useful to implement the effects of installation. However, there aren’t such
options in the available FEM software to this day.
• Cyclic loading: Due to the nature of the wind and wave loads, cyclic loading could
be of interest when modelling the foundations of offshore structures regarding the
unloading and reloading effects.
