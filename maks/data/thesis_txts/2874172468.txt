Abstract
The optimization of floating offshore wind farm layouts represents a critical challenge for
achieving commercial viability in deeper water deployments. This thesis investigates lay-
out optimization methodologies for floating wind farms incorporating shared anchor con-
figurations, addressing the complex interplay between turbine positioning, wake interac-
tions, and mooring system economics. Two complementary optimization approaches are
developed and evaluated: a greedy grid-based method for rapid layout generation and a
Random Search algorithm for fine-tuning turbine and anchor positions.
The study employs the UMaine VolturnUS-S platform with IEA 15-MW turbines in a taut
mooring configuration, optimizing layouts for the Havbredey site in northern Scotland us-
ing FLORIS wake modeling coupled with MoorPy quasi-static mooring analysis. The opti-
mization objective focuses on Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) minimization rather than
traditional Annual Energy Production (AEP) maximization, enabling direct evaluation of
the trade-offs between wake losses and mooring system costs.
Results demonstrate that turbine count and spacing fundamentally dominate economic
performance, with orientation refinements contributing less than 0.05% LCOE improve-
ment in most cases. Honeycomb grid configurations consistently achieve about 30%
higher total AEP than rhombic layouts due to higher turbine density, which also causes 2-
6% more wake losses, than rhombic configurations. Conversely, rhombic configurations
deliver consistently lower LCOE values through better per-turbine efficiency. At 4D spac-
ing, honeycomb layouts reach 25.77% wake losses—comparable to the densely-packed
Lillgrund wind farm—yet maintain the highest total energy production due to accommo-
dating 431 turbines versus 288 in honeycomb configurations.
The Random Search fine-tuning approach yields modest LCOE improvements of 0.3-
0.7% at 8D spacing, with gains primarily attributed to reduced mooring line lengths rather
than meaningful AEP increases. Computational scaling analysis reveals that FLORIS
runtime grows strongly with turbine count, creating evaluation bottlenecks that constrain
optimization effectiveness for realistic farm sizes. The integration of platform displace-
ment calculations, while technically feasible, proves computationally challenging without
proportional performance benefits.
Material selection analysis indicates minimal LCOE impact when mooring lines are nor-
malized to equivalent Minimum Breaking Load values, suggesting geometric optimization
takes precedence over material choice in shared anchor systems.
The findings reveal that practical floating wind farm optimization should prioritize turbine
density maximization within acceptable wake loss thresholds rather than pursuing sophis-
ticated micro-level adjustments. The dominant influence of spacing and turbine count
suggests that optimization efforts are most productively focused on these coarse design
variables during early development phases. This work provides essential guidance for
the transition from demonstration projects to commercial-scale floating wind deployments,
highlighting the need for computationally efficient optimization methodologies that address
the unique constraints and opportunities of floating systems.

Introduction
As the world faces a global energy crisis, floating wind is gaining more recognition as a
key technology in the global energy transition. 80% of the world’s offshore wind potential
is located in ares with a water depth greater than 60 m. Floating wind turbines enable
deployment in these deeper waters, thus areas that were previously deemed inaccessi-
ble can now be utilized [29, 87]. This deeper water potential is particularly relevant in
regions such as the US West Coast, Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Ireland, Scotland and
the Mediterranean, where steep continental shelves mean fixed-bottom monopiles quickly
become very expensive even just a few kilometers from shore [48]
Estimates from the World Bank suggest a potential of 71.000 GW offshore wind, including
fixed bottom and floating, while the Global Wind Energy Councils increased their installa-
tion forecast of floating wind by 2030 from 6.5 GW to 16.5 GW. Other industry roadmaps
from DNV now predict more than 250 GW of installed floating capacity by 2050 [2, 28],
further highlighting the potential of floating wind and the need for research advancement
to scale the technology effectively. This potential banks on government support, and is
being put into action by ambitious commitments, such as EU targets and national floating
wind targets in countries like Norway, Japan, and Scotland. In total EU commits to 60
GW offshore wind by 2030, Japan 60 GW floating wind by 2050, and Scotland 11 GW by
2030, 42 GW of floating wind in the pipeline[63, 93, 37].
While the industry has evolved from single-turbine prototypes to fully operational pre-
commercial farms, such as Equinor’s 30 MW Hywind Scotland [36, 114], many challenges
still remain. These include not only technical and economic factors, but also how to con-
figure farms efficiently at scale, as floating wind opens up for new design methods, such
as shared anchors, utilizing turbine displacement and inter-array mooring.
To reach the full potential of floating wind, it is crucial to be able to scale single turbines
into farm-scale designs. Floating turbines introduce new constraints such as mooring
cost and footprints, dynamic motion, as well as complex seabed and cable interactions
[81]. Furthermore, experiences from monopile farms have shown that wake interactions
can significantly reduce the expected power production. Lillgrund, a notoriously densly
spaced fixed-bottom wind farm, is estimated to have up to 25% wake losses [91]. Thus
floating wind poses an interesting question as to how wake patterns changes when the
turbine moves.
Besides improving efficiency, it is vital to lower the cost of floating wind in order to make
it more competitive [23]. As of now, the levelized cost of energy (LCOE) of floating wind
is significantly higher than fixed-bottom offshore wind. The National Renewable Energy
Laboratory (NREL) came out in 2024 with a review of the current cost of wind energy
[115], which estimated LCOE for fixed bottom to be 117 $/MWh and floating wind to be
181 $/MWh. Studies from the industry predicts existing markets with an LCOE down to 62
$/MWh, however a key factor for the realization of this, is not technological readiness, but
rather government support [23]. The technical solutions to reduce LCOE is usually related
to reducing cost and improve efficiency. At farm level a key component for cost reduction
is the mooring system, where shared mooring configurations, can lower material use as
well as the environmental impact [81, 102]. To improve efficiency it is essential to mitigate
wake losses by strategical positioning. Recent studies demonstrate that strategic turbine
positioning can reduce wake losses by up to 18% and mooring costs by 17% [114].

Looking at the significant cost difference and the unique challenges of floating wind sys-
tems, optimizing wind farm layout becomes critical for large scale commercial realiza-
tion. Unlike fixed-bottom installations where turbine positions are primarily constrained
by water depth and seabed conditions, floating wind farms introduce a multi-dimensional
optimization challenge involving turbine positioning, mooring system design, wake inter-
actions, and dynamic coupling effects. Wind farm layout optimization has the potential
tackle several cost drivers, such as: minimizing wake losses to maximize energy produc-
tion, optimizing mooring configurations to reduce material costs and installation complex-
ity. The complexity of the optimization problem is increased by the interdependency of
the turbines position, wake patterns, mooring line tensions and turbine displacement. In
particular when investigating whether the displacement of turbines, can push downstream
turbines out of the wake generated by the turbine in front. Therefore, developing useful
optimization methodologies targeting floating wind farms represents a crucial step toward
achieving the cost reductions necessary for widespread commercial viability.
Despite floating wind’s commercial potential, the field remains relatively new at farm scale,
with established wake modeling tools like FLORIS and PyWake not yet being officially
adapted for floating-specific concerns. Current research has predominantly focused on
small-scale feasibility studies and single or two-turbine analysis, leaving large-scale com-
mercial farm design methodologies largely undeveloped. The transition from small-scale
demonstration projects to commercial-scale floating wind farms (50+ turbines) introduces
optimization complexities not addressed by existing research. The interdependencies
between turbine motion, wake interactions, mooring systems, and installation logistics
at commercial scale still remain inadequately modeled. While shared anchoring systems
provide potential for cost reduction and environmental impact minimization, current design
approaches treat each turbine as an isolated unit, leaving optimization of shared mooring
configurations at large scale vastly underexplored.
Given these research gaps, this thesis aims to develop and evaluate optimization ap-
proaches for large-scale floating offshore wind farm layouts incorporating shared mooring
configurations. Two complementary models are employed for farm-scale design: First,
a Greedy grid based model that packs turbines within the permitted area subject to min-
imum spacing, yielding a feasible layout and second a local refinement that adjusts tur-
bine positions and line-to-anchor assignments by extending FLORIS’s local optimizers
with floating-specific constraints (anchors, shared nodes, mean offsets) via MoorPy. The
optimization objective throughout is to minimize LCOE.
The UMaine VolturnUS-S hull (paired with the IEA 15-MW turbine) serves as the reference
platform as it is widely used as a benchmark. For the purposes of this study, the original
three-line chain catenary station-keeping is replaced by a taut, shared-anchor configu-
ration. Platform geometry, fairlead positions, hydrostatics, and mass properties are not
changed. Further details are provided in sections 1.3 and 1.3.1. The Havbredey area
in northern Scotland provides the site and environmental conditions, reflecting Scotland’s
strong floating-wind potential (e.g., Hywind Scotland, Kincardine, and planned commercial-
scale projects such as Green Volt). Site data are drawn from [24, 82]. The problem is
solved using FLORIS, a wind farm simulation software using steady-state engineering
wake models in a Python framework developed by NREL [92], as well as MoorPy, an
open-source quasi-static mooring toolset used for mooring system applications and anal-
ysis [52]. For the optimization the objective was changed from AEP to LCOE in order to
establish a relation between material cost and power production.

1.1
Thesis Scope and structure
This thesis addresses layout optimization of commercial-scale floating offshore wind farms
using a shared-anchor mooring concept. The analysis is constrained to the following
boundaries:
• Semi-submersible floating platforms only (excluding spar and TLP designs)
• Shared taut mooring systems only (catenary mooring systems excluded)
• The floating platform does not require re-ballasting to accommodate the new moor-
ing system
• Uniform water depth conditions
• Operating conditions only (extreme weather events excluded)
• Wind-generated loads only, as aerodynamic thrust dominates the system dynamics
and loads in floating offshore wind turbines during operational conditions [44]
• Static platform analysis (platform motion effects on wake acknowledged in [119, 105]
but excluded here to focus on layout optimization fundamentals)
• Only quasi-static load analysis is evaluated, due to computational cost
• The effect of tilting of the floater is neglected
• Mooring lines are assumed to be made of a single material
With these constraints this work aims to:
• Investigate optimal spatial distributions that maximize energy production while min-
imizing mooring system costs through shared anchoring.
• Analyze how mean platform displacement affects wake interactions and overall farm
performance (not including dynamic motion–wake coupling)
• Evaluate how different mooring line materials contribute to the cost and turbine dis-
placement, as well as their effect of the wake.
This thesis consists of 6 chapters: Introduction, Literature review, Theory, Methodology,
Results and Future developments. The introduction has already stated the potential of
FOWF and the scope of this work. The next sections will go through important back-
ground knowledge related to the key aspects of the thesis and finally present the setup
for the case study. The next chapter, Literature review, compares existing literature and
developments related to floater technology, in particular the station keeping system, SKS,
as this is one of the major focal points for this thesis. Another focal point is layout optimiza-
tion, where the development from fixed-bottom offshore layouts to floating is established.
Through this, the use of different optimization algorithms and wake models are inves-
tigated. The Theory chapter explains the theory behind loads on the floater, the wake
model and optimization theory behind the model. The Methodology chapter explains in
detail, how two models have been developed, the different ways of optimizing, and flow of
each. Results and discussions are presented in the appropriately named Results chapter,
using Havbredey as a case study. This benefits of each model is evaluated, as well as
exploring how different spacing, mooring material and grid structures affect the LCOE.
Finally, the results are summed up in the conclusion, with Further Research pointing out
areas for improvement and potential for expansion.

1.2
Background knowledge
This section aims to briefly explain different concepts related to floating wind and layout
optimization. It delves into the topics of floater types, mooring, anchors, wake models and
optimization models.
1.2.1
Floater types
Floating wind can be made up of several configurations. Floater designs can typically
be narrowed down into 4 categories: Spar, semi-submersibles, barge and tension leg
platform (TLP). Each uses different principles for hydrostatic stability. Depictions of each
type can be seen in fig. 1.1
Spar types uses a low center of gravity to counteract the center of buoyancy. This is done
by placing heavy ballast at the bottom of a, typically, long cylindrical structure. Although
it is simple to construct, it requires very deep harbours for installation. [133]
Semi-submersibles uses the waterplane area for stability, mainly provided by large columns.
The low draft makes it suitable for many different conditions, but can be more complex to
construct[35].
Barge platforms uses, similar to semi-submersibles, mainly waterplane area for stability.
They consists of a flat hull to create the required waterplane area. It is relatively simple
to install, but also more complex to build[121].
TLP uses a high buoyancy force to generate tension in the legs, or mooring lines, to
maintain stability. It usually is lighter, and has a smaller footprint than other floater types.
However, this also makes it more difficult to install and transport. [122]
1.2.2
Mooring
A key factor of floating wind is the mooring, as it provides stability, in particular, in surge
and sway. Mooing is usually either catenary, taut or semi-taut, a hybrid between the two.
Optimization of floating wind farms with shared anchor configurations

==PAGE:20==
Catenary mooring systems are mainly used in shallow water configurations. They pro-
vide restoring forces trough their own weight, where the line is suspended from the fair-
lead, with a substantial length lying on the seabed. This causes the line to form a catenary
curve, from which it is named [84]. The mooring lines must be long enough to allow the
forces transferred to the anchors to be kept horizontal. One benefit from this, is that
cheaper anchors can be used, however, the watch circle increases [6].
Taut mooring systems are primarily used in deeper water configurations where catenary
systems become impractical and expensive. They achieve restoring forces through their
axial stiffness, with pre-tensioned lines that remain taut throughout operation [6]. The
mooring lines are stretched at steep angles to the seabed, typically 30-45 degrees, re-
quiring specialized anchors that can handle both vertical and horizontal loads [65]. This
configuration results in a significantly smaller footprint compared to catenary systems, but
requires more complex and expensive anchors[74].
Semi-taut mooring systems are a hybrid solution which combines the advantages of both
catenary and taut configurations, making them particularly suitable for intermediate water
depths of 50-100 meters [119]. These systems provide restoring forces through a com-
bination of line weight and axial stiffness, using synthetic rope segments connected with
steel chains in multi-segment arrangements [75]. This ensures a reduced footprint com-
pared to catenary systems while requiring less complex anchoring than fully taut systems
[65].
1.2.3
Anchor types
The different types of anchors usually used in FOWT originally stems from the oil and gas
industry. The most commonly types can be categorized as drag embedment anchors,
suction piles, gravity anchors, and driven piles.
Drag embedment anchors are usually steel plates welded together to create a fluke
shape, that is then embedded into the seabed. They are cost-effective and suitable for
horizontal loading but cannot take vertical or multidirectional loading. This limits them in
supporting taut mooring or complex, shared-load configurations [109, 121].
Suction pile anchors are a hollow cylindrical steel structures that is installed via neg-
ative pressure, can provide multi-directional resistance and are thus suited for shared
anchor configurations. However, they can loose their holding capacity if subjected to
multi-directional cyclic loading [1]. They can be engineered for a broad range of soil types,
but is not suited for harder seabeds [5, 14].
Gravity anchors uses mass and seabed friction to provide holding capacity. They are
often used in shallow waters or in locations where seabed conditions prevent deep em-
bedment. They provide poor weight to holding capacity, due to their significant higher
mass, however, they provide reliable performance in all seabed conditions and allows for
multi-directional loading[4]. Furthermore, they can be constructed near the shore, which
reduces installation complexity [95].
Driven pile anchors offer the greatest weight to holding capacity, especially for large
turbines, allowing for high-load, multi-line connections but requiring complex installation
procedures and specialized vessels [109]. They can be suited for a variety of seabed
conditions, but cannot be installed in harder seabeds or in the presence of larger rocks[95].

1.2.5
Wake models
As mentioned previously, too densely spaced wind farms can lead to great reduction of
the expected power production due to wake losses.
Wind turbine wakes are defined as a velocity deficit caused by the extraction of kinetic
energy from the incoming wind flow. This also causes an increase in turbulence levels
due to the mixing of slower wake flow with the surrounding faster-moving air. The wake
development can be divided into distinct regions: the near wake (approximately 2-5 ro-
tor diameters downstream), where the flow is dominated by complex three-dimensional
effects and rotor-specific characteristics, and the far wake (beyond 5 rotor diameters),
where the flow exhibits more universal, self-similar behavior. For offshore wind farms,
the wake characteristics are usually different from onshore farms, as the turbulence in-
tensity is lower, the atmospheric conditions are more stable and the surface roughness is
much lower. These differences contributes to the offshore wakes being longer and more
persistent, compared to onshore.
For that reason it becomes increasingly important to include wake models during layout
planning. However, accuracy of the model comes at the expense of increasing computa-
tional cost. Currently, several models exist to model wakes, with varying levels of accu-
racy and computational effort. Low cost and accuracy models includes the Jensen Wake
Model, where as high accuracy requires Large Eddy Simulations (LES). An overview of
wake models of varying complexity and their use in optimization, can be found below:
• The Jensen wake model, sometimes referred to as the top-hat model, as it as-
sumes a uniform wake velocity profile across the turbines cross section, whereas it
assumes freestream outside of the cross sectional area. [64] It is one of the most
used wake models for optimization, as seen in [49], [34], [59],[110] and [86]. It is
used for its fast runtime, but know to be less accurate compared to other wake mod-
els, as demonstrated by [132].
• The Gaussian wake model uses a gaussian velocity distribution in the spanwise
direction along the rotor. It is often based on the work by Bastankhah and Porté-Agel

==PAGE:22==
[8], however other models using a gaussian wake distributions has been success-
fully created [11, 27]. The Gaussian model was used for all cases in a study by
NREL to compare the performance of 8 different optimization methods applied to a
wind farm layout optimization problem [120].
• Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) RANS-based wake models solve the
time-averaged Navier-Stokes equations coupled with turbulence models, to predict
mean wake characteristics. These models are usually paired with actuator disk
methods, where the turbine is assumed to be a thin permeable disk. This is done in
order to establish a balance between computational efficiency and accuracy [132].
Recent developments include data-driven RANS closures that use machine learning
techniques to improve wake predictions [68].
• Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) High-fidelity CFD models also relies on
Navier-Stokes equations, but in their full form, rather than the averaged used in
RANS. These models are fundamental for wake physics research and validation
of simplified models, though they require significant computational resources [25].
Modern CFD computations combines both steady RANS and unsteady RANS (URANS)
depending on the application requirements.
• Large Eddy Simulations (LES) represents the current state-of-the-art in wake mod-
eling. LES solves large-scale turbulent structures, while modeling smaller scales
through sub-grid scale models. LES is typically coupled with actuator line meth-
ods (ALM) where each of the turbines blades are represented as lines of distributed
forces, which allows for detailed wake interaction studies [107]. The ALM-LES ap-
proach has been extensively validated against experimental data and is widely used
for studying complex wake phenomena including wake meandering, yawed tur-
bine wakes, and multi-turbine interactions [60]. Recent implementations have also
included fully-coupled aero-servo-elastic models for more comprehensive FOWT
analysis [94].
When considered for optimization, RANS, CFD and LES are usually bi-passed as they
are too computationally expensive to be efficient. A comparative study of the Jensen and
Gaussian wake models, used in optimization [132], found that the Gaussian model ran 2.7
times slower than the Jensen model. However, the study concluded that the Gaussian is
preferred in optimization due to the narrower wake prediction and faster radial recovery,
which better resembles reality. The Gaussian model also proved to be more robust for
multidirectional cases. The study were carried out in FLORIS using a gradient based
optimization algorithm with the objective of maximizing AEP.

Literature Review
This chapter reviews the state of the art in floating offshore wind, with emphasis on
floater technology, mooring systems, and the optimization of layout, anchoring, and cost–
performance trade-offs. It synthesizes evidence from existing projects and leading con-
cepts, compares mooring configurations and materials, and examines how optimization
methods have been applied in design and operation. The goal is to establish the technical
context, modelling assumptions, and optimization criteria that inform the work developed
later in the thesis.
2.1
Floater technology
Floating wind technology is based on learnings from the oil and gas industry. The first
research regarding FOWT started back in the 1990’s, but it was not until 2007, that the
first prototype, the Blue H, was build. From this, the concepts have continued to evolve,
causing a rapid expansion in research, which has led to deployment of several distinctive
designs, spanning from prototypes to commercial farms [32].
This technological evolution has progressed through three distinct phases: direct adapta-
tion of existing oil and gas concepts, specialization for dynamic wind loads and distributed
generation requirements, and current optimization for local environmental conditions and
mass production economics [12].
2.1.1
Timeline of floating wind deployments and platform evolution
The comprehensive timeline seen in fig. 2.1, presented by [32] in their review of at-sea
FOWT shows the rapid technological evolution from 2007 to 2023, showing progression
across the four primary platform types: spar, barge, semi-submersible, and tension leg
platforms. However while extensive, this timeline does not include several significant com-
mercial installations that have achieved operation since 2021, including the Kincardine
Offshore Wind Farm (50 MW, completed October 2021), Hywind Tampen (88 MW, com-
missioned in August 2023), and Provence Grand Large (25 MW, fully commissioned June
2025), both representing achievements towards commercialization of semi-submersible
and TLP technologies respectively [32].
2.1.2
FOWT stability concepts and commercial implementations
Unlike fixed-bottom, FOWT experience motion in six degrees of freedom. The two most
critical responses, in terms of power production, are surge and pitch motions. Platform
surge creates horizontal displacement in the streamwise direction, causing changes in
the experienced velocity, while pitch rotation affects rotor orientation, changing the angle
of attack[76]. Research demonstrates that platform pitch motion creates average rotor tilt
angles that reduce effective swept area, typically decreasing power output by 1.5-2.5%
compared to fixed-bottom installations, though this loss can be partially offset by increased
relative wind speeds from platform motion [44].
The floating offshore wind industry has converged on four primary stability concepts orig-
inally developed for offshore oil and gas operations, each offering distinct advantages for
different deployment scenarios.
Spar foundation obtains stability through ballasting. Here, dense materials are placed at
the bottom of cylindrical structures to create large restoring moments through the displace-
ment between center of gravity and center of buoyancy. The first commercially success-
ful implementation, was Equinor’s Hywind Scotland, the world’s first commercial floating

2.1.3
Mooring Lines
Floating offshore wind turbines rely on station-keeping systems (SKS) to maintain posi-
tion while withstanding environmental loads from wind, waves, and currents [130]. The
mooring system design significantly influences both the structural integrity and economic
viability of FOWTs, with mooring costs accounting for over 10% of total project costs and
potentially reaching 30% of total system costs for some configurations, surpassing even
wind turbine equipment costs [130, 115].
Mooring System Configurations
FOWT usually uses one of three mooring configurations: catenary, taut, and semi-taut
systems, each having different force characteristics and restoring. Catenary mooring sys-
tems use free hanging chain mooring lines, where the station-keeping force is provided
by the mass of the chain. The anchor is then loaded exclusively horizontally and the ra-
dius is large enough to give the mooring lines their catenary shape. Usually about six to
eight times the water depth [10, 98]. The catenary mooring system is the most utilized
for FOWT[130, 32]. Semi-taut or taut mooring systems uses synthetic rope to provide
restoring force through the line stiffness, typically at inclinations of 30-45 degrees to the
seabed. Synthetic mooring lines are usually cheaper which allows for access to deeper
seas, where catenary chain system would become too expensive [6].
Material Properties and Performance
Common mooring line materials includes chain, wire rope and synthetic fiber rope each
exhibit distinct characteristics, with chain being relatively cheap, heavy and sturdy with
good resistance to seabed abrasion, while wire rope is lighter but more susceptible to
friction damage and less elastic than synthetic alternatives. Chain and wire rope are
much more resistant to damage from seabed interaction than fiber ropes, however the
lighter synthetic ropes can drive cost down, as buoyancy force can be reduced[130, 103].
Chain and Steel Wire Steel chain provides excellent load-carrying capacity and durabil-
ity with well-established manufacturing standards, with chains being the most commonly
utilized material in catenary mooring systems due to their high tensile strength and re-
sistance to wear. However, the heavy weight of steel components increases installation
complexity and increases costs in deepwater applications [130, 67]. Steel wire rope con-
sists of individual wires wound in helical patterns, with six-strand configurations and spiral
strand designs optimized for anchoring and service life, though corrosion-resistant sheath-
ing enhances longevity at the expense of increased installation complexity in deeper wa-
ters [67, 103].
Synthetic Rope Materials Due to their reduced weight, synthetic ropes has incredible
strength-to-weight ratios, which also reduces installation complexity and increasing ves-
sel payload capacity [67]. For synthetic rope, mainly polyester and nylon are of interest.
Polyester has very interesting stiffness characteristics where the mooring line’s stiffness

Multi-Segment and Hybrid Systems Multi-segment mooring systems have been widely
used in the construction of FOWT. Here segments of the mooring lines are replaced with
cheaper materials, typically, chain at the bottom for seabed abrasions, synthetics to re-

reliability [67]. A study by [98] compared a chain catenary mooring system to polyester
ropes of novel mooring tethers, and found that, synthetics effectively reduces peak anchor
loads by up to 84% and 75% respectively for smallest footprints (293 m), and up to 80%
and 59% respectively for largest footprints (838 m). It should be noted, however, that the
load reduction comes with trade-offs such as significantly increased platform displacement
that may exceed allowable limits. Advanced multi-segment configurations incorporating
clump weights and floats allow for tailored system response, with ground chain maintain-
ing horizontal anchor loading as seen by [66].
Optimization and Design Methods
Modern mooring design has evolved from iterative engineering assessment to computa-
tional optimization frameworks, with automatic optimization procedures for FOWTs during
the design stage required to help reduce costs and improve economic viability [124, 53].
Genetic algorithms and surrogate modeling have become prevalent approaches, with
[125] developing a multi-objective genetic algorithm approach that efficiently screens de-
sign space for mooring systems balancing component cost and footprint, while [124]
demonstrate genetic algorithms with Kriging surrogate models for automated mooring
optimization based on dynamic response criteria. The optimization challenges are de-
fined by the complex physics of synthetic materials, leading to development of advanced
modeling capabilities such as MoorDyn’s supplemented linear elasticity model support-
ing user-defined stress-strain curves adjusted to represent synthetic mooring materials
[53, 126, 126]. Complementing these dynamic simulation tools, MoorPy provides a quasi-
static mooring analysis framework that allows for rapid design space exploration and op-
timization of mooring system layouts. This is particularly valuable for preliminary design
phases and parametric studies where computational efficiency is prioritized over full dy-
namic coupling [54].
2.1.4
Anchors
The expansion of floating offshore wind energy has driven significant advances in anchor-
ing systems, with shared anchor system increasing in popularity as a way to reduce cost,
when moving into farm scale.
The concept of shared anchors, where multiple mooring lines from separate FOWT’s con-
nect to a single anchor, has evolved from multiline approach used in the oil and gas indus-
try. Shared anchors provide a way to reduce the footprint and material usage for FOWT
farms [1, 42]. Field studies indicate that shared anchor systems can significantly reduce

create uneven loading distributions as well as directional load reversals within dynamic

to conventional three-line mooring systems. In extreme cases with turbines operating at
full rated thrust, shared anchor designs demonstrate offset reductions of more than 50%,
from 1,370 meters to 580 meters for upwind turbines. This could potentially reduce loads
on power cables significantly.
Shared Anchors in Wind Farm Optimization
Shared anchor configurations introduce an additional layer of complexity and opportunity
in layout optimization for floating wind. Recent research has shown that system-level op-
timization frameworks can integrate shared anchor design, considering both quasi-static
load distribution and dynamic turbine-floater-mooring-anchor interactions [56, 51]. Opti-
mization studies demonstrate that shared mooring and anchor systems can lower total
station-keeping costs by up to 34% for commercial-scale wind farms, primarily by reduc-
ing anchor counts and installation effort [102, 56]. One of the most challenging aspects
of shared anchor design is ensuring ”layout equilibrium”, ie. maintaining equilibrium at
the wanted position with zero external forcings. A novel approach, [55], have developed
a linearized model of a shared mooring floating wind array, that uses the ratio or weight
required between the mooring lines to reduce the optimization process. However, should
be noted that this was mainly developed for catenary mooring. [42, 51, 55].
Research Gaps and Future Directions
Although mooring systems are fairly well known from the oil and gas as well as the ship-
ping industry, there are still many aspects yet to be covered when used for FOWT projects.
Long-term performance validation of synthetic materials under FOWT loading conditions
remains incomplete. DNV is currently working to demonstrate fitness of nylon rope for ro-
bust, long-term floating wind turbine moorings, though extensive field data is still needed
[124]. As with mooring lines, significant knowledge gaps still remain about the long-term
performance characterization of shared anchor systems, in particular under the distinc-
tive loading experienced by FOWT. Current design standards primarily derive from oil and
gas applications, which may not adequately address floating-wind operational character-
istics. Thus the development of floating wind-specific qualification procedures therefore
represents a critical priority [14, 129, 102].

Layout Optimization
Layout optimization of offshore wind farms can be traced back to the 90s, with the most
noticeable contribution being [86]. Mosetti et. al [86] managed to improve a wind farm’s
efficiency by using a Genetic Algorithm (GA) combined with a simple cost function and
Jensens wake model to calculate the power produced. In all cases the model returned
a layout, that included fewer turbines than initially installed, showcasing the relation be-
tween power production and installation cost.

sign variables and complex design space. For this reason many studies shy away from
gradient based optimization, and focus more on gradient-free heuristic algorithms[70].

2.3
Floating Offshore Wind Farm Layout Optimization
Floating offshore wind turbine (FOWT) layout optimization has emerged as a specialized
field that diverges significantly from fixed-bottom approaches due to the unique opportu-
nities and constraints presented by floating platforms. The literature demonstrates sub-
stantial potential for improvements, with studies reporting efficiency gains ranging from
modest percentages to dramatic claims of up to 42.7%, though the practical achievabil-
ity of such extreme improvements remains questionable given the idealized conditions
typically assumed in optimization studies.
2.3.1
Algorithmic Development and Layout Optimization Approaches
The evolution of optimization algorithms for floating wind farms has focused primarily on
addressing the computational challenges associated with dense arrays and the coupling
between platform motion and wake effects. Early work by [77] addressed a fundamental
limitation in random search algorithms when applied to densely populated floating arrays
by introducing an adaptive scale factor that reduces step sizes based on infeasible itera-
tions. This modification proved particularly valuable for floating systems where mooring
constraints create complex feasible regions that traditional algorithms struggle to navigate
efficiently.
However, the field suffers from a concerning lack of systematic algorithm comparison. [38]
demonstrated improvements through surrogate modeling coupled with random search,
achieving modest AEP gains of 0.57-1.55% in reduced wind conditions. While these
results highlight the potential importance of optimization for challenging sites, the small
magnitude of improvements raises questions about the cost-benefit ratio of complex opti-
mization procedures, particularly when considering the uncertainty in wake modeling and
economic assumptions.
The development of multi-objective approaches has shown more promise, with [40] de-
veloping the Multi-Objective Random Search (MORS) algorithm that simultaneously op-
timizes turbine positioning and quantity selection. The superior performance compared
to NSGA-II suggests that problem-specific algorithms may be necessary for floating wind

farm optimization, though the lack of standardized benchmarking makes it difficult to as-
sess the true significance of these algorithmic advances.
A critical limitation across all optimization studies is the predominant reliance on the
Jensen wake model [77, 117, 40], despite well-documented concerns about its appro-
priateness for floating systems where platform motion and vertical wake deflection signifi-
cantly alter wake behavior. [106] specifically noted ”relative differences” between Jensen

2.3.2
Dynamic Positioning and Platform Mobility
The concept of leveraging platform mobility for optimization represents the most inno-
vative yet controversial aspect of floating wind farm research. [71] introduced the Yaw
and Induction-based Turbine Repositioning (YITuR) concept, claiming efficiency gains
of 42.7% through passive repositioning.
However, these dramatic results depend on
highly specific and potentially unrealistic mooring system configurations, including ex-
tremely long mooring lines and carefully positioned anchors that may not be practical in
real deployments.
More realistic assessments of dynamic positioning potential have yielded more modest but
still significant results. [106] demonstrated efficiency gains of up to 4.4% with larger watch
circles, translating to potential 5% reductions in levelized production cost. [3] achieved
20% wake loss reductions using checkerboard patterns with optimized farm orientations,
though the practical implementation of such coordinated repositioning strategies remains
unclear.
The most comprehensive framework for dynamic optimization is presented by [61], which
developed the ”Wake Escape” methodology addressing both layout and operational opti-
mization levels. While achieving a more modest 1.183% reduction in levelized production
cost, this work provides a more realistic assessment of the trade-offs involved in dynamic
positioning, acknowledging that mobility can have adverse effects if not properly man-
aged.
A fundamental concern across all dynamic positioning research is the lack of considera-
tion for practical constraints including dynamic cable limitations, maintenance access, and
safety implications of large platform movements. [80] partially addresses this by demon-
strating that allowing turbine relocation can decrease wake losses by 18% while reducing
mooring costs by 17%, but still does not adequately address the dynamic cable design im-
plications that could severely constrain allowable platform movements. In practice, elec-
trical design and cable mechanics constrain feasible motions and layouts. Array/export
cable routing, minimum bend radius at touchdown and J-tubes, and allowable dynamic
tensions limit large platform displacement and favor conservative mean-offset assump-
tions.
2.3.3

shared approaches. However, detailed cost analyses have revealed more nuanced re-
sults. [16] found that while shared anchor layouts are technically feasible, they may not
improve procurement costs due to spacing constraints between turbines. Critically, this

of sharing, and that arrays with greater sharing exhibit larger deviations in platform off-
sets. These findings highlight a critical trade-off that is often overlooked in cost-focused
analyses: increased sharing may reduce anchor quantities but at the expense of system
complexity and potentially reduced performance.
2.3.4
Economic Integration and Techno-Economic Optimization
The development of comprehensive economic models for floating wind farm optimization
has progressed from simple cost comparisons to integrated frameworks that capture the
complex interdependencies between technical and economic factors. [58] achieved a
notable 34.5 million € profit increase compared to grid-based layouts in a ScotWind case
study, with AEP improvements, anchor cost optimization, and availability loss reduction
identified as primary drivers.
However, economic modeling remains constrained by significant uncertainties, particu-
larly in operational expenditure (OPEX) estimation. [85] highlighted the lack of standard-
ized operation and maintenance approaches in the floating wind industry, where prototype
projects continue to test new methods. This uncertainty fundamentally limits the reliability
of economic optimization results, as OPEX can represent a substantial portion of total
project costs.
Sensitivity analyses have revealed the dominance of external economic factors over tech-
nical optimization decisions.
[58] identified electricity price fluctuations as having the
greatest impact on NPV outcomes, with even small price deviations having significant
financial implications. This finding suggests that technical optimization, while valuable,
may be secondary to market risk management in determining project success.
The integration of electrical network optimization represents a particularly underexplored
area despite its potential significance. [128] demonstrated savings of up to 14% in cable-
related investment costs through integrated electrical infrastructure optimization, yet most
floating wind farm optimization studies treat electrical systems as post-optimization design
tasks rather than integrated components.

2.3.5
Wake Effects and Platform Dynamics
Research on wake-platform interactions has revealed both the complexity of floating sys-
tem behavior and the limitations of current modeling approaches.
[69] demonstrated
through large eddy simulations that floating platform motion primarily affects wake be-
havior through mean platform pitch rather than dynamic motions, with floating turbine
wakes deflecting upwards compared to fixed systems. This work suggests that existing
reduced-order wake models could be adapted with appropriate pitch-deflection adjust-
ments, though such modifications remain largely unimplemented in optimization studies.
The interaction between wake steering and platform motion presents particular challenges
for floating systems. [79] discovered that platform offsets under yaw-misaligned condi-
tions can counteract wake steering benefits, creating least-optimal watch circle widths
that vary with yaw misalignment and turbine spacing. This finding fundamentally alters
the optimization landscape for wake control in floating systems, yet most optimization
studies continue to treat wake steering and platform motion as decoupled phenomena.
[22] demonstrated that wake losses are sensitive to both power density and layout ge-
ometry, with different anchor configurations showing varying sensitivity to environmental
headings. This work revealed the counterintuitive result that baseline designs with higher
power density can have lower wake losses than shared anchor systems with lower power
density, highlighting the complex interactions between layout geometry and wake behav-
ior that are poorly captured by simplified optimization models.
2.3.6
Validation Crisis and Methodological Concerns
A fundamental weakness across the entire field is the lack of validation against opera-
tional floating wind farm data. Most studies rely on simplified wake models, steady-state
assumptions, and extrapolated cost data from fixed-bottom systems or prototypes. The
few attempts at validation, such as [106]’s comparison between different wake models,
have revealed significant discrepancies in optimization outcomes, yet the field has not
adequately addressed these validation challenges.
The computational limitations of current approaches also raise scalability concerns. Most
optimization studies focus on small arrays (10-50 turbines) using simplified models, while
commercial floating wind farms are expected to contain 100+ turbines operating under
complex environmental conditions with multiple regulatory and stakeholder constraints.
2.3.7
Critical Research Gaps and Future Directions
Despite the optimistic claims in much of the literature, several critical gaps limit the prac-
tical applicability of current research:
Model Validation: The field urgently needs validation against operational floating wind
farm data. Industry-academia partnerships are essential to develop benchmark datasets
that can validate optimization assumptions and methodologies.
Integrated System Modeling: Current approaches often treat aerodynamics, hydrody-
namics, structural dynamics, and electrical systems in isolation despite strong coupling ef-
fects. More sophisticated integrated modeling frameworks are needed that capture these
interactions while remaining computationally tractable for optimization.
Economic Realism: OPEX modeling needs refinement using data from demonstration
and commercial projects rather than extrapolations from fixed-bottom systems. The high
uncertainty in economic parameters may limit the practical value of technically sophisti-
cated optimization approaches.

Scalability and Practicality: Future frameworks must address industrial-scale farms

Risk and Uncertainty: Most current methods remain deterministic, offering limited treat-

Theory
This chapter establishes the theory behind the modeling framework used to evaluate
shared-anchor mooring concepts for floating wind arrays. First the governing loads and
simplifying assumptions are defined, then describing the relation between displacement
and stiffness through the equation of motion, then how mooring material properties and
configurations affect the stiffness, and how this translate into anchor design. Next the the-
ory behind the Gaussian wake model is established as well as the makeup of the LCOE
calculations.
3.1
Loads on FOWT
A FOWT differs from a fixed bottom turbine as they have the ability to move in an addi-
tional 6 degrees of freedom. The displacement is due to an environmental loading, from
thrust, waves and current, which is then balanced by the restoring forces from the moor-
ing system and floater design. In this work, wind thrust is the mean driver, as waves add
oscillations but negligible mean. The station-keeping is a taut mooring system, modeled
quasi-statically in MoorPy. This means that at each step, the system is in static equi-
librium, with nonlinearity being introduced from line elasticity and geometry. The loads
generated from the wind, is translated through the lines and down to the anchors. Based
on the line configuration (honeycomb or rhombic grid), the anchor loads differs and thus
the holding capacity has to be properly adjusted by accounting for the anchor mass and
seabed interaction.

3.2
Wake Theory: Gaussian Wake Models
This section describes the theory behind the wake models used in the FLORIS optimiza-
tion. When a wind turbine extracts momentum from the wind to produce power, it creates
a wake characterized by a reduction in mean wind speed (velocity deficit) and an increase
in turbulence intensity. Downstream turbines that are operating inside a wake, thus ex-
perience reduced inflow velocity and increased turbulence, which leads to lower power
production and higher fatigue loading. To accurately and efficiently estimate the deficit,
becomes increasingly important when doing optimization that includes AEP, such as this
work intends. Using AEP in the objective function, the quality of the optimization relies
on having a wake deficit model that is both accurate and efficient, in order to obtain a
meaningful solution that can be used in practice. In this work, the Gaussian wake model
is therefore chosen as the balance between accuracy and computational efficiency, en-
abling optimization of wind farm performance while capturing the most important wake
interaction dynamics.
Analytical wake models aim to predict the spatial evolution of the wake in a computation-
ally efficient way. These models typically rely on conservation of mass and momentum
applied to a control volume around the turbine and make simplifying assumptions about
the velocity deficit distribution.

3.3
Optimization Theory
Optimization is the process of finding the best solution from a set of feasible candidates,
depending on a chosen objective function. Algorithms can generally be divided into two
categories: deterministic methods, which follow a fixed computational path for a given
starting point and often exploit derivative information for efficiency on smooth problems;
and stochastic methods, which introduce randomness to explore complex or multimodal
landscapes, at the cost of repeatability.
In this work, these two categories are represented by two distinct models. The first is
a greedy algorithm, used here as a deterministic approach that incrementally builds a
solution by always choosing the option with the largest immediate gain. The second is a
random search algorithm, serving as a stochastic counterpart that explores the solution
space by sampling candidate layouts. The greedy model offers efficiency and determin-
ism but is limited to local improvements, whereas the random search model sacrifices
efficiency for broader exploration and the ability to escape local optima.
Both models aim to minimize the LCOE of a wind farm layout, but they embody funda-
mentally different philosophies of search. Their detailed formulations and applications are
presented in the following chapter.

Methodology
This chapter addresses the methodology behind the two main approaches used to solve
the wind farm layout optimization problem. Before that, the chapter also explores key
behavior such as the wakes relation with displacement, how the optimization depends on
the LCOE definition and how grid configurations can affect the layout. The findings from
these initial explorations is what lead to the development of the two models. The models
are referred to as the Greedy model and the Random Search (RS) model.
The first model uses Greedy optimization to minimize Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE)
rather than merely maximizing Annual Energy Production (AEP). The design variables
include turbine spacing and farm orientation. The model is then expanded into a two
step approach with a fixed turbine spacing. The first step maximizes the number of tur-
bines, whereas the second step minimizes the LCOE. This stage is crucial for establishing
baseline optimization capabilities that can be rapidly deployed in early-stage project de-
velopment.
The second model investigates layout optimization through RS, an approach similar to
those used in fixed-bottom wind farm design, but using a fixed number of turbines and
anchors. By allowing both turbine and anchor positions to be design variables, this stage
breaks away from rigid grid structures to explore more flexible optimization possibilities.
This approach allows for exploration of complex seabed conditions—such as heteroge-
neous soil properties or abrupt depth variations, where a standard grid configurations
becomes impractical or impossible. The freedom to adjust positions independently could
yield economic benefits by positioning turbines out of wakes, accommodating site con-
straints, and optimizing mooring costs that would otherwise force compromises in the
overall layout design. This model is expanded into incorporating turbine movement and
the dynamic relationship between anchor positioning and mooring system tensions. As
anchor locations change, both mooring line angles and lengths must be adjusted, di-
rectly affecting line tensions under operational and extreme weather loads. These ten-
sion variations alter turbine watch circles, potentially impacting cable management costs
and system survivability. By integrating tension analysis with anchor optimization, this
stage ensures that layout improvements do not compromise structural integrity or create
unforeseen operational limitations. A summary can be seen in table 4.1.
4.1
LCOE and Updating CAPEX

To include turbine displacement can be toggled on and off. If active, an additional con-

Meaning that the maximum turbine displacement cannot exceed 30% of the water depth,

thrust is used in MoorPy to get the displacement. The turbine layout is updated accord-
ingly and the AEP is calculated.
During the implementation phase, several key methods and parameters required care-
ful consideration, particularly when incorporating turbine movement into the optimization
framework. These choices typically involved balancing accuracy against computational
costs, making it essential to investigate these trade-offs. The first methodological con-
sideration involves how to update mooring line lengths following turbine repositioning.

Without proper length adjustments, turbines would simply return to their original positions
once the mooring system reaches equilibrium, negating any optimization benefits. Equally
important is the method for calculating individual turbine wind speeds. Since wind veloc-
ity directly influences turbine displacement, which subsequently affects wake patterns
experienced by downstream turbines, the accuracy of wind speed calculations creates
cascading effects throughout the optimization process. Finally, the parameter selection
for expanding CAPEX to include mooring and anchor costs is crucial for realistic optimiza-
tion outcomes. Without incorporating these cost components, the optimization algorithm
would unrealistically maximize turbine to turbine distances to minimize wake losses, ig-
noring the substantial economic penalties associated with extended mooring systems.
Including these costs ensures the optimization balances wake mitigation benefits against
mooring infrastructure expenses.

4.4.3
Updating mooring line lengths
In order to find a suitable balance between displacement accuracy and computational
efficiency, the following 3 strategies are investigated:
• Pythagoras: Using pythagoras theorem and the geometric points of the turbine and
anchors, the line length can be calculated quickly by c =
√
a2 + b2, with c being the
new line length, a the water depth and c the horizontal distance from the anchor to
the turbine. One drawback of this method is that the line tension will be zero.
• Hookes Law: A way to include the line tension is to model the mooring lines as
spring using Hookes law as described in chapter 3. Thus the final unstreatched
line length becomes L0 =
EA·L
T+EA, where L is the stretched lenght, found by using
pythagoras, T is the desired pretension, set to 15% of the MBL, and EA is the axial
stiffness of the mooring lines, determined by the material and diameter. A fault of this
assumption is that synthetic mooring lines do not have a linear stiffness. However,
as long as the range of operation is not too severe, ie. more than 10% of line
elongation, it is assumed that the stiffness is constant for that range.
• Moorpy convergence This method optimizes mooring line lengths to achieve target
pretension through a systematic two-phase approach using scipy’s L-BFGS-B opti-
mizer for both phases. L-BFGS-B is specifically chosen because it handles bound
constraints efficiently (essential for preventing physically unrealistic line lengths),
uses limited memory making it suitable for this moderate-scale problem, and pro-
vides robust convergence for the smooth, continuous relationship between line length
and tension in mooring systems. Phase 1 performs global scaling by using L-BFGS-
B to find the optimal uniform scaling factor (searched within bounds of 0.3x to 3.0x
of original lengths) that minimizes the error of the best-performing line, continuing
until at least one line achieves tension within 10% of the target pretension. Phase
2 then applies L-BFGS-B optimization to individual lines sequentially, targeting the
worst-performing line in each iteration and optimizing only that line’s length (within
bounds of 0.5x to 2.0x of its current length) while keeping all other lines fixed. The
process repeats for up to 500 iterations until either all lines are within the final toler-
ance (typically 3% of target pretension) or no further improvement is possible. This
approach is robust because it first addresses system-wide scaling issues that affect
all lines equally, then fine-tunes individual lines to account for geometric differences
between mooring legs, ensuring that each line can independently achieve the de-
sired pretension regardless of variations in anchor positioning or fairlead geometry.

the new position, then applying varying forces to determine corresponding displacements.

5.3.2
Experiment design and computational budget
The study reuses the six initial layouts from the previous section (two grid types × three
spacings). For each layout, three mooring-material cases (polyester, nylon, wire) were
optimized, yielding 6 × 3 = 18 experiments. Each RS run used 5 iterations with a runtime
of 40,000 s per iteration (approximately 11.11 h), for a total of ≈55.5 h per case. Under this
time-limited process, the number of objective evaluations depends strongly on layout size
(number of turbines/anchors) and on whether the AEP calculation includes static displace-
ment coupling, because FLORIS must then be evaluated for every wind-direction/speed
bin per candidate.
5.3.3

subtle. This behavior follows from the geometry: moving an anchor with three attached

Modeling effects and practical implications
Because runs were stopped by timers, layouts with more turbines obtained fewer objective
evaluations, which degraded convergence and, in some cases, prevented improvement
Optimization of floating wind farms with shared anchor configurations
71
==PAGE:86==
Figure 5.18: AEP differences for optimized grids at 1000 m spacing.
(notably honeycomb at 1000 and 1500 m). When static displacement was included, each
candidate had to be evaluated in both FLORIS (for every wind-speed/direction bin) and
MoorPy, cutting the evaluation count by orders of magnitude under the same runtime. A
coarse wind rose accelerates RS but under-resolves directionality of wake interactions
and tension peaks; a pragmatic workflow is multi-fidelity: explore with a coarse rose and
relaxed constraints, then re-evaluate high-performing candidates with a finer rose and
displacement coupling.
A second, non-trivial consequence of RS is layout individuality. Small, turbine-specific off-
sets (0–250 m) and anchor relocations yield non-uniform mooring geometries across the
farm. Loads and displacements therefore differ from turbine to turbine, which cascades
into additional engineering effort: more unique line-length/tension checks, turbine-specific
ULS/FLS load cases, watch-circle verifications, and expanded certification documenta-
tion. The installation process also becomes more complex and costly, because individual-
ized layouts require varied cut lengths and terminations, more intricate deck planning and
sequencing, tighter as-laid survey tolerances, and a greater risk of rework if components
are mismatched. Cable routing and metrology likewise lose the efficiencies of repetition
when turbine positions are non-repeating. These additional costs (engineering hours,
vessel time, and quality assurance) is not included the modeled LCOE and can quickly
outweigh the small gains observed from RS (typically 0.3–0.7% at 2000 m). Taken together
with industry practice that disfavors spacings below about 7D due to wake losses, the net
message is that micro-adjustments should only be pursued when they are tension-aware,
respect watch-circle limits, and deliver benefits that clearly exceed the added design and
installation complexity.
In summary, the modest LCOE improvements achieved by RS at 8D arise mainly from
mooring/anchor cost reductions rather than AEP changes, while the induced turbine-by-
turbine variability in loads materially increases downstream engineering and installation
burden. A multi-fidelity, constraint-aware approach mitigates runtime limits, but the value
proposition of individualized layouts should be judged against the real project costs they
introduce.

Displacement and tension
Tension maps for unloaded vs. loaded states (10 m/s from south) at 2000 m spacing are
shown in figs. 5.21 and 5.22. Honeycomb layouts tend to split the front load between two
upwind lines while the remaining line slackens, whereas rhombic layouts exhibit stronger
single-line loading for some units. Maximum tension increases of roughly 100% (honey-
comb) and 125% (rhombic) were observed. The larger rhombic increase is consistent with
longer spans and directional alignment that channels more load into a single upwind line
for certain turbines.
Across materials, the trends mirror axial stiffness EA. Wire, being the stiffest, limits plat-
form excursions the most and therefore shows the smallest displacements, but this comes
at the cost of the highest peak tensions. Nylon sits at the opposite end: its compliance al-
lows the largest displacements yet tempers peak tensions. Polyester falls between these
extremes (figs. 5.24 and 5.25). From a system viewpoint, stiff lines are effective at sup-
pressing rotor–cable motion but transmit larger loads to anchors and can elevate ultimate
demands and CAPEX; more compliant lines soften transients and reduce snap loads, but
their larger tension cycles can be more damaging in fatigue. Polyester offers a workable
compromise, although ultimate and fatigue verifications remain necessary before com-
mitting to a design.
Directional sweeps over a 60◦symmetry sector (fig. 5.23) put these tendencies into con-
text. Rhombic layouts exhibit greater offsets than honeycomb at comparable tension lev-
els because their longer spans permit more elongation for similar strain changes. Both
topologies show dips in the mean displacement accompanied by wide min–max ranges
when the inflow aligns turbines nose-to-tail, placing wakes directly on downwind neigh-
bors. The resulting thrust variability and mooring response are consistent with the AEP
patterns seen in the layout maps and visualization can be seen in appendix K.1.

benefits require either more compute or algorithmic acceleration.

5.4
Cross-model synthesis and final remarks
Taken together, the results point to a clear division of strengths between the two optimiza-
tion models. The greedy framework (including the two-phase variant with fixed spacing)
consistently identifies layouts whose economics are driven primarily by turbine count and
gross spacing, while Random Search adjustments of turbine and anchor positions deliver
only marginal additional value and can introduce undesirable mechanical side effects.
From an economic perspective, the dominant lever is the number of installed units at a
given spacing. Phase 1 of the two-phase greedy method maximizes turbine count and
largely determines the achieved LCOE band, Phase 2 orientation refinements add at most
a few tenths of a percent and often less than 0.05% as seen in table 5.4. This pattern holds
across topologies and spacings in table 5.2: honeycomb layouts fit more turbines and thus
yield higher total AEP, whereas rhombic layouts occupy a larger footprint per unit and ac-
commodate fewer turbines but tend to realize a lower LCOE at the same spacing, due
to the higher turbine efficiency. The wake-loss progression with decreasing spacing is
monotone and steep, with the 4D honeycomb case reaching ∼26% loss, yet total AEP

remains highest in that dense configuration because of the sheer turbine count. Practi-
cally, this highlights a classic trade-off: minimizing LCOE and maximizing profit are not
synonymous, and a multi-objective stance that explicitly values farm density would be
better aligned with developer objectives than single-objective LCOE alone.
Random Search does not materially change this narrative. With spacing fixed, the RS fine-
tuning of turbine and anchor positions produces LCOE shifts on the order of 0.3–0.7% at
8D (table 5.8). The gains arise mostly from shorter boundary lines and marginal anchor
savings; AEP is effectively unchanged. At the same time, RS perturbs mooring geometry
in ways that increase static displacements and alter loaded tensions, even when pre-
tensions are held constant. Engineering practice limits watch-circle radii to roughly 30%
of water depth; widespread boundary-anchor relocations and one-rotor-diameter turbine
nudges push designs toward this constraint without offering commensurate energy ben-
efits. The coupling between layout geometry and line tensions is therefore too strong to
ignore in a placement optimizer—leaving tension out of the objective invites mechanically
unrealistic solutions that later fail detailed checks.
Computational scaling further tilts the balance toward the greedy approach. FLORIS run-
time grows strongly with turbine count (table 5.1), and under time-capped runs RS suffers
evaluation starvation, especially when AEP is coupled to static displacement and must be
re-evaluated across many wind bins. This effect is evident in table 5.3, where layouts with
more turbines complete far fewer objective evaluations. In contrast, the greedy method
concentrates computational effort on the coarse variables (spacing, count, orientation)
that most influence economics, reaching useful solutions even at large array sizes.
Topology choice remains consequential once loads enter the discussion. Rhombic grids
often channel a larger share of the 10 m/s south-wind load into a single upwind line for
some turbines, elevating peak tension and tension growth from unloaded to loaded states;
honeycomb grids can be oriented to split the front load more evenly across two lines. At
sites with strongly directional metocean forcing, this difference can be leveraged: a hon-
eycomb orientation aligned to the prevailing direction can distribute loads and reduce
required MBL margins on select lines, while a rhombic pattern may demand higher local
MBL or more conservative anchors for turbines that see single-line dominance. These
trends reinforce the need to co-optimize energy and loads rather than treat AEP in isola-
tion.
The overall takeaway is therefore twofold. First, for fixed spacing and boundary, increas-
ing turbine count delivers the largest economic effect; orientation tweaks provide small
refinements; RS-scale micro-moves of turbines and anchors do not materially shift LCOE
and can degrade mechanical feasibility. Second, meaningful placement optimization in
floating farms must internalize line-tension constraints and displacement limits. A practical
formulation would embed constraints such as tension fractions of MBL and explicit watch-
circle bounds, include direction-dependent loading, and penalize designs that concentrate
load into a single line. Under such a formulation, the greedy search should remain the
backbone for spacing and count, while any local refinement step should be tension-aware
and limited to bounded, engineering-relevant moves. In the near term, priority should be
given to parallel wind-bin evaluation, limited batch updates of high-leverage boundary an-
chors, and a coarse-to-fine (multi-fidelity) screening pass; the subsequent subsections
detail how to extend the formulation, accelerate evaluations, and add cost/load realism.

5.5.2
Algorithmic upgrades
Greedy search remains the backbone for spacing and count, but local refinement should
be tension-aware and bounded.
• Hybrid strategy: greedy for spacing/#turbines →constrained local search (e.g.,
Bayesian optimization with penalties/augmented Lagrangian) on selected variables
(boundary anchors, a subset of border turbines) with explicit constraints on T/MBL
and watch-circle.
• Batch moves: move k > 1 elements per iteration (e.g., an anchor cluster plus
adjacent turbine) to exploit correlated savings; restrict moves to ≤1D for turbines
to avoid cable/layout penalties.
• Multi-start & seeding: seed local refinements from the best N greedy orientations
to avoid local traps observed near equal-LCOE configurations (fig. 5.7).
• Multi-fidelity AEP: explore with a coarse wind rose and simplified wake model, then
re-evaluate elites with fine roses and displacement coupling to control compute cost.
5.5.3
Compute acceleration
Evaluation scarcity is the main limiter once turbine count grows.
• Parallelization: distribute wind bins and candidate evaluations; cache partial FLORIS
results for small perturbations; precompute wake superposition for invariant sectors.
• Surrogates: train local response surfaces for ∆AEP and ∆-tension versus small
geometric moves (e.g., Gaussian processes with uncertainty checks) and use them
inside the optimizer with periodic truth-model refresh.
• Adaptive budgets: enforce a minimum evaluations-per-iteration quota and use
early-stopping/early-accept if no improvement is possible within the current trust
region.
5.5.4
Modeling fidelity and cost realism
Several model extensions close the gap to design sign-off.
• Tilt and platform–wake coupling: include platform pitch/roll–AEP coupling (e.g.,
applying orientation- and wind-speed–dependent AEP derates) so that small geo-
metric gains do not mask dynamic energy penalties.

• Non-linear rope behavior: reflect stiffness changes with tension range for synthet-

• Array cable routing: penalize cable length, crossings, and thermal limits.

5.5.5
Topology- and site-aware guidance
Topology should be chosen for load sharing as much as for wakes. Honeycomb can
be aligned to split upwind loads across two lines for prevailing directions; rhombic may
require higher local MBL where single-line dominance occurs. A site-specific study that
sweeps orientations against the joint wind–wave rose and applies tension constraints will
formalize this guidance and can justify topology mixing across the lease area if permitted.
5.5.6
Validation and benchmarking
Before deployment, validate against higher-fidelity tools and field data where available.
• Cross-check representative cases with aero-hydro-servo-elastic and dynamic moor-
ing solvers; compare line tensions, offsets, and AEP deltas for key wind sectors.
• Establish benchmark cases (e.g., the 8D honeycomb and rhombic at Havbredey)
with fixed seeds and share reference outputs to enable regression testing of algo-

signal from spacing and turbine count while eliminating mechanically fragile layouts. This
trajectory also creates room for genuine local gains by targeting boundary anchors and
orientation under explicit load-sharing constraints, rather than relying on unconstrained
micro-moves that offer limited LCOE benefit and complicate engineering.

6
Conclusion
This work has investigated layout optimization methodologies for floating offshore wind
farms utilizing shared anchor configurations, addressing a critical gap in commercial-scale
floating wind development. Through the implementation of two complementary optimiza-
tion approaches—greedy grid-based optimization and Random Search fine-tuning—this
study has demonstrated both the potential and limitations of current optimization strate-
gies for floating wind farm design.
6.1
Key Findings
The results reveal that turbine count and spacing fundamentally dominate the economic
performance of floating wind farm layouts. The greedy optimization approach successfully
identified that maximizing the number of turbines within spacing constraints serves as the
primary driver of LCOE minimization, with orientation refinements providing only marginal
improvements of less than 0.05% in most cases. This finding contradicts the expectation
that advanced local optimization methods will deliver significant improvements in floating
wind farm performance.
Concerning grid configurations, the honeycomb layout consistently accommodated higher
turbine densities and achieved superior total Annual Energy Production (AEP), while
rhombic configurations demonstrated lower LCOE values at equivalent spacings due to
improved per-turbine efficiency. The trade-off between wake losses and turbine count
emerged as a critical design consideration, with the 1000m spacing honeycomb con-
figuration achieving 25.77% wake losses—comparable to the Lillgrund wind farm—yet
delivering the highest total AEP due to turbine density.
The comparative analysis between honeycomb and rhombic configurations revealed dis-
tinct performance characteristics that inform design selection criteria. At 2000m spacing,
honeycomb layouts accommodated 108 turbines generating 8356 GWh annually with
9.76% wake losses, while rhombic configurations managed only 71 turbines producing
5614 GWh with reduced wake losses of 7.78%. This pattern persisted across all spac-
ing configurations, with honeycomb consistently achieving 32% higher total AEP despite
experiencing 2-6% greater wake losses. However, the rhombic configuration’s superior
per-turbine efficiency translated to consistently lower LCOE values, with differences rang-
ing from 89.50 $/MWh at 2000m spacing to 102.45 $/MWh at 1000m spacing, compared
to the honeycombs 91.3 $/MWh and 110.30 $/MWh for the same spacings. The geomet-
ric constraints of rhombic layouts, requiring larger footprints per turbine due to mooring
line angles, fundamentally limit turbine density but improve individual turbine performance
through reduced wake interference.
The Random Search optimization, despite allowing micro-adjustments of turbine and an-
chor positions, produced modest LCOE improvements of only 0.3-0.7% at 2000m spac-
ing. These gains originated primarily from reduced mooring line lengths and anchor costs
rather than meaningful AEP improvements. Notably, the computational burden of evalu-
ating displacement-aware AEP calculations severely constrained the optimization effec-
tiveness under time-limited conditions.
Material selection analysis indicated minimal impact on overall LCOE when normalized
to equivalent Minimum Breaking Load (MBL) values, suggesting that the choice between
polyester, nylon, and wire rope materials becomes secondary to geometric optimization

decisions in shared anchor configurations. However, material selection contributes greatly
to displacement response, which can indirectly affect costs in ways that have not been
considered for this work.
6.2
Methodological Insights
The comparison between optimization approaches revealed fundamental scaling issues
with current methodologies. FLORIS runtime exhibited strong dependency on turbine
count, creating evaluation bottlenecks that favored sparser layouts under time-constrained
optimization. The greedy approach demonstrated superior computational efficiency by fo-
cusing effort on variables with the greatest economic impact—spacing and turbine count—
rather than pursuing marginal gains through local position adjustments.
The integration of platform displacement calculations into the optimization loop, while
technically feasible through MoorPy, proved computationally prohibitive for realistic farm
sizes. The displacement magnitudes observed (typically < 50m) produced negligible
wake interaction changes, questioning the cost-benefit ratio of including dynamic cou-
pling in layout optimization workflows. The findings suggest that practical floating wind
farm optimization should prioritize turbine density maximization within acceptable wake
loss thresholds rather than pursuing sophisticated local refinements, unless other factors,
such as loads, are of interests. The dominant role of spacing and turbine count implies that
layout optimization efforts would be most productively focused on these coarse variables
during early design phases.
6.3
Limitations and Future Research
This work acknowledges several limitations that constrain the generalizability of results.
The assumption of uniform water depth and only focusing on operational conditions sim-
plify the optimization problem beyond realistic deployment scenarios. The quasi-static
analysis approach, while computationally tractable, neglects important dynamic coupling
effects that may influence optimal layouts and neglect design deciding factors.
Future research should address the integration of higher-fidelity wake models with layout
optimization, development of multi-fidelity approaches that balance accuracy with compu-
tational efficiency, and validation of optimization results against operational floating wind
farm data. The incorporation of realistic constraints including regulatory requirements,
installation logistics, and maintenance access represents critical areas for methodology
advancement.
The optimization of electrical infrastructure, including inter-array cables and offshore sub-
stations, remains largely unexplored in floating wind applications despite representing
significant cost components. Integration of these systems with layout optimization could
yield meaningful economic benefits.
6.4
Concluding Remarks
The transition from single-turbine demonstrations to commercial-scale floating wind farms
requires optimization methodologies that acknowledge the unique constraints and oppor-
tunities of floating systems. This work demonstrates that while sophisticated optimization
techniques can achieve measurable improvements, the greatest economic benefits de-
rive from fundamental design decisions regarding turbine density and spacing rather than
micro-level position adjustments.
The practical deployment of floating wind farms at commercial scale will likely benefit more
from robust, computationally efficient optimization approaches that focus on high-impact
86

design variables than from complex algorithms pursuing marginal gains. As the floating
wind industry matures, validation of optimization methodologies against operational data
will become essential for establishing reliable design practices that can support the large-
scale deployment targets envisioned by industry and policymakers.
The integration of shared anchor systems with layout optimization represents a promising
avenue for cost reduction, but requires careful consideration of the trade-offs between
anchor count reduction and increased system complexity. Success in this area will depend
on developing design methodologies that can accurately predict and optimize these trade-
offs while maintaining system reliability and performance.

Brute method
The results where generated using a brute method with a square boundary, sized 8000m

D.1
Honeycomb
The brute force optimization results, presented in fig. D.1 and fig. D.3, reveal a funda-
mental trade-off in wind farm design. While increasing turbine density predictably raises
total farm AEP, individual turbine efficiency simultaneously decreases. This inverse rela-
tionship between farm-scale energy production and turbine-level performance highlights
why it is crucial to include economical factors, such as LCOE, to account for the econom-
ical impact of additional turbines, and increased spacing, rather than focusing solely on
energy output.
