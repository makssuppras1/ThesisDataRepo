Terms 
In this thesis, some terms will be elaborated in this section, and are thus considered pre-defined for 
the entirety of the following text.  
• A recombinant (implied: snake) antivenom is here defined as recombinantly produced 
antibodies (typically human) that in rationally designed, oligoclonal mixtures (or on its own 
in typically IgG format) can be applied to neutralise a given (or several) snake species’ 
venoms, as a therapy.  
• A consensus toxin is here defined as a protein comprised of the consensus amino acid 
composition across more than one toxin. Specifically, in this report, the term is later used, 
implying the relevant, specific type I a-neurotoxin, derived from 8 cobra species and 3 mamba 
species, which was recombinantly produced. 
• A “naïve rabbit” refers to an unimmunised rabbit of the same physical characteristics as the 
immunised rabbits. 
• When analysing ELISA results, “response” signifies the signal strength of the Abs405 
measurements at the various serum dilutions. 
• a is used to indicate the specificity of a given antibody, e.g. an anti-8xhistidine antibody will 
be abbreviated aHis, or aFLAG-tag meaning the antibody has binding specificity to the 
FLAG-tag. 
• “Coupling” a toxin or tagged toxin in ELISA refers to the binding event between the given 
tag and e.g. aHis antibody, or simply between a protein and a (MaxiSorpTM) plastic well.  
• A number of molecular interactions (e.g. when the tagged toxin binds the aHis antibody) are 
designated with a colon (:), e.g. aHis:tagged-toxin.

0. ABSTRACT 
In 2019, it has been 125 years since the invention of snakebite antivenom. However, every year, 
81,000-138,000 people still succumb to venomous snakebites, and many due to the lack of antivenom 
treatment. To increase accessibility to effective antivenom, cross-neutralising antivenoms offer a way 
to generate region-specific antivenoms, to replace current monovalent antivenoms.  
In this thesis, recombinant mipartoxin-1 (rMTx) from Micrurus mipartitus was produced in 
the LE577 E. coli strain from the University of Copenhagen and was used to immunise rabbits. 
Obtained anti-rMTx serum recognized rMTx and M. mipartitus snake venom, and is hoped to 
recognise other three-finger toxin (3FTx)-dominated venoms of Micrurus spp. Conversely, a 
consensus type I a-neurotoxin (called cNTx) was produced, against which raised antiserum 
significantly recognized venom fractions from Dendroaspis polylepis, Naja kaouthia and N. 
melanoleuca, in ELISA assays. These fractions contain type I a-neurotoxins (T1ans) of 63-90% 
homology to cNTx. Anti-recombinant-mipartoxin-1 serum protected mice from 1 LD99s when 
injected i.p. with recombinant mipartoxin-1, incubated with the antiserum, but so did serum from a 
naïve rabbit. Immunoglobulin Gs (IgGs) were purified, but neither IgGs from naïve- nor immunised 
rabbits were able to protect mice from 2 LD99s. Likewise, for anti-cNTx, both immunised- and naïve 
rabbit serum protected mice from 1.3 LD50s of tagged cNTx, whereas purified IgGs did not.  
Taken together, this study demonstrates that it is possible to generate bioactive, soluble snake 
toxins by expression in the LE577 strain, and that a specific IgG immune response can be obtained 
from immunisation with the recombinant toxins. However, due to the very low immunogenicity of 
the toxins, higher dosages must be utilized and/or immunogenicity-enhancing adjuvant approaches 
must be explored in the future for T1an immunisation.

1. General Introduction  
Labelled as one of WHO’s current 20 Neglected Tropical Diseases (NTD), snakebite envenoming 
was officially re-recognized in 2017, after having its label removed in 2013 [1–3]. To this very day, 
125 years after the invention of conventional antivenom [4,5], venomous snakebites still remain a 
large public health problem in many countries of the world. In 2017, it was estimated that between 
81,000-138,000 deaths occur on a worldwide basis, whereas 400,000 victims are left permanently 
disabled as a consequence of venomous snakebites [2,6,7]. Adding to these numbers, previous studies 
have found that many incidents are never reported. Indicatively, a study in Kenya found that 68% of 
snakebite victims questioned, initially sought therapy by alternative medicines, before turning to 
hospitalization [8]. Mostly, areas at risk are located in Africa, East- and Southeast Asia, Australia, 
and America, as seen in Figure 1 (global distribution of snakebite incidents from Harrison et al. 2009 
[9]), and in particular, impoverished, rural communities within these areas are mostly at risk 
[1,2,6,10].  
 
==PAGE:11==
 
 
 
 
Page 10 of 72

One might feel tempted to ask; if antivenom was invented 125 years ago, how can it still 
remain a problem in today’s society, when so many people are affected every single year? The answer 
is multifactorial. Firstly, influential power on international politics by the affected impoverished 
nations is weak, resulting in little attention and economic incentive for increased research into solving 
the problem [9,11–13]; which the relabelling snakebite as a NTD may help with [1,3,14,15], and 
especially the more recent announcement of the Wellcome Fund’s support for the field [16]. 
Secondly, impoverished nations have limited access to necessary technology for antivenom 
development, and/or education for establishment of prominent research institutions.  
Seen from a technological point of view, heterologous antiserum obtained from immunising 
large mammals (e.g. horses & sheep) has many drawbacks, but remains standard of care today [7,17]. 
Snakebite therapy has not drastically improved with advances in biotechnology, mainly because 
snake venoms are a complex mixture of many toxins of varying toxicity and immunogenicity, as well 
as many other small molecules [6]. Furthermore, the most medically relevant toxins are not always 
the most immunogenic, as has been demonstrated for three-finger toxins (3FTxs) and phospholipase 
A2s (PLA2s) [18–21]. As a result, specific antibody titres from immunisation has been shown to only 
contain 5-36% relevant antibodies, as a consequence increasing the necessary envenomation-
neutralising-dosage for patients [17,18,22,23]. This higher dosage heightens the exposure to 
heterologous proteins of antivenoms, simultaneously increasing the risk of (severe) adverse reactions 
in patients, which by themselves can be life-threatening [23–25].

To mitigate the drawbacks of conventional antiserum and the disadvantages of its production 
(see overview of drawbacks in Figure 2), the scientific community has attempted many approaches 
of improvement both in techniques relying on animals and animal-independent technologies [26,27]. 
An example of a method from the animal-dependent, innovative immunisation techniques, is 
immunisation with recombinantly produced immunogens; that is, producing only medically relevant 
toxins, rather than whole-venom or fractions of whole-venom from snakes. An example of an animal-
independent antibody discovery technique is phage display, in which single-chain variable fragments, 
scFvs, can be discovered against e.g. medically relevant toxins, enabling the opportunity of producing 
IgGs in large scale with a 100% target-specific immunoglobulin G (IgG) yield [28].

In the work behind this thesis, both techniques have been utilized; production of recombinant 
peptides as immunogens for animal immunisation, but also for in vitro phage display to discover 
scFvs. Two peptides were investigated. The first, mipartoxin-1 (MTx); a 3FTx from M. mipartitus 
which was chosen, as there is currently no commercial antivenom available for 3FTx-dominated 
venoms from Micrurus snakes in Central- or South America. Furthermore, Micrurus venom is in 
general difficult to procure, and so, there is a huge advantage in being independent of snakes for toxin 
obtainment. The hope is to generate an antitoxin that may neutralise M. mipartitus venom and toxins 
of high homology in other 3FTx-dominated venoms.  
The second peptide, cNTx, was generated through multiple alignment of peptide sequence of 
multiple 3FTxs, more specifically type I a-neurotoxins (T1ans) from mamba and cobra snakes. The 
hope is to generate an inter-mamba and cobra antitoxin for sub-Saharan Africa, which can be used to 
fortify existing antivenoms [29]. Following recombinant expression, rabbits were immunised with 
the peptides, and corresponding rabbits’ antisera were used for in vivo neutralisation studies in mice.  
 
1.2 Project Aim  
The aim of the project was three-fold. Firstly, to generate cNTx and recombinant MTx (rMTx) by 
recombinant expression. Secondly, to discover scFvs by phage display against either protein with 
their Ub19His10-tag (called t-cNTx and t-MTx). Thirdly, to generate antisera against the proteins by 
immunisation of rabbits. Then, purify IgGs from the antisera and then antisera, IgGs and scFvs were 
tested for neutralisation capacity against the recombinant toxins. Furthermore, rMTx- and t-MTx-
immunised rabbits’ sera were tested for neutralisation against M. mipartitus snake venom in mice. 
Conversely, cNTx antiserum was tested for neutralisation against toxins with 63-90% homology, in 
order to test the limitations of cross-reactivity. All sera were tested for antigen recognition by enzyme-
linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) as well. However, discovery of scFv binders to t-MTx or t-
cNTx was unsuccessful. More details will be given in the phage display section, later in this report. 
The three overall goals are shortly stated here: 
 
1. To produce a consensus T1an, and mipartoxin-1, by recombinant expression, using an E. 
coli strain engineered for soluble expression of disulphide-rich proteins.  
2. To generate a mipartoxin-1 antitoxin in the form of scFvs, discovered against t-MTx – or 
as antisera from rMTx or t-MTx-immunised rabbits.

2. Theory 
2.1 Aspects of Toxinology  
There are about 3,500 snake species in the world, of which 725 (20.7%) are venomous [30], spread 
across 4 families; Atractaspidiae, Elapidae, Viperidae and a few species of Colubridae [24,31]. In 
contrast to poisons, generally, toxins in venom cannot cross the intestinal barrier and thus must be 
injected to exert their toxic effect. Snake venoms are generally not poisonous, but consists of toxins, 
being neurotoxic, myotoxic, hemotoxic and/or cytotoxic. In the family of elapid snakes, and relevant 
for this thesis, there are 58 genera, whose venoms most frequently contain neurotoxins [24,32].  
 
In 2015, it was estimated that only about 8-10% of total estimated 19,000-25,000 unique snake 
toxins have been sequenced [30]. The known 8-10% are divided into 26 protein families. The largest 
of these snake toxin families so far, is the 3FTx family with their characteristic three-finger motif; 
beta strands connected by three loops (see examples of structures in Figure 3) [33,34]. 3FTxs 
predominantly target the cholinergic system, and can further be divided into functional classes 
[30,35]. Within the functional classes is “Type I a-neurotoxins”, which comprise the most relevant 
toxins for this thesis. Toxins from this class are antagonists of a1 nicotinic acetylcholine receptors 
(nAChR), effectively causing paralysis upon human envenoming [34,36]. T1ans are relatively shorter 
than other 3FTx a-neurotoxins, and thus sometimes also called short a-neurotoxins [34]. 
 
2.1.1 Mipartoxin-1 and Micrurus 3FTxs 
 
In this thesis the first, relevant immunogen is mipartoxin-1 (UniProt ID B3EWF8, see Figure 
4A); a T1an with surprisingly low homology to other known T1ans; closest identity being 70% to 
frontoxin from M. frontalis. MTx belongs to the so-called “Clade B” [37] of 8 monophyletic clades 
within 3FTxs of Micrurus species, having developed in the course of evolution. The 8 clades were 
suggested by Dashevsky and Fry in 2018, based on homology of known Micrurus 3FTxs [37]. It is 
the thought that an MTx-antitoxin might cross-react to other clade B toxins and potentially other 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
 
Page 14 of 72 
clades with high overall homology, or homology of paratope1-interacting amino acids. MTx was 
previously suggested as having potential for generation of a cross-reactive antitoxin [38]. By using 
the WebLogo software (see Tools & Software section), it is possible to generate a consensus sequence 
for each Micrurus 3FTx clade, based on the same sequences as Dashevsky and Fry used in 2018. 
Analysing MTx’s homology to each clade’s consensus sequence, a homology analysis (seen in Figure 
3 below) reveals that MTx has an overall homology to the other clade B members of 65%, but 75% 
to G and H. However, focusing on only the putative paratope-interacting amino acids (not just 
epitope), which may be the only medically relevant amino acids, MTx has the highest (37.5%) 
homology to the paratope-interacting amino acids of clade B, followed by clades F and C. Thus, MTx 
may be more likely to cross-react with clade F and/or C Micrurus 3FTx clades, due to highest 
homology of the paratope-interacting amino acids. In Figure 3 below, both the homology analysis of 
paratope-interacting amino acids (grey bars) and whole-proteins (blue bars) are shown.

2.2.1 The Humoral Immune Response 
The recombinant, Ub19His10-tagged toxins were used as immunogens for animal immunisation 
procedures and so was sr3- and rMTx (no tags). In this work’s protocol, rabbits were initially injected 
subcutaneously (s.c.) with Freund’s Complete adjuvant (FCA) and immunogen. FCA contains heat-
inactivated Mycobacterium tuberculosis, which has LPS and other antigens that stimulate immune 
cells vigorously via pathogen-associated molecular pattern (PAMP) recognition by Toll-like 
receptors on immune cells’ surface [45]. This induces production of cytokines that recruit 
granulocytes and phagocytes, such as macrophages, and other components of the innate immune 
system. These cells phagocytose pathogens and either exocytose antigens into extracellular matrix or 
display them (unprocessed4) on their surface-exposed major histocompatibility complex II proteins 
(MHC II) [46]. These antigens will accumulate in secondary lymphoid organs, where antigen-
presenting cells, such as dendritic cells (DCs), can capture them and display them to B-cells. DCs can 
also endocytose them from the initial site and transport the antigen to a secondary lymphoid organ.  
 
After B-cells bind their antigen and process them, they will display T-helper cell epitopes and 
migrate toward the T-cell zone (see Figure 6). The interaction between B-cells and T-helper cells 
ensures B-cell proliferation, whereof activated B-cells become antibody-producing plasma cells, and, 
together with T-helper cells, migrate into the follicle to form a germinal centre, in which affinity 
maturation occurs to increase affinity of the antibody for the antigen [47–49]. For an overview, see 
Figure 6, below.

2.2.2 Antibody Structure and Function 
The second immunological aspect relevant for this thesis is the structure of antibodies and the 
mechanisms which generated the scFvs in the phage library. In our bodies, B-cells are responsible for 
the production of immunoglobulins (Igs), also called antibodies (see example of immunoglobulin G, 
IgG, in Figure 7).  
==PAGE:20==
 
 
 
 
Page 19 of 72 
 
Figure 7: Illustration of the structure of an IgG antibody. The gene for the constant heavy region, CH, contains 
3 exons that is translated to the Fc region (dark grey). Variable regions are located on both heavy- and light 
chains (light grey and orange), being identical on each side. The variable regions each contain 3 CDRs, 
marked as red lines on the Figure. The biggest variance in amino acid composition stems from CDR3 on VHH,

from Fuglsang-Madsen et al. 2018 [27]. 
 
An IgG is the effective antibody type which circulate the blood stream and neutralise foreign, toxic 
molecules or pathogens. It consists of two identical heavy- and light chains, interconnected by 
disulphide bridges (see Figure 7). In the variable regions, there are specifically 3 hypervariable amino 
acid regions called Complementarity-Determining Regions, or CDRs. CDRs are not encoded by 
unique genes, but are recombined by a process called V, D, J recombination, which will not be further 
elaborated in this thesis. CDRs comprise the paratopes, and are thus responsible for the 
antibody:antigen interaction which occur by paratopes and epitopes. An antigen may contain more 
than one B-cell epitope, but not all paratope:epitope interactions will lead to neutralisation of a 
potential toxic effects of the antigen [48].  
 
In this study, the IONTAS phage library was utilized, wherein variable regions of light- and 
heavy chains were cloned from peripheral blood donations of 42 individuals, and one donation from 
tonsils [50]. In their approach, total B-cell mRNA was used to clone individual heavy-/light chain 
variable regions into phage plasmids, or phagemids. Subsequently, the pools of heavy chain variable 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
 
Page 20 of 72 
regions were cloned into one of two pools of two different types5 of light chain variable region-
containing phagemids [50]. When phage particles are assembled by a phage-transfected (F+) E. coli 
cell, the IgG variable regions will be displayed on the phage surface in its single variable fraction 
form, scFv (see structure in Figure 8, more details in section 2.4). Due to the cloning strategy, the 
expressed scFvs may be self-targeting, as specific recombinations of heavy- and light chain variable 
regions were not preserved. Furthermore, the library may be biased to contain more variable regions 
targeting specific antigens, depending on the antigen exposure of the donators and time span since 
last infection. Nevertheless, diversity was confirmed to be more than 1·1010 unique scFvs [50], but 
clonal copy numbers may introduce a bias during initial panning of phage display.

2.3 Aspects of Recombinant Expression  
In this thesis, E. coli was used as a host cell, which confer the advantages of rapid growth, cheap 
growth media, and usually high protein yields. A challenge is that cysteine-rich peptides (such as 
3FTxs) are natively produced in the oxidative environment of a eukaryote’s ER, rather than the 
reductive environment in the E. coli prokaryotic cytoplasm [53]. To tackle this challenge, one may 
either use a signalling peptide to target the folding event to the oxidative periplasm or use commercial 
E. coli cell lines with oxidative cytoplasm [53]. The oxidative cytoplasm does however not guarantee

correct folding, as no catalyst for formation of the correct disulphide-bridges are present. Correct 
folding is necessary to ensure native-like structures of recombinant proteins, which is critical for

2.4 Aspects of Phage Display 
Phage display was used for in vitro scFv discovery, in which the IONTAS Ltd. phage library was 
applied, developed by Schofield et al. in 2007 [50]. In this technology, M13 phages were genetically 
engineered to carry a human scFv-encoding gene, expressed specifically fused to the surface 
glycoprotein G3P, in extension of one the five G3Ps (see Figure 8) [51].  
 
Phage display has the purpose of discovering scFv that bind a certain POI. The principles are 
outlined in the following. The POI is immobilized directly or indirectly6 onto a plastic surface, 
following which the phage library is applied and incubated. Binding events between the (mostly) 
singly-expressed scFvs on each phage particle and the POI will lead to co-immobilization of the 
relevant scFv-carrying phages, called “binders”, after which the remaining non-binders are washed 
away. Binders are then eluted by trypsin-mediated digestion of a linker between the G3P and scFv 
(see Figure 8), after which the corresponding phages can be collected and used to infect F+ TG1 E. 
coli cells. Here, non-scFv carrying phages, so-called “bald” phages, are also stripped of G3Ps by 
trypsin, yielding them noninfective, and washed away. The TG1 cells are ensured to contain an F-pili 
by culturing in specific media, temperature-depended plasmids, incubation conditions (temperature 
& shaking), and cell optical density at 600nm (OD600) between 0.1-0.5. Expression of the F-pili is 
supressed by quorum sensing as density increases, and phagemid-expression (phage assembly) is 
inhibited by glucose, whilst M13-infected cells are selected by kanamycin-resistance.

The transfection process itself is thought to occur by physical interaction between the G3P 
protein (or G3P fusion protein) and the F-pili. The F-pili continuously build and auto-degraded by E. 
coli cells until the M13 phage is in close enough proximity to the TolA protein to initiate translocation 
into the cell [51,58,59]. The M13 phage only carries the scFv-encoding gene, but none of the genes 
required for replication. After transfection, the phage-infected bacterial cultures are grown in 
expression-suppressing media to an optimal density for re-infection with helper phages, M13KO7, 
which carry all the genes necessary for phage particle assembly, replication and budding [51]. 
Positively M13 and M13KO7 TG1 cells are selected by kanamycin- and ampicillin resistance. After 
M13KO7-helper phage infection, maximum cell density is achieved by overnight (ON) incubation in 
phagemid-supressing conditions, and then the TG1 cells are subjected to glucose-free media for onset 
of phage particle assembly, which is neither a lytic or lysogenic production, but continuous [51]. As 
a last step, phages are isolated from the bacterial culture (see phage display overview in Figure 9). 
Resulting phage concentrate can either be reused for another round of panning (Figure 9, arrow in 
parenthesis from point 4 to 1) or can be sub-cloned to generate BL21(DE3) E. coli cultures expressing 
monoclonal scFvs with a FLAG-tag.

The single scFv fused to a G3P on the M13 bacteriophage is ensured by an experimentally-
determined promoter, yielding low expression of the fusion protein, corresponding to only a small 
fraction of phages containing a fusion protein7, and in most cases, just single copy (as seen in Figure 
8) [51,60,61].  
The phage display process has two biases. Once TG1 cells have from a previous round has 
been inoculated to 0.1 OD600, they are grown to 0.5 for re-infection by MK13KO7-helper phages, 
which are then grown for one hour. This introduces a growth disadvantage for scFv genes comprised 
of more rare codons. However, a later bias is even more important; when the phages are assembled, 
the clonal variation may also create a bias in translation, cellular transport, protein stability, and scFv 
folding energy post-translation, effectively causing relative higher abundance of certain phages, 
harbouring more favourable scFv clones [24,51,60,61]. This is particularly critical because M13 
phages are constitutively assembled, rather than a lysogenic or lytic life cycle with their E. coli hosts, 
and thus bias the relative abundance of certain binders.  
In order to isolate the strongest binders subsequent panning rounds are performed, in which 
high-affinity binders are amplified by outcompeting weaker binders [51,61]. Higher affinity scFvs 
may, to a certain limit, be responsible for higher neutralisation capacity in vivo. After 2-3 rounds of 
phage display, high affinity-scFv-carrying phages can be isolated, their phagemids can be extracted 
and cloned into a BL21(DE3) commercial, high-protein-yield E. coli cell line. These monoclone-
expressing BL21(DE3) cells can later be used for recombinant (monoclonal) scFv expression [51], to 
test relative affinities by ELISA. However, because of the folding-energy bias, relative abundance of 
some monoclonal scFvs may again bias to what may seem like higher relative affinity.

4. Module A: Production of recombinant toxins 
4.1 Short Introduction 
By using the LE577 E. coli, it should be possible to recombinantly express correctly folded, cysteine-
rich toxins. Also, recombinant production may in the long run enable higher yields of specific low-
abundance toxin than what can be achieved through HPLC fractionation of whole-venom, due to the 
need of snakes, simultaneously circumventing dangerous and extensive animal maintenance and 
handling. Furthermore, higher purity may be achieved by chromatography methods of bacterial 
lysates, whereas irrelevant toxins in a venom can be difficult to separate by RP-HPLC [29]. Thus, it 
is a particularly advantageous method for low-immunogenic peptides like MTx, or non-naturally 
occurring peptides such as the cNTx.

4.2.1 pLE577 E. coli transformation 
Per transformation, 50 µl of LE577 E. coli cells from -80°C storage was kept on ice. One µl 
pET39b_Ub19His10_MTx or pET39b_Ub19His10_cNTx was added, respectively. Cells were 
incubated on ice for 15 minutes, heat shocked (42 °C) for 45 secs, put back on ice and incubated for 
5 minutes. Then, 500 µl TSB media (1 L TSB: 17 g tryptone, 3 g soy, 5 g NaCl, 2.5 g K2HPO4 and 
2.5 g D-glucose) was added to each vial. Both samples were incubated on a shaker for 45 min at 37 
°C. Cells were spun-down at 2,000x g for 5 min, and then resuspended in 200 µl TSB media. 
Transformed cells were grown on LB petri plates with 50 µg/ml chloramphenicol and 33 µg/ml 
kanamycin for retainment and selection of plasmid-harbouring cells. Resulting transformation plates 
were stored at 4 °C with parafilm seal.  
 
4.2.2 Recombinant Expression 
Pre-cultures were prepared, in which a single colony from the transformation plates was inoculated 
into 200 mL LB (with 50 µg/ml kanamycin and 33 µg/ml chloramphenicol) in a 1 L Erlenmeyer flask. 
The flask was incubated at 37 °C ON (12-16 hrs) with shaking (250 rpm).  
 
 
For t-cNTx expression, 5x 5 L Erlenmeyer flasks was added 1 L LB media (1 L LB: 10 g 
tryptone, 5 g yeast extract and 10 g NaCl). Conversely, for t-MTx-expressing cells, the 5 Erlenmeyer 
flasks were filled with fresh autoinduction media: 24 g yeast extract, 10 g tryptone (autoclaved) + 1

mL 50x5052 (glycerol 0.5%, glucose 0.05%, a-lactose 0.2%), 20 mL 50xM (25 mM Na2HPO4, 25 
mM KH2PO4, 50 mM NH4Cl, 5 mM Na2SO4,) 1 mL 33 mg/ml kanamycin, 1 mL 50 mg/ml 
chloramphenicol.

by multiplication with the dilution factor. Pre-cultures were then added to each Erlenmeyer flask to 
an OD600 of 0.1. The 5x 5 L Erlenmeyer flasks (of each protein-expressing culture) was then incubated 
at 37 °C on a shaking table. Cultures were grown to an OD600 of 0.8, after which a 250 µl sample was 
taken out for subsequent analysis, called T0. Then, for t-cNTx, each flask was added IPTG to a final

4.2.3 Cell Lysis 
Plastic centrifuge bottles (500 ml) were weighted, and all cell cultures were distributed in them. Cells 
were centrifuged for 15 min at 12,200x g, 4 °C. Supernatant was discarded. Bacterial cell pellets were 
weighted, and re-suspended fresh lysis buffer; 10 ml/10 g pellet (10 mM imidazole, 40 mM NaH2PO4, 
250 mM NaCl, pH 8, adjusted w/ NaOH), on ice. A one-time-use Pasteur-pipette was used for 
resuspension.  
Cells were kept on ice and sonicated for 1 cycle, 50% amplitude for 10 x 14 sec with 14 secs 
pause between pulses. Then, samples were centrifuged for 50 min at 15,000x g, 4 °C. For t-MTx, 
pellets were resuspended in fresh lysis buffer an additional time, and sonicated using the same 
settings, again. After centrifugation, supernatant was saved and assumed to contain the soluble protein 
fractions, whilst pellets (presumed cell debris and inclusion bodies) were re-suspended in the same 
volume of lysis buffer, but with 8 M urea, and stored at 4 °C. This was termed Tpellet and signifies the 
insoluble proteins. 
0.5 µl benzonase was added per L of ON culture (i.e. for the 5 L total initial cell culture, 2.5 µl was 
used), and incubated with magnetic stirring at 4 °C for 30 min. Subsequently, the lysate was pressed 
through a 2.5 µm filter.  
 
4.2.4 Ub19His10-tagged Consensus Type I a-neurotoxin Purification by Ni-NTA 
Superflow Beads, Gravity Flow 
The filtered t-cNTx lysate was added to 8 mL Ni-NTA beads (Qiagen ID #30210). One column 
volume (1 CV; 20 ml) lysate was added per run, to a gravity flow column (Bio-Rad Econo-Column® 
Chromatography Columns, 1.5 x 10 cm). Flow-through was collected, and the total volume of lysate 
was added in the same fashion. After binding the peptides, the column was washed with 3 CVs in 
wash buffer (20 mM imidazole, 40 mM NaH2PO4, 250 mM NaCl, pH 8). Peptides were then eluted 
from the Ni-NTA beads by elution buffer (400 mM imidazole, 40 mM NaH2PO4, 250 mM NaCl) for

initiated. After trial runs, a final program was set as 0-30% solution B over 0.3 CVs, followed by 30-

trifluoroacetic acid (TFA), whilst solution A was 5% EtOH and 1.1% TFA. Subsequently, fractions 
corresponding to peaks were zip-tip’d before it was sent to MS. 
Zip-tip is a buffer exchange/desalting protocol (basically a micro-RP-HPLC), where 5 µl C18 
material (dissolved in 60% Acetonitrile (ACN) and 0.2% TFA) is sucked into a pipette long-tip, after 
which the tip of the pipette tip is bent to ensure the material cannot escape. A syringe is then used to 
push out liquid with air, trapping the C18 material inside. The micro-column was then washed with 
20 µl dH2O and 0.1% TFA, then, 10 µl sample was injected. The micro-column was washed twice 
with 10 µl 0.1% TFA in dH2O, then eluted with 60% ACN and 0.2% TFA followed by 10 µl 0.2µm-
filtered dH2O, resulting in 20 µl sample in 30% ACN 0.1% TFA to be sent for external MS analysis.

4.4 Discussion 
In this work, toxins with 4 disulphide bonds each, were expressed in soluble form and obtained 
through sonication-mediated cell lysis, as seen in Figures 12 & 13. Solubility has historically been 
difficult to achieve with cysteine-rich peptides [53,55,63], for which reason the LE577 E. coli strain, 
used in this study, was engineered.  
 
Because autoinduction media was acquired later in the process, t-MTx, but not t-cNTx, was 
expressed by autoinduction. As a result, t-cNTx had an approximately 10-fold lower, final peptide 
yield, relative to t-MTx. For t-cNTx, the OD600 was 3.8, whereas for t-MTx it was 17. Taking into 
account the differences in protein yield and OD, t-MTx (autoinduced) had a yield of 2.55 mg/OD600 
while it was 1.18 mg/OD600 for LB-IPTG-induced t-cNTx expression. These results may suggest that 
autoinduction is preferable for recombinant expression with the LE577 strain; however, it may be not 
directly comparable seeing as the t-MTx-producing culture was lysed twice, in contrast to t-cNTx, 
and it was not tested whether the HPLC-mounted Ni-NTA column was overloaded with t-MTx. Thus, 
both fusion proteins may have had a higher actual, soluble, yield than what was reported here. In 
comparison, de la Rosa et al. reported 1.5 mg/L for ScNTx, which was higher than cNTx, but 5.7-
fold lower than t-MTx. This indicates LE577 with autoinduction has a yield advantage relative to 
==PAGE:41==
 
 
 
 
Page 40 of 72 
Origami cells (which was used by de la Rosa et al.), under the described conditions, but may vary 
with toxin sequences.  
Both t-MTx and t-cNTx had been tested for optimal growth conditions with regards to 
temperature and rhamnose concentration, by small-scale (50 ml) cultures prior to recombinant 
expression (data not shown). It was found that 0 mM rhamnose led to the highest quantity of soluble 
protein (but not total protein). The presence of insoluble protein (as seen in e.g. Figure 13) at 0 mM 
rhamnose could have several explanations. E.g., it could indicate a toxic level of protein expression, 
but as the total protein yield increased whilst soluble protein levels decreased with more rhamnose, 
this is unlikely. A more probable explanation is that higher expression of chaperone molecules is 
necessary for folding of all the expressed, tagged toxins. Yet again, a third explanation could be that 
the conotoxin-specific disulphide isomerases have reduced efficiency at folding snake toxins, and 
snake-specific PDIs may be a necessary future avenue for generating more soluble, recombinant 
snake toxins. 
 
For t-cNTx the pre-induction fraction lane in SDS-PAGE had run awry (Figure 12, T0), 
possibly due to an air pocket in the gel. Later, another obstacle was encountered when the tag of t-
cNTx was unable to be cleaved by TEV. After 24 hrs of incubation, the positive control was 
completely cleaved, but t-cNTx had only minor band shifts, if any (data not shown). This may indicate 
that the t-cNTx protein has a conformation wherein the TEV-cleavage-site is physically inaccessible 
for the TEV enzyme.  
 
 
Besides high and soluble expression levels, it is also important to achieve the correct folding 
of POIs for phage display; but perhaps less so for immunisation. Previous studies of immunisation 
with snake toxins have both reported successful immunisation with- [64,65], and without [66–70] 
native-like folding of immunogens. This may be explained by the presence of linear [71] vs. 
discontinuous/structural epitopes [72]. If for instance only structural epitopes exist for neutralising a 
given toxin, it will not be of use to immunise with a toxoid. In contrast, for phage display it has been 
shown that native-like structures and mode of antigen display are critical to best mimic natural 
conditions [51,54]. Correct folding of t-cNTx and rMTx was initially meant to be indicated by 
confirming presence of expected disulphide bridges, by the use of Mass Spectrometry, wherein a two-
Da-shift would signify the reduction of a disulphide bridge. This would correspond to a gain of two 
hydrons to each thiol groups of cysteines. In Figure 18B of t-cNTx, a discrepancy between the 
theoretical weight (19163 Da) and the measured peaks (19161 Da and 19185 Da) is evident. This 
==PAGE:42==
 
 
 
 
Page 41 of 72 
may be explained by the presence of two subpopulations of fully oxidized (19163 Da) and fully 
reduced (19171 Da; 4 disulphide bridges reduced) peptide isotypes. However, the remaining 14 Da 
from 19171 to the measured 19185 may indicate the presence of a contaminating peptide, but the 
likelihood of a bacterial protein of that exact size and isolation by the same purification conditions is 
small. Instead, it could be explained by a point mutation, e.g. a V->I/L mutation, but this was not 
further investigated. The two Da discrepancy between theoretical and putative oxidized sample 
cannot be a matter of an additional cysteine bridge as there are no more cysteines to form bridges. 
However, there is a 0.02% instrumental error, which corresponds to ~4 Da, making it more likely to 
be responsible for the two Da discrepancy. 
 
Conversely, for rMTx, in Figure 19, the theoretical- and measured molecular weight fits 
almost perfectly, although the reduced rMTx (by 30 min incubation with 40 mM DTT) is four Da 
short of the expected, fully reduced peptide, indicating the retention of two disulphide bonds, and 
cannot be explained by instrumental error. This may indicate either partial re-oxidization during zip-
tip preparation or perhaps insufficient reducing agent concentration (or incubation time) prior to the 
zip-tip protocol. Importantly, despite all four expected disulphide bridges being formed in both rMTx 
and t-cNTx, it does not necessarily mean it is the correct connections of cysteines. This was not 
further analysed in vitro but was assumed to be possible to investigate by in vivo bioactivity testing 
of the peptides, reported in the next module.  
 
4.5 Module A: Short Summary 
Overall, recombinant expression of soluble, cysteine-rich snake toxins was successful via expression 
in the LE577 strain. rMTx: 0.5 mg was obtained, 0.5 mg of srMTx, 17.2 mg t-MTx and 4.5 mg t-
cNTx. These proteins were subsequently used for immunisation procedures, discussed in the next 
module.  
5. Module B1: Generation of Anti-Mipartoxin-1 Serum 
5.1 Short Introduction 
Mipartoxin-1 is a small, 7.1 kDa, highly toxic [38], lowly immunogenic 3FTx [26], type I a-
neurotoxin. The venoms of Micrurus snakes have been observed to be divided in two types; PLA2-
dominated or 3FTx-dominated [31]. Instituto Clodomiro Picado in Costa Rica have succeeded in 
maintaining M. nigrocinctus in captivity, thus enabling production of antivenom against M. 
nigrocintus, which shows para-specificity and ability to cross-neutralise the lethal effects of PLA2-
==PAGE:43==
 
 
 
 
Page 42 of 72 
dominated (but not 3FTx-dominated) Micrurus venoms in mice9. However, as 3FTx-dominated 
venomous Micrurus snakes cannot be kept in captivity (and only have small venom yield [43]), an 
antivenom against 3FTx-dominated venoms does not exist. In previous studies, mipartoxin-1 was 
suggested as a potential key toxin to neutralise 3FTx-dominated Micrurus venoms [31,38], which 
may enable the possibility of fortifying the M. nigrocinctus antivenom with Micrurus-3FTx-antitoxin. 
However, seeing as mipartoxin-1 has homology to clade B members (or F, and G if looking at 
paratope-interacting amino acids), the antitoxin will most likely be able to cross-recognise only a 
subset of the 3FTx, and other 3FTxs may be useful for other clades. By combining these anti-clade 
toxins, the hope is to generate a Micrurus pan-American antivenom [31].  
 
In this study, two rabbits were immunised with rMTx and two with t-MTx, respectively. 
Antiserum was generated and was evaluated in its ability to recognize rMTx, srMTx and M. 
mipartitus whole-venom. Furthermore, IgGs were purified and tested for neutralization capacity 
against srMTx.

5.2.1 Bioactivity testing 
Bioactivity of rMTX, t-MTx and srMTx was tested by intraperitoneal (i.p.) injection of 6 putative 
LD50s in naïve 17g mice at the Instituto Clodomiro Picado. The LD50 of native MTx was previously 
shown to be 1.02 µg per mouse [38], and so, 6.12 µg was used for the rMTx, srMTx, and 16.72 µg 
for t-MTx, to take the molecular mass of the tag into account. 
 
5.2.2 Rabbits & Immunisation Procedure 
3-month-old rabbits were immunised with a total of 180 µg rMTx or 580 µg t-MTx with an initial, 
subcutaneous (s.c.) injection and 5 subsequent intramuscular (i.m) booster injections every second 
week (see full immunisation schedules in Appendix B). An additional booster injection for one of the 
rMTx-immunised rabbits was 40 µg of srMTx, rather than rMTx (rabbit called “sr-rMTx”-
immunised). In the first injection and first booster injection, FCA was used, and subsequently 
aluminium hydroxide (Al(OH)3) was used as adjuvant. After completion of immunisation, the rabbits 
were bled from the ear vein and sera, from which IgGs were purified, were collected for murine 
lethality-neutralisation assays.  
 
5.2.3 ELISA Serum Response Monitoring 
After booster injection two of six, the immune response of the rabbits was monitored by testing serum 
recognition of toxins. For t-MTx-immunised rabbits, half a 96-well MaxiSorp was coated ON at room 
temperature with 100 µl of 1 µg/well antigen of t-MTx (24 wells) or 0.2 µg/well srMTx (24 wells).  
 
The day after coating, the antigen solutions were discarded, and wells were washed 5 times 
with PBS. They were then blocked with 2% BSA in PBS, by incubation for 1 hr at RT. The 2% BSA-
PBS was then removed by decanting. Rabbit serum from the two immunised rabbit and a naïve rabbit 
was diluted in 1% BSA-PBS in 500, 1,500, 4,500 and 13,500-fold. Each rabbit serum was applied in 
duplicate against t-MTx, srMTx and BSA or rMTx and incubated for 2 hrs at room temperature. Then, 
serum was decanted, and plates were washed 5 times with PBS. Anti-rabbit IgG with conjugation to 
==PAGE:45==
 
 
 
 
Page 44 of 72 
human alkaline phosphatase (AlkP) in a 1/2,000 dilution (in 1% BSA-PBS) was then applied to each 
well, and plates were incubated in darkness at room temperature for 1 hr.  
 
Then, the anti-rabbit IgG:AlkP solution were decanted and plates washed 4 times in PBS and 
once in AlkP substrate buffer. Afterwards, pNPP (AlkP substrate) was added in 100 µl of 1 mg/ml 
concentration to each well, and simultaneously, a timer was started. At each 10th minute, absorbance 
at 405nm measurements (Abs405) were made on an ELISA plate-reader, with 5 seconds of plate-
shaking prior to each measurement. Measurements were stopped when the response from the naïve 
rabbit’s serum reached 0.2 Abs405, or after 60 minutes.  
 
5.2.4 Purification of IgGs from Rabbit Antiserum 
IgGs from each rabbit’s antiserum was purified by affinity chromatography on a Protein A column. 
A column volume of about 1.5 mL was utilized. Six CVs of serum was run through the column, 
followed by washing with PBS until the baseline signal was restored and stable. Then, a second 
injection of the flow-through was performed followed by washing. Once the signal was stable, elution 
was made by addition of 4 mL 0.1 M Glycine at pH 2.8. To preserve IgG activity, elution fractions 
were captured in 0.5 mL 0.5 M Tris-HCl at pH 7, per 4 mL collected elution. Elution fractions from 
each run were then pooled and dialyzed in 4 L miliQ water at 4 °C with magnetic stirring. The 4 litres 
were changed twice in 3-hr intervals, and then left with stirring at 4 °C for 72 hrs. After this, the pools 
were lyophilized before usage in lethality-neutralisation assays.  
 
5.2.5 Mouse lethality-neutralisation assay 
For the initial test with crude serum, three mice received 6.12 µg srMTx incubated 30 minutes at 37

5.4 Discussion 
The studies in this module are interesting, because they shed light on different variants of recombinant 
toxins as immunogens. These were t-MTx, rMTx and srMTx. It was established by MS that rMTx 
and t-MTx contain all four expected disulphide bridges, whereas srMTx only has two. Interestingly, 
it turned out that only srMTx had bioactivity, demonstrating the importance of correct disulphide 
bridges, and the necessity of testing it in future recombinant production studies. Another explanation 
of the lack of bioactivity of t-MTx might also be that the larger tag sterically hinders the toxic moiety 
of access to the nAChR. This was however not the case for t-cNTx, as will be shown later, but the 
differences might also exactly be the reason why it was impossible to cleave the tag off of t-cNTx.  
 
The purpose of t-MTx immunisation was to test whether the increased molecule size would 
increase immunogenicity of the toxin moiety. Comparing Figures 21 & 22, it shows that significant 
recognition of rMTx can be achieved by immunisation with a tagged, recombinant toxin, and after 
98 days, srMTx and M. mipartitus was also recognized more strongly. However, in the future, 
==PAGE:50==
 
 
 
 
Page 49 of 72 
immunisation dosage should be increased, or potentially other adjuvants, e.g. known Th-2 promoting 
adjuvants, should be attempted. LPS-mediated immune engagement might also be a hindrance, seeing 
as a Th-1 response has, in some cases, been documented, and is detrimental to production of 
efficacious antiserum [74]. On the other hand, immunisation with only the recombinant toxoid, rMTx, 
showed a strong response already by day 37 (Figure 23), which did not increase dramatically for day 
98, judging from difference from the naïve rabbit’s serum response to srMTx. This may indicate a 
tolerance response, and may also indicate that other, perhaps immunogenicity-enhancing, adjuvants 
may be beneficial to use. When comparing rMTx to srMTx-immunisation (Figure 24 vs. 25), it 
becomes evident that a single booster injection from the bioactive immunogen enhances the humoral 
response. In fact, the single booster injection seems to have increased response against all 
immunogens with about 0.5 AU, corroborated by the fact that data from both Figure 24 & 25 originate 
from the same physical ELISA plate. Thus, a potential solution in the future may be to alternate 
between immunisations with toxoid and bioactive toxin (starting with toxoid to be able to use higher 
dosages). A factor holding back more srMTx booster injections in this work was however the srMTx 
antigen stock, and a potential solution to achieve potentially a 4-disulphide-bridge correctly folded, 
recombinant toxin, may be to engineer the LE577 strain with a PDI from a snake with known T1an 
production.  
A curious aspect is that regardless of whether t-MTx or rMTx was used for immunisation 
(Figures 21 & 23), t-MTx was recognised more strongly than srMTx. This may be an experimental 
factor, as t-MTx was applied in 1 µg/well in the ELISA plate, whereas srMTx was applied at 0.2 
µg/well, which was done to compensate that the Ub19His10-tag comprise 63.4% of t-MTx. 
Furthermore, 0.2 µg/well was thought to saturate the well; but perhaps that was false, or the 
Ub19His10-tag allows retainment of the native-like structure of the toxin, whilst direct binding of 
srMTx to the plastic surface distorts the toxin conformation, lowering affinity of the serum antibodies. 
A third option would be if the tag allows for a denser antigen-packing on the well surface, by acting 
as “linkers” from the bottom of the well.  
When studying the neutralisation capacity of serum-derived IgGs, a higher amount of toxin 
was used to challenge the mice. This was done because the naïve rabbit serum was able to save mice 
from six putative LD50s, hinting that perhaps the toxicity of srMTx may be lowered in comparison to 
native MTx. However, at least 12 µg srMTx could be seen as 2 LD99s, as both mice died at 6.12 µg 
during bioactivity testing, but perhaps IgG titres were not sufficient for neutralisation of 2 LD99s. The 
failure of isolated IgGs from antiserum that was confirmed to bind the recombinant toxin by ELISA, 
==PAGE:51==
 
 
 
 
Page 50 of 72 
has been documented once before, by Ramos et al. in 2016 [74], where a suggested solution was 
utilization of a different adjuvant, as well.  
Despite low responses, this study does confirm that recombinant mipartoxin-1 is a viable 
solution to obtain recognizing antiserum by immunisation, in line with previous studies with 
recombinant snake toxins [26,29,43,62,64,75–77], enabling the possibility of procurement of 
antitoxin against hard-to-obtain toxins (due to unavailability of snake venom). In the future, it will be 
determined whether this antitoxin can cross-recognise other clade B Micrurus 3FTx members, and 
potentially other clades, such as F, G and/or H, and thus whether the antitoxin could be used to 
generate an efficacious Micrurus-3FTx-neutralising serum.  
 
5.5 Module B1: Short Summary 
srMTx, putatively only containing two of four disulphide bonds, was lethal; LD99 at 0.34 µg/g, but in 
contrast, the 4/4-disulphide bond-containing rMTx was not bioactive. Immunisation with rMTx 
seemed to lead to a tolerance response, possibly due to low immunogenicity of the immunogen. 
Antiserum recognizing srMTx, and more importantly, M. mipartitus snake venom was successfully 
obtained. This was achieved by both t-MTx (MW 19.4 kDa) and rMTx (7.1 kDa) immunisation, but 
the rMTx-immunised rabbit serum’s recognition of M. mipartitus snake venom was stronger than the 
t-MTx-immunised rabbits’. Neutralisation of srMTx in vivo was not achieved by IgGs, and results 
were ambiguous with antiserum. 
6. Module B2: Generation of Type I a-neurotoxin antiserum 
6.1 Short Introduction 
Type I a-neurotoxins induce flaccid paralysis by blocking acetylcholine entry to, and thus inhibit 
excitation of, motor neuron cells. This neurotoxicity is observed in many snake venoms, and is the 
dominant mode of action in many elapid snake venoms [24,31,38,41]. Utilizing recombinant 
immunogens, it is possible to circumvent the use of snake-derived toxins, but it also enables the 
opportunity to investigate methods of intentional cross-reactive immune responses, e.g. by consensus 
toxins. By minimising the number of immunogens needed to neutralise as many T1ans as possible, 
the same antitoxin may be used in several pools to reconstitute an antivenom [42]. For example, if 
cNTx in the future proves to invoke an antiserum capable of neutralising T1ans from mamba and 
cobra species, it can be used to fortify existing antivenoms to have a higher neutralisation capacity 
==PAGE:52==
 
 
 
 
Page 51 of 72 
per dosage. Conversely, other consensus toxins may in the future constitute immunisation mixtures 
to invoke broadly neutralising antivenoms, such as e.g. pan-mamba or pan-cobra antivenoms. 
The idea of generating a single consensus toxin, which would be able to invoke an effective 
antiserum to neutralise all the T1ans with homology to it, has been seen once before in 2018 by de la 
Rosa et al. [29]. In de la Rosa’s study, ScNtx was generated by 12 T1an sequences stemming from 
the Acanthophis, Oxyuranus, Walterinnesia, Naja, Dendroaspis and Micrurus genera of Elapids [29]. 
Conversely, in this work more (and other) T1an amino acid sequences were used to generate cNTx; 
concretely 18 sequences, stemming from 8 cobra species and 3 mamba species. Furthermore, in their 
study, ScNtx-antiserum was tested for toxin-recognition in ELISA in vitro studies and confirmed to 
cross-recognize other unincluded T1ans, which has also been shown for cNTx in Figure 27. Lastly, 
in this study, raised antisera was tested for neutralisation capacity in vivo.  
 
6.2 Methods 
Methods for immunisation, monitoring serum response, IgG purification, lethality tests and 
neutralisation assays were identical to the ones reported for t-MTx/rMTx, in chapter 2A, with the 
following exceptions: 
• For the bioactivity assay, six putative LD50s, corresponding to 22.3 µg, was injected 
intravenously (i.v.), in two 17g mice. Because cNTx was generated based on several T1ans, 
black mamba short neurotoxin-1 (uniprot ID P01416) was chosen as theoretical LD50 for 
cNTx, as P01416 was one of toxins used to generate the consensus toxin [78]. 
• 320 µg t-cNTx was used in total for immunisation procedures, corresponding to 115 µg cNTx. 
• For ELISA-mediated monitoring of serum response, different antigens were used for coating 
ELISA plates. These were t-cNTx, D. polylepis-, N. kaouthia- and N. melanoleuca whole-
venom, Dp4 (RP-HPLC fractionated D. polylepis fraction no. #4, known to contain T1an 
uniprot ID P01416 [78]) and Dp27. The latter was included as putative negative control, 
seeing as Dp27 was reported to contain solely metalloproteinases, which do not have high 
homology to 3FTxs [78]. Later, other T1an-containing venom fractions were included, 
namely Nm3 (P01424) and Nk5 (P59275 & P59276).  
• For crude serum neutralization assays, 30 µg t-cNTx was injected i.v. with the same mice 
setup as for sr-rMTx. For IgG-neutralization assays, mice were challenged with 30 µg of t-
cNTx, incubated in 13 mg/ml IgG or PBS, mimicking physiological IgG concentrations.

6.4 Discussion  
The LD50 of cNTx was assumed to be comparable to that of Dp4, because a T1an from Dp4 had been 
used to generate cNTx. However, the assumption was evidently misplaced, as Dp4 has a reported i.v. 
LD50 of 0.06 µg/g of body weight, and cNTx was found to have an i.v. LD50 of 0.42 µg/g body weight; 
a 7-fold increase in LD50. This might be partially explained by the fact that homology between cNTx 
and Dp4 only is 62.5%. Howeverm the homology to P01424 from N. melanoleuca is 90%, and cNTx 
still has a 3-fold lowered toxicity [79]. The lowered toxicity could thus be due to the Ub19His10-tag, 
if for example the accessibility of cNTx to the nAChR is lowered due to the increased molecular size. 
Another explanation could be the placement of the altered amino acids, in comparison to P01424. 
The differences are exactly placed close to the DHRG motif, shown to be recognized by neutralising 
antivenom [71], and placed in Loop II, which is known to be partake in binding the nAChR [80]. 
Thus, because the consensus sequence was not directed by natural selection, the alteration of amino 
acids seems to affect toxicity. This was also the case for ScNtx of de la Rosa’s study [29].  
 
Comparing ELISA responses of day 37 vs. 98, there is evidently a dramatic increase 
in response against the immunogen and various tested venoms and toxins. However, as there is also 
signal against Dp27, the putative metalloproteinase-containing fraction, it may indicate either that the 
results cannot be trusted, or that Dp27 somehow was contaminated with 3FTxs, either during-, or 
more likely, post fractionation. A third explanation may be presence of 3FTxs due to venom 
variability, which has been documented before [81]. However, the naïve serum, tested in the same 
plate was not affected, meaning perhaps either that the antibodies of the naïve serum does not react 
against anything, or that Dp27 was indeed contaminated. It is however known that the naïve serum is 
functional, as it was possible to neutralise t-cNTx in the crude serum neutralisation assay (whereas 
the control died). For the IgG-mediated lethality-neutralisation assays, a concentration of 13 mg/ml 
was tested because it reflects physiological conditions. However, it turned out to be ineffective, and 
higher concentrations were deemed irrelevant to test. IgGs were tested in immunodiffusion assays, 
where they did not precipitate t-cNTx, which could indicate IgGs were somehow rendered 
unfunctional during purification, but t-MTx precipitation was observed when applying t-MTx-
antiserum, and thus inactive IgGs are an unlikely explanation (data not shown). 
==PAGE:56==
 
 
 
 
Page 55 of 72 
As a rule of thumb, a serum ELISA response ≈2 AU (at 1500-fold dilution) after 40 minutes 
AlkP-pNPP incubation indicates a sufficient neutralisation capacity for 3 LD50s (personal 
communication, professor Bruno Lomonte). Looking at Figure 27, the response at 1/1500-dilution of 
the crude serum was 3.8, and so, it would be expected to have been able to neutralise t-cNTx but 
failed to. This may indicate the generated response of IgGs were largely specific to the Ub19His10-
tag, rather than the toxin moiety and that this did not neutralise the cNTx toxicity. The cNTx-specific 
IgGs response may be more similar to the tested venom fractions (Nm3, Nk5, Dp4, (Dp27)), having 
a response around 0.8 AU. The antiserum response demonstrates that immunising with a consensus 
toxin can induce a humoral response, even for T1ans at 62.5% homology.  
Previous studies have shown that only 5-36% of antibody from horse antisera are specific 
against the immunogens; and that is with high immunisation dosages than this study [17,82–84]. In 
this study, the antigen stock was a limitation, and so, perhaps only up to 2% of the IgGs actually are 
toxin-specific, explaining the failure to neutralise in vivo. Alike with mipartoxin-1, if immunisation 
dosage had been higher, and/or immunogenicity-enhancing adjuvants had been utilized, perhaps the 
T1an-specific IgG titre had been sufficient to neutralise the various T1an in vivo. However, whether 
consensus toxins in general are viable solutions for generating cross-neutralising antisera may be a 
case-by-case basis, and bioinformatic and statistical analyses (such as what was done in Figure 5) 
prior to work onset is crucial. Specifically, conducting experiments to determine the homology-limits 
of recognizability (such as what was done here), could be a generalizable strategy for the future. This 
may help predict which toxins can be neutralized, before tests in vivo.  
 
6.5 Module B2: Short Summary 
Antiserum recognizing t-cNTx, and more importantly, N. kaouthia fraction 5, D. polylepis and N. 
melanoleuca snake venom was successfully obtained by t-cNTx (MW 19.16 kDa) immunisation. 
Furthermore, T1ans of down to 62.5% homology was recognized significantly better by immunised 
rabbit serum, in comparison to naïve rabbit serum. However, the IgG titre was not sufficiently 
neutralising in vivo when mice were challenged with 1.3 LD50s incubated with 13 mg/ml IgGs.

7. Module C: Phage display 
7.1 Short Introduction  
Phage display is in short, an in vitro technique to discover POI-binding scFvs. The strengths of the 
technique are that it circumvents the animal immune system and generates monoclonal scFvs against 
any desired POI. This has a strong therapeutic advantage in comparison to conventional 
immunisation, in the sense that only POI-specific IgGs are generated, avoiding irrelevant antibodies. 
A feature in the phage library that was utilized in this study, is that the scFvs are human-derived [50], 
and as such, in formulated (recombinant) antivenoms, adverse reactions due to the heterologous 
nature of conventional pAbs are avoided. Looking at the biopharmaceutical potential of antibodies, 
they have shown remarkable promise, exemplified by global best-selling drug in 2018; Humira [85], 
adalimumab, an anti-TNF monoclonal antibody. Due to the popularity of mAbs as therapeutics, they 
are becoming increasingly cheaper to produce in Chinese Hamster Ovary (CHO) cells [86].  
Combining the strengths of phage display discovery, and the need for improving antivenoms, 
it is the scope for the Tropical Pharmacology Lab at DTU to generate recombinant antivenoms for 
human envenomation therapy by designed mixtures of monoclonal, human, antibodies. To achieve 
this goal, several criteria are relevant to consider, as described in Laustsen 2016 [24]. In particular, 
initial focus should for instance be put on prioritizing antitoxin development after 1) toxicity score 
(relative abundance in a given venom (in %), divided by toxic potency; LD50 (µg toxin/g animal) 
[18]), 2) frequency of human envenomings and 3) scarcity of current antivenom for the given snake 
species [18]. Based on these criteria, T1ans were prioritised for discovery of broadly-neutralising 
scFvs, by generating the consensus toxin, cNTx.  
Cross-reactivity can also be rationally achieved in conventional antiserum production by 
immunising with heterologous venoms (immunisation with various snake species’ venom). However, 
it is also possible for homologous venom to evoke production of cross-neutralizing antivenom due to 
homology between toxins in the different snake species’ venom, and so it is termed para-specific if it 
can cross-neutralize a snake venom “by chance”, which was not in the original immunisation mixture 
[87]. In this work a different rational approach is taken to intentionally discover cross-neutralising 
scFvs via phage display. By having a significantly higher average homology between the chosen 
cobra- and mamba T1an amino acid sequences, it is the hypothesis that scFvs may recognize all the 
T1ans utilized to generate cNTx and perhaps other T1ans with high homology (exemplified in this 
work by fraction 4 from black mamba). To produce the most cost-efficient recombinant antivenoms 
it is desired to have as few mAbs which neutralise as many toxins, as possible. This would bring

down the production cost, as fewer mAbs would be necessary. It would furthermore increase 
accessibility for impoverished people, whom predominantly are affected [12,88]. 
 
Conversely, mipartoxin-1 was chosen to be recombinantly expressed, as the Micrurus T1ans 
share surprisingly low identity to other T1ans (i.e. cross-neutralization has proved difficult to 
achieve), is difficult to obtain by conventional methods (capturing of wild snakes with low venom 
yield), and has no available, commercial antivenom to treat envenoming. Furthermore, it is the hope 
that anti-mipartoxin-1 can neutralise other clade B T1ans of the Micrurus 3FTxs, and potentially 
some of Clades G, F and/or H, taking a step closer to a pan-American 3FTx-Micrurus-antitoxin.  
For phage display purposes, solely t-MTx and t-cNTx was used, seeing as appropriate antigen 
display was sought attempted by capturing the Ub19His10-tag, using an aHis mAb coating. Peptide 
display could have also been attempted by direct coating (in which the tag may be used to display the 
antigen) or by biotinylated toxins displayed by streptavidin-capture. As the ubiquitin tag contains 7 
lysines (with amine groups to bind biotin), and as there are 7 lysines in the MTx or cNTx, there would 
be a good chance of monobiotinylation on the tag, achieving optimal display of the recombinant toxin. 
However, there is a risk of polybiotinylation, but it can be mitigated by utilizing a 1:1.5 ratio of 
toxin:biotin [51,54]. Optimal antigen display is to mimic the native conditions of free-flowing toxin 
as best possible [24,51,54], increasing the chance of discovering therapeutically relevant scFvs.

7.2.1 Phage display protocol 
MaxiSorpTM vials (Thermo Scientific #44202) were coated with aHis mouse mAbs (Thermo 
Scientifc #MA1-21315) by applying 250 µl 10 µg/ml in the bottom of each vial (dissolved in PBS 
(0.010 M Na2HPO4, 0.002 M KH2PO4, 0.137 M NaCl, 0.003 M KCl, pH 7.4), and sealed with 
parafilm at 4 °C ON. The next day, the coating liquid was pipetted out and vials washed 3 times with 
PBS, using a squirt bottle. Antigens were dissolved in fresh 3% MPBS (PBS + 3% w/v skimmed milk 
powder) to a concentration of 10 µg/ml, and 250 µl antigen was applied to the bottom of MaxiSorp 
vials (2.5 µg antigen per vial). t-MTx and t-cNTx were each applied to an individual aHis-coated 
vial and incubated for 1 hr. 
 
50 µl of IONTAS k-scFv phage library [50] (1013 phage particles per ml), corresponding to a 
50x library coverage, was incubated in 150 µl 3% MPBS (blocking) for an hour. After this, free 
Ub19His10-tag and mAb aHis was added to a concentration of 10 µg/ml to library solutions and 
incubated 1 hr (additional blocking). The IONTAS phage library solutions’ unspecific, aHis mAb 
and Ub19His10 binders were thus blocked. 
 
After incubation with antigens, the solution was pipetted out of the Maxisorp vials and vials 
were washed once with 0.2% PBS-T (0.2% v/v TWEEN®, P1379, Sigma-Aldrich) and three times 
with PBS. After this, 250 µl of the phage solutions were added to each vial and incubated for 1 hr at 
room temperature. Subsequently, vials were washed 6 times with PBS-T and 6 times with PBS. Then, 
TG1 E. coli were ensured to be at OD600 0.3, grown from OD600 0.1 at 37°C and 280 rpm in max a 
10th volume of an Erlenmeyer flask. If cells indeed had an OD of 0.3, 250 µl of 100 µg/ml trypsin 
(dissolved in 50 mM Tris pH 8.0, 1 mM CaCl2) was applied to the antigen-containing vials for phage 
elution, and vials were incubated for 15 minutes. After 10 minutes, TG1 cells were confirmed to have 
an OD600 between 0.4-0.5 and then, 5 mL of the TG1 cells were aliquoted to 50 mL falcon tubes (one 
TG1-containing falcon tube per selection vial). The phage-containing trypsin solution was then added 
to respective falcon tubes and incubated at 37 °C with 150 rpm for 1 hr.  
After the 1-hr infection, in the first round, 20 µl of each infected culture was vortexed and 
plated onto a pre-warmed, sterile 2TYGA plate (16 g/l tryptone, 10 g/l yeast extract, 5 g/l NaCl, 10% 
==PAGE:60==
 
 
 
 
Page 59 of 72 
v/v glucose and 100 µg/ml ampicillin + 10% bacto-agar), using one-time-use plastic spreaders. This 
plate is termed ‘dilution plate’. Then, remaining culture was centrifuged at 4,400x g for 15 minutes 
and supernatant was decanted. The residual ~100 µl liquid was used to resuspend the pellet, and the 
phage-infected TG1 cells were plated on 2TYGA plates, termed “output plates”. At least 500 µl of 
uninfected TG1 culture was saved and 100 µl were plated onto 2TYGA and 2TYGK plates (50 µg/ml 
kanamycin instead of ampicillin) as control. 
The following day, infected cultures were counted on the dilution plates and corresponding 
output plates were harvested. Harvest was carried out by applying 1 mL freezing medium (2TY with 
100 µg/ml ampicillin, 2% v/v glucose and 16% v/v glycerol) to the plates and then using a one-time 
plastic spreader to dissolve the bacterial lawn. This was done twice, a total volume of 2 ml, which 
was collected and mixed by end-over-end rotation for 15 minutes. OD600 was measured and an aliquot 
was taken out to make 5 mL of OD600 0.1 infected cells in 5 mL 2TY media, for incubation at 37 °C, 
280 rpm. Remaining culture was saved as a glycerol stock at -80 °C. When the inoculants reached 
OD600 0.5, 75 µl M13KO7 helper phages were added (1.77·1012 phages/mL)  [51], and cells were 
infected a second time by 37 °C and 150 rpm incubation for 1 hr. After this, cells were centrifuged at 
1,750x g for 10 mins, then, supernatant was decantated, followed by additional 2 mins of 
centrifugation, after which remaining supernatant was removed by pipetting. Cells were resuspended 
in 10 mL 2TYAK and aliquoted to 2x 5 mL in 50 mL falcon tubes and incubated at 25 °C at 280 rpm 
ON. Kanamycin selects for helper-phage infected cells, and absence of glucose initiates expression 
and assembly of phage proteins and phagemid. An ON culture of TG1 cells were prepared for the 
next day, and new MaxiSorp vials were coated ON as previously described. 
 
 
On the third day, phages were recovered by pooling the pair of falcon tubes with infected TG1 
cells (supernatant now containing assembled phage particles), and the pooled cells were centrifuged 
at 10,500x g for 10 mins at 4 °C. The 10 mL phage-containing supernatant was then mixed with 2.5 
mL ice cold PEG-8000 & 2.5 M NaCl and incubated on ice for 1 hr. After this, the solution was 
centrifuged at 12,300x g for 20 mins at 4 °C. The supernatant was decanted, and the phage pellet was 
dissolved in 0.5 mL PBS and transferred to an Eppendorf tube. To get rid of residual bacterial cells, 
the sample was then pelleted by centrifugation at 9,300x g for 2 minutes, and the final, 20x phage-
concentrate was moved to a new Eppendorf tube. This would constitute the “library” for the next 
round.  
==PAGE:61==
 
 
 
 
Page 60 of 72 
 
Subsequent rounds proceeded as described for the first round with a few changes. Firstly, 130 
µl of the 20x phage concentrate was blocked with 130 µl 6% MPBS, instead of using new 50 µl of 
the IONTAS phage library. Secondly, instead of a 20 µl aliquot for dilution plates, a 10-4 dilution was 
made for the second round, and a 10-6 for the third and final round.

7.2.4 scFv subcloning protocol 
Libraries that exhibited a-greater-than-3-fold increase in numbers of CFUs from the dilution plates 
stemming from toxin-coated vial relative to their respective aHis-coated (only) vial, were selected to 
be continued for subcloning. Subcloning itself consists of isolating the phagemid from the phage 
libraries, excising the gene from the phagemid, restrict a receiving vector (here, PSANG10-3F), 
recombining scFv-gene and restricted vector and transforming the ligated products into BL21(DE3) 
E. coli cells. Details are given below. 
 
50 µl glycerol stock from round 2 or round 3 was inoculated into 950 µl 2TY media in an 
Eppendorf tube, spun down at 6,000x g for 3 minutes, and then the pellet was resuspended in 5 mL 
2TYAG in a 50 mL falcon tube, and incubated at 30°C 280 rpm for 1 hr. Then, the GeneJETTM 
Plasmid MiniPrep Kit (Thermo scientific, #K0503) was used according to manufacturer’s 
instructions, to isolate the phagemids. In the end, phagemids were eluted in 50 µl 60 °C nuclease-
free miliQ H2O. DNA concentrations were then measured by NanoDrop.  
 
 
 5 µg phagemid (and 5 µg PSANG10-3F plasmid in a separate tube) was mixed with 
5 µl 10x Cutsmart buffer, 1.5 µl NotI-HF (NEB Cutter 500 U, #3189S), 1.5 µl NcoI-HF (NEB Cutter 
1000 U, #R3193S) and final volume adjusted to 50 µl with nuclease-free water for restriction of the 
plasmids. The restriction reactions were carried out at 37 °C for 3 hr. Then, the restriction products 
were applied to a 1% agarose gel and run for 25 min at 100 V. Corresponding DNA bands (700-800 
bp for scFv genes; 5000 bp for pSANG10-3F) were excised and isolated by a commercial GeneJET 
gel extraction kit (Thermo Scientific #K0691), according to manufacturer’s instructions. Three 
controls were run along with the pSANG10-3F sample; two with each respective restriction enzyme 
and one without any restriction enzymes. Final DNA yields were measured by NanoDrop.  
 
Following purification of restricted products, ligation was performed using 50 ng of restricted 
pSANG10-3F vector DNA and 25 ng of purified scFv genes. The ligation mixture was added 2 µl 
10x T4 DNA ligase buffer, 2 µl T4 DNA ligase and reaction volumes filled to 20 µl with nuclease-
free water. Simultaneously, a control ligation was included with no scFv for insert, and all reactions 
==PAGE:63==
 
 
 
 
Page 62 of 72 
were incubated at 16 °C for 16 hrs. For each transformation, respective 50 µl aliquots of competent 
BL21(DE3) E. coli cells were kept on ice and mixed with 5 µl ligation product and incubated for 30 
mins. After incubation, cells were heat shocked at 42 °C for 10 secs followed by 5 mins on ice. Then, 
950 µl of room temperature SOC medium was added and the transformed cells were transferred to 
50 mL falcon tubes and incubated at 37 °C for 1 hr and 250 rpm shaking. Then, 20 µl and 100 µl 
transformed cells were plated out onto 2TYGK plates (dilution plates). Remaining cells were 
centrifuged at 2,400x g for 5 mins. Supernatant was decanted and cells resuspended in the remaining 
supernatant and plated onto a 2TYGK plate. All plates were incubated ON at 37 °C. Furthermore, in 
the protocol, a positive control vector from the manufacturer’s package was included. 
 
 
Each individual transformation colony corresponded to one scFv-inserted pSANG10-3F, and 
thus represent one phage from each selection round. This yields monoclonal scFv-expressing E. coli 
colonies. For both selection rounds, 288 colonies were picked using sterile pipette tips, and incubated 
in 150 µl 2TYGK medium, in a polypropylene microtitre plate. Plates were sealed with an air-
permeable seal and incubated at 30 °C with 800 rpm ON. The following day, 50 µl 50% glycerol was 
added to each well, and plates were sealed with an aluminium-foil seal and stored at -80 °C.  
 
7.2.5 Monoclonal ELISA testing 
The protocol spans three days. First day, 2TYGK media was distributed to 150 µl per well, in a 96-
well, polypropylene plate to make a starter culture. Sterile pipette tips were used to inoculate each 
monoclone from the glycerol stock into the new polypropylene plate. The new plate was then covered 
with a gas-permeable seal and incubated ON at 30 °C and 400 rpm.  
 
The second day, 150 µl autoinduction media was distributed to each well of a new 
polypropylene microtitre plate. Sterile tips were again dipped in the starter-culture-polypropylene 
plate and transferred to the autoinduction plate. The plate was once again closed with a gas-permeable 
seal and incubated ON at 30 °C and 400 rpm. Six MaxiSorp 96-well plate (Sigma Aldrich cat. No. 
#M9410-1CS) were incubated with 60 µl aHis mAb at 2.5 µg/mL (in PBS) in each well at 4 °C ON. 
Likewise, three MaxiSorp 96-well plate was incubated in identical conditions, but in PBS, only.  
 
On the third day, the solution was removed from all three PBS-coated plates, by flicking and 
washing 3 times in PBS. Two hundred µl 3% MPBS were then added to each well and incubated for 
1 hr, for blocking all adhesion sites. Then, the 3% MPBS was removed and plates were washed 3 
times in PBS. Three aHis-coated MaxiSorp plates were then added 60 µl of t-MTx (diluted in 3% 
==PAGE:64==
 
 
 
 
Page 63 of 72 
MPBS) at a concentration of 2.5 µg/ml (0.15 µg/well). The PBS-coated plate was not added any t-
MTx, and neither was the three remaining aHis-coated MaxiSorp plates. The aHis-coated MaxiSorp 
plates were all incubated for 1 hr with either t-MTx or more 3% MPBS. 
 
Then, 30 µl of 6% MPBS was added to each well. The autoinduction plates were centrifuged 
at 2,100x g for 10 minutes in a 96-well plate centrifuge. Thirty µl of supernatant from the 
autoinduction plate was moved to each corresponding well, and mixed with a pipette, with the 6% 
MPBS. In this way, all picked 288 monoclones were tested for their binding to either 3% MPBS, 
aHis-antibody alone or aHis-coupled t-MTx. The plates were incubated for 1 hr, then washed 3 times 
in PBS-T and 3 washes in PBS. After that, 60 µl HRP-conjugated anti-FLAG antibody (0.05 µg/ml, 
diluted in 3% MPBS) was added to each well and incubated in the dark, at room temperature. Excess 
HRP-conjugated anti-FLAG mAbs were removed, and plates were washed 3 times with PBS-T and 
3 times in PBS. Then, 60 µl of activated OPD solution (SIGMAFASTTM OPD tablets dissolved 
according to manufacturer’s protocol) was added to each well, in a fumehood. The plates were placed 
in the dark, and time was tracked from moment of OPD application. After maximum 20 minutes the 
reaction was stopped by addition of 30 µl/well 3 M HCl; reaction was stopped when visible colour 
developed. Total time was noted down, and as soon as possible after reaction termination, each plate 
had its absorbance measured at 420 nm (Abs420) in an ELISA plate-reader. Data was analysed by 
simple EXCEL spreadsheets.

7.5 Discussion  
The initial thoughts prior to work onset was that it might be unnecessary to directly de-select aHis 
binders, but rather, simply blocking aHis-specific binders would be sufficient to avoid high 
abundance of aHis binders10. The plate tests prior to monoclonal ELISA showed no increase of 
colonies from t-cNTx rather than the aHis-antibody alone (see Figure 30) and were not chosen for 
continuation to subcloning of scFvs. In retrospective, this may have been an early warning that de-
selection was necessary. While the selections initially appeared to have been successful (Figure 32), 
further testing revealed that only scFvs with undesirable specificity were obtained, and that only three 
potential true “hits” had been achieved, which is already far from the norm [24]. To alleviate this 
problem, it could be a solution to biotinylate the tagged toxins and switch to strepta- or neutravidin 
capture after the first round of selections. To complement this, it may be an option to incubate the 
initial phage library in an aHis:Ub19His10-coated vial, and afterwards, without eluting the bound 
phages, move the phage solution to a aHis:t-toxin-coated vial. An alternative approach in the future 
may be to genetically fuse a SpyTagTM-tag to the N-terminal, instead of the Ub19His10-tag.This way, 
the SpyCatcher protein would be an antigen-capturer molecule for phage display, and one could 
change to anti-SpyTag mAb-mediated antigen-capture in subsequent panning rounds. The advantage 
of doing so is that SpyTag is just 13 amino acid residues [89] vs. the 12.3 kDa Ub19His10-tag, 
reducing the risk of anti-SpyTag antibodies during phage display. Also, pure SpyTag can be readily 
obtained [90] for de-selection or blocking, and for evaluation of SpyCatcher:SpyTag-binders (only) 
during monoclonal ELISA and plate testing. This also brings the point that the Ub19His10-tag should 
had been included in evaluation of aHis (only) binders as to evaluate aHis:tag binders, instead of 
only aHis, during plate testing and in monoclonal ELISAs. However, the Ub19His10-tag is not 
readily commercially available.  
 
Looking at the results overall, it can be concluded that simply blocking was insufficient to 
filter away aHis- or Ub19His10-binders. This is underlined by the fact that far the majority of hits in 
the monoclonal ELISA turned out to be against the aHis-antibody. In the future, the aforementioned 
solution with preincubation of the library in aHis:Ub19His10-coated vials could be key to identifying 
true toxin binders. In this way, a proper de-selection step would be introduced early in the protocol, 
                                               
10 Blocking refers to incubating the library with molecules to block unwanted binders, whereas de-selection refers to 
actually removing unwanted binders by separating them from the library. 
==PAGE:69==

which could be further supplemented by a streptavidin-capture switch (if deemed necessary) in the 
second round, but it would require continuous production of an Ub19His10-only stock.  
 
7.6 Module C: Short Summary 
Discovering mAbs against cNTx and MTx via their Ub19His10-tagged recombinant versions was 
unsuccessful. Out of the four potential positive hits against MTx, which is already a very low number 
in comparison to the norm, the lead candidate turned out to be a false positive. All in all, the discovery 
protocol itself has room for improvements to get rid of certain expression biases, and introduce de-
selection steps to sort away binders against the aHis antibody and/or against the Ub19His10-tag. In 
the future, it may be very beneficial to switch to the SpyTag/SpyCatcher system for optimal selection. 
8. Overall Discussion  
Recombinant toxins have been utilized in antivenom research since 1996 in the field of scorpion 
antivenom [91]. It has been particularly applied in fields where venom yields are scarce, and the 
animals are rare and/or cannot be kept in captivity. The first time recombinant immunogens were 
seen in the snake antivenom field was in 2010, to circumvent procurement of venom from a highly 
lethal, but lowly venom-producing snake, Bungarus multicinctus [64]. The situation with M. 
mipartitus is similar with the additional caveat that the snake cannot be maintained in captivity 
[43,73,77,92]. In the work of this thesis, it has been shown that a recombinant toxin can invoke M. 
mipartitus venom-recognising antiserum, which may potentially be cross-recognising as well. This 
will be further tested in the future, by a group in Colombia.  
 
For t-cNTx, sr-rMTx and t-MTx antisera was achieved, but the specific IgG titres were not 
sufficient in vivo neutralization of toxicity. However, the response in ELISAs, reflecting IgG titres, 
increased throughout the immunisation scheme. Thus, in future experiments, it might be possible to 
achieve higher titres than those achieved here, by using higher immunogen dosages, in order to 
achieve neutralisation. The t-MTx and t-cNTx responses increased dramatically from day 37 to 98 
relative to average serum response of the naïve rabbit’s. In contrast, the rMTx response was initially 
high but did not increase significantly to day 98. Taken together, perhaps the initial response of t-
MTx and t-cNTx was predominantly against the Ub19His10-tag, but IgG titres steadily increased 
against the toxin moieties (and tag; compare Figures 21 vs. 22), demonstrating increased 
immunogenicity of the toxin moieties, seeing as, in contrast, rMTx-immunised rabbits seemed to 
develop a tolerance response. Thus, the increased molecular size may have increased 
==PAGE:70==
 
 
 
 
Page 69 of 72 
immunogenicity, but a higher immunisation dosage (and/or longer immunisation interval) would 
have been necessary. This also falls in line with the debate that smaller peptides may need carrier 
proteins for increased immunogenicity, such as Keyhole Limpet Hemocyanin [26,93–96]. Here, 
Ub19His10 was used, and the benefits of having a peptide-based tag is that it can be directly 
expressed, instead of it being necessary to conduct post-expression chemical modifications. On the 
other hand, the srMTx booster injection in rabbit 1 of the rMTx-immunised rabbits led to an increased 
response against M. mipartitus whole venom. This shows that it is possible to increase serum response 
by non-tagged recombinant proteins; but perhaps the change from rMTx to srMTx (which is assumed 
to have a different conformation) re-invoked an immune response, possibly due to the difference in 
peptide conformation and toxicity of srMTx. An alternative immunisation procedure could also be to 
alternate between rMTx and srMTx booster injections, to enhance immunogenicity. In this way, 
starting with higher dosages of the toxoid, rMTx, may correspond to lower levels of srMTx, but then 
switch to the bioactive immunogen at a later stage. 
 
 
 
One of the benefits with producing cNTx and rMTx for immunisation procedures is that they 
would be directly implementable in current antivenom manufacturing facilities; simply to either add 
them to immunisation mixtures or generate antitoxin-antiserum that could be added to existing 
antivenom. Adding the T1ans to existing immunisation mixtures, however, may not be advantageous 
due to their low immunogenicity, and so, fortification with pure anti-T1an may be better. Potential 
scFvs that might had been discovered would have needed a new production facility setup for CHO 
cell IgG production, before entry-to-market. Thus, recombinant immunogens could represent a 
transition phase of improved antivenoms in the waiting time until production facilities are setup for 
fully human, recombinant antivenoms and are furthermore very advantageous in cases of artificial or 
hard-to-procure snake toxins. As significant, cross-reactive IgG titres by immunisation were 
demonstrated here, it suggests further studies of consensus toxins and their corresponding cross-
recognising abilities (in contrast to natural toxins) are warranted. Consensus toxins and resulting 
antitoxin may represent a possible solution for the future of antivenom production, prior to the 
implementation of fully human, recombinant IgGs. 
9. Overall Conclusions 
Going back to address the initial project aims, production of recombinant snake toxins by the LE577 
strain was successful. Discovery of human scFvs against either t-cNTx or t-MTx failed. 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
 
 
Page 70 of 72 
Neutralisation by respective antisera was ambiguous but indeed IgGs failed to protect the mice against 
12 µg (2 LD99s) srMTx, or 1.3 LD50s t-cNTx. However, antisera were confirmed to recognize POIs 
after 98 and 112 days of immunisation injections every second week. This was concretely M. 
mipartitus snake venom for t-MTx-, rMTx- and sr-rMTx-immunised rabbits’ antisera, and Dp4, Nk5, 
Nm3, D. polylepis- and N. melanoleuca snake venom for t-cNTx-immunised rabbits’ antisera (under 
the assumption Dp27 contained 3FTxs). It is concluded that increased immunisation dosages and 
enhanced molecular size (or alternation between toxoid and recombinant toxin), in combination with 
immunogenicity-enhancing, Th-2-inducing adjuvants, may represent the best optimisation of antisera 
production with in vivo neutralisation capacity. This would enable an inter-Micrurus clade B-3FTx 
antitoxin, possible also clades F, G and/or H, and potentially an inter-T1an cobra- and mamba 
antitoxin. Thus, taken together, recombinant expression is a promising solution for enhancing 
currently established antivenom manufacturing, and it may improve therapeutic efficacy until new 
production platforms enable the fully human, recombinant antivenoms in the future.  
10. Entrepreneurial Aspects & Perspectives 
10.1 Potential IP & Commercialization  
Having a significantly higher average homology to whichever toxin subclass one uses to generate a 
consensus toxin, the probability of discovering cross-reactive antibodies by phage display or 
immunisation should increase. However, as the toxins’ amino acid sequences are not naturally 
occurring, they are patentable, which ensures a time-limited monopoly. Firstly, one might then focus 
on immunogen production for conventional antiserum manufacturing, making production venom-
independent. Secondly, a new product can be launched, by using the artificial proteins to discover 
scFvs (via phage display), leading to production of cross-reactive IgGs for rational mixing of 
monoclonal IgGs, for recombinant antivenoms. To generate the most market-relevant consensus 
toxins, firstly, one should identify important snake species in a certain geographical area and then 
their medically relevant toxins, from toxicity score (personal communication, professor José María 
Gutiérrez Gutiérrez). The academic research project by de la Rosa et al. that was conducted in 2018 
[29] would not have high product value proposition, as the targeted snake genera are across many 
countries, rather than focusing on individual countries. cNTx used in this study would be relevant for 
Sub-Saharan African countries’ mamba and cobra T1an neutralisation; yet other consensus toxins 
should be developed for the rest of the Sub-Saharan African snakes’ medically relevant toxins. 
Mixing antisera from the various consensus toxins, rather than immunisation mixtures, to avoid 
==PAGE:72==
 
 
 
 
Page 71 of 72 
immunogenicity-suppressing effects, could lead to the first pan-African antivenoms. Likewise, this 
strategy could be employed to make pan-Asian, pan-Oceania, pan-north American and pan-South- & 
Central American antivenoms (personal communication, professor José María Gutiérrez Gutiérrez). 
During this time, research could be done to discover human monoclonal IgGs against the same 
consensus toxins, and CHO cell production facilities could be set up during the integration process 
of the new immunogens in existing antivenom production facilities.  
 
Mipartoxin-1 is on the other hand readily translatable. As the sequence is naturally occurring, 
it cannot be patented, unless one makes a consensus toxin of clade B, F, G and/or H (or certain 
combinations of these). However, addition of antiserum against recombinant mipartoxin-1 to existing 
M. nigrocinctus antivenom might neutralise 3FTx-dominant venoms containing predominantly clade 
B 3FTxs; or toxins from other clades with sufficient homology to MTx. Examples may include M. 
surinamensis and M. isozonus as these were found to be neutralized by native M. mipartitus antiserum 
[39]. Another opportunity for IP protection might be patenting the recombinant production processes 
with MTx. However, the market itself is not big; only 1-2% of envenomings in the Americas are 
caused by Micrurus snakes, being approximately about 600 to 1200 snake bites per year [97], from 
which not all are by 3FTx-dominated Micrurus species.  
 
10.2 Scientific Perspectives  
The power of high-throughput peptide microarray has earlier been proven by identification of (only) 
linear epitopes of neutralising antivenoms. Continuing such experiments would allow design of 
consensus epitopes from e.g. all cobra snake species’ toxins in Africa, which could be synthesised 
and chemically conjugated to Keyhole Limpet Hemocyanin (200 peptides per KLH molecule) and 
used for immunisation purposes. KLH has previously been shown to be an efficient adjuvant [94–
96]. This would skip the process of recombinant expression and allow much higher yield by chemical 
synthesis. Future research avenues could test whether such linear peptides would invoke sufficiently 
neutralising IgG titres, or whether introduced cysteines would be necessary to mimic the native 
epitope structures. For instance, for T1ans, the 14 amino acids surrounding the DHRG motif 
identified in previous studies could be used for synthesis as a proof of concept; it has been shown that 
a specific DHRG- (in cobra/mamba) or HDGR (in Micrurus T1ans) motif in Loop II is recognized 
by commercial antivenom [65, and unpublished data from the same study]. By chemical synthesis, 
very high peptide titres and purity can be obtained, and these can be directly implemented in existing 
antivenom manufacturing facilities, circumventing the need for snake venoms.   
==PAGE:73==
 
 
 
 
Page 72 of 72 
Identifying epitopes and designing consensus toxins/epitopes is also an approach that could be 
utilized in many other fields of medicine. For example, vaccine development against bacterial toxins 
from multi-resistant bacteria, or development of consensus sequences of cancer-specific surface 
proteins to target different types of cancer at once. The latter of course assumes variants of surface 
proteins are present between different variants of the same cancer; or across different cancer types. 
However, it would mean that many genetic analyses need to be performed to assess exactly what 
surface protein a given cancer expresses, before being able to deploy an epitope that could invoke a 
strong immunological response in the host (T and B cell epitopes).  
Lastly, other than coupling synthetic consensus epitopes to KLH, DNA vaccinations has 
previously been applied in the field of snake antivenom research [26,74,98,99], but neutralising IgG 
titres as efficacious as conventional immunisation has yet to be achieved. However, in 2019, it was 
shown that DNA immunisation of a construct with the antigen, genetically fused to an scFv targeting 
a dendritic cell endocytic receptor (e.g. in 2019; DEC205), led to significantly improved specific-IgG 
titre, in comparison to non-targeting DNA immunisation [100], particularly in combination with Th-
2-promoting adjuvant. Thus, genetic fusion of consensus epitopes to dendritic cell endocytic 
receptors-targeting nanobodies, may solve the insufficient specific-IgG titres of previous DNA 
immunisation efforts. A possible optimisation avenue would be to utilize mRNA immunisation rather 
than DNA-based, as these have proved to invoke a stronger immune response in some studies [101–
105], with several advantages to DNA-based immunisation. Both techniques rely on host cells to 
produce and secrete the fusion protein, which would then be directed to DC-cells, where the 
nanobodies would increase uptake of epitopes to the DCs.  
Taken together, future solutions might be to generate consensus toxins for regional antivenoms, 
which combined might be capable of neutralising all snake species’ venoms in a given region, e.g. in 
certain countries or if possible, across multiple countries. Where relevant, e.g. if many countries share 
snake species with high homology, high toxicity score toxins, it may be advantageous to firstly 
conduct high-throughput microarray peptide assays to identify epitopes of known-neutralizing IgGs 
from current antivenoms, before designing consensus epitopes; leading to mRNA-based 
immunisation approaches. This would result in a future where every country has antivenom, 
neutralizing the snake species relevant to them, with relatively cheap and easy manufacturing, 
bringing high accessibility to even the impoverished people of rural areas, whom are the truest victims 
of the snakebite tragedy.

Appendix C: Explanations of t-MTx pooling 
 
Supplementary Figure 1: Example of typical fractions from HPLC-mounted Ni-NTA column, affinity 
chromatography. Three pools were made; “first pool” consisting of fractions A5-A10, containing 
more impurities (see area around 34-95 kDa). “Main pool” consisting of A11-B1, and “Post pool” 
containing B3-B5. Of course, fraction numbers do not correspond in other runs, but this is merely an 
example of how pools were generated. Main pool was used for immunisations, first and post pool 
were used for ELISAs and phage display.
