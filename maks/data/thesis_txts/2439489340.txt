Abstract
Since the 1960’s, plastic production has grown exponentially making plastic-based prod-
ucts universal in today’s society (Bombelli et al., 2017). The extensive production is a
result of plastic material’s several attributes, including its durable, light, and versatile
properties (Christensen, 2011). In 2017, the annual plastic production exceeded 350 mil-
lion tonnes (Nova-Institute, 2018). Due to a continuous growth in the world population,
the demand of plastic materials are expected to further increase, ultimately resulting in
an increase of plastic production and the quantity of plastics in the waste streams of the
society (Emadian et al., 2017; European Commission, 2013; Shimao, 2001).
The main objective of this study was to investigate the possibility of plastic degrada-
tion under anaerobic conditions, to allow degradation during anaerobic digestion (AD)
in Danish biogas plants. The degradation of four different plastics was hence investi-
gated based on laboratory batch experiments: pellets of virgin low-density polyethylene
(LDPE) (P1), pellets of bio-LDPE (P2), bio-degradable polycaprolactone (PCL) film
from organic household waste collection bags (P3), and LDPE film from freezer bags
(P4). The chosen polymers - LDPE and PCL - were identified as plastics potentially
ending in the AD feedstock in Danish biogas plants as a result of incorrect source segre-
gation of municipal household waste or inefficient screening of collection bags for organic
household waste prior to biogas production.
Two different microorganisms, Bacillus circulans and Bacillus licheniformis, as well as
the microbial culture within Galleria mellonella wax worms were used to investigate the
degradation of P1, P2, P3, and P4 through three different bio-methane potential (BMP)
tests under mesophilic conditions and an incubation period of approximately 70 days.
The BMP test bottles, furthermore, included inoculum from a pilot scale biogas reactor
including a biopulp of municipal household waste. The BMP tests were used as a tool
to assess plastic degradation as a result of CH4 production. As an attempt to increase
the biodegradability of the plastics during the BMP tests, several batches were tested
with ultraviolet (UV) irradiated plastic material.
Based on the results gained from the BMP tests, it was concluded that a combination
of UV-treated PCL with inoculum and B. licheniformis showed the most promising re-
sults, providing a final CH4 yield of 545 NmL CH4/g VS. This was 73 % more than what
was achieved with UV-treated PCL with inoculum and B. circulans, and seven times
more CH4 than what was produced with PCL without UV-treatment with inoculum
and B. licheniformis. For plastics without UV-treatment, PCL and LDPE film showed

The produced sludge from the BMP test was investigated to determine if microplastics

used as a substitute to mineral fertilizer on agricultural land. The sludge investigation
revealed that all samples, that in the BMP test had been added with plastic material,
had remains of microplastics as a result of plastic degradation. It is therefore important
to further investigate the subject on plastic degradation under anaerobic conditions to
prevent that microplastics could accumulate in the environment, thereby potentially
having damaging effects on ecosystems and human health.

Sammenfatning
Produktionen af plastik har siden 1960’erne været eksponentiel voksende, og brugen af
plastikbaserede produkter er udbredt i nutidens samfund (Bombelli et al., 2017). Denne
stigende produktion skyldes, at plastik er et let og holdbart materiale med mange anven-
delsesmuligheder (Christensen, 2011). Den årlige plastikproduktion oversteg i 2017 350
million tons (Nova-Institute, 2018). I takt med at verdensbefolkningen stiger, forventes
behovet for plastik tilsvarende at være stigende, hvilket vil medføre en stigende plastik
produktion og samtidig en øget mængde plastikaffald (Emadian et al., 2017; European
Commission, 2013; Shimao, 2001).
Dette studie undersøger mulighederne for plastiknedbrydning under anaerobiske forhold
i danske biogasanlæg. Dertil undersøges forekomsten af mikroplastik som følge af plas-
tiknedbrydning under biogasproduktion.
I dette speciale blev nedbrydningen af fire
typer plastik undersøgt som et laboratoriebatchforsøg. Følgende typer plastik blev under-
søgt; jomfruelige lav densitet polyethylen (LDPE) pellets (P1), bio-LDPE pellets (P2),
bionedbrydelig polycaprolacetone (PCL) film fra poser til organisk husholdningsaffald
(P3) og LDPE film fra almindelige fryseposer (P4). Polymererne – LDPE og PCL – blev
udvalgt, da disse anses for at have sandsynlighed for at ende i råstoffet på danske bio-
gasfabrikker, og således indgå i den anaerobiske nedbrydning af organisk affald. Dette
vil kunne skyldes fejlsortering af plastikaffald eller ineffektiv screening af bioaffaldsposer
inden biogasproduktionen.
Til test af nedbrydningen af P1, P2, P3, P4 er der anvendt to forskellige mikroorganis-
mer Bacillus circulans og Bacillus licheniformis, samt den mikrobielle kultur fra Galleria
mellonella voksmøllarver. Forsøget kørte gennem tre forskellige bio-methan potentiale
(BMP) test under mesofile forhold og gennem en inkubationsperiode på cirka 70 dage.
BMP testflaskerne indeholdt inokulum fra en biogasreaktor med biomasse fra kommunal
husholdningsaffald. Methan (CH4) produktionen i BMP testene blev målt som et udtryk
for nedbrydning af plastik. Som et forsøg på at øge bionedbrydningen af plastik i BMP-
testene blev flere af forsøgsrækkerne testet med ultraviolet (UV)-strålebehandlet plastik.
Resultaterne af BMP testene viste en nedbrydning af plastik i kombinationen af UV-
behandlet PCL med inokulum og B. licheniformis med et CH4 potentiale på 545 NmL
CH4/g VS. Dette CH4 potentiale er 73 % højere end det der blev opnået med UV-
behandlet PCL med inokulum og B. circulans, og syv gange mere produceret CH4
sammenlignet med PCL, uden UV-behandling med inokulum og B. licheniformis.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Since the 1960’s plastic production has grown exponentially making plastic-based prod-
ucts universal in today’s society (Bombelli et al., 2017). In 2001, the annual plastic
production was approximately 140 million tonnes. In 2015 the annual plastic produc-
tion exceeded 300 million tonnes, which in 2017 had increased to an annual production
of 350 million tonnes was (Nova-Institute, 2018; Emadian et al., 2017; Shimao, 2001).
The extensive production of plastic materials is a result of its many attributes, such as
low production costs and its durable, light, and versatile properties (Christensen, 2011).
Due to a continuous growth in the world population, the demand of plastic materials will
further increase, which ultimately will increase the plastic production and the quantity
of plastic materials in the waste streams (European Commission, 2013). As the stability
and durability of plastic materials have improved through time, synthetic plastics are
today considered resistant to environmental influences (Shah et al., 2008). This charac-
teristic raises concerns in regard to pollution and accumulation of plastic waste in the
environment having destructive effects on wildlife, cities, and forests (Shah et al., 2008).
Meanwhile, in the 21st century, bio-waste has grown to become an important part of en-
ergy production and soil improvement through anaerobic digestion (AD). AD processes
are widely used within waste management systems, resulting in the production of bio-
gas, which can be utilized directly for the production of electricity and district heating
or conversion to fuel (Christensen, 2011). In 2014 the Danish government set a target
with the aim of Denmark being independent of fossil fuels, coal, oil, and natural gas
by 2050 (Danish Ministry of Energy, Utilities and Climate, 2018). This has led to an
increased energy production from renewable resources, and biogas production through
AD is therefore continuously increasing in Denmark. In March 2017, a total of 156 bio-
gas plants were operating in Denmark and eight biogas plants were, furthermore, under
construction (Appendix D) (Danish Energy Agency, 2018). In the period from 2015 to
2017 a total of 10 new biogas plants were put into operation (Danish Energy Agency,
2018).
The utilization of bio-waste for biogas production requires a complex segregation system
within the waste management. However, no segregation system can exist without the
risk of incorrect source segregation. If plastic waste ends in the bio-waste fraction, as
an impurity, plastic waste materials will be present in the AD feedstock. In addition to
incorrectly source-segregated plastic waste, plastic collection bags for organic household
waste also have the potential of ending in the AD feedstock. In Denmark, 31 munic-
ipalities have included waste management of organic household waste to their source

segregation system (Danske Kommuner, 2018). The collection bags for this waste frac-
tion are made of plastic, which depending on the municipality can be either synthetic
plastic or bio-plastic. In the current waste management system in Denmark, organic
household waste is utilized for biogas production through AD. Prior to the biogas pro-

feedstock. The collection bags are instead utilized for energy recovery at a waste-to-

During biogas production residues in the form of digestate are generated, which can be
applied on agricultural soil as a substitute to mineral fertilizer. This resource recovery,
however, may be restricted due to the presence of plastic substances, including microplas-
tics, in the digestate originating from the AD feedstock. Accumulation of microplastics
in the environment can potentially have damaging effects to the ecosystem and human
health (Wagner and Lambert, 2017). Understanding the fate and degradation of plastic
materials during biogas production is an important progression to improve Danish waste
management systems and minimize environmental impacts.
1.1
State-of-the-art
During recent years, the importance of biodegradable plastics and bio-degradation of
plastic waste has increased, and novel solutions for the degradation of plastics are needed
(Bombelli et al., 2017; Shah et al., 2008). Today, various chemical processes have been
replaced with biotechnological processes in which microorganisms and enzymes are be-
ing used (Priya and Sashi, 2014). In recent studies, microorganisms with the ability of
producing enzymes have shown potential of degrading plastic materials. As a founda-
tion to this study, microbial organisms containing plastic degrading abilities have been
investigated through extensive literature research. Several studies were found available
on the topic of bio-degradation of plastics under aerobic conditions, however, literature
is scarce on the subject of anaerobic plastic degradation.
Sukkhum et al. (2012) investigated the bio-degradation of polylatic acid (PLA) under
aerobic conditions, with PLA degrading enzymes produced by the bacterium Actino-
madura keratinilytica NBRC 104111 strain T16-1, which was isolated from forest soils.
This bacterium had the potential of degrading PLA powder with approximately 40 %
within four days of incubation at 60◦C and pH 9. Approximately 60 % degradation was
achieved within 14 days (Pathak et al., 2017).

enzyme concentration of 0.8 µg/mL. Degradation of polybutylene succinate (PBS) and
polycaprolactone (PCL) was obtained with an enzyme concentration of 8 ng/mL. Com-

Theoretical background
This chapter provides a general description of both petrochemical and bio-based plastic
materials, including an introduction to the three plastic types used for the laboratory
experiments. Furthermore, the concept of microplastics will be introduced as well as
methods for microplastic identification. Finally, different degradation processes for plas-

2.1
Description of plastic materials
Plastics can either be categorized as biodegradable or non-biodegradable and exist in
one of the following forms: petrochemical, partly bio-based, or bio-based (Figure 2.1).
In the following sections the three different forms will be described, as well as biodegrad-
able or non-biodegradable plastics.
Plastics can, based on their characteristics, be classified as either thermoplastics or ther-
mosets. Thermoplastics are the most dominant type on the market and accounts for
approximately 80 % of the plastics produced (Christensen, 2011). The chemical struc-
ture of thermoplastics is based on long polymer chains, which at increased temperature
and pressure become soft and mechanically doable (Christensen, 2011). When cooled
down the plastic will hold its form, while, maintaining the ability of being reshaped at
increased temperature (Christensen, 2011). Plastics classified as thermoplastics include
polyethylene (PE), polystyrene (PS), polypropylene (PP) and polyvinylchloride (PVC)
(Christensen, 2011). Thermosets on the other hand have grid structures, which makes
the polymers highly resistant to mechanical forces, and can therefore not be reshaped
upon reheating (Encyclopædia Britannica, 2017a; Christensen, 2011). Thermoset plas-
tics include phenoplast, aminoplast, unsaturated polyester (UP), epoxide (EP), and
PUR (Christensen, 2011).
To achieve appropriate product properties plastic polymers can be combined with addi-
tives. This is obtained when mixing additives, such as elasticizers, colorants, reinforce-
ments, and stabilizers with the plastic during its processing (Encyclopædia Britannica,
2017a).

2.1.1
Petrochemical plastic
Petrochemical plastics are synthetic plastics originating from fossil oil and produced
through distillation. The distillation process results in the formation of hydrocarbon
chains, which are cracked into monomers, and finally synthesized into polymers (Chris-
tensen, 2011).
Synthesized polymers can be manufactured into different shapes and
products by means of mechanical processes such as molding and extruding (Christensen,
2011).
2.1.2
Bio-plastic
The term bio-plastic covers a number of plastics with different properties. Bio-plastics
can be characterized as biodegradable, degradable, or bio-based plastics. Even though
bio-plastics are a (more) sustainable substitute to synthetic plastics, as it is possible to
reduce the use of fossil fuels during production, it is not possible to characterize bio-
plastics as carbon dioxide (CO2) neutral. Some of the most common bio-plastics on
the market today are PLA based on cane sugar, bio-derived PE based on bio-ethanol,
starch-based plastic, and poly-3-hydroxybuturate (PHB) based on glucose or starch
(Christensen, 2011).
Bio-based plastics are plastics that originates either entirely or partly from renewable
biomass, such as corn starch, cane sugar, and vegetable oil (Christensen, 2011). Bio-
based plastics can either be biodegradable and non-degradable.
Biodegradable bio-
plastics are mostly utilized for the production of disposal items such as cutlery, cups,
straws, shopping bags and foils. Non-degradable bio-plastics are on the other hand used
for long-life products such as automotive parts, cell phones, and sport shoes.
Biodegradable plastics can be produced from synthetic plastics by adding specific ad-
ditives to the plastic causing willingness to microbial degradation (Singh and Sharma,
2008). Biodegradable plastics have the ability of decomposing into CO2, methane (CH4),
H2O, inorganic compounds, or biomass, through enzymatic and microbial actions. The
degradation of biodegradable deepens on the chemical structure of the plastic and not
on the origin of the raw material used for the production of the plastic material (SCION,

2011). Biodegradable plastics can thus be produced from both fossil oil and renewable
resources.
Degradable plastics that are not biodegradable can decompose through abiotic degrada-
tion processes such as photodegradation, mechanical degradation, oxidative degradation,
and hydrolytic degradation, which will be described in Section 2.2.
2.1.3
Microplastic
Microplastics are commonly referred to as plastic particles with a diameter <5 mm, how-
ever, a microplastic size definition has not formally been recognized (GESAMP, 2018;
Wagner and Lambert, 2017).
However, activities within the scientific community in
terms of improving the definition of microplastics are currently growing (Wagner et al.,
2018). Andrady (2011) and Browne et al. (2011) suggest microplastics to be defined
as particles with a diameter <1 mm, thus only including particles of a micrometer size
range. Lambert et al. (2014) suggests defining microplastics as ≤1 mm to >0.1 µm,
mesoplastics as ≤5 to >1 mm, and macroplastics as particles with a diameter >5 mm.
GESAMP (2018) considers microplastics of particles in the range of 1 nm to <5 mm.
For this master thesis microplastics were considered as particles with a diameter <1 mm.
Microplastics can, based on their origin, be defined as either primary or secondary prod-
ucts (Wagner and Lambert, 2017). Primary microplastics are plastics that are intention-
ally produced to be applied in e.g. cosmetics. Secondary microplastics are contrarily
produced during the degradation of larger plastic substances, including both synthetic
plastics and bio-plastics (Wagner and Lambert, 2017; Cole et al., 2011).
The presence of microplastics in the ecosystem is a global environmental challenge, that
to some extent are different from the environmental challenges caused by larger plastic
substances (Rillig et al., 2017; Brander et al., 2011). One of the challenges arisen as
a result of microplastic accumulation in the environment is that the plastic particles
due to their small size can be ingested by different organisms, leading to accumulation
of microplastics in the food chain and potentially have damaging effects on ecosystems
and human life (Maes et al., 2017; Rillig et al., 2017). Microplastics are, as opposed to
larger plastic particles, highly mobile, thus easily transported from soil sediments to wa-
ter bodies, reaching groundwaters and oceans, as a result of surface runoff or dispersion
(Wagner and Lambert, 2017). Microplastics are, however, not likely to reach ground-
waters in Denmark, due to the tightly packed characteristics of Danish soil sediments
(Mackevia and Hartmann, 2018). One of the concerns related to the mobile nature of
microplastics is the potential of invasive species being carried with the microplastics and
consequently being introduced to new environments (Barnes, 2002).

2.1.4.1
Polyethylene
PE belongs to the group of polyolefins (POs), which includes all plastics that are pro-
duced by polymerization of olefins (alkene monomers). PE can either be produced as a
synthetic or partly bio-based plastic. Synthetic PE is created by the polymerization of

units with a double bond connecting the carbon atoms. This double bond can be broken
down by polymerizing catalysts, leading to the formation of an extra single bond that
can be linked to a carbon atom in another C2H4 molecule (Encyclopædia Britannica,
2017b). By replicating this structure PE is created (Figure 2.2). PE is one of the most
extensively used plastics for production worldwide and is transformed into final products
such as food packaging film, plastic bags, and water bottles (Shah et al., 2008).

both PE types is formed by extruding and pelletizing the plastic. Additives are here-

Virgin LDPE is newly produced plastic usually in the form of pellets that have not been
manufactured into a product yet. Virgin plastic is produced directly from the petro-
chemical feedstock such as natural gas or crude oil.

Plastic degradation
The degradation of plastics, synthetic plastics as well as bio-plastics, is defined as a
detrimental change in the plastic’s appearance, mechanical and physical characteristics,
as well as chemical structure (Mostafa et al., 2010; Griffin, 1994). During the degrada-
tion, plastic polymers are transformed into smaller molecular units such as oligomers,
monomers, and if possible completely mineralized (Wagner and Lambert, 2017). Plastics
can also be degraded into microplastics. The degradation of microplastics include break

tion and degradation into non-polymer organic molecules (Wagner and Lambert, 2017;
Gigault et al., 2016; Lambert et al., 2014). The degradation of plastics can be char-
acterized as either abiotic or biotic and can furthermore be divided into five common
degradation processes: photodegradation, mechanical degradation, oxidative degrada-
tion, hydrolytic degradation, and bio-degradation (Figure 2.4).
Synthetic plastics are generally known for their high resistance towards environmental
influences, which consequently leads to low degradation rates and long residence times
once they have entered the environment (Wagner and Lambert, 2017). Biodegradable
plastics can undergo degradation as a result of microbial actions. The degradation does
not entail non-toxic end-products, and there is no defined time period in which the
degradation should be obtained (World Centric, 2018). Degradable plastics can undergo
significant changes in its chemical structure under specific environmental conditions,

2.2.1
Abiotic plastic degradation
This section provides a brief description of the different abiotic plastic degradation pro-
cesses.
2.2.1.1
Photodegradation
Photodegradation is caused by the effect of light, leading to changes in the plastic
material’s physical and optical properties (Polymer Properties Database, 2015a; Singh
and Sharma, 2008). UV irradiation attacks the surface of the plastic leaving visible
cracks and discoloration, thus resulting in loss of mechanical properties and molecular
weight (Polymer Properties Database, 2015a; Singh and Sharma, 2008). Plastics such
as PE are, in theory, stable when exposed to UV radiation, due to their C-C and C-
H bonds, which do not absorb UV radiation (Polymer Properties Database, 2015a).
Nevertheless, these plastic types generally contain additives, allowing the initiation of
photodegradation. The degradation usually provides fast degradation rates, however,
the degradation can be slowed down by additives such as antioxidants that have an
inhibiting effect on the degradation process, due to its ability of preventing oxidation
(Wagner and Lambert, 2017). The maximum photodegradation is achieved at different
wavelengths depending on the polymer bonds of the specific plastic (Singh and Sharma,
2008). The maximum damage for PE is obtained at a wavelength of approximately 300
nm with an exposure time of 40-200 hours (Qin et al., 2003).
2.2.1.2
Mechanical degradation
Mechanical degradation is a physical degradation that results in bond cleavage due to
a decreased molecular weight of the plastic material (Booth, 1963).
A reduction of
molecular weight will lead to an increased particle size and plastic surface area, which
finally will cause higher reactivity. Ultimately, a higher reactivity will cause an increased
degradation rate (Wagner and Lambert, 2017).
2.2.1.3
Oxidative degradation
Oxidative degradation can be divided into three steps: initiation, propagation, and ter-
mination. Initiation occurs when the plastic is exposed to light or heat. During the
initiation, polymer chains form radicals either by C-C bond cleavage or transfer of hy-
drogen (H2) atoms (Polymer Properties Database, 2015b). During propagation, a free
radical reacts with an oxygen (O2) molecule forming a peroxy radical, which has the abil-
ity of removing a H2 atom from another polymer chain to form hydroperoxide (H2O2)
(Polymer Properties Database, 2015b). The H2O2 will cleave into two new free radicals,
which will keep propagating the reaction to other polymer molecules (Polymer Proper-
ties Database, 2015b). Termination can either occur by recombination of two radicals or
chain cleavage. If the termination is carried out as a result of recombination of radicals,

14

the molecular weight of the plastic and its cross-linking density will increase, causing
embrittlement and cracking of the plastic (Polymer Properties Database, 2015b). Ter-
mination through chain cleavage, on the other hand, will decrease the molecular weight
of the plastic, causing softening of the plastic, thus reducing its mechanical properties
(Polymer Properties Database, 2015b). The route of the termination step depends on
the plastic type. POs with short alkyl side groups, such as PP, will terminate through
chain cleavage, whereas, PE will terminate because of radical recombination.
2.2.1.4
Hydrolytic degradation
Hydrolytic degradation occurs based on four stages as illustrated in Figure 2.5. The first
step includes diffusion of water (H2O) (Engineer et al., 2011). In the following stage,
oligomers with acidic end-groups autocatalyze a hydrolysis process (Engineer et al.,
2011). Mass loss of plastics is achieved in the beginning of the third stage. This stage
also includes diffusion of oligomers from the plastic, causing H2O molecules to diffuse
into the void (Engineer et al., 2011). In the final stage the plastic becomes porous to
such a degree that the degradation will continue, though at a slower rate than before
(Engineer et al., 2011).

2.2.2
Biotic plastic degradation
A degradation can be characterized as biotic if it is based on biological activity. Bio-
degradation of plastic can be defined as the alteration of polymer properties through mi-
crobial digestion, including enzymatic digestion, reduction of molecular weight, and loss
of mechanical strength and surface properties (Pathak et al., 2017). The bio-degradation
of plastics includes three essential steps (Emadian et al., 2017; Lucas et al., 2008):
• Bio-deterioration: modification of mechanical, chemical, and physical proper-
ties, as a result of microbial growth on, or inside, the surface of the plastic.
==PAGE:35==
2.2 Plastic degradation
15
• Bio-fragmentation: microbial conversion of plastic to oligomers and monomers.
• Assimilation: microbial conversion of carbon, from fragmented plastic, into CO2,
H2O, and biomass.
Plastic bio-degradation can proceed in both aerobic and anaerobic environments, but
must always adhere to the following criteria (Wagner and Lambert, 2017):
• The degradation must include microorganisms with the ability of depolymerizing
plastic and mineralizing monomer units with enzymes.
• The degradation must be carried out under optimal environmental conditions with
regard to temperature, pH, moisture, and salinity.
• The degradation conditions must enable microbial attachment and biofilm forma-
tion on the plastic.
• During the degradation, microbial activity must not be prevented by the struc-
ture (chemical bonds, degree of polymerization, degree of branching) or properties
(hydrophobicity, crystallinity) of the plastic.
Enzymes are proteins, produced by bacteria, fungi, or actinomycetes, consisting of long
linear amino acid chains, with the ability of catalyzing a specific reaction or a number
of reactions, including oxidation, reduction, and hydrolysis (Madigan et al., 2015; Sa-
dava et al., 2012; Singh and Sharma, 2008). Enzyme catalyzed reactions thus proceeded
toward an equilibrium at a faster rate than uncatalyzed reactions (Singh and Sharma,
2008). Enzymes are generally larger than their targeted substrate, however, only two
to four of its amino acids are involved in the catalyzing process (Gurung et al., 2013).
Enzymes are sensitive to their surrounding environment and can be affected by factors
such as pH and temperature.
Enzymes can be classified into different Enzyme Commission (EC) numbers based on
their catalyzing properties (Table B.1 in Appendix B). Hydrolytic enzymes, such as hy-
drolase, are widely used in industries for degradation purposes, because of their ability
of catalyzing the breakdown of molecules (Gurung et al., 2013). Hydrolase includes en-
zymes such as amylases, cellulases, lipases, mannanases, pectinases, phytases, proteases,
pullulanases and xylanases (Gurung et al., 2013).
Polymer side chain- and backbone cleavage can be achieved through enzymatic hydrol-
ysis, leading to the formation of short polymer chains or smaller polymer units of low
molecular weight such as oligomers, dimers and monomers (Wagner and Lambert, 2017).
These smaller polymer units are water-soluble, which allows them to cross the semi-
permeable outer membranes of the microorganisms, where they can be utilized as carbon
and energy source, and finally fully degraded (mineralized) (Alshehrei, 2017; Gu, 2003).
During mineralization plastic materials are transformed into inorganic compounds such
as H2O, CO2, and CH4 gases (Alshehrei, 2017).

2 Theoretical background
The biodegradability of plastics can be determined by measuring the production of CO2
within a defined period. Single polymer plastics are considered biodegradable if 60 % car-
bon is converted into CO2 within 180 days (World Centric, 2018). Plastics with polymer
mixes, however, need to convert 90 % carbon into CO2 within 180 days to be considered
biodegradable (World Centric, 2018). Bio-degradation of plastics infrequently achieves
100 % degradation, because a small fraction of the plastic will be combined with micro-
bial biomass (Gu, 2003).
Bio-plastics are, compared to synthetic plastics, more convenient to undergo microbial
degradation and enzymatic hydrolysis (Pathak et al., 2017). Synthetic plastics usually
prevent microorganisms from transporting the plastic from the outer cell membranes
into the cell. Plastics such as PE, PP, and PS are hydrophobic polymers and thus not
containing any hydrolysable bonds. These plastics are therefore expected to be more
stable and therefore less prone to hydrolysis (Singh and Sharma, 2008; Zheng et al.,
2005). The bio-degradation of synthetic plastics is not unfeasible. However, synthetic
plastic usually undergo degradation through photodegradation and thermo-oxidization
(Pathak et al., 2017). POs are generally known as non-biodegradable because of three
essential reasons: 1) their hydrophobic character resulting in resistance to hydrolysis 2)
the addition of additives during manufacturing 3) their high molecular weight. To make
POs available for bio-degradation their molecular weight and mechanical properties need
to be modified (Albertsson et al., 1994).
2.2.2.1
Bio-degradation under anaerobic and aerobic conditions
The bio-degradation process of polymers depends on the availability of O2 (Gu, 2003).
When the bio-degradation occurs in the presence of O2, aerobic microorganisms are
responsible for the plastic degradation.
The end-products of an aerobic plastic bio-
degradation will include microbial biomass, CO2, and H2O (Figure 2.6, Equation 2.1)
(Pathak et al., 2017).

H2O, CO2, and CH4 gases are produced, or under sulfidogenic conditions where H2O,
CO2, and hydrogen sulfide (H2S) are produced (Figure 2.6, Equation 2.2) (Pathak et al.,

2.2.3
Factors affecting plastic degradation
The degradation of plastics can be affected by several factors including: chemical com-
position, chemical bonding, molecular weight, molecule size, hydrophilicity, polymer
additives, polymer stress, and environmental conditions (Emadian et al., 2017; Wagner
and Lambert, 2017). The following sections provide a description of the different factors
affecting plastic degradation.
2.2.3.1
Chemical composition
The chemical composition of a plastic is an important factor in regard to its degrada-
tion abilities. Thermoplastic POs solely consisting of long carbon chains are resistant
to bio-degradation. Thermal degradation and bio-degradation can be achieved, by in-
cluding hetero-groups such as O2 in the plastic (Singh and Sharma, 2008).
Plastics
with complex structures such as high crystallinity have more tightly structured polymer
chains, which affect the microorganisms’ ability to attach to the plastic, and can prevent
particle degradation (Wagner and Lambert, 2017). Plastics such as natural rubber, PS,
and polycarbonate (PC) are, based on their low crystallinity, more appropriate for bio-
degradation than plastics such as HDPE, cellulose acetate (CA), and PVC (Table B.2
in Appendix B).
2.2.3.2
Chemical bonding
Polymer linkage based on branching increases the rate of thermal degradation, while
crosslinking decreases the rate of photodegradation (Singh and Sharma, 2008).
==PAGE:38==
18
2 Theoretical background
2.2.3.3
Molecular weight
Studies have shown that plastics with low molecular weights have a faster degrada-
tion rate compared to plastics with high molecular weights (Singh and Sharma, 2008;
Yamada-Onodera et al., 2001; Santos et al., 2000). Microbial degradation of plastics can
be increased by the reduction of their molecular weight. Studies indicate that microor-
ganisms favor a molecular weight less than 620, however, less than 500 for POs, in order
for bio-degradation to occur (Pathak et al., 2017; Bonhomme et al., 2003). Reduction
of molecular weight can be achieved by pre-treatment with photooxidation, hydrolysis,
and chemical oxidation (Wagner and Lambert, 2017).
2.2.3.4
Molecule size
The molecule size in plastic is a factor that affects mechanical degradation, thermal
degradation, and bio-degradation. Faster degradation rates are achieved for plastics of
small molecule sizes (Singh and Sharma, 2008). The degradation of a plastic can thus
increase by a decrease of its molecule size.
2.2.3.5
Hydrophilicity
Synthetic plastics are highly resistant to hydrolysis due to their hydrophobic nature
that prevents microorganisms from attaching to their surface (Wagner and Lambert,
2017; Yamada-Onodera et al., 2001). Hydrophobic plastics are difficultly degraded in
the environment, and it is therefore beneficial to perform a combined abiotic and biotic
degradation on such plastics. The biodegradability can be increased through chemical
and physical pre-treatment including UV irradiation, thermal treatment and oxidation
with nitric acid (Pathak et al., 2017). The pre-treatments will result in the formation of
carbonyl groups with increased surface hydrophilicity that can be utilized by microor-
ganisms (Hadad et al., 2005).
2.2.3.6
Polymer additives
Additives such as antioxidants and stabilizers prevent the degradation of plastics. By us-
ing pro-oxidant additives, it is possible to make plastics hydrophilic, and thus biodegrad-
able (Singh and Sharma, 2008). It is possible to increase the biodegradability of plastics
by blending it with starch or other polyesters during production (Singh and Sharma,
2008).
2.2.3.7
Polymer stress
Photodegradation is increased by tensile stress, whereas compressive stress has a decreas-
ing effect on photodegradation (Singh and Sharma, 2008). Thermo-oxidative degrada-
tion occurs at a faster rate for plastics in a state of stress compared to unstressed plastics

2.3 Anaerobic digestion
19
(Singh and Sharma, 2008). A stressed plastic will be more likely to crack, while its ac-
tivation energy will decrease (Singh and Sharma, 2008).
2.2.3.8
Environmental conditions
Bio-degradation of plastics depend on the surrounding environmental conditions such as
moisture, temperature, O2, and microbial population (Singh and Sharma, 2008; Orhan
et al., 2004).
Microbial degradation increases in climates with a relative humidity above 70 % (Singh
and Sharma, 2008). Moisture benefits photodegradation, as it causes removal of solu-
ble photo-stabilizers from the plastic, making the stabilizers less effective (Singh and
Sharma, 2008). At high temperatures, photodegradation can be accelerated even by
small increases of UV radiation (Singh and Sharma, 2008; Rabinovitch et al., 1983).
Degradation as a result of weathering, depends on both temperature and humidity. The
degradation occurs at a faster rate under conditions of high temperatures and wet cli-
mates (Singh and Sharma, 2008).
Oxygen plays a significant role in the degradation of plastics. Generally, aerobic bio-
degradation is more efficient compared to anaerobic bio-degradation because aerobic
processes produce more energy. Aerobic conditions furthermore, support more microor-
ganisms because O2 is a better electron acceptor than sulfate (SO4
2–) and CO2 (Gu,
2003).
2.3
Anaerobic digestion
AD is a biological conversion process without an external electron acceptor, such as O2,
nitrate (NO3
–), or SO4
2– (Christensen, 2011). During AD processes, two main outputs
are produced: biogas and digestate. Biogas is an energy-rich gas, mainly consisting
of CH4 and CO2, which can be utilized directly for the production of electricity and
district heating or conversion to fuel (Christensen, 2011). The CH4 and CO2 ratio in
biogas depends on the composition of the applied substrate. Biogas substrates consisting
of fats yields a biogas composition with a high CH4 content, whereas substrates based
on hydrocarbons will produce a biogas with an equal distribution of CH4 and CO2. The
composition of the substrate, furthermore, influences the amount of produced biogas
as well as the biogas production rate. Usually biogas consists of 55-65 % CH4 and 35-
45 % CO2 (Angelidaki et al., 2011). Ammonia (NH3), H2S, and a number of volatile
organic compounds only make up less than 1 % of the biogas (Angelidaki et al., 2011).
The digestate is a nutrient-rich substance, which can be of either solid or liquid form
depending on the AD process. A liquid digestate, also called sludge, generally consists
of 4-10 % total solids (TS). The composition of the produced digestate depends on the
composition of the treated waste during the AD. The digestate can either be disposed

at landfills or further utilized. The utilization of sludge can include processes such as
energy recovery through incineration or use on land as a substitute for mineral fertilizers.

2.3.1.1
Hydrolysis
Hydrolysis is the first step of AD in which solubilization of solids occur.
Organic
compounds are, during hydrolysis, broken down into smaller molecular units such as
monomers and oligomers. The process is carried out by extracellular enzymes catalyz-
ing the molecule cleavage by the utilization of H2O (Angelidaki et al., 2009; Novaes,
1986). During hydrolysis simple sugars, amino acids, and long-chain fatty acids (LCFAs)
are produced (Christensen, 2011). Carbohydrates, proteins, and lipids are among the
main compounds appropriate for enzymatic hydrolysis. Carbohydrates are hydrolyzed to

monosaccharides by cellulase, proteins to amino acids by protease, and lipids to LCFA)
and glycerol by lipase (Gerardi, 2003). Other organic compounds such as lignin, keratin,
plastic, wax, and minerals are due to their complex structures generally known to be
resistant to enzymatic attachment and hydrolysis (Christensen, 2011).
2.3.1.2
Fermentation
In the fermentation process sugars and amino acids produced during hydrolysis are con-
verted into volatile fatty acids (VFA), alcohols, H2, and CO2 by fermantative acidogenic
bacteria (Christensen, 2011). Fermentation is a rapid and energy rich process includ-
ing several pathways, intermediate, and end-products. The process can operate under
different conditions of pH and oxidation/reduction potential. During the fermentation
process of sugars, glucose is used as an electron acceptor being able to convert both re-
duced and oxidized products. Fermentation of sugars can occur in one of two pathways
resulting in the production of either: 1) propionate through pyruvate in a net electron
consuming reaction, or 2) a number of products such as acetate, butyrate, and ethanol
through acetyl-coenzyme (CoA) in a net electron producing reaction (Christensen, 2011).
The fermentation process of amino acids involve a coupled oxidation/reduction reaction
known as the Stickland reaction (Angelidaki et al., 2011). The Stickland reaction causes
one amino acid to be oxidized while reducing another amino acid.
2.3.1.3
Acetogenesis
Acetogenesis can be defined as the synthesis of acetate in which acetate is formed by the
reduction of CO2. In this step the VFA and alcohol produced during fermentation are
oxidized to acetate (Christensen, 2011). This process is carried out by obligate hydrogen
producing acetogenic bacteria using acetyl-CoA for the synthesis pathway and CO2 as
electron acceptor (Angelidaki et al., 2011).
2.3.1.4
Methanogenesis
Methanogenic microorganisms belong to the Archaea domain and are responsible for
the final step in AD where CH4 is produced. The CH4 production is carried out by
two different pathways: hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis and aceticlastic methanogen-
esis. Hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis converts H2 and CO2 into CH4, and aceticlas-
tic methanogenesis converts acetate, produced during acetogenesis, into CH4 and CO2
(Christensen, 2011). Hydrogenotrophic methanogensis takes place in close coexistence
with acetogenesis and typically accounts for 30–40 % of the CH4 (Equation 2.3). Aceti-
clastic methanogenesis typically generates 60–70 % of the CH4 (Equation 2.4).

2.3.3
Bio-methane potential test
When determining the biogas potential of a specific substrate a BMP test can be carried
out. The BMP test is an experimental batch method where the CH4 potential of a given
substrate is measured. Methane is the dominant energy carrier in biogas, making it the
main interest when quantifying the potential yield from AD. The BMP test can be used
to observe if the plastic is being degraded, as the production of gas is a result of or-
ganic degradation (Christensen, 2011). The substrate is mixed with a diluted anaerobic
bacteria culture known as inoculum. It is important to select a substrate concentration
that, on one hand, allows sample sizes providing good representability as well as high
gas production, and, on the other hand, does not require impractical rector and gas
volumes, or causes inhibition from VFA and ammonia (Hansen et al., 2004). In case
of observed inhibition new measurements with lower substrate concentration should be
carried out (Hansen et al., 2004). The inoculum used for a BMP test is usually sampled
from a reactor of a working biogas plant. Under optimal conditions, the inoculum should
always be kept at a stable temperature. The optimum temperature is reached at 37◦C

for mesophilic inocula and 55◦C for thermophilic inocula (Hansen et al., 2004). The final
amount of produced CH4 can be quantified by accumulating the CH4 production as a
function of time. The final produced CH4, or maximum, can be determined when the
accumulated gas production stabilizes, thus reaching its plateau. In most cases 80-90 %
of the CH4 potential is generated within the first 8-10 days of incubation (Hansen et al.,
2004). However, as some waste types might have a slow degradation rate, the BMP test
should progress for a total of 50 days (Hansen et al., 2004).
2.3.3.1
Measured methane potential
The theoretical CH4 potential is usually difficult to determine, and it is therefore favor-
able to measure the CH4 potential in the laboratory instead. Three different methods
can be applied for measuring the CH4 potential of a substrate: volumetric, manometric,
or gas chromatography (GC). Measurement with a GC provides an analytical method,
and was therefore chosen for the experimental phase of this study. The GC method,
moreover, provides quantitative results, while being less time consuming than other
methods. The method is also considered the most accurate for biogas determination
(Kolb and Ettre, 2006). GC measurements can be carried out by two different instru-
ments: a thermal conductivity detector (TCD), measuring the composition of the biogas,
and a flame ionization detector (FID), only measuring the amount of CH4 in the biogas.
By the means of a thermal conductivity detector, the TCD instrument measures the dif-
ferent gasses in the biogas by analyzing the changes in the thermal conductivity (Kolb
and Ettre, 2006). The FID instrument is equipped with a flame ionization detector that
measures the CH4 concentration by detecting formed ions during combustion of organic
matter (Kolb and Ettre, 2006).
2.3.3.2
Practical methane potential
The theoretical and measured CH4 potentials give an indication of how much biogas one
can expect from a given substrate. The theoretical and measured CH4 potentials are,
however, not reachable in practice due to factors such as (Hansen et al., 2004):
• 5-10 % of the substrate is used to synthesize bacterial mass.
• 5-10 % of the organic matter will be lost in the effluent.
• Lignin is not degraded under anaerobic conditions.
• Some of the organic matter will be unattainable, because of binding of particles or
structural organic matter.
Conversions up to 85-95 % can be achieved if the substrate mainly consists of water
soluble matter (Christensen, 2011). If the substrate, however, is highly particulate or
structural, only 30-60 % conversion is expected (Christensen, 2011).

2.4
Microplastic identification
Sludge and digestate produced during AD of biological matter are often utilized as
a substitute to synthetic fertilizers.
In case of plastic materials present in the AD
feedstock as a result of e.g. incorrect source segregation, microplastics could potentially
be present in the produced sludge. The accumulation of microplastics in the ecosystem
is an environmental concern and it is thus essential to examine the potential presence of
microplastics in the sludge. In this study, the sludge produced during the BMP tests was
investigated. This section describes different technologies for microplastic identification.
2.4.1
Sample purification
To decrease the risk of misidentification and to improve the detection of microplastics,
it is necessary to carry out pre-treatment of a sample before initiating the identification
process (Wagner and Lambert, 2017). Through pre-treatment samples are purified by
the removal of organic materials. Sample purification thus allows handling of larger
sample volumes resulting in higher result representatives and minimization of misiden-
tification. In the following section, a number of different pre-treatment methods will be
described.
2.4.1.1
Chemical degradation
Sample purification through chemical degradation can be achieved with chemicals such
as H2O2 or alkaline and acid solvents, such as nitric acid (HNO3) (Erni-Cassola et al.,
2017; Wagner and Lambert, 2017; Kim et al., 2009). Removal of organic matter can, fur-
thermore, be achieved by a combination of chemical and enzymatic degradation (Löder
et al., 2017).
Alkaline and acid hydrolysis are effective methods for the degradation of organic matter,
however, these methods have been found damaging to microplastics originating from
PE, PVC, and nylon (Cole et al., 2014).
Removal of organic matter with HNO3 is
known to be efficient. The application of HNO3 for purification of plastic containing
samples is, however, limited, due to its detrimental effect on pH-sensitive polymers such
as PS and nylon (Erni-Cassola et al., 2017).
To prevent plastic destruction during
sample purification it was decided not to apply HNO3 for the sample purification in
this study. An alternative to HNO3 is treatment with H2O2, which common synthetic
plastics have shown resistance toward (Erni-Cassola et al., 2017). Pre-treatment with
H2O2 has previously shown promising results by degrading organic compounds into CO2,
H2O, and other intermediate organics through oxidation (Kim et al., 2009). Chemical
degradation can if necessary be followed by a filtration process, where organic matter is
removed and plastic particles are maintained (Nuelle et al., 2014).
==PAGE:45==
2.4 Microplastic identification

2.4.1.2
Physical separation
Sample purification through physical separation can be carried out with density separa-
tion, filtration, and sieving methods.
Density separation can be used for the segregation of light plastic particles from heavier
organic materials by mixing the sample with a saturated solution followed by a mixing
period, after which the organic matter will settle to the bottom while plastic particles
will remain in the solution or rise to the surface (Hidalgo-Ruz et al., 2012). The plastic
containing supernatant is usually extracted for further processing e.g. with filtration.
Sodium chloride (NaCl) has shown to be effective for the separation of plastic particles
from PE and PP in sludge samples (Mintenig et al., 2017). However, Mintenig et al.
(2017) acknowledges that the method fails to detect higher density polymers such as
PVC.
Filtration can be used to separate microplastics from their liquid solution.
This is
achieved by passing the sample through a filter, usually equipped with a vacuum pump,
resulting in the removal of organic matter and conservation of microplastics (Nuelle
et al., 2014; Hidalgo-Ruz et al., 2012). Before the filtration larger plastic particles can
be removed using a sieve with a 500 µm mesh size (Andrady, 2011). Filter separation is
rarely used as a single treatment due to the occurrence of clogging as a result of incorrect
filter pore size. This method is therefore generally used in combination with chemical
degradation.

2.4.2.1
Microscopy
The simplest microscopy method is visual examination under a light microscope follow-
ing criteria such as shape and colour to avoid misidentification and underestimation of
microplastics (Hidalgo-Ruz et al., 2012). Another microscopy method is the Hot Needle
Test, which is carried out by poking potential plastic substances with a hot needle un-
der a light microscope. If the substance is plastic, it will melt or curl, however, if the
substance is of organic matter or of other means non-plastic, it will either burn or not
be affected (De Witte et al., 2014). Identification based on microscopy can be difficult,
if many fragments are closely located to each other, which have been proved to cause
both false positive and false negative interpretation, causing errors of up to 70 % (Song
et al., 2015; Rocha-Santos and Duarte, 2015). Microscopy based methods are, however,
widely used due to their low cost and simple execution. To verify results achieved from
microscopy methods, chemical identification has to be carried out (Rocha-Santos and
Duarte, 2015).

2.4.2.2
Spectroscopy
Detection of microplastics with spectroscopy includes methods such as FTIR spec-
troscopy and Raman spectroscopy (Wagner and Lambert, 2017).
FTIR and Raman
spectroscopes are based on energy absorption of functional groups of plastic polymers.
Raman spectroscopy, however, applies smaller wavelengths for the excitation than the
FTIR spectroscopy, and can therefore detect microplastic substances of smaller particle
size (Wagner and Lambert, 2017; Song et al., 2015). FTIR and Raman spectroscopes
are both reliable methods for microplastic identification. However, they are expensive
and time consuming. Consequently, alternative methods of low-tech character might be
favourable for the identification of microplastics.
2.4.2.3
Selective fluorescent staining
The method of selective fluorescent staining can be carried out with dyes such as Nile
Red and Rose-Bengal.
Nile Red is a lipophilic dye that stains plastic particles, making them detectable when
using a confocal microscope. Studies show that the method provides better particle
identification when using green-yellow fluorescence (excitation 450-490 nm and emission
551-565 nm) rather than red or blue fluorescence (Erni-Cassola et al., 2017; Shim et al.,
2016). Fluorescent staining with Nile Red is an inexpensive high-throughput sample
analysis, which can be carried out with readily accessible equipment. Erni-Cassola et al.
(2017) demonstrated that the method effectively detects small microplastics (20 µm to 1
mm) from hydrophobic plastics such as PE, PP, PS, and nylon. However, Erni-Cassola
et al. (2017) address method limitations when investigating less hydrophobic polymers
such as PC, PUR, PET, and PVC. During plastic identification with fluorescent mi-
croscopy the investigated sample is located on a filter e.g. Polycarbonate Track Etch
(PCTE). PCTE filters prevent Nile Red background fluorescence due to the filters hy-
drophilic surface (Erni-Cassola et al., 2017). Furthermore, PCTE filters are translucent
when exposed to methanol allowing the use of bright-field microscopy as well as fluores-
cence microscopy (Erni-Cassola et al., 2017). If microplastics are present in the sample
they will absorb the Nile Red dye, thus being detectable with a fluorescent microscopy
(Maes et al., 2017). Visual quantification of plastic particles can be carried out directly
under the microscope or by the implementation of a macro allowing for automation of
particle counting (Erni-Cassola et al., 2017). To prevent false positive results it is im-
portant that the investigated samples are purified before staining with Nile Red.
Opposite to Nile Red, Rose-Bengal dye only stains natural and non-synthetic materials
leaving plastic particles unstained (Ziajahromi et al., 2017).
Plastic and non-plastic
particles can thus be visually separated, however, detection of transparent plastics is
challenging using this method. Furthermore, studies using Rose-Bengal have reported
incidents with false negative and false positive results (Lachenmeier et al., 2015).

3.2
UV irradiation
This section describes the UV irradiation that was carried out in Chromatography Lab
in Building 113, Room 131, at DTU Environment. UV irradiation was carried out on
P1, P2, and P3. The aim of the UV irradiation was to increase the bio-degradation of
the plastics during the BMP test. P4 was not included in the UV pre-treatment, as this
plastic type was not chosen to be investigated at this early stage of the project.
3.2.1
Experimental set-up
Plastic pellets (P1 and P2) were put in a beaker with a diameter of 8 cm, and placed
in a UV box containing two 9 Watt PL-S UV-C light bulbs. The plastic film (P3) was
cut into a sheet of 30x30 cm, and placed in the UV box with four 9 Watt PL-S UV-C
light bulbs. The plastic material was placed with a distance of 5 cm from the light
bulbs and exposed to UV-C light with a wavelength ranging from 290 nm to 100 nm for
116 hours. The applied wavelength and exposure time was based on Qin et al. (2003)
where an exposure time of 40-200 hours and a wavelength of approximately 300 nm is
recommended for PE destruction. These conditions were also used for the UV-treatment
of PCL, as there was not found any information on UV-treatment of this plastic type.
The inside of the UV box was covered with aluminum foil as an attempt to obtain
reflection of light, thus increasing the plastic’s exposure to UV light (Figure 3.3). The
temperature within the UV box was 30◦C. The plastic was weighed before and after 116
hours of exposure to detect any weight loss. The exposure time was chosen based on
literature findings recommending an exposure time of 40-200 hours (Qin et al., 2003).

3.3
Bio-methane potential test
This section gives a detailed description of the three BMP tests, which were carried out
in respect to the anaerobic plastic degradation. The execution of the BMP tests followed
the method described in Angelidaki et al. (2009) and Hansen et al. (2004).
3.3.1
Inoculum
The inoculum used for the three BMP tests was collected from a pilot scale biogas reactor
located at BIOFOS Avedøre wastewater treatment plant.
The reactor was operated
under stable conditions with an active volume of 500 L and a hydraulic retention time of
20 days. The reactor was furthermore operated under mesophilic conditions (37◦C). The
biogas reactor was fed with biopulp consisting of source-segregated municipal organic
waste that had been pre-treated with a pulper. A total volume of 15 L inoculum was
collected 10 days before starting the experiments to allow degassing of the inoculum, thus
ensuring a low CH4 production from the organic matter within the inoculum during the
BMP tests.
3.3.2
Microbial culture
Based on literature research it was found that specific bacteria, including B. circulans,
B. licheniformis, and bacteria within the gut of G. mellonella wax worms can be utilized
for biological plastic degradation. The BMP test was therefore also carried out with the
use of microbial cultures of B. circulans, B. licheniformis, and G. mellonella, both with
and without the presence of inoculum. A mix of the three cultures was not investigated.
3.3.2.1
Bacillus circulans and Bacillus licheniformis
The microbial cultivation of B. circulans and B. licheniformis was carried out in Microbi-
ology Lab in Building 115, Room 227, at DTU Environment. The cultivation was carried
out under sterile conditions in a Laminar Air Flow (LAF) cabinet, for one microorgan-
ism at the time. The microorganisms were purchased from Leibniz Institute DSMZ -
German Collection of Microorganisms and Cell Cultures GmbH. Each microorganism
was chosen due to their ability of producing enzymes that, based on literature, would be
able to catalyze the plastic degradation process (Table 3.1). Both microorganisms were
tested on P1, P2, and P3.
To cultivate the purchased bacteria, a culture media that suited the cultivation condi-
tions of the bacteria was prepared. Both selected bacteria originate from the B. genus
and required the same conditions. Based on information provided by DSMZ (2007) the
media was prepared in 1000 mL distilled water containing 5.00 g peptone (manufactured

Galleria mellonella wax worms

Substrate concentration
Cellulose ((C6H10O5)n) was used as substrate for the positive control samples, holding
a theoretical methane yield of 50 % (Table D.1 in Appendix D) (Christensen, 2011).
Bottles containing virgin LDPE, bio-LDPE, or LDPE had an OLR of 5.0 g VS/L. PCL
was tested with three different concentrations: 1.0, 2.5, and 5.0 g VS/L. The added
substrates were weighed on an analytical scale with a precision of 0.1 mg.
3.3.4
Controls and blanks
Controls, containing inoculum and cellulose, were included to account for quality of
the inoculum. Blank samples only containing inoculum were included to account for the
produced CH4 from the organic matter of the inoculum (Christensen, 2011). The average
of the blanks was subtracted from the average of the remaining samples to determine the
detection limit. The BMP test included blank samples to measure the CH4 production
originating from the inoculum and the grown microbial culture.
3.3.5
Experimental set-up
The experiment was carried out with 105 test bottles, all of which had been acid washed
before starting the experiment. To allow statistical interpretation of the experimental
results, the BMP test was carried out as a triplicate batch experiment. The batch ex-
periment was carried out in 321 mL bottles, with an active volume (AV) of 100 mL, and
a headspace of 221 mL. The bottles were filled with inoculum, water, grown microbial
culture, G. mellonella wax worms, and plastic material in different concentrations and
combinations (Figure 3.4, Table 3.2, Table 3.3, and Table 3.4).
The inoculum used for the experiment was thoroughly stirred and flushed with N2 gas
to avoid sedimentation, as this could have a negative effect on the CH4 production. Due
to its heterogeneous characteristics, the inoculum was transferred to the test bottles in

Monitoring
A GC (Trace 1310, Thermo Scientific) was used to measure the content of CH4 and
CO2 of the produced biogas present in the headspace of the sealed bottles. The CH4
content was measured continuously 11 times for sample no. 1-66 and sample no. 67-84,
10 times for sample no. 85-105 during the incubation period of 73, 70, and 67 days,
respectively. Measurements were taken approximately twice a week for the two first
weeks, after which measurements were taken approximately once a week due to a more
==PAGE:61==

41
stable gas production.
Each day before starting the CH4 measurement of the samples a calibration curve was
conducted. The standard curve was created using four different standard gases with
known CH4 concentrations (5, 40, 60, and 100 %) and based on triplicates. The stan-
dard gasses were collected and maintained in foil gas sampling bags. Before making the
calibration curve each foil bag, containing the standard gases, were released and refilled
with fresh standard gas. This procedure was repeated twice before the bags were ready
to be used for the calibration. The CH4 concentration of the measured samples was
estimated based on the calibration curve. To maintain samples in mesophilic conditions,
they were kept in the incubator during the execution of the calibration curve, as this
procedure lasted approximately one hour.
The CH4 measurements were sampled with a Myjector U-100 insulin syringe equipped
with a pressure lock (Figure 3.7). Before taking a sample, the syringe was unlocked
and flushed with air 10 times and locked again. The rubber septum was penetrated
with the syringe, after which the syringe was unlocked and flushed with the gas of the
headspace 10 times, to ensure that the gas sample was collected at the actual pressure
of the headspace. A fixed volume of 0.2 mL gas was collected in the syringe, after which
the syringe was locked and redrawn from the bottle and injected to the septum of the
GC. In a rapid, however steady, movement the syringe was unlocked, gas was injected
to the GC, and the analysis was started.
To avoid sedimentation of inoculum each
bottle was shaken before a measurement was taken. During the CH4 measurements the
bottles were kept in an insulation box to maintain mesophilic conditions. To minimize
the period of the bottles kept outside the mesophilic incubator, only 15 samples were
transferred to the insulation box to be measured at a time. The measurement of 15
samples lasted approximately two hours if no bottles had to be degassed. Depending on
necessity gas release, the measurements could last up to four hours in case all 15 bottles
had to be degassed.

3.3.7
Data treatment
The statistical analysis of the BMP tests was carried out based on triplicate samples
using Microsoft Excel 2016. Outliers were excluded based on a criteria requiring a dif-
ference between the average and a sample value <1 x the standard deviation. The CH4
content in each bottle was calculated based on the measured CH4 from the fixed sample
volume, and knowing the volume of the headspace (Hansen et al., 2004). The calculated
CH4 content was hereafter translated into accumulated CH4 as a function of time. The
results from the GC analyses were given as a methane mass percentage. The data was
treated in multiple steps corrugating for the CH4 production from blanks, standard tem-
perature, and water vapor. When treating the data the pressure was not corrugated for
as it was assumed that the measurements were carried under standard pressure (1 atm).
The calculated CH4 production was based on the assumption that the temperature of
the samples, when measuring the CH4 concentration, were 10◦C below the incubation
temperature (Equation 3.5). As a results of a reduced temperature, water vapour is
affected leading to a lower partial volume, which may cause an overestimation of CH4 of
2-8 % (Walker et al., 2009). An overestimation of 4 % was assumed in the calculations.
The final BMP results were expressed in NmL CH4/g VS by adjusting for the OLR
(Equation 3.6).
For the samples without inoculum and thus no addition of methanogenic bacteria, it was
not possible to measure the degradation of plastics based on the methane production as
no or very little methane would be produced in these samples. VFAs are during degrada-
tion transformed into acetate, hydrogen, and carbon dioxide. For samples without added
inoculum, the production of CO2 during the BMP test was therefore investigated.

of the microbial communities present during BMP test, and more specific microbial
communities responsible for the plastic degradation. This section describes the sample
preparation of the two selected triplicate samples (sample no. 79-81: LDPE film with
inoculum and G. mellonella wax worm; sample no. 82-84: LDPE film with inoculum).
The prepared samples were shipped to Australia for analysis. The results, however, did

and it has therefore not been possible to make an interpretation of the results.
3.4.1.1
Sample preparation
At the final stage of the BMP test 1.5 mL of each of the six samples was collected in
2 mL eppendorf tubes and centrifuged for 5 minutes at 13,000 g. The supernatant was
discarded and the remaining pellet was weighted. The experimental execution of the
DNA extraction followed a protocol created by Senior Researcher at DTU Environment
Laura Treu (Appendix E). The extracted DNA samples were kept on ice.
The DNA concentration of each sample was determined by measuring 2.5 µL DNA on
a nanodrop (NanoDrop 2000 Spectrophotometer, Thermo Scientific). From the DNA
concentration, it was possible to calculate the DNA volume corresponding to 300 ng
DNA, which was needed to carry out the 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing. The calcu-
lated volume was transferred to new sterile eppendorf tubes and kept on ice.
To allow samples to be shipped without being kept on ice it was necessary to freeze dry
the prepared DNA samples. Each sample, still kept on ice, was enclosed with parafilm,
transferred to a rack, and placed in a -50◦C freezer in Demonstration Lab in Building
114, Room 025B for one hour. This was done to ensure that the samples were com-
pletely frozen when carrying out the freeze-drying. While the samples were still kept in
the freezer, a sterile syringe was used to poke holes in the parafilm of each sample to
allow evaporation of water during the freeze-drying. The applied freeze dryer (ScanVac
CoolSafe Freeze Dryer) had a working temperature of -101◦C and a pressure of 0.013
mbar. The ScanVac CoolSafe Freeze Dryer was opened and the samples were placed
inside. When opening the freeze dryer the temperature within the machine increased as
a result of increased pressure. It was important that samples did not melt as a result of
the high pressure, and could be detected by bubbling or movement of samples, in which

3.4.2.1.1
Sample purification
The sample purification was carried out on 11 test bottles. The sample purification was
carried out with filters of 1000 and 500 µm. The purification was a simple treatment
were each bottle was opened and purred through a 1000 µm filter to a 250 mL conical

500 µm filter, following the same procedure as before.
Figure 3.6: Sludge filtration of samples for microplastic identification.
3.4.2.1.2
Nile Red staining
The fluorescence of Nile Red is affected by its concentration, and must therefore be
dissolved to obtain the optimal concentration of 0.1 to 2 µg/mL (Erni-Cassola et al.,
2017). For this purpose methanol was applied due to plastics’ resistance to this substance
(Erni-Cassola et al., 2017). The Nile Red solution was prepared with a concentration
of 1 µg/mL. 500 µL of each sample was transferred to sterile eppendorf tubes, after
which 2 µL of the prepared Nile Red solution was added to each sample tube. Each
tube was thoroughly vortexed and hereafter incubated for 15 minutes in the dark. After
the incubation, 500 µL of each solution was transferred to clean PCTE filter membranes
connected to a vacuum pump. The PCTE filters had a diameter of 25 mm and a pore
size of 10 µm. The vacuum pump was turned on, and the sample liquid was filter out.
The filters were now placed on standard microscope slides, each supplied with 20 µL
distilled water. One drop of immersion oil was added on top of each filter, after which
the microscope slides were covered with a cover glass. An additional drop of immersion
oil was added on top of each cover glass. The samples were here after retained in the
dark for 10 minutes at 60◦C. To prevent laboratory contamination all microscopy slides
had been washed with acetone, and other glassware and filtering equipment had been
cleansed with Mili-Q water before use.

3.5
Bio-degradation with Galleria mellonella
wax worms under aerobic conditions
This section describes the attempt of bio-degradation of the plastic with G. mellonella
wax worms. Approximately 300 pieces of G. mellonella wax worms were purchased from
the Danish online company, Creeps4you ApS, and received in sturdy plastic tubs con-
taining sawdust. Each plastic tub contained approximately 50 worms. G. mellonella
wax worms develop into wax moths after a month if retained at room temperature, the
development can, however, be kept on hold if stored in cold environments such as re-
frigerators (Creeps4you Aps, 2018). The worms can be maintained fresh for up to four
months under cold conditions (Creeps4you Aps, 2018). For this experiment the G. mel-
lonella wax worms were stored in a refrigerator of 4◦C in Biotechnology Lab in Building
113, Room 037, at DTU Environment, for three weeks before the experiment was started.
Three different set-ups were investigated using plastic type P3 and P4 (Table 3.6 and
Table 3.7). Before starting the experiment, plastic, G. mellonella wax worms, and wax
worm diet were weighted. The experiment was carried out in glass beakers and kept in
aerobic conditions at room temperature (Figure 3.9). The experiment was carried out
as a triplicate batch experiment. Two different approaches were examined; one with G.
mellonella wax worms placed on top of a 5x5 cm plastic film, and another with G. mel-
lonella wax worms placed inside a tied 10x10 cm sheet of plastic film. Each experiment
was carried out during a period of 48 hours. The detection of plastic degradation was
visually carried out by investigating if the plastic film had been penetrated during the
experimental phase. In the second approach it was assumed that plastic degradation
could be observed if the G. mellonella wax worms could eat themselves through the
plastic film. The plastic film was furthermore weighted to investigate potential weight
loss as a result of plastic degradation during the experiment.

4.6
Bio-degradation with Galleria mellonella
wax worms under aerobic conditions
Bio-degradation with living G. mellonella wax worms under aerobic conditions did not
succeed. Different experimental set-ups were tested, however, none of them resulted in a
degradation of the tested plastic materials (PCL film and LDPE film). G. mellonella wax
worms have in recent studies been discovered to have LDPE degrading abilities under
aerobic conditions (Bombelli et al., 2017). The BMP results from samples containing G.
mellonella wax worms resulted in production of CH4 in samples with LDPE film, and
it is therefore presumed that it is possible to degrade plastic with these worms. Factors
such as age of the worms, surrounding environment, and quantity of worms used for the
experiment has to be further investigated to improve the experiment. When considering
the results gained from the experiments under both aerobic and anaerobic conditions,
it is presumed that plastic degradation with G. mellonella wax worms to a greater
extent is facilitated by the microbial culture within the wax worms rather than a result
of the worms eating the plastic. This presumption advocates further investigations of
the microbial culture within the G. mellonella wax worm to in order to identify the
microorganisms responsible for the achieved plastic degradation.
4.7
General discussion
To manage the issue of plastic particles present in the sludge produced during biogas
production, it is necessary to investigate the behavior of plastic materials under different
environmental conditions as well as the consequences of plastic materials present in the
AD feedstock. When reflecting on the achieved results during this master thesis the
following aspects has been considered.
During BMP test, it was seen that UV-treated PCL film was associated with higher
biogas than plastics that had not been exposed to UV radiation, thus increasing the
plastics’ ability to undergo bio-degradation. UV-treated LDPE pellets, however, did
not gain abilities of microbial attachment, thus unable to produce biogas. The results
thus indicate that UV-treated PCL is better suited for bio-degradation during biogas
production than UV-treated LDPE plastics. However, it should be considered if UV-
treatment of plastics before AD is feasible in real life biogas production. It is difficult
to imagine how this would be managed and if it would provide any benefits. If only the
plastic materials should be exposed to UV radiation, it would be necessary to separate
all plastic impurities, not necessarily consisting of solely plastic films. It would therefore
not be possible only to carry out a plastic screening equal to the one that is carried
out for the collection bags for organic household waste. However, if this was possible
it should be investigated if it would be environmentally reasonable to UV-treat plastic

4.7 General discussion
71
impurities to subsequently include them in the AD feedstock. Other waste management
alternatives such as utilizing the plastic for energy recovery through incineration at a
waste-to-energy (WTE) plant should then be considered, as plastics holds high calorific
values, thus yielding a high energy recovery. If plastic separation from the AD feedstock
is feasible in a real life scenario, plastics would be absent in the AD feedstock, thus pre-
venting the production of microplastics in the produced sludge during biogas production.
In a scenario where it is not possible to separate plastics from the AD feedstock, but
UV pre-treatment is desired, UV irradiation should be carried out on the total quantity
of the AD feedstock. Such pre-treatment would, however, entail that the organic food
waste also would undergo degradation as a result of UV irradiation. Consequently, less
organic material will enter the biogas reactor, ultimately leading to a decreased biogas
production. For the investigation of plastic degradation it is therefore crucial to keep in
mind that it in this case does not only concern the waste management of plastic waste,
but specifically plastic materials as an impurity in AD feedstocks and biogas production.
This ultimately means that it is not enough to find a solution for plastic degradation
or even bio-degradation if the solution does not comply the processes within biogas pro-
duction.
The ability of plastics undergoing bio-degradation might depend on not only the plastic
type, but the form of the plastic material as well. Plastic film holds a larger surface area
than plastic pellets. A larger surface area will provide a larger area for UV exposure and
for microbial attachment, ultimately being more prone to degradation. It is assumed
that the majority of the plastic materials potentially ending in the AD feedstock are
plastics that have been in contact with food waste. Such plastics could originate from
e.g. freezer bags, bags for organic household waste, and food packaging. These plastic
types are all expected to hold a larger surface area than the investigated plastic pellets
for this study.
It is important to investigate methods that provides a total degradation of plastics
during biogas production to prevent presence of microplastics in the produced sludge.
During the experimental phase of this study it was not managed to develop a method
that provided plastic degradation without the production of microplastics.
Without
methods providing total degradation, it cannot be guaranteed that the produced sludge
will not contain microplastics, and can therefore be of environmental concern if utilized
as fertilizer on agricultural land.
It is therefore necessary to further investigate the
subject of plastic degradation under anaerobic conditions, to find a solution to prevent
microplastics to enter the ecosystems and potentially be threatening to human health.
Based on the BMP test UV-treated PCL with inoculum and protease producing B.
licheniformis gave the most promising result. This finding is in accordance with the
literature where Oda et al. (2000) demonstrated PCL degradation with protease produc-
ing bacteria from Bacillus species in aerobic conditions. B. circulans which in literature
was found to have LDPE degrading abilities under aerobic conditions (Pathak et al.,

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
The main objective of this study was to investigate the possibility of plastic degra-
dation under anaerobic conditions, to allow plastic degradation during AD in Danish
biogas plants. The degradation of four different plastics was hence investigated based
on laboratory batch experiments: pellets of virgin LDPE (P1), pellets of bio-LDPE
(P2), biodegradable PCL film from organic household waste collection bags (P3), and
LDPE film originating from freezer bags (P4). The chosen polymers - LDPE and PCL
- were identified as plastics potentially ending in the AD feedstock in Danish biogas
plants as a result of incorrect source segregation of municipal household waste or ineffi-
cient screening of collection bags for organic household waste prior to biogas production.
Two different microorganisms, B. circulans and B. licheniformis, as well as the microbial
culture within G. mellonella wax worms were used to investigate the degradation of P1,
P2, P3, and P4 through three different BMP tests under mesophilic conditions and an
incubation period of approximately 73 days. The BMP test bottles furthermore included
inoculum from a pilot scale biogas reactor including a biopulp of municipal household
waste. The BMP test was used as a tool to identify plastic degradation as a result of
CH4 production. The investigated plastics were tested with different combinations of
the different substrates, to determine which combination of microbial culture and plastic
that allowed the highest CH4 yield. As an attempt to increase the biodegradability of
the plastics during the BMP test, several batches were tested with UV-irradiated plastic
material.
Based on the results gained from the BMP test the combination of UV-treated PCL
with inoculum (sample no. 58-60) contributed with the highest CH4 yield of 1156 NmL
CH4/g VS. This result was however not considered to be trustworthy as sample no- 58-
60 produced 164 % more CH4 compared to its control containing inoculum and cellulose
(sample no. 10-12). To allow further interpretation of this result a reconstruction of the
experiment should be carried out. Due to uncertainties regarding the result of sample
no. 58-60 the obtained CH4 yield was disregarded.
UV-treated PCL with inoculum and B. licheniformis (sample no. 46-48), producing
the enzyme alkaline protease, had a final CH4 yield of 545 NmL CH4/g VS, which was
73 % more than what was achieved with UV-treated PCL with inoculum and B. cir-
culans (sample no. 49-51). This result complies with finding in literature where PCL
was degraded by protease produced by the B. species (Oda et al., 2000). UV-treated
PCL with inoculum and B. licheniformis produced seven times more CH4 compared to

as a substitution to mineral fertilizer on agricultural land will not contain remains of
plastic particles.

CHAPTER 6
Future Recommendations
Based on the knowledge attained throughout this study a list of practical recommen-
dations for further research has been conceived. The recommendations are given with
the ambition of improving future laboratory tests by constructing experimental set-ups
with a closer proximity to real life conditions.
• Investigation of incubation period
The BMP tests carried out in this study had an incubation period of approximately
70 days. However, Danish biogas plants only operate with a hydraulic retention
time of approximately 20 days, and methods for plastic bio-degradation should
therefore be tested with a corresponding incubation period. Furthermore, to obtain
a greater understanding of the microbial behavior when interacting with plastic
materials it is recommended to carry out BMP tests of different incubation periods.
• Investigation of mixed plastic materials
The experimental set-up of the BMP test should include test bottles containing
different plastic materials. In a real life scenario, more than one plastic type would
be present in the AD feedstock, and it is therefore important to identify microbial
cultures with the ability of degrading a mix of different plastic types.
• Investigation of used plastic materials
The experimental set-up of the BMP test should include test bottles with plastic
waste materials. In a real life scenario the plastic present in the AD feedstock
would not be intact plastic or virgin plastic as applied for the experiments of
this study, but used plastics discarded as waste. It should be investigated if it is
easier for used plastics to undergo degradation, as it can be presumed that used
plastics have been exposed to stress during their lifetime. Experiments with used
plastics would, however, make samples more heterogeneous and therefore more
difficult to characterize. It is therefore important to be careful when designing the
experimental set-up and interpreting the result.
• Investigation of plastic surface area
Plastic materials of the same type but with different surface areas should be tested
to investigate if the difference in surface area has an effect on degradation of plastic
materials.
• Improvement of environmental conditions
It is recommended to investigate possible improvements of the environmental con-
dition during microbial cultivation to increase the microbial density of the plastic

degrading microorganisms. It should furthermore be investigated how the envi-
ronmental conditions could be improved during AD to achieve maximum plastic
degradation.
• Identifying microorganisms responsible for plastic degradation
The BMP test showed that the microbial culture within G. mellonella wax worms
had plastic degrading abilities. The responsible microorganisms, however, have
not been identified. During this study samples for 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing
was prepared. For further research, results from the 16S rRNA amplicon should
be analyzed and further investigations with the microorganisms responsible for the
observed plastic degradation should be carried out.
• Degradation detection based on advanced technology
For this study, the degradation detection was based on low-tech methods such
as weight loss measurements. To increase knowledge on how plastics are affected
during plastic degradation more advanced examinations should be carried out e.g.
with FT-IR spectroscopy.
During this study, it was not possible to identify methods for plastic degradation during
AD without the production of microplastics. A final recommendation is therefore given
to investigate other waste management possibilities for plastic materials present in AD
feedstocks.
• Separation of plastic materials from AD feedstocks
It is recommended to investigate the possibilities of separating plastic materials
from AD feedstocks to prevent plastics to enter the AD process leaving traces of
microplastics in the produced sludge. Management options for the plastic waste
may include energy recovery or plastic recycling.

General information about anaerobes 
 
In the broadest sense obligate anaerobes can be defined as microorganisms which are unable to utilize molecular 
oxygen for growth. A further differentiation is possible based on their relationship to the presence of oxygen. 
Aerotolerant anaerobes are only slightly inhibited by significant levels of oxygen in the atmosphere. For instance 
Clostridium intestinale DSM 6191T can grow well on the surface of agar plates incubated in air at atmospheric 
pressure.  
The other extreme is represented by strict anaerobes, which die, or immediately stop growing, upon exposure to 
low levels of oxygen. It is therefore important to retain anoxic conditions during all steps of handling of these 
microorganisms. Most strict anaerobes require not only the absence of oxygen to initiate growth, but also a redox 
potential below -300 mV, which can be only achieved by the supplementation of media with reducing agents (see 
section on Reducing agents and resazurin). Between both extremes all kinds of adaptation exist. 
The majority of anaerobic microorganisms are fastidious and require complex media with many supplements.  
In the DSMZ catalogue of strains (https://www.dsmz.de/catalogues/catalogue-microorganisms.html) each DSM 
strain is linked with a specific medium number. It is strongly recommended to use the respective media formulations 
given for each strain, because only those media were tested at the DSMZ for culturing and a transfer to alternative 
media may cause a delay or complete failure of growth.

Before ordering an anaerobe from the DSMZ it is advisable to have a look on the recipe of the medium necessary for

It only makes sense to purchase a strain of interest, if you are convinced to be able to handle it properly! 
A large number of strict anaerobes are available from the DSMZ only as actively growing cultures. We recommend

Anaerobic strains that are available from the DSMZ as lyophilized cultures are normally not sensitive to a short

Recommended vials for culturing strict anaerobes 
 
Suitable containers for pre-reduced media are an important prerequisite for the successful culturing of strict 
anaerobes. For this purpose special glassware has been developed which enables the easy use of completely gas-
tight closures. Of crucial importance is the material of the rubber stoppers. Only stoppers or septa made of butyl 
rubber can efficiently prevent permeation of air into the vial. Nevertheless, a repeated puncturing of stoppers with 
injection needles could make them become permeable to oxygen. As a rule, the thicker the stopper the more often it 
is possible to reuse it without loss of impermeability. 
Two types of vials are commercially available for anaerobic culturing (Fig. 1):  
The Hungate-type tubes are closed with a flange-type butyl rubber septum and a screw cap with 9 mm opening to 
allow puncturing of the septum with injection needles. 
Balch-type tubes are more stable than Hungate-type tubes and recommended if an overpressure of 2 to 3 bar can be 
expected during culturing. They are closed with a thick butyl rubber stopper which is hold in place by sealing with an 
aluminium crimp. For sealing and removing of the aluminium crimp special devices (crimper/decapper) are 
necessary.

Pre-reduced media can be stored in both types of vials at room temperature in the dark for several weeks without

Gassing of media and cultures with oxygen-free gas 
 
When vials of pre-reduced media or anaerobic cultures are opened a constant flow of oxygen-free gas over the 
surface of the medium is necessary to avoid exposure to oxygen. The used oxygen-free gas should have the same 
composition as that used for medium preparation. We recommend to use oxygen-free gasses of high purity 
(containing less than 5 ppm oxygen), that are delivered as compressed gas cylinders. Normally, oxygen-free gasses 
of high quality do not require an additional oxygen removal system (e. g., heated copper column) and can be used 
directly for culturing a broad spectrum of anaerobes. Suppliers of compressed gasses are for instance Messer 
Griesheim GmbH (http://messergroup.com) or Linde AG (http://www.linde.com).

Special Instructions

The Hungate technique is based on the use of gassing cannulas. Usually, several cannulas are connected by butyl 
rubber tubing (e.g., Iso-Versenic) to a manifold supplying oxygen-free gas with an overpressure that should be 
adjusted to approx. 0.5 bars. At least two cannulas are needed: one for the vessel to be inoculated or filled with 
medium and one for the vessel containing the inoculum or the medium to be dispensed. When an aseptic gassing of 
media or cultures is necessary a barrel of a glass syringe is packed with cotton and fitted between the gassing needle 
and the butyl rubber tubing (Fig. 2A)

After assembly, autoclave the cotton-filled glass syringe and needle, dry in a drying oven at 100°C, allow to cool, and 
connect to the manifold. Prior to the first use flush the gassing cannula for approx. 15 min with oxygen-free gas to 
make it anoxic and then flame the needle to sterilize it.  
Caution: Make sure that needles sterilized by flaming are cooled down prior to using gas mixtures containing H2. 
Hydrogen gas is highly combustible and even only contact with hot surfaces may cause ignition. Wear protective 
goggles while overpressurizing vials. 
For the overpressurizing of cultures with H2 or H2/CO2 gas mixtures use disposable, sterile injection needles (i. d. 0.4 
mm or 27G) connected to cotton-filled glass syringes as described above. To keep the pressure within the glass 
syringe barrel at a constant level during overpressurizing it is necessary to avoid an imbalance between the inflowing 
gas stream and the outflow. This can be achieved by puncturing the rubber stopper of the cotton-filled syringe with a 
steel needle (approx. 20G) which is connected to the rubber tubing by an appropriate valve with Luer-Lock fittings. 
Adjust the gas pressure to the desired value (in most cases 0.5 to 2 bar overpressure). Turn the vial with the culture 
upside down and puncture the sterilized septum with the injection needle (Fig 2B). A sputtering of gas bubbles 
indicates the inflow of gas into the medium and can be observed as soon as the tip of the cannula enters the liquid. 
When the flow of bubbles slows down the pressure within the vial reaches equilibrium with the external pressure of 
the gas supply. Withdraw gassing needle immediately when the gas flow stops.

Preparation of anoxic media buffered with bicarbonate 
 
Anaerobic microorganisms growing at a circumneutral pH usually require a buffered medium that keeps the pH at a 
suitable value. Otherwise fermentation products like butyrate could lead to an acidification of the medium, while for 
example ammonification or sulfate-reduction would lead to an alkalinisation. 
 
Media prepared under 100% N2 gas atmosphere often contain a biological buffer, e.g. HEPES or MOPS, to keep the 
pH in a suitable range. However, more often a HCO3
-/CO2 buffer is used, because CO2 is required by many anaerobic 
microorganisms for growth. Therefore, if the medium recipe in our catalogue indicates the use of an 80% N2 and 20% 
CO2 or 80% H2 and 20% CO2 gas atmosphere, it cannot simply be replaced with a medium containing a biological 
buffer that has been prepared under 100% N2 or 100% H2 gas atmosphere. 
 
In principal two options exist for preparing bicarbonate buffered anoxic media. Sometimes, it is indicated to add 
solid sodium bicarbonate or sodium carbonate to the anoxic medium prior to autoclaving. In this case you have to 
check the pH of the medium after the solid bicarbonate or carbonate has been completely dissolved. If the medium 
pH is above the desired value you have to continue sparging the medium with the CO2 containing gas mixture until 
the correct pH value is reached. 
In most cases it is however more favourable to add bicarbonate or carbonate to the autoclaved medium from sterile 
anoxic stock solutions prepared under 80% N2 and 20% CO2 gas mixture. The amount of bicarbonate or carbonate 
stock solution that has to be added to the medium depends on several factors, like for example the ratio of liquid 
medium to gas phase in the cultivation vessel, so that the amounts given in our medium recipes are only rough 
estimates. However, usually mineral media prepared under 80% N2 (H2) and 20% CO2 gas atmosphere are buffered 
with 30 - 40 mM NaHCO3 or 15 – 20 mM Na2CO3 to reach a pH around 7.0. In order to determine the exact amount 
of NaHCO3 or Na2CO3 stock solution that is required for a distinct pH value, we recommend using one vial with the 
completed sterile medium and adding small aliquots of the stock solution (e.g., 0.1 ml of a 5% (w/v) Na2CO3 stock 
solution per 10 ml medium. Thereafter, check the resulting pH value by removing a small volume of medium while 
retaining anoxic conditions. These steps have to be repeated until the desired pH has been reached. The determined 
amount of stock solution can then be used for all other vials with the same volume of medium. 
 
 
Reducing agents and resazurin 
 
A redox sensitive dye is usually included in media used for culturing anaerobes to monitor the redox potential. The 
most commonly used redox indicator is resazurin, because it is generally non-toxic to microorganisms and is effective 
at very low concentrations of 0.5 to 1 mg/l. This indicator dye is dark blue in its inactive form and first has to undergo 
an irreversible reduction step to resorufin, which is pink at pH values near neutrality (the colour may change to blue 
under alkaline conditions). This first reduction step normally occurs when media containing an excess of organic 
nutrients are boiled for a few minutes or mineral media are heated under an oxygen-free atmosphere. 
In a second reversible reduction step hydroresorufin is formed which is colourless. The resorufin/hydroresorufin 
redox couple becomes totally colourless below a redox potential of about -110 mV and regains a pink colour at a 
redox potential above -51 mV. 
 
Please note, that some organisms require redox potentials lower than -110 mV and hence may not start to grow 
even if the medium is colourless. On the other hand, a pink colour of the medium does not automatically imply that 
it became oxidized by oxygen (e.g., through a gas permeable rubber septum). For instance, certain nitrate reducers 
produce nitrite during growth which acts as potent oxidant and so may raise the redox potential above -51 mV. 
==PAGE:126==
Special Instructions 
 
 
              © Leibniz-Institut DSMZ-Deutsche Sammlung von Mikroorganismen und Zellkulturen GmbH

Reducing agents are added to most anaerobic media to depress and poise the redox potential at optimum levels. The 
most common reducing agents are sodium thioglycolate, L-cysteine-HCl x H2O, Na2S x 9 H2O, FeS (amorphous, 
hydrated), dithiothreitol and sodium dithionite. 
Sodium thioglycolate is often used in combination with ascorbate and mainly incorporated in some traditional media 
for culturing anaerobes (e.g., Postgate's media for sulfate reducers, DSMZ medium 63). Thioglycolate as reducing 
agent has the advantage that it is relatively stable at room temperature and can be therefore included in media prior 
to flushing with oxygen-free gas. It is only activated by heating above 100°C and then efficiently removes oxygen. 
Hence, not so much care has to be taken in avoiding exposure to oxygen of the prepared medium prior to dispensing 
in anoxic vials. For this reason many commercially available media contain thioglycolate as reducing agent. However, 
the standard redox potential of thioglycolate alone (around -100 mV) is generally not low enough to allow initiation 
of growth of a majority of strict anaerobes which need highly reduced media.  
For maximum effectiveness of reducing agents other than thioglycolate, stock solutions under nitrogen gas should be 
prepared. While preparing stock solutions of reducing agents avoid insertion of the gassing cannula into the liquid, 
because this can have negative effects on the reducing capacity. Sodium dithionite, which reacts extremely fast with 
oxygen, has to be dissolved in oxygen-free water and sterilized by filtration. Freshly prepared stock solutions of 
dithionite can be stored only for up 2-3 weeks at room temperature in the dark. 
Add appropriate concentrations of reducing agents to the autoclaved medium just prior to use and allow the 
medium to sit until it becomes colourless (incubation at 37°C may accelerate this process). If the medium stays pink 
despite addition of reducing agent exchange the septum of the vial under a flow of oxygen-free gas, because it might 
have become permeable to oxygen. Finally, add a small amount of dithionite for final adjustment of the redox 
potential to a value below -300 mV. If this does not help discard the medium tube. 
 
 
Handling of vacuum-dried anaerobic cultures 
 
The DSMZ delivers lyophilized (freeze-dried) cultures of anaerobic strains exclusively in double-vial preparations, 
heat-sealed under vacuum. Double-vial preparations have the advantage that a contamination of the atmosphere by 
aerosols that can be produced by sudden release of the vacuum in single-vial preparations is efficiently prevented. In 
addition, the cell pellet is protected from contamination, because inflowing air filters through the sterile cotton plug 
of the inner vial. Before opening of the ampoule please identify the culture by the label on the inner vial which 
indicates the DSM strain number and date of preservation. Confirm that the ampoule is under vacuum by checking 
the colour of the desiccant at the bottom of the outer vial. 
 
Note: The DSMZ has changed the indicator stain of the desiccant. From January 2002 on the used desiccant is red, 
instead of blue. The indicator stain will change its colour if the outer vial is damaged due to an increase of 
humidity inside the ampoule. The red colour changes to orange and the blue to pink. 
 
It is important to retain anoxic conditions during all steps after the opening of ampoules with freeze-dried 
anaerobes. This can be achieved in several ways depending on the used anaerobic technique and available 
equipment in the laboratory. For general information about vacuum dried cultures please visit the following site 
Handling of cultures and opening of ampoules with freeze-dried material. 
The freeze-dried pellet of most anaerobic strains available from the DSMZ is protected against short exposure to 
oxygen by amorphous ferrous sulfide (FeS) which confers a black colour to the pellet. However, certain nonstringent 
or spore forming anaerobes are suspended prior to lyophilisation in skim milk without addition of FeS, so that the 
ampoules display a white pellet.  
If the Hungate technique is routinely used in the laboratory, open the ampoules as described in Handling of cultures 
and opening of ampoules with freeze-dried material.

Handling of actively growing anaerobic cultures 
 
For the aseptical injection and removal of samples from anaerobic cultures it is recommended to use the Hungate 
technique which is essentially based on the use of disposable syringes and has the advantage that it allows the use 
of defined, oxygen-free atmospheres for culturing. The anoxic removal of a sample from a Hungate tube is 
demonstrated in Fig 3. 
First, sterilize the butyl rubber septum by flaming it using a drop of ethanol which has been placed on the septum. If 
overpressure in the vial can be expected due to microbial growth (e.g., gas production by fermentation) remove 
excess gas by puncturing the septum with a sterile injection needle. Then a sterile, disposable 1 ml syringe with a 
25G to 23G hypodermic needle (i. d. 0.50 to 0.65 mm) is made anoxic by displacing the dead space with sterile 
oxygen-free gas or a reducing agent.
