CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Figure 1.1: Resulting 6D pose estimation of cluttered screws in one scene
Bin picking, at it’s core, involves the identification, selection, and retrieval of
objects from a bin or a container by an autonomous system. This task has been
a topic of research and development since the introduction of the first popularized
industrial robots.
Despite significant advancements, it remains one of the most
challenging tasks in industrial automation and persists to be a famous challenge in
academic research.
To understand the complexity, consider how a person retrieves an item from a
bin. First, our stereo vision allows us to perceive depth and compute the geometry of
the scene. We recognize patterns and identify objects using a vast database of known
items stored in our memory. By computing depth from the disparity between our
two eyes, we accurately judge distances. Next, we plan our movement by letting the
brain coordinate the actuation of necessary muscles to such positions that, together,
they lead our arm to the desired location. We decide where to grab the item based
on its pose, using our sense of touch, to grasp the object securely. Throughout this
process, we avoid collisions and ensure we don’t harm ourselves or others, thanks to
our sophisticated planning algorithms. Remarkably, we perform all these complex
computations and actions almost effortlessly, highlighting the incredible capabilities
of the human body and mind.
Replicating this in robotic systems is a monumental challenge.
There can be a multitude of options for how a bin picking system can look. In
our setup, we imagine a six degree-of-freedom robotic arm in an industrial plant
(Fig. 1.2), operating nonstop with minimal operator interference. A container full

Simply knowing the 2D location of an object (as in picking items from a flat

Bin picking is a complex task spanning multiple disciplines, and we can divide the
problem into stages. The perception stage detects and determines the positions
of randomly piled, cluttered objects inside a container. Following this, planning
determines the optimal strategy for grasping the identified components.
Finally,

This cycle is continuously repeated, allowing the system to adapt to changes in the
environment and maintain eﬀicient operation in the dynamic industrial setting.

Perception
In bin picking, perception is the first step, responsible
for localizing parts within the bin. Before any manip-
ulation can occur, the system must establish a known
relationship (a transformation) between the robot and
each target object, defining its spatial coordinates and
orientation relative to the robot (Figure 1.2).
This
transformation is fundamental for all subsequent plan-
ning and manipulation.
To accurately represent an object’s pose in 3D space,
we use six degrees of freedom (6DOF). These six param-
eters define the object’s position and orientation within
a specific coordinate system, typically the robot’s base
frame or the camera’s frame.
Three degrees of free-
dom describe the object’s position through transla-
tional movement along the X, Y, and Z axes. The re-
maining three degrees describe its orientation through
rotations around these axes, commonly represented as
roll, pitch, and yaw (Figure 1.3).
The 6D pose pro-
vides a complete and unambiguous description of the
object’s location and orientation, enabling the robot to
perform precise manipulations.
Figure 1.3: 6 Degrees of
Freedom in 3D space
For a perception systems, industrial environments present a complex landscape
of occlusions, reflections, and inter-object relationships that must be accurately in-
terpreted. As parts pile upon each other in bins, they create arrangements in which
partial visibility becomes the norm rather than the exception. This scenario is fur-
ther complicated by the diversity of industrial parts, where the perception system
must be capable of localizing and interpreting components with a wide range of
surface properties.
These components can vary from simple flat sheets of metal to complex geome-
tries found in engine parts, such as turbine blades and gear assemblies. To effectively
handle this variability, the perception system, along with its associated computer vi-
sion algorithms, must accurately analyze and interpret these different shapes and
surfaces under varying lighting conditions.
The complexity further adds up when considering the dynamic nature of bin pick-
ing operations. As parts are removed, the remaining objects may shift, creating an
ever-changing scene that the system must continuously reassess. Such environmen-
tal variations demand sensing and algorithmic strategies that can adapt to changing
conditions while maintaining reliable performance.
In this work, 6D pose estimation will be the primary focus of the overall system,
as without vision, both humans and robots are effectively blind.

Planning
Following object localization by the perception system, the planning phase deter-
mines how to effectively grasp and manipulate the identified parts. This involves
deciding which object to pick, the optimal grasping pose, and the approach trajec-
tory to avoid collisions within the cluttered bin environment. The planning process
is required for ensuring eﬀicient and safe robotic operation.
The effectiveness of the planning phase is directly dependent on the accuracy
of the perception system. The vision system’s interpretation of an object’s position
and orientation dictates the ability to determine a suitable grasping pose. Inaccurate
object localization or misinterpretation of geometry by the perception system can
lead to ineffective or damaging manipulations.
Several factors influence the object selection process, including object accessibil-
ity, its position relative to other parts, and task-specific operational priorities. For
instance, in densely packed scenarios, the system must evaluate which object can
be extracted with minimal disruption to surrounding items. Once an object is se-
lected, the system determines the optimal grasping pose and calculates the approach
trajectory to minimize collisions with adjacent parts or the bin itself.
Planning
algorithms must consider the robot’s kinematics and reachability to ensure path
feasibility. Furthermore, the dynamic nature of the bin environment necessitates
real-time adjustments to the planned approach as parts are removed and the config-
uration of remaining objects changes. Variations in force and torque due to these
shifts further complicate planning, requiring continuous adaptation of the robot’s
strategy and movement.

Manipulation
The manipulation phase is necessary for executing the planned actions and success-
fully interacting with the identified parts. Kinematics of the robot allows the system
to translate desired end-effector (gripper) positions into joint movements. Forward
kinematics calculates the position and orientation of the end effector based on given
joint angles, while inverse kinematics determines the necessary joint angles to achieve
a specified end-effector position.
The choice of gripper plays a significant role in the manipulation process too.
Traditional rigid grippers may not be suitable for all parts, particularly those with
varying shapes and sizes. Therefore, adaptable grippers, which can be 3D printed to
match the specific geometries of the components being picked, offer a flexible solution.
These custom grippers can conform to the contours of different parts, enhancing the
robot’s ability to securely grasp items while minimizing the risk of damage.
Optimization of movements is another aspect of manipulation. The system must
eﬀiciently execute the planned actions while minimizing cycle times and energy con-
sumption. This involves control algorithms that coordinate the robot’s movements,
ensuring that each action is performed smoothly and effectively. If a part falls or
a pick-up attempt fails, the system must have predefined protocols to manage such
situations, which may include reattempting the grasp or adjusting the approach an-
gle. This capability is dependent on the sensors integrated into the gripper or on
the perception system. In this manner, the robot can react proficiently and reduce
the necessity for manual intervention. Then, in cases where parts are picked but
from a pose that deviates from the optimal configuration, the system may execute
alignment movements or reposition the component correctly.
Although some tasks may require complex research and development, many of
these functionalities are either already integrated into the robotic system or can be
addressed through straightforward integration tasks. Additionally, certain aspects
may not need to be implemented at all to ensure the system’s robustness and oper-
ational speed. And again, if reliable perception is not achieved, the effectiveness of
the entire manipulation process diminishes, making it the primary focus of ongoing
efforts.

1.2
Bin Picking Perception Challenges
While marketing materials often present bin picking as a solved challenge, robust
and generalizable solutions remain diﬀicult to achieve due to several key perception-
related issues.
A primary challenge is the occlusion of objects. It makes it diﬀicult for sensors
to capture complete views of individual objects, restricting accurate object detection
and subsequent pose estimation. The problem is compounded by object variabil-
ity. Parts can vary in shape, size, material properties, and pose. Differences in
reflectivity can then alter how objects appear to sensors, particularly depth cam-
eras. In RGB images, high reflectivity can appear as overexposed, unusable pixels,
while in depth images, it can lead to spurious depth readings, placing the reflected
point at an entirely incorrect distance. This variability makes it diﬀicult to develop
algorithms that generalize well across different object types.
Then, lighting conditions in industrial environments are often uncontrolled and
can vary significantly. This, too, can affect sensor readings and object appearance,
further complicating the perception task. The need for real-time performance
in many industrial applications adds another layer of complexity. Systems must be
able to process sensor data quickly and reliably to meet production demands. Finally,
the current trend in research favors deep learning architectures, which often require
dedicated and clustered GPUs. This creates a disconnect with industry’s desire
for compact, self-contained ”black box” solutions, a goal that is currently diﬀicult to
achieve with the computational demands of deep learning solutions.
These combined challenges explain the diﬀiculty in creating truly general-purpose
bin picking solutions and justify the ongoing research in this area.

thesis, discusses the achieved results in the context of the initial goals, and
reflects on the methodology and its limitations.
Let us reiterate our goals from Section 1. This thesis addresses the challenges
of bin picking in cluttered environments with featureless, textureless, and highly
reflective objects, with an overall focus on deployment in the challenging industrial
settings:
• Robustness: Developing perception algorithms that are resilient to variations
in lighting, object pose, and sensor noise.
• Precision: Commissioning a system that is able to achieve the spatial accuracy

• Repeatability:
Ensuring consistent performance in multiple selection at-

• Speed: Achieving fast object localization and pose estimation for eﬀicient

• Handling Challenging Object Properties: Developing methods capable

different object types without requiring extensive retraining or manual adjust-

CHAPTER 2
State-of-the-Art
As outlined in Chapter 1, a fully capable bin picking system requires robust percep-
tion, planning, and manipulation capabilities. While bin picking presents challenges
across all these areas, this work focuses specifically on the perception aspects. Key
challenges in computer vision relevant to bin picking, such as occlusions, cost, com-
putational demands, reflections, and the handling of novel objects, were identified
in the previous chapter, along with an initial overview of existing technologies. The
main goal is to develop a more accessible solution while maintaining state-of-the-art
performance.
In order to select appropriate methods, sensors, hardware, and define relevant
constraints, an investigation of the current state-of-the-art is required. This chapter
presents a brief survey of both early academic and industrial bin picking perception
methods, as well as recent advancements utilizing deep learning architectures.

Numerous influential feature detection algorithms followed.
The Sobel opera-

and Hildreth [17] introduced the theoretical framework of edge detection through
zero-crossings of the Laplacian of Gaussian. This culminated in Canny’s paper [18]
that introduced optimal edge detection with hysteresis thresholding, becoming the
standard in industrial machine vision applications of the late 1980s.
The challenge of determining object pose from these 2D features led to theoret-
ical developments in projective geometry and camera calibration. Work by Fischler
and Bolles [19] introduced the well-known Random Sample Consensus (RANSAC)

algorithm, a robust model fitting algorithm for data containing outliers. RANSAC
iteratively selects random subsets of data points, fits a model to these subsets, and
evaluates the model’s support within the entire dataset.
This process helps identify the model that best fits the inliers while rejecting
outliers. This robust fitting capability proved particularly useful in computer vision
for tasks such as pose estimation and feature matching. Lowe [20] developed meth-

matching edge features to object models (see Fig. 2.2). These approaches, particu-

solutions through the 1980s.
However, these model-based approaches required significant manual effort to cre-
ate accurate object models, and their performance was highly dependent on the
quality of the model. For instance, creating a robust model for even a relatively
simple object like a razor required intensive work, and there was no guarantee that
the resulting method would generalize well to other object types.

2.2
Geometric Registration Approaches
While edge detection and 2D feature matching dominated the 1980s, the limitations
of purely appearance-based methods became increasingly apparent in industrial ap-
plications. A key drawback was the lack of explicit depth information, which made it
diﬀicult to reason about the 3D structure of scenes and objects. For example, similar
2D features could arise from objects at different depths, leading to ambiguities in
pose estimation and object recognition.
The emergence of cost-effective laser range finders in the early 1990s represented
an advancement in direct 3D sensing technology. This new technology provided di-
rect measurements of depth, opening up new possibilities for 3D scene understanding.
The Iterative Closest Point (ICP) algorithm, introduced by Besl and McKay [21] and
further explored by Chen et al. [22], provides a method for aligning 3D point clouds,
laying the groundwork for geometric registration approaches that would become es-
sential to several modern methods in bin picking.

Point clouds, a direct output of some 3D sensors, have become a widely used
representation of spatial data. They are defined as a collection of data points in a
three-dimensional coordinate system, where each point is represented by its (x, y, z)
coordinates. The ability to directly capture 3D information with point clouds enabled
new approaches to object recognition, pose estimation, and scene understanding that
were previously impossible with only 2D image data.
However, the transition to 3D data also presented new challenges. While pro-
viding direct depth information, 3D sensing introduced issues such as combinatorial
explosions in matching algorithms, limitations in hardware processing capabilities,
sensor noise in three-dimensional space, and the presence of reflections, particularly
with shiny metallic objects common in industrial settings, which introduce spatial
outliers. These challenges obstructed the widespread adoption of 3D vision systems
for many years. It took decades of research and technological development until
cost-effective and commercially viable solutions, such as the Microsoft Kinect and
Intel RealSense sensors, became available, finally making 3D vision more accessible

The origins of a full 3D object localization for robotics can be traced back to the
work of Faugeras and Hebert [24] in 1986. They explored paradigms and processing
approaches for industrial object localization in 3D space, emphasizing the necessity
for algorithms that could generalize across various sensing modalities, such as laser
range finders, ultrasound, and stereo systems. This insight remains highly relevant
in today’s technological landscape.
In 1992, Besl and McKay [21] advanced the field with a more generalized ap-
proach to 6DoF pose estimation, utilizing the ICP on scanned objects, particularly
human faces. Their work introduced notable improvements also applicable to in-
dustrial contexts and acknowledged the drawbacks of existing geometric registration
methods. They highlighted the susceptibility of these methods to outliers, a common
issue in noisy sensor data, and the need to subdivide pose estimation into smaller,
more manageable sub-problems to improve convergence. Furthermore, they pointed
out that mathematical limitations in the ICP itself, such as sensitivity to initial pose
estimates and the possibility of converging to local minima, could prevent conver-
gence even in the absence of sensor noise for certain object geometries. This raised
the possibility that either significantly better analytical methods would need to be
developed to overcome these constraints, or that a truly generalized bin picking
solution based solely on geometric registration might not be feasible at all.
From Single Challenge to Complex Field
The above-described early contributions illustrate a growing concern: what began
as a singular challenge has evolved into a major field encompassing numerous sub-
problems. This complexity complicates the tracking of which methods are employed,
when, how, and for what specific goals. Hoover et al. [25] effectively captured this di-
vergence in their examination of segmentation methods. They identified a wide array
of algorithms, including iterative region growing [26], edge-based methods [27], se-
quential estimators [28], Bayesian approaches [29], clustering and Hough transforms
[30], diffusion and morphology techniques [31], and the early use of perceptrons [32]
in this domain [33]. Hoover et al. acknowledged the importance of performance eval-
uation in the field, taking the initiative to develop ground truths for their dataset,
set baseline metrics, and assess over a dozen different methods. This effort motivated
further improvements in algorithms through competitive evaluation.
The evolution of 3D reconstruction techniques continued into the 2000s, marked
by several developments.
Curless and Levoy [34] pioneered volumetric scene re-
construction methods, enabling more accurate 3D modeling from multiple images.
Zhang et al. [35] enhanced these capabilities by developing algorithms that incorpo-
rated color information to refine depth acquisition, thereby increasing the versatility
of systems in complex environments. Koninckx and Van Gool [36] further advanced
the field by introducing adaptive structured light techniques, which improved ro-
bustness under varying lighting conditions. Subsequently, Winkelbach et al. [37]
contributed to the field with a cost-effective laser range scanner that enhanced sur-

Further in the 2010s, several approaches continued to refine analytical methods
for bin picking. Buchholz et al. [46] developed the RANSAM algorithm for robust

as sensitivity to noise, occlusions, and the requirement for accurate object models,
continued to slow down the development of a truly generalized bin picking solution.
Summary
A key challenge in bin picking has been the development of a truly general solution
using purely analytical methods. These approaches, relying on predefined geometric
models and rigid algorithms, face several limitations. First, they are highly sensitive
to variations in the environment. Changes in lighting, shadows, and reflections can
significantly alter object appearance, leading to recognition failures. Second, many
analytical methods rely on iterative processes that are sensitive to initial conditions.
Partial occlusions or poor lighting can prevent convergence, resulting in inaccurate
or erroneous results. Third, these methods often lack scalability. Adapting to new
object types or even minor modifications to existing objects often requires extensive
recalibration or redesign.
Therefore, while analytical methods have provided valuable foundational knowl-
edge for bin picking, their rigidity, sensitivity to environmental changes, convergence
issues, scalability challenges, and lack of adaptability have held back the development
of a general solution. This motivates the exploration of more flexible, learning-based
approaches, which offer the potential to overcome these shortcomings.

Deep Learning Techniques
The limitations of analytical methods in addressing the complexities of real-world bin
picking scenarios, as discussed in the previous section, motivate the exploration of
more adaptable approaches. Deep learning techniques, in contrast to the rigidity of
analytical methods that rely on precise object models and controlled environments,
offer a dynamic and data-driven alternative.
Neural networks, the fundamental
building blocks of deep learning, possess the ability to learn complex patterns and
generalize from vast amounts of data, enabling them to handle the variability in bin
picking, such as varying object shapes, lighting conditions, and occlusions[49, 50].
Neural networks are composed of interconnected layers of nodes, or neurons,
where each connection is associated with a weight that adjusts dynamically during
the learning process. This structure allows neural networks to process complex in-
puts, making them particularly effective for tasks such as image recognition and
robotic applications.

(compared to CNNs) for image recognition, paving the way for their application in
more complex vision tasks, including 6D pose estimation. These transformers are
adapted from natural language processing to image recognition by dividing images
into patches and treating them as a sequence of ”words,” enabling the model to
capture long-range dependencies and global context. .

Many 6D pose estimation methods rely on prior object detection as a pre-processing

that does not need retraining, given a CAD file.

challenging real-world scenarios like those encountered in bin picking.

Some approaches are trained on tailored datasets focused on specific tasks or ob-

PoseCNN [58] demonstrated strong performance on specific object categories by di-

Sensing Concepts
Reconstructing three-dimensional scenes from two-dimensional images is a core con-
cept in computer vision. This involves understanding the projection of 3D points
onto 2D image planes and reversing this process to infer 3D structures. The math-
ematical relationship between 3D world points and their 2D projections forms the
basis for tasks like 3D reconstruction, camera calibration, and, in our case, pose
estimation.
This chapter presents the fundamental mathematical formulations and vision
system methodologies that underpin these concepts. These foundations will be used
in subsequent sections and chapters. The content is inspired by works such as [76,
77, 78, 79, 80]. We will cover monocular, stereo and Time-of-Flight (ToF) systems.
3.1
Monocular Systems
Monocular vision, utilizing a single camera, is the most common and cost-effective
approach to image acquisition. This section details the fundamental principles and
mathematical models for monocular image formation and projection, providing the
basis for many computer vision tasks.
3.1.1
Forward Imaging Model
The pinhole forward imaging model [81] describes the relationship between 3D world
points and their 2D projections in an image.
Considering a point P in the world coordinate system W, the goal is to project
this point onto the image plane of a camera with its own coordinate system C. This
involves world-to-camera transformation, projection, and pixelization. In practical
scenarios, additional steps like undistortion are also considered.
The camera coordinate system C has its origin at the center of projection OC, with
axes xc, yc, zc. The focal length f is the distance between the center of projection
and the camera’s image plane.

3.1.2
Monocular Vision Depth Perception Techniques
Building on the concepts discussed in the previous section, we now turn our atten-
tion to the possibilities of perceiving depth with a monocular system. Unlike stereo
vision (discussed in a later section), which relies on the disparity between two images
to infer depth, monocular vision must derive depth information from a single image.
This is challenging because depth perception typically requires either disparity infor-
mation or known scene geometry.
While recent advances in self-supervised vision transformers [84], multi-scale clas-
sification networks [85], and other experimental techniques [86] have shown promising
results, their direct application to estimating the depth of industrial metallic parts is
currently limited by the availability of suitable datasets (see Section 4.2). Given the
==PAGE:39==
3.1 Monocular Systems
29
scale and complexity of our industrial use case, these methods may not be immedi-
ately practical. Additionally, these advanced techniques are often computationally
demanding, which further limits their feasibility for our application. Therefore, we
focus on more established techniques that can be reliably deployed in our specific
context.
We now briefly introduce two common categories of monocular depth estimation
techniques: feature matching and template matching.
Feature Matching
Feature matching aims to establish correspondences between points in an image and
their corresponding locations in the 3D world. These techniques rely on detecting
distinctive features, such as edges, corners, or blobs, and then matching these features
based on their appearance or geometric relationships.
Once correspondences are
established, depth information can be inferred using geometric constraints.
Common feature descriptors include SIFT (Scale-Invariant Feature Transform)
[87], which detects keypoints by finding extrema in the Difference of Gaussians (DoG)
scale space:
DoG(x, y, σ) = (G(x, y, kσ) −G(x, y, σ)) ∗I(x, y)
(3.27)
where G is the Gaussian function, σ is the scale, k is a constant multiplicative
factor, and I is the input image. The asterisk ∗denotes convolution. Other feature
descriptors include SURF (Speeded Up Robust Features), ORB (Oriented FAST
and Rotated BRIEF), and others.
As previously stated, recently, self-supervised
methods like DINOv2 [84] have emerged, learning visual representations without
explicit labels. They offer highly effective general-purpose feature descriptors, but
they can currently exhibit instability.
Template Matching
Template matching approaches use a predefined template or model of the object
of interest, typically a 2D image or a rendered 3D model. The goal is to locate
the best match of the template within the input image. The location of the best
match provides information about the object’s 2D position and, if the template’s 3D
geometry is known, can be used to infer depth. This approach is effective when the
object’s appearance is relatively consistent.
Template matching operates by comparing the template T with sub-regions of
the input image I using a similarity metric. One such metric is Normalized Cross-
Correlation (NCC), which measures the correlation between the template and the
image patch:

3.1.3
Challenges with Monocular Vision
Monocular vision, relying on a single camera, faces problems in perceiving depth and
3D structure. While offering advantages in terms of cost and simplicity, it presents
specific diﬀiculties:
• Depth Ambiguity: The most obvious challenge is the loss of depth informa-
tion during the projection from 3D world coordinates to the 2D image plane.
A single image provides no direct way to determine the distance of objects.
• Scale Ambiguity: Related to depth uncertainty, the scale of an object in the
image is also uncertain. A small object close to the camera can appear the
same size as a larger object farther away.
• Occlusions: In cluttered environments, occlusions are frequent. Because only
one viewpoint is available, recovering information about occluded parts of ob-
jects is impossible without prior knowledge or assumptions.
• Indirect Depth Inference Limitations: Although techniques like feature
matching and template matching can sometimes provide cues about relative
depth based on object size, perspective, and other geometric constraints, and
depth estimation methods exist, they often do not provide the accuracy and
robustness required.

3.2.1
Challenges with Stereo Vision and the
Correspondence Problem
A significant simplification for solving the correspondence problem is achieved by
image rectification3. With rectified images, the search for corresponding points is
constrained to horizontal lines, significantly reducing the computational cost.
3Rectification is the process of transforming images so that corresponding points lie on the same
horizontal line. This simplifies the correspondence problem by reducing it to a one-dimensional
search along the epipolar lines. For more details, see [89].
==PAGE:43==
3.2 Stereo Systems
33
The correspondence problem, also known as stereo matching, involves finding
corresponding points in the left and right rectified images. This process is often
performed using similarity metrics to determine how well image regions match. While
various metrics exist, the core idea is to compare the appearance of image patches
in the left and right images to find the best match.
Several factors complicate the correspondence problem and the stereo vision:
Stereo vision, while offering direct depth information, also presents its own set
of challenges that can affect the accuracy and robustness of 3D reconstruction and
pose estimation:
• Occlusions: Objects closer to one camera can occlude parts of the scene visible
to the other camera, leading to missing correspondences and incomplete depth
maps.
• Repetitive Patterns and Textureless Regions: Areas with repetitive pat-
terns (e.g., a grid of identical objects) or a lack of distinct texture (e.g., a plain
white wall) can make it diﬀicult to find unique and reliable matches between
the stereo images, leading to incorrect depth estimations.
• Noise and Illumination Differences: Noise in the images (due to sensor
limitations or environmental factors) and differences in illumination between
the cameras (due to varying camera parameters or lighting conditions) can
affect the accuracy of similarity metrics used for correspondence matching,
introducing errors in the calculated disparities and depth.
• Calibration Errors: Accurate calibration of the stereo camera system is
needed as errors in calibration can lead to significant distortions and inaccura-
cies in the reconstructed 3D scene.
3.2.2
Structured Light Systems
Structured light systems offer a potential solution to the correspondence problem. By
actively projecting a known pattern onto the scene, structured light creates artificial
texture, simplifying matching even in challenging conditions where passive vision
systems struggle. Unlike passive stereo, which relies on ambient light and natural
texture, structured light effectively controls the illumination and feature generation
process. Figure 3.5 illustrates a typical setup, where a projector casts a pattern onto
a 3D object, and two cameras capture the distorted pattern. One of the first pioneers
of this setup was Oh et al. [42] discussed in Chapter 2.
As depicted in Figure 3.5, the projected pattern is distorted by the object’s sur-
face. This distortion provides direct information about the object’s 3D shape. This
is especially beneficial for objects with featureless surfaces, which pose significant
challenges for traditional feature & template matching methods.
The working principle [91] is to project a known pattern onto the scene and
analyze its deformation as captured by one or more cameras.

encodes the 3D structure of the scene. Different structured light techniques employ
various patterns and analysis methods. One common approach uses sinusoidal fringe

3.2.3
Time-of-Flight Systems
Time-of-Flight (ToF) cameras represent another active depth sensing technique that
directly measures distance by analyzing the time it takes for a modulated light signal
to travel from the camera to the object and back. Unlike passive stereo, which infers
depth from geometric relationships and struggles with textureless and reflective ob-
jects as discussed in Section 3.2.1, ToF cameras directly measure distance, making
them less susceptible to these issues.
The working principle involves emitting a modulated light signal, typically in the
near-infrared (NIR) spectrum, and measuring the phase shift or time delay of the
reflected signal (Figure 3.6). Several modulation techniques exist, including pulsed
light and continuous-wave modulation.

Sensor Evaluation Results and Discussion
The experiment conducted revealed that the measured depth accuracy of the Intel
RealSense family was either better than or comparable to the stated values. However,
the significantly more expensive SICK sensors exhibited considerably lower accuracy
at the distances relevant for our bin-picking application.
Given that the vision
system must oversee the bin from a distance suitable for robotic arm manipulation—
approximately 1-2 meters—while maximizing pixel utilization, the SICK sensors
proved unsuitable.
The Visionary-T system presented a significant drawback with its intensity-based
sensor, the specifics of which are undisclosed. This sensor performed even worse
than a standard monocular black and white imaging sensor and offered the lowest
resolution among the tested systems. While the time-of-flight methodology is initially
promising due to its potential for rejecting specific light wavelengths in industrial
settings, practical testing disproved this theory. As shown in the second frame of
Figure 4.4, the sensor was often overexposed by its own emitted light, resulting in a
complete loss of depth information when pointed at a pile of screws. Furthermore,
the point cloud quality of the Visionary-T was highly dependent on material color,
likely due to the absorption of emitted light by darker materials. This effect is visible
==PAGE:52==

in Figure 4.4, where depth information is completely lost in areas corresponding to
the black portions of the ArUco tags.
Then, the bulkiness (as visible in Figure 4.2), high cost, proprietary software,
and overheating issues of the Visionary-S discouraged its use. More importantly, the
measured accuracy at the required distances rendered it unusable for our application.
Therefore, we concluded that the Intel RealSense cameras represent the most
suitable option for our project. They offer a significantly lower cost (sometimes by

for future work but was considered inappropriate for the moment due to the diﬀiculty
in obtaining the components shown in the dataset.

objects in bins, ground truth 6D poses, available CAD models, several scenes are

Tobj2cam = Tworld2cam × Tworld2obj

masks of the objects by filling the projected triangles in the image space.
For annotations, bounding boxes for each object were calculated by deriving the
minimal rectangle containing all non-zero pixels in the mask. Additional visibility
information, such as the number of visible pixels and the visibility fraction, was also
computed.

the necessary directories for RGB images, depth images, masks, and annotation files.

4.3.1
Preliminary Instance Segmentation
Strictly speaking, instance segmentation—which assigns a pixel-level mask to each
object instance—is not strictly required. However, the concept of identifying and
delineating individual objects within an image, which instance segmentation accom-
plishes, encompasses the necessary requirement for 6D pose estimation. A 6D pose
estimator would face significant challenges attempting to fit a pose to an object
within the entire image, especially in scenes with numerous objects. Therefore, we
first use 2D object detection to narrow down the region of interest, passing only the
==PAGE:63==
4.3 Pose Estimation Methods
53
bounding box of each detected object to the 6D pose estimator. With that, we are
tasked with finding the 2D location of each object, as well as its class. In our bin-
picking application, we focus on a single class (the screw), as we retrain the network
and regenerate the pipeline when a different part is introduced.
Object locations are represented by bounding boxes, and object identity by class
probabilities. Prior to YOLO, methods like Mask R-CNN [71] were dominant. Mask
R-CNN uses a CNN to create convolutional feature maps (representations of the
input image at different levels of abstraction). These maps are then used to propose
regions likely containing objects (bounding boxes). In a second stage, a technique
called Region of Interest (RoI) pooling extracts features from these maps for each
proposed region, which are then used for object classification and bounding box
refinement. This two-stage process, while effective, adds training complexity due to
the separate optimization of each stage.
YOLOv1 simplified object detection by performing both bounding box regres-
sion and class prediction in a single pass through a CNN (hence YOLO: You only
look once). This single-stage approach significantly simplifies training and improves
inference speed.
In YOLOv1, the input image is resized to Iw × Ih pixels and divided into S × S
grid cells. Each grid cell is responsible for predicting bounding boxes whose centers
fall within that cell.
A bounding box prediction consists of 5 components:
• (x, y): The center coordinates of the bounding box relative to the grid cell,
normalized between 0 and 1.
• (w, h): The width and height of the bounding box relative to the entire image,
also normalized between 0 and 1.
• C: The confidence score, representing the probability that an object is present
in the grid cell and the accuracy of the bounding box.
If the center of an object falls within a grid cell, that cell is responsible for
predicting the bounding box for that object. The x and y coordinates are offsets
relative to the top-left corner of the grid cell. These offsets are calculated as:
∆x = (x −xa)/Gw
∆y = (y −ya)/Gh

Training
We utilize Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) as our optimization algorithm. SGD
updates model parameters iteratively by taking steps proportional to the negative
gradient of the loss function with respect to those parameters, calculated on individ-
ual or small batches (mini-batches) of training examples. The update rule for SGD
is given by:
wt+1 = wt −η∇L(wt; xi)
(4.4)
Where:
• wt+1: Updated weight at time step t + 1.
• wt: Current weight at time step t.
• η: Learning rate, a hyperparameter controlling the step size.
• ∇L(wt; xi): Gradient of the loss function L with respect to the weights wt for
a single data point xi.
Because SGD applies a constant learning rate across all parameters and iterations,
it can sometimes lead to slower convergence compared to adaptive methods. However,
its stochastic nature allows it to potentially escape local minima and explore a wider
range of the loss landscape, increasing the chances of finding a better global minimum,
which can result in a more performant model.
Experiments have shown slightly
improved results using this optimizer compared to Adam [105] in our scenario.
For our application, we also utilize oriented bounding boxes (OBBs) (Figure 4.10)
compared to the standard horizontal bounding boxes (HBBs). They were first intro-
duced in our context by [106], with further notational development in [107], and sub-
sequently implemented in YOLOv5 [108]. To train a network capable of outputting
oriented bounding boxes, training data with corresponding oriented bounding box
annotations is essential. Thanks to our created dataset in previous section, we have
access to the necessary OBB annotations.

6D Pose Estimation
Having successfully implemented and evaluated a 2D object detector, as detailed in
the previous section, we now turn our attention to the task of 6D pose estimation.
As discussed in the introduction (Chapter 1), accurate robotic manipulation requires
knowledge of an object’s full six degrees of freedom (DOF)—its 3D position (x, y,
z) and 3D orientation (roll, pitch, yaw). While 2D detection provides the object’s
location within the image, it lacks the the information about its orientation in 3D
space, which is essential for successful grasping and manipulation.
We initially investigated NVIDIA’s FoundationPose as a potential solution. Foun-
dationPose offers the advantage of reduced reliance on complete CAD models, re-
quiring only a small set of reference images and corresponding depth maps. This
is achieved through its use of a neural implicit representation (NIR) for synthesiz-
ing novel views, effectively generating 3D shape representations from limited input
data [109]. However, despite this appealing feature, our evaluation, shown in Fig-
ure 4.12, revealed suboptimal performance in our specific use case, even when pro-
vided with ground-truth segmentation masks. This performance gap led us to explore
alternative 6D pose estimation methods better suited to our challenging industrial
setting.
NIR learns a function that maps 3D coordinates to occupancy or signed distance
values, encoding the object’s shape. This representation is highly sensitive to noise

affect this learned function, leading to inaccuracies in the reconstructed 3D shape.

as input the observed image Io and a rendered image Ir(T k
CO) generated from the
current pose estimate T k
CO, predicting an updated pose estimate T k+1
CO . The iterative

θk+1 = θk −λ∇θL(θk),

where θk is the current pose estimate, λ is the learning rate, and L(θ) is a loss
function quantifying the difference between the observed image Io and the synthetic
rendering Irender(θ).
The render-and-compare strategy involves rendering a synthetic image Irender(θk)
using the object’s CAD model and the current pose estimate θk with Physically-
Based Rendering (PBR) to simulate lighting and material properties. An L1 distance
is proposed by the authors as the loss function:
L(θk) = ∥Io −Irender(θk)∥1
(4.12)
The pose estimate θ is updated by minimizing the loss function using gradient
descent, as shown in Equation (4.11). The gradient of the loss function with respect
to the pose, ∇θL(θk), is used to update the pose estimate. It is important to note
that the gradient is computed with respect to the 6D pose vector θ, and this gradient
is then used to update the pose. This is done by converting the pose to a suitable rep-
resentation for gradient calculations (e.g., axis-angle or quaternion representation),
computing the gradient, and then converting the updated representation back to a
homogeneous transformation matrix.
Experiments and improvements
With the MegaPose pipeline now described, we proceed to evaluate its performance,
beginning with a validation of its capabilities against our initial requirements (Fig-
ure 4.15). Through experiments similar to the technique visualized in Figure 4.11,
we assessed MegaPose’s out-of-the-box performance on ground-truth segmentation
masks, as well as YOLO detections, and observed the following strengths:
• Robustness to Occlusions: The pipeline exhibits a degree of robustness to
partial occlusions, a common occurrence in bin-picking applications.
• Effective Handling of Reflective Surfaces: The use of Physically-Based
Rendering (PBR) in conjunction with the proposed L1 loss function (Equa-
tion 4.12) effectively mitigates the negative impact of reflective surfaces on
pose estimation accuracy.
• Generalization to Novel Objects: The pipeline demonstrates generaliza-
tion capabilities by successfully estimating poses of previously unseen objects,
setting a very promising baseline before further training.

The evaluation using BOP metrics provides valuable insights into the performance of

The VSD metric of 14.9, seemingly low, indicates a substantial difference

directly reflected in the VSD error. Another contributing factor is the heavily clut-

and isolation of individual screws. This clutter can lead to occlusion and inaccurate

Summary of Results
The objects (screws) are small, featureless, and occupy only a small fraction of the
image, making both detection and pose estimation diﬀicult. Unlike some other pose
estimation benchmarks that provide ground truth bounding boxes, our pipeline be-
gins with 2D object detection using YOLO. This reliance means that any detection
error—including incorrect or missed detections, each contributing a 100% error for
that instance and reducing recall—directly impacts the subsequent pose estimation.
This, combined with the cluttered and reflective environment, significantly increases
the task’s diﬀiculty. We also aimed to propose a general methodology without object-
specific fine-tuning. Futhermore, only monocular system and monochrome frames
were used for evaluation to assess worst-case scenario. Despite these challenges, the
method demonstrates an ability to capture the visible geometry of small, symmetric
objects, suggesting a degree of consistency in in-plane pose estimation. As illustrated
in Figure 4.22, even with visually plausible 2D projections, Z-axis deviations of up
to ∼0.6 cm are observed; assuming perfect 2D segmentation, this actually translates
to a relatively small depth error (approximately 20% of the screw’s length).
If the ROBI dataset were to become a standard benchmark, these challenges
should be considered inherent to the task, establishing a new, more demanding
standard. Therefore, given these complexities, the obtained results are considered
promising and provide a valuable foundation for future research.
However, the current runtime of approximately 30 seconds per frame on an Nvidia
A40 GPU is far from real-time and necessitates substantial optimization. The auto-
matic adaptation time of less than 24 hours for new parts without manual interven-
tion is a great achievement.
These initial findings provide a valuable foundation for future research, as will
be detailed in Chapter 5.

CHAPTER 5
Future Work
While the proposed method has yielded satisfactory results, certain aspects require
further investigation. Due to time constraints, scope limitations, or the need for
more extensive validation, some potential improvements and extensions were not
fully explored within this thesis. The following discussion highlights several beneficial
directions for future work.
5.0.1
Robust Object Detector
Future work should focus on developing a more robust 2D object detection methods
that are less susceptible to reflections and clutter. This could involve exploring alter-
native detection architectures, data augmentation techniques specifically designed to
handle reflections, or incorporating robust feature descriptors that are less affected
by specular highlights. This is important because inaccuracies in the 2D detection
will carry over and negatively affect the performance of the 6D pose estimation.
5.0.2
Automation and Robotic Integration
Integrating the developed pipeline with a 6DoF robot, such as the KUKA KR 150
R2700 shown in Figure 5.1, would be a next step. The developed method has reached
a level of maturity that justifies real-world testing. Without this integration, the
method remains a visualization tool rather than a functional bin-picking system.
Integrating the pipeline with a robotic system requires several steps: intrinsic and
extrinsic camera calibration, communication setup (likely using OPC UA), mission
control creation in KRL (KUKA Robot Language), development of an execution
system, target transformation from camera to robot coordinates, gripper actuation
control, pick operation planning with trajectory generation, and a decision-making
system to determine the optimal picking order. These steps will be the focus of
future work to create a fully autonomous bin-picking cell.
5.0.3

Reflectivity and depth acquisition have been recurring challenges throughout this
work. While different sensors theoretically mitigate some reflections, they do not
entirely resolve the issue. Therefore, we explore the potential of foundation models,
specifically DINOv2 [84], for depth estimation.

Algorithm 4 describes the depth fusion process, which combines depth informa-
tion from a stereo camera and the DINOv2 monocular depth estimation network.
DINOv2, a foundation model pre-trained on a massive image dataset, provides a
dense depth map from a single RGB image. While useful, this depth map is not
metrically accurate. A stereo camera, on the other hand, provides metrically accu-
rate depth, but it is often noisy. The fusion process aims to combine the strengths
of both approaches.
The core idea is to use the stereo depth as a geometric reference to refine the
DINOv2 depth.
This is achieved by converting both depth maps into 3D point
clouds. Then, ICP is used to align the DINOv2 point cloud with the stereo point
cloud. We would therefore transfer the metric accuracy of the stereo depth to the
denser DINOv2 depth.
However, this is a naive assumption. Given the distinct projective distortions of
the two depth maps and their lack of an aﬀine relationship (a linear transformation
followed by a translation), a direct ICP application is unlikely to converge, requiring
substantial further investigation.
5.0.4
Click-to-Grab Interface
The pipeline, even with the depth fusion enhancements, provides the 6D pose of an
object relative to its center point. While center point picking is sometimes valid, it
can lead to erroneous picks or even damage to the gripper, the part itself, or the robot.
Therefore, a mechanism for specifying the desired picking point is necessary. Our
pipeline’s use of CAD models provides a natural solution to this problem, enabling
the creation of a ”click-to-grab” interface, as illustrated in Figure 5.4.
This interface introduces a manual step into the otherwise automated pipeline.
An operator interacts with a 3D visualization of the CAD model and selects the
desired picking point. The system then calculates the transformation between the
object’s center point (as estimated by the pipeline) and the selected picking point.
This transformation is then used to guide the robot’s gripper to the correct picking
location.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
Bin picking, the autonomous retrieval of randomly oriented objects from a container,
remains a significant challenge in robotics. This thesis has tried to address the per-
ception component of this problem, resulting in a functional pipeline for 6D object
pose estimation. We set out to develop a system capable of robust, repeatable,
precise and fast object picking, handling heavily cluttered scenes, dealing with
objects exhibiting featureless, textureless, or non-Lambertian (highly reflec-
tive) surfaces, utilizing off-the-shelf vision sensors in a cost-effective manner,
and facilitating part changeovers with minimal intervention.
The following subsections provide a retrospective and chronological analysis of
each component of the thesis, summarizing key findings, lessons learned, challenges
acknowledged, and some flaws in the work identified.
Scope Definition
Chapter 1 defined the bin picking problem and outlined the components of a bin pick-
ing system. While the initial aim was to develop a complete bin picking system, the
problem’s complexity led us to focus specifically on the perception component. An
evaluation of commercially available bin picking platforms (Section 1.1) revealed lim-
itations in their generalizability, cost-effectiveness, or real-world performance, high-
lighting the need for further research. Section 1.2 identified key challenges in bin
picking, explaining the diﬀiculties in achieving robust and generalized solutions, and
formulated goals to guide the subsequent thesis research.
State-of-the-Art Review
Chapter 2 presented a review of existing bin picking methodologies. Early analytic
methods (Section 2.1), employing geometric approaches such as convolution edge
detection, model fitting (e.g., RANSAC), and point cloud alignment (e.g., ICP), were
examined. Structured light or SVD were first introduced, methods still used today.
However, the methodologies demonstrated limited generalizability despite increasing
complexity. In contrast, deep learning approaches (Section 2.3) marked a significant
advancement. Starting with basic neural networks and progressing through CNNs
(including AlexNet), we traced the evolution of deep learning frameworks applied to
bin picking, beginning with template matching and advancing to feature-matching
approaches. While CNNs have driven significant progress, the chapter concluded
that a fully generalized solution remains an open challenge.

Mathematical Fundamentals
Chapter 3 established the foundational principles of camera operation. It covered the
pinhole camera model, homogeneous transformations and projections, and monocu-
lar vision (Section 3.1.1). Stereo vision systems were discussed in Section 3.2, along
with an explanation of structured light and Time-of-Flight (ToF) systems in Sec-
tion 3.2.3. This analysis highlighted the influence of hardware limitations on the bin
picking problem, raising the question of whether alternative hardware methodologies
could significantly impact the software pipeline.
Sensor Selection
Chapter 4.1 evaluated various sensor technologies, including structured light stereo
and ToF, identifying reflection and point cloud resolution as key performance factors.
The Intel RealSense family was selected for its cost-effectiveness, extensive commu-
nity support, and well-documented APIs. Experimental results demonstrated that
more expensive industrial sensors did not offer significant performance improvements.
The hypothesis that ToF methodologies would provide superior performance was dis-
proven, as all tested sensors exhibited susceptibility to reflectivity issues.
As for the sensor selection process, we must conclude that while repeatable and
quick, lacked a rigorous quantitative evaluation. A more thorough comparison of
different sensor technologies based on metrics such as accuracy, precision, resolu-
tion, and robustness to environmental factors (e.g., ambient light, reflections) would
provide a stronger justification for the chosen sensor and a better understanding of
its trade-offs. Then, emphasizing and using off-the-shelf sensors aided in creating a
more cost-effective solution, as the used sensors were up to 5x more cost effective
than the evaluated competition.
Synthetic Data Generation
To address the challenges posed by heavily cluttered scenes and facilitate general-
ization to unseen objects, a custom dataset generation tool was developed, utilizing
a physics engine to simulate realistic clutter and lighting conditions (Section 4.2).
This tool generates thousands of photorealistic images from CAD models, varying
object size, clutter, pose, background, container, field of view, camera distance, and
object quantity. Section 4.2.2 assessed existing datasets and concluded that most
were unsuitable for our specific research focus.
Real-world images for evaluation
were also introduced. This dataset generation strategy was key to addressing the
challenge of heavily cluttered scenes and accommodating part changeovers.
Pose Estimation Methods
Chapter 4.3 evaluated several 2D detection methods, including CNOS and DETR.
CNOS, while offering segmentation without retraining, required fine-tuning for each
scene. DETR proved susceptible to false positives in cluttered environments and
YOLO was chosen. For 6D pose estimation, various open-source algorithms were

was the assumption that its small size and lack of distinctive features represent
a particularly challenging case, and that successful performance with this object
would suggest generalizability to other, more easily handled objects. However, this
assumption requires empirical validation with a wider range of objects exhibiting
varying shapes, sizes, and material properties.
Interpretation of the Evaluation Results
The final pipeline requires only a CAD model of the target part. It automatically
generates a training dataset for both the 2D detector and the 6D pose estimator.
The entire training process can be completed within 24 hours on a powerful single
GPU, enabling rapid adaptation to new parts and addressing the goal of minimal-
intervention part changeovers. The YOLO detector achieved a mAP@0.5 of 0.879,

Future Work
The developed pipeline, while demonstrating promising results, necessitates real-
world robotic testing. A key limitation identified is the persistent challenge of re-
flections, which future work should address through mitigation strategies. One such
strategy, outlined in Section 5.0.3, proposes integrating a foundation model (DI-

incorporated into MegaPose (which can handle both RGB and RGB-D data), aims
to improve robustness. While the method currently outputs the estimated object’s
center of gravity, future work should also focus on mapping this to the optimal
gripper grasp pose to enable a complete pick-and-place solution.
