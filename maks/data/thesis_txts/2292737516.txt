Abstract
This thesis considers the derivation, implementation and application of the topology
optimization (TO) method of ﬂuid-structure-interaction (FSI) problems. The density-
based topology optimization framework is set up by solving the Navier-Cauchy and
the incompressible, steady state Navier-Stokes equations in monolithic ﬁnite element
(FE) formulation.
The TO of FSI problems conﬁnes itself to two dimensional
academic size problems with low to moderate Reynold numbers. The governing
equations are solved in an uniﬁed domain by using the Brinkman penalization
approach and a pressure-coupling ﬁlter function. The matrix integration are carried
out in the deformed conﬁguration by introducing a Jacobian transformation matrix.
The sensitivities and objective function is computed with the adjoint method and
the optimization problem is solved using the Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA).
A sensitivity ﬁltering scheme is used to obtain checkerboard-free designs in structural
compliance and displacement problems. Three objective functions are considered:
dissipated energy in the ﬂow, structural compliance and structural displacement.
The FSI implementation is validated by comparing a simple numerical example
with an analytic benchmark which rely on simple ﬂuid and structural mechanical
reasoning. The TO FSI methodology is validated by comparing the design solutions
with pure ﬂuid TO problems from the literature. The comparisons show good results
for both benchmarks.
A new formulation of the pressure coupling in the Navier-Cauchy equation is
suggested. The reformulation alters the design solutions which are signiﬁcant diﬀerent
from the results presented in the literature. The derivations, implementation and
application of the FSI TO framework are described in details and can help other
researcher in the development to the methodology.
The coupling between the ﬂuid and the structure are captured by the TO
framework via the Jacobian tensor which transforms the derivatives from the deformed
to the undeformed conﬁguration. The coupling is demonstrated in a dissipation and
several compliance and displacement problems. The problems demonstrate that the
ﬂuid-structure dependency is captured by the TO algorithm and the structure is
optimized according to the underlying physical formulation.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This thesis considers the application of the topology optimization (TO) method of
ﬂuid-structure-interaction (FSI) problems. FSI concerns the interaction between an
elastic mechanical structure and an internal or surrounding ﬂuid ﬂow. FSI problems
are multiphysics of nature, and the coupling between the physics are constituted by
the exchange of momentum and energy between the ﬂuid and the structure. FSI
problems are strongly coupled, as the ﬂuid inﬂuences the structure and the structure
inﬂuences the ﬂuid.
TO is a mathematical approach which optimizes the material layout for a pre-
scribed set of performance targets. The performance targets are in this thesis aimed
on minimizing compliance structures and dissipation in ﬂuid ﬂows.
The numerical simulations in the present thesis conﬁnes itself to two-dimensional,
steady state, in-compressible laminar ﬂows for low to moderate Reynold numbers.
The FSI problems are governed by the Navier-Cauchy and Navier-Stokes equations,
which are discretized and solved by the ﬁnite element method (FEM).
1.1
Motivation
FSI is observed and signiﬁcant in many physical systems and engineering applications,
which makes FSI an important ﬁeld to consider.
In biology, the circulating blood and elastic blood vessels in the human body are
governed by FSI. The elastic eﬀect of the vessels has crucial importance in the blood
circulation in the human body, as the elastic eﬀect of the blood vessels maintain a
constant blood pressure despite the irregular volume ﬂow of the heart.
In civil engineering, many structures are subjected to ﬂuid loads. The Tacoma
Narrows Bridge collapsed in 1940 due to wind-induced oscillations, as the vortex
shedding frequency was critically close to the natural frequency of the bridge.
In aero-engineering, the design performance of airfoils (such as a sail or a wing)
requires that the FSI phenomenon is taken into account. The exchange of momentum
between the ﬂuid and the structure deﬂects the airfoil and its lift, drag and strength
is changed.
The design of racing yacht keels are an engineering application, where FSI is
important to consider and TO is a suitable approach to optimize performance targets.
The keel and the keel blade is an essential part of a yacht as it counteracts the sideway
motion of the boat, the heeling eﬀect of the wind and it increases the stability of the
boat in rough weather. It is important to capture the interaction between the ﬂuid
and the structure when designing airfoils as a good performance is required for all
loads and deformation states.
An example of a badly designed keel blade is seen in Fig. 1.1, where Exide
Challenger, a racing-sailing yacht designed to participate in the 1996 Vende Globe
race, capsized due to a broken keel blade.

1.2
Solution Strategies of Fluid-Structure-Interaction prob-
lems
FSI problems are often too complex to solve analytically, so it is necessary to
develop experiments or numerical methods to investigate such problems. Two main
approaches in solving FSI problems exist
1. The staggered approach1: The ﬂow ﬁeld and structural displacement ﬁeld are
solved in separated domains with two distinct solvers; a ﬂuid and an elastic
structure solver. The velocity and pressure ﬁeld are computed by a designated
ﬂuid solver, which transfers the pressure ﬁeld to a designated elastic solver.
The elastic solver computes the displacements of the structure and transfers
the displacements back to the ﬂuid solver. This routine is continued (in time
independent problems) until a steady state solution is reached. Most FEM
state-of-the art commercial software such as Comsol and Ansys provide routines
which perform this type of analysis.
2. The monolithic approach: The ﬂow ﬁeld and structural displacement ﬁeld
are solved simultaneously with one single solver. The monolithic approach
employs an uniﬁed computational domain, where the ﬂuid and structural
domains are solved simultaneously and the solid is treated by an immersed
body method. The ﬂuid and structural domains are modeled by interpolating
the permeability and modulus of elasticity of the ﬁnite elements. The monolithic
approach requires a specialized system of equations for a speciﬁc combination of
physics, as the interaction between the physics varies according to the problem
considered.
Most state-of-the-art commercial software provide routines which allow FSI problems
to be analyzed, but these routines are limited within the ﬁeld of density-based TO.
TO becomes arduous in multiphysics problems with pre-deﬁned explicit boundaries,
as the module based framework requires explicitly deﬁned interfacing boundary
conditions to couple the interacting physics.

1.3
Introduction to Topology Optimization
The aim of structural optimization is to systematically improve the material layout
in a design space, so that the performance of a system with respect to a speciﬁc
quantity is the best possible. Structural optimization are roughly classiﬁed by three
diﬀerent methods: sizing, shape and topology optimization (please confer Fig. 1.3).
The three methods share the same objective but are very diﬀerent in methodology
and mathematics.
Sizing
Shape
Topology
Figure 1.3: The three main structural optimization approaches.
In the ﬁeld of sizing optimization, a structural dimension constitute the design
variable, and the design is optimized accordingly to a prescribed quantity. The shape
of the design is limited to the initial design, which means that the design process
does not allow new geometries (occurrence of holes and change of boundaries). The
lack of geometric agility poses the main limitations of sizing optimization.
The design variable in shape optimization is the form or contour of the structure.
The optimization strategy is to tune the shape of the structure so that the performance
of the design for a desired quantity is increased. Shape optimization has two main
limitations: The ﬁrst limitation concerns the allowable adjustments size of the
boundaries; large adjustments causes problems with the underlying discretization of
the design. Remeshing of the design domain can resolve the discretization limitations,
but it increases the computational time and involves other challenges. The second
limitation concerns the appearance of new holes and boundaries, which is not possible
in the shape optimization framework, as the method requires that an initial shape
and boundaries are deﬁned apriori. The optimized design will therefore to a great
extend resemble the initial design. Shape optimization is well suited for ﬁne-tuning
an existing close-to-optimal design, but limited when the designers have small or no
knowledge of the ‘optimal’ design.

Topology optimization is the most general form of structural optimization, and
shape and sizing optimization can be considered as a subclass of TO. Though,
practically implementations and mathematics are based on diﬀerent techniques for
which reason they are treated diﬀerently in the literature [26]. In TO the design ﬁeld
does not require a qualiﬁed initial guess, which is illustrated by the grey color in Fig.
1.3. TO allows holes to occur in the design ﬁeld, and the optimized result has few
limitations with respect to the initial guess or discretization. TO is well suited for
problems in which the designer has small or no knowledge of the optimal design.
A density based TO method is used in present thesis. The design domain is
divided into a number of design elements and parameterized by deﬁning a continuous
elementwise design variable. The continuous design variable can in each element
take all intermediate values between 0 and 1. Solutions with no intermediate element
densities are desired (also called 0-1 solutions or designs) and to obtain these solution
penalizations of the material properties is introduced. The strict partitioning of
the boundaries between the solid and void are relaxed by interpolating the material
properties as function of the element design variables. Interpolation of the material
properties is an important part of density based TO, has the choice of parameters
has a large inﬂuence on the optimized designs.

2.2
Fluid-Structure-Interaction Model Assumptions
The Navier-Stokes equations are non-linear partial diﬀerential equations, which must
be treated in their simpliﬁed forms. Setting up appropriate assumptions is key to
successfully constructing an adequate mathematical model of the FSI problem. To
deal with the complexity of the Navier-Cauchy and Navier-Stokes equations following
assumptions are introduced:
1. Small structural displacements: The structural stiﬀness term in the Navier-
Cauchy equation is assumed geometric linear.
2. Constant Transport Properties: There is no change in conductivity, diﬀusion,
temperature or density, which makes the energy equation redundant.
3. Laminar ﬂow: The ﬂow is assumed to be laminar, which only allows low-
medium Reynold numbers.
4. Newtonian ﬂuid: The viscous stresses are assumed to be proportional to the
local strain of the ﬂuid, which means that the viscosity of the ﬂuid is constant.
5. No slip condition: The no-slip condition, i.e. the ﬂuid velocity is zero indeﬁnitely
close to the structures.
6. Steady volume ﬂow: The ﬂow and the structure is considered time in-dependent,
for which reason the time dependent terms are neglected. The dynamic and
kinematic coupling between the ﬂuid and structure is therefore not considered.
7. Negligible ﬂuid body forces: The body forces of the Navier-Stokes equations
are neglected.

2.7.1
The Brinkman Penalization Coeﬃcient
The Brinkman term α is a penalization of the velocity ﬁeld which is used to model
the solid domains. The solid domains are modeled as a porous media by imposing a
large velocity-dependent Brinkman penalization in the solid elements. This activity
penalizes the ﬂow velocity to (almost) zero inside the elements and at the boundary
(no-slip condition). Mathematically, it is desired to impose a inﬁnite Brinkman
penailzation in the solid element, but numerical stability requires that the upper
limit of the penalization factor is chosen. The Brinkman penalization parameter is
continuous and takes αmin and αmax in the limits. A large α-value imposes a zero
velocity ﬁeld, for which reason the element achieve structural behavior. A zero α
value will on the other hand impose a ﬂuid behavior as the element will be governed
by the original momentum equation.
A completely structural element is modeled by imposing a large Brinkman
penalizaition parameter α. The numeric choice of α is conﬂicting between numerical
stability and appropriate physical modeling. A too large number will cause a low
numeric resolution as the diﬀerences between the upper and lower limit of the
Brinkman penalization becomes close to machine precision. A too small number will
make the element permeable to ﬂuid ﬂow, which will cause an imprecise result. The
Brinkman penalization is a numerical trick but Khadra et. al. [25] suggest physical
scaling of the maximum penalization parameter

2.7.2
Element Stiﬀness
The element stiﬀness is governed by Young’s modulus and can take intermediate
values within an upper and lower limit. The Young’s modulus of a ﬂuid element Emin
is small and only contributes little to the stiﬀness of the structure. On the other
hand the Young’s modulus of the structural element Emax is large and contribute
signiﬁcantly to the overall stiﬀness of the structure.
2.7.3
Pressure-Coupling Filter Function
A continuous pressure-coupling ﬁlter function Ψ is introduced. The ﬁlter function
takes the values Ψ = Ψmin = 0 and Ψ = Ψmax = 1 in the limits, where zero and unity
are void and solid elements, respectively. The ﬁlter function insures that the pressure-
coupling between the ﬂuid and the structure are suppressed in void elements, which

constitute that the Navier-Cauchy equation is consistently formulated in an uniﬁed
domain. The pressure-coupling ﬁlter function allow the Navier-Cauchy equation to
be deﬁned in the uniﬁed domain tΩwhich is a key feature in the monolithic FSI

The pressure-coupling ﬁlter function does also serve as a numerical TO trick, as

The intermediate ﬂuid-solid elements have a small stiﬀness, and large displacements

function ensures that the ﬂuid traction force in the ﬂuid and intermediate ﬂuid

maintained.

3.1
The Galerkin Methodology
The Galerkin method is attributed to the Russian mathematician Boris Galerkin and
was introduced within the ﬁeld FEA in the beginning of the 1970s[40]. The method
was extremely successful within the ﬁeld of solid mechanics and heat transfer for
which reason it was predicted to revolutionize the ﬁeld of ﬂuid FE modeling, but the
prevalence of the method suﬀered from stabilization problems of the Navier-Stokes
equations. Today, the FE method is a popular numerical techniques in solving ﬂuid
and structural problems. An overview of the solid and ﬂuid FEM techniques are
found in following books: [18, 41, 51, 16].
The Galerkin ﬁnite element methodology is a technique to discretize continuous
partial diﬀerential equations. The technique is very popular within the ﬁeld of ﬁnite
element modeling due to its systematic and robust nature. The procedure of Galerkin
method is summarized in the following list
1. Formulate the governing diﬀerential equations of the physical problem and
state appropriate boundary conditions.
2. Derive the Galerkin weak form of the governing diﬀerential equations.
(a) Multiply the equations with a suitable test function.
(b) Integrate all terms over the domain.
(c) Use integration by parts to reduce the diﬀerential order of appropriate
terms.
3. Discretize the domain into a ﬁnite number of elements, where the governing
diﬀerential equations are fulﬁlled globally in an average sense.
(a) Approximate the solution of the ﬁeld variables by inserting suitable shape
functions.
(b) Set up the analytic element matrix equations and derive the matrix system.
Page 18 of 111
==PAGE:27==
Chapter 3: Finite Element Modeling of Fluid-Structure-Interaction Problems
The test functions is approximated by a combination of basis functions, where each
basis function satisfy the value of the nodal points. The domain is discretized into
several sub-domains and the solution is fulﬁlled element wise in an average sense. The
methodology is robust, because the basis functions are constructed systematically,
which makes the method very versatile.

the pressure coupling term 2 is signiﬁcantly larger than pressure coupling term 1

governing equations.

3.6
Stabilised Galerkin Finite Elements
The Galerkin ﬁnite element formulation of diﬀerential equation is a strong and
systematic procedure but it is associated with two potential numerical instabilities
[42, 7]
1. The presence of the convection terms in the momentum equation may cause
spurious node-to-node oscillations in the velocity ﬁeld. The instability and
oscillations becomes more distinct for convection dominated ﬂows, i.e. high
Reynold numbers, where the gradients in the ﬂow are large.
2. Inappropriate combinations of the velocity and pressure interpolation functions
representing the velocity and pressure ﬁeld, may cause oscillations in the
pressure ﬁeld.
The problems are handled by introducing stabilization terms in the governing equa-
tions. The stabilization technique for the oscillatory pressure ﬁeld is denoted Pressure-
Stabilizing Petrov-Galerkin (PSPG) stabilization and is observed for certain com-
binations of interpolation functions (for example u1p1). The pressure stabilization
techniques smoothes large gradients in the pressure ﬁeld. This prevent spurious node-
to-node pressure osculations to occur. The occurrence of these pressure oscillation
can also be reduced by implementing higher-order velocity interpolation spaces in the
FE formulation. Though, TO is performed by repeating a sequence of FE analysis,
which makes the introduction of higher-order elements computationally expensive.
The convective instabilities are treated in a similar way by introducing Streamline-
Upwind Petrov-Galerkin (SUPG) terms in the momentum equation. In convection
dominated ﬂows non-physical oscillations occur when downstream boundary condi-
tions impose large gradients in the velocity ﬁeld. These oscillations can be avoided
by extreme mesh-reﬁnement, so that the convection does not dominate on an element
basis. This solution strategy is though in-appropriate due to its high computational
cost.
General for the stabilization techniques are, that they are imposing an artiﬁcial
diﬀusion or viscosity in the Navier-Stokes equations which smoothes large gradients
in the velocity and pressure ﬁeld. The solution to the original equation in strong form
is still a solution to the modiﬁed weak form of the stabilized equation. The problem
is said to be consistent. The meaning of consistence is that the original problem
(original equations) is still a solution to the modiﬁed equations. The stabilization
methods can be considered as a Petrov Galerkin method, where the test and trial
functions are diﬀerent.
A comprehensive review of PSPG and SUPG stabilization are found in the paper
by Tezdyar [42].

Globalization
Globalization refers to the discipline dealing with the overall convergence performance
of the Newton method. The Newton method is initialized by an initial guess of the
state variables, which signiﬁcantly inﬂuences the convergence performance of the
solver. A bad initial guess leads to a slow or no convergence, where a good initial

In the present study, a lot of highly non-linear problems are considered, which
requires a fast and robust non-linear solver capable of solving a large sequence of
problems. To deal with the convergence problems, three diﬀerent initiatives are
taken: ramping, damping and hybrid solvers.
3.7.1.1
Ramping of the Reynold’s Number Method
The method of ramping the Reynold number takes advantage of the solvablity of
low Reynolds number ﬂows to improve the chances of solving high Reynolds number
ﬂows. The Reynolds number is slowly increased within the ﬁrst Picard iterations.
The initial guess which is transferred to the Newton-Raphson solver is improved
drastically, as the initial guess is originated from a somewhat similar ﬂow.
I this occasion it is important to emphasize that a small change in the Reynolds
number might cause a large change in the ﬂow ﬁeld. These occurrences will not be
captured, and the impact of the ramping method will be signiﬁcantly reduced.
3.7.1.2
Damped Newton-Raphson Method
Two problems in relation to the Newton solver occur, when dealing with very
non-linear systems. The ﬁrst source of error concerns the precision of the Newton
approximation which is limited in systems with large gradients. The second source
of error concerns systems which are close to singular. A sudden change from a low to
a high ﬁeld variable gradient, might result in an oscillating behavior of the Newton
approximations causing bad or no convergence. To deal with these problems, a
damping factor λn+1 is multiplied to the solution update in Eq. (3.42)
sn+1 = sn + λn+1∆sn+1
(3.45)
The damping factor reduces the size of the Newton step with a prescribed ratio
λn+1 ∈]0; 1]. The damping factor increased the likelihood that the solver converges
toward a solution, but the quadratic global convergence is lost. A unity damping
factor leads to fast convergence, however it exhibit lack of convergence of highly
non-linear problems.
A sophisticated choice of the damping factor signiﬁcantly increases the conver-
gence rate. The reduced Newton steps are necessary when solutions are far from
convergence, but as the approximate Newton solution approaches the exact solution
of the mathematical model, a larger size of the Newton steps is allowed.
The damping factor is heuristically determined on basis of a trial and error
process. A suitable damping factor yield a robust converging solver, which is desired
when dealing with TO, as the same FE simulations are run multiple times.
3.7.1.3
Hybrid Solver
The hybrid solver combines the good global convergence of the Picard iterations and
the good local convergence of the Newton solver. The Picard iterations has excellent
global convergence properties, but converges very slowly when near the solution.

Chapter 4
Topology Optimization
The basic ideas and solutions strategies of TO of FSI problems are outlined in
this chapter. The framework of TO is set up and followed by an overview of ﬂuid
and FSI TO in the literature. The topology matrices are derived and the adjoint
problem is formulated. Finally, the relevant details in the solution strategy, such as
interpolation functions, ﬁlter functions and optimization algorithms are described,
and the framework for suitable optimization problems are established.
4.1
General Form of the Topology Optimization Problem
There are in general two main solution strategies of TO problems: The nested
approach and the Simultaneous ANalysis and Design (SAND) approach. The nested
approach is the most disseminated within the ﬁeld of TO, but for the sake of generality
both approaches are described.
The SAND approach solves the original combined optimization problem in one
routine. This means that the design and state variables are used as optimization
variables. The SAND approach has no premature termination of the procedure and
does not provide any results of the current design. The equilibrium constraint might
not be fulﬁlled so-forth the optimization routine is stopped prematurely. Furthermore,
the method requires full integration of the optimal design, which makes demand on
CPU time and memory, as the problem size is extended by the number of design
variables.
The nested approach solves the equilibrium and the optimization problem in-
dependently. The equilibrium equation is solved by a FE solver, which in many
applications are proﬁcient and available in commercial codes. The nested approach
decouples the design variable optimization and computation of state variables, which
constitute the reason why the methodology is the most propagated within the ﬁeld
of TO.
All optimization problems solved in present thesis can be cast from the general
nested constrained TO problem

4.2
Litterature Review
TO has been developed within the ﬁeld of mechanical elasticity, but with the increased
computational power and the maturing of the ﬁeld, the methodology has been
extended to a wide range of diﬀerent physics such as acoustics [19], electromechanics
[49], photonics [20], ﬂuids [11], thermomechanics [2] and many more.
The book by Bendsøe and Sigmund [10] contains a comprehensive review of
topology optimization of continuum and discrete structures.
The overview pa-
per by Sigmund and Maute [36] treats the trends and directions in TO since the
publishing of the book. The following subsections contains a literature review of
topology optimization of elastic structure problems, ﬂuid problems and FSI problems.
4.2.1
Topology Optimization of Elasticity Problems
The ﬁeld of structural TO was pioneered by Bendsøe and Kikuchi, who published their
seminal paper [9] on optimal topology in structural design using a homogenization
method in 1988. The new optimization methodology avoided drawbacks such as
re-meshing of the ﬁnite element approximation, which was a necessary step in other
design optimization approaches at that time. Since Bendsøe and Kikuchi’s paper
on homogenization, the approaches of TO have developed in diﬀerent directions:
Density, level set, topological derivatives, phase ﬁelds, evolutionary approaches and
several others.
The density approach is the most famous and popular of the TO approaches,
and named Solid Isotropic Microstructure with Penalization (SIMP) by Zhou and
Rozvany [50]. The SIMP or power law approach was originally suggested in a paper
by Bendsøe [8], and reduces the complexity of the homogenization approach and
pushes the design ﬁeld toward 0-1 solutions. In the SIMP approach The density

4.2.2
Topology Optimization of Fluid Problems
Borrvall and Petersson [11] pioneered ﬂuid TO by introducing a Brinkman penaliza-
tion term in the Stokes equations. The Brinkman penalization enables interpolation
between ﬂow and non-ﬂow regions. The Brinkman penalization term simulates a
solid body as an artiﬁcial permeability and imposes a zero velocity in the prescribed
elements. By imposing the Brinkman penalization term and formulating the govern-
ing equations in a monolithic form, no internal boundary representation is necessary,
and density-based TO is possible.
The Brinkman penalization approach has earlier been used in a lot of diﬀerent
branches of numerical ﬂuid dynamics (confer for example [24] and the references
herein), but Borrvall and Petersson were the ﬁrst to use it in ﬂuid TO. The Brinkman
penalization term has been applied in a lot of diﬀerent problems such as transport
problems [5], reactive ﬂows [30], transient ﬂows [17, 29], ﬂuid-structure-interaction
[45, 46] and coupled convection problems [2].
Fluid TO was extended to moderate Reynolds numbers governed by the Navier-
Stokes equations on channel ﬂows between plates by Gersborg-Hansen et. al. [21].
The Brinkman penalization term was introduced in the equations for in-compressible
laminar viscous ﬂows, which signiﬁcantly altered the physics compared to the work
by Borrvall and Petersson.
Olesen et al. [31] continued the ﬂuid TO research by implementing the Navier-
Stokes equations in the high-level programming-language software package FEMLAB.
A comprehensive parameter study of the Brinkman penalization term was carried
out, and they showed that the Brinkman penalization term has signiﬁcantly inﬂuence
on the ﬂow ﬁeld solution and the convexity of the optimization problem.
Guest and Prevost [22] suggested a new mixed formulation methodology of TO in
Darcy-Stokes ﬂows. The no-slip condition of the solid-surface interface are resolved
by treating the solid phase as a porous medium governed by Darcy’s law. The ﬂuid
domain is then modeled using a combination of the Stokes and Darcy’s equations.
They showed that the diﬀerence between the method vanishes in the limit of 0-1
solutions. In 2008, Aage et al. [1] presented a large scale three dimensional ﬂuid TO
results using parallel code.
The Brinkman penalization and density-based TO has been popular, but there
exist alternatives which allows similar analysis to be conducted. Density-based TO
models the ﬂow in the solid domains, which requires an unnecessary amount of CPU
time and memory. Furthermore, the method imposes the no-slip boundary condition
weakly, which allows the ﬂuid pressure (and ﬂuid) to diﬀuse through the media and
cause non-physical solutions in some problems.
Two approaches are mainly used to solve ﬂuid TO problems: the density and the
levelset approach. The levelset approach provides good ﬂexibility in solving optimiza-
Page 41 of 111
==PAGE:50==

tion problems with a broad range of constraints and objectives. Computational time
in the FE routine is saved, because the routine is enable to distinguish between solid
and void. The level set approach does not require speciﬁc objectives or constraints
to obtain a 0-1 solution; a problem which is paid a lot of attention in density based
TO. Fluid TO was carried out using the level set approach by Challis and Guest [15],
who demonstrated the method on large-scale three dimensional problems. Kreissl
and Maute [27] performed TO on the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations for low

ﬂow ﬁeld was discretized using the extended ﬁnite element method (XFEM), which

There are a lot of interesting applications of the levelset approach, but the
weakness of the method rely on the bookkeeping and re-meshing, which must be
conducted during the optimization process. The computational eﬀort saved in the
FEA is to some extend claimed in the bookkeeping and re-meshing of the optimization
routine. The bookkeeping and remeshing which has conﬁned the popularity of the
method. On the other hand, the material distribution density approach does not
require remeshing during the optimization process, but the pressure diﬀuses trough
the solids, which might cause nonphysical results.
4.2.3
Topology Optimization of Fluid-Structure-Interaction Problems
Topology optimization of FSI problems is still limited to simple two dimensional
problems and have only recently been approached. Yoon [45] presented a monolithic
formulation that describes both ﬂuid and solid in a uniﬁed domain by converting
the stresses at the ﬂuid-solid interface into a volume integral. Yoon [46] applied the
same TO approach in the the optimization of the material distribution of planar
passive ﬂap micro valves. In [47] the same author applies the methodology in stress
based problems. The governing equations in mentioned papers are implemented in
the Comsol framework, but despite the three papers on the subject the ‘show of
method’ and strength of the results still are still weak.
Andreasen et. al. [4] presented a method for applying FSI problems in saturation
poroelastic media using a multiple-scale method. The coupling between the Stokes
ﬂow in the pores of the structure was coupled with the deformations of the elastic
skeleton through a macroscopic Darcy-type ﬂow law. The material distribution is
optimized by TO for a shock absorber and an internally pressurized lid.
Kreissl et. al. [28] suggested a multi-objective one way coupling TO formulation
for the design of dynamically tunable ﬂuidic devices. Flow channels was embed into
a ﬂexible structure which deforms in response to internally or externally applied
mechanical loads. The perfomance of the design was optimized in the deformed
and undeformed conﬁguration. In contrast to the monolithic approach suggested by
Yoon, the separate treatment of the ﬂuid and structural domain allows the usage of
module based solvers.

4.3.2
Filtering
Filtering is a methodology which ensures manufacturability and mesh independence
in density-based schemes. There exists generally three reason, which explain why it
is beneﬁcial to introduce a ﬁlter in a TO algorithm

1. The ﬁlter ensures that the checkerboard pattern is avoided.
2. The ﬁlter ensures mesh-independent solutions.
3. The ﬁlter ensures a structural lengthscale in the optimization problem.
Checkerboard solutions were a problem in the beginning in the ﬁeld of TO. A large
number of mesh independence solution strategies have been developed, and most
of them are described in the TO book of Bendsøe and Sigmund [10] and the paper
investigating various ﬁlter types by Sigmund [35].
The checkerboard pattern is recognized by alternating solid and void elements,
which are connected in the corners only (please confer Fig. 4.1).
Figure 4.1: A topology optimization solution illustrating the checkerboard phenom-
ena.
The nonphysical connection of the elements result in an artiﬁcially large stiﬀness.
The artiﬁcially large stiﬀness is captured by the FE and optimization algorithm, but
the result is nonphysical and the good performance of the design does only exists in
the FE discretization. The disadvantages of ﬁltering is that a grey scale transitions
between the solid and the void regions is created. The occurrence of the band is
undesired in an manufacturing perspective, and the problem can be solved using
robust formulations [35].
In ﬂuid TO problems where the dissipated energy is minimized, the optimization
problems are often well-posed, so that no ﬁltering routines are necessary [2, 11]. The
solid micro structures in the ﬂow are self ﬁltering, because they impose a larger
dissipation of energy than macro-structures, entailing that the numerical solutions
are not prone to numerical instabilities such as mesh-dependency and checkerboard
patterns.
In compliance minimization problems the appearance of the checkerboard patterns
and mesh-dependency are expected, so-forth no regularization is performed. This is
the reason why the subject is considered in present thesis.
The ﬁltering routines introduces a length scale in the solution, which ensure
mesh independent design solutions. The mesh-independence is important because a
ﬁner mesh – in an unﬁltered solution – allows smaller structures. Smaller structures
generally improve the performance of the design and material usage in structural com-
pliance or similar problems. The ﬁltering regularization ensures that the optimized
solution is independent of mesh reﬁnements.

4.3.5
Optimization Algorithm MMA
The problems considered in this thesis are non-convex and non-linear, with makes
high demand on the optimization algorithm, as non-convex problems are diﬃcult to
solve. To deal with this, large scale optimization problems are often divided into
a sequence of explicit sub-problems, which are solved as an approximation to the
original problem.
There exists a large number of optimization routines such as Sequential Linear
Programming, Sequential Quadratic Programming, Convex Linearization and so
on and so forth. The optimization scheme used in present thesis is the Method of
Moving Asymptotes (MMA), which is a popular algorithm within the community of
TO.
MMA is an optimization method based on a special type of convex approximation.
The method was introduced by Svanberg [39], and is an invaluable tool for TO
problems. MMA solves non-linear optimization problems by dividing the problem
into simpler subsproblems which have separable and convex solutions. The genera
non-linear optimization problem is then approximated locally by a convex problem.
The sub-problems are based on the computed sensitivities in the present, foregoing
iteration and other iteration history. The advantages are summarized in the following
list
1. The method handles all kinds of constraints soforth the constraint function
can be diﬀerentiated with respect to the design variable. The derivation can
be both numerical or analytic, but in present project the analytic approach
is used, as relevant analytic derivations are successfully computed using the
adjoint method.
2. The method handles general non-linear programming problems, where it is
able to calculate gradients for expensive function evaluations.
3. The method show excellent convergence properties for complicated problems
with several constraints.
4. The solution show excellent stability in relation to improve feasible solutions.
The MMA is based on a sophisticated convergence routine, which increases and
reduces the convergence rate in relation to some criteria. The agile step size determi-
nation provides a robust stability performance, but it also causes that the method
never provides the exact minimum of the minimization problem. The closer the
solution gets, the smaller the steps becomes, entailing that the exact solution of the
sub-problem is never obtained.
The routine used in this thesis been provided by Svanberg[39] after agreement.
The MMA algorithm is the only code which is not developed by the author.

Interpolation functions for α, E and Ψ
Interpolation schemes was introduced by Bendsøe [8] in the ﬁeld of linear elasticity.
Bendsøe suggested to penalize the elasticity by the power law E(ρ) = ρpE0, where p
is the penalization power and E0 is the stiﬀness of the solid elastic material. The
power law is inappropriate in FSI TO problems for two reasons: Void material has
no stiﬀness which makes the state equations unstable, and the sensitivities are zero
in this in the limits (solid and void). To resolve these problems Stolpe and Svanberg
[38] suggested a so called RAMP interpolation, which introduces a small artiﬁcial
stiﬀness in void material. The interpolation function used in present thesis sharer
the structure as the RAMP scheme.
One of the main diﬃculties in TO of FSI problems is to introduce suitable inter-
polations for the permeability, elasticity and pressure-coupling ﬁlter function. The
interaction between the interpolation functions are not well explored, as only few
papers on the density-based TO of FSI problems have been published. The interpo-
lation functions governs to a large extend the optimization process, as appropriate
choices of interpolation functions typically provide 0-1 solutions.
A lot of eﬀort have maked in density-based TO to determine appropriate interpo-
lations. Gersborg-Hansen et. al. [21] suggested to interpolate the viscosity-diﬀusion
term in the Navier-Stokes equations. The argument was that the viscosity-diﬀusion
term is the counterpart to the elasticity in the Navier-Cauchy equation. In spite of
the intuitive hypothesis the success was limited.
Several interpolation schemes have been investigated in present thesis, but the
best performing is suggested by Borrvall and Petersson [11]. The q-parameterized
interpolation function is

4.4.1
The Continuation Approach
The concept of the continuation approach is to gradually increase or decrease the
penalty factor while solving the optimization problem. The concept is well known in
structural mechanics and heat transfer, where the continuation approach is used to
increase the penalization of the stiﬀness and conductivity. The structural compliance
minimization problem is convex for q = 1, which motivate the continuation approach
to start from unity and hereby increase during the design optimization. The success
of the non-compliance continuation approach is not guarantied but experience tells
that it in general provide good results in practice [37].
The optimization approach is initiated with a large penalization parameter,
which provides smooth and well scaled sensitivity analysis. During the optimization
process the penalization parameters are progressive decreased, which increases the
convergence rate of the design variables toward 0-1 solutions. The penalization

The stability of the model is dependent on the element size. Fine meshes are generally
less robust in relation to large deformations as the mathematical formulation require
that the determinant in the deformation tensors remain positive. Large deformations
are correlated with large element distortions, and large distortions cause large values
in the (2,1) and (1,2) entries of the Jacobian matrices. Large non-diagonal values in
the Jacobian matrix cause the determinant to go toward zero which destabilize the
formulation of the ﬁnite elements.
5.1.2.1
Comment on the Brinkman Penalization Parameter
The choice of Brinkman penalization is heuristically determined, where the choice
of penalization parameter needs to fulﬁll two requirements: The ﬁrst requirement
concerns the magnitude of the pressure which is imposed in the solid. A too small
penalization parameter causes the pressure to diﬀuse through the media too rapidly
(please confer Fig. 3.6) and the deﬂections of the solid domains are underestimated.
The second requirement concerns of the Brinkman penalization which ensures that
the velocity inside solid elements are suﬃciently small. ‘Suﬃciently small’ is a vague
formulation, so to demonstrate the problem the mean velocity in the beam has been
investigated as function of Brinkman penalization in Fig. 5.3.
The relationship is plotted on a double logarithmic scale, and as expected the mean
velocity is decreasing with Brinkman penalization parameter. There are no clear
cut choice of an adequate Brinkman penalization. The upper limit of the Brinkman
penalization is therefore determined by the range in which the transition from
permeable to impermeable of the solid domain are occurring (please confer Fig. 3.6).
The choice of Brinkman penalization parameter is a compromise between appropriate
physical modeling, numerical stability and scaling of the sensitivities. A Brinkman
penalization parameter which penalize ﬂuid velocities in solid domains to a ﬂuid
magnitude around 10−4 are typically providing the best results.

5.3
Minimization of Structural Compliance
This section considers the minimization of structural compliance in the obstacle
subjected to a perpendicular ﬂow problem. Compliance problems require more
eﬀort than dissipation problems as the interaction between the objective function
and the governing physics are more outspoken. Compliance problem embrace the
interaction between the drag and the strength of the structure, where dissipation
problems primary considers the drag (unless large deformations in the structural
domain is considered as in Sub-subsection 5.2.1.1). Dissipation problems in the
FSI framework for stiﬀstructures are almost stand-alone ﬂuid problems. Structural
compliance problems embrace the compliance and the drag of the structure which
constitute a communal interaction as more material doesn’t necessarily entail a better
performance which is the case in pure compliance problems.
The sensitivities in compliance and displacement FSI TO problems requires
adequate scaling. The sensitivities are scaled by tuning the structural stiﬀness and
permeability so that the computational domain undergoes moderate displacements
(approximately 10−4 −10−2). The dependency between stiﬀness, permeability and
Reynold number makes the parameter tuning cumbersome and time consuming. The
optimized design are presented for diﬀerent Reynolds number, but the problems

parameters. The permeability penalization is stopping the ﬂow in the entrance of the
design domain and the ﬂuid pressure is almost not imposed which makes the scaling
the the sensitivities of the FSI problem inadequate. The almost zero sensitivities
cause the optimized design to end up in intermediate conﬁgurations.
The second type designs are characterized by a lot of upstream material in the
design domain (see for example design solutions #0, #1 and #29). Common for the
examples are, that the permeability and pressure-coupling penalization are large,
which creates large deformations and hereby large sensitivities in the upstream part of
the design domain. Due to the large pressure-coupling penalization, the sensitivities
are captured and hereafter rectiﬁed by the optimization algorithm. The material
layout is adequate in relation to the sensitivities but the designs end up in poor
minimum conﬁgurations for diﬀerent choices of penalization parameters.
The third type of designs are seen in #18 and #19. The designs are characterized
by a large amount of downstream material. This type of designs are driven by the
small pressure-coupling continuation parameter which imposes the forces directly on
the wall. The pressure-coupling penalization is so non-linear that the advantage of
relaxing the wall by placing material upstream in the design domain is not captured.
The wall is hereby relaxed most eﬃciently by placing material downstream in the
design domain.
The fourth type of optimized designs are for example seen in #15 and #16. The
designs are characterized by a pyramid shape and are generally the best performing
designs.
The design process are governed by the small permeability, moderate
pressure-coupling and large stiﬀness penalization parameters. The ﬂuid forces are
imposed in the wall but the sensitivities are still scaled so it is eﬃcient to optimize
the structural drag and structural strength simultaneous.

Chapter 6
Discussion
In present chapter the capabilities and limitations TO FSI framework is discussed. I
will comment in the diﬃculties and problematic which have been encountered during
the project, and ﬁnally I suggest possible directions of further work on the subject
of TO of FSI problems.
6.1
Comments on the Modeling
Density-based TO rely on repeated FE analysis, for which reason the accuracy,
reliability and relevance of the physical modeling is crucial. TO results based on
inaccurate physical modeling are useless. To formulate the FSI problem there are in
general three governing assumptions, which has signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the modeling.
In the following sections I will comment on these assumptions.
6.1.1
Assumption about Geometric Linearity
The structural stiﬀness of the Navier-Cauchy equation is assumed geometric linear.
The element distortions due to structural displacements are therefore neglected,
causing that the element integration of the structural stiﬀness is carried out in the
undeformed domain. The assumption is unnecessary when considering the eﬀort
required to derive and implement the geometric non-linear framework. Only one out
of seventeen element matrices are linear for which reason another nonlinear matrix
is manageable. A geometric non-linear implementation ensures correct structural
stiﬀness for large deformations, which will beneﬁt the TO optimization models where
the ﬂuid forces are large compared to the structural stiﬀness.
TO of non-linear structures have been investigated in [14, 13] where the geomet-
rically nonlinear behavior of the structures are modeled using a total Lagrangian
ﬁnite element formulation. The modeling of geometric nonlinear structures in TO of
FSI problems have, as far as I am informed, never been applied.
A geometric non-linearity implementation is adequate in order to apply the FSI
TO method on engineering applications such as the design of a keel or an airfoil as
many applications operate under large structural displacements.
6.1.2
Interpolation Functions and choice of parameters
The TO process is to a large extend governed by the penalization updating scheme,
penalization parameters and the choice of interpolation functions. The optimization
problem presented in present thesis are non-convex, which means that the optimized
solution is dependent on initial conditions and choice of various penalizations param-
eters and schemes. The quality of an optimized design are generally governed by two
conﬂicting parameters: The computational time and the quality of the design. Expe-
rience has taught that slow convergence (narrow upper and lower bound of the MMA
algorithm and small penalizations of qα, qE, qΨ) generally provide better optimized
Page 74 of 111
==PAGE:83==
Chapter 6: Discussion
designs. To obtain a satisfactory design is therefore based on a heuristically process,
where a signiﬁcant amount of time is used on determining appropriate parameters.
To determine appropriate updating scheme are a diﬃcult task, which rely on a
fundamental discussion about how the physics and the optimization algorithm best
captures the characteristic of the physics.
6.1.3
The Brinkman Penalization
The Brinkman penalization term in the Navier-Stokes equation enables solid and
impermeable domains to be modeled in a monolithic formulation but it is also
concerned with some non physical issues as the diﬀusing pressure through solid
domains. Elements have porous media behavior in the upper and intermediate
Brinkman values which to some extend can cause non-physical behavior of the
mathematical model. The Brinkman penalization term is necessary in order to carry
out density-based TO, but it is concerned with computational and physical modeling
diﬃculties.
6.1.4
The Pressure Coupling Filter Function
The Navier-Cauchy equation is reformulated, so that traction forces on the surface
can be considered as volume forces in the domain. The surface pressure is modeled
as a volume force, which to some extend are inappropriate but necessary in order to
carry out density-based TO of FSI problems. The pressure-coupling ﬁlter function
interpolate the volume pressure in the intermediate regime between ﬂuid and solid,
which insures small nodal displacements in the ﬂuid domain. The pressure-coupling
is necessary to enable the formulation of the Navier-Cauchy in the uniﬁed domain,
but more research is required to determine the inﬂuence of the pressure coupling
ﬁlter function on the optimized design solutions.
6.1.5
Matlab Implementation
All FE and topology element matrices are derived from tensor operations, which
have been diﬀerentiated and manipulated in the framework of Maple. The inverse
Jacobian matrix (please confer Eq. 2.6) causes a drastic increase in computational
time, as the element matrices are exported to Matlab format with Maple’s code
generator operation. Primary driven by the inverse Jacobian matrix, the number
of terms in the element matrices are drastically increased which for some matrices
exceed 1,000,000 terms. Exporting the code from Maple to Matlab generate matrices
which are formulated on more that 5,000 lines of code, which is time consuming
for Matlab to evaluate. Despite the usage of diﬀerent code generation optimization
techniques, no good solution to this problem was established.
A adequate solution to the problem is to derive the element matrices using
‘matrix multiplication’ approach, which is applied in many FEM related papers and
books (see for example reference [3, 16]). This implementation requires quite a lot of
debugging eﬀort, but it will decrease the computational time due to the evaluation of

the long Maple outputs. The largest eﬀort is still in evaluating the tangent matrices,

A faster Matlab implementation will allow ﬁner discretization of the problems.
The problems presented in present have been rather coarse, due to the computational

6.2
Physical Relevance of Results
The physical relevance of the results presented in this thesis are questionable due
to the number of dimensions modeled the coarse discretization and the choice of
material properties the low to moderate Reynolds numbers.
6.2.1
Two and Three Dimensional Modeling
The modeling is conﬁned to two dimensions which questionable the physical relevance
and application of the results. All results obtained are intuitive, and does not provide
any better design which isn’t obtainable by simple engineering intuition. Despite the
limited physical relevance of the results, which do not have any value beside academic
interest, the problems still provide many useful observations and experiences which
can be used for further developments.
A three dimensional implementation of the method will provide a large amount of
exciting applications, as optimization of FSI problems in large scale three dimensional
models are arduous and almost impossible to design close to optimal by intuition. In
this occasion a material distribution method is adequate. To implement the method
in three dimensions is straight forward, as all derivations have been carried out and
documented. A three dimensional formulation call for a parallel implementation,
which will allow large scale models to be analyzed.
Such implementation will
drastically increase the amount of applications of the method, as common engineering
applications such as wind mill blades, airfoils and yacth keel can be analyzed with
details that is not provided by alone standing ﬂuid or structural TO solvers.
6.2.2
Low to Moderate Reynold Numbers Models
Engineering application are generally operating under high Reynold numbers, where
the results presented are limited to moderate Reynolds numbers. The physical and
engineering application relevance of the results are therefore conﬁned especially for
the creeping ﬂow simulations. The computational time increases with the Reynold
number as the non-linearity of the Navier-Stokes equations increase, for which reason
more Newton and Pichard iterations are required to solve the state equations. For
suﬃcient large Reynolds numbers, the systems become unstable which means that
no steady state solution exists, and the presented framework is not suited for solving
these systems. The upper limit of Reynold numbers are problem dependent but
typically above 1000 for very simple problems and less for complicated problems
(Re < 40).
Page 76 of 111
==PAGE:85==

The Newton solver provide a better framework for solving the state equations that
the Picard iterations, but unfortunately the Matlab implementation of the Newton
solver is so in-eﬃcient that it has only been used to deﬁne the tangent matrix in
the adjoint problem. Despite the limited usage of the solver, it has been tested
on several problems, where it shows good convergence quantities. A fast Matlab
implementation of the Newton solver will deﬁnitely outperform the Pichard iteration
method in the necessary computational time to solve the state equations.
6.3
The Fluid-Structure Coupling
The FSI problem presented in present thesis are strongly coupled which means that
the ﬂuid is dependent on the structure and the structure is dependent on the ﬂuid.
The Jacobian matrix is a focal point in this coupling. The FSI TO framework call for
a geometric non-linear implementation so large deformations can be modeled. A good
example to consider is a structure subjected to a ﬂuid load which undergoes visually
large deformations. Relieving the load, the structure is returning to its underformed
state, which has a visually diﬀerent conﬁguration that its deformed counterpart. Such
results will deﬁnitely prove that the FSI coupling is captured by the TO algorithm,
which rectify the elastic behavior of the structure and optimizes it in its deformed
conﬁguration. The diﬀuser problem for large structural deformations show such
behavior but more convincing results are desired. I am convinced that the coupling
is captured in present implementation, but I would appreciate to have more visual
indisputable results. To obtain such result will require a lot of parametric tuning
eﬀort, as the deformations can’t enter the unstable regime during the optimization
process, but the ﬁnal results still need to provide visually large deformations.
6.4
Suggestions to Further Work
In this section, I have summarized suggestions for further research which can mature
the FSI TO method against the goal to established a method capable of analyzing
engineering applications in the industry. The suggestions are described in details in
previous sections of present chapter and summarized in the following list
1. A three dimensional derivation and implementation of the method.
2. A parallel implementation for example in C++ or similar parallel framework
to analyze large scale models.
3. A ‘matrix multiplication’ derivation of the element matrices to speed up
computations.
4. Implementation of geometric non-linearity.
5. A parameter study to tune the algorithm to determine adequate model param-
eters.

Conclusion
The aim of the project is fulﬁlled as the thesis contribute with new details, ideas and
demonstrative examples to the ﬁeld of TO of FSI problems. TO of FSI problems
are not very well documented in the literature and present thesis contribute with
a detailed description of the derivation, implementation and application of the
framework.
The problems presented in the thesis conﬁnes itself to two dimensions and
moderate Reynold numbers and the results remain only of academic interests. Though,
it is predicted that the of the methodology, by further development, can be applied
in realistic optimization problems such as the design of a keel blade or an airfoil.

algorithm suggests two diﬀerent designs in order to minimize the dissipated energy.
The design problem is dependent on the Reynold number, where two diﬀerent
design types are achieved: The ﬁrst design type connects the inlets and outlets by
straight pipes. This design is seen viscous shear dominated in the small length-to-
height and inertia dominated model. A signiﬁcantly diﬀerent topology is achieved
in the second design type. The viscous shear stress dominated ﬂow are collected in
a single pipee as the shear stress-ﬂuid volume ﬂow ratio is smaller for wider pipes.
This ﬂuid behavior is captured in the sensitivities of the TO problem, which causes
