Abstract 
The rapid rise of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) has highlighted the need to find new 
antibiotics and drugs that allow us to face a global health problem. Efforts in the field of 
natural products (NPs) have tried to develop new strategies to expand our knowledge 
about the biosynthetic potential of microorganisms. These compounds encoded in 
Biosynthetic Gene Clusters (BGCs) can be classified into different types depending on 
the structural characteristics of the molecules they encode for and a large fraction of 
them remain undiscovered. Bioinformatics and genome mining techniques have 
emerged as essential tools to decipher the biosynthetic capabilities hidden in genomes 
and metagenomes, but the increasing volume of sequencing data and the bias generated 
by the known genomic fraction is hindering current studies. In this project I propose a 
computational workflow that integrates gene clusters (GCs) generated by AGNOSTOS 
and state of the art genome mining tools and databases like Antibiotics and Secondary 
Metabolite Analysis Shell (antiSMASH) and Minimum Information about a Biosynthetic 
Gene Cluster (MIBiG) together with Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques and 
Machine Learning (ML) to delve into the unknown biosynthetic fraction of genomes and 
metagenomes. This approach encodes genomic unknown biosynthetic regions as 
strings of ANGOSTOS GCs and generates embeddings which capture biological 
meaningful information and are then used to analyze and classify those genomic regions. 
The results obtained indicate that this workflow can effectively group unknown genomic 
regions with similar biosynthetic potential and suggest the use of resistance genes as 
potential biomarkers in genome mining tasks. The practice of embedding genomic 
regions shows successful results and can be a good strategy to develop new ML 
approaches for the discovery of new BGCs.

væsentlige værktøjer til at dechifrere de biosyntetiske egenskaber skjult i genomer og 
metagenomer, men den stigende mængde af sekventeringsdata og bias genereret af 
den kendte genomiske fraktion hindrer nuværende undersøgelser. I dette projekt foreslår 
jeg en beregningsmæssig arbejdsgang, der integrerer genklynger (GC'er) genereret af 
AGNOSTOS og avancerede genome mining værktøjer og databaser som Antibiotics and 
Secondary Metabolite Analysis Shell (antiSMASH) og Minimum Information aobut 
Biosynthetic Gene Cluster (MIBiG) sammen med Natural Language Processing (NLP) 
teknikker og Machine Learning (ML) for at dykke ned i den ukendte biosyntetiske fraktion 
af genomer og metagenomer. Denne tilgang koder genomiske ukendte biosyntetiske 
regioner som strenge af ANGOSTOS GC'er og genererer indlejringer, som fanger 
biologisk meningsfuld information og derefter bruges til at analysere og klassificere disse 
genomiske regioner. De opnåede resultater indikerer, at denne arbejdsgang effektivt kan 
gruppere ukendte genomiske regioner med lignende biosyntetisk potentiale og foreslår 
brugen af resistensgener som potentielle biomarkører i genomminedriftsopgaver. 
Praksisen med at indlejre genomiske regioner viser succesfulde resultater og kan være 
en god strategi til at udvikle nye ML-tilgange til opdagelsen af nye BGC'er.

1. Introduction 
The development of high-throughput sequencing techniques and their application to the 
field of metagenomics has enabled the acquisition of enormous amounts of genomes, 
metagenomes, and metagenome-assembled genomes (MAGs) [1] (Figure 1). Exploring 
these extensive data using bioinformatic approaches has uncovered vast new 
information related to unculturable microorganisms and unexplored genomic regions. It 
has allowed the recovery of hidden functional and phylogenetic diversity in broad 
microbial populations and the identification of many genes whose products are linked to 
the production of molecules of great interest in the pharmaceutical and medical industries 
[2, 3, 4]. 
 
Figure 1. The number of bacterial and archaeal genomes sequenced each year and submitted 
to NCBI until 2021. Source: GenBank prokaryotes.txt, file downloaded May 2022. 
However, most of the analyses carried out using genomic data continue to be biased by 
the knowledge that we have about bacteria and other microorganisms [1, 4], e.g., the 
distribution of sequenced genomes is skewed towards a few phyla that contain model 
microorganisms such as Escherichia coli and Bacillus subtilis, known genes with well-
characterized protein products, genes and proteins with annotations in widely used 
databases, etc. [1]. This situation affects not only bacteria and archaea but also many 
other organisms and studies [5, 6]. In this context, the development of strategies that 
consider uncharacterized genomic data or that delve into it, trying to shed a little light on 
the unknown fraction, is becoming a pressing need to improve our understanding of 
biology, particularly microbiology and microbiomes [4].

2. Background 
The bias generated by the known fraction of biological and genomic data is also affecting 
the investigations in the field of natural products (NPs) and bioactive compounds 
produced by microorganisms because many of the approaches that are being used are 
based on the information that we already know, leaving unknown data aside. 
2.1 Natural products 
Natural products (NPs) represent a large group of diverse chemical compounds with a 
broad variety of biological activities and a wide-ranging origin, as they have been 
discovered from bacterial, fungal, plant, and animal sources [7, 8]. A lot of the NP found 
in bacteria and fungi are products of their secondary metabolism, meaning they are 
molecules that are not required for the survival of the host, but which provide some 
advantage to the host in its native environment. They are thought to play important roles 
in defensive, predation, and mediation of eukaryotic hosts interactions [9, 10]. The 
interest of academia and pharmaceutical companies in these compounds is due to their 
multiple uses in human and veterinary medicine (antibiotics, immunosuppressors, anti-
cancer drugs, etc.), agriculture (herbicides, insecticides, etc.), food additives, and 
cosmeceuticals [7, 11]. 
2.1.1 Early discovery methodologies 
Initially, when some of the first NPs were discovered (e.g., penicillin, actinomycin, 
streptomycin, etc.) (Figure 2), phenotypic screenings were used: cell lines were exposed 
to a test compound or organism, and the effect was assessed using different methods 
(such as solvent extracted culture broths deposited on filter paper discs that were placed 
on agar plates containing a susceptible organism), but without prior knowledge of the 
mechanism of action of the compound [7]. This approach, with minor variations 
depending on the final use application, was used between the 1940s and 1970s. From 
1970 to 2000, the processes used to discover NPs remained unchanged, but 
advancements in recombinant DNA and other technologies enabled the rapid 
development of biochemical assays to detect NPs in screening samples, transforming 
the previous phenotypic screenings into target-based screening approaches. In target-
based drug discovery, the target of interest is first identified (e.g., an enzyme involved in 
a specific disease), and then compounds are screened to find those with high binding 
affinity to the target [12]. During this period, the sources of NPs was expanded and new 
types of samples beyond soils were analyzed, which included marine environments [7, 
13].

2.1.2 Genomic techniques and Biosynthetic Gene Clusters (BGCs) 
During the 2000s, the development of genome sequencing technologies allowed to 
obtain detailed information about the genomic nature of NPs [7]. The synthetic 
instructions to produce these compounds are encoded in physically co-localized genes 
known as Biosynthetic Gene Clusters (BGCs) [7, 8, 14]. These gene clusters typically 
encode one or more enzymes that synthesize the core structure of the molecule, some 
enzymes that modify the core structure, and other proteins such as transporters and 
transcription factors (TFs) [15]. When the first bacterial genome sequences were studied 
(especially those from the genus Streptomyces), it was revealed that many more BGCs 
are encoded in these large genomes than predicted from expressed secondary 
metabolomes. Some of these studies concluded that less than 10% of BGCs are 
expressed in sufficient quantities to be detected under routine analyses and that many 
of them require special conditions to activate the production of their final compounds [7, 
16]. The development of bioinformatic, genetic and analytic tools has contributed to new 
approaches and strategies for discovering BGCs and has manifested how the ability of 
microorganisms to produce bioactive compounds has been enormously underestimated 
[7]. Moreover, research on BGCs can also contribute to reduce production costs of both 
natural and engineered products because it has been observed that some BGCs contain 
elements that can affect the production of bioactive compounds [16, 17].

2.1.3 Importance and application 
The identification of new BGCs and the isolation of their final bioactive products have 
been essential in medicine and pharmacology, allowing the treatment of infectious 
diseases and the development of modern medical procedures, such as organ 
transplantation and open surgeries [18]. It is estimated that half of the approved drugs 
for the clinic come from natural products and their derivatives [7, 19]. Their rich structure 
and bioactive potential have also been influential in the field of agriculture, where many 
NPs have shown great capacity to control pests because of their excellent activity and 
environmentally friendly features compared to chemically synthesized pesticides [13]. 
However, the rapid rise of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) has changed the situation and 
the number of untreatable infections increases every day (Figure 2). Improvements in 
the use and application of antibiotics and new therapies, e.g., phage therapy, have been 
proposed as potential solutions of this issue. In this context, the search for new NPs has 
regained some attention specially for treating antibiotic-resistant bacteria infections that 
lack remedies nowadays. This has made the discovery of new BGC a pressing need 
where new bioinformatic tools for genome mining are essential [18]. 
2.1.4 Biosynthetic Gene Cluster classes 
BGCs classes are defined based on the type of NP they produce. Here are described 
the most well-known and popular classes of NPs: 
2.1.4.1 Polyketides (PKs) 
Polyketides (PKs) form a group of NPs with structurally diverse and complex carbon 
skeletons (including polyenes, polyphenols, macrolides, enediynes, and polyethers). 
Their biosynthesis is carried out by polyketide synthases (PKSs), encoded in the 
corresponding BGC. PKs usually are biosynthesized through the decarboxylative 
condensation of malonyl-CoA and then PKSs further modify the different chains in the 
molecule to produce bioactive NPs. These enzymes are classified into three types 
depending on the organization of their catalytic domains. Type I PKSs use a modular 
strategy, where each module oversees different catalytic steps. Types II and III have 
their catalytic sites organized into separate proteins. Some examples of PKs are 
macrolides, erythromycin A, tetracyclines, or azithromycin [19, 20, 21, 22].  
2.1.4.2 Non-ribosomal peptides (NRPs) 
Non-ribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPSs) are a family of enzymes that work 
assembling acyl substrates into non-ribosomal peptides. They can use a wide range of 
acyl monomer substrates (amino acids, fatty acids, and hydroxy acids) with different 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
topologies. The NRPSs macromolecules use a modular, assembly line synthetic logic: 
they are organized into modules where each module adds one specific amino acid to the 
final peptide product. Nowadays, some NRPSs have also been reported to have a 
nonmodular organization, with domains encoded in separated proteins [22].  There are 
many subtypes of NRPSs depending on the number and order of modules, but most of 
them include these three domains: adenylation domain, peptidyl carrier protein domain 
and the condensation domain. This last domain catalyzes the amide bond formation, an 
important step in peptide elongation. They are popular because they synthesize many 
different drugs with pharmaceutical applications (enterobactin, cyclosporin, actinomycin 
D, etc.) [19, 22, 23]. 
2.1.4.3 Ribosomally-encoded and post-translationally modified peptides 
(RiPPs) 
RiPPs biosynthesis starts with the ribosomal synthesis of a precursor peptide, which 
usually has an N-terminal leader region that is bound to a particular biosynthetic protein 
through a recognition sequence and a C-terminal core region that receives post-
translational modifications to become a mature RiPP. The biosynthetic strategy of these 
molecules is also modular based and accepts different substrates. These compounds 
are known for their extraordinary biosynthetic malleability, but their poor solubility and 
bioavailability have hindered their clinical deployment. This group includes lasso 
peptides, lanthipeptides, thiopeptides, and cyanobactins [24, 25].  
2.1.4.4 Oligosaccharides 
Oligosaccharides are a class of carbohydrate polymers composed of two to ten 
monosaccharide units that are linked together by glycosidic bonds. The skeleton of these 
molecules can be quite diverse due to the diversity of monomeric sugars and modified 
side chains. They are known for their potent and selective bioactivity and some of them 
have been developed to treat bacterial infections. Examples of oligosaccharide NPs are 
moenomycins, orthosomycins, saccharomicins [26]. 
2.1.4.5 Terpenoids 
Terpenoids demonstrate a large diversity of structures and functions, but they all share 
common five-carbon precursor units of isopentenyl diphosphate and its double-bond 
isomer dimethylallyl diphosphate. Two different pathways have been identified in 
bacteria to produce these precursors: the mevalonate pathway (MVA) and the 2-C-
methyl-D-erythritol 4-phosphate (MEP) pathway. It has also been observed that the 
plasticity in prokaryotic organisms to produce terpenoid precursors has been greatly 
underestimated and many of these products are essential in important biological 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
processes such as cell wall biosynthesis, membrane function, and electron transport. 
Some secondary metabolites (SMs) of prokaryotic organisms have terpenoid origin and 
pharmaceutical and biotechnological relevance [27]. 
2.1.4.6 Bacteriocins 
Bacteriocins are antimicrobial proteins or peptides synthesized by ribosomes that may 
be modified (class I bacteriocins) or not (class II bacteriocins) by enzymatic complexes. 
Class I bacteriocins are typically classified in the group of RiPPs. Bacteriocin BGCs 
include genes encoding for the bacteriocin itself and/or enzymes involved in the 
biosynthesis, export proteins, self-resistance mechanisms and regulators. Bacteriocins 
are popular for their small size and relatively high stability and diffusibility. They show a 
high diversity, and whereas some are active only against closely related members of the 
same species or genus, many other bacteriocins have shown to have broader activity 
and affect unrelated bacteria [28]. 
2.1.4.7 Alkaloids 
Alkaloids are low-molecular-weight compounds that contain nitrogen and in most cases 
are derived from amines produced by the decarboxylation of amino acids like histidine, 
lysine, ornithine, tryptophan, and tyrosine. Some alkaloids have caught the attention of 
pharmaceutical companies because of their high biological activities. Although plants 
produce most alkaloids, it has been observed that some bacteria also have BGCs for the 
production of alkaloids and some of them have been identified in bacteria living in 
rhizosphere systems [29, 30]. 
2.2 Bioinformatic approaches in BGCs research 
Considering the significant impact of the antimicrobial resistance increase on human 
health, in the past decade, genomic methods have been developed and used to search 
for novel BGCs [18] [31]. Moreover, the improvement of genome sequencing 
technologies has drawn the attention of pharmaceutical industries and research groups 
as they allow to detect and obtain new drugs in a faster and cheaper way [31]. 
2.2.1 Genome mining 
The identification of previously uncharacterized BGCs within the genome of sequenced 
organisms and the sequence analysis of the enzymes encoded by these genes and the 
experimental identification of specialized metabolites is known as genome mining [31]. 
Genome mining depends on computing power, bioinformatic tools, and publicly 
accessible databases containing nucleotide/protein sequences and annotations. Well-
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
organized and standardized databases are essential in genome mining to ease the 
annotation and identification of genes in every new genome that is sequenced [31].  
2.2.2 Databases used in genome mining 
Many efforts have been made in bioinformatics to continuously improve databases and 
the way they organize the data. One example of this is the Genome Taxonomy Database 
(GTDB) which covers 94,759 bacterial genomes and corrects the taxonomy of many of 
them by using genome-based taxonomy classification methods [32]. Another example in 
the field of BGCs and natural products is MIBiG 2.0 (Minimum Information about a 
Biosynthetic Gene Cluster) that was initially established in 2015. It is a standardized 
deposition of BGCs and their association with molecules of known chemical structure, 
which also facilitates its curation and storage [33]. A summary of the last updates added 
to MIBiG 2.0 in 2020 are shown in Figure 3. In genome mining, the knowledge on 
previously characterized genes and in this case, BGCs, is fundamental. When working 
with data from proteins or genes involved in NP biosynthesis that have already been 
collected in these kinds of databases, the identification of biosynthetic pathways in which 
they might be involved is a straightforward process [31]. Most of these databases are 
publicly available and their website can be used to compare target gene clusters with 
known gene clusters and predict their potential function and structure. In the last decade, 
the performance of some in silico approaches that have been developed has been 
strengthened by these databases and the effort of many scientists collecting and 
annotating BGCs and their products [31]. Homology criteria are commonly used when 
comparing new data with the genes and proteins stored in the databases; however, this 
approach can lead to erroneous results when working with BGCs data because many 
biosynthetic enzymes share similarities in their sequences but participate in different 
chemical processes and pathways leading to completely different final products [31].

2.2.3 Genome mining tools in the field of BGCs discovery 
Early approaches for identifying BGCs relied on simple reference alignment techniques 
using traditional bioinformatics programs like BLAST, paired with manual curation of 
BGCs [8]. Later, the discovery and identification of new BGCs were enormously 
promoted by developing and applying rule-based algorithms [8, 11, 31]. The most 
important ones are:  
❖ Antibiotics and Secondary Metabolite Analysis Shell (antiSMASH) was launched 
in 2011 and has become the most broadly used tool in bioinformatics for detecting 
and characterizing BGCs in bacteria and fungi. It offers public and free web 
service and a standalone open-source tool. antiSMASH identifies many different 
biosynthetic pathways involved in secondary metabolite production using a rule-
based approach. It can perform more detailed predictions for several types of 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
BGCs and offers a wide range of optional analyses that can be selected by the 
user [34].   
❖ Prediction Informatics for Secondary Metabolomes (PRISM) is a computational 
resource that can predict chemical structures using a comprehensive genome-
guided approach. It was launched in 2015 and offers a web application [35]. It 
compares the input genetic information with 57 enzymatic reactions using 
different algorithms [31]. 
❖ Integrated Microbial Genomes Atlas of Biosynthetic gene Clusters (IMG/ABC): a 
web database containing predicted and experimentally verified microbial BGCs 
[36]. Version v.5.0 of this system uses antiSMASH to predict BGCs in an 
extensive collection of genomes that includes metagenome bins [37]. 
2.2.4 Advantages and disadvantages of genome mining  
Genome mining has emerged as the most crucial approach for discovering BGCs in the 
last decades [11], but its wide use has exploited many of its capabilities and shown some 
of its limitations (Table 1).

The development of many genome mining tools with user-friendly web interfaces has 
brought complex computer methods closer to users without bioinformatics experience or 
skills, turning the identification of BGCs into a common process in many laboratories that 
do not require expensive equipment [31]. However, when the user wants to delve deeper 
into the field of BGCs, it might become a need to acquire higher bioinformatics 
knowledge and considerable computational resources, especially when we scale the 
number of genomes that will be analyzed [11].  
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
On the other hand, genome mining allows for detecting many BGCs even when 
analyzing a few genomes or contigs, particularly when analyzing members of the 
Actinomycetales order [38]. Also, the analysis of the enzymatic products encoded in 
BGCs and the biosynthetic logic they show has eased the prediction of chemical 
structures when identifying new regions with biosynthetic potential, but these final 
products must always be empirically tested to assess their capacity [31]. Finally, one of 
the main disadvantages of genome mining is that it is only based on known BGCs, which 
restricts the discoveries of wholly unknown and unclassified genomic regions [31]. 
Despite this, genome mining is an evolving technique, and some approaches are being 
developed to cover the unknown fraction of the BGCs space [4]. 
2.2.5 New bioinformatic approaches in the field of BGCs discovery 
In recent years, scientists have tried to face some of the limitations of genome mining by 
proposing new strategies that tackle the problem of large-scale analysis. High-
throughput sequencing has opened the door to extensive genomic analysis and genome 
mining of the entire data known in a bacterial genus, extensive strain collections, and 
metagenome-assembled genomes (MAGs) datasets. Widening the study of BGCs to 
entire taxonomic groups of organisms (or entire ecosystems) can provide important new 
knowledge, such as the identification of thousands of BGCs and their distribution within 
the tree of life, finding BGCs with high degrees of similarity when compared to the ones 
already identified and BGCs that encode non-annotated enzymes [11]. 
One of the approaches that have been used to study the complex biosynthetic diversity 
of BGCs has been to generate gene cluster families (GCFs) using comparative 
architectural relationships that group BGCs sharing similarity, e.g., similar product 
chemotype, and sequence similarity [11]. The computational workflow proposed by 
Navarro-Muñoz et al., 2020 [11] was based on this idea, where the software tools 
biosynthetic gene similarity clustering and prospecting engine (BiG-SCAPE) and core 
analysis of syntenic orthologues to prioritize natural product gene clusters (CORASON) 
were integrated with antiSMASH and MIBiG. In this case, BiG-SCAPE was used to 
perform rapid calculation and exploration of BGC sequence similarity networks, and 
CORASON included a phylogenomic approach to analyze the evolutionary relationships 
between gene clusters [11]. 
The use of GCFs to group BGCs has been useful to deepen the biosynthetic diversity of 
different bacterial strains and ease the path in the field of metabologenomics. GCF can 
also be used as functional biomarkers in human health and ecology. In summary, 
grouping BGCs has been a successful strategy to reduce the dimensionality of large-
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
scale genomic datasets and smooth the analysis processes [39]. However, the 
increasing number of BGCs continuously deposited in public databases boosts the need 
for more computationally efficient methods that scale better with the genomic data 
available now and in the future. Same authors of BiG-SCAPE proposed Biosynthetic 
Genes Super-Linear Clustering Engine (BiG-SLiCE) in 2020, which projects BGCs into 
Euclidean space to use a partitional clustering algorithm that runs in a time complexity 
near linear [~O(n)]. This approach makes it possible to analyze extensive BGCs datasets 
in a feasible time [39]. 
On the other hand, some scientists have focused on developing methods targeting 
previously unknown BGCs. In contrast with those approaches that used reference-based 
methods, these new algorithmic proposals have started using machine learning 
techniques that can explore the unknown biosynthetic fraction of genomes [40]. In this 
direction, ClusterFinder was published in 2014. A hidden Markov model-based 
probabilistic algorithm finds BGCs by converting nucleotide sequences into strings of 
Pfam domains with a probability assigned to each of them. As nature assembles the 
same enzymatic domains to produce different NP, the use of Pfam domains in 
ClusterFinder has allowed the identification of new classes of BGCs [41]. In 2019, 
DeepBGC was proposed as a new strategy that addressed some of the limitations of 
HMM-based algorithms (like ClusterFinder) of only remembering partial sequence 
genetic information and its incapability to capture high-order interaction patterns between 
entities [40]. DeepBGC implemented a Bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (Bi-
LSTM) model and protein family (Pfam) domain embedding representations. Using this 
approach, DeepBGC can capture short- and long-term dependency between contiguous 
and distant genomic entities and has demonstrated to outperform ClusterFinder in 
identifying BGCs in several samples of bacterial genomes [8, 40]. This model was 
improved in 2021 by embedding multi-source protein family domains and integrating 
customizable strategies in a new framework named Deep-BGCpred. This method has 
predicted more BGCs, decreasing the number of false positives that usually accompany 
other machine learning-based methods [8]. 
Previous studies that have started to apply Deep Learning (DL) and Machine Learning 
(ML) techniques to deepen into the unknown fraction of genomes and protein domains 
required the application of Natural Language Processing (NPL) techniques [40, 42, 43, 
44]. These techniques are used to encode biological data in a meaningful way 
compatible with the mathematical operations used in DL and ML models [42]. A good 
representation that uses dense vectors and preserves context is word embeddings, 
which associate words with the analogous context in a document to similar regions in a 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
vector space. They assign a vector of real numbers to each different word in a corpus 
(text collection) [43]. Word2vec is one of the most popular algorithms used to generate 
word embeddings. It is a model that embeds words in a lower-dimensional vector space 
using a shallow neural network. This process results in a set of word-vectors 
(embeddings) where vectors close together in the vector space have similar meanings 
based on the context, and word-vectors distant to each other have differing meanings 
[43]. These algorithms have been applied to biological objects like proteins and genes 
and they have been successful in increasing our knowledge about the unknown fraction 
in biology [44]. For example, in the work of Buchan & Jones, 2020 [44], word2vec 
embeddings generated using Pfam protein domains could be used to assign new gene 
ontology (GO) terms to domains of unknown function (DUFs), suggesting function roles 
for unannotated Pfam domains. 
The fact that genome mining is only based on the known fraction of genomes and MAGs 
is also becoming an important issue if we consider that between 40% and 60% of the 
new genes that are identified cannot be assigned to a known function [4]. This 
uncharacterized fraction is generally excluded from the analyses, restricting results and 
conclusions to conserved pathways and known functions [4]. However, many times, it 
has been claimed that a substantial biosynthetic potential within microorganisms remains 
unexplored [39] and the inclusion of anonymous data might be an excellent strategy to 
unravel some of this unknown capacity. 
Bioinformatics plays an essential role in this context because experimental methods are 
laborious and can significantly benefit from the shortcuts that computational analysis can 
confer [4]. In 2022, Vanni et al. [4] published AGNOSTOS, a computational workflow that 
generates, validates, and refines gene clusters (GCs) and classifies them into four 
categories: Known with Pfam annotations (K), Known without Pfam annotations (KWP), 
Genomic unknown (GU) and Environmental unknown (EU) [4]. It has been proposed as 
an approach that integrates the known and unknown genomic fractions of metagenomic 
studies to specific genomes using GCs. The GCs produced by AGNOSTOS have a 
highly conserved intra-homogeneous structure in terms of sequence similarity and 
domain architecture homogeneity. It has proven the integration and analysis of the 
unknown fraction to be possible, increased the understanding of the unknown functional 
fraction of genomic datasets and motivated the development of methods for its 
experimental characterization [4]. 
Following this logic, AGNOSTOS GCs present an excellent opportunity to encode 
regions, involved in the biosynthesis of NPs, in microbial contigs as strings of GCs that 
are susceptible to being embedded using NLP techniques and could be potentially used 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
to expand our understanding of the unknown biosynthetic fraction of genomes and 
metagenomes. 
2.3 Aim of the project 
This project aims to explore up-to-date genes of unknown function reservoirs in BGCs 
and examine their genomic location and context to see if there is any relationship that 
can be used to develop an alternative approach to expand our current understanding of 
the microbial biosynthetic landscape. The inclusion of genes of unknown function in the 
BGCs identification analysis may broaden and facilitate the obtaining of more precise 
and complete results, which could mean the discovery of new antibiotics or other 
substances with potential applications in human health. 
The main hypothesis of this project is that the integration of AGNOSTOS GCs (A-GCs), 
MIBiG, and antiSMASH can be used to enhance our knowledge about the unknown 
biosynthetic fraction of microbial genomes. In the approach proposed, the genomic 
environment of seed GCs obtained through the ANGOSTOS workflow in [4] and 
classified as GU and EU will be used to perform a rapid screening of contigs to find 
regions with potential unknown biosynthetic capability (Figure 4). This hypothesis will be 
tested during the realization of this MSc Thesis project: 
• 
GCs classified as genomic unknown (GU) and environmental unknown (EU) by 
AGNOSTOS and integrated with MIBiG 2.0 will be selected (seed GCs). 
• 
The contigs where these GCs are located will be analyzed using antiSMASH to 
determine which of them are in genomic regions where antiSMASH is not 
detecting BGCs. 
• 
The genomic environment of these seed GCs will be extracted (10Kbp upstream 
and 10Kbp downstream). 
• 
The genes and their genomic environment will be translated into ANGOSTOS 
GC IDs and transformed into strings generating a corpus of genomic fragments. 
• 
This corpus will be embedded using the natural language word embedding 
technique word2vec, following an approach like the one described by Buchan & 
Jones, 2020 [44]. 
• 
The embeddings generated in the previous step will be used to cluster the 
genomic fragments using Hierarchical Density-Based Spatial Clustering of 
Applications with Noise (HDBSCAN). 
• 
The clusters generated will be analyzed, looking for patterns that could be used 
to identify unknown BGCs, and the results will be biologically interpreted to 
==PAGE:22==
21 
 
enhance our knowledge of the unknown biosynthetic fraction of microbial 
genomes.

3.3 Selection of unknown seeds candidates 
From the GCs database, including the integration of MIBiG, all the A-GCs containing 
MIBiG genes were retrieved. From this subset, the A-GCs classified as GU (Genomic 
unknown) and EU (Environmental unknown) were selected (seed GCs). This dataset 
containing genes of unknown function that share AGNOSTOS GCs with genes from 
MIBiG entries has all the seed GCs that were analyzed in the project and can be 
considered the starting point of the analysis.  
3.4 Preparation of validation dataset and unknown dataset 
FASTA files for the genomes where the seeds GCs are located were downloaded from 
NCBI (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/). Using the GCs database from AGNOSTOS, gff3 
format files were prepared for each genome containing the coordinates for each of the 
genes in the AGNOSTOS database. The gff3 files only contained information for those 
contigs where unknown seed GCs were found and were prepared using a python script. 
antiSMASH standalone version 6.0.1 [34] was run in all the genomes with the customized 
gff3 files (--genefinding-gff3 option) using a Snakemake workflow [45]. Two different 
datasets were prepared from the results: a validation dataset and an unknown dataset. 
3.4.1 Validation dataset 
The validation dataset was prepared by selecting all the seed A-GCs located in the 
regions with a hit (BGC prediction) from antiSMASH. A schematic representation of this 
process can be found in Figure 8. The genes that have a fragment inside the region 
detected by antiSMASH are also included. 
3.4.2 Unknown dataset 
The unknown dataset was prepared by selecting all the seed GCs located in regions that 
did not have a hit from antiSMASH. The adjacent genomic regions of these seeds were 
extracted using the data from the A-GCs database and a python script using the 
datatable python package (https://github.com/h2oai/datatable). The adjacent regions 
extracted are 10Kb long and include those genes that have a fragment inside the region. 
A schematic representation of this process can be found in Figure 8.

3.5 Corpus building logic and steps 
Using the datasets described in 3.4.1 and 3.4.2, a corpus of genomic fragments was built 
following a similar approach as the one described by Buchan & Jones, 2020 [44]. Genes 
were grouped in fragments; in the validation dataset each fragment corresponds to a 
genomic region identified by antiSMASH, and in the unknown dataset each fragment 
corresponds to a seed GC and its genomic environment or several seeds and their 
genomic environment if the adjacent regions were overlapping (Figure 6). Each gene 
was translated to an A-GC ID and the fragments were represented as strings 
constructing a corpus for each of the datasets.  
 
Figure 6. Scheme of how seeds and their genomic environment were represented using A-GCs. 
(A) When seeds did not overlap with any other seed’s upstream and/or downstream regions they 
were simply represented as the sequence of AGNOSTOS IDs in the upstream and downstream 
region. (B) When the upstream and/or downstream regions of different seeds had overlapping of 
some A-GCs they were encoded in the same fragment imitating the real relative position of each 
gene in the contig (avoiding repetitions of genes). Following this logic, the seeds and their 
genomic environment were treated as strings where each A-GC ID is a word. 
3.6 Word2vec embeddings model 
The corpus was embedded using gensim python package, which implements the 
word2vec family of algorithms using highly optimized C routines [46]. Word2vec is a 
model that embeds words in a lower-dimensional vector space using a shallow neural 
network. The result is a set of word-vectors (embeddings) where vectors close together 
in the vector space have similar meanings based on the context, and word-vectors 
distant from each other have differing meanings. From the algorithms available in the 
gensim python package it was used the Word2Vec Skip-gram model, with a window size 
==PAGE:26==
25 
 
of 5, embeddings size of 100 and a minimum word count of 0 (all words would be 
positioned in the vector space). The lower bound for the size of the embedding was 
calculated following the indications in Patel & Bhattacharyya, 2017 [47] using the Parallel 
Maximum Clique library developed by Rossi et al., 2013 [48]. With a lower bound of 10 
for the data in the project, several embeddings’ sizes were tested (20, 50, 100, 120) and 
a final value of 100 was selected like the authors in Buchan & Jones, 2020 [44].   
3.7 Calculation of distances between embeddings and HDBSCAN clustering 
Using the word embeddings for each A-GC generated using the Word2vec Skip-gram 
model, the embeddings vector for each fragment was calculated as the mean of the 
embeddings for all the A-GCs in the fragment in a specific position (Figure 7).  
The distances calculation between the embeddings of each fragment and the HDBSCAN 
clustering were performed using the hdbscan python package [49]. According to the 
authors' suggestions, the distance was calculated using the L2-norm and Euclidean 
distance as a similar approach to cosine distance [49]. The cluster selection method 
parameter was set to eom (Excess of Mass) as the preferred method for the selection of 
flat clusters from the cluster tree hierarchy. 
 
Figure 7. Scheme of how the embeddings for each fragment were calculated. The fragment

the A-GCs embeddings. The numbers and A-GCs shown in the figure are fictitious and were 
created solely to exemplify the process. 
Scaled cosine similarity between 0 and 1 was used instead of cosine similarity (ranging

3.8 Lineage-specificity analysis 
The F1-score developed for AnnoTree [50] was used to identify the lineage-specific GCs 
and to which rank they are specific. An A-GC is considered to be specific if it is present 
in less than half of all genomes and at least 2 with an F1-score > 0.95 [50]. This analysis 
was run in R using an adaptation of the code used by Vanni et al, 2022 [4].

The graphs included in the project were made using Matplotlib library from python, 
ggplot2 system from R and Anvi’o [51]. For the Anvi’o’s plot the clustering of the H-GCs 
patterns was performed using Ward’s minimum variance method (with dissimilarities 
squared before clustering) in R. The basic data wrangling was performed mainly using 
Pandas and Numpy libraries from python and tidyverse from R.

4.1 BGC detection using antiSMASH 
antiSMASH was run in all the contigs containing seeds GCs to see which of them are 
located in regions with a hit from antiSMASH (predicted BGCs) and which are located in 
regions without a hit. According to the A-GCs database, the 1,744 unknown seeds 
(containing 184,107 genes) are located in 19,896 genomes (and 87,000 different contigs 
within those genomes). antiSMASH was run on these genomes using annotation files in 
gff3 format comprising the coordinates of the 184,107 genes in the seeds. Of the 19,896 
genomes analyzed in antiSMASH, there were four that raised an error (Appendix A), 
and they were finally included in the later analysis as if they had no hits from antiSMASH. 
The results obtained show that from the 19,896 genomes analyzed (four were 
removed/classified as no hit) 7,360 showed a hit from antiSMASH (Figure 9) and 12,532 
did not. If we have a look at the contig level, we see that 7,360 contigs showed a hit 
(same number as genomes because only one contig showed a hit in each genome), 
which means that 8.46% of the contigs showed a hit whereas 91.54% did not. The fact 
that some seeds are placed in contig regions that did not have a hit from antiSMASH 
suggests that some of the contigs might have more biosynthetic potential that 
antiSMASH is not detecting.

4.4.2 Method validation  
To validate the method proposed in this project, the validation dataset containing the 
fragments identified by antiSMASH was also used to build a corpus of fragments, 
generate embeddings using word2vec Skip-gram model, and perform HDBSCAN 
clustering in the same way as explained for the unknown dataset. The BGC class 
assigned by antiSMASH to each of the fragments was tracked in the H-GCs to see if the 
method could cluster together fragments that had similar biosynthetic potential. The 
results show that the approach proposed can group genomic fragments that encode

5. Discussion 
5.1 Technical aspects and workflow improvements  
The vast majority of the contigs analysed in this project did not have any hit from 
antiSMASH and there are several hypotheses proposed to explain this. The first idea 
could be that these contigs do not contain any BGCs; however, in the AGNOSTOS and 
MIBiG integration dataset, their seeds contained BGC genes from MIBiG, suggesting a 
potential biosynthetic capacity on them. If it is the case that these contigs contain 
completely unknown BGCs, the rule-based method used by antiSMASH would have no 
==PAGE:44==
43 
 
information to detect these potential BGCs, because the information necessary to detect 
them might remain undiscovered yet. 
However, as antiSMASH has included the ClusterFinder tool in their project [34], it would 
be interesting to repeat this part of the analysis, including this option, to see if it can 
identify more regions. Moreover, ClusterFinder uses genes encoding for enzymes 
commonly found in the context of the BGCs, some of the seeds in the dataset could be 
good candidates for this, as they have some A-GCs in their genomic environment that 
contain genes encoding for different enzymes (Figure 16, Figure 17). 
In Figure 10, we can see some conserved patterns of A-GCs on both sides of the seeds 
and that these patterns are more evident in the regions closer to them. Regions 
separated by 13Kb or more from the center of the seeds show much less conserved 
patterns, suggesting that filtering the most extreme regions of the fragments could 
improve the later results by removing noise. Applying this filtering step could be a way of 
refining the method proposed and should be tried with different sizes (e.g., filter all the 
A-GCs separated by 13Kb, 14Kb and 15Kb from the center of the seeds) to see if it 
brings improvements. 
Word2vec Skipgram model gave successful and reasonable results and some previous 
studies have used this approach to encode genomic sequences in a similar way [42, 43, 
44]. However, the approach proposed in this project has two limitations that should be 
considered in further investigations. Using around 10Kbp of the regions surrounding the 
seeds to build the fragments can be a good starting point because this way, the potential 
biosynthetic regions of the contigs are ‘enhanced’ and we eliminate the noise that other 
genomic regions could add. For example, suppose we had longer fragments containing 
information linked to other biological functions unrelated to the secondary metabolism 
and NP production. In that case, they could potentially be classified as similar in the 
same cluster, even when their biosynthetic genomic information is very different. 
Horizontal transfer processes in bacteria have evidenced that organisms from the same 
genera might have different biosynthetic capacities [53] and these differences could be 
masked if we encode longer fragments using more genomic information. However, using 
less genomic information for the encoding can also hinder the process of clustering 
similar fragments if they have relevant genomic information in regions not included in the 
fragment. 
Another limitation of the approach proposed in this project that was handled in the work 
of Buchan and Jones, 2020 [44] is that fragments have been encoded without adding 
gap regions. In their sequences, Buchan and Jones included extra ‘words’ to encode the 
==PAGE:45==
44 
 
gaps between protein domains (DOM1 - DOM2 - G300 - DOM3, being G300 a gap of 
300 bp). Including gaps in our analysis could be a way of adding more information to the 
encoding and a good starting point for future tests. 
The HDBSCAN clustering manifested that a high percentage of the contigs (62.81%) 
could not be assigned to any cluster. According to the similarities distribution in Figure 
15, we can see that most of these fragments had a lower scaled cosine similarity among 
them compared to the ones that were clustered. There might be multiple reasons for this 
and some limitations of the approach used. The first explanation could be that these 
contigs do not share relevant patterns in their A-GCs that can be used to group them.  
The results of this project highly depend on the input AGNOSTOS database data taken 
from Vanni et al., 2022 [4]. The fact that some fragments cannot be clustered can also 
be because the input data does not contain enough seeds with similar adjacent regions. 
Moreover, the mean length of the BGCs in MIBiG [33] is 38Kbp and the mean length of 
the fragments in the unknown dataset is around 21Kbp. This means that many fragments 
have a size much lower than many BGCs collected in MIBiG, which could be why these 
fragments are classified as noise in HDBSCAN (they do not contain all the relevant 
information to cluster them with similar fragments). The fragments' size depends on the 
seed's position in the contig, i.e., if a seed is located at the beginning or the end of the 
contig, it will have less genes surrounding it because the method only extracts the 
adjacent regions located in the same contig. However, this limitation is difficult to solve 
in future analyses because it directly depends on the performance of genome 
assemblers’ algorithms, which is out of the scope of this project.  
On the other hand, some of the parameters used in the HDBSCAN clustering could be 
refined to improve the results. The largest H-GC in both unknown and validation datasets 
had an unexpectedly high diversity in terms of scaled cosine similarity between the 
fragments and the biosynthetic classes provided by antiSMASH. Also, when looking for 
patterns using n-grams, it was found that most of the fragments contained in this cluster 
did not share a consecutive pattern of A-GCs as the other H-GCs tended to do. The 
reason for this diversity can be attributed to the method used for the flat clusters selection 
from the cluster tree hierarchy inside the HDBSCAN algorithm. In this project this 
parameter was set to use the Excess of Mass method that according to the authors 
documentation tends to pick one or two large more heterogeneous clusters and then 
several small more homogeneous extra clusters [49]. If it is preferred to perform a more 
fine-grained clustering, it is suggested to use the leaf method, which will select the leaf 
==PAGE:46==
45 
 
nodes from the tree in the algorithm, producing a higher number of small homogeneous 
clusters [49]. 
Another thing that could be improved in the analysis of HDBSCAN clusters is the use of 
n-grams to investigate potential patterns in the fragments. N-grams are useful to look for 
strict consecutive patterns, e.g., if we consider the fragment sequence GC1-GC2-GC3 
and we look for tri-grams in our data, we will be able to detect all those fragments where 
the sequence GC1-GC2-GC3 is found as well, but we will miss the fragments that look 
like this GC1-GC4-GC3, which might also be relevant. Word2vec embeddings can catch 
similarities between contigs that do not share a strict consecutive GC pattern, so when 
looking for patterns in the selected HDBSCAN clusters, it can happen that clusters with 
several fragments do not show relevant consecutive patterns, and it does not mean that 
they do not have them, but that the technique used here has some flaws in when used 
for this purpose. To inspect these clusters, a potential alternative could be using 
frequency scatter plots as done in Figure 10 to see if we can detect relevant spaced 
patterns. 
Despite method refinements and improvements, the results of the validation dataset 
clustering show that genome fragments can be encoded using A-GCs and transformed 
into embeddings using word2vec Skip-gram model and that these embeddings capture 
enough biological information to group BGCs sharing the same biosynthetic class. 
5.2 Biological hypothesis and findings 
According to the results obtained, when looking for strict consecutive patterns in terms 
of A-GCs in the data, we see that some fragments have several GCs related to transport 
processes, osmotic stress protection strategies and enzyme deactivation. The fact that 
potential resistance genes are found in genomic regions around seeds that have been 
linked to BGCs genes seems to coincide with the resistance hypothesis in BGCs. This 
hypothesis states that within BGCs there is at least one gene that confers resistance 
against the bioactive compounds that the organism produces [53]. Some of the main 
resistance mechanisms found in bacteria are drug efflux and enzyme deactivation, which 
agree with the function of some A-GCs found around the seeds (Figure 16, Figure 17). 
These results strengthen the approach used in AGNOSTOS. It seems that the genes 
grouped in the same A-GCs with MIBiG BGCs (i.e., the MIBiG integration in the 
AGNOSTOS database) have elements in their surrounding genomic regions that support 
this allocation.  
Following similar reasoning, some of the most frequent patterns in some of the H-GCs 
seemed to support the duplication hypothesis in resistance genes. In this case, the 
==PAGE:47==
46 
 
resistance gene located in the BGCs shares a high sequence similarity with a crucial 
gene that participates in a primary function in the organism [53]. Many antibiotics share 
common target essential sites, like ribosomes or nucleic acids synthesis enzymes, that 
are also present in the producer. Thus, if the producer has a very similar copy of the 
target sequence that is expressed when the antibiotic is produced it will protect itself by 
providing excess targets or targets with greater binding affinity [53]. Again, many of the 
most frequent patterns found are related to purines and pyrimidines synthesis processes, 
cell-wall synthesis and electron transporter domains, supporting this hypothesis (Figure 
16, Figure 17). 
Moreover, some of the most frequent patterns in some H-GCs encoded information for 
genes involved in the generation of pili used during the conjugation process in bacteria. 
These findings are also interesting because they support the horizontal gene transfer 
hypothesis in BGCs. Horizontal gene transfer is a phenomenon that often occurs among 
bacteria as a strategy to gain genetic advantage [53]. This hypothesis states that at least 
one of the genetic elements in BGCs has been horizontally acquired across species 
because NPs production is commonly used to gain an ecological advantage [53]. The 
fact that some seeds have elements related to this process in their genomic environment 
supports this hypothesis. 
The findings in this project suggest that investigating the biological role of the genomic 
environment of unknown seeds can give relevant information about potential biomarkers 
that could be used to detect BGCs. Many of these biomarkers seem to be related to 
resistance processes in bacteria. 
Most of the genomes where antiSMASH found hits had BGCs candidates to produce 
terpenoids as SM. It is important to note that the function linked to some of the genes in 
the genomic environment of the seeds also suggests that these regions might be 
encoding information to produce terpenoids. Terpenoids play key roles in many biological 
processes in prokaryotic organisms: cell-wall biosynthesis, membrane functions, 
electron transport, and some other processes [27]. Some of the A-GCs surrounding the 
seeds had Pfam annotations related to electron transport proteins and the production of 
bacterial polymers, thus suggesting that enzymes to produce terpenoids might also be 
encoded in these regions. 
The proposed method can also provide relevant information about the phylum 
distribution within the H-GCs and the lineage-specificity of the A-GCs. This knowledge 
can be used to select genomic regions of interest that might be specific for a particular 
==PAGE:48==
47 
 
phylum (or rank) or to select genomic regions that might contain more conserved 
patterns and genes throughout the tree of life.  
5.3 Future perspectives 
Expanding the use of the approach proposed to larger datasets like the one used by 
Gavriilidou et al., 2022 [50] that included the catalog of 52,515 metagenome-assembled 
genomes prepared by Nayfach et al., 2021 [51] can be a good starting point to integrate 
the unknown fraction in genome mining tasks. The combination of the known and 
unknown fraction in future analyses is presented as an interesting alternative to expand 
our knowledge about the biosynthetic capacities of microorganisms and potentially find 
new NPs with applications in human health. 
After realizing this project, a potential next step could be to use this approach to train a 
DL model and perform different tasks. One example could be to train a transformer to 
process the A-GCs strings and use the model to classify regions of the genome that 
could potentially be BGCs. Transformers are neural networks that can learn context and 
meaning in sequential data like word strings [55]. They have been previously used for 
bioinformatics tasks and have reported very successful results: AlphaFold2 has been 
used to process amino acid chains to describe how proteins fold [56] and MegaMolBART 
from AstraZeneca has been used in the field of cheminformatics for drug discovery [57]. 
Another example of future applications could be generative adversarial networks (GAN). 
GAN is a ML model in which two neural networks compete to improve the accuracy of 
their predictions. It comprises a generator (convolutional neural network) and a 
discriminator (deconvolutional neural network), the first manufactures artificial outputs 
based on a training dataset, and the second filters the outputs by assigning them an 
authenticity probability [58]. This approach can be used to produce synthetic BGCs that 
could be used in genome mining tasks to find new unknown biosynthetic regions in 
genomes or to design novel synthetic BGCs. 
The approach proposed in this project can also be used as a starting point for laboratory 
experiments, where specific unknown genomic regions are cloned in microorganisms to 
empirically look for new NPs products with potential applications in human health and 
agriculture.

6. Conclusions 
The work presented in this MSc Thesis evidences that the word2vec Skip-gram model 
embeddings of genomic regions encoded using A-GCs contain sufficient biological 
meaning to classify different types of BGCs. Not only this, but the clustering of these 
embeddings using distance metrics can group unknown genomic fragments sharing 
similar biosynthetic characteristics. The integration of A-GCs gives relevant information 
about the Pfam annotations of GCs included in potential biosynthetic unknown regions, 
suggesting interesting biomarkers that could be useful in future studies. Many of these 
biosynthetic biomarkers seem to be related to resistance mechanisms in 
microorganisms. Further investigations and laboratory experiments are necessary to 
deepen these hypotheses and increase our current knowledge of BGCs.  
This work presents new insights into the unknown fraction of bacterial genomes and 
suggests new strategies for identifying potential BGCs. It also strengthens the proposal 
of using resistance genes as potential markers in genome mining tools, as suggested by 
Tran et al., 2019.
