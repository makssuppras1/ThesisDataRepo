Abstract
This thesis investigates the modeling and identiο§¹cation of underwater vehicles. A virtual reality environ-
ment, called V-Rep, has been used to implement different simulation setups. V-Rep is a robotic simulator
which is free under educational license and which features and characteristics allow the user to implement
models of different robots, from robotic arms to aerial vehicles, and the performance of the models can be
tested in virtual environments. In these simulation setups, a model of a low cost and open source underwater
robotic platform, OpenROV, has been implemented. These simulation setups represent the performance of
the robot in different scenarios. The objective of implementing these simulation setups is to use them to
carry out tests in this virtual environment before the experiments with the true robot are conducted. The
simulations, which are performed in the virtual environment, will help the user to test and debug the control
program before conducting the experiments with the robot.
An underwater vehicle needs a precise control of velocities and forces in order to carry out a complex task,
such as inspecting pipes to ο§¹nd cracks in their structure or to carry a tool to a certain place. A mathematical
model which accurately represents the dynamics of the vehicle is necessary to design the control systems.
System identiο§¹cation deals with the estimation of the parameters in the estimated models and the veriο§¹cation
of the estimated models. Different models which represent the dynamics of the system in different scenarios,
when the vehicle moves forward, maneuvers in the horizontal plane or dives, have been identiο§¹ed and
veriο§¹ed.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The deployment of different offshore technologies increases year by year in order to extract the natural
resources to satisfy the market needs. Offshore windmill farms generate electricity out of the marine wind
streams and oil ο§¹elds extracts oil. On the other hand, the application of offshore technologies implies chal-
lenges because structures and pipes need inspections, maintenance, and repairs in order to keep operations
running at all times. For example, at the North Sea oil platform Siri the discovery of cracks in the platform
support structure have cost the operator thousands of millions of euros since 2009, due to the reduction in
the output (Christensen et al. 2015).
Unmanned Underwater Vehicles (UUVs) are vehicles which operate without any human occupant and
they can be classiο§¹ed into two different categories:
βÄΔ Remotely Operated Vehicles (ROVs): The robot is tethered and connected to a "mother ship" from
which it is operated. The cable is used to carry power, transmit commands to the ROV, a video stream
and sensor readings to the operator.
βÄΔ Autonomous Underwater Vehicles (AUVs): The robot is autonomous and is not connected to the
surface through any cable.
UUVs have different applications in engineering research, oceanographic research or military missions,
and ROVs and AUVs present advantages and disadvantages. ROVs are physically connected to the operator
and based on the live information sent by the vehicle, the operator can monitor the procedure and the
occurrence of any failure. On the other hand, ROVs are limited in range and velocity and they can not
operate under bad weather conditions. AUVs have an extended operational range and no operator is needed
to control the vehicle, but the duration and complexity of the missions are limited.
UUVs can accomplish complex tasks in maintenance of underwater structures by inspecting them,
detecting cracks and repairing them. The increase in sea depth where offshore ο§¹elds are deployed and the
limitation in the space and maneuverability, make it more difο§¹cult for human divers to carry out these tasks.
UUVs have been used to work in this kind of harsh environments to avoid the risk of losing any human life.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.1.1
Context of the project
The maintenance of offshore installations is a key aspect in keeping these installations working properly,
and the operator reduces losses due to a poor performance. The big size of some windmills farms (for
example, the Anholdt farm in the North Sea is composed of 111 turbines), requires a big effort in inspecting,
maintaining and repairing. It would take a long time for a single UUV to check all the structures, and
during bad weather conditions, time spent may be longer because the UUV cannot operate under unsafe
circumstances. The same scenario may occur in other installations such as oil ο§¹elds, power cables or
underwater telecommunication installations.
This project is a part of a bigger research activity which aims to create a collaborative underwater system
composed of different modular robots, swimming and legged, which work together to solve a certain task.
OpenROV is the swimming robot which has been chosen, and its main characteristics are explained in
detail in Appendix A. Modular robots are those which have the ability to adapt their shape and functions to
changing environments and demands. This kind of robots has several advantages compared to traditional
robots, such as self-assembly, self-repair and scalability (Murata and Kurokawa 2007). By changing the
conο§¹guration of the robotβÄôs actuators, a single robot may carry out different tasks, instead of having speciο§¹c
robots for each speciο§¹c task. In an underwater environment, self-reconο§¹gurable robots can dock together
such that one robot carries another to the place which needs to be inspected or repaired, shown in Figure 1.1a,
or several robots are attached together to carry a heavy object which could not be moved only by one of
them, shown in Figure 1.1b.
(a) Structure inspection carried out by different robots.
Courtesy of (Christensen et al. 2015).
(b) Transportation of an object by several robots. Courtesy
of (Christensen et al. 2015).
Figure 1.1: Examples of applications of self reconο§¹gurable robots in underwater environments.
The different robots which compose the swarm, would work together to accomplish the maintenance of
the installation more efο§¹ciently, reducing the time spent in carrying out the mission. A hub, where the robots
can dock when they are low on battery and recharge themselves, would be a part of the system. While the
robots are in the hub, they can also send data collected by the sensors to the on-shore system and receive
commands from the on-shore system. During this time, in case of different actuators or a new conο§¹guration
of the robot is necessary to perform the next mission, the robots can reconο§¹gure themselves.
By processing the readings from the sensors, the robot can detect not only a fault in itself, but also a
fault in the whole system. Therefore, robots may help each other to keep the system operating properly, for
example a swimming robot detects that one legged robot is out of battery and carries it back to the hub.

1.2. PROBLEM STATEMENT
3
1.2
Problem Statement
The goal of this project consists of modeling an underwater robot in a virtual reality environment and
performing system identiο§¹cation of an underwater vehicle.
Experiments involving robots are resources and time consuming. The control program has to be tested at
different steps of the implementation to verify that the system works as supposed, and the experimental setup
has to be carefully planned to get all the necessary information out of the tests. By using a virtual reality
environment, in which a model of the robot has been implemented, the design of the control program can be
tested at different benchmarks to monitor and debug the implementation. The virtual reality environment
chosen to implement a simulation setup is V-Rep, and its main features and characteristics are explained in
Section 3. The simulation setup consists of an OpenROV model which represents the behavior of the real
robot during different maneuvers. By implementing a simulation setup, the performance of the robot may be
tested before carrying out the experiments and the program system may be debugged.
UUVs work in a harsh environment and under difο§¹cult and changing circumstances, because they are
affected by sea currents and need to avoid obstacles. The tasks performed by these vehicles are complex
and a high precision level is required. Therefore accurate and robust control of forces and velocities of the
vehicle is necessary (Karras et al. 2013). The identiο§¹cation of the mathematical model which represents the
dynamics of the system is a key aspect in the design of the control system. Two different approaches can be
considered to estimate the model of the system:
βÄΔ Off-line system identiο§¹cation: Data is collected during the experimental phase and then processed to
obtain the model of the system. This approach requires less processing power, but the model will not
be able to adapt to a change in the robot performance.
βÄΔ On-line system identiο§¹cation: Data is collected on the ο§²y and processed while the robot is running. It
requires more processing power, but the model is updated when the system dynamics changes.
In this project, the approach followed is off-line system identiο§¹cation in order to implement the routines
which identify different models of an underwater vehicle. These models represent the dynamics of an AUV
which is performing different maneuvers. The necessary data to estimate the parameters have been generated
by using the Matlab Guidance, Navigation and Control Toolbox (Perez and Fossen 2009).
1.2.1
Scope of the thesis
The scope of this thesis can be summarized as follows:
βÄΔ Investigate the theoretical models of an underwater vehicle.
βÄΔ Study the system identiο§¹cation and model validation techniques which can be used to estimate the
mathematical model of an underwater robot.
βÄΔ Research about how to implement a model of the underwater vehicle in a virtual reality environment.
βÄΔ Implement different models of an underwater vehicle in a virtual reality environment to represent
different scenarios.
βÄΔ Identify the parameters which compose the mathematical model of an underwater vehicle and assess
the model, based on the veriο§¹cation techniques.

The mathematical theory explaining the identiο§¹cation of a model of a certain system and the validation

models, many research articles have been published about off-line and on-line techniques. Two different
methods for the off-line identiο§¹cation of the parameters were compared by (Ridao et al. 2004), where

A stable online identiο§¹cation technique for the identiο§¹cation of ο§¹nite-dimensional dynamical models is
described by (Smallwood and Whitcomb 2003). A global derivative-free optimization algorithm which
estimates online the dynamic parameters of the vehicle, is presented in (Karras et al. 2013), while in (Hong

2.2
System Identiο§¹cation
Mathematical models provide information about the behavior of a system, and based on the model, the
response to a certain input, the system stability or the frequency and time behavior are analyzed. The model
is not always available but it can be computed by using different techniques. System identiο§¹cation deals
with the problem of computing the model by using collected data from both the input and the output (Ljung
1999). Not all systems have the same characteristics, therefore different techniques of modeling, such as
linear time-invariant systems or non-linear systems, are used. In order to characterize a system there are
different approaches:
βÄΔ Black box model: No prior knowledge about the model structure is known and the data is used both to
model the structure and estimate the parameters.
βÄΔ Grey box model: Even though the structure of the model is known, the parameters of the system are
estimated by using the collected data.
βÄΔ White box model: Prior knowledge about the system dynamics is used to build the mathematical
model.

Simulation Environment
The design of a control system in order to make a robot to carry out certain tasks is time consuming
and difο§¹cult, and its development consists of several implementation steps and tests. The difο§¹culty of the
development of a control system can be reduced by using a robotics simulator. The time spent in carrying
out tests may be reduced and as many experiments as necessary may be done during the implementation,
so the program is debugged easily. Besides, the simulation environment lets the designer have perfect
knowledge of the different variables which are involved, such as velocities, forces, torques and distances.
Industrial environments, where robotic arms are used, are hazardous because of the huge forces the robot can
potentially apply to a person in case of controller failure. The implementation and testing of these programs
in a simulation environment, may reduce the risks and avoid situations in which the security of employees
may be compromised. In case many robots are carrying out a task together or if the task is carried out in an
environment which is not easily accessible, the complexity of the experiments is increased and a simulator
will yield a big advantage in terms of reducing the time and resources spent during the implementation.
Different models of robots are available in these kind of programs alongside with models of objects so an
accurate representation of the environment where the task is carried out may be designed, so the control
program can be exported to the real robot.
Different simulators are on the market with different features and characteristics, such as Gazebo, Webots,
V-Rep, MORSE and SimSpark. Some of the features of these programs are summarized in Table 3.1. V-Rep
is the simulator chosen in the project because of:
βÄΔ Free educational license.

βÄΔ Different physics engines to choose from.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusions
In this Thesis, two models of an underwater robot have been implemented in a virtual reality environment.
The simulation setups have been developed in the simulator, V-Rep, to model the performance of the robot
for different maneuvers:
βÄΔ Horizontal simulation setup: Models the robot maneuvers in the horizontal plane.
βÄΔ Longitudinal simulation setup: Models the robot maneuvers when it dives.
Different tests have been conducted by simulation to assess the performance of the robot, such as
accelerating and decelerating the robot in the forward direction, making it to follow an arc trajectory or a
zigzag trajectory. Based on the performed tests, in sections 3.1.1 and 3.1.2, both models perform as expected.
Then, three different models of an AUV were identiο§¹ed using data simulated in the Guidance, Navigation
and Control Toolbox in Matlab. The three models have been estimated to represent the dynamics of the system
during different maneuvers, referred to as the forward speed model, horizontal model and longitudinal model.
Even though the agreement of the true and predicted outputs is considered good enough and the predicted
outputs follow the true outputs, the results show that the linear models are not perfectly describing the highly
nonlinear dynamics of the AUV. Different models have been identiο§¹ed depending on the speed of the AUVβÄôs
propellers. Regarding the horizontal and longitudinal models, according to the results the dynamics of the
system changes depending on the speed of the AUV, thus different controllers may be necessary in order to
control the vehicle properly at different speeds.
Different noise variances were applied to the data in order to assess the performance of the estimation
under different noise scenarios. The greater the noise variance, the poorer the identiο§¹cation of the models.
As the signals become mostly noise, and the routines fail to estimate the parameters of the models properly.

OpenROV
OpenROV is an underwater vehicle designed for educational and exploratory purposes. The robot has
been developed with the intention of being accessible for users who do not have previous experience or a
deep knowledge of underwater robots. Besides, a community consisting of users and designers has been
created to share ideas, get help and work together on future improvements. In order to fulο§¹ll these purposes,
the robot is light weight, low cost, open source and easy to repair. Since the robot is open source, users
can add as many modiο§¹cations to the software architecture as needed, and then publish their work to the
community.
OpenROVβÄôs structure is composed of an outer polypropylene shell and an acrylic inner frame. Besides,

and battery tubes by using straps. The frame is placed into the shell and the robot is held together by using
metal rods, which prevents it from coming apart while it is operating.
OpenROV is provided with an inertial measurement unit (IMU) which includes a 3-axis magnetometer,
a 3-axis accelerometer and a 3-axis gyroscope, which measures the angular velocity, linear acceleration
and the orientation of the ROV, respectively. The measurements are digitized by internal analog-to-digital
converters (ADC), which provide the data to the processor. An on-chip motion processing unit (MPU) is

unit, composed of a second IMU and a pressure sensor, can be attached to the board in order to measure
variations in pressure, and thus determine the depth of the robot.

the vehicle is connected to a computer through a tether, its maximum range is limited to the length of the
tether, 100 meters. The battery life is around two or three hours depending on use, and the camera records
live video up to 1080p which is streamed to the computer through the tether.

Communication
A bidireccional communication between the robot and the computer in established through two 100
meters long tether wires, which is connected on one side to the robot and to an adapter on the other side. The
adapter extends a network connection to the robot by converting the internet connection into a powerline
communication of up to 200 Mbps. The adapter is also connected to the computer and the broadband modem.
As soon as the adapter is connected to the computer, the robot is powered on by the on-board batteries.
Therefore, the robot and computer are connected through an IP network and the user can send commands to
the robot from the computer, while the recorded video is sent to the computer from the OpenROV. Wireless
underwater communications are not easily established due to the high attenuation of the radio waves in water,
this conο§¹guration allows the OpenROV and the computer to transmit and receive data and commands, but on
