Abstract  
Urban environments at the core station areas in the two largest cities in the Municipality of 
Rudersdal, Birkerød and Holte, are currently facing a need of better physical accessibility and 
perception of safety to provide inclusive, safe, and social sustainable public areas for all citizens. 
In Birkerød, it is expected that there will be an increase in the number of citizens with 10 % in 
2023, however decrease in Holte with 2 %. Nonetheless, approx. 40% of the population have at 
any time an impairment that to some extent gives them physical limitations. Additionally, the 
perception of safety can affect everyone. In the worst case, the fear of crime can cause people 
to avoid certain areas.   
This study aims to identify challenges related to the physical accessibility in the urban environ-
ments for people with physical and sensory impairments (temporary or permanent) and the 
perception of safety to make the cities more inclusive, safe, and social sustainable. Specifically, 
it investigates the challenges in the urban environments by involving informants with different 
points of views at different times of day. In this context, challenges are defined as either barriers 
in the urban environments related to the physical accessibility or elements that contribute to 
the fear of crime. Consequently, the study asks the users what they need instead of telling them 
what they need.  
To identify challenges in the urban environments a literature review has been used to provide 
awareness of possible barriers and elements that contribute to the fear of crime, which has 
been found in previous literature and a safety survey. This was supplemented with an empirical 
case study with qualitative data in terms of interviews that identifies challenges in the urban 
environments and quantitative data in terms of Geographical Information System (GIS) anal-
yses. All of which have been used to provide suggestions for improvements.  
The results showed that there are lots of room for improvement in both Birkerød and Holte. 
Also, the involvement of the informants with different points of views was essential to locate 
the challenges along with GIS maps. In Birkerød, numerous of barriers were identified at the 
path between the station and city centre due to the unclear route, the common path shared 
between cyclists and pedestrians, and the conditions in the tunnel. Whereas the barriers in 
Holte were centered at the station due to the heavy door, the missing tactile guidance path and 
tactile writings, as well as the poor signage.  
In terms of the perception of safety, more elements were identified in Birkerød than in Holte in 
both the interviews and the safety survey. It was due to the smell of pee in the elevators, the 
undefined area, and the lack of lightning and shelter at Birkerød Station. As well as the 
==PAGE:6==
6 
 
conditions at the administration center and main library with large, boring, and inactive facades 
and the tunnels that are shielded from the surroundings, has poor acoustics, maintenance, 
lightning, escape, and overview conditions. Whereas only a few elements were identified in 
Holte, which were related to the feeling of being secluded, the undefined area, the poor light-
ning, and the overview conditions near the parking lot.  
These results suggest that improvements need to be conducted by the land register owners, 
the Municipality of Rudersdal, Danish State Railways (DSB) and Banedanmark to provide more 
inclusive, safe, and social sustainable urban environments. To achieve the best possible results, 
the solutions must be made in collaboration.

Definition of Terms 
Terms 
Definition 
Urban environment 
The surroundings of an area with people and infrastructure such 
as houses, commercial buildings, roads, railways, green places etc. 
within a city [1].  
In this thesis, when mentioning the urban environments that are inves-
tigated is meant the routes from the S-train stations to the city centre 
and the nearby areas. 
Urban space 
Urban spaces are the physical spaces that exist between buildings. 
Urban spaces are e.g. streets where people walk, cycle, or drive, 
and squares where many different people meet, such as railway 
stations and smaller parks [2]. 
The urban environments in this thesis thus contain urban spaces in 
terms of streets that can lead people back and forth from the station 
and the city centre, and squares where people can gather e.g. the S-
train stations, and squares near the cities centre. 
Barrier 
A society created obstacle that prevents people from using an ur-
ban space on equal terms as others that affects the physical acces-
sibility. 
Fear of crime 
Place-based  
Unsafety 
A fear of crime (place-based unsafety) that is activated due to con-
ditions at specific areas. Theory states, that it is possible to de-
crease fear of crime by making changes to the urban environment 
to make it harder for potential offenders to commit crime [3].  
Challenge 
Either barriers in the urban environments in relation to the physi-
cal accessibility or elements that contribute to the fear of crime. 
Impairment 
A loss or abnormality of psychological, physiological, bodily struc-
ture, or function [4].  
Disability 
A functional impairment + a society-created barrier [5]. 
Social sustainability 
A process for creating places that promotes wellbeing, by under-
standing the need of the people who uses them [6].

1.0 Introduction 
This section presents the importance of creating inclusive, safe, and social sustainable 
urban environments and the background of why this is relevant in Birkerød and Holte, 
the two largest cities in the Municipality of Rudersdal. The scope and the objectives of 
the thesis are then presented. Which leads to the research question of the assignment, 
around which all research is centered. Finally, the thesis structure is described, which 
provide an overview of the structure and content.

1.0 Introduction 
In today's society, the social sustainability plays a new important role in the planning of our 
cities since the world’s population is constantly growing. To accommodate everyone, the need 
to create inclusive, safe, and social sustainable cities is increasing. This is expressed in the Sus-
tainable Development Goal (SDG) 11 Sustainable Cities and Communities. The SDG 11 is a part 
of the 17 Global Goals by United Nations (UN) that were created in 2015 with the purpose of 
creating a better world by 2030 [7]. Three target goals from the SDG 11, shown in Figure 1, 
describes that in order for cities to become inclusive, safe, and sustainable it is among other 
things essential ensure access for all to basic services (target 11-1), to provide access for all to 
safe, accessible transport systems (target 11-2), and to create universal access to safe, inclusive, 
and physically accessible urban spaces in particular for women, children, elderly people, and 
people with disabilities (target 11-7) [8].  
 
Figure 1 - Three Target Goals from UN Development Goal 11 from [8] 
Consequently, it is important to make sure that the design of urban environments in cities 
strives to ensure access to basic services, transport systems, and green and public spaces for 
everyone. This means, that there should not be any barriers making areas difficult or impossible 
to use for a group of people or areas with elements that create fear of crime.  
It is not only people with disabilities, who may experience barriers in urban environments. It 
also applies for people with temporary impairments, such as injuries or people with prams or 
heavy luggage etc. In addition, we are all getting older, and with age, mobility decreases for 
most of us. Therefore, there is no reason to distinct between people with disabilities and people 
without when planning our cities since everyone can have challenges with their mobility at 
some point in their life [9].

1.0 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
Birkerød and Holte are the two largest cities by population in the Municipality of Rudersdal in 
Denmark with 20,908 and 19,952 inhabitants, respectively [10]. As mentioned, there is a need 
to ensure that everyone can have access to their basic services, transport systems and green 
and public areas, which also applies in Birkerød and Holte. A population forecast from 2021 
shows that the population in Birkerød will increase with approx. 10 % over the next 12 years. 
However, the population in Holte would slightly decrease with approx. 2 %. Nevertheless, it is 
expected that both cities will have an increase in the number of elderly citizens, especially in 
Birkerød [11].  
Additionally, the Danish Association of the Physically Disabled (DHF) states that it is hard to 
know the exact amount of people with disabilities, however it is estimated that approx. 10% of 
the Danish population has a disability [12]. For Birkerød and Holte this corresponds currently 
to approx. 2,000 citizens per city. However, disability is a wide term that can vary a lot from 
person to person and not all disabilities affect the physical accessibility. Nonetheless, approx. 
40% of the population have at any time an impairment that to some extent gives them physical 
limitations [13]. For Birkerød and Holte this would correspond to approx. 8,000 citizens per. 
city. Moreover, almost all citizens will have a temporary impairment at some point in their lives. 
Consequently, everyone can experience barriers that prevent access to certain areas and to 
provide equal access, barriers for people with temporary and permanent impairments need to 
be eliminated. 
Additionally, the perception of safety related to the fear of crime can affect everyone. To ensure 
inclusive and safe access, elements that create fear of crime need to be reduced. Various theo-
ries are based on the fact that it is possible to reduce unsafety in public space by making 
changes to the urban environment to make it harder for potential offenders to commit crime 
[14].   
1.2 Scope 
While the need for better physical accessibility and higher perception of safety in Birkerød and 
Holte is growing, it is unknown which challenges people are experiencing. Hence, the aim of 
this study is to investigate barriers to the physical accessibility and elements that contribute to 
the fear of crime in two urban environments in Birkerød and Holte. The two urban environ-
ments include the route and nearby areas from the transport system (S-train station) to basic 
services (city centre), see Figure 2. The routes, which the urban environments are based on, 
have been limited to 600 m from the S-train stations to the city centres to be within the core

1.3 Objective  
 
The objective is first of all to investigate universal design methods in order to identify barriers 
related to the physical accessibility and to identify elements that contribute to the fear of crime 
based on crime prevention and urban space theories. Secondly, results from a previous safety 
survey will be presented and analysed to locate elements that effect the fear of crime. Hereaf-
ter, an in dept analyse of a total of eleven interviews conducted on location in Birkerød and 
Holte with people with physical and sensory impairments will be described and the results will 
be presented. This provides actual inputs from users, which is supported by the findings in the 
theory. To investigate the reasons for the identified challenges analyses based on the theory 
will be made with the use of Geographical Information System (GIS) and space syntax.

2.0 Method 
In this section, different methods that are used in this thesis will be presented. First, a 
literature review is used to provide awareness of possible barriers for people with phys-
ical and sensory impairments (temporary or permanent) and elements that contribute 
to the fear of crime in urban environments based on previous literature and survey. 
This is supplemented with an empirical case study with qualitative data that identifies 
specific challenges in the urban environments and quantitative data in terms of GIS 
analyses and a space syntax of the urban environments. All of which will be used to 
provide suggestions for improvements in the urban environments. 
 
 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
2.0 Method 
2.1 Literature Review 
To explore and evaluate existing literature on barriers for people with physical and sensory 
impairments (temporary or permanent) and elements that contribute to the fear of crime in 
urban environments different topics have been investigated. First, the physical accessibility and 
the concepts of universal design, also known as inclusive design, are presented. Then two doc-
uments, a guide published by the Ministry of Urban, Housing and Rural Affairs [16] and a hand-
book published by the Danish Road Directorate [13], which are based on universal design meth-
ods, will be used to identify main struggles with the physical accessibility in urban environments 
and how to avoid barriers for people with physical and sensory impairments.  
To state which elements that effect the fear of crime, which has a geographical dimension, the-
ories on perception of safety, crime prevention, and human perception of urban spaces are 
studied. Based on these theories, elements that effect the fear of crime are identified.  
2.1.1 Mapping Land Register Ownership  
The land register ownership is based on data from the Municipality of Rudersdal and are used 
to assign the suggested improvements to the owners who are responsible. 
2.1.2 Survey 
To support the case study in this thesis, a prior safety survey from 2018 with quantitative and 
qualitative data from Passagerpulsen, which is a part of the Danish Consumer Council that 
works for a better public transport and for an increased environmentally friendly mobility, has 
been studied as a part of the literature review [17]. 26 users from Birkerød Station and 17 users 
from Holte Station have been providing information about their perception of safety at the S-
train stations. The data were provided in an Excel-file and to illustrate some the results percent-
ages have been calculated and presented in the section.

Districts: Areas characterized by common characteristics 
- 
The districts that are identified have common characteristics and image.  
Nodes: Strategic focus points for orientation like squares and junctions 
- 
The nodes include the intersections and places where the paths meet. 
Landmarks: External points of orientation, usually an easily identifiable physical object in the 
urban landscape 
- 
The landmarks are characterized by a physical form, includes buildings and statues, which is 
easy to recognize.

3.0 Theory 
3.1 Physical Accessibility and Universal Design 
In order to make the urban environments in Birkerød and Holte more inclusive it is essential to 
investigate the physical accessibility and the concepts of universal design, also known as inclu-
sive design.  
The physical accessibility correlates to the way that people move around in cities to do their 
daily chores. The goal is to ensure that all people have access on equal basis to the urban envi-
ronment, transportation, and information [19]. Thus, it is important to avoid any barriers that 
would make the urban spaces inaccessible, inefficient, or uncomfortably, especially if the bar-
riers are only for a selection of people due to individual abilities. Since people have individual 
abilities and are able to do different things through life, urban spaces have to be adapted for 
everyone no matter the age, gender, or disability to ensure better mobility and equal rights.  
3.1.1 Dimensions of Disabilities 
In this project, disability is defined by a relational concept of disability, which is used by the 
Danish Association of the Physically Disabled (DHF) and the World Health Organization (WHO). 
The concept is that disability should not be seen as a characteristic of the person him/herself, 
but rather as: a functional impairment + a society-created barrier [5].  
 
Figure 7 - Illustration of how an Impairment and a Barrier in the Environment create a Disability from [20] 
Impairments are not fixed nor confined to a particular part of the population and can be both 
permanent and temporary. They can be described as a loss or abnormality of psychological, 
physiological, bodily structure, or function [4]. Some people might require personal aids (such 
as wheelchairs, sticks or rollators), which makes them particularly depended on the design of 
the urban environment.

Disabilities related to functional impairments can be divided into three dimensions:  
- 
Physical impairments (e.g., muscular dystrophy, epilepsy, cerebral palsy).  
- 
Sensory impairments (e.g., deaf or limited hearing, blind or visually impaired). 
- 
Cognitive impairments, which can be divided into developmental (e.g., autism, down 
syndrome, fragile X syndrome) or behavioral or emotional (e.g., ADD, bipolarized, oppo-
sitional defiant disorder) [21].

and sensory impairments in relation to the ability to see and hear. In the following, the concept 
of universal design will be investigated followed by the identified barriers in urban environ-
ments for the selected impairments based on theory.

3.1.2 Universal Design in the Urban Environment 
The concept of universal design was formulated by architect Ronald L. Mace in the mid-1980s. 
He believed that universal design requires an understanding of the needs to be able to achieve 
a common approach to everything we design to make it usable for everyone in the best possible 
way [23].  
Therefore, universal design strives to be as inclusive as possible. Not only focusing on designing 
for people with functional impairments but for all. Thus, it is important to design inclusively and 
to avoid designing based on special needs, as it has been done in the past. The differences 
between special needs and inclusive design are shown in Table 1.

3.2 How to avoid Barriers in Urban Environments  
It is important to avoid unnecessary barriers in urban spaces to create urban spaces that ben-
efit everyone. As described in section 3.1 Physical Accessibility and Universal Design, a disability 
occurs when an impairment meets a barrier in the immediate environment. Hence, it is essen-
tial to gain knowledge of the barriers that may exist. 
In the following, the main struggles in urban environments for people with physical and sensory 
impairments are examined and elements on how to avoid barriers are identified. The identified 
barriers are based on the 7 principles of universal design, a guide published by the Ministry of 
Urban, Housing and Rural Affairs [16] with inspiration and instructions to design urban spaces 
for all, and a handbook published by the Danish Road Directorate [13] on universal design of 
streets, urban spaces, and other traffic areas.

personal aids, such as wheelchairs, crutches, canes, or rollators [16]. In addition, people with

3.3 Perception of Safety 
In this thesis, perception of safety is included, as an important element to ensure that the urban 
environments can be used by everyone and thus that they do not appear dangerous or unsafe. 
Unsafety is a feeling of restlessness directed at something where one may feel worried, anxious, 
or scared, whereas the perception of safety is a feeling of inner calm, carefreeness, and seren-
ity.  
There are many different forms of unsafety. In the context of this investigation it is about the 
unsafety that relates to the fear of crime and the geographical dimension that can be associ-
ated, since the unsafety can be linked to specific areas.  
The unsafety that relates to a geographical dimension is an objective unsafety that corresponds 
to the actual risk of being exposed to crime, whereas a subjective unsafety is about the feeling 
of unsafety in relation to fear of crime that can vary a lot from individual to individual. Beside 
distinguishing between being and feeling unsafe, unsafety can be divided into a concrete and 
inconcrete unsafety. The concrete unsafety is short-term, tangible, and situational, where the 
non-concrete unsafety is persistent, diffuse and underlying. By distinguishing between the dif-
ferent types of unsafety, it is possible to make a targeted effort to strengthen the perception of 
safety, since it makes it easier to locate the unsafety issues in the urban environments.  
Consequently, to increase the perception of safety in the urban environments, it is relevant to 
look into why unsafety occurs. For unsafety to occur there must be three elements present: a 
susceptible individual in combination with an unsafety activator in a place with an absence of 
reassuring conditions that provides safety, see Figure 16 [27].   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 16 - Perception of Safety Triangle 
Unsafety Unsafety 
Unsafety 
Susceptible individual  
==PAGE:40==
40

The susceptible individual is about the fact that there are some individuals who are more likely 
to feel unsafe as they are more susceptible to elements that can create unsafety. How suscep-
tible an individual is may depend on various factors, but it is especially the underlying concrete 
unsafety in relation to the fear of crime. The unsafety may depend on how resourceful the 
individual is and how his/her well-being and health are.  
Whereas the unsafety activator is the element that triggers the unsafety. It can be both the 
geographical area itself, but also signs of crime, disorder, norm-breaking behavior, or certain 
groups of people.  
Lastly, unsafety can only occur if there is an absence of reassuring conditions. As an example, 
a certain situation might not seem unsafe if you are surrounded by friends or family [27].  
3.3.1 Geographical Dimension of Fear of Crime 
The perception of unsafety (fear of crime) is very much about people, but it can also be activated 
due to specific geographic areas. This place-based unsafety can arise both as hotspots of con-
crete experiences of unsafety and as urban areas that are mentally perceived as being danger-
ous and unsafe.  
Consequently, to prevent unsafety, it is essential to look at the area where it takes place in order 
to make targeted safety-creating efforts. Also, the resources will be better used if the efforts 
are focused on the areas where the biggest problems lie.  
Today, it is relatively common to look at unsafety with a spatial perspective and we know which 
elements that makes an area more unsafe than others. On the other hand, it is limited how 
much focus there is on the place-based unsafety, which is the experiences of unsafety that 
relate to a specific area. This is due to the fact that we know very little about how unsafety is 
connected to different areas or which areas, people find unsafe on their daily bases. Instead, 
we know something about the elements that affect the perception of unsafety, which could be 
a dark long tunnel.  
To get a better understanding of how unsafety is connected to a place you can look at the con-
crete and non-concrete unsafety that relates to a geographical location. The concrete unsafety 
could be if you were afraid of being assaulted while waiting for the train because there is a 
group of drunk people being aggressive (see Figure 17). This is place-based since it takes place 
in a specific location. Whereas the non-concrete unsafety could be if you choose a route where 
you avoid a specific part of the city, since you are afraid of being exposed to crime, because you 
find the area dangerous in your mind (see Figure 18).  
==PAGE:41==

Nevertheless, the two different views of unsafety in urban environments are connected, since 
a lot of concrete unsafety experiences might make specific areas into areas that you in the 
future would avoid making it a non-concrete unsafety. Also, if you find the area unsafe, some

3.4 Crime Prevention 
You cannot investigate unsafety (fear of crime) in urban environments without talking about 
crime and what can be done to prevent it. If crime becomes inconvenient, some people will 
most likely feel safer than if the chances of crime are high. In 1979, sociologists Lawrence E. 
Cohen and Marcus Felson defined the routine activity theory, which stated that for crime to 
occur, there must be three elements present: an offender, a target/victim, and a place where 
the crime can take place. In 1994 criminologist John E. Eck further developed the routine activity 
theory by stating that there has to be an absence of: a handler of the offender, a manager of 
the place and a guardian of the target/victim for crime to happen, see Figure 19 [28]. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 19 - Crime Triangle based on [28] 
In short, the offender does not want to be discovered in committing crime. Therefore, the of-
fender often searches for places where there are a low amount of people and where there is 
no one looking out for the place. 
Thus, to improve the perception of safety, it is not only about areas with a lot of crime, disorder, 
and antisocial behavior, but also to a large extent about improving the design of the urban 
environments, so that they do not have poor overview and lighting conditions that contribute 
to make the place attractive for crime. Furthermore, a limited amount of escape routes makes 
it harder to escape from a potential crime. As an example, long passages and underground 
tunnels will most often be described as unsafety environments if there are no reassuring con-
ditions present.

3.4.1 Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design (CPTED) 
Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design (CPTED) is an interdisciplinary approach used 
to reduce crime through the design of the urban environment. CPTED is pronounced ‘sep-ted’ 
and focuses primarily on physical efforts to make it risky or inconvenient for offenders to com-
mit crime [3]. It suggests that the design of the urban environment either can encourage or 
discourage crime [29]. Consequently, CPTED principles can be used to strengthen the percep-
tion of safety, as they are trying to make urban environments safer and crime-free.   
CPTED has existed for 50 years or 60 years if you include journalist Jane Jacob’s book “The Death 
and Life of Great American Cities” from 1961. Jacobs described a need for safer streets in New 
York as a response to the urban modernist planning projects she meant were a huge treat to 
the urban settings of New York. In specific she created a focus on just how important it is to 
have ‘eyes on the street’ [30]. 
The modernist ideologies were among other things to divide the human habitat to create better 
living conditions, light, and air. However, this was far from the reality of the life in these mod-
ernist urban environments. The act of spatial separation and the large scale increased the pos-
sibilities for crime and intensified the fear of crime. To improve safety in the modernist urban 
planning projects that were constructed through the years, architect Oscar Newman developed 
a concept of ‘Defensible Space’ in 1972. As Jane Jacobs he focused his work on natural surveil-
lance and social control to everyday activities. Though, he meant that social control only could 
take place if the design of the urban spaces has a clearly designed territoriality [30].  
The term CPTED was created in 1971 by criminologist C. Ray Jeffery, who assumed that the 
interactions between humans and the environment are more complex than what Newman was 
stating. Jeffery created CPTED expressing that it is not possible to be certain of the results of 
our planning and designing of urban environments. Yet, it is possible to judge whether or not 
it is likely that certain events would take place and then making the basis from this judgement 
[30].

The 1st generation of CPTED was divided into six categories and a wider environment by R. E. 
Moffat in 1983. The categories are territoriality, surveillance, access control, target hardening, 
image/maintenance and activity support, see Figure 20.  
 
Figure 20 - 1st Generation of CPTED based on [55] 
The concepts of CPTED have been developed further in the 1970s, where the routine activity 
theory was connected to situational crime prevention and in the 1980s, where it was adjusted 
to fit the conditions of different societies around the world and then again in 1997, where an 
extended 2nd generation of CPTED was made to include e.g., social factors [30].     
It is fair to say that CPTED is a rather complex approach that needs to be adjusted to the specific 
situation for it to be effective. In this project CPTED will be used to judge whether or not it is 
likely that crime events would happen in certain urban spaces to prevent the fear of crime. This 
by investigating the territoriality, natural surveillance, and image/maintenance of the urban 
spaces, see Figure 21.

3.4.2 Defensible Space 
Defensible Space, or territoriality, is according to Oscar Newman in the book “Creating Defen-
sible Space” (1996) about designing spaces that promote responsibility and joint ownership. 
The goal is to ensure increased social control, which encourages people to protect the area by 
looking out for intruders. This by expanding the area that people have influence over beyond 
what is perceived as theirs. In order to create a sense of responsibility and joint ownership, 
spaces must be clearly demarcated or defined into public, semi-public, semi-private and private 
zones. These zones can be referred to as transitional zones, see Figure 22.  
 
Figure 22 – Transitional Zones from [31] 
The transitional zones are used to avoid potential conflicts of use of space. The personalization 
of space can be reached by defining the design and layout of the urban space and the land-
scape. This by e.g. paving stones, pavement stamps, raised or textured surfaces, fences, sign-
age, use of color, etc. [32].  
3.4.3 Eyes on the Street 
‘Eyes on the street’ is a term from Jane Jacobs book “Death and Life of Great American Cities” 
(1961) [33]. Instead of only focusing on crime against property or the offender and target/victim 
of criminal acts, Jacobs were ahead of her time linking the fear of crime to the urban design 
[34]. Jacobs stated that it is important to have eyes on the street, or natural surveillance, to keep 
an eye on the urban space. This would make the place less attractive to commit crime, since it 
==PAGE:46==
46 
 
3.0 Theory 
would make it harder to commit crime undetected. However, there should not be less than 2 
m2 available per person, which will result in congestion, making pickpockets easier for potential 
offenders. 
 
 Figure 23 - Natural Surveillance from [35] 
There are two types of natural surveillance type 1, which is natural surveillance by people in 
buildings, who monitors people and the local properties in the urban space, and type 2, which 
is natural surveillance by people in an urban space, who monitors other people and the local 
properties in the urban space, see Figure 23. Hence, the people in the buildings keep an eye on 
the urban space and other properties out of windows and doors, whereas the people in the 
urban space keep an eye on other people and entrances to buildings in the urban space [35].  
3.4.4 Broken Windows Theory 
The Broken Windows Theory is a criminological theory introduced by social scientist James Q. 
Wilson and criminologist George L. Kelling in an article in 1982. The theory states that visible 
signs of crime, anti-social behavior, and disorder encourages potential offenders to commit 
further crime and disorder [36]. This because people would assume that no one cares about 
the area making them believe that they are allowed to act as they like. Hence, even the slightest 
rule-breaking behavior and disorder, such as vandalism, graffiti, drunk people, loud people, etc. 
need to be prevented to ensure order and lawfulness, as well as to prevent the fear of crime 
[28].

3.5 Human Perception of Urban Space  
In the 1960’s the Danish architect Jan Gehl also dealt with trying to solve some of the problems 
that were related to the modernist urban planning. In the modernist urban planning there were 
thought of light and air, but not the life between the buildings. 
After several years of studying urban spaces around the world, Gehl discovered that there are 
a number of basic rules that have proven to be constant even though society changes. After 
collecting and sorting data, Gehl developed an assessment tool with quality criteria for urban 
spaces, known as ‘the 12 quality criteria’ [2]. 
3.5.1 Gehl's 12 Quality Criteria 
The purpose of the quality criteria is to research how public spaces are experienced by the

and cyclists and safe crossings are identified as a part of the physical accessibility, whereas the 
protection against crime and violence and against unpleasant sensory experiences affect the 
perception of safety.

3.5.1a Mixed-use, Diversity of Functions

diversity of features creates less life on the streets at different times of day. Hence, an area with 
exclusively monofunctional buildings can have a negative impact on an area. An example of this 
is an area with exclusively shops since no one is keeping an eye on the area after closing time. 
Then conditions that can be attractive to potential offenders to commit crime might appear. On 
the other hand, multifunctional buildings, buildings with more than one function, will create 
more life in the urban space both during day and night.

3.7.1 Mental Mapping  
An analysis that can be created with the use of GIS is a Kevin Lynch analysis. In 1960 urban 
planner Kevin A. Lynch wrote the book “The Image of the City” focusing on how individuals (who 
are able to use their visual sense) perceive urban environments in cities, which he called the

of how easy they are to understand and navigate. Lynch defined legibility as:

In the book Lynch presents an analysis to investigate how certain people perceive urban envi-

3.7.2 Space Syntax  
Another method to investigate the ‘paths’ of the urban environments is to make a space syntax. 
Space syntax is a mathematical network theory model that contains an area of space in which 
there are different room patterns, such as pedestrians, cyclists, or cars. The purpose is to be 
able to decode the social logic that takes place in space that affects human behavior and creates 
special patterns in order to optimize our urban planning and other physical planning. Hence, a 
space syntax analysis can give a theoretical justification for design choices and be used on any 
type and size of spaces (e.g. large cities, smaller urban spaces, shopping centers, etc.). A space 
syntax analysis can be made with the use of the program depthmapX [47]. 
To map and build a network model for a settlement an axial map can be used, see Figure 33. 
The starting point for an axial map is empirical, as it is created on the idea that the human field 
of view affects the experience of space. The axial lines will be given colors differing on the de-
gree of attractiveness depending on the purpose of the axial map [47] [48]. Examples of axial 
maps are integration (accessibility), connectivity (how many lines are connected to a given line) 
and choice (how important a line is as a shortcut in the network) [49].   
 
Figure 33 – A Settlement, Axial Map and Axial Integration Analysis from [46] 
In this thesis, a space syntax will be used to investigate the integration (accessibility) of the 
roads and paths in the urban environments. The integration calculates how close a specific line 
is to the other lines [50].

5.5 Mapping Human Perception of Urban Space 
In the following, two analyses will be made. First, the areas will be investigated in connection to 
the building type, which has an effect on the use of the area and the number of people during 
the day and night. Secondly, the facades will be given categories based on whether they are 
active, friendly, mixture, boring or inactive. This among other things to investigate whether it is 
possible to look outside of the buildings. Hence, both analyses are investigating the natural 
surveillance in the areas. E.g. building types, which are used day and night, like retail with resi-
dential above, are providing more eyes on the street than a civic institution, which are closed in 
the evening and night. However, it also depends on whether the facades are open or closed. 
The more open and friendly facades, the more eyes will be able to be watching the outside 
areas.

the use of the areas and number of people in the urban environments. This is due to the fact

safety.

6.0 Discussion 
In this section, the key findings are presented and interpretations of the thesis will be 
provided with the importance of involving stakeholders to identifying challenges for 
people with physical and sensory impairments. Finally, the limitations and recommen-
dations for the thesis are described. 
 
==PAGE:110==
110 
 
6.0 Discussion 
6.1 Key Findings  
The research question was to identify challenges in the two urban environments in Birkerød 
and Holte for people with physical and sensory impairments and to provide suggestions for 
improving the conditions.  
The obtained qualitative data from the interviews showed that the challenges in relation to the 
physical accessibility in Birkerød are centered around the common paths according to Figure 
45. This is due to the fact that it is shared between pedestrians and cyclists, that the route is 
unclear, and that the conditions in the tunnel are providing bad acoustics and high water levels 
when it has been raining. Whereas the challenges related to the perception of safety mostly are 
centered at the station and the tunnels according to Figure 49. At the stations it is due to the 
smell of pee in the elevators, the undefined area, and the lack of lightning and shelter, whereas 
at the tunnels it is due to the fact that it is shielded from the surroundings, has poor acoustics, 
maintenance, lightning, escape, and overview conditions.  
In Holte, the obtained qualitative data from the interviews showed that the challenges of the 
physical accessibility are centered at the station according to Figure 54. This is among other 
things due to the high level differences at the transition, the heavy door, the fact that there are 
no tactile guidance path or tactile writings, the poor signage, and the missing signage at the 
elevator. The identified elements that contribute to fear of crime in Holte are according to Fig-
ure 58 near the undefined area, due to the poor lightning and dark housing, and near the park-
ing lot, due to the poor lightning and overview conditions. 
Consequently, the results indicate that there are a need for improving the current conditions in 
relation to the physical accessibility and perception of safety to ensure inclusive, safe, and sus-
tainable urban environments in Birkerød and Holte. 
6.1.1 Comparison of the Urban Environments  
If one compares the two urban environments in relation to the physical accessibility, it is noticed 
that similar barriers in Birkerød and Holte are identified at and near the station, some of which 
are related to the poor signage, the missing signage at the elevator, and the accessible infor-
mation. The information should be perceptible (principle 4 in Table 2), which means that the 
station needs to provide the necessary information effectively to the user, regardless of the 
user's sensory abilities. However, this is not always the case. The informant 'blind' reported that 
it is impossible for him to find the right bus and that a lack of tactile writings at the stations 
prevents him from finding the right track of the S-train unless he is standing on the platform 
which has a sound system.  
==PAGE:111==
111 
 
6.0 Discussion 
In addition, there are lots of challenges in Birkerød between the station and the city centre, 
both in terms of the occupancy and orientation, see Figure 43. This is a problem, as two of the 
universal design methods from Table 2 are stating that the urban space must be flexible in use 
(principle 2), thus accommodating a wide range of individual preferences and abilities and be 
simple and intuitive to use (principle 3) to make sure the layout of urban spaces eliminates 
unnecessary complexity. However, this is not a problem in Holte, where it was the lack of tactile 
guidance path and randomly placed signs that created the biggest challenges between the sta-
tion and the city centre, see Figure 52. However, the same problems also applied at the city 
centre in Birkerød, where it was mentioned that the tactile guidance path is too narrow and 
that the signs were randomly placed. In addition, the steep ramp at the center in Holte required 
a greater physical effort to use. This is a problem as the design must be efficient and comfort-
able to use with a minimum of fatigue (principle 6). Finally, it is mentioned that in both Birkerød 
and Holte, there were problems with creating appropriate space for the wheelchair and the 
pram (principle 7). This was i.a. by the elevator and the sidewalk by the vicarage garden in Birk-
erød and by the tunnel in Holte. In conclusion, it can be said that there are different challenges 
in the two urban environments, but also some similarities at e.g. the station and the city centre. 
In relation to the fear of crime, it can be seen from the safety survey, the interviews, the mental 
mapping based on the interviews, the space syntax, the building types, and the facade catego-
ries that each area has its own strengths and weaknesses. In Birkerød, uncomfortable areas 
near the station were mentioned by 50% of the informants from the safety survey, while it was 
only 24 % of the informants in Holte, see Table 3 and 4. In Birkerød, it was mentioned, among 
other things, that this is due to the fact that there are many entrances and access roads to the 
station, which can create confusion in relation to the orientation. This is supported by the space 
syntax in Figure 61, which at the same time shows that the access roads are not highly inte-
grated with the rest of the road network and the mental map in Figure 60, which shows that 
there are many nodes where paths meet and edges that can create good conditions for hiding 
and poor visibility. At the same time, the many accesses to the station also make the areas at 
the station feel abandoned in the evening according to informants from the survey. This can 
create good conditions for potential offenders to commit crime unnoticed and create fear of 
crime due to the lack of natural surveillance, which is described by Jane Jacobs.  
In Holte there are not many accesses, but due to the slightly secluded location, this can also be 
a problem in relation to the perception of safety. In the survey, it is mentioned that the area 
feels enclosed and deserted at some times of the day despite the beautiful location with lake 
and forest. Which is supported by the space syntax in Figure 61 and the mental map in Figure 
60, where it is possible to see that there are many edges.

6.0 Discussion 
Based on the building types in Figure 62, it can be seen that there are large areas in Birkerød 
with monofunctional buildings between the station and the city center, which is not the case in 
Holte. This creates no activity at night from the buildings. If one supplement this with the facade 
categories in Figure 63, it can also be said that the boring and inactive facades do not help to 
strengthen the perception of safety at daytime either. This is due to the fact that the natural 
surveillance is limited from the buildings. The fact that the facades appeared large and boring 
was also expressed by the informants 'suitcase' and 'pram' from the interviews as a problem in 
Figure 47. Furthermore, if there are no people present and no one feels protected over the area 
this can be a problem to prevent fear of crime according to the defensible space theory by 
Oscar Newman.  
Thus, it can be said that there are greater challenges in Birkerød than in Holte in relation to the 
geographical dimensioned perception of safety based on the findings.

6.0 Discussion 
6.2 Interpretations  
It was rewarding to experience the urban environments with people, where the design of the 
urban environments affected them differently. The results showed that there were areas where 
people, despite different points of views, weather, times of day etc., faced the same challenges. 
Among other things the route in Birkerød to the city centre that seemed unclear for the inform-
ants who were unfamiliar with the area and at the common path, where the present of cyclists 
and cobblestones were challenging more than one informant.  
Even though no elements that contribute to the fear of crime were identified at the city centre 
in Birkerød in Figure 47, this does not mean that there are no elements that create fear of crime 
in the area. However, the analyzes with building types and facade categories showed that there 
are multifunctional buildings that create life in the streets day and night and that the facades 
are friendly with several units that makes it possible to monitor the area from the buildings. 
These conditions are providing perception of safety according to the eyes on the street theory 
from Jane Jacobs. Therefore, the result is not seen as unexpected. However, additional elements 
might be identified if the study were made with more informants with different ages or more 
interviews during the evening. 
Consequently, not all barriers that exist in the urban environments according to the theory, has 
been identified in this study. This is also due to the fact that the life in the urban spaces and the 
urban environments changes depended on the time of day, weather, and season. As an exam-
ple, the wheelchair user did not mention that the cyclists on the common path were a problem, 
which most likely are due to the fact that no cyclist went by at the time the informant was on 
the path. This highlights the importance of experiencing urban spaces at different times of day. 
It is not possible to predict all the challenges that may exist at any time in the urban environ-
ments and the challenges can also vary a lot from person to person, but by gaining knowledge 
and experiences from a selection of users with different points of views at different times of 
day, many challenges have still been identified which are supported by the theory.  
The study can be used as a start to optimize some of the challenges that have been identified, 
which is described under section 5.6 Suggestions for Improvements. If there is a desire to provide 
a greater overview of the conditions, this will of course require more resources and informants.  
6.2.1 Importance of Involving Stakeholders 
As mentioned, the results are supported by existing studies of barriers that are related to the 
physical accessibility and the elements that contribute to the fear of crime. However, even 
==PAGE:114==
114 
 
6.0 Discussion 
though previous studies have been made, to locate some of the challenges in the specific urban 
environments the inclusion of the stakeholders are highly important.  
The study verifies the importance of involving stakeholders in the development of improving 
conditions in the urban environments in Birkerød and Holte. This is due to the fact that by con-
ducting the interviews on location with users with physical and sensory impairments unex-
pected challenges appeared. Hence, the identified challenges of the interviews are providing 
an insight into how the areas are perceived by people with different points of views. Although 
theory supports the challenges the informants identified in the study, it can be difficult for a 
one person without disabilities to understand how it must be. Additionally, the perception of 
safety is in many cases very individual. Nevertheless, theory do indicate that certain elements 
are creating conditions for geographical dimensioned fear of crime, among other things due to 
lack of social control, lack of eyes on the street, lack of maintenance, lack of human-scale, poor 
lightning, visibility, inactive facades, and unclear land-use. By joining a selection of users with 
different points of views at different times of day, you gain a much greater knowledge and 
awareness of the area and the conditions that are present. As an example, it can be difficult to 
locate problems with the orientation if you are not out and experiencing the urban spaces with 
people who do not know the area. Hence, the study asks the users what they need instead of 
telling them what they need. Consequently, the involvement of stakeholders is a central part of 
this thesis.

6.0 Discussion 
6.3 Limitations and Recommendations 
Due to the fact that the life and conditions in the urban environments change over time due to 
the change of time of day, weather, ongoing maintenance, etc. it is impossible to make a paper 
with all the present challenges in the urban environments for every citizen. Additionally, since 
the interviews take place over several months, the eleven informants are not experiencing the 
exact same urban environments. Nevertheless, this is not necessary a weakness, since this, 
within the time limit of this thesis, provide a broad picture of the challenges in the urban envi-
ronment for people with physical and sensory impairments. 
It is beyond the scope of this study to involve more informants and to expand the area of in-
vestigation. Nevertheless, to strengthening the findings and to strive for ensuring that the ur-
ban environments are inclusive and safe, an increased number of informants with different 
ages and abilities can be involved. Hence the thesis could be expanded by walking the routes 
with e.g. children, teens, and elderly with physical and sensory impairments or include people 
with different cognitive impairments. Also, a higher number of people who would walk the 
routes at night, would most likely provide more identified elements that contribute to the fear 
of crime in the urban environments. An analysis of people counts during different times of day 
at different locations in the urban environments could support the findings from the interviews 
in relation to the perception of safety, since it would provide a picture of the natural surveillance 
in the urban spaces. The more people, the higher are the perception of safety according to the 
theory unless there are less than 2 m2 available per person, which will result in congestion, 
making pickpockets easier for potential offenders. Also, as it can be seen in Figure 20, the wider 
environment and the socio-economic conditions have an impact on the perception of safety. 
However, this is not included in this thesis as it will require a broader analysis. However, it can 
be mentioned that the Municipality of Rudersdal is a wealthy municipality with a general high 
standard of living [56].  
The further work to improve the conditions in the urban environments that were identified in 
this thesis could, besides expanding the study, be for the involved parties, the Municipality of 
Rudersdal, DSB and Banedanmark to investigate how the suggested improvements would af-
fect the current urban space and life to make sure, that the quality of life is not compromised. 
After which they need to collaborate when improving the conditions as a whole to achieve the 
best possible results.

7.0 Conclusion 
In this section, a conclusion of the thesis will be presented, which summarize and re-
flect on the findings of the thesis and make recommendations for further work.  
==PAGE:118==
118 
 
7.0 Conclusion 
This thesis aimed to identify challenges in two urban environments in Birkerød and Holte re-
lated to the physical accessibility for people with physical and sensory impairments (temporary 
or permanent) and the perception of safety to make the cities more inclusive, safe, and social 
sustainable. Based on the qualitative and quantitative analyses of the urban environments, it 
can be concluded that there is a need of improving the current conditions. Also, the involve-
ment of informants with different points of views is found to be essential to locate challenges 
together with GIS maps to illustrate the findings, compared to only using the barriers and ele-
ments that contribute to the fear of crime stated in the theory. Based on these conclusions, to 
improve the conditions the involved land register owners, the Municipality of Rudersdal, DSB, 
and Banedanmark should either expand the study with more informants with different points 
of views over a longer period of time or investigate the suggested improvements in further 
depth. The thesis further supports that it is essential that challenges that are distributed over 
more than one owner have to be solved in collaboration to get the best possible results.
