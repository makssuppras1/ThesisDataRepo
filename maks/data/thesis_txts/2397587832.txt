Abstract
In this work, the environmental consequences of Greenhouse Gases is introduced.
The mechanisms
of NOx formation in the industry is investigated along with the most common methods used in the
industry to abate the emission of harmful gases. The most used method is Selective Catalytic Reduction
(SCR), which is a very mature and eﬃcient technology for destroying NOx in ﬂue gases.
However
there are problems inherent to the technology, such as deactivation, which are hard to solve without
compromising on some parameter.
Ionic Liquids (IL) is a relatively unexplored class of salts which
melt at 100°C or below, and has shown promise in gas absorption.
A study on the development of
IL for gas absorption use is shown.
As IL exhibit high viscosities, mass transport through them is
often slow. Supported Ionic Liquid Phase (SILP) is a compound material consisting of IL supported
on a porous material to improve on the gas absorbing properties of pure IL. Previous work in SILP
research is brieﬂy explored. Furthermore a series of SILP materials containing [BMIm][NO3] on SiO2
in varying Pore Volume Fraction, alpha = [0.1, 0.2, 0.3], was prepared.
Bulk [BMIm][NO3], 5.76M
NaNO3 and SILP materials was tested for their NOx-abative properties by exposing the samples to a
simulated ﬂue gas of NO, Air, and H2O. The samples were analysed by periodically weighing them, i.e.
Gravimetric Analysis, and by titrating the products after the conclusion of the absorption experiments.
From gravimetric analysis, it was found that bulk [BMIm][NO3] performed better than aqueous NaNO3.
This was conﬁrmed by titration analysis, where NaNO3 absorbed roughly 1.25 mol NO / mol [NO3]–
after 55 hours, where [BMIm][NO3] absorbed 2.05:1 mol NO / mol [NO3]– after only 48 hours. It was
shown that SILP materials was able to absorb 0.8:1 NO / mol [NO3]– after only 24 hours, which is in
agreement with the literature.

Introduction
It has long been known, that the extensive use of fossil fuel in the post-industrial world has caused severe
consequences for the global climate. Atmospheric concentrations of CO2, MeOH, and N2O are higher
now than in the past 800,000 years. As a result the mean temperature has risen nearly a degree celcius
since 1900, causing disruptions to climate in many regions the world over. Is is the general consensus
that anthropogenic contributions to green house gas (GHG) emissions have been the dominant cause for
climate change since the middle of the 20th century[1].
CO2 caused by fossil fuels and industrials processes is the largest contributor to anthropogenic GHG
emissions accounting for 65% of the global annual total[1]. As a consequence, many proposals have been
made to reduce the emission of CO2. Examples include switching from fossil fuels to biomass, municipal
waste or other fuels with a low carbon footprint in energy production. While alternative fuel sources,
such as biodiesel, can be eﬀective in reducing the emission of CO2, Particulate matter, and hydrocarbons,
NOx emissions are often the exception[2, 3]
While CO2 emissions aﬀects the entire globe, the consequences of NOx and SOx emissions are more
conﬁned locally. NOx and SOx are primarily formed in combustion processes in vehicles and fossil-fueled
power plants[4]. Both are contributing to acid rain and smog formation, while also having detrimental
health eﬀects on human and animal life. As the eﬀects of air pollution became known, exempliﬁed the
Great Smog of London in 1952, regulations were quickly put in place to reduce sulphur content in fuels[5].
SOx exclusively originates from sulphur content in the fuel, so this is a simple, but eﬀective method
of reducing pollution. However, while nitrogen-rich fuels also emit more NOx gasses, ﬂue gas NOx pri-
marily originates from oxidation of nitrogen in the air caused by the high temperatures reached during
combustion. Since NOx formation during combustion is inevitable, eﬃcient methods of NOx abatement
are valuable to the global community.
1.1
Traditional NOx removal methods
NOx formation is a problem in a variety of chemical processes. Common sources of NOx emission include
power plants, incineration facilities, the transport industry and the process industry [6, 7]. NOx gasses
are mainly formed through three mechanisms[7, 8]:
• Fuel NOx, formed by oxidation of nitrogen present in the fuel
• Thermal NOx, formed by oxidation of atmospheric nitrogen from the combustion air
• Prompt NOx, formed by oxidation intermediate species such as HCN
There exists a number of methods to reduce the emission of NOx gasses to the environment, each with
their advantages and drawbacks depending on the situation. For the incineration processes happening
in power plants, DeNOx methods can be categorized into three categories [6, 8].
• Pre-combustion includes measures taken to reduce the nitrogen content of fuel or choosing a low
nitrogen fuel such as natural gas [7]. Another method of reducing NOx formation is by replacing
the air in the combustion gas with pure oxygen, eliminating formation of thermal and prompt
NOx. According to Gabrielsson et al.[7], NOx reductions in the order of 70-90% can be achieved.
In practice, however, this method is costly and is rarely employed in the industry [8].
• Combustion modiﬁcation seeks to reduce the amount of thermal and prompt NOx formed by ad-
justing the reaction conditions. The most common parameters under optimization include: Tem-
perature, Pressure, Stoichiometry, Flame Stability, and Flue Gas residence time. [7, 8]. Skalska et
al.[6] has reviewed a number of combustion modiﬁcation methods, and assessed their advantages
and disadvantages along with their eﬃcacy at reducing ﬂue gas NOx content.
• Post-combustion or ﬂue gas treatment entails techniques to remove NOx gasses from the ﬂue gas
after it has exited the burner. The two main approaches to ﬂue gas treatment are NOx removal

from ﬂue gas and NOx destruction. The main drawback of the ﬁrst strategy is that, by ad- or

NOx destruction approaches doesn’t have this drawback, as the products usually are benign.
1.1.1
Selective Non-Catalytic Reduction
Selective Non-Catalytic Reduction (SNCR) is a method of NOx destruction, in which ammonia, aqueous
urea, or cyanuric acid is injected into the hot ﬂue gas to reduce NO to elemental nitrogen (eq. (1).
Around 95% of ﬂue gas NOx content is NO[9]. SNCR gained attraction because the simplicity of the
operation allows it to be easily installed in all types of stationary-ﬁred equipments. Furthermore, as no
catalyst is needed, the operation costs are low. In order to have any meaningful conversion rate of NO,
temperatures need to be high. Jakobsen reports a reaction temperature between 600-750°C as being
optimal. Below 600°C the reaction becomes too slow to be viable, and above 750°C, selectivity towards
N2 goes down resulting in N2O being produced. As N2O is a potent GHG, absorbing IR radiation with
270 times the intensity of CO2 this is undesirable[6]. As the conversion of SNCR is between 30-75%, it
is not the preferred option for NOx abatement.

1.1.2
Selective Catalytic Reduction
The most commonly employed technology for controlling NOx emission is Selective Catalytic Reduction
(SCR)[8, 6, 10]. First installed on industrial plants in Japan in the late 1970’ies[11], SCR is a ﬂue gas
treatment method, which reduces NO and NO2 with NH3 according to one of the following reactions[10]
with the help of a catalyst:
4 NO + 4 NH3 + O2 −−→4 N2 + 6 H2O
(Standard SCR)
(2)
6 NO2 + 8 NH3 −−→7 N2 + 12 H2O
(NO2 SCR)
(3)
NO + NO2 + 2 NH3 −−→2 N2 + 3 H2O
(Fast SCR)
(4)
The standard SCR reaction is most important when the NOx originates from high temperature com-
bustion, as the NO proportion of the ﬂue gas will be higher[10].
The most used catalyst for large scale applications consist of V2O5 on a TiO2 support, occasionally
doped with WO3 or MoO3[9]. Under optimal conditions, it has been shown SCR is able to remove up
to 98% of ﬂue gas NOx[7, 6].
Temperature dependence
The desired temperature range for SCR is 200-450°C depending on the
catalyst. Below 200°C, deposits of ammonia nitrate and nitrite may form, and above 400°C, undesirable
parallel reactions can take place, forming N2O or oxidizing NH3 to N2, N2O, or NO[10, 7].
Process conﬁguration
When designing an SCR unit, a choice has to be made between various
installation conﬁgurations. Three common conﬁgurations are High Dust, Low Dust, and Tail End with
advantages and disadvantages at each conﬁguration.
As a high reaction temperature is required for the SCR process, it is attractive to place the unit
close the the exhaust gas from the burners, called High Dust. As this conﬁguration will not require any
additional heating of the reaction mixture, money can be saved on operating costs. The downside to
this approach is that the ﬂue gas will contain a lot of by products from the combustion at this stage,
which can damage or inhibit the catalyst. For a Low Dust conﬁguration, the SCR unit is placed after an
Electrostatic Precipitator (ESP), which removes particulate matter (PM) from the ﬂue gas. Generally,
the gas is still suﬃciently hot at this stage that reheating is unnecessary to stay within the desired

temperature range. Finally, the Tail End conﬁguraion entails placing the SCR unit after a Flue Gas
Desulphurisation (FGD) unit. While the gas is free from most catalyst poisons and inhibitors at this
point, the gas will need reheating in order for the SCR process to become eﬀective[12].
Deactivation
In an ideal setting, a catalyst is deﬁned by its ability to accelerate or enable a chemical
reaction without being spent in the process. In real world applications, however, catalysts are exposed to
a variety of compounds, some of which can have a negative inﬂuence on the performance of the catalyst.
The catalyst is often built of a porous material, and can either be in particle form or made of monolith
extrusions with long channels for the gas to run through.
One type of deactivation occurs when in a high dust environment. As particulate matter from the burner,
typically unburnt organic matter, passes through the catalyst, pores and channels can get clogged up,
rendering a large part of the surface area inaccessible for the reaction gas.
For many biofuels it is
furthermore a problem, that a high alkaline content will irreversible poison the commonly used V2O5
catalyst[12].
While the risk of alkali poisoning is greatly reduced in a low dust conﬁguration, even a low sulphur
content can still be damaging to many metal catalysts[13]. Therefore it is sometimes preferable to put a
SCR catalyst after all the other ﬂue gas puriﬁcation processes have taken place if the catalyst in question
is vulnerable.

Ionic Liquids and SILP
1.2.1
Ionic Liquids
In recent years, a lot of attention has been brought to a subset of salts called Ionic Liquids. The deﬁning
characteristic for IL is, that they are salts which has a melting point at or below 100°C.
While the ﬁrst ILs were reported in 1914[14], it is only within the last 20 years the subject has gained
popularity. This trend can be seen in the amount of scientiﬁc articles written on the subject. ﬁg. 1 shows
the amount of publications per year containing the keyword "Ionic Liquid" and derivatives thereof found
using the multidisciplinary scientiﬁc search engine, SciFinder.
Recently ILs have begun seeing commercial use, as a petrochemical company in 2012 employed 60
tons of supported ionic liquid phase (SILP) in a gas puriﬁcation process, making it the ﬁrst large scale
application of this technology.
It has been shown that ILs can be applied as solvents in various reactions. By exchanging or modifying
either the anion or the cation of the IL, it is possible to obtain "designer solvents" appropriate for the
reaction at hand.
It is furthermore attractive to employ ILs in applications where the reaction conditions either contain
very low pressure or elevated temperatures, as ILs have an extremely low vapour pressure. As such the
upper operating limit of an IL is only given by its thermal decomposition temperature[15].
In general it is diﬃcult to accurately summarize ILs as they have proven to be a diverse group,
and much is yet to be discovered regarding the correlation between structure of the ions and the ﬁnal
properties of the IL.

conductivity which, among many applications, are useful in absorbing and extracting useful chemicals

Gas uptake in ILs are primarily governed by one of two mechanisms; Physisorption and Chemisorption.
In physisorption, molecules are weakly bonded to the the IL. The capacity of the sorbent is therefore
increased with increased partial pressure of the gas. In chemisorption molecules are chemically bound
to the sorbent, making capacity less dependent on gas partial pressures. The partial pressures of ﬂue
gas constituents, CO2, SO2, NO, are typically very low. It is very impractical to increase the pressure of
the ﬂue gas stream, and this would complicate the gas treatment process greatly. A suitable IL sorbent
is therefore required to mainly absorb gas by chemisorption.
A major advantage in using ILs for gas absorption, is the reversibility of the process in conjunction
with the non-volatile solvent. As the IL are very thermally stable, the expected life span of IL absorbers
are high, and if the absorption is selective, a high purity gas can be obtained directly from the ﬂue gas.
This could be the basis for an added value stream later in the process. In order for IL absorption to be
of interest in the industry, it is paramount that the association energy between gas and IL is not too
high, as that would lead to increased energy costs. Ideally, the association energy should only just be
high enough to absorb the gas at the desired levels, and a small temperature increase would cause the
gas to be desorbed completely. This is, however, unlikely to be achieved in practice.
The most eﬃcient IL absorbers in the literature, employ base functionalised anions to achieve high
molar absorption capacities. This allows the anion to interact with the gas through Lewis acid/base
interactions[5]. Most of the target gases in ﬂue gas interact as weak acids. The acid strength of ﬂue gas
species is higher for SO2 and lower for NO, with CO2 lying somewhere in between. As Lewis acid/base
interactions are not selective, there exist the possibility of competitive absorption with other Lewis acid
species, such as water.
The possibility of using ILs to absorb CO2 has received the most attention. As CO2 levels in the at-
mosphere is rising exponentially and CO2 accounts for 65% of anthropogenic GHG emissions, counted
in CO2 equivalents, this is understandable. Another factor in the popularity of this new method of

CO2 abatement, could be the fact that there is no truly viable technology for reducing the CO2 that is
produced[5]. The most commonly method for CO2 removal used today involves scrubbing the gas with
an amine solution, absorbing CO2 and H2S. However as this process signiﬁcantly increases the energy
consumption of a power plant by around 40%, this process is less than desirable. In addition, with amine
solutions, there is the risk of evaporation and leeching of the basic amine to come in contact with other
parts of the plant, causing corrosion[18].
Traditionally, SO2 absorption is done by scrubbing with a limestone solution. The by product of which
is gypsum and can in some cases be used for construction. However the quality of the gypsum is often
too low, so the process ends up generating a lot of waste. Anderson et al.[19] found that in addition to
ILs showing a high CO2 solubility, SO2 also absorbed readily in the IL. It was suggested that ILs could
be used to absorb CO2 and SO2 in a single step, simplifying the process.
Using ILs as sorbents also opens up for other chemical processes. By using another IL, Wu et al.[20]
was able do show high solubility of and selectivity towards SO2, concentrating the SO2 gas stream. This
allows for eﬃcient conversion of a waste gas into something more valuable, such as sulphuric acid.
While the use of ILs as an absorption media for NO has been investigated, the extent to which is
lower than for CO2 and SO2. A reason for this could be that Selective Catalytic Reduction already oﬀers
an excellent method for NO abatement in some cases. Another explanation could be, that NO has a
lower aﬃnity to many liquids because it interacts to a lesser extent than SO2 or CO2[5]. The reports

substituted by [NO3]– in the process. Investigating this eﬀect further, it was found that Ionic Liquids
react willingly with NO to form HNO3 when water and oxygen is present. The reaction rate was found

that the dynamic viscosity of the IL is two orders of magnitude higher.

1.2.3
Supported Ionic Liquid Phase
One of the challenges when working with ILs is that transport limitations can hinder the reaction in
question, as diﬀusion is low in a high viscosity ﬂuid. Additionally, a liquid is not always appropriate for
the process in question. As such a number of ways to transfer the properties of ILs to solids have been
invented. These include, but are not limited to, supported ionic liquid catalysis (SILC), supported ionic
liquid catalysts (SILCA), solid catalyst with ionic liquid (SCIL), supported ionic liquid nanoparticles
(SILnPs), supported ionic liquid phase (SILP), supported ionic liquid phase catalyst (SILPC). Common
to all these methods are, that they take advantage of the intrinsic properties of ILs on the surface of a
solid[16]. All of these methods are variations upon the general SILP concept, where a porous media is
covered with a thin layer of IL. As Ionic Liquids do not evaporate, SILP materials remain coated under
even relatively high temperatures.
As the properties of ILs are highly variably depending on the choice of anion and cation, it is possible
to create solid designer materials, which exhibit behaviour of both the support and the IL.
It is possible to treat a support material in such a way that only a single layer of IL is present.
By doing so, the bulk properties of the will be lost, but may still alter the surface functionality of the
support, such as acidity or hydrophobicity[16].
However if the bulk properties of IL are of interest to the process at hand, a higher will be optimal.
The method of preparation of SILP materials is usually to bind the IL to the support via physisorption.
The IL is dissolved in a low boiling solvent and mixed with the support. The solvent is then removed
either by evaporation or freeze drying, yielding a free ﬂowing powder.
Support Materials
Due to their large surface area, highly porous materials are usually of interest
when searching for a support material for a catalyst or an adsorbent. Porous materials are available
widely in nature, and human made porous materials have been manufactured since the end of the eigh-
teenth century. However it was not until 1919 when the ﬁrst technical gas phase adsorber was established
at Bayer for the removal of ethanol and benzene in ﬂue gasses[16].

Since then a lot of work has been done to further the ﬁeld of porous materials. Especially porous
metal oxides have proven to be a diverse and useful class of materials. For example, while zeolites were
ﬁrst discovered in 1756, it is only in the last 60 years it has been known how to synthesize them. Since
then, over 230 diﬀerent framework types have been discovered.
An important aspect of porous materials, for the purpose of catalysis and absorption, is the pore
size and pore size distribution of the material. In general the classiﬁcation is that pore sizes are divided
into micropores, mesopores, and macropores, depending on whether the pores are below 2 nm, between
2 and 50 nm, or above 50 nm respectively.
For some materials, such as zeolites, the pore structure is very well deﬁned and regular.
Such
crystalline usually contain micropores throughout the structure, whereas silica gels and precipitated
silicas for example contain a much broader range of pore sizes due to the amorphicity of the material.
These pores usually lie in the meso- and macropore region.
While small pores does lead to a very high surface area, it is not always favourable if transport limi-
tations occur. In order to allevieate this problem hierarchical porosities have been invented. Hierarchical
materials contain pores of at least two diﬀerent length scales, and aid in diﬀusion by having larger pores
easing the transport into the material, while still containing many smaller pores. Hierarchical pore struc-
ture can either be introduced by synthesizing the material around a skeleton of particles in the shape of
the desired pores, and then treating the material post-synthesization, or by synthesizing materials with
a hierarchical pore size distribution in the crystal structure, as have been seen with certain zeolites[16].

2.4
Absorption
The general procedure for all of the absorption experiments was the same. Gas containing nitric oxide
was mixed with air which had been passed through a bubble ﬂask containing de-mineralised water.
The humid gas mixture was passed through an absorption medium, which were either a liquid or SILP
particles. After the completion of the absorption, the product was transferred to a volumetric ﬂask and
dissolved in degassed water. The ﬂasks were then wrapped in aluminium foil to block out sunlight and
set aside until needed for titration. See ﬁg. 5 for visual representation of the setup.

2.4.1
Gas Mixture
The reaction gas was an equal volume ﬂow mixture of nitrous oxide in nitrogen and water saturated air
(WSA). In order to saturate the air with water, the compressed air gas stream was bubbled through a
vessel with water. In total, 4 distinct diﬀerent gas mixtures ended up being used. This was necessitated
by the ﬁrst NO gas running out without an exact replacement at hand and ﬂow rate control becoming a
problem at low ﬂow rates possibly caused by equipment degradation as a result of contact with corrosive
gasses.
A gas solely consisting of Water Saturated Air was also used to test water absorption of SiO2 with-
out the presence of nitrogen oxides.
All gas ﬂows were calibrated by measuring the ﬂow rate just before the reactor.
The absorption experiments fall within three categories:
• Bulk absorptions were conducted on a 5.76 M NaNO3 solution and on bulk [BMIm][NO3]in order
to assess the eﬀect of the ionic liquid in facilitating NO absorption and conversion. Absorptions
were run for a set time and then titrated to reveal the acid content.
• SiO2 and SILP materials were exposed to the absorbing gas until saturated. Frequent measure-
ments were taken during the process to investigate the rate of absorption and the saturation
limit.
• SILP materials were exposed to the gas mixture for a set duration with few measurements during
the experiment. The samples were then titrated to obtain the acid content. This allows for a
direct comparison between the various SILP materials.

Degassing Water
To degas water, boiling was chosen as the best method, since it doesn’t require
complicated equipment and is quick to perform. More de-mineralised water than needed was put in an
250 ml Erlenmeyer ﬂask with a magnetic stirring rod and covered with a watch-glass to limit evaporation.
The ﬂask was set on a hotplate with stirring enabled until boiling and left to boil heavily for at least 15
minutes. When the degassing process was complete, the ﬂask was partially submerged in ice water to
cool it down to room temperature in a timely fashion. The content of the ﬂask was used for dilution in
airtight volumetric ﬂasks and the excess was discarded.
2.4.2
Bulk Absorption
Bulk absorption was performed on 1-butyl-3-methylimidazolium nitrate and on a 5.76 molar solution of
sodium nitrate to evaluate the ability of ionic liquid to absorb nitric oxides. The molarity of the NaNO3
was chosen to be the same as for the [BMIm][NO3]

1.1599 g

For the bulk [BMIm][NO3], the IL was prepared by degassing in a 100°C oven overnight in order to
evaporate any absorbed water. A large volume NaNO3 was prepared and kept in a volumetric ﬂask with
a stopper and paraﬁlm to prevent evaporation.
For the absorption itself, approximately 1 ml absorbent was transferred to a reactor of known mass
and the vessel was weighed to obtain the dry weight. The gas mixture was passed through the reactor
at the Volume Flow Rate (VFR) described in 3 before being bubbled through a NaOH/H2O2 mixture
in order to oxidise the excess NO and partly absorb it. The reactor was disconnected brieﬂy at various
times in order to weigh the mass of the reactor, to be able to assess the absorption rate. The absorption
was terminated after a set duration had been reached. The product was transferred quantitatively with
degassed water to an airtight volumetric ﬂask and wrapped in aluminium foil to block out any sunlight
until analysed.
2.4.3
SiO2 & SILP Absorption
For the solid particle absorption, approximately 1 g of material was weighed and dried in a 100°oven
at least overnight in order to evaporate any absorbed water. After drying, the SILP was weighed again
and transferred to a ﬂuid bed reactor of known mass. The reaction gas (described in table 4) was passed
through the reactor and the exhaust gas was bubbkled through a NaOH/H2O2 mixture in order to
oxidise the excess NO and partly absorb it. At various times, the reactor was disconnected brieﬂy in
order to weigh the reactor. The absorption was terminated either when the material stopped gaining
mass or after a set period of time, depending on the experiment. The SILP powder was transferred
quantitatively to an volumetric ﬂask with degassed water and diluted to the volume mark on the ﬂask.
The ﬂask was wrapped in aluminium foil to keep out sunlight until analysed. No further analysis was
performed on the pure SiO2 particles.
2.5
Analysis
In order to investigate the ability of the tested materials to absorb NO gas, two methods were used in
tandem. in order to assess the rate of absorption it was decided to weigh the reaction vessel at certain
intervals throughout the experiment. Even though it was known that water would absorb along with
NO, it was assumed that it would still be possible to analyse the rate of absorption by measuring the
weight, albeit not quantitatively.
As the gravimetric analysis cannot discern what species are being
absorbed, just the combined mass, it was decided to also titrate the samples in order to ﬁnd out the
acid content. Assuming that the only acidic protons present in the samples are from HNO3, titration
serves as a means to ﬁnd out the exact amount of NO absorbed after a given time of absorption. This,
16
==PAGE:17==
however, introduced some new problems. As the experiments need to be stopped in order to perform a
titration, acquiring data took a lot longer. To compensate for this, absorption series of a predetermined
duration was planned, for which few gravimetric data points were recorded.
Another analysis method which could be useful in analysing the uptake of IL is CHN analysis. This
method is not covered in this rapport, however.
2.5.1
Gravimetric
For the gravimetric measurements, the entire reactor was disconnected from the setup for a very brief
period (30-40 seconds, timed with a stopwatch) in order to be weighed. This is done by closing the exit
and entry valves, in that order, prior to removing the gas tubes. This is to insulate the contents of the
reactor to atmospheric air.
The measurements were frequent at ﬁrst, but was spaced out more and more as the experiment proceeded.
Since the rate of absorption is very high, but decreasing quickly in the beginning of the experiment, the
additional measurements were required to obtain a satisfactory resolution.
One of the advantages of the gravimetric method is the fact that it is very quick to perform and thus
it allows for a lot of data points throughout the experiment. It is also non-destructive, and apart from
the fact that the ﬂow of reaction gas is interrupted for a brief period, it has no impact on the experiment.
The main disadvantage of the method is, that although it is possible to get a lot of data points through-
out the experiment, it is not possible to know the composition of the absorbed matter. In this particular
experiment, it is known that the materials absorb both NO and water. As such the gravimetric method
falls short of completely characterizing the materials. It should also be mentioned that even though it
doesn’t require many seconds to perform a measurement, the time spent without gas ﬂow does add up,
and need to be accounted for in the data collection. The resulting species after absorption are not know.
The possibilities are NO, NO2, HNO2, HNO3 and condensates, e.g. N2O4.
2.5.2
Titration
For the titration analysis, the content of the volumetric ﬂasks was titrated on a MetrOhm 835 Titrando
automatic titration unit. The titration was repeated multiple times for each sample and the average
value is reported.
The primary advantage of titrating the sample is, that only the acidic protons will register in the test,
thus allowing for an accurate measurement of how much NO was absorbed and converted in the material.
The major disadvantage however, is that the analysis is destructive and that it cannot be performed as
the experiment is running, thus requiring lengthy experiments for each data point. Additionally, there
can be other contributing acid/base eﬀects from the surface of the support material to account for.

Discussion of bulk absorption
It was observed with both methods of analysis, that [BMIm][NO3] achieved a higher absorption of NO
at all points in time. This is caused by the fact that while the concentration of the anions in both liquids
are the same, the higher ionic strenth of ILs make the conversion more favorable. Aqueous NaNO3 also
has other drawbacks, which makes it a worse absorption medium. As it is based on water, the solvent
will evaporate over time and will need to be replenished in order to avoid crystallisation. Crystallisation
could cause a lot of problems in a industrial sized plant, as it could block the ﬂow trough the medium
or trap a lot of the nitrate ions in solids, thus deactivating the absorber.

Conclusion
Throughout this report, a range of materials have been tested for their ability to absorb NO and convert
it to HNO3. It was found that in bulk phase, pure [BMIm][NO3] performs better than a 5.76 M NaNO3
with the same anion concentration as the IL. As the NaNO3 solution is water based, there is furthermore
the disadvantage in using it in gas absorption, that the water will evaporate over time, eventually causing
the nitrate salt to precipitate out of solution. As Ionic Liquids have no vapour pressure, this problem is
not shared by [BMIm][NO3].
Three SILP materials were prepared with a pore volume fraction, α, of 0.1, 0.2, and 0.3. In compari-
son with bulk materials, it was shown that SILP materials have a faster absorption rate, when accounting
for the amount of Ionic Liquid. It was shown that the support itself will also absorb matter from the
gas stream, which will add to the energy cost of desorbing the targeted compounds.
By dissolving the SILP after absorption, quantitative numbers on NO uptake were achieved.
SILP
with a pore volume fraction of 0.2 and 0.3 was able to absorb around 0.8:1 mol NO / mol IL within
24 hours, which is close to the value of 1:1 mol/mol reported as the theoretical limit by P. W. Jakobsen[5].
Gravimetrical data was converted into a comparable HNO3/IL molar ratio by assuming that all absorbed
mass was pure HNO3. In comparing gravimetry and titration values, it was found that gravimetry is
a ﬂawed method for evaluating NO uptake quantitatively.
For bulk 5.76M NaNO3, the values from
gravimetric analysis were about 50% lower than the ones from titration, when converted.
For bulk
[BMIm][NO3] the values were close to those obtained by titration.
For SILP materials, the converted molar ratios were all signiﬁcantly higher, around twice the value, than
those found by titrating the samples.
In conclusion, Ionic liquids and SILP materials are still unexplored materials, but their ability to absorb
dilute ﬂue gas constituents and, in the case of NO, convert it into a valuable product in a single step
shows potential in future industrial emission abatement.
