Abstract
Tree cores were collected at contaminated site Brede and Raadvad.
Chlorinated solvents and
high concentrations of heavy metals were detected in samples. Results showed there was a wide
spreading of VC at Brede, and potential contamination of Zn after compared with the concentrations
measured at Raadvad. At Raadvad one hot spot was located by tree coring. Signiﬁcantly elevated
concentrations of heavy metals can be seen in tree core samples, when the absolute concentrations
of heavy metals are high in soil.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
GOALS AND OBJECTIVES
The overall goal of this study was to have a better understanding of tree coring by practicing the
technic at contaminated sites.
Objective 1: Test tree coring as screening tool to detect Chlorinated solvent and heavy metals at
contaminated sites
At sites with shallow aquifer, contaminates can be taking up by trees together with groundwater,
the evelvetd concentrations can be detected by taking core samples from the trees.
Objective 2: Evaluate the inﬂuence of weather on chlorinated solvent and heavy metal concentration
in trees
Hypothesis: Concentration of chlorinated solvent in trees will vary seasonally due to transpiration
and phytovolatilization, while concentration of heavy metal will stay constant.
Objective 3: Test the ability of tree coring for detect VC
Hypothesis: Detection of VC by tree coring is possible, however the period of the time for application
may be limited, due to the short half live time of VC in trees.

Chapter 2
Study sites
Mølleåen is the longest river of north Zealand, starts at Bastrup Lake and runs through Farum
Lake, Furesøen, Lyngby Lake and ﬁnally ends at Øresund.
The river has a total length of 36
kilomters, catchment area of 126 square kilometers(Nielsen and Johansen). As a result of its huge
water level drops, the river has supplied hydropower to various industries in the early age, and
played an important role in the danish industry history. Brede and Raadvad are the two locations
among the industry areas along the river.

2.1
Brede
Before it became the museum of Brede Works, many diﬀerent industrial activities were take place at
the area. Until 1831, there has been copper and brass workshop at the site, gasworks between 1857
to 1907. There has been textile factory from 1883 to 1956 and metal foundry since 1883. Besides, a
wide range of other activities have been occupied, such as furniture manufacturing, machine shop,
textile printing, production of hearing aids, weaving and manufacture of electrical items.
Chlorinated solvents were detected at site by groundwater and soil gas sampling. The primary
aquifer was report at 0.5 meter below ground surface, the ﬂow direction was easterly. On the east
side of Mølleåen, the secondary aquifer was to the west, and southwesterly on the west side of
Mølleåen.
2.2
Raadvad
At raadvad there has been a knife factory from 1879 to1972. A broncefoundry and metal works at
site for unknown period.
Chlorinated solvents were detected at site by groundwater and soil gas sampling. Heavy metals
were detected in soil samples. The primary aquifer was reported at 5 meters below ground surface,
, the ﬂow direction was easterly. The secondary aquifer was located at 1 to 1.5 meters below ground
surface, no detailed information of the ﬂow direction.
Brede and Raadvad were selected at study sites, due to the shallow aquifer at the sites and locations
are very close to DTU, both are greening public areas. Brede is mildly contaminated site with
chlorinated solvents,while Raadvad is heavily contaminated site with chlorinated solvents and heavy
metals.

Chapter 3
Method
3.1
Sampling campaigns
Three solo sampling campaigns were carried out during the project. The ﬁrst sampling was aimed
to screening chlorinated solvents and heavy metals on both sites, took place in week 38. Based on
the results from the ﬁrst campaign, the second and third campaigns were performed in week 48
and week 50, with emphasis on heavy metals and chlorinated solvents respectively. The procedure
applied for sampling was followed the guideline adopted by Nielsen and Trapp (2014). Figure3.1
and 3.2 show the locations of sampled trees at Brede and Raadvad.
3.1.1
First campaign
The ﬁrst sampling campaign took place on 22nd of September 2016 at both Brede and Raadvad.
In total 26 trees were sampled. The sampling at Brede was performed in the forenoon, and the
analytical vials were prepared in the morning before leaving to the site. Twenty two tree core
samples were collected from 11 trees. All samples were kept in a thermal insulation box during
sampling and transportation. After sampling, all samples from Brede were transported back to the
laboratory of DTU around noon, and stored in the refrigerator at DTU. Afterwards the preparation
of analytical vials was repeated, and the sampling at Raadavd was performed in the afternoon
followed the same procedure. Thirty tree core samples from 15 trees were collected and stored
together with the samples from Brede over the night, till they were analyzed the following day. No
blank samples were prepared for this campaign.
The species of all sampled trees were identiﬁed by the leaves during the ﬁrst campaign. The cir-
cumferences of the trunks 1 m above ground were measured to ensure the sampled trees are mature
and healthy. The locations of the sampled trees were recorded with handheld GPS(GPSMAP®

3.1.3
Third campaign
The third sampling campaign was carried out on 14th December 2016, to determine the inﬂuence
of seasonal variation. At Brede tree 8 and 9 were sampled again. Tree 8C and tree 9C which grown
3,5 and 4 meters away were sampled to investigate the eﬀect of repeat sampling. One air blank
was collected by holding the vial open for 2 minutes around sampled trees. At Raadvad tree 14,
15, 17, 18 and 26 were sampled again. Two air blanks was collected at site, one covered the area
around tree 17 and tree 18, the other covered the area between tree 14, 26 and 28. Moreover, one
excursion blank was prepared for this campaign.
3.2
Analysis
All analyses were performed by laboratory technicians at Department of Environmental Engineering
of DTU.
3.2.1
Chlorinated solvents
The analyses of chlorinated solvents were performed the next day after sampling, by HS-GC/MS
(Headspace -Gas Chromatography / Mass Spectrometry) on an Agilent 7980 gas chromatograph
system equipped with a Agilent 5975C electron impact (70 eV) triple-axis mass-selective detector.
7
==PAGE:18==
Sample vials were incubated in a rotary shaker at 250 rpm 85 °C for 5 min and 250 µl headspace was
injected in splitless mode at 80 °C. Chromatographic separation is achieved on a 30 m x 0.32 mm
I.D x 20.00 µm ﬁlm thickness HP-PLOT/Q capillary column (Agilent Technologies). The initial
column temperature is set to 40 °C for 4 min then ramped at 35 °C min-1 to 290 °C. The ﬁnal
temperature is held for 7 min and the total run time was 18.1 min with Helium (1.6 ml min-1) as
carrier gas. Quantiﬁcation limits were reported with the results. The tree core samples from the
ﬁrst campaign were analysed for chlorinated solvents ﬁrst, then prepared for heavy metal analyses.
3.2.2
Heavy metals
Tree core samples were dried at 65 °C to constant weight, and then divided into pieces in millimeters.
0,25 g samples were digested by microwave(US EPA 3051A) with 9 ml, HNO3 65% and 3 ml HCl
37%. Digested samples transferred to volumetric ﬂasks and diluted with MiliQ water to 50 ml. 20
ml of the dilutions transferred to plastic (PE) bottles for storage at room temperature till analyses.
For the samples from the ﬁrst campaign, two cores from the same tree were randomly mixed into one
sample. Tree core samples from the same tree were analysed separately for second campaign. For
analyses, 10 ml of samples were transferred to test tubes and then analysed by Agilent ICP-OES.
Method detection limits and instrument detection limits were reported with results.

Results from three sampling campaigns are presented in this section. Concentrations of chlorinated
solvents in tree core samples are expressed in microgram per liter of solvent. A conversion from
microgram per liter to nanomole per liter was performed to express the compositions of each com-
pound in the samples. All concentrations below quantiﬁcation limit are represented as “<QL”. For
heavy metals, all concentrations are expressed in microgram per kilogram of dry weight of tree
cores. All concentrations below method detection limit are reported as “<DL”.

Chapter 5
Discussion
5.1
Findings at study sites
5.1.1
Chlorinated solvents
Contaminations of chlorinated solvents at both Brede and Raadvad were detected by tree core
screening. At Brede, in the late summer campaign, chlorinated solvents were detected in 10 trees,
apart tree 12 which was located at the south east corner, as shown in Figure4.1. PCE and TCE were
measured in tree 1, 6 and 11 at very low concentrations, and total concentrations of all sampled
trees were below 10 nmol/L, therefore no source area was located. In the building area, the number
of trees was limited. The widely-spreading of VC in the study area was indicated by tree coring. VC
was measured in 9 out of 10 sampled trees, with 6 contained pure VC among. Results from tree7, 8,
9 and 10 indicated equal distribution of VC in the area, the slight diﬀerences of the concentrations
was believed due to the higher transpiration rate of the trees sampled later during the morning.
In the winter campaign, no chlorinated solvents were detected, for the reasons that all sampled
trees were deciduous plants, transpiration almost stopped in the wintertime, along with the low
concentrations of chlorinated solvents in the area, as shown in Figure 4.2.
At Raadvad, one hot spot was identiﬁed by tree coring in the late summer campaign, between tree
17 and 18, shows in Figure4.5. Considering the total concentration in tree 17 was 3 times higher
and contented nearly pure PCE, the location of the hot spot was evaluated more towards to tree
17. Due to the limited number of trees in the building area, the hot spots at northern part of the
site were not directly detected by tree coring. However, results from tree 14 and 13 indicated the
spreading of chlorinated solvents were in the direction of south east, seeing there was a decrease
of total concentration in tree13 yet an increase of cis-DCE in mole percent. Similar results were
observed in tree 15 and 16, suggested the migration of chlorinated solvents were towards north
east. In the central east part, there was a spreading of chlorinated solvents to the south, based
23
==PAGE:34==
on the results from tree 19, 20 and 22. At southern part of the site, no clear relationships were
observed of PCE, TCE and the degradation compounds. This suggested the chlorinated solvents
were originated from diﬀerent areas located in the north. Results from the winter campaign showed
signiﬁcant developments of the concentrations in the resampled trees as shown in Figure 4.6. The
increased total concentration in tree 14 was due to the increased concentration of TCE. Result from
tree 15 indicated that there was no longer groundwater ﬂow against the river on the south side of the
channel, therefore no chlorinated solvent was measured in tree 15. The most signiﬁcant decreased
concentration was seen in tree 17. The concentration of PCE was nearly 100 times lower compared
with the result from late summer campaign, which was believed due to the development of the
groundwater conditions at the area. One hypothesis could be the surface water was recharging the
groundwater which caused the dilution, corresponding leaded to the increased total concentration in
tree 18. Besides, tree 17 was studied and sampled under other projects that occurred simultaneously,
along with poor performance during sampling might aﬀect the concentration in samples. No VC
was detected in this campaign, indicated the winter period was not suitable for VC detection by
tree coring.
5.1.2
Heavy metals
Signiﬁcantly elevated concentrations of Zn were detected by tree coring in the late summer campaign
at both sites. At Brede, high concentration of Ni was as well measured. After the winter campaign,
signiﬁcantly higher concentration of Zn was conﬁrmed alone, in tree 3 at Brede and in tree 14, 15,
16, 17 and 23 at Raadvad. The concentrations of Cr and Cu were relatively low at both sites, all
below 2 mg/ Kg DW, more speciﬁc concentrations were not able to given, due to the variations
of the results between the two campaigns, one or the other was below detection limit. The lower
concentration of Pb was conﬁrmed at Brede in tree 4 at concentration 0.9 mg/ Kg DW, as well as
at Raadvad, in tree 14 at 2.3 mg/ Kg DW, tree 15 at 1.3 mg/ Kg DW, tree 23 at 0.7 mg/ Kg DW.
Concentrations of Cd were below detection limit in all samples, expect tree 5 sampled in winter
campaign, at very low concentration of 0.2 mg/ Kg DW.
Spearman rank correlation was preformed to determine whether the concentrations of heavy metals
in sampled trees covary. All measurements below detection limit were replace with DL/√2, Cd was
not included in the test. Applied data can be seen in Appendix E . As a result of the diﬀerent
detection limit of the two campaigns, the tests were carried out separately for each campaign.
As shown in Table5.1, no signiﬁcant correlation was found based on the result form late summer
campaign, while signiﬁcant correlation was observed between Ni and Cr from the results of the
winter campaign shown in Table 5.2. This observation corresponded with the previous industrial
activities at Brede, where Ni and Cr were widely used in metal surface plating, electroplating,
pigments and textile production. All sampled trees were located very close to the river, as shown in
Figure4.4, as well as the highest concentrations of Zn were detected in the same trees. At Raadvad,

metals at Raadvad.

Comparison of results with previous data
At Brede, previous investigations were carried out by Capital region (2009) and Orbicon (2012).
High concentration of TCE was detected by soil gas sampling in 2012, at concentration of 2300
g/m3, under the building located at east of tree T9 and 9C, mapped as hot spot in Figure2.1. Low
concentrations of TCE, cis DCE and VC were measured in groundwater sampling at Boring B2 north
east of Tree 8 and 8C bewtwwn 5 to 8 g/l. The groundwater ﬂow was reported to the southwest.
The results from this study was not fully consistent with the previous ﬁndings. Neither TCE nor
cis-DCE was detected at western of the previous sample locations, instead TCE was decected in
tree 1 which was located at south east. Those might due to the change of the groundwater ﬂow in
the area, however groundwater level was not measurement in this study, therefore this explanation
was not curtained.
At Raadvad, tree core samples were collected in 2015 autumn under the investigation by Danish
EPA(2016). Tree 13, 14 , 15 ,16 and 17 were sampled, and the concentrations of chlorinated solvents
were reported in g/Kg. A comparison of the results is shown in Table5.6. In tree 13, no TCE, cis-
DCE was detected; therefore a decreased total concentration was seen in 2015. In tree 14, increased
concentrations were seen all three componds, particularly for cis-DCE was much higher than the
result form this study. Based on the results of tree 13 and 14, it believed the groundwater ﬂow was
more to the south during the sampling time in 2015. A signiﬁcant higher concentration of PCE was
detected in tree17, validated the previous speculation that the hot spot was located in this area.
This suggested by comparing the tree core data collected from the same season, it is possible to
speculate on the changes of groundwater conditions in the area.
Comparison of the heavy metals concentrations in soil and tree core samples was attempted. Due to
many measurements were below detection limit, comparison of concentrations of Zn was performed
alone, based on the results of this study and the results from the investigation by Capital region
(2009). As shown in Figure5.1, the soil concentration was the concentration of a mixed sample
where 5samples were collect from the four corners and the center of each square. Table 5.7 shows
the concentrations of Zn in soil and related tree core concentrations. As shown in Figure5.2, there
seemed a correlation between the concentrations. The point generated by O7 and T20 seemed an
outlier, and discarded. With 5 datasets, linear, exponential and power regression was performed as
shown in Figure5.3 Figure5.4 and 5.5. As shown in Table5.9, for power regression, the correlation
coeﬃcient r was above critical r, indected signiﬁcant correlation between soil and tree concentra-
tions of Zn. After discarded the point generated by O3 and T16, stronger correlations was observed
in both exponential and power regressions, with correlation coeﬃcients at 0.9945 and 0.9858 respec-
tively, seen in Table5.11. Based on this ﬁnding, the soil concentration of Zn was expected at 1300 to
1500 mg/ Kg DW, when the tree core concentration of Zn was 40 mg/ Kg DW, as well pointing to
the potential contamination of Zn at Bere when compared with the conﬁrmed high concentrations
in trees.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Tree coring can be applied at locations with shallow aquafers and trees. For prescreening chlorinated
solvents such as hot spot or screening heavy metals, the sampling can taking place nearly whole
year, or at least from late summer to winter. Hot summer may not suitable for screening chlorinated
solvents, due to the volatilities of the compounds.
For prescreening, few trees can cover a huge area, with intensive sampling, results can provide useful
information of the groundwater conditions and the spreading of the plume. During late summer,
the degradation products such as VC can also be detected by tree coing, provided more detailed
information of the degradation conditions around the sampled trees. All experience has been based
on the study with deciduous plants.
Both campaigns were performed during dry weather, no signiﬁcant precipitation before or on the
sampling day. The eﬀect of precipitation was not included in this study.
When performing tree coring, an extended screening is always recommended.

Chapter 7
Recommendations for future work
At Brede, further tree coring can be performed west of the study area, to investigate the spreading
of chlorinated solvents at site, considering the equal concentrations of VC in tree7, 8 ,9 and10, a
larger contaminated area was expected. Furthermore, the area can be applied to study the eﬀect
of repeat sampling on tree coring, seeing the same species of the trees and the similar size. It is
recommended to investigate the concentrations of Zn, Ni, Pb , Cr and Cu at site, particularly in
the areas around tree 1, 3, 4 ,5 and 12.
At Raadvad, further investigation is recommended east of the site. Considering its large area in
the forest, it’s one of the most suitable conditions to perform tree coring. Continuous tree coring
at site can be performed in the further, for monitoring and a better understanding of groundwater
condition as well the distribution of chlorinated solvents, considering two consecutive years of data
for chlorinated solvents has been collected at site.
When performing tree coring, an extended screening is always recommended.

Appendix A
Site selection
The selection of the study site has been phased over two stages. The ﬁrst suggestion was to perform
a tree coring screening at Lyngby campus of Technical University of Denmark(DTU). Due to the
hydrogeological condition this idea was abandoned. With expanded screening, two contaminated
sites at north of Lyngby campus along Mølleåen were selected, namely Brede (Locality Number:173-
05029) and Raadvad (Locality Number:173-05036).
DTU
Lyngby campus covers an area of 1.06 square kilometers, as urban area most of the space is covered
with buildings and hard surfaces. In addition the campus was designed with wooded areas, the
large number of trees, making it a possible candidate of study site.
Additionally one area at Kollegiebakken( locality number 173-00102) is mapped as V2( Danish
EPA knowledge level 2) site, which is a conﬁrmed site with contaminations of tar and heavy
metals(Miljøportal). A historical review of the campus was performed to investigate the further
potential contaminations. Before the relocation of DTU to Lundtofte between 1962 to 1974(Rambøll
et al., 1974), the area was a green ﬁeld and applied formerly as airﬁeld back in 1950s(Zwisler). A
hangar was located at the central area, which is currently the parking lot between building 101 and
201. Potential contamination of hydrocarbons and heavy metals might occur due to leakage from
storage and refuelling of fuels and oils, as well as spills(Nunes et al., 2011). However, there was no
evidence reported.
Moreover the geological and hydrological information of Lyngby campus is reviewed. During the
construction of a underground heat storage water pit at Lyngby campus in 1982, the groundwater
level was reported at 40 meters below the ground surface. The soil structure was described as
sand, with a 4-5 meter thick clay layer(Hansen et al., 1983). This information is in very good
agreement with proﬁle of borehole 201.2065. In 2014 new test borehole was drilled to investigate
the possiblities for the establishment of groundwater cooling system at Lyngby Campus.
The
37
==PAGE:48==
investigation uncovered the expected aquifer at 60 meters below the ground surface together with
a deeper limestone aquifer located at 90 to 100 meters below the ground surface(Geo).
Due to lack of suﬃcient evidence and the locations of deep aquifers, Lyngby campus was evaluated
as unsuitable for testing of tree coring. Thus, it is decided to expand screening area for the selection
of the study site.
Based on the experience of information review of Lyngby campus, it is decided to search for con-
ﬁrmed sites (V2) with contaminations of Cl-VOC and/or heavy metals as study sites. The attention
was soon drawn to Mølleåen located at north of Lyngby campus.
