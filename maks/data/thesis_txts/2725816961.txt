Abstract
The primary objective of this project is to identify the most effective indoor localization
model to allow a hospital robot to work in highly dynamic environments in a robust,
eﬀicient and safe way.
After analyzing all the possible models’ outcomes, it has been determined that AMCL
model is the most suitable for this project’s requirements. Thus, an optimization process
is needed to determine the optimal parameters.
This optimization process started by utilizing the default parameters and a proposal
obtained from a technical paper. Then, a continuous iterative process of improvement or
refinement was employed to tackle the limitations of previous proposals. Each proposal
was tested across different diﬀiculty levels (easy, medium, and hard), which were defined
based on the features appearing on the scene, such as turns, people or objects.
Through all the tests, the parameters giving the best results were identified, leading
to a proposal that not only presented better covariance values when the robot encounters
different features but also demonstrates consistency across all diﬀiculty tests.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
One of the main problems that robots working in highly dynamic environments are
facing is semi-static objects, which are those objects that change their position slowly,
i.e. trolleys or wheelchairs, which can be parked and suddenly moved around. When
these kinds of objects are on scene, the localization methods can’t rely on a static map
robustly and accurately.
Some possible solutions in this sense are SLAM solutions -which unfortunately don’t
give enough robustness or accuracy [1]-, positioning methods -with high economic cost
and limited reconfigurability (due to its dependency on fixed infrastructures) [2]-, and
last but not least, there are localization methods, such as Monte Carlo Localization.
In this project, all the current localization solutions are analyzed and compared to deter-
mine which ones are good fits for the current application on Yuman’s robot depending
on its sensors and working requirements (see section 3).
Given a static map and measurements from the robot sensors, a reliable, robust, and
accurate pose estimation is determined and tested in simulation.
1.1
Yuman
The start-up named Yuman, based on DTU Skylab and part of Skylab Pilots program
is a nascent company that develops innovative robots to assist nurses as their transport
buddies by automating the transport of food, laundry, and medical equipment in hospital
wards.
By automating these tasks, nurses have more time to take care of patients,
improving nurses’ satisfaction and decreasing burnout. 1
Their mobile robot called Buddy is equipped with a 2D 230º LiDAR, two RealSense
D455 cameras (one working as a normal camera and the other as the IMU of the robot),
and ultrasound sensors.
One of the main challenges the company is facing is the localization of the robot in closed
environments where GPS signals can’t be used. In addition, since it is a highly dynamic
environment, most methods are not robust enough when semi-static objects enter the
scene. Thus, one of their most necessary tasks is to implement a method that doesn’t
completely rely on the static map but adapts to the current scenario. Thus the robot
knows where it is located at all times, lowering the chances of getting lost or bumping
into patients or objects.

Objectives
The main objectives of this thesis project are:
• Look into and comprehend variations of Monte Carlo Localization, such as NDT-
MCL, DT-NDT-MCL, IRON, etc. Have an overall idea of how each method works,
as well as detect the strengths and weaknesses of each one
• Have an in-depth knowledge of how AMCL works. Understand both pros and cons
of the method as well as the parameters’ impact on the overall performance
• Get data from the Yuman robot in different environments, simulating most likely
hospital situations
• Implement NDT for ROS2 when mapping
• Combine NDT into AMCL algorithms
• Evaluate the effect of AMCL’s parameters in terms of covariance
• Implement the chosen methods on the recorded data simulation
• Evaluate the outputs from each chosen model and compare in terms of pose esti-
mation covariance
• Document all options, decisions, and justifications related to the project, as well
as the outcome of the project

Requirements and limitations
The requirements for this project are as follows:
• Find and understand indoor localization models that can work with the following
prerequisites:
– Sensor requirements: 2D 230º liDARs, RealSense D455 camera with IMU and
wheel odometry
– Dependencies available for ROS2 humble version
– Good review obtained from previous research (see section 2.State of Art)
• Implement the chosen localization models in pre-recorded real-world tests which
can work fast with the minimum computational cost possible and has a good
performance in highly dynamic environments
• Implement the chosen localization model for hospital environments that fulfills the
current needs; reliability in its initial pose and fast re-localization when being lost

2.1
Introduction
In the following chapter, all the ABCs of Navigation are stated and described, from the
most elemental subjects to the most challenging matters.
Starting from the problem root of navigation robustness and accuracy, passing through
different ways of pose estimating, and to the specific localization methods that are most
likely to solve this growing need, the following section goes from the simplest way which
would be the use of Kalman Filter, to the most advanced models combining Particle
Filter and neural network.
For each one of the models described below, a brief analysis of the pros and cons are
settled as well as compared to each other in order to get a clear idea of which ones are
better for the Yuman’s robot application.
2.2
Autonomous Navigation
Autonomous robot navigation is one of the most challenging problems that the industry
is currently facing in most of the growing sectors such as the medical field, manufacturing,
and automotive. This self-controlled navigation has 4 different phases [2];
• Perception: extraction of meaningful data by interpreting the sensors
• Localization: robot’s current location estimation in the environment using sensor
information
• Cognition: steps planning to reach a goal pose
• Motion control: modification of motor’s outputs to reach the goal pose following
the steps planned in the previous phase
Among all the stages above, the localization problem is the one with more research at-
tention due to its complexity and importance in order to reach an accurate and robust
autonomous way to navigate [2].
To perform the localization, sensor measurements have to be modeled. Traditionally,
measurement models were the beam and likelihood field models [3] [4], which treat dy-
namic objects as outliers, so are ignored for calculations. The main problem with this

2.3
Kalman filter vs Particle filter
Successful robot navigation requires both determining the path to the given goal and
localizing the robot within a known map. Thus, indoor localization is an essential build-
ing block of navigation systems for service robots, such as hospital robots.
When autonomously navigating around a closed building or room, both position and
orientation (known as pose) are determined using sensors and motion models.
On one hand, robots can use LiDAR sensors (see section 3.3.1), which return the dis-
tance to objects within the field of view as point clouds. But measurements are noisy
so apart from the LiDAR sensor, robots also have a way to dead reckon their position
using odometry data, which means that a future position can be calculated using a past
position and relative measurements like velocity.
Knowing those two possible ways to determine location, one approach could be the
known Kalman filter (see figure 2.2), which can blend noisy measurements with noisy
process models into an estimated state.

In this example, both LiDAR measurements and the

A Particle filter (PF) is an estimation filter that can handle non-Gaussian probabil-
ity distributions, employing a set of particles to model the probability density function
(PDF) of the system state.
In the beginning, the robot doesn’t know where it is, so there’s equal probability over
the whole map (figure 2.4(a)). Then the particle filter randomly generates a number
of discrete poses; random x and y positions and random orientation θ (figure 2.4(b)).
The number of generated particles will depend on the application, so there’s a trade-off
between computational cost and accuracy.
Taking into account LiDAR measurements, some particles can be removed already, since
they are clearly wrong poses. This is done by comparing the correct pose LiDAR mea-
surement with each of the measurements of the generated particles. Thus, some particles
are definitely more likely to be in the correct pose than others (figure 2.4(c)).

At this point, the distribution is no longer uniform, since there are some areas with
more probability than others. The next step is to resample the particles around these
new likely pose areas.
Once the localization method is run, a movement is also ap-
plied both in the real robot and all the particles in the filter to be able to compare the
final pose. This procedure is then done over and over again, creating an iterative process.
That random generation of particles around the most likely poses could be done with
a known method called Monte Carlo Localization or MCL. In this case, the method
is always generating the same number of particles, working in an ineﬀicient way.
MCL is the most popular one and it is a probabilistic map-based localization method
with good robustness level in real-world tests, but low accuracy in repeatability [8].
Getting back to the particle generation, in order to make this process in smarter way,
AMCL or Adaptive Monte Carlo Localization is applied. This Kullback-Leibler
Distance Sampling Monte Carlo Localization, or AMCL, recalculates the number of
particles after each generation depending on the system state, so the computational
resources are the minimum without compromising its performance.
2.4
Robot localization
Robot localization is a collection of non-linear state estimation nodes for robots to
move in 3D or 2D space. Those nodes are working under the frames convention REP105
(Coordinate Frames for Mobile Platforms), which states that at least 1 TF tree is needed
containing certain transformations [9] (see figure 2.5). The main transformations that
are needed in this TF tree are from map to odom, from odom to baselink, and from
baselink to whatever sensor frame in the robot.
Each of the state estimation nodes is able to fuse an arbitrary number of sensors to track
a maximum of 15 dimensions in the robot; X, Y, Z, ˙X, ˙Y , ˙Z, ¨X, ¨Y , ¨Z, roll, yaw, pitch,

Some potential alternatives to indoor robot localization are as follows [10]:
MCL
MCL or Monte Carlo Localization is an algorithm that estimates both position and
orientation of an object (in this case a robot) while moving in the environment, using a
particle filter localization and a given map of the environment [11].
The particle filter in this case is the one in charge of generating a specific number of
particles randomly distributed around the likely areas of the environment.
It is an
iterative process, meaning that round after round the particles are closer to each other,
concentrating all of them in those workspaces where is more expected for the real robot
to be.

sequence of observations (zt), as well as scaling factor (η), as shown in equation below

AMCL
AMCL stands for Adaptive MCL and it is basically an improved version of Monte Carlo
Localization, where the number of particles that are randomly generated and distributed
in the map is recalculated after each iteration. This calculation is performed thanks to
the incorporation of Kullback-Leibler Divergence sampling, or KLD, which measures the
difference between the two distributions and, subsequently, determines the number of
particles to generate depending on some statistics; how many samples have a probability
of 1-δ at each iteration and the error between the true posterior and the sample-based
approximation [15], as seen in equation 2.4. These statistics determine how good the
estimate is, based on the samples.

ICP
ICP or Iterative Closest Point is a point-wise matching method that aligns 2 or more
point clouds by, iteratively, refining the transformation between one point cloud to the
other (see figure 2.6). It starts with the estimation of the transformation, which can
be rotation, translation, scaling, or any combination of them.
Afterward, the corre-
spondences between point clouds are selected. This is the most time-consuming step of
the process since it is done by picking for each point its closes neighbor by Euclidean
distance [16] [17], as explained in equation 2.5. With those correspondences, the trans-
formation is estimated again and applied to the first point cloud. The process iterates
until it converges in order to obtain the minimum distance between two scans. This pro-
cess finishes after a maximum number of iterations or when the transformation between
two consecutive iterations reaches a determined threshold [17].

The main drawback of the ICP process is the high computational cost of big data
analysis, making it slow to process. In addition, dynamic environments generate prob-

On the other hand, ICP method is considered a robust solution, even with noise or
missing data. It has also high predictability because of its well-defined and predictable
process. So, overall, the Iterative Closest Point method is a good choice only for small
data in terms of time and computational cost [16].

NDT
NDT stands for Normal Distribution Transformation. It is also a point-wise matching
method that aligns two or more point clouds by, iteratively, finding their transformation.
Firstly, the space is divided into cells, all of them the same size, as represented in figure
2.7. Afterward, the probability distribution function (PDF) is computed per cell. This
probability distribution function (see equation 2.6 [20]) represents in the space how the
points in a cell are distributed, assuming that it follows a Gaussian distribution. Once
the PDF is computed for all the cells in both point clouds, the transformation (tf) that
minimizes the distance between the PDF of the two point clouds is computed. Thus,
that transformation with the minimum distance will be the one applied to align the
other point cloud.

NDT-MCL
Normal Distributions Transformation Monte Carlo Localization algorithm uses the NDT
to represent both the map and the sensor data in the MCL algorithm, releasing the dis-
cretization of the traditional method. Thus, a more compact representation can model
the environment with fewer cells than the occupancy grids used for the standard MCL.
On one hand, this upgraded localization method gives the same good performance in
terms of precision and repeatability as AMCL [12], but it has better memory require-
ments while improving its overall performance in a static environment. On the other
hand, when working with dynamic objects its performance could be equated to AMCL.
The main problem with both NDT-MCL and AMCL methods, though, still are the
semi-static objects.
DT-NDT-MCL
Dual-Timescale NDT-MCL is an upgraded version of the NDT-MCL method above. In
this case, it consists of two different maps; one static and one short-term map, which will
be used if the static map fails. With this map configuration, the system doesn’t have to
choose one timescale for the whole performance but can choose the best one locally. Thus,
this method gives a good performance for static, semi-static, and dynamic environments.
NDT-MCL with IRON
Another alternative for the NDT-MCL algorithm is adding IRON, which stands for
Interest point descriptor for RObust NDT-map matching. Adding IRON to an NDT-
MCL algorithm makes it faster and more accurate in terms of registration.
Other current point-based methods, such as SURE, ISS, SHOT, etc., build a local frame
of reference around the key points, which can lead to a problem if the direction is
unknown. In the case of IRON, the method does not rely on the sign, so the results
would be the same no matter the direction [22]. This means that no matter the sign
of the surface, the descriptors look always the same, giving IRON registration a higher
success rate than the other methods.
L3-Net
L3-Net is a learned LiDAR localization method that uses several DNN (Deep Neural
Networks) that, from a 3D point cloud and the online LiDAR point cloud, it gives the
optimal offset between the final and the estimated pose (figure 2.8).
This learned method is created by taking multiple sequences of testing data in different
temporal moments. Then, key points are extracted and evaluated in terms of linearity,
as well as feature descriptors, that are used to encrypt information about the points
regarding its statistics. To improve the accuracy, 3D convolutions are used, known for
their implementation in stereo vision. Finally, all probability matching of the dimensions

12

is computed, giving an optimal pose estimation.
As a result, this method achieves a 3-5 cm error in Real Time processing, which makes
this approach an accurate and fast option. Unfortunately, its accuracy and speed depend
on the GPU, which makes this method dependable on the hardware the robot has.
Figure 2.8. L3-Net architecture [23]
RT 3D LiDAR
This method approaches the localization problem by dividing the map into smaller points
in order to speed up the registration and, at the same time, increase the robustness of
the point localization, giving good output in terms of accuracy and frequency of the
localization.
It is based on two sensor inputs; LiDAR and any type of odometry. The odometry is
applied to the last LiDAR output, allowing for the estimation of the robot’s pose. By
using the information obtained from the LiDAR sensor, which provides measurements
of the surrounding environment, the odometry process incorporates the robot’s motion
and control inputs to estimate its current position and orientation (see Figure 2.9). This
prediction is then used to determine the ROI (Region of Interest) to match the current
LiDAR measurement. Then, ICP or Iterative Closest Point registers the current LiDAR
point cloud into the ROI point cloud. Finally, the odometry and the LiDAR are fused.
Instead of using the traditional EKF (Extended Kalman Filter), the SSKF is applied,
which stands for Steady-State Kalman Filter. The main advantage of this change is that
SSKF can work with delayed measurements, while EKF does not.
The main conclusion extracted from this method application is its low error at speeds
between 20 to 30 km/h.

Summary
In the section above, some localization models have been described, stating each one’s
characteristics and improvements regarding the others. As a recap, in the list below a
brief summary of the pros and drawbacks of each one is stated:
• MCL →Generally standard performance but sampling process very ineﬀicient,
leading to more positioning error.
• AMCL →Good performance overall but it depends on the size of the grid cells.
• ICP →Robust and predictable solution but only for small data due to its high
computational cost.
• NDT →Top options for its high accuracy and low computational cost, even
though it needs a precise initial pose estimation to give good results.
• NDT-MCL →One of the best options in terms of errors but its performance is
not robust enough in dynamic environments.
• DT-NDT-MCL →Improved performance compared to the NDT-MCL method
due to its higher robustness when working with dynamic objects.
• IRON →Best option in terms of matching due to its high speed and accuracy
levels. It aligns NDT maps with high accuracy and speed.
• L3-Net →Accurate and fast performance for autonomous driving, so some adap-
tations are needed for it to be a fit for mobile platforms. The main drawback is
that it depends on the GPU, so it might not be doable with certain robots.
• RT 3D LiDAR →Good option because of its way of using ROI (Region Of
Interest) for the matching process. On the other hand, its performance is better
at high speeds (≈20km/h), which is not suitable for this project’s application.

5.2
AMCL
Adaptive Monte Carlo Localization (AMCL) is a localization algorithm based on the
algorithm known as Kullback-Liebler Distance (KLD) Sampling [10] that uses relative
entropy to measure the approximation error. This localization method adapts the num-
ber of particles of the pose estimation during the execution depending on the states of
the systems, so it adapts. If the robot position uncertainty is high, more particles will be
used for the pose estimation, while for lower, levels it reduces the number of particles.
This localization method is based on a particle filter (PF) that, as explained in [10] [31],
works in a loop of:
• Re-sampling →Replaces random samples depending on the belief their belief in
the hypothesis represented (high belief, low chance of replacement).
• Sampling →Use the belief and control information to sample the solution space
(generate a new hypothesis).
• Importance sampling →Compute the importance weight of the particles depending
on the likelihood of a new measurement and what should be observed.
The main advantage of AMCL is that, since it is based on a particle filter, the estimator
is a multi-model state. On the other hand, it is a method that can only be applied if
==PAGE:38==

the space can be sampled small enough so the hardware is able to manage that amount
of discrete particles [10].

7.2
Default vs Proposal 1
As the first stage of testing the AMCL model, the parameters Default [33] and the
proposal 1 taken from [34] [35] parameters are compared in order to assess if the publi-
cation’s optimal values are close to this project’s application and, in that case, if they
can be taken as a baseline for new parameters proposals.
From publications [34] and [35], a big range of possible values for each parameter is
shown, so deeper research on each one of them and its effect on the algorithm is done in
order to define the ”optimal” parameters for this project and use them as the proposal 1.
The first parameters are the minimum and maximum number of particles (min_particles
and max_particles), which are set to 2000 and 5000 respectively for the next simulation
tests. One of the main characteristics of this pair of parameters is that the higher the
minimum value is, the less variance in pose and orientation, meaning that the minimum
value has a way greater impact than the maximum one. This is because the algorithm
doesn’t use the maximum number of particles in all the pose estimations. Taking this
into account and with some tests where the variance in terms of pose (x and y) and
orientation are analyzed, the best values are determined.
For the Kullback Liebler Divergence parameters, kld_err and kld_z, both are ana-
lyzed together. The main observation taken from [35] is that the lower the error is, the
lower influence has kdl_z on the overall performance. At the same time, though, the
lower kld_z is, fewer samples are needed on the particle filter. Then, kld_err has to be
high enough to make kld_z have a high influence and, thus, be able to lower the number
of particles. From pose and orientation comparisons the best values to use are 0.5 error
and 0.8 as kld_z. Nevertheless, the pose estimation with different parameters doesn’t
differ significantly.
As per minimum translation and rotation before filter update, both distance in meters
(update_min_d) and rotation in radians (update_min_a) are giving important informa-
tion for resampling and updating the particle filter. The best values are 0.05m and
0.125rad [35], since the smaller the distance is, the higher the filter rate and the faster
the variance decreases. These values are found after comparing the pose and orientation
variance when scaling the default values (x2, x0.5, and x0.25).
For the resample_interval [34], four different values are tested and compared; 1, 2,
4 and 8. The main conclusion taken from this parameter is that a trade-off is needed
between computational costs and sample interval. The lower the interval (1 and 2), the
higher is the cost since more information is taken and the lower the initial variance. On
the other hand, the higher the interval is (4 and 8), the less computational cost but also
less information, giving a bad performance for initial pose estimation. Since the best
value for this parameter will depend purely on the application, for this thesis a value of

The last overall filter parameters are the recovery alphas, recovery_alpha_slow and
recovery_alpha_fast. These two parameters are the ones that avoid the system to get
stuck into a wrong global localization and also the ones that help the robot with the
kidnapping challenge. After trying different combinations between 0.1 and 0.001 [34],
two main conclusions can be taken. First, the lower both fast and slow values are better
results in terms of convergence and covariance. Second, on the other hand, the higher
both values are, the more likely it is for the system to include random particles, making
the system perform better when turning. Since in this case the robot is aimed to move
through a hall, the turning localization is not one of the main issues to tackle. Thus,
the best pair of values for this thesis application is a recovery_alpha_slow value of 0.001
and a recovery_alpha_fast value of 0.01.
Starting with the laser model parameters, the first one is the maximum number of
beams (max_beams). As seen in [35], a higher number of beams reduces the covariance
value and, thus, improves the pose estimation. The main drawback of using a high num-
ber of beams is the computational cost. Even though, the maximum value of the robot
laser scanner (max_beams = 128) 1 will be the one considered for testing and might be
reduced in case the computational cost is not tolerable.
The z_hit and z_rand are the weight of each noise in the final sensor measurement.
The combination of both is the overall weight, so z_hit + z_rand = 1. This means
that if z_hit is bigger than z_rand, the weight of the sensor measurement noise is big-
ger than the random measurement noise. The bigger the random value is, the higher
oscillations are in terms of covariance, which will lead to non-optimal pose estimation.
Thus, the best pair of values according to the analysis performed in [34] are z_hit=0.8
and z_rand=0.2. Even though, different parameters do not change the pose estimation
in a significant way.
The last parameters to analyze and determine as ”optimal” are the alphas. Since
the odometry model type considered is ”diff-corrected”, only alphas from 1 to 4 are used.
As per the analysis in [34], 4 different options of alpha values are taken and analyzed in
terms of pose variance, orientation variance, and also visual external tracking position.
Taking into consideration all the results in the publication, the best option for our ap-
plication is option C; alpha1 = alpha2 = alpha3 = alpha4 = 0.05.

7.3
Proposals 2 and 3
The main conclusion taken from the comparison between Default and Proposal 1 param-
eters is that, even though the Proposal 1 values give a way better performance in terms
of covariance for the robot, there’s still space for improvement regarding how the sys-
tem reacts to turns and the combination between dynamic (people) and semi-dynamic
(objects) inputs at the same time.
The aim of the new proposals is to combine the benefits from both parameters, while
achieving the minimum overall covariance possible. Thus, the following two new propos-
als (2 and 3) are presented and tested.
The first one, Proposal 2, is obtained from an average between both Default and
Proposal 1 for each parameter, making the model reach a midway point between both
performances.
On the other hand, for proposal 3 a more exhaustive analysis of the problem has
been made.
It is known that if the robot’s covariance increases when it stops and
turns, the problem has a relation with the motion model, which is the model that deter-
mines how the pose changes depending on inputs such as speed. The main parameters
that are related to this model are alphas, which control the noise of the model, both
update_min_a and update_min_d, which sets the minimum distance and angle the
robot can move before updating the particle filter, and finally the maximum number of
particles or max_particles.
As explained in section 5.2.2, the lower the alpha values, the tighter the estimation
is, so based on Proposal 1 values, the new value for alphas is decided to be 0.03 instead
of 0.05. In terms of minimum distance and angles for filter updating (update_min_a
and update_min_d), a lower value produces more frequent updates and, subsequently,
to a lower covariance. Thus, the new values for those two parameters are 0.1 and 0.01
respectively. Finally, the maximum number of particles is known, which leads to better
results the bigger it is, improving the robustness of the model, which potentially results
in better pose estimation. For this reason, the new value will be increased from 5000 to
7000.

7.4
Proposals 4 and 5
The core deduction from comparing proposals 1, 2 and 3 is that the best option up to
this point is proposal 3, which combines a good performance in terms of turning and
dynamic objects but still has some margin for refinement. For this reason, and taking
proposal 3 as a baseline, two new proposals are being created, tested and analyzed in
all the easy, medium and hard tests.
The two new proposals are created by checking the parameters which make a dif-
ference between an improvement (proposal 3) and a setback, such as proposal 2. The
main differences between both are the alphas, the maximum and minimum number of
particles and the maximum number of beams.
The first new proposal, then, called Proposal 4 is a copy of Proposal 3 but with fewer
beams. Even though a big number of maximum beams may lead to covariance reduction
and, consequently, pose estimation improvement [35], it also involves a high computa-
tional cost. In addition, lowering slightly these parameters have some advantages, such
as reducing the noise introduced by laser measurements and reducing the magnitude of
the problem, making it easier for the particle filter to converge to the correct pose. For
this reason, the proposal 4 has 60 as max_beams instead of 128.
Alternatively, the last proposal, number 5, is also a variation of proposal 3 but in this
case the improvement is pursued by tuning the alpha values. These values have to be
tuned creating a balance between adding enough noise so the model can accommodate
uncertainties and avoiding too much noise that might lead to divergent and inaccurate
estimation. Hence, proposal 5 will have 0.05 instead of 0.03 in all alpha’s values.
Besides, for both proposals, the updating d value is increased to 0.07 to reduce the com-
putational cost, since proposal 3 was too time-consuming.
The final values for each parameter regarding Proposals 4 and 5, compared to the
baseline (proposal 3) are as shown in the table 7.3 below.

7.5
Future work
All the results and model modifications within this project will establish, hopefully, a
solid foundation for the Yuman robot in terms of AMCL parameters.
On one hand, and without significant additional effort, the obtained value could be
tested in real-world scenarios, tuning the model before running the robot and evaluating
in real life how the robot reacts to different working conditions.
On the other hand and in order to push it forward, the optimal values obtained in the
section above could be used as a baseline when implementing and testing other models
into the systems. For instance, as an outcome from chapter 2 State of Art, a good
fit model for this application would be NDT combined with AMCL. In this case, an
NDT-AMCL model could be implemented and the parameters from this project taken
as reference values to start a similar iterative process to determine the optimal values
for the new model.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
The main objective of this project was to find the optimal model for the Yuman’s robot
application; wander around hospital halls to deliver food, laundry or medical equipment
while knowing at all times its localization. This goal was achieved by tuning the already
known AMCL algorithm to find those parameters that enable the system to achieve
good performance when facing dynamics and semi-dynamic objects. Even though not
all the requirements were met, the aim of this project can be considered fulfilled.
The core outcome concerning parameters is that, even though proposal 1 already
presented a huge improvement compared to the default parameters, its gap for refine-
ment in terms of semi-dynamic objects has been used to create better proposals such as
3 and 4, which covariance are relatively stable. Even though proposal 3 is the one with
lower covariance values, overall proposal 4 presents a crucial advantage in terms of its
consistent accuracy across varying diﬀiculty levels, meaning that those parameters can
work for all types of conditions and still give an outcome pretty close to the optimal one.
Regarding the diﬀiculty levels, the primary finding is that there is a noticeable up-
ward trend in the overall covariance evolution as the diﬀiculty level increases. Unantic-
ipatedlyt, the test categorized as easy exhibits a performance that falls below expecta-
tions, ranking as the second poorest outcome, just after the hard test number 3. This
unexpected outcome can be attributed to the static object, initially perceived as a minor
obstacle for the robot, turning out to be significantly larger than anticipated. As a result,
what was originally considered an easy test is transformed into a moderately diﬀicult one.
All in all, the project followed objective justifications and showed positive results,
which hopefully will be used for improving the current solution.
