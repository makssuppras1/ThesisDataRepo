Abstract
(+)-Sieboldine A, an alkaloid found in the clubmoss Lycopodium Sieboldii, has
been of interest to the organic synthesis community since its discovery in 2003,
due to its intricate and unique structure, and the synthetic challenges they rep-
resent. In the course of this project, an attempt towards total synthesis of (+)-
Sieboldine A was made, following a synthetic strategy using (R)-5-methylcyclohex-
2-en-1-one as the starting material, and proceeding through a ring closing methathe-
sis/isomerisation reaction developed here at DTU. The synthetic work during the
project did not reach this far, however, as practical work ended with 5 out of the
planned 12 steps achieved.

Introduction
The discovery of the alkaloid (+)-Sieboldine A - isolated from the plant L. Sieboldii, a
clubmoss of the Lycopodium genus - was ﬁrst reported in 2003 by Zhang et al [1]. The
compound has shown promising medical properties on two fronts: It inhibited acetyl-
cholinesterase with an IC50 of 2.0 µM, and showed cytotoxicity against lymphoma L1210
cells (IC50 5.1 µg/mL) in vitro. The inhibition of the former is very relevant for treatment
of several neurological diseases, including Alzheimer’s disease.
Beyond its possible medicinal applicability, however, it is the structure of Sieboldine A -
elucidated through 2D NMR and X-ray analysis - which has rendered it of great interest
to synthetic chemists.

A year later, the Tu et al reported a successful total synthesis of Sieboldine A which
progressed via a biomimetic transformation of (+)-Alopecuridine, another member of
the Lycopodium species[3]. This transformation is shown in Scheme 2. The concept of
’biomimetic synthesis’ was introduced by Sir Robert Robinson in 1917[4], and involves
utilising reactions which progress via pathways mimicing those found in natural biosyn-
thesis. In the mechanism proposed by Tu, oxidation of Alopecuridine would create an
N-oxide, which might isomerise into a hydroxylamine. From here, the compound could
form the corresponding nitrone, either directly through oxidation of the hydroxylamine,
or via an imine intermediate formed by elimination. Finally, a nucleophilic attack from
the hydroxy-group on the nitrone would form Sieboldine A. Following up on their pre-
vious article, Tu’s group reported further developments of the synthesis in 2012, now
featuring (R)-5-methylcyclohex-2-en-1-one as the starting material[5].

The aim of the project detailed in this report was to develop a novel total synthesis of (+)-
Sieboldine A. Like the Tu group’s synthesis, the strategy utilises the chiral cyclohexenone
shown in Scheme 3 as its starting material, and is described in Scheme 4. The strategy
proceeds via a ruthenium-catalysed tandem ring closing metathesis/isomerisation reac-
tion developed by Ascic et al here at DTU (step 9 →10)[6, 7, 8]. Should this strategy
work, it will be the shortest route to a total synthesis of (+)-Sieboldine A yet discovered.
Unfortunately, the synthesis of the ester 3 proved problematic, with much time being
spent on a reaction that despite many iterations never proved workable, before another
approach proved very eﬀective. In addition to this, in order to have enough substrate for
later reactions, signiﬁcant time was spent repeating and scaling up the initial reactions.
As a result, the bicyclic compound 4 was the last compound synthesised, and due to time
constraints, only in crude form. As well as this, the symmetric hydroxylamine 5 was also
synthesised. A brief description of the strategy follows - reactions performed during the
project will be examined in greater detail within the Results and Discussion section.

Scheme 15: Organocatalytic synthesis of 1
The synthesis of 1 is a two-step, one-pot reaction, consisting of an organocatal-
ysed enantioselective Michael reaction, run under neat conditions, followed by an acid-
catalysed decarboxylation and cyclisation. The mechanism for the reaction is shown in
Scheme 16.
The catalytic cycle for the enantioselective Michael reaction consists of four steps:
Trans-crotonaldehyde 14 reacts with Jørgensen’s catalyst 15, producing an iminium
compound, as well as a hydroxide ion (a). The 1,4-addition of tert-butyl acetoacetate
then takes place, with the large aryl groups on the catalyst directing the attack from the
β-ketoester to take place on the opposite side (b). The hydroxide ion formed in step a
acts as a base to activate the reaction, forming a water molecule in the process.
The water can then act as an acid, protonating the enamine alkene under reformation of
the iminium compound (c), as well as a hydroxide ion. Finally, hydrolysis of the iminium
compound reforms the catalyst, and expels the Michael addition product.
At this point, toluene is added, along with para-toluene sulfonic acid. The acid hydroly-
ses the tert-butylester (e), which is followed by decarboxylation (f). Acid catalysis then
helps to tautomerise the compound into the terminal enol, proceeding via the correspond-
ing ketone (g). Finally, this enol undergoes an intramolecular aldol reaction, forming the
product (h,i).
The reaction was carried out according to literature procedure, and run four times, in-
creasing in scale each time. This culminated in a 2x4 g reaction (run simultaneously in
two separate 500 mL ﬂasks) producing a 69% yield, with an enantiomeric excess of 75%,
not too far from the 80% ee listed in the literature procedure.
This is the only compound in the project for which ee was calculated - literature values
for the ee of following compounds are not available, nor are racemic analogues available
for use in chiral chromatography.

Scheme 18: Mechanism for formation of 2 via Hosomi-Sakurai reaction
TiCl4 activates the cyclohexenone 1, forming an oxonium ion with the ketone. This drives
the reaction between the electrophilic β-carbon and the alkene in the vinyl bromide 16,
resulting in a cationic intermediate. Silicon is particularly adept at stabilising positive
charges in the β-position, and thus the silane group helps to stabilise this intermediate.
The σ-orbital of the carbon-silicon bond aligns with the empty p-orbital on the carbo-
cation, resulting in a particularly eﬀective form of σ-conjugation - stabilisation of the
empty p-orbital via donation of electron density by the ﬁlled σ-orbital (Figure 4).
SiMe3

Figure 4: Illustration of the β-silicon eﬀect
The delocalisation of electrons from the carbon-silicon bond also weakens it, easing the
elimination of the silane group from the compound, and reforming the vinyl bromide
functionality. Quenching the reaction with water breaks the interaction with titanium
tetrachloride, and the resulting enol will quickly tautomerise to reform the ketone.

producing 12 grams of the product, 73% yield.
The 8 g scale reaction also behaved
somewhat diﬀerently than its smaller-scale predecessors. While the small scale reactions
proceeded in liquid form without incident, the larger scale reaction mixture turned into
a slurry almost immediately upon cooling, thick enough to prevent the magnetic stirrer
from rotating, despite being run in the same concentration as previous reactions. By
nearly doubling the amount of solvent, and applying vigorous manual stirring, the slurry
was diluted enough for the magnetic stirrer to move again, and reaction mixture slowly
returned to liquid form. The synthesis was not negatively impacted by this sequence of
events - in fact, this last reaction had the highest yield of the three performed.
Throughout all iterations, the methyl group on the cyclohexenone seems eﬀective enough
at directing the attack of the vinyl bromide that none of the cis-isomer was formed.
Along with this compound, an analogous compound lacking the chiral methyl group was
also made with the achiral cyclohex-2-en-1-one as the starting material.

Scheme 20: General illustration of synthesis of 3 through Heck carbonylation
The synthesis of the ester 3 was performed via a Heck carbonylation - palladium-
catalysed formation of a aryl- or vinyl carbonyl compounds by reaction between aryl/vinyl
halides, carbon monoxide, and, in this case, an alcohol corresponding to the desired ester
product. The procedure was ﬁrst reported by Heck et al in 1974[23], an evolution on the
original Heck reaction[24].
The problematic factor in the Heck carbonylation is the use of carbon monoxide, which
11
==PAGE:19==
combines the unattractive qualities of being extremely toxic and odourless. For this rea-
son, various methods of generating carbon monoxide in the reaction mixture, so as not to
have to handle it in its gaseous form, have been developed. Two methods were attempted
in this project: Microwave-promoted carbonylation using molybdenum hexacarbonyl as
a CO-source, and a two-chamber system wherein carbon monoxide is developed in one
chamber, and diﬀuses into the reaction mixture in the other chamber. The former was
attempted ﬁrst.

The reaction between 4 and 7 is a simple α-carbon deprotonation followed by an SN2 reac-

vinylmagnesium bromide as a reagent. Victor Grignards work with alkyl-magnesium bro-

Scheme 35. The six-membered intermediate between the carbonyl and two molecules of
Grignard reagent pictured therein was ﬁrst proposed by Maruyama & Katagiri in 1989[33].
The α-protons between the two carbonyls in 4 should be acidic enough that they can be
removed selectively by hydroxide ions. Of course, hydroxide - being an excellent nucle-
ophile - could also react in an SN2 reaction with 7; by using only equimolar amounts of
hydroxide to ﬁrst deprotonate 4, and then slowly adding 7, hopefully this side reaction
could be avoided. Another alternative would be to ﬁnd a base which was a poorer nucle-
ophile - but this would still need to be of a basicity appropriate to selectively deprotonate
the α-carbon between the carbonyls, and not react with the one on the other side of the
cyclohexanone carbonyl.
As mentioned in the introductory section, the large alkyl group should direct the proper
stereochemistry in the Grignard product. It is hoped that it will not be necessary to
add an actual protection group to the cyclohexanone carbonyl prior to this reaction, as
it would add an additional two steps to the overall synthesis. Rather, since the cyclohex-
anone carbonyl is capable of forming an enolate - in contrast with the other ketone, now
bereft of α-protons - hopefully running the Grignard reaction at a pH under which this
enolate should form would ensure creation of only the desired Grignard product.

3.4
Cyclisation via RCM/isomerisation reaction (10)
The mechanism for the formation of 10 via ruthenium-catalysed RCM/isomerisation is
presented in Scheme 36.
The ruthenium catalyst undergoes a [2+2] cycloaddition with the alkene allyl to the hy-
droxylamine, under formation of a metallacyclobutane. The ring quickly collapses again,
but with the ruthenium complex having replaced the carbon at the end of the double
bond.
The ruthenium complex now undergoes an intramolecular [2+2] cycloaddition
with the alkene attached to the cyclopentane, forming the desired nine membered ring
in addition to the new metallacyclobutane. Once again, the metallacycle eliminates to
produce a double bond in the nine-membered ring, and expelling the ruthenium complex,
which can now act as a catalyst for the next molecule.
The RCM mechanism is followed by isomerisation before yielding the product, 10. Two
possible mechanisms for the isomerisation, published in a 2014 review by Nielsen &
Ishoey[34], are shown in Scheme 37. In short, the isomerisation can take place by the
metal hydride adding over the alkene, and then eliminating the metal hydride again, but
with a proton from the side opposite from where one was added previously. Or by the
metal associating itself to the alkene, making a π-allyl complex much like the one shown
earlier in Scheme 30, which is in equilibrium with both the original alkene and the one
in which the alkene has been moved.
As previously mentioned, one of Grubbs’ catalysts or its analogues seems a likely choice
for the ruthenium complex. The 2nd generation Grubbs’ catalyst is likely a good starting
point.

possible. In principle, either of the carbonyl compounds produced from ozonolysis can
end up with the oxide prior to reduction; but since it is an unstable compound, and CO2
is released as a gas, the oxide being on the ketone seems more likely.

4
Conclusion
The time spent on the carbonylation reaction, and speciﬁcally on one variation of it, along
with time spent scaling up reactions, has meant that the variety of experiments run dur-
ing the project is perhaps smaller than desired, as evidenced by the relative brevity of this
report. However, though the project did not proceed as far into the synthesis strategy as
desired, it was successful in ﬁnding an carbonylation reaction that worked for the vinyl
bromide 2 under formation of 3. Seeing how easily the two-chamber reaction worked
after a month of attempts at microwave chemistry was a high point of the project. It has
also been proven that the cyclic compound 4 can be formed from from the ester, moving
everything one step closer to testing the RCM/isomerisation reaction.
Several grams remain of 2, providing ample opportunity to synthesise more ester now
that the the two-chamber method has proven so successful at it. The synthesis of (N,N)-
hydroxylamine could perhaps be attempted again, but if the yield does not go up sig-
niﬁcantly, other avenues should be examined for the synthesis hydroxylamine alkylating
agent 7.

5.1
General Methods
Unless stated otherwise, all reactions were run in a nitrogen atmosphere. Glassware was
ﬂame dried while under vacuum to remove water, with rubber septums ﬁtted through
which solvents and reagents were added. Unless stated otherwise, all reagents were used
without further puriﬁcation.
All solvents were HPLC grade, and anhydrous solvents were obtained from Innovative
Technology Puresolv system. Reactions were monitored with thin layer chromatography
using Merck aluminum sheets coated with silica gel C-60 F254, and visualized either un-
der UV-light or stained with KMnO4-dip. In addition to this, UPLC/MS was also used
regularly.
Flash column chromatography was performed using glass columns packed with Matrex 60
˚A silica gel as stationary phase. Liquid phase is speciﬁed under experimental procedures.
Unless stated otherwise, all products were characterized with 1H-NMR, 13C-NMR and
COSY, recorded on 400 MHz Bruker Ascend with a Prodigy cryoprobe (400.13 MHz
for 1H and 100.61 MHz for 13C). Chemical shifts are reported in ppm (δ), and coupling
constants (J) in Hz. CDCl3 and DMSO-d6 were used as NMR solvents, speciﬁed in the
experimental procedures.
Optical rotation was measured on a Perkin Elmer Model 341 Polarimeter, at 589 nm.
For syntheses run multiple times, one journal entry is chosen as a representative ex-
ample, with the exception of the synthesis of 2, wherein the larger scale reaction behaved
in a signiﬁcantly diﬀerent way compared the the smaller iterations.
