2. Abstract 
Oceans cover more than 70 % of the Earth’s surface, and marine ecosystems and fish populations are 
of great importance to billions of people worldwide. Simultaneously, they are also impacted by 
multiple environmental and anthropogenic stressors e.g., increasing sea temperature and high levels 
of commercial exploitation. Changes in environmental variables can affect the reproductive success 
of fish and cause large-scale distribution shifts of fish populations, while an intense exploitation can 
induce changes in population size structure and increase the vulnerability to climate changes.  
Traditionally, taxonomic diversity has been used to investigate the resilience of ecosystems, 
ecosystem functioning or for monitoring changes in biodiversity over time or space. However, 
accepting the theory that not all species contribute equally to ecosystems, the trait-based approach 
has been suggested as a method for capturing the complexity of ecosystems, e.g., species’ habitat 
requirements and the interactions between species and their environment.  
In this study, by applying the trait-based approach to a trawl survey data set, I investigated the 
temporal and spatial changes of the fish trait composition in the western Baltic Sea and the Kattegat-
Belt Sea from 2001-2022. I used fish species abundances, and trait information on size, reproduction 
and life-history strategy and feeding ecology from a trait data base and calculated the community-
weighted mean of each trait (CWM). I investigated the temporal trends and spatial patterns of the 
individual traits and by ordination also the changes in the fish trait composition. Furthermore, I 
examined whether the observed temporal trends and spatial patterns could be explained by a range of 
environmental variables and fishing pressure. 
The results showed noticeable temporal changes in several of the traits, indicating that the ecosystem 
– of especially the western Baltic Sea – is moving towards a fish community characterized by species 
with fast life-history strategies. While this is clear trend for the Baltic sub-area, the Kattegat sub-area 
showed greater fluctuations with no clear trend. At the same time, the overall feeding mode trait 
composition showed a decreasing trend for planktivores, while benthivores had an increasing trend 
in both sub-areas. Using ordination techniques, I found the early years (until 2011) in one of the sub-
areas to be associated with changes in length, lifespan, age at maturity, fecundity and offspring size, 
while the late years (after 2011) were associated with changes in benthivores. 
The spatial analyses revealed that especially the northern edges of both sub-areas (Kattegat and the 
western Baltic) were associated with higher or lower values of CWM for most traits, and that the 
==PAGE:6==
 
IV 
 
temporal changes were not uniformly distributed in space but restricted to sub-areas off the coast of 
Sweden and in the southern Belt Sea. Moreover, I found sea bottom temperature, salinity, and fishing 
pressure to be important predictor variables in the models of temporal changes in traits, though the 
correlations with the individual traits showed opposing trends.  
The most parsimonious model of the spatial patterns of the trait composition included all of the 
environmental variables and fishing pressure. While the Kattegat sub-area were most associated 
changes in temperature, salinity and chlorophyll α, oxygen and depth were important predictor 
variables for the western Baltic Sea. Not surprisingly, the analyses revealed salinity to be an important 
driver of the spatial patterns for the data in both quarters, which is consistent with the findings of 
similar studies of fish trait composition in the same area. 
Overall, the results demonstrate that the survey areas, especially the sub-area Baltic, are undergoing 
major changes in fish trait composition, largely driven by environmental heterogeneity in interplay 
with fishing pressure. Kattegat sub-area showed greater fluctuations masking the potential overall 
trends. This might be attributed to the high degree of connectivity with the North Sea and the dynamic 
environmental conditions.  
The findings in this study provide valuable information about the temporal changes and spatial 
patterns of fish traits in a large marine ecosystem under influence of anthropogenic pressures, and the 
findings provide valuable information for the advances in ecosystem-based management of marine 
resources.

3. Resumé (dansk) 
Hav dækker mere end 70 % af jordens overflade, og marine økosystemer og fiskebestande er af stor 
betydning for milliarder af mennesker over hele verden. Samtidig påvirkes de også af flere 
miljømæssige og antropogene stressfaktorer såsom stigende havtemperaturer og kommercielt fiskeri. 
Ændringer i miljømæssige forhold og variable kan påvirke fisks reproduktive succes og forårsage 
store skift fiskepopulationers udbredelse, mens kommercielt fiskeri kan medføre ændringer i 
populationeners størrelsesstruktur og resultere i øget sårbarhed over for klimaændringer.  
Traditionelt set er den taksonomiske biodiversitet blevet anvendt til at undersøge økosystemers 
robusthed, økosystemsfunktioner og til overvågning af ændringer i biodiversitet over tid og sted. 
Såfremt man accepterer teorien om, at ikke alle taxonomiske arter bidrager lige meget til 
økosystemers funktion, kan man i stedet bruge funktionel diversitet (diversiteten af forskellige 
egenskaber) som undersøgelsesmetode (”the trait-based approach”) i et forsøg på at illustrere 
kompleksiteten af økosystemer, fx de individuelle arters krav til habitatet og interaktionerne mellem 
forskellige arter og mellem arter og deres omgivende miljø. 
I dette studie undersøgte jeg ved hjælp af ”trait-based approach” samt et datasæt fra den 
videnskabelige trawldata base (Baltic International Trawl Survey, BITS), de tidsmæssige og rumlige 
ændringer i sammensætningen af fiskesamfundets egenskaber i den vestlige Østersø og Kattegat-Bælt 
havet i tidsperioden fra 2001-2022. Jeg anvendte informationer om fiskearternes abundanser og 
indhentede informationer om værdien af de forskellige fiskeegenskaber fra en data base. De udvalgte 
egenskaber indeholdt information om størrelse, livshistoriestrategier, reproduktion og fødeøkologi. 
Jeg beregnede et vægtet gennemsnit af fiskesamfundets egenskabs-sammensætning (community-
weighted mean, CWM), og brugte denne variabel til at undersøge de tidsmæssige tendenser og 
rumlige mønstre i de individuelle egenskaber. Ved hjælp af ordinationsteknikker undersøgte jeg også 
ændringerne i fiskesamfundet samlede egenskabs-sammensætning. Derudover undersøgte jeg, om de 
observerede tidsmæssige tendenser og rumlige mønstre kunne forklares via en række variable, 
relateret til det fysiske miljø og kommercielt fiskeri.  
Resultaterne viste markante tidsmæssige ændringer i flere af egenskaberne (CWM), hvilket indikerer, 
at økosystemet – i særlig grad i den vestlige del af Østersøen – udvikler sig mod en 
fiskesammensætning, der er karakteriseret ved arter med hurtige livscyklusser. Mens der var en klar 
tendens for Østersø-området, viste Kattegat-området større udsving uden en meget tydelig tendens. 
Samtidig viste analysen af planktivorer en faldende trend, mens benthivorer viste en stigende tendens.

Ved hjælp af ordinationsteknikker fandt jeg at de tidlige år (indtil 2011) i Østersøen var forbundet 
med ændringer i fiskesamfundets gennemsnitlige længde, levetid, alder ved modenhed, frugtbarhed 
og størrelsen af larver/æg, mens de sene år (efter 2011) var forbundet med ændringer i benthivorer. 
Analyserne af de rummelige udfbredelser afslørede, at især de nordlige områder (af både Kattegat og 
den vestlige Østersø) var forbundet med højere eller lavere værdier af CWM for de fleste egenskaber, 
og at de tidsmæssige ændringer ikke var ensartet fordelt over hele området, men at de tidsmæssige 
ændringer var størst ud for kysten af Sverige og i det sydlige Bælthav. Desuden fandt jeg, at 
havbundstemperatur, saltindhold og kommercielt fiskeri var vigtige prædiktorvariable i modellerne, 
hvor jeg undersøgte de tidsmæssige trends, selvom korrelationerne med de enkelte træk viste 
modstridende tendenser. Den endelige og mest parsimoniske model for de rumlige mønstre indeholdt 
alle miljøvariable og kommercielt fiskeri. Dette viser, at fordelingen af fiskearter og egenskaber i det 
undersøgte område er bestemt af miljømæssig heterogenitet i samspil med kommercielt fiskeri. Mens 
Kattegat-området var mest forbundet med ændringer i temperatur, saltindhold og klorophyll α, var ilt 
og dybde vigtige prædiktorvariabler for Østersøen. Ikke overraskende viste analyserne, at saltindhold 
var en vigtig variabel, der forklarede de rumlige mønstre i hele området, hvilket er i overensstemmelse 
med resultaterne fra lignende studier i samme område. 
Samlet set viser resultaterne at det undersøgte område, især Østersø-området, har gennemgået store 
ændringer i sammensætningen af fiskearternes egenskaber, drevet af en kombination af kommercielt 
fiskeri og ændringer i vigtige miljøvariable. Kattegat-området viste større udsving. Dette kan 
tilskrives at området er tæt forbundet med Nordsøen, og at området er karakteriseret ved meget 
dynamiske miljømæssige variable. Resultaterne af denne undersøgelse giver værdifuld information 
om de temporale ændringer og rumlige mønstre af fiskesamfundets egenskaber i et stort og vigtigt 
marint økosystem der er under indflydelse af flere antropogene påvirkninger. Studiet indeholder 
desuden værdifuld information til fremme af økosystembaseret forvaltning af marine ressourcer.

5. General introduction 
5.1. Global climate change  
The Earth's climate has been changing constantly throughout its history, with periods of warming and 
cooling occurring naturally over long periods of time. This has previously caused extinction of species 
and destruction of habitats and ecosystems. However, scientists are now observing changes in the 
Earth’s climate across the entire globe happening at a much higher pace than previously seen. This is 
believed to be due to anthropogenic activities, especially burning of fossil fuels for energy and 
transport and changes in land use (e.g., deforestation, agriculture) (IPCC, 2022). The most recent 
IPCC report shows that anthropogenic activities are responsible for approximately 1.1°C of warming 
since the industrial revolution (1850-1900), which also marks the beginning of human-caused climate 
change (IPCC, 2022). The impacts of rising global temperatures are many, including melting glaciers, 
rising sea levels, and more frequent extreme weather events, e.g. heat waves, floods or storms (Ripple 
et al., 2022), but the warming of the Earth’s climate also affects human population through changes 
in available water resources (e.g. causing water scarcity) (Klare, 2020), uncertainty in food security 
(Molotoks et al., 2021) and implications for social equality (Schilling et al., 2020), mental health 
(Cianconi et al., 2020) and global security (Kalliojärvi, 2020; von Uexkull & Buhaug, 2021). 
Climate change influences organisms in terrestrial, freshwater, and marine ecosystems. Scientists 
have documented extinctions, and changes in distribution and abundances of species and recognized 
the cause to be climate change across all taxonomic groups (Parmesan & Yohe, 2003; Thomas et al., 
2004; Walther et al., 2002). In terrestrial ecosystems, scientists have investigated climate change 
effects on amphibians, a taxonomic group that has shown large population declines (Borzée, 2022). 
Though it is difficult to confirm, scientists believe that especially amphibians’ breeding phenology 
and the timing of prey availability are affected by climate changes (Green, 2017). Additionally, 
amphibians are also very vulnerable to loss of suitable habitats because of their weak dispersal 
abilities (Blaustein et al., 2010), which can result in population declines or extinctions. Similar results 
are documented for migratory bird species, for which it is crucial that they time their breeding with 
the time of maximal food abundance. In a long-term study of the migratory fly catcher species, 
Ficedula hypoleuca, Both & Visser, 2001 found that birds had advanced their laying date due to an 
advancing spring, but had not advanced their arrival date over 20 years. In a later study of the same 
species, Both et al., 2006 found a large decline in the same  areas compared with the earliest food

peaks to areas with later food peaks – indicating a mis-match due to climate change (Both et al., 
2006).  
Furthermore, Earth’s freshwater ecosystems are under pressure from a changing climate. Many 
freshwater ecosystems are naturally fragmented, limiting the potential dispersal of species 
(Woodward et al., 2010) and both lentic and lotic systems are already threatened by eutrophication, 
introduced and invasive species and hydrological modifications (Allan et al., 2005; Dudgeon et al., 
2006). Moreover, global climate change is expected to add additional pressure to systems via 
increased water temperatures and altered water flow (Döll & Zhang, 2010). In North America, a 
future extinction rate of 4 % per decade has been projected for freshwater species (Ricciardi & 
Rasmussen, 1999), while a report from 2022 estimated that since 1970, freshwater ecosystems have 
experienced a decline in number of species with an average of 83 %, though the decline cannot be 
attributed to climate change only (WWF, 2022).

5.2. The marine environment 
In the marine environment the effects of climate change can be categorized into three main trends: 
ocean acidification, sea level rise and global increases in sea temperature (see fig. 1.) (Doney et al., 
2012; Mendenhall et al., 2020). These effects are considered to be widespread and complex due to 
==PAGE:17==
 
3 
 
the vast size and the connectivity of the global oceans (Levitus et al., 2000). Ocean acidification can 
cause dissolution or weakening of the calcium carbonate structures in species, e.g., mussels, clams, 
and corals and thereby destruction of important habitat and food sources (Guinotte & Fabry, 2008; 
Hall-Spencer & Harvey, 2019). Sea level rise is caused by melting of ice sheets and glaciers in the 
Arctic and Antarctic region, causing more water to flow into the oceans, and by the thermal expansion 
of sea water when the temperature increases (Prakash, 2021). It has significant consequences, 
including flooding of coastal areas (Snoussi et al., 2009), displacement of human populations (Hauer 
et al., 2020), loss of biodiversity and habitat (Feagin et al., 2005; Mukul et al., 2019), and damage to 
infrastructure (Kumar et al., 2021; Salimi & Al-Ghamdi, 2020). Furthermore, several aspects of 
global climate change are contributing to an increasing sea temperature: The increase in atmospheric 
concentrations of CO21 traps heat in the atmosphere (IPCC, 2022) and changes in ocean currents can 
bring warm water to new regions (Timmermans & Marshall, 2020). 
5.2.1. Effects of climate change on the marine environment 
Oceans cover more than 70 % of the Earth’s surface and around 2.2 billion people worldwide live 
near a coastline, while many more depend on the ecosystems services that the marine environment 
provides (Kaiser et al., 2011). Important marine ecosystem services include: the provision of food 
(e.g. fish, seaweed and invertebrates), regulation of the climate (by absorbing and storing CO2 and 
solar heat), providing recreational opportunities (e.g. diving, snorkeling, bathing, whale watching), 
carbon sequestration and shoreline protection (Barbier, 2017; Costanza et al., 1997; Spalding et al., 
2014). It is uncertain how many marine species exist in the oceans, though some studies have tried to 
give estimate of the numbers, e.g. Appeltans et al., 2012 estimates that there are 222,000–230,000 
eukaryotic marine species, with more than 17,000 fish species described already. The impacts from 
global climate change on the oceans, will also affect the marine species that live in the ocean or 
depend on the oceans for some of their life stages.  
Marine fishes are found at every trophic level in most ecosystems across the global oceans (Downie 
et al., 2020). Many marine fish species depend on specific temperatures for different life stages, e.g., 
spawning, larval development and swimming, and changes in sea temperature can affect population 
growth and survival. In a review, Downie et al., 2020 found that swimming performance in fish larvae

were overall driven by temperature, though with a high degree of interspecific variation not related 
to temperature. Cod (Gadus morhua), a fish species of major ecological and commercial importance 
for the Baltic Sea, has an optimal growth temperature of 10 °C. Outside this optimum, fish can 
experience oxygen limitations leading to inhibited growth and lower fecundity (Pörtner et al., 2001). 
Changes in other environmental variables, e.g., oxygen and salinity can also affect the reproduction 
of some fish species. Laboratory experiments have shown that cod (Gadus morhua) eggs need at least 
2 ml/l oxygen for successful development and at least 11 psu for egg buoyancy (Wieland et al., 1994). 
As a response to climate change and the accompanying changes in the environmental conditions, fish 
species can adapt or migrate to more suitable conditions (Perry et al., 2005). Although some studies 
show that adaptation is possible but at low rates to climate change in some marine species, e.g. coral 
fish (Munday et al., 2017; Visser, 2008), many studies find that fish species tend to move to meet 
their thermal needs (Dulvy et al., 2008; Nye et al., 2009). Studies have already documented shifts in 
marine fish species in the North Sea, e.g. Dulvy et al., 2008 who found that the winter bottom 
temperature had increased by 1.6 °C in 25 years, while the fish assemblages responded by a latitudinal 
northwards shift for thermal specialist fish species, and the entire fish assemblage had deepened 
several meters per decade. They also found that this shift covaried with the changes in sea temperature 
(Dulvy et al., 2008). Parallel, in the North Sea, Perry et al., 2005 found that 2/3 of the fish species 
examined had shifted their distribution to the north and that these fish species were characterized by 
faster life cycles and smaller body sizes. This observed pattern indicating that species encompassing 
specific characteristics are more likely to respond to climate change has important implications for 
fisheries management.  
Similar results are found across the globe. In the northeastern part of the U.S., Nye et al., 2009 
examined 36 fish stocks, and compared the change in their spatial distribution They found that the 
center point of biomass had shifted poleward over the study time period from 1968 to 2007, yet with 
a high degree of interspecific variation. Similar to Dulvy et al., 2008, they also found an increase in 
the depth distribution of fish stocks.  
5.2.2. Commercial fisheries 
Many fish species are also commercially important species that provide valuable ecosystem services, 
e.g., food for humans. In addition to the effects of climate change, commercial fisheries put extra 
pressure on fish populations by removing large biomasses of specific species and size groups from 
==PAGE:19==
 
5 
 
certain trophic levels (Crowder et al., 2008; Genner et al., 2010). Indirectly, commercial fisheries 
also affect fish populations and habitats by e.g., disturbance, removal of non-target species, habitat 
destruction or alterations and modification of behavior (Crowder et al., 2008; Jennings & Kaiser, 
1998). 
Historically, there has been several examples of collapses of fish and shellfish stocks around the world 
due to over-exploitation, e.g. the Newfoundland Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) collapse in  in the 
1990s (Myers et al., 1997), oysters fisheries collapse in estuarine ecosystems in North America and 
Australia (Kirby, 2004) and the Atlantic herring (Clupea harengus) in the North Sea in the second 
half of the 20th century (Beaudry-Sylvestre et al., 2022; Dickey-Collas et al., 2010). Some of the 
collapsed fish stocks have not yet returned to before-collapse conditions.  
Recently, scientists have suggested that intensive exploitation decrease the marine ecosystems’ 
resilience against climate change (Pauly et al., 1998; Perry et al., 2005). While exploitation affects 
the larger size groups (Genner et al., 2010), climate variability primarily impact eggs and larvae and 
hence reproduction and recruitment success (Brander et al. 2010). 
Ottersen et al., 2006 investigated an Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) stock in the Barents Sea and found 
that the age and length of the spawning stock had decreased from the 1940s to the 1990s. They found 
this decrease to be influenced by climate effects on recruitment and exploitation. Similar results are 
found in the central Baltic Sea with a collapse of the cod stock believed to be because of climate 
variability and exploitation (Casini et al., 2008; Möllmann et al., 2009). 
 
5.3. Biodiversity in the light of a changing climate 
A common method to quantify changes in species, populations, communities or ecosystems over time 
or space is the use of the term “biodiversity”. The first concept of “biological diversity”, or 
“biodiversity”, can be dated back to scientific papers from 1950s and 1960s2 (Magurran, 2004) but it 
was first used in a 1986 report by Norse et al. for the National Forum on Biodiversity, which was 
organized by the United States National Research Council. The report defined biodiversity as "the 
variety of life forms at all levels of organization, from the genetic and species level to the entire biota 
and ecosystem levels" (Norse et al., 1986). The definition has since then been adjusted, but the term

a priority at national and international scale. At the UN Biodiversity Conference (COP 15) in

stated: “Reversing biodiversity loss is about saving our life-support systems, as well as our economy.

and livelihoods, and helps us fight climate change” (EU, 2023).

5.3.1. Measuring biodiversity  
Following the UN Convention on Biological Diversity’s definition of biodiversity, biodiversity can 
be divided into three different dimensions: within a species, between species and in ecosystems. 
These dimensions that biodiversity can be measured on are based on the fundamental hierarchies of 
biological organization, ranging from the genetic to ecosystem level (Díaz & Malhi, 2022; Noss, 
1990): genetic diversity, species diversity and ecological diversity. Species diversity considers the 
variety of species within a habitat, region, or other defined area, while the ecosystem diversity is the 
variety of ecosystems in a defined area, see Table 1 (Nonić & Šijačić-Nikolić, 2019; Noss, 1990; 
Schulze et al., 2019).  
Various aspects of biodiversity are used for quantifying changes in biodiversity, and the four most 
common ones are: phylogenic diversity, genetic diversity, taxonomic diversity, and functional 
diversity. In this thesis, though, the focus will be on the functional diversity and partly also covering 
taxonomic diversity and the interactions between the two.  
Phylogenic diversity is measuring the number of different evolutionary lineages (Magurran, 2021; 
Swenson, 2011). The concept takes into account the evolutionary relationships between species and 
is based on the idea that species that are more distantly related on the tree of life contribute more to 
biodiversity than closely related species, as they represent unique evolutionary lineages (Winter et 
al., 2013). The genetic diversity is the diversity of genes at the different levels of biodiversity 
(described above). Nonić & Šijačić-Nikolić, 2019 also use the definition: “a wealth of different alleles 
in the genetic makeup within individuals, populations, or species”.  
Taxonomic diversity is the most popular way of measuring and quantifying biodiversity by using the 
number of different species in a given area (Gaston & Spicer, 2004; Purvis & Hector, 2000). There 
are several indices that can be used to describe or monitor the changes in taxonomic diversity, e.g., 
species richness, evenness and α-, β-, or γ-diversity (Magurran, 2021). Species richness refers to the 
number of different taxonomic species present in a given area or ecosystem and is one of the simplest 
and most widely used methods in ecology. It is often used for investigating the resilience of 
ecosystems, e.g. Isbell et al., 2015, Mori et al., 2013, the functioning of ecosystem, e.g. Lepš, 2013, 
Walther et al., 2002, Hooper et al., 2005 or to monitor changes in biodiversity over time or space e.g. 
Jones, 2011, Yoccoz et al., 2001, Pollock et al., 2002. 
However, taxonomic species richness may not fully capture the ecological diversity of species needed 
for conservation planning (e.g., Halpern & Floeter, 2008; Lelli et al., 2019). Species can exhibit 
==PAGE:22==
 
8 
 
functional redundancy, where different species have similar ecological roles and functions, or 
functional complementarity, where different species have distinct ecological roles and functions, 
which can camouflage the ecosystem consequences of species loss. Species richness does not hold 
information about the function of the species, and therefore other measures of biodiversity, may be 
needed to fully understand the biodiversity within an ecosystem (Fleishman et al., 2006; Hillebrand 
et al., 2018).  
 
5.4. Diversity of traits in ecology 
While taxonomic biodiversity measures assume that all species contribute equally, and that only the 
biomass are separating the individual species’ contribution, this is not always true in ecology (Díaz 
& Malhi, 2022; Leitão et al., 2016; Magurran, 2004). Some species are considered to be keystone 
species4 encompassing characteristics that increase their impact on their environment and the 
interactions in the community represents more than expected from their abundance or biomass (Mills 
et al., 1993; Paine, 1969).  
In contrast to taxonomic diversity, functional diversity is measured based on functional traits. A trait 
is defined as: “any morphological, biochemical, physiological, structural, phenological, or behavioral 
characteristics of an individual”, while the function of the traits captures information about the 
individual species’ environmental tolerances, competition within the ecosystem and the species 
habitat requirements (McGill et al., 2006; Reiss et al., 2009; Violle et al., 2007). Instead of 
quantifying every species’ interaction with each other and their surrounding environment, traits might 
be better at capturing more of the complexity that the species in the ecosystem possess (Anderson, 
2005; MacLean & Beissinger, 2017). 
In this manner, traits define the set of environmental conditions for individual organisms to live, grow 
and reproduce (Violle & Jiang, 2009), the level and manner of interaction with other species in the 
ecosystem (Werner & Peacor, 2003), and the species’ contribution to ecosystem services, functions 
and processes disturbances (Díaz et al., 2007; Suding et al., 2008). At the same time, an individual 
species’ contribution will often depend on the combination of traits in a community and not on

5.4.1. Trait-based approach to community ecology 
Using traits as a measure of biodiversity instead of the traditional taxonomy, has been named “the 
trait-based approach”, and was originally developed in plant ecology (McGill et al., 2006), but has 
been applied to other systems as well. Yet, in the marine and freshwater environment most studies 
have focused on descriptive studies of filtering processes (Green et al., 2022; Özkundakci et al., 
2016), but during the last 10 years the number of studies adopting the trait-based approach have 
increased significantly and scientists have started to gain interest in the field of trait-based predictions 
of climate changes (Green et al., 2022; Verberk et al., 2013). Though most studies using the trait-
based approach are focused on exploring and descripting patterns in community composition, 
predictive studies of such have been published for the marine environment, e.g. Eklöf et al., 2015; 
Frainer et al., 2017; Jacob et al., 2011, similar for the terrestrial environment, e.g. Knott et al., 2019.  
The trait-based approach has especially been used in community ecology. The definition of a 
community is not carved in stone, and scientists have tried to define the concept. McGill et al., 2006 
defined a community as “the study of a set of species cooccurring at a given time and place”, while 
Magurran, 2004 had a more broad definition: “an assemblage consisting of populations of different 
species within a given area, where its constituents are linked by biotic interactions”. In community 
ecology, scientists seek to understand the relationships between species, the structure of communities 
(Mouillot et al., 2013), and how communities respond to environmental change over time 
(Brind'Amour et al., 2011). The high degree of complexity in community ecology calls for a method 
that can capture and describe that complexity in a simple manner and increase the mechanistic 
understanding of the responses of communities.  
Lately, the increasing focus on the applicability of using trait values in community ecology and 
conservation planning, has shown some promising outlooks for creating a better understanding of 
communities, which can be used for improving the basis for management decisions. Lefcheck & 
Duffy, 2015 examined an estuarine community and showed that using trait values instead of species 
richness as a measurement of biodiversity can give a more accurate prediction of the biomass of the 
community. Thereby, they managed to connect important properties (trait values) of a community to 
ecosystem services (biomass). Other studies support these findings, e.g. a study by Fontana et al., 
==PAGE:24==
 
10 
 
2018 that investigated the diversity of morphophysiological traits of phytoplankton in a lake 
community, and found that the use of traits improved their ability to estimate the biomass yield of the 
community.  
Though the trait-based approach has its roots in plant ecology, the methods have also been widely 
applied to marine communities, e.g. for phytoplankton (Weithoff & Beisner, 2019), zooplankton 
(Litchman et al., 2013), invertebrates (Bremner, 2008; Henseler et al., 2019) and fish communities 
(Beukhof et al., 2019b; Pecuchet et al., 2016). 
 
5.5. Denmark: Kattegat-Belt Sea and the Baltic Sea 
In light of the concerning current loss of biodiversity (EU, 2023) and with an increasing pressure on 
the marine environment, especially on commercial fish populations impacted by both a changing 
climate and fisheries, understanding, describing and predicting changes in fish communities becomes 
a critical research field.  
In this thesis a fish community inhabiting a sensitive marine area in Northern Europe and that is 
exposed to anthropogenic activities is investigated. This study covers a large survey area, ranging 
from the northern part of Kattegat to the southern-western parts of the Baltic Sea. The study area is 
characterized by a strong salinity gradient – ranging from above 30 psu in the northern part to below 
10 psu in the southern-western part. Therefore, to avoid clouding the results with the salinity gradient, 
the study area was divided into two sub-areas by the 15th degree longitude. One sub-area covering the 
Belt Sea, the Sound and Kattegat, hereafter simply called KATTEGAT (in capital letters to distinguish 
it from the sea called Kattegat), and the other covering the western part of the Baltic Sea, hereafter 
simply named BALTIC (in capital letters to distinguish it from the Baltic Sea), see fig. 2011.  
In KATTEGAT, marine ecosystems are located in the shallow waters between Denmark, Sweden, 
and Norway and are connected to the North Sea through the Skagerrak strait. KATTEGAT is 
characterized by a high level of productivity, which supports a diverse range of marine fish species, 
and acts as an important breeding ground for many species of fish, e.g., cod and herring. The nutrient-
rich waters of the Kattegat are influenced by both the inflow of freshwater from the surrounding rivers 
and the Baltic Sea and the upwelling of nutrient-rich waters from the North Sea. The BALTIC sub-
area is part of the Baltic Sea, which is one of the largest marine-brackish areas in the world, bordered 
by nine countries: Germany, Sweden, Denmark, Russia, Finland, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, and 
==PAGE:25==
 
11 
 
Poland. The area is highly influenced and dependent on saline, oxygen-rich water coming through 
the narrow connection to the North Sea, and it contains a unique mix of marine and freshwater species 
(Leppäranta & Myrberg, 2009). One of the key features of the Baltic Sea is the presence of extensive 
seagrass beds, which provide important habitat for fish larvae. The area is also an important area for 
commercial and recreational fishing, with a range of fish species, including herring, cod, and flatfish 
being caught in the region.

5.5.1. Aim of the study 
In this study, I examine a long-term scientific fish community data set, based on the Baltic 
International Trawl Survey (BITS). I investigate temporal and spatial changes in the individual traits 
of the fish community and the trait composition of the fish community. The selected morphological 
and physiological traits cover life-history, reproduction and feeding mode.  
Additionally, I examine potential predictor variables – fishing pressure, salinity, sea temperature, 
chlorophyll α and oxygen content – for the observed temporal trends and spatial patterns. These 
predictor variables are used to investigate the drivers of the temporal trends and spatial patterns in the 
individual traits and the trait composition of the community. 
Hence, the objectives of this thesis are to:  
a) 
examine and describe the temporal and spatial trends in the fish community trait 
composition, 
b) 
examine the temporal trends and spatial patterns in predictor variables,  
c) 
analyze the response of the individual trait and the trait composition to these environmental 
variables, and 
d) 
discuss the potential changes in and drivers of fish community trait composition in relation 
to a changing climate and put the findings into context with other relevant scientific studies. 
For the temporal analyses, the data were analyzed separately in two sub-areas, BALTIC and 
KATTEGAT. Additionally, the analyses were carried out for the two quarters, Q1 and Q4, separately. 
For the analyses of spatial patterns, the entire survey area was analyzed together but divided into two 
analyses using the data from Q1 and Q4 separately, which resulted in four separate data sets for the 
temporal analyses (BALTIC, Q1, BALTIC, Q4, KATTEGAT, Q1 and KATTEGAT, Q4 and two 
==PAGE:27==
 
13 
 
separate data sets for the spatial analyses. The structure of the analyses can be seen in the flowchart, 
see fig. 3.  
By investigating both the individual traits and the trait composition of the fish community using 
statistical tools, I will acquire a better understanding of the changes in the community, but also a 
greater understanding of whether the individual traits are good indicators for change in ecosystems. 
Additionally, I expect the analyses will provide insight into the differences of the drivers of individual 
traits and the trait composition. Hopefully the findings in this study will contribute to the growing 
pool of knowledge in the research field of traits, the trait-based approach and fish community ecology.

Patterns and trends in fish trait composition in the Kattegat-
Belt Sea and the Baltic Sea: community response and the 
underlying drivers 
___________________________________________________________________________ 
1. Abstract 
Marine ecosystems and fish populations are under pressure from climate changes and exploitation. In 
this study, by applying the trait-based approach to a trawl survey data set, I investigated the temporal 
and spatial changes in the fish trait composition in the western Baltic Sea and the Kattegat-Belt Sea from 
2001-2022. I used fish species abundances, and trait information on size, life-history strategies, 
reproduction and feeding ecology, and calculated the community-weighted mean of each trait (CWM). 
Using ordination, I examined temporal and spatial changes in the fish trait composition. Furthermore, I 
examined whether the observed temporal trends and spatial patterns could be explained by a range of 
environmental variables and fishing pressure. The results showed noticeably temporal changes in several 
of the traits, indicating that the ecosystem is moving towards a fish community characterized by fish 
species with fast life-history strategies. While this was a clear trend for the Baltic sub-area, the Kattegat 
sub-area showed greater fluctuations with no clear-cut trend. Spatial analyses revealed that especially 
the northern edge of the area was associated with higher or lower values of CWM for most traits, and 
that the temporal changes were not uniformly distributed in space but restricted to sub-areas. Moreover, 
I found sea bottom temperature, salinity and fishing pressure to be important predictor variables in the 
models of temporal changes in traits, while the analyses of the spatial patterns displayed sea bottom 
temperature, salinity, and chlorophyll α as important variables, which is consistent with other studies in 
the area. Overall, the results demonstrate that especially the Baltic area are undergoing major changes 
in the fish trait composition, driven by the combined effects of exploitation and environmental 
variability. Kattegat sub-area showed more fluctuations masking the potential overall trends. This might 
be attributed to the high degree of connectivity with the North Sea. The findings in this study provide 
valuable information about the temporal changes and spatial patterns of fish traits in a large marine 
ecosystem under influence of anthropogenic pressures.

2. Introduction 
Marine ecosystems are under pressure from anthropogenic activities, e.g., pollution from 
agriculture runoff and industry (Ducrotoy & Elliott, 2008), introduction of non-native species 
(Gallardo et al., 2016), overexploitation of marine resources (Scheffer et al., 2005; Sumaila & 
Tai, 2020) and climate changes (Doney et al., 2012; Hoegh-Guldberg & Bruno, 2010). The 
response of individual species, ecosystems and communities to these stressors have been 
investigated in many different ecosystems across the globe (Carrier-Belleau et al., 2021; Gissi 
et al., 2021; Hewitt et al., 2016; Jackson et al., 2021). Yet, the comprehensive complexity of 
ecosystems and communities challenge researchers in predicting the future response of marine 
ecosystems and communities to these stressors. 
Marine fish populations are a vital part of marine ecosystems and provide the human population 
with important ecosystem services, e.g. the provision of food (e.g. fish, seaweed and 
invertebrates), regulation of the climate (by absorbing and storing CO2 and solar heat), 
providing recreational opportunities (e.g. diving, snorkeling, bathing, whale watching), carbon 
sequestration and shoreline protection (Barbier, 2017; Costanza et al., 1997; Spalding et al., 
2014). They are also considered vulnerable to climate change because the successful 
reproduction of many fish species depend on water temperature (Pörtner et al., 2001; Wieland 
et al., 1994). Simultaneously, many fish stocks are heavily overfished and due to the size 
selectivity of fishing the size distribution of heavily fished stocks shifts towards smaller 
individuals (Brander, 2007; Johnson & Welch, 2009). While it is difficult to separate the effects 
of fishing from those of climate change, Planque et al., 2010 argue that fishing might act by 
altering the size structure of the population, and thereby changes the populations’ response to 
climate change. The effects of climate change on marine fish populations have been intensively 
studied, and climate changes have been linked to changes in species distributions (Hiddink & 
Ter Hofstede, 2008; Parmesan & Yohe, 2003), locally increasing species richness (Gordo-
Vilaseca et al., 2023), changes in the relative abundance of species with different biogeographic 
origins within communities (Beare et al., 2004; Fossheim et al., 2015), changes in reproductive 
success (Pankhurst & Munday; Servili et al., 2020) and alteration of food webs (Ainsworth et 
al., 2011; Brown et al., 2010). 
Typically, changes in communities and ecosystems have been examined at a taxonomic level 
using different measurements of taxonomic biodiversity (e.g., species richness, evenness, 
Shannon-Wiener index etc.) to investigate ecosystem functioning (Lepš, 2013) and describe the 
==PAGE:37==
 
23 
 
changes in relation to exploitation (Nañola et al., 2011) or climate changes (Albouy et al., 2012; 
Hiddink & Ter Hofstede, 2008). Taxonomic richness has also been linked to ecosystem 
resilience and functioning (Isbell et al., 2015; Lepš, 2013).  
Yet, in recent years various studies have focused on the functional (or trait) diversity of fish 
communities, and some have shown that functional diversity can be a better estimate of 
ecosystem functioning and ecosystem changes than the traditional taxonomic diversity 
(Coleman et al., 2015; Trindade-Santos et al., 2020). A trait is defined as any characteristic of 
an individual that can be measured at an individual level and is related to physiological, 
morphological, behavioral or life-history features of the species that control the individuals’ 
overall fitness (McGill et al., 2006; Violle et al., 2007). Moreover, traits have also been used 
to describe how functional diversity/trait composition is affected by climate change (Wiedmann 
et al., 2014) and exploitation (Petrou et al., 2021). This trait-based approach is believed to give 
a better understanding of community structure and ecosystem functioning and increase the 
predictability of species distribution under different environmental settings (Kiørboe et al., 
2018; Violle et al., 2014).  
Numerous ways of calculating trait diversity have been suggested (Laliberté & Legendre, 2010; 
Petchey & Gaston, 2002), though functional group richness is one of the earliest and best known 
(Tilman et al., 1997). Recently, the community-weighted mean (CWM) of traits has been 
suggested and used as a way to quantify the trait composition of communities (Beukhof et al., 
2019b; Frainer et al., 2017; Lavorel et al., 2007). CWM is the average trait value of species in 
a community weighted by their relative abundance or biomass. Studies of CWM of fish 
communities have been conducted in various areas, e.g. the North Sea (Beukhof et al., 2019b), 
Barents Sea (Frainer et al., 2017) and the Artic ocean (Emblemsvåg et al., 2022) and have been 
used to investigate changes in and drivers of trait composition.  
The study area, the Baltic Sea and the Belt Sea-Kattegat (fig. 4), are two interconnected bodies 
of water in northern Europe. The area is characterized by dynamic and complex marine-
brackish environmental conditions, and is highly dependent on the inflow of cold, oxygenated, 
saline water from the North Sea to Baltic Sea (Leppäranta & Myrberg, 2009). The area has also 
been exposed to several major perturbations in the past few decades, including overexploitation 
of some fish species and eutrophication (Bryhn et al., 2022) and most recently an increase in 
temperature (Belkin, 2009; Meier et al., 2022).

In recent decades, the many fish stocks in the area have undergone significant changes, largely 
due to overfishing, climate change, and eutrophication, and especially the commercially 
important species cod (Gadus morhua) has undergone the greatest changes. Historically, it is a 
heavily exploited species, and while it was very abundant in the 1970s and 1980s, the stock 
collapsed in the 1990s (Ask & Svedäng, 2019). The causes were believed to be combination of 
intense and long-term exploitation (Cardinale et al., 2010; Möllmann, 2019), eutrophication 
(Bryhn et al., 2022; Limburg & Casini, 2018) and climate change effects benefitting other fish 
species, e.g., an increase in zooplankton leading to an increase of sprat (MacKenzie & Köster, 
2004) that prey on cod larvae and eggs (Köster & Möllmann, 2000; Soudijn et al., 2021).  
In this study, I examine a long-term scientific fish community data set, based on the Baltic 
International Trawl Survey (BITS). I investigate temporal and spatial changes in the individual 
traits of the fish community and the trait composition of the fish community. The selected 
morphological and physiological traits cover covering life-history, reproduction and feeding 
mode. Additionally, I examine potential predictor variables – fishing pressure, salinity, sea 
temperature, chlorophyll α and oxygen content – for the observed temporal trends and spatial 
patterns. Hence, the objectives of this study are to i) examine and describe the temporal and 
==PAGE:39==

spatial trends in fish community the traits and the individual traits, and ii) investigate the 
response of the individual trait and the trait composition of the fish community to environmental 
variables and anthropogenic stressors – in both time and space. 
Investigating both the individual traits and the trait composition of the fish community, I will 
provide a better understanding of the changes in the community, but also of whether the 
individual traits are good indicators for change in ecosystems. Additionally, I expect the 
analyses will provide insight into the differences of the drivers of individual traits and the trait 
composition. 
 
3. Materials and methods 
3.1. Data handling 
3.1.1. Fish survey data 
Data on fish community composition were downloaded from DATRAS (the Data base on Trawl 
Surveys) (accessed October 24, 2022) hosted by International Council for Exploitation of the 
Sea (ICES) using the Baltic International Trawl Survey (BITS) data (ICES, 2022a). The BITS 
data were available from 1993-2022, and data was collected by 23 different fishing vessels from 
Denmark, Estonia, Germany, Latvia, Lithuania, Poland, Russia, and Sweden during the entire 
period. Data from the first (January, February, March) and fourth (October, November, 
December) quarter of the years were used, as very few hauls were performed in Q2 and Q3 and 
only for the earlier years. The data set contained weight and length information on all species 
caught, as well as information on haul time, duration, depth, and position (latitude and longitude 
coordinates). Data were gridded into survey grid cells, based on the ICES statistical rectangles, 
which is a stratification of 1° longitude x 0.5° latitude. The gridding of data aids in visualization 
and spatial analyses of data. Additionally, information on trawl gear, country, and ship was 
included. 
Before 2001, the surveys in BITS were performed using many different gear types (ICES, 
2017). Consequently, to avoid misinterpretation of the fish species composition due to 
differences in gear catchability, only data from 2001 and onwards were used in the analyses, as 
was done in Hiddink & Coleby, 2012. Since 2001, the gear type has been standardized so that 
research vessels only use two kind of gears, TV-3#520 (TV3) for smaller vessels and TV-3#930 
==PAGE:40==
 
26 
 
(TVL) for larger vessels (ICES, 2017), while 18 different gear types were used for in the BITS 
survey prior to 2001.  
To exclude coastal sampling, only hauls with a depth above 15 meters were kept in the data set. 
When information on depth was missing from specific hauls, a GAM that predicted the depth 
from position (longitude, latitude coordinates) was used to calculate the missing values. 
Furthermore, only hauls performed during the day and with a duration between 25 and 35 
minutes were kept and hauls that were listed as “invalid” were also removed from the data set.  
Information on species caught in the entire survey period were cleaned, removing all non-fish 
species and species with invalid or incomplete species information. The scientific names of the 
remaining species were checked and updated if needed, using their AphiaID in the WoRMS 
(World Register of Marine Species) data base (https://www.marinespecies.org/). Fish species 
that are known to be difficult to identify on species level were either excluded or merged into 
higher taxonomic level. Additionally, freshwater species were also removed from the data set 
(see suppl. material for complete list of species, section 8.1). Subsequently, to avoid 
overestimating the area’s resident species pool, very rare species were excluded, using the 
criteria that only species present in > 5 year of the survey period were kept. This total cleaning 
process excluded 32 species out of a total of 120, corresponding to < 0.00001 % of the total 
biomass. As a result of this, 88 fish species were kept in the data set.

3.1.2. Fish trait data  
In this study, I used eight traits that mainly covered life-history strategies, reproduction and 
feeding ecology to investigate the biogeographical patterns in fish species and changes in the 
community composition over time and space in an area under anthropogenic influence (Alheit 
et al., 2012; Hiddink & Ter Hofstede, 2008; Hoegh-Guldberg & Bruno, 2010; Sparrevohn et 
al., 2013). Traits covering especially life history strategies and reproduction have been linked 
to environmental conditions for different ecosystems, e.g. by Pecuchet et al., 2017 in the 
European shelf seas and (McLean et al., 2019b) in a coastal area near France, making them 
good indicators when studying community changes in time and space (Winemiller et al., 2015).  
The eight selected traits, representing different physiological and life-history characteristics of 
the fish ecology, were: Size (length), maximum age (lifespan) and reproduction (A50 maturity, 
the age at which 50 % of the population mature) and the growth coefficient, K5 - related to life 
history strategy and energy allocation, fecundity (no. of eggs/year) and offspring size – related 
to reproduction strategy and energy allocation and finally trophic level and feeding mode – 
related to trophic ecology and position in the food web (see Table 2).

3.1.3. Environmental variables 
Mean daily values of bottom temperature and bottom salinity data were obtained from the Baltic

chlorophyll α and oxygen content were downloaded from Baltic Sea Biogeochemistry Analysis 
and Forecast and Baltic Sea Biogeochemistry Reanalysis (MOTU) from the Copernicus Marine 
Environment Monitoring Service (http://marine.copernicus.eu/). Mean daily values of sea 
bottom temperature (SBT), sea bottom salinity (SBS), bottom oxygen content (BOC) and 
chlorophyll α concentration (CHL) were matched by day, month and year and position (latitude

Simultaneously, to investigate potential lagged effects of the environmental variables in the

Temperature, salinity and oxygen are variables that are known to have a big influence on 
recruitment and growth of fish species in the survey area (Möllmann et al., 2021; Stenseth et 
al., 2020), and all these variables are also highly influenced by the climatic conditions. 
Chlorophyll α (a proxy for phytoplankton) is expected to indirectly affect food web structure 
through bottom-up processes.  
3.1.4. Fishing effort data 
To get an estimate of the temporal fishing pressure, time series of total commercial landings of 
the most important fish species were deducted from ICES’s official nominal landings, which 
are available for download from ICES’ catch statistics (ICES, 2023). This was used as a proxy 
for temporal fishing pressure use. For the KATTEGA sub-area, yearly total landings of cod 
(Gadus morhua), herring (Clupea harengus), flounder (Platichthys flesus) and plaice 
(Pleuronectes platessa) were used in the ICES areas 3.a.21 (Kattegat), 3.c.22 (Great Belt), 
3.b.24 (Arkona basin) and 3.b.23 (the Sound). Additionally, sprat (Sprattus sprattus) was added 
to the list for the BALTIC sub-area, while data was obtained from the areas 3.d.25 (Bornholm 
basin), 3.d.26, 3.2.28.2 (Eastern Gotland basin) and 3.d.27 (Western Gotland basin) (see fig. 18 
and Table 3).  
The time series data set of total commercial landings were constructed using landings data for 
2001-2006 from ICES’ historical nominal catches (ICES, 2019b) and using landings data for 
2007-2020 from ICES’ official nominal catches (ICES, 2022c), and the preliminary catch 
statistics for 2021 (available upon request, obtained in January, 2023). Temporal fishing 
pressure (FP) and a three year rolling mean of fishing pressure (FE-3) were used as a predictor 
variable in the analyses of temporal patterns.

In the analyses of the drivers of the spatial patterns in the CWM of the traits, I used spatial data 
layers of fishing intensity for surface abrasion for the HELCOM region from 2009-2016. The 
data layers are available for download from ICES, 2019a. The data contained information on

The total surface area swept was summed in each survey grid cell, followed by finding the mean 
swept area across all the available years. This mean value (in km2) was then divided by the total 
area of each survey grid cell to find a swept area ratio (SAR, yr-1), which was used as spatial 
fishing pressure. The data was not available per quarter, so the spatial fishing pressure was used 
as a predictor variable for both Q1 and Q4 (ICES, 2019a, 2022b). 
3.1.5. Sampling effort (CPUE) 
Fish species abundance in separate length classes were converted into biomass using the 
information on number of species caught in the length class and the length class itself, using 
the length-weight power relationship:  
W = a ∙ Lb 
where W is the weight of the individual fish (kg), and L is the length of the fish (cm) while a 
and b are species-specific parameters. The species-specific parameters were downloaded from 
FishBase (Froese & Pauly, 2023), using the ‘rFishbase’ package (Boettiger et al., 2023) in R. 
For some fish species, several a and b parameters were available, hence when choosing the a 
==PAGE:45==
 
31 
 
and b parameters, the most appropriate parameters were selected: Firstly, matching the area 
(e.g., BALTIC/KATTEGAT), and secondly matching the entire or parts of the survey period 
(2001-2022). To standardize the sampling effort, catch per unit effort (CPUE) was calculated 
using biomass per one hour of trawling.  
The yearly sampling effort in each of the survey grid cells varied from 1 to 29 hauls, and as the 
number of species caught increase with increasing sampling effort until a certain point, 
compensation for the uneven sampling effort were applied. A method, similar to the one 
presented in Hiddink & Coleby, 2012 was used: For each survey grid cell in each year in the 
survey a random haul was selected. The procedure of selecting a random haul was performed 
100 times per survey grid cell per year. After selection of 100 random hauls for each grid cell 
in each year, the CPUE of each of the fish species in all length classes from all these 
permutations were used to calculate a mean CPUE per survey grid cell and per year. This 
standardized CPUE was used for the further analyses. 
 
3.2. Data analysis 
For the temporal analyses data were analyzed separately for the two sub-area, BALTIC and 
KATTEGAT, while the analyses were also carried out for the two quarters, Q1 and Q4, 
separately. For the analyses of spatial patterns, the entire survey area was analyzed together but 
divided into two analyses using the data from Q1 and Q4 separately (see fig 3 in section 5.5.1, 
page 13). All analyses were performed using R (4.3.2) (RCoreTeam, 2022). 
3.2.1. Community-weighted mean of traits (CWM) 
The functional diversity of a community is highly linked to ecosystem functioning and can be 
quantified by the community-weighted mean (CWM) of each trait in the community. CWM is 
the mean value of a trait, weighted by either abundance or biomass of each of the species in the 
community carrying the trait (Violle et al., 2007). The concept of CWM is based on mass-ratio 
hypothesis, that states that the most dominant species (in terms of biomass or abundance) have 
the biggest influence on ecosystem structure and functioning (Grime, 1998). Changes in CWM 
of a community, can indicate potential changes structural changes in the entire community in 
both time and space, which can be hard to detect looking at individual species (Mouillot et al., 
2013).

3.2.2. Trends and patterns in temporal and spatial changes in the fish community 
Firstly, the temporal trends in the CWM of the individual traits were investigated by plotting 
timeseries of the CWM against year, using a three-year rolling mean value to illustrate the trend 
in the data. The timeseries data on the CWMs of all traits was also combinedly analyzed using 
Principal Component Analysis (PCA), an analysis that can help identifying the underlying 
structures and patterns in the community trait composition.  
The spatial patterns in the individual traits were investigated using the mean value of CWM 
across all years and using a PCA as described for the temporal trends. The spatio-temporal 
trends were investigated by applying linear regression models to the times series of CWM in 
each survey grid cell over the entire survey period using year as predictor variable. The models 
gave information on the direction of change (positive or negative slope) and the significance 
level of the linear regression models. The PCA was performed in R using the ‘vegan’ package 
(Oksanen et al., 2022).

3.2.3. Drivers of temporal changes in traits 
I investigated both the drivers of the individual traits and the fish trait composition of the 
community (represented by the PC1 axis). To investigate the drivers of the temporal changes 
in the individual trait values I used Generalized Additive Models (GAMs). GAMs are statistical 
models that can be used in data analysis to investigate non-linear relationship between response 
and predictor variables, and it allows for a smoothing functions of the predictor variables 
instead of assuming a specific functional form (Hastie & Tibshirani, 1990; Zuur et al., 2007).  
For the temporal time series of the individual trait values, five predictor variables were included 
in the model: sea bottom temperature (SBT), sea bottom salinity (SBS), sea bottom oxygen 
content (BOC), chlorophyll α content (CHL) and fishing pressure (FP).

Each model was checked for temporal autocorrelation (AC) in the residuals, by investigating 
the Autocorrelation Function (ACF) and Partial Autocorrelation Function (PACF) plots. If AC 
was found in the residuals, a Generalized Additive Mixed Model (GAMM) was constructed 
instead using an Autoregressive (AR) and Moving Average (MA) correlation structure with 
year as a dummy factor, as GAMMs allows for this correction. The ARMA correlation structure 
allows for correction for both autocorrelations found in the ACF plot and PACF. Based on this, 
GAMMs were constructed for the following traits in Q1: Lifespan (BALTIC) and fecundity 
(Baltic), and for Q4: Offspring size (Kattegat), lifespan (Baltic) and growth coefficient (Baltic).

Model selections and predictor variable selections were done using the Relative Variable 
Importance (RVI) and the Akaike Information Criterion corrected (AICc). RVI measures the 
relative importance of the model variables in predicting the outcome variable. RVI calculates 
the percentage of variation in the outcome variable that can be explained by each individual 
variable, while controlling for the effects of the other variables in the model. AICc on the other 
hand is a measure of the goodness of fit of the model for small sample sizes, while penalizing 
for the complexity of the model. It balances the trade-off between the model's fit to the data and 
the number of parameters used in the model (Harezlak et al., 2018; Zuur et al., 2007) This was 
performed by constructing models for each trait/PC1 with different predictor variables (1-5 
variables in the model) and calculating both RVI and AICc. The modelling, fitting and selection 
of variables were done using the ‘mgcv’ (Wood, 2023) and ‘MuMIn’ packages in R (Bartoń, 
2023). 
3.2.4. Drivers of spatial changes in traits 
The drivers of the individual traits were not investigated in this study, but for the trait 
composition of the fish community, the drivers were investigated using a Redundancy Analysis 
(RDA). RDA is a multivariate technique that is used to explore the variation in the trait 
composition in a community in relation to environmental variables (Borcard et al., 2018; Zuur 
et al., 2007). Furthermore, all the environmental variables were checked for collinearity, and a 
VIF < 10 was accepted. To prevent too many explanatory variables in the model, a forward 
selection process of the variables were performed using the R2adj as stopping criteria (Borcard 
et al., 2018; Kindt & Coe, 2005). The analyses of spatial patterns were carried out using the 
‘BiodiversityR’ (Kindt, 2023) package for ordination and the ‘packfor’ package (Dray et al., 
2022) for the forward selection. 
The drivers of the spatio-temporal trends were not investigated further in this study.

4.1. Temporal trends and spatial patterns in environmental variables  
No clear long-term pattern was visible in the timeseries of the environmental variables, though 
SBT in the BALTIC increased from 6.5-7.5 °C in the first part of the survey (2001-2012) to 8-
9 °C in the second part (2013-2022; fig. 7a). Environmental variables in the Kattegat fluctuated 
more strongly (fig. 6a), with a few cold years in the beginning, stabilizing around 12 °C in later 
years. The mean SBS for KATTEGAT is stable, showing minor fluctuations (fig. 7b), while 
the BALTIC are affected by larger temporal fluctuations in SBS (fig. 7b).   
For BOC, the trends in the two areas show opposite trends with an increasing BOC in the 
KATTEGAT (fig. 6c) and decreasing BOC in the BALTIC (fig. 7c). Timeseries of CHL show 
relatively stable values throughout the survey period, yet with large deviations ins some years.

5. Discussion 
5.1. Temporal trends 
The long-term temporal changes in the CWM of the individual traits were most pronounced in 
the BALTIC, with length, trophic level, lifespan, and offspring size showing a decreasing trend, 
while the growth coefficient showed an increasing trend (fig. 9). Additionally, similar trends 
are found for the BALTIC, Q1 data (fig. 30 in suppl. material, section 8.7.2), which supports 
the indication that the fish community in BALTIC has changed towards smaller, faster growing 
species, shorter living at a lower trophic level with smaller offspring size and lower fecundity 
(Q4 only).  
The observed changes in the temporal changes in the CWM of the fish community in BALTIC 
are likely to a large extent driven by a decline in the Baltic cod stock. Historically, the Baltic 
Sea ecosystem has been dominated by this large, predatory fish species. The cod stock peaked 
in the 1980s (Eero et al., 2011) but hereafter declined drastically not returning to prehistoric 
levels (Eero et al., 2020). Simultaneously, studies have documented that the length of cod 
individuals has been constantly declining (Orio et al., 2022), and that the high fishing pressure 
in the Baltic Sea, especially targeting cod, have caused increases in cod prey, the smaller 
predatory fish, e.g., sprat, gobies and sticklebacks which are smaller fish species at a lower 
trophic level (Eriksson et al., 2011). Cod is the most abundant species caught in the survey in 
BALTIC, and timeseries of the total yearly CPUE of cod show that since 2010 cod has 
decreased drastically (fig. 28a in suppl. materials, section 8.6).  
The same pattern is evident for another large fish species, the European flounder, the fourth 
most abundant species, in the KATTEGAT (fig. 28d in suppl. materials, section 8.6). This 
support the fact, that the temporal decrease in CWM of length, lifespan, trophic level, and 
offspring size since the midpoint of the survey periods is primarily driven by a decrease in cod 
and to some extent also the European flounder.  
Comparable changes in the CWM of traits have been observed in adjacent ecosystems, e.g. 
Beukhof et al., 2019b found similar trends in CWM of length, trophic level, fecundity and 
growth coefficient by investigating a long-term scientific survey data set in the North Sea. 
Furthermore, this observed trend is supported by McLean et al., 2019a, that also found that the 
changes in CWM of traits in the North Sea indicates that the fish species community is moving 
towards smaller, fast-growing species. 
==PAGE:65==
 
51 
 
Whilst the timeseries of CWM of traits showed conspicuous trends in BALTIC, the same clear 
trends were not found for KATTEGAT. Instead, the timeseries of CWM displayed greater 
fluctuations throughout the survey period and additionally also some opposite trends between 
Q1 and Q4, e.g., for the growth coefficient for the last 10-12 years: data in Q1 displayed a 
positive trend, while the trend in Q4 were negative. The lack of overall clear trends in data is 
similar in the data for Q1 (fig. 29 in suppl. material). These great fluctuations are likely inherent 
to a dynamic environment with rapidly changing conditions. KATTEGAT is a transition area 
with environmental conditions varying greatly throughout the area (e.g., salinity ranges from < 
15 psu in the south of the area to < 30 psu in the north) and is inhabited by fish stocks from 
both the North Sea and the Baltic Sea that migrate in, out or through the area depending on the 
season. Besides climatic fluctuations affecting the inflow of saline water through the Danish 
straits, other factors could also contribute to the lack of clear trends.  
As an example, the North Sea cod stock, which extends into the northern part of KATTEGAT, 
has declined since the 1950s (Bartolino et al., 2012) and has periodically been overexploited 
(ICES, 2017). As for the BALTIC, cod is an important predatory fish species and 
overexploitation of large predatory fish are known to affect food webs (Myers & Worm, 2003). 
Concurrently, records of new, opportunistic warm-water adapted species in areas have been 
reported in the North Sea and in Kattegat during the last 20 years (Beare et al., 2004; Swaby & 
Potts, 1990), e.g. anchovy (Alheit et al., 2012) and sole and turbot (Sparrevohn et al., 2013). 
This may have affected not only the taxonomic composition but also the trait composition of 
the fish community in KATTEGAT, yet such species will not influence the CWM to a large 
extent until they have reached significantly large biomasses. Furthermore, the temporal changes 
in CWM of the traits cannot be explained by changes in the dominant species. From 2015 and 
onwards, two large, long-living species declined drastically (fig. 27 a & f in suppl. material, 
section 8.6), which is not reflected in either the lifespan or trophic level (that both slightly 
increases from 2015, fig. 8 b & d) or length (that fluctuates, fig. 8 a).  
Moreover, the slight temporal increase in benthivores in both sub-areas were surprisingly not 
driven by changes in the most dominant species (CPUE biomass) in the BALTIC. For this sub-
area, three out of the six most dominant species (fig. 28 d, e & f in suppl. material, section 8.6), 
categorized as ‘benthivores’ (see Table 9 in suppl. material, section 8.2), showed decreasing 
trends over the time period. For KATTEGAT, two of the six most abundant species were 
categorized as ‘benthivores’ (fig. 27 b & f in suppl. material, section 8.6), but these species 
showed opposite trends. Opposite, the decrease in planktivores in the BALTIC, seems to a large 
==PAGE:66==
 
52 
 
extent to be driven by decreases in the planktivores species, sprat (Sprattus sprattus) (fig. 28 b, 
in suppl. material, section 8.6). These results indicate that there might be other species or groups 
of species are driving the observed temporal changes, and this should be investigated further. 
Though, pelagic species were not excluded in this study, as e.g., Beukhof et al., 2019b and 
Hiddink & Coleby, 2012 have done, the pelagic species caught in this survey might be 
underestimated due to the survey design and the different catchability of pelagic species. This 
would infer that for further studies biomass (CPUE) should be standardized based catchability 
of the individual species (Walker et al., 2017). This might also be part of the explanation to 
why the temporal trends of CWM are not closely linked to the most dominant species (top six 
most abundant). 
The timeseries of PC1 show periods (4-10 years) with positive and negative values. It could be 
investigated whether some of these persistent, long-term changes in PC1 can be associated with 
a regime shift in the ecosystem. Regime shifts refers to a sudden, abrupt, and persistent change 
in the structure and function of an ecosystem. Since the beginning of the 1990s regime shift 
have been described for the Baltic Sea (Alheit et al., 2005), linked to trophic cascades in the 
Baltic (Österblom et al., 2007) and a recent study by Blöcker et al., 2023 have suggested that 
regime shifts can prevent recovery of depleted fish stocks. It might therefore be relevant to 
further investigate the presence of regime shifts. 
 
5.2. Spatial and spatio-temporal patterns 
Looking closer at the spatial patterns of CWM of the traits, the CWM for the traits is not 
homogeneously distributed within the two sub-areas. For KATTEGAT, the northern part of the 
sub-areas was characterized by a CWM of traits with fish species with greater body length, 
long-living, later maturing with larger offspring size. This can to a large extent be attributed to 
the area’s connectivity to the North Sea through Skagerrak, wherefrom fish species can migrate 
into the area. Some of the northern parts of the North Sea is characterized by large, slow 
growing, long-living fish species (Callaway et al., 2002; Daan et al., 1990). In the southern part 
of KATTEGAT, the low salinity shapes the fish species composition, filtering out species, for 
which their physiology does not tolerate low salinities. Salinity put constraints on fish 
physiology, e.g. on osmoregulation (Bœuf & Payan, 2001) or on fish reproduction success, e.g. 
egg buoyancy (Wieland et al., 1994). This environmental limitation by salinity has been 
==PAGE:67==
 
53 
 
demonstrated for the western Baltic Sea (coincide with the sub-area, KATTEGAT in this study) 
by Pecuchet et al., 2016. 
The same clear patterns are not visible for BALTIC, though the southern part over time seem 
to dominated by longer living, higher trophic level species, which is consistent with the findings 
of Orio et al., 2019, that documented distribution shift in cod (Gadus morhua) to the southern 
part from the late 1980s to 2016. There might also be large fluctuations between the years which 
are disguised when taking the mean value, because every year the BALTIC experience hypoxia 
at the bottom in large areas, which makes fish species change their distribution accordingly 
(Carstensen & Conley, 2019; Kõuts et al., 2021; Savchuk, 2013). In the northern part of 
BALTIC, the fish species community consists of fewer species, which has adapted to the low 
salinity, and though most of the traits shows lower values in the northern edge, some traits e.g., 
the CWM of the growth coefficient, K shows high values. This could be associated with fast-
growing opportunistic species, which are known to inhabit habitats with high environmental 
variability (Pecuchet et al., 2017; Winemiller & Rose, 1992). Contradictory to this, the CWM 
for offspring size seems to be higher in the northern part.  
The PCA revealed that the sub-areas are associated with two different trends (fig. 13), and it 
seems that the trends for KATTEGAT (positive values of PC1) are mostly driven by the 
northern part of the sub-area, supported by the spatial patterns. Similar for BALTIC, the 
negative values are associated with an increase in K and decrease in the planktivores – which 
is also supported by the temporal trends (fig. 9c and 10e) 
The analysis of the spatio-temporal patterns shows that some survey grid cells are characterized 
by highly significant changes in the CWM of traits, and it becomes evident that the temporal 
trends (fig. 8 & fig.9) might to a large extent be driven by these areas. Previously, the southern 
Belt Sea have been heavily influenced by eutrophication, leading to change in phytoplankton 
(Henriksen, 2009), hypoxia in the sediment (Karlson et al., 2002), which can have significant 
influence on the benthic fauna (Göransson, 2017) and hence the fish species composition. 
Though, eutrophication has stopped, these trends might illustrate this. In the northernmost part 
of KATTEGAT, the significant positive trends in CWM of length, lifespan, A50 maturity and 
trophic level is probably due to a constant migration of larger, longer living fish species fish.  
The drivers of these spatio-temporal patterns have not been investigated in this study but should 
be explored further.

5.3. Drivers of changes in traits 
5.3.1. Changes over time 
I found the most important predictor variables in the GAMs for both sub-areas to be the three 
year-rolling mean of sea bottom temperature (SBT-3), but surprisingly chlorophyll α (CHL-3) 
did not prove to be an important variable. This is contradictory to the study by Beukhof et al., 
2019b that found chlorophyll α to be an important predictor variable for the temporal trends in 
the CWM of fish in the North Sea. Other studies have shown that increasing temperature and 
phytoplankton biomass can lead to rapid changes in fish composition, shifting from larger, cold 
water adapted species to smaller warm water adapted species, e.g. red mullets and 
Mediterranean scald fish (Beare et al., 2004; Corten & van de Kamp, 1996; Hiddink & Ter 
Hofstede, 2008). This is also supported by the temporal trends in CWM for the BALTIC 
demonstrated in this study, showing an overall change towards smaller, faster growing species, 
shorter living at a lower trophic level with smaller offspring size and lower fecundity, which 
could be a consequence of both increasing temperature and phytoplankton biomass, though the 
latter is not found to be significant in this study. Similar, McLean et al., 2019b found an increase 
in fish species characterized fast life history traits and a concurrent decline of fish species with 
longer lifecycles, and argued that this might be due to a re-organization of the functional 
structure of the fish community induced by climate changes, making poleward distribution 
shifts, and by an intensive fishing pressure (McLean et al., 2018). 
The temporal models demonstrated that temporal fishing pressure (FP-3) was also an important 
variable, though the variable did not show an unequivocal relation with all the traits. A50 
maturity, length and fecundity (BALTIC, fig. 24 in suppl. material, section 8.5) decreased with 
increasing fishing pressure. As fishing is selective, directly removing large, mature individuals, 
indirectly making space for smaller, fast-growing species, these results were as expected.  
Surprisingly, an increase in trophic level is also seen for the model for BALTIC with increase 
in fishing pressure (BALTIC, fig. 24 in suppl. material, section 8.5) which unfortunately does 
not support the other results as smaller, fast-growing species rather would be at lower trophic 
level and which is opposite to the findings here. It is commonly accepted that intense fishing 
can influence species richness, D’agata et al., 2014; Henriques et al., 2014 also demonstrated 
that fishing can influence trait composition of fish communities. 
For the temporal trends for feeding mode, temperature was also an important predictor variable 
and were included in five out of the seven final models (for the non-categorical traits), though 
==PAGE:69==
 
55 
 
all the models had a low R2adj and should therefore be interpretated with caution. In the BALTIC 
sub-area, both fishing pressure (FP-3) and bottom salinity (SBS-3) were important predictor 
variables in the models. Even though the fish species inhabiting the Baltic Sea, are adapted to 
the low salinity, many of them are living at the edge of their physiological tolerance 
(MacKenzie et al., 2007), which can limit or expand their distribution on a yearly basis – 
depending on the environmental conditions.  
5.3.2. Changes in space 
Expectantly, I found depth to be an important driver of the spatial patterns of the CWM trait 
composition. Depth is a proxy variable for many other parameters (e.g., substrate type, light 
and temperature), influencing fish distribution. These findings are supported by other studies, 
which also found depth to be an important driver of spatial patterns of species richness (Gagné 
et al., 2020; Stefanoudis et al., 2019) or of CWM of traits (Beukhof et al., 2019b) until a certain 
depth where light become the limiting factor for life (Cornell & Karlson, 2000; Stefansdottir et 
al., 2010). Additionally, I found that salinity as an important driver of the CWM of traits in the 
area, using both the Q1 data (fig. 43 in suppl. material, section 8.7.6) and the Q4 data (fig. 16), 
which is consistent with the findings of both Pecuchet et al., 2016 and Frelat et al., 2018. 
Oxygen was found to be important driver of the spatial patterns using data from Q4 (fig. 16), 
and Q1 (fig. 43 in suppl. material, section 8.7.6), though in Q1 changes is oxygen is 
characterized by the RDA2, which explain less of the variation in data. Some areas of both 
KATTEGAT and BALTIC experience hypoxia in the bottom water each year due to algae 
blooms during the summer or early autumn. Oxygen is again supplied to the area during the 
late autumn or winter with inflows of cold, oxygenated water from the North Sea, re-
oxygenating the water (Leppäranta & Myrberg, 2009), which might explain why oxygen is a 
stronger driver in Q4 than Q1. Beukhof et al., 2019b also found chlorophyl (PCI) to be an 
important predictor variable of the spatial patterns of CWMs, which only were included in the 
model for Q4. This was as one could have expected, as phytoplankton blooms are associated 
with late spring, summer, and early autumn, whereas the Q1 survey is carried out during late 
winter. Concurrently, I found an overall decrease in the CWM of the feeding mode, 
planktivorous (fig. 11a & e), therefore the link in the food web might not be as strong.  
The spatial fishing pressure was included in the final models for both quarters describing the 
drivers of spatial patterns in traits (RDA). Intensive fishing pressure is known to negatively 
affect the structure of food webs (Preciado et al., 2019), abundances of target species (Jennings 
==PAGE:70==
 
56 
 
& Kaiser, 1998), the size structure of fish populations (Robinson et al., 2017) as well as the fish 
community trait composition (D’agata et al., 2014; Henriques et al., 2014) Historically, fish 
stocks in both KATTEGAT and BALTIC have been exposed to very high fishing pressure. The 
spatial fishing pressure variable in this study is based on a mean value of a swept area ratio 
(SAR) based on VMS data from the years 2009-2016. The VMS data has been identified as a 
promising tool to use as a standardized ecological indicator of spatial fishing pressure to aid in 
an ecosystem approach to fish stock management (Russo et al., 2013). Using the VMS data as 
an indicator of spatial fishing pressure, a study in Bay of Biscay on a bentho-dermersal fish 
community found that the spatial distribution of fishing pressure negatively affected both 
biomass, species richness and mean trophic level of the fish communities (Preciado et al., 
2019). The study by Preciado et al., 2019 also highlights the need for high resolution spatial 
fishing pressure data. However, a recently published study by Thoya et al., 2021 show that 
VMS data might not be sufficient to give a reliable estimate of spatial fishing pressure in the 
Baltic Sea, Kattegat and the North Sea. Thoya et al., 2021 investigated both VMS and the data 
from the global fishing watch - Automatic Identification System (AIS) - and found 
discrepancies both in time and space, which makes them difficult to use as an accurate estimate 
of spatial fishing pressure separately. Therefore, to improve the predictive power of the 
analyses, a better an estimate of spatial fishing pressure should be incorporated.  
5.3.3. Choosing the predictor variables – in time and space 
For the analyses of the drivers of both temporal trends and spatial patterns, I used a three-year 
rolling mean value of the environmental and fishing pressure variables, which proved to be 
better predictor variables than using the current year. The lagged effects of environmental 
variable have been demonstrated for sea temperature (Ikpewe et al., 2021; ter Hofstede et al., 
2010). For fishing pressure, McLean McLean et al., 2019b conducted more than 300 potential 
lags for the correlation of fishing landings and species abundances for more than 50 fish species, 
and found significant time lags in the negative effects of fishing to be 4-5 years, while (Faillettaz 
et al., 2019) found a 16-year lagged effect from fishing on recruitment in the case of the bluefin 
tuna (Thunnus thynnus). Examining the effects of different lag on the analyses could be further 
investigated. 
Though I found several significant drivers of the observed changes in spatial CWM trait of the 
fish community (fig. 16 and fig. 43 in suppl. material, section 8.7.6), a proportion of variation 
in the RDA remains unaccounted for. Similar patterns are evident for the temporal models of 
==PAGE:71==
 
57 
 
CWM for the individual traits, for which many of the models for the CWM of traits have low 
R2adj scores (Table 4 and Table 5). This implies that the predictor variables chosen for this study 
do not fully capture the spatial or temporal variation in the CWM traits of the fish community, 
or that the traits are not very good indicators of the response of fish communities to 
environmental change and fishing, and the selection of those should therefore be examined 
further.  
Other studies have investigated broader scaled environmental factors as predictor variables for 
temporal patterns of fish species, e.g. variations in the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO) 
(McLean et al., 2019b) and the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) (Ottersen et al., 2001; Stige 
et al., 2006) and the Northern Hemisphere temperature anomalies (Brunel & Boucher, 2007) 
Besides the broad-scaled environmental factors, other variables have been suggested, e.g. 
temperature seasonality which Beukhof et al., 2019b found to be an important predictor variable 
for CWM of traits in space. Moreover, Beukhof et al., 2019b used substrate richness in spatial 
analyses, while suspended sediment and bed stress (water velocity at the bottom) were 
suggested by McLean et al., 2019b. In addition, the proxy for temporal fishing pressure used in 
the GAM/GAMMs to investigate the driver of the CWM was based on the total landings of 
four/five commercially important species, while other studies, e.g., McLean et al., 2019b used 
the total landings of the individual species as predictor variable in a model explaining temporal 
trends in abundance of individual species. Choosing the most appropriate variable to 
demonstrate temporal fishing pressure should be carefully considered in future studies. 
 
5.4. Future perspectives 
In this study, for the analyses of temporal trends, I divided the area into two sub-areas based on 
information on salinity. In a study by Frelat et al., 2018, they also defined different fish 
assemblages inhabiting different geographical areas using a hierarchical cluster analysis. They 
found three fish assemblages in contrast to the two sub-areas I used in these analyses. Furthermore, 
in a study in Bay of Somme, McLean et al., 2019b also used clustering techniques to investigate 
and define sub-assemblages of fish species based on the functional structure. Clustering 
techniques to statistically define fish species assemblages could also be applied in this study to 
verify the definition of the sub-areas. 
In this study, I have used the BITS data set to investigate changes in the trait composition of 
fish species in an important ecosystem. Understanding the drivers of deterioration of 
==PAGE:72==
 
58 
 
ecosystems and changes in fish species composition in the Baltic Sea and the Kattegat-Belt Sea 
area, remains an important task as the ecosystems in the survey area are of great socio-economic 
importance for millions of people, proving important ecosystem services (e.g., food, coastal 
protection, recreation, and nutrient cycling). Though the trait-based approach to marine ecology 
recently has gained momentum with many new publications (Beukhof et al., 2019b; Kiørboe et 
al., 2018; McLean et al., 2019b; Pecuchet et al., 2016), taxonomic biodiversity is routinely used 
to investigate and quantify changes in marine ecosystems (Gamfeldt et al., 2015; Hiddink & 
Ter Hofstede, 2008; Tittensor et al., 2010). The complexity of ecosystems might make 
taxonomic biodiversity a too simple measurement, which is supported by several studies 
(Dencker et al., 2017; Villéger et al., 2010). Dencker et al., 2017 demonstrated that 
measurements of taxonomic and trait biodiversity diverge in both temporal and spatial analyses, 
and therefore highlights that biodiversity of traits can bring new important information about 
interactions and community structure. Understanding the discrepancies between trait and 
taxonomic diversity can provide valuable information to be used in management practice.  
The Baltic Sea, Kattegat, Belt Sea area have been heavily studied regarding climate change 
effects on fish species (Ammar et al., 2021), changes in fish communities (Viitasalo & 
Bonsdorff, 2022) and impacts of eutrophication (Snickars et al., 2015). In parallel, there is a 
need for an ecosystem approach to management which require a better understanding of the 
complex interactions (Curtin & Prellezo, 2010; Lindegren et al., 2009; Möllmann et al., 2014). 
This trait-based study brings understanding of changes in the temporal and spatial structure of 
fish communities and what the drivers are in a large marine ecosystem.

6. Conclusion 
Using the trait-based approach to a marine fish community, this study has demonstrated 
pronounced temporal and spatial changes in the trait composition of the fish community in the 
Baltic Sea and the Kattegat-Belt Sea are from 2001-2022 and that the changes can largely be 
explained by environmental variables and fishing pressure.  
Temporal trends in the CWM of the individual traits revealed that BALTIC has changed 
towards a community characterized by fast life-history strategy fish species, though the same 
trend was not as clear for KATTEGAT. The overall feeding mode trait composition showed a 
decreasing trend for plantivores, while benthivores had an increasing trend in both sub-areas. 
Using ordination techniques, I found the early years (until 2011) in the BALTIC to be associated 
with changes in length, lifespan, age at maturity, fecundity and offspring size, while the late 
years (after 2011) were associated with changes in benthivores. In KATTEGAT the fluctuations 
of PC1 were greater without clear distinction. Spatial pattern analysis showed that the trait 
composition was not uniformly distributed throughout the survey area, and especially the 
northern edge of the area was associated with higher or lower values. Moreover, I found that 
the temporal changes in the trait composition were most restricted to sub-areas off the coast of 
Sweden and in the southern Belt Sea. The ordination of the spatial patterns supported the 
findings of the temporal trends, as changes in the BALTIC were associated with changes in 
traits related to fast life-history strategies. 
For the temporal models, the predictor variable with the highest RVI score for both sub-areas 
were sea bottom temperature, though the correlations with the individual traits showed 
opposing trends. Comparable, the fishing pressure in the KATTEGAT showed the lowest 
importance in the models, while for BALTIC it was one of the variables with the highest relative 
importance. For feeding mode traits, the temporal models of feeding mode had very low 
explained variance. 
The most parsimonious model of the spatial patterns of the trait composition included all the 
environmental variables and fishing pressure, which indicates that species distribution in the 
survey area is determined by environmental heterogeneity in interplay with fishing pressure. 
While the KATTEGAT were most associated with changes in temperature, salinity and 
chlorophyll a, oxygen and depth were important predictor variables for the BALTIC. Not 
surprisingly, the analyses revealed salinity as an important driver of spatial patterns for the data 
in both quarters, which is consistent with the findings of similar studies. 
==PAGE:74==
 
60 
 
A proportion of the variation in the models remains unaccounted for, indicating either 
individual traits not performing well as indicators of changes or that the composition or the 
number of lags of the predictor variables should be further investigated. Additionally, 
standardizing survey data to account for the catchability of fish species to avoid underestimation 
of especially semi-pelagic species is advised for further studies and investigating the amount of 
best lagged-ness the best could also improve the predictive power of the analyses.  
Using the trait-based approach to this fish community follows a trend in the scientific literature, 
that recently has gained momentum as a promising method to achieve better understanding of 
the changes in fish community and their drivers. The findings in this study provide valuable 
information about the temporal changes and spatial patterns in an important marine ecosystem 
under influence of anthropogenic pressure.

9. Original project plan 
 
Project Plan:  
Temporal and spatial trends in fish community traits in the Baltic Sea: How has the fish community 
composition of traits changed, and what are the environmental drivers? 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Project description 
Increasing sea temperature caused by global climate changes are expected to affect the marine 
environment in many ways (Parmesan and Yohe 2003; IPCC 2022). It can cause changes in species 
abundance, redistribution of biodiversity or “introduction” of non-native species. This can lead to 
changes in both ecosystem functioning and food web dynamics. Marine fish and the diversity of 
marine fish species play an important role in the ecosystems and they highly influence water quality, 
nutrient cycling, carbon storage and the functioning of food webs (Holmlund and Hammer 1999; 
Villéger et al. 2017). Consequently, it is crucial to investigate the temporal dynamics of fish 
communities to predict and understand the impacts of climate changes on ecosystem services and 
functioning (Worm et al. 2006; Frainer et al. 2017). Traditionally, researchers have used a taxonomic 
approach to biodiversity to analyze and describe the temporal and spatial dynamics of fish species 
communities, but recently, researchers have started to investigate the temporal trends in the trait 
diversity of a community (Dencker et al. 2017; Beukhof et al. 2019b). A trait of a species is a 
morphological, physiological or phenological characteristics and can also be defined as: “any 
biological attribute measurable on an individual that impacts organism performance and thus fitness”

The Baltic Sea is an important and sensitive ecosystem that historically has been subject 
to a high fishing pressure and is now also experiencing increases in sea temperature and declining 
commercial fish populations (The BACC II Author Team 2015; Orio et al. 2022). The changes in the 
environmental variables can lead to changes in food webs and ecosystem functioning. In this project, 
I examine a long-term high resolution scientific fish data set from the Baltic Sea to investigate the 
changes in the fish community trait composition over time and space. In the analyses, I use several 
traits and environmental variables to describe and explain the changes in the fish species community.  
Hence, the objectives of this project are to i) examine and describe the temporal and 
spatial changes/trends in multiple fish community the traits using the BITS (Baltic International Trawl 
Survey) fish survey data for the Baltic Sea and a fish trait data base (Beukhof et al. 2019a), and ii) 
analyze the response of these traits to environmental variables (e.g. sea temperature and salinity using 
different statistical tools and methods and iii) discuss whether the potential changes in trait 
composition of the fish communities can be explained by commercial fishing or by changes in 
environmental variables.

Learning objectives 
After completing this master’s thesis project, the student will be able to: 
• Build hypotheses, design a project, extract data from international data bases and conduct 
analyses of a large data set  
• Explain the general concept of the trait-based approach to ecology and community assembly 
processes – and relate both to the relevant fish communities   
• Investigate and describe the role of time and space for changes in fish community traits 
• Demonstrate, and explain how the changes in fish community traits can be a correlated to
