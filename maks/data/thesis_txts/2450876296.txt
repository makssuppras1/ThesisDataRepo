Abstract
This Master thesis project is used as a pilot study for a PhD project initiated by the
International Centre for Indoor Environment and Energy at DTU Byg. It focuses on
building up a comprehensive protocol that aims at characterizing oﬃce buildings’ indoor
environment and occupants’ well-being and health conditions.
This protocol was tested in ﬁve oﬃce buildings on DTU campus and the data obtained
during the practical phases were analyzed to assess the validity of the methodology both
in terms of use for indoor environment characterization and for the development of a
consistent benchmarking tool.
Fundamental tools were developed such as buildings’ and oﬃces’ checklists, occupants’
well-being and health questionnaires and a systematic process focusing on an accurate
organization of the measurement campaigns.
The data obtained from these tools were used to characterize the ﬁve assessed DTU
buildings with particular focus on buildings’ characteristics, occupants’ satisfaction with
indoor climate and health related issues as well as an assessment of the impact of these
issues on their working conditions. Objective indoor environment conditions were de-
scribed through temperature, relative humidity, CO2 concentration, illuminance and
sound pressure level. The subjective data, from occupants’ evaluation, and objective
data, from the measurement campaigns, were gathered in a database and analyzed to
characterize the buildings’ indoor environment and their occupants’ satisfaction.
A benchmarking tool was developed based on a preliminary analysis of building owners’
interests for indoor environmental quality matters. It was, then, tested using the data
from the DTU buildings’ database. Applying speciﬁc weights for subjective and objec-
tive assessments as well as for the diﬀerent indoor environment metrics, the buildings
were benchmarked and provided with a certiﬁcate evaluating their indoor environmental
performance.

Preface
This Master thesis was realized as a ﬁnal project of a Master degree in Architectural
Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark, corresponding to 30 ECTS. It was
carried out with the support of the International Centre for Indoor Environment and

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
General context
Typically, oﬃce buildings have been managed as facilities mostly unrelated to their
occupants’ well-being and comfort. Therefore, with the thought of minimizing costs,
and especially energy costs, as much as possible, the main building practices have been
focusing on energy savings and airtightness without looking at any other parameter.
However, in the last decades, a change has occurred mainly due to a large number of
research projects investigating the relation between indoor environment and occupants’
well-being and productivity in oﬃces (Wargocki et al., 2000; Sepp¨anen et al., 2006;
Allen et al., 2016) as well as the value gain of enhancing indoor environmental quality
in these oﬃces (?MacNaughton et al., 2015). Thus, oﬃce buildings have recently been
depicted more and more as assets instead of only being objects of expenditure based on
the fact that high indoor environmental quality has been shown to be a very eﬃcient
way to improve eﬃciency and well-being. Moreover, these changes do occur at a large
scale, through national and European building standards and regulations for instance,
since the impact of indoor environmental issues go beyond the time spent in oﬃces.
Therefore, it is particularly useful to characterize periodically the indoor environment
in oﬃce buildings in a country such as Denmark since the last large-scale surveys, such
as the European audit project to optimize indoor air quality and energy consumption in
oﬃce buildings (Bluyssen et al., 1995) were carried out more than 20 years ago.

1.2
Background project
The section for Indoor Environment at DTU Byg has launched the project Benchmark-
ing the indoor environment in oﬃce buildings - characterization, synthesis, and practical
application (Toftum, 2018) that aims at characterizing the indoor environment and oc-
cupants’ well-being, health and productivity in a wide range of oﬃce buildings located
in Denmark and Greenland. This study is completed as a PhD study by the candidate
Emilie P. Dam-Krogh and supervised by Professor Jørn Toftum. The overall goal of this
study is to be able to use the data obtained along the project in order to ﬁll a database
which will be expanded even after the completion of the PhD study. Moreover, this
database will be the opportunity to develop a standardized indoor environment bench-
marking tool. This tool will be developed in order to oﬀer the possibility to building
stakeholders such as buildings owners, facilities managers and tenants, to assess their
built environment and workplace performance and determine the potential value gain of
undertaking indoor environment improvement actions. This study, hereinafter referred
to as background project, is organized in four phases (Toftum, 2018):
• Build up the experimental protocol including measurement procedures, experimen-
tal metrics and tools, building and test space checklists and occupants’ perceptions
questionnaire.
• Carry out a pilot study in several buildings to test the experimental procedure
from the ﬁrst phase and adapt it when it is needed.
• Carry out actual measurements for the study in at least 50-100 buildings in Den-
mark and Greenland.
• Analyse the results from the third phase and set up the database and develop a
benchmarking tool aiming at being commercialised afterwards.

Objectives
This Master thesis takes place as part of the background project and aims at covering its
two ﬁrst phases. The ﬁrst weeks of the project, mainly focusing on developing the ques-
tionnaire, were undertaken in collaboration, including joint meetings, with another MSc

adaptable enough to the larger scale of the background project.
• Organize a pilot study involving the necessary stakeholders and carry it out in

• Analyse the data obtained from the measurement campaigns and use it to charac-

• Develop a benchmarking tool exploiting the metrics from the database and used to
survey oﬃce buildings’ indoor environment and benchmark it against other build-
ings’ data to highlight the potential gains from indoor environment improvements.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
This review focuses on the knowledge acquired during the ﬁrst weeks of the Master thesis
project aiming at understanding how indoor environment is usually assessed in large scale
studies through accurate scientiﬁc protocols. In fact, this knowledge represents the basis
for the ﬁrst objective of this project.
2.1
Methodology
For IEQ data to be reliable, it is mandatory to take into account a large number of
parameters for the measuring phase as well as during the data analysis. In order to
ﬁnd the most relevant parameters, it is interesting to focus on previous studies involv-
ing indoor environment assessments in oﬃce buildings and pay speciﬁc attention to the
protocol used in these studies. To do so, the methodology has been, ﬁrst, to look for
research papers dealing with indoor environmental quality assessment studies in oﬃce
buildings. These papers allowed us to understand which parameters have to be taken
into account when designing not only the measuring protocol but also selecting the
buildings, the areas to be studied inside these buildings or how to gather eﬃciently all
the measurement data. A systematic method has been used to look for these papers by
focusing on inclusion and exclusion criteria as well as on speciﬁc keywords as listed below.

2.2
Findings
This section presents ﬁndings from the eleven above-mentioned papers. The main focus
is to ﬁnd successful practices about IEQ assessment, and, at a larger scale, the main
points to consider when building up a study protocol. The ﬁndings are organized in a
way that reﬂects the diﬀerent steps to follow for a complete study about indoor environ-
ment assessment in oﬃce buildings including several facilities: choice of buildings as well
as the speciﬁc oﬃces and monitoring locations, set-up of both the questionnaire and the
checklists and ﬁnally the parameters to be considered and how to measure them. These
diﬀerent steps are brought into focus by the two large-scale studies carried out in the
United States (EPA, 2003) and in Europe (Bluyssen et al., 1995).
9
==PAGE:29==
Chapter 2. Literature Review
Building selection
The European study (Bluyssen et al., 1995) shows that the ﬁrst stage is to choose the
correct buildings to investigate. In each country where this study was done, the aim was
to characterize the building stock, thus the selected buildings had to be representative of
it. The two main criteria for this representativeness were the age of the building and the
type of construction. In the same study, other constraints are taken into account such as
the number of occupants (at least 125 people), the age of the building (at least two years
old) or the location (the outdoor air should be reasonably fresh). Some demographic
parameters were also taken into consideration, although not mandatory. For instance,
it is better to have a homogeneous group of people in terms of sex and in terms of type
of white-collar and clerical workers since women are usually more sensitive to the envi-
ronment conditions and social workers usually describe more symptoms than architects,
engineers or technicians (Clausen et al., 1993).
Test space selection
Once the buildings have been chosen, the test spaces where the measurements are per-
formed must be identiﬁed. In the EPA study (EPA, 2003), the potential study areas
were identiﬁed during the initial building visit and a number was assigned to each of
them. The actual study areas were randomly selected afterwards. The selection criteria
of the study areas vary according to the studies. In the European project (Bluyssen
et al., 1995) as well as for Hwang and Kim (2012), the oﬃces have to be representative
of the whole building. In other projects, the choice of the study areas is deﬁned by more
precise parameters, for instance the number of occupants (EPA, 2003), the orientation
and the activity performed in the area (Silva et al., 2016), or the fact that only one air
handling unit provides the air into the test space (EPA, 2003).
Monitoring location selection
The monitoring locations, where the measurement instruments are precisely located in
the test space, must be deﬁned. Sekhar et al. (2003) highlight the importance of the
oﬃce’s layout and activity as well as the furniture and partitions. One speciﬁc study
shows that the distance between the sensor and the workplace is important so that the
measurements are reliable (Kooi et al., 2018). Indeed, heat sources such as machines
(especially computers), occupants or even solar radiation have to be avoided in order
not to aﬀect the measured values. Other projects give precise values for the distance
between the monitoring locations and the building components (EPA, 2003; Da Silva

et al., 2015). For instance, the sensors should be put at least 1 or 2 meters away from
corners, walls, windows and doors. Moreover, sensors must not be placed under direct
sunlight and not in front of heaters, fans or air supply diﬀusers, and distances between
the sensors and pollutant sources such as printers or photocopiers should be respected.
Importance of buildings’ checklists
When assessing the indoor environment, research has shown that it is important to
know the background state of the building when the measurements are being done as
well as the systems which are used for ventilation, heating and cooling. In the study
assessing the air quality of air-conditioned oﬃce buildings in Singapore (Sekhar et al.,
2003), a particular focus is made on the systems, how they have been designed, how
they are operated and how the maintenance is realized. In the survey by Frontczak and
Wargocki (2011), a speciﬁc questionnaire is given to the facility manager so that he/she
can ﬁll in the information regarding the building characteristics such as age, number of
ﬂoors, occupants, and building’s systems. For the two large-scale projects (EPA, 2003;
Bluyssen et al., 1995), a checklist is developed. In the American study (EPA, 2003), this
checklist is divided into several sections. The ﬁrst one focuses on general information
about the building, such as the age of the building, the number of ﬂoors, the activities
in the building, the external pollutant sources, the systems used, the building envelope.
This part is ﬁlled in during the initial building visit. The second section is about the test
space itself, and is ﬁlled in when the measurements are done. The information needed
is related to the geometry of the room, the occupancy pattern, the cleaning schedule.
The third part of the checklist deals with all the systems, to know how they operate or
how they are scheduled. Finally, a short part concerns a noise and odours subjective
assessment before starting the measurements.
Importance of occupants’ questionnaire
The papers also show the importance of a questionnaire which would be ﬁlled in by
the occupants of the assessed oﬃces. When dealing with indoor environment, the oc-
cupants’ perception is necessary. This questionnaire may be available online (Hwang
and Kim, 2012; Da Silva et al., 2015) and generally includes diﬀerent kinds of questions:
multiple-choice, scales, or free comments (Hwang and Kim, 2012). The questionnaire
usually starts with personal information such as gender, age, type of work performed, or
health status and workplace’s characteristics (Hwang and Kim, 2012; Silva et al., 2016;
Frontczak and Wargocki, 2011; Da Silva et al., 2015; Bluyssen et al., 1995). Then, ques-
tions are generally related to visual and noise comfort, environmental conditions, such as
11
==PAGE:31==

thermal sensations and air quality, cleanliness or unpleasant odours (Cheong and Chong,
1999; Silva et al., 2016). Then, studies can be organized in diﬀerent ways. For instance,
the questions can be more or less linked to the measurements performed: they would
be divided into sections, each section corresponding to one of the parameters measured
during the experiments (Hwang and Kim, 2012). Questions can also be asked for several
periods of time. Indeed, it is interesting to know how the occupants felt during the past
month, but also at the point in time when the measurements are being done (Sekhar
et al., 2003; Bluyssen et al., 1995), in order to see both the inﬂuence of the parameters as
they are at a given moment and on a longer period of time, in case they can cause long-
term health eﬀects for instance. Another way of doing the questionnaire is to change
the conditions under which the occupants have to ﬁll in the form. For instance, one
of the studies focuses on the thermal sensation of the occupants and the feeling of air
movement when conditions both inside and outside change, and also when the windows
and doors are both opened or closed (Croome et al., 1992). This enables to compare
under which conditions the workers feel better but in this case, researchers are not only
dealing with IEQ characterization but also looking for solutions to improve it. Finally,
some questionnaires also include a part about performance of the workers according to
the indoor environment. The performance can be assessed by the occupants themselves,
through several questions (Frontczak and Wargocki, 2011) or it can be assessed via a
series of short tests (Da Silva et al., 2015).
Parameters to be assessed
The parameters usually used to assess the indoor environment can be classiﬁed into six
main categories: thermal environment, indoor air quality, biological matters, ventilation
parameters, acoustics and lighting.
Thermal environment gathers indoor temperature, relative humidity, air velocity (Cheong
and Chong, 1999) and for more complete studies radiant temperature as well (Sekhar
et al., 2003). These parameters are usually assessed with continuous measurements. In
the incubation centre study (Silva et al., 2016), measurements are performed at ﬁve
diﬀerent heights (0.1, 0.6, 1.1, 1.7, 2.3m), these methodology details are not arbitrary
choices, they comply with requirements from standards such as DS/EN ISO 7726.
Indoor air quality (IAQ) assessment consists of measurements of CO2, CO, PM10,
PM2.5, formaldehyde, VOCs and TVOC (Hwang and Kim, 2012; EPA, 2003; Bluyssen
et al., 1995).
These measurements are usually average values over a limited period
(Da Silva et al., 2015) and can be realized several times during the measurements’ pe-
12
==PAGE:32==

riod. For the particulates, especially PM10, the most common methods are either to use
a particle counter, or for high precision data, an environmental laser aerosol spectrometer
(Sekhar et al., 2003). Other pollutants such as CO2, CO or TVOC are also continuously
measured based on diﬀerent methods such as photo-acoustic infra-red detection.
Biological matters include fungi and bacteria. Both are assessed using petri-dishes, tryp-
ticase soy agar (TSA) for bacteria and potato dextrose agar (PDA) (Cheong and Chong,
1999; Sekhar et al., 2003) or malt extract agar (MEA) (Da Silva et al., 2015) for fungi.
These samples are then incubated at diﬀerent temperatures.
The ventilation parameters are often assessed using the air exchange rate. One of the
most common methods to measure it is to use the tracer-gas decay method, usually using
sulphur hexaﬂuoride (SF6) (Cheong and Chong, 1999). This method consists in inject-
ing the gas in the oﬃce and measure the concentration variations on a certain period of
time. Fans are used in order to ensure a homogeneous distribution of the gas in the test
space (Croome et al., 1992). CO2 from the occupants could also be used as the tracer
gas, from the moment when the occupants leave their workplace (Silva et al., 2016).
Other parameters are sometimes measured to assess the ventilation characteristics, for
instance the local air change eﬀectiveness (Cheong and Chong, 1999), the quantity of
fresh air or the age of air (Sekhar et al., 2003).
For acoustic measurements, the A-weighted sound pressure level is measured, and for
lighting, illuminance is assessed (Hwang and Kim, 2012). In the American study (EPA,
2003), both parameters are assessed at the height of a seated person’s head.
When performing indoor environment measurements, it can be necessary to get insights
about outdoor weather conditions as climate variations can help explain indoor param-
eters discrepancies. Wind direction, wind speed, outdoor air temperature and outdoor
relative humidity are usually measured throughout the measurement period (Croome
et al., 1992). These measurements should be done protected from direct sunlight or rain
(Da Silva et al., 2015) or next to the outdoor air intake of the air handling unit which
is used in the space where the measurements are done (EPA, 2003).
Moreover, some studies point out the importance of external factors that may have an
inﬂuence on the IEQ assessment. For instance, if there is a construction site, that could
increase the concentration of PM10 (Hwang and Kim, 2012). Paints, new furniture or
new carpets in the oﬃces can aﬀect the VOC and formaldehyde concentrations (Cheong
and Chong, 1999). External factors can also have an impact on the occupants’ comfort,
it is especially the case of the view through the window, or the contact with nature

(Frontczak and Wargocki, 2011). Therefore, it is very important that all these parame-
ters inﬂuencing indoor environment measurements and comfort are noticed while ﬁlling
in the buildings’ and test spaces’ checklists.
Time schedule for measurements
Finally, the need for an accurate measurement schedule was also highlighted in some of
the studies. It is essential to pay attention to the occupancy of the building, especially for
spot measurements: they should not be realized when workers just entered the building,
as indoor air conditions are not stable, so it is better to wait a little after the building’s
completion (Hwang and Kim, 2012). As a result, planning all the measurements needs
an accurate organization, that is why schedules are often done (Da Silva et al., 2015),
and especially for large-scale studies (EPA, 2003) for which a schedule is done on an
hourly basis, for each oﬃce being assessed.

Chapter 3
Preliminary stage: checklists and
questionnaire development
The ﬁrst and preliminary step of this project consisted in developing two comprehensive
tools: the building and test spaces’ checklists as well as the occupants’ questionnaire.
A speciﬁc chapter is dedicated to these two documents since, based on the background
project’s and Master thesis’s requirements, it has been chosen to entirely build both
these tools. These checklists and questionnaire are meant to be tested in all the three
pilot studies (see other projects of Jonathan Kalf Hansen and Rebecca Tinggaard Nielsen
& Marc Surrow in Greenland), they have thus been built in a cooperative way.
The main objective of this section is to explain accurately how the checklists and ques-
tionnaire were built up.
Therefore, the goal is not only to present an eﬃcient and
ready-to-use set of checklists and occupants’ survey but also to allow the user to under-
stand its structure and thus make it easier to adapt or complete, if needed, based on the
speciﬁc objectives and requirements that could be added in a diﬀerent project.
3.1
Checklists
3.1.1
Purposes and organization
When characterizing the indoor environmental quality of a building, it is necessary to
start with a background assessment. Only characterizing the indoor environment using
indoor climate metrics would make it impossible to analyse which inputs are leading to
the current observations. Therefore, the characterization would only be valuable for the
speciﬁc building assessed in the conditions at the time of measurement. Thus, in order

==PAGE:36==

to end up with more qualitative knowledge about the buildings assessed in this study
and further on in the background project, building checklists are used to characterize
the environment background through diﬀerent items such as building materials, HVAC
systems, cleaning schedules or occupancy patterns. When creating these checklists, we
have been particularly careful to make it as exhaustive as possible in order to be able
to investigate the potential impact of many parameters, but at the same time these
checklists have been designed to be ﬁlled in in a reasonable amount of time and with a
limited need for information from external people.
Figure 3.1.1: Organization of the checklist
The building level is used to characterize the building as a whole, looking at general
parameters not peculiar to the oﬃces. The test space level, on the other hand, focuses
on every information related to the test space itself. In fact, it is also essential to char-
acterize the diﬀerences between several oﬃces located in the same global environment.
Regarding the diﬀerentiation between descriptive elements and source information, the
former focuses on describing the built environment and its occupancy and activity pat-
terns whereas the latter deals with the sources of pollution having a potential impact on
the indoor environment.

Eventually, the subjective assessment checklist is used in order to describe general ele-
ments potentially aﬀecting the occupants’ perception of their indoor environment. This
part is similar to the occupants’ assessment performed with the questionnaire but it
aims at identifying other elements, based on a skilled external point of view, which oc-
cupants could omit in their assessment especially because they are used to their working
environment.
3.1.2
Building level
3.1.2.1
Descriptive elements
The descriptive elements at the building level can be found in the form A-1 BUILDING
DESCRIPTION (see Appendix F).
General building characteristics
General information about the buildings such as the dates of construction and major
refurbishments is important especially to analyze the impact of building regulations and
guidelines throughout the last decades.
Space usage
The activities carried out in the assessed building are used to analyze the potential cor-
relation between a speciﬁc activity and its impact on the surrounding areas. Therefore,
the activities carried out in the buildings should be included, at least on a ﬂoor basis,
to assess the potential impact on the direct surroundings.
Occupancy information
The number of occupants in the building and its occupancy pattern are raising more and
more interest since the recent trends have shown a clear will for decreasing the ﬂoor area
per occupant in order to save costs. The main solution used is usually to organize the
oﬃces with open-plan spaces. However, as shown in various recent research projects, this
trend is very likely to have a negative impact on occupants’ working conditions raising is-
sues such as personal vs. collective indoor environment perception or noise disturbances.

==PAGE:38==

Site information
The building’s surroundings constitute an important source of information regarding is-
sues such as contamination, noise or access to amenities that potentially have an impact
on the occupants’ perception of their working environment.
Building equipment information
Information about ventilation, heating and cooling systems inﬂuence the building’s in-
door environment. IEQ outputs are impacted not only by the type of systems used in
the building but also the way they are controlled, operated and maintained.
3.1.2.2
Source information elements
The source information elements at the building level can be found in the form A-2
BUILDING SOURCE INFORMATION (see Appendix F).
Fire and water damages
Fire and water damages have well-known direct impact when occurring but also sec-
ondary eﬀects that have to be taken into account. Regarding ﬁre damages, diﬀerent
contaminants not only from burnt components but also from cleaning process have been
shown to be hazardous for human health. This means that even if the ﬁre damage was
restricted to a speciﬁc area of the building, other surrounding spaces could then be af-
fected. Concerning water damages, the main post-damage issue regards water stuck in
non-visible parts of the building thus creating mold issues afterwards.
Cleaning schedules and products
Cleaning is an important factor for occupants’ health and comfort since it is necessary
to remove particles becoming hazardous over time. However, if it is done with harmful
products or within wrong conditions, it can also be counterproductive. Thus, the clean-
ing process as well as the means used to perform it must be investigated.
Special use area
In relation with the activities identiﬁed in the descriptive elements, the special use spaces
concern areas inside the building used for speciﬁc activities that are likely to have an
impact on the IEQ of the surrounding spaces (usually cooking, printing, parking or
special storage facilities).

3.1.3
Test space level
3.1.3.1
Descriptive and source information elements
The descriptive and source information elements at the test space level can be found,
respectively, in the forms B-1 TEST SPACE DESCRIPTION and B-2 TEST SPACE
SOURCE INFORMATION (see Appendix F).
A diﬀerence should be made between the characteristics of the macroscopic environment,
here the whole building, and the microscopic one, the speciﬁc oﬃce assessed. Because
the scale is smaller, more accurate elements are checked in form B-1 than in form A-1.
In fact, elements such as the orientation of the room, the view from the window, the
speciﬁc building materials as well as the speciﬁc HVAC systems’ elements used in a given
oﬃce can be assessed individually.
Analyzing these A and B forms together allows us to investigate whether two given
microscopic environments have diﬀerent IEQ even being located in the same macro
environment. The outputs of this observation can then aﬀect the results analysis in a

Occupant-system interaction
The same space can be used in very diﬀerent ways depending on the occupants’ working

Indoor climate assessment
Noise and odours in a working environment are usually inappropriate and can be un-

in some environments they are so common that the occupants are not conscious about
them anymore. These parameters have to be identiﬁed in order to help understanding
the actual correlations between indoor environmental quality and occupants’ complaints.

This chapter is meant to serve two main objectives. Since this Master thesis project is
used as a pilot study for the background project, it is therefore focusing at the same time
on describing a comprehensive protocol prepared along this thesis project as well as the

4.1
Scale adaptation
The aim of this Master thesis is to set up an experimental procedure and run a pilot
study during a ﬁve-month period. Therefore, this creates several constraints. It would
not be possible to investigate buildings at diﬀerent locations around the country with-
out important logistic means (people, transportation, measurement tools) and a strong
contact database to cover eﬃciently a large area. Moreover, running the measurements
in only a very limited amount of oﬃce buildings could increase the risk of only testing
the procedure in buildings where the conditions are not representative of the rest of the
buildings. For instance a building where the owner and the facilities manager are able
to provide very accurate information about the building and occupants are told to par-
ticipate in the study in a suitable way could not be representative of the majority of the
buildings in the country. Then, it is very likely that it introduces misleading conclusions

such as unsuitable checklists, too short schedules for experimental preparation or irrel-
evant/insuﬃcient number of contact persons. Therefore, a scale adaptation process has

Characterization of the parameter
Several standards are used when dealing with thermal environment. DS/EN ISO 7730 is
used to characterize thermal conditions as a function of air temperature, radiant temper-
atures, air velocity and relative humidity. DS/EN ISO 7726 focuses on the requirements
for instrumentation and measurement methodology. DS/EN 15251 can be used to ﬁnd
recommended design values related to thermal environment.

In this pilot study, thermal conditions are characterized by air temperature (considered
as a satisfying assumption as long as no signiﬁcant radiant asymmetries nor air velocity
issues are observed in the room) and relative humidity.

data logger U12-012. This logger is able to measure the air temperature and relative

4.6.2.2
Indoor Air Quality (IAQ)
Characterization of the parameter
In recent oﬃces, occupants are usually identiﬁed as the main pollution source, mainly
due to CO2 production. However, as described in DS/EN 15251, if speciﬁc complaints
such as smell issues and sick building syndrome symptoms are observed, it makes sense
to investigate the IAQ more in depth considering more metrics. The most usual metrics
used in this case are Volatile Organic Compound, especially formaldehyde, indoor par-
ticulate matter, especially PM10 and PM2.5 and carbon monoxide. In this pilot study,
it has been decided to focus on CO2 concentration when characterizing the IAQ in the
oﬃces assessed.
Instrumentation characteristics
The instrument used to assess the CO2 concentration in this study is the Vaisala data
logger GMW22 which has been hooked on the HOBO logger presented before, as shown
on Figure 4.6.1. This logger can be characterized as infrared absorption sensor, it is
based on the speciﬁc IR absorption characteristics for CO2 thus being highly selective to
this speciﬁc parameter. Unlike the HOBO logger, the Vaisala logger needs to be plugged
in all the time in order to record CO2 concentration.
According to the manufacturer, the Vaisala logger does not need to be recalibrated more
than every ﬁve years. However, it has been decided to calibrate the loggers that were not
directly retrieved from a researcher using it for another experiment right before. The
calibration process consists, basically, in using two perfectly known calibration gases.
The ﬁrst step consists in ﬁlling in the measurement chamber (between the light source
and the receptor) with 100% nitrogen in order to calibrate the 0ppm value. Then, a
second gas with a ﬁxed CO2 concentration of 5000ppm is used to ﬁll in the measure-
ment chamber and calibrate the 5000ppm value. The calibration software is then able
to ﬁnish the calibration process based on these two minimum and maximum values.
As well as the Hobo logger, the sampling rate for the Vaisala logger is set to one mea-
surement every ﬁve minutes and one logger is installed in every assessed oﬃce.

Lighting conditions
Characterization of the parameter
Appropriate lighting conditions are achieved by daylighting and artiﬁcial lighting. Light-
ing conditions are usually deﬁned using diﬀerent parameters, the main one being the
mean illuminance on the work plan. Other values can also be taken into account when
doing a full assessment of the lighting conditions such as the illuminance uniformity, the
minimum colour rendering index (Ra) and the uniﬁed glare rating limit (UGR). The
standard giving all the required insights about these parameters is DS/EN 12464.
In this study, the lighting conditions are assessed by continuously measuring the illu-
minance at the workstations. Since lighting conditions are particularly dependent on
the location, representative spots have been deﬁned. However, based on instruments’
availability, no more than two lux meters are used per oﬃce.

4.6.2.4
Acoustic conditions
Characterization of the parameter
Acoustic conditions can be characterized in two major ways, the ﬁrst one is to measure
continuously the A-weighted sound pressure level (SPL in dB(A)), the second one is to
characterize the space based on its reverberation time. This second method requires
proper material such as well calibrated and controlled sound source and sensor as well
as a stable background. It characterizes the time needed for the sound to decrease by
60dB. Measurement guidelines for reverberation time are described in standard DS/EN
ISO 3382. In this study, the acoustic conditions are assessed measuring SPL.
Instrumentation characteristics
Sound pressure level is assessed using Elma DT173 loggers. The loggers are working on
battery with a service life of less than a week. Thus, the methodology for sound logger
measurements must be adapted from the systematic process by installing the instru-
ments only on Monday during the measurement week.
The calibration process is only doable for non-accurate adjustments of ±0.1 dB.
The sampling rate for the Elma logger is set to one measurement every minute. As well
as the Extech loggers, Elma loggers are not installed in all the oﬃces assessed, due to
a limited number of instruments. As many Elma logger as possible are installed on a
tripod (see Figure 4.6.3) so that the sensor is not in contact with any surface. However,
because of availability issues not all the instruments could be installed on these tripods.

source in question would have ﬁt in one of the existing categories. For instance, ”mate-
rial odours” would have ﬁt in the ”furniture” source category. Therefore, these answers
were moved in the right categories, being careful that the respondent did not check the

Data from measurement campaigns
The data from the measurement campaigns require a more speciﬁc preparation work so
that the data from every instrument are based on the same data format for instance.
More importantly, an analysis of potential inconsistent and extreme values must be done
for every parameter since these particular values are not representative when character-
izing the indoor environment. These conditions should happen rarely and only last for
a very limited amount of time.
A ﬁrst common situation is when occupants come very close to the instruments to in-
spect them for instance and can generate abnormally high CO2 concentration values for
a very limited time or when they cover the instruments with clothes therefore perturbing
all the measurements.
Another unsuitable, but very diﬃcult to avoid, condition is direct sunlight on the mea-
surement tools. This is one of the basic requirements, however it is not always possible
for continuous measurements to place the instruments at a perfect location without any
time during the day under direct sunlight. Therefore, an analysis of inconsistent extreme
values due to these conditions must be done, especially for illuminance and temperature.
However, it can be diﬃcult to determine the exact thresholds for these values. Thus,
arbitrary decisions have been made and typically illuminance and CO2 concentration
values suddenly skyrocketing to values, respectively, above 4000lux and 1500ppm have
been deleted. However, it has been assessed that many of the extreme values due to
direct sunlight exposure actually happened out of occupied hours, thus out of the con-
sidered time requirements.
Then, only the data recorded during occupied hours have been taken into account for
the analysis. However, it is diﬃcult to deﬁne an exact schedule that is consistent for
every employee in the assessed buildings. Therefore, it has been decided, mainly based
on information from heads of departments, to set occupied hours from 8:00 to 17:00,
Monday to Friday (excluding Friday due to instrument pickup at this time).

Speciﬁc assessment for the measurement week
When designing the questionnaire it has been decided to ask the respondents about their
indoor environment based on the four weeks before the measurement week. However, we
also judged that it would be interesting to investigate the occupants’ assessment for the
speciﬁc measurement week in order to investigate if there was any major discrepancy in
the occupants’ IEQ satisfaction pattern.
Therefore, the point here has been to compare, for every building and every issue re-
ported before, the number of times the issue has been experienced during the measure-
ment week with every frequency category for the past four weeks. Thus, to illustrate the
reasoning more accurately, if among the respondents reporting that they never experi-
enced a given issue in the past four weeks, the majority of them experienced it at least
two times this week, this would show an incoherence. However, this basic investigation
can be discussed on several points such as the fact that the questionnaire is not ﬁlled in
on the same day of the measurement week by everyone and also that it seems that many
respondents did not consider this question and usually submitted with the standard an-
swer stating that they did not experience it at all during the measurement week.
The four most representative issues from each of the previous indoor climate metrics are
represented in Figure 5.2.18, all the other graphs can be found in Appendix D.
In Figure 5.2.18 below, each graph represents a speciﬁc issue where the four frequency
categories for the past 4 weeks are represented for each building and the data concerning
the measurement week are displayed in color in the stacked bar-chart. A blue curve is
added, based on a secondary vertical axis, representing the number of answers in each
of the frequency categories.

6.1
Background for tool introduction
This chapter aims at presenting arguments to support the development of a commercial
benchmarking tool based on a large database of Danish and Greenlandic oﬃce facilities.
These arguments are organized so that they explain the potential value of acquiring
data about IEQ especially in relation with occupants’ well-being and performance, show
the building stakeholders’ current interest in IEQ issues in relation to productivity and
eventually introduce a new benchmarking tool addressing building stakeholders’ needs.
Research about IEQ and occupants’ well-being and productivity
A recent report, from the World Green Building Council (Alker et al., 2014), claims that
90% of the typical operating costs of a building are actually staﬀrelated. Therefore, for
several companies especially looking at costs’ optimization, occupants’ well-being and
performance ﬁnally appear as major levers. At the same time, indoor environmental
quality also becomes a central parameter as it is one of the main factors inﬂuencing
occupants’ well-being and performance.
Several research papers have investigated the potential correlation between a higher in-
door environmental quality and better occupants’ performance. Wargocki et al. (2000)
as well as Allen et al. (2016) investigated the correlation between IAQ issues, focusing
on outdoor air supply rate and VOC and CO2 concentrations, and increased workers’
productivity and performance. Also, Sepp¨anen et al. (2006) investigated the correlation
between thermal comfort and productivity for oﬃce related tasks. All of these research
projects ended up with the conclusion that productivity could be increased by focusing
92
==PAGE:112==
Chapter 6. Commercial benchmarking tool
on improving the oﬃces’ indoor environmental quality. Moreover, the economic beneﬁts
of undertaking IEQ improvement actions have also been investigated. For instance, in
relation with the project from Allen et al. (2016), MacNaughton et al. (2015) showed
that the return on investment from IEQ improvement would be much greater than the
resulting energy costs.
Putting into perspective these diﬀerent ﬁndings, IEQ data present a great potential when
it comes to oﬃce workers’ productivity as well as an interesting return on investment.
Therefore, this supports the idea that a commercial tool based on oﬃce buildings’ IEQ
assessment is promising. The next issue is, thus, to investigate whether building owners
would be interested in such a commercial product.
Building owners’ interest for improved indoor environment
The Smart Market report from 2016 (Jones et al., 2016) investigated building owners’
interest in healthier buildings focusing on the goals they would like to achieve when
improving, for instance, IEQ in their facilities or the potential added-value of such im-
provements when compared to conventional buildings. Their ﬁndings show that 75%
of the respondents stated that they want to improve their tenants or employees’ satis-
faction with the facility and be able to get occupants happier and healthier in general.
This shows that when assessing IEQ, not only quantitative data should be assessed to
reach a theoretically perfect indoor environment but also occupants’ experience must be
accurately assessed. It is also interesting to note that 47% of the interviewed owners
stated that they mainly use metrics to measure their facilities’ impact on occupants
because they want to attract or retain tenants and employees. Therefore it is important
to consider the question of benchmarking the quality of a building compared to the sur-
rounding buildings on the market that tenants could choose to lease instead.
Then, when it comes to the added-value of a better indoor environment, owners were
asked about their feelings regarding the ﬁnancial impact, meaning the ability to rent
the building more easily, charge a higher rent or increase the value of the building, of
such an improvement of their facility. The main conclusion from the study is that for
all these ﬁnancial parameters, almost half of the respondents are not sure about the
actual impact of such actions, which shows that more research and practical tools could
be developed but also more communication about successful case studies is needed in
order to help make things clearer. However, when considering the owners who have an
opinion about these questions, 73% think they would be able to lease their facility more

quickly, 42% expect to charge a premium rent and 62% believe that the value of their
building would increase (by 2.5% on average).
Many building owners are deﬁnitely interested in these issues. Thus, the speciﬁc bench-
marking concept seems to be a clear added-value when it comes to attract new tenants
or recruit new employees. However, it is crucial that the potential of such a product is
clearly communicated with successful case studies.
Another way of assessing buildings’ IEQ: the benchmarking tool
These arguments show that there is a good potential for a commercial tool allowing build-
ing owners to rate their buildings in order to create leverage on renting more quickly,
charging premium rent and adding value to their building. It has also been shown that
oﬀering a possibility to characterize a building’s IEQ as more attractive than in other
buildings would be a strong commercial argument. It is thus interesting to consider one
speciﬁc tool developed in Australia, the NABERS Indoor Environment rating tool for
building owners, managers and tenants (NABERS, 2015). This tool aims at providing
facility managers, building owners and tenants with information about their facilities’
indoor environment by assessing and benchmarking it against market performance stan-
dard. Therefore, this tool is in perfect alignment with the data collected in this Master
thesis as well as the background project.

6.3
Practical application
This section focuses on the detailed calculation methodology used to benchmark the
assessed buildings. Every step of the calculation is illustrated with examples using the
data from the pilot study and more speciﬁcally focusing on building 202.
From a general point a view, as shown in Figure 6.2.3, each IEQ metric is assessed
with objective and/or subjective data, these two types of data are processed using two
diﬀerent methods:
• Objective data: Depending on the speciﬁc IEQ metric we are working with, a
speciﬁc range of value or upper/lower limit (from a relevant standard) is used to
benchmark the buildings against each other. The ranges and limits used here are
close to the ones presented in the benchmarking paragraphs of section 5.3.
• Subjective data: Every subjective data comes from respondents’ answers to the
questionnaire’s indoor climate and ergonomics sections. Therefore, regarding the
indoor climate part, every answer relates to the frequency with which respondents
experience the diﬀerent issues during the past four weeks.
The answers are weighted based on the number of days they reported experiencing
the issue. ”Never” is weighted with 0; ”1 to 3 times” is weighted with 0.1 since
it represents an average of 2 days experiencing the issue in four weeks or 0.1 per
day; ”1 to 3 times per week” is weighted with 0.4 following the same rule; ”every
day” is weighted with 1. Then, considering that the number of answers in each
of the previous categories have been divided by the total number of answers in
this building they can be, now, weighted with the factors described just before.
Eventually, an average value for the potential multiple issues related to the metric
(for instance, too humid and too dry for air humidity issue) is calculated and, as
for the objective data, the buildings are compared with one another.
With reference to the ergonomics’ data, the questionnaire asked for a satisfaction
evaluation concerning the chair and desk setup. An average of each evaluation is
calculated and then an average of these two grades is calculated to benchmark the
buildings (the lower is the answer, the better is the ergonomics’ evaluation).

must be the most distinguishing since the choice for new oﬃce building to work in will
be done in this category. Therefore, a basic ”good” level is used, an ”excellent” level
shows a rather better performance and ﬁnally the ”leading market” position must be
a restrictive category with a limited number of oﬃce buildings. Regarding the below-
average levels they can be restricted to a large basic ”below-average” level and a more
restricted ”poor” level with which only a limited number of buildings are rated.
The six basic rating categories are described by the following ranking ranges:
• 1 star (poor IEQ): 0% to 14.9%
• 2 stars (below-average IEQ): 15% to 44.9%
• 3 stars (median IEQ): 45% to 54.9%
• 4 stars (good IEQ): 55% to 74.9%
• 5 stars (excellent IEQ): 75% to 89.9%
• 6 stars (market leading IEQ): 90% to 100%

Chapter 7
Discussion
This Master thesis aimed at building a comprehensive protocol, including building check-
lists, occupants’ questionnaires and objective measurement campaigns to characterize the
indoor environment in oﬃce buildings. This methodology has been tested and optimized
in ﬁve diﬀerent buildings and the data obtained along this practical phase have been
processed to characterize the buildings’ indoor environment and eventually develop a
benchmarking tool for further commercial purposes.
7.1
Innovative characteristics of the benchmarking tool
Beyond the fact that this tool oﬀers a new way to characterize indoor environmental qual-
ity by focusing on benchmarking a building against the corresponding national building
stock, another important objective was to create innovative solutions that would help
counter usual barriers experienced with conventional green building certiﬁcations such
as LEED, BREEAM, WELL or DGNB. Many of these issues were discussed in the paper
published by Harrison and Seiler (2011). A ﬁrst barrier was the high investment cost of
these accreditations due to the large number of parameters assessed. This tool focuses
on IEQ with an adequate number of metrics assessed and includes subjective as well as
objective data. Therefore, this solution allows for a more eﬃcient certiﬁcation process.
A second barrier, already stated in the tool development part, is the coordination issue
that can happen when a single building is rented by multiple tenants. This could limit
the tenants’ ﬂexibility and put at risk the building’s accreditation in case of litigation.
That is why IEQ T BOB includes diﬀerent accreditations depending on the audience
targeted and place the certiﬁcation responsibility into the right stakeholder’s hands.

==PAGE:134==
Chapter 7. Discussion
7.2
Limitations of the benchmarking tool’s practical use
Representative aspect of the database
Since it is based on a benchmarking concept, every building evaluation is based on the
reference indoor environmental quality level of the database used to benchmark the
buildings. Thus, it is crucial to ensure that this database is representative enough of
the actual indoor environmental quality conditions of the building stock. For instance,
in this pilot study, the very limited number of buildings assessed makes it unrealistic
to rank these ﬁve buildings on such a large scale, but suﬃcient to develop and test the
suggested benchmarking methodology.
To achieve representative conditions, the main goal is to reach a large number of build-
ings. The advantage with the background project is that the commercial benchmarking
tool can be developed and applied after completion of the PhD study. This allows the
tool to be used based on an already fairly comprehensive database that has been built
up as part of minor separate research projects.
Also, this tool is based on subjective data obtained through a questionnaire. The repre-
sentativeness of these data can be diﬃcult to control, especially in case of low response
rate which would not be representative of the building’s occupant population. However,
the strength of this tool is that this issue should be overcome, to some extent, by the
inclusion of objective data from the measurement campaigns.
Certiﬁcation validity
A particular point of a benchmarking-based evaluation is that every time a new building
is assessed, thus added to the existing database, all the other evaluations become inher-
ently outdated. Even if this issue becomes less important when the number of buildings
in the database increases, it is important to take it into account when designing the tool.
The main solution is to adapt the recertiﬁcation process based on this issue. Regular
green building certiﬁcations, such as LEED for instance, need recertiﬁcation every ﬁve
years. In the case of this benchmarking tool it could be lowered down to three years dur-
ing a given period of time when the updating frequency is high and the database size is
not large enough. Then, if a clear decrease in the registration frequency pattern appears
and the database has become suﬃciently comprehensive, the recertiﬁcation period can
evolve to become longer. Every time a building gets recertiﬁed, it must be benchmarked
against all the new buildings that have been registered since its ﬁrst certiﬁcation, and
its position in the market may evolve.

Chapter 7. Discussion
Diﬀerentiation between Denmark and Greenland
The tool has been designed to be adapted to the Danish and Greenlandic building
stocks. However, the weather conditions being sensibly diﬀerent between Denmark and
Greenland, it would not be completely coherent to benchmark buildings from these two
locations all together. Weather conditions in Greenland generate much more constraints
on the indoor environment mainly due to outdoor temperatures, wind speed, snowfall and
pollution rates. This argument would aﬀect the objective IEQ measurements. Moreover,
part of the rating is using subjective assessments, and occupants’ individual comfort
may vary between Denmark and Greenland because of Greenlandic people being used to
harsher conditions and covered with diﬀerent clothing levels especially during winter.
7.3
Discussion of the experimental protocol
Building and test space selection
The number of buildings assessed during this study was satisfactory in order to char-
acterize their indoor environment with a suﬃcient number of parameters. However, it
was too low to overcome the limitations stated before when it comes to benchmark a
building’s IEQ against the others in order to rank their performances. Nevertheless, the
main objectives aiming at characterizing the IEQ of the diﬀerent buildings as well as
developing a methodology for the benchmarking tool were fulﬁlled.
The number of oﬃces assessed in every building varies for diﬀerent reasons:
• The size of the buildings and the number and activity of occupants are diﬀerent
from one building to another.
• The oﬃce layout changes from one building to another.
• Some buildings must be assessed at the same time (for organizational purposes)
because of the multi-tenancy pattern.
Therefore, we have tried to investigate the best way to measure how representative the
oﬃce sample is, based on the following factors:
• Occtestspace & Occbuilding represent, respectively, the number of occupants in all
the test spaces assessed and the total number of occupants in the building.
• Offtestspace & Offbuilding represent, respectively, the number of oﬃces/test spaces
assessed and the total number of oﬃces/test spaces in the building.

Chapter 7. Discussion
Questionnaire
The importance of considering the occupants’ evaluation when assessing the indoor envi-
ronment has been clearly demonstrated. However, using questionnaires is also debatable
for multiple reasons, especially when the purpose is to benchmark the results. First,
the response rate is diﬃcult to control and has a great eﬀect on the representativeness
of the results as well as respondents starting to answer the questionnaire but stopping
before the end. Moreover, low response rates may cause bias due to an overweight of
critical occupants. Eventually, dishonest answers due to respondents worrying about
consequences of their answers being revealed because of anonymity failures can be an
issue. However, questionnaires remain the most eﬃcient and practical tool to use both
in terms of cost and time.
The questionnaire was distributed to every occupant of the assessed buildings. This
allowed us to increase the number of responses, compared to sending the questionnaires
only to occupants in oﬃces where we performed objective measurements, and therefore
get a better representation of the buildings’ overall occupants’ satisfaction. However, it
makes it more diﬃcult to ensure a good support of the respondent population. Indeed,
in the main studies analyzed in the literature review, questionnaires usually focused on
the occupants present in the test spaces where the objective measurements were per-
formed. This solution presents two main advantages. It allows the research team to
closely support the respondents in order to make sure that they understand everything
accurately but also that they ﬁll in the questionnaire in due time and in similar condi-
tions. The second point is that potential correlations between the subjective data from
the questionnaires and objective data from the measurement campaigns can be inves-
tigated. However, it would not have been very consistent to implement this solution
for this study based on the selected goals. Indeed, we wanted to give the employees
suﬃcient ﬂexibility when ﬁlling in the questionnaire as well as get a representation as
large as possible of the occupants’ satisfaction with the IEQ.

7.4
Discussion on data validity and IEQ characterization
Corrupted raw data
As stated in the data analysis, some data, especially from the measurement campaigns,
were corrupted and deleted before characterizing the indoor environment. Part of these
data came from unrepresentative measurement conditions that were diﬃcult to avoid,
such as instruments exposed under direct sunlight. Most of these data were recorded out
of the occupying hours and therefore it did not have a signiﬁcant impact on the results.
However, malfunctioning sound meters were a particularly major issue. Even if all the
Elma loggers were tested before the ﬁrst measurement campaign, diﬀerent instruments
started malfunctioning (sometimes a new instrument each measurement week). If the
SPL reached peak values (around 60dBA), it seemed that the instrument was not able to
measure correctly the following points for a while and was stuck at this very high value
before getting back to a logical state some days after. These very high SPL conditions
were assessed not to be representative of the actual conditions. Thus, the accuracy of
the objective acoustic conditions assessment can be discussed for the characterization
phase of the report as well as in the benchmarking tool.
Comprehensiveness of the IEQ parameters’ characterization
When characterizing the indoor environment, the measured metrics were chosen so that
they are representative of the IEQ parameters stated before. However, it is important
to discuss which metrics could be added to get a more consistent representation of the
assessed parameter. For instance, we know that air velocity and radiant temperatures
may impact the thermal conditions.
Moreover, the comfort ranges for temperature
conditions used when benchmarking the buildings are based on operative temperature
and thus include the previous metrics.
However, the objective data focused on air
temperature and relative humidity to characterize the thermal conditions. Therefore, it
can be discussed whether adding objective measurements of air velocity and/or radiant
temperatures to characterize the thermal conditions may be worth it, considering the
extra instruments and organization needed, when characterizing and benchmarking the
buildings’ indoor environments.
This discussion may also be considered for the air
quality by adding TVOC and particulate matters measurements, the lighting conditions
by considering the colour rendering index and the uniﬁed glare rating limit or the acoustic
conditions by taking into account the reverberation time of the test space assessed.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
8.1
Fulﬁllment of the project’s objectives
Along this project, the four objectives set at the beginning of the Master thesis and
mentioned below have been addressed through the following stages:
Objective 1: Building up a protocol
• Checklists were developed to characterize the buildings and test spaces with phys-
ical information that can be related to the indoor environment assessment.
• An online questionnaire was implemented, aiming at assessing the employees’ per-
ception of their working environment. This questionnaire mainly focused on indoor
climate matters as well as potential health issues and their impact on the employ-
ees’ working ability.
• A speciﬁc methodology for objective measurement campaigns in the test spaces
was built up and a systematic process was elaborated to maximize the process
eﬃciency.
Focusing on the three weeks preceding the measurement week, it is
deﬁned so that all the essential upstream steps are realized successfully and in due
time.
Objective 2: Testing the protocol
• A scale adaptation was suggested for the pilot study in order to use DTU as a
living lab to test the protocol and optimize it.
• Five DTU buildings were selected to perform the measurements. These buildings
as well as the test spaces selected in each of them were meant to be as representative
as possible of DTU building stock.
120
==PAGE:140==
Chapter 8. Conclusion
• Temperature, relative humidity, CO2 concentration, illuminance and sound pres-
sure level were measured during one week in each of the selected buildings.
• Questionnaires were distributed to every occupant in each of the assessed buildings
and checklists were ﬁlled in by the research team.
Objective 3: Characterizing the buildings
• A detailed description of the buildings’ and test spaces’ characteristics was done
using the checklists’ information.
• Data from the questionnaires were used to characterize the buildings’ indoor envi-
ronment through their occupants’ assessment. Employees’ health conditions and
working ability in relation to the buildings’ IEQ were also represented.
• A comprehensive characterization of the objective IEQ conditions in each of the
buildings was performed based on the diﬀerent metrics from the measurement
campaigns.
Objective 4: Developing a benchmarking tool
• Indoor environment aﬀects the occupants’ productivity, which is a key element
when it comes to oﬃce buildings. A benchmarking tool would help building own-
ers and tenants understanding the main conditions in their building as well as
promoting their facilities’ indoor environment compared to the other oﬃce build-
ings on the market.
• The benchmarking tool, IEQ T BOB, was developed based on both objective and
subjective data, from measurement campaigns as well as questionnaires. The data
were divided in ﬁve categories with diﬀerent weights on the ﬁnal grade: thermal
conditions (30%), indoor air quality (30%), lighting conditions (20%), acoustic
conditions (15%), ergonomics (5%).
• A global evaluation, based on a six-star scale, and a detailed evaluation of each IEQ
parameter, were provided for every assessed building in the form of a certiﬁcate.
• This tool was applied to benchmark the ﬁve DTU buildings against each other.
Each building was provided with its respective certiﬁcate stating its global and
detailed IEQ characterization compared to the others.

8.2
Future use of the pilot study
Considering the methodology developed along this pilot study, the next stage is to in-
vestigate how to implement it as part of the background project. Because of the larger
scale of the project, diﬀerent issues should be considered. Also, developing a systematic
process must remain a core condition when implementing the protocol.
Building selection
The person responsible for the project can focus on selecting coherent areas for building
selection and appoint a speciﬁc research team to select building(s) in each of these area
based on a systematic method.
• An investigation of the oﬃce building stocks allocation in Denmark and Green-
land can be performed to identify consistent areas for further building selection.
Danish building stock’s information found on Danmarks Statistik’s website would
be valuable.
• A research team can be allocated to a speciﬁc area and take responsibility for
selecting the building(s) based on pre-deﬁned criteria inspired from those from
this pilot study and adapted to a larger scale study.
Coordination of the building assessment process
Every research team must follow the same systematic methodology when assessing the
diﬀerent buildings in order to get consistent and comparable data.
• The checklists and questionnaires have been designed so that they are comprehen-
sive and adaptable enough to be used at a larger scale.
• The systematic process should be applicable for any oﬃce building in order to get
in touch with the right contact person and correctly perform the measurement
campaigns in due time.
• Measuring instruments’ availability and reliability must be considered carefully.
It has been assessed, for instance, that a correct illuminance assessment requires
many instruments and that the sound meters were not reliable at all.

Methodology for data implementation in the database
It is crucial that the database is completed in a systematic way since it is meant to
include a large number of oﬃce buildings. Also, feedback to the companies owning the
assessed buildings must be considered in advance.
• The digital implementation of the checklists suggested in the discussion would help
automatizing the database completion process.
• Buildings’ indoor environment characterization should follow a systematic pattern
in order to be eﬃcient when providing building owners with feedback about their
facilities.
• A large database is an opportunity for investigations of potential correlations be-
tween building characteristics and occupants’ satisfaction or objective IEQ condi-
tions.
Development and commercialisation of the benchmarking tool
The benchmarking tool developed and applied in this pilot study presents a basic
methodology for oﬃce buildings’ benchmarking and can be optimized for further ap-
plication.
• The comprehensiveness of the metrics considered in the IEQ T BOB tool may vary
as well as their respective weights in the ﬁnal grade.
• The tool has been designed to target diﬀerent audiences (building owner, tenant)
and must be adapted for these diﬀerent cases especially in terms of IEQ metrics
to be considered.
• It would be very eﬃcient to automatize the data processing from the database to
the benchmarking tool in order to obtain an IEQ performance certiﬁcate for each
new building implemented in the database.

Report for building 201
This report presents the results of the indoor environmental quality survey performed in
building 201, during week 15, as part of a research project at the International Center
for Indoor Environment and Energy, DTU Byg.
The survey included objective data from on-site measurements of speciﬁc indoor climate
parameters, as well as subjective data assessed through a questionnaire distributed to the
occupants of the building in order to describe the occupants’ experience of their working
indoor environment. The results from both the objective and subjective metrics are used
to characterize the building’s indoor environmental quality.
Results from questionnaire
The questionnaire aimed at characterizing the occupants’ assessment and feeling about
their working indoor environment using four main sections. The ﬁrst one asked for de-
mographics’ information, such as the age, the sex, or the type of job performed, in order
to deﬁne the respondents’ population. The second one focused on the respondents’ ex-
perience of the indoor climate based on four main parameters: thermal conditions, air
quality, lighting conditions and acoustic conditions. The third section assessed potential
health issues that the occupants could experience in their oﬃce as well as the impact of
such issues on their working abilities. Eventually, the last section asked the occupants
about comfort at their workstation when it comes to ergonomics.

Indoor climate
Thermal conditions
Figure H.0.1 shows the frequency of thermal comfort issues over the past four weeks.
The main issue regarding air movement is that there is too little for 25% of the respon-
dents. One third of them experience this issue at least once a week.
Temperature issues are also important in the building as about 30% of the respondents
ﬁnd the temperature too high, when about 40% think it is too low. Looking at the
comments stated by the respondents, several reasons may explain this situation:
• The indoor temperature is aﬀected by the outdoor temperature, as well as by the
other weather conditions. On the one hand, as soon as there is direct sunlight
in the room, the indoor temperature increases a lot.
On the other hand, cold
weather will decrease the temperature inside. Oﬃces are very sensible to outdoor
temperature variations.
• In shared oﬃces, the occupants’ comfort standard varies individually and the op-
timal temperature is not the same for all the occupants. Thus, some employees
report that they cannot ﬁnd a temperature which is suitable for everybody, that
is why some of them feel too cold when others feel too warm.
• Issues with the thermostats have also been reported. Either the thermostats are
not working at all, or they are not working properly. Temperature must then be
adjusted by opening the window for instance.
• The ventilation ceiling grilles are sometimes cold, and people sitting under or in
the vicinity of them feel cold.
Regarding humidity issues, the occupants report that they are either ﬁne or experiencing
too dry conditions for 24% of them.

Air quality
The air quality was characterized by a single question about unpleasant odours. If the
respondents experienced the issue at least once a week, then a few follow-up questions
would appear in order to determine the gravity of the issue, as well as potential causes.
Figure H.0.2 shows that unpleasant odours are an important issue in the oﬃces, as 54%
of the respondents experience it, and half of them do at least once a week.
Figure H.0.2: Frequency of air quality issues in the past 4 weeks
Among the respondents experiencing smelliness issues at least once a week, it is assessed
as being quite a major problem, as seen on Figure H.0.3b.
Figure H.0.3a and the
respondents’ comments state that the main causes are outside sources as well as furniture
and materials smells. Another main source, represented here in the ”other” category,
is paint odours, which have been noticed by a signiﬁcant number of occupants in the
building. Moreover, many occupants report a generally bad air quality, a bit stuﬀy in
the morning, with a need to open the window when entering the oﬃce.

Acoustic conditions
Noise issues are reported very often in the building, as seen on Figure H.0.6, with almost
60% of the respondents mentioning an issue, and half of them at least once a week.
Figure H.0.6: Frequency of noise issues in the past 4 weeks
The main reason why respondents are disturbed is because of the neighbours’ conver-
sations, as illustrated on Figure H.0.7. Looking at the comments, occupants in shared
oﬃces relate problems when the other people in the oﬃce are talking to someone, for
instance if someone comes to ask them a question, and keep talking in the oﬃce. Other
issues linked to the shared oﬃces, such as phone conversations or phones ringing, are
also noticed. Another important cause is the HVAC systems which make the occupants
have a background noise all the time. Also, traﬃc from the main road located on the
southern side of the building seems to be an issue.

Overall assessment
Once the respondents have gotten a clearer understanding of the diﬀerent factors char-
acterizing their working indoor environment, they are asked for an overall assessment.
This assessment is done on a six-point scale and general comments about the working
indoor environment can be stated.
Figure H.0.8 shows that the overall assessment of the IEQ in building 201 is in the good
half of the scale for 78% of the respondents and even assessed with the two best grades by
half of them. However, some people are still disappointed with the IEQ in the building.
The main comments stated are the following ones:
• Being able to open the windows to regulate the thermal conditions inside the oﬃces
is crucial for many occupants.
• Noise issues, especially due to the ventilation system, can be very disturbing and
a sensible diﬀerence is felt when it gets down in the evening.
Figure H.0.8: Overall assessment of the building
Health
Figures H.0.9a and H.0.9b characterize health symptoms among the occupants of the
building: on the left, the graph shows the proportion of respondents reporting each
symptom, as well as the frequency; on the right, the graph represents whether each
particular symptom gets better or stays the same when occupants leave the building.
This second information is essential, as it helps understanding whether the symptom is
likely to be due to the building’s indoor environment.

The fatigue symptoms (headache, tiredness, concentration issues) are the most reported
ones, with between 40% and 60% of the respondents experiencing at least one of them.
At least 30% of the respondents experience irritation or dryness problems. Respiratory
issues are also reported by the occupants, mainly sneezing and stuﬀy or runny nose.
Regarding the evolution of the symptoms when leaving the building, Figure H.0.9b shows
that for all the symptoms, a majority of respondents did not specify any answer for this
question which makes it diﬃcult to analyze. However, for the fatigue symptoms, most
respondents indicated that they got better when leaving the building, therefore they
may be due to the building’s indoor environment. Regarding the irritation issues, it
seems to get better for a majority of respondents, especially for the eyes, again showing
a potential building’s indoor environment responsibility for this issue.

Ergonomics are not directly part of the indoor environment, but they are linked to oc-
cupants’ well-being and comfort. Issues related to these parameters could also aﬀect

health symptoms.
Figure H.0.11 shows the respondents’ satisfaction regarding ergonomics on a scale from
”comfortable” (0) to ”very uncomfortable” (100). The intermediary comfort levels are
”slightly uncomfortable” at 33 and ”uncomfortable” at 66.

a vast majority giving a grade between 0 and 20.

Results from objective measurements
The objective measurements were performed in 10 oﬃces chosen in order to be repre-
sentative of the ﬂoor level, the orientation, and the oﬃce size. The assessed parameters
were air temperature, relative humidity and CO2 concentration in every selected oﬃce,
as well as illuminance and sound pressure level in some of them. The assessed oﬃces are
presented on Figure H.0.12.
Figure H.0.12: Locations of the selected oﬃces (from top to bottom: 2nd ﬂoor, 1st
ﬂoor, ground ﬂoor)
The following results are organized according to the diﬀerent metrics. For each met-
ric, a cumulative chart is plotted, with information presented about comfort ranges and
thresholds for Category II buildings (normal level of expectation for new and renovated
facilities) from European standard DS/EN 15251. The graphs are plotted only consid-
ering the occupied time, which corresponds to the weekdays between 8am and 5pm.
Thermal environment
Thermal environment includes temperature and relative humidity. The ranges from the
standard are the following:
• Temperature: Between 20◦C and 24◦C (heating season)
• Relative humidity: Between 25% and 60%

Indoor air quality
Indoor air quality is characterized by CO2 concentration. The threshold for category II
is 850ppm. As shown on Figure H.0.15, the building fulﬁlls the requirements during the
occupied hours, with a maximum value of approximately 600ppm. This result illustrates
a satisfactory ventilation strategy regarding bioeﬄuents for the oﬃces assessed.
Figure H.0.15: CO2 concentration cumulative chart
Lighting conditions
The standard gives a minimum value for illuminance in oﬃce rooms of 500 lux. As seen
on Figure H.0.16, this requirement is fulﬁlled 48% of the occupied time.
Very low values of illuminance are however diﬃcult to analyze, as the instruments are
located close to the working area (in order to be representative of the employees’ working
conditions) and could thus be covered or shaded for some time. Also, other low values
have been assessed to be due to the occupant not being in his/her oﬃce some day, but
with the blinds down because they had been closed the previous day for instance.

Acoustic conditions
The standard gives a maximum value for sound pressure level of 40 dBA. This require-
ment is fulﬁlled 62% of the time, as shown on Figure H.0.17. The higher values of sound
pressure level could be explained by neighbours’ conversations in shared oﬃces, or in
oﬃces where the occupants work with the door open, as well as by the constant noise
from ventilation systems which has been pointed out multiple times before.
Figure H.0.17: Sound pressure level cumulative chart
Conclusion
Indoor environmental quality of building 201 has been assessed considering both occu-
pants’ well-being assessment and objective measurements. Considering occupants’ data,
the overall indoor climate assessment is good with speciﬁc complaints about tempera-
ture dependence over outdoor conditions, air quality issues from materials’ smells and
acoustic disturbances due to surrounding conversations and noise from HVAC system.
Regarding occupants’ health, many respondents stated fatigue problems that they re-
lated to the building’s indoor environment. This issue should be carefully considered
since many respondents reported that it consistently impacted their ability to work.
Then, concerning objective measurements, temperature tends to be out of the recom-
mended range and this may be related to malfunctioning thermostats. Ventilation seems
to regulate CO2 concentration in a proper way. Eventually, acoustic conditions must be
considered since objective measurements comply with occupants’ complaints about too
noisy working conditions.
