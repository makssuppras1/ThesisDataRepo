Abstract
3D models show great advantages compared to 2D models in biomimetic systems for muscle tissue.
They can offer the mechanical and electrical stimulation crucial for the correct development of mus-
cle tissue physiology. Commonly used models for engineered cardiac and muscle tissue are limited in
design options and connected to the tissue dimensions used. They are often made using complex man-
ufacturing techniques, making design adjustments laborious and time-intensive. A good 3D biomimetic
system for muscle models should incorporate proper mechanics to support muscle cell differentiation
and development while offering easy modifications of the system.
This project aims to manufacture a biomimetic muscle tissue platform using highly customisable 3D
printing with functional materials for conductive purposes. This approach takes advantage of multi-
material 3D printing of soft polymeric materials. It allows for in-air printing of conductive wires, simulta-
neously serving as anchor points for muscle cell bundles. Within this project, a framework is created to
print such devices while allowing for rapid adjustment of the printed construct to ensure its mechanical
properties match the physiological range.
Such a device was successfully 3D printed into a common 6-well plate. It was made by 3D printing
graphite ribbons in free air, which allowed them to be suspended between 3D-printed support strands.
Modifications to the printing code allowed changes to the system’s mechanics. This was mainly gov-
erned by the length of free suspended wires, the anchor points for human cell bundles. Longer ribbons
were softer than shorter ones. Human skeletal muscle cells were also 3D printed onto the platform
using embedded printing into a hydrogel. They were maintained successfully over several days.
No visible contractions of the tissue could be observed, likely due to the high stiffness of the anchor
points. Future work should focus on testing softer ribbons or softer geometries like cantilevers with
human muscle cells.

Introduction
Developing physiologically appropriate in-vitro models of human muscle is crucial for disease modelling,
drug screening and regenerative medicine applications [1]. Muscles are one of the largest human
organs, taking up to 40 % of human body weight. According to its function, it can be classified as cardiac,
skeletal or smooth muscle. Skeletal muscle is mainly responsible for generating forces for locomotion,
breathing, posture and whole-body homeostasis [2], [3]. The structure of skeletal muscle is shown
in Figure 1.1. A muscle consists of many aligned muscle fibres. Each fibre is a collection of bundled
myofibrils. These fibrils are made of repeating contractile units called the sarcomeres. In the sarcomere,
the different filaments, actin and myosin, generate force when they slide over each other, contracting
the sarcomere. Naturally, this process can be initiated by a small electrical nervous stimulus, the action
potential; however, skeletal muscle tissue can also contract spontaneously. Therefore, skeletal muscle
tissue is both mechanically and electrically active. [2]
Figure 1.1: Physiology of skeletal muscle [2].
1
==PAGE:14==

In-vitro models of muscle tissue often lack the electrical and mechanical aspects of native tissue. Still,
they have allowed better insight into muscle and metabolic disease by visualising the differentiation
processes of cells and drug effects. Common models of human muscle tissue include two-dimensional
(2D) cell culture models, small animal models or muscle biopsies from humans. 2D models are easy
to use and are low-cost. However, they have limited ability to translate into the human body, which
can affect the study of disease or the effects of drugs when used on humans. 2D models of skeletal
muscle have shown difficulty in long-term maintenance and lack of function and organisation of their
natural counterparts. Particularly, they lack the three-dimensional (3D) structure of the native muscle
environment and miss the electrical and mechanical input muscle cells receive in native development.
Both these factors have been shown to benefit muscle tissue maturation and force development. [1],
[4]
In 2D in-vitro models, cells are cultured in a monolayer at the bottom of the flask on the plastic or glass.
Due to the stiff substrate, cells tend to develop immature myotubes, the precursors to myofibrils, and
they replicate native tissue inaccurately [4]–[6]. Hence, coupling the muscle cells to a substrate with ap-
propriate stiffness supports their differentiation. Softer substrates used successfully for 3D engineered
muscle tissue (EMT) can be hydrogels. These can be tuned to showcase the stiffness cells experience
in the body, which has been reported to be up to 500 kPa [7]. Furthermore, the 3D environment is
closer to the native tissue structure of cells in the human body. [1], [8]
The next step after 2D cell culture is often animal models and clinical trials with humans, which impose
further ethical concerns. The limitations of current models have driven the field to develop newer
technologies, like 3D models of human muscle tissue [6], [9]. Their advantages include increased
culture time of samples, increased myotube size, improved maturation, and the ability to measure
the contractile function of the muscle tissue [1], [6]. Assessing a muscle’s contractile force allows
investigation into the tissue’s function and quality and allows quantification of the effect of drugs [10].
Measurements of contractile EMTs have not been reproduced with human muscle cells [4]. In current
human tissue models, the force-tracking capabilities and the pacing of human cells through the anchor
points are limited. For example, muscle fibres’ force generation and quality are affected in muscular
dystrophies. Currently, the only models capable of investigating the effects of this disease rely on the
use of animals [2]. This, too, can be prevented by using 3D biomimetic human muscle models.
Human muscle models have been developed but are facing some drawbacks. First examples of 3D
models produced muscle bundles in gel-like support baths [11]. 3D models later evolved to two post
systems (see Figure 2.1) [1]. The posts are typically manufactured a few micrometres to millimetres
apart and made of Polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS). They serve as anchoring points for cell bundles,
which are seeded around the top of the posts. While these designs are closer to replicating the native
3D environment of muscle tissue, some drawbacks have been reported. These include the tissue
slipping off the posts, necking of the tissue and cells ripping off the posts, as the post material is too stiff.
The models create tissue bundles around wires or cantilevers made of elastomers and electrically pace
cells with external sources [12]. This offers an advantage in tissue modelling, as electrical stimulation to
muscle cells in-vitro has shown improved differentiation and maintenance of tissue [4], [13]. Including
this into current models would be beneficial. [12]
Manufacturing of such models can be complicated and involve multiple steps of clean-room micro-
lithography, with reduced customisation and force tracking options. As an alternative manufacturing
procedure, additive manufacturing has emerged. In additive manufacturing, an object is created from
digital models employing 3D printing. One of these methods is Direct ink writing (DIW), where complex
structures can be built on the micro-scale to produce highly complex devices of multiple layers. DIW
is highly customisable, including modifiable feedstock of printing material. A schematic showing the
production flow differences is shown in Figure 1.2.

In conclusion, 3D models can offer a great advantage over 2D models. They can replicate the native
environment of human muscle tissue better, and cells benefit from an environment similar in stiffness
and when stimulated electrically. There is outstanding work in the field to tailor 3D models further to
improve their outcome and mimic the human tissue environment. Previous work in the Tailor Materials
and Tissues (TMAT) group has shown that a polymeric carbon composite can be 3D printed suspended
in-air over multiple millimetres [17]. This allows the creation of a customisable 3D printing protocol.
However, there are some outstanding points which have led to the key aims of this project. This project
employs suspended printing of conductive composite materials with a soft polymeric base to develop
EMT platforms of various designs and, thus, mechanical properties.
1. In this project, a solid framework for the production of suspended micro-beams of multiple
millimetre lengths into a common well plate is established. A wire system for a 3D biomimetic
muscle culture will be manufactured with a 3D printer using DIW. A polymeric graphite composite
is to be used for this and printed suspended. Slight modifications of the 3D printing code or the
printing ink should vary the system’s mechanics.
2. The results of this thesis will show how the stiffness of the construct can be modulated to
demonstrate stiffness in the relevant range of muscle tissue. Varying the device’s stiffness
can be completed independently of the cells used. Different geometries are explored to make

To work towards these objectives, this project aims to streamline further the manufacturing process of
the biomimetic 3D platform. The manufactured device’s characterisation is essential to better under-

crucial as they serve as the anchoring points. Detecting the stiffness of the wires will allow better estima-

of muscle disease pathology, drug screening and discovery.

Background
This chapter provides the background for the processes and reasons for this project. Some similar ap-
plications known in the literature are discussed first. Then 3D printing and ink properties are explained
further. Lastly, the background for some platform characteristics and corresponding testing is provided,
primarily focused on the stiffness of materials.
2.1. Status Quo - Muscle Tissue Platforms
As the limitations of 2D models in replicating native tissue became increasingly recognised, the devel-
opment of 3D models started. The state of the art for producing 3D muscle models and measuring their
contractility can be broadly divided into 2-post or suspended wire systems.
2.1.1. Two-post systems
Nearly 40 years ago, one of the first models that can be considered a 3D model was developed. Here,
Vandenburgh et al. cultured muscle cells in a collagen gel anchored to a stiff substrate, a nylon support
or stainless steel mesh [11]. They saw improvements in differentiation of the tissue and long-term
survival of their samples compared to 2D models and found a greater similarity to in-vivo tissues [11].
Since then, 3D models have evolved to two-post and wire systems. Typically, the posts are erected
within a few micrometres to centimetres from each other, and cells are seeded or cast around the tips
(see Figure 2.1) [18].

PDMS has been a popular choice for support structures or anchoring points in 3D models. This is
due to its preferential properties like biocompatibility, favourable mechanical properties, low cost, easy
manufacturing, suitability for micro-scale and various production methods [24]. Unfortunately, PDMS
has been found to absorb and retain small hydrophobic compounds such as drugs, potentially skewing
experimental data [14], [24]. Other materials can, therefore, be of preferential use. The two-post
system has also been prone to tissue slippage, leading to device failure and therefore terminating
the experiment [7], [14], [19]. Despite being a popular application, as highlighted in Table 2.1, these
platforms require careful and time-consuming manufacturing [14]. This also suggests the need for a
different anchoring structure.
2.1.2. Wire systems
Alternatively to the two-post systems, wire systems have emerged. Contrary to the two-posts, these
consist of free suspended wires supported by boundary side pillars. The cell bundles can then be added
between the wires. Some examples of this approach are shown in Figure 2.2. Figure 2.2a shows the
I-Wire system. This comprises a PDMS insert with titanium wires. A cell bundle is highlighted in the
white box and attached to the titanium wires. It is electrically stimulated through electrodes placed in
the additional ridges above and below the white box in the image. [7]
Figure 2.2c presents a platform, with thermoplastic elastomer (TPE) / quantum dot (QD) nanocomposite
micro-wires with conductive polymer (CP) micropillars for electrical tissue stimulation. DIW is used to
create the wires and the CPs. The authors chose a SEBS co-polymer as the polymer matrix. Into
which the QDs are dispersed. These are made by mixing core-shell CdSeZnS. The QDs’ fluorescence
of the wires allows for optical tracking of them. As the cells contract, they pull on the wires, leading to
deflection. The movement of the wires can be measured, which allows for the quantification of cellular
contraction force. In this setup, the electrical stimulation of the cells is achieved through the CP micro-
pillars under the tissue, although the pillars might cause minor interference. This platform is assembled
using 3D printing. [23]
The wires of a suspended system can be made of different materials. In Figure 2.2b, Zhao et al.
show the Biowire II platform with suspended wires made of Poly (octamethylene maleate (anhydride)
citrate) (POMaC). The wells are patterned into a Polystyrene (PS) sheet through hot embossing, and
the POMaC wires are glued onto the sheet. Parts of the assembly process of this platform are shown
in Figure 1.2. Changes to the design require a restart of the manufacturing process and the creation
of new moulds for new designs. This process is time-intensive and requires manual input; which is
unsuitable for automation. However, the cells can be paced electrically by carbon electrodes parallel
to the POMaC wires. Electrically stimulated cells contract, pulling the wires closer to each other; the
deflection of the wires can be measured. [14], [23], [25]

Some platforms, like the platform by Zhao et al. [14] or the I Wire by Sidorov et al. [7], allow a 3D
model of muscle tissue with non-invasive pacing options. The electrodes are placed parallel to the
wires carrying the cells, and they are not in direct contact with the tissue. Other platforms like the plat-
form by Wu et al. [23] rely on direct contact of conductive material with the cells. This risks mechanical
interference with the tissue. The recognised issue with using external electrodes is a mismatch in me-
chanical properties. Often, these electrodes are multitudes stiffer than the cellular constructs, leading
to damage to the tissue [23]. The same has to be considered when making the support materials in the
first place; the mechanical properties of cantilevers or wires, which cells anchor to, heavily influence the
properties of attached 3D tissue [26]. None of the platforms can stimulate cell bundles directly through
the anchoring points like posts or wires; additional material and manufacturing are required to establish
conductive paths in the platforms, leading to improved design ideas.
2.1.3. TMAT biomimetic micro-physiological tissue platform
Following the constraints outlined in the previous subsection, the TMAT group has developed an al-
ternative wire system to create a 3D micro-physiological biomimetic muscle platform. It uses a TPE
polymer base of Styrene-Ethylene-Butylene-Styrene (SEBS) as the matrix dissolved in Butyl Acetate
(ButAc) and supplemented with solid particles to create suspended wires called ribbons. The filler par-
ticles are graphite. [27]–[29]. The wires in the device shown in Figure 2.3 are 3D printed onto a PS
substrate.
For this device, a series of SEBS support pillars is 3D printed onto a PS substrate. Then, graphite wires
are 3D printed from one support pillar to another, leaving the wire suspended in-air. To complete the
device, vacuum-formed wells made of PS are attached to the substrate. For this, a layer of SEBS is
3D printed around the graphite wires; the vacuum-formed wells are stuck on top. The approach offers
customisation through the 3D printing protocol (Figure 1.2). [17], [27], [28].

In-air 3D printing
The wires are made of graphite ink using in-air 3D printing. The ink is freely suspended over multiple
millimetres, as seen in Figure 2.4. As the ink is extruded, it is pulled between its anchoring point on the
support strip and the extruding nozzle (Figure 2.4a). Various parameters control the balance between
ink suspension and ink sagging. Gravity is pulling the suspended down. Simultaneously, the solvent
evaporates from the ink, and it shrinks, tightens, and stiffens. The stiffness of the ink keeps it suspended.
If the printhead moves too slowly, the ink sinks and sticks to the substrate. If too much ink is printed, the
wire material sinks to the bottom. It is printed downwards and pulled towards the substrate by gravity.
On the other hand, if too little material is extruded for the wires, they become too thin. They either
detach from the support strip or snap during the stiffening when the solvent evaporates. Additional
mechanics that keep the ribbon suspended are interactions between the matrix and the solid particles
and the gelation of those particles. They aggregate and impose solid-like qualities on the material
(Figure 2.4b). [17]
The ratio of the speed of the nozzle movement (feed rate) and the volumetric flow of the ink has to be
balanced. The setup of this thesis allows for changing both these parameters. The feed rate or print
head speed is controlled in the geometric code (G-code). The G-code determines the print path of the
nozzle and is often created in computer-assisted design software and imported to the printing software.
The printing pressure is controlled manually on the side of the printer. Suspension of the ink further
relies on the attachment of the wire to the support strips. In black, these can be seen on the right of
Figure 2.4a. For this, the print height of the nozzle with the wire ink has to be adjusted. If it is too
high and far from the support, the ink will not attach to it. The material will then twirl around the nozzle,
not forming a suspended print. If it is too low, the nozzle will sink into the support material, causing
potential clogging issues and hindering suspension. [17].

2.2. Additive manufacturing methods
Both the TMAT platform and the example by Wu et al. show that 3D printing can be a suitable method
for manufacturing biomimetic 3D micro-physiological platforms [17], [23]. This method has evolved over
the last few years and allows multi-material manufacturing using the same printer. DIW enables the
creation of highly complex structures in the range of a few micrometres to centimetres by printing layer
on layer. For this, the printhead moves in three dimensions across the printing stage while depositing
material continuously at a given flow rate [30]. Material is extruded through the nozzle by three main
principles (Figure 2.5). Ink can be extruded pneumatically with varying pressure (p). It can be extruded
using a piston, where the piston movement is based on set volumetric extrusion. Or the material can
be guided out through a screw [31]. The feedstock for the printing, or simply ”ink”, is hereby extruded
through a micrometre-sized nozzle and, therefore, has to fulfil specific requirements [15].
Figure 2.5: Different working principles of ink dispensing for DIW [31].
2.3. Ink design for DIW 3D printing
DIW ink has to be extruded at moderate pressure (<700 kPa), should retain the designed shape after
printing and to print layer on layer, it has to maintain structural integrity. Printable inks are viscous,
have a high solid content, and must be extruded by a nozzle on a micrometre scale. Inks have to
solidify after printing either through curing (ultraviolet (UV) or chemical), solvent evaporation, heating
or cooling. Typically, the inks used for DIW are viscoelastic. They have a solid (elastic component) and
a liquid-like (viscous) component, both contributing to the printability of the material. Shear-thinning of
materials is another crucial property, as this enables the flow of the ink once pressure is applied to the
material. A shear-thinning material’s viscosity decreases when shear is applied, resulting in easier flow
[32]. If the ink returns to its previous properties, it is thixotropic. Ink properties change after printing
due to curing or solvent evaporation, leading to solid structures [33]. [15], [34]
Polymers are a popular choice for DIW inks as they fulfil many requirements. Amongst those, the TPEs
are used especially often because they do not require cross-linking after deposition. They are block
co-polymers made of repeating units, of which SEBS is an example. It is made of repeating units of
styrene, then ethylene, butylene, and repeats with styrene. These blocks can be tuned, allowing for
modification of the co-polymer. The TMAT platform uses a SEBS with a high ethylene butylene (EB)
content. The EB units are more flexible compared to the styrene units. TPEs can be dissolved in
solvents, forming a viscous solution. They can showcase good printability and find many applications
in DIW. Additionally, they exhibit the ink requirements at room temperature and can be extruded without
heating. [34]
Inks can also be composite materials. For such cases, the polymer often acts as the matrix, which is
supplemented with other functional materials. Additives can be used to modulate ink properties. They
affect the mechanics of the ink and can add functionalities. For example, an increased concentration
of solid additives drives the stiffness up. Electrical conductivity can be implemented by supplementing

(the polymer), the solvent and the additive. Typical fillers are particles in the micrometre range [35].

on layer is not as straightforward. This can be circumvented by printing into a support bath, often a

2.5. Bending Beam Theory

recognised that force exerted by the cells can be quantified by the deflection of anchor points, whether
cantilevers, posts, films, or wires. As mentioned before, the stiffness of the substrate is especially cru-
cial when culturing muscle cells [6], [8]. Stiff environments do not support correct myotube formation
the same way soft scaffolds do, and cell bundles have been found to rip off of stiff anchors more easily,
thereby terminating the experiments [6]. Therefore, it is sensible to try and estimate the stiffness of
printed constructs before seeding or 3D printing cells onto the device. Table 2.1 shows the stiffness

Materials and Methods
This section describes the materials and methods used in establishing the ribbon platforms. Followed
by details on relevant experiments conducted to characterise them. Additionally, we carried out prelim-
inary work to enhance our design and improve the outcome of the platforms.
3.1. Ink Preparation
Protocols related to the preparation of the ribbon platform and their 3D printing design have been
previously established in the group [27], [28]. They have been refined for this project to produce a print
in a commercially available 6-well plate (Corning, 3516).
For all inks used, we first removed potential additives in commercial SEBS granules (KRATON, G1645)
by Soxhlet extraction (set up provided in Appendix A.1). Other students in the group developed this
protocol before this project. The purified SEBS was dried in a vacuum oven overnight at 50 °C.
All ink calculations refer to the dry state of components as the solvent evaporates over time.
3.1.1. Glass ink
The glass ink was primarily used to print the ribbon platform’s support strands and boundary pillars
(see Figure 3.1). SEBS granules were combined with ButAc to a 33 %w/w stock solution in a glass
bottle. The lid was sealed with parafilm, and the polymer dissolved for 2-3 days. Hollow glass spheres
(CAS: 65997-17-3, Sigma- Aldrich Product No., 440345) were chemically functionalised with hexyl
silane before mixing. This step was also completed within the group before ink preparation.
The ink was made by combining the 33 %w/w SEBS stock with the functionalised glass spheres to
achieve a 75 %v/v ink. The lid of the mixing cup was closed and fixed with parafilm. The mixing cup
was placed in the DAC 150.1 FVZ speed mixer (German Engineering by Hauschild) and mixed at 2500
rotations per minute (RPM) for 5 minutes.
3.1.2. Graphite ink
The suspended ribbons of the platform were printed with graphite ink. Purified SEBS was mixed with
ButAc to a 37 %w/w stock solution. The printing ink was made by combining 37 %w/w SEBS stock
and 1 µm graphite (Nanografi, SKU NG04EO072379) to achieve a 20 %v/v graphite concentration. A
glass mixing ball was added to the mixing cup, the lid was closed, and the ink was mixed in the speed
mixer for 10 minutes at 2500 RPM.
All inks were loaded into 3 CC cartridges (Nordson EFD, 7366095) using a micro-spatula and trans-
ferred to the printer (3D Discovery G5 RegenHU Ltd, Switzerland). Glass ink syringes were always fitted
with an 840 µm plastic nozzle (Cellink, NZ4180005001). Syringes with the graphite ink were equipped
with metal precision tips of 150 µm and 200 µm diameter (Adhesive Dispensing Ltd. MMT015, MMT02).

3.2.2. Post treatments to 3D printed devices
The 6 mm long ribbon printed with the 150 µm nozzle using the 20 %v/v graphite ink was subjected
to post-treatments to investigate its characteristics, effects on the material’s stiffness and potential pre-
stress from the printing procedure. We tested a variety of approaches. The ribbon was UV treated for
1 hour (same as Section 3.1.3). Then, it was heated in the oven at 80ºC for 2 hours. Heating to 80ºC
was repeated overnight and then left to cool in the oven to room temperature.
Next, the ribbon was subjected to solvent treatment, always in Isopropyl alcohol (IPA). First, we used
10 %v/v heptane for 30 minutes, then 10 %v/v dodecane for 30 minutes. We followed up with 20 %v/v
dodecane for 30 minutes. The solvent was gradually removed from all samples. Half the solvent was
removed, and that half was supplemented with fresh IPA. This was repeated 3 times. The sample was
left in the last solution for a few minutes. Then, all liquid was aspirated, and the sample was left to dry.
Lastly, we plasma-treated the sample at 99 W for 135 seconds. Next, we washed the sample with
water after plasma treatment. After each single treatment, the sample was stiffness tested to verify the
effects of the treatment. We only used one sample to perform the post-treatment.

The stiffness testing was performed on a custom setup. It was built by a group in the department. The

experiment. During the measurement, the sample is deflected to the predetermined displacement by
a point load while the scale records the weight shift this creates.
In the bioprinting process, the ribbon was displaced up to 1.5 mm and beyond by the print nozzle to

• Number of cycles: 1
• Pause time between cycles: 3 s
• Offset: 0 mm

• Sampling rate: 0.1 mm/s

• Offset: 0.2 mm

• Sampling rate: 0.01 mm/s
• Displacement: 1000 µm
• Number of cycles: 3
• Pause time between cycles: 0 s
• Offset: 0.2 mm

Data was exported as .csv or .xlxs files and then analysed in MS Excel or Python.
3.5.1. Mechanical characterisation of platform
The data from the stiffness testing was analysed in two different ways. First, we assumed a linear
displacement for small deflections of the ribbon and thus analysed the data by fitting a straight line.
This fit was based on results from the bioteam working with the cells: it was determined that the ribbons
were pulled up to 400 µm by the cells. Therefore, the area for analysis was 0 - 400 µm. Thus, we fitted
a linear line to the loading curve between 0 and 400 µm. The slope of the line gave rise to the stiffness
of the ribbon in µN
µm.
R2 was calculated to show the suitability of the fitted line. Unless otherwise specified, stiffness data
is analysed in this manner. For larger displacements, we expected non-linear behaviour of the ribbon
and thus pursued a non-linear analysis approach by fitting a 2nd order polynomial to the entire loading
curve of our data [23].

Discussion
This chapter elaborates on the findings of this project. Results are placed into context and discussed.
5.1. 3D Printing of the Ribbon Platform
The ribbon platform 3D printing process has been streamlined with developments over multiple projects
in the group. The 6 mm long ribbon platforms were repeatedly printed into the 6-well plates, see Fig-
ure 4.1a. Suspending the 20 %v/v ink over the distances 3 mm, 6 mm and 9 mm was possible repeat-
edly. For the longer ribbons of 12 mm and 15 mm, suspending the ink was possible only with the higher
graphite loading. This shows that higher solid loading in the ink supports suspended printing over
longer distances.
The 3D printing code can easily be modified to produce longer ribbons. Quick code modification allows
the 3D printing of multiple platform configurations within an hour, as shown by the different ribbon
lengths in the well plates, see Figure 4.1. The cantilever prints are a good example of this, too. With the
same setup, the different meander sizes can be printed one after the other without delay or production
changes (Figure 4.9). Producing one 3D print of the ribbon platform takes 15 min to 20 min, increasing
in time for the longer ribbons.
Preparation for printing, including fresh ink mixing from stock solutions and printer setup, takes 45 min
to 60 min. In total, the time frame for a platform requires more time. The soxhlet extraction to purify
SEBS takes a day, and then the material needs to cool off and dry. The polymer needs about 3 days
to dissolve in ButAc; only then can the printing be completed. The production from raw material to the
completed device takes about a week. However, the purification step and mixing the solutions was not
completed for every print run. The stock solutions were prepared in bulk. The material can be used
over weeks if stored properly in airtight containers. The printing process alone can be completed within
a few hours. This approach is significantly quicker than other manufacturing, like soft lithography, which
Zhao et al. reported takes six days to produce one device [14]. Preparing material beforehand allows
the 3D printing to be a rapid and versatile production method.
Careful nozzle alignment before printing alleviates the printing process for the bioprints using cells and
ensures better positioning of the cell bundles. Nonetheless, the printing pressure might require minor
adjustments during a print. This has to be done actively based on careful observation of the print.
Initially, the ribbon platforms were printed using conical plastic nozzles made of Polypropylene (PP)
[27], [28], [43]. However, during this project, micro metal precision tapered tip needles became the
preferred choice for graphite ink printing. Plastic nozzles clogged quickly, required frequent pneumatic
printing pressure adjustments and produced inconsistent ribbons. In contrast, metal nozzles produced
more consistent print results and showed fewer clogging issues. They may also reduce the necking of
the ribbons, although this was not studied systematically. For these reasons, all platforms were printed
using metal nozzles.
A possible explanation for the weaker performance of plastic nozzles could be the hydrostatic interac-
33

tions between the nozzle material PP and the SEBS:ButAc and graphite ink. On the vendor’s website,
the metal nozzles are advertised as stainless steel for printing with ”thermoplastics and other harsh
materials” [44]. Perhaps this avoids potential hydrostatic interactions and smoothens the 3D printing
process. Henceforth, the metal nozzles should be used for reliable platform printing.
An additional advantage of metal nozzles is their multi-use. They can be cleaned and reused. However,

test this theory. Regardless, the 6 mm long ribbons can be printed at a success rate of roughly 90 %.
This decreases slightly with increasing ribbon length until it reaches approximately 60 % for the 15 mm
long ribbons.
For the bioprinting, the ribbons had to be at least 500 mm above the substrate height, and this allows
sufficient space for 3D bioprinting. Glass ink supports printed with one pass of the 840 µm nozzle were
both above that threshold, allowing successful bioprinting (Figure 4.2b and Table 4.1). The ribbon length
was measured carefully during platform printing to ensure appropriate length. Despite a 1 mm printing
tolerance, the samples were printed slightly shorter than intended (Table 4.2). Shorter ribbons would
usually occur if the support strips were over-extruded and thus took up more space than designed in
the 3D printing code. To avoid this for the ribbon platform, the printing pressure was adjusted to achieve
ribbons of the right length. In the bioprinting process, the positioning of the ribbons was not the main
issue, but the stiffness was.
5.2. Stiffness Test
The first round of experiments showed that the 6 mm and 9 mm long ribbons could withstand the 3 mm
displacement without plastic deformation. The results show that the 6 mm, and any longer ribbon and
its mechanical properties, should remain intact after displacement by the bio-print.
5.2.1. Cyclical Testing
Cyclical testing was performed to observe the repeated strain in the ribbons and replicate cell behaviour
better. For this, tests were run at 0.1 mm

However, several issues with these cycles emerged. Early cycles reported erratic data such as square
wave patterns, force readings of –2000 N and flatlining. Hysteresis loops were inverted, implying energy
was created in the experiment. This measurement error can be attributed to a delay in the measurement
scale and MATLAB data acquisition. An offset or dummy cycle was ineffective in correcting this, so
the sampling rate was decreased. Quick sampling rates performed poorly on every occasion. This
is a limitation within the custom setup and prevents quicker testing mimicking cell behaviour. These
findings led to sampling rate tests and showed a sampling rate of 0.01 mm

from each measurement. Cyclical testing at the chosen rate of 0.01 mm

was not possible. Fatigue testing would mean many cycles of a small displacement to observe the
mechanical behaviour of the ribbons over a prolonged time. This would replicate the behaviour of cell
bundles better, tugging on the ribbon repeatedly. Fatigue test results could show a ribbon’s behaviour
under continuous cell attachment.
5.2.2. Ribbon Characteristics
Figure 4.4, Table 4.3 and Figure 4.5 show that the ribbons behaved partially as expected according to
the Euler-Bernoulli theory. If the ribbon length is increased, it becomes softer. If the nozzle diameter
is increased, thereby increasing I, the ribbon stiffens (Figure 4.5). These relationships are governed

increase in stiffness between the 20 and 30 %v/v ink for the 6 mm, 9 mm and 12 mm long ribbons can be

The ribbon stiffness emerged as a key issue. According to the measurements, the 6 mm long ribbon is

show any visible contractions under pacing conditions, likely attributable to the excessive stiffness of
their anchor points, the ribbons.
Following this, the softening treatments were performed, see Figure 4.6a. A first hypothesis suggested
that the ribbons might be pre-stressed from the printing process which could be reduced through various
approaches. Due to the in-air printing nature of producing the suspended ribbons, the ink material is
stretched in the air without any structural support. Additionally, as the solvent evaporates during printing,
the material shrinks and stiffens. These components contribute to the pre-stress in the material. The
pre-stress is confirmed by the effects of the softening treatments Figure 4.6a. The material softens
under the treatments, whether heat or solvent, releasing some pre-stress.
Neither of the treatments had a strong enough impact on reducing the stiffness to 0.5 µN
µm to 1 µN
µm by
itself. Only a combination of treatments would have reduced the stiffness to the desired range. A
more vigorous treatment emerged from solvent use. Here, the ribbons were swelled slightly without
dissolving. They recovered and dried before testing. While this worked slightly, one singular treatment
with solvents did not show sufficient effect to bring the pristine ribbon into the desired stiffness range.
Additionally, a plasma treatment was performed to potentially enhance charge injection. The effect on
the stiffness was measured, it appears to have slightly hardened the ribbons (Figure 4.6b). Plasma
treatment etches the SEBS, so the sample has to be washed to remove potential residue. This made
no difference to the stiffness. The etching effect explains stiffening the ribbon; it removes some of
the elastic components of the ribbon. This would simultaneously mean it decreases the radius and,
therefore, I of the ribbon. Considering Equation (2.1), this increases the stiffness. A charge injection
study was not performed in this project to confirm the effects of plasma on this aspect.
Alternatively, to reduce the stiffness, the length of the ribbons can be increased according to Equa-
tion (2.1). Therefore, the design of the 12 mm and 15 mm long ribbons was developed and printed
with the 20 %v/v and 30 %v/v graphite ink Figure 4.4. As the equation suggests, the measurements
support that ribbons become softer with increased length. The following ribbons were measured to
have a stiffness that the literature reflects.
• 9 mm and 12 mm, 150 µm 20 %v/v graphite ink.
• 12 mm and 15 mm, 150 µm 30 %v/v graphite ink (Table 4.3).
However, these ribbons have not yet been tested with 3D bioprinting to confirm their suitability. This is
further discussed in Chapter 6.
5.3. Euler-Bernoulli bending beam theory
Generally, the behaviour of the ribbons follows the principle of the theory. Longer ribbons are softer.
Thicker ribbons, printed with a bigger nozzle, therefore driving the radius and I up, have an increased
stiffness. The data shows that the faster a ribbon is displaced, the softer it appears to behave, as seen
in Figure 4.3. This can be attributed to the viscoelastic properties of SEBS. The elastic part, urging a
material to return to its original state instantaneously after deformation, is matched with the viscoelastic
properties, causing time-dependent deformation of the material [45]. This is the typical behaviour of
polymers [46]. Additionally, many models assume uniform stress distribution in a material, which might
be shifted for a polymer matrix with solid particles [35]. Lastly, this time-dependent behaviour is not
accounted for in the Euler-Bernoulli bending beam theory and requires further analysis.

Expanding on the bending beam theory for the ribbons of this project, the measurements and theoreti-

found across all ribbon lengths. According to theoretical values, the ribbons should be in the stiffness
range of similar applications in the literature. But the practice proves otherwise. Additionally, according
to the Equation (2.1), the stiffness should decrease 8-fold if the length is doubled. The experimental
measurements do not match this prediction, as the measurements are only 3.3-fold softer when dou-
bling the length. Through all possible ribbon lengths, the theory predicts greater softening effects than
were measured.
Based on this, the values used for the equation and calculations of theoretical values are incorrect. The
printing procedure governs the length and the radius. However, the radius is measured by hand in the
software Image J, and an error might be caused by that value. Further, this approach assumes the
ribbon to be perfectly circular. Investigating E relates to the notion that the material experiences pre-
stress in the printing process, affecting its stiffness. Another student measured the E used for theoretical
calculations from a dogbone of the same material. The dogbone was printed onto a substrate, and the
ribbon was printed in-air. Therefore, it is stretched in the printing process by the printing motion of
the nozzle; simultaneously, it stiffens and tightens because the solvent evaporates. This might have
induced pre-stress into the ribbon but might have also had some effect on the dogbone. Most likely,
the Young’s modulus used in the equation is not representative of the platform obtained in the end.
The softening treatment confirms the pre-stress, which is released in the treatments. A pre-stress of
the material is not accounted for in Equation (2.1). In addition to the reasons above, Equation (2.1)
holds for small displacements in the linear region [47]. The stiffness test is outside this area, where the
behaviour becomes non-linear and analysis becomes increasingly complex. It might be beneficial to
attempt to model the stiffness of the ribbons with a more suitable equation that can contain pre-stress
and time-dependent behaviour at large displacements.
The Young’s modulus was derived from measurement data in Section 4.2.2 to investigate this further.
The Young’s modulus is magnitudes higher than first anticipated. This is most likely why the theoretical
and measured stiffness values are so different, in addition to measuring the stiffness outside the validity
of the Equation (2.1). At the large displacement in this project, the material might be pulled rather than
bent. However, the calculated E, being much higher, would match what was also observed by the cell
experiment. The ribbons were too stiff to be pulled by the cell bundles, hindering visible contractions
on the ribbons. Investigating the measurement data for the entire loading curve (Figure 4.8) gives rise
to the force required for specific displacements and non-constant stiffness curves. If the assumptions
necessary for the theoretical modelling were met in the experiment, a linear force curve and constant
stiffness value would be expected. This shows the necessity for higher fidelity theoretical models like
non-linear beam theories and finite element analyses to predict trustworthy stiffness values. Such
analysis was outside this project’s scope [47].
5.4. New cantilever ink and design
Results from the ink sweep show a limit to particle loading into a polymer matrix, see Table 4.4. Small
particles at high loading were not printable. Due to their high surface area, they bind to each other, form
aggregates in the ink and clog the nozzle [35]. Still it is this surface area that makes them the preferred
choice for creating a highly conductive material, as they can bind to each other and form a conductive
path [15], [29], [35]. A poly-dispersed ink of multiple particle sizes has shown greater printability; this
can be attributed to small particles working as lubricants between the bigger ones [15]. This can be
taken advantage of to produce inks with high particle loadings while maintaining printability.
As such, the cantilevers with the 50 %v/v ink made of particles with different sizes (43 %v/v 1 µm and
7%v/v 20 µm) were successfully printed with 150 µm and 200 µm nozzles (Figure 4.9). The 400 µm ×
400 µm meandered design (printed with a 150 µm nozzle) was selected for further study. The porous
structure was clear on the top view, but the sagging of the structure on the side view was absent.
Further work characterising the cantilever is outstanding and thus discussed further in Section 6.2.
To conclude the device section, the data above shows that different geometries and stiffnesses can be
produced easily using the same setup. Printing inks can be varied and made with the same protocol
provided in this project, allowing the printing of varied graphite concentrations.

5.5. Cell integration into the platforms
The bio-prints showed success for multiple days. In Figure 4.12, the cells anchored to the ribbons
can be observed. In Figure 4.12b, the cell bundle shows a dogbone structure with a slimmer centre
and wider anchoring points, and it tugs on the anchoring ribbon. The fact that cells attached to stiffer

a result of muscle tissue being highly dependent on the mechanics of its environment. Additionally,
paced cell bundles produced more force, supporting evidence that electrical input to muscle cells can
be beneficial in force generation.
At the lower rate (Figure 4.12a), the cells do not pull on the ribbons as much. However, the bioteam
did not record any active contractions under pacing; a likely reason for this was the high stiffness of
the ribbons. The stiffness could lead to cell bundles being unable to exert enough force to pull the stiff
anchor point. [42]

enable visible contractions of cell bundles.

7
Conclusion
This project produced a ribbon platform by 3D printing suspended graphite wires into a standard well
plate. The 3D printing materials are based on soft polymeric composite materials, where properties
are modulated based on different fillers. The mechanical properties of the platform were characterised
by thoroughly testing the stiffness of the ribbons. The results showed a discrepancy between the
theoretical values and measured values. In parallel, a new ink and geometry were explored to produce
a soft cantilever with a high electrical conductivity to pace the cells directly through the anchor points.
Lastly, muscle cells were added to the device to test if cells would contract under electrical pacing.
The ribbon platforms of this project can be printed easily at different geometries using the same ink
recipe. This project contains the protocol and printing procedure to print this device into a standard
well plate repeatedly. Only minor modifications to the printing code are required to produce a device
with different mechanics. The length, thickness and solid particle loading of the ribbon mainly determine
this behaviour.
Assuming the ribbon to be a doubly-fixed Euler-Bernoulli beam, the effects of changing its dimensions
can be predicted in limits. The stiffness measurements performed in this project were performed outside
the validity of this equation and did not match theoretical values. A system was printed where slight
changes to the code allowed for the elongation of the ribbon. This allows modulation of ribbon stiffness
and produces ribbon in stiffness ranges similar to applications in the literature. Similarly, the effects
of varying the ribbon radius were demonstrated by using different nozzles. Decreasing the nozzle
diameter reduces the stiffness. Both these effects are accounted for in the equation.
Changing the solid content has shown several effects. Increasing the particle loading of the ink in-
creases its viscosity, making it thicker. Past a certain point of particle loading, the particles aggregate
and clog the nozzle, making printing impossible and the material brittle. The 50 % ink created in this
project has shown these properties. This behaviour is dependent on particle properties like shape and
size. Small particles, due to their comparatively large surface area, bind to each other and can form
agglomerates.
A higher solid content is beneficial when printing in-air over longer distances. The ink is then stiffer and
can remain free-suspended over longer ranges. There is a trade-off between how many particles can
be loaded into the matrix, the printability of the ink and the stiffness of the final construct. Increasing
the graphite particles drives the conductivity up but needs to be balanced so it can still be extruded
through the print nozzle and suspended in-air.
In conjunction with the embedded printing of cells into this platform, a 3D model of muscle tissue was
achieved. HSkM were 3D printed into the platforms and maintained attached over numerous days. The
fact that cells attached to stiffer ribbons printed with the bigger nozzle grew thicker and exerted higher
force is, per current literature, a result of muscle tissue being highly dependent on the mechanics of its
environment.
The stiffness of the anchor points limited this system. Cells could not pull the stiff ribbons far enough

Cell work on those ribbons should be incorporated into the next steps.
Alternatively, a cantilever structure can be explored. Following the Euler-Bernoulli principle, these
should be softer than double fixed beams. The initial work in this project shows that various geometries
can be printed at higher graphite loadings. This should also allow direct electrical pacing of cells,
improving this model and its biomimetic properties.
