Abstract
The aim of the following thesis is to examine an analytical solution to the induced velocities
of a lightly loaded actuator disk with radial loading and validate the results against those
obtained from a vortex-based model.
The ﬁrst part of this report will go through the theory used, both for the actuator disk
solution and for the vortex model used. The second part describes the blade element
momentum (BEM) model used, and the corrections to be examined, including a shape
correction factor to BEM based on the actuator disk solution. The thesis then describes the
case studies to be examined and the method of validation followed by the results obtained.
The results validates the actuator disk solution as several diﬀerent cases are examined.
Furthermore the results also reveals the need for a correction to the BEM-method, as
radial loading is not properly accounted for. This means that the axially induced velocities
obtained from BEM diverge from results obtained from both the vortex model and the
actuator disk solution. The results obtained for a non-planar disk with the proposed
correction factor to BEM shows good correlation with the results obtained from the vortex
model, at a lower computational cost.

Introduction 1
Wind turbines have been around since 1887 when Charles F. Brush constructed what
is known as the ﬁrst automatic wind turbine [9]. The diameter was 17 meter and the
output, used to charge batteries, was 12 kW. In Denmark, the wind turbine industry
was kickstarted by Poul la Cour, building the ﬁrst electricity producing wind turbine in
1897 [28]. In 1918 there where 120 diﬀerent locale utility companies with a wind turbine,
totalling 3 MW, and covering approximately 3 % of the consumption in Denmark.
Since then the wind turbine industry has been ever developing, with the record breaking
year of 2015 when 42.1 % [11] of the total electricity consumption in Denmark came from
wind power. As electricity became a larger part of the supply chain, a shift also came to
focus on more eﬃcient wind turbines. This shift meant an increase in size and output
as bigger turbines are often more eﬃcient. In 2011 the largest blades measured around
62 m [22], whereas the biggest wind turbine from Vestas in 2017, the V164-8.0 MW [38],
measures 80 m from hub to tip. However increased dimensions are not simply about up
scaling everything. With increase in blade size, manufacturers are also looking at more
ﬂexible blades. This means that deﬂections are more pronounced, and that rotors are often
coned or prebend to avoid the blades hitting the tower in heavy winds. A illustration of
the coned rotor is shown in ﬁgure 1.1. Because of new designs with prebend, coning, and
the deﬂection of the blades, the swept area can no longer be seen as simply a ﬂat plane.

1. Introduction
The method prevalent in the wind turbine industry to predict induced wind by the blades,
and thus loads and power output is the blade element method (BEM). This method is
developed on an ideal wind turbine, divided in smaller segments to more accurately predict
each segments induced velocities. However, the method itself is developed for planar disks,
only accounting for loads axial and tangential to the blades. This leaves the radial inﬂuence,
which for small turbines with a plane rotor may be negligible, but for big turbines may
have a signiﬁcant eﬀect on loads and power output.
As stated, the BEM is the prevalent method used by the industry, but many methods
exists, varying in complexity and accuracy. This thesis will examine one of these other
methods. An analytical model based on actuator disk theory. The goal of the thesis is to
validate this model against a well examined vortex based model. And to ﬁnd a correction
to the BEM method to make it more accurate for use on a wider array of rotors.
1.1
Scope
The objective of this thesis is to examine the validity of the analytical solution for a lightly
loaded disk with an additional radial loading. The idea is that the analytical method can
be used to model coned, curved rotors, and rotors with winglets in a fast way in engineering
models. The validity is checked against a simple vortex-based method, ﬁrstly for a planar
disk with inﬁnite tip speed and gradually including complexity until lastly a curved rotor
with non-uniform load and ﬁnite tip speed is examined. After the validation the method
is used to develop an easy implementable correction to BEM to account for non-planar
rotors.
1.2
Structure
The structure of the thesis following this introduction is ﬁrstly general theory, followed by
description of the actuator disk model and the vortex based method. After the theory is
outlined the correction factors implemented and developed are explained. In the chapter 7,
the test-cases and the method of comparison is explained and selected results are included.
Finally a discussion of the results and a conclusion will close the thesis.
All programming was done in Matlab and the codes used can be seen in appendix C.

to a torque on the rotor. As energy is extracted the velocity of the incoming wind will
inevitably be altered creating an induced velocity by the blades. The forces on a blade can

disk was introduce as show in ﬁgure 2.1. This is a permeable disk inﬁnitely thin, but
covering the same area as the rotor. The forces from the rotor is now distributed over
this disk, and the changes in forces and velocity must balance out because of the mass
conservation law and conservation of momentum.

Actuator Disk Theory 3
The actuator disk model is devised in [24]. The scope of the model is to ﬁnd the induced
velocities and pressure from an arbitrary known geometry and a known loading. The model
is derived for a lightly loaded disk. The following section will give a brief overview of the
derivation, for a complete description see [24].
3.1
Derivation

the angular velocity Ω, like the vortex model. However as opposed to the discretization of
the curvature in a number of cylinders, the curvature of the disk or rotor is described as a
function of s and the pressure jump over the disk (∆p(s)) is also described as a function
of s. This is shown in ﬁgures 3.1a and 3.1b. The inﬂow is assumed axi-symmetric and a
cylindrical coordinate system (x, r, φ) is used.

3.4
Implementation
To evaluate the induced velocity numerically outside of the disk is straightforward and
done by numerical integration of the integrands 3.16 and 3.17 using the Matlab function
’trapz’ (a numerical integration using the trapezoidal method).
To evaluate them on the disk an algorithm is used, here shown for the axial velocity:
• The integrand in equation 3.16 is calculated at a number of points from hub to tip.
• The singular logarithmic term from equation 3.25 is subtracted.
• The integral of the logarithmic term is calculated by the Matlab function ’quadgk’
(an adaptive Gauss-Kronrod quadrature).
• Close to the singularity point (x′, r′) the Cauchy terms from equations 3.34 and 3.35
are subtracted and evaluated by ’int’ to ﬁnt the Cauchy principal value as described
in equation 3.36.
• The remaining integrand, a smooth function, is integrated using ’trapz’.
• The three parts, the logarithmic, the Cauchy term and the remaining integrand is
then added to give the total axial velocity.
The same procedure is applied to the radial velocity. The evaluation of the tangential
velocity is straightforward and poses no problems.

Vortex Tube Theory 4
The current chapter will give an introduction to the vortex model and how it is derived.
The vortex model is used in this thesis as a tool against which the actuator model is
validated. It is based on vortex tube theory, or more speciﬁcally it is based on a numerical
model developed by E. Branlard and M. Gaunna [7], also referred to as the cylindrical
vortex model, coupled with later work by the same authors on a superposition of several
vortex tubes to account for varying load along the blades [5]. The beneﬁts of using this
model is that the computational power is lower than solving the full system, however the
precision is still kept at a reasonable level.

Discussion 8
The following discussion will have a focus on the results obtained and the limitations of
the models used. A great deal of focus will also be on what the results of this thesis might
be used for in applications and suggestions for further work is made lastly.
One error found to late in the project to be properly corrected was the integration of the
Cauchy singularity found in equation 3.32 and 3.31. The method used in this thesis was a
numerical approximation using the trapezoidal integration method from Matlab. However
a proper integration would require the method described in 3.36. The error produced
was, as seen in the results, however negligible. This most likely stems from the choice of
integration points. The dummy points were equidistantly placed, and one of these were
chosen as evaluation point. This means that the dummy points near the singularity point
were symmetrical. Error produced on the left side of the singularity was thus cancelled out
by the error produced on the right side.
The same error occurs in the shape correction factor. This also means that a proper
evaluation with analytical integrals will aﬀect the computation time negatively. Further
study may shed light on when numerical integration can be substituted and thus reduce
the computation time to that obtained in this report.
8.1
Results
Looking at the results from the actuator disk model, it showed very good correlation with
the vortex model for all cases examined. It also showed great correlation with BEM for
the planar disk, and a validation of the model is therefore possible.
When looking at the comparison with the non-planar rotor, the BEM model underestimated
the axial velocity by a factor depending on the degree of non-planarity, seen in ﬁgure 7.9.
This was as expected as BEM neglects to account for the radial inﬂuence, meaning that a
heavily coned rotor will have a bigger discrepancy than a rotor with less coning.
The load correction factor from equation 6.5, seen in ﬁgure 7.10 showed the possibility
to correct the model developed for a lightly loaded disk to cover heavily loaded disks.
The method implemented in this thesis was based on an integral correction factor, not
accounting for non-uniform load, this meant that the correction were only locally accurate.
To increase accuracy a local correction factor with the local coeﬃcient of thrust should
therefore be used.
40
==PAGE:53==
8.2. Limitations of the methods
DTU
The shape correction factor from equation 6.13 showed very promising. The correlation
proved quite accurate and the complexity of the method did not prove excessive, the only
higher order mathematics was the Legendre functions. This also meant that computationally
BEM combined with the correction was faster than the vortex model, seen in ﬁgure 7.15.
Note should be taken as this was done with a numerical integration scheme, which does
not account properly for the singularities which may be present as described in section 3.2.
8.2
Limitations of the methods
One of the main limitations of the actuator disk model, is the computational eﬀort required
as seen in ﬁgure 7.15. The code used here is not optimized, so great improvements are still
possible. But even assuming great improvements the code will still be slower than using
the vortex model. Here vortex model also has the advantage of solving the non-linearized
loaded, so it can be used for heavily loaded rotors as well.
Both of these models, actuator model and vortex model, are however magnitudes slower
than the BEM model used. Which in turn has the disadvantage of not accounting for
radial loading or radially induced velocity and only being applicable on the actuator disk.
A solution to a computationally light solution could be the shape correction factor. Whilst
still being slower and more complex than BEM it did oﬀer the ability to more accurately
predict induced velocities.
Another limitation applying to all the methods is the assumption of axisymmetry. The
occurrence of axisymmetric ﬂow over a wind turbine is rare. Most often wind speeds
increase with distance from the ground. A correction to the methods would therefore need
to be implemented.
8.3
Proposal for further study
An interesting study that was unfortunately not possible due to time constraints would be
the study of winglets using the actuator disk method, and subsequently construction of a
shape correction using the results to adapt BEM to account for these. However to analyze
these most likely requires the proper implementation of the evaluation of the Cauchy
singularity. The method used in the current report produced negligible error, however as
the geometry changes to a more drastic curvature the error when evaluating the Cauchy
singularity is also expected to be more pronounced. Suggestions to a proper evaluation is
given in [4].
Further study proposals could be the examination of the actuator disk solution with other
higher ﬁdelity methods. What could be interesting would be to compare to a model
including such things as wake expansion and non-constant far wake velocity, to evaluate
the error produced by these simpliﬁcations in the actuator and vortex model. A few
possibilities are given below.

8. Discussion
The ﬁrst being the actuator line model [34]. The merits of this model is that it does not
assume axissymmetry, but is still relatively simply to compare with the models used in the
thesis, as it takes the loading on the blades like the actuator disk model and the vortex
model.
And the second being the generalized actuator disk model [33]. Whilst still assuming
axisymmetry, this model is based on a ﬁnite diﬀerence solution to the Navier-stokes
equations. This method has furthermore been validated against experimental data.
A comparison with the obtained results from [23] could also be interesting. Here coned
rotors have been examined in diﬀerent cases including the real case of the Tjæreborg 5 MW
wind turbine. A comparison between the results obtained here and the results obtained
from the current thesis could be interesting to see the validity of the models here.
Another proposed study could be the optimal distribution of evaluation points for the
vortex model and dummy points for the actuator disk model. Currently they are chosen
as equidistant nodes, but another way could be to have a more reﬁned grid near strong
gradients in load or geometry.
8.4
Applications
For either of the methods described in the current thesis to be applicable in models such
as HAWC2 two aspects needs consideration: the assumption of axisymmetric ﬂow, and a
tip correction to account for a ﬁnite number of blades [30].
Besides these two the models needs to account for the unsteadiness of wind. This is done
by creating an iterative loop in which the inﬂow angle is dependent on the loads on the
blades and vice versa. Thus by importing proﬁle data dependent on inﬂow angle the models
can be made adaptive to account for varying wind speeds and pitching of the blades. An
unsteady model is examined for BEM in [31], whereas an unsteady model for the vortex
model is examined in [5].
42
==PAGE:55==
Conclusion 9
The conclusion drawn for the current thesis is that the actuator disk model is validated
for the case of a lightly loaded rotor. The results showed very good correlation with a
linearized vortex model for all cases, and good correlation with a linearized BEM model
for a planar disk.
The results also showed the shortcomings of the BEM method when analyzing non-planar
rotors, here the axial induced velocity was not properly accounted for and a new model
or a correction factor is therefore necessary. Using the actuator disk model with the high
thrust correction was a possibility, however the high computational eﬀort required proved
infeasible in large scale applications.
Combining the BEM method with the proposed shape correction, based on the actuator
disk model and a high thrust correction, a solution was obtained showing good correlation
with the solution obtained through vortex theory. The computational eﬀort of this solution
also proved to be more eﬃcient, making the correction a possible in further applications.
However the shape correction requires further examination to either incorporate analytical
integration of the singularities or determine in which cases numerical integration suﬃces.
