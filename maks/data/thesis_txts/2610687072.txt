Abstract
Once a windfarm is constructed, it enters the Operation & Maintenance (O&M) phase un-
til the end of its manufacturer-certiﬁed design lifetime of 20-25 years. Windfarm owners
are focused on maximizing revenue from their assets and operate their assets for maxi-
mum plant availability and efﬁciency. Their operational strategy is pre-dominated by the
time value of money and the need to recover loans as soon as possible. But with increasing
subsidy-free tenders and economies of scale, revenue over the lifetime and knowledge of
asset life reduction become more important. Situations with unfavourable market prices
and post-rated wind speeds imply reduction of turbine fatigue life with low proﬁtability.
Such conditions call for smarter turbine operation. Therefore, this project focuses on op-
timization of the operational strategy of a wind turbine to achieve maximum revenue with
a constraint on the Damage Equivalent Loads (DELs) on the turbine’s main components.
The project explores the possible life extension and proﬁtability of such an optimization
through wind turbine induction control such as de-rating and power boosting. The op-
timization problem uses the following data inputs: a) power and lifetime loads obtained
from surrogate models developed by Andreas Thorsen in his MSc Thesis, b) mesoscale
wind time series based on a 35 year database from the Weather Research and Forecasting
(WRF) model and c) energy price time series from Nordpool. An example problem on
layout optimisation from DTU’s opensource software TOPFARM is used to investigate
optimisation techniques and it’s further applicability. An optimisation problem is set up
for individual turbine optimisation and the results are analysed. This problem can be
further integrated into Topfarm. Suggestions on future work are provided.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The past two decades have seen tremendous growth in wind energy capacity around the
world, with 2019 closing at 651 GW of global wind energy capacity. There were 60.4 GW
of installations worldwide in 2019 alone [1]. Annual installations are expected to reach
65 GW in 2020 and possibly even in 2021, despite effects of the COVID-19 pandemic
[16].
Figure 1.1: Global yearly wind energy capacity addition, [1]
The industry continues to advance in technology growth and smarter market dynam-
ics. With growing grid integration, Offshore wind is becoming the only variable baseload
power generator [17]. Additionally, various new concepts are being actively researched
such as Power-to-X, where wind power could be temporarily stored as hydrogen or nat-
ural gas to be used later in production of electricity or heat or as fuel for transport. It is
worth to note that the market growth is higher outside of the EU, especially in onshore

wind. The offshore market has expanded from Europe to other countries such as United
States and to East and South East Asia. Thus there are new challenges and environmental
conditions for which windfarm developers and turbine designers must prepare for. Also,
as lower tender strike prices and even zero-subsidy tenders are increasingly seen, espe-
cially in offshore wind, there is added pressure on developers to ensure proﬁtability.
As wind energy grows in the world energy mix, it is important that windfarm oper-
ational efﬁciency and lifetime economics continue to improve for sustainable growth of
this industry. This thesis explores this by tuning wind turbine operational strategy with
the objective of revenue maximisation while ensuring turbine component fatigue limits
are not surpassed. In theory, at the end of the turbine lifetime certiﬁed by the manufac-
turer, the asset could then be in a better condition for re-sale or continued operation from
a fatigue point of view.
In order to highlight the background and motivation for this project, some basic tech-
nical and economic concepts of windfarm operation are ﬁrst explained.
Wind turbine and plant operation and lifetime
Wind turbines are generally designed for an operational lifetime of 20-25 years. Once
installed and commissioned, a wind turbine or a windfarm enters the Operation and Main-
tenance (O&M) phase of its lifecycle. O&M or service costs grow along with windfarm
life to the point that continued operation may not be so economically attractive and to-
wards the end of the 25-year mark, the asset owner takes the decision to either sell or
decommission or re-power the windfarm.
During the O&M phase, windfarm owners focus on efﬁcient operation of the wind
power plant with minimal downtime in order to generate maximum Annual Energy Pro-
duction (AEP). This has a direct impact on plant LCOE as explained later. Monitoring
during the O&M phase involves keeping track of certain metrics such as AEP, plant avail-
ability, Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF), Mean Time To Response (MTTR), etc or
even detailed Structural Health Monitoring (SHM). This enables corrective (and some-
times predictive) maintenance. But this is a short-term response and long-term actions,
or strategies are unknown to asset owners. This project aims to derive insights that will
inform long-term operational strategies for optimum proﬁtability over plant lifetime.

Power curve and AEP
Annual Energy Production (AEP) is a combination of the wind distribution and the
wind turbine power curve. The power curve indicates the electrical power output of a
pitch-controlled wind turbine at different wind speeds as shown below in Figure 1.2 and
Figure 1.3.
Figure 1.2:
Sample wind turbine power
curve, [2]
Figure 1.3: Sample wind speed distribution
and turbine power curve, [3]
Turbine operation varies with the wind speed. Since power grows with the cube of
wind speed, turbine operation is aimed at maximum power generation between cut-in and
rated wind speed. Rated wind speed is when the turbine reaches the rated power output
of its generator and post this limit, turbine operation is controlled to maintain constant
power.
Turbine Control
Turbine control can be either induction based or based on wake steering. Induction
control involves pitch or torque control applied to adjust the axial induction factor of the
turbine and hence the rotor’s ability to extract energy from incoming wind.
Wake steering pertains more to windfarm level control where overall plant efﬁciency
is improved by steering individual turbine wakes through yaw or tilt methods.
This thesis project only deals with turbine control using blade pitching.

Asset Life Management
Wind turbines are fatigue-critical machines operating at higher no. of cycles com-
pared to aircraft, [6]. Cyclical loads from time-varying wind speeds contribute to accu-
mulated fatigue damage.
Figure 1.6: Schematic S-N diagram for various fatigue-critical structures [6]
Effects of fatigue damage are seen in failures of major components like main bearing,
tower, blades, etc. Main Component Exchange is a signiﬁcant blow to the proﬁtability of
the asset and windfarm owners should have a plan to prevent and/or manage such situa-
tions.

The state of the market
Traditionally wind power plants have received subsidies or have been on long term
Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs). In the absence or expiry of these schemes, wind
power plant owners are increasingly ﬁnding themselves in a ‘merchant market’ environ-
ment where proﬁts are also dependent on external factors and market players. The current
trend of the market is to move towards zero subsidy environments [22], [23]. Windfarm
developers are able to achieve lower LCOE values as they see a year-on-year increase in
the nameplate capacity of wind turbines, [24].

Electricity prices tend to be low during high inﬂux of wind energy, [25]
Based on how the day ahead market is cleared with a spot price decided by the con-
sumer demand curve and the supply curve built according to merit ordering of unit com-
mitments of generating plants, an increase in wind energy in the system results in a lower
wholesale electricity spot price. Since wind energy does not have a fuel cost, its low
marginal cost implies that it pushes out other expensive generators thereby shifting the
supply curve to the right. This effect is called the merit order effect.
Note on negative electricity prices [26]
Negative prices tend to occur on the electricity exchange during very high and in-
ﬂexible power generation simultaneous with low electricity demand, such as on public
holidays like Christmas. This happens when power producers prefer to keep the plant
running despite low proﬁt to perhaps avoid other costs such as ramp up costs, costs of not
meeting contractual auxiliary services, [27].

Given this background of windfarm economics, turbine operation, lifetime fatigue
loads and market conditions, this thesis investigates the possible proﬁt in reducing DELs
at the expense of power production by de-rating a turbine/plant using pitch control when
the wind speed is above rated.

sought. The problem is classiﬁed as an optimization exercise and the output of this exer-
cise is to be an operational strategy that maximizes revenue whilst applying a constraint
on the DELs.

2.1
Wake models
A wind turbine is always associated with a downstream rotor wake where the wind speed
is reduced and turbulence is higher. When considering wind turbines in a windfarm, in-
teracting turbine wakes have an effect on energy production and loads. Therefore, this
has to be considered during windfarm design using an appropriate wake model.

symmetric about the rotor axis, without turbulence, based on a top-hat inﬂow proﬁle at
the rotor and wake radius expanding linearly with downstream distance, [9].

b. Models for Load Calc: Frandsen, DWM – turbulence intensity model
While the velocity deﬁcit enables calculation of energy production of downstream

capture some of the physics to model the wake turbulence ﬂow ﬁeld.
Dynamic Wake Meandering (DWM) model
As explained in [28],[10] and [29], the Dynamic Wake Model considers the wake as
a passive tracer driven by large-scale turbulence in the atmospheric boundary layer. The
DWM model captures physical effects and applies it with low computational demand. It
consists of three components as explained below and in ﬁgure 2.2 below.
Figure 2.2: Components of the DWM model, [10]
• Axisymmetric wake deﬁcit - The wake deﬁcit is initialised by the pressure gradient
at the upwind turbine rotor. It is assumed to be steady and symmetrical about the
rotor axis. It is calculated based on the Navier-Stokes equations. The ambient
turbulence is included through a modiﬁcation to the eddy viscosity component.

2.2
Wind Turbine Control
As the industry gained a better understanding of losses due to wake effects, turbine control
strategies and optimization have progressed from being focused on the individual turbine
to a more plant wide focus as the beneﬁts have become more apparent.
Studies have shown that the turbine control strategy can be used to mitigate wake ef-
fects and lower loads on downwind turbines [31] as well as aid fatigue load reduction of
the turbine being controlled (or upwind turbine), [32].
Modern wind turbine control is of three types: Yaw control, blade pitch control and
generator torque control [33]. Yaw control is used to steer the wake of upwind turbines
away from downwind turbines and thereby reduce loads from wake turbulence by in-
troducing a yaw misalignment ([34], [35], [36]). Generator torque control monitors the
tip-speed ratio while adjusting generator reaction torque (or the electromagnetic torque)
keeping the blade pitch constant. This control strategy is active when the turbine operates
below rated wind speed and wind speed when the turbine reaches maximum allowable
rotor speed. Post rated wind speed, the generator torque is kept constant by blade pitch
control, [37].
Blade pitch control can also be used to down-regulate the turbine i.e. to work under
sub-optimal operational setpoints. Down-regulation is usually done to control power out-
put to fulﬁl grid code obligations such as a load shedding notice from the grid operator.
The plant operator may also de-rate or down-regulate turbines to adjust market participa-
tion when electricity prices are negative whilst keeping the turbines running and thereby
avoiding an unnecessary shutdown. The advantages of down-regulation are seen as re-
duced thrust on the downregulated turbine which could mean an increased total power
production and also lower wake turbulence intensity implying lower fatigue loading of
the downstream turbine, [38]. This was proved through a ﬁeld experiment at the Alpha
Ventus windfarm where the upwind turbine was 50% downregulated to study the impact
on the normally operating downwind turbine. It was seen that the ’free streaming’ turbine
experienced a load reduction of upto 40% whereas the downwind turbine experienced
upto 30% load reduction [39].
There are different levels of turbine downregulation that can be applied and also a few dif-

2.3
Optimization Theory applied to Wind Energy
The wind energy industry draws concepts and methods from various other industries and
disciplines. Therefore windfarm design and control problems prove to be quite complex
and dependent on various factors and constraints. There is a need for optimisation con-
sidering different variables and objectives [42].
A narrow viewed approach to optimisation would result in impractical concepts, some
satirically represented in ﬁgure 2.4 below.
Figure 2.4: Wind turbine design concepts from single discipline focused optimisation,
[12]
Optimisation theory consists of many categorisations and many different algorithms
exists for solving optimisation problems in each category. Some broad optimisation prob-
lem classiﬁcations are linear vs. non-linear problems, gradient based vs. non-gradient
based optimisation, constrained vs. unconstrained problems, single objective vs. multi-
objective optimisation. Whenever gradients exists and are known, gradient based solvers
such as Sequential Least Squares Programmming (SLSQP) enable faster solution conver-
gence. When gradients are unknown, solvers such as the Constrained Optimisation BY
Linear Approximation (COBYLA) method are able to better search the design space.
There are different kinds of constraints in optimisation problems in wind energy. In an
offshore windfarm layout optimisation problem, they can be the windfarm boundary con-
strained by environmental or shipping restrictions or seabed conditions. In the Topfarm
==PAGE:29==
2.3. Optimization Theory applied to Wind Energy

example problem adapted in chapter 4, layout optimisation is done with the objective of
maximising AEP while minimising wake losses. The turbine positions have a minimum
spacing constraint and are allowed to spread out within the windfarm boundary to reduce
wake losses. In a 2013 study, Topfarm was used to optimise the layout of a windfarm
with the objective of maximising lifetime proﬁt with respect to AEP, electrical grid costs,
foundation costs and fatigue degradation of turbine components from wake induced tur-

Wind turbine constraints can be the power rating, power curve limits, hub height,
tower clearance of the blade, rotational speeds, tip height restrictions, noise restrictions,
fatigue load limits, electrical operational constraints such as reactive power limits, fault
ride through limits, etc. An interesting recent study [44] used a gradient based approach
to optimise windfarm design with turbines of different hub heights. Similar to Topfarm,
the open source model FLOw Redirection and Induction in Steady-State (FLORIS) was
used.
Optimisation could also be done with constraints on ﬁnancial parameters such as in-
ternal rate of return (IRR), LCOE, NPV to ensure the economic viability of a windfarm
project. Much of the optimisation studies within wind energy have been focused on AEP
and ﬁnancial parameters. Optmisation within wind energy is a relatively new topic and
research output from before the year 2000 was focused on optimising power and AEP.
The 2000s saw a shift towards economic output related studies, increased use of heuris-
tics, advanced wake models [45]. Surrogate based optimisation, such as employed in this
thesis, is a relatively new topic [46]. Windfarm fatigue loads and power production can be
calculated based on the operational conditions of the surrogate, namely at various power
set points, turbine position and wind inﬂow.
The optimisation problem in chapter 5 of this thesis is tackled as a non-linear, con-
strained, multi-disciplinary problem using the gradient free COBYLA solver. A detailed
note on COBYLA is provided in chapter 5.

• Power Set Point (PSP): The power output of the turbine is capped by the PSP
choice. Normal operation (ﬁgure 5.1) corresponds to PSP = 1. Higher PSP val-
ues mean that the turbine is in Power Boost mode and lower PSP values represent
Derated operation. PSP levels vary from 0.4 to 1.11. It is the design variable in
this optimisation study and the resulting optimised turbine control strategy implies
a strategy on the choice of PSP.
• Turbulence Intensity (TI): TI is the ratio of the standard deviation of wind speed
in a time interval to the mean wind speed within that interval.
• Mean wind speed, U (m/s): The mean wind speed at hub height in a time interval
• Wind shear, α: It is the change in wind speed with change in height. A high wind
shear implies that the upper tip section of the wind turbine rotor experiences higher
wind speeds as compared to those at the bottom tip of the rotor. Wind shear is
assumed as a constant of 0.2, in accordance with the IEC 61400-1: 2019 standard
(Wind energy systems - Part 1: Design requirements) and also as applied in [51].

40

• Air density, ρ (kg/m3): Assumed as a constant of 1.225 kg/m3 at hub height [51].
Since wind shear and air density are assumed as constants and since TI is a function
of wind speed, the surrogate model outputs are primarily dependent on PSP and wind
speed. Wind speed is read from site speciﬁc data detailed in chapter 3 of this report. Thus
PSP is the deciding factor and it’s impact on the surrogate power and DEL models are
investigated below.

Note on COBYLA
The Constrained Optimisation by Linear Approximation (COBYLA) algorithm was cho-
sen for this optimisation problem as it can handle non-linear constraints [52]. COBYLA
was formulated by M.J.D. Powell in 1994, building on the Nelder-Mead algorithm [53].
Within the optimisation space, COBYLA forms a trust region which is a polytope or sim-
plex of N+1 vertices where N is the number of dimensions of the design variable vector.
In this optimisation exercise, the design variable is PSP and it is passed as a vector of
size: 5 wind speed bins x 3 electricity price bins. Thus the simplex has 15+1 vertices.
COBYLA evaluates the objective function and constraint function at each of these ver-
tices and then establishes a linear interpolation. The algorithm carries out this evaluation
iteratively by adjusting the simplex shape and size. The user deﬁnes the initial value of
the simplex vertices, the initial step size and the lower bound of the trust region. Random
initialisation was chosen to better search the optimisation space. A set of random initial
values were generated from a uniform distribution between PSP=0.4 and PSP=1.11. The
initial step size (rhobeg) was chosen as 0.1 as explained later in this section. The trust re-
gion lower bound was chosen as 0.001 (tol) which means that the optimisation is deemed
to have converged when the PSP values don’t change by more than 0.1%. Additionally,
a constraint tolerance (catol) of 0.01 was set to render constraint evaluations greater than
0.01 as a constraint violation.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
Hourly wind data from 3 windfarm sites and corresponding electricity market price data
were time synchronised and analysed in chapter 3. The data was binned to form a fre-
quency distribution representative of the sites. A 2D frequency distribution was obtained
by binning into wind speed and price bins. Similarly, a 3D frequency distribution was ob-
tained by considering wind direction bins as well. These frequency distributions formed
the ’ﬂow cases’ for use later in this project.
Chapter 4 dealt with layout optimisation of a 4 turbine windfarm in DTU’s optimisa-
tion software Topfarm. The optimisation setup was tested with different initial positions
and wind distribution types to better understand the impact on the results.
These techniques were later applied for investigation of results from the surrogate-
based optimisation of turbine control done in chapter 5. The power and load surrogate
models prepared by Andreas Thorsen in his MSc thesis [51] were used in this optimisation
exercise. Site-speciﬁc wind data analysed in chapter 3 was used as inputs to the surrogate
models. The objective of the optimisation study was to maximise revenue by varying
turbine Power Set Point (PSP) between 0.4 and 1.11 subject to a constraint on the design
Damage Equivalent Load (DEL). This resulted in a revenue improvement, associated
optimised PSP choices for each wind speed-price bin and an impact on the fatigue life
consumption i.e. the remaining design DEL as shown in ﬁgure 5.18. Under constrained
optimisation, a maximum of 3.031% revenue improvement is achieved on average across
all three sites. This results in a PSP strategy as shown in ﬁgure 5.18. For positive prices,
the turbine power boosts to its limit (PSP=1.11) in the (12,16] m/s wind speed bin, is
close to normal operation in (16,20] m/s bin and derates in the (20,24] m/s bin. There
is some spread in the results for the wind speed bins (4,8] m/s and (8,12] m/s due to
lower variation of surrogate operation with PSP change. At negative prices, the very
low bin frequency renders insigniﬁcance in the results and hence there is a large results
spread here as well. The corresponding fatigue life consumption shows that the Edgewise

It was seen that in unconstrained optimisation i.e. when only revenue maximisation is

Further work
• Uncertainties from the input data and surrogate model were not taken into consider-
ation. Future work could assess optimisation results under quantiﬁed uncertainties.
• The results from chapter 5 could be investigated under conditions of higher fre-
quency of negative electricity prices.
• The optimisation exercise could be repeated considering two turbines operating in
tandem. The results under waked and non-waked conditions could be investigated.
It would also be interesting to see the overall fatigue life of the windfarm and the
differences in impact on the upwind and downwind turbines.
• The individual turbine control optimisation should be applied in Topfarm in dif-
ferent use cases. The layout optimisation exercise from chapter 4 could have the
turbine PSP strategy as an added variable. This could be further extended to a row
of turbines and then on a windfarm level. The impact of different wind distributions
such as in chapter 4 could be investigated.
• Currently the design life is almost entirely used to achieve the optimal revenue.
The optimisation exercise could be repeated after applying a ’leeway’ or a factor of
safety on fatigue life
