ABSTRACT 
Microbial fuel cells (MFCs) can generate electricity through the oxidation of reduced 
compounds such as sulfide and therefore have the potential to be used as a sulfide remediation 
tool. This thesis focuses on the potential application of MFCs for sulfide oxidation in marine 
sediments and water. Samples from two different locations were tested: the Black Sea, 
Bulgaria, and Roskilde Fjord, Denmark. Fuel cells were used to test the resistances and 
spontaneous oxidation of sulfide. Three different electrodes were also tested namely stainless 
steel, carbon brush, and Wolfram coated electrodes. The highest current and power density 
achieved with stainless steel was 2102 mW m-2 and for carbon, brush was estimated at 700 
mW m-2. Wolfram-coated electrodes were considered unsuitable for MFCs application in 
marine waters due to corrosion concerns. Black Sea reactors produced inconclusive results on 
sulfide oxidation. Open circuit potential and closed-circuit were reversed halfway through the 
Roskilde Fjord experiment to observe the rate of sulfide removal. The MFCs significantly 
increased oxidation-reduction potential in the sediment and anoxic water while reducing sulfide 
concentration and preventing sulfide accumulation. In conclusion, this thesis has demonstrated 
that MFCs can be used to remove sulfide from sediment and sulfide-rich waters, while also 
generating electricity.

INTRODUCTION  
Hydrogen sulfide (H2S) is a poisonous, corrosive, and flammable gas with a distinct foul-odour 
(Abdollahi & Hosseini, 2014; Bai & Bai, 2019). It occurs in nature due to volcanic and bacterial 
actions (Greenwood & Earnshaw, 1997) and can be found widely throughout the aquatic 
environment (Cutter et al., 1999). During organic matter degradation in the anoxic (oxygen 
depleted) seabed, sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB) link the geochemical cycles of carbon and 
sulfur by utilizing dissolved sulfate (SO42-) as a terminal electron acceptor resulting in the 
production of H2S and CO2 (Yao, et al., 2018; Muyzer & Stams, 2008). Accumulation of H2S 
can be observed in fjords, estuaries, and marginal seas where stratification is present. However, 
fluxes of H2S to the water column are small as sulfide is essentially oxidized within the oxic 
zone back to sulfate or elemental sulfur (Brüchert, et al., 2003). If hypoxic (low oxygen < 2 
mg/L O2) conditions are present and continue, increasing hydrogen sulfide concentration can 
be highly toxic to aquatic life even at small concentrations - 96h-LC50 of 19.8 µg/L for bluegill 
fish (Smith, et al., 1976). Typically, hydrogen sulfide production is common for marine water 
due to the abundance of sulfate compared to freshwater. In seawater different speciation of H2S 
can be found including, undissociated sulfide H2S, free sulfide (HS-, S2-), and metal sulfide 
complexes and compounds (Siang, et al., 2017; Letelier-Gordo, et al., 2020).  
Although naturally occurring in the ocean and sea, hypoxic or anoxic conditions have also 
become more commonly observed due to anthropogenic activities. (Conley, et al., 2007; Levin, 
et al., 2009) Runoffs from wastewaters, agricultural and industrial processes contribute to high 
loads of nutrients and organic matter; consequently, leading to algal proliferation and 
eutrophication (Bonaglia, et al., 2019; Glibert & Burkholder, 2006). In addition, climate 
warming is another driving factor which further leads to deoxygenation and stratification 
(caused by surface layers warming faster relative to deeper layers) of the oceans  (Limburg, et 
al., 2020; Shepherd, et al., 2017). As a result, hydrogen sulfide production increase has been 
observed in many coastal waters around the world – such as those of Denmark (Conley, et al., 
2007), Japan (Moqsud, 2020), Northern Gulf of Mexico (Rabalais & Turner, 2019), Peru 
(Schunck, et al., 2013), and recently detected in the Marmara Sea, Turkey (News2Sea, 2021). 
Hydrogen sulfide production has also been identified as a problem in recirculating aquaculture 
systems (Letelier-Gordo, et al., 2020). H2S is also formed during anaerobic digestion for biogas 
production and upon combustion is converted to environmentally hazardous sulfur dioxide 
(Dykstra & Pavlostathis, 2021).

Unless removed, sulfide can be poisonous to oxygen-respiring cells if it diffuses in the oxic 
waters (Daghioa, et al., 2018). Remediation techniques are urgently required to reduce the 
increasing dead zones in marine waters. Physicochemical methods based on chemical oxidation 
and catalytic conversion have been implemented in wastewater for S2- removal where 
precipitate is formed, for example ZnS. However, such methods can be expensive and 
environmentally incompatible (Caliari, et al., 2017). Several studies have investigated the 
potential of microbial fuel cells (MFCs) to stimulate sulfide oxidation (Algar, et al., 2020; 
Zhong, et al., 2017; Rabaey, et al., 2006). 
1.1 Microbial fuel cells  
MFCs are bio-electrochemical systems (BES), or primary microbial electrochemical 
technologies (METs), which utilise electrochemical interactions of microorganisms and 
electron conductors (f.e. physical electrodes made of metals or carbon) (Schröder, et al., 2015). 
MFC (Figure 1) comprises an aerobic cathode and an anaerobic anode chamber, typically 
separated by an ion/proton selective exchange membrane (IEM/PEM).  In the anode chamber, 
exoelectrogenic microbes, accumulated as a biofilm, carry out degradation (oxidation reaction 
– half cell) of organic matter by producing protons and transferring electrons to an electron 
acceptor outside their cells. Carbon dioxide is produced as an oxidation product in the anode 
chamber (Du, et al., 2007). In the presence of electrodes, the electrons can travel from the anode 
to the cathode, directly or in the presence of mediators (electron shuttles), through an external 
load, resulting in the production of an electrical current. Mediators can be both chemical, which 
can be toxic, and self-produced by the microorganisms such as pyocyanin produced by 
Pseudomonas aeruginosa (Roy & Soumya, 2019). Simultaneously, the protons diffuse through 
the PEM to the cathode where they reduce oxygen to water with the arriving electrons. (Logan, 
2007; Pandit & Das, 2018; Roy & Soumya, 2019)

1.2 Sediment MFCs  
While in an MFC the anode electrode is placed in a liquid anolyte solution, in a SMFC (Figure 
2, left) the anode anolyte is the sediment (seafloor) and the electrode is embedded in it while 
the cathode electrode is placed in oxygen-rich water (surface water layer). Oxygen typically is 
the most thermodynamically favourable electron acceptor however, the supply is limited in the 
sediment due to diffusive transport; therefore, alternative electron acceptors are used up 
according to available energy (Figure 2, right). Thus, utilizing the naturally occurring potential 
difference between the anoxic sediment and oxic surface seawater. By connecting the 
electrodes in the oxygenate water (cathode) and reduced layer of the sediments (anode), oxygen 
can be used as the electron acceptor bypassing the diffusive transport limitation and 
consequently generating a current. Similarly, as in an MFC, microbes facilitate most of the 
processes of oxidation and reduction (Tamboli & Eswari, 2019; Roy & Soumya, 2019; Algar, 
et al., 2020; Jørgensen & Kasten, 2006).

In nature, terminal acceptors (Figure 2,right) such as metals ions, sulfate, and nitrate are utilized 
by electroactive bacteria, which occur naturally in marine sediments, for anaerobic respiration, 
while oxygen is utilized for aerobic respiration. The most electropositive electron acceptors are 
used first in the following order: nitrate (NO3-) manganese (Mn4+), iron (Fe3+), sulfate (SO42-) 
and lastly CO2 (Daghioa, et al., 2018). In the MFC, the reaction is split. The anode is the 
electron acceptor when the exoelectrogenic bacteria oxidize the organic substrate but the 
dissolved oxygen in the cathode is the terminal electron acceptor and is continuously reduced 
by the arriving electrons and thus, maintaining the potential for electricity generation. Overall, 
the electricity generation process is thermodynamically favourable. The theoretical potential

1.4 Sulfate reduction and sulfide oxidation

mM at an ocean salinity of 35) and thus act as an electron acceptor for the respiration of sulfate

organic material). In the upper sediment layers, the sulfate reduction rates are generally high 
without a significant depletion in concentration due to fast reoxidation of sulfide and rapid 
supply of sulfate from the seawater through bio-irrigation (Jørgensen, et al., 2019). In Figure

Sulfide can be oxidized to over 30 different species of sulfur compounds due to its oxidation 
state which can vary between -2 to +6 and the environment’s redox conditions. The most 
common oxidation products include elemental sulfur and soluble oxyanions such as sulfite, 
sulfate and thiosulfate, and polysulfides (Figure 3) (Brock & Trapp, 2019).

The sulfur cycle in marine sediment can be seen in Figure 4. In the long-term, elemental sulfur 
could further undergo several reactions such as a reverse reaction back to sulfide, iron sulfide 
(FeS) and consequently immobilised in the sediment as pyrite (FeS2), or further oxidized to 
sulfate (Algar, et al., 2020).  Pyrite is an iron-sulfide mineral and along with reactions 
producing organic sulfur, pyrite formations provide a deep sink for sulfur. Depending on the 
redox potential and the conditions present, other species can be formed such as polysulfides 
when sulfides and elemental sulfur are both present, thiosulfate when in the presence of heavy 
metals and light exposure, and sulfite at higher redox potentials (Keller-Lehmann, et al., 2006; 
Rabaey, et al., 2006; Jørgensen, et al., 2019)

1.5 Objectives 
This MSc thesis investigates the applicability of MFCs/SMFCs for remediation of hydrogen 
sulfide in affected marine environment such as dead zones . The main objectives of this study 
are: 1) to develop a method for analysis of oxidized sulfide products 2) carry out laboratory-
scale experiments to test the performance of electrodes made of different materials 3) evaluate 
and compare the electrodes and their resilience towards environmental impacts, 4) evaluate and 
compare MFCs performance for hydrogen sulfide removal, and 5) compare the performance 
of fuel cell (chemical oxidation) and MFC (bio-oxidation). The hypothesis of this study is that 
in operating MFC reactors sulfide oxidation of hydrogen sulfide will be observed and thus, 
lower concentrations compared to control reactors. In addition, it is hypothesised that MFC 
will have a faster oxidation rate of sulfide compared to fuel cells.

2.2 Scoping experiments 
Batch experiments were conducted during the method development at different resistances to 
measure the rates of spontaneous sulfide oxidation, analyse the products, estimate the columbic 
efficiency, and develop a quantification method for chemical analysis. The external resistances 
and sulfide concentrations have been summarised in Table 2 and the duration in Table 1. 
 
The initial scoping experiment included duplicates with resistances of 100 Ω, 200 Ω, and OCP 
acting as control, a total of 6 reactors. The rest of the experiments during this part of the study 
were conducted with only one OCP due to consistent leakage of anolyte from the anode 
chamber in one of the reactors. Cathode and anode solutions were prepared before each 
experiment. The cathode solution consisted of a buffer prepared in a 10 L of 18MΩ water with 
5 g of Na2HPO4∙2 H2O, 5 g KH2PO4, 10 g NaCl (kitchen salt; Salina) per litre. The kitchen salt 
was later substituted with sodium chloride (NaCl; ACS Reagent Grade, Honeywell Fluka) due 
to iodine presence and interferences with the Ion Chromatogram (IC). The anode sulfidic 
solution was prepared in a fume hood by washing sodium sulphide nonahydrate (Na2S.9H2O, 
99.99%, Sigma-Aldrich, DK) with deoxygenated water under vacuum suction to remove any 
contaminants and oxidized products (Keller-Lehmann, et al., 2006). This however was not 
performed for all experiments to observe if there was a difference in ion products. Half of the 
buffer solution (5 L) was sparged for an hour with nitrogen gas to achieve anaerobic conditions 
until the dissolved oxygen was measured to be below 0.1 mg L−1. The solution was spiked with 
the sodium sulfide crystals, and the anode chambers were carefully filled, while intentionally 
overfilling them to ensure no oxygen was present in the system and to remove any solution that 
might have been oxygenated. Rubber stoppers and Vaseline were used to prevent oxygen 
intrusion in the systems. Lastly, after allowing them to settle for an hour the reactors were 
connected to a data logger (ADC-24 Data Acquisition system, Pico Tech) and treated using 
PicoLog 6 software.

2.3 Experiments with SMFC/MFC  
Two different locations were chosen for this part of the project, Roskilde Fjord, Denmark and 
Black Sea, Bulgaria. The main reason was the different salinity concentrations of the waters 
and the potential effect it might have on the chosen electrodes, as well as the difference in 
organic matter content in the sediment and sulfate concentration. Once assembled and filled, 
the reactors were placed on an electrically non-conductive surface and clear film and plastic 
bags were placed over the magnetic stirrer when used. All reactors were left in an open circuit 
for a day (Roskilde)/ two days (Black Sea) to ensure all oxygen had been consumed before the 
reactors were connected.  During sampling, only Roskilde anode chambers were refilled with 
deoxygenated distilled water (sparged with nitrogen gas for 25min or until ≥0.02 O2 mg/L) 
since a suspicion that hydrogen sulfide escapes to the headspace were raised after Black Sea 
experiment. 
2.3.1 Black Sea 
The Black Sea is considered to be the world’s largest waterbody containing H2S with the sulfide 
layer beginning at around 200 m below the surface (app 2200m maximum depth) and 22 g/L 
of aqueous sulfide at depths of 2200 m (Beschkov, et al., 2021). The anoxic conditions have 
existed for approximately 7,300 years and the H2S rate of production is estimated at 10 000 
tons per day (Demirbas, 2009) 
Water and sediment samples were collected on the 10.04.2021 from the North Bulgarian Black 
Sea region (43°24'18.8"N, 28°14'32.2"E) and were kept at a 4°C room until 05.05.2021.  A 
total of 16 MFC of similar configuration were employed in the experiment with external 
resistances starting with 1500 Ω and later replaced with 680 Ω, and 100 Ω. This was done as 
some microbial community can take longer to colonize the electrode (Logan, 2007).A table 
with the duration for which the external resistances were applied and the type of electrodes 
used has been summarised in Table 1. The controls and OCP were in duplicates and CC in 
triplicates. The electrodes were placed in the water without having contact with the sediment 
which was approximately 170-200 g in each reactor Figure 6.

2.3.2 Roskilde 
The Roskilde Fjord is a shallow estuary in Denmark with length of 30 km and average depth 
of 3 m and basins of  >15m. Hypoxic condition are observed especially in the warm seasons 
and in the deeper basins due to nutrient loading during the 1970-80s, which resulted in poor 
water conditions (Jane M Caffrey, 2018).   
Water and sediment samples were collected on the 10.03.2021 (55.7496215°N, 12.1007702°E) 
from Roskilde and were kept in the laboratory at room temperature (18-24°C). A total of 17 
SMFC of similar configuration were employed in the experiment with external resistances of 
1500 Ω. The resistance was not changed for the duration of the experiment.  The controls, OCP, 
and CC were in triplicates and two reactors with different electrodes (carbon brush and stainless 
steel) had magnets. Half the volume of the anode chamber was sediment approximately 300

2.4.1 Ion Chromatography  
The samples were analysed for sulfur species namely sulfide (S2-), sulfite (SO32-), sulfate 
(SO42), and thiosulfate (S2O32-). The analysis was done with Thermo Scientific Dionex™ ICS-
5000+ with a Dionex ASRS 300 4 mm (Thermo Scientific) suppressor operating at 92 mA and 
a conductivity detector ICS-5000 (Thermo Scientific) held at 30 °. The guard column used was 
IonPac AS25, 4 x 250 mm, IonPac AS25, 4 x 50 mm (Thermo Scientific) and 37 mM KOH 
was used as eluent. With 25 µl injected sample, the flow was kept constant at 1 ml/min, at

absorbances were set at 230 and 210nm. 
 
2.4.2 Inductive coupled plasma- optical emission spectroscopy/ mass spectroscopy  
Water samples from cathode chambers for wolfram (black sea) and stainless steel (Roskilde) 
electrodes were also analysed to investigate any potential metal corrosion. Metals and sulfur 
contents for both water and sediment were determined by Perkin Elmer Avio 200 ICP-OES. 
Processing of the data was carried out in the SyngisticTM for ICP Software v. 2.0 from Perkin 
Elmer. Before the measurement, the sea water samples for Roskilde were diluted 10x and Black 
Sea 10x, in 2% suprapur HNO3 to acidify them and lower the concentration of suspended 
solids. Samples were digested in an Anton-Paar Multivave 3000 microwave using USEPA 
3051A method with HNO3 65% (9 ml) and HCl 30% (3 ml) all suprapur grade. Before the 
analysis the digested sediment samples were diluted 5x MQ water.  
2.4.3 Scanning electron microscope  
The microscopic images of the electrodes were made using scanning electron microscope 
(SEM) QFEG 200 Cryo FEI Quanta FEG (Field Emission Gun) with a spatial resolution of 2 
nm for the ETD detector in high vacuum at 30 keV. The apparatus was equipped with Energy 
Dispersive Spectrometry X-ray (EDS) Oxford Instruments 80 mm2 X-Max silicon drift 
detector, MnKα resolution at 124 eV) allowing for detection of elements. The SEM was able 
to operate at high vacuum (10-4 Pa), low vacuum and environmental mode (up to 2700 Pa) as 
well as room, low, and cryo temperature. SEM images were taken to examine any inorganic 
deposition (mainly sulfur) on the surface of the electrodes.   
2.4.4 Colorimetric analysis 
Hack LCK 653 Sulfide cuvette test 0.1 - 2.0 mg / L S²⁻ were used as a fast and simple method 
to estimate the sulfide content present in each anode chamber. The method procedure was 
followed as described by the manufacturer and analysed with Hach DR3900 
Spectrophotometer.

2.4.6 Physical, Chemical and Electrochemical analysis 
The pH, DO and EC were regularly measured throughout all experiments using WTW Multi 
3510 IDS and WTW Multi 3410 meter. The pH was measured using WTW SenTix 41 and 
WTW SenTix® 940-P pH probes.  DO concentrations were measured using a WTW FDO 925

reference electrode (unused, stored in electrolyte 3 mol/L KCl, Mettler Toledo) before each 
daily measurement. 
2.4.7 Sample preservation and tracer  
Sample preservation was done with sulfide anti-oxidizing buffer (SAOB) which allows 
samples to be stored for at least four days at 4°C with minimal deterioration of the sulfur species 
while minimizing oxidation and volatilization of hydrogen sulfide. The buffer was made new 
for each experiment and not used past 4 days, after which orange/pink colour occurred. The 
procedure was based on Keller-Lehmann, et al. (2006). A volumetric flask with 250 mL 
distilled was placed over a magnetic stirrer and was sparged with nitrogen gas to create an 
anaerobic environment (≥ 0.02 O2 mg/L) to which 0.8 g NaOH and 0.7 g L-ascorbic acid were 
added. The solution was stored in plastic vials at 4°C. In order to ensure no oxygenation of the 
SAOB, a vial was used only when first opened. Samples were fixed by adding 0.5 mL SAOB 
to each vial (2 mL) along with round glass beads to ensure mixing. Only samples from the 
method development part were preserved using SAOB whereas, samples from the Black Sea 
and Roskilde Fjord were not preserved due to the formation of precipitate and potential issues 
with the IC equipment even after filtration. Water samples were filtered through 0.2µm Agilent 
Nylon filters. For Roskilde samples, a bromide tracer was added to identify peaks correctly.

4 DISCUSSION 
In this project it has been demonstrated that hydrogen sulfide can be oxidized using MFC while 
simultaneously generating electricity using different electrode materials. The project mainly 
targets sulfide removal and the potential of MFC for dead zone remediation in coastal regions. 
4.1 Interpretation of results 
4.1.1 Scoping experiments 
During the scoping experiments different resistances were tested to estimate the rate of the 
spontaneous sulfide oxidation and the production of sulfur species were also analysed. The 
highest working potential was achieved with resistances of 100 Ω which also gave the highest 
Faradaic efficiency. The working potential varied across resistances in an unpredicted manner 
where the highest potentials were measured at 100 and 980 Ω with not very significant 
difference in sulfide concentrations. Extremely low potential was measured at 10, 44 and 220 
Ω. In addition, although expected, the removal rate did not increase with lowering the external 
resistance (Dutta, et al., 2008). In fact, the rate varied not only across treatments but also across 
controls. For example, during experiment 2 presented in Table 4, the controls had a higher rate 
of change compared to treatments. For experiment 1, the highest rate of change was observed 
with resistance of 220 Ω compared to that of 100 Ω.  In addition, the differences in rate of 
change and faradaic efficiency showed no correlation to the resistance used. Αn issue that was 
encountered during the scoping experiment was the O2 intrusion and inability to keep the anode 
anolyte completely anoxic, also visible from Table 8. Although original feed was anoxic the 
conditions were not maintained. This indicates that oxidation of sulfide also occurred in the 
anode.The  pH changes in the cathode, namely increase, indicate removal of H+ which is used 
for the reduction of O2 by the arriving electrons from the anode. Further, the DO is expected to 
drop due to the formation of water in the cathode. This however was not clearly observed in 
any of the reactors but one, experiment 3 reactor 2. Although there was no clear indication of 
sulfur species production based on the IC analysis, sulfur deposits were found on the carbon 
brush from the anode during SEM analysis and as the sulfide concentration decreased the cell 
potential also decreased (Figure 10-Figure 13). Thus, affirming that spontaneous electrochemical 
oxidation does occur, however, the resistances effect expected was not observed which 
indicates a possible certain lower cut-off of useable resistance.  
4.1.2 Black Sea 
The experiment with the Black Sea sediment did not give conclusive results due to no or very 
minimal production of hydrogen sulfide. This could mainly be attributed to very little organic 
==PAGE:40==

matter in the 200g of sediment. The total sulfur content in the sediment was not as high when 
compared to that of the sediments from Roskilde and was not as high as initially expected. At 
the very beginning of the experiment, the low cell potential was attributed to the high external 
load (1500 Ω) and hence over time the resistance was reduced to 680 Ω followed by 100 Ω. A 
very slight increase was observed only for brush electrodes but nothing of great significance. 
With reference to the results of the scoping experiment, where the cell potential dropped to 0 
upon complete removal of sulfide; the production of even a small voltage in the steel and brush 
reactors indicate a possibility of sulfide production in the sediment followed by oxidation on 
the electrode surface. The highest potential for the reactors with the brushes was 5.10 mV (100 
Ω) whereas for the steel 4.8 mV (680 Ω). In contrast, reactors with 220 Ω from the scoping 
experiment with a known sulfide concentration did not go over 2.5 mV. The chromatogram 
from the IC (Figure 20) does show thiosulfate peak, an oxidation product, and hence indicates 
sulfide oxidation.  
Although the pH increased overtime in CC reactors the same was observed in the control and 
OCP reactors. The inconclusive DO results also do not indicated rapid oxygen depletion. 
However, DO decrease was also not observed in the scoping experiment however, sulfide 
oxidation was present.  Hence, the oxygen diffusion rate could be faster than the oxygen 
reduction at the cathode.  
The higher cell potential produced by the reactors with the wolfram electrodes is attributed to 
corrosion due to presence of oxygen in the cathode and possible galvanic corrosion as the anode 
electrode was bigger than that of the cathode. The anode electrode did not show any signs of 
corrosion hence the water salinity was not considered to have had a great impact for the 
duration of the experiment. However, the effect of high sulfide concentrations on the wolfram 
electrode is unknown.  Further, the precipitate in the cathode from the corroding electrode was 
initially distributed in the whole cathode anolyte and eventually settled towards the bottom. 
The potential decreased overtime which suggests most of the cobalt in the electrode had 
reacted. This is also supported by the increase in oxygen over time in the cathode. The EC for 
both OCP and CC increased slightly overtime which can predominantly be attributed to 
evaporation. 
Lastly, the oxygen concentration was rather low (mostly bellow 0.5 mg/L) in the anode 
compartments which indicates that present microorganisms had consumed, it further 
suggesting that sulfide was indeed produced though in minimal concentrations.

4.1.3 Roskilde Fjord 
The experiment with the Roskilde Fjord sediment and water clearly demonstrated that the use 
of MFC can prevent the production and accumulation of sulfide in the water and can be used 
to remediate polluted waters. In the first part of the experiment the reactors with connected 
electrodes contained significantly less sulfide, less than 2 mg/L after 3 weeks. The controls and 
OCPs on the other hand had over 10 to up to 27mg/L for the same period. Reversing the OCP, 
and CC also led to a drop in the previous OCP reactors and high increase of sulfide 
concentrations in the previous CC reactors. In addition, the chromatograms from the IC where 
thiosulfate was observed further indicate that sulfide was oxidized. This is also supported by 
sulfur deposits on the electrodes measured with SEM/EDS (Figure 33, g) and sulfur 
precipitation in anode observed in Figure 35. However, thiosulfate and sulfur deposits were 
also found in controls and OCP. The ICP-OES results indicate 6-9 g of iron present, among 
other metals, in the sediment. Therefore, the presence of sulfur species in the controls and OCPs 
are an indication of possible iron reduction which is illustrated in Figure 4 and formation of 
iron-sulfide minerals. This is also supported by the occurrence of black colour in the anode 
chambers of all reactors. However, this needs to be further investigated as sediments were not 
tested from each reactor individually.  
Moreover, the sulfide oxidation is also supported by the parameters measured in the cathode. 
The pH was higher for CC reactors suggesting consumption of H+ and low in OCP. Once 
reversed, the same was observed.  The final measurements in the anode chamber also showed 
that pH was lower for reactors which were connected compared to that of which were not 
(Table 10). This indicates a release of H+ in the anode (Algar, et al., 2020). The DO 
consumption in the carbon brush CC followed by OCP reversed, further indicates oxidation 
reaction as oxygen is reduced in the cathode by arriving electrons, which can be attributed to 
sulfide oxidation. The DO in the reactors with the steel electrodes is much higher than that of 
those with brushes. However, as the electricity generation in the brushes is higher, so is the 
electron flow which will lead to faster reduction of oxygen. Nevertheless, it is worth 
mentioning that the DO of the brush OCP is unexpectedly low.   
Generally, the unconnected reactors produced large amounts of sulfide with highest 
concentration measured at 65 mg/L. The rate of change for sulfide was most prominent for 
carbon brushes where the removal rate was faster compared to that of steel. However, as seen 
from the power and current density curves, normalised by the electrodes surfaces areas, the 
steel outperformed the carbon brushes. The faster rate hence was likely due to the bigger 
==PAGE:42==

surface area of the brushes. Nevertheless, it is also worth mentioning that the production of 
sulfide in the reactors with the steel mesh was much slower compared to that of the brushes. 
The reason for this could be attributed to the composition of the steel mesh and hence sulfide 
mineral formation.  It is noteworthy, to mention a decrease of sulfide concentration in the first 
and second control reactors. The reason for which is unknown. Over a period of a month the 
concentration from control 1 decreased from 20.4 mg/L to 11.8 mg while control 2 decreased 
from 33.8 mg/L to 28.0 mg/L. Meanwhile the third control reactor increased from 19.0 to 65.0 
mg/L over the same period. The reason for the decrease is unknown.  
Based on physical observations presented in Figure 35, both electrodes had issues withstanding 
either salinity (stainless steel) or hydrogen sulfide (brushes). The brush connection can 
however be easily fixed by removing the iron connectors and using a more suitable material.  
4.2 Comparison between the three stages 
It is difficult to draw conclusions when the sulfide rate of change is compared between the 
scoping (between -1.7 to -2.8 mg/L and Roskilde (-0.012 to 0.266 mg/L) experiments. The 
spontaneous oxidation reaction appears to be faster based on calculations however, the constant 
production of sulfide is not considered for the MFC in that calculation.  From Table 6 the 
production of sulfide is considered and after 1st week the oxidation is estimated between -4.5 
to -5.4 mg/L when compared to controls. First week was taken, as fuel cell experiments did not 
run longer than 5 days.  
In addition, the electricity generated from the MFC is much greater compared to that of the 
spontaneous sulfide oxidation which can be attributed again to the constant production of 
sulfide in the anode and microorganisms mediating the oxidation process. Moreover, the 
resistance used in the Roskilde experiment was the highest one applied throughout the whole 
study, 1500 Ω, and nevertheless despite expectations of low electricity generation at a high 
resistance, the potential steadily increased reaching a maximum of 0.496 V (brush electrode). 
Consequently, based on the analysed data the MFCs performed better than the fuel cells.  
 Due to the corrosion which occurred in all reactors which contained wolfram and 
environmental concerns, the electrode is unsuitable for MFC application in marine waters. The 
steel electrodes showed no corrosion in the black sea water with high salinity. However, 
corrosion was observed in the Roskilde reactors in the cathode compartment once the CC were 
disconnected. The corrosion did not occur while the reactors were connected. This was the only 
reactor where visible corrosion occured which led to a colour change.  Despite concerns of

sulfide driven corrosion in the anode chamber, such was not observed for the steel mesh 
electrodes and was only seen for the connector of the carbon brushes.  
Lastly, throughout this study, the H+ was identified as the limiting factor rather than the oxygen 
concentration in the cathode for all experiments. The study could not conclude weather 
electrodes performed better while submerged in the sediment or placed in the anoxic waters. 
Therefore, the optimal location of the electrode requires further investigation. 
4.3 Comparison with literature  
The spontaneous oxidation of sulfide has also been demonstrated by Dutta et al. (2008) on 
which the scoping experiments were primarily based on. As their sulfide concentration 
decreased similar observations were made for the cell voltage and current, which decreased 
overtime as sulfide was oxidized in the anode chamber. Similar results were also obtained by 
Brock & Trapp (2019) where similar reactor’s configuration and the same carbon brushes were 
used. Both studies, Dutta et al. (2008) and Brock & Trap (2019), suggest and indicate that the 
current production increased at lower external resistance. However, the present study observed 
very similar current production for all resistances used (from 10-980 Ω) and only at resistance 
of 100 and 980 Ω a much higher current was achieved. In addition, Dutta et al., (2008) also 
observed production of elemental sulfur, thiosulfate. However, during the scoping experiment 
no sulfur species or precipitation of elemental sulfur were detected or observed. Dutta et al. 
(2008) also notes a decrease of pH in the anode attributed to the release of protons, which was 
not consistent with this study’s results.  
Nevertheless, thiosulfate production was observed also in the OCP which was credited to the 
presence of oxygen in the anode and thus, acted as an electron acceptor. This could potentially 
explain the presence of thiosulfate in the OCP and controls in the Roskilde experiment as some 
oxygen could have been introduced during the refilling process. However, the Roskilde anolyte 
already contained sulfate and the reason for detection of sulfur species could be different. 
Spontaneous sulfide oxidation in saline environment close to the Black Sea with catalysts has 
been reported also by Beschkov et al., (2021) where the rate-determining step has been 
identified as the oxygen reduction. From the scoping experiment alone as mentioned before, 
this was not observed.  
Sulfide oxidation using MFC coupled with sulfate removal has been demonstrated by Rabaey 
et al., (2006) with inoculated aerobic sulfide oxidizing culture and granulated graphite as an 
electrode. The authors showed a clear electricity production and removal of sulfide from a 
==PAGE:44==

digester’s effluent where sulfide was removed greatly in a CC and minimal removal was 
observed in the OCP. Sulfur precipitate was also observed on the electrode surface.  
A closer set up to that of the Roskilde fjord reactors where half of the anode was filled with 
sediment is that of Algar et al., (2020). The authors used a SMFC (air-cathode) and sulfide 
production was observed at different depths overtime while lower sulfide concentrations were 
observed compared to controls. It is noteworthy to mention that although the SMFC has some 
influence on the sulfide oxidation and pH, there was no considerable effect on the DO in the 
upper water layer. Lower DO concentrations had been observed in functioning MFCs (CC) of 
during this master thesis, which raised concerns of potential effect on the aquatic life. However, 
with the sediment acting as a PEM and a fully exposed cathode to the air, allowing for higher 
oxygen diffusion, there appears to be no measurable effect according to Algar et al., (2020) 
results. In addition, natural effect of water flow and exchange would counteract that, but it 
might be a concern for stagnant waters.  
The authors however also observed no difference between the control and SMFC which is 
dissimilar with this study.  They also reported a decrease in pH with depth influenced by the 
production of protons and removal of alkalinity due to HS- consumption. Similar results were 
observed in the Roskilde reactors with lower pH in the anode water in CC reactors. Although 
not observed in their study, the authors warn of potential FeS dissolution with a decrease in 
pH. The dissolution of FeS could results in positive feedback due additional oxidation caused 
by the release of sulfide and Fe2+ is normally oxidized. In the reactors however, the iron cations 
are likely to be re-bonded with sulfide again upon re-filling of the anode due to a change in pH. 
This statement is based on the appearance of black precipitate around the headspace where 
water would normally be refilled. A possible explanation for the cause of the “smoked” anode 
chambers, which needs further investigation.   
Reimers et al. (2006) reported a power density of 34 mW m-2 with graphite anodes with area 
of 0.184 m2 at a cold seep using a benthic MFC. The surface area of cathode carbon brushes in 
the current was slightly higher, 0.23 m2, which led to production of higher power density up to 
700 mW m-2. However, although a higher power density was achieved the environmental 
conditions the benthic MFC were different which could further account for the difference. The 
study also reported biofilm layer formation at the anode and cathode, also observed in other 
studies (Brimblecombe, 2014), which was not clearly observed on any of the electrodes in the 
present study. The duration of this experiment however is much shorter than the quoted studies.

As the bio-electricity production is based on electron transfer from microbes via direct contact, 
nanowires or by electron shuttles such as flavins (Logan, et al., 2006; Schröder, 2007), biofilm 
formation is considered crucial for MFCs and is shown to increase the power density 
(Baranitharan, et al., 2015). However, as this study observed a higher power density than 
Reimers et al. (2006) which suggests electrochemical spontaneous oxidation of sulfide could 
potentially also have occurred along-side the bio electrochemical sulfide removal. 
4.4 Limitations and Outlook  
The small scale of the reactors resulted in headspace formation upon sampling, potentially 
allowing space for hydrogen sulfide volatilization and consequently underestimation of the 
sulfide content in the water. The issue was solved with immediate refilling which however, 
results in dilution and possible introduction the oxygen in the environment. A possible solution 
is upscaling to size that will not suffer from headspace formation and impact of sampling. 
During the Black Sea and scoping experiments reactors leakage occurred, often in the anode 
which has had an impact on the quality of some of the data during that stage. Meaning, missing 
data for certain days and reactors, while also having affect on the experiments if they were 
running for a longer term. During the Black Sea experiment, an issue was caused by the 
connector on the carbon brushes which would not register a voltage. This was solved for the 
Roskilde experiment by removing the connector.  
The DO probe which ideally is used in water with flow might have not always reported the 
correct value as the probe had to be moved around until the value shown did not change. This 
could be solved by use of oxygen micro-sensors and fiber optic oxygen meter placed in each 
anode that could allow for faster and more accurate measurements.  
The quantified amounts of produced species measured with IC has not been presented in this 
report. Presence of only clearly visible peaks was identifying only qualitatively thus more input 
is required for the clear understanding of the sulfur speciation that occurs during sulfide 
removal in an MFC. In addition, the sulfide test kits used during this thesis are temperature 
sensitive and with increased summer temperatures it could have resulted in inaccurate 
measurements of sulfide content. Polysulfides production was reported (Rabaey, et al., 2006; 
Dutta, et al., 2008; Beschkov, et al., 2021) and it appears to be a common sulfide conversion 
product. The analysis for which could be implemented to gain a better understanding of the 
sulfide oxidation species produced with MFC.

Oxidation of other compounds such as phosphate could have occurred in the anode resulting 
in an overestimation of the presented results. For this SKALAR analysis can be performed 
along the IC analysis to observe any potential reduction in other compounds. In addition, due 
to the high affinity of sulfide to metals and considerable iron present in the sediment, sulfide 
mineralisation could have occurred underestimating the dissolved sulfide in the water.  
A microbial community analysis was also not performed to investigate the presence of sulfate 
reducing bacteria which could give a better idea of the microorganisms present in the 
environment and mediating the occurring processes. In addition, cyclic voltametric analysis 
was not carried out and polarization curves were not created which could give more insights 
into overpotential and ohmic loss.  
The MFC with brushes showed 90-92% removal of sulfide when compared to the OCP and 
controls, respectively. Slightly smaller results were observed for steel electrodes with 65.4-
81.5%. However, for MFC to be employed as a remediation tool for sulfide removal or 
prevention the values should be impactful in environmental scale (Algar, et al., 2020). 
Although samples used in this study can translate to those present in certain environments, the 
conditions, and the scale the experiment ran do not.  A pilot scale study in marine environment 
employing the MFC to observe applicability in real life conditions could provide a better 
understanding of the potential use of MFC for sulfide removal or prevention.

5 CONCLUSION  
During this master thesis, the potential of microbial fuel cells (MFCs) for sulfide oxidation in 
marine sediments, using different electrodes, were tested with water and sediment from two 
locations: the Black Sea, Bulgaria, and Roskilde Fjord, Denmark. The results were compared 
to initial scoping experiments using fuel cells. The main hypothesis that MFCs will 
successfully oxidize sulfide was observed. The second hypothesis, that MFCs will have a faster 
oxidation rate of sulfide compared to fuel cells, was also observed.  
The scoping experiments did show spontaneous oxidation of sulfide however, results from tests 
with various resistances did not deliver theoretically supported results. Hydrogen sulfide was 
only detected once in a Black Sea reactor and clear observation of sulfide removal was not 
observed. The Roskilde Fjord reactors showed a clear sulfide production of up to 65 mg/L in 
controls. The results from the Roskilde experiment provide proof of sulfide oxidation using 
MFCs.  
Due to the immediate corrosion of Wolfram electrodes in oxic water, the electrodes are 
considered unsuitable for MFC application in marine waters. Carbon brush and stainless-steel 
mesh performed well, except for one stainless steel reactor corroding. Nonetheless, stainless 
steel had a higher current and power density than the carbon brush.  
In conclusion, this thesis has demonstrated that MFC can be used to remove sulfide from 
sediment and sulfide rich waters, while also producing electricity.
