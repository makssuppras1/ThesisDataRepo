Abstract 
Wind power technologies are evolving continuously, over the past 10 years wind energy capacity is 
doubling every three years. Cold regions provide a lot of advantages when installing a wind farm. 
From a physical point of view, the density of cold air is higher than warm air, thus for the same wind 
speed the power harnessed is greater. Moreover, colder regions are less populated, which facilitates 
to find the best site for installing a wind farm. However, wind turbines are exposed to icing 
phenomena in cold regions, reducing the aerodynamic performance, i.e. power loss and increasing 
the loads such as fatigue and extreme. For wind energy to continue growing in the cold climatic 
areas, solutions to mitigate the problems should be found.  
This thesis serves various purposes. The main purpose is to identify problems and propose solutions 
by performing various measurement campaigns under the control environment. Secondly, 
understanding the physics behind ice growth and carrying out measurements with the cloud droplet 
probe and cobra probe to map the conditions at the climatic wind tunnel with different 
configurations in the spray bar. Lastly, measurements using rotating cylinder to verify the liquid 
water content using Finstad’s theoretical formula.  
In this research 26 Irwin type vortex generators are used to create a turbulent boundary layer. The 
flow regime becomes turbulent and causes the formation of small eddies, which in turn mix the 
spraying water coming out of the spray bar.  
The results from the cobra probe validate the installation of the vortex generators. The valuable 
environmental parameters such as MVD, LWC are measured with CDP at the climatic wind tunnel by 
controlling air and water pressure from the spray bar. Different wind speed conditions are 
considered in order to map the environmental condition of the CWT. It will be used for further 
research in the CWT. Finally, the rotating cylinder test is performed with a 32mm diameter cylinder. 
There is a big discrepancy of the LWC’s values between the ones measured by CDP and by the 
rotating cylinder method. This is because the theoretical formula by Finstad cannot capture the 
relationship of drag and inertia forces of the droplets for low wind speeds.

1 Introduction 
Before addressing the physics behind ice accretion, a brief history of the wind turbines and general 
information about icing occurrence are introduced. 
1.1 History of Wind Turbines 
Mankind have harvested energies from the wind for many years, mainly for pumping water or 
grinding grain, by the use of windmills. The first wind turbine to generate electricity was invented by 
Charles F. Brush in 1888 in Cleveland, USA. The diameter was 17 meters with 144 rotor blades, which 
were made of cedar wood, and it had a power output of 12 kilowatts [1]. Poul la Cour, was a Danish 
scientist and was considered as a pioneer of electricity generating wind turbines. He firstly to 
connect a wind turbine with a generator. Pour la Cour installed a wind tunnel, in his school, to 
investigate rotor aerodynamics in 1896 and after a couple of months of experiments he reached to 
general conclusions, which are still used today, produce maximum energy for a given wing area, 
wind turbines should consist by small number of blades and rotational speed should be fast. 
Unfortunately, due to the industrial revolution the main source of electricity was fossil fuels and the 
growth of wind energy was stagnant. That was until 1973, when oil crisis hit the world and wind 
energy start to flourish again. Many countries were investing in research and development programs 
regarding renewable energy.

2.2 Ice accretion  
In this chapter the different types of ice are going to be introduced. We will see that parameters 
such as the median volume diameter, MVD, liquid water content, LWC, temperature and time play 
are the key parameters on ice growth. Moreover, ice growth modelling introduced by Makkonen is 
going to be analysed. More details about different ice types will be addressed below. 
2.2.1 Types of ice 
There are two formation processes of atmospheric icing conditions. 
• 
In-cloud icing, which is a weather phenomenon that occurs when super cooled liquid water 
droplets at a temperature below 0° C impact on a cold surface and freezing immediately. 
• 
Precipitation icing that forms due to wet snow or freezing rain 
The resulting types of ice according to ISO [18] are glaze, rime (hard or soft), wet snow and hoar 
frost. Hoar frost, which happens when water vapor transforms into ice crystals. However, hoar frost 
will not be analysed further since it doesn’t result in a heavy ice load on structures due to its low 
density. 
2.2.1.1 In-cloud icing 
In-cloud icing is divided in three categories. 
1. Rime ice: Super cooled liquid water droplets from cloud transported by the wind hit an 
object and freeze immediately. Accretion rate of rime icing depends mainly on 
▪ 
Wind speed 
▪ 
Object 
▪ 
LWC 
▪ 
MVD 
▪ 
Temperature 
Rime ice can be either soft or hard rime. Soft rime is fragile and the formation of it consists mainly of 
thin ice needles or flakes of ice. It starts from a small point and gradually grows into the windward 
direction. Density of soft rime is in between 200-600 kg/m3. It is usually connected with low LWC, 
MVD, wind speed and temperature below -8° C. (see Figure 5 ). Hard rime on the hand is usually 
connected with higher LWC, MVD, wind speed and temperature between -2° C to -8° C. The colour is 
opaque/white, and the density range is between 600-900 kg/m3. (see Figure 6)  
2. Glaze ice: Is caused when the water droplets doesn’t freeze immediately upon impact but 
create a water film and freeze later on. This leads also sometimes to the formation of icicles. 
Glaze ice is characterized by strong adhesion on the structure with a density around 900 
kg/m3. (see Figure 7)

2.2.3.2 Sticking efficiency 
Sticking efficiency denoted as a2, is the ratio of the particles that hit the object to the particles that 
stick to the object. In rime icing there is not rebounding of the particles since they are freezed 
immediately on impact, therefore the sticking efficiency is equal to unity. When there is a liquid film 
the particles don’t freeze immediately but they freeze further on, hence there is no rebounding also. 
[12] 
2.2.3.3 Accretion efficiency 
Accretion efficiency is the ratio of the particles that freeze on impact and the particles that hit the 
object. In dry growth the accretion efficiency is unity, since all particles freeze on impact. However, 
in wet growth where a water film exists, the freezing rate is extremely complicated to calculate. 
When the particle impinges the surface, heat is released during the freezing process, one have to 
calculate the rate at which it is transferred away from the freezing surface. The heat balance can be

4.1 Spray bar system  
The biggest challenge one has to achieve before addressing the actual measurements in a wind 
tunnel, is to have uniform cloud along the test section. In the beginning of this thesis a pilot 
experiment was conducted to gain experience and be familiar with the environment of the CWT. It 
was observed that the spray bar was performing nonuniformly, resulting in different values of LWC 
in the test section, so as a first task for this thesis was to understand how the spray bar is operating, 
identify the problem and come up with a solution. Inside the spray bar are 4 pressure transmitters, 
two for water and two for air as seen in Figure 24. From those, one can read the pressure for water 
and air inside the spray bar. Those values are identical which means that each nozzle has the same 
air and water pressure. Of course, the pressure is not identical in the nozzles due to different lengths 
of the pipes coming from the connectors to each nozzle, hence the pressure loss is different. In 
Appendix B in Figure 81 a panoramic view of the spray bar is presented, in which one can see the 
difference in pipe lengths. However, the difference in the length between the pipes isn’t big enough 
to have a major impact on the final pressure on each nozzle. That led to the conclusion that the 
uniformity that occurs has to be due to leakage in the pipes or due to the nozzles.

5 Problems and possible Solutions 
The most important problem encountered in this master thesis was the spray bar. Due to the way 
that it is mounted, it is very difficult for one person to unmount it. Leakage is very often inside the 
spray bar resulting in pressure loss, mostly because the pipes inside get loosen. Moreover, the 
nozzles are consisted by many parts as seen in Chapter 3.1, which they should be connected very 
tight to avoid losses of air or water. However, after usage those connections become loosen and one 
has to tight them up to ensure smooth operation of the spray bar. The best solution to this problem 
would be DTU or Force Technologies or both to allocate funds to build the spray bar from the 
beginning. However, this is costly, and a cheaper solution could be to change the nozzles with new 
ones, but instead of consisting of many parts, they should be welded inside the spray bar.  
Vortex generators were mounted in the spray bar with duct tape. However, due to the conditions 
inside the spray bar, high humidity, vortex generators were falling, and one had to reattach them to 
the spray bar. That caused the vortex generators to bend or even broke, and one had to replace 
them with new ones. If it is decided to keep the vortex generators a permanent solution must be 
found. A design of tracks in the spray bar can be implemented, where the vortex generator can fit 
and easily mounted and unmounted. However, the design must be very delicate to not disturb the 
airflow. 
Another problem that was observed is that in high wind speeds and in low air pressures, resulting in 
higher MVD, the water droplets doesn’t have the required time to become super cooled and freeze 
on impact even at temperatures of -7°. The reason this happens is that inside the spray bar are 
==PAGE:66==
53 
 
placed heating wires to avoid water from freezing, thus    However, an investigation has to be made 
to make sure that this is the case before reducing the voltage of the heating wires.  
Finally, there is a big uncertainty in the rotating cylinder when calculating the length and the 
diameter. After each test the length of the cylinder that is accreted with ice is measured with a ruler 
and the diameter with a slide caliper in 3 different positions, one in the middle and two on the sides. 
Moreover, the LWC varies along the span of the test section and when using Finstad formula one can 
calculate the average LWC in the wind tunnel. One solution is to either manufacture or cut the 
existing cylinder in a way that the cylinder consists of 3 parts, from which the middle one can be 
detached and measured alone. That way one can adjust the length of the test section to a fixed 
length e.g. 10 cm, which will be on the middle of the test section, where the LWC is known.  Another 
solution could be to manufacture a streamlined half-moon bar to block the water from the sides and 
expose only the middle part of the cylinder to the airflow. A schematic view of the solution is seen in 
Figure 71.

6 Conclusion and further studies 
6.1 Conclusion 
It is clear from the cobra probe results that the installation of the vortex generators has an impact 
on the flow downstream. The focusing effect at high speeds is not eliminated but Vg’s reduce the 
effect and provide a better mixing of the cloud.  
LWC and MVD mapping with CDP for different wind speeds and configurations of the spray bar, 
provide valuable information for the environmental conditions inside the CWT, which will be used 
for future experiments.  
Rotating cylinder method isn’t validated due to numerous factors. Temperature must be as low as 
possible to ensure rime ice accretion on the cylinder and the minimum temperature in the CTW can 
reach values until -8° C. This temperature however, is sufficient even for high wind speeds but 
combining with the hitting wires inside the spray bar the water droplets don’t have the time to 
become super cooled at high wind speeds or at high MVD, resulting in glaze ice on the cylinder. 
Applying Finstad’s theoretical collision efficiency formula to the numerical code to estimate LWC, 
gives high LWC values which contradict with the measured ones from the CDP. The difference is 
decreasing with the wind speed, which indicates that Finstad’s formula collapses for low wind 
speeds due to the fact that the relationship between inertia and drag of the particles cannot be 
captured. 
6.2 Further studies 
Rotating cylinder: A set of tests should be performed for high wind speeds at a control environment 
ensuring that the water droplets freeze on impact and rime ice accretes on the cylinder. The length 
of the cylinder that ice accretes has to be reduced to facilitate the measurements and omit the 
uncertainty.  
Vortex generators: An investigation for different size of vortex generators can be made and an 
installation of a permanent system for mounting the vortex generators on the spray bar.  
CDP measurements: The heating in the CDP is used only for de-icing. A system controlling the 
heating must be installed to allow using the heating during measurements. Measurements with the 
new installation should be performed again to verify the existing one. 
NACA0015 measurements: In cloud-icing tests should be performed on the NACA0015 airfoil, which 
is located at the CWT, for different LWC, MVD and angle of attacks to see their influence on the 
aerodynamic behaviour. Ice shapes can be extracted and compared for different cases.
