Abstract 
The rise in interest in eating a more plant based and ecofriendly diet, results in more people 
changing dietary habits. With dairy products being responsible for 40% of the Danish intake of 
riboflavin (vitamin B2), investigating differences in content and bioaccessibility between dairy 
products and plant-based alternative, is highly relevant.  
The standard analysis method of riboflavin in food relies on the breakdown of FAD and FMN to 
riboflavin using acid hydrolysis and enzymatic treatment. Complete breakdown can however be 
challenging especially of FMN.  
The thesis investigates the optimal analytical methods to use for the determination of the 
bioaccessibility of Vitamin B2. The precision of three different extraction methods was 
examined, one using enzymatic treatment followed by acid hydrolysis, a second using acid 
hydrolysis followed by enzymatic treatment and third a method only using acid hydrolysis. The 
precision was determined on the four different food matrixes, broccoli, cheddar, liver and milk. 
The acid hydrolysis had the best overall precision as well as the highest overall vitamin B2 
extraction. 
The trueness of the extraction method was determined using spike recovery of riboflavin, FMN 
and FAD. The recovery was acceptable with a mean recovery of 95% (riboflavin), 90% (FMN) 
and 95%(FAD).  
The acid hydrolysis extraction method is promising for the determination of vitamin B2, however, 
analysis of certified reference material and ruggedness testing is required. 
Analysis of INFOGEST 2.0-digesta after a sample clean-up resulted in a too high riboflavin 
content. The reason for this could not be determined.

Summary 
Riboflavin (vitamin B2) is a water-soluble vitamin present in almost all foods, but 
generally most abundant in foods of animal origin like meats and dairy products. 
Riboflavin is necessary for the formation of the coenzymes flavin mononucleotide 
(FMN) and flavin adenine dinucleotide(FAD) which are important for the human 
metabolism. Vitamin B2 is found in the form of FAD and FMN in most food, however, 
milk and egg are rich in free riboflavin.  
Riboflavin is very heat stable in pH below 5.5 but is readily degraded by heat in pH 
above 8. In solutions it is highly photosensitive to wavelength in the range 350-520 nm 
with the 415-455 nm range being especially damaging. The main breakdown product of 
riboflavin is lumichrome (LC) and lumiflavin (LF). Riboflavin is fluorescent with 
maximum fluorescent intensity around λex/λem of 270/525nm, 370/525nm, and 
450/525 nm.  
The European standard EN 14152:2014 for riboflavin determination in food uses HPLC 
with florescent detection. However, a method using UHPLC-MS/MS is also being 
developed for detection in food. The analysis method uses acid hydrolysis and 
enzymatic treatment to breakdown FAD and FMN to riboflavin for detection.  
The aim of the project is to select and validate the optimal analytical methods for 
assessment of bioaccessibility. 
The project can be split into three parts. First part aim to optimize the UHPLC-FLD 
system for riboflavin determination. Second a selection and validation of an optimal 
extraction method. And third, the clean-up of INFOGEST 2.0-digesta sample followed 
by riboflavin determination. 
The UHPLC-FLD system was optimized by choosing the optimal column, mobile phase 
composition, as well as the detector parameters PMT-gain, excitation and emission 
wavelengths.  
Three extraction methods were compared. The IVC 2023 method using enzymatic 
treatment followed by acid hydrolysis. The internal method using acid hydrolysis 
followed by enzymatic treatment. And last, a proposed method only using acid 
hydrolysis. The method was compared analysing 4 different samples, freeze dried 
spinach, cheddar, liver and milk.  
The INFOGEST 2.0 sample clean-up consist of a centrifugation followed by filtration.  
The optimal was found in excitation and emission wavelength of 273/525 nm. 
Best overall precision of the three methods was achieved with the acid hydrolysis 
method with repeatability and reproducibility of 0.61-2.41 % and 1.65-4.46% for the for 
4 different food matrices. Acceptable spike recovery of the acid hydrolysis extraction 
method was achieved with a recovery of vitamin B2 at around 95% (riboflavin), 90% 
(FMN) and 95 % (FAD). The lower spike recovery of FMN is expected to be due to 
sodium in the FMN standard which was not quantified and accounted for.  
The sample clean-up method of the INFOGEST 2.0 achived good separation on the 
UHPLC. However, the determination of riboflavin content was too high. No good 
explanation was found for this. 
A limit of detection (LOQ) of 0.024 mg riboflavin/100g food was achieved at a 0.5 g 
sample size. Achieving a LOQ of 0.01 mg/100g food would require a sample size of 1.2 
g.

1. Introduction 
Riboflavin, also known as vitamin B2, is one of the 9 water soluble vitamins essential 
for human health. Riboflavin is used in the body to make the coenzymes flavin mono 
nucleotide and flavin adenine dinucleotide important for the human metabolism. The 
animal-based product is the main source of riboflavin intake in the Danish diet with 
about 40% coming from dairy product(Pedersen et al., 2015). The rising food trend of 
eat more plant based and climate-conscious food potentially have a big impact on 
riboflavin intake. Dairy products contribute to more than 10 % of the Danish intake of 
B12-vitamin, riboflavin, calcium, zinc, phosphor and iodine. 
This project is part of the bio micro project which aims to investigate difference 
between the bioaccessibility of micronutrient in dairy products and plant based 
alternatives.  
The bioaccessibility of nutrient not necessarily the same as a high content. Determining 
the bioaccessibility therefor give a better understanding of the nutritious effect of a 
food. 
Its is essential for the determination bot content and accessibility to have precise and 
accurate method for analysing the nutrients in food. In vitro models can potentially be 
used for the estimation of bioaccessibility and are much cheaper and laborious than 
human and animal trials. 
The EN14152:2014 standard for determination of riboflavin in food relies on acid 
hydrolysis and enzymatic treatment for the breakdown of FMN and FAD to riboflavin. 
The breakdown to riboflavin was necessary since pure FMN standard was not 
available. The total breakdown of FMN has been shown to be especially challenging.  
Pure standards of FMN and FAD are now available to allow the quantification of these 
and can potentially be used to improve quantification without complete conversion to 
riboflavin. 
 
1.1 Aims 
The amins of this project is to develop and optimize a UHPLC-FLD procedure for 
detection of Riboflavin, FMN and FAD based on current methods and research. This is 
used to select and validate an optimal extraction procedure for the determination of the 
bioaccessibility of Vitamin B2. 
 
The hypothesis of the project is: 
 
1. The UHPLC-FLD detection of Riboflavin, FMN and FAD can be improved to 
achieve a LOQ similar to result by (Jacobsen, 2008) (0.01 mg/100 g) at 0.5 g 
sample instead of 5g, by optimizing the excitation and emission wavelength, 
mobile phase composition and column choice. 
2. The vitamin B2 content in food can be determined without enzymatic treatment 
but only using acid hydrolysis for the extraction. 
3. The riboflavin in INFOGEST 2.0-digesta samples can be determined using 
UHPLC-FLD detection after a cleanup using centrifugation, filtration, and 
dilution in Milli-Q water.

2. Literature review 
2.1 Vitamin B2 
 
2.1.1 Introduction 
Riboflavin, and its derivatives, is part of group of organic compounds containing 
isoalloxazine or its isomer alloxazine. The word flavin is derived from the Latin word for 
yellow. This is due to the yellow colour of these compounds. 
The average daily requirements set by European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) ranges 
from 0.6 mg/day for children 1-3 years old, 1.3 mg/day for adults (≥18 years) and to the 
highest at 1.7 mg/day for lactating women. Riboflavin is only stored in small amounts 
with the highest concentrations found in the liver, heart and kidneys. This is estimated 
to be sufficient to meet metabolic requirements for 2-6 weeks (Gropper et al., 2021). 
Riboflavin is necessary to form the two coenzymes Flavin Mono Nucleotide (FMN) and 
Flavin Adenine Dinucleotide (FAD). These coenzymes are important for many 
processes within the human metabolism. These include the metabolism of 
macronutrients and vitamins, as well as regeneration of reduced antioxidants like 
glutathione and thioredoxin (Gropper et al., 2021).  
Proteins/enzymes that utilize the riboflavin derivatives FMN and FAD are referred to as 
flavoproteins or sometimes flavoenzymes. In humans, there are 90 genes encoding for 
flavin-dependent proteins, with most of them catalysing oxidation-reduction 
reactions(Lienhart et al., 2013).  
 
 
2.1.2 Chemistry 
Riboflavin consists of an isoalloxazine ring with a ribitol side chain. FMN is made by 
adding an inorganic phosphate on the hydroxyl group at the end of the ribitol. From that 
FAD is made by adding an adenosine monophosphate on the phosphate groups. The 
chemical structure of isoalloxazine, Riboflavin, FMN and FAD is seen in Figure 2-1. 
Due to the isoalloxazine ring, flavins can exist in different oxidation states. It exists as 
either fully oxidized flavoquinone, flavosemiquinone or as flavohydroquinone. The 
colour of the riboflavin will change depending on the redox state with flavoquinone 
being yellow, semiquinone being red or blue depending on the pH, while the 
flavohydroquinone is colourless (Golbach et al., 2014). The normal form of riboflavin in 
a neutral aqueous solution is the flavoquinone form seen in Figure 2-1, Depending on 
the pH of the solution, it can exist in either a cationic, neutral or anionic form (Drössler 
et al., 2002).

Riboflavin is very heat stable at acidic pH and has been shown to be stable in the pH 
range 2.0-5.0 when being held at 120 °C for one hour with complete destruction at pH 
≥8 (Farrer & Macewan, 1953). In lower temperatures stability has been shown over 
broader pH ranges from 1.7-5.5 at 100°C and 1.3-6.5 at 80°C. FMN is like Riboflavin 
quite heat stable, however, FAD is readily hydrolysed to FMN in acidic conditions, with 
almost complete hydrolysis after 2 hours at 38 °C in 10% trichloroacetic acid 
(pH=0.67). Hydrolysis was also shown to take place at 0°C with a half time of 12 
hours(Bessey et al., 1949).  
Riboflavin and its derivatives are very photosensitive when in solutions. This has been 
observed in milk when exposed to 30 minutes of sunlight resulting in the destruction of 
30 % of its riboflavin content(Wishner, 1964). In dry form it’s not very effected by light. 
Light with a wavelength of 350-520 nm, and especially 415-455 nm, has been shown to 
be damaging to riboflavin. (Sattar et al., 1977). Riboflavin photosensitivity is highly pH 
dependent with a maximum photostability around pH 5-6. The rate of photolysis will 
increase up to 80-fold around pH 10 and 2-fold around pH 3 (Ahmad et al., 2004). The 
maximum rate of photolysis corresponds to pKa values 1.7 and 10.2, where the slight 
decrease after pH 10 is due to anion formation and the increase around pH 3 is due to 
the direct formation of lumichrome(LC) through excited singlet state pathway and

Riboflavin and its derivatives are fluorescent molecules meaning they can be excited 
by absorbing electromagnetic radiation at specific wavelength, which is emitted almost 
immediately after. In fluorescence, a photon is absorbed resulting in the excitation of an 
electron from a lower to a higher energy state. The higher energy electron then loses 
some of the absorbed energy due to vibrational relaxation without emitting a photon. 
After this loss of energy, the electron returns to its ground state, emitting a lower 
energy photon with a longer wavelength. The process is often visualized with a 
Jablonski diagram, with the shift in wavelength being called the Stoke shift.

Riboflavin has several wavelength areas at which it can be excited, the maximum being 
around 270, 370, and 450 nm, all with a maximum emission wavelength around 525 
nm (Yang et al., 2016). The fluorescence intensity of riboflavin is pH dependent and is 
much lower in strong acidic (pH 1,95) and strong alkaline (pH 11.92). FMN and FAD 
have similar fluorescence properties and are often analyzed at the same wavelength as 
riboflavin. The fluorescence properties of flavins are due to the isoalloxazine ring. 
 
2.1.3 Prescence in food  
Vitamin B2 is present in almost all food but is most abundant in in those of animal 
origin. Meats like pork, poultry and beef typically contain around 0,1-0,3 mg/100g and 
liver, one of the highest concentrations, around 2,95 mg/100g. Dairy products like milk 
and cheese also contain a high concentration of around 0,17 and 0,33 mg/100g 
respectively. In comparison, grain products and vegetables, like wheat flour and 
cabbage, are quite low in Vitamin B2 with contents of around 0,035 and 0,027 
mg/100g. On the contrary, food matrices like oats and spinach have quite high contents 
of 0.128 and 0.24 mg/100g.  Although spinach and oats contain a relatively high 
concentration of vitamin B2, it’s easier to consume 200 g of milk than eating the same 
amount of spinach. This is why dairy products are a more significant source of vitamin 
B2 in Denmark. 
 
The form of Vitamin B2 varies in different food matrixes. The coenzymes FMN and 
FAD are the most abundant forms in most foods, accounting for approximately 80–
90%(Kohlmeier, 2003).The riboflavin form, however, is abundant in dairy, eggs and of 
foods enriched with riboflavin. The flavins found in many food matrices are non-
covalently bound to proteins by intermolecular forces, with a small percentage being 
covalent bound to the amino acid histidine or cystine. In plants, some riboflavin is found 
as beta-glucoside. The significance of this form appears to be minor to 
humans.(Gregory III, 1998).

2.1.4 Digestion and uptake 
The absorption of riboflavin primarily takes place in the small intestine, and to a small 
degree, the large intestine. The uptake is facilitated by the RiboFlavin Vitamin 
Transporter (RFVT3) across the brush border membrane of the intestine. The RFVT3 
only facilitates the transport of riboflavin, which is done by active transport requiring 
ATP(Gropper et al., 2021). 
For vitamin B2 to be available for absorption the loosely non-covalent bound flavins 
must be released. This is facilitated by the release of hydrochloric acid and proteases 
in the stomach. Since only riboflavin can be absorbed by the RFVT3, FMN and FAD 
need to be converted into riboflavin. FAD is broken down into FMN and AMP by the 
nucleotide pyrophosphatase located at brush border of the intestinal cells, while FMN is 
dephosphorylated into inorganic phosphate and riboflavin by alkaline phosphatase in 
the small intestine. The digestion of the covalently bound FAD results in the release of 
amino acid-linked 8-alpha-FAD which is not biologically active(Kohlmeier, 2003). The 
beta-glucoside form of riboflavin needs to be broken up by a beta-glucosidase for it to 
be absorbed. 
 
2.2 Vitamin B2 analysis 
Riboflavin can be analysed in different ways with varying pretreatments, separation and 
detection techniques, depending on the purpose, content and matrix of the sample. 
Since food is a complex matrix with Vitamin B2 found in different forms, its necessary 
to first extract it from the food matrix, involving steps like homogenization, acid 
hydrolysis and enzymatic treatments. After an extraction the sample containing many 
different compounds often needs to be cleaned and separated. To do this, one of the 
most common methods is liquid chromatography. After separation, riboflavin is 
detected and quantified, most often by florescence detection or mass spectroscopy. 
However, other ways of detection include immunoassay and microbiological assay.  
Several methods have been developed and standardized to determining vitamin B2 in 
foods. 
The European standard EN14152 (CEN, 2014), is the European standard specifying 
the determination of food using high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) 
followed by fluorescence detection (FLD). 
The ISO 21470:2020(ISO, 2020) standard is developed for the simultaneous 
determination of vitamins B1, B2, B3 and B6 in infant formula and adult nutritional’s, 
using ultra high-performance liquid chromatography with mass spectroscopy (UHPLC-
MS/MS). A new method being developed at the Institute of Marine Research was 
presented at the International Vitamin Conference 2023 and aims to simultaneously 
determine vitamin B1, B2, B3, B5 and B6 in food using UHPLC-MS/MS, similar to ISO 
21470:2020.  
 
2.2.1 Extraction 
Before the vitamin B2 can be extracted, it’s necessary to homogenise the food matrix 
to ensure uniform vitamin B2 distribution, and to ensure proper extraction during acid 
hydrolysis and the enzymatic treatment.  
The extraction procedure used in the EN14152 standard for food, include an acid 
hydrolysis, followed by an enzymatic treatment.

The method presented at the IVC 2023 first uses and enzymatic treatment then 
followed by an acid hydrolysis. 
 
The acid hydrolysis of the homogenized food matrix has the purpose of denaturing 
proteins and releasing protein bound flavins as well as hydrolysing FAD into AMP and 
FMN. The acid hydrolysis can be performed using either hydrochloric acid or sulfuric 
acid with a pH of the solution no higher than 2. The acid hydrolysis typically performed 
for either 30 min at 121 °C or for 60 min at 100 °C, though data has shown 
temperatures of 95-130 °C can be used for duration of 15-60 minutes (the higher 
temperature, shorter the time). 
 
The purpose of the enzymatic treatment is to dephosphorylate FMN into riboflavin as 
well as to liberate protein- and matrix bound vitamin B2. The enzyme or enzyme mix 
used should have the ability to release and convert vitamin B2 into free riboflavin. An 
enzyme that has been used for the enzymatic treatment is Taka-diastase from Pfaltz 
and Bauer which is an impure diastase with phosphatase and protease activity. The 
challenge with using Taka-diastase is that it relies on the impurities where other 
fabricants with less of the desired impurities will reduce extraction efficiency. However, 
a mix of α-amylase, proteinase and acid phosphatase have been shown to be 
successful (Jakobsen, 2008). In addition, it has shown to be especially difficult to 
convert all the FMN to riboflavin and since the methods only quantifies Riboflavin the 
vitamin B2 content might lower than the actual content. 
 
In vitro digestion models are used for food to estimate the accessibility of vitamin in the 
body. The INFOGEST 2.0 method used by(Jensen et al., 2022) consist of 3 phases 
representing the oral, gastric and intestinal phases of digestion. Solid samples that was 
not previously homogenized or liquid was crushed using mortar and pestle to simulate 
chewing. The INFOGEST 2.0 method aim to simulate the human digestion. By 
analyzing the riboflavin in this sample in the sample it can be estimated how much 
riboflavin is accessible for uptake. Analyzing these samples, it is important not to cause 
further breakdown of FMN and FAD.  
 
 
2.2.2 HPLC separation  
High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) and Ultra High-Performance Liquid 
Chromatography (UHPLC) operates by having a continuous flow of mobile phase 
through a column containing a stationary phase. HPLC and UHPLC operates at high 
pressure with maximum pressure around 400-600 bar for HPLC and for UHPLC, 
around 800-1300 bar. Injecting a sample containing different compounds will have 
elution times through the column depending on the affinity for the stationary phase. The 
eluted compound is detected by an appropriate detector, yielding a chromatogram 
were detected compounds will be shown as peak on the base line at a specific 
retention time. There are many different types of HPLC, however, since flavins are 
soluble in water, reverse phase chromatography is the most used. Reverse phase 
chromatography uses non-polar stationary phase and a polar mobile phase whereas a 
normal phase uses a polar stationary phase and a non-polar mobile phase.

surface, a non-polar bonded phase is added with the most common being the C18 
(octyldecylsilane). The particles porosity and pore size determine the size of the 
molecules able to diffuse into the particle. Small pore size 80-120 Å are suitable for 
small molecules like vitamin B2. The particle size of the stationary phase can vary 
greatly, and though smaller particles result in better resolution, they also require higher 
pressure. Superficially porous particles have been developed to reduce the pressure 
while maintaining high efficiency. This is done by having a larger particle with a solid 
non-porous core and a porous shell, resulting in shorter diffusion path reducing the C-
term in the Van Deemeter equation. 
 
The mobile phase most used for riboflavin is a water-based buffer solution mixed with 
methanol and in some cases acetonitrile (Golbach et al., 2014). Using a less polar 
mobile phase in reverse phase chromatography result in stronger mobile phase. 
Increasing the organic part of the mobile phase (e.g. methanol) will result in vitamin B2 
elution faster from the column. The buffer is used to stabilize pH making it resistant to 
change, and therefore not affecting the fluorescence properties and retention time. 
Degassing mobile phase is an important step since air bubbles impair the performance 
of the pump, column and detector. HPLC grade solvent and filtration is also necessary 
to avoid clogging of the system and degradation of the column. 
The EN14152:2014 standard and the method used by (Jakobsen, 2008) both use 
HPLC with a Supercosil LC-18-DB (5µm particle, 250x4.6 mm) column from Supelco 
and an isocratic methanol:buffer (35:65) mobile phase. The IVC2023 method uses 
UHPLC with a Waters Acquity BEH C18 (1.7µm particle size, 2,1x100 mm) coulmn and 
due to different vitamins being analysed uses several different gradients of 
methanol:buffer. 
 
2.2.3 Detection 
The detection techniques most commonly used for detection in food samples are, Mass 
spectroscopy and Florescence. 
Mass spectroscopy (MS) is a selective detection method relying on the ionization of the 
molecule followed by fragmentation as part of the detection. The most common form of 
ionization technique is electrospray ionization (ESI), which is known as a soft ionization 
technique resulting in fewer fragments than other techniques. Different compounds 
have different fragmentation patterns, with different size fragments created. The 
created fragments have masses compared to their charge (m/z) and can be separated 
by m/z using a mass analyser like a Quadrupole. The Quadrupole uses oscillation 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
Riboflavin – selection and validation of analytical methods for assessment of bioavailability 
15 
voltage at a specific frequency to stabilize only molecules with specific m/z ratio. In 
MS/MS a collision cell is used after the first mass analyser resulting in new fragments 
which again is selected on m/z increasing the selectivity. The selected ions then hits a 
detector and is translated into a signal. The selectivity of UHPLC-MS/MS is what 
makes it possible to determine vitamin B1, B2, B3, B5 and B6 simultaneous. This 
technique often requires an internal standard in compensate for variation of the signal, 
due to matrix effect, ionization efficiency, etc. Isotope labelled standards of the analyte 
molecule are often used, like the Riboflavin-(dioxopyrimidine-13C4,15N2) used for 
riboflavin analysis in the ISO 21470:2020. The limit of detection of Mass spectrometry 
are in the nanogram range(Harris & Lucy, 2016).  
 
Fluorescence detection is very selective since it requires the compound to be 
fluorescent. The detector operates by sending a specific excitation wavelength through 
the flow cell which is absorbed by the analyte. The analyte then emits the absorbed 
light at a different wavelength which is picked up by the detector. For a compound to be 
detected is must have the same excitation and emission wavelength as the setting of 
the detector. Fluorescence detectors are up to 100 times more sensitive compared to 
UV detectors and have detection limits in the picogram range. 
The fluorescence detection of riboflavin can be achieved at many different wavelengths 
within the excitation and emission ranges as seen in the EN14152:2014, with emission 
and excitation ranges of Ex: 410-468 and Em: 510-530 respectively.  
 
 
Photomultiplier tubes (PMT) are important when lower detection limits are needed in 
detectors like the fluorescence (FLD) detector. The PMT is a signal multiplier working 
by multiplying the electrons generated when photons enter the PMT. The amount of 
multiplication can be set on the detector, as the setting often called PMT gain. The 
PMT gain will amplify both the signal of the analyte and the background noise as well. 
Optimizing PMT gain is for this reason done by finding the best signal-to-noise ratio, at 
the highest expected concentration of analyte. A high PMT gain result in overflow of the 
detector is seen as a flattening of the top of the peak in the chromatogram. 
 
 
2.2.4 Method validation 
The purpose of the method validation is to show if a method is suitable for its intended 
use. For this reason, a method validation can vary greatly depending on intended use. 
There are several different guidelines for method validation like the ICH Q2 and the 
NMKL procedure No.4. The NMKL procedure 4. was established by the Nordic 
Committee on Food Analysis (NMKL) and include a practical food example of the 
procedure.   
Before the validation process is started, it’s important to make a validation plan 
describing what is being evaluated and specific requirements of the parameters 
evaluated.  
The validation of an analytical method is the determination and examination of the 
method parameters. The requirement of a validation is dependent on the purpose of 
the method. Methods used for public food controls will require a full internal validation. 
A full internal validation includes the following characteristics:

Specificity is defined as the ability to distinguish the desired analyte from other 
substance that are present in the given sample. When multiple peaks are present in the 
sample, spike can be used to determine if interfering peaks are present in the sample. 
Standard curve and measurement range are used to determine the function which 
corelates the analyte content to the measured response. The correlation should be 
shown in the entire relevant concentration range with at least 5-6 points. The standards 
should be injected at least twice. If the correlation is linear, it can be shown using 
regression analysis. The measurement range should be the expected concentration 
range of samples and should be covered by the calibration curve. 
Precision is the agreement between independent measurements obtained on 
replicates from the same sample. The precision is often displayed as a relative 
standard deviation or coefficient of variant. The precision can be divided into 
repeatability and reproducibility. Repeatability is when the results are gathered under 
as similar conditions as possible, using the same sample, laboratory, equipment, 
person and within a short period of time. Reproducibility, however, is results gathered 
using the same method and sample but in different laboratories and different 
equipment. Internal reproducibility results are obtained on different days by different 
persons but in the same laboratory. 
Trueness is the agreement with the true content of the sample and the result from the 
analysis. Trueness can be evaluated using Certified reference material or inhouse 
reference material, comparing to a reference method which have been validated in a 
collaborative study with acceptable trueness. Recovery is another method that can be 
used when the above are unavailable. Recovery can be used by spiking a sample with 
known analyte content, wherefrom the spiking can be calculated from amount in the 
spiked sample, the original amount in the sample and the amount of spiking using the

Limit of Quantification (LOQ) and Limit of Detection (LOD) is the lowest signal in 
which the amount of analyte can be either reliably quantified or detected. There are 
many different ways of determining LOD and LOQ. A common way of determining the 
detection or quantification limit is to analyse 20 blank samples or samples with very low 
amount of analyte added, and multiply the standard deviation by 3 for the LOD and 10 
for the LOQ. It’s also possible to calculate the LOD and LOQ from the calibration curve 
using the standard deviation of this. Signal-to-noise is another way of determining LOD 
and LOQ where the limits are 3 and 10 times the signal of the noise of the baseline. 
Ruggedness is the methods stability to small changes in conditions. A good analysis 
should be rugged and not be affected by small changes in condition. The parameters to 
test for ruggedness dependent on the analysis method and should include the most 
likely parameters to vary between different runs. Common parameters tested for 
ruggedness include sample composition, pH and extraction time. The ruggedness data 
can be used to determine the condition for which the method can be reliably used. 
Evaluation of the validation result should continuously be performed so that the 
method can be changed when the specified parameters are not met, so that the whole 
validation process should not be unnecessary repeated.

The optimization of the UHPLC-FLD detection was necessary since a new UHPLC-
FLD system had been acquired to replace the old HPLC-LFD. 
The purpose of the optimization of the UHPLC-FLD detection was to use the current 
HPLC-FLD detection method (Jakobsen, 2008) results and optimize it for the detection 
Riboflavin, FMN and FAD on the new UHPLC-FLD system. The first step was to 
optimize the excitation and emission wavelength used for the detection to achieve the 
strongest signal and to improve the LOD and LOQ. The five different columns available 
in the lab was then compared and the most promising was used for further optimization 
of the separation by changing the mobile phase composition and flow rate. The PMT 
gain of the FLD detector was then optimized to achieve the highest signal-to-noise 
ratio. A six-point calibration curve was made to control the linearity of riboflavin, FMN 
and FAD. 
 
For the method comparison of the extraction of vitamin B2 in food Three different 
methods on extraction of vitamin B2 was compared. One was the current internal 
method based on the results from (Jakobsen, 2008). One based on the method 
presented at the International Vitamin Conference (IVC) 2023, and at last, a method 
only using acid hydrolysis.  
The internal method uses the same steps (acid hydrolysis, enzyme treatment, filtration) 
as the EN14152:2014 standard. The method relies on the acid hydrolysis for 
breakdown of FAD to FMN and the enzymatic treatment to break down FMN to 
Riboflavin. For the enzymatic step a new supplier (Sigma-Aldrich) of taka diastase 
enzyme was used since Pfaltz and Bauer Taka diastase was not available from the 
main supplier of the lab. The internal method was used as comparison since it is the 
standard method and to determine if taka diastase from Sigma-Aldrich can be used as 
a replacement of Pfaltz and Bauer taka-diastase. 
 
The method presented at the IVC 2023, is being developed at The Norwegian Institute 
of Marine Research and has the purpose of the simultaneous determination of the 
vitamin B1, B2, B3, B5 and B6. The procedure of extraction method was shared 
personally and is not referenced. This method uses two enzymatic treatments followed 
by an acid hydrolysis.  This is inverse compared to the internal method and the 
EN14152:2014 standard. The enzymatic treatment uses α-alpha amylase and acid 
phosphatase to break down the food matrix and hydrolyse FMN to riboflavin. FAD

3.2.2 Standard preparation 
The riboflavin, FMN and FAD standard solution was prepared using the same 
procedure as the internal method. The standards consist of a stock solution (≈25-100

µg/ml for the FMN and FAD solutions, and 25 µg/ml for riboflavin due to its lower 
solubility. To ensure all riboflavin, FMN and FAD was dissolved, the absorbance at 444

The sample clean-up used for INFOGEST 2.0-digesta samples includes a

The INFOGEST 2.0-digesta used were previously prepared samples used for vitamin K

The samples were weight in 15 ml amber Eppendorf tube at a weight of 0.4-1 g.  
For the oral phase 1 ml simulated salivary fluid was added and incubated at 37°C for 
two minutes.  
For the gastric phase the sample was added 2 mL of simulated gastric fluid along with 
pepsin and gastric lipase to a concentration of 2000 U/mL and 60 U/mL respectably. 
The pH adjusted to a pH of 3.0, incubating for 37°C for 2 hours. 
For the intestinal phase the sample was added 4 ml of simulated intestinal fluid along 
with bile and pancreatin to a concentration of 2000 U/mL and 60 U/mL respectable. 
The pH was adjusted to a pH of 7.0 and the sample was incubated at 37 °C for 2 
hours.  
For the sample cleanup 0,5 ml INFOGEST 2.0-digesta was pipetted into a 2 ml 
centrifuge tube and centrifuged at 10000g for 10 minutes. From the supernatant 0.4 ml 
was pipetted into new centrifuge tube and added 0.8 ml Milli-Q water for a total of 1.2 
ml (1:3) dilution. The sample was the vortexed thoroughly and filtered in HPLC vial 
using a syringe and a 0.2 µm filter.

4.1 HPLC-FLD optimization 
 
The spectrum of the excitation scan of the 200-500 nm range with emission wavelength 
of 520 nm (Figure 4-1), showed excitation through much of the wavelength range with 4 
excitation peaks. The highest response was found at 273 nm with the second highest 
at 450 nm having a relative response of 70 % compared to the 273 nm. The lowest 
peaks at 378 nm and 233 nm had relative responses of around 53% and 43% 
respectively. Minimum excitation was observed at wavelength 315 nm and 200 nm. 
The strongest excitation at 273 nm wavelength was used for emission scan in the 
range 400-700 nm. The emission spectrum (Figure 4-1) showed only one emission peak 
at wavelength 525 nm. The emission declined rapidly on each side of the peak with a 
50% drop at wavelength 565 nm and 520 nm.  
An emission scan in the range 500-700 nm using excitation at 450 nm, showed the 
same emission peak at 525 nm.

Column choice  
The columns were compared on their separation of a mixed standard solution

higher efficiency at larger particle size. The Poroshell and Zorbax columns have a 
carbon load of 10% and 9% respectively, while the Aquity column have carbon load of 
18 % making it a more hydrophobic stationary phase compared to the others.

riboflavin (1,37 min) and FAD (1,73 min), expected due to selectivity change from the 
higher carbon load.

The data for the calibration curve is shown in in appendix A.4.  
The FAD standard solution was shown not to be stable with hydrolysis to FMN taking

A relative residual plot (Figure 4-5) of riboflavin showed an increase in the deviation 
from the linear model at concentration 0,01-0,05 µg/mL equal to an injection volume of 
1-5 µL. The increase in relative residual corresponds to the precision of the 
autosampler which typically is <1% RSD of the peak area in the 1-5 µL compared to < 
0.25 % RSD < 5 µL to 100 µL. With the highest relative residual 0,81% and no 
systematic trend, the riboflavin curve is acceptable and linear.  
The same pattern is seen for the relative residual of FMN with higher deviation with the 
injection volumes of 1-5 µL. The highest relative residual for FMN is at 1,60 % and no 
systematic trend the curve is acceptable and linear. 
The relative residual for FAD showed a major increase in relative residuals at the 
concentration 0,008 µg/mL with a deviation of 17,76 % and 16.38%. This large 
deviation might be due to the fact that the actual FAD concentration is calculated using 
the FMN found in the FAD standard. The FAD standard also has a much lower signal 
compared to the same concentration of FMN and FAD as well as lower estimated 
concentration of 0,008 µg/ml contribution to the uncertainty. 
The FAD calibration is not precise at the concentration of 0,008  µg/ml using the 
equation without forcing the intercept through zero. The FAD calibration showed

IVC 2023 
One sample of liver in the second series was lost during extraction giving a total of only 
7 replicates. 
The result from the IVC 2023 method (Figure 4-6) showed almost complete breakdown 
of FAD with all samples being below the LOQ and most below the LOD. FMN was 
below the LOQ for all samples in both series of Cheddar, Liver and milk. FMN was 
quantified in 7 out of the 8 samples with Freeze dried spinach. The FMN of the spinach 
in series 1 corresponded to approximately 10 % of the vitamin B2 and 7.6% in the 
spinach series 2. Combined, it would result in approximately 9% of the vitamin B2 in 
the spinach not being quantified if FMN is not quantified. The method generally 
resulted in a better breakdown of FMN than expected. The release and breakdown of 
FMN and FAD appears to be efficient during the enzymatic treatments. This appears 
since additional FMN released during acid hydrolysis would not be broken down from 
the acid phosphatase due to denaturization of the enzyme. The FMN leftover in the 
spinach is suspected to have been liberated by the acid hydrolysis.

2023 method and the acid hydrolysis method in the milk sample (p-value=0.811). 
The significant difference of the methods in the dairy samples might be due to the low 
standard deviation of these samples. The content of FMN and FAD being below the 
LOQ in the samples might also contribute to enough variation between the methods for 
them to be significantly different.  
The acid hydrolysis method was further validated for trueness using spike recovery. 
This was chosen based on the low RSD of the method and that this method found the 
highest content in the samples except for the cheddar sample.

Trueness 
The trueness was determined by spike recovery in milk. 8 replicates of high spike and 
low spike concentration was used for RF, FMN and FAD. The average recovery of 
vitamin B2 in the riboflavin sample spiked with riboflavin was 95.3(±1.5) % and

to be heat stable at the pH range 1.7-5.5 °C, low amount of heat degradation could 
occur at the extraction conditions pH≈1 and 99°C. Results from (Cellar et al., 2016) 
which found sample recovery vitamin B2 ranging from 95.1-100.7%, on infant formula 
and adult nutritional’s using only an enzymatic treatment. Compared to these results 
the 95% was deemed acceptable. 
Vitamin B2 recovery of FAD spike showed recovery of 95.3(±2.6) % and 95.9(±2.9) % 
which was also acceptable with no concentration dependence.  
Vitamin B2 recovery of FMN show 89.8(±2.2) % and 90.6(±2.6) % recovery. The lower 
recovery could be explained with the FMN standard used was on a sodium salt form 
with no stochiometric info on sodium content.

4.3 Riboflavin determination in INFOGEST 2.0 digesta samples. 
Three different INFOGEST 2.0 digesta was analyzed, broccoli, cheddar and 
Klovborg(cheese).  
The INFOGEST 2.0-digesta samples was cleaned-up by centrifugation and filtration 
before injecting into the UHPLC.  
The chromatogram from the sample showed multiple peaks (A.8), however, the peaks 
of riboflavin, FMN and FAD showed good symmetric peaks and a resolution >1.5. The 
peak could again be confirmed using multiple excitation wavelengths or by spiking and 
observing the peak change. 
Since the INFOGEST 2.0 digesta cheddar samples was made with the same cheddar 
as the one used for the extraction part, it was possible to determine the bioaccessibility 
of vitamin B2. 
The analysis of the INFOGEST 2.0 digesta showed a very high riboflavin content of 
4.851 µg riboflavin compared to the ingested vitamin B2 of 2.47 µg resulting in a bio 
accessibility of 196% which is not possible. No method blank is available to determine 
the riboflavin added during the INFOGEST 2.0 method. The content seems quite high 
to be explained by the method, otherwise a dilution mistake could explain the 
difference. However, this seems highly unlikely due to the simplicity of the clean-up 
method. 
Despite the high content found in the sample, the clean-up resulted in good separation 
on the chromatogram. The lowest resolution at 1.7 was between FMN and FAD. The 
relative standard deviation of riboflavin in the cheddar, Klovborg and broccoli samples 
was 0.2%, 0.1% and 6.7 %. 
Spiking of the INFOGEST 2.0 sample could be used to determine whether the right 
peaks were identified. Furthermore, using multiple excitation wavelengths, including the 
450 nm range, could be used to determine interference of other compounds.

5. Conclusions 
The optimization of the UHPLC-FLD was successful at separating and determining 
riboflavin, FMN and FAD in all methods. The acid hydrolysis method had the best 
overall precision of the methods tested for vitamin B2 analysis.  
The UHPLC-FLD detection optimization did not achieve LOQ of 0.01 mg/100 g at 0.5 g 
sample. The LOQ of 0.01 mg/100g would be achieved by using a sample size of 1.2 g. 
The vitamin B2 content in the sample was successfully quantified using only acid 
hydrolysis for the extraction. The acid hydrolysis resulted in good precision and 
trueness using spike recovery. Further validation using certified reference materials 
would be required for determination of trueness. 
Determination of riboflavin in the INFOGEST 2.0-digesta sample was achieved, 
however, unexpectedly high content was found. No explanation was found for the high 
content in the samples. Further validation of precision and trueness is required.

6. Further perspectives  
Several optimizations could be performed of the standard solution to potentially 
improve the method. The optimization of the standard solution so FAD would be stable 
would reduce the uncertainty of the FAD. Increasing the pH of the standard solution to 
the same pH as the mobile phase of pH 3.3 could potentially reduce the breakdown of 
FAD to FMN in the standard.  
Determining the sodium content in the FMN standard to determine the FMN 
concentration more accurately could improve the trueness of the FMN detection. 
The use of a lower concentration (e.g. 0.001-0.05 µg/ml) of the calibration curve could 
potentially lower the LOQ and LOD, this might require a higher PMT gain, however.  
A challenge of the acid hydrolysis method was that the concentration of either 
riboflavin, FMN or FAD was below the LOQ, resulting in a lower vitamin B2 content 
quantified. This could be improved by aiming for a higher vitamin B2 content in the 
injection of 0.2 µg/ml instead of the 0.1 µg/ml used in the project. 
Higher fluorescent intensity could possibly be achieved by using a higher pH of the 
mobile phase, since the highest florescent intensity of riboflavin was found at 5.66 by 
(Yang et al., 2016). The optimization of the pH of the mobile phase could potentially 
also be used to improve resolution between FMN and FAD.  
Further validation of the acid hydrolysis extraction method is required. The analysis of a 
certified reference material will be required for confirming the trueness of the method. 
Ruggedness testing should also be performed, investigation how small changes in the 
extraction time and temperature effect the vitamin B2 extraction. 
The INFOGEST 2.0 samples should be analysed with a reagent blank in order to 
determine the riboflavin added by the method. Precision of the INFOGEST 2.0-digesta 
clean-up should be determined using 8 replicates. Trueness of the sample could be 
tested with spike recovery and interlaboratory comparisons since no reference material 
is available.
