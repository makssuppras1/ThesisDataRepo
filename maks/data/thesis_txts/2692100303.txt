Master thesis
Cross disciplinary assessment of autonomous shipping, ports and intermodal innovations
by Antonis Podiotis, s196128
Filio Daskalaki, s192535
This thesis is investigating on a preliminary level the ﬁnancial, environmental and social perfor-
mance of a novel water-based EU transportation system focusing on vessels and ports automation,
as proposed by the Advanced, Efﬁcient and Green Intermodal Systems (AEGIS) Project, funded
by the European Union. Herein, we focus on Work Package 9 (WP9) of the Project, which describes
the establishment of an intermodal transportation chain of trailers handled by the AEGIS partner
DFDS A/S, utilizing the inland waterways (IWW) in the Netherlands and Belgium. For this rea-
son, a new, autonomous Ro-Ro vessel will be designed by the ISE, which should have minimum
or zero-emissions propulsion system. A comparison of KPIs between the current transportation
chain and the suggested one by AEGIS WP9 was carried out. The results showed that the novel
transportation chain will have higher autonomy level, will perform better in greenhouse gases
(GHG) emissions and will minimize accidents. Nevertheless, the cost of distributing the trail-
ers via IWW will be slightly higher and the delivery times will be longer, which is the greatest
disadvantage of the proposed transportation chain.

1.1
Climate Change
The October-November 2020 Eurobarometer 500 joint survey revealed that most European Union
citizens rate “Climate change and environmental issues” as the most threatening matter the Eu-
ropean Union (EU) has to face in the future [1]. Undoubtedly, climate change is one of the major
problems societies globally are obliged to encounter in the following years – after the COVID-19
pandemic is confronted. Although climate change is an ongoing process on Earth, anthropogenic
activity has accelerated the alterations of the weather patterns.
Carbon dioxide (CO2) is the main greenhouse gas (GHG) contributing to global warming, along
with gases such as methane (CH4), ozone (O3), nitrous oxide (N2O) and more. These greenhouse
gases are important elements of the Earth’s atmosphere, as if not for their existence in sufﬁcient
concentrations, our planet would be cold and not able to sustain life. However, anthropogenic
activity has increased the GHG concentration in the atmosphere, causing the greenhouse effect -
more heat generated by solar radiation is trapped in the atmosphere and Earth becomes warmer.
Fossil fuels’ consumption, electricity and heat production, industrial activity, mining and trans-
portation are major sources of CO2 released to the atmosphere. As global population is growing
rapidly and the energy requirements of modern societies constantly increase, reducing the CO2eq
levels is a tricky exercise to perform. Nevertheless, solving this “Gordian knot” is vital in order
not to deteriorate the current situation and to ensure that the atmospheric air will not further affect
human health worldwide.
The European Union has set an ambitious goal of becoming “the ﬁrst climate-neutral continent”
through the European Green Deal Action Plan [2]. This target will be achieved by implementing
actions that will bring greenhouse gases’ emissions down to zero by 2050. Additionally, economic
growth of member states will not depend on natural resources but on the circular economy prin-
ciples [2]. Inevitably, this plan will affect all sectors, as well as transportation across the European
continent. One of the fundamental values of the EU is the license to transport cargo among the
EU members without border controls [3]. Goods transportation in EU is a critical factor for the
member states’ economies and societies. Consequently, the EU transportation system should con-
tinuously enhance its performance and in parallel utilize zero-carbon technologies in order to
comply with the Union’s newest environmental requirements.
Already, the Trans-European Transport Network (TEN-T) policy - funded by the Connecting Eu-
rope Facility (CEF) for Transport - aims into establishing novel aviation, maritime, road and rail
networks across the EU countries by modernizing the current facilities and creating new ones in

continent, EU practices for freight transportation should not only be followed in the geographical
regions of the 27 countries members of the EU, but also across all regions EU-based companies
operate. All in all, the EU transportation sector is in the process of shifting to more sustainable

The Union’s willingness to shift to more sustainable ways of transportation lead into searching for
new freight movement concepts and inland waterways have the capability to support the novel,
cost-effective and environmentally neutral transportation system the EU member states need for
the future. Maritime transportation is highly advantageous when it comes to cargo movement,
thus it is the dominant freight transportation mode globally. As the shipping sector is also search-
ing for the best technologies that will allow maritime transportation to become carbon-free and
more efﬁcient, the EU has decided to utilize to its upmost and upgrade the existing water-based
transportation chain. The EU funds several projects that search for innovative applications to im-
prove the freight distribution performance, one of which is the AEGIS Project, to which this thesis
is connected.
1.2
AEGIS Project
Advanced, Efﬁcient and Green Intermodal Systems (AEGIS, https://aegis.autonomous-ship.
org/) is a newly established project funded by the European Union’s Horizon 2020 research and
innovation program. The objective is to develop and promote a sustainable water-based EU trans-
portation system, focusing on vessels and ports automation [6]. This novel system should be com-
petitive to road transportation, so that moving cargo by ships instead of trucks is cost-effective and
has minimum or zero environmental footprint, due to advanced propulsion systems utilized [6].
The project consists of 10 Work Packages (Figure 1.1), each one focusing on different aspects of
the novel waterborne transportation system. Work Packages 8, 9 and 10 investigate the potentials
of the short-sea shipping and inland waterways freight transportation by studying three real life
cases in Northern Europe (Use Case A, B and C respectively) [7]. The ambition is that the results
of the use cases will be applied in several areas in Europe. This thesis will focus on a preliminary
evaluation of the potentials of Work Package 9 (WP9 or Use Case B).

Sustainability in maritime transportation addresses three aspects: the environment, the society
and the economy. AEGIS ambition is to propose a transportation system that will target all three
aspects and will overcome current limitations. The key factors are the zero emission propulsion
systems, the levels of autonomy utilized on and off shore and digitalization. The merits of such a
water-based inland cargo transportation system, as described by the AEGIS consortium are [6]:

The question is whether the novel transportation system proposed for WP9 by AEGIS will have a
better sustainability performance compared to the current inland transportation principles, which
is what this thesis examines. Our motivation on investigating this project is that it comprises sus-
tainability and automation regarding the transportation sector. The challenge of integrating zero-
emissions propulsion systems and automation on vessels while keeping up with the commodities
demands is an intriguing topic to research, so we took the chance to examine the potentials of
such a novel inland transportation chain and the possible limitations.

1.3
Thesis Objective
Ths thesis will investigate Work Package 9 (WP9) of the AEGIS Project. The target of WP9 is to
establish a sustainable inland water-based EU transportation system in the Netherlands and Bel-
gium, focusing on newly designed autonomous vessels and enhanced ports and cargo handling
operations. The new inland waterways (IWW) transportation chain should be cost-effective and
reliable in order to replace the use of trucks, at least for the greatest distance of the routes "Euro-
pean hub port - ﬁnal customer".
The leader of WP9, project partner DFDS A/S, is currently transporting trailers to the hub ports of
Rotterdam, Ghent and Zeebrugge by Ro-Ro vessels. Afterwards, the trailers are delivered to their
ﬁnal customers in Europe by trucks. Work Package 9 aims to boost modal shift for the "last mile"
transportation from trucks to autonomous vessels with zero-emissions propulsion. In the future,
a number of trailers arriving at the hub ports will be loaded into autonomous Ro-Ro shuttles
and through the inland waterways in the Netherlands and Belgium they will be unloaded at hub
ports as close to their ﬁnal destination as possible, then collected by the customers. The inland
waterway routes identiﬁed by DFDS are listed below and depicted in Figures 1.2, 1.3 and 1.4.
• Route 1 (R1): Zeebrugge – Ghent (Figure 1.2a).
• Route 2 (R2): Ghent - Antwerp (Figure 1.3a).
• Route 3 (R3): Vlaardinger (Rotterdam) - Ghent (Figure 1.4a).
The thesis objective is to examine - on a preliminary level - whether modal shift for the “last mile”
transportation of trailers from road to inland waterways is ﬁnancially, socially and environmen-
tally beneﬁcial. To attain this, a comparison of Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) related to trucks,
vessels and ports sustainability performance will be carried out between road and inland water-
ways transportation scenarios. Speciﬁcally, the comparison will be performed for the waterways
distances of the aforementioned routes and the adequate road distances. The current and AEGIS
scenarios for each route and their respective comparison boundaries (indicated by the red-dotted
lines) are illustrated in Figures 1.2b, 1.3b and 1.4b.
Yet, there are some limitations on how many trailers will be transported in the hinterland by
shuttles in the future. In some cases the decision maker has a strong preference for road trans-
portation, while in others the customers are not close to ports along the aforementioned routes, so
there is no beneﬁt on inland waterways transportation of theirs. Thus, we developed a simulation
in Python Programming Language which calculates the cost and environmental impact of IWW
transportation when different percentages of cargo is transported by vessels.

Study limitations

Methodology and Structure
The ﬁrst step was to understand in depth what the AEGIS Project’s goals are and which are the

results.

2 Maritime transportation and EU
2.1
Maritime sector
Maritime is the major mode of transportation, considering that 80% of the global goods’ volume
is transported by vessels [8]. Seaborne freight transportation is the main mode of moving cargo
from and to EU [9]. Commercial relationships wouldn’t have been this extensive if it wasn’t for the
advantages maritime transportation offers to the shippers. The ﬁrst time the human kind started
exchanging goods dates back to the ﬁrst agricultural communities. Since then, commerce has re-
volutionized and today all kind of products and commodities are shipped around the globe. Con-
sequently, efﬁcient and cost-effective cargo transportation and logistics systems linking markets
across continents are vital. Thus, it is important to acknowledge shipping’s contribution to global
warming, the current regulations and possible improvements towards zero CO2eq emissions.
According to the 4th IMO GHG Study, in 2018 the maritime sector (international, domestic and
ﬁshing) accounted for 2,89% of the global anthropogenic CO2 emissions [10]. As depicted in Fi-
gure 3.2, in 2016 international shipping was one of the least GHG emitting sectors in the EU (147
million tonnes of CO2eq) [11]. Evidently, GHG emissions generated by maritime transportation
are far lower compared to other human activities. Nevertheless, as the importance of the shipping
sector cannot be neglected, since all countries around the globe depend on shipping for fuel and
goods’ transportation [12], enhancement of the environmental, social and ﬁnancial impacts of the
maritime sector should be addressed.
Even though transportation is a crucial sector which supports all industries, actions on improving
the sector’s environmental performance and contribution in sustainable growth were not clearly
introduced as an article in the Paris Agreement on climate change, or as a Sustainable Development
Goal (SDG) in the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, both established in 2015 [12]. As global
supply chains and markets heavily depend on maritime transportation, it was important to deﬁne
what the sustainable shipping sector should be comprised of. Benamara et al. concluded into
Figure 2.2 summarizing the basic principles maritime sector should fulﬁl in the future [12].

Taking into consideration the aforementioned agreements and objectives, in 2018 the International
Maritime Organization took the initiative to adopt the "Initial IMO Strategy on reduction of GHG
emissions from ships". As stated in the Resolution [13], various measures are to be enforced aim-
ing into reducing:
1. the carbon intensity of each ship by fully utilizing the EEDI for novel vessels
2. CO2 emissions per transport work in average across international shipping, by at least 40%
by 2030 and towards 70% by 2050, compared to 2008 emission levels
3. the total annual GHG emissions by at least 50% by 2050, compared to 2008 GHG levels
The measures aiming to an environmentally neutral maritime sector fall into three categories [14]:
• Technological measures: energy saving engines and propulsion, alternative fuels, scrubbers,
cold ironing and more
• Market Based Measures (MBM), for example Emissions Trading Scheme or carbon taxes
• Logistics-based measures: speed reductions, ﬂeet management, optimized routing and
others
It is worth mentioning that all efforts on reducing the environmental impact of maritime trans-
portation should be combined with adequate proposals and actions of enhancing the ports and
the cargo handling systems. Furthermore, improving the environmental indicators of shipping is
the most urgent issue right now, but it is important to bear in mind that sustainability also refers
to social and ﬁnancial impacts, which shouldn’t be disregarded.
The advantages of maritime transportation and the potentials identiﬁed for future improvements
make shipping an ideal mode of transportation to be utilized by the EU. Already, almost 40% of
cargo commerce among the EU member states is conducted by sea [15]. Partially, this is due to the
fact that some EU countries are island states - such as Malta and Cyprus - and their commercial
activity can only be conducted by sea. Yet, goods’ exchanging by sea also applies in cases where
road or rail transportation takes longer and results being more expensive than shipping the cargo
by vessels, for example moving timber from Sweden to the Netherlands.
Complementary to boosting freight transportation by sea, the European Commission has set a
goal to increase cargo moved by short sea shipping and inland waterways, referred to as water-
based transportation. Short sea shipping (SSS) is deﬁned by the EU as “maritime transportation of
goods between ports in the EU [. . . ] on one hand, and ports situated in geographical Europe, on
the Mediterranean and Black Seas on the other hand” [16]. The later means that short sea shipping
is thought to be conducted between EU and non-EU countries, such as Egypt in the Mediterranean
Sea or Ukraine in the Black Sea [16]. In 2019, the total gross weight of cargo transported by SSS was
approximately 1.8 billion tonnes [17]. In the same year, Italy accounted for 15% of the short-sea
transportation of freight, followed by the Netherlands and Spain [17].

As described in the “Commission Communication - The Development of Short Sea Shipping in
Europe” document [18], SSS should become a competitive alternative for the transportation and
logistics systems in the EU, since it is highly advantageous because:
i it is environmentally friendlier and safer than the current jammed road network
ii it can promote interoperability and boost cohesion among the EU countries, and possibly re-
vive peripheral regions
iii it offers potentials of strengthening the current supply chains by transporting goods as close
to their ﬁnal destinations as possible
Yet, as Psaraftis and Zis (2020) mention, road haulage of goods is - and will continue being - the
dominant transportation mode in EU [19]. The latest data depicted in Figure 2.3 [20] clearly show
that the gap among the transportation modes utilization cannot close unless radical measures are
imposed [19]. There are several reasons behind the lower ﬁgures of SSS against road transporta-
tion. Firstly, it was only in the last decade that the EU regulatory bodies came up with some
tangible statutes about port services and ﬁnancial transparency in ports. Ports also have to deal
with security issues, for example cargo security audits [19].
Moreover, environmental issues emerge, both for the vessels and the ports. Shuttles’ emissions
while travelling and when berthing are a considerable challenge for SSS, while in the future the
sulphur cap can probably inﬂuence modal shift the other way round, moving freight by road in-
stead of water-based transportation. Ballast water and waste management along with potential
oil spills are issues to be dealt with for the SSS to expand. Furthermore, the fact that the road net-
work is extensive and has minimum bureaucracy to move cargo across the EU countries makes
road haulage more appealing to the decision maker. Strangely enough, EU’s choice to boost the
rail way network across the member states have made shippers to prefer rail instead of short sea
shipping. What makes things more complicated is that SSS can be competent providing that rail
interface is available [19].

Additionally to short-sea shipping, broader utilization of inland waterways is an EU objective. In
order to attain this goal, the NAIADES and NAIADES II Action Projects were introduced. Their
target was to promote modal shift and launch actions so that inland waterways are integrated
in the EU transportation system [21]. Navigable inland waterways are deﬁned as the natural or
artiﬁcial rivers, canals and lakes that are appropriate for navigation of shuttles of 50 tonnes carry-
ing capacity when normally loaded [22]. Inland water-based transportation is already preferred
for dangerous goods, as the accident probability is less than road or rail transportation. Moreover,
moving cargo into the hinterland by waterways is far more energy saving (per km/ton) compared
to the other transportation modes [23].
Navigating in inland waterways has more or less the same advantages and limitations as short sea
shipping, but if integrated in the transportation and logistics systems it can have a signiﬁcant im-
pact on the sustainability of the freight transportation sector in EU. Despite the fact that it’s been
more than 20 years that the signiﬁcance of shifting freight from road to rail and water-based trans-
portation was recognized, the latest data show little improvement towards this goal. However,
the newest targets on cutting down transportation emissions by 60% by 2050 [24] make reshaping
of the EU cargo transportation system imperative.

2.2.1
Road
Undoubtedly, road transportation plays a signiﬁcant role for the supply and logistics chains in
Europe. Human and industrial activity depends on the road network for the movement of crucial
products, such as food or medicines, and for the function of vital services – trash collection, fuels’
supply and more [29]. There are several reasons why road transportation is the preferred one to
carry freight across EU. Geographically, the EU countries are connected through an extensive road
network with the exceptions of Cyprus, Malta and the islands of several EU countries.
Modern highways with novel facilities are safer compared to previous years. Additionally, there
are cases where no other transportation modes than vehicles can be used in order to access certain
places, due to geographical restrictions. From a business aspect, choosing road transportation
has certain advantages, such as ﬂexibility, speed and reliability. Moreover, moving freight with a
truck doesn’t require great investments and minimizes time loss, because of the lack of complex
cargo handling [29]. Furthermore, there are limited administrative burdens in crossing borders
through the EU road network, fact that doesn’t apply to maritime transportation. As the EU
population increases and demand for more freight to be distributed door-to-door rises constantly,

road network will be heavily burdened in the following years. That is to say, a challenge emerges
for the logistic chains, infrastructure and transportation supporting services.
Nevertheless, road transportation is not environmentally, socially and ﬁnancially the optimal
mode of transportation to choose, especially for freight distribution. Road transportation in 2018
accounted for 71% of overall transportation GHGs emissions (EU-27) [30]. Not only GHG emis-
sions are important environmental indicators to be addressed. Improvements on dust emissions,
levels of noise and visual pollution caused by the diverse and large numbers of vehicles should
be met. Additionally, even though the latest technological improvements of carbon neutral power
engines are mainly utilized by the automotive industry, for instance electric cars, trafﬁc jams and
number of accidents can only be reduced if the number of vehicles travelling decrease. Finally,
road transportation is cost-efﬁcient for short and medium distances, but not for long distances,
especially for bulk cargo [29].
2.2.2
Rail
Until the 1990s, rail transportation was dominated by national operators and the EU didn’t pay
much attention on the potentials trains can bring in moving freight across the continent. Nowa-
days, in order to boost interoperability and interconnection among the member states’ railway
systems, the EU has introduced the TEN-T program [31]. In terms of GHG emissions, rail is a
highly competitive mode, especially against road transportation. As shown in Table 2.1, the CO2
emissions in 2014 (grams per ton-km) of rail were by far the least compared to the other inland
transportation modes [32], mainly due to the fact that 80% of the trains operating in the EU use
electric traction [33]. Furthermore, technological improvements have allowed the construction of
appropriate infrastructure so trains can pass over great massifs, such as the Alps [32]. Additio-
nally, several European countries have extensive railway networks, which in many cases reach
hub ports, so modal shift is possible.

2.2.3
Inland waterways
Utilization of inland waterways for cargo distribution is not a novel application in Europe. Rhine
and Danube rivers have been used for commerce and transportation of goods since the ancient
times. Their signiﬁcance as trade routes is evident if one takes a look at the number and the pres-
tige of the cities built on the rivers and their basins. The two rivers are linked together through
small waterways and both of them cross several countries, creating a natural transportation net-
work in central Europe. Nowadays, the EU waterways network is approximately 37.000km, ex-
tended in 13 member states [23] and it is primarily utilized for transportation of bulk and ha-
zardous cargo.
In 2019 the main type of cargo distributed by inland waterways (EU-27) was Metal ores and other
mining and quarrying products (156.964 tonnes in total), followed by Coke and reﬁned petroleum produ-
cts (87.749 tonnes in total) [36]. The need of transporting gas, oil, coal and iron ores across the EU
by IWW will decrease, as the greatest amounts of gas and oil will be distributed through pipelines,
while coal and iron ores demand is expected to decline [37]. Yet, new market segments, such as
certain ﬁnished steel products, steel scrap and construction materials are expected to develop and
expand, so IWW transportation can be a safe and cost-effective mode for their movement [37].
Furthermore, the fact that there is still space for more shuttles to travel in the EU waterways
brings up potentials of transporting more containerized cargo and trailers [37].
Though, integrating inland waterways in the EU transportation system is a considerable chal-
lenge for the supply chains. Currently, the logistics systems require quick delivery of goods with
a steady supply and should be capable of adapting quickly to demand changes. Vessels are not as
quick as trucks or trains, however they carry larger volumes and they are optimal for transporta-
tion of consolidated cargo. While navigating into a canal, it is possible that shuttles deliver cargo
in several ports, thus shipping results in being the most cost-effective mode. Transit times can be
a questionable issue, which depends on several factors, for instance on the ports’ infrastructure
and equipment availability. Safety of the cargo and the already low environmental footprint of
shuttles can make it up to the decision maker, especially when carbon free propulsion systems
will be widely utilized in maritime transportation.
2.3
Intermodal transportation
The aforementioned characteristics of the different modes of transportation indicate that there is
no ideal solution towards a totally de-carbonized transportation system across the EU by utilizing
only one type of cargo movement mode. Furthermore, shifting from road to rail or water-based
transportation is not always possible due to geographical restrictions. An efﬁcient combination of
the various transportation modes is the key to success, namely intermodal transportation. That
is to say, modal shift will be the lever towards a sustainable freight transportation system across
EU. Along with the technological improvements that allow trucks, trains and vessels to become
environmentally neutral, the EU has the opportunity to eliminate emissions, improve the member
states’ ﬁnancial performance and reduce societal impacts.

Today, an EU target is that by 2030, 30% of cargo transported by road over 300km should have
shifted to rail or water-based transportation [38]. This target not only aims to reducing GHG
emissions but also decreasing road accidents, noise pollution and cutting down costs. Taking into
account the pros and cons of the transportation modes described previously, it would be fair to
consider that a functional intermodal transportation system can have a signiﬁcant impact on the
EU sustainability targets. Nevertheless, before making a decision into realizing modal shift, there
should be a thorough investigation on each case, as it is not guaranteed that intermodal freight
transportation will indeed be beneﬁcial for all types of cargo and routes.
Figure 2.6 shows the transportation chain of an arriving container in EU by sea transportation
[37]. The intention is that modal shift occurs for the “last-mile” transportation in the European
territory. After the cargo arrives at an EU hub port (port where both deep and short sea shipping is
scheduled [39]), it is transported by trains or vessels as close to the ﬁnal destination as possible, for
the greatest part of the “last mile” route. Road transportation is utilized for the last kilometers of
this route if needed, in order to link the cargo destination to the corresponding train station or port.
The later transportation part is called post-haulage [40]. For water-based inland transportation,
the aforementioned means that a network of ports in the hinterland - with proper facilities - should
be established [16].

become imperative where possible, there are several practical and mindset obstacles to tackle
in order that modal shift is effective. The most critical constrain in order to realize intermodal
transportation is that it should be accompanied with investments in infrastructure, so as for the
rail and/or maritime network to be competitive against road freight transportation [41]. The hubs
where modal shift will take place should also have the appropriate cargo handling equipment
and their location should be easily accessed by all relevant modes. Another reason why shippers

at least for the "last mile" transportation [41].
Furthermore, current supply chains depend on fast and on-time delivery [42]. Particularly water-

2.4
Levers towards sustainability
As the EU is moving towards a decarbonized future, reshaping of the EU cargo transportation
system cannot only rely on intermodal shift and changes in the logistics chains. Innovations that
will improve the environmental performance of transportation means and relevant infrastructure
are necessary levers. Navigation in waterways seems to be the most promising transportation
mode concerning all three sustainability aspects; social, ﬁnancial and environmental. Thus, novel
applications that enhance vessels’ efﬁciency should be considered, legislated and introduced as
quickly as possible in the EU transportation system.
The most important innovation that can address decarbonization directly is the low-carbon and
zero-carbon fuels, combined with the development of relevant engines, new propulsion systems
and changes in vessels design. As fuels constitute the greatest cost of a shipment, the motivation
on ﬁnding cost-effective green propulsion systems and alternative fuels is strong [44]. The latest

Liqueﬁed Natural Gas (LNG) is already utilized by several maritime operators who have retroﬁtted
their vessels or have ordered new shuttles with proper engines for LNG consumption. The LNG
CO2 emissions are 20-25% lower compared to conventional liquid fuels, as its carbon content is
lower. However, the methane slip throughout the whole LNG life cycle (production, transporta-
tion and consumption) is a considerable drawback that urges the sector into experimenting with
other propulsion ways [45].
Hydrogen is the most appealing alternative fuel, since when consumed it emits a minor amount
of NOx and no CO2 and SOx. That is to say, no additional applications on vessels are necessary,
for example sulfur scrubbers. Nevertheless, hydrogen today is mainly produced by reforming
natural gas, which results in emitting 9-12 tonnes CO2 per ton of hydrogen produced [45]. Water
electrolysis is also a way of producing hydrogen, however it can only be environmental friendly
if the large amounts of electricity needed for this process is produced from renewable energy.
For this reason, several Danish companies are collaborating in order to create the ﬁrst electrolysis
facility that will sustainably produce alternative fuels for buses, trucks, vessels and airplanes, at
industrial scale [46]. Still, there are limitations on using hydrogen in maritime transportation, as,
==PAGE:29==

in liquid phase, its storage requires more space than conventional fuels, very low temperatures
and is highly volatile [45].
Ammonia is another alternative fuel that has potentials in the maritime sector. The advantage of
ammonia compared to hydrogen is that its storage in liquid phase is less complex [45]. Again,
the production process is the one burdening the fuel’s environmental footprint, since it ﬁrst re-
quires isolation of nitrogen and hydrogen and later their combination. Hence, only if produced
by utilizing energy from renewable sources can ammonia be a sustainable fuel [44]. Compared to
hydrogen, methanol is also easier to handle as a fuel; yet, the two fuels have the same production
process, so the methane slip problem rises again, along with safety issues and the fact that it is
highly toxic [45]. Lastly, ethanol is also thought to be an alternative fuel, though, it is produced by
organic biomass and waste, which causes several environmental impacts, for example deforesta-
tion [45]. Moreover, ethanol’s quality depends on the resources and the processes utilized, while
the current ethanol quantities produced are not enough to cover the needs of the shipping sector
[47].
Improvements have also been made in the shuttles propulsion systems, allowing for less impact-
ful voyages. Electric engines are mainly utilized today in vehicles, yet some short-sea shipping
vessels are powered by batteries [44]. Especially when the electricity is sourced sustainably, bat-
teries constitute a non-emitting energy provider and research on how to increase the batteries
capacity while being cost-effective is conducted globally [44]. Electric engines on vessels can also
be supplied with electricity generated from fuel cells on board. Fuel cells are electrochemical cells
that transform the chemical energy released when redox reactions between fuels and oxygen take
place into electricity, without combustion [47]. The most common fuel used in fuel cells is hydro-
gen, without eliminating the possibility of the aforementioned fuels to be utilized.
Evidently, there are still numerous limitations for the R&Ds to overcome, in order that the ma-
ritime industry widely introduces alternative fuels and novel propulsion systems. Nonetheless,
trials on shuttles have already started and the results of the real case applications are highly im-
portant for the research. Alternative fuels technologies require more time and more investments
to ﬁnd optimal solutions, thus until they become environmentally efﬁcient and cost-effective, the
maritime sector should put efforts on implementing the Market Based and Logistics’ Measures.
This will probably inﬂuence cargo movement in the EU, but for the moment it seems unavoidable.
Other radical innovations towards more sustainable maritime transportation systems is adjusting
and integrating principles of the newest manufacturing and industry applications in the shipping
sector. As we are in the era of the Fourth Industrial Revolution, supply chains across the globe
should be able to serve the needs of modern markets. Digitalization is one of the key elements
leading industries to become faster, more efﬁcient, more cost-effective and indirectly more sus-
tainable. Transportation and logistics systems have started integrating the potentials big data,
internet of things (IoT), autonomous robots and machine learning provide, bringing in new ways
of interacting with the markets. As the maritime sector is the greatest contributor in transporting
goods, moving towards Shipping 4.0 is a necessity.

Remote control of vessels and cargo handling equipment on shuttles and ports can decrease hand-

and can potentially create a novel, competitive transportation system [49]. Considering that hu-
man factor is responsible for 80% of the accidents in sea [51], human errors leading to incidents
and/or accidents can only be minimized - not eliminated -, so unmanned shuttles will suffer fewer
accidents, and there will be no casualties. Furthermore, there will be no requirements for accom-
modation facilities, thus no additional power needed from auxiliary engines [49]. This will lead
into lowering the environmental footprint of the vessel.
Nevertheless, there are certain drawbacks and limitations regarding autonomous shuttles. There
are many cases when seafarers prevent navigation accidents or react to an accident or an incident,
focusing on protecting the vessel, the cargo, the surrounding areas and human lives [52]. That is to
say, automated vessels should be equipped with reliable control systems that will guarantee safety.
Unmanned ships also have a social impact, as jobs related to maritime transportation - especially
seafarers’ job positions - will be endangered. Furthermore, digital threats are rapidly increasing
and it is inevitable that supply chains will suffer cyber-attacks, so operators should be ready to
efﬁciently encounter those. Legal and insurance matters are also a tricky issue to deal with in case
autonomy is introduced in maritime transportation. All in all, autonomous applications on vessels
and ports have several advantages related to more sustainable transportation systems, however
crucial issues as safety and levels of human involvement are constraints still under discussion.

3.2
Roll-on/roll-off and Lift-on/lift-off
3.2.1
Ro-Ro
Ro-Ro is a method of loading and unloading a ship by which the cargo is rolled on and off the
vessel. Such loading is feasible due to the design of ships (also known as Ro-Ro vessels), which
are ﬁtted with ramps. Built-in ramps enable transported trucks and vehicles to drive on and off
the deck or to be pulled within. Ro-Ro shuttles provide efﬁcient loading and transportation of
wheeled freight as well as large and complicated freight. In comparison to Lo-Lo, the possibility
of cargo to be damaged while being moved on/off the ship is lower. The ﬁrst ship of its sort in
history sailed the seas and oceans approximately 70 years ago, in the 1950s. The history of Polish
Ro-Ro freight ships, for example, stretches back to the early 1970s.

3.2.2
Lo-Lo
Lo-Lo focuses on vertical rather than horizontal loading. Lo-Lo vessels are eqiuped with on-board
cranes capable of moving containers. Cargo is loaded using the cranes or other cargo handling
equipment on board, as well as dockside cranes. This loading method is most commonly em-
ployed in transcontinental and cabotage shipping. Furthermore, Lo-Lo shuttles have a lot of room
accessible from the top of the vessel and on the ship deck. Lo-Lo delivery is slower than Ro-Ro
but more environmentally friendly [60].

Cargo handling
Loading and unloading goods, transporting, storage and picking up cargo are all examples of
cargo handling, sorting, and picking. These duties have a signiﬁcant impact on logistical produ-
ctivity and quality. Containers are the most often carried cargo type in the world, as they can be
transported by all three modes of transportation, land, air and sea. The aspect of cargo handling
of various sizes becomes extremely important as they are subjected to harsh weather and high
winds in the middle of the sea.
The following are some of the most essential container freight handling equipment:
• Straddle carriers for container transfer on berth are among the port’s equipment.
• Tractors and trailers/semi-trailers for supplemental hauling and mobility within a terminal
area.
• Stacker cranes and side loaders are used to help in freight handling.
• During cargo operations, the cargo handling equipment utilized for ships is gantry cranes
on the pier.
• Container handling is a common use for gantry cranes. These cranes have spreaders that
can be modiﬁed to accommodate 20ft, 40ft, and 45ft containers.
• Some ships are equipped with cranes that may also be utilized for container handling. These
cranes are driven by professional and experienced operators. [61]

offer an insight into any hot spots and emission trends in the port over time [69].

the North Sea, creating the Rhine-Meuse-Scheldt delta located in the Netherlands, close to the
Belgium borders.
The two countries host several intergovernmental organizations and they are signiﬁcant hubs for
cargo transportation from and to Europe. Rotterdam, located in the Netherlands, is the busiest
port in Europe, and one of the greatest hub ports in the world. The second busiest European
port is Antwerp, in Belgium. Evidently, reshaping of the EU transportation system shall include
the aforementioned ports, as the greatest incoming amounts of cargo are handled there. Freight
transportation through the inland waterways is already well developed, but there is still space for
more cargo to be distributed via waterways. Thus, this region is ideal for the purposes of AEGIS
and this is why it was chosen for one of the program’s real life use case (WP9).
The leader of WP9 is DFDS A/S, a Danish shipping company established in 1866. They provide
cargo shipping, passenger and container routes, as well as logistic solutions for freight delivery

5.3
Work Package 9 - Alternative 2
The target of WP9 is to create an effective inland waterways transportation system that will link
hub ports in the Netherlands and Belgium, so that cargo is transported by vessels instead of trucks.
This water-based inland transportation system should be competitive against the current road
freight transportation one, in order that the congested road network in the Low Countries area is
relieved [7]. Moreover, the new transportation chain should have minimum or zero emissions, as
the EU requirements for a decarbonized continent by 2050 oblige. In order to attain this, a new,
autonomous vessel with zero emission propulsion system is under development.
==PAGE:46==
Chapter 5. Problems’ description and available data
36
WP9 is the 2nd alternative studied in Problem B, in which we came up with the appropriate
assumptions in order to have tangible results. “Last mile” haulage of trailers will be conducted by
inland waterways and road. Figure A.5 illustrates the WP9 proposed transportation chain. As in
Alternative 1, Euroliner trailers will arrive at hub ports by Ro-Ro or Ro-Pax shuttles and then, they
will be loaded in autonomous Ro-Ro vessels. The plan is that the loading and unloading processes
relevant to the inland Ro-Ro shuttle will be performed by the “Vera” vehicle (described in section
5.3.3). When the trailers reach the destination port, the customers are responsible to pick up their
trailers and transport them most probably by road to their facilities. Similar to Alternative 1, we
consider that the “last mile” transportation starts when the trailers are loaded into the autonomous
Ro-Ro vessel.
Nevertheless, we should take into account that not all trailers departing from hub ports can be
distributed by intermodal transportation. This is attributed to two reasons. Firstly, the route the
trailer should follow from the hub port to the customer depends on the end destination. If the
customer’s facilities are not in a sufﬁcient distance to a hinterland hub port (such as Antwerp,
or Ghent), then inland waterways transportation will probably not be selected for the speciﬁc
freight delivery. Secondly, whether a trailer will be moved in the hinterland by a second vessel
also depends on the shippers’ or the customers’ requirements. Delivery time, reliability and time-
consuming bureaucratic procedures are some factors that inﬂuence the decision maker to choose
road transportation.
That is to say, assuming that all trailers that arrive at the hub ports are later distributed by IWW
is not realistic. Moreover, since the AEGIS scenario is not yet implemented, there are no data or
forecasts on how many trailers will be distributed by road haulage or by inland transportation.
Thus, we introduce parameter i , which is deﬁned as:
i: percentage (0-100%) of overall trailers departing from each hub port that will continue
travelling to the end destination by inland waterways.
This parameter will be used in the simulation we developed in Python Programming Language.
When running, the simulation calculates the number of trailers distributed in the hinterland
through IWW and provides results for the KPIs that depend on the number of voyages. Fol-
lowing, information about the routes, the new autonomous Ro-Ro vessel and the novel cargo
handling concept are presented.

Evidently, this factor is important especially for the equipment required and the handling times.
It is worth mentioning that the efﬁciency and capability of ports to handle the incoming trailers
can be an inﬂuential factor when choosing intermodal or road transportation for inland freight
distribution. However, this determinant will not be investigated in this thesis.

predeﬁned (short) routes. It is currently utilized by Volvo in Gothenburg, in order to link the
port with a warehouse facility of DFDS close to the port [101]. Our proposal is that such a tractor
is onboard the Ro-Ro and performs loading and unloading of trailers in all ports. The vehicles’
supervision can be conducted by the control room monitoring the autonomous Ro-Ro vessel.
FIGURE 5.6: The "Vera" automated vehicle. Source: Cargo Partner webpage [102]
This solution has several advantages. Firstly, trailers can be shipped unaccompanied, so truck
drivers won’t be required on board, eliminating occupational hazards. Furthermore, the ship-
ping cost of a single trailer will be lower compared to transporting a trailer and their tractor.
Additionally, a dedicated “Vera” vehicle in each shuttle will be able to handle trailers transported
regardless the cargo handling equipment of the ports to be called. The only requirements the ports
must fulﬁl is that they have a ramp for the vessels’ stern door and that they create and keep clear
outlined corridors for the vehicle to move along. So, the overall cargo handling costs come down
to the initial investment for the vehicles and the electricity needed for their movement.
Nevertheless, loading and unloading times can be an issue, as only one autonomous tractor will
perform these tasks. Furthermore, "Vera" will probably be slower than the human-driven terminal
tractors. Assuming a 30% additional time to the current loading or discharging procedures, that
is to say that it will need 8 minutes to either load or unload a trailer. So, berthing time of each
autonomous Ro-Ro will be more than 2,8h, when 21 trailers (maximum capacity) are to be loaded
or discharged. Since the vessels will most probably have two ramps (one at the bow and one at

6.2.3
Comparison of Alternatives 1 and 2
As mentioned in Chapter 4, there are KPIs that do not depend on the power supply system of the
new, autonomous Ro-Ro vessel, neither on the number of trailers to be transported via IWW or
road network. Thus, these KPIs can be directly benchmarked, so the comparison will start with
these indexes.
The payload capacity per shipment (F1) is obviously greater when utilizing a vessel than when
delivering trailers by trucks. This is an advantage of maritime transportation, especially when
consolidated cargo is to be distributed. As the vessel carries more cargo and has a more powerful
propulsion system that the truck’s engine, the vessels energy consumption (F2) is greater. Au-
tonomy (F6) is one of the AEGIS objectives and by implementing the WP9 proposal, the target is
achieved. The current trucks distributing cargo in Europe are human-driven (Degree of Auton-
omy: none), while the vessel will be either remote-controlled (IMO Degree of Autonomy: Three)
or only supervised (IMO Degree of Autonomy: Four). Noise (E6) is also an index not dependent
on the power supply system. While trucks can have a maximum of 79db acoustic emissions, ves-
sels have minimum or none. Moreover, the probability of fatal accidents (S1) related to freight
distribution in the Flanders are minimized (if not eliminated) in case autonomous Ro-Ro vessels
will be introduced in the transportation chain.
Nevertheless, the greatest disadvantage of inland navigation is the sailing (and overall voyage)
time required (F4). The calculations based on the proposed vessel speciﬁcations indicate that -
per 100km - the sailing time of a shuttle will be twice as long as the drive time of a truck. When
adding the average loading and discharging times of a trailer the overall voyage time seems totally
unappealing (Table 6.11). Moreover, even though we were not able to ﬁnd the potential delays
(due to waterways congestion, speed limits, delayed cargo and more), they should also be taken
into account. Loading / discharging times (F5) are also thought to be long when utilizing the
"Vera" autonomous vehicle. For the ports efﬁciency, this is not as optimal, because delays can
cause several problems in the ports operations.

7 Conclusions
Herein, a preliminary analysis on the sustainability performance of the AEGIS WP9 proposal of
distributing trailers into the hinterland through inland waterways compared to the current road
haulage was performed. Additionally, an evaluation of what the potential power supply system
for the new, autonomous Ro-Ro vessel can be and their performance was carried out. The compa-
risons were performed by benchmarking Key Performance Indicators for ﬁnancial, environmental
and social aspects. The results of the investigation are highly interesting and show how complex
it is to compromise all requirements for cost-effectiveness, performance efﬁciency and environ-
mental competence of a novel "last mile" transportation chain.
The comparisons among the power supply system of the vessels showed that a hydrogen-powered
shuttle has the best performance regarding the life cycle emissions and has competitive advan-
tages, yet the battery-powered vessel is slightly more cost-effective. For the completion of the
thesis questions, we chose the hydrogen-powered shuttle as the most optimal. As this is a pre-
liminary analysis that can only provide indications, further investigation on the newest and best
propulsion and power supply technologies should be made. Furthermore, there should be com-
prehensive theoretical and use case studies on the sustainable production of hydrogen and elec-
tricity, in order to conclude into the best option. Their cost is also a subject to consider.
The KPIs benchmarking showed that the AEGIS proposal performs better in most of the indexes
examined herein. Even though inland navigation will cost more than road haulage, the emissions
averted when utilizing the inland waterways are signiﬁcant. Nevertheless, regardless the volumes
to be transported, the costs and the emissions, the greatest drawback of the AEGIS proposal is that
it will take more time to distribute freight through inland waterways compared to road haulage
delivery. Unless signiﬁcant changes are introduced, it seems that sailing time for the trailers to
be distributed through inland waterways will need at least twice the drive time a truck currently
needs. Inland navigation has to overcome several administrative and regulatory obstacles, as well
as practical bottlenecks in the waterways. Speed limits, ports’ and waterways’ congestion are
issues to be addressed in the future, as they can signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the voyage times. The
decision maker can be compensated for the delay by the fact that cargo transportation is more
environmentally friendly.
Our suggestions for the AEGIS community would be that comprehensive studies for the environ-
mental and ﬁnancial performance of the vessels are carried out. Furthermore, the delivery times
issue should be checked into thoroughly. While most of the targets set by the AEGIS are fulﬁlled,
yet the logistics demands cannot be fully met right now. The investigations the AEGIS partners

they work more closely in order to conclude into solutions that will enhance the logistics chains.
