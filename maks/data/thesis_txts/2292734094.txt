The world we are living in is changing rapidly. Especially, the climate change is important for build­
ing optimisation and indoor climate. This aspect of the global warming has given significant impact 
to the pattern of energy consumption of buildings that increase the cooling demand.
According to recent COP21 in Paris, the impact of the global warming significant that raises the av­
erage temperature of 1.5 [°C] in the last decade.
The thesis took a starting point in the climate change, analysing the weather data from Danish design 
reference years (TRY, DRY95, DRY13) and 20 years of latest actual weather data where located in 
near Lyndby. As a consequence, over 50 years of the weather data was analysed. The analysed weath­
er parameters were dry-bulb temperature, relative humidity and solar radiation. These weather data 
was used to create the future weather forecasting in 2020 and 2050. Because, 2020 and 2050 are the 
benchmark year among Danish building regulation. The Danish government set 2020 for as ‘net-zero’ 
energy consumption.
As a result of the weather analysis, cooling demand will raise 30% and heating demand will drop 11% 
in 2050, and in 2020 cooling will raise 9% and heating will drop to 7%.
Lyngby store center was one of the oldest shopping centre in Denmark which was build in 1973. 
Currently it is claimed too much energy consumption of the lighting. In order to optimise the building 
energy consumption, similar case study from Sweden was referred. A cross-checking method, that is 
used in the case study was applied for this thesis.
For the computer energy simulation, IDA-ICE was used. IDA-ICE was great tool for the building 
energy simulation. The computer simulation was carried out through the process of the building 
optimisation in this thesis. Several problems are identified in the calibration process. In order to op­
timise these several problems, some options were tested and studied. The options are mainly focused 
on night ventilation strategy, airflow rate adjustment, replacement of LED and upgrade the building 
envelope.
Consequently, building energy consumption was reduced over 50% comparing with the latest energy 
consumption. Moreover indoor climate achieved class II of EN15251.
Finally the future weather forecasting data, which was created in the early chapter, was applied for 
the optimal strategy, and evaluated for the future energy consumption pattern. This evaluation would 
show the change of building energy consumption pattern in the future. In the conclusion, the theses 
address how to adapt the future energy consumption pattern to LSC.

The world we are living in is changing rapidly. Especially, the climate change has a great impact on 
the building energy consumption and indoor climate that need to be addressed. 
According to recent COP21 in Paris, the impact of the global warming is significant. The average 
temperature raised 1.5  [°C]   in the last decade. And will raise more. This means, the cooling con­
sumption will increase in the future. COP21 stated all the issues needed to be addressed as a matter 
of urgency, which is stated in the agreement between the attended world 195 countries. In summary, 
there is an increasing need to deliver a holistic engineering approach and integrated solution in order 
to achieve a low energy consuming society.
Regarding the climate change, it is an inevitable problem the world faces today. The impact of the cli­
mate changes is still not certain. However, many scientists believe that the climate change will lead to 
more extreme weather, which means hotter and wetter summer during the warm period. At the same 
time, the winter becomes warmer (Kalvelage, et al 2014).
In addition, the climate changes are happing rapidly. Due to research, “eleven of the last twelve years 
(1995-2006) are ranked among the 12 warmest years in the instrumental record of global surface 
temperature (Since 1850). Meanwhile average Northern Hemisphere temperatures during the second 
half of the 20th century are very likely higher than during any other 50-year period in the last 500 
years and likely the highest in at least the past 1300 years” (Zhu.M, 2013).
The climate changes leading to more extreme weather will impact the energy consumption of the fu­
ture. At the current status of energy consumption, research showed that in Europe, buildings account 
for 40–45% of total energy consumption, contributing to a significant amount of CO2 emissions. 
(Energy Star, 2008) CO2 emissions are a great factor for increasing greenhouse gas (GHG). GHG 
concentrations in the atmosphere will continue to increase unless the global fossil-energy consump­
tion decreases considerably. 
Observation of climate changes is providing clearer evidence that climate change is a response to hu­
man activities. Increasing atmosphere concentration of GHG increases Earth’s average temperature 
(Kashif, et al 2013). GHG and increasing average temperature, this two connections will change the 
pattern of the energy consumption of the buildings, that cooling demand would increase. The climate 
change would reduce energy consumption in cold climates by approximately 10%, while energy use 
in tropical climates would increase by more than 20% (Kalvelage, et al 2014). In consequence, GHG 
cause a problem for the human future. According to Kharseh (2014), 33% of the global GHG emis­
sions are originally, building heating ventilation and air conditioning (HVAC) systems. Therefore, 
controlling HVAC system will make a great impact in order to reduce of GHG.

The focus of this thesis is to optimise energy consumption and indoor climate of Lyngby Storcenter 
(LSC), so the centre is adapting to the future climate change. LSC is one of the oldest shopping centre 
buildings in Denmark, which where located in Lyngby city, 15km north of Copenhagen. The con­
crete structured building was built in 1973. Since the building was erected, the building has carried 
out major renovation works for HVAC system between 1995-1998. As a result, LSC has installed 17 
different HVAC systems, which were installed in different years that work fuel gas for heating and 
electricity for cooling. Currently, it has a high-energy consumption of lighting and a poor indoor cli­
mate, which an analysis in this project will show. 
For the optimisation of the indoor climate and energy consumption, a yearly dynamic simulation 
was carried out using computer energy simulation soft ware called IDA-ICE. The IDA-ICE model 
was created through a calibration process. For the calibration of the building model, similar case 
study from Stensson (2015) was referred. Stensson used a cross-checking method. The cross-check­
ing method would be described in a later chapter, but it is basically a double check with the theory 
and real measurements. It was a challenge to integrate the optimal theory into a real project. I faced 
immediately of a complexity of integration between the theory and the real project, because I had to 
deal with uncertain parameters in LSC. It is quoted from Stensson (2015) that: “although sustain­
able technologies have been extensively discussed in literature, the available data mainly concerns 
strategies, methods, and hypothetical evaluations, while lacking in most cases the reference to actual 
experimental data and on-field achieved results”. 
In my case I also faced this problem; the theory lacking reference to the real project. In order to 
achieve the goal and obtain valid data, the architecture model, energy consumption monitoring record 
from CTS, real measurements on the site and engineering reports, which are provided by the several 
engineering firms for this project, are used for the cross-checking method.
The calibrated model was beneficial to identify the problems that need to be addressed in order to find 
the optimal strategies.
For the optimal strategies several holistic options were tested and studied, which included the pos­
sibility of introducing night ventilation system, emphasising of natural ventilation, replacement of 
LED light, HVAC system control adjustment of the airflow rate and renovation of the glazing facade 
based on the latest building regulation. In the first stage, the optimal strategies are formulated into 
8 different options in order to evaluate the impact of each option. In the second stage, these optimal 
strategies were combined together to formulate the final optimal strategy. The final optimal strategies 
are formulated in order to focus on to night ventilation, airflow adjustment and the improvement of 
the building envelope.
And finally the final optimal strategy was assessed with future weather data in the third stage.

As written in the introduction, climate changes will most likely impact our future energy consump­

will affect the energy consumption, it is essential to discuss more fundamental what kind of climate 
changes we can expect in the near future, before we look into the future energy simulation.
It is not an easy answer, because the conclusions about the future climate vary among scientists. The 
climate changes are complex. It is determined not only by human’s activities as green house gas 
(GHG), but there are also external factors, that have got influence on the climate change, for example 
volcanism and plate tectonics (DeConto, R M, 2009).
This means that the impact of climate change is different in different locations. However, it is a com­
mon understanding among many researchers that the average temperature will raise. There are for 
instance some predictions published by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) that 
indicates an increase in the global average surface temperature in different scenario ranges of 1.1–2.9 
°C to 2.4–6.4 °C from a 1990s baseline towards the end of the 21st century (IPCC, 2007).
One study from Hong-Kong, which where located in a sub-tropical area, concluded that the average 
annual cooling load during the 2009–2100 period would be 6.1% and 9.8% more than that during

There has been debated much about the importance of the future energy simulation in recent years, 
since there has been developed several energy simulations software to predict building performance 
in term of energy consumption and indoor climate. It is said that the importance of the future energy 
simulation is increasing especially in the early stage of building project. The result of the early stage

material choice, thermal mass and building services (CIBSE, 2005) (Holmes & Hacker, 2007). It is 
recognised that there will be a significant climate change due to global warming in the coming de­
cades, and therefore the energy and thermal performance of buildings under future climatic condition 
should be investigated and evaluated. Moreover, new buildings should be designed and constructed 
with effective measures and environmental features to cope with the impact of climate change (Chan

In order to carry out the future energy simulation in this thesis, over 50 years of weather data in Den­
mark was analysed. Based on the study of these weather data, future weather forecast was created. 
The method creating future weather forecast was called ‘Extrapolating statistical method that using 
degree-days’. This method is one of the simplest methods to create future weather forecasting. The 
detail how to create the forecasting is described in later chapters.
When using weather data, it is crucial that the data is from a trustable source. The weather data should 
be precisely and correctly selected and it should be as close as the area, where the project is.  Danish 
Meteorological Institute (DMI), which is used for this project, is one of the authorities that provides 
trustable weather data and their weather station was located near LSC. And the weather data mea­
sured in DTU is also used, which is a trustable source as well.
It is certain that the future energy simulation is important for the optimisation, however the future 
energy simulation is still an on-going subject. The future is unpredictable. Holmes and Hacker (2007) 
mentioned the risk of future energy simulation “However, the current building simulations were car­
ried out based on the past measured weather data that was recorded near the project area normally. 
These building simulations were only based on historical weather data that cannot analysis the poten­
tial impacts of the climate change. (CIBSE 2005), Therefore they tend to failure to reduce the energy 
consuming and unacceptable indoor climate. Furthermore, this type of design approach adds to the 
running costs of a building as architectural failures are compensated by energy consuming mechani­
cal equipment”

Our natural resources are running out. Especially, in the building industry who is the main consumer

specialists.

One of the key roles for future engineering is communication I believe. It would be essential to share 
knowledge and understand specialised knowledge from each project members. Also all the knowl­

to take communication from different hierarchy. The Project manager, facility manager, and each

The Danish building regulation is important for this thesis, because the building regulation set a 
framework for lower energy consumption which apply for LSC as well. The building regulations 
contain rules for construction of private and commercial buildings in Denmark. The regulation is pri­
marily based on functional requirements. It has been announced that the existing version of the Dan­
ish building regulations BR10, will be replaced by the new BR15, which enter into force and become 
compulsory on the 1th of July 2016. The first half of the 2016 is determined as a transition period, 
during which the BR15 will be voluntary. This thesis will adapt with the latest BR15 requirements. 
Especially, the energy frame is an issue, that will be described in this chapter. 
In the latest issue of the 21st Conference of Parties to the United Nations Framework Convention on 
Climate Change, in short COP21, the energy framework was discussed (COP21, 2015). In COP21 
tighten the requirements of the energy framework in order to reduce the energy to adapt with the cli­
mate change.
 In COP21,(2015) the global business leaders agreed to have a long-term provision to energy frame 
to achieve ‘net-zero’ emissions by 2050. In addition, the European Union energy policy gives highly 
requirements for the energy frame that required reducing 80-95% compared with 1990s figure in 
2020. Denmark is the only country in Europe trying to achieve ‘net-zero’ energy consumption, which 
require energy consumption of 25 [kwh/m2] for commercial buildings, therefore, the new BR15 has 
become a benchmark in order to achieve the long-term goals.
The energy frame of new building regulations requires lower energy consumption. The BR15 low 
energy class will have approx. 40% lower energy consumption than the existing requirements of the 
BR10.

5.4 Internal gains
In LSC, the main internal heat loads can be considered in three elements, which are lights, equipment 
and humans. Lighting and most equipment heat loads are sensible heat, while the heat generated by 
human body’s metabolic rate is a combination of sensible and latent heat. In general, heat load from 
lighting and equipment is equal to their energy power, while heat from humans depends on people’s 
physical activity. 
Lights
Lighting is of a great importance for shopping centres, since it can drive the sales higher. In this case

that is used to power electric lights and for a building are often referred to “lighting power density” 
that is measured in [W/m2].

estimated from a sample of stores that is significantly huge. According to ASHRAE HAND BOOK

Occupancy
Building occupancy is a key parameter for the heat load to the energy simulation. The parameter in­

system that bring significant impact to the result of the energy consumption and thermal comfort. The 
occupant behaviour was investigated further in order to decide the heat load. The occupancy rises

the night time except for a few staffs. The occupant increases in the weekend. Moreover, during the 
Christmas season, occupancy rate reached to their max, which are almost double compared to the 
quiet season. In the appendix J, the actual/average occupancy rate was calculated based on the mea­

According to the report from Siemens (2015), major renovation works for plants were carried out 
between 1995-1998. In the LSC, there were 19 different HVAC systems, mostly Constant Air Volume 
(CAV) that makes it difficult to control indoor environment, because it can not adjust air volume in 
each shop. In the other areas they are using Variable Air Volume (VAV). In the selected area, all zones 
are installed HVAC system with VAV. Both systems measured the air temperature and CO2 level at

One of the advantages of this method is that the future weather forecast is based on the local weather 
data, since there are 2 decades of actual weather data available in the area of Lyngby and therefore it 
is possible to use the future forecast on LSC as it is located in Lyngby. Guan L (2008) stated this ad­
vantage of using local weather data: ’The method is essentially a single-measure steady- state method 
used for the estimation of building energy consumption. It is also a common approach adopted by the 
building industries to relate the trends of building energy consumption with local climate conditions.’
In order to apply this method on the case study, LSC, Danish reference years and actual weather data 
1996-2015 were used. Firstly, the number of degree-days for the heating (HDD) and cooling (CDD) 
were calculated in these weather data. The calculated HDD and CDD are summed up for the yearly 
values. Secondly, simple linear regression was used to extrapolate the number of degree-days in the 
future.  
Each year is used for the scalar predictor variable X, and the value of the degree-days is set for the 
scalar response variable Y. For the scalar predictor variable X the reference year is used, when it is 
published. For example, TRY set the scalar predictor variable as a published year 1975. DRY95 set it 
as a published year 1995, and DRY13 set it as a published year 2013. The actual weather data from 
1996 – 2015 sets the scalar predictor variable X, and the degree-days corresponded the year set as the 
scalar response variable Y. 
In this way it would be able to calculate the number of degree-days in the future date. The calculation 
was used to estimate the degree-days for 2020 and 2050. 2020 and 2050 are benchmark years for the 
Danish building regulation and COP21 agreement as Danish building regulation targets ‘net-zero’ 
energy consumption in 2020. Also, COP21 (2015) agreed to target the goal of zero emission for 2050.
Certainly, it is a very simple and fast way to evaluate the future energy consumption. However, there 
are several discussions towards the accuracy of the result. It is said that the energy estimates from 
the degree-day calculations can be fairly coarse, because the degree-day method is appropriate only 
if the building use and the efficiency of the HVAC equipment are constant (Guan.L 2008). Also, this 
method only considers the effect of dry bulb temperature; and therefore its application (in energy 
predictions) is also limited. It has been found that the use of annual wet bulb temperature CDD could 
be a more suitable indicator for the cooling load, than that based on the dry bulb temperature (Guan 
& Bannister 1995). However it is still useful to use the method. Since the weather data was focused 
on Lyngby area.
In order to bring accuracy to the result of the energy consumption in the LSC selected area, it is 
important to calibrate the building energy modelling as close to the real measurements as possible.

IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA-ICE) is a whole year dynamic multi zones simulation software 
for the assessment of indoor climate as well as energy consumptions of an entire building. The in­
door climate can be described by several parameters, which include temperature, humidity, and CO2 
moreover PMV and PPD. The software is well integrated with BIM, that possible to import 2D and 
3D CAD files and help to develop Industry Foundation Classes (IFC) models that are generated by 
Auto-CAD and Revit. The remarkable feature of IDA-ICE is the flexibility of Macro-features, that 
is transparency and clear to understand with no limitations of variables. For this simulation, 2D Au­
to-CAD data (dxf) is imported from architecture drawings, and used for the simulation.
For the IDA-ICE simulation, it is required to transform the weather data to a prn file, which is the for­
mat for IDA-ICE weather simulation. Firstly, it is essential to prepare the six weather variables, which 
is required for IDA-ICE. The weather parameters are 1: Numbers 1-8784. 2: Dry-Bulb temperature 
[°C]. 3: Relative Humidity [%]. 4: Wind direction [°]. 5: Wind speed [m/s]. 6:Direct normal radiation 
[W/m2] and 7: Diffused radiation on horizontal surface [W/m2]. The weather data were put in to an 
excel file and saved as prn file. The prn file should be applied for the IDA-ICE simulation.  
Since IDA-ICE requires of 8,684 hours of weather data for the yearly simulation, it is essential to 
create a leap year (29th of Feb). In order to create the extra day, the weather data of 28th of February 
were duplicated.

8.0 Discussion
In the below discussion the results of this project and the data and methods will be discussed
8.1 Weather data and climate change
For the process of creating a future weather forecasting, three different weather data were used: 
Danish design reference year, DTU measurements and DMI weather data. One thing to consider was 
that the DTU measurements and DMI weather data were at different locations. DTU weather data 
combined data from the DTU weather station and the weather station in Roskilde. Roskilde is locat­
ed about 30km west of Copenhagen, which means that measurements can differ slightly. Especially 
cloud cover and radiation are different in the two areas, which can lead to slightly different results for 
the global solar radiations. However, it was found that it was only in other parameters such as relative 
humidity and wind speed, the result differed, whereas when it came to dry bulb temperature, it was 
almost same result, which make it useable for the project to create future forecasting weather data by 
using degree-days.
As said, one of the theories concerning the climate change was that the summers have become hotter 
and wetter.(Reference) Also, winters have become warmer(reference) in the last two decades. The re­
sult of the project successfully showed the same result: that the summers are indeed becoming hotter 
and wetter, with an average increase in temperature of 0.083 degrees a year. Consequently the cooling 
demands would increase, according to the degree-days analysis, it will increase by 30% of cooling in 
2050. Moreover the winter has become warmer. The average HDD were reduced about 11%. How­
ever, it is important to stress that there are many discussions concerning the climate changes and the 
impact on building simulation, but these figures are still reliable, because they are based on over 50 
years of the weather data analysis. 
The result of the future energy consumption with future weather forecasting for 2020 and 2050 
showed moderate change of the climate towards building energy simulation. 
Currently LSC is using fuel for the heating, which will be replaced by district heating in 2016. The 
district heating is regarded a sustainable and clean heating resource which and is therefore preferable. 
When it comes to the cooling, you have to use electricity, which is regarded as an expensive source 
– according to COP it was 2.5. The degree-days calculation concluded that the cooling demand will 
increase. Therefore it is recommended to prepare for the increasing electric demands as mentioned in 
this discussion under paragraph “the Seasonally adjustment of airflow rate”
==PAGE:96==
96
8.2 BIM
The importance of the use of BIM is increasing dramatically in the current building industry. BIM can 
be used in many aspects of the industry, including dynamic energy simulation, in this project, BIM 
was only used for the early stage of the modelling process, but it could be interesting for LSC to use 
it to a larger extent, where it is also focus on wider disciplines as well. For the purpose of creating a 
model for this project, the basic footprint was imported from dxf file by Auto CAD. The Auto CAD 
data was drawn in 2D and extruded to the 3D model for the energy simulation. Therefore, windows, 
doors and several details were separately input after the creation of the energy simulation model. As 
a consequence, there is a small difference between the original architectural model and the energy 
simulation model. Importing the dxf file to IDA-ICE was a very straightforward process without any 
difficulties. IDA-ICE is a great building energy simulation tool which can import from many other 
building models, including Revit. It could be interesting if LSC would use the Revit model, because 
Revit is a great tool for using BIM, and gives very accurate data, which can be shared by many disci­
plines that are part of a project. 
8.3 Cross-checking method and calibration
The calibration model was created based on the cross-checking method. The purpose of this cross 
checking method was to eliminate uncertain factors as far as possible, so the model could get as close 
to the real building character as possible.
At the end of the simulation, the final model was quite close to the measurements on the minenergi.
dk. However, some issues occurred during the calibration process. Firstly, almost all the documents 
were written in Danish. As a non-Danish speaker, I had to translate them into English using Google 
translate in order to understand the context. This was a long process. There was also a risk that I 
would miss some information due to the translation. To make sure that I got everything as correct as 
possible, I did some measuring myself, for instance of several key parameters, indoor temperature 
being on example. Secondly, much information was missing from the beginning, and no one had clear 
answers. Especially, the information at minenergi.dk was not clear. This BMS system measures many 
parameters, however, the names of the parameters were not always clear, which meant that the mea­
surements were not always clear. Therefore, it was a challenge to carry out the simulation, many fig­
ures being uncertain, but at the end of the calibration process, the result was compared with the mea­
surements and I could figure out what minenergi.dk was saying. Thirdly, we measured some building 
parameters ourselves. In the process of this measuring, some differences were found between the real 
measurements and figures from the document and Honeywell. However, a crosschecking method was 
used on the basis of several sources (drawings, documents, Honeywell, minenergi.dk, interviewing, 
own measurements, regulations and meetings) to make sure the numbers were correct.

ate the model, if the area of LSC, which we had to measure, had been clear from the beginning. The 
selected zone that constituted the area used for this project was divided into two groups: 204A, B, C 
and 202,203. These two groups are attached together with the corridor. 
In the theory, the energy simulation model should have a clear purpose, a balanced model as simple 
as possible and as precise as necessary to accurately reproduce real world conditions. () However, in 
reality, It was required to change the focus of the modelling several times, because the area of LSC 
was not clear from the beginning. IDA-ICE is a great simulation tool, but in this case it was very com­
plicated to go from a broad perspective (the large area of LSC) into details (a smaller area of LSC). 
8.6 Leap year 
IDA-ICE requires 8784 hours of hourly data for the yearly simulation. Therefore 29th of February 
was created by copying the weather data of 28th of February in order to carry out the yearly simula­
tion. For the calibration, the actual weather data 2015 was used in order to add leap day for February, 
which measns that the actual energy consumption was different due to one extra day compared with 
the result of the simulation.
8.7 Occupant behaviour
The occupant rate that was used in this project was based on the record, which was monitored at the 
entrances of LSC in October 2015. The result of the measurements was very close to the recommend­
ed value from the EN15251. Therefore measurements numbers was used for the input of IDA-ICE. 
However, it is quite likely that there are different occupants rates in different shops. Moreover, the 
season would affect to the occupant rate, for example December when Christmas season comes, it 
would be a larger occupant rate than the other months.
Since occupants behavior in buildings has a strong impact on the energy consumption and is an im­
portant factor for energy waste reduction, it would be an important further study to investigate the 
impact of the occupant rate in different seasons.
8.8 Energy simulation in IDA-ICE
The yearly dynamic simulation that was carried out in IDA-ICE took many hours to complete and 
used a lot of gigabites on the computer. It was essential to carry out many simulations to adjust the 
model for the purpose of this project. Therefore, in the early stage of the project, degree-days were 
used with one-day energy simulation in IDA-ICE, because it was simpler.  There are some differences 
in the results between the yearly dynamic simulation and hand calculation based on degree-days. The 
hand calculation based on degree-days is not accurate and was not used ofr the final result. However, 
it gave some adequate directions to the simulation.

8.9 The result of the dynamic simulation and stage 1 and 2.
LED and Adjustment of the airflow rate and supply air temperature from AHU
Through the simulation process, the importance of using LED instead of current lightning and adjust­
ment of the airflow rate was realized.
However, it was found when making an LED replacement in the simulation process (which is de­
scribed in stage 1), extra heat is needed, because the current heat balance is relying on the current 
lighting, which emits a large amount of heat. This problem – the extra heat, which is needed – is 
however possible can adjust with the airflow rate and supply air temperature.
These two options  - switch to LED and adjustment of the airflow rate  - should therefore take togeth­
er, because the adjustment of the airflow rate and a supply air temperature can minimize the rise in 
heating energy consumption during the winter season. This strategy will therefore lead to good indoor 
comfort.
8.10 LED and Night ventilation
My result did not show significant efficient use of night ventilation in LSC. Switching to LED would 
support the effect of the night ventilation. The current lighting emits a huge amount of heat, even 
though the night ventilation effectively dropped the morning temperature. As a result, the indoor 
temperature rises immediately to cooling set point in the early morning. This happened on many days 
in the simulation of stage 1, when only the night ventilation strategy was introduced. Substitution of 
LED lightning can reduce the raise in temperature compared with the current lightning. Moreover, 
the heat which was kept in the concrete thermal mass was also reduced as can be seen in Scenario 1. 
In conclusion, cooling consumption can be reduced with combinations of those two, LED lightning 
and night ventilation.   
Natural night ventilation is a very useful strategy for cooling, since it is not necessary to use the 
HVAC system. However, in this particular case, there are some notes on how to maximise the benefit 
of the night ventilation strategy.  Firstly, it was not a particularly hot summer, which means that there 
was not a big difference between the indoor temperature and the outdoor temperature, which mini­
mizes the effects of the night ventilation strategy.  Thus, the days suitable for night ventilation were 
very limited. Secondly, many leaks from the building envelope were found, especially in zone 204. 
Moreover many leaks were found in the simulation. The leaks allow the outside air to get in, therefore 
a night strategy ventilation did not seem like as a good solution if it was only focused on zone 204 
in the current stage. However, if the conditions - for example the airtightness or night temperature, - 
were right, it might be worth to consider the night ventilation as the first choice.
Seasonally adjustment of airflow rate
Scenario 3, the seasonally adjustment of the airflow rate, (which was included in stage 2), is the most 
recommendable option before LSC would do well to try it out before looking at the other options, 
==PAGE:99==
99
because this scenario could achieved a good energy consumption and indoor climate, even without 
changing LED lights or night ventilation, making it a very economical solution. 
In the long-term perspective, improving the building envelope is recommended, because it would 
allow for better indoor climate. In stage 1 and 2, each individual option did not reduce the energy 
consumption dramatically, but a combination of options selected from the stage 1 is recommendable 
for an optimal result.
8.11 The result of stage 3
In stage 3, the scenario 3 (seasonally adjustment of airflow rate), which is the most efficient optimal 
strategy, was applied to future weather forecasting. The result indicated a change of heat balance 
since the cooling demand was increased, and at the same time the heating was reduced. The result 
brings another discussion to the study “how the energy optimal strategy adapt to the climate change”. 
The key for this discussion was in the scenario 2 which focused on the improvement of the building 
envelope. In Appendix G, the heat balance of the project area was calculated by hand. The calculation 
also indicated that it is important to improve the building envelope, since the thermal loss from the 
envelope is considerable. For the first step of the improvement of the building envelope, replacement 
of the skylights was recommended. Because the zone 204 has poorly insulated skylights they are 
particularly important to replace. And also replacement of skylights can work together with night 
ventilation strategy as well as natural ventilation during the working hours.  
9.0 Conclusion
In the thesis, the aim was to analyse the future weather data and climate change that impact the energy 
simulation for LSC, and on basis of this analysis, to suggest some different options for optimisation 
of the energy consumption and indoor climate of LSC.
LSC is one of the oldest shopping centres in Denmark with a concrete structure. The intention of this 
thesis was to make a case study, which might also be useful in the other building energy optimisation 
studies. Below, a sum-up of the results of this project.
9.1 Climate change and future energy consumption
For determining the impact of the climate change towards LSC, the prospective energy consumption 
of 2020 was calculated based on the extrapolating degree-days method. There are three different 
weather data were used for the method that was reference year, DTU measured weather data and DMI 
weather data. 
As predicted by theory, the results indicated that the average temperature has risen 1.25 [°C] in the 
last two decades. The results also showed that the summer have became hotter and wetter, and winter 
warmer, in the course of this period. Using the degree-days, the future weather forecasting was also 
==PAGE:100==
100
calculated. The results showed that the HDD will be reduced in the future. It is expected that the HDD 
will be 3885 [day k] in 2050, which means a reduction of 11% compared to the current weather data 
from DRY2013. At the same time, cooling degree-days will increase. It is expected that the cooling 
degree-days will be 71 [day k] in 2050, which means an increase of about 30% when comparing with 
DRY2013. Even though the degree-days measurements for the cooling was relatively small compared 
with the heating, it should be taken into account for the early stage of the building energy assessment. 
9.2 Building energy optimisation
In this project, one of the most significant challenges to the building energy consumption was the high 
heat emission from the existing lighting design in the shops. On this background, the thesis suggests 
achieving a better heat balance using scenario 3, which would lead to a reduction of the total energy 
consumption by 60%, using seasonal airflow rate adjustment and substitution of LED lights for the 
current lights. The results also showed that it is possible to reduce energy consumption by 40% with­
out switching to LED lights, but only by adjusting the airflow rate. 
This scenario 3 also achieved a better indoor climate with over 75% of occupants hours,which was 
the best comfort in EN15251 class I.
This option would be very economical, because no improvement of the building envelope would 
be needed, nor employment of a night ventilation strategy, as tested in this project. There are many 
options when it comes to reducing the total energy consumption, however, few of these could reduce 
the consumption without huge financial impact.
9.3 Future study
For the future study, there are some recommendations.
	
Building simulation with future weather data.
For the thesis extrapolation of degree-days was used for the future energy simulation. The method 
is basically using degree-days that only focus on the dry bulb temperature only. Thus other weather 
parameters, such as humidity or solar radiation, were not taken into account for the calculation. There 
are several methods to create future weather data. When it was researched, the only available weather 
data was very expensive to carry out for the simulation. The future weather data is still in progress 
currently; therefore it would be interesting to use future weather data for further simulation.  
	
Detailed energy simulation in limited space.
The thesis has focused on the optimisation of LSC in the ground floor. The result of the thesis gives an 
overview of the energy optimisation of over 11,000 m2 of space. As a consequence of this big space 
some details were embedded and were not taking into account. It could be interesting to do an energy 
optimisation in a small zone, but with more details to make accurate for a smaller area. 
One of characteristics of the project was too much energy consumption of lighting in LSC. The light­
==PAGE:101==
101
ing differed considerably from shop to shop. However, in the simulation of the project, an average 
lighting consumption was used for each zone. Therefore it could be interesting to check the lighting 
consumption in each shop and see the simulation, since that would make it possible to identify prob­
lems with lightning more specific.
	
Occupant behaviours
Occupant rate based on occupant behaviour is also one of the parameters which was not clearly de­
fined. Indeed occupant behaviour could make the result different and therefore it could be interesting 
to look into it for the further studies.
