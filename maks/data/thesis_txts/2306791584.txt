Abstract 
Master’s Thesis: Construction of high-rise buildings using SL-deck technology 
with fixed-end connections 
Keywords: SL-deck, Super Light Structures, Skyscraper buildings; High-rise buildings, 
Fixed-end connections, Building Information Modeling 
The purpose of this thesis is to determine the possibility of implementation of SL-
deck technology with fixed-end connections into a high-rise building. The study 
focuses on the building design improvement based on a case study building Q22 
located in Warsaw. The emphasis was put on elongating the slab spans and therefore 
providing with better architectural qualities. 
This thesis presents a thorough investigation of the problem. Following the short 
introduction of the academic background there is the design part divided into four 
major steps. It opens with a proof of concept section which resulted in an initial check 
of its feasibility. Continuing, there is a more in-depth analysis of the building, based 
on 3-dimensional structural model. A check on the limit states was performed 
providing with accurate values of the section forces for consecutive chapter – 
enabling detail design. Based on these values two details that enable the fixed-end 
connection to the core were developed. The main emphases in this section were the 
cost efficiency of the solutions and its compatibility with the construction sequence 
and specification of a high-rise building. The design part closes with the cost analysis 
of implementation of both connection designs which was obtained using Building 
Information Modeling technology. 
Concluding, the results obtained from the method presented above indicate that the 
SL-deck technology with fixed-end connection is a viable solution for the use in high-
rise buildings. The solution fulfilled both ultimate and serviceability limit states even 
when utilizing the 50% longer spans. Both of the connection concepts were 
structurally designed and are able to provide sufficient capacity along with required 
boundary conditions. A final and equally important point is, the cost estimations 
proved that the solution might be highly cost competitive compared to the original 
design: post tensioned in-situ slab.

1. Introduction 
1.1. 
Area of the research 
The area of the research falls into two main categories. First and foremost the focus will be on 
innovative precast solution as innovative and light solutions are going to be analyzed. Several 
existing technologies will be presented along with the SL-deck technology that will be of the 
greatest importance. Fixed-end connections are going to be implemented what will create a 
need for analysis of the whole building. That leads to the second category which is high-rise 
building analysis. The aforementioned precast solutions will be implemented to what was 
designed as an in-situ building. Impact of that kind of solution will be analyzed to determine 
whether that kind of a solution is feasible or not. 
1.2. 
Thesis objectives 
This thesis was written to combine all of the main concepts described above. Based on the 
building case described further in Chapter 4 a technology of light slabs will be implemented, 
including fixed-end connections of the slab to the core of the building. There are challenges 
and questions associated with it: 
o Does the SL-deck allow for significantly longer spans in a high-rise building? 
o What impact does the fixed connection has on the buildings core and what solution is 
needed for it to remain stable? 
o How does the fixed-end connection work and look like? – Proposal of a structural 
detail that allows for core erection with Automatic Climbing System (ACS). 
If the solution is feasible stability-wise, another serviceability check will be needed as there 
are more criteria to be fulfilled: 
o If it is possible to increase the span, how does that affect the vibration criteria for 
people walking on it 
Summing up, after working the solution out additional, BIM based aspect will be 
implemented, providing answers to the final and equally important question: 
o Is the solution cost effective? BIM based comparison to the original solution

Throughout my entire studies I have considered skyscrapers and high-rise structures as the 
pinnacle of structural engineering. Incredible architectural design and challenges imposed by

combination a try. 
1.4. 
Research method in brief 
The analysis was divided into four main parts. First of all, a short feasibility study was 
performed to see if the solution has any chance of proving right.  
Secondly, more detailed analysis using Finite Element Method was performed to check the 
proof of concept and obtain section forces. Based on those slab, strength was analyzed.  
Part three was based on enabling detail analysis based on section forces from part 2. A 
connection was developed which enabled the solution to be easily implemented and provide 
with a straightforward construction process. 
The fourth, last part of the research consists of cost analysis of the solution using modern 
BIM-based tools combined with standard methods. This part proved if the solution is cost 
efficient and therefore competitive. 
1.5. 
Structure of the report 
This thesis is divided into 10 chapters and the content of each will be briefly summarized 
below. 
The introduction chapter indicates the area of the research and provides with its brief 
description. Thesis objectives are formulated, mainly identified as proof of concept, detailed 
analysis (incl. modal analysis and footfall analysis) and detailing exercise. Personal motivation 
is described along with brief description of the research method.

The literature review chapter indicates the most relevant papers, articles and theses in each of 
three main categories: super light structures, prefabrication in high-rise and high-rise design. 
Each position is provided with short description and indicates its main points. 
The state of the art chapter presents the achievements and the current state of the industry. 
It treats about the high-rises and proceeds to the concept of light structures along with 
presentation of the most relevant structural solutions.  
The fourth chapter describes the case study building. It provides with general information 
about the use and features, followed by structural systems and member sizes that were used 
during the design. The chapter finishes with the current state and aims for redesign. 
The fifth chapter is the proof of concept. It provides with a quick check if the general idea has 
any chance of working as assumed. With the use of simplified bar model and cantilevered 
representation of the core first results are drawn and the ground is set for a detailed analysis. 
The sixth chapter continues the building analysis in a wider scope. 3D analysis model is 
created and modeling process is described along with the software used. Loading and load 
combinations are analyzed in depth and model verification is described. The main points of 
the chapter are the analysis of the SL-deck elements, core walls verification and data take out

3. State of the art 
3.1. 
High-rise structures 
3.1.1. Idea behind building high 
The idea of building high was known since ancient times – with the mythical Tower of Babel 
being a way to be closer to god. Each country was trying to create impressive monuments 
with Egyptian Pyramids and Roman Pisa Tower being only one of the many examples. 
However, the modern, very tall building tendency emerged in nineteenth century in United 
States of America and from there it has spread all over the world with the Middle East and 
Asia being candidates for new leaders. In fact, North American domination has ended what 
can be seen in statistics. In 1982 around 50% of the entire high-rise was located in North 
America, with Europe and Asia being 2nd and 3rd respectively with around 20% each. However, 
in study from 2006 Asia gained the lead with 32.2% of all the tall buildings, North America 
with 23.9% being 2nd and 3rd, only 0.2% short, Europe (Moon, 2007). It is important though to 
answer what was the reason behind the whole movement? There are two more simplistic 
reasons than the ancient aim for reaching for the god. 
First of all, there has been the economic aspect behind it and simple calculation that 
increasing the height will result in the increase of rentable area by creating more office space. 
In early twentieth century tall buildings were built only to fulfill the commercial use – and the 
return rate was based purely on the rent. Years on, mixed use was introduced – tall structures 
accommodate flats, apartments, hotels and commercial as well – sometimes even at the same 
time. People are building tall to fit into already crowded spaces, and as the ground prices are 
constantly on the rise it is more and more cost efficient to build higher. 
The second reason is to create a statement. High buildings are treated as the ‘state of the art’ 
and are the subject of “the World’s Tallest” race. Each country that can afford it tries to build 
impressive structures as it results in more and more tourists and visitors and leads to higher 
income for the country. However, building high is also a great way of using the newest and 
best technologies in practice what results in bringing the structural engineering to the edge 
of its knowledge, helping to broaden it in the result.

It is very important to underline that the nomenclature is very flexible and there are many 
variations what kind of building should be called a tall building, a high-rise or a skyscraper. 
Years ago, even ten stories and several dozens of meters height were impressive from the

3.1.3. Classification of the high-rises interior structural systems 
Lateral and gravity load resisting structures (see Figure 2) in a high-rise building can be 
divided into two main categories: moment resisting frames and shear trusses or shear walls. 
These systems are usually designed in two principal orthogonal directions and are assembled 
so they can interact as a combined system. In very tall structures an outrigger system 
supported by the core is another very important and popular system. 
Moment resisting frames usually consist of a horizontal and vertical element connected 
rigidly at the nodes, creating a grid. In this system sizes of the vertical elements are usually 
gravity-load driven causing the member size to increase towards the building’s support while 
the lateral elements are sway-control driven to prevent building from deflecting by providing 
enough stiffness.

3.1.5. Use of prefabrication in high-rise structures 
Since the early days of high-rise buildings’ history the structure was usually made of one type 
of material – e.g. Monadnock Building in Chicago was purely brick structure while later 
Empire State Building was a steel structure. As a breakthrough in late 1960s a revolutionary 
hollow-core slab was used and the concept of prefabrication was again redefined. Those 
1200mm wide pre-stressed elements enabled the buildings to be much cheaper and 
construction process to be much faster. Regarding high-rise structures the arising trend is 
hybrid construction – which is combination of in-situ concrete or steel with prefabricated 
elements. Used in more than 50% of all multi-story buildings nowadays it allows to build high 
and relatively cheap, requires less formwork, allows for faster erection and creates safer work 
environment. There are many examples of such structures with two iconic Malietoren and 
Belvedere Office Tower in Netherlands (Vambersky, 2004). Nevertheless, there is still room for 
improvement and new technologies are being created. This leads to the second important 
concept in this thesis – the Super-Light Structures. 
3.2. 
Concept of super-light structures 
In each type of a building the pursuit of lighter and cheaper structures is observed. This is 
visible e.g. in using hollow ceramic slabs in single family houses or countless material saving 
technologies in more complex structures. Few milestone technologies are pointed out and 
described below. 
3.2.1. Hollow-core slabs 
Described in the previous point prefabrication in high-rise buildings would not exist if it was 
not for the hollow-core slab. Typically used in multi-story apartment buildings in Europe 
(especially Northern Europe and former socialist countries of Eastern Europe) the popularity 
of the hollow-core slab grew not only in Europe but also in United States and in the Middle 
East – where it was introduced in 1979. 
The main structural principle of the hollow-core is tubular voids that go through the whole 
length of the slab, being typically 2/3-3/4 the height of the slab. It leads to a massive 
reduction in weight what reduces carbon footprint and cost. 
The production of hollow-core slabs is usually done in long tracks (up to 200meters) where 
lines of pre-stressed tendons are stretched. The process is based on wet concrete extrusion 
and after the seasoning the deck is cut in the lengths by a diamond saw.

A big drawback of hollow-core slabs is the fireproofing, soundproofing and vibrations. The 
structures designed with hollow cores post tensioned slabs frequently need to be additionally 
fire-boarded as the fire resistance of a Hollow-core is very low. The tendons are placed very 
close to the bottom of the deck and therefore, exposed to fire are not sufficiently protected 
and therefore explode. Vibrations and acoustic problems are usually solved by adding soft 
floor covering on top of the deck or rubber strips underneath. A great advantage of a 
Hollow-core slab (apart from cost and material efficiency) is the fact that properly coated 
core can be used as a conduit for installations. 
Hollow-cores are produced in width range from 600 to 2400mm and thicknesses from 160 to 
500mm. The length of single element ranges up to 24m. 
3.2.2. Voided slabs 
The voided slab idea evolves from the hollow-core slab idea, trying to implement the most 
important principles like material and cost savings and furthermore, adding flexibility to the 
design of the slab. There are different types of voided slabs, but they can be divided into two 
main categories: one-way spanning and two-way spanning voided slabs. 
In the first category the most popular type of slab is filigree slab (although two-way spanning 
filigree are also produced, the one-way spanning version is more popular). It is a reinforced 
concrete slab with minimum thickness of the plate of 5-6cm. It is a semi precast slab that 
includes the lower floor slab reinforcement and the prefabricated plate acts like a formwork. 
The plate is precast in factory and contains torsionally stiff reinforcement in form of a truss 
that provides with stability after installation of the element. There are two ways of finishing 
the filigree slab: to fill it with solid concrete or, what is more relevant to this topic, place a 
void in between the reinforcement and then pour concrete in the remaining part.  
Second category with slabs spanning in both directions has more representatives. In this 
section two most important will be mentioned: waffle slabs and Bubble Deck. The first one is 
created by placing big voids on the formwork. Voids are usually made of plastic and covered 
with hydrophobic layer that makes them easier to remove from the forms after casting or 
forms made of light expanded polystyrene – which are not removable. This creates a slab 
with thin topping and narrow ribs that span in both directions between columns and beams. 
It is important to mention that e.g. column heads are created by not placing voids over the 
column. Benefits of such a structure are flexibility, lightweight, slim depths, vibration control 
and very high fire resistance.  
The latter representative is Bubble Deck. It is a slab that is created using three types of 
materials: reinforcement mesh, ellipsoid voids and concrete. After placing the lower

3.2.3. Introduction of the SL-Deck 
The concept of Super-Light structures was invented in 2008 by Kristian Hertz at the Technical 
University of Denmark and was followed by the concept of Pearl-Chain technology in 2009 
also by Kristian Dahl Hertz. The concept bases on combining two types of materials – light 
and strong concrete to create optimized product that will not only perform in terms of 
structural design but also will have smaller environmental footprint compared to widespread 
solutions. On those grounds a roll-out start-up company was founded and first mass 
produced element was developed – the SL-deck. It is a precast slab that is made out of 
lightweight concrete blocks of 600kg/m3 density and outer box dimensions of 
500x400x185mm (Christensen & Hertz, 2012). On its top there is a layer of ordinary 55MPa 
plastic concrete what creates compression zone and load carrying ribs (Hertz, 2015). The use 
of two types of concrete and their shape apply the principle of “form follows force” created 
by Prof. Curbach (Curbach, 2013). It results in a strong yet lightweight element with total 
weight of 321kg/m3 that can take heavy loads and span really long. The aforementioned

4.2. 
Structural design and systems 
First of all, the stability of the building is provided by the internal core. The design and 
thickness of the core was driven by deflection limits rather than by the ultimate state criteria. 
The core consists of five separate parts connected by beams. As the building consists of 
podium and the tower only three out of five core elements reach the highest stories. The 
core’s wall thickness varies, with outer walls being 450mm thick and internal core walls being 
450, 350 or 300mm thick. The core was designed with consistent wall thickness until the very 
top of the building. As a construction method Automatic Climbing System was chosen. 
To complete the gravity load carrying system two types of columns were designed: typical 
reinforced concrete columns and steel-concrete composite columns. First type has been a 
typical reinforced concrete rectangular concrete, with different sizing depending on its 
location on plan and in the height. The second type of columns is the composite cross-
section being mainly designed for tower part of the building due to high loads. Additional 
reason for that kind of solution was to make the outer diameter of the column smaller, what 
was one of the architects’ requirements. As a steel part H sections or custom designed steel 
plate reinforcement was used. 
In the building there have been four different slab thicknesses provided. In the underground 
part the slab is 300mm thick and it increases to 600mm thick column header for lowering the 
deflection.  In the ground level the majority of the slab is 300mm with the entrance hall being 
an exception of 250mm (due to thicker finishing) and therefore post tensioned. All the other 
slabs in the building are 270mm concrete in-situ, post tensioned to reduce the deflections.

5.3.1. SL-deck fixed-end moment loading 
Fixed-end connections were implemented to achieve greater spans while not increasing the 
bending moment in the middle of the slab – the negative (maximum) moment will appear in 
connection and the different moment distribution will result in reducing deflections. With 
simple supported slabs in Case 3 the moment increased more than twice (to Case 1) in the 
middle of 12,4m span. However, with fixed supports as in Case 4 the magnitude of the 
moment in the middle of the span was only 32% higher compared to the original set up 
(Case 4/Case 1=132%). The highest moment remained almost the same what complies with 
the theory (𝑀𝑀𝑎𝑥=
𝑞𝑙2
8  𝑓𝑜𝑟 𝑏𝑜𝑡ℎ); however, it is negative and appears in the support rather 
than positive and appearing in the mid-span. Due to the structural layout of the building 
fixed-end connections were implemented only in the connection to the core. This solution 
will not lower the maximum moment in the slab, but it should significantly lower the 
deflection. Nevertheless, there are ongoing tests analyzing the possibility of fixing the 
supports of the slab in-between the columns, however, the solution is not widespread yet.

side of the core work against each other. However, as the building is asymmetric, the bending 
moment on each story will not even itself out. The difference of bending moments in the

6. Detailed Analysis 
6.1. 
Analysis software 
6.1.1. Introduction and products shortlisted 
The software to perform structural analysis was chosen from few shortlisted products 
available on the market. Originally, the structure was calculated using ETABS and another 
design and concrete dimensioning software available and widely used on Polish market such 
as ABC Plyta for calculating reinforced concrete slabs. 
Various factors influenced the software choice for this thesis and several attempts have been 
made before finally deciding upon which one should be used as a final version. Latest 
considerations included StruSoft 3D calculation software called FEM-Design. Huge advantage 
of FEM design is the uncomplicated, trouble-free meshing of elements and great Revit-to-
FEM-Design plug-in that allows for direct analytical model export.  
The second software considered was Autodesk Robot. Its main merits were identified as the 
same producer as Autodesk Revit resulting in good interoperability. Great disadvantages of 
which Robot is widely known for are instabilities and meshing problems that include mesh 
inconsistences and modeling inaccuracies. 
6.1.2. BIM Integration – Revit to Robot/FEM Design – conversion process 
The structural engineering industry is heading towards enhancing full Building Information 
Modeling possibilities such as modeling to analysis software integration and therefore there 
was additional emphasis put on this aspect in the thesis. As mentioned above in Chapter 6.1 
two pieces of software have been ‘shortlisted’ for final use and for both the integration was 
checked. It was not before it was checked that the software was chosen 
Firstly, StruSoft’s FEM-Design integrates to Revit by additional plug-in. The transition works 
by exporting the analytical model from Revit through the plug-in, to external file with 
extension characteristic for FEM Design. What is very useful is the material and section 
matching - based on all the structural materials and section suitable equivalent in FEM-
Design can be assigned. After the whole process, the file can be imported to FEM-Design. 
However, it does not work as it should. For complex geometry as in this thesis the way of 3D 
modeling was proven wrong by FEM Design standards and the process result in hundreds of 
errors rather than calculation model ready for analysis. Company’s support mentioned the 
probable problems as slanted columns that need to be modeled as beams and slanted walls 
as plates. Next way of obtaining the structure sketch in FEM Design was to import bar 3D 
.DWG model with walls, floors and columns sketches what proved to work fine.

Similar compatibility problems were encountered while trying to import the geometry to 
Autodesk Robot. The in-built plug in that connects the software from the same company also 
resulted in several errors that made the process unsuccessful. Again, in this case the .DWG file 
import with geometry worked fine, however, with good lesson learnt for the future use. 
Robot characteristics show point coordinates with, usually, two decimals. However, this does 
not mean that the coordinate itself is rounded to the shown value. Robot sees at least four 
decimals and therefore what is presented as X=24.00m can be in the model considered as 
X=24.0006m what results in coordinates out of plane that make the whole geometry 
impossible to connect and, what more important, mesh. This problem was solved while 
remodeling the building by simply adjusting the Z-coordinates to the same value manually 
for each story. 
6.1.3. Software choice for final analysis 
Eventually, for this particular thesis calculation software from Autodesk package was chosen 
for the final use. It outranked FEM Design with the ability to calculate footfall accelerations 
and is known to perform better with bigger structures. Greater user abilities in Robot of the 
graduate were also one of the key factors.  
Autodesk Robot 2016 is software produced by Autodesk, however, it originates from France, 
where it was started by a company called Robobat. However, their R&D department for many 
years has been located in Poland, Krakow before it was acquired by Autodesk in 2008. 
(BIMware, 2016). Autodesk Robot is considered to be one of the most widely used software 
in Europe, being considered number one e.g. in Denmark and Poland.

6.5. 
Model verification - modal analysis and load transfer 
Before using the results from the analysis software a verification of a model should always be 
performed. It is important to check with engineer’s knowledge if the model resembles the 
structure correctly not only in terms of appropriate structural elements but also in terms of 
correct load transfer, modeling principles and structural behavior.  
First of all, Autodesk Robot Structural Analysis has verification module embedded that allows 
tracking of material consistency, overlapping bars, structure separation or isolated nodes. 
This allows the user to know straight away if anything is wrong and needs to be remodeled 
or reconsidered – places of issue are highlighted for easy tracking. The model proves to be 
working with neither no instabilities and any warnings nor errors. 
Secondly, the load application should be checked. It is important to calculate the structural 
model and check if the load applied is transferred to the ground (modelled as supports in the 
model). In this case the entire vertical and horizontal load (wind in four directions) is 
transferred properly. The easiest check can be made for horizontal load as the exact value is 
known – for each case the reactions equals load applied.

6.7. 
Vibration analysis – footfall 
The vibration requirements are described in the Eurocode 0 PN-EN1990:2002 Annex A. The 
vibration criteria should be fulfilled by buildings and their structural members to ensure the 
comfort of the user and the functioning of the structure. Further arrangements need to be 
discussed separately for each particular case and agreed with the investor. 
Few of the very possible causes of vibrations, as per point A1.4.4.4, are vibrations caused by 
footfall or synchronized people movement. Such criteria are considered in this chapter and 
compared with design criteria presented in Chapter 4.2. 
Footfall analysis module in Autodesk Robot Structural analysis has been used and simplified 
model of typical, repetitive story has been prepared. This includes slab in level 8 along with 
walls and columns of the stories below and above the analyzed slab. The columns and walls 
have been modelled with pinned connections where connection to other stories was 
supposed to exist. This simplification was purely made for the purpose of shortening the 
calculation time. Since the footfall analysis was made on one floor, there is no need for the 
program to calculate the entire meshed structure. 
Footfall analysis settings were set according to design criteria and requirements – which in 
this case were defined as the response factor R. This factor defines how many times 
calculated vibrations exceed the allowable ones that are perceptible to a human. The 
excitation will be modelled as one and two people walking on the slab in different 
configurations. The walkers’ weight was set as 76kG each and the walking frequency was 
defined from 1.8Hz to 2.2Hz (Czajewski, 2012). Moreover, as the loads are working in favor of 
the vibration reduction appropriate load to mass transfer ration have been used. The self-
weight of the slab was converted with mass factor 1.0, however, other dead loads has been 
added with factor of 0.7 and live load as only 0.3 (as it can be almost non-existent).

7. Enabling detail 
7.1. 
Detail aims and functions 
The detail for the high-rise building, apart from the need of being able to transfer the 
moment to the walls, needs to be suitable for mass use and must adjust to the erection 
method keeping in mind the construction sequence. 
While the first criterion of having sufficient strength needs no explanation, at this point it is 
crucial to mention the most common construction process for high-rise structures build in 
reinforced concrete core technology. As these buildings are dozens of stories high and the 
core footprint is usually repetitive for formwork placement Automated Climbing Systems 
(ACS) have been introduced.  These are usually regular formwork systems designed for the 
geometry of the core, however, with hydraulic cylinders attached to it. The system is 
continuously connected to anchors which are already placed in the wall, and it moves itself 
higher to build another story. This specification leaves the casting of the precast slab 
connection to a point after the story is built. Since the formwork is present and sliding on the 
face of the wall no parts of reinforcement can stick out of the wall to allow for its smooth 
transition. 
A final and equally important point is that the connection needs to be cost efficient. In 
the high-rise structures there are lots of connections like this to be used (considering the 
case study building and SL-deck on each story the building will need more than 3000m of 
such connections) so by minimizing the cost of a single element and its optimization, high 
amount of money is spared.

good solutions were found - some of them were patented. Some papers of Kristian Dahl 
Hertz provide with two widely used connections.

be impossible to put an infill to allow for further cast-in. The integrity of the wall would be 
significantly violated or the story above could not be casted at all. Therefore a need for a new 
connection was created what led to concept designs as follows.

Firstly, a fixed-end connection with the use of U-profile was proposed – as in Figure 25a). Its 
main point is a U-profile section that goes along the core wall. It provides stiff, fixed 
connection at the end of the slab by locking the SL-deck with bolts that connect the top and 
bottom flange of the profile. The bolts go through holes that are drilled at the stage of SL-
deck prefabrication and anchor in the threaded part of the bottom flange. The U-profile is 
connected to the main tension reinforcement with U-shaped dowels. The dowels are welded 
to the top flange and loop behind two bars of the main reinforcement. To ensure a proper 
force transfer (tension in dowel) additional re-bar (or circular hollow section – CHS) is 
provided for required force distribution. This solution struggles with tolerances of SL-deck: 
for proper fixed-end connection there is a need for special washer to fill in-between the top 
flange of the U-profile and SL-deck. 
The second solution is an evolution of the first one (as per Figure 25b). It bases on a steel 
plate that is bended to an L-shape and welded to the tension reinforcement. The support 
works similar to the first one, however, the SL-deck is put on a supporting steel plate that 
runs along the wall for better force distribution to the wall. The top of the SL-deck is fixed 
using bolted connections that connect the steel plate to the threaded steel plate that is cast 
in during the pre-casting process. Similarly to the first proposal, there is a reinforcement bar 
put along the bending line of the L-plate to provide proper force distribution. 
Moreover, to visualize the construction process better construction sequence visualizations 
for both solutions have been prepared and are presented in the Appendix G. For both 
solutions design spreadsheets have been created as shown in Appendix H – Design 
spreadsheet for the details.

8. Simplified BIM-based cost analysis 
8.1. 
Introduction and model conversion 
The trend of using models and digital representation of the structure as a virtual database to 
store information is already widespread. Building Information Modeling (BIM) is evolving and 
every single party in the designing process can benefit from it. Some attempts were already 
made in the analysis process of the building and were described in Chapter 6.1.2. However, 
they have not been as successful as they should according to BIM standards of compatibility 
and integrity.  
However, another attempt has been made to convert the models the other way around. 
Based on the structural models in Autodesk Robot Structural Analysis (developed as 
described in Chapter 6.2.1) a 3D digital representation and database was created by 
exporting the geometry to Autodesk Revit with the integration add-in. Here the user can 
choose from sending only the model, sending the model with the results or just updating 
already sent model. Moreover, additional options such as transfer of reinforcement projects 
for beams, columns and spread footings or steel connections can be included in the transfer. 
For the purpose of this cost analysis direct integration has been chosen and model was 
exported without major errors. Some warnings appeared to inform about the columns that 
are off main axes (slanted columns in the building). After few minutes the geometry was 
imported and ready for further use. 
It is crucial to mention how important this can be for the project and for project coordination. 
Especially in enormous project cases there is a need for 3D based clash detection, quick 
synchronization and accurate cost estimation. And especially in the latter the 3D modeling 
software outperforms the structural analysis counterparts by far. Although the analysis 
software usually has a module of quantity and cost estimation, they account for node-to 
node lengths and do not subtract the shared volume e.g. with slabs or walls. This might run 
into hundreds of cubic meters of concrete on a scale of a big project. As it is the most useful 
feature in this thesis it was used to provide with bills of quantities and basing on them and 
Polish pricing tables cost evaluation of the solution was done.

9. Discussion 
9.1. 
Interpretation of the results 
Four main chapters treating about the implementation of the solution itself (Chapters 5-8) 
proved that the concept works and is economically feasible what provides with direct answers 
to all of the research questions from Chapter 1.2.  
Analyzing all of the results as a whole it was observed that the proof of concept, being the 
preliminary study of the problem, provided with very accurate results. Those results were 
further confirmed by the full scale detailed analysis. It is important to perform such checks as 
they are very accurate indicators of the actual feasibility of a design. 
Continuing, the detailed analysis confirmed the results from the proof of concepts. The 
analysis is considered to be accurate as all major building elements were modelled. Results 
drawn from the detailed analysis are trustworthy and they are valid for slab verification. 
However, for even higher accuracy complete building design should be performed to check 
every single aspect. E.g. the core stability results are considered to be the least accurate of all 
data presented in this thesis. It is so mainly because of the wind loading that was not 
established by wind tests. This aspect should be reconsidered and core evaluation should be 
performed to check if the load assumed was conservative or not. Moreover, additional load 
of foundation settlement should be analyzed as during the full design what also will affect 
the results. 
Moreover, it was observed that the fixed-end connections applied to only one edge of the 
slab provide significant benefits in form of deflection reduction. Thanks to it, the elongated 
span fulfilled the design criteria and conformed to the code. However, to fully utilize the 
possibilities of the fixed-end connections they should be applied to both sides of the slab 
element. That solution will reduce not only the deflections but also the magnitude of bending 
moments in the span. 
For achieving the fixed-end connection two details have been developed and they both are 
confirmed to have enough capacity to transfer the forces. It is clear that one of the solutions 
is much cheaper than the other comparing prices of materials only. However, it is worth 
mentioning that the implementation in the scale of the entire structure was not that much of 
a difference when comparing it to the price of the slab itself. Although the details are proved 
to work they might be troublesome during construction as the margin of the tolerance is very 
small. The details should be considered as fully developed concepts; however, they are not 
faultless.

9.2. 
Recommendations for further research 
Although the research study has considered all the important aspects of the design, there are 
still few more areas that should be developed further.  
First of all it would be beneficial to perform a complete design of the entire building using 
the SL-deck with fixed-end connections. Although the implementation of the solution to an 
existing design is interesting and gives great insight into possible benefits – the scope of 
them can be much bigger if a building is specifically designed for the use of such slabs. 
Furthermore, such task can confirm all of the aspects of the design such as core stability or 
column design. 
Benefiting from the fact of having the complete design a detailed cost analysis can be 
performed to confirm the cost efficiency as it was investigated to a limited scope. As the cost 
evaluation consists of many small and seemingly irrelevant details, in large amounts they can 
surprisingly make a huge difference to the total price of the structure. 
Moreover, there is still room for improvement of the details developed in the aspects of both 
cost efficiency and design itself. Most important issue that these connections are really prone 
to is the tolerance of the slab itself. The cost efficiency aspect was tackled during the 
connection design by modification of different size parameters in the design spreadsheet; 
however, it can be refined further. Possibly, Finite Element Method design can be 
implemented to confirm the design and allow for computer-based optimization. 
The SL-slab as a long precast element has fulfilled the vibration criteria for people walking 
close to the communication shafts. However, vibrations imposed in the mid-span are still 
problematic and for using the full space flexibility obtained by removing the column row they 
should be limited. Vibration limitation methods should be investigated further. 
The design considered fixed-end connections to the walls on one edge of the slab. The 
connection on the opposite side leaves space for further research as there are already

10. Conclusions 
This Master’s Thesis study was set out to investigate the implementation of SL-decks with 
fixed-end connections to high-rise buildings. To answer the research question a complete 
design process with several steps was performed. It started with a preliminary check to prove 
the concept and was followed by detailed analysis covering wider scope to confirm the 
promising results. After their validation two details have been developed to enable the 
solution implementation. The process finished with cost estimation of their implementation. 
All the results obtained from the entire process confirmed the hypothesis and proved that 
the analyzed solution works in the high-rise building type. The examined SL-deck with fixed-
end connection at one edge provided with sufficient capacity for utilizing the internal forces 
and fulfilled the code requirements in terms of deflections and vibration criteria. Following, 
the two fixed-end connection details demonstrated sufficient capacity of transferring the 
forces into the walls. At the same time they fit into the construction sequence and were cost 
efficient. 
The most important results of this research are the benefits of the fixed-end connections. It 
should be highlighted that the fixed-end connection on only one side of the slab significantly 
reduces the deflections but does not reduce the magnitude of the bending moment. To 
reduce also the latter, fixed-end connections should be applied to both slab edges. Another 
finding worth emphasizing is the cost efficiency of the solution. Both of the developed 
connection details were highly cost competitive with preferable solution being even cheaper 
than the original design. Moreover, it was observed that the SL-deck technology relocates the 
cost from labor on site to material cost. 
Summing all up, it was proved that the SL-deck technology with fixed-end connections works 
well in high-rise structures. The designed solution creates cost competitive, highly efficient 
structural system, providing invaluable flexibility for interior arrangements. It is a great 
alternative for in-situ slabs and is worth investigating in every newly designed high-rise.
