Abstract
The Architecture and Engineering Construction (AEC) industry is characterized by an
increased complexity in products, processes and organizations. Complexity in construction
projects, and more speciﬁcally in building projects results in challenges of planning, coordination
and operation processes. The introduction of Information Communication Technology tools
(ICT) and the development of Building Information Modeling (BIM) and Virtual Design
Construction (VDC) in construction industry and building projects, in the last decades,
addressed some aspects of these challenges by improving the management of documentation,
the collaboration of the diﬀerent stakeholders and provided a better building information
platform.
BIM has helped to improve the cost estimation of building projects, vital for decision
making.
BIM supports the automation of traditional time consuming tasks such as the
quantity-take-oﬀs, resulting in a more accurate estimates in less time. However, the challenge
in the early stages of project planning is the low amount of project information available,
which is also the reason for not having many reliable estimating techniques. In the early
stages of project delivery, BIM alone is not enough to address the challenges of the early
stages of project delivery.
This research focuses on the exploration of cost estimation techniques for the early stages of
project delivery. Based on the literature review, several studies have implemented machine
learning techniques.
However, despite some studies have addressed the application of a
Bayesian Networks, no study could be found within the context of early stage cost estimation
of building projects. Bayesian Networks are probabilistic graphical models, often used as
for risk analysis and decision making.
Therefore, in this study, the aim was to explore
the applicability of Bayesian Networks for the early of cost estimation of building projects.
For the construction of the model based on the Bayesian Networks, a list of relevant cost
parameters was developed based on the literature review and validated through interviews
with industry experts.
Apart from that, the current situation regarding cost estimation
in the AEC industry was explored. Two interviews with experienced cost estimators from
a leading construction company were conducted.
Moreover, the currently available cost
estimation software programs and their capabilities regarding estimating practices were
presented.
The main objective for the qualitative situation analysis was to reveal the
professional cost estimation practices, methods, tools, but also the issues related to the
current cost estimating situation in the AEC industry. Focus was particularly on the early
project stage cost estimation. As a practical output, an expert based Bayesian Network
model was constructed.
The model was implemented and evaluated on a acquired data
sample from a doctoral thesis.
The results of this research highlight the usefulness of the Bayesian Networks for the early
stage cost estimation, but also the limitations of the constructed model. Recommendations
for future researchers are made.

Introduction
This chapter lays out a general overview of the thesis project and provides reasons why
undertaking this research.
In this chapter, a short introduction to the problem ﬁeld is
presented, followed by the identiﬁcation of a speciﬁc problem to be addressed in this thesis.
Moreover, the research aim and objectives of the project are presented, and the research
questions are formed, along with the research methodology. In the end of this chapter, the
overall structure of the research is provided, with a short description of the content of each
chapter.
1.1
Motivation and Problem Statement
The Architecture and Engineering Construction (AEC) industry is considered as one of
the most complex industries due to great diversity of interests, structural complexity of
products, processes and organizations, and relatively long project cycle times.
Chan et
al.[1] stated that building projects have become especially complex in the recent decades.
Gidado [2] argued that the two perspectives to conceptualize compliexity in construction
include: (1) the managerial perspective, which involves the challenges related to the planning
and coordination of construction project delivery and (2) the operations of the production
system, where the complexity emerges from the actual execution of the work inﬂuenced by
the surrounding project environment, resources and the technology being used.
For addressing the challenges related to the complexity, digital solutions have been developed.
The wider use of Information Communication Technology tools (ICT) in construction industry
and the development of the Building Information Modeling (BIM) and Virtual Design in
Construction (VDC) concepts have lead to many improvements in the construction practices.
BIM concept, which was initially proposed in 1975 [3], has greatly improved the collaboration
and communication practices. BIM has oﬀered better means to increase the eﬃciency of
managing building information. BIM also had a major impact on improving the practices for
cost estimation of building projects by automating time-consuming methods as quantity-take-oﬀ
[4].

2.1.1
Building and Asset Deﬁnition
Built environment plays an important role in the daily lives of humans and also organizations.
Every organization, whether public, private or family unit, needs suitable and well-maintained
real estate in order to manage their main operations and activities. The BSI-ISO Standard
15886-1:2011 provides the following deﬁnitions [9]:
Building is a construction work that has the provision of shelter for its occupants or contents
as one of the main purposes and is usually enclosed and designed to stand permanently in
one place.
Constructed asset is anything of value that is constructed or results from construction
operations.
Facilities, and therefore, buildings are designed and constructed to house the everyday
human activities in a useful, functional, aesthetic and pleasing way. The process to deliver
a construction facility/building is the coming of an owner who wants a new facility with
those who design and those who construct it. This process is known as the project delivery
process.

However, despite project delivery is a common process, planning, designing and constructing

One of most discussed issues is cost overruns.
Many

2.1.2
Building Information Modeling
Building Information Modeling (BIM) and Virtual Design in Construction (VDC) in construction
and building projects helped cost estimators reduce project costs by automating time consuming
tasks such as quantity-take oﬀs, which enabled them to focus more on value-added activities
as risk analysis and project simulations.[4]
The National BIM Standards Project Committee (NBIMS) deﬁned BIM and VDC as [11]:
Building Information Modeling is a digital representation of physical and functional
characteristics of a facility. A BIM is a shared knowledge resource for information about
a facility forming a reliable basis for decisions during its lifecycle, deﬁned as existing form
earliest conception to demolition.
Virtual Design in Construction is the use of multi-disciplinary performance models
of design-construction projects, including the product(i.e.
facilities), work processes and
organizations of the design - construction - operation team in order to support business
objectives. VDC models represent the functional intent, designed choices (form) and predicted
and measured behaviours of the product, organization and processes.
With focus on the
facility design, BIM is a subset of VDC.
Several BIM-software tools for cost estimation are available in the market.
The main
advantage of these software applications is that they provide opportunities to automate
quantity take-oﬀs [4]. But, the main diﬃculty in cost estimation and budgeting is that an
initial target cost has to be set from the very early stages of the project. This cannot be
overcome only with the use of BIM, as in these stages, the BIM model represents just an
overall building massing model with no elements [4].

clear distinction between the diﬀerent project lifecycle stages.

estimate stands as a guide for the development of the next one [14][15].

2.2.2
Levels of Estimates
The WBDG Whole Design Building Design Guide proposes ﬁve levels of cost estimates
which correspond to phases of building design and construction processes [5]. These ﬁve
levels of cost estimates are related to each other and follow the overall project progress.
Levels indicate the level of abstraction of building data. Each level, actually, illustrates the
content of information available for making costs estimates in speciﬁc time periods of project
delivery [14]. These levels of cost estimates are [5]:
• Level 1 - Order of Magnitude: Level 1 cost estimate is developed for determination
of project budget and feasibility analysis of a project. The basis for calculation of this
estimate is historical information of previous projects considering also speciﬁcations
and diﬀerences of the ongoing project.
The required information for this type of
estimate includes general characteristics of a building project such as:
– general functional description
– schematic building layout
– geographic location
– building size
• Level 2 - Conceptual Design: Level 2 estimate is developed for determination of
the best design alternative scheme in respect to project budget, which was developed
in Level 1-estimate. The information needed for Level 2 estimate includes:
– Information from Level 1 estimate
– preliminary design of the building
• Level 3 - Design Development: The purpose of this level of estimate is to evaluate
whether the selected building design ﬁts the available budget. This level of estimate
requires that the building design is more than 75% completed in respect to the architectural,
structural, building services and geotechnical design of a building project. Detailed
design documentation is essential for this type of estimate.

• Level 4 - Construction Documents The Level 4 - Construction Documents Cost
requires not less than 90% of the building design complete. In this type of estimate, the
estimator tries to identify items whose costs are uncertain, to include these contingencies
in the detailed design cost.
• Level 5 - Bid phase: For this level of estimate the building design should be 100%
complete with coordinated and detailed documentation. This level of estimate is used
in the tendering processes for developing and signing a contract with the client.
2.2.3
Planning Stage Cost Estimation Methods
The purpose of cost estimation is to provide the owner/client an accurate forecasting of the
anticipated project costs. The main problem with cost estimation of projects in the planning
stage is that only minimum amount of project information is available, as building design
has not yet developed. However, the estimator has to predict in this stage, as accurately
as possible the total cost of the project, to minimize the risk of possible cost overruns in
the future and to provide a valid cost estimate, which stands as a basis for the later stages.
Planning stage cost estimates are extremely useful as they can also be used in the conceptual
design phase to compare alternative designs.
In the construction industry there are two types of cost estimation methods used in the
early stages of project delivery [14]: Rough-order-magnitude (ROM) cost estimation and
Assemblies Cost Estimation. These two methods can be used for cost estimation in the
planning stage of a project and can be considered as Level 1 - Order of Magnitude estimates,
as in this stage only general characteristics of the ongoing project are known. However, cost
estimation based on the assemblies requires a little bit more than the minimum information
provided by the general project characteristics, as selection of subsystems, for example type
of foundation, type of HVAC systems, structural system. These two methods are described
in more detail below:
• ROM cost estimation: ROM cost estimation or the square-meter estimate is a
method which predicts the building cost, based on estimated gross square-meters of
the project and estimating tables. In these tables, average costs of various building
types per square-meter are listed.
The data in these tables are based either on a
company’s historical data or on published cost reference books [14]. Figure 5 shows an
example of a square-meter cost database.

2.2.4
Design Stage Cost Estimation Methods
In the design stage two estimating methods can be observed: the traditional cost estimating
method and the model-based (BIM) cost estimation method. Both of these methods are
based on the quantity take-oﬀprocess, which consists of the following steps [4]:
• The project is divided into parts, for instance a building if there are several to be
delivered
• Assemblies are identiﬁed within each part
• Take-oﬀof all the materials in each assembly is performed
• A list of materials with their exact quantities is generated
Traditional cost estimation is a long task, in terms of time. At ﬁrst, the cost estimator
must thoroughly review the contract documents: general conditions, working drawings and
speciﬁcations [4]. The estimator calculates the material quantities from the drawings and
inputs the quantities on paper or in a spreadsheet. Then, price for material and labour costs
are assigned to each item and the total project cost is calculated with the bill-of-quantities
eq. 1, which was mentioned in subsection 2.2.3.
Model-based or BIM-based cost estimation saves time to the cost estimator by generating
automatically the list of materials. As a result, the estimator can focus on other tasks,
as design of possible project alternatives and risk analysis [4].
There are several BIM
cost estimating software available in the market. The current available software and their
estimating procedure are more thoroughly presented in Chapter 4, in subsection 4.2.2.

Phaobunjong and Popescu (2003) [23] constructed a model which used relevant historical

at various cities in Texas between 1990 and 2000. Cost indices were used to normalize cost
data in respect to time and location. The building parameters which were included in the
regression analysis model were: building gross square footage, number of ﬂoor levels, space

resulting on a percentage error of −0.8% −13.5%
Kim et al[20] examined and compared the performance of three early planing phase estimating
cost models based on diﬀerent intelligent techniques. The data consisted of 530 residential
building projects between 1997 and 2000, in Seoul.
The collected data was randomly
divided into 490 train data and 40 test data. The three estimating models used multiple
regression analysis, neural networks and case-based reasoning. For the construction of the
aforementioned models, ten building parameters were considered: year, gross ﬂoor area,
number of storeys, total unit, project duration, roof type, foundation type, basement usage
and ﬁnishing grades. The test data sample was composed of 40 projects and gave for the
regression, best NN and case-based reasoning model a mean absolute percentage error of
6.95, 2.97 and 4.81, respectively.
Ofori-Boadu [19] proposed a cost estimating model for high-rise building projects. For this
study, a data sample of 102 high-rise buildings constructed in 20 diﬀerent countries was
used. Since these high-rise buildings had diﬀerent varying construction completion dates
and currencies, building cost values were adjusted to one speciﬁc year. Based on literature
and the available data, the model contained 12 building parameters: location, ease of doing
business, monthly wages, completion date, building duration, type of project, type, shape
complexity, height of building, number of ﬂoors, ﬂoor area and structural material.
Eight diﬀerent conceptual models were developed and tested in this study. It was shown
that ﬁve parameters were signiﬁcant in all these regression models: gross ﬂoor area, location,
structural material, height and completion date. The best model ﬁtted the dataset was tested
on two other projects and the percentage errors were 13.89% and −4.33%.

2.3.2
Neural Networks
Artiﬁcial Neural Networks are mathematical models which have a similar basic structure
as the biological neurons of the human brain. Through ANNs a diﬀerent computational
approach from conventional analytical methods is possible. Boussabaine [25] conducted an
extensive research about ANNs and how they can be used in construction management.
His study indicated that ANNs can assist in the prediction, update and management of
construction costs by providing early cost estimates through collected data.
A neural network model is constructed as a network of information processing units which
are named as neurons. The neurons have weighted connections between them. Also, neurons
are separated into layers with connections also from one layer to another. As a result of this
structure, the information is processed in a sequencial order, from the ﬁrst to the last layer.
The ﬁrst layer is called the input layer and consists of as many as neurons as the number
of input variables of the model. The information is processed then to one or several hidden
layers and ﬁnally, to the output layer, which has the same number of neurons as the number
of output variables of the model. In Figure 8 a simple neural network with one hidden layer
is visualized. The illustrated neural network has a input layer of three neurons, a hidden
layer of four and an output layer of two neurons.

2.3.3
Case-Based Reasoning
Case-Based Reasoning (CBR) is problem solving technique in artiﬁcial intelligence (AI)
which provides solutions by reusing and retrieving previous cases and experiences.
The
main principle of CBR is that similar problems also have similar solutions.
Case based
previous cases are stored and organized in a case base. CBR solves new cases by comparing
and matching the features of a new problem to the old problem cases, which are stored in
the case base. CBR methods follow the 4RE process [29]:

• REtain: The new solution is stored as a new case in the case base, placing also after
an indication whether the solution was successful or not.

2.4.1
Structure of a Bayesian Network Model
As aforementioned, a Bayesian Network is a directed graph, with no cycles. A Bayesian
Network Model Graph consists of:
• A set of variables and a set of directed edges between variables
• Each variable has a ﬁnite set of mutually exclusive states (if it is discrete)
• The variables and the directed edges form the directed acyclic graph (DAG); a directed
graph is considered acyclic if there is no directed path A1 →. . . →An so that A1 = An
• To each variable A with parents B1, . . . , Bn, a conditional probability table is attached.
Nodes which have no incoming arcs are called root nodes, whereas nodes which have only
incoming arcs are named as leaf nodes. If there is a directed path Ai →. . . →Aj, Ai is an
ancestor of Aj, whereas Aj is an descendant of Ai. If two nodes are separated by one arc,
the ancestor node is called parent for the other node, whereas the other is called child. The
union of the parent and child nodes for a node Ai are called neighbours. Figure 11 shows the
ancestors, parents, descendants, children and neighbours of a node A in a Bayesian Network
Model.

Statistical independence between variables in a Bayesian Network can be interpreted by the

• The connection is serial or diverging and V is known with certainty or
• The connection is converging, and neither V nor any of V’s descendants is known with
certainty.

Advantages of Bayesian Networks
Bayesian networks oﬀer some advantages regarding other advanced machine learning techniques:[44]
• Any variable in the Bayesian Network Model can be used as a known input or can be
evaluated as an output by calculating the likelihood of this particular variable.
• New evidence can be inserted in Bayesian Networks Models and the diagnosis of the
network can be updated.
• Bayesian Networks can be developed though expert opinion and knowledge.
• Insights into relationships among the variables can be easily understood due to the
graphical network display
Apart from that, Bayesian Network Models, in some cases, can be used when there are
missing values in the dataset in contrast to the other multivariate models. Finally, BNs
consider dependencies between the diﬀerent parameters/variables by nature [40], where as
in the other models the diﬀerent input parameters are considered independent [20].

Chapter Summary
In this chapter, the objective was to understand the current academic practices of early stage
cost estimation. The limited amount of information which characterizes the early planning
phase of a construction project is a huge drawback for the accuracy of cost estimates in
this project phase. Although implementation of Building Information Modeling changed
radically the information ﬂow and exchange and management of the projects improving cost
estimation in the design phase by automating quantity-take-oﬀ, it has little to oﬀer for early
stage cost estimation by its own. Two are the main cost estimating methods in the early
stage: Rough-Order-of-Magnitude(ROM) and assemblies cost estimation.
For improving
cost estimation techniques in the early stages of building projects, several studies have been
conducted focusing on applying advanced mathematical techniques for estimating building
costs. Regression analysis, neural networks and case-based systems are machine learning
techniques which seem to be possible to improve cost estimation practices, especially in the
early stage. The most common parameters in these studies can stand as a basis for the
determination of the parameters of a cost estimating model.
Bayesian Network Models is another a probabilistic modelling and machine learning technique,
which have already been applied in the AEC industry, mostly as a risk analysis and decision
making tool.
The conducted literature review indicated that there is not a published
study regarding early cost estimation of building projects using Bayesian Networks. Their
mathematical formulation and advantages compared to other techniques indicate that they
could be used as a predictive tool for cost estimating.

EstiModel cost estimating tool
EstiModel was demonstrated for the purpose of this research through an online presentation

EstiModel is an internally developed software platform by NCC Finland for cost estimating of
building projects. EstiModel estimates costs in all project design stages, until the initiation of
construction works. For early stage cost estimation, EstiModel makes a numerical model and
generates cost estimates by a list of rooms (spaces) and site factors as project location, soil
conditions, total gross planned area and site area, by also considering constraints such as city
plan regulations. The generated cost estimate at the early phase sets the target price for the
project. After that, the ﬁrst conceptual drawings and BIM models are produced by architects
and EstiModel is enriched with new information, leading in re-estimations of project costs,
which should usually not exceed the ﬁrst target price, which was set. This iterative procedure
continues until the completion of the detailed project design. EstiModel also performs LCC
(Life-Cycle-Cost) Analysis and calculates the O&M (Operation and Maintenance) costs.
In EstiModel a design management of various solutions in relation to project costs can also
be performed in the early project stages. Diﬀerent solutions regarding rooms (spaces) for a
speciﬁc building project can be tested in terms of cost, even before architects produce the
ﬁrst drawings, which contain building elements.

EstiModel is also linked with unit prices. Unit prices are derived and, after being adjusted

Model-based Cost Estimation
Nowadays, several model-based cost estimating commercial software programs have been
developed and are available in the industry. These BIM cost estimating software can generate
automatically quantity take-oﬀs directly from a 3D model.
However, model-based cost
estimation can be applied only when there is enough detail in the model. Thus, BIM-based
cost estimation cannot be used at the early stages of design, when information is very limited.
BIM-based cost estimation oﬀers several advantages in project estimating and commercial
cost estimating software programs oﬀer great potential value to estimators, consultants and
contractors. Apart from the automatically generation of quantity-take oﬀs which results in
creating faster cost estimates, model-based estimation oﬀers a better understanding of the
estimating process through the visualization of projects and elements. Additionally, cost
information can be easily provided to the client during the design stage. Estimators are also
able to explore diﬀerent design solutions within the available budget.
Known model-based cost estimation software programs in the AEC industry are: Vico Cost
Planner, iTWO, CostX, Sigma Estimates, CostOS, Innovaya, D-Proﬁler
Each of these software programs has some characteristics that diﬀerentiate it from the others,

The 3D model is imported to cost estimating software, which automatically generates an

pulls cost data with unit prices for resources and materials from its connected database and
calculates construction costs using the quantity take-oﬀequation (eq.1).

• D-Proﬁler requires information about systems apart from the mass modes in order to

• Taku can create early cost estimates with total planned area and building type-function

• Aditazz is probably the most advanced software for cost estimating, but , unfortunately,

Application of Bayesian Networks for Cost Estimation
This chapter is the third stage of the DRM: the Prescriptive Study stage. It describes how,
starting from the evaluation of the literature review and the situation analysis of construction
cost estimation, a Bayesian Network model for early stage cost estimation can be developed
and tested. In the ﬁrst section 5.1, the problem which is attempted to be solved is established
and its requirements are clariﬁed. Speciﬁcally, the section provides the aim of a Bayesian
model for cost estimation and deﬁnes its boundaries and model parameters. The second
section 5.2 corresponds to the conceptualization of the PS process: a Bayesian Network
Model based on expert experience is constructed. In section 5.3, the proposed model is
adjusted in respect to the available data sample and implemented in Hugin Lite. In section
5.4, the model is compared with two linear regression models and validated. In the end, in

5.1
Task Clariﬁcation
This section focuses on the determination of the model purpose and aim, boundaries and its
parameters. The ﬁrst part explains why Bayesian Networks are chosen for the construction
of an early stage cost estimation model. Then, the boundaries of the model are set; the
project stage, the proposed model for building cost estimation refers to, is speciﬁed. After
considering the type of project information, which can be known at the focusing phase, the
model parameters are determined and justiﬁed.
5.1.1
Aim of a new model
The purpose of the Bayesian Network model is to propose a new approach to cost estimation
in the early stages of project delivery. As it is stated in section 2.4, Bayesian Networks can
model possible dependencies between the input variables through the connections of arcs.
One of the disadvantages of other intelligent techniques as Neural Networks and Regression
Analysis is the consideration of input building variables as independent. However, building
characteristics - parameters are related to each other and that is the reason why Bayesian
Networks will be used.
5.1.2
Boundaries and Scope of a new model
Before the model development, the scope and the boundaries of the model have to be
established. Speciﬁcally, the exact phase of the early project stags the model focuses on
has to be determined.
Pikas et al [57] realized the need to accurately deﬁne the diﬀerent phases-steps of the
early project stages and proposed a relevant framework for that.

The model will ba characterized by a low degree of detail, taking into

early in the planning phase in order to analyze diﬀerent mass model design options.
5.1.3
Causal Networks based on Expert Experience
As it was pointed out in section 2.4, the direction of an arc between two network nodes
does not necessarily imply a causal relationship between them. Figure 23 presents a causal
relationship structure between two nodes.
Figure 23: Causal Relationship between two network nodes
[43]
However, as Scutari and Denis [43] denote, a ’good’ Bayesian Network should represent the
existing causal relationships in the data which is describing. Causality not only reduces the
complexity of the network, but also oﬀers a clear and meaningful interpretation. As Pearl
[58] explains about causal relationships:
It seems that if conditional independence judgements are byproducts of stored
causal relationships, then tapping and representing those relationships directly
would be a more natural and more reliable way of expressing what we know or
believe about the world. This is indeed the philosophy about causal BNs.
This is the reason why Bayesian Networks which are built by expert experience usually
express causal relationships from a given phenomenon and this is why a causal network
based on expert experience for cost estimation is constructed in this research.

The logic behind network construction is based on the following procedure and framework
of an early stage cost estimating software program. The structure of the Bayesian Network
based on expert experience should reﬂect a cost estimating procedure in the early planning
stage of a building project. It was decided that the relationships in the Bayesian network
should reﬂect the estimating procedure of EstiModel, which was thoroughly presented by
NCC Finland. Figure 24 illustrates a simpliﬁed version of EstiModel cost estimating framework.

Information about these parameters is provided by the client. Several of these parameters
- building characteristics have a causal eﬀect on the building height and building shape.
Of course, building shape and height are also determined by the client, but the initial
building characteristics impose limitations on them. For instance, city plan regulations deﬁne
limitations in building heights at certain locations. After the parameter deﬁnition, EstiModel
builds on them and calculates an initial project cost. The proposed Bayesian Network Model,

5.4.2
Cross-validation
One powerful way of comparing and validating machine learning models and, generally,
models is by cross-validation [21].
Cross-validation method divides the dataset into two
subsets: train set and test set.
The most powerful cross-validation is method is K-fold
cross-validation. In K-fold cross-validation the dataset is splitted into K subsets of equal
size. The model is trained on K-1 subsets and, then, tested on the last K part. The method
is repeated K times.
The most eﬀective K-fold cross-validation method is Leave-One-Out cross-validation, in
which K is equal to the number of observations of the dataset. The model is trained on
n-1 observations and tested on the remaining one. However, in this thesis research, it would
be too computationally expensive in terms of time, so instead K-fold cross-validation with
K=10 is used.
The model performance is quantiﬁed in this research by using the mean absolute percentage
error (MAPE):

Chapter Summary
In this chapter, a Bayesian Network Model was constructed based on expert experience.
The dependencies between the model nodes and the model structure were based on the cost
estimating procedure of the rule-based software program EstiModel, on expert experience and
in the literature. The constructed model included six variables, which were determined based
on the most common parameters of the diﬀerent literature review studies and the available
information in the speciﬁc project stage, the purpose phase of the planning stage. For the
implementation and testing of the model, a data sample including building characteristics
and project costs, which was acquired from a doctoral thesis, was used. The data sample did
not include information about the location and the soil conditions, but as all the projects
were in the same area, according to the PhD thesis, it was assumed that they did not aﬀect
the network, so they were omitted. Building shape, considering previous studies and the
lack of information in the dataset, was decided to be represented with the average building
perimeter.

This chapter is the fourth and last research stage: the Descriptive Study II in the DRM.
Section 6.1 underlines the contribution of this research to knowledge and practice and section

6.1
Contributions
Academic contributions
This research contributed to the academic knowledge by applying a Bayesian Network
Approach for early stage cost estimation of building projects, an approach which have not
yet been studied in the literature. It was showed that BNs are applicable for the purposes of
early stage cost estimation. The constructed Conditional Linear Gaussian Bayesian Network
of case 1 performed better than the two regression models showing that BNs can be used
for cost estimation in the planning stage. The predictive case 2 of the Bayesian Network
Model also showed that cost prediction can be performed even with two outputs. The model
performance was slight worse than the regression models in this case, but the main advantage
was that costs could be predicted by providing only two variables as inputs. These ﬁndings
indicate that there is a potential use of Bayesian Networks as cost estimating models in the
early project stages, but further research should be conducted.
Furthermore, the proposed cost estimation methodology for constructing a model can be
utilized by other researchers in the future. The developed list of model parameters, which
was approved by experienced cost estimators, can and should be used for the development
of similar cost estimating models to enable, eventually, a meaningful comparison between
diﬀerent studies.
Practical contributions
One of the main contributions of this research to practice is that the state-of-the-art of
cost estimation in the construction industry was investigated and mapped. Consequently,
this research can be used as overall guide for a better understanding of the cost estimating
practices, methods and tools developed until today in order to develop better and more
eﬀective solutions in the near future. As it is derived from the interview with company’s
representatives, major companies as NCC still use conventional methods in early stage cost
estimation. The largest barrier for adapting to new methods and tools is the lack of organized
databases. The developed list of relevant parameters showed what kind of information should
be collected and stored in databases for calculations in the early planning stages.

6.2
Limitations
The main limitation encountered in this research by using Conditional Linear Gaussian
Bayesian Networks was that no multivariate dataset follows a multivariate normal distribution
in the real world [34]; there was an eﬀort to transform the local distributions with the natural
logarithm to follow the Gaussian distribution, but it does not imply that the dependence
relationships between the transformed variables are also linear.
Discretization of continuous variables could be a solution to the above problem, as discrete
Bayesian Network Models can represent non-linear relationships between the variables[34].
But, the number of observations in the available data sample was limited: the approximate
determination of the required number of observations for a discrete Bayesian Network Model
considers the dimensions of the largest Conditional Probability Table (CPT) of all the model
nodes.
Assuming that each variable could be discretized into three possible states, the
variable Total Unit Cost would have the largest CPT, with dimensions: 81 columns·3 rows.
This means that the available 138 data observations would have to be distributed among
the 81 columns.
It is obvious that it would be extremely hard to learn a probability
distribution of the project cost node with such few records, even using Bayesian Estimation
and Expectation-Maximization Algorithm for parameter learning.

Conclusion
The main purpose of this master thesis was to investigate whether Bayesian Networks can be
applied to improve cost estimation in the real early planning stages of building projects. To
understand the state-of-the-art of cost estimation, and more precisely, the current academic
and professional practices, methods and tools of early stage cost estimation of building
projects, a literature review and interviews with cost estimators from a construction company
were conducted. For constructing a Bayesian Network Model for early stage cost estimation
of building projects, the parameters of the BN were determined on the past literature
review cost estimating studies and the dependencies between them were based on engineering
experience. The model was implemented in a specialized commercial software for modeling
Bayesian Networks and other graphical models.
To meet these objectives, the Design
Research Methodology was followed. The research indicated that Bayesian Networks can
be used for early stage cost estimation of building projects, however due to limitations of
the available data in this research, further investigation should be made to realize the full
potential of BNs for early stage cost estimation.
In the following, the research questions established in section 1.2 are answered more speciﬁcally:
What are the cost estimating practices, methods, tools and issues in the AEC
industry for building projects and more speciﬁcally for early stage cost estimation?
The ﬁrst research question is addressed in the literature review and in the situation analysis
chapter. The current research shows that cost estimation practices can be distinguished in
early stage and design stage cost estimation. Design stage cost estimation can be performed
using traditional methods or BIM software, which automates the quantity-take-oﬀprocedure
and enable the cost engineer to focus more on other aspects as risk analysis and design of
alternative solutions. There are several commercial BIM software programs available for cost
estimating projects in the design stage. Early stage cost estimation is performed in the early
project stages, where there is limited amount of information for delivering a model containing
building elements.
Two are the most common methods for early stage cost estimation:
ROM estimation and Assemblies Cost Estimation. In these methods, cost indices are used
for normalizing costs in respect to time and location. Leading companies in the industry
as NCC use that methods for early stage cost estimation. Although several studies have
developed throughout the years to provide more accurate methods, using Machine Learning
Techniques as Regression Analysis, Neural Networks and Case-based reasoning, companies
and most commercial software still estimate project cost in early stages by using the simpler
aforementioned methods. For construction companies, the main cost estimating issue, which
is the absence of organized databases containing information about past building projects,
is a dissuasive factor towards the utilization of machine learning techniques.
How can the Bayesian Network technique applied to early stage cost estimation?
This research question was addressed in section 5. The most important step before starting
to construct an intelligent model for cost estimation is to specify the focus project stage
of the cost estimating model so that its parameters can be accurately determined from the

available information in this stage. The parameters have to be selected based on the previous
literature review studies with intelligent models and approved by expert practicioners. The
parameters selected in this research are: Location, Soil Conditions, Building Shape, Total
Floor Area, Total Cost per m2, Building Height and Building Function - Type. The Bayesian
Network Structure in this research is based on expert experience and on the estimating
procedure of a company’s cost tool, considering also the properties of Bayesian Networks.
The next step is the selection of network type.
In this research, due to types of model
variables and the limited number of available observations, a Conditional Linear Gaussian
Bayesian Network was chosen to predict costs. The continuous variables were transformed
to follow the normal distribution, if it was necessary, in order to comply with the speciﬁc
requirements of CLGBNs. After the implementation of the network using a free version of a
commercial software tool for Bayesian Network, cost prediction can be made by considering
the expected value of the cost node. For validating the model, 10-fold cross-validation is
applied and the model performance is measured with the mean absolute percentage error.
What are the beneﬁts and limitations of Bayesian Networks for early stage cost
estimation?
The research carried out shows that Bayesian Networks are applicable for the purposes of
early stage cost estimation. A major advantage of using Bayesian Network Models is that the
model can be updated at any time with new information. In the current thesis, a bit better
results compared to linear regression models were achieved in one case, considering only
prior information. However, more accurate representation of the non-linear relationships
between the diﬀerent model variables is essential in order to enhance the predictability of
the BN model. The research in this thesis investigates mostly the use of Conditional Linear
Bayesian Networks for predicting costs. Therefore, other types of BNs should also be tested.
Discretization of continuous model variables and Discrete Bayesian Networks may be able
to model the non-linear dependencies between BNs’ nodes, but in this case, due to the
structure of the proposed network in this thesis, a larger data sample is needed. Finally, a
further development that would increase the usefulness of BNs for a cost estimator is the
construction of a tool which would visualize the cost prediction in a CAD software.
