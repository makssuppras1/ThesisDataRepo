Abstract
In recent years the use of Internet connected hardware or Internet of Things have increased signiﬁcantly
as the application for such devices have been seen in regards to the Smart City. Sensors on city lights can help
collect data and save energy, streamline operations and communicate to citizens to give them information
about their environments. Smart city light that collect data are nothing new, but the infrastructure for such
luminaire are often based on the data being interesting for the city light owners such as the technical heads in
a municipality. Often the lights are retroﬁtted with sensors that are “nice-to-have” and does not necessarily
provide data that can be translated to real actions. These solutions can show a lack of understanding from
the perspective of the light manufacturer who are ﬁtting the lights with the IoT connectivity and sensors
and stakeholders such as municipalities, furthermore such solutions do not communicate to citizens near the
light. The aim of this project is to close the gap between light manufacturer and stakeholders such that the
sensor hardware in the lights are based on what kind of data might be interesting for stakeholders, thereby
translating the data to actionable tasks. Furthermore the project will help communicate to nearby citizens
about the environment around the light by using the upper part of the light to change color. The project will
ﬁnalise through a physical prototype that will be installed in a physical location and can be developed further
by the light manufacturer.

city lights that can communicate through different colours, opens the door a potentially new standard in
city illumination and visual communication. To properly assess the potential of combining city lights, IoT

The investigation also assesses current IoT technologies and most viable industries with the expected risks

Tecnologies and Market
With the development of LED technology, traditional light sources used in city lights such as metal
halide and sodium, ﬂuorescent and incandescent lamps have been replaced by more energy efﬁcient LEDs,
which are more controllable and have a longer lifespan[1]. Haitz’s law and LED ﬂux results over time show
that LED systems have overtaken the best legacy lighting in the industry in regards to cost and efﬁciency[4].
The controllability of LEDs have introduced new innovative products on the market that can change the
lights’ intensity, temperature and hue such as the Philips Hue series1. The controllability has allowed for
a new ecosystem of connected LED lights that can be controlled through mobile and desktop apps, and
includes functionalities such as scenes that can group lights together, and change the colour of the lights
from room to room. The number of companies developing controllable and cloud connected lights have
been increasing together with the functionality, which now includes smart indoor lights that can change color
based on sensors, actuators, human circadian rhythms and more[14][5]. The controllable and smart LEDs
have emerged as a new market in the lighting industry where the simple on and off functionality of light have
been expanded with features, such as bridge with internet connectivity, a web server, and apps for smart
phones with open-source API’s to send and recieve commands. The new city lights with LEDs also allow
implementation of lower power peripherals such as microcomputers and sensors as LEDs typically run at
much lower power levels. With the lights running at a lower power level, devices with internet connectivity
also known as Internet of Things can be programmed and connected to the power systems inside the lights.

CHAPTER 3
Visual communication
In this chapter the concept of nudging and nudging through visual communication is described. The
theories are supported by research in the ﬁeld with respect to driving habits. Finally the chapter describes
how sensors in the city can help in communicating environmental factors.
3.1
Nudging theory
The term nudging is used to describe how an individual can behave in a particular way or make a particular
choice by changing the environment to trigger cognitive processes to achieve a desired outcome[15][11].
Such techniques are popular as humans are known to not be rational beings and thereby do some things
that is not in their own self interest even if they know that their actions are not in their own best interest[6].
Nudges are small and inexpensive ways to change the environment to alter bad decisions into decisions that
are in their best self interest.
3.1.1
Nudging in trafﬁc
Driving a vehicle on a highway is a cognitive process that includes both mental and physical awareness
and skills. Drivers can encounter several signs with maximum driving speeds along their trip. The speed
annotations on these signs are thoughtfully placed and adjusted to speciﬁc areas e.g. 110 km/h on the
highway and 40 km/h when exiting a highway through a curved road. An important function of speed
signs is that the drivers will move at approximately the same speed further decreasing the chances of road
accidents happening. By decreasing the chances of accidents it is assumed that road signs and in particular
speed signs are in the drivers best interest, but cannot be considered a nudge as the speed signs are mandated
by law which nudges should not be[7][19].
Although speed signs are placed around Denmark, 563.755 speeding tickets were issued in 2015[3].
Several reports and campaigns by "Rådet for Sikker Trafﬁk"1, the danish council for safe trafﬁc, has been
published showing the increased dangers of driving to fast. In later years equipment such as radar speed
signs have been implemented in key urban areas to show the driver their speed directly through an LED
display such as the one shown in Figure 3.1. The use of radar signs can be considered nudging as they are
not issuing users with tickets or in other ways enforcing laws, they are showing the speed of a vehicle as it
passes by. Studies in the US and UK show that such devices are effectively slowing trafﬁc down[13][23].
Furthermore on the basis of the studies it is assumed that nudging can indeed help in slowing trafﬁc down.

CHAPTER 4
Colorimetric analysis of RGB light
A color characterisation of the CPH light through colorimetry methods is needed to assess the capabilities
of the RGB light. The RGB light is emitted through the upper plastic sphere to the outside environment, as
the light is intended to communicate through its colours through the RGB modules. The color gamut for the
RGB light is found in the chromacity color space to determine if the hue and saturation of the emitted color
through the light is approximately uniform in color representation. In this chapter a short description of the
human vision is given in section 4.1 followed by description of the standards and formulas in determining
the chromacity primaries following the CIE1 standards described in section 4.2. Following this section, an
overview of the formulas used to calculate the chromacity coordinates are presented in section 4.3.2 from
the tristimulus values found through the formulas described in section 4.3.1. These are used in calculating
the primaries for the color gamut in the chromacity color space for the light from two different measurement
setups described in section 4.4. The chromacity coordinates found through the experiments are then used to
characterise the RGB light through its color gamut which are analysed in sections 4.4.3 and 4.4.3. Finally
the chromacity coordinates are used to calculate the CIE 1978 color coordinates to better visualise the color
distribution the proportional perceived color difference.
4.1
The human eye
The inside wall of a human eyeball contains a sheath called the retina which is the light-sensitive part of
the eye. The central region of the retina contains the fovea which is a cone-dense region, which enables the
sharpness in central vision. The structure of the retina consists of light-sensitive rod cells and cone cells,
where rod cells are are more light-sensitive and cover the entirety of the visible spectrum, and cone cells are
more sensitive to red, green and blue spectral ranges[16]. See Figure 4.1 for a visual representation.
The receptors of the human eye are relevant at different illumination levels seen in Figure 4.2. At low
luminance the rod-cells of the eye are more prominently in use, in medium levels of luminance both the
rod-cells and cone-cells are in use, and at high luminance the cone-cells are most prominent. The different
prominence of cells at different luminance levels can be separated in to three categories photopic regime,
which relates to human vision at high ambient levels, scotopic regime relates to human vision at low ambient
light levels and mesopic regime which related to human vision light levels between the photopic and scotopic
regimes.

4.2
Color matching and chromacity
To assess the quality of color emitting from the RGB modules it is necessary to understand chromacity,
which is an objective speciﬁcation of the quality of colours regardless of luminance. The chromacity of a
light source consists of two independent parameters called hue and saturation2. The chromacity diagram
stems from a standard formulated in 1931.
4.2.1
CIE 1931 and CIE 1978 standards
The sensation of color can be considered a subjective quantity, to create a standard the CIE introduced
the standardized measurement of color with the use of two methods. These are color-matching functions and
the chromacity diagram called CIE 1931 introduced in 1931 with a modiﬁcation introduced by Judd and
Vos in 1978, called CIE 1978[16]. The color gamut for the RGB light is calculated both in terms of the CIE
1931 coordinate system which is the U.S standard and CIE 1978, which is the more accurate and improved
color chromacity diagram. Since the module inside the RGB light only accepts color commands given in
terms of x and y i.e. CIE 1931 coordinates, the CIE 1978 results are used as the newer color coordinate is
more spatially uniform i.e. color changes in terms of u and v are much less rapid than in the CIE 1931. For
example changes in the x-coordinate in CIE 1931 have much more rapid color changing effects than changes
in the y-coordinate. Thus, the color difference between two sets of coordinates in the uniform chromacity
diagram (CIE 1978) is approximately proportional to the geometric distance between the two points. An
example of the two color coordinates can be seen in Figure 4.3, notice the difference in spread of colours
between the two chromacity diagrams.

4.3
Color matching and chromacity diagram
The color matching properties describe the RGB modules’ capability to emit colors in the color spectrum
and is found by calculating the tristimulus values in the XYZ color space which allows us to calculate the
color gamut in the chromacity diagram. This is possible by converting the tristimulus values X,Y and Z to
the hue in the x-axis and saturation in the y-axis. The chromacity diagram contains the full gamut of colors
that the human eye is able to register[16] and the color gamut of the RGB module described in section 4.4.2
shows the colors that the module is able to emit.
4.3.1
Tristimulus values

color matching functions ¯x(λ), ¯y(λ) and ¯z(λ) it is possible to calculate the stimulation of X, Y and Z needed
to match P(λ)[16].

4.4
Measurement methodology
To measure the wavelength λ and spectral power distribution P(λ), to calculate the CIE 1931 and CIE
1978 color gamut of the RGB light, a measurement setup was designed with the RGB light, and measurement
devices in the light laboratory at DTU Fotonik. The measurements are carried out to characterise the RGB
part of the light both in terms of "truthness" in color and uniformity. The two will reveal the imperfections
and the magnitude of these which can be used to determine if the light is correctly displaying the color,
set by the user and if it suffers from severe non-uniform color distribution. To acquire the data, a colour
photometer was used together with an ampliﬁer, and software for spectrum analysing and export of data.
4.4.1
Measurements setup
To characterise the light a total of 8 measurements at a 90 degree angle are carried out, as this is the
surface pointing outwards towards vehicles, pedestrians and more. The spectrometer is then raised and
pivoted in a downwards direction at an approximately 40 degrees angle towards the lights’ outer shell, where
another 8 equally spaced measurements are made. This method is repeated for red, green and blue with 4
measurements being made for each of the 8 positions, to ensure minimal variance and erroneous readings,
resulting in a total of 192 measurements. Several considerations were made to make sure a controlled data
acquisition was achieved, such considerations were the:
• position and rotation of the light during each measurement
• only changing the position of the spectrometer when measurements for all colours were made in one
color series

dataset recorded by a static video-camera was used as the data closely resembles the use case for the CPH
RGB light. An image from the video dataset can be seen in Figure 6.4. The video dataset is made possible
through the extensive work done by the team at the Faculty of Information Technology, Brno University of
Technology, Brno, Czech Republic[18] who have recorded several vehicles on a highway and calculated
their speeds through strategically placed LIDARS10. The team behind the dataset has released full video
recordings from different perspectives with the speed measurements, such that future users of the dataset can
validate their calculations against theirs. The team has also released 5 minute clips for the measurements
as the full recorded video ﬁles are much larger in size, we use these in programming the detectors. The
session is with the camera placed on the left side of the road and was picked because of the orange markings
in the video, which represent the placement of the LIDARS together with a known distance between them.
With the distance given the speed calculations are considerably less complex, as this knowledge can be used
directly to calculate vehicle speeds. The LIDAR setup on the road is graphically presented in[18] and can be
seen in Figure 6.5. The method for ﬁnding when the car enters and exits the two beams of the LIDARs can
be seen in Figure 6.6. The same methodology is used to calculate vehicle speeds from pixel values in camera
images. To detect vehicles three 1-dimensional vectors with 30 values for each channel are used for each
of the two detectors. Each detector contains values for each of the red, green and blue channels covering
a speciﬁc part of the highway. In this project the detector locations used are close to the orange markings
by[18], as these have a known distance between each other. The detector locations together with their three
1-dimensional vectors and their respective channels can be seen in Figure 6.7.

6.2.2
Detection of vehicles with line-detectors
After acquiring and resizing an image the values of the line-detectors are compared to identify if a
vehicle is in the line of pixels or not. The line-detectors are manually adjusted through the pixel values of an
image, and once set can only be changed if the software is stopped and values are changed manually. The
detectors consist of a 1D array of pixels crossing the vehicle lane of interest visualised in Figure 6.7, and are
initially populated with empty values before and after each image has been processed. When a car is not
present the average values of the red, green and blue color channels are similar to each-other, as the road is
mostly grey. When a vehicle is present the channels will differ in colour. To visualise this an example of
a detection of a vehicle is used and can be seen in Figure 6.8. When the vehicle is not passing any of the
detectors, the detector channel values will be close to each-other as shown in the histograms in Figure 6.9
for detector 1, and Figure 6.11 for detector 2. The RGB values being close to each-other means that the
color is in the white to black specturum such as grey colours, which is appropriate as the highway is a grey
color. When the vehicle passes the detectors the RGB channels start to differ in values for example the red
channel will have higher values than the green and blue channels, if a red car is passing through. For the
example shown in Figure 6.8 the histogram for the three channels can be seen in Figure 6.10 for detector 1,
and 6.12 for detector 2. For detector 1 when the vehicle is passing through the pixel array values, the red and
green channels have lower values ranging from [0, 100], while the blue values cover a larger range in the
lower value range between [0, 150]. In the case where the vehicle is not present for detector 1 the values
all range between [125, 141]. Some variance is expected between the detectors primarily because of two
factors. The ﬁrst is the varying speed of the vehicle causing the two detectors to get their number of valid
detection at two different positions on the vehicle shown in Figure 6.8, where the ﬁrst detector is getting 7
valid reading across one of the headlights, while the second detector gets 7 valid readings well above the
same headlight. The second and most prominent factor is the change in scale as the vehicle travels closer to
the camera position. Although the two factors change the position of the valid readings for both detectors,
the change in color for the RGB channels between vehicle and non-vehicle is great enough to differentiate
between the vehicle and non-vehicle case.
To use the RGB values in the 1-dimensional arrays for the two detectors, the average value is found
for each channel, followed by the absolute value between each channel to compare them to one another.
If the color is grey the absolute difference between the channels will be small. On the other hand if the
difference in absolute value is great it suggests that one or more channels are more saturated than the others.

6.2.3
Timing and saving a valid vehicle image
When a line detector has registered a vehicle 7 consecutive times the vehicle counter will increment by
1, thus accepting that an actual vehicle is passing through the line detector. Since the vehicles are moving
in one direction they will always pass through one line detector ﬁrst. When an actual vehicle is registered
the ﬁrst line-detector will start a timer which is used to calculate the speed of the vehicle after it has passed
through the 2nd line-detector. Furthermore, the passing of a vehicle through the 1st line-detector will trigger
the software to save a timestamp at the 7th valid vehicle reading. The same is true for the 2nd line-detector
which will save a timestamp when it has completed 7 valid readings of a vehicle, the timestamps are used
when saving a vehicle image to a ﬁle. The saved image is moved to a vehicle image directory with a naming
convention shown below

6.3
Acquisition of environmental sensor data
The SCD30 environmental sensor hardware described in section 5.2.3 is used to acquire environmental
data outside the light. On ﬁrst boot the sensor needs to be conﬁgured depending on the hardware bus
conﬁguration which in this project is the I2C14 communication protocol. Furthermore, the sensor compares
environmental values set by the user to change the RGB color inside the light, through the RaspBee shield
mounted on top of the Raspberry Pi, to the RGB module through ZigBee and the deCONZ REST API. Lastly
the software pushes the data to the Firebase cloud database, which at this point is ready to be pulled into the
web-platform or can be accessed through the Firebase Console Dashboard. The communication with the
RGB module is through the deCONZ REST API, which can be used with a third party open-source Python
API on top.
6.3.1
Conﬁguring the environmental sensor
To acquire data from the SCD30 environmental sensor the measurement interval and sampling mode is
conﬁgured. The ﬁrst conﬁguration is to set the measurement interval for the sensor, which is described in
the manual see Appendix 14. The conﬁguration commands are sent through Python with pigpio15, which
enables control of the GPIO ports on the Raspberry Pi. In this project the reading measurement interval of
2 seconds is used. The hex command for setting the measurement interval is then 0x4600 0x00 0x00
0x02 0xE3, which is set through the SCD30 Python developed software shown in Appendix 12. After
setting the measurement interval the continuous measurement with optional ambient pressure compensation
from the manual needs to be enabled, to properly calibrate the environmental sensor. To set this conﬁguration
the MSB and LSB must be calculated to make use of the automatic pressure compensation. Setting this
conﬁguration helps the sensor automatically calibrate while operating.
To compute the LSB of the millibar value a pressure of 972 mBar was used, which is approximately
the atmospheric pressure at sea level. The pressure value is multiplied with the maximum hexadecimal

memory on the Sensirion SCD30 sensor, and will not be reset after rebooting the device.

Light setup
The setup process of a RGB light is designed in a way such that non-technical personal will be able
to conﬁgure similar lights as easily as possible without programming knowledge. The RGB part of the

RGB installation guide16 where the light is turned on and off for a speciﬁed amount to ensure the module is
connected to the Raspbee module described in section 5.2.6. It is assumed that the Dresden RGB module
has successfully been connected to the Raspbee module before going through the setup of the prototype in
this project.
To avoid the webapp from requesting data from the cloud database when its not needed the user will ﬁrst
need to click the "start setup" button on the webapp to allow new lights to be added to the ﬂeet.
Assigning ID’s to new lights
When the "start setup" button is pressed a database variable new_luminaire_web is switched from
"False" to "True", this allows a secondary script on the Raspberry Pi to assign the new light a unique
id. This is possible by ﬁrst collecting all the current ids from the database and assigning the new light
afterwards. The assignment of id is only possible if the "start setup" button was pressed before switching
the light on, otherwise the light is blocked from getting a unique id. The software ensures that all lights
have unique ids and that lights are only allowed to be assigned a new id when the manufacturer or customer

8.3
Characterisation of RGB light
To characterise the RGB light, the light was instructed to display red, green and blue for the analysis.
An issue arose when a deeper investigation showed, that the sRGB color space is meant to be used in
characterising monitors and displays, and thus the color gamut does not accurately represent the larger range
of colours that the RGB LEDs typically are able to emit[20]. To accurately ﬁnd the color gamut for the RGB
light the color commands should instead have been based on typical CIE coordinates for each colour of
LEDs, or even nanometer-based, and the saturation and brightness should have been considered as well as
the color temperature. Although the ﬁnal color gamut is not an accurate representation of the RGB light
capabilities, it is used to describe how well the RGB module can represent sRGB colours, which still vary
enough in color value to be noticeable to the human eye. in regards to visual communication, a more in
depth characterisation of the RGB light is required to quantify the impact of the saturation and brightness
parameters. A higher saturation might give a better "true" color but could also be a nuisance to pedestrians
and drivers, thus not necessarily being the best solution for emitting colours. A full characterisation of the
CPH RGB light would also include an analysis of the downward facing white light as it emits a much higher
intensity light than the upper RGB part. Such a characterisation is also useful as the downward light can be
controlled through the Raspberry Pis GPIO pins, and introduces new possibilities for visual communication
and control.
8.4
Hardware
The hardware chosen in this project shows, that a simple but effective prototype can be built from
off-the-shelve components. These are either open-source libraries provided by a third party company such
as Dresden Elektronik, or by open-source contributors, as in the case for the environmental sensor Python
library. Documentation played a great role in the hardware design and considerations as it helped getting the
environmental sensor and its clock requirements conﬁgured quickly through Python. The vast amount of
documentation also helped connecting the hardware components painlessly, and which in turn allowed for
more development.
8.5
Software
Although the Raspberry Pi camera is explained both in the hardware section and software section, the
camera was never utilised. Instead the video recording from[18] was used. Although the camera was not
used, the video recording from the dataset has a resolution of 1920 x 1080, which is resized to a smaller
resolution. The resolution step is required when using the video recording, but using the camera directly it is
possible to set the default capturing resolution, such that all images from the camera are directly outputted in
the correct format. This step is carried out on the chip level of the Raspberry Pi, which results in images
being processed faster than the resize command used for the processing images from the video recording. It
is therefore assumed that the camera module will operate at a higher FPS than the video recording. Since
the FPS with the video recording is concluded to be fast enough, it will be as fast or faster with the camera
stream. The software developed during the course of the project shows that it is possible to count vehicles
with a small microcomputer with cloud communication. The error in counting the vehicles can be solved
with further development of the counting software, , in order to account for multiple vehicles on both
line-detectors at the same time in a captured image. With the initial prototype an error between 2% - 6%
shows that further development on the software could pay off, especially considering the price of such a
system compared to the manual methods and semi-automatic methods described in section 2.

8.6. MECHANICAL PROTOTYPE FOR FIELD TESTING

The speed measurements in the vehicle software is not used to determine speeds of the vehicles, as it was
not possible to validate the measurements with the ground truth speed measurements. The validation could
instead be carried out by installing a physical light with a video recording software running on the RPi, and
then driving in the ﬁeld of view of the camera installed. The vehicle speed should be kept constant, such that
parameters in the vehicle software can be adjusted to return the correct speed that the vehicle is travelling,
thus calibrating the speed measurement software. With the calibration of speed, the RGB light can be fully
utilised for sensor-based communication through nudging. Although without the calibration i.e. in its current
state, the color change due to uncalibrated speed measurements could be confusing to vehicle users. This
would defeat the nudging principle, as the message could be lost in confusion. The environmental sensor

mapping of environmental data to RGB light output allows for gradual color changes, which gives the light a
more aesthetic appeal with a wide range of color choices available.
The initial webapp allows the user to see useful vehicle and sensor information with images and plots.
The webapp serves as a successful ﬁrst prototype of what a front-end could look like to clients buying the
RGB light. The platform could be further improved by removing the manual method of getting the newest
data, to an automatic implementation, which could be achieved through jQuery1. Furthermore, the platform

user-experience and user-interface with clients would be useful to further develop the webapp i.e. start a

Furthermore, the communication between the database and the webapp through the Python API shows how
two online services, such as Heroku and Flask developed independently from each other can communicate,

8.6
Mechanical prototype for ﬁeld testing
The hardware and software was tested with the Raspberry Pi microcomputer and RaspBee module
outside the RGB light. For a ﬁeld-ready prototype, the hardware should be mounted inside or on the RGB
light itself. Without doing so the ﬁnal prototype cannot be validated as unforeseen issues, such as EMC
cannot be accounted for. The mechanical prototype should also be implemented in such a way that it does
not obstruct both the downfacing white light, and the RGB light or introduce any other kind of light artefacts.
The software in this project was tested on recorded data in a controlled environment, which showed
promising results. To fully validate the software solutions the RGB light needs to be placed in the environment
it will operate in, for example on a light pole near a highway or in the city. Additionally, the hardware should
be mounted inside the light in a production friendly manner such that it can be mounted by facility personal
at the Signify facility.
Ideally the physical test would help in determining the performance of the hardware, software and
webapp. For the hardware, the test would show the impact of having more hardware inside the light, which
could raise issues with temperature, EMC, power distribution, communication, light artefacts, and even
pressure. For the software the test would show if immediate or long term bugs would appear, thus crashing or
stopping the software operating on the Raspberry Pi. This test would also help deciding the optimal position
