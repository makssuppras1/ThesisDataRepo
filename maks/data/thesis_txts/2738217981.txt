Abstract
We examine and establish a notion of ’Attention’ within search problems. This definition
is applied to the Blockworld and hospital domain of planning problems and we look at differ-
ent ways attention can be defined and used within these. We see that attention can provide
strong gains when applied in different manners. These gains can be found by creating a sub-
problem out of the original problem or by using a focused derivative algorithm from Iterative
Width which searches primarily on the objects we are paying attention to. However we are
unable to pinpoint domain-agnostic methods of determining attention which would function
across search problems in general. Additionally we look at Least Width Search, establishing a
notion of ’reducing width’ through the Iterative Width search algorithm. This results in some
interesting gains in certain types of search problems. Lastly we look briefly at a separate,
failed attempt at applying attention to Iterative Width.
2
Introduction
Search problems are difficult to solve, partly because there exists no one-size-fits-all al-
gorithm to simultaneously efficiently and optimally find general solutions to search problems
in less than exponential time. Both problem domain specific heuristics and more general
methods exist to guide algorithms through searches more efficiently, and we are interested
in contributing to this field by examining additional options.
An observation within search problems is how the number of literals or objects in the
problem which may have their state changed, such as doors opening or agents moving,
determines how large the problem’s state space is, and problems generally explode in size
complexity as this number of literals increases. This phenomenon of the number of ways any
state of the search problem can transform into new states is also called the branching factor.
Any search algorithm wrangling down the problem to a smaller subset of its given literals
will have its branching factor trimmed and its state space limited, which ultimately means
searches are performed faster and more efficiently. We propose the concept of ’attention’ to
assist in performing such reduction of problems.
With a thorough examination of ’attention’ we aim to provide further enhancements
to existing search algorithm enhancement tools such as Iterative Width and domain-specific
heuristics, in addition to definitions and understandings that may lay the groundwork for
future forays into the topic. Iterative Width is further explained and explored in section 3
and is described in-depth by Gabriele Bazzotti et al. in [1].
We aim to define ’attention’ as a clear, usable tool to be applied within searches, along-
side or in substitution of existing tools to improve state searches by reducing the state space
and/or otherwise narrowing or bounding the search to find results more efficiently.
We will start out by formally defining attention and discussing the different forms it
may take. This will include not just defining what an attention and an attention set is, but
also discuss the options for determining this attention set, that is to say ’paying attention’

3
Background
To detail our examination into improvements to search algorithms, we first discuss search
problems and present a couple of search problem domains which serve as the theoretical
ground to build our planning algorithms upon. We introduce PDDL and further examine
the Iterative Width searching algorithm which is the theoretical background for some of our
work.
3.1
Planning problems
Planning problems, interchangeably called search problems, exist everywhere around us
in real life. Deciding which turns to make to reach a destination is a search problem. So is
figuring out which series of actions to take with a list of ingredients to cook a ragu bolognese.
Any sort of problem where we must find out which actions to take to accomplish a goal can
be framed as a search problem.
Search problems are composed of three parts: An initial state, si, containing all the
’literals’ or atomic truths which are initially correct in the problem, a list of permissible
actions in the problem, and a condition to be fulfilled, called the goal. This goal consists
of a list of literals which must be either true or false for the goal to be fulfilled. An action
consists of preconditions and effects: What must be true for the action to be taken, and
when that action is taken, what changes are made. When an action is taken on a state, the
truths of that state change and we are brought to another state.
A solution to a planning problem is any series of actions which, when applied to the
initial state and then sequentially on the subsequent states, ultimately lead to a state where
the goal condition is true (also called a goal state). There may exist many solutions to a
search problem, and even multiple optimal solutions.

Actions often have a cost associated to them, and any solution has a total cost equal to
the sum of the cost of its actions. In the domains we will explore throughout this project,
actions have a simple cost of 1 and any solution simply costs the number of actions taken,
but this is to say that the costs of search problems might differ in other domains.
We say that a solution is the optimal solution to a search problem if there exists no
other other solution with a lower cost. In many contexts we are interested in finding optimal
solutions to search problems (we don’t care for a route from Greece to Italy crossing the
pacific) though often we are satisfied with solutions that are ’good enough’, within a certain
acceptable margin.
The Breadth-First-Search (henceforth called BFS) is a simple planning algorithm which
searches for a solution by attempting every possible action at every possible state, starting
at the initial state and moving outwards. BFS is guaranteed to find optimal solutions by
searching through every possible action, but unfortunately the algorithm suffers when applied
to search problems of large state spaces.
When looking at a given initial state in a search problem, one can extrapolate a con-
tinuous series of action-taking and state changes, and can surmise that there exists a finite
universe of possible configurations for these literals, which materializes as all the uniquely
different possible states the problem may expand into. This universe is called the ’state
space’. A large state space means there are many different states we can arrive at, and often
mean computationally expensive solutions. Lower state spaces, on the other hand, often
mean solutions are easy to find.
We observe that in a given search problem, there is a rough average of number of actions
to be taken in each state, which expand that state into a number of new states; this is called
the ’branching factor’ of a given search problem. A high branching factor means that for a
given state there are many possible actions to take, and to find a solution one must explore
many different routes, which in turn makes for computationally difficult problems.
Low
branching factors characterize problems that are solved faster because there are less options

3.2
PDDL & the Blockworld problem
For the purpose of working on planning problems, we build our definition of attention
on top of the PDDL language; that is to say, we use the notions of actions and literals given
in the PDDL language to define the meaning of attention and explain its usage later on.
PDDL, which stands for Planning Domain Definition Language is explained in-depth at [5],
but a brief explanation follows.
PDDL presents planning problems in its own language. Problems consist of a series of
literals and actions, in addition to a given initial state and goal state. Literals (also often
called ’atoms’) here are the ’truths’ of a given state, and actions are the permissible ways to
change the state, defined by preconditions that detail which atoms must be true to perform
the action, and the effect of the action, which is the way the state changes when the action
is taken.
To exemplify PDDL, consider the Blockworld domain. In this domain, for any given
instance of a problem, there are a number of building blocks set in a configuration on top of

3.3
The Hospital Domain
For the purpose of additional examination of graph searches, we use the hospital domain
[2] implemented in the MAvis tool [6]. This domain provides a strong foundation for exami-
nation of performance of planning algorithms, and results are easily visualized in MAvis. A
brief description of the domain follows here.
In the hospital domain, problems are presented with an initial state and a goal state
consisting of a 2D level set over a square map of grids. This map is bounded by and often
contains inaccessible walls, and contain a number of objects: Agents and sometimes boxes
in a number of colours. In addition to these objects, the map further has a number of goals,
with always at least one goal present in every hospital domain problem. These goals are
identified by an id of a corresponding agent or box, and solving a hospital domain problem
entails finding a series of actions that bring the agents or boxes to their corresponding goals.
In figure 3 we can see an example of such a level, with one blue agent with id 0, a red
agent with id 1 and a blue box with id A. All agents have id’s of numbers 0 and upwards,
while boxes have letters as id’s. This means the target object of a goal is always determinable
by the id which is specified on its face. Additionally, all boxes with the same id also carry
the same colour.
For the sake of comprehension we note in the figure 3 the x and y coordinates of each
field. Notice that in the hospital domain, x-values increment as we move east and y-values
increment as we move south.
All agents in the Hospital domain are able to take 1 action each, which is then resolved
(almost) simultaneously, at which point they are all allowed to perform a new action. This
combination of actions is called the ’joint action’, and could be represented as an array of
actions of exactly size equal to the number of agents. Every agent is required to take an
action; though a common default to assume for an irrelevant agent is that it simply takes
the ’NoOp’ action.
This creation of a joint action and applying it to all the agents then continues until a
state is found where all goals are occupied by their corresponding objects.

We emphasize the notion of an ’optimal’ solution; this is the solution for which there
exists no other solution which takes fewer steps.
The interesting aspect about the hospital domain is its simultaneous logical accessibility
and computational expense. Any human can easily deduce a solution to most hospital domain
problems quickly by simply looking at an image of the initial state, but brute-forcing a search
for a solution involves a search through a massive state space. Consider in figure 3 that there
are 75 empty spaces. Each of these 75 empty spaces may be occupied by one of the three
objects, either the two agents or the box. For each object there are 75 possible spaces, minus
the spaces already occupied by other objects, creating about 75 · 74 · 73 = 405.150 possible
permutations of objects, and thereby a state space of just north of 400 thousand states.
Even seemingly simple levels such as the one shown in figure 5 has 9·8·7·6·5·4·3·2 =
362.880 possible states in its state space.
Figure 5: The initial state of the MAPFSlidingpuzzle level
An ordinary Breadth-First-Search searching algorithm will always find the optimal so-
lution for a given Hospital domain problem, but unfortunately it will not often do so within
reasonable time or memory usage on levels with large state spaces. Here, other methods
are necessary. A typical way of guiding a search is by providing a heuristic cost to every
action, forcing the algorithm to prioritize searching down routes that are more likely to pro-
duce a solution; an obvious heuristic for the hospital domain would be one which prioritizes
expanding the search into states with the most goals fulfilled already.

3.4
Width of a problem
In [7], Nir Lipovetzky and Hector Geffner specify a definition for a width in a given
problem. The aforementioned paper expresses a precise definition, but a brief clarifying
explanation follows, as width and later Iterative Width, explained in subsection 3.5 is built
upon by ideas presented later in this paper.
While most classical searching is defined by how the search attempts to move every
individual atom in relation to all other atoms to methodically examine each possible state in
the state space, Lipovetzky and Geffner propose a different approach to searching. Observing
how the states of many atoms are irrelevant to a search, a search for a solution could often
be performed much faster if performed on a lot fewer permutations of the literals in a given
problems.
In the hospital domain problem given in figure 6, most people can intuit that a solution
can be found by only moving the agent with id ’0’. We find that the width of this problem
is 1, which aligns with this observation.
Figure 6: A given problem in the Hospital domain.
Given a ground set of atoms for a problem At we define T i to be the set of all non-empty
possible subsets of size equal to or less than i. For example, in the aforementioned hospital
domain problem, all subsets in T 1 would only contain the location of a single agent.
All subsets for T 2 would contain not only all sets with locations about single agents as
for T 1, but also all subsets containing all pairs of agents.
We make the observation that the size of this set increases exponentially in i instead of
in the size of atoms |Atn|, particularly relevant when this growth is observed later in Iterative
Width.

3.5
Iterative Width
Recall how search problems must have an optimal plan. That is, there exists a (or
sometimes multiple) plan(s) over any planning problem which requires the least cost, often
simply the least number of actions taken, to move from the initial state i to a goal state G.
A plan consists of a series of actions.
We further observe that every state s contains one of those states t in T i if and only if
that substate is also observed in s.
Then, we may define that for any planning problem Π we let Π(t) define the planning
problem where we have replaced the goal with t. In such a case, we may build a graph Gi
over a given T i for a problem, defined by the following:
• t is a root vertex in the graph if and only if it is contained in the initial state s0.
• There is an edge from t to t′ if and only if every optimal plan for Π(t), (that is, for
the optimal plan up to this substate t), can be extended with a single action to give
an optimal solution to Π(t′)
After building this graph Gi, if there exists a path from a root vertex to a vertex which
satisfies the initial goal, we have an optimal solution.
A path may not exist for a given i, but if it exists for a graph Gi it is guaranteed to
exist for all higher i. We are interested in the lowest value of i for which Gi can give us a
solution; this i determines the width of a given planning problem.

The optimal plan for the plan-

find the substate Location(Agent0, X = 10, Y = 2), successfully determining a path from
t = [Location(Agent0, X = 10, Y = 1)] to t′ = [Location(Agent0, X = 10, Y = 2)]. This
process continues as we examine the possible substates from T 1 which we might arrive at
through the optimal plan, until we reach t = [Location(Agent0, X = 8, Y = 1)]. Since there
exists then a path from the root vertex of agent 0 at (10,1) to (8,1), found by only using
substates used in T 1, we can say that we have found an optimal solution to the planning
problem at width 1. We further exemplify width along the introduction of Iterative Width.
3.5
Iterative Width
Heuristics are however often quite domain-specific and are not applicable across do-
mains.
The Iterative Width algorithm provides a far more domain-agnostic method for
searching efficiently through planning problems.
Iterative Width (IW) is an effective searching algorithm for classical planning problems
introduced by the same authors leveraging the width introduced in subsection 3.4[7].
At first, IW attempts to search for a solution to the problem at width 1. This means the
search prunes any explored states which do not have a sufficient novelty of containing at least
1 literal in a new value which has not been seen previously. (Iterative Width actually starts
Page 11 of 67
==PAGE:15==
DTU - Paying Attention
3.5
Iterative Width
at IW(0), but unless the initial state is a goal state, all other states are pruned because no
new states are permissible with novelty 0. We usually make the assumption that IW starts
at IW(1) to get to the interesting part.)
If a solution to the problem is not found at this width, the width iterates and the
algorithm starts over; a solution is now searched at width 2. At width 2, any new state is
pruned if it does not contain a configuration of literals in a novel pair against each other.
This, we call the state having a novelty of 2 and is a more relaxed requirement than at width
1. Such a novelty may for example, include a state with two separate literals which have
been seen in previous states in their respective values, but not simultaneously, together in
a single state. This simultaneous presence introduces a novel pair of literals which ensures
the state is explored.
Width 3 is simply an even more relaxed novelty requirement: A state is pruned if it
does not contain at least a trio of literals which have not been seen before in their current
configuration. In theory, Iterative Width will continue iterating its width until a solution
is found. If a solution could be found for a problem by brute breadth-first-searching, then
Iterative Width will also eventually find a solution, even if that ultimately requires its width
to increase until it permits novelty of all objects in relation to each other.

4
Defining Attention
Attention is, as a term, defined in the Britannica as the psychological concentrated
awareness on a specific phenomenon or stimuli. While expectation might be for a person’s
current awareness to observe the totality of the myriad of impressions around us impinging
upon all their senses, attention is the subversion of this expectation: The person’s very
omission of total awareness and instead heightened concentration on a slice of that totality.
4.1
The language of Attention
The purpose of our concept of attention is to provide a clear and simple language to
define the matter of focusing particularly on a lesser part of a given problem.
The most basic form of our attention in a search problem is to maintain a subset of
objects in the problem, dubbed the ’attention set’. This set determines which objects we
are ’paying attention to’, and in this way, which objects we are particularly focused on when
performing our search.
Therefore, to say that we are ’paying attention’ to an object in a given problem is to
say that we are including that object, and, in the case of PDDL the literals representing the
presence of that object (understood through a translation depending on the domain of the
problem) in our attention set. We might also, similarly, have to ’determine’ our attention set,
making the decision of which objects to include in it. This ’determination’ is not necessarily
static. We will be discussing the possibilities of paying attention to different things during
a search in section 6.
Then, with a given attention set, we say that we ’apply’ this attention by doing some-
thing with it. In section 5 we examine a simple application of attention which prunes literals
outside of the attention set. In section 7 we instead combine our understanding of attention
and Iterative Width search to create a hybrid search.
While the application of this attention set shifts and changes to accommodate different
applications and the usage of other tools, the concept of the attention set itself remains the
same; a subset of literals in the problem.

Attention-Based Pruning
5.1
Overview
Attention can be applied to problems in different ways, revolving around the fact that
we pay attention to the important (or perhaps all necessary) objects in the problem.
A straightforward and effective application of attention is performing an attention-based
pruning of all literals outside of the attention set.
It must follow that by removing literals from a planning problem, the state space of the
planning problem is reduced and, if the literals we decide to keep in the search contain the
literals necessary for a solution, any search for a solution will be faster.
The caveats to this plan is that we must still have a solution to the problem when
pruning literals.
Furthermore, we should also take care not to dispel the objects which
might be necessary to finding an optimal solution. These issues spring up when we attempt
to determine how to pay attention in given problems. In this section we will merely use
statically pre-determined attention sets handpicked to provide optimal solutions, but in
section 6 we examine the algorithmic options for paying attention.

Discussion
It is undeniable how beneficial pruning the state down to an intuitively defined attention
set is to the search. Even a brute-force search would only have to search through a fraction
of possible states in this reduced state than in the original problem. The problem is of course
that any effective pruning search seems to depend on some magically defined attention set.
We can, as humans intuit what we must pay attention to, but this is the critical key: How
do we know? Can we move this intuition into a general sense? If we could, and all problems
could be pruned down to just their critical components, there would be massive gains to
find in many search problems. We investigate some of the options we have for determining
attention sets in section 6.

Throughout most of the examinations we show in this project, we are using a static

or to prove a point about the application of attention to the given problem.
6.2
Paying Attention in the Blockworld domain
In the Blockworld domain, it’s trivial to specify that we should pay attention to the
blocks mentioned in the goal, but we must additionally be able to pay attention to the blocks
which are not in the goal but are still important to the solving of the problem.
We examine the Blockworld problem given in figure 16. The goal is any state containing
the following truths: On(A, G) ∧Clear(A) ∧On(G, table), that is, the 2 block tower of A
on top of G.
We intuit that any goal we wish to create can be created from entirely new towers placed
on the table. Further, each block mentioned in the goal, in this case blocks A and E must
be able to move to create these new towers. To enable the movement of these blocks, we see
how we must move all blocks on top of them. Towers made of blocks entirely unmentioned
by the goal can be disregarded in totality.
We define a ’subtower from X’ in a Blockworld problem to be the blocks in a tower from
the topmost block down to the block X in the tower. A subtower contains subtowers of all
blocks which are contained in that subtower.
We can reduce any Blockworld problem down to each subtower from each block associ-
ated with the goal. In figure 17 we illustrate the two subtowers from A and from G. Human
intuition tells us that the blocks contained in these towers must be all we require to find a
solution to the given problem; since we need not move any of blocks E, D or F to rearrange
the blocks in the aforementioned subtowers, this would give us a valid attention set to be
used for a attention-based prune search.

So, to summarize this semantic definition: To perform an attention-based prune search
in a given Blockworld problem, we can simply take the following steps:
1. Determine which blocks are in the goal.
2. Determine the subtowers from each of these blocks.
3. Create an attention set out of all the contents of these subtowers.
4. Perform the attention-based pruning of the problem, anchored in the attention set,
and then perform the search on the remaining subproblem. The optimal solution to
this subproblem is an optimal solution in the original problem as well.
This semantic definition is intuitive, but insufficient for an implementation in PDDL.
It becomes unfortunately clear that determining this sort of attention set within PDDL
is just as well as performing a search of the problem in reverse, an endeavour doomed to
mediocrity. Any search in a planning problem from an initial state to a valid goal state can
be reversed and examined as a search from a valid goal state to the initial state; but doing
this does not guarantee an easier search. We examine the process which we explored.
Our intuition for finding the attention set for a Blockworld problem through PDDL
is to ’flood’ through the problem, starting from the goal literals and moving back through
the actions that would affect these, finding out ultimately the subset of literals which are
necessary to perform the actions we need in our solution. The idea here being, that we
did have to perform a search to determine this attention set, but at least the search might
be only linearly dependant on the size of the problem. If we could perform a linear search
first, which would lay the foundation for an attention set which could reduce the subsequent
search for a solution, this second search being exponential in the size of the problem, we

Let’s take a look at what a series of steps could look like when performing this flooding
in PDDL.
First of all, we determine which literals are in our goal; like previously established,
they are: On(A, G) ∧Clear(A) ∧On(G, table). We add these to a precondition set, a set of
literals which we wish to be able to affect through actions. The objective here is to perform
a fast search through the problem which will ultimately reveal to us which literals we should
concern ourselves with to solve the problem.
Ultimately, this should allow us to determine the subtowers in a similar sense as the
semantic definition we established just previously. We would like to do this without the
previously discussed notion of what a subtower actually is; rather we are interested in whether
we can recognize a ’subtower’ from merely performing a pefunctory search throughout the
problem stated in PDDL.
We start out by working our way backwards from the first entity in our precondition
set, the On(A, G) literal, which is not true in the initial state. There exists only one type of
action schema which may provide this effect in the initial state; the Move(A,table,G) action.
Therefore, we know that a Move(A, table, G) action is necessary to attain this literal.
Next, we examine the Move(A, table, G)’s preconditions, and compare these to the
literals which are true in the initial state. Block(A) and Block(G) are both true already.
On(A, Table) is already true. Clear(A) and Clear(G) are however not true. We add these
to our precondition set; previously On(A, G), Clear(A), On(G, table), and now additionally
Clear(G).
Finally, we can look at all of the literals related to the Move(A, table, G) action. Every

them all. So we establish a new set of literals, with the anticipated label of ’Attention Set’.

Next we would like to see what is necessary to make Clear(A) true. Here’s the trouble.
It is important to note that previously, when we determined the Move(A, table, G) action,
this decision hinged on a couple of incredibly important facts which ultimately prove to be
the downfall of this method of determining attention. Firstly, that we are attempting to
move through the problem only once: We are looking at the action which has the correct
effect from the initial state, and we make no assumptions that this action and subsequent
actions (Albeit in a reversed order) could form the solution to the problem. When that action
must be taken, it is not possible to guarantee that it’s preconditions are still true, as other
actions might affect these unwittingly. The intuition here is merely that a flooding through
these actions might give us an overall ’biggest size’ subproblem of the original problem which
we could count on as a valid subproblem to find a solution inside.
However, consider this: We were able to determine Move(A, table, G) as the action
necessary to make On(A, G) true, because no other action would create that effect. Now
tasked with making the next literal in the precondition set true, which could be Clear(A), we
realize there are many effects that make Clear(A) true. Any MoveToTable(B..F, A) would
make this true, as would any Move(B..F, A, B..F). We are able to compare the literals
in the preconditions of these options with those found in the initial state. This guides us
somewhat. While no such action that makes Clear(A) for which all preconditions is true in
the initial state, some actions have more fulfilled preconditions than others:
• MoveToTable(B, A): This is the most intuitive option to take - from our human
perspective. However we cannot determine this in a favourable way in PDDL. Our
search observes that 5 of its preconditions are true and the only precondition which is
not true is Clear(B).
• Move(E, A, H): The feasability of this action is the blow that kills the search. Notice
that 5 of its preconditions are true: the only one which is not is On(E, A).
Unfortunately, pinpointing why MoveToTable(B, A) is the action to take (that the box
will move out of the way of everything in the problem, reducing our necessary focus and
making a solution more accessible) is inexplicable within PDDL without performing any
complicated search - and as soon as we’re performing a search anyway, we might as well give
up on determining an attention set in linear time.
This conundrum turns our at-first ’flood’ for an attention search into just a regular
breadth-first-search. We would have to search through all the actions for turning Clear(A)
true, and in turn find the actions which turn the preconditions for those actions true. This
would ultimately boil down to a BFS, and in the end we would be sitting with a series of

2. For each item in the precondition set, find the action which makes that item true.

• If the item is already in the initial state, simply remove it.
3. When the precondition set is empty, all literals in the attention set constitute a sub-

6.5
In subgoals
In general subgoal planning, the idea is for problems to be divided into subproblems,
and partial solutions to the overall problem presented as ’subgoals’ summed together to
produce solutions much faster. It could be particularly advantageous for subgoal searchers
to make further use of this attention, as much as it already is focusing its attention down to

the problem might not be as feasible). Why not make use of attention in these situations,
with an attention set containing the important objects and then pruning everything else, as
we saw in attention-based pruning? The notion translates seamlessly and we observe that
for any problem divided into lesser portions, we can just as well use our notion of focusing

6.6
Reducing Attention
As we will also observe in section 8, search problems are rarely static in the size of
objects necessary to be manipulated for a solution. A problem might often have areas of a
solution where few options are necessary to decide between to move towards the solution,
while other times we might have many different objects that could all be necessary to the
solution; and this may shift and waver as the search proceeds.
While we spend much time discussing possibilities for paying attention, the inverse and
even harder problem is that of how to neglect items again after we have paid attention to
them. Objects must necessarily leave our attention space as well, or attention would only
grow, reducing its effectiveness as we see in section 8 when the local width of the problem
decreases. It’s not hard to make domain-specific notions of leaving attention (an example
could be that, in a hospital level, objects on their respective goals are removed from attention,
in most cases), but we cannot provide a universal way to decrease attention in all search
problems.

Auxiliary Width Search
7.1
Overview
An intuition was made that while we could not necessarily create a domain-agnostic
specification for an attention set for attention-based pruning for many search problems, they
could be solved primarily by the same literals which were featured in the goals.
Consider the level shown in figure 21. There exist many pairs of objects, but the most
important objects we wish to move are agent 0 and box A, the same objects featured in
the goals, and creating an attention set solely based off what we see in the goals is an easy
determination. So we allow the search planner to create an attention set from just these
objects (Agent 0 and box A) before running the search itself.
In an Attention-based pruning search, the planner would dismiss boxes B and C, but in
this case that would make the problem entirely unsolvable, since box B turning into a wall
would block necessary passage.
Figure 21: A level in the hospital domain.
Further, we were concerned with how paying attention (explored in section 6) is a
particularly tough problem to crack. While we investigated a method of adding box B to the
attention in 6.3 we would like a generalized solution that would work across PDDL problems.
A search algorithm which allowed for more lax requirements for the given attention
set allows for simpler definitions of paying attention, which permitted less domain-specific
solutions while still making gains in search efficiency.

7.2
In practice
The next natural step was then to build a more relaxed version of the Attention-Based
prune search.
Auxiliary width search is based upon the Iterative Width search algorithm, however
with a few alterations. We summarize the differences here and then examine the algorithm
in detail.
• The primary difference is the inclusion of the attention set as a bare minimum of a sort
of exclusive width. This exclusive attention contains all the objects in an attention set,
and we expand any state in which any of these objects have changed their values. Any
state where no objects from the attention set have had their values changed is pruned.
The ’width’ of this attention is the number of objects in the attention set, understood
from the perspective of their respective domains. In the hospital domain this could be
a specific agent and a specific box.
• Objects that may become indistinguishable from one another are given an additional
identifier. In practice this means boxes with the same id are given a unique ID. This
allows us to track boxes which we have decided to pay attention to, despite other boxes
of similar properties existing in the level.
• In addition to this exclusive set of objects in attention, we provide an auxiliary width
which starts at 0. If no solution is found at auxiliary width 0 with search only performed
on the objects in attention, we increment this auxiliary width and restart the search.
Then, in this new search, we permit any state where either an object in the attention
has changed value, or in which we see a sufficiently novel set of foreign objects similarly
to Iterative Width.
This should mean that Auxiliary Width Search is able to solve problems for which
objects might block the solution are not in the attention set, by raising a separate, auxiliary,
width which allows movement of additional objects.
While this approach works, it is not terribly efficient.

In practice
We provide a simple example. The problem presented in figure 22 has a total state
space size of around 49·48 = 2.352 different states, with about 49 possible locations for each
agent, minus the position of the other agent. Test results found at 11.2.15.
We predetermine the attention set to consist simply of agent 0. This we assert statically
and intuitively, but any simple planner could determine this itself by simply looking at the
goal and determining that moving agent 0 is necessary for a solution.
Now, when running the search, any state where only agent 1 has moved will be pruned.
This means we will only examine states where agent 0 has moved (though, due to the nature
of joint-actions in the hospital domain, agent 1 may also perform moves simultaneously).

7.3
Discussion
It becomes clear to us that the compromise of relaxed requirements to the attention set
comes at a cost that seems steep. Auxiliary Width can handle problems where we ordinarily
might only be able to guess at an appropriate attention set, and with correct attention sets
handles problems in about a similar number of states as an attention-based pruning search
would do.
Auxiliary Width is further able to find solutions to problems faster than Iterative Width
when IW reaches comparable widths, but where Auxiliary Width is able to focus on the
objects it already knows are necessary to a solution. Like we saw in the levels in figures 22
and 23, Auxiliary Width found solutions by expanding only a fraction of the states Iterative
Width did.
Currently, Auxiliary Width underperforms drastically when the attention set misguides
it, and just as with Attention-Based prune searches we must again point at the ’paying
attention’ part of attention as the keystone of speeding up our planning searches. But we
see also, that this doesn’t necessitate an entire dismissal of Auxiliary Width Search.
With some adjustments, we could rewrite Auxiliary Width Search with the following
critical changes. When the search is exhausted at auxiliary width 0, that is, when the search
has attempted everything it could by only manipulating the literals in attention, perhaps
instead of increasing the auxiliary width right away, we could allow for different literals but
within the same width as the attention width.
Consider again the level given in figure 26. Consider this time that the attention set
consisted of only a single box A and no agent. At auxiliary width 0, we would investigate
no state in which only an agent moved, and since the box is incapable of moving on its
own, the search would want to increase the auxiliary width. But instead, consider that we
remain at total width 1 (from the attention set consisting of 1 object), but now allow any
Page 40 of 67
==PAGE:44==
DTU - Paying Attention
state in which this novelty is found from any object, not just the box in our attention. Here,
Auxiliary Width Search would find a solution at width 1, through a search that would be
identical to IW(1): Sure, there is no gain, but more importantly: There is no loss. And
so, we can reconcile Auxiliary Width Search with attention in a manner that allows us to
benefit with very primitive attention sets. An attention set consisting only of the objects in
the goal now promises to either find a solution at least as effectively as Iterative Width.
Consider further, if through a prioritization in the search, we still search through the
states where the box in the attention set move primarily, prioritizing less the states where only
agents move, without culling them entirely. This might only be a domain-specific observation
to the hospital domain set of problems, but it’s clearly beneficial without hinging entirely
on domain intuitions: Many search problems require at least that the object which must
accomplish a goal value should be moved, so prioritizing states where that occurs seems
intuitive.
So while Auxiliary Width Search, especially in its current iteration, provides only out-
lying benefits in certain cases, it provides a strong ground for something deeper.
8
Least Width Search
8.1
Overview
We recognized that while Iterative Width is very effective at solving many problems far
more efficiently than BFS, there is still great potential for improving upon the search with
just a few simple observations.
In Least Width search, when exploring every action possible in a state, all these poten-
tial states are investigated for novelty, like ordinarily in Iterative Width. However, unlike
Iterative Width, all these states are added to the search frontier regardless of width. The
frontier then prioritizes exploring states which have the lowest width. Of these, states are
sorted on whichever are oldest (similarly to BFS, which does this to guarantee an optimal
solution), but this is secondary to the sorting on width.
States which thus require a novelty of 2 to move into only occur when the search has
exhausted all possible states which can be reached with a novelty of 1.
This way, after performing any action which has a width of 2, that is, an action which
results in a state that can only be added to the search by presenting novelty for at least 2
objects in relation to one another, the search will prioritize states which present novelty only
at width 1 again (following typical rules for search planners that any state already seen will
not be explored again).

In BringFriendsNotB2, featured in the leftmost image, a problem width of 2 necessitates

In CleanTunnel, while the same overall phenomenon is applicable as to BringFriend-

After bringing Agent 0 past the first two boxes by first pulling out the leftmost box, then
increasing the width to 2 for agent 0 to pass back into the first tunnel, the agent then pushes
the second box out of the way. However, now faced with the second tunnel and second set
of obstacles, the search necessarily must increase its width, which prompts it to suddenly
explore all of the states it previously avoided where it moved around the two previous boxes
unnecessarily. Test results for CleanTunnel are found at 11.2.11.
8.3
Discussion
Least Width Search proves to be incredibly effective in certain types of problems, where
a large width is required, as long as it is only once during the problem that it must rise
and decrease. With this albeit strict requirement, Least Width Search explores orders of
magnitudes fewer states than Iterative Width.
Page 44 of 67
==PAGE:48==
DTU - Paying Attention
However, in many problems it does not provide any clear benefits over Iterative Width.
In tests made on the levels BringFriendsNotB (section 11.2.6), BringFriendsNotB2 (section
11.2.7) and BringFriendsNotB3 (section 11.2.8) we see that Least Width provides no signifi-
cant gains. But, at the same time, with only minor losses in overhead computational expense,
Least Width performs overall similarly, indicating that Least Width may be a candidate for
a just-as-good.
It is clear that Least Width Search provides huge gains as it allows us to ’leap’ over a
large obstacle, whereupon the rest of the problem can be solved at a lower width, but since
this oscillation of width does not occur localized where it is needed, it instead stumbles the
whole search.
If, for example, we could in some way apply Least Width but ’forget’ about previous
high-width states later in the search, we could perhaps ’partition’ Least Width, allowing
it to rise and fall to meet obstacles within areas of the problem without then falling back
to searching through previous, superfluous states. We provide a visual example of such a
partitioning of the level CleanTunnel in figure 31.
Figure 31: A suggested ’partitioning’ to CleanTunnel in which, if LeastWidth could distin-
guish solving these areas separately, we could have huge gains in efficiency.
Partitioning these sections is however not as intuitive. Doing so can be straightforward
enough when using domain-specific intuition derived from looking at the hospital domain
level, but recognizing viable partitions when looking at a problem in PDDL is a lot harder,
and doing so algorithmically especially so.
Least Width Search generally performs identically to Iterative Width in Blockworld
domain problems, since they often have a width of 2 and maintain this required novelty
throughout the problem.
9
Progressive Width Search
9.1
Overview
Progressive Width Search was developed on a (misguided) assumption that Least Width
would explore too many states at a lower width when constantly attempting the search at
the lowest width possible.
The design turned out to be presumed on wrong assumptions, but we include this brief
section for comprehension. We will briefly explain the underlying thoughts and the results.
Page 45 of 67
==PAGE:49==

9.1
Overview
As an example, observe the level in figure 32.
Figure 32: The hospital domain level ’DontStopNow’.
We made the supposed observation that when a Least Width search performs an action
at width 2, it seemed to be presented with a new small state space of states to search
through at width 1. This work seemed superfluous. Often, what the search needed to do to
find the solution was keep performing a series of actions with novelty 2, until it was free of
the ’obstacle’ in its way. See figure 32 for a visualization.
When all states at a certain width are exhausted, we increase the width of the search
and continue the search, similarly to how Least Width Search would do.
However, we
theorized that instead of simply using the lowest novelty action possible at all times, we
instead maintain a decreasing counter. When the width is increased, we raise this counter.
This counter is set to a certain ’exploration value’, either statically or dynamically
determined, and then the search explores this number of states at the current width. If we
exhaust all possible states again, the width is further raised and the counter set again. If
the counter runs out, we instead lower the width at this current point in the search and keep
searching on this lower width instead.
The counter would have to be raised to an appropriate approximated exploration value.
In static solutions, the counter could be set to decrease after exploring e.g. 1000 states. This
allows for a measure of granularity, that after every 1000 states the search would ’try to see’
if a lower width was sufficient for a solution. In development of the algorithm, we explored
how dynamic exploration values could be determined. The problem with a static value is
that it does not account for the fact that search problems can vary hugely in size; a dynamic
value that was raised exponentially could better accommodate the necessary flexibility of
search problems.
The idea here, was that Progressive Width would overcome certain obstacles faster,
where Least Width would spend too much time on superfluous lower width states.

10
Conclusion
A notion of attention within search problems aligns neatly within a both human un-
derstanding of the concept and gains from applying this attention to search problems in
different ways. However it seems futile to attempt to reconcile this notion of attention with
the ideal of finding solutions that work across search problems without deriving intuitions
based on the specific domains in which this attention is to be used.
It’s clear that benefits can be extracted from reducing any problem down a lesser sub
problem. This understanding is only reinforced by the many tests we’ve run, comparing
this attention-based pruning to other search engines. Auxiliary Width also provides some
strong results, especially when run on problems with a width much lower than the number of
objects in the problem. Here it performed better than Iterative Width when it could focus
its search through only relevant objects. Though we see in some instances how it would
awkwardly search on higher widths than Iterative Width, this could be improved with some
minor adjustments to the algorithm’s width expansion.
All of this, however, is frustrated by the requirement of having to actually determine
which objects to pay attention to.
In any domain-agnostic sense, we can define simple
attention sets by using the literals provided in the goal of the problem. But doing anything
else seems to require either using higher-level conceptual intuitions about the problems,
creating very domain-specific attention determinations, or simply result in actual searches
to determine attention sets. This latter simply brings us back to step one and is provides no
benefits.
We see that Auxiliary Width Search alleviates this issue somewhat though, providing
overall gains by reconciling Iterative Width with the notion of attention in a more forgiving
union. We’re excited to share these results, as they do indicate that we can make gains
within the field by using some notion of attention.
Additionally, Least Width Search surprises in both its simplicity and effectiveness. Of
course it is only applicable to certain classes of problems where a width increases and then
decreases, but there is huge potential for combining the practices within Least Width Search
with any sort of compartmentalization of search problems or with other heuristics. Obvious
unions would be combining Least Width Search with Auxiliary Width, containing perhaps a
’prioritized attention’ - attempting to move some objects over others, and as few as possible,
like those contained in the goal, over a given secondary category, over finally a general
category for remaining objects, could perhaps provide further gains, at least in some types
of problems like those in the hospital domain.
We intuited no important gains from these different search algorithms, with the ex-
ception of attention-based pruning, in the Blockworld domain.
We hold the belief that
the Blockworld domain simply does not lend itself to improvements through leveraging the
width of search problems in algorithms. Most Blockworld problems with simple goals have a
width of 2 and as most of our attempts build on Iterative Width there simply is no inherent
effective power being found in these searches.
