Abstract
Increasingly intense and more frequent rainfall due to climate change poses major chal-
lenges for stormwater management in Copenhagen. To enhance climate resilience in the
city, HOFOR has implemented multiple Green Road projects integrating rain gardens and
underlying infiltration trenches designed to retain and infiltrate stormwater runoff or lead
it to the sewer with a delay. This project evaluates the hydraulic performance of six in-
filtration trenches at Ærtebjergvej in Husum, Copenhagen using 1.5 years of continuous
water level and precipitation measurements. Hydraulic conductivity (K) was estimated
using an infiltration model that assumes unit-gradient flow through both trench sides and
bottoms. A key objective was to maximise data utilisation to see if this could provide a
more robust analysis of K, rather than relying solely on selected single events. For this
purpose, a Python script was developed for data processing, where data were smoothed
and filtered to automatically select all water level recession data suitable for K estimation.
K values were estimated using the full filtered dataset for the entire measurement period,
individual seasons, and selected single events (one recession limb), allowing assessment of
variability due to seasonality, water depth, and event to event differences, as well as com-
parison between data inputs. Water levels varied substantially between trenches during
the 1.5 years, resulting in differences in amounts of usable data for K estimation. Results
showed seasonal variation (highest K in summer/autumn and lowest in winter), decreas-
ing K with successive rain events, and dependence on water level due to varying soil
saturation with trench depth. Comparing average K for full dataset and multiple single
events showed comparable though slightly lower averages for full dataset, likely reflecting
the influence of not filtering out periods with continued inflow to the trench. Using the
full dataset maximised data utilisation and efficiently captured a wider range of variability
compared to a single event, thereby providing more robust seasonal and annual averages
of K. Slug test results from nearby locations were about two orders of magnitude higher
than results obtained in this project, emphasizing the influence of spatial variability and
applied method on K estimation.
During the 1.5 years, total trench capacity was never exceeded, and only one rain event
caused overflow to the sewer in three trenches. Observed return periods differed from more
detailed design tool estimates, likely due to uncertainties in actual catchment areas and
observed water levels. These uncertainties in combination with the six trenches’ deviations
from HOFOR’s new design criteria, complicated the assessment of the new design’s effec-
tiveness and influence of K on total trench capacity. Given more general uncertainties in
K estimates from slug tests and catchment specification, a simpler design approach such
as HOFOR’s new practice however appears more practical than detailed dimensioning. To
account for the variety of K that influences capacity, a future guideline could benefit from
more flexible designs that adapt throttle unit height or specific storage volume to differ-
ent K ranges. Further research should include refining the data filtering procedure, field
measurements (soil moisture/temperature) to support K variability assessment, improved
approach for estimation with slug test and defining a reasonable design K, as well as
evaluating throttle unit height and specific storage volume in relation to runoff reduction
and sewer surcharge, to strengthen the basis for a future design guideline.

Introduction
Cities around the world are increasingly exposed to the risks posed by climate change,
including flooding, droughts, and extreme precipitation and temperatures (World Bank,
2021). These climate-related hazards have adverse impacts on ecosystems, economic sec-
tors, and human health and well-being, therefore making adaptation to climate change
crucial (European Environment Agency, 2024).
In Denmark more frequent and intense heavy precipitation events present one of the great-
est challenges that calls for adaptation actions (The Danish Ministry of Environment And
Gender Equality, 2024).
Most of the sewer system in the Danish capital Copenhagen
handles both wastewater and stormwater, but during heavy rain events the system may
become overloaded, resulting in flooding and damages, as the city is not built to handle
large amounts of water on the surface (City of Copenhagen, 2012). To increase resilience
to future rainfall patterns and reduce flood risk, the City of Copenhagen adopted a Cloud-
burst Management Plan in 2012. The plan recommends adaptive measures which both
protect the city against pluvial flooding and at the same time reduce the pressure on the
drainage system during ordinary rainfall, which will also become more frequent due to
climate change. Adaptive measures at ground level are one of the preferred solutions to
manage stormwater and reduce runoff, as they are both easier and cheaper to effectuate
than underground measures and can contribute to blue and green infrastructure in the
city (City of Copenhagen, 2012).
The multiple types of surface adaptation solutions are based on combinations of the pro-
cesses delay, evaporation, infiltration, transport (discharge) and purification. Some exam-
ples are green roofs, rain gardens, infiltration trenches, and elements for delay and storage
such as emergency flood channels and basins (Vezzaro et al., 2022). Many of the solutions
are in Danish context referred to with the term Lokal Afledning af Regnvand (LAR), while
in a more global context several different terms are used such as Nature Based Solutions
and Sustainable Urban Drainage Systems (Vezzaro et al., 2022; Fletcher et al., 2014).
This project focuses on six infiltration trenches established by Greater Copenhagen Utility
(Hovedstadsomr˚adets Forsyningsselskab, HOFOR) to manage stormwater runoff in an area
in Husum, Copenhagen. HOFOR is the utility company for the Copenhagen metropolitan
area, and besides supplying drinking water, district heating and cooling, town gas and
disposal of wastewater and stormwater, HOFOR is also working on a large number of
cloudburst projects to secure the capital area against flooding after cloudbursts and heavy
rain (HOFOR, 2024a). The six infiltration trenches placed on the street Ærtebjergvej
in Husum are part of one of HOFOR’s Green Road projects, which are roads with rain
gardens and underlying infiltration trenches. This type of surface solution is often used
in cities as it can be adapted to the existing urban design and does not take up a lot of
space compared to other types of surface solutions (HOFOR, 2024c). HOFOR has already
established 18 Green Road projects with almost 400 rain gardens in total and is still
working on several new projects as part of their efforts for undertaking climate adaptation
(HOFOR, 2024b).
A small-scale stormwater infiltration system such as HOFOR’s system consisting of a
rain garden and underlying infiltration trench is designed to temporarily store stormwater
1
==PAGE:9==
runoff underground, where it will slowly infiltrate into the surrounding soil or be led to
the sewer with a delay (HOFOR, 2024b; Bergman et al., 2011). Stormwater runoff will
flow to the rain gardens instead of the sewer, thereby decreasing the total amount of water
entering the sewer and during heavy rainfall minimizing the risk of sewer surcharge and
flooding (HOFOR, 2024b). The runoff entering the rain gardens will infiltrate through
filter soil and then be led to the underlying infiltration trench from where it will infiltrate
the surrounding soil over time. If the water table in the infiltration trenches reaches a
certain level, it will start overflowing to the sewer to relieve pressure on the infiltration
trench system and avoid local flooding.
To examine the performance of the infiltration trenches, sensors have been installed in
each of the six infiltration trenches on Ærtebjergvej to collect measurements for data
analysis. The sensors measure the water level in the infiltration trenches and have been
collecting data since the late summer of 2023. The infiltration into the soil is essential for
the infiltration trenches’ function and how they perform, and it depends on a number of
conditions. These conditions include the soil’s ability to transport the water, also called
hydraulic conductivity, dimensions and geometry of the infiltration trench, the level of
the water table in the infiltration trench, and the soil’s structure and water saturation
(Mikkelsen et al., 1998). With the measurements and data available for Ærtebjergvej it
is possible to describe some of these processes mathematically and set up an infiltration
model for data analysis of the infiltration trenches’ performance.
1.1
Study aim
This MSc project will evaluate the hydraulic performance of the six infiltration trenches
for stormwater runoff on Ærtebjergvej in Husum based on data from the obtained mea-
surements. An infiltration model that uses water level data will be applied to estimate
the hydraulic conductivity for each of the six infiltration trenches.
The analysis aims
to maximise the use of the available data to provide a more robust assessment of hy-
draulic conductivity, and to investigate how different factors affect system performance.
By obtaining a better understanding of the hydraulic performance, the study also aims to
assess possible improvements for HOFOR’s current design guideline for infiltration trench
systems. The research objectives of the study are:
• Estimate the hydraulic conductivity in the six infiltration trenches on Ærtebjergvej
using an infiltration model set up in Python
• Assess how the variation in hydraulic conductivity is affected by annual seasonality,
water depth, and from rain event to rain event
• Evaluate how the analysis results compare with HOFOR’s current design guideline
and assess whether the guideline can be improved.

2
Theoretical Background
This section provides the theoretical foundation for the analyses conducted in this project.
It begins with a general overview of stormwater management and the generation of runoff,
followed by a more detailed description of infiltration trenches, including design principles,
function, dimensioning and capacity, and a description of the infiltration model used in
the analysis for estimating hydraulic conductivity.
2.1
Stormwater management
Increasing urbanization worldwide, with the construction of buildings, roads, and other
impervious surfaces, significantly alters the water balance. More rainwater flows as sur-
face runoff into drainage systems, while less infiltrates the soil, reducing evaporation and
groundwater recharge. At the same time climate change predictions point to a higher fre-
quency of heavy rain events, increasing the amount of surface runoff even further (Lerer
and Sørup, 2016; Ebrahimian et al., 2021).
In Copenhagen most surface runoff is handled by a combined sewer system, where wastew-
ater and surface runoff flow into the same sewer pipe (City of Copenhagen, 2018). An
increased amount of surface runoff will cause a relatively lower capacity of the combined
sewer system, resulting in more frequent flooding and discharges into the environment
(Lerer and Sørup, 2016).
To prevent overloading of the sewer system and the risk of
flooding in Copenhagen as well as other Danish cities, managing the stormwater locally
has become popular. In Danish context local stormwater management is refereed to with
the term Lokal Afledning af Regnvand (LAR), which directly translates to Local Drainage
of Rainwater. In other parts of the word terms such as Nature Based Solutions (NBS),
Sustainable Urban Drainage Systems (SUDS), Green Infrastructure (GI), Low Impact De-
velopment (LID), and Best Management Practices (BMP) are used to describe similar
types of stormwater management practices (Vezzaro et al., 2022; Fletcher et al., 2014).
The term LAR refers to a concept focused on managing stormwater runoff from impervi-
ous urban surfaces near its source. The goal is not only to reduce strain on the drainage
system but also to improve the local environment by integrating visible water features
and vegetation that creates greener and more attractive urban spaces (Lerer and Sørup,
2016). LAR includes measures aimed at reducing and delaying stormwater runoff. This
can be achieved through methods such as infiltration into the ground, temporary deten-
tion, storage in landscape features or constructed basins, and evaporation either directly
from surfaces or through uptake by vegetation. These components can be applied individ-
ually or as part of a combined system (Teknologisk Institut, nda). Concrete examples of
LAR elements include infiltration trenches, rain gardens, green roofs, swales and channels,
basins (wet and dry), permeable pavement, and other decentralized structures (LAR i
Danmark, ndb).

2.2
Runoff generation
To describe the function of infiltration trenches it is important to understand the path
of water before it enters the system. When precipitation starts to fall, dry surfaces will
become wet and the water will start to accumulate in small puddles, eventually generating
surface water runoff also called stormwater (Mikkelsen et al., 1998; Butler et al., 2018).
However, not all water that falls as precipitation and reaches the ground will run off to
the drainage system, as some of the water is lost immediately or as it runs overland.
Water lost immediately is referred to as initial losses i (often given in mm), which is the
proportion of precipitation that needs to fall before surface runoff is created (Butler et al.,
2018). After the initial losses, some of the runoff will also infiltrate through unpaved areas
and thereby not reach the drainage system. The amount of rainfall that actually becomes
runoff that reaches the drainage system is represented by the runoff coefficient, denoted
as φ (Mikkelsen et al., 1998).
2.3
Design principles for an infiltration trench and rain garden
In principle, an infiltration trench is a hole in the ground filled with a porous material.
Surface runoff is directed to the infiltration trench, where it is temporarily stored, allowing
for gradual infiltration into the surrounding soil through the sides and bottom (Petersen
et al., 1994). Figure 1 illustrates the basic design principles of an infiltration trench. The
porous material in an infiltration trench can consist of stones, LECA (light expanded
clay aggregate) balls, or plastic crates, which all have different void percentages (LAR i
Danmark, nda). When using plastic crates that are specifically designed for infiltration
trenches the void percentages can be up to 95%, whereas using stones typically results in
a void percentage of around 25% (Teknologisk Institut, ndb).
Runoff entering the infiltration trench - typically via pipes or surface channels - first passes
through a sedimentation well (sand trap), which serves to remove coarse particles such
as sand and leaves from the water (Klimatilpasning.dk, nd). However, not all suspended
solids are removed, and over time, the bottom of the infiltration trench will often become
clogged. As a result, the majority of infiltration tends to occur through the side walls.
For this reason, infiltration trenches are typically designed with a narrow and elongated
storage volume to maximize the wall surface area. This design allows for faster infiltration
compared to a more cubical trench design (LAR i Danmark, nda).
An infiltration trench can be combined with an overlying rain garden. Runoff will then
flow to the rain garden, from where it will infiltrate a layer of filter soil that purifies
the water before it reaches the underlying trench. A rain garden will typically contain
vegetation that enhances both water uptake and infiltration. Furthermore, the vegetation
helps to stabilize the filter soil (HOFOR, nd). When selecting vegetation, it is important
to choose species that are robust and capable of withstanding both prolonged dry spells
and periods where water accumulate in the rain garden (LAR i Danmark, ndc). Suitable
plants includes shrubs, perennials, and ornamental grasses (Rørcentret, 2012).

2.4
Infiltration trench function
In general, the infiltration capacity is essential for the function of the infiltration trench
and it depends on a number of conditions listed below (Mikkelsen et al., 1998):
• The soil’s ability to transport the water (hydraulic conductivity)
• Dimensions and geometry of the infiltration trench
• The level of the water table in the infiltration trench
• The soil’s structure, heterogeneity and stratification as well as the temperature of
the soil and water
• Soil water saturation conditions (capillary action)
• The location of the groundwater table
The following sections elaborate on some of these factors influencing infiltration from a
trench, as well as the setup of an infiltration model to describe the function of an infiltration
trench.

2.4.1
Hydraulic conductivity and influence of temperature and moisture con-
ditions
Hydraulic conductivity describes how easily water or any other fluid moves through a
porous medium such as soil, and it is represented by the parameter K, with units of velocity
(Nazaroff and Alvarez-Cohen, 2001). K primarily depends on the pore size of the porous
medium and the viscosity of the fluid passing through it. For soils with varying grain sizes,
a wide range of K values is observed, where clays typically have low conductivity (often
less than 10−8 m/s), while coarse sands have much higher conductivity (often greater than
10−2 m/s) (Nazaroff and Alvarez-Cohen, 2001).
When water begins to infiltrate into dry soil, the initial process is characterized by unsatu-
rated flow, where capillary forces draw water into the soil pores. As infiltration continues,
the pores gradually fill, reducing the influence of capillary forces, and the flow transitions
to saturated conditions. Under saturated flow, gravity becomes the dominant driving force
for infiltration, where the rate also depends on any hydraulic head above the soil surface
- where a greater head results in faster infiltration (Bockhorn et al., 2015). In saturated
soils, K is referred to as the saturated hydraulic conductivity (Ksat). Infiltration under
saturated conditions proceeds more slowly than during the initial, unsaturated phase.
Therefore, the antecedent moisture conditions of the soil prior to infiltration (e.g., follow-
ing prolonged dry periods or multiple successive rain events) are also important factors
influencing K.
Infiltration-based LAR systems, such as infiltration trenches, are typically dimensioned
based on Ksat, which is a critical factor for their design and performance (Bockhorn et al.,
2015; Ebrahimian et al., 2020). Ksat should not be regarded a constant, as it can vary
both temporally and spatially due to a number of factors that can be both complex and
interrelated. Factors that might cause spatial variation include soil texture, plant root
structure and morphology, sediment deposition, porosity, and soil moisture, and factors
that might cause temporal variation include clogging, soil compaction, and temperature
changes (Ebrahimian et al., 2020).
Especially variation in K with temperature is a key factor in understanding the dynamic
performance of infiltration-based LAR systems, and temperature effects on K are also the
primary cause of seasonal variation (Ebrahimian et al., 2020). Seasonal changes in both
infiltration rates and recession rates have been observed in multiple studies, with higher
rates in summer and lower rates in winter (Ebrahimian et al., 2021; Emerson et al., 2010;
Lin et al., 2003). The increased infiltration rates with temperature are caused by several
temperature-dependent changes in soil and water. These include lower soil water viscosity
at higher temperatures as well as thermal expansion of soil particles, reduced surface
tension and reduced entrapped air in the soil with increasing temperature (Ebrahimian
et al., 2021).
2.5
Infiltration model
Mathematically describing all the processes that influence infiltration in an infiltration
trench system can be highly complex. Consequently, previous studies have employed a
6
==PAGE:14==
variety of models with differing levels of detail to simulate the infiltration process. Some
authors have applied detailed 2-dimensional models that account for soil moisture and the
effect of water head (Duchene et al., 1994; Browne et al., 2013), however the complexity
of these models may limit their implementation in modelling and design of infiltration
systems (Sokolovskaya et al., 2021). The use of soil equations such as the Green-Ampt
equation has also been widely applied to model infiltration systems (Lee et al., 2013;
Heasom et al., 2006), while a simplified assumption of constant infiltration rate is used
in other models (Argue and Pezzaniti, 2003).
Some studies also consider clogging in

Model for estimation of hydraulic conductivity
In this project the estimation of hydraulic conductivity K will be based on an infiltration
model introduced by Mikkelsen (1995), which has been applied in several studies of an
infiltration trench system in Nørrebro, Copenhagen (Larsen and Jacobsen, 1995; Mikkelsen
et al., 1998; Warnaars et al., 1999; Bergman et al., 2011). The infiltration model assumes
unit-gradient flow through the wetted surface area of the trench (sides and bottom), which
varies with the water level in the trench (Figure 2).

2.6
Infiltration trench dimensioning and capacity
2.6.1
SVK’s LAR spreadsheet
In Danish context the The Water Pollution Committee’s (Spildevandskomiteen, SVK)
LAR spreadsheet (Spildevandskomiteen, 2023) is widely applied when dimensioning infil-
tration trenches. It builds on the principles of SVK’s guideline no. 25 (Petersen et al.,
1994) but uses updated rainfall statistics and a more robust calculation method. The gov-
erning design parameter is the return period T for overload, which reflects how frequently
an exceedance of the trench’s capacity is considered acceptable (Klimatilpasning.dk, nd).
The K value for the soil is also a key parameter for the calculation of dimensions, as it
controls the emptying time (te) of how fast the trench can empty between rain events.
The spreadsheet uses a statistical rainfall series as the basis for calculations. The main
input parameters for the spreadsheet include:
• The municipality, which determines local annual mean precipitation
• The design return period T
• A combined safety factor (typically including a climate factor, model uncertainty,
and future changes in imperviousness)
• The contributing reduced area
• The soil hydraulic conductivity K
• Selection of with or without infiltration through the trench bottom
From these inputs, the spreadsheet calculates the specific storage volume (vt) in mm, the
total required trench volume (Vt) in m3, the corresponding geometric dimensions, and the
resulting emptying time.
2.6.2
HOFOR’s design guideline
In 2023 HOFOR developed a new design guideline for hydraulic dimensioning of Green
Roads, which is also the current guideline (Figure A1, Appendix A.1). The dimensioning
principles from HOFOR’s design guideline determines that the infiltration trench under
the rain gardens must be dimensioned based on a specific storage volume of 30 mm. The
rain garden itself provides approximately 8 mm of retention volume, resulting in a total
retention-detention storage of 38 mm. The guideline notes that a K value of 1 · 10−5 m/s
would result in a volume of 38 mm for a return period T = 10, with a total safety factor
of 1.0, according to SVK’s LAR spreadsheet (calculated with no infiltration through the
trench bottom). The variability of K values that can affect the capacity are taken into
account, by distinguishing between the following cases:
• K > 1 · 10−5 m/s: infiltration only
• K < 1 · 10−5 m/s: combination of infiltration and sewer discharge
10
==PAGE:18==
For the combined infiltration-sewer discharge case, the guideline dictates that an adjustable
throttle unit must be installed at a level halfway up the trench (30 cm above the bottom),
with a fixed discharge rate of 1 L/s.
The primary objective for HOFOR is to reduce sewer surcharge. One of HOFOR’s reasons
for not using SVK’s approach is that, at low K values, the governing rainfall duration be-
comes unrealistically long (representing events that the sewer system would generally be
able to manage), which increases the risk of over-dimensioning relative to HOFOR’s de-
sign objective. The simpler design also makes communication easier, allowing the project
team to proceed directly with the design without additional input from the planning de-
partment or hydraulics specialists, which is another of HOFOR’s arguments for not using
SVK’s LAR spreadsheet for dimensioning (Rozario, 2024).

2.6.4
Retention-detention volumes
A study by Locatelli et al. (2015) investigated a retention-detention unit (similar to the
infiltration trenches on Ærtebjergvej) with a retention volume aimed for storage and in-
filtration and a detention volume aimed at delaying peak flows (Figure 4).
Figure 4: Illustration from the study by Locatelli et al. (2015). The retention-detention unit
(left) and simulation results of the effect of different retention-detention volume combinations on
annual runoff and single event peak runoff reduction (right).
By using the modelling software MIKE URBAN the impact of retrofitting a retention-
detention unit with a total specific storage volume of 33 mm was simulated for a small
12
==PAGE:20==
catchment in Copenhagen. The study showed that the retention-detention unit provided
a more robust solution for peak runoff reduction than using retention volume alone, as
the detention volume is empty at the beginning of single events and has the capability
of detaining peak flows. Furthermore, it was showed that detention requires significantly
less storage compared to retention, where a retention volume designed for a 5-year return
period required 69 mm of storage capacity and a detention volume designed for a 10-year
return period required 19 mm of storage capacity.
It was examined how different combinations of retention and detention volumes affected
both annual runoff and single event peak runoff reduction. The simulation results, shown
in Figure 4, indicated that a distribution of 40% detention and 60% retention of the total
volume achieved the most optimal effect, with an 80% reduction in single event peak
runoff and approximately 78% reduction in annual runoff. From the simulation results the
study draw two conclusions that allocating part of a retention volume to detention can
significantly improve peak runoff reduction, while allocating part of a detention volume to
retention can enhance annual runoff reduction with little impact on peak runoff reduction.

the Python-based script developed for processing and analysing the measurement data,
including steps for handling outliers, data smoothing, adjusting temporal resolution, fil-
tering relevant data, and the input data for analysis including selection of single events.
Finally, the use of AI in the project is also described.

4.2
Data processing and analysis
4.2.1
Data input for K estimation
In previous studies of infiltration trenches in Denmark, the estimation of K has typically
been based on water level data from single events being continuous periods with water
in the system or periods covering recession limbs after a rain event (Mikkelsen et al.,
1998; Bergman et al., 2011; Rindorf, 2024). With the availability of an extensive dataset
consisting of 1.5 years of continuous monitoring at resolutions down to one minute, a
central objective of this project is to maximise data utilisation in order to see if this can
provide a more robust analysis of K, rather than relying solely on selected single events.
To maximise data utilisation, the analysis of K will not be limited to selected single
events, but will include all usable water level data throughout the measurement period.
In this way, valuable data is not omitted, even if it occurs during short periods between
rain events. K will also be estimated based on data from selected single events within
the measurement period to analyse variation in K from rain event to rain event and to
compare with results obtained from all usable water level data. Thus, in this project, K
will be estimated based on two different data inputs:
• Full dataset: All usable water level data from the entire measurement period and
from individual seasons within this period.
24
==PAGE:32==
• Single events: Selected usable water level data covering a single recession limb fol-
lowing a rain event.
For both data inputs, the usable water level data will be selected by filtering all available
data, based on a number of criteria defined in Section 4.2.5.
4.2.2
Outliers
By observing the raw water level data, it is evident that outliers occasionally occur. There-
fore, a gradient range test is applied to validate the data and help identify such outliers.
The gradient range test validates time series data based on knowledge of the usual dy-
namics of a phenomena, where too sudden changes in a time series can be considered as
doubtful data (Clemens-Meyer et al., 2021). If a data point will be considered doubtful or
not will be based on gradient range thresholds.
For a value of water level (h1) recorded at two different time points (t and t + ∆t), the
value of h1,t+∆t will be considered as doubtful if one or both of the equations in (19) are
verified (Clemens-Meyer et al., 2021).
Gh1,t+∆t = h1,t+∆t −h1,t

Gh1,t+∆t = h1,t+∆t −h1,t
∆t

(19)
where Gradientmax and Gradientmin are the maximum and minimum likely gradients for
water level.
Based on the 1.5 years of observed water level data, the typical dynamics of the system
are well known, and realistic gradient thresholds are estimated to be a maximum of 2
cm/minute for rising water levels and a minimum of -1 cm/minute for declining water
levels. Thus, with ∆t equal to 1 minute, a data point will be flagged as doubtful if the
difference h1,t+∆t −h1,t is higher than 2 cm for rising water levels, and lower than -1 cm
for declining water level. These doubtful data points are classified as outliers and replaced
with NaN (Not a Number) values in the raw dataset.
4.2.3
Data smoothing
To reduce noise from measurement error, the smoothing technique moving average is
applied on the water level data. Smoothed data values are calculated as the average of
all surrounding data points within a sliding frame (window). The size of this window
determines how much the data is smoothed (Mangrulkar and Chavan, 2025). The moving
average calculation is performed in Python (Appendix B, Section 2.1), where the rolling
function in Python’s Pandas library is applied (Pandas, ndb). The calculation is set to
be centred, meaning that the moving window is centred around each data point during
smoothing. The calculation also requires a window size and a minimum period as input.
The window size defines the number of observations over which the average is computed.
The minimum period defines the minimum number of data points required within the
25
==PAGE:33==
specified window for computing a smoothed value.
If the requirement is not met, no
smoothed value will be computed for that window, and the data point will be assigned a
NaN value.
4.2.4
Temporal resolution
In the analysis and estimation of K, utilising as much data as possible is an important
aim. The time resolution of the data influences how much detail and how many valid data
points can be included in the analysis. However, it is also important to ensure that the
data is not significantly affected by measurement noise, which is more likely to impact
data with high time resolution.
The highest available time resolution from the SmartBrønd dashboard is 1-minute inter-
vals. To evaluate the impact of different time resolutions, new intervals are computed
from the original 1-minute data using Python (Appendix B, Section 2.2), by applying
the resample function from the Pandas library (Pandas, nda). To compute, for example,
5-minute intervals, the following aggregation methods are applied:
• Water level: The mean of five consecutive 1-minute values.
• Precipitation: The sum of five consecutive 1-minute values.
4.2.5
Data filtering
For water level data to be considered usable for estimation of K, certain criteria must be
met. The criteria are:
1. Only periods covering recession limbs (decreasing water levels) are analysed.
Measurements of inflow are not available in this project, therefore it is only pos-
sible to close the water balance in Equation (7) by assuming no inflow during the
analysed periods. Furthermore, Mikkelsen et al. (1998) found that better interpre-
tation of data was also obtained, when only focusing on periods with no inflow to
the infiltration systems.
2. Only periods without rainfall are included in the analysis.
During rainfall, inflow to the infiltration trenches is assumed to occur, and data from
these periods are therefore excluded.
3. The water level in the infiltration trench is below the level of the throttle unit.
When the water level exceeds the level of the throttle unit, water will start over-
flowing to the sewer, and the water balance will no longer be closed. As a result,
Equation (8) for calculation of infiltration flow rate becomes inapplicable.
4. The water level in the infiltration trench is above 1 cm.
Inspection of the data show that the water level in the infiltration trenches often
stagnates around 0-1 cm for extended periods (with only small fluctuations likely
26
==PAGE:34==
due to measurement error), likely indicating that the trenches are emptied of water.
This data is not useful for estimating K, so a lower threshold of 1 cm is set for usable
data in the analysis.
5. A substantial water level is present in the infiltration trenches in between dry periods.
This is to ensure, that the data is suitable for estimation of K. As the applied model
assumes saturated conditions, the data used for the analysis must originate from
periods where the water level in the trench has been sufficiently high to reasonably
assume saturation.
Observations of data indicate that when the water level in a trench reaches a maxi-
mum of around 5 cm after a dry period, infiltration occurs faster than it does at the
same level following periods with higher maximum water levels in the trench. This
suggests that the soil is initially unsaturated, and capillary forces draw water out
quickly. In contrast, when the trench has previously held more water and the soil is
assumable more saturated, infiltration is slower and more stable. K is expected to
be more reliably estimated under these conditions following more significant trench
filling, and therefore only data in between dry periods where the maximum water
level exceeds 5 cm will be used.
6. The water level data are reliable.
In some cases, the data show unusual or inconsistent behaviour without any clear
explanation, which in most cases is likely due to sensor or measurement issues. Such
data is identified visually from the SmartBrønd dashboard, marked as unreliable and
excluded from the analysis.
7. Only valid, non-missing data points are used in the analysis.
Any data points labelled as NaN are identified and filtered out.
In the Python script, data are filtered according to the seven criteria described above
(Figure 13) - see Appendix B, Section 4. First, a new DataFrame is created containing
only data from the selected infiltration trench. Then, for each criterion, a separate column
is added to the dataset, where each data point is assigned a value of 0 if the criterion is
met (indicating usable data), or 1 if the criterion is not met (indicating unusable data) -
see Appendix B, Section 4.1. In the DataFrame for the selected infiltration trench, data
points that do not meet all criteria for usability (i.e., those containing a value of 1 in one
or more of the seven added columns) are filtered out. Finally, a new DataFrame is created
for the selected infiltration trench, containing only the data deemed suitable for analysis -
see Appendix B, Section 4.2. Water level over time is plotted for the selected infiltration
trench, both before and after filtering, to illustrate the effect of the data filtering procedure
- see Appendix B, Section 4.3.
Limitations of the filtering procedure
When observing the water level data in Smartbrønd’s dashboard, it appears that the water
level in the infiltration trenches often decreases slowly immediately after a rain event has
ended, which likely indicates that there is still inflow into the infiltration trenches. Ideally,
27
==PAGE:35==
these periods with signs of inflow would have been automatically excluded through the data
filtering setup. Inflow measurements to the infiltration trenches or possible measurements
of soil moisture in the rain garden would allow more reliable identification and removal of
all data points where inflow is still occurring, but such measurements are not available for
the trenches at Ærtebjergvej.
Runoff reaching the trenches must first pass through rain gardens with a 30 cm filter soil
layer. Factors such as temperature, soil moisture, the K value of the filter soil, and the
intensity and duration of the preceding rain event are all likely to influence how long it
takes for water to percolate through the filter soil. Accounting for these processes could
quickly become complex and might even require a separate model to accurately filter out
periods with continued inflow. Due to this complexity and the time constraints of the
project, it has not been a priority in this project to automatically identify and filter out
periods of continued inflow after rain events.
4.2.6
Selection of single events
When estimating K from single events, the water level data will still be filtered according
to the seven criteria described in Section 4.2.5, but in this case K will be estimated only
from data covering a single, defined recession limb following a rain event.
The single events are selected visually from Smartbrønd’s dashboard, and therefore it is
possible to exclude periods with signs of continued inflow. The start of a single event is
defined as the time when data indicate that there is no longer inflow into the infiltration
trench - i.e., the time when the water level no longer appears to be slowly decreasing
after the end of a rain event. To ensure this, the start and end times of single events are
determined in two steps:
1. A recession limb is visually selected from Smartbrønd’s dashboard. The initial start
time is defined as the point where rainfall stops and the water level curve begins
to decline, while the end time is defined as either the point where a new rain event
begins or when the trench is empty.
2. Based on this initial start and end time, recession rates are plotted against water
level to identify the point where the slow decrease in water level ceases. The water
level at this point is used to determine a new and final start time for the single event.
The exclusion of periods showing signs of inflow for single events is also intended to enable
comparison between results based on the full dataset and single events, thereby assessing
the influence of these periods.

window size; however, it may require a very large window size, possibly of 600 minutes
(10 hours) or even more, which could introduce some disadvantages. For example, there
may be a risk that many values cannot be computed if the data contain a lot of periods
with missing values. A large window size may also pose a problem in accurately capturing
sudden changes in the data, such as a rapid increase in water level after rainfall. Figure 20
illustrates this, where data with a window size of 600 minutes are affected by increases and
decreases in water level five hours forward and backward in time. As a result, after rainfall,
the signal rises sooner and stagnates later compared to data with a smaller window size.
Therefore, the following section explores an alternative approach for further smoothing
by combining a moving average with data aggregated into time intervals greater than 1
minute.

6.1
Estimation with full dataset
With the model described in Section 2.5.1, K values for each of the six infiltration trenches
are estimated from the full dataset of filtered water level data. K values are estimated

6.1.1
Infiltration plots
Based on the full dataset for the entire measurement period, the calculated infiltration
flow rates as a function of water level are shown in Figure 23.
For all six plots in Figure 23, regression equations have been fitted, allowing the estima-
tion of Kside and Kbottom using Equations (11) and (12). Since the plotted data represent
periods following multiple rain events over approximately 1.5 years and across different
seasons, a considerable degree of scatter in the data points is observed. For the infiltra-
tion trenches ÆR15, ÆR19, ÆR30, and ÆR34, the plots show an approximately linear
relationship between infiltration flow rate and water level, with the data points roughly
distributed around the regression lines. However, the plots also show that in some cases
the relationship is more exponential, resulting in a curved trend, particularly at higher
water levels. This tendency is likely linked to situations where the water level in a trench
rises to a higher level, where the surrounding soil is unsaturated, thereby enhancing in-
filtration through stronger capillary forces. In the applied model, the estimation of K is
simplified, and such capillary effects are not represented. At higher and less saturated
levels in the trench, the model therefore does not provide a fully realistic interpretation
of the trench’s function. Variations from a linear infiltration trend could in principle also
reflect stratification of the surrounding soil with differing K values, although no clear ev-
idence of such stratification appears in the plots in Figure 23.
In the four plots for ÆR15, ÆR19, ÆR30, and ÆR34 in Figure 23, many cases of vertical
”tails” of data points can be observed. These tails consist of several points where the
infiltration flow rate approaches 0 m3/s at roughly the same water level. They most likely
originate from periods immediately after rain events, when the water level - although high
- decreases only slowly, because water is still dripping from the rain garden into the trench.

6.1.3
Variation affected by seasonality and water level
Figure 25 shows that Kside values in particular vary in magnitude across the seasons, and
with a general pattern of higher values in summer and autumn than in winter and spring.
For the individual trenches, a maximum seasonal variation in Kside of roughly a factor
of 1.5-8 is observed. Across the individual seasons, there is no clear pattern for which
infiltration trenches have the highest or lowest values, and differences between trenches
within the same season are at most a factor of 5. For example, ÆR30 generally has among
the highest Kside values in the three seasons where estimates are available, while ÆR15
records the lowest values in two out of four seasons. ÆR34 alternates between having
some of the highest Kside values and, in other seasons, among the lowest. The lack of a
consistent ranking of K values between the trenches across seasons shows that seasonal
variations affect the trenches to different degrees, likely due to the influence of additional
factors.
The generally higher Kside values in summer and autumn than in winter are fairly con-
sistent with the theory in Section 2.4.1, describing how temperature effects are the main
driver of seasonal variation in K. Differences in soil moisture conditions between seasons,
driven by variations in both rainfall and temperature, may also explain the observed sea-
sonal patterns - particularly the lower Kside values in winter 2023/2024, when rainfall was
both more frequent and considerably higher (almost 400 mm in total according to the
measurements from SmartBrønd) compared to the other seasons.
The seasonal variation trend is most evident for ÆR34, where estimates of K values exist
for almost the entire measurement period. For ÆR34, Kside is clearly higher in summer
and in autumn 2024. In autumn 2024, large rain events that produced high water levels
in the trench occurred mainly in September and mid-October, when the average monthly
temperatures in Copenhagen Municipality were 15.8 ◦C and 11.2 ◦C, respectively (Figure
A15 Appendix A.4). In contrast, during autumn 2023 most major rain events occurred
in November, when the average temperature was 5.0 ◦C.
Accordingly, the Kside value
estimated for autumn 2023 is noticeably lower than that for autumn 2024.
For ÆR34, the lowest estimated Kside value is for spring rather than for winter. The
average temperature for April 2024 (when nearly all rain events of that spring took place)
was 8.3,◦C, whereas for December 2023, and January and February 2024 the average
temperatures were 3.6,◦C, 1.1,◦C, and 4.6,◦C, respectively. Based solely on temperature,
higher Kside values would thus be expected for spring.
However, in spring, only two
recession limbs were recorded with maximum water levels of 24 cm and 9 cm, whereas in
winter there were many rain events with continuous water in the trench, often reaching
levels above 40 cm. As shown in Figure 26, during winter data at higher water levels
display a curved pattern due to very high infiltration flow rates, likely influenced by
capillary forces. These points steepen the regression line and lead to a higher Kside value,
though the effect is moderated by several of the previously mentioned tails at water levels
of 35-40 cm. Hence, water level conditions can also influence estimated K values, showing

that temperature alone cannot explain the observed seasonal differences. This difference
in water levels may partly explain why Kside for ÆR34 is higher in winter than in spring.
Another possible explanation is that several short rain events occurring during the larger
of the two analysed recession limbs in spring may have affected the infiltration flow rates,
resulting in a lower Kside.

6.2.3
Variation from rain event to rain event and with water level
During shorter periods with successive rain events, Kside values generally decrease from
one event to the next. As shown in Figure 28, Kside decreases between the three events on
17, 20, and 24 November 2023 for ÆR15, ÆR30, and ÆR34. For ÆR19, however, Kside
increases slightly from 17 to 20 November, although the two values are nearly identical.
The results show that the soil was about 1.6-2.8 times more permeable on 17 November
compared to 20 November, and 1.2-3 times more permeable on 20 November compared to
24 November. This suggests that repeated filling of the trenches leads to increasing soil
saturation, which can explain the declining Kside values. Possible temperature effects are
assumed to be minor when the events occurred only a few days apart.
The water levels during these events also varied, which can affect K estimates. On 17
November, ÆR15, ÆR30, and ÆR34 had initial levels of 42-50 cm and final levels of
37-43 cm - the highest observed in the entire measurement period.
At these heights
the soil was likely unsaturated, causing higher infiltration flow rates and thereby steeper
regression lines. On 20 and 24 November, initial water levels were lower (33-36 cm and
24-41 cm, respectively) at depths where water had remained for several days, resulting in a
more saturated soil. Figure 29 shows this clearly for ÆR34, where the 17 November event
produced steeper infiltration curves and thereby higher Kside values than the 24 November
event. A similar trend is observed for ÆR15 and ÆR30 when infiltration flow rates are

repeated rain events as well as differences in the water levels used for estimation. For the
1 and 7 July events, K values were estimated from similar water levels (17-10 cm and 18-7
cm, respectively), and temperatures were fairly stable, with average daily values around
13-15 ◦C. The decrease in Kside by a factor of 2.1 is therefore more confidently explained
by increased soil saturation between rain events. For the 14 July event, however, the ini-
tial water level was 23 cm, the highest since April, meaning that a part of the infiltration
occurred in more unsaturated soil layers, resulting in a higher estimate of Kside. For the
17 and 24 July events, Kside decreased again by factors of 2.5 and 1.5, respectively, from
event to event. For both events infiltration took place in likely more saturated soil layers,
as initial water levels were only 15 cm and 11 cm, respectively.
The two examples of K values estimated from single events following successive rain events
highlight two key insights:
• K values can vary between events, as repeated filling of the trench increases the
saturation of the surrounding soil.
• Soil saturation differs with depth, as layers higher up in the trench are generally less
saturated due to less frequent water exposure. Consequently, the water level during
a given single event can have an influence on the estimated K values.
The applied model assumes that infiltration occurs under saturated conditions and de-
pends linearly on the water level. Under this assumption, variations in water level for the
infiltration trenches would not influence the estimated K values. In reality, however, the
soil is not always saturated, and data show that many infiltration plots exhibit curved
rather than linear infiltration patterns. As a result, estimated K values can depend on
whether they are derived from higher water levels, where the soil typically is unsaturated
and infiltration curves thereby steeper, or from lower levels, where soil is more saturated
and infiltration curves thereby less steep. This dependence complicates direct comparisons
of K values across single events with different water levels, particularly when assessing
seasonal effects or event-to-event variation. Ideally, single events would be based on reces-
sion limbs that begin at relatively high and comparable water levels and extend down until
near empty, ensuring more consistent comparisons. However, such “ideal” events were rare
in the dataset, since infiltration was often slow and new rainfall typically occurred before
water levels had dropped substantially.

6.3
Comparison of results from full dataset and single events
Direct comparison
As K values are estimated both from the full dataset and from single events, the results
are compared to assess potential differences. The plots of K values estimated from both
data inputs can be seen together in Figure A16 in Appendix A.4 to allow for easier vi-
sual comparison. Overall, Kside values show much greater variation for single events than
for the full dataset, with variation for the individual trenches up to a factor of 100 for
single events, and only up to a factor of 8 for the full dataset. Single event estimates
were found to be sensitive to preceding moisture conditions. In many cases they are also
based on recession periods covering relatively short ranges of water levels, compared to
the full dataset that integrates all available water levels for recession periods and thereby
cover larger ranges of water level. Consequently, varying soil saturation with trench depth
can have a greater influence on single event estimates compared to full dataset estimates.
Due to these varying factors affecting K, one single event will not necessarily provide
a representative K estimate on either a seasonal or annual basis. Most likely, multiple
single events would need to be analysed to obtain more robust seasonal or annual averages.
Comparison of averages
Consequently, multiple single events coving most larger recession limbs observed during the
measurement period have been analysed, and to further compare the two data approaches,
average K values are calculated.
Estimates based on both types of data input show
seasonal variation in K values. However, the 1.5-year measurement period does not cover
all seasons equally, and the amount of usable data differs considerably between seasons.
Deriving average K values either from all single events or based on the full dataset for
the entire measurement period would therefore bias the results toward seasons with more
data. To avoid this, seasonal averages are first calculated. For the full dataset, these
are based on K values estimated for each season (Table 8 and Figure 25), while for the
single events, averages are derived from all K estimates within the season (Figure 27).
Finally, overall annual average K values are derived from these seasonal averages. The
average K values are illustrated in Figure 30 for the individual infiltration trenches, with
the exception of ÆR22 and ÆR26, due to lack of available K value estimates.
The four plots in Figure 30 show that all annual average K values, and nearly all seasonal
average K values, are higher for single events. For the annual averages, the difference
between single events and the full dataset is in most cases relatively small, where Kside
values are 9-21% higher for single events, and Kbottom values are 1-90% higher for single
events. When comparing single events with the full dataset for the seasonal averages, the
values are also, in most cases, closely aligned. The largest differences in Kside values are
observed for ÆR15 in autumn (62%), for ÆR19 and ÆR30 in winter (121% and 70%,
respectively), and for ÆR34 in spring (83%). The largest differences in Kbottom values
range from 33-135% for the individual trenches. Furthermore, the average K values derived
from both the full dataset and the single events generally demonstrate the same trends in
relation to seasonal variation.

6.4
Comparison with slug test results
For the K values estimated with slug tests at Toftevang Vejlaug (Table 4), most values
are on the order of 10−5 m/s. In comparison, the K values estimated for the infiltration
trenches on Ærtebjergvej - where both the majority of values and the annual averages of
Kside are on the order of 10−7 m/s - are approximately 100 times lower than the slug test
results.
There can be several reasons why the K values estimated from slug tests differ from the
results obtained in this project. First, the slug tests were conducted at locations 50-225 m
away from the infiltration trenches on Ærtebjergvej (Figure 12), where spatial variation
factors such as soil texture, plant root structure and moisture conditions may differ. The
study of the infiltration system at Nørrebro by Mikkelsen et al. (1998) found that the K
values of two identical infiltration trenches located only about 7 m apart differed by a factor
of 10. This demonstrates that even relatively small distances can lead to spatial variations
with significant impact on K. However, for the infiltration trenches on Ærtebjergvej the
difference in K values between trenches was generally smaller than a factor of 10.
Second, the slug test method for estimating K values differs substantially from the ap-
proach applied in this project. The slug test results appear to be based on measured
recession rates that were directly converted into K values, although recession rates are
not a direct measure of K. This direct conversion does not account for the wetted surface
area from where infiltration occurs, which affects the infiltration flow rate. Furthermore,
the estimates are based on a single, very short-term recession event of 10 minutes, where
the surrounding soil was possibly unsaturated from the outset and could not be expected
to reach steady moisture conditions within the 10 minutes. Finally, the water levels during
the slug tests were more than two to three times higher than the maximum levels observed
in the infiltration trenches during the measurement period. Since higher hydraulic heads
generally produce faster recession rates, the high water levels used for the slug test have
likely also influenced the estimated K values.
Based on the results obtained in this project for the six infiltration trenches on Ærtebjerg-
vej, the K values estimated from slug tests are not representative. To what extent this is
due to differences in location or to differences in the estimation methods, however, requires
more investigation to determine.

6.5
Main findings
Estimated K values
Using the full dataset, Kside values for the infiltration trenches on Ærtebjergvej are gen-
erally on the order of 10−7 m/s. Single event estimates show a wider spread: most Kside
values are also on the order of 10−7 m/s, although several are higher, up to approximately
2 · 10−6 m/s, and a few are lower, on the order of 10−8 m/s. Using both full dataset and
single events, Kbottom values are on the order of 10−8 to 10−7 m/s.
Variation in K values is affected by:
• Seasonality: A seasonal variation is observed, likely driven mainly by temperature
differences, where Kside values are generally highest in summer and autumn and
lowest in winter. For the individual trenches, estimates from the full dataset show a
maximum seasonal variation in Kside of roughly a factor of 1.5-8, while single event
estimates from different seasons vary far more, up to a factor of 10-100.
• Rain event to rain event: During shorter periods with successive rain events,
estimated Kside values generally decrease from one single event to the next, with
reductions of up to a factor of three, reflecting the effect of repeated trench filling
that increases saturation in the surrounding soil.
• Water level: Data plots show that infiltration flow rate is not necessarily linearly
dependent on water level but in several cases follows a more exponential trend,
particularly at higher water levels. These observations illustrate that soil saturation
differs with depth, as layers higher up in the trench are generally more unsaturated
due to less frequent water exposure. Consequently, the water level of the analysed
recession periods can influence the estimated K and may also explain part of the
observed variation between seasons and from rain event to rain event.
Comparison of full dataset and single events
Overall, K values show greater variability when estimated from single events than from the
full dataset. Average K values from the full dataset are broadly comparable to those from
multiple single events, with maximum differences in Kside of 9-21% for annual averages
and 62-121% for seasonal averages. Averages from full dataset are generally lower, likely
because periods with continued inflow are not filtered out, which also prevented estima-
tion of K in certain seasons. Still, using the full dataset maximises data utilisation and
captures a wider range of variability compared to one single event, thereby providing more
representative and robust averages across seasons and years. In addition, the predefined
filtering procedure makes the method substantially more efficient and easily transferable
to other datasets compared to manual selection of single events.
Comparison to slug tests
Slug test results mainly on the order of 10−5 m/s are about two orders of magnitude higher
than results estimated in this project. The discrepancy is likely due to spatial variations
58
==PAGE:66==
and methodological differences, as the slug test results were based on a single, very short-
term recession event and likely reflected raw recession rates rather than K values.
6.5.1
Recommendations and further research
Based on the findings, several recommendations including further research are presented
below.
• Field measurements: For further investigation of variations in K values, measure-
ments of soil temperature and moisture content could be conducted to support and
explain the observed variability.
• Filter refinement: If estimation of K with full dataset is further pursued, refine-
ment of the filtering procedure is recommended. In particular, periods influenced
by continued inflow should be excluded, as they introduce errors that often lead
to underestimation of K values. If possible, direct measurements of inflow to the
trench - or alternatively soil moisture measurements in the rain garden - could be
conducted to support identification of continued inflow.
• Design K: If K values are to be applied in the future design of infiltration trenches,
it should be further investigated what a reasonable design K should be when ac-
counting for the seasonal variations both in K and in rainfall.
If a conservative
approach is desired to be on the safe side and minimize the risk of system overload,
the design should be based on the lowest seasonal K value, which in this project
corresponds to winter.
• Slug tests: If K values are to be applied in the future design of infiltration trenches,
it is recommended to improve the approach for K estimation with slug tests. This
includes conduction of measurements closer to the intended trench locations, using
a method that better reflects the actual trench conditions, and applying a formula
such as Darcy’s law to estimate K from measured recession rates. Furthermore,
special attention should be given to the season when conducting the measurements,
so that the results can be used to define an appropriate design K value accounting
for seasonal variability.

the emptying time for the part of the trench above the throttle unit is calculated solely

7.3
Evaluation of HOFOR’s design guideline and comparison with re-
sults
7.3.1
Consideration of K in design
In SVK’s guideline no. 25 and LAR spreadsheet, the return period T is the governing
design parameter and the K value is also considered when determining the required specific
storage volume of the trench.
In contrast, infiltration trenches are dimensioned for a
fixed specific storage volume of 30 mm in HOFOR’s design guideline.
In addition, it
is specified that the filter soil in the rain garden itself provides approximately 8 mm of
storage, resulting in a combined storage of 38 mm for the rain garden and infiltration
trench.
With HOFOR’s design criteria of a fixed specific storage volume, the return period T will
vary with different K values. For a K value of 1·10−5 m/s and total storage of 38 mm, the
return period is T = 10 years (calculated with SVK’s LAR spreadsheet with total safety
factor of 1 and with no infiltration through the trench bottom). To take into account the
variability of K values that can affect the capacity, HOFOR’s guideline specifies that a
combination of infiltration and sewer discharge will be applied when K < 1·10−5 m/s. For
K values below 1·10−5 m/s this means that part of the total trench volume is allocated to
detention. As illustrated in the study by Locatelli et al. (2015), a detention volume requires
significantly less storage compared to a retention volume to meet the same capacity, which
is due to a much higher outflow rate than the infiltration flow rate. Consequently, the
total trench volume does not need to be as large to avoid overload.
7.3.2
Assessment of HOFOR’s design criteria based on the analysis of trenches
on Ærtebjergvej
The results of this project show that the estimated K values for the six infiltration trenches
are typically in the order of 10−7 m/s, which is considerably lower than 1 · 10−5 m/s. For
example, with a K value of 5 · 10−7 m/s, a trench volume of 30 mm would result in a
return period T = 0.2 years (without bottom infiltration) or T = 0.9 years (with bottom
infiltration), meaning that with pure infiltration the trench volume would frequently be
exceeded.
Therefore, HOFOR’s current design, which incorporates a throttle unit to
increase the overall trench capacity, is considered an appropriate and effective solution for
sites with low K values.
Data from the measurement period show that there were no instances where the total
trench volume was filled, and thus no cases of total trench overload. ÆR34 came closest
on 16 November 2023, when the water level reached approximately 56 cm, only 4 cm from
the top of the trench. Based on the trench dimensions and the specified catchment area,
the specific storage volume for ÆR34 was calculated to be around 32 mm, which is close to
the 30 mm specified in HOFOR’s design guideline. With a throttle unit height of 50 cm,
65
==PAGE:73==
a larger proportion of the trench volume is allocated to retention compared to the 30 cm
height specified in the guideline. The results for ÆR34 therefore suggest that HOFOR’s
design criteria are effective in preventing total trench overload when K values are in the
range of 1 · 10−7 to 1 · 10−6 m/s, as estimated for this trench.
However, several factors introduce uncertainty in assessing whether HOFOR’s current
design criteria are effective based on the infiltration trenches at Ærtebjergvej:
• The dataset covers only 1.5 years of observations, making it difficult to draw defini-
tive conclusions regarding the total trench capacity, and thus it remains uncertain
whether the entire system may be overloaded more frequently than desired.
• There is uncertainty regarding the observed water levels due to possible leakage from
the butterfly valves
• The designs do not comply with HOFOR’s new guideline in terms of throttle unit
height and specific storage volume (even though some of the calculated specific stor-
age volumes are close to 30 mm).
• There is uncertainty regarding the actual catchment areas connected to the trenches
and thereby also regarding the calculated specific storage volumes.
Due to these uncertainties, the results should therefore be interpreted as indicative rather
than conclusive, and further monitoring would be required to confirm the effectiveness of
HOFOR’s current design approach.
7.3.3
Placement of throttle unit
The placement of the throttle unit in the infiltration trench, which divides the trench into a
retention volume and a detention volume, not only affects the total capacity of the trench,
but it also plays an important role in relation to HOFOR’s objective of reducing sewer
surcharge. At the beginning of a new rain event, the detention volume above the throttle
unit will most likely be empty, as it drains quickly, and therefore it has the capability of
detaining peak flows. The higher the throttle unit is placed, the smaller this detention
volume becomes. If the water level in a trench is already high at the start of a new rain
event (which the results of long emptying times indicate is a likely possibility), a relatively
small detention volume above the throttle unit may result in a lower degree of peak runoff
reduction.
The study by Locatelli et al. (2015) illustrates that including a detention volume within
the total trench volume can result in greater peak runoff reduction than using retention
volume alone. For the specific retention-detention unit analysed by Locatelli et al. (2015),
peak runoff was reduced by 80%, when detention made up 40-100% of the total volume.
However, allocating more of the total volume to retention at the same time also improved
annual runoff reduction. Therefore, when designing HOFOR’s infiltration trenches, there
may be benefits to positioning the throttle unit higher than the 30 cm specified in the
design guide - particularly at sites with higher K values, where the retention volume drains
more rapidly. It should be noted, however, that the results reported by Locatelli et al.
(2015) are case-specific and cannot be directly applied to the trenches at Ærtebjergvej.

7.3.4
Recommendations for a future design guideline
In HOFOR’s current guideline, the design principles are based on a fixed specific storage
volume and, when K is below 1 · 10−5 m/s, a sewer outlet with fixed throttle unit height
and discharge, meaning that the capacity in terms of return period can vary. To better
control the capacity and overload frequency of the infiltration trenches, the design could
in principle be adapted to the K value and a specific return period, with retention and
detention volumes sized according to separate return periods, e.g., T = 0.1 and T = 10
years, respectively.
However, this project has shown that K values estimated from slug tests for the use of di-
mensioning infiltration trenches can be unreliable, as results for the trenches at Ærtebjerg-
vej deviated by a factor 100 compared to slug test results from Toftevang Vejlaug. In
addition, the project has also shown that there can be uncertainty regarding the actual
catchment areas connected to each trench, which is also an important parameter for the di-
mensioning. With these uncertainties a highly tailored design is not practical or justified,
and a simpler approach, such as HOFOR’s current practice, might be more appropri-
ate. The simpler design also makes communication easier, which is also one of HOFOR’s
arguments for not using SVK’s LAR spreadsheet for dimensioning.
The results of this project have shown that K values can be much lower than the baseline
of 1·10−5 m/s in HOFOR’s design guideline. To account for the variety of K values below
1 · 10−5 m/s when designing infiltration trenches, a future design guideline could consider
different design options:
• Design 1: Fixed storage volume with variable throttle unit height for specific K
value ranges - lower throttle unit height for lower K values to increase detention
relative to retention and thereby the overall trench capacity.
Advantage: The throttle unit can be adapted after construction if the K value turns
out to differ from the measured value (e.g., from a slug test).
Disadvantage: Very low K values may require the throttle unit to be set very low,
which can result in frequent overflow to the sewer and reduced annual runoff reduc-
tion
• Design 2: Variable specific storage volumes for specific K value ranges - larger
specific storage volumes for lower K values to increase the overall trench capacity.
Advantage: The throttle unit can be positioned higher in the trench, also for low K
values, potentially increasing annual runoff reduction.
Disadvantage: Uncertainties in the measured K value may result in a specific storage
volume that, once constructed, turns out to not match the actual infiltration capacity
of the surrounding soil. As a result, the trench might be overdimensioned if the K
value is higher than anticipated, or require lowering of the throttle unit to increase
the overall trench capacity if the K value is lower than anticipated.
Considering HOFOR’s main objective of reducing sewer surcharge, Design 1 appears to
be the most suitable solution. This design is both simple and flexible, and it allows for
67
==PAGE:75==
a more efficient utilisation of the constructed storage volume in relation to the primary
objective. Design 2 could potentially achieve greater annual runoff reduction, but this
represents only a secondary objective for HOFOR. Furthermore, this design may prove
more costly to implement at sites with low K values, as it would require a larger storage
volume. In such cases, limited available space could become a constraint, and alternative
solutions might offer a more favourable overall business case.
7.3.5
Further research
Before deciding on a future design approach, it is recommended first to conduct further
research of the function and capacity of trenches designed according to HOFOR’s current
guideline as well as their impact on the sewer system and runoff reduction, in order to
evaluate their performance in practise for different K values. Based on the findings from
this project, a number of further investigations are suggested:
• Continued monitoring of water levels in other infiltration trenches designed under
HOFOR’s new guideline is recommended to evaluate the overall trench capacity for
different K values, given the uncertainties in catchment areas (and thereby specific
storage volumes) for trenches at Ærtebjergvej. For future measurements, special
attention should also be paid to ensuring that butterfly valves do not leak, so that
observations regarding trench capacity are not misleading. K values can be esti-
mated in a similar manner as in this project.
• System-level impact assessment, by applying estimated K values in the modelling
software MIKE+ to evaluate the effect of infiltration trenches on the sewer system.
An analysis by HOFOR has shown that the trenches at Ærtebjergvej are located
in an area where the sewer system is generally not challenged. Considering this as
well as the uncertainties regarding catchment areas and specific storage volumes for
the trenches at Ærtebjergvej, it would however be more reasonable to conduct such
system-level impact assessment in another area where trenches have been constructed
under the new guideline and where the sewer system is more challenged during heavy
rainfall events, causing surcharge.
• An investigation of varying throttle-unit heights - and thereby differing retention-
detention volume ratios - to assess their impact on both single-event peak runoff
reduction and annual runoff reduction, as well as on overall sewer surcharge. The
study could be conducted based on the methodology applied by Locatelli et al.
(2015).
• Assessment of the discharge to the sewer, by investigating the effect of allowing
higher discharges to the sewer than the current limit of 1 L/s specified in HOFOR’s
guideline, and its influence on overall trench performance.
• Rain garden monitoring and analysis to provide a more detailed assessment of the
combined storage capacity of both trench and rain garden is recommended. The
water level measurements in this project indicated prolonged and varying inflow
from the rain gardens into the trenches after rainfall had ceased.

8
Conclusion
Scope and methodology
This project has investigated the hydraulic performance of six infiltration trenches on
Ærtebjergvej in Husum, Copenhagen by applying an infiltration model that estimates
hydraulic conductivity K from water level data for both sides and bottom of the trenches.
The analysis aimed to maximise the use of available data and to evaluate how seasonality,
water depth, and variation between rain events affect system performance. In addition,
trench function and capacity were assessed as part of the overall evaluation and compared
with HOFOR’s design guideline from 2023 to identify possible improvements.
A Python script was developed for processing and analysing 1.5 years of data. The script
incorporated several filtration criteria that automatically selected all water level recession
data suitable for estimating K, thereby maximising data utilisation. Average recession
rates were generally low (0.4-5.3 mm/hr), and together with low measurement resolution,
this caused fluctuations in datasets with 1- and 10-minute resolution. Initial testing using
data from a single trench (ÆR15) showed that smoothing by applying a moving aver-
age with a two-hour window and aggregating to 10-minute resolution effectively reduced
fluctuations and thus the influence of measurement error on K estimation, while at the
same time increasing the share of filtered data for analysis by approximately 5% com-
pared to data with 1-hour resolution. Based on further testing, this smoothing setup was
deemed suitable and applied to all six infiltration trenches. Across the full measurement
period, the share of filtered data suitable for estimating K ranged from 0.8% (ÆR26) to
51.6% (ÆR34). The large variation between trenches reflected the significant differences
in observed water levels, which also varied seasonally.
Hydraulic conductivity and drivers of variation
Estimation using all filtered data (full dataset) as input resulted in Kside values generally
on the order of 10−7 m/s. Estimation with single events showed a wider spread with most
Kside values also on the order of 10−7 m/s, although several were higher, up to 2 · 10−6
m/s, and a few were lower, down to 2 · 10−8 m/s. Using both full dataset and single
events, Kbottom values were on the order of 10−8 to 10−7 m/s and generally lower than the
corresponding Kside. Seasonal variation was observed, likely driven mainly by temperature
differences, where Kside values were generally highest in summer and autumn, and lowest
in winter. For the individual trenches, estimates from the full dataset showed maximum
seasonal variations in Kside of a factor of 1.5-8, while single event estimates from different
seasons varied up to a factor of 10-100.
During shorter periods with successive rain events, Kside values generally decreased from
one single event to the next, with reductions of up to a factor of three, reflecting the effect
of repeated trench filling that increases saturation in the surrounding soil. Data plots
showed that infiltration flow rate was not necessarily linearly dependent on water level
but in several cases followed a more exponential trend, particularly at higher water levels.
These observations illustrated varying soil saturation with depth, as layers higher up in
the trench are generally less saturated due to less frequent water exposure. Consequently,
the water level of the analysed recession periods can influence estimated K and may also
explain part of the observed variation between seasons and from rain event to rain event.
70
==PAGE:78==
Data comparisons
Average K values from the full dataset were broadly comparable to averages from multiple
single events, with maximum differences in Kside of 9-21% for annual averages and 62-121%
for seasonal averages.
Averages from full dataset were generally lower, likely because
periods of continued inflow were excluded from single event data but not filtered out in
the full dataset. Still, using the full dataset maximised data utilisation and efficiently
captured a wider range of variability compared to a single event, thereby providing more
representative and robust averages across seasons and years.
Slug test results from nearby locations deviated about a factor of 100 compared to the
results found in this project, showing that slug test-based K estimates can be unreliable.
The found discrepancy can be explained both by spatial variability and by methodological
differences, as the slug test results likely reflected raw recession rates rather than K values.
System capacity and design evaluation
The six infiltration trenches were constructed prior to HOFOR’s 2023 guideline, and do
not comply with the design requirements of 30 mm specific storage volume and 30 cm
throttle unit height. Instead volumes were calculated to 26.3-57.0 mm and throttle unit
heights were about 50 cm. The emptying times estimated for the trenches were strongly
dependent on K values and ranged from 10.3-32.8 days for the lowest seasonal K values and
5.8-24.7 days for the highest. During the measurement period, the full trench capacity was
never exceeded, though one rain event caused overflow to the sewer from three trenches.
For these, return periods and overload frequencies were calculated to 1.43-1.5 years and
0.7 overloads/year, respectively. The results from observations did not align with results
estimated from The Water Pollution Committee’s (SVK) LAR spreadsheet, likely due to
uncertainties of actual catchment area sizes or in observed water levels caused by possible
leakage during the measurement period.
Even though observations showed that the full trench capacity was never exceeded, the
deviations from HOFOR’s design criteria, uncertainties in actual catchment areas and
possible leakage, and the limited observation period of 1.5 years, introduced uncertainty
in the assessment of whether the design criteria of 30 mm specific storage volume and
30 cm throttle unit height are effective based on the trenches at Ærtebjergvej.
More
generally, when establishing infiltration trenches, uncertainties in both K estimation from
slug tests and the specification of catchment areas suggest that a simple design approach,
such as HOFOR’s current practice, is more practical than more detailed dimensioning
methods like applying SVK’s LAR spreadsheet. However, since K values are critical for
trench performance, a future design guideline could benefit from a more flexible approach.
Two adapted designs are suggested: (1) fixed storage volume with variable throttle unit
height for defined K ranges, and (2) variable specific storage volumes adapted to specific
K ranges. Considering HOFOR’s main objective of reducing sewer surcharge as well as
efficient utilisation of the constructed volume, design (1) appears more suitable.
Recommendations
Further research is recommended both to improve K estimation and assessment, and
to strengthen the basis for a future design guideline.
For K estimation, this includes
field measurements of soil temperature and moisture to support analysis of variability,
71
==PAGE:79==
refinement of data filtering to avoid inflow-related errors, and improved slug test meth-
ods conducted closer to trench-sites while accounting for seasonal variability to define a
reasonable design K.
For supporting a future design guideline, monitoring of water levels in other trenches built
according to HOFOR’s current guideline, as well as measurements in rain gardens, are re-
commended to provide a more robust assessment of the combined system capacity. Finally,
the effect of infiltration trenches and variations in throttle unit height on runoff reduction
and sewer surcharge should be assessed to enable a more comprehensive evaluation of the
design criteria.

Hydraulisk dimensionering af grønne veje med nedsivning 
Indledning 
Følgende dimensioneringsprincipper skal anvendes i forbindelse med etablering af grønne 
vejbede med nedsivning.  
Grønne veje – med ren nedsivning eller kombination af nedsivning og afløb 
• 
Faskinevolumen under regnbedene skal dimensioneres ud fra 30 mm regn. Det vil 
sige, at faskinevolumen/oplandsareal = 0,03m for hver enkelt faskine. OBS: 
Filtermulden har i sig selv ca. 8mm volumen, hvilket betyder at regnbedet samlet 
tilbageholder 38mm.  
• 
Samtlige faskiner skal kunne tømmes til en udløbsbrønd via en tømmeledning i 
bunden af faskinen. I udløbsbrønden skal det være muligt at installere og drifte en 
vandbremse (drosselenhed), som kan tømme faskine af til afløbssystem. 
Drosselenheden skal være justerbar i højden efter idriftsættelse uden gravearbejde. 
Se bilag. 
• 
Anlæggenes tømmetid estimeres ud fra nedsivningsevnen som findes ved slug-test. 
Følgende gør sig gældende 
o K > 1 x 10-5 m/s ren nedsivning 
o K < 1 x 10-5 m/s kombination mellem nedsivning og afløb til kloak 
Med en K-værdi på 1 x 10-5 m/s tømmer 40mm efter ca. 26 timer. Ved kombination 
mellem nedsivning og kloak skal drosselenheden isættes, så tømmeniveauet er 
halvvejs oppe i faskinen (30cm over bunden) med et afløb på 1 l/s. 
• 
En K-værdi på 1 x 10-5 m/s vil resultere i 38mm og 44mm volumen ved T=10, med 
total sikkerhedsfaktor på henholdsvis 1,0 og 1,1 ud fra SVK LAR regnearket.  
• 
Rensning af afstrømmende vejvand før nedsivning sker således at myndighedens 
rensekrav kan overholdes. Det kan betyde, at regnbedes areal skal være af en vis 
størrelse og dybde ift. oplandsareal. HOFOR har erfaring med, at 
oplandsareal/bedareal skal være mindre end 20-30, men det vil afhænge af de 
konkrete krav der stilles, filtermaterialevalg, volumen i regnbedet under 
bypassbrønden mm. 20 vil betyde 20 m2 tilsluttet oplandareal pr. 1 m2 bedareal.
