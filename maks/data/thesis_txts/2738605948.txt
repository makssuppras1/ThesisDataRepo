Abstract
Because Faroese is a relatively small and distinctive language compared to most lanВӯ
guages, there is not much to find online concerning Faroese pronunciation. This thesis
examines the existing phone recognizers available for CAPT systems specifically aiming
at systems that can be used in Faroese. The result of the thesis project is a CAPT sysВӯ
tem called Phoneunciation, which makes it possible for users to learn the pronunciations
of different Faroese words. The program uses the phone recognizer Allosaurus with the
default universal phone recognizer fineВӯtuned using Faroese data provided by a research
group at the Centre for Language Technology at the University of the Faroe Islands. AddiВӯ
tionally, the program provides feedback and suggestions on where to make improvements
when pronouncing. The results show that the performance was improved by retraining
the universal phone recognizer in Faroese. Different validation suggestions are provided
as well to explore additional possibilities and examine if the methods are reliable, which
resulted in durable concepts, though further testing is needed.

Introduction
In recent decades it has become more and more popular to use computers, telephones, or
television to learn new languages and improve language skills. The interest in computerВӯ
assisted language learning (CALL) and computerВӯassisted pronunciation training (CAPT)
has subsequently increased. The systems seem to be able to provide good feedback on
spelling and grammar, but seem to lack pedagogical feedback on the pronunciation part
of the system. Therefore, proposing to integrate AIВӯgenerated feedback for the pronunciВӯ
ation performance might benefit the learning experience for the user.
The need for the Faroese language to align with modern technological development is
increasing. The Faroe Islands is a country with only 55.000 residents[1] and only 69.000
people around the world speak the language1. The preservation of the language is cruВӯ
cial, with increasingly more young people moving abroad to study and not returning. In
the process of writing this Master thesis, Faroese was introduced to Google Translate23
confirming the interest in preserving the Faroese language.
Figure 1.1: Google translate Вӯ Danish to Faroese
Now with Google Translate and the online dictionary, it is easier for foreigners to learn the
Faorese language online, but to learn the pronunciations of the words online is almost
impossible, because there is little to find. Therefore, specifically proposing a Faroese
phone recognizer as the AIВӯgenerated feedback could be a possible solution targeting
the need for online pronunciation systems and language learning systems in general for
the Faroese language. Choosing phones as feedback, will enable the users to visualize
pronunciations from audio. In short, phones are symbols that represent speech sounds.
Duration, intonation, emphasis, and plosives can also be included when using phones.
The objective of the thesis is to provide sufficient feedback based on transcribing to a
phonetic alphabet in order to assist pronunciation training learning results in Faroese for
second language learners.

Related Work
Studies indicate that CAPT has an overall positive effect when learning a foreign language
[2], [3]. While it is probably true to think that a classroom teacher can not be replaced by
a computer, Thomson argues that CALL and CAPT in particular are also imperative tools
that can not be replaced by teachers as well [4].
Studies show that feedback in CAPT is a possible way to learn new languages and imВӯ
prove pronunciation, but at the same time these applications seem to be missing an enВӯ
vironment that is realistic and pedagogically sound [5], [6].
Different types of feedback have been explored. One method described in [7], is using the
sound wave of an audio to visualize and compare the correctness of the pronunciation.
Another method described in [8], examines the use of ultrasound images of the inside
of mouths, to visualize realВӯtime pronunciations. For speech recognition and possibly
pronunciation training, images can also be used as feedback, as described in paper [9].
Some attempts have been made to improve phonetic speech recognition. There are some
papers that examine universalВӯlanguage (UL) phone recognition [10], [11], [12], [13], and
there are some papers that examine languageВӯspecific (LS) phone recognition [14], [15].
Overall, there are strengths and weaknesses with both kind of models. The UL models
are not good at recognising LS phones and LS models are not good at recognizing other
languages than the trained language. Therefore, if the model is needed for first language
(L1) learners, the LS models perform better when trained on that language and if the
model is needed for second language (L2) learners the UL models perform better.
Paper [16] examines two different state of the art phone recognizers, discovering that
overall the universal Allosaurus phone recognizer performs better than the Brno phone
recognizers. The phone recognizer Allosaurus described in paper [17] will be used. AlВӯ
losaurus is trained on the international phonetic alphabet (IPA) and on a few separate
languages to improve recognition accuracy. With Allosaurus it is possible to train the
model in other languages as well.
Paper [18] examines such a tool but focusing on English language learners instead of
Faroese. It is stated that вҖқthe robust 88% accuracy is cruacial in evaluating intonation
and stress patterns is crucial for achieving the research goal of providing comprehensive
feedback on speech dynamicsвҖқ. Moreover the paper states that integrating interactive
learning into language learning tools possibly will contribute to the learning experience.
Finally, paper [19] examines a audio to phone tool that is fineВӯtuned on the wav2vec 2.0
phone recognizer. Although the dataset used to retrain the new recognizer is less than
used in wav2vec 2.0, the new dataset is of better quality. The quality of the dataset results
in a close to human annotator.

3.4
International phonetic alphabet
To get a wider understanding of the output generated in Allosaurus, mentioned in secВӯ
tion 3.5.2, an explanation of the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) is given. The IPA is
a standardized system of symbols that represent speech sounds internationally [23]. The
IPA phones are mostly used in association with language learning.
An example on how to understand the representation of a phone can be explained by
looking at the combined letters вҖқthвҖқ in English words. [Оё] and [Г°] usually represent the
letter combination вҖқthвҖқ and they have a slightly different pronunciations. [Оё] is for example
represented in the words вҖқthickвҖқ, вҖқthoughtвҖқ and вҖқbathвҖқ, and [Г°] is for example represented
in the words вҖқthisвҖқ, вҖқmotherвҖқ and вҖқbatheвҖқ.
Prosodies are also part of the phonetic alphabet. These can be duration, intonation,
emphasis, and plosives. The symbol for duration is [:] indicating that the sound should
last longer. There is not a specific phonetic symbol for intonation, but in normal writing
the symbol is typically [вҖҷ] indicating that the primary stress is the phone placed after the
symbol. Similarly, the symbol [,] indicates that the secondary stress is the phone placed
after the symbol. There is not a specific phonetic symbol for emphasis, but in normal
writing the symbol is typically [!] indicating that word after the symbol should be focused
on and which can change the meaning of a sentence. There is not a specific phonetic
symbol for plosives, but in normal writing the symbol is typically [/] enclosing a phone like
/p/, indicating that the airway is closed followed by releasing the air while pronouncing the
sound for p.
In this project only the length is considered as part of the phonetic alphabet, to avoid
causing confusion among the users.

Preparing wave files
The original data provides a folder of .flac audio files. A simple command of how to convert
all .flac in a folder to .wav, can be found in appendix A. Afterwards, the .wav files were
split into .wav files shorter than 10 seconds. Because the audio files needed to contain
full words, this part had to be done manually by defining where the audio files should start
and stop, with the help of the code provided in appendix B.
4.1.2
Preparing IPA files
The original data also provides a folder of .textGrid files. The .textGrid files are split in to
different sections (text, SAMPA, sounds and other) and intervals (timestamps). First the
SAMPA phones were extracted from the files. Afterwards the SAMPA phones were conВӯ
verted to API phones. The paper [24] gives an explanation of how to generate Faroese
SAMPA from Faroese text and provides mapping from Faroese SAMPA to IPA. The auВӯ
thors provided a pdf containing the comparison of symbols, making it possible to convert
directly for the Faroese language1. The code to convert .textGrid to .txt can be found
in appendix C. The extraction of SAMPA phones can be found in appendix D. And the
conversion from SAMPA to IPA phones, can be found in appendix E. The code shown in
screenshots in the appendix can be found on GitHub2. To quickly transform the data, the
python code was generated with ChatGPT.
Additionally, a dictionary in SAMPA phones is provided from the Ravnur project3, which
can be used to check whether a word is pronounced correctly.

6.1
Thinking aloud test
The Thinking Aloud test was done to see if the program is easy to navigate and if the users
are able to understand the provided feedback. Before the test started the following was
read out loud to them: вҖқThis is a program for people who want to learn a new language
and want to learn how to pronounce the words correct. This is not a program where you
can learn the grammar, but rather how you can say the words. The feedback you get back
are phones that are captured based on the pronunciation and then the program decides
if the pronunciation is good enough or not. Phones are letters that represent a sound. If
you are Faroese, you should now imagine that Faroese is not your mother language, and
you want to learn how to pronounce Faroese words.вҖқ. The subjects were then asked if
they understood what was said, and if there was any doubt, a more detailed explanation
was also given. The subjects were then asked to do some tasks and say everything out
loud that came to mind while doing the tasks, such as вҖқnow I am going to do..вҖқ or вҖқI donвҖҷt
really know what to do hereвҖқ. Each test lasted between 4 to 8 minutes.
The following tasks were given:
1. Look around without clicking anything and say how you understand the program.
2. Listen to the reference word.
3. Find out what the reference word means.
4. Record yourself saying the word as many times as you want.
5. Look for your feedback.
6. Try to understand the colors and sizes of the phones.
7. Try a new word.
The key observations of the test are the following:
вҖў It is not easy to know what the feedback is, especially the feedback given in text.
вҖў The colors of the phones are fairly easy to understand, but the sizes are not. This
might be because colors are universal knowledge in other programs as well, but
sizes are not.
вҖў The subjects did not search for information (icon) about the program on the site as
expected, so maybe the program should be more informative. The info could for
example be visible directly on the site in text and not a hover text.

Discussion
There are different types of possible feedback that can be given when learning how to
pronounce words. Other than phones, which are used in this project, some of the ways to
receive feedback could for example be by comparing audio waves, showing ultrasound
images of the inside of a mouth, or using AIВӯgenerated images based on the transcription
of an audio file.
вҖў The benefits of using audio waves is that the user is able to see the visualization
of the correct audio, where it is also possible to see the duration, intonation, and
emphasis of the pronunciation. The drawbacks are that the user is not able to see
articulation and tongue placement when pronouncing a word, which is very important
when learning foreign languages.
вҖў The benefits of using ultrasound images is that the user is able visualize how the
inside structure of the mouth is suppose to move while trying to pronounce a word.
The drawbacks are that the images might contain different pronunciations that visuВӯ
ally seem similar. Therefore only providing ultrasounds might not be enough, also
in relation to intonation.
вҖў The benefits of using AIВӯgenerated images is that it engages the users in an intuВӯ
itive way which might lead the users to remember the lessons easier, especially of
the users are visual learners. The drawbacks are that the user might not get any
specific feedback on how the pronunciation was, but rather if the word was correct.
For example if the user is trying to say the word вҖқboatвҖқ and an image of a boot
is shown, then the image doesnвҖҷt really give any feedback on how to improve the
pronunciation.
вҖў The benefits of using the phonetic alphabet is that phonetic feedback provides a
standardized alphabet that is known around the world by students, teachers, speech
experts, and others. The phones provide precise dialect and sound symbols which
is perfect for foreign languages. Additionally, the phones are able to show duration,
intonation, emphasis, and plosives, which is about everything needed for pronunciВӯ
ation training. The drawback is that it does not show a visual representation of the
pronunciation, but this can be added by implementing colors and sizes.
From the user tests conducted in this project, the subjects would not like another feedback
than the phonetic alphabet. Therefore, the tests and the analysis made above conclude
that phones most likely are the best option for the Phoneunciation program. Because
the subjects of the tests might be biased towards the method used in the Phoneunciation
program, the result need to be considered with caution.
The Phoneunciaiton system is different from other systems because the system is manВӯ
ageable to use, which is on purpose to not confuse the user. The other systems use
phones, colors, translation, descriptive feedback, and the possibility to compare the auВӯ
dio of the correct text and the audio of the recorded text. What makes the Phoneunciation
program stand out from the existing programs is that the program provides probability
measures of the phones and a language model that is trained on a language that is not
known by many. The probability measure is shown by setting the sizes of the phones. If
a printed phone is large then the phone is probably detected correctly from the audio, and
if the phone is small, then it might not be the correct phone detected from the audio. This

gives the user the possibility to interpret the feedback themselves and examine which
phone in the word to improve. A universal language model is supposed to work on most
languages, but a language as distinctive and incomparable as the Faroese language is
hard to get to work on just any universal model. Instead of using the universal language
model, the retrained Faroese language model gives the user the possibility to get a more
precise output of phones. Although the Faroese model could be fineВӯtuned on more data
to achieve a more precise outcome, the concept has already shown better results than
the universal language in section 7.1.
The Phoneunciation program uses the closest, second closest, and third closest probable
phones to verify the pronunciations. Other methods such as euclidean distance and PCA
shown in section 7.2 and section 7.3 are also good suggestions of verification. Which
method is the best of these three is not explored further as it is not in the scope of this
thesis. Furthermore, the user tests suggested that the colors were a good solution, beВӯ
cause the users understood the verification.
When comparing the Phoneunciation system to other existing Faroese systems, the conВӯ
clusion is quite clear. Based on the research made in this thesis, no other language
learning systems specifically focusing on Faroese pronunciation were found. The most
recognized ways to learn Faroese online are with Google Translate or Sprotin1. The
Ravnur system explained in section 3.3 is also an existing Faroese system, but it is used
for transcription and not pronunciation training. On Google translate the user is only able
to learn the spelling and grammar of the language and no indications of how the text is
supposed to be pronounced are given. The user isnвҖҷt even able to record a Faroese text
to be translated or listen to a translated text in Faroese like with many other languages on
Google Translate, because the record button is disabled. On Sprotin the user is able to
translate one word at a time. The inflected form of the words are provided and it is possiВӯ
ble to listen to an audio of how the word is suppose to sound, still there is no feedback of
the pronunciation of the user.
The Phoneunciaton program can benefit existing systems related to the Faroese language
learning. The research group at the Centre for Language Technology at the University of
the Faroe Islands, who have been working on the Ravnur Project and getting Faroese on
Google Translate, were thrilled to discover that someone other than them was interested
in developing tools for the Faroese language and they would like to use the findings of
the thesis for future work. It also benefits the preservation of the language and the rights
to the learning material created online remaining inside the country. Furthermore, Google
Translate could for example use the ASR model used in Ravnur to transcribe the text
when translating from Faroese to another language. And the language model created for
the Phoneunciation program could benefit by further developing a model that converts
phones to audio, which could be used to be able to listen to the translated text in Faroese.
Finally, the Phoneunciation system is able to benefit migrants wanting to integrate into the
community. This provides them the opportunity to study at home and not be dependent
on physical attendance to get feedback on pronunciations at learning institutions.

Conclusion
The aim of the thesis was to create a phonetic recognizer in Faroese, making it possible
for L2 learners to learn the language online and to provide them with sufficient feedback.
This has been accomplished by using the Allosaurus model and fineВӯtuning it in Faroese
resulting in a pronunciation learning system that is able to generate phones from audio and
the related feedback. The feedback is generated by using the features already present in
the Allosaurus system. The system provides the option to use the topВӯk phones, meaning
that if вҖқtopkвҖқ is set to 3 then the top 3 probable phones are given. This is used for coloring,
by visually representing the correctness, and validating the pronunciations. The other part
of the feedback is generated by using the probability measure also provided from the topВӯ
k. The probability measure is used for the sizes of the phones, to give the user an idea
of how the system captures the audio. The bigger the phones are the more likely they
are to being correctly identified from the recorded audio. The probability measure is not
used for validation, but the user is able to create their own assessment on which phone
to improve. Still, there is potential for enhancements.
The following describes potential future work that can be explored to enhance the project.
вҖў Conducting user tests on the concept of the program could have been done to see
if there is any demand for a Faroese CAPT system.
вҖў The Faroese language model trained for the Phoneunciation system can be fineВӯ
tuned with additional data to achieve a more precise representation of the Faroese
phonetic transcription. Currently, the amount of data used to train the model is not
sufficient, meaning that the phones generated in the Phoneunciation program do
not quite reflect the accurate phones from the true Faroese audio.
вҖў Suggesting a more flexible program could offer the user the possibility to enable and
disable feedback functions, meaning that the user could choose to be able to see
the colors, sizes or additional feedback such as intonation, emphasis, and plosives.
Audio waves, ultrasound images, or AIВӯgenerated images could also be optional,
depending on which learning method is preferred.
вҖў The implementation of different types of validation methods can also be suggested.
These could include methods, such as the topВӯk, the euclidean distance or the PCA
validation. After implementing each method a test could be conducted to evaluate
the effectiveness of the validations. From the test conducted the most effective
method would be used for validation.
Moreover, the user tests described in appendix F conclude that the program should be
made more userВӯfriendly and accessible for older people to use. The tests also verify that
phones are the correct feedback tool to use for this specific problem. The correctness of
the Faroese language model produced in this thesis is not sufficient, but it has improved
from the universal language model set as default in Allosaurus, explained in section 7.1.
Based on the statement given in this section, it can be concluded that the requirements
for this thesis project are fulfilled with room for further improvements.
