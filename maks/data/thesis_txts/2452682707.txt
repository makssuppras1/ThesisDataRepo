Abstract
Deﬁning porous structures as a collection of closely spaced structural components, this
project investigates the dissipation pattern of the individual components, based on forces
exerted in all three dimensions. To reduce complexity of the ﬂow, the structural members
are in all simulations represented as quadratic cubes, making it possible to investigate ﬂow
ﬁelds in details numerically, when the structure is exposed to an oscillatory ﬂow.
A good numerical model provides a solution to the accuracy needed for a speciﬁc
problem, at the lowest possible computational cost. The desired accuracy of the model in
this project is determined by the minimum diﬀerence between numerical- and experimental
results of two similar domains. To solve the numerical model at the lowest computational
cost, the amount of cells in the numerical domain (i.e. the domain resolution), providing
the most accurate solution, is wanted.
Conducting a convergence analysis for a ﬂow around a single cube, provided the most
accurate solution at the lowest computational cost. The convergence analysis provided
information as to how the numerical model should be divided into several subdomains,
in order to capture key ﬂow characteristics.
Initially, the ﬂow around a single cube
was analysed in terms of nominal- and average forces at a range of diﬀerent external
(undisturbed) ﬂow characteristics. With an acceptable correspondence between numerical
and experimental results, the model was then extended to encompass larger constellations
of cubes, namely 3x3- and 2x6 constellations, named after amount of rows and columns
of cubes.
The single cube constellation, revealed how horizontal forces are symmetric during the
oscillatory ﬂow cycle. A comparison between theory of the ﬂow around circular cylinders
and the numerical solution, revealed that diﬀerent mechanisms govern the lift forces on
cubes compared to circular cylinders. Consequently, an analysis of the ﬂow in between
several cubes, provided information on which mechanisms caused forces to either dissipate
or accumulate the cubes, depending on the undisturbed ﬂow characteristics. Also, it was
i
==PAGE:8==
ii
found that forces on components located on the perimeter of porous structures are governed
by diﬀerent ﬂow mechanisms compared to components inside the porous structure.
The overall ﬂow features identiﬁed around a single cube was concluded to also prevail
for cubes in a porous structure. Diﬀerences were however noticed for cubes situated on
the porous structure perimeter, compared to the ones located inside the porous structure.
It was therefore concluded that dissipation or accumulation tendencies are diﬀerent for
cubes on the perimeter compared to the ones inside the porous structure. Additionally,
cubes on the perimeter are dissipated or accumulated in diﬀerent directions, depending
on which side of the porous structure they are located.

Chapter1
Introduction
Coastal and marine engineering is a science on the establishment of structures at or near
the coast, both onshore and oﬀshore. Onshore coastal and marine engineering most often
focuses on coastline development, ﬂood protection or harbour design whereas oﬀshore
coastal and marine engineering concerns submerged parts of foundations, pipeline/cable
routes, sediment transport mechanisms near shore and in particular scour around sub-
merged structures. Oﬀshore submerged structures are through coastal and marine engi-
neering investigated by means of stability based on the forces that acts on the structures.
Examples of the mentioned structures are the jacket type foundations used on oﬀshore
wind turbines or substations in Figure 1.1a and a rubble mound breakwater shown in
Figure 1.1b.
(a) Oﬀshore substation with jacket type founda-
tion, Ramboll, 2019
(b) Sketch of rubble mound breakwater, Dentale,
Donnarumma, and Carratelli, 2013

enough for shadow eﬀects to cause diﬀerent forces.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Rubble mound breakwaters can be referred to as large porous structures, i.e. structures
with elements spaced close enough to feel the presence of other elements. Some elements
might therefore be located in the "shadow" of elements on the porous structure perimeter,
and is henceforth termed shadow zone.
Most rubble mound breakwaters are designed with multiple layers, usually a core, a
ﬁlter and an armour layer, all of which comprised by rock. The core layer ensures low wave
transmission and the material sizes are smaller than both ﬁlter and armour layer materials.
To keep the core layer intact, the ﬁlter layer surrounds the core, and the armour layer
stabilizes the entire structure due to its weight, and is placed around and on top of the ﬁlter
layer. With the armour stone sizes generally being comparable to the ones investigated
in this project, the results in this project apply directly to the understanding of ﬂow
mechanisms in rubble mound breakwaters, and due to the similar structural composition,
this includes scour protection. Although the jacket type foundations does not consist of
components so closely spaced as breakwaters, shadow zones and velocity/pressure ﬁelds
do behave in roughly the same manor. Therefore, the general tendencies found in this
project can explain why peak loads diﬀer when comparing each structural element in such
a foundation.
Depending on the challenge, the ﬁrst task a marine/coastal engineer is faced with, is
the determination of the ﬂow type aﬀecting the structure. Sediment transport mechanisms,
scour mechanisms and force ﬁelds are worlds apart if the governing ﬂow is a unidirectional
current compared to oscillatory ﬂow posed by e.g.
waves.
In extension, the types
of currents and waves will too play a signiﬁcant role when designing marine/coastal
structures and may be determined through experimental tests.
During the early 20th century where computers were barely invented, let alone having
any substantial power compared to the computers of today, the theory which was developed
in the marine and coastal industry, was based on empirical data. Performing countless
amounts of experiments, the overall tendencies for ﬂows described by e.g. the Keulegan-
Carpenter number KC and Reynolds number Re were tabulated and standardized for future
reference. The ﬁrst actual computational models were not used before the mid 1960’s,
paving the way for numerical parametric investigations instead of costly experimental
setups, Jameson, 2015.
Diﬀerent types of numerical models have been developed since the 1960’s, Jame-
son, 2015.
In relation to this project where a ﬂow surrounding structures consisting
of submerged cylinders/cubes, so-called large porous structures, it is necessary to have
a solver with the ability to deal with turbulent incompressible ﬂows. Due to the cur-
rently changing weather conditions, and the growing demand for installation of oﬀshore
power plants, the need for modelling ﬂows in large porous structures such as breakwaters,
clustered pipelines/cable routes or even foundations with closely spaced members, has
==PAGE:23==
3
increased. To understand if and how such structures are slowly disintegrated (broken),
successful numerical investigations will provide the opportunity to ﬁnd even the smallest
ﬂow structures, which leads to destructive pressure and velocity ﬁelds.
As will be seen in this project, the literature is well developed regarding two-
dimensional ﬂows around cylinders. As an extension to works performed by Bjørke,
2015 and Nielsen and Borup, 2019, the three-dimensional eﬀects on cubes are to be ad-
dressed in this project. This project will refer to the velocity- and pressure ﬁelds inherent
in-between each structural member in large porous structures. Some ﬁndings can also
be extended to similar structures with shadow eﬀects present. Mapping the velocity- and
pressure ﬁelds along with force patterns will help to understand dissipative processes in
oscillatory ﬂows (waves) or magnitudes of e.g. inline forces on each structural member
of pipelines/foundations spaced closely.
This project will primarily extend the ﬁndings of the experimental study performed
by Nielsen and Borup, 2019 at the Technical University of Denmark. The experimental
study has successfully compared force measurements for several constellations of porous
structures to the literature (and previous projects). It is therefore the intention to create
a numerical model to represent the experimental ﬂows, making it possible to perform
parametric studies on large porous structures without having to create large experimental
setups.
Additionally, the numerical model may serve as a predictor of velocity- and
pressure ﬁelds and ﬂows patterns for ranges of governing parameters not yet investigated.
This project is the ﬁrst numerical model to investigate armour layer sized cubes in
oscillatory ﬂows and contributes to an extensive investigation of ﬂows in large porous
structures. The intention in this project is therefore to map overall ﬂow tendencies in
large porous structures with components being quadratic. For future projects, it may be
used as validation when arbitrary cross sectional shapes are investigated in more realistic
constellations with a free surface.

Chapter2
Theory behind ﬂow around
cylinders/cubes
Starting from how the oscillatory ﬂow is enabled in the numerical model, this chapter
describes how forces on submerged cylinders/cubes are calculated, mainly in the inline
and vertical directions. The ﬁrst part concerns the applicability of the solver that is used,
as it applies a sinusoidal velocity signal on the inlet boundary. Secondly, theoretical
ﬂow regimes are described with reference to Sumer et al., 2006 comprising an extensive
collection of empirical data and theoretical points of views.
The inﬂuence of main
governing parameters is discussed through a dimensional analysis, ultimately leading to
the signiﬁcance of each parameter aﬀecting the force.
A thorough description will be given in terms of inline forces and how they are
predicted by the Morison equation, and the calculation method for acquiring the inertia-
and drag coeﬃcients. Also to some extent, the vertical forces induced on cylinders/cubes
near the seabed are described. Note that these descriptions are based on cylinders with
circular cross sections in Sumer et al., 2006, which is why parallels are drawn to quadratic
cross sections through the available literature.
Concluding this section is the stability of a single cube exposed to oscillatory ﬂows,
providing an expression for the density ρc needed for stability at diﬀerent KC numbers.

the entire project, where diﬀerent cube constellations are placed symmetrically around the
bounding box (x, y)-plane center. The slope of the domain is kept at a constant zero. This
model is shown in Figure 2.1 for single cube (henceforth referred to as 1x1 constellation).

above domain exposed to oscillatory ﬂows, which is why a free surface model has not been
implemented. In order to simulate the subsurface particle motion an oscillating external

The bodyforce is applied as a sinusoidal signal on the inlet (top red surface in Figure
2.1) along with a periodic boundary condition on the outlet (bottom red in Figure 2.1).
The maximum velocity Um of this motion is used in Equation (2.1)
U(t) = Um sin(ωt)

maximum value of the free stream velocity, t being the temporal variation integer and ω is
the angular frequency related to the period by Equation (2.2), where T is the ﬂow period.
ω = 2π/T
(2.2)

2.2
Flow regimes and governing parameters
Given the physical environment and body force application, the ﬂow around cylinders is
well described through the ﬂow around a circular cylinder in Sumer et al., 2006. The
works conducted by Sumer et al., 2006 consists of large amount of test results where
correlations have been made for steady currents and oscillatory ﬂows, and thus describing
ﬂow types based on ﬂow regimes and associated forces.
Common to ﬂows in steady currents or oscillatory ﬂows is that certain parameters gov-
erns the overall ﬂow regimes. In steady currents the Reynolds number Re is the parameter
that governs the ﬂow regimes, where in oscillatory ﬂows the Keulegen-Carpenter number,
KC, in combination with Re are the governing parameters. A dimensional analysis might
convince the reader why this is, and is given later in this section. A thorough description
of both parameters will be given in the following subsections.
The theory of ﬂow characteristics in Sumer et al., 2006 is however based on a two-
dimensional approach and circular cross sections. This project compares the theory behind
2-dimensional considerations and results of 3-dimensional simulations.

submerged square cylinders/cubes, some key features from circular cylinders in steady

The methodology adopted in this project is quite similar to the ones described in Nielsen

Convergence analysis
Breaking down the steps in a convergence analysis, an accepted process is to run simula-
tions for continuously increased amount of cells until the solution is independent of the
amount of cells in the domain, [Boz, Erdogdu, and Tutar, 2014]. It is a trial and error
process which can be quite time demanding, however in the long run worth the eﬀort.
Please note that a convergence analysis provides only the optimal amount of cells for a
converged solution. It is most likely that the converged solution is not equal to the correct
solution compared to experimental results, but conveys a solution independent of number
of cells only. In this project, the converged solution portrays forces when e.g. averaging
governing equations and modelling turbulence instead of solving it. Hence, the results are
bound to diﬀer from experimental ones.

the vorticity created along the cylinder walls is fed into the shear layer, hence the amount
of vorticity has a signiﬁcant impact on the forces created. With the boundary layer being
relatively small, reﬁnement of the model near the cube walls has to be large enough to

Serial vs parallel simulations
As mentioned, splitting the numerical model into 12 instead of 20 cores leads to a slight
deviation in the results as seen in Figure 4.4. This deviation is more or less signiﬁcant,
depending on the amount of subdomains used.
==PAGE:71==
4.4. CHOOSING SUITABLE ∆T
51
Subdomains are created when large domains are solved. In this case OpenFOAM
simply divides the entire domain into x amount of subdomains, all of which can then be
solved independently at the same time. This drastically reduces the computational time
spent on an investigation. Once the simulation has completed, the results from each core
are used to reconstruct the solution for the entire domain. The reconstruction is however
associated with round oﬀerrors at the subdomain boundaries.
The reconstruction does not only compromise accuracy, it can also slow down com-
putations signiﬁcantly if the wrong amount of cores is used. Using 20 cores can in some
cases lead to signiﬁcantly slower computation than e.g. 10 cores, as the majority of the
time is spent on reconstructing the entire domain (i.e. communication between each core)
rather than actually solving the problem. This is often seen when assigning large amount
of cores to small domains. In the CFD community, this is referred to as Amdahl’s Law,
Amdahl, 2011, and is to some extend used to estimate the most optimal amount of cores
depending on the domain size. Without complicating this potential issue, a recommended
50.000-100.000 cells per core is the rule of thumb used in this project.
Altering the relative tolerances of the variables being solved for, this can however be
mitigated. The signiﬁcance of this lies both with accuracy compromises and computational
time. The eﬀects of using 12 cores rather than 20 does not compromise the computational
time greatly as the domain is rather large. The accuracy however is shown with ⋄in Figure
4.4. The diﬀerences in CM and CD are 1% and .6 % when using 20 cores instead of
12, for the inertia and drag coeﬃcient respectively. Although the diﬀerences would most
likely not change the reﬁnement level choice in the convergence analysis, the fact that it
originates from the amount of subdomains used, makes it relatively large.

suggest that if ∆t is too large, vortices near periodic boundaries can make a particle
appearing at time step m leaving the domain, reappear at time step m + 1 as is has curled
back into the domain, terminating the simulation due to too large Cr or forces in O(1010)

Numerical validation
Prior to descending into the details of the ﬂow in large porous structures, the numerical
model needs to be validated by ensuring that governing physical entities are present in
the solution. Therefore, the validation of the numerical model is based on a comparison
with CM and CD obtained by Nielsen and Borup, 2019 containing results of a 1x1 cube

Before concluding on the validation, the inﬂuence of some parameters in the numerical
setup has been investigated. Starting with a physical aspect, namely the so-called blockage
eﬀect, which relates the "ﬂume" width W with the parallel cylinder width, in this case
D. The degree of blockage eﬀect is calculated as D/W. For the validation cases D/b =
0.5/5 = 0.1, i.e. a 10 % blockage is present. According to Bjørke, 2015 the blockage
eﬀect is however associated with an increased inline force as the ﬂow contraction becomes
more signiﬁcant for increased blockage. Hence, for D/W →1 the ﬂow resistance is
increased leading to a larger inline force proportional to the free stream velocity magnitude.
Therefore, CD would be expected to increase with the blockage eﬀect present, leading to
the conclusion that the blockage eﬀect is not what is causing the diﬀerence found in Figure
5.7.
Turning the attention to the numerical environment, which through convergence anal-
ysis is seen to provide diﬀerent results based on e.g. reﬁnement levels, timestep ∆t etc.
It was found that the explicit feature edge reﬁnement had a great inﬂuence on the calcu-
lated force coeﬃcients. The explicit feature edge reﬁnement is simply the cell reﬁnement
around sharp corners in the model. In the case of cubes this would be along sides and at
the corners. The signiﬁcance of this parameter was investigated in two identical simula-
tions where the explicit feature edge reﬁnement is set to level 4 and 5 respectively. The
diﬀerence in CM and CD for several cubes in the same model (this case a 3x3 constellation)
is shown in Figure 5.13.

of lift and inline forces related to the free stream velocity corresponds with the literature

Chapter 8
Conclusion
A numerical model has been used to solve a 3-dimensional ﬂow problem around quadratic
cubes undergoing an oscillatory motion. The main feature of the model is averaging
processes which are used in combination with turbulence models and wall functions with
the aim to reproduce ﬂow mechanisms established in an experimental setup.
In order to reproduce the experimental ﬂow, a proper compromise between computa-
tional eﬀort and accuracy was found through a convergence analysis, thoroughly described
in the methodology and accepted in the literature. This led to the achievement of the ﬁrst
milestone in the project, namely validation of the numerical model.
To validate the model, a comparison of inertia- and drag coeﬃcients, CM and CD,
showed a clear resemblance between experimental and numerical results regarding the
inertia coeﬃcient CM. The relative diﬀerence in CM was in general below 4 %, whereas
the relative diﬀerence for CD was averaged to approximately -14 %. As no numerical
cause for the systematic deviation in CD was identiﬁed, the basic assumption of complete
Reynold’s number independence on forces, is questioned for this setup. The combination
of possible (minor)errors in the experimentalstudy and the impactof e.g. single reﬁnement
parameters in the numerical model, led to the conclusion that the variation of CM and
CD for both experimental and numerical investigations, showed similar behaviour with
relatively low deviation. It is therefore concluded that the numerical model is validated.
The model validation allowed for investigation of 1x1, 3x3 and 2x6 constellations
by means of forces exerted on the cubes in three dimensions.
Results from the 1x1
constellation conﬁrmed a symmetric behaviour of horizontal forces, and correspondence
between peaks in vertical forces from numerical results and theory. Consequently, the
stability analysis for a single cube provided insight in the minimum required density ρc,min
to ensure horizontal and vertical stability. At KC = 27.5, it was found that ρc,min = 12000
[kg/m3], equivalent to approximately 4 times the normal armour layer density. Thus,
it is concluded that the instability for single cubes in oscillatory motion at large KC is

signiﬁcant.
Creating ensemble averages for 3x3- and 2x6-constellations, provided the ﬁrst visual
information of the average direction where a force is exerted to each structural member,
arranged in a structured grid. The tendencies for both constellations were that cubes in
columns located on the perimeter of porous structures, are exposed to larger horizontal
forces compared to the cubes situated inside the porous structure. For the columns on
the perimeter, the average direction of which the horizontal force is exerted, depends on
KC. Speciﬁcally for the 3x3-constellation, when KC < 12, the perimeter columns were
dissipated in positive and negative x1-direction. For KC > 12, the average horizontal
forces are directed towards the porous structure center. The 2x6-constellation showed that
without a porous structure boundary, the cubes in columns on the perimeter are forced
almost in the same direction as the oscillatory ﬂow.
In addition to forces exerted to each structural member of a large porous structure, it
was found that the ratio of inertia- and drag terms of the inline force changed signiﬁcantly
inside the structure. For a large range of KC, components in the middle are dominated by
inertia, whereas cubes on the perimeter are dominated by drag terms from KC = 12 −15.
Compared to the inertia/drag regime deﬁnitions for single circular cylinders, and according
to the literature, the transition is at KC = 20 −30.
To determine the bed shear stress for several types of sediments at diﬀerent sizes, it
can be concluded that the method presented in this project performs well. The results
revealed an increase of τ when transitioning between diﬀerent roughnesses. Furthermore,
the results revealed a constant value in the middle of the sediment patches comprised by
the same roughness equivalent. It is therefore possible to estimate the bed shear stress
in a vast selection of ﬂow problems with the presented method. This may enhance the
understanding of magnitude of τ in complex ﬂow problems.
Lastly, it was possible to map the diﬀerences between ﬂow structures described in
the literature and results in diﬀerent constellations. Although force oscillations might be
similar for 2-dimensional ﬂows around circular cylinders and 3-dimensional ﬂows around
quadratic cubes, the origin of the forces are not necessarily the same. It is concluded
that the origin of e.g. vertical forces are not induced by the same vortex mechanisms
in the two cases mentioned. Extending the ﬁndings of ﬂow mechanisms around a 1x1
cube constellation to the larger constellations, revealed that even though the complexity
of the ﬂow increased, overall tendencies prevailed and have therefore been described in
this project. A streaming function above the cubes have in particular shown to dominate
the vertical forces on quadratic cubes.

Appendix B
Ensemble averages
The ensemble averages presented in this annex, are average force time series based on
10 periods of simulation, both for 1x1, 3x3 and 2x6 constellations. For each full period
simulated, a force time series has been stored as a vector and an average of each force at
each time step has then been computed and plotted as function of ωt. All forces are as
pr. section 2.4 non-dimensionalized. The title of each plot relates the forces shown to a
speciﬁc cube in the particular constellation of interest with the speciﬁc KC.
For 1x1 constellations, a total of 12 ensemble averages are shown leaving out the one
where KC = 30.
For 3x3 constellations, the ensemble averages are calculated for each of the 9 cubes with
numbering convention adopted in Figure 4.1. The range of KC investigated are 6, 9, 12,
15 and 18. For 2x6 constellation the KC range is from 6 to 13 and again, the numbering
convention is the one shown in Figure 4.1.

Appendix C
Average and maximum horizontal
forces applied to cubes in oscillatory
motion
During the ensemble average calculation, the average and maximum horizontal forces
have been calculated as Equation (2.31) for each cube in the relevant constellation and
plotted in the (x1, x2)-plane in this Annex. The cube numbering corresponds to previous
deﬁnition. Each vector arrow is scaled so that the length of it is equal to one. The vector
itself does therefore not resemble the relative magnitude, which is why the average and
maximum horizontal forces experienced are listed on the left hand’s side of each ﬁgure.
Each ﬁgure caption shows the governing KC.
Please note that the listed average and peaks values should be held against the direction
of the arrow for each cube. E.g. for KC = 18, cube 4 experiences a maximum of 1104.6
[N] in the left hand’s side direction, where as cube 6 experiences 1093.5 in the opposite
direction.
