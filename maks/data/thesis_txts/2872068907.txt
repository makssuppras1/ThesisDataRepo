Abstract 
Corporate Venture Capital (CVC) is becoming growing in significance of fostering 
external innovation, the capacity of corporates and startups to collaborate efficiently has taken 
front stage in strategic relevance. But often the translation of pilot-stage success into long-term 
integration is complicated by cultural misalignment, organizational complexity, and operational 
asymmetries. This thesis investigates how individuals who is balancing organizational and cultural 
boundaries, as boundary spanners, allow partnership across two key phases of CVC–startup 
engagement: pilot execution and post-pilot integration. Grounded in a conceptual framework 
combining the Corporate Venturing Network (CVN) model with four boundary spanner 
competency domains (Organizational Navigation, Strategic Bridging, Relationship & Cultural 
Competence, and Entrepreneurial Flexibility), the study draws on semi- qualitative interviews with 
corporate innovation leads and startup founders. The results show that maintaining continuity of 
collaboration, mediating between different organizational logics, and aligning expectations 
depend on boundary spanners. Structured collaboration models improve predictability but must 
remain adaptable to adapt to startup dynamics. The paper provides micro-level analysis of the 
integration mechanisms and human competencies supporting them. 
 
 
Keywords: Corporate Venture Capital (CVC); Corporate–Startup Collaboration; Boundary Spanner; 
Innovation Management; Strategic Alignment; Cultural Integration.

1. Introduction 
Established business enterprises are under growing pressure to transform, grow, and continually 
create innovative concepts. This pressure has driven companies to look beyond their capacity and 
engage in strategic alliances with startups, businesses mostly known for their speed, 
inventiveness, and disruptive power. Thus, corporate–startup collaboration (CSC) has become an 
essential strategic tool allowing large businesses to leverage outside sources of innovation and 
provide startups with necessary funds, networks, and credibility. 
According to this context, corporate venture capital (CVC) has become rather important. CVC aims 
to invest in startups with the dual goals of promoting innovation and maintaining market 
relevance, functioning as a hybrid model that combines the objectives of strategic partnership and 
financial return. Still, many such partnerships crash short of their potential, regardless of this 
encouraging model. Studies repeatedly show that although first collaborations usually engage 
in the form of pilots or proof-of-concept (PoC) projects, the actual difficulty arises during the post-
pilot phase: integration (Weiblen & Chesbrough, 2015). Initially, enthusiasm and ambition drive 
these projects. 
The foundation of this challenge is basic related to organizational and cultural mismatch. 
Operating logics, speed, risk tolerance, and communication styles define corporations and startups 
rather differently in terms of business culture. Startups run with agility, experimentation, and lean 
structures, while corporations are often risk-averse and process-driven. Although they could be 
complementary, when transitioning from an initial pilot to a stronger, long-term alliance, these 
variations often cause friction, misinterpretation, and problems in partnership (Rigtering et al., 
2021; Corvello et al., 2021). 
My curiosity in the subject started with this recurring misalignment. Through scholarly research 
and practical knowledge in the corporate-startup ecosystem, I started discovering a recurring 
theme: success or failure in these partnerships often depended not only on strategy or funding 
but also on people, individuals capable of bridging two quite different organizational worlds. 
Frequently referred to as boundary spanners, those individuals seemed essential in helping to set 
expectations, strengthen communication, and establish trust. 
Boundary spanners are certainly significant in practice, yet the scholarly literature still underlines 
their under discovery. What happens after the initial collaborative phase is a matter of concern. 
Although the scouting and pilot phases have drawn more attention, the pilot execution and 
integration phases are still much overlooked and raise unresolved crucial issues regarding what 
promotes or avoids effective long-term cooperation. 
1.1 
Problem Statement 
Under the umbrella of CVC, CSC has become a rather popular approach for encouraging innovation 
and competitiveness in large corporations. Though these alliances provide corporations access to 
==PAGE:10==
 
 
 
10 
agility and emerging technologies and startups with capital and credibility, their long-term success 
is often compromised by misalignment between the two organizational worlds. Although these 
partnerships hold strategic value, most of the academic research has concentrated on the initial 
stages of involvement, specifically deal sourcing, scouting, and pilot testing. 
The post-pilot integration phase, when partnerships must move from experimentation to 
operational and strategic embedding, is a noteworthy and overlooked area. This moment 
frequently exposes unconscious organizational and cultural tensions that might compromise the 
sustainability of a partnership. Present literature still fails to sufficiently cover the specific 
processes and actor-level behaviors that either support or hinder effective integration. Moreover, 
although the function of boundary spanners who enable cooperation across institutional 
boundaries is well-known conceptually, there is limited empirical research on how these actors 
behave in CSC. 
Thus, this thesis aims to address these entwined gaps by investigating how integration processes 
develop in CSC and how boundary spanners enable cultural alignment and strategic continuity 
during this transition. 
1.2 
Research Objectives 
This study acknowledges that effective transitions from first collaboration to long-term alliances 
cannot occur unconsciously. Rather, they are inspired by the actions of boundary spanners who 
cross organizational boundaries and coordinate stakeholders across various organizational 
settings. The present research aims primarily to investigate the operation of boundary spanners 
over key periods involving collaboration. Two significant steps in the collaboration lifecycle are the 
pilot execution phase, which refers to early testing and validation, and the post-pilot integration 
phase, in which decisions including formalizing the partnership are the primary focus of the study. 
Three objectives for this study are, first, to identify the main cultural and operational challenges 
that surface during CSC's pilot and integration phases. Second, the study will examine the skills, 
behaviors, and relational strategies that boundary spanners use to reduce disagreements, enable 
knowledge circulation, and coordinate strategic objectives. Third, to investigate the changes in the 
roles of boundary spanners between the pilot and integration phases and evaluate how their 
contributions support long-term cooperative success. By means of these goals, the present study 
aims to offer a micro-level, actor-centered perspective on cooperative processes.  
1.3 
Research Questions 
In what ways might boundary spanners support organizational integration and cultural 
alignment throughout the pilot implementation and post-pilot CSC? 
 
This question seeks to clarify how the activity of boundary spanners supports more effortless 
transitions throughout important collaboration phases and captures both their structural and

relational areas.   It places boundary spanners as central players in resolving the recurring 
organizational and interpersonal conflicts defining CVC–startup projects. 
 
First sub-question: 
In what ways might boundary spanners help to reduce cultural conflict and match expectations

This question addresses on the early engagement period, when initially trust and mutual 
understanding have to grow. It tries to investigate how boundary spanners understand and 
translate between several organizational logics and objectives.   
 
Second Sub-question:

This question transforms focus to the phase of post-pilot integration. It investigates at how

dynamics needed for effective adoption and scaling. 
Empirical research based on these questions is grounded in a conceptual lens shaped by the 
literature on boundary spanning, cultural integration, and inter-organizational collaboration. 
 
1.4 
Hypothesis development 
The formulation of conceptual hypotheses strengthens the analytical clarity of the research even 
though the study uses a qualitative and exploratory method.   These hypotheses assist in defining 
the expected dynamics that will be investigated by means of empirical data and thematic analysis. 
 
Hypothesis 1 – Pilot Execution Phase 
By promoting mutual understanding and aligning short-term partnership expectations, boundary 
spanners help minimize the negative effects of differences in culture during the pilot phase. 
This theory suggests that during the PoC or Pilot phase boundary spanners function as important 
the translators and relationship brokers. Through straightforward objectives, control of 
expectations, and building trust, they help both sides negotiate early-stage cultural and 
operational differences. 
 
Hypothesis 2 – Integration Phase 
By allowing strategic alignment, relational continuity, and internal adoption inside the corporate 
organization, boundary spanners favorably affect post-pilot integration.

Successful integration in the post-pilot phase requires for governance and relationship-based 
alignment in equal measure as technical viability. This theory maintains that by controlling internal 
resistance, guaranteeing executive contributions, and providing continuous stakeholder 
involvement, boundary spanners essentially translate pilot success into scalable outcomes. 
These theories taken together help to provide a sophisticated knowledge of how strategic 
mediation, interpretive capacity, and interpersonal competencies support the success of 
innovation alliances at several phases of partnership.

2.1 
Theoretical Foundations 
The present study incorporates the combination of three interrelated theoretical lenses to 
investigate the cultural integration issues and the function of boundary spanners in CSC. Every one 
of these models presents a complementary viewpoint that together helps one to better grasp how 
collaboration between organizations develops and where frictions are and opportunities for 
alignment. This chapter presents the main ideas and theoretical underpinnings that guide the 
study, offers a summary of related scholarly arguments, and clarifies how each viewpoint supports 
to answer the main research question. 
Open Innovation Theory provides a foundation for this research since it has profoundly altered the 
way companies handle their innovation processes.   Open innovation, as Chesbrough (2003a, 
2003b) clearly places it, represents a change from closed, internal research and development 
(R&D) models toward broader boundaries, where outside ideas and paths to market are actively 
sought and integrated. According to this paradigm, knowledge moves bi-directionally across 
organizational bounderies, allowing companies to both incorporate outside innovations internally 
and profit from internal innovations externally.   
Corporate Venturing Theory (Maula, 2007) provides a useful framework for recognizing the 
strategic and conflicting interests that underlie CVC activities. Emphasizing that some CVC 
investments are meant to create strategic value through innovation, capability development, or 
long-term business alignment while others are made with a primary goal of financial return, this 
theoretical perspective distinguishes between financial and strategic venturing logics. 
The most actor-centered and carefully calibrated prism through which to view the process of 
collaboration is offered by Boundary Spanning Theory (Williams, 2002; Champenois et al., 2017). 
Operating at the intersection of companies, boundary spanners are translators, negotiators, and 
mediators. Their significance becomes especially crucial in complex, inter-organizational settings 
where different goals, cultures, and communication approaches interact. 
These three theoretical models taken combined form a conceptual basis for analyzing the central 
research question: How do boundary spanners enable cultural alignment and organizational 
integration across the pilot execution and post-pilot phases of CSC?  
The study subsequently explores the empirical gaps in the literature and presents the conceptual 
framework that directs the next data collecting and analysis in the next chapters.

2.1.1 Open Innovation  
Introduced by Chesbrough (2003a), the open innovation paradigm has fundamentally changed 
how businesses approach innovation. Open innovation challenges business enterprises to make 
full use of outside knowledge, technologies, and alliances to complement internal capabilities, so 
connecting the traditional approach of internal research and development (R&D). In 2003a 
Chesbrough defines open innovation as a model in which "firms can and should use external ideas 
as well as internal ideas, and internal and external paths to market" (Chesbrough, 2003a, p. 43). 
In his landmark paper in MIT Sloan Management Review (Chesbrough, 2003b), Lucent 
Technologies and Cisco Systems are compared to show this change. While Lucent mostly relied on 
its legacy internal R&D from Bell Labs, Cisco developed its capacity for innovation mostly through 
outside collaborations including strategic startups investments. " Leading industrial enterprises of 
the past have been encountering remarkably strong competition from many upstarts... [who] get 
new ideas to market through a different process," Chesbrough notes (Chesbrough, 2003b). More 
recent entrepreneurs have been able to remain competitive because of outside innovation, 
without the substantial infrastructure usually needed for in-house R&D. 
Large enterprises have operational as well as strategic reasons for implementing open innovation 
strategies.   Working with startups gives access to new technologies, market segments, and 
business models that might be too experimental or incompatible with present corporate 
strategies. Open innovation also helps companies to speed time to market, diversify their 
innovation portfolio, and distribute the expenses and risks related with technological development 
(Chesbrough, 2003a). 
Finally, open innovation indicates a strategic change in how businesses grow, integrate, and 
commercialize novel concepts.   It helps companies not only stay competitive in fast-paced 
surroundings but also transform within by adopting an experimental culture, external interaction, 
and cooperative value creation. In an environment of a variety of knowledge, companies which 
can combine and implement both internal and external innovations will most likely be able to 
sustain innovation-driven growth, as Chesbrough (2003) argues. 
2.1.2 What is Corporate Venturing 
Often by engaging with businesses outside of the existing organizational structure, corporate 
venturing - a broad umbrella used to describe the activities carried out by established companies 
to promote innovation and create new businesses - is being done by Maula, 2007, and Casanova 
et al., 2017. Targeting to better capture the value from movements of new technology and 
innovation, corporate venturing is considered a strategic choice to complement internal R&D 
programs (Casanova et al., 2017; Di Lorenzo et al., 2023). Corporate venture capital (CVC) is 
regarded as a specific tool within the external corporate venturing portfolio (Maula, 2007). 
Equity investments made by large corporations in entrepreneurial startups, CVC, are defined as 
minority equity investments made by established companies in privately owned companies (Di 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
15 
Lorenzo et al., 2023). Considered a particular tool in the external corporate venturing (ECV) tool 
set is CVC. From the standpoint of the corporate perspective, it can be seen as a means of ECV as 
well as an alternative source of funding from the entrepreneurial company perspective (Maula, 
2007; Corvello et al., 2021; Dushnitsky et al., 2005).  
2.1.2.1 
How Corporate Venture Capital Functions 
Specific groups within established businesses carry out many of the CVC initiatives (Corvello et al., 
2021). Independent venture capital (VC) companies (Corvello et al., 2021) often syndicate them. 
Operating at the intersections among established companies, startups, and VC investors, which 
often have different points of view, goals, and practices, CVC is a boundary-spanning operation 
(Corvello et al., 2021; Maula, 2007). CVC represents a complex phenomenon characterized by 
tensions (Dizdarevic et al. 2023). CVC units simultaneously relate to both the corporate parent and 
the startup/VC community (Corvello et al., 2021; Dizdarevic et al., 2023). The source of external 
knowledge from the startup and VC communities for the corporate parents, they function as 
"knowledge brokers" (Corvello et al., 2021). 
With a focus toward maximizing value from technological and innovation activities, organizations 
use CVC as a strategic tool to augment internal R&D programs (Casanova et al., 2017b). The 
primary objective is to actively participate in "disembodied experimentation," a method of 
learning characterized as outside of the boundaries of the organization (Keil et al., 2008). By using 
this process, companies can break free from their usual limitations and better understand the gaps 
in their skills, which helps them make better decisions about developing their abilities. As a means 
of outside R&D to build their knowledge base, competencies, technologies, products, and 
processes, some companies use CVC for "venture-specific learning" from portfolio companies 
(Maula, 2007). Realizing this advantage usually involves more frequent contact with the portfolio 
companies and closer collaboration. Ventures operating in the same or related sectors often make 
investments aimed at venture-specific learning and external R&D (Maula, 2007).This challenges 
communication channels and follow-on strategies, companies can also use CVC to investigate 
entirely novel and unrelated business areas (Sykes, 1990). 
Processes must be established for CVC units to maximize the innovation-enhancing value for the 
parent company, the CVC unit, and the startups (Dizdarevic et al., 2023). Establishing avenues to 
seize the strategic worth of investments is crucial. These steps differentiate between exploring 
new possibilities, like gaining market insights and learning about new technologies, and using 
existing resources, like accessing additional technologies and expanding into new markets.  
2.1.2.2 
Why Parent Company Needs CVC  
In general, CVC is viewed as a strategic tool accessible to companies. The importance of it results 
from several strategic advantages, usually given higher priority than financial returns but also 
effectively used as strategic tool for parent company to investigate exceptional advantages in 
market (Casanova et al., 2017b).  
==PAGE:16==
 
 
 
16 
Driven by a wide range of strategic goals that correspond to their long-term internal strategy, 
enterprises in many different sectors are progressively participating in startup partnerships 
through CVC. Although every company runs through a different industry framework and 
organizational manner. Furthermore, these partnerships reflect a desire to create strategic value, 
improve organizational learning, and promote long-term adaptability compared to solely financial 
motivation. 
Startups sometimes bring innovative ideas, disruptive technologies, and agile working models that 
can enable established businesses get beyond internal resistance and react to fast shifting market 
conditions. Investing in or cooperating with startups allows corporates early access to innovative 
concepts and new revenue sources outside of their regular scope.   
Increasingly closely linked to this is CVC's contribution to complementing in-house R&D. Although 
conventional R&D teams usually concentrate on small-scale innovations in related fields, startups 
provide paths for more exploratory and high-risk innovation. By means of this external orientation, 
businesses can diversify their innovation pipeline and test technologies, and business models, they 
might not be ready to develop internally. 
Furthermore, numerous companies see CVC as a "window on emerging technologies and trends" 
(Dizdarevic et al., 2023, Maula, 2007, Di Lorenzo et al., 2023), a strategic tool to track, evaluate, 
and comprehend disruptive forces in their sectors. By means of focused investments or alliances, 
companies acquire exclusive access to early-stage innovations in areas including artificial 
intelligence, biotech, fintech, or sustainability (Dizdarevic et al., 2023, Rigtering et al., 2021). This 
supports long-term positioning in still developing markets and allows strategic foresight. 
Beyond technology scouting, investigation of new business possibilities is another basic motivator. 
Working with startups provides companies investigate consumer markets, geographies, or 
product categories they might not be ready to launch on their own. Startups are agile probes into 
unexplored areas, and the knowledge gained will enable corporates to shape future products or 
pivot strategies with fewer risks and more market intelligence (Oxford Research et al., 2019, 
TechBBQ, 2024). 
From a capability-building viewpoint, CVC helps academics sometimes refer to as "disembodied 
experimentation" - innovation efforts outside the conventional boundaries of the corporate 
structure - by means of through observing or supporting with startup operations, corporates may 
discover areas of weakness in their own business entities and acquire firsthand experience in 
guiding internal growth. These joint efforts over time can help to create new competencies in 
digitalization, customer-centric design, or agile project management (Maula, 2007). 
Equally important is CVC's contribution to corporate entrepreneurship. Engaging startups provides 
a cultural intervention for numerous businesses, particularly those with deeply rooted established 
structures (Rigtering & Behrens, 2021). Internal teams can be inspired by entrepreneurial ideas, 
iterative development cycles, and resource-constrained innovation as well as by a change toward 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
17 
more experimental, flexible, and entrepreneurial approaches of working. CVC is not only an 
instrument for innovation but also a means of organizational renewal (Rigthening et al., 2021). 
Particular times, CVC additionally serves as the foundation for strategic acquisitions or 
integrations. Before committing to a complete acquisition, a minority investment provides 
companies examine the startup's potential in a low-risk environment, track team dynamics, and 
evaluate technological fit (Brigl et al., 2021, Dizdarevic et al., 2023). 
Lastly, it is crucial to understand that many companies have established CVC as a long-term 
strategic tool. Venture arm is closely included into the corporate development plan, so enabling 
the alignment of innovation efforts with changing long-term objectives (Maula, 2007). The dual 
function of CVC is both a strategic tool for external exploration and an internal catalyst for change, 
which reflects the increasing awareness that sustainable competitive advantage in the modern 
corporate environment requires involvement outside of company boundaries. 
In simple terms, even if different companies have different specific targets for working with 
startups, the fundamental reason for doing relates to a mix of internal strategic alignment and 
outside innovation seeking. Particularly for businesses trying to remain agile, forward-thinking, 
and adaptable in an increasingly complex environment, this dual role makes CVC a vital component 
of corporate innovation strategies today. 
2.1.2.3 
Internal vs. External Corporate Venturing 
Internal corporate venturing refers to new ideas developed within the company, while ECV 
involves activities conducted outside the organization, highlighting a fundamental difference 
(Maula, 2007). Internal and external forms of corporate venturing are abundant. Internal 
corporate venturing is the development of novel ideas inside the confines of the company from 
internal resources (Sharma et al., 1999; Maula, 2007). By comparison, ECV is minority investments 
in startups through CVC programs, venturing alliances, or transformational acquisitions, which 
refers to activities outside of the organizational bounds (Keil, 2000; Maula, 2007). 
2.1.2.4 
Activities of External Corporate Venturing 
Strategic tools known as ECV activity allow companies to interact with outside businesses 
employing equity and non-equity relationships. Apart from a means of financial returns, ECV is a 
conscious method to support strategic learning, market positioning, and acceleration of 
innovation (Enkel et al., 2020; Maula et al., 2005). Within ECV modes, connect to business activities 
by interacting with startups. Please locate in Appendix A, a comprehensive summary of the 
corporate activities together with their correspondence to ECV models. 
CVC is among the most often used forms. Like traditional venture capital (VC), CVC consists of 
minority equity investments in outside businesses either through internal CVC units or dedicated 
or collected venture funds (Maula et al., 2005; Schildt et al., 2005; Maula, 2007). While letting the 
startup remain independent, these investments give companies early access to disruptive 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
18 
technologies, new business models, and fast-growing ventures. CVC offers strategic visibility and 
optionality, so it performs as a pipeline for following acquisitions. 
Venture alliances are non-equity alliances and joint ventures. Agreements like these allow 
companies access to markets, innovations, or capabilities that complement their own without 
having to commit substantial upfront financial resources (Maula, 2007). For example, alliances 
might improve a current product line or encourage research of related fields (Enkel et al., 2020). 
These approaches also support agility and learning, which helps companies to follow startups' 
journeys and observe changes in the sector. Particularly, joint ventures involve co-investment and 
shared control, usually pursued when mutual strategic interests align (Covin et al., 2007). 
Transformational agreement is shown by including mergers and acquisitions (M&A) as well as 
spin-offs (Maula, 2007). By means of M&A, companies acquire complete or majority control of 
external companies to quickly gain access to strategic assets, sources of revenue, and customers 
or consumers (Schildt et al., 2005). Acquisitions help corporations to combine skills, improve their 
market standing, and gain knowledge in transaction and post-merger integration. 
Apart from these formalized investment systems, many companies also use startup programs 
comprising incubators, accelerators, and innovation labs (Enkel et al., 2020). These are meant to 
investigate novel opportunities, increase digital skills, and encourage entrepreneurship. For rapid 
prototyping of new products, test solutions, and networking with outside innovators, for instance, 
innovation labs and accelerators offer safe settings. While innovation outposts integrate corporate 
teams inside startup hubs to search and interact with emerging trends, incubators help nurture 
early-stage startups matched with the strategic focus of the company. 
Startup programs, accelerators, incubators, innovation outposts, events, venture building, startup 
supplier programs, venture clienting, and coworking spaces (Demir et al., 2024) are other ways 
one can interact with startups mentioned. These modes vary in duration, goals, organization, 
equity participation, startup stage, risk tolerance, engagement length, and degree of integration 
(Enkel et al., 2020). 
2.1.3 What is a startup?  
Companies in the first stage of their life cycle, "start-up," are referred to as Corvello et al. 2021. 
They typically exhibit a high level of technological innovation and a strong drive for creativity. 
Other terms used include "new tech startups" and "high-tech entrepreneurial endeavors." A 
startup is an entrepreneurial endeavor looking for enough financial or resource support to launch 
(Corvello et al., 2021). Furthermore, Dizdarevic et al., 2023, describe them as small, inexperienced 
businesses. 
2.1.3.1 
The Processes of Startups 
Under high uncertainty, startups are newly founded businesses seeking to create scalable, state 
of art products, services, or business models (Blank et al., 2012, p. 16; Maula, 2007; Corvello et al., 
2021). Unlike conventional small enterprises, startups are distinguished by a strong emphasis on 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
 
19 
growth and development, technology, market disturbance, and the search for repeatable and 
scalable business strategies (Ries, 2011, p. 22). Startups sometimes run in fast-paced 
environments where survival and expansion depend on flexibility, experimentation, and customer 
validation. They have novel technologies, business models, agility, creative thinking, and often 
disruptive perspectives. Startups can create new products more cheaply and quickly than 
corporations due to their independence and often higher-risk approach (Oxford Research et al., 
2019). 
Investors help reduce the significant risks involved in startup operations by providing governance 
support and monitoring, which raises the prospects of startups for long-term survival and growth 
(Maula, 2007; Corporate Startup Collaboration Report, 2019). 
2.1.3.2 
Why do startups need partners 
Strategic collaboration is exceptionally essential when establishing the direction of development 
of a startup in the highly uncertain and resource-constrained settings that define businesses. 
Although startup operations center on innovation, agility, and experimentation, the quality of 
outside partnerships and investor involvement often determines whether a startup can 
successfully scale, validate its offering, and enter its target market (Rossi et al., 2022). 
Not only to access financial resources but also to acquire strategic capabilities they cannot yet 
develop internally, startups usually look for alliances and partnerships (Rossi et al., 2022, Basu et 
al., 2015). Investors, especially those with strategic objectives like CVC, bring far more than funds, 
as Gompers and Lerner (2001) and Di Lorenzo and Sabel (2023) argue. They provide credibility, 
industry knowledge, commercial network access, operational direction, most importantly, market 
validation (Briglet et al. 2021). These alliances let startups test and iterate their technologies in 
real-world environments, so accelerating the process of product-market fit and decreasing time-
to-impact. 
Startups, especially in early development stages, form alliances to do minimum viable product 
(MVP) launches with possible consumers, technical feasibility studies, and pilot programs (Corvello 
et al., 2021). Gathering feedback, improving main offers, and proving high value to the market are 
all dependent on this phase. Through increased access to manufacturing resources, distribution 
channels, and established supply chains, cooperation helps later on enable scalability (BCG, 2019). 
Working with corporates or business leaders can also be a strong reputational signal that will help 
startups win the confidence of first clients and investors. 
Reducing operational risks helps partners and investors as well. By means of strategic control, 
monitoring systems, and governance structures, they help to raise the long-term survival and 
expansion probability of a startup (Maula, 2007; Banka et al., 2024). Crucially, cooperation also 
helps startups to become early suppliers for larger firms, so they integrate themselves into the 
specific related ecosystem of their target market, rapidly increasing revenue generation and 
technology deployment. 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
 
20 
Not all relationships, meanwhile, are equally appropriate for every phase of a startup. The needs 
of a pre-seed company testing its basic hypothesis are very different from those of a growth-stage 
company seeking global scale. While later-stage ventures gain from partners with market access, 
infrastructure, and strategic alignment (Banka et al., 2024; Schuh et al., 2022), early-stage startups 
sometimes need hands-on partners with strong technological and product development capacity. 
Startups must thus assess the intentions and degree of commitment of possible partners in line 
with their internal capabilities, growth goals, and degree of integration readiness. 
Ultimately, strategic alliances, business relationships, or ecosystem partnerships are fundamental 
enablers of startup development. Startups that can properly negotiate such partnerships and 
match them to their changing needs are more likely to overcome early-stage development 
obstacles and move forward toward sustainable scalability. 
2.1.4 Strategic Motivations for CSC  
Studied in previous subchapters through academic research and ecosystem studies, collaboration 
between corporations and startups has evolved into a strategic focus within modern 
entrepreneurial ecosystems. Both industry analysts and scholars have underlined the increasingly 
important part that these partnerships play in promoting technological innovation, market 
growth, and entrepreneurial energy. These partnerships are now seen as intentional, strategic 
means for mutual value creation as well as reactions to changing market pressures (Corvello et al., 
2021). 
Often defined by hierarchical systems, set processes, and risk-averse cultures, companies search 
for agility, innovation, and close access to new technologies. On the other hand, startups, 
constrained by limited resources and legitimacy, seek corporations for capital, market access, 
technical infrastructure, and credibility (Brigl et al., 2021; Rigtering et al., 2021). 
Aimed at either mutual innovation, market expansion, or strategic renewal, corporate–startup 
cooperation is a formal or informal arrangement between an established company and an early-
stage venture. These partnerships can take the following several forms: co-development projects, 
procurement alliances, equity investments made through CVC, joint ventures, and acquisitions 
(Oxford Research et al., 2019). Often beginning with exploratory, non-equity interactions that may 
develop into deeper strategic relationships, such partnerships. 
Current innovation ecosystems now define themselves mostly on partnerships between 
enterprises and startups. These alliances are becoming more and more acknowledged as a 
strategic road to mutual value creation as well as a reaction to market pressures (Corvello et al., 
2021). Though their sizes, structures, and cultures vary, the two performers work together to 
overcome their respective constraints and highlight their respective outstanding qualities. By 
doing this, they accelerate innovation, open market possibilities, and create adaptive skills 
challenging to reach on their own (Brigl et al., 2021). 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
21 
For companies, working with startups presents a route to access outside innovation (Brigl et al., 
2021; Rigtering et al., 2021), a major goal in the fast-paced and uncertain corporate environment 
of today. Startups often run at the technological front, testing ideas and solutions in ways that 
conventional corporate structures might find difficult to support internally. Through alliances, 
corporations acquire innovative technologies, new business models, and niche knowledge 
domains (Oxford Research et al., 2019), which enables them to experiment outside their main 
operations with reduced risk and increased speed. 
Leveraging startups' agility and customer-centricity, these partnerships also help companies to 
reach new or related markets (Rigering et al., 2021). Faster paths to market, shorter innovation 
cycles, and chances to test products or services in real time help companies. These alliances allow 
business entities to temporarily insource outside capabilities by using startups as agile extensions 
of their R&D, product development, or service innovation efforts. More generally, these activities 
support organizational transformation (Rigering et al., 2021), so encouraging an entrepreneurial 
attitude among employees and regenerating rigid structures by exposing teams to novel 
approaches of thinking and operation. 
On the other hand, startups interact with business organizations to acquire resources, 
trustworthiness, and scale (Brigl et al., 2021). For early-stage businesses, longevity and expansion 
usually depend on access to capital, consumers, and knowledge in all those areas in which 
companies have considerable strength. Startups can access distribution networks, technical 
infrastructure, industry know-how, and client bases (Brigl et al., 2021; Corvello et al., 2021; 
Rigtering et al., 2021) that would otherwise take years to build by working with established 
companies. Apart from market access, startups frequently gain from reputation credibility 
(Corvello et al., 2021). Being connected to a widely recognized company indicates quality and 
minimizes perceived risk among investors and consumers. 
In addition, CSC can provide startups financial investment (Rigtering et al., 2021), either via direct 
procurement or equity-based instruments including CVC. Some startups even manage to turn their 
relationships into paying customers, hastening their journey to operational sustainability and 
revenue. Others deliberately use the cooperation to improve their value propositions, deepen 
their market knowledge, or scale their technologies faster by matching with the strategic 
objectives of the corporate partner, so aligning with the strategic priorities. 
These alliances can take the form of co-development pilots, procurement plans, direct 
investments, or acquisitions, and they often evolve in intensity over time (Oxford Research et al., 
2019). Crucially, partnership can start without equity involvement so that both sides may 
investigate mutual value before pledging to a closer, formalized relationship. The ability to arrange 
these interactions is what makes CSC a progressively more attractive instrument for strategic 
innovation and growth. 
Despite their differences, companies and startups are realizing to a growing degree how 
complementary the relationship is (Rigtering et al., 2021; Oxford Research et al., 2019 ). By using 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
 
22 
scale and agility to negotiate complexity and co-create the future of their sectors, together they 
create a dynamic engine for invention (Dizdarevic et al., 2023). 
Through their venture activities, companies sometimes pursue a combination of strategic and 
financial goals. Learning about new technologies, entering new markets, increasing demand for 
basic products, and generating acquisition options are among the strategic objectives (Maula, 
2007; Sykes, 1990). Although they are secondary, financial goals remain very important and help 
ensure discipline in investment choices (Keil et al., 2008; TechBBQ, 2024). As described in this 
chapter, the several reasons behind CSC are further combined in Appendix B. 
2.1.5 What is boundary spanner 
In collaborative environments, boundary spanners are becoming increasingly essential individuals 
in today's interconnected organizational landscape. As agents bridging organizational, cognitive, 
and cultural gaps between corporates and startups, boundary spanners are vital and multifarious 
in CSC. Their purpose is not only managerial but also highly proactive, facilitating alignment, 
building trust, and dynamic knowledge sharing across institutional borders. In the framework of 
CSC, this review summarizes concepts from the given literature to define the boundary spanner, 
outline their multiple roles and responsibilities, and clarify their critical relevance.  
A boundary spanner is usually defined as an individual who works at the outer edge or boundary 
of an organization and connects the internal organization with outside components (Keszey, 2018; 
Zhao et al., 2013). Boundary spanners act as interfaces between a unit and its surrounding 
environment, linking groups of people divided by geography, occupation, hierarchy, or function 
(Champenois et al., 2017; Keszey, 2018). They also represent people who interact with others 
outside their group (Keszey, 2018). Others characterize them as "persons who operate at the 
periphery or boundary of an organization... relating the organization with elements outside it" 
(Jesiek et al., 2018). In collaborative settings, boundary spanners might have formal, designated 
job roles or responsibilities (Williams, 2002; Williams, 2013). Alternatively, they might function in 
emerging and informal capacities as "boundary spanners-in-practice" (Williams, 2013; Jesiek et al., 
2018). Boundary spanners are often framed as the link between the organization and the external 
environment. 
2.1.5.1 
Roles of a Boundary Spanner 
Particularly in complex and cross-organizational environments like CSC, boundary spanners are 
essential and extensive in helping to enable working together. Their ability to span and connect 
several social, cultural, and organizational areas defines their fundamental value. Scholars have 
labeled these people using a range of names, each stressing different roles they carry in 
cooperative ecosystems. The section that follows examines the main roles usually ascribed to 
boundary spanners and emphasizes how they help to enable mutual understanding, knowledge 
transfer, and creativity across organizational boundaries (Williams, 2002, Williams 2013). 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
 
23 
The linking pin is one of the most often used analogies to explain boundary spanners (Jesiek et al., 
2018). In this capacity, they are the vital link between the internal and external environments of 
the company. Boundary spanners help translate goals, align expectations, and guarantee strategic 
coherence whether they link departments inside a company or a startup to a more established 
corporate entity. This framing puts them as the organizational sensors, picking up external signals 
and forwarding them inside to improve responsiveness and adaptability. 
Another generally accepted accountability is the broker. As middlemen, boundary spanners help 
to enable the flow of knowledge, trust, and resources between otherwise isolated people or 
groups (Champenois et al. 2017). This is especially important in environments when silos of 
organizational units or innovation ecosystems coexist. Leveraging social network theory, brokers 
occupy "structural holes" by connecting separate actor clusters who would otherwise remain 
unconnected (Swartz et al., 2024, Jesiek et al., 2018). Their brokerage function is thus crucial for 
promoting cross-pollination of ideas and knowledge recombining. 
Boundary spanners control the information flow across the organizational boundary, usually 
filtering and translating data to guarantee relevance and coherence in the capacity of gatekeeper. 
They have a selective role in determining what knowledge comes in or leaves the company 
(Champenois et al. 2017, Williams, 2017). This function becomes especially important in 
innovative settings since gatekeepers decide which outside ideas are feasible for integration and 
which internal projects should be presented to outside target groups.   
Boundary spanners are relational nodes as connectors, allow interactions among distributed 
stakeholders (Jesiek et al., 2018). Like routers or conduits in information networks, they help data, 
energy, and ideas to be transmitted. In long-term strategic alliances where continuity and trust 
are prerequisites, this role emphasizes their ability to maintain engagement across time and space, 
so rendering them indispensable. 
Reticulist emphasizes the networking abilities and awareness of interdependencies both inside 
and across systems of the boundary spanner. They are quite good in creating and preserving 
intricate networks of personal interactions (Williams, 2002, Williams, 2017). Their strength resides 
in their capacity to foster trust, interpret unofficial signals, and coordinate activities across several 
interests, so facilitating better cooperation and the unofficial conflict resolution. 
Boundary spanners, acting as a catalyst, find gaps, organize many stakeholders, and encourage 
alignment around common goals so sparking change (Williams, 2017). Especially in hybrid 
companies or innovation ecosystems, they are crucial in starting structural changes, completing in 
institutional gaps, and accelerating idea execution. Their work often combines several inputs and 
creates actionable paths for co-creation (Champenois et al. 2017). 
Serving as interpreters, these people are skilled in negotiating several organizational and cultural 
languages (Williams, 2017). They translate frames of reference so that partners may grasp one 
another's goals, constraints, and motivations. In cross-cultural or cross-sectoral projects, this 
communicative purpose is vital in avoiding misalignment and promoting mutual respect. Boundary 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
 
24 
spanners oversee interdependent tasks and processes spanning organizational or national 
boundaries in their coordinator capacity. Particularly in multicultural teams, this function is crucial 
for clearing misunderstandings, harmonizing work styles, and making sure that cooperation stays 
free from conflict and productive (Champenois et al. 2017, Williams, 2017). Their coordinating 
action guarantees operational integration while recognizing local or functional diversities. 
Lastly, by encouraging knowledge sharing, learning, and relationship building (Kanwal et al., 2019), 
boundary spanners sometimes act as enablers of collaboration. Though behind-the-scenes, they 
are essential in promoting trust, maintaining dedication, and offering consistency across time. Key 
players in maintaining innovation-driven alliances, their enabling contribution spans both strategic 
alignment and operational execution (Williams, 2017, Dizdarevic et al., 2023). 
2.1.5.2 
Boundary spanner importance in CSC 
Boundary spanners are especially important in the continually evolving and at times uncertain 
ground of CSC. Such individuals help to bridge institutional, operational, and cultural gaps, thus 
facilitating effective cooperation between large corporations and agile startups. Effective 
boundary spanners are not only advantageous but also usually necessary given the structural and 
strategic asymmetries that define such alliances (Banka et al., 2024; Dizdarevic et al., 2023). 
Their contribution mostly stems from their ability to lower information asymmetry between the 
two parties and enable mutual learning (Dizdarevic et al., 2023). While corporations may 
encounter it difficult to grasp the innovation potential and operating logic of startups, startups 
sometimes lack visibility into the internal priorities and processes of large enterprises. Boundary 
spanners close this discrepancy. Whether formally assigned or informally developing, people like 
corporate innovation managers or business developers often enter this position to match 
expectations and guarantee convergence of interests all through the cooperation process 
(Dizdarevic et al., 2023). 
Boundary spanners additionally function as knowledge brokers, converting outside 
experimentation into insights and opportunities suitable for corporate internal needs. In 
CVC settings, where strategic fit and integration with central business units can define the success 
or failure of a partnership, this function is especially essential. CV entities themselves are able to 
function as institutionalized boundary spanners. Through stages including opportunity research, 
selection, due diligence, and post-transaction integration, these units actively link startups to 
related internal departments, so guiding them through the sometimes complex and bureaucratic 
systems of corporate organizations (Dizdarevic et al., 2023). 
Crucially, boundary spanners help overcome a number of structural and relational constraints 
often mentioned in the research. These comprise the challenges of finding suitable startup 
partners, internal challenges to outside innovation, cultural misalignment, rigid organizational 
hierarchies, and lack of top management support (Banka et al., 2024; Dizdarevic et al., 2023). 
Boundary spanners minimize these difficulties and encourage alignment by working across 
organizational levels and acting as constant champions of cooperation. 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
 
25 
Navigating the cultural and philosophical differences between corporations and startups requires 
particular attention to their capacity to build trust and create meaningful relationships (Williams, 
2002; Williams, 2017; Banka et al., 2024). While startups provide agility, adaptability, and 
experimentation top priority, corporations often run with longer time frames, structured 
processes, and a strong focus on risk reduction. Boundary spanners reach an agreement with great 
communicative and relational strength. 
Moreover, their translational work, both linguistically and culturally, is essential. By using broad 
definitions of reference, they can comprehend and adjust communications among various actors, 
thereby outlining intentions, controlling expectations, and promoting a shared understanding of 
value and progress (Banka et al., 2024; Williams, 2017; Champenois et al., 2017; Zhao et al., 2013). 
This process involves aligning strategic logics, operational rhythms, and creative mindsets. This 
translation is about aligning strategic logics, operational rhythms, and creation mindsets. 
Boundary spanners help CSC grow by making the unfamiliar more accessible, the complex more 
navigable, and the different become more closely connected. Trust, knowledge, and shared 
commitment underpin their presence, adding not only operational value but also strategic 
coherence to cooperative efforts. The function of boundary spanners becomes central in ensuring 
that these partnerships transcend transactional exchanges and lead to long-term strategic impact. 
Whether in formal or informal roles, boundary spanners are especially the complex connections. 
From information management and coordination to relationship building and impact on results, 
they carry out vital tasks. Overcoming the major obstacles that might affect effective CSC requires 
their capacity to bridge many points of view, negotiate difficult structures, and promote 
understanding. Boundary spanners are essential enablers of innovation and successful 
cooperative outcomes by helping communication, knowledge transfer, and relationship building 
between parties with perhaps conflicting objectives and cultures. 
2.2 
Theoretical Framework  
Many of these relationships, lack scale or long-term value even if they are increasingly relevant. 
Academic literature and practitioner accounts have as their recurring theme that the difficulty is 
not the lack of innovative thinking per se but rather the challenge in converting cross-
organizational engagement into managed. Often resulting from misalignment across several 
phases of the collaboration journey as well as from organizational asymmetries in power, pace, 
culture, and governance (Chappert et al., 2023; Grichnik et al., 2022; Rigtering et al., 2021). The 
key theoretical and methodological foundations of the initial materials applied to guide the debate 
on CV and CSC are presented in this chapter. Every source looks at several aspects of this 
phenomena using different theoretical lenses and research methods. 
This thesis presents a theoretical framework integrating a structured five-phase process of CSC 
comprising Problem Framing, Scouting and Evaluation, Pilot Execution, Integration, and Scaling, 
with the longitudinal enabling role of the boundary spanner, therefore addressing this gap. 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
 
26 
Theoretical insights from Corporate Venturing Navigator (CVN) (Grichnik et al., 2022), resource 
dependence and asymmetry theory (Chappert et al., 2023), CE (Rigering et al., 2021), and 
empirical observations taken from leading corporate innovation practices including those at 
Maersk Growth, NOVO Holdings, and Combient Foundry inform this integrative model (See 
Appendix D, E, F and H). 
The recognition that collaboration is not only a set of discrete stages but a dynamic, journey 
dependent process that demands relationship-based continuity, governance navigation, and 
knowledge trading throughout (Dizdarevic et al., 2023; Williams, 2017) drives the decision to 
embed the boundary spanner across phases of the model. Therefore, the boundary spanner is 
perceived not as a phase-specific actor but rather as an enabler whose competencies change in 
scope and relevance as the partnership evolves.   
2.2.1 Theoretical Background 
The CVN framework  is conceptualized in the paper "Corporate Venturing Navigator: Conceptual 
development". This approach is used to create evidence that advance knowledge by tackling 
practical issues. The evolution of the CVN is anchored in evidence-based conclusions derived from 
both scholarly publications and rational knowledge.    
The second study "Corporation–start-up collaboration: how can the tensions stemming from 
asymmetries be managed?" examines at the strategic and managerial challenges inherent in CSC 
by the analytical view of asymmetries and tensions. Its theoretical model combines ideas from the 
CSC literature with allied disciplines including open innovation and asymmetrical partnerships. The 
primary illustration is that different tensions resulting from asymmetries between companies and 
startups indicate for efficient leadership to enable the successful evolution of innovations 
(Chappert et al., 2023). 
Third, with a focus toward individual-level corporate (Rigering et al., 2021) research article "The 
Effect of Corporate — Start Up Collaborations on Corporate Entrepreneurship" searches at how 
CSC influence CE and strategic renewal. It combines institutional theory with CE literature insights 
to better understand how the larger interpersonal, including organizational structures, practices, 
routines, and cultures, may either enable or limit the capacity of corporate individuals to exercise 
transformational agency. Transformational agency is the deliberate activities of people to modify 
current policies and practices (Rigtering et al., 2021).

2.2.3 The Five-Phase Framework 
2.2.3.1 
Problem framing  
A successful corporate-startup relationship begins long before a contract is signed, or a pilot is 
started. It begins with strategic clarity, sometimes best described as problem framing. By defining 
a suitable, high-priority challenge internally in the corporate environment, a startup is maybe 
suitable for resolution, as this phase sets the groundwork for the CSC process. 
Academically, this stage is rather similar to the "Profiling" phase described in the CVN framework 
(Grichnik et al., 2022). Whether it seeks strategic renewal, cultural transformation, innovation 
acceleration, or financial returns, the company is not only spotting a problem but also defining the 
ideal venturing profile here. The CVN method emphasizes strategic intent: what is the intended 
cooperation to achieve? Which players have to get involved? Which tools are needed? And what 
advancement could define success? These guiding questions help to align internal expectations 
and clarify the justification for interacting with a startup. 
Reducing future tension, foremost what Chappert et al. (2023) define as expectation asymmetry, 
depends also on early alignment. Startups may misunderstand the ultimate objective or expected 
timeline of a partnership when challenges are only partially aligned with corporate strategy or 
have unclear definitions, so eliminating trust. Establishing a shared vision helps to define clear 
objectives from the start, including what makes a successful pilot and what the next actions would 
be should success prove to be the result. Furthermore, clarity at this level facilitates later 
integration discussion since the expected company result of the solution has already been decided 
upon. 
This phase is strategically crucial as it protects against disagreements and downstream 
inefficiencies. As Grichnik et al. (2022) underline, the success of the CV program depends critically 
on a clear knowledge of its intended objective and expected results. Unclear strategic goals or 
unrealistic expectations from top management can cause internal conflicts and reduce project 
momentum. Chappert et al. (2023) similarly advise allocating enough time and money to assist 
this early-stage framing process since this helps to avoid cooperation resistance and supports 
organized decision-making. 
To effectively operationalize the problem framing phase, s set of deliberate activates must be 
undertaken and defined with corresponded BU's and main decision-making team. Second, the 
company has to specify the intended area of business progress: either entirely new business 
development or core business enhancement with regard to adjacent business exploration. Third, 
stakeholder mapping should be carried out to find and involve relevant corporate actors, 
especially functional representatives and internal sponsors whose support will be 
fundamental during the partnership time span. Fourth, early conversations on funding needs and 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
 
29 
budget availability help to guarantee that the suggested interaction is both practical and given top 
priority. To offer a foundation for assessment, strategic goals must also be precisely expressed, 
categorized, and internally validated. 
2.2.3.2 
Scouting and evaluation 
Once a strategic intent and a validated internal need are clearly expressed, startup scouting is the 
next stage in CSC process. This phase consists in systematically choosing startup partners that fit 
the operational process, strategic position, and technological priorities of the company. While 
simultaneously building trust, relevance, and long-term compatibility with startup companies, a 
well-organized scouting and evaluation program helps corporates source the novel concepts.   
This process is partially addressed in the Operating phase, involving with sourcing and transaction 
management, and further supported in the Designing phase, where the portfolio logic and 
ecosystem linkages are defined (Grichnik et al., 2022). These two CVN dimensions taken jointly 
show a methodical path for both active and passive identification of startup prospects as well as 
for building a pipeline of investable or well matched partner - startups in line with corporate 
strategic direction. 
The significance of this phase is found in its effect on success downstream. Choosing a startup that 
defies corporate internal logic, or time could result in wasted resources, negative reputation, and 
failed next collaboration stages. Described internal teams to startups with different ideas and 
logics can be beneficial, as Rigtering et al. (2021) contend, since it stimulates involvement and 
education of stakeholders. 
This phase also often reveals what Chappert et al. (2023) define as test-driven against goal-driven 
sourcing tensions. Companies might want low-risk, test-driven scouting and view startups as 
potential experiment vendor partners. On the other hand, startups frequently seek goal-oriented 
involvement with an approach toward strategic integration or co-scaling. The outcome is a power 
asymmetry whereby one side searches for commitment and clarity while one side is looking for 
speed and flexibility.  
Control of this tension requires formal systems. Companies can set rigid, consistent selection 
criteria ahead of time and apply filtering systems that provide quality above quantity of top priority 
startup. Here, the CVN Designing phase provides insightful direction since it motivates corporates 
to define their portfolio logic, including number of ventures, industry verticals, target maturity 
levels, and the type of instruments. Such clarity guarantees that chosen startups demonstrate both 
aspiration and feasibility as well as supports internal decision-making (Grichnik et al., 2022). 
Regarding main activities, this phase is distinguished by several elements. First, the portfolio 
strategy must be clearly stated: for what strategic goals and what kind of ventures the company is 
looking for? Corporates then have to map applicable ecosystems and networks, pointing up 
accelerators, VCs, academic institutions, and other players able to supply the access to 
entrepreneurs. 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
 
30 
The sourcing process itself can be organized in several ways: active scouting depending on a 
technological thesis, thematic challenges, or BU needs; or passive reception through open calls 
and partner channels. Businesses that engage also must decide which evaluation system they want 
internal assessments using venture teams or outside validation by accelerator partners or domain 
experts. Particularly in dangerous or unfamiliar fields, Chappert et al. (2023) underline that 
including third-party validators can bring neutrality and increase selection objectivity. 
Corporates require to make sure the organizational contact point is well-defined. Finding a suitable 
internal champion is a challenge for startups, thus misalignment here causes delays and reduced 
results. 
Furthermore, this is exactly where the function of the boundary spanner becomes crucial since it 
guarantees continuity, keeps alignment across stakeholders, and serves as the clearly defined 
point of contact guiding the startup through corporate structures. Under the framework, this 
function is seen as starting from the very beginning of the cooperation process and indicating its 
basic relevance all through the scouting and beyond. 
Additionally, this phase also marks the beginning of reflexivity in CSC actors. Startups bring 
institutional conflicts into corporate environments, as Rigtering et al. (2021) highlight, which forces 
internal stakeholders to reevaluate their practices, biases, and innovation strategies. 
2.2.3.3 
Pilot execution 
Following the scouting and evaluation phase, the partnership moves to the pilot execution phase 
after the scouting and selection of a strategically aligned startup. At this stage, real-world 
conditions are used for proof-of-concept (PoC) and pilot projects meant to test functional fit as 
well as technological viability. This phase is a relational turning point where the intentions of both 
sides are realized as well as an experimentation phase.   Generating value and laying the 
foundation for possible integration and scaling depend on this phase being executed under solid 
project governance, explicit performance measures, and trust-building mechanisms. 
Richnik et al., (2022) Operating phase of the CVN framework directly corresponds with this phase. 
The framework separates this as a stage of active collaboration in which businesses and ventures 
have to cooperate closely to validate the solution of the venture and get early insights. This is 
where actual partnership takes place as Grichnik et al. (2022) contend, and where the strategic 
promise of the corporate venturing program begins to materialize. 
Another phase of pilot execution where many tensions surface also where agile versus anchored 
tensions emerge due to differing operational rhythms, startups operate with speed and 
informality, while corporates require governance, approval layers, and resource validation.  Often 
the challenge appears between vision and execution results from insufficient communication 
between execution teams and execution teams. 
Managing these tensions requires for both official and unofficial systems. Formal systems include 
defining intellectual property (IP) protection systems, creating CSC-specific agreements (such as 
NDAs), and distributing shared digital platforms for data exchange. Chappert et al. (2023), for 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
 
31 
instance, advise flexible but regulated data-sharing platforms that preserve control while allowing 
sharing.   Informally, individuals ties, open dialogue, and an internal champion and trust-building 
tools are essential in this phase.  Moreover, when built as a "third space", in dedicated physical or 
virtual environments covers project-specific timelines, and access to decision-makers—that helps 
to foster autonomy and reduces cultural friction (Chappert et al., 2023). Grichnik et al. (2022) claim 
that effective implementation here often determines whether a project will be quietly abandoned 
or advance toward integration. 
Managing the venture portfolio and project pipeline, actively working with BU's technical experts, 
boundary spanner, setting and tracking KPIs aligned with initial strategic goals, and organizing 
knowledge flows define this phase. Companies may also provide resources including marketing 
channels, distribution network access, or board observation rights (Grichnik et al., 2022). 
Operationally, internal alignment should be guaranteed over technical departments and 
executive management. Providing conflict resolution methods, feedback loops, and project 
management tools improves the possibility of a good pilot result. 
Most importantly, during this phase boundary spanners are significant asset in collaboration.   
Boundary spanners, as already mentioned in the previous phases, translate technical 
requirements, enable knowledge sharing, and keep alignment between startup agility and 
corporate formality so bridging the cultural and operational divide. This function is shown in the 
visual framework this work developed as an ongoing enabler. 
2.2.3.4 
Integration  
One of the most complicated turning points in a CSC is the one from experimentation to 
integration. The real organizational change starts in the integration phase, while the pilot phase 
tests the feasibility of a startup's solution. This phase marks a change from an exploratory active 
environment to one controlled by corporate system guidelines.   Without diligent control, shifted 
expectations, procedural obstacles, or lack of long-term commitment may cause the value 
developed during experimentation to be lost in translation. 
This phase corresponds the Industrialization phase described in the study on CSC by Chappert et 
al. (2023) as well as the Harvesting phase in the CVN model (Grichnik et al., 2022). The main 
emphasis moves from testing potential to realizing value, it is requiring organizational alignment, 
secure governance, and focused initiatives to overcome structural inertia. It is also an area where 
challenges sometimes get escalated, especially those involving knowledge management, 
timelines, and structures. 
This phase is important since it acts as the link between exploration and exploitation. It is the point 
at which the cooperation either slows down or becomes the primary subject of activities. Moving 
from CSC-specific agreements to general corporate contracts can cause delay or breakdown of 
implementation if not expected early, as Chappert et al. (2023) find.  
Formal systems govern this phase, it usually entering the process to validate the project in line 
with corporate standards are legal, procurement, and IT departments. Standard compliance 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
 
32 
procedures replace the flexibility of the pilot stage, stricter IP protection clauses are included. This 
is the point where the startup must adjust to institutional routines. It is generally without losing 
the unique value proposition that first attracted their solution. The Harvesting phase of the CVN 
promotes the methodical assessment of pilot results and a decision on whether to move on with 
investments or additional scaling (Grichnik et al., 2022). 
Still, unofficial systems are necessary. Internal connectors are still tremendously  important since 
they keep momentum going, negotiate organizational compromises, and highlight the startup 
solution inside the company.  
The main tasks in this stage start with a methodical assessment of pilot data. These cover official 
KPIs, feedback from stakeholders, cost-benefit investigations, IT validations and cyber security 
approaches. Standardizing agreements which including data access and IP agreements becomes 
essential to safeguard both sides during the process of implementation. At this point, strategic 
relationships inside the ecosystem could also be used to assist with scaling or localization (Grichnik 
et al., 2022). Standardized handover templates, business case evaluation systems, and governance 
policies developed by corporate venturing teams could help business units be guided during 
implementation. 
Throughout this phase, boundary spanners are once the key to continues collaboration. They help 
both sides keep perspective and change with the times by acting as translators and linkers 
between corporate logic and startup ambitions.   This function is seen in the framework already 
as constant and especially active during periods of transition such as this transformational phase. 
2.2.3.5 
Scaling 
Scaling, the last stage of CSC, is extending a validated solution outside its initial pilot 
implementation into the corporate structure. By now the startup's innovation moves from a 
centralized investigation to a solution implemented across departments, markets, or business 
divisions. The objectives are realizing long-term strategic value, embedding change, and producing 
observable impact rather than experimentation. 
This phase corresponds to the Harvesting phase of CVN (Grichnik et al., 2022) and the Deployment 
phase defined in the "Corporate–start-up collaboration" framework (Chappert et al., 2023). 
Achieving broad implementation, tracking KPIs, implementing constant feedback loops and 
ensuring the initiative significantly improves the strategic performance of the parent company 
take front stage. Importantly, this is not only a functional exercise but also a turning point where 
strategic renewal is feasible. 
Scaling effort to be successful, the company gains not only a new tool, service, or product but also 
the possibility to change internal processes, enhance agility, and improve competitiveness in 
market presence. This is closely related to the idea of strategic renewal, which is the ability of a 
company to adapt by means of intentional transformation motivated by entrepreneurial activity 
(Rigtering et al., 2021). 
==PAGE:33==
 
 
 
33 
Supporting scaling mostly depends on formal mechanisms. These comprise embedding the startup 
solution into operational systems, in line with IT infrastructures, departmental integration of 
processes, and strong KPI-based performance monitoring.  The CVN Cockpit tool also tracks the 
performance of scaling initiatives against portfolio-level strategic mandates, in order supporting 
this phase (Grichnik et al., 2022). 
Still, maintaining momentum depends equally on informal systems. The success of complete 
adoption sometimes depends on the human relationships developed in previous phases. 
Champion inside the company help negotiate internal complexity, expand the reach of the solution 
and customization of the solution while responding to feedback findings, and ingrain it into the 
corporate culture. Fundamentally remains trust, openness, and alignment of interests. Weak 
relational infrastructure makes even strong technological solutions difficult to acquire traction. 
Expanding the rollout of the solution, guaranteeing operational readiness across business units, 
onboarding new internal users, customizing the solution to fit various functional contexts, and so 
enabling interdepartmental cooperation to constitute the main activities in this phase. While the 
venturing team tracks performance and records knowledge for next projects, constant knowledge 
sharing and feedback loops help iteration and optimization. 
2.2.4 Conceptual Framework  
This thesis presents a dual-dimensional conceptual framework to critically investigate how 
boundary spanners enable cultural alignment and integration across several phases of CSC. Using 
both processual and relational viewpoints, this integrated framework evaluates the enabling 
factors behind success of inter-organizational innovation. 
Comprising Sourcing, Problem Framing, Pilot Execution, Integration, and Scaling, the framework 
adopts a five-phase process structure reflecting the temporal evolution of collaboration on the Y-

this sequence and extend to fit pragmatic complexity in how CSCs develop.   From opportunity 
identification and experimental validation to formal adoption and strategic institutionalization, 
every phase catches different organizing goals and coordination challenges. 
Combing this process logic, the X-axis presents four thematic competency categories derived from 
boundary spanning literature. Organizational navigation captures the ability of boundary spanners 
to negotiate corporate hierarchies, secure internal buy-in, and use structural embeddedness 
(Dizdarevic et al., 2023).Strategic bridging covers abilities in translating between domains, aligning 
expectations, and managing cooperative interdependencies (Williams, 2017; Champenois et al., 
2017).Building trust, controlling cultural differences, and maintaining relational capital across 
organizational boundaries (Williams, 2002; Banka et al., 2024) under relationship and cultural 
competency seeks for reflecting the capacity to adapt under uncertainty, initiate concepts and 
broker innovation in dynamic partnership environments, entrepreneurial flexibility shows how 
capable they could be in the environment (Jesiek et al., 2018; Swartz et al., 2024).

3.1 
Research philosophy  
Guiding the strategy, approach, and assessment of research requires a clear understanding of 
fundamental philosophical assumptions. This study, which investigates the dynamics of CSC, 
adopts a layered philosophical approach that reflects the complex and socially embedded nature 
of the topic. A coherent philosophical stance is essential in organizational research, where 
phenomena are situated, multifaceted, and mediated by social interactions. To ensure 
methodological alignment and interpretive validity, this study combines critical realism, 
interpretivism, and pragmatism in a blended philosophical position. 
Using both primary and secondary data sources, the study plan is a qualitative, mixed-method 
design. This method enables the study of CSC from several angles, so it guarantees analytical depth 
and contextual richness. With its focus on pragmatic relevance and methodological adaptability, 
pragmatism's philosophical basis directs the general process (Johnson et al., 2007). 
From an ontological standpoint, the research is rooted in critical realism, which holds that reality 
exists independently of our knowledge but notes that access to it is mediated through social, 
cultural, and linguistic interpretations (Ryan, 2018). Within the framework of CSC, this means that 
objective structures exist, but their meanings and effects rely on the interpretive frameworks of 
individual participants, including corporate managers, startup founders, boundary spanners, and 
ecosystem actors. 
Since it allows researchers to acknowledge underlying structures while also realizing that these 
structures are experienced and interpreted differently depending on one's positions and context, 
critical realism is especially suitable for studying activities deep in power-structured environments 
(Howell, 2013). Corporate and startup players working together must negotiate institutional 
asymmetries, resource differences, and various motives. Strategic and market logics, along with 
broader socio-cultural factors such as organizational culture, institutional power, and 
communication techniques, help define these dynamics. 
Combining constructivist ontological assumptions, the present study also observes that ongoing 
interaction, negotiation, and mutual adaptation help to co-create the relational and strategic 
meanings of cooperation. Actors' sense-making processes, organizational histories, and 
embeddedness in the innovation ecosystem dynamically shape these meanings. Interpretivist 
==PAGE:36==
 
 
 
36 
ontology, as Ryan (2018) notes, sees knowledge and meaning as created rather than discovered 
emerging from lived experience and social interaction. 
Combining these ontological stances helps the research remain sensitive to both the underlying 
causal mechanisms and the contextual construction of meaning, enabling a closer study of how 
cooperative partnerships are generated, maintained, and experienced. 
From an epistemological perspective, this study highlights what functions in real-world settings 
and uses pragmatism, which values knowledge for its practical consequences. Pragmatism 
supports methodological pluralism to handle difficult research issues instead of the binary 
between positivist and interpretivist traditions (Ryan, 2018). 
This perspective supports the application of several techniques, including academic literature 
review, desk research, and ecosystem interviews. Every approach adds a unique layer of 
knowledge: Desk research reveals structural and institutional context; literature grounds the study 
in theoretical traditions; and interviews provide insights into the subjective, lived experiences of 
ecosystem players. 
Especially important in the study of interview data is the interpretivist component. Interpretation, 
as Ryan (2018) underlines, emphasizes the part context, language, and subjectivity play in helping 
us to understand events through the meanings people assign to them. This fits into the research's 
goal to find how cooperation is seen, carried out, and negotiated among several stakeholder 
groups. 
3.2 
Rationale for mixed - method of qualitative approach 
CSC combine informal processes with formal structures to create complex interactions.   This 
investigation utilizes a mixed qualitative approach combining desk research with primary data 
gathered via ecosystem interviews in order to capture this complexity. This study uses a mixed 
qualitative methodology, combining primary data from in-depth interviews carried out within the 
entrepreneurial and corporate ecosystem with desk research—including secondary data and 
academic literature. Recent academic readings have expanded the paradigm to include 
methodological pluralism within the qualitative domain, provided the mixing occurs across 
philosophical traditions, data sources, and analytical strategies. 
Mixed methods research is especially suited to handle complicated interpersonal situations where 
perspective can adequately reflect every dimension of reality (Johnson et al., 2007). CSC consists 
in interactions at multiple tiers, ranging from daily communication and relational trust to strategic 
alignment and governance systems. Embedded in more general innovation ecosystems, these 
interactions are shaped by different interests, expectations, and temporalities. A mixed qualitative 
approach allows the study to examine these dynamics holistically. While interviews give light on 
micro-level practices, such how trust is built, decisions are made, and conflicts are negotiated, 
desk research provides specifics about macro-level industry structures, investment rationales, and 
cooperation models. 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
 
37 
Combining primary qualitative data with literature review deepens the contextual awareness of 
the research. Desk research finds trends or conflicts in corporate-startup cooperation and helps 
place the topic within accepted theoretical debates. Interview data enhances this strategy by 
showing how practitioners in real-world environments understand and experience those trends. 
Ryan (2018) claims that in qualitative research, such an interpretivist approach is indispensable 
since it favors the interpretations given by individuals instead of looking for objective predictions. 
Furthermore, Johnson et al. (2007) contend that the ability of mixed methods to combine 
broadness with depth presents one of the main justifications for them. The paper creates a 
multidimensional picture of cooperative innovation processes by combining knowledge from field-
based interviews and scholarly literature. This integration makes not only theoretical progress 
possible but also pragmatic relevance. This method appeals to interpretivist logic since it respects 
the viewpoints and interpretations of the involved people. Simultaneously, it helps theory to grow 
by means of a comparison and synthesis of empirical observations with current conceptual 
frameworks. 
A fundamental ability of mixed qualitative research is triangulation which is using several sources 
to support conclusions. This study increases the validity and credibility of its conclusions by cross-
referencing first-hand interviews, secondary data, and knowledge from academic publications.   
Different sources are seen as useful inputs for critical thought rather than as problems.   
Triangulation so guarantees a more solid and balanced interpretation, reveals neglected elements, 
and strengthens analytical depth (Johnson et al., 2007).  This corresponds with the pragmatic and 
interpretive logic that respects both convergence and divergence in meaning-making.

3.5 
Methodical limitations 
Although the selected approach gave a logical and perceptive basis for investigating CSC, several 
constraints surfaced during the research process that demand serious thought. 
One obvious restriction relates to geographic and cultural bias. Accessibility and proximity shaped 
the first choice of participants, mostly depending on professional and academic networks mostly 
anchored in Denmark, Germany, and Netherlands. Given most interviewees reflected experiences 
established in European innovation ecosystems, this geographical concentration may have limited 
==PAGE:42==
 
 
 
42 
the variety of points of view. Their opinions were thus shaped by context-dependent institutional 
logics, so perhaps restricting the general applicability of results to different areas.  
Time-related restrictions also arose in the study, so limiting the capacity to iteratively improve and 
combine the results of desk research with the knowledge acquired from primary interviews. 
Deeper validation of emergent themes and a richer synthesis would have been possible with a 
broader analytical link between several data sources. 
Furthermore, imposing some limitations were the composition and sample size. Though the study 
included startup founders as well as business leaders, the total number of participants stayed 
modest. Including more a wider spectrum of stakeholders would have improved the depth and 
representativeness of the study. Including longitudinal data also could have provided a more 
dynamic picture of how collaborative effort changes with time. 
Access to sensitive operational details was restricted by a general lack of transparency among 
corporate and startup participants. Although many recognized the importance of scholarly 
research, they were hesitant to provide details that might expose their organizational background 
or current projects. This made it more difficult to compile thorough, multi-actor perspectives in a 
single collaboration. 
Despite these constraints, the methodological decisions taken in this study were precisely in line 
with the goals of the study and driven by a clear philosophical justification. Still, appreciating these 
constraints helps one to properly understand the results and direct next research in this 
developing field. 
Combining primary interviews with secondary sources to build an extensive, multi-layered 
empirical basis, this chapter has given a thorough overview of the data collecting strategy

methodology allowed the study to capture complex insights into the dynamics of corporate–
startup interaction. Developed throughout this chapter, the organized thematic tables and graphic 
representations become both analytical tools guiding the interpretation of results and synthesized 
outputs of the data collecting process.

4.2.1 Collaboration Framework 
The efficiency and results of CSC  activities are much influenced by the design and formalizing of 
the collaboration framework. Most interviewees cited organized models, such as phased

Collaboration models

Unless they are guided by a clearly expressed internal need and supported by an active coordinator, 
projects will lose steam mid-way, as Interviewee CP07 noted, even if the technology is exciting. This 
reflects earlier research on strategic intent in corporate venturing (Grichnik et al., 2022), which 
stresses the need of internal readiness and commitment.

5. Discussion 
The key findings of the previous section are placed in perspective and interpreted in the following 
discussion chapter. It investigates how the performance of boundary spanners affects the success 
of CSC over the integration and pilot stages. 
5.1 
Summary of the findings  
Establishing on the qualitative insights from the last chapter, this section collects the empirical 
data to answer the main research question. By means of this research, the chapter shows how 
boundary spanners enable smoother transitions between the pilot execution and post-pilot 
integration phases in CSC, highlighting their important impact on cultural alignment, stakeholder 
coordination, and strategic continuity. By means of an analytical chapter evaluating the proposed 
hypotheses against the observed empirical patterns, the theoretical contribution of the study is 
strengthened as well. 
5.2 
Interpretation of Findings 
Research Question: In what ways might boundary spanners support organizational integration 
and cultural alignment throughout the pilot implementation and post-pilot CSC? 
 
The results confirm that negotiating between the challenges of corporate governance and startup 
agility depends much on boundary spanners. They ensure strategic alignment, mediate language 
and culture, and act as relational brokers decoding expectations during the pilot execution. Their 
role changes in the post-pilot integration phase to become internal champions and translators 
guiding adoption through internal systems, so reducing conflict, and preserving relational 
continuity. 
 
First sub-question: In what ways might boundary spanners help to reduce cultural conflict and 
match expectations during the pilot  phase of cooperation? 
 
The first hypothesis, which holds that boundary spanners minimize the negative consequences of 
cultural distance during the pilot phase, is rather supported by the data. Their contribution is in 
==PAGE:52==
 
 
 
52 
helping corporate stakeholders who present governance, strategic alignment, and risk reduction 
top priority, and startup founders are often running with lean, fast-paced assumptions to have 
shared understanding. Interviewees underlined that "you must speak 'startup' and 'corporate' 
fluently to bridge those worlds" (SF01), hence boundary spanners are the central communication 
link keeping the pilot together.   
While corporates tend to highlight long-term fit and structured decision-making, startups value 
agility and fast learning. Boundary spanners confirm Hypothesis 1 by means of strategic bridging 
and entrepreneurial flexibility, so resolving this tension. According to the study, boundary 
spanners foster trust by consistent, open communication, an attribute in overcoming early-stage 
uncertainty and in creating mutual dedication. 
 
Second sub-question: What difficulties arise during the phase of early-stage cooperation to 
post-pilot integration and how do boundary spanners assist to support this phase? 
 
The empirical results support Hypothesis 2: boundary spanners enable strategic alignment, 
relational continuity, and internal adoption, so improving post-pilot integration. The majority of 
individuals agreed that the integration phase was the most difficult and resource consuming. 
Major obstacles cited by interviewees were cultural misalignment, stakeholder fragmentation, and 
bureaucratic difficulties.   While formal governance systems and onboarding procedures start 
integration, success depends on informal relational work. 
The key between venture teams and business units are boundary spanners, which translate the 
pilot results into internally acceptable forms in order reducing friction. For instance, CP01 detailed 
how the boundary spanner created a phased integration strategy including stakeholder 
coordination, risk analyses, and clear KPIs. This planned organization of integration paths 
guarantees not only validation but also taking in and scale of startup innovations. 
Another significant obstacle is cultural adaptation, which falls under the scope of the boundary 
spanner. Harmonizing the pace and values of startups and corporates depends critically on their 
ability to control interpersonal dynamics, grasp different incentive systems, and predict resistance. 
"The boundary spanner helps translate corporate language and processes to startups and vice 
versa" (CP01), one respondent observed, stressing their fundamental function in both an 
operational and organizational approaches. 
 
The empirical results from the phases of pilot execution and integration are mapped  figure 5, into 
the conceptual framework comprising the five-phase CSC process and four main boundary 
spanner competency domains. This helps the study to show how boundary spanners 
operationalize their competencies to support organizational integration, strategic coherence, and 
cultural alignment over an extended period of collaboration.   Their success depends on a careful 
==PAGE:53==
 
 
 
53 
application of four interrelated domains: Organizational Navigation, Strategic Bridging, 
Relationship and Cultural Competence, and Entrepreneurial Flexibility. 
Early-stage alignment in the Pilot Execution Phase is much shaped by boundary spanners. 
Interview data point to them managing pilot scoping, organizing pilot sponsorship, and translating 
stakeholder expectations across organizational lines. This corresponds with the competency of 
organizational navigation since they have to negotiate approval boundaries and identify internal 
champions to validate the pilot. Strategic bridging also shows itself through the articulation of pilot 
activities with long-term innovation goals, so ensuring that the relationship stays strategically 
relevant long beyond brief experimentation. Moreover, the domain of Relationship and Cultural 
Competence is crucial during this stage. Boundary spanners must mediate between corporate 
formality and startup informality, modify communication style, and build first trust. At last, 
entrepreneurial flexibility helps boundary spanners to fit the speed of startup experimentation, so 
promoting continuous improvement and contextual responsiveness in pilot implementation. 
These skills are refocused in the Integration Phase toward institutionalization enabling. Boundary 
spanners engage business units outside the innovation team, advocate adoption, align 
expectations, and secure resources for scaling is a central goal of Organizational Navigation. 
Translating experimentation learnings into strategic business cases that may receive executive 
support and resist corporate scrutiny becomes increasingly dependent on the strategic bridging 
competency. Relationship and Cultural Competence in this phase means mediating more 
fundamental cultural frictions including variations in time horizons and governing structures. As 
the interviews show, these performers sometimes work behind the scenes to preserve 
interpersonal continuity and promote alignment on values and priorities. 
This cross-phase mapping supports the theory that boundary spanners are fundamental not only 
in controlling operational processes but also in converting cultural and strategic differences into 
mutual value creation. 
By including this mapping into the five-phase CSC process, the research offers a disciplined but 
adaptable framework for comprehending how cross-boundary cooperation develops. 
Organizations trying to turn pilot success into scalable innovation outcomes require close 
attention to boundary spanner development, placement, and autonomy, according to the data.

5.3 
Implications of the Study 
By linking their competencies to specific phases of collaboration, this study clarifies knowledge of 
boundary spanners in corporate innovation. With a dynamic, phase-specific lens that connects 
individual action to organizational outcomes, it enhances open innovation and corporate 
venturing literature. 
This paper emphasizes for practitioners the need of formally identifying and funding boundary 
spanner roles. Particularly among leaders in corporate innovation and startup founders, the results 
highlight the need of early stakeholder involvement, methodically executed pilots, and culturally 
responsive integration planning. Investing in people who cross different worlds is not a matter of 
choice rather, it is a strategic need for ongoing innovation output. Corporations should fund 
internal navigation systems, stakeholder mapping, and cross-functional training to enable these 
players. Startups also gain from finding internal champions and setting early expectations. 
5.4 
Limitations of the Study 
We should recognize several limitations.  First of all, the absence of embedded longitudinal case 
studies reduces the capacity of the research to assess long-term integration results. Confidentiality 
issues and access restrictions restricted more thorough organizational the field of ethnography   
Second, even if the interview sample was varied, it is still rather small and regionally limited, so 
restricting generality.   Third, the results depend on self-reported data, which might represent 
attitude rather than behavior, quantitative data could have supported and shown alternative 
trends in addition.   The study keeps analytical validity in despite these restrictions by triangulating 
roles and competencies among different stakeholder groups. 
5.5 
Future research recommendations  
Especially in the pilot implementation and integration stages, this study has improved knowledge 
of boundary spanners and structured cooperation in CSC. Future research is encouraged first to 
take a multi-stakeholder approach in single-case partnerships. Through the capture of insights 
from many roles, such as startup founders, corporate sponsors, legal advisers, and business unit 
leads, researchers may uncover the subtle interaction among strategic alignment, internal 
conflicts, and decision-making silos. Such methods would present a more complete and systems-
level perspective of CSC dynamics. At last, future studies could investigate the asymmetric power 
structures deeply embedded in CSC interactions. Startups and corporations sometimes run with 
different resources, decision power, and strategic urgency. By means of political economy or 
resource dependence theory, investigating how boundary spanners negotiate and possibly 
minimize these power asymmetries would offer closer understanding of the structural elements 
influencing partnership success. These directions taken together highlight the need of more 
integrated, mixed-method, context-sensitive research to help to further explore the complexity of 
CSC and the changing function of boundary spanners.

6. Conclusion 
6.1 
Summary of Key Findings 
This paper examined how structured cooperation frameworks and boundary spanners affect the 
success of CSC, especially in relation to the pilot execution and integration stages. Under the 
direction of a conceptual framework and anchored in semi-structured interviews with twelve 
practitioners, the study looked at how boundary-spanning competencies interacted. 
Results show that strategic alignment, translating organizational logics, and reducing cultural 
frictions depend on boundary spanners in outstanding component.   Boundary spanners enabled 
to build trust and expectations by ensuring that proof-of-concept initiatives matched internal 
priorities and stakeholder needs, so guaranteeing alignment. They supported project continuity 
through internal resistance and enabled onboarding procedures, so acting as institutional 
navigators and advocates in the integration phase. 
Furthermore discovered to improve predictability, lower uncertainty, and promote common 
understanding were organized collaboration structures including phased playbooks and venture 
clienting models. But there was always conflict between structure and flexibility, particularly in 
balancing startup agility with corporate governance requirements.   
These observations taken together give strong empirical support for both hypotheses: (1) 
boundary spanners help to manage cultural alignment and expectation control in early-stage 
collaboration; (2) they enable strategic adoption and relational continuity during integration. 
6.2 
Theoretical Contributions 
This paper makes several contributions to the collection of knowledge on CE, innovation 
collaboration, and boundary spanning. First, it helps to see boundary spanners as dual agents 
navigating interpersonal dynamics and organizational structures at once. The study honed and 
complemented boundary-spanning theory within CSC by mapping competencies including 
strategic bridging, entrepreneurial flexibility, and cultural competency. 
Second, the study offers an empirically supported framework connecting in the pilot and 
integration phases boundary-spanner competencies to collaboration results. This study shows 
how both actor-level skills or process conception interact dynamically in real-world partnership 
environments, while previous studies usually focus either on these aspects. 
Third, the results underline the need of formalized collaboration models in operationalizing 
strategic intent. The study links micro-level mechanisms (e.g., structured processes and 
governance protocols) with micro-level behaviors (e.g., communication, stakeholder alignment), 
so enabling a more integrated knowledge of inter-organizational innovation dynamics.

6.3 
Final Thoughts 
Deep curiosity about how fundamentally distinct companies, corporates and startups can 
effectively cooperate to promote innovation resulted me to develop this thesis. These actors often 
operate under different business cultures, strategic decisions, and organizational logics even if 
they have same ambitions.   By means of close interaction with practitioners, this study revealed 
the central importance of human dynamics, people, processes, and partnerships, in enabling such 
collaborations not only feasible but also perhaps transforming. 
Core of this dynamic is the function of boundary spanners. Often underappreciated or 
misinterpreted, but individuals translate between worlds with different speeds, priorities, and 
decision-making structures, in order bridging structural and gaps in culture.   A key enabler of 
cross-organizational innovation turned out to be their ability to maintain trust, coordinate 
different goals, and negotiate governance constraints. The study process itself reflected many of 
the complexity it set out to investigate. Getting access to CSC settings, handling uncertainty, and 
adjusting to the actualities of fieldwork highlighted the relational and contextual character of the 
phenomena. It also clarifies the fundamental human difficulties that result from coming together 
of various backgrounds, expertise, and value systems in the pursuit of a common goal.   
In particular, the results highlight how rarely a journey for collaboration is straight-forward. 
Variations in risk tolerance, speed, and level of power can cause continuing friction. These tensions 
are profoundly cultural, not only operational. The study emphasizes that while ideal, matching 
expectations is not always immediately realistic. Especially in early-stage activities involving 
cutting-edge communication, openness, and mutual understanding become fundamental 
instruments for navigating uncertainty.  
In the end, even if corporate and startup partnership is challenging, it is not unmanageable. 
Organizations can start to close the cultural gap gradually by realizing the value of boundary-
spanning roles but flexible partnership initiatives and keeping openness to ongoing learning. This 
thesis provides minor addition but significant contribution to knowledge of the construction 
strategies and reasons behind the significance of these bridges.
