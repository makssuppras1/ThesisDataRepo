Abstract
Support structures represent a significant part of the total capital cost in offshore
wind turbines. In this thesis project, the possibility for reduction of the costs for off-
shore wind turbine jacket support structures by applying structural optimization tech-
niques is investigated. Two-step approaches using structural optimization techniques
have been previously documented for use in jacket support structures, consisting of a
topology optimization step, followed by sizing optimization for meeting local failure
criteria. In the current project, inclusion of local stress and buckling constraints to
the topology optimization problem is investigated in an attempt to create a unified
optimization procedure, that saves time and arrives at better results. To address the
well known problem of singular optima in stress constrained topology optimization,
the ε-relaxation and qp-relaxation techniques are additionally examined. Discrete
topology optimization was used following the Solid Isotropic Material with Penal-
ization (SIMP) parameterization, to derive and formulate optimization algorithms
implemented in the Frame Analysis and Optimization Software (FAOS).
The results of this study proved that stress constraints can be used in topology
optimization of jacket support structures with load cases and member geometries
derived from literature [1], to extract optimal designs with satisfactory discreteness
for the SIMP artificial density variable. The use of relaxation techniques in the stress
constrained problems successfully revealed new optimal solutions which the algorithm
could not obtain with the original constraints. Inclusion of buckling constraints also
proved possible with certain limitations being noted, and further research into the
matter of column buckling is recommended in the future.
In the last part of the project, a comparative study is conducted, benchmarking
the performance of the stress constrained problem and the ε-relaxed and qp-relaxed
problems when used for a variety of ground structures and Design Load Cases (DLCs).
The optimization problems with the original stress constraints performed adequately
in producing optimal designs and benefits were observed from the use of the ε- and
qp-relaxation techniques .
The study concludes that topology optimization with local failure criteria can
be applied for obtaining optimal designs of jacket support structures, but further
research is needed before the designs reach the level of industrial application.

CHAPTER 1
Motivation
In this chapter, the motivation for the author to undertake this project is laid out
through an introduction to the concepts and economics of offshore wind energy, fo-
cusing on turbine support structures. Furthermore, the outline of the thesis project
and the main goal are presented.
1.1
Offshore wind energy
Wind energy has demonstrated rapid growth in recent years.
With 168.7 GW of
total installed capacity in 2017, wind energy overtook coal as the second biggest
power generation capacity in the European Union [2]. Prospects for the future of
the industry in the E.U. also look bright, with new capacity installed being on a rise
through recent years as can be seen in figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Annual new onshore and offshore wind energy installations in the EU [2].
While onshore installations currently dominate the market of wind energy, offshore
wind could be the next turning point for further growth. What used to be a small,
specialized section of the wind industry until recent years is quickly developing to
a mainstream power supplier with numerous big projects already in operation and
many more underway. The growth rate for offshore wind energy capacity in Europe
has been very impressive in recent years. An increase in capacity of 108% took place
in 2015 compared to 2014 [3] which amounted to 3 GW of installed capacity, with a
similar increase of 101% being noted in 2017 compared to 2016 [2].
==PAGE:24==
2
1 Motivation
Offshore wind power holds distinct advantages over its onshore counterpart. Off-
shore wind turbines are subjected to higher wind speeds leading to more power gen-
eration potential. The capacity factor for offshore turbines installations in the E.U.
is also higher, reported at 41% in Denmark [4], compared to 26% for onshore installa-
tions in the U.K. [5]. The capacity factor of a wind turbine is calculated as the actual
electrical energy output over a set time frame compared to the nameplate capacity
of the turbine. The higher the value, the closer a turbine produces to its potential
optimal level. Noise generation, shadow flickering and visual impacts, which are mat-
ters of great importance when assessing a potential site for an onshore wind project,
are of small to no importance in offshore installations. This also allows for the use of
bigger turbines compared to onshore sites, which is ideal for the resource-rich offshore
environment. Making turbines bigger has been a trend over time, with turbines of
9.5 MW capacity currently commercially available [6], and big manufacturers hinting
that bigger designs are being considered.
Figure 1.2: Average water depth and distance to shore of operational offshore wind
farms, projects under construction and consented projects in Europe up to the end
of 2013. Circle size stands for the capacity of the project [7].
1.2
Offshore wind turbine support structures
Water depth is a crucial factor in the further development of technologies in offshore
wind energy. The average depth for offshore wind installations in 2012 was at 22
m [8], but as figure 1.2 indicates, interest is growing for projects in deeper waters.
Future unrestricted potential for wind energy as forecast by EEA for 2030 is depicted
in figure 1.3, where deep seas at 40-50 m are shown to hold the most potential for
power generation at approximately 2100 GW [9].
One of the main aspects to consider for the erection of an offshore wind turbine as
water depth increases is the support structure used. Since the turbine tower cannot
==PAGE:25==
1.2 Offshore wind turbine support structures
3
be directly fixed to the ground with foundations as in onshore wind turbines, support
structures are used to hold the turbines in place.
The support structure is then
fixed to the seabed with foundations. Different types of support structures are used
by the wind energy industry for offshore applications, with depth being the main
decision making variable between each type. Typical choices for support structures
in different depths can be seen in figure 1.4. Monopiles are mainly used for shallow
water installations up to 30 m, whereas jacket structures or tripods are more common
in deeper waters. Fixed seabed designs are typically used in depths up to 50 m, as for
deeper waters they become uneconomical and floating support structures are preferred
instead.
Figure 1.3: Total unrestricted potential for offshore wind energy at different water
depths in Europe in 2030 [9].
Driving down the cost of support structures could benefit the offshore wind indus-
try greatly. The main goals behind every aspect of designing a wind turbine is the
maximization of the annual energy production (AEP) and the minimization of the
levelized cost of energy (LCoE). A decrease in LCoE would increase the competitive-
ness of this particular technology in the market, making it a more lucrative business
venture and attracting further investment. Foundations can account for up to 17%
of the total capital cost of an offshore wind turbine [10], a significant percentage that
justifies the effort of investigating into ways to lower this cost if possible. Treading
into deeper waters is also a driving factor towards reducing foundation costs. Increase
in depth or distance to shore mainly affects foundation and installation costs. More
specifically, it has been noted that an increase in either depth or distance to shore
of 10% implies a 1% increase in said costs [11]. Lower foundation costs could be
achieved by reducing the materials used to manufacture the support structures, or
the manufacturing costs themselves.
Reducing the materials used in the support structure while ensuring structural
integrity is a task well suited for the use of structural optimization techniques. Among
the fixed seabed support structure concepts in figure 1.4, the monopile’s simple design
allows for limited freedom in optimization.
The jacket is a much more complex
structure, which could greatly benefit from an investigation into generating optimized

designs that could carry the loads of the structure in the best possible way while
conserving material. Among structural optimization techniques the one providing
the greatest degree of freedom in the conceptual design phase is structural topology
optimization. Topology optimization is a technique aiming to provide optimal designs
by removing material from an initial design domain that holds great potential in this
field of application.
In this thesis project, structural topology optimization techniques will be stud-
ied for use in the conceptual design phase of jacket support structures for offshore
wind turbines. Relaxation techniques will be investigated to address the issue of sin-
gular optima related to stress constraints in topology optimization. A quantitative
comparison of different relaxation techniques will also take place.
1.3
Thesis outline and goal
This thesis project deals with structural topology optimization of jacket support struc-
tures for offshore wind turbines. The inclusion of local constraints such as stress and
buckling is included in the optimization problem with the aim of producing more real-
istic optimal designs, and relaxation techniques are investigated into for the successful
implementation of the stress constraints. The main goals of the project are:
1. Describe and explain the procedure and problem formulation behind topology
optimization methods for offshore wind structures.
2. Derive and formulate relevant stress and buckling constraints and sensitivities
suitable for structural topology optimization algorithms.
==PAGE:27==
1.3 Thesis outline and goal
5
3. Implement and validate stress and buckling constraints and sensitivities for
topology optimization of offshore wind turbine support structures.
4. Compare existing relaxation techniques for stress-constrained topology opti-
mization applied to offshore wind turbine structures.
Following this introductory chapter, the structure for the rest of this report is as
follows. In chapter 2 the background and theory behind the concepts investigated
in the current work are presented. This chapter is the result of a literature study
by the author prior to the beginning of the actual investigation. In chapter 3 deriva-
tion of local stress constraints in topology optimization takes place. Derivation and
implementation for a truss topology optimization problem with stress constraints is
conducted in chapter 4, as well as the definition of stress used in this project. Chapter
5 introduces relaxation techniques for stress constraints, with an investigation into
the ε-relaxation technique. The derivation and implementation of an ε-relaxed stress
constrained topology optimization problem is also presented.
Another relaxation
technique, qp-relaxation, is looked into in chapter 6. Derivation and implementation
of a qp-relaxed stress constrained topology optimization takes place in this chapter.
Buckling constraints are investigated in chapter 7. In chapter 8 a benchmarking study
takes place to compare the qualities of the optimization problems introduced in the
previous chapters, followed by results. Finally, in chapter 9 the conclusions of the
study are displayed and recommendations for future improvement are made.
The implementation of the optimization problems derived in this thesis project
was conducted in MATLAB environment, using DTU Wind’s Frame Analysis and
Optimization Software (FAOS). The optimization algorithm used by FAOS is the
interior-point optimizer IPOPT.

CHAPTER 2
Literature review
Attempting to develop stress and buckling constraints for an offshore wind turbine
support structure can be a complex endeavor. Many concepts being examined for the
purposes of this thesis project were not familiar to the student prior to the beginning
of the project. To that amend, the theory on which this work will be based in later
chapters is presented here.
2.1
Structural optimization
A structure can be defined as “any assemblage of materials which is intended to
sustain loads” [13]. The notion of optimization is to work toward achieving the best
possible desired outcome from solving the problem at hand.
As such, structural
optimization can be defined as “the discipline dealing with optimal design of load
carrying structures” [14].
In general, structural optimization problems are solved
as nonlinear optimization problems (i.e. minimization and maximization problems)
subject to a set of constraints. This formulation allows for the optimization of an
aspect of the problem, such as minimization of the mass or compliance, while keeping
other aspects (stress, displacement, member geometry etc.) within a certain desired
standard by introducing them as constraints.
Figure 2.1: Resulting structures from the three structural optimization categories,
sizing optimization (a), shape optimization (b) and topology optimization (c). Initial
structures are depicted on the left and their optimized counterparts on the right [15].
When considering the design of a structure in structural optimization, it can be

divided into three distinct classes: shape optimization, sizing optimization and topol-
ogy optimization. The first class concerns the geometry of the structure. The goal
is determining the optimal shape of the structure boundaries, but without changing
the connectivity of the structure. Thus, new boundaries cannot be formed. In siz-
ing optimization the variable can be any kind of transverse dimension. Thicknesses,
areas and volumes are some very characteristic examples of variables used, and the
goal is the optimal distribution of material in an existing design. As in shape opti-
mization, new boundaries cannot be formed, meaning that existing material on any
part of the domain cannot be reduced to zero.
Finally, in topology optimization,
which is considered the structural optimization method providing the most freedom,
no a priori assumptions on the existing geometry of the structure are made. What
this method offers is the possibility to remove material completely in parts of the
structural domain, changing the shape of the structure in the process. In the case of
a frame structure, this translates to the opportunity to create optimized designs in
which elements can be removed freely, as long as their removal complies to the prob-
lem constraints. Figure 2.1 provides an illustrative example of the result expected
from the three categories of structural optimization discussed.
2.2
Topology optimization
Topology optimization is the structural optimization method offering the most con-
ceptual design freedom and potential for optimality. Taking the definitions in section
2.1 for the three classes of structural optimization in mind, one could conclude that
topology optimization is mostly used in the conceptual phase of the design process.
In the conceptual phase, an effort to meet a more broad set of design requirements
is made using a set of initial concepts [16]. This means that the designs generated
are not final, and in need of post-processing to meet certain structural standards.
In contrast, shape and sizing optimization are used for detailed design [14] in which
the parameters of the structural problem are well-defined and finalized. In general,
topology optimization problems can be separated into two basic categories, contin-
uum and discretized problems. In continuum problems every part of the design space
can vary between being a structural member (i.e. consisting of material) or being
void. Structural optimization problems usually appertain to this category and the
homogenization and SIMP (Solid Isotropic Material with Penalization) methods have
been widely used for solving continuum problems [17], as has the level-set method
[18]. In discretized problems, which are more suited to optimizing structures with a
certain expected design such as trusses and space-frame structures, an initial set of
nodes to be used in the design domain is set beforehand. This approach, known as
the Ground Structure Method (GSM) is the one used in this thesis, as it was deemed
to be the most suitable for generating designs for jacket frame structures. Figure
2.2 provides an insight in the steps taken in continuum and discrete formulations by
comparison of the homogenization method and the ground structure method.

The Ground Structure Method was introduced by Dorn et al. [20] in 1964 as
an attempt to simplify a topology optimization problem by introducing an initially
chosen set of nodal points [15]. This set of nodes is used to generate an initial frame
structure, from which the optimal structure is found as an element subset of the initial
set of elements generated. All nodes in the structure can potentially be connected
by structural elements, depending on the desired initial design complexity. What is
phenomenally a single design can provide numerous ground structures depending on
the degree of connectivity. An optimal design is found in one of two ways. One is by
deciding the existence or not of connections between nodes in a binary fashion, which
is only suitable when confronted with small and relatively simple problems. The other
is by iteratively shifting material density in the structural elements and removing
elements that are deemed by the algorithm to be redundant for the structure.
The way of conduct for the GSM approach raises some considerations for ensuring
the quality of the resulting outcome. It is evident that the final optimized design relies
heavily on the initial design it is based on. The number of nodes considered for the
creation of the initial design as well as the extent of connectivity between nodes plays a
very important role, as for more complex initial structures, the optimization algorithm
may provide a more optimal design compared to using a more simple structure. On
the other hand, increasing structural complexity is sure to manifest in a proportional
increase in computational time which should also be taken into consideration. Ohsaki
and Swan [21] have also noted the difficulty of adding members and nodes to the
existent design, and the fact that if nodal locations are also to be optimized, an
infinite number of nodes and members need to be considered. Another difficulty is
that unrealistic optimal solutions may arise, and removing too many elements from
a certain region may destabilize the structure. Overlapping members may also be
encountered in certain designs. Finally, it has been shown that when formulating a

on single load cases may lead to unrealistic designs, like the Eiffel Tower example

derive realistic and all-around functional topology optimized designs [22].
2.3
Stress constraints
Stress is defined as the internal force applied across a small area boundary by its
neighboring elements [23]. The stress is a local parameter used as an indicator of
the limit of applied force that the individual structural elements of a larger structure
can withstand before the element material fails. The design process must produce
optimal structures that take this failure under applied load into consideration. One
way is to use topology optimization to produce optimized structure designs which are
then post-processed with the use of other methods. Sizing optimization can be used
for the inclusion of local constraints such as stress in an additional sizing optimization
step on the topology optimized design. However, the option of incorporating local
material failure constraints such as stress into the topology optimization problem
also exists, although their inclusion has been a challenge since the birth of topology
optimization for discrete truss structures.
Incorporating stress constraints in topology optimization presents several difficul-
ties. The main difficulty is that stress is a local state variable and results in a large
number of constraints, and therefore, computationally expensive sensitivity analysis.
The stress at each point must be evaluated in each iteration of the optimization al-

gorithm, and most importantly only at the members that exist at that point in the
iteration progression [14]. The cause is that since the geometry of the structure can
change between iterations, the points in which critical stress is expected to appear
cannot be pointed out a priori, thus making the computational burden in each itera-
tion much larger [24]. Solutions to this matter have been presented since it proves of
great importance in large scale topology optimization problems with local variables,
the most common one being the selection of one global stress measure [25].
Another problem presented by stress constraints is the appearance of singular
optima. The topology optimization problems formulated are generally non-convex
and may contain degenerate subspaces, in which global optimal solutions may be
present known as singular optima. These optima are often inaccessible with the use
of standard optimization techniques, which in that case will often fail to converge to
a global optimum [14]. The case of singular optima in topology optimization was first
introduced by Sved and Ginos [26] who demonstrated a 3-bar truss problem in which
the optimum was inaccessible by using gradient-based optimization. It was stated
by Kirsch [27] that singular optima often appear due to accurate stress constraints,
since the elimination of members can eliminate constraints that previously existed
in the problem formulation. This may cause a discontinuity in the stress constraint
function when elements take a value of zero. Changes introduced by the topology
optimization algorithm can alter the design space and result in inaccessible singular
solutions in degenerate subspaces. Kirsch also states that these issues may be circum-
vented by neglecting compatibility conditions [28] and with the use of approximate
stress constraints.
2.4
Constraint relaxation techniques
The problem of singular optima is often encountered in gradient-based optimization
algorithms. As stated above, the existence of accurate design dependent constraints
such as stress can cause discontinuities in a topology optimization problem when
structural elements vanish [25], leading to solutions residing in degenerate subspaces
of the design space [24]. Relaxation techniques aim to solve this issue by introducing
a new problem which is a close approximation of the original one, but easier to solve.
Essentially, relaxation perturbs the feasible domain space around the degenerate sub-
space in which an optimal solution resides to make it accessible by the optimization
algorithm [29].
This is based on observations by Cheng and Jiang [30], who showed through their
work on truss topology optimization problems with stress constraints that singular
optima are not isolated points, but the end points of lines that are attached to the
feasible domain as depicted in figure 2.4a. The measure of the feasible region around
the degenerate subspace may be zero, rendering the singular optimum inaccessible,
but by modifying the feasible domain shape they can be accessed. A visual repre-
sentation is given in figure 2.4b of the perturbed design space caused by the use of

The difficulties presented by singular optima are not found only in truss topology
optimization problems. In continuum formulations the problem of singular optima
has also been prevalent. It has been noted that in density based topology optimization
with stress constraints discontinuities due to the nature of topology optimization al-
gorithms present a problem due to singularities [31]. The use of relaxation techniques
is necessary in these cases to avoid numerical hazards, and provide access to optimal
solutions.
2.5
Buckling
When investigating the structural integrity of a frame structure, a crucial failure
mode to be taken under consideration is buckling of columns. Buckling is a structural
failure that can typically occur to long slender structural members under compressive
stress. Members subjected to axial loading can manifest nonlinear deformations when
under a critical amount of load, without further increase in the load. The formula
for calculation of the critical load for columns was first presented by Euler, who
demonstrated that it bears no relation to yield stress. Buckling can occur while still
in the elastic range, depending on material properties and geometry of the column.
For long slender members under compressive stress the critical load is lower than the
yield stress, making them more prone to failing due to buckling. In trusses, this can
be avoided by placing the members in diagonal patterns [32], which is implemented
in jacket support structures with the use of different brace types as the ones in figure
2.5.

Dealing with local buckling constraints has been a great challenge in structural
topology optimization. Similar to local stress constraints, they can turn the feasible
set non-convex and cause the appearance of degenerate subspaces in the design do-
main [34]. While in the case of stress the singular optima are still attached to the
feasible region [30], for buckling constraints it has been shown by Cheng that the
situation is more severe since singular optima are completely disjointed [35]. One so-
lution to this predicament was suggested by Guo et al. [36] who proposed connecting
the disjointed feasible region and then relaxing the constraints to make the singular
optima accessible. Another very severe issue emerging when attempting to create a
buckling constrained truss topology optimization problem was identified by Zhou [37]
and Rozvany [38] and is commonly known as the jump in buckling length phenomenon.
Removal of members acting as braces between members under compressive stress (e.g.
members serving as the legs of a jacket structure) can double the effective length of
segments in the design, with severe consequences to buckling strength.
For the purposes of this thesis project, an investigation into local buckling con-
straints resulting in a buckling analysis will not take place.
Instead, the column
buckling assessment criterion proposed by DNV in the Recommended Practice DNV-
RP-C202 is used [39]. The buckling criterion is recommended in the standards as a
check which if satisfied, a full buckling analysis need not take place in the investiga-
tion.

The objective function or a number of the constraint functions of the problem stated

Heuristic methods of optimization are also commonly referred to as evolutionary
algorithms, which are inspired by biological evolution. One very popular example
is the Genetic algorithm which mimics the concept of “survival of the fittest” [40].
The algorithm focuses on passing on good traits and discarding bad traits as the
optimization proceeds, eventually leading to optimal designs. Heuristic algorithms
such as the genetic algorithm do not require the calculation of gradients to estimate
an optimal design, but the computational resources needed for the use of this class
of algorithm proves problematic to solve large scale topology optimization problems
[41]. Thus, in this thesis project the gradient-based method has been selected as the
basis for optimization.
Gradient-based methods of optimization make use of the partial derivatives (or
gradients) of the objective function and the constraint functions in respect to the
design variables. These gradients are used to calculate the trajectory the algorithm
needs to follow to arrive at an optimal solution. In structural optimization, this proce-
dure is also known as the sensitivity analysis for the optimization problem considered.
Numerous optimization algorithms following the gradient-based method exist (exam-
ples include MMA [42], GCMMA [43], IPOPT [44], SNOPT [45], FMINCON [46]).
The main differing attributes of most optimization algorithms are how the choice of
the search direction is made, and how the step size is determined [47].
In this thesis project, the open source interior point solver IPOPT [44] is used.
Interior point algorithms, as the name suggests, traverse the interior of the feasible
domain while searching for an optimal solution, instead of following the feasible region
boundaries. To ensure that the algorithm stays away from the feasible domain bound-
aries, barrier functions are used whose value approach infinity as a point moves closer
to the boundaries. Slack variables are used to transform the inequality constraints
of the problem to equality constraints, transforming the initial problem to an equally
formulated barrier problem. The barrier problem makes use of a barrier function in
the objective function to simulate the bound constraints. The goal is then to deter-
mine the step and ensure that the search direction is appropriate to progress toward
a Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) point. The process of finding the correct search direc-
tion is the most computationally demanding in interior point algorithms [41]. The

2.7
Optimization in offshore wind turbine support
structures
The discipline of structural optimization has been advancing steadily along structural
analysis. The increasing availability in computational power provides an opportunity
to analyze numerous possible scenarios in the search of optimal structures with the
use of computer aided optimization, a feat which could not be achieved by manual
optimization. These techniques have been highly utilized in the aerospace and auto-
motive industries [50], but are underdeveloped in the wind turbine industry, which
also holds true for use in optimizing support structures. This is partly because the
most used support structure designs (e.g. the monopile) are very straightforward and
simple, but also because of the complexity of the loads a wind turbine sustains, which
must be considered in the optimization procedure. Nevertheless, since the support
structure system for an offshore wind turbine typically covers about 17% of the total
capital cost [10] , and with more complex designs being considered such as the jacket
support, the need for optimization in order to reduce costs and use of materials is
considerable.
Muskulus and Schafhirt [50] have identified numerous challenges in the process of
designing a wind turbine structure to which the reader can refer to in more detail.
Nonlinearities are a severe obstacle in wind turbine optimization.
The nature of
wind and wave loads is such that they are dynamic and thus dependent on the time
domain. Fatigue is also an important parameter in the design, as wind turbine support
structures are expected to maintain structural integrity for the entire life cycle of the
turbine. Fatigue calculations are further complexified by the existence of dynamic
effects. The number of variables and constraints in an optimization method derived
for producing optimal support structure designs is generally high, and the problem
==PAGE:38==
16
2 Literature review
is usually non-convex, which makes finding a global optimum challenging as noted in
section 2.3.
The analysis of a wind turbine system is recommended to be conducted using time
domain simulations, in order for dynamic effects and nonlinearities to be accounted
for [51]. This presents a considerably taxing computational procedure. Given that
structural optimization methods are already expensive computationally, implement-
ing a time domain simulation is not always realistic with respect to time and resource
expenditure.
Effective and efficient ways to simulate the dynamic and sensitivity
analyses are therefore needed. Static analysis is common for large wind turbine struc-
tures, carried out with the inclusion of a single or numerous static loads [50], and
dynamic sensitivity factors can be incorporated for a more refined analysis [52].

3.3
Design parameterization with the SIMP method
When considering the topology of a structure, the most important aspect to take
into consideration is the distribution of material in the given space. In continuum
formulations, the goal is to determine which points in space contain material and
which remain void. The design variables used in this case are the material properties.
In a discrete formulation made for a truss structure, an initial design represents all the
optional structural members of the structure, and the design variables are quantities
that define the geometry of the structural members. For cylindrical members, the
variables needed to describe the geometry are the thickness t and diameter d of the
cylinders.
An increasingly popular design parameterization method for topology optimiza-
tion is the Solid Isotropic Material with Penalization (SIMP) method, first introduced
by Brendsøe in 1989 [55]. The SIMP method was developed as a means to solve what
is known as black and white or 0-1 problems. The decision of whether material exists
or not in a structural element, which happens in topology optimization, is such a
problem. To avoid the use of integer 0-1 variables for solving the problem, they are
substituted by continuous variables. In both cases, the variables are simulating the
‘density’ of the material in the specific element. Thus, a value of one means material
exists, while zero means the element in question is void. Material properties of ele-
ments in continuum formulations also change according to the value of the density
variable [15]. By using continuous variables, intermediate values between zero and
one become accessible and gray areas can appear in the optimized design. Since a
black and white optimized design is desired, intermediate values are then penalized
by use of the power law criterion using a penalization factor p, which typically takes
a value of p = 3 for topology optimization problems.
In discrete formulations, there are differences to the SIMP method parameteriza-
tion compared to the facts laid out in the paragraph above. The SIMP method was
developed for continuum formulations, and the methodology explained above is for
such a formulation. In discrete formulations, such a notion as material properties
changing as dictated by the design variable cannot exist. The problem is still formu-
lated by use of an artificial density variable ρ but this has no physical meaning for
the structure. It is instead used merely as a numerical way to express the existence
or not of structural members. The design variable vector x for discrete optimization
problems following the SIMP parameterization can then be defined as
x = [dT tT ρT ]T .

3.5
Sensitivity analysis
In order to solve a topology optimization problem using gradient based optimization,
the objective function f and constraint functions g needs to be differentiated with
respect to the design variables x. One needs to produce the gradient of the objective
function ∇f and the Jacobian matrix of the constraint functions J. The procedure
followed to obtain the derivatives needed is called sensitivity analysis.
The way sensitivity analysis is carried out can be separated in two main categories,
numerical and analytical methods. Numerical methods provide approximations of the
sensitivity calculations. Analytical methods provide exact results, and are used in
this thesis to calculate the sensitivities. The analytical methods that are of interest
here are the direct analytical method and the adjoint analytical method. The direct
method is to be preferred when the design variables are more than the problem
constraint functions, otherwise the adjoint method is computationally less expensive
and consequently more efficient [13]. A topology optimization problem with stress
constraints presents a very high number of constraint functions, owing to the fact that
stress is a local constraint. To that amend, the sensitivity analysis will be carried out
using the direct analytical method.

Constraint relaxation is used as a way to circumvent the appearance of singular
optima by solving a new, easier problem which is based on the original problem. In
this chapter, an investigation takes place on applying the ε-relaxation technique to
the original stress constrained topology optimization problem presented in chapter 4.
The problem definition and sensitivity analysis of the topology optimization problem
is laid out, and validation of the analysis is made by presenting relevant examples.
5.1
Introduction to ε-relaxation

6.1
Introduction to qp-relaxation
The qp-relaxation technique was first introduced by Bruggi [62] in 2007 as an al-
ternative to the ε-relaxation technique earlier proposed by Cheng and Guo [29] for
addressing the problem of singular optima in stress constrained topology optimization.
The concept is based on the definition of a stress model by Duysinx and Bendsøe [31]
that mimics microscopic (or local) stress for rank-2 layered composites with the use
of SIMP parameterization. The criteria the stress model needs to fulfill are:
1. That the local stress is a function of the inverse of the density parameter, and
2. That the local stresses remain finite at zero density.
This ‘local’ stress is defined as
σij = ρp

where p is the power law penalization exponent which usually takes a value of p = 3
and q is a number greater than one. Its exact value is determined by the second
criterion listed above. In order for the stresses to remain finite at zero density and
the final designs to be physically consistent, the only choice for the value of q is that

of q = p. It is clarified at this point that this is not the notation used to define stress
throughout this thesis, and it is presented here only to show the basis upon which
the qp-approach was based by Bruggi.
Adhering to criterion 2 also presents an issue. The fact that stress converges to a
finite value at zero density presents a discontinuity in the stress constraint function,
which means that optimization algorithms may be unable to remove void members
from the design. This leads to degenerate subspaces in the feasible domain and the
appearance of singular optima. It can be deduced by inspection of equation 6.1, that
use of values p > q would prevent the discontinuity of stress at zero density. Duysinx
and Bendsøe state that point in their work, but mention that while this observation
could prove very useful in simplifying the computation of topology optimization prob-
lems with stress constraints, the problems solved would no longer be consistent with
physics [31].
The qp-relaxation approach proposed by Bruggi is based on this property of p > q
to overcome singularities by enlarging the degenerate subspace. It is stressed in the
article that “the proposed power relaxation must be regarded as a pure mathematical
manipulation to solve the problem” [62]. The qp-relaxation parameter is usually seen
taking fairly large values when used in topology optimization problems, typically
around εqp = 0.5.

previously inaccessible solutions.

algorithms [24]. Relaxation is mainly of interest in the vicinity of zero ρ in stress
constrained topology optimization, and an effort should be made to provide the same
amount of relaxation to the problem with the use of each relaxation technique for
small ρ values.

In this chapter a comparative study takes place between the stress constrained topol-

In general, introducing relaxation resulted in designs with equal or less elements

distinct patterns in most cases.
The first, most usual pattern is for members that form the braces in the lowest

prominent in the 4-legged designs where more loading can be distributed to the legs,
than in the 3-legged designs. It is also more common in cases with more levels, where
removing the braces from one level affects the structure less. It is assumed that this
is due to the load cases used not being enough to simulate realistic loading even
in the 10 LC cases, but more importantly due to the point of application for the
loads being at the top of the transition piece.
Distribution of the loading to the
bottom of the structure is inadequate, and the algorithm focuses on strengthening
the top of the structure where the effect of the loading is more prominent. Discarding
braces also gets more pronounced with increasing relaxation. This is showcased by

In general, the resulting optimal designs rely heavily on the parameters used for
the run and the cases examined themselves. Using initial d and t at the middle of
their respective fields provides consistently better results in general but exceptions
have been noted. The load cases used have a big impact on the designs as well. Designs

problems present very similar solutions for all formulations. The same observation
has been made for values of the objective function.
This leads to the conclusion
that convergence to global optima for these cases may have been reached. For more
complex cases, new solutions of increasing difference are seen as the magnitude of
perturbation introduced by relaxation increases.
8.3
Limitations of the benchmark
It is acknowledged that certain limitations exist to this benchmarking study. The
most important limitations recognized are the following:
• The load application point. The loads are not realistically distributed on the
structure.
• The number of DLCs used and the static loads considered.
• The non-inclusion of a dynamic analysis.
• The symmetry of the loads with respect to the number of legs of the jacket
structures.

• The absence of buckling constraints in the formulations considered.
• Not taking into account member connectivity and stress concentration factors.
• The different configurations in implementation used for the qp-approach.
• Inclusion of more complex ground structures.
A number of the limitations listed are directly tied to the complexity of the cases
considered.
Topology optimization with local constraints is highly demanding in
computational resources, and documenting more complex cases for the benchmark
proved challenging. It was instead decided to focus on a small yet functional set
of cases, to develop a representative view of the topology optimization problems
researched.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusions and
recommendations
9.1
Conclusions
The aim of this thesis project was the development of topology optimization problems
with stress and buckling constraints for offshore wind turbine support structures. An
additional investigation into relaxation techniques was carried out to address the well
known problem of singular optima in local stress constraints, and a study was carried
out in the form of a benchmark for the comparison of relaxation methods.
In the study it has been documented that generation of jacket support struc-
tures using the topology optimization problem with local stress constraints defined in
chapter 4 is possible. Using load cases derived from the investigation Rambøll con-
ducted for the INNWIND reference jacket [1], optimal designs for three-legged and
four-legged jacket support structures were successfully obtained with the use of FAOS
software. The problems solved in chapter 8 performed well, obtaining convergence
in reasonable run times and numbers of iterations.
The capacity of the topology
optimization problem to produce discrete designs where the distribution of the SIMP
artificial density variable is close to the bounds was also satisfactory.
Buckling constraints were included in the topology optimization problem in chap-
ter 7 in the form of the column buckling assessment criterion proposed by DNV [39].
Implementation of the topology optimization problem with stress and buckling con-
straints revealed that for the constraint to be satisfied the upper bounds set for the
diameter and thickness design variables (d, t) needed to be relaxed to a large degree.
The new bounds showed great deviation from values found in the literature [1]. Ad-
ditionally, the buckling constraint greatly affected the ability of the algorithm to
arrive to discrete designs, due to the limitations set on the values of (d, t) for existing
members. In summary, while functional, the constraint was deemed too restricting
for efficient use in the topology optimization problems considered and was excluded
from the benchmarking study that ensued.
The issue of singular optima in topology optimization problems with local stress
constraints was addressed by researching relaxation techniques. An investigation was
carried out into the well established ε-relaxation technique presented by Cheng and
Guo [29] and the qp-relaxation technique presented in more recent years by Bruggi
==PAGE:94==
72
9 Conclusions and recommendations
[62]. Stress constrained topology optimization problems were derived and formulated
for both techniques, in chapters 5 and 6 respectively. Both techniques successfully
provided optimal jacket support structure designs, while arriving to new respective
solutions.
A direct comparison of the effects of each relaxation technique on the
problem proved, unfortunately, not possible. The operating range of the relaxation
parameters for each technique was found to be vastly different during the investigation.
This resulted in a more relaxed problem when using the qp-approach compared to
ε-relaxation.
In the benchmarking study that followed, considering different ground structures,
loading conditions and values of the relaxation parameters, all three topology opti-
mization problem formulations performed adequately. With respect to time, ε-relaxed
formulations required the most CPU time and number of iterations to reach conver-
gence, owing to the increased relative size of the problems solved. Cases solved with
the original constraint and with the qp-approach, which share the same problem size,
displayed similar results among them. Relaxation of the original stress constraints
allowed for new solutions to be found for most of the cases considered especially
in cases of increased complexity. The use of relaxation revealed to a great extent
the need for better application of the loads used, and the use of additional DLCs.
Increasing the magnitude of relaxation showed a positive effect on the objective func-
tion values achieved as seen in table B.3, with the lowest values demonstrated when
the qp-approach is used. Better convergence to discrete ‘0-1’ designs with the use
of relaxation was also noted, with ε-relaxation with a parameter value of ε = 0.1
presenting the most discrete designs overall. Conclusive results on which relaxation
technique performs better for the current analysis could not be found. However, it
can be mentioned that the qp-relaxation technique seems to provide the largest de-
gree of freedom to the algorithm which allows for easier arrival to new solutions, a
feature well suited to topology optimization.
9.2
Recommendations
Although this study has arrived to some useful conclusions on the use of stress and
buckling constraints in topology optimization and the benefits of relaxation tech-
niques, additional research can improve various aspects of the work presented here.
Recommendations for further study include:
• Improvement to the load distribution in the structure. Application of the loads
on a single point at the top of the structure proved to be a great obstacle in this
study in terms of arriving to realistic optimal designs, and better distribution
is expected to greatly improve the performance of the algorithm in that aspect.
• Use of more realistic DLCs. The number of DLCs proved to be a bottleneck
in terms of computational resources in the current investigation. Nonetheless,
efforts should be made to include DLCs that provide symmetrical loading for
==PAGE:95==
9.2 Recommendations
73
more than one type of jacket, and to take more loading conditions into consid-
eration.
• Inclusion of a dynamic analysis would greatly increase the accuracy of wind and
wave loading on the structure and provide more realistic results.
• A more detailed investigation into the operating range for the relaxation param-
eters could provide for better implementation of relaxation techniques.
• An analysis based on the fatigue limit state (FLS) should be included to produce
more accurate results.
• Member connectivity should be taken into consideration, as well as a study into
stress concentration factors.
• A full buckling analysis for topology optimization problems should be investi-
gated into, for a more accurate study of buckling in the structural members.
• An economical assessment of the designs could be considered. Additionally, an
analysis using the same dimensions for groups of structural members could be
of interest in this case for more realistic manufacturing costs.
