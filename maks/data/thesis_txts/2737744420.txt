Abstract
This thesis project investigates the application of Large Language Models (LLMs)
within Science, Technology, Engineering & Mathematics (STEM) education, aiming
to enhance personalized learning through advanced agentic systems. It focuses on the
development and assessment of three systems: the Teaching Agent System (TAS),
the Teaching Multi-Agent System (TMAS), and the Personal Memory Agent Sys-
tem (PMAS). These systems leverage the capabilities of LLMs to provide dynamic
and personalized educational experiences, by conversationally interacting with stu-
dents in real-time, adapting to their learning preferences, and simulating personal
memory. In order to measure the generative performance of these systems, agentic
evaluation, which is able to read and score outputs based on specified criteria, is
further developed. Evaluations demonstrate that these systems are not only able to
deliver high quality personalized learning content, but also infer learning preferences
of students from educational conversations, enabling for continuously better person-
alization in learning. The research underscores the potential of LLMs to transform
educational paradigms, making learning more inclusive and tailored to individual stu-
dent’s needs. Future work will concentrate on refining these technologies to ensure
their reliability and effectiveness in diverse educational settings.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Context and Motivation
Large Language Model (LLM) technology has matured greatly over the last few years,
following its arguably major breakthrough with GPT-3 [4], introduced by Open-AI
in July 2020. Since the, these advanced language models have seen a skyrocketing
popularity, largely due to the mainstream adaptation of Chat-GPT introduced in
November 2022.
Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 have
been proven capable of exhibiting human-like intelligent behavior, for example by be-
ing able to reason about their answers and decisions [38], and by being able to avoid
cognitive biased behaviour [14]. The competitive space around the so-called foun-
dation models, large pre-trained LLMs, is extremely fast-paced, fueling continuous
technological advances, and the opportunities arising with LLMs are many, covering
numerous industries. The utilization of these models in education presents a partic-
ularly interesting opportunity as of 2024 and onwards.
Opportunities in education arise from the fact that educational systems suffer from
a lack of innovation [27], especially apparent during the Corona pandemic [11]. This
lack of innovation has at least one major implication: Students’ capabilities are de-
clining, and young people attending school are learning less.
Over the past several years, PISA scores have seen large declines in reading and
mathematics capabilities for 15-year old’s [25]. The decline in PISA scores for Danish
students can be seen in figure 1.1, but similar decline has been documented globally,
and is a trend that may likely continue in the future.
Such declines in fundamental prowess gained from education, referred to as crystal-
lized intelligence, not only impacts the lives of individual students during and after
their educational tenure, but has negative consequences for society as a whole. For
the sake of societal progress, by educationally lifting its citizens, this trend must be
reversed. In order to do so, given recent advancements in LLMs, AI-based technology
should be utilized for enhancing educational practices.
Currently, one of the biggest issues in conventional education is its inability to accom-
modate the varied learning needs within diverse student populations. Students are
forced to submit themselves to established teaching conventions and methodologies,
adapting their learning preferences to best fit into already established systems of teach-

Personalized learning is shown to be hugely beneficial to all students, and the chal-

AI-powered personalized learning may be the key component required for elevating

Even though LLM-based products like OpenAI’s ChatGPT have been around for

This notion highlights a key deficit in many peoples’
perception of the capabilities these LLM-based systems posses. Their untapped po-
tential lies not in conveying existing information, but in using existing information in
order to simulate and create completely new types of interactions. These models are
incredible at generating completely new content, and this is why they are potentially
hugely impactful in education. Given the current capabilities of LLMs, it is not far
fetched to imagine that models like GPT-4 can be used to personalize learning expe-
riences for students, in the same way teaching assistants do.
An LLM-based teaching system could carry out highly technical conversations, and
convey existing as well as novel teaching material, tailored to a student’s personal

learning preferences. Such system could at the same time maintain a digital represen-

Using LLM-based systems to carry out personalized learning presents both theoreti-

First and foremost, personal memory is needed to enable personalized learning, the
digitally maintained interpretation of a student’s knowledge base and learning prefer-

a teaching body, LLM-based agents are needed, which are proficient in both under-
standing and generating personalized learning material. For personalized learning to
be put to use in the near future, these systems must be able to run on-device, and
for that smaller, lighter models in multi-agent systems need to carry out the above
tasks.
Hence, personalized learning requires systems for inferring personal memory of the
student, and conveying course material and subjects aligned to the inferred persona.
Whilst foundation LLMs like GPT-4 have demonstrated advanced language under-
standing and generative abilities, adapting them to work effectively in this endeavor
necessitates substantial enhancements. Employing LLM-based systems which can un-
derstand student’s learning preferences, and convey subjects aligned to these, requires
the investigation and application of methodologies relating to retrieving external infor-
mation, agent-capabilities such as reasoning and tool utilization, as well as advanced
agent setups like multi-agent systems.
The significance of this endeavor extends beyond the immediate enhancement of ed-
ucational outcomes; it harbors the potential to revolutionize teaching and learning
methodologies. Successfully enabling LLMs to understand individual students and
their learning needs on a fundamental level could potentially provide several benefits:
• New ways of mapping out learners and identifying needs on an individual level,
not possible in current educational systems.
• The possibility of accommodating atypical and sub-functional learners, who are
otherwise neglected by educational practices.
• Advancing tools for supplementing aspects of education, such as teaching assis-
tance.

1.2
Main Hypothesis and Research Questions
In order to explore and investigate how, and how well, LLMs can be brought to work
in educational settings, a main hypothesis and three underlying research questions
are posed. These are stated below.
Main Hypothesis:
LLM-based systems can be utilized for enabling personalized learning in
Science, Technology, Engineering & Mathematics (STEM) education.
First Research Question:
RQ1: Can RAG-enabled, autonomous ReAct-like-agents be leveraged in order
to convey personalized learning material for STEM students?
Second Research question:
RQ2: Can smaller foundation LLMs be made to achieve enhanced generative,
education-appropriate capabilities, by collaborating in multi-agent systems?
Third Research question:
RQ3: Can LLM-based systems enable personal memory simulation for stu-
dents interacting with agentic learning systems?
1.2.1
Research Goals for RQ1
To address the main hypothesis, the first research question sets out to investigate
if and how State of the Art (SOTA) LLM-based agents can be used for generating
personalized learning material. Answering this research question entails investigating
a conversational LLM system, which is able to retrieve subject-relevant course mate-
rial and convey topics in a personalized manner, to individual students with different
learning preferences.
==PAGE:15==
1.2 Main Hypothesis and Research Questions
5
A system called the Teaching Agent System (TAS), comprising of a large foundational
LLM-based agent and additional methodologies, will be built using the LLM frame-
work LangChain.
The TAS will be investigated and evaluated using SOTA LLM
evaluations approaches, in order to verify whether it is able to carry out personalized
teaching, pertaining to the principles of the Universal Design for Learning (UDL).
1.2.2
Research Goals for RQ2
In order to practically employ the use of LLMs for not only generating personalized
teaching content, but also inferring personal traits like learning preferences, these
systems eventually need to be light enough for them to run on local devices. Large
foundation models such as GPT-4 are too large to run on user’s own devices, like
laptops and smartphones, however smaller models can be made to perform similarly
in advanced setups. Splitting and solving tasks into sub-tasks is a very human thing
to do, and recent research indicates how LLM-based systems can benefit from doing
the same [16] [22] [28].
To address the main hypothesis, the second research question seeks to enhance gener-
ative performance of smaller, cheaper models such as GPT-3.5 to be similar to larger
ones. This is done by employing several GPT-3.5 LLMs in multi-agent systems, which
use different forms of task distribution.
State of the Art (SOTA) methods for multi-agent collaboration will be implemented
on top of the TAS to create the Teaching Multi-Agent System (TMAS). This multi-
agent system will be evaluated in the same manner as the TAS, in order to grasp
what effect distribution of sub-tasks and agent collaboration has.
1.2.3
Research Goals for RQ3
The ultimate goal is to create education-appropriate personal memory for the students
using an AI-based learning system, such as the TAS and TMAS.
With LLM-based agent systems, which can generate personalized teaching content,
these same systems should also be able to infer personal traits for the individual
student. Such teaching systems will continuously update their understanding about
the student, and be able to identify a student’s learning preferences which can be
used to enhance learning personalization and experiences further.
To address the main hypothesis, the third research question seeks to investigate how
this personal memory inference can be carried out. An agent-based system called
the Personal Memory Agent System (PMAS) is built, which is given conversations
between students and teaching systems, and must identify student-specific learning
preferences.

CHAPTER 2
Related Work
This chapter contains a description of the related work and literature, hence prior
research on which this thesis builds is outlined in relative detail.
2.1
Research Context
The research context of this thesis exists primarily in the space of utilizing and ex-
panding LLM capabilities, such that the interdisciplinary domain of Artificial Intelli-
gence (AI) and educational technology can be elevated.
A lot of research is being done on foundational LLM models, especially as the big
five, Microsoft, Google, Meta, Amazon and Apple, are racing to deploy the best - and
what has until recently tended towards being the largest - LLMs.
With these ever larger foundational models being introduced, improvements in quali-
ties not directly anticipated or associated with sheer model scale tend to follow. An
example of this trend was presented in the paper ”Human-like intuitive behavior and
reasoning biases emerged in large language models but disappeared in ChatGPT”
(Hagendorff et al. September 2023) [14], which showed how the flawed behaviour
associated with human cognitive bias fallacies became much less frequent as LLMs
got larger.
Current unresolved problems and shortcomings relating to utilizing LLMs in various
use-cases, may over the coming years disappear simply through the introduction of
new larger foundational models.
Other research is being done to improve performance on specific tasks through in-
creasing quality of the training data, making for smaller models with similar capabil-
ities.
In the paper ”Textbooks Are All You Need” (Gunasekar et al., October 2023) [13],
the LLM phi-1 is introduced, showing decent performance on the coding benchmark

3.1
Attention and Transformer-Based LLM Technology
The overarching focus of this project lies on utilizing Large Language Models (LLMs)
in intricate ways in order to solve advanced tasks. Specifically, the goal is to enable
LLM-based systems to infer learning-related personal memories of students, and gen-
erate teaching appropriate, personalized learning material using the same systems.
Transformers represent the technological foundation of transformer-based LLMs, and
a basic understanding of transformers is hence needed in order to grasp the learn-
ing systems presented in this project. The most central components of transformers
are their attention mechanisms, and understanding attention is important in order
to both practically but also conceptually grasp the workings of these LLM systems.
Attention enables the core functionality of transformer-based LLMs, but transcends
being merely the computational backbone of transformers. Conceptually, attention
serves as the most advanced method for computationally modelling and simulating
complexities of the world we live in.
Transformer-based LLM architecture has been foundational in developing recent State
of the Art (SOTA) LLM models capable of understanding and generating human-like
text, facilitating tasks ranging from translation to text summarization to having con-
versations and beyond.
3.1.1
Introduction to Transformer-Based LLM Models
Transformer-based models represent a groundbreaking shift in the field of Natural
Language Processing (NLP). Prior to the recent popularization of Large Language
Models (LLMs), NLP models primarily relied on architectures like Recurrent Neural
Networks (RNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory Networks (LSTMs), which were
constrained by sequential data processing and often struggled with long-range de-
pendencies. This was a big limitation, as important inter-textual relevance is often
present across longer corpora of texts, for example in most books.
Introduced by Vaswani et.
al.
in their 2017 paper ”Attention is All You Need”
[35], attention-reliant transformers are designed around the self-attention mechanism,
enabling them to weigh the significance of different words within a sequence of text,
regardless of their positional distance from each other. This architecture allows for a
nuanced understanding of language, capturing complex syntactic and semantic rela-
tionships, thereby overcoming problems related to long-range dependencies.
On a practical level, transformers consist of two main components; an encoder and
a decoder. The encoder processes the input text, embedding information about each
word’s context into a high-dimensional space. The decoder then generates output
text based on this encoded, contextual information.

3.1.2
Self-Attention Mechanism
The self-attention mechanism is what truly sets transformers apart from previous
NLP model architectures like RNNs.
The attention mechanisms in LLMs allow these models to simulate a kind of ’arti-
ficial consciousness’ in which the system dynamically moves its focus based on the
relevance of different parts of the input data. Attention is selective and adaptive,
focusing computational resources on aspects of data deemed more informative at any
given time. Hence, attention extends beyond its technical functionality, essentially
carrying out cognitive and perceptual processes not far from those observed in hu-
man cognition. Analogous to a person who, when for example listening to a lecture,
pays more attention to elements that are crucial for understanding the subject, rather
than all details equally, the selective focus of attention also allows for singling out
specific stimuli among a plethora of environmental inputs. Such selective attention
allows LLMs to model complex dependencies and interactions within data, akin to
understanding nuanced and interconnected real-world scenarios.
Practically, attention allows the model to dynamically adjust the focus on differ-
ent parts of the input sentence as it processes information.
This means that for
any given word, the model can assess which other words in an input sequence of
text, say for example a sentence, are most relevant for ’constructing’ (explaining) its
meaning, thereby creating a dynamic, context-aware representation of the entire text.
The attention mechanism operates by generating three vectors for each word in the
input sequence X:
• Query vector Q = EW Q
• Key vector K = EW K
• Value vector V = EW V
These three vectors are computed from the embeddings of the individual words
E = Embed(x), multiplied by their respective weight matrices W Q, W K, and W V .
Embed(·) is the embedding function chosen to be used in a particular LLM, while the
weight matrices are learned during the training process and are specific to the model
and data.
For a single word in the input sequence, the vectors do the following:
• The Query Vector Q essentially represents the word in terms of what it is
looking for in other words of the input sequence. It is used to measure the
relevance of each of the other words with the current word. Q can hence be
thought of as a way for the word to ask, ”Which other words should I pay
attention to?”
• The Key Vector K represents how strongly all other words can be queried by
the current word. When query Q of one word interacts with key K of another,

Q should give to the word represented by K.
• The Value Vector V carries the actual content of the word, which is either
passed on to deeper layers of the model or used in constructing the attention
layer output.

The attention output is yielded first by computing attention scores, then normalizing
these scores. For each word, the dot product is computed for its query vector Q with

word (as a key) should contribute to the output of the word associated with the query.

now represents the relative importance of the corresponding word’s value vector V ,
in regards to forming the final output for the word represented by the query.
As such, attention forms the fundamental basis of what enables transformer-based
LLMs to capture both local and global context.
3.1.3
Attention Windows
To optimize computational eﬀiciency and focus, transformer-based LLMs utilize a
technique known as attention windows, or localized attention. This approach restricts
the attention mechanism to consider only a subset of positions within the input se-
quence, rather than attending to every single position. Such localized perspective
can dramatically reduce the computational complexity, especially in scenarios deal-
ing with very long sequences.
Attention windows operate by defining a fixed-size window around each token, within
which the attention mechanism is allowed to operate. For instance, a window size of
n means that for any given word, the model only computes attention scores for the
n words before and after it, ignoring the rest of the sequence. This localized focus
helps in maintaining a balance between contextual understanding and computational
feasibility.
The implementation of attention windows varies, but one common method is the
sliding window approach, in which the window slides across the input sequence, and
at each step, attention calculations are confined to the tokens within the window.
This method ensures that each token influences, and is influenced by, its immediate
==PAGE:27==

neighbors more strongly than those farther away. This not only reflects the typical
structure of language, where nearby words are more contextually relevant than dis-
tant ones, but also mimics human cognitive attention by focusing on more immediate
elements in the stream of information.
From a practical standpoint, attention windows help in scaling transformer models
to handle longer texts more eﬀiciently. By reducing the number of pairwise compar-
isons in the attention calculations, the computational load is significantly decreased,
enabling the use of transformer-based LLMs in applications where input sequences
are lengthy, such as document summarization, without a substantial trade-off in per-
formance.
3.1.4
Next-Word Prediction
In order to turn the fundamental attention mechanism into a working LLM, a few
steps are needed with which these models gain the capability of next-word prediction,
ie. the ability to output text, usually one word at a time.
First, multi-head attention expands on the attention mechanism by parallelizing mul-
tiple attention heads. Each head independently applies attention to the input, but
with different, learnable linear transformations to the queries Q, keys K, and val-
ues V . This design allows the model to diversely focus on different features across
different positions in the input text.

In the decoding process, each decoder layer starts with a multi-head attention mecha-
nism where the decoder’s previous output serves as queries, and both keys and values
come from the same output. This attention mechanism allows each position in the
decoder to attend to all positions up to and including that position in the previous

18

step of the decoder sequence. This is achieved by, in training, masking future posi-
tions (making them inaccessible) so that the predictions for position i only depend
on the known outputs at positions less than i.
Following the masked multi-head attention, the next step in each decoder layer is the
encoder-decoder attention mechanism. Here, the queries Q come from the previous
decoder layer’s output, and the keys K and values V are the outputs from the en-
coder. This allows every position in the decoder to attend over all positions in the
input sequence, thereby integrating the input information into the decoding process.
Each layer in the decoder further includes a position-wise feed-forward network. This
consists of two linear transformations with a ReLU [1] activation in between. These
networks are applied to each position separately and identically, which means they
transform the representation independently at each position.
After the decoder has processed the input through its layers, the output of the top
decoder layer is transformed into a vector of logits representing the probability dis-
tribution over the output vocabulary.
This is done using a linear transformation,
followed by a softmax function, which converts the logits to probabilities. The high-
est probability word is selected as the next word in the sequence, and fed back into
the decoder during inference when generating new text sequences.
The process repeats for each new word until a special end-of-sequence token is pro-
duced or the sequence reaches a predefined maximum length.

3.1.5
Pre-Training Transformer-based LLMs
The training process for these types of LLMs, such as OpenAI’s Generative Pre-
trained Transformer (GPT) and others, is divided into two categories; pre-training
and fine-tuning.
Pre-training is by far the most elaborate process and generally entails training on a
very, very large, diverse text corpora. Pre-training on vast amounts of text essentially
guides the attention mechanisms to learn where to place attention in a given context.
In practice, pre-training also includes doing optimizations and compute considera-
tions to eﬀiciently manage the scale and complexity of data and the model.
Pre-training LLMs like an OpenAI GPT adheres to the following principles:
1. Model Architecture Setup
The architecture of the LLM is defined, including number of layers, attention heads,
hidden units, as well as other hyperparameters. The total number of learnable pa-
rameters vary, but for OpenAi’s GPT-3, this number equates to a total of 175 billion
[4]. Choice of architecture depends on a variety of factors, and especially size follows
the use case, for instance, a locally running LLM needs to be small, while a general-
purpose one may need to be quite large.
2. Data Preparation
Pre-training data for LLMs typically consists of a very large corpus of text gathered
from books, websites, articles, and other textual sources. The data is preprocessed
using tokenization, explained in section 3.2 on page 21, where text is split into smaller
chunks, using methods like Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) [4]. This preprocessing step
helps manage vocabulary size and deal with intricacies like rare words.
For OpenAI’s GPT-3 model, the training data size was 260 billion tokens, and each
patch of training data is split into 2048 tokens [4].
3. Pre-training
For autoregressive models like GPT, the model is trained to predict the next word in
a sentence given the previous words, which is referred to as causal language modeling.
Typically, this is done using a masked attention mechanism where each token can
only attend to previous tokens in the sequence, hence training to predict the next
token in the data sequence.
This training method stands in contrast to that which pertains to autoencoding mod-
els like BERT [7], where the model is trained to predict masked tokens in a sequence
given all surrounding, non-masked tokens.
A percentage of input tokens are ran-
domly masked, and this kind of LLM is hence trained to predict these masked tokens,
a method referred to to as masked language modeling.
Objective function is (usually) cross-entropy loss [4], measuring the difference between
predicted token probabilities and actual distribution of the tokens.
4. Optimization Techniques
==PAGE:30==
20
3 Methodology
Due to the large size of these models, several optimization techniques are employed
such as Adam optimization, which adapts the learning rate for each parameter. Us-
ing gradient clipping, gradients are prevented from becoming too large and leading
to unstable training dynamics. Learning rate scheduling is typically also employed,
in which learning rates are decreased based on a schedule, or the opposite with warm-
up steps, where learning rates gradually increase to a stable value, used to stabilize
training initially.
5. Distributed Training
Due to computational cost, training these large LLMs on vast amounts of data often
requires distributed training techniques. This is carried out in two ways, through
data- and model-parallelism.
With data-parallelism, training data is split across multiple processing units (GPUs/T-
PUs), with each device computing gradients for a subset of the data, subsequently
synchronizing computed gradients across all devices to update model weights.
The model itself is also split across devices, with different parts of the model’s layers
being trained on different devices, naturally instrumental to training extremely large
models.
6. Regularization
In order to prevent overfitting, regularization techniques such as weight decay are
used during training, which helps in making the model robust to noise in the input
data.
7. Evaluation and Iteration
Throughout the training process, models are evaluated using a validation set, in order
to monitor performance and prevent overfitting. Based on these evaluations, adjust-
ments to the model architecture, training procedure, or hyperparameters can be made
to improve performance.
3.1.6
Fine-Tuning Transformer-based LLMs
Fine-tuning is an essential phase in optimizing pre-trained transformer-based LLMs
for specific tasks, refining a broadly trained model to excel in particular applications
and follow desired behaviour. This is done the following way:
1. Task-Specific Adaptation
After pre-training, transformer-based LLMs have acquired broad linguistic capabili-
ties, yet require tailoring to perform specialized tasks effectively. Fine-tuning adjusts
the model’s parameters to enhance its proficiency in specific applications, this could be
sentiment analysis, question answering, or legal document review. This adjustment
occurs through additional training on a smaller, task-oriented dataset that embed
context and relevant nuances into the model.

2. Fine-Tuning Dataset
The selection of an appropriate fine-tuning dataset is key, as it must reflect the real-
world context of the task. It typically holds input-output pairs, that exemplify the
task-specific linguistic relationships, and hence guides the model in refining its capa-
bilities to be in line with task-specific demands.
3. Fine-Tuning Procedure
Fine-tuning involves few additional training epochs, for which the learning rate is
often much lower than during pre-training. This ensures that the model’s pre-trained
weights are refined rather than drastically altered, which helps in preserving the
general language understanding capabilities, while still adapting to the specific new
tasks. The optimal duration of fine-tuning, along with specific learning rates and
other hyperparameters, is typically determined through iterative testing and valida-
tion against a performance benchmark.
4. Overfitting Considerations
A significant challenge in fine-tuning is avoiding overfitting, particularly when the
fine-tuning dataset is relatively small. Techniques such as dropout, early stopping, or
introducing L2 regularization are commonly employed. These methods help to ensure
that the model generalizes well to new, unseen data, rather than simply memorizing
the fine-tuning dataset.
3.2
Tokenization and Prompting
With attention-based transformers, highly complex data can be processed in order
to model and simulate intricate problems which, up until now, were generally only
solvable by humans. Attention is what makes the transformer-based LLMs work as
they do, but they are useless without a method for receiving data in a way which,
while being computationally applicable, retains complexity of the source data.
Tokenization is the method that allows for encapsulating complexities in data, while
converting it to a uniform format that can be eﬀiciently processed in the transformer.
It is important on a practical level, and some of the fundamentals of tokenization are
also employed across different modalities, when for example encoding visual informa-
tion into transformer architectures.
On a conceptual level, everything inside the LLMs is essentially attention, and for
them to function well on complex tasks, requires structuring information in such a
way that it becomes manageable for the model. Tokenization in this context acts
similarly to cognitive processes of segmenting perception into interpretable units, like
the human mind breaking down complex speech into words and phrases, tokenization
breaks down text into discrete, processable units. How and with which granularity
the input data is tokenized determines how finely it can be understood, and hence

22

changes how the input space is perceived by the attention mechanisms of the LLM.
The process of tokenization, how tokens are defined and used, can hence significantly
influence how an LLM works, and in turn the LLMs’ performance and ability to un-
derstand and generate coherent, contextually appropriate text.
Tokenization is practically applied in this project as a data pre-processing step, in
which tokens can be words, subwords, or even single characters, depending on the
granularity required for the specific NLP task. For LLMs such as GPT-4, subword
tokenization is utilized, in which each token can represent parts of a word. The fol-

3.2.1
Introduction to Prompts and Prompt Engineering
LLMs’ ability to generate meaningful and contextually appropriate responses rely
heavily on the design and structure of the prompts they are given. Where attention
allows the LLM to work dynamically within a complex textual input space, and tok-
enization allows for encoding the relevant data into the LLM, prompting is the way
LLMs are told on what to focus within the tokenized input space.
Prompt engineering as it is referred to, is essential in all practical LLM applications,
in order to make them reliably output relevant responses given an input text from
a user or other entity/system, a query. In the paper ”Unleashing the potential of
prompt engineering in Large Language Models: a comprehensive review” (Chen et.
al. October 2023) [5], prompts and prompt engineering is reviewed in detail. More
advanced prompting schemes, relating primarily to enforcing ReAct-like agentic be-
haviour, are explained later in section 3.6.4 on page 43.
When receiving an input query, ie. a specific question or task, it is usually given
as a part of a larger more general set of instructions, the entirety of which is refereed
to as the prompt. Looking at an example can help convey what a prompt does and
why it is important.

Practical aspects of State of the Art (SOTA) LLMs used in this project are impor-

For everyone not working directly with Artificial Intelligence (AI), Large Language
Models (LLMs) are synonymous with ChatGPT and Microsoft Copilot, both using
OpenAI’s Generative Pre-trained Transformer (GPT) models. However, the GPT-4
model is as of 2024 just one of several foundation (large, general-purpose) models
which have similar performance.
All such foundation LLMs have several defining
characteristics, yet are typically distinguished in terms of model size, ie. number of
trainable parameters, context window sizes and modality types. Two more worth
mentioning are whether the given LLM is open-sourced or not, and whether it can
run on a local machine, both factors having implications primarily related to devel-
opment and productionalization.
Context window size, modality types and especially model size are limiting factors,
with several key implications and considerations for the present educational context.
3.3.1
Context Window Sizes
Context window size in LLMs define the amount of total tokenized data (usually text,
but can be sound, image etc.), the model can consider at one time when processing
inputs in order to generate responses. This data is the entirety of which the attention
can encompass in one prompted sequence, and using attention windows these will
slide inside the context window. The context window size is denoted in number of
tokens, typically in the thousands.
The limitation of context window size plays a crucial role in the model’s ability to
carry out certain tasks. Some tasks may require a model which can read large sets of
long-term memory or extensive context, in which cases smaller context-size models
may fall short. GPT-4 Turbo and GPT-3.5 Turbo for instance, have different context
window sizes of 128k and 16k respectively [26]. Putting this into perspective, the
number of tokens in ”Deep Learning” by Ian Goodfellow is roughly 470k tokens in
total, meaning only around 38% of the book would fit into the context window of
GPT-4, and only 3.4% in GPT-3.5.
For an LLM to utilize task-critical data, this could be recent news articles, such data
must fit inside its context window, otherwise it will not be able to process it all in one
prompted sequence. If there is too much data, say the entirety of ”Deep Learning” is
needed, clever techniques such as Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), described
later in section 3.5 on page 35, can be used to retrieve the most relevant bits when
they are specifically needed.
Some models are specifically being promoted in terms of their context window size,
and this is especially true with Google’s Gemini 1.5 [17], which currently has the
largest context window of 2 million tokens.
With such a large context window,

3.3.2
Modalities
Modalities of LLMs refer to the different types of data the models can process and
generate. LLMs have been predominantly focused on textual data, however recent
advancements have seen a shift towards multimodal capabilities, allowing these mod-
els to handle a combination of text, images, audio, and sometimes even video (which
are pictures in rapid succession..).
In the endeavour of education, a model that is able to understand meaning from a
figure, for example presented in course material relating to a STEM subject of inter-
est, will inherently be much more potent in teaching scenarios, as many concepts in
STEM are conveyed well visually. With multiple modalities, an LLM-based teaching
tool could for instance analyze a graph presented in a textbook, or a video demon-
stration of a physics experiment, and generate explanatory text, questions, or further
visualizations to aid student understanding.
In most LLMs, handling other modalities, such as extracting information out of a
graph, is handled by separate sub-models working in tandem with the text-based
model.
However, with the release of GPT-4o which saw an increase in eﬀiciency compared to
GPT-4, one of the main contributors to this increase was its ability to handle different
modalities inside the same neural network (model). Handling multiple modalities in
the same model is achieved through a sophisticated architecture that pre-processes
images and video, as well as audio, by encoding them into tokens usable by the same
model that handles text.
The exact details of visual and audio encoding are not
publicly available.
For reasons mainly related to current feasibility of implementation, handling mul-
tiple modalities as inputs is not explored in this project. Visual material as outputs,
in the form of code-generated plots, are part of this project.

3.4
Memory and Personal Memory
In order to make LLMs work in educational settings, they must be conversational in
nature. For that, memory is critical.
Memory, ie. the ability to recall past experiences, is a fundamental aspect of human
cognition. In humans, memory involves complex biological processes, which allow for
storage and retrieval of information over time. For machines, and in the context of
LLMs, the concept of memory operates quite differently, but is crucial for their ability
to interact intelligently and contextually with, users over the span of one conversa-
tion, or several.
Though memory can be thought of as simply maintaining all past information in a
given setting, real memory is not stringent in nature, as different previous events
will inherently be more or less important to understanding current events. To ac-
commodate this crucial notion, personal memory is introduced, serving as one of the
backbones of personalized learning.
3.4.1
Short-Term and Long-Term Memory
Using the term ”memory” with LLMs can be misleading, as it suggests that these
models remember previous interactions and outcomes like humans do. Each interac-
tion with an LLM is from the offset stateless, hence it will not retain past interactions,
neither across a single conversation or multiple, unless explicitly designed to do so.
To achieve a semblance of memory, all relevant past information must be included
in the present prompt, which typically means that for an LLM to ”remember” pre-
vious interactions, conversations or details about the user, their contents must be
included in the prompt for the current interaction. If the context window size, ie.
the model’s capacity for information at one time, is too small to fit the entire conver-
sation, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) setups, explained in section 3.5 on
page 35, can be employed to retrieve only the most relevant memories.
Short-term memory is what enables the LLM systems presented in this project to
maintain context over a sequence of interactions within the same conversation. This
is crucial for maintaining the flow and relevance of ongoing dialogue, highly impor-
tant for any conversational system.
Implementing and utilizing short-term memory is straight forward; Each user query
and appertaining agent response is temporarily stored, updated for each new interac-
tion, and iteratively fed back into the prompt of the agent. This is in practice done
using the LangChain memory function Conversation Buffer Memory, which acts like
a suﬀiciently large sliding window over past interactions.
Long-term memory, in contrast to short-term memory, involves the storage of in-
formation over extended periods of time and across conversations. However, other
than that it represents exactly the same idea, which in turn poses a problem. Con-
versational systems are on the basis of just a few conversations likely to be given
==PAGE:43==
3.4 Memory and Personal Memory
33
long-term memory that is both too long for the LLMs’ context window, and sparse in
information. This can be partially circumvented by either using RAG or summarizing
previous conversations, but what we really want is to keep only the information that
can enhance subsequent conversations. This functionally means building a digital
representation of the user.
3.4.2
Personal Memory
Personal memory is essentially the center piece of enabling personalized learning, as
personalization of content requires the identification of a student’s key personal traits,
such as interests and preferences. Personal memory goes beyond conventional mem-
ory, ie. straight forward maintaining past information, by dynamically attributing
information from conversations to the specific student. By essentially simulating a
model of the students’ learning journey, LLM-based systems can be given user-specific
information from their personal memory. Hence, this provides a more nuanced un-
derstanding and retention of user-specific information, enhancing contextuality and
relevance of LLMs’ responses over multiple conversations.
For personalized learning, the information that personal memory can be used to dy-
namically maintain are many-fold, pertaining to the student. However, to give con-
text, the following are some especially interesting aspects of a student in a learning
setting:
• Knowledge base: What has the student previously learned, and what are they
struggling with?
• Learning preferences: What are the most beneficial types of learning material
for the student?
• Inherent strengths: What is the student good at, bad at, and what topics or
types of interactions enforce their learning?
Identifying such aspects of a student requires a reasoning system that can observe
interactions between a student and a teaching entity, and infer personal memories
from these. The value of attaining such key information also lies in the fact that
users, and in this case students, do not necessarily know themselves. Hence, while
an LLM system could simply ask a student what their learning preferences are, they
might not know.
Instead, personal memory is build around inference, based in what is refereed to as
formative events/interactions. For any interaction, responses and subsequent interac-
tions may be directly attributed to that interaction, say an LLM explains the concept
of moment, and the student next asks about inertia. Such an interaction is formative
in the sense that it taught the student suﬀiciently about one subject, for them to be
able to ask a related question.
Likewise, a formative event may consist in a series of interactions, in which a student
is trying to learn a given topic, say tensor computation. Responding sub-optimally
==PAGE:44==
34

to an explanation in which tensors are written in mathematical formula, and then re-
sponding positively to a new explanation containing a coded example, will essentially
be a formative event in regards to learning preferences.
Progression towards advanced personal memory will bridge the gap between artifi-
cial and human cognitive processes, and be a key pillar in digital twinning, ie. the
ability to create virtual representations of users.

3.5
Retrieval and RAG
Fundamental concepts of transformer-based LLMs have been established, and put
into practical context given the current landscape of SOTA LLMs like GPT-4. The
core idea of personal memory, as well as more conventional notions of memory, have
further been introduced as means to enabling advanced conversational systems, which
for this project seek to dynamically attain user-specific understanding.
If these conversational systems are to be utilized for personalized learning, they need
the ability to access and utilize external data, not inherent to the trained LLM. In
order to fully incorporate personal memory into personalized learning systems, such
dynamic, external information must be retrieved and incorporated into the systems
when relevant.
Information retrieval in LLMs, and the ability to generate responses based on this
information, encapsulated in the concept of Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG),
has represented a paradigm shift by combining the generative power of transformer-
based LLMs with information retrieval systems. RAG solves the problem of LLM
context window size, as mentioned earlier, and addresses one key limitation in stan-
dalone, foundational LLMs; their reliance solely on internal representations and pa-
rameters for generating responses. In generating a response, RAG utilizes retrieval
of external relevant data, integrating fresh and context-specific knowledge that is not
necessarily stored within the model’s parameters. Hence, the attention mechanism
inside the LLM is primed further towards the correct contextual understanding of
the given task, as it is exposed to information which has not been trained on.
The integration of RAG into LLMs necessitates an eﬀicient mechanism for storing
and retrieving smaller pieces of a given document. This is done using vector storage
and retrieval, an essential part of RAG systems, which allows for the augmentation
of available context by dynamically sourcing information from a corpus of external
documents.
3.5.1
Embedding Functions for RAG
Embedding functions are are typically derived from the pre-trained layers of an LLM
or a specialized embedding model, and are tasked with transforming text into high-
dimensional vector representations in latent space. These vectors capture the seman-
tic essence of the text, enabling effective document retrieval by comparing vectors
of different documents, in order to identify which ones are most relevant within the
database.
In this project, OpenAI’s embedding function is utilized [23].
Embedding is not
overly complicated, but needs to be done well, as more descriptive embeddings makes
retrieving relevant information easier.
Key considerations for using OpenAI’s embedding function include the dimensions
==PAGE:46==
36
3 Methodology
of the generated vectors and the granularity of the text input which is controlled
primarily through how a document is split. The size of document splits/chunks, ie.
length of segment of text each vector represents, varies depending on the application,
and is important for balancing retrieval accuracy and computational eﬀiciency. The
selection of an appropriate chunk size ensures that each embedded vector captures
a useful level of semantic information without being overly specific or too generic.
Smaller chunks may lead to a higher resolution of understanding and finer retrieval
granularity, which is beneficial for complex queries where precise information is nec-
essary. Conversely, larger chunks can encapsulate more comprehensive information,
reducing the vector space’s complexity, and improving computational eﬀiciency.
3.5.2
Vector Storage
Given the embedding function, vector storage works the following way:
1. Document Splitting:
To manage and utilize larger documents eﬀiciently, this could be a PDF containing
”Deep Learning” by Ian Goodfellow, they are first split into manageable chunks, each
typically consisting of several hundred up to a couple thousand tokens. This process
can be formalized as the following, where C represents the set of chunks derived from
the document, and each ci is a contiguous segment of the document:
C = {c1, c2, ..., cn}
2. Embedding the Chunks:
Once the document is split, each chunk is embedded into high-dimensional vector
space using a given embedding function, for this project provided by OpenAI. These
embeddings serve as compact representations of the chunks’ semantic content.
If
Embed(·) denotes the embedding function, the embedding of a chunk ci is given by:
vi = Embed(ci)
3. Vector Storage:
The embeddings are now stored in a vector storage system, also known as a vec-
tor database. For this project, the open source vector storage system ChromaDB
is utilized, Github: https://github.com/chroma-core/chroma. Each vector vi is
indexed for eﬀicient retrieval, V is hence the set of all of the documents’ embedded
chunks:
V = {v1, v2, , vn}

3.5.4
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)
With the ability to embed and store data in vector storage, and further retrieve
chunks of the data which relate most to a given retrieval query, Retrieval-Augmented
Generation (RAG) can now be carried out.
As explained earlier about prompts, see section 3.2.1 on page 23, the output of an
LLM is altered based on the input it gets. In essence, this is the last part that makes
up RAG, in which the LLM can base its response on the expanded prompt which
now contains information that it has not been trained on. The fundamental workflow
of RAG hence involves two main steps, firstly the formulation of a search query and
retrieval of relevant documents, and secondly the generation of responses based on
the retrieved content together with the user query and additional prompted context.
RAG represents the first radical divide from conventional LLM behaviour, as it entails
agentic behaviour. Agents, LLMs that are able to interact with external systems, are
the fundamental enablers of making teaching-appropriate LLM-based systems possi-
ble. Agents and advanced agent capabilities used in this project are described a little
later in section 3.6 on page 40.

3.6
Agents and Agentic Behaviour
The goals of this project consist in utilizing LLMs to infer personal education-related
memory, described in section 3.4 on page 32, and use this to guide the generation of
personalized learning material. In order for LLMs to be used in accomplishing these
two overall goals, they need to be structured such that they can intelligently process
the given task. They must do this while using not only learned, inherent knowledge,
but also external data through Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) as explained
in section 3.5 on page 35, in order to provide solutions. For handling these complex
tasks, LLMs need the ability to reason on how they will solve tasks, while being able
to utilize external systems, and hence they are set up as autonomous agents.
Implementing LLMs as agents introduces dynamic, autonomous units capable of
performing complex tasks through reasoning and acting strategies, tool usage, and
advanced prompt engineering. The technical details of these agents, their actions,
and the methodologies employed to enhance their functionality, are important to
understand, as agents represent the core functional foundation for enabling teaching-
appropriate LLM-based systems.
3.6.1
ReAct Agents
ReAct Agents, conceptualized in the ReAct framework [42], allow for the application
of LLMs on interactive tasks requiring both reasoning and acting, ie. active decision-
making.
ReAct-like agents are designed to synergize reasoning processes with actionable tasks,
enabling a dynamic interaction between thought generation, ie. the agent thinking
of and deciding on what actions to take in order to give a good response to the user
query, and action execution, ie. the agent taking that action and observing its out-
put. In theory, this synthesis allows agents to adapt to new information, reconsider
their strategies, and navigate complex problem spaces much more effectively than
traditional, static LLM setups.
In relation to the three research questions, which all revolve around creating responses
on the basis of personal memory as well as external data like course material, the ca-
pabilities of ReAct-like agents are key. An LLM-based system that can handle the
tasks presented in this project needs to be able to consider how it will respond, take
actions such as retrieving data and generating code, while aligning these to the given
student, in one or several reasoning steps.

3.6.2
Agents in Practice
ReAct agents operate through a structured cycle comprising perception, reasoning,
and action. The technical workflow of these agents in practical applications can be
broken down into the following key steps:
1. Perception:
Initially, the agent gathers data from its current environment, ie. everything included
in the prompt as well as information about external actions which can be take. The
purpose of this step is to collect all relevant information that will inform the agent’s
decision-making process.
2. Contextual Understanding:
After perceiving its current environment and possible actions to take, the agent uses
its pre-trained LLM knowledge to understand the context of the query and task at
hand. This step helps align the agent’s response mechanisms appropriately.
3. Reasoning and Thought:
Here, the agent processes the information to formulate a response or series of actions.
This involves the agent assessing various possible actions, predicting outcomes, and
making decisions based on its understood goals and objectives.
4. Action Formulation:
Once the reasoning process is complete, the agent has formulated an action plan.
This could be generating a text response, writing a retrieval query for RAG, writing
a piece of code to be executed etc. The action is directly derived from the reasoning
process and is aimed at fulfilling the query/task effectively.
==PAGE:52==
42
3 Methodology
5. Action Execution:
The planned action is now executed. The execution could be delivery of the text-
based answer to the user, calling and receiving a retrieved chunk of data from RAG,
getting an output from the executed code and so on. Execution results seek to be
precise and aligned with the expected outcomes as reasoned in the previous steps.
6. Reiteration or Final Answer:
Agents may iteratively reason and act, before finally providing a response to the user,
essentially looping steps 3-5. Having for example retrieved a chunk of relevant data,
this could be a page from relevant STEM course material, the agent may reason for
and subsequently execute a summation of this piece of text, before responding the
student with this summary. When the agent determines it has an appropriate answer
to the user’s query, it will respond accordingly.
This iterative behaviour can be refereed to as reflection-style, explored further in
relation to multi-agent systems in section 3.7 on page 45.
3.6.3
Tools
In ReAct agent frameworks, the effectiveness and scope of actions are greatly influ-
enced by the array of tools available to them. These tools enable agents to extend
their capabilities beyond native functionality, allowing them to interact with and ma-
nipulate their environment in more sophisticated ways. The primary tools utilized in
this project are described below.
RAG as a tool:
Besides use-cases where LLM-based systems have only the single purpose of retriev-
ing and generating on external data, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), as
described in section 3.5 on page 35, is typically employed as a tool. Agents can call
a RAG-enabled tool in cases where external information is beneficial, without being
required to do so for interactions in which it can suﬀiciently respond to a query using
internal knowledge.
Search as a tool:
Retrieving up-to-date information from the web is an action which can enable gen-
eration based on relevant information that is not available internally or in present
vector-store. Some systems in this project utilize search as a separate tool, via the
”Tavily” search engine (https://tavily.com/), which is implemented as a tool that
very alike RAG takes a search query and returns relevant material.
Code execution:
For tasks that require computational solutions or visual data representations, ie.

Advanced prompting techniques are utilized in order to enable ReAct-like agents, and
much functionality lies specifically in having their prompts be a combination of con-

The term contextual prompting refers to incorporating outside context besides the
input query, such as a list of available tool and other external information which en-

desired in this project, short-term memory containing previous interactions for the

Dynamic prompting refers to the adaptive part of the prompt, which in real-time

In this framework, the LLM must hence at some point in its reasoning process pro-

task at hand. It then follows that thought up with specifying the tool it will use in

3.7
Multi-Agent Systems
With the introduction of agents, utilizing SOTA LLMs with memory and Retrieval-
Augmented Generation (RAG), the foundation upon which LLM-based systems are
able to carry out complex tasks has been established. However, singular autonomous
units such as ReAct-like agents face two major challenges:
• Overwhelming Task Complexity:
The underlying LLM needs to be capable enough that the attention mechanism
is able to correctly maintain all aspects of the task. Many tasks are complex to
such a degree that even the larger foundation models like GPT-4 have trouble
encapsulating them. This is seen to be the case for single-agent systems in this
projects’ result section.
The human approach to solving complex tasks is to divide them into sub-tasks,
which can be completed separately and/or iteratively. While this is somewhat
similar to single-agent behaviour, currently even larger models are prone to lose
track of steps in a solution process.
• LLM Size:
For the purpose of using agentic systems in actual products, these ideally need
to be runnable on-device in the future. Running on-device has several benefits,
such as cost, security and accessibility, which are important to especially the
prospects of employing personal memory for students. If agentic systems are
to be used for maintaining personal memory, such systems must be local and
constantly available for the given student.
If agentic systems are to be run on-device, they need to be based on small
enough foundation models.
In the paper ”More Agents Is All You Need” (Li et. al. February 2024) [22] described
in related work section 2.6 on page 11, it is shown how task-solving performance
using smaller models can be improved, for a variety of tasks, simply by employing
more agents for solving the same task simultaneously. Taking these multiple agents
and more explicitly having each carry out sub-tasks, like with ”ChatDev” [28] or
”MetaGPT” [16], is further shown to effectively guide the solving of complex tasks.
The purpose of utilizing multi-agent systems is hence enabling the utilization of
smaller LLMs, GPT-3.5 in this project, for carrying out personalized learning. If
smaller LLMs, in multi-agent systems, can be made to perform well for generating
personalized learning material, they can also be used for simulating personal memory.
By being able to use less computationally heavy foundation models for running agen-
tic systems, progress towards running personal learning systems on-device is made.

3.7.1
System 1 and System 2 Thinking
Human cognition is a highly complex science, and still much more obscure than the
processes that LLMs undergo using attention to generate a response. However, in
more psychological terms, the notion that humans have two levels of processing in-
formation and making decisions, based on the complexity of the task at hand, is a
widely accepted theory. Popularized in the book ”Thinking, Fast and Slow” (Daniel
Kahnemann 2011) [18], it is theorized that there are two ”systems” for thinking:
It states; ”System 1” is fast, instinctive and emotional, ”System 2” is slower, more
deliberative, and more logical.”
As LLMs are getting increasingly better, user’s expectations in relation to aspects
such as shorter response times are continuously elevated. However, humans are not
expected to make all their decisions using only quick judgments and assessments
based on accumulated experiential knowledge, ie. ”system 1” thinking, so why should
LLMs?
Instead, humans engage in detailed analysis, and employ rational thought processes
to solve complex problems, ie. they take their time using ”system 2” thinking in order
to consistently reach the correct conclusion.
This is highly analogous to what complex agentic systems, and especially these multi-
agent systems do. They take vastly longer, spending many tokens, and likely reiterate
their findings numerous times, in order to arrive at responses which are more refined.
Sometimes, the users of agentic systems will be willing to wait for an extended period
of time, in order to receive better answers for questions which are important to get
right.
3.7.2

responses, ie. their generative performance, through a continuous verbal reinforce-

response is iteratively refined based on critique given in each cycle. Reflection-style
agentic setups are particularly effective for tasks requiring high fidelity and alignment,
such as with personalizing learning material to specific student preferences.
This class of multi-agent system are in this project implemented according the the
ideas raised in the paper ”Reflexion: Language Agents with Verbal Reinforcement

3.7.4
Corrective RAG (CRAG)
The quality of RAG-enhanced responses depend heavily on the relevance of the re-
trieved documents. Conventional Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) approaches,
described in section 3.5 on page 35, will ideally retrieve the most relevant external
information, but this depends on which search query has been used in retrieval. Also,
the retrieved information will in any case be integrated indiscriminately, and this
may potentially compromise the quality and reliability of the RAG generated out-
puts, whenever retrieved documents are in fact not relevant.
Ranking algorithms and continual updating of document embeddings are two ways of
enhancing and maintaining high relevance retrieval, however more advanced methods
are utilized in this project in order to enhance relevance of retrieved documents.
Corrective Retrieval Augmented Generation (CRAG), introduced in the paper ”Cor-
rective Retrieval Augmented Generation” (Yan et al., January 2024) [40], is incor-
porated into the multi-agent system developed in this project. CRAG is designed
to address key shortcomings associated with the retrieval process, in particular sce-
narios where the retrieval may yield inaccurate or irrelevant results. It does so by
incorporating an agentic retrieval evaluator, and three sets of actions to take given
the quality of the retrieval. The setup of CRAG can be seen in figure 3.9.
The retrieval evaluator assesses the quality of documents retrieved during the RAG
process, evaluating their relevance and accuracy in relation to the input query. This
evaluator utilizes a lightweight model, in the case of CRAG a T5-large [30], which is
fine-tuned to predict the relevance of document-query pairs, and generates a relevance
score that dictates subsequent actions within the CRAG framework. By thresholding
the relevance score, a given retrieved chunk is categorized as correct when clearly
above the threshold, incorrect when clearly below the threshold, or ambiguous when

in and around the threshold. An assessment of correct triggers knowledge refinement,

3.8
Evaluation and Experiment Design
With all that has been presented so far, actual systems for personalized learning can
emerge. This entails attention-based transformer-LLMs, which can retrieve external
information and carry out actions using external systems as tools, while maintaining
conversations in memory for conversational interactions. The research questions of
this project pertain to two main types of tasks, inferring personal memory and gen-
erating personalized learning material. For the first task, human inspection will be
used primarily, as the output of this task is short and feasible for human evaluation.
However, in order to evaluate the performance of systems generating personalized
learning material, a lot of intricacies must be dealt with.
This section explains how evaluation of the two material-generating systems, the
Teaching Agent System (TAS) and Teaching Multi-Agent System (TMAS) explained
in sections 3.10 on page 56 and 3.11 on page 65 respectively, is carried out. Doing
evaluation of systems producing long, complex outputs is a complex and novel task
in its own right, and best practices for doing so are not established as of 2024, even
according to Andrew Ng [24]. Doing evaluation for generated personalized learning
material took a lot of effort to make work suﬀiciently in this project.
3.8.1
Evaluation Approach
For most machine learning or deep learning systems, tasks are usually clear and per-
formance is straight forward to evaluate using computed metrics. For LLM systems
which seek to maximize performance in a concrete domain, this could be how well a
system is able to produce correct code, benchmarks like HumanEval [6] are utilized.
With such benchmarks, a data set is constructed to contain a number of concrete
tasks and their corresponding correct answer. A task could be to generate a Python
function that divides two numbers, to which the generated output can be verified
directly as being either correct, the generated code correctly divides two numbers, or
false if it does not.
For this project however, the outputs which the Teaching Agent System (TAS) and
Teaching Multi-Agent System (TMAS) must produce cannot be benchmarked directly,
as they are not verifiable in the same sense as above. There is no definitive best output
when it comes to generating personalized teaching material, and verifying whether an
agentic system suﬀiciently fulfills the goal of, for example, rewriting a piece of course
material to focus more on mathematical formulation, is subject to scrutiny. Further,
it is inherently hard to score such an output, in order to determine not just if but
also how well the system solved its present task.
With these challenges in mind, the straight forward method of evaluating the TAS
and TMAS simply entails having human experts read and evaluate all outputs/con-
versations.
This is potentially feasible, however both impractical and a deterring
factor in terms of working with such systems in the future. In this regard, agentic

Human expert evaluations are used in the GPT-4-based evaluations through few-shot

3.8.2
Evaluation Approaches for the LLM-based Teaching Systems
The use of LLM evaluators is being researched actively, such as in the paper ”Replac-
ing Judges with Juries: Evaluating LLM Generations with a Panel of Diverse Models”
(Verga et. al. April 2024) [36]. The gist of this paper is that it improves evaluation
precision by having several smaller GPT-3.5 LLMs work in concert as a jury, instead
of using just a single GPT-4 model as a judge. This specific finding is not utilized,
however inspiration is taken in terms of the first of its three main approaches to do-
ing evaluation using an LLM, besides referring to the evaluating agent as a ”judge”.
Similar approaches are explored in several other papers, including [19], [44] and [43].
The three ways of scoring an agent output are as follows:
• Single-point Scoring
The Judge is tasked with rating the quality of a single model output, indepen-
dently of any comparison. The Judge’s prompt includes written instructions on
how the grading should be performed, and the rating is hence based solely on
the Judge’s internal representation of what a quality output is. The scoring is
done on a scale from bad to good, numerically as a grade between 0 and 1.
• Reference-based Scoring
The Judge is provided a ’gold’ reference, which contains the information that
should be included in the model output. Scoring the output is then just like
single-point scoring, but grounded in a reference output.
• Pair-wise Scoring
In pair-wise scoring, the goal is to choose which of two outputs is better. Given
outputs a and b on the same generative task, from two separate models A and B,
the Judge compares their outputs, generating a preference score. This score can
vary based on the use-case, but it is often a three or five point scale pertaining
to 0 = a > b, 0.5 = a ≈b, 1 = a < b.
From these three approaches, which are here considered best practice, it is clear that
evaluating the complex outputs of agents is a limited endeavour. For this project, only
single-point and pair-wise scoring is relevant, as currently there are no gold standard,
call them label, personalized learning materials. These can not be course material,
as this is not personalized, so even though the same information is conveyed, it may

have a completely different format compared to the original.
Single-point scoring is ultimately utilized in this project, as it enables evaluation of
outputs which are inherently subjective to some degree. Pair-wise scoring could have
been utilized for comparing two different models, but this was at-the-time not feasible
using available frameworks such as LangSmith enacting cloud storage for the model
outputs.
3.8.3
Few-Shot Learning through Prompting
As mentioned earlier, few-shot learning/prompting will be utilized in order to guide
the Judge in evaluating the Teaching Agent System (TAS) and Teaching Multi-Agent
System (TMAS) using single-point scoring.
Few-shot learning, or prompting, is especially beneficial in scenarios where data is
sparse or as in this case, when the given agentic LLM is expected to perform under
conditions where examples are limited. Three few-shot examples are given, each of
which represent a conversation which has been evaluated in accordance to the specific
evaluation approach and criteria, by a human expert. Many more few-shot examples
would have been ideal, but the evaluation approach was ultimately limited by com-
putational and API-pertaining constraints.
Alternatively, fine-tuning could have been used. By tuning the Judge to the evalu-
ation task, the LLM would not require examples for improving evaluation accuracy,
as these would be tuned into the inherent knowledge of the model. For this project,
fine-tuning a GPT-4 or GPT-4o model has not been possible, as it is currently only
available for GPT-3.5 models through OpenAI’s API, and turned out to be an other-

3.10.1
Purpose of the TAS: Inclusive Learning
The Teaching Agent System (TAS) is built with the purpose of fulfilling some of the
ideas raised in the paper ”A framework for inclusive AI learning design for diverse
learners” (Song et. al. June 2024) [34], described in related work section 2.2 on page
8. It further seeks to accommodate the notion made in ”An astonishing regularity
in student learning rate” (Koedinger et. al February 2023) [20] also described in the
aforementioned section, by being able to re-iterate the same ideas multiple times to
a given student for their learning benefit.
Specifically, the main goals of the TAS prescribe that it can do the following three
things well, all three of which align with the Universal Design for Learning (UDL)
guidelines shown in figure 3.11. The TAS needs to exhibit capabilities that clearly
indicate how these types of LLM-based systems can carry out personalized learning.
Personalized Learning Material:
In accordance to the UDL presented in [34], one of the important capabilities for an
AI-powered teaching system is enabling multiple means of representation.
Hence, the TAS needs to be able to generate different variations of learning material,
personalized to a student. To narrow this personalization down in the scope of this
project, it is decided that the representations which the TAS must move between are
on a spectrum between more math-focused to more code-focused. Figure 3.12 visually
represent the idea that for STEM students in maths and computer science, mathe-
matical formulas and code/coded examples represent two different ends of a spectrum
on which a given student is placed based on preferences and inherent understanding.
Figure 3.12: Computer Science Learner Spectrum Idea.
Enforcing Engagement:
In accordance to the UDL, AI-powered tools should engage students in multiple means
of action and expression. A key aspect of learning consists in attaining the ability
to explain and contextualize subjects, and use acquired knowledge to engage in tasks
which require the application of said knowledge. The notion pertaining here is that
one of the best ways to learn something is to teach it. In conventional teaching, stu-
dents are prompted to demonstrate their knowledge and skills in various ways, from
simple questions/quizzes to carrying out projects, allowing them to demonstrate and
strengthen their understanding of a given subject.
The Teaching Agent System (TAS) should be able to engage students, by posing
questions and asking them to explain their understanding. Besides enhancing the
==PAGE:68==
58
3 Methodology
effectiveness of student’s personalized learning, this enables assessing their under-
standing of a given subject, and helping decide what subjects to further expand or
repeat.
Re-formulating and Re-contextualizing Learning Material, Avoiding Rep-
etitions:
In accordance to the UDL, and especially notions made in ”An astonishing regularity
in student learning rate” [20], student’s capabilities for learning (diﬀicult) subjects is
not pre-determined by their inherent ability in a given overall topic/category of sub-
jects. Instead, according to the paper, it is largely a matter of enabling the exposure
of a given subject to the student a suﬀicient number of times. In other words, if a
student can be engaged in the same subject multiple times, they are likely able to
learn it.
Hence, the TAS needs to be able to re-iterate subjects multiple times without re-
peating content, while dynamically adjusting the means of representation to fit the
current state of the students learning path. One of the biggest potentials within AI-
powered learning tools lies in their contant availability, with which student interest
and motivation can much more effectively be sustained.

3.11
The Teaching Multi-Agent System (TMAS)
In order to answer the second research question, see section 1.2.2 on page 5, every-

Teaching Multi-Agent System (TMAS).
The second research question states:
RQ2: Can smaller foundation LLMs be made to achieve enhanced generative,
education-appropriate capabilities, by collaborating in multi-agent systems?
The TMAS is a reflexion-style multi-agent system, using solely GPT-3.5 LLMs, which

3.11.1
The purpose of the TMAS
The purpose of the Teaching Multi-Agent System (TMAS) itself is essentially the
same as for the Teaching Agent System (TAS), explained in section 3.10.1 on page
57. Both the TAS and TMAS are conversational generative systems, built to deliver
personalized learning in accordance to key notions of the Universal Design for Learn-
ing (UDL).
However, for the sake of this project, the purpose of the TMAS extends further. The
Teaching Multi-Agent System (TMAS) is built specifically to investigate how smaller
and cheaper LLMs in collaboration can be made to have similar performance (or ide-
ally better), compared to single-agent systems based on larger LLMs. The scope of
this endeavour lies in the idea that agentic systems for personalized learning must be
light enough to run on-device, and hence employing smaller LLMs will be striding in
that direction.
3.11.2
TMAS Architecture
The final Teaching Multi-Agent System (TMAS) is built on a Reflexion-style setup,
the architecture of which is shown in figure 3.13. This system has access to the same

48, is a multi-agent system specifically built for retrieval of external data.
The architecture consists of the following agents:
1. plan_agent takes in the query and formulates a plan for the draft with which
the query will be answered.
2. research_plan is the CRAG system, which takes the plan and retrieves rele-
vant external information, which can be used for generating the initial draft.

3.12
The Personal Memory Agent System (PMAS)
In order to answer the third and final research question, see section 1.2.3 on page
5, agentic capabilities employed in order to simulate personal memory. The system
which has been named the Personal Memory Agent System (PMAS) is built for this
purpose.
The third research question states:
RQ3: Can LLM-based systems enable personal memory simulation for stu-
dents interacting with agentic learning systems?
The PMAS is a GPT-4-based single-agentic system, built to be integrated in Lang-
Mem for continuous personal memory simulation.
3.12.1
Purpose of the PMAS
Having personal memory for a given student is sought to enable personalization of
learning material, as performed by the Teaching Agent System (TAS) and Teaching
Multi-Agent System (TMAS). Hence, the purpose of the Personal Memory Agent
System (PMAS) is to infer student-specific learning-relevant traits from interactions
with the TAS or TMAS.
The PMAS does this by identifying formative events/interactions, as described in
section 3.4.2. Interactions are formative when they represent a state-change for the
fundamental understanding of the student in relation to learning STEM subjects.
Mainly, this PMAS as developed in this project pursues inference of the following
types of personal memory:
• Learning Preferences:
Which types of interactions and modes of explaining a subject does the student
responds to the best, and hence learn from the best.
• Learning Shortcomings:
Shortcomings and key misconceptions in the student’s understanding of a given
subject can be identified, providing potential focus points for future interactions.
• Learning Interests:
Identifies which subjects the student has interests in, in order to better trigger
their attention and engagement.
Of these main purposes, the personal memory simulation relating to learning prefer-
ences was the main focus, as this is directly usable in the TAS and TMAS. Evaluation
of the PMAS focuses on inferring learning preferences from conversations with the
TAS.
==PAGE:80==

3 Methodology
3.12.2
PMAS Architecture
In practice, the PMAS takes in conversations, simulates personal memory, and ap-
pends these to the student’s personal learning profile.
The entire PMAS hence has two components:
• Formative event identifier:
A GPT-4 agent, with RAG as a tool, which given a new conversation with the
same student, reads the present interactions between the student and teaching
system, and extracts key information as personal memory.
• Append LangMem system:
The key information, denoted as ”facts” in the PMAS, are appended to a con-
tinuously expanded student-profile. The facts contained in a student profile are
then retrieved upon initiating subsequent conversations, and implemented into
the prompt of of the relevant teaching system, such as the TAS. The facts in
the student profile are available to the PMAS, such that it can continuously
simulate personal memory on the basis of already developed facts about the
student.

5.5.1
Issues with Consistency in Agentic Behaviour
Having ReAct-like agents utilize tools in-between reasoning steps proved more chal-
lenging than first anticipated, and the results show that the different GPT models
behaved differently in this regard.
GPT-3.5 frequently hallucinates, which is not only detrimental in terms of yielding
satisfactory conversational outputs, but also contributed to a disrupted agentic be-
haviour and hence practical application of the system. For longer conversations, with
short-term memory growing to be an ever larger part of the GPT-3.5-given prompt,
this problem became more frequent, enforcing the notion that LLMs become more
==PAGE:142==
132

hallucinatory as the length of their input grows in size and complexity. Smaller mod-
els will inherently possess a lesser ability for managing long, complex instructions, as
their attention mechanisms are inherently less capable in this regard. Hence, if the
problem of non-compliance with agentic behaviour is solely founded by input length,
methods like summarization of short-term memory could as future improvement be
employed to alleviate the issue. However, this problem is also present in larger mod-
els.
During initial testing and building learning systems, the newly introduced GPT-4o
model was seen as an option for employing the same capabilities as with GPT-4, but

5.5.2
RAG Utilization Importance
In results section 4.2.4 on page 4.2.4, it is shown how the ReAct-like TAS is inconsis-
tent in tool usage, despite being given instructions for when to utilize said tools. Tool
usage is important, partly because it is an integral part of solving some tasks like
executing code examples, but further in order to mitigate hallucinations, especially
pertaining to Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG).
Retrieving external information, by calling RAG-based tools, represents a key part
of ensuring that a learning system like the TAS does not hallucinate on matters
which deal with factual knowledge. A GPT-4 model will, in any scenario where it
is not given external information, naturally resort to its inherent representations for
conveying factual knowledge, when for example explaining technical subjects. These
inherent representations may be factually correct, but having generated its response
based on retrieved relevant information, such as course material pertaining to the
given subject, is a strong indication that the response will in fact be correct. With-
out utilizing RAG, responses of a personalized learning system may require more
thorough verification in terms of correctness, when employing such systems in real
applications.

5.5.3
Prompt Structuring and Wording
The results in 4.2.4 on page 4.2.4 show how tool usage is not just a given, but needs to
be enforced actively. This result sheds light on the importance of prompt formulation
and structuring, when using LLMs as agents.
In order to enforce Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) utilization, the experi-
ments showed three different archetypes of prompted instructions:
• Weak Instructions (recommendations, ”you should..”).
• Strong Instructions (guidelines, ”you MUST..”).
• Input Query Specified Instructions.
Changing the RAG instructions from weak to strong led to a large increase in consis-
tency of tool utilization, and giving instruction alongside the input query, ie. as part
of the student’s question, also hugely enforced RAG utilization.
This result is interesting, because it clearly shows how both wording and prompt
structure are hugely important to the reasoning and generative process of an agent.
The fact that these models are essentially attention computers prescribes that every-
thing in the prompt is naturally not perceived as being equally important, and the
amount of attention given to different parts of the prompt is in fact affected by rather
simple factors, such as wording and structure.
The underlying LLM will read the entire prompt, and have higher attention on specif-
ically the input query of the student, as everything else in the prompt essentially
directs its attention here. This notion explains why placing important instructions in
the same paragraph as the query makes the LLM more likely to actually follow those
instructions.
Strong instructions may alternatively be placed elsewhere in the prompt, and for en-
forcing RAG utilization in the TAS, its prompt was updated to include the strongly
worded; ”When explaining a subject, ALWAYS use the Retrieval Tool for retrieving
course-relevant material (...)”. The attention mechanism will put more focus on this
instruction, as it has through training learned that words like ”always”, ”you must”
etc. are stringent in nature, in contrast to more suggestive wording like ”you should”.
These insights entail that working with attention-based models can be problematic
in terms of getting consistent desirable behaviour, as they in some cases need specific
and possibly hard-to-determine wording. Yet, at the same time, it also implies that in
some cases their attention mechanisms will be suﬀiciently adaptable for circumvent-
ing any prompting deficiencies, enabling the completion of complex tasks regardless
of the quality of prompting.

5.6.1
Predefined vs. Undefined Solution Structures
In philosophical terms, the future potential of LLMs comes not from what it is known
to be able to do, but what it will be able to do, that is not yet known. More prac-

tically speaking, their potential lies in the idea that they are able to solve complex
tasks without needing to be specifically instructed in how to do so. Current LLMs
are however not this intelligent, and still require suitable instructions and multi-agent
setups to solve a given category of tasks. With predefined systems and sub-tasks for
individual agents, the solution path and structure is set in advance, and undefined
intelligent behaviour first materializes as the given task is being solved. This is for
example the current state of LLM-based software development, as shown with the
virtual software development company ”ChatDev” presented in ”ChatDev: Commu-
nicative Agents for Software Development” (Qian et. al. December 2023) [28].
In this regard, accommodating current LLM capabilities ultimately comes down to a
question of ”How freely do I need my agent(s) to operate?”. The solution to tasks such
as generating personalized teaching material is open ended, but no without structure
that can be determined in advance. For the Teaching Multi-Agent System (TMAS),
it is found that better learning material is generated after pre-defining the solution
structure further.
The TMAS is expanded to have three differently tasked agents, one which creates
outlines, another agent which retrieves outline-relevant material, and one which takes
these as inputs to create an initial draft, before running the reflexion-style critique-
update loop. This is shown to be beneficial to the system’s generative performance,
compared to having a less predefined solution structure, in which one agent essentially
must be solely responsible for doing all these three things.
Having a completely free solution structure makes for a simpler system, which ide-
ally avoids being confined by human assumptions about best practice for solving a
given task. However, until humans become clearly outsmarted by whatever SOTA
models are available, partly predefined solution structures may likely enforce desired
behaviour and predictability, without compromising performance.

5.8
Evaluation of LLM-Based Systems
With LLMs, an archetype of deep learning models has solidified in which the outputs
are of much higher complexity than the inputs. Let’s quickly explore this notion.
For most State of the Art (SOTA) deep learning based systems, their main purpose is
to take a complex input and return a simple output. An example of this is how image
classification models take an image, an input of high dimensionality and complexity,
and seek to return a simple output, which in the simplest case might be a binary
classification as to whether the object in the image is a hotdog or not. Another exam-
ple is how all forms of regression algorithms in essence seek to take in a larger set of
data, with potentially many dimensions, and produce single descriptive coeﬀicients,
ie. relating y target variable to X input variables. These types of methods could be
thought of as ’boiling down’ information.
LLMs essentially do the opposite, they ’blow up’ information. Their inherent ability
is to, given a ”simple” input in the form of a prompt, generate an output that exists
as part of a very large probabilistic output space. Given a query that simply requests
”Write a novel about a prince who lives on a comet, together with his rose!”, an LLM
can generate pages of content. These models can also be used for summarizing large
corpora of text, but this task still adheres to blowing up information, as the output
space in which potential summaries lie is probabilistically much larger than the input
itself. Hence, this is what makes evaluating LLM-based systems that produce large
==PAGE:148==
138
5 Discussion
amounts of text incredibly diﬀicult.
Despite this apparent diﬀiculty, reliable approaches to evaluation are imperative in
order to ensure performance, reliability and consistency in real-world applications. In
this project, evaluation of the Teaching Agent System (TAS) and Teaching Multi-
Agent System (TMAS) was done using another large LLM, reading the output and
scoring it, but there is currently no best practice for evaluating these types of sys-
tems. The evaluation approach developed here, using a separate GPT-4o-based sys-
tem, thereby represents a significant effort in establishing and testing plausible new
methods for evaluation of complex personalized learning systems. Future work must
identify even better ways of performing such evaluation, and especially methods for
making comparable performance metrics will be of immense value to developing these
types of LLM-based systems.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This thesis embarked on an exploration of leveraging agentic Large Language Mod-
els (LLMs) in the context of personalized learning within Science, Technology, Engi-
neering & Mathematics (STEM) education. Through the development and evaluation
of agentic systems, this project has demonstrated the substantial capabilities of LLMs
in transforming conventional learning to be more personalized and hence inclusive.
Two different systems were developed for generating personalized learning material
in dynamic conversations with STEM students, the single-agentic Teaching Agent
System (TAS) and the multi-agentic Teaching Multi-Agent System (TMAS). These
two systems shed light on the effectiveness of larger agentic LLMs in personalized
learning, and how smaller LLMs can be leveraged in multi-agentic systems, in order
to potentially allow running such systems on-device, hence potentially at low costs.
The Personal Memory Agent System (PMAS) was developed in order to explore simu-
lating personal memory, specifically to infer learning preferences for students, trough
their interactions with the TAS. These systems in combination showcased the poten-
tial of LLMs to both understand and adapt to individual students’ learning needs.
In order to measure the generative performance of the TAS and TMAS, an agentic
evaluation system was further developed, which could evaluate and score personalized
learning material on several criteria.
The findings indicate that LLMs are not just advanced question-answering systems,
but could become central to creating new educational interactions, which mimic the
nuanced and personalized approach of human teachers.
Looking ahead, the future of agentic learning systems lies in refining these technolo-
gies to ensure their reliability and consistency in real-world applications. Developing
robust methods for evaluating and improving these complex agentic systems will be
crucial to their success and adoption in learning environments.
In conclusion, this project not only highlights the transformative potential of LLMs
in enhancing personalized learning, but also illustrates the complexity of integrating
such advanced technologies in learning. Continued research and developments are
essential to harnessing the full potential of agentic LLMs, making education more
adaptive, inclusive, and effective for diverse learner populations. Moving forward,
these intelligent systems could fundamentally change how educational content is de-
livered, making it accessible and tailored to every student’s unique learning journey.

In the context of decision trees, entropy is used to measure the impurity or
randomness in the dataset at each node. It is computed using the formula
H(S) = −∑n
i=1 pi log2 pi, where pi represents the proportion of class i in-
stances at the node. The aim in using entropy is to choose the splits that result
in the most significant decrease in entropy, leading to branches with higher pu-
rity. This method helps in building an eﬀicient and effective classification tree.
