Abstract
This thesis consists of two projects, called the ALK- and LIAI1 project. Both projects
study next generation sequenced T cell receptor repertoires of human donors.
ALK. House dust mite (HDM) allergy reduces patients’ life quality significantly
in many cases and the allergy affects an increasingly large fraction of the industrialised
world’s population, hence treatment of it is beneficial for society. Promisingly, the newly
developed next generation sequenced (NGS) T cell receptor (TCR) repertoire provides us
with a direct look into the immune system’s current state. Thus, TCR repertoires derived
from blood of donors with HDM allergy may provide us with new details about HDM
allergy and how to treat it. This project is a bioinformatic study of TCR repertoires of
HDM allergen group 1, 2 and 1+2 stimulated CD4+ T cell cultures, CD4+ T cell single-
clone cultures with known receptor-specificity and unstimulated CD4+ PBMC samples,
all derived from four donors with HDM allergy. Our studies show 1) the in-body TCR
repertoire of a HDM allergy sufferer has extreme receptor diversity, 2) allergen-specific
T cell clones have very low frequencies and 3) T cells with the same receptor-specificity
have similar receptor structures, despite of originating from different donors. In stim-
ulated T cell cultures, we propose 4) that most sig. expanded stimulation-specific T
cell clones have low frequencies before stimulation and 5) the degree of received by-
stander cytokine stimulation positively correlates with the pre-stimulation frequency of
the stimulation-unspecific T cell clone. Finally, we observed 6) a synergistic effect be-
tween unique stimulative epitopes, as an increase in their numbers lead to a more than
linear increase in high frequency T cell clones post stimulation.
LIAI. A hope in the scientific community is that TCR repertoires may be used to
track medical treatment over time, specifically immunotherapy, in patients with allergy.
As a part of the development of a treatment tracking method, this project assists in
the design of a method that quantifies the similarity of significantly (sig.) expanded
T cell clone populations, extracted from two or more allergen-stimulated CD4+ T cell
culture repertoires. The quantification method is visualised in a matrix reproducibility
plot. The method is able to differentiate between a pair of T cell culture repertoires that
are stimulated with the same peptide and a pair that is not. Surprisingly though, the
similarities between the differently stimulated culture pair were quite high. We suspect
it is because the majority of sig. expanded T cells in both cultures are stimuli-unspecific
T cells, who have received bystander cytokine stimulation. Thus, only a small fraction
of T cells is peptide-specific in each peptide stimulated T cell culture.

CHAPTER 1
General Introduction
House dust mite allergy
House dust mite (HDM) allergy is caused by the immune system’s excessive reaction to
the presence of certain proteins in house dust mite droppings [11]. In Europe, the most
prevalent allergenic mite species are Dermatophagoides pteronyssinus and D. farinae,
who are between 0.1 - 0.4 mm in length and thus invisible to the naked eye. The mites
are found indoors in carpets, rugs, linens and mattresses, and reach a peak population
size in the late summer, when the heating is turned off and humidity is high [14]. The
HDM proteins that cause the immune response are called ”Der p” and ”Der f”, when
they are from D. pteronyssinus and D. farinae, respectively, and are numbered 1 - 33
i.e. Derp1, Derf1, Derp2, Derf2 and so on [14]. The proteins are further divided into
HDM allergen groups. Derp1 + Derf1 are the major components in group 1 and Derp2
+ Derf2 are the major components in group 2 [14]. Almost all HDM allergy sufferers
have allergic reactions to allergen group 1 and 2, which is why these protein groups
are used in allergen immunotherapy (AIT) that targets and may cure HDM allergy [15,
43]. ALK provides a sublingually administered allergy immunotherapy (SLIT) based on
HDM extracts containing the allergenic proteins of allergen group 1 and 2 [12]. ALK are
therefore researching the effects of the these proteins on immune cells and have provided
cell cultures samples for this project.
Signs and symptoms of HDM allergy are often nonspecific, but many sufferers have
manifestations of inflammation in the nose epithelium (allergic rhinitis), in the mem-
brane over the white part of the eye (allergic conjunctivitis) and asthma [14].
The
inflammatory states show symptoms of mucosal edema, redness and mucus secretion,
causing itching of the eyes and nose, teary eyes, sneezing fits and nasal obstruction [5].
These symptoms have been found to affect allergy sufferers’ lives in numerous negative
ways, resulting in sleep disturbances, concentration problems or reduced work capabil-
ities, hereby generally lowering life quality [31].
The number of affected people are
90 million in Europe, North America and Japan, and 100 million in China (statistics
from 2013) [20]. Additionally, the latest major study in Denmark showed an increase
in HDM allergy sufferers from 2.6% (1990) to 7.1% (1998) [3] and that the number of
people affected by allergy in general is rising [26].
CD4+ T cells and their role in allergy
HDM allergy sufferers are most likely exposed to allergenic proteins at mucosal linings
in their upper and lower airways [23]. The proteins, in this context called antigens, are

internalised by a set of cells called professional antigen-presenting cells (APCs), who
are located in the mucosa [21]. The APCs process the antigen and presents a peptide
fragment of it, also called an epitope, in a complex with a MHC class II molecule on
the APCs’ membrane [21]. This allow other immune cells to get into contact with the
epitope [21]. The described pathway is important in the immune system’s battle against
harmful foreign organisms, such as bacteria. But despite the fact that HDM proteins
and mites are not harmful as such, the pathway and immune system still react to the
proteins’ presence in HDM allergy sufferers.
Two time-separated response types are
mounted by the immune system of allergy sufferers: 1) An immediate hypersensitive
reaction and 2) a late phase reaction [34].
An introduction of two important immune cells is needed before getting into the de-
tails of the responses. First, the B cell, who is able to secrete antibodies (immunoglob-
ulins) and present antigens on its surface [22]. Second, and the focus of this project,
the T cell, whose main characteristic is a T cell receptor with the ability to recognise
presented antigens on APCs [35]. T cells are divided into CD4+ or CD8+ T cells, which
are references to two membrane proteins. It is the CD4+ T cells who are involved in
allergy and this population has been found to be quite diverse [22]. As of now, it en-
compass subpopulations of (at least) T helper 1, -2, and -17 cells (TH1, TH2 and TH17),
T regulatory cells (TReg), T follicular helper cells (TFH) and possibly the presence of T
follicular regulatory cells (TFR) [18, 22]. In the excessive reaction to allergenic proteins,
it is especially TH2 and TFH cells [32, 33] who are involved, and possible TH1 and TH17
cells [33]. TReg cells seem to downregulate the reaction [33]. However, no method in this
project differentiate between subpopulations of CD4+ T cells. Thus, the purpose of the
following two sections are exclusively to illustrate that some members of the CD4+ T
cell population have important roles in allergic reactions.
Response type 1, the immediate hypersensitive reaction, occurs within minutes of
allergen exposure and is mediated by immunoglobulin E (antibody IgE) [42]. Among
other locations, IgE is found in the respiratory tract and gastrointestinal tract, and
is produced by mature B cells, when they are specialised in antibody IgE production
[32]. The B cells switch from antibody IgM production to IgE due to a stimulus from
a subpopulation of CD4+ T cells. This subpopulation is probably either TFH, TH2 or
a combination (dispute between ref. [22] and [32]). Nonetheless, it is agreed upon that
the release of IL-4 from certain CD4+ T cells provoke the IgM to IgE class switch [22,
32, 34]. The antibody IgE binds to the Fc receptor on mast cells and basophils, which
causes a release of granules, containing inflammatory mediators, into the cellular envi-
ronment. The mediators cause vasodilation, vascular leakage and in some body parts
mucus secretion i.e. the first symptoms of an allergic reaction [32].
Response 2, the late phase reaction, occurs 2-24 hours after initial allergen exposure
and is characterised by an influx of additional immune cells to the inflammatory area,
which may result in local tissue damage [34]. The initiation of response 2 is in part
caused by tissue-residing CD4+ T cells, who bind to the APC-presented class II MHC

molecule:epitope complex, mentioned above. When CD4+ TH2 and TFH cells establish

(ILs), who promote inflammation indirectly in various ways. Mentioned consistently in

If a similar environment is created in the lungs, it is called asthma, and may cause air-
way hyper-responsiveness and airway remodelling (fibrosis and collagen deposition) [17].
These are effects seen in a severe allergic reaction [17, 33].
The T cell receptor
The T cell receptor (TCR) is the protein complex on the T cell membrane that recog-
nises and binds the MHC class II molecule:epitope complex, as described previously.
The receptor consists of a proximal constant domain and a distal variable membrane
domain, where the variable domain is further divided into an alpha and beta chain [10].
Each variable chain has three hyper-variable regions called complementary determining
regions (CDR) 1, -2 and -3, who are separated by constant flanking regions [35]. The
CDR3 in the beta chain is believed to have the biggest influence on the receptor:peptide
recognition [13]. Thus, this region is used as a ”barcode” to separate different T cell
receptor clones in this project and in others, as it defines their functional uniqueness
[19, 13]. T cell receptor chains acquire their uniqueness in a genomic rearrangement
of three germline genes: V, D and J. The merge-site of the three genes are located in
the CDR3, which is why it becomes so diverse. A complete TCR variable chain can be
reconstructed, if the germline genes identeties and the CDR3 composition is known [10].
Next-generation sequencing of T cell receptors
The combination of next-generation sequencing and highly optimised multiplex primer
sets allow the isolated sequencing of variable T cell receptor alpha or beta chains in
large scale [7]. This project uses the service of Adaptive Biotechnologies, who applies
Illumina sequencing technology [7]. The data output of such a sequencing is called a
TCR repertoire, independent of the T cell sample type, and is a collection of all the
unique CDR3s in the sample, including observation counts and additional annotation
for each unique CDR3. The underlying assumption of such repertoires are that a unique
CDR3 links to a unique receptor, which links to a single T cell clone, as every clone
only expresses receptors with identical specificity (fig. 1.1). A CDR3 can hereby act as
a TCR clone ”barcode”.

2.1
Introduction
Much of ALK’s allergy and immunology research has been focused upon the analysis
and description of T cell populations, as they are crucial components in the allergen
induced immune response [4, 6]. Previous research in T cells has improved ALK’s im-
munotherapy treatment and hereby improved the life of people who suffer from allergy
[34]. ALK are therefore very interested in the new and growing scientific field of next
generation sequenced immune cell repertoires, because they allow for the analysis of
many thousand receptors of unique B- and T cells at once [19]. Knowledge gained from
these repertoires may help improve immunotherapy even further, and perhaps act as a
diagnostic tool on a personalised level.
ALK applies different types of allergen stimulation to T cell cultures to expand
allergy-relevant T cells in their research. For this project, they received T cell receptor
(TCR) repertoires of 28 CD8+ depleted stimulated T cell cultures and unstimulated
samples (see table 2.2). These will make up the project’s data set and will be referred to
as the ALK data set. All the TCR repertoires are based on blood samples from donors
with house dust mite (HDM) allergy, and scientific conclusion drawn from this project
may advance ALK’s research, which could improve HDM allergy treatment strategies in
the future.
The following subsections will in detail describe questions regarding 1) TCR reper-
toire diagnostics, 2) T cell receptor specificity limitations and 3) T cell culture stimula-
tion methods. Every question is followed up by aim(s), describing how the project will
attempt to unravel parts of these questions.
Why the assessment of TCR repertoires’ diversity may be
important in future diagnostic methods
A general description of the diversity of immune cell repertoires is always helpful, when
you are in the process of understanding the data. However in this project, the description

is extra important, since the project is a frontrunner of a possible bigger study of TCR
repertoires’ ability as diagnostic tools. In the big study, the primary goal will be to
uncover the possibility of measuring state-changes in the in-body TCR repertoire of a
patient, using NGS TCR repertoire samples, and from them extrapolate the progress of
allergy on a cellular level. This diagnostic manoeuvre requires the ability to obtain a
reasonable overview of the current state of the in-body TCR repertoire. But such an
overview might not be attainable, if the T cell receptor diversity is extreme. An extreme
diversity means that there are millions of TCR clones who are present in few copies.
Thus, when a sequenced TCR repertoire sample consists of only 60k - 200k clones, it
will show only a tiny fraction of the complete in-body TCR repertoire and not give an
overview. The only way of acquiring the true overview of the in-body TCR repertoire in
such a situation, would be to sequence every T cell in a patients blood. A description of
the diversities in unstimulated peripheral blood mononuclear cell (PBMC) repertoires
and stimulated T cell culture repertoires is therefore needed to confirm or debunk the
described ”extreme diversity” scenario. A stringent definition of the analysis’ purpose
can be seen in section 2.2, Aim 1.
How to associate unstimulated PBMC repertoires and
stimulated T cell cultures directly to HDM specific receptors
The ALK data set contains three types of TCR repertoires: 1) unstimulated PBMCs,
2) stimulated T cell cultures and 3) T cell single-clone cultures with known receptor
specificity. Type 3 are specific to peptide-fragments of allergenic house dust mite proteins
in allergen group 1 or 2. For repertoire type 1 and 2, there is no direct knowledge about
receptor specificity, but they are still essential types, as they give direct insight into the
in-body TCR repertoire composition and frequency distribution. Thus, finding TCR
clones similar to the clones with HDM-specificity (type 3) in PBMC or T cell culture
repertoires (type 1 and 2), would associate in-body T cells directly to HDM allergy
(see schematic in fig. 2.1). This could be a method of tracking HDM allergy progress in
patients and hence a diagnostic tool. Aim 2 and 3 (sec. 2.2) describe the peptide-specific
TCR clone tracking in two situations: Where the clones are subsamples of T cell culture
repertoires and where the clones and cultures are unrelated.
A population of TCR clones with the same specificity are
likely to have similar T cell receptor structures
Four TCR clones, originating from two donors, have binding specificity against peptide
110. This peptide is 20 amino acids long and a fragment of allergenic HDM proteins
Derf2 and Derp2 (app. A.1 and A.2). Theoretically, these four clones may be similar
on a primary, secondary and tertiary structural level for reasons presented next.
The stimulative peptides in this project are generally short, between 20 - 22 amino
acids. Due to their length, it is presumed that they require limited trimming before

they should be detectable in the amino acid sequences. The four clones with peptide

they are therefore most likely similar in structure. The fourth aim of this project is to
investigate the similarity of these clones on multiple structural levels (Aim 4, sec. 2.2).

Locating and describing expanded and suppressed T cell
populations in T cell cultures
When T cell receptor clones bind MHC class II molecule:epitope complexes to which
they are specific, they initiate a signal transduction pathway. This pathway leads to the
transcription and translation of cytokines, who cause autocrine stimulation, resulting in
a clonal expansion of the epitope-specific T cell clone [22, 34]. Since the ALK data set
contains TCR repertoires of both unstimulated PBMCs and stimulated T cell cultures,
this expansion effect should be detectable in a repertoire comparison. Aim 5 (sec. 2.2)
attempts to locate significantly expanded and stimulative unresponsive (frequency-wise
suppressed) unique TCR clones in all T cell cultures of the ALK data set. The observa-
tion of expanding TCR clone populations will confirm previous laboratory results made
at ALK, and act as an approval of the new immune cell repertoire sequencing method.
Aim 6 and 7 (sec. 2.2) investigates the pre-stimulation origin of the expanded and
suppressed TCR populations in T cell cultures. Specifically, wether they had a low-,
medium- or high copy number (or frequency) at pre-stimulation. The purpose of the
analysis is to characterise the two populations and to learn more about the dynamics
within a T cell culture, during different types of stimulation.
Number of unique stimulative epitopes
Stimulative allergen groups with varying size have been applied to T cell cultures in
this project. Due to the size differences, the groups should produce an equally different
number of unique epitopes. We want to investigate if the number of unique epitopes
have an effect on the T cell cultures’ clone compositions. We suspect that the TCR
clones with high copy numbers (high frequencies) after stimulation are specific to the
stimulation type – at least a fraction of them. Thus, it could be interesting to see, if
something happens with this group’s size, when a different number of unique epitopes
is provided to the culture. An analysis investigating the correlation between unique
stimulative epitopes and the high-frequency clone group size is performed in Aim 8 (sec.
2.2).

TCR repertoire types
1. PBMC samples: The PBMC repertoires are based on fresh or frozen blood
samples. They were hereafter CD8+ T cell depleted and not manipulated further.
2. Stimulated T cell cultures: The T cell cultures consist of CD8+ depleted T cells.
They have undergone stimulation 2-3 times with 2-4 protein. Stimulative round 1
and 2 are always allergen specific, while round 3 is an unspecific PHA stimulation.
All T cell cultures in the data set have undergone 3 rounds of stimulation except
culture KFA HDM-02, hence its ”-02” annotation.
3. T cell (single) clone cultures: The clone cultures contain 1-3 dominant T cell
clones and the specificity of the clones are known from laboratory tests. The clone
cultures were constructed using a dilution method: A PBMC sample or T cell
culture is diluted until the expected concentration of T cells in the sample is 0-1
T cells per unit. One unit is then placed in a well and exposed to a peptide of
interest. If the T cell within the well is specific to the peptide, a clone culture is
founded on the T cell. This allows the T cell clone to increase in copy number,
which will make it available for TCR sequencing.

Bar plots of groups containing high-frequency TCR clones
from stimulated T cell cultures (Figure 2.26 and 2.27)
The purpose of this analysis was to organise the clones with most observation counts into
groups, separately for each T cell culture, and then compare the groups’ sizes between
the T cell cultures.
Depending on the data, the unique T cell group assignment procedure and group
vs. group comparison may be biased by the differences in T cell culture repertoire sizes.
Thus, to avoid this potential bias, a multiple-sampling approach was applied to each
repertoire. This approach is described below and visualised in fig. 2.6.
1. Repeat the following procedure for 100 iterations:
a. Sample 60.000 non-unique clones with replacement from the clone distribution
of an allergen-stimulated T cell culture repertoire (A, fig. 2.6).
b. Sum the total sampling-count (Sct) of every unique clone (B, fig. 2.6).
c. Transform the Sct to a frequency (Sfreq): Sct/60000*100 = Sfreq.
d. Divide every unique clone into one of seven percentage groups, using their Sfreq
(%).
The percentage groups are: [0-0.001], [0.001-0.005], [0.01-0.005], [0.01-
0.05], [0.1-0.05], [0.1-0.5] and [>0.5] (C, fig. 2.6).
e. Calculate the size (%) of each percentage group based on the number of unique
clones assigned to the group, relative to the number of unique clones sampled
in step a. (C, fig. 2.6).
2. Average the group sizes (%) outputted over the 100 iterations.
3. Plot the sizes in a bar plot.
For the theoretical HDM samples (fig. 2.27), the sampling was done from the com-
bined clone population of a HDM group 1 and a HDM group 2 repertoire, originating
from the same donor. Data manipulation was done in Python 3 [38] and plotting in R
[36] using qplot (geom="bar"), from library ggplot2 [44].

Aim 6. The relative distribution of pre-stimulation frequencies
of expanded and suppressed TCR clone populations in
allergen-specific stimulated cultures
The main result of Aim 5 was that sig. expanded and sig. suppressed TCR clone popu-
lations were present in allergen-stimulated T cell culture repertoires. In this analysis, we
ask, where did most of them come from? Did they mostly have low frequencies before
stimulation, maybe high frequencies, or they were perhaps uniformly distributed in the
pre-stimulation culture? So basically, we want to know the pre-stimulation frequency
distribution of the clone populations. However, due to the extremely skewed clone obs.
count distributions in pre-stimulated cultures (PBMC cultures) that were studied in
Aim 1, we are forced to look at the relative distribution and not the absolute. If we

Aim 8. Investigating a correlation between the number of
unique epitopes and the size of high-frequency clone groups
The ALK dataset contains T cell cultures stimulated with three types of epitope groups,
namely house dust mite protein group 1 and 2, and a combination of them called HDM
(Appendix A.2). The proteins in group 1 are bigger than those in group 2. Group 1
should therefore, at least in theory, produce more stimulative epitopes than group 2. As
HDM is a combination of both groups, it should produce the highest number of epitopes.
Following this line of thought, the stimulative groups rank as follows in unique epitope
production: HDM > Group 1 > Group 2.
In this analysis, we investigate if the size of the high-frequency TCR clone groups
correlates with the epitope production ranking. Thus, we divide every unique clone into
a group, separately for each culture, using the following data processing steps:
A. Every unique TCR clone was divided into one of seven ”percentage groups” ([0-
0.001], [0.001-0.005], [0.01-0.005], [0.01-0.05], [0.1-0.05], [0.1-0.5], [>0.5]), based on
the clone’s percentage size in the T cell culture. The percentage signifies how many
copies there are of a specific clone in a repertoire in relation to the repertoires total
size.
B. The above procedure was adjusted using a multiple sampling approach to avoid a
bias due to different sizes of T cell culture repertoires. Thus, every culture was down-
or upsized to 60.000 TCR clones. This is described in the methods section.
C. A certain number of unique clones would have been assigned to a percentage group
such as [0.1-0.5] to give an example. This number may be 100 unique clones. The
percentage size of the group is then calculated in relation to the total number of
unique clones within the culture. This number may be 13.000 unique clones. Thus,
group [0.1-0.5] has a percentage size of 0.77%. These group size are presented in bar
plots, figure. 2.26 for all cultures in the ALK data set.
During the discussion of fig. 2.26, we hypothesised about the possible presence of an
epitope-synergy effect in the HDM stimulated cultures. The HDM cultures seem to have
a higher number of high-frequency clones than what a theoretical summation of group 1
and 2 can account for. To investigate the possible synergy effect, two theoretical HDM
culture were constructed from a group 1 and a group 2 culture of donor IIJ and MGA,
using the multiple sampling approach mentioned in A., from above. The theoretical T
cell HDM cultures are compared to the true (practical) ones in fig. 2.27.
The multiple-sampling approach is described in more detail in Methods, sec. 2.3.

2.5
Discussion
Unstimulated PBMC T cell receptor repertoires have extreme
diversities
The TCR clone observation count distributions of PBMC repertoires show that about
90% of the clones were observed once, while 8% were observed twice (Aim 1, fig. 2.8).
This is about 60% and 15% for stimulated T cell cultures (Aim 1, fig. 2.9). Both results
reveal an extreme T cell receptor clone diversity, which is underlined by the low number
of shared clones in the two venn diagrams, presented in Aim 1, of intra- and inter-donor
shared clone populations (fig. 2.10 and 2.11, respectively). However, the repertoires
did also have a few TCR clones with very high frequencies (tab. 2.7). The distribution
patterns observed in fig. 2.8 are confirmed by another study [41].
In the introduction, the effect of an extreme diversity was discussed in relation to
a TCR repertoire state-change measuring method. We hypothesised that an extreme
diversity would be an obstruction in the development of a simple measuring method.
From the results here, we find that an extreme diversity is present in the in-body TCR
repertoire and the development of a simple measuring method should therefore not be
pursued. An alternative way of measuring state-changes in allergen-specific TCR clone
populations are described in the LIAI part of this project (chap. 3).
Allergen-specific T cell clones have very low pre-stimulation
frequencies
Only 2/12 TCR clones with known allergen-specificity were found in a PBMC reper-
toire of their own donor, when including both the alpha and beta chains (fig. A.1). The
refinding of the clones were marginally better in stimulated T cell cultures, where the
clones and cultures were based on separate blood samples, i.e. 2/9 (fig. 2.12). For the
clones who were directly related to their cultures, the refinding was consistent, i.e. 3/3
(fig. 2.14).
Since the sequenced PBMC TCR repertoire of MGA alpha contained 2 million cells
(super-deep sequencing, tab. A.4), and 1/3 MGA alpha clones (αMGA2, αMGA24-1,
αMGA24-2) were found in the repertoire, we suspect that the clones’ frequency are
sometimes less than 1 in 2 million T cells. This may also be true for other clones in
the ALK sample, making the frequency of allergen-specific clones super low and their
sampling in NGS TCR repertoires rare.
T cell clones with same-specificity receptors are similar
The comparison of clones with peptide 110-specificity showed that clones J46 and KFA80-
1 had similar CDR3 amino acid compositions in both the alpha and beta chains (tab.
2.16). Furthermore, substitutions in the amino acid sequences were kept inside the same

side chain group (nonpolar, polar, acidic or basic) in 1/1 times in the alpha chain and
4/6 times in the beta chain (fig. 2.17). In contrast, the germline genes were determined3
as quite different, hence J46 and KFA80-1 shared only the J-gene allele in the alpha
chain and only the V-gene allele in the beta chain. Despite this, the CDR3 still ended

The CDR3s of KFA80-2 and -03 sow doubt about the validity of their peptide

peptide specific TCR clones in a clone culture. It is perhaps best to stay conservative,
and only define the most frequent clone as peptide specific.

For no. 1, the correlation seems to be linear, while no. 2 seems to increase more dras-

The above observation could come from a synergy effect. Studies have shown that a
microenvironment populated by proliferation-signalling cytokines is formed in stimulated
T cell cultures [8, 18]. These cytokines have been seen to work synergistically, causing
a stronger increase in cell proliferation when different types are combined, than when
they are separated [1, 16]. The effect that we observe in our bar plots (fig. 2.26 and

stimulative epitopes instead. The epitopes could create a synergistic effect indirectly by
activating a higher number of T cells, hereby populating the microenvironment more
densely with cytokines. If this is true, we suspect the size increase in the high-frequency
clone group to be caused mostly by a stimulation of bystander clones and not allergen-
specific clones. This is supported by the results from the previous subsection, where
unspecific clones with high pre-stimulation frequencies seem to gain most advantage
(expansion-wise) from a cytokine dense T cell culture environment (seen in fig. 2.25).
It is also supported by the high amount of unspecific stimulation observed in the LIAI
data (fig. 3.12).
Discussion summary
The major tendencies discussed in the previous subsections are presented here:
1. The receptor diversity is high in the in-body TCR repertoire.
2. Allergen-specific T cells have very low frequencies (< 1 in 2 million).
3. T cell receptors with same-specificity are similar on multiple structural levels, de-
spite of originating from different donors.
4. In allergen stimulated T cell cultures:
a) Sig. expanded T cells are detected, regardless of stimulation types.
b) Sig. expanded T cells with very low pre-stimulation frequencies may be mostly
allergen-specific.
c) Allergen unspecific T cells receive bystander stimulation to a degree that
correlates with their pre-stimulation frequency.
d) Correlations are seen between the number of unique epitopes and 1) the
percentage of sig. expanded clones, and 2) the size of high-frequency clone
groups. There is possibly a synergy effect between the epitopes.

3.1
Introduction
Measuring change over time
The recent technological development of large scale TCR repertoire sequencing has
opened up the possibility of studying the composition of TCR repertoires in humans
at a previously unimaginable level of detail. As an extension to this development, the
idea of measuring changes in the human TCR repertoire in relation to medical treat-
ment arose. A TCR repertoire should in theory contain information about the immune
system’s reaction to the treatment it is exposed to [27]. Measurement of change could
therefore act as a diagnostic tool. ALK and LIAI are interested in this opportunity
and have ambitions of measuring TCR repertoire changes in patients who are under
immunotherapy (IT) treatment.
Variance introduced at TCR repertoire sampling: A large
group of low frequency and sample-unique TCR clones
A few studies have attempted to measure changes in TCR repertoires over time [24, 41].
One problem they face is the extreme diversity in the in-body TCR repertoire, which
causes every repertoire sampled to have many sample-unique TCR clones. The ALK-
part (chapter 2) have studied the TCR diversity and shown that about 90% of unique
TCR clones in unstimulated PBMC repertoires are observed only once (fig. 2.8). Thus,
the biggest fraction of TCR clones (millions of cells) in the in-body TCR repertoire must
have super low frequencies and cannot be thoroughly analysed by the relatively small
NGS TCR repertoire samples (60k – 200k cells). Even TCR repertoire sets that should
be quite similar have few shared clones, such as biological- and technical replicates.
Our studies have shown that the shared clone population in a quadruple set of similarly
stimulated T cell cultures, originating from the same donor, was less than 10% of unique
TCR clones pr. repertoire (fig. 3.10). The large fraction of repertoire-unique TCR clones
in unstimulated PBMC repertoires is described as sampling variance in this project.
Thus, a large in-body diversity causes a large sampling variance which results in few
shared TCR clones between NGS TCR repertoire samples (see fig.
3.1 for concept
illustration). This is an essential change-measuring issue, as it makes consistent tracking
of TCR clones and measurement of their frequencies very difficult over time.

This specific type of difference or variance between a set of two TCR reper-

A peptide stimulation of T cell cultures is not a fixed process
Some believe that a fraction of TCR clones are specific and highly reactive to a stimula-
tive peptide and will therefore almost always expand, when the peptide is present in a T
cell culture. However, the biggest fraction of T cells in a culture are peptide-unspecific
and are thus unresponsive to the stimulation, and will not expand in culture (Results
from ALK-part). In between the two opposites, there probably is a group of T cells
who will expand only when the conditions are just right. Hypothetically, when these
T cell clones are located optimally in a T cell culture, have repeat contact to antigen
presenting cells and perhaps gain slight amounts of paracrine cytokine stimulation, then
they expand. But when the conditions are suboptimal, they will not expand. This clone
population may be described as unpredictable and we can therefore not count on them to
expand every time. In this project, we describe the presence of this unpredictable group
or lack thereof, in a T cell culture’s expanded clone population as biological variance,
which has been introduced during the allergen (or peptide) stimulation.
Concept of a TCR repertoire change-tracking method
A good TCR repertoire change-tracking method that targets allergen-specific T cells
must handle three problems well:
1. Allergen-specific TCR clones are rarely found in unstimulated PBMC repertoires.
A clear tendency observed in the ALK-part of this project (fig. A.1).
2. Sampling variance: A large group of TCR clones in repertoire samples have low
frequencies and are sample-unique.
3. Biological variance: A peptide stimulation of T cell cultures is not a fixed process,
resulting in differences in the expanded TCR clone population between replicate
culture stimulations.

Aim 1: A method of quantifying the similarities between sets
of TCR repertoires’ allergen-specific clone populations
The method takes the combined TCR clone population of two or more TCR repertoires,
whose difference needs to be quantified. The method then executes the following data
processing steps:
1. Quantification
a) Isolation of the allergen-specific (peptide-specific) clone population.
b) Calculation of the reproducibility measure (R) of each individual clone, which
is a quantification of the similarity between TCR repertoires.
2. Visualisation
a) Divide individual TCR clone observations from the TCR repertoires into
groups based on their pre-stimulation frequency and degree of expansion.
b) Visualise the groups in a matrix by calculating the average R measure within
each group.
A walkthrough of the data processing steps from above is described below using the
data set of donor 1580 (fig. 3.5), which was used to construct the matrix in Aim 2
(matrix: fig. 3.11).
1. a) Isolation of the allergen-specific clone population

TG 1.3 and TG 1.4 repertoires were analysed. Note that all of the TG repertoires are
biological replicates (fig. 3.5). To isolate the allergen-specific clones, a statistical test
were used to determine if a unique TCR clone had become expanded post allergenic
peptide stimulation. We (slightly controversially) assume that expanded T cell clones
are allergen-specific as well. This is done in other studies too but with a proviso on the
assumptions validity, which this project will return to in the discussion [24]. Thus, ev-
ery unique clone were tested for significant (sig.) expansion in all of the TG repertoires,
using Fishers exact test (details in ALK-part, fig. 2.4). Clones who were sig. expanded
(unadj. p-value < 0.05) in at least one TCR repertoire were selected for further analysis,
and hereby act as the allergen-specific clone population.

The plan (see Introduction) was to quantify the difference between two allergen-
specific clone populations, but we found that the similarity is easier to handle. Thus,
measuring the similarity of the TCR repertoires was done by counting the number of
repertoires in which each unique clone had been observed as sig. expanded. This num-
ber, which is between 1-4 in the case of donor 1580’s data set, was plotted into the
reproducibility measure formula:
R = (n-1)/(N-1)
R is the reproducibility measure, n is the number of TCR repertoires wherein the clone
in question is expanded and N is the number of TCR repertoires used in the analysis.
It is called a Reproducibility Measure because it illustrates the number of times
that a previously sig. expanded TCR clone has been observed as sig. expanded in
an additional TCR repertoire i.e. reproduced.
As there are four biological replicates in this analysis, the R measure can take on a
value of 0, 1/3, 2/3 or 1, signifying a non-reproduction, single reproduction, double- and
triple reproduction of TCR clone expansion. Table 3.6 shows how each unique clone was
assigned an R measure and fig. 3.7 shows how the R measure behave illustratively.

2. a) Divide unique TCR clone observations into groups
To visualise and make sense of the data from above, every unique TCR clone obser-
vation in a TCR repertoire was divided into groups based on two factors:
1. The unique TCR clones pre-stimulation frequency (from PBMC repertoire)
2. The observed degree of expansion of the unique TCR clone in a TCR repertoire.
Factor 1, the pre-stimulation frequency, will tell us how affected a clone will be by
the TCR repertoire sampling variance. A clone with a low pre-stimulation frequency has
a high probability of not being sampled and oppositly, a clone with a high frequency has
a low chance of not being sampled. Sorting groups based on this factor will therefore
give us clone groups, who should be sampled consistently and clone groups who should
not be. The influence of the sampling variance is thus described by this range of groups.
Factor 2, the degree of expansion, as measured by the p-value of a Fishers 2x2 exact
test [25] (details fig. 2.4) tells us how affected a clone will be by the biological variance
during allergen stimulation. A clone who reacts powerfully to stimulation will be cat-
egorised as sig. expanded almost every time. A clone who reacts less powerfully may
be categorised as not expanded 50% of the times, due to biological variance. Grouping
by this factor will hereby give us groups, who are categorised as sig. expanded often
and groups who are not. The influence of the biological stimulation variance is thus
described by this range of groups.
Finally, it is possible for a unique TCR clone to be observed in multiple of donor
1580’s TCR repertoires, as sig. expanded. Thus, a unique TCR clone may have multiple
”sightings” or observations, and in this analysis, we use all sightings. Each sighting of

3.5
Discussion
Quantified and visualised similarities between biological
replicates using the R measure in a matrix plot
When looking at the comparison of four biological TCR repertoire replicates in fig. 3.11,
an increase in the average group R measure is observed diagonally from the upper left
corner to the lower right corner. The group in the upper left corner has an R measure of
0.07, meaning that almost all the clone members of this group have been observed as sig.
expanded in only one culture. In contrast, the lower right group shows an R measure
of 0.9, meaning that almost all clone members of this group have been observed as sig.
expanded in all four biological replicates. Revisit fig. 3.7 to see how the R measure
works and note that each matrix-group (square) contains at least 50 TCR clone data
points. The diagonal structure of the R measures in matrix figure 3.11 tells us that:
1. The adjusted (adj.) PBMC count groups, based on the pre-stimulation frequencies,
clearly describe the sampling variance.
2. The p-value groups clearly describe the biological stimulation variance.
TCR clones with low adj. PBMC counts and high p-values are often not sampled and
often not expanded, which is reflected in the low R measures in the upper-left matrix part.
In contrast, TCR clones with high adj. PBMC counts and super low p-values are often
sampled and often determined as sig. expanded, resulting in high R-measures in the
lower-right matrix part. However, another R measure-affecting factor must be present,
since an R = 1.0 is not reached. It is probably because the size of the pre-stimulation
culture is not big enough. This is discussed further in the subsection below.
Surprisingly high similarities between TCR culture repertoires
stimulated with different peptides
The biological replicate matrix of donor 1594 (A, fig. 3.12) shows a similar diagonal
signature of R measures as seen in donor 1580’s matrix (fig. 3.11), but donor 1594’s R
measure values are generally lower. As an example, see the upper left corner of 1594’s fig.
3.12 that has a measure of 0.01 (in donor 1580: 0.07) and the lower right corner that has
a measure of 0.78 (in donor 1580: 0.9). The R measure differences is probably caused by
1594’s culture sizes at pre-stimulation, since they were 10 times smaller than the cultures
of donor 1580. When the pre-stimulation culture size is smaller, fewer T cell clones with
low copy numbers are sampled. And since the allergen-specific (peptide-specific) clones
are typically among this low-count group, fewer of them will be sampled consistently in
a TCR repertoire, hereby reducing the overall R measure. This is probably also why an
R measure of 1.0 was not observed in 1580’s matrix, even though the pre-stimulation
culture sizes were 2 million. To reach and R = 1.0, we may have to use cell cultures of

3-4 million1 T cells.
The TCR repertoire reproducibility matrices of comparisons between cultures stim-
ulated with different peptides (B, C and D in fig. 3.12) also have a diagonal pattern,
as seen in fig. 3.12, but the pattern is not as smooth. This was expected. Strikingly
though, the degree of similarity, i.e. the R measure values, is much higher than expected.
A maximum R measure of 0.45, 0.62, and 0.62 is reached in B, C and D, respectively.
This makes the general R measure differences between the biological replicate pair in A
and the repertoire pairs in B, C and D quite low. A clear visualisation of the differences
between A and B, C or D, using the column-wise averaged R measures, is seen in the
right matrix of fig. 3.13. This shows that the R measure difference is between (-)0.23
and (+)0.01, and that A is consistently having a better, however only slightly better, R
measure.
So, it seems that the R measure differences are small, when only the peptide-stimulation
type is changed, i.e. the culture sets stay quite similar. In other words, the change in
stimulation type have almost no impact on the R-measures, even though we are clearly
increasing the biological stimulation variance. What effect is causing the similarity be-
tween culture sets to stay this high? We believe that the main effect causing the high
R-measures, is coming from a group of T cells who expand regardless of stimulation
type, i.e. peptide-unspecific expansion. This would explain why an R = 0.62 is reached
between two T cell culture repertoires treated with different stimulation types. The high
similarity between A and B, C and D also suggests that the peptide-unspecific clones
are the majority of TCR clones characterised as sig. expanded in our statistical analysis.
The expanded population of peptide-unspecific T cells must have high pre-stimulation
copy numbers, as col. ”23.1 (14-66)” has the highest R measure consistently in the matri-
ces (B, C and D, fig. 3.12). The high clone copy numbers combined with the dense and
artificial in vitro environment of a T cell culture, may work as an advantage for these
T cells in regard to expansion. Thus, when the stimulated T cell culture environment
is filled with proliferation-stimulating cytokines, originating from true peptide-specific
T cells, the peptide-unspecific T cells may receive bystander (paracrine) stimulation in
great amounts, due to their high copy numbers [8, 18]. And if our results are true, the
degree of stimuli received is high enough for the peptide-unspecific T cells to expand
significantly.
Bystander T cell populations have been described before [2, 9], but the population’s
dominating percentage size among the sig. expanded TCR clones is alarming, in this
example. These results and discussed tendencies may cause for a reevaluation of the
matrix quantification measuring method proposed in Results, Aim 1.

Discussion summary
The previous two subsections pointed out four tendencies:
1. The matrix plot clearly illustrates the effect on a TCR repertoire from 1) sampling
variance and 2) biological stimulation variance.
2. Biological replicate sets show slightly higher similarities (R measures) than sets of
TCR repertoires stimulated with different peptides.
3. The similarity (R measures) between biological replicates of stimulated TCR cul-
ture repertoires increase, when the pre-stimulation T cell culture size increase.
4. A big population of stimuli-unspecific bystander T cells expand during T cell
culture stimulation. This population may be the majority of T cells determined
as sig. expanded with the fishers exact test in our analysis.
