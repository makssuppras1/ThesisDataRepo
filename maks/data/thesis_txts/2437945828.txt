Abstract
The content of this thesis regards modelling of the liquid chromatography process. The topic
was initiated by introducing the general concepts of HPLC, alongside mass balances, ther-
modynamic interactions and a selection of distribution isotherms. This forms the basis of
modelling the liquid chromatography system. The equilibrium-dispersive mass balance model
was implemented in Matlab 2017a by the ﬁnite diﬀerence method.
An arbitrary set of system parameters was adopted, which facilitates comparison between
an analytical and the numerical model.
This serves to validate the model derivation and
implementation.
The analytical model is only applicable for linear isotherms, where the
numerical model was used for more complex systems.
Experiments were conducted with hesperidin, to determine any system and compound speciﬁc
parameters required for simulating the elution process.
The inverse method was applied
for determining the Henry coeﬃcient and the apparent dispersion coeﬃcient. An excellent
conformity between the simulation and the experimental data was obtained. It is possible to
accurately predict the puriﬁcation of hesperidin in the linear concentration range.
The model was extended to the separation of sodium lactate enantiomers. Similar experiments
were conducted, as for the hesperidin system. This includes an in-depth analysis of several
solvent and buﬀer systems, as to optimize the separation. The inverse method was applied to
the experimental data. Simulating the pure enantiomers provided very accurate results, where
the retention time was oﬀby 0.5%. Extending the simulation to both enantiomers produced
retention times, which were oﬀby 1.9% for D-lactate and 5.1% for L-lactate. The peak shapes
of all simulations have a high conformity to the experimental proﬁle.
The model was extended to continuous operation by a simulated moving bed system. This
allows for a larger productivity and a lower solvent consumption. Mass balance limitations
were introduced, and the conformity of the model was validated by a case study. The enan-
tiopurities of the extract- and the raﬃnate stream were, respectively, 100% D-lactate and 97%
L-lactate.

Resumé
Denne afhandling omhandler modellering af væskekromatograﬁ. Emnet påbegyndes med en
introduktion af generelle koncepter indenfor HPLC. Et udvalg af gængse brugte masse balan-
cer, termodynamiske relationer, og isotermer er gennemgået og udledt. Equilibrium-dispersive
modellen er implementeret i Matlab 2017a med en ﬁnite diﬀerence diskretisering.
Et arbitrært sæt parametre er benyttet til at sammenligne en analytisk model med den imple-
menterede numeriske model. Dermed valideres model udledningen og implementeringen. Den
analytiske model er kun brugbar for lineære isotermer, hvor den numeriske model bruges for
systemer af højere kompleksitet.
Der er fortaget forsøg med stoﬀet hesperidin, for at bestemme de system- og materiale speci-
ﬁkke parametre, der kræves for at simulere processen. Den inverse metode er benyttet til at
bestemme Henrys konstant og den tilsyneladende dispersionskoeﬃcient.
Modellen er opdateret til separation af natrium laktat enantiomerer. De samme forsøg, som
er foretaget for hesperidin, er udført for dette system. Der er lavet en dybdegående analyse
af ﬂere solventer og buﬀer systemer, for at optimere seperationen. Simuleringen for en enkelt
enantiomerer giver meget præcise resultater, hvor opholdstiden er 0.5% fra den experimentalle
værdi. Udvidelsen til begge enantiomerer introducerer lidt større fejl. Her er opholdstiden
forkert med 1.9% for D-lactate og 5.1% for L-lactate.
Modellen er udvidet til den kontinuerte SMB model. Dette system giver mulighed for en
større produktivitet og der bruges mindre solvent. Masse balancen er opdateret til at inkludere
masseoverførselsbegrænsninger, og system parametre fra et andet studie er brugt for at validere
model udvidelsen. Ved steady state opnås der to produkt strømme indholdende hhv. 100%
D-laktat og 97% L-laktat.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Chromatography is an important unit operation in chemical engineering, which has the ca-
pability of separating speciﬁc mixtures into their individual respective components [1]. It is
initiated by a sorption operation, in which the components of a ﬂuid phase are selectively
transferred to insoluble, rigid particles in a column, followed by a desorption process where
the components are detached from the rigid particles. The solution, which is to be separated,
is typically injected into the column as a small batch, where a steady ﬂow of the ﬂuid (mobile
phase) transfers it through the column by either gravity or pressure [2, 3]. The various com-
ponents of the mixture exhibit diﬀerent degrees of aﬃnity to the rigid particles (stationary
phase) and the mobile phase, thus resulting in diﬀerent velocities for the respective components
(diﬀerential migration). These diﬀerent compounds’ speciﬁc aﬃnities are the fundamental of
the separation process and are attributed in the attempt to minimize the Gibbs free energy
of the system. If the solute has a high aﬃnity towards the stationary phase, its residence
time (retention time) in the column will be longer, where a large aﬃnity towards the mobile
phase shortens the retention time. A variety of chromatographic techniques exists, where the
separation relies on diﬀerent chemical properties, though the majority is based on adsorption
onto the stationary phase. The diﬀerent techniques and the physical explanations of their
principles are elaborated in Section 2.1. The relationship between the concentration in the
mobile- and the stationary phase is typically evaluated by a distribution function (isotherm).
This will be elaborated in Section 3.3.
The separation of a solution consisting of a solvent and three solutes is illustrated in Figure
1.1.
The mobile phase has been omitted from the schematic drawing for clarity reasons.
The illustrations show the current state of the column at diﬀerent time stamps. The sample
solvent is washed out (eluted) ﬁrst followed by the three diﬀerent solutes, which are completely
separated by the last time stamp of Figure 1.1. The volume occupied by each solute is denoted
as a band, where the length in the ﬂow direction is the band width [4]. As illustrated, the
band width broadens relative to the travelled length through the column, as the solutes are
diﬀusive. This eﬀect, which restricts the applicable column length, should be minimized if
possible.

The chromatographic separation may be carried out in both small scale (analytical) and large
scale (preparative) [4]. Analytical chromatography is used for detection, identiﬁcation and
quantiﬁcation of compounds, for evaluating compound speciﬁc parameters or as a precursor
to preparative chromatography. It may also be denoted linear chromatography, as the solute
concentration in the sample usually is low enough to remain in the linear range of the isotherm.
Preparative chromatography is e.g. used in the chemical and pharmaceutical production for
isolation and puriﬁcation of high-value compounds. It is often operated in the non-linear area
and is expensive and of high complexity. Figure 1.1 illustrates the chromatograpic process as
a batch operation, which is usually the case during analytical chromatography. Modernized
preparative columns do typically operate continuously in a pseudo-steady-state by periodically
supplying the solution in bursts, where the individual compounds may be collected at speciﬁc
time steps of each cycle. This is particularly useful for separations of compounds with similar
properties, as enantiomers.
Typically, two operational modes of the column are considered; isocratic elution, where the
composition of the mobile phase is ﬁxed, and gradient elution, where the solvent concentration
is deliberately changed gradually [3, 5].
Gradient elution introduces changes in e.g.
the
polarity or ion strength, which facilitates the separation of compounds, which are inseparable
in a single composition mobile phase. Generally, isocratic elution is, due to its simplicity and
the faster experimental procedure, preferred for samples with less than 10 compounds and
also for small sample volumes relative to the separation space of the column. If the separation

is diﬃcult and the stated conditions do not apply, it may be advantageous to use gradient
elution, where the separation has additional degrees of freedom.
The industrial applications of chromatography are broad. They e.g. include the separation
of sugars, perfume ingredients and C4 - C10 paraﬃns [1]. However, one of the major reasons
why the ﬁeld of chromatography is experiencing an increased interest, is its important role in
the biopharmaceutical industry. It is one of the most capital intensive of all industries, where
50-80% of the total cost of biomanufacturing is caused by downstream processing, puriﬁcation
and polishing [6, 7]. Chromatography is the most costly step in the puriﬁcation process, as
it separates the ﬁnal high value product of the production, where any waste is costly. The
technique is, however, essential due to the high purity requirements of pharmaceuticals and
since traditional separation methods, as distillation, centrifugal extraction and crystallization,
are inapplicable to the desired separations due to e.g. temperature limitations and puriﬁcation
requirements [8, 9]. It is predicted that within 10 years, 50% of all drugs in development will be
biopharmaceuticals, illustrating the essentiality of optimizing this technology [8]. Similarly,
40-70% of the costs associated with the chemical industry are used for separations, where
chromatography is likewise frequently utilized [10].
In addition to the rapid growth in the bioindustry, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA)
initiated the Pharmaceutical Current Good Manufacturing Practices (cGMPs) in 2002 with
the objective of improving and modernizing biopharmaceutical manufacturing [11]. Process
Analytical Technology (PAT) is a key element of this initiative. The regulatory framework’s
essential objective is to achieve health and economical beneﬁts by applying process control
to the manufacturing process. This allows for minimizing the variability in a process and
thereby ensures a consistent product quality with optimized operational conditions and pa-
rameters. This practice is currently still limited in the biopharmaceutical industries, where
a lab technician usually empirically determines the operational conditions, often resulting in
sub-optimal performance. After the product receives approval, the production path is ﬁxed to
ensure consistency and compliance with the speciﬁcations, which is costly to alter. Developing
a predictive model would minimize the required experiments and accelerate optimization of
the process. This is the motivation of developing predictive models of the system, to ensure
compliance with the cGMPs and optimize the production costs.

Two systems will be considered during the span of this thesis.
The applications and the

Initial experiments are conducted on hesperidin, which is a naturally occurring compound in
citrus peels, from which it may be extracted [12]. It possesses a wide range of pharmaco-
logical properties, as antihypertensive and anti-inﬂammatory eﬀects. Certain clinical trials
have furthermore reported positive response for patients suﬀering from pleurisy and tuber-
culosis. Other research highlights its usability as a natural sweetener, by converting it into

hesperitin [13]. Due to its natural occurrence and its medical attributes, it is widely sold as
herbal medicine. It is highly non-polar, which is a useful property when separating it. The

Lactic acid has wide applications
in the food industry, pharmaceutical-, textile-, and chemical industries [10]. Recently, the
demand has increased greatly due to its new-found application as a monomer in the formation
of polylactic acid, which is a biodegradable polymer. The properties of the polylactic acid is

is formed in the human body, where this isomer is preferred for food applications due to its
faster metabolic conversion. The two isomers must have diﬀerent aﬃnities to the stationary
phase in order to facilitate the separation.
1.2
Problem statement
The scope of this thesis will be limited to high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC),
where liquid solvents and solid ﬁxed particles will be utilized. The objective is to characterize
an existing HPLC setup available in the laboratory including a selection of diﬀerent columns.
The ﬁnal aim is to formulate and implement a mathematical model describing the system,
thus being able to reproduce experimentally obtained results, which in terms would facilitate
process optimization.
An initial literature survey is conducted on the concepts of liquid chromatography, to provide
a baseline for understanding the actual process. The study will review basic chromatographic
theory essential to performing and evaluating the separation, along existing models and ther-
modynamics characterizing the separation capability of a column. A review of the various
chromatographic techniques has been performed, as the method of the separation is depen-
dent on the compound speciﬁc properties.
A mass balance model, along with the distribution isotherm, is implemented in Matlab, both
for an analytical and a numerical model, to validate the derivation and implementation of the
approximate model. The evaluation is conducted on an arbitrary system stated by Qamar
et al [14]. Subsequently, isocratic experiments are performed with hesperidin, as to evaluate
system and compound speciﬁc parameters. The arbitrary system parameters are replaced by
the experimentally obtained, which allows for predicting the elution of hesperidin.
The system is adapted to lactic acid, where an actual separation now takes place due to
having multiple components. Similarly to the case of hesperidin, the same parameters are

Chapter 2
Concepts of liquid chromatography
This chapter will review the fundamentals of liquid chromatography to provide the required
knowledge for understanding the process. This includes the HPLC setup, separation mech-
anisms and diﬀerent methods of evaluating separation speciﬁc parameters. It is an overall
review, however it particularly applies to analytical chromatography.
The schematic of an analytical HPLC system is given in Figure 2.1, with an illustration of
the ﬂow path through the system. The solvents are supplied to a mixer using high pressure
pumps, and the mobile phase, along with the sample, is injected into the column. The column
consists of a packed cylindrical tube, which may be oriented either horizontally or vertically
depending on the setup. The operating pressure interval for HPLC columns ranges from tens
to several hundred bar, where a maximum of approximately 200 bar usually is preferred in
order to prolong the lifetime of the equipment [3, 4]. This high pressure is required, as the
pressure drop over the column is extremely large due to the ﬁne particle size of the stationary
phase. Subsequent to the column is a detector, which usually is an UV/Vis or mass spectrum
(MS) detector, which detects the composition of the throughput. Finally, the liquid phase is
transferred to a waste container. Instead of discarding the throughput, it may be collected if
desired. A preparative setup would be similar, where the throughput would be collected in
several fractions.

2.1
Types of liquid chromatography
The mechanisms responsible for the mass transfer between the free (mobile phase) and bound
(stationary phase) states include surface absorption, ion exchange, relative solubilities and
steric eﬀects, where the contributions of the individual mechanisms depend upon the sub-
stances and the method of HPLC [4, 15–17]. The diﬀerent operational modes of HPLC are
==PAGE:19==
2.1 Types of liquid chromatography
9
used, depending on the properties of the solutes and solvent in the mixture, to facilitate
separation. They are usually classiﬁed according to the mechanism of the separation as:
• Adsorption
Processes relying on adsorption are probably the oldest and most common type of chro-
matography. The stationary phase is chosen according to its absorption power relative
to the solutes in the mobile phase and the solvents properties. The individual solutes
are adsorbed with diﬀerent powers, which facilitates the separation.
This technique
highly relies on hydrophobic eﬀects, which are observed in e.g. normal-phase chromatog-
raphy, reverse-phase chromatography, non-aqueous reverse-phase chromatography and
hydrophobic interaction chromatography. They all operate with a non-polar stationary
phase, except normal-phase chromatography, which has a polar stationary phase.
• Partition
This technique is based upon applying a thin ﬁlm of a liquid on the inert matrix. The
liquid must be immiscible with the mobile phase. It is typically utilized for the separation
of polar compounds. A method used here is hydrophilic interaction chromatography,
where the stationary phase is polar, which attracts the polar solutes in the mixture.
• Ion exchange
Ion-exchange chromatography is used for separating ionizable samples as acids or bases
and especially for large biomolecules (e.g. proteins and nucleic acids). The stationary
phase contains charged groups capable of binding sample ions of opposite charge, where
the mobile phase typically consists of an aqueous solution of a salt and buﬀer.
• Size exclusion
Size-exclusion chromatography separates the substances on the basis of molecular weight
and is mainly used for large biomolecules or synthetic polymers. The stationary phase
is inert and the mobile phase is either aqueous or organic.
• Aﬃnity
It is based upon highly speciﬁc aﬃnities of proteins to speciﬁc ligands, such as enzymes.
The ligand is attached to a suitable polysaccharide polymer.
• Chiral
This technique allows for separating isomers. The isomers are inseparable based on size
or polarity, due to their great similarities. They can be separated by either one of two
methods; the indirect method, where the isomers are reacted with a chiral derivatizing
reagent, which facilitates separation by conventional HPLC techniques, or by the direct
method, where a chiral selector is introduced in the stationary phase, towards which the
isomers have diﬀerent aﬃnities.
A reverse-phase column is used for the rectiﬁcation of hesperidin and a chiral column with a
chiral selector in the stationary phase is used for the separation of the lactate enantiomers. The
chiral column further operates by an ion exchange principle, where an organic acid competes
with the lactate.
These techniques, along with a few of the major utilized methods, are
elaborated here.

2.1.1
Reverse-phase chromatography (RPC)
This is in general the most abundant method of liquid chromatography, as it has the highest
resolution of the available techniques, and is particularly used for water-soluble mixtures
[4, 16]. Its applications are widespread within analysis of proteins, peptides and nucleic acids,
and is often used as a mean of ﬁnal polishing. The hydrophobic solutes reversibly associates
with the ligands to form a complex by non-polar interactions, to minimize their surface area
and thereby decrease the system’s free energy due to the disorganization of water molecules.
The least hydrophobic solutes are eluted ﬁrst.
The stationary phase (e.g.
C18) is highly
hydrophobic, where non-polar substances bind strongly to the medium. The mobile phase is
a mixture of water and organic solvent (e.g. acetonitrile).
2.1.2
Hydrophobic interaction chromatography (HIC)
This technique is highly similar to that of RPC, as the separation is equivalently based on
hydrophobic properties.
The bindings to the stationary phase is, however, weaker due to
the stationary material being less hydrophobic for HIC [4, 16]. Thereby milder solvents may
be used, ensuring a non-denaturing environment if e.g. proteins are to be separated. A salt
solution is used as the mobile phase, as the ions increase the hydrophobic eﬀects, by increasing
the polarity of the mobile phase. A high salt concentration thus promotes the binding to the
stationary phase, while a low salt concentration facilitates elution of the solutes.
2.1.3
Hydrophilic interaction chromatography (HILIC)
The method is utilized for highly polarized substances, meaning RPC is a poor choice for
the separation [4, 18].
The stationary phase is polar (e.g.
silica or amide-bonded phase)
and the mobile phase is a mixture of water and organic solvent (e.g. acetonitrile). The actual
mechanism is still not fully comprehended, however it is believed that the main eﬀect is caused
by polar analytes partitioning into and out of a layer of water, which is adsorbed onto the
surface of the polar stationary phase. During elution, the less hydrophilic compounds have a
shorter retention time.
2.1.4
Ion-exchange chromatography (IEC)
Ion-exchange chromatography is fundamentally a process, where a protein is exchanged with
ions and binds with the ligand by electrostatic interactions, which is the most common technol-
ogy in preparative chromatography [4, 17, 19]. The technology allows for separating molecules
on the basis of their diﬀerent net surface charges, even with minor diﬀerences in their charge
properties, where the principle is similar to that of an ion-exchanger. It is useful for sepa-
rating acids or bases, which are only weakly retained in RPC. The conditions of RPC are

ions of opposite charge. The proteins net charge changes with the pH value. A protein at its
isoelectric point will not interact with a charged medium. However, at a higher or lower pH
value it respectively binds to a positively or negatively charged medium. When the desired
molecules are bound, the system conditions can be altered in order to facilitate elution. This

2.1.5
Chiral chromatography
The use of chiral chromatography is widespread in the pharmaceutical industry, where it is
crucial to obtain e.g. pure enantiomers of a drug [4, 20]. The direct method will be reviewed
during this subsection.
The chiral column is of higher specialization than the previously
mentioned types, as the chiral stationary phase (CSP) must interact more strongly with one
enantiomer than the other. The chiral selector is a single enantiomer of a chiral compound,
typically made by derivatives of amylose or cellulose bound to inert silica. The mobile phase
can interfere or promote speciﬁc interactions, where e.g. polar solvents attenuate the electro-
static interactions. Similarly, the pH of the system is of high importance.

2.4
Band-broadening processes
This subsection will review the processes responsible for the band-broadening in the column,
which is depicted in Figure 2.5. The initial broadening is from the sample injection, where
the solute initially occupies a volume [4, 21]. This eﬀect is usually small relative to the others,
and can typically be neglected, though it may have some eﬀect in small volume columns with
small particles. The same applies to the case of Figure 2.5f, where the band is broadened from
ﬂowing through the detector and the connecting tube.
The longitudinal diﬀusion is time dependent, meaning the eﬀect varies inversely with the ﬂow
rate. This process would occur even if the mobile phase was stagnant. Eddy diﬀusion is a result
of the variation in the particle packing, where the molecules are driven through the column by
diﬀerent ﬂow streams, as depicted in Figure 2.5c. The occurrence of these channels leads to
particles in restricted ﬂow streams lag behind, while the particles in fast ﬂowing streams are
carried ahead. The contribution to band-broadening from eddy diﬀusion is almost independent
of the ﬂow rate, and only depends on the particle size and the packing of the column. Mobile
phase mass transfer is the result of the velocity proﬁle between particles, where the ﬂow in the
stream center is faster. If the ﬂow rate is increased, the particles in the center of the stream
move relatively faster. Finally, there is stationary phase mass transfer. Some particles will
travel further into a pore by diﬀusion and therefore have a longer residence time than other
particles diﬀusing a shorter distance into the particle. The contribution increases with the
ﬂow rate.

Peak shape
The peaks depicted so far in Figure 2.3 and 2.4 are all symmetrical. In reality, this is rarely the
case. Peaks are usually asymmetrical rather than following a perfect Gaussian distribution,
as illustrated in Figure 2.6 [4].
It may be asymmetry at both the left and right side of
the peak, which respectively are denoted fronting and tailing. Fronting is usually the result
of overloading. This should be avoided during analytical chromatography, but is usually a
necessity during preparative chromatography to achieve a large productivity. Tailing occurs
when some sites of the stationary phase retain the solutes more strongly than others. The
degree of asymmetry can be measured as various quantities, where tailing usually is quantiﬁed
by the tailing factor (TF). The method is illustrated in Figure 2.6, where the distances A and

2.5
Resolution
Resolution is a quantitative expression of how good the separation of several compounds is,
which considers a combination of the selectivity, the eﬃciency and the total amount of mass
subjected to separation [4, 16]. The separation of two peaks, i and j, is usually expressed by
their resolution Rs:
Rs = 2 (tR,i −tR,j)
Wi + Wj
(2.13)
where tR and W are the retention time and baseline width of the respective compounds. A
large value of the resolution facilitates easy distinction between the peaks, where a value of
Rs > 1.5 corresponds to very little overlap if the peaks are of similar size and symmetrical
(TF = 1). For curvatures with minor tailing (e.g. TF = 1.2) a resolution of Rs > 2 may
be required. For a separation with multiple peaks, this criterion must be satisﬁed for the
least-well separated peak pair, which is referenced as the critical resolution.
2.6
Summary
This review of the concepts of liquid chromatography has formed the baseline for under-
standing the separation process and in terms modelling it. The column is ﬁlled with porous
material, which facilitates the separation or puriﬁcation of the sample mixture. The separa-
tion is attributed diﬀerent chemical phenomena depending on the mechanism applied. Two
of the most important types are adsorption chromatography, which usually is driven by hy-
drophobic eﬀects, and ion exchange chromatography, which separates charged molecules by an
ion exchange principle. The elution of the solutes is observed by a detector, which creates the
chromatogram. From this the retention time, eﬃciency, resolution and asymmetry factor may
be determined, which are all important factors for characterizing the separation capability of

Chapter 3
Modelling of the liquid
chromatography process
The previous chapter only considered linear chromatography. It is easily evaluated by algebraic
equations, due to the systems simplicity and as the equilibrium of each solute is decoupled
from the other species. This is not the case of non-linear chromatography, where non-linear
partial equations are normally used to describe the process. This vastly complicates the mod-
elling, however it is a necessity due to the non-ideality of the process. This chapter will review
and derive existing knowledge on modelling of liquid chromatography for various chromato-
graphic methods. This includes mass balances and thermodynamic relationships, along with
characterization methods for determining the system and compound speciﬁc parameters. The
thermodynamic interaction is the most important aspect of modelling the process, if the mass
transfer is not limiting. The evaluation of the equilibrium between the stationary and mobile
phase is considered the fulcrum point [22].

1. The column is unidimensional, i.e. radially homogeneous, which reduces the independent

3. The axial dispersion coeﬃcients of the components are constant.

3.3
Distribution isotherms
At this point the distribution isotherms will be reviewed. This is the equilibrium relation
between the solute concentration in the stationary phase q and in the mobile phase c and
thereby describes the mass transfer during the adsorption [29]. The equilibrium is coupled by
a function f, which typically depends on the concentration of the solutes, as q = f(c). This
relationship depends on the separation mechanisms the system is based upon, as the binding
mechanisms are diﬀerent. The model complexity is highly dependent upon the simpliﬁcations
made during the derivation. The general diﬀerences between the distribution isotherms are
whether it is ideal or non-ideal adsorption and whether the surface is considered homogeneous
or heterogeneous.
There are distinctions between single-component and multi-component
(competitive) isotherms, depending on the applicability to mixtures containing multiple so-
lutes. A selection of frequently used isotherms, with their respective application, is reviewed
below.

3.4
Determination of model parameters
In order to evaluate the retention factors of the solutes and in general model the chromato-
graphic column, several initial experiments are required in order to evaluate essential system
speciﬁc parameters [25, 34]. The precision of the mathematical model is greatly aﬀected by
any errors in the determined system parameters, meaning the accuracy is highly dependent
on the preliminary experiments. Three diﬀerent sources of error may be introduced to the
parameters during the estimation: Experimental error, error due to model simpliﬁcations and
numerical error from the simulation program. This stresses the essence of ensuring high qual-
ity experimental work and utilizing the same mathematical tools for estimation and modelling
for all data, as it ensures consistency of the computations and thereby reduce the eﬀect of the
last two types of error.
It has been proposed to structure the parameter determination process in several parts, ordered
by increasing complexity of their determination process [25, 34]:
1. Determine extra-column eﬀects.
2. Detector calibration.
3. Determination of void fraction, porosity and axial dispersion.
4. Estimation of adsorption equilibrium for pure components and mixtures.
5. Determine transport coeﬃcients for solutes.
The parameters may be evaluated from various techniques with varying accuracy and experi-
mental eﬀort as suggested by Schmidt-Traub et al. [25]. Reviewing the determination of the
transport coeﬃcients will be neglected, as the equilibrium-dispersive model is evaluated in this
situation, where they are irrelevant. The determination techniques generally comprises experi-
ments (E), parameter estimation (PE), moment analysis (MA) and correlations (C). They may
be ordered by both accuracy and experimental eﬀort as: E+PE+MA > E+PE > E+MA > C.
Isocratic experiments with pulse injections are usually the preferred experimental technique,
which can either be done by small- (peaks) or large pulses (breakthrough curves) with sub-
sequent analysis of the chromatogram. A large range of parameter estimation tools are used
during the determination of the isotherms (e.g. peak ﬁtting or inverse method), which will be
elaborated on later.

3.4.1
Determine extra-column eﬀects
The initial parameter determinations are conducted by evaluating whether the extra-column
eﬀects caused by the mixer and the tubing prior and subsequent to the column contributes to
the broadening [25, 34]. If this is the case, the volume of the extra-column must be evaluated
and treated separately during the chromatogram analysis. The extra-column volume (or dwell
volume) may be important to determine if large, as the parameter will otherwise be lumped
into the porosity of the column, providing erroneous results. The volume may be estimated
by “bypassing” the column by disconnecting it and replacing it with a zero volume connector,
thereby evaluating the corresponding retention time. The extra-column eﬀects are greater
during gradual elution.

Summary
A selection of frequently utilized mass balance models has been reviewed with the main fo-

selection of distribution isotherms, which are suitable for the cases covered during this thesis,

column. Isotherms speciﬁcally for ion-exchange and hydrophobic adsorption chromatogra-

parameters have been reviewed, which generally compromises moment analysis, parameter
ﬁtting and experimental determination.

Chapter 4
Implementation and simulation
This chapter is devoted to solving the system characterized in the previous chapter.
All
simulations will be conducted in Matlab 2017a. Generally, the method of solving the model
can be divided into analytical and numerical solutions [9]. Due to the non-linearity of the
chromatographic models, there only exists exact closed-form analytical solutions for relatively
simple models. No analytical solutions have been found for non-linear isotherms, except when
applying the ideal mass balance model. Analytical solutions similarly exist for the equilibrium-
dispersive model with a linear isotherm, but only for columns of inﬁnite length [9, 39–41]. The
analytical solutions are used as a well-known solution for a speciﬁc case, as well as an initial-
izing guess for certain numerical models. The literature suggests a large variety of methods
for solving the set of PDEs for the system, where the emphasis is on ﬁnite element (FE),
ﬁnite diﬀerence (FD) and recently ﬁnite volume (FV) solutions. The ﬁnite diﬀerence method
introduces greater computational errors, however it is simple and fast (for one-dimensional
columns), where the ﬁnite elements method is of higher accuracy and undergoes faster com-
putation times if multidimensional columns are used, but is of higher complexity. The ﬁnite
volume scheme is intermediate in terms of both complexity and accuracy.
It is chosen to utilize the equilibrium-dispersive mass balance, as the model provides accurate
results for moderate mass transfer and an exact closed-form analytical solution exists [9]. The
analytical solution does however have a limitation, as it is only applicable to a system of
inﬁnite length, i.e. the end boundary condition is:

These various ﬁnite approximations can be combined in many ways, however if both variables
are discretized, there is a practical limit due to the requirement of numerical stability and con-
trol of the numerical error. Each discretized partial derivative adds a truncation error, which
accumulates during the numerical solution, where appropriate selections of the discretization
must be in order. Several authors report of utilizing ﬁrst order accuracy FD methods for both
variables in an explicit scheme [9, 43]. The ﬁrst order discretizations do, however, introduce
truncation errors of the same order as the true dispersion of the solute. Thus a very ﬁne
discretization is required to reduce the numerical dispersion, which otherwise competes with
the apparent dispersion of the solutes. Various attempts to overcome this has been made,
either by the introduction of a correction term or by neglecting the apparent dispersion and
discretizing time and space accordingly to achieve numerical dispersion equal to the apparent
dispersion. These methods are however crude and inapplicable to non-linear isotherms, where
stability issues arise. Similarly, higher order discretizations of the explicit schemes introduce
stability issues.
Instead, the method of lines will be used for the implementations of the PDEs, where only
the spatial domain is discretized to recast the PDEs as a set of diﬀerential algebraic equa-
tions (DAEs). A minimum of second order accuracy is required, as the truncation error will
otherwise compete largely with the apparent dispersion. Higher order discretizations may be
used for increased accuracy. This thesis will consider ﬁnite schemes of both second and fourth
order of accuracy, where central ﬁnite diﬀerences will be used for both spatial terms.

4.4
Summary
The equilibrium-dispersive model has been implemented both analytically and numerically
with the system parameters stated by Qamar et al [14]. The analytical model is limited to
linear isotherms and columns of inﬁnite length, which restricts its applicability. The numerical
model is derived from the ﬁnite diﬀerence method, which poses a simple method of recasting
the partial diﬀerential equations as a set of diﬀerential algebraic equations.
This is done
both for second and fourth order accuracy, to illustrate the signiﬁcant performance boost
obtained from increasing the system order. The models are compared in order to validate the
derivation and implementation of the numerical model, which is found well behaved, where
the error decreases as the number of discretized segments increases.

a longer duration, it should be ﬂushed with 100-200 mL of the shipping solvent, which is

lower levels may strip away the bonded phase. The column is applicable to all common HPLC
grade organic solvents and ion pair reagents. Solutions or sequencing of solutions that might

limited to 275 bar. The column can be used at temperatures up to 75 ◦C, though prolonged

6.2
Experimental procedure
The determination of the extra-column eﬀects, detector calibration, porosity, and dispersion
coeﬃcients is highly similar to the experiments on hesperidin. Solutions of the pure enan-
tiomers dissolved in mobile phase are made, which are diluted to obtain a linear dilution
scheme. It is neglected to create the Van Deemter curve, as it is only applicable to the true
dispersion coeﬃcient, where the equilibrium-dispersive model uses an apparent dispersion
coeﬃcient, which then has to be ﬁtted to the experimental data.
Determining the isotherm parameters is similar to the case of hesperidin, however the inverse
method is extended to a non-linear isotherm range. The risk of locating a local minimum,
rather than the global, is considerable, as multiple independent parameters are ﬁtted simul-
taneously, where several of them have a large cross-correlation. The procedure of determining
the parameters should optimally be extended to include frontal analysis, to evaluate the appli-
cability of the inverse method by the deviation between the two methods. Another possibility
would involve simultaneous ﬁtting to a pulse injection and a breakthrough curve, which yields
more consistent results. This would save both time and material compared to extending to
frontal analysis. These considerations are reviewed during the actual determination of the
parameters.

Later during the experimental stage, some correlation

report a reaction between lactic acid and methanol, where lactic acid undergoes an esteriﬁ-

6.4
Summary
The separation of the sodium lactate enantiomers was facilitated on a chiral anion exchanger.
The process was initiated with a method development for the separation, where the mobile
phase and operational conditions were to be determined. Large diﬃculties were associated
with this determination, as the measurements were hard to reproduce and the lactate was
diﬃcult to detect. Methanol and acetonitrile were the primary solvents used, and two diﬀer-
ent buﬀer systems were investigated thoroughly: acetic acid/ammonium acetate and formic
acid/aqueous ammonia. The formic acid buﬀer was recommended by the column manufac-
turer, however regardless of any tweaking of the separation method it was found infeasible.
This is likely contributed impurities in the buﬀer. The highest absorbance of lactate was
observed around 225 nm, at which the absorbance of most of the solvents and buﬀers were
very large and introduced signiﬁcant interference. The detector signal from the lactate was
exceedingly low, wherefore mainly the non-linear range was investigable. All sample injec-
tions produced a large oﬀset from the baseline during the elution of the solutes, which caused
large problems regarding obtaining optimal peaks. Due to time limitations, it was required to
select a method, whereas the oﬀset was simply truncated. The ﬁnal mobile phase consisted
of methanol/acetic acid/ammonium acetate (100:2, v/v/pH=6.00). The HPLC was operated
at 25 ◦C and with a ﬂow rate of 1 mL/min. The isotherm parameters of the competitive bi-
Langmuir isotherm were determined for diﬀerent sample concentrations, where the average
parameters were determined. The dispersion coeﬃcient was found concentration dependent,
suggesting an invalid assumption of a constant apparent dispersion coeﬃcient. An excellent
ﬁt was, however, obtained for the single enantiomers and along a reasonable ﬁt to the system
injected with both enantiomers. It was possible to predict the retention time of the D-lactate
with an error of 1.9% and an error of 5.1% for the L-lactate.

Chapter 7
Extension to continuous operation
7.1
Introduction
The chromatographic processes mentioned up till this point are operated in a simple elution
mode. This means, that the separation is achieved through pulse injections to the column,
which is only operable in a continuum of batch operations. It is desired to extend the pro-
duction to a true continuous operation in order to maximize productivity and reduce the
operational costs by decreasing the solvent consumption [59]. This is particularly applicable
to the separation of enantiomers, as it is a binary separation. The column may be operated
in a counter-current mode, meaning the net velocity of the two solutes, which are introduced
in the feed, would be opposite. The optimality of counter-current processes is well known
throughout the chemical industry, where its application to chromatography has experienced
increased interest over the last years. The traditional approach of a counter-current ﬂow is
by opposing moving phases, i.e. the stationary and mobile phase. This is the principle of the
true moving bed (TMB), which is illustrated in Figure 7.1.
Figure 7.1: Schematic of a true moving bed system.
The system is classically divided into four sections. The feed is introduced between section
2 and 3, and the desorbent, which is pure mobile phase, is introduced between section 4 and
==PAGE:86==
7.1 Introduction
76
1. The system should be designed such, that the liquid leaving section 4 is solute free and
may therefore be recycled to section 1, to reduce the solvent consumption. Similarly, the solid
phase is recycled from section 1 to section 4. The less retained solute is carried by the ﬂuid
and collected in the raﬃnate, while the more retained solute is carried by the solid phase to
the extract port. There is no requirement of a complete resolution between the two solutes, in
order to obtain two pure products. This is advantageous when handling enantiomers, as their
retention times often are highly similar. The advantages of the TMB process are increased
productivity, which allows for a larger throughput, and the solvent consumption is reduced
by recycling it. The continuous process, however, poses a more complex system, where both
control and operation are increasingly complicated. Additionally, the TMB process introduces
experimental diﬃculties due to the moving solids, which causes mixing and attrition.
A
practical way of implementing the system and eliminating most of the disadvantages, is by a
simulated moving bed (SMB) system, which is illustrated in Figure 7.2. Rather than moving
the solid phase, the inlet and outlet ports to the unit are switched periodically with the ﬂow
direction, as indicated by the dashed arrows. Thus, this simulates the continuous counter-
current process in a discontinuous manner. The resemblance to the TMB is greater when each
section consists of a larger number of ﬁxed columns.
Figure 7.2: Schematic of a simulated moving bed system.
The SMB process utilizes a single column model as the building block for the overall system.
Mass balance equations are introduced at the nodes between columns, in order to connect
the columns. Implementing the SMB model is thus greatly simpliﬁed due to the previous
model derivations. A TMB model is simpler, but would require the development of a new
model, which introduces movement of the solids. It is neither physically feasible, due to the
operational diﬃculties, and the representation of a true SMB system would be slightly oﬀ. The
SMB process is advantageous both during operation and with retrospective to the previous

discretization, longer simulation times or increased solver accuracy inﬂuences the proﬁle. In
order to investigate the origin of the error, the mass balance model of each column is reduced
to the equilibrium-dispersive model to facilitate comparison with both the previously used
analytical and numerical model. They produced the same results for a single switch, which
suggests the error is not introduced during the upscale to multiple columns. It was thus not
possible to determine the origin of the error. The model is assumed to behave accordingly,
although there are slight deviations from the case study, as the overall model behaviour is
consistent. This concludes the case study, whereas the experimentally obtained data on lactate
is substituted into the model.

Separation of lactate enantiomers
The SMB model is reverted to the equilibrium-dispersive model, to apply the experimentally
determined parameters for the lactate enantiomers. The model easily iterates over the ﬁrst few
switches, until the solutes of a previous column reaches the current column, which is being fed.
The computation of the Jacobian to the ODE solver has an exceedingly long computational
time and the simulation never converges. Due to the complexity of the non-linear Jacobian of
the competitive bi-Langmuir isotherm, it is not possible to deduce an analytical expression for
the Jacobian to the ODE solver. A numerical Jacobian could possibly be supplied to the solver,
but due to time limitations this was not a possibility. It is thus required to use the solvers built-
in ﬁnite diﬀerence method on the supplied Jacobian pattern, which has long computational
times. It is realized, that the model cannot be solved as of now, which suggests the reason
that every author uses a non-equilibrium model for the SMB system. The transport-dispersive
model is thus adopted again. Due to time limitations, it was not possible to determine the
mass transfer coeﬃcients, however they could easily be determined by parameter ﬁtting with
the inverse method. Very large mass transfer coeﬃcients are assumed, as this corresponds to a
system with instantaneous equilibrium. Several studies have been reviewed, which all use mass
transfer coeﬃcients in the interval 0.1 - 3 s−1 [14, 14, 60, 62]. The mass transfer coeﬃcient has
thus been assumed to be 100 s−1. A sensitivity analysis was performed on the mass transfer
coeﬃcients, where it was found that a change of several magnitudes is required to observe any
diﬀerences in the outlet concentrations. Decreasing the mass transfer coeﬃcients results in the
same concentration proﬁle over the columns, where the concentration gradients are smoothed.

7.4
Summary
It was desired to extend the separation of the lactate enantiomers to a continuous method.
The true- and simulated moving bed were considered, where it was found favorable to use
the SMB model, due to its physical feasibility. In order to verify the expansion of the single
column model, the case study of Minceva et al. was reproduced [63]. Several case studies were
reviewed, which all uses the transport-dispersive mass balance model, whereas the current
model was updated to include mass transfer limitations. The concentration proﬁle reported
by Minceva et al. was obtained during the simulation, however the concentration was slightly
oﬀ.
Due to the accurate proﬁle of the system, the model was considered acceptable and
usable for further application to lactate. It was sought to revert to the equilibrium-dispersive
model, as the compound parameters were speciﬁed for this system. However, the ODE solver
exhibited convergence problems and it was not possible to simulate more than a few switches.
The transport-dispersive model is thus adopted again, where the mass transfer coeﬃcient is
assumed very high, as this approximates the equilibrium. The switch time and volumetric
ﬂow rates are determined through the triangle theory and all system parameters are thus
obtained. The separation is simulated, where a pure extract stream and almost pure raﬃnate
stream is obtained.

Chapter 8
Discussion
This chapter serves as an overview of the choices and results from the report, to put the
ﬁndings in perspective. This includes possible future considerations, as to improve the results
and at some point extend it to a state, where it is feasible for industrial implementation.
For the main scope of the thesis, it was chosen to operate with the equilibrium-dispersive
model, due to its simplicity and accurate results. It was recast into a DAE system by the
ﬁnite diﬀerence method of both second and fourth order accuracy. The model performance
was good and accurate results were obtained.
However, due to the high eﬃciency of the
columns used throughout the thesis, a very large number of discretized segments are required
to accurately predict behaviour of the system.
It would be relevant to either replace the
ﬁnite diﬀerence method by the ﬁnite element method, or to develop nth order ﬁnite diﬀerence
models, to reduce the simulation time of the system. The simulations have computed over
very large time spans, where the model development could have been eased signiﬁcantly by
reducing the simulation time. Another possibility is to utilize a variable grid size over the
spatial domain, where a coarse grid size could be applied to the areas without any solutes.
This is somewhat complicated, as the grid has to shift as a function of time.
The multivariate chain rule was applied to the time derivative of the isotherm in the equilibrium-
dispersive model. This step is problematic, as the Jacobian introduces strong non-linearities
to the model solution, which is time consuming and diﬃcult to solve in the SMB model. Ad-
ditionally, it is a non-conservative step, which requires the number of spatial segments to be
large. A diﬀerent method has been suggested, which strictly relies on conservation laws [64].
This would ensure mass conservation and increase the robustness of the model. Alternatively,
the assumption of an instantaneous equilibrium could be relaxed by including mass transfer
coeﬃcients in the model.
Problems were experienced during the hesperidin experiments due to the low purity of the
material. This could easily have been avoided by procuring higher grade hesperidin for the
characterization. At the current state of the work, it is possible to simulate the puriﬁcation of
hesperidin by HPLC. It is even possible to separate substances of similar chemical structure
==PAGE:95==
85
by the separation method. Any other solutes in a feed mixture would have to be characterized
as well, to extend it to an actual separation. The productivity of the system would, however,
be low due to the poor solubility of hesperidin in any tested solvent. To increase productivity,
a method should be formulated, which allows for dissolving a larger amount of hesperidin per
volume. The separation would be of interest for companies processing citrus peels, where the
hesperidin could be a valuable by-product. Due to the low solubility of the hesperidin, it is
bound to crystallize on the surfaces of the equipment, when the main products from the citrus
peels are puriﬁed. This could potentially be avoided by separating the hesperidin further
upstream.
The lactate characterizing experiments proved highly troublesome, due to interference in the
absorbance from both the solvent and the buﬀer system. It would be optimal to use substances,
that do not absorb at the same wavelength as the lactate, where other organic acids and
solvents could be tested.
This would allow for operating at lower lactate concentrations,
which would diminish the oﬀset, as the absorbance could be measured at a lower wavelength.
It is diﬃcult to apply frontal analysis, as the oﬀset is included in the absorbance and the
plateau is unsteady due to overshooting.
It would be interesting to redo the formic acid
experiments with higher purity chemicals, as the impurities were found to aﬀect the system
to a great extent.
The inconsistencies, between the work done by Franco et al.
and the
novel work, are likely contributed these impurities of the mobile phase components [56]. To
eliminate all discrepancies, the experiments should be performed with lithium lactate.
The calibration curves of the lactates are found highly dependant on small changes in the
mobile phase composition, wherefore it has to be redone for each batch of mobile phase. This
is important to be aware of, as erroneous results are otherwise obtained. It would be beneﬁcial
to extend the model to include mass transfer limitations, as the apparent dispersion coeﬃ-
cient is found dependant on the lactate concentration. A linear dependence is determined
for the apparent dispersion coeﬃcient. It is adequate for interpolating between single solute
concentrations, but for multiple solutes or in continuous operation, the approximation has to
rely on crude assumptions. A good ﬁt is obtained for the single solutes, where the deviations
of the retention time and the concentration proﬁle are minor. These slight deviations are
contributed the truncation of the peaks, where certain peak characteristics are removed. The
extension to both enantiomers introduces larger errors. This is likely due to the bi-Langmuir
model being insuﬃcient for determining the enantiomer-enantiomer interactions. It may also
originate from the truncation, as it introduces inconsistencies in the isotherm coeﬃcients.
The enantiomer-enantiomer interactions could be included by a quadratic isotherm. Another
improvement would be the extension to the generalized ion-exchange isotherm, which would
account for varying the buﬀer concentration. It is also possible by the bi-Langmuir model, if
the isotherm parameters of the competing ion are modelled. The overall results are satisfying,
and provides a reasonable approximation to the elution. The lactate experiments illustrates
the complexities of extending to multi-component systems, opposed to the hesperidin experi-
ments, which were conducted on a single component.
The separation method, for the lactate enantiomers, is developed for injections of 10 µL of a
solution with 10 g/L of each enantiomer. If larger amounts of lactate were injected, a better
resolution is required to avoid overlapping of the compounds. It would also be advantageous to
==PAGE:96==
86
use a column, which has a higher enantioselectivity. The Chiralpak QN AX column provides
a better separation, as illustrated in Figure 6.9. This is also the case of the Chiralpak MA(+)
column. In order to scale the process to an industrial scale, it is crucial to increase the accuracy
of the elution proﬁle. As the retention time of the enantiomers are highly similar, even small
deviations may prove critical for simulating the separation.
The separation is extended to continuous operation with the SMB method. Diﬃculties were
experienced with convergence, due to the non-linearities and the stiﬀness of the equilibrium-
dispersive model.
The problems are solved by including mass transfer limitations, which
stresses the relevance of extending the mass balance model. Some discrepancies were observed
in the case study of reproducing the results by Minceva et al [63]. In order to fully validate
the model, the comparison should be extended to an additional case study. It was possible
to determine the operational conditions from the triangle theory by linear assumptions. The
design could be optimized by rigorously evaluating the implicit constraints, which would
increase both the purity and the productivity. Very pure components are, however, obtained,
and the applicability of the SMB system is well illustrated.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
An introduction to the general concepts of HPLC has been conducted. Subsequently, mass
balances and the thermodynamic interactions and distribution isotherms have been reviewed
and derived.
This forms the basis of modelling the liquid chromatography system.
The
equilibrium-dispersive mass balance model, along with a selection of isotherms, have been
implemented by the ﬁnite diﬀerence method of both second and fourth order accuracy.
In order to validate the model derivation and implementation, a test study was made between
an analytical- and the numerical model for an arbitrary set of system parameters. An accurate
correlation between the two models was found, where the largest error was introduced at the
inﬂection point of the breakthrough curve. The model derivation and implementation was
veriﬁed from the relation between the sum of errors and the discretized step length. The
analytical model is only applicable for linear isotherms, where the numerical model can be
used for systems of higher complexity.
It was desired to simulate the puriﬁcation of hesperidin on a RPC column. The model was
adapted to the compound speciﬁc parameters of hesperidin, which were determined through
experimental eﬀorts. The hesperidin had a relatively low purity. This gave cause to sub-
par peaks with similarities to fronting, due to the detector’s inability to distinguish between
the solutes. An in depth method development was conducted, to ensure optimal conditions of
purifying the hesperidin. A very low solubility of hesperidin was observed in all tested solvents.
Therefore it was only possible to investigate the linear concentration range. Both the Henry
coeﬃcient and the apparent dispersion coeﬃcient were ﬁtted with the inverse method, by both
the analytical and numerical model, which produced consistent results. The conformity to the
elution proﬁle was excellent, which proved that the model is applicable to the system. Apart
from obtaining the substance speciﬁc parameters, this served as validation of the applicability
of the inverse method to this system.
The model was extended to handle multiple solutes, to predict the separation of sodium lactate
enantiomers. Similar experiments were conducted, as for the hesperidin system. Chiral ion-
exchange chromatography was used, where two diﬀerent buﬀer systems were tested: acetic
==PAGE:98==
88
acid/ammonium acetate and formic acid/ammonia. Large diﬃculties were associated with
detecting the lactate, due to the interference of the other constituents of the mobile phase.
This required operation with heavy overloading of the column, which produced an oﬀset from
the baseline. Despite these problems, a suitable separation method was determined, which
was applicable to predict the elution and separation of the enantiomers. A very good ﬁt was
obtained for injection of the single enantiomers, by the inverse method. For the injection
of both enantiomers, there were some discrepancies. This was contributed the enantiomer-
enantiomer interactions, which were not accounted for. The overall ﬁt is satisfying, where the
retention time is oﬀby 1.9% for D-lactate and 5.1% for L-lactate.
The model was extended to continuous operation by a simulated moving bed system, as this
allows for a larger productivity and a lower solvent consumption. This is essential due to
the large operational costs of downstream separation by liquid chromatography, where even
a slight increase in eﬃciency can be highly cost saving.
It was not possible to solve the
equilibrium-dispersion model due to convergence problems, whereas it was adapted to the
transport-dispersive mass balance model. This reduces the non-linearity and the stiﬀness of
the system, and facilitates evaluation of the process. The system is constituted of 8 inter-
connected Chiralpak QD-AX columns. The enantiopurities of the extract- and the raﬃnate
stream were, respectively, 100% D-lactate and 97% L-lactate. The system was found highly
applicable for separating the enantiomers.

UNCERTAINTY STATEMENT 
Uncertainty values in this document are expressed as Expanded Uncertainty (Ucrm) 
corresponding to the 95% confidence interval.  Ucrm is derived from the combined 
standard uncertainty multiplied by the coverage factor k, which is obtained from a t-
distribution and degrees of freedom. The components of combined standard uncertainty 
include the uncertainties due to characterization, homogeneity, long term stability, and 
short term stability (transport).  The components due to stability are generally considered 
to be negligible unless otherwise indicated by stability studies.
