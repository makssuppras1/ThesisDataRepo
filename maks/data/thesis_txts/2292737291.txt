Abstract 
MHC class II molecules are widely used in tetramer staining of CD4+ T cells. A fast 
and versatile method to produce MHC class II in E. coli exists, but so far only stable 
HLA-DR molecules have been successfully refolded. HLA-DQ molecules lose 
conformational structure when purified. Stable HLA-DQ molecules can be produced 
in insect cells, where the peptide is covalently attached to the β chain. By using 
sortase we reason that covalently attaching the peptide to the β chain, either before or 
during, the refolding will stabilize HLA-DQ molecules.  
 
In this master thesis two strategies for attaching a peptide to the β chain will be 
explored. One strategy attaches the peptide during refolding and the other attaches the 
peptide before refolding. Since sortase A is Ca2+ dependent, the refolding buffer based 
on PBS cannot be used. We will show that it is possible to refold MHC class II in a 
sortase A suitable buffer and that sortase A is active in that refolding buffer.  
 
The strategy with attaching the peptide before refolding has two requirements. First 
that SrtA is active in urea, and second that the β chain can withstand treatment of both 
low urea concentrations and higher temperature. We will show that sortase A is active 
in 1M urea buffer. Furthermore we will show that the β chain can withstand higher 
temperatures requered for sortase A activity.  
 
The combined results indicate that it is possible to attach a peptide to the β chain and 
hopefully allowing refolding of stable HLA-DQ molecules.

Resumé 
MHC klasse II molekyler anvendes ofte til tetramer farvning af CD4+ T celler. 
Fremstilling af MHC klasse II molekyler i E. coli er en hurtig og alsidig metode, men 
ind til nu har kun været muligt, at fremstille stabile HLA-DR molekyler. HLA-DQ 
molekyler mister deres struktur, når de bliver oprenset. Stabile HLA-DQ molekyler, 
hvor peptidet er kovalently bundet til β kæden, kan produceres i insekt celler. Vi tror, 
ved enten at binde peptidet covalently til β kæden før eller under refolding, at det vil 
stabilisere HLA-CQ molekyler. 
 
I denne master opgave vil to strategier, for binding af peptid til β kæden, blive 
undersøgt. Ved en strategi bliver peptidet bundet til β kæden under refoldingen mens i 
den anden strategi bliver peptidet bundet til β kæden før refolding. Idet sortase A er 
Ca2+ afhængig kan en refoldingsbuffer baseret på PBS ikke anvendes. Vi vil vise, at 
det er muligt at refolde MHC klasse II molekyler i en passende buffer for sortase A, 
og at sortase A er aktiv i denne refoldingsbuffer. 
 
Strategien, hvor peptidet bliver bundet til β kæden før refolding, kræver to ting. Den 
første er, at sortase A er aktiv i urea, og den anden, at β kæder kan overleve 
behandling af både lavere urea koncentrationer og højere tempereturer. Vi vil vise, at 
sortase A er aktiv i 1M urea buffer, og videre vil vi vise, at β kæder kan overleve 
temperaturer passende for sortase A aktivitet. 
 
Tilsammen viser resultaterne, at det er muligt, at binde et peptid til en β kæde og 
forhåbentlig tillade refolding af stabile HLA-DQ molekyler.

Introduction 
Tetramers have become a widely used tool for identifying and discriminating between 
different T cells. Studying the functionality and specificity of T cell populations is 
necessary for a mechanistic understanding of human diseases (101, 103). CD4+ T 
cells are important cells in the adaptive immune system as they are instrumental for 
generating effective immune responses against pathogenic microorganisms, vaccines 
and tumors autoantigens (101, 102). CD4+ T cells express T cell receptors (TcR) 
which recognize a unique peptide presented by Major Histocompatibility Complex 
(MHC) class II molecules on antigen presenting cells (101). MHC class II presents 
peptides which are derived from degradation of proteins in endosomes. This specific 
binding between peptide-MHC (pMHC) and TcR can be detected with tetramers. A 
tetramer is formed of four monomers bound to a fluorescently tagged streptavidin 
complex (102). 
 
Several approaches for the production of MHC class II molecules exists, each with 
their own advantages. The production in E. coli, where α and β chains are refolded 
with a peptide of choice, is a fast and versatile method for producing MHC class II 
molecules but also has some limitations (106). Refolding of HLA-DR molecules has 
been shown to be stable and functional (101), but this is not the case with HLA-DQ, 
which has been shown in previous work. It is possible to refold HLA-DQ molecules 
but as soon as purification is attempted the complex loses functionality. 
 
We reason that the refolded HLA-DQ molecule will stabilize if the peptide can be 
covalently attached to the β chain during refolding. This will also help to keep the 
versatility of the method intact. In the production of HLA-DQ by insect cells the 
peptide is covalently linked to the β chain during the expression and stable complexes 
can be formed (104). This attachment could be performed enzymatically by 
implementing necessary motifs to β chain and peptide.  
 
The enzyme sortase A (SrtA) is found in most gram-positive bacteria and is 
responsible for covalently attachment of surface proteins to the bacteria envelope. 
SrtA recognizes the motif LPXTG where X can be any amino acid. After recognition, 
the enzyme will cut between T and G, and attach the protein to a glycine linker (107). 
 
The construction and expression of a protein with a SrtA binding motif is time 
consuming and therefore the desired β chain will not be tested in this thesis. However 
we assume that implementing a SrtA binding motif to covalently attach the peptide to 
the N-terminal of the β chain will stabilize the refolded molecule. Therefore a range 
of steps, which could indicate the outcome, will be tested. The design of SrtA-DQB is 
based on HLA-DQB1*02:01 (Appendix B.1). 
 
The peptide can be attached either before or during the refolding. The advantage of 
attaching it during the refolding is that SrtA is added to the refolding buffer and no 
extra steps in the refolding process are needed. However a disadvantage is that during 
the refolding there is an excess of peptide which means SrtA can attach a peptide to 
the β chain that is not bound in the binding groove.

SrtA is a Ca2+ dependent enzyme which means it will not be active in a phosphate 
based buffer like PBS (Phosphate-buffered saline) since phosphate binds Ca2+ (108).  
Instead

optimized in PBS based buffer (106) and either the refolding should take place in a 
SrtA friendly buffer or a buffer change is needed before SrtA is added.  
 
We will conduct a refolding test in TBS and HEPES, and compare them to refolding 
in PBS. This will determine if a buffer change is needed for an attachment of peptide 
during refolding. If a buffer change is needed it could decrease the yield since results 
from unpublished work have shown that purification and especially buffer change 
destabilizes HLA-DQ molecules. 
 
Covalently attaching the peptide before refolding requires non optimal conditions for 
both β chain and SrtA. The purified β chain is contained in 8 M urea buffer (8M urea, 
150mM NaCl, 25mM Tris) and stored at −20 °C to −80 °C. SrtA should be active in 
1-2 M urea (110) but at higher concentrations urea will denature SrtA (111). The β 
chains will partly renature in 0.5-2M urea and combined with temperature treatment it 
could cause aggregation and degradation (138). The β chain would have to withstand 
decreased urea concentration and at the same time increased temperature for 1-16 
hours. Naturally SrtA is active at 37 °C but experiments have shown activity at lower 
temperatures around 20 °C (112, 113). Experiments are often conducted for 1-2 hours 
at 37 °C and approximately 16 hours when room temperature is used (113, 114). In 
this thesis we will test the activity of SrtA in 0.5-2M urea as well as the stability of β 
chains in various urea concentrations and temperatures. 
 
Hypothesis and aims 
We reason that attaching the peptide to the β chain should stabilize the refolded 
complex. The aim is to use sortase A to covalently attach a peptide to the N-terminal 
of the β chain by incorporating sortase A binding motifs.  
 
A number of tests have to be conducted in order to see which approach of the 
attachment that should be used: Attachment during refold (1) or attachment before 
refolding (2). 
 
1) Refolding have to occur in buffers not based on phosphate since sortase A is 
Ca2+ dependent. Buffers to be tested is Tris based and HEPES based buffers, 
since sortase A is active in them (109, 110). Different additives will also be 
tested. 
Sortase A activity have to be tested in buffers contain additives used in refold-
ing to support the proteins. Additive like glycerol, tween20 and pluronic F68 
will be tested (101, 106). 
 
2) Sortase A has to be active in urea concentrations up to 2M. Activity will be 
tested in concentrations between 0-2.5M urea buffers (110). 
β chains, normally stored in 8M urea buffer and −80 °C, have to withstand 
lower urea concentrations and temperature treatment. Urea concentration 
between 0.5-2M will be tested as well as temperature treatment at room 
temperature and 37 °C.

Theory 
In this chapter we will briefly explain the theory behind MHC class II, recombinant 
MHC class II production and sortase. In a previous project (unpublished) leading to 
this thesis a fully detailed version of MHC and its role in the immune system has been 
described.  Only the relevant part will be presented here. The description of 
recombinant MHC class II production in E. coli is based on protocols in Justensen et 
al. (106) and Braendstrup et al. (101). 
MHC class II 
MHC class II is one of the two major histocompatibility complexes. The complex 
derives from the degradation of exogenous antigen by antigen presenting cells (106, 
124). The complex is assembled with an invariant chain in RER (Rough Endoplasmic 
Reticulum) and transported through the golgi apparatus to endocytic pathway 
compartment. Here the invariant chain is degraded to the peptide fragment CLIP, 
which is exchanged with a peptide fragment from the endocytic pathway before the 
complex is presented on the cell surface. 
 
The complex consists of a α and β chain both of which have two domains (α1/α2 and 
β1/β2). The peptide binding groove is formed by α1 and β1 domains (106) as seen on 
Figure 1 whereas α2 and β2 domains are highly conservative and contains the 
transmembrane region which anchors the complex in the cell membrane. The 
transmembrane region is truncated in recombinant MHC class II production and is 
replaced by a leucine zipper (LZ) thus assembling α and β chains (106). The class II 
binding groove, which can be seen containing a peptide (red) in Figure 1, is open and 
allows binding of peptides longer than the 9 core amino acids. This open groove 
enables modifications of peptides for recombinant MHC class II production (101). 
Peptides can be targeted with e.g. affinity tags for purification or be attached to the β 
chain without affecting the recognition by T cell receptors (TcR) (101, 104).

Peptide loaded MHC (pMHC) class II is recognized by a unique TcR on a CD4+ T 
cell. TcRs are highly polymorph and undergo selection to establish and maintain 
immunity against pathogens and avoid auto immunity (101). When the CD4+ T cell 
recognize the pMHC class II it becomes activated and starts to proliferate and secrete 
cytokines. This activation starts a cascade of reactions in the immune system, and 
among others, activates B cells and cytotoxic T cell lymphocytes (CTLs) (101). The 
activated CD4+ T cells can also act as CTLs themselves (128). CTLs are capable of 
eliminating altered self cells by apoptosis. 
 
MHC class II is highly polymorph and consists of three iso types, HLA-DR, DQ and 
DP, all of which have many subtypes (106, 125). Even though there are many 
subtypes, only one set of DR, DQ and DP is inherited from each parent, thus limiting 
the variety of MHC class II in each individual. HLA subtypes can be linked together 
in haplotypes (125) e.g. the HLA A1-B8-DR3-DQ2 haplotype. MHC classes (I and II) 
can be linked to diseases e.g. celiac disease which is only seen in individuals with 
DQ2 (DQA1*05:01:DQB1*0201, HLA-DQ or DQA1*0201:DQB1*0202) or DQ8 
(DQA1*03:DQB1*0302) subtypes (104). This does not indicate that everyone with 
DQ2 or DQ8 will develop celiac disease, but there is a risk if an individual expresses 
one of those HLA types. This link between HLA type and disease is a common 
occurrence (125). 
 
Recombinant MHC class II can be used for tetramer production. Tetramers are a 
widely used tool for studying T cells (102). The pMHC-TcR binding is unstable and 
specific CD4+ T cells are represented in very low frequencies (from 1:1000 down to 
1:1 million), which is the reason for using tetramers instead of monomers. By 
tetramerizing MHC class II the binding to T cells is increased, since up to four 
pMHC-TcR interactions per tetramer are possible (101). Tetramers are produced by 
binding 4 biotinylated MHC class II monomers to a fluorescently tagged streptavidin 
which has a very high affinity to biotin (101, 102, 104). 
 
Recombinant MHC II production 
Different approaches have been made for the production of recombinant MHC class 
II. Basically the methods can be divided in two. Production in yeast, mammalian cells 
and insect cells are quite similar (133, 134). They all express folded molecules which 
only have to be purified, since they have RER and golgi apparatus. This makes them 
suitable for production of the different iso types of MHC class II: HLA-DR, DQ and 
DP. Although the methods produce stable and functional MHC class II molecules 
their disadvantage is the versatility of the methods (106). A construct for each pMHC 
have to be created, transformed into the given cell and expressed. Depending on the 
cell type this can be complicated and time consuming. 
 
The other approach is expression of α and β chains in E. coli followed by a refolding. 
The refolding is needed since E. coli lacks RER and golgi apparatus, and therefore is 
unable to fold proteins. The lack of protein folding contributes to a versatile method 
for MHC class II production. α and β chains are expressed separately and thereafter 
refolded with a peptide of choice (101). This leads to a very versatile method since 
every chain only have to be produced once and then refolded, compared to e.g. insect

cell where a new construct is needed for each new pMHC (134). In addition E. coli is 
easy to genetically manipulate and has a fast production rate (135).  
 
α and β chain construct 
Certain manipulations of the original amino acid sequence, of the different chains, are 
needed to optimize the production of MHC class II molecules in E. coli. As mention 
the C-terminal transmembrane region is truncated and replaced by a leucine zipper 
(LZ). Without the leucine zipper it is not possible to detect functional MHC class II 
(133). The leucine zipper consists of a pair of alpha helices, fos & jun, where a 
leucine is placed at every seventh residue. Leucine on the two helices interacts with 
the complement leucine on the other zipper, thus assembles α and β chains (133). The 
LZfos is placed at the C-terminal on α chains and LZjun is placed at the C-terminal on 
β chains (106). Furthermore a biotinylation signal peptide (BSP) has been added at 
the C-terminal of LZjun on the β chains. Between the chain sequences and leucine 
zippers (and between LZ and BSP) a glycine linker is added to allow the chains to be 
able to rotate to obtain proper refolded structure (133). 
 
The N-terminal sequences of the chains are also manipulated to enhance expression in 
E. coli. A methionine or natural histidine affinity tag (HAT) can be added to the N-
terminal of proteins to increase expression in E.coli (106). Due to the purification 
method any unpaired cysteines have to be modified (explained later). Substitution to a 
serine will only alter sulphur to oxygen but not the structure of the amino acid side 
chain. This modification can be a problem if it occurs in α1/β1 domains since it could 
change the affinity of peptides to the binding groove. Modifications in α2/β2 domains 
are not expected to have an influence on the peptide binding (106).

Expression and purification of α and β chains 
The proteins are expressed in a fermentor where d-Biotin and IPTG are added when 
optimal OD (Optical Density) is reached. Inclusion bodies are harvested after 3 hours, 
and proteins extracted from them (106, 136). The proteins are kept in denaturing but 
non-reducing buffer during purification thus maintaining already formed disulphide 
bonds. The proteins are purified on various columns and molecules with correctly 
formed disulphide bonds can be extracted. This approach has a major impact on later 
refolding yields since aggregation by incorrectly folded proteins are reduced (106, 
136). After the purification, the level of biotinylation is determined by Avidin test on 
SDS-PAGE as seen in Figure 3D, where an increasing amount of avidin will bind the 
β chain. Saturation is reached when the intensity of the β chain band is stable. 
Hereafter a biotinylation level, based on the avidin concentration, is calculated (101).  
Refolding of MHC class II 
For refolding to occur, a mix of α and β chain must be drop wised added to the 
refolding buffer which contains the peptide of choice and protease inhibitors (101). 
For purification purpose the peptide contains a His-tag at the C-terminal. This allows 
correctly folded pMHC to be purified by using IMAC (Immobilized Metal ion 
Affinity Chromatography). Using a two-step gradient, correctly folded molecules can 
be separated from low affinity molecules and collected in fractions (Figure 3A). SDS-
Page and AlphaScreen assay of the collected fractions determines which ones contain 
correctly folded molecules. An example can be seen in Figure 3B and Figure 3C.

Composition of the refolding buffer has been tested in Justensen et al. (106) where a 
buffer based on PBS, glycerol and Pluronic was chosen. Pluronic prevent attachment 
of proteins on surfaces, PBS is a buffer and glycerol helps stabilize the protein during 
refolding (137).  
 
Refolding buffer 
Many factors have been found to affect protein aggregation during refolding, which 
include temperature and buffer composition (138). The process of going from 
unfolded to folded proteins (or the other way) occurs by creation of intermediates. 
The cause of aggregation is patches of contiguous hydrophobic groups in the protein 
intermediate. This creates unstable intermediates during refolding that can aggregate. 
Compared to this, completely folded or unfolded proteins either have hydrophobic 
side chains buried out of contact with water or they are randomly scattered. The 
initially aggregates are soluble but gradually they become insoluble (138). Figure 4 
shows a diagram of the relations between unfolded, folded, intermediate and 
aggregated protein.   
 
Figure 4 Diagram showing the relations 
between unfolded proteins, folded proteins 
and aggregates (138). 
 
The kinetics of aggregation may be faster than folding kinetics, which will increase 
aggregation (137). This can be prevented by either modifying the structure of the 
protein or by changing solution conditions (137). Modification of the protein structure 
can be a substitution of a cysteine (106) and solution conditions can be modified by 
using additives (138). Additives can prevent protein aggregation by either enhancing 
the protein stability or by suppression of residue side chain interactions without 
interfering with the refolding process (137, 139).  
 
A widely range of additive has been used for different protein refolding or storage 
solutions. The additives can be divided into groups depending on their function. 
Kosmotropes (salts like NaCl, KCl and (NH4)2SO4) stabilizes intermolecular protein 
interactions and preventing intermolecular interactions that leads to aggregation (137). 
The folded protein is stabilized by increasing interfacial tension between protein 
surface and solvent, which increases the solubility of proteins and aggregates (139). 
Hence the intermediate aggregation decreases. Chaotropes (like urea) replaces or 
prevents intermolecular interactions that lead to aggregation. In high concentration

this ability makes urea a denaturant (137, 138). Sugars and polyhydric alcohols (like 
Sucrose, Glucose, PEG, and Glycerol) stabilize the protein with the smallest surface 
area, which are the folded protein. Since they interact more weakly with the protein 
than water, they are excluded from the protein surface thus stabilizing the folded 
protein (137). High concentration can stabilize the protein but at the same time 
decrease refolding (139). Detergents, like Tween, prevent aggregation and assist in 
the recovery of activity in the refolding process. They also dissolve aggregate, leading 
to refolding intermediates, which are turned into native enzymes (140). Finally small 
amino acids, like L-arginine and Glycine, may act as denaturant but their mechanism 
is still unclear (139). 
 
The choice of additive can differ depending on the buffer type. In PBS one additive 
enhances refolding while another additive enhances refolding in HEPES or TBS. 
Preventing protein aggregation during refolding is a trial and error process since no 
protein act in the same way. Increasing temperature can increase refolding rate but it 
also increases aggregation due to intermediate interactions (138). A optimal relation 
between buffer composition, temperature and protein have to be tested for each 
protein to determine optimal refolding conditions. 
 
Sortase 
The enzyme Sortase A (SrtA) from Staphylococcus aureus was discovered over a 
decade ago by Schneewind and colleagues (115, Mazmanian et al 1999) and since 
then several sortase subfamilies has been found. The group contains 4 subfamilies 
which attach proteins to the cell wall (class A, B, D and E), one assembles pili (class 
C) and one has a yet unknown function (class F) (115). A phylogenic tree (Figure 5) 
shows the correlation of sortase enzymes in different gram-posisitive bacteria.  
 
 
Figure 5 Phylogenic tree showing correlations between sortases in gram-positive bacteria 
arcording to their classes (115). 
 
==PAGE:17==
9 
 
 
Sortase enzymes are cysteine transpeptidases that covalently anchors proteins to most 
gram-positive bacteria envelopes (or link proteins together to form pili). The enzyme 
catalyzes the reaction by first cleaving a surface protein substrate at the binding motif 
LPXTG and then covalently anchors it to the peptidoglycan layer of the envelope 
(107, 119). 
 
The activity of SrtA is not essential for bacteria growth but has been shown to have a 
key role in reactions that facilitates adhesion, colonization and surface presentation of 
virulence factors for numerous infectious diseases involving gram-positive bacteria 
(115, 121). Gram-positive bacteria such as Staphylococcus aureus and Enterococcus 
faecium causes problems with antimicrobial resistance and SrtA inhibition could be a 
target to prevent virulence (109, 121). 
 
A large number of substrates are accepted by SrtA whereas other sortase sub families 
have a more specialized function and thus accept fewer substrates (115). Nearly all 
gram-positive bacteria contain one or more sortases and most have at least one class A 
sortase (119). SrtA’s recognition motif is highly conserved (116) but it is possible for 
SrtA to recognize binding motif similar to the conserved sorting signal (121). The 
binding motif LPXTG is kinetically favored as substrate over variants of the sequence 
(116). So far at least 800 sortase substrates in a wide range of bacteria have been 
identified (107, 115). 
 
Functionality of SrtA 
SrtA is located on the extracellular side of the envelope where it is a part of the cell 
wall sorting pathway of surface proteins. It targets proteins which have been partially 
secreted across the membrane and is anchored by hydrophobic domain in the 
membrane. The enzyme recognizes the binding motif LPXTG and cleaves off the 
protein before it attaches it to the peptidoglycan on the cell wall (Figure 6) (107). 
 
 
 
Figure 6: Cell wall sorting pathway of surface proteins in gram-positive bacteria. P1 is the 
surface protein which is transported across the membrane (P2), where SrtA binds it and 
cleaves between T and G (AI). Polyglycine on peptidoglycan (Lipid II) is covalently attached 
and SrtA is released (P3) (107). 
 
The transpeptidase mechanism of SrtA acts as a cysteine protease (122). Through a 
ping-pong mechanism the reaction is initiated when Cys184 performs a nucleophilic 
attack on the scissile peptide bond between threonine and glycine in the LPXTG motif 
(107). A transient tetrahedral intermediate is formed which upon breakage of the T-G 
==PAGE:18==
10 
 
 
peptide bond is rearranged into the more stable thioacyl enzyme-substrate linkage 
which can be seen in Figure 7 (ii-iv) (116). The exact role of His120 is not fully 
understood but as seen in Figure 7 (iii-iv) it is suggested that it supports the leaving 
glycine by protonating the amide group and deprotonating the amid group of the 
attacking polyglycine (116). Arg197 is shown to interact with the carbonyl group of the 
scissile bond in x-ray structures. Frankel et al (111) have proposed that the 
guanidinium group of Arg197 interact with the carbonyl group of the scissile bond and 
forms an oxyanion hole that may stabilize the acylated intermediate. Finally the 
amino group of the incoming glycine attacks the thioester of the thioacryl enzyme-
substrate and the deacylation step results in the regeneration of the free enzyme (132). 
In the lack of oligoglycine nucleophiles the acyl-enzyme intermediate is hydrolysed 
by a water molecule (118).

The three amino acids His120, Cys184 and Arg194 are highly conserved in sortase active 
site which indicates they are responsible for the reaction mechanism (116). 
Mutagenesis studies of the three amino acid residues shows that a mutation of Cys184 
resulted in complete loss of activity (132). Mutations of His120 and Arg197 shows 
significantly loss of activity (111) which strongly indicates that the amino acids, 
together with Cys184, are crucial for the activity of sortase. 
 
The activity of SrtA from S. aureus strongly depends on Ca2+ (108). In Figure 8A the 
difference of the structure with and without Ca2+ can be seen. The Ca2+ ion binds 
Glu171 in the β6/β7 loop and to amino acids in the β3/β4 loop and stabilizing the 
active closed conformation (108).

SrtA in molecular biology 
Wild type SrtA is a type II membrane protein that consists of two parts (118), an 
unstructured amino-terminal tail of nonpolar residues which is embedded in the 
membrane and a folded catalytic domain which performs the transpeptidase reaction 
(116). A truncated recombinant SrtA (aa60-201) has been produced and studies have 
showed activity similar to the wild type (122) and ability to ligate polyglycines 
instead of peptidoglycan (117). Only one amino acid after the LPXTG motif is needed 
for SrtA to recognize the substrate. Compared to SrtA conserved binding motif it is 
more promiscuous against the glycine substrate. Two or more glycine is optimal for 
ligation but even ligation with only one glycine is possible (117, 120).  
 
SrtA has developed into a powerful tool in molecular biology since it occurs at 
physiological conditions and can tolerate moderate organic solvents. It is used for 
ligation of proteins and peptides, protein modifications and immobilization (115, 
120). Protein labeling with fluorescent proteins, chemical reagents and inorganic 
probes has contributed significantly to antibody-mediated assays, spatiotemporal 
tracking of cellular proteins and studies on protein-protein interactions (123). Protein 
==PAGE:20==
12 
 
 
ligation has been used in vaccine production and therapeutic peptide cyclization to 
improve both peptide stability and affinity (122). 
 
Various modifications to SrtA have been made over the years to enhance the low 
activity of the enzyme. Among them is a circular SrtA which is more active in 
denaturing conditions than linear SrtA (122). Because the reaction is reversible the 
yield of SrtA ligation is low and long incubation times can decrease it further because 
of hydrolysis of the ligated products (123). This can be prevented by large excess of 
nucleophiles or introducing a hairpin structure. The principle of the hairpin structure 
can be seen in Figure 9.  The complement hairpin structures on the ligation product 
binds together and SrtA is not able to bind the motif (123).

AlphaScreen Technology (Perkin Elmer) 
AlphaScreen assay is a LOCI (Luminescent Oxygen Channeling Immunoassay) two-
bead assay system. Donor beads can convert ambient oxygen to energy-rich singlet 
oxygen upon illumination with a laser at a wavelength of 680 nm. This short-lived 
oxygen can amplify a signal in AlphaScreen acceptor beads which releases a signal in 
the range 520-620 nm. The released oxygen will only react with acceptor beads within 
a distance of 200 nm. Several types of acceptor beads are available, among others, 
AlphaScreen and AlphaLISA. AlphaScreen have Rubrene emission detectable in the 
range 520-620nm whereas AlphaLISA have Europium emission detectable in the 
range of 607-623nm. This much narrower range is useful for experiment conducted in 
serum. Donor beads are photosensitive thus working in dark or green light is 
recommended (www.PerkinElmer.com). 
 
The donor beads are pre-coated with streptavidin which bind to biotin. Unconjugated 
acceptor beads were coated with 9.3F10 antibody by following the manufactures 
recommendation (www.PerkinElmer.com). 9.3F10 coated acceptor beads were used 
to detect MHC class II, DR and DQ, molecules since the antibody recognizes the 
conformational structure. A pre-coated Nickel chelate acceptor bead were used to 
detect 6-HIS on peptides, both on refolded MHC molecules and sortase ligated 
peptides. 
 
The beads are diluted depending on the experiment, and is added the sample in equal 
amount (e.g. 15 μl beads + 15 μl sample). Phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) or Tris-
buffered saline (TBS) with 0.1% Pluronic F68 was used as dilution buffer for the 
beads. The incubation time depended on bead type and type of experiment. The 
samples were read in EnVision, 2104 Multilabel reader from PerkinElmer (105). 
 
The AlphaScreen assay is a sensitive assay and signals will decrease when saturation 
in the system is reached. This phenomenon is called a hooking effect. It can be seen 
by a peak when measuring a dilution series of a given substrate. To determine the 
optimal concentration of the measured substrate a concentration should be selected 
below the max capacity of the system, e.g. the linear part of the increasing signal. 
 
Refolding 
The refolding is based on published protocols (101) and results from previous 
unpublished work. A mix of α and β chain is drop wise added to the refolding buffer 
containing both peptide and protease inhibitors. After 3-7 days of incubating at 18° C 
(or room temperature) the refolds are either measured using AlphaScreen or purified 
by IMAC on an Äkta purifier. 
 
The α and β chains are contained in 8M urea buffer and must therefore be diluted at 
least 50 times, preferably 100 times (101, 106), to avoid interference from urea 
denaturation. The concentration of α and β chains added to the refolding buffer is 
100nM in a standard refolding (101). In a cross dilution assay the concentration used 
are from 0-50nM. The lower concentrations are used because of the hooking effect 
when measuring using AlphaScreen assay (105). Peptide is used in a 10 fold excess to 
prevent unnecessary refolding without a peptide present (101). The refolding buffer is 
based on PBS pH 7.4, glycerol as a stabilizer and pluronic F68 as a surfactant (105, 
==PAGE:24==
16 
 
 
106). For small pre-tests of refoldning (1ml), the refolding buffer is added directly to 
the tube containing α and β chain mix. 
Sortase activity 
The activity of sortase was measured by its ability to cleave between T and G in the 
binding motif. The Sensolyte® 520 Sortase A assay kit uses a FRET based substrate 
(peptide) to detect SrtA activity. The peptide consists of 5-FAM fluorescence end and 
a QXL quencher end, and in between is the sortase binding motif sequence (LPXTG). 
As long as the quencher is attached to the substrate it will absorb the emission from 5-
FAM. When the quencher is cleaved of the relative fluorescence units can be 
determined by a reader capable of detecting emission at 520nm with an excitation at 
490nm (www.anaspec.com). 
 
Emissions from the experiments were measured at 535nm with an excitation at 
485nm. Since all experiments were measured with the same emission and excitation 
the experiments are comparable. Recommended incubation time is 60 minutes but 
longer incubation (up to 180 minutes) was used due low activity. 
  
For measuring Sortases transpeptidase activity, a two peptide based assay was tested. 
A biotin-tag on one peptide and a His-tag on the other peptide, can combined, be 
measured by using Nickel chelate acceptor beads and Streptavidin Donor beads in the 
AlphaScreen assay.

Discussion 
In this chapter we will discuss the results of the experiments conducted. The goal of 
the thesis is to determine if it is possible to refold stable HLA-DQ molecules by 
covalently attaching a peptide to the β chain by using the transpeptidase reaction of 
sortase A. The results of several steps that are required in order to get a stable 
refolding of HLA-DQ molecules will be discussed.  
 
A two peptide AlphaScreen assay for determining the activity of SrtA was tested but 
the outcome was not conclusive. Several issues could have interfered with the assay 
hereunder sortase activity, buffer composition and competition for the beads. The 
assay is based on a peptide with His-tag and since both Sortase from AnaSpec and 
Sollid have a His-tag for purification purposes, this could have interfered with the 
assay. The activity of sortase could be the issue but tests with SrtA (AnaSpec) showed 
similar results. 
 
Therefore a FRET peptide based assay was used to determine activity. The difference 
of the two assays is that the AlphaScreen assay would measure the transpeptidation 
activity whereas the FRET peptide assay measures the cleaving by SrtA. In the FRET 
based assay sortase from Sollid and Buus showed less activity than sortase from 
AnaSpec.  
 
Two strategies were tested simultaneously. One strategy is to attach the peptide 
during the refolding. The advantage is that by adding SrtA to the refolding buffer, it 
would not require any extra steps in the refolding process. A disadvantage can be that 
SrtA could attach a peptide, which is not present in the binding groove since excess of 
peptide is present during the refolding. Excess peptide could be removed before 
adding SrtA but unpublished data have shown that refolded DQ molecules lose their 
conformational structure when removing excess peptide. Another disadvantage is that 
the ligation is reversible during long incubation times since SrtA can cleave an 
already ligated product. A hairpin structure could be included in the design to prevent 
SrtA from recognizing already ligated products (123).  
 
The second strategy is to attach the peptide before refolding. This will prevent the 
binding of a peptide outside of the binding groove. Also the reversible ligation can be 
avoided since the incubation time goes from 3-7 days to 2 hours. The disadvantage is 
that both SrtA and β chains have to withstand non-optimal conditions.  
 
Since SrtA is Ca2+ dependent the refolding has to be conducted in another buffer than 
PBS. SrtA has been shown to be active in TBS and HEPES buffer (109, 110). 
Refolding test with different additives showed that besides glycerol, Tween20 could 
be used to enhance refolding. HEPES turned out to be a better refolding buffer than 
TBS and PBS. There was no significant difference between using glycerol or 
Tween20 (Figure 13).  This result was unexpected since refolding buffers have pre-
viously been optimized (106).  
 
The activity of SrtA was tested in the same refolding buffers, except PBS, and a 
similar result was seen. But the difference was between the sortases used in the 
==PAGE:37==
29 
 
 
experiments. Sortase A from Sollid and AnaSpec showed improved activity in 
HEPES buffer compared to TBS buffer. Sortase A from Buus showed less activity in 
buffer containing Tween20 compared to glycerol. This could be because of the 
difference in the length of the different sortases. The catalytic domain of SrtA is 146 
amino acids (residues 60-206 of the wild type SrtA) (116).  SrtA (Buus) only contain 
those 146 amino acids whereas SrtA (Sollid) contain another 77 amino acids 
(Appendix B.2). The results indicates that a refolding buffer containing of HEPES and 
glycerol or Tween20, depending on the SrtA used, will be suitable for attaching the 
peptide during refolding. 
 
The strategy with attaching the peptide before refolding requires two things. The first 
is that SrtA is active in urea and the second is that the β chain can withstand treatment 
of both low urea concentrations and higher temperature. SrtA should be active in up 
to 2M urea (110) but above 2M it will start to denature (111). Experiment confirmed 
that SrtA is active in up to 2M urea, but above 1M the activity is negligible. Therefore 
the β chain has to withstand urea concentrations of 0.5-1M urea compared to the 8M 
they are stored in. This means that they will partly renature, thus risking aggregation. 
At the same time they will be temperature treated. The temperature treatment is 
necessary since SrtA need at least room temperature to be active (113) and 37 °C is 
preferable (114).  
 
As the results show, it looks like the β chain can withstand the treatment and still 
refold. This means that the aggregates formed during the treatment can be denatured 
again. 37 °C for 2 hours was better than 18 hours at room temperature, which can be 
because of the forming of irreversible aggregates or protease activity. Combining the 
results it could be possible to attach the peptide in 1M urea buffer at 37 °C for two 
hours before refolding.  
 
The possible aggregation could prevent the attachment of the peptide since 
aggregation could hinder free access to the sortase binding motif. That will make it 
impossible for sortase to cleave the β chain that, when cleaved, act as a glycine 
substrate. This will have to be tested when a β chain with sortase binding motif is 
produced.

Conclusion 
The goal of this thesis was to show if it is possible to produce stable HLA-DQ in E. 
coli. We have shown two strategies that utilize sortase A in order to covalently attach 
a peptide to the β chain.  
 
The first strategy involves attaching the peptide during refolding. We have shown that 
a HEPES buffer with either glycerol or Tween20 is a better refolding buffer for MHC 
class II molecules than PBS and TBS. Experiments of sortase A activity in various 
refolding buffers showed most activity in a HEPES buffer with either glycerol or 
Tween20. Unexpectedly, the additive had an influence on the activity of the different 
sortase.  
 
Attaching the peptide before refolding is the second strategy we attempted. Activity 
of sortase in urea buffers was tested and showed a considerable activity up to 1M 
urea. β chains were tested to see if they could withstand both partly renaturing by 
decreasing urea concentrations from 8M to 1-2M and higher temperatures for several 
hours. Refolding of the treated β chains showed that a considerable amount survived 
the treatment.  
 
Both strategies are promising and indicate that it will possible to attach a peptide both 
before and during refolding.

Future Perspectives 
Several experiments are still needed in order to reach the end goal of successfully 
refolding HLA-DQ in E. coli. 
 
Here we will present some ideas for future work and improvements on the 
experiments covered in this thesis. 
 
The two peptide AlphaScreen assay should be tested with a sortase A without His-tag 
in order to determine if it possible to detect transpeptidase activity. It would also be 
interesting to see if changing buffer from TBS to HEPES could make a difference. 
Finally testing a peptide with another tag e.g. NIP instead of His, could prevent 
influence from sortase A. 
 
Comparing refolding in PBS and HEPES buffer, since refolds up to now have been 
conducted in PBS. At the same time both glycerol and Tween20 as additive should be 
tested. The refolding should be conducted in large volumes for purification and 
further stability and functionality tests. 
 
Finally conducting experiments on a β chain with a sortase A binding motif should be 
done. This will be of great interest, if successful.
