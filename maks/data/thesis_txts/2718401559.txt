Abstract
Several studies have found a clear association between the indoor environment in classrooms
and the performance and well-being of the pupils. Since Danish pupils annually spend 20
% of their time at school, it is essential to provide the best educational environment possible.
In this intervention study, the indoor environment at Skt. Klemensskolen, a Danish pri-
mary school, in Odense was investigated in four classrooms. The decentralized ventilation
unit DEX3090 was installed in two classrooms, while the two other classrooms served as
control classrooms. The measured indoor environment parameters were CO2 concentration,
relative humidity, air temperature, VOCs, illuminance, and sound pressure level. Associ-
ations between selected indoor environment parameters and pupils’ cognitive performance
and well-being were studied. The recorded data were processed and analyzed in univariate
linear regression with a correlation and probability analysis.
It was found that the CO2 concentration in classrooms exceeded the recommended levels,
but the DEX3090 unit reduced the peak concentration in the classrooms by half. The illu-
minance and sound pressure levels in the classrooms were unchanged after the installation,
and the ventilation unit was insuﬃcient to meet current CO2 recommendations. The tem-
perature in the classrooms decreased by approximately 2 ◦C, likely due to seasonal changes
and government heating regulations.
The study also found several relationships between the indoor environment and pupils’ well-
being and performance, including a positive relationship between thermal comfort and mea-
sured temperature. Negative relationships were found between measured sound pressure level
and pupils’ perception of noise and occurrences of headaches and the measured CO2 concen-
tration and the pupils performance. The implementation of the ventilation unit increased
the energy use, but energy reduction measures can be taken.

Introduction
The purpose of ventilation in classrooms is to create indoor environmental conditions that
improve the pupils’ well-being, reduce discomfort, alleviate health problems and contribute
to reducing negative e↵ects on pupils’ learning and performance. The aim of this study was
to compare the performance of a decentralized ventilation solution to improve classroom
ventilation and the indoor environment in classrooms at Skt. Klemensskolen in Odense,
along with testing the impact on pupils’ well-being and performance. The impact of the
ventilation solution on the energy use is also investigated in this thesis.
1.1
Background
Studies ﬁnd that a poor indoor climate decreases the ability to focus and learn. Several
studies have shown that pupils were especially sensitive to a poor indoor climate in terms
of high temperatures and low air supply rates [59]. All studies suggest that better Indoor
Environmental Quality in classrooms leads to increased performance and well-being, making
it important to provide the best possible educational environment for pupils.
Danish primary schools are known for their long school days, as pupils spend more time in
school than the global average [39]. With the implementation of the new primary school
reform in 2014, the amount of time that Danish primary school pupils spend in school has
increased further. On average, a Danish primary school pupil spends 10,960 hours in school
over the mandatory 10 years of primary education in Denmark, compared to the global av-
erage of 7,540 hours. This means that Danish primary school pupils spend approximately
20% of their waking hours in school each year [26].
The Danish primary schools are built from before year 1800 until today[26]. Figure 1 shows
the cumulative area of primary schools in Denmark built from 1900-2015.
==PAGE:10==
Figure 1: The cumulative area of primary school buildings in Denmark from 1900-2015 and
their relation to the building code. [22]
Figure 1 illustrates the year in which di↵erent building regulations were implemented. As
seen in ﬁgure 1 more than 80% of the Danish school building mass was built before 1982, when
regulations in regards to insulation were introduced in the Danish building code. Only 10%
of school buildings were built after 1995, when the building code implemented requirements
for air quality in schools and under 5 % were built after the implementation of requirements
to the energy frame in 2006 [50] [26].
This means that the majority of Danish primary schools were originally built without me-
chanical ventilation systems and were only equipped with them if they were installed at a
later date. In 2021, an investigation into ventilation principles in Danish primary schools
revealed that 27% of primary schools still relied on natural ventilation as their primary ven-
tilation method[35].
In addition to the increase in school hours, the number of pupils per classroom has also risen
since 2009. Studies have shown that more than 20% of classrooms in Danish primary schools
have more than 24 pupils [26], which can contribute to an overloaded building system and
an aggravated indoor environment. These factors highlight the importance of a good indoor
environment in schools, as the quality of the learning environment depends on many factors,
including the indoor climate.

1.2
Objectives
The main objective of this thesis is to study how the implementation of a decentralized
ventilation system inﬂuences the conditions of the classrooms, the window opening behavior
of children and teachers, pupils’ perceptions of the environment in classrooms, their acute
health symptoms, their cognitive performance, and energy use.
For evaluation, six sub-
questions are deﬁned:
• How was the indoor environment prior to the intervention study and did it meet the
recommendations for school buildings?
• Do the installation of ventilation units improve the indoor environment to satisﬁed
standards?
• Can a relationship between the indoor environment and the pupils’ well-being be found?
• Can a relationship between the indoor environment and the pupils’ cognitive perfor-
mance be found?
• Can a relationship between pupils’ cognitive performance and their well-being be
found?
• How does the implementation of a mechanical ventilation solution a↵ect energy use?

2
Indoor Environment Quality in schools
Indoor Environment Quality (IEQ) encompasses the conditions inside a building, this in-
cludes: Indoor Air Quality (IAQ), thermal-, visual- and acoustic comfort. IAQ is the main
parameter in relation to ventilation, but it is important to work with all parameters when
planning or working with indoor environments in school buildings [30].
These comfort parameters function individually and together. All of them are bound to the
room, which can be deﬁned as a ﬁfth comfort parameter with variables such as materials,
size, cleanliness, and inventory for learning.
2.1
Thermal comfort
Thermal comfort is deﬁned as ”that condition of mind that expresses satisfaction with the
thermal environment and is assessed by subjective evaluation” [40].
The human body has a constant inner temperature of approx. 37 ◦C, which will not vary by
the temperature of the surroundings, unless it is under extreme circumstances. The condi-
tion of the humans’ constant inner temperature is a balance between the heat produced in
the organisms and the heat transferred to the surroundings [48].
From the knowledge of the human body heat balance the six following variables are used
to determine thermal comfort; radiant temperature, air temperature, air velocity, relative
humidity, clothing insulation, and activity level (or metabolic rate).
Figure 2: The six variables used to determine thermal comfort. (1) Radiant temperature, (2)
Air temperature, (3) Air velocity, (4) Relative humidity, (5) Clothing insulation, (6) Activity
level.
4
==PAGE:13==
The radiant temperature is deﬁned as the uniform temperature of absolute black surroundings
which will result in the same radiant heat loss as the actual environment taken into consider-
ation [52]. The air temperature is deﬁned as the temperature of the air around the body [14].
Both the radiant temperature and the air temperature can be measured and used to calculate
the operative temperature. The operative temperature is deﬁned as the uniform temperature
of an enclosure in which an occupant would exchange the same amount of heat by radia-
tion plus convection as in the existing non-uniform environment[14]. The equation for the
operative temperature is shown in 2.1.

Air velocity is a quantity deﬁned by its direction and magnitude. The quantity to be consid-
ered is the speed of the air, i.e. the magnitude of the velocity vector of the ﬂow at a certain
measuring point [14].
Relative humidity is deﬁned as the amount of water vapor in the air in relation to the max-
imum amount that it can contain at a given temperature and pressure [14].
Clothing insulation and activity level do not depend on the conditions in the classrooms
or the building but are individual for the occupants as opposed to the ﬁrst four variables.
Clothing insulation and activity level can be adjusted so that optimal thermal comfort is
achieved for the occupants [48]. The pupils in this study have not listed/informed their
clothing or activity level and the variables are therefore not analyzed in this study but do
have a large impact on the occupants’ thermal comfort.
Local thermal discomforts could be draughts, vertical air temperature di↵erences from dis-
placement ventilation, radiant temperature symmetry, and ﬂoor surface temperature [49].
These are not investigated in this study. The measurements performed in this study contain
the air temperature and relative humidity.

2.2
Indoor air quality
The atmosphere contains nitrogen, oxygen, argon, carbon dioxide, and small amounts of
other gases. The air quality changes when the ratio of these components changes. The
indoor air quality (IAQ) deﬁnes the quality of the indoor air in terms of the concentration
of the di↵erent components in the air. Bad indoor air quality can cause both biological and
psychological health risks, such as headaches and stress [56].
The purpose of using ventilation is to provide air of a quality that is perceived as acceptable
for most people. The ventilation will replace the pollution from sources, such as occupants
and buildings, by supplying air from outdoor sources [38].
Figure 3: Variables within air quality illustrated. (1) Outdoor concentration of CO2, (2)
CO2 production, (3) Air change.
Carbon dioxide (CO2) is often used as an indicator of indoor air quality, in spaces where the
main pollutant is the occupants, by building regulations and standards. The CO2 concentra-
tion builds up in a space by the respiration of the occupants. An average adult produces 20
L of CO2 per hour and 14-16 year old child produces 23 L of CO2 per hour, when performing
sedentary work [46] [7]. The production rate highly depends on the occupants’ activity level
and size. The fresh outside air in Denmark is approx. 400 ppm [41]. In this study, the CO2
concentration is measured.
Research has linked air pollution to acute and chronic illness and even premature deaths [1].
It is strongly linked to particulate matter (PM). Particulate matter is referred into varying
sizes from ultra-ﬁne particles with a size of less than 0.1 µm, ﬁne particles with a size of less
than 2.5 µm, and coarse particles with a size of up to 10 µm [27].
6
==PAGE:15==
Particulate matter is deﬁned as a mixture of both solid and liquid particles in air containing
hazardous materials, of various species of chemicals [21].
Another reason for illnesses is Volatile Organic Compounds, VOCs. VOCs are human-made
chemicals that often relates to the production and use of paints, refrigerants, pesticide,
building materials, and cleaning products in many forms. VOCs are emitted as gases from
di↵erent products either in a solid or liquid state, and many of them are used in households
so concentrations can increase up to 5 times higher than outside concentrations [2].
In order to reduce levels of VOCs the most eﬃcient factor is to reduce the use of materials
consisting of these compounds or to use them in regard to label precautions. If necessary,
higher ventilation rates should be used in order to attenuate the high concentration with
fresh air [2]. In this study, the amount of VOCs is measured.

2.3
Visual comfort
The human eye is created to see in daylight and it is therefore natural to create artiﬁcial
lighting with the same abilities as daylight to reach good visual comfort. Visual comfort is
deﬁned as ”a person occupying a room with either natural daylight or artiﬁcial lighting, while
expressing satisfaction towards the lighting conditions” [48].
Figure 4: Visual comfort illustrated. (1) Daylight, (2) Artiﬁcial lighting, (3) Internal solar
shading
The most fundamental measures of light in buildings are luminance, luminous intensity, lu-
minous ﬂux, and illuminance.
Luminance (candela/m2) is deﬁned as ”the luminous intensity of a surface in a speciﬁc di-
rection, divided by the projected area as viewed from that direction”. Unless the object is
perfectly di↵using, the luminance will vary with the angle from which it is viewed. A per-
fectly di↵using surface is called a Lambertian emitter [3].
Luminous intensity (candela) is a quantity that describes the power of a light source to emit
light in a given direction. Luminous intensity is the fraction that is emitted into a certain
direction or into a certain solid angle, where luminous ﬂux (lumen) reﬂects the total of all
emitted light [3].
Illuminance (lux) is deﬁned as ”the luminous ﬂux that is received per unit area of a sur-
face”. When stating the illuminance of a surface, orientation is necessary. For instance, the
illuminance on a desk is measured as horizontal illuminance and the illuminance on a wall
as vertical illuminance. Illuminance is a quantity that can be measured very easily with
illuminance meters or lux meters [3].
8
==PAGE:17==
The lighting environment in the occupied zone is a↵ected by three di↵erent factors: re-
ﬂectance (⇢), transmittance (⌧), and absorption (↵). The reﬂectance factor is an indicator
of how much light hitting an object will be reﬂected back. The transmittance factor indicates
how much light will go through the object. Absorption is a measure of how much light is
neither transmitted nor reﬂected but instead absorbed by the object. The unit is given as
percentages but can be normalized into units. The sum of reﬂectance, transmittance, and
absorption for any material is always 100% or 1 [3].
Daylight varies with the sun’s position, which varies with the time of day, time of year, and
location on earth. These daily, seasonal, and geographical changes are due to the earth’s
rotation around the sun and its daily rotation around its own axis, which is 23.5o tilted.
The relation between the weather and daylight is caused by the sky conditions, which a↵ect
the luminance distribution and level of daylight, as well as the daylight spectrum [18]. The
daylight factor and daylight autonomy are often used to describe the daylight conditions in
a zone.
The daylight factor is deﬁned as ”a daylight availability metric that expresses as a percentage
the amount of daylight available inside a room (on a work plane) compared to the amount of
unobstructed daylight available outside under overcast sky conditions” [53]. The equation for
the daylight factor is shown in equation 2.2. The daylight factor is expressed in percentage
and the unit for daylight is lux. Ev,d,med is dependent on the geographical location. The
value for Denmark is 14200 lux [36].

Regulations and guidelines for Danish primary schools
As previously mentioned, regulations within indoor environment were not implemented until
1995, when the Danish building regulation included an area requirement for each pupil, a
minimum ventilation rate, and a temperature range in the classrooms. Today the Danish
building code, BR18, includes regulations for all four IEQ comfort parameters.
The industry guidance, published in 2021, helps architects, engineers, and constructors of
school buildings, in the design phase and to ensure a good indoor environment for pupils
[6]. Recommendations in the industry guidance are divided into 2 categories - Standard and
Standard+. Standard is based on a balance between indoor environment, performance level,
energy use, and expenses, while still improving indoor environment compared to the Danish
building code. Standard+ is given as guidance in order to improve pupils learning. The
pupils’ performance levels are weighted higher in Standard+ than in Standard [6].
The Danish building code and the industry guidance are based on international, European,
and Danish standards, such as DS/EN ISO 7730 and DS/EN 16798. The regulations from
the Danish building code and recommendations from the industry guidance are shown in
table 5 in section 2.5.5 [42][6].

Ventilation
3.1
Ventilation principles
There are 3 types of ventilation installed in Danish classrooms; Balanced mechanical venti-
lation, exhaust, and natural ventilation. Each of these is described in detail in the following
sections [34].
3.1.1
Balanced mechanical ventilation
Balanced mechanical ventilation can be used in two di↵erent ways, either a central or de-
centralized ventilation system. A central system is typically a larger ventilation unit used
to ventilate an entire school or building. However, due to the size of a central system and
schools’ tendencies to have di↵erent room types, multiple decentral units are often installed
e.g classrooms, toilets, and chemistry rooms. A decentral system is more compact and in-
stalled on zone level. Such a system is easier manageable when renovating due to space. The
major di↵erences between the two di↵erent types are lower service and the total cost of a
central system. However, the investment can be divided into multiple stages with decentral
units, and the risk of problems with duct routing is minimized [24].
With the use of balanced ventilation four di↵erent control principles exist, CAV, VAV, DCV,
and aDCV. Each principle can be used individually or in combination in a ventilation system.
Constant Air Volume, CAV, is a simple way of controlling a ventilation system. CAV is
comparable to a time switch shutting o↵or turning on in a certain period of time. CAV
is recommended when the need for ventilation is similar during hours of use. It is a simple
and cheap control principle but uses more energy compared to other control principles in
balanced ventilation systems.
Variable Air Volume, VAV, is a more complicated control principle. VAV allows users to
vary between certain ventilation air ﬂows depending on the use. It is often dependent on
switches, clocks, and movement sensors for control. The use of VAV is recommended where
the ventilation need varies, without large di↵erences in heat loads. VAV is simple in terms of
installations and minimizes energy use. A disadvantage of VAV is that the ventilation rate
does not change depending on occupancy loads, but only on pre-speciﬁed control settings.
17
==PAGE:26==
Demand Controlled Ventilation, DCV, is a complicated control principle, and further sensors
are used in order to vary ventilation air ﬂows depending on use, heat, and occupant loads.
Use of DCV is recommended where use and loads vary during the day, often used in south-
facing classrooms, meeting rooms, and other areas with varying loads. DCV is expensive
in installation but reduces the cost of energy use such as space heating. Furthermore, it
automatically regulates air ﬂows to obtain optimum conditions.
Adaptive Demand Controlled Ventilation, aDCV, is a pressure regulating control principle.
It di↵ers from VAV and DCV by regulating pressure within the ventilation unit with de-
creasing air ﬂows. VAV and DCV have principle work by using constant pressure within the
ventilation unit. aDCV can obtain savings of 15-20% energy compared to a DCV principle.
The savings are especially gained with lower airﬂow levels [24].
3.1.2
Exhaust ventilation
The principle relies on depressurization in the building. An exhaust fan creates negative
pressure that pulls outside air into the building through leaks, vents, etc. The system is very
easy and cheap to install. Exhaust ventilation contributes to high heating and cooling cost.
Furthermore, it can be linked with poor air quality with di↵erent unwanted pollutants being
drawn into the building. [32].
3.1.3
Natural ventilation
Natural ventilation is driven by pressure di↵erences generated by wind and temperature dif-
ferences. Furthermore, it is highly dependent on external and internal conditions, local site
conditions, and building geometry. When it comes to natural ventilation through openings
there are two types; cross-ﬂow and single-sided. Cross-ﬂow ventilation is more e↵ective since
air ﬂows travel through the building, while single-sided ventilation can have either enhanced
or reduced air ﬂows due to wind and buoyancy forces working in the same or opposite direc-
tion.
There are 2 types of pressure di↵erences impacting natural ventilation, either buoyancy pres-
sure or wind pressure. Buoyancy pressure happens when there are temperature di↵erences
between indoors and outdoors. Due to di↵erences in temperature air densities cause pressure
gradients across the building which are di↵erent for various heights. These di↵erences cause
18
==PAGE:27==
air to enter the building through air leaks at lower levels and air escapes through leaks at
higher levels. The height where the transition of inﬂow and outﬂow occurs due to identical
air pressure indoors and outdoors is called the neutral plane. Wind pressure depends on
multiple factors such as the roughness of the terrain, the shielding of the building by its
surroundings, and the wind’s direction and speed [55].

4
The e↵ect of indoor environment on pupils perfor-
mance and well-being
This chapter outline a literature study regarding the e↵ect on pupils’ performance and well-
being of the indoor environment in classrooms based on di↵erent indoor environment pa-
rameters. The literature references for this study are listed in Appendix C.
4.1
Thermal
Research has shown that elevated temperatures in classrooms reduce the ability of pupils
to perform typical school tasks and that it has a substantial e↵ect on the learning process [10].
A study with several experiments was conducted in order to determine the impact of temper-
ature on the performance of pupils aged 10 to 12 in ﬁve di↵erent schools. The experiments
revealed that higher temperatures negatively a↵ected the pupils’ ability to complete school
tasks. However, when the air temperature was lowered from 25 ◦C to 20 ◦C, the pupils’
performance in arithmetic and language-based tests improved. The study found that the
speed at which the pupils completed the tasks increased by up to 4 % [58].
A meta-analysis found that the metabolic rate for pupils in elementary schools is about 15-20
% higher than adults, which is consistent with ﬁndings that suggest that children and adults
have di↵erent thermal perceptions. The analysis showed that the children prefer a classroom
temperature that is 2-3 ◦C lower than adults. This suggests that there are two perceptions
of the optimal operative temperature - one for pupils and one for teachers. Furthermore, the
meta-analysis showed that pupils performance in school decreases by approximately 20 %
when temperature goes from 20 ◦C to 30 ◦C, with the largest decrease occurring between 26
◦C and 20 ◦C. The analysis concluded that the optimal temperature in terms of performance
is lower than 22 ◦C for temperature climates such as in Denmark. Studies included in the
meta-analysis also showed that pupils’ relative performance tends to decrease at tempera-
tures below 20 ◦C [10].
4.2
Indoor air quality
A relationship between improved school performance and an increase in the outdoor air
supply rate, as well as the perception of fresher air by pupils, was found in a study. The
summary showed that increasing the air supply rate from 3.0 to 9.5 L/s per person led to
20
==PAGE:29==
a signiﬁcant improvement in the speed of numerical and language-based tests. The results
of the study indicated that doubling the outdoor air supply rate from 4 to 8 L/s per person
would improve school performance by 8% in terms of speed. However, the study did not ﬁnd
any measurable e↵ect in terms of errors [58].
An experiment also found improvements in school performance when the ventilation rate
was increased from 1.7 to 6.6 L/s per person in schools with pupils aged 10-12 years. The
increased ventilation rate resulted in a decrease in CO2 concentration from 1500 to 900 ppm,
which signiﬁcantly improved the pupils’ performance in terms of correct answers in number
comparison (4.8%), grammatical reasoning (3.2%), and reading and comprehension (7.4%).
As in the previous study [9], it was concluded that the increased ventilation rate did not
have any e↵ect on the error rate [43].
Multiple studies have been conducted and gathered in a study to investigate the relationship
between air quality and learning in schools. The results of these studies suggest that re-
ducing the concentration of CO2 in the air can signiﬁcantly improve learning outcomes. For
example, a reduction in CO2 concentration from 2100 ppm to 900 ppm was found to increase
the speed of learning by 12% and reduce errors made by 2%. Furthermore, a reduction in
CO2 concentration from 2400 ppm to 900 ppm was associated with a 5% improvement in
learning, while a reduction from 4200 ppm to 1000 ppm led to a 2.5% increase in daily
attendance among pupils. [9].
In a study of the relationship between ventilation rates and academic achievement, in the
southwest United States, it was found that there is strong evidence that higher ventilation
rates inﬂuence the performance of pupils. It was found that for every unit (1 L/s per person)
increase in ventilation rate within a range of 0.9-7.1 L/s, would increase the performance in
math by 2.9% and 2.7% for reading [11].
A recent intervention study in England examined the e↵ect of improved ventilation on the
performance of 200 school pupils. The study installed a mechanical ventilation system, which
increased the ventilation rate from approximately 1 L/s per person to 8 L/s per person. The
results of the study provided strong evidence that low ventilation rates negatively impact
the performance of pupils. Speciﬁcally, the improved ventilation was associated with faster
and more accurate responses in choice reaction tasks by 2.2%, an 8% improvement in picture
memory, a 15% improvement in word recognition, and a 2.7% improvement in color word
vigilance [12].
21
==PAGE:30==
4.3
Acoustics
In a research journal, 158 British pupils were exposed to 3 levels of noise in order to evalu-
ate the inﬂuence on their performance. Three di↵erent noise settings were created, a base
condition which is normal classroom conditions while quietly working. A babble condition
consisting of children￿s babble at 65 dBa. Lastly, a babble and environmental conditions
which are outdoor unwanted noise e.g traﬃc at 58 dBa in addition to the children￿s bab-
ble. From the experiment, it could be concluded that babble had a substantial impact on
performance in terms of reading, spelling, and speed of information processing of 16%, 20%,
and 26%. With respect to environmental noise, it only impacted negatively in terms of the
speed of information processing [45].
According to statistics, 50 % of those exposed to noise levels above 75 dBa for a prolonged
time lose their hearing. Teachers in the study approved these statistics and reported that
they su↵er from problems such as ear pain, ear ringing, and even hearing loss. Furthermore,
teachers reported that their work eﬃciency occasionally decreased due to discomfort because
of high sound pressure levels [5].
4.4
Visual comfort
Several studies have been conducted in the United States to examine the relationship be-
tween daylighting and student performance. One study conducted in 1999 found that pupils
in classrooms with high levels of daylight performed 7-18% better than those in classrooms
with the least daylight. Another study conducted in 2001 found that pupils located in class-
rooms with the most daylight had a 21% improvement in learning rates. [28].
A paper has conﬁrmed previous theories about the di↵erence in pupils performance between
winter and summer. The study found that students were approximately 21% more produc-
tive in summer, even though the buildings used for the study did not meet British Standards
for daylighting [37].
A study on the e↵ects of variable lighting in schools found that overall performance increased
when teachers changed the light settings for di↵erent purposes. When the light setting was
changed from ”standard” (300 lux) to ”concentrate” (1060 lux), test results showed that
students processed characters 16.8% faster, had a 28% decrease in total errors, and a 21%
decrease in errors of omission. The study also found that the ”standard” light setting, which
22
==PAGE:31==
is typically used at all times in schools, was only used 6.8% of the time. [4]
A similar study conducted in the Netherlands, dynamic light settings were tested to deter-
mine their e↵ect on pupils performance. The ”Focus” light setting (1000 lux) was found to
signiﬁcantly improve performance compared to the ”Standard” light setting (300 lux). The
experimental group had an average test score increase of approximately 30%. In contrast,
the control group, which did not have a change in light settings, had a 14% decrease in test
scores. The same tests were also conducted in classrooms without windows, and the ”Fo-
cus” light setting did not have a signiﬁcant impact on pupils performance, with a maximum
increase of 1% [8].
4.5
Summary
The following conclusions were drawn from the literature study:
The optimal temperature in regards to pupils performance in schools is around 22◦C. It has
been found that students generally prefer classrooms to be 2-3◦C cooler than adults. Pupils
performance can decrease by up to 20% if the temperature rises above this optimal level [10].
Several studies conﬁrm that a doubling in outdoor air supply from 4 to 8 L/s per person can
improve the performance of school work by 8 % in terms of speed [58]. Another study showed
that lowering the CO2 concentration from 2400 to 900 ppm by increasing the ventilation rate
can improve pupils learning by 5 % and a decrease in CO2 concentration can improve pupils’
attendance by 2.5% [9].
Pupils babble in classrooms can reduce their performance by approximately 16 % for read-
ing, 20% for spelling, and 26% for information processing [45].
A high level of daylight can improve performance by 7 to 18% compared to low-level daylight
in classrooms [28], while productivity due to daylight in classrooms can vary up to 21%
between summer and winter [37]. Increasing the illuminance in the classroom from 300 to
1000 lux can decrease pupils’ number of errors and errors of omission by 28% and 21% [4].

5.5.3
Cognitive performance
Two performance tests were performed on the pupils to examine their cognitive performance.
Cognitive functioning refers to multiple mental abilities, including learning, thinking, reason-
ing, remembering, problem-solving, decision-making, and attention [19]. The performance
tests was performed on the same platform as the questionnaire.
The ﬁrst performance test was based on Baddeley’s 3-min grammatical reasoning test and
is an e↵ective way of measuring reasoning, often used in studies on cognitive abilities and
the impact of stresses and environmental factors on cognitive performance [20]. The test
was modiﬁed, using geometrical shapes instead of letters and the test was in Danish. The
test consisted of 24 ﬁgures and statements that pupils shall go through. The scoring of the
Baddeley test was the number of correct answers to the statements. The results of the test
are the basis for an analysis of the pupils’ concentration and reasoning. A question from
the Baddeley test can be seen in ﬁgure 19. The test in its entirety can be seen in Appendix A.
Figure 19: Layout of the Baddeley test
To examine the e↵ect on pupils’ attention and concentration a d2-test was performed on the
pupils. The test is a simple identiﬁcation test of a certain combination of the letter ”d” with
two marks (”). The two marks (”) can either be above or under the ”d” or in a combination
with one above and one under the ”d”. To distract, a number of other combinations exist.
These combinations are ”d” with either 1, 3, or 4 marks called d1, d3, and d4. Furthermore,
another distraction is the letter ”p” with either 1 or 2 marks, called p1 and p2. The pupils
completed 14 rows of combinations to ﬁnish the test. Figure 20 illustrates one row in which
the pupils have 20 seconds to ﬁnish.
Figure 20: Layout of the d2-test
37
==PAGE:46==
The purpose for the pupils tested is to identify as many ”d2” as possible by marking them
with a line. The distractions should not be marked and will count as an error if marked.
Not marking ”d2” is also seen as a mistake. The results of the test are the basis for an anal-
ysis of the pupils’ concentration and attention. The indicators used to analyze the pupils’
performance are concentration e↵ort and concentration performance.
Concentration e↵ort, CE, is the number of errors subtracted from the total number of marked
characters processed. CE is calculated as shown in equation 5.1 and is the expression of con-
centration abilities since the total number of marked characters processed was modiﬁed by
the pupils’ carefulness in the testing(their total errors).
CE = E −TN
(5.1)
Where:
CE is the concentration e↵ort.
E is the sum of total errors, either errors of omission or commission.
TN is the total number of processed combinations, either relevant or irrelevant.
While CE was the ﬁrst expression of concentration abilities, it did have disadvantages with
failing cooperation from pupils, such as marking every single symbol or jumping over sym-
bols in the line. Because of this concentration performance, CP, was introduced. CP, is the
total sum of correctly marked symbols minus the sum of the number of non-target symbols
marked. CP is calculated as shown in equation 5.2. CP does not reward arbitrarily marked
symbols, the fake positives, but only rewards the correctly marked symbols, true positives.
CP = TCM −C
(5.2)
Where:
CP is the concentration performance.
TCM is the total sum of correctly marked symbols.
C is the sum of the numbers of non-target symbols marked.
In this study, both CE and CP are calculated and analyzed. A high positive score is seen as
good for both CE and CP. Both of them can have a negative score. This can be a sign of
failing cooperation, misunderstanding the test or bad concentration abilities.

5.6
Dynamic simulation
It has not been possible to obtain the speciﬁc energy use at classroom level at Skt. Kle-
mensskolen and a dynamic analysis is therefore carried out to evaluate the energy use in
the four classrooms with the use of the simulation program IDA Indoor Climate and Energy
(IDA ICE). IDA ICE is a simulation tool used to simulate the overall building performance
by numerous input parameters as seen in the following section. IDA ICE calculates many
aspects of the Indoor Environment at zone level, such as temperature, relative humidity,
CO2 concentration, etc. Figure 21 shows the classrooms modeled in IDA ICE.
Figure 21: The modeled classrooms from Sankt Klemensskolen in IDA ICE.
Two simulations are run, the ﬁrst with the heating set point at 19 ◦C to simulate the actual
2022 conditions, and the second simulation is run with the heating set point at 21 ◦C to
simulate the future conditions in the classrooms.
5.6.1
Input data to IDA ICE
The input data in the IDA ICE model are based on true conditions on Skt. Klemensskolen,
but since many conditions from Skt. Klemensskolen are uncertain, historic estimates, indus-
try guidance and Danish standards are used. The following sections describe the di↵erent
inputs, while the exact input parameters, schedules, and other information relevant to the
model are listed in Appendix B.
Geometry and constructions The geometry, window sizes, and orientation of the model
are based on measurements from the school, as described in section 5.2. Other input pa-
rameters such as inﬁltration, thermal bridges, and constructions are based on estimates of
39
==PAGE:48==
typical buildings from the same time period in the 1960s.
Installations During the preliminary investigations, it is observed that heating is done by
the use of radiators at the school. Due to the lacking information on the properties of these,
ideal heaters have been used in IDA ICE.
In order to mimic the DEX3090 aggregate in IDA ICE inputs in regard to the aggregate
are based on knowledge gathered from Exhausto through data sheets and direct information
from the product expert at Exhausto, Henning Grønbæk.
Schedules and setpoints The model consists of various schedules and set points that

Internal gains The internal gains are based on the number of teachers and pupils present

Discussion
The intervention study is an ongoing ﬁeld experiment performed in four classrooms on Skt.
Klemensskolen in Odense. The intervention study was undertaken to evaluate the perfor-
mance of a decentralized ventilation solution. The performance was evaluated by the indoor
environment in four classrooms, including CO2 concentration, temperature, relative humid-
ity, illuminance and sound pressure level, the e↵ect on pupils’ perceptions of the classroom
environment, their symptoms and cognitive performance, and the system energy use. In this
section, the main ﬁndings and associated sources of errors are discussed.
As seen in the results, pupils perform better at lower temperatures, and the measured air
temperature decreases over time in all classrooms due to the outdoor temperatures. There
is therefore no correlation between the installation of DEX 3090 and lower temperatures
in the classrooms. This could also be due to the fact that primary school can not heat to
more than 19 ◦C. To see whether the ventilation solution has an impact on the tempera-
tures in the classroom, it would be beneﬁcial to measure the temperatures over an entire year.
Previous studies, mentioned in section 4.1, show a decrease in the pupil’s relative performance
with temperatures below 20 ◦C, and the optimal temperature in terms of performance is lower
than 22 ◦C. Pupils should according to the literature, perform better at a temperature range
of 20-22 ◦C. The pupils in this intervention study performed best at temperatures below
22 ◦C. Two-thirds of the classrooms with temperatures below 22 ◦C were also intervention
classrooms with the DEX3090 installed. Opposed to the literature, the classroom with tem-
peratures below 20 ◦C performed better than the classrooms with temperatures above 22 ◦C.
The correlation analysis showed an increase in pupils’ performance with a decrease in CO2
concentration. The installation of DEX 3090 reduced the mean CO2 concentration with
approx. 6 % in classroom 2 and 20 % in classroom 3 compared to the ﬁrst measuring period.
The CO2 concentration in the control classrooms has in the same period increased by 60
% and 36 % for classrooms 1 and 4, respectively. This could be because of fewer window
openings with the outdoor temperature decreasing between measuring periods 1 and 2. The
large reduction in measured VOC is a good indicator of the eﬃciency of the DEX3090 unit.
The intervention classroom experienced a decrease of 4 to 7 times larger than the control
classrooms.
The biggest issue with air quality is found in the smallest classrooms 3 and 4. The CO2
90
==PAGE:99==
concentration is less than 1000 ppm in approx. 35 % of the occupied time. The pupils in
the smaller classrooms have approx. 9.3 and 9.8 m3 per pupil, while pupils in the larger
classrooms have 8.3 and 10.9 m3 per pupil. So the volume per pupil is not the contributing
factor. This could indicate a smaller air change in the smaller classrooms due to closed
shafts or a larger di↵erence in the occupied time than expected. The smaller classrooms are
occupied by the oldest pupils, who have longer days compared to the younger pupils.
The occupied time in this study is set from 8 am to 4 pm each weekday, but the pupils’
schedule is looser than this time range. As seen in ﬁgure 30 and 29 the pupils have no teach-
ing in the classrooms on Wednesdays, sometimes they are taught in di↵erent classrooms or
they are o↵from school at 1 pm. This included unoccupied time is a contributing factor
to lower CO2 concentrations in this study. The actual occupied time could be found with a
PIR sensor or by logging the already installed PIR sensor in the DEX3090 unit.
As previously mentioned, the illuminance measurements indicated an error since many data
points were 0. The correct way to measure illuminance is horizontally on a surface, while
Roomalyzer was wall mounted and measured the illuminance level vertically.
The mea-
surements can therefore not indicate whether the classrooms uphold the requirements or
recommendations from the current Danish building code or the industry guidance.
Despite the wrong placement of the Roomalyzer in terms of illuminance levels, high illumi-
nance does occur at certain time intervals in the di↵erent classrooms. Peaks in illuminance
in spotted in Classroom 2 in the morning period and in classroom 3 in the afternoon, due
to the classroom’s orientation. These peaks in illuminance are linked to a high increase in
temperatures of more than 28 ◦C.
A large correlation was found between the measured SPL and the performance indicator
CE in the d2-test, as seen in ﬁgure 56. The correlation was not investigated further due to
the varying correlations between the performance tests, but also due to observations dur-
ing test days. Several pupils were observed not cooperating by marking all symbols in the
test and since CE rewards fake positives, their CE score is not a symbol of their performance.
The pupils were tested twice in this study and from the ﬁrst alteration to the second, a
larger part of the pupils in the intervention classrooms performed better on the second test
day compared to the pupils in the control classrooms. This could indicate a performance
reward from repetition.

Upon collecting data from the HOBO state loggers placed in a window in each classroom,
it was clear that the loggers had fallen o↵, some had fallen out of windows and others were
missing after only a few days. Data from three remaining loggers were collected and from
these only four days of comparable data were analyzed. The analyses showed that control
classroom 4 had approximately 3 times as many window openings compared to intervention
classroom 3. Furthermore, the duration of the opening was more than 3 times as long, and
the mean duration of the opening was 44 minutes compared to 19 minutes in the intervention
classroom. The results are only based upon one of the 6 openable windows in the classroom,
which does make it uncertain to conclude that pupils in the mechanically ventilated class-
room do not feel the urge to enter fresh air actively through openings of windows. On the
other hand, it could state that pupils in the control classrooms feel it necessary to enter fresh
air into the building due to the observed poor indoor air quality and their more frequent
occurrences of headaches, as an example.
Data from the Roomalyzer and Vaisala devices have been merged to obtain more trustwor-
thy data in terms of CO2, especially. Both devices have a measuring interval of 5 minutes
meaning that the measuring time between measurements of temperature and illuminance
can have a di↵erence of less than 5 minutes. This can, but is not expected to have a large
inﬂuence on the results.
As seen in the results, intervention classrooms 2 and 3 have a higher total energy use than
control classrooms 1 and 4. For comparable rooms in terms of size results show that improv-
ing the indoor environment with the installation of DEX3090 comes with an energy increase
of 48 % for the larger classroom, and 83 % for the smaller classroom at a temperature
set-point of 19 ◦C. The energy increase is 37 % for both classroom sizes at a temperature
set-point of 21 ◦C.
Since the air quality is controlled by the installed ventilation solution, it could be recom-
mended to improve the building envelope by removing or closing the shafts. A reduction of
16 % and 21 % is seen for classrooms 2 and 3 after a simulation without the shaft. It should
be mentioned that it is unsure whether shafts are functional or not. Through observation,
some shafts had free passage through the ceiling and some were shut.
Results show an unwanted energy loss due to set points for heating and air supply temper-
ature. At a heating set-point of 19 ◦C, results show an increase in energy use due to the

The physical measurements of the CO2 concentration in the classrooms showed a larger de-
crease in the concentrations above 1000 ppm in the intervention classrooms but no major
di↵erence in concentrations under 1000 ppm as seen in ﬁgure 31 and 32. A conclusion can
therefore be drawn, that the DEX3090 unit is not designed correctly for loads in these spe-
ciﬁc classrooms. As mentioned the unit can supply up to 900 m3/h which in this case is not
suﬃcient in order to comply with regulations of 1000 ppm in the current Danish building
code. If correctly designed the unit should be able to keep a CO2 concentration of 900 ppm
due to the control principle where the unit will run at maximum capacity.
Despite indications of DEX3090 not being suﬃcient, it is seen that it has a positive impact
on the mean CO2 concentration and especially on peaks in CO2 concentration. As seen in
table 14 standard deviations in intervention classrooms decreased by 44 % and 48 % after
installation of the DEX3090-unit, which is a good indicator of that CO2 concentration peaks
are reduced signiﬁcantly.

for a year, more physical measurements will contribute to a better understanding of the in-

of the pupils’ performance and well-being in the classrooms.
In this thesis, the physical measurements were analyzed separately and the data were used to
ﬁnd correlations between the physical measurements, subjective evaluations, and the pupils’
cognitive performance. How the indoor climate parameters a↵ect each other is not analyzed
in depth.

Conclusion
In this thesis, the indoor environment and energy use on Skt. Klemensskolen was analyzed
before and after the implementation of the ventilation unit DEX3090. The e↵ect of the in-
door environment parameters on the pupils’ well-being and their performance, was analyzed.
The air temperatures measured in the classrooms are within the recommended temperature
ranges for operative temperature from winter situations in category C in DS/EN ISO 7730.
A temperature reduction of approximately 2 ◦C is seen from the ﬁrst measuring period to the
second. This is concluded to be because of seasonal changes in weather and the government-
regulated heating set point in schools.
The results showed that all four classrooms in the study experienced CO2 concentration
above the requirement of 1000 ppm from the current building code with peaks of up to 4500
ppm in some classrooms. With the implementation of the ventilation solution DEX3090, the
CO2 concentration decreases with the CO2 concentration peaks halved.
It has not been possible to measure the horizontal illuminance levels in the classrooms and
it can therefore not be concluded whether the classrooms uphold the regulations from the
building code. The illuminance level is unchanged after the installation of the ventilation
solution and the measured change in the vertical illuminance level is concluded to be from
seasonal changes in daylight.
The SPL in the classrooms is unchanged from before and after the installation and it can be
concluded that the ventilation solution does not a↵ect the classrooms’ SPL.
It can be concluded, that the ventilation solution DEX3090 is not suﬃcient to comply with
the recommendations on CO2 and it would be necessary to install a unit with a higher air
ﬂow rate.
Multiple relationships between pupils well being and the indoor environment is found. It
is found that the highest level of thermal comfort is achieved with a temperature of 21.7
◦C. In terms of pupils’ perception of noise and headaches, it is found that increases with
higher SPL. Higher levels of illuminance increased the pupils’ perception of brightness and
headaches, stating that higher illuminance levels will increase their well-being.
95
==PAGE:104==
Relationships between the indoor environment and the pupils’ performance are found. Pupils’
performance in the d2-test increased with a decrease in both temperature and CO2 concen-
tration. While the pupils’ performance in the Baddeley-test increased with an increased
illuminance level.
Relationships between pupils’ cognitive performance and well-being are found. Negative cor-
relations between the pupils’ performance in the d2-test and their perception of tired eyes,
headaches, and motivation are found. While a relationship between the pupils’ performance
score in the Baddeley-test and their stated ability to concentrate is found.
The implementation of the ventilation unit increases energy use according to simulations,
but with the implementation of a ventilation unit, several actions can be taken to reduce
energy use.

GMW90 Series CARBOCAP£ Carbon Dioxide, Temperature and Humidity Transmitters
for HVAC are based on new measurement technology for improved reliability and
stability. With the new technology the transmitter’s inspection interval is extended to
ﬁve years.
Designed for demand controlled
ventilation, GMW90 series transmitters
measure carbon dioxide and
temperature, with the option for
humidity measurements. The
instruments come with a calibration
certiﬁcate that meets traceability and
compliance requirements.
Reliability from unique
measurement technology
GMW90 series transmitters use
advanced Micro-Electro-Mechanical
System (MEMS) technology for
measuring carbon dioxide.
The CARBOCAP£ carbon dioxide
sensor's continuous reference
measurement enables reliable and
accurate readings and outstanding long-
term stability also in buildings with
round-the-clock occupancy.
The latest-generation CARBOCAP£
sensor no longer uses an incandescent
light bulb, which limits sensor lifetime.
This unique sensor consumes very little
power compared to other sensors
on the market. As a result, instrument
self-heating is low and humidity and
temperature can be measured correctly.
Convenient installation
GMW90 series transmitters have been
designed for quick and easy installation
and maintenance. Every model includes
a display for easy startup and convenient
maintenance. To protect the sensor from
dust and dirt during construction and
installation, the units can be cabled with
back-plate only. Electronics can be
snapped on later at an appropriate phase
in the construction project. DIP switches
make it quick and easy to conﬁgure
the transmitters.
Easy calibration
Regular instrument maintenance
guarantees a long product lifetime.
Calibration is easiest done with the
exchangeable measurement modules.
Sensor traceability and measurement
quality is easily maintained by snapping
on a new module calibrated at Vaisala
factory. The instrument can also be
calibrated using a handheld meter or
reference gas CO2 bottle. The service
interfaces are easy to reach by simply
sliding the cover down. The closed cover
keeps the measurement environment
stable during calibration and ensures
top-quality ﬁnal result.
