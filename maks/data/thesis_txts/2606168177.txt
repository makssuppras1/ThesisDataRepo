Abstract
The semiconductor industry is rapidly approaching fundamental limits in terms of feature
sizes with traditional bulk semiconductors. Thus, if the impressive development pace in
the industry is to be kept up, there is a need for alternative paths to explore. A promis-
ing alternative to traditional semiconductors is the world of 2D materials. This class of
materials contains the full range of materials from metals to insulators, all with highly en-
gineerable properties. Well known 2D materials, such as graphene and hexagonal boron
nitride, have already received a lot of attention in the research community, and have
been demonstrated to be suitable in many different applications. In this work, the transi-
tion metal dichalcogenide and 2D semiconductor molybdenum disulfide is under specific
investigation. In monolayer form, this semiconductor has a 1.8 eV direct bandgap, which
makes it interesting in a range of electronic applications. However, so far its low carrier
mobility has limited the practical applications of the material. Here, the interactions be-
tween monolayer molybdenum disulfide and a substrate are investigated and the state of
the art within field effect transistors using this 2D semiconductor is described. A fabrica-
tion process for FET devices with MoS2 channels is presented, and the best way to cre-
ate rough substrates using electron-beam lithography is specifically investigated. Finally,
using this knowledge it is investigated whether substrate-induced strain in molybdenum
disulfide monolayers can increase the mobility in the material, and thus make it a more
attractive candidate for device applications.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In the last century, the huge advances made within the semiconductor industry have
formed the basis of a huge part of the technology we use today. The invention of the
transistor in 1947 kick-started everything, and since then the industry has spun out tech-
nologies like solar cells, lasers, LEDs, and nonvolatile memory devices [1]. As the tech-
nology has evolved, the minimum feature size in all devices has decreased dramatically,
and in fact since the 1970s the decrease has been exponential. In the early 2000s the era
of nanoelectronics began when the minimum feature size was reduced below 100 nm [1],
and it is now down to few nanometers [2]. At this scale, the industry is thus approaching
fundamental limits in the world of 3D semiconductors, and the search for new materials
that can sustain the dramatic development in the semiconductor industry is on.
A path that seems to offer promising substitutes for traditional semiconductors is the world
of 2D materials. This research field was kick-started by Novoselov et al. in 2004 with the
discovery of graphene [3], which is an atomically thin plane of carbon atoms sitting in a
hexagonal lattice [4]. Graphene has many unique properties including high carrier mobil-
ity at room temperature ( > 200,000 cm2/V s) and fantastic thermal conductivity ( 5000
W/m K) [5]. It is almost transparent (97.7 % transmittance) [6], and it is extremely strong
with a Youngs modulus of 1.1 TPa and a fracture strength of 125 GPa [7]. These prop-
erties has led to graphene being used in a wide range of applications, including in solar
cells [8], as composite reinforcement [9], in catalysis [10], in sensors and in nanoelec-
tronics [11]. However, one drawback of graphene is the zero-bandgap in its monolayer
form. Among other things, this feature in graphene has sparked an interest in other 2D
materials such as hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) known as white graphene, and transition
metal dechalcogenides (TMDs) such as molybdenum disulfide (MoS2). The structure of
monolayer hBN is very similar to graphene, with the boron and nitrogen atoms sitting in a
planar hexagonal lattice [5], but as opposed to the semi-metal graphene, hBN is a wide-
gap semiconductor with a bandgap of 5.97 eV [5]. Many TMDs, e.g. MoS2 and WSe2 are
semiconductors in their monolayer form, with direct bandgaps on the order of 1-2 eV [12].
Both hBN and TMDs have been thoroughly researched and has found applications within
several fields. hBN is used as a dielectric in nanoelectronic devices [13], in protective
coatings [14], as gas sensors [15] and as an anode material in lithium ion batteries [16].
Meanwhile MoS2 among other things has been used in catalysis [17], in a wide range of
sensors [18, 19, 20], as an antibacterial system for wound healing [21] and in electronic
devices [22].
A large advantage of 2D materials is the fact that their properties are highly engineer-
able, due to the direct access to all atoms in the bulk of the material because of their 2D
nature. For instance, a bandgap can be opened and tuned in graphene e.g. by fluorina-
tion [23], doping with BN [24], through substrate interactions [25], through nanopatterning
[26] or by applying an external field [27]. The engineerability of material properties in 2D
materials is the subject of this project. In this work, the relatively low room temperature
carrier mobility of MoS2 is investigated, with the aim to use MoS2 as the channel ma-
terial in field effect transistor (FET) devices. The stability, relatively easy fabrication on
both small scale (exfoliation) and large scale (Chemical vapor deposition growth) [28, 29]
and the direct bandgap of 1.8 eV [30] of MoS2 monolayers make them a very interesting
candidate for nanoelectronics. However, the low carrier mobility has so far limited the

practical applications of the material. Many attempts have been made to raise the mobil-
ity in monolayer MoS2, e.g. through doping with high-κ dielectrics [31], through polymer
or hBN encapsulation [31, 32, 33] and through strain [34]. The most promising method so
far is the use of strain engineering as a way to raise mobility in MoS2, and that is exactly
the purpose of this project.
1.2
Thesis Scope
The scope of this work is to investigate the hypothesis that strain-induced in a MoS2
monolayer by the roughness of a substrate can significantly enhance the carrier mobility
in the monolayer. This is a further investigation of the results reported by Liu et al., who
achieved a maximum carrier mobility of 820 cm2/V s in a MoS2 monolayer on a delib-
erately roughened SiNx substrate, and saw a general trend of increasing mobility with
increasing substrate roughness, irrespective of the substrate material. The hypothesis
is tested by fabricating MoS2 FET devices on rough hBN and silicon dioxide (SiO2) sub-
strates. The roughness is made with electron-beam lithography (EBL) which gives a good
control over the appearance of the roughness. The substrates are patterned with different
roughness patterns, in order to test the effect of strain in both one and two-dimensions
and along different crystal directions. The MoS2 and hBN is mechanically exfoliated using
the scotch tape method [3] and the MoS2 is transferred to the patterned substrate using
cellulose acetate butyrate (CAB) transfer. In an additional two EBL steps, the contacts
for the devices are defined and deposited with e-beam evaporation, and the devices are
shaped with dry etching. The final devices are characterized electrically, and the rough-
ness and quality of the devices are characterized with atomic force microscopy (AFM) and
Raman spectroscopy. The fabrication of the devices has proved somewhat troublesome,
and several devices were fabricated that never worked. A large section of this report is
thus dedicated to the investigation of why these devices malfunctioned and to present
possible solutions to fix these problems in future projects.
1.3
Thesis Outline
This thesis is devided into several sections. Chapter 2 contains a description of 2D ma-
terials in general, in particular MoS2 and hBN. It describes the working principles of FET
devices in general, and presents a review of the state of the art within MoS2 FET devices.
Finally it contains a description of the interactions between a substrate and a TMD mono-
layer, including hysteresis effects and strain induction. A model based on the Hamaker
method is developed, that investigates the van der Waals (vdW) interactions between a
monolayer MoS2 and a patterned hBN substrate. In Chapter 3 the different techniques
used to fabricate and characterize devices are described. This includes mechanical exfo-
liation, CAB transfer, dry etching, EBL, different microscopy and spectroscopy techniques
and electrical characterization. The full fabrication process flow is also presented here.
Chapter 4 presents the results of all the experiments carried out during the project. This
includes investigations of different e-beam exposure strategies, electrical characterization
of the fabricated devices, and an investigation of the malfunctioning devices. The results
are discussed in Chapter 5, where possible explanations for the observations are also
stated. Finally, the conclusion on the experiments as well as the outlook of the project is
presented in Chapter 6.

Theoretical Background
In the following sections 2D materials and strain in those materials, FETs and the use of
2D materials in FET devices, as well as the influence of a substrate on TMD monolayers
will be discussed.
2.1
2D Materials
2D materials are a class of materials that are atomically thin in one dimension, but extend
out in the other two dimensions. Graphene is probably the best known example of this
type of material, and has gotten a lot of attention due to its unique and highly engineerable
properties, which stems from the 2D nature of the material. In the intrinsic form, monolayer
graphene has no bandgap [35], but this is one of the parameters that can be engineered,
e.g. via nanopatterning through lithography [26]. Graphene can be exfoliated from bulk
graphite, due to the layered structure of graphite [28]. Graphite is a stack of layers in
which carbon atoms have strong covalent bonds to each other in the plane, but each
layer is only weakly bound to the others through vdW forces. This structure enables
relatively easy exfoliation of a single layer of carbon atoms (a monolayer), which is known
as graphene. However, graphite is not the only material with this structure, and in fact the
2D material class includes both metallic, semiconducting, and insulating materials. For
the purpose of this project, focus will be on the class of 2D materials known as transition
metal dichalcogenides (TMDs), in particular the semiconducting TMD MoS2, as well as
on the wide-gap semiconductor hBN. The properties of these materials will be discussed
in the following sections.
2.1.1
TMDs
TMDs are one of the most researched class of 2D materials aside from graphene, due to
their relative stability and easy exfoliation [28, 34], and their unique and highly engineer-
able properties [36]. It is a broad class of materials, which includes both semiconducting
and metallic materials, but also more exotic materials like superconductors [37]. TMDs are
materials of the type MX2, where M is a transition metal (e.g. molybdenum or tungsten)
and X is a chalcogen (e.g. sulfur or selenium) [12]. In their bulk form, these materials are
inherently layered in the same manner as graphite, making it possible to exfoliate mono-
layers of material fairly easily. A sketch of a three-layer TMD and the structure within the
layers can be seen in figure 2.1.
TMD monolayers are not only one atom thick like graphene, as is illustrated in figure
2.1. In a monolayer the transition metal atoms are sandwiched between two layers of
chalcogens, e.g. a layer of Mo atoms sandwiched between two S layers in MoS2. The
atoms within a monolayer are covalently bound to each other, while each TMD layer is
bound to the next via vdW forces. Typically the bulk TMD crystal exists in different forms
that are distinguished by the stacking order of both the chalcogen/transition metal planes
withing a layer, and the layers in the bulk crystal [39]. Three general bulk forms exist: 1T,
2H and 3R. Generally, the 1T form exhibits metallic behavior, while the 2H and 3R forms
are semicondcuting [38]. The three forms are illustrated in figure 2.1b. The most common
bulk form of MoS2 is 2H, which is commercially available. The dimensions of an MoS2
monolayer from a 2H bulk crystal can be seen in figure 2.2.

In monolayer form most TMDs are semiconducting, which makes them suitable material
candidates for several different technologies, including photovoltaics [41], photodetectors
[42], and FETs [22]. Usually monolayer TMDs have bandgaps in the range 1-2 eV [12],
which due to quantum confinement in the ultrathin structures are direct bandgaps as op-
posed to their bulk counterparts [43]. Bulk MoS2 has an indirect bandgap of 1.2 eV [30],
while monolayer MoS2 has a direct bandgap of 1.8 eV [44]. The presence of a bandgap
in MoS2 and the fact that it is one of the most robust TMDs [39], makes it especially in-
teresting for applications in nanoelectronics, e.g. in transistors as is the purpose in this
project.
2.1.2
hBN
The main material used as substrate in this project is hBN, because it has an atomically
flat surface, a large bandgap, and no surface charges [40]. In many ways, boron nitride
behaves very much like carbon. This is, for instance, evident in the three main crystalline

forms in which boron nitride exists: hBN which resembles graphite, and sphalerite and
wurtzite boron nitride that are similar to a cubic and hexagonal diamond structure respec-
tively [45]. The most common form of crystalline boron nitride is hBN. Like graphite, hBN
has a layered structure. Each layer consists of a hexagonal lattice with alternating boron
(B) and nitrogen (N) atoms, and the layers are held together by vdW forces. The B-N
bond is covalent and has a typical bond length of 0.145 nm. Typically hBN exhibits AA’
stacking, meaning that each B atom in layer 1 is positioned over an N atom in layer 2 and
vice versa [45]. The typical structure of hBN is summarized in figure 2.3.
An hBN layer can be terminated by three different edges (see figure 2.3): The armchair
edge (AC), the boron terminated zigzag edge (B-ZZ) and the nitrogen terminated zigzag
edge (N-ZZ). In general, the N-ZZ is the most stable of the two zigzag edges [46].

Structure in the hBN substrate
For this project, the substrates are patterned to make them rough. This means that the
MoS2 placed on the hBN substrates will see several different hBN facets. The equipment
and recipe available for this project consistently yields an etch angle in hBN of 66◦[26].
This indicates that the exposed edge most likely consists of two-layer steps, as illustrated
in figures 2.4 and 2.5. This assumption is used in all calculations. In the following section
the hBN surface (hexagonal lattice), the armchair edge facet, and the zigzag edge facet
will be described.

vectors spanning the unit cell are a1 = a2 = 0.250 nm and there are exactly NB = NN =

2.2
TMD Transistors
The scope of this project is to investigate the effect of strain in MoS2 monolayers on the
electrical properties of the material, for the purpose of using MoS2 as the channel material
in FETs. In the following the physics of FETs will be explained, state of the art within FET
devices fabricated with TMDs will be discussed, and typical Key Performance Indicators
(KPIs) for these devices will be listed.
2.2.1
Field Effect Transistors
A transistor in the traditional sense is a multijunction semiconductor device, used to switch
or amplify electronic power and signals. Today transistors are mostly made of silicon, and
the key part of this technology is the p-n junction, which is a junction between two semi-
conductor bulks that are doped to have a surplus of free holes (p-type) and free electrons
(n-type) respectively [1]. Several different types of transistors exist, but only the FET
type will be investigated here. Transistors in general, and in particular the metal-oxide-
semiconductor FET (MOSFET), have revolutionized our technology, and forms the basis
of all modern electronics [48]. The first MOSFET ever produced in 1960 had a channel
length of 20 µm, and an oxide thickness of 100 nm [1]. Ever since the dimensions of the
transistor have shrunk in order to fit as many as possible onto a single chip. In 1971 the
first microprocessor was produced, containing around 2300 transistors with feature sizes
on the order of 10 µm, while in 2012 excess of 1 billion transistors fit on one processor
chip, all with channel lengths of 22 nm and oxide thicknesses around 1 nm [49]. This min-
imization has led to great advances in digital electronics. The basic physics and working
principles of the MOSFET will be described in the following sections.

Semiconductor Doping
A semiconductor is a material with a small bandgap on the order of a few eV. Due to
the small bandgap, some electrons can be excited to the conduction band via thermal
energy alone at room temperature. This means that only a small applied potential is
necessary to excite many electrons to the conduction band and cause a small current to
run through the material [1]. In the un-doped or intrinsic state, the semiconductor has
few to no impurities and the number of charge carriers in the material depends solely on
material properties such as the size of the bandgap [1]. In the doped or extrinsic state,
many impurities have been introduced into the semiconductor lattice, each with either
Electric Properties of 2D Materials
9
==PAGE:20==
more or less valence electrons than the bulk atoms [1]. In an n-doped semiconductor,
impurity atoms with extra valence electrons have been introduced, and these electrons
are easily excited to the conduction band due to their relatively small binding energy.
The impurity atoms, known as donors, thus effectively donate electrons to the conduction
band. In a p-doped semiconductor, the impurity atoms have fewer valence electrons than
the bulk atoms, and need extra electrons from the lattice to form the necessary bonds.
These atoms, known as acceptors, thus effectively accept electrons and generate holes
[1]. A p-doped semiconductor has a Fermi level close to the valence band and an n-doped
semiconductor has one close to the conduction band (see figure 2.8). Examples of donor-
and acceptor-impurities can be seen in figure 2.7. If the semiconductor is sufficiently
doped, the number of charge carriers is determined mainly by the doping concentration,
and the intrinsic properties become less significant [1].
2D semiconductors such as TMDs are very sensitive to their surroundings, due to their
large surface area and ultra thin nature. This means that there is a range of options when
doping 2D materials. In a similar manner to bulk semiconductors, 2D materials can be
doped by introducing defects in the form of vacancies or foreign atoms [50, 51]. However,
other methods such as physi- and chemisorption of gasses on the surface and surface
modification can also be used to dope 2D materials e.g. through electron charge transfer
or by perturbing the original band structure of the material [52]. MoS2 has a native n-
type doping, and a lot of research has gone into finding p-type dopants with one option
being Nb doping with CVD [51]. The wide range of options for doping TMDs and other 2D
materials, further increase the versatility of these materials and make them interesting in
a wide range of applications.
The p-n Junction
The ability to dope silicon locally enables the formation of so-called p-n junctions. Once
a junction between two such semiconductors is made, the electrons from the conduction
band on the n-doped side will start to drift into the valence band on the p-doped side to
minimize energy. The gain in energy depends on the bandgap of the semiconductor. The
electrons fill some of the empty holes states, and effectively annihilate the electron-hole
pair. This process thus builds up a region near the interface where no free carriers exist,
and where a net charge is present due to the formation of ions (see figure 2.9). This is
known as the depletion region, and is of great importance for e.g. the construction of
a FET. The charge on the n- and p-doped side of the junction is positive and negative,
respectively, resulting in the build-up of an electric field going from the n- to the p-doped
side [48]. This makes the system resemble a capacitor. Once the electric field builds up,
the electrons have to overcome a greater and greater potential to drift across the junction
and fill the empty hole states. An equilibrium arises, where the depletion region and the
resulting electric field grows to a size that yields a potential equal to the energy gain from
the annihilation process [48]. The equilibrium state is depicted in figure 2.9.

The MOSFET
A classic FET is a device with a semiconducting channel between a source and drain
electrode, where the channel conductivity is controlled by a transverse electric field sup-
plied by a gate electrode [53]. Several types of field effect transistors exist, but the most
widely used is the MOSFET [49]. These have been highly succesful due to their very low
power consumption and high fabrication yield [1]. Sketches of an n-channel MOSFET in
two different configurations can be seen in figure 2.10.

In the modern MOSFET, the semiconductor is usually silicon and the oxide is silicon diox-
ide [1, 48]. The basic working principle of the MOSFET is as follows: Two isolated doped
regions are created a short distance from each other in a differently doped semiconduc-
tor substrate. For an n-channel MOSFET, the substrate is p-doped and the two isolated
regions are n-doped, and vice versa for a p-channel MOSFET. This is effectively just two
pn-junctions placed a small distance apart (see figure 2.10a) [48]. An electrode is placed
on top of each of the junctions, and these are the source and drain electrodes. In this
configuration, no current can run between the source and drain without any outside in-
fluence, due to the depletion region formed at each junction. Between the two junctions,
an oxide layer and a third electrode, the gate, is added on top of the substrate, and the
substrate is grounded [1]. In some cases, no gate is added on top of the channel, and the
doped substrate is then acting as a so-called back gate. When a voltage is applied to the
gate electrode (VG), the gate and substrate act as the two plates in a capacitor, meaning
that charge will accumulate at the oxide/substrate interface. In an n-channel device, the
voltage applied is positive with respect to the substrate, resulting in electrons being at-
tracted to the oxide/substrate interface. If the voltage is larger than some threshold (Vth),
enough electrons are attracted to make the region right underneath the oxide n-like, effec-
tively opening up a current channel between the source and drain electrodes (see figure
2.10b). This effect is what is known as the field effect [48]. When the gate voltage is above
the threshold, the current running through the channel is controlled by the voltage applied
over the source/drain (VSD). The output of the transistor, i.e. the current running through
the device known as the source/drain current (ISD), is characterized by three regimes:
Subthreshold, Linear, and Saturation [1]. In the subthreshold regime, the gate voltage is
below the threshold voltage, and the channel region only has a few carriers. However, a
small current, the subthreshold current, can still run between the source and drain due to
carrier drift, weakening the contrast between the on and off states of the device if it is too
large. In this region the relation between ISD and Vg is log-linear. The parameter used to

2.2.2
TMD FET
In pursuit of still smaller, flexible and low power transistors, 2D semiconductors have
received a lot of attention, and particularly MoS2 has proven to be an attractive channel
material in FETs [19, 22, 54]. They are constructed slightly different from the traditional
MOSFET, but the operational principle is very similar. A sketch of a TMD FET can be
seen in figure 2.12. These devices usually consist of a highly doped silicon substrate,
with a thin silicon dioxide layer on top. The TMD is placed on top of the oxide layer.
Contacts are added on both sides of the channel to form the source and drain electrodes.
In a top gated FET a gate dielectric and a gate electrode is also added (see figure 2.12).
The choice of gate dielectric has been found to be especially important for this kind of
transistor, because the mobility in the TMD can be significantly increased by choosing a

dielectric with a high dielectric constant [22, 37]. The substrate can also function as the
gate electrode (back gated FET), and the conductivity of the TMD channel is controlled
by applying a voltage to the gate, just as in the traditional MOSFET.
Aside from the electronic properties of the FET channel itself, the channel contacts, namely
the source and drain electrodes, are very important for the effectiveness of the device.
For this reason, contact resistance is a common KPI for TMD FET devices. Poorly fabri-
cated contacts or a poor choice of contact material, leads to large contact resistances and
ultimately small current flows and poor device performance. Overcoming the large con-
tact resistance between a 2D material and a bulk 3D metal, has been one of the largest
challenges in creating 2D electronic devices [55]. Contacts to bulk semiconductors, e.g.
silicon, are characterized by covalent bonds between the metal and the semiconductor.
In this configuration the contact resistance stems from the Schottky barrier formed at the
metal/semiconductor interface due to differences in the work function of the materials [55].
2D materials on the other hand, do not necessarily form these covalent bonds, but are
instead contacted through a small vdW gap. The vdW gap acts as an additional tunnel
barrier to the existing Schottky barrier, and ultimately increases the contact resistance
of the interface. One approach to improve the contact resistance is to contact the edge
of the 2D material (edge contacts) instead of the surface (top contacts), as this should
shorten the bonding length and create stronger hybridization at the interface [55]. How-
ever, since the surface to edge ratio of 2D materials is very large, pure edge contacts are
hard to fabricate, and top contacts are commonly used [22, 54, 56, 57]. Fabrication of
via-contacts, i.e. contacts embedded in e.g. an hBN layer, has also proven to yield lower
contact resistance [58], but give some challenges in the fabrication process. For these
reasons top contacts are used here. Although top contacts cover a larger area of the 2D
material, it has been proven that the length of the contact that contributes to the charge
transfer (charge transfer length) is in the range 2-74 nm for few layer MoS2 contacted
by Ti [59]. With contacts usually being of micrometer size, this renders the length of the
contacts irrelevant. Reported contact resistances for monolayer MoS2 is in the range 10
kΩto 1 MΩ[55]. For MoS2 channel devices, gold is frequently used as a contact mate-
rial [22, 56, 60], and often in combination with titanium [20, 54, 61, 62, 63]. This metal
combination leads to more or less ohmic contacts, and a subsequent annealing of the
device can further improve the contact [20, 22, 54, 61]. Results comparable to the metal
contacts were also achieved by Roy et al., who reported an all 2D material transistor, with
graphene contacts [64].

some TMD FET devices, in particular MoS2 devices, and their performance with respect

an n-channel MOSFET [19, 22, 56, 64, 65]. Usual KPIs for the electronic properties
of TMD FET devices are the carrier mobility in the channel, the on/off current ratio, the
subthreshold swing [37], and in some cases the channel width and length are also used
to compare devices [54]. The on/off current ratio and subthreshold swing are related to
the resolution of the device. The on/off current ratio describes the difference between
the on and off states of the device, i.e. how well a ’1’ can be distinguished from a ’0’. In
general this ratio is in the range of 103 −106 for a range of reported MoS2 devices [20,
33, 54, 60, 61, 65], while a few devices have reached ratios higher than 106 [22, 62, 64].
For a traditional MOSFET, the subthreshold swing is in the range 70-100 mV/decade, and
similar results are reported for MoS2 FETs with a range of 70-360 mV/dec [19, 33, 56, 62,

2.3
TMDs on Substrates
The choice of substrate for devices with 2D materials is not trivial. As opposed to 3D
materials, the atoms in a 2D monolayer will all be very close to the substrate and the
interaction between the two thus becomes important for the properties of the monolayer. In
the following sections, the interactions between atoms as well as monolayer and substrate
will be discussed. The effect of the substrate in terms of strain on the monolayer will be
explored, and a simple model to estimate induced strain in a MoS2 monolayer on an hBN
substrate will be developed using the Hamaker method.
2.3.1
Substrate Interactions
When a molecule approaches another molecule or a surface it will start to interact with the
other molecule or the atoms in the surface when the distance is a few Å. This interaction
consists of a range of dipole-dipole interactions known as vdW forces, and is a purely
physical interaction, meaning that no electrons are shared between the two molecules
[67]. If the molecule is brought even closer to the surface or molecule and there is sufficient
energy in the system, a chemical interaction can happen, meaning that the two parts will
share electrons and be bound very strongly together [67]. The chemical interaction is
much stronger than the vdW interactions, but also require more energy. As previously
discussed, TMDs are layered materials by nature, and the layers are held together by
vdW forces. It is therefore reasonable to assume that the interaction between a TMD
monolayer and a substrate at room temperature is also a vdW interaction. This interaction
and the consequences of it will be discussed and described in more depth in the following
sections.

where r is the distance between the two interacting objects, and typically the London dis-
persion forces are dominating [69]. The potential energy U between to objects in this

The Hamaker Method
Forces such as vdW forces are in general a description of the interaction between pairs of
atoms or molecules. However, they also play a role on the macroscopic scale, e.g. in the
stickiness of a geckos feet [76]. One way to compute the energy and force between two
objects, e.g. two silicon wafers, due to vdW forces is the method proposed by Hamaker
in 1937 [77]. The assumptions necessary for this method are [78]:
1. Additivity: The total interaction between the objects is the sum of all pairwise inter-
actions between the atoms in the objects.
2. Continuous medium: The sum over interactions can be replaced by integration over
the volumes of the interacting objects, with number density, ρ, of atoms, each of
which occupies a volume dV .
3. Uniform medium: The material properties of the objects are constant, i.e. the num-
ber density, ρ, of atoms and the interaction constant are in fact constant over the
volume of the objects.

2.3.3
Hysteresis
Hysteresis is generally an unwanted effect in FET devices. In this context hysteresis is
the shift that may occur in the transfer characteristics during a gate voltage sweep. This is
effectively a shift of the threshold voltage during the gate sweep. Effectively, the properties
of the device becomes dependent on the specific sweep parameters, which hinders the
examination and comparison of true device properties [80]. The origin of the hysteresis
in MoS2 devices is still debated, but is generally attributed to the adsorption of water
molecules on the surface [54, 81], oxide charge traps [81], charge traps at the oxide/MoS2
inferface [80] and gate voltage stress [82]. The extrinsic effects, e.g. the adsorption of
water and oxygen molecules can be minimized by characterizing devices in controlled
atmospheres, or by encapsulating the MoS2 in e.g. Si3N4 [54]. The rate and range of the
Electric Properties of 2D Materials
23
==PAGE:34==
gate voltage sweep has also been proven to have an influence on the hysteresis, with a
slower rate and large range both increasing the hysteresis [82]. Experiments suggest that
the reason for the hysteresis increase with slower sweep rate, is that the charge trapping
processes in MoS2 FET devices measured in ambient conditions, are rather slow [54],
and a fast sweep rate is thus preferable to minimize the effects of hysteresis [34, 54].
2.3.4
Strain
The final substrate effect to be discussed here, is the influence of strain on the properties
of TMD monolayers. It is well known that straining thin films affects the electronic band
structure of the material. This is e.g. utilized when designing catalysts by depositing and
overlayer of a transition metal on a substrate with a different lattice constant. This leads to
either a strain or stress of the overlayer and a resulting increase or decrease in reactivity
due to shifts in the d-band [67]. In a similar manner, calculations and experiments show
that straining 2D materials, e.g. TMDs, yields changes to the band structure, and thus the
electrical and optical properties of the material. Conley et al. observed a linear decrease
of the bandgap in MoS2 mono- and bilayers on the order of 45 meV/% strain and 120
meV/% strain respectively, as well as a decrease in photoluminescence with strain, indi-
cating a direct to indirect bandgap transition [83]. These findings are supported by Yu et
al., who used first principles calculations to predict the direct/indirect bandgap transition
and the semiconductor/metal transition as caused by biaxial strain. The direct to indirect
bandgap transition in a MoS2 monolayer happens under biaxial strain when εx + εy =
0.66% and the semiconductor/metal transition happens at εx +εy = 19.06%, with εx being
strain along the armchair direction and εy along the zigzag direction [84]. These findings
may be used as guidelines for device fabrication that utilizes strain to control electrical
output, e.g. a flexible transistor device using strain and the semiconductor/metal transi-
tion as the effective gate [85]. Another interesting consequence of the predicted change in
bandstructure is that the effective carrier mass and thus the carrier mobility also changes
[84]. For isotropic biaxial strain, the effective carrier mass is expected to also be isotropic.
However, it responds to strain differently along different crystal directions with the arm-
chair direction giving the largest response. The carrier mobility is also expected to be
isotropic under isotropic biaxial strain, with a predicted one order of magnitude increase
in the electron mobility for 9.5% isotropic strain. Bertolazzi et al. proved that high qual-
ity crystalline MoS2 monolayers can sustain strains of up to 11% [30], meaning that this
ten-fold enhancement of the mobility should in theory be attainable. In fact, Liu et al.
have utilized rough substrates to induce strain in MoS2 monolayers and seen a signifi-
cant increase in mobility, with a maximum of 820 cm2/V s on a rough SiNx substrates
[34], with a strain assumed to be much smaller than 9.5%. A general trend in increasing
mobility with increasing surface roughness is also observed. The goal of this project is to
further inverstigate how rough substrates can be utilized to enhance the mobility in MoS2
monolayers.
Designing roughness
Since the direction of the strain and the level of isotropy of the strain is predicted to have
an effect on the resulting TMD mobility, three different types of patterns are chosen for
the device substrates, to cover a wide range of strain effects. The first pattern is a close
packed array of holes, which creates a two-dimensional roughness (figure 2.17a). This
pattern creates roughness similar to that used by Liu et al. when it is overexposed (see
section 4.2), and should on average induce isotropic strain in any TMD monolayers placed
on top of it. The other two patterns are both close packed line arrays, and thus one-
dimensional in nature. One pattern is 20 x 20 µm and consists of an array of long lines
(figure 2.17b). When placing a TMD monolayer on this pattern, the entire flake is strained
in the same direction, but devices can be made at different angles with respect to the lines,

Fabrication
The fabrication of TMD FETs on rough substrates involves several fabrication steps. First,
the substrates are created and patterned, the TMD monolayers are exfoliated and trans-
ferred to the substrates, and then the devices can be created by adding metal contacts
and shaping the TMD channel. The following sections give an overview of the complete
fabrication process flow and the individual techniques used in the fabrication and char-
acterization of the devices. The process flow will be described in section 3.3 and the
individual fabrication and characterization techniques will be discussed in section 3.1 and
3.2, respectively.
3.1
Fabrication Techniques
The fabrication of TMD FET devices involves exfoliation and characterization of hBN sub-
strates and TMD monolayers, transferring of TMD monolayers using cellulose acetate bu-
tyrate (CAB) transfer, advanced electron-beam lithography (EBL) and dry etching. These
techniques and how they have been utilized are described in this section.
3.1.1
Mechanical Exfoliation
Mechanical exfoliation using the scotch tape method [3, 28] is used to prepare both hBN
substrates and TMD monolayers. Depending on the material, different kinds of tape are
used for the exfoliation of crystals onto SiO2/Si substrates. The following sections de-
scribe the exfoliation and identification of hBN flakes and MoS2 monolayers in detail.
Exfoliation of hBN substrates
The mechanical exfoliation is done using the scotch tape method and consists of several
steps. First, millimeter sized hBN crystals are deposited on to the mold-tape (EPL BT-
150E-KL, by Nitto) (figure 3.1a). The crystal is split into several smaller crystals and
spread over the surface of the tape, by tapping the larger crystal to different positions on
the adhesive surface of the tape (figure 3.1b). A copy of the hBN crystals is made by
applying a piece of thermal release copy-tape (SWT 20+ Lb B 140.0 MM, by Nitto), to
the crystal covered surface of the mold-tape (figure 3.1c), and removing it again. Several
copies can be made of the same mold-tape, but each copy is only used once. Because
the exfoliated hBN flakes will be used as substrates for electrical devices, chips with high-
quality Nova silicon oxide on top of highly doped silicon are used. This enables the use
of the silicon as a back gate, and minimize the risk of fatal gate leakage through the SiO2
when a gate voltage is applied. Before transferring the hBN flakes to an Si/SiO2 chip,
the chip is cleaned. This involves baking the chip at 200◦C for 5 minutes to remove water
(figure 3.1d), and an O2 plasma ash at 300 mbar pressure and 120 W power in the Plasma
Etch PE-50 plasma asher (figure 3.1e). Immediately after the plasma ash, the copy-tape
is applied to the chip, with the hBN covered side towards the SiO2 surface of the chip. The
back of the tape is rubbed gently with the back end of a diamond scriber to enhance the
flake transfer yield (figure 3.1f). The chip is then heated to 80◦C while it is held in place by
a vacuum pump, to facilitate easy removal of the thermal release tape with minimal tape
residue left on the chip surface (figure 3.1g). Once heated, the tape is slowly removed at
a low angle, to not destroy the transferred hBN flakes, and discarded. The chip is now
ready to be inspected in a microscope in order to identify useful flakes (figure 3.1h).

CAB Transfer
A fairly simple way to transfer TMD monolayers from the exfoliation substrates to the
patterned device substrates is the CAB transfer technique [88]. This technique is gentle to
the TMD, since it involves a minimum of heating, and allows for fairly easy alignment of the
TMD with the substrate patterns. The chip with exfoliated TMDs is covered with the CAB
polymer, and since the SiO2 substrate is hydrophilic, a drop of water can creep in between
the substrate and polymer to gently lift the CAB covered TMD from the substrate. The
CAB covered TMD can then be picked up and aligned with the pattern on the hBN or SiO2
device substrates. The alignment is done on a so-called stacker, which is a microscope
fitted with 5x and 10x objectives, a vacuum pump, a small heater, and a micromanipulator
on the stage. The CAB process is described in detail below and illustrated in figure 3.14.
First, a solution of CAB in ethyl acetate is spin-coated onto the chip containing the TMD
monolayers (see figure 3.14a-b). In order to bake out the solvent from the solution, the
chip is baked at 80◦C for 5 minutes on a hotplate. A 3x3mm square is cut in the CAB
around the TMD flake (figure 3.14c). The square is lifted from the chip by adding a drop
of demineralized water on the edge of the square. The water wedges in between the CAB
covered TMD and the SiO2, lifting the square (figure 3.14d). The square is then picked
up with tweezers and placed on a small PDMS block attached to a glass slide (figure
3.14e). The chip with patterned device substrates is then placed on the vacuum stage of
the stacker, and the glass slide is mounted in the micromanipulator with the CAB square

3.1.3
Electron-beam Lithography
The method for fabricating the TMD FET devices for this project is EBL. With this method
structures on a scale down to ten nanometers can be fabricated, with complete control
[26]. It is fairly time consuming compared to other lithography methods, but it gives com-
plete control over the design and has nanometer resolution, making it a great way to make
small scale tests of new technology. The fabrication of the TMD FETs investigated in this
project involves three e-beam steps: Substrate patterning, creating a mask to make metal
contacts with lift-off, and making the etching mask used to shape the TMD channels. In
this section, the equipment, parameters, and materials needed to successfully complete
these e-beam steps will be described, and different patterns and strategies for creating
these patterns will be discussed.

EBL basics
The instrument used to do electron-beam exposures for this project is the JBX-9500FSZ

It features a Zirconium-oxide/tungsten thermal field emitter electron source and a four

3.1.4
Dry Etching
The etching steps in the device fabrication process are important because this is where the
patterns defined during the lithography step are transferred to the samples. This requires
an etching process that is highly tuneable and well-controlled to achieve good results.
This means dry etching is preferable to wet etching. A dry etch process uses a plasma,
usually containing some form of the halogens (flourine, bromine or chlorine), to etch into
materials [91]. The tools available for this purpose at DTU Nanolab are the III-V ICP and
Metal ICP tools, which are both PRO ICP etchers from SPTS. Both are inductively coupled
plasma (ICP) etchers, capable of performing precise nanoscale etching. In an ICP tool,
the samples are electrostatically clamped to a holder inside a vacuum chamber. In the top
of the chamber, a coil connected to an RF generator generates a symmetric magnetic field,
which creates a plasma by inducing an electric field in any gas present in the chamber. The
direction of the ions and free radicals in the plasma is controlled with a platten electrode
positioned under the sample and connected to a separate RF generator. By controlling
the power of the coil and platten, the etching process can be controlled, with a higher
power in general yielding higher etch rates. A high coil power yields a higher plasma
density, resulting in a higher etch rate, while a higher platten power makes the etch more
directional and gives a larger ion bombardment, thus yielding a higher etch rate. Aside
from the plasma generation parameters, the gas mixture used, the temperature during the
etch, the gas flow rates and the pressure in the chamber also influence the etch. Different
gasses etch different materials at different rates. For the purpose of etching hBN and
monolayer MoS2, sulfur hexaflouride (SF6) is used [26, 92], while the choice for SiO2
etching is carbon tetraflouride (CF4) mixed with H2, a standard recipe developed at DTU
Nanolab. The SF6 etch used here was developed and optimized by Lene Gammelgaard
prior to the start of this project. The flow rate of the gases is important because it may
limit the etch rate of the process. At low flow rates the etch is limited by the amount of
34
Electric Properties of 2D Materials
==PAGE:45==
gas present, while high flow rates may actually pump out the reactive species before they
can react with the sample. The temperature of the etch influences the rate by influencing
the volatility of the etch products. Higher temperatures may yield higher etch rates if the
chemical reactions at the sample surface are temperature limited, but may also damage
resist etch masks which are usually polymers. Finally the pressure in the chamber affects
the plasma density and the directionality of the etch. High pressures reduces the mean
free path of the ions in the plasma, and thus also the directionality of the etch, and at too
large pressures a plasma cannot be sustained at all. The interplay of all these parameters
makes the optimization of dry etch processes a necessary and often difficult step. The
SF6 etch used here has an etch rate in hBN of 9.5 nm/s in hBN, 1.4 nm/s in PMMA and
3.9 nm/s in MoS2. The CF4 etch has an etch rate of 0.5 nm/s in SiO2 and 0.8 nm/s
in PMMA. These etch rates were measured in collaboration with Dorte Danielsen and
Lene Gammelgaard. Prior to the main etch, all samples get a short 5 s descum etch to
remove PMMA residue from the mask openings. This etch uses an oxygen/argon gas mix,
low flow rates, low pressure and low coil/platten power to make it as gentle as possible.
After PMMA stripping in acetone, the hBN and SiO2 substrates receive an additional pure
oxygen etch at low power to remove PMMA residues. This is not done to the samples
with TMD monolayers, as the oxygen may damage the monolayers.

Characterization
Characterization of the samples throughout the fabrication process and of the finished
devices is a crucial part of the project. Each step in the fabrication process is moni-
tored, in order to avoid errors and to be able to troubleshoot if the final results are not as
expected. The finished devices are characterized electrically, and additional characteri-
zation is done in order to be able to interpret the results of the electrical measurements.
Optical microscopy is used as a quick quality check of the samples throughout the fab-
rication process, and is discussed in section 3.1.1. The following sections describes all
other characterization methods used, namely atomic force microscopy (AFM), scanning
electron microscopy (SEM), transmission electron microscopy (TEM), focused ion beam
processsing (FIB), Raman spectroscopy and electrical characterization techniques.
3.2.1
Atomic Force Microscopy
Traditional optical microscopy utilizes light to visualize structures and samples, but the
diffraction limit prohibits the use of this method of visualizing anything smaller than the
wavelength of light, i.e. anything on the nanometer scale. With the evolution of micro-
and nanotechnology, the need to visualize structures on this scale has grown, and several
techniques adressing this have been developed. One of these methods is AFM. AFM
works by scanning a small tip across a surface, and recording its vertical movements
to visualize the topography of the sample. The tip interacts with the local forces of the
surface, usually vdW forces as described in section 2.3.1. The AFM setup is as follows:
The tip sits on a small flexible cantilever positioned above the sample. When the tip scans
across the surface, the local forces on the tip translates to a bending of this cantilever,
which is monitored by reflecting a laser beam of the cantilever surface and onto a quadrant
photo diode. The electrical signal from the photo diode depends on where the laser hits
it, and is used as feedback to keep the force between tip and sample constant, by moving
the cantilever up or down. The height change is recorded and can be plotted to visualize
the sample topography. The motion of the cantilever in the XYZ directions is controlled by
a sensitive piezo-electric ceramic, which gives very precise control of the tip position. The
scenario described here is the most simple operation mode of the AFM, known as contact
mode in which the tip touches the surface. Other operation modes are the non-contact and
tapping- or intermittent-contact modes. In the non-contact mode, the cantilever is lifted a
Electric Properties of 2D Materials
35
==PAGE:46==
little bit above the surface and set to oscillate. When it is scanned across the surface, the
tip then feels the local force gradient, which effectively changes the resonance frequency
of the cantilever, resulting in a change in the amplitude of the oscillation. The topography
of the sample is then visualized by keeping the amplitude of the oscillation constant by
moving the tip with respect to the surface as in the contact mode. The tapping-mode is
very similar to the non-contact mode, but here the oscillations of the tip are large enough
for the tip to briefly touch the sample. The non-contact and tapping modes are more gentle
to the sample than contact mode, and is thus suitable for nondestructive examination of 2D
materials. The tapping mode is slightly less sensitive to external vibrations than the non-
contact mode, and is thus less noisy. The tapping mode is used for all AFM measurements
in this project.
3.2.2
Electron Microscopy
Another way to visualize structures that are smaller than the wavelength of light, is to use
something with a smaller wavelength, e.g. electrons. Electrons can be regarded as either
particles or waves with a deBroglie wavelength on the order of 1 nm or smaller depending
on their energy, and as such they can be exploited to create microscopes that works in a
very similar way to a classic optical microscope.
Scanning Electron Microscopy
The working principle behind SEM is relatively simple: A beam of electrons is scanned
across a sample using a series of magnetic lenses, and the electrons then interacts with
the sample. These interactions yield several different signals, e.g. electrons, photons,
etc. that are reflected back, giving a lot of different information about the sample. The
electron signal consist of two main parts: The secondary electrons and the back-scattered
electrons. The secondary electrons are created from interactions between the primary
beam of electrons and the sample, and are usually only detected if they have energies
below 50 eV. For this reason the collected secondary electron signal can be considered
to stem from the very surface of the sample, and gives a good impression of the surface
topography. The back-scattered electrons are electrons from the primary beam that have
been back-scattered from below the surface of the sample. These electrons have higher
energies, which to some extend depend on the material composition of the sample. Here
all SEM images are made with the SEM Supra 3, a SEM Supra 40VP from Zeiss, at DTU
Nanolab. All images are formed using secondary electrons, because the SEM images
are mainly used to visualize the roughness created in the substrates, meaning that the
information about the topography is the most important. A disadvantage of SEM is the
hydrocarbon contamination that is created during the SEM scans. The naturally occurring
hydrocarbons are polymerized by the electron beam, and deposited on the sample. For
this reason, SEM is only used here to image substrates and samples that are not used to
fabricate electrical devices. All finished devices are instead imaged with AFM.
Transmission Electron Microscopy
The working principle of TEM is similar to SEM: A beam of electrons is directed towards a
sample through a series of magnetic lenses, and the transmitted signal is collected. When
the electrons travel through a sample they are either absorbed, scattered or transmitted.
By collecting the different signals, different information about the sample can be obtained.
In bright-field TEM imaging, only the transmitted electrons are collected. The intensity of
the signal from different areas in the sample thus depend on the absorption and scattering
in that area. Areas of a sample that either scatter or absorb a lot will appear dark, i.e.
usually heavier materials, and this is how the image contrast is created. Dark-field imaging
is the reverse of bright-field imaging, i.e. here only the elastically scattered or diffracted
electrons are used to form an image, and non-scattering areas thus appear dark in dark-
field images. Bright-field images are most commonly used, but dark-field images may in
36
Electric Properties of 2D Materials

some cases offer better contrast, e.g. when imaging smaller crystalline features. Here,
TEM is used to characterize some of the devices that malfunctioned, in order to determine
why they do not work. Both types of imaging modes are used. The MoS2 monolayers
are observed best in the dark field images, while. e.g. the layered structure of the hBN
substrate is better observed in the bright-field images (see section 4.5). Moreover, TEM
can be used to perform local spectroscopy. The electrons that are inelastically scattered
when travelling through the sample, lose energy characteristic for the atoms they scatter
off. If they are only scattered once, these electrons thus contain information about the
material composition of the sample. Collecting and analyzing these electrons with respect
to their energy thus gives an understanding of the material composition in the sample. This
method is known as electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS), and is used here to verify
the interpretation of the bright- and dark-field images.
Since TEM relies on electrons transmitting through a sample, it is important that the sam-
ple under investigation is sufficiently thin. The method used here to prepare a sample
for TEM is focused ion beam (FIB) processsing. This is done in a SEM tool fitted with
an additional ion beam column, and gas needles. First a protective coating is deposited
over the area of interest on the sample, and a beam of ions (in this case gallium ions),
is used to cut out a thin cross section lamella of approximately 10 µm length and 1 µm
width from the area. The lamella is then picked up and attached to a TEM mounting grid.
Once attached, the ion beam is used to further thin down the lamella to a final thickness
below 100 nm. The protective layer is usually a platinum/carbon mix [93]. However, when
preparing samples containing 2D materials, this deposition step may damage the mono-
layers beyond recognition. To solve this issue, the method developed by Andersen et al.
is used [93]. Before the the sample is transferred to the SEM tool for deposition of the pro-
tective layer, a thin carbon coating is applied over the sample. The protective Pt coating
is then deposited in two steps: First a thin layer is deposited using an electron beam, and
then the rest is deposited using the ion beam. This has proven sufficient to protect the
monolayers during the FIB process, and enables the study of samples containing mono-
layers in a TEM. All FIB processing and TEM imaging presented here is carried out by
Anton Bay Andersen, using the Helios SEM (Dual Beam FEI Helios Nanolab 600 SEM)
and Titan ATEM (Titan Analytical 80-300ST TEM) at DTU Nanolab.

Electrical Characterization
Several different approaches to measuring and calculating the mobility of the MoS2 chan-
nels in TMD FET devices exist. Here, the devices are fabricated in a geometry which
allows for both 2-, 3- and 4-point measurements. The 2- and 4-point measurements can
both be used to characterize the electric properties of the MoS2 channel, but the 4-point
measurement has the advantage that it eliminates the influence of contact resistance on
the measurements. The 3-point measurement is used to measure the contact resistance
of the individual electrodes.

The final technique used to characterize the fabricated devices in this project is 3-point
measurements. This method is used to measure the contact resistance of the individual
voltage probes in the devices. As before, Vg is applied to activate the channel. A voltage
is then applied between the probe under investigation and the drain electrode, causing
Electric Properties of 2D Materials
39
==PAGE:50==
a current to run between the two. The voltage drop Vp is measured between the probe
and the original source electrode (see figure 3.12). The assumption is that a negligible
current runs in the voltage probe loop, and the voltage drop is thus effectively measured
over only the beginning of the current path, i.e. the probe. The drop can then be assumed
to be only due to the contact resistance of the probe RV p. As described in section 2.2.2,
it is assumed that the current only flows into the channel at the very edge of the probes,
meaning that only the width and not the length of the probes is important. For this reason
the contact resistance is usually given in units of [Ω· µm]. The actual characterization of
the devices consists of all three types of measurements, which is necessary to measure
the different characteristics discussed in section 2.2.1. The 2-point measurement is used
to measure the output characteristics of the device, i.e. the current ISD running in the
channel as a function of source/drain voltage VSD at different gate voltages Vg. The 4-
point measurement is used to measure the transfer characteristics of the devices, i.e.
the current ISD running through the channel at a specific source/drain voltage VSD, as a
function of gate voltage Vg. For all devices the gate voltage is swept from -40 to 80 V with a
step size of 0.5 V. Finally, the 3-point measurement is used to measure RV p of the voltage
probes on select devices. This is done by applying a constant VSD, sweeping Vg from -40
to 80 V and measuring the ISD, as in the 4-point measurement. All devices are measured
on the probe station situated in the NANOMADE lab at DTU Physics. Keithley 2450
sourcemeters are used to apply the gate and source/drain voltages, and a Keithley 2000
multimeter is used to measure the voltage drop in the the 3- and 4-point measurements.
Measurements are carried in ambient conditions at room temperature. The results of the
electrical characterization will be presented in section 4.4.
3.3
Processflow
In this section an overview of the complete fabrication process of TMD FET devices is
given. The process consists of four overall steps: 1) Substrate preparation and patterning,
2) TMD exfoliation and transfer, 3) metal contact deposition, and 4) device shaping. A
complete summary of the processflow with parameters and equipment specifications can
be found in appendix A.
3.3.1
Substrate Preparation
The main hypothesis tested in this project, is that the strain induced in TMD monolayers
by rough/patterned substrates increases the carrier mobility in the monolayers. The first
step in the fabrication is thus to fabricate controlled and deliberate roughness in different
substrates. Two types of substrates are used: hBN and SiO2. hBN is atomically flat, and
is thus a good starting point for complete control of the substrate roughness, while the
effect of rough SiO2 is already benchmarked in the literature [34].
The only difference in the fabrication of the two types of substrates is that the hBN in-
cludes a mechanical exfoliation step (figure 3.13b), in order to prepare hBN flakes with
a thickness of 20-50 nm. These flakes are exfoliated onto a low resistance silicon chip,
covered in a 300 nm thick nova SiO2 layer. The exfoliation process is described in section
3.1.1. These nova oxide chips also consitutes the SiO2 substrates. Once the chips are
ready, they are brought into the cleanroom, by cleaning them with acetone, IPA and a
nitrogen blow. They are then baked at 180 ◦C for 5 minutes to remove water, spin-coated
with 1.5% 2200k PMMA in anisole at 2000 rpm for 1 minute at 500 rpm/s acceleration,
and finally baked for 10 minutes at 180 ◦C to remove the solvent. The spin-coating is
done in the Labspin manual spin-coaters at DTU Nanolab. The patterns are written with
the e-beam writer in the cleanroom at DTU Nanolab (figure 3.13) and developed for 30s
in IPA (figure 3.13). The PMMA is used as an etching mask for the subsequent dry etch
processing. The chips with hBN substrates are etched in the III-V ICP etcher and the pure
40
Electric Properties of 2D Materials
==PAGE:51==
SiO2 chips are etched in the ICP Metal etcher in the cleanroom. Both receive a 5 s des-
cum oxygen etch to remove PMMA residues from the mask openings. The hBN is then
etched for 15 s in SF6 and the SiO2 in a CF4/H2 mix (figure 3.13f). The remaining PMMA
is removed by first immersing the chips in acetone and rinsing with IPA and a nitrogen
blow, and secondly with a short oxygen plasma etch (g). The substrates are then ready
for TMD FET fabrication.
3.3.2
TMD Preparation
Apart from controlling the roughness of the substrates, the ability to exfoliate and transfer
large TMD monolayers is the most important step in fabrication of TMD FET devices. The
exfoliation of these monolayers and the transfer of them is described in detail in section
3.1.1 and 3.1.2. The process is schematically depicted in figure 3.14.
3.3.3
Creating Contacts
Once the TMD monolayers have been transferred to the patterned substrates, the metal
contacts need to be created. This is done in another e-beam lithography step, and a metal
deposition and lift-off step. The process is schematically shown in figure 3.15. First, the
chips with TMD monolayers on top of the rough substrates are brought into the cleanroom,
where they are gently rinsed in acetone and IPA and dried with a nitrogen blow to remove
dirt. They are then spin-coated with a 5% 996k PMMA in anisole solution as 2000 rpm and
500 rpm acceleration for 1 minute, followed by a 1 minute post-bake at 180◦C. The baking
is kept at the absolute minimum necessary to bake out the solvent, in order to spare the
TMD from the heat since it may damage it. The pattern is then written in the e-beam writer
9500, and developed for 30 s in an IPA:H2O mix in a 3:1 ratio. After an optical inspection
of the pattern, the metal is deposited in the Temescal e-beam evaporater system at DTU
Nanolab. First, 10 nm of titanium is deposited as an adhesion layer, and then 50 nm of gold
is deposited, both at a 1 Å/s deposition rate. A dummy chip is included in the deposition,
in order to be able to perform subsequent resistivity measurements of the deposited metal
as a quality control. After deposition, all chips are transferred to an acetone bath for at
least 30 minutes to perform the Lift-off step. Once the excess metal and the PMMA is
completely removed the chips are rinsed in IPA and dried with a nitrogen blow. Finally,
the newly created electrodes are inspected in an optical microscope.
3.3.4
Device Shaping
The final step is to shape the devices, i.e. define the TMD channels. This makes sure
that the channels are well defined and that the different contacts are not short-circuited by
excess TMD. This step involves an EBL and a dry etching step. The process is schemat-
ically depicted in figure 3.16. First, the chips are covered with a PMMA layer, the etch
mask pattern is written in the e-beam writer and developed in the exact same manner as
in section 3.3.3. The chips are then transferred to the III-V ICP etcher at DTU Nanolab.
They are treated shortly with a descum etch (O2/Ar plasma) and then etched for 15s using
SF6. The PMMA mask is then removed in acetone, followed by a IPA rinse and nitrogen
blow. In most cases, the devices are now finished, but in case there are defects in the
metal contacts, the metal deposition step, described in section 3.3.3 can be repeated
to fix the electrodes. The finished devices are then inspected in an optical microscope
and transferred to electrostatic discharge (ESD) protected sample boxes before they are
removed from the cleanroom.

Discussion
All relevant data and results are presented in section 4. In this section, the consequences

5.1
Substrates
The e-beam exposure step is crucial in the fabrication of substrates with a controlled
roughness. The two exposure strategies investigated in this project yield lines of similar
dimensions for similar total doses, but the quality of the final pattern varies depending on
the exposure strategy and the initial pattern design. Figure 4.2 illustrates that for rela-
tively low total doses (1.8-2 pC), the single-shot exposure strategy yields a line pattern
with much smoother and well defined lines than the regular exposure strategy, while the
dimensions of the lines are similar. For higher doses (3-4 pC), both strategies, in general,
yield relatively smooth lines, but with some defects. In particular, the regular exposure
strategy yields the most defects (see figure 4.3). Based on these results, the single-shot
exposure strategy with low doses is deemed the best choice when writing vertical lines.
However, as is evident in figure 4.4, this strategy fails when writing tilted lines. The prob-
lem seems to be an issue with the software for the EBL system rather than with the actual
exposure, since the flaws in the pattern are evident already after compilation of the mag-
azine files at the EBL system (see figure 4.4a2-4.4c). More investigation is required to
determine the precise cause of the failure, and since the periodic nature of the patterns
is deemed more important than the roughness of the line edges, the regular exposure
strategy with a designed line width of 50 nm is chosen for the patterning of all substrates
in this project.
The examples of patterned substrates presented in figures 4.6 and 4.7 are from the
SiO2_1, SiO2_2, and hBN05 samples. The measured roughness varies slightly from sam-
ple to sample for both substrate-types. This variation might be due to slight variations in
the fabrication process, e.g. the PMMA resist thickness and uniformity. For the hBN sam-
ples, the variation is also due to variations in the initial thickness of the hBN flake, which
ranges from 20-50 nm for the samples fabricated here. With the current etch recipe, most
flakes of this thickness are expected to be etched all the way through, putting a natural
limit to the roughness. The actual depth of the etched structures in hBN is difficult to
measure with AFM due to the small dimensions of the structures, and the values given in
table 4.1 is thus considered the minimum roughness of the samples. The two-dimensional
roughness of the SiO2 samples is very similar to that created by Liu et al., to increase the
mobility in MoS2 monolayers [34]. In all cases, the roughness created in both the hBN
and SiO2 substrates is expected to be sufficient to induce strain in any MoS2 monolay-
ers placed on top of them. However, as is evident from the results presented in section
4.4 and 4.5, no clear trends in the effect of the substrate on the mobility appear and in
most cases, the MoS2 layer breaks over the substrate surface. This phenomenon will be
discussed further in the coming sections.
5.2
Device Performance
The overall performance of the fabricated devices is similar to what is normally observed
in MoS2 devices [20, 54, 60]. However, when comparing devices on the different types of
substrates there are no clear trends in terms of mobility, output characteristics, or contact
resistance (see figures 4.14, 4.15, and 4.16). The mobility fluctuates in the range 0-2.5

cm2/V s for 2-point measurements and 1-45 cm2/V s for 4-point measurements. Figure
4.14 shows the data grouped by substrate type, and it is clear that the spread within each
group is so large that no possible difference from substrate to substrate is visible. Look-
ing at the output characteristics, most devices show a Schottky-like behavior (figure 4.15)
and a few devices show a more ohmic behavior (figure 4.18). Again, no clear connection
between performance and output characteristics is found. Figure 4.16 shows the rela-
tionship between contact resistance and mobility. The expectation is that there is a clear
relationship between the contact resistance and the 2-point mobility and no clear relation-
ship to the 4-point mobility, because the effect of contact resistance should effectively be
filtered out in the 4-point measurement. For the resistance of the individual voltage probes
measured with 3-point measurements nothing can be concluded, since resistance varies
too much from probe to probe. Moreover, the average contact resistance shows a clear
trend in both 2- and 4-point mobility. It is also clear that the contact resistance varies a lot
from device to device, irrespective of substrate type, although the quality of the metal in all
depositions is similar based on the calculated resistivity. The large variances in contact
resistance may stem from the fabrication, e.g. misalignment of the contacts and channel,
or it might simply reflect the seemingly large variation in the quality of the MoS2 channel.
The contact resistances observed here are all in the MΩµm range, which is up to an or-
der of magnitude higher than in other reports [55, 56, 99]. The large resistance may also
partially explain why there is a large spread in the results, since it leads to small currents
in the channels even at high voltages, making the measurements noisy.
Due to the large variations in performance, the only devices that are directly compara-
ble are from the same sample. The sample hBN03 contains three functioning devices:
Two on flat hBN and one on flat SiO2. The three devices have seen exactly the same
processing and stem from the same original MoS2 monolayer flake. All three devices ex-
hibit ohmic behavior (see figure 4.18), and n-type FET behavior (see figure 4.17). The
individual device performance suggests that the substrate has an influence on the device
performance. The mobility of the hBN devices (0.6 and 0.56 cm2/V s) is an order of mag-
nitude higher than the SiO2 device (0.05 cm2/V s) and the contact resistance in the hBN
devices is almost half that in the SiO2 device. The hysteresis measured at ISD = 2 nA is
similar for the three devices with
HDev E = 27.0V
HDev F = 16.5V
HDev G = 19.5V.
The similar hysteresis is somewhat surprising, as the SiO2 substrate is expected to have
more defects, and thus induce a larger hysteresis effect. However, this same trend re-
peats for the rest of the devices, which all show large hysteresis that is seemingly irrespec-
tive of substrate. This may be due to the oxygen plasma used to remove PMMA residue
after the substrates are patterned, which may induce defects in the hBN [103, 104]. In any
case, the presented results suggest that hBN is a more suitable substrate choice for this
type of MoS2 FET devices. A larger collection of devices should be investigated though,
before making any conclusions on this question.
Several factors may contribute to the large spread observed in the device performance,
and ultimately make it impossible to conclude on the effect of the substrate roughness
on the device mobility. An obvious difference between the devices is that they are not
from the same original MoS2 flake, and that they have been fabricated in three batches.
This may introduce small differences in the MoS2 quality, e.g. during the individual CAB
transfers or in how much PMMA residue is left after the metal deposition and shaping
steps. The metal deposition and device shaping steps both depend on an e-beam step,

in which alignment of patterns is crucial. Here, a large misalignment may also influence
e.g. the quality of the contacts to the MoS2.
Aside from the intrinsic properties of the devices, the measurement results may also be
influenced by the measurement conditions. The effect of measurement conditions, such
as the sweep rate, is eliminated by measuring all devices with the same sweeping pa-
rameters. All devices were measured on the same probe station as described in section
3.2.4, and all measurements were carried out in air at room temperature, similar to what
is done by Liu et al. [34]. This means that the conditions in the lab may influence the
measurements, if they change over time, which is one of the things suspected to cause
some spread in the results. Specifically, the humidity was observed to vary from 30%-
60% from day to day during the measurement period. Measurements on the same device
in this period show very large variations between the days with high humidity (September
14 and 15) and the days with lower humidity, as illustrated in figure 4.20. This trend is
particularly prominent in devices on rough substrates, but is present for all devices. The
humidity is expected to have a large negative effect on the mobility [105], and this effect
has actually been utilized to create very sensitive humidity sensors [20]. Here, however,
the mobility drastically increases on humid days. This indicates that other factors may
also have an influence on the device performance, but in order to conclude anything on
this matter, a more thorough investigation of the effect of different lab conditions on the
device mobility must be carried out.
5.3
Device Malfunction
Many of the devices fabricated for this project never worked. The TEM and AFM results
presented in section 4.5 suggest that the main reason for this is that the MoS2 monolayers
break in the regions where they are suspended due to the roughness in the substrates. It
has been proven that defect-free crystalline MoS2 monolayers can survive a strain of up
to 11% [30], and based on the estimations made with the Hamaker model presented in
section 4.5.2, the vdW forces between the substrates and the monolayers should not be
large enough to break the MoS2. Therefore, other explanations must thus be explored.
The TEM images presented in figure 4.22 show that the MoS2 has completely conformed
to the ridges in the hBN substrate, but that this comes at the cost of it breaking towards
the bottom of the ridges. The breakage seems to have happened some time after the
deposition of the metal contacts (figure 4.23), since the cavities under the electrode are
filled with silicon (see appendix B). The silicon is most likely redeposited in the cavities
during the FIB process, meaning that before the FIB processing these areas were empty,
suggesting that the MoS2 was intact right before the metal deposition. After the metal de-
position, the devices see several fabrication steps, including an e-beam step, two PMMA
stripping steps and a dry etching step (see appendix A). This leaves many possible expla-
nations as to when the MoS2 breaks. However, the final PMMA stripping in acetone and
subsequent drying, is especially interesting. During the shaping step, the excess MoS2
is etched away to form well defined channels and avoid short circuiting of the contacts.
As a consequence, the exposed parts of the hBN substrates are also etched away once
the MoS2 is removed. This effectively opens up small channels between the MoS2 and
the hBN substrate, in which the acetone used to strip the PMMA after the etching step
can flow. The samples are rinsed with IPA and dried with a nitrogen gun after the PMMA
stripping. The hypothesis is that the capillary forces present in the small channels due to
surface tension during the drying step effectively pull the MoS2 into the ridges and break it.
This hypothesis has not been tested, but a simple way to check whether this is in fact the
case, is to dry the samples in a critical point dryer after the stripping. This method uses a
72
Electric Properties of 2D Materials
==PAGE:83==
super-critical liquid, typically CO2, to dry the samples without introducing surface tension
and is typically used to dry samples with fragile membranes or structures without damag-
ing them. The SiO2 substrates are much less rough than the hBN samples, and are not
etched by the SF6 gas used in the shaping step. However, when the devices are shaped,
the rough substrate is exposed around the channel, and this may grant fluids access to
any channels under the MoS2. This means that the same effect could be responsible for
breaking the MoS2 on both types of substrate.
Other steps may also damage the MoS2 monolayers. As described in section 4.5, heat-
ing seems to be one way to destroy the MoS2, but this process has only been briefly
investigated here. The sample that was placed on a warm hotplate, i.e. it experienced no
temperature ramp, and baked in air, exhibited breakage along several of the ridges in the
substrate (figure 4.24), while the sample that was heated in a controlled atmosphere with
a short temperature ramp showed no breakage at all (figure 4.27). A possible explanation
for this is that when the sample is heated in air, some moisture is present in the channels
created between the substrate and the monolayer, which then rapidly expands when the
sample is placed on the hotplate, breaking the monolayer. When the sample is instead
heated in a controlled atmosphere with a brief temperature ramp, the expansion of any
moisture is slower and there is possibly less moisture present. Another thing worth noting
here, is that the ridges along which the MoS2 layer is broken induce a phase difference
in the AFM scan (see figure 4.25). This is not the case for the MoS2 that is proven to
be broken towards the bottom of the ridges (4.22 and 4.21a), and may indicate that on
the heated sample, the MoS2 breaks at the top of the ridges, and that the exposed MoS2
edge induces the phase shift. If this is the case, it supports the hypothesis that the MoS2
is bot pulled into the ridges when it breaks, consistent with breakage due to expansion.
However, further investigations of both the heating effects on MoS2 and the AFM phase
shift must be carried out before drawing further conclusions.
Aside from the MoS2 monolayers breaking on the rough substrates, no other reasons
for the device malfunction have been determined. This means that an explanation as
to why so many devices on flat substrates malfunctioned has not yet been found. The
Raman spectra shown in figure 4.30 show that neither the CAB process nor the heating
in a controlled atmosphere changes the signal from the monolayers. This indicates that
the structure of the monolayers is unchanged. A similar investigation into whether some
of the other fabrication steps influence the quality of the MoS2, e.g. PMMA residues or
damage and residues from the dry etching steps, is also relevant to do. Another option
to try and diagnose the malfunctioning devices on flat substrates, is to use kelvin probe
force microscopy (KPFM). This method is very similar to AFM, but here a conductive tip
is oscillated over the sample. A voltage is applied to the sample and tip, creating electric
forces between the two, and the feedback loop is used to keep these forces constant.
In this way the local potential at the sample surface can be mapped. The technique has
been succesfully used to study the effect of defects in similar graphene devices [106].
In the investigation of substrate induced strain and its effects on the mobility in MoS2
monolayers, the observed breaking of the MoS2 has been an obstacle. However, one
can imagine other useful applications of this phenomenon. For instance, in order to pre-
serve the quality of the MoS2 monolayers and save time, it would be beneficial to be able
to shape or pattern MoS2 monolayers without exposing it to EBL and dry etching. The fact
that the monolayers here break nicely along the structures in very rough substrates may
lead to a way to do exactly that. The results here show that fairly thin MoS2 nanoribbons
( 50 nm width, see figure 4.24) can be fabricated in this manner. A possible application
of such nanoribbons is demonstrated by Kotekar-Patil et al. who succesfully fabricated

MoS2 nanoribbon FET devices using 50 nm wide nanoribbons, and achieved mobilities in
the range 15.1-50 cm2/V s and 105 on/off ratios [92]. However, it is also conceivable that
substrate structures other than lines can be used to make more complicated MoS2 struc-
tures, and open up a range of other possible applications. Whether the MoS2 is broken
through heating or rapid sample-drying after immersion in acetone, this study presents
possibly a very simple way to perform etch-free nanopatterning of MoS2 and other 2D
materials. This might be an interesting way to create small high quality 2D nanostruc-

6
Conclusion and Outlook
Many different aspects of MoS2 FET devices and their fabrication process have been
investigated throughout this project.
In the fabrication process the EBL steps, the CAB transfer, and the heating have been
examined, specifically. Different e-beam exposure strategies have been tested and com-
pared and it has been demonstrated that both hBN and SiO2 substrates can be patterned
in a reproducible and controlled manner with EBL. The effect of CAB transfer on the qual-
ity of the transferred MoS2 monolayers has been investigated and found inconsequential,
while the the effect of heating MoS2 monolayers placed on rough substrates is found to
depend on the specific conditions of the experiment. The performance of the fabricated
devices has also been examined in depth. A large spread is found in the data with regards
to the calculated mobility, and the values are all relatively low. The spread is persistent
over the full range of substrates and substrate roughnesses, which means that no conclu-
sions can be made with regards to the effect of substrate-induced strain on the mobility
in MoS2 monolayers. However, the results suggest that there may be an advantage to
using hBN substrates as opposed to SiO2 substrates with regards to the mobility of the
devices, although no certain conclusions can be made from the current data set.
The large spread in the results may stem from several different factors. First, it seems to
be related to the conditions in the lab during the electrical characterization. Specifically,
changes in the humidity in the lab seems to be correlated with the performance of the
devices. Second, all devices show a significant hysteresis effect, which directly influences
the results. The hysteresis varies a lot from device to device, but does not seem to depend
on the substrate material. Finally, the contact resistance observed in the devices is large
(on the order of MΩ), but varies from device to device and even from electrode to electrode
within the same device. The large contact resistance may also have an influence on the
results, because it hinders larger currents from running in the devices, and thus introduces
a high noise-level.
Several of the fabricated devices never worked, and an investigation of the reason why
was carried out. In many cases the MoS2 monolayers placed on rough hBN substrates
break during the fabrication process. The data suggests that this may happen in two
main ways: During the final PMMA stripping step or during one of the heating steps.
When the devices are shaped, channels between the MoS2 and substrate is opened up
due to the substrate roughness. The TEM data suggests that when the PMMA etch mask
is subsequently stripped in acetone and dried, capillary forces in the channels may break
the MoS2. It is also shown that heating a sample with MoS2 on a rough hBN substrate
can result in the MoS2 breaking under the right conditions.
The results here neither proves nor disproves the hypothesis that substrate induced strain
in MoS2 monolayers can increase their mobility. This means that there are still several in-
teresting research paths to explore from here on. The first thing to do is to finish the inves-
tigation into why so many devices malfunctioned. Not only devices on rough substrates
malfunctioned, but also a significant amount on flat substrates. A further investigation
with TEM and FIB may be sufficient, but it is also suggested to do KPFM measurements
on the samples, in order to possibly locate fatal defects in the MoS2 channel or in the
electrodes.

Secondly, it would be very interesting to investigate the effect of heating MoS2 on rough
substrates. If it can be determined under which conditions the MoS2 breaks, it can be used
to protect the samples during the fabrication process and possibly to make annealing of
the finished devices possible. Annealing can be used to increase the conformity of the
MoS2 on the substrates, and to remove any PMMA residues left from the fabrication. Both
of these effects could lead to an increase in the general device performance.
Thirdly, it would be meaningful to optimize the fabrication process used here. Specifically,
it should be investigated whether the use of critical point drying in the PMMA stripping
steps can spare the MoS2 from damage. The goal of this is of course to develop a process
flow to consistently fabricate functioning devices on rough substrates. This is the most
important prerequisite to have in place, in order to be able to properly test the hypothesis
that substrate-induced strain can increase the mobility in MoS2 monolayers. Another
prerequisite for this would be to create an environment for electrical characterization, that
minimizes the influence of e.g. lab conditions on the measurements. One solution to this,
is to use a probe station situated in the cleanroom at DTU Nanolab, where the temperature
and humidity is kept constant at all times.
Finally, if it is possible to control how and when the MoS2 breaks on rough substrates,
either through heating or capillary forces, it would be interesting to utilize this for creating
nanostructures. The types of structures that can be created, the minimum feature sizes,
the exposed MoS2 edges, and the quality of the MoS2 monolayer after patterning are all
interesting aspects to explore. The results from such a study may possibly result in a
whole new approach to patterning 2D materials on the nanoscale.
