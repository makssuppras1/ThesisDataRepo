TECHNICAL UNIVERSITY OF DENMARK
Abstract
Department of Earth and Space Physics and Engineering
Lightning Activity and Cloud Formation at the Tropopause
by Krisztina-Noemi GALI
This thesis addresses the following topics: the use of different overshooting top (OT)
detecting methods based on a brightness temperature difference of different chan-
nels facilitated by the SEVIRI instrument on top of the MSG satellite, the comparison
of different lightning detecting networks, the relation between the presence of an OT
and severe weather happening on the ground and the relation between the behavior
of lightning and convective cloud development in cases of extreme weather phe-
nomena.
Three cases have been analyzed consisting of two tornado events (Venice and
Germany) and one thunderstorm (Spain). OTs were only detected in the case of the
Venice tornado, whereas areas of deep convection were detected for the rest of the
cases. For the purpose of detecting OTs the COMB method has proven itself once
again to be the best BTD detection method.
The spatial distribution of lightning showed a signiﬁcant correlation with the
spatial distribution of the convective cloud. The same went for the temporal distri-
bution. The lightning behavior around the OT or the presence of deep convection
was consistent with what one would expect in such a case, inferring that maybe an
OT did occur in the other two cases but was not visible in the 3 km spatial resolution
images provided every 15 minutes by SEVIRI.
In the cases where severe weather is known to have appeared on the ground (the
two tornadoes), correlation was found between the cloud activity and the event. A
signiﬁcant correlation was also found between the height of the cloud and the height
of the IC lightning.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Every year Europe, and the rest of the world, is hit by a high number of severe
weather phenomena such as tornadoes, damaging lightning, large hail, strong winds
and heavy rainfall. These catastrophic climate events have a heavy impact on peo-
ple’s lives from an economic and social standpoint.
According to the European Environment Agency (EEA) [5], Europe has lost more
than 330 billion £ between 1980 and 2013 due to extreme weather events. The report
also states that these events have claimed the lives of up to 85,000 people.
Serious effects of these hazardous weather incidents, excluding loss of life and
cases of injury, consist of personal property being damaged and transport infrastruc-
ture being completely destroyed.
Houses have to be demolished, oftentimes leaving their owners without a home.
Cars are smashed by falling trees or, in tornado cases, are frequently lifted into the air
only to be hurled down again. Many times buildings of great historical signiﬁcance,
such as churches and museums are badly damaged or destroyed. Transit can be
rendered impossible due to fallen debris on rail tracks and roads.
It is therefore of great importance to analyze these severe weather events for the
purpose of understanding the way they are formed, how they behave during their
life cycle, and allow for the future possibility of developing more accurate severe
weather forecasts.
The purpose of the present thesis is to analyze different cases of extreme weather
happening on the ground from two angles. With the help of satellite imagery the
cloud formation at the tropopause will be investigated and through use of different
lightning networks, the lightning activity for every event will be analyzed.
Two countries in Europe which seem to be more frequently affected by violent
weather phenomena are Germany and Italy. Therefore, out of the three analyzed
cases, one will contain a storm over Germany and one will contain a storm over
Italy. The third analyzed case will consist of a storm developed over Spain.
When analyzing the top of the clouds different methods will be applied in order
to observe the existence of a speciﬁc element. That speciﬁc element is known as an
overshooting convective cloud top (OT), which will be explained more thoroughly
in Chapter 2.
This OT has been associated with the presence of hazardous weather happening
on the ground slightly after its appearance. Also, the lightning frequency has been
seen to increase in the location where an OT has been spotted.
Due to these two effects created in the near proximity of an OT, its presence
has great potential to also disrupt the normal course of operation for in-ﬂight and
ground based aviation. Therefore detecting the exact location of a possible OT is of
great importance.

2.1
Cloud Terminology
Clouds represent visible masses composed of cooled down water vapors suspended
in air, to which dust, dirt and smoke can become attached.
They can be found at various altitudes in the sky and can develop differently. De-
pending on their height, clouds can be classiﬁed into three distinct categories: high,
middle and low clouds. Another often used category contains the so-called verti-
cal clouds. Here we can ﬁnd cumulonimbus (Cb), cumulus (Cu) and nimbostratus
(Ns) clouds, which rely on a vertical development rather than a horizontal one. The
different types of clouds can be seen in Figure 2.1.
The present thesis is only interested in the Cb clouds and their vertical develop-
ment.

Cb can be extremely large and tall. They are often followed by Cu. The base
of a Cb can be found starting at 200 to 4,000 m and can reach altitudes of up to
12,000 m (21,000 m in really extreme cases). Furthermore they can be classiﬁed into
cumulonimbus calvus, cumulonimbus capillatus and cumulonimbus incus clouds.

Cumulonimbus calvus are clouds which are recognized by their puffy tops. Cu-
mulonimbus capillatus have tops more similar to those of cirrus clouds and cumu-
lonimbus incus are characterized by an anvil top.
Usually clouds that have an anvil top are very well developed. Cb clouds are
known to cause atmospheric instability and thunderstorms (also known as convec-
tive storms). A thunderstorm is generally deﬁned by the presence of lightning and
therefore of thunder. During the formation of a thunderstorm clouds spread ver-
tically until the moment when the warm rising air has reached a height where the
environment has either the same temperature as the rising parcel or is a bit warmer.
This is known to be an equilibrium level. The equilibrium level is frequently con-
sidered to be at the tropopause. At this point, instead of a vertical development, the
cloud starts spreading horizontally thus forming the anvil top.
The height of the tropopause varies depending on two factors: the speciﬁc lati-
tude and the season. The tropopause has higher altitudes near the Equator (ranging
from 16 to 18 km) and lower altitudes near the poles (ranging from 6 to 8 km). This
is in accordance with what can be observed in the behavior of the tropopause during
different seasons. Its height tends to be lower during the winter season and higher
in the summer.

the rising air and the momentum caused from the rapid ascent. OTs can spread to

hour. Cb clouds as well as OTs have been associated with the presence of hazardous

2.2
Lightning Terminology
Lightning is the natural phenomenon that takes place during a convective storm. It
can be described as a rapid spark caused by an electrostatic discharge. This electro-
static discharge is possible due to the negative and positive charges that can be found
in a cloud, between two clouds or between a cloud and the ground. The visible part
of lightning is generated by light, while thunder can be described as representing
the audible part.
There are different types of lightning among which the most common ones are
described below:
• Cloud-to-ground (CG) lightning
They can be of two different types: positively charged (+CG) or negatively
charged (-CG). Both types are characterized by a downward movement. A
distinction between the two types can be made by looking at their branches.
Negative CGs tend to have a lot of downward branches, as well as several
strokes that return back to the clouds, whereas positive CGs are characterized
by the absence of branches. Also, most of the times, they consist of a single
stroke that returns back to the clouds. When comparing the frequency of their
appearance, negative CGs are more numerous than positive CGs. The two
different types of CGs can be observed in Figure 2.4a) and Figure 2.4b).
• Ground-to-cloud (GC) lightning
Ground-to-cloud lightning can be described as the opposite of CG lightning.
Instead of the discharge moving downwards, in this case it moves upwards

from the ground towards the cloud. This is possible in regions where we have
tall buildings and towers. GC lightning can also be positive or negative. An
example of GC lightning can be seen in Figure 2.4c).
• Intra-cloud (IC) lightning
The most frequent form of lightning is called the intra-cloud (IC) lightning.
As can be deducted from its name, this type of lightning takes place within a
single convective cloud. Compared to CG lightning, IC lightning often can not
be observed with the naked eye. One visible example of this type of discharge
is presented in Figure 2.4d). Due to the fact that ICs happen within a Cb cloud,
they are often called anvil crawlers. Anvil crawlers are horizontal discharges
with the presence of a lot of branches that can spread over a large area of the
sky and can develop at very high altitudes.
• Cloud-to-cloud (CC) lightning
Cloud-to-cloud lightning can often be mistaken with IC lightning, but in this
instance instead of the lightning happening within one single cloud, the dis-
charge takes place between two or even more convective clouds as seen in
Figure 2.4e). The frequency of a CC lightning is really low.
Lightning can be classiﬁed in several ways. The most general type of classiﬁcation
is displayed above, but other types of lightning can be encountered under various
other names such as ribbon lightning, sheet lightning, bolt from the blue, cloud-to-
air lightning, bead lightning etc. For the purpose of this project only CG and IC
lightning will be processed.

2.3
Types of Severe Weather
The presence of a cumulonimbus cloud can produce different kinds of severe weather
on the ground. The most common manifestations include tornadoes, large hail,
heavy rainfall and severe wind.
Among the most violent weather phenomenon existing on Earth, and found
across the globe are tornadoes. Tornadoes can also be found under the name of
twisters. They can be described as a thin column of air which is twisting or rotat-
ing at a high speed and touching both the base of a cumulonimbus cloud and the
ground. Tornadoes become visible when the rotating air has gathered enough dirt,
dust, debris and water droplets.
Tornadoes range in their intensity. Some of them create light damage, while other
are devastating. In order to be able to classify them, a scale was created in 1971 by
a severe storm researcher named Tetsuya Fujita. The Fujita scale or an enhancement
of the Fujita scale are still in use today. The tornadoes studied in this thesis will be
classiﬁed on the Fujita scale which can be seen in Table 2.1.

Another damaging example of severe weather is hail. Hail is formed when water
droplets within a convective storm are lifted to the very cold parts of the atmosphere
because of the storms updraft. Due to the cold temperature these water drops freeze
and fall to the ground when their weight manages to overcome the updraft.
Severe winds can present a danger for many people, but those who are exposed
most to them are people living in mobile houses. Severe or damaging winds are
also called straight-line winds in order to make a distinction between devastations
caused by them or by a tornado.
Lightning can also be categorized as a type of severe weather since the number
of people killed by lightning is signiﬁcantly greater than that of any other event such
as tornadoes, hurricanes or ﬂoods.

3.1.1
EUMETSAT
EUMETSAT stands for European Organization for the Exploitation of Meteorological
Satellites and it represents an international governmental organization formed in
1986 with the aim of gathering accurate data from operational meteorological satel-
lites. EUMETSAT consists of 30 member states and one cooperating state with the
headquarters in Darmstadt, Germany.
The data collected by EUMETSAT inspects the ocean, the atmosphere and the
surface of the Earth. This data is collected 24 hours a day, 7 days a week with the
help of different satellites. The current operational satellites used by EUMETSAT are
Meteosat, Metop, Sentinel-3, Jason-3 and Jason-2.
The Meteosat satellites are a series of geostationary satellites that produce weather
data which can be used for forecasts and images of the entire globe. They can be bro-
ken down into three generations: Meteosat First Generation (MFG), Meteosat Sec-
ond Generation (MSG) and Meteosat Third Generation (MTG).
The Meteosat First Generation (MFG) was composed out of 7 satellites (Meteosat-1 to
Meteosat-7) and was active until this year. The Meteosat Second Generation (MSG)
is currently active and is composed out of 4 satellites (Meteosat-8 to Meteosat-11 or
MSG-1 to MSG-4) and the Meteosat Third Generation (MTG) will be composed of
six new satellites w planned launch date starts with the year 2021.
3.1.2
Meteosat Second Generation (MSG)
For the purpose of this project only data provided by the satellites from the second
generation was used and was accessed through the Data Centre Online Ordering
Client from the EUMETSAT website. The MSG programme was set in motion by
the cooperative efforts of ESA and EUMETSAT. ESA is in charge of the satellite’s
development and their launch in space, while EUMETSAT is responsible of the de-
velopment of the ground systems that are going to provide products, services and
required help by its users. The MSG satellites are placed 36,000 km above the equa-
tor, in geostationary orbit. Each satellite was launched at a different time and put at
a different place in orbit. The ﬁrst launched satellite was the Meteosat-8 satellite or
commonly known as the MSG-1 satellite. It was launched on the 28th of August 2002
at 41.5◦E longitude and it operates over the region of the Indian Ocean, therefore the
service provided by it are the Indian Ocean Data Coverage or IODC services. The
life expectancy is predicted to last until 2019.

3.2
Lightning
For the current thesis, lightning data that came from three different networks was
processed: the Global Lightning Dataset 360 Network, the Lightning Detection Net-
work and the Ebro Valley Lightning Mapping Array. More information on each of
these network is provided below.
3.2.1
Global Lightning Dataset 360 Network (GLD360)
The Global Lightning Dataset 360 network or often abbreviated as GLD360 or just
GLD is a lightning network conducted by Vaisala, a company with its headquarters
in Finland, that offers services and products for environmental and meteorological
assessments, as well as industrial applications.
The network supplies data in real time for the purpose of gathering information
that will be used in order to distinguish early on, signs that lead to the occurrence of
severe weather.
The beneﬁt of using GLD data is that it can cover any geographical area wanted
and it is not restricted to area over land. Also there is no need to invest in hardware
as the data is provided as a service.
There is an annual fee for using the GLD data, that varies depending on the
dimensions of the speciﬁc area.
The network measures CG lightning and it is composed out of sensors that are
operating in the VLF (Very Low Frequency) band and that use the MDF (Magnetic
Direction Finding) method in order to detect the location of the lightning stroke. The
MDF method measures the time-of-arrival (TOA) of the returned stroke and it also
measures the angle of the horizontal magnetic ﬁeld.
A typical GLD data ﬁle will contain the date and the time of the event in UTC
with a precision added in milliseconds, the location of the lightning strike offered
in latitude and longitude, the peak current amplitude in kA and the polarity of the
peak current (+/-).

3.2.2
Lightning Detection Network (LINET)
The Lightning Detection Network or LINET is a VLF/LF lightning detection sys-
tem that was developed by nowcast and it covers a large area; approximately from
longitude -10◦to 35◦and from latitude 30◦to 65◦.
The company has its headquarters in Germany. From the early development
stages of the network, the German services got involved and they were also one of
the ﬁrst users of the system. Large networks provide the advantage of being able
to track the lifetime of a storm that spreads around a wide area and make early
==PAGE:32==
14
Chapter 3. Data
lightning detections possible which can provide information on the behavior of a
storm.
Every ﬂash of lightning emits electromagnetic radiation which is dispersed at
nearly the speed of light and can be picked up by the VLF/LF antennae. In order
to determine the precise location of the lightning strike, a series of antennae are
needed. Several antennae register the event and by applying certain algorithms, the
exact location of the strike can be established. Due to the fact that LINET is able
to always measure the lightning strike with multiple sensors at the same time, it
enables a highly accurate detection.
LINET is a highly sensitive network that can detect lightning strokes that have a
peak current which is below 5 kA and is a system that detects the total lightning, but
with the advantage of being able to separate CG lightning strokes from IC lightning
strokes. The distinction between CG and IC lightning is made possible with the use
of the three dimensional TOA method as long as the distance between the event and
the sensors does not exceed approximately 250 km. In order to detect IC lightning
there is a need of at least 4 sensors.
A typical LINET data ﬁle will contain the date and time in UTC with a millisec-
ond precision, the location of the strike (longitude and latitude), the altitude of the
strike in km, the type of lightning (usually 1 refers to a CG stroke and 2 refers to a
IC stroke) and the value for the peak current in kA.

Chapter 4
Overshooting Top and Convective
Cloud Detection Methods
The presence of lasting overshooting tops have been known to cause one form or
another of hazardous weather on the ground such as strong winds, heavy rainfall,
damaging lightning, large hail or tornadoes. Therefore it is extremely important to
use methods that can detect them with high precision. A good way of detecting
overshooting tops is by observing them with the use of satellite imagery. Based on
the channels provided by the SEVIRI instrument on board of the MSG satellites, a
series of detection techniques have been developed.
The easiest way of identifying an OT is by looking at the HRV (high resolution
visible) channel as OTs tend to have a lumpy structure when examining them in the
visible channels. Even though using visible channel imagery may be the easiest way
to detect them, their detection is limited by the time of day when they appeared.
OTs can be detected during daytime, but during nighttime other techniques must be
used.
All of these techniques use the brightness temperature difference between the
SEVIRI channels. It is important to mention that these brightness temperature dif-
ference methods depend greatly on the spatial resolution of the used instrument.
For the SEVIRI instrument the BTD methods have a 3 km spatial resolution, there-
fore some OTs detected in the HRV images, channel which has a spatial resolution
of 1 km, cannot be identiﬁed using these methods.
4.1
WV −IR BTD method
The WV −IR brightness temperature difference method uses the SEVIRI water va-
por 6.2 µm channel (channel 5) and the infrared 10.8 µm channel (channel 9). The
method has to fulﬁll two criteria:
WV 6.2 −IR10.8 > 4K
(4.1)
IR10.8
brightness temperature < 215K
(4.2)
The WV 6.2 channel is used to measure the water vapor content at the higher
levels of the troposphere. This can help provide information on atmospheric insta-
bilities by being able to record the path that the water vapor is taking due to wind,
even in areas with no clouds. The IR 10.8 channel gives information on the temper-
ature of the clouds and of the surface. Above the cloud tops the temperature rises in
direct proportion with the height, therefore the temperature of the water vapor rises
and it will exceed the temperature of the cloud top. The warmer water vapor will
contribute to the water vapor band and it will show higher values for the brightness

by looking at the water vapor content through another channel, but unfortunately
the infrared channels do not detect water vapor, as it is transparent to them. The
positive values achieved through the performance of the brightness temperature dif-

can be found on top of the clouds which may have nothing to do with the thunder-
storm. If the brightness temperature difference between the two channels is greater
than zero, the clouds are considered to be convective. This method is known to be
faulty as it often detects a large number of fake OTs and sometimes the detected OTs
have a much larger diameter than the normally observed ones. This is caused due to
the fact that the moisture projected by the colder overshooting top, quickly reaches
the temperature of the surrounding warmer water vapor.

O3 −IR BTD method
The O3 −IR brightness temperature difference method is based on the infrared 9.7
µm channel (channel 8) and the infrared 10.8 µm channel (channel 9). The infrared
9.7 µm channel is also known as the ozone channel due to the fact that it measures
the concentration of the ozone at the lower part of the stratosphere.
This method needs to meet the following requests:
O3 −IR10.8 > 13K

Compared to the previous method (WV −IR BTD), studies [10], [11] have shown
that the O3 −IR BTD method has proven itself to be more reliable in detecting deep

4.3
CO2 −IR BTD method
The CO2 −IR brightness temperature difference method uses the SEVIRI infrared
13.4 µm channel (channel 11) and the infrared 10.8 µm channel (channel 9). The
infrared 13.4 µm channel is also known as the CO2 channel.. It is used to determine
the atmospheric instability and the height of the semi-transparent cirrus clouds.
The criteria that this method has to satisfy is:
CO2 −IR10.8 > 3.5K

brightness temperature < 215K
(4.6)
The positive values of CO2 −IR are given because in the case of a thunderstorm,
the absorption of CO2 becomes less for very high altitude clouds.

4.5
Global Convective Diagnostics
The Global Convective Diagnostics or GCD is a method that allows the detection of
convective clouds. This method is also based on a brightness temperature difference
between the infrared 10.8 µm channel (channel 9) and the water vapor 6.2 µm chan-
nel (channel 5). The method uses the same channels as the WV −IR BTD method,
but the algorithm and the criteria that it follows are different.
IR10.8 −WV 6.2 > 1K
no convection
(4.10)
IR10.8 −WV 6.2 ≤1K
convection
(4.11)
When we do not have convection the brightness temperature difference tends to
be positive. For no convection the temperature measured by the IR 10.8 µm channel
belongs to the surface of the Earth and the temperature measured by the WV 6.2 µm
channel belongs to the layer of moist that can always be found in the upper levels.
For the case of convective clouds, the brightness temperature difference tends to
be close to 0, or even negative for extremely deep convection. In this instance the
temperature measured by the infrared channel belongs mainly to clouds.

Comparison between GLD and LMA lightning data
One of the main differences between the GLD and LMA networks consists in the
fact that GLD gathers two dimensional lightning data and LMA can capture three
dimensional lightning data. Therefore the GLD network only detects CG lightning.
However, compared to the LINET network, the LMA network does not provide data
ﬁles where IC and CG lightning is distinguished.

vides the peak current intensity in kA, whereas LMA provides the power in dBW. If
one were to compare the ease of use for the data ﬁles, GLD deﬁnitely would top the
LMA network. GLD data ﬁles come in .txt format, whereas the LMA ﬁles come in
a complex .dat format that might require some work in order to subtract the needed
information. An example of an LMA data ﬁle can be seen in Appendix G. On the
other hand, LMA data ﬁles provide much more information than GLD.

Chapter 6
Discussion
6.1
Comparison of presence/absence of OTs
As stated in Chapter 5 the existence of OTs was analyzed using four different meth-
ods based on brightness temperature differences. The four BTD methods were the
WV −IR BTD method, the O3−IR BTD method, the CO2−IR BTD method and the
COMB method. For the three analyzed cases, where we only considered the main
severe weather event described more thoroughly, only the case for the tornado that
took place in Venice on the 8th of July 2015 showed the presence of a valid OT.
The Ebro valley case also showed the presence of an OT by using the CO2 −IR
BTD method, but it was proven that it was a false OT since the detection method
often mistakes areas of high convection with an OT.
Since the SEVIRI instrument takes images of the entire globe every 15 minutes
there is a chance that an OT might have been present for the other two cases, but
was not detected because of the temporal resolution of the equipment.
In the case where the OT was present the lightning frequency started to increase
signiﬁcantly just before the OT was observed and the same behavior was observed
for areas with deep convection.
6.2
Comparison of tornado cases
The tornado that hit the Mira municipality on the 8th of June at 15:30 UTC was con-
nected to the presence of an OT at 14:45 UTC, whereas the Bützow tornado recorded
at 16:48 UTC on the 5th of May 2015 did not show the presence of an OT in satellite
imagery. However, the presence of deep convection observed in the global convec-
tive diagnosis at 16:30 UTC over the region of Bützow might be connected to the
severe phenomenon. There is also the possibility of an OT existing on that particular
day, but because of the temporal resolution of the SEVIRI instrument which captures
images every 15 minutes of the entire globe, it may have escaped detection.
The spatial distribution of lightning and spatial distribution of the convective
cloud show a signiﬁcant correlation in both cases. The total, IC and CG lightning
follow the pattern mentioned by Mikuš in her paper [11] after the 10 kA ﬁlter was
applied, where the TL and IC lightning have similar dispersions and the CG light-
ning decreases considerably compared to the unﬁltered case.
The two tornadoes share a resemblance in the TL and IC lightning behavior near
the time of the OT or deep convection. In both cases there is a sudden increase
slightly before the OT or deep convection was noticed in the satellite images.
The behavior of the CG lightning is not a good indicator for tornadoes, therefore
they were not compared.

Chapter 6. Discussion
Both cases showed a higher altitude in IC lightning at the time of the OT or deep
convection which decreased somewhat at the time of the extreme weather happen-
ing on the ground.
The number of measurements for the intensity of the peak current was also
higher in the presence of an OT or deep convection for both tornadoes, nonethe-
less the Venice tornado showed a maximum in the number of measurements and
the Germany tornado did not. But since the Germany case was so complex and it
consisted of six cases of tornadoes, that might have been the reason for the discrep-
ancy.
When analyzing the cloud top height the ﬁrst case showed altitudes of up to 13
km and the second case had altitudes below 12 km. This might be the reason why
the ﬁrst case had a verifyable OT and the second case only had a region of deeper
convection.
6.3
Example of classic thunderstorm behavior
Since no record was made of the event that took place on the 24th of July 2015 over
the Ebro valley in Spain one could conclude that the event was not of extreme nature.
Even though the analysis of this case consisted in a lot of gaps between certain hours
there was a clear connection between higher frequencies in lightning and the pres-
ence of deeper convection. Another connection was found between the maximum
number of measurements detected and highest cloud top altitudes.
Therefore, not knowing exactly what happened on the ground, no connection can
be made between the weather phenomenon and the lightning and cloud behavior.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The essence of the present thesis consisted of an analysis of satellite imagery regard-
ing cloud tops and lightning activity for three different cases involving the presence
of a convective storm.
The SEVIRI instrument on board the MSG satellite was used to gather informa-
tion on clouds, while three different networks were used for the detection of light-
ning events. A search for the presence of OTs was conducted utilizing four differ-
ent brightness temperature methods, of which the COMB method has once again
demonstrated itself to be the most useful.
Of the three lightning detection networks LINET surpassed the other two for
various reasons. One of them consists of the fact that LINET is able to detect three
dimensional lightning while at the same time making a distinction between CG and
IC lightning. Another beneﬁt of the LINET network consists in its easy usage and
precise measurements drawing on a large network. LMA also provides three dimen-
sional data, but its data ﬁles are hard to process and LMA does not have such a good
data coverage. As for the GLD network its easy ﬁle use is eclipsed by the fact that it
only utilizes two dimensional measurements.
All three cases were analyzed from a spatial and temporal point of view. The
spatial analysis regarding the behavior of the lightning in a certain area frame and
the development of the convective clouds in that same frame was consistent for all
three of the cases, as was the temporal analysis between total lightning development
and satellite observations.
All three cases showed an increase in total lightning near the area of an OT or
deep convection. One must keep in mind the fact that the current thesis was based
only on a visual interpretation and not on a statistical study.
For the development of future satellites a few things should be taken into con-
sideration.
A problem encountered in the analysis of the case studies was the uncertainty
of the overshooting top detection. It was stated several times that, for the Germany
and Spain cases, there was the chance of an OT appearing but not being identiﬁed
due to the spatial and/or temporal resolution of the SEVIRI instrument.
In order to improve on this problem the next generation of satellites should con-
sider upgrading the spatial resolution of the equipment from 3 km spatial resolution
to a possible 1 km spatial resolution to be in conformity with the HRV channel of
the SEVIRI instrument. Also a higher image resolution should be considered for the
HRV images.
Another thing to look at would involve the temporal resolution of the instru-
ment. The chances of ﬁnding an OT, if one exists in cases like the Germany and
Spain severe weather events, would increase considerably if images where provided
every 5 minutes instead of every 15 minutes.
==PAGE:70==
52
Chapter 7. Conclusion
One should also play with the idea of adding more channels to the instrument in
order to be able to establish new, and perhaps better, OT detecting methods.
Regarding the data ﬁles, in order to improve the processing time, data should be
considered to be delivered already unzipped from its C format. In this way there
would be no requirement for accessing extra software from EUMETSAT.
The next generation of weather satellites, Meteosat Third Generation (MTG),
consists of six new satellites that have already taken care of some of these aspects.
MTG will have on board a SEVIRI instrument and an IASI instrument which have a
signiﬁcantly higher number of channels.

Appendix H
Notes on program development
Satellite data
The satellite data is processed with the help of the Python programming language
and of the pytroll project developed speciﬁcally for Python. When developing the
algorithm necessary for the analysis of SEVIRI data one must be aware of the de-
compression that the HRIT ﬁle necessitates. As stated in the thesis after the .tar
decompression, a software must be downloaded from the EUMETSAT website and
used for further decompression.
Once all the data has been decompressed it is not necessary to convert the ef-
fective radiance into brightness temperature for every channel since that conversion
has already been taken care of.
In order to obtain the altitude of the clouds, the cloud top height product must be
downloaded from the EUMETSAT website. The CTH comes in GRIB format which
consists out of gridded data. This is analyzed with the help of the pygrib Phyton
package.
Lightning data
The lightning data provided by the three networks (GLD, LINET and LMA) was
processed with the help of the Matlab programming language.
