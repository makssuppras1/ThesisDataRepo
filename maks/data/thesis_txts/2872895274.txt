Abstract
Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs) are a widely adopted yet contentious model for deliv-
ering major transport projects, praised for leveraging private sector efficiency but criticized
for fiscal risks and public value failures. This thesis critically examines the question: To
what extent do PPPs remain a viable model for financing and delivering trans-
port infrastructure projects in the 21st century?
Employing a mixed-methodology of literature review, comparative case studies and stake-
holder interviews, this research analyzes high-profile failures, including Australia’s Airport
Link Tunnel and the London Underground PPP, to distill the factors determining project
outcomes. The findings reveal that PPP success is contingent upon three critical factors:
financial structuring, contractual design and institutional capacity. The research demon-
strates that demand-based revenue models are exceptionally vulnerable to forecasting bias,
while contractual ambiguity and fragmented governance can lead to project collapse, even
when technical delivery is successful.
Ultimately, the thesis concludes that institutional capacity is the single most important
determinant of PPP viability. A competent public sector capable of rigorous project ap-
praisal and active oversight is a prerequisite for success. Therefore, PPPs remain a viable
but highly conditional tool, with their future relevance likely shifting towards different
revenue model, smaller and tightly scoped projects where public interest is protected by
strong governance.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation of the Research
Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs) have become a widely adopted strategy for funding
government infrastructure projects, particularly in the transport sector. They are often
seen as a tool to bridge the infrastructure gap by leveraging private sector efficiency, exper-
tise, and capital. However, global experiences have increasingly questioned the effective-
ness and desirability of PPPs, raising concerns about their long-term viability. Denmark’s
approach is particularly interesting because it combines elements of both public and pri-
vate sectors without fully adhering to the traditional PPP model, offering a potential
alternative to conventional PPP frameworks.
Key railway transport entities such as Banedanmark and Danske Statsbaner (DSB) op-
erate under the Ministry of Transport. Although both are established as independent
public enterprises, they incorporate elements of public and private sector engagement.
This raises a critical question: Why has the Danish government chosen this operational
structure instead of a conventional PPP framework? Moreover, what defines a PPP? Is
the distinction based on risk allocation, financing mechanisms or governance structures?
This thesis is motivated by the need to critically evaluate the role of PPPs in trans-
port infrastructure projects worldwide. Reviewing regulatory developments highlighted
by the World Bank and other relevant sources, the research will analyse successful and
failed PPP cases to assess their applicability in different contexts, alongside interviews
conducted with relevant stakeholders.
1.2
Market Update on Public Private Partnership Projects
In 2024, the European public-private partnership market saw a total of 11.47 billion EUR
in transactions reaching financial close, a 17% decline from 13.9 billion EUR in 2023 [1],
highlighted in the Figure 1.
The number of closed transactions remained relatively stable, with 39 deals completed
compared to 38 the previous year. However, the average transaction size fell from 367 mil-
lion EU down to 294 million EUR in 2024, indicating a shift toward smaller-scale projects.
Six large-scale transactions closed in 2024, totaling 7.3 billion EUR and accounting for
64% of the total value of the market (compared to 69% in 2023). These included the
Ghent R4 Highway West and East in Belgium (1 billion EUR), the Aragon Extraordinary
Road Plan in Spain (630 million EUR), the Athens Ring Road Retender in Greece (3.3
billion EUR) and few more [1].

1.3
Research Question
One significant issue in the assessment and evaluation of PPPs is the over-reliance on
Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA) and Public Sector Comparators (PSCs) as primary analyti-
cal tools. These methods are limited in their ability to identify and quantify the microe-
conomic effects and potential impacts of future events related to PPP procurement. For
instance, PSCs often tend to exclude non-financial costs to society when calculating Value
for Money (VfM), as noted by Demirag and Khadaroo [3].
2
==PAGE:9==
This issue is partly due to the lack of sufficient historical data, which hinders the compar-
ison of benefits and costs among different procurement options. Moreover, both CBA and
PSC tend to emphasize the financial benefits of PPPs, neglecting alternative measures of
success. These alternative measures could include the quality of services provided, con-
sumer satisfaction and broader economic and social impacts. By focusing solely on the
monetary aspects, these tools may not capture the full range of factors that influence the
desired results of PPP projects. Therefore, the following research question is posed:
To what extent do Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs) remain a viable model
for financing and delivering transport infrastructure projects in the 21st cen-
tury?
In order to tackle this question, the thesis breaks down the main question into defin-
ing what is a successful PPP delivery and what led to it by examining the following
sub-questions:
• What factors influence the success or failure of PPPs?
• How does institutional capacity affect project delivery under PPPs?
• Under what conditions do public-led or hybrid models outperform PPPs?
This thesis adopts a mixed-method approach, combining internet research, a structured
literature review, stakeholder interviews, and comparative case studies. The theoretical
background for PPP model is gathered by official government documents, contract design,
and public sector webpage.
The Literature Review chapter synthesizes existing research on PPP models, highlighting
recent trends in literature, statistics on projects and brief case studies.
The Method-
ology chapter presents the research design structured around the thesis. It outlines the
methodological approach and data collection techniques used in the study. The subsequent
chapters present thematic analysis based on the interviews, Case Studies (deep-dives), then
followed by a discussion that has a summary of key insights and integrates these results
with the theoretical framework to interpret the viability of PPPs. By the end, the conclu-
sion will try to directly answer the main research question and addresses study limitations
and identifies areas for future work.

Dehornoy’s analysis reveals that rail PPPs have evolved significantly over the past two

2.2
Why Public-Private Partnerships Are Not the Solution
The Eurodad (2022) report [2] provides a critical assessment of transport infrastructure
PPPs, arguing they often compromise fiscal stability, transparency and social equity.
Drawing from global project data, the report challenges the dominant narrative of PPPs
as efficient, risk-sharing instruments and points to systemic failures in cost control, gov-
ernance, and public accountability.
Key financial criticisms include persistent cost overruns and the use of high-cost private
capital, which often exceeds sovereign borrowing rates. In the Expressway Yellow Line
project in Peru, renegotiations triggered escalating tolls and fiscal strain, highlighting the
fragility of revenue assumptions. Similarly, many PPPs exhibit optimistic demand fore-
casts that unravel post-implementation, transferring financial risk back to the state.
Governance failures are another recurring issue. The report cites the Interoceanic Corridor
in Mexico, where opaque contract terms and limited public oversight led to misalignment
with public interest goals. These dynamics are compounded by the complexity of PPP
legal structures, which often conceal accountability and dilute state leverage during rene-
gotiations.
Socioeconomic impacts are also significant. The report argues that user-fee-driven PPPs
frequently restrict access for lower-income populations, prioritizing investor returns over
equitable service delivery. By embedding commercial logic into essential infrastructure,
PPPs risk creating two-tiered access regimes. While Eurodad is clearly bias for publicly
financed infrastructure with transparent governance, its position must be understood in
the context. Yet, its empirical findings are worth consideration, especially in contexts
where fiscal capacity is sufficient to support public delivery.
The report concludes with recommendations for reform - enforce meticulous Value-for-
money assessments, enhance transparency in procurement and teach public sector on
participation in project execution. These suggestions aim to restore alignment between
infrastructure delivery and public interest.

Advantages of Public-Private Partnerships in Transport Infrastruc-
ture
The literature review examines the benefits of PPPs in roads and highways [5], focusing
on increased funding, private sector efficiencies, public sector inefficiencies, and risk re-
duction. The review aims to address the main research question by presenting a positive
perspective on PPP projects, highlighting their role in mobilizing additional funding, im-
proving efficiency and managing risks.
The review synthesizes findings from various PPP projects, starting with their ability

allow the public sector to spread project costs over a longer period, freeing up funds for

PPPs also introduce private sector efficiencies through a life-cycle approach, which in-
cludes work planning, cost optimization, risk management and innovation. Claiming that
long-term contracts enable better planning and resource allocation, leading to timely per-
formance and reduced operational costs. The life-cycle approach allows the private sector
to balance expenditure over the project life, resulting in cost savings and improved service
delivery. Additionally, PPPs can reduce the likelihood of delays in timeline by transferring
risks to the private sector, which is better equipped to manage them. This risk transfer
is a key incentive for PPPs, allowing the public sector to maintain better control over
project costs, delivery timelines and quality of outputs.
The review critically evaluates the benefits of PPPs, demonstrating that they can signifi-
cantly increase investment in transport infrastructure and drive efficiency improvements.
While PPPs offer advantages in terms of funding and risk management, the review also
highlights the importance of careful financial structuring and robust governance to avoid
fiscal liabilities. The findings suggest that PPPs can be a valuable tool for governments
seeking to enhance infrastructure development, provided they are well-regulated and trans-
parent. The review emphasizes the need for a balanced approach to risk allocation and
contract management to ensure long-term infrastructure sustainability and public welfare.

2.4
Private entities motivations to participate in public-private partner-
ships
Kopa´nska et al. (2024) examine what drives or discourages private companies from join-
ing PPPs, using transaction cost economics (TCE) as their lens [6]. Although the study
focuses on Poland, its insights are broadly applicable to transport infrastructure projects.
High transaction costs are a major deterrent. Rigid contracts that transfer demand risk
to the private sector, especially in volatile sectors such as transport, are less attractive.
In contrast, availability-based contracts, which pay based on infrastructure performance
rather than usage, are preferred. These models align better with private capabilities in
construction and maintenance.
Long project durations also increase uncertainty and costs, making firms more hesitant to
bid. Stable public partners and strong local economies, on the other hand, increase private
interest. Wealthier and fiscally sound regions attract more bidders, which raises equity
concerns - investment may be easy to get in developed countries, however, less developed
regions are left behind leaving World Bank as their main option to be financed.
Interestingly, the study finds that technical support from central PPP units can sometimes
discourage participation, likely due to added complexity. In contrast, financial incentives
such as EU grants significantly boost private interest by reducing risk.
The key takeaway is that successful PPPs must minimize transaction costs and offer pre-
dictable and balanced contracts. Private firms respond to practical risk-return signals, not
just policy goals. For governments, this means designing flexible frameworks and targeting
support where private interest is lacking.
2.5
Data Review of Transportation Infrastructure Public– Private Part-
nership: A Meta-Analysis
Chen (2015) conducts a meta-analysis of 95 empirical studies on transport PPPs [7], drawn
from a larger pool of over 345. The goal is to assess how data is used across five research
themes: performance, contracts, risk, value for money and institutions.
The analysis shows clear patterns. Case studies dominate research on performance and
institutional issues, while surveys are more common in contract-related studies. Risk and
VfM topics often rely on mixed or less robust data. Institutional aspects, despite being
critical to PPP success, are understudied and poorly supported by empirical evidence.
A major insight is the limited availability and quality of PPP data. Without consistent
disclosure of performance metrics and contract outcomes, robust evaluation is difficult.
This lack of transparency undermines accountability and weakens policy learning.
8
==PAGE:15==
Chen calls for improved data collection, standardized reporting, and greater access to
project performance indicators. Better data would enable more rigorous research, inform
policymaking and help refine PPP models for transport infrastructure. This is especially
relevant to evaluating the continued viability of PPPs in the 21st century, as robust,
transparent data is essential for assessing whether these partnerships are delivering on
their financial, operational and public value promises over time.
2.6
Barriers to PPP in municipal and regional public works contracts
The PPP model has been extensively studied in various contexts, with a focus on un-
derstanding the factors that drive or deter municipalities and regions from adopting this
approach. The Danish Competition and Consumer Authority [8] has identified several bar-
riers to PPP implementation. These include themes such as time and resource-intensive
process surrounding PPPs, the belief that municipalities or regions can create better so-
lutions internally and the consideration that public works contracts are too small in value
compared to PPP. Additionally, private finance is perceived as too expensive, competi-
tion in the PPP market is considered lax and potential financial investors are seen as
risk-averse.

Research Methodology
In this chapter of the paper, an overview of the methods employed in the study is provided.
In order to collect and analyze data on the research topic that addresses the research
question: To what extent do PPPs remain a viable model for financing and delivering
transport infrastructure projects in the 21st century?, a variety of methods are drawn
from, which are explained below. The following sections will outline the research approach,
purpose and design and the data collection and data analysis process. In addition, the
usage of artificial intelligence (AI) in supporting various stages of the research process is
reflected upon.
3.1
Qualitative Methodology
Strauss [9] highlighted that qualitative research adopts a naturalistic approach to under-
stand phenomena within specific contexts, while quantitative research uses experimen-
tal methods and statistical measures to test hypotheses and generalize findings. These
methods reflect distinct research paradigms, shaping the researcher’s approach. However,
qualitative research, as defined by Strauss and Corbin [9], generates findings without sta-
tistical or quantitative methods, focusing on illumination and understanding rather than
causal determination or prediction. It produces a unique type of knowledge compared
to quantitative inquiry. When choosing qualitative research, it is useful to explore poorly
understood phenomena, offer new perspectives on familiar topics or capture in-depth infor-
mation that is hard to quantify. Research questions are typically open-ended to facilitate
the discovery of new insights.
This thesis employed a qualitative research methodology, utilizing case studies and inter-
views for exploratory investigation. Data collection was conducted using various sources,
including academic databases such as DTU FindIt and Google Scholar, as well as general
search engines. The case study of Airport Link Tunnel and London Underground were
selected due to its distinct contractual terms and private funding. These cases provided a
11
==PAGE:18==
rich and diverse set of data sources, which were fundamental in the analysis.
In qualitative research, the researcher lacks prior knowledge of outcomes or key variables.
The case study approach allows for flexible exploration of ”how” and ”why” questions,
offering deep insights into complex phenomena. Unlike quantitative methods, qualitative
research avoids statistical procedures, focusing instead on real-world contexts, including
personal experiences, biases and organizational dynamics.
3.2
Bias
Bias is a central concern in qualitative research, particularly when selecting case stud-
ies, as choices made during case selection can significantly shape findings and limit their
transferability. To limit researcher bias, case studies were chosen based on pre-defined,
transparent criteria aligned with the research objectives rather than convenience or prior
familiarity. This approach follows the guidance of Flyvbjerg [10], who emphasizes strategic
case selection to maximize information value. Reflexivity was maintained throughout the
process, with continuous questioning of assumptions about what is a “typical” or “critical”
case.
In terms of selection bias, cases were drawn from PPP transport infrastructure projects
across different geographies and governance models to capture variation in context, scale
and maturity. However, the final set of case studies was partly influenced by accessibility
and data availability, which may introduce bias by over-representing projects with more
transparent documentation. This is also reflected in the literature review by Chen’s Meta-
Analysis [7].
While every effort was made to ensure honest representation of information, the focus
on well-documented, relatively successful PPP projects may limit the inclusion of cases
marked by failure, conflict or opacity, thus constraining the diversity of experiences and
lessons. Nonetheless, including a mix of projects with differing degrees of institutional
complexity and stakeholder dynamics allowed for cross-case comparison and enriched an-
alytical insights.
3.3
Case Study
Case study research is particularly effective for understanding complex issues and build-
ing on existing knowledge [11]. It is an exploratory method that investigates phenomena
in real-life contexts, distinguishing it from other research approaches. Case studies can
utilize multiple resources and explore historical and behavioral aspects of a phenomenon.
However, Rowley [12] criticized case studies for lacking rigor and objectivity compared to
other social research methods, arguing that reliance on a single case limits generalization.
In this thesis, case studies were used to validate the social benefits of PPP infrastructure.
12
==PAGE:19==
Data from various sources ensured accurate analysis and interpretation [9]. The chosen
case studies, in PPP procurements, highlighted feasibility studies risks and unreasonable
contract flaws.
3.4
Analytical Approach and Data Structure for Interviews
Thematic analysis, as described by Braun and Clarke [13], was applied to structure and
interpret the qualitative data presented in this paper. The purpose of this method is to sys-
tematically identify, analyze and interpret patterns or themes across a dataset. Although
the original interview transcripts were not available, detailed summaries were provided by
a previous colleague who conducted the interviews herself. These summaries served as the
empirical basis for the thematic analysis. The absence of the transcripts limits the granu-
larity of interpretation, but was replaced with the high level of details in the provided notes.
According to Braun and Clarke’s methodology, the first phase involved familiarization
with the dataset. This entailed repeated readings of the summaries to extract prominent
information, with particular attention to statements relevant to research questions on PPP
applicability, risk and sustainability.
The second phase focused on theme construction. Keywords were reviewed and grouped
into preliminary thematic categories based on conceptual and contextual coherence. An
initial set of themes was drafted to reflect recurring patterns in the responses of the inter-
viewees.
The final phase involved reviewing and refining the themes to ensure complete consistency
and alignment with the dataset. A thematic map was constructed to visually represent the
interrelations among the themes. In addition, generic labels such as “efficiency” were re-
formulated as “profit orientation and efficiency” to more exactly capture the intended logic
in PPP narratives. The thematic map was also adjusted to reflect the relative positions
of the themes, those more aligned with the logic of the private sector on the left side and
the themes reflecting the considerations of the public sector and long-term sustainability
on the right side.

Theoretical Background for PPP Model
4.1
What Are Public-Private Partnerships?
Public-Private Partnerships are long-term contractual arrangements between public au-
thorities and private entities to deliver public projects or services.
These agreements
typically involve the private sector handling the design, financing, construction and oper-
ation of the projects, while the public sector maintains strategic oversight and regulatory
authority. PPPs differ from traditional procurement by transferring risks to private part-
ners, who are compensated through performance-linked payment models.
The concept of PPPs has been around for centuries. Historical evidence trace PPPs back
to the Roman Empire, where private partners built and managed postal stations for 5-year
periods, sometimes including maintenance of associated highways, to support the vast ex-
pansion of the cursus publicus, the state transportation system of the time[14].
The World Bank defines PPPs as:
“A long-term contract between a private party and a government entity, for
providing a public asset or service, in which the private party bears signifi-
cant risk and management responsibility, and remuneration is linked to perfor-
mance.”[15]
The distinction between public and private entities in PPPs isn’t strictly defined and varies
according to the legal framework of the country where the partnership is established. Ac-
cording to the World Bank Guidance on PPP Legal Frameworks[16], both public and
private entities can include state-owned enterprises (SOEs). This is particularly evident
in China, where SOEs have become the primary partners of local governments. Unlike
traditional private sector entities that operate with more independent market-driven ob-
jectives, Chinese SOEs often function as extensions of government policy implementation.
They combine elements from the public and private sectors, allowing governments to main-
tain strategic control while leveraging private-sector efficiency and expertise. This hybrid
model raises unique questions about risk distribution, accountability, and the true nature
of ”private” participation in infrastructure development[17].
14
==PAGE:21==
4.2
PPP Typologies and Contractual Structures
PPPs includes a spectrum of contractual model types designed to establish collaboration
between the public and private sectors in the delivery of a project. These frameworks vary
significantly from each other and it is arguably why PPP models are so applicable in all
projects. Three main parameters define any PPP arrangement: 1) the type of project,
2) the allocation of functional responsibilities to the private sector and 3) the revenue
mechanism used to compensate the private party.
1. Project Type
The type of project typically determines the scope and intensity of capital of the PPP.
Two primary categories are used[18]:
• Greenfield Projects: These involve building entirely new infrastructure on un-
developed sites, such as new highways, rail links or airports. Greenfield projects
typically require substantial capital expenditures (CAPEX) and involve higher de-
mand and construction risks.
• Brownfield Projects: These aim to rehabilitate, expand or operate existing in-
frastructure. Examples include upgrading a congested highway or optimizing rail
station capacity. Brownfield projects often have lower CAPEX requirements and
benefit from established demand patterns but may face operational inefficiencies or
institutional legacy issues.
2. Private Sector Functions
The second parameter relates to the scope of responsibilities transferred to the private
sector. These functions are typically defined during the feasibility study and encoded in
the contract. Core responsibilities may include:
• Design (D): This involves the creation of a comprehensive project plan, which in-
cludes defining the scope of the project and identifying key project milestones.
• Build (B): In the context of PPPs for new infrastructure assets, the private partner
is generally required to construct the asset and install all necessary equipment.
• Rehabilitate (R): When a PPP involves an existing asset, the private partner may
be responsible for rehabilitating or expanding the asset.
• Finance (F): When a PPP includes the construction or rehabilitation of an asset,
the private partner is typically required to finance all or part of the capital and
operational expenditures.
• Maintain (M): This entails maintaining the infrastructure to a specified standard
throughout the duration of the contract.
15
==PAGE:22==
• Own (O): The private partner retains ownership of the asset throughout the duration
of the contract.
• Transfer (T): This involves the transfer of the asset or the operations and mainte-
nance of a particular project.
• Lease (L): In some cases, the private partner leases assets to the public entity for
the duration of the contract.
• Operate (Op): The operating responsibilities of a private partner in a PPP can vary
widely depending on the nature of the underlying asset and associated service. For
example, a private partner could be responsible for:
– Operating an asset and providing a bulk service to a government off-taker; for
example, a bulk water treatment plant.
– Operating an asset and providing services directly to users; for example, a water
distribution system.
– Providing support services, with the government agency retaining responsibility
for delivering the public service to users; for example, a school building that
includes janitorial services.
Figure 5: PPP models summarized and ordered based on private sector involvement
3. Revenue Mechanism
How the private partner is paid determines financial viability and risk exposure:
• User Pays: Revenue is generated directly from end users (e.g. toll roads, fare-
based rail). This model exposes the private partner to demand risk but reduces
fiscal pressure on governments.
• Government Pays: The public sector makes availability or performance-based
payments. This model limits demand risk, but transfers fiscal risk to the state.
16
==PAGE:23==
• Hybrid Payments: Combines user fees and government subsidies to balance af-
fordability, cost recovery and investor returns. Common in sectors with partial cost
recovery potential e.g. rail, wastewater.
Each mechanism carries distinct implications for risk-sharing, project bank-ability and
long-term sustainability.
Poor alignment between the payment model and the project
characteristics often leads to renegotiations, fiscal burdens or service shortfalls. Figure 5
illustrates how different functions impact different factors, highlighting the importance of
selecting an appropriate model to ensure successful PPP outcomes.
4.3
Motives for Public-Private Partnerships
Governments often turn to PPPs to address infrastructure needs without bearing the full
financial burden upfront. Beyond financing, PPPs facilitate the transfer of certain project
risks, such as construction delays and cost overruns, to private partners, who are suppos-
edly better equipped to manage these challenges. Furthermore, the involvement of private
entities often brings specialized expertise, innovation and efficiency to project execution,
potentially leading to better service delivery and operational performance. Thus, while
PPPs can demand higher long-term costs compared to traditional public procurement,
their strategic advantages in risk management, efficiency and resource allocation make
them a compelling sourcing option for infrastructure development [19].

4.4
Assessing and Structuring PPP Projects
Before selecting a PPP model, governments must conduct a structured assessment to
determine its suitability. This typically involves five key dimensions [21]:
• Public Need and Benefits: The project must align with national priorities and
demonstrate clear public value. Feasibility studies should confirm technical viability,
cost-effectiveness, and long-term socio-economic benefits, including environmental
and social considerations.
• Private Sector Appeal: The project must be structured to attract credible private-
sector interest, offering a balanced risk-reward profile and clear revenue mechanisms.
Market sounding during procurement helps validate this.
• Value for Money (VfM): A PPP should only proceed if it offers better VfM than
traditional procurement. For instance, the Public Sector Comparator (PSC) is a
tool used in countries like Australia and India to benchmark expected performance
and life-cycle costs against traditional procurement methods [22].
• Budget Fit: The project must fit within the government’s fiscal constraints. This
includes assessing long-term liabilities, contingent risks and affordability.
• Government Readiness: Success hinges on the public authority’s ability to man-
age the project life-cycle. This includes having the mandate, skilled personnel, gov-
ernance structures and financial capacity to oversee the contract
Figure 6: Key Dimensions in PPP Project Assessment

The UK’s Five Case Model[23] exemplifies this approach. By thoroughly assessing these di-
mensions, governments are ought to ensure that PPP projects not only meet their strategic
objectives but also deliver sustainable and efficient outcomes for the public. Similar frame-
works are used in countries like the Philippines [24], where large infrastructure projects
must undergo rigorous multi-dimensional feasibility reviews prior to approval.
4.5
Systemic Failures in Infrastructure Planning and Project Selection
When governments fail to assess and structure PPP projects, across the five dimensions
mentioned earlier, they often encounter systemic breakdowns in planning and project se-
lection. This results in less value from infrastructure investments, waste public resources,
deliver limited value and dissipate trust in the PPP model. The most common failure
modes fall into three connected categories:
1. Lack of Coordinated Planning
Infrastructure initiatives are frequently selected without coherent national or sectoral
strategies. Weak coordination between agencies leads to fragmented investment pipelines,
misaligned priorities and projects that lack integration or responsiveness to actual de-
mand. As a result, governments struggle to sequence projects for maximum value.
Estimates from McKinsey & Company reinforce the urgency of addressing these ineffi-
ciencies. Their 2016 report projected a global infrastructure need of approximately USD
49 trillion between 2016 and 2030 [25]. However, a 2013 McKinsey study concluded that
improving infrastructure productivity - primarily through better planning and selection
practices - could save up to USD 1 trillion annually over the same period. [26]
2. Flawed Analytical Foundations
Project assessments often rest on biased or weak analysis.
Overly optimistic demand
forecasts, underestimated costs and insufficient risk evaluation result in over-sized or mis-
aligned infrastructure. The UK Government’s Green Book [27] recognizes this “optimism
bias”, and mandates adjustments to improve forecast realism. Supplementary guidance
(UK Treasury, [28]) provides empirical evidence of this optimism bias, dating back to early
2000s assessments.
A global series of studies by Bent Flyvbjerg was done that exposes this misrepresenta-
tion in project forecasting: in a study of 258 transport projects, actual costs exceeded
estimates by 28% on average and up to 65% in non-OECD countries.
Rail passenger
volumes were overestimated by more than 100% in many cases. [10]

The third failure, political instability and hidden agendas, is the most deeply embedded

A Case Study of the Airport Link Tunnel Project

privately funded transport infrastructure project to date [31]. The Airport Link Tunnel
was part of a portfolio that included the Northern Busway, the Airport Roundabout

The project has significantly reduced travel times, improved public transport efficiency and
created numerous jobs during its construction. It also includes advanced safety systems
and measures to manage environmental impact, making it a landmark achievement in

Factors Leading to Failure
In order to understand what and why it went wrong, a detailed review of the Airport Link
Tunnel project’s financial and risk-related shortcomings reveals a structurally flawed PPP
model that ultimately led to the project’s financial failure. These shortcomings can be
systematically grouped into three mutually exclusive but collectively exhaustive (MECE)
categories: financial structuring failures, capital market breakdown, revenue model flaws.

First, the project’s financial model was inherently fragile; heavily dependent on toll
revenues without availability payments or contingent government support.
This one-
dimensional revenue base, combined with a complex Initial Public Offering (IPO) funding
mechanism, led to insufficient capital uptake, particularly when major parties such as Mac-
quarie and Deutsche Bank failed to deliver the full committed amounts. Retail investors
were left to bridge the shortfall, exposing the project to market volatility and triggering
systemic confidence issues [39].
Second, investor confidence was even more diminished by BrisConnections’ previous mis-
takes on the Sydney Lane Cove Tunnel, where it had suffered substantial financial losses.
This reputational setback, together with the financial market’s general skepticism toward
toll-based infrastructure, beard with similar failures such as the Cross City Tunnel and
EastLink, meant that equity and debt markets were reluctant to get involved further.

Lastly, the project was plagued by residual risks that were neither quantified nor mit-
igated. For instance, the demand-risk management strategy was insufficient. Traffic vol-
ume projections were overly optimistic, despite claims of conservatism, and failed to reflect
real-world variables such as changing motorist behavior, economic conditions and availabil-
ity of toll-free alternatives. Independent studies and commentators such as Flyvbjerg has
described such traffic forecasts as “delusional”, reducing the creditability of the financial
features for the project. This misalignment between financial exposure, revenue structure
and risk transfer mechanisms highlights why the project failed to deliver a sustainable or
resilient PPP outcome, despite successful engineering and on-time delivery.
5.1.3
PPP Infrastructure Projects in Australia
One can assume that this project was an exceptional case, however, toll road infrastruc-
ture developed under PPP models in Australia reveals recurring patterns across financial,
operational and policy dimensions. The financing challenge in these projects often require
coordination across multiple banks, leading to delays and increased complexity. Despite
strong engineering execution, these projects struggle with financial sustainability, largely
due to weak federal support and a narrow dependence on toll revenue, disconnected from
actual traffic performance. Demand forecasts, often built on inaccurate assumptions, con-
sistently overestimate user consumption, with projects such as EastLink achieving only
45% of predicted traffic. EastLink project is another project ran by Thiess John Holland,
the same constructor partner of Airport Link Tunnel, and suffered a loss of $93 million in
first six months of operation [40].
In addition, unseen tunneling risks and broader unpredictable events expose both public
and private actors to unmanageable liabilities. The policy framework itself contributes
to failure with federal authorities offloading responsibility to states and governments pre-
ferring public transport subsidies over tolling.
This results toll roads as a politically
unsupported and socially regressive solutions. Equity inefficiencies, such as disproportion-
ate impacts on low-income users, further undermine their legitimacy. As Flyvbjerg stated
[10], PPP under-performance is less about technical delivery and more about persistent
financial structuring weaknesses. Even nominal successes like EastLink required repeated
capital injections from the main investors, showcasing that the PPP model, in its current
form, remains not suited for high-risk, user-paid infrastructure projects.
5.2
A Case Study of London Underground
This section examines PPP case for the London Underground project, which was one of
the United Kingdom’s most ambitious infrastructure rehabilitation programs. The London
Underground PPP involved two major consortia, Metronet and Tube Lines, for upgrading
and maintaining a vast network of aging underground assets [41]. Initiated in the early
2000s, the program was part of a broader strategy to modernize London’s transport infras-
tructure without full public sector funding. The initial value of the contracts was estimated
24
==PAGE:31==
at approximately 17 billion EUR over a 30-year term. However, unforeseen asset condi-
tions, undefined performance measures, and contractual ambiguities led to significant cost
overruns and disagreements. Metronet, responsible for two-thirds of the network, entered
administration in 2007, effectively collapsing the PPP structure and forcing Transport for
London (TfL) to reabsorb the contract scope. This case shows the risks of incomplete
contracts and inadequate risk allocation in large-scale PPP railway projects [42].
5.2.1
Project Overview
The London Underground PPP project was a large-scale initiative designed to modernize
and maintain the city’s extensive and aging metro infrastructure. Implemented under a
PPP model, it followed a long-term Design-Build-Finance-Maintain (DBFM) structure,
dividing the network into two major concessions managed by Metronet, covering nine lines
and Tube Lines managing 3 lines [43].
The private sector was tasked with upgrading rolling stock, signals, tracks, and stations,
while London Underground retained responsibility for train operations. The model aimed
to leverage private sector efficiencies and transfer long-term asset risk. However, the PPP
faced early criticism for complex contract structures, lack of transparency, and unrealistic
performance expectations. Metronet collapsed in 2007 due to cost overruns and misman-
agement, resulting in a 2 billion EUR public bailout and re-nationalization of its share of
the program [44].
The project naturally delivered some upgrades and job creation, and it introduced per-
formance benchmarking mechanisms. However, it is widely regarded as a cautionary case
study in PPP implementation. Regardless of the final outcomes concerning the potential
downfall of the London Underground project, it is clear that there is a problematic dy-
namic between the private sector and the public sector. This is largely due to the nature
of the contract that was initially signed and subsequently passed down to the involved
parties [42]. As shown in Figure 11, the map highlights the consortia and government
party engaged in the London Underground rehabilitation and restoration project.
The stakeholder map of the London Underground Metronet [39] PPP project illustrates
a structurally complex and risk-laden governance model, where financial and operational
responsibilities are heavily fragmented. Despite being framed as a PPP, the risk trans-
fer is asymmetrical — 95% of the debt is guaranteed by the Department for Transport
(DfT) [45], effectively socializing downside risk while privatizing potential upside through
the Metronet consortium. The reliance on multiple public entities (DfT, GLA, TfL, LUL)
introduces bureaucratic layering, diffusing accountability and creating decision-making in-
efficiencies. The presence of a PPP Arbiter to resolve pricing disputes further highlights
the contract’s incompleteness and the high likelihood of renegotiation. This structure,
rather than ensuring value for money, established moral hazard and weakened commercial
discipline.

This case study showcases the significance of well-developed governance, risk allocation,

The contractual design of the PPP was quite flawed. Technical specifications were quite
brief and ambiguous, Metronet’s deliverables were defined in only a few hundred words
[44]. This vagueness resulted conflict with the interpretations, ignoring enforcement and
contributing to recurring disagreements with London Underground Limited. In addition,
performance monitoring was as complex and vague as the contractual framework. Work-
ing with such contracts and KPI’s, alongside the complex governance structure and its

stakeholders, appears to be the catalyst for the cascade of issues that have emerged in this

Secondly, risk was not carefully transferred. Even though the PPP was designed to shift
financial and operational risks to the private sector, 95% of Metronet’s debt was guar-
anteed by the Department for Transport [45]. When Metronet failed, the public sector
absorbed the liabilities, resulting in public losses of approximately 410 million EUR.
Moreover, oversight was limited. Transport For London lacked real-time access to Metronet’s
financial and operational data, making it nearly impossible to intervene proactively. The
PPP Arbiter concluded that Metronet had not been working in an “economic and effi-
cient” manner [46]. Despite this, the public sector lacked effective contractual levers to

Finally, political dynamics further disabled the project, which is believed to be the core

between the Greater London Authority and central government.
By 2010, the PPP model was abandoned. Transport For London reacquired Tube Lines
and brought maintenance and capital expenditure back in-house.
The National Audit
Office and Parliamentary committees later concluded that the PPP had failed to deliver
value for money and left the public sector exposed to unmanageable financial risks [45].
In conclusion, the failure of the London Underground PPP reflects a convergence of sys-
temic issues: poorly specified contracts, conflicted governance, ineffective oversight and
asymmetric risk-sharing. The case offers a cautionary tale about PPPs in complex trans-
port systems. It shows the necessity of aligning incentives, enforcing transparency and
designing enforceable and balanced risk frameworks.

Another prominant theme relates to the structural financial implications of PPP models.

Theme 3: Time Sensitivity and Financial Incentives
The interviews also revealed a theme centered on time sensitivity as a core driver in PPP
project delivery. Unlike traditional public infrastructure models, where timelines are of-

incentive to minimize time-to-revenue.
The project manager explained that faster construction reduces financing costs and accel-

making a profit on it, making the interest lower.” This orientation often shifts decision-
making away from technical optimization toward delivery speed. This theme reinforces
how financing structures influence contractor behavior and is positioned on the left side
of the thematic map, closely related to the logic and financial efficiency of private sector.

National Road Directorate, which had to manage an inherited PPP contract. This con-
text proves how PPP implementation can be shaped by political and administrative shifts,
highlighting a governance-related risk.
The Austrian contractor Strabag was noted for offshoring construction activity to Ger-
many, a move perceived as economically rational but socially contentious. Danish stake-
holders expressed dissatisfaction, pointing to expectations that public infrastructure projects
should contribute to local economy. This case illustrates the tension between cost-saving
strategies and societal legitimacy, mapping onto the right side of the thematic map under
public value considerations.
To reduce long-term financial burdens, the Road Directorate decided to repay the full
construction cost immediately upon completion, converting the PPP into a maintenance-
only arrangement over time. This decision exemplifies hybrid financing strategies used
to mitigate interest burdens, reinforcing the interplay between financial engineering and
public accountability.

Discussion
The research has conducted an analysis of PPPs through a literature review, case studies,
and interviews. This discussion will try to connect together the insights gathered from
theoretical frameworks, real-world case studies, and empirical data to address the three
sub-questions and integrates them to answer the main one.
“To what extent do Public-Private Partnerships remain a viable model for financing and
delivering transport infrastructure projects in the 21st century?”
7.1
Summary of Key Findings
The literature review revealed that PPPs exhibit a complex and evolving nature, which
requires meticulous structuring to ensure equal risk allocation and long-term financial
sustainability. Although PPPs have facilitated substantial infrastructure investments and
technical advancements, their financial performance has frequently encountered challenges,
including cost overruns, inflated financing costs, unrealistic demand projections, lack of
transparency and socioeconomic inequities. A contributing factor to these financial chal-
lenges is the phenomenon of optimism bias, which has been widely documented in infras-
tructure projects, for instance the Expressway Yellow Line (Peru). Optimism bias often
leads to overly optimistic projections of passenger numbers, revenues and cost estimates,
artificially inflating the perceived value for money for the private sector. Such unrealistic
30
==PAGE:37==
forecasts can result in projects being approved and implemented based on expectations
that are not met, ultimately leading to financial difficulties and potential government
intervention. This highlights the importance of addressing optimism bias through more
meticulous and realistic projections to ensure the long-term viability and sustainability of
PPP projects.
The case studies discussed in the interviews displayed mixed results of the PPPs. The
Kliplev-Sønderborg highway in Denmark was completed two years earlier than planned
and within budget, delivering the promised benefits without public intervention. In con-
trast, the Arlandabanan rail project in Sweden encountered financial difficulties due to
overestimated passenger numbers, which required state intervention to stabilize opera-
tions. On the other hand, the two case studies that were investigated thoroughly, Airport
Link Tunnel in Australia and the London Underground PPP in the UK show how tricky
PPP projects can be. The Airport Link Tunnel started in 2008 with a budget of $3.4
billion but ended up costing $4.8 billion. It was finished on time but failed financially
because it relied too much on toll fees and had overly optimistic traffic predictions. Mean-
while, the London Underground PPP, worth 17 billion EUR, aimed to fix old subway lines
but faced major issues due to unclear contracts and poor risk management. Metronet, one
of the main companies involved, went bankrupt in 2007, costing taxpayers 2 billion EUR.

7.2
What Factors Influence the Success or Failure of PPPs?
One of the most significant drivers impacting PPP outcomes is financial structuring, also
known as Commercial Logic, particularly the nature of the revenue model. PPPs that
depend a lot on demand-based revenue, for instance toll roads or fare-based rail lines, are
especially vulnerable to failure when forecasts end up being overly optimistic. This pat-
tern of overestimated demand is well-documented in the literature, most notably by Bent
Flyvbjerg, who identifies “optimism bias” as a systematic flaw in infrastructure planning.
The Airport Link Tunnel in Australia serves as a clear example. It collapsed financially
due to traffic volumes falling far short of projections, leaving investors exposed and re-
quiring public sector intervention.
Such failures are not limited to Australia only.
The Expressway Yellow Line in Peru
and Taiwan High-Speed Rail, as mentioned in Literature Review, showed similar vulner-
abilities. These cases illustrate that misaligned financial expectations, which are often
driven by political pressures to justify choosing PPP as procurement model, can even af-
fect negatively technically sound projects. That is why perhaps many jurisdictions have
shifted toward availability-based models, where payments are linked to infrastructure up-
time and performance, rather than user demand. This shift reflects a recognition that
long-term stability depends on designing risk-sharing arrangements that the private sec-
tor is both willing and able to manage.
A fundamental driver of PPP performance is the clarity and quality of Contractual In-
centives and risk allocation between public and private partners.
PPPs are long-term
arrangements, 15-30 years, and are prone to unforeseen challenges, thus, contracts must
be detailed enough to allocate risk and define responsibilities, yet flexible enough to adapt.
The failure of the Metronet consortium in the London Underground PPP illustrates the
risks of vague, incomplete agreements. Responsibilities were described in overly broad
terms, performance targets were ambiguous, and enforceable oversight mechanisms were
weak. Transport for London lacked real-time access to financial and operational data,
limiting its ability to intervene. The result was cost overruns, inefficiencies, and a 2 bil-
lion EUR bailout. Similar contractual opacity and oversight gaps were seen in Mexico’s
Interoceanic Corridor project, where misalignment with public interest goals emerged.
Themes 1, 2, and 3 from Empirical Data directly address this question too by spot-
lighting how financial incentives, profit orientation, and time sensitivity can shape project
outcomes. For instance, Theme 1, Profit Orientation and Efficiency, suggests that
private sector involvement can enhance project performance through life-cycle cost reduc-
tion and design pragmatism. This supports the argument that Contractual Incentives and
Commercial Logic are key success factors, but only when aligned with public goals and
needs.
32
==PAGE:39==
The problem may arguably be that the concept of ”Value for Money” is frequently invoked
by governments, yet it remains poorly defined and inconsistently applied. It is often un-
clear whether VfM refers to financial returns on investment, post-construction economic
benefits to society or perhaps a combination of both.
A critical distinction exists be-
tween a project’s economic desirability and its VfM. while economic desirability assesses
whether a project is inherently worthwhile before any resources are committed, VfM con-
siders whether a project, once implemented, delivers sufficient benefits relative to its cost
- which is something that is required to perform as mentioned in Theoretical Background.
VfM typically extends to factors such as cost-efficiency, service quality and affordability,
measured against the social and economic outcomes produced. Despite policy assertions,
such as those from HM Treasury, that PPPs deliver VfM and broader economic gains for
taxpayers, the utilization of VfM remains ambiguous and demands clearer categorization,
particularly in relation to different types of infrastructure.
Overall, the success or failure of PPPs in transport infrastructure is not determined by
the model itself, but by how it is applied. Financial structuring, contractual clarity and
alignment with public values are all significant. Projects fail when demand forecasts are
unrealistic, contracts are vague and institutions are weak. On the other hand, PPPs works
when they are carefully designed, closely monitored and adapted to context. As govern-
ments increasingly shift toward smaller, availability-based and hybrid models, these lessons
must be documented to ensure future PPPs deliver both efficiency and public value.
7.3
How does institutional capacity affect project delivery under PPPs?
This question examines how institutional capacity, such as the administrative, legal, finan-
cial, and technical competence of public sector influences project delivery across its entire
life-cycle. PPP model is not about the public sector sitting back and delegating all the
work and responsibility to the private sector to complete the project. Instead, it is about
sharing project work and responsibility with the private sector to help execute the project
efficiently and incentivize profit-making. First, governments must be able to conduct rig-
orous project preparation and feasibility assessments.
This includes identifying public
needs, conducting VfM evaluations, structuring appropriate risk-sharing arrangements,
and aligning projects with broader fiscal and policy goals. Second, public authorities must
possess the legal and technical expertise required to draft and enforce complex long-term
contracts.
The absence of any of these capabilities introduces structural vulnerabilities. As men-
tioned in the literature review, governments need to designing flexible frameworks and
targeting support where private interest is lacking. This is how you attract private sector
and get the best from them.
The London Underground PPP, particularly the failure of the Metronet consortium, pro-
vides a cautionary case study. Although the UK had developed a PPP framework, men-
33
==PAGE:40==
tioned earlier in Theoretical Background for PPP Model, and created a PPP unit such as
the PPP Arbiter, critical weaknesses in institutional oversight were exposed. The contract
assigned extensive responsibility to Metronet, yet Transport for London lacked real-time
access to the consortium’s financial and operational data. Moreover, the vagueness of the
performance standards in the Metronet contract limited accountability. These gaps led to
cost overruns, delivery delays, and ultimately a 2 billion EUR public bailout. The failure
was not due to technical complexity but rather institutional incapacity to manage and
enforce the PPP structure.
On the other hand, the Kliplev–Sønderborg highway PPP in Denmark offers a mixed
example. Initially driven by local authorities seeking to bypass national resistance, the
project was later absorbed by the national Road Directorate after administrative restruc-
turing. While the project was delivered early and within budget, this governance transition
highlighted a different kind of institutional risk, fragmentation and instability. The shift in
ownership created uncertainty and blurred lines of accountability. Furthermore, interviews
revealed that the national authority treated the PPP more as a maintenance contract than
a fully leveraged public-private collaboration. This diluted the scope of innovation and
cost-sharing that PPPs are intended to offer. The case underscores that institutional con-
sistency is as important as institutional strength. The interviews conducted in this thesis
reinforced this point. Officials from the Danish Road Directorate described PPPs as a
form of “consumer loan” for the state, i.e. front-loading infrastructure delivery at the
cost of long-term repayment burdens. This analogy underscores the need for institutional
foresight: even technically efficient PPPs may fail in fiscal terms if governments lack the
capacity to manage contingent liabilities, future budget impacts, and contract renegotia-
tion scenarios.
Beyond technical and fiscal dimensions, institutional capacity includes the ability to man-
age political dynamics and coordinate across agencies. Political interference, inconsistent
priorities, or general conflict can derail even well-structured projects. This was evident in
the Kliplev–Sønderborg case, where initial regional enthusiasm for the PPP model clashed
with national-level skepticism and eventually led to a central takeover. While the outcome
was operationally successful, the political transition created discontinuities that could have
destabilized the project under different circumstances. Overall, Institutional capacity is
not merely one factor among many, it is the enabling infrastructure that successful PPP
model depends on.

7.4
Under what conditions do public-led or hybrid models outperform
PPPs?
One of the clearest advantages of public-led models is their ability to leverage public bank
credit. Governments can typically borrow at lower interest rates than private entities, es-
pecially in developed economies with strong credit ratings. In such contexts, PPPs often
function as expensive off-balance-sheet borrowing mechanisms, in spite of the ability to
mobilize additional funding for infrastructure and to spread project costs over a longer
period as stated in Literature Review. This cost differential can outweigh the expected
efficiency gains from private sector involvement.
The interviews conducted for this thesis reinforce this point as well, especially Themes
2 and 4. Officials from the Danish Road Directorate referred to PPPs as a form of “For-
brugsl˚an” (consumer loan) for the state, showcasing the long-term cost burden associated
with private capital. When governments have fiscal space and strong institutional control
over public procurement, direct public investment is often more financially responsible
than paying for private financing through PPP contracts.
As discussed earlier, PPPs require a high degree of institutional capacity and legal sta-
bility to function effectively over long contract periods, often 15 to 30 years. However,
most countries usually have a short political cycles, i.e. elections cycles, where regulatory
frameworks and ideologies could differ from the previous elected ones, therefore, public-led
or hybrid models are less risky in these situations.
Hybrid models provide flexibility to adapt contract terms without the legal and finan-
cial rigidity of full PPP structures. They also reduce exposure to costly renegotiations,
which are frequent in unstable environments. The London Underground PPP’s failure,
particularly in the Metronet case, shows how poor contract design combined with institu-
tional lack of capacity can lead to political blame-shifting and financial collapse. Public
control, known to be potentially slower or less innovative, tends to be more resilient in
such contexts. This is also supported in the Literature Review, where Dehornoy’s analysis
states that there is no evidence that PPPs are better value-for-money than public projects.
In straightforward infrastructure projects, for instance, standard highway resurfacing or
routine bridge maintenance, public-led models are often more efficient. The transaction
costs of PPPs including procurement, legal advisory and ongoing monitoring may exceed
any savings from private sector efficiency. Moreover, when the benefits of infrastructure
are intangible, such as in improving regional equity or environmental sustainability, quan-
tifying value-for-money through a PPP model becomes challenging for the public sector.

7.5
To what extent do Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs) remain a
viable model for financing and delivering transport infrastructure
projects in the 21st century?
Public-Private Partnerships remain viable, but only selectively, under specific conditions
and with readjusted expectations. Their continued relevance does not depend on their
broad applications, but in their adaptability to evolving fiscal, institutional and technical
contexts. The evidence, both historical and currently, suggests a shift from early-stage
optimism to cautious pragmatism.
First of all, the commercial viability of PPPs is critically linked to revenue models. The
failure of demand-based projects like Australia’s Airport Link Tunnel and Taiwan High
Speed Road showcase a systemic flaw. that is traffic projections are often inflated due
to optimism bias.
This can be driven by political imperatives.
Such failures are not
specific to few cases, they have occurred across different geographies and decades. As of
2025, many infrastructure/rail PPPs had been launched globally since the 1980s, but most
traffic-based rail concessions ended as financial failures, despite being technical successes.
Naturally, these failures exposed losses that public projects may have simply covered.
However, a gradual change is taking place. Availability-based models, where payments
are tied to infrastructure performance rather than user demand, have become widely pop-
ular, especially since the third wave of rail PPPs post-2006, as seen in Figure 2. These
contracts reduce private sector exposure to uncontrollable risks and better align public and
private incentives. The ongoing shift toward brownfield concessions, life-cycle renewals,
and integration into existing networks suggests that PPPs are maturing, not declining.
PPP implementation has historically followed cyclical patterns. The rail sector alone has
seen three distinct waves highlighted in the figure below:
Figure 14: Timeline for Revenue Models development for PPP model

While Europe has been scaling down PPP pipelines post-financial crisis, shown in Intro-
duction in Figure 1, early signs of a fourth wave are visible, that is prioritizing smaller,
brownfield, and renewal-based projects with refined contractual logic and improved public-
side innovation.
Second, the Metronet case in the London Underground PPP illustrates that even well-
funded, technically sound projects will collapse without competent public oversight. Con-
tractual vagueness, limited access to financial data and weak enforcement mechanisms
prevented the governmental institute of the UK to succeed. On the opposite, projects like
Kliplev–Sønderborg in Denmark, delivered on time and budget, show how institutional
consistency and governance experience can impact PPP potential.
Therefore, institu-
tional capacity is not a supportive condition, it is the enabling condition. Obviously, these
two projects aren’t comparable and railway projects tend to be more complex, but gov-
ernments must be able to define, monitor and enforce long-term contracts amid political
transitions and market fluctuations. The failure to do so translates directly into fiscal
liabilities and lost public value.
Finally, PPPs are not a cost-effective financing method when public bank credit offers
cheaper capital, particularly in fiscally stable nations. The public-led model often delivers
better value in low-complexity, low-risk projects (e.g. resurfacing highways), where trans-
action and monitoring costs of PPPs erode potential efficiency gains. Moreover, Hybrid
models increasingly offer a middle ground—combining public financing with private de-
livery. They preserve flexibility, reduce renegotiation risks and maintain strategic public
control, which is relevant for politically sensitive or rapidly evolving infrastructure de-
mands.
What is interesting is not the volume of PPP projects per say, but the level of sophisti-
cation of their design and governments have been learning from this. Hence, they shift
now to a lower-risk, technically integrated and fiscally transparent frameworks. In conclu-
sion, PPPs will continue to exist, however, their continued relevance depends on smarter
applications, not larger ones.

Conclusion and Future Works
8.1
Conclusion
Public-Private partnerships have been progressing into various waves and typologies since
the 1980s, where each wave revealing hard lessons through trial, error and monitoring
project. Early traffic-based concessions, especially in stand-alone rail and airport links,
proved to be financially unsuccessful, for one party at least, due to optimistic demand
forecasts and limited integration with existing environments. Later on, availability-based
PPPs gained popularity, offering greater predictability and aligning better with public
37
==PAGE:44==
sector capabilities.
Even though many PPPs have delivered infrastructure successfully and on-time, they
have often failed financially. One example mentioned is the Expressway Yellow Line, in
Peru, where tolls were raised eight times, yielding $23 million for the operator, while the
state lost $1.2 million due to contract mismanagement, and over a decade later, Lima’s
residents still lack proper access to infrastructure funded by public money [2]. Other cases
suffered from problems such as going over budget, unclear contract changes and unfair
sharing of risks between public and private partners. These are not rare mistakes, they
are natural to experience with PPP procurement model. Sometimes, PPP model allows
governments to spend money upfront without showing it on their balance sheets, while
pushing financial risks and costs into the future. This either can be a constraint for the
public or allow it to spend money today on other projects.
The empirical findings conclude that while private partners can deliver efficiency and
speed through profit motivation, this often comes at the cost of long-term affordability
and reduced flexibility. In the meanwhile, public sector struggle with contractual com-
plexity and inconsistent oversight, especially when institutional capacity is unstable or
politically bias. As Robert Bain quoted in his review paper:
”If you’re a good public sector, you shouldn’t need PPPs. If you’re bad, you shouldn’t
go near them.” [47]
Future PPP projects should be smaller, more context-aware and embedded in long-term
national planning frameworks. Institutional capacity, not contract complexity, will be the
enabler to determine whether PPPs can deliver the desired outcome and desired value for
the money. The lesson for countries considering PPPs is clear: If a public sector is capable,
it may not need a PPP. If it is not, it should avoid one. Yet, this does not mean that PPP
model is not a viable model for financing and delivering transport infrastructure projects
in the 21st century. Rather, it marks a shift in their role. The next wave of PPPs is ought
be smaller, transparent and tightly scoped. They should focus on brownfield concessions,
railway renewal and maintenance, not large-scale, demand-exposed greenfield projects.
This brings the analysis full circle to the initial question motivating this research: Den-
mark’s comparatively cautious approach to traditional PPPs. By holding on strong public
sector control through entities like Banedanmark and DSB and by carefully assessing the
true cost of private capital, the Danish approach fully acknowledges the great governance
challenges that comes with PPP model. Denmark’s model, therefore, should not be seen
as a simple rejection of private sector efficiency, but perhaps as a pragmatic and risk-averse
strategy, built on the hard-learned previous lessons.

8.2
Future Works and Limitations
To gain a more comprehensive understanding of PPP viability in transport infrastruc-
ture, future research should expand to wider range of case studies both geographically
and contractually. Inclusion of diverse project environments, especially in developing and
politically complex regions, may reveal new risk patterns and success factors. Quantitative
data access was lacking in the thesis due the extensive process to find such data. Future
work could benefit from closer collaboration with public sector and private contractors to
grasp a better understanding of the situation.
Structured interviews with stakeholders across multiple PPP phases; planning, execu-
tion and post-completion are recommended to capture the full picture. Broader sampling
across various PPP model typologies, for instance leasing and contract types would also
strengthen the credibility of findings.
As PPP models continue to evolve with financial innovation and shifting public policy
priorities, closer studies could reveal the long term impact on value for money, service
quality and accountability. These directions would enrich the empirical foundation for
decision-making in infrastructure financing.
