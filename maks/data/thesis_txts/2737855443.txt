Abstract
This thesis investigates the dynamic responses of individual floating offshore wind tur­
bines (FOWT) and an array of FOWTs with shared mooring systems, using both frequency­
domain and time­domain analyses. The study aims to understand how these coupled
floater­mooring systems behave under various environmental conditions and to determine
the implications for their design and operation. The frequency­domain analysis done by
Sauder [1], reveals distinct differences between the two mooring configurations. The sin­
gle mooring system exhibits a single peak in its power spectrum density for horizontal
motions, indicating only one dominant mode of motion, while the shared mooring system
displays multiple peaks corresponding to various eigenmode shapes, suggesting a more
complex dynamic response.
Time­domain simulations and analysis done by Hall et al. [2] disagree with these findings
and conclude that there is no significant difference in the dynamic response as the second­
order slow drift response of the FOWTs is low compared to wind­induced motion.
To understand the reason for these contradictory conclusions, methods used by both
research groups (frequency and time­domain analysis) were used in this thesis to analyse
the dynamic response of individual mooring systems and a 2­by­2 lattice shared mooring
system. The analysis revealed that the dynamic response of the shared mooring system
is significantly different from the individual mooring system. Each FOWT and their nodal
response contribute to the modal responses at different natural periods. This is evident
from the multiple peaks in the predicted power spectral density of the nodal response, by
both the frequency­domain and time­domain approaches.
The results of this work highlight the practical implications for the offshore wind industry,
where shared mooring systems offer potential cost savings despite their complex dynamic
responses. These findings underscore the necessity for careful design, enhanced mon­
itoring, and maintenance strategies to manage the additional dynamic complexities of
shared mooring systems.

Introduction
The rise in energy demand is a growing concern globally due to increased population,
economic development and technological advancements [3]. As such, there is a need to
develop reliable and sustainable energy sources. For decades we have relied on coal and
fossil fuels to meet the majority of our energy needs. However, due to their environmen­
tal impact and their non­renewable nature, there is a need to explore more renewable
and green options to meet the energy demands [4]. Moreover, the world saw an un­
precedented spike in energy prices due to the war between Russia and Ukraine, further
underscoring a need for stable and cost­effective energy sources as well [5]. To alle­
viate these problems, renewable energies such as wind, wave, solar, geothermal, and
hydrogen have been identified as crucial alternatives.
In recent years, offshore wind power has gained prominence in the global renewable
energy market. According to the Offshore Wind Market Report: 2023 Edition [6], the
global offshore wind pipeline had a growth of 69%, 60,746 MW to 102,529 MW, from
2022. Moreover, The Global Wind Energy Council have prospected 680 GW of newly
installed wind capacity globally between 2023 and 2027, to meet the UN energy needs
[7]. The most prominent ways to scale wind energy are to either increase the individual
turbine capacity or to deploy multiple turbines in a concentrated area with high potential
for energy generation [8].
Wind farms refer to a cluster of wind turbines, both on and offshore, that produce electricity.
These wind farms vary in size from a few turbines to hundreds, covering large areas. Of
the 830 GW produced by wind energy, 93% are onshore and the remaining 7% is offshore
[9]. That being said, there is significant interest and growth in offshore wind turbines due
to the abundance of space available in the ocean.
The bulk of operational offshore wind turbines are situated in shallow waters, typically
around 90 meters deep, utilizing bottom­fixed monopiles [10]. However, around 80% of
the world’s wind energy potential lies in deeper water regions, where depths exceed 60
meters [11]. Yet, deploying monopiles in these areas poses significant risks of failure. As
such, there is a growing interest in the development of Floating Offshore Wind Turbines
(FOWT) to harness this untapped potential.
As of 2023, four operational commercial floating offshore wind farms around the world: are
Hywind Scotland, WindFloat Atlantic, Hibiki Floating Offshore Wind Farm, and Kincardine
Offshore Wind Farm, totalling 176 MW. A 250 MW floating wind farm off the coast of
Brittany is being tendered by France [11] making it the largest wind farm project in Europe.
Despite the potential of floating wind turbines, their cost—at $ 145 USD/MWh—is nearly
1.5 times higher than that of bottom­fixed offshore wind turbines, which cost $ 95 US­
D/MWh. This significant price difference hinders the global expansion of floating wind
technology. This is mainly due to a component that bottom fixed turbines do not need,
the mooring system. In the 2022 Cost of Wind Energy Review by NREL, it was reported
that about 30% of the total cost of floating wind installation is attributed to the mooring
system [12]. Thus it is of interest to further explore novel mooring solutions that are cost­
saving.

Background
2.1
Mooring Systems
Mooring is the most commonly applied approach for the station keeping of floating offshore
structures, e.g. Floating Offshore Wind Turbine (FOWT). Restraining the floating structure
to a precise position and controlling its motion against wave, wind, and current is crucial
to ensure structure integrity and safe marine operation. This could be achieved with the
use of mooring. Floating offshore systems are made up of three main components: the
floating structure, cables, mooring lines and the anchor. Mooring lines are attached to
the floating structure on one end (fairleads), and anchor to the sea bed on the opposite
end. By carefully employing various mooring design strategies, this mooring line provides
restoring forces to prevent excessive motion of the floating structures. [13].
2.1.1
Floating Platforms
There are several types of floating platforms that are moored by mooring systems. The
four main types of floating platforms used for offshore wind turbines: Barge, Semi­submersible,
SPAR, and Tension Leg Platform (TLP), as illustrated in Figure 2.1 [14]. These platforms
differ in their hydro­static stability and mooring system characteristics.
Figure 2.1: Various floating platforms used for floating wind turbines [14].
2.1.2
Mooring Lines
Mooring lines vary in material and configuration. These mooring lines can also have
different segments with different materials providing different cross­sectional properties
and different costs. Some common materials are chains, synthetic fibre, and steel wire
ropes.
The selection of the floaters and mooring system is dependent on the location of de­
ployment and the geographical conditions present there. These systems can both be
temporary or permanent depending on the operation. A summary of the three main types
of mooring systems is presented below and illustrated in Figure 2.2.

Catenary Mooring
The catenary mooring system provides restoring forces due to the weight of the line, this
weight causes the line to have a catenary line profile where a significant part of the line
will lie on the sea bed. This system is the most commonly used, especially in shallow and
intermediate waters [15].
Taut Mooring
Unlike the catenary system, the taut system does not have any part of the line lying on
the sea bed. As the name suggests, the line is taut between the fairlead and the anchor.
As such the line uses less material as compared to the catenary system. This system
provides restoring forces due to its elasticity. The taut mooring system might be too stiff
in shallow water and thus performs better in very deep water [15].
Tendon Mooring
The tendon mooring system is a vertical mooring system that provides restoring forces
using large tensions in the lines due to the difference between the buoyancy and weight
of the floating platform. This system provides large resistance against heave motion. If
deployed with multiple lines, it can provide resistance for roll and pitch motions as well.
However, it does not provide much resistance in the planar directions (surge, sway) and
is thus best deployed in locations with large tidal waves [15].
2.1.3
Anchor
Anchors provide the final resistance needed to secure floating structures and mooring
lines to the seabed. The choice of anchors depends on the characteristics of the mooring
line, design requirements, and soil properties present in the deployment location. The
main types of anchors used in floating wind farms are suction anchors, pile anchors, plate
anchors, and gravity anchors [16].
2.1.4
Cost of Wind Farm Installation
The primary drawback of floating offshore wind farms is the escalating cost, especially
when these farms are installed in deeper waters. Deep water installations necessitate
longer mooring lines and more complex anchor installations, which significantly drive up
the overall costs [17]. The expense of mooring lines and anchors alone accounts for
approximately 30% of the total installation cost [12]. Consequently, there is considerable
research interest aimed at reducing installation costs by developing innovative mooring
systems.

2.1.5
Shared Mooring
Shared mooring is an innovative design concept aimed at reducing the construction costs
of floating wind farms. This system minimizes the number of mooring lines and anchors
required by either sharing anchors or connecting structures to adjacent ones. Conse­
quently, this approach decreases both installation costs and material usage. However,
shared mooring lines introduce coupling between the floating structures, adding complex­
ity to the system’s dynamic characteristics by introducing additional natural frequencies.
Wind and wave forces can excite the structures at these frequencies, potentially caus­
ing large responses that increase tension on the mooring lines and power cables, which
can lead to breakage. Therefore, a thorough understanding of the dynamic behaviour
and careful analysis of the system is essential to assess its feasibility. Several numerical
studies on the shared mooring concept for Floating Offshore Wind Turbines (FOWTs) will
be discussed in the next section.
2.2
Literature Review on Analysis of Shared Mooring
Systems
Various research has been done on shared mooring systems. Namely, static analysis,
frequency­domain analysis and finally time­domain analysis. This chapter will summarise
the methods and the conclusions drawn from each of these analyses.
2.2.1
Static Analysis
Liang et al. [18] conducted a static analysis on a dual spar (the NREL 5MW wind turbine
on the OC3 spar floating system) shared mooring system and different mooring properties
were investigated. Irvine’s method [19] is used to model the shared mooring line by using
static positions obtained from static analysis in SIMA. Their results showed that the shared
mooring lines significantly affects the restoring force when there is an offset to the FOWT.
Connolly and Hall [20] conducted a quasi­static analysis on a pilot­scale floating wind
farm using the NREL 5MW offshore reference wind turbine. Both frequency and time­
domain analyses were conducted on various mooring configurations of 4 FOWTs in a 2­
by­2 square array as shown in Figure 2.3 [20] and compared to a based­line single FOWT
set­up. An 800 kN static force was applied at the nacelle position to approximate the wind
loads at rated wind speed. The results show that a wind direction of 45◦represents as
the worst scenario with maximum displacement and line tension. They also show that
significant cost savings are possible at water depths 400 m and above. At these depths,
the cost of individual mooring lines attached to the seabed is much more than that of
shared lines. The design analysis shows that square 3 (see Figure 2.3) has the least line
tension. However, slow­drift forces have been neglected in this study which may have a
significant impact.

2.2.2
Dynamic Analysis
Gözcü et al [21] conducted a shared mooring analysis using HAWC2Farm [22] on two
15 MW floating wind turbines on spar floaters with both shared mooring lines and shared
anchors. They evaluated the mooring line design and the design parameters that affect
the eigenmodes and natural frequency. The dynamic analysis was done under steady
wind and regular wave load conditions.
Their results concluded that the natural frequencies for the shared mooring line design
with heavy chain mooring can be designed to be similar to the single FOWT by reducing
the length of the shared line. However, since it is in shallower waters (200 m), even at tight
lengths where the mooring forces are already significantly higher than the single FOWT,
the shared lines tend to touch the sea bed. For the shared anchor design wiht polyester
mooring lines, where wind and wave loads were simulated, the loading on the upstream
mooring line was 30% higher than the single FOWT counterpart and also twice as much
surge motion as both the downstream FOWT and an individual FOWT.
Dynamic time­domain analysis of a row arrangement of FOWT farms was done by Gold­
schmidt and Muskulus [23]. The station­keeping performance of the wind farm was as­
sessed in the presence of important forces such as irregular wave force, estimated wave
drift, and wind forces. Forces due to current were excluded in this study. Moreover,
diffraction effects were estimated using the McCamy­Fuchs theory. Newman’s approxi­
mation was used to determine mean­drift and slowly­varying forces. Designs with 1, 5,
and 10 floaters were considered in there study.
The results in [23] showed that increasing the number of coupled FOWTs by shared moor­
ing lines increases the horizontal displacement. Moreover, the first mooring line in the
force direction transfers the largest force to its anchor while the last FOWT in the chain
moves relatively close to its anchor. This makes the realization of power cables difficult.
This behaviour is attributed to the nonlinear relationship between the mooring line dis­
placement and stiffness. Increasing the distance between the fairlead and anchor point
results in a much higher restoring force as compared to decreasing the distance, which
occurs in the last FOWT. As such, the last FOWT experiences a smaller force against the
environmental load thus experiencing large displacements. When observing the power
spectral density (PSD) curve of the displacement, there are peaks in the first­order wave
period range. However, no resonant period is observed. Although, for shared set­up, the

Liang [24] conducted dynamic modelling and hydrodynamic model tests on two spar buoys
with shared mooring lines. The dynamic time­domain simulations were conducted in SIMA
with the coupled SIMO (motion simulator for the floater) and RIFLEX (finite­element solver
for slender bodies such as mooring) model. Both aerodynamic load and hydrodynamic
loads were considered for this simulation study. Drag force of both the floater and the
mooring lines were included as well. Three shared mooring configurations were consid­
ered in the study:
1. Standard shared line,
2. Shared lines connected to a tethered buoy,
3. Both shared and individual lines are connected to a buoy.
The hydrodynamic performance of the designs was tested under various loading condi­
tions for configurations 1 and 2. Decay tests, regular, and irregular wave tests were con­
ducted and compared to results from the numerical study. A clumped mass was added
in the connection between the shared line and its effects were also tested.
Results for the time­domain simulations showed that larger motion ranges, dynamic mo­
tion and higher tension in single lines are present in the shared mooring set­up as com­
pared to the single FOWT. The shared set­up also showed higher dynamic tensions along
with higher snap loads on the mooring line. Adding the tethered connection (configura­
tion 3), although increased the mean displacement and dynamic motions, was shown to
reduce both the snap load and dynamic tension on the shared line.
Results from the hydrodynamics model tests agree with the findings in the numerical
simulation, also presented in [24]. Moreover, the addition of a clumped mass between
the shared line reduces the natural period and the dynamic motion of the system. The
clumped mass was also shown to reduce snap loads and dynamic tension, although pre­
tension is increased.
Research was done in the National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) by Hall et
al. [17] to optimize the layout of 10 FOWTs using quasi­static and dynamic analysis.
Firstly, the array layout was decided using a linear model to approximate the mooring
line stiffness. The final design was selected based on cost and the extreme value of hor­
izontal offset under loaded conditions (the wind load is approximated as a 1.5 MN static
force at nacelle) at various angles. Array layouts that result in rounder ”watch circles” were
preferred due to their omni­directional station­keeping, which meant that the mooring sys­
tem was efficiently used. After selecting an array design, mooring sizing at a component
level was conducted. This includes the selection of mooring line material, shared mooring
line configuration, and anchor mooring line configurations. This was done using a nonlin­
ear quasi­static model developed for this project known as Moorpy [25]. All the mooring
lines were designed to adhere to constraints such as maximum line tension, maximum
displacement, minimum sag of line and line stiffness. To create a final optimized design,
an iterative method was employed that combines the linear array level and nonlinear com­
ponent level design optimization. The steps to optimization are shown below:
1. Linear array optimization is performed based on assumed mooring properties.
2. Optimized properties are then used as target values for a nonlinear mooring opti­
mizer that considers constraints given by the user.

3. Nonlinear optimization outputs are then linearized and fed back into the linear opti­
mizer, initiating a new iteration.
4. This iterative process continues until the mooring properties and assumptions con­
verge, yielding an optimal, cost­effective mooring system design.
Dynamic time­domain analysis of the optimized design was done to assess its station­
keeping capabilities. The array was subjected to various wind and wave loading cases
(calm, severe, and 50­year storm) using MoorDyn [26], OpenFAST, and FAST.farm [27].
OpenFAST is used to model kinematics of wave and wind loading, FAST.farm is used
for array­level floater motion and MoorDyn is used to simulate the mooring lines of the
system. These three models were coupled to produce the full time­domain simulation of
the selected wind farm. Loading conditions were tested at 45◦wind and wave direction.
Results showed that the best design with the considered constraints was the novel array
design shown in Figure 2.4 [17]. The mooring lines used were polyester taut lines for both
anchor and shared lines. The shared line also had ballast to keep the line at the same sea
level throughout, for ease of line stiffness optimization and low cost of implementation.
time­domain analysis in [17] also showed that the maximum line tensions were around
20 −30% of the maximum breakage load, for all load conditions. More importantly, the
array design showed no problematic resonance behaviour between FOWTs. Second­
order wave loads induced slow­drift motions were considerably lower than the motion
due to wind loads, and thus were considered negligible.

2.2.3
frequency­domain Analysis
Sauder [1] has carried out a frequency­domain analysis specifically on nonlinear wave
loads for a 2­by­2 lattice array as shown in Figure 2.5 [1]. The FOWT considered in this
study was the INO WINDMOOR 12MW design [28]. In this analysis, a load of 1.5 MN
approximating wind loading at a 45◦angle was added to each FOWT in the array, and the
eigenmodes and eigenvalues were obtained by analyzing the coupled motion equations.
The coupled stiffnesses in the motion equations are obtained by a nonlinear static analysis
of the mooring system. The damping term in the equations of motion was approximated
using a Rayleigh damping model. It is important to note that for slow­drift motions, vis­

cous drag­induced damping plays a crucial role. This type of damping is inherently nonlin­
ear. Therefore, to apply the linear Rayleigh damping model effectively, calibration against
model tests or more advanced numerical models is required. The authors note that this
damping model might not be suitable for shared mooring systems. Second­order Wave
loads were obtained using the difference­frequency quadratic transfer function (QTF) for
the given FOWT at each degree of freedom (dof). In this model, the FOWT was consid­
ered as axis­symmetric such that the QTF can be used for all directions of analysis. The
equation for slowly varying forces was derived using deep water linear dispersion theory.
The nodal response and forces were then converted to the modal space by projecting the
response and the loads using the eigenvectors obtained earlier. Moreover, Sauder [1]
also compared the accuracy of Newman’s approximation for the QTF with the full QTF
method.
The results of the analysis in [1] revealed that resonance is observed in modal responses
at difference frequencies that match the eigenfrequencies of the modes. The modal load
spectra show that the system is loaded over all frequencies. However, modes that are or­
thogonal to the load direction have significantly lesser modal load. The modal response
is high at the natural frequencies and tends to zero as the frequency increases. The
Rayleigh damping approximation was not based on any physical attribute of the FOWTs
and thus may introduce uncertainties in the result as resonant responses may be signif­
icant reduced if damping is high enough. The author also states that linear wave loads
that were not included in this study are likely to produce a substantial modal load and
response at frequencies near the peak frequency of the wave energy spectrum. [1] also
noted that using Newman’s Approximation for the QTF can underestimate the modal loads
and response by 20% at the frequencies relevant to the lattice system studied. Thus, it
was suggested in that Newman’s approach should be avoided even though it is more
computationally efficient.

2.2.4
Conclusion and Motivation
From the previous discussion, it is clear that shared mooring saves considerable cost but
with it may introduce complex dynamic phenomena that must be studied to assess its fea­
sibility. Studies in static, dynamic and frequency­domain have brought significant insight
into the station­keeping capabilities of various design and loading conditions. What is par­
ticularly interesting, however, is that the time­domain analysis presented in the NREL re­
port by Hall et al. [17] states that no additional challenges or resonance phenomena occur
for shared mooring compared to the individual mooring system. However, the frequency­
domain analysis conducted by Sauder [1] concludes that there is a significant difference in
the resonance behaviour between the single and shared mooring systems. However, as
noted by Sauder [1], there are uncertainties associated with the Rayleigh damping used
in the study. These uncertainties necessitate calibration using model tests or more so­
phisticated numerical models. Both papers employ different analysis methods and reach
different conclusions. The reasons for these discrepancies are not well understood.
2.2.5
Objectives of this thesis
This thesis aims to bridge the gap between the research of Sauder [1] and Hall et al. [2]
by applying both frequency­domain and time­domain analyses to the FOWTs with shared
mooring that were proposed by Hall et al. [2]. The FOWT model used in this thesis will be
the DTU 10 MW Reference Wind Turbine [29] with a spar buoy floater. The objectives of
this thesis are summarized as follows:
• Utilize the quasi­static solver MoorPy [25] to obtain static parameters.
• Employ difference­frequency QTF for the frequency­domain analysis specifically for
slow­drift surge and sway motions.
• Analyse frequency­domain response and power spectral density of response.
• Perform a time­domain simulation and analysis on the same set­up using the cou­
pled SIMO­RIFLEX solver on SIMA.
• Compare the results from the frequency­domain analysis and the time­domain sim­
ulation for validation purposes.
• Address the Discrepancies between the findings of Sauder [1] and Hall et al. [17].

2.3.2
Modelling
Linear­Spring Modelling
The approximation of mooring lines as linear springs is the simplest method. This method
models the mooring lines as a simple spring system where the restoring force, Fr, is given
by Fr = −ksx, where ks is the stiffness coefficient of the spring and x is the displacement
of the spring. Eigenmode and eigenfrequencies of this simple system can be obtained
by applying two degrees of freedom (dof) spring motion equation [30]. Here, coupled
equations of motion matrices are built using force balancing of simple spring systems by
assuming a small displacement to each mass/floater while the rest are held in place. The
stiffness of the lateral lines connected to the mass can be approximated using the pre­
tension, T0, of the line. The lateral stiffness, kl = T0

line length. Although this method is very fast and can provide information on the static
properties of the shared mooring system, it does not include nonlinear static effects or
dynamic effects. Forces that cause large deformation cannot be modelled accurately by
this method.

Quasi­Static Model
Quasi­static model of mooring lines is based on the mooring line shape and tension from
the catenary formulations. The model assumes that for a constant load, the mooring line
has a uniform linear motion between two positions across each time step. This approach
neglects dynamic effects on the motion such as inertia and damping forces. Quasi­static
models are fast and can provide an accurate estimation of responses in the frequency­
domain. However, under extreme load conditions, the quasi­static model can have high
inaccuracies. The quasi­static model is widely used for mooring configuration optimisation
[31]. Free open­source software, such as Moorpy [25], is very versatile at modelling both
individual and shared mooring systems.
Dynamic Model
In the dynamic model, time­varying effects of the mooring line due to inertia and damping
are captured by calculating the motion of the fairlead points. This is done by considering
the mooring line as multiple discrete elements that take into account the inertia and drag
forces acting along its length. These are many methods that can be used, among which
the clumped­mass method and Finite Element Method (FEM) have been implemented in
the state­of­the­art mooring dynamic solvers. In the clumped­mass method, the mooring
line is represented by a series of node points with concentrated weights, connected by
segments that possess both stiffness and internal damping properties. FEM method uses
governing equations with an integration of the mass over the entire element. This results
in a more accurate representation of the dynamic properties of the mooring line [31]. As
mentioned previously, the FEM method will be employed to solve the dynamic behaviour
of the mooring lines in RIFLEX.
The dynamic analysis method is the most accurate as it captures all the dynamic effects
present in the system including snap loads and sea bed effect. However, this comes with
a high computational cost as compared to the other methods mentioned in this section.
Conclusion
This study will utilize various methods, with a particular focus on the quasi­static and dy­
namic time­domain approaches. The quasi­static approach will primarily be used for con­
ducting modal analysis under loaded and unloaded conditions, with subsequent frequency­
domain analysis using its outcomes. Additionally, dynamic time­domain simulations will
be performed on a shared mooring setup similar to that employed in the quasi­static anal­
ysis. Comparative analysis of the two modelling methods will be conducted to discern
differences and implications.

Methodology
4.1
Modal Analysis
Modal analysis is a technique used to study dynamic characteristics, such as eigenmodes,
and natural frequencies, by decomposing its response into a series of vibration modes
known as modal shapes. Each mode represents a pattern of motion at a specific natural
frequency given the system characteristics.
By understanding the modal properties of a structure, engineers can predict how the struc­
ture will respond to various types of loads or dynamic inputs. As said before, only surge
and sway directions are considered in this thesis (2 dof). As such for 4 floaters this is
a total of 8 dofs, and thus would produce 8 different mode shapes and 8 corresponding
natural periods.
The NREL­optimized set­up presented in Hall et al. [17] has included ballasts to lower
costs, for ease of tension adjustment, and to have an even line level. However, these
ballasts are not included in the modal analysis study as the details of the optimized lo­
cation of the ballast are not available in the NREL report, thus to have the spar buoys at
the initial location at static equilibrium will require an optimization algorithm. Moreover,
MoorPy [25] identifies these ballasts as an additional mass with additional dof, meaning
each spar buoy will be coupled with a ballast and given additional eigenmodes that are
not significant. Since the mass of these ballasts is significantly lower as compared to the
spar buoy the analysis is simplified by not including these ballasts. The bridle lines were
also omitted as their primary role is to reduce yaw motion which is not considered in this
study. Furthermore, the addition of the bridle line introduces a massless connection node
that further complicates the formulation of the frequency­domain solution.

4.14
Time­Domain Simulation Set­Up
Time­domain simulations provide a more robust and detailed approach for modelling float­
ing offshore wind turbines (FOWTs) in various sea states. Unlike the quasi­static solu­
tions and frequency­domain analysis, which simplifies variables and nonlinear effects for
ease of implementation, time­domain simulations accurately capture the complex, non­
linear, and dynamic interactions between the floating structure, mooring line and the ma­
rine environment. This leads to more precise assessments of the performance, safety,
and longevity of FOWTs under realistic operating conditions. However, these simulations
come with significantly higher computational costs.
For the time­domain analysis of both single and 4 FOWT set­ups, a SIMA solver cou­
pling SIMO [38] and RIFLEX [39] is used. SIMO simulates the rigid body (spar buoy)
and RIFLEX simulate the mooring lines using the finite element method (FEM). Damping
from the mooring lines have not been considered in this study for comparison with the
frequency­domain solution.
To set­up the model in SIMA, firstly, the hydrodynamic information about the spar buoy
obtained from HydroD must first be imported. This includes the full QTF for a 45◦angle,
structural mass, hydrostatic stiffness, first­order motion transfer function, second­order
wave force transfer function and radiation data (added mass and wave­radiation damp­
ing). These data were provided by the main supervisor of this thesis, calculated in the
DNV software HydroD/Wadam. Next, the environment must be set up. For this case, it
would be the irregular waves described by the JONSWAP spectrum with Hs = 7 m and
Tp = 10 s with waves coming in at a 45◦angle.
4.14.1
Individual FOWT Set­Up
The individual FOWT simulation is done in the time­domain to compare with the frequency­
domain solution and to the shared mooring system. The setup for the time­domain simu­
lation is kept the same as the quasi­static method.
Supernodes representing the fairlead points and the anchor points are created. To rep­
resent the rigid body for the coupling, 2 dummy nodes are made, one MASTER node for
the main body and a SLAVE node to represent a rigid body. The fairlead nodes are set
to be SLAVE to the main MASTER node so that they move with the spar buoy.
The 4 anchor nodes are set to be fixed and they have both initial location and prescribed
location. The initial position of the anchor nodes is such that the mooring lines between
the fairlead and anchor are at their unstretched length. The final location is where the
anchor is attached to the seafloor at static equilibrium where pre­tension is applied.
Properties of the mooring line cross­section are taken from MoorPy’s MoorProps library
as shown in Table 3.1. 2 types of lines are created, one for the 4 anchor lines and one
for the dummy line. The dummy line is set as the slender system connection that couples
the SIMO [38] simulation of the spar buoy body with the RIFLEX [39] simulation of the
mooring lines.

5.7.3
Discussions on Time Domain Analysis
Time domain simulation was done for a individual FOWT and for the 4 FOWT shared
mooring system. The set­up of the time domain simulation was designed to closely match
that of the frequency domain analysis for comparison in terms of location and line tensions
at equilibrium.
The impact of slow­drift motions is not obvious while analysing the time series displace­
ment results. However, doing a spectral analysis revealed multiple peaks at the low­
frequency (LF) range. The results from the time domain match closely with the findings of
the frequency domain analysis. Although the peaks of the Power Spectral Density (PSD)
differ slightly between the time and frequency domain results, the standard deviation (σx)
results indicate a close match between the time domain simulations and the frequency
domain results for the individual and shared mooring setups. The difference in peaks is
likely because the response is narrow­banded and thus sensitive to the duration of the
simulation. The latter is due to the fact that the sampling frequency of the spectral analysis
based on Fourier transformation increases with the duration of the time series. Compared
to the peak value of the PSD curves, the standard deviation is less sensitive to the duration
of the simulation and thus provides a more reliable indication of the correlation between
frequency and time domain results.
The natural frequencies from the time domain results are slightly higher than those from
the frequency domain results for both setups, suggesting that the time domain system is
stiffer. This is due to the difference in the method to solve mooring dynamics between the
simplified catenary equation used in the quasi­static solver and the more complex finite
element method used in SIMA.
In conclusion, the results of the frequency domain solution match closely with the time
domain results and thus validate the finding of the theoretical frequency domain analysis.
The frequency domain analysis may be used to provide a much faster and reasonably
accurate approximation of the actual dynamics of the system during the design phase. It
is also important to note that the results were obtained without any pitch or roll motion. The
coupling between pitch and heave, as well as roll and sway, will introduce new modes and
responses that were not studied in this thesis due to limited time. However, the procedure
presented in this thesis can be extended to include other degrees of freedom.
5.8
Final Discussions
The frequency domain analysis, as have been employed by Sauder [1], highlights distinct
dynamic responses of the shared mooring system compared to the individual floater and
mooring system. This can be observed when comparing the power spectrum density of
the individual floater response, or nodal response, for both shared and individual mooring
systems as shown in Figure 5.27 and Figure 5.32. The individual FOWT mooring system
has a single peak at the natural period. In contrast, the shared mooring system has
multiple peaks corresponding to multiple eigenmode shapes shown in Figure 5.2. modal
excitation­load and modal response spectra also reveal the most important modes that
are likely to be excited at their natural frequency. This conclusion aligns with the findings
of Sauder [1] paper on shared mooring systems.
The present time domain simulations in SIMA, as utilized in the method outlined by Hall
et al. [17] also closely match the result of the frequency domain analysis as shown in
Figure 5.46 (individual) and Figure 5.51 (shared). The power density spectrum obtained
from the time series results also reveals multiple peaks in the shared mooring system,
corresponding to different modes. This shows that although the methods used are differ­

ent they both conclude that there are significantly different dynamic responses between
an individual moored system and a shared mooring system. The second­order slow­drift

The presence of multiple modes highlights the need for more robust design considerations

Conclusions and Future Perspectives
6.1
Conclusions
The primary aim of this thesis was to investigate the dynamic responses of floating off­
shore wind farms with shared mooring systems to address the discrepancy between the
findings of Sauder [1] from SintefOcean and Hall et al. [17] from National Renewable
Energy Laboratory (NREL). The former’s study concluded that there is a significant differ­
ence in the dynamic response of the shared mooring system compared to the traditional
individual mooring system, and the latter’s study concluded that there is no significant
difference.
To identify the source of the differing conclusions, a 2 by 2 lattice shared mooring system
was designed based on NREL’s specification [17] with some simplifications. These sim­
plifications include not including clumped mass and bridle lines. Both frequency domain
analysis, used by Sauder [1], and time domain analysis, used by Hall et al. [17], were
conducted.
Frequency domain analysis was done for the surge and sway directions of the floating off­
shore wind turbines with spar buoy floaters. Dimensions of the floater were modelled after
the design specifications in [17]. This includes the draft, centre of mass and submerged
volume. Strip theory was used to obtain the added mass of the Spar buoy. For this, the
spar buoy was approximated as a cylinder. The error between the actual added mass
and the one obtained from WADAM was 7%. This was deemed an acceptable margin of
error for ease of implementation.
Then, the quasi­static solver MoorPy [25] was used to obtain the static stiffness matrix
and final offset positions of both the individual FOWT and shared mooring system under
a wind load of 1.5 MN. The QTF of the spar buoy was obtained from HydroD and was
used to calculate slowly varying forces in the system. In [1] it was highlighted that Rayleigh
damping approximation could potentially result in errors in the system. As such stochastic
linearization was used as a close approximation to the actual damping. The drag coef­
ficient was found to greatly affect the response of the system. The drag coefficient was
approximated using the method highlighted in DNV recommended practices [37]. Mooring
line damping was not considered in this study.
Nodal analysis and Modal analysis were both conducted and the results revealed that the
shared system indeed presented a unique dynamic response with multiple peaks corre­
sponding to multiple eigenfrequencies and mode shapes. This is in contrast to individual
mooring systems that only have one peak response at the natural frequency. This result
is in agreement with the findings by Sauder [1]. The modal analysis also revealed the
modal shapes associated with higher modal excitation­loads, highlighting the important
mode shapes. A detailed study on the nodal response revealed how each mode shape
would affect the shared and anchor lines.
The comparison between Newman’s Approximation and Full QTF revealed that while
Newman’s Approximation tends to underestimate the loads, it also predicts lower damp­
ing characteristics. Consequently, this leads to higher responses at lower frequencies but
a lower response at higher frequencies.
The time domain analysis was conducted on SIMA using a SIMO­RIFLEX coupled solver.
The static configuration of the time domain set­up was kept as close as possible to the one
Dynamic Responses of Floating Offshore Wind Farms with Shared Mooring Systems
77
==PAGE:86==
used in the frequency domain analysis. This includes the initial and final positions after
wind force, tension on the mooring lines and mooring line properties. Only surge, sway
and heave were simulated for comparison between frequency and time domain results.
The coupling effect on surge and sway due to pitch and roll was not considered in this
analysis. The yaw motion was omitted as bridle lines were not modelled.
The full Quadratic Transfer Function (QTF) for 45° was utilized to predict the slow­drift
forces on the floaters caused by second­order wave loads.
The linear damping was
adopted from the stochastic linearization. The simulation results were compared to the
frequency domain solution, revealing a close match between the two. This validates the
frequency domain solution and confirms the presence of multiple nodal peaks for shared
mooring systems at different frequencies corresponding to the eigenfrequencies predicted
in the frequency domain simulation as compared to the individual mooring that only has
one peak.
These result further emphasises the fact that the dynamic response of the shared system
is significantly different from the individual mooring system highlighting the need for a
more robust design consideration.
6.2
Limitations
The standard deviation of FOWT responses is around 1 m, which is lower than the motion
due to wind loads. However, at extreme conditions when wind loads are lower due to pitch
control of the wind turbine, wave loads dominate and might contribute to large slow­drift
motions.
The method chosen for predicting damping due to drag on the spar buoy has a signifi­
cant impact on the results. In this thesis, Cd was chosen based on DNV recommended
practises [37]. Although the uncertainty of the selection of the drag coefficient is reduced,
the actual choice of drag is more complex and is based on the KC number and Reynolds
number which changes along the spar buoy. The coupled response and the presence of
the free surface further complicate the choice of Cd. Damping due to mooring line drag
also has a large impact on the response of the system which was also neglected. Detailed
computational fluid dynamic or model tests have to be conducted to obtain an accurate
Cd and other damping effects. However, this is not within the scope of this thesis and
the drag due to mooring lines were not included in both the time domain and frequency
domain analysis.
The model of the shared mooring system was simplified by removing the bridles and
clump mass ballast due to the complexity of implementation. MoorPy was not able to
give a coupled stiffness matrix between FOWTs, rather it was between each free node
(the clumped mass and the massless connection point for the bridle lines). To tackle this
issue a linearized stiffness matrix subroutine was developed. However, to identify the
location of the connection point of bridle lines, pre­tension required at each segment, and
the locations of clumped masses have to be solved using a more complex optimisation
technique similar to the one done by NREL [2]. This is a much more complex task and is
not included in the scope of this thesis.
The coupling between surge and pitch, as well as sway and roll, will also introduce new
eigenmode shapes and frequencies that will introduce added complexity to the dynamic
response of the shared mooring system. The project scope focuses on the slow­drift
motion in the surge and sway directions only.

6.3
Future Work
This thesis primarily focuses on the surge and sway slow­drift motions and the associated
modal responses. Investigating the effects of other dof and the coupling between them will
be of interest and give a more complete understanding of the unique dynamic properties
of shared mooring systems.
A linearised stiffness subroutine has already been programmed to find the coupled stiff­
ness of shared mooring systems with ballasts and massless points but has not been used
in this thesis due to difficulties in optimising pretension in shared moored structures. If an
optimization tool is developed, this subroutine will assist in frequency domain analysis of
a variety of mooring designs quickly.
The frequency domain nodal responses of the floaters in shared mooring systems have
been identified and analysed. However, further work can be done to understand the ten­
sion caused by these responses. A tension response, in terms of Power Spectral Density
(PSD) function, based on the relative motions of the floaters can be derived and analysed
to understand potential failure modes.
The wind loads have indeed been simplified as a constant force for the purposes of this
thesis. However, in reality, wind loads are dynamic and vary significantly with time due to
changes in wind speed and direction. To accurately capture the effects of wind loads, it
is essential to consider the stochastic nature of wind. This involves using wind spectra,
which describes how wind energy is distributed across different frequencies.
The inclusion of a more accurate damping coefficient, especially by accounting for moor­
ing line damping and wave­drift damping, will significantly improve the reliability of both
time domain and frequency domain analyses. This crucial damping information can be
obtained through detailed Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulations or dedicated
physical model tests.
The validation of results from the frequency domain and time domain using model tests
will further assist in ensuring the accuracy and reliability of the analysis.
Full CFD simulation which includes rigid­body dynamics, aerodynamics and hydrodynam­
ics with proper turbulence modelling will provide a complete understanding of the dynamic
response of the shared mooring system.
