Preface 
This master Thesis is a result of study performed at the Department of Environmental and 
Resource Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark. The project corresponds to 30 ECTS 
points. 
The study was conducted from October 2021 to March 2022 at the International Centre for Indoor 
Environment and Energy (ICIEE). The study focuses at the feasibility of using CO2 concentration 
for prediction of risk of airborne transmission indoors, and the influence of ventilation flow rate, air 
distribution and indoor layout on infection probability of the exposed manikin.

Abstract 
Since the end of 2019, COVID-19 has rapidly disrupted the world, infecting about 270 million 
people, and killed 5.3 million (in updating) so far. It has caused a huge devastation to human health 
and the world's economy. In order to prevent people from gathering in public places and causing 
serious cross-infection by asymptomatic virus-infected people, many countries have introduced 
corresponding policies to limit the density of people in some indoor places. But, in fact, people 
always live and work in an indoor environment with a certain density of people. Cross-infection at 
this time may have unpredictable consequences. To minimize the chance of airborne cross-infection 
in the indoor environment during this special period, the World Health Organization has updated the 
requirements for indoor ventilation systems, recognizing that indoor ventilation systems play an 
extremely important role in cross-infection. Once an asymptomatic case occurs indoors, other 
people in the same room are at risk of being infected. The indoor ventilation system will have a 
huge impact on this transmission process. The currently most popular ventilation strategy adopts 
demand control and uses the carbon dioxide (CO2) concentration measured in the exhaust room air 
The CO2 concentration is considered as an indicator to adjust the amount of clean outdoor air 
entering the room in order to keep the CO2 level in the occupied zone within the standard 
requirements. However, when there is a new source of transmission of coronavirus indoors, due to 
the limitation of sensors, the ventilation system cannot detect the virus that it spreads and cannot 
effectively suppress the possibility of indoor infection. At the same time, the installation of detection 
sensors for the new coronavirus in the ventilation system is severely limited by cost and technology, 
so the feasibility is extremely low. Therefore, it has become the focus to determine whether it is 
possible to use CO2 monitoring indoors and use the data to predict the risk of airborne cross-
infection, which is a significant part in cross-infection containing direct physical contact, indirect 
contact as well. And another purpose is to control the ventilation system’s supply flow rate based 
on the probability of infection. In this way, there is a chance to reduce the probability of indoor 
airborne cross-infection. 
The objective of this project is to validate or reject airborne cross-contamination risk prediction 
methods based on CO2 concentration measurements and to assess how much influence airflow rate, 
air distribution and room layout would have on infection probability. To achieve this objective, full-
scale experiments were conducted in the ICIEE Field Lab 2, which was arrange as a two-person 
office. The laboratory was ventilated by mixed air distribution. Supply air rates were kept at 20 L/s, 
40 L/s and 60 L/s, based on standard EN-16798-1 to provide clean supply airflow rate per person. 
Two human size breathing thermal manikins simulated infected and exposed seated persons in the 
office. The manikins generated heat as an average seated person. Nitrous oxide (N2O) tracer gas and 
CO2 tracer gas were dosed into the exhaled air of the infected manikin, while the exhaled air of the 
exposed manikin was dosed with CO2 only. N2O was used to simulate the emission of exhaled 
infectious particles. N2O and CO2 concentration was measured in the inhaled air of both manikins 
as well as at multiple locations in the room, namely in the supply and exhaust air, on the walls, and 
at several points in the room. Infection probability was calculated based on CO2 and N2O 
concentration measurements and the improved Wells-Riley model. The experiment also included 
measurements of air speed and air temperature at multiple locations in the room, along with a smoke 
visualization of the supply air distribution. In addition to the impact of the flow rate on the infection

probability, the importance of two other factors namely supply air distribution and layout in the 
room was studied. The importance of the air distribution was studied in the case of office set up 
with the same ventilation airflow rate 60 L/s but with a 2-way, 3-way 4-way air discharge diffuser, 
while the importance of the layout was studied for office and classroom arrangement of the 
simulated occupants at ventilation flow rate at 40 L/s and 2-way diffuser 
The results show that all the studied parameters had an influence on the exposure and the infection 
probability. When increasing the ventilation flow rate, the CO2 concentration inhaled by the exposed 
manikin decreased but after 40 L/s the decreasing slowed down. And for N2O it also decreased when 
increased the flow rate. The infection probability showed the same trend. However, the exposure of 
N2O of the exposed manikin under 60 L/s case was higher than that under 40 L/s which led to 
increased infection probability. The air distribution also had an effect on the predicted and actual 
infection probability. When using the air diffuser with wider opening angle, the inhaled CO2 
concentration by the exposed increased and the predicted infection risk also increased, while the 
inhaled N2O concentration decreased and the actual infection probability decreased. In addition, the 
layout also has a significant influence to the infection probability even if the other ventilation 
conditions were all the same. As the experiment results showed, the exposed manikin in the 
classroom layout would inhale higher concentration of CO2 and N2O at the same time which made 
it had a higher risk of getting infected. 
For the CO2 prediction method, it is hard to be used due to the predicted results always with error 
differed from the actual value. For some of the studied conditions the calculated infection 
probability based on CO2 measurements indoor and N2O measurements in the inhaled air by the 
exposed manikin differed by less than 0.5%, while for other conditions the difference was ranging 
from 0.6%-1.37%. The differences make its acceptance based on the subjectivity. As a summary, 
the method using CO2 concentration to predict the actual infection probability of the exposed 
manikin cannot be used. Higher airflow rate would bring lower infection probability, but when the 
ventilation rate increased over 40 L/s the infection probability also increased. And the exposed 
manikin would get lower infection probability if the diffuser changed from 2-way to 4-way at 
ventilation rate 60 L/s. At last, changing the layout from office to classroom would make the exposed 
manikin get a higher risk of infection.

Chapter 1 Introduction 
This chapter presents the requirements for indoor ventilation systems under the current global 
epidemic of COVID-19 and attempts to achieve a quantitative assessment of the risk of indoor 
infection with COVID-19 based on existing ventilation systems. First, the indoor air quality is 
introduced with focus on CO2 as an indoor pollutant and current indicator of bioeffluents level. 
Second, an overview of the current standards for ventilation design mostly based on the CO2 
concentration as the criterion and higher requirements for ventilation system to reduce the risk of 
infection are presented. Finally, a method using CO2 concentrations to be calculated as proximation 
for the infection probability is introduced based on DCV systems currently in use. 
1.1 Indoor air quality 
Today people spend most of the time indoors working and living. Indoor air quality (IAQ) is 
part of the whole indoor environment, which is important for occupant health, comfort and 
performance [1,2]. Indoor environmental requirements only for temperature and relative humidity 
could not provide a suitable environment for occupants indoor. Indoor environment also includes 
thermal, acoustic, olfactory lighting environment by definition. But for IAQ, it refers to inhaled 
air and comprises temperature, humidity and pollution in the indoor and inhaled air. To improve 
the occupants’ performance, the assessment of IAQ has become significant as reference.  
The assessment of IAQ is based on the occupants’ exposure to various pollutants in the indoor 
air. Indoor air pollutants could be generated outdoors and penetrate into the indoor air 
environment, or produced indoors by the occupants’ behaviors and emissions from the building 
materials [3]. People are significant sources of various gaseous and particulate pollutants such as 
volatile organic compounds emitted from their skin and exhaled air, skin flakes shed from their 
skin. Due to respiratory disease, people generate virus-laden particles when breathing, speaking, 
coughing, and sneezing. Some indoor air pollutants can cause discomfort to occupants after a 
short time of exposure, while others may not have an effect until a person is exposed to them for 
a long time or repeatedly [4]. Different people also have different levels sensitivity to the same 
pollutants.  
As already mentioned, an important source of indoor air pollution comes from occupants 
themselves, by producing metabolic CO2 in breath and other bioeffluents from their bodies [5]. 
The threshold limits of CO2 presented by the Association Advancing Occupational and 
Environmental Health (ACGIH) are 5000 ppm as the 8 h time weighted average threshold limit 
value, and 30000 ppm as the short-term exposure limit for 15 minutes only. Although CO2 is 
relatively harmless, excessive exposure can still have adverse effects on people in decision 
making or mental influences [1, 6]. However, in reality, the above levels of CO2 concentrations 
are difficult to reach. But CO2 is often used as a significant marker to indicate the concentration 
of the other bioeffluents, because it is always associated with the presents of people and 
accompanying bioeffluents. It’s easier to detect CO2 than to detect those bioeffluents directly [7].

1.2 Ventilation systems during epidemics of infectious diseases 
Nowadays, the coronavirus disease SARS-CoV-19 (COVID-19) is sweeping all over the world. 
It has caused significant harm to people’s health and life. Many countries have also adopted 
lockdown measures in an attempt to slow the spread of COVID-19 by reducing human-to-human 
contact. However, the lockdown will also bring a huge blow to the country's economy and 
people's livelihood, and it is never a long-term solution. In order to open up society without the 
possibility of large-scale virus transmission, many countries have also introduced virus quick 
tests or PCR tests to obtain public passes for individuals. To gain more knowledge about the 
transmission of SARS-CoV-2, many studies and experiments were conducted. According to 
existing available results from the World Health Organization (WHO), SARS-CoV-2 mainly 
spreads between people when an infected person is in close contact with another person [11]. 
Transmissibility of the virus depends on the amount of viable virus being discharged by the 
infected person, the type of contact they have with others, the environment conditions and what 
infection prevention and control measures are in place [11]. The virus can spread from an infected 
person’s mouth or nose in small liquid particles when he or she coughs, sneezes, sings, breathes 
heavily or talks [11]. According to [11], different behaviors will bring different levels of infection 
risk. These liquid particles exhaled by infected occupants are of different sizes, ranging from 
larger respiratory droplets to smaller aerosols. Close range contact (typically within 1 m) can 
result in inhalation of, or inoculation with, the virus through the mouth, nose, or eyes.  
In the REHVA COVID 19 guide [12], the routes of virus transmission are described in more 
complete detail. For COVID-19 and for other respiratory viruses, three transmission routes are 
==PAGE:19==
5 
 
dominant:  
1) combined droplet and airborne transmission in 1-2 m close contact region arising from 
droplets and aerosols emitted when sneezing, coughing, singing, shouting, talking and 
breathing;  
2) long-range airborne (aerosol-based) transmission;  
3) surface (fomite) contact through hand-hand, hand-surface, etc. contacts.  
These means to deal with these routes are physical distance to avoid the close contact, 
ventilation to avoid airborne transmission, and hand hygiene to avoid surface contact. This study 
only focused on measures related to airborne transmission and ventilation systems. The main 
airborne transmission mechanisms are illustrated in Figure 1.1 [13].

In order to effectively control the spread of the virus, it is necessary to take actions from the 
transmission route. Based on the above known transmission methods and forms of the virus and 
the traditional infection control hierarchy in Figure 1.2 [14], it can be known that measures related 
to the air environment such as ventilation and other HVAC measures are effective. Other levels 
such as elimination could refer to remove the pathogen from water by membrane filtration and 
other similar operations, and administrative controls could refer to the required social safety 
distance. This special period presents higher requirements for indoor ventilation systems due to 
the need to physically remove the pathogen in time. To prevent the spread of COVID-19 in the 
indoor environment, WHO has presented many measures and recommendations, such as ensuring 
good quality ventilation in different indoor situations, etc. A well-designed, maintained and 
operated system can reduce the risk of spread of COVID-19 in indoor spaces by diluting the 
concentration of potentially infectious aerosols through ventilation with outside air and filtration 
and disinfection of recirculated air.

For mechanical ventilation systems in nonresidential buildings, the minimum ventilation rate 
requirement is 10L/s per person. If the limit of the system supply cannot be increased, the help of 
natural ventilation, such as opening windows for ventilation, could be used and an indoor stand-

similarly, the HVAC system should not be working with heat recovery. Finally, the maintenance 
of the systems should also be paid attention to. Regular maintenance of the ventilation system is 
needed, such as cleaning and replacement of filter equipment, etc. Exhausted air should be 
managed correctly as last.

1.3 Cross-infection risk prediction 
The amount of the virus infectious doses (quanta) inhaled by a susceptible person determines 
the probability of infection and it is highly related to the infected occupants’ behaviors [16]. For 
example, resting with oral breathing leads to 2 quanta/h emissions, while resting with speaking 
leads to 9.4 quanta/h in this estimator, etc. But in any case, it is only assumptions based on 
currently knowledges that fit most people (with the exception of super-spreaders, who have 
normal behavior but have extremely high emissions) rather than accurate facts. The uncertainty 
of these values is high. So absolute values of infection risk are difficult to be gave out. To 
overcome this obstacle, the concentration of these pathogenic aerosols is necessary. However, 
when breathing, pathogenic aerosols is not felt by the human body, nor do their concentration 
data help draw meaningful conclusions about the risk of infection. Therefore, it is a meaningful 
research direction to obtain a quantitative and easily assessable infection probability in the form 
of an equation by rational calculation and the assumption of the concentration of pathogenic 
aerosols. However, if the equation needs the exact concentration of the pathogenic aerosols, it 
raised higher requirement for the sensors to detect the specialized aerosols and give out the data 
with high level of accuracy. And if it refers to various kinds of aerosols, it is even harder for single 
sensor to meet the requirement. In this way, another tracer gas in the air should be found to work 
as an indicator for these aerosols. 
Indoor CO2 was started to be used as an indicator of ventilation of indoor space from the 19th 
century and recently as a practical proxy of respiratory infectious disease transmission risk, as 
pathogen-containing aerosols and CO2 are co-exhaled by infected person at the same time. Due 
to the fact that the background CO2 concentration is almost stable depending on the natural CO2 
concentration in the outdoor air entering the indoor space and the indoor excess CO2 
concentration is mostly from exhalation, measurements of indoor CO2 concentrations by low-
cost CO2 sensors might also be good indicators of the risk of COVID-19 infection after 
subtracting the CO2 concentration in the supply air [17].

concentration to predict disease infection [18]. Peng’s thesis also proved that in theory, CO2 
concentration could be used to simultaneously reflect the quantum amount of infectious virus

Chapter 2 Justification 
The use of CO2 as an indicator of indoor air ambient levels was first proposed in the 19th century 
[7]. Indoor air quality (IAQ) is determined by the various pollutants to which the occupants are 
exposed. A large number of studies have found that occupants themselves are a major source of 
indoor pollutants and will continuously produce CO2 and other bioeffluents from breathing and skin, 
which in turn pollute the indoor air environment. At the same time, the generation of bioeffluents is 
accompanied by the production of CO2. Therefore, considering that the indoor CO2 concentration 
has few other sources except occupants and the difficulties of directly detecting the various 
bioeffluents, the CO2 concentration has become an important factor to judge the indoor air 
environment [1, 2, 6]. 
In 2003 the CO2 concentration was proposed as a tool to estimate the risk of transmission of 
indoor airborne infections [18]. The corresponding calculation method was proposed by Rudnick 
and Milton, based on the optimization of the Wells-Riley model as an alternative. Now, based on 
the importance of CO2 concentration in the control of the indoor air environment and the reasonable 
estimation of the probability of infection, a large number of indoor ventilation systems have begun 
to use demand control ventilation (DCV). It realizes automatic control of fresh air volume based on 
the number of people in the room and the CO2 concentration and other parameters measured at the 
exhaust. This method not only ensures a healthy air environment for occupants, but also reduces 
operating costs and usage costs.  
However, this method is still not working optimally. Studies [23, 33] have shown that the supply 
air is rarely completely mixed with the room air, which means that indoor CO2 concentration 
distribution is not uniform. In ventilated rooms the air flow is a result of interaction of several flows 
including ventilation flow, thermal plume generated above occupants and heat sources, transient 
flow of breathing, etc. Changes of the ventilation flow rate and direction, changes of the position of 
the heat sources (occupants) and the buoyancy flows they generate will change the room airflow 
pattern.  
What is more, there are plenty of evidence now that one of the main transmission routes for 
respiratory diseases such as COVID-19 is air, which means that the changes of the room airflow 
pattern tightly relate to the transmission of the exhaled viruses by the infected occupants.  
Therefore, the indoor ventilation system plays a significant role during the transmission. A good 
ventilation system can timely discharge aerosols containing viruses outside to reduce the infection 
probability of indoor occupants, while a poor-quality system may cause a large number of cross-
infections. Based on the above knowledges, more and more studies are being carried out on indoor 
high-quality ventilation methods. The principle for evaluating whether these ventilation methods 
are good or not is the airborne infectious diseases infection probability under the corresponding 
methods. In the study [21], Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulations were used to study 
the infection probability distribution in a classroom with a large number of occupants. Different 
positions of the infected occupants were studied under different room air distribution methods. The 
study evaluates the advantages and disadvantages of the characteristics of different ventilation 
methods, and points out the impact on risk of being infected. The distribution of the simulated CO2 
is shown in Figure 2.1 in a horizontal plane in the classroom at 1.1 m height under the different air 
distribution methods [21]. A dilution rate was introduced to the original Wells-Riley model by Shao 
==PAGE:25==
11 
 
and Li [22], so as to realize the calculation of infection probability at any location in the room. 
Similar topic about exposure to metabolic CO2 with different room air distribution methods and 
its effects on the non-uniform spread of CO2 was studied by Tereza et al. [23]. They measured the 
CO2 concentration in the inhaled air by using the breathing thermal manikin and the CO2 
concentration at the exhaust when four different ventilation methods were used (mixing ventilation, 
displacement ventilation, ventilation with box diffuser, ventilation with a textile duct) in the same 
space. The results are shown in Figure 2.2. It also strongly supports the idea that different ventilation 
conditions can provide completely different mixing levels of air environment. And CO2 is not evenly 
distributed in the room and it is affected by the air distribution methods. 
For viruses and infectious aerosols generated by the infected occupants, the transmission pattern 
is also important. Although in the ventilated space the supply airflow is not completely mixed with 
the room air and the CO2 is not evenly distributed, it was still a question that viruses and pathogenic 
aerosols have the similar trend or not. In a recent study [20], they used CFD to simulate the actual 
spread of the pathogenic aerosols to model the aerosol transport and virus exposure. Figure 2.3 
shows the CFD simulations results. As shown in the figure, aerosols do not necessarily spread 
uniformly in space due to complex character of air flows. This would certainly make influence to 
the exposure of viruses and pathogenic aerosols. In this way, it is proved that the viruses generated 
by an infected occupant are also not evenly distributed which is similar to the CO2 transmission.

Additionally, in a recent study [17], the topic of using exhaled CO2 concentration indoors as an 
estimate of infection risk for different indoor environments and activities was theoretically 
demonstrated. The study assumed complete indoor air mixing, and found out that exhaled CO2 
concentration of occupants can be used as a computational parameter to estimate infection risk. 
Therefore, combining the above arguments and the existing research results can lead to the 
following question: is it possible to predict the infection risk of respiratory diseases such as COVID-
19 by introducing the dilution rate into the Wells-Riley model by using different concentrations of 
CO2 throughout the room without assuming complete mixing of indoor air? And is this CO2 
prediction method accurate enough under the realistic situation where the air is poor mixing? 
This leads to the topic of this study, which was to check the feasibility of using CO2 concentration 
to predict the probability of COVID-19 and other airborne infectious diseases when there is one 
infected occupant. In a nonbiological laboratory and without protection, considering the harm 
caused by COVID-19 and other airborne infectious diseases to the human body and the possible 
fatality rate after infection is clearly dangerous. There is also the risk of possible sequelae after 
recovery, using the real pathogenic virus of COVID-19 in in-field propagation to detect predictive 
calculations. Therefore, in this experiment, a tracer gas other than CO2 was used to replace the actual 
pathogenic virus to further simulate its distribution characteristics in a specific environment and

ventilation method and then prove or falsify this hypothesis. 
If the study proves the CO2 prediction method is reliable, the infection probability based on the 
CO2 concentration can be added to the current control of the ventilation system based on the CO2 
concentration to better control the ventilation system in special periods. In special spaces such as 
hospitals and isolation places, the concentration of CO2 in most spaces can be monitored by 
increasing the number of CO2 concentration sensors, to monitor the probability of infection and 
control it within a reasonable range. If the study rejects the CO2 prediction method, the effects of 
different indoor parameters on infection probability can still be obtained during the experiment.

4.2.5 Tracer gases 
Pure CO2 tracer gas was dosed in the exhaled air of both manikins at the same rate as on average 
people emit when performing light sedentary work. An additional tracer gas nitrous oxide (N2O) 
was used in the experiment to simulate the spread of pathogenic aerosol emitted from the exhaled 
air of the infected person and to compare with infection probability based on CO2 concentration 
measurement. Since this calculation method took CO2 concentration as the focus, artificial lungs, 
thermal manikins and dosed CO2 were used in the experiment to achieve CO2 distribution 
characteristics that match the real and usual situation. The tracer gas CO2 was dosed by two sealed 
compressed gas cylinders equipped with pressure reducers and each was connected to a CO2 gas 
flowmeter (with an accuracy of 1% of the reading) to regulate the desired gas flow rate. Each CO2 
flow meter was connected via a tube (Ø5 mm) to one of the artificial lungs’ exhalation tubes. A 
schematic of the tracer gas dosing into the exhalation air is shown in Figure A.14 in Appendix A. 
To simulate a metabolic CO2 production of a person with 1.2 metabolic rate, i.e., 10 breathing 
cycles/minute with pulmonary ventilation of 6 L/min, 0,771 L/min of CO2 gas was dosed to the 
exhaled air from the artificial lungs. This corresponded to the CO2 emission rate of 19 L/h per 
occupant as stated in CEN CR 1752 (2001) [39]. 
As already mentioned, simultaneously with the CO2 dosing, 0.334 L/min of nitrous oxide tracer 
gas was dosed into the exhaled air of one of the manikins to simulate the emission of exhaled 
infectious aerosols. The N2O tracer gas was released from a compressed gas cylinder equipped with 
pressure reducer and gas flowmeter (with an accuracy of ±5% of the reading) to control the N2O 
flow rate. The connecting method is shown in Appendix A.

4.4.1 CO2 concentration 
 4.4.1.1 Vaisala Transmitters 
The CO2 concentration in the supply air diffuser and on the left wall near the door after entering 
the room at a height of 1.7 m, was measured by two Vaisala GMP343 CO2 probe with accuracy of 
1% of the reading. The Vaisala transmitters use advanced Micro-Electro-Mechanical System 
(MEMS) technology to measure CO2 concentration and enables continuous long-term 
measurements. Each of the Vaisala transmitters was integrated in a small case with an attached 
HOBO analog data logger. Measurement range of the Vaisala transmitter used to measure in the 
supply air was 0-1000 ppm (named Vaisala 1000) and for the other one (named Vaisala 5000) it was 
0-5000 ppm. The Vaisala transmitters measured the CO2 concentration every 30s.  
In the experiment, the concentration data measured by the Vaisala instruments is considered to be 
extremely accurate. Therefore, the two instruments were used as references during the inter-
calibration with the INNOVA gas analyzer described in the next section. In fact, INNOVA has a 
fixed range of measurements. It simply shifts the range as a whole and the difference between any 
two measurements does not change. At this time, Vaisala, which is highly accurate and has almost 
no shifts, can be used as a reference for correcting the offset of the instrument INNOVA. Vaisala 
1000 was used as a reference instrument to correct Vaisala 5000 at the lower CO2 levels (<1000 
ppm) and Vaisala 5000 was used as a reference for the upper CO2 levels (1000-5000). The 
calibration of the measuring equipment is described in detail in chapter 4.6. 
4.4.1.2 INNOVA Photoacoustic Gas-monitor coupled with Multipoint sampler/Doser 
The CO2 concentration distribution was one of the main interests of this study. Therefore, it was 
important to measure it at numerous locations and with sufficient sample rate during each case. 
Limited by the sets of Vaisala, another instrument for CO2 concentration measuring was needed. 
The CO2 concentration was measured at 12 points by two sets of Photoacoustic Gas-Monitor 
INNOVA 1312 and each coupled with a Multipoint Sampler and Doser INNOVA 1302.  
The Multipoint Sampler and Doser INNOVA 1302 has 6 channels, each with a valve to connect 
to the sampling tubes via an air filter to keep the particles out of the inside of the instrument. The

destination of the air sample, to the gas monitor for analysis or through a pump to the exhaust gas 
outlet on the back panel. The exhaust gas from the instruments were connected to the exhaust grill

detection method, so it can detect almost any gas that absorbs infrared light. At the same time,

4.4.3 Flow rate 
Measuring of not only the ventilation airflow rates, but also the dosing of tracer gas was necessary 
in order to control and maintain the designed experimental conditions. The flow rate of the 
ventilation system was adjusted by the balancing dampers and the circular valves. The flow rate on 
each branch were measured by the use of balancing dampers. Those enable flow rate measurements 
based on flow pressure in the pipe p1. After entering the nozzle, the air converges and due to this, 
==PAGE:44==
30 
 
the pressure is reduced until it reaches the minimum cross section area - the throat. Close after the 
nozzle, the air reaches its minimum pressure p2. The pressure difference (p1-p2) was measured 
using a different pressure sensor. And the flow rate is determined by the pressure difference and K-
factor shown in Equation 4.6. 
𝑞= 𝑘· √∆𝑝𝑚                                (4.6) 
Where q is the airflow rate (L/s), k is the K-factor, given by the manufacturer and Δpm is the 
pressure difference (Pa). The K-factor depends on the dimension of the measuring and the current 
position of the damper. In the experiment, there was an integrated operation platform in control 
room, where the airflow rate of the ventilation system can be directly set and monitored according 
to the above principles.  
The flow rate of the dosing of the tracer gas was measured and adjusted by the flowmeters which 
are connected in the dosing loop. A flowmeter consists of a conical glass tube with a float in it. 
When higher volumetric flow rate pass through the spacing area, the flow speed and drag force 
would increase, so the float will be pushed upwards. However, as the inside of the rotameter is cone 
shaped, the area around the float through which the medium flows increase, the flow speed and drag 
force decrease until there is mechanical equilibrium with the float's weight, combined use of valve 
to achieve the purpose of controlling and monitoring the amount of the tracing gas. Since the 
experiment involved two CO2 dosing and one N2O dosing, a total of three sets of rotameters were 
used.

4.5.2 Procedure 
The day before each case, the desired conditions were adjusted in field lab2 i.e., the layout, the 
airflow rate, the sealing method of the diffuser and the room air temperature. The power of the 
radiator would also be turned on due to the low temperature outside and it would be turned off 
before the case started for the space to reach the heat balance quickly the next day. The lighting 
inside lab2 would be off. All the measurement instruments and PCs inside field lab 1&2 were off 
and the thermal manikins as well. 
In the morning before the experiment started, the actual conditions in lab 2 were checked and 
adjusted if needed, especially the airflow rate, the sealing condition of the window and the type of 
diffuser. The sampling tubes connecting condition was important as well. Note that after confirming 
that there was no problem in lab 2 and turned on the measuring instruments, the lab could not be 
entered again until the measurements finished, so as not to affect the experimental results. 
Afterwards the thermal manikins and artificial lungs were connected to PC and turned on. The 
INNOVA sets, the GASERA, the two sets of Vaisala were started and checked the working and data 
==PAGE:49==
35 
 
logging conditions. If everything was working well then started dosing the tracer gases to the 
artificial lungs to start the build-up process inside lab 2. Started the temperature measurement 
instruments to monitor the working condition of artificial lungs. By watching the logged data and 
the figures from the two sets of INNOVA the build-up process could be monitored without entering 
the lab 2. Once the concentration of CO2 kept stable it meant the environment inside lab 2 reached 
steady state and the data afterwards was the focus of the study.  
Due to the different speed at which each instrument analyzes the concentration, in order to 
obtained as much data as possible, the steady state in lab 2 would last about 6-8 hours. During this 
period, the researcher should pay attention to ventilation flow rate in lab2 displayed in the control 
room and the fluctuation of the amount of dosing gases shown by the rotameters in lab1 at 10-minute 
intervals. If there was a large deviation, it needed to be adjusted immediately. 
When the required amount of data was collected, the measurement of that case could be stopped. 
Generally speaking, it took the longest sampling time to obtain at least 20 sets of steady-state data 
when using the all of the channels of GASERA. Because each channel took a certain amount of 
time in a complete channels cycle. For the ending of the measurements, the measurement 
instruments would always be stopped before stopping dosing tracer gas. The two sets of INNOVA, 
two sets of Vaisala and GASERA stopped at first and checked the data logging files. If all correct 
then the dosing of tracer gas would be stopped and closed the gas flowmeter valve and safety valve. 
Then the two thermal manikins and the artificial lungs would be turned off, so did the other 
measuring instruments. After exporting and collecting all the needed data from the PCs and 
GASERA inside lab 1, the condition inside lab 2 would be checked and turned all the power of 
except the radiator to keep the temperature inside it. If the preparation for the next day was done 
then the experiment was finished that day. 
4.6 Calibration 
As already mentioned, the two INNOVA gas analyzers were inter-calibrated with the two Vaisala 
transmitters before the experiment. The data from the inter-calibration were used to obtain a 3rd 
order polynomial relationship for correcting the INNOVA results. Vaisala (0-1000) was used as a 
reference instrument for the CO2 concentration range of up to 1000 ppm. And Vaisala (0-5000) was 
used as a reference to correct INNOVAs with measuring range over 1000 ppm. The inter-calibration 
was to correct the measurement data of INNOVA series instruments to finally obtain more accurate 
results. The uncertainties of both Vaisalas are ±17 ppm.   
A test rig consisting of flexible ducts Ø80 mm and a fan (with frequency-controlled speed) was 
used to perform the intercalibration. The test rig was located inside field lab 1 with controlled 
conditions. About 3 m long ducts were connected to the fan as shown in Figure 4.17, The duct 
connected at the suction side of the fan was placed outside through the lab’s window. Pure CO2 was 
dosed from a compressed gas cylinder before the fan of the test rig at constant emission rate. 
==PAGE:50==
36 
 
 
Figure 4.17: Sketch of the test rig used for intercalibration 
After the fan at distance of 2 m, the gas concentration was sampled by the INNOVA gas analyzers 
and Vaisala transmitters. The measurements were done at steady state gas concentration in the test 
rig and room temperature of 22℃. 
After collecting about 10 gas concentrations samples at a certain constant tracer gas level, the 
supply flow rate and CO2 dosing rate in the duct were changed to obtain a different CO2 
concentration level. Seven (eight with outdoor background concentration of CO2) different levels 
of CO2 were used to get proper polynomial relationship between the reference instrument Vaisala 
GMP 343 probe and the INNOVA gas analyzers. 
Due to the high precision of GASERA, no intercalibration was performed for it. 
4.7 Smoke visualization 
The smoke visualization of all the cases were done separately before and after the main 
measurements. Before each visualization test, it was, checked whether the experimental conditions 
in the room met the indoor requirements, mainly the air jet distribution of the diffuser, ventilation 
flow rate and room temperature. After that, smoke was generated in the supply air and a green laser 
was used to visualize the flow in a vertical plane between the supply diffuser and either the exhaust 
grill or the walls. The visualizations were recorded by a camera. The purpose of the visualizations 
was to check for supply air shortcut and also to visualize the room air distribution.

Chapter 5 Results 
This chapter presents the results obtained during this study. The results are separated and 
presented in four comparing groups. First, the results regarding the measured CO2 and concentration 
are described. One of the main focuses in this study was the averaged concentration measured in 
the air inhaled by the exposed manikin (in the jar) and the occupied zone and also the concentration 
measured at the walls and at the exhaust. Second, the average concentration of N2O is presented to 
be compared with the CO2 concentration at the same location. Finally, the results of the infection 
probability, calculated based on both the CO2 and N2O measurements, are compared together with 
the resultant uncertainty to validate or reject the method of using CO2 as a proxy for infection 
probability risk. Also, the air temperature and air speed results are presented afterwards. 
5.1 Impact of supply airflow rate on the CO2 and N2O concentration

5.4 Infection probability  
In this chapter, the results from the estimated infection probabilities based on both the CO2 and 
N2O measurements at the studied conditions are presented. The infection probabilities were 
calculated using the original Wells-Riley model (Equation 4.8) and the modified Wells-Riley model 
with introduced dilution ratio (presented in chapter 4.8.2). In this experiment, the infection 
==PAGE:64==
50 
 
probability calculated based on the N2O concentration and the revised Wells-Riley model would be 
used as the actual infection probability of airborne respiratory diseases. 
It is worth noting that if only the Original Wells-Riley model is used in the calculation process 
without using the dilution ratio, the result obtained would be infection probability assuming uniform 
indoor air mixing. The results calculated without dilution ratio are for reference only in the analysis. 
The infection probability was calculated for all the measuring locations in Appendix C. In this 
chapter only several critical points’ infection probability was presented: at the exhaust, in occupied 
zone middle at 1.7 m height, in the inhaled air by the exposed manikin, 1 m above the desk of 
exposed and wall between desks. These locations were tightly related to the effectiveness of 
ventilation system and the risk of the exposed manikin.  
Figure 5.14 shows the infection probabilities and their uncertainties as error bars of the results 
obtained with the 2-way supply diffuser and different airflow rates. The infection probabilities (IP) 
based on the original Wells-Riley represent the results during fully mixing condition in the room.  
At 20 L/s 2-way office layout case, the infection probability based on the original Wells-Riley 
model and based on the N2O-lungs and N2O wall between desks was the highest. Overall, the results 
based on N2O were about 1% higher than that calculated by the excess CO2 in absolute difference. 
However, due to the fact that in general the infection probabilities in this case were not higher than 
6%, this 1% could not be underestimated which brought around 16.7% difference. No matter for IP 
based on CO2 or N2O, almost all different locations made changes to the infection probability. For 
example, if focusing the N2O part in 20 L/s office case, the highest IP difference occurred between 
inhaled air and middle occupied zone, and the difference reached 0.94% without considering the 
uncertainty. It could be higher with uncertainty. Similarly, this 0.94% difference could not be 
ignored when compared to the overall risk level based on N2O, which means that in a room with not 
fully mixing room air the risk could vary among the occupants with different locations. If 
considering the feasibility of the calculation method, took the uncertainty into account, the infection 
probabilities difference reached 2.73% in the inhaled air by exposed manikin. Compared to the 
actual infected risk 5.84%±0.37% (IP based on N2O in the inhaled air by the exposed manikin), this 
difference could be evaluated as huge, which means that the CO2 prediction method for infection 
probability at the same location could not be used in this case. 
When the ventilation rate changed to 40 L/s, the IP based on the original model decreased but not 
the lowest, which means the location “wall between desks” with lower IP than original model had 
“over dilution” than designed. In this case the overall difference between IP based on CO2 and N2O 
was around 0.13%-0.63% in absolute value without considering uncertainty. Except the infection 
probability based on the N2O in inhaled air by the exposed manikin and N2O above the exposed 
desk, all the other results were about 3 % if we exclude the uncertainty. However, if we consider the 
uncertainty of the infection probabilities shown with error bars, then we can see that the IPs based 
on CO2 can be in the range of 3% ± 0.8%. The 0.8% uncertainty in predicted IP could lead to a 
percentage of 21.7% shifting from the actual IP based on N2O concentration in the inhaled air by 
the exposed manikin. Combined the above conditions, it is impossible to say the calculating method 
is feasible. At the same time, the change of location still has a significant impact on the risk 
prediction of infection. 
When the airflow rate reached 60 L/s, the IP based on the original model was the lowest among 
==PAGE:65==
51 
 
all the measured locations. The highest IP was obtained based on N2O-exposed lungs. The IPs based 
on CO2 were all around 2.4% except in the inhaled air by the exposed manikin and so were the IPs 
based on N2O. The only exception was the inhaled air which both reached the highest in CO2 group 
and N2O group. Limited by the accuracy of CO2 concentration measuring instruments, IP based on 
CO2 always had a higher uncertainty. Therefore, even though the IPs calculated using CO2 and N2O 
were very similar at most of the corresponding locations, the overlap of IP became very small once 
uncertainty is taken into account. As at exhaust, there was only a 0.05% IP gap without accounting 
for uncertainty, but it reached 0.89% when uncertainty is included. Although the absolute value is 
very low, the relative proportion reaches 36.3%. And similarly, the relative proportion in the inhaled 
air by the expose manikin gets even bigger. Therefore, in this experiment, it is hard to use CO2 as 
an accurate way to estimate actual infection probability at the same location. 
If comparing horizontally within the group, the probability of infection decreased with increasing 
airflow. However, the decrease slowed down after supply flow increasing over 40 L/s. Similar 
situations were observed in regarding to infection probability calculated based on concentrations 
measured in the exhaust and in the occupied zone. The inhaled air was the only exception, where 
the airflow rate of the supply increased from 40 L/s to 60 L/s led to the increasing of the probability 
of infection based on inhaled air measurements by 4.8%. Similar phenomenon has previously been 
observed [32, 33]. Therefore, increasing the indoor ventilation within a certain range (40 L/s in this 
experiment) can effectively reduce the infection probability. When the threshold is exceeded, the 
effect of increasing ventilation will gradually weaken, and it may even make people more 
susceptible to infection.

the same location. The gap remained at around 0.6% when uncertainty was excluded. At the same 
time, the two IPs measured at the above exposed desk were almost same. All of the above were 
features that first appeared in these three cases in this group. If uncertainty is taken into 
consideration to examine the feasibility of the calculation method, a negative result is still obtained. 
Taking inhaled air as an example, the IP based on excess CO2 can be as high as 4.59%. The IP based 
on inhaled N2O is 2.8%. The gap ratio can be as high as 62.4%. 
The tracer gas concentration measured in the exhaust was constant between the experimental 
cases (differences within 1 ppm). In this way the infection probability calculated for the exhaust 
was at a level of 2.5±0.03% and only 0.5% higher than the value obtained with the original Wells-
Riley model. The probability of infection based on N2O in the occupied zone tended to slightly 
increase with increasing air discharge directions: increased by 0.55% between 2-way and 4-way air 
discharge. The infection probability based on the tracer gas concentration in inhaled air of 
suspectable person was in all studied cases higher than the infection probability obtained in the 
occupied zone and the exhaust. The highest infection probability of 3.9% was obtained at 2-way air

discharge and tended to decrease with the extension of discharge directions. Change in air 
distribution from 2-way to 4-way discharge resulted in a decrease in infection probability of 1.0%.  
To sum up, under this experimental site and mixing ventilation, a more uniform air supply method 
will make the exposed manikin to inhale a higher concentration of CO2, but reduce the risk of 
infection to a certain extent. Correspondingly, this proved that under these experimental conditions 
the CO2 and N2O didn’t have similar pattern of motion, which means that the accuracy of the 
calculation method is difficult to guarantee.

Chapter 6 Discussion 
This chapter analyzes the infection probability results and their implications. First, the percentage 
of exact infection probability is introduced to discuss the feasibility of the use of CO2 for prediction 
of infection probability based on the method with the dilution ratio. The exact infection probability 
means the infection probability of the exposed manikin based on the N2O concentration in its inhaled 
air. Then, the influence of the airflow rate, air distribution and layout on the exact infection 
probability of the exposed manikin would be investigated. Third, the relatively most convincing 
concentration measurement location is investigated by analyzing the results to give 
recommendations. Furthermore, the acceptance of the errors is discussed. Finally, the limitations of 
the present study are summarized. 
6.1 Percentage of exact infection probability 
As the results showed in chapter 5, almost none of the IPs based on CO2 are the same as IPs based 
on inhaled N2O concentration by exposed manikin. If taking the uncertainties into account, the 
differences between exact infection probability and CO2-based predicted infection probability 
would even increase. However, it is impossible to measure the CO2 concentration in the inhaled air 
by people or continuously monitor the concentration of viruses in the space. As a substitution, the 
CO2 concentration is always measured in the exhaust or somewhere on the walls to work as a 
reference for controlling the ventilation. Previous studies [19, 21, 23] have already showed that CO2 
distribution indoors depends on many factors such room air distribution, heat source distribution, 
room layout, etc. and CO2 always distributes non-uniformly. As shown in Figure 5.14, 5.15 and 5.16, 
if CO2 concentration measured on the wall or in the exhaust was used to calculate predicted risk of 
infection, these might not be always the best representative locations. Also, an important factor in 
evaluating the feasibility of the method using CO2 concentration to predict the infection probability 
of the exposed manikin is the deviation of the estimated value from the exact probability of infection. 
The prediction method can be validated or rejected by evaluating the error. 
In order to study the problem above, this study considered the infection probability calculated 
based on the N2O concentration in the inhaled air by the exposed manikin (calculated infection 
probability based on “N2O-exposed lung”) as the exact IP. On the other hand, the predicted IPs were 
determined by results calculated based on the CO2 concentration measured in the inhaled air, exhaust, 
mid occupied zone, wall between desks and above the exposed desk which are the most related or 
most probably places to install the CO2 sensors. To compare the calculated IPs based on CO2 
measurements with the exact IP in the form of percentage of the exact IP, the Equation 6.1 was used: 
𝑃𝑒𝑟𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑡𝑎𝑔𝑒 𝑜𝑓 𝑒𝑥𝑎𝑐𝑡 𝐼𝑃= 
|𝐼𝑃𝐶𝑂2−𝐼𝑃𝑁2𝑂|
|𝐼𝑃𝑁2𝑂|
                  (6.1) 
where IPCO2 is the infection probability (%) calculated based on the CO2 measurements measured 
at corresponding measuring locations, IPN2O is the infection probability (%) calculated based on the 
N2O concentration measured in the inhaled air of the exposed manikin. The larger the percentage, 
the larger the difference between the infection probability based on CO2 measurements and the exact 
infection probability, which means that the CO2 prediction method cannot be used due to the 
significant error.

Figure 6.1: Percentage of exact IP for the cases with different supply airflow rate 
Figure 6.1 shows the percentage of exact IP for the cases with different supply airflow rate during 
the cases with three studied ventilation rates and same 2-way diffuser. During the cases at 20 L/s 
and 40 L/s the lowest percentage are 14.3% and 15.6% in the exhaust air respectively. There was 
another relatively low percentage of 17% during the case of 40 L/s in the inhaled air. However, in 
the case at 60 L/s, the lowest percentage no longer occurred in the exhaust at the previous level, as 
in 40 L/s case the lowest percentage was 15.6%. It reached 28% in the inhaled air by the exposed 
manikin. The IP based on the CO2 in the exhaust air at 60 L/s has a higher percentage of 35.6% of 
the exact IP. These results show that infection probability approximation based on CO2 
measurements for exposed occupants vary depending on the measuring locations and airflow rate. 
Bivolarova et al. (2020) also found that the CO2 concentration can vary widely in a meeting room 
with mixing air distribution. 
Through the above analysis and the related IP results shown in chapter 5, it is summarized that 
calculating the predicted infection probability of the exposed manikin by CO2 concentration 
measured at the exhaust has a certain error differs from the exact infection probability based on the 
inhaled N2O concentration by the exposed manikin. By comparing with each other among the three 
cases with different airflow rate, the predicted infection probabilities based on the CO2 concentration 
in the exhaust air in the cases at 20 L/s and 40 L/s showed relatively smaller errors 0.84% and 0.58% 
in absolute value difference respectively without considering the uncertainties. If take the 0.78%-
0.79% uncertainties into account, the error would even increase. What’ more, when the ventilation 
rate reached 60 L/s, the error between predicted infection probability based on the CO2 
concentration in the exhaust air and the exact infection probability based on the inhaled N2O 
concentration in the inhaled air by the exposed manikin increased (from around 15% to 35.6%), and 
the absolute value difference reached 1.37% which could not be underestimated (the values are 
shown in Table 6.1 and 6.2). Hence, in this group with cases at different airflow rates and same air 
diffuser and layout, the method of using CO2 concentration to predict the actual infection probability

of the exposed manikin was rejected. Cases with lower velocity jet (20 L/s 2-way and 40 L/s 2-way) 
would have the lowest percentage of exact IP with absolute error around 0.58-0.84%. And case with 
higher velocity jet (60 L/s, 2-way) would have a lowest error around 1.4% when the measurement 
location was actually measurable.

values had deviations higher than 0.5% when uncertainty was not considered. After considering the 
influence of uncertainty, this calculation method is even more infeasible and the accuracy is greatly 
reduced. Therefore, the method using CO2 concentration to predict the actual infection probability 
of the exposed occupant is rejected.

Before the analysis it should be noted that the CO2 prediction method is rejected because it cannot

comparison to choose relatively more suitable location to calculate the predict infection probability,

studied in the present study. 
6.4 Acceptance of the differences 
As discussed before, the method of using CO2 concentration to predict the actual infection 
probability of the exposed occupant is rejected. But for few cases (40 L/s 2-way and 60 L/s 4-way 
office) the obtained error was around 0.5% when compared to the value based on N2O concentration 
in the inhaled air. For other cases, the predicted infection probability based on CO2 concentration in 
the exhaust air was with an error within the range of 0.6%-1.4%.  
It is important to note that the importance of the accuracy in prediction of infection probability is 
not the same for different diseases. A 1% change in the predicted infection probability would not be 
alarming if only the risk of infection from the common cold was evaluated. When assessing the risk 
of airborne respiratory diseases infection with a high mortality rate, even a 1% change in the 
infection risk can be important and can make people feeling anxious. In the research by Durbas et 
al. [34] about anxiety and stress levels associated with COVID-19 pandemic of university students 
in Turkey, it shows that the anxiety prevalence rates in regarding to the COVID-19 are relatively 
higher than other types of anxiety prevalence in the studies before. And the anxiety and stress levels 
are related to losing a family member due to COVID-19. Therefore, it is not possible to objectively 
assess what should be an acceptable accuracy for the infection probability. In the current study, it is 
assumed that the CO2 prediction method can be used (i.e., has high-enough-accuracy) when the 
percentage of exact IP is ≤ 1%. 
6.5 Limitations of the present study 
Unlike previous research in this field, which is mostly based on simulation or theoretical 
deduction, the results in this experiment are based on spot measurements. For this reason, the 
assumption of complete indoor air mixing is abandoned in this study, and objective measurements 
can be obtained under real conditions. However, this experiment still has several limitations. 
First of all, this experiment is limited by the type of tracer gas used. Since the subject is related 
==PAGE:82==
68 
 
to airborne respiratory diseases and CO2, the use of CO2 is necessary, but it is impossible to 
introduce aerosols that actually cause these diseases infection in the experiment. Such experiments 
must be carried out in the corresponding biological laboratory under safe conditions. Previous 
studies have shown that the transport behavior of tracer gas is similar to the transport pattern of 
airborne particles with size of less than 5 µm [35, 36]. It has been reported that the size of the virus-
laden particles of COVID is ranging from respiratory droplets to aerosols within the range of 100 
µm [37, 38]. However, the tracer gas N2O used as an alternative cannot be completely equivalent to 
the pathogenic aerosol in terms of physical properties, specifications, etc. N2O tracer gas might not 
be 100% accurate to simulate all sizes of infectious particles, but it has been proven that small 
particles within 5 µm and tracer gas follow the same airflow pattern. However, infectious particles 
are within wide range including above 5 µm. In this case tracer gas cannot be used to simulate the 
airborne behavior of the larger-than-5-µm particles, although these particles anyway settled very 
quickly onto surfaces. Therefore, the results obtained in this experiment could not include all the 
sizes of the particles and part of the results might be loss due the disability of simulating the particles 
larger than 5 µm.  
Secondly, the experimental results were obtained under limited conditions. In practice, the 
conditions can be different. For example, the use of air purifier will help removing the pathogenic 
particles and aerosols in the air. However, it would not make any contributions to the CO2 
concentration indoors if the air purifier does not filter CO2. Under this condition, if there is a CO2 
sensor in the exhaust air to give a predicted value of infection risk indoors, the value can be 
overestimated because part of the aerosols and particles will be removed while the CO2 
concentration will not change. 
In addition, both thermal manikins in this experiment were in Comfort mode during the 
measurements without generating any movement. In real life, occupants will not keep sitting still 
for up to 6-8 hours. Future investigations, which take occupant behavior into account (for example, 
going in and out of the room), on the reliability of infection probability based on CO2 measurements 
are needed. The breathing mode of the thermal manikins was set to exhale through the nose and 
inhale through the mouth, which could be different for people depending on their individual 
preferences. It is recommended to study the influence of the breathing mode on the infection 
probability. 
The quantum generation rate of 2 quanta/h was used in the data analysis in this experiment. This 
amount is closely related to the behavior of the inhabitants and it has been estimated to be 2 quanta/h 
for seated person and no talking [16]. However, 2 quanta/h is also an estimate based on assumptions 
due to lack of sufficient data on COVID-19[16]. It should be noted that the obtained in this study IP 
results will take different values if the quanta generation rate is different than 2 quanta/h. Sitting for 
8 hours without talking is also an abnormal working state, and there is still room for more 
experiments in this regard.

Chapter 7 Conclusions 
The main focus of this study was to validate or reject the method of cross-infection risk prediction 
based on CO2 concentration measurement. Another objective was to study how much supply airflow 
rate, air distribution and room layout affect the infection probability and whether it is substantial or 
not.  
The most important findings are summarized in the following to meet the objectives: 
⚫ 
The infection probability calculated based on the CO2 concentration measured indoors was 
not the same as the exact infection probability based on N2O measured in the inhaled air of 
the exposed occupant in all studied cases; 
⚫ 
However, when the indoor air is almost evenly distributed and mixed (such as in case 40 
L/s-2-way, 60 L/s-3-way, 60 L/s-4-way, office layout), the CO2 prediction method based on 
concentration measured in the exhaust air could be used as an approximation to predict the 
risk of airborne transmission indoors with an error of 0.58%-0.59%; 
⚫ 
For all cases (except for 40 L/s-2-way, 60 L/s-3-way, 60 L/s-4-way, office layout), the CO2 
prediction method of infection probability differed with about 0.84%-1.37% error compared 
to the exact infection probability. 
⚫ 
When the infection probability of the exposed occupant was predicted based on CO2 
concentration, the predicted value was different for the different measuring locations. The 
difference was higher for the most of measuring locations on the walls compared to the 
points in the exhaust air and occupied zone. 
⚫ 
The results of the present study reveal that the exposure to airborne contaminants and thus 
the infection probability in spaces depends on the supply flow rate, supply air distribution 
and layout of occupants. 
⚫ 
The results support previous findings that increase of the supply flow rate not always leads 
to decrease of the exposure to infected exhaled air and thus to lower infection probability.  
⚫ 
When the ventilation rate is kept the same, but only the jet air distribution of the supply 
diffuser is changed, the overall infection probability level in the room will not change greatly. 
However, wider opening angle of the diffuser with lower supply air velocity resulted in lower 
infection probability for the exposed manikin. 
⚫ 
The exposed manikin in the classroom got almost 1% higher infection probability than in 
the office. 
The following are some recommendations summarized based on the study’s results: 
⚫ 
Indoor ventilation flow rate should not be as high as possible. It could bring even higher 
infection probability. 
⚫ 
If installing the sensor to estimate infection probability of occupants based on CO2 
concentration estimation is a must, it is recommended to be installed in the exhaust duct. 
Because it has lower error than other indoor measuring points in most of the cases. 
⚫ 
If a circular air diffuser is used in a ventilated room, it is recommended to keep the discharge 
==PAGE:84==
70 
 
flow direction from all sides (4-way) than to be partly blocked. It helps increasing the room 
air mixing and decreasing the infection probability of the exposed occupant. 
⚫ 
Extra attention should be paid to the air quality in rooms where people sit behind each other. 
If people sit behind an infected person, they might be at higher risk of infection than being 
on the side of the infected person at 2 m distance.  
Recommendations for further studies: 
⚫ 
Using concentration measurement instruments with higher accuracy and response time is 
recommended because it affects largely the accuracy of the measurements and thus will 
affect the estimation of infection probability based on CO2. 
⚫ 
More ventilation rate levels between 40-60 L/s could be applied to the experiment to find 
the best performance for a 2-person office with 2-way diffuser. 
⚫ 
More types of supply air terminal devices could be applied to the experiment to investigate 
the influence of air diffuser type on the infection probability. 
⚫ 
It is possible to deeply study the impact of different behaviors of occupants (speaking loudly 
when sitting, oral breathing or speaking when standing, etc.) on the infection probability of 
indoor exposed occupants using different quantum generated rate. 
⚫ 
Shorter distance between the occupants could be studied to find out what would be the 
infection probability in spaces where occupants are forced to sit close to each other such as 
in public transport.

Appendix B 
Calibration and uncertainty 
Calibration 
The two sets of INNOVAs were calibrated before the experiment. The calibration method 
included the INNOVAs and the reference instrument Vaisala 1000 and Vaisala 5000, also with the 
device showed in Figure B.1. The calibration was performed in the field lab 1 with the instrument 
inside. The field lab 1 was the same size of field lab 2 and the instrument was placed near the window. 
The device consisted of an air inlet duct, an air exhaust duct, a pump and a control device. It 
absorbed the clean air from the outside air through the inlet duct by the window and the mixing and 
amount was kept by the air pump controlled by the controller. Before the pump the tracer gas was 
dosed inside the duct and the air was sampled after the pump by the reference instrument Vaisala 
and INNOVA. At last, the 3rd polynomials for correcting the INNOVAs’ data can be obtained by 
comparing exact values (measured data from Vaisala 1000 and 5000) with INNOVAs measurements. 
 
Figure B.1: A sketch of the Instrument for calibration

relationship at first, it brought a large error to the experimental results. Finally, it was decided to use 
3rd polynomial for INNOVAs. Eight (background CO2 concentration and seven different levels of 
concentration) levels of CO2 concentration were measured during the inter-calibration. After the 
inter-calibration, two 3rd polynomials formulas were obtained to correct the INNOVAs results. They 
are presented in Equation B.1 and B.2. The calibration curves are presented in Figure B.2 and B.3.

of one breathing cycle.
