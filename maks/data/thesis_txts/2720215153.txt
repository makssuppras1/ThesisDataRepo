Abstract
A layered solid shell finite element is created for the use in high-resolution structural
analysis of wind turbine blades. For this element type, different enhancements have been
proposed in literature. After the verification of simplified load cases, this work focuses
on the implementation and validation of four of those enhancements: the enhanced as-
sumed strain (EAS) method, the assumed natural strain (ANS) method for out-of-plane
shear strains, the assumed natural strain (ANS) method for the thickness strain and
a through-thickness orthogonalization. They are tested in cases of shear-, membrane-,
thickness- and trapezoidal locking.
The enhanced element is implemented successfully
and manages to eliminate or massively reduce all of the regarded locking effects. Thereby
the force-displacement behaviour exceeds the accuracy of Abaqus elements in some load
cases. Based on those results, the element is rated to be suitable for the targeted blade
simulation.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In order to become climate neutral until 2050, the European Union plans to get 40% of
their energy form sustainable sources in 2030 [1]. Having reached 21.8% in 2021 [2], this
and other ambitious international climate goals show the need for the fast expansion of all
sustainable energy sources in the upcoming years. In the case of wind energy this means
that not only the number, but also the capacity of wind turbines needs to increase as
fast as possible. The increased capacity is achieved through larger rotor diameters. For
longer blades, the gravitational loads which are a main source for fatigue become more
important relative to the aerodynamic loading [3].
This means that any potential for
weight reduction should be exploited in order to optimize the structural design and life
time of the blades. This is one of the reasons why high resolution structural analysis is
crucial for such large structures. Moreover, a higher accuracy in the simulation of the rotor
decreases the risk of unexpected results in the testing phase [4]. This reduces costs and
accelerates the development of new wind turbine generations. Overall, good structural
simulations are one of the keys to increase the capacity of future wind turbines.
Finite element method (FEM) is a simulation tool in which structures are divided in a
chosen number of polygons or prisms called elements. The structural analysis in each
element can be easily done with the help of continuum mechanics. Thereby every element
parameter is defined as a field variable that can be integrated over the element volume [5].
Following on that, large irregular structures can be solved as the combination of all the
small elements they are composed of. According to D.S. Malkus, R.J. Witt, M.E. Plesha
and R.D. Cook [5], FEM is the only method that combines the following characteristics:
• Applicable to any field problem
• No restrictions to geometry
• No restrictions to boundary conditions
• No restrictions to loading
• Allows orthotropic materials
• Different materials can be used in different parts of the structure
• Different element types can be used in different parts of the structure
• The accuracy can be adapted across the structure by varying the element size
Finite element analysis has been known since the late 19th century [5]. The concept to
transform continuous structures into discrete models can be used in many different ways
1
==PAGE:14==
and application fields. This is why over time, a large variety of element types and use
cases have been developed. Typical parameters that vary with the element definition are:
• The number of simulated dimensions
• The geometry of the element, e.g. the number of corners in the case of polygons
• The integration method
• The number of integration points and the order of the integrated fields
• The use of reduction techniques
• The implementation of non-mechanic parameters like temperature and magnetic
fields
When setting up a finite element model, the choice of all those options comes down to
a trade-off between two opposing aspects: the fidelity and accuracy of the solution vs.
the computational load needed for the simulation. Depending on the weighting of those
two aspects and possible simplifications, the appropriate type of the finite element can be
determined.
For preliminary examinations, a low complexity and high computational eﬀiciency is the
most important factor. If the targeted geometry can be simplified to a prismatic shape,
the use of beam theory is one option to reach this goal [5]. In beam theory FEM models,
structures are defined by cross sections. In those cross sections, a full structural analysis
is conducted leading to an accurate stiffness matrix and mass properties even for complex
internal geometry [6]. Then those results are interpolated along the beam towards the next
cross section. This means that beam theory is very accurate for prismatic geometries, but
cannot take longitudinal variations into account. A beam theory model does not need
much computational time as each prismatic section only requires one finite element. For
the simulation of wind turbine blades, beam theory has advantages and diﬀiculties: On
the one hand, blades have prismatic sections over the majority of their length. This means
that the simulation with beam theory is a perfectly suited and computationally eﬀicient
approach on those large areas. On the other hand, the root, tip and inside of blades which
are extremely important for the overall behaviour cannot be properly addressed in beam
models. On the tip, attachments like winglets can cause problems because curvature is
introduced [7]. Within the blades, the reinforcement structures are often not designed in
a prismatic way [4]. In the root, rotor blades have a tapered section going from a round
cross section to the first airfoil [4]. All those parts of the wind turbine blades are not
prismatic and therefore cause inaccuracy in the beam model. It is the main purpose of
this work to create a simulation tool that can represent those irregularities in great detail.
Some beam model applications such as BECAS created at DTU include enhancements
extending the capabilities of beam theory [8]. They are highly valuable for broad and fast
assessments, but not meant to take longitudinal geometrical variations into account.
In order to create a high fidelity model for wind turbine blades, the material specification
needs to be taken into account. Rotor blades are made of fibre composite materials [4].
This means that they consist of several material layers which have different properties. As
a result, 2D elements with a constant thickness are not an option for this simulation. The
same counts for simple brick elements because they lack the ability to respect the layering
[5]. Shell elements are intended for thin flat or curved structures and allow several layers
through the thickness in one element as in composite materials [5]. Thereby following
classical shell theory, those elements are defined only by nodes in the shell midsurface
with translational and rotational degrees of freedom [5]. This means that classical shell
2
==PAGE:15==
elements have no thickness strain and stress which makes them inaccurate in cases of eg.
shell stretching and shell connections [9]. Solid shell elements combine the capabilities of
classical shell elements with the 3D description of solid elements. Their geometry is defined
through nodes on the upper and lower shell surface whereas only translational degrees of
freedom are enabled [9]. As thickness strains and stresses are needed for the rotor blade
simulation for example to examine delamination [10], this work chose to implement a solid
shell element.
Both classical shell and solid shell elements experience locking effects [10]. This means
that in some situations, the elements behave stiffer or more flexible than expected. Those
locking effects can be reduced or eliminated by implementing enhancements in the element
formulation. In order to choose the order of the shell element, find out which locking effects
need to be considered and which enhancements are promising for the elimination of those
locking effects, different approaches found in literature are reviewed.
When linear interpolation functions are used to describe the displacement vector field, the
strain vector field is automatically constant in some directions as it results from partial
derivatives of the displacement vector field. In order to recover some chosen degrees of
freedom for linear strains, the enhanced assumed strain (EAS) method was developed.
This is done by introducing an incompatible strain in combination with the compatible
strain. One of the first extensive analyses on the enhanced assumed strain method was
published in 1990 by J. C. Simo and M. S. Rifa [11]. They tested this method on different
elements, compared it to assumed stress methods and concluded that enhanced strain
mixed methods are very suitable especially for non-linear analysis.
Vu-Quoc and Tan published two papers looking at solid shell finite elements. The first
[12] looked at static models, the second [13] looked at dynamics. In the static paper,
they did an analysis on the EAS interpolation matrix. By varying the number of internal
EAS parameters, they compared different interpolation matrices in terms of accuracy and
computational eﬀiciency. Their conclusion was that an EAS interpolation matrix with 7
internal parameters is optimal [12].
Another big contribution to the optimization of shell elements was delivered by S. Klinkel
and W. Wagner. In a first paper, they applied the enhanced assumed strain method on an
8-node brick element [14]. Thereby they chose to enhance the strain field to a full trilinear
field leading to 30 EAS parameters. With a focus on geometrical non-linearity, they found
out that this element could be used on beam and shell applications where it behaved
similar as beam and shell elements. Later they published another paper together with
F. Gruttmann proposing a second optimized solid shell element type [15]. In this paper,
they evaluate different numbers of EAS parameters and different versions of the assumed
natural strain interpolation (ANS). The ANS method is a re-interpolation enhancement
that can be used to eliminate different locking effects caused by large element deformation.
For their analysis, they provide detailed information about tests done on beam, plate and
panel structures with different combinations of both methods. They conclude that for the
non-linear analysis of thin shells, the solid shell element with 5 EAS parameters and all
ANS re-interpolations is most beneficial [15].
The previously mentioned authors all chose their solid shell elements with 8 nodes and
trilinear shape functions [12, 13, 14, 15].
For cuboid shapes, this presents the lowest
possible order and shows that their approach was to reach a high eﬀiciency by combining
low-order elements with enhancements instead of adding more nodes or quadratic shape
functions. M. Harnau and K. Schweizerhof analyzed this decision by comparing an 8-
node solid shell element with linear shape functions to an 18-node solid shell element
3
==PAGE:16==
with quadratic shape functions and applying the EAS and ANS among other methods to
both of them[9]. Their result was that the quadratic interpolation is especially useful for
curved elements. Moreover the quadratic element did not experience trapezoidal locking
and volumetric locking. For the linear element, trapezoidal locking could be eliminated
with the ANS method. Volumetric locking has no significance for this work as it only
appears in the case of incompressible materials [9].
A paper written by L. Johansen and E. Lund in 2009 yields to sum up a big part of the
previous research using the information for an optimized solid shell element [16]. Again,
the proposed element is an 8-node solid shell element with tri-linear shape functions. It
uses the EAS method with 7 parameters as proposed in [12], all ANS interpolations as
proposed in [15] and a through-thickness orthogonalization as proposed in [10] in order
to avoid locking effects.
The paper verifies the correct structural response to fully 3-
dimensional problems [16].
Moreover failure effects are successfully simulated and an
analysis of the impact of singular elements is made.
It is the goal of this work to reproduce the element proposed by Johansen and Lund, test
it against known FEM weaknesses and evaluate the choices of enhancements and their
eﬀiciency. This testing phase then leads to the question if this 8-node enhanced solid shell
element is suitable to describe wind turbine blades in high-resolution models.
The first focus lies on the accuracy aspect of the element. Does is react well to simple linear
loads? Does it experience shear locking under pure bending and if so, do the enhancements
eliminate this successfully? Is it possible to add more than one layer in the material and
to choose the fibre direction implementing a transversely isotropic material? After those
tests on single elements, another element is added to answer the questions if the coupling
between elements is well implemented and if the enhanced element prevents trapezoidal
locking. For more complex testing, an arc and a plate are introduced. Plates present
a challenging situation for finite element analysis due to the high amount of boundary
conditions resulting in hourglassing effects and a high stiffness. The response of the element
in plate structures is examined in convergence studies on regular and distorted meshes.
The topic of geometrical non-linearities is not addressed in this work.
Finally with the knowledge gained from all the tests and analyses conducted throughout
this work, an overall assessment of the element can be formulated. Thereby the focus lies on
the suitability for high-resolution blade simulations representing longitudinal geometrical
variations and opening the door for new structural optimizations.

iteration, computation time is saved. At this point, the constitutive matrix has not been
transformed according to fiber orientation. This is done at the level of integration points
with the transformation matrix Te as further described in section 2.5.

Within the element integration, the first step is to calculate the shape functions NI, their
derivatives NI,i, the covariant and contravariant bases gi and gi at each of the integra-

description of the integration and geometry connecting the element with the global scale.
Following on that, the transformation matrix Te is determined and used to rotate the
constitutive matrix C in the desired reference frame.
The strain-displacement matrix B is calculated as explained in section 2.6. Then finally,
the stiffness matrix in each integration point can be determined and assembled in the
element stiffness matrix Ke.
As highlighted in green in the code structure diagram, the enhancements added to the
element formulation are implemented at different points of the code. They are elaborated
on further in section 2.10.
The global stiffness matrix K is then obtained by assembling all the element stiffness
matrices Ke according to the map connecting elements to their nodes.
The resulting
matrix has one column and one row for every degree of freedom in the structure.
In
this case, this corresponds to three translational degrees of freedom per node. Before the
displacement can be determined, the boundary conditions need to be enforced. The whole
process of the assembly and enforcement of boundary conditions is further explained in
section 2.8.
Finally, the system is solved for the nodal displacement in the whole structure. The post-
processing of the code contains the determination of the stresses and strains in a point
of choice. The strain-displacement matrix B and constitutive matrix C are recalculated
at the chosen point that can be any other point inside the element.
The solving and
post-processing is elaborated on in section 2.9.
2.2
Coordinate systems
Three coordinate systems are used in the definition and calculation of this finite element
simulation. The first one is the orthonormal system generally referred to as the global
coordinate system Cg. This is used for the general description of the structure and elements
in the inputs and outputs of the finite element method. Coordinates formulated in this
system are referred to with the coordinates x, y and z.
For calculations within specific elements, a local curvilinear coordinate system Ce is used
as visualized in fig. 2.3 [16]. This coordinate system is defined by the element geometry.
Thereby every node is set to coordinates of ±1 in the different directions respectively as
shown in fig. 2.3. This has the advantage that every element shape is transformed to a
cubic element with parallel edges which makes it easier to reach consistent field values
with the interpolation described by the shape functions. The figure also shows the node
numbering used within the element. It is chosen this way to be consistent with the node
numbering in Abaqus elements for easy comparison. The axes in Ce are called ξ1, ξ2 and
ξ3.
The third coordinate system is the material coordinate system Cm. This coordinate system
is gained by a rotation of the global coordinate system Cg with the angles θ, ψ and ϕ as
further described in section 2.5.
Parameters and coordinates given in this coordinate
system carry the subscripts 1,2 and 3 as for example the material parameters.

2.3
Shape functions
Shape functions NI are one of the most important tools in the analysis of a finite element.
Their function is to approximate continuous fields by a finite and discrete set of numbers.
This is why they serve as a basis for the analysis where every variable is defined as a
field variable. The shape functions are defined in the isoparametric element space. This
simplifies the analysis as all elements use the exact same shape functions regardless of
their physical shape. So there is one shape function NI for every node I taking the value
1 at this node and 0 at all the others.

The shape functions NI are defined as polynomials whereas their degree is a free choice
[5]. The higher the degree of the shape functions, the higher the continuity of the field
values between two elements. In the case of trilinear interpolation, this means that the
field value varies linearly in each direction within an element and meets the value in
the neighbouring element where as the slope between elements would have a kink. Its
derivative is constant within an element and changes the value at the element border.
While this is the lowest practical interpolation order, it is used in this case due to its
high eﬀiciency. In this case, eﬀiciency means that the element deformation is described
with a low number of degrees of freedom leading to low computational costs.
As the
created element is 3-dimensional, the resulting shape functions are called trilinear shape
functions. The order of the shape functions also determines the number of nodes and
integration points needed in one element. In every interpolation direction 1, 2 or 3 points
are required to describe the constant, linear or quadratic behaviour in this direction. This
means that trilinear elements are fully described by 2 · 2 · 2 = 8 nodes while a quadratic
behaviour in x- and y-direction would require 3 · 3 · 2 = 18 nodes and integration points.

2.4.2
Curvilinear approach
The second approach is referred to as the curvilinear approach in this work. It basically
turns the use of the covariant and contravariant bases around. This approach is further
called the curvilinear approach as the outputs from the strain-displacement matrix B are
given in the curvilinear element coordinate system Ce. This means that in the strain-
displacement matrix, the contravarient base vectors gi are replaced by the covariant base
vectors gi. Section 2.5 and section 2.6 explain how this approach appears in the formula-
tion of the strain-displacement matrix B and the element transformation matrix Te.
This approach is used by several papers in connection with solid shell elements [15, 16].
Both papers do not comment on the reason for this choice or its origin. Assuming that the
origin lies within the enhanced assumed strain method, the original paper of J.C. Simo
and M.S. Rifai [11] was consulted. They refer to a finite element textbook that uses the
cartesian approach [17]. Another paper written by U. Andelfinger and E. Ramm using
EAS also chooses the cartesian approach [18]. This means that the origin of the approach
can not be found here. R. Hauptmann [10] mentions that the curvilinear approach can be
computationally cheaper in some use cases. It is therefore assumed that this is the reason
for the implementation of this approach.

2.8
Global assembly and boundary conditions
The global stiffness matrix K has one row and one column for every degree of freedom
in the system which is three times the total number of nodes. For the assembly, the map
connecting each element to the structure nodes is needed. With this map, each element
degree of freedom k or l can be transformed into the corresponding structure degree of
freedom i or j. Then the assembly of K can be done with
Kij = Kij + Ke,kl.
(2.28)
When K is fully assembled, the boundary conditions can be enforced. This is done for
K by setting the row corresponding to the restricted degree of freedom to zero.
The
only exception is the diagonal entry that needs to be 1 in order to preserve a full rank.
Moreover, the diagonal entry allows to use a displacement different from zero as boundary
condition. All deleted entries of K are stored in a new matrix Ksave of same size as K
for the use in the post-processing of the reaction forces. The enforcement is also done on
the load vector f setting every restricted degree of freedom to the specified displacement
that is given by the boundary condition. After this measure, the load vector f and global
stiffness matrix K are ready to be used for the determination of the displacement vector
u.

2.10.1
ANS method against shear locking
Shear locking is an effect that happens in pure bending load cases for elements of low
order shape functions [10]. As fig. 2.5 (b) visualizes, the trilinear element cannot deform
with a curvature as the physical element (a) would. In those cases, the vertical edges
of the deformed element are not perpendicular to the element mid-surface.

the compression on one side and stretching on the other side, this is interpreted as an
additional shear load in the Gauss points. The combination of bending stiffness and shear
stiffness leads to a lower deformation than expected. Especially in thin shells where the
shear stiffness is much higher than the stiffness against bending, this shear locking effect
becomes very strong [10].-

2.10.3
Through-thickness orthogonalization against trapezoidal locking
For big distortions, another locking effect is described in [16] when vertical edges are not
perpendicular to the element mid-surface. This effect is called oblique director locking,
but the distinction between trapezoidal locking and oblique director locking is not clear to
the author. It is therefore assumed that trapezoidal locking and oblique director locking
are the same phenomenon.
According to [16], the locking effect can be eliminated by
enforcing orthogonality in the covariant base. This means that the thickness base vector
g3 is manipulated by setting g3(2) = g3(3) = 0. This mechanism originates in the concept
of degenerated shell elements [10]. This concept includes several assumptions and one of
them is that the normal vector always stays normal to the element mid-surface in non-
deformed elements [10]. By implementing this assumption in the solid shell concept, the
reduced definition of the covariant base is reached. For distorted elements, this means
that even though the coordinates of the element remain distorted, the integration over the
thickness will assume vertical edges. In the following course of this work, this enhancement
will be referred to as through-thickness orthogonalization.
2.10.4

Membrane locking can appear if bending is combined with additional membrane stresses.
This can happen for example when the element mid-surface is not a neutral surface and can
be caused by asymmetric material properties [5]. Another effect called thickness locking
arises due to the assumption that the deformation varies linearly through the thickness
[16]. This happens as the strains ϵxx and ϵyy have a linear variation and are coupled to
ϵzz through the Poisson effect [9].

freedom that are reduced by the partial derivations in the strain formulation in eq. (2.19).

The code for this project is written in C++. For the element calculation, the Eigen package

3.1
Verification methods
The verification of the created element is done with two different methods. The first are
analytical calculations. Those are helpful for simple single element tests. Moreover for
example for plate tests, the calculation reveals the limit towards which the model should
converge.
For more complex cases, analytical solutions do not agree with the expected FEM results as
discretization errors appear. Moreover it is not always the goal to reach perfect agreement
with theory when verifying a basic finite element model. This is why the second verification
method is the comparison to Abaqus elements [22]. This makes it possible to analyse bigger
structures and to compare the different simulations to models that are created in a similar
way and should lead to the same accuracy. Therefore different element types in Abaqus
are used.
The C3D8 element is the simple 8-node linear brick element in Abaqus. It is fully inte-
grated which means that it has 2x2x2 integration points. This means that this element
has the same shape functions as the C++ model and should correspond to it when no
enhancements are used.
When the same element is used with reduced order modelling, the integration points
reduce to one integration point in the element center. This element is called C3D8R in
Abaqus. It carries some risks as the example of pure bending shows. In this case the only
integration point lies in the neutral axis of the element which makes the material seem
to be very weak leading to highly overestimated deformation. In the created solid shell
element, reduced integration is not used which is why this Abaqus element is only used
for the comparison and analysis of Abaqus results and not for the direct verification of
C++ models.
Another variation of the C3D8 element is the C3D8I element whereas I stands for incom-
patible modes. This means that additional degrees of freedom are added at element edges
away from nodes by adding an enhancement to the strain-displacement matrix. This can
allow quadratic behaviour in the desired places even though the general interpolation is
still linear. On the other hand, those modes allow that neighbouring elements have gaps
between their surfaces or overlap because the nodal deformation is no longer containing
all geometrical information that is relevant for the surface behaviour. Because of this
23
==PAGE:36==
phenomenon, the technique is called incompatible [5]. Abaqus promises that shear locking
is totally eliminated by the use of the incompatible modes in the C3D8I element [23].
SC8R is the continuum shell element in Abaqus. Continuum shell means that this element
has 8 nodes and only translatory degrees of freedom like a continuum element while the
response is shell-like [24]. As the C3D8R element, this element has reduced integration
which makes it unsuitable for some applications.
The last element which is used for verification is the CSS8 element. This is a continuum
solid shell element. The CSS8 element is equal to the C3D8I element with an additional
ANS enhancement.
For different load cases, the different enhancements will be used separately and in com-
bination. In order to ensure an easy overview over the use of enhancements and base
approaches, a nomenclature for the new element has been developed. It is dislayed in

Transversely isotropic material
The pure tension load case is repeated for an element with transversely isotropic material.

first property, the material is rotated. Fig. 3.5 shows the deformation for 4 different fibre

The case of ϕ = π/2 is especially interesting because the load acts only in the symmetry
plane of the material. This means that the reaction should be the same as in an isotropic
material with the properties E = E22 and ν = ν13. In the case of the example, E22 = E11/2
and ν13 = ν23 which means that the deformation should double in comparision to ϕ = 0

validated by the result which gives an x-deformation of a = 0.4 and a poisson contraction
of b = −0.12.
The remaining fibre angles are confirmed through a script implemented in Maple [27] that
implements the coordinate transformation in the tension calculation [26]. The performed
tests lead to the conclusion that the implementation of transversely isotropic materials is
successful and prepared to be used in more complex applications. The exact deformation
for all angles is given in appendix A.

3.11
Summary of the verification tests
A variety of tests have been executed to verify the proposed element in different load
cases.
First, it was verified that for a single cubic isotropic element with side length
2, the force-displacement behaviour under pure tension and pure shear agrees with the
analytical solutions. Those load cases proved to be equal for the cartesian and curvilinear
approach and require no enhancements.
Following on that, pure bending was applied
on the same element which verified that the ANS enhancement completely eliminates
shear locking. After the confirmation of those basic load cases for isotropic materials,
the implementation of transversely isotropic materials and several layers were validated.
Thereby it was proved that the difference between an implementation according to the
law of mixture and the accurate inplementation according to the position of layers within
the element can be significant. In a shape analysis, the error in the cartesian element
description was proved in comparison to the curvilinear formulation which could be scaled
correctly. The error could be tracked down in the shear rows of the strain-displacement
matrix. The corrected strain-displacement matrix was implemented and used on a relevant
test proving that it can be scaled correctly with different element shapes. Three different
tests have been executed with oblique directors. Those tests showed that the through-
thickness orthogonalization enhancement resolves the trapezoidal locking completely for
some cases and eliminates the large scale error for other cases. In those cases, the ANS
method in thickness direction proved to improve the displacement behaviour notably when
it was used in combination with the through-thickness orthogonalization. Moreover, the
third oblique director test which combines shear, oblique director and membrane locking
showed that all 4 proposed enhancements were required to obtain the best displacement
behaviour and that this was even closer to the reference result than the enhanced Abaqus
element. In a pure tensile load case where the oblique directors had no effect on the non-
enhanced element displacement, the enhancements introduced a slight error. Finally, a
test on a clamped plate was carried out in order to validate the effect of the EAS method.
In this test, the enhanced element proved to deform more accurately for a coarse mesh
than all elements that were tested in Abaqus. For a distorted mesh, the deformation was
similarily stable as other results reported in literature.

Chapter 4
Conclusion
The layered solid shell element was implemented successfully. The behaviour of the nodal
displacement, strain tensor and stress tensor were tested against analytical results and
Abaqus in various test cases. Thereby all 4 enhancements proposed by L. Johansen and
E. Lund [16] could be verified. Apart from isotropic materials with and without Poisson
effect, transversely isotropic materials were successfully implemented both alone and in
layered materials. In the layering, it could be demonstrated that the difference between
the law of mixture and the more accurate separated formulation is very relevant for some
materials. Moreover, the curvilinear base approach was tested against the cartesian ap-
proach. Thereby the cartesian base approach showed errors for non-cubic elements whereas
the curvilinear approach could be accurately scaled to different element shapes. The cor-
rected strain-displacement matrix was implemented. It could be verified that the scaling
error is eliminated. From this point, it can be expected that the cartesian and curvi-
linear approach reach equal results for all non-enhanced applications. In order to verify
the enhancements for the corrected cartesian approach, more tests are required. Overall,
the N8CUSEOT element was found to have the best force-displacement behaviour. This
is the curvilinear element including all enhancements which means that it is the exact
element proposed in [16]. In the more complex cases in an arc and a plate, this element
even reached results closer to the analytical solutions than the tested Abaqus elements.
Hourglassing was not experienced in any of the tested load cases.
Overall, the choice of an 8-node layered solid shell element proved to be reasonable for
the application in fibre composite structures as wind turbine blades. The brick-like im-
plementation of thickness strains proved to be valuable and the shell-like possibility of
laminated materials is crucial for fibre composite materials. The verification showed that
there is no need for more nodes and a quadratic element formulation in order to create
high-resolution simulations.
Based on the verification done in this work, the proposed element is well suited for the cre-
ation of high-fidelity simulations of wind turbine blades. In future analyses, a larger struc-
ture could be examined as for example the truncated wedge proposed by L. Taglialegne
[30]. As this beam is not prismatic, this leads to a combination of locking phenomena and
complex stress and strain fields even for simple load cases. This is a good approach to the
case of wind turbine blades. When the errors in the cartesian base approach are resolved,
the computational eﬀiciency of both approaches could be compared. As R. Hauptmann
indicates, there could be a difference whereas the curvilinear approach would be slightly
more eﬀicient for linear applications [10]. Another extension to the model would be to
take the effects of geometrical non-linearities due to large displacements into account.
59
==PAGE:72==
Thereby the benefits of this measure in terms of accuracy should be evaluated against
the additional computational costs. Finally, the element would be ready for the imple-
mentation in a wind turbine blade model. Different reduction techniques as for example
static condensation and selective mesh refinement could be applied in order to reduce the
computational costs while keeping the high resolution of the solid shell element.
The final simulation will be able to describe the structural behaviour of wind turbine
blades with high accuracy while taking all required longitudinal geometrical variations
into account. This means that the structural impact of the blade tip, root and internal
structures can be analyzed in great detail. As a consequence, specific structural optimiza-
tions and weight reduction at those complex areas will be possible. Those improvements
will facilitate the development of more eﬀicient and long-lasting high-capacity wind tur-
bines and finally make a contribution on the path towards a climate neutral future.
