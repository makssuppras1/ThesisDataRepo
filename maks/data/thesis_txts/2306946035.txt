Personalizing Hearing Care by Adapting Devices to the Mobile Context
by Jonathan KLUBIEN
This paper will introduce two of the main issues for people who wears
hearing aids: The initial ﬁtting process of the device and the tuning and
optimization process that occurs after the user has received the hearing aid.
Background research is completed on both issues before proposing a sepa-
rate solution for each of the issues, both in the form of a mobile application.
For each of the two applications, methods are developed to allow the user
to circumvent the current issues. In order to improve the initial ﬁtting, sev-
eral tests are developed that will both let user know if they have a hearing
deﬁciency as well as providing data for their audiologist to create the initial
setup of the hearing aid. In order to improve the optimization process, an
application is developed that can gather data about the listening environ-
ment of the user when they are experiencing hearing issues. This is done
in order to provide data for the audiologist to improve the listening experi-
ence of the user in various listening environments.
Throughout the report, different aspects of the two applications are pre-
sented, veriﬁed, and discussed before concluding on the problem statement
of the paper.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This paper aims to look at current problems within hearing aids, speciﬁcally
regarding the feedback loop between the user and the audiologist, which is
used to improve the tuning of the hearing aid for the user, as well as the
initial ﬁtting of the device. Initially, the introduction will go through the
motivation for the subject. Based on this motivation, a hypothesis will be
formed, which in turn leads to a desired outcome for the paper. Lastly, a
project plan will be formulated to provide a sense of the overall structure
of the work behind this paper.
1.1
Motivation
1.1.1
Impact on daily life of users
Having a hearing impairment can often lead to a psycho-social factor either
developing or being increased in its scope. In the paper "Satisfaction with
Fitted Hearing Aids. II. An Investigation into the Inﬂuence of Psycho-social
Factors", by T.S. Kapteyn, some of these factors are explored. (Kapteyn,
1977) These factors includes how hearing impairment can lead people to
withdraw more from society, how hearing impairment in elderly people
can be confused with senility, and how people living alone will more often
have a higher level of satisfaction with their hearing aid. These factors de-
scribe how hearing impairments that are not addressed properly can lead
to isolation from the person’s surroundings. With ongoing developments
in sound processing used in hearing aids, these issues does not need to be
as consequential as they will otherwise become. The algorithms designed
to simulate how the brain perceives and ﬁlters sound are being improved
daily, with more and more people starting to use hearing aid devices. These
algorithms cannot however automatically take into account the differences
in individual hearing among people. The values of the variables are dif-
ferent between people, and these values must be discovered prior to the
user receiving a hearing aid, in order to give the user the experience, that a
hearing aid device will solve their current issues.
1.1.2
Impact on signiﬁcant others
A person with a hearing impairment is not alone in being affected by the
disability. Other people in their life, friends and family, are burdened by
the situation as well. The article "Outcomes of hearing aid ﬁtting for older
people with hearing aid impairment and their signiﬁcant others", by Stark
and Hickson, explores this issue. (Stark and Hickson, 2004), where they
write: "... both the person with hearing impairment and the signiﬁcant

other (SO) will experience effects as a result of the impairment and subse-
quent rehabilitation." If we use this perspective, then both the user and their
signiﬁcant others are stakeholders in this feedback loop of the hearing aid.
The feedback loop describes how the user of the hearing aid reports their
experiences back to an audiologist, who will then try to change parameters
to improve the experience. The feedback loop is currently the only method
for the user to improve their listening experience, after they have received
their hearing aid device. Bearing that in mind, it might prove useful for the
signiﬁcant others to be included in the feedback loop.
This leads us to an overall hypothesis for this paper.
1.2
Problem statement
Users of hearing aids are faced with two main problems.
1. The initial tuning of the hearing aid is often completed using very little
information on the speciﬁc hearing deﬁciencies of the user.
2. The hearing device does not adapt to changing environments of the user
throughout the day.
Can mobile applications gather data from the user that will improve the
initial ﬁtting of a hearing aid as well as estimating the activity of the user in
order to suggest different settings throughout their day.
1.3
Desired outcome
The desired outcome of this project is to identify the underlying issues be-
hind initial ﬁtting of hearing aids as well as adapting the hearing aid based
on contextual data. The goal is furthermore to develop methods for the
front- and back-end of two independent mobile applications that can solve
these issues. One application will be a pre-sales application that can be used
before the user acquires a hearing aid device. This application will help the
user and audiologist in determining the extent of the user’s hearing loss
and will help provide data for the initial ﬁtting of the device. The other
application will be post-sales, and can be used alongside the hearing aid
device. This application will gather data about the listening context of the
user in order to determine when problems occur, and if the device should
change its settings.
1.4
Project plan
This paper will start by researching subjects that have importance to hear-
ing aid device usage as well as their users. It will look into how the pro-
cess of data gathering is currently done, what data is gathered, and which
parties are involved in this process. After this, an iterative process begins
where different aspects of the two applications will be in development.
Firstly, this paper will look at the gathering of data for the post-sales app,
and what this data can be used for. Secondly, the design for gathering and

Chapter 2
Background
This chapter will delve into topics that are related to hearing aid device
usage. A brief overview of hearing and hearing loss will be given, followed
by a description of the current feedback loop and the issues within it. The
stakeholders in this project will also be provided in this chapter. In the
end, an idea for the two applications will be given. This idea will then be
changed and improved upon throughput the paper.
2.1
What is hearing loss
There are three types of hearing loss: Conductive hearing loss, Sensorineu-
ral hearing Loss, and Mixed hearing loss (Association, 2016c).
Conductive hearing loss occurs when sound cannot travel properly from
the outer ear to the inner ear, due to some type of blockage. That is to say,
there is a problem with the outer or middle ear. The causes of this type of
hearing loss includes for instance ear infection, allergies, impacted earwax
and more (Association, 2016a). This type of hearing loss is often temporary
due to causes such as the ones described above being easily treatable. For
this reason, conductive hearing loss will not be the focus of this paper.
Sensorineural hearing loss can occur in a person due to damage to the
cochlea (inner ear), or the nerve-pathways between the coclea and the brain.
Sensorineural hearing loss cannot be treated medically, and cannot be im-
proved through surgery (Association, 2016b. This type of hearing loss is
normally treated through the use of a hearing aid device. Sensorineural
hearing loss will be the focus of this section as well as the rest of this thesis.
As the name suggests, Mixed hearing loss is a combination of condictive
and sensorineural hearing loss. This type of hearing loss will not be ex-
plored further, since the two (conductive and sensorineural) are indepen-
dent from one another.
2.1.1
Sensorineural hearing loss
Sensorineural hearing loss is, as mentioned above, damage to the cochlea or
nerve-pathways. When damage occurs to the cochlea it is due to the hairs
in the inner ear being damaged. The main cause of all types of hearing loss,
and therefore also sensorineural, is aging (WIDEX, 2016), which affects 1 in
7 people over 65 years old (HearingLink, 2016). Other causes can be due

to loud noises, genetics, certain illnesses, drugs, head trauma, and birth-
related issues (WIDEX, 2016).
Regardless of the cause, hearing loss is an issue that a huge amount of
adults deal with on a daily basis. In the United States, the National Insti-
tute of Deafness and Other Communication Disorders (NIDCD) reported

2.2
The current feedback loop
The current feedback loop refers to the current process that patients will
undertake after receiving their hearing aid, in order to optimize the settings
for the device. The process is divided into two sections. The ﬁrst is at
the initial meeting where the user has no prior experience using a hearing
aid, and how sounds are experienced through this device. The second part
can take place several times over an extended period of time. Here, the
user is asked speciﬁc questions about their experiences with the hearing
device, and how they would like to alter the way it outputs sounds to them.
Oticon provides a framework for these processes called Alta. Alta helps
audiologists to personalize the settings of the hearing device in a structured
fashion. The processes, as described below, are found in the Alta Success
Manual. (Oticon, 2016b)
2.2.1
The initial ﬁtting
After being introduced to the hearing aid device, the user will be asked ﬁve
questions, all of which have associated sound samples to help them answer
more precisely. The answers to these questions will form the initial param-
eters for the underlying algorithms that deﬁne how the sound is processed
by the hearing aid. As explained in the paper Perception-Based Personaliza-
tion of Hearing Aids Using Gaussian Processes and Active Learning by Nielsen
et. al: "In practice, the multi-parameter adjustment—traditionally referred to as
ﬁtting—is done in ﬁtting software supplied by the HA company: A restricted set of
meta parameters is available, that controls the entire set of algorithm parameters."
(Nielsen, Nielsen, and Larsen, 2015) In Alta, the meta-parameters in the
initial ﬁtting is determined by the following ﬁve questions or statements,
which have accompanying sound samples.
• I prefer sound to be
– The user must either answer "Sharp and Distinct" or "Soft and
round".
• If I can hear well at a lower volume, I prefer
– The user must either answer "That volume" or "A volume slightly
higher".

2.2.2
The personalization process
The personalization process takes place after the device has been used for
an extended period of time by the user. The process will often be reiterated
several times in order to optimize the experience for the user. A ﬁrst-time
user will wear the device between 2-4 weeks before visiting the audiologist
again. The experienced user will wear the device for around two weeks
before visiting the audiologist and report on the behaviour of the device.
(Oticon, 2016b)
Before the user is sent home by the audiologist, they are explained how to
evaluate the sound of the hearing device. The user is asked to use the Alta
Diary (Oticon, 2016a) application in order to evaluate situations.
The Alta Diary is an iPhone and Android application developed by Oticon
to help users evaluate speciﬁc situations where they had difﬁculties with

2.3
Factors important in determining hearing loss
In order to provide as precise a ﬁtting as possible, several factors should
be used in determining the hearing loss of a user. Three factors will be
explored in this section; The audiogram of the user, their cognitive function
(or working memory capacity), and their temporal and spectral resolution.
2.3.1
Audiogram
FIGURE 2.3: A standard audiogram graph used to plot the
results of the hearing test
An audiogram is the standard output of a normal hearing test (known
as a pure-tone audiometry test). The test is carried out by having a user
be in a quiet environment while listening for tones. When the user hears a
tone, they must indicate so by pressing a button. The tones vary in loud-
ness as well as in frequency. The resulting audiogram output is a standard
x,y graph whereupon the indications of the user is plotted. Typically the
y-axis will contain the loudness in dB, while the x-axis will contain the fre-
quencies in Hz. An example of such a graph can be seen in ﬁgure 2.3.
The determining factor in an audiogram is the threshold of the user in dB.
==PAGE:20==
10
Chapter 2. Background
Normal hearing occurs if the softest sounds that the user can hear (the
threshold) lies between -10 dB and 20 dB. If the user still does not perceive
notes at a higher loudness than 20 dB, a hearing loss is present (Link, 2016).
The hearing test is conducted one time for each ear, and the results are plot-
ted separately on the graph, in order to differentiate between hearing loss
in either ear. A common phenomenon that occurs with increasing age is
losing the ability to hear high-pitched notes. This is known as presbyacu-
sis and would show on the audiogram by the results being within normal
hearing at lower frequencies, but being increasingly worse as the frequency
increases (Peter S. Roland and Joe Walter Kutz, 2016).
2.3.2
Cognitive function
The cognitive function, or working memory capacity, of the user has been
shown to have a relationship with hearing aid use, according to the article
"Cognitive function in relation to hearing aid use by Thomas Lunner (Lunner,
2003). In this article, a span test was used to measure the working-memory
performance of the users. The span test measures how many "chunks" a
person can hold in their working memory. A chunk is an unspeciﬁed item.
It can for instance be a letter or a word. The speciﬁc test used in this article
was developed by Rönnberg and Lyxell in 1989 (“Information-processing
skill and speech-reading”). The users are presented ﬁrst with three sen-
tences. Half the sentences are absurd sentences that does not make sense
and the other half are normal sentences. After the sentences have been
presented, the user is shown either the word "First" or "Final", which indi-
cates that the user must remember either the ﬁrst words or the last words
of each sentence. This will then indicate the working memory capacity of
the user. The working memory capacity was suggested by G. A. Miller to
be 7 plus-or-minus 2 chunks as described in the book Cognition - Exploring
the Science of the Mind by Daniel Reisberg (Reisberg, 2010). Since chunks
are intentionally a non-speciﬁc term, there can be variations in the amount
of chunks that can be remembered. It will depend on what information
and how well-known that information is to the user. An obvious example
would be that Chinese symbols would be harder to store in the working
memory as compared to Latin letters for most western individuals.
The results from the article by Lunner showed a correlation between the
cognitive performance of a user and how well they managed in a demand-
ing listening environment such as speech-in-noise (Lunner, 2003 p. 55).
Looking at the relationship between the cognitive function of the user and
hearing aid use we can try to determine the cause behind it. Daniel Reis-
berg writes that "... someone with a larger-capacity working memory is likely also
to be a more efﬁcient reader, conﬁrming the role for working memory in reading."
(Reisberg, 2010 page 141) If we can translate reading with listening it would
be natural to hypothesise that a large working memory capacity would in-
dicate that someone is a more efﬁcient listener, and then take this factor
into account in regards to the ﬁtting of their hearing aids. The reason for
this correlation would be that when we are reading a given sentence, our
brain will try to process the sentence as we are reading it. As soon as we
have read the ﬁrst couple of words, the brain will try to guess the rest of the
sentence. Similarly, when we are listening to a sentence being spoke to us,

our brain will try to guess how it ends as it is spoken. Reading a sentence
and listening to one should therefore work in very similar fashions.
2.3.3
Temporal and Spectral resolution
Temporal resolution and spectral resolution are together the third compo-
nent in determining hearing loss in a user. Problems within spectral and
temporal resolution describes difﬁculties in distinguishing between tones
that are close either in time, in frequency, or in both. Difﬁculties in distin-
guishing these features of sound are independent of the cognitive function
of the user according to the article "Deﬁcits in auditory temporal and spectral
resolution in language-impaired children" by Wright et. al. (Wright et al., 1997).
In this article, the authors tested children both with and without the disor-
der speciﬁc language impairment. They found that differences in how good
the temporal and spectral resolution of the children was, was independent
from their cognitive performance. This is supported in the article "Auditory
Temporal Processing as a Speciﬁc Deﬁcit Among Dyslexic Readers" by Fostick
et. al. (Fostick, Bar-El, and Ram-Tsur, 2012) where they write "Dyslexic
readers’ deﬁcit on auditory temporal and spectral processing was evident even af-
ter controlling for working memory." on page 85. Even if they are testing for
a relationship to dyslexia, the results still show that temporal and spectral
resolution is independent of working memory. Therefore, if an impairment
is present in the areas that affect the resolution, this impairment should be
taken into account in the hearing aid to counter the effects of the deﬁciency.
A method of measuring the resolution can be found in the article by Fostick
et. al. (Fostick, Bar-El, and Ram-Tsur, 2012). In this article, a test was set
up to determine whether differences could be found between dyslexic and
non-dyslexic readers. All their subjects had normal hearing, since the pur-
pose of the test was to determine if a correlation existed between dyslexia
and temporal and/or spectral resolution, and not to determine the relation-
ship to hearing impairment. The two tests are described brieﬂy below.
Temporal resolution test
The temporal resolution of the test subjects was found using a Gap detec-
tion test. Here the test subject were presented with two tones of 1kHz that
lasted 500 ms. One of the tones was the target tone that contained a gap
between 0.5 ms and 36 ms. in 9 steps. The other tone was the reference
tone with a gap of 0 ms. The reason for the reference tone containing a 0
ms. gap was to avoid test subjects noticing the changing sound envelopes1
of the two tones. The test subjects must indicate which of the two tones is
the target tone. The score for each participant was the percentage of correct
answers after 144 trials.
1A sound envelope describes the attack, sustain, and decay of a sound. Large changes in
envelopes between sounds are easily recognisable.

Spectral resolution test
The spectral resolution of the test subjects was found using a spectral tem-
poral order judgment test. In this test, the test subject is presented with two
tones diotically. One tone is 1kHz and the other is 1.8kHz. Both tones are 40
dB in loudness. Each tone is presented with a random inter-stimulus inter-
val, ranging from 2ms to 240ms in 9 steps. The order of which tone comes
ﬁrst is randomized as well. The test subjects were then asked to indicate
which of the two tones appeared ﬁrst.
Since this test was performed on normal hearing test subjects we can see
the results for non-dyslexic test subjects as possibly being the normal score.
These results vary from approximately 78% to 90% dependent on the inter-
stimulus interval (ISI) between the tones. The results from the article can

2.3.4
How the three work together
The audiogram, the cognitive function, and the temporal and spectral res-
olution are three parts of a puzzle that collectively provides a basis for
determining the extent of hearing loss for the user. The audiogram cov-
ers issues with loudness at different frequencies. Cognitive function ex-
plains how a user might have a harder or easier time distinguishing speech
in difﬁcult-to-hear environments. Temporal and spectral resolution shows
how users can have trouble distinguishing between tones at different fre-
quencies and/or tones that are close to each other in time. Gathering these
three types of information from the user at the ﬁtting phase should there-
fore provide a lot of information for the audiologist to setup the hearing aid
initially.

calendar can be used to determine the location if a location has been entered

3.3.1
Gathering the data
GPS location data
There are two ways of gathering GPS data in iOS. The ﬁrst is the Standard
Location Service (SLS). This service can run in the background and collect
GPS data continuously. The service takes two parameters; the accuracy
of the location data and a threshold for when to report new data. This
threshold determines how many meters should be traveled before collect-
ing new information. Since this service will have the GPS running in the
background a high power consumption should be taken into account.
The second service is the Signiﬁcant-Change Location Service (SCLS). This
service will only report data when a large change in location is detected.
The threshold is around 500 meters. This service will run in the background
and collect data every 15 minutes, regardless of whether the location has
changed beyond the threshold.
A mixture of the two services could be used. The system will start with
SCLS on and check every 15 minutes for large movements. If a large move-
ment is detected, SCLS will be turned off, and SLS will be turned on. The
SLS will then gather data for a minute, then sleep for 5 minutes in a loop
(see what data and the reason for it in section 3.5), until it detects that the
user has not moved for a period of time, say 15 minutes. At this point the
SLS will be turned off and the SCLS will be turned on (Apple, 2016d). An
overview of how this process would work can be seen in ﬁgure 3.2.
Another valuable information would be to know when the user was home
and when they were at work. This could be important if we wanted to
set up speciﬁc parameters for the hearing aid while at these two locations.
Home could be found by seeing where the user would normally sleep.

6.3.1
Pure-tone audiometry test
The speciﬁc test that the application provide for the user is known as Air-
conduction audiometry without masking, and is described in the paper
"Recommended Procedure - Pure-tone air-conduction and bone-conduction
threshold audiometry with and without masking" from The British Society
of Audiology (BSA, 2011). The reason a bone-conduction test is not per-
formed is because it requires a so-called bone vibrator. Bone vibrator head-
phones can be bought by consumers but since most will not own such a pair
of headphones, this paper will not focus on it. It could however be consid-
ered for a future implementation, since it could provide a more accurate
result, and performing both tests could provide even more accuracy.
Test setup and design
The test setup for the air-conduction audiometry test is as follows: The user
is presented with different tone frequencies at different levels of loudness
(dB HL). The user is instructed to press a button when they hear a tone and
==PAGE:47==
6.3. Design of the initial ﬁtting
37
hold the button for as long as they can hear the tone. The test is performed
for both ears, one at a time. The speciﬁc frequencies and loudness levels are
presented as follows (The methods presented here are discussed in (BSA,
2011)):
• Frequencies.
– 1000 Hz, 2000 Hz, 4000 Hz, 8000 Hz, 500 Hz, and 250 Hz.
– The order of the frequencies should be as presented above
• Duration and interval of tones
– The duration of each tone should be between 1 and 3 seconds.
– The interval between each tone should be between 1 and at least
3 seconds.
– This is to make sure the user cannot guess when a tone will ap-
pear, which would bias the result.
• Loudness of tones
– If we assume the user has normal hearing at the start of the test,
we start at 40 dB HL.
– If the user does not indicate that this tone is audible, increase the
dB HL in 5 dB intervals until a response is received.
– If the user indicates that this tone is audible, decrease the dB HL
in 10 dB intervals until response is no longer received.
• Environment
– In order to make sure the user will not be distracted by surround-
ing sounds, they should be instructed to be in a low-noise envi-
ronment.
– The ambient sound level around the user should not exceed 35
dB HL.
• Duration of test
– The full test will normally take around 20 minutes.
– Since some users will have shorter attention span than others, it
can be important to let them take a break during the test.
– The results of the test could otherwise be biased if the user ﬁnds
the task cumbersome.
From the perspective of the user, the implementation of this test needs 4
parts. An explanation of how the test will be carried out, a way to measure
if the environment is quiet enough, a way to start and pause the test, and
a control for the user to indicate that they can hear the tone that is played.
A screen design that includes these factors can be seen in ﬁgure 6.1. The
design of the screen follows the ResearchKit design, like the other screens.
At the top, the text instructing the user how to perform the test is present.
Below to the left is the start button for the test. When the test is started, the
icon will change to a pause icon, which will allow the user to pause the test.

6.3.3
Cognitive function test
When deciding upon a test to accurately measure the cognitive function of
the user there are a few things to keep in mind. The test must be imple-
mentable through a mobile device, and the user must be able to take the
test on their own. While facilitating the test themselves, the impact of bi-
ases on the accuracy of the test should be held as small as possible. When
writing biases here there is meant subconscious biases that the user has no
control over. If the user decides to consciously bias the results of the test, it
cannot be helped.
Daniel Reisberg discusses an operation span test in his book (Reisberg,
2010). This test is very similar to the test described in section 2.3.2. The
difference here is that test subjects are asked to read aloud a series of sen-
tences instead of reading internally. The participants are then asked to read
the ﬁnal words of the sentences, as opposed to either the ﬁrst or the ﬁnal
words of the sentences. It seems plausible that test subjects would per-
form better when reading a sentence aloud instead of reading it internally.
Reading something aloud is after all a common technique to remembering
things like phone numbers. Reisberg writes about this test: "This juggling
of processes, as you move from one part of the task to the next, is exactly what
==PAGE:51==
6.3. Design of the initial ﬁtting
41
working memory must do in its functioning in day-to-day life. Therefore, perfor-
mance in this test is likely to reﬂect the efﬁciency with which working memory
will operate in more natural settings."(Reisberg, 2010 pp. 143-144). Since we
wish to measure the cognitive function (or working memory) performance
of the user in their daily settings, this seems like an apt test for that. A so-
lution to the differing in performance based on whether the sentences are
read aloud can be countered by telling, and stressing the importance of,
reading each sentence aloud to the user. A mixture of the two variations
will therefore be used. The users will be asked to read the sentences aloud
(as in the Reisberg method), and will be asked to either recall the ﬁrst or
last words of each sentences afterwards. Also to make sure that the user
comprehends the sentence, they must answer whether the sentence makes
sense or is non-sensical (as in the Lunner method from section 2.3.2).
Test setup and design
The test starts with an explanation of what the user will have to do. An
example of the explanation can be seen in ﬁgure 6.5 in the leftmost screen.
When the user has read and accepted the information, a test sentence is
given to make sure the user understands his task. Afterwards, the ﬁrst sen-
tence is shown, one word at a time. Each word will be shown for 0.8 sec-
onds, which is the amount of time used by Lunner (Lunner, 2003 page 51).
An example of a nonsensical sentence, also borrowed from Lunner’s article,
can be seen in ﬁgures 6.5 and 6.6, The train sang a song. After all the words
in the sentence has been shown, the user will have to indicate whether this
sentence made sense or was nonsensical. The design for this screen can
be seen in ﬁgure 6.6, in the second screen from the right. First the user
is shown three sentences. If they are able to recall the correct words from
both, this number is incremented by one. This continues up until 6 sen-
tences. The rightmost screen in the same ﬁgure appears after all sentences
has been shown to the user, for each incrementation. In this screen the user
will have to show that they remember either the ﬁrst or the ﬁnal word of
each sentence. The order of ﬁrst and ﬁnal is randomized. A possible issue
with this test could be that the user will have to write down the words in-
stead of simply speaking them to the test conductor. A way of countering
this is to only use sentences with simple words, such as the example sen-
tence given in ﬁgure 6.5/6.6. Another solution could be to let the user pick
the words from a list of words, however that would create a huge bias in re-
gards to helping them remember the sentence. After the test is completed,
a percentage of correct answers will be given. In order for this percentage
to be usable, a normal score should be calculated by performing the test on
a large amount of people. The mean score will then be considered normal,
and the more below the norm a user lies, the hearing aid should take more
into account how it behaves in speech in noise.

This section will describe the biases that can lead to accuracy issues within
the tests. The problems are common for all tests where the user is asked
to make decisions based on audio input from headphones/earphones. The
cognitive function test is therefore not applicable to following discussion.

A factor to consider when setting up such a test on a mobile device is
how accurate the results will be compared to the same test being performed
at an audiologist. Part of this is due to what equipment is available to the
user as compared to the audiologist, speciﬁcally in regards to headphones
and the mobile device itself. According to the British Society of Audiol-

as long as there are no infections, obstructions etc. (BSA, 2011) An image

authors explore whether a smartphone application can be used to create an

valued around $35, which should be within the price range of most users.

headphones were able to produce the loudness levels that were required
to perform the test. In this paper, the microphone from the smartphone
was used to ﬁnd the noise-level of the environment. The maximum vari-
ability was found to be 0.9 dB at 1 1 kHz, 0.6 dB at 2 kHz, and 2.9 dB at 4

that the microphone of the smartphone can ﬁnd the real-time noise-level
in the environment within a reasonable error margin. It should be noted
that the Samsung smartphone used in this paper is not high-end, and since
this application is being developed for the iPhone, it would be reasonable
to expect better performance from the microphone. In regards to the actual
hearing test, the referral rate between the application developed in the pa-

they ﬁnd with hearing issues.
Another aspect to the accuracy of performing a hearing screening through
a mobile device is the communication that can often be a large part of the
test. When people take this test at a consultation with an audiologist, they
have a constant source for help if they have problems understanding how
to perform the task they have been presented with. This help is harder to
come by when performing the test on a mobile device. It is difﬁcult to com-
pletely remove this problem, however a way to lessen it could be to create a
Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ) after determining what the main issues

Normally, before performing an audiometry test, the users are presented
with an orthoscopic examination by the audiologist. The purpose of this
examination is to among other things, to discover presence of ear wax. If
the user has a large amount of ear wax, the results from the test can be bi-
ased, since an air-conduction test relies on air being able to travel through
the ear canal. This issue is not easily solved, since several often-used reme-
dies for removing ear wax, such as a cotton swab, are as likely to increase
the blockade as they are at diminishing it. Most people however, does not
need to have their ear wax removed (Tonkin, 2016). In cases where the re-
sults will be biased due to a blockade in the ear canal, the correct result will
be given when the user visits an audiologist.

An issue that must be addressed before continuing is regarding playing
tones at a speciﬁc loudness level through the earphones that are connected
to the mobile device of the user. This is particularly important when per-
forming the pure-tone audiometry test, but should also be considered if we
wish to output tones at a speciﬁc loudness level in the spectral and tempo-

The main issue is, that when sound is being played through any device,

the actual dB HL (decibels Hearing Level) will depend on a two things: the
source and the speakers. The source is the device that the sound is com-
ing from. In this case it will be the iPhone of the user. The speakers in
this case are the earphones or headphones of the user. The volume of the
device ranges 0.0 (0%) to 1.0 (100%) The information that is needed is the
dB HL that is outputted when the device is set to a given volume percent-
age. It would then be possible to decide what percentage the device volume
should be in order to output a speciﬁc dB HL.
There are two variables that must be gathered in order to calculate the dB
HL output.
From the source, we need to know the maximum voltage output of the
device jack. The higher the voltage output of the device, the louder it will
be able to play, since it provides the earphones with more power. This
value can change between devices. It can be gathered using a digital os-
cilloscope where the device is connected and is playing sound at 100% of
the device volume. This method was used in a blog article named "MEA-
SUREMENTS: Apple iPhone 4 iPhone 6 audio output." (Archimago, 2014) in
order to retrieve the value for the iPhone 4 and the iPhone 6. The values
were measured at 1.3V for the iPhone 4 and 1.4V for the iPhone 6. The
value for the iPhone 6 will be used in this calculation, however automatic
detection can be created in the application to detect which iPhone model
the user has, and then applying the corresponding maximum voltage out-
put value to the equation. Each iPhone model has an identifying string
that can be checked. The iPhone 6 string identiﬁer is "iPhone7,2". After the

given voltage level (Shure, 2016).

Chapter 7
Discussion & Future
Development
This section will go through each of the two applications, in terms of what
was discovered throughout the different iterations. These ﬁndings will be
related to the problem statement which reads:
_______________________________________
Users of hearing aids are faced with two main problems.
1. The initial tuning of the hearing aid is often completed using very little
information on the speciﬁc hearing deﬁciencies of the user.
2. The hearing device does not adapt to changing environments of the user
throughout the day.
Can mobile applications gather data from the user that will improve the
initial ﬁtting of a hearing aid as well as estimating the activity of the user in
order to suggest different settings throughout their day.
_______________________________________
7.1
Pre-sales application
The original purpose of the pre-sales application was to ﬁnd an alternative
to extremely long waiting periods for determining hearing loss in users.
In order to achieve this, the current practice of performing a pure-tone au-
diometry test was moved to a mobile platform. Allowing the users the
opportunity to take this test themselves, would let them bypass the wait-
ing list, which in particular will help the people who was discouraged by
the amount of time they had to wait for a consultation. The research made
for the development of this application led to a method for performing the
pure-tone audiometry test. This answers the ﬁrst part of the problem state-
ment, which asks if a mobile application can improve the initial ﬁtting, with
a yes. This yes does however come with a few issues.
One of the largest problems with letting users take this test by themselves
is the lack of a professional audiologist to guide them. A large amount of
users, especially within the age-range for hearing aid users, are likely not
to be interested in learning new technology. What is required for getting
these users to embrace a new product is two-fold. The product must pro-
vide them with methods that make their life easier, and the product must
be as user-friendly and easy to learn as possible.
The ﬁrst point seems to already be true of this product, due to the problems
that arise from untreated hearing loss (see section 1.1.1) paired with the
==PAGE:66==
56
Chapter 7. Discussion & Future Development
waiting lists to receive treatment at public clinics. The second point how-
ever, requires much more user testing and likely more alterations before an
answer can be given.
A more precise answer to the ﬁrst part of the problem statement would
then be that; yes, a mobile application can improve the initial ﬁtting, but
the pre-sales application developed in this paper is only part of the answer
as to how.
Within the term improve in the problem statement is not only the chance
to optimize the current methods within the ﬁeld. This paper also sought to
apply new ways, that other methods could be included within the process.
This is done by developing methods for letting the user perform a cognitive
function test as well as a spectral and temporal resolution test. These tests
will allow for more depth and insight into the hearing deﬁciencies of the
user.
Before they able to provide this data, they will have to be tested in order
to ﬁnd the average performance for the tests. When the average is found
it will be possible to for instance heighten the threshold for noise if a user
scores low on the cognitive function test. An issue that has not been pre-
viously addressed in this paper is whether the cognitive function of a user
changes throughout the day. It is possible that at the end of the day, users
would have a lower cognitive function. This could be tested by having the
user perform the test over a period of time at speciﬁc times of the day. Their
cognitive function would then be based on the average of those tests at that
time of day. Consequently, the hearing aid would then lower or heighten
the noise threshold accordingly.
However in order to use any of this information optimally, it will be nec-
essary to see how much improvement is experienced by the users, when
the data is applied to the sound processing algorithms of the hearing aid
device.
After the second meeting where the ﬁndings were presented at an Oticon
meeting, two audiology students showed interest in further developing this
application from a clinical perspective. From this perspective, testing and
improvement of the data collection methods could be prove useful.
7.2
Post-sales application
From the problem statement at the beginning of this chapter the second
part of the statement asks whether it is possible for a mobile application to
determine the activities of the user.
The purpose of determining this activity is based on the notion that dif-
ferent listening environments demand different variables within the sound
processing algorithms of the hearing aid. This notion is evident by the fact
that Oticon customers already enjoy the ability to switch proﬁles as they
move throughout their day.
==PAGE:67==
7.2. Post-sales application
57
These proﬁle were created from information the user provided to their au-
diologist concerning hearing problems they experienced. The main prob-
lem within this execution is that the proﬁles created for the users were
based on feedback that was provided days or weeks after the hearing issues
occurred. This feedback loop between the audiologist and the user could
therefore easily be improved by using the sensors that the user is constantly
walking around with in their phone, to detect what they are doing.
During iteration I, the different data points that could be gathered from
their mobile device was researched and developed.
While it should be
noted that the analysis of these measure points is in no way infallible in re-
gards to guessing the current activity of the user, they will, in many scenar-
ios, be able to provide a reasonably good estimate. This estimate is however
partly dependant on the user. If the user is very used to entering details for
what they are doing throughout their day into the calendar, the estimate
will have more data available and will consequently more often be correct.
So to answer the second part of the problem statement. Yes, a mobile ap-
plication can be used to gather information about the activities of the user.
Iteration I focused on which of the data that could be gathered would be
useful in determining this activity. Iteration II and III instead focused on
how this data could be presented to the user and how the user could mod-
ify the collected data, in order to make sure that it was correct.
By developing, testing, and altering the design of the post-sales application,
new functions were removed while others were created. The importance of
the design of this application lies in the issue with the automation system
not being able to estimate the activity in all situations. Having an appli-
cation that would guess the correct activity half of the time and provide
correct data for altering the sound processing would not be interesting if it
meant that the other half of estimations would be wrong and would pro-
vide bad data for the sound processing algorithms. Having a design that
allows the user to easily provide the information that the system cannot
guess, provides the second half of this application. When the user can see
that entering this information pays off by improving their hearing experi-
ence, it will provide a large incentive to continue providing the data to the
application.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
Throughout this paper, two applications have been developed. The pre-
sales application, developed to optimize and improve the initial ﬁtting pro-
cedure, and the post-sales application that created to estimate the user’s
activities to allow for more personalized tuning of the hearing aid by an
audiologist.
In the pre-sales application, the current method for the initial ﬁtting pro-
cess was researched. The current methods that are used showed room for
improvement. A method for allowing the user to perform a pure-tone au-
diometry test was created. The standard Oticon ﬁtting questions were also
developed for a mobile context. Together these two represented the transfer
of the current ﬁtting process from its current form, into a mobile application
where users could discover if they had a hearing impairment.
From further research, other methods were found that could give a more
detailed overview of the hearing deﬁciencies of a person.
The ﬁrst was the cognitive function, also known as the working memory
capacity, of the user. From the research, it would appear as though a high
cognitive function in a user would allow them to better cope with difﬁcult
listening environments. The increased coping could then materialize in the
hearing aid as a higher or lower tolerance for noise. A method for testing
the cognitive function of the user in a mobile context was developed in re-
gards to the test design and interface design.
The second was temporal and spectral resolution. If a user performs low
in either or both of these two, they might beneﬁt from having the hearing
correct for it. This is in regards to tones that are close together in either
frequency or in time. A method was developed that allows users to test
their performance in these two categories. The results from how well the
user performed on these tests would then be used by the audiologist when
tuning the hearing aid.
In regards to the post-sales application, the current feedback loop was re-
searched. The process was found to primarily be based on user memory
of past events when they visited their audiologist. The audiologist would
then tune proﬁles to try to improve the experience for the user.
An alternate method for the feedback loop was proposed. In this method,
an application would gather data about the activities of the user. When the
user reported a problem, the system would be try to make an estimate of the
listening environment they were in. The user would then be able to accept
or correct this estimate. Besides providing much more detailed information
==PAGE:70==
60
Chapter 8. Conclusion
for the audiologists to use when tuning the hearing aid proﬁles, it would
provide another beneﬁt to the user.
If the application knows the activity of the user, it will be able to change be-
tween proﬁles based upon it. This way, the users would no longer have to
switch between proﬁles whenever the listening environment changed be-
cause the application would be aware and make the necessary changes for
them.
In the end, both parts of the problem statement was answered with a yes
that contained certain reservations discussed in Chapter 7.
