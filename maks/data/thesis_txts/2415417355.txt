P R E FA C E
This master’s thesis was conducted at DTU Environment, Technical University of
Denmark, Department of Environmental Engineering from August 2017 to March
2018 and constitutes 35 ECTS.
The aim of the project was to determine whether or not a protected wetland in
Odense is affected by an increased water abstraction from a water abstraction site
nearby.

ment starting with landowner Svend Robert Petersen for allowing me to access

A B S T R A C T
The EU Water Framework Directive states that groundwater abstraction must oc-
cur only to a degree to which it does not cause damage to associated groundwater-
dependent terrestrial systems such as wetlands. Hence, there is need for a con-
venient way to determine the resilience of wetlands as well as the hydrological
impact from changes in abstraction schemes. The creation and use of a simple 2D
numeric model or the use of an already existing large-scale 3D integrated model
would be a convenient approach for predicting hydrological impacts.
A wetland on Fyn, located near an abstraction site where the annual abstraction
rate is set to be increased by 400,000 m3, was used as study site, and the hydrolog-
ical impacts and the wetland sensitivity evaluated through ﬁeld monitoring of the
wetland water level, GSI typology and modeling in MIKE SHE using two models
of different complexity and scale.
The applied investigation methods essentially coincide, and overall the wetland
is found to be resilient and not signiﬁcantly affected by the planned increase in
water abstraction nearby.
Thus, ﬁeldwork in combination with model simulations proved useful for esti-
mating the wetland resilience. As for the employed models, different limitations
are associated with each, and their predictive capabilities were found to be useful
as an initial screening tool and for overall assessment of whether a hydrological
impact is likely or not. However, it was not able to be used for accurate predictions
on ﬂow paths and quantiﬁcation of changes in ﬂow on a local scale.

I N T R O D U C T I O N
Wetlands are a transition zone between terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems [6] typi-
cally deﬁned as having a water table near, at or above the land surface, reduced soil
conditions and organic matter accumulation, and ecological communities adapted
to the wet conditions [12].
The wetland hydrology controls the wetland ecology i.e. vegetation, fauna and
biochemistry [12, 30], and is therefore the determining factor in maintaining spe-
ciﬁc types of wetlands [39]. Consequently, the primary source of water is used
as the deciding criteria in wetland classiﬁcation here distinguishing between bogs
(only precipitation dependent), fens (mainly groundwater dependent) and swamps,
marshes and shallow open waters (mainly dependent on surface water) [12].
The primary source of water is also an indicator of the hydrologic interaction be-
tween a wetland and its surrounding environments, as a groundwater fed wetland
interacts more with its surroundings hydrologically than one fed by precipitation
[36], which in turn impact the wetland sensitivity. When hydrologically connected,
activities such as water abstraction causing a downward shift in the water level
may result in negative impacts on a wetland [30]. The hydrologic change may for
example alter pH and redox conditions, increase nutrient availability and reduce
species diversity [31, 41].
However, the wetland-groundwater interaction can vary from being completely
absent or limited to such a degree that water abstraction from local aquifers more
or less does not affect the wetland, to levels of interactions where abstraction of
water from deep regional aquifers at a signiﬁcant distance can have considerable
impacts on the wetland hydrology [50].
Through the European Water Framework Directive (WFD) and the European
Habitat Directive Denmark is obligated to take a holistic approach to protecting
and managing water resources and ecosystems. The Habitat Directive lists a num-
ber of habitats directly or indirectly dependent on groundwater, among these the
groundwater-dependent wetland ecosystems listed as particularly sensitive to en-
vironmental change [17, 30], while the main purpose of the WFD is to promote
sustainable water use. As such, the WFD states that the exploitation of the avail-
able groundwater resource must only occur to a degree at which it does not keep
associated surface waters from maintaining their ecological status or cause dam-
age to groundwater-dependent terrestrial ecosystems [18] such as riparian areas
and wetlands [12].
Avoiding damage to wetlands means maintaining water levels sufﬁciently high
for conservation of the desired species [40]. Predicting impacts on wetlands due to
hydrological changes caused by e.g. water abstraction is therefore necessary when
planning and managing sustainable water abstraction.

1.1
bolbro water abstraction site
Bolbro and Eksercermarken (ﬁgure 1) situated in Odense Vest are two out of the
seven water abstraction sites belonging to VandCenter Syd (VCS) [58]. The sites
are hydrologically connected [47], though for the scope of this project only the
Bolbro site is of interest.
Figure 1: The project site, nearest water abstraction wells and Ryds å (å=stream) and its
tributaries. Top right: Location of project area within Odense municipality. [Au-
thor’s creation based on [14, 34]].
The Bolbro abstraction site was established in 2006 and comprises the three east-
ern wells with DGU numbers 145.2552, 145.2740 and 145.2739 [58]. In 2011 the site
was expanded with the addition of the three western wells (145.3085, 145.3084 and
145.3083) which went into operation in 2013 [58]. All six wells are screened within
the interval 44-89.5 meters below terrain (mbt) [14] in a buried valley aquifer com-
prised of meltwater gravel and coarse sand which is oriented parallel to Middel-
fartvej (seen north of the wells in ﬁgure 1), but with a width down to 400-600 m
in some places [58].
The catchment zone for the Bolbro site stretches west-southwest relative to the
abstraction site, and the groundwater is moving in eastern and northeastern di-
rections [58]. The extent of the catchment zone and potential lines are shown in
ﬁgure A, Appendix A. Also in Appendix A is a proﬁle sketch of the geology in the
catchment, clearly showing the buried valley aquifer (in future referred to as the
primary aquifer or the Bolbro aquifer) as well as another aquifer closer to terrain
level (in future referred to as the secondary aquifer).
As of December 2015, the Municipality of Odense granted VCS permission to
increase the annual water abstraction from the primary aquifer from 1.4 million
m3 to 1.8 million m3. However, the permission is subject to the condition that
VCS must establish a monitoring program by the end of 2018 in order to observe
possible hydrologic changes in areas with valuable nature due to the increased
water abstraction [47].

1.2
streams and protected habitats
Ryds å (å = stream) (ﬁgure 1) north of the Bolbro site is categorized as having a
high ecological status (fauna class 7), while its tributaries have a good ecological
status (fauna class 5) [34, 38]. The fauna class refers to the Danish Stream Fauna
Index (Dansk Vandløbsfauna Index, DVFI) with a scale ranging from 1-7 where 7
is the highest quality class [38]. The ecological state refers to the terms in the WFD,
where “high” is the reference state in which the stream is unaffected by human
activity [3]. According to the goals of the WFD all streams should at least obtain
a good ecological state [3], and as the Ryds å system is already “good” or “high”
these states must be maintained [38].
Model scenarios have shown that an increased water abstraction from Bolbro re-
duces the ﬂow in Ryds å by 2-5 % [45, 58]. Simulations with an annual abstraction
of 1.4 million m3 as well as 1.8 million m3 were run and the resulting difference
was smaller than what could be expected from model uncertainties alone [45, 47].
The stream system will therefore not be considered further in this project.
Figure 2: Black dotted line encircles the area where an additional lowering of the water
table in the secondary aquifer is expected with increased water abstraction at
the Bolbro site. The blue area marks the zone with a potential effect at terrain
level. A small stretch of the Munkerod tributary to Ryds å and the project site
are visible within the pink circle. Modiﬁed from [47].
Other modelling calculations have estimated an area in which the increased
water abstraction from Bolbro might cause an additional lowering of the water
table in the secondary aquifer by 0.25-1 m (black dotted line in ﬁgure 2) [47]. The
blue area in ﬁgure 2 comprises the area where the modelled water table is above
or close to terrain and, hence, where the effect of the increased abstraction might
be observable at terrain level.

1.3
project site
The wetland encompassing the project site is located roughly 1.5 km north of the
western wells, 2 km northwest of the eastern wells at the Bolbro water abstraction
site and approximately 150 m east of the Munkerod tributary to Ryds å. Images of
the tributary and the project site are shown in ﬁgure 4.
A visit to the site in May revealed saturated conditions close to the slope seen in
ﬁgure 4d, while there were dry areas closer to the stream. On later visits in August-
December increasing amounts of ponded water was observed in the wetland and
on the far side of the wetland relative to the slope, while the hillslope remained
saturated.
The extent of the wetland and the other habitats found in the area seem to be
quite consistent when comparing the current map (ﬁgure 3) and the historic maps
(ﬁgure 5). The land use is much the same with only very little urban development
in the surrounding area.

1.4
aim
Based on the issues outlined above the aim of this study is
• to assess the hydrological impact on a speciﬁc wetland due to an increased
water abstraction from the Bolbro abstraction site.
And from a wider perspective
• to examine if it is possible to assess the hydrologic resilience of groundwater-
dependent wetlands through modelling and ﬁeldwork, and evaluate the ca-
pability of the utilized models to predict a realistic future scenario at the
necessary scale.
The objectives will be addressed through ﬁeldwork and numeric modeling with
MIKE SHE.

1.5
overall method
To obtain a conceptual understanding of the wetland and surrounding area with
respect to geology and hydrogeology a desktop study was conducted using infor-
mation from literature as well as existing data from the Bolbro/Munkerod area
speciﬁcally. Additionally, elements of the groundwater-surface water interaction
(GSI) typology were applied to characterize the wetland hydrology (Chapter 2).
The ﬁeldwork consisted of hand augering six boreholes for monitoring of the
wetland water level. To gain an indication of the level of hydrologic connection,
analyses of water samples from the wetland were compared with existing water
analyses from the secondary and primary aquifers (Chapter 3).
A 2D numeric MIKE SHE model was constructed as a basis for understanding
the general hydrologic mechanisms of the area (Chapter 4).
A fully distributed and integrated MIKE SHE model (climate data excluded)
for Odense Vest was provided by The Geological Survey of Denmark and Green-
land (GEUS) and applied to evaluate the effects of different abstraction scenarios
(Chapter 5).
For details on the applied methods see the individual chapters.

1.5.2
mike she
To numerically model changes in groundwater level and water ﬂow through the
wetland the MIKE SHE software was chosen. It is a complex modeling tool capa-
ble of integrating processes such as ﬂow in the saturated and unsaturated zones,
inﬁltration, overland ﬂow, plant uptake etc. [25], which is necessary when want-
ing to obtain a dynamic output showing the effect of seasonal variation and water
abstraction.
The processes and the level of complexity at which they are represented can be
assigned individually and incorporated according to need, data availability and
purpose of the given model. The most important processes may then be calculated
using advanced and computationally demanding physics-based equations, while
less important processes may be represented with a simpler approach. Similarly,
the spatial and temporal distribution may be varied for each process individually
[25].
The ﬂexibility of MIKE SHE and the range of the spatial scale, process com-
plexity and detail in computational layers depending on model purpose are nicely
exempliﬁed by the two models used in this project. The set up of each of the
models is explained in further detail in sections 4.1 and 5.1.

borelogs. Likewise, sand covers a range of sandy descriptions though not including
coarse sand/gravel, but connecting sand and gravel nonetheless as one layer. In the
conceptual model (ﬁgure 8) sand is used as a common term for both sand and

to the assessment of the hydrology at the project site. Furthermore, only few wells

2.3
gsi typology
The following section is based upon the review, descriptions and guides by [9, 10,
12].
GSI is a typology for characterizing the interaction between groundwater, ripar-
ian areas and streams by assessing geomorphology, hydrogeology and ﬂow paths
on three different scales starting with landscape type (catchment scale), then riparian
hydrological type (reach scale) and ﬁnally the riparian ﬂow path type (local scale).
Landscape type classiﬁes the complexity of the groundwater ﬂow processes
based on an assessment at the regional scale. Besides geomorphology, the clas-
siﬁcation criteria include the following four hydrogeological settings:
1. A single dominant unconﬁned aquifer
2. Two interconnected aquifers of equal importance
3. A three-unit system consisting of an unconﬁned aquifer, a conﬁning layer
and a conﬁned aquifer
4. A complexly interbedded sequence of aquifers and conﬁning layers with no
dominant aquifer
18
==PAGE:30==
The GSI typology distinguishes between ﬁve important Danish landscape types:
moraine, outwash plain, raised seaﬂoor, dunes and large freshwater deposits.
The riparian hydrogeological type characterizes the groundwater exchange through
a riparian area aquifer based on the physical contact between a groundwater body
and the riparian area aquifer. The typology lists ﬁve contact types ranging from
no contact to full contact as seen in ﬁgure 15.

local groundwater body Typically shallow and small, connected to the ri-

regional groundwater body Deeper and normally larger than the local ground-

overland Overland ﬂow passing across the riparian area. Short contact time
with riparian deposits. Residence time of hours to days.
direct Flow path beneath riparian area aquifer, through an underlying ground-
water body and directly through the stream bed. Contact with riparian de-
posits beneath stream bed may or may not occur.
drainage Flow through drainage pipes or ditches directly into the stream.

The shape of the riparian area aquifer, i.e. the depth, width and height, inﬂuence
the water exchange. The width and depth inﬂuence the natural ﬂow paths and
residence time in the riparian area aquifer, which in turn inﬂuence biochemical
processes and hence water quality.
The height and slope gradient, on the other hand, has a direct inﬂuence on the
hydraulic gradient as well as the volume and velocity of the water entering the
riparian zone [59].
Both [59] and [5] found that certain topographic characteristics inﬂuence the
hydraulic dynamics of the riparian zone. In a ﬂat riparian zone the stream water
level can become a ﬁxed point around which the water table of the riparian area
aquifer ﬂuctuates, while if the hillslope almost directly borders the stream then
the stream water level does not have an impact on the hydraulic dynamics in
the riparian zone [5]. A ﬂuctuating water table occurs when the recharge varies
seasonally, which, as mentioned above, can occur especially if the source is a local
groundwater aquifer. However, a steep slope in combination with a continuous
hillslope discharge can maintain a gradient toward the stream throughout the year
even on the ﬂat areas of the riparian zone. Conversely, a more gentle slope means
a lower gradient between the upland, riparian area and stream, which increases
the probability of the water table in the riparian area aquifer falling below the
water table in the stream and reversing the ﬂow [59].
Given the slope and width of the project site a ﬂuctuating water table is a pos-
sibility, but the volume of groundwater entering the riparian area aquifer also
largely depends on the contact type between the riparian area aquifer and the
groundwater body. For determining the contact type, the geology proﬁles espe-
cially around boreholes 145.3452, M4, M9 and M11 were considered as well as
the soil map in ﬁgure 16. Adjacent to the riparian area aquifer on either side
are relatively extensive occurrences of sand, which could constitute shallow, local
aquifers.
As described in section 2.1, the peat is generally underlaid with a thin layer of
sand, followed by 10-15 m of clay, followed by the secondary aquifer.
The contact type is then either lateral/bottom local if the shallow, local aquifer(s)
dominate the water exchange, or conﬁned if the low-permeable clay layer is of
greatest importance. If lateral/bottom local, then water will enter the riparian
area aquifer at the hillslope and through the bottom. If conﬁned, then only small
amounts of groundwater pass through. The amount of water permeating through

from the secondary aquifer beneath the clay and to the riparian area aquifer de-
pends on the hydraulic conductivity of the clay and the layer thickness [11].
As explained in section 2.2, the inﬂux of water to the Ryds å system is noticeable
in the potential map of the upper water level, suggesting that the lateral/bottom
local contact type might be a more likely scenario than the conﬁned contact type.
Field observations also support the lateral local contact type. The eastern hills-

2.3.2
flow path type
The ﬁnal classiﬁcation system by which the dominant ﬂow path is determined re-
quires a substantial amount of data, such as hydraulic conductivities of the stream
bed and the riparian area aquifer, baseﬂow, water levels and potentials, seepage
and other elements inﬂuencing the hydrologic dynamics in order to quantify the
ﬂow through each ﬂow path type. Given the limited available data and the fact
that ﬁeldwork for the purpose of quantifying the ﬂow path distribution was not
carried out, because of resource and time constraints, the dominant ﬂow path will
only be estimated qualitatively.
Often one of the main purposes of determining the main ﬂow path type is to
estimate the quality of the water entering the stream. Diffuse ﬂow, for example,
has the longest residence time and hence a greater potential for nitrate reduction.
However, in this case the objective of assessing the ﬂow paths is to determine
whether water ﬂows through the riparian area aquifer and the wetland in question
or if it predominately bypasses it through drainage or direct ﬂow.
Starting with drainage; the location of drainage pipes in the area and thus water
bypassing the riparian area aquifer and discharging directly into the stream was
discussed in section 1.3. And, as mentioned, the drainage pipes do not appear to
be working properly, hence drainage is not assumed to be a dominant ﬂow path.
Overland ﬂow entering the riparian area aquifer through drains ending at the
hillslope or ﬂooding from the river do not seem likely either given the height of the
stream banks compared to the water level in the stream, and the fact that no drain
outlets have been observed on the hillslope. Also, if the groundwater potential
is above terrain and the permeability of neither the riparian area aquifer nor the
stream bed is sufﬁciently high to let the water pass through the subsurface, then
overland ﬂow can occur. A requirement is that the water on terrain is moving and
not stagnant, as is the case at the site in question.
This leaves direct ﬂow and matrix ﬂow as possible dominant ﬂow paths.
With direct ﬂow of groundwater the surface of the riparian zone is quite dry,
because groundwater bypasses the riparian area aquifer and enters the stream
directly through the stream bed. Therefore, a requirement for direct ﬂow to occur
is a good hydraulic connection between the water bodies.
As a rule of thumb, potential lines parallel to a stream indicate a good hydraulic
connection between the deeper regional aquifers and the stream. Conversely, po-
tential lines orthogonal to a stream indicate a poor hydraulic connection, because
22
==PAGE:34==
the groundwater then ﬂows along the stream. Such streams will usually receive
water from local aquifers, but not regional ones.
Considering the above and the general groundwater ﬂow direction, as described
in section 2.2, groundwater from the secondary or primary aquifer could bypass
the riparian area aquifer and enter Ryds å directly if the geological layers and
the corresponding hydraulic conductivities allow it. As discussed in section 2.2,
there appears to be an area just west of the project site where water ﬂows all the
way from the limestone aquifer into the stream. For the Munkerod tributary, the
situation is reversed. The groundwater ﬂow is in the direction along the stream,
indicating a poor hydraulic connection with recharge mainly from local aquifers.

Unfortunately pH was not measured for any of the wetland boreholes, and
hence it is not possible to either conﬁrm or reject whether pH is in fact low at
borehole B2.
Additionally, oxygen was not measured for the wetland boreholes due to a lack
of ﬁeld measuring equipment, though it is a very useful parameter when deter-
mining redox conditions.
Oxic environments have the highest redox potential, followed by nitrate, man-
ganese, iron and sulfate reducing environments and ﬁnally methanogenic condi-
tions [48] The measured manganese and iron concentrations are total concentra-
tions i.e. the sums of oxidated manganese(IV) and reduced manganese(II) and
oxidated iron (III) and reduced iron (II) respectively, hence the individual ion con-
centrations cannot be used in determining the redox state as is normally done.
However, the total concentrations can also be used as indicators. Typically, the
total concentrations are low in oxidized waters but increase as waters become
more reduced. If the iron concentration alone is elevated and the manganese con-
centration low it suggests more reduced conditions than if both are elevated [48].
Conversely, high concentrations of nitrate indicate oxidized or nitrate reducing
conditions [48].
The redox conditions in the wetland are then estimated based on the available
measurements of nitrate, total manganese, total iron and sulfate of the wetland
samples.

145.2722 is located closer to the wetland than the wells in the Bolbro aquifer, and

Similarly, given the difference in water type the wetland presumably does not

4.1.3
climate and land use
Daily values of precipitation and evapotranspiration from January 31st 1990 to
December 31st 2012 from the DMI klimagrid were used as climate input data. The
resolution of the data is 10 x 10 km for the precipitation and 20 x 20 km for the
reference evapotranspiration. For the applied grids see Appendix I.
The actual evapotranspiration depends on soil type and land use [8], which
within the model domain is primarily grass and agricultural land. The soil type
and soil properties related to the amount of water available for evapotranspiration
are described in a later section.
Model parameters related to land use and vegetation are the leaf area index
(LAI) and the root depth (RD) that may vary depending on vegetation type and
season, and for RD also soil type [8]. For simplicity and considering the relative
similarity of the land use throughout the model domain, constant and uniform
values of LAI = 4 and RD = 700 mm corresponding to permanent grass were
applied [22]. For the ET parameters the default values were used, i.e. a canopy
resistance of 0.05 mm and reduced ET below ﬁeld capacity.
4.1.4
processes
Processes included in the model are overland ﬂow, unsaturated zone and saturated
zone for modelling water movement. The water quality component was included
in order to perform particle tracking.
Overland flow
The overland ﬂow component is calculated using the ﬁnite difference method,
which utilizes the diffusive wave approximation of the Saint Venant equations.
For details see [8].
40
==PAGE:52==
When using the ﬁnite difference method three additional parameters are re-
quired:
• The Manning number describing the channel/surface roughness. When mod-
eling channel ﬂow, values are typically in the range from 100 m1/3/s (smooth
channels) to 10 m1/3/s (thickly vegetated channels), but when modelling
overland ﬂow the Manning number is in general lower [8]. Here a Manning
number of 2 m1/3/s was chosen.
• The detention storage regulating the depth that the ponded water must ex-
ceed before it can start ﬂowing across the ground surface, which in the model
was set to 6 mm.
• The initial water depth on the ground surface. Any value but zero will result
in permanent ponded water and an inﬂow of water to the model if the depth
is less than the speciﬁed value [8]. The initial water depth was therefore set
equal to zero.
For all three parameters, the speciﬁed values were inserted as constant and uni-
form throughout the model domain.
Unsaturated zone
The water ﬂow in the unsaturated zone is calculated using the simple Two-Layer
Water Balance approach, in which the unsaturated zone is divided into two layers:
a root zone from which ET occurs and an underlying zone without ET. The two
layers represent average unsaturated soil conditions, the soil proﬁle is uniform
and the calculation of the actual evapotranspiration is simpliﬁed [8].
The main purpose of using the Two-Layer model is to quantify the actual evap-
otranspiration and the groundwater recharge, contrary to more complex and com-
putationally heavy approaches that take into consideration aspects such as the
moisture retention curve and effective conductivity in order to model the ﬂow
dynamics in the unsaturated zone [8].
For the purpose of this model a detailed description of the unsaturated ﬂow
processes is unnecessary, and given the thickness of the top clay layer a uniform
soil proﬁle is sufﬁcient. In addition, the Two-Layer model is well suited in wetlands
or areas in general with a shallow water table [8].
When using the Two-Layer Water Balance method, the soil properties and the
ET surface depth marking the depth at which the evapotranspiration starts to de-
crease must be speciﬁed. As clay is deﬁned as the only soil type in the unsaturated
zone, only one set of soil properties was deﬁned:
• The water content at saturation: 0.366
• The water content at ﬁeld capacity: 0.295
• The water content at wilting point: 0.105
• The saturated hydraulic conductivity (inﬁltration rate): 6 ∗106m/s

with the model domain of the cross-sectional model, and the soil property values
were conﬁrmed with literature values for various soil types. The water contents

Saturated zone
The ﬂow in the saturated zone is calculated using the ﬁnite difference method in
which the 3-dimensional Darcy equation is the governing equation [8].
Using the ﬁnite difference method involves deﬁning the geological model, the
vertical numerical discretization (computation layers), initial conditions and bound-
ary conditions [8]. If pumping wells are a part of the model, the wells are also
deﬁned in the saturated zone component.

Particle tracking
Particle tracking is part of the water quality component, which is a separate sim-

simulation period of 500 years by recycling ﬂow results from 2009-2010, and the
sources of the particles deﬁned as precipitation over the entire model domain and

The initial particle locations were speciﬁed to be random distribution in the cells

tracking method chosen for calculations only considers the saturated zone.

uniform and constant strength of 0.1 µg/m3 was deﬁned.

Four overall aspects, i.e. overland ﬂow depth, seepage ﬂow, hydraulic head and
particle tracking, were the primary focus when processing the simulation results.
Where nothing else is stated results from 2012, the ﬁnal year of the simulation

Overland flow

seepage ﬂow in the following though it is in fact a ﬂux with unit mm/day.

for each time step from 2003-2012. For each combination of pumping rate and

4.3.5
discussion
The behavioral explanations for the observed patterns in seepage ﬂow, hydraulic
head etc. coincide and support each other as the hydrologic processes are linked
together.
Up to and including the high pumping rate, the overland ﬂow depth and seep-
age ﬂow in the wetland are reduced with higher pumping rate and lower hydraulic
conductivity (K3) due to less available water and a larger resistance. For the very
high pumping rate the response is the opposite, because the pumping rate is large
enough to cause the ﬂow direction to switch from upward to downward. Rather
than facilitating a larger ﬂow to the wetland, a high conductivity then enables a
faster ﬂow away from the wetland. In this case, the low conductivity provides a
barrier between the primary aquifer and the overlying layers. The seepage ﬂow
to the wetland then remains positive, though reduced compared to the scenarios
with lower pumping rates.
The mechanisms are naturally also reﬂected in the overland ﬂow depth. With
a high seepage ﬂow and saturated soil, ponding of water is likely to occur, while
inﬁltration is possible with a low or even negative seepage ﬂow and unsaturated
soil.
All processes generally exhibit the same seasonal trend of low points in summer,
due to lower recharge.
54
==PAGE:66==
The part of the wetland which, according to ﬁgure 31 of the overland ﬂow depth,
is least likely to dry out in summer is the area close to or at the hillslope. Compar-
ing to ﬁgures 34-35, this is the area where the seepage ﬂow is greatest.
The distinct shift in the seasonal pattern of the seepage ﬂow for the very high
pumping rate mentioned in the description of ﬁgure 33 is caused by a pronounced
decrease of the hydraulic head in the primary aquifer of several meters (ﬁgure
AF, Appendix K). From ﬁgure AF, Appendix K it is also clear that while the very
high pumping rate overall continuously lowers the hydraulic head with signiﬁcant
effects on seepage ﬂow, the remaining pumping rates appear to be sustainable as
the corresponding hydraulic heads are essentially stable, though with a slight
decrease for the high pumping rate.
The considerable decrease in head in the primary aquifer is not only visible in
the magnitude and direction of the seepage ﬂow; it might also be the explanation
for the distortion of the seasonal pattern of the overland ﬂow depth in ﬁgure 31e,
as well as the cause of the annual increasing seasonal ﬂuctuations of the hydraulic
head in the top clay layer for the very high pumping rate (ﬁgure 40).
The increase in the seasonal ﬂuctuations of the hydraulic head in the top clay
layer with decreasing hydraulic conductivities (especially for the no pumping to
moderate pumping rates) coincides with the reduction in seepage ﬂow under the
same conditions. For the high pumping rate the increase is less prominent, as the
decrease in seepage ﬂow between the scenarios is also less pronounced.
As for the hydraulic heads there are apparent numerical issues in the scenar-
ios with the high and very pumping rates and K3 of 10−8 m/s (ﬁgures Z-AA,
Appendix K), which manifest as improbable curves of the hydraulic head in the
intermediate clay layer. The cause of the instability is likely the drying out of the
aquifer, due to the large pumping rates and a low recharge because of the low
hydraulic conductivity.
The effect of an increased pumping rate on the hydraulic head is evident, but
despite signiﬁcant declines in hydraulic head in large parts of the model domain
for all pumping rates larger than zero compared to the no pumping scenario, the
effects on the wetland are more subtle. It takes a (high to) very high pumping
rate or a K3 of 10−8 m/s before the hydraulic head in the wetland is lowered
substantially below terrain.
The many scenarios discussed so far were created in order to clarify under
which conditions the wetland would be affected, if at all. Hence, not all scenarios,
such as the one constituting one large conﬁned aquifer (K3 of 10−5 m/s), are
realistic, but together they provide useful insights into the hydraulic mechanisms
of the area.
With the assumed baseline condition of K3 of 10−7 m/s, the differences in the
effects on the wetland between the low and moderate pumping rates representing
the 1.4 million m3/yr and 1.8 million m3/yr respectively are minor. The hydraulic
heads and seasonal ﬂuctuations thereof are more or less within the same range,
and likewise for the difference in the overland ﬂow depth. The largest difference
occurs for the seepage ﬂow, which in total is 10-15 mm/day lower with the mod-
erate pumping rate than with the low pumping rate.
55
==PAGE:67==
However, the outcome is only indicative and not directly applicable or quan-
tiﬁable for decision making, as there are a number of limitations associated with
using a cross-sectional model.
First of all the magnitude of the downscaled pumping rates is, as discussed
in section 4.1.4, an uncertainty, and there are effects of essentially having a two-
dimensional aquifer with a limited extent, e.g. that the aquifer might dry out more
quickly. Another subsequent implication might be an elongation of the catchment
zone, as water can only ﬂow to the wells from two sides.
As for the overland ﬂow, it is in reality guided by the topography, ﬂow resis-
tance etc. and chooses a ﬂow path accordingly. In the cross-sectional model there
is in principle only one possible ﬂow path toward the stream, but the water might
also spill over the boundaries and leave the model. In order to contain potential
overland ﬂow within the model, the boundary topography was increased by 5
cm. However, by doing so the maximum overland ﬂow depth is manually de-
termined by the chosen boundary topography elevation, as the water leaves the
model across the boundaries if the depth surpasses 5 cm. The simulated numerical
values of the overland ﬂow depth should therefore not be ascribed great impor-
tance, but merely used as a mean to acquire a graphic overview of which areas
are most likely to dry out, which areas experience the highest overland ﬂow depth
etc.
Another aspect is the uncertainty associated with the chosen parameter values.
The cross-sectional model was not truly calibrated, but the initially chosen baseline
hydraulic conductivities merely adjusted until a reasonable ﬁt with the expected
values (section 2.2) was obtained in a scenario without pumping. None of the
remaining initially estimated parameters were altered.
Despite the limitations of the cross-sectional model, it does appear realistic that
the effect on the wetland from the proposed increase in annual abstraction will
be minor as the primary aquifer needs to be essentially pumped dry before the
wetland is greatly affected according to the simulated results.

adjacent cells. In the eastern 1/3 of the model the runoff coefﬁcient varies from
approximately 0.3-0.9, though coefﬁcients of 0.4-0.6 are most prevalent. In the re-
maining 2/3 of the model domain the runoff coefﬁcients are approximately zero.
The city of Odense is located in the eastern third of the model domain, while
there are large areas of agricultural ﬁelds in the remaining part. The name of the
gridﬁle containing the runoff coefﬁcients is PavedArea, and as such the runoff co-
efﬁcients of 0.3-0.9 ﬁt nicely with the most densely populated areas where the
fraction of impervious areas will be high.
Unsaturated zone
The water movement in the unsaturated zone is calculated with the Two-Layer
Water Balance method, and a number of soil proﬁles with varying water content
at saturation, ﬁeld capacity and wilting point are deﬁned. However, common for
all soil proﬁles is that the saturated hydraulic conductivity is set to 6*10−6 m/s,
with only few exceptions where it instead is set to 6*10−7 m/s.

The ET surface depth is set to 0.0001 m and macropore ﬂow is included as
simple by-pass ﬂow, which means a part of the inﬁltrating water is routed directly
to the groundwater table [8].
Saturated zone
The ﬂow in the saturated zone is calculated with the ﬁnite difference method and
includes pumping wells as well as subsurface drainage, but not dispersion.

Pumping wells
Pumping wells are placed according to their UTM coordinates and the abstraction
rates deﬁned in time series ﬁles.
The well editor contains 2890 pumping wells and the abstraction rates for 70 of

assumption that VCS provided data for their own abstraction wells.
The Bolbro wells are 6 of the mentioned 70 VCS wells. All are screened in the

nual pumping rates varying between 0.66 million m3 to 1.28 million m3 in the
years 2007-2013 according to the mentioned time series ﬁle, yielding an average
annual abstraction of 0.95 million m3.
Particle tracking
The water quality simulation using the results from the water movement simula-
tion was set up for random walk particle tracking with a simulation period of 100
years by recycling ﬂows from 2009.
Precipitation was speciﬁed as the particle source throughout the entire model
domain with initial particle locations distributed randomly within the cells and
placement above the water table avoided. The chemical species was deﬁned as
dissolved, with a particle mass of 10 g and a uniform and constant strength of 0.1
µg/m3.

discussion
The seepage ﬂow to the wetland and the hydraulic head in the wetland both
appear to be largely unaffected by an increase in pumping rate at the Bolbro
abstraction site.
For the two pumping rates representing the former and current maximum al-
lowed annual abstraction rate, the hydraulic head in the top computational layer
(top5m) is lowered by a maximum of 2 cm when the annual pumping rate is in-
creased from 1.4 million m3 to 1.8 million m3, while in the deeper layers such
as ks2, representing the secondary aquifer, the hydraulic head is lowered 16 cm
with the same increase in pumping rate. The impact on the seepage ﬂow seems
to be slightly greater than for the hydraulic head in the wetland; nevertheless, the
seepage ﬂow is only reduced by 2.5 %.
Including the scenario with a pumping rate of 2.7 million m3/yr, which is well
beyond the allowed annual abstraction volume, the lowering of the hydraulic head
in the wetland (top5m layer) is still less than 10 cm compared to the baseline sce-
nario, which is based on actual abstraction rates from the years 2007-2013. How-
ever, in the secondary aquifer (ks2) the hydraulic head is lowered by 71 cm com-
pared to baseline and 53 cm compared to the scenario with a pumping rate of
1.4 million m3/yr. For the same scenarios the average seepage ﬂow is reduced by
71
==PAGE:83==
9.4% and 8.1 % respectively, which shows that it is possible to impact the wetland,
but even with a pumping rate well above that allowed the changes are not severe.
The magnitude of the changes in hydraulic head in the top5m and ks2 layers
respectively indicates a better hydrologic connection between the Bolbro aquifer
and the secondary aquifer (ks2) than between the Bolbro aquifer and the wetland
(top5m). While the changes in hydraulic head in the wetland are quite small, the
maximum change in the secondary aquifer is of the same magnitude as in the
Bolbro aquifer (Dal2) as seen in table 16.
The year to year variations in the seepage ﬂow are larger than the changes
caused by varying the pumping rate, which suggests that the abstraction rate for
the Bolbro wells might not be the sole determining factor. The annual precipitation
volume (ﬁgure AN, Appendix M) correlates with the variations in seepage (table
J, Appendix L). 2007 was the year with the greatest amount of rain and is also the
year with the highest seepage ﬂow. Similarly, the low rainfall volume in 2009 is
reﬂected in the amount of seepage ﬂow.
A similar trend is observed with regard to the hydraulic head. Comparing the
seasonal variation in the wetland (ﬁgure 52) to the monthly precipitation volumes
(ﬁgure AO, Appendix M), there is an almost immediate response as the peaks and
low points follow the pattern of the rainfall. The difference between the maximum
level in 2007 and the minimum in 2009 is less than 5 cm, and yet it is higher than
the change caused by increasing the pumping rate to 1.4 million m3/yr and 1.8
million m3/yr. Correspondingly, the seasonal changes in seepage ﬂow (ﬁgure 45
and ﬁgures AG-AH, Appendix L) reﬂect the pattern of the monthly precipitation
rates, though slightly delayed and with less peaks than the hydraulic head. This
indicates a gradual response to the amount of rainfall rather than an immediate
one.
The original purpose of the model was not to model ﬂow patterns on a local
scale, and the coarse 50 m grid does give rise to some uncertainty.
In some areas the topography might change signiﬁcantly within 50 m, causing
the interpolated elevation used in the model to be higher than the actual elevation.
One such place could be the hillslope bordering the wetland in which the elevation
drops several meters within a short distance. In the model (ﬁgure AM, Appendix
N) the elevation at boreholes B3-B6 is 9 masl, which is about 2 meters higher than
the actual elevation levels, which in turn affects the simulation of seepage ﬂow.
Another source of uncertainty is the fact that the simulation results are saved
every 30 days yielding one result pr. month. However, as a month is not always
exactly 30 days, the date on which the simulation results is saved changes. The
yearly seepage ﬂows listed in Table J, Appendix L are the sum of the 12 simulation
results saved within a year, but all have different dates. As discussed previously
there is a correlation between seepage and precipitation, which again changes
according to a somewhat regular seasonal pattern. Whether the simulation results
are saved at the beginning, middle or end of a month might therefore to some
degree affect the difference in ﬂow between the years, though it is likely small.
Nevertheless, as all simulations were run with the same conditions, the fact
remains that the apparent impact on the wetland when increasing the pumping
rate from 1.4 million m3/yr to 1.8 million m3/yr is limited.

D I S C U S S I O N
6.1
comparison of obtained results
The results of the analyses considered in the previous chapters largely support
each other. The GSI typology study indicated a local source of recharge to the wet-
land, which often causes noticeable seasonal variations. The occurrence of distinct
seasonal changes is conﬁrmed by the ﬁeld monitoring of the hydraulic head in the
six boreholes in the wetland, showing a difference of at least 40 cm between the
highest hydraulic heads in winter and the lowest during summer. This pattern is
replicated in the 2D model, in which the seasonal variation in head in the wetland
is up to 45 cm for the moderate pumping rate and up to 30 cm without pumping
when simulating with the baseline values of hydraulic conductivity. With the two
highest pumping rates, which to a large degree empty the primary aquifer, the
ﬂuctuations increase to about one meter.
The hydraulic head in the wetland simulated in the 3D model follows the same
pattern of high head in winter and low in summer as the 2D model and the mon-
itoring data, but the seasonal variations are less than 5 cm and show barely any
difference between the scenarios with different pumping rates. The low seasonal
variation compared to the monitoring data might be due to the model scale or
related to precipitation as the simulated years were considerably wetter than 2017.
However, both models show notable variations in seepage to the wetland. The
total seepage varies about 20 mm/day throughout the course of a year in the 3D
model and 40-50 mm/day in the 2D model, with the baseline hydraulic conduc-
tivity and pumping rates from zero to moderate.
Both models show seepage to be largest near the hillslope, which in the 3D
model is the grid encompassing boreholes B3-B6 with a value of about 15 mm/-
day +/- 5 mm/day seasonally. However, when interpolating the highest seepage
is found just south of the boreholes, which instead have values of about 3 mm/day
(B3/B4) and 7 mm/day (B5/B6). This is in accordance with the maximum values
of seepage ﬂux calculated based on the monitoring data. For boreholes B3/B4 the
maximum seepage ﬂux is 4.3 mm/day and for boreholes B5/B6 the maximum
seepage ﬂux is 6.9 mm/day, while the average values are 2.7 and 1.9 mm/day
respectively. Thus, on average the seepage is higher at B3/B4, but in terms of ab-
solute maximum ﬂux it is higher at boreholes B5/B6 located just above the base of
the hillslope. At boreholes B1/B2 the modelled seepage is just above zero ranging
from 0.3-0.9 mm/day, while the average calculated ﬂux based on the monitoring
data is just below zero, but ranging from -3.5-1.9 mm/day during the monitoring
period.
Considering changes to the hydraulic head and seepage in the wetland due to
different pumping rates, the variability is overall lower than the seasonal ﬂuctu-
ations. In the 2D model a high or very high pumping rate is required before a
considerable effect is observed, and even then the water table is not lowered more
73
==PAGE:85==
than one meter below terrain. The largest difference in hydraulic head in the wet-
land between the scenarios with low and moderate pumping rate is 50 cm, but the
average difference is only 1.7 cm. This is consistent with the results of the 3D mod-
eling, where the maximum difference in hydraulic head in the wetland between
the scenarios with pumping rates of 1.4 million m3/yr and 1.8 million m3/yr is 2
cm.
The effects on seepage from pumping are not quite as concurring in the 2D
model and 3D model respectively. For the 2D model, the difference in total seepage
between the low and moderate pumping scenarios is roughly 15 %, while the
difference between the 1.4 million m3/yr and 1.8 million m3/yr scenarios in the
3D model is only 2.5 %. However, it is important to keep in mind the uncertainty
related to the downscaled pumping rates applied in the 2D model as well as
the fact that an increased water demand at the wells can only be acquired in
a two-dimensional direction stretching toward the wetland; hence, an increased
abstraction will have to draw water away from the wetland. The catchment zone
will then extend further in the direction of the wetland than it does in reality,
which is visible in the comparison of the catchment zones of the 2D and 3D model
respectively. Despite the extended catchment zone in the direction of the wetland
in the 2D model, it is only with the very high pumping rate that the wetland
and stream are in fact reached. With the 3D model it is seen that rather than
stretching north toward the wetland, the catchment zone stretches toward the west
and shows barely any difference between the scenarios with pumping rates of 1.4
million m3/yr and 1.8 million m3/yr.
Consequently, with differences between the scenarios of a magnitude possibly
being explained by model uncertainty and monitoring data showing seasonal ﬂuc-
tuations in hydraulic head of at least 40 cm, the wetland appears to be fairly
resilient and not signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced by an increase in pumping rate.
Furthermore, the simulated seasonal and yearly differences are larger than the
differences between pumping scenarios. Seepage to the wetland in the year with
minimum ﬂow is about 25 % lower than in the year with maximum ﬂow, while the
difference is only 10 % between the baseline scenario and a pumping rate of 2.7
million m3/yr, implying that precipitation is a greater factor. Similarly, the precip-
itation patterns are reﬂected in the seasonal and yearly changes of the hydraulic
head.
The ecological state of the wetland is maintained under the current hydrological
conditions and the related ﬂuctuations in which the water table is about half a
meter below terrain during dry periods in July. In other words, before the wetland
is sensitive to changes the ﬂuctuations must be greater than 50 cm. However, as
the monitoring only took place from July to December, the period for which the
wetland can tolerate a water table 50 cm below terrain is unknown. Additionally,
the non-monitored period encompasses the growing season in which the water
demand of the plants is greatest, assuming a growing season beginning in March
and having the characteristic plants blooming at varying periods between April
and August [20]. However, as discussed in section 3.1.1 the height of capillary rise
in peat may be 40-50 cm depending on the degree of decomposition, which in this
case possibly ensures a sufﬁcient supply of water to the wetland vegetation during
dry periods.
74
==PAGE:86==
2017 was as previously mentioned a dry year compared to the years 2004-2012
with low rainfall from January to May, but the habitat classiﬁcation completed
in May nonetheless included observations of a number of characteristic wetland
species, conﬁrming the resilience of the wetland. In addition, the observed plant
species are predominately indicators of chalky soil in compliance with the water
analyses classifying the water as Ca-HCO−
3 type, though with one species prefer-
ing acidic soil.
The redox conditions, which are currently likely nitrate reducing, as well as
the water composition and pH are important factors in maintaining the speciﬁc
wetland ecology. A change in water level may alter the redox conditions by, for
example, oxygenation, which in turn increases the nutrient availability due to in-
creased peat mineralization or causes acidiﬁcation due to a shift in the balance
between groundwater and rainwater [16]. However, given the presumed negligi-
ble changes in hydraulic head at the wetland from increased water abstraction
a shift in either geochemistry or vegetation seem unlikely. Explicit investigations
into how the wetland vegetation might be affected by water level variations or
geochemical changes are beyond the scope of this report.
Evaluating the overall hydrology in the area including both wetland and ab-
straction site, the sounding time series and 3D model simulations both indicate
a connection between the secondary aquifer and the Bolbro aquifer, though nat-
urally most evident near the abstractions wells. Here the difference in hydraulic
head of only about 1 m in the primary aquifer between the scenarios with pump-
ing rates of 1.4 million m3/yr and 1.8 million m3/yr is replicated in the secondary
aquifer, where the difference is 0.5-1 m at the abstraction wells, but decreases to
less than 20 cm toward the wetland. Conversely, the difference in hydraulic head
in the wetland is as previously mentioned less than two cm, implying only a weak
to non-existant connection between the wetland and the aquifers, which supports
the conclusion from the GSI analysis that the wetland is primarily connected to
local hydrologic systems.
6.2
fieldwork and model capabilities
As described above the ﬁeldwork and model results essentially coincide and to-
gether form the basis for the conclusions outlined above. It is often necessary to
evaluate the hydrological impact on wetlands and other groundwater-dependent
terrestrial systems, and thus it would be convenient if one of the approaches were
sufﬁcient for assessing the resilience of the ecological system as well as predicting
the associated hydrological effects from, for example, an increase in water abstrac-
tion nearby.
Monitoring the wetland water level over an adequate time period obviously pro-
vides solid data on the magnitude and frequency of ﬂuctuations, and thus the level
of disturbance from which the wetland is accustomed to recover. Furthermore, it
gives an indication as to whether a given increase in the amplitude of the ﬂuctua-
tions will cause the water table to be lowered beyond a level where capillary rise
might be able to keep the soil saturated and sustain the wetland vegetation.
By placing monitoring boreholes or nests of piezometers at varying depths
within and around the wetland, knowledge of the hydraulic gradients and the
75
==PAGE:87==
ﬂow ﬁeld can be obtained. Both gradients and ﬂow generally vary in both space
and time [56], which is also evident from the borehole pairs in this study. Con-
sequently, a high sampling resolution both vertically and horizontally as well as
over time is necessary in order to establish the ﬂow ﬁeld accurately [56].
The hydraulic gradient and the hydraulic conductivity are essential parame-
ters when determining ﬂow, and as the spatial variability of both can be high it
adds uncertainty to the calculation of ﬂow. In this study, the uncertainty is fur-
ther enhanced as the hydraulic conductivity used for the ﬂux calculations of the
monitored data is estimated based on literature values rather than determined
experimentally by, for example, slugtests in the ﬁeld. Thus when comparing the
simulated seepage of the 3D model and the ﬁeld calculated ﬂux it is essentially a
comparison of two estimated values.
Nonetheless, the interpolated estimates by the model at the locations of the
boreholes ﬁt well with the maximum values of the ﬁeld calculations, but overall
the model values appear to be slightly overestimated. Additionally, the grid size
of 50 m causes four of the boreholes to be located within the same grid, which
means the spatial variation in ﬂux, evident from the monitored data, cannot truly
be replicated by the model. The lack of detail is one of the challenges of a large-
scale model as well as the smoothening of, for example, the topography, which
as discussed in section 5.3.4 becomes substantially higher in some areas than in
reality, in turn affecting the simulation of seepage as it is calculated from saturated
zone to overland.
The grid in which four of the boreholes is located is also the grid with the
highest seepage, which is appropriate considering the seepage of the last borehole
pair located further away, and the results of the 2D model showing the seepage to
be highest near the hillslope. With the interpolated model estimates the zone with
the highest ﬂow is located south of the boreholes, which is still near the hillslope
and thus still in agreement with the results of the 2D model; however, there are
no boreholes in that area to conﬁrm or disprove its accordance with reality. As it
is, the inadequate ﬁeld data makes it difﬁcult to conclude whether the model is in
fact overestimating the values as well as to determine the accuracy of the spatial
distribution of seepage.
The possible overestimation of seepage might also account for the much smaller
seasonal variations in hydraulic head in the 3D model than in reality, i.e. if the
seepage and thus recharge to the wetland is generally larger and perhaps stead-
ier it would likely uphold a more stable water level. Yet it might also be related
to the model scale and the frequency of data storing that the nuances of change
in water level are not reproduced by the model. A third possible explanation is
that while the monitored ﬂuctuations are from 2017, which was a dry year, the
simulated results are based on climate data from 2007-2012 which had larger an-
nual precipitation volumes. However, the fact that the 2D model, which uses the
same climate input data, does replicate the seasonal variations in hydraulic head
quite well suggests that the difference in precipitation between the simulated and
monitored years is not the cause of the discrepancy. While the 2D model is able
to reproduce the seasonal variations in hydraulic head better than the 3D model,
there are other challenges associated with it such as the scaling of the pumping
76
==PAGE:88==
rate or the two-dimensional ﬂow direction deﬁned by the location of the model
transect.
Nonetheless, Joris & Feyen [32] created a 2D model that was able to reproduce
the groundwater dynamics and seasonal variations in soil moisture content in
a groundwater-fed wetland. The model showed promising results as to being a
useful tool for assessing the hydrological impact from changes in boundary con-
ditions. Besides placing the model transect between two natural boundaries (a
river and ditch) and applying Richards equation for the unsaturated ﬂow, one of
the main distinctions between the 2D model by Joris & Feyen and the 2D model
applied in this study is that there are no pumping wells included in the former.
Changes due to variations in pumping rate are incorporated as a change in the
hydrologic boundary condition. However, such an approach requires that a pre-
existing knowledge of the effect of a given change in pumping rate on the bound-
ary condition is available.
If the effect on the boundary conditions is unknown and pumping wells must
be included within the model, then the challenge of downscaling the pumping
rates remains, along with the fact that the catchment zones can only be outlined
along the model transect regardless of how they would expand in reality.
Thus, 2D models are potentially useful management tools, especially if the chal-
lenges regarding pumping wells are overcome. As it is, a 2D model could be appli-
cable as a screening tool as long as the results are cautiously considered and not
used for exact quantiﬁcation of the components in the water balance, but rather
as initial insights into the ﬂow mechanisms and hydrology in general in order to
assess whether an impact is likely or not.
The completed ﬁeldwork is not sufﬁcient to determine whether a large-scale
3D model is adequately capable of reproducing the wetland hydrology and pre-
dicting a future impact. The seasonal variations in head are, as mentioned, not
quite captured, and the ﬁt with regard to seepage is uncertain. Overall, the esti-
mated change in seepage when increasing the pumping rate to 1.8 million m3/yr
matches the estimated impact on the stream discharge for Ryds å as modelled by
Niras [45].
As a minimum, the large-scale 3D model may serve as an initial screening tool
for assessing whether an impact is likely or not. If the answer is borderline, or
detailed information on ﬂow paths or precise quantiﬁcation of the hydrologic
changes are required, a model with a ﬁner grid should be developed. House et
al. [30] use a grid size of 1 m to simulate the hydrologic processes in a wetland,
while Woodward et al. [61] use 2 m grids for investigating the ﬂow paths of shal-
low groundwater.
In a more detailed model Richards equation, which takes soil moisture into
account, could be utilized for modelling the unsaturated ﬂow as done by [30,
32]. This would provide a better understanding of the root zone dynamics when
considering the eco-hydrological impact on the wetland [32]. However, the choice
of model detail regarding both grid size and included processes should depend
on data availability, the model purpose and the degree of complexity needed.
In summary, ﬁeld data may provide an indication of the wetland resilience, and
a sufﬁciently high spatial sampling resolution is useful for mapping ﬂow paths,
estimating ﬂow etc. to characterize the current situation as well as for model cali-
77
==PAGE:89==
bration. The models utilized in the study are useful as initial screening tools and
overall estimates of impact, but are not capable of predicting speciﬁc hydrologic
impacts on a local scale. For this purpose a model with a smaller grid size, ideally
using the large-scale model as boundary condition, would be better suited.

C O N C L U S I O N
In December 2015, Vandcenter Syd was granted permission to increase the water
abstraction from the Bolbro abstraction site from 1.4 million m3 to 1.8 million m3
annually with the requirement that a monitoring program must be established
by the end of 2018 in order to observe possible adverse hydrological effects on
groundwater-dependent terrestrial systems nearby.
In this report, the hydrological impact of increased pumping on a § 3 protected
groundwater-dependent wetland located 1500-2000 m from the abstraction wells
was assessed through monitoring of the water level, water analyses, GSI typol-
ogy and numerical modeling in MIKE SHE using two models of different scales
and complexity. Additionally, the potential for assessing the resilience of wetlands
through ﬁeldwork and modeling was considered, and the capability of the utilized
models to predict realistic scenarios at the necessary scale evaluated.
In summary, the difference in hydraulic head in the wetland with an annual
abstraction of 1.8 million m3 rather than 1.4 million m3 is less than two cm ac-
cording to model simulations, while ﬁeld monitoring showed seasonal variations
of at least 40 cm, with the water level about 50 cm below terrain when at its lowest
during summer. The examinations of modeled seepage showed only a minor dif-
ference between the scenarios and that the annual precipitation has a considerably
larger effect on the recharge of the wetland than the rate of water abstraction at
the Bolbro site.
An assessment of the overall hydrology in the study area, including both wet-
land and abstraction site, showed that there is a connection between the Bolbro
aquifer and the secondary aquifer, as the latter is to some extent affected by the
former. A connection between the secondary aquifer and the wetland is much less
pronounced, and between the wetland and the Bolbro aquifer there is no direct
connection. The Bolbro aquifer does not extend all the way to the wetland, and
a resulting effect on the wetland from a change in water abstraction would need
to happen through the effect on the secondary aquifer, given that the secondary
aquifer is a major source of recharge to the wetland; this does not appear to be
the case. On the contrary, the wetland seems to be connected primarily with local
hydrologic systems.
Thus, overall the wetland is considered fairly resilient and not signiﬁcantly im-
pacted by the increased abstraction from the Bolbro site.
As for the predictive capabilities of the utilized models, they are useful as initial
screening tools and for overall assessment of whether a hydrological impact is
likely or not, but not for detailed predictions on ﬂow paths and quantiﬁcation of
changes in ﬂow on a wetland scale.
