Abstract
This thesis investigates the feasibility of using wearable technology to assess anxiety
in children during pediatric emergency care through heart rate variability (HRV) anal-
ysis. Anxiety in the Pediatric Emergency Department (PED) is common but often dif-
ficult to assess objectively, as self-report and behavioral measures can be unreliable
in young patients. HRV represents a promising physiological indicator of autonomic
activation related to emotional arousal.
To explore this potential, the study combined literature review, contextual inquiries,
and prototyping with feasibility testing at Herlev Hospital. Thirteen children aged 3–9
years participated in short examination sessions (5–25 minutes) where photoplethys-
mography (PPG) data were collected using a wrist-worn Polar Verity Sense device,
while clinical events were annotated by the researcher through a custom Android
application.
Of the 13 recordings, 10 were analyzable and 8 met inclusion criteria for short-term
HRV analysis. All children wore the device throughout their examination, 77% found
it comfortable, and 54% expressed willingness to wear it again. Nurses evaluated HRV
visualizations, time-series plots, bar charts, and heatmaps as intuitive and clinically
relevant.
The findings suggest that HRV-based monitoring in pediatric emergency care is both
technically and practically feasible, providing initial evidence that wearable sensing
can complement clinical observation in identifying children’s emotional responses.
The study establishes a methodological foundation for integrating physiological sens-
ing into clinical workflows and highlights its potential for more data-informed, child-
centered care.

Introduction
This chapter introduces the thesis by outlining its motivation, initial research problem,
and overall structure.
1.1
Motivation
Pediatric emergency care can be stressful and overwhelming for children, often re-
sulting in strong emotional and physiological reactions. Among these, anxiety is par-
ticularly important, as it affects both treatment decisions and the child’s overall well-
being. Traditional way to assess anxiety relies on either self-report or behavior cues,
both of which become compromised as young children have difficulty articulating
their feelings.
Anxiety can be heightened by unfamiliar surroundings, medical procedures, and care-
giver stress. These experiences can lead to visible signs, such as crying or restless-
ness, and subtler physiological responses such as increased heart rate or sweating.
Because these physiological signs are difficult to detect, they might go unnoticed
by healthcare staff, leading to longer procedures, delayed treatment, and hospital-
related phobias. With over 30,000 [2] emergency visits annually at Herlev’s Børn og
Unge Akutmodtagelse, many children are likely affected by these challenges.
These challenges highlight the need to examine anxiety in pediatric emergency care
(PED) in more objective and continuous ways. One evidence-based non-observational
measure of stress is Heart Rate Variability (HRV), which refers to the variation of time
between consecutive heartbeats and can be captured through wearable sensors dur-
ing clinical care.
Gitte Maria Flyger Würtz, a nurse and researcher at Herlev Hospital, is currently study-
ing the use and impact of the Apple Watch to monitor heart rate as part of efforts to
understand anxiety in children. As this wrist-based wearable is already integrated
into clinical practice, a wrist-worn device was selected for consistency and familiarity.
The focus of this study was directed toward HRV, which provides a more sensitive
measure of autonomic nervous system activity than heart rate alone.
Addressing anxiety in the PED is essential to improve treatment and support chil-
dren’s emotional resilience. Despite growing awareness, objective assessment of
physiological signs remains limited, underscoring the need for more child-centered
approaches.

1.2
Initial Problem Statement
The following problem statement reflects the initial formulation this thesis. Refine-
ment of problem statement can be seen in Chapter 2.9.
How can heart rate variability (HRV), measured through wearable technology,
be used to detect and monitor anxiety in children during emergency care in a
way that supports real-time clinical decision-making?
As well as the research questions:
• How does HRV change over time during a child’s emergency care visit?
• Can distinct HRV patterns be identified that correspond to different stages of
the emergency care process (e.g., waiting, treatment, recovery)?
• Do certain children exhibit consistently lower HRV, potentially indicating higher
levels of distress, compared to others?
• How can HRV trends be visualized and presented in a way that is accessible and
meaningful for clinical staff, particularly nurses, to support decision-making and
anxiety management?

2.1
What is Anxiety
Anxiety is a feeling of stress, fear, and tension, which triggers the body’s stress re-
sponse, also known as the autonomic nervous system (ANS) or, as some know it, the
fight, flight, or freeze. The ANS controls sudden functions necessary for survival, in-
cluding heart rate, blood pressure, digestion, and breathing (Brennan, 2021).
The autonomic nervous system has two main parts, the sympathetic nervous system
and the parasympathetic nervous system. These works to maintain balance in the ANS
(Brennan, 2021):
• Sympathetic nervous system (SNS) : Controls the ”fight-or-flight” response
and is activated when scared or anxious. When activated, it increases the heart
rate and blood pressure, and prepares the body for action by releasing adrenaline
• Parasympathetic nervous system. Controls the ”rest-and-digest” process and
promotes relaxation, especially slowing down the heart rate and blood pressure
after a sympathetic response.
These emotions can become intensified in children, particularly within clinical set-
tings, and often manifest through physiological signs such as elevated heart rate,

sweating, restlessness, behavioral changes, and low heart rate variability (HRV). Such
reactions can be captured and quantified as physiological indicators, providing objec-
tive and continuous measures of anxiety.

Among these, pupil dilation is frequently cited as an indicator of anxiety, as it re-
flects increased cognitive load and emotional arousal associated with the sympa-
thetic “fight-or-flight” response (Clinic, 2022; Bradley et al., 2008). A recent student

plementing such methods in everyday clinical practice is challenging, as eye-tracking
requires bulky apparatus and controlled conditions that are not feasible in a busy
pediatric emergency environment. In contrast, heart-based measures offer a more
practical approach for continuous anxiety monitoring in clinical settings.
2.2.1
Heart Rate
Heart rate (HR) refers to the number of times the heart beats in a minute and usually
reflects a person’s emotional or physical state (Clinic, 2024). The heartbeat increases
in response to factors such as anxiety, excitement, physical activity, fear or fever, and

drops when resting. Because of this, HR could be a potential biomarker for moni-
toring anxiety, an approach Herlev Hospital is using. However, there are limitations,
such as accurately interpreting the relationship between HR and anxiety. To do so,
it requires constantly observing the child throughout the entire visit, since it can be
difficult to distinguish whether an elevated heart rate is due to emotional distress or
physical activity, such as running.
2.2.2
Heart Rate Variability
Heart Rate Variability (HRV) refers to the variation in time intervals between consec-
utive heartbeats (Shaffer and Ginsberg, 2017) and is recognized for detecting auto-
nomic nervous system (ANS) responses to stress. By looking into HRV, it is possible
to assess the imbalance between sympathetic nervous system (SNS) and parasympa-
thetic nervous system (PNS), providing an indication of stress levels. A higher HRV
reflects lower stress levels, and it suggests the ANS is working in a ”rest-and-digest”
mode, dominated by the PNS, while a lower HRV indicates high SNS activity, associ-
ated with heightened stress (Brennan, 2021).
Heart Rate Variability Calculation Methods
Heart rate variability (HRV) is calculated by analyzing the variation in time between
successive heartbeats, measured in milliseconds. The intervals between consecutive
heartbeats are known as RR intervals, which are traditionally derived from electrocar-
diography (ECG) (Selvaraj et al., 2008).
HRV can be analyzed using different methods, the most common measures are the
following:
• Time-Domain analysis calculates statistical measures from RR-intervals over a
given period.
– SDNN: Standard deviation of all normal RR-intervals, representing overall
HRV
– RMSSD: Root mean square of successive RR-intervals differences, often
used to assess the parasympathetic activity
– pNN50: The percentage of the successive RR-interval that differ by more
than 50 ms, also linked to PNS.
• Frequency-domain analysis helps differentiate between sympathetic and Parasym-
pathetic influences.
– High-frequency (HF): Associated with PNS

– Low-frequency (LF): Associated with both PNS and LF
– LF/HF ratio: Used as an indicator of ANS balance.
Although ECG has traditionally been considered the standard for obtaining RR inter-
vals, wearable devices increasingly rely on photoplethysmography (PPG) to derive
HRV in a less invasive and more practical way. PPG have made it possible to obtain
interbeat interval (IBI), which is the changes in the time intervals between consecu-
tive heartbeats (Shaffer and Ginsberg, 2017) through wearable devices such as smart-
watches (Hinde, White, and Armstrong, 2021). Some authors distinguish PPG-derived
variability by using the term pulse rate variability (PRV), to differentiate it from HRV
obtained from ECG. However, since the underlying construct of interest is still heart
rate variability, this thesis will consistently use the term HRV, while referring to IBI
when specifically describing interval data.
Several studies have compared HRV indices derived from ECG and PPG and found
a high correlation between the two modalities, particularly under resting conditions.
Mirescu and Harden (2012) found a high correlation between ECG and PPG, stating
that PPG is a reliable instrument to assess HRV parameters, showing no statistical dif-
ference. Similarly, Hsiao et al. (2017) found a significant positive correlation btween
PPG-and ECG-derived HRV across different wearable. These findings are consistent
with other reports demonstrating strong agreement between ECG- and PPG-based
HRV measures [14] [15] [16].
2.2.3
Duration and Pediatric Ranges in HRV
A practical consideration in HRV research is the required length of recording for reli-
able analysis. While 24-hour monitoring has long been regarded as the standard for
assessing ANS activity, shorter recordings are increasingly used in both research and
clinical contexts [9] [17]. A study by Min et al. (2008) demonstrated that 5-minute HRV
recordings produce moderate but significant correlation with 24-hour HRV measures.
Their findings suggest that 5-minute HRV is suitable for screening purposes, even if
it does not fully capture the complexity of 24-hour variability.
Another study by Shaffer and Ginsberg (2017) supported this finding, highlighting
the different HRV calculation techniques, with particular attention to their minimum
required and gold-standard recording durations. Table 2.1 summarizes the recom-
mended recording lengths for common HRV metrics.

Early pediatric reference data were provided by Silvetti, Drago, and Ragonese (2001),
who evaluated 24-hour time-domain HRV in 103 healthy children and adolescents
(ages 1–20 years), using ambulatory Holter monitoring during normal daily activities.
These data established foundational 24-hour recording, providing physiologically rel-
evant values during the sleep–wake cycle. For short-term HRV metrics (5-minutes),
Michels et al. (2013) provided data from 460 children ages 5-10 years, using the Po-
lar chest belt for ECG-based RR interval. Their study generated age- and sex-specific
percentile curves for both time- and frequency-domain HRV parameters, including
SDNN, rMSSD, and pNN50.
Given the methodological comparability to the present study’s short-term, wearable-
derived data, these reference percentiles were used as the primary benchmark for
evaluating whether the observed HRV magnitudes fall within realistic physiological
limits (Table 2.2).

2.2.4
Photoplethysmography
Photoplethysmography, also known as PPG, is an optical technique used to detect
changes in blood volume in the microvascular bed of tissue (Allen, 2007). In wearable
technology, PPG is commonly used to monitor blood circulation by detecting changes
in blood volume. The device includes a light source that directs infrared light into the
skin and a photodetector that measures the amount of light reflected back. Because
the reflection varies with the volume of blood in the tissue, these fluctuations can be
captured as a signal. By analyzing the intensity of the reflected light, the device can
calculate changes in blood volume, which form the basis for deriving cardiovascular
measures such as heart rate and heart rate variability (Castaneda et al., 2019).
PPG signals can be affected by factors such as motion, or sensor placement, which
may introduce noise into the measurements. These limitations are addressed through
preprocessing and filtering steps, which are discussed in Chapter 9.
2.3
Anxiety in the PED
Anxiety in pediatric healthcare settings is a common and critical issue, which can pro-
long treatment duration, cause hospital-related phobias, and complicate the proce-
dure. Understanding and preventing these factors can improve the medical expe-
rience which will benefit the children, caregivers and healthcare professionals from
reduced procedural complications. Research highlights that anxiety during hospital
9
==PAGE:19==
CHAPTER 2. BACKGROUND
visits can intensify procedural pain, especially children who have had prior negative
experiences within the health care environment, potentially leading to post traumatic
stress disorder (Winskill and Andrews, 2008). In addition, anxiety can negatively affect
the relationship between the doctor and the patient, which can make the procedure
more difficult and complicated (Longobardi et al., 2019). Other studies emphasize
that procedures can be extremely distressing, not only for the child but also for the
caregiver and healthcare providers, sometimes causing physiological and psycholog-
ical consequences for the child (Kennedy and Luhmann, 2001).
In response to this growing concern, some hospitals have begun to redesign pedi-
atric emergency departments to create calmer environments and reduce sensory
triggers. For example, Nationwide Children’s Hospital identified that the traditional
emergency rooms with equipment, such as wires and cords, furniture with sterile
design, and medication closets heightened problems such as anxiety, disorientation,
and violent behavior (Hospital, 2025). To address this, the hospitals introduced dedi-
cated spaces such as rooms or hallways redesigned to be less stimulating and resem-
ble living rooms rather than clinical areas. Similarly, the new Mary Elizabeth Hospital
(BørneRiget) in Copenhagen integrates play and healing architecture to create com-
forting, child-centered spaces (Yousef, 2019). These changes provide the children
with a calmer, comfortable, and familiar space to wait before or between the services,
helping to reduce stress and improve the overall care experience.
These initiatives demonstrate the relationship between environment, emotion, and
treatment outcomes in pediatric care, an aspect further explored in the context of
the Pediatric Emergency Department at Herlev Hospital.

2.5
Self-assessment of Anxiety
The assessment of anxiety has traditionally relied on self-report-tools. These assess-
ment tools can be broadly divided into self-report measures, where the child ex-
presses their own feelings, and observer-based measures, where clinicians or care-
givers interpret behavioral and physiological cues. Such tools are essential for guid-
ing clinical decisions and tailoring interventions to each child’s needs.
2.5.1

mon examples is the Visual Analogue Scale (VAS), which evaluates a child’s emotional
distress based on verbal expressions and observable behaviors.

Previous research has consistently highlighted challenges with the acceptability of

2.6.1
Discussion
While there are validation studies showing consumer and research-grade wearables
can closely approximate HRV, trustworthiness and practical use are limited in these
devices. The Apple Watch has been tested in both resting conditions (Turki et al.,
2021) and in daily life (Velmovitsky et al., 2023), with results broadly comparable to
ECG.
However, the inability to retrieve raw RR intervals or PPG data, combined with a high
sensitivity to motion artifacts limits its use for research, since meaningful HRV analy-
sis depends on beat-to-beat precision.
The Empatica E4, by contrast, has been widely used in academic studies and initially
seemed ideal because of its multi-modal sensing capabilities. However, have been
conducted during multiple days of monitoring contexts rather than acute clinical en-
vironments. While such designs demonstrate the feasibility of continuous data collec-
tion in daily life, they do not capture the short, high-stress encounters characteristic
of pediatric emergency care [42][41][40][39]. Also, a significant limitation is the E4 is
no longer available on the market, even though it represents one of the most vali-
dated research wristband devices for HRV-analysis.
More recently, the Polar Verity Sense has shown closer alignment with ECG chest
straps, suggesting greater potential among PPG-based devices (Kim et al., 2021). How-
ever, supporting evidence is limited to just a few papers that describe its measure-
ment validity, and measurement validity has just been evaluated in non-clinical cir-

cumstances. Overall, the number of validation studies on PPG devices relatively to
ECG devices is small, suggesting there is a lot of uncertainty about validity and appli-
cability of PPG devices in dynamic or pediatric environments. The limited research
base potentially prevents a reasonable and conclusive answer regarding the Polar
Verity Sense for short, anxiety-provoking clinical interactions, even though it appears
to be technically valid.
2.7
Reducing anxiety in the PED
Understanding how to manage anxiety once detected is as important as measuring
it. Over the years, several interventions have been introduced to mitigate anxiety
among children in healthcare settings. These interventions fall into two broad cate-
gories: Play-based-and digital interventions.
2.7.1
Play Interventions
Play intervention helps children navigate the unfamiliar environments by regaining a
sense of control and redirecting a child’s attention away from stressful stimuli. Dis-
traction techniques, such as toys, games, music and storytelling during medical proce-
dures have shown to reduce anxiety and improve cooperation during medical proce-
dures (Yati, Wahyuni, and Islaeli, 2017). Creative activities like coloring and drawing
further support emotional expression by enabling non-verbal communication and
helping children process their experiences (Harnilawati and Asrianto, 2021).
Familiarization and preparation programs represent another form of play-based in-
tervention. Li et al. (2016) demonstrated that structured and age-tailored interven-
tions, like puppet use and cognitive games for older children lowered anticipatory
anxiety and improved procedural cooperation. Such interventions reduce the ”fear
of the unknown” and builds trust with medical staff.
2.7.2
Digital Interventions
With the growing availability of technology, digital interventions have become a valu-
able complement to traditional methods. These interventions range from mobile
application to virtual reality experiences, designed to distract the children from the
hospital environment. These tools not only helps with reducing anxiety, but also min-
imizes the additional burdens on the medical staff.
Virtual reality (VR) has proven especially effective as a distraction tool during pro-
cedures. Sabinash (2023) found that those who engaged with VR experienced the
greatest reduction in anxiety scores compared to standard distraction methods, a
finding supported by Butt et al. (2022), who reported decreases in in distress. Simi-
larly, mobile apps and gamified approaches, such as Clickamico, SmilingMind, Chill-
17
==PAGE:27==

Panda and RootD, provide mindfulness exercises, guided breathing, and biofeedback
to promote emotional awareness and self-regulation [51], [52], [53] [54].
However, the effectiveness of these digital tools often depends on a child’s age, moti-
vation, and level of engagement. Furthermore, some studies have shown that video-
based interventions are less effective than in-person therapy due to the absence of
one-on-one interaction and emotional support (Sugiyama, Koseki, and Kishino, 2024).
This highlights the potential benefit of combining digital tools with physical interac-
tion to balance efficiency with emotional support.
2.8
Conclusion
The background chapter shows that anxiety in pediatric emergency care is a signifi-
cant issue with clear physiological markers, that can serve as indicators for anxiety.
Existing interventions, both play-based and digital, demonstrate that targeted sup-
port can reduce anxiety, yet a gap in providing continuous, objective monitoring that
integrates seamlessly into clinical workflows.
This thesis directly addresses that gap by exploring the feasibility of real-time HRV-
based anxiety detection in the pediatric emergency department, thereby extending
previous research that has primarily focused on either self-report or long-term mon-
itoring in controlled conditions. By investigating whether HRV can be reliably mea-
sured in a short, high-stress clinical environment, the thesis contributes new empiri-
cal knowledge on how wearable technology can complement existing interventions.
Furthermore, the relationship between continuous HRV monitoring, clinical interven-
tions, and observed responses can be understood as a feedback cycle. As HRV data
are monitored, potential signs of heightened anxiety can be detected. These detec-
tions can result in clinical interventions, such as distraction, explanation, or calming
strategies, which in turn influence the child’s physiological state and feed back into
ongoing monitoring. This relationship is illustrated in Figure 2.7, summarizing the key
concepts of the background chapter and demonstrating how detection, intervention,
and response are interconnected.
However, the present thesis focuses specifically on the detection component of this
cycle, establishing whether HRV can be reliably measured in a clinical environment,
and whether such data provide meaningful indicators of anxiety. Building on this
conceptual understanding, the next step is to clearly define the specific focus of this
research.

2.10
Scope
This thesis examines the feasibility of using wearable technology to collect heart rate
variability (HRV) data from children aged 3–9 years during pediatric emergency care.
The focus is on short examination-room sessions (10–15 minutes), where movement
is limited but anxiety-provoking events (e.g., palpating the body, medical explana-
tions) occur. The study does not aim to clinically validate HRV as a diagnostic tool
for anxiety; rather, it explores whether usable HRV data can be obtained under real-
world conditions and how such data might be visualized in a way that is interpretable
and potentially useful for nurses in their daily practice.
The chosen age range of 3–9 years represents a developmental period in which chil-
dren are often unable to fully articulate their emotions, making physiological mea-
sures particularly relevant for assessing anxiety.
The study was conducted at the Pediatric Emergency Department (PED) at Herlev
Hospital as part of an ongoing research collaboration between Technical University
of Denmark (DTU) and the hospital. Within this collaboration, Clinical Nursing Special-
ist and researcher Gitte Maria Flyger Würtz is conducting a PhD focused on ways to
assess and intervene in children’s anxiety. Likewise, this relationship allowed both
clinical accessibility, and contextual familiarity with existing research on wearable
monitoring, ensuring the study remained feasible within routine clinical workflows.
Having defined the problem and scope, the next chapter outlines the methodological
framework adopted to investigate the feasibility of HRV-based anxiety monitoring.

3.2.6
Platform exploration
A preliminary study conducted prior to this thesis investigated the use of the Apple

decision to conduct clinical recordings in controlled examination-room settings.
3.2.9
Preprocessing methods
According to Mani Sekhar, Singh, and Doegar (2021), data processing represents a
critical step between data collection and analysis in healthcare, ensuring that the in-
formation obtained from sensors is accurate, reliable, and ready for interpretation.
In this study, established and commonly applied steps for preprocessing for photo-
plethysmography (PPG) signals used in heart rate variability (HRV) computation were
followed, including filtering, peak detection, and signal quality assessment, as sup-
ported by multiple studies such as Mejía-Mejía et al. (2021) and .
The preprocessing workflow aimed to minimize noise from motion, sensor place-
ment, and ambient light, common challenges in wrist-based PPG recordings. Each
26
==PAGE:36==
CHAPTER 3. METHODOLOGY
data stream was time-aligned using recorded timestamps, and background noise
was reduced by subtracting the ambient-light channel from each PPG channel.
The three PPG channels were then averaged into a single composite waveform to
enhance signal robustness. A band-pass Butterworth filter (0.5–5 Hz) was applied
to isolate the cardiac frequency range while removing low-frequency drift and high-
frequency noise.
Accelerometer (ACC) data were processed in parallel to identify movement artifacts
and align motion patterns with the PPG-derived fluctuations. Missing samples were
linearly interpolated, and the ACC signal was downsampled to match the PPG fre-
quency, allowing direct alignment of physiological and movement traces on a shared
time axis. Peaks were detected using the HeartPy Python library, and implausible
IBIs were automatically rejected using adaptive outlier thresholds.
To maintain data quality, HRV metrics were calculated only from five-minute seg-
ments with at least 80% valid IBIs, in accordance with short-term HRV standards.
Recordings that did not meet these inclusion criteria were excluded from further anal-
ysis. All preprocessing steps were implemented in Python.

collected health data support accurate diagnosis and treatment decisions, improves

A mixed-methods approach was applied to capture the physiological patterns and

To ensure methodological alignment with existing clinical practices at Herlev Hospi-

While previous implementations have focused primarily on heart rate (HR) as an in-
dicator of physiological arousal, this study emphasized heart rate variability (HRV) to
obtain a more detailed understanding of autonomic nervous system activity. HRV
provides a finer-grained measure of emotional and physiological regulation, offering
greater sensitivity to stress and anxiety-related changes than HR alone. Incorporating
HRV analysis therefore allowed for a more comprehensive assessment of children’s
physiological responses during clinical care, complementing the qualitative insights
gathered through observations and participant feedback.
Quantitative data collection
Physiological signals were collected using the Polar Verity Sense wearable sensor,
which recorded PPG, ambient-light and accelerometer (ACC) data during examination
room sessions. Clinical events were annotated through a custom Android application
in real-time, allowing synchonization of physiological signals with observed activities.
These recordings were later used to calculate heart-rate-variability (HRV) metrics and
asses signal stability under realistic clinical conditions. In addition, quantitative user
feedback was obtained from nursing staff through a short survey, evaluating the use-
fulness, and clarity of HRV visualizations.
Qualitative data collection
The qualitative insights focused on the acceptability and comfort of the wearable de-
vice. After each recording session, short verbal feedback was gathered from children
and caregiver regarding their experience of wearing the wristband, perceived com-
fort, and willingness to use the device again. These reflections helped evaluate prac-
tical feasibility and user acceptance within the clinical setting.
3.2.11
Data analysis methods
Data analysis plays a vital role in healthcare by transforming collected data into evi-
dence that informs clinical decision-making Kwok et al. (2022). Similarly Mani Sekhar,
Singh, and Doegar (2021 emphasize, the insights gained from analyzing data from
sensors can be used to trace the course of health issues from the onset of symptoms
to post-treatment recovery. In this thesis, analysis of wearable sensor data focused
on evaluating heart rate variability (HRV) as potential indicators of children’s physio-
logical responses during short examination sessions.
Following preprocessing, HRV analysis was applied to calculate short-term HRV met-
rics from the processed interbeat interval (IBI) data derived from photoplethysmogra-
phy (PPG) signals. The measures included RMSSD, SDNN, pNN20/pNN50 and frequency-
domain indices LF, HF, LF/HF, calculated according to the recommendations for short-
term HRV analysis described by Shaffer and Ginsberg (2017). These same analytical

Data Visualization Methods
Data visualization was used to make HRV trends more interpretable and to explore
anxiety-related patterns across recordings. According to Backonja, Haynes, and Kim
(2018), data visualizations play a crucial role in healthcare by transforming complex
data sets into interpretable formats that support understanding and decision making.
By building on human cognitive strengths, such as pattern recognition, visualization
helps clinicians and patients interpret information that would otherwise be difficult
to understand in numerical form.
Time-series plots illustrated changes in HRV metrics, by displaying interbeat inver-
vals (IBI) over time, aligned with accelerometer (ACC) data and annotated clinical
events. This allowed the understanding of the HRV values in respondence to activi-
ties. Window-based visualizations displayed HRV metrics accross 5-minute interval
blocks, highlighting shifts in physiological state (bar plots). Comparative visualiza-
tion, such as heatmaps and bar plots provided participant-level overviews of HRV
distribution and data retention, enabling assessment of both variability between chil-
dren and the overall feasibility of the recording approach.
In addition, pie charts were used to visualize acceptability feedback, summarizing
children’s and caregivers’ responses regarding wristband comfort, usability, and will-
ingness to wear the device again. These visual summaries provided a clear overview
of user experience and device acceptability within the clinical setting.
All visualizations were created using Python (Matplotlib and Seaborn libraries) and
designed with consistency and readability. Color palettes, axis scales, and layouts
were standardized across figures, while labeling was simplified to enhance clinical
readability.

anxiety, movement and context of the environment. The analysis revealed that while
distress was visible in a number of cases, at the examination room, children often
remained physically still. This limited physical movement reduced the likelihood that
motion artifacts interfere with HRV measurements.

Conclusion
Recordings from calm adult and child participants produced stable, physiologically
valid HRV values (Stas, 2023; Michels et al., 2013), confirming that the Polar Verity
Sense and preprocessing pipeline are reliable under low-to-moderate movement con-
ditions. Moderate emotional engagement slightly reduced HRV, while active conver-
sation caused implausibly high values due to motion artifacts, underscoring the need
for movement control during data collection. Overall, the Polar Verity Sense can gen-
erate HRV data within expected physiological ranges when movement is minimal, but
data quality declines rapidly with increased activity. These preliminary findings estab-
lished confidence in the Polar Verity Sense as a suitable tool for HRV monitoring under
controlled conditions. The following chapter translates this validation into practice,
detailing the system’s integration within the Pediatric Emergency Department.

represent their actual resting state.

8.3
Dataset Overview
The data collected for this study consisted of both physiological signals recorded from
the Polar Verity Sense and contextual markers that supported the interpretation of
these signals. The device was worn on the wrist and streamed data continuously to
a custom-built application, which logged both raw sensor signals and event annota-
tions. An overview of the collected signal characteristics and data types is presented
in Figure 8.1.
PGG- and accelerometer data were collected using the Polar Verity Sense optical
heart rate sensor.
Photoplethysmography (PPG): Raw PPG signals were recorded at a sampling rate
of 28Hz. These signals formed the basis for subsequent derivation of heart rate and
inter-beat intervals during preprocessing (see Chapter 9). The Polar Verity Sense uses
three pairs of green LEDs to create three PPG channels (PPG0, PPG1 and PPG2), along
with an ambient-light channel for background noise compensation. The PPG signal
shows raw light-intensity values and does not have a standard physical unit, since
each manufacturer uses its own internal scaling (Polar Verity Sense Support, personal
communication, 2025).
Accelerometer (ACC): Three-axis movement data were collected to identify periods
of restlessness or motion artefacts in the PPG signal. These data also provided a po-
tential complementary indicator of stress-related movement, as suggested by clinical
staff.

Contextual Markers
To support the interpretation of the physiological signals, the custom Android applica-
tion designed for the study included the functionality for marking context during the
data collection. The contextual markers allowed timestamping of clinical events and
situational prompt within the data collection session. In this way, the app allowed
raw PPG data to be synchronized to clinical activities,providing essential reference
points for later analysis.
• Timestamps: The application stored precise start and end times for each record-
ing session.
• Clinical events: Key moments in the examination room (e.g., blood sampling,
doctor explanations) were manually annotated as event markers to allow align-
ment with physiological data.
• Self-reported anxiety (VAS): Where possible, children’s subjective distress was
recorded using the Visual Analogue Scale (VAS).

• Observational notes: Notes on visible signs of anxiety (e.g., crying, fidgeting,
caregiver interventions) were made to contextualize the physiological signals.
These contextual markers ensured that physiological recordings could be linked to
specific clinical and behavioral events, supporting a more comprehensive interpreta-
tion of the HRV data. Event annotations were done manually during data collection
which resulted in a minor time delay in seconds between the observed event and the
time stamp. This minor delay would be expected to affect the exact alignment with
physiological data, but not the interpretation of the more generalized HRV patterns
and should be considered in future real-time applications. The app had predefined
event buttons to minimize this delay, but were not consistently used, as each child’s
examination was following different events that required flexible, real-time annota-

about comfort, perceived intrusiveness, and their willingness to wear the device again
in a clinical setting. These responses provided qualitative insights into the user expe-
rience of the wristband.

8.5
Practical Challenges of Data Collection in the PED
In addition to technical and ethical considerations, the practical realities of conduct-
ing research in the PED presented considerable challenges for the research. The PED
operates in a constant state of urgency and unpredictability, with the arrival of each
patient fully determined by the needs of the clinical environment.
A central challenge was the unpredictability of timing. When a child arrives at the PED,
they are assigned a triage number, which indicates when the child should expect to
be examined by a doctor. Depending on this priority, the waiting could range from
one hour to six hours. The position of a child on the list was not fixed, it could change
if new patients arrived with a higher urgency level, resulting in previously scheduled
examinations being delayed. This meant that not only was the opportunity to collect
data infrequent, but also subject to constant revision, requiring prolonged periods of
waiting and readiness while not knowing when a session would begin.

Coordination around the actual start of consultations was equally demanding. Before
a child was called in, the doctor had to log their name in the hospital system, which
then appeared on a large overview screen in the nurses’ common room. This was
the only sign for the researcher to know that the child was about to be called in. The
time span between the doctor signing in the child’s name and calling the child to
the consultation was usually a matter of minutes. Some doctors entered the nurses’
room to discuss with staff before calling the child, others proceeded directly, leaving
almost no time to prepare. Missing this short window meant losing the entire data
collection opportunity, as clinical care could not be delayed for research purposes.
As a result the researcher needed to be constantly monitoring the overview screen,
combined with the need to rush equipment into the consultation room and set up
within seconds.
Finally, the independence required in this setting further intensified the challenge.
Doctors and nurses, as always, were concerned only with clinical care and frequently
forgot about the ongoing study. The researcher could not expect reminders or co-
ordination and needed to be acting independently to align with the clinical workflow
without disrupting care.
In total, the researcher spent approximately 29 hours on site across three days in the
Pediatric Emergency Department, resulting in data collection from 13 participating
children. In addition, five families were approached but declined participation.
All these factors show that feasibility in this context goes far beyond technical reliabil-
ity of the wearable device. It is also depends on the researcher’s ability to deal with
uncertainty, wait long periods of time, and move quickly with a sense of urgency in a
high-pressure environment.

adjusting the sweatband, initiating the recording). Comfort was always prioritized,
including checks on device fit.

Signal Preprocessing
Building on the data collected in the Pediatric Emergency Department, this chapter
describes the processing steps required to convert raw Polar Verity Sense PPG sig-
nals into a clean signal suitable for HRV analysis. This involved careful filtering and
artifact rejection covered in this chapter through steps including signal preperation,
detecting peaks, and extracting interbeat intervals (IBI), to ensure reliable and physi-
ologically meaningful data for analysis. The analytical outcomes from the processed
signals are presented in Chapter 12.
9.1
Signal Preparation and Filtering
The raw PPG data were streamed from the Polar Verity Sense in SDK mode and logged
with timestamps. The procedure used to prepare the signal was as follows:
1. Time alignment: Time stamps were converted from nanoseconds to seconds
to establish a uniform time axis.
2. Sampling rate estimation: The effective sampling rate (fs) was calculated from
timestamp differences, following guidance from HeartPy documentation (paul-
vangentcom, 2017).
3. Ambient light correction: An ambient channel provided by the device was
subtracted from each PPG channel prior to further analysis, in order to reduce
the effect of background noise and sensor offset (KennethEvans, 2021).
4. Channel combination After this correction, the three available PPG channels
(ppg0–ppg2) were averaged to form a single composite waveform. This mean-
combination strategy reduces random channel-specific noise while preserving
the underlying cardiac rhythm, and has been recommended for improving ro-
bustness in multi-channel PPG (KennethEvans, 2021).
5. Band-pass filtering: A band-pass Butterworth filter (order 2) was applied to
the PPG data, from 0.5 - 5.0 Hz, to isolate the heart-rate component of the PPG
signal. The goal was to remove low frequency drift, as well as high frequency
noise. Bandpass ranges between 0.5–10 Hz are commonly reported for PPG
preprocessing [86] [87].
6. Accelerometer alignment: The accelerometer magnitude signal was converted
to numeric values and linear interpolation was applied to fill in missing samples,
creating a continuous and time-aligned motion trace. To maintain consistency
with the PPG data, the signal was downsampled by the same factor and stan-
==PAGE:70==
CHAPTER 9. SIGNAL PREPROCESSING
dardized for visualization, allowing direct comparison with IBI fluctuations on
the same time axis.
7. Down-sampling: To reduce computational load and align with HRV analysis
standards, the filtered signal was optionally downsampled to approximately 25
Hz using decimation. This step is consistent with the approaches described in
HeartPy (Gent et al., 2019).
9.2
Peak Detection and IBI Interval Extraction
HeartPy, a Python library optimized for PPG peak detection, was used to identify
heartbeats in the filtered signal. The output included metrics such as BPM, mean
IBI, SDNN, RMSSD, pNN20, and pNN50 (Gent et al., 2019).
9.3
Criteria for Usable Data
To ensure that heart rate variability (HRV) metrics were calculated only from reliable
recordings, explicit criteria were applied to define usable data. These criteria bal-
anced both signal quality (proportion retained) and the absolute duration of artifact-
free data:
9.3.1
Recording-level inclusion criteria
• 5 minutes minimum: Recordings were only included if at least 5 minutes of
artifact-free data remained after preprocessing. This threshold aligns with es-
tablished standards for short-term HRV analysis, as described in Section 2.2.3.
• Low rention: Recordings with low retention (e.g., 30–40%) were still included
if the absolute usable duration exceeded 5 minutes. For example, a 25-minute
recording with 30% retention yields 7.5 minutes of usable data, which satisfies
the inclusion criterion. Even with substantial data loss (>30%), time-domain HRV
metrics such as RMSSD and SDNN can remain valid, provided that the remaining
data are artifact-free Sheridan et al. (2020),
9.3.2
Recording-level exclusion criteria
Recordings with less than 5 minutes of usable data after preprocessing were excluded
from HRV analysis, regardless of percentage retained.
9.3.3
Window-level inclusion criteria
HRV metrics were calculated during independent, 5-minute windows. To ensure re-
liability at this level of analysis, each window needed at least 80% valid IBI intervals
for the entire window. Windows that did not meet this criterion were excluded from
61
==PAGE:71==
CHAPTER 9. SIGNAL PREPROCESSING
windowed analysis (e.g., heatmap visualizations) even if the overall recording met the
recording-level inclusion criterion.
The complete preprocessing pipeline (Figure X) was implemented in Python using
open-source libraries (pandas, SciPy, HeartPy). It included timestamp normalization,
ambient correction, multi-channel fusion, zero-phase band-pass filtering, and down-
sampling to 25 Hz. HeartPy was then used to extract beat-to-beat intervals, apply
robust inter-beat interval cleaning, and compute HRV metrics globally and within
5-minute windows. Only physiologically plausible intervals (300–1200 ms) were re-
tained, and data coverage thresholds (≥5 min recording, ≥80% window validity) were
enforced. The resulting clean, standardized PPG signals formed the basis for HRV
analysis presented in Chapter 12. The following chapter builds on this preprocessing
stage by detailing the analytical methods applied to assess HRV during short pediatric
emergency examination sessions.

Data Analysis
This chapter details the analytical approach used to evaluate heart rate variability
(HRV) in short pediatric emergency examination sessions. The goal was not to estab-
lish statistical proof but to assess the feasibility of extracting meaningful HRV features
from brief, real-world recordings. Emphasis was placed on time-domain measures
such as RMSSD, which are reliable in short segments and sensitive to parasympa-
thetic activity, while SDNN was included for completeness but interpreted with cau-
tion. The analytical workflow and selected methods provided the basis for the visual-
izations and interpretive results presented in Chapter 12.
10.1
Objectives and Workflow
The objective of the analysis was to determine whether HRV features derived from
the processed PPG data could reveal patterns related to children’s anxiety during
emergency care. The workflow followed a structured process:
1. Extraction of inter-beat intervals (IBI) from pre-processed PPG data
2. Calculation of HRV metrics (RMSSD, SDNN, pNN20, pNN50)
3. Alignment of HRV metrics with annotated clinical events
4. Preparation of outputs for visualization and comparison
Only data segments meeting the inclusion criteria outlined in Chapter 9 were included
in the analysis. Each recording was required to contain at least five minutes of artifact-
free data, and every analysis window needed a minimum of 80% valid inter-beat inter-
vals (IBIs). Calculated HRV values were also reviewed against established physiolog-
ical ranges reported in prior HRV research to verify plausibility and exclude outliers
before further processing. All analytical steps were implemented in Python using the
HeartPy and Pandas libraries (Kwizera, 2025) . Since event annotations were automat-
ically synchronized with the PPG stream during recording, these contextual markers
were already embedded in the dataset and available for analysis and visualization.
An overview of the complete analytical workflow is illustrated in Figure 10.1.

This chapter describes the visualization methods designed to help make heart rate
variability (HRV) interpretable and more useful in a clinical setting. Using the analyt-

HRV results into clear formats that highlight changes in physiological change during
pediatric emergency examinations. The resulting figures and visualization outcomes
are presented in Chapter 12.
11.1
Purpose and Role of Visualization
Data visualization was important for both analysis and communication. Since the
dataset included several layers such as physiological signals, event annotations, and
user feedback, using visual tools helped reveal connections that were not obvious in
the raw data.
The main purposes were:
1. Exploration: to examine temporal patterns in HRV and their relation to clinical
events or motion
2. Interpretation: to contextualize physilogical variability within short, dynamic
sessions
3. Communication: to translate complex signal data into interpretable forms for
clinical and research audiences.
By combining quantitative HRV measures with qualitative event data, visualization
enabled a holistic understanding of what occurred during each session and how dif-
ferent factors interacted.
11.2
Visualization Strategy
The visualization approach was guided by the structure of the dataset and by the
need for readability in a clinical research context. Different visualization types were
developed for distinct analytical purposes:
Time-series visualization: were developed to explore how heart rate variability (HRV)
evolved over time and across different timeframes of the pediatric examination room.
The aim was to observe changes in HRV and relate them to specific clinical events.
By plotting inter-beat intervals (IBI) together with event annotations and accelerom-
eter data, it became possible to identify when variations in IBI occurred and to inter-
pret their potential causes. This approach also helped detect signal artefacts: when

quality throughout each session.
Comparative and summary visualizations
To complement the time-series analyses, comparative visualizations were designed
to provide an overview across all participants. Since the study aimed to assess both
feasibility and variability of HRV among children, aggregated representations were
used to highlight patterns of consistency, deviation, and data completeness.
Heatmaps were applied to compare HRV values across children during the initial

Pie charts and bar plots were used to visualize children’s feedback on wearable com-

fort ratings and willingness to wear the device again, these charts helped reveal key
factors influencing the acceptability and practical integration of wearable sensors in
the pediatric emergency department.

• Clinical readability: Graphs used intuitive time units (minutes) and minimal

As described in in Section 9.3, inclusion was applied at two levels: recordings were

for these children, but the resulting estimates were physiologically implausible (e.g.,
RMSSD >180 ms). Ultimately, that is the main reason ≥80% coverage was retained for
the main analysis. This distinction highlights the difference between overall feasibility
and the validity of specific time segments.

12.5
Nurse Evaluation of HRV Visualizations
To explore the interpretability of HRV data from a clinical perspective, five pediatric
emergency nurses completed a short evaluation survey of two visualization formats,
as shown in section 7.1.1 in Figure 7.3. The survey aimed to assess whether nurses
could identify phases of heightened anxiety, evaluate the clarity of the visualizations,
and provide feedback on the potential clinical usefulness of HRV-based visualizations.
A detailed overview of individual responses is provided in Appendix A.13.
12.5.1
Participant characteristics
Five female nurses participated in the evaluation, ranging in age from under 30 to
over 50 years. Four of the five participants were registered nurses, and one was
an acute care nurse. Clinical experience varied, with two participants reporting less
than one year of experience and the remaining three reporting between one and
over twenty years. Only one nurse had prior experience working with children in an
emergency department setting.
12.5.2
Variation in interpretation.
The nurses’ responses differed notably between visualization types. For the time-
series plots, 20% identified triage as the phase of greatest anxiety, 40% selected the
examination room, and another 40% identified the waiting room. When shown a
version with exaggerated inter-beat interval (IBI) time frames, agreement increased,
with 60% identifying the doctor’s examination as the most anxious phase.
In terms of clarity, 60% of participants rated the time-series plots as moderately easy
to understand (score 3), while the remaining participants rated them as easy or very
easy (scores 4–5). Regarding clinical usefulness, 80% rated the visualizations as po-
tentially useful (score 3). All nurses provided ratings of 4 or higher for the design
quality, including colours, axes, and titles. Confidence in applying such visualizations
in practice varied, with scores ranging from 2 to 5.
For the bar plot and heatmap visualizations, differences were again observed in how
nurses identified the phases when children appeared most anxious. However, most
participants agreed that these formats were useful for evaluation or documentation
purposes. Regarding the heatmap, a large majority (80%) indicated that they could
quickly identify when a child appeared anxious, while one participant (20%) noted
that one child seemed anxious throughout the entire session. When comparing pa-
tients, 60% perceived patient 2 as the most anxious and 40% selected patient 3. All

12.6
Summary of Key Findings
Wearable PPG recordings were successfully obtained from thirteen children during
routine clinical examinations. Most participants tolerated the device well, and data
collection was completed without disruption to care. Signal quality varied, but ten
recordings were analyzable and eight met criteria for short-term HRV analysis. HRV
results showed substantial variability across participants, with both stable and dy-
namic patterns observed across sessions. Accelerometer data provided contextual
information on movement, with peaks aligning closely to annotated activity periods.
The nurse evaluation highlighted clear differences in how visualization formats were
interpreted. Responses to time-series plots were varied, with nurses identifying dif-
ferent phases as the most anxious, whereas the heatmap format produced greater
agreement among participants. Most nurses rated the visualizations as clear and
potentially useful for clinical documentation, and all found the color scheme mean-
ingful. Qualitative feedback emphasized the importance of contextual information
and the ability to focus on individual phases or shorter time intervals when interpret-
ing HRV patterns. Together, these results summarize the main findings presented in
this chapter.

their implications for feasibility, data interpretation, and the potential clinical value
of wearable-based HRV monitoring in children.

Discussion
This chapter discusses the findings presented in Chapter 12 in relation to study’s prob-
lem statement, focusing on whether it is feasible to collect usable HRV data from
children during short examination-room visits in a PED using a wrist-worn wearable,
and whether such data can be presented in ways that nurses find interpretable and
potentially actionable.
13.1
Research Question 1
Can wearable devices reliably collect usable HRV data from children in a
pediatric emergency department, given movement, short examination times,
and noise?
This study provides preliminary evidence that wearable HRV monitoring is both ac-
ceptable to families and technically possible in the PED context, though reliability
must be considered conditional.
From a practical perspective, the device was well tolerated, with all participating chil-
dren wearing it for the full examination and no disruptions to care. Parents expressed
enthusiasm toward the design and wristbands, suggesting the device may have pro-
vided a small positive distraction during stressful visits.
From a technical perspective, around 60% of the recordings contained sufficient data
for analysis, while the remainder were either too short or affected by signal loss. For
this study, three recordings were not able to be processed, and some recordings were
below the five minutes inclusion threshold. Such failures were related to observable
child behaviors, such as playing with the blinking light or adjusting the wristband,
highlighting that feasibility challenges are not random noise but directly linked to
predictable aspects of pediatric interaction. These findings mirror earlier reports that
wrist-based PPG is particularly vulnerable to movement compared to ECG and that
children are especially prone to signal loss due to restlessness and curiosity.
Taken together, the findings suggest that feasibility in this context should be defined
as conditional feasibility. Reliable HRV data should be achievable in suitable contexts,
such as secure fit of the wristband, and relative stillness, though these conditions may
not be consistently generalizable across all patients. This distinction is important for
clinical translation. For nurses, practical feasibility does not mean that every child’s
HRV can be analyzed, but that most likely provide usable data, with some failures
expected. In practice, this means careful attention to device placement, ensuring
the wristband is tight enough to maintain stable contact without causing discomfort.

Further improvements in hardware design and signal preprocessing are necessary
before reliable clinical deployment can be achieved. Finally, broader validation with
a larger and more diverse sample is needed to determine the proportion of cases
from which usable HRV data can be collected.
Having established that HRV data can be collected from most children under appropri-
ate conditions, the next question concerns whether these data hold clinical meaning.
Specifically, it is essential to assess if the collected HRV measures can be interpreted
as valid physiological indicators of anxiety within the pediatric emergency setting.
13.2
Research Question 2
Is the HRV data collected interpretable, providing meaningful physiological
indicators of anxiety in this setting?
Having established that HRV data can be feasibly collected in most cases, this section
examines whether the recorded data were interpretable and reflected meaningful
physiological responses.
The collected HRV values were physiologically plausible and aligned closely with the
short-term pediatric reference ranges reported by Michels et al. (2013), indicating
that the wearable successfully captured authentic autonomic variability. Case exam-
ples illustrate this nuance.
For Child 5, HRV (RMSSD, SDNN, pNN20, and pNN50) followed a V-shaped trajectory:
initially high, dropping sharply in the middle 5–10 min window, then recovering by
15 minutes. This pattern, observed in the absence of physical activity, suggests a
temporary period of heightened arousal that may have been linked to restlessness
or anticipation during waiting, followed by relaxation once the procedure began.
For Child 2, HRV indices increased toward the end of the consultation, with the LF/HF
ratio peaking during the X-ray procedure and declining afterwards. This indicates that
HRV shifts aligned with procedural phases, reflecting situational stress and recovery
rather than a uniform trend.
In contrast, Child 6 showed persistently low HRV alongside flat ACC, consistent with
clinical observations of fatigue. This demonstrates the interpretive challenge: low
HRV may signal sympathetic activation, but in some contexts it may equally reflect
exhaustion or illness.
Together, these findings show that HRV is interpretable in terms of autonomic acti-
vation, but its meaning depends on clinical context. It can therefore be viewed as a
marker of physiological arousal that overlaps with, but is not limited to anxiety.

Building on this, the next step is to examine how HRV changes correspond to specific
moments during the examination.
13.3
Research Question 3
How can the collected HRV data be analyzed to reflect a child’s experience dur-
ing the examination room session and how specific triggering events may relate
to HRV changes
Having shown that HRV changes are interpretable in physiological terms, this section
examines how those changes can be analyzed in relation to specific clinical events
and patient experiences.
The combination of HRV measures, the accelerometer signals, and observational
notes serves to systematically relate physiological change to the child’s clinical ex-
perience. By breaking recordings into short time windows (5-minutes), shifts in au-
tonomic activity can be aligned with concrete events, allowing a time-resolved repre-
sentation of autonomic changes across care phases.
This was evident in individual recordings, where HRV patterns reflected varying phys-
iological and behavioral states. In Child 5, HRV declined steadily during a prolonged
period of dialogue but recovered when the doctor initiated the procedure, suggesting
that arousal was tied to waiting and anticipation. For Child 2, HRV changes aligned
with clinical phases, including movement during the X-ray session and then a restful
state. For Child 6, the consistently lower HRV values and low ACC aligned with visible
fatigue, indicating a different physiological state altogether. This again illustrates that
HRV can reflect not only acute arousal but also broader physiological states such as
fatigue or illness.
Taken together, these cases support that HRV information can be meaningfully seg-
mented into clinically relevant windows and contextualized against movement and
annotations both individually and as a whole. This generates a physiological trace
of the experience of the child capturing subtle patterns of arousal and recovery that
may not be readily observable to clinicians during care. For nurses, such information
could potentially support awareness of a child’s stress level during different phases
of care and guide the timing of interventions such as reassurance or distraction. Ad-
ditionally, the results suggest that HRV is not correlated solely to anxiety: similar pat-
terns may arise from overlapping processes including stress, cognitive load, fatigue,
or illness.

13.3.1
Nurse Interpretation of HRV Visualizations
While the previous analyses focused on how HRV patterns could be quantified and
aligned with clinical events, this section discusses how such information was per-
ceived by nurses in practice. The evaluation provided insight into how visualization
format influenced interpretation, perceived clarity, and potential clinical usefulness.
The differences between visualization types highlight that interpretation depends not
only on the physiological data but also on how it is represented. Responses to the
time-series plots varied widely, indicating that continuous HRV plots require more
technical interpretation and may lead to individualized readings rather than a shared
understanding. In contrast, the greater agreement observed for the heatmap format
suggests that aggregated, color-coded representations communicate physiological
changes more intuitively and consistently among staff.
These distinctions have practical relevance for clinical use. In a pediatric emergency
setting, nurses must make rapid judgments under time pressure. Visualizations that
present an at-a-glance overview are therefore more feasible than those demanding
detailed analysis. Feedback indicating that shorter intervals and contextual markers
made interpretation easier supports the value of embedding contextual information,
such as care phases or annotated events, directly within the visualization.
In summary, the nurse evaluation emphasized that the effectiveness of HRV visualiza-
tions depends on their format and contextual clarity, with aggregated, color-coded
representations supporting more consistent interpretation than detailed time-series
plots.
13.4
Comparison with Literature and State-of-the-Art
Having discussed the findings in relation to the study’s research questions, this sec-
tion compares them with existing literature and current state-of-the-art approaches
in HRV monitoring.
The findings of this thesis align with and extend existing literature on HRV moni-
toring through wearable devices. Prior validation studies [37][40] [35] report that
consumer-grade wearables can approximate ECG-derived HRV under resting or con-
trolled conditions, with multiple studies confirming strong correlations between PPG-
and ECG-based measures. At the same time, these studies emphasize that data qual-
ity declines significantly under motion. The present study confirmed this limitation,
showing that while short episodes of usable data were obtainable in the PED, contin-
uous monitoring across the entire patient journey was not feasible due to movement
artifacts.
86
==PAGE:96==
CHAPTER 13. DISCUSSION
In terms of HRV as a physiological marker of anxiety, the results are consistent with
Shaffer and Ginsberg (2017), who highlighted HRV as a sensitive indicator of auto-
nomic balance, and Silvetti, Drago, and Ragonese (2001), who provided pediatric ref-
erence values. While these studies establish HRV’s theoretical and normative basis,
few have investigated it in short, high-stress pediatric emergency settings. This thesis
contributes by demonstrating that short 5–25 minute recordings can be both feasible
and meaningful in aligning HRV changes with essential clinical events such as medical
explanations or procedures.
As noted in previous studies, the acceptability of wearables in children has been a re-
curring concern in the literature. Mackintosh et al. (2019) reported that while many
children and parents considered wearables usable, issues of comfort affected compli-
ance. Similarly, Shaffer and Ginsberg (2017) emphasized that comfort, fit, and water-
proofing were critical for feasibility, with wrist-worn devices such as the Polar Active
being most tolerated. These findings suggest that acceptance is possible when design
factors align, but challenges remain when devices are expected to be worn continu-
ously in daily life. In contrast, the present study found that the Polar Verity Sense
was well tolerated by children and accepted by parents in short examination-room
sessions, suggesting that prior constraints may be less pronounced when wearables
are used in targeted, time-limited clinical contexts.
Finally, relatively little research has addressed how HRV data should be presented
to clinicians. Most validation work has remained signal- or algorithm-centric, with
limited focus on clinical usability. In this thesis, tailored visualization methods (time-
series plots, heatmaps, bar charts) were developed to illustrate how HRV data could
be made accessible to end-users. Preliminary feedback from nurses suggested that
such visualizations have potential value, although more systematic evaluation will be
required to confirm their clinical applicability.
In summary, this thesis validates prior research on the feasibility of HRV measured
using a wearable device, while extending the state-of-the-art by demonstrating its
potential in a short, high-stress pediatric emergency context and exploring the first
steps toward clinical visualization. Similarly, the work highlighted a number of open
issues and methods limitations, which are relevant when drawing conclusions. The
following section discusses these limitations in more detail and outlines possible di-
rections for future research.
13.5
Limitations
This study has several limitations that should be acknowledged. A key limitation of
the study was the absence of individual baseline HRV recordings, which restricted
87
==PAGE:97==
CHAPTER 13. DISCUSSION
the ability to separate trait variability from acute stress responses. As discussed in
Section 8.2.1, collecting baselines in an acute emergency context was impractical, as
children typically arrive already distressed. Future studies could include recordings
from stable inpatients or during rest periods to establish more reliable reference
values and strengthen interpretation of HRV changes.
Another limitation is the limited availability of HRV reference data for children,
particularly from wearable devices such as wristbands. Most existing studies focus
on older children and adolescents, with minimal data available for those under five
years of age. For instance, in this study, participants aged three and four could not
be directly compared to published reference values, as the youngest age group in
relevant studies began at five years.
The study was also limited by its small sample size and single site implementation at
Herlev Hospital, which restricts generalizability. A larger sample would provide higher
statistical power, enabling more reliable detection of patterns in HRV, and would al-
low to examine variability in HRV across age groups, health conditions, and anxiety
levels. Furthermore, Increasing recruitment through multiple hospitals, would also
strengthen the validity of the study, especially since there may be differences in hos-
pital workflow, access to staff, or physical space.
The nurse evaluation was similarly limited by its small sample size. With only five
participants, the survey could not capture the full range of perspectives or ensure
representativeness of clinical staff. A more iterative and participatory process involv-
ing nurses at multiple stages of visualization design and testing would likely have
resulted in clearer insights into which formats are most interpretable and clinically
relevant.
Another constraint, is the challenges of motion artifacts and loss of signal, contribut-
ing to incomplete data. While most of the recordings were fairly complete, three
recordings were not able to be analyzed, and other recordings suffered from de-
creased usable segments. This limitation is particularly important given the small
sample size. When complete recordings are lost or shortened, the potential to evalu-
ate HRV dynamics throughout the entire clinical visit is further reduced.
Another limitation concerns the consistency of self-report measures. Although age-
appropriate tools such as smiley-based scales were planned, their use was not always
feasible in the fast-paced PED environment. This may have reduced the comparability
of subjective anxiety ratings across children.
Finally, although visualizations were developed and evaluated with nurses, they
were not tested in real-time clinical workflows. As a result, usability and impact in
88
==PAGE:98==
CHAPTER 13. DISCUSSION
practice remain untested.
13.6
Future Work
Taken together, these limitations illustrate the exploratory nature of the research,
while also pointing towards specific areas for future research: establishing individual
baseline recordings to strengthen data interpretation, increasing participant recruit-
ment across sites and populations, reducing motion artifact, and implementing the
visualizations in practice. Future research could also expand to multi-modal combi-
nations to include biomarkers such as galvanic skin response, pupil dilation, or heart
rate. The combination of the above measures could offer more nuanced understand-
ing of children’s stress responses and potentially provide more accurate and reliable
anxiety predictions in real-time clinical settings. Addressing these areas will be critical
for translating HRV-based monitoring from feasibility into meaningful clinical applica-
tion.

Conclusion
This thesis examined the feasibility of using wearable technology to collect heart rate
variability (HRV) data from children in the Pediatric Emergency Department (PED)
and to present this data in a way that is interpretable and potentially actionable for
nurses.
The work combined insights from literature, contextual inquiries, prototyping and fea-
sibility testing, resulting in the development and evaluation of a wearable-based HRV
monitoring prototype for use at Herlev Hospital. Iterative platform testing guided the
final selection of the wrist-worn device Polar Verity Sense, supported by a custom An-
droid application for recording and event annotation.
Short examination-room sessions of 5–25 minutes resulted sufficient photoplethys-
mography (PPG) data for reliable HRV analysis. Despite minor signal loss due to move-
ment, preprocessing and filtering produced usable HRV segments in most cases. Of
13 total recordings, 10 were analyzable, and 8/10 (80%) contained at least five min-
utes of artifact-free data, meeting established criteria for short-term HRV analysis. All
children (100%) wore the device throughout their examination, 77% found it comfort-
able, and 54% expressed willingness to wear it again, confirming both technical and
practical feasibility.
Collected HRV values were physiologically plausible and aligned with age- and sex-
specific short-term pediatric reference ranges. When linked with annotated clinical
events, HRV patterns reflected meaningful autonomic activation during examination,
supporting HRV as an indicator of emotional arousal in this context.
Visualizations such as time-series plots, bar charts, and heatmaps made these dynam-
ics interpretable and were positively evaluated by nurses for clarity and clinical use-
fulness, showing that physiological data can be contextualized to represent a child’s
experience.
Overall, the study demonstrates that HRV-based monitoring in pediatric emergency
care is both feasible and meaningful. Although limited in scale, it provides a method-
ological foundation for integrating physiological sensing into clinical workflows and
highlights the potential of HRV as a complementary indicator of children’s emotional
states. The findings suggest a path toward more data-informed, empathetic, and
child-centered emergency care.

In depth description of Observations at Herlev Hospital
At triage, one child arrived visibly nervous, likely due to previous hospital visits, sug-
gesting possible hospital-related anxiety. The caregiver attempted to use breathing
exercises when the child became distressed and crying, which appeared to help calm
the situation. However, when the child later underwent blood sampling in the exami-
nation room, the calm state quickly changed. Staff attempted to distract the child with
a tablet but were unsuccessful in their efforts. Ultimately, the caregiver took to the
phone to provide distractions to calm the situation. This example demonstrated how
stress can escalate quickly with invasive procedures. Importantly, while distress was
clearly observable, the child’s physical movement remained limited, meaning that no
excessive activity was present that could have caused substantial noise in physiolog-
ical measurements.

A.9
Pilot Test Design and HRV results
Self-monitoring
In the first scenario, a 27-year-old woman wore the Polar Verity Sense while working
on the thesis in a quiet and distraction-free setting. This resulted in a low-stimulus
environment, with minimal physical or emotional variation. In a second session, the
same participant viewed a documentary to explore changes in HRV during emotional
engagement. The purpose was to determine whether the device could reliably cap-
ture low-noise PPG signals suitable for HRV analysis under resting conditions.
Conversational Interaction
The second test involved a 41-year-old woman engaged in a lively conversation. The
session featured expressive hand gestures and emotionally charged dialogue, rep-
resenting a context with moderate physical movement and heightened emotional
arousal. This scenario allowed assessment of how well the system captured HRV data
during natural social interaction, moments of emotional elevation and movements.
Gaming
The third scenario included an 8-year-old boy playing a video game. This test was
designed to reflect a real-world pediatric use case, aligning closely with the target
group of this thesis. The child exhibited periods of concentration, excitement, and oc-
casional physical movement. This setting helped evaluate how the system performs
under cognitively stimulating and emotional conditions typical for children.
• Young Adult: The recording under calm, minimal-movement conditions pro-
duced stable and physiologically valid HRV values. The heart rate was approx-
imately 87 BPM, which is within the normal resting range, though slightly ele-
vated, possibly due to focused activity or environmental factors. RMSSD and
SDNN suggested high parasympathetic tone, consistent with a relaxed state.
This validated that the processing pipeline and device are reliable under con-
trolled conditions.
• Young Adult (Watching documentary): The recording during passive emo-
tional engagement (watching a documentary about a missing person) produced
stable and interpretable HRV values. The heart rate was approximately 71 BPM,
well within the normal resting range. RMSSD (69.1 ms) and SDNN (64.4 ms) were
slightly lower than in fully relaxed conditions, suggesting a mild reduction in
parasympathetic activity. Despite the absence of physical movement, the emo-
tionally charged content appeared to influence autonomic balance subtly. This
finding illustrates the sensitivity of HRV to affective states even in physically pas-
sive scenarios.

• Child: The child participant also showed healthy HRV, with slightly lower values
of RMSSD and SDNN, and a heart rate of approximately 80 BPM. The signal
remained stable, indicating that the device can reliably measure pediatric HRV
when the child is calm and stationary.
• Adult: In contrast, the participant who engaged in active arm movement and
verbal conversation showed implausibly high HRV metrics RMSSD, and a low av-
