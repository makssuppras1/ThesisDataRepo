Abstract
The thermal mass of buildings can be increased by integrating phase change materials
directly in its structure (walls) or as HVAC components (radiant ceiling panels). This helps
reduce temperature fluctuations and peaks in the indoor thermal environment. Consider-
ing their high thermal capacity per unit volume, PCM integration is especially relevant for
building refurbishment. This study investigated the influence of different cooling condi-
tions on a novel macro-encapsulated PCM panel (MEP) with embedded pipes. The Exper-
iments and a simulation were created to characterize the panel and provide the basis for
creating design curves and design methods.
The average cooling power of the panel during occupancy was between 6 W/m2 to 15.3
W/m2 with the lowest observed for the scenario where ventilation was integrated, and the
greatest value was observed for the scenario with increased internal heat gains. The re-
sults confirmed that a high supply water temperature ranging between 16 and 21 °C was
able to discharge the panels, despite having no significant impact on the indoor thermal
environment. However, reduction in supply water temperature and/or an increased water
flow rate could result in a significant shorter pump operation time, and vice versa. The
supply water temperature resulted in a maximum difference of approximately 9 hours with
a temperature difference of 5 °C. The PCM model created in IDA ICE was validated as the
operative temperature, and water extraction were within the requirements for simulation
models given by VDI 6020 standard, with an RMSE of 0.35 °C and 54.9 W, respectively.
However, the model should be further improved as the cooling power of the panels was
outside the requirements with an RMSE of 88 W.
Higher supply air temperatures may be used in conjunction with PCM systems than with
traditional all-air ventilation systems for the same indoor thermal environment - which may
lead to energy savings. However, the results show that further investigations were needed
to improve the operation of the system as the indoor thermal environment was within Cat-
egory II or better from EN16798-1:2019 for only 25 % of the occupied hours. This was
significantly lower than the reference scenario without ventilation, with an indoor thermal
environment within Category II or better for 79 % of the occupied time.

Introduction
In European climates, the need for interior cooling has increased during the last decades.
Highly insulated building envelopes, higher thermal comfort levels required by occupants,
and climate change are all factors contributing to this phenomenon [1, 2]. Another reason
for overheating is the use of large glazing areas [3].
In Europe, the building sector accounts for more than 40 % of the primary energy use
[4]. Moreover, HCAC systems in Europe are responsible for 50 % of the energy use for air
conditioning [5]. As the population continues to increase and the fact that people spend
90% of their time indoors makes indoor environment quality a vital parameter to account
for in new and existing buildings [6, 7].
Numerous studies have shown that the use of phase change materials (PCMs) in build-
ings could improve thermal comfort by minimizing temperature fluctuations, peaks, and
overheating due to their high latent heat storage capacity [8, 9, 10]. The latest advance-
ments in this field have opened up a wide range of phase change material applications,
reflected by the increased research over the last decades [8]. An active cooling system
with PCM may represent an efficient cooling method. By integrating PCM, cooling may
only be required at night, reducing the need of an active cooling system concurrently with
the cooling load. However, more research is required for characterizing the technologies
present and realize design guides for an easy market implementation.
This study focused on characterizing an active cooling ceiling panel with PCM. Experi-
ments were ran to study the effect of different supply water temperatures, flow rates, and
heat gains on the performance of the panel. Multiple control strategies such as water
circulation all-day or only outside occupancy were tested. Moreover, investigating the in-
tegration of ventilation on the performance of the panel. Finally, an IDA ICE model of the
ceiling panel was created and validated, which can further be used for obtaining informa-
tion about the PCM ceiling panel operation in a wide range of scenarios.

Indoor environment in buildings
It has been scientifically proven that a healthy and comfortable indoor environment greatly
influences an occupant’s performance of regular tasks (Appendix A) [1]. Consequently,
indoor environment has gained increasingly more focus when designing buildings and
rooms nowadays [1].
The indoor environment in buildings can, in general, be divided into four main parameters:
Thermal environment, indoor air quality (IAQ), lighting, and acoustics [11]. This thesis will
mainly address the thermal environment and the indoor air quality, but it should be noted
that all parameters of the indoor environment are essential when designing buildings.
This thesis uses the indoor environment recommendations established in the European
Standard DS/EN 16798-1 & 2:2019 [12, 13], as the requirements for the thermal environ-
ment and indoor air quality. Other standards such as DS/EN ISO 7730:2006 [14] and ISO
17772:2019 [15] are used to describe the specific parameters
According to DS/EN 16798-1:2019, the indoor environmental classes are divided into four
categories, I to IV, with different requirement levels as seen in Table 2.1. In this study, the
target indoor environmental quality was Category II.

It is important to note that PMV and PPD is a systematic procedure for evaluating and

categories based on fixed clothing and activity values [6].

Operative temperatures
Due to the earlier mentioned high complexity of determining PMV and PMD, the design
recommendations are given as a function of the operative temperature. Thermal comfort
depends on four physical parameters: Air temperature, mean radiant temperature, rela-
tive humidity, and air velocity, and two personal parameters: Metabolic rate and clothing
insulation [11]. In this study, an activity level of 1.2 met and a clothing level of 0.5 clo will
be assumed, corresponding to a sedentary adult office worker wearing summer clothing.
Moreover, Table 2.3 shows the operative temperature limits for offices and spaces for the
aforementioned personal parameters.

2.1.3
Local thermal discomfort
As mentioned, PMV and PPD express thermal discomfort for the body as a whole. How-
ever, thermal dissatisfaction may be caused by unnecessary cooling or heating of a spe-
cific part of the human body. This is known as local discomfort [14]. Typical parameters
that cause local thermal discomfort are draught, floor surface temperature, radiant tem-
perature asymmetry, and vertical air temperature difference [11, 14]. These parameters
need to be considered when designing the mechanical system that is conditioning the
indoor environment [14].
According to ISO-7730:2006, the design thermal environment for a room can be se-
lected between three categories. All criteria should be satisfied simultaneously for each
category. Each category specifies the maximum PPD and percentage dissatisfied (PD)
for each of the four local discomfort parameters [14]. The different percentages reflect a
compromise between the aim of a few dissatisfied and what is practically feasible using
traditional HVAC systems that aim to condition the entire occupied space uniformly [14].
Table 2.5 provides values for each parameter.

Introduction to Phase Change Material
3.1
Thermal storage
Thermal storage systems (TES) play an essential role in many technologies, including
solar heating systems, cooling or heating dwellings, commercial buildings, and waste heat
applications [18]. It balances out the discrepancies as it shifts the load to a later time. It
tries to sync the active part (e.g. water circulation, ventilation) with the electrical grid by
absorbing/releasing heat passively [19, 20].
All storage systems should have a high energy storage density and a high power capacity
for charging and discharging. According to multiple review papers [8, 19, 21], three meth-
ods of TES are used: Sensible, latent, and thermochemical energy storage (Figure 3.1).
Figure 3.1: Methods of thermal energy storage. (a) Sensible heat, (b) latent heat, (c) ther-
mochemical reaction [19].
3.1.1
Sensible heat storage
With sensible heat storage (SHS), thermal energy is stored by applying a temperature gra-
dient to the material (solid/liquid) to accumulate or release heat [8, 19, 21]. The most
common material used to store heat as sensible heat is water. The two main advantages
of SHS are the reduced price and the lower risk associated with toxic materials [8, 19].
One of the main disadvantages of using SHS in the building sector is the large volumes
required [8, 19]. The stored energy can be quantified using eq. (3.1).
Q = m · Cp · (Tf −Ti)
(3.1)
• Q is the Thermal energy stored as sensible heat [J].
• m is the material mass [kg].
• Cp is the specific heat capacity [J/kg.K].
• Tf is the final temperature of the material [K].
• Ti is the initial temperature of the material [K].

3.2. PHASE CHANGE MATERIALS (PCMS)
3.1.2
Latent heat storage
The latent heat storage (LHS), is based on the heat absorbed or released when the material
changes from one state to another (e.g. solid to liquid) [8, 21]. It is worth noting that each
LHS system also stores sensible heat. However, the sensible heat stored in LHS is very
limited [22]. Ordinary building materials like concrete and gypsum only represent the SHS
capacity, which ranges between 0.75 and 1 kJ/(kgK) [22]. In contrast, the average paraffin
materials that experience phase change have a LHS capacity of approximately 110 kJ/kg
[22]. This ensures that a much lower volume of material is used to retain the same amount
of energy due to the higher heat storage capacity of the material [21, 22]. This process
can be quantified with eq. (3.2).
Q = m · [Csp · (Tm −Ti) + am · ∆hm + Clp · (Tf −Tm)]
(3.2)
• Q is the thermal energy stored as latent heat [J].
• Csp is the average specific heat between Ti and Tm [J/kg.K].
• Clp is the average specific heat between Tm and Tf [J/kg.K].
• Tf is the final temperature of the material [K].
• Ti is the initial temperature of the material [K].
• Tm is the melting temperature [K].
• am is the fraction of material which change phase [-]
• ∆hm is the latent heat per unit of mass [J/k.g]
3.1.3
Thermochemical reactions
According to Gracia et al. [19], thermochemical materials (TCM) store and release heat
through a reversible endothermic/exothermic reaction process (Figure 3.1). During the
charging process, heat gets supplied to the material, which separate into the material into
two parts. The heat can be stored in the two separated parts until the discharge process
is required. Then the two parts are mixed with a suitable pressure and temperature and
energy is realising. [19]. Despite the fact that this technology is the most energy efficient,
it is still in its early stages of development, with no real-world applications in the build-
ing industry [19]. The technology has to overcome essential barriers such as corrosion,
unstable heat, and mass transfer efficiency [19].
3.2
Phase Change Materials (PCMs)
PCMs can change phases at a specific temperature and pressure. According to Akeiber
et al. [8], the phase change process can be divided into four: Solid-solid, solid-liquid, gas-
solid, and gas-liquid. However, in practice, only the solid-liquid form can be used for build-
ing cooling and heating since the other types have technological limitations such as higher
weight and larger volume of material, as well as unstable temperatures [8, 23]. Figure 3.2
shows the temperature T and heat flux of a liquid-solid PCM during solidification as a
result of temperature change ∆T [24].
During the phase change process, three stages are reviewed. The three steps are de-

3.2.2.1
Organic phase change materials
Organic materials are subdivided into paraffin and non-paraffin (e.g. fatty acids, glycols,
polyalcohols, and sugar alcohols [9, 21, 25]). Paraffins are widely used for thermal energy
storage due to their flexibility in phase change temperature ranges, high heat of fusion,
negligible supercooling, lower vapor pressure in melting, and stable behavior [27]. Further-
more, paraffins are commercially available for a reasonable cost, environmentally friendly,
chemically inert, and non-toxic [27]. However, organic PCMs have some drawbacks, as
they have low thermal conductivity at around 0.2 W/mK [8, 9], low volumetric latent heat
storage capacity, and are flammable depending on their containment [9, 21, 24, 25].
According to Kalnæs et al. [9] fatty acids have received the most attention in building appli-
cations. The most common fatty acids used as thermal storage materials are lauric acid,
myristic acid, and stearic acid [9]. However, there are also drawbacks for these PCM types
as they are highly flammable, which restricts them from being exposed to high temper-
ature, fire, and oxidizing substances [8, 24]. Like paraffins, these PCMs also suffer from
low thermal conductivity ranges from 0.15 to 0.17 W/mK [9]. In addition, non-paraffins
can be corrosive [8, 24].
Overall, organic PCMs have many qualities that makes it very suitable for building appli-
cations. However, it should be noted that many organic PCMs are considered flammable,
which is a crucial disadvantage in the building application as it could have a significant
impact on occupant safety [9, 24].
3.2.2.2
Inorganic phase change materials
Inorganic PCMs can be classified as hydrated salts and metals. Metals do not have the
required melting range (e.g. Carbon melts at 3527 °C [25]) for building applications [21].
Moreover, the high weight of metals is not suitable for building applications. As a result,

14

hydrated salts are the most well-known type of inorganic PCM, and several experiments
have used them for thermal storage applications [8]. The phase change transformation
of salt hydrates is a hydration or dehydration of salt, which is a process that is similar to
the melting or solidification process [21]. Hydrated salts usually melt in two ways: Either
they melt into a hydrated salt with fewer moles of water or melt into an anhydrous form,
where salt and water are separated from each other [21].
One of the major advantages of using inorganic PCMs is that they have almost a double
volumetric heat storage capacity (250-400 kg/dm3) compared to organic PCMs (128-200
kg/dm3) [21]. Furthermore, they consist of a higher thermal conductivity (0.5 W/mK) [8],
smaller volume change, lower cost, non/less flammable, and easy availability [8, 24, 21,
9]. A typical problem for hydrated salts is that they suffer from incongruent melting. The
melting process leads to salt releasing water and turning it into its anhydrous form or a
different salt [9]. When an inadequate amount of water is released during the melting
phase, the crystalline structure of the PCM does not melt [25]. At the same time, gravita-
tional forces interact with a significant variation in densities between the solid and liquid
content and cause less hydrated salt to desegregate and settle in the container [25].
Furthermore, hydrated salts can suffer from supercooling, which is caused by poor stabil-
ity properties. When supercooling occurs, the liquid phase of a PCM does not solidify at
the desired temperature, but instead cools to temperatures below the solidification point
[9, 27]. This is a serious problem since it changes the phase change temperature [9].
3.2.2.3
Eutectic phase change materials
Eutectic mixtures are a combination of at least two components [9]. Both components
should melt and solidify congruently without segregation, and during the melting process
all components simultaneously change to liquid [8, 21]. In the crystallization stage, a
mixture of components is formed, which act as a single component. These mixtures can
consist of inorganic-inorganic, organic-organic, or organic-inorganic [8, 21]. This gives a
wide variety of combinations for the specific application [9].
Eutectic mixtures can obtain more of the desired properties such asspecific melting point,
as well as higher heat storage and capacity per unit volume [9]. Even though the eutectic
mixture has vast potential, its properties still need further investigation as many combi-
nations have not been tested [9].

3.2.4
PCM application in buildings
To ensure PCM can be successful in building applications, PCM and PCM-container sys-
tems should present long-term stability without showing any signs of degradation. Two
factors are essential to avoid. Firstly, using poor stability materials which can trigger su-
percooling, phase segregation, and corrosion between the PCM and the PCM container
system [9]. Secondly, the degradation of PCMs may lead to a decreased ability to store
latent heat and thereby change the phase transition temperature [9].
Kalnæs et al. [9] created an overview of how the PCM technology has already been com-
bined with several products. The type of PCM has been limited to organic and inorganic
products. The PCM enhanced materials include e.g. wallboards, floor tiles, ventilation
systems, mats, and tubes [9]. Furthermore, Kalnæs divided the most common application
into four categories: Free cooling, peak load shifting, active building systems, and passive
building systems. [9]. The PCM panels were employed as ceiling panels for cooling pur-
poses in this study. As a result, this section will discuss the strengths and weaknesses
of the application.
One of the essential advantages of using a combination of PCM and a hydronic system
is the ability to shift the heat loads from the peak hours to a later time, where it can be
discharged [31]. During the day, the added thermal mass will absorb and store heat from
the room, which allow the mechanical cooling to be reduced throughout the day. The PCM
panels can be discharged during the night with higher inlet temperatures than traditional
all-air devices, which improve the energy efficiency of the chiller. [9, 30]. Moreover, the
energy cost is lower at night, resulting in cost effective electricity usage. [30].

Research concerning PCM in buildings
The literature was chosen based on either the experimental work, simulation tool, or the
way to actively couple PCM to a cooling system. The scientific papers have been struc-

Koschenz et al. [33] developed a thermally activated ceiling panel with micro-encapsulated
paraffin for incorporation in lightweight and retrofitted buildings. The panels had a total
thickness of 5 cm and the PCM constitute 25 % of the total weight. By incorporating alu-
minum fins, Koschenz increased the thermal conductivity of the panels from 0.2 W/mK
to 1.2 W/mK. Moreover, a water circulation system was used to remove the heat from the
panels. The study showed that a panel construction with a water circulation of 15 kg/h
per m2 at 18 °C from 22:00 to 06:00 could keep the room temperature at an acceptable
range [33].
Zhou et al. [34] reviewed thermal energy storage with PCMs in building applications. Ac-
cording to the authors, the phase change temperature of PCM in building applications
should be between 18 and 30 °C to meet thermal comfort. In addition, the following PCM
properties need to be considered: Chemical stability, flammability, and if the material is
compatible with the constructional material. According to Zhou, previous studies have
shown that latent heat storage with PCM in walls, ceilings, and floors has a significant
impact on reducing temperature fluctuation by storing the energy during peak hours. The
simulation and experimental work clarifies that a PCM incorporation can reduce the in-
door temperature significantly. However, due to the lack of long-term stability and other
issues that may affect the safety, reliability, and feasibility of thermal energy storage, fur-
ther research on the combination method is still required [34].
Tzivanidis et al. [32] conducted numerical and experimental studies to establish the most
relevant factors for PCM panels with embedded pipes for water circulation. A parametric
analysis was performed to evaluate pipe spacing, PCM layer thickness, pipe depth, cooling

Eilersen and Krintel [35] studied the effect of different PCM types with different heating/-

which had a significant influence as the discharge of the PCM would be solely dependent
on the outdoor temperature fluctuations. The study revealed a potential for energy sav-
ings of 25.7% when integrating PCM ceiling panels. As a consequence of not using active
discharge, the study showed that if the chosen PCM did not suit the climate or the heat

indoor climate. In addition, the study showed that the high cost of PCM products resulted
in more than 20 years of payback time for the majority of the climates [35].
Nielsen [36] used IDA ICE to simulate the indoor environment in a two-person office. The

tems was tested during the cooling season in Copenhagen, Denmark, and Rome, Italy.
This study found that the all-air systems created an acceptable indoor environment and
resulted in the lowest energy use. The use of TABS, radiant panels, and PCM panels in-
creased the energy use compared to the all-air mechanical cooling system by 126%, 106%,
and 101%, respectively. In Rome, the mechanical cooling system resulted in the highest
energy use. The use of TABS, radiant panels, and PCM panels had energy reductions
of 35%, 39%, and 39%, respectively. However, in both locations, TABS provided the best
conditions for the thermal environment. Compared to previous research, the integration
of PCM had a limited impact on the indoor environment and energy use. According to
Nielsen, this can be explained by the low PCM content, non-optimum conditions between
the room, and PCM heat of fusion temperature. The PCM properties were lower than the
theoretical values and numerical limitations related to the PCM in the IDA ICE software.
Therefore, the behavior of active PCM ceiling panels was only verified to a limited extent
in this research [36].

28 mm, and 38 mm) and water supply temperature (14 °C, 15 °C, 16 °C, and 18 °C). The

3.3.1
Limitations of previous studies
There is sufficient information on the applicability of PCM and the material properties.
This makes it easier for future studies to continue research in the right direction. The
literature review revealed that using PCM for indoor thermal conditioning is a promising
technology. However, the technology still requires improvement. PCMs are designed to
be installed for their entire lifetime (up to 30 years [45]) and cannot be removed without
special intervention. The research discussed is mainly concerned with the cooling season,
with little to no long-term data included. Therefore, long-term measurement should be
investigated to ensure the stability of the use of PCM. Several field experiments have been
performed in recent years, but PCMs in real buildings are still limited.

Methodology
For this study, several experiments have been conducted from February 2021 to May 2021
to investigate panels containing PCM. These experiments were carried out at Denmark’s
Technical University, Lyngby. The used methodology is similar to the one presented in
previous studies [10, 43].
The experimental chamber was located in the ICIEE Laboratory. Chamber 6 was
designed as a two-person office with water running through the ceiling to observe the
behavior of the panel and the performance in obtaining a comfortable indoor thermal en-
vironment. In addition, a ventilation system was added for one of the experiments.
4.1
Office room
Full-scale measurements were carried out in the same climate chamber as in Bogatu et al.
[10], located on the ground floor inside a laboratory at the Technical University of Denmark
(DTU), Lyngby.
The dimensions of the room were 4.2 m x 5.4 m x 3.2 m, W x L x H. The 0.595 m x 0.595
m ceiling panels were installed in the suspended ceiling at a height of 2.7 m to fit a sus-
pended ceiling tile of 0.6 x 0.6 m, W x L. This formed a plenum between the suspended
ceiling and the ceiling slab of 0.5 m. In total, 48 active ceiling panels were installed in
the chamber covering 76 % of the total ceiling area, 22.7 m2 (Figure 4.1c). The remaining

a rate of 1.5 K/0.5 h until it reaches the desired comfort level of 23 - 26 °C [43, 48]. The
maximum temperature increase in 30 min is 1.7 K/0.5 h [49]

4.2.2
Cooling system and water distribution
A pipe system was installed in the plenum of the chamber. A series of four manifold pipes,
two for supply and two for return, was extended over the length of the room, alongside
the walls (Figure 4.1c). The coils of the panel were attached to each other. The manifold
pipes with flexible connections created a pipe network ( Figure 4.1c) to maintain a bal-
anced temperature and flow rate distribution across the ceiling. The chamber received
cold water from the laboratory’s cooling system through manifold pipes on the left side of
the room (Figure 4.1b). Water was then distributed equally across the length of the ceiling
in supply-return loops (Figure 4.1c) before being extracted via the return manifold piping.
The water circulation system was not installed in the middle row of the suspended ceiling
as this was reserved for lighting and ventilation systems (Figure 4.1c).
Two different water operation strategies were used in the experiments: One available dur-
ing occupancy (08:00 to 18:00) and another outside occupancy (18:00 to 08:00). For each
scenario the two schedules could be used independently or together. In the first strategy,
with a water operation schedule running during occupancy, the starting setpoint was set
at 26 °C with a deadband of ±1 °C.. This ensured the water circulation started when the
operative temperature reached 26 °C and ended if the operative temperature went below
25 °C. This schedule was added to ensure the operative temperature did not exceed 26 °C
during occupied hours according to Category II DS/CEN/TR-Standard 16798-2 (Table 2.3).
The second strategy was available outside occupancy (18:00 to 08:00), where the water
circulation started with a setpoint of 22 °C with a deadband of ± 1 °C. This system ensured
that the water circulation system was running until the room met an operative tempera-
ture of 21 °C. This was chosen, as it was assumed that an operative temperature of 21 °C
would fully discharge the PCM [10, 43].
4.2.3
Ventilation control
Two diffusers were installed in the middle row of the ceiling: One for supply and one
for extract (Figure 4.1c). A schedule for the ventilation system was created, operating
between 06:00-18:00 with two different ventilation rates if used. Between 06:00 and 08:00,
a ventilation rate of 0.5 air change hours was used and between 08:00-18:00. Here a
higher ventilation rate, which complied with the number of occupants present in the room,
was used (Section 2.2). The ventilation control was set to have an equal minimum and
maximum damper opening to ensure a constant air volume (CAV) during the operation.
During operation time, the supply air temperature was kept constant with a fluctuation of
± 0.2 °C. However, a temperature difference of 2 °C was registered between the morning
period and the period during occupancy. This can be explained by the low ventilation rates,
as the supply air was only delivering to a single room and the damper was being closed
further during the morning period to get a lower flow rate. This means that the air flow
rate in the pipes was relatively low compared to a ventilation system that had to supply
an entire building. Therefore, two different air temperatures were used for the morning
period and the occupied period.

macro-encapsulated PCM ceiling panel (MEP). Therefore, only cooling conditions were

In all scenarios, the heat gains from occupants, computers, and lights were active between

PCM assumption of enabling full utilization (full discharge) of the added thermal mass
[10, 43].
During the discharge/solidification phase, different supply water temperatures and flow
rates were used. A more detailed description of each scenario can be found in Table 4.6.

was decided based on a maximum water velocity of 1.2 m/s due to noise problems [51].

difference for these scenarios was the location of the occupants (Figure 4.5a and Fig-

influence the MEP’s charging and discharging. An assumption was made that more heat
was extracted right above the heat gains in the room.
In the second group of scenarios, the supply water temperature (18 °C) and flow rate

these scenarios, different total heat gains and water circulation operation was examined.
Higher total heat gains were implemented for these scenarios corresponding to a three
and four-person office with higher solar heat gains. In addition, different water circulation
operation was examined for both scenarios. HHG A and HHG C had similar water circu-
lation operations as scenario Ref (running outside occupied hours). HHG B and HHG D
had the water circulation running for the entire day (with the settings explained in sec-
tion 4.2.2).

Reference scenario
MEP operation
As seen in Figure 5.1 the melting phase ended just before 18:00 for the Ref scenario,

the PCM during occupancy, and preparing the system for a new cycle with a supply water

tive temperature was below 23 °C in 75 % of the occupied hours. One way to optimize this
scenario would be to increase the supply air temperature setpoint. This would reduce the
cooling power, which could lead to energy savings. Another way to improve this specific
scenario is to change the operation control and have water circulation ending after the
Top reached 22 °C. This way, the MEP would not get fully discharged and less heat would
be absorbed by the panels. However, the operative temperature would have a starting
temperature at around 22 °C, which would lower the time before the temperature reached
the desired temperatures according to Bourdakis et al. [43]. Another way to increase the
indoor thermal environment would be to decrease the air flow rate. However, this would
cause a lower air quality in the room and Category II for the air quality in the room would
not be obtained [12].
5.2.7
Discussion
Panel construction
The MEP consists of a radiant panel with PCM poured directly over the pipes to provide
direct thermal contact [10]. This results in a higher discharge rate as seen in the experi-
ments and by Bogatu et al. [10]. It was observed that the MEPs had a greater influence
on the indoor thermal environment when water circulation was active, as the MEP would
cool the room while discharging the PCM. This was due to the pipe positioning as the
pipes were in direct contact with the bottom steel surface [10]. As seen in Figure 5.11
and Figure 5.12, this has a positive effect on the experiments where cooling was needed
during occupancy, by removing the extra heat faster and thus conditioning the indoor en-
vironment.
Operation and performance
In scenarios with the same stationary heat gains and solar heat gain schedule (Figure 5.8),
a significantly higher heat extraction was seen by the water circulation system rather than
by the PCM panels. Same trend was observed in other studies [10, 43]. This indicates the
presence of residual heat in the MEP, and that the assumption of the PCM completely
regenerating if the operative temperature would reach 21 °C during the unoccupied time
might be incorrect. Nevertheless, during the occupied hours (08:00 and 18:00), the MEP
could absorb the accumulated heat and sustain a temperature of less than 26 °C for all
of the experiments.
As an operative temperature of 21 °C does not guarantee a complete discharge of the
PCM, another way to control the system should be considered in order to obtain a com-
==PAGE:67==
5.2. RESULTS
55
plete discharge. It could be considered to lower the setpoint of the operative temperature
for when the water circulation ends. However, the setpoint was selected to avoid over-
cooling as Bourdakis et al. [43] suggest a minimum of 20 °C. Therefore, it is difficult to
decrease the operative temperature further. Another solution could be to use the surface
temperature of the panels as an indicator or creating a model of the PCM and integrat-
ing it in the control. However, the latter options might give a better result in regards to
discharging the PCM completely, but by doing this, no control of the thermal indoor envi-
ronment would be present. It should be noted that the different supply water temperatures
and water flow rates either increase or reduce the duration of the water circulation, and
thereby could be used to optimize the operation of the MEP. Another solution could be to
use another PCM with a higher melting range (e.g. 22-25 °C or 22- 26 °C).
A change in location of the stationary heat gains from the centre to the side of the room
showed a change in the surface temperature in the ceiling. It could be seen that the sur-
face temperature of the panels increased right above the heat gains, which should in-
crease the cooling power for the panels with a higher surface temperatures. Even though
the cooling power of the panels increased for the Loc scenario, the variation between the
the measured heat flux for the left and right side of the ceiling did not change signifi-
cantly. This could be explained by the some of the panels on the right side was close to
fully charged already when the occupied hours began. However, these variations in the
surface temperatures could be reduced by adding mixing ventilation to the room, which
would create an even air distribution over the surface of the panels.
The higher heat gains in Scenario HHG A and HHG C showed that the MEP could not
shift the entire heat load to off-peak hours. However, the panels absorbed more heat for
these scenarios than the scenarios with lower internal heat gains (THGA1). As a result,
the chamber reached operative temperatures above the upper limit of Category II for the
cooling season, 26 °C [12], after only 4 and 5 hours (Figure 5.11 and Figure 5.12). It was
shown that activating the water circulation during the day helped maintain the operative
temperatures within the acceptable limits.
5.2.8
Cooling power
The cooling power of the MEPs during occupancy was between 6.0 and 15.3 W/m2. How-
ever, a difference of maximum 1.8 W/m2 was registered for all scenarios with the same
internal heat gains. It was shown that the average measured cooling power of the panel
increased as the internal heat gains rose. This was expected, as the as the enthalpy of
the PCM increases with the temperature. The lowest cooling power values were observed
for the Vent scenario and was significantly lower than the other scenarios. However, the
measured and calculated cooling power was following values reported using the same
chamber [10, 43].
Different cooling powers were registered over the ceiling region (Figure 5.7), showing an
unequal heat absorption throughout the surface of the ceiling (Figure 5.8 & Figure 5.14).
However, the estimated cooling power between the room and the MEP (Qcalc,PT ) followed
the same trend during occupancy as the registered heat flux (Qmeas,HF ) for all the scenar-
ios. The disparity between the means of Qcalc,PT and Qmeas,HF on the other hand, varied

5.2.9
Water circulation
Lower supply water temperature and a higher flow rate improve the discharge rate. For
Des B and Des C, the water circulation did not reach the desired operative temperature
setpoint of 21 °C. However, a greater operative temperature in the morning had no sub-
stantial influence on the indoor thermal environment. In the long run, this could present
an issue, as the PCM never gets fully discharged and residual heat would affect the PCM
further when the occupied period began. However, the PCM system provides flexibility in
terms of selection of water supply temperatures and water flow rates, making it compat-
ible with an extensive range of renewable energy sources [30, 40].
Furthermore, showed Des C that a supply water temperature of 21 °C still discharge the
panels, thus the supply water temperature is equal to the lower limit of the melting phase.
This proves that no supercooling effect is present in this phase change material. This was
in line with the findings of Bogatu et al. [26, 30]
5.2.10
Ventilation
In one of the experiments ventilation was added leading to a Category II thermal environ-
ment and below for 25 % of the occupied time. This was significantly lower than the other
examined scenarios. However, the scenario showed that almost all heat added to the
room was extracted by the ventilation or the MEPs during the occupied hours. As lower
operative and surface temperature was registered in this scenario, the PCM panel could
not be fully utilized. Based on previous scenarios, the PCM could serve as a sufficient
supplement to other sources of heat extraction as they have the ability to shift the heat
loads to the off-peak hours. In order to improve this scenario, a higher supply air tem-
perature setpoint could be used, or by having the ventilation and water circulation control
communicate. This might also improve the energy savings as the air doesn’t have to be
significantly cooled during the cooling period. Moreover, the risk of draft will be reduced,
as supply air temperatures gets closer to the operative temperatures used.
5.2.11
General considerations
The present study showed that the temperature conditions of the laboratory played a sig-
nificant role in the conducted experiments. Even though the laboratory temperature dif-
ference is small for each experiment, the operative temperature inside the chamber varied
==PAGE:69==
5.2. RESULTS
57
with more than 0.8 °C. In order to control the heat gains from the outside into the room, the
same outdoor conditions were needed. Another solution could be to increase the num-
ber of sensors, so the heat gains/losses can be calculated more accurately. As only one
sensor was placed outside the room for these experiments, assumptions had to be made
when calculating the heat transfer through the walls. A uniform air temperature outside
the laboratory was assumed even though this might have not been the case. Addition-
ally, the heat flux through the floor could only be assumed based on standard equations,
as no sensors were placed on the floor. In order to properly determine the heat balance,
more sensors would be beneficial for higher accuracy. However, the results show the im-
portance of having the same outdoor temperatures to compare the experiments with a
certain accuracy.
Due to short research periods, there is insufficient information on the application of PCM
in buildings as a thermally active building structure. Therefore, more research is needed,
such as field tests to determine the benefits and drawbacks of active cooling panels using
PCM in real-world situations and to define effective control strategies to test the operation.
Further research on PCM could also investigate the stability of the PCM by evaluating the
efficiency after several phase transitions.
As explained in Bogatu et al. [10], the experiments had approximately 76 % of the ceiling
area covered by MEPs. This is quite high as the usable ceiling area is confined by the
existence of other systems such as ventilation, fire alarms, sprinklers, and lighting fixtures.
However, values of 50 % or less were found in the literature [18] for ceiling panel field tests.
As a result, the total energy absorbed in the room would be reduced. The thickness of
the PCM layer could be increased to provide the same total heat storage capacity for a
lower active ceiling area. Nevertheless, due to the limited heat conductivity of the PCM,
an increase in PCM thickness would have minor impacts [32].

6.1
IDA ICE
The software used in this analysis is IDA ICE 4.8 SP 2. IDA ICE is a dynamic building sim-
ulation tool used to model whole buildings or smaller spaces. The application is used
to study the indoor environment and the energy consumption of the entire building [54].
Furthermore, the IDA ICE tool is adaptable to local requirements, including climate data,
standards, special systems, special reports, products, and material data [54]. Finally, the
computed results can be compared with the measured data to validate experimental re-
sults [54].
IDA ICE provides a graphical interface from which the user can design and simulate
a system. Components are added, linked, and configured to meet the user’s needs. In
addition, various toolbar options support the construction of the building or a particular
area.
EQUA developed three stages of software. For this model, the second level and the
advanced mode have been used. It was used to define the office room, location, weather,
ventilation system, i.e. to define the room parameters. Then, a more detailed model is cre-
ated for improvements in the advanced environment using Modelica-like Neutral Model
Format (NMF) coding [54]. Finally, the advanced setting was used to import measured
data and improve or change the operating system of the individual components such as
PCM, hydronic circuit, heating net, and weather data.

A mathematical model of different temperature enthalpy relations during melting and so-

data provided by the manufacturer [46] only provide a part of the temperature range (16
to 31 °C), a difference in total heat capacity was shown. The total heat capacity given
by the manufacturer for melting and solidification was 165 kJ/kgK and 161 kJ/kg, re-
spectively. Therefore, was the partial enthalpies used in the simulation model marginally
adjusted to ensure that both melting and solidification had the same total heat capacity -
a requirement of the IDA ICE software. Figure 6.7 Illustrates the adjusted data used in the
simulation model.

7.3
Discussion
The operative temperature trends from the experimental and the simulated model, with
a maximum difference of 0.4 °C. This is significantly lower than the maximum difference
observed by Nielsen [36] and Pavlov’s studies [58] where a maximum temperature differ-
ence of 0.8 °C is seen. As seen from Figure 7.1, the average surface temperature differs
significantly from the experiment to the simulated model. This major difference can be
explained by the uniform ceiling temperature in the model. Moreover, a simplification
was made as the heat conductivity of the PCM was increased to 1.2 W/mK instead of
implementing an aluminum fin. Additionally, the registered surface temperature in the
simulated model was extracted from the lower part of the PCM layer, instead of the bot-
tom metal plate as in the experiments. This could result in a slower response time for the
experimental studies.
The main difference between the experimental and simulated model was an increased
cooling power of the PCM in the simulated model. During occupancy, the PCM had almost
a double maximum mean cooling power as the examined Ref scenario (Figure 7.1). Some
of these major differences can be explained by the higher surface temperatures, or the
fact that the simulated model only had two big surfaces instead of 48. Moreover, as seen
in Appendix E, the heat extracted by the PCM in the simulated scenario was equal to the
calculated total energy absorbed by the panel (243 Wh/m2). This means that the PCM in
the simulated model was fully utilized, which was significantly lower for the Ref scenario
(160 Wh/m2).
Even though it is difficult and time consuming to validate models and experts are needed,
models are beneficial as they can easily validate data without creating an even more time
consuming experiment. Having a validated model would help create design curves that
people in the building sector could use to dimension the panels. This could be done by
running a parametric analysis for the validated model, which would help market integra-
tion of the PCM. As a validated model could save time for engineers, as it would be able
to create graphs of what should be integrated in the panel in order to optimize the indoor
environment (e.g. thickness of PCM, supply water temperature, water flow rate, supply air
temperature etc.).

7.3. DISCUSSION

Discussion
Within this study, the characterization of a novel ceiling panel containing PCM was ana-
lyzed through experimental research with the aim of locating how changes of the operat-
ing system affected the PCM panels.
All the external heat gains could not be accurately accounted for since certain indicators
such as infiltration and heat loss through the ceiling and floor could not be quantified. In
order to qualify how the PCM reacted to specific changes, outdoor conditions needed to
be constant across the scenarios. Another way to mediate these issues, is to increase
the number of sensors in the experiment to account more accurately for all external heat
gains. The sensors placed, measured the indoor conditions with high accuracy, however,
heat losses through walls, and especially through the floor, cannot be calculated to a sat-
isfying extent at present time.
The results from the varying operation parameters showed a significant change for the
discharge rate. A lower supply water temperature seems to have a greater impact than
increasing the water flow rate. This corresponds to the results shown in previous studies
[18, 32]. However, Des E with a water flow rate of 220 kg/h, registered the highest cooling
power, but the starting operative temperature was also correspondingly higher. A more
linear curve was shown for the scenarios with a water flow of 128 kg/h, and after 20:00
Des A was extracting more heat than Des E, even though the operative temperature for
Des E was greater at that point.
Des C demonstrate that the PCM used did not have a supercooling effect even at tem-
peratures as high as 21 °C, the discharge of the panel was enabled. The was in line with
Bogatu et al. [26, 30]. Nevertheless, the novel ceiling panel containing PCM showed flexi-
bility by being able to discharge the panels during night and to change operation system.
The results from the varying operating parameters and same internal heat gains showed
that changing the operation had marginally impact on the indoor thermal environment.
However, it should be noted that these categories from EN 16798-1:2019 are only based
on the operative temperatures within the occupied hours. However, Bourdakis et al. [43,
48] suggest a temperature in the morning between 20 and 21.5 °C for countries/environ-
ments where most people commute on foot or bike, which is not taken into consideration
in the standard. However, it can be seen that different control strategies had a great im-
pact on the indoor thermal environment.
In this study, energy savings and the economy associated with PCM panels were not in-
cluded. However, this was already studied by Boccardo et al. [45]. His findings showed
that as cooling loads increased, the cost difference between PCM ceiling panels and all-
air systems decreased. However, the operation strategy also has an impact on the energy
use of the system. Therefore, is it not only relevant to optimize the operation system ac-
cording to indoor thermal environment. This make a validated model even more relevant,

Conclusion
An experimental analysis was made to study the influence of different conditions on the
panel, which would help towards creating a design guide. The performance and load shift-
ing ability was experimentally compared by varying operating parameters for the active
cooling system at night, extending water circulation schedule, changing the location of oc-
cupants, including higher heat gains in the room, and by including ventilation. The main
conclusions are summarized as follows:
• The operation of the MEP as a high-temperature cooling system was confirmed by
the high supply water temperature ranging between 16 and 21 °C. Despite having
no significant impact on the indoor thermal environment, a reduction in supply wa-
ter temperature and/or an increase in water flow rate, would result in a greater dis-
charge rate and shorter pump operation time, and vice versa.
• Throughout the melting and solidifying stages, the steel box macro-encapsulation
maintained the PCM’s characteristics (enthalpy distribution and phase transition
range). Because of the piping profile position across the thickness of the MEP, the
steel box worked as a radiant cooling panel with extra thermal mass. This was found
to be an advantage in situations where active cooling is required during the day, as
the MEP cooled the room while being discharge. This further reinforce the findings
of Bogatu et al. [10].
• Even though a temperature of 21 °C. was employed, the results in section 5.2.2
demonstrated that the PCM did not present a supercooling effect. This is in line
with the findings of Bogatu et al. [26, 30]
• The addition of the PCM allows for higher supply air temperatures for the cooling
season. However, further investigation is needed to improve the operation of the
system, as a suboptimal strategy would have a negative impact as shown in the
results.
• The PCM model created in IDA ICE was validated as the operative temperature and
water extraction were within the requirements for simulation models given by the
VDI 6020 standard. However, the model should be improved as the cooling power
of the PCM was outside the limits. This error could be reduced by validating the
model to the other presented scenarios.
• A validated IDA ICE model would allow obtaining information about the PCM opera-
tion in a wide range of scenarios. The model can therefore be used to create design
curves which could be used by design engineers which speeding up the design pro-
cess while reducing costs.
• The cooling power of the PCM was between 5.3 30.2 W/m2 with an average between
12.3 and 15.3 W/m2 for the scenarios with higher heat gains during melting. When

Future Work
For the future work, it would be relevant to compare the created simulation model with
the other presented scenarios. This could be done to reinforce the precision of both the
operative temperature and water extraction in the created simulation model. It would
also give an indication on what errors were obtained or experienced in the model, which
ultimately could help improve the simulation model in order to get closer to a design guide
for the building sector for the implementation of the PCM panels. In addition, it would
be interesting to expand the period to a yearly investigation examining both the cooling
and heating season, as this would complete a performance comparison for a whole year.
Moreover, would it be beneficial also focus on the energy use of the different strategies
and operation systems (e.g. supply temperatures, flow rates etc.), which could lead to
a more detailed comparison of the different systems and the economy associated with
implementing PCM.
Moreover, it could be interesting to compare different simulation tools in order to examine
the different implications and limitations for each simulation tool. For instance, several
simulations have been conducted with the use of TRNSYS [18, 30, 33, 43]. Furthermore, it
would be interesting to increase the number of sensors in order verify how much influence
the laboratory temperature had on the chambers external heat gains. It would specially
be beneficial on the floor as no sensors has been placed on the floor at this point.

NMF code for the PCM in IDA ICE
This appendix describes the PCM mathematical model that was employed in the simula-
tion investigation. The mathematical model is written in NMF [59] which is the program
language used by IDA ICE. This model is created by Lars Eriksson, a member of EQUA
simulation AB. Heat capacity, density, and thermal resistance are used to calculate the
temperature of the material in the liquid and solid states. Thermal resistance, density,
and partial enthalpy coordinates are used to calculate the temperature of the material
during solidification and melting. Thermal resistance and density of a solid are employed
during the melting phase, while thermal resistance and density of a liquid are employed
during the solidification phase; however, with this phase change material, these values
are the same for both phases. Specific heat of 2000 J/kgK is employed during reversing
(transition from melting to solidification). The NMF code is as follows:

   IF      NINT(Mode) == -2 THEN           /* solid phase */ 
     CUsed := cpSol; 
     RUsed :=  RSol;

   ELSE_IF NINT(Mode) ==  2 THEN           /* liquid phase */
