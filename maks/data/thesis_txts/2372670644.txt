Abstract
June 30, 2017
Abstract
A large demand for renewable energy is expected in the near future. Some of the energy
needs to be stored, and this can be done by converting electricity to hydrogen. Electrolyzers
are developed for this purpose, converting water and electricity directly into hydrogen. To
split the water, a catalyst is needed, which today is IrO2 or RuO2 in a Proton Exchange
Membrane Electrolyzer Cell (PEMEC). To lower the amount of catalyst needed, replace
existing expensive supports (Ir metal), or to enhance performance, it can be deposited onto
a support, and this support is the focus of this work.
Three materials have been selected for testing: LaCrO3, Sc-doped TiO2 and Cr-doped TiO2.
TiO2 has been used as support before, but an improvement of it is desired. The methods
used were XRD-measurements to see if the materials synthesized were pure, corrosion
experiments in acid to test the stability in an environment similar to that in a PEM-
electrolyser, and conductivity measurements to see if the support materials can transport
the electrons created by the catalyst. The doping level of Sc in TiO2 was very low, at most
1 at% Sc was able to go into solid solution with the TiO2. The conductivity of the Sc-doped
TiO2 was at the same level or lower than for pure TiO2, and is thus a bad candidate for the
catalyst support. In the Cr-doped TiO2, the conductivity increased 108 times compared to
pure TiO2, which makes it a very good candidate for a support. All doped TiO2 samples
was found to be stable in acid.
Mg-doped LaCrO3 displayed the highest conductivity
of all the candidates investigated, but was not quite stable in the corrosion experiment.
Nonetheless the Mg-doped LaCrO3 is a promising support material.

Introduction
In 2050, many countries - especially European - have goals of drastically reduced greenhouse
gas emission, and greatly reduce the usage of fossil fuel. For example, the UK has a goal
set of 80 % reduced greenhouse gas emission in 2050, compared to 1990 levels [1]. Denmark
has a target of being independent on fossil fuels by 2050 [2]. The German government
has decided (16th November 2016) to cut CO2 emissions by 80-95 % [3]. The eﬀect on all
these initiatives is primarily to limit the global warming, on which almost all nations of the
world has agreed ”to keep well below 2 ◦C warmer than pre-industrial level” in the newly
signed Paris agreement [4]. Grand politics play a just as large role as the technological
advancement - the President of the United States, Donald Trump, has expressed his desire
to leave the Paris Agreement [5]. The US is the biggest economy in the world, and emits
almost 18 % of the human-made greenhouse gasses [4], and thus has the potential to be a
big factor in reaching climate goals. Many technologies are to be used for the respective
goals to be fulﬁlled. For the energy supply sector, renewable energy sources are the main
candidate for producing power with no CO2-emissions during operation. Hydropower has
been a well-established power source for more than a century, wind power has experienced
a massive growth in the past few decades, and photovoltaics (solar power) is probably
the fastest growing energy industry on the planet. Nuclear power likewise does not have
any CO2-emission associated with it’s power production, however it produces dangerous
radioactive waste.

1.1
Hydrogen Society
”The hydrogen society” is based on access to clean, renewable energy. For most stationary
applications, electricity directly from the grid can be used - basically to power every device
in a modern house (lights, stoves, TVs, computers, washing machines and so on). However,
two problems arises, where renewable energy can’t be directly used as it is produced:
• Mobile applications, mainly cars and buses, must have an energy source on board
(only electric trains are exempt from this rule). At present, this energy source is
almost universally a fossil fuel (gasoline, diesel, fuel oil etc.) that all emits CO2.
There are several suggestions for carbon-emission free energy sources, where hydrogen
is one possibility, and which will be the focus here.
• The energy is not necessarily available when needed. The energy demand is highest
in the evening (around 7:00 pm) [6], but the wind is blowing at relatively random,
and solar power is only available during the day, and only when the sun shines. Some
renewable sources, like hydropower, can be harvested when needed.
For nuclear
power, the opposite problem can be present, as it is fairly diﬃcult to change the
output of the nuclear plants.
The role of hydrogen would be to function as an energy vector. It would be produced from
water, stored until needed, and then converted back to electricity - probably via a fuel cell
- when and where it is needed. The only waste product will be water, which is harmless to
the environment - it will simply be an extra step in the water cycle.
The focus of this work will be on the hydrogen production part with an electrolyser. The
electrolyser focused on in this work, will be the low-temperature Proton Exchange Mem-
brane electrolyzer, but the other forms of electrolyser cells are described as well.
1.2
Working Principles of a PEMEC
The working principle of a Proton Exchange Membrane Electrolyzer Cell (PEMEC) is that
it converts electric energy to chemical energy, by splitting water into oxygen and hydrogen.
For a single cell, as shown in Figure 1.2, the electric ﬂow can be followed: The electrons
are added to the right-hand side, at the copper current collector. It is conducted through
the ﬂowplate, the carbon GDL (Gas Diﬀusion Layer) and down to the cathode Pt catalyst,
where it reacts with the H2O, and produces H2. The water is constantly provided from
an inlet at the ﬂowplates, and the produced H2 is diﬀused through the GDL and out
at the ﬂowplates. The electric current continues through the proton-conducting Naﬁon-
membrane, but as protons moving in the opposite direction than the electrons, from left to

right on the ﬁgure. The protons are created by the oxidation of water on the anode side,
where water is split into oxygen and electrons on the IrO2 catalyst. The electrons continue
from the catalyst through the Ir-metal powder layer, and into the Ti-felt. This Ir-metal
is the primary focal point to be substituted by a non-precious-metal support. The electrons
continue out through the Ti-ﬂowplate, and out to the current collector. As the reaction
is endothermic, the electric current loses voltage, which is ideally similar to the chemical
energy gained. The main sources of losses are
• The reaction at the anode, splitting water into oxygen, protons and electrons, is
relatively ineﬃcient, even with a catalyst. This is a large fraction of the loss at low
currents
• The H+ ion transport through the membrane. This is loss is ohmic, which means it
is directly proportional with the current
Other, but typically minor losses, are:
• The reaction at the anode. Even though it is very eﬃcient, it will always be respon-
sible for a small voltage loss.
• The electron transport, which happens through the whole cell, apart from the mem-
brane. Also ohmic, but typically much lower than the membrane with state-of-the-art
(expensive) materials.
• Mass transportation limitation - if the gas diﬀusion layer can’t transport the product
gasses quickly enough to the ﬂowplates, the catalysts will become saturated, which
will lead to a huge loss.
This is extremely insigniﬁcant at low currents, but can
become very important at high current, and eﬀectively limit how much current can
be put through.
On Figure 1.2, the electrons go out through the copper current collector. In a cell stack,
the titanium ﬂowplate is placed ”back-to-back”, and the electrons resume into the next
cell. It may be noted that the two ﬂowplates will be be placed with channels perpendicular
to each other, so the O2 and H2 gas ﬂows are also perpendicular to each other, to increase
safety and make the engineering easier.

1.3
Alkaline Electrolyser Cells
The alkaline electrolyser is a developed technology for hydrogen production in the megawatt
range. It is the most extended electrolytic technology at a commercial level global. The
alkaline electrolyte consists of a potassium hydroxide solution at a level of 20-30 % KOH.
At the cathode the water is decomposed into gaseous hydrogen and hydroxide ions. The
hydroxide ions travel through the diaphragm towards the anode. The diaphragm separates
the two electrodes, and should be permeable to hydroxide ions but not to the product
gasses. At the anode the hydroxide ions decomposes into gaseous oxygen, electrons and
water. The water molecules produced at the anode will travel back through the diaphragm
to the cathode. The half-cell reactions on the electrodes and the net reaction are expressed
in equations 1 to 3.

The materials used as containment materials, electrodes and diaphragms need to be highly
corrosion resistant at operating temperatures around 80 ◦C. The containment is in gen-

a relative low cost [7, 8].
The alkaline electrolyzers have three main issues.
• The diaphragm, it is not eﬃcient in preventing the product gasses to diﬀuse across.
This leads to lower eﬃciency of the electrolyzer and menace the safety (mixture of
hydrogen in oxygen).
• The alkaline electrolyzer has a low maximum achievable current density, caused by
the high ohmic losses across the electrolyte and diaphragm.
• The inability to operate at high pressure, also due to the liquid electrolyte, which
enforces the requirement for a bigger stack design conﬁguration [7].
1.4
Solid Oxide Electrolyzer Cells
In a solid oxide electrolyzer cell (SOEC) the water is dissociated into hydrogen gas and
oxygen ions at the cathode. The hydrogen is collected at the cathode, and the oxygen
ions are transported through the electrolyte to the anode. On the anode the oxygen ions
are oxidized into oxygen gas and collected.
The operating temperature for SOECs are
varying from 700-1000 ◦C. Operating at high temperatures provide a high eﬃciency and
the opportunity of utilizing waste heat. The half-cell reactions on the electrodes and the
net reaction are expressed in equations 4 to 6.

cially the electrodes. Further improvement in the development of inexpensive and stable
electrolysis cells are necessary before commercialization of competitive SOECs.
1.5
Molten Carbonate Electrolyzer Cells
Another high temperature electrolyzer is the Molten Carbonate Electrolyser Cell (MCEC),
which is operated around 600 ◦C. The MCECs conduct CO32– ions from the cathode to
the anode. However the MCECs require not only water but also carbon dioxide - for the
production of carbonate ions. This increases the electrochemical reactions which decreases
the overall eﬃciency. However, the direct electrolysis of carbon dioxide to carbon monoxide
may take place, yet the kinetics of this reaction is much slower compared to electrolysis of
water on nickel-based electrodes. If the cell is operated under high pressure or has high
conversion, the production of methane through the methanation process may take place as
well [12]. The half-cell reactions on the electrodes and the net reaction are expressed in
equations 7 to 9.
Anode :
CO32−−−→CO2 + 1
2O2

Cathode :

(8)
Net reaction :
H2O + CO2 −−→H2 + 1
2O2 + CO2

The electrolyte in a MCEC is a mixture of lithium and potassium carbonates; sometimes
sodium carbonate can replace or be included in the mixture. The electrodes are separated

by a porous LiAlO2 matrix, which is supporting the electrolyte. The high temperature
cancels the need for noble metals to facilitate the electrode reactions. Therefore nickel is
typically used on the anode and nickel oxide on the cathode. Furthermore, the requirement
of advanced ceramic materials for the higher temperatures are not needed [13, 14].
The MCEC can be operated in reverse - then it becomes a Molten Carbonate Fuel Cell
(MCFC). The MCFCs are much more mature as technology and have reached commer-
cialization in USA, Japan, South Korea etc. It is the leading fuel cell technology in terms
of installed power generation units [14]. This opens the opportunity for reversible molten
carbonate fuel cell (RMCFC), which is capable of operating as a fuel cell or a electrolyzer
cell. This could possibly improve the eﬃciency and the economic beneﬁts of the system.
But so far the performance and durability of both MCEC and RMCFC is not very clear.
In fact, during long-term MCEC operation, the cell voltage initially increased and then
slightly decreased, instead of increasing continuously. Another problem is the corrosion
stability of the materials, since the electrodes can be dissolved in the electrolyte. Hence
further research in the MCEC technology is necessary in order to achieve improved and
competitive MCECs [12].
1.6
PEM-Electrolyzers
A PEM-based (Proton Exchange Membrane) electrolyzer was ﬁrst made by General Elec-
tric in 1960, when they succeeded in creating a thin, proton conducting membrane based
on (solid) polymers, called Naﬁon. Naﬁon quickly became the membrane used, as the prop-
erties which made it useful in a fuel cell are the same that are needed in an electrolyzer cell:
High proton conductivity, electron insulating, a 20-300 µm thickness which can be used
up to 350 bar pressure, and a hydrogen permeability of 1.25*10−4 cm3s−1cm−2. PEM-
electrolyzers are capable of operating at more than 2 A/cm2, which is comparable to fuel
cells and considerably higher than alkaline electrolysis. PEM-electrolyzers can also be oper-
ated from 10 % load to full load with a fast responsetime, something both high-temperature
electrolyzers and alkaline electrolyzers are unable to [7].
Catalyst Material
The catalyst materials in PEM-electroysers are operating very harsh conditions. Platinum
is not useful as an OER catalyst as it is as ORR catalyst - it corrodes at the pH value
of approx. 2 combined with a voltage of 2 V. Instead, Iridium is used as active catalyst.
Iridium is 10 times more rare than platinum, which already represents one of the biggest

Reducing the Precious Metal Usage
Eﬀorts have been made to mix IrO2 with non-noble metals or -compounds. In 1995, it
was showed that SnO2 mixed in a liquid acid with 10 % IrO2 rendered the surface of SnO2
almost completely saturated with Iridium. However, OER was not demonstrated for this
compound. More extensive research was conducted in a mixture of IrO2 –SnO2 –Ta2O5,
which showed very good properties of surface area, electronic conductivity, charge storage
capacitance, and most importantly, OER activity. This could still be achieved at an Ir
content of 15 mol%. The challenge for this catalyst is the incompatibility with current
collector plates and membranes - the mixture was only deposited on solid titanium. This
resulted in low (almost geometrical) surface area, and the impermeable plates are not
possible to use in an MEA. A range of diﬀerent non-noble oxides can be used in conjunction
with IrO2, but they are not actively catalyzing the OER. Promising results was achieved
by molecular modelling of the OER with either a mixture of Ir and Ru oxides, or ternary
structures of Ru/Ir/Sn oxides. Only the Ru/Ir oxide catalysts were tested, and with a
catalyst loading of 0.6 mg/cm2 at the anode, 1 A/cm2 was achieved at 1.72 V [7].
Catalysts are usually not tested directly in a PEMEC, but in a RDE (Rotating Disk
Electrode; newer systems are often Rotating Ring Disk Electrodes, RRDE). This is an
electrode set up in a three electrode system, where the working electrode is rotating between
100 and 1600 RPM to eliminate (or control) mass transportation losses, and get detailed
information of the reaction taking place on the anode [7].

Cathode catalyst
On the cathode side, the Hydrogen Evolution Reaction is taking place. Like in fuel cell
development, the hydrogen-reaction has a much lower activation energy than the oxygen

graphite, and wolfram nanorods - however, the activity is not on par with platinum. Like

Membranes
The membrane in the electrolyzer cell is a critical component, which transports the H+
from the anode to the cathode. As in fuel cells, neither gasses (O2 and H2) or electrons
are allowed to go through the membrane - to avoid a decrease in the eﬃciency. The similar
properties required leads to the same membrane being used: The Naﬁon membrane, which
is a copolymer of tetraﬂuoroethylene and a sulfonic acid (perﬂuoro-3,6-dioxa-4-methyl-

1.6.2
Current Collectors
The current collectors are transporting the electrons from the catalyst sites to the separator
plates, diﬀuse the product gas through, and allows the water to diﬀuse through to the
catalyst. Oxygen and a high overpotential of around 2 V on the anode, combined with an
pH-value of 2, also results in high demands for the current collectors; apart from being good
conductors, they must be chemically very stable. Furthermore, a high mechanical stability
is also important, especially in pressurized systems. Thermally sintered titanium powder
is usually chosen as the material to deal with these challenges, which is not extremely
expensive [7]. Some key physical properties have been found from the current collectors:
The porosity should be between 30-50 %, which gives a suﬃcient gas and water transport,
while not compromising the conductivity too much. A mean pore diameter of at least 10
µm, but not much larger was found to be optimal - if these are too big, too many parasitic
ohmic losses would be the result. The particle size for a spherical powder should be between
50 µm and 75 µm, which limits mass transportation losses to capillary eﬀects (too small
particles), while still keeping a good electronic contact [17].
On the electrode surface, gas bubbles are evolved by the reaction, and growing until they
release by the buoyancy force and shear forces. Preferably, these (small) bubbles will be
transported through the porous current collector as a dispersed bubble ﬂow, but the pores
are usually large enough that the ﬂow is slow-moving, and thereby coalesce into larger ”gas
pockets”, creating a slug ﬂow. The slug ﬂow can give mass transportation losses. At high
current densities, and longer into the gas channel is where the ﬂow is becoming a slug ﬂow.

1.6.3
Separator Plates
Separator plates usually don’t get a lot attention in electrolysis development, as they are
not part of the MEA, and often not considered in single-cell experiments (where they
technically are ’backplates’). They contribute tremendously to the stack cost - 48 % of the
price comes from the separator plates, which consequently gives a huge potential economical
saving [7]. The separator plates keeps the stack mechanically together, as well as having
gas channels which transports reactants to and from the MEA. Graphite are a material
commonly utilized to make the plates, and while it in smaller amounts are ”cheap” -
especially when replacing rare metals like Pt or Ir - the bigger size of the separator plates
make graphite an expensive solution. [7] Pure titanium is cheaper than graphite [7], and has
many desirable properties, such as high electrical conductivity, low heat conductivity, low
gas permeability and high mechanical strength. The drawback is stability, as oxygen can
form a titanium oxide layer, which increases thermal conductivity and decreases electronic
conductivity. These problems can be solved with gold or platinum coatings, but this in
turn negates the cost advantage. A less expensive base is needed, and as there are no
development speciﬁc for electrolyzers yet, room for improvement is likely possible. Stainless
steel have been tried, as it is cheap, but too fast corrosion means a coating is necessary -
and satisfactory coating is yet to be made [7].
The separator plates, electrodes and membranes are assembled by pressing them together.
This is done to ensure the materials stick together, to promote the contacts in the cell,
and to prevent leakage. This operation is similar to what is used in fuel cells, and it does
not destroy the MEA. However, a detailed study of possible eﬀects at diﬀerent pressing
magnitudes are not done - especially the eﬀects on the catalyst sites are a point of interest,
as they are somewhat fragile.

2.1
Literature of Catalyst Supports
While IrO2 and RuO2 catalysts often are used without support, improvements can often
be made using a support. With a support, the amount of Ir or Ru is reduced, the dura-
bility can be increased, and the performance can also be slightly increased due to various
mechanisms. It is important the support has a high surface area, is stable at the voltages
and pH which are at the anode, and that is does not inhibit the electron current - either
by being intrinistically electron conducting, or forming a surface of catalyst particles, that
can conduct the electrons.
2.1.1
Sn-based Supports
One of the early investigations of catalysts support for the Oxygen Evolution Reaction was
carried out in 1984 by Hutchings et al , where the interaction between a 50:50 mixture of
IrO2 and RuO2, and supporting SnO2. It was found that the two components could be
mixed to form a metastable phase, which was in contrast to an unsuccessful attempt to
mix IrO2 and RuO2. The RuO2 and IrO2 could undergo complete mixing (or so called
”solution in each other”), due to the similar size of the +4 anions of the metals. They
found the catalyst system had a slightly lower performance initially than ”pure” IrO2/RuO2
catalysts, but after an initial performance degrade had similar performance. Importantly,
however, was that the durability tests showed a signiﬁcantly increased lifetime for the
SnO2-supported catalyst, which was attributed to the metastable phase containing all three
metals [22].

TiO2 nanotubes
TiO2 nanotubes (TiO2 –NTs) were doped simultaneously with both RuO2 and IrO2 by
one-step anodization. It hasn’t previously been successfully attempted to dope TiO2 –NTs
with more than one catalyst component (Ru or Ir), as the chlorine-based precursors will
damage the nanotubes. Uniform doping is also a challenge, since they have high-aspect-
ratio structure, and require ”highly sophisticated and cumbersome pre- or post-processes”
[26]. The simultaneous doping was done with IrCl3, which were heated with KOH to create
IrOx nanoparticles, and KRuO4 as the two precursors. They were anodized in an elextrolyte
consisting of H3PO4, NaOH and 0.3-0.7 vol% HF for between 1-8 hours. The content of
HF is very important, since the F– ions are playing a critical role during the anodization,
as a content of 0.3 vol% HF is not enough to nucleate nanotubes - which means no NTs are
formed. Instead, it stabilize the IrOx nanoparticles, and that has a higher aﬃnity for the
F– ions, and must thus ”use up” all the ions before the nucleation of the nanotubes can
take place. The IrOx has a lower binding energy to the TiO2 substrate than the competing
ion RuO4 –, and is consequently only Ir will be present in the catalyst at 0.3 vol% HF.
At concentrations between 0.4 and 0.7 vol%, the nanotubes are growing perpendicular to
the TiO2 substrate during the anodization. The IrOx nanoparticles are too large to diﬀuse
through the nanotubes, so instead they are deposited at the ”top” (furthest away from the
TiO2 substrate), where they generate IrO4 – ions. The RuO4 – and IrO4 – ions can diﬀuse

When doping with Mg, the solubility limit of Mg in LaCrO3 is between 10 and 20 mol%
[35, 36].
The function of the interconnects are to separate the fuel and oxidant gases,
while having high electronic conductivity. Tatsumi listed the following requirements for
the interconnects [37]:
• Density and gas tightness
• High electronic conductivity without oxygen electrochemical leak
• Chemical stability in both oxidant and fuel atmospheres
• Thermochemical compatibility with the other cell components
In order to fulﬁll the requirements LaCrO3 can be doped with a lower valence ion, such
as Mg2+, at the Cr3+ or La3+ sites. By substitution on either sites, with appropriate el-
ements, can increase the electronic conductivity and decrease the sintering temperature.
The interconnects must separate the fuel gas and the oxidant gases, hence the demand
of a dense sintered body. LaCrO3 is poorly sinterable in air, due to the vaporization of
chromium, which prelimits the densiﬁcation of the perovskite. By substituting Mg for Cr,
the doped LaCrO3 structure obtained a relative density of 96 %, which is above the 94 %
acceptable target [38].
The interconnects must maintain high electronic conductivity under oxidizing and oxidant
atmospheres. Doped LaCrO3 is a p-type conductor, and the electronic conductivity in-
creases with concentration of doping level. When substituting a lower valence cation into
LaCrO3, such as Mg2+ for Cr3+ (which is due to the similar atom dimension), the forma-
tion of Cr4+ will enhance the electrical conductivity. This will establish a formation of an
electronic hole on Cr4+, allowing the conduction mechanism as a small polaron hopping
process through the Cr4+ sites. Additionally, doping LaCrO3 not only lead to the formation
of Cr4+ but also development of oxygen vacancies - the oxygen vacancies are the diﬀusion
pathway of oxide ions [37, 39].

2.3.1
Conductivity
The conductivity of rutile TiO2 at room temperature is reported to generally be low. A
study of a thin ﬁlm got a conductivity of 10 −13 S cm−1, and the activation energy of the
conductivity was estimated to be 0.54 eV. [45]. A data sheet over the conductivity of TiO2
stated the conductivity was between 10−13 and 10−18 S cm−1. Another report found the
conductivity at 400 ◦C was 10−7 S cm, with an activation energy of 1.67 eV. [46]. Generally,
the reported conductivity of rutile at room temperatures are extremely low.
Especially in small samples, the conductivity can vary. The reason are the conductivity
is not the same in diﬀerent crystal planes. The < 110 > plane has a conductivity 5-10
times lower than the <001> direction. This anisotropy was observed in reduced TiO2 -
that is, TiO2 with oxygen defects. Under these conditions, the material was an n-type
semiconductor. The ¡001¿ plane was the best conducting plane, superior to both <100>
and <010> [47].
If TiO2 is calcined in an oxygen-less atmosphere, the conductivities rise by several orders of
magnitude. Experiments with doping of TiO2 typically use this as a baseline. For example,
a paper reported conductivity of amorphous TiO2 to be 0.03 S cm−1 [48]. This conductivity
didn’t change in various oxygen pressures, and doping with minor amounts (around 5 %)
Ta did not aﬀect the conductivity by much either. However, as the TiO2 was heated by
10 ◦C per minute up to 550 ◦C in vacuum (”Annealed”), the anatase phase were formed,
which drastically increased the conductivity to 100 S cm−1. The reason for this was quite
complex, but the main reason was formation of double oxygen vacancies in the lattice, due
to the defects giving easier electron paths. Another experiment in doping rutile titanium
oxide with 3 % Nb, increased the conductivity from 10−5 S cm−1 to 20 S cm−1 - but this
was also done in oxygen-free atmospheres [49].

The crystal structure of Sc2TiO5 was rhombic, with lattice parameters A=10.105, B=3.843
and C=10.272 ˚A. By (rapidly) cooling down the mixtures, the original compounds Sc2O3
and TiO2 reformed. They also showed the solubility of TiO2 in Sc2O3 was 5 mol%, while
the solubility of Sc2O3 in TiO2 is 2 % (in the paper, the opposite was actually written, with
solubility limits of 5 % Sc2O3 in TiO2, and 2 % TiO2 in Sc2O3, but that seems to be a mis-
take as it contradicts the phase diagram). The report also describes the optical properties
of the compositions, and it was found that the refractive index of diﬀerent compositions
was improved. The refractive index is inversely proportional to the dielectric constant,
which is proportional to the conductivity, which suggest mixing Sc2O3 and TiO2 lowers
the conductivity compared to the pure compounds. Doping TiO2 with 1 mol% Sc2O3 gives
the crystalline structure of the compound the lattice parameters A=B=4.598, C=2.959 ˚A,
with an uncertainty of 0.001 ˚A. [51]. This is slightly larger than pure rutile, which has
lattice parameters of A=B=4.594, C=2.958 ˚A [52].
2.3.3
The Cr2O3/TiO2 system
Mixing of various amounts of Cr2O3 with Titanium oxide was investigated by E.A. Belaya
et al [53]. They showed that under 6.5-7.4 wt% Cr2O3, the system will consist of only the
rutile phase. Up to 10 wt%, Cr2O3 will go into solid solution in rutile TiO2. Above this
threshold, phases with the general formula Cr2Tin−2O2n−1 were formed (Magneli phases).
It is not possible to dissolve Cr2O3 in anatase TiO2. If anatase was used, the transformation
to the rutile phase was actually accelerated - in pure Ti2O3, the transitioning almost didn’t
happen at 850 ◦C, and needed 1000 ◦C to (almost) fully transition with 1 hour holding
time.
If 5 wt% Cr2O3 was added to the anatase titanium oxide, the transitioning to
rutile already halfway occurred at 850 ◦C, with almost full transitioning at 900 ◦C. It
was also found that in both heating with rutile and anatase, Cr3+ ions were oxidized to
Cr6+ ion, forming Cr(VI) compounds. The formation of this ion and these compounds was
signiﬁcantly higher for the anatase than the rutile mixtures.

X-ray Powder Diﬀraction
X-ray powder diﬀraction is a technique used for phase identiﬁcation of a specimen, based
on a database of diﬀraction patterns. Furthermore it can be used to observe the lattice pa-
rameters, particle size, degree of crystallinity, etc. The X-ray diﬀraction is the constructive
interference of monochromatic X-rays and a crystalline sample. When exposing a crys-
talline sample to monochromatic light, it is observed that at certain angles the crystal will
interfere with the light in a constructive manner - when conditions satisfy Bragg’s law [54].
When measuring the light reﬂected from the crystal as a function of angle of incidence a
peak will occur in the diﬀractogram. Crystalline powder has randomly oriented crystallites.
To ensure all diﬀraction directions the sample is rotated during the measurement.

derivation of Bragg’s law [55].
2d sin(θ) = nλ

Bragg’s law is given in equation 21 and is illustrated in Figure 3.1. It is possible to see that
the extra distance travelled by x-ray beam n + 1 as compared to x-ray beam n is given as
2·sin(θ). In order for the two beams to be in sync the extra distance travelled by the x-ray
beam must be equal to an integer number of wavelengths. This statement holds under the
assumptions of perfectly in sync and monochromatic x-rays, perfect and inﬁnite crystals
and no instrumental ﬂaws.
Ideally, powder diﬀraction patterns should show narrow and symmetrical peaks that are
placed in accordance with deﬁned unit cells.

3.2
Conductivity
A semiconductor is characterized by having all electrons bound to the orbitals of the atoms
in the material at 0 K. The ”highest” (most energy) electrical band is called the valence
band, and the electrons can’t move from this orbital, as the ”lower” electron orbitals (less
energy) is already occupied by 2 electrons, and there is no energy to push the electron in
a higher energy state at 0 K. When energy is added to the system, it becomes possible for
electrons to ”jump” from the valence band to the conduction band. However, there is an
energy gap between these two bands, called the ”forbidden energy gap”. For semiconduc-
tors, this gap is around 2 eV - that means an electron must be excited by at least that
amount to get into the conduction band, and move freely in the material. For conductors,
the conduction band overlaps the valence band, and for insulators the band gap is consid-
erably larger (whether a material is a semiconductor or an insulator is not only dependant
on the band gap, but also on the material’s ability to be made conducting via doping). [57]
The electrons in the conduction band will move in an electric ﬁeld. However, the valence
band they left behind, is now empty. These vacancies are referred to as ”electron holes”,

Temperature dependence of the conductivity
At low temperatures in a doped metal oxide, few electrons will be ionized, and the con-

and more electrons will be excited until all donors are ionized. At even higher tempera-

plotting ln(σ) vs 1/T, it is possible to calculate the activation energy from the slope of the

5.1.3
Unit cell parameters
From the obtained diﬀractograms the lattice parameters can be calculated. The software
PDXL can with prior knowledge of the crystal system (and to the software itself) determine
the unit cell dimensions. The unit cell parameters are shown in Table 5.2. The volume of the
undoped LaCrO3 is 233.19 ˚A3, from ICSD database LaCrO3 unit cell volume is 234.15˚A 3.
The trend shows a decrease in unit cell volume as low amounts of Mg is substituted into the
perovskite structure (disregarding the 2 % Mg-doped sample). As the Mg content reaches
5 % or more the unit cell volume increases until it reaches 15 % and begins to decrease
again. Shannon et al. [59] determined the ionic radii for elements related to charge and
coordination - from here values for Mg2+, Cr3+ and Cr4+ are used. The substituting of a
Mg2+ ion, 0.72 ˚A ionic radii, for a Cr3+ ion, 0.616 ˚A ionic radii, should increase the unit
cell volume. However, the substitution of a lower valence ion causes the formation of some
Cr3+ to Cr4+ ions, 0.55 ˚A ionic radii, or oxygen vacancies, see section 2.2. Although the
Cr4+ ion is smaller than the Cr3+ the substitution of the Mg2+ ion should contribute to
an increase in unit cell volume. This is not the case when substituting 1 % Mg into the
structure. On the other hand when doping with more than 5 % Mg the unit cell volume
increases compared to undoped LaCrO3. Mori et al. [38] calculated the lattice parameters
of orthorhombic 10 % Mg-doped LaCrO3, the unit cell volume was 234.17 ˚A3. Montross
et al. [36] calculated the unit cell volume of 10 % Mg-doped LaCrO3 as well to 236.10 ˚A3.
Despite this it has to be mentioned that if the solid sample is not perfectly placed in the
sample holder, tilted or oﬀ-centred, a oﬀset in the peaks will occur. Hence, the deduction
of the results is tentative, and it cannot be concluded how much the unit cell changes with
the substitution of Mg into the LaCrO3 structure.

Discussion of results
6.1
LaCrO3
The Mg-doped LaCrO3 demonstrated the highest conductivity of the candidates tested in
this study. The highest measured conductivity at 80 ◦C was 0.218 S cm−1 for the 1 %
Mg-doped LaCrO3. The undoped LaCrO3 performed 10 times lower with a conductivity
of 0.0189 S cm−1, which highlights the improvement of substituting magnesium into the
LaCrO3 structure. Substituting more than 1 % magnesium into the LaCrO3 contributed to
a slightly lower conductivity - 0.154 S cm−1 for 15 % Mg-doped LaCrO3. Except for the 5
% Mg-doped sample the other Mg-doped LaCrO3 exhibited similar values for conductivity.
Jiang et al. [61] reported the conductivity of undoped LaCrO3 at 800 ◦C to 0.33 S cm−1,
in comparison the conductivity measured at 780 ◦C for undoped LaCrO3 in this study was
0.285 S cm−1. The activation energies for the samples were calculated for a small polaron
mechanism, the slope of ln(σT) vs 1/T plot. The activation energies were in the range
of 0.146-0.187 eV with 10 % Mg-doped LaCrO3 and 15 % Mg-doped LaCrO3 showing the
lowest and highest value, respectively. Hence, no trend in the activation energies could
be derived from the results, the values were too close in value. Minh et al. [62] listed
the activation energy of undoped LaCrO3 to 0.187 eV and 0.197 eV for 10 % Mg-doped
LaCrO3, similar to the values obtained in this study.
The corrosion experiments revealed that doping LaCrO3 with 15 or 20 % magnesium re-
sulted in total loss of the sample, after just 24 hours exposure in the acid. The undoped,
1, 2, 5 and partially 10 % Mg-doped LaCrO3 demonstrated comparable corrosion losses
0.46-0.84 wt%, and doubled the value for 10 % Mg-doped specimen. The white areas ob-
served at the two highest doped samples, believably excess magnesium (solubility of Mg in
LaCrO3) between 10-20 mol% [35, 36], were deemed the reason for the complete dissolution.
The XRD results indicated that all the prepared samples contained LaCrO3, besides the 5
and 10 % Mg-doped samples, with none or small amount of impurity. It is worth mention-
ing that the 15 and 20 % Mg-doped samples had no visible diﬀerence in their respective
diﬀractograms compared to the undoped LaCrO3 diﬀractogram. However, the 5 and 10 %
Mg-doped samples had a large peak, and several small peaks, that the program/database
was unable to designate to any substance. A likely suspect is lanthanum oxide - both sam-
ples displayed, too some extent, worse conductivity, and the average resistivity for La2O3
at room temperature is 10 kΩcm [63]. The resistivity for the processed undoped LaCrO3
bar is 0.13 kΩcm at 50 ◦C. Thus, the addition of La2O3 contributes to a lower conductivity
- in coherence with the measured conductivities. The lattice parameters were determined
from the diﬀractograms, when doping with 1 % magnesium the unit cell volume decreased
and when doping with more than 5 % magnesium the unit cell volume increased, compared
to undoped LaCrO3. The inﬂuence of magnesium doping on the unit cell is resolved, due

to the uncertainty of the measured lattice parameters. In general the Mg-doped LaCrO3
demonstrated promising results, considerable for further suitability and durability investi-
gations.
6.2
Discussion of Sc-doped TiO2
For the Sc-doped titanium oxide, no results indicated it would be beneﬁcial to add Scandium
to an TiO2 support. With respect to stability, it could not be dissolved in acid, neither
the doped rutile TiO2 phase, nor the Sc2O3 phase shown to be present. An - for maybe
other applications - interesting result is that the solubility limit of Sc in TiO2 was found
to be under 1.5 at%, which corresponds to approx (not exactly) 0.75 mol% Sc2O3. If more
is added, it will create a 2nd phase. Likewise results were found by Magunov et. al. [50],
although they got that 2 mol% Sc2O3 was possible to dissolve with their method - a reason
for this may be they heated to above the melting point, or simple inaccuracy, as it was
found from a sample with 10 mol% Sc2O3. The doping was found to have no measurable
impact on the size of the unit cell, which means if there is a change it should be less than
0.01 ˚A - else the calculations should have detected them.
The conductivity results were not giving very accurate result, with calculated values of the
conductivity at 80 ◦C being in the range 10−14-10−18 S cm−1. It thus seems the conductivity
surely isn’t improved by adding Sc to TiO2. It doesn’t necessarily lower the conductivity,
and as pure TiO2 has been used before as support, the Sc-doped titanium oxide could also
be used in the same setup - the doping of scandium would just be a pointless step, that
wouldn’t yield any improved performance.
6.3
Discussion of Cr-doped TiO2
On the contrary to the Sc-doped TiO2, the Cr-doped TiO2 showed to have properties that
could improve the performance of a TiO2 support signiﬁcantly: The conductivity at 5
at% Cr was calculated to be 8 orders of magnitude higher than the pure TiO2, with the
lower doped samples having a lower conductivity, which is logical. This could give a lower
internal resistance in the electrolyzer cell, which means especially to get high currents,
lower voltages should be needed. Structurally, the unit cell may have gotten a little bit
smaller, in the order of 0.01 ˚A on both the A and C parameter - this is also expected, as
the Cr4+ is slightly smaller than the Ti4+ it will replace. The trend observed on the size
of the unit cell was however very weak, so no decisive conclusion can be reached - only a
tentative result. On the stability-side, the Cr-doped TiO2 also performed well, basically
being completely stable in the acid tests performed, for the 5 at% Cr in 14 days, and for
the lower doping level only in 24 hours, but highly likely also for at least 14 days. As 5

mol% - around 15 at% - in TiO2 while still keeping the rutile structure, the potential for

7
Conclusion
Three materials to be used as a catalyst support for a proton exchange membrane were
successfully synthesized and analyzed. Mg-doped LaCrO3 and Cr-doped TiO2 both had
compositions with comparatively high conductivities and low degradation rates in acid,
which is the desired support properties, while Sc-doped TiO2 didn’t have a composition
with increased conductivity, compared to pristine TiO2. For the Mg-doped LaCrO3, any
doping level from 1 mol% to 20 mol% increased the conductivity of pristine LaCrO3 -
doping with 1 % Mg exhibited the highest increase from 0.0189 S cm−1 to 0.281 S cm−1
at 80 ◦C. The compositions with 5 and 10 mol% Mg had unidentiﬁed impurities, which
were detected in the XRD scans, the identity of the impurity is suggested to be La2O3
but could not be conﬁrmed. From the 15 and 20 % Mg-doped LaCrO3 scans no other
phases were visible, but in a microscope a second phase were detectable (white areas -
excess magnesium). This most likely resulted in the total decomposition in the corrosion
test, whereas the other samples lost around 1 wt%.
The chromium-doping of titanium oxide was successful in incorporating the Cr into the
rutile TiO2 structure. The doping increased the conductivity by a factor of almost 108, from
around 10−14 S cm−1 for pure TiO2 to a calculated value of 2.27∗10−6 S cm−1 at 80 ◦C, or
a measured value of 4.19 ∗10−5 S cm−1 at 200 ◦C. The conductivity decreased drastically
with decreasing Cr-doping level. The Cr-doped TiO2 was for all doping levels stable in
acid with pH=0 for for 24 hours, and for 14 days a very minor degradation measured to be
0.161 % was measured, but the result was not clear enough that degradation is certain. In
literature TiO2 [25] has been successfully used as catalyst support, which means the better
conducting Cr-doped TiO2 give a lower overall cell resistance.
The scandium-doped titanium oxide was found to have a solubility limit between 1-1.5 at%
Sc in TiO2. Excess Sc2O3 was found to be in a separate phase. Even though the Sc-doped
TiO2 was completely stable in acid with pH = 0, even after 14 days, the conductivity were
the same or worse than pure TiO2, it showed very poor conductivity. It was diﬃcult to
determine the value of the conductivities, but it is around 2 orders of magnitude within
10−15 S cm−1, which is on the level or worse. Scandium-doping is thus concluded to have
no (or negative) impact on the ability of TiO2 to function as support.
Both Mg-doped LaCrO3 and Cr-doped TiO2 displayed promising results. Doping TiO2

7.1
Future work
Based on the conclusion of this work, attempts to make a Sc-doped TiO2 catalyst support
should not be pursued further. For the Cr-doped TiO2, there is basis for trying it as a
support for an electrolyzer catalyst. The 5 at% composition should primarily be tested
at ﬁrst, as that showed by far the highest conductivity. When used as support, tests to
carry out are Rotating (Ring) Disc Electrode measurements, to determine if the activity
of the catalyst is actually maintained when the Cr-doped TiO2 is used as support.
It
should also be attempted to get Cr-doped TiO2 turned into ﬁne powder with a surface
area of around than 100 m2 cm−3, and deposit catalyst particles on the material. Most
importantly, the support should be tested in a full PEM electrolyzer cell, and especially the
ohmic overpotential should be compared with non-supported catalysts, and other support
materials. Much of the relevant literature is reviewed in Section 2.
Furthermore, the same experiments carried out in this work should also be done with higher
doping levels of Cr. In an article, the solubility limit of Cr2O3 in rutile TiO2 was found to
be 7.4 mol% [53], which corresponds to 13.8 at% Cr. Compositions with doping levels up
to this point (and slightly above) can be synthesized with the same method, and especially
the acid stability should be tested, to make sure it doesn’t ”fall oﬀ”, like with Mg-doped
LaCrO3. The conductivity of these compounds are expected to increase, but how much is
diﬃcult to predict. If the TiO2 with a higher Cr-doping level are conductive and stable,
these should also be tested in an RRDE and electrolyzer.
