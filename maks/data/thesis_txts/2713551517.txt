Abstract
The combination of different technologies within the hybrid renewable power plants is a
good solution to palliate with the non controllable nature of the wind and solar energy.
However, the batteries are subject to degradation which requires their replacement during
the lifetime of the power plant and thus impact its economic characteristics. Degradation
needs to be quantified and integrated into the sizing optimization models of the hybrid
power plants. This paper develops different types of degradation models for the batteries
which rely on different parameters. These degradation models are then compared to a
reference model that has an significant complexity and thus computational time. This
comparison results in the selection of the best degradation model that combines accuracy
and rapidity. The chosen model is then integrated into the energy management system
optimization model in order to take into account this degradation in the optimization.
The is a linear model that provides accurate results when it comes to the selection of the
better suited location. It also allows a reduction of 50% of the computational time.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Context
The current climate context is one of the most important global concerns. The objective
of limiting the increasing in global warming to 1.5°C seems to get out of reach. The
previous policies appear inadequate and too weak for such a goal. The CO2 emissions
have kept increasing and, according to the UNEP and IPCC, the trend is going towards
an increase higher that the 2°C limit ((UNEP, 2021), (IPCC, 2021)). The coal remains
the main energy source used in the world, with a share of 35% of the power generation
in 2020 (IEA, 2021a), as well as being the major source of CO2 emissions. In 2020,
world CO2 emissions reached 35 billion tons, of which 15 billion were related to coal
consumption (Ritchie and Roser, 2020). Despite experiencing a decrease in fossil-based
energy production in 2020, the COVID 19 crisis pushed many countries, and especially
the emerging markets such as India, to go back to these energy sources in 2021 (IEA,
2021c). In addition to that, the global energy demand is increasing: a increase of 50% in
the global energy usage is forecasted for 2030(IEA, 2021c).
For these reasons a shift towards the renewable energies is of the outmost importance.
Even if the recent crisis led to an increase in the use of fossil fuel based energy, the
sustainable energies also benefited from it. The future installed power capacity in the
next five years are, in fact, forecasted to be composed at 95% by renewables, which
would represent an increase of 60% of the installed renewable capacity (IEA, 2021b).
This continues the trend of the recent years which has especially been promoted by
a significant decrease of the CAPEX for wind and solar energy (IEA, 2021b). These
decreases respectively reached 20% and 50% from 2015 to 2020. Even if the last year
observed an increase in the price of commodities such as copper, steel and aluminium
which impacted the CAPEX of wind and solar energy installation. The CAPEX increases
for these technologies did not exceed 10% and is forecasted to fit again the former
decreasing curve by 2025.
The renewable sector also benefited from generous policies and different supports, espe-
cially the development of Power Purchase Agreements (Dykes et al., 2020). In the case of
India, the government has clearly stated its will to develop the renewable energy sector by
setting the goal of installing 175 GW of renewable energy sources by 2022 and continuing
their developing trend of the past decade ((Indian Ministry of New and Renewable
Energy, 2021), (Elavarasan et al., 2020)). In the case of Denmark, the European Union
has also set a goal for renewables to represent 32% of the global EU energy production by
2030 (THE EUROPEAN PARLIAMENT AND THE COUNCIL OF THE EUROPEAN
UNION, 2018).

These different aspects combined together form a very favorable landscape for the
development of hybrid power plants. Hybrid Power Plants are on-grid utility-scale power
plants, combining various technologies (renewable energy production, storage) connected
to the grid in one unique point (Wind Europe, 2019). Different type of combinations
have been studied with the most popular being wind or solar combined with storage and
wind combined with solar and with storage. This paper will focus on the later type of
HPP. This kind of installation present many benefits compared to traditional renewable
power plants that will be developed in next chapter, however, only a few projects have
already been installed or are planned to be installed.
1.2
Problem Definition, Assumptions and Limitations
The main objective of this paper is to improve the sizing optimization model developed
in Murcia et al., 2022 to determine the optimal sizing and operation for an Hybrid Power
Plant and later apply it to real-life cases in India. The improvement will be focused on
the battery degradation model and its integration to the Energy Management System
(EMS) optimization model.
The sizing of an HPP corresponds to the determination of the different technologies com-
posing the power plant as well as their capacity. The HPPs considered in this paper are
wind-solar-battery HPPs. Different configurations can be chosen for such implantation,
however for this study the configuration considered is shown in the following figure:
Figure 1.1: Chosen HPP configuration.
The three assets are separated but share the same substation and grid connection. This
==PAGE:13==

configuration present some advantages that will be covered later.
The operation of an HPP concerns its functioning and participation in the grids and the
markets. For simplicity, this paper will focus on the participation of the HPP to the
spot-market. The ancillary markets are out of the scope of this study.
The objective of the sizing optimization model is the maximization of the profit and take
into account the physical, technical and economic constraints of the projects. The model
will take as input historical data for the electricity prices and meteorological conditions.
Moreover, it will also consider the degradation of the batteries and their participation
into the energy management system. Their degradation will be characterised by altered
lifetime and additional cost.
The original optimization model developed by (Murcia et al., 2022) already has a
degradation model incorporated however it has a high complexity and is based on
non-linear equations. Its computation requires a significant amount of time and its
non-linearity makes it impossible to combine with the EMS. The final goal is to develop
a better way of incorporating the degradation into the model. Particularly, this thesis
will aim to answer the following questions:
• How does the degradation of the batteries impact the feasibility of the development
of HPPs?
• How can the battery degradation be modeled accurately?
• How can the computational time of the EMS optimization model be reduced?
• How can the degradation be incorporated within the EMS?
The new degradation model should be incorporated to the existent model without com-
promising its integrity. This limits the modeling possibility.
As mentioned earlier, the optimization model will take as input economic and me-
teorological data. These data sets will be built based on historical data. Random years
will be chosen and used to forecast the data for the whole lifetime of the power plant.
This choice put a limit on the realism of the model, however a thorough sensitivity
analysis will be conducted in order to evaluate the robustness of the results.

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
2.1
HPP
The HPP can be generally described as follow: “Hybrid energy power plants are on-grid
combinations of two or more energy conversion devices (e.g., electricity generators or
storage devices), or two or more fuels for the same device that, when integrated, overcome
limitations that may be inherent in either"(Manwell, 2004). This general definition already
hints towards the main goal of HPPs, that is compensating flaws of power plants based
on a unique non pillotable technology by integrated one or several other technologies.
As previously mentioned, this paper focuses on renewable HPPs and more precisely on
the ones composed of wind energy, solar PV and Li-ion battery storage. The chosen
configuration consist of the three technologies sharing the same substation and one single
connection point to the grid.
Other configurations are also possible (Wind Europe, 2019). For example, solar pan-
els can be integrated with wind turbines. This configuration gives the advantage of
eliminating the inverters for the solar panels and thus reducing the cost of the whole
plant(L. Petersen et al., 2018). However, having solar panels connected to wind turbines
can induce an important shading of the panels. It is also important to note that the
power outage of the panels would be limited by the turbines power due to the fact that
they are directly connected(Wind Europe, 2019).
For these reasons, the configuration wind-solar-ESS is preferred. In general, the HPP
combining wind, solar and storage present several advantages. The main goal of the
hybridization of RES such as wind and solar is to compensate their inherent intermittent
nature. The combination of wind and solar appears natural, especially due to the fact
that they have an interesting correlation factor (Fasihi, Bogdanov, and Breyer, 2016). In
many parts of the world the two of them are negatively correlated, this allows the plant
to have a higher number of full load hours.
Another important advantage of this type of power plant is from the economic aspect.
By developing the different technologies at the same time, the owner can benefit from a
synergy in the developing and permitting process(Ericson et al., 2018). The synergy in
the processes is a way to reduce both costs and waiting time. Moreover, the CAPEX and
OPEX are also beneficially affected by the hybridization. Combining the different assets
allows the developer to minimize these costs by sharing the labour, hardware, cabling
and land costs between them. The presence of only one connection point is also a great
economic advantage.
Finally, the hybridization gives the opportunity to the plant to participate not only to

In 2022, Wind Europe’s HPP database identified 27 HPPs in operation or in develop-

Currently, India has a political landscape favorable towards HPPs, which enhances the

combined with 28.8 MW of solar PV. The main goal of this project was to enhance and
flatten the total power output.
Another more recent project worth mentioning is the Haringvliet project in the Nether-

was commissioned in 2020. This HPP is composed of 21 MW of wind, 31 MW PV and
12 MW storage. This HPP combines the three different assets that will be used in this
paper and thus appears as a possible case to refer to (Wind Europe, 2019).
2.2
Battery Storage
The real amelioration of the hybrid power plants, compared to the more traditional RES
power plants, comes from the addition of the energy storage. Different technologies can
be considered for the storage system ((Jensen, 2021), (Cossu, 2020), (Nivin and Rosaline,
2021)). It can take the form of batteries, power to X or even thermic storage. However,
the most popular solution is a BESS system composed of lithium-ion batteries.
==PAGE:17==
2.2 Battery Storage
7
in International Renewable Energy Agency, 2019, the popularity of the Li-ion bat-
teries is shown to have exponentially increased during the last decade. The percentage
of utility-scale storage systems composed of this type of batteries is increasing as well as
the total capacity of utility-scale storage system. This attractiveness is the results of
different factors. Firstly, the maturity of the Li-ion batteries, with the first commercial
model dating back to 1991 (Reddy et al., 2020). The Li-ion batteries have undergone
several developments and are now a proven technology. Secondly, their energy density
(200-400 Wh/l) and efficiency (85%-95%) is relatively high compared to other battery
technologies such as the lead-acid (Environmental and Energy Study Institute, 2019).
Lastly, the Li-ion batteries present a relatively low cost compared to other solutions.
in International Renewable Energy Agency, 2019, it is shown that Li-ion batteries is
the technology with the lowest levelized cost. A cost that is even reduced to 108$-140$
when the BESS is paired with a utility scale PV system, due to the synergy men-
tioned before. (International Renewable Energy Agency, 2017) also underlines the fact
that these prices are very likely to go down by 2030. The CAPEX of BESS based on
Li-ion technology has been, in fact, evaluated to decreased by 50% to 60% by 2030.
This evolution makes the Li-ion technology attractive for the development of future HPPs.
Different strategies can be implemented for the use of the storage system in HPPs.
For example, this asset can be added as a supplementary component. In this case the
ESS is not controlled independently and serves as a support to the energy production
systems to which it is connected to. Another possible solution is to use the storage as
an independently controlled component. The ESS is then controlled by the owner of
the HPP, who can decide to use it either as a support to the generators or to provide
ancillary services to the grid itself. Finally, the ESS can have a mixed role: the storage
is separated in two different parts, one part solely dedicated to support the generators
and the other one solely dedicated to the interactions with the grid. In this case, the two
parts should be registered and used only for their own purpose (generation support or
ancillary services) (Wind Europe, 2019).
The choice of the strategy will vary depending on whether or not the owner of the
HPP decides to participate in the different ancillary markets. If the BESS is providing
ancillary services to the grid, these services can vary depending on the needs of the grid.
The different services can be grouped into 5 main categories, which are illustrated in
the figure below. For simplicity, in this paper the BESS system will only provide energy
services.

The last aspect important to mention regarding the use of a BESS based on Li-ion
batteries is their degradation. In this paper, the HPP will be optimized for its whole
lifetime, which will extend for a longer period than the traditional lifetime of Li-ion
batteries (Environmental and Energy Study Institute, 2019). Moreover, the batteries
lifetime is evaluated in “normal conditions”, but the use of batteries in a HPP induces
irregular cycles, changes in temperature and overall very different stress conditions, which
reduce the lifetime of the batteries. A shorter battery lifetime is a source of additional
costs that must be taken into account when planning the development of a HPP. The
method to evaluate the degradation and its economic impact will be developed later in
the paper.
2.3
Optimization
2.3.1
Focus of the Optimization
When a new HPP is to be developed, the project needs to go through an optimization
stage. The optimization can be applied to different aspects.
One aspect is the physical design (Dykes et al., 2020). A HPP is a complex power plant
with several technologies interacting with each others. Decisions must be made on the
type and dimensions of these technologies, whether it is solar panels, wind turbines or
storage units. Moreover, the developer of such infrastructures does not have unlimited
space and the proximity of different installations can impact their productivity. The
shadow cast by the wind turbines on the solar panels, or the one cast by other solar
panels due to their different inclination, is one important example.
Another aspect that is subjected to optimization is the topology. The internal electrical
grid needs to be designed and optimized in order to reduce the cost and losses of the
cable topology. A usual solution in the design of HPPs, which is the one adopted in this
paper too, is that all assets share the same substation (Wind Europe, 2019). For this
design, different topologies can be adopted; (L. ; Petersen et al., 2018) presents the main
ones.
==PAGE:19==
2.3 Optimization
9
• The "collocated Plant" design, where each asset has its own connection point to
the AC cable connected to the substation.
• The "WTG-Coupled" solution, which takes advantages of the different existing
converters by connecting the battery, solar PV and the AC/DC converter of the
wind turbines in order to have only one DC/AC converter that is then connected
to the substation.
• The "DC-coupled" design, where each asset has its own DC/DC converter which
are connected to a single DC/AC converter for a HVAC grid connection or a single
DC/DC converter for HVDC grid connection.
The last aspect of a HPP thatcan be optimized is the sizing. The objective is to
determine the capacities of each assets that needs to be installed to obtained the best
solution depending on the objectives decided by the HPP developers((Nivin and Rosaline,
2021), (Cossu, 2020), (Jensen, 2021)). The capacities will depend on the chosen objective,
the meteorological data and the technologies considered.
This paper is based on the sizing optimization model developed in Murcia et al., 2022
and aims at furthering its accuracy.
2.3.2
Objectives
As mentioned earlier, the results of the optimization problem will depend on the chosen
objectives. In the case of a sizing optimization problem, the objectives are generally the
maximization or minimization of economic indicators (Lian et al., 2019). The main ones
are the Net Present Value (NPV), the Internal Rate of Return (IRR) and Levelized Cost
of Energy (LCOE) (Luna-Rubio et al., 2012). in Murcia et al., 2022, the objective is to
minimize the LCOE or maximize the NPV/CAPEX, so in other terms, the objective is
to minimize the present cost of producing one unit of energy or maximizing the present
value of the project.

Surrogate based optimization uses an initially built surrogate based on the results and
experiments already available. This surrogate will then be searched by an optimization
model in order to find the optimal solution within this surrogate. Then, a new surrogate
will be built based on the new results and these steps will be repeated for a specific
number of iterations or until the final solution reaches a specific threshold (Han and
Zhang, 2012). This process reduces significantly the number of designs fully evaluated
by the optimization model and thus reduces the total computational time.
The optimization model developed in this paper (Murcia et al., 2022) is to be run
over a very large set of locations in India. The surrogate based optimization is thus a
good option in order to perform such a study.

3.1.1
General Description
The optimization model used in this paper, that is the one developed in Murcia et al.,
2022, solves the sizing optimization problem for a HPP including a BESS composed of
lithium-ion batteries. The batteries capacity is degraded over time and thus will have
to be replaced one or several times throughout the lifetime of the power plant. The
objectives of the optimization problem being economic, the replacement of the batteries
will have an impact on the model output. Therefore, the degradation of the batteries
must be taken into account in the model in order to have more realistic results.
A lithium-ion battery is a storage solution that allows to store the energy in a chemical
form. It is composed of different parts: an anode, a cathode, a separator, an electrolyte,
and two current collectors (positive and negative). The anode and cathode contain the
lithium (Manthiram, 2017).
Figure 3.1: Schematic representation of a lithium-ion battery (Ghiji et al., 2020).
The battery charge and the discharge processes are based on an redox reaction. The
following equations are the one occurring for a battery using cobalt:

The degradation of lithium-ion batteries comes from the loss of active lithium ions
and other active materials and from the stress induced by the usage of the battery (Xu
et al., 2018). When modeled, the degradation is often separated into two different type:
the calendar ageing and the cycling aging. As their names suggest, the calendar ageing
refers to the inherent degradation of the battery capacity over time, independently of its
usage; whereas the cycling ageing refers to the degradation induced by the accumulated
charging-discharging ((Xu et al., 2018), (He et al., 2016), (Wang et al., 2016)). The
difficulty of modeling the battery degradation, in the context of a sizing optimization
problem, resides in its non-linearity. This characteristic makes it difficult to be integrated
within the optimization model. For this reason, several models and methods are explored
in this paper.
3.1.2
Rainflow Counting
Before presenting the different models considered for the problem, it is worth introducing
the rainflow counting, which is used in many models.
The rainflow counting is a method used to count the cycles within a graph repre-
senting the stress applied to a material. It determines in the graph, that represents a
series of stress levels, a list of cycles characterized by a minimum and a maximum level
of stress.
To determine the optimal battery capacity necessary to the project studied, the optimiza-
tion model developed in Murcia et al., 2022 contains an Energy Management System
optimization algorithm. This algorithm will maximize the revenue generated by the power
plant over a certain period of time. Moreover, it will determine the behavior of the power
plant and thus the one of the BESS, including the specific quantities of energy charged and
discharged. As a results, the algorithm will return the battery capacity needed through-
out the period of time, as well as the series of SoC (State of Charge) levels of the batteries.
The rainflow counting method will be used to extract cycles from the series of SoC
returned by the EMS optimization algorithm. By doing so, the degradation model can
determine the cycle ageing of the batteries.

As mentioned in the chapter 3, this paper aims at improving the battery degradation
modeling and the handling of this degradation within the optimization model developed
in Murcia et al., 2022. As illustrated on chart 4.1, the EMS and the degradation model
are separated. The way they interact with each other has been explained earlier in the
chapter 3.This separation can cause some problems during the optimization. In fact,
the battery storage does not have an operational cost. Therefore, when batteries are
present in the design, and the EMS is optimized, the batteries will be used without any
restriction. This causes an overuse of the batteries and so a faster degradation.
To palliate this issue, three methods are available. However, only two of them have been
implemented in order to keep the integrity of the original optimization model.
5.1
Constraints Method
The first method is based on constraining the use of the batteries. It is mentioned in
Wang et al., 2016. When batteries are present in the design, what is most costly is
the need to replace them once they are to degraded. The standard threshold for the
replacement is 80% remaining capacity. So, if the cost of replacing the batteries needs to
be constrained, it is possible to do so by constraining of the battery use.

the current battery capacity (BE(t)) and the battery power (BP). Here the battery

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
6.1
Discussion
The main objective of this paper was to improve the current degradation model present in
the sizing optimization model developed in Murcia et al., 2022 and integrate it to the EMS.
Different solutions have been considered for the degradation model, integrating dif-
ferent parameters and based on different assumptions. Overall, the linear model is the
closest to to the reference model. This model allows important savings in the computa-
tional time by reducing it by 50%. However the linear model is not perfect. The model
is based on the original battery data present in Murcia et al., 2022 and it would require
a specific tuning to fit other data, if they were to be changed. The main advantage of
this model is its computational time reduction along with its accurate estimation for the
better suited site to developed the HPP.
The goal of the model developed in Murcia et al., 2022 was to evaluate the feasi-
bility of the development of HPPs over a large number of locations in India. In order
to do that, the sizing optimization model is run all over India and returns the optimal
design of a HPP and its economic characteristics for each locations. From these results, it
is possible to identify the better suited ones. In order to facilitate this research, the linear
model could be used to significantly reduce the important computational time required
to run the optimization for each location in India. Its low computational time, combined
with its accurate estimation of the better suited locations, make it a useful tool for such
studies. A good practice would be to use the linear model to run the optimization over
a large set of locations, in order to select the best ones. Once the best ones have been
selected, a more precise optimization could be run using the model developed in chapter
5.3, in order to find the optimal design adapted to the specific locations.
6.2
Future Work
To continue this project, the linear model should be tested and compared to the reference
model on a larger set of locations and weather data, in order to tune its parameters and
obtain a more accurate model. Another term could be added to the linear model in order
to take into account the calendar ageing, while preserving its linearity. Moreover, the
reference model should also be completed. As for now, it considers the temperature as a

constant. However, this is not realistic and the change in temperature has an impact on
the degradation of the batteries. This is not striking in the locations studied, since they
are all located in India with a favorable weather. However, if future studies were to be
made in another countries, like Denmark, then the actual temperatures should be taken
into account when calculating the degradation.
If not compared to Model 1, the linear model should be trained via machine learning
with real battery degradation data collected on HPPs in operations.
With a more accurate linear model it will be possible to better the fully integrated
model. The EMS and the degradation could then be combined and not separated, leading
to better optimal solutions for the sizing of HPPs.
The fully integrated model should then be compared, in terms of results and com-

The linear model combined with the EMS should reduce significantly the computa-
tional time for the sizing optimization models of HPPs.
