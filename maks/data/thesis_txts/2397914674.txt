Abstract 
The purpose of this thesis has been to investigate a specific Ni-S electroplating bath utilizing 
thiourea as sulphur donor, and the electrodeposited coatings’ electrocatalytic properties in 
alkaline water electrolysis. The maximum lifetime of the bath has been attempted to be 
reached through a small scale bath experiment. The coatings produced have been tested in 
alkaline water electrolysis and investigated in SEM and through EDS. The electrolyte of the 
bath has been tested for chloride and nickel concentrations by titration. Several methods for 
extending the maximum lifetime of the bath has been investigated; separation of cathode 
and anode by membrane, use of thiosulfate as an alternative sulphur donor and removal of 
ammonia through synthesis of struvite. The maximum lifetime of the electroplating bath was 
not reached, due to degradation of the mechanical properties of the coating, believed to be 
caused an increased chloride concentrations. The mechanism of sulphur donation from 
thiourea has been found to most likely produce carbon monoxide and ammonia as 
byproducts, without consuming electrons from without the molecule. The coatings have 
been observed to contain microcracks, which grow during alkaline water electrolysis use. 
The amount and size of the cracks increase with the bath age, as do their growth during 
alkaline water electrolysis. The sulphur content of the coatings decreased to around 4% 
(w/w) through their use in alkaline water electrolysis from a sulphur content varying from 
12,81% (w/w) to 7,59% (w/w). None of the methods for extending the lifetime of the bath 
were successfully implemented. Several untested methods for extending the lifetime of the 
bath, and other unused testing methods have been outlined.

1 INTRODUCTION AND BACKGROUND 
In a world of rapidly evolving technology, energy resources and distribution is highly 
important. The demand for electricity is far from constant, as many of the consuming 
processes are dependent on for example the time of day. Many of the newer and more 
sustainable forms of energy production, such as wind, wave or solar power are very difficult 
and inefficient to throttle. This means that more easily controllable fossil energy sources, 
like combustion power plants, are difficult to escape from. This problem can be remedied by 
having a buffer to save the energy when demand is lower than supply, and in turn to draw 
energy from when the demand is higher than the supply, effectively like a large rechargeable 
battery. But while batteries can be, and have been, used for this purpose, the efficiency is 
limited, and the scalability on a global scale is difficult due to the need for rare and 
expensive materials, such as lithium, needed for construction. Luckily there are other 
options for the storage of electrical power, one of them being hydrogen. 
Hydrogen can be made from water electrolysis (the splitting of water into hydrogen and 
oxygen by electricity), stored and then later recombined with oxygen from the atmosphere 
to yield energy. Historically the best catalyst for this reaction is platinum, which does 
present a scalability issue as this is an expensive and rare material. Another promising 
catalyst however is an electroplated mixture of nickel and sulphur, which is both much more 
affordable and abundant than platinum. The process of electroplating used to manufacture 
this catalyst is in need of investigation, and so this thesis will focus on the process used to 
manufacture the Ni-S catalysts, as well as the catalysts themselves. This is done to better 
understand the process and its effect on the quality of the catalysts. Which makes it so the 
process itself can be optimized, to be better and more efficient at producing catalysts for 
hydrogen production. The thesis will attempt at addressing the following 
problems/questions: 
 
 
What is the maximum lifetime of the Ni-S electroplating bath before the 
electrochemical properties of the catalysts starts to degrade? 
 
What is the mechanism of sulphur donation in the electrochemical deposition of the 
Ni-S catalyst, and how does it affect the bath? 
 
How does the state of the bath affect the surface of the coatings, their sulphur 
content and their efficiency in catalyzing water electrolysis? 
 
Is it possible to extend the lifetime of the bath, either by modifying the composition, 
or by wholly or partially regenerating the bath throughout its use, and if so how?

2.1 ELECTROCHEMISTRY 
Electrochemistry is a field of chemistry in which reactions occur that exchanges electrons 
between molecules, atoms or ions. To keep track of how many electrons are assigned to 
which atom, they are given an oxidation state number (in roman numerals or zero), where a 
negative number designates more electrons than the atom would have in a neutral state and 
a positive number less. For example a neutral nickel atom as found in an ingot of pure nickel, 
will be in the oxidation state 0, but a nickel ion in a solution will have two fewer electrons 
and therefore the oxidation state of II. Because of this relation between electrons and 
oxidation states, the charge of an ion, atom or molecule must be equal to the sum of the 
oxidation states of all the atoms contained in it [1]. The process of lowering an atom’s 
oxidation state is called reduction, the atom is said to be reduced, and the opposite process 
is called oxidation, the atom is said to be oxidized. Reactions in which atoms are reduced 
and oxidized are referred to as redox reactions (reduction-oxidation reactions). 
A common redox reaction is the combustion of carbon. When you burn pure carbon, which 
is in the oxidative state 0 it reacts with oxygen in the air, which is also in the oxidative state 
0. This reaction creates either carbon monoxide (CO) or carbon dioxide (CO2) depending on 
the availability of oxygen as written out in equation (2-1) and (2-2).  
 
 
2C     +     O2     →     2CO 
 
 
 
 
      
(2-1) 
C    +     O2     →     CO2 
 
 
 
 
      
(2-2) 
 
As they both have a neutral charge, the sum of the oxidation states of each molecule must 
be zero. Different atoms are more likely to exhibit different oxidation states, which can be 
looked up. Oxygen is most commonly found in the –II oxidation state, but it can be in –I, 0, I 
and II as well. Carbon is known to exhibit oxidation states from IV to –IV, but is commonly 
found in IV or –IV than the other states [2]. 
In the case of carbon monoxide, oxygen is in the –II oxidation state and carbon is in the II 
state, whereas in carbon dioxide carbon is in IV with the two oxygen atoms still in –II. Even 
though some states are more likely than others it is not always possible to get one definitive 
result when assigning oxidation states to a molecule, from purely looking at the reaction 
equation and molecule structures. There are several general rules to follow that can help, 
but exceptions do exist [1].

At this point it should be noted that the oxidation states do not always directly translate to 
electrons belonging to that atom, as the ion charge does in ionic substances, it is merely a 
useful tool when trying to keep track of the electrons in a reaction. 
Another rule that follows from this understanding of chemistry is that the cumulative 
changes in the oxidation states in a reaction must be 0 unless electrons are supplied or 
removed externally [1]. Take the burning of carbon to carbon dioxide for instance, the 
carbon is being oxidized from 0 to IV (a change of +4) and the two oxygen atoms are being 
reduced 0 to –II (a change of -2∙2). 
 
2.2 ELECTROPLATING 
Electroplating is a group of electrochemical reactions where an electrical current is used to 
reduce metal ions in a electrolyte, in order to precipitate them on a surface. Some variants 
occur without the use of an external current called electroless plating, but they will not be 
discussed further [3]. The setup for an electroplating reaction is typically two electrodes 
submerged in an electrolyte. The two electrodes, are connected to a power supply, 
supplying them with a current. The electrolyte contains the metal ions to be deposited on 
the surface, along with other additives to ensure a 
desirable result. 
One of the electrodes acts as the cathode, 
supplying the system with electrons. It is at the 
cathode that the positive metal ions accept 
electrons and are reduced to the neutral state as 
they deposit themselves on the cathode surface. 
The other electrode acts as an anode, removing 
electrons from the system. In many cases the 
anode is made of the same metal as is being 
deposited. In those cases, the anode replenishes 
the ions lost from the electrolyte at the cathode, 
by removing electrons from the outermost atoms 
on its surface [3], and thereby dissolving them. 
This is depicted as an example in Figure 2-1 where 
nickel is being dissolved at a nickel anode and 
deposited at a metal surface. 
When a current is applied to a system containing a 
multitude of chemicals, it is often impossible to keep 
several reactions from occurring. In electroplating this could for example express itself as 
electrons from the cathode being used to generate elemental hydrogen from water [4]. 
These reaction are almost always unwanted, and steps are therefore taken to keep them to 
a minimum. The reactions, often referred to as competing reactions, can in electroplating 
easily lead to problems in the composition of the electrolyte, which in turn can degenerate 
the bath to a point where it needs to be replaced entirely. Different competing reactions can 
occur at the anode and cathode, and to a different extent. This may lead to the cathode and 
anode not being able to utilize the current equally efficiently to drive the precipitation and 
dissolution of metal respectively [4].

The specific way that the metal ions are deposited on the cathode, is often influenced by the 
settings of the reaction. Additives in the electrolyte, temperature of the electrolyte, stirring 
of the electrolyte, acidity (pH) of the electrolyte and applied current and voltage, are some 
of the factors that has an effect on the result [3]. These all affect the surface in various ways, 
and can cause everything from a slightly dimmer shine, to a complete failure as the surface 
coating cracks and falls off. To understand how and why these factors change the outcome, 
and in some cases even predict it, an examination of the reaction mechanism is required. A 
complete understanding of the reaction mechanism of a specific reaction can sometimes be 
difficult to obtain, and in many cases, you settle for the more pragmatic approach of trial 
and error, when deciding how to engineer the electroplating setup.

Watts Bath 
The Watts bath is a formulation of a Nickel electroplating bath developed by O.P. Watts in 
1916 [3]. It is very widely used, however often as a variation of the original bath, tailored to 
the specific needs. The general formulation of a Watts bath is given in Table 2-1.

The main ingredient is of course nickel ions, mostly in the form of nickel sulfate, but also 
nickel chloride to add an amount of chloride ions. The chloride ions are an important factor 
in the dissolution of the anode, and are also beneficial for the overall conductivity of the 
electrolyte. Too much chloride however, will increase the tensile stress in the coating. The 
boric acid is added to serve as a pH buffer, ensuring that the electrolyte does not locally 
become overly alkaline. If the pH level is too high, nickel hydroxide might be co-deposited 
with elemental nickel [3] [4]. 
Because of the chloride in the bath, the dissolution of the anode experiences nearly no 
competing reactions, and so virtually every electron absorbed by the anode comes from a 
nickel atom being dissolved, this is described by an anode efficiency of 100%. The cathode 
generally has a lower efficiency and has a tendency to discharge protons in the electrolyte to 
elemental hydrogen, causing the pH to rise. Another side effect of this is an increase in the 
nickel ion concentration, since the amount nickel ions being precipitated per unit of charge 
is lower than the amount of nickel ions being dissolved [4]. 
The bath used in this thesis is a variant of the Watts bath, with the specific purpose of 
producing a nickel-sulphur coating, with excellent properties for hydrogen production via 
water electrolysis.

The addition of thiourea makes the bath deposit an amorphous Ni-S mixture rather than 
elemental nickel. The exact reaction of how thiourea donates the sulphur atom is not fully 
understood, so it is not possible to ascertain exactly what other molecules thiourea 
decomposes to in the process. However, studies have shown that when heated, thiourea 
decomposes to thiocyanate and ammonia [5]. Since the sulphur atom is being deposited, it is 
unlikely that thiourea turns into thiocyanate as a part of the electroplating. Hence, the 
working theory is that when the sulphur atom is donated, thiourea decomposes to carbon 
dioxide and ammonium ions (2-3) or carbon monoxide and ammonia (2-4). In the case that 
ammonia is created, it would almost immediately react with hydronium ions to form 
ammonium ions, and raise the pH level (2-5). 
Although the reactions may seem very similar, and of equal plausibility, one important 
difference is the oxidation states of carbon in carbon dioxide and carbon monoxide 
respectively. While the carbon in carbon dioxide is in an oxidation state of IV, in carbon 
monoxide it enters an oxidation state of II. In urea, carbon has an oxidation state of IV and 
oxygen an oxidation state of –II. In thiourea, sulphur has an oxidation state of –II, and so 
drawing a parallel to urea, carbon is assumed to have an oxidation state of IV [6]. When 
sulphur is donated it’s oxidized to the state of 0, which means that something must accept 
two electrons, signified by the 2 electrons on the product side of the reaction (2-3). If carbon 
forms carbon dioxide it cannot accept electrons, however if carbon forms carbon monoxide, 
it’s reduced to the oxidation state of II, meaning that it accepts two electrons. This means 
that carbon dioxide and ammonium cannot be produced directly, without an outside 
electron acceptor, where as carbon monoxide and ammonia can be produced without.

In both of these “most likely” cases, ammonium ions would build up in the electrolyte, as 
well as thiourea gradually being used up. Logic demands that this will have an effect on the 
surface being deposited after a certain point. If the thiourea concentration lowers to a 
critical point before the ammonium ion concentration becomes a problem, simply adding 
more thiourea would remedy the issue. Thus the true problem will arise when the 
ammonium ion concentration reaches a certain level. Ammonia is however quite difficult to 
remove from water based solutions without undergoing cumbersome operations, such as 
boiling it off, which would likely require the bath to be brought into the alkaline region of 
the pH scale, as well as a lot of waste energy. 
On account of the difficulty of removal of ammonium ions, the concentration of ammonium 
ions in the bath at a given time is difficult to measure. No readily available method for 
measuring the amount of thiourea was found either. A company specializing in the analysis 
of wastewater was presented with the task of measuring the ammonium ion and thiourea 
concentration, but refused. Later the Kjeldahl method [7] was considered, but was found to 
be too extensive and difficult a setup to achieve in the available laboratories. The last step in 
the Kjeldahl method could be used to test for ammonia as described in 6.2. 
 
 
2.4 WATER ELECTROLYSIS 
In water electrolysis H2O is split into H2 and O2 by creating a potential between two 
electrodes submerged in a water based solution. The products of this reaction has higher 
energy than the reactants, and as such, energy needs to be supplied to drive the reaction. 
This energy comes in the form of electricity by way of the electrodes. 
Today three major techniques for hydrogen production via water electrolysis exists, Alkaline 
Water Electrolysis (AWE), Proton Exchange Membrane electrolysis (PEM) and Solid Oxide 
Electrolysis (SOE) [8]. 
In AWE hydroxide ions migrate across the membrane from the cathode side to the anode 
side, where they will form water and elemental oxygen through releasing electrons to the 
anode. The Excess water travels the other way through the membrane, and is reduced at the 
cathode to hydroxide ions and elemental hydrogen. The typical operating temperatures for 
AWE systems is 60oC-90oC [8]. 
PEM systems contain a membrane that, as the name implies, allow protons to pass through 
it. The protons travel from the anode side to the cathode side where they are reduced to 
elemental hydrogen. On the anode side, water is oxidized into elemental oxygen and 
protons. The typical operating temperature for PEM systems is 20oC-100oC [8]. 
==PAGE:15==
 
7 
 
The SOE variation uses oxygen ions as a charge carrier that 
moves through an oxygen ion conductor from the cathode 
side to the anode side. The oxygen ions are created when 
water is reduced at the cathode, also creating elemental 
hydrogen. The oxygen ions themselves are oxidized into 
elemental oxygen at the anode. The typical operating 
temperature for SOE systems is 600oC-1000oC [8]. 
Out of these three, the most proven technology is the AWE, 
and it is also the technique this thesis will focus on. 
An AWE cell functionally consists of four different 
components; an anode, a cathode, a diaphragm and an 
electrolyte. The efficiency of the electrolysis in the cell is 
determined by the losses of these four components. 
Sometimes the cell is made as a zero gap cell, in which the 
electrodes are directly touching the membrane, see Figure 2-
2. This eliminates some losses in mass transfer from the 
membrane to the electrode discussed later in 2.4.2. An 
electrode in a zero gap cell needs to have holes of some sort, allowing the gas developed by 
the reaction to escape. In this thesis the Ni-S coatings are deposited to plates, and as such 
do not have holes. Therefore the testing was not done in zero gap cells, and the resulting 
efficiency is not as low as it would have been with a zero gap cell. However, since the results 
are only meant to be compared to each other, using the same testing method throughout 
will be sufficient. 
 
2.4.1

thermodynamics. A simple example of an electrochemical system is two electric conductors 
connected to each other by a wire, and an electrolyte. That in itself would not necessarily 
result in a reaction, as the system very likely could be in a state of equilibrium, determined 
by a Gibbs free energy difference of zero for the reaction in question. If the Gibbs free 
energy difference is less than zero, the electrochemical system will be able to perform work.

The thermoneutral voltage is the voltage which, will supply precisely enough energy to 
satisfy the electrical and thermal energy demands of the reaction. If the reaction runs at a

2.4.2 
Losses 
The loss of energy in the system is understandably a matter of concern in water electrolysis. 
The loss is closely related to the current density of the system. The higher the current 
density, the higher the loss [8], but too low a current density and the output of the system 
would drop to a point where the process is no longer economically feasible. 
Since a loss will always occur, the potentials discussed earlier will not be practically possible 
to reach. The loss of the cell in its whole can be split into Ohmic contributions and 
overpotential contributions. The overpotential contributions can further be broken into the 
overpotential contributions of the anode and the cathode respectively [8]. Therefore the 
overall potential of the cell will be able to be expressed by (2-10).

Where the total potential of the cell (Ecell) is the summation of the reversible minimum 
splitting voltage (Erev), the overpotential for the anode (ηanode) and the cathode (ηcathode) and 
the contribution of ohmic resistances (iR) in the cell. 
The ohmic resistances are found in the transfer of ions through the electrolyte and the 
membrane [8]. These contributions are however not influenced very much by the choice of 
catalytic electrode, so they will not be discussed further. 
The overpotential term is used to describe the additional potential needed for a half-cell 
reaction to occur, besides the reversible minimum splitting voltage [8]. The overpotential 
can be further split into different contributions. The activation overpotential is the energy 
needed to transfer the electrons from the coating of the electrode to the electrolyte or vice 
versa. The concentration overpotential is caused by resistance due to the limits in mass 
transfer of the electrolyte. This part of the overpotential contribution to the cell loss is 
diminished in a zero gap cell. The coating’s effect on the concentration overpotential in a 
non-zero gap cell is believed to be negligible, and so the tests performed in this thesis should 
lead to a similar conclusion as for zero gap cells. A third contribution to the overpotential is 
sometimes added, addressing the extra energy needed on account of bubbles in the 
electrolyte [8]. 
2.4.3 
Efficiency 
The efficiency of water electrolysis is generally presented in two different ways. The Higher 
Heating Value (HHV) and the Lower Heating Value (LHV), the difference between which is 
accounting for the energy needed to vaporize the water [8]. The HHV is the ratio between 
the thermoneutral potential, and the actual potential of the cell, whereas the LHV is using 
the reversible minimum potential. The LHV is useful in cases where energy is not spend on 
transforming the water into gas form, for example in a cell fed with steam instead of water 
[8]. In the cases described in this thesis, the HHV is the more appropriate representation for 
efficiency. 
 
2.5 ELECTRON MICROSCOPY 
Scanning electron microscopy, or SEM, is a useful tool, when the need to examine the 
surface of an object, in a higher magnification than optical microscopy allows. The SEM 
obtains its information by bombarding a point on the surface of the specimen with a 
concentrated beam of electrons. The specimen reacts by sending out various signals 
containing information about its surface. These signals can then be interpreted to create a 
data point. The electron beam is then continuously directed at another point, scanning an 
area of the surface. This process yields an array of data points that is then used by software, 
for example to create an image [9]. The functions used in this thesis rely on the following 
signals: 
==PAGE:18==
 
10 
 
Secondary electrons are electrons that are produced by atoms when hit by an electron from 
the electron beam. These relatively low energy electrons are guided towards a detector by 
positively charging a grid which attracts the negatively charged electrons. The amount of 
detected electrons determines the strength of the signal recorded for that point. Because of 
the low energy of the secondary electrons, their penetration depth is small which mean that 
they can’t travel very far through the specimen. This effect entails that a larger amount of 
electrons escape the surface when the beam is inclined to the surface than when the beam 
is perpendicular to the surface. That happens because a larger volume of the specimen 
struck by the electron beam is close to the surface. Because of this, the topography of the 
surface is visible when the data for a multitude of points are aligned to form an image. The 
secondary electrons low penetration depth also mean that the surface area for each data 
point collection is very small, which yields a high resolution [9]. 
Characteristic X-ray’s are generated when the surface is struck by the electron beam. The 
atoms of the specimen are hit by electrons, which excites electrons in their orbitals. When 
the excited electrons revert to their original state, they release energy in the form of 
electromagnetic waves. The energy of the waves depend entirely on the energy difference 
between the electron in the excited state, and the original state. These energy differences 
are different from atom to atom, and so by measuring the energy of the waves, the atom 
producing the wave can be identified. Recording the electromagnetic waves in the X-ray 
region of the spectrum, will yield an Energy-Dispersive x-ray Spectrum, or EDS for short. The 
EDS can be interpreted by software to yield a composition of the specimen for any given 
point [10].

3.1 LIFETIME TEST OF BATH 
In order to find out how long the Ni-S bath could be 
used for, before the surface deposited would begin to 
deteriorate, a lifetime test was performed on a 1,7L 
bath seen in Figure 3-1. After the bath was mixed (as 
per 2.3), steel plates were electroplated for 3 hours, 
and then stored for later electrolytic tests. The plates 
were pretreated by the following steps: 
1. Washing with soap 
2. Electrochemically degreased at 5V for 5min. (at 
first switching between cathodic and anodic 
degreasing, due to differing recommendations, 
but after no difference was observed, anodic 
degreasing was used) 
3. Dry acid treatment  
5min. 
4. Woods nickel bath 5A/dm2  
5min. 
In the beginning, 5x5cm plates were used, but in order 
to be able to reach the lifetime end before the project 
period ended, plates measuring 9x10cm were used 
shortly after. These dimensions were chosen as they 
were the largest that could comfortably be submerged 
in the bath. The larger plates were later cut to 5x5cm to 
fit in the equipment for the electrolysis test. In between 
electroplating the plates, some samples were taken 
from the bath electrolyte (approximately every 4th 
plate). The pH was also measured after electroplating 
every plate, and adjusted with 0,5M HCl. This would 
later turn out to be an erroneous decision as the amount of HCl added over the course of 
the test would increase the chloride ion concentration. This high chloride ion concentration 
is suspected to have caused the coating to crack during the formation in the later 
electroplating tests, by increasing the internal tensile stress, see Figure 3-2 and Figure 3-3. 
This caused the test to end prematurely, before finding a point where the bath produced 
electrolytically bad coatings. The last plate structurally sound enough to test electrolytically, 
was produced at the bath age of 160,6Ah. A choice was made, to not try to redo the test 
with another acid for pH adjustment, as the time left in the project would be almost 
completely consumed by a test of the same length, and even then, that might not reach the 
endpoint of the lifetime of the bath. 
Throughout the test, water evaporated from the bath, which was subsequently replenished 
with demineralized water. Though this may cause the concentrations to vary slightly over 
time, its effect has been neglected as the volume of the bath was readjusted before and 
after every plating operation. Furthermore the samples drawn from the bath may have 
diluted the bath as some of the electrolyte has been removed. The effect of this is also 
believed to be miniscule enough to be ignored.

In an attempt to remove the evolution of ammonia all together, a bath was developed using

3.5 CHLORIDE TEST OF BATH SAMPLES 
After the lifetime test was terminated due to mechanically bad coatings and the chloride 
concentration level was suspected to be the cause, the samples drawn from the bath in 
between the electroplating were tested for their chloride concentration. This was done by 
titration with 0,1M AgNO3, using K2CrO4 as an indicator. The sample was diluted 1:50 with 
water to make it easier to spot the endpoint of the titration. When adding silver nitrate to a 
solution containing chloride ions and chromate ions, the silver ions first bind to the 
chromate forming a red precipitate. This red precipitate then turns white or grayish as the 
chloride ions replace the chromate. The end point of the titration is then marked by the 
precipitate staying red indefinitely, signifying all the chloride has reacted with the silver ions. 
In some of the samples from when the bath was quite young, the silver chromate precipitate 
formed as lumps rather than fine particles, and as such the chloride ions of the solution was 
not able to replace all the chromate inside the lump, making the test invalid. As such, the 
earliest bath samples with reported chloride concentration is the one at 26,6Ah. 
 
3.6 NICKEL TEST OF BATH 
The nickel content of the bath has been tested by titration. The titration was done by 
alkalizing a sample of 2ml with excess ammonia, after which it was diluted by 100ml of 
water, and a very small amount of murexide was added as an indicator. This was then 
titrated by 0,1M NaEDTA until the color changed from yellow to purple. Murexide is purple 
in alkaline solutions, but it forms a yellow complex with nickel ions when both exists freely in 
a solution. EDTA also forms a complex with nickel, and will remove the complexed murexide 
from any nickel complexed to it. Thus when the color of the solution changes, an amount of 
EDTA equal to the amount of nickel ions must have been added. 
The final bath electrolyte has been tested for nickel content, but when testing the other 
samples drawn from the bath, no titration end point could be reached. Thiourea is known to 
form complexes with nickel ions [11], but if this is affecting the result, it would most likely 
give a lower apparent nickel content (signified by reaching the titration end point sooner), as

some of the nickel is already complexed to thiourea. It is possible that thiourea has other 
effects on the titration reactions, somehow hindering their function. Though the thiourea 
concentration in the latest sample drawn from the bath should not be so different from the 
concentration in the final bath electrolyte, that this could have caused the difference. 
 
3.7 STRUVITE SYNTHESIS EXPERIMENT 
A method to capture ammonia from the bath and precipitate it in a crystal form was 
investigated. The mineral struvite or magnesium ammonium phosphate (NH4MgPO4), is 
formed when equal parts of ammonium ions magnesium ions and hydrogenphosphate ions 
are mixed together [12]. The idea was to add magnesium ions and phosphate ions to the 
bath, and filter out the solid precipitate. An experiment was carried out to investigate this 
reaction. It was found that ammonia mixed with disodium hydrogen phosphate (Na2HPO4) 
and magnesium chloride (MgCl2) would produce a white precipitate. This was not the case 
with disodium hydrogen phosphate and magnesium chloride on their own, and neither if 
either of those two were replaced by sodium chloride. Thus the precipitate was believed to 
be struvite. However the mineral would not precipitate at acidic conditions. A larger amount 
of the mineral would precipitate at more alkaline conditions, which indicates that not all of 
the ions precipitated to struvite at neutral pH. Because of this, the method was deemed 
unusable for ammonia removal of the bath as it is acidic in nature. Also according to [12] 
struvite absorbs nickel ions, which would mean that removing ammonia from the bath using 
this method would also mean removing nickel ions. This is not necessarily detrimental to the 
bath quality, as nickel ions tend to accumulate in the electrolyte (see 2.2.1). However it is 
something to be aware of if this method is ever used. Another method to separate the 
ammonia by precipitation is outlined in 6.3.

the plate that had not been tested was also cut and analyzed. Pictures of the surface 
morphology were recorded, and three points were analyzed by EDS, in order to evaluate the

5 CONCLUSION 
This thesis has had the primary goal of investigating the lifetime of the Ni-S electroplating 
bath, with regards to optimizing the electroplating process. A secondary goal has been to 
investigate the bath’s electroplating process itself and the coating it produced. 
The lifetime experiment was halted due to degradation of the mechanical properties of the 
coating. This is thought to be caused by the choice of hydrochloric acid to adjust pH. 
The thiourea decomposition has been found to most likely produce carbon monoxide and 
ammonia, in a process that does not consume electrons from without the molecule. 
The coatings have been observed to contain microcracks in their surface. The amount of 
these cracks seem to increase with the bath age. The cracks seem to grow during the AWE, 
with the coating also undergoing a decrease in sulphur content. The crack growth also seem 
to increase with bath age, however the sulphur content and sulphur content decrease is 
uncorrelated with bath age. The sulphur content after AWE was found to be relatively 
constant (at a value around 4% (w/w)) across the bath age, in contrast to the starting 
sulphur content, which varied greatly from plate to plate. 
No plausible way of extending the lifetime of the bath was tested successfully. Under the 
assumption that the ammonia content is the cause for the eventual degradation of the bath, 
methods of removing ammonia or stopping it from being formed have been tested. 
Although unsuccessful the base idea might be used in other methods that could lead to 
successful solution to this problem. 
This thesis does not conclude the study of the Ni-S electroplating process or the coating, and 
in some ways, it has left more questions than it tries to answer. However, it has shed a light 
on some of the aspects of these topics, and can be a stepping-stone towards a more 
thorough series of experiments, leading to a deeper understanding of the process.

6 FURTHER STUDIES 
Owing to the somewhat limited success of the experiments performed and described in this 
thesis, some of the further experimentation and possible implementations to be considered 
have been outlined. 
 
6.1 LIFETIME TEST REDO WITH OTHER ACIDS 
Since the lifetime test of the bath had to stop before the plates produced exhibited a 
considerable worsening in the AWE test, it would be prudent to do the test again using 
another acid for pH adjustment. It could prove useful to first test if the theory that the 
hydrochloric acid was to blame for the degradation of the bath holds true. One way of doing 
that could be mixing a new bath with a start concentration of chloride similar to the end 
concentration of the current run, and check how the plates turn out. This test will be 
indicative because a lot of other unknown factors could play a part in the bad plating of the 
current bath. 
 
6.2 AMMONIA TEST 
Unless the assumptions of the thiourea byproducts is wrong, there are ammonium ions to 
be found in the bath electrolyte, as well as the samples drawn from it along the way. The 
concentration of these ions could be interesting to investigate, as it would determine once 
and for all whether ammonia is a byproduct of the plating, and provide a rate of production. 
One method for doing this would employ the last step of the Kjeldahl method [7]. In this, the 
pH is shifted to the basic region, turning ammonium into ammonia, and then the ammonia is 
separated by distillation. The distillation would have to be done at a lowered pressure, to 
keep the temperature from reaching points where the thiourea remaining in the electrolyte 
decomposes making more ammonia. A gas washer would have to be applied to redissolve 
the distillate ammonia in a water based solution, in which the ammonia content could be 
determined by back titration. 
 
6.3 AMMONIA SEPARATION BY HEXACHLOROPLATINIC ACID 
In the case that the ammonia concentration is the first barrier stopping the bath from 
further use, ongoing removal of the ammonia could be one way to extending the lifetime of 
the bath as described in 2.3 and 3.7. One way that does not seem too expensive in operating 
costs, takes advantage of a known step in platinum refining. Hexachloroplatinic acid 
(H3OPtCl6) can bind ammonium as ammonium hexachloroplatinate ((NH4)2[PtCl6]) as a very 
slightly soluble inorganic compound. This precipitate could be filtered off, washed and then 
reclaimed as pure platinum. When heated in a hydrogen rich environment, ammonium 
hexachloroplatinate will turn into elemental platinum, under the evolution of ammonia and 
hydrochloric acid gasses. The platinum can be dissolved into hexachloroplatinic acid again by 
use of aqua regia (a mixture of hydrochloric and nitric acids), and can then be reused in 
removal of ammonium. 
==PAGE:34==
 
26 
 
This method would be costly to set up, on account of the platinum, but if done correctly very 
little platinum should go to waste. Hence, the only components continually needed would 
be hydrochloric acid, nitric acid and hydrogen. A byproduct of hydrogen and gaseous 
hydrochloric acid and ammonia could be separated and sold to drive operation costs further 
down. 
However, the method still needs to be proven to work in an electroplating bath, without 
interfering with the electroplating process itself. There is a possibility that the presence of 
other molecules will inhibit the precipitation of ammonium hexachloroplatinate, either by 
making it more soluble, or more likely by producing another precipitate instead, for example 
with nickel ions. It is also quite possible that the presence of small amounts of 
hexachloroplatinic acid has large detrimental effects on the plating capabilities of the bath. 
 
6.4 LOW SULPHUR COATING 
Since the coatings seem to shed the sulphur in electrolysis down to around 4% (w/w), and 
no correlation between the original sulphur content and the electrolysis efficiency, it could 
be interesting to investigate if plates with a lower sulphur content would also bring the 
sulphur content down to the same level. If this was the case, and it did not affect the 
electrolysis efficiency, it is possible that the thiourea content of the bath could be lowered, 
reducing the production of ammonia along with the sulphur codeposition.  
 
6.5 TITRATIONS 
The nickel titration experiment’s result is questionable, since the method was not able to 
obtain results from the earlier samples. The thiourea content in the samples could have had 
an influence in this matter, and it could be fruitful to re-do the titration without the 
thiourea. As stated in [5] the thiourea can be decomposed by heating the solution, and this 
effect could be utilized to remove thiourea and do the titration again. This method could 
also be used to repeat the chloride titration of the earlier samples to investigate if the 
anomaly encountered in those tests were caused by the thiourea. 
 
6.6 GAS EVOLUTION TEST 
As stated in 2.3 the electroplating of Ni-S coating likely also produces either carbon 
monoxide or carbon dioxide. Another way of confirming this, in excess to what has been 
previously mentioned in this thesis, would be to capture the gases formed during 
electroplating, and analyze the composition.
