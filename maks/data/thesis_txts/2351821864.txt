RESUMÈ 
Mange studier har påvist at indeklimaet i de fleste klasseværelser er utilstrækkeligt. 
Klarer sammenhænge mellem dårligt indeklima i klasseværelser og elevers helbred, 
præstation og ikke mindst fravær er blevet påvist. Folkeskolen er springbrættet for 
elevernes fremtidige uddannelse og dårligt indeklima bør derfor undgås.  
Formålet med dette studie var at undersøge de sæsonbestemte variationer af 
indeklimaet imellem klasseværelser på og imellem skoler. Indeklimaet blev 
undersøgt i ni forskellige danske folkeskoler placeret i Storkøbenhavn, både i og 
udenfor opvarmningssæsonen. Skolerne var allerede undersøgt i opvarmnings-
sæsonen fra december 2015 til februar 2016, mens målingerne udenfor 
opvarmningssæsonen blev udført fra august til oktober 2016. Dette projekt vil 
sammenligne resultaterne for begge sæsoner. 
Indeklima parametre såsom CO2 koncentration, temperatur, relativ luftfugtighed, 
lydniveau samt lysforhold blev målt i 82 klasseværelser for begge sæsoner. 
Derudover blev der undersøgt om det var en større sammenhængen mellem de 
ovennævnte parametre og de bygningsmæssige forhold. 
En klar sammenhæng mellem CO2 koncentration og ventilation typen blev observeret. 
Klasseværelser med balanceret mekanisk ventilation havde generelt lavere CO2 
koncentrationer i begge sæsoner. De værste forhold opstod i klasseværelser med 
manuel naturlig ventilation i opvarmningssæsonen, hvor der kun i 26% af brugstiden 
var registreret CO2 koncentrationer under 1000 ppm. Jo ældre skolerne var, jo ringere 
var luftkvaliteten, hvilket kan skyldes at klasseværelserne hovedsageligt har manuel 
naturlig ventilation.

ABSTRACT 
Several studies have shown that the indoor environment in many school classrooms 
is inadequate. Also, poor indoor environment in classrooms has been associated with 
reduced pupil well-being and learning performance, and increased absenteeism. 
Primary school is the foundation of the pupils’ future and further education and 
therefore poor educational environments should be avoided. 
The aim of this study was to investigate the seasonal variation of the indoor 
environment between classrooms within and between schools. The indoor 
environment in classrooms at nine primary schools located in the Greater 
Copenhagen Area was studied, in both the heating and non-heating seasons. The nine 
schools were investigated earlier in the heating season from December 2015 to 
February 2016, while the measurements in the non-heating season were performed 
from August to October 2016. This thesis will compare the results of the 
measurements made in both seasons. 
In the classrooms, indoor environment parameters such as the CO2 concentration, 
temperature, relative humidity, sound pressure level and illuminance level were 
measured in 82 classrooms, for both seasons. Additionally, the association between 
these parameters and observed building characteristics was studied. 
A clear association between the CO2 concentration and the ventilation type was found. 
Classrooms with balanced mechanical ventilation had lower CO2 concentrations in 
both seasons.  The worst conditions occurred in classrooms with manual natural 
ventilation in the heating season where the CO2 concentration was below 1000 ppm 
in only 26% of the occupied time. The older schools had the worst air quality, since 
the classrooms in these schools mostly were equipped with manual natural 
ventilation. A strong inverse correlation between the outdoor temperature and the  
maximum CO2 concentration was found in the heating season in classrooms with 
automatic and manual natural ventilation (correlation coefficients of ρ = -0.69 and ρ 
= -0.60, respectively).  
Generally, the temperature and relative humidity were within the recommended 
ranges, but high temperatures occurred in both seasons. The results showed that in 
~25% of the occupied time was the temperature above 26 °C in the non-heating 
season. 
The mean sound pressure level was ~65 dB(A), but levels above 85 dB(A) occurred 
over relative short time periods. The reverberation time was within the 
recommendations in classrooms with sound absorbing ceiling material. In more than 
half of the occupied time during the heating season, the illuminance level was below 
the recommended 300 lux, especially at locations furthest away from the windows, 
indicating that the conditions need to be improved.

01 
INTRODUCTION 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
Page 17 of 160 
 
It is well known that we in general spend more than 80-90% of our time indoor, in 
our homes, at workplaces, at day care centers, at schools etc. This sets great demands 
to buildings and their indoor environments in order to create a healthy and 
productive environment. Poor indoor environment can cause symptoms as fatigue, 
headaches, allergic symptoms and in worst cases more serious symptoms as lung- and 
cardiovascular diseases or even cancer [15]. Good IEQ in classrooms can increases the 
pupils learning ability, their performance levels, health and well-being and lower 
absenteeism can also be obtained [Bakó-Biró et al. 2012, Toftum et al. 2011 and 
others]. 
Children are particularly sensitive to the indoor environment, several studies have 
shown that there is a clear association between children's performance and the 
indoor environment in classrooms [Toftum et al. 2015, Sullivan et al. 2015, Wargocki 
& Wyon, 2013 and others]. Due to high occupant density CO2 has been registered as 
the most and main dominant indoor pollutant in classrooms which influences the 
pupils health, perception and performance [Wargocki & Wyon, 2013, Mendell et al. 
2013]. 
Around 50% of the Danish primary schools were built in the 60´s and 70´s and not 
many new schools have been built in the last couple of years [Berg and Dam-Krogh, 
2015]. A third of the Danish primary schools are not installed with mechanical 
ventilation, and half suffers from too high CO2 concentrations [17]. 
The old school buildings and the poor indoor environments are a problem for both 
pupils and teachers. There is therefore a need for renovations of Danish schools. 
In 2007 ~50 mia. DKK were reserved to improvements in the Danish municipalities 
during 2009-2018. Almost half was reserved to institutions, schools, sports facilities 
and the elderly and in 2009-2014 ~18 mia. DKK were invested in renovations and 
new school buildings [10]. No significant improvement of the air quality in the Danish 
schools was registered for that period - only 10-20% of the renovation initiatives each 
year concerned the indoor air quality [Berg and Dam-Krogh, 2015]. 
Furthermore, the Danish Working Environment Authority visited in 2014 and 2015, 
835 Danish schools and found that 1/6 had problems with the indoor environment, 
while 2/3 of these schools violated the law (arbejdsmiljøloven) [17]. 
The poor indoor air quality affects the pupils and teachers on a daily basis and the 
new school reform from 2014 with longer school days for both teachers and pupils 
has not remedied the situation. A school year has a total duration of 1200 - 1400 hours 
depending on the grade. With a school year of approximately 40 weeks, this 
corresponds to a mean weekly distribution of hours of 30, 33, and 35 hours in school 
including breaks for 1.-3. grade, 4.-6. grade and 7.-9- grade [16, § 14b]. 
A study has shown that pupils spend almost 30% of their life in schools and about 
70% of their time inside a classrooms during a normal school day [Bakó-Biró et al. 
2012]. It is therefore our obligation to ensure good and healthy learning 
environments for the pupils well-being and Denmark's competitiveness.

Objective of this study 
The objective of this study was to investigate the seasonal variation of the indoor 
environmental quality (IEQ) between classrooms within the same school and 
between schools, both during the heating and non-heating season.  
Based on the conducted measurements the results would be evaluated in order to find 
out which factors have an important role when it comes to obtain the best indoor 
conditions to support the pupils well-being, health and learning abilities. This thesis 
would give an overview of the overall knowledge of the annual IEQ in the nine 
participating Danish primary schools.

The indoor environment in primary schools has been studied rather intensively 
during the past years [Bakó-Biró et al. 2012, Barkmann et al. 2012, Kjeldsen et al. 
2013, Sleegers et al. 2012, Sullivan et al. 2015, Wargocki & Wyon, 2013 and others.]. 
From these investigations an overall finding was that poor indoor conditions affect 
the pupils´ well-being and learning ability and thereby also their performance at 
school.  
In the following the main principles of the known theory and research will be 
described. The main parameters characterizing the IEQ will be described together 
with knowledge about how the indoor environment affects the pupils and their 
learning ability. Also, the rules and recommendations regarding a healthy learning 
environment will be reviewed, together with the newest knowledge about the indoor 
environment in Danish primary schools. 
Parameters that describe the Indoor Environmental Quality 
During a normal school day the pupils and their teachers spend most of their time in 
the classrooms, where they are exposed to several parameters which affect their 
comfort, health and performance.  
Figure 1: Parameters influencing the indoor environmental quality. 
It is important to remember that not only physical and measurable parameters, but 
also factors as interior materials, cleaning routines, maintenance, aesthetics etc. have 
an influence on the perception of the indoor environment [Larsen and Andreasen, 
2016].

“...What requirements do people have in relation to indoor air? The desire is that the air 
can be perceived as fresh and pleasant, that it has no negative impact on their health, 
and that the air is stimulating and promotes their work, i.e. it increases their 
productivity and the learning of their children in the classroom at school” 
 [Fanger, 2006]

In outdoor air the CO2 concentration is around 380 ppm, while a typical range for the 
indoor concentration is from 500 to 2500 ppm [Toftum et al. 2011]. 
CO2 concentrations is often the main sources of contamination of the indoor 
environment. The production of CO2 depends on body size and activity level of a 
person and older and larger pupils emit more CO2. A sedentary adult produces around 
17 liters of CO2 per hour, whereas a pupil in Pre-preparatory classes (0. to 3. grade) 
with a high activity level can produce almost as much as a sedentary adult. 
This is important to account for in the design of ventilation systems in classrooms for 
pre-preparatory classes, because the pupils may have a high activity level due to 
playing and movement [Masseeksperimentet 2009 & Kjeldsen et al. 2013]. 
CO2 is not harmful in the concentrations which is typically experience indoors, but 
studies have shown that high CO2 concentrations may affect our ability to make 
rational decisions [Satish et al. 2012]. 
Studies have shown that the IAQ in schools often is very poor and that an increase of 
the ventilation rate from 5 to 10 l/s has a significant and positive influence on the 
pupils performance, by more than 15% [Wargocki et al. 2005 & Wargocki and Wyon, 
2007]. 
People emits bioeffluents that contains water vapor, CO2 and various VOC´s. The 
number of bioeffluents are variable and depends on the hygiene and activity level. In 
classrooms the pupils are often the main source of pollution and therefore it is mostly 
the number of pupils which determines the ventilation needs. To avoid poor air 
quality an appropriate ratio between ventilation rate and number of occupants must 
be obtained. The CO2 concentration is an indicator of the amount of bioeffluents in the 
indoor air and the CO2 concentration can be expressed as: a measure of how well a 
room is ventilated, in relation to the number of people present [Bakó-Biró et al. 2012]. 
In worst case poor air quality can cause health problems as allergies and asthmatic 
diseases, which are expensive to society in terms of general public health, costs in 
medicine treatments, absenteeism etc. [Fanger, 2006]. 
In this thesis, CO2 concentrations will characterize the air quality in the measured 
classrooms. 
Thermal indoor environment 
Both air- and radiant temperatures, air velocity and relative humidity affect thermal 
comfort and even small temperature changes, within the comfort zone, can have a 
considerable effect on our well-being. In classrooms temperatures can easily increase 
significantly during a lecture [Toftum et al. 2011]. 
Temperatures and temperature variation affect the performance of pupils and 
teachers in classroom, but mostly the pupils since they are more susceptible to 
changes in the environmental conditions, compared to teachers who may have a 
higher adaptability. The pupils are obliged to learn and perform new tasks every day, 
while the teachers are more used to work with routines, deadlines and are teaching 
well-known and practiced material and tasks [Wargocki and Wyon, 2013]. 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
Page 23 of 160 
 
For each 1°C the temperature is lowered under 25°C, within the range of acceptable 
temperatures, a better performance up to 2-4% can be obtained. If the temperature 
can be kept between 20-21°C in a classroom, it is possible to reach 20% higher yields 
of tasks including high concentration and understanding [Toftum et al. 2011]. 
Draught can cause discomfort even at low air velocities, if the air temperature is below 
23°C. At temperatures higher than 23°C there is a reduced risk of draught discomfort 
[Toftum et al. 2011]. 
The indoor air humidity air has an impact on the pupils and the teachers well-being. 
The relative humidity typically ranges from 20-30% in the heating season up to 70% 
in the non-heating season. When the relative humidity is between 30-70% few 
problems with thermal comfort and mold growth may occur. Most people prefer 
values between 35-60%. Too dry air can cause a sensation of dryness and irritation 
of skin, eyes and nose/throat, while high air humidity can cause growth of mould, 
fungi and even house dust mites, which can cause allergy and malodours. The Asthma 
Allergy Association recommends that the relative humidity is kept below 45% in 
order to minimize the risk of dust mites [9]. 
In this thesis, air temperature and relative humidity will characterize the thermal 
indoor environment for the classrooms. 
Lighting 
Both natural and artificial light affect our health, mood and well-being. The light 
intensity and color temperature of artificial light affect many physiological processes 
in our bodies and also the quality of our sleep. Specific lighting conditions, as artificial 
light can optimize general learning conditions and increase human performance as; 
working/reading speed, accuracy, concentration, comprehension and last but not 
least achievement and motivation [Sleegers et al. 2012]. 
Light visible to the human eye is electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths ranging 
from approx. 380 to 780 nm. Parameters as brightness, contrasts, reflectances, 
illuminance, daylight and color temperature are the most known parameters to 
describe lighting conditions. Especially illuminance which is given as luminous flux 
per square meter, measured in lux and color temperature which is measured in Kelvin 
(K). Earlier studies show that at higher illuminance, cooler color temperatures (17000 
K) leads to increased concentration levels and at lower illuminance and warmer color 
temperatures the communication and social behavior seemed to improve [Barkmann 
et al. 2012]. 
Glare can be a issue for the pupils learning in classrooms when great contrasts occurs. 
Both direct and reflected glare can give these problems, especially when glossy paint 
is used on the surfaces. The perception of reflected glare can vary from person to 
person and is therefore very individual. A result of both direct and reflected glare can 
cause disability glare or discomfort glare. Disability glare can result in reduced visual 
performance and visibility is often accompanied by discomfort glare which can result 
in headaches [Nielsen, 2015 & Tsangrassoulis et al. 2004]. 
==PAGE:25==
 
Page 24 of 160 
 
Especially daylight (~5000 K) is an important factor for our health, well-being, 
immune system and circadian rhythm. The effect of light varies from human to 
human, but daylight gives energy and counteracts fatigue, affects the 
psychophysiological activity levels, mood and social behavior [Barkmann et al. 2012 
& Sleegers et al. 2012].  
Light is a necessary factor for learning in schools, since about 70-80% of all sensory 
perceptions is through our vision and therefore a good lighting environment is 
important for the pupils’ ability to learn and perform, be creative and prosper [[8] & 
Barkmann et al. 2012]. 
By ensuring the right amount of daylight in classrooms the amount of artificial light 
can also be reduced significantly, and thereby also the energy needs for artificial light. 
Today, most of the old classrooms are installed with smart- and whiteboards since 
the old blackboard teaching has disappeared. This provides challenges in relation to 
the existing artificial light in the classrooms, because it is designed for blackboard 
teaching and in most cases not replaced when smart- and whiteboards are installed.  
Studies have shown that in classrooms with daylight there was a trend towards 
increased concentration with daylight white light, an increase of communication with 
warm white light and delay in the seasonal cortisol rhythm without artificial or 
natural daylight [Barkmann et al. 2012]. 
Studies have also found positive effects on reading, writing and math exercises with 
stronger lighting (500 lux) compared to standard lighting of approx. 300 lux, among 
pupils in elementary school - this questions whether or not today's standards and 
recommendations are acceptable and adequate! [Sleegers et al. 2012]. 
In this thesis, only the illuminance level will characterize the lighting conditions in the 
classrooms. 
Acoustics 
Great acoustics environments are also important when it comes to the pupils learning 
abilities. Results show that pupils auditory working memory and comprehension are 
negatively affected by too much noise. The communication between the teachers and 
the pupils are important in learning contexts. The acoustic conditions of classrooms 
should thereby substantiate this communication, so the teacher can save time, effort 
and energy. The speech levels we can hear are in the range from 100 to 8000 Hz and 
it can be rather difficult to understand speech in both noisy and reverberant 
conditions. The acoustic characteristics of the classrooms means something for the 
noise levels and in general the noise suppression. A to high reverberation time can 
cause problems with understanding simple messages and thereby cause to high noise 
levels. Studies have even shown that an increase in background noise and 
reverberation time had a negative effect on the pupils performance [Sullivan et al. 
2015]. 
The sound pressure level (SPL) is used to describe the sound level in a room, from 
technical installations and the human speech. The SPL, the reverberation time (RT) of 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
Page 25 of 160 
 
the room and the speech intelligibility have an important role when it comes to 
acoustics and the pupils learning abilities. 
The SPL will vary throughout a school day due to different tasks and activities in the 
classrooms and the locations of the lectures. The SPL is a summation of the direct 
sound from the sound source and the reflections from the room surfaces, at the place 
of the receiver. In classrooms, it is desirable to minimize the reflections by using 
sound absorbers, but materials with too high absorption coefficients can affect the 
SPL and thereby create bad conditions for the speech intelligibility [Acoustics, F16]. 
The RT describes the room's acoustical conditions and is measured in seconds. It is 
described as the time it takes for an emitted sound to decrease with 60 dB (RT60). In 
some cases are 20 or 30 dB decreases (RT20 and RT30) used to describe the 
reverberation time in a room.  
To ensure good conditions for comprehension and speech intelligibility in classrooms, 
the RT should be 0.6 sec. or below, which can be insured with good sound absorbing 
materials in the rooms [Maekawa et al. 2011]. 
So sound is an important factor when it comes to the indoor environment and it is 
especially important to distinguish between what/when it is noise and what/when it 
is sound! 
In this thesis, SPL and RT30 will characterize the acoustic conditions in the 
classrooms.

03 
PUPILS PERFORMANCE 
 
 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
Page 29 of 160 
 
Pupils performance is influenced by the indoor environmental quality, especially the 
air quality, but also the temperature, noise and lighting conditions [Bakó-Biró et al. 
2012, Satish et al. 2012, Wargocki and Wyon, 2013, Toftum et al. 2015, Pelegrin-
Garcia et al. 2014, Turunen et al. 2014, Sleegers et al. 2012 and others]. 
Reduced pupil well-being and performance caused by poor IEQ in classrooms can lead 
to reduced learning outcome, which can have economic consequences both on 
individual and societal levels [Toftum et al. 2015].  
“Impairment of learning performance and increased absenteeism are partly due to 
inadequate ventilation and unsuitable thermal conditions in classrooms”  
[Bakó-Biró et al. 2012] 
Air quality and ventilation 
Ventilation is often inadequate in classrooms and can cause increased risk of asthma 
and other health related symptoms among the pupils [Mendell et al. 2005 & Bakó-
Biró et al. 2007]. 
Satish et al. (2012) tested the effect of different CO2 concentrations on decision 
making performance. It was concluded that at 2500 ppm and even lower, the 
performance of decision making would become marginal and in some cases even 
dysfunctional. It was  thereby concluded that some students in classrooms with high 
CO2 concentrations were disadvantaged in learning. This was also confirmed in other 
studies where it was stated that pupils would perform up to 15% worse and their 
ability to learn fall drastically when the CO2 concentration is between 1000-2000 
ppm. [12]. 
Studies show that by exposure to CO2 concentrations in the range of 3000-3500 ppm 
alterations in the breathing and heart rate as well as loss of concentration and well-
being can be expected [Bakó-Biró et al. 2007]. 
Bakó-Biró et al. (2007) showed that the ventilation rate has a significant impact on 
the pupils performance. They increased the pupils performance levels with up to 
5.8% under improved ventilation conditions and for some pupils the percent were 
even higher ~7%. 
Also Wargocki and Wyon (2013) tested that increased ventilation would have a 
positive effect on the pupils performance. Higher ventilation rates can be associated 
with lower absence rates in elementary school and increased ventilation rates can 
lower the amounts of airborne particles in classrooms.  
Increasing the ventilation rate from 6 to 10 l/s per person, may increase pupils 
performance will with up to 9%. To have CO2 concentration below the limit value at 
1000 ppm, ventilation rates at 7-8 l/s per person is needed [Toftum et al. 2011 & [11]]. 
Wyon et al. (2010) and Wargocki and Wyon (2013) showed that doubling the outdoor 
air supply rate would improve the pupil's performance of schoolwork with 8% and 
up to 14%, in terms of speed. Other studies showed that good ventilation and air 
quality in classrooms could improve the pupils performance so they were 10-15% 
quicker than normally [Wargocki & Wyon, 2007]. 
==PAGE:31==
 
Page 30 of 160 
 
Wargocki and Wyon (2013) stated also that seasonal changes can affect the 
schoolwork performance. The pupils performance may be 30% below what it could 
be all year around, due to conditions, like the air quality in classrooms, that varies 
seasonally with the outdoor climate. 
Toftum et al. (2015) stated that pupils in schools with balanced mechanical 
ventilation perform better than pupils in schools with manual natural ventilation, due 
to lower CO2 concentrations in classrooms. 
In poorly ventilated classrooms, pupils are likely to be less attentive and 
concentrated. Bakó-Biró et al. (2012) showed that the higher the ventilation rates 
were, the faster and more accurate responses in different tasks. The tests were 
performed at eight primary school in England, and more than 200 pupils participated 
in these experiments – in the age from 9-10 years old. Based on studies 8 l/s was 
recommended for all teaching facilities to prevent impairments of pupils performance 
due to inadequate ventilation. Questions about the classroom environment, thermal 
sensation, mood, SBS symptoms, lifestyle and sleep during the night were also asked 
to the pupils since all of the above mentioned factors are believed to affect the 
concentration and performance [Bakó-Biró et al. 2012 & Wyon et al. 2010]. 
As mentioned have studies showed that pupils performance level can be improved by 
up to 14% by doubling the ventilation rate, from a low level. This is a great potential, 
since more than half of the Danish primary schools have inadequate ventilation due 
to no mechanical ventilation systems [Wargocki and Wyon, 2013 & Toftum et al. 
2011]. 
Temperature conditions 
Studies by Bakó-Biró et al. (2012) showed that low temperatures have a positive 
impact on student performance. 
Studies of occupants in offices showed that thermal discomfort distracts attention and 
generates complaints, increases SBS symptoms and has a negative effect on mental 
work and performance. This corresponds well with Wargocki and Wyon (2013) 
results from seven field intervention experiments, where 380 pupils in the age of 10-
12 years old participated. The study showed when the air temperature was reduced 
from 25 to 20°C the pupils performance improved significantly. It was improved with 
2-4% for every 1°C the temperature was lowered and the pupils indicated that the air 
felt much fresher at lower temperatures. 
Earlier studies by Wargocki and Wyon had also stated that improvements with up to 
10-20%, especially in terms of concentration and response speed, would occurs by 
reducing the air temperature in classrooms to the recommended level. No increase in 
the error rate was observed in terms of increased response speed [Wargocki and 
Wyon, 2007 & Wyon et al. 2010]. At 27°C findings predicted that the pupils 
performance would be 14-28% worse than at 20°C.  It was also stated that if the 
temperature could be kept between 20-21°C, a 20% higher yield could be obtained, 
including tasks with concentration and understanding [Wyon et al. 2010]. 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
Page 31 of 160 
 
Room acoustics and noise 
A room's acoustic conditions are characterized by the reverberation time. The 
reverberation time is depending on the room volume, the used internal surface 
materials and the interior of the room.  
Poor room acoustics affect the communication between teachers and students and 
can be a barrier for the pupils personal development and academic achievement 
[Pelegrin-Garcia et al. 2014]. 
When pupils are exposed to noise a reduction in their performance occurs. Several 
studies have shown that noise is the most frequently factor in classrooms which daily 
causes inconvenience for the pupils. These studies conclude that noise has a 
significant impact on the pupils auditory working memory and listening 
comprehension [Turunen et al. 2014, Sullivan et al. 2015 & Børnerådet 2012]. 
Studies by Ljung et al. 2009 stated that traffic noise had a significant influence on 
pupils concentration and performance in reading speed and mathematics, compared 
to irrelevant speech which did not influence their performance on any of the tasks. 
Sullivan et al. (2015) stated also that an increase of background noise and RT had a 
negative effect on the pupils performance, since children have difficulty in 
understanding speech in noisy and reverberant environments. Pelegrin-Garcia et al. 
(2014) also stated that long reverberation time and too high noise levels increased 
the annoyance among teachers as well and that long reverberation times were 
associated with higher self-reported noise exposure among pupils. 
Pelegrin-Garcia et al. (2014) recommends that the value of reverberation time in fully 
occupied classrooms is between 0.45 sec. and 0.6 sec., for flexible teaching methods. 
In unoccupied but furnished conditions should the reverberation time in the same 
classroom be between 0.6 and 0.7 sec. These recommendations applies for 
classrooms with less than 40 pupils and volumes below 210 m3. A reverberation time 
in unoccupied furnished classrooms designed for high speech intelligibility should not 
exceed 0.7 sec. in any case [Pelegrin-Garcia et al. 2014]. 
The best acoustic conditions for groups with up to 20 pupils are obtained with a 
reverberation time around 0.45 sec, in occupied classrooms not larger than 120 m3. 
In an unoccupied situation should the reverberation time be around 0.55 sec. 
[Pelegrin-Garcia et al. 2014]. 
Lighting 
The lighting conditions not only affect the pupil's performance, but impacts how the 
IEQ is perceived. 
Sleegers et al. (2012) tested the influence of light on pupils concentration. The study 
showed that artificial light can have positive effects on working speed accuracy and 
task performance. It was also stated from the Dutch study that “focus light” (1000 lux, 
6500 K) had a positive effect on the concentration compared to “standard settings” 
(650 lux, 12000 K). The findings underline the importance of good lighting for 
learning purposes, but suggested also that older pupils concentration might be less 
==PAGE:33==
 
Page 32 of 160 
 
affected by the lighting conditions than younger pupils - the reason could be that they 
are more trained to concentrate and used to tests [Sleegers et al. 2012]. 
Another study tested variable light in different classrooms and found that lighting 
adjusted to “concentrate mode” (Bright cold light, 1060 lux, 5800 K) instead of 
“standard mode” (300 lux, 4000 K) reduced the number of errors by 20.8% for an 
average student, together with a significant improvement in answering, reading 
speed and comprehension. The students read 16.8% more words under changing 
lighting conditions [Barkmann et al. 2012]. 
Absenteeism 
Limited studies have shown associations between the IEQ and absenteeism among 
pupils and teachers.  
Increased illness absence can be a consequence of poor classroom ventilation, which 
indirectly can lead to a decrease in the pupils learning outcome. It is also important 
to remember that the health status of the pupils, their immune system and conditions 
at home has an important role [Toftum et al. 2015]. 
Studies in USA have shown that illness absence affects the performance, due to fewer 
lessons attended and that high CO2 concentrations resulted in significantly higher 
absenteeism among the pupils [Mendell et al. 2005 & 2013]. Studies have shown by 
increasing the CO2 concentration with 1000 ppm, the pupils absenteeism increased 
from 10 to 20% [11]. 
Danish studies from 2013 showed that pupils in schools with mechanical ventilation 
had lower absenteeism and better results in the National Tests, than schools where 
ventilation relied on manual opening of windows and doors [[11], Kjeldsen et al. 2013 
& Toftum et al. 2011]. 
Mendell et al. (2005) states that when it comes to cleaning it is known that increased 
cleaning can reduce illness absence, but not directly increase the pupils performance 
levels.  
Based on a literature study of the relationship between sick leave and ventilation in 
office buildings have DTU estimated that the teachers absenteeism can be reduced 
with 4% by using Swedish ventilation rates. This corresponds to a half day per year 
or 0.2% of the working hours. The same tendency was also seen for the pupils and 
their absenteeism which could be reduced with 1-2 days per year, by using Swedish 
ventilation rates [Slotsholm, 2012]. When the ventilation rate was increased to 10 l/s 
the illness absence could be reduced with 4-8% [Toftum et al. 2011]. 
Symptoms and diseases caused by the IEQ 
“Children have greater susceptibility to some environmental pollutants than adults, 
because they breathe higher volumes of air relative to their body weights and their 
tissues and organs are actively growing“  
[Mendell et al. 2005] 
==PAGE:34==
 
 
Page 33 of 160 
 
Individuals perceive the indoor environment differently depending on gender, age, 
clothing insulation, activity level, health etc. Some children are even allergic or 
hypersensitive and can therefore be more vulnerable [Kjeldsen et al. 2013]. 
This may be problematic as the recommendations and design values regarding indoor 
environment in schools are based on an “average and standardized (adult) person”. 
An insufficient indoor environment increases the risk of adverse perceptions and 
symptoms (odor nuisance, headache, nausea, fatigue, lethargy, irritation eye, nose 
and/or throat, breathing difficulties, asthma, skin irritation or even hypersensitive 
reactions for the occupants) [Masseeksperimentet 2014, Mendell et al. 2005 & Toftum 
et al. 2011]. 
Turunen et al. 2014 tested IEQ in classrooms at fifth and sixth grade pupils in Finnish 
elementary schools. 297 schools participated and the most common weekly 
symptoms among the participating pupils in the spring semester was fatigue followed 
by stuffy nose and headache. Rhinitis and concentration problems were also among 
commonly reported weekly symptoms but stuffy nose and stuffy air/poor IAQ were 
mentioned as the most frequent factors causing daily inconvenience [Turunen et al. 
2014]. 
Studies from the heating and non-heating season by Gao et al. (2014) showed that the 
perception of the indoor environment was most positive for classrooms with 
automatic natural ventilation and exhaust fans. In these classrooms few of the above 
mentioned symptoms were reported. The air was also perceived fresher in the non-
heating season in classrooms with automatic natural ventilation, than classrooms 
with balanced mechanical or manual ventilation. The intensity of symptoms varied 
depending on the season and the ventilation type. Symptom intensity was highest in 
classrooms with mechanical ventilation, in both the heating and non-heating season. 
The above mentioned symptoms are normally caused by many factors occurring at 
the same time and when a group of people, in the same building, shows the same type 
of symptoms the phenomenon is described as the Sick Building Syndrome (SBS) - 
symptoms which disappear when leaving the building [Toftum et al. 2011]. 
In the long run, an unhealthy indoor environment can cause severe and chronic 
diseases as for example heaves because of too high concentrations of allergens in dust 
in classrooms [Toftum et al. 2011]. Many pupils suffer from allergies and asthma, and 
according to Astma-Allergi Danmark around 8-10% of all Danish school children have 
asthma, while up to 80% suffer from allergies, too. This can cause a high rate of 
sickness absence for each student every year and thereby affect their learning [[14], 
Toftum et al. 2011 & Mendell et al. 2005]. 
A high occupancy rate in poor ventilated classrooms can also increase the risk of 
airborne infections as sore throat, a cold or even otitis, between the pupils [Toftum et 
al. 2011].

VENTILATION PRINCIPLES 
 
 
==PAGE:44==
 
 
Page 43 of 160 
 
Below is listed the four most used ventilation principles in Danish primary schools 
[Hviid et al. 2014]:  
 
Natural ventilation: Ventilation provided by natural forces - temperature 
differences between indoors and outdoors and pressure differences across 
windows, air valves and/or shafts. Natural ventilation in schools is mostly 
controlled manually by teachers or pupils, but it can also be automatic. 
Automatic regulation of opening and closing of windows can be controlled by 
time schedules, CO2 concentration and/or temperature. 
 
Mechanical exhaust ventilation: Air from the room is removed by a fan and 
air is supplied through ventilation openings (windows, air valves in windows, 
shafts etc.). 
 
Balanced mechanical ventilation (mechanical supply and exhaust): Both air 
supply and exhaust to and from the room is controlled by a ventilation system, 
which results in balanced airflows. The ventilation can be centralized or 
decentralized. 
A mechanical ventilation systems provides fixed airflows over time or by 
current demand, and can be controlled by temperature or CO2-concentrations 
or according to time schedules. 
 
Hybrid ventilation: A combination of both natural and mechanical 
ventilation (exhaust or balanced ventilation).

REGULATIONS AND 
STANDARDS 
 
 
==PAGE:54==
 
 
Page 53 of 160 
 
For Danish schools laws and recommendations have been made by the authorities to 
ensure a sufficient learning environment for the pupils. In the following, these laws 
and recommendations will be shortly described. 
The Danish Building Regulation 
The Danish Building Regulation describes the conditions which should be achieved 
for new buildings and renovation projects. For older buildings going through a larger 
renovation process and maybe even a change of use, the building has to comply with 
the applicable regulation. 
The Building Regulation takes the IEQ into account by defining recommendations and 
requirements for thermal and atmospheric comfort, acoustics and lighting conditions. 
In the following are the applicable paragraphs from the Building Regulation (BR15) 
listed with explanations: 
 
It is stated that the indoor environment in buildings must be designed so it 
ensures the users a healthy and safe environment, during their stay. (6.1 §1) 
 
No specific requirements to the thermal indoor environment are stated, but 
healthy and comfortable temperatures corresponding to the activities in the 
classrooms and the clothing insulation of the users must be obtained. (6.2 §1) 
 
Schools must be equipped with mechanical ventilation, which can provide a 
ventilation rate of 5 l/s per person and 0.35 l/s/m2. While ensuring that the 
CO2 concentration indoors does not exceed 0.1% over a longer period. (6.3.1.3 
§2) 
 
The reverberation time in classrooms must not exceed 0.6 seconds. (6.4.3 §3)  
 
For daylight the Building Regulations do not give any direct values referring 
to schools, but for workspaces in general is the daylight considered 
acceptable; when the window/glass or skylights area corresponds to min. 
10% or 7% of the floor area, respectively. The daylight can also be considered 
acceptable if there is a DF of 2% on the workplane. (6.5.2 §1) 
 
The windows should allow for view to the surroundings, but should also have 
the possibility to be shaded properly to minimize the risk of overheating and 
adverse perceptions by direct solar radiation.  (6.5.2 §1) 
 
Regarding artificial light in classrooms refers BR15 to standard DS/EN 12464-
1 which states classrooms, tutorial classrooms should have a illuminance level 
at 300 lux. (6.5.3 §1) 
The Danish law on Education Environment 
The Danish Ministry of Education implemented a law in August 2001 to ensure a 
satisfying educational environment for children and students of all schools and 
educational institutions in Denmark. 
 
(§1) Pupils and students have the right to a good educational environment, so 
the teaching can take place under safe and healthy manners. Also the 
educational environment should promote the participants’ opportunities for 
development and learning [7]. 
==PAGE:55==
 
Page 54 of 160 
 
The Danish Working Environment Authority recommendations 
The Danish Working Environment Authority gives recommendations and minimum 
values to avoid poor indoor environments. 
For schools they recommend, [1]: 
 Temperatures between 20-22°C and never exceeding 25°C for sedentary 
work. 
 The air velocity should not exceed 0.15 m/sec to avoid possible discomfort 
due to drafts. (section 2.1) 
 The CO2 concentration should be kept below 0.1%. If the CO2 concentration 
exceeds 0.2% for a longer time period (20 minutes), during a day, the air 
change will be defined as insufficient. (section 3.1) 
 The SPL should not exceed 85 dB(A) during a work day, due to hearing 
damages. 
 The reverberation time in classrooms the Danish Working Environment 
Authority refers to the value listed in the Danish Building Regulation. 
 Regarding the light conditions in classrooms, it is recommended to have an 
illuminance level of 300 lux on the workplane and 200 lux on the black-
/whiteboards [3,4]. 
The Danish Working Environment Act: 
The law is from the 7th September 2010. It does not state specific values for the IEQ, 
but is made as a guideline to create a safe and healthy working environment to be 
obtained at all Danish workplaces. The goal is to ensure a good working environment, 
at all times, which complies with the technical and social development of the society. 
Pupils are not covered by the law, but by ensuring the teachers good working 
conditions, the children indirectly have the same conditions, since they stay in the 
same classrooms for longer time periods [2]. 
Danish Standard 
Standards are used worldwide to ensure a good quality of the built environment. 
Standards are a common language, which makes it easier for companies to work 
together despite language and distances, because the basic guidelines are the same. 
In order to obtain satisfying indoor environments laws, as the Building Regulation, 
refers to standards for functional requirements.   
DS 3033 is a standard from 2011 for voluntary classification of the indoor 
environment in buildings (Residential houses, schools, children's day care centers 
and offices) based on selected indoor environment parameters. This standard works 
with five classifications where A++ defines the excellent indoor environment, A+ the 
good indoor environment, A the satisfactory indoor environment corresponding to 
requirements in the Building Regulations. B is only reaching the minimum 
requirements in the Building regulation and C is defined as poor indoor environment 
[DS 3033, 2011]. 
==PAGE:56==
 
 
Page 55 of 160 
 
DS 474 is from 1995 and defined as the code for Thermal Indoor Climate. The code 
contains a set of requirements which aims to ensure a good and acceptable indoor 
climate for the occupants [DS 474, 1995]. 
DS/EN 15251 is from 2007 and defined as a standard with indoor environmental 
input parameters for design and assessment of energy performance of buildings 
addressing IAQ, thermal environment, lighting and acoustics. The standard works 
with four quality categories, where category I is the high level of expectation and 
category IV defines values, which only can be accepted for a limited time period. 
Category II describes a normal level of expectation and is commonly used for new 
buildings and renovations [DS/EN 15251, 2007].

The selected schools 
The participating schools are located in Greater Copenhagen and surrounding 
municipalities. They were already studied during the heating season, before the 
beginning of this thesis. The schools were selected based on their location, 
construction year, typology, architecture, ventilation principle etc. to represent as 
wide a range of characteristics as possible of the Danish primary schools. Below is 
listed the procedure used in Hansen (2016) and Larsen and Andreasen (2016) to 
select the participating schools: 
 
From the webpage of the Ministry for Education a list of all the ~1300 public 
primary schools in Denmark was downloaded. 
 
The geographical area of interest was narrowed down to schools on Zealand, 
to optimize the work process. In particular, schools from Zealand which also 
participated in the Mass Experiment in 2014 were selected. 
 
Schools which had participated in DTU studies before, beside the Mass 
Experiments, were not asked to participate and deleted from the list. 
 
The edited list was compared to a list of school typologies made in the thesis 
by Berg and Dam-Krogh (2015). 
 
In Hansen (2016) the typology village school was eliminated from the study, 
since there was no information about the schools, that were listed as “Village 
schools”. 
 
Two schools in each of the five remaining typologies were then selected for 
further study. It was aimed to find schools with opposite characteristics, 
within each typology. For example to find schools with the same topology, but 
different ventilation principles etc.     
 
Each school was then examined with relevant sources (school's web pages, 
pictures, Google maps etc.) to confirm the typology. If the typology couldn't be 
confirmed, another school was chosen. 
Out of the ten schools Hansen (2016) contacted, only three wanted to participate in 
the study. Hansen (2016) selected and contacted then four other schools, without 
taking into account participation in the Mass Experiments in 2014, due to the strict 
time schedule of the study. In total seven schools participated in the study by Stine 
Rolle Hansen, Hansen (2016).

Selection of classrooms 
When selecting the classrooms at each participating school, it was important to fulfill 
the listed criteria, the best possible way: [Hansen, 2016 & Larsen and Andreasen, 
2016] 
 
Select a classroom in each building, if the school consists of more than one 
building. 
 
Select classrooms with different orientations, floor heights and floor levels. 
 
Select classrooms with different class levels/grades. 
 
Select classrooms with different ventilation types. 
Meeting these criteria was done to ensure that the collected data would give a broad 
perspective of the indoor environment in Danish primary schools. 
Preparation before going to the schools 
The schools were contacted by mail and telephone weeks before the measurements 
were scheduled to start in order to arrange meetings at the schools, get keys and the 
missing information on the school buildings, etc. 
From the given classroom numbers and locations, from the previous measurements, 
class schedules were studied for each classroom. This was done in order to place the 
instruments without disturbing the lectures, for example when the pupils had a break 
or when they were at gymnastics or physics. A matrix was made to have a clear 
overview of where each class would be during the day the given school was visited. 
Together with a floor plan this matrix formed the basis for planning the “setup” day 
at each school. An example of one of those school matrices can be found in appendix 
D. 
Classrooms and measurement duration 
The same classrooms which were visited during the heating season were also studied 
in the non-heating season. The person-load and activity levels may have changed in 
the classrooms, due to a new school year and classes changing classrooms. It was also 
experienced that some classrooms which were used the year before, were not 
currently in use. Instead, classrooms as close to the geometry, orientation etc. as 
possible were then selected instead. At Kirkebjerg skole the tested classroom (2.v) in 
the heating season was transformed into a library, so a similar classroom (3.z) was 
chosen instead. 
Due to additional measurement instrumentation available, extra classrooms and 
specialized rooms as woodworks, arts and physics were investigated in the non-
heating season. 
The instrumentation was set up in each classroom as close as possible to the position 
used in the heating season. In some cases the optimal placement of the instruments 
was not feasible due to the classroom layout (eg. Hobo light loggers), power supply 
etc. However, no critical positions as too close to windows or radiators were chosen. 
All the instruments were logging for approximately one week at each school. 
==PAGE:65==
 
Page 64 of 160 
 
On general, ten classrooms and three specialized rooms were studied at each school. 
In each classroom the CO2 concentration, temperature and relative humidity were 
measured. In two or three classrooms out of the ten classrooms full set of 
measurements were performed. This included also measurements of sound and 
lighting conditions. User behavior with regard to opening of windows and doors and 
the use of artificial light were also measured. 
Protocol and checklist 
A protocol and a checklist were developed in the previous study [Hansen, 2016] and 
used again in this study. 
The protocol was primarily filled out before going to the school, but also during the 
visit at the school and after the visit. The protocol was made to ensure that the 
instruments were pretested and placed correctly at the schools. 
The checklist ensured that all information about the school and classrooms were 
registered during the visit. It summarized questions to the school janitor and the 
administration about the school, such as construction year, potential renovations, 
cleaning, maintenance, heating and ventilation principles, location, geometry etc. 
were noted for each classroom. Another part focused on the classroom, window size, 
interior materials, equipment, geometry, orientation etc. On the last pages a drawing 
of the “floor” plan and a cross-section were made to note the placement and the 
identification of the equipment, to make the data processing easier, and in order to 
check that everything still was positioned as it should be when collecting the 
instruments. An example of a classroom drawing can be seen in appendix G. 
The protocol and checklist were modified and developed further for this thesis and 
the new versions can be seen in appendix E and F. 
Updating the digital checklist 
All the information about the participating schools from the protocols and checklists 
were digitized in an Excel sheet after the heating season measurements. After each 
school visit in the non-heating season the digital checklist was updated with the 
missing information about classroom and window sizes, orientations, surroundings 
etc. Classroom numbers and which class level had been in the classroom before, was 
added to the database. 
Feedback reports 
Each school received a feedback report with the results of the measurements from the 
heating season. A similar report was created and provided to each school after the 
non-heating season measurements. Each report contained data about the air quality, 
sound and light levels, temperatures and the relative humidity for each regular and 
specialized classroom, together with an overall review of the quality of the indoor 
environment for the school for both seasons. An example of a feedback report can be 
seen in appendix H.

Preparation of collected data  
The light and sound loggers did not stop logging before they were connected to a 
computer, so the data between pickup and connection to the computer were deleted 
before the data sorting took place. 
All measured data were extracted to excel sheets by using the different software 
connected to each instruments. Before the data could be used the data had to be 
converted and distributed to different rows and columns. 
A half hour in the beginning and the end of the data for each classroom was deleted 
before the data was inserted in the custom made excel sheets. This was done to avoid 
misleading results, due to placing and collecting the instruments, especially for the 
classrooms where full measurements were conducted, since we were present in these 
classrooms for a longer time period. 
If the instruments were placed after twelve o'clock in the classrooms at the set up day, 
only few hours of data would be present for that given day. From the class schedule 
and the CO2 concentrations it was then evaluated if the data was included or omitted 
from the analyzes. 
The light and sound data were corrected with the correction coefficients from the 
correction curves made in the thesis, Hansen (2016). 
Occupancy schedules and assumptions 
It was only interesting to evaluate the occupied hours, therefore were the weekends 
and holidays removed from the data, together with the evening and nighttime. The 
breaks were also removed, even though not all pupils would leave the classroom 
during the breaks. It was decided to remove the breaks since these hours are not 
counted as learning hours. In order to ensure all occupied hours were included, in the 
data analysis, an extra hour was added in the end of each school day, since the 
schedules were not reliable in all cases. 
It was experienced that the schedules on the web pages did not always match the real 
situation when arriving to the schools. Classrooms could be occupied even though 
they were indicated in the schedule to be vacant. All data have been evaluated 
individually based on the CO2 concentrations, RH, schedules and sound pressure 
levels, so only data, when the pupils were present, were analyzed. 
It was not always easy to evaluate if the pupils were present or not, since high CO2 
concentrations could be a result of insufficient ventilation, different teaching methods 
etc. The CO2 concentration together with the RH, temperature and sound pressure 
level for the whole week was plotted in the same graph to make it more clear, when 
data had to be removed. An example of this graph can be seen in figure 16. If there 
was doubt whether or not the pupils were present, the data was included in the 
analysis.

Expanding the digital checklist 
After all the data from the measurements were processed, the digital checklist was 
expanded to maintain both the results from the heating and non-heating seasons. RH, 
temperature, CO2 concentration, sound and light data were merged into the digital 
checklist, while the (light) spot measurements was collected in its own data sheet. 
Data from reverberation time measurements 
The reverberation time was not measured in the non-heating season, since none of 
the classrooms were renovated or rearranged with new inventory during the time 
between the measurements.  
The reverberation time measurements were conducted in the classroom where the 
full measurements were performed in the heating season and only people from DTU 
were present in the classrooms during the measurements. 
The measurements were conducted with sinusoidal frequency sweeps according to 
SBI anvisning 217, 2008, in unoccupied classrooms [Hansen, 2016; Larsen and 
Andreasen, 2016]. 
The reverberation time was recorded in five different frequencies, 250, 500, 1000, 
2000 and 4000 Hz for both RT20 and RT30. During the measurements in the pilot 
phase [Hansen, 2016] it was found that the recordings in the frequencies 250 and 
4000 Hz were inaccurate, so these data were not taken into account in the data 
analysis of the nine schools.  
The reverberation time was therefore calculated as the average of the three 
frequencies 500, 1000 and 2000 Hz for respectively RT20 and RT30.  
Only the data for the RT30 measurements will be evaluated in this study.

Data limitations 
It was experienced that data was not available from some measurements in the 
heating and the non-heating season, due to instrument failures or that the power 
supply was disconnected during the measurements. 12 classrooms distributed evenly 
on both seasons had few data logged. Only four out of the 164 classrooms had few 
data for the CO2 concentration, temperature and RH measurements, while 8 
classrooms had few sound data.  
There was few data for CO2 concentration, temperature and RH for three classrooms 
in the non-heating season and for one classroom in the heating season. The three 
classrooms was 4.a and 4.b at Bakkegårdsskolen and the group room at 
Vejlebroskolen. For the heating season the results for 8.r at Gungehusskolen was 
based on few data. In appendix J can the detailed list be seen. 
Data analyses 
Excel was used for the analysis of the measured parameters. 
Descriptive statistics 
The main results are presented by statistical values as, mean, median, maximum, 
minimum, 1st and 3rd quartiles together with the percentage of time where the 
measured parameters were above or below recommended levels. To illustrate the 
results graphically box plots, bar charts, scatterplots etc. were used. 
Regression analysis 
Simple linear regression was used to analyze the relation between the measured 
parameters, building characteristics and outdoor weather conditions. The R2 value 
will be plotted in the figures. The correlation coefficient was used to validate the 
models and to evaluate if there was a relation or not between the two variables. The 
correlation coefficient is the square root of R2 and the value will be written in a 
parenthesis in the results chapter. 
The correlation coefficient is always between +1 and -1 and to interpret the calculated 
values following terms were used: 
 
ρ=0:        No linear relation 
 
ρ=± 0.3: A weak linear relation 
 
ρ=± 0.5: A moderate linear relation 
 
ρ=± 0.7: A strong linear relation 
 
ρ=± 1:    A perfect linear relation 
Weather data 
Weather data from both DMI and DTU´s weather station were used in this study.  
Mainly the weather data from DTU was used because it collected data with a one 
minute logging interval, compared to DMI´s weather archive where only daily mean 
weather data was available. The outdoor weather data from DTU aggregated for 8-15 
for each measurement day. The number of daily hours of sunshine was from DMI´s 
weather archive for the location Copenhagen. 
Even though some of the schools were placed ~30 km away from DTU, no greater 
variation in the outdoor weather conditions for Greater Copenhagen was expected.

Reverberation time  
The results of the measured RT30 in the heating season can be seen in figure 46. At 
each school reverberation time was measured in three classrooms. At Højdevangens 
skole (I) and Kirkebjerg skole (X) the RT was measured in two classrooms. At 
Dansborgskolen (V), Gungehusskolen (VI) and Vejlebroskolen (IX) more classrooms 
had the same reverberation time. 
 
Figure 46: The measured reverberation time distribution for the nine schools. 
In most of the classrooms, except for the classrooms in Vibeholmskolen (III) was the 
RT30 below 0.6 sec. This could be due to the ceiling material. Plates with a hard 
surface which is not a proper material for sound absorption and reduction were used 
in these classrooms. In all of the other classrooms sound absorbing material was 
registered as the ceiling material. 
One of the RT30 measurements for Kirkebjerg (X) skole was measured to 1 sec. which 
is above the recommended value. This was remarkable compared to the other 
classroom at Kirkebjerg skole (X) since no greater variation in volume size and 
interior was experienced. 
Some classrooms had more message boards, which work as sound absorbers like the 
acoustic ceiling. This could be one of the reasons why some of the classrooms had a 
low reverberation time. Reverberation time slightly lower than 0.4 sec. can be critical 
for the pupils learning ability and provides “just acceptable” vocal comfort according 
to Pelegrin-Garcia et al. (2014). 
In the classrooms at Bakkegårdsskolen (II) and Ishøj Skole (VII) the reverberation 
time RT30 was measured to 0.3 sec. this means that especially in these classrooms 
the pupils learning ability may be affected. No values below 0.3 sec. were registered 
during the experiments.

Detailed classroom results  
The following figures show some of the clearest seasonal variations within the schools 
and classrooms for the measured CO2 concentrations, temperatures, RH and SPL.  
The box plots show the results for two classrooms at each school. The first two 
boxplots represent one classroom for both seasons, while the next two box plots show 
the results aggregated for the other classroom. 
The classrooms were selected based on these three different parameters; variation 
between the median CO2 concentration, different ventilation types and different class 
levels within and between the seasons. 
At Højdevangens skole the two classrooms 6.k/8.c and 9.xp/7.c had balanced 
mechanical ventilation. Figure 49(A) show that the CO2 concentration reached 5000 
ppm in 6.k, this was in the middle of a double lecture. The mean CO2 concentration in 
6.k was ~2400 ppm compared to 9.xp where the concentration was ~700 ppm in the 
heating season. 
This leads to the questions whether or not the system was insufficient in 6.k, because 
the CO2 concentration during more than 90% of the occupied time was above the 
recommended level. Even though the volume of the two classrooms was the same, the 
number of pupils occupying the two classrooms differed. In 6.k there was 25 pupils, 
while 9.xp only had 17 pupils and therefore the CO2 production could be higher in 6.k. 
A great temperature variation can be seen for 6.k/8.c compared to 9.xp/7.c between the 
seasons. The lowest temperature range was observed in 6.k compared to 9. xp in the 
heating season, since in 75% of the occupied time the temperature in 9.xp was above 
24°C with a maximum above 26°C.  
6.k was facing south and 9.xp east and this could be the reason why 9.xp had higher 
temperatures.  
The measurements were performed in January and September/October and the mean 
outdoor temperatures were registered to respectively -0.6°C and 16.8 °C for the 
measurement periods. 
The RH varied a lot between the two season in classroom 9.xp/7.c with values from 
16 to 33% in the heating season and 36 to 63% in the non-heating season. In 50% of 
the occupied time in 9.xp was the RH below 25% for the heating season. 
The SPL was observed to be higher in 6.k than 9.xp in the heating season. This may be 
due to age difference, and thereby more active pupils in younger classes and the larger 
amount of pupils in 6.k.

Associations between measured parameters. 
The associations between the measured parameters was studied. Different 
associations were tested and the results can be seen in the following figures. The 
results are based on the “instantaneously measured values” and the mean and 
maximum values for the classrooms. All the different parameter variations were 
plotted for both balanced mechanical, automatic natural and manual natural 
ventilation, for both seasons. 
Instantaneous measured values 
The following described figures was based on the instantaneously measured values 
for each classroom. 
Figure 64 (B and C) shows that only a weak relation between the CO2 concentrations 
and the indoor temperature in the heating season was applicable for the classrooms 
with automatic and manual natural ventilation (ρ = 0.33 and ρ = 0.38). 
Figure 65 (A and C) shows the linear relation between the CO2 concentrations and RH. 
In the classrooms with balanced mechanical ventilation a moderate relation between 
the two parameters was observed in the heating season (ρ = 0.53). In the classrooms 
with manual natural ventilation a moderate relation was seen for the heating season 
(ρ = 0.58), while only a weak relation for the non-heating season occurred (ρ = 0.39). 
Both CO2 and humidity are produced by the human metabolism. 
Figure 66 shows that there was a weak linear association between the CO2 
concentration and the SPL, in the heating season in classrooms with balanced 
mechanical and automatic natural ventilation (ρ = 0.27 and ρ = 0.26). Wide scattering 
occurred mostly for classrooms with manual natural ventilation in the heating season.  
The relation between the indoor temperature and RH can be seen in figure 67. A weak 
inverse linear relation occurred for both seasons for classrooms with balanced 
mechanical ventilation and for classrooms with manual natural ventilation in the non-
heating season (ρ = -0.25 and ρ = -0.24). A moderate inverse linear relation was 
observed for classrooms with automatic natural ventilation in the non-heating season 
(ρ = -0.49).

orientation, window type, type of heating etc. 
No significant association between the temperature and SPL was observed as seen in

classrooms with automatic natural ventilation in both seasons (ρ = 0.26 and ρ = -0.24),

Comparison with the Mass Experiment 
Figure 75 and 76 shows the distribution of CO2 concentrations measured in the Mass 
Experiment 2014 for Region Zealand and Greater Copenhagen, compared with the 
results from this study for both seasons. Since the location of the nine schools were 
on Zealand in Region Greater Copenhagen, these two regions were chosen in the 
comparison. 
It should be noted that the measurements in the Mass Experiment were conducted at 
the end of a lesson (before lunch) in order to simulate “worst case”. In order to 
compare these results with this study, the maximum CO2 concentration was used as 
the “worst case” because there was no guarantee that the windows and doors were 
closed in the lesson before the lunch break, as it was in the Mass Experiment. 
In 1% of the occupied time the classrooms in the heating season had maximum CO2 
concentrations below 1000 ppm, while it was 21% in the non-heating season. As it 
can be seen these percentages were lower compared to the 41% and 36% observed 
in Region Greater Copenhagen and Region Zealand, indicating that the air quality may 
have been poor in this study. 
In the Mass Experiment 35% and 42% of the classrooms had balanced mechanical 
mechanical and manual natural ventilation, respectively. For this study the 
percentage was 33 % for balanced mechanical and 28% for manual natural 
ventilation in the heating season, while it in the non-heating season was 42% and 
29%. From the percentages it can be seen, that the schools in this study had a majority 
of classrooms with balanced mechanical ventilation in the non-heating season 
compared to the Mass Experiment.  
However, a lower percentage of occupied time below 1000 ppm was observed in this 
study. The remaining classrooms in the Mass Experiment had exhaust ventilation, 
while no classrooms with exhaust were included in this study. Automatic natural 
ventilation in this study represented 39% and 29% of the classrooms in the heating 
and non-heating season, respectively. The variation of the ventilation types 
represented in the two studies influenced the results and should be kept in mind.

Summary of the results for the 9 participating schools 
CO2 concentrations 
Large seasonal variation in the CO2 concentrations occurred within and between the 
schools and classrooms. The later the schools were built, the more likely was that 
balanced mechanical ventilation was installed. 
In general higher CO2 concentrations was observed in the heating season. In 95, 89 
and 77% of the occupied time in the non-heating season the CO2 concentrations were 
below 1000 ppm for balanced mechanical, automatic natural and manual natural 
ventilation. In the heating season the CO2 concentrations were below 1000 ppm in 66, 
33 and 26% of the occupied time for balanced mechanical, automatic natural and 
manual natural ventilation. 
In the heating season a weak linear association between the CO2 concentration and 
the indoor temperature was seen in classrooms with automatic and manual natural 
ventilation, while a strong inverse linear association between the maximum CO2 
concentration and the outdoor temperature occurred in the same classrooms. 
Temperature 
In general, the temperatures were within the recommended ranges for both seasons, 
but too high temperatures were observed in the non-heating season. Outliers in the 
heating season also occurred, but not for longer time periods. 
In 85, 91 and 81% of the occupied time in the heating season the temperatures were 
within the acceptable range of 20-24°C for balanced mechanical, automatic natural 
and manual natural ventilation. In the non-heating season the temperatures were 
within the acceptable range of 23-26°C in 77, 67 and 76% of the occupied time for 
balanced mechanical, automatic natural and manual natural ventilation. 
A strong association between the number of pupils and the maximum temperature 
was found for the heating season. No association between temperature and 
orientation was found, but highest temperatures occurred in classrooms facing 
northwest and southwest in the heating season, while it for the non-heating season 
was for east and southwest. A weak linear relation between the indoor temperature 
and the openable window area was observed for the non-heating season. 
No association between the indoor temperature and the SPL was found.  
Relative Humidity 
The RH was within the acceptable range of 25-60% in most of the occupied time. No 
larger problems occurred in the heating season where 92, 99 and 100% of the 
occupied time was within the recommended range, while the percentage was 91, 60 
and 72% in the non-heating season for balanced mechanical, automatic natural and 
manual natural ventilation.  
The relative humidity was higher in the non-heating season. The highest observed RH 
was for classrooms with manual natural ventilation. 
==PAGE:130==
 
 
Page 129 of 160 
 
For the classrooms with balanced mechanical ventilation and manual natural 
ventilation a moderate relation between CO2 concentration and RH was observed for 
the heating season. A moderate inverse linear relation between the indoor 
temperature and RH was observed for classrooms with automatic natural ventilation 
in the non-heating season. 
Sound pressure level 
No major problems concerning the SPL in the classrooms were observed during the 
study, regardless of season. The schools were all located in calm areas where no heavy 
traffic occurred. The mean SPL was ~65 dB(A), for both seasons which corresponds 
to normal indoor speech. The max SPL fluctuated between the class levels and 
seasons, but no SPL above 90 dB(A) was measured and the high SPL´s occurred only 
for short time periods. In classrooms with 20-25 pupils the highest SPL was observed. 
The highest SPL occurred in classrooms with automatic natural ventilation in the 
heating season, while the SPL tended to be lower in classrooms with balanced 
mechanical ventilation. 
In the non-heating season SPL´s above 85 dB(A) was observed in classrooms with 
balanced mechanical ventilation.  
A weak linear association between increasing CO2 concentration and SPL in 
classrooms with balanced mechanical and automatic natural ventilation was found. 
Reverberation time 
The RT30 was below 0.6 sec. in most of the classrooms. Values above 0.6 sec. and 
below 0.4 sec. occurred in some cases, which can be critical for the pupils´ learning 
ability.  
RT30 values above 0.6 sec. were measured in classrooms without sound absorbing 
ceiling material. 
Lighting  
The illuminance levels below the recommended 300 lux varied between the seasons 
and the measured locations, worse illuminance levels occurred in the heating season.  
The observed illuminance levels were assumed to be influenced by the outdoor 
weather conditions, for both seasons but especially for the non-heating season due to 
direct solar radiation. The illuminance level showed some association with the 
classroom orientation, while south facing classrooms tended to have highest 
illuminance levels. 
Large seasonal variation occurred in the spotlight measurements within each 
measured scenario. There was need for artificial light, especially in the heating 
season, to obtain satisfying illuminance levels in the classrooms. Also in large amounts 
of the occupied time the artificial light could not alone ensure illuminance levels 
around 300 lux as The Danish Working Environment Authority recommends.

12 
DISCUSSION 
 
 
==PAGE:134==
 
 
Page 133 of 160 
 
In the following the main findings from the literature study and the measurements 
will be discussed, together with an evaluation of the sources of errors. 
Seasonal variation  
The main objective of this study was to investigate the seasonal variability of the 
classroom environmental quality within and between nine Danish primary schools. 
Measurement data from 82 classrooms in both the heating and non-heating season 
was included in this analysis. CO2 concentrations below 1000 ppm occurred less 
frequently in the heating season compared to the non-heating season, while high 
temperatures occurred in both seasons. 
The Building regulations and The Danish Working Environment Authority state that 
the CO2 concentration in classrooms should be below 0.1% during the occupied hours 
[6]. The results showed that in the non-heating season the percentage of occupied 
time with a CO2 concentration below 1000 ppm was 95, 89 and 77%, while it was 66, 
33 and 26% in the heating season for balanced mechanical, automatic natural and 
manual natural ventilation. Classrooms with balanced mechanical ventilation 
typically had better air quality in both seasons and the worst air quality was observed 
in classrooms with manual natural ventilation in the heating season. Most of the older 
schools had typically manual natural ventilation while the new schools had balanced 
mechanical ventilation. 
When comparing these findings with the results of the Mass Experiment from 2014, 
the measured conditions in this study were worse. In only 1% of the occupied time, 
classrooms in the heating season had maximum CO2 concentrations below 1000 ppm, 
while 21% of the occupied time was registered in the non-heating season. These 
percentages were clearly lower than the 41% and 36% observed in Region Greater 
Copenhagen and Region Zealand [Masseeksperimentet, 2014]. Since the location of 
the nine schools were on Zealand in Region Greater Copenhagen, these two regions 
were chosen in the comparison.  
The results from the Mass Experiment were based on measurements of the CO2 
concentration made an hour before the lunch break, while data from this study was 
based on the maximum CO2 concentration. This was considered the best option for 
comparing the CO2 concentration between the two studies.  In addition the maximum 
CO2 concentration was used, since there was no guarantee that the windows and 
doors were closed in the lesson before the lunch break, as it was supposed to be in the 
Mass Experiment. 
The distribution of ventilation type between classrooms differed between this study 
and the Mass Experiment. In the Mass Experiment 35% and 42% of the classrooms 
had balanced mechanical and manual natural ventilation respectively. In this study 
the percentage was 33% for balanced mechanical and 28% for manual natural 
ventilation in the heating season, while in the non-heating it was 42% and 29%, 
respectively. Automatic natural ventilation was included in this study, while exhaust 
was included in the Mass Experiment. This could also be the reason why a higher 
percentage of occupied time with CO2 concentrations below 1000 ppm was obtained 
==PAGE:135==
 
Page 134 of 160 
 
in the Mass Experiment, 41% in Region Greater Copenhagen and 36% in Region 
Zealand. 
The Danish Working Environment Authority state that if the CO2 concentration 
exceeds 0.2% for a longer time period (interpreted as 20 minutes), the air change rate 
will be defined as insufficient [[1] - section 3.1]. In the heating season the CO2 
concentration in 5, 4 and 21% of the occupied time was above 2000 ppm with 
balanced mechanical, automatic natural and manual natural ventilation. This 
occurred in only 1% of the occupied time in classrooms with manual natural 
ventilation in the non-heating season, confirming that windows are opened more 
frequently in the non-heating season. 
Satish et el. (2012) stated that CO2 concentrations at ~2500 ppm could affect mental 
performance. The performance on decision making would become marginal or even 
dysfunctional. Especially at Højdevangens skole, Vibeholmskolen, Dansborgskolen, 
Gungehusskolen and Ishøj Skole, quite high CO2 concentrations were measured in the 
heating season. The detailed classroom descriptions showed that the CO2 
concentration in 6.k at Højdevangens skole even reached 5000 ppm in the middle of 
a double lecture, while the mean CO2 concentration was ~2400 ppm - in a classroom 
with balanced mechanical ventilation! 
In this study a weak association between the openable window area and the CO2 
concentration was observed in the heating season in classrooms with manual 
ventilation (ρ =0.30). However, in some classrooms the measured openable area was 
not the actual openable area, but only the size of the glazing of the openable window 
and therefore the associations with the real openable area may not be exactly as 
shown in figure 70.  
A strong inverse correlation between the maximum CO2 concentration and the 
outdoor temperature was found in the heating season in classrooms with both 
automatic and manual natural ventilation (ρ =-0.69 and ρ =-0.60). 
An acceptable range for the operative temperature (DS474, 1995) in the heating and 
non-heating season of 20-24°C and 23-26°C respectively, was used in this study to 
evaluate the thermal environment. In 85, 91 and 81% of the occupied time the 
temperature in the heating season was within the acceptable range of 20-24°C for 
balanced mechanical, automatic natural and manual natural ventilation. The 
percentage was somewhat lower in the non-heating season when it was within the 
acceptable range of 23-26°C in 77, 67 and 76% of the occupied time. The measured 
temperature reflects the air temperature as radiant temperature and air velocity were 
not measured in this study. 
Occupant interaction with radiator thermostats was not studied. This could have 
given another perspective on the variability of the temperatures in the classrooms, 
since temperatures below 17°C and 14°C occurred at Dansborgskolen and 
Bakkegårdsskolen. Knowledge of thermostat settings could have contributed to 
explaining such low temperatures. 
==PAGE:136==
 
 
Page 135 of 160 
 
From the literature study it was found that with temperatures in the range 20-22°C 
in a classroom, it is possible to reach 20% higher yields of tasks including high 
concentration and understanding, compared to the performance at 25°C [Toftum et 
al. 2011]. This temperature range is also recommended by the Danish Working 
Environment Authority. The temperature was within 20-22°C in 26, 36 and 21% of 
the occupied time in the heating season and 1, 4 and 0% in the non-heating season for 
balanced mechanical, automatic natural and manual natural. From these results it can 
be concluded that there is a potential to improve the pupils performance. The range 
20-22°C represents rather low temperatures and a conflict between the comfort and 
performance may therefore occur, especially for sensitive pupils. 
High temperatures occurred in classrooms with manual natural ventilation in both 
seasons. Temperatures above 30°C occurred in classrooms facing south and 
southwest in the non-heating season. Wyon et al. (2010) stated that indoor 
temperatures at 27°C or above could decrease the pupils performance by 14-28% 
compared with a classroom temperature of 20°C. In general, an association between 
the maximum indoor and outdoor temperature in classrooms with manual ventilation 
was observed in both seasons. 
In the heating season the classrooms complied with the acceptable range for RH of 
25-60% in almost 100% of the occupied time, while acceptable RH occurred in the 
non-heating season during 91, 60 and 72% with balanced mechanical, automatic 
natural and manual natural ventilation. 
Few problems with thermal comfort and mould growth may be suspected when the 
RH is between 30-70% [9]. Most people prefer if the RH is between 35-60%, but the 
Asthma Allergy Association recommends that RH should be kept below 45%[9]. The 
relative humidity was within 30-70% in 71, 88 and 98% of the occupied time in the 
heating season and 100, 97 and 99% in the non-heating season with balanced 
mechanical, automatic natural and manual natural ventilation. Only few hours with a 
RH below 30% occurred in the heating season. However, RH was still within the 
acceptable range of 25-60%, so no significant risk related with the air humidity 
seemed to occur in the schools in this study. 
In the classrooms at Ishøj Skole the RH was below 25% in 10-30% of the occupied 
time in the heating season. In one classroom at Højdevangens skole the RH was below 
25% in ~ 45% of the occupied time. In these cases it could be rather uncomfortable 
for the pupils to be in the classrooms, especially for specially sensitive pupils. Too dry 
air can cause a sensation of dryness and irritation of skin, eyes and nose/throat, while 
high air humidity can cause growth of mould, fungi and even house dust mites, which 
can exacerbate asthma and allergy incidences and cause malodours [9]. 
In the heating season the illuminance level was below 300 lux in ~ 55% of the 
occupied time for the four measured locations, while the illuminance level was below 
300 lux in ~ 35% of the occupied time in the non-heating season.  
The measurements in the heating season were mostly affected by the artificial light, 
while the measurements in the non-heating season were affected by direct solar 
==PAGE:137==
 
Page 136 of 160 
 
radiation. This had to be taken into account when comparing the lighting conditions 
between season, together with the orientation. The results showed that positions as 
RF and RB, furthest away from the windows, in general had illuminance levels below 
the minimum level of 300 lux, especially in the heating season. 
The recommendations from the  Danish Working Environment Authority state that 
prolonged exposure to noise of 90 dB(A) or higher can cause hearing damages almost 
three times as severe as at 85 dB(A). No SPL above 90 dB(A) was recorded in this 
study. However, values above 85 dB(A) were experienced in 9. grade in the heating 
season and in 3. and 6. grade in the non-heating season, which could be rather critical 
levels for longer exposure, but this was fortunately not the case in this study. 
A significant difference in the RT measurements was found between RT20 and RT30, 
and RT30 was desired to be used in this study.  
The results of the RT30 measurements showed that great variations occurred when 
comparing the schools. Values above 0.6 sec and below 0.4 sec occurred, which can 
be critical for the pupils learning ability. It was experienced that in classrooms 
without sound absorbing ceiling material the RT30 was generally too high. 
Most criteria for the indoor environment are based on average (adult) persons. We 
all perceive indoor parameters individually, and some humans are more sensitive 
than others. It can therefore be difficult to satisfy everybody by complying only with 
the standards [Kjeldsen et al. 2013 & Toftum et al. 2011]. 
Selection of schools and classrooms 
The participating schools and classrooms were selected at an earlier stage of the 
project and it can be discussed if the chosen schools, in general represent Danish 
primary schools. Four schools were located in Ishøj and two in Hvidovre. Schools in 
the center of Greater Copenhagen would have been preferred, due to the noisy 
surroundings. As noisy surroundings may prevent users from opening windows 
during the school day. Also, more classrooms with exhaust ventilation would have 
been preferable, since this ventilation type was omitted from the non-heating season 
measurements. As mentioned one school declined to participate in the non-heating 
season measurements in this study, leaving ten schools in the heating and nine 
schools in the non-heating season. Therefore the overall distribution of the ventilation 
type on schools differed between seasons. Before The Pilot Phase could begin in 
December 2015 many other schools were contacted, but declined to participate. 
Reasons for declining are not known, but, Larsen and Andreasen (2016) suggested 
that many of the schools already knew about their poor indoor conditions and did not 
want this knowledge to become more visible or to be held responsible for the outcome 
of the findings. 
The schools typologies were defined based solely on the construction year, but six 
schools were renovated and thereby changed characteristics. This classification 
approach may therefore not have been proper since there is no guarantee that the 
typologies assigned to the school buildings corresponded to the given definitions, due 
to the renovations. 
==PAGE:138==
 
 
Page 137 of 160 
 
The typologies and class levels were not evenly distributed, within and between both 
seasons. Some schools covered more than one typology, due to different generations 
of school buildings. Typologies were studied, but only on classroom level.  
Regarding the performed measurements in this study one classroom measured in the 
heating season at Kirkebjerg skole could not be measured due to change of use. A 
similar classroom was then chosen to make a fair comparison, with the same size, 
layout and orientation. 
In addition it can also be discussed if it is fair to compare classrooms where the same 
class was not present in both seasons, but the focus was on studying the building 
characteristics and not the user behavior.  
Source of errors  
The quality of the equipment for the measurement of SPL was poor, especially the 
Elma DT 173 as mentioned previously. The recorded SPL´s were not reliable when the 
measurements were below 40 dB(A). The Elmas were in some cases measuring below 
20 dB(A) at night, while the PeakTechs had its minimum at ~60 dB(A) at night. 
Below 40 dB(A) corresponds to the SPL at a library, so if the pupils were reading in 
the classrooms during the occupied hours this was not taken into account in the 
results. Some SPL results were based on few data, either because the equipment was 
unplugged  from the power supply or the battery level was poor. In other cases no 
data at all was saved, even though the equipment was plugged in to the power supply 
or if the battery was changed before going to a new school. In general better and more 
trustworthy equipment for sound measurements will be needed to validate the 
results and give a fair evaluation of the SPL´s in classrooms. 
The reliability of the measurements made with HOBO state loggers can also be 
discussed, since low illuminance levels were observed in the occupied hours. These 
loggers were placed at the pupils tables and could therefore easily be affected for 
longer time periods, for example with a book or other items used by the pupils. 
Illuminance levels below 70 lux were removed from the data analysis. This limit was 
chosen based on the lowest measured illuminance level added with the highest 
correction coefficient. Books and laptops could have shaded the HOBO loggers for 
shorter periods. These periods were not removed from the data set, because the small 
decreases did not influence the aggregated illuminance levels significantly. 
Outdoor weather conditions 
In the non-heating season measurements were carried out into the transition to the 
heating season. This could affect the results of the indoor environment measurements 
in the classrooms. Højdevangens skole, Kirkebjerg skole and Ishøj Skole were 
measured late September (from 21/9 to 3/10), which were at the very beginning of 
October. 
The user behavior could influence the late measurements in September especially the 
CO2 concentrations and temperatures in the classrooms, due to the opening of 
windows. However, no larger difference in the CO2 concentrations in the three schools 
==PAGE:139==
 
Page 138 of 160 
 
was observed. Furthermore at Højdevangens skole and Kirkebjerg skole, the indoor 
temperature was lower compared to the other participating schools. Højdevangens 
skole had the lowest number of mean sun hours, which seemed to have had an 
influence on the results. Fortunately, temperatures below 18°C were not recorded at 
Højdevangens skole or at Kirkebjerg skole. 
Højdevangens skole, Kirkebjerg skole and Ishøj Skole had a lower median RH than the 
other schools, which could indicate that these schools were visited during the 
transition to the heating season. The ventilation type, pupils and user behavior have 
a higher influence on the CO2 concentration, temperature and RH compared to the 
outdoor conditions. 
Recommendations 
Different technological solution can be installed in classrooms to improve the IEQ. 
Bakó-Biró et al. 2012 suggested that CO2 concentration, temperature and RH monitors 
should be installed in classrooms in general. This will give the pupils and teachers the 
opportunities to improve their indoor environment in classrooms. 
Investigations have shown that it is quite important to visualize the indoor conditions 
to affect and change the pupils and teacher user behavior [Wargocki & Da Silva, 2015]. 
This could e.g. be by using “the traffic light” to indicate CO2 concentrations or the “Ear” 
to indicate when the SPL is too high. A test made by Sundhedsmagasinet and DTU 
showed that pupils performed 11% better in the week when the traffic light was 
installed and they were made aware of, when to open the windows or even leave the 
classrooms for a small break [17]. 
It is remarkable that even small factors can impact pupils performance and it 
questions why classrooms, especially in old school buildings, are not equipped with 
visualization tools like this. This is an inexpensive solution, compared to larger 
retrofits and the user behavior would be affected in a positive direction.  
The most common applied initiatives regarding school renovation focus on improving 
energy efficiency. The municipalities focus is in general on financial initiatives and not 
as much on improving the indoor environment. In order to improve the air quality in 
the long run more durable solutions through larger renovations should be 
implemented.

CONCLUSION 
 
 
==PAGE:142==
 
 
Page 141 of 160 
 
In this study, the CO2 concentration, temperature, relative humidity, sound pressure 
level and illuminance were measured in the non-heating season from August to 
October in nine Danish primary schools. Together with matching results from the 
heating season, these parameters were investigated to study the seasonal difference 
in the classroom IEQ and potential associations between the IEQ and observed 
building characteristics. 
Of the nine primary schools, 82 classrooms participated in this study. The results 
provide an overview of the indoor environment in the Danish primary schools, both 
for the heating and non-heating season. The measured indoor environmental 
parameters varied both considerably within and between classrooms and between 
seasons. The heating season was especially critical for the indoor environment in the 
classrooms and thereby the pupils health and performance levels.   
Of the investigated parameters the air quality constituted the main problem. The 
percentage of occupied time with CO2 concentrations below 1000 ppm was less in the 
heating season than in the non-heating season. The ventilation type had a significant 
impact on the CO2 concentration in the classrooms. Classrooms with balanced 
mechanical ventilation typically had better air quality in both seasons. In 95% and 
66% of the occupied time occurred CO2 concentrations below 1000 ppm, for the non-
heating and heating season respectively in these classrooms. As a consequence, the 
worst air quality was observed in classrooms with manual natural ventilation in the 
heating season, where CO2 concentrations below 1000 ppm only occurred in ~26% 
of the occupied time. Older schools typically had manual natural ventilation, while the 
new schools had balanced mechanical ventilation. 
Concentrations above 2000 ppm occurred in ∿21% of the occupied time in the 
heating season in classrooms with manual natural ventilation. In only 1% of the 
occupied time this was the case in classrooms with manual natural ventilation in the 
non-heating season, confirming that windows were opened more frequently in the 
non-heating season. 
The temperature was mostly in the acceptable range in the heating season compared 
to the non-heating season, but generally, high temperatures occurred in both seasons. 
In the non-heating season temperatures above 30°C were mostly observed in 
classrooms facing south and southwest. It was assumed that user behavior had an 
impact on the registered temperatures, since the highest temperatures were 
observed in classrooms with manual natural ventilation. However, recordings of 
window opening behavior were not analyzed in this study.  
Generally, relative humidity was in the recommended range of 25-60% so no 
significant risk of problems with thermal comfort and mould growth related with the 
air humidity seemed to occur in the participating classrooms. However, in the non-
heating season the relative humidity was above 60% in ~30 and 40 % of the occupied 
time for classrooms with manual and automatic natural ventilation respectively, but 
it never exceeded 75%.   
==PAGE:143==
 
Page 142 of 160 
 
The measurements of the sound pressure levels were rather uncertain. Sound 
pressure levels above 85 dB(A) were observed, in both seasons and in different class 
levels and schools. These sound pressure levels did not occur in more than ~1% of 
the occupied time. The reverberation time measured in the heating season was below 
the requirements of 0.6 sec in classrooms with sound absorbing ceiling material. 
Rooms without sound absorbing ceiling material had reverberation times above the 
threshold. 
The illuminance levels were below 300 lux in ~55% of the occupied time during the 
heating season. This occurred in ~35% of the occupied time in the non-heating 
season. Especially the locations furthest away from the windows had problems with 
maintaining illuminance levels of 300 lux in the heating season. In the non-heating 
season, the positions closest to the windows were rather affected by direct solar 
radiation and problems with glare could therefore occur. 
The measurements were conducted to investigate how the different schools complied 
with current indoor environment recommendations during both seasons. The 
strength of this study is that the measurements were performed for a longer time 
period than in several earlier studies and in both the heating and non-heating season. 
The results provide a description of the IEQ in Danish primary schools. The results 
showed that ventilation type, orientation of classrooms, outdoor conditions and the 
number of pupils present had an influence on the indoor environment. 
This study supports already existing results of the IEQ in Danish primary schools, due 
to the fairly large sample size and thereby the results may be representative for 
schools with comparable characteristics.

==PAGE:146==
 
 
Page 145 of 160 
 
Further analyses 
Suggestions for further work concerning the data from the heating and non-heating 
season for the nine participating schools are listed below.  
 The data for the user behavior was recorded and collected at each school, 
but was not analyzed in this study. The data concerned window and door 
opening and the use of artificial light. Associations between measured 
parameters and occupant behavior should be examined for systematic 
patterns concerning the seasonal variation in occupant behavior. 
 The teachers could overrule the automatic opening of windows in 
classrooms with natural ventilation, since discomfort due to draught 
could occur. It could therefore be relevant to study the interactions 
between occupants and the automatically controlled systems and see how 
it affected the CO2 concentrations. 
 Classroom temperature, rather than poor IAQ, is most likely the main 
reason why windows are opened by pupils or the teachers in schools [Gao 
et al. 2014]. I would be desired to investigate if this was the case for both 
seasons. 
 Both window opening and use of artificial light should be studied in 
classrooms where the same class had been present in both season.  
 Compare the IEQ in public and private schools, since it is assumed that the 
private schools might have better resources to ensure good indoor 
environment conditions for the pupils. 
 It is known that pupils in classrooms with mechanical ventilation 
performed better in the national tests compared to pupils in classrooms 
without [Toftum et al. 2015]. With the detailed knowledge of ventilation 
type in the nine schools it could be interesting to study the National Tests 
to see if this was the case. 
 
 
==PAGE:147==
 
Page 146 of 160 
 
Further studies 
Transition seasons should be studied to see if other conclusion may appear. This 
would require more measurements, especially to cover the spring season. Taking the 
transition between the four seasons into account could perhaps create a better basis 
for recommendations of the indoor environment and energy needs, across seasons. 
The widespread use of whiteboards sets new requirements to the lighting conditions 
in classrooms. Different artificial light systems and zones should be studied for 
different classrooms. 
Data should be collected before and after school renovations e.g. in a large database 
for further use in renovation initiatives. The database could be used for development 
of design guidelines for optimization of energy consumption and in particular to 
suggest efficient renovation methods for school buildings. 
The benefit of this database would be that municipalities and schools could share 
experiences of different renovation projects and thereby make it easier to navigate 
through the right renovation initiatives for the specific school. Both public and private 
schools could be added to this database. 
Investigations have also stated that pupils in lower secondary education (7. to 9. 
grade), especially the girls are affected by the poor indoor environment [Toftum et al. 
2011]. It could be desirable to further examine the impact of poor indoor 
environments on pupils across class levels, gender and particularly socioeconomic 
status.
