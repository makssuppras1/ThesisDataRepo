| Abstract
As the pharmaceutical industry investigates the possibility of continuous manufacturing,
the manufacturing processes will increasingly rely on process analytical tools, such as
spectroscopic methods and in-line particle size measurements.
It is well established that the particle size distribution is an important parameter when
producing solid dosage forms. This study aims to show that it is possible to represen-
tatively sample in-line of a drop stream to measure particle size distribution. However,
in order to have representative sampling the instrument range and sampling techniques
must be well understood.
For this study a Malvern Insitec was used for in-line sampling. The Insitec measurements
were veriﬁed by oﬀ-line measurements from a Malvern Mastersizer 3000. The Theory of
Sampling was used to minimized the errors related to the sampling of solids.
The operation range of the Insitec had to be established ﬁrst. The Insitec showed good
robustness and repeatability on both glass beads and microcrystalline cellulose.
The
particle size measurements were aﬀected by air ﬂow and obscurity, but suitable operation
parameters were found.
Based on these parameters it was possible to make experimental setups where the viola-
tion of the Theory of Sampling was reduced, and representative sampling was achieved.
Collection probes with diﬀerent number of holes and sizes showed representative sampling
when placed correctly within the dropstream.
These results suggests that the Insitec can be applied as an in-line measurement of the
particle size distribution, if the collection probe is placed correctly, as the equipment is
both robust and reliable.

1
| Introduction
To this day, pharmaceutical manufacturing has mainly been carried out as a batch process.
However, as FDA and other regulatory agencies have encouraged the use of continuous
manufacturing, more pharmaceutical companies have investigated the possibility of con-
tinuous production. This has led to an increase in the interest in continuous manufacturing
and process analytical tools to monitor production lines. In the production of tablets the
particle size distribution of powder blends is an important powder characteristic. The
particle size can be determined by many methods but most common is the use of laser
diﬀraction.
In the following a Malvern Insitec will be tested for applicability of in-line sampling of
powders. This will cover representative sampling from a drop stream and the operation
of the Insitec compared to Malvern Mastersizer, which is an oﬄine analyser.
This is
important as a correct measurement requires correctly sampling and an understanding of
the applied technology.

2
| Literature review
This section will cover a literature review of the need for continuous manufacturing,
key unit operations in the formulation of pharmaceuticals, particle size analyzers and
representative sampling of bulk solids.
2.1
| Why continuous manufacturing
Batch manufacturing is the most common method of production in the manufacturing
of pharmaceuticals. It is therefore regarded as a trusted approach in development and
manufacturing of pharmaceuticals.
Batch processing is more simple to setup for new
products as there is more data and experience on how to setup and run batch manufac-
turing.[McLaughlin, 2019] In batch manufacturing each unit operation is separated. This
is convenient in the manufacturing of pharmaceuticals as this naturally produces batches,
also known as lots, between each unit operation. The product quality can therefore be
tested and veriﬁed between each unit operation and for every batch.[Chatterjee, 2012]
There are, however, several drawbacks of batch manufacturing.
The time to market,
which is the time from ﬁrst production step until ﬁnished product, can vary from days
to months depending on the time for analysis, transport and release of the product. If
the release of a batch is slowed down by quality control or handling of deviations, the
entire production schedule needs to be changed. This can result in an extended downtime,
while there is a potential risk of product degradation.[Han et al., 2013] [Chatterjee, 2012]
Furthermore, equipment must be cleaned and prepared between each batch. This is time
consuming and leads to downtime of the equipment. In older production lines parts of the
cleaning and preparation are often carried out manually by an operator. This increases
the risk of compliance failures. [Lee et al., 2015]
In recent years, both the pharmaceutical industry and regulatory agencies, such as FDA
and EMA, have been encouraging a change from batch to continuous manufacturing.
[McLaughlin, 2019] [Chatterjee, 2012] Continuous manufacturing is an end-to-end method
of production in a single, uninterrupted production line. Continuous manufacturing is a
more agile, ﬂexible and robust production method.[Lee et al., 2015] Ideally, a continuous
process only stops for cleaning and maintenance. The total production time is reduced as
the holding time between production steps can be eliminated. Downtime of the equipment
is also reduced due to less cleaning and preparation needed. The direct operator inter-
ference will be reduced thus reducing the risk of contamination or mistakes. [Chatterjee,
2012] While the initial cost of setting up a continuous production line is higher, it can
reduce the total cost over time or ensure a better product quality. Switching from batch
to continuous manufacturing is time consuming and diﬃcult. It is therefore rarely feasi-
2
==PAGE:14==
ble for already marketed products with an established and eﬀective production process.
[Chatterjee, 2012] The transition from batch to continuous manufacturing is therefore
more likely to occur for new products and production lines.
In continuous manufacturing there are no natural stops which can be used to deﬁne the
lots. Historically, the lack of a deﬁnition of a batch has been an issue for continuous
manufacturing of pharmaceuticals. This issue can be resolved by using residence time
distribution (RTD). RTD is the probability distribution of time in which a material will
be inside a unit operation. The RTD can be obtained for each unit operation and for
the whole production process. This makes it possible to follow powder through the entire
process and make adjustments where needed. However, if there are any changes to the
parameters used to the determine the RTD, such as big variations in raw material proper-
ties, such as ﬂow behaviour, particle size, this may aﬀect the RTD rendering it no longer
valid. The lots are no longer representative of the process.[Engisch and Muzzio, 2016]
Monitoring the process parameters in continuous production is vital as this ensures that
the process is in control and therefore ensures the product quality. The monitoring is
preferred to be automated.[McLaughlin, 2019] If monitoring is automated, feedback con-
trol is commonly used to ensure the systems stay in control.[Chatterjee, 2012] Automated
systems instead alert an operator in case of fouling or other issues in the production. To
reduce challenges during development, scale-up and production it is important to apply
Quality by Design (QbD).
QbD is a framework based on thorough understanding of material properties and the
process parameters. QbD includes risk assessment, design of experiment (DoE), critical
quality attributes (CQA) and critical process parameters (CPP). During development the
CQA and the respective CPP are identiﬁed. The CPPs are then used to determine the
setpoints and warning limits to ensure the process is in control. A process is in control if
it stays within the predeﬁned warning limits of a process. [Zhang and Mao, 2016] Process
analytical technologies (PATs) can monitor and prove that the process was in control
during production.
A drawback of automated systems is the large increase in data collection, which must
be stored, processed and understood. If QbD is applied successfully, more data will be
collected, but due to automated processes the data can be analyzed automatically. The
workload is therefore reduced. [Chatterjee, 2012] An example of a continuous tablet pro-
duction line can be seen in ﬁgure 1. The system uses PATs to monitor the process and
provide a feedback control to the process.

2.2
| Roller compaction
Roller compaction can be used to increase the ﬂowability of a powder, control the particle
size, or avoid segregation. Continuous granulation in pharmaceutical manufacturing is
often done by using a combination of roll compaction and a milling step after.
Roll
compaction can be done as both dry compaction and wet compaction. In dry compaction
the compression is done without addition of a liquid. Dry granulation is often preferred in
manufacturing of pharmaceuticals as many API-mixtures are sensitive to heat, moisture
and have a poor ﬂowability.[Gerteis, a]
Roller compaction works by compressing the powder between two counter rotating rolls.
These are often fed by a feeding screw. The rolls force the powder to compress. When
powder is compressed by a roll compactor the particles will adhere to each other and
therefore increase the particle size. As the powder deforms, the particles will bond together
and form ribbons. After pressure is applied, the ribbon will drop into a milling setup.
The milling determines the granule size distribution. After the ribbons have been reduced
in particle size, they are referred to as granules.[Khorasani et al., 2016] In practice the
milling and roller compaction steps are carried out in the same equipment.[Gerteis, a] An
schematic of a roll compactor can be seen in ﬁgure 3.
4
==PAGE:16==
Figure 2: A Schematic overview of a Gerteis roller compactor. (1) Inlet funnel with agitator,
(2) Feed auger, (3) Tamp auger, (4) Small quantity inlet funnel, (5) Press rollers
with ribbon, (6) Rotor with desired granules.[Gerteis, b]
The ﬁnal particle size distribution of the granules is dependent on the parameters of the
roll compaction.[Khorasani et al., 2016] The ribbons are aﬀected by the process properties,
such as gap width, the roll pressure and roll speed, and the raw material properties, such as
the particle size and shape.[Wu and Sun, 2007] The ribbon composition can be monitored
by Near-Infrared Spectrometer (NIR).[Khorasani et al., 2016]
The granule size distribution is a signiﬁcant parameter in the following processes as it
aﬀects the compact strength and the dissolution proﬁle. These parameters are key in the
ﬁnal product. The most important granulate characteristic, with regards to particle size,
in the pharmaceutical industry is the D50, which is the particle size at 50% quantile. The
granule size distribution is greatly aﬀected by three key parameters. These are the true
density of the raw material, compaction force and gap width during roller compaction.
The particle size of the ribbons increases if the compaction force is increased, whereas the
particle size of the ribbons will decrease as the gap width between the rolls are increased.
However, each powder composition is aﬀected diﬀerently by these parameters. The eﬀect
of these parameters on the granule size distribution needs to be determined individually
for each powder blend.[Kazemi et al., 2017]
2.3
| Tablet compression
Compaction of powder into a tablet is a fundamental operation in manufacturing of phar-
maceuticals.[Sun et al., 2009] Below in ﬁgure 3 a schematic of an industrial tablet press
can be seen. A tablet press works by ﬁlling powder into the dies of the tablet press. The
powder is then compressed by pistons before being ejected out of die.
5
==PAGE:17==
Figure 3: Schematic overview of an industrial tablet press.[Shanghaimachinery]
Both the powder properties, handling and processing of the powder will aﬀect the qual-
ity of the tablet. When discussing the tablet quality, the eﬀects are often divided into
equipment inﬂuence and powder properties. However, these are often indivisible as one
will aﬀect the other. The quality of a tablet can be quantiﬁed by many parameters. This
could be the tensile strength, the uniformity of the content or the dissolution proﬁle. All
these are inﬂuenced by the properties of the powder used to compress the tablet. A lack of
control in the tabletting process can lead to weak tablets or tablets with varying content
of API.[Cabiscol et al., 2020] Parameters related to the equipment are the compression
force and punch velocity as well as the ejection force. An example of this could be uneven
edges of the tablet if the ejection force is too high. This will result in a weaker tablet.[Sun
and Grant, 2001]
As the size of the particles decrease, the cohesion between the particles will increase.
[McKenna and McCaﬀerty, 1982] Cohesion is a common term used to describe frictional
forces, mechanical interlock, inter-particular forces and liquid bridging.[TimFreeman] As
smaller particles tend to pack more eﬃciently the density of the tablet will increase.[McKenna
and McCaﬀerty, 1982] Monitoring and controlling of the particle size distribution of gran-
ules and powder blends prior to tabletting is therefore important in both batch and
continuous manufacturing.

2.4
| Particle characteristics
2.4.1
| Particle size and shape
The particle size distribution is an important parameter in both roll compaction and
tablet compression. Several techniques have been developed to investigate and measure
the size of a particle. Traditionally, sieving analysis and laser diﬀraction have been used
for oﬀ-line measurements. Today most particle size distributions are measured by laser
diﬀraction as this is a fast and simple measurement.[Silva et al., 2013] Laser diﬀraction
can also be used for in-line measurements. Many of these techniques have an assumption
about the shape of the particle. For laser diﬀraction the particles are assumed to be
spherical, which is often not the case.[Silva et al., 2013]
The assumptions are made to simplify and speed up the calculations.
However, this
assumption about the particle shape is dependent on the particle size, as smaller parti-
cles are more spherical, whereas larger particles tend to be more angular.[McKenna and
McCaﬀerty, 1982] The assumption of spherical particles can be partly checked by a mor-
phology analysis of the particles. The simplest measurement is a close up picture of the
particles. This can give an estimation of how spherical the particles are.[James and Ender,
2000]
2.4.2
| Laser diﬀraction
Particle size measurements by laser diﬀraction are preformed by measuring the scattered
light from a laser beam as the beam passes through and around a dispersed particle. This
can be seen in ﬁgure 4. Small particles will scatter light at large angles, relative to the
laser beam, whereas large particles will scatter light at small angles.[Boer et al., 1981]
When the angular scatter intensity has been obtained, the particle size can be estimated.
Two theories are often applied to determine the particle size from laser diﬀraction. These
are the Mie theory, developed by Gustav Mie, and the Fraunhofer principle, named after
Joseph von Fraunhofer although he did not develop the method. [Weisstein] Both theo-
ries are based on Maxwell’s equations for an electromagnetic ﬁeld and result in an exact
description of the ﬁeld when an interaction takes place. [Boer et al., 1981]
7
==PAGE:19==
Figure 4: Interaction between the laser beam and the particle of Mie’s theory. [Horiba]
The Fraunhofer theory is based on a number of assumptions. The theory assumes that
particles are opaque disks, light only scatters at narrow angles and the light scatter is in-
dependent of the particle size. The Fraunhofer theory is therefore a better approximation
for unknown particles as it does not consider the optical properties of the measured ma-
terial.[Boer et al., 1981] Mie’s theory can be applied to predict the light scatter regardless
of the particle being transparent or opaque. [MalvernPanalytical, a] The optical proper-
ties of the medium and the particles must be known. The particles are also assumed to
be uniform in composition. Today, the ISO13320, a standard used for determination of
particle size by laser diﬀraction, recognizes the Mie theory as superior.[Silva et al., 2013]
One of the major drawbacks of laser diﬀraction is the lack of information about the particle
composition and morphology as laser diﬀraction does not provide any information about
this.[Silva et al., 2013]

The transmission and obscurity is a measure of the same thing. Transmission is how
much of the laser is observed on the lens. A laser diﬀraction will need to have a trans-
mission below 100% before it starts to measure. The correlation between obscurity and
transmission is found by the following equation:

2.6
| Theory of sampling
The importance of representative sampling can not be underestimated as non-representative
sampling can lead to wrong conclusions of the data. As more processes are automated,
more data will need to be collected to control the processes. The statistical principles
of random sampling are well known and applied in other manufacturing industry. This
method is extremely eﬀective if the individual units or lots can be identiﬁed. This gives
assurance that the sampling process is unbiased. In traditional statistical sampling it is
assumed that every individual from a population has the same probability of being within
the sample. Theory of Sampling (TOS) was developed by Pierre Gy, who recognized the
lack of correctly sampling bulk solids in the mining industry. TOS is a practical frame-
work used to reduce or eliminate sampling errors of solid samples. The objective of TOS
is to carry out representative sampling with minimal complexity. To achieve represen-
tative sampling, both the sampling techniques and the manufacturing process must be
well understood. A lack in either will result in undesired variance or a non-heterogeneous
sampling. An estimation of the Measurement Uncertainty (MU) can only be applied when
the signiﬁcant sampling errors have been addressed. When accuracy and precision of a
measurement have been established both solid and solid/liquid phase materials can be
sampled representative.[DanskStandard]
2.6.1
| Sampling of solids
For bulk materials it is not possible to identify every individual particle. Another approach
is therefore needed to correctly sample bulk solids. There are two key requirements where
TOS diﬀers from the statistical principles of random sampling.
The ﬁrst is sampling
correctness. First, instead of individual particles it is assumed that equal sized portions
of the lot have the same probability to be in the sample. The second requirement is
related to the handling of samples. The integrity of the sample must be preserved from
the sample is collected until the sample is analyzed.[Augsburger and Hoag, 2008]
A solid sample can be obtained in many ways. A grab sample is the simplest sample of a
bulk solid. Here a portion of the bulk is obtained. However, if the sample is taken from a
big bag, the particles from the bottom of the big bag have zero probability of being in the
sample. It is therefore a violation of the ﬁrst principle. Another method of sampling is
to use hand samplers. These are diﬃcult to use and the speed of the sampler often varies
through the falling stream. One side of the stream is therefore favored over other sides
of the stream. Furthermore, a probe collecting material in a pipe cannot obtain a correct
sample of the ensured stream. All these situations violate the ﬁrst principle. An example
for violation of the second requirement, could be if the bulk interacts with moisture from
the container.[Augsburger and Hoag, 2008]
14
==PAGE:26==
In order to understand how a lot or sample should be taken, the dimension of the sam-
ple must be determined.
Classical statistical sampling will often be zero-dimensional
according to Gy.[Augsburger and Hoag, 2008] Here all individuals can be identiﬁed. In
sampling of bulk solids, the sampling will often be three-dimensional lots. In theory, a
three-dimensional lot can be correctly sampled. However, a random sample of a three-
dimensional lot cannot be achieved in practice. This is because it is not possible to sample
the middle of bulk without alternating the surrounding material when moving through
the sample. The dimensions must therefore be reduced.[Augsburger and Hoag, 2008]
The drop stream can be reduced to a one dimensional lot by taking a slice of the stream.
By doing this the height and depth dimensions can be removed. The only remaining
dimension is the length. However, the length can be randomized and thus this method
upholds the principles. This poses some technical issues as it can be diﬃcult to take
the sample in a randomized length. A sample thief is often used as it can take several
samples in a dimensional plane. However, as the spear moves though the bulk there is a
risk that the particles are aﬀected and thus the sample is not representative. Furthermore,
a thief cannot take samples from the point of the spear. There is therefore not an equal
probability between each of the lots.[Augsburger and Hoag, 2008]
A correct way of sampling a dropstream is a one-dimensional slice is with a cutter. A
cutter rotates around an axis. It is therefore a great tool to collect samples from a drop-
stream. A schematic of a cutter can been seen in ﬁgure 10 B. In order to compensate for
the diﬀerence rotational speed, the cutter will move faster towards the edge of the pipe
compared to the middle of the pipe. The shape of the cutter must be a wedge.[Augsburger
and Hoag, 2008]
Figure 10: (A) Correct one-dimensional sampling of a process stream. The sample will include
the entire width and depth of the stream. (B) A wedge-shaped cutter is the correct
way to do rotational sampling. [Augsburger and Hoag, 2008]

2.6.2
| Sampling errors
In Gy’s Theory there are seven diﬀerent errors. These are split into errors and funda-
mental errors. An example of a fundamental error could be the natural variance in a raw
material. By understanding the diﬀerent types of error the total error can be reduced or
eliminated.[Augsburger and Hoag, 2008]
One type of error is related to the material itself. These errors, or variations, are impossible
to avoid as no material is completely homogeneous. This is because particles are irregular
and vary in size, shape and density. By optimizing the production process of the raw
materials this can be reduced. However, it cannot be eliminated. This error is referred to
as a fundamental error.[Augsburger and Hoag, 2008]
The second error is related to the homogeneity of the powder, as particles can segregate.
If a result is obtained from a largely segregated sample, it is not representative of the
bulk. As with the fundamental error this cannot be eliminated, but it can be reduced by
proper mixing.[Augsburger and Hoag, 2008]
Errors which are related to not following the principles of correct sampling are divided
into three types of errors. Delimitation error, extraction error, and preparation error. The
delimitation error is a result of not deﬁning the portions of the lot correctly. An example
of this could be incorrect determination of the sampling dimension. The extraction error
occurs if the desired material is not extracted from the lot. This error often occurs if the
sampling tool is not correctly designed or incorrectly used. Preparation errors are related
to issues with handling the sample.[Augsburger and Hoag, 2008]
The last two errors are related to non-random ﬂuctuation of the sampling. These are
grouped into non-periodic heterogeneity and long-range periodic heterogeneity. A non-
periodic error could be a change in the mixing properties of a stream. In continuous
manufacturing, changes in the degree of mixing can occur. [Gao et al., 2012] This could
be caused by diﬀerent batches of raw material. Long-range periodic changes could be
caused by shifts in the operators running the equipment. This happens roughly three
times a day in continuous manufacturing.
This can cause periodic oscillations in the
obtained data.[Augsburger and Hoag, 2008]
2.6.3
| Sample frequency and mode
The sampling frequency can be determined by regulatory demands or standards set for the
process. Both the frequency and mode must be determined in order to have representative
sampling over space or time. Oversampling is time consuming and expensive. This leads
to an ineﬀective process as natural ﬂuctuations could be viewed as changes in the process,
causing unnecessary corrections or disruptions of the production ﬂow. On the other hand,
16
==PAGE:28==
under sampling might fail to detect process changes over time or space. The system is
therefore not in control. Thus this leads to an increase in oﬀ-speciﬁcation products or
lack of compliance. The product must therefore be discarded. The sample frequency is
therefore an important parameter when discussing representative sampling.[Augsburger
and Hoag, 2008]
Sampling of solids is often carried out in one of two ways. One method is systematic
random sampling. Here the initial sample is randomized in time. However, every sample,
after the initial sample, must be taken at the same time in each time interval. An example
could be that the time interval is set to one sample per day. Then the time which the
sample should be taken is randomly selected to be taken at 9 am. Every sample taken
from this point on must therefore be taken at 9 am. This method is very convenient in
manufacturing. However, there is a risk that ﬂuctuations within the time frame is not
detected. There is therefore a risk that the process is only in control during the time of
the measurement.[Augsburger and Hoag, 2008]
Another method is stratiﬁed random sampling. Here, time or space is divided into strata,
which is the period where a sample must be taken. A sample is then randomly taken
within each stratum. This method can test if the system ﬂuctuates within the strata.
However, it might be diﬃcult to compare data as the samples are not uniformly taken in
time or space. There is a risk of uneven sampling and therefore a risk that the system is
not monitored over a longer time period. Stratiﬁed random sampling can be diﬃcult to use
in manufacturing as it is preferred that sampling is scheduled in a production.[Augsburger
and Hoag, 2008]
The time or space interval should be determined based on the sampling technique. A fast
measurement such as NIR can have a short cycle, whereas measurements, which needs to
be analyzed in the laboratory, will have a longer cycle. For continuous manufacturing,
fast measurements such as NIR are preferred as these measure fast and can be used in-
line to control the process. However, there will be ﬂuctuations in the measurements. It
is therefore important that the system does not over adjust over a single measurement,
for example by having a moving averaging over a period of time.[Augsburger and Hoag,
2008]

6.5
| Measurement intervals and handling
As seen in table 14 the PSDs are given in intervals. An example could be that the Insitec
has an interval from 1219µm to 1637µm. The particles can have any size within this
range. All the particles within this range could potentially be either 1220µm or 1637µm,
which will aﬀect the PSD greatly. However, since there is no information about the size
in the interval, it is assumed that all the particles have the size of the upper limit of the
interval. If 9% of the particles are observed in the range, it is therefore assumed that
9% of the particles have a size of 1637µm. This will likely give an overestimation of the
particle size. This method is used for both the Mastersizer and the Insitec.

6.6
| Statistical methods
For the statistical analysis the software Rstudio is used (RStudio Team, Boston, Mas-
sachusetts, USA). Rstudio is an integrated development environment for R. In the scripts
diﬀerent packages are used. In installation guide for these can be found in the scripts.
Matlab (Mathwork, Natick, Massachusetts, USA) version 2018b was used to plot and
explore data. As a standard the conﬁdence interval is set to 95% when data is analysed.
For comparing variance between diﬀerent groups or measurement the ANOVA test was
used.
An ANOVA test assumes that the sample size is the same for each group.
If
this is not the case the ANOVA can not be applied.
In case of uneven sample sizes
the Tukey-Kramer method was applied. This method takes diﬀerent sample sizes into
account. Both the ANOVA and the Tukey-Kramer test assumes that data is normally
distributed. If a histogram is not conclusive a Shapiro-Wilk test is used to determine that
data distribution.
When 95% conﬁdence intervals for the means of two independent populations do not
overlap, there will indeed be a statistically signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the means (at
the 0.05 level of signiﬁcance). However, the opposite is not necessarily true. Conﬁdence
intervals may overlap, yet there may be a statistically signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the
means.[Tan and Tan, 2010]
Linear trends and correlations are tested by a linear regression. In case that multiple
parameters are used in the model, backwards selection is used to reduce non-signiﬁcant
parameters. Each model is analysed by the following methods: residuals versus ﬁtted plot
and quantile-quantile(QQ) plot.
The residuals versus ﬁtted plot investigates non-linearity, unequal error variances, and
outliers. The residual vs ﬁtted must be randomly distributed and with a random pattern.
The QQ plot is used to control the probability distributions of the model. This can be
used to control skrewness of data, such as a control that data is normally distributed.
Linear models of the same data are compared by analysing their sum of residual least
squares (RSS). This analysis is the total distance from each individual observation to the
linear correlation line. A low RSS indicates that the observations are located close to the
linear trend.
When a model is used to predict values, with either a training/test set or new data,
the model and its’ correctness is controlled by evaluating the Root Mean Square Error
(RMSE). This is the average error from the observations to the predicted values.[Keppel,
1993]

To ensure that the Insitec measured correctly the lens needs a good signal from the laser.
If this was not the case a realignment would be needed. The aﬀect of the laser realignment

It appeared that some glass particles would get stuck to the lens and record faulty mea-

cleaned by "ﬂushing" the equipment. This was done by increasing the ﬂow rates of the

cleaned. This was time consuming and there was a risk of unaligning the laser. As the
lens was taken apart it was possible to see a small an amount of glass beads on the lens,
which was likely trapped on the lens or in the measurement cell.
A realignment of the laser could be needed after moving the equipment or cleaning the lens.

was after cleaning of the lens, as the laser signal was not as strong as prior to the cleaning.
The laser was slightly aligned to give a higher intensity. The second adjustment of the
laser was after the Insitec unit was moved.

value, see section 7.2.1. The samples come from the same container and is of the same
distribution. The D50 of each observation was tested against each other for signiﬁcantly

| TOS investigations
In the following section the applicability of the TOS on the obtained results is discussed.
From ﬁgure 66 an updated Ishikawa diagram can be seen. The risks which have been
tested are ticked-oﬀ. Since the equipment was only handled by one operator the eﬀect of
this was not investigated. As the conditions of the environment were constant the eﬀect of
these were not tested either. The methods and parameters used to calculate the particle
size were not investigated.
Figure 66: Updated Ishikawa diagram. The risks which have been investigated are marked. The
rests needs more tests.
An important error in the TOS is the fundamental error of variance in raw material.
In this report this issue was addressed by investigation of the material properties. The
uniformity of the particle size and particle shape of the diﬀerent powders were investigated.
It was observed that most powders were uniform in particle size and shape with a narrow
distribution. However, medium sized glass beads were not uniform between the containers.
The majority of the powders were circular, however the large sized glass beads were not
as circular as the other materials. The variation in these parameters was found to be
small and does not contribute much to the overall measurement uncertainty.
Another fundamental error is the homogeneity of the powder. In this report it was tested
if samples could segregate. It was observed that measurements of unmixed samples were
diﬀerent than mixed samples. This could be caused by two diﬀerent errors. One error
could be the particle segregation, while the other could be an delimitation error as the
samples were taken from the same location. It was possible to reduce this error by mixing
the sample prior to measurement.
76
==PAGE:88==
Diﬀerent feeding systems were also tested for segregation of the samples. This showed
that the feeding systems did not lead to segregation of powder and thus these did not
contribute to the measurement uncertainty.
As the materials were handled and stored in a temperature and humidity controlled area
the eﬀect of these were not investigated. This also means the storing and handling errors
were reduced.
A funnel was used to collect an entire sample for Insitec measurements. As the funnel did
not have a smooth surface there was a risk of small particles adhering to it, and thereby
alternating the sample. This was not observed when measuring on single materials but
there were some irregularities when measuring on mixtures.
More work is needed to
establish the error the funnel contributes to the measurement uncertainty.
Errors related to the instrument were also investigated. Over the course of the experiments
the lens was fouled several times. The equipment can still record data, however, the data is
faulty. This is an error related to incorrect use of the equipment. It is therefore important
to run measurements with suitable air ﬂows as this prevents fouling. In case of fouling
cleaning of the lens should be carried out carefully.
Another issue related to the instrument was the aﬀect of obscurity during the measure-
ment.
This was found to be material dependant.
An incorrect understanding of the
obscurity could lead to an increase in variance. This was only observed on the Insitec
which conﬁrms that the error was related to the equipment and not the theory used to
calculate the particle size.
In this report a major focus was on the errors related to extraction as this is the most
challenging when sampling from a drop stream.
It was possible to sample representative with two diﬀerent sized holes, 4 and 8 mm.
This was tested by measuring an entire drop stream with the funnel and compared it to
measurement of a free-ﬂowing drop steam.
The placement of the probe in the drop steam was tested. If the probe is placed within
the drop stream it can collect representatively, however, if the probe is placed outside of
the drop steam it does not. Using a probe to collect material is a violation of the TOS
but the diﬀerence in D50 was acceptable.

| Conclusion
In this study the particle size distributions of glass beads and MCC were tested. Laser
diﬀraction was used as the main method to calculate the particle size distribution. The
particle size was calculated by using Mie’s theory. This theory has some assumptions
about the particle which it measures.
To verify these assumptions the materials were analysed on the Morphologi 4.
This
analysis showed that the assumption of round particles was good for Prosolve, small and
medium sized glass beads. The large glass beads were more elongated and the assumption
of circulatory was therefore not as accurate for the large sized glass beads. The particle
size measurements were also compared to sieving analysis to further verify the ﬁndings.
The experiments on the Insitec showed that the Insitec provides robust measurements
as it was possible to do both repeatable sampling of the same mean over a number of
experiments as well as over a longer period and realignments of the laser. This ensures
that while the equipment might need to be manually cleaned, there is only a small risk that
it will aﬀect the PSD measurement. Furthermore, the measurement was not dependant
on the type of feeding as both scoop feeding and vibratory feeding was tested.
There were, however, a few settings for which the Insitec provided faulty measurements.
This was observed when the air ﬂow rates of the system was too high, and resulted in a
drastic increase in the D50. A drastic increase in the particle size was also observed if
the lens had been fouled. During sampling from a drop stream it was also observed that
the D50 would approach the reference D50 as isokinetic conditions around the probe hole
was reached.
From this study the optimal ﬂows were determined to be a cell ﬂow of 150 l/min, a venturi
ﬂow of 100 l/min and booster ﬂow of 95 l/min. The reason for the choice of these settings
is that a large diﬀerence between the cell and venturi ﬂow protects the lens from fouling.
The D50 of glass beads measured on the Insitec were not aﬀected by the transmission of
the laser, given that there were enough particles to provide an adequate PSD. However,
it was observed that the D50 of Prosolv was aﬀected by laser obscurity, and it was found
that the transmission, during Prosolv measurements, should be below 90%, and thus the
transmission was kept between 90% and 80% for the measurements. This shows that
there needs to be an individual assessment on diﬀerent material and blends to give an
accurate determination of the particle size.
The feed ﬂow of Prosolv needed to provide a transmission range of 90% to 80% was be-
tween 3 kg/h and 6 kg/h with a single 4 mm hole. It was observed that as the number
of holes or size of the holes increased the feed rate would have to be adjusted accord-
78
==PAGE:90==
ingly. While the laser can handle lower transmissions, it is important not to ﬂood the
measurement cell as this leads to fouling of the lens. The number of holes and size should
therefore be taken into consideration when designing experiments or processes where the
Insitec is used to sample.
In order to correctly measure the PSD in a drop steam, the sampling needs to be repre-
sentative. It was observed that if the probe holes were placed directly in the drop stream
below the vibratory feeder the sampling was representative in any location, within the
drop steam, with any number of holes or hole size. However, it was not possible to cor-
rectly sampling in positions which were not in the drop stream. The placement of the
probe is therefore important to correctly measure the particle size distribution.
A correlation between the oﬄine and inline measurement was observed. This was done
by measurement on powder collected in the same space time. As powder would drop
from the vibratory feeder it was collected and analysed on the Malvern, while the drop
stream was also analysed by the Insitec. It was attempted to collect a sample of the
drop steam after the Insitec measurement. This was a success for the glass beads, but
was not representative for the Prosolv. More work would be needed to correctly use the
Autosampler to collect representative samples.
The analysers measured a diﬀerence of 6 µm on the medium sized glass beads.
The
Prosolv diﬀerence was roughly 13 µm, while the Avicel PH-200 had a diﬀerence of 26 µm.
This suggest that the diﬀerence between the analysers is dependant on the material.
To verify this it was attempted to create a linear model to predict the particle size. As
the model contained groups of particle sizes, and these were diﬀerent materials, it was not
possible to extrapolate a linear model to predict particle size. However, all the data of
the Prosolv, PH-200 and medium sized glass beads were split into a train/test test. From
the training set it was possible to estimate the particle size with a RMSE of 3.6 µm. This
gives conﬁdence can the D50 can be interpolated by a simple linear regression. However
more data will need to be collected.
Overall, representative sampling with the Insitec from a drop stream was achieved and
the data was comparable to the oﬀ-line measurements of the Mastersizer.

8.1
| Future work
The samples used in this project have mainly consisted of a single material. A total of
three diﬀerent sized glass beads and two diﬀerent sized MCC were tested. There needs to
be a further research into measuring a bigger variety of diﬀerent materials, granules and
blends.
It was also observed that the Mastersizer measurement could be aﬀected by where the
sample was collected on the vibratory feeder. This was not proven in this report. However,
the analysis was carried out on a small amount of data. There is therefore a possibility
that there is a diﬀerence, which was not observed due to the small data size.
