Abstract
Formation evaluation through surface gas measurements at a well site has long been
considered unfeasible as their quality is believed to be affected by the drilling process
and acquisition environment. The project seeks to address the uncertainties associated
with gas measurements through a discussion of the technological evolution of the gas
chain for gas extraction from the mud. The mechanism by which gas traps extract gas
from the mud is the primary controlling factor of the quality of data. Factors such as mud
temperature returning in the flow line, and gas trap placement were also found to play
a vital role in the quality of gas data. The uncertainties due to drilling operations were
also studied in order to comprehend the essence by which the gas trapped in the mud is
transported in the borehole. It was identified that gas entering the bottom hole due to
pipe connections had a significant effect on modelling the gas data. The development of
an in-place hydrocarbon reserves gas model through this project has proven the feasibility
of the mud gas data. The model combines the total gas data measured through mud
logging with the rate of penetration of the bit, and the geometry of the borehole. The
gas model considers an amplifying factor for the in-place hydrocarbon reserves that is
optimised using wireline logging results to overcome the uncertainties associated with
the data. A method for hydrocarbon fluid identification and evolution in a well was also
developed from the gas while drilling method developed by Total and Eni. Through
examination of two Maersk Oil wells drilled with water based mud in the Danish North
Sea, the model provided feasible results to mimic the same in-place hydrocarbon reserves
trend observed from wireline logs. The amplifying factor for the results was also found
to be in the correction range suggested for poor gas data acquisition. Moreover, using
specific chromatography ratios from the gas while drilling method, it was found that
fluid evolution in a reservoir can be accurately quantified in comparison to conventional
well logs through the studied wells.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The aim of this project is to quantitatively analyse gas in mud measurements collected
through mud logging, to achieve in situ hydrocarbon reserve estimates. The reserve
estimates are formulated using a mud gas model that utilises both gas and drilling data
measured in real time at a well site. The project also seeks to recreate the main results
of well log analysis, such as fluid type and fluid contact points in the reservoir by the
incorporation of mud gas data. Essentially, the project’s goal is to create profit of all
mud and drilling data to reach viable formation evaluation results.
In order to achieve such quantitative analysis of gas data, one must consider the overall
role of the mud logging unit, and the drilling fluid in drilling operations of a well. Mud
logging has been a crucial part of the drilling process since development in the 1930s,
where its role is ever-expanding in well site operations, as new technology continuously
emerges.
As mentioned, mud logging records and monitors gas levels in the drilling mud, for both
safety and early indication of hydrocarbon presence in the borehole. The gas dissolved
in the mud can provide such details because the drilling mud pumped into the borehole
is in constant contact with the drilled formation. Thus, the gas diffuses out of the for-
mation and into the drilling mud, and through the cuttings produced by the drilling
action. This relates to one of the functions of the drilling fluid where it is used as a
means to clean the borehole of the produced cuttings, by circulation back to the surface.
Hence when the mud is circulated back to the surface, measurements can indicate gas
levels. Although these measurements can infer reservoir fluid properties, it is dependant
on the optimisation of the mud logging unit for mud logs results. Mud logging also en
roles the collection, analysis and interpretation of drill cutting samples. The analysis of
drill cuttings allows for the preliminary interpretation of formation properties such as
porosity and permeability (Petrowiki 2016).
Mud logging also includes maintaining and monitoring of drilling and safety related sens-
ing equipment at a well site. It is utilised for estimation of pore pressure to maintain
hydrostatic equilibrium. This is because the drilling fluid is used as a means of well
control, by exerting hydrostatic pressure in the borehole while drilling to balance out
the formation pressure. Maintaining pressure equilibrium using drilling mud is therefore
important with regards to safety, to avoid the uncontrolled influx of reservoir fluids into
the well bore that may lead to a blow out. The drilling fluid aids in maintaining pressure
equilibrium by the mud logger planning, and assessing the density and composition of
the drilling mud used. This is necessary as the formation pressure increases as a function

However, the density of the mud needs to be carefully altered because if the density
of the mud is too great then it would result in formation breakdown. This leaves mud
to be lost in resultant fractures, hence reducing the exerted hydrostatic pressure. Fur-
thermore, keeping the optimal mud density helps in maintaining borehole stability, by
preventing the collapse of the hole. This is essential during static conditions such as
tripping, where the drill string is pulled out of the hole, or when casing is inserted into
the hole (Petrowiki 2016).Further planning of the composition of the drilling fluid is
necessary depending on the lithology and properties of the drilled geological formations.
Drilling mud can be categorised into two main type: water based mud(WBM), and oil
based mud (OBM). OBM are the preferred types when drilling into formations that are
shale based, to avoid the mechanism ,where shale formations swell due to the introduc-
tion of water to shale formations (Geehan and Mckee 1989).
The versatility of the role of drilling mud in drilling operations can be observed from its
further use to cool and maintain the drill string and bit. This is due to the release of
high amounts of thermal energy as a result of the force of the drilling action, and the
high revolutions per minute of both the drill string and bit. The cooling effect reduces
the friction between the drill string, and the formation during the drilling procedure.
Moreover, the mud acts as a lubricant when new drill pipes or bottom-hole assembly
are added, to be able to manoeuvre through tight angles in the borehole, such as when
drilling horizontal holes or due to swelling shales. Finally, it is also incorporated in the
transmission of hydraulic energy to the bit through the nozzles, to direct the bit to the
planned course (Petrowiki 2016).

CHAPTER 3
The Gas Chain
In order to monitor the amount and composition of gas circulated in the mud, unique
gas extraction and analysis equipment is installed at the surface in a rig site. The nature
and functionality of the gas sensing equipment is pivotal when discussing a quantitative
mud gas model.
In order to comprehend the importance of the efficiency of the gas extraction tools, one
must consider the overall gas chain, and the process by which mud is circulated through
the well.
The gas chain consists of the following components:
• A gas trap installed in the flow line between the bell nipple and the shaker (Figure
3.1).
• A gas line connecting the gas trap to the mud logging unit (Figure 3.1).
• Gas chromatography and total gas analysing equipment in the mud logging unit.
The drilling fluid cycle starts at the suction pit where mud is pumped by a mud pump
through the suction line, and into the borehole. The mud travels through the drill string,
and is circulated back to the surface with cuttings from the bit while being in contact
with the drilled formation. The fluid is then carried at the surface by a flow line from
the bell nipple to the shaker pit.
As the mud travels to the shaker pit, mud is sampled through a gas trap. The sampled
gas is then transported through the gas line to the mud logging cabin for analysis. Prior
to recirculation, the mud passes through the shaker pit where the mud is separated from
the cuttings, and back into the suction pit (Figure 3.1).

3.1
Gas Traps
There are several models of gas traps available in the current market, all with varied
efficiencies. For example, there are standard floating gas traps that are placed in the
header tank as can be seen in figure 3.3. These traps sample an unknown volume of mud
where the volume sampled is dependant on the volume of mud incoming from the flow
line. This mud volume is in turn proportional to the mud flow rate and pump pressure.
The mud is passed through a mechanical agitator that in theory causes the mud to
liberate the contained gas. The released gas is then mixed with air in the degassing
chamber, where it is then transported through the transport line to the mud logging
unit for analysis (Mckinneyy et al. 2007). The efficiency of the conventional gas traps is
said to be between 15-70% dependant on the model of degasser used, and various mud
properties (Pixler 1969).
Figure 3.3: Schematic of a standard gas trap with placement in header tank (Mckinneyy et al. 2007).
The inefficiency and inconsistency of mud sampling using standard gas traps have led
to wide spread prejudice of mud gas data, and have labelled them unfeasible. This is
because standard gas traps measure gas in air rather than gas in mud quantities. How-
ever, independent research projects by both Geoservices and Texaco have led the initial
development of a new generation of gas traps by the early 1990s.
Both research projects have focused on the extraction efficiency of the gas traps by sam-
pling a constant volume of mud through the gas chamber, and by the improvement of
mud agitation techniques. The work carried out by Texaco have led to the modification
of traditional gas traps to give continuous and consistent gas in mud measurements while
drilling. The measurement method uses a highly sensitive, controlled sample gas trap
that provides stable gas extraction over a wide range of operating conditions. Moreover,
==PAGE:24==

3 The Gas Chain
the developed trap can be calibrated for actual gas-in-mud values, resulting in accurate
gas-in-mud measurements that can be used for feasible analysis. This is demonstrated
by the effort to eliminate external factors by totally sealing the trap to gas phase loss,
and to allow the controlled addition of air by the means of a vent port (Wright, Hanson,
and DeLaune 1993).
The GZG degasser formulated by Geoservices represents one of the most widely used
gas sensors (Figure 3.4). It is a constant volume degasser (CVD) coupled with a suction
probe that is mounted close to the bell nipple (Figure 3.5)(Pazzi et al. 1989). The CVD
maintains a constant and known volume of sampled mud, and in turn extracts constant
samples of gas (Mckinneyy et al. 2007). This is achieved by using two volumetric pumps;
the first used for the suction of mud into the degassing chamber, while the second is
for transportation of the air and hydrocarbon gas mixture from the chamber to the gas
detection system in the mud logging unit. The second pump also ensures a constant
ratio between mud and air. Furthermore, the degassing efficiency has been greatly en-
hanced compared to traditional gas traps, by altering the chamber volume and mud
transit time. This increases the mechanical energy per unit mud volume implying better
agitation techniques (Pazzi et al. 1989).
The CVD trap utilises the gas samples by analysis using two parallel flame ionisation
detectors (FID). One of the FIDs is responsible for the measurement of total gas (TG)
measurements as percentage of methane. While the other is responsible for examining
the percentage hydrocarbon components in the gas samples which are separated by chro-
matography.

3.2
FLAIR System
With the progression of drilling technology through out the years and the emphasis of
increasing the drilling rate to cut down costs, the use of a FIDs that derive results in
less than 6 minutes has become an issue in modern drilling. Moreover, as the objec-
tives for exploration shift toward thin pay zones, the limited vertical resolution of FIDs
have posed issues in using gas data for formation evaluation (Breviere, Herzaft, and
Mueller 2002). Thus, the FLAIR system is introduced by Geoservices which consists of
specialised gas chain equipment in the means of a FLEX fluid extractor, an improved
transport line and high speed gas chromatography coupled with a mass spectrometer
(Services and Products 2016).
The FLEX extractor is a further improvement on the CVD gas trap, because of the
built in heating system in the design of the gas trap. This heating system allows for
the mud samples to be heated to a temperature of 80°C. The heating action allows for
heavier C6-C8 components with higher boiling points to be liberated from the mud and
wouldn’t be possible at ambient conditions (Albard et al. 2012). The heating action is of
prime importance when drilling in deep offshore locations, where the temperature of mud
circulated back to the surface is too low to liberate the hydrocarbons trapped (Blanc
et al. 2003). The importance of the elevated temperatures for the specific hydrocarbon
components can be viewed in Figure 3.8, where the concentration of heavy C6-C8 is
directly proportional to the temperature in OBM (Breviere, Herzaft, and Mueller 2002).
The effect of heating WBM prior to gas extraction was suggested by Brumboiu et al.
2005, to improve standard gas trap gas results between a factor of 1.2-45.

Although the development of CVD and FLAIR systems have greatly increased the effi-

ratio of the individual hydrocarbon components upon which much of gas chromatogra-
phy analysis relies (Pinna and Law 2008).
The main drawbacks with the gas traps introduced in this chapter arise due to the fact
that the simple mechanical agitation method used to extract the gas is wholly inade-

Gas While Drilling Method (GWD)
Gas While Drilling (GWD) is a joint research project between Total and Eni for the
development of a method of gas data interpretation. The key ideas of the method were
used as a base line for the development of the fluid type, contacts and uncertainties in
Chapter 6. The interpretation is based on tests on an extensive set of wells that has
led to a simplified catalogue of ratio log and cross plots for formation evaluation (Beda
et al. 1999). A summary of the ratios used for analysis are presented in Figure 4.1, and
are discussed in further details in the subsections of this chapter.
Figure 4.1: Summary of ratios and their use in the GWD method as described by Blanc et al. 2003.
The method utilises PVT, well logs, geochemical and surface gas data to aid in reservoir
characterisation. GWD has demonstrated the potential for enhancing the understanding
and location of hydrocarbon bearing zones, fluid variations and contact, as well as seal
efficiency (Blanc et al. 2003).
GWD uses a systematic approach to combine gas shows with drilling and mud logging
events together with the raw data. The raw gas data incorporated comprises of the total
gas (TG) and the chromatography data from C1 to C5 in ppm (Blanc et al. 2003). The
main work flow of the model is illustrated in Figure 4.2 and is discussed in detail in the
next sections. It is crucial to comprehend that the mud gas data are greatly affected
by gas chain characteristics and drilling events. Therefore, it is mandatory to collect all

4.1
Quality Control
The role and importance of quality control is directly related to the data acquisition
environment and method at the well site. Therefore, it is important that the well site
geologist checks if there are changes in the mud system and drilling conditions (Beda
et al. 1999).
==PAGE:37==
4.1 Quality Control
21
In relation to Beda et al. 1999, Blanc et al. 2003 stressed the importance of quality
control of the mud logging data. The data has to be filtered for special drilling events
such as trip, or connection gas. The addition of pipes can cause connection gas, which
will show as an anomalous high gas peak on the mud log with respect to background
gas levels. The high gas peak is a result of a short influx of gas in the well-bore that
occurs when the mud pumps are stopped; typically appearing after one lag interval on
the mud logs. Similarly, the tripping action on a pipe where the mud pumps are again
shut off and the swabbing effects due to returning the drill string to the surface causes
trip gas (Glossary 2016). Therefore it is important when analysing mud logs to closely
correlate with the mud pump graphs and the well operations summary, to identify in-
tervals of connection or trip gas (Albard et al. 2012). The effect of connection gas is
further discussed in Section 6.3.4.

4.5
Gas Chain Technical Specifications
In order to improve the reliability of the gas data used in the GWD method, techni-
cal specifications for the gas chain have been suggested by Blanc et al. 2003. These
technical specifications and calibration techniques have been mentioned to be the mini-
mum requirements to correctly sample and record the gas data, to avoid external factors
affecting the measurements.
4.5.1
Gas Trap
A CVD or closest equivalent gas trap should be used for mud extraction. The trap
should not be placed downstream of major steps or turbulent zones in the flow line. As
previously mentioned in Section 3.1, the gas trap should be located near the bell-nipple
with consideration to the safety of the equipment and accessibility for maintenance.
Moreover, the sampling rate should be as high as possible, with a specific recommen-
dation of every 20-25cm. Figure 4.6 shows the major effect of sampling every 20cm
compared to every 1m, where certain peaks in the 1m case does not detect certain thin
peaks compared to 20cm.

4.5 Gas Chain Technical Specifications

4.5.2
Gas Line
The gas line requires that there are two lines preferably with continuous purge capacity;
one line as active and the other as a spare. The line should be kept to a minimum length
so that there are no loops or twists. Hence, the elimination of the gas line in the GC-
tracer, and introduction of controlled pressure and temperature in FLAIR to minimise
these effects. The installation of a condensation collector was also recommended on each
gas line to avoid trailing peaks and ghost peaks as previously seen in Figure 3.10. PVC
gas lines are recommended as compared to polyethylene or steel to avoid segregation
effects of the gas components.
The gas line should be able to function efficiently at extreme temperatures (i.e 5-40°C).
Furthermore, an insulation system for the gas line should be used in order to eliminate
external temperature influence on the line. The air inlet should be kept far away from
any pollution sources, such as rig engines to avoid contamination of the gas samples.
In order to keep a constant ratio of the gas mixture to air, the pressure and suction
flow should be constant, which can be done using the FLAIR system. This should be
coordinated with the specifications of the equipment used. It is recommended to be
able to visually monitor the pressure and suction flow using visual control from the
mud logging cabin. The suction flow should be inspected daily with the changes to the
suction flow recorded on the mud log. Also, the maximum transit time through the gas
line to be 2 minutes depending on the gas line diameter and length.
4.5.3
Gas Detector
The gas detector recommended for the GWD method is the FID. The FID should mea-
sure the total hydrocarbon gas in equivalent methane in air in a continuous or semi
continuous manner (sampling rate <15 sec). The relative error between observed values
and true values should be +/- 5%. The readings should be taken with a resolution of 10
ppm, because this is considered as the smallest variation in the gas composition which
produced a detectable change in the signal.
The transit time for the gas trap to TG detector to be checked routinely to maintain
consistency. Alarms to be set for threshold TG measurements inside mudlogging cabin.
The threshold should be set according to the company’s request.
4.5.4
Gas Chromatography
The type of chromatography used is a FID with analysis ranging from C1 to C5 mea-
sured in ppm . It is recommended to use high resolution gas chromatography with a
maximum cycle time of 90 seconds. The components should be computed using peak

areas as opposed to peak height. The analysis should have an observed value accuracy
of +/- 5%, as mentioned in Section 4.5.3, and a resolution of 1 ppm.
The threshold of the quantifiable value is 10 ppm, and the saturation limit must be clearly
defined. The C1/C2 separation capacity must have a minimum of 200. Moreover, no gas
sample should be trapped into the chromatograph for later analysis, to avoid possible

The fluid analysis comprised of identifying the hydrocarbon fluid type through the use
of the GWD method in Chapter 4. This is through the use of the Wh-Bh ratios as a
function of measured depth from the chromatography data. The hydrocarbon type can
be deduced using Figure 4.4. Fluid transition zones and contacts can be deduced from
combining both ratios to indicate the fluid type present at specific depth. The gas oil
contact (GOC) would show an increase in the Wh in the range of 17-40, and the Bh
would exhibit lower values . A gas or oil-water contact would be observed from specific
trend breaks in the ratio plot, but absolute values for observing hydrocarbon-water con-

6.3.2
Digitisation of Mudlogs
All of the mudlogs used for the analysis presented in Chapter 8 were digitised using a
digitisation program. The mudlogs are scanned into high quality TIFF files that can
be converted into numerical data which can be used in mathematical software such as
Microsoft Excel or Matlab. However, there are uncertainties associated with the digiti-
sation process, and hence the achieved model results.
In order to grasp the importance of the uncertainties associated with the digitisation
process, a summary of the work flow for digitisation is necessary. Maersk Oil use Neura-
log; a specialised digitisation program for well and mud logs. A serious attempt has been
made for this project to use Neuralog for gathering more mud log data, but due to the
difficulties with the digitisation process, it could not be achieved. However, the skills
gained through the experience of using Neuralog has enlightened upon the technique
and the possible uncertainties that could arise in the gas data.
The logs are digitised by first setting a depth grid. This is done manually by zooming in
on the points on the depth grid, and setting the top and bottom scale for the depth. The
program then automatically sets a line where it assumes that the grid follows. Although,
this is not always the case as some of the logs can be skewed to one side or the other,
depending on the quality of the log and the scan itself. This in turn is related to the
paper quality and the condition of the printed mud logs. Therefore, it is essential that
time is spent on manually placing grid points to adjust the depth scale to be in line with
==PAGE:59==
6.3 Mud Gas Uncertainties

the depth axis. It is hence important to ensure that the depth is correctly positioned
when starting the digitisation, in order to avoid the offset of the measurements. The
same method also applies for setting the horizontal scale for the different measurements.
After placement of the grids, Neuralog is used to automatically fit a curve where it de-
tects the data. This is not always the case, and hence more time needs to be spent to
ensure that the curve is correctly positioned, and is not offset. Any offset would hinder
the data, and would not be detectable when initiating the mud gas analysis. Therefore,
this procedure is crucial for correct digitisation of data.
From experience, it was also deduced that the scale of the logs plays a vital factor for
successful digitisation. It was observed that digitising with a scale 1:1000 is too uncer-
tain and inaccurate at tracking the curves in the tracks. It became quickly clear that
errors due to digitisation would increase dramatically at this scale. This was mainly
due to the poor clarity of chromatography curves, and similarity between the legends
of the hydrocarbon components. The similarity created a chaotic pattern of the chro-
matography results when it came to the reservoir intervals, and so was unfeasible.The
recommended scale for digitisation is 1:200, where the better resolution can improve the
efficiency of digitisation.
The process of digitisation is very subjective, and the uncertainty cannot be quantified,
as it solely depends on the amount of time consumed on every single track created.

6.3.4
Gas from Drilling Procedure
The gas discussed in this model has so far been assumed to be liberated from the for-
mations drilled.
However, the influx of gas into the well bore is not always a true
representation of the hydrocarbons in the formation. There are several drilling factors
that can cause anomalies and variations in gas readings that must be addressed in such
a gas model.
One must discuss the types of gas that are recorded and observed in the mud logs. When
a pipe is added to the assembly for a pipe connection, the mud pumps are shut and this
creates a temporary under-balanced pressure scenario. This causes an influx of gas in
the well-bore that shows on the mud logs after one lag interval. The same applies dur-
ing pipe tripping action, the well-bore when the mud pumps are also shut. Initially,
it would seem that connection gas peaks should be omitted from the model results as
discussed by Blanc et al. 2003, however this gas is representative of the hydrocarbon
bearing formations. This gas could be a result of readings from the formations drilled
and undrilled as there is an under-balanced scenario as discussed earlier. Although the
connection or trip gas peaks may show a sharp peak, it can be expected that the readings
for a certain depth interval after the connection would show reduced gas readings due
to the depletion of hydrocarbons.
Moreover, when mud is passed on the flow line, it often goes through a mud gas separator.
This mud gas separator separates the gas entrapped in the mud from the drilling fluid.
Although it is meant to degas all of the gas, not all of it is liberated from the mud, and
therefore mud recirculated back into the hole could still contain hydrocarbons. This
means that when mud reaches the surface the same gas readings are picked up again
through the gas trap. This will create a constant background gas reading that needs to
be altered for in the gas model (AAPG 2016). This can be observed from the model if
the HCPV as a function of depth shows a constant overestimation of hydrocarbons in
place below the hydrocarbon-water contact.

Reservoir Fluid Properties
PVT properties of the reservoir fluid need to be taken into account when analysing mud
gas data, as the behaviour of the gas at the surface compared to the reservoir.
With respect to the points discussed in Chapters 3-4, it can be seen that the extent of
the uncertainties are associated with formation fluid properties. If the density of hydro-
carbons is light, then it would contain lighter C1-C3 components. Hence, gas and gas
condensates would be more prone to bubble out of the mud in the flow line on the way
to the gas trap. Thus, would cause gas readings to be underestimated due to the effect.
Therefore it would be ideal to place a gas trap as close as possible to the bell nipple
to reduce the uncertainties, due to gas bubbling at the surface of the mud in the flow line.
On the other hand if the reservoir contains medium to heavy API oil then there would
be a larger concentration of heavier carbon components. Hence in this scenario it would
be harder to extract gas samples from the mud leading to suppressed gas readings. This
is especially the case when the temperature of the mud returning in the flow line is below
the boiling point of these heavier hydrocarbon chains. In this instance the use of the
FLAIR and GC-Tracer system would be ideal.

The density and neutron log were used for identification of gas bearing zones in the
drilled sections by distinct crossovers in the logs. These crossovers were used to accu-
rately place the gas-oil contact, as will be seen in Section 8.2. The observed crossovers
in the density-neutron log is directly related to the way of porosity measurement by each
==PAGE:67==
7.6 In-place Hydrocarbon Reserves
51
technique.
The neutron log is a useful tool for differentiating between oil and gas in the reservoir.
The neutron tool functions by the measure of the formation’s reaction to the bombard-
ment of high energy neutrons. Neutrons have no charge, and their mass is approximately
the same mass of a hydrogen atom. Therefore, when bombarded with high energy into
the formation, they can either be slowed down, or absorbed depending on the hydrogen
content of the formation. So, the neutron tool is essentially a measure of the hydrogen
richness of the formation (hydrogen index), and the tool is calibrated for limestone sat-
urated with fresh water. Then gas present in the reservoir with a very low density will
have a low hydrogen index which will skew the neutron porosity results to indicate very
low porosity results (Rider 2002).
On the other hand, the density tool is a direct measure of the formation bulk density.
The density tool operates by the measurement of the attenuation of medium to high
energy focused gamma rays between the source and the detector, when bombarded into
the formation. The attenuation is a function of the electron density sitting in the for-
mation which is related to the bulk density. The bulk density is thus a measure of ρma,
and the volume of free fluid in which the minerals enclose. Hence, given that gas has a
low density, then this will translate into a lower bulk density (high apparent porosity if
considering Equation 7.1)(Rider 2002).
Combining both effects of gas on the density-neutron porosity log, it can be expected
that when passing a gas zone the density log will read a low density. On the other hand,
the neutron will indicate a very low porosity if plotted on the same axis.

Comparison of Hydrocarbon Reserves
The methodology for processing the mud gas data follows on from proposed model in
Chapter 6. Firstly, the ROP was calculated from the given drilling data (Equation 6.1)
and then the VOC was derived using the radius of the hole from the 8.5 inch bit size used
(Equation 6.2). Furthermore, the sampling rate for the drilling measurements was on
average every 3.048m, and the gas measurements varied between 9.144 and 3.048m. The
variability in the sampling rate is because the measurements are recorded with respect
to time rather than depth. Hence, the depths for the gas data had to be matched to
that of the ROP by matching every data point manually.
The results of the mud model were also optimised by using η to account for the unreliable
acquisition of gas data. η was deduced by plotting the results of the HCPV mud model
and the the results from the well logs together using Equations 6.3-7.8 respectively. The
model was amplified using η until the results are within reasonable comparison of the
well log results, which in the case of the of well 1 η=80. This can be seen from Figure
8.6 where both results match to some extent in terms of hydrocarbon volume estimation
as a function of depth, and as cumulative reserves. From Figure 8.6, the following can
be observed:
• The slope for the mud gas model follows the trend observed from the well logs.
• The slope for the cumulative model curve also follows the same shift in gradient
trend observed from the well logs.
• The extent of underestimation of the mud gas model compared to the well logs is
variable throughout the HCPV log.
• The model is suppressed by an approximate factor of 2 between 2246-2340m, as
can be observed from the HCPV log.
• There is a distinct peak (CG) at 2307m that correlates to a connection gas peak
noted in the printed mud logs (see Table 8.5). Below this peak, there is an abrupt
drop in the modelled results.
• Below the OWC, there seems to be a constant overestimate of the HCPV by the
mud model.

Comparison of Mud Gas Model Results
Firstly, the mud gas measurements from the two wells examined in Chapter 8 are consid-

The model results in Figure 8.6 show the underestimation of it in the first half of the
HCPV log and is highlighted by a sharp connection gas peak at 2307m. The connec-
tion gas causes a temporary under balance between the pressure from the mud and the
formation. Therefore, gas from the undrilled formation diffuses into the well bore until
the mud pumps are restarted. This causes a lower gas readings for the depth interval

because of the exhaustion of the gas in place due to the diffusion into the well-bore
during the time of the connection. This is further supported by the return of higher gas
readings at 2340m, to correlate completely with the well log results.
Gas below the OWC can be explained either by condensation of heavy hydrocarbon
components and aromatics in the gas line, which would produce trailing peaks in the
gas readings, as discussed in Section 3.9. Secondly, it could be due to unconsidered back-

the constant peak is due to gas recycling, then this would imply inefficiency in the mud
gas separator that would cause influx of gas back into the hole. Hence, it is crucial that
gas is measured in mud returning back into the hole to investigate if gas recycling is
the cause of the trailing peak. The two reasons suggested for the constant gas readings
are supported by the Wh-Bh graph, where there is a continuous indication of oil even
beyond the OWC suggested by the well logs (Figure 8.1.4). As previously stated using
gas show analysis at the well-site, it was mentioned that there was no strong indication
of the continuity of the oil signature beyond the OWC.
On the other hand for well 2, It can be observed that there is a near perfect trend of
the model with the well logs using η=32. However, there is a distinct 2 peaks observed
at the top of the model that are anomalous and comparable to the connection peak
observed in well 1 (Figure 8.14). Upon investigation with the drilling parameters, it was
found that there is a a drilling break through the ROP graph (Figure 8.15). This in turn
causes a significant increase in the VOC which is multiplied by the gas readings. Hence
the drilling efficiency and the ROP has a direct influence on the model results.
The factor for well 2 is in reasonable range expected for the correction of mud temper-
ature and positions of the gas trap wit respect to the flow line (Chapter 3). Well 1 is
over half of the factor for well 2 but still in line with the uncertainties factor. There
are several difference that can cause such a discrepancy. As well, it is not expected
that the uncertainties associated with mud temperature retuning back from the flow
line, and the position of the trap are the sole factors that contribute to the η factor.
As can be seen from the introduction of the GC-tracer in Section 3.3, the solubility of
the hydrocarbons in the mud does play a role in the gas measurements. Furthermore,
although the distance to the seabed does not seem to be a major contributing factor on
the temperature of the mud, it can however be suspected that there is an effect of gas
bubbles escaping when the mud enter the flow line, as discussed in Section 6.1 by Liege
2006.

9.2
Comparison of Fluid Contact Results
Judging from the age of the well and the theory introduced by Haworth, Sellens, and
Whittaker 1985, it was quoted that the Wh-Bh would not be able to derive fluid contacts
because it would be too turbulent in the zone due to fluid transition. This is clearly
seen from the Wh and Bh curves in Figure 8.8, where there is a 5m overestimation of
the GOC due to chaotic behaviour of the Wh-Bh log in fluid contact levels suggested
by the well logs (Well 2). However, it can be observed that there is an exact trend
break at OWC. On the other hand for well 1, it can be seen that the OWC was over
estimated by 55m. This can be related to the effect of a constant gas signature below
the OWC, which has been explained previously in Section 9.1. Although the method
does not always indicate fluid contact levels, the fluid type can be correctly identified
with the aid of Figure 4.4.
The TG/ΣCcor does indicate accurate hydrocarbon-water contacts as can be seen from
the two wells. The contact points are demonstrated by a sudden significant increase in
the ratio that extends towards the target depth. This when related to well log results
show an exact correlation.
9.3
Applicability of Current Model
The current mud model has only been investigated for WBM, and hence the effect of
OBM on the model is unknown. The model has proved that for wells drilled with WBM,
it can be effectively utilised in exploration operations. η for the model would be challeng-
ing to accurately derive without the presence of well logs. However from the essence for
the factors discussed, it can be observed that the uncertainties can be accounted for to a
large extent. The main important result is that the model does follow the general trend
observed in well logs, and hence can model the general trend of a prospectus reservoir.
This can aid in the planning of zones of interests in drilled formations for fluid sampling
for PVT evaluation.
In fields where well data is already available, the η factor can be deduced by correlation
from nearby wells and utilised in the model. This is specifically relevant when planning
for enhanced recovery wells.
The model can also be effectively incorporated to identify oil-water contacts through the
TG/ΣCcor ratio. This can be used to cut down costs and save time when modelling the
fluid contacts across a field. Moreover, the model can be utilised as cost effective method
when a fast decision needs to be taken as it can present real-time results at a well site.
This can aid in optimising well trajectories as well as further planning for development.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
Application of a hydrocarbon reserves model using mud gas data can achieve accurate
in-place hydrocarbon reserves in a well drilled with water based mud. The model is
based on a combination of total gas and rate of penetration data that is applied using
the geometry of the borehole. The achieved model results were successful of mimicking
the hydrocarbon trend observed from well logs to a high degree of accuracy. The reserves
are achieved by the application of an amplifying factor (η) to model results to account
for the uncertainties associated with gas extraction at a well site. The amplifying factor
was accurately derived from optimising the model results with conventional well log re-
sults.
Moreover, the use of the wetness-balance ratio was found to be a useful tool in iden-
tifying hydrocarbon types in a well and identifying fluid transition zones that can be
compared to well logs. The TG/ΣCcor ratios can identify hydrocarbon-water contact
points to a high degree of accuracy that can perfectly correlate with water saturation
results.
The model was successfully applied to two wells in the Danish North Sea, where approx-
imate exact results were achieved. The results for in-place reserves for the gas model
in well 1 underestimated the cumulative reserves. This is as a result of the effect of
connection gas due to the addition of pipes as well as inefficient drilling. The effect of
connection gas on gas data is seen as a spike in results rather than spread over a depth
interval. Inconsistent mud flow rates due to shutting of the mud pumps had a negative
effect on model results. The fluid type results achieved from gas data showed correlation
to oil, and gave conclusive results for transition zones as well as contact depths compared
with well logs.
Analysis of well 2 showed exact hydrocarbon reserves from the model compared to well
logs with an amplifying factor within the uncertainty range suggested for correction of
mud temperature, and position of gas extractors in the flow line. Results on fluid type
showed accurate detection of both gas and oil in the reservoir through the use of hy-
drocarbon cross plots. Fluid transition zones were also successfully incorporated with
comparison of well logs.
Finally, through this project it can be concluded that utilising the mud gas data for early
formation evaluation can provide innovative results. Integrating the model developed can
effectively aid in decision making for drilling operations that could hold great potential
for cost efficiency and risk in decision making.

CHAPTER 11
Proposal for Further Research
Further research based on the model formulated in this thesis must include :
• Testing the model in real time at a well-site for detailed calibration of mud logging
equipment as well as accurate recording of vital drilling events.
• Testing the model with known gas trap models to compare the variability of the
amplifying factor. As well as strengthening of the model foundations using gas
trap information.
• Experimentation of the solubility of hydrocarbons in different types of drilling
mud.
• Development of the model for oil based muds and cored wells.
• Further research into the uncertainties associated with the drilling operations, es-
pecially the extent of the temporary under-balance due to pipe connections or
tripping effects.
