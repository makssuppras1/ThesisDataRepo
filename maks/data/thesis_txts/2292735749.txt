Dansk Resume
Følsom spektroskopi i det midtinfrarøde (2-15µm) er brugbart for en række områder, både
akademiske og industrielle. Der er dog det problem, at lys i det midtinfrarøde har kun lidt
energi per photon (0,62eV-0,083eV). Dette er sammenligneligt med energien associeret med
termiske vibrationer ved stuetemperatur, så for at få et godt signal-støj forhold, er det nød-
vendigt at køle måleinstrumenter, der måler direkte ved disse bølgelængder, ned til væsentligt
under 0◦C.
Sum frekvens generering (SFG) kan bruges til at omgå dette problem, da det kan addere et
signal i det midtinfrarøde med en pumpelaser og generere et nyt signal med den samme infor-
mation ved en kortere bølgelængde. Pointen er så, at ved det nye signals kortere bølgelængde
er det muligt at bruge silicium detektorer, der er billigere og har et væsentligt bedre signal-støj
forhold uden køling.
For at gøre dette så eﬀektivt som muligt, kan det være en fordel at placere den ulineære krystal
brugt til SFG inde i pumpelaserens kavitet, da det giver en højere eﬀekt for den samme energi.
Optisk Sensor Technologi gruppen ved DTU Fotonik har allerede publiceret resultater, hvor
dette er gjort for signaler ved 3-4µm med en 1064nm pumpelaser[2, 3, 4, 5]. En udfordring ved
at udvide dette til længere bølgelænder, er at de ulineære krystaller, der bruges, skal være være
gennemsigtige ved signal-, pumpe- og den genererede bølgelængde. Målet med dette projekt
har været at bygge en pumpe laser til SFG med en bølgelængde på 1,9µm. Der ligger to årsager
bag ønsket om en pumpelaser med en længere bølgelængde. Den første er at kunne udvide
metoden til længere signal bølgelængder, da dette åbner for at bruge ikke lineære krystaller,
der er gennemsigtige ved 1,9µm, men ikke ved 1064nm. Den anden er at spontan nedkon-
vertering skaber støj ved bølgelængder, der er længere end pumpelaserens. Med en 1064nm
pumpelaser, kan dette give dårligt signal-støj forhold ved opkonvertering af signaler ved fx.
1500-1600nm. Ved at ﬂytte pumpelaseren til længere bølgelængder kan dette undgåes.
I løbet af projektet har der været fokuseret på at konstruere en eﬀektiv 1,9µm laser, med
opkonvertering af 1064nm i en periodisk poled lithiumniobat (PPLN) krystal, som proof-of-
concept. Ved udgangen af dette projekt var der blevet bygget en 1,9µm laser der:
• Havde god bølgelængde kontrol og stabilitet.
• Kunne tunes mellem 1900-1935nm.
• Havde meget eﬀektiv udkobling af det opkonverterede lys.
• Havde en intrakavitetsfelt på 25,2W.
Den maksimale teoretiske konverteringsgrad for 1064nm med en 25,2W 1908nm pumpelaser er
1,3% i en 10mm lang PPLN krystal. Konverteringseﬀektiviteten blev ikke målt ved de 25,2W
grundet tids- og udstyrsbegrænsninger, men den blev målt i en tidligere iteration ved 3.5W
pumpelaser eﬀekt. Her var konverteringseﬀektiviteten 0,019%, og det teoretiske maksimum
var 0,18%.

Abstract
A tunable Tm:YLF laser centered at 1908nm was built for low noise infrared upconversion
imaging and spectroscopy. Tuning was possible across the range 1900-1930nm. 0.019% upcon-
version eﬃciency of 1064.5nm light was measured for a 3.5W intracavity ﬁeld. To increase the
achievable conversion eﬃciency, steps were taken to increase the intracavity ﬁeld of the mixing
laser. An intracavity ﬁeld of 25.2W was reached with a reduction of the intracavity loss and a
doubling of the pump power. Due to time and material constraints, the upconversion eﬃciency
was not measured at 25.2W pump power, but the theoretical limit is 1.3% for the 10mm long
periodically poled lithium niobate crystal used.
3
Introduction
Spectroscopy is the study of how light and matter interact. In the infrared (IR), the absorp-
tion of molecules is determined by their speciﬁc rotational and vibrational modes. This gives
molecules a "ﬁnger print" like absorption spectrum, that can be used to identify what molecules
are present in a given sample. The ability to quickly and non-destructively characterize a sub-
stance has numerous scientiﬁc and industrial applications, from detecting carcinogenic gasses
to determining the protein content of milk. The major diﬃculty with mid-IR spectroscopy is
the detectors. To detect light with wavelenths in the range 5-15µm, a detector needs a bandgap
or transition with a corresponding energy, 0.25eV-0.083eV. This is comparable to the thermal
vibrations at room temperature, which means the detectors must be cooled signiﬁcantly to
reach a useful signal to noise ratio (SNR).
Sum Frequency Generation (SFG) can be used to combine a weak signal in the IR with a pump
laser to generate a new signal, at a shorter wavelength. This process is known as upconversion
and it can be used to move the signal we wish to detect from longer wavelengths, where the
available detectors have poor performance and need cooling, to shorter wavelengths where
cheap high performance silicon based detectors are available.
SFG can be done in bulk material or in a waveguide, and it can be done as a single pass of the
mixing laser through the active material or by placing the active material inside the mixing
laser cavity, to increase the eﬀective ﬁeld and thus the conversion eﬃciency. While conversion
in waveguides can reach very high conversion eﬃciencies[1], imaging and spectral information
can be retained during upconversion in bulk material[2]. This means fast spectroscopy can
be achieved, as diﬀerent wavelength will be upconverted at diﬀerent input/output angles, and
a spectrum can thus be measured in parallel. A single pass approach has the advantage of
simplicity, separating the operation of the mixing laser and the upconversion process, but it is
possible to achieve much higher mixing ﬁelds for the same input power with intracavity, which
in turn reduces cost and simpliﬁes cooling, expanding the possible application.
The Optical Sensor Technology group at DTU photonics have previously published results
showing eﬀective intracavity upconversion in bulk materials using 1064nm mixing lasers[2, 3,
4, 5]. The goal of this project was to build a 1.9µm Tm:YLF laser for intracavity parametric
upconversion, and to get experience working at 1.9µm and with Tm:YLF, as these are both new
for this speciﬁc group. The motivation for introducing a 1.9µm pump is twofold. Firstly, we
wish to be able to use intracavity upconversion for longer wavelengths. This requires that the
nonlinear crystal used is transparent at both the pump wavelength, at the signal wavelength,
and at the target wavelength. Currently Lithium Niobate is used, which is transparent up to

Project Goals
The core purpose of this project, was to
• Construct a Tm:YLF laser, capable of intracavity upconversion.
• Describe any important experiences made, to lay the groundwork for further applications.
A secondary goal was to measure the noise for upconversion with the Tm:YLF laser, and
compare it to the 1064nm laser upconversion system for CO2 detection. This was to see how
changing the pump laser in the CO2 detection project might aﬀect the signal to noise ratio.

Theory
4.1
Lasers
At the most basic, a laser is ampliﬁcation of light, with feedback from the ampliﬁed light. This
can in practice be done by placing a gain medium between two mirrors. To support lasing,
the two mirrors must have radii of curvature and be placed, such that the light mode is stable,
i.e. in a way that conﬁnes the light to the cavity. Further, the gain medium must be supplied
energy or pumped until population inversion is reached, basically, enough electrons must be in
elevated energy states, so a photon passing through the gain medium have a higher probability
of stimulating emission of another photon, than the probability of the photon being absorbed.
4.2
Three Level System

energy states in the gain medium. Light passing through the medium have a probability of
stimulating emission, and a probability of being absorbed. For a material with energy levels
E1 and E2 with an electron population N1 and N2 respectively, the probability density that an
unexcited atom absorbs a single photon is[8]

4.3
Tm:YLF
The gain medium used in this project is Tm:YLF 5% doped with Tm3+. The energy diagram
for Tm-Tm transitions can be seen in Figure 2.
The crystal is pumped at 792nm, which
corresponds to the 3H6 : 3H4 transition. From the 3H4 level the excited electrons decay to the
3F4 level by one of several routes. Most important is the cross-relaxation transition, in which
a second electron from the 3H6 level is excited, signiﬁcantly increasing the quantum eﬃciency
of this three level system. Another important eﬀect of this transition is that more energy is
converted directly to light instead of heat, which means a reduced need for cooling. This is
especially important in a material with such a large diﬀerence between the pumping and the
lasing wavelengths.
The emission spectrum of the Tm:YLF is quite broad as can be seen in Figure 3. This can
potentially be used to provide gain over a large bandwidth. Tunable lasing have been achieved
the interval 1850-1940nm[11],[12].
The refractive index of Tm:YLF is 1.45 at 792nm and 1.44 at 1908nm[13]. Tm:YLF have a
small negative temperature gradient of the refractive index[14], so for the power levels and
resulting temperature gradients expected in this project, we expect only a very minor negative
thermal lensing eﬀect.

4.11
Raman
Raman scattering is the inelastical scattering of photons in a material. The photons either
gain energy from or lose energy to rotational or vibrational modes of molecules, called anti-
Stokes scattering and Stokes scattering, respectively. Stokes scattering is happens at a much
higher rate than anti-Stokes, which means a high intensity ﬁeld will have two sidebands a
red-shifted sideband and a much smaller blue-shifted sideband, each at a frequency diﬀerence
matching the energy of the relevant rotational or vibrational modes of the material. Unless this
frequency shift places the Raman scattered light at the signal wavelength, it can be removed
with ﬁltering.

6.1
Introduction
In the following the orientations of the polarization of light, the laser diode (LD), and the
Tm:YLF crystal is described in the following way.
The light is described as horizontally or vertically polarized, which refers to the direction of
the electric ﬁeld compared with the surface of the breadboard. Horizontally polarized is par-
allel with the breadboard surface, and vertically polarized is perpendicular to the breadboard
surface.
The output facet of the LD is a rectangle, which is wider in one direction than the other. The
output light diverges faster along the direction, where the output facet is narrow, than it does
in the broad direction. For this reason they will be referred to as the "fast axis" and "slow
axis", respectively. The LD is polarized along the slow axis.
The absorption of Tm:YLF depends on the relative orientation of the crystal structure and
the polarization of the light. π-polarized refers to light polarized parallel to the crystal axis,
σ-polarized refers to light polarized perpendicularly to the crystal axis.

7.1
Folded Cavity Design
The purpose of building this 1.9µm laser, was to use it for intracavity upconversion.
As
described in Section 4.9, the conversion eﬃciency is dependant on the intensity of the pump
and the mode overlap. Therefore, we wish to construct a setup with a second, high intensity,
focal point inside the cavity, where the PPLN crystal can be placed. We also must take into
account how the light, that is to be upconverted, can enter the PPLN cavity, and how the
upconverted light will exit the cavity.

mirrors to three, as shown in Figure 26. This places the focus of both the 1.9µm pump and the

7.4
Temperature Sweeps
To investigate the upconversion, sweeps where done over the temperature of the PPLN crystal,
while the intensity of the upconverted light was recorded. This was done with two diﬀerent
etalons in the cavity to control the wavelength of the pump light. The etalons used were E1
(0.25mm) and E2 (0.5mm), described in Section 4.6. For both etalons, mode hops could still
be observed. With E1, the output still showed mode shifts, which moved the upconversion
peak ca. 10◦C, as can be seen in Figures 30a and 30b. This corresponds to a wavelength
shift of the pump of ca. 4nm, which matches the free spectral range. With E2, we instead see
upconversion 20◦C from the main peak in Figure 31a. This corresponds to a wavelength shift
nearer 8-10nm. It was also possible to achieve upconversion with no major mode jumps with
etalon E2, this is shown in Figure 31b. Though the major mode jumps have been suppressed
here, we still see a jagged central peak, as a result of an unstable mode. In conclusion, while
upconversion was demonstrated with the current setup, the main problems identiﬁed are;
1. The wavelength of the 1.9µm laser was not stable enough.
2. The setup had no way to monitor the power or wavelength of the 1.9µm laser.
3. The upconverted light was only weakly transmitted through the end mirror. This is only
an issue for the upconversion of 1064.5nm, as the mirror is transmissive at most other
wavelengths in the visible and near IR.
These issues are addressed in the setup described in the next section.

Design of the Prism Cavity
The problems with the previous folded cavity design was that:
1. Even with etalons introduced, there was a tendency to jump between wavelengths.
2. Due to the reﬂectivity of the mirrors at 1.9µm, it was diﬃcult to monitor the intensity
and wavelength of the intracavity ﬁeld.
3. Due to the reﬂectivity of the mirrors at 685nm, most of the upconverted light remained
in the cavity till it was scattered from the PPLN crystal and etalon surfaces.
These problems can be solved by introducing a prism. As described in Section 4.7, a prism
can be used to provide tuning capabilities. This gives better control of the wavelength, solving
the wavelength instability. If the coupling into the prism is not done exactly at the Brewster
angle, there will be a small surface reﬂection, providing an outcoupling from the cavity which
can be used to monitor power and wavelength.
And ﬁnally, if an appropriate prism and
mirror conﬁguration is chosen, the diﬀerence between the refraction angle of 685nm and 1.9µm
provides the possibility of directing the upconverted light completely past the end mirror.
The main design parameters to consider for the design of a cavity with a prism are
1. The cavity must be stable.
2. The mirrors are coated for 0◦incidence, which means the angles of incidence on the
mirrors must be kept small.
3. The beam waists in the Tm:YLF must be small to minimize loss to absorption in the
non-pumped volume of the crystal
4. The beam waists in the PPLN crystal must be small to increase intensity for a high
eﬃciency of the upconversion process.

The design for the prism cavity shown in Figure 32 is a modiﬁcation of the basic folded cavity
design used earlier. M2 has been moved closer M1, as this allows for a longer pathway in the
branch containing the prism and PPLN crystal. The separation between 685nm and 1.9µm

prism. This means, that for the upconverted light to pass by the end mirror and exit the
cavity, 100mm or more is needed between the prism and the mirror. At the same time, it is
preferable to have the focus and PPLN crystal before the refraction in the prism, as this keeps
the pathway through the PPLN crystal constant during wavelength tuning. For these reasons
the end mirror, M5 with a radius of curvature 75mm, has been replaced with M8, that has a
radius of curvature of 150mm. This allows for the required distance between the outcoupling
mirror M8 and the prism, while keeping the focal point 4cm before the prism. The placement
of the focal point means there is space for the PPLN crystal and the oven it is mounted in,
both with the current length of crystal, but also for a 20mm crystal. A 40mm crystal would
require an adjustment of the prism position of a few millimetres for optimal placement of the
focus.
As mentioned in Section 4.3, tuning of Tm:YLF laser have previously been shown across the
interval 1850-1940nm. As described in Section 4.7, we can calculate the angular sensitivity of
the tuning for a given combination of prism material, prism position, and radius of curvature
of the mirror. It was suspected that the introduction of the extra loss of the prism would bring
the laser closer to the lasing threshold, so angular wavelength resolution was calculated for
the gain peak, 1900-1930nm[11],[12]. For the current conﬁguration, with SF14, the diﬀerence
in output angle of 1900nm and 1930nm is 978µrad. This means M8 must be turned 293µrad
to tune from 1900nm to 1930nm. To give systematic control of the tuning, M8 is placed in a
mount with electronically controlled actuators to adjust the angle. The smallest step size for
this mount is 1µrad, and it is capable of turning across an interval of ±35mrad. The current
conﬁguration should therefore give adequate control of the wavelength tuning.

10.2
Addition of a Second Laser Diode Pump
As discussed in the earlier chapter, we wished to increase the 1.9µm intracavity ﬁeld, and one
way of achieving this was to increase the power used to pump the laser. There are several
ways this could be achieved. A second LD pump could be added to pump from the opposite
side of the existing pump, in a bidirectional pumping conﬁguration, as used in [9]. This has
the advantages of yielding a more uniform gain proﬁle in the laser crystal, and spreading the
thermal load through the crystal. However, this requires laser mirrors coated to be highly
reﬂective near 45◦for 1.9µm light, which we have not been able to ﬁnd readily available.
A second option is to combine laser diodes into an array, using a ﬁbre pigtail, also used in [9].
This would optimally be done with diodes made directly with ﬁbre coupling, which our diodes
not have, and coupling into ﬁbre to combine would give signiﬁcant coupling losses.
A third possible solution is to combine the light from two diodes using a polarizing beamsplitter
cube (PBC), as shown in Figure 45. This was chosen, because it has the advantage of requiring
very little change to the setup used so far. Some absorption and reﬂection losses are expected
in the PBC. Figure 46 shows the power after the focusing optics, with and without the addition
of the PBC. The power from LD 1 is reduced 11% and the power from LD 2 is reduced 7%.

Summarizing Discussion
In this chapter I will summarize the results found during the project, and compare the results
of the diﬀerent setups with each other, and with other published results. The points I wish to
discuss in this chapter are the following:
• Upconversion in a cavity with no wavelength selectivity, or with an etalon.
• The wavelength stability and tuning in the cavity with prism based wavelength control.
• The diﬀerent values found for the conversion ratios of pump to intracavity ﬁeld power.
• The theoretical limit for conversion eﬃciency, with the achieved intracavity ﬁelds, and

11.1
Upconversion in Folded Cavity Without Prism
During the experiments described in Chapter 7, it was found that, without any eﬀorts to con-
trol the wavelength, the Tm:YLF laser suﬀered from a general instability of the wavelength.
To counter this diﬀerent etalons were introduced to the cavity, which improved the stability,
but not enough for stable low noise upconversion. Due to this, it was decided to go to a setup
with prism based wavelength control. A secondary beneﬁt of this was the greatly improved
outcoupling of 685nm light produced from the upconversion of 1064nm light, which was prob-
lematic in the previous setup, due to the low transmission of the mirrors in the 660-690nm
range. This was considered a minor problem, as it would not be an issue for the upconversion
of light in the range 1350nm-10µm, which is a long term goal of this project.

which is especially a problem at high conversion eﬃciencies. However, with the reduction or
elimination of SPDC noise that comes inherently with the use of a long wavelength mixing
laser, it is possible to have a lower conversion eﬃciency and still have a higher SNR. If, for
instance, the conversion eﬃciency of the 1.9µm system is a factor 10 lower than the 1064nm
pumped system, but the SPDC noise intensity is a factor 100 lower, the SNR will be the same.
Therefore, if the 1.9µm system can reach conversion eﬃciencies on the same order of magnitude
as the 1064nm system, it will have a superior SNR.
For the prism controlled cavity with the N-SF14 glass prism, the intracavity ﬁeld was calcu-
lated to be 3.5W. With the beam waist in the PPLN crystal, this gives a theoretical maximum
conversion eﬃciency of 0.18%. The achieved conversion eﬃciency was 0.019%. This shortfall
is believed to be, in part, an issue of a mismatch in the alignment of the focus of the 1.9µm
and the 1064nm lasers, as well as a mismatch of the size of the two foci. It might also be a
result of a overestimation of the intracavity ﬁeld, due to a false assumption in the calculation
of the reﬂection coeﬃcient, as discussed above. In any case, conversion eﬃciencies in this range
was below what we aimed to achieve, and as the conversion eﬃciency scales linearly with the
intracavity ﬁeld, we wanted to increase the intracavity ﬁeld to remedy this problem.
To increase the intracavity ﬁeld the loss in components inside the cavity must be reduced, or
the pump power increased to compensate for the loss. Calculations and measurements showed
the N-SF14 prism to have a loss of ca. 5%, while a prism of the comparable material N-SF11
should have a total loss of 2.0-2.5%. Replacing the prism was therefore the ﬁrst step in reduc-
ing the loss. The power characteristics for the prism controlled cavity presented in Figure 33
show the threshold of the laser to be at 2000mW out of the available 2500mW. Therefore it
was decided to add another pumping diode to bring the laser further above threshold.
Changing the prism from Schott N-SF14 to Schott N-SF11 glass was shown in Chapter 10
to increase the intracavity ﬁeld from 3.5W to 9.1W. The additional power available from the
second LD, further increased the intracavity ﬁeld to 25.2W. This brings the theoretical limit
for the conversion eﬃciency to 1.3% for a 10mm crystal and 5.1% for a 20mm long crystal. If
these eﬃciencies can be reached experimentally, it begins to be interesting for CO2 and CH4
detection, as the SNR will beneﬁt from the absence of SPDC noise.

Conclusion
Having gone through several iterations, we have reached a prism tuned laser system that has
• Good wavelength control and stability for the 1.9µm laser.
• Tunability over the 1900nm-1935nm range.
• Highly eﬃcient out-coupling of the upconverted light.
• An intracavity ﬁeld of 25.2W.
With the current mirrors it was found that a separate outcoupling was needed to characterise
and monitor the intracavity ﬁeld, for the prism tuned laser, the surface reﬂections from the
prism was used for this purpose. Tuning of the laser showed a number of well deﬁned modes,
deﬁned by the absorption lines of water vapour.
The introduction of the prism reduced the intracavity power to 3.5W, and gave correspondingly
a low theoretical maximum for the conversion eﬃciency of 0.18% and a maximum conversion
eﬃciency measured of 0.019%. This was countered by changing the prism material from Schott
N-SF14 glass to Schott N-SF11 glass, and by introducing a second LD pump. This brought
the intracavity power to 25.2W and the theoretical maximum conversion of 1064.5nm light in
a 10mm PPLN crystal to 1.3%. Upconversion was not done with the higher intracavity ﬁeld
due to time constraints.
The core purposes of the project were
• Construct a Tm:YLF laser, capable of intracavity upconversion.
• Describe any important experiences made, to lay the groundwork for further applications.
and both of these have been completed. The secondary goal of measuring spontaneous para-
metric downconversion noise was not reached.
13
Outlook
The immediate next steps if the project is continued would be to perform spectral measure-
ments on the tuning capabilities of the laser after implementation of the two diode pumping
scheme, and measuring the upconversion eﬃciency.
In the slightly longer term, upconversion of 1572nm and 1643nm for CO2 and CH4 detection
could be upconverted using a PPLN crystal with poling periods of 23.5µm and 24.5µm respec-
tively. Alternatively, if a PPLN crystal with a 24.2µm poling period could be obtained, both
could be phase matched with the same poling period. Another future possibility would be to
replace the PPLN with AgGaS2, for upconversion of 6-11µm light.
A more long term possibility would be to introduce modelocking. In [24] passive modelocking
using a polycrystalline Cr:ZnS saturable absorber was demonstrated. Pulsed operation has
the advantage that it gives higher peak intensities. This means, that if the signal is timed to
pass through the PPLN simultaneously with the pump pulse, it is possible to achieve higher
conversion eﬃciencies with the same average power, than is possible for continuous operation.
In the long term, long wavelength pumped upconversion opens for a number of exiting future
possible implementations. Pumping with a wavelength longer than the signal wavelength will

 Determination of reflectance (R) by transmission measurements
Reflectance
Sputtered optical coatings for the VIS and NIR exhibit extremely low scattered light and absorption losses ( both
in the order of some 10-5... 10-4). This has been confirmed by direct measurements of scattered light and
absorption as well as by highly accurate reflectivity measurements (e.g. by cavity ring down spectroscopy). With
these very small optical losses, the reflectance of sputtered mirrors can be determined by measuring the
transmission T and the simple calculation R=100%-T.
In a normal spectrophotometer, the transmission can be measured which an accuracy of about 0.1...0.2%
(depending on the absolute value), whereas reflectance measurements in spectrophotometers mostly have errors
of about 0.5%. Thus, the determination of the reflectivity of sputtered  coatings in the VIS  and NIR  by
transmission measurements is much more accurate than direct reflectance measurements. Please note that this
method can only be applied because the optical losses are very small (which is one of the advantages of sputtered
coatings). Evaporated coatings, especially for the UV, usually show scattered light losses in the order of some 10 -
3 ... 10-2, depending on the wavelength. In these cases, direct reflectance measurements are necessary.
Indirect determination of the  reflection at AOI=0°  by transmission measurements at AOI=0°
AOI (angle of incidence = angle between the incident beam and the surface normal of the mirror).
It is also possible to determine the reflection at AOI=0° from transmission measurements at 0°. The procedure is
the following:
First the transmission at normal incidence is measured (dashed line in fig.1). Computer refinement of the
theoretical design to the measured curve provides then the real design of the coating. This refinement procedure
simulates all errors which may occur during the coating process (e.g. slight deviation of the sputter rate with
growing number of layers, rising substrate temperature etc.). It is continued until the measured and the calculated
curve match very well (especially in the side bands outside the reflection band). If this is the case, the reflection
and other optical properties can be calculated from the refined design (continuous line in fig.1). 
This method proved to be very accurate.

Tunable 1.9µm Laser System for Mid-IR
Upconversion Detection
Rasmus Lyngbye Pedersen, Peter Tidemand-Lichtenberg, Lasse Høgstedt
Technical University of Denmark, Department of Photonics Engineering, Frederiksborgvej 399 4000 Roskilde,
Denmark
Abstract:
A tunable 1.9µm Tm:YLF laser was built for low noise infrared upconversion
imaging and spectroscopy. Its noise properties are compared to that obtained using a 1064nm
mixing laser, focusing on spontaneous parametric downconversion generated noise.
OCIS codes: 190.7220, 300.6340.
1.
Introduction
Sum Frequency Generation (SFG) can be used to combine a weak signal wavelength in the infrared (IR) with a more
powerful pump laser to generate a signal at shorter wavelengths. This will move the IR signal from a region with
inherently noisy detectors to the visible regime or near IR, where low noise silicon based detectors can be used.
Upconversion for IR detection has been demonstrated earlier in a short wavelength pump scheme [2, 3] and using
longer wavelength mixing ﬁelds for pumping in waveguides [4,5].
The goal of this project was to build a 1.9µm Tm:YLF laser for intracavity parametric upconversion. The motivation
for introducing a 1.9µm pump is to reduce the noise in CO2 and CH4 detection systems. CO2 and CH4 can be detected
using their absorption peaks at 1572nm and 1643nm, respectively. This can be done by upconverting the 1572nm and
1643nm light with a 1064nm mixing laser, but at the high intracavity ﬁeld required for a high conversion efﬁciency,
spontaneous parametric downconversion (SPDC) produce noise at the signal wavelength. This cannot be removed by
ﬁltering [5].
Fig. 1: Diagram of the tunable setup of the Tm:YLF laser. This conﬁguration uses a prism to support lasing
at different wavelengths depending on the angle of mirror M8. M8 is a highly reﬂecting mirror,
with absorption and scattering losses on the order of 10−4-10−5. M8 has a radius of curvature of
R = 150mm, and it is placed in a a piezo controlled mount to give systematic and precise control
of the angle. M1: Plane mirror, M2: 150mm radius of curvature, M6, M7: Plane metal mirrors. L4:
lens with a 250mm focal length.

As both SPDC and SRS mainly scatter light to longer wavelengths [5], changing the pump wavelength from being
shorter than the signal wavelength to a longer wavelength would eliminate this problem [4,5].
In this project a 1064nm signal has been upconverted using the 1.9µm source for proof of concept, to compare the
noise of a weak short wavelength signal upconverted with a long wavelength high intensity mixing laser, with the
reverse scenario.
2.
Setup
Figure 1 shows the experimental setup. The gain material used was Tm:YLF, which can lase in the range 1860-
1940nm, [6]. The laser crystal was pumped with a 3W laser diode (LD) at 792nm through the plane end mirror, M1.
Tm:YLF is a three level system [8], which means it is not by default transparent at the lasing wavelength. For this
reason, and to suppress higher order transverse modes, the beam radii in the Tm:YLF was kept narrow. This means
a 100µm beam radius for the 1.9µm ﬁeld, and the 792nm pump is focused to a 80µm beam waist. The beam waist
radius achieved in the PPLN crystal is calculated to be 100µm horizontally, and 120µm vertically. Before reaching
the end mirror, M8, the light was refracted through an equilateral prism of Schott N-SF14 glass. The prism has been
incorporated for three reasons. Firstly, the difference in refraction between the upconverted light and the pump lets
the upconverted light bypass the end mirror, providing direct outcoupling. Secondly, to allow for stable, low noise
upconversion the wavelength of the pump laser must be stable. The angular wavelength separation from the prism
allows for the selection of a less than 1nm wide mode, and thus a more stable laser. Thirdly, the exact wavelength that
receives feedback can be controlled by turning the mirror M8, which gives some tuning capability. At 1908nm, the
refraction difference is 32.6µrad per nm, which means M8 must be turned 10.9µrad. per nm of tuning, which is possible
to control with a motorized mount. To couple the signal laser into the cavity, M6 and M7 was used for alignment of
the 1064nm signal, and L4 was a 250mm focal length spherical lens, focusing the light to a beam waist matching that
of the 1.9µm light in the PPLN. The surface reﬂection from the prism provided outcoupling from the cavity. During
operation, the wavelength or power of the 1.9µm laser, was monitored measuring this outcoupling, with an optical
spectrum analyser (Yokogawa AQ6375) or a thermal power meter.
3.
Results and Discussion
The tuning characteristics of the laser were investigated, while turning M8. The peak wavelengths seen during tuning
are shown in Figure 2, these are measured for the single modes shown in the inset. Notice that for each mode, the peak
wavelength is stable, over a substantial angle range. The modes are observed within the range 1902-1935nm, which is
==PAGE:76==
narrower than has been reported in [6,7]. This is a result of the low pump power.
The reason we observe discrete modes with variable spacing is the absorption lines of water vapour [6]. The ab-
sorption forces the laser to run at the transmission peaks of water vapour, and the measured positions of the modes
perfectly match these peaks. The maximum intracavity ﬁeld reached was 3.5W. This is lower than what has been
reported for the output power of Tm:YLF lasers [6, 8], albeit that these used signiﬁcantly higher pump power. With
this intracavity ﬁeld, we upconverted 1064nm light while varying the temperature of the PPLN crystal, the results are
shown in Figure 3. We reached a maximum conversion efﬁciency of 0.02%, compared to a theoretical maximum of
0.18%. The shortfall of the achieved conversion efﬁciency stems from the difﬁculty of overlapping the 1064nm focus
and the 1.9µm focus precisely. The reason for the low theoretical maximum is the low intracavity ﬁeld, which is a
result of the loss from surface reﬂections and absorption in the prism.
A solution could be to reduce the loss or increase the pump power. Reducing the absorption loss could be done by
choosing a different material for the prism. The surface reﬂection of the prism could be reduced, by having the prism
cut to exactly match the Brewster angle. The pump power could be increased by using a more powerful pump diode,
an array of pump diodes for unidirectional pumping, or two diodes or array of diodes for bi-directional pumping [8].
Currently we are implementing a change of the prism, to decrease the loss, and the addition of a second pumping LD,
to increase the available pump power.
4.
Conclusion
Tuning capabilities and good wavelength stability was demonstrated for the 1900nm-1930nm range. The supported
modes were found to be determined by the absorbtion of water vapour. Intracavity upconversion was demonstrated,
with an intracavity ﬁeld of 3.5W and a conversion efﬁciency of 0.02%. This was done using relatively inexpensive
components, with a power usage of ca. 10W. Future plans to improve this include changing the prism and increasing
the pump power, to increase the intracavity ﬁeld.
5.
Outlook
Apart from the immediate improvements in CO2 and CH4 detection, a longer wavelength pump also opens the possi-
bility of expanding intracavity upconversion further into the IR. Efﬁcient low noise intracavity upconversion requires
that the non-linear crystal used is transparent at both the pump wavelength, at the signal wavelength, and at the upcon-
verted wavelength. Most published work on upconversion detection use periodically poled lithium niobate (PPLN),
which is transparent up to ca. 4.5µm. Very few appropriate materials are transparent at both 1064nm and wavelength
longer than 4.5µm. Changing the pump from 1064nm to 1.9µm would open up a range of new non-linear materials.
The material of primary interest is AgGaS2, which is transparent up to ca. 11µm [1]. Intracavity upconversion in bulk
material opens the possibility of achieving fast spectroscopy of incoherent light at these wavelengths, with the same
method used in [2].
