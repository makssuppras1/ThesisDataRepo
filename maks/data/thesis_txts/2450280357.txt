Abstract 
The demand for plant-based alternatives to dairy products is growing exponentially and it 
entails the challenge of meeting consumer’s organoleptic and nutritional expectations. 
Furthermore, the environmental impact of dairy production compounds the need to develop 
new substitutes for animal milk and for fermented dairy products. The underlying question that 
led this study was if dairy-adapted starter cultures are poorly suitable for plant fermentations 
as compared to strains originating from plants.  
Thus, plant-isolated Lactococcus lactis and Leuconostoc spp. strains where characterized and 
screened for their fermentation performance in soy-based and oat-based drinks. Their ability to 
degrade model plant di- and oligosaccharides was tested and compared to commercial starter 
cultures Flora Danica (FD) and TCC-20. A genomic comparison among Leuconostoc spp. 
provided an overview of the carbohydrate-degrading enzymes they carry in their genomes, 
showing that they appear to remarkably encode genes for plant carbohydrates utilization and 
present diversity on a strain level e.g. for raffinose (GH36). Aiming to optimize fermentation 
of soy drink, dairy-adapted starter cultures were complemented with two plant-isolated 
Leuconostoc mesenteroides strains (strain 80 and strain 609) and inoculated in the substrate. 
Moreover, carbohydrate supplementation was effectuated to investigate potential 
improvements in fermentation time and pH drop. 
Both commercial cultures showed differences in the degradation of carbohydrates after 24 
hours. FD was not able to metabolize sucrose, xylose and maltose, and TCC-20 did not degrade 
raffinose, xylose and maltodextrin. Supplementation of soy drink with carbohydrates only 
improved their acidification performance when lactose and glucose were added. Ability of 
strain 609 to acidify markedly faster than FD in soy drink supplemented with xylose was 
revealed. Furthermore, all combinations of FD with strain 80 and with strain 609 in different 
ratios were able to acidify faster or reach a lower pH value than FD on its own.  
This study outlines the potential of a plant-isolated strain collection which could offer 
interesting applications for plant-based fermentations.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Interest in plant-based alternatives to dairy: sustainability 
approach 
The increase of global human population is accompanied by an excessive demand for food that 
the earth is not able to afford in the actual production conditions. In order to meet this demand, 
the industry should increase its production taking two of the following measures: expansion, 
which would require extra land and therefore encourage competition among producers to 
obtain it, or intensification, which would inevitably have a high environmental impact [1]. 
Therefore, the need for a sustainable food production system is clearly urgent [2]. 
One of the major causes of global warming are greenhouse gases (GHG) produced by the food 
industry, specially coming from the livestock sector. As one of the main contributors to climate 
change, it represents 18% of anthropogenic GHG emissions. Under the term ‘greenhouse 
gases’, the following compounds are compromised: methane from enteric fermentation, nitrous 
oxide from manure and fertilizer, carbon dioxide from land use changes and agricultural energy 
[3]. The main critical factors of livestock in agricultural environments that propitiate climate 
change are land use and changes in soil. These changes comprise eutrophication, generating 
excessive vegetable growth, and acidification, fostering species extinction due to severe 
ocean’s pH drop [4]. 
Emissions in the cattle dairy sector were 1969 million tons carbon dioxide equivalents (CO2eq) 
according to a Life Cycle Assessment conducted in 2007, of which 1328 million tons were 
attributed to milk production [5]. Furthermore, Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) 
estimated an average per capita global milk consumption of 100 kg of milk/year, with an 
excessive consumption of 300 kg/year in Western Europe [6]. Considering the amount of milk 
globally consumed and the amount of CO2eq generated for its production, the need for a more 
sustainable production system is imminent. 
In contrast to meat and dairy production, the production of plant-based foods generates less 
emissions and presents less land requirements per kg of produced product. For each kg of cow 
milk, 0,84-1,3 CO2eq are generated, whereas for oat drink and soy drink production, the 
generated CO2 eq per kg are 0,21 and 0,31 respectively [4]. 
The main interest of people in plant-based alternatives to dairy relies on health-related issues 
such as lactose intolerance or cow’s milk allergy, as well as veganism and cultural reasons in 
==PAGE:12==
2 
 
some countries [4]. Nonetheless, within the past years, an increasing concern about the 
environment has been observed and more people look for alternative options simply for 
sustainability reasons. 
Four out of nine planetary boundaries have already been crossed (climate change, biodiversity 
loss, changes in the global nitrogen cycle and land-system change) and therefore, and for the 
previously explained facts, a change towards mostly plant-based diets is extremely necessary. 
Research should focus on finding more efficient and novel ways to generate plant-based foods 
and alternatives to animal products that fulfill both nutritional and food cultural demands. 
1.2 Major challenges in fermented plant-based alternative products to 
dairy 
Nutritional challenges 
Dairy milk is a good source of macro and micronutrients such as proteins, vitamins and 
minerals. Therefore, a substitution with plant-based alternatives that generally do not contain 
them or just in low quantities, could be leading to vitamin D, calcium, phosphorus or protein 
deficiency that could cause severe health damages [7]. One of the most relevant risks of plant-
based diets is the lack of vitamin B12, obtained from animal origin foods such as fish, meat 
and eggs, as well as milk. Vegetarians can complement this deficiency with dairy products, 
improving vitamin B12 levels and decreasing cardiovascular risk [8], but strict plant-based 
diets might be facing nutrient deficits if the plant-based alternatives are not nutritionally 
complete. Regarding the protein content from commercialized plant-based alternatives to dairy, 
only soy drink reaches protein values high enough to replace cow milk (approx. 3,1 grams of 
protein per 100 grams of product). In contrast, other popular options such as rice or almond 
milk contain below 1 gram of protein per 100 grams of product [7, 9]. 
Health aspects, together with moral principles are some of the main reasons for consumer’s 
interest in plant-based products, but taste is the most important and decisive criteria when it 
comes to new food products purchases [4]. And there is where one of the challenges relies on: 
the organoleptic profile of plant-based alternatives to dairy. 
Organoleptic challenges 
Vegan consumers are generally open to new and innovative alternatives to animal products, 
but, as Mäkinen et al. stated in 2016, the ‘unwillingness of the mainstream consumer to try 
unfamiliar foods may be a limiting factor’. Regarding fermented plant-based alternatives to 
==PAGE:13==
3 
 
dairy, already commercialized products are generally not appealing, neither for vegans nor for 
regular consumers [4]. 
Fermented dairy products present unique flavor and aroma profiles that are extremely difficult 
to reproduce yet with the addition of flavorants. The main aromatic compounds generated 
through animal milk fermentation are diacetyl and lactic acid, defining the characteristic flavor 
or a fermented dairy product. Generally, the development of these compounds is not achieved 
in the production of fermented plant-based alternatives to dairy, either because fermentation is 
not occurring properly or because the products are simply a mixture of non-fermented 
ingredients. The aromas and flavors in this kind of products tend to reveal the original flavor 
of the main raw material. This remarks that if the plant-based yogurt is produced with oats, 
there will be a ‘starchy’ off flavor in the final product. Furthermore, fermentation of bean-
based milks such as soy drink or pea milk lead to difficulties in eliminating ‘beany’ off-flavors 
produced due to lipid oxidation or protein bitterness. However, their intensity can be controlled 
with processing and special storage conditions [4]. 
Regarding the texture, plant-based alternatives to fermented dairy are dense and heavier in 
mouth than dairy yogurts. This is due to protein curding in milk and different lipid composition 
in both substrates. Large insoluble particles from the grains used as raw material generate a 
chalky and, in some cases, unpleasant mouthfeel. Plant-based milks and dairy milk are two 
different matrixes and thus they have different physicochemical properties. Although bacterial 
fermentation can be achieved in both, obtaining a gel structure with plant-based drinks such as 
the one dairy milk offers is challenging. This occurs because the protein content in plant-based 
drinks is generally extremely low or even negligible (soy drink as an exception), and therefore 
it is not possible to obtain a structured network such as the one casein micelles generate in dairy 
yogurts. 
Technological challenges 
Plant-based alternatives to dairy milk are suspensions of grinded or mashed raw material 
(legumes, seeds, cereals) in water that resemble dairy milk’s appearance [4]. Therefore, in order 
to obtain a homogenized matrix, it is essential to control phenomena such as sedimentation, 
flocculation or fat separation to achieve stability [10]. 
A big challenge regarding the technology behind plant-based alternatives to fermented dairy is 
achieving product stability. This happens because there is a lack of proteins in plant-based 
substrates, and yet soy drinks with similar protein content as milk do not present the same 
==PAGE:14==
4 
 
functionality as dairy proteins. Some producers add texturizing agents such as hydrocolloids, 
for their ability to modify the viscosity and the texture of food products. They can have an 
impact in their rheology acting as thickeners or gelling agents. Also, some producers add 
emulsifiers such as vegetable oils to reduce phase separation and improve mouthfeel. 
Regarding shelf-life, the addition of acidulants generates anaerobic conditions and enhances 
probiotic cultures life, as Kumar et. al. stated in their study about trends in probiotics [11]. 
The technological challenge approached in this study is acidification in fermented plant-based 
raw materials. The problem relies on slow acidification processes and in some cases 
insufficient pH drop in comparison to dairy milk fermentations and it could be overcome by 
developing and optimizing new starter cultures suitable for plant substrates. 
Developing a plant-based yogurt that fulfils nutritional, organoleptic and technological 
requirements of dairy yogurt is a big challenge. However, lactic acid bacteria, suitable raw 
materials and food ingredients are the key to achieve its nutritional value, as well as improve 
texture and flavor of plant-based alternatives to dairy [12]. 
1.3 Raw materials of interest 
With an increasing demand for plant-based alternatives to dairy products, the search for the 
optimal raw materials includes a wide variety of substrates with different chemical 
compositions, with the goal of mimicking milk’s functionality in yogurts as well as achieving 
a pleasant taste for consumers. Table 1 shows a classification of the raw materials of interest 
in the actual plant-based dairy alternatives landscape, organized in cereal-based, legume-based, 
nut-based, seed-based and pseudo-cereal-based substrates.

A common aspect of all raw materials comprised in this classification is the presence of 
oligosaccharides in their chemical composition.  
Oligosaccharides are polymeric chains formed by more than two monosaccharides 
(monomers). The main oligosaccharides present in the legumes of interest are raffinose and 
stachyose, as well as verbascose in smaller amounts [13]. They are short chain polysaccharides 
formed by 3, 4 and 5 carbon atoms, respectively. In the cereals of interest, starch-derived 
oligosaccharides such as maltodextrin predominate. Some oligosaccharides are considered 
prebiotics for their beneficial health effects in human microbiota. However, raffinose, 
stachyose and verbascose are attributed antinutritive properties, as they are non-digestible in 
human intestine, and fall into the category of FODMAPs (Fermentable Oligo-, Di- and 
Monosaccharides and Polyols). They travel through the digestive tract and arrive intact to the 
intestine, where they are fermented by the gastrointestinal microbiota, generating gas that 
causes flatulence and abdominal pain [14]. 
Another common aspect among all raw materials mentioned in Table 1 is that all of them lack 
any competence; either their protein content is too low, they are not able to create gel structures, 
or they generate off-flavors.  
So far, there is no evidence that the perfect substitute for dairy milk that covers its nutritional, 
as well as its functional properties, has been found. However, there are some candidates that 
have acquired popularity in the production of plant-based alternatives to dairy: 
Soy 
Soy beans are an excellent source of nutrients whose ‘milk’ has been the most popular plant-
based alternative to dairy milk for decades. Although it can be an allergen for some consumers, 
it presents the highest protein content among all plant-based milks (2,5-3,1 g per 100 ml [9]), 
and its amino acid profile is the only one that gets closer to bovine milk. Chalupa et. al proved 
this in their study, where the protein digestibility-corrected amino acid score of bovine milk 
was 100 and soy drink and pea milk were the only plant-based substrates getting closer to this 
value, with averages of 94,6 and 78,4, respectively. [9] 
In contrast to this beneficial aspect, soy contains a high amount of non-digestible 
oligosaccharides (NDOs) such as raffinose and stachyose in about 4-6% of soybean dry mass. 
Their digestion in human intestine can be very weak if there is an α-galactosidase deficit due 
to genetic mutations or to age factors, and they have gained some bad reputation for releasing 
==PAGE:16==
6 
 
gas and generating bloating and gastrointestinal disorders, as Leblanc et. al highlighted in 2004 
[15]. Moreover, some studies reflect a potential negative effect of soymilk phytates in mineral 
absorption, as well as potential development of allergy towards soy proteins from infants that 
present allergy to dairy milk proteins [16]. 
Regarding its fat content, soy drink presents lower saturated fat content than milk and higher 
mono- and polyunsaturated fat content [9]. It also contains isoflavones and phytosterols, which 
are known for having protective effects against cardiovascular diseases [17]. However, this 
alternative to animal milk is not very accepted in some cultures due to its characteristic beany 
flavor, generated by pentanal and n-hexanal [18]. 
Soy drinks are good candidates for yogurt-like products, as they enable curd formation after 
fermentation but their off-flavor, as well as the low acidification, still leave room for 
improvement in their application [19]. 
Oat 
In the past years, oat drink has gained popularity due to its high dietary fiber content and its 
health benefits. Oat soluble fibers β-glucans are considered prebiotic since they are feed for the 
intestinal microflora. They also act as stabilizing agents, increasing product viscosity and 
contributing to its stability. Moreover, they reduce total and LDL (low density lipoproteins) 
blood cholesterol levels and keep satiety levels stable [17,16]. In contrast, oat drink is deficient 
in protein (less than 1 gram per 100 ml of product). 
Oat lipids are responsible for the typical off-flavor of this cereal. Their oxidation, together with 
the heterocyclic compounds generated in oat processing, gives oat drink a characteristic flavor 
that is generally not accepted by consumers [20].  
1.4 Lactic acid bacteria and their functionalities in food fermentation 
Lactic acid bacteria are a group of gram-positive, non-spore-forming, facultative anaerobic 
bacteria whose main carbohydrate fermentation product is lactate. They lack cytochrome and 
are catalase-, oxidase- and benzidine-negative [21]. Despite being mesophilic, some lactic acid 
bacteria can grow at low temperatures (<5ºC) and some at high temperatures (<45ºC), as well 
as at different pH ranges (3,2 – 9,6).  Their primary energy source are carbohydrates, although 
they need other nutrients such as B group vitamins and amino acids, puric and pyrimidine bases 
to grow [22].

LAB used in food are characterized in two main groups: rods (Lactobacillus) and cocci 
(Lactococcus, Streptococcus, Pediococcus, Leuconostoc and Oenococcus). Based on the 
products of their carbohydrate metabolism, lactic acid bacteria are considered 
homofermentative or heterofermentative. 
Homofermentative lactic acid bacteria generate one single product by fermenting glucose: 
lactic acid. This group includes cocci such as Lactococcus and S. salivarius subsp. 
thermophilus, as well as the rods Lb. delbruckii subsp. bulgaricus and Lactobacillus 
acidophilus [24]. For their performance in dairy milk, where they can rapidly generate lactic 
acid and reduce pH, they are used as starter cultures for fermented dairy products. 
Heterofermentative lactic acid bacteria generate more than one by-product by fermenting 
glucose, namely lactic acid, carbon dioxide and ethanol. They include cocci such as 
Leuconostoc spp. and the rods Lb. brevis, Lb. fermentum. Lb. reuteri. Lb. plantarum, Lb. casei 
and Lb. curvatus are facultatively heterofermentative bacteria that are also used in food-grade 
applications [24]. Due to reduced diversity in dairy isolated and greater metabolic diversity in 
plant isolates, heterofermentative LAB might be of interest for plant fermentations.

Generally, LAB present ‘GRAS’ status (Generally Regarded As Safe) as they are considered 
safe microorganisms [25]. However, this qualification is limited to a specific application. For 
an overall safety certification, QPS status is required, which is described in 1.6. 
Fermentation of plant raw materials has been practiced since ancient times. However, as the 
focus of this study relies on plant-based alternative to dairy, a model of lactic acid fermentation 
for a yogurt-like product will be examined.  
LAB contribute to food preservation, as well as to sensorial improvement in terms of flavor, 
aroma and texture [23]. 
From a preservation approach, lactic acid fermentation has been widely used to preserve animal 
milk in the form of yogurt and cheese. LAB extend the shelf life of the final product, generating 
metabolites with beneficial effects. Food spoilage prevention is achieved with the production 
of antimicrobial and antifungal compounds that inhibit the growth of undesirable and 
pathogenic microorganisms [29]. Also, the pH decrease acidifies the media and hinders their 
growth.  
From an organoleptic approach, LAB can generate an extended portfolio of aromatic 
compounds, endowing fermented products with characteristic flavors and aromas [11]. 
Through fermentation, a range of alcohols, aldehydes and acids are obtained from pyruvate 
precursors and amino acids metabolism. When LAB die, they undergo lysis and thus they 
release enzymes and metabolites that contribute to complex flavor development also in dry and 
solid environments that have lost their water content with time, such as aged cheeses [35]. 
Characteristic aroma profiles from LAB fermentations are originated with the breakdown of 
the predominant macronutrients in the food matrix. Carbohydrates, proteins, and lipidic 
components of the raw material being metabolized are the initial point for the development of 
complex flavors. LAB also have the capacity to produce exopolysaccharides (EPS), 
polysaccharides that may improve texture in fermented products, increasing viscosity and 
improving stability. EPS production is affected by temperature and incubation time. It was 
demonstrated by Ben Ounis in 2008 that low temperatures stimulate exopolysaccharides 
synthesis in S. thermophilus, Lb. bulgaricus and Lac. lactis subsp. cremoris [36]. The presence 
of these polysaccharides improves texture characteristics such as creaminess and mouth 
fullness [37]. This feature can enable the replacement of thickening agents [36, 38]. 
From a health approach, lactic acid bacteria became more popular among regular consumers, 
as well as among the food industry, with their association to the term ‘probiotics’ in the early

90s. As the World Health Organization defines, probiotics are “live microorganisms which 
when administered in adequate amounts confer a health benefit on the host”.  The mechanism 
probiotics use to benefit human health haven’t been completely defined yet. However, three 
potential modes of actions have been defined by Oelschlaeger: I) probiotics could modulate 
the immunologic system of their host. II) probiotics could directly affect other microorganisms, 
III) probiotics could affect microbial products or food components [26]. The functionality of

e.g. lactose digestion with β-galactosidase [27]. 
1.4.1. Macromolecules utilization in Lactic Acid Bacteria: Carbohydrate metabolism 
Lactic acid bacteria, as every other living organism, need energy to proliferate and survive. 
Lipids, vitamins and amino acids present in the environment they live in, are essential to 
produce adenosine triphosphate (ATP) in anaerobic or aerobic conditions. However, the energy 
source for its production are carbohydrates. The two main metabolic processes where these 
compounds are involved as energy sources are respiration and fermentation.  
During respiration, microorganisms degrade simple carbohydrates, such as glucose, with 
oxidation-reduction (redox) reactions, generating CO2 and water as final products. For this 
process, the presence of oxygen is a requirement, as it is the final electron acceptor before being 
reduced to water. This process comprises three phases: (1) Glycolysis, where glucose is 
oxidized to pyruvate, generating 2 ATPs and NADHs, and pyruvate is converted to acetate, 
generating 2 NADHs; (2) Citric acid cycle, where acetate is metabolized generating 2ATPs, 
6NADHs and 2 FADHs; and (3) Electron Transport chain, where the obtained NADHs and 
FADHs are oxidized and donate their electrons to all the elements in the chain until reaching 
O2, which is transformed into water. The energy that the electrons transfer while they travel 
through the transport chain, allows the pumping of H+ from the matrix to the intermembrane 
space through proton pumps. These H+ return to the matrix through the different complexes in 
the transport chain (NADH coenzyme Q reductase (I), succinate dehydrogenase (II), 
cytochrome bc1 (III) and cytochrome c oxidase (IV)), complex protein structures that profit 
the energy of the proton flow to act as ATP-synthetases and charge ATP molecules. Respiration 
has a high energetic yield, generating 38 ATP molecules per molecule of glucose. This is 
enabled by substrate level-phosphorylation (oxidation of glucose) and oxidative 
phosphorylation (NADH and FADH2 oxidation). 
==PAGE:20==
10 
 
LAB have never developed the ability to produce heme, an organic compound containing a 
central iron atom that plays an important role in respiration, both aerobic and anaerobic [41, 
42]. Heme prosthetic groups are present as cofactors in hemeproteins with enzymatic activity 
such as cytochromes, catalases, peroxidases. As a result of this incapability, some pathogenic 
LAB species utilize their host’s reserves as a source of heme to increase oxygen supply and 
therefore accelerate their metabolism, still non-pathogenic LAB will survive with lower 
energetic yields [42].   
However, lactic acid bacteria developed another method to obtain energy: fermentation. It is 
an anaerobic process where carbohydrates are metabolized without oxygen requirements, 
generating an organic acid as main product (lactate in case of homofermentation and lactate, 
acetate and ethanol in case of heterofermentation). In contrast to aerobic respiration, 
fermentation has a low energetic yield, generating 2 ATP molecules on average from one 
glucose molecule, as it is only possible to obtain energy through substrate-level 
phosphorylation.  
In summary, to obtain energy through fermentation, lactic acid bacteria utilize carbohydrate 
metabolism.  
As stated by Kandler et. al [28], “carbohydrate conversion to lactic acid by lactic acid bacteria 
may be considered as the most important fermentation process employed in food technology”. 
As mentioned above, carbohydrates are the main source of energy for lactic acid bacteria, 
although there are different existing pathways that lead to different final products, as well as 
different complexity degrees between the utilized carbohydrates. 
Metabolism of monosaccharides 
Monosaccharides are the simplest form of carbohydrate and therefore, the preferred one for 
LAB to obtain energy. Three main pathways have been identified as direct processes to obtain 
energy from hexoses, being glucose the preferred. 
One of them is glycolysis, an homofermentative process where this monosaccharide is 
degraded to pyruvate and one single product is generated: lactate. The yield of this set of 
reactions is 2 ATP, as well as 2 pyruvates per 1 molecule of glucose. The other two pathways 
are heterofermentative pathways: the Bifidus pathway, where glucose is degraded generating 
2,5 ATP, 1,5 moles of acetate and 1 mole of lactate [29], and the 6P-Gluconate pathway, which 
yields 1 ATP and 2 moles of ethanol [28]

Metabolism of disaccharides 
In the case of disaccharides such as lactose, formed by a molecule of glucose bonded by a beta-
1,4-glycosidic linkage to a molecule of galactose, fermentation occurs following the glycolysis 
pathway. After lactose molecules enter the cytoplasm with the help of carbohydrate 
transporters such as phosphor-transferase systems or permeases, the enzyme β-galactosidase 
breaks the glycosidic bond between glucose and galactose. 
 These monosaccharides are degraded into pyruvate through the tagatose-6P-pathway, the 
glycolysis pathway or the Leloir pathway. In the case of other disaccharides, also an initial step 
where a specific enzyme breaks the linkage between both monosaccharides is required.

Sucrose is a disaccharide present in soybeans in proportions of 68 mg per gram. Literature

Metabolism of oligosaccharides 
Metabolic pathways for simple carbohydrates have been deeply studied and well understood. 
Nevertheless, the unreliable specificity of carbohydrate transport systems and glycoside 
hydrolases hinders the clear understanding of oligosaccharides metabolism in LAB [34]. 
Raffinose (one galactose molecule, one glucose molecule and one fructose molecule) and 
stachyose (two galactose molecules, one glucose molecule and one fructose) derive from 
sucrose and are comprised in the Raffinose Family Oligosaccharides (RFOs), together with 
verbascose, another oligosaccharide from lupins and peas [35]. They are generally present in 
plant material such as cotton, seeds or beans, playing roles in carbohydrate transport and

1.4.2. Flora Danica and TCC-20. 
Flora Danica (FD) is a mesophilic aromatic culture known for its flavor and CO2 production. 
The bacterial strains it contains correspond to Lac. lactis subsp. cremoris, Lac. lactis subsp. 
lactis, Lac. lactis subsp. lactis biovar diacetylactis and Leuconostoc. It is not specified which 
species of Leuconostoc they are. The main application of this culture is in cheese production, 
either ‘continental cheese’ types such as Gouda or Edam, or soft cheeses such as Camembert 
or Blue cheese [40]. 
TCC-20 is a thermophilic lactic acid culture containing Lb. helveticus and S. thermophilus 
strains. The incubation temperature that it requires is higher than FD. It is a medium to fast-
acidifying culture and it is generally applied in ‘pasta filata’ cheese types such as mozzarella, 
providing them with sweet and nutty flavor [40].

1.5. Genomics of Lactic Acid Bacteria 
Due to the exceptional attributes of lactic acid bacteria, not only in the food industry but also 
in other contexts such as biorefinery in cell factories, hundreds of genomes of this bacterial 
order have already been sequenced and published for wide research purposes [25, 41]. The 
discovery of novel genes and proteins, as well as metabolic pathways, is a key factor to explore 
the immense applicability of LAB. Therefore, the availability of these genomic sequences is 
essential to identify genomic variations that can lead to unknown qualities of these bacteria. 
Over the years and with consequent studies, it has been shown that bacteria experience gene 
loss and gene acquisition [42]. A potential reason could be the process of adaptation to 
nutritionally rich environments such as foods or the human intestinal tract, as Wu et. Al 
suggested in 2017. One of the examples they give is the case of S. thermophilus, a species that 
showed genome reduction in carbon utilization genes, which correlates to the low level of 
carbon in the habitats where it grows, such as dairy milk. 
One of the main purposes of understanding lactic acid bacteria genome is to study their amino 
acid metabolism, as they are the origin of flavor and aroma compounds of fermented food 
products. Sulfuric amino acids metabolism generates savory flavors such as the ones present 
in meat and garlic; degraded aromatic amino acids produce floral and chemical flavors and 
branched-chain amino acids (BCAA) catabolism develops fruity and malty flavors [25]. 
Another purpose of lactic acid bacteria genomics is the discovery and understanding of new 
and existing bacteriocins. These compounds, together with enzymes, lytic agents and other 
metabolic products, contribute to food preservation and safety of fermented foods [43]. 
In correlation to this study, LAB genomics provide an overview of the glycoside hydrolases 
families that are present in each species of the genus Leuconostoc, with differences within 
subspecies. Knowing the genes they carry, it is possible to understand which enzymes they 
could be able to synthesize and which carbohydrates they should be able to degrade. 
1.6. QPS status of Lactic Acid Bacteria 
Lactic acid bacteria have a long history in food applications and most of them are identified as 
safe microbes. However, new species have been discovered over time and screened for their 
potential use in food fermentations. Thus, it is essential that they undergo a safety assessment 
to avoid risks for human health. This assessment, carried by the European Food Safety 
Authority (EFSA), covers risk for human, animals and the environment [44] and it is updated 
every year with pertinent changes relying on the discovery of potential negative effects of any

1.7. Aim of the study 
The aim of this study was finding plant-isolated lactic acid bacteria strains with potential for 
plant fermentations. With these strains, new plant-based starter cultures can be developed, and 
commercial starter cultures can be complemented to cover a broader performance range.  
It was intended to show performance differences between dairy-adapted starter cultures and 
plant-derived strains in carbohydrate degradation, as well as identifying which ones yield faster 
acidification profiles, as typical in yogurt fermentation.

2. Materials and Methods 
2.1. Identification of strains 
90 bacterial strains, previously isolated from plant material and purified by the Research Group 
of Microbial Biotechnology and Biorefining were streaked on De Man, Rogosa and Sharpe 
agar (MRS), M17 and skim milk agar plates to determine their identity with mass spectrometry.  
For the preparation of MRS and M17 agar plates, 62 grams of MRS agar and 50 grams of M17 
agar formulations (Sigma Aldrich, composition in Appendix I) were diluted in 1 liter and 950 
milliliters distilled water, respectively, and autoclaved. M17 agar media required addition of 
50 sterile 10% w/v lactose solution, following the protocol provided by the producer. 10 
milliliters of sterile filtered glucose (10% w/v) were added after autoclavation. For the 
preparation of milk plates, 20 grams of plate count skim milk agar formulation (Sigma Aldrich) 
were mixed with 24 grams of skim milk powder (Sigma Aldrich) and diluted in 1 liter of 
distilled water, followed by autoclavation. The selected strains were streaked on MRS, M17 
and milk agar plates with sterile plastic inoculation loops and incubated at 30oC for 48 hours. 
When individual colonies were visible, a representative sample of bacterial colony was taken 
with the tip of a sterilized metal inoculation loop and was placed on polished steel MALDI 
target plates from Bruker Daltonik GmbH, overlaid by 1 µl of a specific matrix (Bruker HCCA 
solution) that crystalized while drying and immobilized the bacterial samples. The plate was 
air dried and introduced in the MALDI Biotyper CA System from Bruker Daltonik, where the 
analysis was performed by the MALDI Biotyper software.

3. Results and discussion 
3.1. Diversity of species in the collection of plant-isolated bacteria  
This strain identification began as part of a project preceding to this master thesis and it was 
performed together with other members of the Research Group for Microbial Biotechnology 
and Biorefining. With MALDI-TOF-MS analysis (matrix assisted laser desorption ionization-
time of flight-mass spectrometry) it was possible to identify different genus in the strain 
collection with protein analysis, through the creation of a mass spectrum that is specific to each 
genus and species [51]. 
Although the isolates have been previously streaked and purified several times, some of the 
strains still seemed to be impure. Colonies that were supposed to be from the same strain, 
presented different micromorphology (colony sizes and colors) after 48 hours incubation on 
agar plates. From each sample that seemed to be impure, one colony of each growing strain 
was grown in 5ml M17 broth for 24 hours and a stock solution was prepared with 50% glycerol 
and the overnight grown culture (1:1). Both strains were subjected to identification again. 
Glucose was added to the M17 media formulation to assure the growth of strains that cannot 
utilize lactose, the carbohydrate that is already present in the formulation provided by Sigma 
Aldrich. Growth was observed in MRS, M17 as well as in milk agar plates. 
So far, 453 plant isolates have been isolated and characterized by genus. Figure 6 shows the 
diversity of genus that have been identified in total, not only the ones identified during this 
project, with the purpose of reflecting the wide variety of bacteria that can be found in plant 
material.

The results of this characterization were performed with mass spectrophotometry as mentioned 
in 2.1. as a more rapid alternative to 16S sequencing [52]. This type of analysis matches the

As it was expected, in media with glucose as the only carbon source, FD and TCC-20 showed 
ability to grow. Since glucose is a simple carbohydrate and the main energy source for LAB, it 
was easy to metabolize for both. However, TCC-20 almost doubled FD’s OD600 value after 24 
hours, which could potentially mean that it showed a better growth performance utilizing this 
carbohydrate. Alternative explanations could be a higher bacterial concentration in TCC-20 
sample or the fact that the media could sustain higher OD600 values for the different species 
present in this culture. 
In media with sucrose as only energy source, TCC-20 culture was more efficient. OD600 was 
measured after 24 hours and it reached 2,5, while FD reported 0,4 but showed growth after 48 
hours. Previous results show that S. thermophilus and L. helveticus, the components of TCC-
20, can successfully metabolize sucrose, according to these results [53, 54]. 
As expected, both cultures were able to grow in lactose, the main carbohydrate present in the 
media they are designed to grow in (milk). Nevertheless, TCC-20 culture reached higher OD600 
values than FD. TCC-20 is a culture used in yogurt production, where fermentation times are 
shorter than other dairy fermentations such as cheese production. This could be an explanation 
why these strains are able to grow faster. 
Regarding their growth in media with oligosaccharides, different performances were observed. 
In the case of raffinose, FD showed an outstanding ability to metabolize it after 24 hours in 
comparison to TCC-20, surpassing OD600 values of 2,5, while the second one remained at 0,2 
and started growing after 24 hours. 
In media with xylose as the only carbon source, both cultures revealed low ability to utilize this 
carbohydrate in the first 24 hours of incubation, not reaching OD600 values of 1. Despite this, 
TCC-20 started growing after 24 hours reaching an OD600 value of 3, following a similar pattern 
as in media with raffinose. 
Their performance in media with D-glucose polymers as carbon sources was interestingly 
different between them. Regarding maltose utilization, TCC-20 culture showed much better 
ability to grow than FD, almost doubling growth already after 24 hours. This disaccharide 
formed by two glucoses bound by an α-1-4 linkage needs the presence of α-glucosidase to be 
degraded. An explanation for this performance could be that the species present in FD do not 
present GH families with this enzyme in their genome and therefore they do not have the ability 
to metabolize it. In contrast, FD was outstandingly efficient utilizing maltodextrin. The sample 
inoculated with this culture surpassed OD600 values of 2 after 24 hours of incubation, while 
==PAGE:43==

TCC-20 stayed at 0,5 at the same time point. However, the second culture reached OD600 values 
as high as FD but in twice its time.

This experiment was initially performed in 96-well plates with M17 media and OD600 was

after incubation and the results were showing potential background growth or contamination,

encourages bacteria to use the added carbohydrates instead of other compounds from M17 and

In media with glucose as energy source, all Lac. lactis strains grew to OD600 values above 1,

Regarding growth in SA media with lactose, almost all strains were able to grow reaching 
OD600 values slightly above 1. Strain 280 and 391 showed higher growth. However, MG1363 
and IL1403 were not able to grow in this media, due to their inability to metabolize lactose as 
they lack lactose operon [55]. This fact can be used as a negative control, as well. One of the 
plant isolates, 566, was also not able to grow in this media, potentially lacking the genes that 
codify for β-galactosidase. 
In SA media with raffinose, neither the plant-isolated Lac. lactis strains nor the known Lac. 
lactis strains were able to grow, except from KF147, reaching an OD600 value of 1,1. This can 
be explained with the complexity of raffinose, as these species need a specific transporter to 
metabolize this, but KF147 presents the ability to degrade α-galactosides [56]. It encodes 
specific mobile genetic elements encoding α-galactosidase and a transporter and therefore, it 
was able to grow. 
In concordance to this competence of KF147, this strain was also able to grow in xylose, as 
well as IL1403 and IO-1, correlating to literature findings [57]. Growth in SA media with 
xylose presented different patterns in each strain. MG1363, the most studied strain among all 
LAB research, is known for not being able to grow in xylose and this is confirmed by the results 
reflected in graph D. NCDO2118 was able to grow only after 24 hours of incubation. Regarding 
the plant isolates, small growth was observed among them (OD600 values below 1), except from 
strain 280, that showed an OD600 value of 1,9.

3.3. Comparative genomics within Leuconostoc species 
A genomic approach was given to the study to analyze occurrence and diversity of 
carbohydrate-degrading genes in Leuconostoc. The goal was to observe if the selected LAB 
strains carry them, and if so, which species present them. Due to the time frame of this project, 
it has not been possible to sequence and analyze the genomes of the selected isolates. However, 
the genomes of some Leuconostoc spp. among those isolates were analyzed using public 
genome data from NCBI database. Geneious was used as a bioinformatic predictive tool to 
compare them. The hypothesis of this analysis is that the species who carry glycoside hydrolase 
genes for a specific carbohydrate degradation in their genome will be able to metabolize it 
when present in the media they grow e.g. GH36 encodes α-galactosidase and thus the 
occurrence of a GH36 could correlate with the ability to utilize raffinose. However, some GH 
families e.g. GH42 host multiple specificities and therefore caution must be taken to correlate 
occurrence within this family. 
To test this hypothesis, the carbohydrate-active enzyme database CAZy (www.cazy.org) was 
used to obtain information about each of the strains and determine which of them present genes 
that catalyze carbohydrates breakdown. This databased provides a classification in families of 
the glycoside hydrolase enzymes present in different organisms such as bacteria, viruses or 
eukaryotic cells. Glycoside hydrolases are key enzymes of carbohydrate metabolism and have 
been distributed in 162 families depending on the similarities of their primary sequences, and 
therefore their putative functionality [62].   
Table 20 in Appendix IV shows a comprehensive classification of the different GH families 
present in the Leuconostoc genomes selected for this study, showing each series of enzymes 
that each of the families comprises as may serve as a reference for the coming discussion. Table 
12 in this report reflects the main activity of each glycoside hydrolase family present in all 
Leuconostoc species, in some cases regarding carbohydrate degradation and in others relating 
to basic cellular functions.

Evaluating the GH families these subspecies carry in their genomes, it is observed that GH13

about their functions at this point. GH13 comprises enzymes responsible for the degradation 
of substrates with α-glucoside linkages, namely α-amylases for starch degradation. GH73 
contains enzymes playing an important role in cell wall transformation during vegetative 
growth, respectively. Their presence correlates to the need of metabolizing starch and other 
amylolytic compounds present in their most common habitat (plants) to obtain energy, as well 
as to the changes their cellular structure undergoes during their growth. Families GH23 and 
GH25 are also present in all subspecies. They comprise enzymes such as lysozymes, whose 
activity is mainly structural, metabolizing compounds such as peptidoglycan and chitin in the 
cell wall. GH32 and GH70 are present in almost all Leuconostoc subspecies and they contain 
enzymes responsible for simple carbohydrate metabolism such as invertases, which convert 
sucrose into fructose and glucose, and transglucosylases, which catabolize the transformation 
of one glucoside into another, respectively. Regarding disaccharide metabolizing enzymes, all 
species present GH1. This family involves β-galactosidases among others, which are lactose-
degrading enzymes. However, some exceptions such as Leu. cremoris, Leu. carnosum and Leu. 
fallax, potentially lack β-galactosidase as they are the only ones that do not present neither 
GH2 nor GH42, unlike the rest of the species. That correlates to the low growth in media with 
lactose shown in 3.2.3. Furthermore, family GH68 comprises sucrose-degrading enzymes, and 
they are only present in Leu. mesenteroides and Leu. gelidium, reflecting that species such as 
Leu. lactis or Leu. citreum would not have the ability to metabolize sucrose unless they have 
sucrose-degrading enzymes from other families such as GH32, as mentioned above. GH36, 
family of interest for this study for its direct relationship with raffinose metabolism, is present 
in most of the Leu. mesenteroides and in Leu. gelidium, although the number of sequences is 
low. It should be highlighted that Leu. citreum genome does not contain GH36, which 
correlates to the results from 3.2.3., where the plant-isolates from this species did not grow in 
raffinose.

3.4. Ingredient composition of selected substrates 
Four already commercialized plant-based drinks provided by Naturli Foods were used for the 
experiments for being reproducible substrates, namely, unsweetened soy drink, oat drink, rice 
drink and almond drink. In addition, Hemp milk was included in some of the experiments due 
to its lately raised interest. Initially, their ingredient composition was compared based on their 
labeling. Table 19 in Appendix III shows the nutritional composition of the substrates. 
Despite the products are called by the name of the raw material, the main ingredient in all of 
them is water. The percentage of cereal or legume in the product varies from 3 to 12%. In the 
case of soy drink, the only ingredients are soybeans and water, and no sugar is added, keeping 
the ingredient formulation simple and aligned with the ‘clean label’ concept. In contrast with 
this two-ingredient product, oat drink also contains inulin, a soluble fiber that increases the 
density of the drink, and sunflower oil, acting as emulsifier between water and oats. Rice drink 
does not contain inulin and therefore its texture is lighter and waterier. Yet, it still contains oil 
to blend the cereal with water. Almond milk and hemp milk present 5 and 3 extra ingredients 
respectively to meet consumer’s organoleptic expectations. They include stabilizing agents 
such as guar gum, thickening agents such as tapioca starch and emulgents such as sunflower 
==PAGE:60==
50 
 
lecithin. For this reason, almond milk and hemp milk were discarded for the screening 
experiments, as their ingredients could interact with bacterial growth and hinder the study of 
carbohydrate breakdown. Soy drink was selected as substrate of interest due to its satisfactory 
nutritional profile and its simple composition. Regarding oat drink and rice drink, the first one 
was included in the study, as oats are commonly harvested in Denmark and its production could 
be more sustainable and contribute to the use of local resources. Rice was discarded because 
of its higher sugar content, which is not the target for bacterial fermentation in this study. 
It should be considered that since the manufacturer did not provide with information regarding 
production processes, it was not possible to know the exact carbohydrate composition of the 
standardized product. Therefore, the results of these analysis lead to estimates and assumptions 
regarding which carbohydrates are present in the plant-based drinks. 
Due to thermal or enzymatic treatments in the production process of plant-based alternatives 
to dairy milk, their nutritional content may differ from the theoretically estimated one (based 
on the nutrients present in the main raw material). For this reason, the samples were subjected 
to high performance liquid chromatography to identify some of the carbohydrates they contain.  
3.4.1. Analysis of carbohydrates present in plant-based substrates 
Soy drink and oat drink from Naturli Foods were analyzed with HPLC to identify their 
carbohydrate composition. The technology behind this method is explained in Appendix II. 
The output of the HPLC experiment is a chromatogram where the absorption of the analytes is 
expressed over the retention time (time between the injection of the solvent and its detection). 
This temporal variable depends on several factors such as the pressure affecting the flow rate, 
the nature of the stationary phase (material and particle size), the composition of the solvent 
and the temperature inside the column. Those parameters need to be defined before starting the 
experiment. A series of peaks in the chromatogram represent the different compounds in the 
mixture that have absorbed different UV light amounts and have been detected at different 
times. The area under the peaks is proportional to the amount of compound that went through 
the column. To identify the compounds, it is necessary to use a retention time reference for 
each of them. Therefore, the standard solutions with different known carbohydrates were 
prepared and analyzed. Figure 17 shows an example of the chromatogram in which the results 
of an HPLC essay are obtained.

3.7. Effect of supplementation with carbohydrates in soy drink 
fermentation with commercial starter cultures 
Soy drink was spiked with different carbohydrates to observe their effect in acidification. As it 
can be seen in Figure 20, soy drink fermentation with dairy cultures presents a different timing 
when certain carbohydrates are added to the substrate. Nevertheless, not all scenarios are 
optimal for what this study is focusing on. 
 
Figure 20 Soy drink fermentation with supplementation of different carbohydrates by Flora Danica (A) and TCC-20 (B). 
Plain soy drink (●); soy drink supplemented with glucose (●); sucrose (●); lactose (●), raffinose (●) and xylose (●) 
Graph A represents fermentation of soy drink with FD and it can be observed that glucose and 
lactose supplementation are the only combinations that increase the acidification rate, and all 
carbohydrates but sucrose entailed a slightly deeper pH drop. The fact that acidification 
increased under these conditions suggests that carbohydrates naturally present in soy drink 
could be a limiting factor for the tested cultures [19]. On the one hand, adding glucose to 
commercialized soy drink is what is generally done in already commercialized products to 
produce fermented plant-based products. Added carbohydrates do not have to be labelled if

relies on avoiding this practice and fermenting complex carbohydrates already present in the 
substrates. On the other hand, adding lactose to soy drink is not considered as an option, since 
consumers expect this product to be not only plant-based but also lactose-free. Supplementation 
with sucrose, raffinose or xylose did not show any improvement in acidification in comparison 
to soy drink without supplementation, at least under the incubation temperature and time 
restrictions of this experiment.  
Fermentation of supplemented soy drink with TCC-20 is reflected in Graph B. No 
improvement was observed in samples spiked with any of the carbohydrates, since the best 
acidification was showed in plain soy drink. However, if the commercial starter cultures did 
not utilize complex spiked carbohydrates such as raffinose, it could potentially be because other 
compounds present in soy drink are being metabolized first. As Mital et al. suggest, the 
preference for simple carbohydrates results in a pH drop that is fast enough to avoid utilization

FD was not able to metabolize xylose and maltose in a timeframe of 24 hours. In correlation to 
that, from the plant-isolated bacteria, strain 80 can degrade xylose and maltose, and strain 609 
can degrade the second one. Therefore, the combinations were designed using these two Leu. 
mesenteroides strains and supplementation of xylose and maltose. The results are explained 
below. 
It can be noticed that the initial pH value plotted in all graphs appears after 1,6 hours of 
incubation and therefore it is different in all combinations. The pH value at t=0 should be the 
same for all samples, 7,1 in the case of soy milk. Unexpectedly, the fluorescence values of the 
samples in the first sampling times (before 1,6 h) exceeded the fluorescence values of the 
standards. This should not happen since the maximum calibration standard should correspond 
to a pH 9. The reason for this contradictory result could probably be an error in the calibration 
standards, which apparently did not relate to their expected acidity and alkalinity. Despite this 
and because the pH drop occurs later than 1,6 hours, it was possible to plot the data and observe 
different acidification profiles in all combinations. 
As it can be observed in Graph A from Figure 25, supplementation of soy drink with xylose 
did not remarkedly affect acidification by FD. FD combined with strain 80, which resulted to 
be optimal at xylose degradation, achieved much better acidification in plain soy drink, 
reaching pH values of 4,5 after 5 hours. Furthermore, this combination reached even lower pH 
in soy drink spiked with xylose, getting stable at around pH 3,9. Despite that, it can be remarked 
that it took slightly more time than in media without xylose supplementation to reach the lowest 
pH value.  These results point to the likelihood that, in case there is any xylose in soybean 
slurries before being processed, they would not need to be pre-treated to eliminate this complex 
carbohydrate, as a combination of FD and strain 80 could potentially metabolize it and ferment 
the product.   
Regarding supplementation of soy drink with maltose (graph B from Figure25), acidification 
time unexpectedly increased in comparison to plain soy drink. It could be attributed to bacteria 
trying to degrade this complex carbohydrate into small oligosaccharides and therefore spending 
more time until they are able to ferment them and decrease the pH of the samples. However, a 
combination of FD with strain 609 reflected better acidification than FD on its own, but the 
performance was considerably better in combination with strain 80, reaching pH 4,9 after 5 
hours. When the substrate was spiked with maltose and inoculated with the combinations of 
commercial culture and plant isolates, both combinations showed similar acidification patterns,

although FD with strain 80 dropped the pH slightly faster. This suggests that the 
complementation of FD with strain 80 could reach faster fermentations and lower pH values 
when supplemented with maltose.

could be the same one explained above. Regarding the combination of TCC-20 with the 
selected plant isolates, slightly faster and more efficient acidification was achieved with strain 
111. When soy drink was spiked with raffinose, both combinations performed almost 
identically, but still better than TCC-20 on its own.

4.  Conclusion 
Commercial dairy-adapted starter cultures lack effectiveness in fermenting some of the 
carbohydrates naturally present in plant-based raw material. Flora Danica reported inability to 
metabolize sucrose, xylose and maltose and TCC-20 could not degrade raffinose, xylose and 
maltodextrin after 24 hours of incubation at 30oC and 37oC, respectively. However, they were 
able to utilize glucose and lactose, acting as a positive control. Among the selected Leuconostoc 
plant-isolates, 61% of them were found to grow in raffinose, 22% in xylose, 22% in maltose 
and 22% in maltodextrin. They showed preference for simple carbohydrates such a glucose 
when inoculated in media with different carbon sources. 
The conducted genomic analysis correlates to their ability to degrade different carbohydrates. 
Leu. lactis presented GH36 in their genomes and all the isolates from this species were able to 
grow in media with raffinose, whereas none of the Leu. citreum plant-isolates could degrade 
this trisaccharide, correlating to the absence of this family in their genome. Only four of the 
plant-isolated strains were able to metabolize xylose, namely four Leu. mesenteroides strains. 
Thus, it can be suggested that all other plant-isolated strains could lack a xylose transporter or 
a xylose isomerase to convert this monosaccharide into glucose for ATP production.   
Carbohydrate supplementation only presented improvements in soy drink fermentation by 
commercial cultures when glucose and lactose were added, as expected. However, 
supplementation with plant carbohydrates enhanced acidification when they were combined 
with some plant-isolated Leuconostoc strains shown to effectively utilize added carbohydrates. 
This was demonstrated in the case of FD combined with strain 609 in soy drink supplemented 
with xylose. No remarkable differences were found when combining FD with both plant-
isolated strains at different ratios. In contrast, strain 609 performed outstandingly better than 
FD in xylose-supplemented soy drink. This plant-isolated Leu. mesenteroides strain revealed 
promising attributes for applications in soy drink fermentation. 
The screening of plant-isolated strains for their ability to metabolize carbohydrates present in 
plant raw material correlated to GH profiling among their species enabled the design of 
acidification experiments for starter culture optimization. It was shown that plant-derived lactic 
acid bacteria are more suitable for fermentation of carbohydrates present in plants than dairy-
adapted starter cultures. This establishes a basis for further research on the plant-isolated strain 
collection and development of new starter cultures for plant-based fermentations.

5. Future perspectives 
As this study aimed to characterize strains that have been recently isolated from plants whose 
identity was unknown, it would be convenient to sequence their genome to investigate which 
genes they carry related to carbohydrate utilization and safety and compare to previously 
published genomes to show differences.  
Knowing the exact composition of the plant-based substrates will be required to both design 
the experiments without assumptions and understand bacterial performance with precision. It 
will be necessary to obtain information about the carbohydrate composition and processing of 
the drinks from the manufacturer. The latter is especially relevant in the case of oats, as 
unprocessed oats would adopt oat meal consistency rather than that of a liquid. Although this 
study focuses on the presence or addition of carbohydrates present in plants as a model of use 
when commercial cultures did not acidify, qualitative and quantitative methods could be 
applied to identify carbohydrates in raw materials e.g. Barfoed’s test for hexoses identification 
or HPLC with a broader range of carbohydrate standard.  
Further experiments modifying one variable at a time, such as incubation temperature, should 
be performed to investigate more about the ability of the strains to ferment complex 
carbohydrates and to optimize fermentation parameters. Testing complex carbohydrate 
fermentation over time for a cheese-like product is considered as a continuation of this work. 
It would also be ideal to test fermentation in media with two different carbohydrates to properly 
observe which one is utilized first and if there is any correlation between the amount that is 
metabolized and the presence of a second carbohydrate. Another aspect to investigate would 
be the relationship between the quantity of consumed carbohydrates and the quantity of 
fermentation products generated. 
Scaling-up of the lab-designed fermentations would be necessary to test strain performance in 
larger volumes, as well as to study texture and flavor properties of the prototypes. Regarding 
the texture, treatments such as ultrahigh pressure homogenization could be applied to the raw 
material to reduce the particle size and improve its viscosity. Addition of thickener or gelling 
agents should also be considered.

Appendix II – HPLC 
High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) is a widely implemented analytical 
technique that separates components of a liquid matrix and allows their identification and 
quantification. A mobile phase carries the compounds present in the samples through a 
chromatographic column at a specific flow rate. The column is filled with solid adsorbent 
material, microparticles with an average diameter of X micrometers. Higher efficiency is 
achieved with smaller particle size [75]. These particles form the stationary phase. A specific 
pressure is applied to the mobile phase to make it flow through the column and get in contact 
with the stationary phase, where the different compounds are separated depending on their 
interaction with the particles. 
The solution used as mobile phase depends on the polarity of the column. In Normal Phase 
HPLC (NP-HPLC), the column contains polar silica particles and therefore, the mobile phase 
should be a non-polar solvent (e.g. hexane), with the purpose of making  
 the non-polar components flow faster and encourage the polar ones to attach to the particles. 
In Reverse Phase HPLC (RP-HPLC), the column contains non-polar silica particles, thus the 
mobile phase should be a polar solvent (e.g. methanol), inducing polar compounds to move 
through the column and non-polar compounds to be attached to the solid adsorbent material in 
the stationary phase [76, 77]. 
For the detection of each specific compound, a UV light source emits a light beam across the 
solution inside the column, which will be absorbed by the compounds present in the sample. 
The amount of light absorbed by the compounds depends on the amount of compound in the 
solvent. Parameters such as flow pressure and temperature inside the column need to be defined 
before starting the experiment.
