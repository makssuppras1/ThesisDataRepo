Preface 
The work presented herein concludes my Master of Science and Engineering in Advanced and Applied 
Chemistry education at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) and is submitted as part of the 
requirements. The work is the result of a 30 ECTS point master’s project, under the supervision of my main 
supervisor, Assistant Professor Katrine Qvortrup, and co-supervisor, Assistant Professor Martin Nielsen, at 
DTU.  
The thesis starts by preparing the reader to two genres of supramolecular chemistry, (1) encapsulation 
phenomena, and (2) the supramolecular self-assembly of metallocages, through a state-of-the-art literature 
study. The study will provide the reader with necessary information, to understand the motivation behind the 
thesis, and the decisions made during the experimental investigation.  
The experimental work is concluded from a discussion on the synthetic work done, to provide the organic 
ligands, that subsequently were employed in supramolecular self-assembly studies, that investigated the 
provision of new assemblies. While the work provided no conclusive evidence of reaction conditions 
successfully yielding assemblies, the work has disclosed fundamental aspects of the self-assembly process. 
Furthermore, a list of analyses was defined, that readily could be investigated through spectroscopically means, 
to provide an initial assessment to disqualify complexes from assemblies.   
It is in my firm opinion, that the work has disclosed information that will aid future research, and somewhat 
simplify an otherwise complex process.

Abstract  
Supramolecular assemblies such as supramolecular coordination complexes (SCC) and metallocages are topics 
with a growing interest. The high modularity of the ligands comprising the assembly, provide measures of tuning 
both chemical- and physical properties to match a desired target. SCC and metallocages have widely been 
explored in relation to catalysis, where enzymatic character has been reported. While significant achievements 
have been accomplished, many assemblies are limited in unimolecular reactions, as a consequence of ligand 
design.  
In the work presented in this thesis, two new ligands were investigated, to probe the resulting assembly, from 
elongation and substitution of structural element, respectively. Provision of these ligands were disclosed in two 
efficient synthetic strategies. While no conclusive evidence was found to unequivocally demonstrate novel 
assemblies, information was gained on critical elements relevant to the self-assembly process, as schematised 
in Figure A.

1 Aim of study 
This study aims to synthesize and investigate novel tetrahedral supramolecular cages of M4L6 stoichiometry. 
Furthermore, it aims at providing a comprehensive understanding of the dynamic properties of the cages. To 
meet these aims, the thesis is divided into two sections: (1) addresses preparation and execution of synthetic 
protocols that provided the target organic ligands, Figure 1 L1, L2 and L3; (2) discusses the host-guest 
chemistry, which was analysed through extensive encapsulation studies, primarily using tetraalkylammonium 
ions +NR4, R = Me, Et, Pr and n-Bu.  
The ligand design of L2 and L3 was based on an empirically derived rationale that expands on structure of L1. 
L1 is known to form the desired tetrahedron-assembly and was included to under the supramolecular self-
assembly process and encapsulation process.1,2 Host-guest chemistry employing the assembly of L1 was 
investigated, to establish points of reference, for assessment of potential novel assemblies. The dynamic nature 
of host-guest chemistry was studied employing different 1D and 2D NMR techniques.

2 Introduction 
Supramolecular chemistry is chemistry beyond the single molecule; the chemistry concerning the formation of 
complex multimolecular entities, held together by intermolecular forces. Such interactions are fundamental 
throughout biology, for example, receptor-substrate interactions, peptide-folding, and base-pairing in DNA.3,4 
A proper molecular design enable the construction of molecules that are predisposed to assemble, enabling 
construction of supramolecular structures with unique properties. One of the properties that are of particular 
interest in this work, is the ability to prepare molecular assemblies that selectively encapsulate distinct structural 
elements through reversible non-covalent interactions. Among the first examples of supramolecular assemblies 
was polycyclic ethers, crown-ethers, which bind monocationic metals through interactions via the oxygen lone-
pairs, Figure 2. While one single interaction is relatively weak, the combined interactions result in a strong 
affinity for K+ of around 106 M-1.5,6  
 
Figure 2. Complexation of K+ to crown-ethers demonstrated the principle of molecular recognition and entrapment.  
The principle of host assembly has since expanded from simple two-dimensional crown-ethers to large 
three-dimensional modular macromolecular assemblies. Exploiting the fundamental hydrogen bonding 
interaction, Rebek and co-workers were able to provide a molecular assembly, comprised of two interlocking 
units forming a “soft-ball” assembly, Figure 3 A. As reversible bonding is the basis for the assembly, it can 
structurally adapt to allow for the encapsulation of specific guest compounds. The assembly was able to catalyse 
the Diels-Alder (DA) reaction for a variety of diene/dienophiles.7 More recently, Tiefenbacher and co-workers 
provided an assembly comprising of a hexameric resorcinarene capsule, Figure 3, B.8 The capsule, again, 
exploits the reversible interactions of hydrogen bonding to form a complex assembly, providing a foundation 
for encapsulation of guest molecules in the assembly. 
 
Figure 3. Complex supramolecular assemblies formed from hydrogen bonds. Adapted from Tiefenbacher and co-workers.9  
==PAGE:20==
Page 4 of 68 
Assemblies formed from reversible interactions, viz. hydrogen bonds, are inherently weak, and design of larger 
ligands quickly become difficult from added complexity resulting from the required complementarity between 
hydrogen bonding acceptors-donors. Metallosupramolecular chemistry, in contrast, relies on the more stable 
bonds between (organic) ligands and metal centres, to form either metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) or 
supramolecular coordination complexes (SCCs). MOFs are comprised of an infinite network of metals centres 
(or inorganic clusters) bridged by simple organic linkers through metal-ligand coordination bonds, whereas 
SCCs are comprised of discrete constructs of metal-ligand bonds, to form a single thermodynamically favoured 
product. SCCs exploit the predictable nature of (transition) metals, to spontaneously form these assemblies.10 
Based on the pioneering work by Verkade and co-worker;11 Fujita et al.12 and Stang et al.13 were able to provide 
molecular square assemblies, Figure 4 A. Fujita et al. later reported an octahedral complex, Figure 4 B, 
displaying distinct regioselectivity in the DA reaction between anthracene and phthalimide. The host induced 
regioselectivity resulting in a terminal DA adduct, that is in strong contrast to the reaction proceeding in the 
absence of a host assembly, vide infra.14  
Developing new SCCs are of interest in both catalysis and drug-delivery, and a better understanding of how to 
manipulate these, to provide distinct physical and chemical properties, would be of high value. To this extent, 
one must consider the basic principles behind encapsulation of guest molecules, and the supramolecular 
assembly process. The Stang group has extensively studied the formation of discrete SCC assemblies 
comprising of Pt(II)/Pd(II), whereas the Raymond group has disclosed elements that ligands must satisfy to 
form a certain SCC geometry. Furthermore, the Raymond group has explored the assembly of a host from a 
1,5-disubstituted naphthalene ligand, Figure 4 (C, L1) unit in combination with various tri- and tetravalent 
metals, Figure 4 C.1 This tetrahedron was later shown to selectively encapsulate certain ions,15 significantly 
increase basicity of amines,16 and encapsulate various cationic guests.17–22 These study address fundamental 
aspects of SCCs that are key for the rational design of future SCC structures.

truncated tetrahedra from 1,5-dicatechol amide naphthalene. B and C adapted from Fujita and co-workers, and Raymond and co-

3 Project background 
3.1 Host-guest chemistry 
A prime goal of synthesising new supramolecular assemblies is to increase selectivity towards certain species, 
to allow for discrimination between an array of guests. The following section will address the fundamental 
aspects enabling host-guest complexation, and parameters that affect the complexation.  
The association between 2 (or more) molecules (or ions), as in Figure 2, illustrates the complexation process. 
The complexation results from host-guest interactions through electrostatic forces, separate from covalent bonds 
and ionic crystals. These interactions encompass weak interactions as hydrogen bonding, van der Waal forces, 
cation-π interactions. Complementary stereoelectronic elements between the host and guest is essential to the 
structural organisation of the host-guest complex. If, for example, binding sites such as heteroatom lone-pairs, 
is present in the host, the guest should have divergent binding sites, such as Lewis acidic properties.23 
3.1.1 Thermodynamic considerations on the complexation process  
The complexation of guest molecules in a host assembly is generally thought of as an overall entropy-driven 
process, as demonstrated in Figure 5, and is significantly affected by the nature of the solvent. Two crucial 
effects accounting for the solvent’s interactions with both host and guest, are (1) hydrophobicity, and (2) 
solvation. The hydrophobic effect accounts for lacking interactions between polar and apolar molecules, e.g. 
the immiscibility of oil in water. In “empty” cages, such as Figure 4 C, the cavity is occupied by water, which 
can be considered as high in energy, because of the poor interactions between the water molecules and the 
hydrophobic cavity walls. As such, the displacement for a guest molecule with better interaction, will provide 
both an enthalpic and an entropic energy contribution. The release results in better stabilisation of the water 
(water-water interaction), in addition to a strong interaction between the host and guest molecule. The solvation 
effect accounts for favourable interactions between host, guest, and solvent, such as the stabilisation of charged 
species in polar solvent.24–26 These interactions are crucial for optimal host-guest interactions, and a significant 
change in stabilisation between the free and complexed guest will promote interaction. 
 
Figure 5. The complexation process between the host and guest schematisised. The overall process is entropically driven.  
When considering flexible assemblies, such as crown-ethers, and their complexation of cations, Figure 5, the 
initial conformation is a solvated-disordered structure. For any complexation to occur, the structure must 
==PAGE:22==
Page 6 of 68 
dissociate the solvent sphere and reorient; undergo preorganisation. Both will introduce an enthalpic penalty, 
but an entropic gain. The guest must as well loose its solvation sphere, which adds an enthalpic penalty and an 
entropic gain. The complexation, while enthalpically favourable, adds an entropic penalty, however, the 
liberation of solvent molecules, overall results in an entropically favourable process.24–27  
The host-guest complexation is affected by other parameters apart from the solvent, and the structure plays a 
pivotal role in both complexation and encapsulation. Most importantly, steric complementarity between host 
and guest is essential for successful encapsulation. Secondly, follows the sum of non-covalent bonds between 
the host and guest. Host scaffolds that can establish strong interactions, will result in a more strong and 
favourable host-guest complex. Considering three examples of complexation, as in Figure 6, the ligand must, 
prior to forming the shown conformation, undergo preorganisation, which adds an energetic penalty. Acyclic 
polydentate ligands, referred to as podands must overcome a higher barrier for complexation, relative to cyclic 
molecules, such as the crown-ether shown in Figure 2. The lower barrier in the latter results from a minimisation 
of preorganisation, which is a consequence of the macrocyclic effect. The macrocyclic effect combines the 
stabilising effect of a ligand’s polydenticity, with the organisation of these coordination sites in a ring structure, 
thus minimising the preorganisation. Further minimisation in degrees of freedom is achieved by ligands such as 
cryptands, that are able to envelop the guest complete through additional binding sites.24–26,28 
 
Figure 6. The difference in host scaffold and the resulting decreased enthalpy penalty associated with complexation (left to right) 
originating from the macrocyclic effect. Red ball represents cationic metal.  
Limiting factors for host-guest complexation are too strong association equilibrium constants, complete 
covering, or too slow exchange kinetics. For a broader application, hosts with adequate exchange properties, 
such as metallocages, are necessary.10,24–26

Molecular recognition is an extension of the association constant. As for the complexation process, two other 
effects are just as important: size complementarity, and (a lack of) host preorganisation. Both affects the 
thermodynamic selectivity towards a certain species.   
Raymond and co-workers further provided an explanation on molecular recognition, and how metallocages, as 
tetrahedral M4L6, (M = Ga(III), L = N,N’-bis(2,3-dihydroxybenzoyl)-1,5-diaminonaphthalene, Figure 4 (C, 
L1) can discriminate between an array of guests. These cages have no preorganisation element, due to the fixed 
tetrahedral structure. Hence, they allow studying the change in energy resulting from the guest undergoing 
encapsulation vs remaining solvated. This gives an approximate explanation of the thermodynamic selectivity, 
and as such, for encapsulation to occur, the energy gain from guest encapsulation must exceed the desolvation 
penalty resulting from taking the guest “out” of the solvent. The Raymond group has elegantly demonstrated 
this concept. By investigating the encapsulation of Et4Si, and the isostructural +NEt4 in D2O, they found the 
former to not encapsulate whereas the latter was fully encapsulated. They explained the lacking encapsulation 
of Et4Si by weak guest interactions with the cavity walls whereas +NEt4 experiences strong cation-π interactions 
with the hosts naphthalene cavity edges.1,27  
As mentioned earlier, size complementarity plays a key role in molecular recognition. As the guest size 
increases, the van der Waal interactions diminish. As a consequence, if an assembly already has a guest 
encapsulated, and a guest is present, that is able to establish better interactions, it will readily displace the former. 
This was demonstrated via the encapsulation of NPr4+ that was readily displaced by NEt4+.27 In a later 
publication, the Raymond group further emphasised the importance of size complementarity, and its effect on 
encapsulation. A competition-study on encapsulation of iminium ions varying in size was conducted, and while 
some encapsulation of the 5 membered complexes were observed, the host demonstrated a high selectivity 
towards linear 4 membered iminium complexes, Scheme 1.17,29

The mechanistic considerations of guest molecule encapsulation into the cavity of a host assembly largely 
depend on the structure. For a given assembly, the degree of encapsulation of a given guest is suspected to 
change (reaching a plateau) with temperature. Because of the dynamic nature of the host, it will permit 
encapsulation of larger guests based on more readily undergoing structural changes. This dynamic is in strong 
contrast to solid-state equivalent e.g. zeolites, that has a defined pore-size entry. The pores precludes absorption 
of guests exceeding the diameter, which is unaffected by temperature.30,31 The process further increases in 
complexity when considering the simultaneous encapsulation of several guests, and whether these form guest-
guest complexes that react or become entrapped.  
In cavitands, such as the resorcinarene, Figure 3 B, the guest interacts intuitively with the host, by becoming 
embedded into the crown cavity.8,32,33 In the presence of a nucleophile, Tiefenbacher and co-workers were able 
to demonstrated the capsule provide an asymmetric environment, that was catalytically active, in which the 
confined reaction space becomes a key factor in the enantioselectivity of the capsule. The confinement facilitates 
encapsulation of an activated complex, an iminium ion, that is fixated in place by cation-π interactions. The 
fixating blocks the Re-face of the proline-iminium adduct, Figure 7 a. Inducing strain to encapsulated (charged) 
species in a confined environment is further emphasised by Tiefenbacher and co-worker in a recent publication 
on carbonyl-olefin metathesis.34  
 
Figure 7. Encapsulation into the resorcinarene crown provide an asymmetric environment. Adapted from Tiefenbacher and co-
worker.32 
Using the resorcinarene capsule, Figure 3 B, as catalysts, while interesting, can be associated with a significant 
product inhibition, as experienced in assembly provided by Rebek and co-workers, Figure 3 A. Employing the 
capsule as catalyst necessitates product dissociation before uptake of the new guest into the crown. A direct 
dissociative mechanism (of the product) is unlikely, because of the significant thermodynamic penalty 
associated with loss of the stabilising bonds without compensation. More likely, the host changes conformation 
to minimise the π interactions, which results in product exclusion. Moreover, considering the solvent, deuterated

release under a certain set of conditions. Drug-delivery applications of SCC and metallocages is still in its

3.2 Supramolecular self-assembly of metallocages 
Any SCC, MOF, or metallocage can conceptually simplify into its constituent metal nodes and ligands. 
Description of the former may include any propensities to form a certain number of available coordination sites, 
the spatial orientation of these, preferable heteroatom-coordination, e.g. Pt/Pd to N-donors. Description of 
ligands accounts for Lewis basic sites, the numbers of these, as well as their spatial orientation. These elements 
are crucial in the self-assembly of both SCC and metallocages supramolecular entities, as an improper 
combination of ligand-metal nodes result in complexes that are not a discrete assembly, such as coordination 
polymers, Figure 11 A.10 The Stang group has widely explored the combination of rigid spacers to form modular 
macrocyclic structures.10,13,44–46 The combination of organic ligands with metal-containing precursors, to form 
the assembly edges and vertices, is referred to as the edge-directed self-assembly, Figure 10 A. In contrary, 
employing ligands that span the faces of the assembly, is referred to as face-directed self-assembly, Figure 10 
B.10,36  
 
Figure 10. (A). Edge-directed self-assembly. (B). Face-directed self-assembly.  
Ensuring the formation of discrete assemblies can be achieved through the installation of end-capping, as was 
done by Fujita et al. in the synthesis of supramolecular cubes and squares.12 For self-assembly employing metal 
nodes without capping, several different (meta-)stable structures may form. To prevent unwanted product 
formation, the ligand must contain directionality to ensure the formation of a single structure, Figure 11 B, 
which can be achieved from introduction of chelating groups.10,24,36,47  
 
Figure 11. Improper pairing results in polymeric species, whereas proper pairing of convergent ligands with metal nodes ultimately 
form discrete assemblies.  
3.2.1 Ligand design and its influence on supramolecular self-assembly  
Assembly of metallocages is the result of mechanical coupling of Lewis basic site to Lewis acid metal centre, 
and generally proceeds under thermodynamic control. Forming the assemblies under thermodynamic control, 
whilst relying on sufficiently labile metal-ligand bonds, effectively installs an “auto-repair” during the self-
==PAGE:28==

assembly. Any meta-stable (kinetic) products can interconvert to a globally-stable (thermodynamic) product. 
The interconversion further becomes facilitated by changing pH and increasing temperature.1,15,48  
In the provision of new metallocages with distinct properties, beyond the Stang group’s work, a rationale for 
symmetrical compounds has been provided by the Raymond research group. Herein, they account for 
symmetrical aspects ligand design must consider for successfully providing supramolecular assemblies using 
non-capped metal nodes.1,49

the chelating vectors, perpendicular to the principle axis of rotation at the metal centre.1,15,49  
The chelation plane becomes less important for rigid planar ligands, as the interaction between a metal centre 
and three chelating groups resulting in the desired tetrahedron is described through an approach angle, φ‡, 
Figure 12 B. φ is given as the angle between the principle axis of rotation of the metal centre and the vector 
containing the atoms comprising the chelating group. The assemblies resulting from this angle are homochiral, 
as either ΛΛΛΛ or ΔΔΔΔ, Figure 15.1,15,49 While the rationale provides a guideline for ligand design, it assumes 
(i) the formation proceed through stricht self-assembly resulting in a single product, as a consequence of 
geometrical restrictions and labile M-O bonds, and (ii) under the thermodynamic conditions, any meta-stable 
geometries interconverts to the desired tetrahedra.49

The self-assembly process is classified according to the thermodynamic profile of the process, whether there is 
involvement of any template compounds, and if the latter remain part of the final assembly. Two classifications 
are noteworthy to the assemblies already mentioned, strict- and directed self-assembly. For further information, 
see work by Lehn,3,53 Whitesides et al.,54 and Steed and Artwood.24  
The template effect is divided into two contribution factors arising from (1) the kinetic effect, and (2) the 
thermodynamic effect. The former relates, how (metal) cations stabilise cyclic intermediates, as in Figure 14, 
whereas, the latter relates how (metal) cations discriminate the more stable product, from an array of reversible 
reactions.24,55 While template-assisted self-assembly is an option to provide metallocages, they can become a 
limitation, as the template can become challenging to remove. Especially if the host is intended to encapsulate 
neutral guests, the presence of cationic templates will completely inhibit the process. There is no driving force 
for displacing the cationic template, as it is strongly stabilised through cation-π interactions.

3.2.2 Metallocages chirality  
Considering the mechanical coupling between the ligands and the metal centre, the resulting assembly will 
become chiral. The chirality is a consequence of three chelating moieties forming a pseudo-octahedral complex 
that are either a left-handed (Λ) or right-handed propeller (Δ), Figure 15.25 The metals widely explored by the 
Raymond group, have no significant crystal field stabilisation energy as a conseuqnece of d0 (Ti(IV)), or d10 
configuration (Ga(III), In(III), Ge(IV), Sn(IV)).15,49 Thus, the distortion of the coordination sphere originates 
from the approach angle, and any necessary adjustments the ligand must accommodate to form the tetrahedra, 
viz. bending.

(ΔΔΛΛ), T (ΔΔΔΔ), and C3 (ΔΔΔΛ) (including their enantiomers), Figure 16.15,49

becomes a topic of interest. As such, the Raymond group explored methods of providing enantiopure 
metallocages. Two means of providing enantiopure assemblies were investigated; chiral resolution and 
diastereoselective assembly. The latter method provided significantly more stable assemblies and generated 
significantly less waste.2,38   
The metallocages were further tested for (1) the effect of a racemic mixture vs enantiopure mixture of host on 
diastereoselectivity, and (2) the effect of increased host cavity volume on the same selectivity. The experimental 
evidence demonstrated the larger cavity had a more significant effect on the diastereoselectivity, which the 
authors claim to originate from the larger cavity volume, and how it accommodates a sterically more demanding 
transition state during a unimolecular Prins cyclisation.38,40

Encapsulation studies of M4L16 
The study of supramolecular assembly’s dynamic properties in solution are important to understand. NMR 
offers multiple opportunities to study the characteristics of a system, particularly, the complexation and 
encapsulation process, e.g. encapsulation of relevant guest molecules, such as quaternary amines. 1D 1H 
methods become a useful tool to provide evidence of encapsulation through significant changes in peak shift, 
as well as the number of peaks. Only a distinct number of peaks are expected for any compounds of high 
symmetry, i.e. the L1 ligand (excluding exchangeable protons) count six H unique signals in a 1H-NMR. If the 
total resonance count adds to less than the expected value, the system may be dynamic (at rt), whereas, if the 
resonances count amounts to a number greater than the expected 6, the assembly process either provided the 
wrong product, or resulted in isomers of different symmetry, e.g. S4, C3 and T. 2D methods provide means of 
interpreting the resulting complexation structure. COSY and HMBC offers through-bond correlation, and 
NOESY provide information on inter- and intramolecular interactions through nuclear Overhauser effect, NOE, 
through-space correlations.66  
Comparing the 1H-NMR spectrum of H4L1, to 1H-NMR spectra of the precipitates of Ga4L16 and Al4L16, 
demonstrate a significant difference in resonances, as both the hydroxy groups, and the amide resonances have 
disappeared, Figure 34. Paramagnetic properties of Fe(III) precludes NMR analysis, however, the collected 
powder display properties alike Ga and Al. The lacking resonances imply that the cluster is (at rt) dynamic. The 
smaller size of Al relative to Ga, results in a greater positive charge density in the former, resulting in a stronger 
and less flexible bond between the metal node and the ligand. The stronger binding to Al consequently mean, 
that the system is less dynamic.

Structural considerations on L2 
While on paper the proposed L2 design is similar to that of the 2,6-bis(catecholamide)anthracene, Figure 13 
(A, c), reviewing the two structures through molecular visualisation software, Maestro69, some differences are 
seen. Most notable is the rotation of the phenyl groups in L2, that in gas-phase become perpendicular to the 
naphthalene ring, Figure 47 A, whereas the anthracene remains planar Figure 47 B, C. The rigidity will 
minimise need for any preorganisation, in contrary to the rotation of phenyls. These observations are based on 
results from Maestro’s in-build force field optimisations, on each of the individual ligands in gas phase. While 
these only provide qualitative structural information. I must emphasise these results are by no means an accurate 
description of the ligand in solution, nor during the self-assembly process. However, they provide a tentative 
explanation to describe the difference observed with and without heat during self-assembly. This still is 
insufficient in providing a reasonable explanation why the tetrahedral do not form. A likely explanation may be 
the position of catechol groups. Comparing with a structure reported by the Nitschke group,50 that reported a 
ligand structure more identical to L2, that successful resulted in formation of a tetrahedral. The difference lies 
in the position of the chelation vector.

5 Future work 
The work presented herein has addresses the working principles of self-assembly and demonstrated the 
importance of especially heat. Assuming the structure of L2 and L3 are able to provide assemblies, the 
appropriate temperature during self-assembly should be determined, as the employed 50℃ seem insufficient. 
Heating to 120℃ is the most apparent approach, and comparison of the resulting spectra between the two 
temperatures, is rather straight forward. Doing so, will hopefully provide either very few resonances, mirroring 
that of Ga4L16, or a few defined peaks. Addition of various cations should resolve the missing peaks and provide 
strongly upshifted values. 
Screening for appropriate template ions can be investigated concurrently with determining optimal temperature. 
I investigated four varied sizes of cations, however, these do not investigate the effect of sterically demanding 
tetraalkylammonium, nor larger sized. Whether a significant effect can be achieved from sterically demanding 
ions, is to be determined. Moreover, rather than probing the system with a single cation, as I initially did, several 
should be done, to establish any possible trends resulting from the interaction.  
NMR provide several techniques of studying the dynamic nature of assemblies, however, provide no definitive 
(to the best of my knowledge) means of unequivocally stating an assembly has been formed. As such, crystals 
suitable for x-ray must be grown of the complex. I have widely been experimenting with vapour diffusion of 
various organic solvents into an aqueous solution of the complex. Future perspectives must expand on this, to 
establish if the complexes described herein are helicates, polymers or something third.  
The ligand designs were based on a scaffold that already has been extensively studied. We assumed that neither 
the phenyl spacing-group between the catechol and the naphthalene core, nor the triazole, would provide 
changes sufficiently significant to disrupt tetrahedron formation. However, from the observed spectra, some 
evidence indicates otherwise. A new ligand design should as such be investigated, expanding on the anthracene 
structure, or combining the phenyl with a triazole.

6 Conclusion 
Efficient synthetic pathways for the novel ligands, L2 and L3, were fully disclosed, building on well-established 
cross-coupling protocols, viz. Suzuki-Miyuara, Sonogashira and “Click”. Employing the commercially 
available 1,5-dibromonaphthalene readily provided the intermediate, compound 6, required to synthesise L2, 
whereas the same compound was unsuccessful in providing the key intermediate, compound 9, for L3. As a 
solution, compound 4, was synthesised that resulted in an overall improved synthetic strategy. Difficulties were 
encountered in the click reaction, which was determined to be of various sources. These included inappropriate 
combination between solvent and copper-catalyst source, lacking reaction time and too elevated temperature. 
Despite these, a successful reaction setup was disclosed, that enabled one-pot conditions to provide the desired 
ligand.   
Both syntheses of L1 and the corresponding supramolecular assemblies were readily synthesised following the 
protocol established by the Raymond group. Studies of the supramolecular assemblies were all able to replicate 
the published work, which provided invaluable experience about the self-assembly process, directly applicable 
to the provision of the novel cages.  
Various conditions to provide supramolecular cage of L2 were investigated, and it was demonstrated, that heat 
is a pivotal element, and outweigh reaction time. The qualitative structural analysis supports the premise that 
heat is necessary, as inherently flexible elements under kinetic conditions, likely retard assembly formation. 
Directed assembly was attempted, however, was unable to provide satisfying evidence that the recovered 
complex was the desired tetrahedron. Interconversion of the recovered complex was investigated, under the 
assumption that a triple helicate structure was formed. Inconclusive evidence was observed, as a different 
template (tetraalkylammonium ion) resulted in different peaks forming and disappearing.  
As L3 was obtained at a late stage, that combined with other external factors, excluded an in-depth analysis. 
NMR of recovered complex provided evidence that the complex likely exists as either (a) several different 
stereoisomers or (b) a low-coordination species. However, this remains to be elucidated.  
While NMR techniques provide means to investigate the dynamic nature of any supramolecular assemblies, X-
ray quality crystals are required to unequivocally demonstrate any complexes to be an assembly, which I am 
yet to provide. 
While the synthesis of novel supramolecular cages provides an exciting challenge, it is inherently difficult. The 
work presented herein provide elucidation on some of the principles that are pivotal for any new structures.
