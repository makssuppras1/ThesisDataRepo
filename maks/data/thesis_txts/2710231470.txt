Abstract
Musculoskeletal modelling has a signiﬁcant impact on joint force estimation. In a clinical
setting, a models ability to provide precise estimates is of major interest since the outcome
can determine which treatment a patient will receive.
Ground reaction force (GRF) and gait trajectory data of 16 anterior cruciate ligament
reconstructed (ACLR) patients was captured using Vicon Nexus. Subjects performed
static and dynamic calibration tasks so that joint center locations could be obtained. All
trajectory data was then processed using Vicon’s gap ﬁlling toolbox.
The four biomechanical models Plug-in Gait (PIG), Conventional Gait Model (CGM),
functional method (FUN) and a non-uniform scaling method (NUS) were used and sub-
jects were modeled in MATLAB using the OpenSim API. Models were scaled using the
estimated joint center locations before performing inverse kinematics (IK), inverse dy-
namics (ID), static optimization (SO) and joint reaction analysis (JRA).
Estimated knee joint loads were found to be similar to force measurements found in other
studies. Using the FUN model as a reference the PIG, CGM and NUS models estimated
32%, 20% and 19% higher loads in the medial compartment of the knee respectively where
as the NUS model estimated 25% higher loads in the lateral compartment of the knee.
It was also possible to show inter limb diﬀerences although we found associated force is
subject speciﬁc rather than only model dependent. Individualised scaling is suggested to
be an important factor to consider when estimating knee joint loads.

Resumé
Muskuloskeletal modellering har en betydelig indvirkning på beregningen af ledreaktion-
skræfter.Der er der i det kliniske miljø en stor interesse i modellernes præcisionsevne,
eftersom at resultatet kan påvirke, hvilken behandling patienten skal modtage.
I dette speciale blev Vicon Nexus brugt til at optage underlagsreaktions kræfter (GRF)
og markørbevægelsesdata fra 16 patienter, der havde fået opereret det forreste korsbånd.
Patienterne udøvede statiske og dynamiske kalibrerings øvelser for at beregne lokationen
af ledcentre. Markørdataen blev derefter behandlet ved at bruge Vicon’s data udfyldnings
værktøj.
Som et led i dataindsamlingen blev ﬁre biomekaniske modeller: Plug-in Gait (PIG), Con-
ventional Gait Model (CGM), funktionel metode (FUN) og en ikke uniform skalering
metode (NUS) anvendt.
Herefter blev patienter modelleret ved brug af MATLAB og
OpenSim API. Modellerne blev skaleret ved at bruge de beregnede ledcenter lokationer,
for derefter beregne invers kinematik (IK), invers dynamik (ID), statisk optimering (SO)
og en analyse af ledreaktionskræfter (JRA).
De beregnede knæleds belastninger fundet i dette projekt var lignende kraftmålinger fra
andre studier. De tre modeller PIG, CGM og NUS beregnede en større belastning på
henholdsvis 32%, 20% og 19% i knæets mediale rum i forhold til FUN modellen, der blev
brugt som reference model, hvor NUS modellen beregnede en 25% større belastning i
knæets laterale rum.
Det var også muligt at bruge modellerne til at vise forskellen i knæledddets belastninger
mellem det rakse og opereret ben, hvor vi fandt at associeret kraft er mere personspeciﬁkt
i forhold til kun at være modelafhængigt. Individualiseret skalering foreslås som en vigtig
faktor at overveje, når man estimerer knæledsbelastninger.

1 Introduction
Motion capture analysis is a clinical tool that is used in diagnosing diﬀerent pathologi-
cal gait patterns [34]. The conventional method is to use reﬂective markers mounted on
the skin surfaces and capture their movement in the three-dimensional space to calcu-
late body segment movements. This process involves some uncertainties and limitations
due to skin artifacts, marker positioning and regression methods used for modelling [3].
These uncertainties can inﬂuence the muscle and joint forces estimation. And therefore it
is important to know how the outcomes of the models are inﬂuenced by uncertainties. It
is critical that the musculoskeletal model accurately represents the subject, that marker
placement is accurate and algorithms used minimize error as they all have a signiﬁcant
inﬂuence on the kinematic and kinetic outputs [34].
The biomechanical model used for marker positioning protocol, the Conventional Gait
Model (CGM) has been in development the last 30 years and progressed from the ﬁrst
version Plug-in Gait to the CGM 2.6 [4]. As technological equipment and designs improved
over the years the CGM protocol has evolved and established itself as the predominant
gait model for clinical research purposes. To deﬁne the anatomical frame and segments,
the models consistency relies on marker positioning on palpable prominent bony land-
marks. Soft tissue markers are then placed related to those in the optimal positions to
deﬁne the segment orientations [4]. The most important development was calculation of
joint angles on the basis of estimated joint centers rather than directly from the marker
positions and then incorporation of inverse dynamics. Locating the hip joint center (HJC)
presents an issue as it is not palpable and the exact location is required to obtain an ac-
curate musculoskeletal model [58].
In contrast a prominent bony landmark such as the anterior superior iliac spine (ASiS) is
easily palpable [58]. The HJC location is a calculation estimate, where as a wrong esti-
mation, may propagate downstream in the lower extremities, in the kinematic and kinetic
analysis. The estimated location of the HJC is important for deﬁning the anatomical
frame such as the long axis of the thigh segment and thus the forces and moment acting
around the hip and knee joints. The estimation has shown to inﬂuence the hip and knee
joint kinematics and kinetics [58].
In the past, various approaches have been used for estimating HJC such as anthropomet-
ric based regression equations and functional calibration methods [47]. The regression
method is based on equations to estimate the position the HJC. The functional method
relies on calibration trials to infer the HJC position from the movement of the thigh
segment in respect to the pelvis segment [58]. The two approaches are diﬀerent as the
functional method uses transformation techniques and sphere adjustment approach and

1.1
History of Regression and Functional Techniques
The regression model by Bell et al. 1989 [6] introduces prediction measure of the HJC
based on the distance between ASIS. Later Davis et al. 1991 [17] introduced a method
to include the leg length in the calculation.
Lastly a machine learning method leave
one out cross validation method was introduced by Harrington et al. [50] in 2007, by
investigating which parameter was the most predictive factor for HJC including also the
pelvis width and depth in the algorithm [26].
Hara et al.
2016 [29] then introduced
leg length to the equations as being the only dependent anthropometric predictor, which
improved the Conventional Gait Model (CGM) location of the HJC [38]. The functional
method is a calibration technique, it requires tracking the motion of markers placed at the
pelvis and femur during a coordinated motion [26]. The method is based on a dynamic
motion of two segments moving relatively to each other. To calculate the HJC the star
arc movement requires moving the leg through its full range of motion about the hip in its
3 degrees of freedom. It was additionally expanded by (Schwartz and Rozumalski, 2005)
[26] presented as a transformational technique and Ehrig et al. 2010. developed a faster
and more robust algorithm named Symmetric Center of Rotation Estimation (SCoRE)
[26]. This demonstrated to be the most accurate technique when both segments move
simultaneously [22]. Ehrig et al. 2010 likewise demonstrated a signiﬁcant beneﬁt of the
Optimal Common Shape Technique (OCST), reducing skin marker artefact, by predicting
the accuracy of determining joint centre positions in functional gait analysis [23].
1.2
Gold Standard in Joint Estimation
Imaging techniques such as X-ray, CT and MRI scans are known to be the gold standard
for HJC estimation with an error as low as 2 mm. Despite the low error these methods
are not used in routine clinical gait analysis due to possible exposure to radiation, high
cost and long processing time which calls for development for non-invasive estimation
methods [33]. A former systematic review found the Harrington regression equations and
functional method are the most accurate predictive and estimation methods respectively
where the latter was found to result in more reliable gait kinematics [34].

Although both methods have been recognized as valid estimation techniques, there are
some practical limitations when applying either method. The functional method was most
applicable for subjects not suﬀering from a hip pathology, other motor impairments or
obesity. The Harrington regression algorithms were most applicable for subjects not able
to perform the motion calibration and those with reduced ROM. The regression method
relies on accurate marker placement and requires well trained staﬀ[58].
1.3
Problem Presentation
The predictions from biomechanical models of human gait analysis has been shown to be
sensitive to joint center locations. Especially the HJC is found to have a great inﬂuence in
the lower limb kinetics and kinematics [26]. In clinical aspects models may provide valu-
able insight in pathological conditions that have shown to alter the locomotion [26]. This
includes cerebral palsy, post and pre operative monitoring, hip arthroplasty, osteoarthitis,
ACL reconstruction and where the objective is to calculate the forces and moments in
the hip joint. These calculation may assist to form the basis for therapy [58]. Several
studies on hip arthroplasty have relied on three-dimensional motion capture analysis pro-
grams and modelling [10]. Joint kinematic in a clinical setting is often determined by two
segments moving relatively to each other around a certain point. The determination of
the Centre of Rotation (CoR), Hip joint center (HJC), knee joint centre (KJC) and ankle
joint center (AJC) can often be diﬃcult to measure in vivo [22].
An important aspect in motion capture analysis is marker placement. The marker loca-
tion can be inaccurate due to soft tissue artefacts as well as poorly positioned marker
placement [22].
Simulation and modelling, for example the regression and functional
method have been shown to be accurate to approximately 2 cm when compared to X-ray
measurements [6]. A diﬀerence in 2 cm at the HJC has been shown to wrongly estimate
forces by more than 40% [22]. Both the regression and the functional method have in
prior studies demonstrated an average HJC location error of 20-30 mm [3].
Errors from regression method originate from combination of marker placement error and
limitations of the regression equation approximating the true value. The general problem
is that regression models do not account for all the variance in the data [3]. Errors in the
functional methods originate from limited range of motion, functional trial performance
and soft tissue artefacts [4]. Kinematic data is often used to assess clinical gait analysis
[4]. This is highly dependent on accurate force or moment data. On the other hand in-
correct exercise prescription can increase the risk of reinjury or delayed recovery [34]. For
example the classiﬁcation of an athlete’s ACL injury risk was determined by correlating a
deﬁned injury risk threshold with the peak knee moments. Consequently, it is important

to evaluate the model methods accuracy and usability in the context of clinical use. This
is important when choosing a method for motion capture analysis as diagnostic tool in
investigating pathological gait patterns [58].
In this thesis the objective is to compare the commonly used biomechanical models for
gait analysis and assess how the HJC, KJC and AJC location aﬀects loading in the knee
joint. A comparison between Plug-in Gait (PIG), the Conventional Gait Model (CGM),
non-uniform scaling (NUS) and functional modelling calibration methods (FUN) are im-
plemented. All the models will utilize the estimates of the joints centers although the
NUS model incorporates non-uniform scaling at the pelvis, thigh and shank.
1.4
Project Research Questions
The purpose of this research is to conduct a gait analysis and compare the commonly
used biomechanical models Plug-in Gait, CGM2.4 and functional model. Additionally a
non-uniform scaling method will be compared. Each model will estimate HJC, KJC and
AJC location. We will assess how these joint centers will inﬂuence forces and estimate

How do the joint center locations measured using the diﬀerent biomechanical models in-

The Ankle Joint
The ankle joint is a synovial joint connecting the bones of the lower leg, by the distal
end of the tibia and ﬁbula to the talus at the foot. This is known as the talocrural joint.
It is a complex hinge joint consisting of two articulations. The ankle joint is primarily
stabilised by the following ligaments and their respective functions. The anterior, poste-

that reinforces the lateral aspect of the ankle joint restricting lateral dislocation. The del-

the joint in eversion preventing medial dislocation and is composed of the tibionavicular,
tibiotalar and the tibiocalcaneal ligaments [53].

inversion resulting in supination, while the dorsiﬂexion is combined with abduction and
eversion resulting in pronation of the foot. The function of the ankle joint is important for
dorsiﬂexion in normal gait, it allows the tibia to move forward relative to the position of

Tibiofemoral and Patellofemoral Joint
There are two joints at the knee the tibiofemoral and patellofemoral joint. The tibiofemoral

2.5
Muscle Activation
2.5
Muscle Activation
Muscle activity during gait is variable between subjects and varies with change in velocity
as with higher speed the more muscle activation is required [24].
Heel strike
At heel strike the hip joint is partially ﬂexed at around 30◦, the knee joint is slightly
ﬂexed at around 5◦and ﬁnally the ankle joint is slightly plantar ﬂexed at around 4◦.
Initially at heel strike the glutes and hamstrings will work concentrically to initiate hip
extension to facilitate forward locomotion, thereby an extensor moment is induced. The
quadriceps will control the knee ﬂexion right after heel strike by eccentric contraction
and then concentrically contract to move the body forward. In the ankle the joint is
in nearly neutral position at heel strike, but due to the slight dorsiﬂexion in the ankle
joint the dorsiﬂexor muscles, mainly the tibialis anterior and extensor digitorum longus
are contracting concentrically prior to the heel strike. At ground contact they contract
eccentrically as the foot is lowered to the ﬂoor increasing plantar ﬂexion.
Mid-stance
At mid-stance the hip joint is beginning to move into an extended position at around 10◦,
the knee joint is slightly ﬂexed at around 6-7◦to cushion the eﬀect of the heel strike and
ﬁnally the ankle joint is slightly dorsiﬂexed at around 4◦as the leg is moving over the
foot. Hip abductors work concentrically during the ﬁrst 66 % of the stance phase inducing
inward rotation for controlling the positioning and tilt of pelvis in the early loading stance
phase. The ﬁrst half of the stance phase the hamstrings work concentrically to induce a
hip extensor moment for forward locomotion [53].
Toe oﬀ
At toe oﬀthe hip joint is partially ﬂexed at around 30◦, the knee joint is slightly ﬂexed
at around 5◦and ﬁnally the ankle joint is slightly plantar ﬂexed at around 4◦. In normal
walking hip extensors are only slightly active and the hip and knee joints are starting to
prepare to ﬂex for the swing phase. The plantar ﬂexor muscles works mainly in the last
half of the stance phase, building up a plantar ﬂexor moment for the push oﬀphase.

The modelling method can have a substantial inﬂuence on the outcome of

3.1
Plug-in Gait
The Plug-in Gait model is Vicon’s implementation of the Conventional Gait Model. This
model is based on the Newington-Helen Hayes gait model [4]. It provides a method to

joint center that deﬁnes the proximal joint for the segment, for example, one of the three
deﬁning points for the tibia is the knee joint center.

4 State of the Art
Musculoskeletal modelling is commonly used in clinical analysis and treatment of injury
[33][57][12].
In order to answer the research question the appropriate motion capture
system hardware and biomechanical modelling software should be determined. Research
conducted at Parker Institute already uses the Vicon motion capture system and OpenSim
for gait analysis. This review is to verify these systems will allow us perform accurate
clinical gait analysis and compare to already existing products.
4.1
Motion Capture Systems
The analysis of movement is accomplished by observing how speciﬁc body segments move
in relation to each other and also their environment. For example in gait analysis the legs
are observed and data such as stride length, heel strike, toe oﬀand pronation of the foot
among many other variables of interest. Movement recorded by a motion capture system
can then be used to calculate body mechanics and retrieve important information such
as forces and moments at joints and muscles. Three-dimensional motion capture analysis
is the gold standard in movement research [14] [55].
The most applicable motion capture systems to use is dependent on environment. There
can be many categories and this state of the art review by Kruk and Reijne, 2018 [36]
has already deﬁned a well designed protocol. For example a diﬀerent a motion capture
system may be more applicable or feasible than another whether the recording is in-
door/outdoor, a team/individual, in water or even within the human body. There are
four main sensors that are applicable these include: Electromagnetic (EMS), Image Pro-
cessing Systems (IMS), Optoeletric (OMS) and Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU). Some
commercially available motion capture systems that are used in human movement anal-
ysis include Xsens, Optitrack, Qualisys, NDI and Vicon, they all have their advantages
and disadvantages and they will brieﬂy be reviewed and compared below.
Xsens provides is a wearable inertial measurement unit (IMU) systems that allows mea-
surement in such sport-speciﬁc settings (Blair et al., 2018). Xsens combines multiple iner-
tial sensors such as accelerometers, gyroscopes and magnetometers ﬁxed to body segments.
It enables acceleration, angular velocity and magnetic ﬁeld measurements (Roetenberg et
al., 2007, 2013). Through integration, 3D position and orientation of each segment can
be obtained.
The other systems mentioned are optical systems. These systems use infrared and the
high-end systems today are able to provide sub millimeter accuracy [36] in tracking the
position of an object. To measure position tracking markers are used, they can be either
active or passive markers. Active markers emit infrared light and operate at a unique
frequency and passive markers are retroreﬂective. Unfortunately tracking of markers can

5.5
Muscle Model Properties
To solve the motion equations, the inverse dynamics tool uses the known motion to ﬁnd
the unknown generalized forces. Multibody dynamics are determined via motion data.
For an observed set of dynamics static optimization is used to solve for related muscle
activations. As the body has more muscles than degrees-of-freedom there are multiple
possible solutions, this is known as the "muscle redundancy problem" [18]. An assumption
made when using static optimization is that the tendons are assumed rigid. Modelling
the tendon as rigid has been shown to signiﬁcantly decrease computation time and has
been shown to be valid for low speed activities such as walking [31].
The length-tension relationship of muscle is measured as a function of sarcomere length.
The Rajagopal model uses Millard 2012 Equilibrium Muscle model.
The model uses
realistic curves to ﬁt the active and passive length curves of muscle from experiments on
sarcomere length [27] [75]. OpenSim allows the conﬁguration of parameters such as the
min/max active normalised ﬁber length, shallow ascending slope and transition of ﬁber
length. These parameters are set speciﬁcally for each muscle giving a realistic force at a
given muscle length.
In OpenSim the musculotendon properties are applied to the model to represent the force
length curves, this is where the force output from a tendon is dependent on its length [44]
[45]. The force-velocity relationships have also been determined [28] [32] and applied to
the model through the Millard equilibrium equation.

6.1
Research Questions
Overview of how we intend to answer the research questions.
Research Question 1:
We aim to obtain the knee joint loads of the study participants when walking for the
diﬀerent biomechanical models. First we acquire the motion capture of subjects from the
MIRAKOS project in Vicon to obtain the joint centers for the models. Then we model
the subjects in MATLAB using the OpenSim API to calculate the joint reaction forces in
the knee.
Research Question 2:
We want to see if there are notable diﬀerences between the biomechanical models. This
can be achieved by analysing the previously obtained joint reaction forces. This will be
understood by investigating the joint center locations. If the models tibiofemoral force
exceeds the FUN model by ∼10% BW contact force(N) it is determined to be notable.
Research Question 3:
We aim to compare the diﬀerences in knee joint loading between the ACLR and CL leg
for the models. We will group the data by ACLR and CL leg and then compare the
diﬀerences with plots.

Gap Filling
In Vicon, several versatile gap ﬁlling options are provided as build ins within the software.
The ﬁve diﬀerent options each have their strengths and limitations in speciﬁc situations,
where the optimal method is used based on the optimal gap ﬁll. In the data processing
of the gait analysis a pipeline called ’Autogap ﬁll MIRAKOS’ is used to automatically
detect the gait cycles in contact with the force plates and used three of the ﬁve gap ﬁlling
options. The chosen options for the pipeline are spline ﬁll, rigid body ﬁll and pattern ﬁll.
Spline ﬁll is used to ﬁll small gaps under 5 frames, which has a straight trajectory by using
a Woltring algorithm to predict the most possible trajectory. The option strengths and
limitation is to not dependent on other markers, ignoring noise patterns and is generally
inaccurate in ﬁlling larger gaps respectively. Rigid body ﬁll is used to ﬁll larger gaps
in rigid segments like shank, thigh, pelvis and torso, where the trajectory is calculated
from the trajectories of three other markers from that segment, thereby applying the
movement of the segment to the missing marker. This is considered to be the most robust
and reliable option but has the limitations of requiring 3 source markers from the same
segment without them having gaps themselves and is potential to propagate noise from
source markers. Pattern ﬁll is also able to compute a possible trajectory from a chosen
source marker, which has the advantage to only require 1 source marker, but has the
limitation being highly inﬂuenced by the chosen marker regarding noise and change of
trajectory. The gait trials are then manually post processed to secure gap free and correct
labelling gait trials before modelling and exporting the data.

Weighting Markers
After scaling the model’s markers must be set to match the experimental marker positions
in the static pose. We weight the joint centers and the anatomical markers the highest.
We set weights to 0 for the thigh and shank markers. The static pose is calculated by
attempting to match based on both the experimental marker position and the generalized
coordinate values. The marker locations are averaged for a set time range. To determine
how strongly the algorithm should try to match markers and coordinates, weights are
used. A weighted least squares algorithm is used once a static pose is set and the all
model markers are moved to the averaged positions of the experimental markers.

landmark but susceptible to skin artefact and conﬁdent valued at 20 due to placement on

6.14
Reserve Actuators
Reserves actuators were required for inadequate muscle force production at a given time
step. Separate reserves actuator ﬁles were used for the left and right leg. Weak reserve
actuators was set for the active leg in the simulation and strong reserve actuators for the
contralateral leg.
The weak reserve actuators aimed to only activate when needed with the minimal applied
of torque. The strong actuators aimed to enforce the model to use the actuators [60].
There were added strong reserve actuators for the subtalar joint constraining the inversion
and eversion movement, to ensure taking the degrees of freedom of the joint into account
for the kinematic analysis, but avoiding modelling inaccuracies causing problem because
of missing ankle ligaments. The muscles were mass less and was acting in the line of
action corresponding to the anatomical muscle going from the origin and insertion.
These actuators are deﬁned in a conﬁguration ﬁle as follows: 3 point actuators and 3
torque actuators which were applied to the pelvis and 6 coordinate actuators for each leg,
which were applied to hip ﬂexion, hip adduction, hip rotation, knee angle, ankle angle
and subtalar angle.

Joint Center Estimation Comparison
The point kinematics analysis tool from OpenSim was used to extract the trajectory of
markers of the hip, knee and ankle joints relative to ground for subjects in standing pose.
The data has been processed so joint marker position estimation for the left and right

8 Discussion
In this section we evaluate our ﬁndings in relation to the research questions. The aim of
research question 1 was to apply four diﬀerent modelling approaches to study participants
and compare the results of the obtained knee joint loads. Using Vicon and MATLAB with
the OpenSim API we were able to model subjects using the estimated HJC, KJC and
AJC by applying PIG, CGM and FUN algorithms to the trajectory data. JC locations
were determined using predeﬁned functions and pipelines in Vicon. Using the OpenSim
API we implemented IK, ID, SO and JRA to ﬁnd the loading in the knee and compared
the four diﬀerent models.
The three models PIG, CGM and NUS were compared to the FUN model as a reference.
In the total TFF plot, Figure 7.13 the PIG and CGM knee joint loading was only slightly
higher than the FUN model and the NUS model showed greater force compared to the
three other models. This result was further explored in research question 2. Here we
wanted to see if there were systematic diﬀerences between the models and determine if
the magnitude diﬀerence was notable. Models that exceeded the FUN model by ∼10%
BW contact force(N) were considered notable.
The PIG, CGM and NUS model were found to exceed BW contact force(N) by more than
10% in the medial TF compartment and only the NUS model exceeded the FUN model
by 10% in the lateral compartment. Force estimation is relevant as joint loads are used to
assess risk of injury and design rehabilitation programs for patients who have progressive
knee loading [42], [62]. The estimated HJC location for the PIG model was estimated to
be ∼5 mm more medial than the CGM and 11.9 mm more medial to the FUN. We suspect
this could potentially account for the higher force found in the medial TF compartment,
see Figure 7.13.
The NUS model was the most similar to the FUN model in JC location of all the models
although it overestimated the TFF more than the other models in the late stance phase
both in the medial and lateral compartment. This suggests that scaling non-uniformly
may have had an impact on the TFF in the knee more than the JC location.

8.2
Inverse Kinematics and Dynamics
8.2
Inverse Kinematics and Dynamics
Movement patterns and kinematics are studied to predict joint health, in particular the
knee ﬂexion angle has been found to inﬂuence knee health. The aﬀect by knee ﬂexion
angle was studied by shabani et al. 2014 [67]. In this study they found the ACLR group
to have a knee joint extension deﬁcit in the standing phase. They concluded the kinematic
ﬁndings could lead to abnormal loading in the knee joint and initiate the process for future
chondral degeneration.
Also a predictor for knee joint injury progression in OA is the knee adduction moment [5].
It has been shown that a greater than normal peak external knee adduction moment was
found in subjects with knee OA during walking [5]. Knee adduction angle has been shown
to mainly load the medial compartment in a varus knee, a neutrally aligned knee loads
the lateral more than the medial compartment and a valgus alignment has been shown
to predominantly load the lateral compartment [2]. Increased knee adduction moments
and external orientation of the leg are also indicative of increased loads on the medial
compartment [37]. In our model we lock the knee with the angle found during the static
calibration trial, so walking trials use a constant knee adduction angle, this would in turn
aﬀect the IK, ID, SO and the knee joint loads found in the JRA. This means we are not
able to see the impact on the knee contact force by changes in knee adduction angle/mo-
ment during the trials.
Overall the kinematic results showed same angle motion pattern, seen in normal gait dur-
ing stance phase when compared to the literature and the validation dataset [15]. The
NUS model showed most consistency with the FUN model as it simulated the similar
angle for hip, knee and ankle during stance phase. In general though, diﬀerences can be
seen between all the models depending on the trial, the variance was only shown for the
FUN model. To visualise the diﬀerences for the other models the kinematic data for each
trial can be seen in Appendix E.
8.3
Static Optimization
Muscle activations have been studied by Lindström et al. 2009[43] and Hart et al. 2010
[30] where they evaluated the ACLD based on the quadriceps, tibialis anterior and the
lateral gastrocnemius. Hart et al. 2010 found muscle activation deﬁcits in quadriceps,
where as Lindström et al. 2009 found an early co-contraction in TA and LG. This suggest
that muscle activations need to be accurately modeled to account for individual diﬀer-
ences.
For example SO has been shown to not completely accurately predict hip muscle acti-
vation patterns, muscle co-contractions and hip muscle forces Hoang et al. 2018 [76].
In their study they used the Calibrated EMG-Informed Neuromusculoskeletal Modelling
(CEINMS) tool in OpenSim which allowed them to incorporate of EMG data in their

model. With this approach they found they were able to predict hip joint contact force
in a group with a high level of co-contraction. Another alternative to SO is the CMC
tool. In the OpenSim documentation it has been noted that the SO may choose to ac-

the CMC tool EMG recordings can be implemented so that the experimental EMG will

of this project we did not include EMG and we did not speciﬁcally modify the variables
mentioned, although we suspect they could aﬀect the knee contact force estimations.

Validating Muscle Activation Patterns
We compare our muscle activation patterns to literature for the quadricep, hamstrings
and calf muscles. The quadriceps showed a ﬁrst peak activation which is consistent with
normal gait activation in the early stance phase. Although the second peak is larger than
described in the literature [68].
For the hamstrings, when the hip is in an extended position the biceps femoris or lateral
hamstrings function as an external rotator, this could explain the late stance phase acti-
vation. In general a larger external rotation was found in the subject group, which could
reﬂect the late stance phase muscle activation [78].
In the muscle activation for the shank a greater second activation peak was found for both
the NUS and FUN model for plantar ﬂexors and dorsiﬂexors see Figure 7.11 and 7.12.
Normally the muscle activation for the dorsiﬂexors concentrates mainly around the early
stance phase as they function by eccentric work to lower the foot towards the ground.
Although a second activation peak was found in the muscle activation for the dorsiﬂexors.
As we know the subject group walked with a greater external orientation of the lower limb,
which is also reﬂected by the greater eversion angle and moment, thereby explaining the
second peak activation [53].

Limitations to The Thesis and Further Development
8.5
Limitations to The Thesis and Further Development
There are some limitations to the thesis needed to be acknowledged. The study could be
validated with a control group to compare the muscle activation and knee contact forces.
In the study from Sanford et al. 2013 [64] they included a control group and an ACLR
group, and found the TFF to be greater at 4-5 × BW compared to the control group at
3-4 × BW.
Furthermore, the thesis is limited by not including an anatomical hip, knee and ankle
joint center location, this can be obtained by X-ray or CT imaging measurements which
are the normal the procedure for determining HJC location [46].
Due to time constraints we are not able to process the data from all trials for each of the
OpenSim processing pipelines as in Figure 6.7. This could potentially risk that we are
using an outlier in our data. This could inﬂuence the results of the analysis so we are
over or under estimating the knee joint loads.
Another possible limitation is the number of subjects and gender diﬀerences, although we
found that we were able to answer our research questions with the amount of data used.
Other studies have looked at gender diﬀerences and found joint kinematics and dynamics
to be aﬀected by anatomical structure eg. pelvis width or body composition [54].
A study by Lima et al. 2013 [42] identiﬁed four limitations which could be applied to
our knee model. First, the geometry of the articulation surface requires a more complex
knee joint model. The knee joint could be modeled as a multiaxial joint with 3 DOF.
This would in turn inﬂuence the forces and kinematics. Secondly, the contribution by
ligaments and cartilage ﬂuid maintaining the stability in knee joint could be included.
Third, there are multiple muscles for the the same movement function. Major muscles
crossing the knee joint also cross the adjacent joints, the hip and ankle joint. Fourth there
is high complexity to determine the resultant contact force location. This introduces ma-
jor complexity in analyzing a common activity motion such as walking.
Lastly the artifacts from soft tissue movements could be considered as a limitation, as
musculoskeletal modelling using inverse dynamics generally is sensitive to soft tissue ar-
tifacts [54]. However the modelling was mainly computed from the static and dynamic
calibration techniques and the markers susceptible for source of errors, were taken out in
the scaling process, as they introduced low weight certainty.

8.6
Alternative Joint Center Estimation Techniques
8.6
Alternative Joint Center Estimation Techniques
We calculated hip, knee and ankle joint center locations using PIG, CGM and FUN meth-
ods although there are alternative estimation techniques available. Clinical gait analysis is
a ﬁeld still under research and being able to ﬁnd simpler, more accurate and non-invasive
techniques for estimating joint center locations is desirable. We acknowledge that alterna-
tive algorithms exist to calculate HJC, KJC and AJC but they were not applicable to our
project. For example an article from Sauret et al. 2016 [65] evaluated three functional cal-
ibration techniques, axis transformation/SARA, geometric axis ﬁt and DynaKAD. They
were compared with conventional marker-based method to ﬁnd the medio-lateral axis of
the femur. Although in our study we used SARA with the Boeth 2013 method [9] to
estimate the KJC, it would be wise to compare and research for method that provides
the best solution in future studies.
In another study by and Passmore et al. 2016 [56] they investigated a 3D freehand ul-
trasound technique. They hypothesized that this method could be used to deﬁne the
anatomical coordinate system of the femur and be used to determine the location of the
HJC.
We suspect these techniques and others are possible alternatives to the methods used
although further investigation is required to know if these methods would improve on the
current techniques used in this thesis.

9 Conclusion
In this thesis we demonstrated the estimated knee contact forces by implementing PIG,
CGM, NUS and FUN models. We found knee contact forces to be diﬀerent from model
to model although the results were in line with other studies such as Obrębska et al. 2019
[54] where they found similar knee joint contact loads between 3- 4 normalised BW %.
The applicability of these results is dependent on whether they are used for academic re-
search or clinical purposes. The latter would require a more reﬁned model that accounts
for individual diﬀerences. The individual diﬀerences in this study were hidden when the
data was averaged. Even though we used the NUS model to account for diﬀerences in
the anatomical structure of individuals, we believe more needs to be done to validate
the technique used. Irrespective of the model chosen using methods such as x-ray or 3D
ultrasound could be used to compare results with an anatomical joint center, for example
a sub sample population of the study could be used.
It was also possible to show inter limb diﬀerences although we found the associated force
is subject speciﬁc rather than only model dependent. Other studies have shown some
ACLR patients replicated gait pattern from the injured leg to the CL leg [43]. We ﬁnd
this factor could inﬂuence the ﬁndings we see in gait trials making it diﬃcult to determine
inter limb diﬀerences. A way to validate the inter limb diﬀerence could be to include a
unilaterally dynamic movement such as a step-up, step-down, sit-to-stand, one-leg verti-
cal jump or lunge.
Based on our results, if we had to choose only one model we would select the NUS
model even though it over estimated knee joint forces compared to the FUN model. We
suggest using a non-uniform scaling method as a preferred modelling technique. This
method incorporates the unique anthropometric measurements of an individual which is
required when wanting to make an accurate assessment. Care must taken to make sure
manual measurements and marker placement are accurate as scaling is dependent on these
values and can highly impact the ﬁndings. In future it is suggested to take a photograph
of individual subjects holding a known dimension as it is a quick and eﬀective way to
validate scaling measurements.

The hip joint is the second largest weight-bearing joint and has intrinsic stability provided

constitute as a attachment for muscles which functions for the mobility in both the hip
and knee joint.

A.5
Kinematics and Kinetics
Gait Walking is a highly energy eﬃcient and complex method of progression involving
rhythmical, reciprocal movements of the whole body in forward locomotion. In normal
walking the gait cycle can be broken down to certain phases, single leg stance phase
composed of heel strike, mid stance and toe oﬀfor the standing leg and swing phase for
the swinging leg followed by double leg support, then switching to the other leg single
support. In this study the analytical data mainly concerns the single leg stance phase
the inverse dynamics is based on the the ground reaction force data since it is measured
directly by the foots contact with the force plate [24]. In gait analysis, when considering
pathological gait the aim is then often to identify the opposing movement force as it will
disrupt the normal propulsive gait pattern force, and is therefore more enlightening for
the pathological tendencies.
Ground Reaction Force The ground reaction force is the force exerted on the foot
by the ground, giving a vertical and horizontal component of the force.
The force is
proportional to the mass and acceleration of the object, thus the mass of the subject as
object and acceleration of the foot at foot strike.
Page 72 of 127
==PAGE:81==
A.5
Kinematics and Kinetics
Vertical Components
The vertical component has a characteristic M-shape curve pattern, due propulsive gait
pattern and ground reaction force as the trajectory of the body mass centre is changing
during the stance phase in gait.
The trajectory of the centre of mass is changing with the ground reaction force changing
as a result of mainly three diﬀerent stages in the stance phase, heel strike, mid-stance and
toe oﬀ.
Upward Acceleration Phase
At point A there is double limb support and the body is at its lowest, as the centre of
mass is travelling horizontally reducing the vertical velocity. As the body is shifting to
single leg support the inverted pendulum mechanism take over and the body is accelerated
upwards so that the combined ground reaction is greater than the body weight. Thereby
also accelerate the centre of mass upwards by the ground reaction, which will continue
until the opposite foot oﬀand the force will drop below the body weight at point B.
Upwards Deceleration Phase
From point B the ground reaction drops below the gravity, the centre of mass will expe-
rience total downward force, which will cause the body to decelerate until point c. From
here the centre of mass is travelling horizontally and the vertically component is close to
0, resulting in downwards acceleration phase. The total force is still acting downwards as
the gravity is greater than the ground reaction. Thus the downward velocity of the COM
will then increase as while decelerate downwards, and continue until the ground reaction
again increases above the body weight close to standing leg toe oﬀphase at point D.
Downward Deceleration Phase
At point D the total force is upwards and the as the GR is greater than the BW, and
after the peak the COM is travelling downwards will lead to a deceleration of downwards
movement, and the standing leg drops below the BW as the foot is coming of the ground,
just after the next double limb support starts at point E which is illustrating the same as
point A.

Moments
Moment is a torque force acting a rotational acceleration about a joint center by acting
on a lever arm resulting in an angular velocity.
Thereby moments is a product of a
magnitude and the perpendicular distance between the line of action, and the axis it
is acting on.
In inverse dynamics analysis, knowing the forces and accelerations, the
moments acting internally at the joints can be computed. By describing the moments
acting on a joint to either be dominated by the ﬂexor of extensor muscle group giving
cause to the measured movement. Though a joint can be described to be ﬂexor dominant,
it does not necessarily mean the joint is doing a ﬂexion movement, but rather that the
ﬂexor dominant groups acting on the joint by either eccentric or contracting activity.

Moments thus can be calculated through inverse dynamics approach from the ground
reaction force exerting on the foot in stance phase, modiﬁed by terms to reﬂect the eﬀects
of gravity and accelerations occurring or inertial terms. As the ground reaction force are
acting on the foot, an externally moment is applied to the foot which must be resisted
by the muscle moment crossing the joint the moment is acting on. The moment exerted
by the ground reaction can be calculated from knowing the magnitude of the force and
the point of interest, center of pressure (COP) and position of the joint through which
the force is acting. As before mentioned the force is measured on a force plate during
gait in the three components x,y and z acting in the three dimensions. The perpendicular
distance from the line of action of the force to the joint centre and multiplied with the
ground reaction. The eﬀects of gravity is then subtracted on the segments distal to the
joint and the acceleration of these segments.
Joint Moments in Normal Gait
Joint moments are net quantities and the result of all internal structures that can produce
moment around the joint center rotation axis.
The COP changes because the body
moves and the body moves primarily because our muscles produce force that turns into
moments.
Joint Moments in Normal Gait The joint moment will change during gait as the
COP will change due to body movement. At heel strike the COP is located under the
heel and then propagate anterior lateral under the foot during mid stance step, and then
ﬁnally anterior medial to the toe oﬀstep. At heel strike the eﬀects of ground reaction
is to plantarﬂex the ankle, which induce opposite internal moment to dorsiﬂex the foot.
As the COP moves anterior the ground reaction eﬀect acts to dorsiﬂexing the foot due
to the joints position in mid stance which induce the opposite internal eﬀect rising to
planterﬂexion of the foot, and continues to rise to the peak at the toe oﬀstep. The
moment then reduce as the ground reaction diminish in magnitude.
Knee moment The knee moment likewise can change during the stance phase depending
on whether the knee is ﬂexed or extended. At heel strike the knee is well extended, the
foot is at this point in front of the knee and the ground reaction force vector passes in
front of the knee and require an internal knee ﬂexor moment to resist the external motion.
The knee then starts to ﬂex in the early stance phase, to move forward over the planted
foot. In mid stance the knee ﬂexion brings the knee in front of the ground reaction to
induce an internal knee extensor moment to reach a peak at the time of maximum stance
phase.
By inverted pendulum mechanism takes over the single support the knee then begins to
extend and the forward velocity reduces. Then the ground reaction passes in front of
the knee leading to internal knee extensor moment in the middle and late single support
phase. In the toe oﬀphase will then begin to ﬂex as the knee moves in front of the
ground reaction, inducing an internal knee extensor moment, preventing the knee over
ﬂexing.
