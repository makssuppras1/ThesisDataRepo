Abstract
In the optic to enhance the diagnoses of the presence or state of abnormalities in biological
tissues, the Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) oﬀers a non-invasive method sensitive to the
optical properties of the sample.
This Master Thesis compiles, in an overview, the actual knowledge about modelling OCT signals
and the advices collected in various articles on the method to extract optical properties.
The ﬁrst step undertook has been to characterise the components of the signal speciﬁc of the
commercial OCT system used throughout this project. Thereby, the sensitivity roll-oﬀand the
confocal point spread function were estimated. This brought to light the elliptical shape of the
OCT beam due to a shift of 0.146 mm between the focus points of each axis.
From there, an experimental campaign has been conducted on well-characterised microsphere
suspensions to investigate the models of light attenuation inside of a scattering medium. The
precision and accuracy of the single scattering model and the multiple scattering models were
examined for samples with diﬀerent scattering coeﬃcient µs. The optical depth τ was appraised
as a criterium for determining the scattering regime. This leads to reﬂections on the optimal
experimental conditions and selection of data to extract optical properties.
This Master Thesis paves the way for observations of biological samples with OCT in order to
guide the diagnosis with extracted optical properties.

Part 1
Introduction
1.1 Context
In the context of healthcare, optical properties of tissues as the absorption coeﬃcient µa, the
scattering coeﬃcient µs, and the anisotropy factor g could serve as indicators for the presence or
the state of abnormalities. Studies have already shown a connection between optical properties
and the status of the tissue observed, as lens micro- and macro-scale features related to possible
cataract development [7], diﬀerent coeﬃcients of absorption and scattering between normal
white matter and tumorous tissue in the brain [8], and the diﬀerentiation between benign nevi
and melanoma. [9] However, measuring the optical properties of a turbid media is surprisingly
diﬃcult, especially in vivo where several additional restrictions are added.
Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT), an optical ranging technique, is sensitive to those opti-
cal properties. Non-invasive and non-damaging, it is used to obtain two-dimensional or three-
dimensional images of biological tissues with a micrometer resolution. The near-IR light typically
employed for OCT enables the light to penetrate into the samples on the order of a few mil-
limeters. Indeed, this range of wavelengths oﬀers low absorption and scattering of the light in
biological tissues. Moreover the relatively low energy of the photons allows to avoid damaging
the tissues while imaging. This is why OCT, with the association of imaging and extraction
of optical properties, has the potential to become an alternative to biopsy for early diagnoses.
Medical personnel could abstain from using this extremely invasive procedure and emit a ﬁrst
diagnosis from OCT inputs. That would limit the number of patients that have to go through
biopsy which would be used only to conﬁrm the diagnoses, if the need arises.
Nevertheless, retrieving optical properties from OCT measurements is an emerging ﬁeld, even
if the theoretical framework exists. [10][11] Several models describing the light attenuation in
samples were established based on diﬀerent hypotheses. This project will investigate three of
the models to determine their range of validity.
1.2 OCT principle
Optical Coherence Tomography is based on computed tomography where a digital volumetric
model of the targeted object is reconstructed from the light scattered through an object. [12][13]
The working principle of a Spectral-Domain OCT (SD-OCT) is described in Fig 1.1. Based on
an interferometer, the light from a broadband and continuous-wave source is split into a sample
arm and a reference arm of ﬁxed length. The light exiting the reference ﬁber is reﬂected back on
a mirror resulting on a delay. The light exiting the sample ﬁber goes through a lens that focuses
the beam on the sample. Once backscattered or reﬂected from the sample, the light makes its
way back to combine with the light coming back from the reference arm.

1.3.1
Scattering coeﬃcient and anisotropic factor
Due to the presence of diﬀerent cells and or-
ganelles, the mean real refractive index of the tissue undergoes small local variations.
This
non-uniformity is the source of deviations of the light from its original straight path. This phe-
nomenon is called scattering. [14] Depending on the size ratio between the scattering particle
and the wavelength of the light, diﬀerent theories describe the phenomenon. In the case of
biological tissue, the scattering particles are on the same scale as the wavelength. [15] Thus, the

Mie Theory is suitable to calculate the optical properties. The scattering coeﬃcient is linked
to the probability of a scattering event happening. For a spherical particle with an eﬀective
scattering cross-section σs [L2], the scattering coeﬃcient is deﬁned as µs = σs · ρs where ρs
[L−3] is the concentration of scattering particles per volume. In other words, 1/µs [L] is the
expected mean free path of a photon, which means the distance a photon can travel without
any scattering event happening.
As the number of scattering event is represented by the scattering coeﬃcient, the direction of
this scattering is depicted by the anisotropic factor g. This factor can also be described as
g =< cos(θ) > where θ is the scattering angle and < ... > the average over all scattering events.
In biological tissue, the scattering is predominantly in the forward direction, which entails that
g ≃1. [14] In the modelling of the light, the eﬀective anisotropic factor geff = cos(θrms) is used
where θrms is the root-mean-square scattering angle.
Due to their nature, the scattering coeﬃcient µs and the anisotropic factor g cannot be measured
independently as they are interlinked. [16][9]
1.3.2 Absorption coeﬃcient
In tissues, certain molecules called chromophores can absorb the incoming photons. [17] If the
incident photon has the energy corresponding to a transition of energy levels for the encountered
molecule or atom, this one absorbs the photon to rise in energy. The absorption coeﬃcient µa
[L−1] is deﬁned in a similar manner than the scattering coeﬃcient as µa = σa · ρa where σa
[L2] is the eﬀective absorption cross-section and ρa [L−3] the density of absorption particles per
volume. The absorption is reﬂected by a negative imaginary component of the refractive index
and thus, µa = 2k · nI where k = 2π/λ is the wavenumber and nI the absolute value of the
imaginary part of the refractive index of the medium. [18]
All in all, the total attenuation in tissues is due to both the scattering and the absorption.
Therefore, the attenuation coeﬃcient µt is deﬁned as µt = µs+µa. To compare the predominance
of absorption and scattering, the albedo is the ratio of those two components: a =

Depending on the penetration depth z and the scatter-

• Single Scattering regime: The photons have

• Multiple Scattering regime: For 1 < τ < 10,
the photons have been scattered several times be-

• Diﬀusion regime: The photons are scattered
many times to the point where they travel through
random walk in the medium and τ > 10.
The transitions between each regime are not as sharp as the previous description can lead to
believe. However, the optical depth can be used to determined roughly the regime for the OCT
measurements, as it results in diﬀerent shape of signals.
1.4 Thesis objectives
The extraction of optical properties from OCT signals has shown to be a promising ﬁeld for early
diagnoses in biological tissues. Several models to described these signals have been developed
to this end. [10][11]
The aim of this Master Thesis is to establish an overview of the scattering models of OCT signals
with their respective application ﬁeld in relation to the sample nature. This split into several
objectives:
• A literature review of the various models made to describe the attenuation of the light in a
sample and the instrumental eﬀects present in an OCT signal, as well as a summary of the
diﬀerent methods used to extract the optical properties with well-characterized samples ;
• A characterisation of the instrumental eﬀects on the OCT system as the sensitivity roll-oﬀ
and the confocal point spread function in order to be able to isolate the signal due solely
to the attenuation from the sample ;
• A study of the diﬀerent models with extraction of optical parameters from well-deﬁned
samples, including an evaluation of the optimal method for extraction and an investigation
of the position of the transition between the single and multiple scattering regime.
This project has taken place within the Biophotonic Imaging group at DTU Health Tech De-
partment, situated on the DTU campus in Risø.

2.1 Light Scattering Modelling
The attenuation of the OCT beam in a turbid medium as in biological tissues is mainly the result
of the scattering of the incident light. For their typical optical properties, the scattering regimes
encountered are the single scattering and multiple scattering regimes.
This section presents
commonly used models for the extraction of optical properties and focuses on the curve ﬁtting
method.
2.1.1 Single Scattering
The Single Scattering (SS) regime concerns an incident beam that propagates in the forward
direction and is attenuated by absorption and scattering until it back-scatters. The assumptions
made for this model are the following: [10][11]
• Plane wave illumination
• Homogeneous optical scattering: isotropic media consisting of identical hard spherical
particles
• First-order of Born approximation: each particle is illuminated by the same incident ﬁeld
• The photons are only scattered once before reaching the objective
The decay of the OCT signal intensity is directed by Beer’s law. However, the light travels
twice the distance in the tissue before been detected, which translates in I(z) ∝exp(−2µsz).

2.1.3 Comparison
The SS model is the one the most widely used for extracting optical parameters [10][11] as it is
the general assumption made for OCT image reconstruction. Indeed, the SS model is a simpler
model, which eases the ﬁtting but it gives less accurate values than MS models. Some eﬀorts
were made to improve its accuracy by introducing the system contributions, as explained in the
next section.
The multiple scattering contribution has for eﬀect to lift the linear decay. This indicates that,
if using the SS model, the scattering coeﬃcient extracted would underestimate the true value of
µs. This is why it is paramount to select the correct model according to the characteristics of
the sample considered.
For Faber et al. [24], the SS model is more eﬃcient for µs between 2 and 6 mm−1 whereas the
MS is more adequate for µs beyond 10 mm−1. The hypothesis explored in this Thesis is that
the limit between the single scattering and the multiple scattering regimes depends both on µs
and on the depth z within the tissue, which can be summarized in the optical depth τ = µs · z.
The boundary between the two regimes should be around τ ≃1.

2.3.2 Overview of Data Treatment
From the overview table (Tab C.3–Tab C.5), a guide line for the data treatment can be made.
Two points are mainly discussed in this section.
Averaging A-scans
The ﬁrst point to discuss is the averaging of the data once one or several B-scans are acquired.
To reduce the noise and the speckle, all articles carry out a type of averaging, whether a moving
average ﬁltering, a lateral or axial averaging, or a combination of several of them. As Faber
et al. [24] pointed out, a moving average ﬁltering only makes sense if the length of averaging
is smaller than the coherence length. Indeed, the averaged data points are sub-resolution and
should have the same amplitude if there was no noise. One should notice that averaging seems
to decrease the precision of the extracted scattering coeﬃcient.
They also mentioned that the number of A-scans averaged should be kept relatively low in order
to be relevant for clinical application. Indeed, one cannot expect a biological sample to have
homogeneous optical properties over too many A-scans. Therefore, the algorithm developed in
§4.2 uses only 50 neighbouring A-scans.
Selection of region of interest
The second moot point concerns the selection of the Region Of Interest (ROI) where the ﬁtting
will take place. The main aim is to obtain an area where the medium presents homogeneous
optical properties.
Thereby, the start of the ROI should not be too close to the interface because of the specular
reﬂection.

3.3.1 Beam Waist measurements
First, after removing the lens, a camera was used to measure the 1/e2 irradiance radius of
the Gaussian beam before the lens. The experiment is explained in details in the Appendixes
§D.c). The results conﬁrms the constant beam diameter of 2W = 4 mm speciﬁed by Thorlabs
in Tab 3.1. However, the beam is lightly elliptical as Wx ≈2.3 mm and Wy ≈2.1 mm. This
means that the beam is collimated and the lens is illuminated with a beam with the optimal
diameter.
To characterise the evolution of the beam depending on the distance to the focus point, images
of identical points were acquired at diﬀerent depths.
Method
In order to obtain identical immobile points for the acquisitions, microspheres with calibrated
diameter of 1 µm were suspended in a viscus gel to impair their movements. The description of
the microspheres can be found in the Appendixes §A). Their size, smaller that the resolution,
enables to obtain the Point Spread Function (PSF) of the system. Indeed, they are small enough
to be perceived only as points by the system. Therefore, their image through the OCT system
is the PSF. This PSF will change according to the distance to the focal point.
At ﬁrst, gels were made without any microspheres in order to determine the gel that displays
the less attenuation on an OCT acquisition. This would minimise the background impact on
the PSF measurements. As Fig 3.7 illustrates, the gelatine gel has an attenuation coeﬃcient
negligible compared to the agarose one.

Discussion on CPSF correction
All in all, for the CPSF correction of OCT data, one need to know the eﬀective Rayleigh length
as well as the factor γ applied for each sample.
From the previous measurements, the correction of the CPSF is conceivable with zR,eff = 0.18
mm and a γ factor changing according to the microsphere suspension used. For the EHF model,
the eﬀective w0 is calculated to be 8.7 µm.
However the correction is not perfect as the model does not ﬁt the data perfectly, as can be
noticed in the Appendixes §E). Especially when the focus position is harder to estimate in the
air because of the two foci of the beam.
Having the focal point in the imaging range provides a higher resolution as one can distinguish
individual microspheres. Therefore, this situation is advantageous for imaging. Nevertheless, for
extracting optical properties, this brings out more issues. Firstly, because of the beam shape,
the CPSF model does not perfectly represent the reality of the signal. Therefore, the data would
not be corrected eﬃciently. Secondly, in the case of samples as in Fig 3.10, averaging over the
focus area only decreases the weights of the data. Indeed, the background signal would be within
the ROI and interfere with the extraction.
Therefore, one should avoid to put the focal point of the beam in the ROI used for ﬁtting the
attenuation due to the sample.
According to §2.3, most articles [9] [31] [21] were positioning the focal plane above the sample
or at the interface of the sample. This led to a sharp decrease of the signal after the focus
because of both the sample attenuation and the CPSF. Therefore, the range of data above the
background level is short. The impact of the position of the focal point on the data range was
studied brieﬂy. The ROI length was calculated for diﬀerent positions of the focus for a cutting
criterium of 35dB. The B-scans acquired are shown in Fig 3.13 as well as the corresponding
A-scans with the ROI.

Optical Parameters
This chapter depicts the extraction of optical properties from OCT data obtained on well-
characterised samples.
After correcting for the system components deﬁned in Chap 3, the
optical properties of the samples were estimated from the light scattering models in §2.1 and
compared to the theoretical values.
The aim of this chapter is to determine the optimal conditions for extracting the optical prop-
erties and investigate the validity of each model of attenuation.
4.1 Method
This section describes the samples used during the experiments and the experimental protocol
followed to collect OCT data for the extraction of optical properties.
4.1.1 Sample properties
The samples were composed of calibrated polymer microspheres suspended in distilled water.
This type of samples combine the simulation of optical properties of biological tissues [39] and
the possibility of theoretical calculations to estimate them.
The microspheres are part of the Thermo Scientiﬁc™4000 Series Monosized Particles that have
a standard size with a very narrow standard deviation. They were delivered along with a Cer-
tiﬁcate of Calibration and Traceability to NIST™. The microspheres are made from polystyrene
that has a refractive index of 1.59 at 589 nm and 25◦C. The supplier, Thermo Fisher Scientiﬁc™,
provides the microspheres in water that contains trace of surfactant to inhibit agglomeration
and promote stability. The product is claimed to be stable for a minimum of 24 months.
After a long time on the shelves, the size and refractive index can be aﬀected. [40] We supposed
this happened to the microspheres stored in the lab for a few years, as the theoretical calculations
do not correspond to the extracted optical properties. When the experiment was repeated with
microspheres bought during this project, the extracted optical properties matched the calculated
ones. So, the sitting time has aﬀected the previous microspheres as the experiment conﬁrmed
the validity of the extraction method.
The initial solution containing the microspheres is diluted with distilled water to obtain various
concentrations of microspheres, which entails various scattering coeﬃcients and absorption co-
eﬃcients. The mixing was made by ultrasonic shacking to get an homogeneous distribution of
the microspheres.
The calculation of the optical properties are based on the Mie Theory mentioned in §1.3. They
are summarised in the Appendixes §A).

The resulting optical properties are saved in result and the statistical properties of the ﬁt are
collected in stat. On top of the RMSE and the ajusted R sq, the goodness of ﬁt is objectively
checked by the following criterium: the ﬁtted parameters must not be equal to the chosen
boundaries in lb or ub.
The ﬁtting is repeated on several selection of A-scans all over the B-scan. From all the extracted
optical properties, the precision and accuracy of the extraction can be calculated.

4.4 Discussion
This section is dedicated to discuss the algorithm developed during this project and the param-
eters impacting on the extraction of optical properties.
4.4.1 Coefficient Estimation Mean and Coefficient Estimation Peaks
As explained in §4.2, two versions of the extraction algorithm were developed: Coefficient Est-
imation Mean and Coefficient Estimation Peaks. The ﬁrst one consists of averaging neigh-
bouring A-scans whereas the second selects only the peak intensity corresponding to the middle
of the spot signal due to one microsphere.
The theoretical models of scattering in §2.1 are not speciﬁc to the mean of the OCT signal.
Nevertheless, the mean is closer to the models because the random perturbations made by the
noise are reduced. This is valid if the averaging is made on homogeneous samples. The ﬁt should
be worse for the peak ﬁnder because each point has this noise that disperses them around the
true value.
Page 35 of 40
==PAGE:44==
Master Thesis
CHAPTER 4. OPTICAL PARAMETERS
This is conﬁrmed by comparing the analysis of the extracted µs in the Appendixes for Coeffi-
cient Estimation Mean in §H.a) and for Coefficient Estimation Peaks in §H.b). The good-
ness-of-ﬁt indicators are worse for the peak ﬁnder than the mean, whereas the precision and
accuracy are equivalent for both.
This can be related to the B-scan appearance. Indeed, on B-scans as Fig 4.2(left), the sig-
nals coming from diﬀerent microspheres are overlapping.
It is harder for the algorithm to
ﬁnd the position of the peaks. In this situation, the Coefficient Estimation Peaks only se-
lects random points within the data. This leads to extracted optical properties equivalent to
Coefficient Estimation Mean but with worse ﬁts, as the data are noisy and less in number.
Coefficient Estimation Peaks would work better in situation where the scatterers are more
dispersed.
Indeed, Coefficient Estimation Mean would then average the scatterers’ signal
with the background signal and decrease the intensity of the scatterers.
Therefore, the averaging should be on lengths smaller than the coherence length [24] in order to
average data points that are supposed to have the same signal. Oversampling in the horizontal
direction could be a solution to increase the number of neighbouring points on the same scale.
According to [24], as µs, geff and µa are macroscopic properties, lateral averaging on a volume
with homogeneous properties is needed to measure with accuracy the optical properties. How-
ever, the comparison with Coefficient Estimation Peaks highlights that averaging is not a
requirement to obtain accurate extracted properties.
4.4.2 Uncertainties on the inputs
The extraction of optical properties is conditioned by each input on the algorithm described
in §4.2. Therefore the uncertainties around these parameters are paramount to understand.
Indeed, they regulate the calculation of distances and the correction of the signal.
In sample info, n 0 was entered in THORLABS Software for the display of the distances on
screen. The refractive index from the cuvette wall n cuvette was given by the supplier [41] with
a precision at 10−3.
However the others parameters, the refractive index of the microsphere suspensions n and the
geometrical distance travelled by the light in the cuvette wall d wall, had to be measured
experimentally. In order to determine the precision and accuracy of the methods, they were
tested on characterised samples. This is explained in the Appendixes §I) and §J).
The optical path shifting method to measure the refractive index of a sample has a precision
of around 10−3 and an accuracy lower than 0.5%. The precision is estimated by the standard
deviation on several measurements and the accuracy is determined by the relative error to the
true value. With the same deﬁnitions, the evaluation of wall thickness has a high precision and
an accuracy of 0.9%.
In OCT, the horizontal pixel size Dx is entered in the software and depends on the positioning
precision of the OCT system. [6] The focal length of the lens f is given by the provider [36][35]
without any precision.
The central wavelength lambda 0 is given with a precision of 1 nm, which transfers to the vertical
pixel size Dz given with a precision of 10−2 µm by THORLABS. However, the wavelengths
used in the sample are not just the central wavelength but a range of wavelengths around it.
Therefore, as the wavelength conditioned the refractive indexes and all the calculations that stem
from them, it could worsen the accuracy of these parameters when comparing to the eﬀective
parameters. Fortunately, n and d wall are measured with the light source of the OCT system.

Thus, the parameters are estimated for the right wavelengths. As for n cuvette, the refractive
index of the cuvette are made to be constant over a wider range than the OCT light source.
w 0 was estimated in §3.3 with a low precision as it is the eﬀective beam waist at the focus
instead of something measured with a precise method.
In the same way, d f0 was already discuss in §3.3. Its estimation rests upon the following of the

by the experimenter. However, as the shape of the beam is more complex due to the double

outside the data range used for ﬁtting. Therefore, the impact of this approximate correction is
diminished.
In analysis param, the spectral ratio was estimated in §3.2 for a speciﬁc range of the imaging
depth. Thus, the roll-oﬀcorrection is deemed enough in the data range used for ﬁtting. The
other parameters are chosen by the users. n row and dynamic range have already been discussed
in §2.3.2, whereas the initial guesses for the ﬁtting param 0, the upper and lower boundaries of
the range for ﬁtting the optical properties ub and lb will be examined in the next section.
Finally, the theoretical values are also subject to uncertainties, products of the uncertainties
of the inputs used for their calculations. It is discussed in deeper lengths in the Appendixes
§A). Therefore, the accuracies measured in §4.3 are also dependent on the accuracy of the Mie

This nuances the results obtained as some theoretical values could be slightly
inaccurate mainly due to the uncertainty on the concentration of microspheres.

Conclusion
Aiming to improve the diagnoses of pathologies and their evolutions, methods to appraise the
optical properties of biological tissues from non-invasive and fast OCT measurements were exam-
ined during this Master Thesis. Three models of light attenuation were considered and compared
during this work: the SS model, the EHF models without and with absorption.
Beforehand, an overview of recent articles using well-characterised samples was undertook to
analyse the methods of extraction of optical properties and collect guidelines for the development
of an extraction algorithm. This helped decide on the selection of the ROI and the number of
A-scans to average.
In order to isolate the attenuation of OCT signal due to a scattering medium, a characterisation
of the OCT system was tackled to ﬁgure out experimentally the sensitivity roll-oﬀand the
CPSF. This disclosed an issue of the focusing condition of the system. Indeed, there are two
focal points separated by approximately 0.146 mm instead of an unique focus as predicted by
the modelling. In the future, one can look into ﬁxing the system but for now, the issue was
handled by calculating eﬀective parameters for the CPSF and putting the focal points outside
of the ROI. Thus, the imperfect correction of the signal has a limited inﬂuence. Furthermore,
having the focus after the data range used for ﬁtting broadens this data range as the signal is
increased before the focus.
This points out to the importance of knowing the position of the focus in the imaging range.
In our experiments, the focal position was characterised in the air. Nonetheless, this means
that all media the light went through need to be identiﬁed with their respective thicknesses and
refractive indexes.
From then, the algorithm developed to extract optical properties was tested on calibrated sam-
ples composed of microspheres suspended in distilled water. On top of estimating the accuracy
and precision of the algorithm, we investigated the potential of the optical depth as a criterium
to determine the domain of validity of each model. The SS model exhibited high accuracy for
τ < 1 but progressively lost it for τ > 2.
All in all, this work highlighted the signiﬁcance of choosing the right model corresponding to the
sample. Indeed, the EHF models were accurate for all optical depths. However, they displayed
lower precision than the SS model, especially with few data points. Perhaps, it would be more
useful to have a precise ﬁtting than an accurate one for application on biological samples. Thus,
one could distinguish between ill and healthy tissues even if the exact optical properties are not
extracted.
To apply the automatic selection of the ROI to biological samples, one have to consider how
to select an homogeneous area in a complex medium. Indeed, some tissues are multi-layered.
Therefore, it is worth to look upon the implementation of detection of interfaces as described
by Callewaert et al. [44] or the adaption of the model to multiple layer of tissue as Thrane et
al. suggested in [19].

The uncertainties on the inputs have a repercussion on the uncertainties of the theoretical
optical properties of the microsphere suspensions. The refractive indexes were measured with
extreme precision and accuracy in their respective articles [45] and [46]. The diameter was well-
characterised by the provider with a standard deviation of 0.010 µm for the 1 µm microspheres
and 0.022 µm for the 2 µm ones.
However the initial concentrations of microspheres were mentioned as ”approximately” on the
Certiﬁcate of Calibration and Traceability to NIST. Therefore, this is the main uncertainty over
the calculated optical properties. Due to the calculation method, the concentration is propor-
tional to µs and µa, as is their uncertainties. Furthermore, to obtain the diﬀerent concentration,
volumes of microsphere suspension and distilled water were pipetted with an uncertainty of 5
µL. Therefore, the diluted solutions have a higher uncertainty.
All in all, one has to be careful when handling the theoretical optical properties and have in
mind the uncertainties around them.

I) Optical Path Shifting Method
The optical path shifting method is designed to measure the real part of the refractive index of
a medium thanks to the displacement of a refractive surface.
Because the light slows down in a medium of refractive index n, a delay of ∆l of the reference
beam (distance displayed on the OCT) corresponds to a geometrical depth of ∆l/n in the
medium. The aim is to track the displacement of a refractive surface when the sample is added
on the path of the light. [50] [51]
The precision and accuracy of the method was determined by using a sample with known
refractive index. In this case, it was a cuvette and a glass slide from Hellma. [41] A ﬁrst B-scan
was acquired of a mirror reﬂective surface. A second B-scan was taken when the sample is added
on the path of the light. This displacement of the mirror surface OS and the optical thickness
OT of the sample are measured as highlighted in Fig I.26. The measurements were made on
three diﬀerent acquisitions, with ﬁve estimations per acquisition.
Figure I.26: B-scans for optical path shifting method
The same protocol was applied to a cup ﬁlled with diﬀerent concentrations of microspheres. The
reﬂective surface tracked down was the interface with the bottom of the cup. The refractive is
then calculated as:

J) OCT measurements of Thickness
The measurement of the thickness of a sample is made from OCT acquisition. On a B-scan,
the distance separating two interfaces is measured by detected the reﬂection peaks. In order to
obtain the geometrical thickness, one needs to known the refractive index of the sample in order
to properly calculate the distances.
This method was tested on the glass slide from Hellma [41] with a calibrated refractive index
and a thickness measured with a vernier caliper. The thickness was estimated as 1.25 mm with
a precision of 0.05 mm. The glass slide was then positioned horizontally under the OCT and
imaged as in Fig J.27.
Figure J.27: B-scan of the horizontal glass slide
The thickness was estimated automatically with a MATLAB algorithm that detects the reﬂection
peaks. Ten measurements gave the same result of 1.239 mm. Therefore, the standard deviation
is of 0 mm whereas the accuracy is 0.9%.
