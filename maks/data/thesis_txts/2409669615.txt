Abstract
The main objective in this thesis has been to gain more knowledge about the emission of tapping
gas in silicon production. Industrial measurement campaigns have been conducted to map and
quantify the levels of emission. These measurements have been applied in establishing important
Key Performance Indicators and a material and energy balance of the tapping gas emission.

Introduction
Silicon is the second most abundant element in the earth’s crust after oxygen and elemental silicon
is usually produced through a carbothermic reduction of quartz in a Submerged Arc Furnace
(SAF). Silicon is almost always found in nature combined with oxygen as silicon dioxide and
silicates. Silicon dioxide and silicates have been in use since the stone age in the form of ﬂint,
ceramics and glasses. In 1823 J¨ons Jakob Berzelius puriﬁed and characterized silicon. Industrial
production was commenced near the en of the 19th century. In the 1950s very pure silicon was
produced paving the way for the advancement in electronics as silicon is the most important
element used in semiconductors.
Silicon and silicon alloys is used in many applications today including alloying of steel and cast
iron, aluminum alloys, silicones/silanes, semiconductors, solar cells, and cosmetics. Silicon is a
material for the future seeing that the world relies more and more on technology, and is in a shift
towards more renewable energy. Some examples of important industries and products heavily
reliant on silicon is shown in Figure 1. In order to get the elemental silicon applicable SiO2 needs
to be reduced to Si. The simpliﬁed version of the reaction happening during the reduction is:
SiO2 + 2C →Si + 2CO
(1)
While in reality a host of intermediate reactions are taking place including physicochemical
transformation of charge material, formation of intermediate compounds and participation of
intermediate gas-phase in the reduction. The result is elemental silicon alloyed with small amounts
of other elements such as iron, calcium and aluminum.[18]
Figure 1: Examples of uses for silicon metal. (A) shows an industrial crystalline silicon
n-PERT solar cell[16], (B) a generic smartphone[21], (C) the aluminum body of a
Tesla Model S[23], (D) Boeing SUGAR Volt concept[1], and (E) computer chip with a
silicon semiconductor[19]

1.1
Motivation
From a sustainability point of view silicon production shows great potential with SiO2 and C
containing reactants being highly available and improvements in eﬃciency of the process are
being implemented in many plants.
The industrial process creates waste and consumes a high amount of energy. Reducing unnecessary
emissions will lead to gains in the economical, environmental and operational aspects of the
industry. The tapping process is assumed to be one of the main sources for the emissions[25]
causing the internal pollution.
In modern silicon production the release of process gases from the taphole is a daily occurrence
at plants all over the world. Workers at all these plants are exposed and may over time suﬀer
respiratory symptoms, decline in lung function such as Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease
(COPD)[11]. To mitigate the health problems the use of dust masks are mandatory in Norway.
This will be further outlined in this chapter.
In the quest to reduce the airborne emissions in the production of silicon there are several
diﬃcult aspects. After developing technical solutions for collection of the emissions, one of the
biggest diﬃculties is the lack of understanding of emission types and sources. See Figure 3 for an
illustration of the conditions the operators work in. This thesis outlines the investigation into the
mechanisms controlling one of the sources, the emissions from the taphole. The objective was to
establish a good foundation of knowledge to enable further work in reducing the emissions from
the taphole. The investigation was carried out by making an material and energy balance of the
tapping area. In addition important Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) were to be established.

1.2
Silicon production
In silicon production the Submerged Arc Furnace (SAF) is where the quartz is reduced to silicon
with reductants like coal, charcoal, wood chips and coke. In Figure 4 a schematic of a typical
plant is illustrated. The raw materials are crushed, weighed and mixed before entering the furnace
where the electrodes also serves as carbon source as it gets consumed. The furnace is often
described as being divided into 2 zones; the upper cold zone and the lower hot zone. In the
upper cold zone SiO gas escaped from the lower zone is recaptured by reacting with the carbon
materials in the following reaction[18]:
SiO + 2C →SiC + CO
(2)
as well as condensing according to either of the following reactions depending on temperature:
2SiO →SiO2 + Si
(3)
or:
3SiO + CO →2SiO2 + SiC
(4)
These silicon-recovering reactions heat the upper layer of the charge to a very high temperature
creating a sticky highly viscous charge that ﬂows slowly to the lower hot zone. The carbon
balance of the raw materials supplied will inﬂuence the Si-yield as it determines how much of the
SiO gas will condensate in the upper charge and how much will leave the furnace from the oven
top to the furnace oﬀ-gas. When the oﬀ-gas leaves the furnace top it will react with the oxygen
3
==PAGE:13==
to give silica fume, CO2 and heat. It has a heat content of the same order as the supplied electric
energy to the SAF[18]:
(1 + x)SiO2 + (2 + x)C + (1 + x)O2 = Si + xSiO2(f ume) + (2 + x)CO2 + heat
(5)
At plants with energy recovery systems the oﬀ-gas goes to the boiler where energy is recovered
through a heat-exchanger and further to the ﬁlter where the microsilica is extracted and the
cleaned gas can exit through the chimney. The microsilica consists mainly of amorphous SiO2
with a particle size of around 0, 1µm and used to be a waste product but today is sold as a
separate product.
Figure 4: Schematic of a typical silicon plant [18].
The typical SAF has a furnace pot with a diameter of around 10m and a height of around 3.5m[6].
The 3 electrodes supply a 3-phase current and creates an arc in the crater where the temperature
is about 2000°C. At these high temperatures the SiO2 is reduced to molten liquid Si that can
be tapped from the taphole. The Si metal is then reﬁned in the ladle by gas purging and slag
treatment to get the speciﬁed composition, and to remove slags and carbides. After reﬁning the
liquid is poured into moulds to solidify and then crushed to speciﬁed sized pieces.

1.3
The crater pressure
The ”high” pressure crater zone is where the gases SiO2 and CO are formed, Figure 5 illustrates
the inside of the furnace. A more accurate description of Equation 1 depicts this reaction:
(1 + x)SiO2 + (2 + x)C = Si + xSiO + (2 + x)CO
(6)
This reaction where the solid materials are converted into a gas phase creates the pressure in the
crater zone. For illustrative purposes imagine 1 liter of solid charge material turns into 3800 liters
of gas. In a medium sized furnace of 24MW the added amount of solid material to the charge is
approximately 1 liter per second [26]. The pressure in the crater zone tend to be in the order
of 1-15 kPA or 0,01-0,12 atm higher than the atmospheric pressure. As derived from industrial
measurements[5]. The pressure behaves dynamically and varies based on 3 main parameters;
furnace electric load, charge permeability and the stoking process.
Figure 5: Schematic of the furnace showing the internal pressure zone called the crater
and the ﬂow of materials[25].
It has been shown that there is a correlation between the furnace electric load and the pressure
in the crater as presented in Figure 6[26]. This is caused by the heat generated by the electrodes
serves as the main driving force for the gas-producing reaction.
5
==PAGE:15==
Figure 6: Measured crater pressure and furnace electric load on Elkem Thamshavn
furnace 2 [26].
The eﬀect of the charge permeability can be summed up with how tight of a seal can the charge
provide for the crater zone. The more porous the crater walls are the higher gas ﬂow towards the
lower pressure zone in the upper part of the charge. With less of a pressure diﬀerence between
the upper- and lower part of the charge less of a driving force is present causing pressure build
up in the crater zone.
The stoking process is when an operator stokes or pushes the charge on the outskirts of the furnace
top towards the electrodes as the consumption of the charge is higher there. The charge materials
are fed periodically by an automated system while the stoking takes place at a lower frequency
based on the operational strategy. The eﬀect of the stoking process is caused by avalanches in
the charge causing a collapse of the crater wall. This leads to a leak of the process gases from the
crater zone to the upper part of the charge.
The eﬀect of the crater pressure
The high pressure zone in the crater is seen in Figure 7 as the part in red with a high pressure
where the process gases are present. The abrupt pressure drop in the crater wall can clearly be
seen here as well.

Figure 8 shows the eﬀect the crater pressure has on the molten metal. The ﬂow pattern is heavily

furnace walls. This indicates that the metal height is increased along the walls of the furnace

gas from escaping from the taphole[3].
If the taphole is closed and no metal is removed from the furnace there will be an even distribution
with a buildup along the wall of the molten metal in the furnace as seen in Figure 8, but that is
normally not the case. All furnaces investigated in this MSc thesis were running a continuous
tapping strategy meaning the taphole is open and metal is ﬂowing at all times. In Figure 9 the
eﬀect of tapping the metal on the metal distribution is evident. The decrease in metal height is
higher by the taphole. Over time the accumulated metal towards the walls not near the taphole
will move towards the taphole in the low pressure zone close to the wall. When the metal height
does not cover the taphole anymore as after 350s in Figure 9 the ”metal lock” will no longer
prevent the gas from escaping causing a rapid increase in tapping gas. This also causes the
pressure to drop in the crater zone and a decrease in the tapping ﬂow rate of the metal[6].

Tapping
The tapping process is seen as one of the most critical processes in the whole process chain of
silicon production. This operation is therefore considered crucial to the overall performance. In
the tapping process the molten silicon exits the furnace through the taphole with a temperature
of 1600 −1700° C. This is done either continuously or discontinuously. Tapping discontinuously
the taphole is plugged for a certain amount of time to let the metal accumulate in the furnace
before opening the taphole and draining the furnace. This method was not used on any of the
furnaces involved in this project however. The condition of the taphole heavily inﬂuences the
8
==PAGE:18==
rate of drainage of the metal from the furnace therefore determining the accumulation of metal
in the furnace. Allowing an excess of silicon to accumulate in the furnace will cause it to react
with carbon or SiO2 causing a lower Si-yield.
One SAF usually has several tapholes spaced out evenly around the furnace. A normal strategy
in furnace operation is tapping from a taphole through a certain area around the furnace where
there is access to the tools etc. As mentioned previously in section 1.3 and illustrated in Figure
7 the pressure is higher closer to the electrode. Figure 10 illustrates this from above where the
tapping from a taphole starts close to one electrode and ﬁnishes close to another.
Figure 10: The position of the taphole compared to an electrode inﬂuences the pressure
observed by the taphole [6]. Inside the two red lines indicate the area where tapping
occurs.
The condition of the taphole is maintained by the operator using several diﬀerent kind of tools,
see Table 1 for an overview of the diﬀerent tools used. Some tools will be consumed and go into
the ladle with the metal and hence will inﬂuence the quality of the product. The utilization of
tools also aﬀects the tapping canal possibly leading to a badly conﬁgured canal. The geometry
and precision of the canal is crucial for a good performing furnace. The geometry and precision of
the canal is also very inﬂuential on the emission of tapping gas [25][26]. The eﬀect of taphole wear
combined with over-use of tapping tools can lead to a tapping channel with a number of ways
for the gas to escape before its lifetime is over. A taphole is used over a period of time before
it is plugged with a carbon paste. It is repaired when it reaches the other side of the furnace
in respect to the currently used taphole[3]. A suﬃcient repair job of the taphole is required for
it to work nominally the next time it is to be used. Monitoring of the condition of the taphole
however is challenging. It is usually monitored by thermocouples in the lining for temperature
measurements[14].

or the outer atmosphere from closed processes where the collection and exhaust systems are not
capable of collecting all of it. Diﬀuse emissions leak through unintended openings instead of
passing through any opening as ducts, vents or chimneys but .[9]

The silicon industry has in general very small emissions. The main emission is to air through
the ﬁlter. There has been very little or no health eﬀects for people who lives in the vicinity of a
modern silicon plant.
The main challenge is the internal environment – and the tapping gas is a major source for
internal pollution in the furnace building[18].
Major Greenhouse Gases
Major Greenhouse Gases (GHG) such as CO2 are inevitably produced in silicon production. CO
gas escaping from the furnace top will oxidise to CO2, methane(CH4). Other volatile hydrocarbons
are generated from the carbon materials including electrodes. In the upper zone of the charge the
Boudouard reaction may contribute to the CO2 total[18]:
2CO →CO2 + C
(7)
The amount of GHG emissions are controlled by three main conditions: Speciﬁed purity of product
will inﬂuence the amount of emissions, increased GHG emission the higher the Si-content. Carbon
balance and mix will aﬀect the GHG emissions depending on the contents of volatile matters in
the carbon materials and the source of these materials[12]. Furnace operations and the charging
strategy will inﬂuence the GHG emissions, speciﬁcally CH4 and NOx [9]. More incremental
charging reduces the emissions compared to batch charging. The use of biological carbon sources
reduces the emissions of fossil GHG.
NOx
Nitrogen oxide or NOx (NO and NO2) have been targeted for a long time in order to reduce the
emission for good reason. They serve a big role in creating ﬁne particles and ozone smog in the
atmosphere as well as acid rain, eutrophication and bronchial suﬀering. In silicon production the
two dominant NOx forming mechanisms are fuel and thermal formation. Fuel NOx is formed by
12
==PAGE:22==
oxidation of nitrogen present in the solid fuel while thermal NOx is formed through oxidation
of nitrogen from the air at temperatures exceeding 1400°C. The production of NOX is inversely
proportional to the Si-yield and there is a general correlation between SiO and NOx emissions [9].
NOx is also formed in the tapping process through the use of oxygen lances (a tool used to open
up the tapping canal and extract slag from said canal) [8].
SOx,H2S and VOC
Other gases in present in the oﬀgas from the furnace include but is not limited to sulfur oxides
(SOX ), hydrogen sulﬁdes (H2S) and various volatile organic compounds (VOC) [18]. The actual
emission of SO2 gas in silicon production is not studied thoroughly enough with measurements,
most emissions are calculated based on a material balance[9]. The reported annual SO2 emissions
are on the same level as reported NOx emissions[15]. Technology for cleaning the oﬀgas of SO2
is available but prohibited by high costs.
Dioxins
The release of dioxins or dioxin-like substances in silicon production is well studied. Dioxins and
dioxin-like substances have long been recognized as having toxic eﬀects including immunotoxicity,
developmental and neuro-developmental eﬀects, and changes in thyroid and steroid hormones
and reproductive function. Dioxins and dioxin-like substances are persistent organic pollutants
(POPs) covered by the Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants. They can travel
long distances carried suspended in the air and can accumulate in food-chains[28].
In modern semi-closed SAFs it is possible to reduce the emission of dioxins and other organic
compounds as achieving a higher temperature in the oﬀ-gas destroys the compounds. This is in
the temperature range where PAH is also destroyed but at a point where thermal NOx can be
formed [2]. Very small emissions of dioxins have been measured in modern Si-production. In fact
the emission level is below the detection limit and is measured to 2% of the emission-limit for
waste incineration[24].
PAH
Poly-cyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAH) are organic structures with more than two aromatic
rings. They are usually formed by incomplete combustion of organic matter. The compounds
with few aromatic rings are in gas form at room temperature while the more large variants are
liquid or solid. PAH compounds are linked to several types of cancer, other health eﬀects and
detrimental to the environment. PAHs are part of a group called Persistent Organic Pollutants
that are especially resistant to degradation in the environment.
They do however get destroyed at high temperatures, in the same manner as Dioxins. The source
of PAHs in silicon production is the carbon-based reductants, electrode and paste. In the same
manner as dioxins the emission of PAHs have been reduced to a negligible level. Through targeted
research the Norwegian Ferroalloy Producers Research Association (FFF) have helped improve
the envoronmental aspect of the industry[24].
13
==PAGE:23==
Heavy metals
Heavy metals are introduced as trace elements in all the raw materials. They end up in the metal,
slag gas or fume depending mainly on their boiling point and the process temperatures. Some
elements such as Cd, Fe, Mn, Na, Pb and Zn can go into the gas phase and condense when the
temperature drops sticking to the dust. There is however large uncertainties in the amount of
emissions as most reported numbers are based of material balances and vary between plants. This
is mostly due to the low concentration in the materials going in and the diﬃculty in measuring
them[13].
Particular matter (PM)
PM or dust emitted from the production is detrimental to the working environment inside
the plant as well as the local and the more global environment. The inhalation of the dust is
especially unhealthy, it has been linked to cancer, pneumonia, chronic obstructive pulmonary
disease (COPD) and other cardiovascular and respiratory diseases [11]. The tapping process
is seen as the process contributing the most to the emission of PMs to the air[9]. One of the
main contributors to this emission is the combustion of SiO(д) outside of the taphole generating
amorphous SiO2 particles that pollute the plant and the surrounding environment. These particles
are respirable as they are generally smaller than 2, 5µm and a small fraction is smaller than
100nm[9].

Experimental work
All experimental work in this project was conducted in an industrial environment under normal
running conditions. Instruments for measurement of the energy in the tapping gas canal were
permanently installed during the last half of 2016 at some of the furnaces at Elkem Thamshavn,
Elkem Bremanger and Elkem Salten. The measuring campaigns were carried out as a team eﬀort.
2.1
Power measurement of the tapping gas
The key to this project was the implementation of the measurement of the power in the tapping
gas exhaust canal. The measurement was the crucial component in evaluating the variation and
the main key performance indicator (KPI) for this project. The measurements were continuously
logged 24/7 in resolutions ranging from per minute to per day. The measurements were ﬁrst
implemented at the Elkem Thamshavn plant for the two furnaces there. Subsequently measure-
ments were established on furnace number 5 at the Elkem Bremanger plant and on furnace 2 and
3 at the Elkem Salten plant.
The power measurement is in reality a calculation that is logged and is based on the measurements
of the temperature and the ﬂow in the canal. The ﬂow measurement is itself based on the
diﬀerential pressure in the canal. Furthermore a reference temperature for the air combusting
with the tapping gas is used. This measurement is located close to tapping area but shielded
from any inﬂuence from the furnace. See Figure 13 for an overview of the measurements. The
speciﬁc heat capacity Cp of the gas is assumed to be 29[

During the campaign something unexpected happened that was luckily documented. At around

Figure 29 and 30. The amount of energy suddenly released from the taphole was too much for
the exhaust system. Where the volume expansion of the gas was so big the ﬂow supplied by the
fan in the canal was too small making the system unable to collect all the gas in the canal. This
could cause a ”back ﬂow” and a surge of dust into the building. Another possible explanation
for the incident was that the fan in the exhaust system had an emergency cutoﬀat a certain
temperature and this limit was exceeded. The investigation was inconclusive.

Ideal gas equation in action
In response to the situation encountered at Elkem Thamshavn during the measuring campaign,
the behaviour of the exhaust system at Elkem Bremanger performed was interesting. The exhaust
system at Elkem Bremanger has a much higher capacity and performed well during the campaign.
When the temperature in the gas rises the volume expands linearly resulting in less ﬂow of
molecules. The Figure 34 show the correlation between the temperature and ﬂow in the canal.
This can be readily illustrated by the ideal gas equation given as follows:
PV = nRT
(9)
P is the pressure, V the volume, n the amount of moles, R the universal gas constant and T the
absolute temperature of the gas.

The eﬀects of tapping tools
The use of tapping tools and its inﬂuence on the power measured in the tapping gas canal is
quite evident as seen in the examples of the uses in Figures 35 and 36. The Winchester cannon is
used for opening the taphole and increasing the diameter of the hole. This again increases the
ﬂow of gas but might decrease the velocity due to the area increase. On the other hand using
the oxygen lance not only opens up the taphole but also is a good tool for removing slag and
carbides that have built up. The two tools are great for opening and clearing the taphole and
ensuring a suﬃcient taprate of the silicon metal. However they can also damage the tapping
channel and the lining causing a bad geometry especially of the inner taphole. In Figures 35 and
36 it is evident that there are direct consequences of the tools giving an immediate reaction but
also might give a more prolonged eﬀect giving a more gradual rise in power.

Material and Energy balance
The data collected from the measuring campaigns at Elkem Thamshavn and Elkem Bremanger
were used to construct a material and energy balance of the tapping gas. This was useful in
mapping the energy emission from the taphole. A schematic of the model can be seen in Figure
37. Experiences from both the campaigns helped construct a balance to ﬁt each of the furnaces.
The resulting material and energy balance is presented in Tables 11 and 12, Figures 39 and 40
but is more visually represented in the Sankey diagrams in Figures 41 to 44.
The measurements from Elkem Bremanger proved crucial for establishing the energy contribution
from the ladle and the surroundings. The energy contribution from the surroundings include heat
stored in the canal and heat from the furnace which was quite a lot higher at Elkem Bremanger
than at Elkem Thamshavn. It is assumed to be constant in this model.

4
Conclusion
The tapping gas phenomenon in silicon production has been investigated through data collected
from industrial measurements. Several Silicon producing furnaces have been compared. A big
variation in the power measurements has been observed, and every furnace behaves in its own
way.
The tapping gas measurements in the tapping gas exhaust system have proven to be a useful KPI
in order to understand and reduce the tapping gas. In addition the dust measurement in the
building has proven to be a useful KPI. Being able to quantify the amount of tapping gas and
the eﬀect it has on the internal environment has proven to be useful.
Some tapping tools and the use or over use of them, seem to increase the amount of tapping gas.
In particular the oxygen lance and Winchester cannon seem to have signiﬁcant and direct impact
on the condition of the tapping canal. As reported in earlier reports[25][26] the most direct factor
in controlling the amount of tapping gas seem to be the geometry and precision of the tapping
channel.
An important aspect of this project has been to form a foundation for future work in controlling
the tapping gas phenomenon. The author hopes this work will inspire others in continued work
in the area.

Further Work
Important parameters for future studies are:
Operational strategies
• Discontinuous tapping
• Semi-discontinuous tapping
• Rotational strategy
New measurements
In order to obtain a better understanding better data is necessary. Providing more quantiﬁable
and precise measurements of aspects that are not covered at this point in time will improve the
analysis. Parameters that could be automatically logged:
• Amount of plugging-mass
• Time of plugging
• Opening of new taphole
• Drilling (size, height, angle, length precision etc.)
• Oxygen lance (ﬂowmeter as at Elkem Bremanger)
• Stoking
Further analysis
Further development of the theoretical models of the physics involved could prove useful.
For continued statistical work with the data not only is better data necessary but also a deeper
understanding of the data and assumptions. This could lead to better cleaning of the data and
the possibility of more decisive results.
Machine learning methods seem like a promising tool for analyzing such large data sets with
many interconnected variables. Undiscovered aspects of the tapping gas phenomenon may be
uncovered through the use of such tools.
