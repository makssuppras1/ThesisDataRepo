Abstract 
The global footwear industry generates substantial amounts of waste, though most of this waste 
is either incinerated or landfilled. As a part of Eco-design for Sustainable Products Regulation 
(ESPR) regulation, the European Union is implementing Digital Product Passports (DPPs) with 
the goal of relaying information to guide waste handling. This project aims to investigate which 
information is important to include in DPPs, by developing a novel way of characterizing post-
consumer footwear using existing footwear classification systems, to recommend 
improvements on these.  
A total of 554 kg of Danish post-consumer footwear was surveyed, characterized and analyzed 
in terms of typology, material composition and complexity. Information on damage patterns and 
footwear condition was also collected. 
This information was used to model the recycling, repair and reuse potentials for Danish post-
consumer footwear through a Mass-Flow Analysis (MFA). 
The findings indicate that the current classification systems for footwear are inadequate to 
guide waste management, as crucial information on materials, recycling disrupters, chemicals 
and anatomy of the footwear. Furthermore, it was found that degradation of plastics was a 
prevalent damage type, raising ground for discussion of the use of biodegradable materials in 
footwear. An analysis of current recycling technologies found that metals and footwear 
complexity are the biggest barriers to recycling, while the results from the survey showed that 
over 33% of post-consumer footwear, contained at least one metal part. 
Based on these findings, a list of recommendations for implementing DPPs were made, 
emphasizing the need for enhanced material transparency, universal terminology and 
classification systems, and design considerations to facilitate circularity in the footwear 
industry.

1 Introduction 
Footwear is a consumer good, that everyone needs, regardless of geography and socio-
economic status(Fabric Innovation Hub, 2023). In 2022 over 21 billion pairs of footwear were 
sold worldwide, with over 44 million pairs being imported to Denmark in 2023. An average of 3-4 
pairs per person globally, and over 8 pairs per person in Denmark (Rossi et al., 2024; World 
footwear, 2023). As all footwear will eventually wear out, the resulting waste will be an inevitable 
problem on a global scale, but it presents particular challenges in countries like Denmark, with 
an annual consumption 2x the global average. 
Modern footwear is comprised of a wide range of materials, of which many are synthetic and 
durable. These components do not break down rapidly in the environment. For example, Leather 
shoes may take up to 25-30 years to decompose, while the estimates for rubber puts the 
degradation time at 50-80 years. Some foams and plastics may persist for up to 1000 
years(DiNapoli, 2024). As most footwear is disposed of in landfills waste managers face 
challenges in terms of landfill space and pollution (Fabric Innovation Hub, 2023; J. T. Staikos & 
Rahimifard, 2007; Van Rensburg et al., 2020). Moreover, in landfills, toxic chemicals, such as 
heavy metals, dyes and other additives, are released as the footwear decomposes, which can 
contaminate soil and groundwater, and emit greenhouse gasses from decomposition of organic 
materials (Dahlberg, 2010; DiNapoli, 2024; Van Rensburg et al., 2020). In Denmark, most 
footwear is incinerated, which also has environmental impacts as the same toxic substances 
are released into the atmosphere along with the carbon emissions from incineration (DiNapoli, 
2024; Van Rensburg et al., 2020).  
In addition to end-of-life (EoL) challenges, the production of footwear also has a significant 
environmental impact. For example, a typical pair of running shoes is estimated to have a 
carbon footprint of 14 – 41 kg CO2-eq. It is estimated that 97% of these emissions stem from the 
production and manufacturing stages. By recycling footwear materials, extending the lifetime of 
footwear, and decreasing the demand for new products through reuse and repair activities the 
industry may decrease the need for continuous extraction of virgin materials for footwear 
manufacturing, potentially reducing the environmental burdens of the footwear industry.

However, current recycling activities are limited to corporate take-back systems, with limited 
circularity, and pilot-scale activities (Hansen, 2024). While reuse through resale is a widely used 
waste management strategy, there are several social and environmental impacts related that 
come as tradeoffs (Van Rensburg et al., 2020). For example, sales often happen in developing 
countries with poor waste management infrastructure and domestic industries that cannot 
compete with cheap reused products (Curtis & Hansson, 2019; NIKE, 2024; J. T. Staikos & 
Rahimifard, 2007; Van Rensburg et al., 2020). Therefore, there is a need to explore the barriers to 
recycling footwear, and potentials for other waste management strategies.  
In the European Union Digital Product Passports (DPPs) are being implemented as part of its 
Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR) legislation. The goal of these DPPs is to , 
guide waste management and circularity by relaying relevant information such as material 
composition, producer information, and other information vital for the circular economy (Office 
of the European Union, 2024). However, what information and how it will be communicated is 
still largely unclear. Existing labelling and categorization frameworks (COUNCIL REGULATION 
(EEC) No 2658/87, 1987; Directive 94/11, 1994) only include basic information about primary 
materials and product type.  
An expanded version of this classification scheme was developed by (Rossi et al., 2024), 
dividing footwear into five product categories based on the information available in the existing 
labelling and categorization frameworks. However, this classification was largely theoretical and 
has not been applied to the sorting and characterization of footwear in end-of-life management. 
Therefore, there is a need to explore if the existing typology can be reliably applied to these DPPs 
and establish what other information is needed to inform decision-making for circular footwear. 
This thesis is an attempt at using the current characterization of footwear as a basis for 
analyzing post-consumer footwear, to investigate how current frameworks can be used to guide 
waste management, and provide recommendations for information to include in DPPs for 
footwear waste. 
1.1 Project scope and research questions 
The overall goal of this thesis is to assess if the existing waste labelling directive can serve as a 
viable basis for characterizing post-consumer footwear and carry out an actual survey on 
Danish post-consumer shoes. To facilitate this, the following sub objectives were set: 
1. Develop a waste characterization survey based on existing typology 
2. Evaluate if the current footwear typology is adequate as a basis for characterizing 
footwear waste 
3. Characterize post-consumer footwear in a Danish context 
4. Analyze Danish post-consumer footwear in terms of typology, material composition and 
complexity. 
5. Assess damage patterns and condition of footwear, to investigate potentials for reuse, 
repair and identify design weak spots 
6. Model material composition and material recovery potentials through a mass-flow 
analysis.  
To guide the research, three primary research questions were formulated:

2 Background and Literature review 
 
The following section will explain the review methodology, and body of research, followed by a

2.1 Methodology 
To cover the body of literature on post-consumer footwear waste management and related 
topics, a systematic approach was used, which was supplemented with “snowballing”. 
Using the research software Publish or Perish (Harzing, 2007) five search strings were applied to 
Google scholar and Web of Science. For each search string, the top 10 results were reviewed, 
and relevant literature was included. All search strings and reviewed papers can be found in 
appendix (A). This was done to ensure a wide range of literature, and to cover all existing 
definitions of footwear. Further literature was found through references, recommendations and 
searches on explicit topics. 
The first round of literature search was conducted before conducting the survey, with another 
round of literature search conducted during the writing process.

2.3.1.1 Global and national production and waste trends 
The production and disposal of consumer goods drive resource extraction, energy use, and 
waste generation. The textile- and apparel industry is widely cited as one of the most polluting 
industries worldwide, and therefore, increasing circularity is an important step in mitigating the 
impacts of the industry on the environment (European Parliament, 2020). For policy initiatives 
such as extended producer responsibility (EPR) and DPP to be effective, it is important to 
understand global and national consumption and waste trends. Therefore, there is a need to 
quantify the scale of the issue both globally and domestically. 
The footwear industry has been growing exponentially since the 1950s, with annual production 
doubling every year, rising from 2.5 billion pairs/year to over 20 billion pairs/year by 2005 
(Rahimifard et al., 2007). Despite a drop in demand due to the COVID-19 pandemic (World 
footwear, 2022, 2023; World Footwear, 2024), the industry sold over 21.9 billion pairs in 2022 
(Andersen, 2023) and is projected to grow by 9% in 2024 (World Footwear, 2024). The European 
and North American markets have seen declining growth, offset by emerging Asian economies 
(Andersen, 2023; World footwear, 2022, 2023; World Footwear, 2024). The top five producers of 
footwear globally include China, the United States, India, Indonesia, and Japan. These 
companies account for 50.9% of global consumption (Andersen, 2023). China exports 63.8% of 
global footwear (World Footwear, 2024). Annually, 91 million pairs of unsold footwear 
isincinerated (Andrade et al., 2023). 
The footwear market is diverse with regards to manufacturers, with the largest company Nike, 
only taking up 7% of the global market, based on revenue, with other notable companies Adidas 
(3%), Jordan (3%) and Skethcers (2%), being the only brands making up more than 1.5% of the 
global footwear market(statista, 2024). 
In Denmark, 44 million pairs of shoes are imported annually, equating to 8 pairs per 
person(Rossi et al., 2024). NewRetex data shows 2.94% of textile waste is footwear(Enevoldsen, 
2024). Using Danish EPA's statistics, this means at least 176.4 tons of footwear waste annually, 
likely higher due to residual waste disposal (Hornung Thorndahl, 2024). If the share of unsold 
footwear from (Andrade et al., 2023) is applicable to Danish consumption, 19,800 pairs of 
unsold footwear is incinerated annually. Seeing as most Danish footwear is imported, most 
incineration likely happens outside Denmark. These trends show a diverse, import-driven 
market with varying waste management practices.  
2.3.1.2 Footwear classification and typology 
As Denmark’s footwear market is dominated by imports it relies on common nomenclature 
systems. 
Footwear classification is inconsistent across regulatory and trade systems. The EU uses the 
Footwear Labelling Directive 94/11/EC (Directive 94/11, 1994), which uses a classification 
system based on a four-category material identification (leather, coated Leather, textiles and 
other), on three major footwear parts (upper, lining/sock, outer sole). The Directive requires that 
only one material is labeled if it constitutes more than 80% of the given part. For parts with less 
than 80% comprised of a single material, the two most prominent materials should be labeled 
(Directive 94/11, 1994). The labeling directive lacks information on manufacturing techniques 
and specific parts, which is important in waste management.

Classification systems are important for effective waste management, as they enable 
standardized data collection and efficient sorting. Without a clear classification system, 
assessing environmental impacts, and optimal waste routing becomes difficult. Multiple 
classification systems already exist, though they have limited granularity. Emerging policies, 
such as EPR and DPP, require a detailed classification system. It is key that the classification 
system provides relevant waste management information, allowing for actionable insights. 
Specific footwear types and parts 
In addition to footwear categorization systems, a long list of specific types of footwear are 
covered in the literature. The European Commission (EC) uses a non-exhaustive list for footwear 
typology (EC, 2024). (Rossi et al., 2024) expands on this list, using different online resources. 
(Hansen, 2024) presents an overview of different footwear types that are presented in academic 
research.  
The typology used for specific parts of footwear varies, though generally, footwear is divided into 
at least two major parts: the “upper” and the “sole”. Often times, the lining/sock is also included 
as a separate part from the upper. Each major part has several “minor” parts, which can be 
identified individually and can overlap depending on the used typology. (Rossi et al., 2024), 
provides a non-exhaustive list, based on (EC, 2024; Gottfridsson & Zhang, 2015; Kowalska et al., 
2016; Singha, 2012). Many other typologies for footwear parts exist, and they vary with culture 
and assembly technique (ASICS, 2013; Double Select, 2024; NIKE, 2022).

Current footwear classification systems are useful for trade purposes but lack detailed 
information on materials, parts, and assembly techniques. Therefore, there is a need to 
understand the different materials and manufacturing techniques used in footwear 
manufacturing, and how they relate to current footwear categorization. 
2.3.1.3 Footwear manufacturing and material composition 
Materiality and manufacturing matter for waste management, as the assembly technique and 
materials determine both the durability and environmental impact of the footwear. Footwear is 
a particularly heterogeneous product category, with a piece of footwear comprising up to 40 
different materials on up to 65 parts and up to 360 processing steps for assembly (Abu et al., 
2017; Cheah et al., 2013a). Materiality and manufacturing also determine the reparability of 
footwear, as well as which recycling technologies can be used. Therefore, there is a need to 
understand the techniques and materials used in footwear production to identify barriers for 
recycling and opportunities for circularity and sustainability.  
Assembly techniques  
Most footwear is manufactured with one of four techniques: Goodyear welt, Direct Injection 
Moulding (DIP), vulcanization and cementing(Dryfit max, 2023). Each of these processes is 
described in Table 2.2. This variety of footwear manufacturing effects the waste treatment of 
footwear as assembly techniques determine how easily footwear can be disassembled, which 
in turn influences reparability, and recycling potentials(Thompson, 2021). Footwear assembled 
through bonding techniques (cementing, vulcanization and moulding) prohibits materials from 
being separated from each other during disassembly, often introduces additives that are 
potentially harmful, and often prohibits reassembly/repair, as complex processes are used in 
the assembly(Lara et al., 2015.). Welting allows for easy repair and disassembly (Karg, 1965).

Footwear materials 
Historically, leather and textiles were the most used materials in footwear. Since the industrial 
revolution, a wide range of materials has been introduced to footwear manufacturing. These 
novel materials have helped improve durability, and decrease price, though often with a 
reduction in reparability and separability of materials (Kelso, 2024; Morgan, 2020). 
The properties needed of materials used in footwear depend on the function of the part in 
question. Upper materials need to be somewhat durable, as they will be exposed to the 
elements. Some upper materials need to be water-resistant. Some materials need to be very

Leather, which is the most used material in footwear, is typically made from bovine sources with 
chromium tanning, which has a particularly high environmental impact (Pringle et al., 2016). 
Plant based alternatives exist, as well as alternatives to chromium tanning, which helps reduce 
the environmental impact of the leather, though with differing reductions in durability and price 
(China et al., 2020; World Footwear, 2022). Several biobased materials are in use in the 
footwear industry. This includes bio-based alternatives to both plastics and leathers. 
Biodegradable materials are also used more and more frequently. When discussing 
biomaterials, it is important to keep in mind that biobased materials are not always 
biodegradable, and biodegradable materials are not always biobased (World Footwear, 2022). 
Additives 
Polymers (plastics and rubbers) usually contain a long list of additives, which are usually not 
labelled. These include but are not limited to, softeners, colorings, and vulcanizers(Dahlberg, 
2010; World Footwear, 2022). These materials are usually found in very small concentrations 
but are often xenobiotic and toxic in large concentrations. (Dahlberg, 2010) analyzed organic 
compounds and heavy metals in three different shoes. A few REACH chemicals were found 
through methanol extraction, though none were above limit values. The tests found 
concentrations of zinc oxide high enough to impact larval growth; through a leach test, the 
zincoxide was theorized to originate from vulcanization(Dahlberg, 2010). While the toxic 
substances in themselves might not prove environmentally damaging, they raise concerns 
related to accumulation through recycling or landfilling(Van Rensburg et al., 2020).  
2.3.2 Waste management of post consumer footwear: practices, issues 
and prospects 
The following section covers waste management practices of post-consumer footwear, along 
with issues in current practices and prospects for more sustainable waste management. 
2.3.2.1 Current practices in waste management of post-consumer footwear 
The waste management approach in use determines the environmental impact of pos-
consumer footwear. Effective disposal directly impacts available landfill space, resource 
depletion and pollution. Footwear waste is a unique challenge due to its complexity and the 
lack of standardized classification systems. Currently most footwear is landfilled, with only 
limited recycling(Van Rensburg et al., 2020). Existing footwear infrastructure is not designed to 
handle complex goods like footwear. Therefore, understanding current practices in waste 
management of post-consumer footwear is important in order to identify areas of improvement, 
and understand what the current barriers to circularity is, along with potentials for 
improvement. 
The following section will review current practices and issues in the management of post-
consumer footwear waste.

Waste management options and framework 
In Europe, the waste hierarchy is a framework used to determine the most preferable option in 
waste management (EC, 2008). In this framework, prevention is preferred above reuse, 
recycling, energy recover and disposal, in order of descending preferability. Expanding upon 
this, (J. T. Staikos & Rahimifard, 2007) Created a framework for post-consumer waste 
management in the footwear industry, dividing waste management approaches into reactive 
and proactive strategies. 
Proactive approaches include waste minimization, that is design and material improvements, 
along with consumer repair. Reactive approaches refer to end-of-life management, which can 
be either reuse, recycling, energy recovery or disposal. In (Rossi et al., 2024), repair is 
considered a possible reactive waste management strategy if applied at the post-consumer 
level. Generally, proactive approaches aim to minimize waste quantities at the design and 
manufacturing stage, whereas reactive approaches aim to mitigate the impact of waste 
management on the use or end-of-life phase (T. Staikos & Rahimifard, 2007). 
The framework divides recycling into destructive, and non-destructive technologies. Destructive 
technologies involves grinding shoes, and separating materials by density. Non-destructive 
technologies are technologies where the footwear is disassembled, and materials are sorted 
manually. Generally destructive processes are more economically efficient and generates a 
larger volume of reprocessed materials, though at a lower purity. Destructive technologies are 
also more prone to recycling disrupters, such as metals and hard plastics.(Lee & Rahimifard, 
2012; Rahimifard et al., 2007; Rahimifard & Lee, 2010; J. T. Staikos & Rahimifard, 2007; T. Staikos 
& Rahimifard, 2007). 
Material and design improvement includes activities such as replacing materials with either 
recycled materials, biobased materials or biodegradable materials, in order to reduce the 
environmental impact from the production phase or speed up decomposition in landfills(Van 
Rensburg et al., 2020).

Current practices 
Current waste management infrastructure is not explicitly designed to handle footwear waste. 
Globally, most footwear is disposed of in landfills, with estimates for the UK of 85% as of 2003. 
The rest of the footwear is reused, which essentially means that they will reach a landfill at a 
later point (Rahimifard et al., 2007).  
In Denmark residual waste is incinerated, which implies that EoL footwear will be treated 
through incineration with energy recovery. A significant share of footwear is collected through 
drop-off containers, by textile recycling companies, that sort out reusable footwear, and sell it 
either domestically, inside the EU, or on non-EU markets(Rahimifard et al., 2007; Rossi et al., 
2024; Van Rensburg et al., 2020). Less than 5% are recycled on a global scale (Rahimifard & Lee, 
2010). 
The only large-scale recycling practice is the NIKE GRIND program, which uses a destructive 
process for recycling sports footwear. Rubbers and foams are reprocessed and used for 
creating sports surfacing, work gloves, and other open-loop applications(NIKE, 2024; NIKE 
GRIND, 2024a, 2024b). 
Issues with current practices 
Using landfills to dispose of post-consumer footwear, carries considerable environmental 
issues, both from predictable sources such as chromium in leather, but also due to the high 
diversity of chemicals and materials in footwear, and unknown metabolites that can form during 
decomposition (Van Rensburg et al., 2020). 
Reuse of post-consumer footwear, through donations to developing economies, has been 
criticized for shifting the burden of waste management to countries with less advanced waste 
management infrastructure, thus essentially increasing environmental impacts. Another 
critique has been pointed at donations causing social dumping, and outcompetition of 
domestic footwear industries in developing economies(Van Rensburg et al., 2020). 
Seeing as reuse only prolongs the lifetime of footwear it does not remove the need for end-of-
life treatment. Current waste management practices for footwear are inadequate in retaining 
materials, and therefore there is a big potential for increasing the circularity through improving 
waste management. Exploring emerging recycling technologies is important to understand the 
potentials for circularity, and the changes needed to be made in order to facilitate a switch to a 
closed resource loop.  
2.3.2.2 Footwear recycling technologies 
Recycling is important is crucial in waste management, as it is the best option for end-of-life 
management, and for footwear in particular, there is a great need to reduce the amount of 
footwear going to landfills and incineration. Recycling of footwear is complex due to the 
inhomogeneity of products and diversity in materials. Recycling in the footwear industry is not a 
mature technology, and the only actor that has been on the market for a considerable amount of 
time is NIKE. Other technologies exists, but it is unclear how and if these would work at scale. 
Most current technologies only focus on specific parts of the footwear, and focuses on 
downcycling rather than closed-loop recycling. Therefore there is a need for evaluating existing 
technologies, in order to identify parameters important for recycling, and the barriers that exist.  
Nike GRIND is the only footwear recycling program operating at a commercial scale, having 
recycled over 67 million kg of used footwear (NIKE, 2024). It is essentially a downcycling

2.3.2.3 Alternatives to recycling: reuse, repair, energy recovery and design improvements 
As per the European waste hierarchy, reuse and repair are more desirable waste management 
strategies than recycling. Recycling footwear is limited, thus it is important to determine the 
best option for end-of-life footwear that is not recycled, as well as material improvements that 
limit the need for waste management in the first place. A comprehensive waste management 
infrastructure should include both repair, reuse, recycling and energy recovery. Waste 
management often tends to neglect reuse and repair activities, and therefore it is important to 
assess the potential for such options, as they are even better at retaining materials than

Reuse 
Reuse is already a widely used waste management strategy for footwear, though currently only 
saleable footwear is reused (Enevoldsen, 2024).  Replacement rates for footwear, accessories 
and footwear, seems to be very low, compared to other goods (>30%). Furthermore a “rebound” 
effect has been discussed in literature, where the savings from acquiring reused goods are used 
to purchase additional goods, thus increasing overconsumption(Behave Green, 2024).  
As post-consumer footwear is only resold, there is a need to explore the potential for alternative 
reuse pathways, by examining the amount of reusable footwear that is currently incinerated or 
landfilled. 
Repair 
Repair is not widely practiced due to a lack of knowledge about cobblers/menders and what is 
repairable (Rahimifard et al., 2007; ’Rossi et al., 2024; Van Rensburg et al., 2020) . (Nazlı, 2021) 
identifies barriers to repair as technical, value-based and emotional, including lack of 
knowledge, skills, reversibility, accessibility and design issues. Conversely, surveys in India 
show that the prevalence of cheap local cobblers deters major retailers from offering repair 
services (Mittal et al., 2023). This indicates that there is a potentially unused waste 
management option in repair, either as a proactive approach, by increasing the knowledge of 
repair options, or by repairing post-consumer footwear. For the latter, it is crucial to estimate 
the amount of repairable footwear in waste footwear. 
The limited literature on repair, such as (Karg, 1965), focuses on Goodyear welted shoes, with 
other sources like (Rossi et al., 2024) and repair websites providing additional insights. This lack 
of literature indicates a need for a unified terminology for post-consumer footwear and 
highlights a potential for including reparability in DPPs. 
Other alternatives to recycling 
Material and design improvements are cited as proactive strategies for reducing or easing waste 
management. Examples of material improvements include removing harmful chemicals from to 
mitigate impacts from landfilling, recycling or energy recovery. The use of biodegradable 
materials has been proposed, and implemented, as a strategy for reducing the amount of 
landfilled waste from post-consumer footwear (Heath et al., 2006). 
Design improvements include using assembly techniques that allow for repair or easy 
disassembly in order to allow for non-destructive recycling (Thompson, 2021). Using fewer 
materials is also considered a viable design improvement, that eases both destructive and non-
destructive recycling (Marias, 2018). Another design improvement is to not include materials 
that overlap in density, to improve recycling (World footwear, 2015). Reducing the number of 
different materials and different parts is another approach to increasing the recyclability of 
footwear through design improvements (Marias, 2018).

characteristics of the waste footwear and the life cycle impacts of the different technologies 
should be explored. A comprehensive LCA, including all life-cycle stages, would be useful to

2.3.3 Waste LCA of footwear 
Life Cycle Assessments (LCAs) can be used to assess the environmental impacts of products 
and systems. For footwear, it is a tool that can be used to compare different products, materials 
and production methods. An LCA should always include the end-of-life phase, but is often 
included as generic processes, because the prerequisite waste LCAs has not been conducted. 
A waste LCA compares the environmental impacts of different waste treatment options. 
 Footwear is a particular complex product in relation to performing waste LCAs, due to the high 
diversity of materials, and number of additives. Furthermore, lifetimes are a particular issue in 
LCAs of footwear, making it difficult to create comparative LCAs of footwear products. Existing 
footwear LCA’s rarely focus on the end-of-life stage, using generic values and treatment options. 
This is important to investigate to know how big of a part the end-of-life stage has in the life 
cycle impacts of footwear. LCA’s are often used to guide management and policy making, and 
therefore it is important to assess the current literature on footwear LCAs in order to identify 
missing information in current research. 
Several LCA’s has been conducted on footwear, though none has focused specifically on waste 
management.  
(Chrobot, 2018) conducted an LCA of the textile and apparel industry, finding that footwear 
accounted for 16-32% of impacts from the industry. Generic processes for textile landfilling and 
incineration were used. Transport to incineration and landfill was not included. 
(Gottfridsson & Zhang, 2015) Conducted an LCA of footwear consumption in Sweden, divided in 
CN-categories. Footwear waste was included as a stochastic variable, showing a need to 
establish the relationship between footwear categories and weight. Waste management was 
found to generally have numerically negative environmental impacts (as in the gains from 
recycling outweighing the impacts). However, average recycling rates and an average split 
between treatment methods in different EU countries were used. 
No Information was found on footwear lifetimes, which was also stated as a limitation 
in(Chrobot, 2018; Gottfridsson & Zhang, 2015). (Rossi et al., 2024) includes an overview of 
lifetime estimations for different footwear types, though as stated in (de Wagenaar et al., 2022; 
Syversen et al., 2023) apparel lifetimes are rarely correlated to durability, and is influenced by 
more intangible factors, such as affectionate value and perceived quality. 
These findings underline the complexity of performing waste LCAs on footwear, and the lack of 
knowledge on lifetimes needed to make comparative LCAs of different footwear types and 
designs.

3.1 Footwear survey 
750 kg of post-consumer footwear was donated for surveying by NewRetex. NewRetex 
is a recycling company that sorts and recycles post-consumer textiles and footwear. 
The company is situated in Bjerringbro, in central Jutland. The company receives 
around 53 tons of textile waste every month. Most of the textile waste comes from 
municipal waste from both recycling stations and household collection schemes (85%) 
with the rest coming from laundries (13%) and industry (2%) (Enevoldsen, 2024). 
Of the 53 tons collected every month, 2.94% is footwear, although footwear accounts for 
25.6% of all the reused textile waste. The reused shoes must be sellable, meaning that 
they must be mostly clean, intact, and without major signs of wear(Enevoldsen, 2024) . 
All the donated footwear comes from municipalities in Jutland (both recycling stations 
and household collection). Saleable footwear has been sorted out, meaning that it is 
representative of footwear currently being incinerated.

complexity within the five product categories. In the first step shoes were sorted into 
one of five different footwear categories. The categories are adopted from the five

and CN-categories (COUNCIL REGULATION (EEC) No 2658/87, 1987; Directive 94/11, 
1994). Any pairs identified in this step were placed on a line within their respective 
categories, and singles were piled within their categories. A description of how the

3.2 Modelling of material composition, repair and recycling potentials 
This section covers how the collected data was used to model material composition, repair 
potentials and recycling potentials. 
3.2.1 Modelling of material composition 
Data collected on the materials of both the major parts of the footwear (upper, lining and sole) 
and on any minor parts of a material different than the corresponding major part, was used to 
estimate a detailed material composition of the waste stream, and each product category.  
The weight or volume of each part was not found in the literature, and thus every part was 
modelled as an equal share of the weight of the major part (eg. upper, lining and sole). A ratio of 
2:1:2 was assumed for the upper, lining and sole respectively, as no information on the weight 
proportions were found. For each minor part with no material listed, the material of the 
corresponding major part of the footwear was ascribed to the given part. For footwear with no 
sock or lining, the weight assigned to the lining was divided proportionally to the other existing 
materials. The category “no single material most prevalent” was treated as its own material 
category. 
Metal parts were modelled as weighing 5 grams each, regardless of which finding it was. For 
pairs the weight of a metal part equaled 10 grams. These weights were subtracted 
proportionally from the materials present in the upper and lining. It was assumed that no metal 
was present in the sole.  
The model was set up so that weighing the individual footwear parts will be possible if found in 
further research. 
3.2.2 Modelling of repair potentials 
To model repairability and the rejects from repair a monetary-based method was used. Repair 
prices were compared to prices of new footwear, where cases where the cost of repair was

lower than the price of new footwear were considered economically viable, and cases where 
the price of repair exceeded the price of new footwear were considered non-viable and rejected 
from the repair stream. 
Two criteria for repair were modelled, one including footwear deemed repairable as per repairs 
found in (Byens Rens, 2024; Neuner, 2023, 2024a, 2024b; Rossi et al., 2024), and another also 
including “dirty” footwear, extrapolated from the dataset, as footwear not deemed reusable, but 
without any visible damage, which were included in case they could be cleaned. All 
combinations of repairable damages were found in the dataset, excluding footwear with non-
repairable damages. 
Average product prices were pulled from (statista, 2024), which used six different product 
categories: “sneakers”, “sandals”, “running shoes”, “gym and training”, “business and formal” 
and “boots”. See Table 3.3 for definitions. 
The price of each category was converted to DKK using a cost-of-living multiplier of 1.72 and a 
conversion rate for USD to DKK of 1:7.15 (NUMBEO, 2024). 
Prices for repair was pulled from(Byens Rens, 2024; Neuner, 2023, 2024a, 2024b), where both 
the minimum and maximum prices were used and converted to DKK using the same cost-of-
living multiplier for American prices. Danish prices were prioritized over converted prices. The 
repairs available in these sources were also the ones used to model repairability, meaning that 
footwear with damages not considered repairable were excluded (see Table 3.3). 
To estimate the percentage of repairable footwear, a normal distribution of the footwear prices 
was assumed within each footwear category. The percentile of footwear with a price above the 
repair was calculated at three different standard deviations (0.05, 0.1, 0.15), for both minimum 
and maximum repair prices. 
A full list of repairs considered, along with prices, definitions and counts, can be seen in 
appendix E. 
As per (statista, 2024) 8% of footwear was assumed to be luxury items, meaning that a repair 
was always modelled as economically viable. 
For footwear with more than one repairable damage category, the prices of the relevant repairs 
were added.

3.2 MFA 
The mass flow analysis was conducted to visualize the findings of the previous sections. The 
transfer coefficients were based on the data from the survey, as well as the modelling of 
material composition, repair potentials and recyclability. 
The MFA was carried out with the software STAN, version 2.7.101. 
This is relevant to identify hotspots for optimizing footwear production for end-of-life 
management, and to assess factors important for waste treatment 
3.2.1 Definition of scenarios 
Four scenarios were assessed to compare waste flows with and without different treatment 
options. 
1.  Best case recycling w. reuse and repair and cleaned footwear 
The recycling technologies used were based on the best combination of recycling technologies 
based on the results of the modelling of recycling potentials, focused on recovering the highest 
mass of recyclables. No prioritization of materials was considered. 
The recycling technologies chosen were: CeTIA for all soles, SOEX for all metal-containing 
uppers, and FFG for all remaining uppers. 
In this scenario, both repairable and cleanable footwear were included for refurbishment, as 
well as reusables. 
2.  Best case recycling w. reuse and repair, no cleaning 
This scenario included the same recycling criteria as above, but did not include cleanable 
footwear for refurbishment. 
3.  Best case recycling w. reuse 
In this scenario, only reusable footwear was considered for refurbishment. This mimics the 
current practice.

4.1.1.2 Material composition 
The material composition of the footwear was assessed both by identification of the most 
prominent material in the upper, lining and sole, and a more detailed assessment, where the 
material for each “part” of the footwear was identified. This was followed by identifying metal 
parts, by type of finding. This allowed for both a quick assessment of the most prominent 
materials, in line with how the EU CN (Directive 94/11, 1994) system works, and a more detailed 
modelling of the material composition of the footwear. 
Plastic and rubber were treated as one fraction, as it was found from evaluating pictures taken 
during the survey, that there were inconsistencies when identifying these. For instance, EVA 
foam was categorized as both rubber and plastics during the survey. 
In Figure 4.6, a basic representation of the material composition, showing the most prominent 
material of the uppers, and soles, of the surveyed footwear in each product category, can be 
seen. Generally, rubbers/plastics is the most used material for soles in all categories except LD, 
which should be leather. The reason for 25% of the LD sole material being cork, is that the 
definition of an LD shoe being comprised of only leather, refers to the outer sole material, and 
not the most prominent material of the sole. Often woodstack, cork or other materials are used 
in the midsole, which typically is the largest part of the sole, and as such cork was identified as 
the most prominent material in one instance.

4.1.1.3 Footwear categories and composition: Findings and limitations 
This section discusses the limitations and findings of the previous section (4.1.1), on the basis of 
RQ1 and RQ2. 
Findings and limitations related to identifying product categories. 
The survey was successfully conducted, with a high internal consistency. Some degree of 
mischaracterization was identified, namely related to the footwear types of CC and SS, and with 
regards to material identification. 
Findings 
Using the product categories to identify footwear was broadly successful, though material 
identification, and distinction between casual footwear and sports shoes proved difficult. 
CC, SS and SF were the most prominent categories, with less than 8% of surveyed entities being 
PF, and less than 1% LD. These numbers were roughly in line with the import statistics reported 
by (Rossi et al., 2024).  
Key findings in identifying footwear using product categories were: 
- 
It is possible to characterize post-consumer footwear based on the adapted version of 
the product categories described in (Rossi et al., 2024). 
- 
In line with import statistics, CC is the most common category, followed by SS, SF and 
PF. LD is the least prominent category. The CC and SS categories were dominated by a 
single footwear type, where the SF and PF categories had a large variation of types. 
Generally, a wide variety of different footwear types were identified. It might be more 
meaningful to use more specific categorization for waste management, as the product 
categories have a large internal diversity of designs.  
Limitations 
The limitations are closely related to the challenges of visually identifying footwear categories 
and materials. The consequences of misidentifying footwear categories are that the following

- 
Frequent misidentification between the SS and CC categories, particular for hiking 
shoes and sneakers, with the latter sometimes being marketed in both categories 
(COUNCIL REGULATION (EEC) No 2658/87, 1987). If a similar distinction were to be 
included in the DPP, clearer criteria should be included. 
- 
It was not possible to distinguish between rubber and plastics, as many polymers and 
rubbers share similar characteristics. A DPP should include more detailed information 
about the presence of polymers and rubbers, and include a way for waste handlers to 
distinguish between them. 
- 
Inconsistencies in leather classification was identified, with faux leather being labeled 
as genuine leather, and faux leather being labelled as “other”. A DPP should include 
clear reporting on which kind of leather is present and include a way to identify it. 
- 
Identifying leather without the presence of material pictograms proved difficult, as 
distinguishing between faux and genuine leather was often not possible. 
- 
Far from all sampled footwear had visible labelling, where it had either been worn off, 
cut out, or not visible at all (likely due to the labelling being present on the packaging the 
footwear was sold in). A DPP should be implemented in such a way that it would not be 
removed by the consumer before disposing of the footwear. 
- 
The survey was limited by the geographic and seasonal scope of the collected footwear, 
why it is recommended to repeat the survey to control variations in season and 
geography. 
Findings and limitations related to categorizing the waste stream. 
The survey showed a high diversity in footwear types and materials, as expected. Almost all 
footwear types included were found. Most of the sampled footwear were shoes, with a 
considerably higher amount of boots than sandals. 
Findings 
The distribution of waste footwear seems to loosely follow import figures, with slight differences 
that could be attributed to differences in identifying sandals, misidentification of sports 
footwear, and a higher selling price of boots and shoes compared to sandals (statista, 2024). 
The weight of the footwear seems to be normally distributed, though a larger sample would be

needed to statistically prove this. The most common footwear types were sneakers and running 
shoes, which waste management strategies should focus on. Rain boots and snow boots were 
also common, which could be ascribed to the Danish climate. Sandals were spread out among 
footwear types, making it harder to focus recycling efforts on a specific type. 
The material composition, measured as the most prevalent material in each major footwear 
part, seems to loosely follow import statistics, with a lower percentage of leather indicating that 
leather footwear might have a longer effective lifetime. A third of footwear had metal parts, 
which complicates recycling. 
 
The key findings related to the characteristics of the footwear categories and material 
composition were: 
- 
Footwear weight is normally distributed. The weight distribution could be used to 
expand on existing work, such as the LCA made by (Gottfridsson & Zhang, 2015) where 
the weight was treated as a stochastic variable. 
- 
33.13% of the sampled footwear had at least 1 metal part. This should be considered in 
waste management. 25.13% of metal parts were nonfunctional, which is a low-hanging 
fruit in terms of design improvements. 
- 
The classification between footwear types in terms of gender showed a particularly high 
amount of footwear being classified as “female”.  Some of this is likely to be explained 
from the fact that specifically female footwear designs is easier to spot, while it is 
possible that male footwear is “hidden” within the unisex category.  The fact that the 
sum of unisex and male is still lower than the share of female footwear, points to the fact 
that there is an overrepresentation of women’s footwear. This indicates that waste 
prevention should be particularly focused on consumers of footwear with female 
characteristics. 
- 
The two most common footwear types were “sneakers” and “running shoes”. As such 
waste management would do well to focus on these footwear types. 
- 
Rubber/plastic soles were most common. 
-

and SF were mixed.  
- 
The material composition of the individual product categories in the waste 
characterization followed the material composition of the import statistics.

have influenced the types of footwear present. The exclusion of saleable footwear is likely to 
have skewed the composition towards less valuable materials and types. The sample size also 
restricted a deeper analysis of less common footwear types and categories, such as LD 
footwear.  
- 
Resalable footwear was excluded, as such the results should be viewed as 
representative of non-salable post-consumer footwear. It is recommended to also look 
into saleable post-consumer footwear.

The sample size did not allow for a detailed analysis of LD footwear, nor did it allow for

The reusability was determined based on the footwear having no damage and not having 
permanent miscoloring or mold. It is also important to keep in mind that reuse generally falls 
within two categories: reusable and resalable. In this study, it is the former that is considered. 
Reusability was considered in the initial survey, giving a total of 264 reusable pairs out of 882 
surveyed pairs.

4.1.2.4 Footwear condition, complexity and design weak spots findings and limitations 
This section discusses the limitations and findings of the previous section (4.1.2), on the basis of 
RQ2. 
Findings 
The results showed that around 1/5 of non-salable post-consumer footwear is still usable, 
around 1/5 was reparable, around 1/5 was dirty/miscolored with the rest being damaged or 
singles. Reusability does not seem to be correlated with weight or material complexity. This 
suggests unfulfilled potential for refurbishment and reuse. 
Damage patterns revealed that holes (in either sole or upper) was the most common damage 
category, followed by degradation of plastics and damaged insole heels, indicating that these 
are weak spots of particular importance with regards to longevity of footwear. 
Material complexity seems to be normally distributed, with most footwear having 3-5 different 
primary materials, though this estimate does not account for polymer variations. Boots 
exhibited a higher reusability compared to shoes and sandals, though it remains unclear if this 
is due to durability or consumer behavior. 
Design improvements should focus on limiting material complexity, reducing metal parts, 
particularly non-ferrous and non-functional, and reinforcing insole heels, as the damage 
analysis showed this to be a particular weak spot. An interesting finding was that a big share of 
the damaged footwear had plastic degradation as the sole damage category, indicating that it 
had been the reason for the disposal. This goes against previous recommendations, as 
biodegradable materials have been widely cited as a design improvement, to lower impacts 
from landfilling. It would be needed to be verified that plastic degradation was the reason for the 
disposal.

parts, leading to inaccuracies. All minor parts were assumed to take up an equal share of the 
corresponding major parts, which does not account for differences in material density or the 
proportional weight of the minor parts. Additionally, metal content was given an arbitrary

detailed anatomical studies of footwear. Finding the specific materials, proportional volume of 
each part and the density of each material would allow for more precise modelling. 
Major limitations are listed below: 
- 
All parts were weighed equally, likely leading to underrepresentation of some materials. 
Including a list of parts in a DPP would be useful for waste management.  
- 
Metal parts are assumed to weigh 5 grams, regardless of type, which is an arbitrary 
assumption. 
- 
Metal is assumed to not be present in the sole. Metal layers, and spikes on the sole are 
present in some footwear types.  
- 
Material density was not considered, meaning the percentages presented are more 
likely to correlate with the volumes than the masses of the materials. 
- 
The chosen ratio between the mass of the three major parts was assumed, which is also 
likely to vary between footwear types and materials. Including the weight of each major 
part of the footwear in a DPP would help future waste management.

Limitations 
Comparing recycling technologies proved difficult due to the limited amount of data available 
on technical specifications on material purity, loss-rates and end-use applications. Therefore, 
the analysis was based on feedstock.  
The analysis of recycling technologies was carried out to assess potential gains and should not 
be interpreted as the actual recovery percentages, as important factors such as loss and purity 
are not included. (Rahimifard & Lee, 2010), mentions purity ranges of 50%-90% for textile, 
rubber and foam streams. These estimates are, however, based on pilot-scale technologies 
from 2009, and it may be possible to achieve higher purity with contemporary or future 
technology. 
Additionally, assumptions about material composition and recycling eligibility, repair/reuse 
destinations, and the eligibility for repair, introduce uncertainties that requires further 
refinement in future studies. 
- 
The analysis does not include material application which was requested of the recycling 
companies. 
- 
No information about material loss is available, which was also requested of the 
recycling companies, limiting insights into the efficiency of the recycling technologies. 
- 
No information about output material purity was available, which was also requested of 
the recycling companies, meaning that the quality of the material output streams were 
not considered. 
- 
An average split of plastics/rubbers were used, whereas a more detailed analysis would 
provide more accurate recovery estimates. 
- 
Recycling of PVC was included for Re_Shoes/CeTIA. PVC often comes in the form of 
softened PVC, which is generally not considered recyclable, though recent research 
(Marsura et al., 2023) and (Sommer-Larsen & Olsen, 2023) suggests that PVC can be 
recycled through mixing with other polymers. 
- 
Added materials from the repair stages added, or materials needed to produce the  
reprocessed materials were not considered, potentially affecting the actual mass-
balance.

5 Discussion 
5.1 Viability of recycling as waste management 
approach to footwear 
Nike Grind is the only recycling company that operates at a commercial scale and is also the 
recycling scheme that has been operating for the longest time. Of the other recycling 
companies, only FFG is operating at a large scale (2400 shoes/h). The other technologies are 
only operating at pilot scale (Hansen, 2024). It could be that the higher recoverability of the 
other companies is a result of an economically unsustainable business model, that might not 
work at scale. Furthermore, there is no guarantee that NIKE Grind in itself is a profitable venture. 
It is important to note that these technologies all depend on labour intensive sorting, which 
challenges the economic feasibility of these business models (Rahimifard & Lee, 2010), without 
substantial shifts in the current waste management system.  
Metal sorting is another barrier to recycling as it requires costly manual sorting. Metals can be 
identified with metal detectors, and removed manually, though this has been cited as being 
labour-and cost-intensive and might make the recycling process economically infeasible 
(Marias, 2018; Rahimifard & Lee, 2010). Automatic metal separation can remove ferrous metals 
with magnetic separation, and non-ferrous metals can be removed with eddy current 
separation. Hydrocyclones have also been proven to work on a pilot-basis though it is unknown 
which technology SOEX uses for metal separation (Hansen, n.d.; Lee & Rahimifard, 2012; 
Rahimifard et al., 2007; Rahimifard & Lee, 2010).  
In(Curtis & Hansson, 2019), the Nike Grind programme is criticized for potentially offsetting the 
environmental savings from avoided landfilling and incineration, by the transport emissions 
from transporting post-consumer footwear, seeing as their only two reprocessing plants are 
located in Memphis, USA and Meerhout, Belgium. Furthermore, they are criticised for using a 
linear recycling process, where the reprocessed materials are not reintegrated into their 
production line, even though a number of products use recycled materials. 
“closed”-loop recycling 
None of the recycling companies are transparent with what materials are used for which 
applications. Only Re_shoes/CeTIA states a closed-loop recycling method, where the 
reprocessed materials are used in new footwear soles. This claim can, however, also be made 
for FFG and Regenely, as one of the stated material applications is soles in new footwear. No 
company currently recycles every material stream in a close-loop manner(CETIA, 2024; 
FastFeetGrinded, 2024; NIKE, 2024; REGENELY, 2024; SOEX, 2024). 
The term “closed-loop recycling” is misleading, as a major part of the item is discarded i.e. the 
upper/lining, or in the best of cases downcycled, and the reprocessed material i.e. footwear 
soles, do not retain the material properties of the virgin materials. 
As such an intermediate category would be appropriate such as “degrading partially closed-
loop recycling”, defined as recycling activities where part of the material is repurposed for the 
same application, but has significant loss of material properties.

5.2 Issues with reuse as a waste management strategy 
Reuse is, after landfilling, the most used waste management strategy for footwear (Rahimifard 
et al., 2007). While the practice has shifted from focusing on donating used footwear to 
developing economies, thus reducing social issues such as social dumping, there are still 
considerable issues with reselling footwear, even in domestic markets. While the obvious issue 
lies with exporting footwear to countries with more environmentally damaging EoL 
infrastructure, reuse in domestic markets, especially with apparel, might lead to increased 
consumption, also called the rebound effect, due to saved money being spent on new items 
from virgin materials. A recent report on the replacement rates of reused items (Behave Green, 
2024) showed that only 33% of apparel acquired through reuse directly, or indirectly replaces 
the purchase of a virgin good. 
5.2 Footwear repair as a reactive waste management strategy 
In the footwear waste management framework developed by (T. Staikos & Rahimifard, 2007), 
footwear repair and refurbishment are considered reuse activities, and as such reactive waste 
management strategies. This is certainly the case when considering post-consumer footwear, 
such as the footwear examined in the survey, though footwear at the consumer level could also 
be considered a preventive waste management strategy, as repair of footwear to extent its 
lifetime could decrease consumption and disposal volumes. 
Footwear repair is commonly cited as being underutilized at the consumer level, as consumers 
lack knowledge of repair locations, and what can be repaired (Rahimifard et al., 2007; Rossi et 
al., 2024; Van Rensburg et al., 2020). This situation seems to be unique to developed 
economies, as a questionnaire survey done in India, showed  the availability of local menders, 
to be a cultural barrier to implement repair services at the company level(Mittal et al., 2024). It 
was found that repair as a product-service system was, from an Indian perspective, only an 
attractive proposition for complicated footwear such as running shoes.  
Historically, footwear repair has been a widespread practice, though the prevalence of cheap, 
mass-produced footwear, has caused a significant decline in the industry, with most cobblers 
and menders promoting other services such as key-cutting and engraving(Rahimifard et al., 
2007). (Karg, 1965) includes a detailed guide on how to perform a wide range of repairs on 
welted shoes, but no such literature exists on more recent footwear assembly technologies, 
indicating that the assembly technology is an important factor in limiting footwear repair 
possibilities. 
5.3 EPR and Digital Product Passports 
This survey used the EU CN and labelling directive (COUNCIL REGULATION (EEC) No 2658/87, 
1987; Directive 94/11, 1994), and was the basis for identifying footwear categories. Following 
the approach used in this thesis, the categories did not seem to be distinguishable when 
looking at material recovery, or best recycling practices. The material classification system used 
in the labelling directive is lacking when it comes to providing detailed enough information for 
waste handlers to make decisions regarding recycling (Marias, 2018). 
To optimize the recycling potential of footwear, knowledge about materials and chemicals is 
important. Namely, information on which polymers and rubbers are used, as it is important to 
be able to separate materials from each other during grinding. Many polymers overlap in 
densities, both with other polymers, but also with rubbers and leathers(J. T. Staikos & 
==PAGE:76==
Villads Milthers Fritsche, S184117 
 
Master Thesis 
75 
 
Rahimifard, 2007). Information on the chemicals contained in the footwear is also important, as 
to ensure no accumulation of harmful chemicals in recycled materials. Lastly the construction 
process used for the shoe would be a valuable piece of information, as footwear that is injection 
moulded or vulcanized is harder to manually disassemble, which makes them less eligible for 
recycling processes that focus on closed-loop recycling the soles. 
The format of the DPP, should also be considered. Currently QR-codes, RFID-tags and NFC 
chips are discussed. The issue with using RFID-tags and QR-codes relates to wear and tear. 
Much of the footwear examined in the laboratory, had labels/tags that were unreadable due to 
the wear from the use-stage. Such a label/tag would have to be both protected from wear and 
not affect the comfort or design of the footwear. NFC chips would add the problem of 
introducing a mandatory metal part in the footwear, that would have to be removed before 
recycling. It can also be expected that manufacturing an NFC tag for every piece of footwear 
would increase the environmental impact of the footwear. Whether a tag/label or a chip is used, 
this would have to be present on both pieces of footwear in a pair, as one can expect to find 
quite a lot of singles when receiving post-consumer footwear. If a chip is used, it would be 
preferable to embed it in the sole as it is less likely to degrade, and easy to remove from the 
upper. 
Instead of implementing a difficult to use identification system, legislation on the design of 
footwear would probably be more effective in increasing circularity. Such legislation could 
include: not using materials of overlapping densities in footwear parts that are not easy to 
disassemble, using biodegradable materials for footwear that is not recyclable or not using toxic 
chemicals in polymers and rubbers.  
5.4 Implications of degraded plastics in post-consumer footwear 
To combat leachate formation from toxic substances in landfilled footwear, the use 
of biodegradable materials has been cited as a strategy to reduce the environmental 
impacts from post-consumer footwear(Heath et al., 2006). 
From a Danish perspective this might be detrimental, as using biodegradable 
materials only lowers the environmental footprint of footwear disposed of in landfills 
but has a detrimental impact on the lifetime of footwear. Seeing as the most 
common damage category in the assessed shoes were degradation of plastics, it 
seems that this strategy might just increase the volume of waste. Environmental 
impacts might also be lowered on paper if the materials are manufactured from 
biogenic sources, as carbon emissions from biogenic sources are often calculated 
as zero. This does not mean that there are zero emissions, just that they are mitigated 
by the continued sequestration of carbon from new crops. Degradation of plastics is 
not a problem solely ascribed to biodegradable materials. Polyurethane, and other 
materials that can decompose through pyrolysis can also degrade, and replacing 
these with materials from a natural source would pose a decrease in the overall 
environmental impacts of the footwear(World Footwear, 2022). 
In a report on materials and sustainability in footwear created by the World Footwear 
organization, the use of different biodegradable, and bio-based materials are 
discussed(World Footwear, 2022). As previously mentioned, bio-based materials 
reduce the environmental impact during the production stage, as a larger share of 
the carbon comes from biogenic sources, and biodegradable materials reduce the 
==PAGE:77==
Villads Milthers Fritsche, S184117 
 
Master Thesis 
76 
 
environmental impacts from landfilling. Biodegradable material can be both fossil-
based or bio-based, and a bio-based material can be both biodegradable and non-
biodegradable. 
Bioplastics are never 100% bio-based and always includes some virgin materials. 
The same report referred to a case study on biobased materials and found that of the 
six investigated bio-plastics, the percentage of biobased carbon ranged from 11%-
82%. It should be noted that bovine-based leathers, both chromium and non-
chromium tanned, only contained 80% bio-based carbon. All eight tested upper 
materials were tested for durability, water vapor permeability and absorption. This 
test found that chromium-tanned bovine leather, was both the most durable, flexible 
and breathable of all tested upper materials(World Footwear, 2022).  
Further work 
An investigation into the life cycle impacts of low-durability footwear compared to 
high-durability footwear should be carried out, with a comparable functional unit, in 
order to further investigate this topic. An important point in potential investigation of 
this is that different types of footwear are not used for the same occasions, the point 
being that an athletic shoe cannot replace a hiking boot and vice versa. Such a 
comparison should be made on footwear fulfilling an equal purpose. For this, a 
determination of lifetimes for footwear of different materials is crucial, which will be 
discussed in the following section. 
5.5 Harmful chemicals and substances in footwear 
Though not considered in this analysis, the contents of harmful chemicals in footwear should 
be considered when recycling. 
A chemical analysis of three different pieces of footwear was conducted by (Dahlberg, 2010). A 
number of organic chemicals were found in each shoe, in both the upper, midsole, outsole and 
the lining through methanol extraction, with three of the identified chemicals being on the 
REACH list, though in concentrations over 100 times smaller than the stated limit values.  
Furthermore, a leaching test was executed, to simulate environmental conditions, where a 
number of chemicals that had not been identified in the methanol extraction were found. A 
subsequent larval development, and growth inhibition test was performed at three different 
concentrations (148 mg shoe/litre, 151 mg shoe/litre and 1,07 g shoe/litre). In all three 
concentrations, a significant decrease in larval development was observed, and for the highest 
concentration, for two of the shoes, a significant mortality was observed. This was concluded to 
be due to a high concentration in Zinc-oxide, an inorganic chemical used in the vulcanization of 
rubber. The contents of organic chemicals were concluded to not be expected to be toxic to 
aquatic organisms. 
Even though the contents of chemicals in footwear are not expected to be a concern with 
regards to pollution of ecosystems, it could have an impact on the purity and toxicity of 
reprocessed materials, as concentrations might aggregate through multiple use-cycles.

5.6 Future research 
5.6.1 Improvements of waste characterization survey 
Along with the limitations discussed in sections 4.1.1.3 and 4.1.2.4, a number of improvements 
to the waste characterization survey were noted. These recommendations would all be of 
interest for both waste management purposes and for implementation in DPPs.  
Recycling disruptors  
The model did not distinguish between ferrous and non-ferrous metals. This would be an 
important information to include in further characterization of waste footwear, as non-ferrous 
metals cannot be removed through magnetic separation, and thus act as a particular difficult to 
remove recycling disruptor (Hansen, n.d.; Marias, 2018). 
Hard plastics have also been cited as being a significant recycling disrupter, and hard plastics 
should also be considered in future footwear waste characterization(Marias, 2018). 
Characterization of polymers 
A generic split between the average content of polymers and rubber in footwear was used. This 
should be improved through a more detailed characterization of polymers. The densities of 
polymers are important when reprocessing shredded footwear (Hansen, 2024; J. T. Staikos & 
Rahimifard, 2007). 
Assembly techniques 
The survey did not characterize assembly techniques. Further surveys characterizing footwear 
waste would do well to include this parameter. The assembly technique has a great influence on 
that can be repaired, how easy footwear is to disassemble, and whether glues are present in the 
footwear(Dryfit max, 2023; Karg, 1965). 
==PAGE:79==
Villads Milthers Fritsche, S184117

78 
 
Test precision of screening 
The method used in the survey should be controlled with a batch of known materials and 
footwear categories, to quantify the uncertainty due to human error in the method. Such a 
screening would be valuable to assess the validity of the findings, related to the use of footwear 
product categories for waste management. If the survey was found to not characterize the 
footwear properly, previous findings related to the product categories would be proven invalid. 
Expand on footwear typology 
A detailed cataloguing of different footwear parts, found in different footwear types, would help 
improve on the dataset, specifically the quantification method used for the material contents. 
The tiered typology used in the survey proved effective, and would be very useful, if the volumes 
of individual parts could be added to the dataset. 
Detailed material analysis of footwear types 
A useful analysis would be to carry out detailed material analysis of a representative number of 
footwear, within the footwear types described in this thesis. That would allow for efficient waste 
management based on the footwear typology used. 
5.6.2 Footwear lifetime 
An important knowledge gap is the effective lifetime of footwear. Carrying out wardrobe analysis 
on a wide range of footwear types would give valuable insights into how footwear is consumed, 
and what the factors driving the disposal are. The literature only describes footwear lifetime in 
terms of durability (Rossi et al., 2024), though wardrobe studies on clothing (de Wagenaar et al., 
2022; Syversen et al., 2023) has shown that durability rarely is the main driver. Effective lifetime 
is probably highly cultural. An interesting note is that (Mittal et al., 2023) described a “short” 
lifespan for footwear being three years, when discussing cultural barriers to implementing 
footwear as a product-service system. Determining lifetimes of footwear is essential before any 
meaningful life-cycle analysis of waste footwear can be carried out. 
6 Conclusions and recommendations 
 
This study aimed to characterize post-consumer footwear waste and evaluate potential waste 
management approaches to improve material circularity, through a combination of lab-work, 
modelling, and mass-flow analysis. The research questions focused on RQ1: Evaluating the 
viability if using existing footwear classification in waste management, RQ2: characterizing 
Danish post-consumer footwear waste in terms of material composition, condition, complexity 
and typology, and RQ3 assessing the potentials for design improvements, repair, reuse and 
recycling through mass-flow analysis. 
To answer the research questions, this thesis proposed a novel methodology for surveying, 
sampling and characterizing post-consumer footwear, along with a comprehensive lab based 
sampling and testing of this methos, which resulted in a comprehensive dataset of footwear 
composition. This dataset includes: information about distribution of footwear on types, 
product categories and footwear groups, along with information on weight distribution, 
condition, damages present and reusability status. From this dataset, an MFA model for

estimating the material composition of waste footwear, using the aforementioned methodology 
for data collection was created, along with a model for estimating economically viable 
repairability. These results were used to create a material flow analysis to evaluate 
improvements for recycling, and missing information for DPPs. 
The categorization of footwear was found to be largely correct, though with a few 
mischaracterizations. However, the proposed method for material identification proved difficult. 
In particular, there is a need to improve the method to include approaches for distinguishing 
between rubber and plastics, as well as genuine and faux leather.  
The use of existing classification systems to guide waste management showed that there is 
need for additional classifications to improve sorting for circularity. The sorting revealed that  
material metal content and material complexity were key factors in determining the recyclability 
of collected footwear. However, neither factor is well linked to the existing product categories. 
Thus, in answer to RQ1, there is a need for higher granularity in the labels which identify these 
materials as the broad categories, such as the ones used in current legislation, are not precise 
enough to guide material identification for recycling.  
The quantitative analysis showed that the typological composition of Danish post-consumer 
footwear waste, largely follows the import statistics derived using existing broad classifications.  
However, it is uncertain if this is also true at the footwear group and type level, as such figures 
for imports do not exist. Therefore in answer to RQ2, there is a clear need to further improve the 
existing import classification system to include details at the group and type levels.  
 In this study, there was a distinct lack of leather footwear, compared to the material 
composition of imported footwear. This may indicate that leather footwear might have a longer 
retention time with consumers, be resold at a higher rate, be repaired more or any combination 
of the three. This survey also found that 33% of footwear was found to contain at least 1 metal 
part, with a further 25% of these being non-functional. Thus, when considering materials to 
extend the lifetimes of footwear, further investigations into the use of leather may be warranted. 
In addition, as metal parts deter footwear from recyclability, there is a need to investigate the 
design choices around the use of metal parts in shoes and determine the tradeoffs between 
aesthetics and recyclability.  
This study also found that there is a clear need to improve communication about repair and 
reuse alternatives, as 38% of footwear was either reusable or miscolored/dirty, while 26% were 
deemed repairable. 
This was confirmed through the MFA as over 50% of footwear was found to be economically 
feasible to repair or reuse. Several limitations might mean that the results are overestimated, as 
the repair potentials were based on a price-comparison model, and the orthopedic and value of 
the reusable footwear was not considered. Therefore, in answer to RQ3, there is a need to 
examine the design choices regarding materials used and the tradeoffs between design choices 
and recyclability. To support any such changes, there is also a need to improve communication 
about alternative longevity, repair, and reuse strategies to help reduce the rate of repairable or 
reusable shoes sent to waste management.  
Finally, this study concluded that no existing recycling technology can recover all materials from 
the footwear stream, even when assuming 100% efficiency. Thus, a combination of 
technologies should be used in combination with an MRF that can sort out the appropriate 
materials.

6.1 Recommendations and future work 
Based on the findings from this research, a list of recommendations was made: 
Digital Product Passports (DPP’s) 
A DPP should be implemented in such a way that is its useable for waste handlers, without 
affecting recycling potentials. 
- 
Current labels on materials are often missing or too worn to read, underlining the need 
for a durable identifier, rooting out label-based DPPs (e.g. QR-codes) 
- 
Metal/electronic based identifiers might prove a challenge for waste management, if 
they are not easily removable 
- 
A standardized model of footwear parts would be useful for disassembly of footwear, to 
prepare for repair or recycling. 
- 
A production date would be a simple and very useful addition, to assess the effective 
lifetime of footwear. 
Material composition and identification Is crucial to waste management, therefore a 
future DPP should include the following. 
- 
Detailed information on polymers and rubbers, including specific polymers, densities, 
and their percentage of mass in each footwear part, as well as a list of additives, both 
REACH and non-REACH, as substances might be found to be harmful in the future. 
- 
Detailed information on leather, including tanning agent, leather type (faux, bovine etc.) 
and coating materials.  
- 
Information on recycling disruptors, including metal parts and parts made of hard 
plastic. Information on metal type (ferrous/non-ferrous) and plastic type (specific 
polymer), as well as the placement of said parts, would help recycling efforts. 
Assembly techniques and disassembly guidelines are crucial for repair efforts, therefore a 
future DPP should include the following. 
- 
Information on assembly method (cementing, vulcanized, goodyear welted etc.). This 
would help identify footwear eligible for non-destructive recycling, as well as ease 
repair. 
- 
Guidelines on how to disassemble footwear, specifically on information on how to 
separate soles from uppers, so as to ease sole recycling. 
- 
Information on easily repairable parts, and how to perform the repairs, would help 
reduce waste quantities, and prolong the footwear lifetime, by enabling both consumers 
and waste handlers to easily repair damaged footwear. 
Information on environmentally harmful substances is crucial to prevent accumulation of 
toxic substances in recycling, and to prevent resale of footwear with legacy chemicals. 
- 
The use of chemicals, additives and heavy-metals should be included, so as to prevent 
their accumulation through recycling. 
- 
In line with recommendations made for materials, it should be stated in which materials 
these additives and substances are embedded, as well as in which parts specifically

All of these factors should be considered when implementing DPP. Testing different solutions 
for identifiers will have to be tested in real-life scenarios, as it is difficult to simulate user 
behavior. 
Footwear design 
Improving material choices for circularity could help to include recovery, and improve 
efficiency for recycling. 
- 
Reducing complexity, i.e. number of different footwear materials, would help to ease 
recycling, and make for purer output streams. 
- 
Avoid materials with overlapping densities, would likewise help to improve purity of 
output streams, thereby widening the potential applications, and making closed-loop 
recycling more feasible. 
For future work, it should be investigated whether using biodegradable materials is net-positive 
environmentally, and if so, in which cases. One could imagine that more often used, everyday 
footwear, such as sneakers, will be disposed of before the degradation of the plastics happens, 
whereas hiking boots, or other specialized or seasonal footwear, will have a longer effective 
lifetime.  
Enhancing repairability by creating footwear that is easily disassembled, would ease repair, 
and probably also lower the costs of repair.  
- 
Prioritizing easily repairable, or reassemblable assembly techniques, such as goodyear 
welting, could help prolong the lifespan of footwear, and make repair a more viable 
waste management strategy. 
- 
Implementing a changeable heelguard, might help prolong the lifetime of footwear, as 
the insole heel was found to be a commonly damaged area. 
An environmental assessment of assembly techniques, through LCA’s or similar methods, 
would help guide which assembly techniques to prioritize. 
A very important piece of further work would be to collect proper information on the effective 
lifetime of different types of footwear, which could be helped along by including a date of 
purchase in DPPs. 
Waste management 
Refurbishment of post-consumer footwear might be a viable waste management strategy. 
- 
Reusing non-salable footwear might be a way to prolong the lifetime of post-consumer 
footwear, as the survey showed a high percentage of reusable footwear. 
- 
Repairing post-consumer footwear might be a viable waste management strategy, as the 
model found a high percentage of reparable footwear 
- 
Cleaning dirty footwear, or probably more effective, ensuring that footwear is collected 
properly and is not spoiled in the collection phase, could significantly improve the 
amount of footwear that can be refurbished, or even resold. 
Further work should be put into creating a viable business model and ensuring that there is a 
demand for refurbished footwear. Optimizing collection schemes, is an issue that is also 
important to tackle for textile waste in general. 
Recycling activities should focus on sorting out the footwear viable for recycling.

- 
Separation of metals is important, as most recycling technologies do not accept metal-
containing footwear 
- 
Sorting activities should focus on removing soles from uppers, as soles are currently the 
only part of the footwear, that can be recycled and used for the same application. 
Further work should focus on exploring novel technologies for recycling, in order to move from 
downcycling, or at best partial closed-loop recycling, to proper closed-loop recycling. This work 
entails improving both material separation processes and remanufacturing processes. The 
degree of accumulation of harmful substances in reprocessed footwear should also be 
examined.
