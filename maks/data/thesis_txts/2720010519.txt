Abstract
This thesis explores the field of sustainable procurement and the potential of software
as a service to support it. The research aimed to identify good practices and behaviors
in sustainable procurement, understand the importance of software integration, and how
to overcome the challenges and barriers faced by the industry and software platform in
promoting sustainability.
Based on interviews with procurement professionals, academic experts, and consultants,
as well as document research, the study served as basis to provide recommendations
for Responsibly, the start-up company that is the subject of this case-study, looking to
improve the sustainable procurement functionalities in their software system. The rec-
ommendations focus on enhancing usability and aligning the product with its purpose to
promote sustainable procurement growth.
The research highlights the crucial role that software can play in promoting sustainability,
specifically in the procurement process. It sheds light on the challenges faced by the in-
dustry and the software platform, and provides practical recommendations for overcoming
them. This thesis concludes that further testing is needed before the recommendations
can be widely implemented.
The keywords were carefully selected and limited to those commonly used in sustain-
able procurement terminology to include: climate change, responsible sourcing, green
procurement, supply chains, renewable energy, software development, carbon footprint,
sustainable development, green procurement strategies, technology and innovation.

1.1
Supply Chain and Procurement towards green future
Climate change is the most significant challenge of the century, affecting society, pop-
ulation growth, the environment, and business operations inexorably (Schneider 2011).
It is well known that it also leads to rising temperatures, changes in seasons, sea-level
rise, extreme weather events, and natural disasters[2]. Climate change can be defined
as ”a change in the state of the climate that can be identified by changes in the mean
and variability of its properties and that lasts for decades or longer” [3]. Despite climate
change being a broadly researched topic, not many empirical studies explore climate
change from the perspective of the supply chain. According to Ecovadis, the world’s most
trusted provider of business sustainability ratings, supply chains represent 50% to 70% of
the Sustainability Footprint (including environmental, labor & human rights, and ethics) of
most major global companies. Consequently, there should be more empirical studies on
this topic. Furthermore, it implies that targeting sustainability efforts in the supply chain
can be an efficient approach to becoming more sustainable.
In line with industries becoming increasingly aware of the concept of sustainable supply
chains, the demand for transparency and sustainable solutions is growing. Furthermore,
this is fuelling peer pressure and competition which reinforces the move towards greener
supply chains.
When it comes to sustainable supply chains, procurement has a key role. While it has
traditionally been focused on cost reduction, procurement is increasingly taking on a more
strategic role in operations. By working closely with suppliers and across business func-
tions, procurement have started to add value by ensuring quality, reducing lead times,
and optimizing production. More recently, the idea of actively using procurement as a
means for operating sustainably is becoming applicable.
With changes in what is required from a business function often comes disruption in the
technology used/serving the function. It is crucial that there exists digital tools that can
contribute and facilitate the needs of sustainable procurement and the ability to build sus-
tainable supply chains. [2]

1.3
The Problem Statement
This case study aims to analyze the current processes and systems of Responsibly’s
ways of identifying the industry’s needs toward a more sustainable procurement goal.
Companies want reliable, high-quality, and trustworthy data on the sustainability of their
suppliers. Certifications and site audits are a go-to solution for this type of data. Nonethe-
less, certifications are often expensive for the supplier to come by both in monetary terms
and man-hours spent on compliance. Audits on the other hand are both costly to carry
out and made even more difficult by the global nature of supply chains.
The solution that many companies turn to fill the data gap is supplier surveys or question-
naires. This however comes with its own set of disadvantages.
• Low response rate - why is that? Time-consuming, survey fatigue, low IT literacy,
low literacy, different hypotheses depending on supplier segment
• How to evaluate responses, compare across suppliers
• Etc.
Based on this, the problem statement for this project is the following:
”How the incorporation of new features and strategic tools in Responsibly platform,
providing technology solutions, can increase the current performance of Procure-
ment regarding sustainable choices?”
The analysis will be done by doing market research, interviews with procurement profes-
sionals and academics, building frameworks to reach customer success, as well as get
an understanding of how the platform innovation is currently working. The implementation
of different scenarios such as news features in the platform and their impact, will also be
studied so that the relationship with the client is improved.

2.1
Sustainability
The essence of sustainability lies in holding our world in trust for future generations. Ac-
cording to the UN, the definition of Sustainability is ” meeting the needs of the present
without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs”.
If we take a look into the biggest impacts of sustainability worldwide, everything passes
through industries and supply chains. This is due to big logistics distributions across the
world since the majority of the production and warehouse are located in Asia and trans-
ported everywhere in the world. Unfortunately, Southeast Asia is the primary sourcing
market for production due to low labor costs, enhanced manufacturing capabilities, diver-
sification, and the many trade agreements - there is a strong business ecosystem, lack of
regulatory compliance, low taxes and duties, and competitive currency practices. Coun-
tries of interest for manufacturing most often include Vietnam, Indonesia, Thailand, the
Philippines, Malaysia, and Singapore.
2.1.1
Importance of Supply Chain sustainability and Scope 1, 2 and 3
The majority of the environmental impact in the consumer sector originates from supply
chains, encompassing energy providers, production, and transportation.
Companies must assess the utilization of natural and human resources throughout the
production process, both in their supply chain and direct operations, to evaluate the impact
on consumer goods production. They must consider a broad range of environmental,
social, and economic factors. Due to the enormous variety of consumer products, these
issues can vary greatly from one product to the next. For instance, the production of LCDs
emits fluorinated greenhouse gases, whereas coffee plantations frequently hire underage
workers to cultivate and harvest coffee beans.
The UN Global Compact encourages businesses to prioritize sustainability from the top
down. If the CEO views the supply chain as an extension of their workforce and commu-
nity, the company can set standards for best practices throughout its supply chain. Se-
lection, training, auditing, and remediation are examples of critical areas. Performance
indicators and reporting systems for sustainability are critical to existing internally in the
company, covering all the consumer products. From this, it is also possible to understand
the hot spots and build a solution based on priorities.
This encourages a broader understanding within an organization of how decisions made
outside of procurement can impact the supply chain. Legal personnel, product develop-
ers, and marketing personnel, for example, can all have an impact. Furthermore, busi-
nesses must consider their entire supply chain, including the suppliers who may face the
most significant challenges.

Scope 1, 2 and 3
The Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Protocol, the most popular international accounting method,
divides greenhouse gas emissions into three categories, or ”Scopes.” [5]
Scope 1 emissions - covers emissions from sources that an organization owns or controls
directly – for example from burning fuel in our fleet of vehicles (if they’re not electrically
powered). [6]
Scope 2 emissions - indirectly caused by that a company y when the energy it purchases
and uses is produced. For example, for our electric fleet vehicles the emissions from the
generation of the electricity they’re powered by would fall into this category.[6]
Scope 3 emissions - encompasses emissions that are not produced by the company itself,
and not the result of activities from assets owned or controlled by them, but by those that
it’s indirectly responsible for, up and down its value chain. For instance, when we buy,
use, and dispose of products from suppliers. It includes all sources, not within the scope
1 and 2 boundaries.[6]
The measurement of Scope 3 emissions has a wide range of advantages. The majority of
greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and cost-saving opportunities for many businesses are
found outside of their own activities. Engaging suppliers and assisting them to implement
sustainability initiatives can be an example of measuring Scope 3 emissions. [6]
2.1.2
Another definition to know: The Triple Bottom Line
The triple bottom line is a business concept that argued companies should commit to
measuring their social and environmental impact in addition to their financial performance,
rather than focusing solely on profit, or the traditional ”bottom line.” Profit, people, and the
planet are the ”three Ps” of sustainability. [7]
Beyond assisting businesses in capitalizing on a growing market for sustainable goods,
adopting sustainable business strategies can be very appealing to investors. According
to Sustainable Business Strategy, evidence is mounting that companies with promising
environmental, social, and governance (ESG) metrics produce superior financial returns.
As a result, when making investment decisions, more investors are focusing on ESG
metrics.
• Profit: The intention is for revenues to benefit the community as a whole, rather than
simply the CEO and shareholders, and to help sustain and empower it.
• People: It’s essential to differentiate between shareholders and stakeholders in a
company. Businesses have always prioritized shareholder value as a measure of
success, which means they work to create value for individuals who own firm shares.
Businesses have switched their attention to generating value for all stakeholders
who are impacted by their actions, including consumers, employees, and community
members, as they have endorsed sustainability more and more.
• Planet: Businesses hold the keys to achieving desired change even though they
have traditionally been the biggest contributors to climate change. Many corpo-
rate leaders are increasingly aware of their obligation to do so. The biggest firms
in the world aren’t the only ones responsible for this effort; practically every com-
pany has the chance to make adjustments that lower their carbon impact. Making
adjustments like sourcing resources from ethical sources, conserving energy, and
optimizing shipping procedures are positive milestones.

The triple bottom line does not, by definition, prioritize societal and environmental effects
over financial success. Instead, by adopting a triple bottom line , sustainable business
practices have paid off for many companies financially. “In many situations, it’s possible to
do the right thing and make money at the same time,” Harvard Business School Professor
Rebecca Henderson says in Sustainable Business Strategy. “Indeed, there’s good reason
to believe that solving the world’s problems presents trillions of dollars worth of economic
opportunity.”
For instance, research by Nielsen found that 48 percent of US consumers would change
their consumption habits to lessen their impact on the environment. In 2018 alone, this
sentiment translated to roughly $128.5 billion in sales of sustainable, fast-moving con-
sumer goods.
2.2
Procurement
The procurement, or purchasing, function includes buying/ acquiring all components and
materials needed by a company, managing the supply, and inbound and outbound logis-
tics management. In other words, purchasing offers a complete system for starting and
managing the flow of components, materials, or products through a supply chain. These
activities involve a lot of time for industrial companies while executing sales orders and
can thus be regarded as an important aspect of the firm’s sustainability. Supplier selec-
tion is significant because it can affect delivery lead times and the purchasing transaction
process (manual/semi-automated/automated) also impacts cycle time.
Finally, depending on the nature of collaborative relationships with suppliers, the pro-
curement cycle time can vary. Strategic purchasing and the firm’s strategic flexibility can
significantly improve supply chain agility. [8] Moreover, a study by the World Economic
Forum found that sustainable projects can decrease costs by 9 to 16%, improve brand
value by 15 to 30%, and increase the revenue of companies by 5 to 20%. [9]
2.2.1
Buyer and supplier characteristics impacting their relationships
The buyer characteristics are the following: (1) supplier management; (2) corporate cul-
ture; (3) stakeholder management; and (4) the level of know-how and expertise. The level
of know-how and expertise is usually dependent on the assessment of the skills level of
purchasing employees on sustainability and sustainability knowledge management. [10]
The concept supplier characteristics covered are (1) corporate culture; (2) know-how and
expertise; and (3) access to resources. The corporate culture includes also the willingness
to participate in the buyers’ sustainability initiatives.
The concept of buyer-supplier relationships is usually in terms of (1) power and depen-
dency; (2) trust and commitment; (3) information exchange and communication; (4) ge-
ographical distance and (5) code of conduct. Moreover, sustainability measurements
are starting to become included in the relationship such as sustainability reporting that
would include: life cycle assessment, material, and part purchasing, manufacturing im-
pact, health, and safety. [10]

2.2.2
The importance of procurement and its goal
The role of procurement has gained widespread recognition in recent years as a crucial
task that can manage the majority of a company’s spending. As a result, this function
has received a lot more attention, and SCs become shorter. It used to be seen of as
little more than an administrative task, buying supplies and parts as needed and tt is
now acknowledged as a crucial management function in and of itself - the changes in
procurement patterns have supported the trend, having no doubt that procurement is
treated now as a role of senior management.
Bearing this in mind, its main goal is to ensure that a business has a steady supply of
commodities (raw materials or components, etc.), making sure to keep the stocks low,
consider policies of inventory, finding suitable suppliers, and develop beneficial relation-
ships, as well as working closely with customer services and the marketing department,
understanding the requirement of customers.
2.2.3
Monitoring suppliers performance and E-procurement
To ensure the continuation and fulfillment of criteria and materials specifications, many
businesses keep a tight eye on their suppliers, which is referred to as ”supply rating”
or ”vendor rating.” The majority of the time, there are quite sophisticated measures for
every facet of supplier performance and typically, this is done informally through subjective
review.
One common strategy or method used by businesses does the verification that the provider
satisfies an accepted specification or standard in the areas by using a checklist of crucial
elements. The checklist may include questions such as: Does the supplier deliver on
time? Is the supplier financially secure? Is there technical support? Is the material quality
high enough? Is the pricing reasonable? The customer must search for new suppliers or
sources if the supplier does not satisfy any needs. The goal is to closely monitor supplier
performance, pinpoint areas that require improvement, and come to an agreement on the
most effective means of making these adjustments rather than actually replacing a current
supplier. A business should only start looking for new suppliers as a last resort.
E-procurement began in the 1980s once Electronic Data Interchange developed (EDI).
A decade later, EDI advancements made it possible for businesses to create online ven-
dor catalogs. E-procurement now includes everything from contract management to elec-
tronic orders and payments to supplier evaluation and selection. Therefore, E-procurement
is the process of requisitioning, ordering, and purchasing goods and services online, and
it is a business-to-business process. [11]
Through the use of a centralized platform, the process connects multiple entities and pro-
cesses. One of the most crucial elements of e-procurement is vendor/supplier manage-
ment, supplier relationship management, and supplier information management. Other
key components are e-sourcing, e-tendering, e-auctioning, and e-ordering.
Potential difficulties can arise when implementing an e-procurement program, especially
when it comes to installing and integrating the software with other enterprise back-end
systems. Another challenge is teaching employees how to utilize it. In order to achieve
a successful transition to the new online system, businesses must also collaborate with
their suppliers.

2.2.4
Procurement and Circular Economy
Procurement plays an important role in CE (Circular Economy) based operations as sup-
plier selection, strategic supplier partnerships, green certifications, and green processes
adopted by suppliers are all activities that enable the supplier to support the Sustain-
able Development goals of a firm. The success of a firm practicing re-manufacturing and
recycling-based operations is entirely dependent on how the firm manages procurement
and corresponding logistics flows. Managing resources efficiently is a crucial element in
managing supply chains based on closed-loop principles.[8]
Green procurement and its impacts: economic and environmental
Rao and Holt (2005) have shown that the introduction of green procurement management
practices in an enterprise leads to an increase in economic performance. [12] Research
has also shown that green procurement has a direct impact on the cost-effectiveness of an
enterprise since such an enterprise pays more attention to developing its own capacity to
increase efficiency (Song et al., 2017). Enterprise executives should develop short-term
and long-term green procurement measures (Malviya, 2017).
At the same time, Zhu and Sarkis (2004) emphasize that in a globalized economy, busi-
nesses need to pay attention to green procurement, which contributes to improving their
environmental performance. By designing and implementing green procurement, busi-
nesses have a positive impact on the environment (Eltayeb et al., 2011; Al-Sheyadi et al.,
2019). To address the environmental problems facing modern society, Noor et al. (2018)
propose to introduce eco-innovations in enterprises, such as green purchasing manage-
ment. Baofeng et al. (2019) point out in their work that green procurement has a positive
effect on the environmental performance of the enterprise.
2.2.5
Sustainable Procurement
All in all, Sustainable Procurement integrates specifications, requirements and criteria that
are compatible with the protection of the environment and society as a whole. It encom-
passes many issues beyond, for example, child labor or the use of harmful chemicals that
can affect people or the environment.
Companies can protect their brand reputation by using sustainable procurement practices,
which involve the development of strong risk management. This makes it possible for
businesses to more accurately identify problematic suppliers and deal with supply chain
weaknesses that could lead to scandals and negative publicity. Finally, because it pushes
businesses to create more inventive and sustainable products, sustainable procurement
creates chances for brand distinctiveness.
Sustainable sourcing methods are already being widely used, which benefits business
growth. According to a World Economic Forum study, implementing such strategies can
boost overall brand value by up to 30 % while boosting revenue by 5-20% and supply
chain costs by 9-16%. Many purchasers are increasingly aware of the limitations of do-it-
yourself and self-declared data collecting as due diligence law becomes more strict and
broadens in coverage across worldwide areas and categories.
According to Ecovadis, the most effective sustainable procurement approaches go be-
yond the simple data collection and basic verification offered by most SAQs (self-assessment
questionnaires). The investment to help suppliers improve their performance is crucial for

Benefits

Challenges

• Lack of expertise and process – 42%

suppliers is essential to overcome these obstacles. [14]

Supplier engagement - challenges

3.1
The Grounded Theory
Grounded theory is a research methodology in the social sciences that involves the con-
struction of a theory that is grounded in data that has been collected and analyzed. It is
a systematic approach to research that aims to develop a theory that explains patterns in
the data and can be used to make predictions about future events or behaviors. This the-
ory was developed in the 1950s by the sociologists’ Barney Glaser and Anselm Strauss,
who were interested in developing a systematic approach to studying social phenomena
that were based on empirical data.
In grounded theory, the researcher begins by collecting and analyzing data from a variety
of sources, such as interviews, observations, or documents. As the data is analyzed, the
researcher develops codes, categories, and themes that emerge from the data. These
codes, categories, and themes are used to construct a theory that explains the patterns
and relationships in the data.
The goal of grounded theory is to develop a theory that is grounded in the data and is
relevant to the research question being studied. It is an iterative process that involves the
constant testing and revision of the theory as new data is collected and analyzed.
This method was chosen since Grounded theory is often used in qualitative research.
Nonetheless, it can also be used in quantitative research or in combination with other
research methods. [37]

Frameworks to use
There are several frameworks that are commonly used in grounded theory research.
These frameworks provide a structured approach for collecting and analyzing data and
developing a theory that is grounded in the data.
One of the most well-known frameworks for grounded theory is the constant comparative
method. This method involves comparing each new piece of data to previously collected
data in order to identify similarities and differences. This allows the researcher to identify
patterns and relationships in the data and to develop a theory that explains these patterns.

It is important to note that grounded theory is an iterative process, and researchers may

3.2
Design Thinking
Design thinking is a methodology or process for creatively and systematically solving prob-
lems. It is a way of thinking that is centered on the needs of people and involves coming
up with solutions that are both functional and desirable. Design thinking can be applied
to a wide range of fields, including product design, service design, and organizational
design. It typically involves a series of steps that include empathy, definition, ideation,
prototyping, and testing. The goal of design thinking is to create innovative solutions that
are both practical and visually appealing.
The importance of using Design thinking for research is because it helps ensure that the
research is focused on the needs and challenges of the people who will be using the
research findings. By starting with empathy and a deep understanding of the users and
their needs, design thinking helps researchers to identify and define important problems
or challenges. It then encourages them to generate a wide range of ideas for addressing
these problems and to prototype and test potential solutions in order to refine and improve
them. [38]
There are many different approaches to applying this methodology, but a common process
includes the following steps:
• Empathize: Gain a deep understanding of the people you are designing for, their
needs, and their challenges. This may involve conducting user research, such as
interviews or observations, to gather data about their experiences and perspectives.
• Define: Clearly define the problem or challenge you are trying to solve. This step
may involve synthesizing the insights you gathered in the empathy phase, and iden-
tifying key themes or areas of focus.
• Ideate: Generate a wide range of ideas for addressing the problem or challenge.
This step is about quantity rather than quality, and it’s important to be open to all
ideas, no matter how seemingly unconventional.
• Prototype: Select the most promising ideas and create rough, low-fidelity prototypes
to test and refine.
• Test: Gather feedback on the prototypes and use it to iterate and improve the de-
sign. This step may involve conducting user testing or collecting data through other
means.
• Implement: Finalize the design and put it into practice.

cial system as a result of this dissemination. When someone adopts, they do something
that is different from what they previously did (i.e., purchase or use a new product, acquire
and perform a new behavior, etc.). Adoption depends on a person’s ability to see an idea,
behavior, or product as novel or inventive. This allows for the possibility of dissemination.

6.1.3
Cultural differences and acceptance
Cultural differences can present a number of challenges when it comes to understanding
and promoting sustainability. First of all, different cultures may have different values and
priorities concerning environmental and social issues, which can make it difficult to find
common ground and agree on the best course of action. Also, some cultures may be more
individualistic, while others may be more collectivistic - this can affect how people perceive
their responsibility for the environment and how they are willing to make sacrifices for the
greater good. [48]
Saying this, in order for Saas companies to be agile and run fast they need to make sure
either the suppliers from their clients are culturally similar or they need to develop an agile
system that works for different locations/ cultures. For instance, a lot of fashion industries
have their suppliers in China and if they used climate software to track suppliers’ data,
most likely they would need translators.
Some cultures are of course more resistant to change and therefore can make it difficult
to communicate information about sustainability and engage people in discussions about
environmental and social issues. It can also shape how people perceive and respond to
environmental problems. [49]
Overall, this contributes to Industries’ concerns related to sustainability investment and im-
plementation. Several have suppliers from different areas around the world with different
cultures. How can they guarantee the same level of education around all their suppliers?
One example is one of the interviewees, a procurement professional in a Chocolate con-
fectionery producer, that has some cocoa farms in Venezuela. While working together
with Rainforest Alliance, this organization stated that they cannot assure the education
level and that requirements can be the same in this country. Therefore, it is a challenge
for companies with suppliers worldwide to compromise on a Saas tool for sustainability, if
it is known beforehand that the cultural difference in each country has a significant impact.
The importance of a strategy behind Expansion
Having a good and structured strategy behind the expansion of a SaaS company is impor-
tant when it comes to cultural differences. While expanding to a new market it is important
to understand who are the majority of the suppliers and where are they from. This helps
the company identify the different needs depending on the country, in terms of regulations
or localization (mitigating the language barriers).
A well-designed expansion strategy outlines specific actions and goals to achieve sus-
tainable growth and can help a SaaS company to manage and mitigate risks, such as
entering new markets or developing new products. Moreover, the requirements when it
comes to sustainability from area to area Overall, a clear and well-executed expansion
strategy can be critical to the long-term success of a SaaS company.

Limitation: Complexity and time-consuming to teach a whole organi-

Some of the interviews answered the difficulty of implementing a new system into an
organization. There are a few reasons that can be demonstrated below.
First of all, employees might have different skill levels. People in an organization have
varying levels of technical expertise, making it difficult to create a one-size-fits-all training
program. Secondly, there is probably resistance to change: as mentioned in chapter
6.1.3, some people are resistant to learning new tools (due to cultural differences and
education or not) especially if people are comfortable with their current methods.
Moreover, the time is limited. Some employees may have limited time to devote to training,
which can make it difficult to provide adequate instruction; or don’t prioritize learning new
tools/systems versus doing the normal tasks.
Lastly, there is a need for integration with existing systems and data migration. SaaS
tools may need to be integrated with other systems already in use within the organiza-
tion (through API or OCPI integration for instance), and this can be complex and time-
consuming. Migrating data from old systems to new ones is known to be a complex and
time-consuming task.

to implement the tool better and in a more suitable way. Providing clear communication
about the benefits of the tools is crucial, as well as proof of how this will improve their
work processes. This can be done through the platform (where Responisbly could share

Limitations of the study due to Qualitative Research
Qualitative research can provide valuable insights and perspectives. While direct inter-
views are super relevant for research and studies, at the same time there are several lim-
itations that should be considered when conducting and interpreting qualitative research.
One major limitation is subjectivity. Because qualitative research relies on the researcher’s
interpretation and analysis of the data, there is a risk of bias and subjectivity in the find-
ings. This can make it difficult to replicate the study or generalize the results to other
situations. Nonetheless, all the interviews from this study were recorded and transcribed
with a feature of Google Meets, which makes the data more accurate and precise.
Another limitation of qualitative research is the small sample size. Unlike quantitative re-
search, which often involves large samples, qualitative research typically involves study-
ing a small number of individuals or groups. In this study case, only 8 different companies
were part of the case and other 6 people were interviewed, so overall 14 cases, and all of
them from Nordic Companies based in Denmark. This can make a generalization of the
findings and make it difficult to draw conclusions about the larger population.
Conducting qualitative research can also be time-consuming and resource-intensive. Re-
searchers may spend months or even years collecting and analyzing data, which can be
challenging for researchers with limited time and resources.
The validity and reliability of qualitative research can also be a limitation. The lack of
standardized methods for data collection or analysis makes it difficult to assess the validity
and reliability of the findings. This can make it difficult to compare results across studies.
Finally, data analysis in qualitative research is complex and time-consuming. [52] [53].

6.4.1
A B2B2B business model
Another suggestion given to Responsibly was to build a B2B2B business model. A B2B2B
(business-to-business-to-business) business is a company that sells products or services
to other businesses, which in turn sell those products or services to their own customers.
In this case, Responsibly sells its service to industries with Procurement Professionals,
which could sell Responsibly Platform to all of their suppliers. The benefits of doing this
are the spread and expansion of one single platform through several different entities just
connecting with one entity - and therefore they could increase its revenue potential by
reaching to a larger customer base. For instance, if the Procurement manager of coffee
beans in Nestlé would share the Responsibly platform with its clients, maybe could reach
100 different coffee bean producers. The good part of this is that the ”Supplier platform”
could connect with the ”Procurement platforms” and facilitate the gathering data process.
Beyond this, B2B2B companies can form long-term partnerships with other businesses,
which can lead to a more stable and predictable revenue stream and also, can often

negotiate better prices and terms with suppliers, which can lead to cost savings that can
be passed on to customers.[54]

6.5
Future Research
In this chapter, the potential future research directions will be discussed, that can be pur-
sued based on the findings and contributions of the study.
First, this study highlights a few interviews, from Denmark industries. To get better results
and findings would be relevant to explore other sectors, such as the Pharmaceutical in-
dustry or fashion industry, as well as get a better understanding of who is the department
that makes the changes and implementations in the company. Sometimes it can be the IT
department, and sometimes is the sustainability department that drives the ambitious or
finance department to include the end reporting for investors. Sustainability is a multidis-
ciplinary issue that encompasses economic, social, and environmental dimensions and
so specializing in only one sector or department may limit the potential for innovation and
identification of new solutions. The broader the study the easier to identify new opportuni-
ties and collaborate for cross-disciplinary solutions, that can be more effective in the long
term. Therefore, it is crucial to understand who the leader driving the change and why.
Secondly, to complement this, having an understanding of how it works outside of Den-
mark and which studies have been done on other continents would be really relevant.
Since a lot of suppliers are located in Asia, South America, or Middle east, it is important
to do an investigation on that area of the globe. With that, different cultural perspectives
of analyses are brought together. For example, the effects of deforestation in one con-
tinent can have impacts on the climate change of another continent. Moreover, deep
research on the contributions and effects of suppliers’ point of view would be beneficial
and interesting to be analyzed. What do they feel when they are faced with environmental
questionnaires? How difficult and complex is it to change processes toward sustainability
innovation?
Thirdly, this study was deeply focused on Responsibly platform. Would be advantageous
to explore other platforms for sustainability in depth as well as other projects for
sustainable procurement, without the use of a platform. This can be beneficial for com-
parison and benchmarking, understanding other platforms’ features and capabilities, and
also market positioning.
Finally, our study also highlights the importance of considering the economic and social
impact of sustainable software development practices. Future research could investigate
the extent to which sustainable software development practices have a positive impact on
the economy and society, and how these impacts can be measured and communicated
to stakeholders.

7
Conclusion
To conclude, this work presents a qualitative exploratory research on software develop-
ment and sustainable procurement. The study has shown the impact of new technologies
and digital transformations in the context of sustainable purchase decisions, specifically
with Responsibly as a case study. Green sourcing is not only a disruptive topic for supply
chains, it also represents changes in the landscape of software and IT systems. By mak-
ing use of software tools, procurement professionals can improve critical decision-making
while accelerating the green transition. Tools that improve current data reliability related
to sustainability, can accelerate the sustainability agenda as it provides improved insights
and data for decision-makers such as investors.
The studies have been performed through structured interviews of procurement profes-
sionals in the industry ecosystem, software professionals, and consultants - providing
valuable insights into the relationship between Sustainability and Sourcing.
With regard to the research questions posed in this thesis, the following summarized
conclusions can be drawn:
• Research question 1:What is Responsibly’s business model and their competi-
tion? What does the market for software development in sustainable procure-
ment look like?
As mentioned in the Case Study chapter, Responsibly’s goal is to provide sustain-
ability and procurement professionals with a supplier data platform for responsible
procurement, with available data from across a wide variety of data providers, and
facilitate the sourcing process done with responsibility. Apart from Responsibly plat-
form, there are a few other SaaS solutions related to Sustainable Sourcing such as
EcoVadis and Integrity Next, as referred to in the first chapter. Apart from scoring
cards and ratings, there is also carbon accountability software, more adapted to CO2
reduction. These tools are not direct competitors, but still in the field of sustainable
sourcing and software.
• Research question 2:What drivers are there in the industries to engage in Sus-
tainable Procurement?
Some of the drivers identified for industries to engage in Sustainable Procurement
are: new regulation, such as CSRD, is coming up and industries need to keep in-
novative and updated to new requirements. Sustainability is really important for
investors and becoming relevant for customers. All in all, the biggest drivers at this
moment are the measuring of carbon emitted in order to reduce it, and the flip of
energy consumption towards renewable energy.
• Research question 3:What is the nature and extent of the Sustainable Procure-
ment implementation in the supply chain through Responsibly platform?
In order to implement SP software the customers need to trust and believe in its
service. Beyond that, the implementation can be made through an API connection
between the Responsibly platform and an ERP system, or even by submitting Excel
or data sheets. Moreover, each procurement professional has the chance to have
onboarding and support to proceed with its implementation.

• Research question 4:What would make Responsibly better prepared when en-
gaging with its clients? Which new trending technologies could be incorpo-
rated into Procurement software to improve Procurement Sustainability?
As mentioned in the Discussion chapter, some recommendations were given to Re-
sponsibly in order to engage better with its clients. There are some new trending
technologies out there that have been applied in SaaS companies, such as de-
veloping an Academic tool, a Help center, offering interactive dashboards and vi-
sualizations for clients, or providing suppliers with the chance to track and trace
products with RFID codes (implementing blockchain technologies). Incorporating
AI/ Machine Learning technologies is more and more used to trace supply chain
transparency. Moreover, making use of social media is highly relevant to increase
visibility and brand awareness for generating leads.
• Research question 5: How can Responsibly’s smart data brain and the possi-
bility of reusing answers help overcome the issues of supplier engagement?
Understanding how Responsibly’s smart data brain can be used fully, was a complex
question to answer through the interviews given the early mature market. Nonethe-
less, some ideas were given into how to make good use of this data, such as provid-
ing it to other entities that share the same suppliers or sustainability organizations
that want to know suppliers’ performances for sustainability. The business of sell-
ing data to other entities is becoming a new trend, but also difficult to regulate and
understand who is the data owner.
This thesis has provided insights into the importance of sustainable procurement and how
it can be effectively managed through software tools, such as Responsibly. It also high-
lights how the tool helps accelerate the process of gathering data and improve decision-
making related to procurement for the companies using the platform. The potential for
software technologies is also highlighted, and how it can enable companies to take a more
proactive and data-driven approach to sustainability in their supply chains, ultimately con-
tributing to positive change. Finally, this thesis should serve as an inspiration for software
solution organizations to develop strategies for attracting and retaining customers, and
ultimately improving their sustainability performance.
