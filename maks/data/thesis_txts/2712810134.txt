Abstract
For modern and large wind turbines, relied on for power generation, damage is both in-
evitable and hugely adverse. On account of their complex operational conditions and possible
inaccessibility, vibration-based damage detection poses an opportunity for improvement in
operation. Through reliable damage detection, operational efficiency is increased and crit-
ical failure may be avoided. In this thesis, this prospect is considered by employing both
experimental and operational data.
As a damage detection tool in an output-only setting, the Random Decrement technique
and related parameters were considered. Its performance and applicability was evaluated and
discussed through the use of synthetic data. An experimental setup featuring a cantilever
beam capable of crude damage imitation was introduced. Also to be analysed, faults for
2 separate offshore wind turbines were described. For the data from operational turbines,
various pre-processing steps were suggested.
For the experimental setup, modelled damage past a certain threshold was detectable
using frequency-related damage indicators. Nonlinearity and damping-related damage in-
dicators were unable to do so. For one operational case, where the blade root joint failed
critically, the same frequency-related indicators successfully signalled damage. A frequency
estimator clearly indicated damage 40 days prior to failure.
A more involved frequency-
related indicator intermittently implied damage as early as 110 days prior. Again, nonlinear-
ity and damping-related indicators failed. For another operational case, where blade shear
web debonding was observed, all damage indicators failed in revealing damage.

Introduction
Vibration-based damage detection for an operational wind turbine generator (WTG) poses
a potentially huge benefit in terms of performance and efficiency, if such could be carried out
reliably. In this thesis work, this prospect is investigated analytically, experimentally and
using operational data, with consideration for damping, frequency and nonlinearity indicators
as potential damage sensitive parameters usable for damage detection.
The wind energy sector is growing in both absolutes and its share on a global scale,
amounting to 6.15% of global electricity production in 2020 [1]. With this sheer market size
and growth, any advances in the field of wind energy may hold significant economical and en-
vironmental value. For both already installed and future WTGs, operation and maintenance
comprise a significant part of efficiency and competitiveness. Parts associated with compar-
atively large risk in terms of production loss (i.e. both high frequency and impact) include
major mechanical components such as gears, blades and support structure [2]. As these parts
comprise large elements of the mechanical structure of a WTG, their deterioration may also
affect the dynamics and subsequently the dynamic response of the WTG. If this change in
response may be detected reliably and in a timely manner, further part damage, collateral
damage and production loss may be mitigated or avoided entirely. Furthermore, reliable
monitoring procedures may reduce the extent to which preventive inspection, maintenance
and remedial action are necessary. For assets installed in remote locations that require com-
prehensive measures for access, such as far-offshore WTGs requiring large service vessels, this
potentially poses a vital advantage. These considerations hold true for many constructions
and monitoring of vibrations is by no means a revolutionary idea. What does separate an
operational WTG from many other structures is the inherent inability to observe it under
controllable conditions, even temporarily. Moreover, the environmental inputs (e.g. wind and
waves) to a WTG are not fully deterministic and often only known in terms of time-averaged
parameters. The prevalence, potential and growth of operational WTGs and their somewhat
special case in terms of monitoring, seen from the perspective of structural dynamics, makes
structural damage monitoring of them both a justifiable and interesting field of research.
In the following paragraphs, a concise and non-exhaustive review of literature relevant to
the case of vibration-based detection of structural damage to operating WTGs is presented.
P´erez et al. have mapped the failures for WTGs, both in terms of frequency and hours
lost per failure, distributed according to the component failing [2]. Confined to mechanical
components, blades and their pitching system were the most frequent failing component with
just over 0.25 failures per year per turbine. After this, gears and yaw systems came next with
approximately 0.13 failures per year per turbine. Combining these rates with the expected
downtime yields the expected hours of production loss per turbine per year, where blades and
gears comprise the most impactful failures. The WTG blades were also attributed 24.90% of
the component cost for a typical 2 MW WTG (at time of publication, 2013). Based on this
impact of WTG blades on production and their role as both load-generating and -bearing,
investigating damage detection for them seems both valuable and viable. For a gearbox,
if present in the WTG, the associated failure modes and relevant mechanical factors, such
as gyroscopic effects, lubrication and drivetrain backlash, are so dissimilar to the remainder
of the WTG that the damage detection approaches relevant here are not deemed effective
elsewhere. Hence, this is not actively pursued. Sørensen et al. [3] characterised some of the

potential damage types polymer matrix composite blades could be subject to. This includes
failure of the adhesive joints between blade sub-components, between the blade skin and
structural components, gelcoat cracks, delamination of laminates and splitting of individual
fibres in laminates. Although these failure modes may sound very dissimilar at first (due to
the damage locations and components involved), they may involve identical elements such
as locally decreased stiffness and increased energy dissipation. From a structural dynamics
point of view, this may entail seemingly dissimilar damage types being detectable using
similar approaches.
Hassani et al. [4] compared the modal quantities that could potentially be utilised for
damage detection in composite structures. For failure modes listed by Sørensen et al. [3],
natural frequency and damping measures are candidates for damage detection. Using natural
frequency is deemed cost-effective and convenient as little access is required for sensor place-
ment, but damage localisation is not possible and the method is sensitive to environmental
and operational variations. Damping is deemed sensitive to even small cracks, but also to
environmental conditions although noise-sensitivity is lesser compared to natural frequency
methods. Material stiffness was found to be strongly influenced by fibre cracks, temperature
and moisture, whilst damping is moderately influenced by matrix- and fibre-cracks, delami-
nation, temperature, moisture and strongly influenced by dirt. For damage detection using
ambient vibration data, damping was found to be the parameter most sensitive to damage.
Khoshmanesh et al. investigated the damping and stiffness properties of a thick adhesive
joint test specimen, subject to tension fatigue test [5, 6]. Through various states of damage
before failure, the specimen stiffness and loss factor was evaluated. The average reduction of
stiffness was 8.6% before failure whilst the increase in loss factor at this state was 111.4%.
The increase in loss factor with respect to number of load cycles was seen to be progressive.
This potentially much greater effect of fatigue damage on damping makes it a very enticing
parameter for damage detection.
For composite structures, Hassani et al. [4] outlines damage detection methods, some of
which are relevant to operational WTGs. Mode shape measurements may be used in a num-
ber of ways, if obtainable. The modal assurance criterion may be used to quantify and localise
damage, through lack of orthogonality in mode shapes measured before and after structural
integrity loss. Modal curvature, the second derivative of the mode shape, employs the connec-
tion between curvature and flexural stiffness for a high sensitivity to damage. However, this
necessitates spatially dense measurements. This mode shape curvature method is deemed
very mature and with excellent potential, but troublesome in application. If several mode
shapes, and subsequently their curvatures, could be estimated then one could potentially
detect and even localise damage. Through concurrent analysis of several modes, each with
varying mode shapes and nodal points, certain faults may be ruled out. Despite its potential
promise, the mode shape curvature method is excluded from analysis in this thesis work due
its immediate inapplicability, courtesy of sensor requirements. The strain energy associated
with a mode shape may also indicate damage, if it compromises the ability of the structure to
store energy [7]. In contrast to the mode shape based damage detection, (structure-) global
parameters may also be employed for damage detection, possibly alleviating the necessity
for numerous and spatially distributed sensors. Natural frequencies may decrease due to
lessened stiffness caused by damage, thus allowing for estimated natural frequencies to infer
damage [4]. Although not readily applicable due to the required sensors and potential noise
==PAGE:13==

contamination from surroundings, acoustic emission from transient elastic waves may be de-
tected and provide qualitative damage detection [8, 4]. Cao et al. [9] presents damping-based
damage detection techniques for reinforced concrete and fibre-reinforced composites, these
two types being the most common to this field of research. For fibre-reinforced composites,
the authors report that damping has by itself shown considerable potential for damage detec-
tion, while combination of it with e.g. modal strain energy or natural frequencies may reveal
damage location additionally. For reinforced concrete, damping of beam structures subject to
corrosion damage of varying degree was estimated using the logarithmic decrement measure
of damping [10]. For damage entailing a change of 2.6% in natural frequency, a change of
59% in modal damping ratio was observed. Using the curve fitting method, damage was seen
to both introduce linear and nonlinear damping. In [11], Frizzarin et al. proposed a damp-
ing model combining viscous and Coulomb damping. For damage detection in a large-scale
reinforced concrete bridge model, the friction model was applied in conjunction with the
Random Decrement (RD) method for analysis of ambient vibration response. The method
successfully detected the damage through reduction of viscous damping and simultaneous
emergence and increase of friction damping as damage occurred and progressed. For the free
decay of a single degree of freedom (DOF) oscillator, the combination of this damping model
and the RD method also yielded good results.
Another method applicable for damage detection is the Holder exponent (or Lipschitz
exponent) [7]. Contrary to previously mentioned methods, this does not rely on a modal
parameter. The Holder exponent is a time-domain applicable technique for identification of
discontinuities in a system, which may be reflected in its response. For instance, if an oscilla-
tor has a spatially discontinuous stiffness term (corresponding to e.g. a rattle or a crack), then
a response to a continuous and instationary forcing may still exhibit discontinuities in velocity
with respect to time. Another way of considering the Holder exponent, is that it quantifies
to what order a signal is differentiable. As a measured signal will always be discrete and
thus discontinuous to some degree, the success of this technique will likely depend on sensor
capabilities. If such discontinuities stem from e.g. a crack opening and closing, the exponent
may be employed for damage detection. Farrar and Worden [7] demonstrated the application
of this in continuation of the wavelet transform on a system with a rattle. Hong et al. [12] did
similar for detection of a notch in an Euler–Bernoulli beam. The non-modal nature of the
Holder exponent makes it valuable in terms of diversifying the process of damage detection.
It is interesting due to its distinct non-modal nature, but is omitted in analysis in favour of
methods deemed more mature. As an alternative to the wavelet spectrum, empirical mode
decomposition may be used in conjunction with a Hilbert-transformation for decomposition
of a signal in the time-frequency domain [13]. This may help to overcome the requirements
of stationarity and linearity inherent to the Fourier-transform.
Vesterholm et al. [14] proposed the RD technique for detection and characterisation of
nonlinearities in an output-only setting. For a single-DOF stick-slip system, the method was
proven using both synthetic and experimental data. Through introduction of 2 scalar criteria
sensitive to nonlinearity in frequency and decay, respectively, wrt. amplitude, interpretation
of results was simplified and improved. The RD’s applicability on scalar data as well as its
potential for quantifying both nonlinearity and damping are deemed to be great strengths and
make it a strong contender. Yang et al. [15] proposed a Kalman filter modified for unknown
input forces to a system, for system identification and damage detection in a structural

setting. For a numerically simulated 3-DOF Duffing system, satisfactory results were found
for sudden changes in both stiffness and/or damping.
For denoising of measured signals, with regard to application in structural health monitor-
ing, Ravizza et al. [16] compared methods for noise reduction, specifically the discrete wavelet

For operational analysis of WTGs, Avenda˜no-Valencia et al. [17, 18] proposed a linear
parameter varying vector autoregressive model for encompassment of variations due to op-
erational parameters such as temperature, wind speed and turbulence intensity. The model
was applied for 6 (virtual) accelerometers in aeroelastic simulation and 11 accelerometers on
an operational WTG blade. Subsequently, the authors were able to determine the influence
from these operational parameters on modal characteristics as well as isolate and normalise
these to reduce uncertainty and improve sensitivity in damage detection. Through eigen-
value analysis of obtained model matrices, instantaneous mode shapes, natural frequencies
and damping ratios can be estimated. This method and in particular the comprehensive and
systematic consideration for operational parameters is deemed very capable. Ultimately, the
method and its application is however judged too complex and immature to fit within the
scope of this thesis work.
This thesis work will investigate the feasibility, challenges and potential efficacy of in-
operation damage detection of WTG blades. The tool for this analysis is the RD method,
which will be relied on for estimation of modal parameters, nonlinearity and changes to
a system. This method will be employed for both experimental and operational data to
investigate its capability in more or less controlled and complex settings. For the operational
case, the supervisory control and data acquisition (SCADA) data available for the WTGs is
employed. For a more profound understanding of final results, the RD method itself is also
investigated as a damage detection scheme. For this, synthetic data from a simpler model is
favoured for the level of control possible.
For the initial investigation of the RD technique for damage detection, a beam model
employing relatively crude damage modelling is proposed. The potential damage indicators
provided by the RD method are then investigated in terms of convergence, after which their
capabilities and variability in a damage detection setting are benchmarked. Furthermore,
some novel RD parameters are suggested for damage detection. For inclusion of far more
effects potentially present for a WTG, an experimental setup resembling a blade is then
introduced. This setup incorporates a possibility for imitating a varying degree of damage.
The suggested damage detection scheme is then applied for both experimental and operational
data. For the operational case, this includes SCADA data for 2 separate and different faults
as well as control WTGs for investigation of potential false positives.
This report commences with the chosen analysis tools and elements, which are individually
presented, discussed and subsequently validated. Purely as recapitulation, the relevant RD
elements are presented in Section 2. The analytical beam model, including failure modes and
their modelling, is presented in Section 3. The proposed damage indicators are investigated
for convergence and performance in Section 4. The experimental setup will then be covered

Background: Random Decrement Technique
This section will briefly introduce the RD technique in the context of application for this
thesis work.
What follows is by no means a comprehensive or exhaustive review of the
RD technique and its developments. For that, the reader is referred to literature; Cole is
accredited with the introduction of RD [19], after which much research and development has
been seen. Vandiver et al. [20] provided a sound mathematical background for a linear time-
invariant system subject to zero-mean, stationary and Gaussian random excitation, proving
the Random Decrement signature (RDS) to be proportional to the autocorrelation of the
output. In more recent years, Vesterholm [21] advanced the use of RD on nonlinear systems
for detecting and characterising the nonlinearity. In the latter, a more detailed historical
review of the technique and its development may also be found. For its completeness, many
illustrations and reader-friendliness, this author recommends [21] for further recapitulation
of the core concepts.
As this presentation of the RD technique only concerns elements relevant to the damage
detection procedures to be introduced, some potentially powerful analysis tools that are
impaired by their dependence on analyst input are omitted.
This includes time-domain
methods used on RDSs, such as e.g. the Ibrahim Time Domain, discussed in [22].
The

3.2
Damage modelling
In Section 3.1, the model introduced is a completely customary and linear model for vibra-
tions of an axially uniform cantilever beam. This will now be modified such that it may
exhibit some of the effects that damage to the beam (or the WTG blade it represents) could
potentially produce. Some challenges and shortcomings of this prospect are considered here.
By the virtue of (3.14) and the MSE carried out, the vibrations of the beam, in the
physical domain, are completely partitioned and represented in a set of R modes. For this
linear model, modal superposition applies, resulting in each mode being decoupled from
the other R −1 modes.
This is inherently problematic for the modelling of damage, as
damage exists in the physical domain and may render the MSE inaccurate or invalid. An
example hereof: A crack breathing under vibration (i.e. opening and closing) will, among
other effects, entail the flexural stiffness changing discontinuously. Such effects should affect
all modes simultaneously, but if all modes are modelled independently, this inherently does
not occur.
However, a key consideration to be kept in mind for this discussion, is the purpose of this
damaged model; This model is to provide synthetic data such that damage indicators can
be evaluated and investigated in completely controllable conditions. Figuratively speaking,
this model is merely a practice target for the tools to come.
Its quality has no impact
on the eventual analysis of operational data, which significantly reduces the demands for
its accuracy.
What is still deemed relevant is that the damage model can provide data
for nonlinear oscillators with characteristics not too dissimilar to what may be seen for real
damage. Revised models that attempt compensation for this were considered. Although some
alleviation was thought possible, the accompanying adverse effects were ultimately deemed
worse than the benefits. Hence, the non-ideal choice of modal-based damage modelling was
taken. In literature, a few examples of similar nature as this approach was found, e.g. [28].

4.1
Convergence of Random Decrement Signatures and Charac-
terisations
As discussed in Section 2.1, application of the RD technique inevitably involves approximat-
ing the true RDS using an ensemble average. This naturally prompts the imperative question
of how many averages should be taken, i.e. when is the signature estimate sufficiently con-
verged. This seemingly very simple question leads to several considerations which will be
addressed in this section.
Firstly, one must decide what the RDSs are to be used for. It may be that for visual
inspection of RDSs, relatively few averages could suffice. On the other hand, if convergence is
measured using the difference between an RDS and its true values (using e.g. the maximum or
RMS value), in the limit of infinite samples, then more samples may be necessary, depending
on the criteria for convergence. To add to this complexity, post-processing type parameters
of RDSs such as RAIC, SIC and NORI may be more or less sensitive to lack of convergence.
Secondly, the manner in which the samples are extracted from the signal can also impact
the number of samples required. As a simple example consider RDSs obtained using an equal
number of samples for two different signals, both from a band-type TC such as PP. One
signal, sampled at a sensible frequency, spans a vast number of eigenfrequency periods and
has no overlapping samples. Another signal, sampled far faster than necessary, spans few
eigenfrequency periods and samples greatly overlap. RDSs for the first signal are very likely
more converged than those for the second signal. Hence, one must consider both number of
samples, sampling frequency and/or overlapping of samples when tracking convergence, as
also discussed by Vesterholm [21].
In literature, some discussion of this issue can be found.
Wu et al. [27] investigated
a numerical model for a single-DOF system sampled at 78.5 samples per eigenfrequency
period, quantifying the error as the difference between the RDS and the true decay for
corresponding initial conditions. They found that a record length of greater than 2000 natural
periods ensured a stable RDS. Chang [30] estimated damping and natural frequencies for a
modelled 2-DOF system using curve fitting methods. Natural frequency estimates were found
to require fewer samples than damping estimates and using 2000 samples was recommended.
Vesterholm [21] investigated the convergence for both a linear and Duffing oscillator. It was
found that for certain TCs, RDSs may converge slower for a nonlinear system.
During the analysis for this thesis work, preliminary results suggested that some conver-
gence issues may have been present, despite compliance with the suggestions from literature.

4.2.3
Natural Frequency Estimator
All RDS characterisations introduced until now have been relative, in the sense that they
have attempted to detect and characterise difference(s) in RDSs, but not to use them for
direct estimates of some parameter for the system in question. For damage detection, such
estimates were found to be useful.

Extraction of modal parameters for a RDS is not a novel problem and proven time-

stabilisation diagrams and user input renders them ineffective for completely autonomous
damage detection. To this author’s knowledge, a time-domain method not dependent on
this does not exist, as mentioned in Section 2. To this end, a simple, pragmatic and crude
approach is suggested here.

Summary
The study of the damage indicators for known and modelled damage types suggests that
they could potentially be used to infer damage from a measured response.
They are all
more or less suited under different circumstances and display varied trends, sensitivities and
variability. The absolute values of the indications here may not be of particular importance
as the realism of the damage modelled is uncertain. The trends and relative uncertainties
are however of potential use.
The issues encountered for the damping estimator, for certain parameter combinations,
suggest that the modelled damage is very severe, as convergence was not achieved. Overall,
it perhaps also seems like the Coulomb friction introduced here has a greater impact than
the other damage types. Even so, this study may serve as a reference when interpreting the
same damage indicators for damage detection.

Setup for Experimental Damage Modelling
Here, an experimental setup for investigation of one emulated damage type is introduced.
The goal of this setup is to investigate how the damage indicators introduced previously
respond to damage of a known type and extent. Once again, the WTG component from
which the setup is derived is the blade. The potential types of damage are many and their
(potentially simultaneous) impact on vibrational characteristics thus also potentially numer-
ous and diverse. The approach taken for this experimental setup is picking one thought both
relevant in terms of occurrence and useful in terms of the physical phenomena potentially
transpiring.
Both in terms of material, geometry and measurements, the setup is not meant to closely
resemble that of some damaged WTG blade. The goal is not absolute predictions of damage
indicators for an operational WTG blade. Instead, the subject central to the experimen-
tal analysis to come is the qualitative impact on the damage indicators in question. This
knowledge may aid in interpretation of the operational analysis.
The experimental setup resembles a homogeneous cantilever beam with constant cross-
section, but is comprised of 3 parts assembled using threaded fasteners. The motivation
behind a beam comprised of several parts rather than one is that damage may be modelled
through removal of the fasteners used for assembly. The choice of using screws for assembly
was made due to the ease with which the experimental setup may be configured. The 3 parts
are: a baseplate and 2 beam halves. The baseplate is used for assembly of the two beam
halves and attempted realisation of the clamped beam boundary, more on this in Section 5.2.
The two beam halves are mounted to each other as well as the baseplate and comprise the
main oscillatory part of the experimental setup.
The interaction between the two beam
halves, if not completely adjoined, may to some extent resemble e.g. delamination in a WTG
blade.

5.4
Discussion and Sources of Error
The presented experimental setup is proposed for modelling of a beam in states between
intact and severely damaged.
A natural question is then whether or not the beam may
ever behave as an intact beam would. If not the case, then the subsequent analysis will
inadvertently answer whether the increase in damage could be detected, rather than if intact
and damaged states could be discerned.
These two propositions are distinctly different,
although not unrelated. For the setup in question here, an obvious concern is the threaded
fasteners used to assemble the 3 components. Ideally, these would induce clamping forces that
force the boundaries of parts together, such that the structure behaves as one homogeneous
solid. However, issues such as insufficiently fine tolerances in threads, insufficient clamping
force and holes not aligning exactly may prevent this. Instead, the structure may exhibit
damage-type phenomena, even when fully assembled, due to e.g. interfacial slipping and/or
==PAGE:59==
5.4
Discussion and Sources of Error
49
clearances opening and closing. A very accurate and simple way to investigate this potential
issue and quantify its effects, would be to simply produce a completely homogeneous beam
assembly with geometry identical to that of the assembled setup. Here, one can be sure that
neither of the aforementioned effects can occur. For practical reasons, this was not carried
out. Ultimately, this concern was not investigated further. It is deemed that these effects are
likely apparent, but not to such an extent that the fully assembled setup may not at least
approximate an intact beam.
Continuing with potential concerns for the setup and how it may exhibit damage-type
phenomena inadvertently, consideration for the excitation level must also be given. Although
systems are most often considered linear for modelling, significant nonlinear phenomena
are bound to occur for any real system if forced sufficiently hard. To investigate how this
may affect the response of the setup, measurements were made for monoharmonic input of
varying strength for system states 1 and 6 (these being deemed the least and most damaged
possibilities, respectively).

As mentioned in Section 2.1, application of the RD technique is reliant on excitation

Site 1

damage indicators is very clear in terms of positives indications, negative indications and

6.3
Data Pre-Processing
Preceding the RD analysis itself, some pre-processing is carried out. The goal of this is to
maximise the chances of successful damage detection. These pre-processing steps will be
presented in the following sections. The order in which these steps are presented corresponds
to the order in which they are carried out.
6.3.1
Wind Speed Filtering
A WTG in operation will most likely exhibit significantly varying response characteristics as
environmental conditions and its operational point is changed. This could be caused by e.g.
varying rotational speeds, angles of attack, wave heights and/or turbulence intensity. If RD
analysis was made disregarding these potential variations, it is deemed that interpretation
of the damage indicators could be complicated and potentially even rendered impossible by
the lack of convergence. Thus, some sort of consideration for this is seen as necessary. The
difficulty of estimating these factors vary greatly. Rotational speed can easily be found from
the azimuth angle. Estimators for turbulence intensity could be found if a satisfactory wind
speed measurement is available. The local sea state, including wave heights and periods,
would likely be much more challenging.
To simplify this consideration, the wind speed is utilised as it is deemed a central oper-
ational parameter, from which other conditions potentially could be inferred. This is by no
means completely accurate, but is deemed sufficient for this thesis. For all positions anal-
ysed, 10-minute averaging was performed for the wind speed. Intervals for analysis were
then chosen as 2-hour periods in which all wind speeds (i.e. 12 10-minute values) are within
a 2 m/s wide bin. For example, when searching for 5 m/s data, all 2 hour periods with mean
wind speeds within 4 m/s and 6 m/s are found. Within these intervals, operational conditions
are deemed sufficiently stable that the RDSs may converge. In relation to the frequencies of
interest, the interval length of 2 hours is also deemed reasonable in terms of convergence for
the RDS, keeping in mind results from Section 4.1. This operation was performed for integer
wind speeds between 2 m/s and 9 m/s, which will be further justified in Section 6.3.3.
6.3.2
Curtailment Check
Immediately following the wind speed filtering, a check for curtailment is carried out. Cur-
tailment of a WTG is an operation carried out when the power demand is less than what
can be delivered if it is controlled optimally. This can entail e.g. pitching of the blades to
reduce drivetrain torque, which is thought to potentially change the dynamic characteristics
of the WTG greatly. To avoid this complicating analysis, checks were put in place to omit
==PAGE:66==
56
6

periods with curtailment from RD analysis. Checks for this possibility were put in place
using the blade pitch angle and the number of rotations undergone in the 2-hour interval. If
the number of rotations in the interval was counted below 100, the results were disregarded.
If the blade pitch, for which zero is approximately the minimum value, was out of the range
of [−1.5°, 0.5°] for more than 20% of the sample, the results were also disregarded.
6.3.3
Azimuthal Blade Weighting
When attempting damage detection for the WTG blades, using the response of the WTG
hub is not optimal; The hub accelerometer measures only the total (i.e. relative to e.g. the
ground) response and this response is the result of dynamics from many WTG components.
This makes it difficult to isolate the response in which the blades have significant involvement
and makes it difficult to distinguish one blade from the others. The former problem will be
addressed in Section 6.3.5. The latter will be considered here.
In Figure 27, a sketched WTG with blade angles θi, where i = 1, 2, 3, and hub side-side
response v can be seen. Modes with large participation of transverse blade vibrations in
the rotor plane (i.e. on the paper, as seen in the figure) can potentially transfer forces in
the side-side direction of the hub, causing significant coupling between these displacements.
For the wind speeds picked out in Section 6.3.1, which are below the rated wind speed,
this corresponds well with the edge modes of the blades (i.e. transverse displacement in the
chord direction) being coupled to the hub side-side displacement. Following guidance from
Ørsted, these edgewise modes are targeted for analysis, as their experience suggests that high
damping for the flapwise modes (orthogonal to the chord line) often impedes analysis.

As may be seen from Figure 27, the proposed approach is such that the cosine factor in

These are the positions in which transverse edgewise blade root forces are aligned with v.
Conversely, the cosine factor is zero for a horizontal blade, where this force is orthogonal to v.
Hence, the weighting scheme for the hub response in (6.1) attempts to single out the response
relevant to blade i. However, it does not do this completely, as the weighted response zi will
still be influenced by transverse forces from at least 1 other blade at all times. Even so, this
weighting for each blade is deemed valuable. This is particularly true because using blades
as controls for one another eliminates some variability that stems from using another WTG
entirely instead.
The approach suggested here is quite simple and not too inconceivable, but to this author’s
best research and knowledge, this exact proposition was not found described in literature. It
was largely inspired by the Coleman Transformation (or Multiblade Coordinate Transforma-
tion), found in e.g. [35]. This transformation is an exact transformation between rotational
and non-rotational blade coordinates. For example, 3 blade edgewise modes (lying in the
rotor plane) may be converted to movement of the rotor centre of mass relative to the hub as
well as a collective lag DOF. As the involved DOFs are relative in nature, however, the single
accelerometer signal does not allow for application of this. If a suitable relative displacement
measure, such as blade strain gauge signals, was available, then the rotating coordinates
could feasibly be converted to non-rotating coordinates.
The proposition is thought useful, but not perfect. One potential problem is if axial blade
forces arise. For S1PA, where blade bolts snapping may have allowed for the blade to slam
into the rotor hub axially, these forces could potentially have occurred. For completeness,
results without this azimuthal blade weighting for all damage indicators will be provided in
Appendix B.
6.3.4
Time Integration Scheme
The RD analysis inherently revolves around using displacement and velocity data to detect
and quantify e.g. nonlinearity. When it was applied in Section 4, this was no hindrance,
as the synthetic data from the beam model in Section 3 included these parameters. For
application with e.g. an accelerometer, as to be done for operational and experimental data,
this now has to be considered. The approach taken is a frequency domain method described
and investigated by Brandt and Brincker in [36].
In short, the method involves a FFT
of the signal, followed by the very simple integration in the frequency-domain, before the
signal is transformed back into the time-domain using a reverse FFT. The signal is then
also detrended, i.e. removal of any mean and slope. To obtain confidence in the method
for application here, the method was benchmarked on data from the model in Section 3.
The data was modelled for a strongly nonlinear 2-DOF system featuring both asymmetric
clearance and Coulomb friction, subject to random excitation. Reviewing the results for both
velocity and displacement, very satisfactory accuracy was obtained for signal lengths relevant
to this thesis.
Although the time-integration scheme described worked very well on synthetic data, a
minor issue was discovered for application on both operational and experimental data; Very
low-frequency components would contribute with non-physically large amplitudes. In both
==PAGE:68==
58
6
Operational Wind Turbine Faults: Measurements and Data Preprocessing
the velocity and displacement time-domain, this would be reflected as very slow variations
that lead to velocities and displacements reaching levels not deemed physical. The solution
put in place for this was to simply set the amplitudes for frequencies below a certain threshold
to zero before performing the inverse FFT. As these problematic frequencies were very low,
this threshold could quite easily be set to a level well below that of interest to the RD analysis.
6.3.5
Band Pass Filtering
The final step before RD analysis aims to address the complexity of the response measured
in the nacelle. The WTG has many modes, only some of which are relevant to damage
detection for the blades. If the raw signal is analysed, what may occur is completely differing
modes dominating the response at different times, and subsequently the RDSs as well. As
was found when this was attempted, this variability makes interpretation of the results very
difficult.
For RD analysis to be deemed tractable, the dynamics (or modes) to be analysed must
include significant displacement of both (some of) the edgewise blade modes and the hub in
the side-side direction. For a modelled WTG (for each site), Ørsted provided mode shapes
and corresponding frequencies. These were used to target one mode, or several modes with
near similar frequencies. A band pass filter was then applied to the signal. The filters applied
were quite narrow and steep, with a width of 0.13 Hz for Site 1 and 0.10 Hz for Site 2. The
stopband attenuation for the filters is 60 dB, the passband ripple is 0.1 dB and the transition
widths (lower and upper) are approximately 0.05 Hz.

states 2 and 3, a slight decrease in TSIC from unity is seen. The TSIC then drops quite
rapidly for damage state 4 after which it reaches approximately zero for state 5. It is then
quite stable, remaining at zero until state 8, after which it regrows to a value above 0.8 for
state 9. Finally, a small increase is seen for state 10, although unity is not reached. For
states 4-8, it is deemed reasonable to conclude that TSIC indicates damage to the structure.
Overall, the standard deviation is quite small in comparison to the changes in mean value.
This is very desirable for a damage indicator, as one may then more easily have confidence
in relatively small damage indications.
Even so, states 2-3 and 9-10 may be difficult to
definitively classify as damaged, as the variations for an undamaged experimental setup have
not been investigated with the procedure used. Without knowledge of the variation in TSIC
for system state 1, more significant deviations from unity are perhaps necessary for positive
damage detection, depending on the confidence level required. If damage was to initiate and
propagate, corresponding to going from state 1 towards 6 or from 1 to 10 and then down to
7, cf. the discussion in Section 5.3, then Figure 31 suggests that one would be able to track
this damage progressively and reliably from either state 4 or 8 and on. However, a problem
arises once damage has initiated and begins to propagate: TSIC will attenuate towards zero
and saturate as a damage indicator. From states 5-8, steady near-zero values are obtained
universally. This makes tracking the damage degree impossible using TSIC.

• The RDSs for the (modelled) intact and (one of) the most damaged states show signif-

large variability for these parameters render them ineffective for damage detection in
this case.

variability. The frequency-oriented TSIC showed little variability and presented strong
indications of damage, when it progressed past a certain threshold. However, TSIC was
shown to saturate at a given damage state, after which no further damage progression
could be monitored.
• The frequency estimate showed insignificant variability and a very clear and inter-

progression monitoring is deemed very good for this indicator. The damping ratio es-

extreme damage states, an outlier in both mean and variability was observed, but the
potential for damage detection is deemed poor.

Conclusion
8.1
Summary
Progressing towards attempted damage detection for operational wind turbine components,
with a focus on blades, the following analysis steps have been carried out.
The Random Decrement technique was briefly introduced, with the more involved parts
of derivation omitted. Various customary trigger conditions for calculation of sets of Random
Decrement signatures were presented. Parameterisations of these signatures, from both ma-
ture and recent literature, for nonlinearity detection and characterisation for a system were
presented.
A modified cantilever Bernoulli-Euler beam model was proposed for synthesis of data
for a relevant system with varying damage types and degrees. Though not ideal, damage
modelling for modal coordinates was deemed sufficient and appropriate for this model. The
objective of this model is merely to provide interim data for development and benchmarking
of Random Decrement parameters used as damage indicators. The included modal damage
models are a discontinuous clearance-type stiffness term and Coulomb friction. For excitation
of this model, the use of white noise was considered, developed and validated, in the setting
of the numerical integration scheme utilised.
For single and double mode expansions of this beam model, 4 configurations of damage
were analysed with the damage indicators already presented. For these results, convergence
of the Random Decrement signatures and subsequently damage indicators was investigated.
Some further damage indicators based on Random Decrement signatures were suggested.
These indicators revolve around estimating frequencies, damping and the change in these.
The performance of all damage indicators, in terms of the ability to detect damage and track
it as well as variability of indications, was investigated using a Monte Carlo simulation for
the beam model.
An experimental setup based on a cantilever beam with modelled damage was presented.
The beam is composed of two halves, which may be adjoined partly with threaded fasteners
for imitation of damage. The experimental setup components and configurations were de-
scribed. Some potential issues and aspects for the experimental setup that may complicate
analysis of results were discussed.
The failure type and progression was described for 2 operational turbines, for which
damage detection was to be attempted. The two faults were snapping blade (root) bolts
and shear web debonding. The blade bolt issue concluded with critical failure of the joint
and loss of the blade. The data channels and record lengths were given. The pre-processing
procedures for the data were given. This includes band pass filtering, for targeting a desired
mode, as well as a method for attempting to extract data relevant to one blade only from
lateral hub accelerometer readings.
The experimental and operational results were presented and analysed. Due to the quan-
tity of operational damage indicators, a select few relevant and representative indicators were
presented.

Conclusion
8.2
Main Results
The most central findings from experimental and operational results, respectively, are out-
lined below.
For the experimentally modelled damage, detection of damage was successful when it sur-
passed a certain threshold. From visual inspection of the Random Decrement signatures for
the most damaged configuration, several noticeable discrepancies were found: The dominant
frequency in the signature had decreased. Also, the overall shape had deviated from the
approximately damped monoharmonic character found for the most intact configuration.
Of the computed damage indicators, a frequency estimate was found most suited. It
allowed for tracking of the reduction in the frequency associated with a mode, which for the
intact configuration bears strong resemblance to the fundamental transverse mode. After
surpassing the aforementioned threshold, this reduction enabled confident and progressive
tracking of the modelled damage extent. A damage indicator oriented towards detecting
frequency anomalies also provided strong and assertive indications of damage for the same
configurations as the frequency estimate. This indicator compares Random Decrement signa-
tures to those obtained for the intact configuration. However, this indicator experienced an
issue regarding saturation, whereby the potential for damage extent tracking was drastically
reduced.
The remaining damage indicators were unable to indicate damage for the experimental
setup. For the different configurations with various degrees of modelled damage, some vari-
ation in these indicators was observed. However, large variation in the damage indicators,
for a given configuration, as well as unclear relationships between the indicators and damage
extent, rendered these indicators ineffective.
For all operational results, counting all positions, blades and damage indicators consid-
ered, visual interpretation of the directly computed values was found exceedingly challenging
due to large variability. The exact cause for this variability was not discovered, but the
suspected cause is unaccounted for variations in operational conditions, e.g. wind and sea
characteristics. To this end, a rolling average post-processing procedure was found appropri-
ate and beneficial.
For the operational fault with snapping blade bolts, damage detection was successful. No
false-positive damage indications were found. When comparing the 3 blades to each other
for the damaged position, the frequency estimate for the damaged blade decreases as critical
failure nears, reaching a reduction of approximately 0.4%. With reasonable certainty, this
can be said to indicate damage 40 days prior to critical failure. The possibility for damage
extent tracking in this case is considered excellent. The other damage indicator which also
saw some success for the experimental results likewise conveyed damage progression close
to critical failure. Furthermore, similar indications were found as early as 110 days before
critical failure. However, intermittency was present for this parameter and a lack of damage
indications appeared afterwards. The root cause of this intermittency was not discovered.
For the fault with shear web debonding, damage detection failed. No damage indicators
suggested damage for the position or blade in question. No false-positive indications were
found either.

8.3
Discussion
Damage detection for operational wind turbine components, specifically blades, has proven
itself to be both possible and difficult using the techniques applied in this thesis. In this case,
this proves only that a certain fault, measured in a certain way, under certain operational
conditions and using certain techniques, could be detected. This consideration is a useful
starting point for a discussion of the proposition in its entirety.
Vibration-based damage detection is a, perhaps unfortunately so, widely applicable hy-
pernym that encompasses a vast collection of diverse analysis challenges. Even limited to
wind turbine components, the variability may be substantial. One may consider damage de-
tection as a space, spanned by all the elements which may vary: structure type, environment,
damage type, damage severity, measurements, analysis technique and the requirements for
detection. What has been proven here, is that one point in this space is feasible, while an-
other is not. The robustness of the successful case is not known, i.e. how much this figurative
point can be shifted and in which direction(s), before damage detection becomes impossible.
In light of this, the significance of the successful damage detection is uncertain due to the
unknown versatility of this exact damage detection scheme.
The Random Decrement technique was chosen not only for its applicability in an output-
only setting, but also for its potential to detect and characterise nonlinearity. Based on lit-
erature, nonlinearity and damping were anticipated to be excellent indicators for structural
damage, more so than frequency. However, in both the experimental and the successful op-
erational case, frequency and frequency related indicators outperformed all other indicators.
This includes those oriented towards nonlinearity and damping. As previously mentioned,
this can only be said to hold for these cases exactly. This unforeseen point was not inves-
tigated further, but suggests that for a parameter considered as a damage indicator, one
should evaluate not only its correlation with a damage type, but also its measurability, i.e.
the potential for accurate and reliable estimation of it.
8.4
Outlook
Sticking to this thought of damage detection as a space spanned by the various elements
affecting it, a reasonable approach may then be thought of as twofold. Firstly, one may
try to limit or focus in terms of the fault type and severity, structures, environment and
requirements for damage detection. This is likely a partly commercial undertaking. Secondly,
and more central to this thesis, a goal would then be to target the most important cases and
develop satisfactory damage detection schemes which are as robust as possible, i.e. covers as
much of this space as possible.
A potential way to practically develop, evaluate and improve damage detection schemes
for a certain damage type, could be leveraging the already very complex and mature time-
domain aeroelastic modelling tools in the field, in combination with experimental testing
of failing/failed components.
If one could estimate the relevant structural properties of
the component in question, e.g. inertia, stiffness and energy dissipation, for the relevant
conditions, e.g. deflections, temperatures and flow conditions, then these potentially even
nonlinear parameters could conceivably be implemented in the modelling tools. With this,
synthetic data with known faults could be obtained and used for benchmarking. This would
