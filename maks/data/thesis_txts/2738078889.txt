Abstract 
 
Carbon capture is one of the most intuitive technologies that can be used to address increasing 
carbon dioxide emissions and thus mitigate global warming. Many capture and separation technologies 
have been proposed and investigated. Among these, chemical absorption using aqueous amine 
solutions followed by thermal absorbent regeneration is one the most widely used and established 
methods.  
The present project aimed to explain, analyze and optimize an alternative and rather less 
established carbon capture technology - CO2 capture by a potassium hydroxide (KOH) coupled with 
electrochemical solvent regeneration. This technology has the potential of decreasing the cost of CO2 
capture, as it utilize a cheap absorbent. Additionally, electrochemical regeneration methods can reduce 
the energy required for absorbent regeneration as they are able to operate at ambient temperature 
and pressure. The project was spilt into two parts, chemical absorption in a packed bed column and 
electrochemical regeneration utilizing electrochemical stack.  
 
The absorption process performance was examined based on two parameters: capture 
percentage and loading of CO2 rich absorbent solution. The influence of several factors, including liquid-
gas ratio, lean absorbent molality, and lean loading, on the absorption performance was evaluated to 
determine optimal operating conditions. A simple absorption model was employed to compare the 
experimental and theoretical results.  
The research outcomes indicate that the capture percentage benefit from the high availability 
of active K+ fed to the column. Therefore, to achieve a high capture percentage, the column should 
operate at high L/G ratio, with high molality and low lean loading absorbing solution. The absorbent 
rich loading was limited by mass transfer limitations during the absorption. In particular, at high L/G 
ratios, increasing gas-liquid contact area and contact time would be necessary to enhance the achieved 
rich loading. Despite these limitations, the packed bed absorption column achieved high rich loadings, 
usually exceeding 0.8 mol CO2/mol active K+, which suggest that KOH can be efficiently used as CO2 
absorbent.  
 
The electrochemical regeneration part of the project involved configuring and optimizing the 
electrochemical installation setup. The final configuration enables continuous operation of the process 
and collection of the regenerated absorbent. Based on that design, the absorption column could in the 
future be coupled with electrochemical regeneration to operate absorption and regeneration 
simultaneously.  
 
The electrochemical regeneration experimental results aimed to determine the influence of 
potassium load ratio (LK+), current density (i) and absorbent molality on the regeneration performance. 
That performance was evaluated based on three main parameters: CO2 removal ratio (RC), CO2 
desorption efficiency (ηc) and specific energy consumption (SEC). The experimental results indicate that 
unwanted ion transport within the electrochemical stack and internal cells resistance are the two main 
factors influencing the regeneration performance. A sufficiently high current density (500 A/m2) was 
required to reduce the intensity of unwanted ion transport and increase ηc and RC. Additionally, both 
ηc and SEC benefited from operating at optimal LK+ value, which was found to be close to 1. During the 
experiments the lowest achieved SEC was 13.38 GJ/ton CO2, which is significantly higher than the 
minimal theoretical energy consumption. This suggest that the process could be further optimized. 
Future studies should focus on decreasing the internal resistance of the electrochemical stack, as 
especially at higher current density, a rapid rise of reference voltage was significantly contributing to 
higher SEC values.

1. Introduction 
In recent years, increasing attention has been paid to ongoing global warming. This phenomenon, 
which was marginalized as recently as the end of the 20th century, has now become one of the most 
important topics in political discourse. This has led to numerous international conferences and 
agreements during which countries around the world committed themselves to taking action to limit 
their impact on global warming. One such action is to reduce carbon dioxide emissions. This can be 
achieved by incorporating carbon capture, utilization and storage (CCUS) technologies. CCUS 
technology aims to capture CO2, either from the emission source or directly from air, and utilize it to 
obtain valuable products or effectively store it. Several technologies such as absorption, adsorption, 
membrane technology or cryogenic technique could be used for CO2 capture and separation[1].   
Chemical absorption in amine-based solvents, followed by thermal absorbent regeneration is one 
of the most established and studied carbon capture technologies. However, the amine-based 
absorption is limited by solvent high corrosiveness, degradation and reagent loss. Additionally, thermal 
regeneration of the amine-based absorbent requires a significant amount of energy due to high 
temperature and increased pressure. For instance, regeneration energy of monoethanolamine (MEA) 
absorbing solution, one of the most commonly used amine-based absorbent, accounts for 70%-80% of 
the total operating cost of the system[2]. 
To increase the economic viability of carbon capture alternative absorbents and regeneration 
technologies has to be developed and optimized. Metal hydroxides, such as potassium hydroxide 
(KOH), are a promising alternative for CO2 absorption. Compared to amine-based absorbent, KOH 
solutions are much cheaper, less corrosive and have high degradation resistance[3,4]. However, the 
conventional thermal regeneration of aqueous KOH absorbents requires a significant amount of energy. 
An electrochemical regeneration, for example via bipolar membrane electrodialysis can address this 
problem. Electrochemical regeneration does not require elevated temperatures; therefore, it can 
significantly reduce the regeneration energy.  
 
The absorption of CO2 in KOH coupled with electrochemical solvent regeneration has been 
relatively underexplored in the existing literature. The research on KOH is mostly limited to its CO2 
absorption performance data, and the electrochemical regeneration research often does not include 
the absorption process[4–6]. This study aims to bridge this gap by exploring both the utilization of KOH 
solution to capture CO2 in a packed absorption column and subsequent solvent regeneration via bipolar 
membrane electrodialysis. The results and knowledge obtained from this project will contribute to the 
design of a pilot scale integrated unit capable of capturing 100 kg CO2/h.  
1.1 Project objectives 
The purpose of this master's project is to acquire practical and theoretical knowledge in chemical 
engineering and electrochemical principles. The project tasks will include the operation of the absorber 
and electrochemical units in separate mode, testing of different configurations for optimization of 
regeneration energy, and possible modelling of the process. The student will be responsible for 
developing an optimal configuration of electrochemical regeneration unit and making it functional. In 
addition, evaluation of the energy consumption and efficiency, which is based on several experiments 
by the most important parameters of operation will be required. The results should be compared to 
relevant amine systems. 
The main objectives of this study are summarized as follows.

2. Literature Overview 
2.1  Global warming and climate change 
Global warming is a fact. The year 2023 was the warmest since the global records began in 
1850. The global land and ocean temperatures exceeded the 20th-century average by 1.18 °C, 
reaching a total of 14.98 °C. On top of that, all of the warmest years within the 174-years record 
took place in the last decade [7]. Figure 2.1 illustrates the global land and ocean temperature 
anomalies. Since 1980  only positive temperature anomalies have been observed. Additionally, the 
magnitude of the temperature anomalies is rising, reaching values never noted before. 
 
Figure 2.1 Global Land and Ocean Temperature Anomalies[7]  
The predicted effects of temperature rise are already being observed worldwide. It is seen 
extreme heatwaves, droughts, heavy precipitation, and tropical cyclones, etc [8]. These extremes can 
directly affect humans. For instance, heatwaves can overwhelm the human body’s ability to cool down 
leading to dehydration, heat exhaustion or even heatstroke. A multi-community study suggests that 
mortality risk increases during heat waves compared to the usual climate in these communities. This 
effect is visible for populations living in moderate cold and moderate hot climate areas as people living 
there are more sensitive to heat waves than those living in cold and hot areas[9]. 
Global warming can also cause more frequent and extreme wildfires such as those observed in 
Canada in 2023 [10,11]. During record-breaking 2023 wildfires season approximately 185 000 km2 of 
Canadian land was burned, which is over 4.3 times the area of Denmark (excluding Greenland and 
Faroe Islands)[12] and over twice more than previous record 71 060 km2 (1995) [7,13,14]. 
Research by Mozny et al.[15] reveals a significant impact of climate change on European hop 
cultivation. It reports a yearly hop production decline of almost 0.2 tons per hectare a year and a 0.6% 
reduction in alpha content since 1994 and a precited decline in hop yield and alpha content of 4-18% 
and 20-31% by 2050, respectively. This could potentially negatively impact not only the price of the 
beer but also its taste and creates a serious threat to traditional European hop species and beer styles 
[15].

2.2 The role of greenhouse gases 
Global warming is the consequence of an unbalanced greenhouse gas effect. In 1827 Jean-Baptiste 
Fourier suggested that some components of Earth’s atmosphere influences its surface, similar to what 
happens under the glass panes of a greenhouse [16]. Nowadays it is known that the factors responsible 
for that effect are so-called greenhouse gases (GHG). Those gases absorb infrared waves radiated from 
the earth’s surface and remit it in all directions. Some of it is emitted to outer space, a part is 
reabsorbed by other greenhouse gas molecules, yet a portion directed back to earth leading to increase 
of the planet surface temperature [16,17]. The primary GHG are water vapor (H2O), carbon dioxide (CO2), 
methane (CH4) nitrous oxide (N2O) and fluorinated gases (F-gases).  
 2.2.1 Carbon dioxide equivalent 
When comparing the impact and emissions of different GHG the global warming potential 
(GWP) is often used.  
Global warming potential 
Global warming potential is a dimensionless metric allowing to compare the global warming 
impact of different gases. It measures how much energy the emissions of 1 ton of gas will absorb over 
a given period of time (usually 100 years), relative to the emissions of 1 ton of carbon dioxide (CO2). In 
simple terms, it quantifies how much 1 ton of a particular gas will warm the atmosphere over a fixed 
period of time, compared to 1 ton of carbon dioxide. The larger the GWP the, the more the given gas 
warms the Earth compared to CO2 over that time period [18,19]. For example: 
The GWP of methane is estimated at 27.0-29.8 over 100 years. Methane is a more potent greenhouse 
gas than CO2 as it absorbs more energy. However, it lasts much shorter in the atmosphere, with an

2.3 Carbon capture and storage 
Carbon capture and storage (CCS) or carbon capture, utilization and storage (CCUS) technologies 
are expected to play an important role in reducing carbon emissions, decarbonizing industry and 
mitigating climate change. This technology involves three or four main steps. 
 CO2 capture 
In this step carbon dioxide is captured either from its source before being released in the 
atmosphere, or directly from air (direct air capture, DAC). Liquid absorbers such as amine-based 
solutions are probably the most widely used, with the 20-30 wt% aqueous monoethanolamine (MEA) 
being arguably the most common. Additionally, adsorbents such as zeolites, carbons, metal organic 
frameworks or membrane materials are used for CO2 capture. [30,31] 
CO2 transport 
Transport of captured CO2 may be accomplished be pipelines, tanks, rail and ships. The pipeline 
transport of CO2 is generally preferred for distances of the order of 1000 km. This technology is 
regarded as well-established with more than 6500 km of CO2 pipelines worldwide. This method of 
transport is often used for enhanced oil recovery (EOR). The ship transport is also a relatively mature 
technology often used as alternative solution to pipeline transport for offshore EOR sites [25,31,32]. 
 CO2 utilization 
Carbon dioxide utilization involves conversion of captured CO2 into range valuable products to 
create an economic value. CO2 can be perceived as source for carbon for products such as polymers 
and plastics, fuels, pharmaceuticals, or synthetic fibers. Many of those utilization technologies are

commercial scale with projects such as Sleipner CO2 Storage, Snøhvit CO2 Storage and Quest [30,31,34].

 2.3.1 Direct Air Capture 
As mentioned before, CO2 could be emitted from industrial sources such as power plants, 
refineries, cement, chemicals, and metals production factories, but also more distributed or even 
mobile sources, which includes car transport, aviation, marine transport, or households. While it is easy 
to imagine capturing CO2 from the big power plant, the technological and logistic challenges associated 
with small and distributed sources require different solutions. Direct air capture (DAC) is a technology 
that can theoretically tackle all the carbon emissions. That concept implies placing DAC plants 
anywhere and removing CO2 directly from the ambient air. However, DAC has many limitations. The 
primary one is CO2 concentration in the ambient air - 400 ppm (0.04%), which is around three hundred 
times lower than the average CO2 concentration in a coal-fired power generation (12-14%). The cost 
and energy penalty for CO2 capture increases with the decreasing CO2 concentration. This energy 
demand could be expressed through minimum work of a given separation process. This relation is 
presented in Figure 2.5, where the minimum work is a function of initial CO2 concentration, capture 
rate and final purity [25,31,35].

method is the generation of pure oxygen on a large scale. Currently oxygen is usually separated from

technologies such as membrane separation can make this process much more economically viable

2.4 CO2 separation techniques 
The aforementioned carbon capture classification is somewhat limited, as it focuses primarily on 
CO2 emissions resulting from fuel combustion. However, significant amount of  CO2 are also emitted in 
other industrial processes, such as ammonia or ethanol production, where emissions are not attributed 
to fuel combustion[42,43]. Because of that an additional carbon capture classification based on 
separation method can be introduced. These include absorption, adsorption, cryogenics, membrane 
separation. 
 2.4.1 Cryogenic separation 
Cryogenic separation separates CO2 from the flue gas by condensation. To achieve sufficient 
separation gas mixture has to be compressed and cooled in several stages. Depending on the operating 
conditions, the CO2 can come out as a liquid or solid together with other components from which it 
can be recovered (at atmospheric pressure CO2 triple point is at -56.6 °C). Cryogenic technologies can 
obtain high purity of CO2, some sources suggest even 99.99% purity [44]. The main disadvantage of these 
technologies is their vulnerability to water. The presence of water in the flue-gas stream can result in 
the formation of ice, which can subsequently lead to plugging and a reduction in pressure. Additionally, 
the cold energy requirements for cryogenics separation are high and maybe only competitive with 
other separation methods if liquified natural gas is available as a cold source [1,45,46].  
 2.4.2 Membrane separation 
The fundamental concept of membrane separation is straightforward. A semipermeable film 
selectively allows CO2 molecules to pass through, while excluding other components of the flue gas. 
The important parameters for membranes are permeability and selectivity. There are three types of 
membrane materials: polymeric membranes, ceramic membranes, and hybrid membranes. The 
polymeric membrane consists of a thin selective layer which is bonded to thicker, non-selective and 
low-cost layer as a support. Polymeric membranes have lower cost and are characterized by a relatively 
high gas flux and mechanical stability. However, they offer generally low selectivity of CO2/N2 which is 
important especially for post-combustion capture. Ceramic membranes allow greater selectivity, but 
they are much more expensive and complicated in production. Hybrid membranes combine the 
advantages of both types of previously mentioned membranes. They have a lower cost of production 
and are more flexible than ceramic membranes and obtain high selectivity [1,47–49].  
The main disadvantages of membrane separation are still insufficient selectivity which implies the 
necessity of multistage separation that increases the capital on operational cost. On top of that, the 
performance of membrane separation is strongly affected by flue gas conditions. Low CO2 
==PAGE:23==
12 
 
concentration (below 20%), low pressure as well as presence of sulfur compounds negatively influence 
the membranes performance [47,50].  
 2.4.3 Adsorption 
Adsorption is a physical or chemical process in which molecules of gas or liquid adhere to the 
surface of a solid adsorbent material [51]. Based on the nature of the forces involved in the attraction 
between the adsorbate gas molecules and adsorbent adsorption can be divided into chemical 
adsorption (chemisorption) and physical adsorption (physisorption). 
In chemical adsorption the adsorbate molecules create chemical bonds with the adsorbent 
surface. In case of carbon capture such bonds can form between a carbon atom from carbon dioxide 
and a nitrogen atom present in the functional group or backbone of the adsorbent (e.g. in an amine 
group) resulting in increased selectivity towards carbon dioxide [30]. At the same time, those bonds 
exhibit high bond energy (around 100 kJ mol-1) which increase the energy required for absorbent 
regeneration [52]. In physisorption the adsorbate molecules are attached to the sorbent surface by much 
weaker van der Waals forces (bond energy 10 kJ mol-1). This may decrease the adsorbent selectivity 
but also decreases the energy required for sorbent regeneration [52].  
The main criteria for adsorbent selection are large specific surface area, high selectivity, and 
high regeneration ability. The typical sorbents that can be used in this separation technique are 
molecular sieves, activated carbon, zeolites, metal oxides or metal-organic frameworks. The adsorption 
and regeneration can also be achieved via various methods such as temperature swing adsorption, 
vacuum swing adsorption, and pressure-temperature adsorption. The main drawbacks of adsorption 
technology are adsorbents vulnerability to water as many sorbents suffer from a dramatic reduction of 
their CO2 capacity when water is present in flue gas. Additionally, the CO2 selectivity of many available 
sorbents is insufficient, the removal efficiency is lower than other existing technologies and 
regeneration and reusability of the absorbents is still challenging [46,48,52].  
 2.4.4 Absorption 
Absorption is a process in which atoms or molecules enter the bulk phase of gas, liquid or solid 
material and are taken up within the volume. The absorption itself can be divided into chemical and 
physical absorption [51]. The mechanism of absorption of various solvents is discussed in the following 
sections, which describe several types of the most popular liquid absorbents. 
For carbon dioxide separation, chemical absorption involves a reaction of CO2 with solvent 
resulting in bond formation. Figure 2.9 presents the typical process scheme of this method involving 
two main vessels – absorber and stripper. The absorber receives the CO2-laden feed gas at the bottom 
and liquid sorbent solution from the top to selectively absorb CO2 in countercurrent fashion. The gas 
stream, mostly depleted from CO2, is released to the atmosphere. The CO2-rich solution is fed to the 
stripper or regenerator at high temperature, in which it is stripped of CO2 and the lean solution is 
regenerated or recycled. The commonly used solvents are aqueous solutions of alkanol amines such as 
monoethanolamine (MEA), diethanolamine (DEA), N-methyldiethanolamine (MDEA), these and other 
sorbents will be discussed in following sections. Amine methods are assessed to be able to capture 
approximately 85-95% CO2 included in flue gas with a purity above 99.95%. Chemical absorption has 
high CO2 selectivity, large absorption capacity and is regarded as the most used and mature 
technology[1,48,49,53].

Physical absorption follows similar trends except there is no reaction between the CO2 and liquid 
absorbent, CO2 is absorbed into a solvent based on Henry’s law. This method performs best for low 
temperatures and high pressures of separated gas. Due to the need for high partial pressure of CO2 for 
absorption, physical absorption is not economical for application when the CO2 partial pressure is lower 
than < 15 vol%. Physical absorption utilizes solvents such as SelexolTM, RectisolTM, IfpexolTM.[48,53]. 
2.5 Liquid absorbents  
As mentioned before, post-combustion is currently the most widely applied process in source point 
emission mainly due to its flexibility and applicability to be retrofitted for a short and medium term 
without any significant technology risks or changes. Chemical absorption of CO2 relies on a reaction 
between CO2 and a chemical solvent and is considered to be the most mature and promising 
technology for post-combustion carbon separation [53,54]. Ammonia solutions, amine-based solutions, 
carbonate solutions, hydroxide absorbents and ionic liquids are some of the liquid absorbents that are 
used. 
 2.5.5 Ammonia solutions 
Ammonia is a cheap and robust liquid absorbent. Ammonia solutions offer several advantages 
in flue gas environment, including low degradation and corrosion rates, easy regeneration, and high 
capacity for absorbing carbon dioxide (1.20 kg CO2/kg NH3). Additionally, these solvents have the 
capability to capture multiple pollutants such as nitrogen oxides (NOx) and sulfur oxides (SOx), forming 
compounds like NH4NO3, (NH4)2SO4 respectively. Those byproducts, along with main products of CO2 
absorption (ammonia carbonates), can be directly used as fertilizers, saving energy otherwise required 
for solvent regeneration, CO2 compression and utilization. The carbon dioxide reacts with ammonia 
forming two possible products ammonia bicarbonate (Eq. 2.2) and ammonia carbonate (Eq. 2.3) [55,56].

Absorption by alkali hydroxide solutions has also draw attention for CO2 capture at high 
temperature and absorbents such as calcium hydroxide Ca(OH)2 [62], sodium hydroxide NaOH [63], and 
potassium hydroxide KOH [5] has been studied. The presented alkali sorbents share a number of 
common characteristics. First of all, the chemical absorption mechanisms of alkali hydroxide solutions 
can be referred to as carbonation. In this process CO2 is dissolved in the water-based solvent (similar 
to carbonate absorbents) and reacts with hydroxide solution forming carbonates and bicarbonates. The 
balance between those two carbon species depends on the amount of CO2 as well as pH of solution. 
For instance, for calcium hydroxide formation of CO32- is favorable at pH >10 and HCO3- at pH between 
6.5-10. Also, those solvents can be regarded as cheap absorbent, especially compared to alkanolamine 
solutions [4,64].  
Nevertheless, hydroxide absorbents also possess certain drawbacks. For instance, Ca(OH)2 - as 
mentioned before - is a cheap absorbent since it can be obtained for abandoned limestone CaCO3. It 
has also great CO2 absorption capacity ranging from 1.0 to 3.05 g CO2/g Ca(OH)2 [4]. However, the 
extraction of CaO, which is the raw material for production of , is energy intensive and emits a 
tremendous amount of CO2. The product of absorption of CO2 with calcium hydroxide is mainly CaCO3 
and although calcium carbonate can be stored or utilized as a building material, maintaining large 
quantities of CaCO3 could be challenging. Obviously, Ca(OH)2 like any other hydroxide absorbent could 
be also regenerated. However, this process is mainly achieved via thermal desorption, as CaCO3 needs 
to undergo calcination process. Calcination occurs at elevated temperatures (approximately 750°C) 
making solvent regeneration energy demanding [4,64,65]. 
Regeneration of NaOH and KOH based absorbents is also challenging. For example,

2.6 Electrochemical carbon capture 
The previously mentioned carbon dioxide capture and regeneration methods have numerous 
drawbacks. Even the well-studied amine-based absorption suffers from amine decomposition, solvent 
loss, equipment corrosion and may be difficult to handle due to their toxicity [68]. However, the main 
challenge for the currently available carbon capture methods is high energy consumption. This energy 
is required for the regeneration of the absorbent, which is usually achieved with thermal desorption 
[70]. For example, the energy consumption of CO2 capture via aqueous solution of MEA using a thermal 
swing is between 170-300 kJ/mol CO2 [70]. Approximately 80% of that energy is required for thermal 
regeneration of the solvent [71]. Electrochemical approaches for carbon capture could address this 
challenge. 
Electrochemical methods usually utilize liquid electrolyte to absorb and release CO2 in two-staged 
process (Figure 2.10). In the first step CO2 from flue stream is absorbed in an absorber. The CO2-rich 
solution is later pumped to electrochemical unit (such as electrodialysis stack) where the CO2 is 
desorbed, and the sorbent is regenerated. Regenerated sorbent is directed back to the absorber and 
the process starts again.  
 
Figure 2.10 Simplified schematic of electorchemical CO2 caputre process 
The main advantage of electrochemical carbon capture is that the electric gradient is the main 
driving force of chemical reactions which enables to target a specific reaction. This is especially 
important for the desorption reaction. Electrochemical methods allow to conduct desorption in 
ambient temperatures eliminating the need to heat the water in the aqueous solutions of CO2 sorbents 
leading to possible smaller energy requirements [68]. Electrochemical technologies also do not require 
high pressures, nor vacuum and can be readily integrated with existing production processes [72]. Since 
electric energy is involved only in absorbent regeneration, not in the CO2 absorption itself, in the 
following part of the report electrochemical regeneration term would be used instead of 
electrochemical carbon capture.

technologies, such as pH-swing, redox-active carriers, molten carbonate, and hybrid methods, have 
been established. Figure 2.11 presents the mentioned breakdown of electrochemical CO2 regeneration 
methods. In this work a pH-swing concept was studied and optimized. This swing refers to 
electrochemical shifting the pH of liquid absorbent to influence the CO2 equilibrium to capture and

3.2  Absorption column 
Absorption is usually conducted in trayed towers (plate columns) and packed columns. The 
absorber used in this project was a packed column. This type of absorber is often used when the 
required column diameter is small (<60 cm), the pressure drop is low, and the desired liquid holdup is 
low[76].  A packed column scheme is presented in Figure 3.3. It is a vertical, cylindrical vessel containing 
one or more sections of packing material. The lean liquid absorbent is fed at the top of the column and 
flows downward by gravity forming film or droplets between packing elements. The gas (also referred 
to as vapor) is fed at the bottom of the column and flows upward through the wetted packing elements, 
contacting the liquid[76]. As the gas mixture is contacted with liquid a certain component of the liquid, 
in this case CO2, is selectively absorbed and CO2-rich liquid stream is collected at the bottom of the 
column. At the same time a gas with decreased amount of CO2 exits the absorber at the top of the 
column.  
==PAGE:32==
21 
 
 
Figure 3.3 Scheme of packed column absorber. 
The role of packing is to increase the liquid-gas contact surface area enhancing the mass 
transfer in absorption process. Random packing and structured packing are the two types of available 
packing materials. There are many types of random packing materials with various shapes and designs. 
Each of them exhibits a different pressure drop per unit height of packing and mass-transfer rates per 
unit height. They also come with different nominal diameters which are optimal for a given column 
diameter. The packing elements are placed randomly within the column, creating void spaces and a 
complex network of pathways for the gas and liquid to flow through. The structured packing is designed 
with a specific, organized structure to optimize surface area and improve separation efficiency. Its 
structure often resembles a honeycomb with different size openings between the adjacent corrugated 
layers that are stacked in the columns. Structured packing exhibit lower pressure drop and have higher 
efficiency and capacity but at the same time it is considerably more expensive than random packing 
[76]. 
Determining an appropriate height of the absorption bed and column diameter, that allows an 
optimal gas-liquid separation, is a separate matter on which another paper could be devoted. Those 
dimensions are dependent on various parameters such as liquid holdup, flooding, pressure drop, 
volumetric mass-transfer coefficients and height equivalent to theoretical plate (HETP)[76]. These 
considerations are not discussed because the experimental column has already been constructed and 
the optimization of the absorption was carried out by changing other process parameters, which will 
be presented in the following sections.

content is quantified by carbon loading.  
 3.3.1 Carbon loading 
Carbon loading (α) describes the amount of CO2 captured by the solvent. It is expressed in 
moles of CO2 per moles of capture-active K+ ions (Equation 3.12). Although the CO2 in the solution was 
expected to be present mainly in form of either carbonate CO32- or bicarbonate ions HCO32-, it should 
be noted that some of CO2 is physically dissolved. 
 The solubility of CO2 in water is a function of temperature and pressure. CO2 solubility decrease 
with temperature and increase with pressure[77]. However, under ambient conditions, fraction of  solute 
CO2 is very low, around 4·10-4 mol fraction[78], which equates to approximately 0.1 g CO2/kg H2O. 
Consequently , it was assumed that the CO2 contribution in the loading value is dependent only on the 
CO32-, HCO32- concentrations. The capture-active potassium ions are all potassium compounds that 
participate in the absorption process including KOH but also KHCO and/or K2CO3. It is important to note 
that if the solution contains other potassium compounds, for example a background electrolyte in form 
of K2SO4, the potassium included in this salt does not influence carbon loading parameter.

3.4 Electrochemical regeneration 
The main component of the regeneration unit – the BPMED electrochemical stack - is presented in 
Figure 3.7. The presented design is similar to the electrochemical cell that has been used by the 
department in other research in collaboration with Wetsus - the European Centre of Excellence for 
Sustainable Water Technology (www.wetsus.eu).  
The stack comprised of 2 cell pairs, and one cell consisted of acidic and alkaline compartment 
separated by a cation exchange membrane (Fumasep FKBPK- 130, FUMATECH BWT GmbH, Germany). 
Each cell was separated from both sides by a BPM (Fumasep FBM). Two PMMA end-plates with meshed 
platinized titanium electrodes (10 g Pt/m2, 10 x 10 cm2, Evoqua Water Technologies LLC/Magneto 
Special Anodes B.V., the Netherlands) were employed as the anode and cathode. The electrolyte 
compartment was located between the BPM and each of the electrodes. The compartments were 
separated by polymeric spacer gaskets of 480 μm thickness (Deukum, Germany).  
The carbon-rich absorbent liquid is directly fed to the acidic compartments, as presented in Figure 
3.7. As the electric potential is applied the K+ ions are transported across the cation exchange 
membrane (CEM) to the alkaline compartment and are replaced by H+ ions generated via water 
dissociation on the BPM. Desorption of CO2 occurs due to these two processes. The depletion of K+ ions 
increase the carbon loading in the solution. When the loading exceeds 1, the amount of K+ in the 
==PAGE:39==
28 
 
solution is insufficient to store CO2 present in the form of HCO3- and CO32- anions. Consequently, CO2 
begins to desorb. Additionally, both the transfer of K+ out of the acidic solution and the introduction of  
H+ into the acidic compartment decrease the pH of solution. This pH decrease leads to  formation of 
carbonic acid, which subsequently dehydrates to CO2. Then, the evolved CO2 and acidified solution is 
directed to G-L separator where CO2 is recovered. 
 
The acidified solution is then directed to the alkaline compartments. During the process the 
alkaline compartment solution is supplied with K+ ions transferred through CEM and OH- from water 
dissociation on BPM. With those two processes the solution in the alkaline compartments is 
regenerated and can be used again to absorb CO2.

 3.4.4 Bipolar membranes 
Bipolar membranes (BPM) are one of the most crucial components of the presented 
electrochemical stack. Figure 3.8 presents a scheme of BPM. BPMs are composed of two layers, a 
negatively charged cation-exchange layer (CEL), and positively charged anion-exchange layer. CEL 
transports only anions, e.g H+ and AEL only cations e.g. OH-. This design property prevents the transport 
of ions across the whole thickness of the BPM and enables operation in distinct electrolytes at either 
side. Between these two layers there is an interfacial layer (IL), also referred to as bipolar junction. It 
consists of a catalyst that facilitates the dissociation or association process[80]. The promoted process 
depends on the orientation of the BPM to the electrodes. When the CEL faces the anode and AEL the 
cathode, BPM operates at so called forward bias, presented on the left side of Figure 3.8. In this 
operation mode H+ and OH- ions migrate from the outside solution across the corresponding layer to 
the bipolar junction, where they associate into water. Subsequently, water permeates out of the bipolar 
junction, leading to the acid-base neutralization in the outside solution.  
The operation mode in which reverse orientation of the BPM is applied, CEL towards the 
cathode and AEL to the anode, is called reverse bias (right side of Figure 3.8). In this mode, water 
present in the bipolar junction dissociated into H+ and OH- ions. These ions leave the BMP through CEL 
and AEL respectively, producing the acid and base on the opposite sides of  the membrane and creating 
a pH gradient over BPM. In the utilized electrochemical stack bipolar membranes were used to create 
such a pH gradient thus they were operating in reverse bias[80,81].  
 
Figure 3.8 Schematic representation of the principle of BPM. BPM on the left represent the forward bias mode, BPM on the 
right represent the reverse bias mode. Adapted from[81]. 
 
The water dissociation process on BPMs occurs without oxygen and hydrogen generation. This 
is an important property distinguishing BPMs from traditional electrodes[81]. Several possible 
mechanisms explaining water dissociation in the bipolar junction have been proposed[82–84]. Some of 
them assume that the water dissociates due to high electric field in the IL[82], other two stage 
protonation-deprotonation reaction, involving a catalyst[83] or a mechanism that combines these two 
theories[84]. The details of those mechanisms would not be discussed in this project. 
In the traditional water electrolysis process, where two separate electrodes are employed, the 
process consists of two steps: water dissociation and gas formation, governed by HER and OER 
(Equations 3.36-3.39). Gas formation requires an additional potential which increases the energy 
consumption. The undeniable advantage of BPM is the previously mentioned ability to split the water 
==PAGE:41==
30 
 
without the generation of hydrogen and oxygen. As a result, the thermodynamic minimum voltage 
required for the water dissociation is reduced, reaching 0.83 V at ΔpH = 14. That value is 2.5 times 
lower than that of traditional water electrolysis at the same ΔpH (0.83 V+1.23 V)[70,81].  
To drive the electrochemical regeneration of the absorbent the electrolyzer requires redox 
reaction, thus hydrogen and oxygen are generated. However, this process occurs only on the distinct 
end-plates, not in each separated cell. Reducing the number of electrodes on which HER and OER are 
facilitated reduces the potential drop across electrochemical stack. The reference voltage was 
measured between the electrolyte compartments; thus, it excludes the contribution of electrodes 
reactions. This correction differentiates the voltage drop due to electrode reactions to the potential 
drop of the membrane, as in large-scale stack the voltage of the electrode reactions is negligible 
compared to voltage of entire stack that usually consist of hundreds of cell pairs [85]. 
 3.4.5 Polarization curve 
To understand how the applied current density affects the voltage during the BPMED 
electrochemical regeneration, it is essential to examine a polarization curve. A polarization curve is a 
graphical representation of the relationship between the potential and the current which is often used 
to analyze performance and behavior of electrochemical cells and ion exchange membranes. Figure 3.9 
presents a scheme of the BPM polarization. 
Three characteristic parts are shown on the presented schematic polarization curve: 
- The first part limited by a limiting current density (jLIM), is the area in which the current is transported 
only by salt ions. In the presented project it would be K+ and HCO3- and CO32- ions. At the limiting current 
density, the slope of the curve is the smallest, which means that the area specific resistance is the 
highest. This increase is caused by depletion of salt ions and water splitting still not proceeding, thus 
the availability of ions capable of carrying the electrical charge is highly limited.   
- As the current surpass jLIM, the curve moves to the second region in which the water dissociation starts 
to proceed and also its products (H+ and OH-) are available to transfer the electric charge. In this region 
the area specific resistance is relatively constant. The operating current density (jOP) is usually chosen 
as high as possible to reduce the salt ion transport and enhance water dissociation efficiency.  
- Beyond the upper limiting current density (jLIM2), water transport to BPM junction is insufficient to 
match the rate of water dissociation; thus, the availability of H+ and OH- decreases [86].

Both calibration curves (Figure 4.6-4.7)  exhibit a non-linear course. There are regions in which 
the graph declines rapidly, but also regions in which the graph flattens out. This fluctuation may 
significantly influence the usability of the calibration curves. 
 For instance, for 1.72 molar K+ solutions, the conductivity varies notably in the range from 0 
to 0.5 with the difference between those points reaching approximately 200 mS/cm. Conversely, for 
loadings above 0.5, the graph flattens out, resulting in minimal differences in conductivity between 
different absorbent loadings. This implies that determine the exact loading for solutions above 0.5 may 
be inaccurate, since small deviations in measured conductivity values, resulting from, for instance, 
imprecise equipment calibration or temperature variations, would result in significant deviations in 
assessed loading. 
 For expected  absorbent loadings it can be concluded that pH is a more suitable parameter to 
determine the actual loading. Although pH calibration curves do exhibit a flattening out in the high 
loading range, this trend is less significant and larger differences between the calibration points are 
observed comparing to conductivity curve. Additional pH calibration curves for different molalities are 
attached in Appendix A4. 
Differences in pH measurements 
Two distinct types of pH sensors were employed in the experiments: online and offline calibration 
sensors. Although the sensors were calibrated before the measurements, a discrepancy was observed 
in the readings. For one of the initial absorption experiments the pH reading of the steady-states 
solvent solution measured by the online pH sensor was 9.89, whereas the pH of the same solution as 
determined by the offline sensor was 9.38. There may be several reasons for this phenomenon. Firstly, 
it was observed that gas often accumulated in the offline sensor housing, either in the form of a single 
bubble or multiple bubbles. The bubbles were in contact with the electrode, which could result in poor 
circuit formation and consequently affect the reading.  
Additionally, the online pH sensor appeared to be drifting from its initially calibrated values after a 
few hours. During one of the experimental sessions, the previously calibrated sensor was cleaned and 
immersed in a pH 10 buffer solution after the experiment was completed, four hours after calibration.

 4.4.5 Online pH and conductivity sensor calibration 
Online pH and conductivity sensors were calibrated within the internal software each time the 
experiments were conducted. For the pH sensors two pH buffers were used (pH= 7, 10). The pH 
electrode was dismounted from the housing, cleaned with mili-Q water, and immersed in the first 
buffer – pH 7. Once the software informed that the measurement was stable the first calibration point 
was saved, and the electrode was cleaned and immersed in a second buffer pH – 10. Once the stable 
value was measured, the calibration was finished, and the electrode was placed back in the housing.  
A similar procedure was applied to the conductivity sensors. The conductivity probe was 
calibrated with one calibration point by immersing the sensor in a buffer solution of 100 mS/cm or 30 
mS/cm conductivity value. The choice of buffer solution was made based on the expected value of the 
measured conductivity. 
 4.4.6 CO2 concentration sensor calibration 
The mini-pilot plant is equipped with two carbon dioxide sensors, the Vaisala GMP251, which 
have a CO2 measuring range of 0-20%. The sensors were located at the absorbers' gas inlet and outlet 
in order to enable the measurement of CO2 concentration before and after the absorption process. The 
sensors were initially calibrated by the manufacturer but their long-term stability deviates with time 
even up to ±1.0% CO2/year according to the manufacturer datasheet. Because of that, the sensors were 
calibrated.  
The sensors were connected to an external data manager Memograph MRSG45. This device 
not only saved the recordings from multiple sensors in the pilot-plant but also allowed for calibration 
and data management. The signal from each CO2 sensor is sent to the Memograph in the form of

Pressure and temperature

calibrated and validated when no liquid was fed to the absorber. In those conditions, the gas was

the temperature of the gas at the outlet, increases during the process. In addition, the sensor was 
calibrated on a specific day at atmospheric pressure of 1010 hPa. The measurements might also be

Gas humidity 
Since temperature increased during the absorption process, more water was present in the 
column compared to the calibration conditions. The outlet gas was more saturated with water vapor. 
According to the manufacturer’s data sheet the accuracy of the sensor could be influenced by relative 
humidity by ±0.7% of reading. Additional precautions were taken to negate the effect of temperature 
and water vapor in the outlet gas on the CO2 sensor reading. The outlet gas passed through a water 
trap before entering the probe. The water trap was a closed empty bottle connected with pipes from 
one side to the absorber gas outlet and from the other to the CO2 probe (Figure 5.1). This bottle does 
not only trap moisture from the gas by condensing the water inside. It also cooled the gas before 
entering the sensor. However, it is possible that the gas was not fully cooled and dried before entering 
the CO2 sensor, which could affect the sensor readings

Impurities 
Another potential source of variability is the presence of impurities in the solvent. The degree 
of the purity of the used KOH was assessed by titration and found to be 86.2%. However, a second 
equilibrium point on the titration curve suggested the presence of another compound that may 
also absorb CO2 or the presence of potassium carbonates and/or bicarbonates. Additionally, ion 
chromatography (IC) analysis of the KOH, performed after the experiments, revealed a minor 
presence of sodium (0.52 wt%). On this basis, it can be assumed that the used granular KOH 
contained a small amount of sodium, e.g. sodium hydroxide or sodium carbonates and 
bicarbonates. In addition, it may have contained potassium carbonates and bicarbonates. These 
compounds would not only affect the absorption process but also the measured pH. Because the 
pH calibration curve did not consider the presence of mentioned impurities, the rich loading 
assessed with it may be inaccurate. A further analysis of the purity of the compounds used, in 
particular the content of carbonates and bicarbonates, would be required to neglect the effect of 
impurities on absorption performance results.

The presented results and discussion have the following consequences: 
- 
With higher molality of the solutions the amount of active K+ fed to a column increases and 
more CO2 can be captured.  
- 
The absorbent rich loading is limited by mass transfer limitations and not by absorbent 
molality. The mass transfer is influenced by a gas-liquid surface area determined by properties 
of absorption column, and contact time determined by L/G ratio. 
- 
To increase the rich loading, one should increase the contact time between the gas and liquid 
by decreasing the L/G. As a result, more CO2(aq) would diffuse into the liquid forming 
bicarbonates and increasing the rich loading.  
- 
Expanding the surface area by increasing the height of the packed column would also enhance 
mass transfer and result in a higher rich loading. Limited surface area also explains why other 
studies on KOH absorption, presented in section 5.1.1, reported much lower rich loading 
compared to this study. Those studies used a cylindrical bed reactor[93] and cryogenic trap[5] , 
which provided smaller gas-liquid contact are than a packed bed absorber column, limiting the 
absorption.

The highest temperature at the top of the column was registered for 0 loaded solvent and the 
lowest for 0.6. A CO2-pure, 0 loaded solution, provides the biggest CO2 concentration gradient, which

reaction (Equation 5.7). This results in the temperature increase at the top of the column.  
Consequently, with a highly CO2-loaded solution (0.6 mol CO2/mol K+ loading), the initial concentration

influence the mass transfer, the 0.6 loaded solution has a lower concentration of OH-, which decreases 
the rate of absorption reaction.  
That is consistent with the temperatures registered in the middle of the column. The lower the 
initial loading of the solvent, the higher the concentration of OH-. The high OH- concentration increases 
the reaction rate and therefore, the temperature. Absorption with low loaded solvent resulted in higher 
temperatures throughout the height of the column. At the bottom of the column the all the solutions 
were highly loaded with CO2 and depleted from OH-. This inhibited the absorption reaction and resulted 
in the lowest registered temperature.

to 0.2 and 0.4 loaded solvents (0.70 and 0.69 mol CO2/mol active K+ respectively). Although the initial 
composition of the 0.6 loaded solution reduced the absorption rate, it needed to capture less additional 
CO2 to reach a high loading. Most likely, the surface area provided by the column and initially high CO2 
loading compensated for the mass-transfer and rection kinetic limitations, allowing the 0.6 loaded 
solvent to achieve higher rich loading.  
Based on the results, it can be concluded that solvents with no initial CO2 content (lean loading 
0) achieve the highest absorption efficiency. This is evidenced by their highest capture percentage and 
rich loading compared to other solvents. However, it should be noted that impurities present in the 
KOH granular could influence the results, obscuring the correlation between the lean loading of 
experimental solution and achieved rich loading. Although, based on the results present in section 
5.1.2, the molality of the absorbent should not influence the rich loading, it could have influenced the 
measurements. Both CO2 sensor calculations and pH measurements could have been unreliable due 
to the presence of impurities. An additional series of experiments with, utilizing solutions with constant 
molality and higher purity KOH would be beneficial for results evaluation.  
 
5.2 Electrochemical regeneration of absorbent  - setup configuration 
One of the main challenges during the project work was to create and optimize an electrochemical 
regeneration unit. The system should meet several requirements: 
- 
Ability to supply rich solvent to the electrochemical stack in a continuous manner. 
- 
Ability to recirculate acid and alkaline solutions to the electrochemical stack independently of 
the rich solvent’s inlet flow. 
- 
Separate CO2 from the solution and be able to quantify the CO2 desorption rate. 
- 
Provide a stable overflow from an acid to  an alkaline recirculation loop. 
- 
Maintain a constant volume of liquid in the system, and an ability to receive regenerated 
absorbent from the alkaline loop to redirect it sot absorption column. 
During the course of the project many different set-ups of electrochemical regeneration units were 
tested and evaluated. This work consumed a significant amount of time but provided a deeper 
understanding of the behavior of the liquid and gas in the system. Figure 5.10 presents a simplified 
scheme of the initial regeneration setup which was left over from earlier work carried out on the 
electrochemical regeneration.  
==PAGE:74==
63 
 
 
Figure 5.10 Initial electrochemical regeneration setup.  
The red lines represent the acid loop and the blue lines the alkaline loop in the system. The 
gas-liquid separator (G-L separator) consisted of a closed bottle with an inlet pipe and two outlet pipes, 
one for gas and the other for liquid. Although the installation appears to be functional and meets all 
the requirements from the diagram, several problems were identified during the test run. 
First, it was difficult to achieve a stable level of the liquid in the G-L separator. Depending on 
the height of the G-L vessel was dropping or increasing constantly. The reason for that was the so-called 
siphon effect. It is an effect where a liquid is moved from higher to a lower level by atmospheric 
pressure and gravity. The atmospheric pressure exerts a force on the liquid, causing it to flow towards 
the pipe on one side. Simultaneously, the force of gravity acts on the liquid, causing it to descend 
towards the pipe on the opposite side. This results in a reduction in pressure at the top of the siphon. 
Additionally, the forces of liquids cohesion are keeping the particles in the liquid stream together 
facilitating that effect[95]. This phenomenon is presented in Figure 5.11. If the G-L separator was placed 
higher than the other part of the installation, the liquid, pushed by atmospheric pressure and gravity, 
flowed out of it until it reached the level of the outlet pipe. This not only made the flow inconsistent, 
as the liquid rapidly leaving G-L separator flowed into other parts of the installation increasing the 
overflow and outlet flow, but also some of the desorbed CO2 would be transferred to installation with 
that liquid. This led to a decreased measured CO2 desorption rate, and also potentially transferring gas 
to electrochemical cell leading to decreased cells performance.  
==PAGE:75==
64 
 
 
Figure 5.11 Presentation of siphon effect. 
 
An additional problem was an overflow of acid to the alkaline loop.  The principal assumption 
of that installation is the capacity to continuously remove a portion of the acidic solution to the alkaline 
side, thereby facilitating the reproduction of lean solvent that can later be used for the absorption 
process. The volume of the liquid flowing from the acid to the alkaline loop should be constant over 
time and equal to the volume of a rich absorbent fed to the installation with pump A1 (Figure 5.10). 
During the operation it was noticed that the overflow was most of the time larger, as the acidic loop 
was gradually drained of liquid.  The reason for this phenomenon was the siphon effect and the 
pressure differences between the acid and the alkaline loops. It was concluded that the overall pressure 
in the acid loop is higher than in the alkaline loop. This pressure difference can be adjusted by the pump 
speeds in both loops. However, ultimately the siphon effect occurring in the G-L separator would shift 
this temporary pressure equilibrium back deregulating the flow.  
To better understand the flow and pressure in the setup it was decided to simplify the 
installation as much as possible. An installation presented in an article by Shu et al. was an inspiration 
for a new approach regarding the G-L separator and an acid-base overflow [69]. The authors used a 
membrane contactor, which primary role is to separate desorbed CO2 from the absorbent, but it also 
acted as an overflow from acid to the alkaline loop. A similar solution was used in the second iteration 
of the regeneration configuration. The difference was that a T-shaped glass vessel was used as the G-L 
separator instead of a membrane contactor. The use of a simple vessel for G-L separation has obvious 
advantages, particularly for large plants, as it reduces the capital expenditure (CAPEX) of the 
installation. An even further simplification was employed by substituting a T-shaped vessel with a 4-
outlets glass vessel (Fig 5.12). Figure 5.13 presents how the liquid streams were distributed depending 
on the type of vessel used as G-L separator.  
 
Figure 5.12 Two types of vessels used as G-L separator, left inital T-shaped, right final G-L separator

The scheme on the right side of Figure 5.13 lustrates that a 4-outlets glass vessel enabled us to 
substitute two T-vessels for just one. The setup was tested, and although the single vessel was capable 
of distributing all the streams a siphon effect present in the new G-L separator was still a problem.  
Figure 5.14 presents how the height of the overflow pipe was influencing the liquid level in G-L 
separator. The height of the overflow pipe (h1) was dictating the height of the liquid in the G-L separator 
vessel (h2) as expected. The liquid was filling up the G-L vessel up to point h1. However, once the 
overflow pipe reached the h1 height the siphon effect was observed (Figure 5.11). The level of liquid in 
G-L separator was decreasing until it reached the G-L overflow outlet level. This caused the same 
problem, which was explained in Figure 5.11, the liquid overflow was sucking some of the gas from G-

Figure 5.14. A siphon effect problem encountered during second configuration. 
 
An initial idea to solve this problem was to employ one more pump to the alkaline loop, as 
presented in Figure 5.15. In this setup two pumps (C2 and B2) were present in the alkaline loop. The 
additional C2 pump placed before the T1 vessel allowed for controlling the overflow rate. Increasing 
==PAGE:77==

the C2 pump rpm was creating more pressure in the T1 vessel, which was pushing the liquid back to 
the overflow pipe. Pump B2 was controlling the recirculation flow to the alkaline compartments of 
electrochemical stack. With the correct pumps setting, it was possible to keep the liquid level in the 
G-L separator (E2) constant, above the pipe outlet. This prevented the gas from entering the alkaline 
loop and stabilized the overflow. This solution was tested successfully, achieving stable CO2 desorption 
and liquid flow. 
Additionally, a second 4-outlet, E-shape vessel (E1) was used as an overflow vessel of 
regenerated absorbent. The vessel had one outlet open to the air which equalized the pressure in the 
installation. A siphon effect was observed in the overflow of the regenerated absorbent, but this time 
it did not have a negative impact on the installation performance. As the volume of liquid in the alkaline 
loop slowly increased the additional volume of regenerated absorbent was directed to the outlet. The 
air that was drawn into the outlet with the flowing liquid did not affect the performance of the 
installation and properties of regenerated absorbent. This solution also eliminated a need for a pump 
removing the liquid from the installation as the correct outflow was achieved with the atmospheric 
pressure and gravity.   
 
Figure 5.15. Electrochemical regeneration installation setup utilizing two pumps in the alkaline loop.  
 
A clear disadvantage of that plant configuration was the need for constant control of the liquid 
level in G-L separator. This was because CO2 desorption, small fluctuations in the fluid flow or other 
factors affected the pressure and flow balance, which was achieved by fine-tuning the operating values 
of pumps C2 and B2. After some period of time, the liquid level in the E2 tended to either decrease or 
increase, requiring stabilization by adjusting the rpm setting in C2 pump. This could be solved by 
implementing a PID controller, that would control the C2 setting based on the liquid level in the E2 
vessel. Since such a controller was not available and the single experiment could last a few hours, it 
was decided to improve the setup to increase its robustness against pressure and flow variations.  
The final installation is presented in Figure 5.16. This scheme is more detailed as it also includes 
pH and conductivity meters, power supply and electrolyte loop. A similar scheme was previously

In this setup a simple anti-siphon device was used (T1). It was a T-connector that was 
positioned at the highest point of acid-base overflow. Its two ends connected the alkaline and acidic 
loops, and one end was open to the air, with a single pipe of approximately 15 cm length facing 
vertically upwards. The purpose of this device is to break the unwanted siphon effect. When the water 
reaches the top of that overflow (T1) it starts to fall to the alkaline loop, the water column in the pipe 
breaks. When the water column breaks, the air is sucked through the open antisiphon outlet instead 
of CO2 from G-L separator. The additional length of the pipe that was open to air prevented the liquid 
from flowing out of the installation. 
The anti-siphon solved the problem of unwanted CO2 flow to the based loop, however, as 
mentioned a portion of air was still sucked to the installation. To solve this problem an additional gas-
liquid separator was employed (E2). This vessel separated the liquid in the alkaline loop from the air, 
preventing air bubbles from entering the electrochemical stack. The final configuration met all the 
requirements set at the beginning of this chapter. In the future, the electrochemical unit can be coupled 
with absorber to conduct CO2 absorption and solvent regeneration in a closed loop.

The inability of the sensor housing to effectively dispose of gas bubbles and, as a result, gas 
accumulation may have significantly affected the reliability of the measured values. This problem was 
due to a simple design flaw, presented in Figure 5.20.   
 
Figure 5.20 The design of pH and conductivity sensors case, present design (left), possible improvement to design (right) 
The sensor housing comprises two chambers, one for the conductivity sensor and one for the 
pH sensor (Figure 5.20). The sensors are connected by short canals, allowing the liquid to flow through 
the housing and contact both sensors. In the design introduced by the manufacturer, the liquid enters 
the housing from the bottom and is directed to the conductivity sensor. The liquid fills the first chamber 
and exits it at the top through the channel. This construction enables the gas bubbles to escape the 
conductivity chamber, thus preventing the accumulation of gas bubbles in that chamber. Subsequently, 
the liquid enters the chamber of the pH sensor, flows down to the active part of the pH sensor, and 
exits the chamber from the bottom. Due to the pH chamber outlet being situated at the bottom and 
the space between the sensor and the walls of the chamber being relatively small, gas bubbles tend to 
accumulate at the bottom of that space. As illustrated in Figure 5.20, the gas bubbles adhere to the 
active part of the pH sensor, influencing its recordings. An alternative design that would prevent gas 
accumulation is presented on the right side of Figure 5.20. The inlet to the second chamber (pH) should 
be located at its bottom and the outlet at its top, in a manner analogous to that of the conductivity 
==PAGE:82==

part. The gas bubbles would naturally flow upwards with the liquid, thereby being removed from the 
system.

The second parameter that was monitored during the electrochemical regeneration of the 
sorbent was conductivity. Figure 5.21 compares conductivity and pH values registered over time.

The acid loop measurements are marked with red, pH-dark red, conductivity – bright red, and 
alkaline loops with blue, pH-dark blue, conductivity-light blue. The graphs for pH and conductivity are

reached thermal equilibrium. Although pH is also dependent on temperature, the pH sensors utilized 
included temperature compensation, which may have contributed to the stabilization of readings. 
Conductivity measured in the alkaline loop were also characterized by abrupt changes in values, similar 
to the pH graph. The acid loop conductivity sensor did not register a sudden increase in the measured 
value at 13:42. Instead, a drastic drop in conductivity was registered at approximately 13:17. This drop 
could also be attributed to the presence of gas bubbles, which adhered to the sensor surface for a brief 
moment and decreased the measured value. However, in this case, the correct design of the sensor 
housing likely removed the bubbles quickly, returning the value to the correct reading.

The stable value of pH during the experiments performed with 500 and 1000 A/m2 could be 
attributed to the concentration of K+ in the BPM junction. As the current density was increased, more 
K+ was fed to the electrochemical stack in order to keep the LK+ constant. Since K+ also transfers a 
positive charge, similarly to H+, they can also be transfer through CEL of BPM, this phenomenon is often 
referred as ion crossover[80]. It can be assumed that even at higher current densities the transport of 
H+ is limited by the K+ ion crossover. This competition reduces the effective H+ flux despite the 
effectiveness rate of water dissociation, maintaining a stable pH even under higher current densities. 
Another conclusion could be drawn by comparing the model predictions to the experimental 
results. Figure 5.28 shows that the applied model expected the RC and ηC  to be constant throughout 
the experiments and not dependent on the current density. Additionally, experimentally achieved RC 
and ηC were lower than model predictions, which indicates that part of the applied current was not 
utilized for CO2 desorption. That current, defined as current loss, was calculated with Equation 5.8. This 
equation calculates the difference between the description efficiency predicted by model (ηC,model) and 
the ηc achieved experimentally. This demonstrates how much of the current entering the system was 
not used to desorb CO2 from the solvent. By dividing that difference by ηC,model it gives a fraction of the 
current that was not used for the desorption.

If the effect of data deviation is disregarded, the variations in the SEC achieved across the

conductivity, which results in smaller Vref. However, a high K+ concentration may also increase the 
membrane resistance. A study by Zhu et al. reported that the type of the ion transported across CEM 
influenced the membrane resistance[101]. This influence was attributed to ion hydration free energy.

absorbent solution, the intermediate (1.5 molal) solvent molality achieved the lowest SEC. However, 
small differences between regeneration performance results suggest that additional repetitions of the 
presented experiments would be beneficial to determine the actual influence of solvent’s molality.

be sufficient to remove all the supplied active K+ and therefore desorb all the CO2. Further increase in 
LK+  do not influence the Rc, since no more CO2 could be desorbed.

6. Conclusions 
The project presented aimed to acquire a practical and theoretical knowledge of CO2 chemical 
absorption coupled with the electrochemical regeneration process. The process was divided into 
chemical absorption and electrochemical regeneration with a BPM electrochemical stack.  
6.1 Absorption performance 
Absorption experiments aimed to analyze how the parameters such as L/G ratio, absorbent 
solution molality and lean loading influence (αlean) the absorption performance. The absorption 
performance was described with two different parameters; capture percentage (cap%) and spent 
absorbent rich loading (αrich). A mathematical model was developed to analyze the results.  
The experimental results demonstrated that the two primary factors influencing absorption 
performance were: availability of active K+ ions in the absorption solution and mass transfer limitations. 
The cap% was found to benefit from availability of active K+ ions. Increasing the L/G ratio, utilizing a 
high molality and low lean loading absorption solutions increased the availability of the K+, and 
consequently increased the fraction of CO2 captured during the absorption process.  The highest cap% 
(85.56%) was achieved with 1.72 molal, 0 loaded solvent at L/G ratio 3.5 
The αrich of spent absorbent was limited by mass transfer limitations. Achieving high rich loading 
requires sufficient gas-liquid contact area and time. For L/G =1, the surface area and contact time 
provided by the absorption column was sufficient to almost completely utilize KOH absorption capacity 
(αrich=0.99 mol CO2/mol K+). However, as the L/G ratio increased the αrich decreased. This suggest that 
at higher L/G ratio, the column capacity to facilitate effective mass transfer was limited. Therefore, to 
increase αrich the height of the column or the surface area of the packing would have to increased. 
Compared to the L/G ratio, the αlean of solution was found to have a less significant influence on 
the αrich. The highest αrich (0.83 mol CO2/mol K+) was achieved when pure lean solution was used as 
absorbent. Low loaded solvents increased the CO2 mass transfer and absorption reaction rate, 
especially at the top of the column, where gas stream has low CO2 concentration. However, the 
experimental result may have been influenced by impurities present in the raw granular KOH used for 
solution preparation.  
The molality of the absorbing solution had a negligible effect on the achieved absorbent rich 
loadings. This is because the solution concentration does not influence the mass transfer efficiency.  
The obtained rich loading was significantly higher than reported literature. In most of the 
experiments with αlean = 0 mol CO2/mol K+, the rich loading reached at least 0.8 mol CO2/mol K. This 
improvement was due to the packed bed absorption column used, which provides much greater gas-
liquid contact area compared to cylindrical bed reactor and cryogenic trap were used in cited studies.  
6.2 Electrochemical regeneration 
 6.2.1 Electrochemical regeneration set-up configuration 
Systematic improvement of electrochemical regeneration unit has resulted in configuration 
capable of continuous operation. This advancement enables future projects to couple absorption with 
chemical regeneration and operate the mini-pilot plant with a closed absorbent loop.  
 6.2.2 Electrochemical regeneration performance optimization 
The electrochemical regeneration experiments aimed to optimize the process by decreasing the 
specific energy consumption and increasing both CO2 desorption efficiency (ηC) and CO2 removal 
ratio (Rc). During the experiments the influence of current density, potassium load ratio and 
==PAGE:105==
94 
 
regenerated solution molality on the electrochemical regeneration was examined. The experimental 
results demonstrate that the performance electrochemical regeneration by BMPED was limited by: 
- unwanted transport of K+ across BPM and H+ across CEM 
- internal resistance of electrochemical stack 
Undesired ion transport 
Both transport of K+ across BPM and transport of H+ across CEM are undesired, as they do not 
contribute to CO2 desorption. Consequently, they negatively impact regeneration performance.  
Unwanted transport processes occurred during all of the experiments with their extent varied with 
experimental conditions. The undesired ion transport was often codependent. The experiments 
indicated a following relation between operating conditions and undesired transport:  
- 
Low current density (250 A/m2) reduced the efficiency of BPM water dissociation, leading to 
decreased H+ generation. Consequently, unwanted K+ transport represented a larger fraction 
of ions transported through BPM.  
- 
The current density of 500 A/m2 was sufficient to increase the efficiency of BPM water 
dissociation and enhance CO2 desorption. Increasing the current density to 1000 A/m2 did not 
improve ηC and RC, this was caused by undesired K+ transport.   
- 
Electrochemical regeneration performed at LK+ values well above 1 (1.23 and 1.55), resulted in 
the depletion of the K+ ions in the acidic compartments. Because of that a larger fraction of H+ 
transport were transported across the CEM.  
- 
Conversely, when the LK+ was smaller than 1, more K+ ions were supplied to the feed 
compartment than removed across CEM. As a result, the concentration of K+ in the BPM 
junction gradually increased, increasing the fraction of K+ transported across BPM. 
- 
The molality of regenerated solution had a less profound effect on ηC and RC. However, it was 
observed that increased the K+ concentration in the feed compartment, limited the desired 
transport of H+ across BPM.  
Overall, experimental results indicated that to increase the CO2 desorption efficiency and CO2 removal 
ratio the electrochemical stack should be operating at LK+ value close to 1, at a sufficiently high current 
density (500 A/m2) and with relatively low absorbent solution molality (1 mol K+/kg water).  
Internal resistance of the electrochemical stack 
The SEC was affected by the unwanted ion transport and cell reference voltage (Vref). Within 
the studied parameters, current density had the biggest influence on Vref. The stack Vref linearly 
increased with the applied current density. This voltage increase was attributed to the electrolyte and 
membrane ohmic resistance. The lowest SEC (13.38 GJ/ton CO2) was achieved with 250 A/m2. Even 
though higher current densities improved ηC and Rc, the additional rise of electrochemical resistance 
resulted in increased SEC.  
Both regenerated solution molality and LK+ value had a less pronounced effect on the reference 
voltage, with the lowest SEC registered at LK+=1.03 and absorbent molality 1.5 active K+/kg water. High 
LK+ and low solvents molalities resulted in slightly increased Vref, which could be attributed to the 
decreased conductivity of the acidic compartment solution. However, since the differences between 
the Vref,  at different LK+ and molalities, were small the SEC was mostly influenced by ηC. 
Based on that, to achieve the lowest SEC the utilized electrochemical stack should operate at 
low current densities, with LK+ value close to 1 and regenerated absorbent solution molality 
1.5 active K+/kg water.

6.3 Comparistion to amine systems 
Monoethanolamine (MEA) CO2 capture and separation, which is often regarded as a benchmark 
technology, typically operates with an energy consumption of 3.6 to 4 GJ/ton CO2 [31]. That value is 
significantly lower than 13.38 GJ/ton CO2, that was the minimum SEC achieved during the presented 
experiments. However, study by Bui et al. estimated that in theory the energy consumption from BPM 
electrochemical regeneration may be reduced below 100 kJ/mol CO2 (2.3 GJ/ton CO2)[99] That suggest 
that the presented process can be further optimized. 
 Additionally, the loading capacity of primary and secondary amines is limited to 0.5 mol CO2 per 
mol of amine. For MEA solvent, the typical operating conditions range from 0.2 to 0.5 mol CO2 per mol 
of MEA[103]. The rich loadings achieved during the majority of the absorption experiments reached at 
least 0.8 which is significantly higher. KOH based absorption solutions are also much cheaper than 
amine base absorbent. The reagent cost ($/ton of reagent) for KOH is estimated at 400-450 $/ton of 
reagent, while the cost of amine sorbents is 1400-1800 $/ton of reagent[3]. The primary disadvantage 
of hydroxide solutions as a CO2 absorbent is the conventional absorbent regeneration process, which 
requires the use of high temperatures. The electrochemical regeneration methodology employed in 
the project has the potential to significantly reduce the SEC, due to the fact that regeneration can be 
conducted at ambient temperature. A further optimization of electrochemical regeneration process is 
required to make it more economically competitive.  
7. Recommendations for future work 
Absorber 
Future experiments with the absorber should focus on a few things to ensure the reliability of the 
results. 
- 
The purity of utilized chemicals should be carefully assessed, as their exact composition affects 
the calculated absorption performance results. An additional total inorganic carbon (TIC) 
analysis would allow to determine carbonate and bicarbonate content in used granular KOH. 
Based on the purity assessment, a new pH and conductivity calibration curve should be 
prepared to increase the accuracy of the results 
 
- 
CO2 sensor should be replaced with a new one, validated and calibrated by the producent. 
Alternatively old CO2 sensor should be verified with another reliable CO2 meter to ensure 
reliability of its recordings. This validation should be performed as the absorber operates to 
neglect the effect of water vapor on measurements.   
 
- 
Understanding the factors limiting the absorption performance, such as gas-liquid contact 
surface area, and possible optimizing the setup to enhance absorption efficiency.  
 
- 
A more complex KOH absorption model should be developed to more accurately simulate the 
absorption process without the need for extensive experiments. Such a model can provide 
accurate predictions and insight, reducing the time and financial resources required for 
experimental procedures especially on a larger scale. 
 
- 
More repetitions of presented experiment should be conducted to increase the reliability of 
the results.

Electrochemical regeneration 
Based on the results presented in this project, future studies should focus on: 
- 
Reducing the internal resistance of the cell, especially at high current densities. This is because 
the high Vref registered was significantly increasing the SEC. 
 
- 
Increasing the water splitting efficiency at low current densities and optimizing the design of 
BPM and CEM to reduce the unwanted transport of ions.  
 
- 
Improving the efficiency of gas-liquid separation. 
 
- 
Conducting more repetitions of presented experiments to increase reliability of results. 
 
- 
Furthermore, the exceptionally high absorbent-rich loadings achieved in the absorption 
column indicates that future research should examine regeneration of solution with higher 
initial carbon loading.  A study by Eisaman et al. reported that the SEC of absorbent 
regeneration decreased with higher loadings[6], which implies that even with the presented set-
up SEC could be reduced by regenerating absorbents with higher initial loading. 
 
- 
Developing a method to assess the molality and loading in the acidic and alkaline 
compartments during the regeneration process. This would allow us to assess the regeneration 
performance and the composition of regenerated absorbent. 
 
- 
Improving the design of pH and conductivity meters housing to effectively dispose of gas 
bubbles. 
 
- 
Possibly reduce the total volume of electrochemical regeneration setup to enhance the rate 
with which the changes driven by electrochemical stack can be observed (pH and conductivity 
measurements) and to reduce the amount of solvent that need to be used for each experiment.

A.2 Solution preparation procedure  
The following procedure was used to prepare each of the solutions. 
1. Mili-Q water was weighed out in a large container on a scale. 
2. Based on the used weight of water the appropriate amount of required compounds (KOH, 
K2CO3, KHCO3, K2SO4) was weighted. 
3. The water container was placed on a magnetic mixer; the solids were slowly added to the water 
while stirring.  
4. The mixture was stirred until a clear solution was achieved 
5. If the solution was not immediately used but stored for the other day the mixing was once again 
performed just before the experiment.  
The gradual and careful addition of compound to the water was especially important during 
KOH dissolution as this reaction is exothermic. The addition of KOH to water at an excessive rate can 
result in a significant increase in temperature, which may potentially damage the container and pose a 
risk to the individual preparing the solution.

A.3 Purity assessment and deviation 
The purity of all the compounds, except for KOH, is very high (≥99%). Therefore, those 
impurities have a negligible effect on the actual molality and loading of the prepared solution. KOH, 
had a purity declared by the producer of ≥84%. Since the exact purity of the compounds were not 
validated before the absorbing solutions were prepared the calculations presented above do not 
consider the purity of chemicals. They are performed as the chemicals were 100% pure. However, in 
the latter part of the project the purity of KOH was assessed to ensure that the results are reliable.  
KOH purity 
The purity of potassium hydroxide was evaluated through titration with an automated Timano 
titration apparatus. The potassium hydroxide (KOH) sample was titrated with a standardized 0.1014 
molar hydrochloric acid (HCl) solution. During the titration, HCl reacts with KOH in accordance with the 
reaction equation (A.14).

Figure A.1.

A.6 Absorber experiments procedure 
The experimental procedure was conducted as follows: 
- 
The absorber experiments begin by calculating the required rpm for inlet pump (A2). The 
required absorbent flow rate for a given G and L/G ratio was calculated using model 
calculations. The calibration curve was then used to determine the appropriate rotations per 
minute for the pump to operate. The model and MFC calibration were also used to set the 
necessary gases (air and CO2) flowrates. 
- 
If the planned experiment was the first of the day, online pH and conductivity sensors were 
additionally calibrated.  
- 
After that the CO2 bottle valve was opened, the pressure at the reductor was set to 6 bar and 
both CO2 and air inlets to the absorber were opened. 
- 
Initially, no liquid was pumped into the absorber and the remaining liquid at the bottom of the 
absorber was drained by opening valves located at the bottom of the absorber, until the level 
was approximately half of the transparent tank under the column. During that procedure both 
inlets to CO2 concentration sensors were open and the CO2 level was observed. When the 
readings of CO2 concentration stabilized, pumping of the absorption solution began. 
- 
If the experiment being conducted was the first of the day, an additional requirement was to 
wait 4 minutes, even if the readings had previously appeared stable. This time is referred to by 
the manufacturer as the minimum warm-up time of sensor. 
- 
As the liquid flow stared the valve to the gas inlet CO2 sensor (CONC1) was closed. This step 
was performed to make sure that all of the gas was injected to the absorber and the assumed 
L/G ratio is obtained. If the valve leading to the inlet sensor were open, some of the gas would 
only be directed towards the sensor and would not reach the column. This could result in a 
different actual L/G ratio and its residence time. The inlet CO2 sensor recording was not 
necessary for the experiment as the CO2 concentration is determined by the MFC settings. The 
initial inlet CO2 level readings were only used to check for any mistakes made during the 
previous steps. 
- 
As the solution was pumped to the top of the absorber the outlet concentration sensor starts 
to register a decrease in CO2 concentration. At that time, the rest of the remaining liquid at the 
bottom of the absorber was drained. This step prevented the old solution from mixing with the 
newly introduced one. Therefore, it allowed for quicker judgement of when steady state is 
reached. 
- 
As the solution was pumped to the absorber the outlet pump (A1) is turned off and the outlet 
valves were closed until the level of a newly formed liquid reached approximately half of the 
transparent tank at the bottom of the absorber. This precaution ensured that gas did not 
escape through the bottom of the column. 
- 
When the required level of the liquid was achieved the valves were opened and pump A1 
turned on. Since pumps A1 and A2 have a single motor, they always operate at the same set 
rpm. The liquid level at the bottom of the absorber was controlled throughout the experiments, 
to high level causes absorber flooding and to low may lead to gas undesired gas flow.  
- 
As the rich liquid is pumped out of the absorber the pH and conductivity sensors register liquid 
properties. The stable pH and conductivity values were evidence that a steady state had been 
achieved in the absorber. Once the steady state had been achieved, the spent absorber liquid 
was collected in a separate vessel for 10 minutes. Approximately 60 ml of the liquid was then 
transferred from the vessel to a closed plastic container, labelled, and stored.

A.7 Electrochemical regeneration experimental procedure 
The experimental procedure was conducted as follows: 
- 
First, pH and conductivity sensors were calibrated with buffer solutions using the internal 
calibration procedure. 
- 
The system was flushed with Milli-Q water and the prepared rich absorbent solution was 
introduced into the system using pump A1. This was continued until the pH and conductivity 
values measured in the base and acid loops reached a constant level. 
- 
Once the pH and conductivity measured were stable the A1 pump flow was set to the desired 
inlet flowrate. The desired flowrate was determined based on the required LK+ ratio, pump A1 
calibration curve and fraction of active K+ in the absorbent solution 
- 
Pumps B1, B2 and C1 were set to 175 ml/min to provide a sufficient velocity of the solvent an 
electrolyte in the electrochemical stack.  
- 
Height of the overflow was adjusted to achieve a required liquid level in gas-liquid separator 
(E3).  
- 
Once a stable flow was achieved the correct current was set on the DC supply. 
- 
The pH, conductivity, CO2 flow, and reference voltage measurements were monitored 
throughout the experiment. A stable CO2 desorption was typically achieved shortly after the 
pH in the acid loop dropped to a certain level and the first gas bubbles were observed at the 
electrochemical stack outlet. Nevertheless, even after the presumed steady state was reached, 
the experiment continued for a minimum of 30 minutes. This was done to ensure the reliability 
of the data and to confirm that the system had indeed reached and maintained a true steady 
state. A more detailed analysis of the steady state and measurements taken during the 
experiments is provided in section 5.3.  
- 
If the same absorbent solution was used in subsequent experiments, the system was not 
completely drained and flushed. Instead, the power was switched off, and the inlet pump speed 
was increased to refill the acid loop with fresh absorbent. Afterward, the required power and 
inlet flow was set, and another experiment was conducted.
