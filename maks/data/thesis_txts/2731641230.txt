Abstract
The rapid growth of photovoltaic (PV) installations in Denmark underscores the nation’s
commitment to renewable energy. With PV capacity surging from a modest 7 MW in 2010
to an impressive 3.3 GW by July 2023, Denmark serves as a beacon of renewable energy
progress. Nevertheless, the public’s acceptance of PV energy is pivotal to its successful
integration, shaping the trajectory of renewable energy adoption.
Acceptance of PV installations in various settings is a crucial area of study in the con­
text of global climate change mitigation goals. Public support and perception of solar
energy technologies are key drivers for their widespread adoption. The different groups
of policymakers, energy planners, and stakeholders must consider public attitudes when
designing strategies to identify optimal sites and locations for future PV projects. How­
ever, the acceptance of PV installations in different settings remains a complex issue,
warranting comprehensive investigation.
This research aims to fill several gaps in the existing literature concerning the acceptance
of PV installations in three distinct locations: private rooftops, large­scale installations in
agricultural land, and industrial rooftops. Specifically, we investigate how that acceptance
is related to the spatial distribution of existing PVs. Currently, there is a lack of com­
prehensive studies examining the acceptance of all three settings together, especially
considering actual spatial data, such as the proximity of existing solar farms to the resi­
dences of survey respondents and its actual distance by the existing roads. Additionally,
the influence of the peer effect on acceptance levels in private installations and the fac­
tors contributing to the ”Not in my backyard” (NIMBY) phenomenon in large­scale projects
require deeper investigation and understanding to identify effective mitigating solutions.
Furthermore, we will deepen our study regarding the acceptance of PV installations on
industrial rooftops.
In light of these considerations, our project would focus more on the spatial issues asso­
ciated with PV acceptance. We aim to investigate how acceptance is intricately related to
the spatial distribution of existing PVs, with a particular emphasis on understanding the
dynamics influenced by factors such as the proximity of solar installations to residential
areas and their accessibility through existing road networks.
The research questions guiding this investigation are as follows: How do individuals per­
ceive PV installations in the various settings described above? Additionally, what are the
significant spatial factors that influence the acceptance of PV installations in these dis­
tinct settings? By addressing these research questions, we will gain valuable insights
into public perceptions and attitudes towards PV installations, enabling us to develop a
comprehensive understanding of the factors that drive or hinder acceptance in this differ­
ent locations.
To answer these questions, this study utilizes data from a survey with 4,133 respondents.
The survey involved a Danish national representative sample, providing invaluable in­
sights into public perception and acceptance of solar energy technologies. To address
the research questions, a spatial analysis will be conducted using both the survey data
and relevant spatial data. Statistical modeling techniques will be employed to examine
the relationships between acceptance levels and various factors, such as the number of
panels in proximity, different socioeconomic groups, and other demographic characteris­
tics.
The findings from this research hold significant policy implications, particularly in the siting
of future PV projects and in addressing the research gap regarding the acceptance of

Introduction
The global shift towards renewable energy sources has become a defining feature of the
21st century, driven by the urgent need to combat climate change and reduce depen­
dence on fossil fuels. In this context, solar energy, harnessed through photovoltaic (PV)
installations, has emerged as a front­runner in the pursuit of sustainable and clean energy
solutions[1]. Denmark, a nation with a progressive stance on renewable energy, reflects
the ongoing global transition. Noteworthy is Denmark’s national target to achieve carbon
neutrality by 2050, underscoring its focus on expanding low­carbon energy technologies,
including solar PV in various settings. Over the past decade, Denmark has experienced a
remarkable transformation in its energy landscape, with photovoltaic installations playing
a pivotal role in this evolution[2].
As of July 1, 2023, Denmark’s photovoltaic capacity has surged from a modest 7 megawatts
(MW) in 2010 to an astonishing 3.3 gigawatts (GW)[3]. This exponential growth under­
scores Denmark’s unwavering commitment to renewable energy adoption. Denmark pro­
duces 39% of its total energy production from renewable sources, compared to Germany’s
19%, United Kingdom’s 18% and the 17% where the global average stands[4]. However,
the successful integration of photovoltaic energy into Denmark’s energy portfolio hinges
not only on technological advancements but on the acceptance and endorsement of solar
energy technologies by the public opinion[5].
Public acceptance of solar energy is key in the transition towards a sustainable energy
future. The support and enthusiasm of the general population for solar energy are pivotal
drivers in the seamless integration of PV installations into various settings. Policy makers,
energy planners, and stakeholders are aware of the fact that public attitudes and percep­
tions must be factored into strategic decisions regarding the deployment of optimal sites
for future PV projects[6].
In this context, the acceptance of PV installations within different settings emerges as a
central focus of inquiry. A comprehensive understanding of public acceptance becomes
imperative for the widespread adoption of solar energy and the attainment of ambitious
climate change mitigation objectives. This research endeavors to address crucial gaps in
the existing literature, specifically within the domain of the acceptance of PV installations
across three distinct settings: private rooftops, large­scale installations on agricultural
land, and industrial rooftops.
The prevailing research landscape reveals an existing void in comprehensive studies that
holistically assess the acceptance of these three distinct locations, particularly when con­
sidering real­world spatial data. This study aims to bridge this gap by examining the
difficulties of acceptance, including:
• proximity of solar farms to residential areas
• the influence of peer effects on acceptance levels in private installations. This con­
cept implies that choices and actions of individuals inside a group shape the deci­
sions of others within that same group towards different areas such as technology
adoption. The peer effect plays a significant role in residential PV adoption. Em­
pirical studies have shown that individuals are more likely to adopt solar panels if
they observe their neighbors doing the same [7], but also that the credibility of the
peers has more influence in the decision to adopt PV systems than than the living

proximity of their peers [8]. This peer effect grows from social influence and shared
information, which can accelerate the diffusion of PV technology within communi­
ties. Understanding how peer networks influence adoption decisions is essential
for crafting effective policies that leverage social dynamics to promote renewable
energy[9].
• the underlying factors contributing to the NIMBY phenomenon in large­scale projects.
This effect encompasses the tendency of individuals or communities to resist local
implementation of necessary projects or facilities, despite recognizing their broader
social benefits, due to concerns about potential negative impacts on their imme­
diate environment [10]. The Not In My Backyard (NIMBY) effect is a social phe­
nomenon that often occurs when communities oppose the installation of PV panels
or other renewable energy projects in their vicinity, despite recognizing their broader
social benefits [11][10]. Residents may harbor concerns about potential negative
repercussions on their immediate environment, such as aesthetic changes, prop­
erty values, or potential environmental and health impacts, which can hinder the
deployment of PV systems [10] [12]. Understanding the NIMBY effect’s underlying
causes and implications is crucial for devising strategies to mitigate opposition and
promote PV adoption[12]. It’s important to note that estimating the NIMBY effect is
challenging, as our survey data does not include precise geotagging for PV accep­
tance questions; rather, only overall location information is available. This limitation
necessitates a more generalized assessment of NIMBY sentiments in the studied
communities.
Furthermore, this research acknowledges the limited amount of knowledge surrounding
the acceptance of PV installations on industrial rooftops, a gap that necessitates further
exploration and understanding.
The overarching research questions that guide this investigation are twofold: Firstly, how
do individuals perceive photovoltaic installations within the diverse settings of private
rooftops, large­scale agricultural installations, and industrial rooftops? Secondly, what
are the significant factors that exert influence over the acceptance of PV installations
within these distinctive contexts? By addressing these questions, this research aims to
offer valuable insights into the public’s perceptions and attitudes towards photovoltaic in­
stallations, fostering a holistic comprehension of the drivers and barriers to acceptance
across different locations.
To facilitate this exploration, this study leverages data obtained from a survey encom­
passing nearly 4,000 respondents. This comprehensive survey features a nationally rep­
resentative sample in Denmark, affording insights into public perceptions and the level of
acceptance surrounding solar energy technologies. To dissect and answer the research
questions, spatial analysis will be employed, utilizing both the survey data and relevant
spatial information. Statistical modeling techniques will be applied to scrutinize the intri­
cate relationships between acceptance levels and various factors, including the density of
PV in proximity, distinct socioeconomic characteristics, and other pertinent demographic
characteristics.
The findings emanating from this research bear substantial policy implications, particularly
in the strategic placement of future PV projects. Moreover, this study endeavors to bridge
the research gap pertaining to the acceptance of PV installations within diverse settings
in Denmark. By unraveling the intricacies of acceptance, decision­makers can pinpoint
optimal locations for PV installations, empowering them to design targeted strategies that
augment acceptance levels and catalyze the widespread adoption of solar energy. This

been a central focus of research and policy discussions worldwide. Solar energy, har­
nessed through PV installations, stands as a cornerstone in the transition toward renew­
able energy sources. While numerous studies have explored the technical aspects of

primarily focused on residential and agricultural settings, leaving the industrial sec­

distinctive challenges and opportunities linked to industrial rooftop PV. However, it’s
important to note that in our study, we solely estimate means across subgroups and
no spatial analysis was conducted.

of solar energy technologies. This knowledge holds significant implications for pol­

adoption of clean and sustainable energy sources. Ultimately, our research endeav­

beyond, paving the way for a more sustainable and environmentally friendly energy

3
Methodology
3.1
Survey Setup
The research methodology for this study focuses on the design and implementation of
the survey used to gather data on public acceptance levels of solar energy technologies
in various settings. The methodology outlines the steps taken to ensure the survey’s
reliability, validity, and representativeness.
1. Survey Objectives and Research Questions
The first step in survey design was to clearly define the survey’s objectives and
research questions. The primary objectives were to gather information and insights
regarding the public’s acceptance of various energy technologies, such as solar,
wind, carbon capture storage (CCS) and others.
2. Survey Instrument Development
The survey instrument was developed based on a thorough review of existing liter­
ature and research objectives. Key components of the survey instrument included:
• Demographic information: Respondents’ age, gender, education, income, and
location.
• Attitudes towards PV installations: Questions assessing overall acceptance
in three different settings: private rooftops, industrial buildings’ rooftops, and
agricultural land.
• Key survey question: The survey inquired about public perspectives on Den­
mark’s utilization of specific technologies for CO2 emission reduction. Partici­
pants were presented with the following questions: ”To what extent do you think
Denmark should use the following technologies to reduce CO2 emissions?”
The technologies specified included increasing the number of solar panels on
house and apartment roofs, increasing solar panels on roofs in industrial areas,
and increasing solar panels in fields. Respondents were then prompted to ex­
press their individual acceptance levels using a 5­point Likert scale, ranging
from (1) to (5), being 1 the lowest and 5 the highest.
The survey underwent a pilot test involving 200 respondents, and subsequent ad­
justments were made based on the insights gained from this preliminary testing
phase.
3. Sampling Strategy
To ensure the representativeness of the survey, a stratified random sampling ap­
proach was employed. The target population consisted of Danish residents of di­
verse demographics. A randomization process on factors such as gender, income,
and geographic location, as well as for age for individuals older than 18 years of
age. This approach aimed to capture a cross­section of the Danish population.
4. Data Collection
The dataset concerning public acceptance of renewable energy technologies was
obtained through an extensive nationwide online survey aimed at uncovering the en­
ergy preferences of Danish households (see Ladenburg et al. [25] for more detail).

The creation of this survey involved a methodical and iterative process, commenc­
ing with an in­depth review of existing literature. Subsequently, consultations were
held with experts in the domains of energy regulation, followed by rigorous individual
survey testing and in­depth interviews.
Approval from the Danish Health Data Authority was secured for the random selec­
tion of 55,000 individuals, identified through their personal identification numbers.
Our research project proposal received the green light. Digital invitations were dis­
patched to potential survey participants via their electronic mailbox, known as ”E­
boks”. Out of the initial pool of 55,000 individuals, representing a cross­section of
the nation, one­third were assigned to the Attitude subsection of the survey, which
is considered the initial sample for the present study. Out of that third (about 18,300
people), 4,133 individuals filled out the survey, being this the effective sample size
of the present study.
Employing online survey design, we randomly assigned respondents into three dis­
tinct sub­sections of the survey, each addressing a unique facet of the energy land­
scape. The dataset comprises responses from all participants in the Attitude sub­
section, totaling approximately 4,133 individuals.
5. Data Collection Period
The data collection period spanned several weeks to ensure a diverse and substan­
tial sample size. Participants were given a reasonable timeframe to complete the
survey, and reminders were sent to enhance response rates. Invitations to partici­
pate in the survey were sent to the respondents’ digital mailbox, ”E­boks,” on June
27, 2022. A reminder was subsequently issued on August 18, 2022, for individuals
who had not yet responded. Following three days without additional questionnaire
completions, the survey concluded on August 31. [25]
6. Data Analysis
Once the survey data was collected, it underwent rigorous data cleaning and valida­
tion processes to eliminate errors and ensure data accuracy. Quantitative analysis
involved statistical techniques, including regression analysis, to examine the rela­
tionships between acceptance levels and various factors.
7. Ethical Considerations
The survey was designed and conducted with strict adherence to ethical guidelines.
Informed consent was obtained from all participants, and their anonymity was pre­
served throughout the study. Ethical approval for the survey was obtained from the
relevant institutional review board.
In summary, the survey methodology employed a systematic approach to gather data on
public acceptance levels of solar energy technologies in different settings. It aimed to
provide a comprehensive understanding of public perceptions and the factors influencing
acceptance, contributing valuable insights to the broader discourse on sustainable energy
adoption.

Spatial Data
In our study, we made sure to utilize real spatial data, taking into consideration the objec­
tive distances traveled by road between participants and designated areas. This practical
approach aimed to provide a more accurate representation of the physical environment

indicate the number and density of PV systems and solar farms within road­network­based

understanding of the spatial elements influencing respondents’ views on solar technology.
In essence, incorporating real spatial data added practicality to our investigation, con­
tributing to a more robust and contextually relevant exploration.
3.3
Statistical model ­ linear regression
The statistical model used to obtain the results is linear regression. Linear regression
is a supervised learning algorithm commonly used to predict the relationship between a
dependent variable and one or more independent variables by fitting a linear equation to
observed data. In the context of their study, linear regression is used to model the relation­
ship between various factors (independent variables) and the acceptance of photovoltaic
installations (dependent variable). [26]
Below is a breakdown of the key components and concepts related to the linear regression
model used in their study:
• Dependent variable (response variable): In this study, the dependent variable is “PV
Acceptance,” which reflects respondents’ willingness to accept PV installations in
different environments, such as private rooftops, industrial buildings, and agricultural
land.
• Independent variables (predictors): The independent variables include various fac­
tors such as age, gender, income, educational level, car ownership, presence of
photovoltaic installations in the vicinity, and other contextual variables.
Each of
these variables aims to capture different aspects that can influence the acceptance
of photovoltaic installations.
• Coefficients: The linear regression model estimates the coefficients of each inde­
pendent variable. These coefficients represent the strength and direction of the
relationship between the independent variable and the dependent variable. For ex­
ample, a positive coefficient for a variable indicates a positive relationship, while a
negative coefficient indicates a negative relationship.
• Intercept: The intercept is the constant term of the linear equation. It represents the
predicted value of the dependent variable when all independent variables are set to
zero. In the context of their study, the intercept captures baseline acceptance when
all other factors are absent.

3.4
Variable Interpretations
1. Gender Dummy Variable (1 = Female, 0 = Male): A dummy variable indicating the
gender of the individual. In this case, the coefficient represents the change in the
dependent variable associated with being female compared to male.
2. Age: The age of the individual, measured in years.
3. Edu_Basic (Basic Education): A dummy variable indicating whether the individ­
ual has basic education. The coefficient represents the change in the dependent
variable associated with having basic education (secondary education) compared
to those having primary education and to those who stated ”do not know” responses.
4. Edu_Vtrain (Vocational Training Education): A dummy variable indicating whether
the individual has vocational training education or not. The coefficient represents
the change in the dependent variable associated with having vocational training ed­
ucation compared to not having it.
5. Edu_High (Higher Education): A dummy variable indicating whether the individual
has higher education (higher than vocational training, includes vocational academic
training, having a bachelor’s degree, and having a master’s or a PhD degree). The
coefficient represents the change in the dependent variable associated with having
higher education compared to not having it.
6. Income_avg (per 1 million DKK) (Average Income per 1 million DKK): The aver­

them accordingly.
The study thus unveils a nuanced relationship between demographic, economic, and spa­
tial variables, shedding light on the complex dynamics shaping public perceptions of solar
energy adoption across different settings.

5
Discussion
5.1
Comparison of results to the preexisting literature
In comparing the research findings with the literature, a face­to­face examination reveals
both consistencies and variations in public attitudes towards PV installations. The prevail­
ing acceptance of PV panels on private rooftops aligns with several studies [1, 5], affirming
a global trend. The influence of age, where older individuals show lower acceptance, con­
tradicts previous research [6], but we have to take into account that this particular article
talks about a specific project with distinguishing particularities in the Swiss Alps and the
results may not translate to our study.
The role of gender in influencing acceptance, with males generally exhibiting higher levels,
is a common theme in [24], contrary to what we have seen in our results and in other
studies such as [6], where females exhibit higher levels of acceptance. Diverse studies
show differing connections between higher income and decreased acceptance levels, as
we can see in [1]. On the other hand, educational attainment consistently emerges as a
positive factor influencing acceptance [29], aligning with the results observed in this study.
The impact of existing PV ownership shows in [7], emphasizing the importance of first­
hand experience. The negative association we found between municipal PV share and
acceptance, clashes with findings in a study done in Germany [8] that concluded the op­
posite. The density effect within a 2 km radius observed in this study, where increased
density diminishes acceptance, contradicts with studies emphasizing the visual impact
and perceived environmental effects [1].
Notably, the spatial discrepancy in the influence of density within a 5 km radius, where den­
sity ceases to significantly influence acceptance, align with some literature [1], suggesting
nuanced regional dynamics. This comparison underscores the complexity of public atti­
tudes towards PV installations and highlights the need for a nuanced, context­aware ap­
proach in shaping future research and policy initiatives in renewable energy acceptance.
5.2
The role of income
The results indicate a significant negative correlation between income and acceptance of
PV installations in private rooftops in table 3. Respondents with higher incomes tend to
show less acceptance of PV installations in private rooftops compared to those with lower
incomes. This finding suggests that income may influence individuals’ attitudes towards
the proximity of solar installations, with higher­income individuals potentially exhibiting a
lower willingness to accept such installations near their residences.
A possible hypothesis for this observed pattern could be linked to aesthetic concerns or
property value perceptions associated with PV installations. Individuals with higher in­
comes may have more substantial investments in their homes, leading to increased sen­
sitivity towards changes in their immediate environment.
However, an intriguing variation arises when considering PV installations on industrial
rooftops. There is a reversal of the trend, showing that acceptance significantly increases
with higher average income. This unexpected shift in the income­acceptance relationship
for industrial rooftops warrants further investigation.A plausible hypothesis for this discrep­
ancy could be linked to different perceptions of industrial and private rooftop installations.
Higher­income individuals may view industrial rooftop installations more favorably, per­
ceiving them as contributing to broader economic development.

In summary, the role of income in shaping acceptance of PV installations appears nu­
anced and context­dependent, with variations observed between different distances and
types of installations. Thus making further investigation needed.
5.3
Owning PV and acceptance
The impact of personal PV ownership on the broader acceptance of PV installations
emerges as a noteworthy aspect of our study. Our findings reveal a substantial and sta­
tistically significant association between owning PV panels and increased acceptance
across all three settings – private rooftops, industrial buildings, and agricultural land. Indi­
viduals who have first­hand experience with PV technology through personal ownership
would show a more favorable attitude toward PV installations in general. This hypothe­
sis is based on the assumption that direct contact with solar energy generation fosters a
greater understanding and appreciation of its benefits, such as economic savings, envi­
ronmental awareness and energy independence. Our expectations were grounded in the
belief that those who have invested in PV panels for their homes would likely recognize
the positive impact of solar energy and be more accepting of its broader implementation.
The empirical evidence strongly supports our hypothesis, as respondents with personal
PV ownership consistently demonstrated heightened acceptance across all surveyed set­
tings. In conclusion, our study establishes a strong link between personal PV ownership
and increased acceptance.
5.4
Rural residency impact towards acceptance
The results show a notable influence of rural residence on the acceptance of PV installa­
tions in agricultural areas. The negative and significant coefficient for the ”Rural” variable
suggests that individuals living in rural settings exhibit lower acceptance of PV panels in
agricultural land. This points towards a potential impact of solar technology on the daily
lives of rural residents.
A possible hypothesis to explain this result has its origin in the lifestyle and priorities of
rural residents. Rural areas tend to have a closer link with agriculture, where land can be
used for crops or other traditional practices. The introduction of photovoltaic installations
in these spaces could be perceived as a disturbance of the existing landscape or daily
activities. Rural communities may place greater value on the aesthetic and environmental
aspects of their surroundings, and the visible presence of solar technology on agricultural
land could conflict with these values.
Conducting more qualitative studies or specific surveys of rural residents could help ex­
plore these hypotheses in more detail and provide information on the specific factors that
influence their lower acceptance of PV installations in agricultural areas.
5.5
PV share and acceptance
The findings suggest a significant negative correlation between the acceptance of PV
installations and the increasing relative share of private rooftop PV in the municipality.
This relationship is consistent for both private rooftops and agricultural land, implying that
respondents in municipalities with a higher proportion of PV installations are less accept­
ing of additional installations. The observed negative correlation raises questions about
the factors influencing respondents’ attitudes in regions with higher PV prevalence. One
possible hypothesis is that increased exposure to existing PV installations may lead to
concerns about visual aesthetics or perceived changes in the neighborhood. Respon­

dents in municipalities with a higher relative share of PV might be more aware of the
cumulative impact of solar installations influencing their opinions.
5.6
Acceptance threshold when taking distance into account
Intriguingly, the research findings reveal a notable shift in public acceptance of PV in­
stallations within different spatial contexts. Within a 2 km radius, a negative association
between increased density and decreased acceptance suggests heightened sensitivity to
the immediate visual impact and perceived environmental effects. However, beyond this
immediate proximity, within the 5 km radius, a shift occurs, marking a transition to positive
acceptance. This shift implies a potential threshold effect, suggesting that, beyond the ini­
tial 2 km radius, individuals may experience a change in perception. The positive trend
in acceptance beyond the 2 km radius could indicate a widening of acceptance thresh­
olds with increased geographic scope. This observation hints at a nuanced relationship
between spatial factors and public attitudes, emphasizing the importance of considering
contextual influences in understanding and predicting public acceptance of PV technol­
ogy.
While the current analysis provides valuable insights into the acceptance dynamics within
a 2 km radius and the 5 km radius, it is important to note that the exact variables for the
intermediate 3 km span are not available in the dataset. Therefore, the observed shift in
acceptance from negative within 2 km to not negative beyond 5 km is indicative but lacks
precise measurement for the 3 km range. The nuanced nature of this transition suggests
that it cannot be solely attributed to a linear extrapolation or subtraction of the 2 km radius
data. Instead, it necessitates a more comprehensive exploration of factors contributing
to this shift, which are currently unavailable in the dataset. This gap in data underscores
the need for further research and data collection efforts.

Limitations
In the course of conducting this research, several limitations were encountered that war­
rant consideration. These limitations may influence the generalizability of the study’s find­
ings.
This study has limitations related to the generalization of PV installations. Specifically,
the study primarily focuses on the general acceptance of PV installations without delving
into respondents’ preferences for specific projects, such as varying sizes and distances
from their residences. This omission may limit the depth of insights into preferences re­
garding installation characteristics, as individuals often exhibit varying inclinations based
on project­specific attributes, as we know from the literature [1][16].
Another limitation pertains to the representativeness of data. The study lacks national
statistical data for certain key variables, such as density of PV. The absence of compre­
hensive national statistics poses challenges in assessing the representativeness of the
study’s findings. Extrapolating results to a broader population may be limited, and caution
should be exercised in generalizing the findings beyond the scope of the available data.
Additionally, there is a limitation concerning limited information on PV installations. While
the study possesses information on the number of PV installations on private rooftops
and industrial buildings at the municipality level, details regarding the area, extent, and
distance to the nearest installations are not available. The lack of information on the size
of the installations weakens the analysis, preventing a comprehensive understanding of
the spatial distribution and density of PV systems in different municipalities. Furthermore,
the absence of information regarding the distance to the nearest installation debilitates our
interpretation of the results given the fact that we do not know if the nearest installation is
right in front of their residence or at the edge of our area of study.
6.1
Limitations in survey design
The research methodology recognizes potential limitations arising from the survey design,
particularly in terms of potential response biases attributed to self­reported data and con­
siderations regarding the placement of subsections and the Likert scale nature of stated
acceptance levels. While acknowledging these limitations, the study also highlights addi­
tional concerns such as potential response bias, the cross­sectional nature of the survey,
and reliance on self­reported data. These identified limitations were taken into account
when interpreting the survey results, ensuring a comprehensive understanding of the po­
tential challenges inherent in the research approach.
6.2
Use of Linear Regression with Discrete Variables
In this study, a limitation is evident in the use of linear regression for statistical analy­
sis, despite the inherently discrete nature of the dependent variable. As Acceptance is
assessed on a discrete scale ranging from 1 to 5, the use of linear regression on this dis­
crete dependent variable becomes apparent. Considering this, the ordered logit model
could present itself as alternative and potentially more appropriate approach. [30]
The implications of employing linear regression on discrete variables are notable. The
theoretical challenges arise from the potential misalignment between the assumptions of
linear models and the nature of certain variables[26]. To address this issue, consideration

approach. Such alternative models are better suited to handle the discrete nature of the

Taking into account these limitations, it is essential to interpret the results with a nuanced
understanding of the study’s scope and potential implications. Future research endeav­
ors may seek to address these limitations by incorporating more detailed data, employing
different statistical models, and expanding the geographical coverage of the study to en­

Conclusions
In this thesis we have addressed the acceptance of PV installation in three different set­
tings. Despite an extensive research base exploring attitudes toward the acceptance of
PV installations in distinct settings—private rooftops, industrial buildings, and agricultural
land—there is limited literature comprehensively addressing public attitudes across all
three settings. This research gap underscores the motivation behind this thesis, which
aims to investigate the relationship between public attitudes toward PV installations and
various spatial parameters.
These parameters include the number of PV installations in the municipality, the quantity
and size of solar farms, the density of said PV installations and the actual distance from
respondents’ homes to the installations. The investigation leverages different linear re­
gression models to analyze and understand the nuanced interplay between these spatial
factors and public attitudes toward PV installations in diverse settings.
In summary, our analysis sheds light on the intricate dynamics shaping public acceptance
of PV installations. When taking into consideration spatial variables with 2km distance
levels, agricultural land emerges as the least accepted location for PV installations, while
private and industrial rooftops exhibit comparable levels of acceptance. The nuanced
interplay of demographic variables changes and deepens our understanding. Gender
differences arise, with females consistently displaying higher acceptance, particularly ev­
ident in the case of agricultural land. Age emerges as a significant factor, indicating that
younger individuals are more inclined to accept PV installations with significance on pri­
vate rooftops. Educational attainment reveals an influence, with higher education levels
correlating with greater acceptance, especially for private and industrial settings. The role
of income proves complicated; while higher income negatively impacts acceptance of PV
on private rooftops, the reverse trend is observed when controlling for variables extending
the variable to 5 km. An intriguing discovery happened related to the ownership of electric
or hybrid cars, which significantly influences acceptance of agricultural land installations
within the 5 km radius.
Our study’s most significant contribution is shedding light on the impact of spatial charac­
teristics on the acceptance of PV installations. Concerning PV­Share, the results indicate
a noteworthy trend. Respondents residing in municipalities with a higher relative share
of PV, compared to those with a lower share, demonstrate a significant decrease in ac­
ceptance levels for PV installations on private rooftops and agricultural land as the share
of PV in the municipality increases. Surprisingly, the presence of one or more PV instal­
lations in the area did not yield significant results. Turning our attention to the density of
solar farms within a 2 km reachable area, a significant difference in acceptance levels for
private rooftop PV installations emerges. Respondents in areas with higher PV density
exhibit lower acceptance of private PV installations. This finding aligns with the principle
observed in the PV­Share variable, where increased density in solar farms within the de­
termined reachable area corresponds to a decrease in acceptance. The estimate values
emphasize the variability in acceptance for a one­unit change in the density of solar farms,
reinforcing the impact of spatial characteristics on PV acceptance.
In summary, this research illuminates the complex dynamics of public acceptance of PV
26

==PAGE:35==
installations, providing insights across diverse settings. Our findings underscore the sig­
nificance of demographic factors and spatial parameters. To conclude, our study has
vital implications for policy makers in Denmark. The insights derived from our research,
especially regarding the impact of spatial characteristics on public acceptance of PV in­
stallations, can significantly guide the planning of future PV projects. Policy makers can
build upon and use these results to strategically site PV installations, taking into account
observed preferences in different environments: private rooftops, industrial buildings, and
agricultural land. Understanding the interaction of demographic factors and spatial param­
eters allows for more appropriate decision making, encouraging greater public support for
renewable energy initiatives in Denmark. Our study is a valuable tool for policy makers
seeking to advance the integration of PV installations and promote the widespread adop­
tion of renewable energy in the country.
