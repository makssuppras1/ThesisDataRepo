This project presents results from simulating the operation of a hybrid power plant (HPP)
in the National Electricity Market in Australia. The simulated HPP was the Kennedy Energy
Park, the world’s ﬁrst utility-scale HPP which is expected to begin operation in 2019. This
project assumed the market was operating under rule changes currently being explored
by AEMO which would enable the registration of bi-directional-capable power plants as a
separate market category. The KEP has a battery of 2MW/4MWh which was simulated over
the ﬁrst week of February 2019 using model predictive control to take advantage of the half-
hourly forecasts published by AEMO. It was found a battery operated conservatively in the
Queensland region could yield positive revenue but under the assumptions applied in this
project, the return is not sufﬁcient to justify the investment.

1 Introduction
The National Electricity Market is an energy market which operates on the eastern seaboard
of Australia and services over 9.7 million customers via a transmission grid stretching from
Port Lincoln, South Australia to Port Douglas in Queensland. The transmission grid stretches
approximately 14,500 km and connects 5 different regions deﬁned by geographic boundaries:
Queensland, New South Wales, Victoria, South Australia and Tasmania (via the Basslink) [1]. In
the ﬁnancial year 2017 – 2018 203 TWh of electricity was traded through the NEM with a market
value of AUD 17 billion [2]. By region, New South Wales (NSW) consumes approximately 40%
the NEM’s total generation, Victoria (VIC) and Queensland (QLD) consume approximately
25% each [2].
Figure 1.1 – Map of the National Electricity Market.
The NEM’s generation mix has historically been comprised of a majority of conventional
generation with coal and gas being primary base generators in the market [3, 2]. Renewable
energy technologies have made headway in recent years. Penetration has been steady but
restricted mainly to South Australia and New South Wales which feature the most wind power
plants in Australia [2, 4]. Renewable generation has faced numerous difﬁculties, mainly from
2
==PAGE:12==
a lack of political momentum which has actively disincentivised investment thereby slowing
the need for the market operator to pursue policies which encourage the uptake of renewable
generation. However, in 2017 the Hornsdale Power Reserve (HPR) was constructed in South
Australia and started operation in the year’s forth quarter. The NEM had previously seen an
increase in market prices of 200% between 2010 and 2018 [5]. The causes were mainly tied to
a increasingly strategic bidding strategies by generators which had enough market power to
dictate market prices, especially in New South Wales, South Australia and Queensland [6, 7, 3].
In South Australia, the ancillary services market was dominated primarily by gas-ﬁred peaking
generators who used strategic capacity bidding practices to drive ancillary service prices high
during peak hour times [3, 8, 9].
Figure 1.2 – Rising consumer price index (CPI) of electricity prices when compared to other
commodities in Australia clearly shows a signiﬁcant increase between 1980 – 2016.
It was the ﬁrst battery system in the NEM and quickly proved its worth by drastically reducing
the cost of ancillary services to the market operator, allowing them to lift a mandatory capacity
reserve margin from South Australia and saving consumers approximately AUD 40 million
in the process [10]. The full impact of the HPR’s operation can be seen in Figure 1.3 which
clearly illustrates the drop in ancillary service prices since the battery system began operation
in 2017.
The success of the HPR in reducing market costs led to the construction of two additional
battery storage systems in South Australia [11] and has galvanised the Australian Energy Market
Operator (AEMO) to address the shortcomings in the current market processes. Current rules
were not designed for renewable generation and especially nor batteries [6, 12]. Battery
systems wishing to register in the NEM have to do so via an impractical and complicated

1.1
Hybrid Power Plants in the National Electricity Market
In addition to batteries a new concept called hybrid power plants (HPPs) has also been making
headway into the National Electricity Market. Hybrid power plants are deﬁned as a generating
system which functions as a single unit as seen from the perspective of the grid operator [14].
Basically, HPPs are a mix of generation technologies which are either co-located, meaning they
are connected to the same substation individually, or they are conﬁgured to share a common
dc bus which is then connected to the point of connection (PoC) which ties the system to the
grid [14]. HPPs offer a number of advantages, especially regarding renewable technologies,
having demonstrated the ability to reduce CAPEX and OPEX costs, reduce power losses and
improve the capacity factor of the aggregated technologies [14].
1.1.1
Kennedy Energy Park
The Kennedy Energy Park is the world’s ﬁrst, utility-scale HPP to be constructed [14] and is
being constructed near Hughenden in Queensland, Australia. The farm consists of 15 MW
solar PV, a 43 MW wind farm and a 2MW/4MWh battery system[15]. The project is currently
in Phase I and will be expanded to 1200 MW if the necessary transmission infrastructure is put
in place [4, 16]. The wind turbines are the Vestas V136 model. The battery storage system is
constructed using Tesla Power Pack 2s [15]. The ﬁnal project costs are AUD 160 million [15].
The plant’s system diagram is illustrated in Figure 1.4.
This timing of the project means it will enter operation shortly before the 5 minute settlement
changes are applied [17, 13].

1.2
Project Aim
The introduction of energy storage into the NEM by the HPR clearly motivated the market
operator to explore options of integrating more battery storage into the grid. Processes have
been set in motion which are clear signals that appropriate changes catering towards bi-
directional energy services will be made and take effect in the NEM in the near future. The
Kennedy Energy Park, as the world’s ﬁrst grid-scale HPP, coming into operation shortly before
the ﬁrst market rule change is applied, provides a unique opportunity to explore how and if a
hybrid power plant operating under the proposed rule changes is a feasible investment in the
NEM.
However, the thesis is limited in the application of practical market data regarding the plant
under study.
The sections following will outline the Project’s assumptions and its limitations.
1.3
Project Scope
This project scope included three core components: (1) understanding the underlying market
rules which dictate a producer’s actions in the market, (2) design and build a model which
would feasibly interpret the operating conditions of the NEM into a simulation environment
and (3) perform an investigation to conclude if operating a hybrid power plant in the Australian
National Electricity Market was feasible.
1. The project focused its investigation on whether the operation of a battery storage
system could lead to sufﬁcient revenues to make its investment feasible.
2. The project investigated the operation of the hybrid plant under the most plausible
market conditions being considered for bi-directional resource providers by the market
operator.
3. Queensland market prices were used in the simulation to more accurately represent

2.1
Battery Storage in the National Electricity Market
Energy storage is a relatively recent addition to the energy mix in the national electricity
market, as stated by Mackenzie and Harley in 2018 who presented the practical considerations
of operating battery storage systems in the current NEM [21]. The Hornsdale Power Reserve
(HPR) was installed in 2017. According to mackenzie and Harley it has cornered 11% and 10%
of the ancillary services market in South Australia as a generator and load respectively while
constituting only 2% of the region’s total regional capacity. It is registered as both a generator
and as a load as the NEM’s registration processes were not designed for battery storage systems.
Furthermore, Mackenzie and Harley conﬁrmed the ”assumption” that the market settlement
rules were changing. Similar sentiments were echoed by Aurecon in the impact study of the
Hornsdale Power Reserve [10]. Aurecon’s impact study found that HPR provides very rapid
response regulation FCAS signals and has shown the ability to track AEMO’s setpoint more
accurately than conventional generators which can lag the AGC signal by up to several minutes
[10]. Aurecon produced Figure 1.3 which clearly illustrates the decrease in ancillary service
prices since the battery system has entered the market. The South Australian market is also
a prime example of a concentrated market with large, conventional generators who have
historically exercised their market power to drive up ancillary service costs during those times
where interconnection constraints between South Australia and New South Wales have split
that market from the NEM (as concluded by Mohammadian, 2015, and Aurecon, 2018) [10, 3].
The main outcome of the HPR was AEMO’s decision to move ahead with introducing a new
market category which allows registering and integrating more battery systems into the NEM
which is catered towards battery system’s bi-directional capabilities. The AEMC is encouraging
in its response and made several recommendations in its Frequency Control Frameworks
Review [10]. In its report, the AEMC supported AEMO’s aim to integrate more battery storage
into the NEM in order to pursue fast frequency response markets. It is thus conclusive that
battery technologies will be further integrated into the NEM [22].
As a ﬁnal point, Aurecon concludes that the introduction of the 5 minute settlement period
being introduced in 2021, will beneﬁt battery storage systems due to their fast response times
in comparison to conventional generators [10]. Furthermore, retiring conventional generators
will leave a gap in the market which will need to be plugged for AEMO to meet its reliability
and stability requirements. The evidence provided in the presented articles as well as AEMO’s

2.2
Impact of gaming practices of generators rebidding in the NEM
Mohammadian (2015) covers the exercising of market power by generators in the NEM and
provides examples of prior art which covered this area, such as Hu and Batten (2005). For
example, Hu and Batten concluded that generators are more likely to use capacity offers as
strategic tools to control market prices, proving a link between large generators and market
power [9]. The conclusion Mohammadian presented from these reports was that generators
could have exercised market power leading to volatile market prices but that other factors such
as high fuel costs and demand would contribute as well. This conclusion was conﬁrmed by the
Australian Energy Market Commission (AEMC) in 2018 who found that gaming by generators
in concentrated markets was a leading cause of volatile and high prices in the NEM [8]. Mo-
hammadian, 2015, examined rebidding behaviour by generators during peak demand hours
and concluded that the activity was indicative of gaming behaviour and most likely resulted in
volatile market prices. Furthermore, the market rules AEMO and market participants follow
do not actively disincentivise gaming behaviour. The only steps AEMO seems to have taken
against such strategies is to introduce a good faith policy whereby generators operate under
the assumption they submit their rebids with a clean, moral conscience [8]. While this project
does not set out to explore this topic in detail, it is important to be aware that price volatility
does exist which would lead to higher inaccuracy in some of the central forecasts provided by
AEMO. Especially as the AEMC, Mohammadian and David and Hu concluded that rebidding
practices within the late rebidding period contribute the most to price volatility. This will be
an issue moving forward in this project as this project proposes a model operating at the start
of this rebidding period.
Additional causes to high prices in the NEM trace back to a legislative loophole when the NEM
was ﬁrst founded which allowed state network operators to recuperate any infrastructure
spending from consumers. The excessive and unnecessary spending, following reports in
[6], [23], [24] and [25]. Furthermore, the high prices in Tasmania are most likely related to
the Basslink interconnector, which ties Tasmania to the Victoria region, becoming congested
and splitting the market. The Basslink is the only interconnector connecting Tasmania to the
mainland, making it very susceptible to congestion [26].

3 National Electricity Market
3.1
Market Overview
The market’s operation is governed by the National Electricity Rules (NER) which are made
by, updated and maintained by the Australian Energy Market Commission (AEMC). The NER
dictates the roles, responsibilities and the market processes which must be followed by the
market participants. The AEMC consults the federal government on energy policy and has the
responsibility of translating the national electricity objective (NEO) into any rule changes
applied to the NER. The NEO is one of three tenets which governs the decisions made by the
AEMC. The NEO obligates the AEMC:
“to promote efﬁcient investment in, and efﬁcient operation and use of, electricity services for the
long term interests of consumers of electricity with respect to:
• price, quality, safety and reliability and security of supply of electricity
• the reliability, safety and security of the national electricity system." [30]
Besides making the NER, the AEMC also sets the market price thresholds meant to protect
consumers from unfair electricity prices. This includes setting a price cap on the maximum
and minimum price levels allowed in the NEM as well as a price trigger AEMO uses to clamp
market prices at a deﬁned level (see Section 3.3.4 for further details). The AEMC’s main role
therefore is to lay down laws which translate Australia’s national energy goals into practical
rule set which serves as the framework for a competitive and consumer-friendly market. This
rule set is enforced by the National Energy Regulator (NER) whose jurisdiction only extends
to those regions who have commenced the National Electricity Rules [31]. The NER has the
power to investigate market participants, set the maximum revenue networks may recover
from their customers and monitor and report on the competitiveness in the market [32]. The
Australian Energy Market Operator (AEMO) manages the day-to-day management of the
market according to the rules laid out by the AEMC in the NERs. This includes managing the
spot market, ancillary services and short-term transmission planning via system adequacy
forecasts [33]. Figure 3.1 illustrates the market hierarchy.
Besides the three stakeholders mentioned above, the Australian government also instructed
the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC), Australia’s competition
watchdog, to monitor and report on market prices in the NEM in response to higher than usual

3.3.1
Settlement Process
The NEM consists of 9 energy markets: the spot or wholesale market on which dispatch bids
and offers are matched and 8 ancillary services markets for frequency balance (FCAS). The
market is split into trading days which each begin and end at 04:00. The spot market opens at
18
==PAGE:28==
3.3. Market Settlement
12:30PM two days before a respective trading day and closes 12:30PM the day before. Market
participants are able to submit rebids (see below) between market open and 5 minutes before
dispatch.
The NEM is a limited nodal market in that all wholesale transactions on the spot market are
settled at one of ﬁve regional reference prices (RRP). Market participants receive and pay at the
RRP adjusted by an annually calculated loss factor called the marginal loss factor (MLF) which
represents the transmission losses between the participants’ speciﬁc point of connection and
the regional reference node (RRN) [37]. The RRN is a speciﬁc point in the region’s network
whose marginal cost of supply is the RRP [37]. Generators can hedge interregional exposures
by acquiring interregional settlement residue (IRSR) units which are offered through quarterly
auctions by AEMO [37]. IRSR units fall into two categories: intra- and inter-regional. Both arise
as a result of the difference between what participants pay AEMO and AEMO pays participants
for spot market transactions [38]. Participants may bid for entitlements to a proportion of the
total settlement residues at the quarterly auction held by AEMO [38]. For more information
on settlement residues the reader is directed to [39] which contains all information pertinent
to understanding the process.
As mentioned, AEMO also manages eight ancillary service markets for frequency control.
Generators who are approved for ancillary service registration are allowed to bid on these
markets [40, 19]. AEMO’s dispatch engine co-optimises the ancillary and dispatch bids from
ancillary service generators [37]. Network control and system restart ancillary services are
procured by AEMO through contracts with participants [37]. The FCAS market is outside the
scope of this project.
Bidding under the current ruleset
Generators submit bids on the spot market, which are then cleared via merit order. Generators
submit bid stacks which are a series of 10, monotonically increasing price levels each of which
speciﬁes the price demanded by the generator for increasing available capacity. The ﬁrst
bid stack submitted to the spot market sets the price levels each producer is bound to for
that trading day. Generators are allowed to submit rebids in response to changes in available
capacity, energy constraints etc up until 5 minutes before dispatch [40]. When submitting a
rebid, the market participant is allowed to change only the offered quantity, not the prices
they previously submitted [40, 3]. The minimum and maximum price bands do not exceed
the market ﬂoor price or the market price cap when scaled by the participant’s respective
loss factor [41]. The loss factor is a scaling factor applied to the price each generator receives
and each customer pays and captures the electrical losses between the point of connection
and the regional reference node. Furthermore, capacities offered in each price band, for
both generators and loads, must be integer MW values [41]. Table 3.1 shows the capacity

3.3.3
Imbalance process overview
Participants are penalised for imbalances via a causer pays process whereby AEMO allocates a
proportion of the total imbalance costs to each market participant via a contribution factor.
To calculate imbalance costs, AEMO assumes each dispatched generator’s setpoint moves
in a straight line between each dispatch intervals, as shown in Figure 3.11. The deviations
from the straight-line reference are measured in 4 second intervals and are used to calculate
each participant’s contribution to system imbalance during a single DI. The aggregate costs
accumulated over each trading day are then incorporated in the process calculating the ﬁnal
compensation each participant receives at the end of a billing period [44]. Contribution factors
(CFs) are only calculated indiviudally for market generators, market customers and market
small generation aggregators. Non-market units, market non-scheduled units, market network
services and residual demand from loads without appropriate metering is all captured in the
regional (residual) CF [45].
The full process for calculating the contribution factor is given in [46]. This section summarises
the main concept.
As shown in Figure 3.11, AEMO expects each participant to move linearly between dispatch tar-
gets and samples actual dispatch data every 4 seconds. Each sample is categorised according
to if the participant was helping or hindering the system and if the participant was scheduled
to provide an ancillary service at that time. Furthermore, individual deviations are scaled
by the frequency indicator (FI), which indicates the system’s state and is used to determine
if the participant is helping or hindering the market at the sampling time. Participants can
not be paid twice for helping the system if they had been previously scheduled to offer such
services during the sampled period. A similar process is applied to calculate interconnector
losses, residual demand components. The ﬁnal contribution factor is then applied to allocate

An Administered Price Period is triggered for each of the following periods in a region when:
1. a trading interval, where the sum of the last 336 TIs’ (equivalent to a full week) spot

day. The AFP does not apply to ancillary services as these can never have negative pricing.

the National Electricity Market, 2018, "An administered price period ends at 0400 hours if, at
that time, the cumulative price (as calculated from dispatch prices without capping or ﬂooring)

Pre-dispatch and Forecasting Services
This section covers the pre-dispatch services offered by AEMO. Special attention is paid to the

Pre-dispatch services are forecasts on the power system’s future status. There are three fore-

3.4.1
P5 forecasts
P5 reports are published every 5 minutes and are immediately available to both the public and
producers at their time of publication [44, 50], in accordance with the NER section 3.8.20 [40].
The P5 report contains market forecasts for the next hour and includes (1) market dispatch
and ancillary service prices, (2) regional demand forecasts, (3) interconnection capacity, ﬂow
and loss forecasts and (4) system constraint forecasts. Unlike the P30 report, covered in the
next section, the P5 report only covers the hour following the publication time. Thus, it offers
producers a real-time opportunity to adjust their operational schedules for the short term.
3.4.2
P30 forecasts
The P30 forecast predicts N number of settlement intervals ahead and is released every half
hour on the half hour. N is the forecast horizon which depends on when the report is released.

4 Designing a Market-adequate Model
4.1
Model Predictive Control Theory
Model predictive control is a means of optimising a plant’s actions via the optimisation of the
plant’s predicted behaviour/states and the application of control actions resulting from the
optimisation [51, 52]. Typically, a model predictive controller is used in the means illustrated in
Figure 4.1 wherein relevant system states are input to the optimisation model, the optimisation
is run over the prediction horizon and the control actions are implemented over the control
horizon. The prediction is the lengths of time for which states are forecast. The control
horizon is the length of time over which the optimised control signals u∗are applied to the
system. Typically, only the ﬁrst set of control inputs are applied to the system while the rest
are discarded.
Figure 4.1 – Model Predictive Control applies optimised control actions discretised timesteps
over a control horizon.
At each discrete timestep, the optimisation process repeats itself. The system is therefore
always operating with updated information about the system states. MPC was thought the
optimal choice for implementing in the Australian NEM as the P30 forecasts were published
by the market operator at the start of each trading interval and the rebidding rules incentivised
market operators to rebid at the latest-possible opportunity to take full advantage of the
up-to-date information.

6 Conclusion
6.1
Conclusion
The installation of the Hornsdale Power Reserve enticed AEMO to pursue policies which would
allow the effective integration of bi-directional power plants including the introduction of a
5 minute settlement period and the introduction of a separate registration category speciﬁc
to bi-directional plants. This project operated under the assumptions these changes were in
effect and modelled the operation of the Kennedy Energy Park (KEP) in Queensland. Due to a
lack in available production data from KEP only the battery system was able to modelled.
The battery system has a capacity of 2MW/4MWh hour. The CAPEX was assumed to be AUD
5.32 million as a proportional division according to the capacity of the plant’s constituent
technologies. Furthermore, the plant was assumed to have a 15 year operational lifetime based
on information provided by [18] which utilised the same Tesla Power Pack 2 to construct the
largest battery storage facility in Colorado.
Price data for the entire 2018 and the ﬁrst week of February 2019 was analysed. It was found
that there was a noticeable difference in price behaviour between weekday and weekend.
Weekdays had two price periods (1) between 06:00 and 07:30 and (2) between 15:30 and
17:30. Weekends only had one signiﬁcant price period between 15:30 and 17:30. Furthermore,
Queensland prices were relatively stable with little difference in price levels between trading
intervals. Expectations were thus set that the battery would be operated primarily in the peak
price periods and that market activity outside these periods would be concentrated in the
trading intervals leading up to the morning peak.
The model was implemented using model predictive control which used a rolling horizon
of varying length, set by the P30 forecast published at the start of each trading interval. The
model optimised u1(i +1 and u2(i +1) and applied these during the respective TI. This was
done to align with market rules mandating rebids in the late rebidding period.
The model was implemented in two stages: (1) yielded only negative revenues and it was
found the battery was operated erratically within the shoulder periods and that the model did
not discriminate heavily enough against price differences between TIs. (2) Attached a penalty
cost of 80 AUD/cycle which led to the battery operation being limited to the expected price
periods, as identiﬁed by the price analysis. The revenues were positive, generating AUD 179.98
over the simulated week. However, a discounted cashﬂow analysis showed the battery would
not be able to make enough return to justify this investment.
