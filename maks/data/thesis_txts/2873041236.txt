Abstract
English
This thesis provides a comprehensive mapping of the innovation and entrepreneurship
ecosystem in Icelandic higher education, with a special focus on the most relevant courses
offered at Icelandic universities.
Through the collection and analysis of course infor-
mation, interviews with key stakeholders, and literature reviews, this research uncovers
and analyzes the driving forces behind these courses, examining how their objectives,
expected outcomes, and broader institutional visions align with established theoretical
frameworks and emerging trends in innovation and entrepreneurship education. By situ-
ating these findings within existing literature and relevant theories, the study offers insights
into the current market and evolving needs for innovation and entrepreneurship education
in Iceland. Ultimately, this research contributes to a deeper understanding of how univer-
sities can refine and expand their educational initiatives, thereby strengthening Iceland’s
capacity for innovation and entrepreneurial growth.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Context
Innovation and entrepreneurship are widely recognized as drivers of economic growth and
societal advancement in the modern “knowledge society” [1]. Over the past few decades,
universities worldwide have increasingly integrated innovation and entrepreneurship edu-
cation into their curricula in response to global economic shifts and the need for adaptable,
opportunity-oriented students [2], [3]. This global trend reflects a broader acknowledg-
ment that cultivating entrepreneurial mindsets and innovation skills among students can
enhance a nation’s capacity for creativity, employment generation, and long-term com-
petitiveness [4], [5].
Within this global context, Iceland presents a unique innovation ecosystem that benefits
from a highly educated population and significant investment in research and develop-
ment [6]. At the same time, Iceland’s small population and insular environment create a
distinctive setting for entrepreneurship: a close-knit community with strong informal net-
works but also limited domestic markets and resources. The Icelandic government has
explicitly emphasized that innovation is “not a luxury but a necessity” for the nation’s fu-
ture prosperity [7]. This policy vision underlines the importance of developing human
capital with innovative and entrepreneurial skills. In light of these factors, examining how
Iceland’s higher education system is supporting innovation and entrepreneurship is both
relevant and consequential.
1.2
Problem Statement
In recent years, Iceland has formulated national innovation strategies that articulate broad
goals for the year 2030, including adapting to the Fourth Industrial Revolution, addressing
environmental and demographic challenges, and building five fundamental pillars of an in-
novative society: mindset, finance, market access, framework, and human capital. Within
these strategy documents, the role of the education system, particularly universities, is
highlighted under the “mindset” pillar, noting the need to cultivate an innovative mindset
among students and to increase the visibility of innovation role models in academia [7].
However, translating these high-level policy goals into practice within universities remains
a challenge. The Icelandic innovation ecosystem has historically relied heavily on indirect
incentives, such as R&D tax credits, to stimulate innovation in industry [8], and a coordi-
nated approach to entrepreneurship education has been less noticeable. A 2017 study by
the Nordic Council of Ministers highlighted that Iceland lacked a coherent national policy
for entrepreneurship education, with efforts essentially being driven by individual institu-
tions rather than a unified strategy [9]. This suggests a potential misalignment between
national innovation policies, which stress the importance of developing innovative mind-
sets, and the higher education strategies and course offerings that are actually in place.
In other words, it is unclear whether Icelandic universities are offering courses and pro-
grams in innovation and entrepreneurship that adequately support the country’s innova-
tion policy objectives. Addressing this gap requires a systematic mapping and analysis of
the current innovation and entrepreneurship courses across Iceland’s universities. There-
fore, the core problem driving this research is the need to understand the landscape of
innovation and entrepreneurship courses offered in Icelandic higher education.

1.3
Research Objective and Questions
Given the above context, the primary research objective of this thesis is to map and evalu-
ate the landscape of innovation and entrepreneurship education (I&E) in Icelandic higher
education and use established theoretical frameworks to categorize and assess the na-
ture of these course offerings. In particular, the study uses the ”About, For, Through”
framework of entrepreneurship education as an analytical lens. This framework distin-
guishes between teaching ”about” entrepreneurship (focusing on theoretical knowledge
about entrepreneurial concepts), teaching ”for” entrepreneurship (developing skills for en-
trepreneurship, such as business planning and management), and teaching ”through” en-
trepreneurship (providing experiential learning through actual entrepreneurial activities or
venture creation) [10]. By applying this framework, the research aims to catalog the ex-
isting courses while also understanding why and how they are taught (i.e., whether the
emphasis is on theory, skill-building, or learning by doing).
From this objective, the following research questions and sub-questions have been for-
mulated to guide the study:
Main Research Question:
• What is the landscape of innovation and entrepreneurship education in Icelandic
universities?
Sub-questions:
• How are innovation and entrepreneurship (I&E) courses distributed across the frame-
works of teaching ”About, For, Through” entrepreneurship?
• What limitations or imbalances exist in the current distribution of I&E courses?
• Why do these limitations or imbalances (structural, cultural, or institutional) persist
within Icelandic higher education?
1.4
Significance of the Study
This study is significant from both academic and practical perspectives. Academically,
it contributes to the literature on innovation and entrepreneurship education by providing
a detailed case study of a national education ecosystem through the lens of established
frameworks. Prior research on entrepreneurship education has often highlighted the need
for context-specific mappings and evaluations to understand how global trends manifest
locally [11]. By focusing on Iceland, this thesis addresses a knowledge gap in the im-
plementation of entrepreneurship education in small economies. It tests the applicability
of the ”About, For, Through” framework and offers insights into how well this framework
captures the nuances of course design and objectives in Icelandic universities.
Practically, the study holds value for educators, university administrators, and policymak-
ers in Iceland (and potentially in similar economies). Strengthening entrepreneurship ed-
ucation has been recommended in international assessments as a means to boost in-
novation capacity. The OECD has emphasized the importance of expanding practical,
experience-based learning to develop an ”innovation-rich” skill base in Iceland [12]. By
identifying the existing gaps between current academic offerings and established theoret-
ical ideals, this research provides guidance for strengthening the role of higher education
in the Icelandic innovation ecosystem. Thereby contributing to both improved educational
outcomes and the broader goal of strengthening Iceland’s capacity for innovation and en-
trepreneurial growth.

Literature Review
2.1
Innovation and Entrepreneurship Education: Concepts
and Evolution
In recent decades, innovation and entrepreneurship education have become foundational
components of higher education reform when reflecting a global shift toward cultivating
adaptable, opportunity-oriented graduates [2]. While innovation and entrepreneurship
education are often used in the same sentence, they remain distinct in both purpose and
pedagogical orientation. Innovation education primarily focuses on cultivating creative
thinking and problem-solving skills, as well as generating solutions and technologies. It
encompasses cognitive and behavioral competencies linked to ideation and interdisci-
plinary collaboration [13]. Entrepreneurship education, on the other hand, has traditionally
focused on preparing individuals to identify, assess, and exploit business opportunities,
typically through skills in venture creation, risk management, and market analysis [2].
This distinction, however, is increasingly blurred in contemporary curricula. For instance,
education for innovation often includes entrepreneurial components such as opportunity
recognition and business modeling [14][15]. Similarly, entrepreneurship education has
evolved beyond business startups to encompass entrepreneurial mindset, social innova-
tion, and intrapreneurship [3][16]. Prior studies suggest that effective education in these
fields requires shifting from isolated content delivery to cultivating a dynamic learning pro-
cess focused on the interaction between individuals and opportunities [17].
In Icelandic higher education, these conceptual overlaps make mapping efforts complex
but also rich in analytical potential. The ”About, For, Through” framework adopted in this
thesis responds to this complexity by examining not only what is taught but also why
and how it is taught. This framework aligns the intent and methods of innovation and
entrepreneurship education with broader institutional and societal contexts.
2.1.1
Historical Evolution and Global Trends in Innovation and
Entrepreneurship Education
The rise of entrepreneurship and innovation education can be situated within broader
socio-economic transformations. As Peter Drucker argued (1994), we are living through a
societal shift where knowledge has become the primary factor of production. This ”knowl-
edge society” is characterized by the emergence of new workforces, new organizational
forms, and, above all, the centrality of education systems in preparing individuals not only
for employment but for lifelong adaptability and contribution. Drucker highlighted how his-
torical transitions such as the Industrial Revolution and the emergence of the university
system have reshaped the expectations and functions of education. Today, the emphasis
is on preparing knowledge workers and encouraging the capacity to create, apply, and
diffuse innovation [1].
This historical trend is reflected in the global expansion of entrepreneurship education.
However, despite the field’s academic legitimacy, concerns persist regarding fragmen-
tation and conceptual clarity [3][17]. Scholars have noted that while entrepreneurship
education initially focused on venture creation and business management, it has since
diversified into areas such as social entrepreneurship, corporate innovation, and cre-
ative industries [16][4]. Meanwhile, innovation education has emerged in response to
the demands of the knowledge economy, emphasizing interdisciplinary collaboration and

problem-based learning [18][5]. Institutions now strive to create innovation ecosystems
through incubators, cross-disciplinary labs, and partnerships with industry [19].
In the Nordic context, and particularly in Iceland, this evolution is influenced by global
paradigms yet shaped by local conditions, including scale, policy, and educational cul-
ture. Drucker’s framing of education as a societal transformer remains relevant. Icelandic
institutions are not only responding to economic needs but also helping to shape civic
identities and organizational norms in a rapidly evolving society [1]. This response can be
reflected through the National Science and Innovation Strategy, where the government
emphasizes that innovation is ”not a luxury but a necessity” for Iceland’s future prosperity
[7].
2.1.2
Shift in Emphasis from Theoretical Learning Toward Practical and
Experiential Methodologies
A significant development in both innovation and entrepreneurship education has been the
pedagogical shift from passive, theory-driven instruction to experiential, practice-oriented
learning. This transformation reflects the influence of constructivist learning theories and
growing recognition of the complexity of entrepreneurial behavior [4][15][17].
Studies reviewed in this thesis underscore the importance of learning entrepreneurship
by doing, not just learning about it [20][21]. Programs that enable students to create
actual ventures, engage in prototyping, or collaborate with stakeholders have a greater
impact on the development of entrepreneurial intention and capability. For instance, the
“Start-a-Business” experiment, documented by Vincett and Farlow (2008), demonstrated
that eliminating rigid coursework in favor of business launch activities led to both high
satisfaction and real-world outcomes [20].
Additionally, OECD reports and other studies emphasize problem-based learning and par-
ticipatory teaching behaviors as essential for cultivating innovation skills. Problem-based
learning challenges students with authentic, complex problems that require interdisci-
plinary analysis, critical thinking, and iterative experimentation. Rather than focusing on
predetermined answers, it encourages discovery-driven analysis that mirrors real innova-
tion processes. Teaching behaviors such as facilitating open dialogue, guiding collabora-
tive projects, and engaging students in shared decision-making can help create dynamic
and inclusive learning environments. These approaches not only stimulate motivation
and deeper engagement but also support the development of adaptable, entrepreneurial
mindsets [18][5].
However, this shift is not universally successful. Some experimental programs, such as
the Dutch mini-company model studied by Oosterbeek et al. (2010), have shown no
significant positive impact on entrepreneurial intention and, in some cases, even negative
effects. This discrepancy points to the importance of program design, mentorship quality,
and contextual alignment [22].
2.2
Frameworks for Entrepreneurship Education: ”About,”
”For,” and ”Through”
Entrepreneurship education can be delivered with different pedagogical goals, and in
this research, they will be classified into three categories: educating ”about,” ”for,” and
”through” entrepreneurship. This” About, For, Through” framework was first articulated
by Jamieson (1984), who distinguished (a) education about enterprise, (b) education for
enterprise, and (c) education in enterprise [10]. Similarly, Middleton and Donnellon (2014)
defined a framework where educating ”about” is referred to as ”know-what, educating ”for”

is referred to as ”know-how,” and educating ”through” is referred to as ”know-why” [23].
Figure 2.1 represents the three approaches that correspond to different primary learning
outcomes: Knowledge, Skills, and Attitudes (KSA). Kakouris and Liargovas (2021) ex-
plicitly frame their analysis of the”About, For, Through” approaches around these KSA
dimensions. Each approach prioritizes one discipline: cognitive knowledge in ”about,”
behavioral skills in ”for,” and affective attitudes in ”through.” Although in practice, a com-
prehensive entrepreneurship program may blend elements of all three [10]. Recognizing
the KSA alignment reinforces the idea that a balanced entrepreneurship education could
benefit from integrating all three approaches to address what students know, can do, and
believe or value concerning entrepreneurship.

and Attitudes: (a) “About” (b) “For” and (c) “Through” [10].
In later studies, the framework has been distinguished into three instructional orientations,
characterized by content focus, teaching methods, and intended outcomes. The definition
of the three frameworks can be described as follows: education ”about” entrepreneurship
focuses on imparting knowledge about the entrepreneurial process, education ”for” en-
trepreneurship targets the skills needed for venture creation, and education ”through”
entrepreneurship involves learning by engaging in real entrepreneurial activity [10], [21],
[24]. This section reviews the meaning of each category and examines supporting liter-
ature before comparing the framework to Neck and Greene’s (2011) ”three knowns” in
entrepreneurship education [4].
2.2.1
Education ”About” Entrepreneurship
Education ”about” entrepreneurship refers to teaching about the entrepreneurial phe-
nomenon in a theoretical or conceptual manner. The goal of this approach is to build
students’ knowledge of entrepreneurship as a subject. This approach is generally de-
scribed as education about enterprise, covering the theoretical aspects of venture cre-
ation and business management and providing learners with a foundation of concepts
and facts [21]. In other words, educating ”about” entrepreneurship is the most theoreti-
cal and content-driven approach. Its primary goal is to impart general knowledge about
the entrepreneurial phenomenon, like the characteristics of entrepreneurs, the role of en-
trepreneurship in the economy, and foundational business concepts such as the basics
of marketing, finance, business planning, and overviews of how businesses are started
and run. Courses in this category typically adopt traditional teaching methods, lectures,
textbooks, and exams and portray students as ”passive receptacles of information” [10],
[21], [24], [25].
The intended outcome is that learners ”know-what” entrepreneurship is [23], attaining a
broad awareness of entrepreneurial concepts and context. In terms of the KSA frame-

work, education ”about” entrepreneurship primarily develops knowledge (K). It builds a
cognitive base and understanding but typically does not aim to actively shape students’
skills or attitudes toward starting a venture. As a result, this approach alone is often con-
sidered insufficient to produce entrepreneurs, though it provides valuable foundational
knowledge [25]. Its strength lies in contextualizing entrepreneurship in society and inspir-
ing interest, while its limitation is a lack of practical application or personal transformation
for learners.
In other words, the ”about” approach treats entrepreneurship as an academic subject to
study rather than a practice to engage in. While this approach can build awareness and
introduce key theories or frameworks, it often falls short of developing the skills or mindset
necessary to launch a venture. Indeed, purely informative courses about entrepreneur-
ship often leave students in a passive learning mode and may not significantly affect
their entrepreneurial intentions [4], [26]. Piperopoulos and Dimov (2015) provide empir-
ical support for the limitations of theory-heavy instruction. In their study, a traditionally
structured, lecture-based entrepreneurship course (oriented ”about” entrepreneurship)
had relatively muted effects on students’ entrepreneurial motivation. Students with higher
entrepreneurial self-efficacy in the theory-oriented class showed lower subsequent start-
up intentions [21]. This contrasts strongly with more practical courses, suggesting that an
overemphasis on abstract knowledge about entrepreneurship may not inspire action (or
could even deter confident students from pursuing entrepreneurship) if not balanced with
experiential elements.
2.2.2
Educating ”For” Entrepreneurship
Education ”for” entrepreneurship covers the teaching that is designed for the purpose of
preparing learners to become entrepreneurs with the skills and knowledge required to
start and grow a business [10]. In theoretical terms, education ”for” enterprise provides
would-be entrepreneurs with practical training and the ”know-how” to set up and run a
venture [23]. The curriculum typically includes applied topics such as opportunity iden-
tification, business planning, financing new ventures, marketing for start-ups, and other
hands-on competencies [10]. Unlike the broad theoretical orientation of the ”about” ap-
proach, education ”for” entrepreneurship is explicitly aimed at venture creation outcomes
and producing entrepreneurs or new businesses.
Pedagogically, the ”for” approach is often linked with a Vocational Education and Train-
ing (VET) paradigm [10]. The philosophy is one of learning by doing, but within a guided
training context, where students practice entrepreneurial tasks such as writing business
plans, pitching ideas, or managing small projects under the supervision of instructors in
order to build specific competencies. This approach is inherently more skill-centered.
The educator often takes on the role of a coach or mentor, and assessment might in-
volve practical project work or simulations rather than purely academic exams. Within the
KSA classification, it primarily develops skills (S) [10]. The expected learning outcomes
include increased entrepreneurial self-efficacy, hands-on experience in venture creation
processes, and the ability to apply entrepreneurial methods in real-world scenarios.
It is worth noting that education for entrepreneurship often implicitly also covers much of
the ”about” content, but always with a view toward application. For instance, a ”for” ori-
ented course might teach financial accounting (knowledge), but mainly as needed for
writing a financial plan (skill).
The philosophical underpinning is more pragmatic and
outcome-driven than the ”about” approach. Hannon (2005) observed that many higher
education programs traditionally focused on teaching ”about” or ”for” entrepreneurship,
aiming to convey knowledge and skills, but often did not yet fully address deeper attitudi-

nal or transformative aspects. By focusing on expertise, the ”for” approach aims to directly
increase the number of entrepreneurs as a key metric of success [25]. However, critics
have pointed out that even skill-focused training may fall short of supporting the adaptive,
resilient mindset needed for entrepreneurial success in uncertain environments [21]. This
concern leads to the third category, ”through,” which pushes the pedagogical approach
further into experiential and developmental territory.
2.2.3
Educating ”Through” Entrepreneurship
Education ”through” entrepreneurship refers to learning experiences where students de-
velop entrepreneurial capabilities by engaging in actual entrepreneurial activities. In this
approach, the entrepreneurial process itself becomes the learning method. Rather than
only learning about or training for entrepreneurship, students learn through doing, typically
by initiating real or simulated ventures, projects, or other entrepreneurial engagements as
part of their education [21]. In modern usage, educating ”through” entrepreneurship has
expanded to include experiential learning for students who may not yet be business own-
ers, for instance, venture creation programs, student start-up incubators, or project-based
courses where students launch a product or service within an academic setting.
The core idea of teaching ”through” entrepreneurship is that by actually experiencing the
uncertainties, iterations, and creativity of entrepreneurship, students develop entrepreneurial
thinking and mindsets that cannot be effectively taught via theory alone [10]. Here, the role
of the educator is to design or facilitate contexts in which students become entrepreneurs
(even if on a small scale or temporarily) and to guide reflective learning from those expe-
riences. The classroom might resemble an incubator or studio, and failure or pivoting in
projects becomes a rich source of learning. In terms of Middleton and Donnellon’s (2014)
terminology, the ”through” approach cultivates the ”know-why,” a deeper understanding of
one’s motivations, identity, and purpose in entrepreneurial action [23]. It targets the attitu-
dinal (A) domain of learning, such as developing initiative, resilience, creative confidence,
and the ability to navigate ambiguity [10].
The pedagogical goals of ”through” entrepreneurship education are thus more ambitious
in scope. Rather than simply conveying knowledge or skills, this approach aims to change
the mindset or behavior. Students learn to think and act like entrepreneurs by perform-
ing and reflecting on entrepreneurial tasks. Kakouris and Liargovas (2021) note that the
”through” approach is the most inclusive and transformative of the three, as it can ap-
ply to learners across disciplines, not just business students, and seeks to fundamentally
transform their attitudes toward uncertainty and innovation. The three approaches differ
in their capacity for transformative impact. Education ”through” entrepreneurship offers
the most substantial potential to encourage deep and transformative learning, followed by
teaching ”for” and teaching ”about,” which have the least. This transformative potential is
linked to changes in learners’ perspectives and self-concept, such as the formation of an
entrepreneurial identity and increased confidence to drive change [10].
It is essential to recognize that, in practice, the boundary between for and through en-
trepreneurship education can sometimes become blurred. Both approaches involve ac-
tive learning and skill application. Indeed, some scholars have observed overlap, where
teaching ”for” entrepreneurship courses (focused on venture creation skills) also employs
extensive experiential methods comparable to teaching ”through” entrepreneurship. For
example, a class might both train students in business planning (”for”) and require them
to launch a micro-business (”through”). Recent critiques have proposed updating the
framework or introducing additional dimensions to reflect these complexities better. [10].
Nonetheless, the ”through” approach remains conceptually distinct in its primary intent.

2.2.4
Alignment with Neck and Greene’s “Three Knowns”
Neck and Greene’s (2011) work on entrepreneurship education, ”Known Worlds and New
Frontiers,” offers a conceptual model that aligns closely with the ”About, For, Through”
framework. In their analysis, Neck and Greene emphasize that to innovate in entrepreneur-
ship education, educators must reconsider what is taught, how it is taught, and why it is
taught [4]. These correspond to three core elements or ”knowns” in teaching: content (the
”what”), pedagogy (the ”how”), and purpose or mindset (the underlying ”why”). Although
Neck and Greene did not label their model with the same terminology, the substance
mirrors the ”know-what, know-how, know-why” triad [23]. Conceptually, teaching ”about”
entrepreneurship addresses the content knowledge (the ”what”) that Neck and Greene
identify as one of the known dimensions of entrepreneurship. Likewise, teaching ”for” en-
trepreneurship is concerned with how we teach and train entrepreneurs, the methods, ex-
ercises, and pedagogical techniques. This method resonates with Neck & Greene’s sec-
ond known (the ”how”) that translates knowledge into practice. Finally, teaching ”through”
entrepreneurship resonates with the why or purpose behind entrepreneurship education:
it seeks to cultivate an entrepreneurial mindset and drive tangible outcomes. This aligns
with what Neck and Greene describe as the new frontier of focusing on entrepreneurial
thinking and behavior beyond the traditional content-driven approaches, the ”why” that
gives entrepreneurship education its significance [4], [21], [23].
In essence, the two models are conceptually congruent. Both recognize that a com-
prehensive entrepreneurship education must encompass (1) a body of knowledge about
entrepreneurship, (2) the skills and methods required to act entrepreneurially, and (3)
the mindset, attitudes, and intent that motivate entrepreneurial action. Neck and Greene
(2011) argued that earlier curricula were too heavily focused on the first known content,
which is comparable to teaching ”about.” They called for more emphasis on teaching en-
trepreneurship as a method, learning by doing, experimenting, and failing forward, which
parallels the idea of learning ”through” entrepreneurship. They also highlighted the im-
portance of developing an entrepreneurial mindset, which corresponds to the attitudinal
goals of the teaching ”through” approach. Meanwhile, their acknowledgment of the value
in specific established contents and techniques reflects the continued relevance of teach-
ing ”for” entrepreneurship (know-how) in combination with experiential learning [4].
By aligning the teaching ”About, For, Through” framework with Neck and Greene’s per-
spective, we see a reinforced message: effective entrepreneurship education operates on
multiple levels, providing knowledge, developing practical skills, and transforming mind-
sets. The ”About, For, Through” framework provides a structured way to ensure none
of these dimensions is neglected, while Neck and Greene’s framework underscores their
interdependence. In this study, it can be concluded that the ”About, For, Through” model
and the ”three knowns” view of Neck and Greene are not competing paradigms but rather
complementary lenses. Both advocate moving beyond a one-dimensional approach. In
practice, an educator might integrate all three modes: for example, about (academic con-
tent on entrepreneurship theory), for (skill-building via business plan workshops), and
through (a semester-long venture launch project), thereby addressing the what, how, and
why identified by Neck and Greene.
In summary, Neck and Greene’s (2011) insights into what to teach, how to teach, and why
it matters in entrepreneurship education conceptually align with the knowledge, skills,
and attitudinal focus of the ”About, For, Through” framework [4], [10]. This alignment

2.3
Universities and Their Role in Innovation Ecosystems
Innovation ecosystems are collaborative networks of diverse actors, including firms, gov-
ernment bodies, and academic institutions, that collectively drive technological innovation
and economic growth. In these systems, universities serve as crucial hubs of knowledge
creation and talent development, linking scientific research with industrial application and
public policy. The traditional boundaries between academia, industry, and government
have increasingly blurred in the modern knowledge economy [27]. Universities today
frequently take on roles that were once exclusive to industry or government, such as ac-
tively engaging in commercialization, regional development, and policy advocacy. This
expanded role places universities at the heart of innovation ecosystems as educators,
researchers, and key innovation agents that encourage entrepreneurship, incubate new
technologies, and support economic development.
To illustrate the importance of universities within innovation ecosystems, the literature
often refers to two influential models. The first is the Triple Helix model, which concep-
tualizes innovation as an interaction between three core institutional spheres: university,
industry, and government. The second is an expanded framework, sometimes referred
to as the “Pentagon model.” The Pentagon model is often represented by innovation
superclusters, which build upon the Triple Helix by incorporating additional actors (such
as entrepreneurs and investors) to reflect a more complex, multi-stakeholder ecosystem.
The following subsections explain each model and highlight how universities contribute
to the functioning and evolution of these innovation systems.

2.3.1
The Triple Helix Model
The Triple Helix model posits that innovation emerges from the dynamic interplay between
universities, industries, and government. Rather than operating in separate silos, these
three spheres form an “evolving network” of communication and collaboration. Each helix
represents a distinct societal function where academia is primarily responsible for knowl-
edge production and human capital development, industry for wealth creation and applied
innovation, and government for providing governance, policy direction, and stability. In a
Triple Helix configuration, these functions do not remain isolated; instead, they influence
and reshape one another through ongoing interactions [29]. As some scholars explain,
the three institutional carriers of an innovation system maintain both institutional relations,

==PAGE:22==
constraining each other’s behavior through formal roles and functional relations, shaping
each other’s expectations in the innovation process [29]. Through this dual network, the
system integrates three crucial functions: the generation of new knowledge, economic
utilization of that knowledge, and the normative regulation of innovation, all in a mutually
reinforcing cycle [30].
A key insight of the Triple Helix model is that universities, industries, and governments
increasingly share overlapping roles in the innovation ecosystem. Each sphere can “take
on the role of another” to some extent [29]. For example, universities today often engage
in entrepreneurial activities traditionally associated with an industry, such as patenting,
licensing, and even forming spin-off companies to commercialize research findings di-
rectly. Leydesdorf and Etzkowitz (1998) described the emergence of the entrepreneurial
university, which actively participates in economic development, in contrast to the older
linear view where universities supplied knowledge to industry. At the same time, industry
and government actors may take on more “academic” or collaborative roles, for instance
through corporate R&D laboratories (industry acting as a knowledge producer) or public
agencies facilitating innovation networks (government acting as an innovation organizer)
[29].
This blurring of roles means that universities are not passive knowledge providers. In-
stead, they become co-equal drivers of innovation alongside firms and policymakers. The
Triple Helix framework thus highlights universities’ expanded contribution: they inject new
scientific knowledge and educated talent into the economy, collaborate with companies
on research and development, and even influence innovation policy and regional strategy.
Studies in this line of research have shown that such university–industry–government link-
ages can yield knowledge-based advantages for regions and nations by speeding up the
circulation and application of new ideas [27]. In summary, the Triple Helix model has be-
come the foundation of the innovation ecosystem, leading to the creation of numerous
science parks and technology transfer centers that foster a close relationship between
industries and universities. The Triple Helix Model eventually became the backbone of
the innovation cluster concept, placing government, industry, and academia in a closed
circle of partnership [31].
2.3.2
The Pentagon Model (The Five-Point Model)
While the Triple Helix focuses on three core actors, it has become clear in the past 10-
20 years that the Triple Helix may be insufficient to support the constantly evolving in-
novation ecosystem worldwide. Eventually, after continually adding to the Triple Helix
Model, academics developed a new model to serve as the backbone of the innovation
ecosystem: the Five-Point Model. This expanded perspective is sometimes described as
a Pentagon model of innovation and is essentially an extension of the Triple Helix into a
multi-helix framework. In the Pentagon model, five key stakeholder groups are empha-
sized: academia, industry, government, plus the addition of entrepreneurs (startups and
scale-ups) and investors (or financial capital providers) [31], [32]. The inclusion of these
additional helices acknowledges the crucial roles of new venture creation and funding in
driving innovation. In other words, the Pentagon model broadens the innovation ecosys-
tem to explicitly include entrepreneurial and financial communities, creating a five-sided
cooperation structure that links knowledge, economy, governance, entrepreneurship, and
capital.
The explanation behind innovation superclusters reflects the multi-actor approach of the
Pentagon Model. Superclusters are large-scale, geographically concentrated innovation
networks designed to support intense collaboration across the public and private sec-

superclusters are often orchestrated through policy initiatives to bring together compa-

Notably, one of the strategic goals of the Icelandic cluster policy is explicitly “to enhance
cooperation between science and industry,” underlining that strong links between univer-
sities and firms are viewed as essential for innovation and competitiveness [32].

3.1
Iceland’s Innovation Landscape: Overview and
Characteristics
Iceland’s innovation landscape is shaped by its small population, unique geography, and
reliance on a few key industries. With approximately 390,000 inhabitants and a low popu-
lation density, Iceland faces structural economic challenges, including limited economies
of scale, high fixed costs for infrastructure, and restricted domestic market opportunities.
Traditionally, the economy has been heavily dependent on natural resources, with fish-
eries and renewable energy (geothermal and hydroelectric power) as foundational pillars.
The rapid rise of tourism in the early 21st century added another major sector to the econ-
omy, yet it also introduced vulnerabilities to external shocks. [35].
Recognizing these structural limitations, Iceland has, since the early 2000s, prioritized
economic diversification through the promotion of knowledge-intensive industries and
innovation-driven entrepreneurship [8]. According to Hannes Ottósson & Rögnvaldur Sæ-
mundsson (2023), Icelandic innovation policy has evolved considerably, emphasizing cre-
ating conditions favorable to innovation through improved infrastructure, education, and
support mechanisms for entrepreneurs. Innovation is now viewed not merely as a means
to economic growth but also as a critical tool to enhance resilience against global market
fluctuations and environmental challenges.
The shift toward a knowledge economy entails significant investments in research and
development, education, and institutional reforms aimed at building an innovation ecosys-
tem that supports sustainable and inclusive growth. The National Science and Innovation
Strategy for Iceland (NSLÍ) further underscores these priorities by outlining strategic goals
to enhance research and development [7]. The newest Science and Technology Policy
report for 2020–2022 further outlines plans for strengthening collaboration between uni-
versities and industry, as well as supporting startups through various incubators, acceler-
ators, and dedicated funding mechanisms [36].
Statistically, Iceland performs moderately well in international innovation metrics. In 2022,
gross domestic expenditure on R&D reached 2.66% of national GDP [6], demonstrat-
ing a serious commitment relative to the country’s economic size. However, Iceland still
lags behind leading innovation nations in patent output, technology exports, and startup
scaling, partly due to its market limitations [35].

on technology development, fostering international competitiveness through strong sup-

3.1.1
Innovation Policy and the R&D Tax Credit
Since the early 2000s, Iceland has pursued a deliberate strategy to diversify its economy
through knowledge-intensive industries and innovation-driven entrepreneurship. This strat-
egy aims to establish a robust knowledge-based economy that complements traditional
economic pillars. The Icelandic government has actively supported this transition through
targeted policy measures designed to foster innovation across various sectors, notably by
establishing conditions favorable for innovation through improved infrastructure, educa-
tion, and enhanced support mechanisms for entrepreneurs [8]. Moreover, the government
acknowledges the role innovation plays not only economically but also socially and envi-
ronmentally. Iceland’s innovation policy explicitly incorporates addressing demographic
challenges, environmental sustainability, and adaptation to rapid technological changes
[36].
Iceland’s innovation policy framework reflects a deliberate and sustained government ef-
fort to foster a knowledge-based economy and drive economic diversification. Over the
past two decades, innovation has been increasingly recognized as essential not only for
economic growth but also for addressing societal challenges, including those related to
environmental sustainability and demographic shifts [8].

Innovation policy initiatives stress cross-sector collaboration, support for human capital

One of the central measures for operationalizing these goals has been the R&D tax credit
scheme, introduced in 2010 via Act No. 152/2009 on Support for Innovative Enterprises
and Companies [37]. The R&D tax credit provides a refundable incentive, reducing the
effective cost of R&D investments and thus directly addressing one of the principal barriers
to innovation in small economies, which is often financing constraints [38].
The evolution of the scheme reflects Iceland’s growing ambition:
• Initially allowing a 20% deduction of eligible R&D expenditures, capped at a modest
ceiling, to stimulate baseline innovation activities.
• A significant 2016 reform raised the expenditure ceiling to ISK 450 million, making
the scheme more accessible to scaling SMEs.
• The 2020 reform differentiated credit rates, with 35% for SMEs and 25% for large
companies, and raised the ceiling to ISK 1.1 billion. This act strategically strength-
ens support for smaller and high-potential firms, which are crucial to the ecosystem’s
vitality.
OECD evaluations show that the tax credit scheme has been effective: micro- and small
firms using the credit significantly increased R&D investment and observed positive im-
pacts, including higher firm-level sales, employment growth, and improved average wages
[38]. However, the OECD also identifies areas for refinement. These include improving
the predictability and transparency of eligibility criteria, strengthening monitoring and eval-
uation practices, and considering additional targeting measures to maximize the scheme’s
effectiveness among startups and emerging tech firms [38]. The R&D tax credit, there-
fore, is more than a financial incentive; it is a structural policy tool underpinning Iceland’s
efforts to foster a resilient, innovation-driven economy. It exemplifies the broader strate-
gic approach: using smart, adaptive public policy to create the institutional foundations for
long-term value creation and global competitiveness through knowledge and technology.
Northstack, Gallup, and the Technology Development Fund conducted a survey to initi-
ate a data-driven analysis of the current environment for entrepreneurs and startups in
Iceland. The survey was sent to all companies that applied for the Technology Develop-
ment Fund between 2016 and 2019. Of all participants who received the survey, 35.7%
or 269 companies responded to the survey. The survey showed that approximately 50%
of participants had already applied for the tax credit, and 7% considered it not the right
time yet to apply. Interestingly, even though the tax credit initiative is one of the key mea-
sures for supporting and encouraging research and development in Iceland, the survey
shows that approximately 23% of participants were unaware of the possibility of applying
for a tax credit for their R&D projects [39]. This result might come as a surprise, as we
could assume that all participants were involved in innovation and some form of technol-
ogy development and would, therefore, be in the main pool of applicants for the R&D tax
credit.

3.3
Challenges and Opportunities
As Iceland seeks to strengthen its position within the global innovation economy, both
the challenges and opportunities ahead reflect the country’s distinctive characteristics.
Iceland’s small population, geographic isolation, and limited industrial base create con-
straints for innovation-driven growth, particularly in terms of market access, scale, and
resource availability. At the same time, these limitations invite a focused and collabora-
tive approach to ecosystem development, where institutions can play a significant role in
shaping outcomes. Within this context, higher education emerges as a central but under-
leveraged pillar. Universities are uniquely positioned to act as both knowledge generators
and drivers of entrepreneurial activity, yet their potential remains only partially realized.
National innovation policy is increasingly acknowledging the importance of education not
only as a provider of talent but also as an active shaper of mindset, skills, and cross-
sector collaboration. Yet this recognition is not always matched by systematic support or
strategic alignment across the ecosystem.
While innovation education is increasingly encouraged in policy documents, its implemen-
tation within higher education often relies on isolated initiatives or individual efforts. There
is a gap between national ambitions and institutional practice, a gap that represents both
a challenge and a strategic opportunity. With clearer direction, targeted investment, and
stronger collaboration between universities and ecosystem actors, higher education could
serve as a cornerstone of Iceland’s innovation agenda.
As this chapter has outlined, Iceland’s innovation ecosystem presents a complex inter-
play of constraints and potentials. This thesis maps and analyzes the current landscape
of innovation and entrepreneurship course offerings in Icelandic higher education, provid-
ing a foundation for understanding how educational institutions are positioned within the
broader innovation ecosystem.

4.1
Research Design
This study adopts a qualitative, exploratory research design to map and analyze inno-
vation and entrepreneurship education across all seven universities in Iceland. The re-
search mentality guiding this study emphasizes openness to emerging patterns, insights,
and contextual understanding rather than testing predetermined hypotheses. Therefore,
an abductive reasoning approach is particularly suited as it iteratively moves between
empirical observations and theoretical frameworks, allowing the research to adapt and
refine theoretical constructs based on the evolving analysis of data [49]. This approach
facilitates the integration of existing theoretical concepts with new insights derived from
course mapping and stakeholder interviews, resulting in a deeper and more contextual-
ized understanding of entrepreneurship education practices in Icelandic universities.
This research combines a qualitative analysis of course syllabi from all seven universi-
ties in Iceland with semi-structured interviews conducted with three selected stakehold-
ers from those universities. The research is designed to explore how the innovation and
entrepreneurship-related courses in the seven universities can be analyzed and mapped
by integrating the ”About, For, Through” framework using dimensional analysis. Semi-
structured interviews, both online and in-person, were then conducted to understand fur-
ther the effect and contribution of selected courses taught at the University of Iceland and
the University of Bifröst. The interviews were later analyzed using thematic analysis to
identify common themes across different interviews, providing a deeper understanding of
the contributions of the courses they teach to the innovation ecosystem in Iceland.

4.3
Dimensional Analysis
A dimensional analysis was used to map the entrepreneurship courses coherently, plot-
ting each course according to pedagogical intent on one axis and course size on the other.
In this mapping, the x-axis represents the teaching approach, whether a course educates
”about”, ”for”, or ”through” entrepreneurship. The y-axis represents the course’s magni-
tude in terms of ECTS credits (European Credit Transfer System), which visualizes the
course’s size or weight in the curriculum. Different colors of the text boxes around the
course name in the mapping represent different credit sizes. Some courses with the
same credit size and similar placement on the x-axis were put in the same textbox. This
two-dimensional mapping enables us to capture courses’ pedagogical intent in alignment
with established categories, while the vertical placement by ECTS highlights differences in
course scope and size. Together, these dimensions provide a structured overview of the
landscape of innovation and entrepreneurship education offered at Icelandic universities.
Assigning courses to the x-axis was guided by criteria drawn from the literature review
(see section 4.2.1). A course’s placement on the horizontal axis was determined by the
degree to which it is theory-driven versus experientially driven. Using these literature-
26

derived criteria ensured that the mapping was conceptually consistent with established
pedagogical models of entrepreneurship education. The summary below outlines the
distinguishing characteristics of the ”About, For, Through” approaches as drawn from the
literature and used in this study’s mapping:
• About: Theory-heavy, conceptually focused learning with passive classroom meth-
ods and minimal hands-on activity. This approach builds students’ cognitive under-
standing of entrepreneurship (knowledge about entrepreneurial concepts).
• For: Skills- and preparation-focused training, using practical tools and structured
exercises (e.g., business plan development, design thinking simulations) to impart
”know-how.” These types of courses are applied in nature but typically lack actual
venture creation.
• Through: Experiential, hands-on learning set in real or realistic entrepreneurial projects.
These courses involve venture creation activities, often with external engagement
(incubation, pitching to investors, internships), making learning feedback-driven and
reflective of real-world entrepreneurship.

curriculum descriptions and the author’s interpretation of these descriptions. Given the
scope of the dataset, it was only feasible to validate a small, targeted subset of place-

final mapping represents the author’s analysis of the published curriculum, which reflects
the pedagogical intent of each course on the map. Below are examples of the authors’

courses that are applied and skill-driven yet do not involve real venture creation fall un-

4.5
Thematic analysis
A thematic analysis was applied to the three stakeholder interviews for this thesis. Braun
and Clarke (2006) describe thematic analysis as a qualitative method for identifying and
reporting patterns (themes) within data. It offers an accessible, theoretically flexible ap-
proach to qualitative data and can be adapted to different scenarios and fields of study.
Unlike analytic methods based on frequency counts, thematic analysis emphasizes the
meaning and narrative of the data and is distinguished into two main approaches. An in-
ductive (data-driven) approach where coding and theme development are strongly grounded
30
Mapping Innovation and Entrepreneurship Courses in Icelandic Higher Education
==PAGE:43==
in the data itself, with little influence from prior theory. In contrast, a theoretical (deductive)
approach uses the researcher’s existing ideas or research questions to guide coding [57].
In this study, both deductive and inductive approaches were used in a complementary
manner. A deductive approach was employed to ensure alignment with the research ob-
jective of mapping the structure of innovation and entrepreneurship education across Ice-
landic universities. Interview data were, therefore, initially coded with a focus on elements
such as course content, pedagogical goals, and institutional strategy to inform the course-
mapping framework directly. Simultaneously, an inductive approach was used to avoid
bias and ensure that potentially significant but unexpected themes were not overlooked.
This dual strategy enabled the analysis to remain open to new insights from participants
while maintaining focus on the research aims.
The thematic analysis followed Braun and Clarke’s six-phase procedure. These phases
were implemented as follows:
1. Familiarization with the data
2. Generating initial codes
3. Searching for themes
4. Reviewing themes
5. Defining and naming themes
6. Producing the report
Throughout the analysis, thematic maps (visual diagrams of themes and sub-themes)
were used as a tool to refine and illustrate the findings. As recommended, thematic maps
were made during and after coding to visualize how codes clustered into themes. By
mapping the relationships among themes, overlaps or gaps were identified in the analysis,
and adjustments could be made (such as breaking a theme apart or merging overlapping
ones) [57].

The results of this study indicate a significant discrepancy between the mode of en-

in Icelandic universities. In particular, there is a noticeable gap in courses that teach

6.1.2
Contrast with Literature and Interviews
This finding regarding the rarity of teaching ”through” entrepreneurship courses stands
in sharp contradiction to the emphasis found in the literature and voiced by stakeholders
in interviews. The entrepreneurship education literature strongly advocates for experi-
ential ”learn by doing” pedagogies, considering them crucial for developing genuine en-
trepreneurial competencies and mindsets. As discussed in the Literature Review (Chapter
2), numerous studies have observed a global shift away from purely theoretical instruction
toward more practical and experiential methodologies. This shift is grounded by construc-
tivist learning theories and the recognition that entrepreneurship is best learned ”through”
experience and active problem-solving. Education ”through” entrepreneurship is regarded
as the most impactful in terms of fostering deep learning and personal transformation.
For example, Kakouris and Liargovas (2021) argue that while all three approaches have
their benefits, learning ”through” entrepreneurship offers the most substantial potential
to encourage deep, transformative learning, more so than learning ”for” or ”about.” The
transformative impact arises from students forming an entrepreneurial identity and gain-
ing confidence as they navigate the uncertainties of creating something new [10]. Like-
wise, Neck and Greene (2011) have critiqued the traditional content-heavy curricula and
called for more emphasis on entrepreneurship as a method (i.e., learning by doing, pro-
totyping, and iterating). They stress that developing an entrepreneurial mindset requires
giving students real-world challenges, which aligns with the ”through” approach [4]. In
short, the consensus in the literature is that experiential courses are indispensable for
truly internalizing entrepreneurial skills and attitudes. Observational evidence supports
this logic; programs that allow students to create ventures or prototypes show a greater
impact on participants’ entrepreneurial intentions and capabilities than classroom-bound
courses. For instance, a study by Vincett and Farlow (2008) demonstrated that replacing
traditional coursework with a venture launch project led to higher student satisfaction and
tangible business outcomes [20].
In this context, the mapping results reveal an inconsistency between recommended prac-
tices and actual practices in Iceland. The literature advocates for more ”through” oriented
courses, while the reality is that very few such courses are currently integrated into cur-
ricula. This gap did not go unnoticed by the stakeholders interviewed for this study. A
recurring theme emerged in the qualitative data that current course offerings do not fully
align with what educators and experts believe are the most important aspects of educa-
tion. All interviewed stakeholders highlighted the importance of hands-on learning and
real-world engagement in entrepreneurship education. They consistently emphasized
that students learn the most when they confront real entrepreneurial projects, work in
interdisciplinary teams, and engage with external partners (such as industry mentors or
community stakeholders) [Fida, Hannes, Rögnvaldur]. This aligns with the literature’s
stance that entrepreneurship should be taught ”through” practice rather than just as an
abstract theory. One interviewee, for example, stressed the value of real-world engage-
ment and interdisciplinary collaboration in course design, indicating a desire to push the
curriculum toward more experiential teaching [Hannes]. Others pointed out that innovative
pedagogies, such as project-based learning, hackathons, or venture incubator programs,
can profoundly shape student mindsets [Fida]. However, such formats are not widely
represented in formal course listings.
Cross-analyzing the literature, mapping, and interview data reveals an apparent contra-
diction: experiential learning is regarded as an essential factor in innovation and en-
trepreneurship education. However, in practice, experiential learning with hands-on projects
and real-world engagement is underrepresented in Icelandic universities. The literature

provides both a theoretical foundation and an ethical responsibility for teaching through
entrepreneurship, and Icelandic stakeholders agree in principle. However, course map-

that the remainder of this discussion will explore.
6.2
Why does the Gap Exist?
Earlier chapters revealed a clear gap in Icelandic higher education; while many courses
focus on teaching ”about” and ”for” entrepreneurship, instances of teaching ”through” en-
trepreneurship are comparatively few. In other words, students may learn entrepreneur-
ship theory and acquire entrepreneurial skills, but they rarely have the opportunity to ap-
ply these skills in an academic setting. The data mapping Iceland’s university offerings
revealed that most courses clustered around theoretical or skills-based instruction, with
very few leaning strongly toward experiential venture creation. The discussion now turns
to analyzing the underlying reasons for this gap. Why have Icelandic universities, de-
spite a growing interest in innovation and numerous ”about” and ”for” entrepreneurship
courses, been slow or limited in adopting learning-by-doing approaches?
Several underlying causes likely contribute to the lack of ”through” entrepreneurship courses.
These range from how such courses originate (often as small grassroots initiatives) to in-
stitutional and cultural factors that impede their growth. In the following subsections, we
examine each factor in turn, including course origin and scale, administrative challenges,
assessment norms, resource limitations, student mindset, policy absence, and economic
incentives. Together, a comprehensive explanation can be built for why teaching through

6.2.1
Grassroots Origins and Course Scale
One primary reason for the lack of ”through” courses is that most innovation and en-
trepreneurship education in Iceland has grassroots origins. New courses have typically
emerged from the bottom-up initiative of individual faculty or departments rather than from
top-down curriculum reform [9]. For example, Fida described how a new entrepreneur-
ship course at Bifröst University began with a simple conversation. A department head in
the Creative Industries recognized her students’ need for business skills and teamed up
with an entrepreneur-lecturer to create a course for them. This bottom-up genesis meant
the course was crafted informally to meet student interests rather than being introduced
as part of any grand institutional strategy. The strength of such grassroots efforts lies in
their innovation, flexibility, and responsiveness. Instructors can quickly tailor content to
perceived needs and experiment with creative approaches. In the Bifröst case, once the
course 104.6.0.NYGV Innovation and Business Planning was launched, it gained pop-
ularity, and the Business department requested to join in, resulting in a relatively large
course with over 60 students. Fida explained that the course is carefully structured based
on how she would have liked to learn about entrepreneurship when she was a student.
This course exemplifies the agility and student-centered design that grassroots initiatives
make possible.
However, the grassroots nature of these courses also brings limitations in scale and
scope. Many teaching ”about” and ”for” courses in Icelandic universities have a limited
credit size, often around 2–3 ECTS or 5-7 ECTS. Well-integrated practical courses fo-
cusing on teaching skills and tools ”for” entrepreneurship usually carry about 5–8 ECTS.
Only a few teaching ”about” or ”for” courses are closer to 10 ECTS and are usually courses
focused heavily on foundational theory and extensive skills building. In contrast, the ex-
periential learning of teaching ”through” courses typically requires more time and credits,
usually at least 5–6 ECTS, with some courses divided into two parts that add up to 12-
15 ECTS. This allows students to thoroughly engage in entrepreneurship activities, such
as developing a venture idea over a semester. A correlation can be noted through the
mapping and stakeholder interviews between course size and teaching methods. There
tends to be more flexibility in the categories of teaching ”about” and ”for,” where courses
can be any size, ranging from very small (2-3 ECTS credits) to relatively large (10 ECTS
credits). There is less flexibility in teaching ”through” courses because such courses are
inherently resource-intensive and require a specific minimum scale to be effective.
Without strong institutional backing, most of these offerings remain small, introductory-
level courses that typically focus on teaching ”about” or ”for” rather than extensive ”through”
courses. Resources are often limited to a single passionate teacher with a smaller bud-
get, and credit allocations are usually modest for these types of bottom-up courses. They
might cover an entrepreneurial mindset and have students draft a simple business plan
but stop short of guiding a real start-up process. Indeed, Fida observed that
”The courses I teach... it had a lot of the ”about” and ”for” elements, but what
was missing was someone with real experience, someone who had actually
gone through the process and could speak from that” to drive a learning-by-
doing element [Fida].
This suggests that, until very recently, Icelandic entrepreneurship courses were heav-
ily content-oriented, lacking the benefit of experienced entrepreneurs mentoring genuine

projects. The small ECTS footprints also reflect this; a 2–3 ECTS class might involve a few
guest lectures or case studies (valuable but primarily informational), whereas a project-

”... if you intend to do something that is thoroughly ”through”, then you need
time for it” [Rögnvaldur].

elective in their institutions, which significantly limits their impact. A notable exception

bottom-up and resource-constrained, which predisposes them to be brief, introductory,
and non-experiential. The following sections will show how institutional factors then rein-

6.2.2
Administrative and Organizational Challenges
Even when educators attempt to design more ambitious, cross-disciplinary entrepreneur-
ship courses, they encounter significant administrative and organizational hindrances.
Universities are traditionally organized into faculties and departments, each with its own
schedule, credit system, and budget. A teaching” through” course, however, often works
best when it brings together students and faculty from different fields (for example, busi-
ness students teaming with engineers or designers). In Icelandic universities, coordinat-
ing such collaboration has proved challenging.
A notable example is the case of Joint Entrepreneurship Education for Business School
and Engineering Students at the University of Iceland by Dr. Stefan Lilischkis (2023).
While the joint master’s program was ultimately successful, it faced internal friction sim-
ply because it spanned two faculties. The biggest challenge, according to those involved,
was working across different faculties [Rögnvaldur],[34]. Decisions had to be made jointly
by multiple units, yet responsibilities were unclear and ambiguous. For instance, if a new
course needed to be approved, who had the authority to do so? Is it the Business School
or the Engineering School? Budgeting and staffing were equally problematic, as although
budgets were allocated for the program, it remained unclear who was responsible for
specific expenses. If an instructor needed to be hired or replaced, no one knew which
faculty should fund that position, and everything had to be negotiated each time. Re-
source allocation within the university poses challenges. Interdisciplinary, experiential
courses may require team teaching or contributions from multiple departments (for ex-
ample, a business professor and an engineering professor co-supervising student startup
teams). Universities without a framework for cost-sharing struggle to accommodate this.
The University of Iceland’s entrepreneurship program found that no central administration
guidelines existed on how to count teaching hours or funding when a course is co-taught
across faculties. If each department is evaluated on its own credit production and student
numbers, they have little incentive to” share” a course [30]. This rigid budgeting and eval-
uation system can thus be a structural disincentive for offering cross-disciplinary” through”

courses.
Another administrative barrier is the inconsistency in credit systems and course formats
across departments. Different faculties may have different” standard” course sizes (in
ECTS) or different grading schemas, which complicates the design of a unified course.
At the University of Iceland, for example, some master’s programs have all courses set
at 6 ECTS, while other departments use 7.5 or 10 ECTS as their standard course credits.

norms can discourage innovative course formats. A mismatch is created when a ”through”
course requires 7 ECTS of student effort, but the department’s credit system only allows
6 or 10 ECTS options. This mismatch makes administrators less inclined to approve un-
conventional offerings.
Furthermore, scheduling conflicts often arise because innovation and entrepreneurship
courses, which aim to attract students from multiple faculties, must compete with stu
dents’ core classes. Without high-level coordination, timetable clashes can prevent non-
major students from enrolling. This was noted in the joint program at the University of
Iceland, where extra steps were needed to synchronize schedules between the faculties.
Aligning academic calendars and timetables across faculties was challenging as depart-

considerable effort. As the case study observed,
”much is based on good will” in a joint program like this [34].

and entrepreneurship courses rely on individuals to navigate around institutional rules
and carry additional coordination responsibilities.
In sum, even if there is demand for a truly experiential, cross-faculty entrepreneurship
course, bureaucratic frictions can hinder its launch or expansion. The lack of clear insti-
tutional structures to support interdisciplinary education, including dedicated budget lines
and standard scheduling blocks, means such courses often occur only through extraordi-
nary effort. The University of Iceland’s program was driven forward by committed deans
and faculty members who effectively worked within the system. However, smaller initia-
tives may fall through the cracks. Without administrative facilitators, an idea for a multi-
department startup course can die on the vine. These organizational challenges help
explain why many” through” concepts never materialize or remain small pilots. They also
highlight that the gap is not only pedagogical but is instead embedded in the university
structures.

Assessment Constraints and Traditional Academic Norms
Another set of barriers relates to how learning is assessed in universities and the prevail-
ing academic culture surrounding what counts as proper teaching. Teaching ”through”
entrepreneurship implies a shift toward experiential learning, students working on real or
realistic ventures, often in teams, with outcomes that are not easily captured by exams
or essays. This poses a challenge to traditional assessment systems. Universities are
accustomed to standardized assessments, such as written exams, individual papers, and
graded assignments, which are converted into numeric grades. Experiential courses may
require assessing elements such as a business plan, a prototype, a pitch to investors,

rubrics, and faculty may be unsure how to evaluate them fairly.
The Icelandic higher education system has historically emphasized formal academic as-
sessment. Some academics are, therefore, hesitant to embrace alternative assessment
methods used in entrepreneurship education, such as portfolio assessment, peer eval-
uation, oral pitches, or pass/fail evaluations based on real-world performance. The de-
fault approach was to teach entrepreneurship as a concept or a set of business-planning
skills and assess it through familiar means (e.g., a report or exam on business plan the-
ory) rather than having students undertake a venture and assess that process. Fida re-
marked that before she got involved, courses were often led by people who only ”read
[entrepreneurship] in a book,” whereas what was missing was someone who ”has gone
through this and knows what they are talking about” [Fida]. Her point illustrates a broader
cultural norm in which purely academic instructors tend to design courses in a scholarly
mode, incorporating conventional academic exercises. In comparison, an experienced
practitioner might be more comfortable devising experiential projects and novel assess-
ments. If most instructors in the system come from academic backgrounds with little
startup experience, they may default to lectures and written exams, not out of ill will but
because that is their area of expertise.

venture project when outcomes can vary widely and failure is often a valuable learning

some faculty and administrators wary. There can be resistance to awarding academic
credit for activities that do not resemble traditional coursework. For example, if a course

question: Is this academic enough? How do we know the students met the learning ob-

pushback against fully experiential courses or pressure to dilute them with more theo-
retical components or to have something ”gradable” in the usual way. As one report on

academic norms in universities, however, tend to favor clarity and control, such as syl-

external feedback (like investor responses) or iterative learning (pivoting a business idea)
disrupts that clarity.
All these factors contribute to a situation where few ”through” courses get implemented,
and those that do may face internal skepticism or pressure to conform to traditional for-
mats. The result is a self-reinforcing cycle: because experiential courses are uncom-
mon, there are no established assessment models for them, and because assessment is
challenging, experiential courses remain uncommon. Breaking this cycle requires institu-
tional leadership to legitimize new forms of evaluation (for instance, creating guidelines for
evaluating team entrepreneurship projects, training faculty in experiential grading, etc.).
Without a systematic change, the academic norms continue to favor lectures, exams, and
theoretical coursework, leaving a gap in entrepreneurship education.
6.2.4
Student Expectations and Mentality
Even if all other conditions are met, the question of student readiness and mindset re-
mains. The teaching ”through” approach demands a lot from students: independence,
tolerance for ambiguity, proactiveness, and often teamwork across disciplines. Many stu-
dents, however, have been conditioned by their prior education to expect a more struc-
tured, instructor-led learning experience. This misalignment between student expecta-
tions and the nature of ”through” courses is another factor behind the gap.
In traditional education, such as secondary school and undergraduate university pro-
grams, students become accustomed to clear syllabi and explicit assignments. They
develop the idea that the teacher will impart knowledge that students then need to absorb
and retain. By contrast, an experiential entrepreneurship course might tell students to
identify a problem they care about, form a team, develop a solution, and educators will
guide students as they iterate. To students who have never experienced this, it can be
overwhelming or even uncomfortable. Some may initially interpret the teacher’s hands-
off approach as a lack of guidance. Others may feel anxious because there is no single
”right answer” or guaranteed successful outcome, which is a significant change from the
traditional goal of obtaining correct answers on an exam.

and reinforce the notion that students may not be interested.

6.2.5
Lack of Centralized Policy and Strategic Guidance
Zooming out to the national and institutional policy level, another reason for the gap is
the absence of a coordinated strategy or national and institutional policy for entrepreneur-
ship education in Iceland. In countries where teaching ”through” entrepreneurship has
risen, there is often a strong top-down driver, such as a government initiative, a national
curriculum requirement, or at least a clear institutional strategy. Iceland, however, lacks
such unifying guidance, which results in a reliance on bottom-up efforts (as discussed in
previous sections).
A comprehensive mapping by the Nordic Entrepreneurship Islands project (2017) noted
that by 2016, there was no national strategy for entrepreneurship in education, and this is
likely one reason why only a few individual institutions have developed their own strate-
gies. The report found that the management of many Icelandic universities believed en-
trepreneurship education was necessary, but the reason so few had institutional strate-
gies was ”due to the lack of incentives for the management and the lack of a national
strategy” [9]. In other words, without a push or support from the government or fund-
ing bodies, university leaders have not been compelled to prioritize or systematically
plan entrepreneurship offerings. There has been some development in Iceland since the
Nordic Entrepreneurship Islands project (2017) regarding the national innovation strat-
egy and broader national vision, as outlined in the published report Nýsköpunarlandið
Ísland (2019) by the Ministry of Industry and Innovation [7]. Following the publication
of Nýsköpunarlandið Ísland (2019), the Science and Technology Policy Council released
Science and Technology Policy 2020–2022 (2020), which outlined a structured and insti-
tutionalized action plan for the national innovation vision [36]. These reports and national
strategies, however, do not provide detailed guidelines or action plans for implementing
innovation and entrepreneurship education. The result is that each university or faculty is
left without guidance and has to operate these broader visions independently.
This bottom-up, decentralized approach meant that interdisciplinary ”through” entrepreneur-
ship courses, which often fall between faculties and departments, usually have no exter-
nal support. A national policy could encourage the integration of entrepreneurship in all
study programs or provide funding for interdisciplinary project courses. In the absence

By contrast, other countries have implemented top-down strategies that have tangibly
increased the presence of teaching ”through” entrepreneurship. The Netherlands is a
prominent example where the national policy has encouraged entrepreneurship educa-
tion so strongly that all universities and universities of applied sciences now offer en-

has not had a comparable initiative. Without top-down support or concrete incentives,
government involvement remains insufficient, and universities have little external pres-
sure to overcome internal barriers. Innovation and entrepreneurship education remain as
a nice-to-have rather than a must-have.
The reliance on bottom-up efforts has yielded slow and uneven progress. As noted in the
Nordic Entrepreneurship Islands project (2017), Iceland is considered to be in the ”initial
stage” of entrepreneurship education penetration. In these early phases, activities rely
strongly on ”individual teachers’ enthusiasm” and isolated initiatives. A more mature stage
would involve systematization, recognition of teachers’ efforts, sharing of best practices,
and the establishment of national or regional support mechanisms. Reaching that stage
requires explicit strategic guidance [9]. The OECD (2017) has recommended that Iceland
strengthen links between education and labor-market needs, for example, by incentivizing
courses that build relevant skills (including entrepreneurship). It also noted positively
that steps like Reykjavík University’s inclusion of a first-year entrepreneurship course and
project work with industry are positive developments [12]. However, these comments
also suggest that such practices remain uncommon and are not yet policy-driven; they
are exceptions that require active support.
In summary, the lack of policy and strategic mandate in Iceland has contributed signifi-
cantly to the gap. There has been no concerted push to integrate entrepreneurship (let
alone hands-on entrepreneurship) into curricula, leaving it dependent on initiatives from
individual educators. In countries with more integrated policies, one sees more inter-
disciplinary project courses, venture incubators tied to coursework, and broad student
participation. The comparison highlights that Iceland’s bottom-up approach, without top-
down reinforcement, has inherent limitations. Until there is either a government initiative
or a coordinated strategy among Icelandic higher education institutions themselves, the
proliferation of ”through” entrepreneurship courses is likely to remain limited.
6.2.6
Summary
In summary, the comparably limited offerings of teaching ”through” courses in Icelandic
higher education are a result of multiple interlocking causes. No single factor alone ex-
plains it; it is a combination of grassroots limitations, institutional barriers, cultural norms,
and systemic disincentives that have created an environment where experiential entrepreneur-
ship education struggles to take root.
At the micro level, courses originate mainly from passionate individuals working in iso-
lation, which results in offerings that are small, introductory, and not deeply experien-
tial. Without broader support, these bottom-up efforts seldom scale up to comprehensive

”through” courses. At the meso (institutional) level, inflexible administrative structures,
isolated departmental budgets, and traditional assessment practices all interfere with the
adoption of interdisciplinary, practice-focused pedagogy. Faculty often lack the training,
time, or encouragement to experiment with venture creation courses, and even when they
do, they face hurdles like scheduling conflicts and unclear budget responsibilities. Mean-
while, students themselves, who are the intended beneficiaries, may be ill-prepared or
initially hesitant to engage in such unusual learning. This hesitation reflects a cultural gap
between traditional education and the demands of entrepreneurship education. Finally, at
the macro level, the absence of strategic policy direction and the nature of funding mecha-
nisms result in few external drivers for change. According to the Nordic Entrepreneurship
Islands project (2017), the lack of a national strategy and incentives has likely resulted in
several institutions not fully committing to entrepreneurship education [9].
Crucially, these factors are mutually reinforcing. For instance, without policy support, in-
stitutions do not dedicate resources, and without these resources, faculty- or department-
initiated courses remain small. Smaller courses do not visibly impact broad student popu-
lations, so students and staff outside those circles remain unconvinced of their value. As a
result, traditional teachings continue to dominate, reinforcing a cautious approach within
institutions. It is a complex pattern that favors the status quo. This helps explain why,
despite a clear interest in entrepreneurship education among educators, the translation
of that interest into curricular innovation has been slow. Iceland’s situation, as mapped by
the Nordic Entrepreneurship Islands project, is that of an early-stage ecosystem reliant on
individual enthusiasts. Moving to a more advanced stage where entrepreneurship educa-
tion is embedded and widespread would require aligned action from multiple stakehold-
ers. The findings here underscore that no single intervention will be sufficient to close the
gap. Instead, a coordinated, multi-level strategy is needed to ensure that future students
can not only learn ”about” entrepreneurship but also learn ”through” practicing it. This
future development benefits both the students and the broader innovation ecosystem of
the country.
6.3
Evaluating the Landscape
The landscape of entrepreneurship education at Icelandic universities has notably ex-
panded over the past decade, primarily driven by evolving student interests and global
trends. Several higher education institutions now offer dedicated courses in entrepreneur-
ship and innovation, reflecting an increased awareness of the need to equip students with
the skills necessary for starting and running a business. However, despite this positive
development, the overall educational culture at universities has not yet placed sufficient
systematic emphasis on innovation and creative problem-solving through experiential,
hands-on approaches. Course offerings largely remain introductory, theoretical, or fo-
cused predominantly on teaching ”about” and ”for” entrepreneurship rather than immers-
ing students in authentic entrepreneurial experiences. While student participation in in-
novation competitions and incubator activities has risen, these efforts typically stem from
individual enthusiasm or isolated institutional initiatives rather than a cohesive, university-
wide commitment. Consequently, although Icelandic universities are gradually adapting to
students’ growing appetite for practical, real-world entrepreneurship education, substan-
tial gaps remain between current offerings and best practices that fully embrace experien-
tial learning methodologies. In short, while Iceland’s curricula have encouraged creativity
and innovation on paper, this emphasis has not been fully translated into widespread
educational practice.
The Nordic Entrepreneurship Islands project (2017) revealed relatively low engagement

experts called for systematic changes to strengthen it.
Fast forward to recent years, and new evidence suggests that progress has been mod-
est. A recent article Knowing Where You Are Heading: Innovation and Innovation Policy
in Iceland 2003-2022 (2024) reviewed two decades of innovation-driven economic pol-

that the Icelandic government has consistently aimed to build a ”knowledge society” by
systematically supporting research and development (R&D) as a means of establishing
a new economic foundation. Innovation-driven industries experienced significant growth
during this period, although not at a pace that outpaced general economic expansion.
Although there was an alignment between government policy and innovation trends, the
study raises questions about whether these efforts have truly transformed the Icelandic
economy [8]. This critical insight is highly relevant to this thesis, as it underscores how Ice-
land’s innovation ambitions, particularly the systemic promotion of R&D, form the broader
policy environment in which universities operate.
The findings of this thesis build on and extend these prior conclusions, revealing that many
of the same challenges identified a decade ago persist today. Although Iceland’s startup
and innovation ecosystem has matured over the past decade, and universities have in-
troduced a few new programs, the overall landscape of entrepreneurship education in
academia remains unchanged. The presence of truly experiential, learn-by-doing en-
trepreneurship courses remains low across Icelandic higher education. Most universities
offer primarily traditional or hybrid courses that teach ”about” entrepreneurship (theory
and concepts) or ”for” entrepreneurship (skills such as management and finance), with
far fewer opportunities to learn ”through” entrepreneurship by actually building ventures
or projects within coursework.
A comprehensive review of Iceland’s seven universities reveals that fully hands-on courses,
where students develop business ideas, collaborate with industry, and practice entrepreneur-
ship in a realistic setting, are the exception rather than the norm. Overall, the educa-
tional culture in Iceland’s universities has not yet undergone the shift toward experimen-
tal, project-based entrepreneurship education that is seen as best practice internation-
ally. Likewise, supporting policies and incentives to encourage such curricular innova-
tion remain weak. To date, Iceland still lacks a formal national policy or funding strategy
dedicated to innovation and entrepreneurship education in universities, and efforts are

There is nothing fundamentally ”wrong” with the landscape of the current innovation and
entrepreneurship education in Iceland. However, without a bolder, more systematic im-
plementation, progress will continue to be slow. Strengthening the educational system
with proven international practices can help bridge the gap, ultimately creating the ”fourth
pillar” of the economy that Iceland’s policies have sought to achieve.
6.4
Practical Recommendations
Drawing on the above analysis, this section provides concrete recommendations for key
stakeholders, including universities, students, and policymakers, to collaboratively ad-
vance experiential entrepreneurship education in Iceland. These recommendations are
hands-on measures that can be implemented within the current policy landscape while
also aiming to inspire broader systemic improvements.
6.4.1
Universities and Faculties
• Universities can take immediate steps to embed teaching ”through” courses within
existing structures.
One key action is to integrate entrepreneurship projects
across departments and faculties, not confining them to business courses. For
example, science and engineering programs could include innovation challenges
or coursework on startup projects, allowing students to apply their technical knowl-
edge to real-world problems. Educators should be encouraged (and rewarded) to
incorporate active learning methods, such as hackathons, prototyping workshops,
student-run companies, and business plan competitions, into their classes. These
practices align with the Nordic model, which gives teachers autonomy and tools to
foster creativity and initiative in any discipline [11]. Universities can support this by
providing faculty development seminars on entrepreneurial pedagogy (e.g., training
in problem-based learning, design thinking, or Lean Startup methods). Even with-
out a national strategy, institutions can develop internal strategies. For instance,
Icelandic universities could develop an ”Entrepreneurship Across the Curriculum”
initiative, setting targets for each faculty to incorporate some form of entrepreneurial
experience into their programs.

Conclusion
7.1
Answering the Research Question
This thesis set out to map the landscape of innovation and entrepreneurship educa-
tion in Icelandic higher education. The findings provide a clear answer to the main re-
search question: What is the landscape of innovation and entrepreneurship edu-
cation in Icelandic universities? Across Iceland’s seven universities, innovation and
entrepreneurship (I&E)courses have indeed gained a foothold in the curriculum, but the
distribution of course types is markedly uneven. In general, the landscape is character-
ized by a strong emphasis on courses ”about” entrepreneurship and innovation. That
is courses that teach fundamental concepts, theories, and the context of innovation. A
substantial number of courses ”for” entrepreneurship focus on developing specific skills
such as business planning, management, or technical tools. In contrast, courses aimed
at teaching ”through” entrepreneurship (offering students hands-on, experiential learning
by engaging in real or simulated venture creation projects) are comparably fever. Ev-
ery university included in the study offers at least some coursework in innovation or en-
trepreneurship. However, the vast majority of these offerings fall into traditional lecture-
based or skills-training formats, with far fewer opportunities for students to ”learn by doing”
in entrepreneurial settings.
Addressing the first sub-question on how I&E courses are distributed across the
”About, For, Through” frameworks.
This research revealed that courses teaching
”about” and ”for” entrepreneurship are the most common, with truly experiential ”through”
courses being the rarest category. For example, larger universities like the University of
Iceland and Reykjavík University offer multiple innovation-related courses in their busi-
ness, engineering, or interdisciplinary programs; however, these courses are weighted
mainly toward theoretical knowledge and skill development through case studies or work-
shops. Smaller institutions, such as agricultural or arts universities, tend to offer only one
or two relevant courses, often tailored to niche contexts (like innovation in agriculture or
creative industries). These courses also focus on fundamentals or practical skills rather
than venture creation.
Addressing the second sub-question on what limitations or imbalances exist in the
distribution of I&E courses. The identified imbalances are clear; there is a noticeable
gap in fully experiential entrepreneurship education. While students across Iceland can
learn ”about” innovation (gaining awareness of its role in society and economy) and learn
”for” innovation (acquiring tools such as marketing, finance, or design thinking relevant to
entrepreneurial activity), they have comparatively limited chances to learn ”through” direct
entrepreneurial experience as part of their formal studies. Notable exceptions do exist;
for instance, Bifröst University offers a course in which students develop a business idea
from concept to implementation. The University of Iceland offers an interdisciplinary Mas-
ter’s program that includes a venture project; however, these are isolated cases rather
than the norm. The overall course mapping indicates that Icelandic higher education has
not yet fully embraced the experiential, learning-by-doing mindsets that the entrepreneur-
ship education literature often promotes as most effective for cultivating entrepreneurial
competencies.
The third sub-question examines why these limitations or imbalances persist in Ice-
landic universities. Several factors emerged clearly from the analysis and stakeholder

interviews:
• Grassroots origins: Innovation and entrepreneurship courses often emerge from
bottom-up initiatives by individual faculty members rather than strategic institutional
mandates, which limits their scale and resources.
• Institutional and administrative barriers: Universities often have rigid departmen-
tal structures, scheduling constraints, and budget divisions, which hinder interdisci-
plinary and experiential courses that require collaboration across faculties.
• Academic assessment norms: Traditional academic mindsets and assessment
methods prioritize structured, lecture-based courses, creating resistance to adopt-
ing the unpredictable nature of experiential entrepreneurship learning.
• Student expectations and mindset: Many students, especially those outside busi-
ness disciplines, are hesitant toward unstructured, risk-oriented courses, leading to
lower demand and fewer offerings of experiential entrepreneurship education.
• Lack of policy and resource constraints: The national innovation strategy strongly
endorses Innovation in education, but there have historically been few direct policy-
driven incentives for universities. Experiential courses demand substantial resources,
making them difficult to sustain within limited university budgets and administrative
support.
These combined factors create a landscape where experiential ”through” entrepreneur-
ship education remains relatively rare in Icelandic higher education.
In response to the main research question, the current landscape of Innovation
and entrepreneurship courses in Icelandic higher education exhibits a partial yet
incomplete alignment with global best practices and national aspirations. Icelandic
universities have embraced Innovation and entrepreneurship as important topics, ensur-
ing that students can learn foundational knowledge and practical skills. However, the
educational offerings have not fully embraced the level of experiential learning advocated
by both scholars and stakeholders in Iceland. The reasons for this gap are multifaceted,
and recognizing these factors is a crucial outcome of this research, as it highlights what
needs to change for the landscape to evolve. In summary, the study finds that while
Iceland’s higher education system has made commendable progress in incorporating In-
novation and entrepreneurship into its curricula, it has yet to fully explore the potential of
immersive, ”learning-by-doing” experiences that truly equip students to become innova-
tors and entrepreneurs. Bridging this gap will require concerted efforts by universities and
policymakers to address the underlying causes identified in this study.
7.2
Limitations and Reflection on the Research Process
While this research provided valuable insights, it is important to acknowledge its limitations
and reflect on the research process. One major limitation relates to the scope and nature
of the data used for mapping courses. The study relied primarily on publicly available
course descriptions, curricula, and information provided by universities. These sources
are generally accurate in outlining course objectives and content but may not fully capture
how a course is taught in practice. Due to time and access constraints, it was not feasible
to have every course validated by its instructor or to observe the courses directly. A more
detailed, instructor-informed verification of the mapping was beyond the logistical scope
of this thesis. As a result, even though the classification of courses into ”about,” ”for,” and
”through” is carefully done using established criteria, it might carry a degree of subjective

judgment. This could affect the precision of the mapping, particularly in borderline cases
where a course combines elements of different categories.
Another limitation concerns the depth of the qualitative insights. The study conducted
three stakeholder interviews to explore the reasons behind the course landscape. These
interviews provided valuable insights into the institutional and cultural conditions that
shape innovation and entrepreneurship education. However, the pool of interviewees was
relatively small and focused on experts deeply involved in entrepreneurship education.
While their perspectives were rich, they may not represent all possible viewpoints, such
as university administrators not directly involved in entrepreneurship courses, students
who have taken these courses, or policymakers in the education ministry. Additionally,
all three interviewees were advocaters of innovation and entrepreneurship education (by
design, as subject matter experts), which may introduce a positive bias in the qualitative
data. Perspectives from skeptics or those indifferent to entrepreneurship education were
not captured but could be relevant to understanding institutional resistance.
The research process itself had practical constraints that influenced the study. Gathering
data across seven universities required navigating the differences in how information is
presented and the language used. This presented a risk of interpretation errors despite
efforts to cross-check terminology and course contexts. Moreover, the rapidly evolving
nature of university curricula means that the study provides a snapshot in time, focusing
primarily on the 2024–2025 academic offerings. Some universities may be in the process
of updating their programs or introducing new courses that were not in effect at the time of
data collection. As such, the findings should be viewed with the understanding that they
reflect the landscape during the study period; changes in the near future could alter some
specifics of the mapping.
Reflecting on the methodology, the combination of quantitative mapping and qualitative
inquiry proved to be a strength of the research but also required careful balancing. The
mapping provided a broad overview and comparability across institutions, while the inter-
views added depth and helped explain the patterns. Challenges lay in integrating these
two approaches and ensuring that the themes from interviews were grounded in and not
overgeneralized beyond the mapping data. The iterative process of going back and forth
between the course data and interview insights was instructive: it underscored the impor-
tance of context when interpreting what a ”gap” in the data truly means. For instance, the
mapping alone revealed a gap in ”through” courses, but the interviews contextualized that
gap as not just an oversight but a consequence of systemic issues. This reflective ap-
proach strengthened the analysis but also highlighted that researcher interpretation plays
a role and becomes another reason to be cautious and transparent about potential biases.
Lastly, it is important to reflect on the researcher’s role and positionality within the study.
Being an observer of the Icelandic higher education landscape, and in this case, affiliated
with an external institution, provided a degree of neutrality in mapping courses across
all universities uniformly. However, familiarity with the Icelandic context was crucial for
making sense of subtle distinctions, such as understanding institutional hierarchies or
cultural norms. It is necessary to recognize that the researcher attended the University
of Iceland for three years during their bachelor’s degree, which inevitably influenced their
understanding of that institution compared to others in Iceland. Recognizing this potential
bias is important for transparency and interpreting the findings within the broader context
of Icelandic higher education. Efforts were made throughout the research to mitigate this
bias by consistently applying objective criteria during the mapping process.

7.3
Future Research Opportunities
Building on the findings and acknowledging the limitations of this thesis, several promising
directions for future research emerge to better understand innovation and entrepreneur-
ship education in Iceland.
• Long-term studies of curriculum development: Given that the innovation and
entrepreneurship landscape in universities is dynamic, a valuable next step would
be to conduct a long-term study. Future research could track changes in course
offerings and program structures over time and revisit the mapping in a few years to
see if the balance of “about,” “for,” and “through” courses shifts as awareness grows
and reforms are implemented. This would help assess the impact of recent initia-
tives, such as new policies, and determine whether the gap in experiential courses
narrows over time. A long-term study could also capture the life cycle of courses,
including which ones are discontinued and which new ones emerge, as well as the
factors influencing these trajectories.
• Instructor- and Student-Centric Research: While this thesis took a high-level
view of courses, future studies could delve deeper into the perspectives of those
most directly involved, such as instructors and students. For instructors, a series of
interviews across all universities could explore their experiences in designing and
delivering I&E courses. Such research might reveal practical insights into the ped-
agogical techniques they find effective, the obstacles they face in expanding expe-
riential learning, and the support or training they may need. Similarly, research fo-
cused on students could reveal the demand for entrepreneurship education among
students and the outcomes of participating in these courses. Understanding student
motivations, engagement levels, and post-course trajectories (e.g., do they start
businesses, apply skills in other jobs, or pursue further education) would provide
a measure of the effectiveness of current courses and highlight areas for improve-
ment.
• Impact Assessment of Experiential Learning: One critical question that remains
is how the relatively few experiential “through” courses in Iceland compare to more
traditional courses in terms of impact. Future research could conduct comparative
evaluations, comparing learning outcomes or entrepreneurial competency develop-
ment between students who took an experiential course and those who took only
lecture-based courses. By demonstrating the value or identifying the weaknesses
of hands-on entrepreneurship courses, researchers can build a stronger case for
universities and policymakers to invest in these approaches.
• Institutional Change Studies: Since this thesis highlighted structural and policy-
related barriers, another promising area is research on how to effect change within
the system. Researching the process and outcome of designing and implement-
ing a new teaching “through” course could yield practical knowledge on overcoming
specific obstacles, such as getting cross-department approval, designing assess-
ments for experiential projects, or attracting students from diverse backgrounds.
Understanding the levers of change is crucial for turning the recommendations of
this research into reality.
In conclusion, while this thesis has mapped the current state and highlighted key issues,
it also opens the door to a rich research agenda aimed at deepening and applying that
knowledge. By conducting further studies, researchers and practitioners can continue to
advance innovation and entrepreneurship education in Iceland, ensuring that it not only

of the Icelandic context.
