Abstract
Failures due to corrosion remain a major challenge in printed circuit boards (PCBs). Besides,
the continuous miniaturisation of electronic devices and high-frequency operation increases the
risk of electrochemical migration. While accelerated testing protocols typically rely on direct
current (DC) bias, real-world conditions in modern electronics often involve alternating signals.
Recent studies suggest that alternating current (AC) produces different corrosion mechanisms
than DC, but the impact of frequency and amplitude remains understudied.
This thesis investigates corrosion on tin-coated copper printed circuit boards subjected to
sinusoidal alternating potentials, to clarify how frequency and amplitude shape electrochemical
migration and related failure modes. Two copper pads with a thin tin substrate layer PCBs were
tested in a controlled MilliQ water layer (32µL using sinusoidal bias at selected amplitudes and
frequencies at fixed durations (30 minutes). The electrochemical response was monitored through
current–time curves and supported by cyclic voltammetry, ICP–OES of dissolved species, SEM
with EDS mapping, and pH indicators.
At 0.01 Hz, dendritic filaments initiated and bridged the gap between the pads, sometimes
rupturing and reforming. Increasing amplitude accelerated initiation and increased dissolution
measured by ICP–OES, and both Sn and Cu were detected in solution for the most aggressive
conditions. At 1 Hz, dendrites were not observed, although corrosion still proceeded at lower
currents, at the tests conducted at high voltage, consistent with intermittent dissolution and
partial passivation.
At 100 Hz, the response was dominated by non-faradaic charging, and
ICP–OES indicated no measurable metal dissolution.
The pH imaging demonstrated acidic
zones that alternated with the applied polarity, and SEM/EDS confirmed redistribution of Sn
and exposure of underlying Cu near attacked regions.
The results demonstrate a strong frequency dependence of AC-driven degradation in these
systems. Compared with DC behaviour reported in the literature, alternating polarity limits
sustained ion transport and suppresses dendrite persistence above a frequency threshold (1Hz).
These findings can help align accelerated testing and failure analysis protocols with the AC and
transient conditions common in modern electronics.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Printed Circuit Boards (PCBs) are the backbone of modern electronic systems, providing
mechanical support and electrical interconnection for components ranging from microchips to
passive elements.
As electronics become increasingly miniaturised and densely packed, the
reliability and long-term stability of PCBs are critical, particularly in high-reliability applications
such as aerospace, automotive, and medical devices.
This ongoing technological development has led to an increased demand for power, necessi-
tating the application of higher voltage levels to PCBs. The concurrent challenges of shrinking
distances between components and rising applied voltages present a significant threat to sys-
tem reliability, especially concerning the risk of corrosion. This is critical as modern industries
like renewable energy, electric transportation, and power conversion are undergoing a robust
transition towards electrification and depend heavily on these advanced and reliable electronic
technologies.
Corrosion poses a substantial threat to modern electronic devices and the infrastructure
they support, given their use in diverse applications and the necessity for them to operate
in challenging environments. In electronic systems, the interaction of high voltages, ranging
from biases of 2V to 450V, and in specific applications up to 1000V [8], with humidity can
trigger various moisture-related corrosion failures.
A direct consequence of reduced spacing
and increased voltage is the intensification of the electric field, which significantly enhances ion
movement, accelerating corrosion processes. A critical hazard associated with specific corrosion
mechanisms, such as Electrochemical Migration (ECM) and dendritic growth, is the potential
for short circuits, [8]. These failures can lead to electrical discharge and pose a serious fire risk
within electronic systems.
One of the most persistent threats to PCB reliability is corrosion. Exposure to humid envi-
ronments, ionic contamination, and fluctuating voltages can lead to electrochemical degradation
of metallic surfaces, especially at solder joints, vias, and exposed copper or tin finishes.
In
harsh or uncontrolled environments, corrosion-driven failures such as short circuits caused by

1.1
Tin-Coated Copper PCBs in Electronics
Copper is widely used in PCB manufacturing due to its electrical conductivity and affordabil-
ity. However, it’s highly susceptible to oxidation and corrosion when exposed to moisture, salts,
or airborne contaminants. To protect copper surfaces and enhance solderability, manufacturers
commonly apply a thin layer of tin. This coating acts as a physical barrier to oxidation and
ensures good wetting behaviour during soldering processes such as wave and reflow soldering.
Several techniques are used to deposit tin onto copper, including Hot Air Solder Levelling
(HASL), immersion tin, and electroplating. These surface finishes are chosen not only for their
performance but also for their compatibility with lead-free soldering, making them standard in
modern electronics manufacturing.
However, tin is not chemically inert. Under certain conditions, particularly in the presence of
electrical bias and moisture, it can undergo localised corrosion phenomena such as the growth of
metallic filaments (dendrites) or spontaneous tin whiskers. These structures can bridge adjacent
conductors and cause short circuits. In systems where both copper and tin are exposed, galvanic
coupling may accelerate copper corrosion.
1.2
AC corrosion on PCBs
Corrosion in PCBs has traditionally been studied using direct current (DC) biasing methods,
where a constant electrical potential is applied across conductive traces to simulate harsh oper-
ating conditions. This approach has proven effective in revealing mechanisms such as dendrite
growth, electrochemical migration, and metallic filament formation across moisture films. One
notable example is the work of Caterina Natali ([8]), who investigated dendrite formation be-
2
==PAGE:19==
tween tin-coated copper electrodes under sustained DC bias. Her study focused on characterising
the electrochemical behaviour of Sn–Cu systems by monitoring current–time responses during
prolonged DC exposure and analysing post-corrosion morphology using SEM and EDS. She
observed the formation of metallic dendrites bridging the electrode gap, with results highlight-
ing how the applied voltage, distance between electrodes, and electrolyte conditions influenced
dendritic growth and failure mechanisms. While this work was centred on DC corrosion, it pro-
vides a valuable reference for understanding electrochemically driven degradation in tin-based
systems.
However, electronics are also used in alternating voltage. Instead, they are exposed to fluc-
tuating voltages caused by switching transients, power supply ripple, pulse-width modulation,
and alternating signal patterns. These conditions give rise to time-varying electric fields that
differ fundamentally from constant DC bias, both in terms of driving forces and electrochem-
ical dynamics. The corrosion behaviour of materials under alternating current (AC) remains
significantly less understood, despite its relevance to modern circuit environments.
1.3
Motivation for the research
This thesis seeks to expand the understanding of PCB corrosion by investigating tin-coated
copper electrodes under sinusoidal AC bias at varying amplitudes and frequencies. The goal is to
characterise how alternating fields influence electrochemical processes compared to traditional
DC-driven degradation, with particular focus on dendrite formation, current density evolution,
and dissolution behaviour. To support interpretation, cyclic voltammetry (CV) is employed to
identify redox windows and reaction thresholds relevant under AC conditions. By addressing
environments dominated by switching noise, PWM, and transient signals—conditions not well
represented by standard DC bias tests. This work aims to provide insights that can improve
accelerated ageing protocols, failure analysis, and reliability assessment in modern electronics.
The central hypothesis is that alternating fields modulate electrochemical migration differently
than DC, potentially delaying or altering dendrite growth mechanisms.
The scope of this thesis is limited to investigating the short-term electrochemical behaviour of
tin-coated copper PCBs under sinusoidal AC bias in a controlled environment. All experiments
were conducted using MilliQ water as the electrolyte. This was done to isolate the influence of
AC amplitude and frequency by excluding interference from additional ions or contaminants.
Because of this, this work does not address the effects of real-world variables, such as industrial
contamination, mixed alloys, or alternative electrolytes. Furthermore, the study is focused on
early-stage corrosion processes such as dendrite initiation, current–time evolution, and disso-
lution behaviour, rather than long-term device lifetime testing or mechanical reliability under
service conditions.

Chapter 2
Theoretical Background
2.1
Printed Circuit Boards (PCBs)
Printed Circuit Boards (PCBs) are the foundational platform for nearly all modern electronic
devices. They provide both mechanical support and electrical interconnection for integrated cir-
cuits, passive components, and connectors. First pioneered in the early 1900s, their widespread
use began during World War II, when the U.S. Army adopted the technology for proximity
fuses.
Since then, PCBs have undergone major evolutions, from single-sided copper boards
using through-hole mounting to today’s compact, multi-layered, surface-mount designs. [9]
Modern PCBs are typically built on an FR4 substrate, a flame-retardant composite made
of woven glass fibre and epoxy resin. This material is favoured for its low water absorption,
mechanical strength, and electrical insulation, even under humid conditions. Alternatives such as
polyimide and Teflon-based laminates are used in applications requiring higher thermal stability
or specific dielectric properties, although these come at a higher manufacturing cost. [9]
Figure 2.1: 3-D schematic design of a multi-layer printed circuit board (PCB) board composed
of four copper layers (two internal layers), five dielectric layers, and the top and bottom mask
layers.
The traces and conducting layers in PCBs are typically made from copper, due to its high
conductivity and cost-effectiveness. These layers are protected with surface finishes that en-
5
==PAGE:22==
sure solderability and provide some resistance to environmental degradation. Common finishes
include Hot Air Solder Levelling (HASL), Electroless Nickel Immersion Gold (ENIG), and im-
mersion tin. HASL is widely used due to its low cost, while ENIG is gaining popularity for
its flat surface and reliable solderability. Newer systems, such as Electroless Nickel Electroless
Palladium Immersion Gold (ENEPIG), offer superior corrosion resistance, albeit at a higher
cost. [9], [10]
A commonly used surface finish, especially for budget-sensitive and lead-free applications,
is immersion tin, [11]. This process deposits a thin layer of tin directly onto the copper traces,
forming a Cu–Sn intermetallic interface. Immersion tin is attractive due to its simplicity, good
solderability, and compatibility with reflow processes. However, it introduces unique corrosion
challenges. Tin is an active metal that can oxidise or dissolve under certain electrochemical
conditions, particularly when moisture and ionic contaminants are present.
Sn-coated Cu systems also pose specific risks for electrochemical migration. If the tin layer
is thin, porous, or damaged during handling or soldering, the underlying copper can become
exposed, leading to the formation of microgalvanic cells. Moreover, tin itself is known to grow
metallic filaments, either in the form of whiskers under mechanical stress, or dendrites under
electrical bias, which can lead to short circuits or erratic behaviour in high-density circuits. [12]
Despite these protective layers, PCBs remain vulnerable to electrochemical degradation. Sur-
face finishes can develop porosity during fabrication or thermal cycling, exposing the underlying
conductive metals to moisture and contaminants. For instance, thin gold layers in ENIG coat-
ings may be insufficient to completely protect the nickel beneath, increasing the risk of galvanic
corrosion. [12]
2.2
General Principles of Corrosion in PCBs
Corrosion, fundamentally defined as the degradation of a metal due to a reaction with its
environment, affects not only metals but also other materials like polymers and ceramics, leading
to a decrease in mechanical properties and alteration of chemical structure.
In the context
of electronic devices, particularly Printed Circuit Boards (PCBs), the issue is magnified by
advancements like miniaturisation, which results in closer spacing between components and
an increased electric field, making corrosion cell formation easier. For corrosion to occur in
electronics, three essential factors must be present: (i) the presence of metals/alloys, (ii) potential
bias/electric field, and (iii) humidity levels that determine the thickness of the water layer.
Environmental conditions and production residues, such as solder flux, also significantly influence
the amount and nature of residues on PCB surfaces, which can accelerate corrosion [12].
These three coexisting factors enable the formation of a corrosion cell, which can be either
galvanic or electrolytic.
An electrolytic cell arises when closely spaced conduction lines or
component terminals are oppositely biased and connected by a water layer due to an external
6
==PAGE:23==
power supply. Conversely, a galvanic cell forms due to a difference in electrochemical potential
between two physically contacting metals connected by an electrolyte, causing the less noble
metal to act as the anode and corrode.
The two cell types are schematically illustrated in
Figure 2.2, which highlights the difference between externally driven electrolytic processes and
spontaneously occurring galvanic coupling.
Figure 2.2: Schematic comparison of a galvanic cell (left) and an electrolytic cell (right), from
[2].
While pure water has limited conductivity, making it an effective insulator, process-related
ionic and hygroscopic residues on the PCB surface dissolve into any accumulated water, signifi-
cantly increasing its conductivity. These residues also lower the critical relative humidity (cRH)
required for a water film to form on the PCB surface, accelerating the initiation of corrosion,
[13].
The theoretical understanding of corrosion also considers both thermodynamic and kinetic
aspects. Thermodynamics dictate the relative tendencies of corrosion reactions, which can be
visualised using Potential-pH (Pourbaix) diagrams, [14]. These diagrams graphically represent
the stability regions of metal and corrosion products as a function of pH and cell potential,
helping predict the types of products expected in different environments and how reactions
might evolve.
Kinetics, on the other hand, describes the speed at which a corrosion reaction proceeds
under given conditions, influenced by electrochemical tendencies, metallurgical conditions (like
electrode composition, structure, and morphology), and environmental conditions (temperature,
relative humidity, ionic contamination, and applied voltage). Phenomena like passivity, where a
metal, though active, corrodes slowly due to a protective film, and polarisation, which involves
changes in electrode potentials due to current flow, also significantly affect corrosion rates.

==PAGE:24==
Failure Mechanisms in PCBs
In PCBs, specific failure modes linked to these principles are particularly prominent. Among
the various degradation processes in PCBs, three failure mechanisms are of primary importance:
Leakage Current (LC) due to a reduction in surface insulation resistance (SIR), Electrochemical
Migration (ECM), and Conductive Anodic Filament (CAF) formation.
Leakage Current (LC) and Surface Insulation Resistance (SIR). Leakage current is
not a catastrophic failure mode itself, but rather an extrinsic material condition that precedes
more severe failure mechanisms, [8]. It represents the progressive reduction in the electrical
resistance of the PCB surface, quantified by the Surface Insulation Resistance (SIR).
The underlying mechanism begins with the formation of a thin electrolyte layer (TEL) on
the PCB surface when exposed to humidity. Water vapour (H2O) condenses and adsorbs onto
the surface, often through hydrogen bonding, creating a film with thicknesses ranging from
approximately 1 nm to 10 µm. The formation of this TEL is strongly promoted by hygroscopic
ionic substances present on the surface.
Manufacturing residues, particularly from solder flux, are a common source of such contam-
inants. Halides, halogens, and weak organic acids (WOAs) readily dissolve into the adsorbed
water layer, converting it into a highly conductive electrolyte. Once this occurs, the effective
SIR is drastically reduced, and a leakage current can flow between biased conductors through
the conductive water film, [15] [16].
Although this current may not directly cause device failure, it significantly hampers circuit
functionality by introducing instability or noise. More critically, it establishes the necessary
electrochemical environment for subsequent failure modes. In this sense, LC and SIR reduction
act as precursors to ECM and CAF, marking the first stage in the degradation pathway of PCBs
under humid and contaminated conditions. [8].
Electrochemical Migration (ECM). Electrochemical Migration (ECM) is an ionic trans-
port process that occurs under a direct current (DC) voltage bias and results in the growth of
conductive metallic filaments (dendrites) between adjacent conductors. The mechanism proceeds
in three main steps, [16].
First, anodic dissolution takes place at the positively biased electrode.
The metal (M)
undergoes oxidation to produce metal ions and electrons:
M →Mn+ + ne−
At the same time, water electrolysis occurs at the anode, generating H+ ions and producing an
acidic local environment.
Second, the metal ions migrate through the electrolyte film toward the cathode.
Their
transport is driven by multiple factors: the applied electric field (E = V/d), concentration
8
==PAGE:25==
gradients created by the accumulation of cations near the anode and hydroxide ions near the
cathode, and, under high potentials, convection arising from hydrogen evolution at the cathode.
Third, reduction occurs at the cathode, where the metal ions accept electrons and are de-
posited as metallic species:
Mn+ + ne−→M
The deposited metal grows in the form of dendritic filaments, which propagate toward the anode
due to electric field intensification at the dendrite tips. Concurrently, water electrolysis at the
cathode produces OH−ions, resulting in a locally alkaline environment.
Conductive Anodic Filament (CAF). Conductive Anodic Filament (CAF) formation is
an electrochemically induced failure mechanism that occurs within the laminate of a PCB rather
than on its external surface. CAF filaments typically propagate along the glass–epoxy inter-
face of FR-4 substrates, where structural weaknesses such as microcracks, voids, or resin–fibre
separations provide preferential pathways.
The process requires moisture absorption into the laminate and the presence of an applied
electric field. Once water and ionic species penetrate the internal layers and connect copper
conductors, electrochemical reactions are initiated. Anodic dissolution and cathodic deposition
occur like ECM, but confined within the bulk material. Unlike ECM, which results in metallic
dendrite formation, CAF produces conductive filaments composed of copper-containing salts.
CAF is particularly critical in multilayer PCBs, where reduced spacing between inner conduc-
tors increases susceptibility, and internal failures are difficult to detect through surface inspection
[12].
Other Corrosion Mechanisms. Beyond these primary modes, additional corrosion pro-
cesses can also compromise PCB reliability. Galvanic corrosion may arise when dissimilar metals,
such as gold and nickel in surface finishes, are exposed to electrolytes; porosity in the noble metal
layer accelerates localised attack of the underlying metal, [17]. Gas-phase corrosion is another
relevant pathway, particularly in polluted or industrial environments. Reactive gases such as
H2S, SO2, or NOx dissolve into moisture films, forming acids that aggressively attack copper
or silver. These processes highlight the influence of material selection, process cleanliness, and
environmental exposure on the long-term performance of electronic systems.
2.3
Capacitive vs. Faradaic Behaviour
Capacitive and Faradaic behaviours represent two fundamental and distinct electrochemical
phenomena occurring at an electrode-electrolyte interface. Capacitive behaviour, also known as
non-faradaic, fundamentally involves the accumulation of charge at this interface, leading to the
formation of an electrical double layer, [18]. From a theoretical perspective, under alternating
current (AC) conditions, particularly at sufficiently high frequencies, almost all current is the-
orised to flow through this double-layer capacitance. This effectively insulates the system by
9
==PAGE:26==
minimising processes like water decomposition [18]. In theoretical models, this phenomenon is
often referred to as background charging current or non-faradaic background current contribu-
tions, as it does not involve electron transfer across the interface, [19].
In contrast, Faradaic behaviour is characterised by electron transfer steps or electrochemical
reactions that occur directly at the electrode surface, [20], [21]. These processes involve the
oxidation or reduction of electroactive species, where material moves to the electrode surface to
exchange electrons.
2.4
Electrochemical Techniques Used in this Thesis
2.4.1
Cyclic voltammetry
Cyclic voltammetry (CV) is an electroanalytical technique used to study electrochemical
reactions by linearly cycling the potential of a working electrode and measuring the resulting
current. Since corrosion is fundamentally a destructive electrochemical reaction of a metal with
its environment involving electron transfer, CV is used for its analysis. It allows for the rapid
observation of redox behaviour over a wide potential range, revealing information about anodic
oxidation (metal dissolution) and cathodic reduction processes, [20].
2.4.2
ICP-OES
Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectrometry (ICP-OES) is an analytical technique for ele-
mental analysis, capable of identifying elements from the periodic table even in low-concentration
solutions. The theoretical basis involves using a high-temperature plasma to ionise a sample.
These ions are then directed into a mass analyser, a quadrupole, that identifies them based on
their mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) through the application of AC and DC potentials. A detector
collects the electrical signal produced by these separated ions.
In the context of corrosion detection, ICP-OES provides information regarding the concen-
tration of dissolved metal ions produced during the corrosion process. By analysing electrolyte
solutions collected after tests, researchers can quantify the mass of corroded metals.
2.4.3
SEM/EDS
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) is an analytical technique utilised to observe the mor-
phology of a sample’s surface with high magnification, providing detailed topographic informa-
tion. The theoretical basis involves directing a beam of electrons onto the surface of a sample.
The interaction of these incident electrons with the sample produces various signals, including
secondary and backscattered electrons, which are detected and then used to construct an image.
Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDS) is frequently coupled with SEM, allowing for
the elemental composition analysis of specific areas on the sample. This is accomplished by
10
==PAGE:27==
detecting characteristic X-rays emitted by elements within the sample as they are excited by the
electron beam, with each element producing a unique X-ray signature. In Figure 2.3, the SEM
setup can be compared to an optical microscope in terms of beam focusing and signal detection,
with the addition of an EDS (here labelled EDX) detector that collects the characteristic X-ray
information for elemental analysis.
Figure 2.3: Schematic comparison of an optical microscope (left) and a scanning electron mi-
croscope (SEM, right). The SEM column shows successive electron lenses focusing the beam,
with detectors for secondary/backscattered electrons and an EDS (labelled EDX) detector for
elemental analysis, [3].
SEM and EDS can be used to study corrosion on PCBs. SEM can be used to detect defects
and corrosion by-products, and EDS identifies the elemental composition of these products,
confirming the presence of metals like tin and copper.
2.4.4
pH testing
pH measures the concentration of hydrogen ions H+ in solution, and its changes can be
tracked using pH indicators that shift colour depending on acidity or alkalinity. In strongly
acidic regions, the colour turns red, while in alkaline environments it turns blue. Regarding
corrosion, pH indicators can be used to visually confirm electrochemical reactions: acidic zones
form near anodic sites where metal ions hydrolyse and release protons, while cathodic areas
tend to generate hydroxides. Thus, pH indicators allow identification of anodic and cathodic
processes, supporting and corroborating the interpretation of corrosion behaviour.

2.5
State of the art
2.5.1
AC and DC induced corrosion
AC-induced corrosion manifests differently and often less severely than direct current (DC)
corrosion, significantly impacting the reliability of electronic systems. While DC voltage often
leads to the formation of an impermeable oxide layer on the cathode and corrosion on the anode,
hindering effective current transfer and reducing efficiency, AC helps overcome these issues by
promoting uniform dissolution of electrodes and resulting in a smoother electrode surface with
less localised corrosion, [4]. In Figure 2.4, it’s possible to see how DC had a much aggressive
effect than AC.
Figure 2.4: SEM images of the anode after electrocoagulation by (a) AC and (b) DC, [4].
Studies show that time to failure increases under AC conditions compared to DC, and this
time further increases with frequency, [7]. A key reason for this is that AC bias, especially
at frequencies above 5 Hz or 60 Hz ([7]), is often unfavourable for the formation of traditional
metallic dendrites. Instead of dendrites bridging conductive tracks, failure under AC often occurs
due to the accumulation of non-metallic corrosion products on each electrode. This failure mode
is attributed to the continuous change of anodes and cathodes and insufficient time for metal
ions to diffuse to the counter electrodes after anodic dissolution. Furthermore, pH changes near
the electrodes are smaller under AC bias compared to DC, which reduces solution conductivity
and the charge transfer reaction, thereby increasing the time to failure due to electrochemical
12
==PAGE:29==
migration (ECM), [7].
The waveform and frequency of the AC signal are essential in the observed corrosion effects.
A DC voltage pattern acts as an accelerated test for dendrite growth compared to other AC
waveforms, such as square, sawtooth, and sine waves. Conversely, triangle and square wave-
forms can improve corrosion robustness by slowing dendrite growth, as dendrites grow slowest
under these conditions, [5]. In Figure 2.5, it’s possible to see how various voltage patterns could
definitely induce shape change of the electrode in the y direction as a consequence of dendrite
formation (figure obtained from [5]). In particular, triangle-waveform currents have been found
to create the highest thermal fluctuations and stress variations in solder joints due to a lack of
relaxation time at critical states, which intensifies stress in the solder material. This intensified
stress, in turn, leads to an increase in brittle intermetallic compounds (IMCs) and a subsequent
degradation of mechanical properties, [22]. While AC generally does not contribute to elec-
tromigration (EM) because any mass migration from a positive half-period is recovered in the
negative half-period, the Joule heating generated by AC stressing creates significant thermal
gradients, making AC useful for studying thermomigration (TM), [23]. This thermomechanical
damage, rather than EM, often becomes the primary failure mechanism under AC stressing,
[22].
Figure 2.5: Electrode shape changes and tin ion flux distribution at t = 50 s under different
voltage patterns: (a) DC, (b) square wave, (c) sawtooth wave, (d) sine wave, [5].
13
==PAGE:30==
Studies have also shown that AC also has a depassivating effect on the normally passive
region of a material’s polarisation curve, an effect that increases with superposed AC density
but diminishes as frequency increases. For certain materials like AISI 316 stainless steel, AC
can cause a substantial shift of the corrosion potential (Ecorr) towards a more active (corrosive)
direction, as seen in Figure 2.6, [6].
However, in mild steel systems, AC effectiveness may
increase with density, whereas in stainless steel systems, it tends to decrease, possibly due to
fundamental differences in their passivation mechanisms (thin adsorbed oxygen film versus thick
oxide diffusion barrier).
Figure 2.6: Corrosion potential (Ecorr)shift caused by superposed AC for AISI 316 SS in sulfuric
acid, [6].
From a practical standpoint, if manufacturing-related impurities are avoided, the effect of
ECM can be virtually excluded with AC voltages over 5 Hz, [24]. Nonetheless, short metal lines
with pre-existing thermal voids, when subjected to symmetrical bidirectional AC currents, can
still experience a notable resistance increase if specific temperature oscillations are synchronised
with current pulses, such as positive pulses occurring at higher temperatures than negative ones,
[25]. Therefore, AC-induced corrosion is a multifaceted phenomenon influenced by electrical,
thermal, and material properties, with its mechanisms differing significantly from those observed
under DC conditions.
2.5.2
Behaviour of Sn and Cu under AC
Tin and copper are widely utilised in Printed Circuit Boards, with copper forming the
conductive traces and tin employed in surface finishes and solder alloys. These materials are
susceptible to corrosion phenomena, notably electrochemical migration (ECM). While ECM
is predominantly associated with direct current (DC) voltage conditions and is generally not
detectable with AC voltage over 60 Hz, research indicates that alternating current (AC) can
still significantly influence the material-specific behaviour of Sn and Cu [24].
Tin’s behaviour under AC bias differs notably from DC. Studies on fine-pitch IC substrates
14
==PAGE:31==
show that ECM time to failure can increase dramatically under AC bias, further increasing in
proportion to the applied frequency, [7]. Under AC bias, typical dendrites or conductive anodic
filaments are often not observed. Instead, residues in powder state are found. This conclusion is
reached based on the images in Figure 2.7, where dendrites of cathodes and conductive anodic
filaments of anodes were not observed, and residue in a powder state was observed. This is
attributed to the continuous alternation of electrode polarity, which provides insufficient time
for metal ions to diffuse and deposit at a stable counter electrode. Consequently, ions tend to
redeposit on the original electrode or form metallic salts with hydroxyl ions.
Figure 2.7: Morphology of COF patterns after water drop test under AC bias for 1800 s at
different frequencies, [7].
Copper (Cu) also exhibits specific behaviors under AC. While AC significantly improves
gold leaching efficiency from PCBs, it offers only a minor increase in copper leaching (around
3%), [26]. Electrochemical oxidation of copper electrodes under pulse alternating current (PAC)
results in the formation of various copper oxide products (Cu2O, CuO, or bilayered composites),
with the oxidation rate correlating with current density, [27]. The cathodic pulse during PAC
helps in the rapid removal of these oxide products through hydrogen evolution. In Sn-based
solder joints under AC stressing, copper atoms are a dominant diffusing species, migrating from
hotter to colder regions and reacting with nickel atoms to form (NixCu1−x)6Sn5 IMCs within
the solder matrix.
Studies on copper-tin alloys under alternate anodic and cathodic polarisation, which sim-
ulates AC conditions, reveal that alloys with low tin content (less than 14 wt% Sn) behave
similarly to pure copper, with tin primarily affecting copper oxidation by reducing passivation
energy and slowing the formation of copper oxides. Alloys with intermediate tin content (14-32
15
==PAGE:32==
wt% Sn) also show behaviour similar to pure copper but with additional oxidation steps at-
tributed to the copper component of the δ phase. Higher tin content alloys (38-59 wt% Sn and
¿60 wt% Sn) behave as a mixture of pure copper and tin, with each metal component oxidising
independently, [28].
In summary, the material-specific behaviour of tin and copper is diverse, which makes it dif-
ficult to extrapolate to different systems. While AC can suppress traditional dendrite formation,
it introduces other degradation mechanisms such as powder-state residues, intensified thermal
stress, and accelerated IMC growth. The varying electrochemical responses of tin, copper, and
their intermetallic compounds are critical for understanding and mitigating failures in modern
electronic devices. This understanding is essential for designing PCBs with improved corrosion
resistance and long-term reliability in varying operating environments.

In ICP-OES, liquid samples are nebulised into a high-temperature argon plasma (∼10,000 K),
where they are atomised and ionised. The resulting ions are guided through a series of cones and
ion optics into a quadrupole mass analyser, which separates them by their mass-to-charge ratio
(m/z). The detected ion signals are converted into elemental concentrations by comparison with
external calibration standards.
The entire 32 µL electrolyte volume, together with an additional 8 µL of MilliQ water added
after the AC tests to rinse the cell and recover the remaining liquid, was collected from samples
destined for ICP testing. No further treatment was performed before analysis. Calibration was
carried out using multi-element standards covering the relevant concentration range.

CV experiment testing method
The cyclic voltammetry (CV) tests were carried out in order to identify which electrochemical
reactions take place on the Sn-coated Cu PCBs under different bias conditions. The aim was
22
==PAGE:39==
to complement the analysis performed with the AC tests by providing potential-dependent
information that could be cross-checked with Pourbaix diagrams and pH measurements.
Two types of CV experiments were performed:
• Low-voltage runs (±1.5 V and ±2 V): designed to resolve reactions that occur close
in potential, such as the successive ionisation of Sn to Sn2+ and Sn4+. At small voltage
ranges, these subtle processes are easier to distinguish, since they are not masked by large
overpotentials or competing reactions.
• High-voltage runs (±5 V and ±10 V): performed to investigate the system’s behaviour
under strongly corrosive conditions. These tests reveal the onset of aggressive oxidation,
oxide/hydroxide formation, and in some cases dendritic growth. At these voltages, the
system evolves dynamically with cycle number, showing changes in passivation and current
response that provide insight into long-term degradation.
In addition, the scan rates ranged from as low as 10 mV/s up to 1 V/s. Slow scan rates
emphasise equilibrium processes, making small redox steps and reaction onsets more visible,
while faster scan rates enhance the capacitive component and highlight kinetic limitations.
Although a broad range of voltage windows and scan rates were tested (as described here),
only one representative sweep is presented in the Results section, since it was sufficient to
illustrate the key electrochemical processes. The CV sweeps covering other voltage ranges and
scan rates are included in Appendix X for completeness.

Chapter 5
Discussion
This section analyses the results presented earlier and integrates the different experimental
techniques into a unified interpretation of the results.
The cyclic voltammetry (CV) and pH indicator experiments provide the fundamental basis
for understanding the electrochemical reactions happening in the system. For the combinations
that generated results of interest (as in, visual corrosion or dendrite formation), the current–time
(i–t) curves were correlated with the elemental concentrations measured by ICP-OES and the
post-test surface characterisation from SEM/EDS.
5.1
CV
To identify the underlying reactions in the samples, the Pourbaix diagram of tin was con-
sulted (Figs. 5.1)
57
==PAGE:74==
Figure 5.1: Pourbaix diagram for tin considering the hydroxides Sn(OH)2 and Sn(OH)4.
Since the tests were conducted in an aqueous environment (a thin MilliQ water layer cov-
ering the solder pads), the Pourbaix diagram including the hydroxides Sn(OH)2 and Sn(OH)4
(Fig. 5.1) is the most relevant reference.
Within this framework, the features observed at 0.3 V and 0.5 V are interpreted as the
oxidation of metallic tin to Sn2+ and the subsequent oxidation of Sn2+ to Sn4+, respectively.
Although the Pourbaix diagram does not directly assign these potentials, it indicates that the
stable aqueous species are the hydroxides Sn(OH)2 and Sn(OH)4. Thus, while CV detects the
electron-transfer steps
Sn →Sn2+ + 2e−
and
Sn2+ →Sn4+ + 2e−,
The actual transformations in the aqueous environment are
Sn →Sn(OH)2
and
Sn(OH)2 →Sn(OH)4.
The higher values observed here (0.3 V and 0.5 V) can be explained by the particularities of
the experimental system: (i) the surface is not pure Sn but tin-coated solder pads, and (ii) the
58
==PAGE:75==
pads are electrically isolated from each other, with conduction provided only by a thin MilliQ
water film that is a very poor ionic conductor. These factors introduce resistance and shift the
apparent potentials compared to bulk electrochemical measurements.
When the scan rate was decreased to 20 mV/s, only the anodic feature at ∼0.5 V remained
clearly visible, while the earlier feature at ∼0.3 V disappeared (Figures 4.21–4.22e). In principle,
reducing the scan rate should enhance the resolution of both oxidation steps, as the slower
potential sweep allows more time for the corresponding transformations to occur. In practice,
however, only the higher-potential step became more distinct, while the lower-potential feature
was no longer observable. A possible explanation is that the first transition (Sn →Sn(OH)2)
involves the formation of a metastable Sn(II) species with a narrow stability window in the
Pourbaix diagram.
At slower scan rates, this intermediate may be consumed more rapidly
or obscured by background processes, causing its current response to merge into the baseline.
By contrast, the subsequent Sn(OH)2 →Sn(OH)4 transition at ∼0.5 V is both slower and
thermodynamically more favoured, which could explain why this step remains as the dominant
anodic feature. This interpretation should be regarded as tentative, and further experiments
would be required to confirm the mechanism behind the disappearance of the lower-potential
feature.
A faint third feature emerges in the anodic branch at approximately 1.0–1.2 V, visible only
from the fifth cycle onwards 4.20e. This is consistent with the onset of the oxygen evolution
reaction (OER),
2 H2O →O2 + 4H+ + 4e−,
which is thermodynamically expected near 1 V in neutral aqueous solutions (see Fig. 5.1, water
stability line). The fact that it becomes discernible only in later cycles can be rationalised in
two ways: (i) the progressive formation of tin hydroxides and oxides (Sn(OH)2, Sn(OH)4) alters
the surface, lowering the local overpotential for OER; and (ii) The accumulation of corrosion
products modifies the conductivity of the thin MilliQ water film, making gas evolution currents
easier to detect against the background. It should be noted that no visible bubbles were observed
during the tests, which is consistent with the very low current densities reached in this thin-film
system: oxygen is indeed generated, but at a rate too small to nucleate macroscopic bubbles.
Thus, the feature at ∼1.2 V represents the transition from metal-centred electrochemical pro-
cesses to electrolyte-driven reactions, marking the water stability limit in this system.
The cyclic voltammetry experiments conducted at high voltage (±10 V, 100 mV/s) revealed
sharp peaks and irregular features. These disturbances, absent in the low-voltage tests, can be
attributed to dendrites forming between the solder pads. This is reflected by the sudden changes
in the current response. Important to note that, because in the third cycle there is no irregular
high current peak reached, there was no fully formed dendrite between the pads at that stage.
59
==PAGE:76==
This means that there was formation ( second cycle), breakage (third cycle), and reformation
(fourth and fifth cycles) of dendrites. Even though the CV waveform differs from sinusoidal AC,
this analysis further justifies the expected behaviour of the system under high voltage bias.
5.2
pH
The pH indicator tests provide valuable insight into the localised reactions occurring on the
PCB surface. Only red zones were observed, while no blue colouring appeared in any of the
tests, meaning that the dominant effect was localised acidification of the water layer rather than
alkalinization. The sequential images at ±10 V and 0.01 Hz (Figure 4.25) demonstrate how
the red zone shifts from one solder pad to the other as the polarity changes. This behaviour
illustrates the immediate response of the system to the alternating potential.
The localised red colouring can be explained by the hydrolysis of tin ions generated at the
anode.
When Sn is oxidised to Sn2+ or Sn4+ (as mentioned above in regards to the cyclic
voltammetry results), the ions rapidly hydrolyse in the thin water layer, releasing protons and
thereby decreasing the local pH:
Sn2+ + 2H2O →Sn(OH)2(s) + 2H+
Sn4+ + 4H2O →Sn(OH)4(s) + 4H+
The appearance of red colouration around corrosion precipitates (Figure 4.26) further sup-
ports this interpretation, as the sites where corrosion products formed were consistently sur-
rounded by acidic regions.
These findings can be linked to the electrochemical steps identified in the cyclic voltammetry
results.
The oxidation of Sn to Sn2+ and Sn4+ corresponds directly to the onset of anodic
processes observed in CV. The pH results provide a complementary validation, showing that
these oxidation steps are accompanied by hydrolysis reactions that stabilise the products as
Sn(OH)2 and Sn(OH)4. This is also consistent with the Pourbaix diagram (Figure 5.1), which
predicts the formation of these hydroxides as the stable phases under the tested conditions.
In summary, the pH tests provide direct visual confirmation that anodic dissolution of tin
under AC bias leads to hydrolysis reactions, producing Sn hydroxides and lowering the local
pH. This observation links the electrochemical signatures in CV with the chemical species pre-
dicted by the Pourbaix diagram, thereby reinforcing the interpretation of the overall corrosion
mechanism in this system.
60
==PAGE:77==
5.3
10V
The behaviour at 10 V varied strongly with frequency. At 0.01 Hz (Figure 4.4), dendritic
growth occurred in most runs, but the stability of these filaments differed substantially. Three
characteristic behaviours were distinguished: (i) dendrites that formed early and produced a
sustained increase in current (Figure 4.5), (ii) dendrites that broke and reformed, leading to sec-
ondary peaks later in the test (Figure 4.6), and (iii) dendrites that broke shortly after formation
without significant reformation, resulting in stable low current levels. Similar scenarios have
been reported under DC bias by Natali [8], but under AC conditions, the timing and magnitude
of reformation events were less consistent between runs. The ICP data (Table 4.1) support these
observations: Run 1, which exhibited repeated dendrite growth and rupture, showed the highest
Sn release and the only measurable Cu. In contrast, Run 2 produced high current values but
a profile consistent with stable dendritic filaments, explaining the absence of Cu in solution.
Runs 3–5 further demonstrate the correlation between current excursions and Sn dissolution:
when the current was larger (Run 5), more Sn was detected, whereas the weakest response
(Run 4) corresponded to minimal Sn release. SEM and EDS analyses provide additional con-
firmation, as the SEM images at 10 V and 0.01 Hz (Figure 4.32) reveal dendritic features and
extensive attack of the solder pad surface, while the EDS maps (Figure 4.33) show significant
Sn deposition in the gap and localised Cu exposure, consistent with the ICP trends.
At 1 Hz (Figure 4.10), dendritic features were no longer observed. The absence of sharp
current transients indicates that the polarity reversal at this frequency suppressed significant
ionic transport, with the response instead dominated by capacitive charging and discharging of
the electrical double layer. Nevertheless, ICP analysis showed measurable amounts of Sn and
even a small quantity of Cu, suggesting that corrosion still occurred in the form of more uniform
surface dissolution. In this case, localised thinning of the Sn coating was sufficient to expose
small areas of the Cu substrate, even without dendrite rupture events. No SEM or EDS was
performed for this frequency, as no visible features were recorded during testing.
At 100 Hz (Figure 4.12), the current response reflected purely capacitive behaviour, with
gradual baseline shifts and no evidence of dendrite formation. At such a high frequency, the
cycle duration is too short for meaningful ionic migration or deposition to occur, and alternating
polarity prevents the build-up of sustained electrochemical processes. The ICP results confirmed
this interpretation, as neither Sn nor Cu was detected in solution (Table 4.1), indicating the
absence of measurable corrosion. No SEM or EDS was performed, and the conclusions for this
condition rely exclusively on the AC and ICP results.
Overall, these findings show a clear frequency dependence: dendritic growth and associated
Sn/Cu release dominated at 0.01 Hz, uniform but non-dendritic corrosion occurred at 1 Hz, and
at 100 Hz only capacitive processes were active. The trend of decreasing corrosion with increasing
61
==PAGE:78==
AC frequency is consistent with reports in the literature. For example, studies on underground
metallic pipelines (X60–X80 steels) [29], stainless steels in acidic media [6], and carbon steel
electrodes [30] all confirm that higher AC frequencies diminish corrosion rates, although the
systems differ substantially from the present PCB setup. Likewise, the observation that AC itself
promotes corrosion aligns with broader findings on metallic systems, where AC interference was
shown to accelerate metal dissolution [30]. In addition, prior work on electrochemical migration
under AC bias has highlighted how polarity reversal alters the failure mechanism compared to
DC, delaying dendritic growth and favouring other corrosion forms [7]. While the materials and
geometries differ, these comparisons support the trends observed here, where dendritic activity
was prominent only at the lowest frequency and suppressed at higher ones.
5.4
48V
At 0.01 Hz (Figures 4.13–4.16), dendrite formation occurred in every run, with earlier initi-
ation compared to the equivalent 10 V tests. This is consistent with the stronger driving force
accelerating ion release into the electrolyte layer. However, the subsequent current profiles var-
ied between runs, producing three distinct behaviours: an early high peak followed by continued
growth (Figure 4.14), a peak followed by decay and slow recovery (Figure 4.15), and a peak
followed by persistently lower currents (Figure 4.16). The variability suggests that dendrite sta-
bility and reformation depend on local factors such as surface condition, passivation, or water
layer thickness.
The ICP analysis confirmed the aggressive nature of this condition (Table 4.2). Considerable
amounts of Sn were detected in both runs, and Cu appeared in solution in Run 1. The detection
of Cu demonstrates that the Sn coating was locally and irreversibly damaged, exposing the
underlying substrate. Once exposed, Cu dissolved more readily than Sn, explaining its higher
relative concentration compared with the 10 V tests.
The strong correlation between peak
current excursions and ICP concentrations supports the link between transient electrochemical
events and the extent of metal release.
SEM images (Figure 4.34) further complement these findings.
Sample 1 exhibited more
corrosion by-products in the gap between the solder pads compared with Sample 2, in agreement
with the ICP results that showed higher Sn and detectable Cu in Run 1.
The EDS maps
(Figures 4.35 and 4.36) also reflect this difference. In Sample 2, a small Cu signal was detected
in the EDS map despite no Cu being measured in the ICP. This discrepancy can be explained by
the fact that after the MilliQ water layer dried, some Cu residues may have remained trapped in
the gap (e.g. retained by capillary effects or incorporated into corrosion products). Such residues
would still be detectable locally by EDS but not fully released into solution for ICP detection.
At 1 Hz (Figures 4.31 and 4.17), no dendritic spikes were observed. Nevertheless, the mea-
sured currents were higher than those typically associated with purely capacitive behaviour.
62
==PAGE:79==
This can be explained by a gradual reduction of surface layer resistance, which allows a leakage
current to persist. Under these conditions, faradaic reactions occur without the formation of a
continuous conductive bridge in the form of dendrites.
The ICP analysis further supports this interpretation (Table 4.2). An inverse relationship
was observed between current amplitude and Sn concentration: runs with higher peak currents
produced lower dissolved Sn, consistent with rapid surface passivation. Under aggressive current
conditions, the Sn surface likely oxidised quickly to form a protective layer, which restricted
further dissolution. In contrast, under less aggressive conditions, dissolution proceeded more
steadily and higher Sn concentrations accumulated in the electrolyte.
The absence of Cu in all 48 V, 1 Hz runs reinforces this interpretation. Unlike the 10 V,
1 Hz tests, where some Cu was detected, the higher voltage appears to favour rapid passivation
of the Sn layer, preventing exposure of the underlying substrate. The passivating species are
most likely Sn oxides such as SnO or SnO2, which act as a barrier against further corrosion.
Three samples tested at 48 V and 1 Hz were also analysed with SEM and EDS. Visually,
the solder pads displayed heavy corrosion along the edges facing the inter-pad gap. In the SEM
images (Figure 4.37), darker spots on the pad surface were identified, and the corresponding
EDS maps confirmed these as regions of partially exposed Cu. This observation aligns with the
ICP results: despite local exposure of the substrate, Cu dissolution was not detected in solution,
indicating that the passivating surface layer limited its release. Moreover, only small amounts
of Sn and Cu were detected in the gap itself, consistent with the low Sn values and absence of
Cu in the ICP measurements.
At 100 Hz (Figure 4.18), the current response was weaker than at 1 Hz and showed no signs
of dendritic activity. The rapid polarity reversals at this frequency effectively suppress ionic
transport and prevent sustained corrosion processes. No ICP, SEM, or EDS measurements were
performed for this condition, as no visible corrosion was expected or observed. The behaviour
at 100 Hz is therefore interpreted solely from the AC results.
The same frequency-dependent trends observed at 10 V were also evident at 48 V, but
the higher potential accentuated both dissolution and passivation effects. At 0.01 Hz, dendrite
initiation occurred earlier and dissolution was more aggressive, consistent with literature showing
that increasing AC potential or current density directly accelerates corrosion rates in steels
[31, 32]. In particular, Goidanich et al. [31], working on carbon steel exposed to soil-simulating
conditions, reported that AC densities above 30 A/m2 markedly increased corrosion rates, and
values above 500 A/m2 produced very severe attack. Similarly, Yunovich et al. [33], studying
pipelines under high-voltage AC (HVAC) corridors, highlighted the role of Faradaic current in
driving this process. At 1 Hz, the 48 V tests demonstrated stronger passivation compared with
10 V, consistent with observations from Ghanbari et al. [32] on API X65 steel and Yang et al.
[34] on pipeline steels in alkaline environments, where AC amplitude was shown to influence
63
==PAGE:80==
oxide stability and localised pH near the electrode surface. While these studies were conducted
on steels in pipeline and soil environments rather than on Sn-coated PCBs, they reinforce that
although frequency governs the suppression of dendritic activity, voltage amplitude plays a
decisive role in controlling the aggressiveness of dissolution and the onset of passivation.

As shown in Figures 4.1–4.3, the measured currents at ±1 V remained in the low microampere
range (2–5 µA). At such low values, small fluctuations can be strongly influenced by ambient
temperature variations, surface irregularities, or residual impurities in the Milli-Q water, which
complicates the identification of consistent trends across different runs.
For the lowest frequencies, 0.01 Hz and 1 Hz (Figures 4.1 and 4.2), the current profiles were
stable throughout the tests. The absence of pronounced variations or transient features indicates
that no significant electrochemical activity occurred under these conditions.
At 100 Hz (Figure 4.3), a gradual increase in current was observed in all runs. This behaviour
is attributed to capacitive charging and discharging of the electrode–electrolyte interface, reflect-
ing non-faradaic processes rather than electron transfer reactions.
The ICP analysis confirmed these observations, as all measured values for both Sn and Cu
were below detection limits. This result is consistent with the absence of dendritic features in
the current–time curves and demonstrates that no measurable dissolution occurred at ±1 V.
Overall, the results at ±1 V confirm that the system response is dominated by capacitive
effects rather than corrosion-related activity.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This thesis has demonstrated that the electrochemical behaviour of Sn-coated Cu PCBs
under AC bias is strongly dependent on both frequency and voltage. Frequency emerged as
the primary controlling parameter: at 0.01 Hz, dendritic growth dominated, becoming more
aggressive with increasing voltage and occasionally leading to Cu exposure; at 1 Hz, dendrites
were suppressed and corrosion manifested mainly as Sn dissolution combined with passivation
effects; at 100 Hz, the response was purely capacitive, with no detectable dissolution or den-
dritic activity. Voltage amplitude further modulated the severity of these processes. At 1 V,
electroactivity was negligible; at 10 V, corrosion was generally limited to Sn dissolution with
only minor Cu release; and at 48 V, the higher driving force accelerated dendrite initiation and
dissolution but also promoted passivation at intermediate frequency, highlighting the dual role
of increased potential in both destabilising and stabilising the system depending on conditions.
These findings are consistent with reports on steels, stainless steels, and pipelines, where
higher AC frequencies suppress corrosion rates while higher amplitudes intensify dissolution or
modify oxide stability. The present results extend these trends to PCB systems, showing that
polarity reversal under AC not only delays dendritic growth but can also shift the corrosion
pathway toward dissolution or passivation. In comparison with DC behaviour, dendrites under
AC were less stable at low frequency and fully suppressed above 1 Hz, confirming that AC bias
mitigates sustained electrochemical migration relative to DC.
Overall, this work establishes that frequency determines the mechanism of corrosion—ranging
from dendritic growth at low frequency, to dissolution and passivation at intermediate frequency,
and to capacitive behaviour at high frequency
Future Work
Because the electrochemical behaviour of Sn and Cu under AC bias is highly diverse and
strongly dependent on the testing conditions, further systematic investigations are required to
65
==PAGE:82==
accurately describe the response of Sn-coated Cu PCB systems.
AC exposure can suppress
traditional dendrite growth, but it may also introduce alternative degradation mechanisms such
as residue formation, intermetallic compound growth, or enhanced thermal stress. These vari-
ations underline the need to map the combined effects of frequency, voltage, waveform, and
environmental conditions in order to develop a comprehensive understanding of reliability in
PCB applications.
Future investigations should extend beyond the parameters studied in this thesis to accu-
rately describe the corrosion behaviour under AC bias. While this work focused on sinusoidal
excitation at selected voltages and frequencies, AC current can be varied in several additional
ways. The waveform shape (e.g. sinusoidal, square, triangular, or pulsed signals) may signifi-
cantly influence ion migration and dendrite dynamics, yet has not been systematically addressed,
and have not been so in systems related to Sn-coated Cu PCBs.
Other system-level variables also require attention. In this study, pure MilliQ water was
used, but real humidity layers typically contain dissolved salts or contaminants, which could
substantially change the corrosion behaviour. The solder pad geometry and spacing should be
varied to assess how it would affect electrochemical migration and overall corrosion of the pads.
Finally, in situ characterisation methods, such as real-time SEM or electrochemical AFM,
could provide more direct insights into dendrite dynamics and passivation processes under al-
ternating bias.
AC corrosion depends not only on voltage and frequency but on a broader set of parameters.
A more comprehensive mapping of these variables would allow a deeper understanding of when
AC bias is advantageous compared with DC, and when it may introduce additional reliability
risks for PCB-based systems.
