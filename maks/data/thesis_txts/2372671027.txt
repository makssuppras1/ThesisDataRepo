ABSTRACT  
 
The study was set to obtain knowledge and insight of occupant presence behaviour in 
residential buildings. The aim is to model presence behaviour patterns of occupants in 46 
dwellings, and determine whether the presence influence indoor environment.  
The investigation was conducted in the period January to May 2017. A PIR and Reed tag 
sensors were installed in the dwellings in order to detect presence, while Netatmo Weather 
Stations were installed to measure indoor temperature, CO2-concentration and relative 
humidity. The approach was to look into the measured data from each dwelling and develop 
models, including statistical analysis using the R software. Two stochastic models based on 
Markov chain were developed. The models predicted (1) the probability of the occupant’s 
arrival, (2) the average duration of occupancy in the dwelling. Furthermore, the percentage of 
time the dwelling is occupied were determined in relation to average indoor temperature, 
average CO2-concentration and average relative humidity.  
Both the models showed a great variation. The time of day has no effect on the occupant’s 
arrival. There was not a big difference between weekdays and weekends. The occupants in the 
area seemed to have different routines with constant movement during the day. 
There is no clear trend between the percentages of the time the dwellings are occupied and the 
average indoor temperature, average CO2-concentration and average relative humidity. The 
occupants seem to not adjust their temperatures whether they are at home or not. A little trend 
showed that the higher the percentage of the time dwelling is occupied, the higher the CO2-
concentration becomes. But that trend was based on only 5 dwellings out of 46 dwellings.   
Overall, this study and the Markov chains models gave a clear picture of the varieties, when 
working with different types of person, families and routines.

PREFACE  
This thesis was prepared at the Department of Civil Engineering at the Technical University of 
Denmark in fulfillment of the acquired requirements for the M.Sc. degree in Civil Engineering. 
The project was carried out from the 2nd January to 30rd June 2017 with 35 ECTS points.  
The Supervisor, during the thesis study was Ph.D. Senior Researcher Rune Korsholm 
Andersen. The thesis is part of a research project carried out by International Centre for Indoor 
Environment and Energy regarding behaviour and indoor environment in residential homes. In 
addition, the multidisciplinary research project UserTEC have also taken part in this study. 
Their focus is occupants’ behaviour patterns and to get a deeper understanding of occupants’ 
needs in relation to energy consumption and to adapt it into the energy-efficient technology in 
the future.   
The paper presents an investigation of different patterns of occupants in private homes. The 
report focusses on modelling behaviour pattern of occupants’ presence, and determines the 
effect on indoor environment. The provided data are from single family homes in an area near 
Copenhagen, who wanted to take part in this project to obtain better knowledge of indoor 
environment and energy consumption in their homes. 
The introduction and background study is influenced by other researchers’ work and studies. 
It has been discovered that most studies on occupant presence are done in commercial 
buildings, since there is great need of behaviour patterns in office buildings to improve 
modeling indoor climate. However, since, there are new energy efficient residential buildings 
as well, they must also be examined. From a presence-related behaviour, there are similarities 
in commercial and residential buildings, while from an energy-related behaviour, there are 
quite a difference, since commercial and residential buildings mostly contain different building 
systems and interaction with the building. For this study, this difference has been taken into 
account; the articles will therefore also be used as an insight.  
The study is aimed at persons who are interested in modelling and implementing different 
behaviour patterns of occupants’ presence for simulations programs in order to improve the 
indoor environment and energy consumption.

1 INTRODUCTION  
In recent years, many goals and policies on improving the energy consumption and making the 
buildings more efficient have been made. Generally, in the EU, the buildings are responsible 
for 40 % of energy consumption, where 35 % of them stem from buildings that are 50 years 
old [1]. Moreover, residential homes account for 24.8 % of energy consumption [2].   
In Denmark, all buildings, both private and commercial follow the rules from Building 
Regulations [3], which contain a set of rules for construction of buildings to ensure that the 
buildings are not irresponsible and inappropriate built but are followed by the guidelines. 
Besides of regulating the rules, they are also being updated during the years to use more 
efficient energy to lower the energy bills and protect the environment.  
However, buildings are not always the problem since the people using it have a crucial role. 
Buildings usually use more energy than what is predicted. These discrepancies lead to big gaps 
in the performance [4], which questions whether the engineers have made wrong estimations 
of the buildings or simply because occupants’ behaviour are not being studied enough. The 
gaps of the predicted and actual energy performance depend on difference between the 
estimated and the final fulfillment of the construction, technical installation and the systems [5, 
6, 7]. The mentioned are mostly related to technological aspects, but since the buildings are 
built for occupants, the behavioural aspects should also be considered. Several studies [8, 9, 
10, 11] have shown that there is amounts of energy waste from occupants’ behaviour, which 
people should be more aware of. Branco et al. noted [12] that the occupant patterns are not in 
accordance with the actual occupant patterns in an experimental study carried over 3 years’ 
period in a multifamily complex in Switzerland. The study showed a big gap between the real 
(246 MJ/m2) and the estimated energy use (160 MJ/m 2).  
Engineers should make more realistic models of buildings and take occupants’ interactions into 
account, when designing the buildings [13]. Many building simulation programs are developed 
to simulate environment and energy consumption by adjusting building control systems with 
some set of standardized behaviour from occupants [6, 14, 15]. Yet, occupants’ behaviour vary 
between individuals, which results in a variety of the indoor environment and energy 
consumption [5, 13, 14]. Seligman et al. [16] studied energy consumption in 28 identical 
==PAGE:20==
2  
 
dwellings, where it was discovered that the largest variation in energy consumption was two to 
one. Also, Andersen’s study [17] showed that the largest variation for heating consumption in 
290 identical houses was 20 to one. Since the houses were identical, the variation must be 
related to the occupancy and the occupants’ behaviour.  
Commercial buildings and some private homes are provided with adjustable devices for 
building control systems, such as opening windows, lighting, temperature set-points, 
ventilation rates etc. Studies have shown that most occupants prefer and become more satisfied 
with the indoor environment, if they can control the building control system themselves [10, 
18, 19, 20, 21]. Leaving the control to the occupants also mean that the energy use of the 
buildings varies, and will mostly increase the energy consumption [16, 17, 21]. Masoso and 
Grobler [8] found out in their study that more energy is used during non-working hours (56 %) 
than during working hours (44 %) in commercial buildings due to poor occupants’ behaviour, 
from leaving the lights and equipment on all day. Mahdavi et al. [22] installed digital cameras 
and other record devices, where they observed that occupants spend more time away from their 
work station, where the lighting and equipment remain turned on. But again, the occupants in 
commercial buildings are not paying the bills themselves, so it is easier not to care about it. 
Usually, people are not motivated to take responsibility for energy savings, because they cannot 
‘see’ it. And simply because we are not taught on how to interact and maintain the buildings 
properly, even though we spend most of our lives in buildings [23]. When it comes to designing 
and constructing buildings, it is always easier to point finger at people and educate others once 
it does not go as predicted. The occupants may not be taught properly, but also the engineers 
have not enough knowledge of the occupants’ habits and routines in buildings, which need to 
be changed.   
The knowledge of occupant behaviour and patterns have become a fundamental role and also 
one of the strongest connection to energy savings, which also makes it as the weakest link [13, 
8]. The buildings are nothing without humans and conversely. Investigating the occupant 
behaviour in private homes and implementing the patterns can provide improvements and more 
realistic outcomes of building simulation performances [13]. 
 OBJECTIVE 
The objective of this study is to obtain knowledge and insight of occupant presence behaviour 
in residential buildings. The aim is to model presence behaviour patterns of occupants in 46 
dwellings, and determine whether the presence influence indoor environment.

2 BACKGROUND  
This section gives an understanding of two main aspects that have an impact in this study. First, 
a brief description of energy classes for residential buildings in Denmark, together with an 
insight of energy consumption during the years. Secondly, the details of the main driving 
factors that can influence energy consumption will be specified.  
 ENERGY: IF YOU DONꞌT NEED IT, DONꞌT USE IT!  
The engineers and owners of buildings get often surprised over the big gap between the 
theoretical and the actual energy consumption. The theoretical energy consumption of a 
building, calculated in relation to energy labeling of the building should not be expected to be 
the same as the actual consumption when the building is occupied by a family [24]. Some 
occupants save on the amounts of water usage, others like to have windows open at nights 
while some never adjusts the radiators etc.  
The Building Regulations (BR) make all the decisions regarding building industry, therefore 
all buildings must comply with the minimum requirements. In the beginning, the BR made 
requirements of certain building components and certain thickness of insulation. Thereafter, 
the requirements focused on the functions of the buildings, which needed to have a certain 
energy class. In the last few years, the BR have made more tight requirements to insure lower 
energy performance which requires high energy efficient components in the buildings.  In order 
to be a step ahead, the BR inform future tightening, so the new buildings can be labeled as low 
energy houses. Furthermore, requirements regarding renovation for buildings are also made 
[24].

The research project UserTEC covers many subprojects; the well-known international 
discussion of the big gap due to theoretical and actual energy consumption is one of them. The 
building industry in Denmark have also taken part in this discussion and have made different 
studies regarding the big gap. One of the studies [24] are made in dwellings, where the actual 
and estimated energy consumption are distributed by energy labels with total of 135.311 
dwellings (Figure 4). The actual energy consumption is from BBR-registry [24], which are 
associated with information about energy consumption for heating. As it can be observed, the 
dwellings with low energy efficiency show a clear trend of using less energy compared to the 
theoretical, while the most energy efficiency houses use more energy than the theoretical.  It 
seems like the more energy-efficient the dwellings become, the less energy saving becomes the 
occupant’s behavior [24]. There is a large spread on both the measured and theoretical 
consumption for each energy label, since heating an area in a house vary from home to home. 
As it has been showed in several studies, the energy usage in identical houses can vary, where 
one dwelling typically uses 2-3 times more energy than the next door. [7, 13, 16, 28]. This can 
depend on many driving factors, including occupant behavior, which lead us to the next section. 
 BUILDINGS DON’T USE ENERGY – PEOPLE DO!  
The building energy consumption is strongly reliant on the building systems, together with 
occupant behaviour. In order to improving energy consumption in buildings, the knowledge of 
the driving factors are required, together with a detailed insight of the effects the identified 
driving factors have on energy performance patterns. The energy consumption for buildings 
can be influenced by several factors (the factors are ranked randomly) [9, 29, 30]: 
1) Climate (e.g. outdoor air temperature, solar radiation, wind speed, etc.) 
2) Building characteristics (e.g. type, area, orientation, etc.) 
3) Building services systems and operation (e.g. space cooling/heating, hot water, etc.) 
4) Occupant behaviour (e.g. presence, preferences, activities, etc.) 
5) Indoor environmental conditions (e.g. indoor temperature, CO2, RH, etc.) 
Among these factors, occupant behaviour is the only factor, that cannot have fixed values, 
because fully predicting occupant’s habits and routines is not always that simple. Furthermore, 
occupants vary from individual to individual, which naturally have an influence and can result 
in different energy consumption patterns [5, 9, 16, 17].

 Occupants’ behaviour 
“If a change occurs such as to produce discomfort, people react in ways which tend to 
restore their comfort” – Humphrey 1998 
Many comfort studies have been carried out, where it has been coincided with Humphrey’s 
hypothesis [5, 31, 32, 33]. The word “behaviour” has several definitions: 
1) A response of an individual (…) to an action, environment, person or stimulus [34].  
2) Range of actions by individuals (…) in conjunction with themselves or their 
environment. Behaviour can be conscious or subconscious, overt or covert, and 
voluntary or involuntary [35]. 
3) It is considered as the collection of decisive processes by which individuals adjust their 
state and situation according to variation in their environment [36]. 
According to these definitions when it comes to building environment, the occupant behaviour 
can be any action or reaction, besides for opening or closing a window. Furthermore, the 
behaviour of occupants can happen without even knowing about it. The occupants make 
unconscious or conscious actions to improve, restore or maintain the comfort conditions, 
wherever they are, by comparing the current situation with the past event [5].  
Generally, behaviour can be divided into internal and external [5, 10, 37]. The internal is the 
invisible outside the body, such as feeling cold or hot, while the external can be observed, such 
as opening or closing the window. By linking “Tinbergen’s four questions” [38, 39], named 
after Nikolas Tinbergen, the behaviour have four categories of explanations; (a) Ontogeny 
describes individual development and how behaviour changes with age, experience and 
environment; (b) Mechanism describes how a  behaviour get elicited and what it requires; (c) 
Phylogeny describes the history of behaviour and how earlier generations have contributed to 
a certain behaviour; (d) Adaptation describes how the behaviour helps individuals with survival 
problem or to be reproductive. The first two are proximate, while the last two are ultimate.  
Transferring these categories to a behavioural action, the occupant becomes aware of the 
external or internal sensation, together with an emotion or feeling. This leads the occupant to 
take a decision whether to act or not, depending on the type of sensation. 
Seligman, Sonderegger and Socolow [16, 40, 41] were the first to point out that the behaviour 
of occupants had a significant impact on the energy performance of a building. The occupants’ 
behaviour is a stochastically and an unpredicted process of many continuous combinations of 
==PAGE:26==
8  
 
many factors. Thus, occupants’ behaviour and interactions with building systems are only one 
aspect of one specific human, and therefore will be different for one other. Observations on 
occupants’ behaviour have showed that occupants are more likely to act, when being forced by 
an incident, so-called “crisis of discomfort” rather than in advance of it, such as switching the 
thermostats or opening the windows. After making the change, the occupants tend to leave it 
like that, until a new “crisis of discomfort” appear again [23, 42]. Steemers et al. [43] noted 
that the occupant and behaviour have a significant role for understanding, evaluating or 
predicting energy use in buildings in this investigation of household heating and cooling energy 
consumption in an existing survey by US Department of Energy. Gram-Hanssen et al. [44] 
worked with different data sets of domestic electricity use, where it was found out that the 
background variables, such as age, routines and habits were explanations of the variations in 
the electricity use patterns. Also, Brohus et al. [45] have made a theoretical and empirical study 
of occupants’ behaviour and the influence on the difference of energy consumption in identical 
domestic buildings in Denmark and concluded that difference of energy consumptions was due 
to occupant behaviour. However, other studies have showed that it is possible to lower energy 
consumption by modifying occupants behaviour [23, 46]. Darby [47] noted big potential in 
reducing energy consumption up to 10 % by using feedback. It motivates and encourages the 
occupants to save energy and also bring awareness of their own interactions in the buildings 
[48, 49, 50].

In Figure 5, it shows the general process of occupants in households related to indoor 
environmental and energy consumption, which leads us back to behaviour distinction. The 
external and internal factors are the reasons, that lead to reactions and results in a specific

“drivers”, since it “drive” the occupant to an action. The drivers contain of physical 
environmental factors, psychological factors, physiological factors, social factors and 
contextual factors (Table 2).

There are several patterns of occupants’ behaviour where the occupants are the main operator 
in the process. The occupants can directly and indirectly affect the indoor environment and 
energy use. Besides of that, the occupants can adjust themselves to the existing environmental; 
clothing, activity, movement from room to room etc. This lead to the action scenarios, which 
are all the interactions that the occupant does in the dwelling; whether it is opening or closing 
a window, changing clothes, using hot water or adjusting the thermostats set-points. The aim 
of the actions is to maintain or improve the indoor environment of own preference [5]. 
 
Figure 5. Flux diagram of process from drivers to indoor environment and energy consumption [5]   
The outcome in the process will be indoor environmental quality and energy consumption, 
which are consequences and effects from the performed actions by the occupants. Note that the 
system is an on-going process. Based on occupants’ behaviour, it is possible to determine type 
of occupants depending on their interactions with the buildings. The occupant will be an energy 
waster because of high energy outcome, while a low energy outcome will specify the occupant 
as an energy saver [5].  
The occupants’ behaviour express their satisfaction with the surrounding environment through 
thermal comfort. But it is difficult to satisfy everyone, since there is a difference between 
occupants. The rule is if 90% expresses satisfaction, it is good, while 80% is considered 
acceptable [51].  The recommended indoor environmental ranges in three categories can be

In the following paragraphs, the mentioned drivers and occupant behaviour are put in relation 
to each other in relation to what studies have found out.  
Physical environmental drivers (e.g. temperature, CO2, relative humidity):  
Occupants adjust thermostats, opening/closing windows, clothing etc., when for example the 
indoor or outdoor temperature seems to be uncomfortable. Studies [53] have shown that there 
is a direct correlation between outdoor temperature and the probability of an open window in 
the range of -10°C to 25°C. And in summer periods, the occupants tend to open the windows 
more often and in longer time [53].  
Psychological drivers (e.g. preferences, habits, lifestyle):  
The occupants prefer to adjust the indoor environment themselves regarding their own 
preferences and habits in order to satisfy the thermal comfort, especially the temperature [53, 
54, 55, 56].    
Physiological drivers (e.g. age, gender, clothing, activity):  
The behaviour of elderly occupants was found to be different from younger people and the 
gender also had an impact [37, 57]. It has been observed that men prefer colder environment 
than women [57], and older people tend to ventilate less than the young [37].   
Social drivers (e.g. opening/closing window, thermostat setpoint):  
The presence of occupants is important in order to interact with the building. The IEA – ECBCS 
Annex 8 [53] showed that the longer the occupants are present, the more the windows are kept 
==PAGE:29==

open. Especially, when the occupants are cooking or sleeping. Furthermore, on average, 
smoking occupants tend to ventilate the rooms twice as long as non-smoking occupants [53].  
These studies are a perfect verification of the occupants having an influence in buildings and 
energy consumption, which underlines that there is a need of guidelines or models of behaviour 
patterns for implementation in simulation program. But all this depend on whether the occupant 
is absent or present, which lead us to the next section. 
2.2.1.1 Occupant presence 
The occupant will only have an impact on the building by presence and the interactions with 
the building. Therefore, it is obvious to firstly determine whether the occupant is present or not 
when modelling occupant behaviour. As the Figure 5 illustrate, the circulation of the system 
will be impossible without the presence of occupants.  
Modelling occupant behaviour and their presence will help understand how to reduce energy 
consumption and thereby improve thermal comfort. This requires incorporating realistic data 
of occupant behaviour and presence in order to develop good building simulations. As 
mentioned earlier, the occupants’ behaviour and interactions with indoor environment such as 
lighting, blinds, windows, thermostats, clothing or activities are subjective and therefore will 
be different for one another. Currently, building performance simulations (BPS) contain of long 
periods in terms of static schedules. This makes the schedules representative, but not realistic 
when simulating several types of building and occupancy. In other words, it will not be able to 
give several alternatives [14].   
Several researchers have developed occupant behaviour models regarding presence, but it is 
not well-represented in building simulations yet [14, 58]. In addition, most studies [32] are 
done in commercial buildings, since it was more required. However, with gaps between the 
theoretical and estimated energy consumption in the newbuilt energy-efficient residential 
buildings (Figure 4), the researchers also have a great interest in it. As presence-related 
behaviour, there are similarities between occupant presence in commercial and residential 
buildings, while from an energy-related behaviour, there are quite a difference since 
commercial and residential buildings mostly contain of different building systems and 
interaction of the building. 
Currently, most BSP consider occupant presence as diversity-profiles [45, 59], because it 
mainly estimates internal heat gains from occupants, equipment and lighting, together with 
loads of heating, ventilation and air conditioning systems. This depend on whether it is a 
==PAGE:30==
12  
 
residential or commercial building. A typical diversity-profile of residential building will 
usually contain data about number of occupants in the dwelling, the type of occupants and 
absence/presence of the occupant in the dwelling. Furthermore, the weekdays and weekends 
are often handled differently. A daily profile contains of 24 hours and varies between 0 and 1. 
United States Department of Energy [60] covers 80% of the commercial building floor area in 
United States and have elaborated occupancy schedules during a week for office buildings, 
where the prototype building models include 16 commercial building types. Figure 6 presents 
the deterministic schedules during a work day, Saturday and Sunday/holiday. The daily 
schedule varies between 0 and 1, describing absent and present occupancy. As it can be seen 
in the figure, there is a clear difference between the days. For a normal work day, the number 
of occupants increases 20 % at 08:00 and increases further to 95 % of full occupancy at 09:00. 
Then it decreases at lunch break to half and returns to 95 % again after lunch. At 17:00, the 
occupants begin to leave the building. For Saturday and Sunday/holiday the number of 
occupants are not that many, as expected [61].  
 
Figure 6. The daily schedules of occupant presence during workday, Saturday and Sunday/holiday [60] 
The profiles are very simple to use. However, the weakness of using them is that they become 
repetitive, consisting of only one to two profiles, sometimes three (weekday, Saturday and 
Sunday/holiday). Additionally, the final profiles usually represent the combined behaviour of 
all occupants in a building. As Feng et al. [61] explained in their study, the profiles of 
occupancy are deterministic and homogeneous schedules. They are, therefore, not capable of 
simulating various building energy consumptions, which does not realistic due to occupant 
behaviour. Furthermore, the profiles make it difficult to figure out unusual behaviour, variation 
during the weekdays or seasonal variation.  
==PAGE:31==
13  
 
In 1995, Newsham et al. proposed for first time an improvement of this method with a simple 
stochastic model for occupant presence, namely the Lightswitch model [32, 59]. The model 
was used to obtain more realistic schedules of arrival and departure of occupants in their offices 
using electric lighting. The proposed model is a combination of diversity-profiles and 
scheduling randomly stochastic variable of arrivals and departures from offices with a range of 
15-minute interval, so the occupants can arrive from 07.45 - 08.15. This makes the model more 
realistic by avoiding the unrealistic peaks, that occur if all occupants arrived or leaved at the 
same time. But this does not change the fact, that most of the profile is still fixed, since the 
presence is 100 % during the day and 0% after leaving the office in the evening until next 
morning. Furthermore, the profile will be a repetition for all weekdays, and for weekends the 
office is assumed to be unoccupied. Additionally, the holidays, trips or occupant sickness will 
not be considered in the model. This will result in an overestimation of occupant behaviour and 
energy consumption [32, 59].  
Wang et al. [14, 32] have used the same modeling approach for a large office building in San 
Francisco, but suggested to include a non-homogeneous Poisson process model (events could 
occur continuously and independently of one another), together with two different exponential 
distributions, that simulate the occupancy sequence in a single office (one for occupancy and 
one for vacancy). In 2007, Yamaguchi et al. [32] improved Wang’s occupant presence model 
by changing the particular order of Poisson processes with a mathematical model 
corresponding to Markov chain. For BSP, the Markov chains are more qualified for 
computational simulations and faster as well. The Markov chains model combine the data 
regarding occupant presence and equipment use; four types of state for each occupant (1) 
Absence, (2) Presence without using pc, (3) Presence using a pc, (4) Presence using two pcs. 
But the model is not suitable for looking at occupant presence independently. Moreover, the 
model does not inform about the duration of the absence and the paper does not mention 
whether the model simulate for one day or for a whole year [32].  
In this study, an occupant presence model based on Markov chain will be developed, together 
with the duration of occupancy in the dwellings, which will be described thoroughly in next 
chapter.

3 METHOD  
This section describes how the study is approached. First, the details of the experimental set-
up will be described followed by a description of the process and preparations of the obtained 
data to acquire results.  
Behaviour of occupants in dwellings have been studied by several approaches, some by 
questionnaire surveys and others by measuring the occupant behaviour. As a follow-up, this 
study will have continuous measurements of occupant presence, window positions and indoor 
environmental variables; temperature, CO2-concentration and relative humidity.  
Invitations to participate in this study, in the form of letters, was sent out to an area near 
Copenhagen. The investigation was conducted in the period January to May 2017, where two 
different types of measurements were used and installed in the dwellings. In the attempt to 
achieve better indoor environment, feedback letters to half of the dwellings were sent out in 
the end of March.  
 EXPERIMENTAL SETUP 
The experimental setup was carried out in the start of January to February 2017, where the 
measurements were installed. The enrolment of the dwellings did not happen at the same time; 
therefore, the measuring period varies from dwelling to dwelling. After the setup, the 
measurements needed maintenance in case the measurements got offline or did not work.  
Each participant was contacted in order to come by and install the measurements. The 
communication was through mail and phone.  It was important that at least one occupant was 
home during the setup of the measurements in order to know exactly which rooms are the most 
occupied. It took approximately 1 hour per dwelling to briefly inform about the measurements 
and setup all measurements. The most occupied rooms were the bedroom and the composite 
kitchen and living room. 
In the middle of the measuring period, there was suddenly change of internet cables in the area, 
which delayed the process, because the measurements got offline and needed to reconnect 
manually.

 PROCESSING AND PREPARATION OF DATA 
This section will present two stochastic models based on Markov chain concept. The first 
model describes the probability of the occupant’s arrival. As a follow up, the second model 
will inform the average duration of occupancy in the dwelling. Furthermore, a look on how 
indoor environmental parameters behave according to occupants’ presence.   
The models give an illustration of occupants’ normal day in the dwelling. The dwelling is 
assumed to be one big room, where the main door has to open when occupants leave or arrive. 
In addition, there is no recording of how many occupants are living in the dwelling, and 
therefore it has been taking into account that more than one occupant can be in the dwelling. 
This can result that the dwelling can be occupied all day, even though an occupant might have 
arrived or left the dwelling.  
The approach is to look into the selected data from each dwelling and develop models, 
including statistical analysis using the R software. The model for occupant presence will only 
depend on PIR and Reed measurements. For indoor environmental, it requires the datasets from 
Netatmo modules (CO2-concentration, relative humidity, indoor temperature).

results. The processing of the data contains of three steps:

3.2.2.2 Step 2: Occupied or not? 
The second step is finding out whether the dwelling has been occupied or not, by combining 
the datasets; EP.Detect, LP.Detect and R.Detect together by conditions. The Presence can 
either be 0 (false) or 1 (true) to describe whether the dwelling has been occupied or not. In 
Figure 23,  
1. The process starts by putting occupied to be false to make sure that the dwelling is 
unoccupied.  
2. At the beginning, row n is the same as startRow and endRow. startRow and endRow 
are used to create an invisible frame (red square) between open or moved, where every 
row in the frame will be checked.  
3. The row n runs through the rows in the measured data in R.Detect[z], until it met an 
open or moved. The status of open or moved indicates that an occupant is presence.  
4. The endRow will then be changed to the current row n for R.Detect[z], which have to 
be either open or moved.  
5. Each row in the frame will then be checked if the EP.Detect or LP.Detect have been 
in Movement. If the conditions are met for one of the rows in the frame, it will indicate 
that the dwelling are occupied, because the door have not been open or moved during 
the time in the frame.  
6. The detected value will then be put in the Presence column replacing NaN.

3.2.2.3 Step 3: Arrival and staying at home? 
The third and last step is to model two Markov chains for each dwelling, which informs about 
when the occupants arrive at the dwelling and the duration of their stay in the dwelling. In 
Figure 24, 
1. The process starts by putting arrival to be false to make sure that no one has arrived.  
2. At the beginning, row n run through the rows, until it meets a condition. 
3. One condition is made, saying: the previous row Presence[n-1] is equal to 0, current 
row Presence[n] is equal to 1 and next row Presence[n+1] also equal to 1. This 
condition makes sure that the occupant have just arrived and at least have been home 
in 10 minutes. If the condition is met, then it detected as true.  
4. However, the current row n in Presence will then be equal to the row i in HomeStay. 
Every row in Presence will then be checked if it is equal to 1 with the i = i +1 entered. 
When the Presence changes status, the HomeStay will take the row difference of i and 
n, and replace the NaN. This is to sum up how many 5-minutes the dwelling have been 
occupied. This value can later be converted to hours.  
5. In order to continue the same process for all the rows in the dataset, the n = n + 1 are 
entered and the arrival are put to be false again.

 Analyzing data: Markov chain approach 
The developed stochastic model is simulating occupant’s arrival to the dwelling and the 
duration of the dwelling that it is occupied. The models will not be dependent of other 
parameters such as indoor environmental, because the occupant arrival and how long (s)he has 
stayed is not determined by other parameters than their own preferences, habits and routines. 
A Markov chain can be divided into two types; discrete-time and continuous-time. This study 
will only focus on the discrete-time Markov chain, because the approach for continuous time 
==PAGE:49==
31  
 
is more technical and advanced than what there is time for and therefore will not be examined 
in this study.  
A Markov chain is a stochastic process, where the future can be predicted on the basis of the 
current system state and where the past is irrelevant [59, 66]. Basically, a discrete-time is a 
sequence of the time in steps. At each time step, the probabilities are determined based on the 
transition from one activity (unoccupied) to another (arrival).

This will eliminate the possibility of the unrealistic pattern of occupants being home in 5 
minutes, then no occupants in the next 5 minutes and so on. Normally, when an occupant leaves 
the dwelling, it usually lasts for more than 5 minutes. The probability of returning after 5 
minutes is quite small, and increases, the longer time have passed.  
The stochastic model is divided into two periods: weekdays and weekends, since there is a

5 DISCUSSION   
This section will give an assessment of the obtained results. 
 MEASUREMENTS 
The chosen measurements had a major role in this study which unfortunately also affected the 
study negatively.  
The main problem with the measurements was the unstable connection to Wi-Fi, together with 
the sudden change of internet cables in the area during the measurement period. This resulted 
in a lot of data loss for the dwellings. The Wireless tag sensors could automatically re-connect 
to the internet router as soon as the cable was connected, but not every occupant was fully sure 
if they would manage to do it properly. On the other hand, The Netatmo stations had to be re-
connected to the internet router manually which required extra time. A lot of the datasets consist 
of periods with no data. Therefore, the datasets were distributed in three ranges regarding the 
amount of observations. The amount of data could have been better, if the delays did not 
happen. The data analysis could have been more useful if there were more than 8 dwellings out 
of 46 dwellings with full datasets. 
The sensors installed on the windows and thermostats did not go as planned, which resulted in 
change of focus. It was discovered that the compass tag sensors could be used for all types of 
windows, however, no clear study has been made for the accelerator tag sensors. Besides, it 
was observed that the compass and accelerator sensors did not always detect all the motions, 
even though the detection sensitivity was set from 85 %, but the uncertainty was still there.  
The accuracy of the PIR and Reed was not controlled, but the PIR and Reed seemed to give 
reasonable detection of the events, except for dwelling 6. The site connected to the sensors 
recorded if a sensor lost connection and when the link was re-established. The PIR in the 
entrance has detected movement in the whole period that it was installed in for dwelling 6, 
which did not comply with the PIR installed in the living room. It was found out that the PIR 
sensors had lost connection to the Wi-Fi, and had since then detected same detection, without 
any update on it having lost link to the internet. Therefore, the Markov models for dwelling 6 
have only been based on L.PIR and Reed, which is not as precise as it would have been with 
E.PIR, which was used for all the other dwellings.  
==PAGE:66==
48  
 
Furthermore, the locations of the two PIR and Reed was found good in this study. The 
combinations of the locations acquired a reasonable view of occupant arrival and occupancy in 
the dwelling. However, it would have been better with more PIR sensors installed in the 
dwellings, since the dwellings tend to not have any movement in several hours in the living 
room or entrance, until another occupant arrives and the main door detect a motion.  
A part of a Bachelor project investigated the accuracy of the Netatmo stations and whether the 
measured parameters compliance with the reality. It was found out that the Netatmo stations 
had a precision, which were compared with other measurements [67]. Besides for that, the 
manual of Netatmo inform that the Netatmo stations have an automatic re-calibration of CO2-
concentration installed. This means that the stations installed in the dwellings recalibrates the 
CO2-concentration automatically in the end of the week, where the CO2-concentration lowers 
to 400 ppm. This re-calibration is initial, when the stations were installed for first time. But re-
calibrating every week could estimate a lower CO2-concentration in the dwellings, than the 
actual CO2-concentration. However, it has not been verified if it has affected our CO2 data. 
And the average CO2-concetration as well.  
 MODELLING APPROACH 
The method for this study have been based on discrete time Markov chain concept, where the 
basic principles of the Markov chains are used. Occupant behaviour consists of a lot of 
complexity. Several studies have developed models of occupant behaviour presence in 
different ways. The developed Markov models in this study are considered as one aspect of 
modelling occupant presence, where the algorithm consists of simple conditions.  
Several studies have showed that Markov chains are reasonable to be used for datasets that 
consist of time interval with a step-time together with transitions from one action to another. It 
has, therefore been used in this study. However, these Markov models are not able to determine 
an occupant’s arrival at a time-step earlier, but precede in present step. This would require a 
higher order of Markov chains. Nor can the Markov models predict future states, that can occur 
later. My developed models do not have that problem, because it does not determine likelihood 
of departure, but instead the models determine occupant’s arrival and how long they stay.  
The developed models do not take the number of occupants into consideration. Thus, the 
probability of an occupant’s arrival in the model is indicated as all the occupants that arrive 
into the dwelling. In that way, it creates a more realistic and reasonable representation of 
==PAGE:67==
49  
 
occupant movement from and to a dwelling because the models give an overview of the whole 
dwelling instead of one particular occupant.  
The measurement carried out in the private homes consisted of different types of persons, 
families and routines, therefore it gives a realistic and diverse insight. Some of the dwellings 
are occupied all day, while others are occupied in less than 50 %. It is important to keep in 
mind that an all-day occupied dwelling can indicate two things (1) the occupants prefer to stay 
home all day, (2) the occupants living together can have different routines. These varieties are 
important to include in simulation programs, when simulating energy consumptions. Instead 
of only having standardized models.  
The approach of determining occupancy with the combination of PIR and Reed sensors appear 
to be good and an algorithm was developed, however, the algorithm can be improved. It will 
be an improvement to include the window opening and CO2-concentration into the combination 
in the algorithm in order to make better models of occupant’s presence.  
The time format has been the major concern in developing the algorithm. To start with, there 
were two timeframes (Wireless tag sensors and Netatmo stations), where the time formats 
varies, because the measurements come from different countries. A self-made 5 minute-interval 
was made, which also had a different time format. The idea was to collect all data into a 
common timeframe by using R-software, but that was not easy, because R also has its own 
aspect of time. In the end, the formatting of time succeeded. There was no time to control the 
datasets why uncertainty might therefore appear.  Few stages in the algorithm determine the 
time difference between the two different timeframes. If the formats for the two timeframes are 
not the same, it will not inform about them. Therefore, the amount of errors is unknown which 
can affect the Markov models and the results, since it was linked to time difference.  
The second step in the algorithm determine whether the dwellings are occupied or not. This is 
done by the combination of E.PIR, L.PIR and Reed, but one situation is not taken into account 
in the algorithm.  In order to determine the presence of occupants, the status of Reed needs to 
be “open” or “moved”, together with either the E.PIR or L.PIR are “movement”. However, 
when the Reed is “open” or “moved”, and both the E.PIR and L.PIR are “TimeOut”, should 
the occupant be determined as present or absent? In the algorithm, the occupants are determined 
to be absent, which can be a wrong assumption, since the door have been in motion. The 
argument for the assumption is that the Reed sensors can be very sensitive, especially if it is a 
little windy outside and the door gets a wind pressure.   
==PAGE:68==
50  
 
 TIME OF THE DAY 
An hourly time interval was chosen as x-axis in order to to illustrate the 5-minute time step 
probabilities of arrivals reasonable. This resulted in having small probabilities, because it is 
dealing with small time steps. A 5-minute time interval on x-axis will have shown the figures 
with an inaccuracy due to little data of observations, but great fluctuation. If the dataset were 
larger with observations over a longer period, for example 5 years, it will have been better to 
use the 5-minute interval. 
There is no information about the occupants living in the dwellings. The models are not 
grouped into types of families because this variety of types of homes should be able to be used 
for all types of homes. The target audience for the Markov models is for all types of private 
homes. 
The graphical representations of occupant’s arrival showed a common pattern for the different 
dwellings. The arrival is ensured to be 10 minutes or more in the dwellings. Overall, it can be 
expected that the occupant arrived to the dwelling every hour during the day with a probability 
of approximately 0.5-1 %, also for weekends. The time of day has no effect on the occupant’s 
arrival. Small and medium datasets are more identical compared to the large datasets, while 
there is a variation of the peaks for the different hour during the day. Additionally, the 
probability of arrivals decreases the larger the dataset becomes. This is because the datasets for 
the small datasets have diversity of arrivals during the hours, but not enough observations, 
which results in higher probability. However, for the large datasets, they also have a diversity 
of arrivals during the hours, but with many observations and results in lower probability. This 
arrival pattern actually came as a surprise, especially in weekdays. It was expected that 
occupants arrive between 15:00- 21:00 from work/ outside activities and between 02:00-07:00 
from night shifts. Instead, the graphs showed variation and it gave an impression of constantly 
movement in the investigated area. This indicate that the occupants in each dwelling have a 
different lifestyle and routines. This is especially shown in weekends since the occupants tend 
to have more free time to have family reunion, invitations, parties, outside activities or simply 
relax at home. These different behaviour usually require a more different distribution of arrival 
among the hours during the day.   
The plots of the average hours an occupant stay home after arrival showed almost a common 
pattern looking a basic manner. Overall, it can be expected that the dwellings stayed occupied 
==PAGE:69==
51  
 
for averagely 20 hours after their arrival during the day, except from 00:00-05:00, which it is 
very low. But it is not necessary the same occupant who stays home for 20 hours. 
The larger the datasets become, the more clear the pattern becomes. The time of day influences 
how long the occupant stays. The pattern is wave-formed if the peaks are neglected for a 
second, where the averagely hours seem to be reasonably distributed during the day. But it was 
expected to be less with the assumption of occupants staying less home during weekdays 
compared to in weekends. 
The unusual high peaks in the plots can be difficult to figure out. If the occupant live alone, the 
high value can be caused of a long sick period, however if there are living more than one 
occupant, the high peak seems suddenly not that high, especially if the occupant’s routines does 
not overlap and therefore the dwellings are occupied in long periods in average.  
 THERMAL ENVIRONMENTAL 
The measurements were carried out from January to start of May. Therefore, there results can 
be influenced by both the cold and warm season. Furthermore, it would have been better with 
a registration of the number of occupants in the dwellings, since occupants influence indoor 
environment by producing heat, moisture and CO2.The arrival of occupants have no influence 
of the indoor climate, while the average of hours for the occupant staying in the home after 
their arrival has an impact on the indoor climate parameters.   
 Temperature 
Studies [53] have shown that the indoor and outdoor temperature have a correlation. When the 
indoor temperature increases, the occupant tend to adjust the thermostats, if the outdoor 
temperature is low, or open the windows, if the outdoor temperature is high. However, it seems 
like the occupant does not adjust their temperatures whether they are at home or not. Because 
it will have been clear in the figure, if the occupant turns the temperature down, when they 
leave the home.  
There is no clear trend between the percentages of the time the dwellings are occupied and the 
average indoor temperature. The occupants seem to prefer different kind of average indoor 
temperatures, but are more inclined to lower temperatures. Most of the dwellings have suitable 
temperatures in category A, which can be justified with more than one reason; (1) The 
dwellings can namely be constructed with good insulation which keeps the dwellings warm, 
(2) the clothing and metabolic rate varies from occupant to occupant, (3) equipment in the 
==PAGE:70==
52  
 
dwelling, (4) occupant prefer temperatures between 20-24 degrees and (5) the measured period 
is not the same for all dwellings (cooling or heating season). Studies have shown that bedroom 
tend to have colder indoor temperature than other rooms [57]. This can also be seen in the 
figure 37, where some of the bedroom tends to have colder indoor temperature than the living 
room. It has also been stated by some occupants that the small window in the bedroom is 
usually open all night, which obviously result in a colder environment. Studies [57] noted that 
men usually prefer colder rooms than women, knowing that the gender of occupants could have 
assessed the correlation between men and colder indoor climate.   
 Indoor air quality 
In this study, the average CO2-concentration indicate the indoor air quality in the dwellings. It 
was expected that if the occupants were not home for a while, the CO2 will decrease. But this 
assumption was not shown in the figure. However, there was a little trend, that the higher the 
percentage of the time dwelling is occupied, the higher the CO2-concentration becomes. 
Overall, the occupants have very high CO2-concentrations, where most of the dwellings are in 
category C and few in category A. However, the bedroom tends to have a higher average CO2-
concentration than the living room, which usually increases at the sleeping hours in the night.   
 Relative humid 
High relative humidity is one of the reasons for mold and moisture which damage the building 
materials. High relative humidity occurs especially when bathing or cooking, if it has not been 
vented. The occupants have complained that the dwellings have a lot of moisture in the 
windows and walls, and condensation on the windows have been seen. This study has not 
looked at windows openings at all, but it would have been good to see whether the occupants 
open their windows or not, or if there is a correlation between opening of windows and relative 
humidity as studies have shown. However, a lot of the dwellings are poorly structured, so it 
will be difficult to make drafts, even if it was assumed that the windows are open regularly. 
In the plot, the average humidity was in category A, which indicate that the natural ventilation 
is being used and it is not a humid indoor climate. This conclusion will maybe not be the same, 
if whole measured data or only for the cold month were considered.  
 
==PAGE:71==
53  
 
 ENERGY CONSUMPTION 
It would have been very interesting to compare occupant behaviour (opening/closing windows 
and adjusting thermostat) with energy consumption based on heating, which was also part of 
this study. 
Without the opening/closing movement of windows and lack of data from heating 
consumption, this study has become limited regarding analyzing and discussing the obtained 
data, such as the indoor temperature. It is unfortunately difficult to examine why some 
dwellings have had low average indoor temperature. It might have been a result of no use of 
thermostats, or a combination of opening windows and use of thermostat, which can result in 
an increased energy consumption. Unfortunately, these speculations need to be studied further.      
 FUTURE WORK 
In order to understand and use the models more, the next step will be to hand out questionnaires 
to the occupants in the investigated area. This will take us a step closer to better understand the 
obtained results of presence behaviour and in relation to indoor environment. The 
questionnaires can help us tell more about the residents' preferences, routines, type of person. 
This will allow categorizing the results and thus the occupants in relation to different 
parameters such as age, gender, family type etc. 
In addition, it would also be interesting to implement the models into simulation programs. The 
data is based on true behavior, therefore it will increase validity and ensure that results are 
closer to reality when simulating energy consumption.

6 CONCLUSION 
This study presents an investigation of different patterns of occupants in private homes. The 
focus was on modelling behaviour pattern of occupants’ presence, and on determining the 
effect on indoor environment. 
With the provided data from dwellings, it was possible to develop two discrete time Markov 
chains models. The Markov chains models were made with a 5-minute time step. The first 
model describes the probability of the occupant’s arrival. The second model informs the 
average duration of occupancy in the dwelling. Overall, the models showed a great variation 
and indicated that the investigated area had different lifestyles and routines. There was not a 
big difference between weekdays and weekends. The time of day had no effect on the 
occupant’s arrival. Occupant stayed less at home during the weekdays compared to the 
weekends.  
There was no clear trend between the percentages of the time the dwellings are occupied and 
the average indoor temperature. The occupants do not adjust their temperatures whether they 
are at home or not. The bedrooms tended to have colder indoor temperature compared to the 
living rooms.  
The occupants have very high average CO2-concentrations in the dwellings, where most of the 
time it was over 800 ppm. However, the bedrooms had a higher average CO2-concentration 
than the living rooms.  A little trend showed that the higher the percentage of the time dwelling 
is occupied, the higher the CO2-concentration becomes. But that trend was based on only 5 
dwellings out of 46 dwellings.   
There was no clear correlation between percentage of the time the dwelling was occupied and 
indoor relative humidity, even though the occupants had complained about the dwellings 
having a lot of moisture in the windows and walls, and condensation on the windows. A lot of 
the dwellings are poorly structured, so it will be difficult to make drafts, even if it was assumed 
that the windows are open regularly. 
Overall, the occupant behaviour consists of a lot of complexity. The Markov chains models 
gave a clear picture of the varieties, when working with occupant behaviour. It is not always 
easy to predict the unpredictable.
