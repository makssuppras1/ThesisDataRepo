Abstract
Extensive water depth challenges due to glacioﬂuvial sediments outside of the existing
harbor in Kangerlussuaq, prevents larger ships to dock. Thus cruise- and container ships
transporting cargo and passengers to and from the settlement, are forced to anchor at deep
sea some distance away from the harbor. This is a time consuming and costly solution, as
containers and passengers must be transported to the existing harbor by smaller watercraft.
Qeqqata municipality has therefore initiated plans regarding the construction of a new
harbor and adjacent marina at a diﬀerent location, where the sedimentation challenges are
less severe. A ledge near Hancock Pynt, located around 10 km from the existing harbor,
has been suggested as a suitable location. The environmental conditions related to the
new harbor and marina have been investigated through the use of the numerical modeling
suite MIKE 21. Simulations of the hydrodynamic and wave conditions in the inner part
of Søndre Strømfjord and outside the harbor area, were used to assess the best suitable
location of the marina. Also, the design of protection measures in terms of a ﬂoating
breakwater was determined based on these ﬁndings and previous model tests. Design
widths of 4.2 m, 4.9 m and 13.5 m for a prism-, catamaran- and tire assembly type ﬂoating
breakwater respectively, are suggested in order to maintain a moderate wave climate
for head sea conditions in the marina, located to the east of Hancock Pynt. The wave
conditions outside of the proposed harbor were found to mainly consist of short-period
waves. The harbor downtime related to the inﬂuence of these waves was investigated and
found to be within acceptable limits (less than 1 week per year). Further analysis of the
inﬂuence of long-period waves and swell, indicates that the proposed location is highly
suitable for a future harbor.

Introduction
1.1
Background
The settlement of Kangerlussuaq is located in the inner part of Greenland’s longest fjord;
180 km long Søndre Strømfjord (Danish name) and is part of the Qeqqata municipality.
Although only inhabited of around 500 residents, the main international airport of
Greenland is located here which makes it a widely used connection point for travelers
and cargo. Being one of the few settlements in Greenland with a road connection to
the inland ice, also makes it a popular tourist destination. Many of them arriving with
cruise ships entering the fjord during the ice-free summer period. However, the existing
harbor in Kangerlussuaq experiences major challenges with vast amounts of glacioﬂuvial
sediments outside the harbor area, preventing larger ships to dock. This is also strongly
aﬀecting the goods transport to and from the settlement where container vessels, as well
as cruise ships, are forced to anchor further out the fjord. Thus, cargo and passengers
must be transported to the harbor by smaller watercraft. The yearly cost of maintaining
a small operative channel for this purpose is estimated to 1.0 mill. DKK (Hansen and
Paulsen, 2014). This is an ineﬃcient, expensive and time consuming solution. Qeqqata
municipality is therefore interested in the construction of a new harbor further out the
fjord, where the sedimentation challenges are less severe. A new harbor location have
been proposed about 10 km from the existing harbor, as depicted in Figure 1.1.

1.2
New harbor location and previous investigations
1
INTRODUCTION
Søndre Strømfjord roughly consists of two distinct parts, as depicted in Figure 1.1; the
inner relatively wide part (about 4 km) with water depths up to 275 m and a more narrow
(about 1 km) and shallow outer part stretching almost 100 km out to the North Atlantic
ocean. The outer part consists of water depths in the range of 25 - 100 m. Both the
existing and the proposed new harbor are located in the inner part of the fjord, which
resembles a U-shaped basin ﬁlled with glaciomarine sediments. Ice also covers the inner
part of the fjord during the winter season, reaching a thickness of about 1 m (Nielsen
et al., 2010). Thus, the harbor is only operational during the summer period, where for
instance the cargo transportation company Royal Arctic Line (RAL) is a frequent user
from the start of June to the middle of September (Hansen and Paulsen, 2014). Large
tidal variations are prominent in the fjord, ranging from about 2 - 4 m which generates
strong currents in the outer narrow part. During the summer period, large quantities
of meltwater enters the fjord, from mainly three rivers transporting freshwater from the
inland ice or the smaller Sukkertoppen Ice Cap on the south side of the fjord. This is
also the primary cause for the extensive sedimentation outside the existing harbor, as
the rivers transport large amounts of ﬁne-grained sediments to the fjord. Located in the
innermost part of the fjord passing just next to Kangerlussuaq, is Watson River. In 2005
the peak ﬂow in Watson River exceeded 1000 m3/s (Nielsen et al., 2010).
1.2
New harbor location and previous investigations
The new harbor is proposed placed at the ledge depicted in Figure 1.2. For convenience,
this location will hereafter be referred to as Hancock Pynt (HP). The selected location is
based on seismic data collected in November 2012, where water depth and thickness of
sediment layers in parts of the fjord were measured (Ploug et al., 2013). The outer part of

part mainly consists of Postglacial sediments. Two stony beaches separate the sediment

mm, one to the west and one to the east (Stenstad et al., 2015). A picture depicting the
beach to the east is seen in Figure 1.2.

2.1
Flow Model Flexible Mesh: Hydrodynamic Module (HD)
The Flow Model FM - HD has been developed for applications within oceonographic,
coastal and estuarine environments and is based on a ﬂexible mesh approach. It can be
used to simulate hydraulic and environmental phenomena like water variations and ﬂows
in lakes, estuaries and coastal areas. Some of the main features and eﬀects included in
the model are the following:
 Flooding and drying
 Bottom shear stress
 Coriolis force
 Barometric pressure gradient
 Momentum dispersion
 Wind shear stress
The Hydrodynamic Flow Model is non-linear, making it one of the most advanced and
comprehensive hydrodynamic models available. It is based on the numerical solution of the
two-dimensional shallow water equations; the depth-integrated incompressible Reynolds

averaged Navier-Stokes equations. This means that the model consists of continuity,
momentum, temperature, salinity and density equations. The spatial discretisation of the
equations is performed using a cell-centred ﬁnite volume method. The spatial domain
is discretised by subdivision of the continuum into non-overlapping elements/cells. An
unstructured mesh is used in the horizontal plane and the elements in the model can
either be triangles or quadrilateral elements.
The HD model requires the following inputs:
 Computational mesh and bathymetry
 Boundary conditions: all open boundaries must be speciﬁed, either with a surface

Further information regarding the Hydrodynamic Flow Model HD module including its
governing equations, can be found in the user guide (DHI-HDu.g, 2014) or the scientiﬁc
documentation (DHI-HD, 2014).
2.2
Spectral Waves Model (SW)
The MIKE 21 Spectral Waves model is capable of simulating the growth, decay and
transformation of wind-generated waves and swells in oﬀshore and coastal areas. One
of the main application areas of the model is the design of oﬀshore, coastal and port
structures. It is based on the wave action balance equation where the wave action density
spectrum represents the wave ﬁeld. Two diﬀerent formulations are included in the model:
 Directional decoupled parametric formulation
 Fully spectral formulation
The directional decoupled parametric formulation follows Holthuijsen et al. (1989), where
a parameterization made in the frequency domain is done by introducing the dependent
variables; the zeroth and ﬁrst moment of the wave action spectrum. In the fully spectral
formulation it is the directional-frequency wave action spectrum which is the dependent
variable and the formulation is based on the wave action conservation equation. Some of
the main physical phenomena included in the model are the following:
 Non-linear wave-wave interaction
 Wave growth by action of wind
 Dissipation due to white-capping
 Refraction and shoaling due to depth variations

Boussinesq Wave Module (BW)

 Wave-current interaction
 Eﬀect of time-varying water depth
The governing equation in geographical and spectral space is discretised using a cell-
centred ﬁnite volume method. An unstructured mesh technique is used in the geographical
domain.
The time discretization can either be applied as an instationary or quasi-
stationary formulation. For the instationary formulation, the time integration is based
on a fractional step approach where every time step involves the calculation of solutions
for the propagation function, as well as for the source function. For the quasi-stationary
formulation, a steady state solution is calculated at each time step using modiﬁed Newton-
Raphson iterative procedure or iteration in the time domain. Here time is removed as an
independent variable.
Some of the most important inputs required in the Spectral Waves model are the following:
 Computational mesh and bathymetry
 Equations, discretisation and solution technique
 Forcing parameters: water level data, current data and wind data
Further information regarding the Spectral Waves model including its governing equations,
can be found in the user guide (DHI-SWu.g., 2014) or the scientiﬁc documentation
(DHI-SW, 2014).
2.3
Boussinesq Wave Module (BW)
The BW module is based on the numerical solution of extended Boussinesq equations
and is ideally suited to asses the combined eﬀects of most wave phenomena of interest in
harbors and coastal areas. This includes:
 Wave propagation over complex terrain
 Refraction and diﬀraction
 Reﬂection and partial reﬂection from porous structures
 Analysis of low-frequency oscillations
The use of enhanced Boussinesq type equations makes it possible to simulate the prop-
agation of non-linear directional waves from deep to shallow water. Both regular and
irregular waves can be used as model input. A computer representation of the area of
interest is created, where the model terrain is represented as depth values over a regular

3.3.1
Extreme wind analysis
An investigation of the extreme winds in order to estimate the characteristic mean wind
speed has been conducted by assuming a Gumbel type distribution of the extreme events.
In Eurocode 1 Part 1-4, the characteristic wind speed is deﬁned as the “fundamental value
of the basic wind velocity with an annual probabilities of exceedence of 0.02, which is
equivalent to a mean return period of 50 years” (CEN, 2005). This means that out of 50
extreme wind events over 50 years, only one of them is stronger than the characteristic
value.
As the wind speed is closely related to the growth of wind waves and thus one of the main
factors for the determination of signiﬁcant wave heights, the analysis of the characteristic
wind speeds has been given considerable attention in this report. The theory behind the
following statistical analysis can be found in Palutikof et al. (1999) and in Appendix A.1.2.
Two methods for determining the extreme winds were chosen. One where the maximum
wind speed for every year of measurements was extracted; the annual method. The
other using a threshold value; the POT method. Additionally, two methods were used to

apparent in the middle ﬁgure displaying the peak periods, where some few measured peak
periods are seen to lay well above the rest of the measurements in a somewhat abnormal
manner. This is believed to be caused by irregularities in the measured data from the
Waverider buoy. As previously mentioned, the sea states have been deﬁned for every third
hour. By manually deﬁning the sea states and at the same time remove possible noise
from the measurements, a more accurate representation of the actual wave conditions
would probably have been obtained. However, as this would be highly time consuming,
the three hour sea state interval was preferred and little emphasis has been put on the
higher irregular wave period peaks. Also, considering that the heave measurements in
general are of very low magnitude, the three hour sea state intervals are believed to be
acceptable in order to represent the conditions for the majority of the cases.

6
Marina investigations
The new harbor in Kangerlussuaq is not only a commercial harbor, handling container-
and cruise ships. It should also beneﬁt smaller watercraft such as local recreational-
or ﬁshing boats by providing safe berthing places and infrastructure required to serve
harbor users. Hence, a marina must be established at the site. A marina, or small craft
harbor can be deﬁned as a place on a body of water that provides protection for a variety
of watercraft against environmental forces such as waves, wind, tides, currents and ice
(ASCE, 1994). The marina should have a total capacity of around 20 - 30 boats, as
expressed by the municipality (Poulsen, 2015, Pers. comm.). Considering the proposed
harbor location south-east of HP, the most likely location of the marina would be to the
East or to the West, adjacent to the harbor infrastructure as illustrated in Figure 6.1. To
attain the required level of protection for moored boats, some type of breakwaters are
usually required at the marina entrance. Also the water depth in the marina must be
suﬃcient, considering safe anchoring for boats and to avoid unnecessary dredging. Some of
these issues are addressed in this section, with the objective of ﬁnding the optimal marina
placement and discussing possible required protection measures. Finally a preliminary
design of the marina is suggested. A brief overview of the most important evaluated
conditions associated with the choosing of marina location is given in Table 6.1.

6.5
Protection measures - ﬂoating breakwater
The large variation in water depth, the overall small incident wave conditions on both
sides of HP, and the signiﬁcant risk of poor bottom conditions as seen in Orlanders study
from 2014 (Orlander, 2014), favors a ﬂoating breakwater (FBW) solution. Also the small
environmental impact as well as economical considerations make this a more feasible
solution than for example a rubble mound structure. A FBW can also be placed on land
during the winter season, preventing damage to the structure from ice forces. Floating
breakwaters do not provide full protection from the wave action, but leads to a reduction

1. Reﬂective system: includes rigid, wall-sided-structures like pontoons, prisms or

Ship movements
A moored ship in a harbor is in continuous movement when under the impact of waves,
currents and wind gusts. The magnitude and direction of the waves and wind govern
the magnitude of the movements which varies over a wide range. Whether the moored
ships experience suﬃciently calm conditions for handling cargo and passengers is one
of the main criteria that characterizes a good harbor (Thoresen, 2010). Too large ship
movements can slow down or cease cargo handling operations and may also lead to damage
to the ship and harbor installations (Elsinga et al., 1993).
The analysis of movements of a ship in berth is a complex matter. The dynamics of ﬂuid
motions and rigid bodies need to be investigated, governed by a combination of external
loads and the inertia of the body. For a moored ship, also the stiﬀness characteristics of
the fenders and moorings must be accounted for. Deﬁning a set of limiting wave conditions
is therefore almost impossible and in many cases of little relevance, as it is the vessels
response to the incident waves that should be the limiting factor. Some attempts of
deﬁning a maximum signiﬁcant wave height criteria for safe mooring conditions have
however been attempted, as the values given in Table 7.1 from Bratteland (1988)1 and
Thoresen (2010)2. These are only valid for wind waves with periods up to approximately
10 s and give the maximum signiﬁcant wave heights for safe mooring conditions in harbors.

7.1.3
Summer period downtime due to waves
For loading/unloading operations, the incident waves during normal conditions are of
much higher relevance than the extreme events. Should the wave conditions cause harbor
operations to be stopped for a total of more than 1 week per year, the harbor would
normally be regarded as having unacceptable conditions for loading/unloading operations
(Bratteland, 1988).
The maximum signiﬁcant wave height before loading/unloading operations of container
ships had to be stopped, was in an article by Velsink H. published in PIANC No. 56, set
to be 0.5 m in head seas. For general cargo vessels the maximum signiﬁcant wave height
was set to 1.0 m in head seas and 0.8 m in beam seas (45 - 90◦) (Velsink, 1987). This is
valid for wave periods in the range 7 - 10 s, thus it is most likely a conservative estimate
for the small wave period conditions outside the new Kangerlussuaq harbor. A maximum
signiﬁcant wave height of 0.5 m has been chosen as the limiting criteria in the further
evaluation.

7.2
Long-period waves investigation
The generation of long-period waves in harbor areas (also called seiches) is primarily
related to the non-linear interaction of short-period waves and swell. Strong seiching
typically occurs during high seas due to the high correlation of the long- and short-period
waves energy level (Kofoed-Hansen et al., 2005). The natural period of the surge and
sway motion of a moored ship often falls within the long-period wave range from about
20 s to several minutes. Long-period waves generated by the previously investigated
wind waves, could therefore be a possible source for the initiation of large movements
of moored ships at the proposed harbor location. Such large ship movements caused by
long-period waves have been reported even with wave heights less than 10 cm (Goda,
1985). The largest inﬂuencing factor of container loading operations is the surge motion,
and a moored container vessel usually holds a natural surge frequency of between 40 s
and 80 s (Moes and Terblanche, 2010). An investigation of possible long-period waves in
the harbor area has therefore been conducted. This has been done by examining wave
energy spectra outside the harbor berthline for four wave scenarios using the Boussinesq
model results, and by investigating the inﬂuence of swell for the summer period Spectral
Waves simulation.
Criteria for the maximum allowable wave heights for cargo handling under long-period
waves are given in the design standard of port and harbor facilities in Japan (OCDI, 1991),
for three ranks of wave heights, as seen in Table 7.6.

The Spectral Waves model only simulates the conditions occurring based on the adjusted
wind input data from the nearby wind station. Thus, winds from more distant weather
systems that may generate swells entering the fjord are not considered. Many fjords
open to the full ocean fetch, can experience large swells. Deep waters which are common
outside the mouth of many Greenlandic fjords, create little energy loss for incoming swells
and may further increase the possibilities for swells entering the fjord (Syvitski et al.,
1987). However, as the considered harbor area is located far from the ocean (around 160
km), and assuming that possible swells entering the fjord are dissipated by the sinuous
geometry of the fjord due to wave diﬀraction, the risk of swells caused by distant weather
systems are considered to be very small.
More local weather systems, for example around the end of the 54 km long fetch in
the south-west direction may however, generate long-period swells causing challenges for
moored ships. Such conditions are not included in the Spectral Waves simulation, nor is
it considered further in this report.

8
CONCLUSIONS
8
Conclusions
Environmental conditions related to the proposed new harbor at Hancock Pynt (HP)
outside the settlement of Kangerlussuaq in West Greenland, have been investigated with
the numerical modeling suite MIKE 21. 2D Hydrodynamic and Spectral Waves models
with ﬂexible mesh have been established and calibrated with available data from the area
of interest. Simulations of extreme wave events and summer period current- and wave
conditions were successfully conducted. A Boussinesq Wave model was created to simulate
the wave disturbance and analyze low-frequency oscillations in the proposed new harbor
area. Important ﬁndings related to the objectives of the report are summarized in the
following subsections.
8.1
Marina investigations
The wind forces exerted to moored boats during 50-year wind events were found to be
slightly higher to the East than to the West of HP. Also the current speed and occurrence
are higher to the East, thus favoring a marina to the West of HP. Still, due to the water
depth and wave conditions, a marina located at the East side of HP is recommended. The
occurring signiﬁcant wave heights were found too high for “moderate” wave conditions to
be maintained in the marina during both a 50-year event and a maximum summer period
event. Protection measures in form of a ﬂoating breakwater (FBW) are suggested. Prism,
catamaran and tire assembly type of FBWs of widths 4.2 m, 4.9 m and 13.5 m respectively
assuming 7 m water depths, were found adequate to maintain a “moderate” wave climate
for head sea conditions in the marina. The design widths are based on incident waves
during the maximum summer event, as suﬃcient protection from the 50-year wave event
results in unrealistically large FBW dimensions. Previous model tests on the three FBW
types in question, have been considered for the design. Some uncertainties are therefore
associated with the results. Due to the similarity with the model test speciﬁcations, the
design widths are however, most likely a good estimation of the FBW widths required
to satisfy the wave height criteria in the marina. A marina design with a capacity of 22
boats, each of 6 m length is presented, where the FBW of a prism or catamaran type is
utilized also as a dock system. The ﬁnal decision of the FBW type, should be based on
economical- as well as practical feasibility deliberations. The latter mainly concerning
maintenance requirements and possibilities for winter storage on land.
8.2
Ship movements
Analyzes of the wave conditions in the proposed new harbor area, indicate that mainly
short-period waves constitute the wave climate. Peak wave periods of 5.4 s and 4.7 s
during 50-year wave events with characteristic winds from southwest (SW) and southeast
(SE) respectively were found. For the maximum summer period event, peak periods of
87
==PAGE:100==

4.7 s and 3.6 s from swell were obtained from the SW and SE sector respectively. A
mode peak period of 1.9 s was found outside HP at the proposed location of the harbor
berthline for the entire summer period simulation. Such short-period waves will mostly

cargo handling operations of larger ships, should the signiﬁcant wave heights exceed a
certain value. Assuming that cargo handling operations have to be stopped at signiﬁcant
wave heights larger than 0.5 m, a total downtime of about 2 days during the summer
period should be accounted for.
Possible long-period waves were studied by closer examination of wave energy spectra
from outside the harbor berthline for diﬀerent scenarios, and of swell inﬂuence from the

were found to occur just outside the harbor berthline for the maximum summer period
events. Such waves may initiate larger movements of moored ships in the new harbor.
However, with a maximum signiﬁcant wave height of 6.2 mm, long-period waves in the
harbor area are believed to exert little risk of any ship movements of signiﬁcance. The
inﬂuence of swell was similarly found to pose no further risk of interfering with the cargo
handling operations at the harbor. Investigations of the motions of a barge outside the
proposed harbor berthline, further conﬁrms that Hancock Pynt seems to be a suitable
location for a future harbor, considering ship movements inﬂicted by waves.

The resistance to ﬂow parameters are in the Mike 21 Flow Model HD given in terms of

variables related to calibration of the HD model (Lambkin, 2010). The bottom stress, τb,
is determined by a quadratic friction law and given as:

where M is the bed roughness value, h is the total water depth and g is the gravitational
acceleration (9.81 ms−1). In MIKE 21, the following relationship is used between the
Nikuradse roughness length, ks and the Manning number:
M = 25.4

Meaning the Manning number will decrease with increasing roughness length. Thus for a
constant ﬂow velocity and water depth, a larger bed roughness (Manning) value indicates
a smaller bed friction. Based on these relationships, the MIKE 21 manual suggests a

Compared to the measurements at Camp Loyd.
It is evident that the best ﬁt is the simulation with a bed resistance of M = 15m1/3/s. In
order to further increase the model accuracy, the Manning number was ﬁnally however, set
to vary with the water depth of the domain. According to (Nielsen et al., 2010) the inner
part of the fjord has the shape of a gorge cut into the bedrock and ﬁlled with ﬁne-grained
sediments, which leads to the simpliﬁed assumption that the land boundaries mainly
consists of rock walls and the bottom of ﬁne-grained sediments. Hence the Manning
number is set to be considerably lower at water depths along the land boundaries (M = 5)
then at larger water depths (M = 30). Limited amount of data is available for closer
determination of the bed roughness in the fjord, but core samples from locations close to
the harbor area in 2012 indicated very ﬁne grained sediments on the bed surface (Orlander,
2014). A Manning value of 30 have therefore been applied also in this area in order to
best possible match the existing conditions in the area of interest.
It could very well be assumed that a Manning value far higher then the that of 30 should be
chosen for the deeper parts of the fjord where ﬁne-grained sediments are assumed located.
However, a review of the bed roughness variable in MIKE 21 Flow Model FM (Lambkin,
2010), indicates that the Manning number experiences a decrease with increasing water
depth for all tested materials (mud, sand, gravel). A Manning number of 30 at the deepest
part of the fjord is therefore believed to be somewhat realistic, even though the bed is
assumed consists of very ﬁne grained sediments. Note that the bed resistance close to
the boundary was set to M = 10 as this proved to prevent stability problems (blow-ups).
This way noise generated by the boundary was removed. This however, should have no
inﬂuence on the simulation results as these are located at safe distance from the boundary
(DHI-HDu.g, 2014).
