Abstract 
Low water activity products do not support growth and survival of bacteria. However, 
they are often associated with foodborne outbreaks. Extended scientific research 
highlighted the effect of the high fat content on bacterial elimination during 
pasteurization. Recently, scientists suggested that especially the microstructure of 
high fat-low water activity products could trigger an increased heat resistance of the 
present bacteria.  
Peanut butter is a low water activity product that is frequently associated with 
foodborne salmonellosis. Food industry have utilized dietary fibers to change and/or 
construct new microstructures in foods. Powdered dietary fibers such as inulin, 
fructooligosaccharides and galactooligosaccharides (in powder and viscous form) 
were mixed with peanut butter to build new structures and evaluate their effect on 
the heat resistance of Salmonella. Cryogenic Scanning Electron Microscopy 
implemented to characterize the structure of peanut butter. Similarities between FOS 
and default peanut butters were observed whereas inulin and GOS fiber created 
distinguishingly different microstructures.  
Pasteurization of peanut butter resulted in approximately 3-logCFU/g viability 
reduction of Salmonella Tyhpimurium U-311 after 80 min at 75o C. Addition of 5 g 
fructooligosaccharide and powdered GOS did not have any significant effect on the 
heat resistance of Salmonella. In contrary, equal amount of viscous GOS and inulin 
demonstrated a significant decrease. Weibull model was used to fit experimental data 
and enable quantitative evaluation. b values of all peanut butter samples were 
calculated below 1 indicating the increased resistance exhibited by the viable cells. 
10.3 min was calculated to achieve 1-log CFU/g cells reduction in plain peanut butter. 
Identical tr was also calculated for the peanut butter containing powdered GOS fiber. 
10-fold bacteria elimination was observed after 7.6 and 4.9 min in FOS and inulin 
peanut butters respectively. 2-fold and 3-fold increase in inulin content did not have 
any significant effect on the bacteria performance. The least heat resistance of S. 
Typhimurium U-311 was observed when peanut butter was mixed with viscous GOS 
fiber resulting in 1-logCFU/g decrease after 2.9 min. 3-logCFU/g elimination was not 
observed in both default and FOS peanut butters compared to the inulin and viscous 
GOS counterparts. Powder GOS peanut butter was calculated to demand 
pasteurization for 125.8 min to eliminate Salmonella cells by 1,000.  
Water activity of peanut butter was also measured. All peanut butter samples mixed 
with powdered fibers were measured a water activity ranging from 0.2-0.27. Plain 
peanut butter had the lowest aw value and whereas powdered GOS the highest.  
Mixing of peanut butter with viscous GOS fiber resulted in a markedly increase in 
water activity to 0.5.

Introduction and aim of the study 
The current study aims at investigating a relationship between food microstructure 
and microbial survival. This link is evident but the underlying mechanism remains 
unclear. Among scientists, there are voices advocating that the interactions of food 
ingredients are responsible for creating micro-environments that on micro-scale can 
trigger bacterial performance (growth, survival, etc.) that is different to the 
conventionally expected (Wilson, et al. 2002). This response can either result in a 
decreased viability of microorganisms (Aguilera 2005) or be benefit to the 
microorganism rendering them more resistant to the surrounding environmental 
niches (FDA 2013). A more resistant microorganism may require demanding 
treatment to be under control. This will lead to implementation of even more 
stringent safety protocols and processing practices with possible side-effects for the 
sensorial attributes. 
It is widely known in the food industry that interactions of food ingredients have an 
impact on the technical characteristics of the end-up product (Gaonkar and 
McPherson 2006). Recently, microstructure was also emerged in food processing as 
an opportunistic factor that could affect the features of products (Aguilera 2005). 
However, a micro- and nano- approach is required to establish the link between 
microstructure and technical characteristics and acquire an insight on the topic (Sohail 
2011). How would a change in the concentration of an element impact to the food 
matrix and the apparent sensory appraisal of the food product? How would this 
change affect the food safety of the product? Such questions need an in-depth 
understanding of the micro-architecture of the food matrix as well as knowledge on 
the microbial behavior in the specific food environment. However, this information 
pair is not always accessible and in order to acquire useful data focused studies are 
needed. 
This report consists of a preliminary project to shed light on the relationship between 
microstructure and heat survival of Salmonella Typhimurium with the ultimate 
ambition to develop a model that predicts the effect of microstructure on heat 
performance of Salmonella in low aw food products. The food matrix selected for this 
purpose is peanut butter, a low moisture product that together with its high fat 
content consists of a non-favorable environment for both growth and survival for 
bacteria such as Salmonella. Traditionally, manufacturing of peanut butter does not 
include any thermal processing after has been produced. Roasting of peanuts is the 
step that is assigned to reduce the water content to such low levels that no 
microorganisms are able to remain viable (FAO 2012). However, peanut butter has 
been linked to outbreaks of salmonellosis at several occasions as the Centers of 
Disease Control and Prevention denotes in the annual reports (CDC 2015). Therefore,

post-pasteurization of peanut butter in jars is getting increasingly used (Lake, et al. 
2010). 
Peanut butter has not been widely used in microbiological studies compared to nuts 
and other butters or spreads. Therefore, there is no clear evidences of the heat 
survival of Salmonella in this environment. However, heat tolerance data, such as 
decimal reduction times (D-values) are available in literature showing increased 
tolerance in peanut butter as compared to other foods (R. Podolak, et al. 2010). The 
underlying mechanism and concrete theory for this difference remains in the dark. 
Another product that also results in increased heat tolerance of Salmonella spp. is 
chocolate. Chocolate is considered to be similar to peanut butter in terms of 
underlying theory and microbiological aspects. In general, low moisture products are 
the product group with highest interest when it comes to microstructural effects on 
microbial performance. The main reason is their low water activity values below 0.6 
(Fraser 2012)  However, it is cited in the scientific literature that if microorganisms 
undergo stresses in low aw matrices that do not eliminate them, then the surviving 
cells become more resistant to further processing and more tolerant to additives, 
preservatives and inactivation treatment (Boekel 2002). Thus, this project will try to 
give an overview on the heat resistance of this Gram – negative bacterium in peanut 
butter and demonstrate how different microstructures could affect its heat survival.  
In order to achieve that, laboratory experiments were conducted to reveal the heat 
resistance of Salmonella in various peanut butter matrices constructed by addition of 
different dietary fibers. Data were coupled with microscopic observations of the 
matrices as a measure to characterize, understand and potentially quantify the 
microstructure. Microscopy in food science is getting increased attention and has 
been used to reveal the structural grid at micro- and nano- scales. Till recently light 
microscopy was the epicenter of food microscopy with limited information down to 
millimeter order. Since microstructure is believed to affect both the sensorial 
attributes and microbiological behavior, microscopy was directed to use electrons 
instead of light to further dig into the structure of foods. This is the reason why 
electron microscopy came into play in food research fields and among all techniques 
scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and its variations gained popularity. In brief, 
electrons are used to scan across the surface of the food sample outlining the 
topography of the sample and carrying out a substantial surface examination (Sohail 
2011). Thus, details in micro- and nano- scales could be visualized in high resolution 
images and evaluated in terms of micro-compartmentalization (Medeiros, et al. 2012). 
The potentials of Electron Microscopy to participate in and play an important role in 
the food assessment was also discussed. 
Therefore, the present study focused on revealing the role of microstructure in the 
bacterial performance with the purpose of serving as a precursor for further research.

Theory of Food Microstructure 
Food product microstructure 
The behavior of microorganisms in food relies on their identity and inherent 
characteristics in combination with the intrinsic and extrinsic features of the product 
(Sieuwerts, et al. 2008). Modern research suggests compartmentalization and 
microenvironments within the food alter the performance of microorganisms 
(Aguilera 2005).  Combination of the properties of the internal structure of the product 
with the characteristics of the microorganism render its localization, distribution and 
performance rather complex to be predicted. Therefore, final distribution of the cells 
of a microbial hazard can be a combination of food type, microbial strain, composition 
and the structural grid of the food product (Bassett, 2010; Fazaeli, et al., 2012). 
To begin with, when investigating the structure of a food matrix a macroscopic to 
microscopic approach normally is followed (Aguilera 2005). Knowing the composition 
of a food is essential to get an insight of the ingredients that aggregate to compose 
the food’s structure. However, knowledge solely on composition of the product does 
not serve as source of information at microscopic level since interactions and cellular 
structures are omitted. This becomes clear in the case of plant origin foods, like 
vegetables and fruits. Different states of the natural food may have different physical 
characteristics; for instance comparison of the apple and its extracted juice (Aguilera 
2005). Thus, composition does not reflect the microstructure of the food product. It 
might provide with a hint but it is essential to dig into the micro-architecture of the 
food to acknowledge the intrinsic physical characteristics such as rheological behavior, 
sensorial attributes, shelf-life and etc. (Aguilera 2005). Unveiling the food matrix can 
assist product manufacturer demonstrating the effect of the processing on the 
microstructure and enabling the development of processes that aim at both modifying 
or retaining the internal food grid (Aguilera 2005).  
Structural elements play an important role in providing with shape, formation and 
complexity of a food matrix. In foods, structural elements are considered the starch 
granules, plant cells, cell walls, meat fibers and other small particulates (Aguilera 
2005). Observations from industrial handling pointed out that there are cases, like 
tomato juice, carrots and starch, where industrial processing resulting in disruption of 
the naturally predominant structure (Sensoy, 2014; Walstra, 2001). Changes in the 
microstructure of the processed foods could affect the bio-accessibility of nutrients 
Thick and rigid structures may harbor nutrients providing with strong protection 
against the environment (Sensoy 2014). Handling of raw food might result in 
alterations in the microstructure and cause disruption of cell walls and molecular 
structures releasing the contained nutrients (Aguilera 2005). Consequently, it can be

expected that microstructure of the food matrix is closely associated with 
microbiological performance such as microbial growth and survival (FDA 2013). In 
general, foods are multi-component products consisting of many various substances 
resulting in different intrinsic characteristics. Therefore, prediction of the final physical 
characteristics and microbial behavior is not always easy and might differ from the 
ones expected based on laboratory observations (Balint, 2001; McClements, 2007). 
The main cause is the presence of elements that although they cannot be visually 
perceived or observed, they markedly impact the formed microstructure. Dairy 
products are such a good example, where a small change in the process condition or 
formulation can alter the food structure and its acquired technical properties. A slight 
change in the homogenization pressure during yogurt processing can lead to different 
size of fat droplets having an impact on the microstructure in terms of rheological and 
water-holding properties (McClements 2007). This is also the case when mixing an 
ingredient of high fat content with a high water content ingredient. It might appear as 
a homogeneous phase on macro-scale but in micro-scale the mixture is structured in 
two separate liquid phases (Li, et al. 2014).  
When discussing the relationship between the food matrix and microbiological 
behavior, it must be taken into account that all elements, nutrients and substances 
that compose the micro-environment will be perceived by the microorganisms. 
Therefore, the interactions between the microorganisms and the surrounding 
environment affect the microbial growth and survival. This makes it clear that the 
predominant intrinsic and technical features of the food matrix have an impact on the 
microbial load (Wilson et al 2002). 
Microbial contamination of a food product can occur at any spot during the 
production. Surface contamination occurs when the surface comes in contact with 
contaminated environment such as equipment, machinery and tools as well as other 
infected ingredients or raw materials (Bassett 2010). Surface contamination leads to 
colonial localized growth which afterwards can spread to the remaining food. In 
principle, microorganisms grow in the aqueous phase of food and their growth is 
either planktonic or colony restricted. Micro-architecture is the key to determine the 
performance of the microorganism (Wilson et al 2002). Changes, due to mechanical 
means or biochemical reactions might have an impact on microbiological stability. 
Formation of glassy regions across the food matrix and formation of droplets in 
emulsions are some of the ways to reduce mobility of microorganisms forcing them 
to grow in restricted areas (Wilson et al 2002). Under immobilized conditions slower 
kinetic reaction rates are expected because localized colonies may be more effectively 
targeted by preservatives, inactivation processes or any other inhibitory parameter 
(Wilson et al 2002). Availability of nutrients, pH and chemical composition of the 
environment, motility of the microbial cells and release of their metabolites, and 
metabolic by-products, determine the growth conditions and, hence, the microbial 
5 
 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
behavior and final load (Wilson et al 2002). In the food industry, many artificial or 
natural additives are added into the food matrix to enhance specific attributes, and/or 
to prevent microbiological growth or for both purposes. Gums, gelatins, thickeners 
and substances assisting with gel formation result in uniform gelled products, or gelled 
regions with specific consequences on the microbial growth. Growth in gelled regions 
is considered to be localized and restricted in colonies since the motility of microbial 
cells is low and cells are almost immobilized in specific spots (Wilson et al 2002). This 
situation drives some localized changes regarding the nutrient availability, oxygen 
availability, pH alteration, concentration of toxic metabolites that can affect their 
growth rate (Wilson et al 2002). It is also accepted that cells in this state could compete 
with each other with regards to survival resulting in an ultimate decrease in their 
numbers (Wilson et al 2002). Similar patterns are observed in water-in-oil and oil-in-
water emulsions. In the former type, the microbial growth is restricted in the water 
droplets dispersed in the oil phase whereas in the latter microorganisms are freely 
grown in the water phase with oil consisting of a major constraint (Verrips 1980). The 
size of the water droplets is the growth limiting phenomenon in water-in-oil 
emulsions. Small sizes do not provide with enough space for the bacteria to grow and 
coalescence (aggregation) of droplets plays an important role. However, the lipid 
phase around the water droplets consists of major barrier for cells diffusion from one 
droplet to another. On the other hand, intact structure leads to the formation of 
individual water droplets wherein both bacterial growth and survival are restricted 
(Wilson et al 2002). In oil-in-water products, the oil concentration determines the 
viability of the microorganism. Level of oil lower than 80 % allows microorganisms to 
grow as planktonic cells whereas higher levels lead to aggregation of oil droplets 
forming over-packed regions where microorganism are limited to grow in colonies 
(Wilson et al 2002). Emulsions and gelled regions are not the most favorable 
environment for growth of microorganisms due to the complexity of the surrounding. 
As a result they are more susceptible to environmental changes induced by their own 
metabolic pathways or by use of external substances added for the intended purpose. 
Although, behavior of microorganisms is expected to be altered in a food matrix, the 
phenomenon is often underestimated or even neglected (Wilson et al 2002). 
To sum up, acknowledgement of the complexity of the food microstructure is 
significant. It provides with insight on both the formation of matrix and interactions 
among ingredients at micro- and nano-level. In addition, observations on macroscopic 
changes triggered by alterations at micro-level provide with a complete awareness of 
the food grid and modes of structural changes. In-depth insight of microstructure, 
could facilitate the development of models employed to assess safety of foods in 
which microstructure could be used as a parameter. Thus, microbial growth and 
survival could be predicted and evaluated taking into account the effect of the 
environment that microorganisms are exposed to. However, it is important to keep in 
6 
 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
mind, that laboratory observations could differ significantly from the predictions of 
such predictive models. The main reason could be that the relationship between 
microstructure and microbial performance has not been rightly established (Wilson et 
al 2002). For instance, Staphylococcus aureus growth in broths is inhibited when 
sucrose is added. Similar effect was expected to be observed when the bacterium was 
immobilized in gelatin gels. Results revealed that sucrose had minor to negligible 
effect on both growth rate and yield on the immobilized bacteria, outlining the need 
for further research in explaining the link between food microstructure and microbial 
performance (Wilson et al 2002). 
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) 
Applications to the Food Science 
A food product can be investigated applying macroscopic, microscopic and lately 
nanoscopic approaches. The macroscopic view of products gave the food industry the 
opportunity to process naturally occurring raw ingredients and by intervening in their 
physical structure to create products of higher marketability without undermining the 
health benefits (Aguilera 2005). However, it wasn’t till the end of 1990’s when the 
food research community realized that an approach at microscopic level in food 
processing is equally important to macroscopic. Microstructure became the epicenter 
of scientific attention and quickly its impact on the properties and attributes of the 
end-product was recognized (Aguilera 2005). Nanoscopy allowed researchers to move 
deeper in the bacterial cell structure and increase visualization at protein level. Using 
new microscopic techniques, like atomic force microscopy (AFM) and SEM images of 
sections across the cells of bacteria, viruses, etc. were captured collecting important 
information. Knowledge on interactions at atomic level, characteristics of the formed 
bonds and visualization of changes on the surface of microbial cells are nowadays 
available, moving towards in depth understanding of the inherent properties of the 
microorganisms with regards to their surrounding environment (Alakomi, 2007; 
Sohail, 2011; Song et al, 2014). 
 (SEM) is one of the most versatile instruments for examination of the structure and 
chemical composition of bulk materials. SEM was developed based on the principles 
of light microscopy in which light was applied on a specimen to visualize its surface 
and structural features (Fazaeli, Tahmasebi and Djomeh 2012). Without the use of any 
instruments, the unaided eye cannot perceive differences or details lower than 0.1 
mm. Light microscopy increased the resolution to 0.2 μm, though it is believed that is 
hard to see much below 1 μm (Groves and Parker 2013). Substitution of the light 
source and the condenser lens with electron beam and electromagnetic coils, led to 
the development of the electron microscope. The very first SEM, developed at 1950’s, 
could form an image with a resolution of 50 nm, which was better than the light 
7 
 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
microscopy resolution, however, the counterpart transmission electron microscope 
could perform even better. Over the decades, SEM has progressed in many ways. 
Improvements in resolutions and new materials used for the numerous parts of the 
apparatus (filaments, coils, detectors, etc.) allowed the microscope to get prestigious 
position among other techniques for structure characterization (Groves and Parker 
2013). 
SEM has a wide range of applications in many sciences in which information on the 
surface of a bulk material is essential. It was primary used in material science, biology 
and nowadays it gets significant importance in the field of food science. (Impoco, et 
al. 2012), (Xiao and Gao 2012). 
SEM analysis assists with characterization of the structure of the food product 
(Impoco, et al. 2012). In short, the microscope utilizes an electron beam to scan across 
the food matrix, interacting with the structural elements of the specimen generating 
signals that are collected by detectors and eventually converted to form the 3D picture 
(James 2009). Thus, even minor changes in the structure at molecular level can 
become visualized and eventually be linked to a changes occurring during production. 
In other words, SEM gives information on the topography and observations of 
structural changes which aids our understanding within the area of food processing 
and food microstructure (Xiao and Gao 2012). SEM pictures can be used as a source 
of getting awareness of how unperceived microstructure could affect the technical 
characteristics of the final product, such as texture and other sensorial features and 
microbial shelf-life (Groves and Parker 2013). Thus, this knowledge triggers 
improvements in terms of manufacturing process, recipe reformulations towards the 
generation of new innovative foodstuff that are characterized with high quality and 
ensured safety for consumption (Tan, et al., 1997; James, 2009; Fazaeli, et al., 2012). 
SEM has successfully visualized the microstructure of oil droplets of bi-layers 
emulsions as mentioned in the report of Tippets (Tippetts, Shen and Martini 2013). 
The effect of mechanical damage on the surface of wheat starch granules at micro-
scale was investigated by Barrera, et al. (2013) using SEM in combination with 
Environmental SEM. It is noteworthy that cryo-SEM was employed to visualize the 
changes of microstructure of milk and curd during manufacturing of Cheddar cheese 
(Ong, et al. 2011). 
SEM and microorganism examination 
SEM can be employed to perform visualization of the microbial contamination status 
of bulk materials. SEM is not limited (such as transmitted electron microscopy and 
light Microscopy) and can show bacteria in a great detail in their natural inhabitat. This 
technique renders the bacteria as 3-D objects providing with morphological 
information of their surface structures. The applications of SEM in microbiology are 
8 
 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
associated with gross morphology and structural identification, changes in 
morphology of the cell during the different growth stages (from spore germination till 
final death), and changes in their size and shape induced by their surrounding 
environment (Ampe, et al. 1999) (Afrikian, Julian and Bulla 1973). Moreover, the 
opportunity to scan the surface of food matrices renders SEM an important tool in the 
distribution theory, revealing distributional patterns of microorganisms in various 
food environments. Thus, awareness of colonization characteristics, localization 
points paired with morphological characterization of matrices provide with an in-
depth understanding on the bacterial distribution across food matrices, (Kalab, Yang 
and Chabot 2008). 
A great number of food specimens have been examined under the electron beam for 
their microbial burden. Microorganisms found in the epicenter of SEM visualization 
are yeasts, bacteria and fungus. SEM can operate under high vacuum and at near to 
environmental pressures. The adaptability of the technique to operate at various 
pressure values is the great advantage that makes microbiologists to choose it among 
other visualization techniques (Kalab, Yang and Chabot 2008). Thus, it is possible to 
examine microorganisms even in hydrated products. Cryogenic atmosphere is another 
option when the aim is to work at high vacuum. The selection of the method is 
dependent mostly on the state of the food specimen (Kalab, Yang and Chabot 2008).  
Although SEM consists of a useful means of identification and characterization of the 
microbial structure in bulk specimens, special preparation is a prerequisite before the 
in-chamber electron scan. Dehydration with ethanol, fixation of the structure to avoid 
disruption during further sample preparation and when applicable freezing belong to 
the steps that must be fulfilled prior specimen introduction to SEM chamber (Kalab, 
Yang and Chabot 2008). Preparation of foods contaminated with microorganisms 
needs to be designed according to the characteristics of the matrix to prevent from 
structure distortion or masking of the cells resulting in misinterpretations and 
undisclosed topographical information (Kalab, Yang and Chabot 2008). 
Principle of scanning electron microscopy 
SEM is the most frequent electron microscopy employed to analyze food samples. It 
can give high resolution images with topographic information. Imaging of the food 
specimen is a result of the electron beam interaction with the examined food. The 
beam of primary electrons is generated from a filament (metallic origin) and focused 
on the surface of the food sample through the use of electromagnetic lenses and coils. 
When the primary electrons reach the surface, they do not bounce off immediately 
but they penetrate into the surface interacting with the inner layers of the surface. A 
specific area of the sample is then excited generating electrons signals that are 
emitted from the surface and eventually detected. The shape of excitation area is 
9 
 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
dependent on the atomic number (Z) of the particles comprising the sample and it can 
be either a hemisphere (high Z) or resembling to “tear drop” (low Z). The area becomes 
larger in volume and size when highly accelerated beam is utilized for the specimen 
scanning. The output of the electron-specimen interactions is a variety of different 
electron signals from which information about the microstructure of the sample can 
be extracted. The list of generated signals includes secondary electrons, backscattered 
electrons, Auger electrons and X-rays (Nixon, 1971; Zhou, et al. ; Groves, et al., 2013; 
Stokes, 2013). 
Cryo Scanning Electron Microscopy (Cryo-SEM) 
Microstructure examination of biological specimen often requires overcoming 
difficulties closely linked to the nature of the viewed sample. Food products belong to 
this category and the main concern during examination is the water content that in 
some cases can reach high levels. Therefore, conventional SEM is not applicable, as 
vacuum will lead to sublimation of water followed by outgassing and distortion of the 
specimen structure or loss of diffusible elements and eventually altering the structure 
of the viewing specimen (James, Advances in "Wet" Electron Micrscoscopy Technqiues 
and Their Application to the Study of Food Structure 2009). As a consequence the 
topographic details acquired might not be relevant as they would not represent the 
real matrix. In addition to this, some specimen can be beam sensitive and be destroyed 
by the primary electron prior acquisition of the 3D image. Thus, the perceived 
microstructure might not be efficiently displayed generating results that cannot be 
reliable and probably lead to mis-interpretations (James, 2009; Stokes, 2013). 
Cryo-microscopy is the solution to the above problematic situation (James, Advances 
in "Wet" Electron Micrscoscopy Technqiues and Their Application to the Study of Food 
Structure 2009). The main principle is that the specimen is viewed, still under vacuum 
alike on conventional SEM, in a frozen state whilst keeping its natural characteristics. 
To achieve that special preparation is necessary to avoid defects occurring during 
scanning. The main concern that rises when hydrated specimen like food products are 
examined, is the formation of ice crystals during the freezing step (EMS 2015). To 
overcome ice crystals, specimen undergoes fast freezing by plunging into “slushy” 
nitrogen at -210o C and then remained at temperatures below -130o C to prevent from 
recrystallization (Tg = 120o C) (EMS 2015). However, it must be taken into 
consideration that sublimation of water occurs but given the temperature is lower 
than the recrystallization threshold, the sublimation rate is almost negligible (0.001 
nm/sec) (EMS 2015). Maintaining the sample at solid frozen state makes electron 
microscopy more efficient with higher resolution images to be captured. The freezed 
specimen becomes less sensitive to the electron beam and offers the analyst the 
option to fracture and get insight on its microstructure. Often the specimen is coated 
with gold or carbon by sputtering to prevent charge aggregation on the specimen 
10 
 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
surface causing difficulties at viewing. At the same time, the coating serves as a source 
of electron signals that can further increase the resolution of the SEM image (JEOL, 
2011; Quo15) 
Cryo-SEM is of markedly importance as it represents a reliable method to examine the 
structure of high water content specimen without resulting in water loss or 
degradation of structure integrity. It has the ability to analyze structures of “difficult” 
considered samples, such as liquid, that are beam-sensitive and of unstable nature, 
and always forming a 3D image of high resolution (James, 2009; Stokes, 2013). 
Among Cryo-SEM applications, food specimens are getting continuous attention and 
interest, with yeasts, milk, cheese curd, and ice-cream to be already part of the long 
list (Rovira, et al., 2011; EMS, 2015). Textural characteristics of boiled and uncooked 
potato were observed using this cryogenic technique whereas rheological 
experiments paired with Cryo SEM revealed the structural performance of dough 
samples with a gradient wheat concentration. In addition, Cryo SEM assisted on 
examination of differences in mealy and non-mealy nectarines by maintaining their 
structural integrity preventing from water loss during analysis (Gaonkar, 
Characterization of Food-Emerging methods 1995). 
In the present study, Cryo-SEM was chosen as the electron microscopy method to 
analyze the structure of peanut butter. The main focus was given on both the 
characterization peanut butter matrix prior and after fiber addition and the 
observation of differences among fiber containing peanut butters. The ultimate goal 
was to perform a quantitative comparison among peanut butter samples with respect 
to structural changes that could explain differences in the elimination profiles of the 
pathogenic Salmonella and the non-pathogenic Citrobacter species.  
Environmental Scanning Electron Microscopy 
(ESEM) 
The main operation feature advantage compared to the conventional SEM is that high 
vacuum of the order of 10-5 – 10-7 is not mandatory during specimen examination. 
Higher pressures values, comparable to environmental conditions, can be selected in 
the chamber up to 2.7 kPa (20 torr). Also a great range of temperatures can be 
employed with a maximum at 1500o C (Groves and Parker 2013). This differentiation 
allows moisture containing products, biological products and samples that are prone 
to outgassing to be analyzed (James 2009). Thus, ESEM allows the specimen to be 
examined at hydrated, ”wet”, state. Special adjustments in the hardware system 
ensure that the resolution of the acquired picture of high quality. Among them, use of 
apertures to create and separate areas of high vacuum and low pressure values, 
whereas integration of scan coils, condensing and demagnfiying lenses retain the 
11 
 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
beam focused onto the surface and avoid significant scattering of the primary beam 
(Groves and Parker 2013). In principle, the ESEM performance is similar to the 
conventional SEM with the sole distinctive difference to be the presence of an imaging 
gas in the chamber. The role of the imaging gas is multifold. Primarily, it serves as a 
means of mitigation of the negative charge build up. Biological samples such as foods 
tend to accumulate negative charge during exposure to electrons as they act as 
insulators which in terms of SEM have a characteristic artifact when viewing the image 
(James 2009). In the case of food products structure characterization in ESEM, the 
water vapors are more often used as the imaging gas because they serve both as a 
good signal amplifier and negative charge neutralizer and provide with 
physicochemical stability all along the electron scanning. For example, chocolate 
structure was observed using ESEM using water vapors at a pressure of 100 Pa (Groves 
and Parker 2013)  
ESEM technique was selected to analyze the microstructure of the fibers used to 
construct different matrices when added into peanut butter. Powdered inulin and 
fructoologisacharride and viscous galactooligosaccharide were analyzed on the 
grounds of their structural characteristics. The aim was to visualized their matrix and 
evaluate their effect on the intrinsic structure of peanut butter.

Peanut butter is a very famous food product worldwide and people often include it in

peanuts that can be consumed in many ways. 
A flow-diagram peanut butter production is presented in order to get an overview of 
the relevant processing steps (Figure 1). According to the Entrepreneurship 
Development Institute of India, production of peanut butter begins with the 
reception, sorting and opening of the nuts (Ediindia.org). Quality assessment plays an 
important role in this step. Next, grading of the peanuts occurs before roasting and 
blanching. Roasting is done at 160o C for 40 - 60 minutes depending on the moisture 
content of the peanut batch (United States Envrionmental Protection Agency 1995). 
Roasting ensures water content to drop to 1% of total sample weight, increasing shelf-

the outer skin of the roasted peanuts prior inspection for discolored (grey/black) nuts.

grinding all other ingredients such as salt, sugar and stabilizers are added and mixed 
together. Upcoming de-aeration assists removal of the air incorporated in the paste 
during milling. Before filling as temperature of the paste is as high as 65 - 75o C 
(Ediindia.org), cooling of the paste at 30 - 45o C takes place (SPX 2012). Peanut butter 
is filled into jars or metal cans which are vibrated to remove any remaining air bubble. 
Afterwards, jars or cans are left at 20o C for 35 - 40 h to let peanut butter to set 
completely before been dispatched and distributed to the retailers (Ediindia.org). 
Other manufacturers suggest the implementation of peanut butter setting at 10o C for 
48 h under continuous exposure to inert gas to prevent from oxygen triggered 
degradation (SPX 2012). Similar manufacturing process is recommended by the 
Environmental Protection Agency of United states when describing the processing 
protocol of peanut butter (United States Envrionmental Protection Agency 1995). It is 
frequent that peanut butter is given a pasteurization (at 70 - 75o C) after filling to 
ensure safety of the product and complete elimination of microbial hazards (Lake, et 
al. 2010).

indicates that fat makes up the50 % of the total weight of the product. Protein, is the 
second most abundant component comprising approximately the 22% of the total 
product weight, followed by carbohydrates with 19%, of 7% is the dietary fiber fraction 
(Foodcomp 2009).

Dietary fibers:  An overview of the types, the 
properties and applications in food industry 
Definition and Sources 
Dietary fibers are not a nutrient but are part of the nutrition and nowadays, health 
authorities and nutritionists denote the need for their higher uptake from food (EFSA 
2010). In 1953, Hipsley defined dietary fibers as the sum of the cellulose, hemicellulose 
and lignin from the diet (Hipsley 1953). This definition could not describe all the 
different types of fiber and Trowell in 1974 suggested that “dietary fibers are the 
remnants of the plant cell that resist to hydrolysis (digestion) by the alimentary 
enzymes of man” (Trowell 1974). Lately, both the American Association of Cereal 
Chemists (AACC) (2011) and European Food Council (EUFIC–2005) recommended 
almost identical official definitions of dietary fibers. According to them, dietary fibers 
are the edible parts of the plant and associate carbohydrates that are resistant to 
digestion in the human small intestine with complete or partial fermentation in the 
large intestine. Dietary fibers include polysaccharides, oligosaccharides, lignin, and 
associated plant substances that promote beneficial physiological aspects, (Bodner 
and Sieg 2009), (Rodriguez, et al. 2006).It is important to distinguish the terms dietary 
and crude fibers since they refer to different plant origin substances. Crude fiber is the 
residue of food treated with strong acids and alkali solutions to simulate the digestion 
process. Crude fiber is listed on the compositional labels as “fiber” and underestimates 
the actual content of dietary fibers in food. The ratio of crude to dietary fiber is 1:2-3 
or in other words, by measuring the amount of crude fiber, the dietary fiber content 
is underestimated by 50% or more (Kasapis, Norton and Ubbink 2009). Dietary fibers 
are found in fruits, vegetables, wholegrains, and nuts. Peas, strawberries, 
blackberries, Brussel sprouts, onion, garlic, corn and oat bran cereals are just some of 
the long list of fiber containing food products. The amount and composition of fibers 
differ among food products (Dhingra, Michael and Rajput 2012).  
Categorization 
Classification of dietary fiber is not limited to one system (Tungland and Meyer 2002). 
However, none of them consists of the ideal system to follow as the boundaries cannot 
be accurately identified. Tungland and Meyer (2002) demonstrated that fibers could 
be categorized depending on role of the fiber in the plant, the type of the fiber, the 
solubility in water or other solutions and its intrinsic physiology. However, the most 
common and universal way to characterize dietary fibers is based on their water 
solubility. Thus, dietary fibers can be grouped into soluble and insoluble to water. 
Insoluble fibers are the ones composed primarily of cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin 
(such as oat fiber). The soluble ones, are the fibers that include substantial portions of 
15 
 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
gums, pectins, polyfructoses and mucilages (e.g. fruits) (Bodner and Sieg 2009). It is 
noteworthy that there is also the group of colloidal fibers. Fibers of this category, such 
as β-glucan, form gels with water resembling to soluble like performance (Slavin and 
Jacobs 2010). The gel that is formed is a stable thixotropic colloidal suspension and is 
a result of the fined milling of insoluble fibers under wet conditions (Bodner and Sieg 
2009).  Lately, it is proposed that dietary fibers can further be categorized depending 
on the processing load required to get them extracted from their starting substrate. 
Native is the type of dietary fiber that demands no or low processing to be extracted 
from the source of origin, whereas refined requires relatively more handling of the 
plant source. For instance, powdered cellulose is an insoluble refined fiber (Bodner 
and Sieg 2009), whereas inulin (Coussement 1999) and xanthan are native soluble 
(Hussein, et al. 2008). 
Applications in food microstructure 
Dietary fibers can be either found naturally in foods as structural element of one of 
many ingredients, mainly plant derivatives, or can be added into the food matrix 
during processing (Slavin and Jacobs 2010).  
More specifically, dietary fibers are the non-digestable carbohydrate fraction that 
belongs to the three dimensional structure of the plant cell. Therefore, they are 
integral components of the plant matrix and inter-connected with other 
macronutrients and macro-molecules such as protein complexes and digestible 
carbohydrates. On the other hand, functional fibers are the isolated form of dietary 
fibers extracted by their natural sources by physical, mechanical, enzymatic and 
chemical means. This group also includes synthetically manufactured or naturally 
occurring isolated oligosaccharides and polysaccharides or oligosaccharides modified 
after extraction of their initial plant source. Oligosaccharides (isolated, manufactured 
or synthetic) subjected to two or more degrees of polymerization belong also in the 
functional fiber group (Medicine 2002). 
Unveiling the possibilities of use of fibers in food processing has triggered food 
scientists to exploit the functionality of those agents in order to generate food 
products of even higher quality and appeal to consumers. Among fiber’s physico-
chemical properties particle size and bulk volume and porosity together with the 
hydration properties are the most significant and, therefore to dictate their field of 
application (Dhingra, et al., 2012; Medicine, 2002; Yangilar, 2013; Redgwell, et al., 
2005; Rodriguez, et al., 2006; Bodner, et al., 2009). 
From an industrial perspective, addition of fiber can improve the technical 
characteristics of the intended food product in many ways. Textural enhancement is 
one technical improvement, contributing to the rheological properties of viscous 
semi-solids and liquids are another aspect without disregarding the increased in shelf-
16 
 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
life associated with the longer duration of their structure integrity (Redgwell and 
Fischer 2005). In overall all sensorial characteristics can be modified in favor of a better 
end product with improved water-binding capacities, anti-caking properties and gel-
forming ability (Brenntag Food & Nutrition Europe). Fat substitutes can be formed, 
and new anti-sticking agents, anti-clumping and thickening agents are now at food 
processor’s disposal (Dhingra, Michael and Rajput 2012) . The great attention given at 
dietary fibers to be used as novel functional constituents is also justified from a 
financial perspective as well (Yangilar, 2013; Rodriguez, et al., 2006). 
Before digging deeper into the applications of dietary fibers in the food industry it is 
significant to have an overview of which fibers are provisionally considered as agents 
with promising functionality. Both polysaccharides and oligosaccharides found 
applications due to their physical properties together with insoluble lignin and plant 
associated substances. There are numerous examples of foods that have been mixed 
with fiber towards the improvement of specific features. In dairies, inulin, pectins and 
guar gum are added to decrease the fat content in cheese, to prevent from syneresis 
in yogurt (Blecker, et al. 2001) and to stabilize the ice-cream emulsion (Rodriguez, et 
al. 2006). In bakeries and pastries fiber fortification is associated with reduction of the 
caloric value whereas in confectionaries as sugar substitutes (Knuckles, et al. 1997). 
Beverages are mixed with fibers for viscosity related applications (Bolinger 2001). 
Fibers find applications also in meat industry, either as fat substitutes (in pork and 
sausages) (Waszkowiak and Szymandera-Buszka 2008) or as structural agents to 
improve water binding and texture appraisal (ground beef) (Bodner and Sieg 2009). 
Deriving from dietary fibers, functional fibers are utilized in food processing to 
improve textural properties, surface hydration and structure formation. The group of 
fibers assigned to enhance these features is known as hydrocolloids (Bodner and Sieg 
2009). Hydrocolloids are the fibrous components that influence the properties of 
water. In particular, hydrocolloids swell to produce a viscous solution or dispersion 
when exposed to water. This category includes gelatin, albumens and globulins, 
together with polysaccharide-based substances such galactomannans, glucomannans 
polymers, 
pectianceous 
materials, 
seaweed-based 
extracts 
and 
other 
macromolecules like cellulosics, glucans and starches (Redgwell and Fischer 2005). 
The advantage of hydrocolloids against the other functional fibers is that they interact 
and form bonds with the surround matrix participating actively in the structure of the 
added product. Thus, buy altering, through interactions with other macromolecules, 
they create a spatial arrangement (Redgwell and Fischer 2005). One application of 
hydrocolloids is the dairy industry in which low amounts of carrageenan are added to 
stabilize a number of dairy products and prevent from whey separation (Redgwell and 
Fischer 2005). In more complex systems such as biphasic foams, emulsions xanthan 
and gum-soybean are tested as interfacial stabilizers (Redgwell and Fischer 2005). 
Confectionaries, dairies, ice-cream and coffee creams could also be subjected to

protein-polysaccharide addition towards a higher shear resistance of the emulsion 
during processing (Redgwell and Fischer 2005). 
In the current study inulin, fructooligosaccharides and galactosaccharides dietary 
fibers were added into the peanut butter to disrupt the existing default structure and 
form a new different structure. Inulin is a polysaccharide naturally occurring in plants 
and most frequently extracted from chicory roots (Flamm, et al. 2001). Inulin is a 
polydisperse β-(2,1)-linked fructan with a glucose molecule at the end of each fructose 
chain (Figure 3). The chain has a length of two to 60 units, with an average degree of 
polymerization of ten. Processing of inulin comprises washing with hot water, purified 
and ultimately dried (Niness 1999). Fructooligosaccharides (FOS) are fructans 
naturally occurring to plants like onions, artichokes, asparagus, chicory root etc. 
Production of FOS is done by two different processes.  Either by hydrolysis of inulin or 
from sucrose using a food grade fungal fructosyltransferase (Bornet, et al. 2002). The 
major difference of FOS compared to inulin is their shorter chain length consisting of 
2-10 fructose units and their polymerization degree which is not higher than 4 (Niness 
1999). Galactooligosaccharides are non-digestible carbohydrates of plant origin which 
are composed by galactose units and obtained from lactose by the action of β-
galactosidases (Velisek 2014). They have now been established as prebiotic 
ingredients after in vitro and in vivo human studies (Torres, et al. 2010).

Foodborne bacteria:  
Salmonella 
Identity of the pathogen 
Salmonella is a Gram negative, rod shaped, motile (equipped with flagella) and 
facultative anaerobic bacterium without the spore forming ability (Hammack 2012). It 
belongs to the Enterobacteriaceae family and the species associated with human 
illness are the S. enterica and S. bongori. The former is considered of major health 
burden and composes a group of several subspecies, each of them consisting of 
various serovars (CDC 2011). Due to the great existing number of Salmonella serovars, 
more than 2,500, one more distinctive identification criteria was incorporated; the 
phage type. Phage type refers to serovar’s resistance against bacteriophages (Ireland 
2011). The phage types are numerous and it is reported that until 2005 170 phages 
names were enlisted (Walker 2007). This present report made use of 
S.TyphimuriumU311, serovar type of enterica subspecies belonging to S. enterica 
species with U311 standing for its phage type. 
Growth and survival conditions 
Salmonella species possess some main intrinsic characteristics which often slightly 
vary depending on the individual subspecies and serovars. In general, Salmonella 
strains easily adapt to the environmental conditions and can survive extreme 
conditions (FAO/WHO 2002). High temperatures and low as refrigerator values are 
often not considered as burden for growth of the bacteria and implications associated 
with frozen and heat processed food products are frequent (L. R. Beuchat 2009). It has 
been observed that Salmonella can grow at elevated temperatures, such as above 
50OC (D Aoust, Maurer and Bailey 2007), or exhibit psychotropic characteristics and 
thrive under refrigerating environment (Ireland 2011). Nowadays, it is a common 
belief that heat treatment of the bacterium at moderate or elevated temperatures 
can induce a change in its cell membrane, triggering a heat pre-shock, rendering it 
more tolerant to high temperatures (R. Podolak, et al. 2010). Improved heat resistance 
might also be exhibited if cells are kept at refrigeration or freezing temperatures 
(Carcia, 2000; Montville, et al., 2008). 
Water activity is a factor that determines the microbiological survival and growth in 
foodstuffs. Water activity values lower than 0.93 are sufficient to inhibit growth of 
Salmonella (Ireland 2011). However, due to the complexity of the food matrix of 
various products, the combination of food composition, solutes concentration, 
storage conditions, pre-treatment of raw ingredients, pH, etc can support survival or 
19 
 
==PAGE:34==
 
 
growth of the bacterium. The ability of Salmonella to adapt to various conditions is 
also justified by the wide range of pH values under which it can survive. pH values 
between 4.5 - 9.5 can support sustained growth and survival of the bacterium with 
optimum conditions around the area of 6.5 and 7.5 (D Aoust, Maurer and Bailey 2007). 
Exposure of the bacterium to gradiently lowering of pH during handling, processing, 
etc. may trigger an acidic pre-shock, resulting in a structural change of the cell 
membrane. The bacterium produces acid shock proteins incorporated in the outer 
surface of the membrane, reducing its fluidity and markedly improving the tolerance 
to the acidified surrounding environment (D Aoust, Maurer and Bailey 2007). This 
phenomenon is known as Acid Tolerance Response (ATR). ATR induced cells are 
subjected to changes in their membrane structure and composition rendering the cell 
able to perform also increased heat resistance (Bacon, et al. 2003). 
Salinity levels up to 2% can be favorable for proliferation of Salmonella, but due to 
combined interaction with additives, solutes and raw ingredients, the bacterial 
tolerance to NaCl could be affected towards both higher and lower levels (D Aoust, 
Maurer and Bailey 2007). Investigations of foodborne outbreaks revealed that several 
cases were associated with modified atmosphere products. Although combination of 
CO2, N2 and reduced O2 levels inside the packaging material inhibit growth of 
Salmonella, the shift to anaerobic metabolic pathways shall not be disregarded. 
(Michaelsen, et al., 2006; D Aoust, et al., 2007). 
Salmonella is a bacterium with the ability to adapt to hostile environments (Doyle and 
Buchaman 2013). Thus, stringent processing is required to sufficiently eliminate this 
hazard and supply the market with products safe for consumption. Normally 
Salmonella is eliminated by employing thermal treatment such as pasteurization. 
However, the situation is more demanding in the case of low water activity products. 
A combination of Salmonella’s inherent property to withstand elevated temperature 
with the composition of the particular products renders it to perform better in terms 
of heat survival (R. Podolak, E. Enache and W. Stone, et al. 2010).  Food products 
belonging to this problematic situation are the dried milk, infant powder, snacks, 
roasted nuts and toasted oats (R. Podolak, E. Enache and W. Stone, et al. 2010). 
Especially when the composition is made up of high fat, Salmonella can perform better 
against the applied heating like pasteurization (Shachar and Yaron 2006). It is believed 
that lipids serve as a protective layer resulting in viability at low concentrations (Chen 
et al 2009). Confectionaries and peanut butter are low water activity products that do 
not support growth of Salmonella, nevertheless these products have been tracked as 
the source of Salmonella outbreaks (Park, Oh and Kang 2008). Silva and Gibbs (2012) 
outlined the influence of the low water activity effect on heat resistance of the 
Salmonella. They reported that 2 min of heating in oil at 126.7o C needed to obtain a 
5-log CFU/g reduction of Salmonella in almonds. In chocolate, 10-fold decrease in cells 
numbers was observed after 20 h at 71o C, but addition of 2% of water reduced the 
20 
 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
time to 4 h. In the case of dark chocolate, the same drop in cell numbers was achieved 
with a 26 min heating at 90o C. Finally, in roasted cocoa, viable Salmonella cells were 
detected after 5 min exposure at 130o C (Silva and Gibbs 2012).  
Reservoirs 
Soil, stagnant waters and sewage water are some of the environmental sources 
Salmonella (Hammack 2012). In mammalian hosts, the bacterium might be found in 
the microflora of the intestinal tract. Reservoirs can be both animals and humans. 
Animals in herds, livestock, domestic and pets are all considered as equal reservoirs 
of this pathogen (Vermont u.d.). It is clear that Salmonella is a bacterium ubiquitous 
in the environment and special precautions should be taken during food production. 
The transmission is mainly the oral-fecal route unless there is direct contact with a 
prior contaminated surface. Therefore, animal origin transmission can occur after 
consumption of foodstuffs containing sustained levels of Salmonella. “In house” 
contamination raises high concerns, via cross-contamination between contaminated 
raw materials or by handlers and employees using utensils that are infected and poorly 
sanitized. The ability of the bacterium to collide to clean surfaces consists of a 
continuous risk of cross-contamination of batches that initially were Salmonella free. 
For instance, S. Enteritidis was able to remain viable up to four days on dry stainless 
steel surface with transfer to food rates varying from 20 - 100 %. (Kusumaningrum, et 
al. 2003) Other vector-borne agents of Salmonella cross-contamination include pests, 
rodents, dust in air as well as operators and floors on the premises (Ireland, 2011; 
BIOHAZ, 2013; Montville, et al., 2008). 
Foodborne outbreaks 
Consumption of food contaminated with Salmonella may trigger a foodborne 
implication known as salmonellosis. There are two types of the disease based on the 
serovar involved and it can vary from high severity to debilitating symptoms. The 
virulence of the specific strain determines mainly the severity of the symptoms; these 
can either be non-typhoid symptoms or onset of enteric fever could be expected, 
known as typhoid fever. Both salmonellosis types are associated with S. Typhimuirum 
and S. Paratyphi A, both recognized as the Salmonella strains able to infect humans 
(Anonymous, 2013; Hammack, 2012; Montville, et al., 2008). 
 
The non-typhoid disease is the most frequent foodborne disease triggered by 
Salmonella infection (Alvarez-Ordonez, et al. 2011). It is a mild infection with 
symptoms to include nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, headaches and diarrhea. It 
resembles gastroenteritis with 60-80% of all cases are not recognized and when they 
are tracked often they stand as sporadic events (WHO 2013). Symptoms may onset 
after 6 to 72 h after consumption and can last from 4 to 7 days the max, with acute 
symptoms for 1 day or longer. The symptoms are debilitating, hence self-limited. 
21 
 
==PAGE:36==
 
 
However, future implications may occur with arthritis, septicemia and bacteremia 
following a non-typhoid salmonellosis (Authority of Ireland 2013) (Montville, 
Matthews and Kniel 2008). This may happen if cells manage to colonize other organs 
after passing through the gastrointestinal track and remained viable while entering 
the bloodstream. Food safety authorities and Health organizations set an infectious 
dose as low as 1 cell highlighting that it is the state of the host’s health that determines 
the symptoms onset and duration, (Hammack 2012). 
 
Incidences of Salmonella contamination in foods are numerous and more and more 
foodstuffs are being linked to foodborne outbreaks. At first, it was believed that 
mainly meat, poultry products and eggs were the incriminating sources in outbreaks. 
Studies pointed out a list of products able to support growth or survival of the 
pathogen (Podolak, Enache and Stone 2009). The list is extensively long and is 
continuously updated with new-entries (Danyluk, et al. 2012). Numerous meat 
products are placed top on the list and product groups that were considered as 
Salmonella free have recently reached high rankings in databases of salmonellosis 
associated products, (Gutierrez 2011), (Shachar and Yaron 2006). For instance, 
aquaculture products such as shrimps, mollusk, bivalves and other seafood must be 
investigated for sustained Salmonella contamination before been released on the 
market (Amagliani, Brandi and Schiavano 2012), (Feldhusen 2000). Lately, products of 
plant origin, like tomatoes, nuts and peanuts, fruits and vegetables are getting 
attention in food industry as being the primary medium of transmission of the 
bacterium, introducing it in the processing premises. The latter is due to the globalized 
profile of trade market with products being imported from across the globe, including 
places where sanitization and hygiene protocols are not adequately implemented. 
Furthermore, improper handling of sewage water or utilization of contaminated 
irrigation water contributes to the increasing occurrence of the pathogen in the food 
chain. It was not until recently, when health authorities (WHO) and food safety bodies 
(EFSA, CDC) recognized the necessity to revise risk assessment regarding Salmonella 
prevalence in low moisture foods even if the water activity is by far lower than the 
one required for growth. The reason is that the composite structure of low moisture 
products may render microbial cells protected towards processing (R. Podolak, E. 
Enache and W. Stone, et al. 2010). Low moisture products are increasingly linked to 
Salmonella outbreaks such as salads dressings, spices, dairy products and high oily 
content products like coconut, peanut butter, chocolates and other confectionary 
products. (EFSA; Hammack, 2012; BIOHAZ, 2013; Montville, et al., 2008).

The list of outbreaks shows that prevalence of Salmonella species in low water activity 
foods is gradually emerging. Products such as confectionaries, pastes and dried 
formulas that were considered as Salmonella irrelevant products, they actually 
represent a possible source of foodborne pathogenesis. The increasing occurrence of 
salmonellosis outbreaks in low water activity products could be traced to changes in 
the processing of food products and/or the globalization of food market (European 
Food Safety Authority 2013). As EFSA reported in 2013, introduction of new 
technologies in manufacturing and novel ways of preparation and handling or raw 
materials could be responsible for salmonellosis rise. A physiology change of 
Salmonella, induced by the new processing practices could lead to increased resistant 
to established inactivation steps and thereby presence in the end-product (European 
Food Safety Authority 2013). Another reason could be the wide product market 
allowing foods to be transferred from one place to another. In other words, raw 
materials are transported around the world to cover the needs of the food industry. 
Thus, raw materials deriving from countries with poor hygiene protocols are possible 
to be introduced to food plants resulting in “in-house” contamination and/or products 
with sustained microbial load (European Food Safety Authority 2013). 
Occurrence in low water activity products 
There is no clear definition of low water activity products by food authorities, however 
this category is well-established in food industry in terms of processing and 
microbiological aspects. Well-known food products falling into this category are 
chocolate and confectionaries, peanuts and peanut butter, powdered milk, infant 
powders, toasted cereals and bakery products, etc. Their main a low water content as 
well as a low water activity (Chen et al 2009). Following a microbiology approach, the 
reduced water availability, the dehydrated environment and the presence of oil and 
fat are considered as no favorable conditions for bacteria to grow. Coupled with the 
low water activity levels, then these products should be considered as matrices with 
multiple barriers for both microbial growth and survival. Surprisingly, low aw food 
products are increasingly implicated with foodborne outbreaks worldwide. (Gruzdev, 
Pinto and Sela 2011). Investigations on these foodborne outbreaks highlighted that 
although Salmonella cannot thrive in the low water activity products, it is possible to 
achieve sustained survival due to the surrounding environment and/or adopt a greater 
resistance, especially during heating, triggered by the exposure to non-favorable to 
growth conditions (Silva and Gibbs 2012).  
Bell and Kyriakides (2002) outlined that Salmonella is expected to be present in all 
foods since the bacteria is wide-spread in nature. Studies in dry foods and feeds 
showed that Salmonella may survive for long periods. This observation was confirmed 
by Hiramatsu (2005) who found that the survival of the bacteria can be increased up 
to 79 times if sucrose is present in the desiccated product (experiments carried out on 
dried squids). Shachar and Yaron (2006) reported that the synergistic effect of high fat 
24 
 
==PAGE:39==
 
 
and low aw might contribute to the pronounced survival of Salmonella cells in 
chocolate, dried pepper and peanut butter. Especially for peanut butter it is suggested 
that viable cells can aggregate or clump near or within the water phase of the colloidal 
suspension of the water-in-lipid emulsion. In chocolate, with water content below 8%, 
it is reported that only few cells, 1 – 3  cells/g, can initiate foodborne salmonellosis 
(Chen et al 2009). Komitopoulou and Penaloza (2009) also highlighted the effect of 
fat-aw combination on the increased Salmonella heat resistance. They reported that 
in Canada salmonellosis outbreaks was traced back to chocolate products 
contaminated with an average 0.005 CFU/g of Salmonella. This is closely linked to the 
cell distribution across the matrix suggesting that although the total amount of cells is 
low, they are aggregated in areas with high concentration. It is also considered that 
the fatty layers might serve as protection against the gastro-intestinal acid in the 
stomach and allows the bacterium to colonize the lower parts of the gastrointestinal 
tract and trigger the disease (Chen et al 2009). Honey is another widely consumed 
product whose water content is markedly low but still salmonellosis outbreaks are 
associated with its consumption (Chen et al 2009). Similar performance is expected in 
halva, a tahini based product, in which Salmonella can survive for long periods (Chen 
et al 2009).  
Citrobacter: An overview of the bacteria 
Identity of the bacteria 
Citrobacter is a Gram-negative, no spore forming and rod-shaped bacterium. It is 
facultative anaerobic and motile by means of flagella and can solely use citrate, a 
derivative of citric acid, as carbon source (Dworkin, et al. 2006). From a taxonomical 
perspective, Citrobacter species belong to the family of Entereobacteriaceae sharing 
all general properties and biochemical characteristics and be closely related to 
Salmonella and Escherichia coli bacteria (Long, Pickering and Prober 2012).  It is a 
hospital related bacteria, nosocomial, but due to its prevalence in the environment it 
might also be considered as potential spoilage bacteria (Varnam and Sutherland 1994) 
, (USFDA 2013). The Citrobacter genus comprises a wide number of bacteria that were 
isolated and classified into groups based on their genomic relatedness, including the 
well-known C. freundii, C. koseri, C. amalonaticus and C. farmeri (Meruvu and 
Donthireddy 2013).  Citrobacter genus was redevised in 1993 and new strain groups 
derived. The new classification gave rise to the following C. youngae, C. braakii, C. 
werkmanii, C. sedlakii, C. rodentium, C. giilenii and C. murlinae species that were prior 
incorporated to the Citrobacter freundii group (Knirel, et al. 2002). Further 
categorization of Citrobacter species is achieved using the O-antigen resulting in a 
number of 43 O-serogroups (Katzenellenbogen, et al. 2008). Another classification of 
Citrobacter species employs the chemical characteristics of the lipo-polysaccharide 
25 
 
==PAGE:40==
 
 
chain of the strains (LPS). Based on the sugar composition of the LPS, 20 chemotypes 
can be identified, 11 of which are identical in Salmonella and Escherichia species 
(Knirel, et al. 2002). 
Growth conditions  
There is little information available on the growth conditions of Citrobacter species in 
the scientific literature. However, it is cited that there is a great resemblance of 
Citrobacter with Salmonella (Ewing and Davis 1972). Despite the little information 
there are some studies which can assist on giving insight on growth conditions of 
Citrobacter species in foods. In the study of Abu-Ghazaleh (2005), it was reported that 
Citrobacter spp can grow at as low as 7o C with optimum conditions at moderate 25 - 
40o C temperatures with optimum at 37o C, as confirmed by Seniya et al. (2012). 
Salinity levels of 3% were responsible for approximately 37 % growth reduction, with 
higher levels such as 5 % markedly inactivating C. freundii. When experiments were 
carried out at 5o C the preservative effect increased significantly, showing the 
dependence of the growth on temperature (Abu-Ghazaleh 2006). 
Moreover, a group of Chinese researchers reported in 2009 that the optimum pH 
growth range for C. freundii was observed within the range of 3.5 and 9.5 (Li, et al. 
2009). Similar results were reported by Seniya et al (2012) with pH 5 standing for the 
optimum value (Seniya, et al. 2012). 
Reservoirs 
Citrobacter is widespread in the environment. It can be present in the terrestrial areas 
like forests as well as is in marine environments such as marine sediments (Meruvu 
and Donthireddy 2013). Citrobacter is prevalent in soil, sewage water, fresh water, 
food and food waste etc (UsFDA 2013). Citrobacter belongs also to the commensal 
community of the intestinal microflora of both human and other mammals (cats, 
cows, dogs, and horses) as well as birds, reptiles and amphibians (Buller 2014). Thus it 
can be found in the gastrointestinal tract, fecal matter and urine. Prevalence in food 
such as fresh vegetables also occurs. Other associated food products include dairy 
products and raw shellfish. The bacterium can be transferred to the raw material 
through the contaminated soil used for crops production or the seawater of the area 
chosen for shellfish harvesting (Lawley, Curtis and Davis 2012). Meat and poultry has 
also been identified as contamination route of Citrobacter spp which is often a result 
of temperature abuse (Lawley, Curtis and Davis 2012). 
Infection and food borne poisoning 
Citrobacter is abundant in the environment and its prevalence comprises a potential 
burden for the public health. In general, Citrobacter is an opportunistic pathogen but 
26 
 
==PAGE:41==
 
 
once introduced to the bloodstream the onset of the associate symptoms must be 
expected (Marth and Steele 2001). The mode of transmission can be both fecal-oral 
route and by person to person contact (Canada 2011). The disease induced by 
Citrobacter is also characterized as nosocomial due to the fact that many patients may 
get infected by the bacteria during hospitalization (Canada 2011) (Doran 1999). 
Concerning human infection by Citrobacter spp. the main focus is given to C. freundii, 
C. diversus and C. braaki (Samonis, et al. 1993).  
The disease is associated with the production of an enterotoxin that triggers 
gastroenteritis alike symptoms. The symptoms of include flu-like symptoms, nausea, 
stomach cramps, vomiting, watery diarrhea and infection of the urinary and intestinal 
tract (UsFDA 2013). Symptoms are exhibited 12-24 h after ingestion of a contaminated 
source but are self-limited after several up to 7 days. However, there are often cases 
of severe infection (Riemann and Cliver 2006). The infectious dose is not well 
established, however, in literature an infectious dose at 107 CFU/ml is cited (Canada 
2011). More specifically, LD50 is possible to vary within the contaminated sources 
ranging from 103 cells in normal carp to 108 CFU/ml (administered by intraperitoneal 
injection) concerning the grass carp (Buller 2014). 
Foodborne disease from Citrobacter cannot be disregarded from a food safety 
assessment due to its association to foodborne outbreaks. To name a few, in 1995 
many children were diagnosed with the associate disease after consumption of green 
butter containing parsley contaminated with Citrobacter. (Dixon 1995). Other 
foodstuff associated with Citrobacter induced symptoms are seafood, dairy products 
and fresh raw vegetables. Meat and meat products are also included in the list, with 
shawarma meat to be responsible for a food poisoning incidence in 2011 in Egypt and 
among bacteria, Citrobacter was also identified (Al-Mutairi 2011).  
Although Citrobacter  has been linked to foodborne outbreaks, most frequently it is 
associated with food spoilage. In general, Citrobacter species, as part of the broad 
Entereobacteriaceae group, serve as food safety indicators since compliance to 
hygiene protocols can ensure elimination of the hazard (Blackburn 2006). Their 
occurrence in foods is associated with improper processing, inadequate hygiene and 
indicate post-treatment contamination. Blacburn (2006) highlighted the role of 
Entereobacteriaceae in food spoilage.  He stated that Citrobacter performs as the 
other Gram-negative bacteria. Some species can ferment lactose to lactic acid 
followed by gas production and metabolic by-products resulting in off-flavoring, 
discoloration and general impairment of the technical features of the product. 
Contamination of fish with Citrobacter triggers an increase in the histamine levels 
present in the gut, gills and muscles as a result of histidine decarboxylation. In the case 
of coleslaw, Citrobacter and Entereobacteriaceae spp occurrence has been connected 
to off-flavoring with the formation of a strong fecal smell closely linked to their

metabolic activity. Long et al (2012) identified that flavor impairment in foods could 
also be a result of H2S production, a derivative of C. freundii metabolism.

Thermal inactivation and bacterial heat 
tolerance 
Food industry aims at producing foods of high quality, high nutritional value that are 
appealing to the consumers. However, the most important aspect that governs the 
food production is generating products safe for consumption, i.e. minimizing the risk 
of foodborne diseases. To achieve inactivation of microorganisms in food, numerous 
processing methods can be applied, including but not limited to: High pressure 
processing, thermal processing, pulsed electric field, pH control, ohmic heating, radio 
frequency, microwave and UV light (Sun 2012). 
Among all, thermal inactivation is the most frequent method used in food industry to 
inactivate microbial hazards and spoilage organisms (Rojas, et al. 2013). Common 
types of heat treatments are pasteurization and sterilization (or canning) (Awuah, 
Ramaswamy and Economides 2007). The former is a relatively mild heat treatment at 
temperatures below 100o C whereas the later consists of heating at temperatures 
within the range of 110-121o C depending on the type of product (Heinz and 
Hautzinger 2007). Pasteurization process encompasses different variations depending 
on the temperature-time combination. For example, vat pasteurization stands for 
heating at 63o C for 30 min, HTST refers to 72 – 75o C for 15 – 20 s and UHT indicates 
heating at 135 – 140o C for few seconds (Bylund 1995).  
The effect of thermal processing on the viability of microbial populations can be 
quantified. The specific elimination profile differs among microorganisms and often 
depends on the product matrix (Bellara, et al. 1999). Typically, the inactivation curve, 
also called the survivor curve, is semi-logarithmic, meaning that a logarithmic 
transformation of the number of surviving microorganisms, as a function of time is 
linear (Kango 2010).

thermal treatment. The most expected shape is the log-linear curve. This log-linear 
curve is associated with first order kinetics of microbial decay following the equation

Weibull fitting has been used before to describe bacterial heat inactivation in this 
particular substrate (Couvert, et al. 2005).

The Weibull model is suitable for tail occurring survivor curves due to the use of α and 
β parameters. However, it is hard to establish them as physical entities in thermal 
foods processing. In practice, the β parameter has been suggested to be more 
important than α in food microbiology as it is associated with the survival of the cells 
when exposed to heating (Boekel 2002). On the other hand, the alpha parameter is a 
metric unit, also called scale parameter, which is linked to the characteristics of the 
plotted survivor curve (Couvert, et al. 2005). Regarding the β parameter, when β is 
equal to 1, the Weibull equation transforms to a first order kinetic. When β < 1, it is 
assumed that the remaining viable cells are less susceptible to further heating, hence 
creating a “tail” on the survivor curve, whereas the opposite effect is expected when 
β > 1 and a “shoulder” is created (Boekel 2002). With respect to α parameter, when it 
gets high values, the curve is stretched out to the right without affecting the shape of 
the curve (Couvert, et al. 2005), see Figure 5.

Water activity-Water content: A review of 
their role in the food products 
Water is the most frequent element in many foods. In food processing, the impact of 
water on both technical characteristics and preservation is under constant 
examination (Belitz, Grosh og Shieberle 2009). Therefore, it is essential to acquire 
information on the amount of water present in food (Maltini, et al. 2003). Scientists 
nowadays water in two terms, the moisture content and the water activity.  
In brief, moisture content refers to the total amount of water present in the food 
(Decagon Devices 2009). On the other hand, water activity (aw) refers to the “free” 
water within the food system that is available for the biochemical reactions (L. 
Beuchat 1987). It represents a thermodynamic property of the food indicating the 
energy status of the dispersed water to participate microbial related reactions. The 
two physical properties are measured using different techniques. Water content is 
defined mainly gravimetrically (or differential scanning calorimetry) whereas water 
activity is measured with more sophisticated means (Wrolstad, et al. 2005). 
Manometric and dew point temperature measurements, Nuclear Magnetic 
Resonance (NMR) and hygrometers are employed to provide with accurate 
measurements of water activity (Wrolstad, et al. 2005), (Rotronic Solutions u.d.) 
By principle, water activity is the ratio of the partial vapor pressure of the water in a 
food (p) with the vapor pressure of the pure water at the same temperature (po); aw = 
p/po. Another method relies on measuring the relative humidity of the air in around a 
food product when the system reaches equilibrium in a sealed chamber (L. Beuchat 
1987). Water activity and water content are two different parameters which are 
closely related. Water content (or total moisture) is divided into bound and free water. 
Bound water is the monophasic layer which is strongly attached to the solid structure 
of the product behaving as a structural element (Gowen 2012). This part of water is 
inert as it does not participate in any physical or chemical reaction. In contrary, free 
water is the fraction that is not bound to the structure and is provisionally active to 
participate in both biochemical and microbiological reactions (Barbosa-Canovas, et al. 
2007). Knowing both the water content and aw levels contributes to the complete 
understanding on the microbial and quality state of foods. 
An overview of the effect of aw on microbial changes in food is given by the food 
stability diagram (Figure 6). This diagram outlines the link between water activity and 
the food related biochemical reactions (M. S. Rahman 2007). Specific values of aw can 
promote or halt different reactions occurring in the food.  
33 
 
==PAGE:48==
 
 
The aw determines both the growth and survival rates of the microorganisms (Troller 
and Christian 1978). As a rule of thumb, the 0.6 – 0.7 water activity range is considered 
as the safety threshold for a food, indicating that no microbial growth occurs 
rendering the product biologically stable (Ayub, Wahab and Durrani 2003).  
The scientific literature reports that pathogenic bacteria normally grow at aw levels 
higher than 0.85 - 0.86 (M. S. Rahman 2007). However, parameters such as 
temperature, product composition, addition of substances and lately microstructure 
could shift the safety threshold both upwards and downwards (Shachar and Yaron 
2006), (McClements 2007). Therefore, risk assessments must be addressed to every 
product individually to ensure the production of well-control biologically stable foods.

Sensory characteristics of peanut butter 
 
Aim 
The organoleptic texture characteristics of peanut butter matrices can signify 
aberrations between samples in the below surface layers and could serve as rapid and 
fast indication of structural modifications. Therefore, a basic overview of the textural 
characteristics of peanut butter matrices proceeded. Both plain (default) and fiber 
containing peanut butters were assessed to highlight differences in the macro-
structure-scale caused by fiber addition. 
Materials and methods 
Experiments 
Evaluation on the organoleptic texture characteristics was carried out on: 
• Peanut butter (default) 
• Peanut butter + 5 g of inulin powder 
• Peanut butter + 10 g of inulin powder 
• Peanut butter + 15 g of inulin powder 
• Peanut butter + FOS powder 
• Peanut butter + GOS viscous syrup 
• Peanut butter + GOS powder 
Preparation of peanut butter samples 
Thirty gram of Green Choice creamy peanut butter was transferred into a glass beaker 
and added 0.5 ml peanut oil, followed by a 2 min manual stirring (1 min stirring per 
direction). Addition of 5 g of inulin,  5 g of FOS or  5 g of GOS viscous syrup to the 
default sample, proceeded with an additional 2 min manual stirring (1 min per 
direction), was performed to obtain the inulin, FOS and GOS samples respectively. 
Following that procedure, the 10 and 15 g inulin peanut butter samples were 
prepared. 5 g of GOS powder added to default sample to obtain the powder GOS 
peanut butter sample.

Sensory evaluation 
The “added-fiber” samples were given scores upon their grade of difference from the 
default performance. At the scoring table, default was set as 0 and all other samples 
were given discrete numbers revealing the level of sensorial change. However, no 
statement of improvement or degradation of quality and technical characteristics 
could be made. The comparison aims mainly at demonstrating the macroscopic 
change from the default properties. The present evaluation consists of a preliminary 
attempt to set the basis about the impact of the fibers on the overall sensory 
attributes of the peanut butter. 
The characteristics subjected to assessment were viscosity, stickiness, brittleness, 
greasiness, and brilliance. The term viscosity was used to demonstrate the resistance 
against manual stirring, stickiness as the difficulty during handling and sampling 
whereas brittleness for the solidification effect. Two more attributes were listed and 
these were the greasiness and brilliance.  
Evaluation of the selected characteristics was performed either during or after 
preparation of the peanut butter samples. Viscosity of the samples was tested during 
the mixing of the fiber with peanut butter (already mixed with 0.5 peanut oil). The 
default sample proceeded first so that the followed-up fiber samples to be compared 
to it. Stickiness was the outcome of the overall easiness of the peanut butter sample 
during preparation and transfer to stomacher bags (Stomacher bags were employed 
to imitate laboratory sampling procedure). Brittleness feature was evaluated during 
stirring the peanut butter. If peanut butter sample appeared brittle, mixing would 
result to crumbles. Brilliance, referred to the shininess of samples surface. All peanut 
butter samples were transferred into disposable cups of the same color and placed 
next to each to perform a simultaneous evaluation of the oil shine. Greasiness 
represented the observed oily film formed on the beakers, cups, and utensils after 
preparation of the samples 
Scores were given for each attribute starting from 0 and ending at 5. 0 stood for no 
observable change on the examined organoleptic property whereas 5 was associated 
with marked aberration from the default characteristics.

To start with, peanut butter is characterized as a viscous fluid, neither liquid nor solid, 
that is mainly used as a spread. Also the “semi-solid” term could be used to describe 
the structure of this particular food. The main attribute of peanut butter is its 
stickiness and the oiled shiny surface (Figure 9) that reflects its composition and 
especially the high fat content that in cases can reach approximately 50% of the total 
product’s weight. Beside its sticky performance, viscosity is another parameter that 
must be taken into consideration on the grounds of manual mixing. During sample 
preparation, fibers were mixed by applying manual stirring to get a homogeneous 
paste. It was decided to follow manual mixing to keep the preparation step simple but 
still reproductive, fast and suitable for handling of a low weight sample. 
The default peanut butter sample was the one with the lowest viscosity and shiniest 
surface amongst all. Mixing was a smooth process resulting in a homogeneous paste, 
whereas the stickiness of the product proved to make both partitioning and transfer 
into stomacher bag, for further sampling, rather demanding. In overall, peanut butter 
behaved as a typical paste. When inulin was mixed into the default sample a grainy 
surface was observed. The inulin powder was well mixed without any difficulties and 
even by naked eye the grains of the powdered fiber could be visualized all over the 
peanut butter mass (Figure 9). It is important to note that viscosity, as measured by 
the resistance against stirring as “sensed” by manually mixing, was slightly increased 
but still comparable with the fiber-free counterpart. The product still remained sticky 
and troublesome for the transfer to stomacher bags for further sampling. FOS had 
almost the similar effect on the textural alteration of default peanut butter (Figure 9). 
It was, though, easily noticeable that FOS could be mixed easier and faster than inulin 
which probably was due to the finer profile of the powder facilitating homogenization. 
The most significant textural change was observed when the GOS syrup was added. 
The viscous liquid GOS resulted in solidification of the default peanut butter, reducing 
its stickiness and shininess and increasing its brittleness (Figure 9). This particular 
peanut butter sample was easy to handle during sampling since it behaved as a solid 
material but exhibited a high viscosity grade. When the syrup type of fiber was mixed 
with the semi-solid peanut butter the product hardened all along the stirring step and 
eventually turned into a solid and compact product. The powdered version of GOS did 
not cause the same degree of hardening but it was evidently harder to mix into the 
default peanut butter compared to the inulin and FOS fibers. Brittleness was not 
observed and stickiness level hit an intermediate between liquid GOS and the other 
fibrous samples (Figure 9). What stroke out during sample preparation was that 
addition of 15 g of the inulin fiber also resulted in hardening of the surface and loss of 
the shiny oily shade (Figure 9). It was clear that further addition would render the 
sample equal to the liquid GOS counterpart.

Water play an important role on the food characteristics and shelf-life respectively. 
The bound water is linked to the technical characteristics whereas the free water (also 
reflects the biological stability of the food. Therefore, it is important to demonstrate 
the effect of the fiber fraction on both sensorial characteristics and preservation of 
peanut butter.  
Experiments 
Moisture and water activity was measured in triplicate for the following samples : 
• Peanut butter (default) 
• Peanut butter + inulin powder (inulin)

Water content-Moisture 
Materials and methods 
The procedure used for measuring the water content of samples complies with the 
measurement techniques used universally within the food science community and 
represents a standardized way to measure the total moisture of food products (FAO 
u.d.), (Isengard 2001), (Ministry of Health 2010). Peanut butter samples were 
prepared and 3 g  transferred to aluminum cups and then placed in an oven for 
overnight drying at 105o C for 24 h. Dried samples were placed in a desiccator for 30 
min to cool down to room temperature while preventing moisture absorbance from 
the ambient environment.  
Results 
Appendix 3 and Figure 17 compiles the results from the water content measurements 
of the peanut butter samples.

moisture in their mass, which was expected by the nature of the product being a paste

default peanut butter. The default sample contained 1.3% water. Next, in a

Heat inactivation of Salmonella and 
Citrobacter 
Aim 
In order to investigate the relationship between the microstructure of peanut butter 
and bacterial heat resistance, peanut butter samples were inoculated with S. 
Typhimurium or C. freundii and heated. Three types of dietary fibers were used for 
manipulation of the microstructure of default peanut butter. Those were inulin, FOS 
and GOS. Knowledge on the heat tolerance of the bacteria in the default peanut butter 
mass will serve as a guidance to interpret the information extracted from the fiber-
containing samples. The main aim of the current experiments was to shed light on the 
heat resistance of S. Typhimurium U-311 and C. freundii in the low water activity 
product of peanut butter and then attempt to set a frame for correlating the 
constructed microstructures on the heat survival of the specific bacteria.  
Materials and methods 
Experiments 
Duplicate heat inactivation trials of S. Typhimurium U-311 and C. freundii bacteria 
were conducted for:  
• Peanut butter (default) 
• Peanut butter + 5 g inulin powder 
o Peanut butter + 10 g inulin powder (only for Salmonella) 
o Peanut butter + 15 g inulin powder (only for Salmonella) 
• Peanut butter + 5 g FOS powder 
• Peanut butter + 5 g GOS viscous syrup 
• Peanut butter + 5 g GOS powder 
Bacterial strains and culture conditions 
The S. Typhimurium U-311 strain used in the experiments, derived from an existing 
freeze stock culture kept at -80o C. 1 μl-loop of the freeze stock was left to grow on 
selective Xylose Lysine Deoxycholate agar (XLD) at 37o C for 24 h. Using a 10 μl loop, 
from Hounisen company, colonies were transferred into a 50 ml tube filled with 20 ml 
of Brain Heart Infusion (BHI) broth to prepare the overnight culture (OC). After 24 h 
incubation at 37o C, the OC was kept at refrigerator temperatures (4 - 5o C) until use. 
When required, new OCs were prepared implementing the previous preparation 
56 
 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
steps1. Prior to any experimental use, the OC was whirly mixed and left standstill for 
2 - 3 min. Concentration level of the OC was enumerated for every experimental run 
and was confirmed to be approx. 109 CFU/g at all times. 
For every experiment, 0.8 ml of the OC was converted into pellets by centrifugation 
for 5 min at 4o C with a rotation speed of 5.000 rpm/min. The supernatant BHI was 
removed and the remaining dry pellet was mixed with 0.5 ml of peanut oil. The oily 
suspension was rigorously mixed before inoculation into peanut butter samples.  
Concerning C. freundii, using a 10 μl loop from Hounisen already grown colonies on 
blood agar were transferred to a 50 ml tube filled with 20 ml of BHI broth. Next steps 
to prepare the Citrobacter OC were identical to S. Typhimurium U-311. 
Inoculation of peanut butter 
All peanut butter samples were prepared as described in “sample preparation during 
characterization” except that the addition of 0.5 ml peanut oil was replaced by 
addition of 0.5 ml peanut oil/bacterium (S. Typhimurium or C. freundii) suspension.  
Each inoculated and homogenized peanut butter sample was divided into 11 2.5-g-
portions (±5mg), each placed in separate stomacher bags. Afterwards the portions 
were subjected to stomaching for 1 min (2 x 30 s) to ensure flattening of samples and 
sufficient removal of atmospheric air from inside the samples. Prior to heat 
inactivation trials bags were sealed to avoid contact with the water bath (to prevent 
from mutual contamination) as well as contamination from external sources. 
Experimental procedure 
Inoculated samples were submerged into a pre-heated water bath at 75o C. One 
sample was excluded from heat treatment and was designated as the untreated 
sample, at time 0. Heat treatments of peanut butter samples lasted 80 min in total. 
Samples were taken at sampling intervals of 3.5, 7, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 70, and 80 
min, respectively. Once the sample bag was collected, cooling followed in ice-cold 
water for 30 - 60 s to prevent from further inactivation of the bacterium. All 
experiments were conducted in duplicate. 
Post-experimental samples handling 
After the pre-determined sampling intervals, stomacher bags were opened aseptically 
and the heat treated peanut butter sample was transferred into 50 ml sterile 
centrifuge tubes filled with 22.5 ml of BHI broth. Stomacher bags were rinsed with 2.5 
ml BHI prior disposal, to assist collection of any bacteria attached to the internal

Discussion 
Heat tolerance of S. Typhimurium U-311 and C. freundii 
The effect of different peanut butter matrices on the heat resistance of S. 
Typhimurium and C. freundii was investigated. Various dietary fibers were mixed with 
plain peanut butter inoculated with S. Typhimurium U-311 or C. freundii and 
underwent heating at 75o C to mimic pasteurization. Results for S. Typhimurium U-311 
showed that approximately 3-log-reduction in viability was obtained after approx. 80 
min. For C. freundii the time to obtain the same reduction was much shorter, i.e. 
approx. 10 min (Table 8). 
Studies on heat inactivation of Salmonella species, using peanut butter as substrate, 
demonstrated that the combination of low water activity and high fat content of the 
product could influence the bacterial heat inactivation. Shachar and Yaron (2006) 
observed that heat treatment for 50 min at 70o C resulted in approx. 2.5-log-reduction 
in viable numbers of a S. Enterica cocktail composed by Salmonella Agona, Salmonella 
Enteretidis and Salmonella Typhimurium ATCC 14028. At higher temperature, such as 
80o C, 3-log-reduction was observed for same heating duration which is comparable 
to results obtained for the default peanut butter in the present study. Shachar and 
Yaron (2006) also showed that death rates and elimination profiles remained the same 
for both high and low initial concentrations. In the present study, the number of viable 
cells dropped 10-fold within the first 10 minutes of pasteurization before slower and 
slower death rates proceeded (Figure 19). This observation was in line with literature 
citations mentioning a 1.5-log-elimination of Salmonella Agona, Salmonella Enteritidis 
and Salmonella Typhimurium ATCC 14028 cocktail in peanut butter after heating for 
12 min at 74o C (Shachar and Yaron 2006). Another heat inactivation experiment, 
carried out by Ma et al. (2009), also showed results comparable to this when S. 
Tennessee was inoculated into peanut butter and pasteurized. Using the Weibull 
model to fit their data and predict the reduction times, they found that at 71o C 1-log-
reduction occurred after 8.4 min whereas at 77o C only 5.2 min were necessary. They 
highlighted the protective effect of peanut butter structure on Salmonella during 
heating, after observing that 3--reduction in viable cells required as long as 103 min 
and 62 min at 71o C and 77o C, respectively (Ma, et al. 2009).  
The reduction times (tr) of 1-, 2-, and 3-logCFU/g of viable S. Typhimurium U-311 in 
the fiber-containing peanut butters were predicted using the Weibull logarithmic 
model (Table 9). Concerning the default peanut butter the predicted reduction times 
for 1-, 2-, and 3-logCFU/g were 10, 41, and 93 min, respectively. Compared to these, 
addition of inulin rendered the bacteria more susceptible to heating at 75o C. 1-log-
reduction was achieved after 4.9 min, almost two times faster than the default 
sample. 2-log-decrease was predicted to occur after 29 min and 3-log-elimination 
73 
 
==PAGE:88==
 
 
would be after 81 min. It is noteworthy that 1- and 2-logCFU/g tr of default and inulin 
peanut butters differed significantly whereas tr for 3-logCFU/g was comparable. When 
comparing heat tolerance observed in inulin to default peanut butter it should be 
taken into account that structural changes may occur in the matrix during the 
pasteurization process and how the fiber/fat composite system behaves after 
excessive exposure to pasteurization temperatures. It could be that the constructed 
fiber-peanut butter system has a heat-performance threshold which, when exceeded, 
becomes distorted returning to a structure more alike to the default.   
When FOS fiber was added into peanut butter 1-, 2-, and 3-log-elimination was 
predicted after 7.6, 36, and 91 min, respectively. Although the final water content of 
FOS peanut butter was higher than the inulin counterpart (Table 5), the predicted tr 
were higher at all times. Concerning the GOS fiber, the powdered form resulted in a 
composite peanut butter that appeared to protect S. Typhimurium U-311 from 
heating. Predicted reduction times were similar or higher to the default sample (Table 
4). The water content of the powder GOS peanut butter was comparable to both inulin 
and FOS samples. This observation highlights that not solely the water content but a 
synergistic effect of many factors affected the heat resistance of S. Typhimurium U-
311 in the particular matrix. As the slightly increased heat resistance in GOS (powder) 
peanut butter might reflect a structural change in favor of heat protection of bacterial 
cells. In the case of viscous GOS, predictions of Weibull reduction times demonstrated 
that created structure did have a significant impact on the heat resistance of both S. 
Typhimurium U-311 as well as C. freundii. When the syrup-alike GOS was mixed with 
peanut butter marked decreases in tr were observed for both bacteria, corresponding 
to 3- to 4-fold (Table 7). It is noteworthy that the water content of the viscous GOS 
peanut butter was 2.3-fold higher than its counterpart (Table 7/Figure 31).  
So far, results have been compared using the tr reduction times. However, a possible 
correlation to α and β values of the Weibull model must not be overlooked. In the 
study, all peanut butter samples, including the default, gave rise to β values below 1 
confirming the tailing occurrence at the end of the survivor curve. β values of all 
peanut butter samples varied in the a close range of 0.4 - 0.5, b values lower than 1 
indicate that the a fraction of the viable bacterial population is less susceptible to 
heating (Boekel 2002). In this case, the bacterial survival could be linked to the 
microstructure of the substrate that plays the role of “protecting” cells from heat. 
Differences in the elimination profiles of S. Typhimurium U-311 might be triggered by 
the variations in both the water content as well as aw. A high water content could be 
responsible for the formation of much more, and of bigger size, water interfaces 
within the composite matrix and promote a bacterial distribution with only few cells 
being dispersed within the remaining dehydrated environment. When fibers were 
added, fiber-associated water was introduced into the system. Increased water 
74 
 
==PAGE:89==
 
 
content could result in the formation of much more water-in-oil emulsion across the 
fat matrix favorizing aggregation of bacterial cells within the water areas. The bacteria 
localized in such areas would be more susceptible to heat compared to the rest of the 
cells dispersed along the complex fat-protein-carbs peanut butter matrix. 
Long lasting heating periods could also cause desiccation of the peanut butter, and 
loss of the excessive water, vanishing the early formed water areas and shifting 
microenvironment to its prior default state. This hypothesis could be a reasonable for 
the increased elimination of S. Typhimurium U-311 and C. freundii in inulin peanut 
butter where inactivation during the early heating times was faster than in default 
peanut butter but became similar around 3-log-reduction.  
Regarding the effect of increased fiber concentration on heat resistance of S. 
Typhimurium U-311, 10 and 15 g of inulin fiber were mixed with peanut butter prior 
pasteurization. Results showed that the two- and three-fold increase in inulin 
concentration in the peanut butter did not result in any change in the heat resistance 
of S. Typhimurium U-311 compared to addition of 5 g inulin (Figure 22). However, 
these observations were only based on the quantitative survival of the Salmonella 
cells. Other side-effects could have occurred that cannot be depicted in the survivor 
curve. When fibers are added, it is expected that more water would be dispersed in 
the matrix. In the example with inulin, the water content of the samples increased 
from 2.1 to 4.1% (Table 24). In theory, this would allow a higher number of bacteria 
to aggregate in the water phase and eventually die off faster during pasteurization. 
However, that hypothesis was not confirmed as similar heat resistance of S. 
Typhimurium U-311 was found in all peanut butters containing inulin. This might 
reflect that the bacterial cell respond to the microniches and the microenvironment 
formed by fiber addition. Two- and 3-fold increase of inulin addition could change the 
interactions between the multiple components or modify the attachment properties 
of the surface (beneficial or maleficent for the bacterial cells). Therefore, a more 
complex structure could be constructed permitting bacteria to be present in near 
water areas but still be protected by the oily environment in the peanut butter matrix. 
Electron Microscopy (such as cryo-SEM) could assist visualization of the effect of 
increasing concentrations on the peanut butter matrix.  
To sum up, the observed bacterial heat tolerance results indicate that microstructure 
could be considered an important parameter that play a role in the behavior of the 
cells. Therefore, more experiments must be scheduled aiming at unravelling the 
changes occurring in the peanut butter matrix during pasteurization. For instance, 
Shachar and Yaron (2006) demonstrated that peanut butter heated for 45 min at 70, 
80, and 90o C turned into a viscous liquid indicating the effect of the inactivation 
method on the inoculated substrate. Knowledge on the behavior of peanut butter 
75 
 
==PAGE:90==
 
 
during pasteurization would allow an in-depth understanding of the matrix changes 
occurred by the fibers. 
Attachment theory 
When you think of the food structure as a microstructural grid creating kind of 
surfaces inside the food matrix, it becomes relevant to look into attachment 
mechanisms of bacteria. It might be the case that substrate itself dictates where the 
bacteria will be present on the matrix and not solely its formed microstructure. In the 
scientific literature there are numerous citations on microbial attachment to surfaces 
and the impact of the substrate to the physiology of the microorganism. In general, 
cells can be found in planktonic form, freely in bulk solution, or as units attached to a 
surface or within a biofilm environment (Garrett, Bhakoo and Zhang 2008). 
Investigation on cell attachment have mostly been done for meat cuts, i.e. high 
moisture food, and observations revealed that the micro-topography of the meat 
tissue might have a significant effect on the attachment mechanism (Thomas, et al., 
1981; Walls, et al. 1993). Especially for meat, the micro-architecture of the muscle can 
provide bacteria with areas where they can “hide” in the deep and remain attached 
all along a decontamination process (Thomas and McMeekin 1981). Krag et al. (2009) 
demonstrated that the bacterial attachment to pork meat was independent of the 
species. It was mostly dependent on the surface characteristics and environmental 
factors rather than the inherent characteristics of the bacterial strain. Researchers 
highlighted the fact that surface characteristics could have a different impact on the 
adhesion mechanism between spoilage and pathogenic bacteria (Krag, Olsen and 
Aabo 2009). The theory of bacterial attachment has been known from the late 1980’s 
and, as Dickson and Koohmarie (1989) reported, it is influenced by the properties of 
the bacterial surface. Surface charge, hydrophobicity, and structures, such as 
extracellular polysaccharides and flagella, play an important role. They also mentioned 
that other reports indicated that motile bacteria were able to attach to surfaces faster 
than the non-motile and that the role of flagella in the attachment was probably 
dependent on the bacterial strain. More recent studies depicted that use of plant 
extracts could actually alter the cell properties, such as hydrophobicity and charge, 
rendering it harder for the bacteria to attach to surfaces (Wang, et al. 2013).  
Effect of fibers on water content and activity of peanut 
butter samples 
Correlating information on water content and aw with the bacterial heat tolerance in 
peanut butter samples did not reveal a clear link. For both S. Typhimurium and C. 
freundii, the default sample, which had the lowest moisture and aw, resulted in the 
best survival during pasteurization at 75o C, and the peanut butter mixed with liquid 
GOS fiber, having the highest moisture and aw, resulted in the least survival (Figures 
76 
 
==PAGE:91==
 
 
17-18). Table 2 shows that the lower aw of a heating substrate, the higher bacterial 
heat tolerance, therefore, this result was expected. However, when comparing to 
peanut butter samples with intermediate moisture and aw compared to those, the link 
was not so clear. For example, the FOS peanut butter and the GOS powder peanut 
butter had identical water content but this was not reflected in the bacterial heat 
survival of S. Typhimurium U-311, as tr for 1-, 2-, and 3-log-reduction was 1.3-fold 
lower in FOS compared to GOS powder peanut butter samples (Table 9). As shown in 
Figure 17, increase of inulin was followed by an increase in the water content of 
peanut butter. However, no apparent effect on the inactivation of S. Typhimurium U-
311 was observed (Figure 22). Increasing the added inulin resulted in a slight increase 
of water activity of peanut butter. The increase in water activity could not be reflected 
in reduction time’s changes of S. Typhimurium (Table 5).  
Although, a clear relationship between moisture, aw and bacterial heat resistance 
cannot be found in this study, the effect of the water content cannot be disregarded. 
Intensive research on this topic has been carried out and some researchers denote 
that combination of low water and high fat content might trigger an increase in 
bacterial heat tolerance. For example, Shachar and Yaron (2006) suggested that both 
the physical properties and matrix composition were responsible to build up a 
microstructure that could protect Salmonella against heating in a peanut butter 
matrix.  This behavior was believed to be associated with the composition itself and in 
particularly the fat content. More specifically, in their hypothesis, they considered that 
homogeneity of the product was a factor that determined bacterial behavior.  
He et al. (2013) highlighted the effect of aw on heat resistance of S. enterica in peanut 
butter. They reported that increased aw in desiccated products, such as peanut butter, 
reduced the heat resistance of the bacterium. More specifically, for peanut butter 
samples of identical formulation, double-fold increase of aw from 0.2 to 0.4 resulted 
in approx. 4-fold decrease in reduction time (from 8 min to 2 min) when heated at 90o 
C (He, Li, et al. 2013). Similar results were reported for heat tolerance of Salmonella 
in whey powders with different aw-values (Farakos, Frank and Schaffner 2013). 
Electron Microscopy of peanut butter samples 
In overall, the changes in the peanut butter matrix due to fiber additions were not as 
pronounced as expected, nevertheless, minor differences in the matrices occurred 
mainly in relation to homogeneity and roughness of the structure (Figure 14). 
Inactivation of S. Typhimurium U-311 in inulin and FOS containing peanut butters was 
comparable, with the inulin created microstructure tending to protect the bacterium 
poorer against heating. Cryogenic electron microscopy showed only minor 
modifications in the created structures (Figures 14-16) indicating that even slight 
77 
 
==PAGE:92==
 
 
changes could trigger differences in the bacterial heat resistance during 
pasteurization. However, this could not be confirmed for C. freundii. 
At high magnifications, the hypothesis of an existing link between microstructure and 
bacterial heat resistance was confirmed. As shown in Figures 14 and 15, the structure 
in FOS peanut butter was in overall comparable with the default’s, with grains in FOS 
peanut butter to be flattened. On the other hand, inulin peanut butter was 
characterized by a pronounced fibrousness with well-rounded and distinctive grains 
(Figure 14). The overall surface of inulin peanut butter appeared inhomogeneous 
compared to the uniform default pattern. It is worth noticing that viscous GOS syrup 
created a completely different and homogeneous structure, with the grains “coated” 
by a liquid-like layer (Figure 14). Based on the cryo-SEM pictures, the descending order 
of homogeneity was viscous GOS >> FOS = default > inulin which to some extent 
correlated with bacterial heat tolerance. The hypothesis, that food microstructure 
plays an important role on the elimination profiles of S. Typhimurium U-311 and C. 
freundii during pasteurization was, therefore, confirmed. Hence, food structure must 
not be disregarded during thermal inactivation and it is recommended to enlist 
microstructure as a parameter affecting the thermal resistance of Salmonella in the 
low aw peanut butter product. 
Furthermore, it is important to remind that addition of fiber resulted in increased 
moisture of the peanut butter. The amount of water was dependent on the fiber type 
(Figure 17). Although, difference in water contents, and aw, also to some extent 
explained the different elimination profiles of S. Typhimurium U-311 and C. freundii, a 
concrete link between the water content, bacterial heat tolerance, and the structural 
pattern could not be created. For example, even though the FOS sample had 2-fold 
higher water content, it had similar microstructure as well as tr’s to the default peanut 
butter (Table 9).  
Evaluation of the 3D cryo-SEM pictures of peanut butter could support the attachment 
theory in the peanut butter matrices investigated in the present study. For instance, 
comparing the different structures of FOS and inulin peanut butters it was clear that 
the latter consisted of uneven surrounding architecture compared to the more 
homogeneous FOS structure. Thus, bacterial cells could be localized in different areas 
across the matrix dependent on the characteristics of the surface. Variation in the 
“sitting” points of bacterial cells would result in differences in cell’s exposure to heat 
during pasteurization. The present study implies that microstructural inhomogeneity, 
such as observed for inulin peanut butter, renders S. Typhimurium U-311 and C. 
freundii more susceptible to heat.

Conclusions 
Pasteurization is a frequently applied technique in food processing to eliminate 
bacteria in foods. Pasteurization of low-water activity products is not mandatory but 
the increasing occurrence of associated foodborne outbreaks promotes towards its 
implementation. Peanut butter is notorious for salmonellosis outbreaks even if 
pasteurized. Results from the present study confirmed the marked heat resistance of 
S. Typhimurium U-311 in peanut butter. On the contrary, C. freundii did not exhibit 
similar heat resistance. Identical heat inactivation resulted in a faster elimination of C. 
freundii compared to S. Typhimurium U-311.  
The hypothesis, that microstructure has an impact on the heat resistance of 
Salmonella, was also confirmed.  Addition of dietary fibers affected the heat resistance 
of S. Typhimurium U-311 and C. freundii with the cell response to be dependent on 
the fiber type. Mixing of peanut butter with FOS and powder GOS fibers did not have 
a significant impact on the heat resistance of S. Typhimurium U-311 resulting in 
comparable elimination profiles and reduction times to the default peanut butter. The 
same effect was observed in heat inactivation trials of C. freundii in FOS added peanut 
butter sample. On the contrary, a significant change in bacterial elimination, for both 
species, was observed when the substrate was mixed with inulin and viscous GOS 
fibers. Especially in the case of viscous GOS fiber, the formed microstructure triggered 
a rapid elimination of both S. Typhimurium U-311 and C. freundii without any tail 
formation within the investigated heating time. Sustained elimination of C. freundii 
was observed also in peanut butter added powder GOS.  
It was assumed that increased water content allowed cells to aggregate in near the 
water areas and become more susceptible to pasteurization. The idea of a synergistic 
effect, of both macroscopic changes and microstructural aberrations from the default 
patterns, on the bacteria heat resistance was promoted. It must not be disregarded 
the fact that the fibers themselves could impact on the heat tolerance of S. Typhimurin 
and C. freundii. Addition of carbohydrates with characteristic chemical composition, 
molecule dimensions, physical properties and etc. could trigger different stress 
responses during heat inactivation of peanut butter. 
Visualization under cryo-SEM revealed different structural patterns among peanut 
butter samples. The formed microstructure of FOS peanut butter was similar to the 
default reflecting the same elimination profile of S. Typhimurium U-311 and C. freundii 
in both matrices. Inulin addition resulted in emergence of discrete microenvironments 
across the matrix suggesting different areas where the cells could be localized. When 
fibers with high water contents, such as GOS viscous syrup, were used then both 
radical changes in the structure and major alterations of the technical characteristics 
were observed.

Future perspectives 
The present study indeed reported some important findings concerning the 
relationship between the microstructure of peanut butter and the heat resistance of 
Salmonella and Citrobacter during peanut butter pasteurization. However, an in-depth 
investigation on the topic is required in order to get a full understanding and establish 
a model that could predict the cell response when it is exposed to different 
microenvironments. 
To start with, it is recommended to outline the behavior of the food during heating 
before engaging with heat inactivation trials. The effect of pasteurization time and 
temperature must be well reported to establish the frame of structural changes during 
heating. Evaluation should include both microscopic visualization and professional 
sensory assessment. In that way, any visualized change during pasteurization could be 
linked to specific parameters, e.g. water content, fiber amount, fiber type, 
pasteurization conditions etc.  
Furthermore, it is advised that electron microscopy should be involved to a greater 
extent to assist findings from heat resistance experiments. Visualization of larger areas 
of the fiber-rich peanut butters must be done to acquire representative topographical 
data. Extensive scanning of the surface must also be conducted when peanut butter 
samples are inoculated with bacteria. Knowledge on the complexity of the substrate 
and the microstructural features cannot predict the localization of cells within the 
peanut butter mass. 3D SEM pictures can shed light on the bacterial distribution 
patterns and attachment sites. However, in order to ultimately establish a predictive 
model, several bacteria with different inherent properties (virulence, Gram type, 
motility, etc.) shall be used and visualized under the microscope.  
Lastly, it is also suggested to use tools that would allow quantitative evaluation of the 
structure captured in SEM pictures. FIGI is a software that could help to this direction. 
It was originally developed to visualize data from light microscopy but due to its 
features people from other disciplines have also become users. FIGI could facilitate 
enumeration of bacteria sitting points as well as provide size and shape measurement 
of both molecules and bacteria present on the matrix of peanut butter or other low 
aw foods.

Appendix 5: XLD use instead of TSA plates 
in enumeration of viable S. Typhimurium 
cells. 
To investigate the dependence of detected viable cells on the agar plate, the complete 
pool of data points was used. However, in the case that TSA-XLD pairs consisted of 
one or two data points with no detected cells, then these pairs were removed from 
the list. Performing the Anova-Test, in Microsoft Excel, the outcome was a P-value of 
0.062, a value higher than the P-critical of 0.05 demonstrating that viable cells based 
on XLD enumeration were not different from those gathered from TSA plating. Thus, 
it was decided to use XLD for plating purposes and for relevant calculations.
