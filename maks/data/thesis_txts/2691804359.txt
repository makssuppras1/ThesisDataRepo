Abstract
The Major Histocompatability Complex (MHC) class II is a vital part in the
immune response to antigens. The MHC class II binds to certain peptides from
digested extracellular proteins and then presents the peptide to T-cells which
may trigger an immune response.
As one of the leading prediction models
of MHC class II-binding, NNAlign MA predicts MHC class II ligands with a
high speciﬁcity. Recent studies suggest that the implementation of RNAseq-
expression data from human genes can improve MHC-ligand predictors.
In
this study, a set of 4,000,000 peptides were annotated with publicly available
RNAseq-expression data from human tissue and used to train a superior MHC
class II prediction model. The model showed a signiﬁcant improvement in the
prediction of cross-validated multi-allele (MA) data (p = 2.6 ∗10−18, N = 98)
and single-allele (SA) data (p = 9.1 ∗10−5, N = 38). Additionally, the model
showed an improved performance on an independent neo-epitope benchmarking
data set (netMHCIIpan-4.0 AUC=0.8433 vs. NNAlign RNAseq AUC = 0.8504).
The current implementation of RNAseq-expression led to nontraditional selec-
tion of hyper-parameters of the NNAlign MA-framework and, therefore, leaves
space for further improvement and rationalisation. Finally, a bias in RNAseq-
expression per peptide length of MHC-binders was discovered. Although the
source of the bias could not be identiﬁed, it is likely a biological factor that
should be further investigated.

Introduction
2.1
Major Histocompatability Complex
The Major Histocompatability Complex (MHC) is a major component of the
immune system of vertebrates, responsible for a vital point in antigen recogni-
tion. The antigen-presenting MHC molecules consist of two classes, the MHC
class I and MHC class II. While the function of both classes involve antigen
presentation of potential epitopes1, the MHC class I molecules are found in
most nucleated cells, whereas the class II molecules are only found on antigen-
presenting cells (APCs) such as monocytes, macrophages, dendritic cells, and
B cells. Due to this cellular distribution of the two classes of MHC molecules,
the class I MHC generally binds peptides from intracellular proteins digested by
the proteasome in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), whereas the class II MHC
binds peptides from extracellular proteins digested by APCs. Once the MHC
has formed an MHC-peptide-complex, this complex travels to the surface of the
cell, after which the complex is presented to either a CD8+ or a CD4+ T-cell,
for class I and class II respectively[1].
In humans, the MHC molecules are often referred to as human leukocyte
antigens (HLA). The class I HLAs consist of the alleles HLA-A, HLA-B and
HLA-C, whereas the class II HLAs consists of the alleles HLA-DR, HLA-DP and
HLA-DQ. While the overall structure of each HLA molecule is generally well
conserved, the complexes display notable polymorphism[2]. The main factor in
the HLA polymorphism is the amino acid (AA) variability in the HLA binding
cleft[1, 2]. The binding cleft also bears the biggest diﬀerence between the class
I- and class II HLA molecules. As shown in ﬁg. 1 the closed binding cleft of the
HLA class I molecule only accommodates peptides of lengths 8-10 amino acids,
whereas the open class II HLA binding cleft generally accommodates peptides of
length 12-24 AAs[3]. Moreover, the HLA class I binding cleft most often covers
the entire peptide, whereas the HLA class II binding cleft covers a variable 9-mer
portion of the peptide, namely the binding core[4].

While structural variation in the HLA binding groove displays a sizable
diﬀerence in the overall presentation of peptides of both classes of HLA, it is

2.2
NetMHCIIpan-4.0
As one of the top performers in MHC class II-binding predictors, NetMHCIIpan-
4.0, running on the NNAlign MA framework, utilizes large data sets consisting
of binding aﬃnity (BA) and Mass spectrometry (MS) eluted ligand (EL) data
to train an artiﬁcial neural network[10]. BA-data is quantitative and highly
eﬀective in establishing the binding between peptides and MHC. The EL-data
consists of peptides whose binding potential have been experimentally tested
in vivo[6].
The BA data is highly relevant to the predictions of the actual
peptide-MHC-binding, however, the EL data has been shown to be of far greater
biological importance[11].
This is due to the fact that EL-data is based on
proteins which undergo all the biological mechanisms related to cleavage of
proteins, chaperone transportation and peptide loading. This allows the EL-
data to relay underlying biological mechanisms related to the presentation of
peptides[6], functioning as a binary descriptor of peptide presentation by HLAs.
Recent studies suggest that introducing RNAseq-expression values onto train-
ing peptides can improve the predictive capability of machine learning models
by a signiﬁcant margin[8, 6].
However, as the implementation of RNAseq-
expression in MHC-ligand prediction is relatively new, the exact reason for the
relationship between MHC binding and RNA expression is mostly unknown.
Currently, human RNA-seq expression data is only readily available through
the human protein atlas (HPA). The human protein atlas (HPA) contains sev-
eral hundreds of thousands of RNA-seq transcripts of human RNA-sequences,
sampled from diﬀerent tissue or cell line[12].
As a principal step in the immune response, the ability to accurately predict
MHC ligands is vital in the recently developing ﬁeld of personalized medicine
for cancer prevention[13]. High-throughput MHC ligand prediction methods can
further the production of personalized neo-epitope2-based vaccines, expanding
the options for cancer treatments.
While the area of personalized neo-epitope-based vaccines is a rapidly ex-
panding area of research, a major problem of this approach is the availability
of accurate neo-epitope data[13, 14].
Furthermore an increasing availability
of next-generation sequencing (NGS) tools allow for identiﬁcation of individ-
ual mutations in cancer patients[13]. With a high demand for precise and fast

2.3.1
Artiﬁcial Neural Networks
The NNAlign MA-framework, used in this project, is an Artiﬁcial Neural Net-
work (ANN). ANNs are extremely useful for problems which cannot be solved
by linear models. ANNs can infer hidden information between any number of
inputs and any number of outputs. As shown in ﬁg 2, ANNs operate by taking
any number of inputs, denoted as ”n”. These inputs are then transmitted along
weights, visualized as edges, to a layer of hidden neurons, shown as black nodes
labeled with ”nhid” in the middle section (hidden layer). Finally, the input for
each hidden neuron is summed up, scaled through an activation function, and
transmitted to each output neuron.

2.3.2
Representing amino acids in ANNs
When utilizing ANNs, it is important to represent the input-data in a meaning-
ful way. As this project focuses on predicting 9-mer peptides, each AA in the
peptide can be represented as an input neuron. However, input nodes of neu-
ral networks can only utilize numerical values, which will always be considered
in a arithmetic context, i.e. categorical values cannot be represented numeri-
cally. To address this problem, the individual amino acids can either be sparsely
encoded, or BLOSUM3-encoded. When utilizing sparse encoding, each amino
acid will be represented by 20 input neurons, also known as 1-hot encoding, or
1-of-k encoding. When utilizing this encoding, one neuron will always be fully
activated, whereas all other neurons will be deactivated, this is shown in ﬁg. 4.
The weights attached to each neuron will then be speciﬁc for each amino acid
at that position in the peptide.
Figure 4: Visualization of
sparse
encoding
on
an
amino acid pos. 3 of a 9-
mer peptide.
While true sparse encoding can be a useful tool, it is highly ineﬃcient, as
it contains 19 dead neurons for every input. Sparse encoding can therefore be
more eﬀectively utilized by setting the binary values to 1/-1 or 0.95/0.05, rather
than 1/0.
Unlike sparse encoding, BLOSUM-encoding can provide homology-based bi-
ological information. The BLOSUM is a 20x20 matrix constructed by observing

Much like the sparse encoding, it is possible to represent every position of
the amino acid with 20 input neurons. However, instead of utilizing the binary
sparse encoding, BLOSUM-encoding assigns values to the 20 input neurons,

Continuing the example from ﬁg 4, the third amino acid, L, would be represented

replaced by any other amino acid. By doing this, homology-based information
can be incorporated into the network, through the 20 input neurons.
2.3.3
Overﬁtting and bias
When utilizing machine learning methods, three sets of data are needed, a
training set, a validation set, and a test set, also called a benchmarking set.
The training set serves as a set of data points, upon which the model can be
trained, whereas the validation set serves as an independent set of data upon
which the trained model can be applied to and evaluated on, in an unbiased
manner.
In cases where the validation set is used to optimize the model, a
third, independent test data set is needed.
Figure 5: Sample training and validation error per epoch for a neural network
trained over 100 epochs. Left plot displays mean squared error (MSE). Right
plot displays Pearson Correlation Coeﬃcient (PCC).
It is possible that the training set and/or the validation set has certain biases
for the speciﬁc partitions. To avoid overﬁtting on the speciﬁc validation sets,
it is preferable to utilize k-fold cross-validation. By partitioning the data set
into K partitions, K models can be produced where each model has a distinct
validation set and training set from all other models. However, as there is no

longer an independent validation set when utilizing K-fold cross-validation, it is
important to remove a sample of the data set before partitioning, such that this
can be used as an independent test set. Additionally, if early stopping is utilized
to avoid overﬁtting on noise, independent test sets are also vital to properly
benchmark the model’s performance. Fig. 5 shows how a neural network can
only generalize to a certain degree, until it begins to ﬁt on noise in the training

Figure 6: Visualization of 5-fold cross-validation. Training sets are marked in

As shown in ﬁg. 6, producing 5 models with unique validation and training
sets will yield 5 separate models with diﬀerent biases. By taking the average
prediction value of all 5 models and using them as an ensemble, it is possible to
eliminate much of the learned noise in the individual models.
It is also important to remember that models with diﬀerent parameters, e.g.
more hidden neurons in an ANN, can produce the same bias as described before.
This divergence means that models with diﬀerent hyper-parameters can overﬁt
in certain ways, similarly to how individual data partitions can carry biases.
Much like K-fold cross-validation, this problem can be solved by training an
ensemble of several diﬀerent models with diﬀerently seeded random starting
weights, as well as diﬀerent numbers of hidden neurons.

the opposite direction of the gradient, at a rate of ϵ for each iteration n. Using

2.3.5
Prediction of binding cores
A major diﬀerence between the MHC class I and MHC class II peptides is
that the binding core of MHC class II peptides can vary in position, whereas
the entirety of MHC class I ligands is engulfed in the MHC. Due to this, it is
important that the network can identify the correct binding core of any length
of peptide.
To identify the correct binding core for each peptide, NNAlign uses a simple
loop with 3 steps. The network starts oﬀwith random weights for the entire
network. For each peptide, the network digests the 13-21 amino acid training
peptides into 9-mers with peripheral ﬂanking residue (PFRs) based on the source
peptide. After predicting every possible binding core, the network selects the
potential binding core which scores the best. Once this has been done for all
peptides in the training set, the prediction error is then calculated, after which
the network weights are updated via gradient descent-based back-propagation
to reduce the prediction error. The cycle is then repeated. The core-assigning
process of NNAlign is visualized in ﬁg 9.

2.3.6
Assigning alleles to multi-allele data
One problem with multi-allele(MA)-data is assigning the correct HLA to each
peptide. To resolve this issue, NNAlign MA utilizes a burn-in period, in which
the network is exclusively trained on single-allele(SA)-data[11].
This initial
period allows the network to establish motifs for each allele. Once the SA-data
has been burned in, the MA data can be annotated based on the allele it matches
the best. Once the MA data has been annotated as a single allele, it can be
used by the network for training.

2.3.7
Network architecture of NNAlign MA
The NNAlign MA architecture consists of an input layer, one hidden layer and
an output layer. The size of the input layer is variable, dependant on whether
the network is trained with context encoding and/or RNAseq-expression. The
base network, without context encoding or RNAseq-expression consist of 913
input neurons. These are:
binding core input (9*20 = 180 neurons)
Peptide length, 13 to 21 (9 neurons)
Peripheral ﬂanking residues (2*20 = 40 neurons)
Length of peripheral ﬂanking residues (2*2 = 4 neurons)
Pseudosequence (34*20 = 680 neurons)
For a total of 913 neurons for the base network. The two neurons encoding the
length of peripheral ﬂanking residues are encoded as PFR Neuron1 = LP F R/3
Page 10 of 44

and PFR Neuron2 = 1 −(LP F R/3), where LP F R denotes actual length of the
PFR (0 to 3). Moreover, these two neurons exist for both the C- and N-terminus,
for a total of 4 neurons. The pseudosequence refers to the sequence of amino
acids in the HLA binding cleft that is within 4.0˚A of the ligand[15]. Context en-
coding is deﬁned as 12 BLOSUM-encoded inputs, leading to an extra 240 input
neurons. Finally, RNAseq-expression is encoded as 3 neurons, represented as
RNAseq Neuron1 = RNAseqnormed, RNAseq Neuron2 = 1 −RNAseqnormed
and RNAseq Neuron3 = 1, wherein Neuron3 is a binary neuron, indicating
whether or not RNAseq-expression is included. The binary neuron is only in-
cluded in models trained with RNAseq-expression and is therefore always 1
when predicting EL-data.
The amount of neurons in the hidden layer is variable. The output layer
has two output neurons, one for BA data and one for EL data. The BA and
EL neurons share weights across both data types, such that the network can be
trained with both data types.
After a raw prediction value has been calculated through the ANN, a ranked
prediction score is produced. The ranked prediction score is a quantitative value
from 0 to 100, wherein 0 is the highest likelihood of being an MHC-binder,
whereas 100 is the lowest likelihood of being an MHC-binder. As some MHC
alleles show a lower overall ability to bind to peptides, the ranked peptide score
is diﬀerent for each allele. For each pseudosequence, a set of random peptides are
predicted using the network architecture described above. The raw prediction
values are then sorted into a set of percentile values. Based on these percentile
values, the raw prediction value of peptides can be assigned a ranked score which
can take into account the speciﬁc allele’s overall ability to bind to peptides.
2.4
Performance metrics
To evaluate the performance of models, three machine learning performance
metrics were used. The Area Under Curve (AUC) of the Receiver-Operator-
Characteristics (ROC) curve is a performance metric used to generalize the
predictive power of a prediction model.
The ROC displays the rate of true
positives as a function of false positives. By integrating this curve, the area
underneath acts as a quantiﬁable performance metric, namely the AUC. Often,
a majority of the performance can be measured on the initial 10% of the ROC
curve.
Due to this, the AUC of the initial 10% of the ROC curve can be
utilized as a performance measure, referred to as AUC 0.1. Finally, the Positive
Predictive Value (PPV) is the proportion of positive values which has been
assigned the correct value. However, as the models used in this project are not
binary classiﬁers, the PPV is calculated as PPV = NtopP os
Npos , where Npos is the
amount of positive data points, while NtopP os is the amount of positive peptides
in the top scoring N data points.

Materials and Methods
3.1
Training- and validation-data
All training and validation data was acquired from the NetMHCIIpan-4.0 publication[10].
The data sets contained 4,082,639 EL data points, along with 108,959 BA data
points. The positive EL-data were retrieved from the immune epitope database
(IEDB)[16], as described in the NetMHCIIpan-4.0 publication[10].
Negative
data was generated by sampling random peptides from the Uniprot protein
database[17].
For each length of 13-21, 411,750±500 negative peptides were
sampled[10].
All EL-based peptides were annotated as described in 3.2.2.
If any EL-
peptide could not be annotated, the peptide would be discarded from the vali-
dation and training data. The EL-peptide data set consisted of a mix of studies
in which cells lines either expressed multiple HLA alleles (MA) or a single HLA
allele (SA). The training/validation data consisted of 98 MA data sets and 38 SA
data sets, with a total of 3,111,801 MA peptides and 970,838 SA peptides. For
the MA data set, 2,830,301 peptides were non-binders (negative), while 281,500
peptides were ligands (positive). For the SA data set, 874,841 peptides were
non-binders (negative), while 95,997 peptides were ligands (positive). After dis-
carding any peptides which could not be annotated, the training set contained
3,036,025 MA data points, and 939,632 SA data points.
The context-encoding of EL-peptides were based on the three peripheral
ﬂanking residues (PFRs), pre- and post- C- and N-terminus, respectively. Each
peptide had its context encoding sourced from the source protein, as described
in the NetMHCIIpan-4.0 publication[10].
BA-based data was not changed from the NetMHCIIpan-4.0 publication[10],
containing 108959 SA data points. As the context of BA-data was irrelevant,
context was annotated as XXX for the C- and the N-terminus of each peptide.
BA-peptides were not annotated with RNAseq-expression data. This was not
done as BA data is often measured on synthetic or non-human peptides, thus,
RNAseq-expression data was not available. Moreover, the BA assays do not
convey any biological information which EL provides.
Both BA data and EL data was partitioned into 5 separate partitions each,
such that training could be performed with 5-fold cross-validation. The BA
and EL data were clustered together via common motif clustering using a motif
length of 9. After clustering, the BA and EL data was partitioned separately
as to avoid shared 9-mer subsequences across partitions, as described in the
netMHCIIpan-4.0 publication[10]. This resulted in 5 EL-partitions and 5 BA-
partitions.
To test and benchmark models, a data set consisting of neo-epitopes were
obtained from a 2019 study by J. Racle et. al[9]. The annotation and pruning
process is described in section 3.6.

The Human Protein Atlas was downloaded from the proteinatlas.org website4.
The data consists of two data sets, both containing 164,728 transcripts anno-
tated with RNA expression, denoted as Transcripts-Per-Million (TPM). One
database contained transcripts of RNA expression in various human tissues.
The other database contained transcripts of RNA expression in cell lines. Fur-
thermore, each transcript was annotated with an ensembl gene ID.
Two data sets were obtained internally, which included ensembl[18] tran-
script IDs, median- and mean TPM values. The TPM-median and TPM-mean
values were calculated from the TPM of all human tissues per transcript, in
the tissue-dataset. The TPM-median and TPM-mean were calculated from all
cell lines per transcript, in the cell line-dataset. Moreover, each transcript was
annotated with an amino acid sequence for the protein that the RNA sequence
would code for. This data set was used for annotation of EL-data and will be
referred to as the HPA-database.
A subsequent analysis of the HPA-database showed that 17,153 of the 97,271
ensembl-transcripts had been annotated as undergoing nonsense-mediated decay
in-vivo.
This indicated that these data points may not have been of much
biological signiﬁcance, as these proteins would never be produced.
Some of
the transcripts that were found to undergo nonsense-mediated decay-annotated
coded for either exact duplicates or similar splice variants of other peptides
found in the HPA-database. A data set was created by utilizing the ensembl
REST api to identify the biotype of all transcripts in the HPA-database. By
identifying transcripts which were not annotated as ”biotype: protein coding”,
any RNA transcript which is not translated in vivo, could be discarded.
A
separate database was created that only contained RNA transcripts that would
be translated in vivo. This data set will be referred to as the protein-coding
HPA-database. The protein-coding database contained 80,118 transcripts.
3.2.2
Annotation of Eluted Ligand data
Annotation of EL-data consisted of identifying every occurrence of a peptide
over all protein amino acid sequences in the HPA-database. If the peptide was
only found in one protein, the expression value of that protein was annotated to
the peptide. If multiple occurrences of a peptide was found in several proteins,
the expression values were summed and annotated, along with their individual
occurrences, such that a mean RNAseq-expression per source protein could be
calculated for each peptide. If no occurrence of the peptide could be identiﬁed,
the peptide would be discarded. 106982 EL-data points were discarded, as no
source protein could be found for these peptides in the original HPA-database,
leaving a total of 3,975,657 RNAseq-annotated peptides. For the protein-coding
HPA-database, 130,562 peptides were discarded, leaving a total of 3,952,077

3.3
Ranked scoring and random peptide generation
A collection of random natural peptides was selected, such that the percentile
ranked prediction score could take allele bias into account. As the MHC class II
can bind to peptides of length 13-21, 20,000 peptides were chosen of each length,
for a total of 180,000 peptides. As all EL-data had an RNAseq-annotation, all
randomly generated peptides had an RNA-seq expression of > 0 as well. To gen-
erate random peptides with an RNAseq-expression of > 0, the random natural
peptides were chosen from the HPA-database, such that every selected peptide
would have at least one match in the HPA-database. These peptides were chosen
without any selection bias. Moreover, peptides were chosen randomly with re-
placements from any protein in the HPA-database that had a length >=13..21,
for each length of random peptide, respectively. Furthermore, context encoding
was added, based on the three C- and N-terminal AAs of the peptide. If < 3
AA were present in the C- and/or N-terminal of the randomly chosen peptide,
an X was annotated in the place of the missing AA. After the peptides were
chosen, the HPA-database was searched for all occurrences of each peptide and
were annotated according to section 3.2.2. The reannotation step was neces-
sary, as the random peptides could often be identiﬁed in other proteins than
the one they were selected from. No peptides were discarded, as they were all
guaranteed to, at minimum, match the source protein.
All RNAseq-expression values for randomly generated peptides were scaled
according to the scaling of the training peptides for their respective model.
3.4
Motif deconvolution
To evaluate the models’ ability to detect allele-speciﬁc motifs in the binding
cores of peptides, sequence logos were created with the Seq2Logo webserver[19].
Furthermore, sequence logos were also utilized to attempt to identify binding
core motifs unique to models with RNAseq-expression included. Default settings
were applied for all logo plots using Seq2Logo. The models -Context -Expression
and -Context +Expression, scaled with a base-10000 logarithmic scaling were
used as a basis for motif deconvolution.
Positive peptides were extracted from 3 sample alleles which covered the
three major class II alleles: DR, DP, and DQ. The chosen alleles were DRB1-
0101, HLA-DPA10103-DPB10401 and HLA-DQA10505-DQB10301. The bind-
ing cores were then extracted to produce two logo-plots for each allele:
1) Positive peptides which achieved a ranked prediction score of <5 when
predicted, for both models.
2) Positive peptides which achieved a prediction score of <5 when predicted
with the model which included RNAseq-expression, but showed a ranked pre-
diction score of >20 in the model which did not include RNAseq-expression.
Plot 1 would serve as a baseline for motifs, whereas plot 2 would display mo-
tifs that could not be correctly predicted as strong binders without the inclusion
of RNAseq-expression.

3.5
Network training
A total of 4 model architectures were trained, one for each combination of in-
cluding or excluding context encoding and RNAseq-expression. For each model,
an ensemble of 150 networks were trained. The networks consisted of 20, 40,
and 60 hidden neurons, with 10 randomly seeded weight initializations each
(3*10 = 30 networks). This was trained for each of the 5 cross-validation folds
(30*5 = 150 networks). All models used BLOSUM50-encoding for the AA input
neurons.
All models and their corresponding networks were trained for 400 epochs
without early stopping with gradient descent-based back-propagation. A burn-
in period of 2 epochs were set to only allow hydrophobic AA at position 1, along
with a burn-in period of 20 epochs where only SA data was utilized for training,
after which MA data was included. For all predictions, an ensemble of all 150
networks was used. All but one model was trained on alternating MA and SA-
data, such that each type of data was equally present in the actual training of
the model. One model was trained, in which SA-data was used for 20% of the
training, whereas MA data was used for 80% of the training.

3.6
Model benchmarking
To allow for benchmarking against NetMHCIIpan-4.0, the same method of
in-silico k-mer digestion was used on the benchmarking peptides, as in the
NetMHCIIpan-4.0 publication by B. Reynisson, 2020[10].
The data was ob-
tained from a 2019 study by J. Racle, et al, 2019[9], in which 928 peptides from
various studies had been compiled to benchmark the eﬃciency of a prediction
model.
The data set contained mutated peptides marked as potential neo-epitopes.
These were annotated with a binary immunogenicity descriptor, an uniprot[17]/ensembl
ID of the protein of origin, a mutated amino acid position of the source protein,
and the original study the peptide was obtained from. A vast majority (846 of
928) of these data points were annotated as point mutations. Due to the gene
ID annotation being inconsistent, a generalized approach had to be adopted to
identify the wild-type (WT) peptide without utilizing the source protein and
mutated AA-position. For this purpose, a library was produced of all potential
wild-type peptides, based on which amino acid was mutated. Next, the HPA-
database would be searched for occurrences of each potential WT-peptide. In a
majority of cases, only one of each potential WT candidates could be identiﬁed
in the HPA-database, which would be chosen. In cases in which several candi-
dates were identiﬁed, the peptide with the highest sum of RNAseq-expression
was chosen. In cases where no peptide could be annotated, the candidate in
which the mutated AA was the closest to the center of the peptide would be
chosen. After the WT-candidates was chosen, each WT k-mer was identiﬁed
and annotated in the HPA-database, as described in section 3.2.2. For each
WT k-mer, the RNAseq-expression which was identiﬁed was annotated onto
the corresponding k-mer from the mutated peptide. In instances where k-mers
could not be identiﬁed, they were annotated with the value 0, rather than being
discarded. Finally, the RNAseq-expression was scaled, using the chosen nor-
malization. This process is illustrated in ﬁg 11. Peptides which could not be
identiﬁed in the HPA-database did not have their RNAseq-expression scaled,
but rather had it set to 0 in all scalings.

Prediction of peptides
For each model, prediction of peptides were done using an ensemble of 150
networks, as described in section 3.5. As the cross-validated EL-peptides were
of the length 13-21 amino acids, the full peptides could be used when predicting
their binding score. However, due to the benchmarking set mainly consisting of
peptides with lengths above 21 AA, the scoring system described in section 3.6
was utilized when the to produce prediction scores for benchmarking peptides.
Moreover, to evaluate the eﬀect of predicting peptides with rescaled RNAseq-
values, the cross-validated EL-peptides were predicted at the values they were
originally trained at, along with separate predictions in which the RNAseq-
expression of the data was scaled with a base-10000 logarithmic-scaling. This
was also done for the benchmarking peptides. This was done for models nor-
malized according to equation 3, at logarithmic bases 100, 1000 and 3000.
Finally, the AUC of the ROC for each length of k-mers 13 to 20 was calcu-
lated for the benchmarking peptides. Only k-mers of length 13 to 20 were in-
cluded, such that an accurate comparison could be made with the netMHCIIpan-
4.0 predictions, which only included k-mers of lengths 13 to 20. Moreover, mean
prediction value for speciﬁc lengths of k-mers over all benchmarking peptides
were calculated to evaluate model performance at diﬀerent peptide lengths. This
was done twice for the model trained with RNAseq-expression capped at 1000,
ﬁrst with benchmarking peptides capped at 1000 and normalized with a base-
1000 logarithm. A second evaluation was done with RNAseq-expression capped
at 10000 and normalized with a base-10000 logarithm. This length-wise predic-

5
Discussion
In this project, it was shown that there is a high correlation between peptide-
MHC class II-binding, and RNAseq-expression in human tissue. Furthermore,
it was shown that applying RNAseq-expression to the NNAlign MA-framework
can signiﬁcantly improve the predictive power for models without context-
encoding, both for multi-allele (MA) data (p = 2.6 ∗10−18, N = 98) and single-
allele (SA) data(p = 9.1 ∗10−5, N = 38). Moreover, it was shown that apply-
ing RNAseq-expression data to a network trained with context-encoding can
improve the predictive power of the network even further, for both MA data
(p = 2.0 ∗10−14, N = 98) and SA data (p = 2.6 ∗10−4, N = 38). Inclusion
of RNAseq-expression was shown to improve the prediction of MA data by a
higher margin than context encoding(p = 1.6 ∗10−7, N = 98), as well as im-
proving the prediction of SA data by a higher margin than context encoding
(p = 0.0167, N = 38). It was also shown that implementing RNAseq-expression
could increase the predictive power of independent neo-epitopes, when RNAseq-
expression of prediction peptides were scaled higher than training peptides
(AUC with RNAseq-expression = 0.8504, AUC of NetMHCIIpan-4.0 bench-
marking predictions = 0.843).
It was discovered that there was a strong bias in the RNAseq-expression
distribution for positive peptides, varying by length. While this phenomenon
was mainly present in multi-allele data, it was also present in single-allele data.
The source of this RNAseq-expression bias per peptide length could not be
accurately identiﬁed.
The RNAseq-expression bias per length found in MA
data may inﬂuence the fact that inclusion of RNAseq-expression lowers the
mean ranked prediction score for both positive and negative peptides. This can
be observed in pg. 28, ﬁg 6, wherein there is a clear decrease in mean ranked
prediction scores of cross-validated EL-peptides for both positive and negative
peptides when RNAseq-expression is introduced. Moreover, the mean ranked
Page 37 of 44
==PAGE:40==
Technical University of Denmark
June 22, 2021
prediction scores of MA and SA data is vastly diﬀerent.
Across all models,
MA data shows a substantially lower mean ranked score. As this diﬀerence is
also present in the models without RNAseq-expression, it can be assumed that
this tendency is inherent to NNAlign MA, rather than being an error in the
process of sampling random peptides or in the annotation of RNAseq-expression
of peptides. As a consequence of this imbalance in mean ranked score between
MA and SA data types, including both SA and MA data in performance metrics
will lower the overall performance. This lowered performance when including
both MA and SA data is shown in pg. 28, ﬁg 6, where all performance values
under the ”All data”-section show a lower performance than that of only the
MA data or only the SA data on their respective models.
It was discovered that a percentage of the RNAseq-transcripts used to an-
notate the peptides were not translated into proteins, but rather were decayed
in-vivo. It was investigated whether this impacted the length-wise distribution
of the RNAseq-expression and/or the predictive performance of the models. Re-
moving the transcripts lowered the mean RNAseq-expression of negative pep-
tides by approx. 5% for each length of peptide. Moreover, the mean RNAseq-
expression of positive peptides were lowered by approx. 20% for each length of
peptide. These values are shown in pg. 25 tab. 3. However, the lengthwise
RNAseq-distribution seen in positive peptides, as shown in pg. 24 ﬁg. 16b, was
still present. As models trained with this database did not show an increase in
performance, it was considered a dead end for now. However, if the problem
with lowered ranked prediction scores for MA peptides can be accounted for, it
may hold a use.
To understand the impact of RNAseq-expression on the prediction of pep-
tides, logo-plots were constructed of three sample alleles. One logo plot con-
tained the overall motif of positive peptides that could be identiﬁed by both the
model with and without RNAseq-expression. The other logo plot contained a
motif of positive peptides that only the model with RNAseq-expression could
properly classify as binders, both are shown in section 4.5. The logo plots of
peptides which could only be properly classiﬁed as binders by the model with
RNAseq-expression, displayed a low information content at all positions. More-
over, any position that showed any information, mostly displayed the same mo-
tifs that was identiﬁed by both the model with and without RNAseq-expression.
As there was little to no diﬀerence between the two plots beyond informa-
tion content, it is likely that the correlation between MHC-peptide binding and
RNAseq-expression is not tied to the amino acid sequence itself, but rather that
RNAseq expression boosts the prediction of peptides which are unlikely to bind,
but are highly expressed.
Following this, the RNAseq-expression bias for each length of peptide may
be linked to the peptides’ overall probability of binding, rather than boosting
the prediction of underrepresented motifs. This is backed up by the trend in
RNAseq-expression of positive peptides showing an inverse relationship with
the amount of positive peptides per peptide length, see pg. 24, table 2, row 3
and row 7. Additionally, studies have shown that MHC class II-peptide bind-
ing generally peaks at approx. 14 to 17 amino acids[21, 9, 6]. Observing the
Page 38 of 44
==PAGE:41==
Technical University of Denmark
June 22, 2021
RNAseq-expression of positive peptides alone, shown in pg. 24, ﬁg. 16b, shows
a directly inverse relationship between the RNAseq-expression and the overall
probability of any length of peptide binding to MHC class II shown in several
studies. This relationship between RNAseq-expression and peptide length of
positive peptides was shown not to be due to highly expressed outliers, as this
trend was seen when peptides had their annotated RNAseq-expression capped
at certain levels, shown in pg. 25 ﬁg. 17. All in all, this indicates that the
relationship between RNAseq-expression and peptide lengths is not due to an-
notation errors or low quality data, but rather is an inherent feature of MHC
class II ligands. As there seems to be an inverse relationship between binding
aﬃnity and RNAseq-expression for each peptide length, it is possible that some
peptides which display a very high RNAseq-expression, generally have a low
binding aﬃnity when considering the AA sequence alone. As peptides with a
higher RNAseq-expression generally will be more present in cells, it is possible
that peptides which display a high RNAseq-expression, but a low-aﬃnity for
MHC class II may be digested more often due to this high presence in in-vivo.
This would lead to more scenarios in which the MHC class II-molecule comes in
contact with the highly expressed peptides. As such, while low-aﬃnity peptides
may rarely bind to the MHC-class II, they are given more chances to bind due
to the peptides being highly expressed.
Moreover, the inverse relationship between RNAseq-expression and binding
aﬃnity per peptide length in training data, was only present to a high degree
in MA data. While SA data showed a similar trend in RNAseq-expression per
length of peptide, it did not show the same increase in RNAseq-expression at
lengths > 16, that MA data showed. This can be seen in pg. 27 table 4, as well
as in pg. 26, ﬁg 18. The cause of this RNAseq-expression imbalance between
MA and SA data could not be identiﬁed. However, given that MA and SA data
were annotated using the same program, it is unlikely to be an error in the
annotation, but rather an inherent feature of the MA data and the SA data,
respectively. Additionally, given the poor performance on the benchmarking
data, the diﬀerence in RNAseq-expression between the two data types may
play a signiﬁcant role in how data is predicted.
This is emphasized by the
strong performance increase on the benchmarking set, when using models which
were trained with RNAseq-expression normalized at a lower logarithm base, but
predicted with a higher logarithm-base. The model trained with a base-1000
logarithm showed the strongest performance on the benchmarking set when
the benchmarking peptides were normalized with a base-10000 logarithm, as
shown in ﬁg 23. Moreover, this performance increase of predicting peptides with
separate scalings, was also slightly present in the cross-validated EL-data, shown
in ﬁg 24. For the cross-validated EL-data, predicting peptides normalized at a

Another possible reason that MA data on average showed a lower ranked
prediction score, may be due to the way that the random peptides, used in
ranked scoring, were generated. Shown in pg. 27, table 5, the randomly gen-
erated peptides show a mean RNAseq-expression of around 44.60 ± 4 for all
peptide lengths. This is in contrast with the RNAseq-distribution of the train-
ing peptides, shown in pg. 24, tab. 2. For the training peptides, we see that the
negative peptides have a mean RNAseq-expression of 22.25 ± 0.75, whereas the
positive peptides have a mean RNAseq-expression of 345 ± 80. Moreover, the
randomly generated peptides do not show the same relationship between peptide
length and RNAseq expression that the positive training peptides exhibited. It
is important to note that the negative peptides were generated randomly from
natural peptides. As all negative peptides which could not be annotated were
removed, the randomly generated peptides should therefore in theory have a
mean RNAseq-expression very close to that of the negative peptides. Due to
the diﬀerence between the RNAseq-expression of the randomly generated pep-
tides and the RNAseq-expression of the training peptides, the ranking system
of the alleles may have been negatively aﬀected. A possible solution to this
issue, could be to implement an RNAseq-expression bias per peptide length to
the randomly generated peptides, such the distribution more closely matched
that of the training peptides. As mentioned above and shown in pg. 26 ﬁg. 18,
the RNAseq-distribution per peptide length was vastly diﬀerent between MA
data and SA data. As models were trained with an initial burnin-period of 20
iterations where only SA data was utilized, this diﬀerence may have impacted
the further training of the models. However, this is unlikely, as the network can
”unlearn” the information from the initial burnin-period, as shown on pg. 10 ﬁg.
10, wherein the amino acid logo changes substantially when MA data is intro-
duced. While the exact reason why the model trained with peptides normalized
with a base-1000 logarithmic normalization showed superior performance when
predicted on peptides normalized with a base-10000-logarithmic normalization is
unknown, the model outperformed the netMHCIIpan-4.0[10] model on the same
subset of the benchmarking data, as well as in predicting the cross-validated EL-
peptides. As this model can outperform a state-of-the-art prediction model, the
implementation of RNAseq-expression into the NNAlign MA-framework should
be explored further, such that the problems discussed in this study can be taken
into account to build a better prediction model.
6
Conclusion
Implementing RNAseq-expression in models yielded a signiﬁcant performance
increase in the prediction of MHC class II ligands. Additionally, implementing
RNAseq-expression can increase the predictive performance of independent neo-
epitope peptides. Although the current model could outperform models that did
not incorporate RNAseq-expression data, there is still a collection of issues that
can addressed to further improve the model.
