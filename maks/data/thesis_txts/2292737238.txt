Abstract
Speech intelligibility models try to predict the human ability to understand speech in adverse
listening conditions. A number of powerful intelligibility models have been proposed in the past,
however, a general framework is still to be achieved. The present thesis attempts to generalize the
performance of two previous approaches. Namely, it proposes an intelligibility prediction model
based on the combination of the auditory processing of the multi-resolution speech-based Envelope
Power Spectrum Model (mr-sEPSM; Jørgensen et al. (2013)) and a correlation-based decision
metric inspired by the Short-Time Objective Intelligibility measure (STOI; Taal et al. (2011a)).
The proposed model ﬁlters clean and noisy speech with a peripheral ﬁlterbank, whose outputs’
envelopes are processed by a modulation ﬁlterbank. The modulation outputs are analysed with
time windows of different durations by calculating the cross-correlation between the resulting
internal representations of the clean and the noisy speech. The correlation values are computed for
each time window, modulation and peripheral ﬁlter individually and later integrated into a ﬁnal
correlation-based metric. This metric is transformed into intelligibility scores using a ﬁtted logistic
function.
The model was tested in conditions of stationary noise, ﬂuctuating interferers, spectral
subtraction noise-reduction algorithms and under the effect of reverberation and phase jitter
distortions. The results were evaluated against human data and predictions from other models.
Furthermore, the internal stages of the model and their individual contributions to speech
intelligibility predictions were analysed.
The proposed model was able to account for all tested conditions except for speech in the
presence of reverberation, proving to be more general than the STOI model that could not account
for ﬂuctuating interferers nor the effect of reverberation. The proposed model can also account for
phase jitter where mr-sEPSM predictions deviated from the human data. The main limitation of the
proposed model is its inability to account for the effect of reverberation. However, it was shown in
this study that the internal representation of the model is sensitive to that effect, pointing to the
need for further research to generalize the model in this condition.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The Oxford English Dictionary deﬁnes Speech as "The expression of or the ability to express
thoughts and feelings by articulate sounds" (2014), that is to say that speech is the main tool used
by humans in order to communicate with one another, making it a key factor in all human and
social relationships. This explains why speech is a central topic in many disciplines, both in the
natural, social and engineering sciences.
The way in which people are able to process and understand speech signals has been the
focus of research for decades. In relation to this, a number of models have tried to replicate the
human ability to understand spoken words. The development of such models is interesting because
they allow the study of critical aspects of intelligibility, i.e. the key factors that make speech
comprehensible to humans in different listening conditions. Moreover, these models have been
able to simulate human behaviour which is important in the development of, e.g., algorithms for
speech transmission, hearing aids or telecommunications technologies.
Ever since the development of the telephone in the late 19th century, great interested was shown
by the scientiﬁc community towards characterizing the transmitted speech, both in terms of speech
quality and speech intelligibility. The ﬁrst objective intelligibility measures started to be researched
already in the ﬁrst half of the 20th century. The work by French (1947) ﬁrst presented a prediction
model that could account for intelligibility. This model set the basis for the Articulation Index (AI),
developed by Kryter (1962), later standardized by ANSI (1969). The AI is based on the fact that
the intelligibility is affected by background noise to different extent in different frequency bands.
The AI model was extended and modiﬁed into the Speech Intelligibility Index (SII) (ANSI,
1997). These two models take a modelling approach based on the long-term spectra of the signals,
calculating the signal-to-noise-ratio (SNR) for different frequency bands and performing a weighted
average of them. This long-term analysis implies that the models are not sensitive to temporal
aspects such as the effect of reduced masking of the signal in the case of temporally modulated
maskers, i.e. masking release. This means that the intelligibility scores, improved in the presence
of ﬂuctuating noise (Festen and Plomp, 1990), are not predicted by these long-term models.

Due to the limitations that these two models presented also when dealing with non-linear
degradations of the speech signals, Houtgast et al. (1980) proposed a different model known as
the Speech Transmission Index (STI), which was based on the concept of the Modulation Transfer
Function (MTF) by measuring the change in the modulation depth of modulated noise in different
acoustic conditions. This model showed very good results for reverberant speech and the presence
of additive noise, although it failed in conditions of processed speech such as spectral subtraction,
as shown by Ludvigsen et al. (1993).
Several models have been proposed that are closely related to the AI and the STI. Examples of
models within this framework include e.g., the speech-based STI (sSTI) proposed by Payton and
Braida (1999), where speech signals are used as input to the model instead of modulated noise,
or the coherence-based STI (CSTI) by Kates and Arehart (2005), that can account for non-linear
processing like peak-clipping. A detailed study by Goldsworthy and Greenberg (2004) investigated
the ability of different speech-based extensions of the STI to account for non-linear distortions
(including models proposed by Holube and Kollmeier (1996); Drullman et al. (1994); Ludvigsen
et al. (1990)), concluding that none of them performed accurately in all the acoustic conditions
tested. Other models include the Spectro-Temporal Modulation Index (STMI) (Elhilali et al., 2003)
or the extended SII model(ESII) proposed by Rhebergen et al. (2006) that tried to improve the
performance of the SII in ﬂuctuating noise introducing a time dependency on the calculation of the
intelligibility scores, but assumes that the clean speech and the noise can be accessed separately
thus, not accounting for cases where the speech mix had been subjected to processing schemes.
Recently two models have been proposed to account for speech intelligibility also in condition
where STI and SII fail: the Short-Time Objective Intelligibility Measure (STOI) by Taal et al.
(2011a) and the speech-based Envelope Power Spectrum Model (sEPSM) by Jørgensen and Dau
(2011). Both models were shown to provide good agreement with human data in a variety of
listening conditions.
Speciﬁcally, sEPSM accounts for effects of reverberation and non-linear processing, such as
spectral subtraction, where the model is able to predict the trend of decreasing speech intelligibility
with increasing spectral subtraction factor. Furthermore, in an extended version of the sEPSM
model, the mr-sEPSM (Jørgensen et al., 2013), effects of speech masking release in ﬂuctuating
noise can be accounted for. However, the mr-sEPSM was shown to fail in the presence of phase jitter
distortion (Chabot-Leclerc et al., 2014), if an additional stage that evaluates speech information
across frequency is not introduced.
Comparatively, STOI accounts for results obtained with speech subjected to Ideal Time
Frequency Segregation (ITFS), a speech separation technique that improves speech intelligibility
by using a binary modulation on the noisy speech by means of an Ideal Binary Mask (for details
see e.g. Brungart et al. (2006)), a condition that has not been tested with the mr-sEPSM. STOI

As reﬂected in Table 1.1, there is a lack of data that would allow a direct comparison of
mr-sEPSM and STOI, i.e. not the same tests have been performed with the two models. Thus, it is
difﬁcult to determine which of them is more promising in terms of predicting speech intelligibility
in a wider range of processing and acoustic conditions. They are quite complementary in terms of
the types of processing that have been considered for evaluation. Furthermore, both models differ
in the signal processing they apply and in the decision metric on which they base their predictions,
as described in more detail in sections 2.3 and 2.4.
In the present project it is hypothesised that, although no data are available, the STOI model
is likely to fail in reverberating conditions since other models, like the CSTI that are also based
on a correlation in the decision metric, have been shown to not account for intelligibility in
these conditions (Goldsworthy and Greenberg, 2004). Furthermore, the STOI model may fail
in conditions with ﬂuctuating noise, which is considered to be one of the major strengths of the
mr-sEPSM. On the other hand, the mr-sEPSM may fail in conditions with ITFS processing as well
as single-channel noise reduction algorithms where STOI has been successful.
Concretely, this project investigates whether these two models can be combined in order to

Chapter 8, the main conclusions of the project will be summarized and new research lines derived
from this work will be proposed.

CHAPTER 2
Background
2.1
Speech Intelligibility
Intelligibility describes the capacity of a certain speech (or speech-like material) to be
understood. The term was ﬁrst used to describe how well speech was transmitted through a
communication channel with regard to how well it could be understood by the end listener (Fletcher
and Galt, 1950; Martin, 1997).
Intelligibility can be measured by means of performance in a recognition task. These tests
measure the percentage of correctly understood speech units (e.g. phonemes, syllables, words
or sentences) at different presentation levels. The presentation level is usually given in relation
to the level of the masking noise, making use of the concept of auditory masking, i.e. the fact
that an auditory signal (target) is less intelligible in the presence of another (masker) signal than
when presented in isolation. This phenomenon has been largely studied, e.g., by Hawkins (1950)
or Festen and Plomp (1990). The resulting outcome of such tests is a mapping between signal-
to-noise-ratio (SNR) and percentage of correctly recognized items in the form of a logistic, i.e.
psychometric, function. Often, speech intelligibility results are presented as speech reception
thresholds (SRTs), which is the SNR that corresponds to a 50% of correctly identiﬁed speech units.
Speech intelligibility and intelligibility tests are affected by various factors that can be roughly
divided into the following categories:
Speech material and test characteristics : The type of speech material to be evaluated is key in
the intelligibility scores. Due to redundancy, sentences are easier to understand than single
words, syllables or phonemes, since context allows listeners to “complete” the missing
information, (Miller, 1950). The size of the speech material is also relevant, as higher
intelligibility is achieved when the listeners have a clear idea of the possible speech units
(words, sentences or other) that will be presented. The design of intelligibility tests also
inﬂuences the resulting scores. Whether the response set is closed or open can lead to
different intelligibility scores, i.e. higher intelligibility is found when test subjects have to

decide between a set of speech units (closed response) than when they have to guess entirely
what they heard (open response).
Transmission and processing A limited frequency transfer function of the transmission channel,
algorithms of speech dynamic compression, quantization of the speech, non-linear processing
or speech-enhancement schemes are some of the attributes that can inﬂuence speech
intelligibility. The presence of reverberation in the transmission channel or the effects of
auditory masking also affect the intelligibility of speech. Furthermore, the type of masker has
a strong effect, for example, studies have shown that listeners can beneﬁt form a phenomenon
commonly referred as “listening in the dips” in the case of non-stationary maskers, which
increases notably speech intelligibility (Festen and Plomp, 1990; Bernstein and Grant, 2009)
Perceptual aspects of the listener Each individual’s hearing abilities have a direct inﬂuence
on the prediction of speech intelligibility: reception thresholds, frequency resolution and
whether the listener has a hearing impairment or not can impact the measured SRTs. Thus,
when evaluating different acoustic scenarios or speech materials, typically the average across
listeners is considered to minimize the personal deviations of each listener. However, in
studies with hearing impaired listeners, it can be crucial to investigate the results from
individual listeners.
2.2
STI
The Speech Transmission Index (STI) (Houtgast and Steenekem, 1971; Houtgast et al., 1980;
IEC, 2005) is an standardized intelligibility metric that can be used both to objectively measure the
intelligibility in different environments (e.g. a room or a communication device) and to produce
predictions in a modelling framework.
The STI introduced an analysis in the modulation domain which differentiated it from previous
models such as the SII or the AI. That its to say it considered the auditory system’s ability to detect
modulations in signals. This allows the prediction of changes in intelligibility with reverberation as

as the modulation transfer function (MTF), which is related to the change in modulation depth
between the probe signal and the measured signal in a given environment and between the clean and
processed signals in the model. The main computational steps in the STI are shown in Figure 2.1.
The measurement aspects of the STI will not be covered in this report, reference is made to standard
60268-16 (IEC, 2005) for details.

2.3
sEPSM and mr-sEPSM
The speech-based Envelope Power Spectrum Model (sEPSM) was ﬁrst proposed by Jørgensen
and Dau (2011). It is inspired by the work of Dubbelboer and Houtgast (2008), which ﬁrst
introduced the modulation variations of the noise in the speech intelligibility prediction (whereas
STI only considered the modulations of the clean speech to be determinant). In the Dubbelboer
and Houtgast model, the signal to noise ratio between the speech modulations and the “spurious”
modulations resulting from the non-linear processing of the noisy speech was considered to be the
key metric that determined intelligibility.
The sEPSM assumes that the signal modulation is not masked by the “spurious‘” modulations
(which are neglected) but by the modulations of the noise itself. The inputs to this model are
the noisy speech and the noise alone. The decision metric of the model is the envelope power
signal-to-noise ratio, SNRenv, a variable ﬁrst introduced in the original Envelope Power Spectrum
Model (EPSM) proposed by Ewert and Dau (2000) in non-speech masking conditions, of which
sEPSM is an extension.

2.4
STOI
The short-time objective intelligibility measure (STOI) was ﬁrst proposed by Taal et al. (2010),
and thoroughly tested in Taal et al. (2011a). It was developed with the aim of accounting for speech
intelligibility with time-frequency weighting using as decision metric the correlation coefﬁcient of
the clean and noisy speech.
This model has only a small number of stages such as discrete Fourier transform (DFT)-based
time frequency decomposition and ﬁxed windowing analysis in the time domain. In contrast to
STI, it does not include any type of frequency band weighting. The model stages are illustrated in
Figure 2.4.
The inputs of the STOI model are the clean and the noisy (or processed) speech (denoted x
and y, respectively), it is crucial in the STOI framework that the two inputs have the same size.
Furthermore, due to the fact that correlation is used as a metric it is determinant that both signals
are aligned in time, that is to say, that the clean speech and the processed speech must start at
the same point in time (i.e. same sample, in discrete-signal terms), the reason for this is that the
correlation measures the similarity of the two signals at lag zero and in order to do that the model
must have access to the same segments of the internal representation.

3.1
Structure
The intelligibility prediction model proposed in this this thesis is based on the hypothesis
that combining the processing stages of the mr-sEPSM model, that relate closely to the auditory
processing, and the correlation-based back-end of the STOI model could bring together the strengths
of both models and allow the proposed model to account for human data in a wider range of listening
conditions, than the STOI and mr-sEPSM individually.
Similar approaches have been researched before, for example by Christiansen et al. (2010)
where a correlation-based back-end over the auditory model presented by Dau et al. (1997a) was
used to obtain speech intelligibility predictions. The model has showed similar results than STOI
(Taal et al., 2011b). However, it lacks a modulation ﬁlter-bank, which was shown to be determinant
to account for the effect of reverberation in the STI model, and that has been included in the present
model.

clean speech and the noisy mixture (x and y, respectively), i.e. the input signals of the “hybrid”
model are the same ones as those of STOI.

3.2
Processing stages
The ﬁrst steps of the processing follow closely those of mr-sEPSM including: ﬁltering through
an auditory ﬁlterbank, extraction of the envelope of the resulting outputs and processing through a
modulation ﬁlterbank. The outputs of this modulation ﬁlterbank are processed in order to preserve
the ﬁne structure of the lower modulations (fc < 10 Hz) and extract the envelope in the higher
modulations as suggested by the work of, e.g., Schreiner and Langner (1988) and Hewitt and
Meddis (1994).
After this auditory processing is carried out the resulting signals are subjected to a time analysis,
on which the output of each modulation ﬁlter is studied in different-sized time-segments obtained
using different window sizes.
The resulting time-segments are used as inputs of a correlation analysis between the clean
signal and the noisy mixture, inspired on the correlation analysis used in STOI. A correlation
coefﬁcient for each time segment, each modulation ﬁlter and each auditory ﬁlter is obtained. The
values are computed into the correlation-based metric of the proposed model. This metric is then
transformed by means of a logistic function into an intelligibility score.

3.2.5
Modulation Envelope Extraction
The outputs of the modulation ﬁlters with center frequencies above 10 Hz are subjected to a
second envelope extraction. This is done to model the amplitude-modulation sensitivity of the cells
of the inferior colliculus central nucleus (ICC) in the auditory pathway, (Hewitt and Meddis, 1994).
Schreiner and Langner (1988) showed in their analysis of the temporal neuronal processing
of an anaesthetized cat that the sensitivity for modulation phase is greatly reduced (i.e. the ﬁne
structure is lost) for modulation frequencies above 10 Hz. In line with these studies, the proposed
model preserves the ﬁne structure only of the lower modulation outputs (fc < 10 Hz), whereas for
higher modulations this information is lost in the processing. Such a non-linearity was already
included in the auditory model by Dau et al. (1997a,b) that could accurately predict modulation
thresholds.
The process of envelope extraction is done in the same manner as described in section 3.2.2.

Detection of Silent Segments
The power of each segment of the modulation outputs of the clean speech is calculated and
compared against a power threshold of -30 dB. Segments that do not meet this power requirement

are considered to not be carrying any speech information and are excluded from computation of the
ﬁnal correlation value.
This is done in order to account for the effect of segments that carried no speech information
i.e. just noise, affected intelligibility. For those segments the correlation metric would represent a
correlation between noise (from the mixture) and "silence" from the clean speech. This introduces
two possibilities: (i) it can lead to high correlations due to the random nature of the noise or (ii) it
can produce very low correlations because of that same random nature of the noise.
This sensitivity to random ﬂuctuations of the noise of the correlation metric is avoided in the
STOI model by detecting the silent segments and not taking them into account for computation.
Segments with "random" correlations should not have an impact on intelligibility, since there is no
presence of speech in them, (Taal et al., 2011a). The same approach is taken in the proposed model.
In STOI the detection of silent frames is performed before any other processing. In the proposed
model, however, the detection of silent segments is done over the internal representation that leads
to the calculation of the correlation, i.e., the outputs of the modulation ﬁlterbank.

CHAPTER 4
Experimental Conditions
The proposed model was tested for three speech corpora, two types of noise and three different
non-linear processing conditions. The different conditions tested have been shown to affect speech
intelligibility and thus, they are relevant to assess whether the proposed model is able to produce
accurate intelligibility predictions in a wide range of listening and processing scenarios.
4.1
Speech Materials
In the context of this study three speech materials were used:
Dantale II : Developed by Wagener et al. (2003) is a Danish sentence test. These sentences consist
of ﬁve words taken from a base of 10 sentences that have the same structure (name + verb +
numeral + adjective + object). They are grammatically correct but meaningless.
CLUE : The Conversational Language Understanding Evaluation developed by Nielsen and Dau
(2009) consists on Danish sentences. They are ﬁve-word sentences constructed from an open
word-set and are both grammatically correct and used in daily life.
PM-material : This test developed by Plomp and Mimpen (1979) consists on Dutch sentences
that replicate the conversational language and are about 8-9 syllables long.
Each speech material has a different psychometric curve that relates SNR and speech
intelligibility scores. Thus, for each of them the logistic function of the proposed model (equation
3.9) was ﬁtted differently to account for the particular behaviour of each speech material. The
ﬁtting was done based on the human data provided in three different studies (Plomp and Mimpen,
1979; Wagener et al., 2003; Nielsen and Dau, 2009) by comparing SNR/intelligibility score pairs
for each material with the model outputs for those same SNRs and materials. This resulted in a
mapping of the correlation to the intelligibility score that could be ﬁtted to a psychometric function
using a least squares optimization. Figure 4.1 shows these ﬁttings.

• The International Speech Test Signal (ISTS), (Holube et al., 2010), which is a non-semantic

4.5
Reverberation
To test the performance of the proposed model when the inputs are inﬂuenced by the presence
of reverberation, the same inputs as in Jørgensen and Dau (2011) were used. Reverberation times
of T60 = 0, 0.4, 0.7, 1.3 and 2.3 were applied to the model’s input. In order to model the effect of
reverberation, signals that simulate the impulse response of a room with the desired reverberation
times were convolved with the mix of SSN noise and speech for various SNR values.
In this case the inputs of the model were the clean un-reverberated speech signal and the
reverberated noisy mixture, i.e. after it had been convolved with the correspondent impulse
response. Because the correlation metric is based on comparing both signals at lag zero to assess

must be performed before the inputs can be subjected to any further processing.

with the same impulse response as the noisy mixture and calculating the correlation between the
reverberated and the dry clean signal. The lag between the two signals is found by calculating the
maximum of their correlation. This lag is used to shift in time the reverberated mixture. Then,
the size of this mixture is adjusted to ﬁt the size of the dry clean speech signal by cutting the
reverberation tail, as shown in Figure 4.2.

Model Analysis
The internal stages of the model and their individual contributions to speech intelligibility
predictions were studied. Speciﬁcally, the inﬂuence of the metric’s computation and the role of the
modulation ﬁlterbank in the model’s predictions were investigated. Furthermore, the reasons why
the proposed model is not be able to account for the effect of reverberation were analysed. The

and (iv) averaging the resulting accumulated correlation across channels. This section describes the
effect of this computation in terms of the internal model representations.

more difﬁcult to ﬁnd similarities between segments.
Three variations of the model were studied: the original proposed model, an alternative version
that did not include the removal of silent frames and, ﬁnally, a modiﬁed model with linear averaging
of the correlation values across time. The results for all the experimental conditions are shown in
Figure 6.3.
The removal of silent frames seems to only inﬂuence the results in the case of spectral
subtraction, resulting in an underestimation of the SRTs of around 1 dB (RMSE = 1.9 dB).
This is not a critical deviation, but it is argued that the logic behind this processing stage holds,
since segments without speech should not affect speech intelligibility. However, the minor effect in
the overall results points to the possibility of removing this processing stage in the future in the aim
of simplicity and computational efﬁciency.
On the other hand, the computation across time of the correlation values shows a more
pronounced effect. The main inﬂuence is shown in the case of ﬂuctuating noise. Without the
accumulative integration, the model cannot account for the human data (RMSE = 4.7 dB) . This
is clear in the cases of the SAM and ISTS interferers. The accurate prediction of the ’Cafe’
interferer’s SRT is due to the fact that there is no decrease of SRT between the training and the test
condition, but a slight underestimation of the training condition that allows a correct prediction of
the ´Cafe’ datum, even when not detecting any making release. The same reason allowed STOI
to predict correctly this condition (see section 5.2). The model is only able to account for one
case of ﬂuctuating interferers, the reversed talker. This is the same behaviour shown by the STOI
model that uses the same linear averaging in time of the correlation values, even though it used
ﬁxed time windows in its analysis. The predictions for speech in the presence of reverberation
are also affected by the change in computation. In this case, the model can account for the SRT
corresponding to T60 = 0.4 s, which is a slight improvement from the proposed model, although it
still cannot account for intelligibility in longer reverberation times.
In summary, the accumulated computation of the correlation values implies that the higher
modulation ﬁlters (fcmod > 32 Hz) are given more weight in the ﬁnal decision metric. This effect
helps the model to account for ﬂuctuating interferers, indicating that high modulations might be
important for the effect of “listening in the dips” as already indicated by Jørgensen et al. (2013).
However, for speech in the presence of reverberation, the extra weight of the higher modulations
negatively affects predictions of SRTs. Nonetheless, the improvement in predicting intelligibility in
reverberant conditions by using a linear average computation is small, indicating that the problems
of the model to account for intelligibility in the presence of reverberation may lay not in the
temporal computation of the correlation, but in the ﬁnal correlation metric and how it is transformed
into intelligibility scores.

6.3
Inﬂuence of the Modulation Filterbank in the Model’s
Predictions
The proposed model includes a modulation ﬁlterbank, in line with the mr-sEPSM model and
STI. In order to assess the impact of this stage, an alternative model was built, where the modulation
ﬁlterbank was replaced with a low-pass ﬁlter with a cut-off frequency of 150 Hz.
Nonetheless, the multi-resolution analysis was maintained, by means of parallel time analysis
with different time windows. The length of these time windows was kept as 1/fcmod, even though
the modulation ﬁlters were not included. In practical terms it means that the time analysis was
replicated over the same output of the low-pass ﬁlter with time windows with lengths ranging from
1 second to 3.9 milliseconds. Results for this model are shown in Figure 6.8 together with the
original model’s predictions.
Without a modulation ﬁlterbank, the model prediction’s become slightly worse, specially in
the case of masking release. The modiﬁed model can still account for conditions of stationary
noise (RMSE = 2.3 dB). In the same manner, data trends are accounted for in the case of phase
jitter distortions, although the intelligibility scores are slightly overestimated (RMSE = 28.0%).
The effect of spectral subtraction is slightly underestimated, compared to the original model,
although the trend is also predicted quite accurately (RMSE = 2.2 dB). In the case of ﬂuctuating
interferers the masking release effect is reﬂected in a reduction of SRTs, however this reduction is
overestimated for all cases except the CLUE-based conditions (RMSE = 7.7 dB).
These ﬁndings support the idea that the multi-resolution time analysis can, on its own, account
for the main trends in the human data in conditions of spectral subtraction and stationary interferers.
However, it is not sufﬁcient to accurately predict masking release. This results are in line with the
ﬁndings by Jørgensen et al. (2013), where the mr-sEPSM was analysed in an analogous manner to
evaluate the inﬂuence of the modulation ﬁlterbank stage. Furthermore, the low-pass modulation
version of the model cannot account for any case of reverberation conditions, not even for the lowest
reverberation time studied (T60 = 0.4 s). A condition that the proposed model overestimated, but
for which it could obtain an SRT prediction.
The critical contribution of a modulation ﬁlterbank is further supported by the fact that the
replacement of such a stage with a low-pass ﬁlter in the STI model, makes it not able to account for
the effect of reverberation. The behaviour of such a modiﬁed STI is further discussed in appendix

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
The model proposed in the present thesis is inspired by mr-sEPSM (Jørgensen et al., 2013)
and STOI (Taal et al., 2011a). The combination of the auditory preprocessing of the mr-sEPSM
and a decision metric inspired by STOI leads to a “hybrid” model that extends the performance of
the two “source”models. It generalizes the results of STOI by accounting for intelligibility in the
presence of ﬂuctuating interferes. It also works well for cases were speech is subjected to phase
jitter distortions, a condition the mr-sEPSM could not account for. Furthermore, it accounts for the
effect of spectral subtraction which makes it more general than the classic STI model. However, it
cannot account for conditions of speech under reverberation.
Inﬂuence of weightings in the correlation, frequency and modulation domains
The idea of combining auditory processing stages with a correlation based back-end has been
investigated before (Christiansen et al., 2010). In Christiansen et al.’s model, a correlation-based
back-end in combination with the auditory model presented by Dau et al. (1997a) was used to
obtain speech intelligibility predictions. The output of the model is obtained by calculating the
correlation in different segments of the input signals. These segments are categorized and weighted
depending on their rms level, assuming that each level-based category has a different inﬂuence in
intelligibility.
The STI also uses a weighting process, in this case applied to the different auditory bands.
The STI weighting implies the assumption that different frequency bands contribute in a different
extent to intelligibility. In contrast, the proposed model does not need any explicit weightings in
the frequency domain or modulation domain to account for the data in line with the approach in the
sEPSM (Ewert and Dau, 2000) and the STMI (Elhilali et al., 2003). It does, however, include an
implicit weighting of the correlation values due to the integration technique for the metric. When
the intermediate metric was analysed (see section 6.1), it was shown that by accumulating the
correlation values across time, more weight was given to the higher modulations (fc > 32 Hz) in
the ﬁnal metric.
51
==PAGE:66==
52
CHAPTER 7. DISCUSSION
Across-channel interactions and phase jitter distortions
Additionally to not including any explicit weighting, the model does not assume any across-
channel computation either. It was shown by Chabot-Leclerc et al. (2014) that an across-frequency
mechanism was needed for sEPSM to account for intelligibility in the case of phase jitter distortions.
The proposed model includes the auditory processing of mr-sEPSM and, by changing the decision
metric, can account for results in the presence of phase jitter. This points to the idea that such
an explicit across-channel mechanism may not be needed in an intelligibility model if the metric
assumed to be related to intelligibility is chosen appropriately.
The STI is also not able to account for phase jitter conditions, mainly due to the fact that it is
based on a temporal modulation analysis, disregarding phase aspects. Such phase effects, matter in
the case of jitter distortion. A similar phenomenon was observed in the mr-sEPSM, that cannot
account for phase jitter since, by working in the envelope power domain, also disregards phase
effects.
Role of the ﬁtting conditions in the reverberation predictions
The proposed model cannot account for effects of reverberation. It was investigated whether
the problem with reverberant conditions might arise from the fact that the model reference is a
clean dry signal (i.e., the inputs to the model are the clean dry signal and the reverberant noisy
mixture). If the reverberation effect dominates over the additive noise, then the correlation between
a dry and a reverberant signal would be very low,independent of the SNR. Other template-matching
based auditory models propose that the reference signal should not be a completely dry or clean
signal, but a supra-threshold template (Dau et al., 1996). However, in the proposed model, if a
reverberant clean signal was used as input together with the reverberant mixture, the intelligibility
scores obtained are close to 100% for each SNR and condition, pointing to the dominance of the
effect of reverberation over SNR in the correlation metric. Thus, the problem might be related to
the correlation’s sensitivity to reverberation. This is consistent with the studies of Goldsworthy and
Greenberg (2004) that pointed out that correlation-based extensions of the sSTI could not account
for such conditions.
It is argued that the metric is too sensitive to reverberation, making it insensitive to changes of
SNR if the training conditions for the model are assumed to be non-reverberant. The difference in
the range of the ﬁnal metric values across SNRs when obtained with a dry input and when obtained
with a reverberant input (see Figure 6.5) lead the model to provide incorrect predictions if the model
is ﬁtted to a dry condition. The classic STI model, that can account for the effect of reverberation,
includes a restriction in the range of its output values. The computation from its intermediate
metric, the MTF, to the ﬁnal STI score includes a logarithmic compression and a range clipping.
Such computational steps are not considered in the proposed model, whose intermediate metric has
also been shown to be sensitive to reverberation. It is argued here that a more compressed range
in the proposed model’s ﬁnal metric can be expected to account for the effect of reverberation in
==PAGE:67==
53
speech intelligibility.
Fluctuating interferers and masking release
The effect of masking release can be accounted for by the proposed model. This is an
improvement from the STOI model, which cannot predict the effect of “listening in the dips”. It is
argued that this is a result of including a multi-resolution time analysis and having an accumulative
computation of the correlation metric across the resulting time segments. As it was shown in
the analysis of the model, this accumulation process gives more weight to the correlation values
obtained from the outputs of the high-frequency modulation ﬁlters. This higher modulations were
shown to be of importance in order to predict intelligibility scores in the presence of ﬂuctuating
interferers by Jørgensen et al. (2013).
In contrast, STI was shown to fail in these conditions. With the STI, intelligibility scores close
to 100% were obtained for all SNRs when the interferer was ﬂuctuating. This is an overestimation
of the masking release effect, that could be due to the fact that the model cannot distinguish between
the modulations that come strictly from the noise and those of the speech when analysing the
processed mixture. When calculating the change in modulation depth, STI compares the modulation
depth of the clean speech with the modulation depth of the noisy mixture, that is dominated by the
modulated noise. This leads to high values of the MTF that, after computation, result in high STI
scores and an overestimation of the release of masking.
Role of the noise alone in conditions of spectral subtraction
Conditions with spectral subtraction processing can be accounted for by the proposed model as
well as by the mr-sEPSM and the STOI. The STI, in contrast, fails in this condition. It was argued
by Jørgensen and Dau (2011), that the sEPSM can account for the increased SRTs with increasing
over-subtraction factor, due to the fact that it analyses the distortions of the noise alone. Spectral
subtraction algorithms affects both the speech and noise enhancing their intrinsic modulations, but
they affect the noise modulation in a greater extent, leading to worse intelligibility. The STI cannot
account for this effect since it does not consider the changes in the isolated noise modulations.
However, both the STOI and the proposed model base their analysis in the clean (unprocessed)
signal and the noisy mixture, as does STI. It was shown that the correlation metric used in these
models can account for the distortions produced by spectral subtraction, even when not considering
the changes in the noise alone.
Comparison to other speech intelligibility mo
Overall, the proposed model is able to account for speech intelligibility in a wider range of
acoustic and processing conditions than the STOI (Taal et al., 2011a) and in at least one condition
where the mr-sEPSM (Jørgensen et al., 2013) cannot account for the data, the presence of phase
jitter. It is also much more general than the classic STI method (Houtgast and Steenekem, 1971;
==PAGE:68==
54
CHAPTER 7. DISCUSSION
Houtgast et al., 1980; IEC, 2005). However it has one clear limitation, since it is not able to account
for the effect of reverberation. Considering that in daily-life listening conditions the presence of
reverberation is quite common, this is a drawback for the model.
Nonetheless, the analysis of the results was promising and the internal correlation metric has
been shown to be sensitive to reverberation. More research into the physical and mathematical
implications of the metric and its range could lead to a more generalized model that could account
also for the data in this condition.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusions and Perspectives
Speech is a basic tool for human communication. It shapes the way on which people relate to
each other and their surroundings. Thus, it has been the focus of research from many perspectives.
One important aspect of this research has been the ability of humans to understand speech. From
early speech intelligibility studies (French, 1947; Fletcher and Galt, 1950), to standardized methods
(ANSI, 1969, 1997; IEC, 2005) and state-of-the-art models (Rhebergen et al., 2006; Taal et al.,
2011a; Jørgensen et al., 2013), the ﬁeld of speech intelligibility has been relevant for many
researchers worldwide.
This thesis, “Analysing the effects of auditory processing and the decision metric on speech
intelligibility prediction”, is placed within this research area, presenting a new intelligibility
prediction model. The main hypothesis driving this project has been that the combination of an
auditory processing mechanism, as the one proposed by Jørgensen et al. (2013) in the mr-sEPSM
and a correlation-based decision metric, as proposed by Taal et al. (2011a) in the STOI, could lead
to a more generalized speech intelligibility model. Such a model could, potentially, account for a
wider range of acoustic and processing conditions, than the two “‘source” models individually.
The proposed model carries out an analysis of the clean unprocessed speech and the noisy
mixture. Both signals are processed by means of an auditory ﬁlterbank. The envelopes of the
outputs of these auditory ﬁlters are then processed through a modulation ﬁlterbank, and the resulting
outputs are analysed with time windows of different durations depending on the centre frequency of
the given modulation ﬁlter. A cross-correlation between the resulting representations of the clean
signal and the noisy mixture is carried out. The correlation values are computed by accumulating
their values across time and then linearly averaging those accumulated values across modulation an
auditory ﬁlter to obtain the ﬁnal correlation-based metric that can be related to speech intelligibility.
The model was tested in conditions of speech with additive stationary noise, speech in the
presence of ﬂuctuating interferers, speech processed by means of spectral subtraction algorithms as
well as speech in the presence of reverberation and under the effect of phase jitter distortions. A
good agreement was found between human data and the proposed model predictions in the cases of
55
==PAGE:70==
56
CHAPTER 8. CONCLUSIONS AND PERSPECTIVES
stationary noise, phase jitter and spectral subtraction. In the presence of ﬂuctuating interferers, the
model could account for the main trends and the effect of masking release, although its accuracy
in relation to human SRTs was inferior compared to that of the mr-sEPSM. The proposed model
was found unable to describe the effects of reverberation. Further analysis showed that the internal
correlation metric is indeed affected by reverberation, but the computation of the metric and the
training conditions of the model did not allow a correct description of the data in these conditions.
In terms of speech masking release, high modulation frequencies (fc > 32 Hz) were found to
contribute substantially to the model’s predictions accuracy. Furthermore, the internal stages of
the model and their individual contributions to speech intelligibility predictions were analysed,
showing that the correlation integration analysis was critical for the model to account for the effect
of ﬂuctuating maskers.
Overall, the model was found to account for human data in conditions where STOI fails
(ﬂuctuating interferers) and in conditions where the mr-sEPSM cannot account for the data (phase
jitter distortions). Moreover, the present model was shown to be a much broadly applicable
framework than the STI, that can only account for data in the presence of stationary noise and
reverberation. However, the proposed model is clearly limited by its inability to account for the
effect of reverberation in speech intelligibility, that could actually be accounted for by mr-sEPSM
and STI.
In the future, it would be interesting to investigate the performance of the proposed model on
a wider range of conditions. Particularly, results with speech subjected to ideal time-frequency
segregation (ITFS) (Brungart et al., 2006; Christiansen et al., 2010; Taal et al., 2011b) can be of
special interest. It is hypothesized that, since the STOI could account for such a condition, the
proposed correlation-based model could also account for this type of processing, whereas it is
unknown whether the mr-sEPSM would be able to do so. However, it was out of the scope of
this project to investigate such a case. It remains, nonetheless, an open line of research to further
evaluate the proposed model. Furthermore, additional research should be directed to improving
the performance of the model in the presence of reverberation. There is potential to generalize its
performance in these conditions, as discussed in the analysis of the proposed model.

Inﬂuence of Signal Pre-processing in the mr-sEPSM
Performance under Reverberation
The model that showed better results in the presence of reverberation in the current study was
the mr-sEPSM (Jørgensen et al., 2013). However, it was found that the pre-processing of the input
of the model was a key factor on its performance.
The original mr-sEPSM uses, in the case of reverberation, two inputs: the reverberant speech
and noise mixture and the reverberant noise. They are obtained from the original dry signals by
convolving them with the impulse response of a room with a given reverberation time. Their lengths
are adjusted by taking only the signal until its envelope has decayed to a value lower than 5% of its
maximum. Such processing is illustrated in Figure A.1, where an example of noisy speech with an
