Abstract
It is very common for children to experience anxiety and distress when they visit the Pedi­
atric Emergency Department (PED) of Herlev Hospital due to the unknown environment,
medical procedures and uncertainty about their illness. If these negative feelings are
sustained for long periods of time, they can lead to complications during treatment and
long­term health consequences, creating maladaptive and uncooperative behaviours, es­
pecially during examinations and procedures.
The purpose of this Master’s Thesis is to design, develop, and evaluate a tool to reduce
anxiety in children aged 3 to 7 years old in the common waiting area of Herlev Hospital
PED, waiting to be called in for triage. The solution aims to reduce anxiety levels in
children and promote parental involvement. It consists of an educational video based on
modelling principles where a friendly character, Søren Frø, provides children and their
caregivers with age­appropriate medical information about the triage. The intervention
features animations, colourful visuals, sound effects, and dynamic elements designed
using the digital FIGMA software to enhance understanding and appeal among pediatric
patients and their caregivers.
This study follows a double­diamond approach, conducting literature research, and field­
work, such as observation, shadowing and interviews with healthcare professionals to
gather information on anxiety interventions and parent­child interactions, explore the prob­
lem space and inform the solution’s design. The solution underwent an iterative process
of prototyping and user testing to ensure its effectiveness and usability. The final test was
conducted with 8 parent­child dyads, where children’s anxiety was measured using the
modified Yale Preoperative Anxiety Scale (mYPAS), and parent­child behaviours were
observed based on control and warmth dimensions. The intervention was well­received,
with 16 QR code scans over two weeks and qualitative data showed that the video was en­
gaging for parents and children, praising its educational and entertainment value. Results
were promising in reducing anxiety in children, with an average of around 33% reduction
after the video intervention. The solution also encouraged positive parent­child interac­
tion, observing a higher number of warm parental behaviours, and involved parents in
the video viewing, with 100% of parents reading the video for their children and 75% of
parent­child dyads tested doing the breathing exercise together.
Overall, this study showed the potential of an educational video solution in reducing anxi­
ety in children aged 3 to 7 years old in Herlev Hospital’s PED, highlighting the importance
of incorporating parental involvement in anxiety reduction interventions.

This introductory chapter provides the context and motivation for the thesis’s project and
presents the initial problem statement that originated from it.
1.1
Context and Motivation
In general, visits to the Paediatric Emergency Department (PED) are delicate for children
and their caregivers, as they take place during an emotionally vulnerable time [1, 2].
When patients are first admitted to the PED, they are surrounded by the unfamiliar and
chaotic hospital environment, filled with medical procedures and equipment and confused
about how to navigate the treatment process [3, 1].
This, combined with the uncertainty of the illness and waiting times, can generate neg­
ative emotions such as fear, anxiety or stress.[3, 4] Pre­procedural anxiety affects 60%
of patients in Paediatric Emergency Departments (PED) [5, 6], making it difficult for them
to cooperate with medical staff and resulting in a lower pain tolerance, which can compli­
cate procedures further and lead to negative long­term heath­related consequences [7,
8]. For this reason, it is necessary to adequately prepare children before medical pro­
cedures, using anxiety management tools that effectively reduce their pain and distress
levels [2, 9].
After being triaged in the PED, children spend long hours in waiting rooms before being
attended or treated; therefore, it is common practice to use this time to influence and
reduce their anxiety [10, 11] with interventions such as distraction techniques [10], hospital
play [2] or providing information about the procedure [12].
Figure 1.1: Children exhibited waiting for a medical procedure or treatment in Herlev
Hospital [13] and Aarhus Universitetshospital [14].
However, children are mostly accompanied by their parents, meaning that anxiety man­
agement mechanisms solely focused on the child may not deliver the desired outcome
due to the caregiver’s presence and influence on the child’s emotional state and coping
mechanisms [8, 15]. Studies have shown that parent and child anxiety are correlated
and highlight the influential role of parental presence during medical procedures on child
anxiety levels [16, 17, 18]. Hence, it is necessary to consider the parents’ presence and
involve them in the anxiety interventions, as they are also susceptible to high levels of
anxiety during PED interventions, especially regarding their children’s health [5, 19].

This Thesis originates from ongoing research and projects in collaboration with Herlev
Hospital, with the main goal of creating a healthy environment in PED waiting rooms
that improves the child’s well­being and guarantees a smoother process. The motivation
behind this Thesis is to explore the solutions that integrate parents into anxiety reduction
interventions to foster joint engagement and effectively create a supportive environment
to reduce the children’s anxiety before receiving treatment.
1.2
Initial Problem Statement
Current anxiety reduction strategies are typically focused solely on the child, forgetting the
influence parents have on their child’s hospital experiences and emotional responses.
Consequently, these interventions may fall short of fully addressing paediatric anxiety.
Recognising these limitations, this thesis will investigate the following problem statement:
How might we integrate parents into anxiety reduction interventions for
pediatric patients in PED waiting rooms?
The problem statement originates from recognising the vulnerable state of children and
their caregivers during PED visits. While interventions to reduce children’s anxiety exist,
the presence and influence of parents on their child’s emotional state must be considered.
Therefore, in order to deliver effective anxiety reduction programmes, it is necessary to
include parents in these interventions and seek to foster joint engagement between par­
ents and children to create a supportive environment. At the same time, these solutions
need to be accessible and feasible in the PED waiting room setting.

Herlev Hospital
Herlev Hospital has been at the forefront of innovation and medical care since its estab­
lishment in 1976. Their mission is to contribute to research, deliver high­quality services
and patient­centred care, empower them with knowledge and shape the future of medicine
[20]. In 2021, the Emergency Department “Akutmodtagelse” and the Department of Chil­
dren and Youth “Afdeling for Børn og Unge” were inaugurated, treating infants and ado­
lescents from 9 municipalities [21].
Figure 2.1: Photo of the main entrance of Herlev Hospital by Jesper Ludvigsen [22].
Herlev Hospital’s Department of Children and Youth has been catered to cover the needs
of children and their families, being known for their predisposition to include parents in
the child’s hospital stay and procedures, constantly seeking feedback and collaboration.
The Department of Children and Youth has 5 main sections: 1 treatment for newborns, 1
centre for social paediatrics, 2 clinics and the PED [23].
The PED comprises the Children and Youth Reception, “Børne­ og Ungemodtagelsen”
which takes care of young patients under 18 with any illness (i.e. concussions, appen­
dicitis, stomach pain, cystitis and others), excluding any injuries or lacerations to arms or
legs [24].

2.2
Previous and current work at Herlev Hospital
To date, several research studies have been conducted around the theme of reducing
child anxiety in Herlev Hospital’s PED. The latest projects surround diverse approaches,
all of them targeting exclusively infants.
Previous work includes a project by Zhang [25] that utilised Augmented Reality to de­
liver age­appropriate information to children using LEGO­themed games and AR cards
to reduce anxiety and enhance coping skills. The study demonstrated the feasibility and
effectiveness of the intervention with a small sample of 3­7­year­old children in Herlev
Hospital.
Figure 2.3: Wireframes from Jiajing Zhang’s AR LEGO game [25]
Emelie Kjeldsen’s master thesis [26] combined the detection of anxiety through biometrics
using a wearable wristband and the development of a tablet­game intervention based on
Cognitive Behavioural Therapy. This study, targeting children aged 5­8, validated the
feasibility of implementation in the PED and received positive feedback from children,
parents, and nurses.

Another project was conducted concerning machine learning models to assess children’s
anxiety levels better using portable devices capable of reading biosignals (i.e. heart rate,

2.3
Anxiety and assessment tools
The definition of anxiety can be distinguished between trait and state anxiety. On the
one hand, trait anxiety is considered a baseline, remaining constant over time and part
of an individual’s personality, reflecting their general tendency to experience anxiety [28,
29, 30]. On the other hand, state anxiety refers to a situational condition determined by
transitory and subjective emotions, which could be feelings of nervousness, tension, or
worry that fluctuate over time. Preoperative anxiety is an example of state anxiety that
arises during events that take place before a medical procedure [28, 29].
Over the years, studies and reviews have explored ways to evaluate preoperative anxiety,
differentiating between biometric and human assessment strategies [30, 31]. Biometrics
include physiological or neuroendocrine measures, such as blood pressure, cortisol lev­
els, heart rate, and skin conductance. However, these tools are less commonly used in
children as they require physical equipment, like electrodes, or samples (i.e. blood, urine,
saliva) to measure them [30, 32].
Human or behavioural assessments entail either self­reporting or observational scales.
Among these, various instruments have been recently defined to evaluate anxiety levels
in adults and children. The most commonly used instruments are:

• State­Trait Anxiety Inventory (STAI): This self­report anxiety assessment was de­
fined by Spielberg (1989) and was considered for many years the standard. [28,
33] It includes an adult (STAI) and children (STAIC) version for those over 5 years.
It consists of two 20­item questionnaires rated on a 4­point scale measuring state
and trait anxiety, where a higher score indicates higher anxiety levels [34, 35].
• Modified Yale Preoperative Anxiety Scale (mYPAS): This observer­rated tool for
preoperative anxiety was developed and validated by Kain et al. [28] in 1997 with
51 children aged 5 to 12. It includes 27 items distributed into 5 categories: activity,
emotional expressivity, state of arousal, vocalisation, and use of parents related to
children’s behaviours. The score ranges from 22.5 to 100, with higher scores indi­
cating higher anxiety. The mYPAS has proven to be a robust anxiety measurement
tool, with strong intra­ and interobserver reliability across all five categories [34, 35].
A breakdown of the items in each category can be seen in Appendix B.1.
• Visual Analog Scale (VAS): Commonly used as a self­report measure of anxiety
and pain, this scale consists of a 100 mm line depicting 2 extremes of behaviour, for
example, not anxious and very anxious [29, 34]. Scores typically range from 1 to 10,
with higher scores meaning higher anxiety levels [34]. A version of this scale, The
Wong­Baker Faces, was developed featuring faces expressing different emotions,
with a numerical rating assigned to each face. This tool can be used by parents and

4
State of the Art
Given the nature of the PED, this section will focus on the most common anxiety interven­
tions used for day procedures, where the patient is admitted and discharged on the same
day and that have been previously conducted with children or their parents as the primary
intervention group. The different strategies and programs will be presented, explaining
their principles and components, providing practical examples and analysing their effec­
tiveness in reducing distress in children and parents. A comprehensive list of the papers
and studies reviewed for this section can be found in Appendix B.4.
In the broader context, anxiety reduction interventions can be divided into pharmacologi­
cal and non­pharmacological categories [6]. This section will primarily focus on the latter,
detailing non­pharmacological methods due to their prominence and safety in PED set­
tings.
4.1
Pharmacological Strategies
Pharmacological interventions are the standard practice in hospitals to reduce anxiety,
primarily involving sedatives or anti­anxiety drugs, such as midazolam, diazepam or ke­
tamine [37, 39, 6], administered to the child moments before treatment. The most com­
mon choice to dilute anxiety for in­day treatment is midazolam [8, 37, 30] due to its rapid
effect, short life span and versatility of administration (i.e.intravenously, intranasal, intra­
muscular) [37, 30]. It can be orally administered, making it easy and non­invasive, often
camouflaged with sweet syrups, making children more prone to take them [39].
Knaeps, Putte, and Velde [39] reviewed the use, safety and effectiveness of pharmaco­
logical strategies to alleviate anxiety in children, found that oral midazolam has been used
in 49 out of 85 cases, highlighting its fast onset time, high tolerance and few side effects.
However, in about 25% of cases, children may become agitated or panic, jeopardising
the medical personnel’s safety and distress and requiring physical restraint by nurses or
family or general anaesthesia to facilitate minor interventions [10, 30].
The use of pharmaceutical premedication comes with side effects such as delayed re­
covery, interference with anaesthetics, breathing problems, and loss of balance [39, 30,
31]. Another downside of this type of intervention is their increased incidence of mal­
adaptive behaviour and their specific time, meaning that they are only helpful during the
time before the procedure in question, not providing children with any skills for anxiety
management. If faced again with a similar situation, children would relapse into the same
negative attitudes, needing the administration of anxiolytics to calm them down [30]. Due
to these disadvantages, non­pharmacological interventions are becoming more popular
and present in hospitals [31].
4.2

According to Agbayani, Fortier, and Kain [47] and Wang et al. [31], non­pharmacological
interventions can be divided into 2 main categories: informative and behavioural. Infor­
mative methods focus on educating children about their medical procedures and prepar­
ing them for what to expect and the outcomes. They are the standard practice in most
hospitals due to their straightforward approach and low cost [10, 47, 31, 48].

4.2.3
Social and Emotional Support
Social and emotional support is essential for managing anxiety among children in the PED.
Parents, medical personnel, child life specialist or even their favourite toy play a vital role
in providing reassurance and creating a safe, comfortable environment for children [8, 9].
Child Life Specialists
Child Life Specialists are medical personnel professionally trained in child development
[6] that employ tailored preparation strategies to reduce anxiety during the waiting period
[30]. They facilitate procedural information, teach coping skills, and offer encouragement
and reassurance [48]. Brewer et al. [38] found that children aged 5­11 who received
preparation from a child life specialist had significantly lower anxiety scores compared to
those without the intervention. Child life specialists also play a role in reducing parental
anxiety, as they provide parents with the needed knowledge to support their child [38,
6]. However, nurses or other medical staff usually fulfil this role due to costs and logistic
factors [6].
Nurses
Nurses can create a therapeutic environment just by being present and engaging in open
conversations about the child’s fears, concerns, and coping strategies [8, 6]. Nurses of­
fer age­appropriate reassurance (i.e. telling them the procedure is harmless), encourage
children to express themselves and ask questions and use non­verbal communication
(i.e. smiling, eye contact) to build trust [2, 38, 56]. They highlight the intervention’s posi­
tive aspects and outcomes through positive reappraisal, fostering optimism and effective
coping in patients and their families [6]. Lastly, through positive reinforcement, such as
verbal praise or gratitude (”Thank you for staying still,” ”You are so brave”) or small re­
wards (stickers, toys), nurses encourage positive and cooperative behaviours [56].
Nurses also support caregivers by encouraging them to share their feelings and engage in
their child’s care, facilitating their emotional processing and strengthening parental sup­
port networks. This reinforces caregivers coping abilities and helps them better under­
stand their child’s experience [4, 6].

age­appropriate activities [2, 6]. These interventions have many benefits, such as gener­

4.2.5
Complementary and Alternative Medicine
Complementary and alternative medicine (CAM) strategies are unique interventions that
support other non­pharmacological techniques. This category includes music therapy,
Hypnosis, aromatherapy, massage acupuncture and environmental design [47].
Waiting room environment
The waiting room experience significantly influences patients during the preoperative pe­
riod. Factors such as long wait times, service quality, and information delivery [8, 9] can
lead to negative feelings like boredom, frustration, and fear, maintaining high anxiety lev­
els [1, 4, 5]. Therefore, waiting rooms must create a relaxing environment that fosters
comfort and safety for children and their families [9]. Ideally, young patients should be
taken to individual private waiting rooms away from the chaotic PED and external stimuli
(i.e. children crying) that can heighten their anxiety. [9]
Standard PED waiting rooms aim to improve the waiting experience and relieve stress by
facilitating play and socialisation. They feature multi­functional and child­friendly spaces
(i.e.
playrooms, outdoor areas) [4, 2], colourful walls, pictures and diversified activi­
ties, such as distraction stations with toys and games [38] Structural enhancements like
video and music systems to create a low sensory environment (dimmed lights and nat­
ural sounds) can also help calm children [8, 9, 40]. Panda, Garg, and Shah [54] study
with children aged 6­11 found that most preferred waiting rooms with music, play areas,
natural light, and aquariums and televisions.
However, caregivers are also affected by the waiting room experience, reacting adversely
to prolonged waiting times and environmental factors (i.e. cleanliness, spaces for adults)
[3, 1, 4]. The studies conducted by Li et al. [2] and Li, Lopez, and Lee [19] showed that
parents felt less distressed and reassured when they observed their child playing and
socialising.
Weber [55], demonstrated that playful waiting rooms with toys, books, and games effec­
tively alleviate anxiety in children, creating familiar environments and making them feel
more at ease compared to environments shared with adults.

Music Therapy
During music therapy, trained staff use music together with relaxation or diversion tech­
niques to relieve the child’s anxiety [10, 6, 30]. It is increasingly popular among hospitals
due to its safe, affordable and non­invasive characteristics [10, 6, 31].
Listening and creating music are both approaches utilised in music therapy. Listening to
music reduces nervous activity, regulates heart and respiratory rate and blood pressure
and distracts them from pain [30, 31]. Diversion techniques, such as songwriting, playing
musical instruments or improvisation, can be an emotional outlet for children, helping them
express their feelings through music [6, 30].
Aromatherapy
This intervention uses concentrated essences and oils extracted from aromatic plants
(i.e. lavender, rose, and citrus) to treat both physical and emotional symptoms related to
preoperative anxiety (i.e. pain, nausea, stress). It does not require licenced experts or
equipment and is easily administered via massage (skin absorption), ingestion, or inhala­
tion [31, 40].
Acupuncture and Acupressure
Treatment originating from China utilises needling specific points through the patient’s skin
for therapeutic purposes and to balance patients’ well­being [31, 47, 40]. Acupressure, a
simpler alternative, uses light pressure on specific body points to reduce pain perception
and is being studied for use in children [47].This anxiety management modality is rapidly
administered and inexpensive but requires specialised professionals [30, 40]
Massage
Massage involves the manipulation of soft tissue for therapeutic intervention, generating a
sensation of well­being [31, 40]. There is an extended variety of massages (i.e. Swedish
massage, shiatsu) that help release endorphins that enhance positive feelings, reducing
pain, blood pressure and heart rate. Hand massages are common due to their cheap,
simple and non­invasive characteristics [31, 40].
Hypnosis
Hypnotic interventions aim to reduce patients’ awareness of external stimuli and real­
ity, leading them into a trance state where their perceptions, feelings, thoughts, and be­
haviours can be influenced [56, 40]. This psychological interaction is based on dissocia­
tion principles by focusing their attention on ideas and images and absorption into imagi­
nation [8, 30, 40]. Hypnotherapists first help patients relax and voice positive suggestions
to focus their attention on pleasant scenarios with an emotionally strengthening element
(i.e. pride, confidence) [8, 31, 56].
4.2.6
CBT and Relaxation
Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is an anxiety reduction technique that combines be­
haviour modification therapies with cognitive interventions [31, 56]. The behavioural com­
ponent emphasises developing adaptive behaviours and skills to counterpart harmful ex­
periences [31, 57]. At the same time, the cognitive aspect aims to restructure biased
thoughts about the procedures by identifying inaccurate thinking and altering dysfunc­
tional behaviours to improve their emotional state [31, 40, 56].
CBT educates children about the relationship between thoughts, feelings and behaviour
and teaches them coping skills to help them face threatening stimuli and regain control
over disruptive feelings and cognitions [56, 40]. CBT is considered the standard treatment

for anxiety disorders and includes techniques such as relaxation, guided imagery and
systematic desensitisation (graded exposure) [9, 31, 57]
Systematic desensitisation or Graded exposure
This approach aims to reduce anxiety through the gradual exposure to fear­inducing stim­
uli. Children are brought to a relaxation state or presented with positive reinforcements
and are slowly introduced to the treatment, starting with superficial and least painful inter­
ventions. This progressive exposure builds tolerance and modifies the child’s emotional
response, reducing their anxiety [56].
Relaxation
Coping skills to manage stress and promote relaxation within the CBT framework are also
beneficial in reducing preoperative anxiety [47]. It is based on the principle that a patient
cannot be stressed if they are physically relaxed, relaxation activities involve repetitive
exercises or actions, progressively practising and assimilating skills and can be delivered
in person via video or audio recordings [47, 56]. Various methods can be utilised, including
deep breathing (diaphragmatic), muscle relaxation (whole body or parts) and mindfulness
approaches such as meditation and guided imagery [47, 40].
Deep breathing is a breath conditioning technique characterised by deep and controlled
inhalation and exhalation using the diaphragm muscle, providing more oxygen to the body
and decreasing heart rate.[47, 56]. It is straightforward to perform and can be quickly
adopted by patients in every setting [56]. Similarly, progressive muscle relaxation focuses
on controlled relaxation of targeted muscle groups or joints until complete physical body
relaxation [47, 56].
Yang et al. [57] looked at a computer­assisted cognitive behavioural therapy (cCBT) pro­
gram that used interactive modules and videos to teach cognitive therapy and techniques,
such as muscle relaxation, breathing exercises, and mindfulness. The intervention group
showed less anxiety, better physical responses during surgery, faster recovery, and higher
satisfaction with nursing care.
Guided imagery
Guided imagery is a therapy technique where children modify negative feelings by con­
structing sensory and affective experiences that they can later recall or return to during
high­anxiety experiences in the PED [31, 38]. These skills work similarly to distraction by
focusing the child’s attention on non­procedure­related tasks suggested by the medical
staff or chosen by the patient [47, 56].
Children are asked to use their imagination to create detailed and pleasant places or
images, including visual, auditory, olfactory or emotional stimuli [8, 47, 40]. This method
consciously guides the patient to psychological and emotional well­being, leading the body
to total physical relaxation [31, 56].

social support from family or friends for companionship and social entertainment [40].

engaging activities [2], but work best with children younger than 12 [10] or avoidant copers
[6]. While toddlers and preschoolers typically respond better to passive distraction due
to their lack of critical thinking and low cognitive processing, older children engage better
in verbal distractions or interactive games [53]. It is the least effective among parents as
they perceive them as diverting attention from medical care [10, 40].
CAM approaches vary in effectiveness. Music and aromatherapy have mixed results while
waiting room environments with natural sounds and lights are effective for all ages, includ­
ing parents [40]. Acupuncture and hypnosis are challenging for children to participate in
[47] but have shown high effectiveness in parents and young adolescents [8, 40].
While cognitive­behavioural therapy (CBT) interventions are less effective for toddlers due
to their cognitive limitations, adults and older children have reported positive outcomes
with relaxation techniques, such as deep breathing, as they can manage their anxiety [40].
The high suggestibility of children makes it easier for them to be immersed in imaginary
or “make­believe” scenarios and exercises, such as guided imagery [8].

5.1
Parental influence and involvement in the ED
Caregiver presence before and during a medical procedure can positively impact a child’s
behaviour and decrease distress [9, 19, 16]. In PEDs is common practice to involve a
paternal figure as a way to calm children before and while administering anaesthesia [17,
12]. Parents can assist with anaesthesia induction by helping their child put on the mask
or preparing them for the procedure [9, 31]. Their presence serves as a familiar face
for the children during the interventions, creating a sense of safety inside the unknown
hospital environment [19].
Figure 5.2: Mother using mask to administer anaesthesia to child [14]
In the trial by Ejiri et al. [17], it was observed that parents who were actively involved in
the anaesthesia induction not only diminished their child’s anxiety levels but also reported
lower distress themselves, fostering a sense of realisation and satisfaction around helping
their child. At the same time, this involvement increases parents’ trust towards medical
professionals and improves their perception of the quality of the service received [19, 17].
However, this is a double­edged sword, as parents’ power over the child’s emotional state
can backfire if the parents exhibit negative emotions [8, 19, 6]. Wright et al. [30] found
that children of anxious parents or with poor healthcare attitudes displayed more distress
than those without parental presence during anaesthesia [16]. Therefore, according to
Dreger and Tremback [6] and Brewer et al. [38] it is crucial for medical staff to monitor
the accompanying caregivers and limit their influence on the child’s distress to ensure no
adverse feelings interfere with the medical procedure.
5.2
Parent­Child Interactions
Parent­child relationships are pivotal in shaping children’s physical, cognitive, and social
development. As children learn to think, communicate, and navigate the world, they draw
from their interactions with their parents [61, 62]. Baumrind [63] and Rapee [62] estab­
lished warmth and control as the 2 key dimensions in parenting behaviours and styles
that account for a small but significant variance in children’s anxiety levels [64].
The warmth dimension, described as traditional care, refers to involvement and support or,
on the reverse side, rejection [62, 65]. It reflects how parents express love to their children
and respond to their needs and demands by supporting their opinions and feelings and
ensuring their emotional well­being [63, 66]. Control, or conversely autonomy, involves
directing the child’s behaviour through discipline management and protecting the child’s
physical well­being [62, 65]. Control is often reinforced by setting standards of behaviour,
but it is not a measure of intrusiveness [61]. Appendix B.5 shows a summary of the
constructs and behaviours associated with each dimension.

5.3
Parent­Child Anxiety Interventions
Parent and child anxiety interventions (PCAI) not only influence anxiety in parents and
children but also affect the entire system, including the transmission of anxiety between
them. Based on the literature and background research, PCAI encompasses studies and
related work where parents are actively involved in child anxiety interventions or prepa­
ration programs designed to target anxiety levels in both parents and children. Including
parents in children’s preparation methods serves a similar purpose to parental involve­
ment in anaesthetic induction, creating a familiar environment and influencing anxiety
transmission, as discussed in Section 5.1.

PCAI has shown significant advantages across various approaches and strategies, such

6.1
Framework: The double Diamond
The design process of this project follows the Double Diamond Model, a design thinking
process developed in 2004 by the British Design Council[71]. It consists of 2 diamonds
representing the problem and solution space, each with a divergent thinking phase, where
the issue is widely explored, and a converging phase, focused on defining the problem/­
solution. The double diamond can be divided into 4 stages:
• Discover:This stage aims to explore and understand the problem by gathering in­
sights. This was done through desktop research and fieldwork, corresponding to
chapters 5 and 7 of this thesis.
• Define:In this stage, the data of the previous stage are filtered and evaluated to
narrow down and clearly define the challenge that needs to be addressed. This
phase encompasses the methods used to distil the findings, documented in chapter
8, and defining the Final Problem Statement in section 9.
• Develop:As part of the second diamond, this step focuses on developing and cre­
ating a diversity of ideas for the previously defined problem. Chapter 10 follows the
process of exploring the solution space, using brainstorming, morphology charts
and design walkthroughs to obtain the MVP concept.
• Deliver: This last stage encompasses the testing and iteration of the solutions,
allowing for gathering feedback and implementing improvements. This phase in­
cluded qualitative and quantitative testing, all sprints can be seen in chapter 11.

6.2
Methods Toolbox
This section defines the main design methods that aided the process of project develop­
ment, differentiating between problem exploration, concept development and prototype
evaluation. Most of the methods and tools utilised in the project were derived from The
UX Book by Hartson and Pyla [73].
Problem Space
After the initial research, various fieldwork methods were employed to explore and gain
insights into the problem space. Observation studies were undertaken to understand the
context inside the PED, including physical space and workflows, and capture the expe­
riences and interactions of parent­child dyads. Interviews and shadowing sessions were
conducted with healthcare professionals of the PED to provide insights into nurses’ roles
and interactions with patients and their families and gather their perspectives on existing
anxiety reduction strategies and challenges related to children’s anxiety inside the PED.
Utilising the facts, observations and information gathered from the fieldwork and desktop
research, a synthesis [73] of the data was conducted. User Journey mapping [74], iden­
tified the anxiety­inducing points of the PED visit by mapping out children’s experience
step­by­step. An Ecosystem Map [75] helped visualise Herlev Hospital’s current network
and understand the relationships influencing children’s and caregiver’s experiences. Em­
pathy mapping helped articulate what users hear, see, think, and feel and identify their
needs. Finally, based on the analysis of the research findings, the scope, problem state­
ment and design requirements [76] were established to guide the concept development.
Solution Space
Brainstorming [73] was employed as an ideation technique to foster diverse ideas for the
intervention. The Morphology chart [77] helped analyse and evaluate the plausible solu­
tions, dividing them into core dimensions. The 7 concepts resulting from the morphology
were evaluated using a Weighted Objective Matrix [78]. The top 3 concepts were pre­
sented in a design walkthrough [79] to Gitte, where she reviewed them based on her
insights and experience of the PED environment, identifying any potential issues or im­
provements to contribute to the decision­making. The chosen concept and its character­
istics were supported by previous studies targeting anxiety in children and their parents
and the theory behind parent­child interactions and age­appropriate strategies.
The concept was developed and prototyped in Figma [80], following an agile and iterative
approach divided into 4 sprints to iterate, gather feedback from users and implement
modifications [73]. The concept was first evaluated with design and innovation students
using a Think Aloud test [81] for them to voice their opinions. The second sprint included
a usability test [73] where participants’ anxiety scores were noted, and questions were
asked to gather their feedback. The third sprint was a quantitative test measuring different
UX metrics of engagement, satisfaction, and the number of interactions with the QR code
[82]. To measure engagement, the theory by Schønau­Fog and Bjørner [83] was used,
and survey questions were based on Baumrind [61] and Kulkarni et al. [58] studies. Lastly,
the final sprint was based on observation data, including anxiety and parental behaviours,
and qualitative feedback was gathered.

During shadowing and interviews, various techniques were employed and mentioned by

Final Problem Statement
After analysing the findings and having a deeper understanding of the problem space
and context, this section defines the scope of the project to update the initial problem
statement and establish the design requirements for the possible solution.
9.1
Scope
The scope of the solution is children aged 3­7 years old waiting to be attended for triage
or treated for a procedure inside the PED. The age range was chosen based on the
observations, shadowing sessions and the Age matrix (Appendix B.2), which show that
younger children struggle the most to cope with anxiety and fearful stimuli.
The solution will target the implementation of preventative strategies during the waiting
period in the PED. Engaging children in anxiety interventions during this “free” period is
crucial, as it can be challenging for them to focus on such interventions while healthcare
personnel is attending or treating them. Insights gathered during fieldwork (see Appendix
C.5), show that children exhibit high levels of anxiety upon seeing medical equipment
or healthcare personnel, making it more difficult to implement anxiety interventions in
the examination or triage room. Additionally, during interviews, nurses emphasised the
relevance of preventing maladaptive behaviours in children ( see Appendix C.4).
The solution will focus on Parent­Child Anxiety Interventions (PCAI) due to the extensive
information and literature available compared to Transmission Interventions., showing
favourable results with different PCAI strategies [31, 48] . Additionally, PCAI interven­
tions also have the potential to affect the whole system: child anxiety, parental anxiety
and transmission. However, the primary target is the children, as they face the most
stressful stimuli and have higher anxiety levels.
9.2
Final Problem Statement
The findings from the fieldwork studies have provided a better understanding of the prob­
lem space and helped identify patterns and possible improvements in Herlev Hospital’s
PED network. With a more precise definition of the current situation at Herlev Hospital,
the problem statement and research questions were revised to better guide the design
and implementation stages.
How might we design a tool to diminish pediatric patients’ anxiety in PED
waiting rooms by enhancing positive parent­child interactions?
1. What anxiety reduction strategies could be implemented to encourage parental in­
volvement and thereby facilitate positive parental behaviours?
2. How can the solution’s format be tailored to address the specific needs and prefer­
ences of the pediatric patient population and their parents?
3. How can the impact of the anxiety reduction intervention on children’s anxiety levels
be feasibly measured and evaluated?

Weighted Objective Matrix
In order to choose a concept, a Weighted Objective Matrix (WOM) was utilised. The eval­
uation criteria were chosen from some of the categories and requirements of the design
specification, and a weight of 1­5 was assigned in collaboration with Gitte.
• Effectiveness: How well does the solution address the primary goal of reducing
anxiety for both children and caregivers?
• Engagement: To what extent does the solution encourage joint engagement and
interaction between children and caregivers?
• Ease of Use: How intuitive and user­friendly is the solution for both children and
caregivers?
• Accessibility: How accessible is the solution to all patients, including those with
diverse needs and illnesses?
• Customizability: To what extent does the solution allow for tailoring to individual
users’ specific needs and preferences?
• Validation: How easy is testing and validating the solution, including gathering feed­
back and measuring user satisfaction?
• Low Workload Impact: How much does the solution impact the workload of nurses
and medical staff (5=lowest), including time and effort?
• Low Cost: What is the overall cost of implementing and maintaining the solution(5=lowest),
and does it align with budget constraints?
• Compatibility: How compatible is the solution with existing systems and infrastruc­

Concept description
The final solution is an educational video designed to prepare children and their parents
for triage at the PED, aiming to educate and comfort young patients. By providing a visual
tool, the aim is to explain triage in a child­friendly manner and empower both children and
parents. The video, which lasts approximately 4 minutes, showcases and explains the
main events and procedures a child might encounter upon their arrival in the PED, and
in particular, the steps involving triage. The video also features sound effects (i.e. frog
croaks, medical instrument feedback) and child­friendly music to highlight some of the

Strategies of the solution
A multi­strategy approach was adopted to cater the intervention for children and their
parents. The decision to implement these strategies was based on the theory highlighted
in Section 4.2.7, which identified the most effective anxiety reduction interventions for
children and adults. The strategies implemented are:
Informational
Providing parents with a written description of the triage process, together with an overview
of the purpose and procedures involved in the journey, helps alleviate anxiety in parents
[5, 30, 58]. Therefore, textboxes are incorporated as an informational strategy for parents
to address their anxiety regarding the uncertainty and need for knowledge of the PED.
Educational
The solution educational strategies to inform children and parents about triage in a clear
and accessible manner. The video explains each step of the process in simple terms,
portraying them as common and routine. The video combines these educational princi­
ples with modelling for children by including the character Søren Frø, who guides viewers
through the hospital journey and helps children learn and practice coping strategies, em­
powering them to navigate their hospital visits confidently.
Modelling: Søren Frø serves as a model, demonstrating positive behaviours and reac­
tions to help normalise the hospital experience By presenting a familiar and friendly figure
for children to emulate, the video helps them feel more comfortable during their visit.

ticularly enjoying humorous elements and the Søren Frø character. Parents appreciated
the initiative, finding it useful for preparing their children for their PED experience. They
praised the explanatory content and noted that the format helped their children feel more
at ease and engaged with the solution. Both children and parents generally understood
the video’s purpose and content. Minor usability issues were detected regarding the vol­
ume and clarity of the sound in the video on some of the mobile devices, which need to
be explored further in the future (see Appendix E.4.2).
Outcome
Based on the interviews, observations, mYPAS, and parenting behaviour data, the main
findings of this test were:
• Validated the solution potential of reducing children’s anxiety, as observed by the
decrease in mYPAS scores.
• Validated the generation of positive parenting behaviours. The solution involved par­
ents and facilitated interactions with children, contributing to a more comprehensive
and engaging experience for the children.
• Confirmed the video’s engagement and entertainment value, its design successfully
captured and maintained children’s attention.

12.1
Overall Results
The present project targeted parent­child dyads of children aged 3 to 7 visiting Herlev
Hospitals PED. An educational modelling videotape was designed to cater to both child
and parent needs and preferences. The videotape delivered relevant and age­appropriate
information about the triage process engagingly, aiming to educate and comfort viewers
to alleviate their anxiety.
A qualitative test was conducted, and results showed that the educational video reduces
children’s anxiety by around 33%, coinciding with the conclusions obtained by Kim et al.
[48] and Wang et al. [31] in their trials. However, the small sample of participants makes
it difficult to conclude these results were representative. The feedback obtained from the
test participants revealed that the solution’s information and content appealed to parents
and children. Children particularly enjoyed the main characters’ friendliness and humour
and the video’s different dynamic and colourful elements. In contrast, parents praised
the informational content of the video and its value in preparing children for triage. This
feedback aligns the theory behind the Effectivity table (Appendix B.3) and the Age Matrix
(Appendix B.2) and ratifies the positive results of similar interventions by Wakimizu et al.
[70].
This consistency highlights the efficacy of using educational videos to decrease preoper­
ative anxiety in children and parents, supporting the broader literature on PCAI (Appendix
B.4).
The qualitative test results also indicated that the solution effectively generates positive
parent­child interactions, observing more warmth than control parental behaviours while
participating in the intervention. These results, together with the observed reduction in
children’s anxiety, align with previous research by Baumrind [63] and [62], suggesting
that the intervention effectively promotes a more supportive and less anxious environ­
ment for children. By fostering warmth and reducing control behaviours, the intervention
encourages a more authoritative parent­child dynamic, known for its positive impact on
children’s effective coping and well­being under stressful situations [66, 63]. At the same
time, this reduction in control behaviours can also be associated with a reduction in par­
ent anxiety. As seen in multiple studies [65, 62, 67], high control behaviours are often
associated with increased anxiety in parents and unfavourable interactions with children.
In conclusion, this study addresses the gap in the existing literature by providing empir­
ical evidence on the role of parent­child interactions during anxiety reduction interven­
tions. The educational intervention reduced children’s anxiety and helped foster warmth
and supportive interactions between parents and children, highlighting the relevance of
addressing both child and parent needs and designing the intervention to actively involve
parents and create a positive and less stressful PED environment.

though not initially part of the current study’s purpose, the results showed increased partic­

educational video to patients in the PED. This finding aligns with the conclusions drawn

Research questions
At this stage, the following section will discuss whether the Final Problem Statement has
been addressed by answering the subquestions presented in section 9 of this thesis.
What anxiety reduction strategies could be implemented to encourage parental
involvement and thereby facilitate positive parental behaviours?
This thesis developed an anxiety reduction intervention consisting of an educational mod­
elling video, including informational strategies for parents, explaining the child’s journey
and medical procedures, and distraction strategies for children. Literature research indi­
cated that parent­child interactions with high­control behaviours often heighten children’s
anxiety and are directly correlated with parental anxiety [61, 62, 67]. Therefore, the inter­
vention was designed to include strategies that target both parent’s and children’s anxiety,
encouraging parental involvement and fostering positive parental behaviours.
Reviews and studies on parent­child anxiety Interventions (PCAI) revealed the effective­
ness of educational modelling videos in involving parents and reducing anxiety levels in
both parents and children [31, 48, 70]], validating the choice of the primary strategy of the
intervention. The intervention’s approach was further developed through an analysis of
anxiety factors, cognitive abilities and proven effective anxiety reduction interventions for
children and parents individually (see B.3). The analysis indicated that besides education,
distraction and emotional support were the most effective interventions for children aged
37, whereas informational strategies and CBT were more beneficial in parents.
A qualitative test confirmed the intervention’s potential to generate positive parental be­
haviours, evidenced by the higher number of warmth behaviours (5) compared to control
behaviours (2) observed while viewing the video. Common control behaviours included
supervision and socialisation demands, which, according to Baumrind [63], are not a mea­
sure of intrusiveness. Furthermore, parents actively participated in the intervention, where
100% of the subjects were observed reading to their child, helping clarify concepts and
laughing together at the video. Most parents were also seen taking part in the breathing
exercises with the children. Post­intervention, more positive interactions were noted, such
as parent and child sitting close together, playing or commenting on the video, suggesting
the intervention’s possible lasting effect.
In conclusion, based on the test results, this study suggests that a multi­approach inter­
vention focusing on educational strategies and incorporating distraction and informational
methods can encourage parental involvement and positive parental behaviours. This ap­
proach aligns with the existing literature, supporting the idea that addressing anxiety in
both parents and children can create a supportive environment and enhance their expe­
rience in the PED.

How can the solution format be tailored to address the specific needs and
preferences of the pediatric patient population and their parents?
To effectively tailor the solution for pediatric patients between 3­7 years old and their
parents, the content and design has to be carefully considered to ensure engagement
and appropriateness for the different age groups. The format of the educational video
intervention was informed by developmental theories, analysing the communication and
cognitive needs and anxiety factors of both groups (Appendix B.2).
Based on the research by Jaaniste, Hayes, and Von Baeyer [42] and Kolucki and Lemish
[41], the video incorporated bright colours, playful visuals and dynamic elements to en­
hance children’s understanding and accessibility. Additionally, the solution utilised clear,
child­friendly language and simple explanations with sensory­based information to cater
to children’s cognitive abilities and short attention spans (see Section 4.2.1 and Appendix
B.2). Familiar characters and objects, like Søren Frø and PED rooms, were also featured
to make the intervention enjoyable and normalise the medical setting for young patients.
Following Kulkarni et al. [58] and Kassai et al. [59] approach of incorporating elements
to anxiety reduction interventions to also cater to parents’ informational needs (Appendix
B.2), the video delivers a comprehensive overview of the triage, including who will be
involved, what instruments will be used, and why the triage is necessary. This dual focus
on children’s and parents’ needs ensures the solution properly addresses both groups.
The solution was iteratively revised and improved based on parent­child dyad input and
nurses’ feedback. Users’ comments and interactions with the solution helped refine the
video’s design elements and content, such as including more animations and colourful
visuals, reducing video length, clarifying the concept of triage and introducing sound to
increase engagement. Nurses’ contributions and knowledge of patients helped adjust the
pacing and language of the video, ensuring its accessibility and comprehension. Their
advice of incorporating the PED instruments and personnel, explaining the triage in a
structured and visual way and modifying the content to avoid confusion was crucial to
covey the essential aspects of triage appropriately and engagingly for children and their
parents. Their insights validated the video’s format, confirming its educational value and
highlighting the appeal of Søren Frø and the animations for children.
Qualitative test results supported the format’s effectiveness, with parents appreciating
the explanatory content and finding the video valuable for preparing them for triage. Ad­
ditionally, children found the video engaging and entertaining, particularly enjoying the
humorous elements and the character Søren Frø. Children also positively reacted to the
sensory information and step­by­step content, finding it easy to understand. These re­
sults are consistent with the developmental theory (Appendix B.2) and ratify the positive
results of similar formats by Wakimizu et al. [70]. However, minor usability issues with the
audio were identified, suggesting a need for improvement in future iterations.
In conclusion, the present study addressed the specific needs and preferences of the pe­
diatric population between 3­7 years old and their parents by adapting the video’s design
and content to both demographics and continuously integrating user and nurse feedback
to refine the solution. The positive reception of the video suggests that incorporating ele­
ments that target parents’ and children’s developmental needs, such as sensory informa­
tion, step­by­step instructions, or animations, makes the intervention more engaging and
effective. This approach aligns closely with the practices identified in the literature (Ap­
pendix B.2) and the format adopted by Wakimizu et al. [70], underscoring the relevance
of ensuring the intervention meets the audience’s cognitive and emotional needs.

How can the impact of the anxiety reduction intervention on children’s anxiety
levels be feasibly measured and evaluated?
Given the challenges posed by the PED setting, selecting an appropriate tool to measure
the solution’s effectiveness requires careful consideration. Through desktop research
and a comprehensive review of anxiety reduction interventions, various methods to eval­
uate children’s anxiety were identified. The most common methods used in trials were
the Wong­Baker FACES Rating Scale (WBF), Spielberger’s State­Trait Anxiety Inventory
(STAI) and the Modified Yale Preoperative Anxiety Scale (mYPAS).
The STAI was discarded due to its lengthy format and having to be answered twice, before
and after the intervention, which is impractical given the short 15­minute waiting period
before triage. At the same time, the vulnerable emotional state of patients and their fam­
ilies made it unrealistic to ask them to complete a 10­minute survey [34]. The WBF was
dismissed because it is also a self­report scale and would be used by nurses during triage,
which could confuse children. Consequently, it was decided to measure children’s anxiety
with the mYPAS, as it is an observer­based anxiety scale and allows the assessment to
be done without interacting with the child, hence not affecting their emotional well­being.
The mYPAS scale is widely used and considered a gold standard in anxiety assessment
due to its structured and clearly defined observation criteria [28]. However, its reliance
on observer­based scoring can introduce bias, particularly in interpreting where patients’
behaviours fall in the scale. This subjectivity is heightened if only one researcher is in
charge of the annotation of the results, not accurately portraying participants’ anxiety lev­
els. To correct this, a second observer was introduced to record the children’s anxiety
scores. Prior to the video viewing, the anxiety scores were noted before approaching
participants. Post­intervention anxiety scores were noted while the child’s attention was
focused on the video or a couple of minutes after finishing the intervention to avoid the
researcher’s presence influencing children’s behaviour.
During a qualitative test involving 8 parent­child dyads, the mYPAS consistently indicated
a reduction in children’s anxiety after viewing the educational video, with an average 33%
decrease (see Appendix E.4.1). These findings align with the existing literature [28, 35,
34] underlining the practicality and validity of the mYPAS as an effective observational
tool to asses preoperative anxiety in children.
Overall, this study proved that the mYPAS is a feasible tool to measure and evaluate the
impact of the educational video intervention on children’s anxiety levels. The mYPAS’s
non­intrusive and observer­based qualities, along with the promising test results and ap­
propriate precautions against observer bias, supported its suitability for use in the busy
and sensitive environment of Herlev Hospital’s PED. Future studies should incorporate
multiple observers and include comparative studies with a larger sample size and control
groups to validate the use of the mYPAS further.
In summary, and as previously seen in this section, the study addressed the Final Problem
Statement by designing an educational modelling video that reduces paediatric patients’
anxiety in the PED waiting room and enhances positive parent­child interactions. By incor­
porating a multi­strategy approach and focusing the format of the solution on both parents
and children aged 3­7 needs, the intervention adequately informed and supported them
during their PED visit.

Limitations:
Certain limitations were identified during the study that might affect the accuracy and
understanding of the results.
• Anxiety Scale: The use of the observer­based mYPAS during testing and observa­
tional studies may introduce subjectivity. Furthermore, relying on one person for the
interpretation of anxiety levels may not provide an accurate picture of participants’
experiences.
• Influencing Factors: Cultural background, socioeconomic status, and family dy­
namics play a role in parental engagement and child response in the PED. Addition­
ally, previous healthcare experiences can shape how parents and children behave
during medical interventions. Therefore, targeting the intervention to adapt to the
user population is crucial for the intervention to be effective.
• Parenting Style Complexity: During the observation and shadowing studies, the
3 parenting styles identified by Baumrind [61] were used. However, these 3 cate­
gories may not fully capture the complexity of individual parenting styles, as parents
may exhibit different styles in various situations. Therefore, utilising parenting be­
haviours, such as the table used for sprint 4 (see Appendix E.4.3, which reflects
specific actions that occur in the context of parenting styles [64], could have repre­
sented more accurately the observed interactions.
• Evaluation Methodology: During the second sprint, there was a low participation
rate from patients in the PED. This could have been because by directly approaching
patients for the test, they deviated from the intended user journey, where patients
and their families would voluntarily approach the video displayed on the common
area TV, affecting the participation and results.
• Survey group: The sprint 3 survey included patients not from the PED, as their
user journey closely resembles that of those waiting for triage. While the emotional
experiences in the common waiting room are comparable to those in the PED, the
relevance of the video’s information may vary for them.
• Barriers: Unfamiliarity with the researcher, who did not have the established trust
that medical personnel in the PED have, and language barriers, using English in­
stead of Danish to approach the dyads, might have affected parents’ and children’s
willingness and comfort in participating.
• Observer Effect: During the tests, the presence of researchers may alter partic­
ipants’ behaviour or lead to selective attention to certain aspects of the observed
interactions. At the same time, the act of approaching parent­child dyads for feed­
back on the solution could introduce a bias, as participants might feel compelled to
provide ”positive feedback.”
• Sample size:The sample used in the study was too small to accurately represent
the PED population waiting for triage. At the same time, one of the participants had
already been through triage, which could have influenced their responses.
• Circumstantial: Given the nature of the PED, concerns were raised about the en­
gagement of children who may be too sick or in pain to take part in the video activities
effectively.

12.4
Further Work
This thesis has examined the effectiveness of an educational video that encourages
parental involvement to reduce anxiety in pediatric parties rat Herlev hospitals PED. Be­
low, based on the results obtained from the study, the possible areas of improvement and
development identified for further work and consideration are presented.
Narrated Audio
Based on some feedback gathered during testing, participants suggested making a more
cartooney version of the video, having the text narrated. This was not implemented in the
final version for two main reasons: First, time constraints and the possibility of audio dis­
turbing other hospital visitors, and second, adding narrated audio would detract from one
of the solution’s key characteristics: encouraging positive parental behaviours by having
parents read the text to their children. Therefore, future iterations should investigate if the
solution should incorporate a narrated speaker to evaluate whether this addition hinders
the test’s purpose and diminishes its effectiveness.
QR code
Another aspect that should be addressed is whether the QR solution is optimal. Although
the initial purpose of the test was to display the video in the common waiting areas on TV,
due to complications with participation during testing, a new test with the QR code was
done to allow users to scan and watch the video independently, without pressure from
external parties, which had a higher participation rate. However, this change should be
properly addressed by conducting a test similar to that of Sprint 3 with the waiting area
TV and determining whether the QR code engages better with PED visitors, as the low
participation could be due to the test approach chosen for Sprint 2.
Improvements to the testing process
Upcoming iterations should encourage further refinements of the solution, ensuring its
educational and engaging values. More importantly, it should address the shortcomings of
this thesis in terms of testing limitations and number of participants so that the conclusions
are significant and reliable.
Nurse Involvement
Given the increased participation when nurses actively encouraged and recommended
patients and their families to watch the video, the effect of nurses’ involvement in the so­
lution should be thoroughly studied. Future research should explore how the guidance
or direct participation of trusted medical personnel in the intervention might influence the
interactions and engagement with the solution and assess whether this involvement pos­
itively impacts the intervention’s overall effectiveness.
Expansion of the solution
The possibilities of customisation for the solution could also be considered in the future.
The structure of the final solution allows for flexibility and expansion to other areas and
scenarios inside the PED, such as more videos including other procedures (i.e. Blood
samples) or inside other anxiety reduction interventions (i.e. private room tablet, hospital
website). This can create a narrative inside the PED journey and cater to diverse patients
and families and their needs and preferences.

Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to investigate how to reduce anxiety in children aged 3­7 years
old by involving parents in the waiting room in Herlev Hospital PED.
A literature review and desktop research uncovered the importance of tailoring interven­
tions to participants’ cognitive development and needs. The findings provided a structured
overview of effective anxiety interventions used in PED settings and approaches that have
successfully involved parents in anxiety reduction. Furthermore, limitations were revealed
concerning the lack of study on the potential influence of parental behaviours on child
anxiety. Fieldwork studies identified current practices in Herlev Hospital’s PED, revealing
various approaches used in the PED and the role of nurses in reducing children’s anxiety,
particularly highlighting the lack of family­focused strategies.
To address these needs, an intervention in the form of an educational video explaining
the triage process was created. The concept was iterated and refined with feedback,
interviews with healthcare professionals and tests with a total of 27 parent­child dyads.
During the development of this study, the influence of nurses’ involvement in the solution
was revealed, as the intervention received a higher number of participants when nurses
actively recommended PED patients to watch the educational video.
Qualitative results showed that the intervention was engaging and appreciated by chil­
dren, who particularly enjoyed Søren Frø’s friendliness and the humour and playfulness
of the video. Parents’ feedback highlighted the explanatory content, finding it useful and
easy to understand, and nurses praised the solution’s educative value and appealed to
children. However, language, content, video length, and audio issues were identified
throughout the development.
The solution received a positive reception from PED visitors, with the QR code registering
16 scans over the course of 2 weeks. The analysis of the study’s results indicated that the
intervention reduced children’s anxiety, as observed by the decrease in mYPAS scores
in the final test, decreasing child anxiety levels by 33% on average on a sample size of
8 children. The solution encouraged the active involvement and engagement of parents,
with 100% of parents reading the video text to their children and 75% doing the breathing
exercise. Additionally, increased warmth and reduced control behaviours were observed
in parents, indicating the solution’s potential to generate positive parenting behaviours.
Overall, the intervention showed promising results in reducing child and parent anxiety in
Herlev Hospital’s PED, ensuring that the video is both informative and engaging. How­
ever, further work is needed to properly evaluate the effectiveness of the solution given
the limitations of the present project, such as the number of participants in the test.

B.1
The mYPAS Scale
The scale was taken from the paper by Kain et al. [28].
A. Activity
1. Looking around, curious, playing with toys, reading (or other age­appropriate be­
havior); moves around holding area/treatment room to get toys or go to parent; may
move toward OR equipment.
2. Not exploring or playing, may look down, may fidget with hands or suck thumb (blan­
ket); may sit close to parent while waiting, or play has a definite manic quality.
3. Moving from toy to parent in unfocused manner, non­activity derived movements;
frenetic/frenzied movement or play; squirming, moving on table, may push mask
away or clinging to parent.
4. Actively trying to get away, pushes with feet and arms, may move whole body; in
waiting room, running around unfocused, not looking at toys or will not separate
from parent, desperate clinging.
B. Vocalizations
1. Reading (non­vocalizing appropriate to activity), asking questions, making com­
ments, babbling, laughing, readily answers questions but may be generally quiet;
child too young to talk in social situations or too engrossed in play to respond.
2. Responding to adults but whispers, ”baby talk”, only head nodding.
3. Quiet, no sounds or responses to adults.
4. Whimpering, moaning, groaning, silently crying.
5. Crying or may be screaming ”no”.
6. Crying, screaming loudly, sustained (audible through mask).
C. Emotional Expressivity
1. Manifestly happy, smiling, or concentrating on play.
2. Neutral, no visible expression on face.
3. Worried (sad) to frightened, sad, worried, or tearful eyes.
4. Distressed, crying, extreme upset, may have wide eyes.
D. State of Apparent Arousal
1. Alert, looks around occasionally, notices and watches what anesthesiologist does
with him/her (could be relaxed).
2. Withdrawn, sitting still and quiet, may be sucking on thumb or face turned to adult.
3. Vigilant, looking quickly all around, may startle to sounds, eyes wide, body tense.
4. Panicked, whimpering, may be crying or pushing others away, turns away.

• Environmental Factors: The hospital environment is designed to be calming and ac­

but toys and materials were left out as the day progressed.
• Educational Materials: Various educational materials, including booklets and posters,
are available to inform patients and their families about procedures and illnesses.

versations.
• Entertainment/Distraction: Children played individually or with caregivers, not with

On the first visit to the PED, the PhD and nurse Gitte gave a tour of the PED, explaining
all the relevant aspects and functions that she considered essential for the project. At
the same time, we had an open conversation about anxiety at the PED and what current
strategies are being investigated to assess it. During the discussion, Gitte provided de­
tailed and valuable insights into her current project and research, ethical considerations
and patient comfort in medical practices. Here is a shortened version of the conversation
with gitte:
Q: Can you tell me about your PHD research project?
Gitte: Sure. I’m currently conducting a research project using eyetracking devices and Ap­
ple Watches to assess pediatric patients’ comfort levels during medical procedures. The
eyetracking device measures pupil size and tracks visual attention, while smartwatches
collect heart rate and respiratory activity data from both the child and parents. The data
and camera recordings help us understand their visual attention and potential discomfort
levels. The data is then sent to DTU servers for analysis, to study its correlation with the
child’s emotional and physiological responses.
Q: What is the age range for your PHD project research project?
Gitte: Although the most common age range we receive in the PED is from 0 to 3, we
focus on children aged 4 to 7 as they are harder to communicate with and its more difficult
for them to distinguish pain and anxiety, also they don’t have the receptors to sweat so
you cannot do skin contact.
Q: How do you address privacy and data security concerns when collecting and
storing sensitive information from pediatric patients, given the nature of the PED?
Gitte: For any studies involving children in the PED, we need to obtain consent from both
parents. As this is a PED, patients do not know they are going to be in the hospital, so
most of the time, children come with only one parent or a caregiver. We first ask for the
first parents’ collaboration and use the REDCap system to get approval from the second
parent, ensuring we meet ethical standards and regulations.
Q: How did you conduct the studies on children, specially when wearing the eye­
tracking set were they cooperative?
Gitte: One of the main tests we did was to assess and understand the child’s comfort level
and potential distress during wearing the glasses, making improvements and optimising
the design so it wasn’t overwhelming for them. To avoid complications with the child, we
take measurements every 10 minutes, with three samples per minute. This approach
ensures that data collection is manageable and minimises any stress or anxiety for the
child. Also, it is very difficult to convince a child to wear a device covering their face for
more than that time.
Q: Can you walk me through the journey a child takes from the moment they en­
ter the Pediatric Emergency Department (PED) to the point where they undergo
surgery?
Gitte: Of course. When a child arrives, they go to the reception area and then wait in the
common waiting areas until they’re called by the triage nurse. The triage nurse assesses

the child’s condition, takes measurements like pulse, and asks about their level of pain,
which is recorded in the system. If the nurse is concerned, the child is admitted to a room
and assigned to a nurse. Then, there’s usually some waiting time before a doctor comes
to conduct further tests or measurements. Based on the results, the child might be sent
for an overnight stay or discharged home.
Q: How do you manage pain and anxiety in the waiting room?
Gitte: In the waiting room, we have a big screen that displays whether a child has pain
and the level of pain they’re experiencing, indicated by a scale. For children above the
age of seven, we ask the children their pain on a number scale, while younger children

Gitte: Managing anxiety varies depending on the medical staff and their approach to ob­

Triage Assessment
Once the concept was chosen, Gitte told me the procedure and journey of triage Assess­
ment.
1. Introduction: Understand why the child and parent are here at the Pediatric Emer­
gency Department (PED).
2. Assessment by Nurse: The nurse initiates the assessment by asking questions,
addressing the child directly if they are old enough to respond, or else communicat­
ing with the parent.
• Duration of Illness: Inquire about the duration of the illness and any associated
symptoms.
• Fever and Hydration: Determine if the child has a fever, if they have been
urinating and drinking adequately.
• Medication: Determine if they have taken any medication such as paracetamol.
3. Vital Signs Assessment: Conduct a systematic assessment of vital signs using
the ABCDE algorithm:
• A: Assess Airway. Observe the child’s respiratory effort by observing chest
movement and muscle use.
• B: Assess Breathing. Evaluate respirations by observing the child’s breathing
rate and effort, as well as feeling their chest. For patients younger than 1 year,
observe for 30 seconds and use a hand to feel the breathing.
• C: Assess Circulation.
Check Pulse and Oxygen Saturation using a finger
probe. Examine skin color and capillary refill time for signs of dehydration:
Press a finger on the child’s chest/finger for 5 sec, and if color returns in no
longer than 3 sec, the child is not dehydrated.
• D: Disability. Ask if they have any previous illnesses or other conditions that
might hinder their capacity and health. Inquire about medications.
• E: Exposure. Look for signs of injury, rashes, infection, or skin changes. Mea­
sure the temperature using an ear thermometer.
4. Pain Rate: Assess pain and discomfort by asking them questions and having them
rate it on a scale of 1­10 or using Wong­Baker faces.
5. Weight and Height: Measure weight and height if necessary, especially if parac­
etamol administration is being considered for children who will wait in the private
room.
6. Consultation with Physician: After the initial assessment, the child and parent
will meet with the physician in the private waiting room. This consultation includes
a discussion of the child’s condition and any necessary treatment. Throughout the
child’s journey in the PED, the nurse and physician will periodically reassess the
child’s condition and adjust the treatment plan as necessary.

disengaged, the critical questions would be answered.

E.4.3
Parental Behaviours
The parental behaviours associated with the warmth and control dimensions are shown
below. This list was compiled based on the behaviours used to conduct the trials by
Baumrind [61] and Woodruff­Borden et al. [67], as well as the theory and constructs seen
previously in section 5.2 defining the constructs inside each dimension.
Warmth:
1. Affection (physical touch, showing interest, verbal expressions)
2. Reassurance (comforting, emotional support)
3. Positive reinforcement (Praise/compliment, verbal affirmation, encouragement)
4. Responsiveness (Empathy, acknowledge child’s opinions, Acceptance, compas­
sion)
5. Open communication (encourage verbal exchange, answering questions, receptive
to child’s opinions)
6. Engagement (Humor/language play, nonsense, jokes, Active listening)
7. Satisfy child needs (physical and emotional to ensure well­being)
8. Encourage independence (Respect child decisions, Allow choice making, foster au­
tonomy)
9. Teaching & guidance (provide instruction, offer assistance)
Control:
1. Punitiveness (disciplinary techniques, punishments)
2. Negatives (complaints, criticism, disapproval)
3. Power (reason to obtain compliance, willingness to influence, ability to resist pres­
sure from child)
