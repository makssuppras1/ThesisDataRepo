Abstract
In this thesis the environmental risk of lithium-ion batteries ending in waterbodies as a result of the dumping of E-
scooters is examined. This is done in three parts; ﬁrstly an assessment of State-of-the-Science literature regarding
ecotoxicity of the battery and the main toxic battery components: lithium (Li), cobalt (Co), nickel (Ni), manganese
(Mn), copper (Cu), lithium hexaﬂuorophosphate (LiPF6) and NMC (LiNiMnCoO2) has been performed. Secondly,
ISO 8620 algal ecotoxicity tests are performed on Raphidocelis subcapitata, with LiCl, NiCl2, CoCl2, and NMC.
The same compounds, except LiCl which was swapped with LiOH, were used for ecotoxicity testing on the basket
willow, Salix viminalis. Thirdly, a leaching test was performed using two ’Ninebot MAX battery pack’ model
’INR18650 M26 Smart Lock’ by LG Chem, with a NMC cathode. The two batteries were placed in separate
test-ponds ﬁlled with approx. 200 L of tap-water. The ponds were located outdoors and were fully exposed to
the weather conditions during the test period. Water samples were extracted from the ponds over a period of two
months and the content of Li, Ni, Co, Cu, and Mn were determined through ICP-MS analysis.
The amount of available State-of-the-Science literature was deemed very scarce and no similar leaching test have to
our current knowledge been performed before. Nonetheless, effect concentrations (EC) were found for the chosen
metals of interest which could then be used to evaluate the EC50 values obtained in our own ecotoxicity tests.
Based on our 72 h growth inhibition EC50-values, Raphidocelis subcapitata was found to be most sensitive to
NMC 38 (95 % CI: 31-46) µg/L followed by nickel 142 (95 % CI: 95-189) µg/L- 186 (95 % CI: 177-195) µg/L,
cobalt 207 (95 % CI: 145-268) µg/L, and lithium 14 (95% CI: 10-18) mg/L. The Salix viminalis turned out to
be most sensitive towards nickel with a 96 h transpiration inhibition EC50 of 4 (95 % CI: 0.6-7) mg/L followed
by NMC, cobalt, and lithium with their respective EC50-values of 11.5 (95% CI: 6.6-16.4) mg/L, 18 (95 % CI:
10-26) mg/L, and 32 (95 % CI: 19-45) mg/L. Based on the results it was concluded that mixture toxicity plays a
role in ecotoxicity. The Ni willow EC50-value was lower than that of NMC, but when the dissolved concentrations
of the individual metals were considered (through ICP-OES analysis), they were all present in levels below their
individual EC10 range except Ni, which was slightly above its EC20.
After the four months, the ﬁnal mean concentrations in Pond A were 12.4 µg Li/L, 2.78 µg Mn/L, 0.18 Co µg/L,
6.64 µg Ni/L, and 39.8 µg Cu/L. The ﬁnal mean concentrations in Pond B were 12.7 µg Li/L, 2.92 µg Mn/L, 0.14
Co µg/L, 10.3 µg Ni/L, and 48.7 µg Cu/L. The ﬁnal mean concentrations in control Pond C were 10.8 µg Li/L,
0.45 µg Mn/L, < 0.05 Co µg/L ,0.37 µg Ni/L, and 0.76 µg Cu/L. The differences between the three ponds were
statistically analysed in Excel. To determine whether the metal concentrations in Pond A and B were signiﬁcantly
higher than those of Pond C, a paired one-tailed t.test was used with an α = 0.05. To determine if the mean metal
concentrations in Pond A and B were the same, a two-tailed t.test with an α = 0.05 was performed. It was found
that the mean concentrations in Pond A were signiﬁcantly higher than in the control pond. This also applies to
Pond B except for Li. It was found that all mean concentrations but Ni were the same in Pond A and B.
Neither cobalt, nickel nor manganese levels leached to Pond A and B exceeded the Environmental Quality Stan-
dards (EQS) set by the EU Water Framework Directive (2010), nor did they exceed the general background con-
centrations, with the exception of Li with a slightly higher average of 12-13 µg Li/L compared to < 10 µg Li/L
(Emery et al., 1981). A previous EQS for copper set by the Danish Environmental Protection Agency of max 12
µg Cu/L (Miljøstyrelsen, 2010) was exceeded in both Pond A and B. Nickel is currently the only metal of those
investigated which is included in the current EQS (EU Commission, 2008).
In urban surface waters possible toxic effects to aquatic organisms cannot be ruled out. Despite the fact that the
mean concentrations of the given metals were below the EQS, the combined toxicity can still pose a risk to the
environment. The various compounds from the batteries will be diluted many times but it will not only be a single
battery contributing to toxic effects but several ones e.g. considering the 144 E-scooters found in Copenhagen
(Engelschmidt et al., 2020). As metals are known to corrode faster in the presence of ions (Odio et al., 2014) the
salinity in marine harbours and canals can increase the corrosion (Zakowski et al., 2014) and thereby potentially
increase the risk. Furthermore, the E-scooters which are being dumped in the waterbodies may have suffered
external damage before ending in the water due to vandalism or accidents. This can, again, potentially increase
the risk of leakage from the batteries. Though there most likely are more concerning sources of pollution, posing
a larger risk than Li-ion batteries, an effort should still be made to retrieve the batteries, preferably within a couple
of days.

Chapter 1
Introduction and aim
Since their introduction to the mass market in 1990, lithium-ion (Li-ion) batteries have been used in applications
such as medical devices, electronics, and power tools. The market for Li-ion batteries has been growing annually
at 26 % in terms of production output and 20 % in terms of value since 2010 (Tsiropoulos et al., 2018) and lithium
is considered a key component in the green energy storage technologies and electric vehicle industry (Kavanagh
et al., 2018).
The popularity of electrical vehicles is increasing along with the increased awareness of the environment and the
climate challenges we are facing. Electrical scooters (E-scooters) have existed for many years, but it was not until
2018 that the ﬁrst scooter sharing system was introduced to the world. In 2018 alone, it was estimated that ﬁve
million E-scooters were sold and the total number of sold E-scooters and bicycles has been estimated to reach
around 50 million in 2020 worldwide. In Figure 1.1 it can be seen that there is an increase of the market worth
for E-scooters and that the projected market worth in 2025 will be nearly $14 million USD. It is expected that the
E-scooter market will be worth approximately $42 million USD by 2030 (Srivastava, 2020).
Figure 1.1: Market worth of electric scooters and motorcycles in million USD (Srivastava, 2020).
It can be seen from statistics that E-scooters are a product which is being increasingly incorporated into private
homes and as a transportation option in many larger cities. Unfortunately, not everyone appreciates the E-scooters
and vandalism of publicly available E-scooters is increasing. The vandalism of the scooters consists, among other
things, of throwing them into trash cans, running them over with a car, setting them on ﬁre, or throwing them into
waterways. In Lake Merritt (USA) almost 100 E-scooters were dumped in the lake over a period of three months
and in San Francisco, more than 200 of the 650 available Lime E-scooters were stolen or irreparably destroyed
within two weeks of their launch (Ho, 2018). In Berlin, some believe that the E-scooters and E-bikes, which
can be rented, are exploitative capitalism in its purest form. One particular group states that the E-scooters are
"climate pigs" due to the batteries they contain and have through online campaigns encouraged vandalism against
not only the E-scooters but also their users. The wheels on hundreds of E-bikes have been ruined while countless
of E-scooters have been destroyed or tossed into the Spree-River (Debionne, 2019). In France, they are also
experiencing problems with the vandalism against the E-scooters. Over a period of months, around 200 scooters
have been pulled out of the Seine, and in Lyon an environment group pulled out more than 100 E-scooters on just
one 800 m stretch along the Rhone (Schmid and Bonnet, 2019). In Denmark, neither the E-scooters nor their users
have been exposed to the same extent of vandalism as the aforementioned places. The destruction is, nonetheless,
still taking place and the E-scooters are also found in Danish water bodies (Bengtsen, 2019; Friesmand, 2019), see

Voi Technologies is a Scandinavian urban mobility company, providing electric scooter sharing in partnership
with cities and local communities. Voi states that their mission is "to provide sustainable and inclusive last-mile
mobility solutions, which enable people to move freely in cities "(Voi, 2020). With their sustainability team
located in Copenhagen, Voi is currently aiming to gather information about the potential environmental risks of
their scooters and particularly the potential risks related to the lithium-ion batteries ending up in water bodies, such
as lakes or canals. As leaching from the batteries potentially poses a risk to the aquatic ecosystems, Voi’s approach
is to collaborate with local authorities and associations to report and retrieve E-scooters as quickly as possible to
avoid any negative impacts on the environment. They have, additionally, established zones near the water where
their E-scooters cannot be parked and driven to minimise the number of E-scooters ending in the water bodies.
Furthermore, they have hired an employee to retrieve the E-scooters from the harbours though recovery attempts
often fail (Engelschmidt et al., 2020)
There is currently a limited amount of research in assessing the environmental impact of Li-ion batteries with the
main assessments being made using ’Life Cycle Assessment’ (LCA). Here the entire life cycle of the scooters is
assessed using computer programs that rely on very large databases which sometimes use ecotoxicological data.
The LCA can be a useful tool to identify the most troublesome areas in the life cycle of product-systems. They
have mainly been used to address how "environmentally friendly" the E-scooters are in relation to climate change
and CO2-emissions. These results are, however, only estimated and are not the same as a physical ecotoxicology
test carried out to quantify the potential risks associated with the batteries of the E-scooters. The environmental
risks will be assessed by examining the following key questions:
1. How much and what is leaking from batteries during the course of two months?
2. Will the levels of metals in test ponds be elevated, compared to existing background levels in water?
3. What is the toxicity of the leaking solution to aquatic organisms?
4. Which components of the lithium batteries are most toxic to aquatic organisms?
5. Transferred to real conditions, does leakage of batteries pose a risk to aquatic organisms living in urban
surface waters?
This will be investigated by performing a pond test where two batteries are placed into two separate freshwater
ponds to determine the leaching rates. A toxicity evaluation will be made on the key components of the battery us-
ing standard freshwater ecotoxicity testing and, additionally, a toxicity evaluation will be made with water samples
taken from the ponds. A comparative toxicity evaluation can hence be made between the controlled concentrations
and the pond water.
The overall aim is thus to determine the environmental impacts of Li-ion batteries thrown into water bodies based
on literature review and the experiments performed.

2.1
General function and structure of lithium-ion batteries
Overall, a battery is comprised of a cathode, anode, electrolyte, separator, and two current collectors (positive and
negative). The cathode and anode store the lithium(Li)-ions. The electrolyte transports positively charged Li-ions
from the anode to the cathode and reversed through the separator, see Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Schematic diagram of charge/discharge reaction in a rechargeable Li-ion battery, modiﬁed from (Qi et al., 2017)
This movement of the ions creates free electrons in the anode which generate a charge at the positive current
collector. The electrical current then ﬂows from the current collector through a device being powered, to the
negative current collector. The electricity is hence created by a redox reaction. The chemical reactions happening
within the battery depend on the combination of the materials used for the anode and cathode. Below is the reaction
for charge and discharge in the electrodes and in the battery as a whole (Panasonic, 2007):

In this example, the anode is made of carbon material and the cathode contains cobalt oxide (CoO2) which is
involved in the charge/discharge cycle. In theory, any conductive materials with different standard potentials can
be used as an electrochemical cell. The best option for an anode material is one which has a signiﬁcantly lower
standard potential than the material used for the cathode, as this makes the ﬂow of electrons between the electrodes
easier (EERE, 2017). The cathode needs to balance the negative charge it gets when receiving electrons from the
anode, which it does by taking up positive ions from the electrolyte. These circuits of the positive and negative
charges are equally important as they help keep the charges balanced and keep the reactions running. If there was an
unrestricted ﬂow of positive ions, they would at some point end up coating the surface of the electrodes completely.
This would lead to the system being clogged and it would stop working. To prevent this from happening, a semi-
permeable barrier is placed within the electrolyte between the anode and the cathode. When the battery is charged,
the chemical reactions within the battery are reversed. The process of reversing the internal reactions is nonetheless
not perfect, as the electrons and positive ions are not returned as neatly as originally. Thus, every time the battery
is recharged the electrodes deteriorate a little, which lowers the performance of the battery (Australian Academy
of Science, 2020).
2.1.1
Battery safety
The main concern when dealing with Li-ion batteries, is the risk of ﬁre within the battery which leads to the
development of toxic and explosive gasses, for example hydrogen ﬂuoride (HF) and phosphoryl ﬂuoride (POF3)
among others (DNV GL, 2020). When a battery starts to burn, the heat from the failing (burning) cell is spread to
the adjacent cells, causing them to become thermally unstable. This is what is called a thermal runaway (Battery
University, 2020). When this happens, a battery pack can disintegrate in a matter of seconds or over a time period
of several hours. Incorrect use of the battery is the most typical cause of battery ﬁres. This includes use of the
battery with an elevated temperature, excessive vibration and charging the battery at temperatures below freezing.
Especially for Li-ion batteries, it also applies that they should never be fully discharged as it is important that
they have a remaining charge when being stored. If a Li-ion battery is left discharged for any period of time it
can lead to an elevation in the self-discharge of the battery or a partial short circuit (DNV GL, 2020). When the
battery is charged, there is a risk of the cells becoming unstable, which among other anomalies can lead to the
production of excessive heat, which will induce more stress to the battery compared to normal recharge/discharge
cycle. When properly storing a Li-ion battery it should be stored in a cool location away from direct sunlight while
being partially charged. Speed charging the battery should also be avoided as this can also be harmful to the cells
(Battery University, 2020). Some of the causes and consequences of a thermal run-away can be seen in Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Causes and consequences of a thermal run-away within a battery pack (DNV GL, 2020).
If a Li-ion battery starts to overheat, bulge or hiss it should immediately be moved to a non-combustible surface
and if possible, it should be removed and placed outside to burn out by itself. If it is a small battery ﬁre, then
it can be handled like any other normal combustible ﬁre which means that extinguishing methods such as CO2,
foam extinguisher, ABC dry chemical and the like can be used. Products which are water based are usually the
ones which are most easily available and due to the low amount of lithium metal in a Li-ion battery they can be
used without problems. Direct water is also useful as it cools the areas around the ﬁre as well as soaking it, which
prevents the ﬁre from spreading (Battery University, 2020).
When handling larger battery ﬁres (could be a burning electrical vehicle) there are several approaches which can
be taken to contain/ put out the ﬁre. Large battery ﬁres might need to be let to burn out by themselves, but expert
advice is increasingly to use water. Approximate 11.000 liters of water would be needed to cool down a burning
Tesla car battery while a lot more would be needed to fully extinguish the ﬁre. It is, therefore, important to have

additional water supplies or a source which can provide a constant ﬂow of water when dealing with such ﬁres
(Tesla, 2020). Due to the amount of water needed to put out the battery ﬁres, this is one of the main ﬂows of toxic
chemicals to the environment from the battery ﬁre. Ideally, the water is led to a treatment plant but if there is no
way to collect the water for treatment then it might end up in the surrounding water bodies and the groundwater
from where it can reach lakes and streams.
2.2
The battery of the Voi scooters
Currently, Voi’s most recent model use Panasonic NCR18650BD battery cells which are of the lithium (Li), nickel
(Ni), cobalt (Co), aluminium oxide (AlO2) type or NCA (LiNiCoAlO2) for short. They are, however, planning to
change to batteries of the Li, Ni, Mn, and cobalt oxide (CoO2) (LiNiMnCoO2) type, or for short, NMC, where the
letters indicate the active materials. In this study, two ’Ninebot MAX battery pack’ will be examined. The battery
cells are manufactured by LG Chem and are of the model ’INR18650 M26 Smart Lock’ which are of the NMC
type. For more information about the batteries, see Appendix A.
The NMC combination is one of the most successful Li-ion systems. Compared to the NCA type, the NMC has a
higher cost but also an increased safety, with the lowest self-heating rate of Li-ion batteries (Cadex, 2020). In the
energy ﬁeld, nickel is known for its high speciﬁc energy but feeble stability, while manganese has the beneﬁt of
forming a spinel structure to attain little internal resistance but low speciﬁc energy. Thus, by combining the metals
the individual strengths are enhanced. The combination of the cathode is usually a 1-1-1 relationship between
nickel, cobalt and manganese, but NCM with ﬁve parts nickel, three parts cobalt and two parts manganese (5-3-2)
are also used (Cadex, 2020). The NMC battery is often chosen for e-bikes, power tools and e-power-trains as it
has a good overall performance and excels on speciﬁc energy and is, therefore, the preferred candidate for electric
vehicles (Cadex, 2020).

In the following chapter, a short description of lithium, manganese, cobalt, nickel, copper (Cu), and the electrolyte
will be presented.
Lithium
Lithium is the third element in the periodic table and has the lightest weight, highest voltage, and greatest energy
density of all metals. The ﬁrst published interest in lithium batteries appeared in 1958 with the development of
rechargeable batteries in the late 1970s and 1980s using lithium insertion compounds as cathodes (Van Schalkwijk
and Scrosati, 2002). When lithium is exposed to air and water, lithium hydroxide (LiOH) is formed along with
hydrogen (H) gas (Krebs, 2006). The same applies to the lithium in the Li-ion batteries, where the LiOH is formed
on the surface of the batteries (Moshtev et al., 1999; Torres and Balbuena, 2018). When LiOH enters the aquatic
environment, it contaminates the aquatic ecosystem by absorbing into biological organisms (Kim et al., 2017).
Lithium is classiﬁed as GHS02 and GHS05 stating that it causes severe skin burns and eye damage and in contact
with water releases ﬂammable gases which may ignite spontaneously. Furthermore, it is classiﬁed as GHS06
indicating acute toxicity if swallowed (ECHA, 2020b).

Manganese
Manganese is the twenty-ﬁfth element in the periodic table and an essential element for all species. Some organ-
isms, such as diatoms, mollusks and sponges, accumulate manganese. Manganese is one of the most abundant
metals in soils, where it occurs as oxides and hydroxides, and it cycles through its various oxidation states (LEEN-
TECH, 2020). It is also one of the many required elements in the human body. But when the homeostatic balance
in the body is tipped, the increased concentration of Mn results in toxicity and is evidenced primarily in the central
nervous system resulting in neurotoxicity due to the accumulation of the metal in brain tissue. Thus, repeated
administration of manganese, or compounds that readily release Mn, may increase the risk of Mn-induced toxicity
(Crossgrove and Zheng, 2004). Manganese is, like Ni and Co, classiﬁed as GHS09 meaning that it is hazardous
to the aquatic environment with long-lasting effects. It is also classiﬁed as GHS03 and GHS07 meaning that it is
oxidizing and poses health hazard (ECHA, 2020d), as mentioned.
Cobalt
Cobalt is the twenty-seventh element in the periodic table and is, in pure form, silvery-blue and brittle. It is similar
to iron and nickel and can be made magnetic (Future US, 2015). Cobalt is an essential element necessary for the
formation of vitamin B12 but excessive intake of this trace element produces goiter and reduced thyroid activity
(Barceloux and Barceloux, 1999). Cobalt is also classiﬁed as GHS08, meaning that it poses serious health hazards,
such as suspicion of causing genetic defects. It is, additionally, classiﬁed as GHS06/07/09, meaning that it, among
others effects, is fatal if inhaled and is very toxic to aquatic life with long-lasting effects (ECHA, 2020a).
Nickel
Nickel is the twenty-eight element in the periodic table and metallic nickel and its compounds are, due to unique
physical and chemical properties, widely used in modern industry. The high consumption of nickel-containing
products inevitably leads to environmental pollution by nickel and its by-products at all stages of production, re-
cycling, and disposal (Denkhaus and Salnikow, 2002). In the correct concentrations, nickel is an essential element
and a needed micro-nutrient in plants (Gerendás et al., 1999; Fabiano et al., 2015; Brown et al., 1987) In excess,
it is toxic to plants, however. (Chen et al., 2009; Shahzad et al., 2018). Nickel is also a known toxicant with
classiﬁcations as GHS07 and GHS09, meaning that it, inter alia, is toxic to aquatic life, is suspected of causing
cancer, and is capable of causing long-lasting unwanted effects in the aquatic environment (ECHA, 2020c).
Copper
Copper is the twenty-ninth element in the periodic table and is in pure form a reddish-gold metal which is easy to
work with. Copper is an essential element needed to help enzymes transfer energy in cells but toxic if excessive
(Royal Society of Chemistry, 2020). In the Nordic countries an adult ingests around 1-1.2 mg/d (Kreutzer and
Borre, 2019). It is also one of the seven micronutrients indispensable for plant growth and there is usually 5–30
mg/kg of Cu in crop tissues (Ballabio et al., 2018). Copper has low chemical reactivity. In moist air it slowly
forms a greenish surface ﬁlm called patina; this coating protects the metal from further attack. Most copper is
used for electrical equipment, construction, industrial machinery and alloys (Royal Society of Chemistry, 2020).
There is no harmonized classiﬁcation for copper, nor notiﬁed hazards by manufacturers, importers or downstream
users (ECHA, 2020d). Copper salts, such as CoCl are, however, both classiﬁed as GHS07 and GHS09, indicating
that the substances are very toxic to aquatic life with long-lasting effects and are harmful to humans if swallowed
(ECHA, 2020).
The electrolyte: Ethylene carbonate and Lithium hexaﬂuorophosphate
In general, the electrolyte is designed speciﬁcally for a particular battery application. Several different types of
electrolytes exist which can be organic or inorganic liquids, solids, polymers, ionic liquids, and a mix of the
mentioned (Goodenough and Kim, 2010). Organic liquid electrolyte carbonates are organic liquids that are fairly
good solvents for Li-salts (Hayashi et al., 1999; Xu, 2004). They have a relatively low viscosity, which results in
low activation energy for Li-ion diffusion. Hence, the most commonly used electrolytes are carbonates or carbonate
blends consisting of one or more of the following: propylene carbonate (C4H6O3), ethylene carbonate ((CH20)2CO)
(EC), diethyl carbonate (OC(OCH2CH3)2, dimethyl carbonate (OC(OCH3)2), or ethylmethyl carbonate (C4H8O3)
(Goodenough and Kim, 2010). Organic liquid electrolyte carbonates have an oxidation potential of approx. 4.7
V and a reduction potential (LUMO) near 1.0 V where the voltages refer to the Li+/Li0 potential. To be able
to use carbon as the anode in batteries, which has an electrochemical potential above that of the LUMO of a
carbonate (Goodenough and Kim, 2010), the solvents in most cases include EC as, the EC provides a passivating
solid/electrolyte-interface (SEI) layer on the surface of a carbon anode. This protects the electrolytes from further
decomposition after SEI formation (Fong et al., 1990; Yazami, 1999).
When considering the available lithium electrolyte salts, lithium hexaﬂuorophosphate (LiPF6) has been the pre-
ferred salt as it holds the best comprehensive properties with its rapid dissolution in carbonate, its low cost (Veit
and Bernardes, 2015), resultant ionic conductivity, and passivisation of the Al current collector. Pribat (2015)

Metals can occur either naturally or from anthropogenic sources in natural waters. These metals can be toxic to
aquatic biota depending on their chemical speciation and concentration. Free hydrated metal ions are often more
toxic to aquatic organisms while complex metal ions are not. The chemical speciation also affects the relative
extent of adsorption which will affect the fate of the metal in relation to sedimentation, precipitation, volatilization
and thereby toxicity (Schnoor et al., 1996). The behaviour of metals can be divided into two categories: hard
A-cations or soft B-cations, see Figure 2.3.

2.4
Drinking water criteria & environmental quality standards of metals
Metals differ from xenobiotic organic pollutants as they frequently have natural background sources from disso-
lution of rocks and minerals (Schnoor et al., 1996). Since metals cannot be "mineralised" into innocuous end-
products, which is often the case with toxic organic pollutants, they are persistent and cause a serious pollution
problem if their concentrations exceed water quality standards.
Drinking water criteria or quality standards are a set of parameters meant to describe the quality of the water.
Despite the importance of clean drinking water, there is still no universal set of drinking water criteria. This means
that individual countries have their own drinking water regulations independent from each other. In the United
States, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) establishes the standards, which are required by the Safe
Drinking Water Act (EPA, 974). In Europe, the European Drinking Water Directive dictates the required standards
for drinking water. This directive applies to all countries part of the European Union. The European Drinking
Water Directive states that a total of 48 different chemical, microbial, and indicator parameters need to be tested
and monitored regularly (EU Commission, 2019). Each member state can add their own requirements such as the
addition of new substances or set higher criteria (limit values) but should as a minimum follow the directions given
by the European Commission. In this case, the substances of interest are lithium, nickel, manganese, copper, and
cobalt. Denmark has applied the same limit value as the EU directive but with the addition of limit values for
cobalt to the drinking water regulation. Neither the Danish regulation nor the European directive have values for
lithium (EU Commission, 1998; Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2019). The Danish limit values for drinking water
can be seen in Table 2.3.

In 2010, the Danish Environmental Protection Agency approved a legislation regarding EQS for aquatic environ-
ments and requirements for discharges of pollutants into streams, lakes, and the sea. The EQS is here deﬁned as the
concentrations of pollutants in water, sediment and biota which should not be exceeded for the protection of human
health and the environment. In this legislation an EQS can be speciﬁed as either a general quality requirement or
as a short-term quality requirement. The general quality requirement is deﬁned as a yearly or periodic average
which should be fulﬁlled in the affected aquatic environment to prevent chronic effects. The short-term quality
requirement states a maximum acceptable concentration in an affected aquatic environment which protects against
acute effects. These quality standards are an expression of the highest concentration by which it is estimated that
no unacceptable negative effects will occur in aquatic ecosystems (Miljøstyrelsen, 2010). Unfortunately, the values
are no longer valid as the legislation has been changed/expired. No new values have been implemented for cobalt,

Background concentrations
In sea water, lithium is the fourteenth most abundant element where it occurs at 140 to 200 µg/L and at 1 to 20
µg/L in fresh waters (Kavanagh et al., 2017). In most freshwater, the highest naturally occurring concentration
is 10 µg/L. If the lithium concentration reaches 100 µg/L then the concentration may be considered "approaching
toxic concentrations" in freshwater (Emery et al., 1981). The lithium concentration is quite dependent on the
geographical location as the high-altitude continental brine aquifers of Argentina, Chile and Bolivia contain most
of the world’s accessible lithium. Here the levels of lithium in the natural waters are very high:≥5 mg/L. Northern
Chile has recorded the highest levels of lithium in surface waters in the world, at levels of 2 to 20 mg/L, 2–3 orders
of magnitude higher than most rivers (Kavanagh et al., 2017).
Manganese is the twelfth most common element in the Earth’s crust and occurs naturally in the environment.
Manganese is commonly found as MnO2 and, among others, as the mineral Rhodochrosite (MnCO3) (NFA, 2004).
The background concentration of manganese in European environments is 15.9 µg Mn/L in surface water, 452
mg/kg in sediment and 428.6 mg/kg in soil (ECHA, 2020a). The natural background concentration in Denmark is
however quite high with a natural average concentration of up to 150 µg/L (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020b).
Copper is a very common substance and occurs naturally in the environment and spreads through natural phenom-
ena in the environment. Anthropogenic sources of copper include the application of agriculture fertilisers. As the
production of copper has increased in the last decade, the copper quantities in the environment have increased.
The majority of the copper compounds will settle and be bound to either sediment or soil particles. Soluble copper
compounds form the largest threat to human health, usually occurring in the environment after release through
application in agriculture (Royal Society of Chemistry, 2020). In the US, the average concentration of copper in
soil ranges from 5 to 70 mg/kg and is higher in soils near smelters, combustion sources, and mining operations.
The median concentration of copper in rivers, lakes, and oceans is 4–10 µg/L and is predominantly in the Cu(II)
state. Most of it is complexed or tightly bound to organic matter while little is present in the free (hydrated) or
readily exchangeable form (ATSDR, 2020). In Canada, dissolved copper levels in uncontaminated freshwaters
usually range from 0.5 to 1.0 µg/L, increasing to > 2 µg/L in urban areas. Copper levels in river waters range from
0.6 to 400 µg/L, with a median of 10 µg/L (Alberta Environmental Protection, 1996).
The background concentration of nickel in groundwater is related to the soil use, pH and the depth of which the
sample is taken from. One of the main sources of nickel in drinking water is leaching from metal surfaces which
are in contact with the water such as ﬁttings and pipes. In the Netherlands, the average concentration of nickel in
the groundwater ranges from 7.9 - 16.6 µg/L in urban areas and rural areas respectively. General mean value for
dissolved nickel in European freshwaters was found to be 2.7 µg/L while in marine waters it ranges from 0.3 to
3.34 µg/L for estuarine/ estuarine-inﬂuenced coastal waters and open water, respectively (European Commission,
2011). For surface water, the average dissolved nickel concentrations (found in the rivers Rhine and Meuse) was
below 7 µg/L (WHO, 2005).
The average concentration of dissolved cobalt for rivers in the United Kingdom ranges between 0.1 µg/L to 1.1
µg/L. Geological survey data from the United States show mean and median cobalt concentrations of 2.9 and 2
µg/L respectively in surface water. It is very rare to detect cobalt in drinking water but the detected concentrations
were reported to range between 0.1 to 5 µg/L which is considered low. In rainwater the concentration ranges
from 0.002 to 2.9 µg/L over the Paciﬁc Ocean and in Wales, respectively (WHO, 2006). The normal background
concentration in Danish ground- and surface water is assumed to be 1.5 µg/L (Miljøstyrelsen, 2009).

2.6
Environmental risk assessment
When performing environmental risk assessment, the potential hazard of a compound of interest is examined. In
the context of this thesis, the ecotoxicity characterises potential adverse effects that a chemical may cause to an
aquatic or terrestrial receptor. This deﬁnition is used in different assessment methods, including many reviewed
frameworks. The ecotoxicity is hence based on the toxicological properties of the compound and the susceptibility
of the organism (National Research Council et al., 2014). To ensure that the tests are reliable and comparable
across many studies, test guidelines/standards from The Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development
(OECD) and ISO, The International Organization for Standardization are used.
The results from the ecotoxicity test will be presented as the effect concentration (EC). EC50 will mainly be used
as it is the median effective concentration; hence the concentration of a substance in water, soil or sediment that is
estimated to cause a deﬁned toxic effect to 50 % of the organisms.

2.7.1
Algae: Raphidocelis subcapitata
Planktonic microalgae play a key role in the aquatic food webs as primary producers. As a change in the density
and diversity of algal population may affect entire ecosystems, it is crucial to understand how algae are affected by
aquatic toxicants, such as metals (Deleebeeck et al., 2009). The freshwater planktonic green algae, Raphidocelis
subcapitata, see Figure 2.4, formerly known as Pseudokirchneriella subcapitata, is a widely used test organ-
ism for ecotoxicity testing. It is recommended by the OECD protocol for bioassays due to its sensitivity to various
substances, high reproduction efﬁciency, and good reproducibility (NCBI, 2020).

2.7.2
Higher plant: Salix viminalis
The total biomass on Earth, excluding biomass from bacteria, is estimated to be 1841 · 109t, of which 99% (1837 ·
109t) is located on land. Of the terrestrial biomass only 0.1% is animal biomass, 0.9% is biomass from fungi
while 99% is phytomass. An estimated 92% of the terrestrial biomass is considered forest (Clausen and Trapp,
2017a). Due to this, it can be concluded that higher plants and especially trees are of great importance for the
global ecosystem and they can therefore not be omitted when running ecotoxicity tests.
Salix viminalis, also known as basket willow, is typically chosen for ecotoxicity tests for several reasons. Willow
trees have been bred and cultivated for centuries which makes them easily accessible from ﬁelds, commercial
suppliers or in the wild. They have meristematic cells in their stem which makes them easy to reproduce as the
cuttings will be genetically identical when obtained from the same tree. They can grow under a broad range of
different conditions such as pH, temperature, light and nutrients while also being a fast-growing tree. The willow
tree test is usually run in a hydroponic solution (Trapp et al., 2000) but can also be done in sand or soil. The
problem with plants grown in hydroponic solution is the risk of them suffering from oxygen depletion at the roots,
which is a common cause of fouling of plant roots. Of trees in Central Europe, the willow is one of the very few
species which can withstand being ﬂooded over a longer period of time as it has pressurised ventilation through its
stem and root aerenchyma. Due to this ability, the willow can survive in a hydroponic solution for at least three
weeks without suffering from fouling of the roots (Clausen and Trapp, 2017a). All these parameters make the
willow tree a great species for testing.

3.4
Principle behind the conducted ecotoxicity tests
3.4.1
Principle of algae testing:
The effect on the algae is assessed as the function of the exposure concentration compared with the average growth
of the control cultures made. The cultures have unlimited possibility for exponential growth with sufﬁcient nutrient
conditions and constant ﬂuorescent lighting. The growth and growth inhibition are quantiﬁed from the amount of
algal biomass as a function of time (OECD, 2011).
Due to the exponential growth, the growth curve (of the control) will be a straight line on a semi-log graph with
biomass on the log scale and time on the ordinary scale. If the natural logarithm is applied to the biomass-axis, the
slope of the growth curve is equal to the growth rate (µ) (Kusk, 2012).
The cell density can be correlated to the absorbance and ﬂuorescence and thus be taken directly as a measure of
the algal biomass. The ﬂuorescence is measured on a HITACHI f-7000 Fluorescence Spectrophotometer.
The following equations are used (Kusk, 2012):
Growth:
Nn = No · exp(µ · td)
(3.1)
Growth rate:
µ = (lnNn −lnNo)/td
(3.2)
Where No is the initial biomass/inoculum (measured ﬂuorescence at t = 0),
Nn is the ﬁnal biomass ( measured ﬂuorescence at t = 72 h) and
td is the length of the test period in days or hours.
Inhibition of growth [%]:
Iµi(%) = (1 −µi/µc) ∗100
(3.3)
Where Iµi is the inhibition of the growth rate for concentration i in percentage,
µi is the mean growth rate of concentration i and
µc is the mean growth rate of the control.
3.4.2
Principle of willow testing:
The purpose of the willow tree acute toxicity test is to measure the acute toxicity of a compound. This is done by
measuring the inhibition of the trees transpiration which is caused by the compound. The inhibition of transpiration
can typically be witnessed/measured after a couple of days while other effects such as growth reduction, necrosis,
decay and death usually require a longer period. The test is fast as it only takes one to two weeks to ﬁnish while
also being easy and cheap to run. Cuttings of willow are placed in containers with a ﬂuid medium containing the
substance of interest at different concentrations with some of the cuttings remaining as control samples. During
the test, the containers will be weighed several times to determine the loss of test solution (equivalent to the
transpiration of the cuttings). From the transpiration, the "Normalised Relative Transpiration" (NRT) will be
calculated to consider growth of controls and differences between individual trees. The NRT can then be used as a
measure for the ecotoxicity of the subject of interest (Trapp et al., 2017).
16
==PAGE:26==
Methodology
Quantiﬁcation of toxicity: Normalised Relative Transpiration
As healthy trees grow more quickly and therefore have higher transpiration, the NRT is calculated to normalise the
results. The NRT is the change of transpiration of exposed trees divided by the change of control trees:
NRT(C, t)(%) =
1
n · Pn
i=1 Ti(C, t)/(C, 0)
1
m · Pm
j=1 Tj(0, t)/(0, 0) × 100
(3.4)
where C is the concentration in (mg/L), t is the time period (h), T is the absolute transpiration (g/h), i is replicate
1, 2, ..., n and j is control 1,2, ..., m.
The NRT of the controls is always 100 %. The transpiration values of the trees with a test solution below 100 %
are caused by an inhibition effect of the test solution. As a second toxicity parameter, the growth of the trees is
determined from the weight difference of the trees before and after the test (Trapp et al., 2000).
3.5
Preparation and treatment of metal mix NMC solution
When preparing the stock solutions for the different NMC tests further treatment of the solution was necessary. As
NMC is a very ﬁne metal powder, it is very difﬁcult to dissolve in water. Several steps were thus needed to ensure
that the NMC solution could be used for the different tests. A concentration was decided upon and an appropriate
amount of NMC powder was weighed and mixed with the desired volume of nutrient solution in a volumetric ﬂask.
To achieve a homogenised mix the solution was treated with ultrasound. It was then transferred to a blue cap bottle
where the pH was lowered to below 2 while being stirred on a magnetic stirrer. The mix was then again treated
with ultrasound before it was ﬁltrated. The ﬁlter used had a pore size of 0.45µm. The pH was then adjusted back
to approximately 8 while on the magnetic stirrer. Due to precipitation in the solution after pH had been adjusted,
the mix was once again ﬁltrated (pore size 0.45µm). Afterwards the solution was deemed ready to be used as stock
solution for the ecotoxicity tests. It should additionally be noted that some of the powder adsorbed to plastic. It
was therefore necessary to use glass equipment only. Images from the preparation are presented in Figure 3.4

The algal tests were performed (with modiﬁcations) according to: Water quality - Fresh water algal growth inhibi-

3.7.1
General approach
The ISO8692 algal growth medium was prepared by adding an appropriate volume of nutrient stock solutions
to sterile ultrapure water. The chemical composition of the nutrient stock solutions that were used is listed in
Appendix B.1 along with the production procedure of the ISO medium.
Stock solutions were created by dissolving the compound of interest in the algal medium. The control and test

all tests, both in the control and toxic concentrations. For each concentration, three glass vials were ﬁlled with
exactly 4 mL of test solution. Five replicates were made for the control. The lid covering the algae suspensions
was perforated in order to ensure mass transfer of air. The glass vials were placed on a continuous shaker and
stored at 21 °C with constant lighting - see Figure 3.8.

From each glass vial, 0.4 mL was extracted and added to a tube, see Figure 3.9, to which 1.6 mL of acetone (satu-

Chapter 5
Discussion
5.1
Willow tests
As a result of the Covid-19 pandemic, the laboratory tests were interrupted in the beginning of March. At that
time, the ﬁrst willow toxicity test (lithium) had just been ﬁnished and a new batch of trees was prepared to perform
the second test (cobalt). As it was unsure when we could return to DTU, all tree cuttings were discarded. During
the lockdown, the laboratory and DTU’s perimeter in general was closed. It was nonetheless still necessary to
make new willow cuttings to ensure the possibility to continue the willow tests once we received permission to
use the laboratories. Cuttings were therefore taken from an elderly willow tree in Værløse in mid-May, where the
initial cuttings were made in February from a healthy willow tree near DTU in Lyngby. The cobalt test ran in
the beginning of June and the “waiting time” of the trees was thus ﬁne. The remaining trees, which were initially
planned to be used for the third test, were deemed unﬁt for further testing as they started to deteriorate. Due to this
a new batch of trees was cut, this time back at DTU from a thriving willow tree in June.
Due to the seasonal change, the temperature in the laboratory where the willow tests were performed increased
vastly, reaching almost 30 °C on the hottest days. Additionally, the risk of the trees getting fungus or suffering
from bug attacks increased. The last two tests performed with nickel and NMC were hence a bit more challenging.
Even though several cuttings were made and prepared for dosing, several of them had a daily transpiration of 5-10
g/d compared to the desired ∼20 g/d. Some of the trees additionally had brown discoloured spots on their leaves
which might have been a fungal attack. These trees were removed and excluded from the test to the best possible
extent.
Despite the difﬁculties experienced during the test period, all tests are still deemed successful with reliable re-
sults. From the ecotoxicity tests four different EC50-values are obtained. The highest toxicity is found for nickel,
followed by NMC, cobalt, and lithium with a transpiration EC50 (96 h) of 3.79 mg Ni/L, 11.5 mg NMC/L, 17.8
mg Co/L, and 32.02 mg Li/L, respectively. The high toxicity of nickel is not surprising, as nickel is known as a
strong phytotoxic compound (Ishtiaq and Mahmood, 2012). Nickel can change the activity of several enzymes and
strongly inﬂuences the metabolic reaction in plants and has the ability to generate reactive oxygen species which
may cause oxidative stress within the plant (Bhalerao et al., 2015). Looking back at Table 4.5 it can be deduced
that nickel on average constitutes 20 % of the dissolved NMC which roughly corresponds to 2 mg of the 11.5 mg/L
- approximately half of the nickel EC50. The remaining part of the NMC is on average constituted of 32 % lithium,
30 % manganese and 19 % cobalt. The dissolved NMC consists mostly of lithium and manganese, but neither is
considered highly toxic which means that the toxic effect of the compound must be caused by nickel and cobalt,
even though these are the two smallest fractions. When looking at the constitution of the NMC at the EC50-value
of 11.5 mg/L, the concentrations of the individual metals are roughly at 3.7 mg Li/L, 3.5 mg Mn/L, 2.3 mg Ni/L
and 2 mg Co/L. Considering this, it can be seen that nickel is the only metal which is present at a concentration
(2 mg Ni/L) which could lead to a toxic effect (see Table 4.3) while all other metals are present at concentrations
lower than their EC10-values. From this it can then be deduced that the combination of the individual metals have
a more toxic effect on the willow trees compared to the individual metals.

5.2
Algae tests
Overall, a growth rate of µ >2 d-1 was seen for the controls and as the largest change in pH was 0.9 (the second
nickel test) both of the validity criteria are fulﬁlled for all tests. The results of the tests are hence considered
reliable. A total of ﬁve growth inhibition EC50 (72 h) values were obtained, with the highest toxicity composed
by the NMC with an EC50 of 38 µg/L. The second highest is nickel with a EC50 of 142 - 186 µg/L, followed
by cobalt and then lithium with EC50-values of 207 µg Co/L and 14 mg Li/L, respectively. Here, the effect of
mixture toxicity or the so-called "cocktail effect" is quite obvious with NMC being more than three times as toxic
as the individual metals all being in the same order of magnitude. At a NMC concentration of 38 µg NMC/L the
individual metals will be present at roughly estimated concentrations of 12 µg Li/L, 11 µg Mn/L, 8 µg Ni/L and 7
µg Co/L. Even though nickel and cobalt pose the smallest fractions, it is still most likely these that contribute most
to the toxicity of NMC due to their low EC50-values. None of the metals are close ti reaching their individual
toxicity-levels, which again supports that the mix of the compounds may cause a much greater toxic effect on the
algae compared to the individual compounds.

5.4
NMC in ecotoxicity testing
When preparing the NMC stock solutions for the experiments it was observed that the solubility of the metal mix
was very low. Several steps were taken to attempt a greater dissolution of the metals. As mentioned earlier in
this thesis, it was attempted to homogenise the solution by the use of ultrasound and the addition of concentrated
HNO3 to ease the dissolution of the metal powder. The results from the ICP-MS analysis shows that some NMC
was dissolved but whether or not this was due to our own initiatives is hard to assess as it was not possible to run
an analysis on the solution without the addition of acid. To efﬁciently dissolve NMC, an acidic solution, a suitable
reducing agent, and thermal energy is needed. Some acids are more efﬁcient than others at dissolving NMC (for
example HCl) but even then, an increase in temperature is still needed for NMC to be completely dissolved (Joulié
et al., 2017). The HNO3 was, nonetheless, used as HCl could cause some issues when the metal content was
determined by the ICP-MS. After acidifying the solution, it was necessary to adjust the pH back to approx. 8 as the
organisms used for testing cannot survive in an acidiﬁed environment; even though the increase of the pH resulted
in precipitation of some of the dissolved metals which needed to be removed by ﬁltration.

5.7
Chronic toxicity and long-term effects
The previously assessed OECD tests (ECHA) are developed for chemical registration (REACH) for inter-comparison
of chemicals. These follow strict rules but not realistic conditions. The PNEC values are, furthermore, derived
from safety factors based on the range of the EC-values and organism level, among others. This is how a PNEC
of 3 µg Mn/L in marine water can be derived for a compound which e.g. is more than 60 times less toxic than
copper. Additionally, the amount of manganese added to the ISO 8692 medium is 115 µg/L, more than 50 times
the PNEC value - see Appendix B.1. Therefore, to assess the chronic toxicity of the metals, additional ecotoxicity
data is found as discussed in this section along with other parameters such as the Bio Concentration Factor (BCF)
and Bio Accumulation Factor (BAF).

5.7.6
Summary of chronic tests from literature
Overall, it is quite conspicuous from the presented chronic toxicity values that there is more to ecotoxicity than
acute effects. Juvenile ﬁsh and ﬁsh embryos are especially sensitive with chronic values as low as 3 µg Cu/L for
growth of Fathead minnow growth. The overall combined mean concentrations of Ponds A and B: 12.6 µg Li/L, 2.9
µg Mn/L, 0.21 µg Co/L, 8.5 µg Ni/L, and 44.3 µg Cu/L are thus high enough to pose a risk of chronic toxicity. The
mean copper concentration is e.g. several times higher than the chronic values also exceeding the 25 % inhibition
concentrations of both survival, growth and biomass for early life-stage ﬁsh - see Table 5.5. Though the lithium
concentration is below those of the chronic studies presented in Table 5.4, the concentration is still high enough
for several abnormalities such as delayed hatching, skeletal deformities, and lack of swimming to be observed in
Zebraﬁsh embryos (Selderslaghs et al., 2012). With a mean cobalt concentration below 1 µg Co/L little to no risk is
combined with this metal compared to the other metals. An increase in the cobalt concentrations was nonetheless
seen (Table 4.16) and toxic concentrations might be reached with time, though this is not considered an emerging
threat based on the current results. With a mean concentration of 8.5 µg Ni/L, no evident risk is posed by the nickel

5.8
Transferred to real-life conditions
When water is contaminated, it is important to understand how the contaminant moves in the environment. Proper
understanding of the fate and transport is necessary in order to best assess the risks associated with the contaminant
(Krupka et al., 1999). In nature, metal contaminants undergo reactions with ligands in water and with surface sites
on the solid materials the water is in contact with. The metal partition coefﬁcient (Kd) is the ratio of sorbed metal
(mg metal per kg sorbing material) to the dissolved metal concentration (mg metal per L of solution) at equilibrium
(Allison and Allison, 2005). Kd is considered one of the most important parameters used when estimating the
migration potential of toxins present in aqueous solutions in contact with surface, subsurface and suspended solids
(Krupka et al., 1999).
Metals Kd values in soil are dependent upon several geochemical characteristics of the soil and its porewater.
Equally, for surface water systems, the Kd for a metal depends on the nature of suspended solids or sediment and
key geochemical parameters of the water. These include the pH of the system and the concentration of sorbents
associated with the soil or surface water (Allison and Allison, 2005). Furthermore, the total concentration of
leachate elements may also affect the metal Kd through competition for sorption sites (Sauvé et al., 2000).

mean of experimentally obtained kd values from seven different sites in Sweden.

adsorbed to particles, with lithium being more likely to be present as anhydrous ions.
The pond experiment compared to the willow and algae test was performed outside, which is the closest attempt at
imitating nature short of throwing the batteries in a real lake. One of the most important things to consider is the
volume of water. In the pond experiments approximately 200 L were used without considering the volume gained
and lost by rain and evaporation. In nature, the volume will be considerably larger, not excluding the natural ﬂow
of water which creates a natural exchange between lakes, rivers, groundwater, the ocean etc. This will naturally
greatly dilute any leaching from dumped batteries and the toxicological effect of the metals will be reduced. It
will, nonetheless, not only be a single battery contributing to toxic effects but several ones e.g. considering the

estimation of the risks gets additionally more complex when the presented Kd values are considered. Manganese,
though only present at very low concentrations, is quite likely to adsorb to e.g. suspended solids or to aquatic
organisms due to the high BCF. With the second highest Kd, cobalt is also expected to adsorb to particles, along
with nickel and copper. Once the particles are adsorbed to soil particles or similar, the bioavailable concentrations
are reduced, thereby also reducing the toxicity to aquatic organisms (Kim et al., 2016; Violante et al., 2010). The
toxins are nonetheless still present and various factors affecting the sorption can result in the desorption of the
metals from their given sorbing material. It is therefore difﬁcult to quantify or rule out potential risks.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and recommendations
Three large tubs, in this thesis referred to as ponds (each containing approximately 200 L of tap water), were used
to examine the leaching from lithium-ion batteries to an aquatic environment. In two of the ponds, a ’Ninebot
MAX battery pack’ (model ’INR18650 M26 Smart Lock’ by LG Chem) was submerged (Ponds A and B) while
the last pond was used as a control (Pond C, no battery). These were then left by themselves for several months,
except for the occasional extraction of water samples. An ICP-MS analysis was run on the water samples to see
whether metals had been leaching from the batteries. In the ICP-MS analysis, lithium, cobalt, nickel, manganese
and copper were examined as these were considered the most important metals in the batteries. A statistical
analysis was performed to ﬁnd the mean concentration of the metals in each pond and to see if the ponds were
signiﬁcantly different from each other (α = 0.05). In Pond A, the concentrations were found to be 12.41 µg Li/L,
1.58 µg Mn/L, 0.18 µg Co/L, 6.43 µg/L and 34.97 µg Cu/L. In Pond B, the concentrations were found to be 12.73
µg Li/L, 2.92 µg Mn/L, 0.14 µg Co/L, 10.6 µg/L and 48.71 µg Cu/L. In Pond C, the concentrations were found to
be 10.77 µg Li/L, 0.45 µg Mn/L, <0.05 µg Co/L, 0.37 µg/L and 0.76 µg Cu/L. It was found that all metals in Pond
A were signiﬁcantly different from the control Pond C. Pond B was also signiﬁcantly different from Pond C except
for lithium. When comparing Ponds A and B it was found that no concentrations were signiﬁcantly different from
each other except for nickel.
The highest natural concentration of lithium in most freshwater is 10 µg/L. Based on this, the mean concentra-
tions of lithium in Ponds A and B are slightly elevated compared to the background concentration. In European
surface waters, the natural background for manganese is 15.9 µg/L. With mean concentrations of manganese at
approximately 3 µg/L in Ponds A and B (below 1 µg/L in Pond C) the concentrations are more than ﬁve times
below the background concentrations. In UK rivers, the cobalt concentration is between 0.1 to 1.1 µg/L, in the
US and Whales the concentration is 2.9 µg/L, while in Denmark the background concentration is 1.5 µg/L. This
makes Ponds A and B lie just above the UK levels but still below the values from the US, Wales and Denmark.
With a natural mean nickel concentration of 2.7 µg/L in European freshwaters, the nickel content in Ponds A and
B both exceed the background concentration. In Canadian rivers copper can be found at concentrations between
0.6 to 400 µg/L, which means that the copper concentrations in all three ponds lies well within the range of the
background concentration.
To assess the toxicity of the water in the ponds, ecotoxicity tests were carried out on two different organisms:
algae (Raphidocelis subcapitata) and willow (Salix viminalis). The tests were performed with lithium, cobalt,
nickel and a metal mix called NMC (LiNiMnCoO2) to determine the EC50 values for the individual metals, along
with the EC50 of the metal combination. The EC50 values for algae were found at 13.94 mg Li/L, 0.2 mg Co/L,
0.14 to 0.19 mg Ni/L and 0.04 mg NMC/L. The EC50 values for willow were found at 32 mg Li/L, 17.8 mg Co/L,
3.8 mg Ni/L and 11.5 mg NMC/L. When comparing these values with the results obtained from the ponds, none
of the individual metals in the ponds reaches the EC50-values for algae or willow. When looking at the combined
metal concentrations in Ponds A and B it was found that both ponds reached the NMC EC20-value for algae.
Through a literature study, EC50-values for Daphnia magna were found for the individual metals. Based on
these, it is concluded that copper was the only metal in Ponds A and B reaching toxic levels, with concentrations
∼20 times higher than in the control. Additionally, studies examining the chronic toxicity of the metals were
reviewed to evaluate this aspect of the potential risk associated with the dumped batteries. Here it was concluded
that the mean concentrations in Ponds A and B were high enough to pose a risk of chronic ecotoxicity to several
species.
Thus in conclusion, toxic effects to aquatic organisms in urban surface waters cannot be ruled out. The various
compounds from the batteries will be diluted many times but it will not only be a single battery contributing to
toxic effects but several ones, e.g. considering the 144 E-scooters found in Copenhagen. As metals are known to
corrode faster in marine water, potential increased corrosion could increase the risk. Furthermore, the E-scooters
which are being dumped in the waterbodies may have suffered external damage before ending in the water, due to
vandalism or accidents. This can, again, potentially increase the risk of leakage from the batteries. Though more
concerning sources of pollution most likely exist, posing a larger risk than Li-ion batteries, an effort should still be
made to retrieve the batteries, preferably within a couple of days.
