Abstract
In this work irradiance models for estimating Global Tilted Irradiance from Global Hori-
zontal Irradiance are validated and benchmarked for use with Vertically mounted Bifacial
Photovoltaics in the Nordics. Accurate information about the available solar resource is
crucial during the feasibility study and design phases of any solar energy harvesting project.
As measured in-plane irradiance is generally unavailable at a new location, decomposition
and transposition methods and models have been developed which, when combined, esti-
mate the solar irradiance incident on a tilted surface. Numerous decomposition models
exist, and it is unknown which model can be expected to provide the most accurate
estimations when applied to data in the Nordic countries Denmark, Norway and Finland.
Both empirical models and software tools for transposition exist, but no studies on vertical
east or west oriented surfaces with 1-minute resolution data were found.
In this work decomposition models observed to provide accurate estimations in climates
similar to the climate of the locations investigated were applied to 1-minute resolution data.
An Artificial Neural Network (ANN) was designed and trained, and validated along with
the decomposition models. The models Yang1, Yang2 and Starke21C were observed
to perform best in terms of correlation coefficient (R2) and normalized Root Mean Square
Deviation (nRMSD), while the ANN performed slightly worse at all three locations.
Based on recent literature three empirical transposition models, with high accuracy
when applied to vertical east or west data, were chosen along with three open-source
transposition software tools, representing both the view factor and raytracing methods.
The models and tools were applied to a 1-minute resolution dataset and compared with
measured vertical east and west incident irradiance from Turku University of Applied
Sciences, which to this author’s knowledge is the first 1-minute validation of transpositon
models. The results were used to benchmark the models and methods used. Of the
empirical transposition models a specific version of the Perez models, Perez94, was
observed to provide the lowest nRMSD of 58.6% (east), 51.4% (west) and highest R2 of
0.82 (east), 0.86 (west), while the raytracing tool bifacial radiance provided the overall
best estimations with a nRMSD of 58.0% (east), 50.4% (west) and R2 of 0.82 (east), 0.86
(west).

indfaldende lodret øst og vest ved Turku University of Applied Sciences. Resultaterne

Chapter 1
Introduction
As a response to increasing demand for cleaner energy, the use of Photovoltaic (PV) for
solar energy harvesting has rapidly increased over the last decades, from 800 GWh in 2000
to 681 TWh in 2019 [6], and is considered one of the cheapest and most climate friendly
ways of reaching the International Energy Agency (IEA) goal of net-zero emissions in 2050
[7]. As with any other method of resource harvesting the first avenue sought is the one
most readily available, i.e. the ’lowest hanging fruit’. In the case of the solar industry
this has been south-oriented Monofacial Photovoltaics (MPV) cells, which collects light
from one side, located in areas of the world with very high annual solar insolation and
high energy consumption, such as Southern Europe and China, where China is the leading
country in cumulative installed capacity [7]. The dominant use of MPV has been driven by
lower panel prices, but as the Bifacial Photovoltaics (BPV) technology develops the price
for BPV panels, which can collect light from both the front and the back, decreases, and
today roughly matches the price per Watt-peak of MPV. The MPV/BPV market share
is expected to change from 71%/29% in 2020 to 21%/79% in 2031 [8]. The rising BPV
market share leads to increasing interest in alternative PV system implementations to
increase the energy yield, such as backtracking, rooftop mounting and Vertically mounted
Bifacial Photovoltaics (VBPV) [9, 10]. As prices for solar panels decrease and module
efficiencies increase [8] the economic feasibility of PV projects in locations with low yearly
solar resource increases.
1.1
Motivation
With the rapid growth in yearly PV installations the need for optimization of PV systems
and the electrical grid to which they are connected increases. Proper utilization of the
allocated space is essential, and a feasibility study is the first step in any solar cell system
project. A well conducted feasibility study reduces the financial risk of a project through
projections of Levelized Cost of Electricity (LCOE), payback time and profit, and depends
on the accuracy of solar resource inputs which often adds significant uncertainty to financial
projections such as yield assessments of PV projects [11, 12]. Optimization of energy
production from a PV system is done with simulations, which include estimation of the
available solar resource (Global Tilted Irradiance (GTI)) at the selected location [13].
==PAGE:24==

GTI can be estimated from Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI), time and location with
empirical models, which is common practice as measuring GTI for a significant period takes
both time and resources, especially if several system configurations are to be analyzed. The
modelling steps involved in estimating GTI have been shown to add the most uncertainty
to overall simulation of PV systems [14, 15].
For south-oriented PV, the power production peaks around noon, which does not match
a typical urban consumption, which rises in the morning around 6-8 a.m., slightly increases
due to industry consumption between 8 a.m. and 5 p.m., and peaks in the evening around
5-6 p.m. due to residential consumption. This means that the residual load, after local
consumption of energy produced by PV systems, varies throughout the day and increases
the need for power system balancing. Using VBPV is a way of alleviating this issue as
the power production of VBPV peaks during the morning and again during the evening,
when the sun is in the east or west as illustrated in Figure 1.1 where the hourly average
irradiance measured on a vertical east and vertical west surface over a one year and four
months period is shown. A combination of south-oriented PV and VBPV can be tailored to

Nordics. The choice of which models to include is based on a literature study performed in

This work is part of a Low-Voltage (LV) grid simulation project in a group collaboration
between Norwegian University of Science and Technology (NTNU), University of Turku
(UTU) and Turku University of Applied Sciences (TUAS). The goal of the project is to
investigate the influence of high penetration of distributed PV systems on stability and
balancing of LV grids during days with different weather conditions and different types
of local PV installations. The LV grid simulations are performed on a 1-minute basis to
capture the high frequency variance in energy production from the local PV system caused
by rapid cloud movement [16]. The LV grid influence of rapid cloud movement demands
that the decomposition and transposition models are evaluated on 1-minute resolution
GTI. Additional motivation for validation of models with 1-minute resolution data is that
not considering the Cloud Enhancement (CE) effects of fast-transient clouds may cause
PV system simulations to underestimate the cost of generated electricity and overestimate
the optimal inverter sizing, tilt angle and row spacing [17].
1.2
Literature review and scientific motivation
During autumn 2021, I performed a literature study on the decomposition and trans-
position modelling steps with a focus on East-West facing Vertically mounted Bifacial
Photovoltaics (E-W VBPV) in the Nordics. The report from the literature study has since
been further worked on and adapted to a publication friendly format, and is currently in the
peer-review phase of being published. The goal of this work is to bridge the gaps found in
the literature. This subsection is based on the results of the aforementioned literature study.
The decomposition and transposition models and tools validated in this work are
referred to with hyperlink names in small caps, referring to a specific model. In the
digital version of this document clicking the model name leads to a table in the appendix
where the original model publication is referenced. Both the term estimation and predicted
are found in the literature referring to the outputs of decomposition and transposition
models, estimation is user over predicted since predicted implies something occurring in
the future.

Estimating the solar resource input, GTI (Egt), is done with transposition of measured
or modelled DHI (Edh) and Direct Normal Irradiance (DNI) (Ebn) on to the solar panels.
While transposition models date back to the 1940’ies [18], the work of Liu and Jordan
[19] from 1961 is regarded as the first well known model. In [19] the diffuse irradiance
emanating from the sky dome was assumed isotropic, which gave acceptable results at
the time. Since then transposition models have evolved to using complex anisotropic
assumptions about the sky dome distribution of DHI [20–22], while the transposition of
DNI has remained the same [23]. Past validation studies focus on a selected set of models
at a specific location [23], which has their merit as transposition models are based on
regression often with location-specific data, such as the model from Skartveit and Asle
Olseth [24], while some are fitted with data from multiple locations, such as the 1990
version of the Perez model from Perez et al. [25]. The accuracy of models in validation
studies varies with the different locations and often different tilt angles investigated. Many
validation studies exist, but very few consider vertical east-west orientations. The most
comprehensive recent review is Yang [23] where 26 empirical transposition models were
validated with 15-min resolution irradiance measurements from Eugene (Oregon, USA),
Oldenburg (northwest Germany), Singapore and Golden (Colorado, USA) in various tilt
and azimuth orientations. For the east and west vertical directions (data from Golden,
USA and Singapore) the best performing models were found to be Perez94, Muneer
and CDRS. Similar results were found in [26] for building facades and in [27] for Poland.
In Gueymard [16] the authors investigated the influence on CE and Albedo Enhancement
(AE) events on expected accuracy of transposition models and found that while Muneer
and Perez90 performed well under more static weather the models showed systematic
performance issues during hours with strong CE effects. The authors suggested that
transposition models with the ability to account for very high frequency weather patterns
be developed. CDRS performed the best for higher values of clearness index (GHI divided
by horizontal extraterrestrial irradiance). Their results indicated that using a different
model for different values of clearness index could improve overall modelling performance.
No recent validation studies specifically on transposition modelling in the Nordics were
found, and in general few validation studies on modelling of GTI on VBPV exists.
The regression based transposition models described above do not consider shading
from the environment such as adjacent PV rows, buildings and trees. In order to assess
the influence of shading on PV panels information about row-to-row spacing, ground
clearance, panel dimensions, and the surrounding environment is required. View Factor
(VF) and raytracing are the two most popular methods of factoring in shading. In Marion
et al. [28] a tool for VF calculations, bifacialvf, is presented and compared with the
raytracing software Radiance and measurements from several locations in a PV array. The
==PAGE:27==

presented tool almost performs on par with Radiance, and the difference in performance
of the two tools is found to be due to the influence of reflections from the environment
such as a concrete foundation and a wall north of the PV arrays, which can be described
in Radiance, but not in bifacialvf. Compared to measured values bifacialvf was
able to estimate backside irradiance in multi-row systems with nRMSD in the range
13.8-31.3% and normalized Mean Bias Deviation (nMBD) in the range -3.7-14.1%. They
also found that bifacialvf is more accurate at estimating front side irradiance than
backside irradiance. Another transposition tool using VF, pvfactors, is presented in
Anoma et al. [29], where the tool’s performance is also compared to simulations with the
Radiance software program. The comparison was based on 1-month averaged modelled vs.
measured (with reference cells) GTI values on the front and back of two south-oriented
PV arrays with 1-axis backtracking. They found that pvfactors estimated monthly
bifacial gain of 6.7%, where the measured value was 7.0%. bifacialvf and pvfactors
are included in the validation study of Riedel-Lyngskær et al. [30], where 1-hour simula-
tions of front and rear side of a bifacial PV array were performed. The GTI estimated
with bifacialvf, pvfactors and other tools were compared to measured data from
both fixed tilt and horizontal 1-axis tracking south-oriented arrays. The authors found
that bifacialvf and pvfactors performed very similarly in terms of both rear-side
GTI estimation and estimation of bifacial gain on a monthly basis. The raytracing tool
bifacial radiance, presented in Pelaez and Deline [31], allows the user to interface
with the raytracing simulation software Radiance through python functions. Asgharzadeh
et al. [32] validated the tools pvfactors, bifacialvf and bifacial radiance and the
PVSyst program, which uses VFs, with data from a south-oriented fixed tilt system and a
sun-tracking system. Based on a single representative day they found that the tools using
VF overestimated the GTI during most hours while bifacial radiance was able to very
accurately estimate GTI during all hours except early morning.
Economic feasibility studies and yield assessments are typically based on annual or
bi-annual PV output estimations. While validation studies on transposition models and
tools exist, they generally focus on estimating the bifacial gain [28, 32, 33] from converting
light from both the front and the back, instead of only the front, and error statistics and
comparisons are often done on monthly timescales. A validation study on transposition
models and tools, with 1-minute time resolution, covering a longer period (> 1 month), for
VBPV panels is non-existent to this author’s knowledge. An aim of this study is to provide
such a validation including the models Perez90, Perez94, Muneer and CDRS, and
the tools pvfactors, bifacialvf and bifacial radiance. The tools are all available
as python packages and represent both VF and raytracing. The main disadvantage of
these tools is the limited programmable information about the PV environment which
pvfactors and bifacialvf can contain compared to bifacial radiance and other

commercial or inhouse PV simulation programs such as PVSyst. NREL’s PV simulation

Since GTI is modelled from system configuration, DHI and DNI, these values must be
known. The issue with DHI and DNI is that these values are rarely measured, due to a
significantly higher equipment cost (sensor and sun-tracking) as compared to measuring
only GHI. Decomposing measured GHI in to DHI and DNI can be done with decomposition
models (a.k.a. separation models). Due to the significance of daylight modelling in several
fields of science the number of models for estimating kd is today greater than 150 [34].
Before 2015 most models were created by regression with 1-day or 1-hour measured or
averaged data [35–38] (from now on referred to as 1-hour models). Higher time resolution
was not in wide demand and data with higher resolution was rarely available, both due
to high cost of digital storage and internet bandwidth limits. With advancement of
data storage and communication systems, measurement equipment and higher interest in
modelling on a 1-min timescale [39], newer models are generally created with 1-minute
data [3–5, 40, 41] (from now on referred to as 1-minute models).
In Gueymard and Ruiz-Arias [39] from 2016 the authors compared 140 decomposition
models based on estimation of 1-minute DNI, believed to be an as exhaustive as feasible list
based on a rigorous literature search, with measurements from 54 different research-grade
meteorological stations from Baseline Solar Radiation Network (BSRN), and a model was
suggested for each K¨oppen-Geiger climate zone (KGCZ). At the time it was the most
comprehensive and significant decomposition model validation study. They found that
while no model performed best at all stations, Engerer2 performed best (in terms of
nMBD and nRMSD) at a significant number of stations and was deemed ”quasi-universal”.
The authors suggested that the dominant performance of Engerer2 and the model
presented in Perez et al. [42], is because they both use variability as a model input, and
that Engerer2 consistently performed best because it was trained with 1-minute data,
matching the time resolution of the data used for validation. The authors argue that
because only marginal improvements in model performance is seen when comparing new
models to old models, further development should focus on models derived for specific
climate zones, referring to KGCZs [43, 44]. These conclusions motivated development of
new 1-min models using some form of variability as a predictor. Since 2016 Engerer2
as been the reference model of choice in publications of decomposition models, used as a
benchmark for model validation [3, 40, 45]. For the major KGCZ D the models Suehrcke,
Yao1, Yao3, Yao4, Skartveit and Engerer2 performed best. These models are
included in this work.

In Starke et al. [45] from 2018 the model proposed is testament to the importance of
CE effects. The authors used a breakpoint in a piecewise logistic model function based
on a comparison between GHI and estimated clear-sky GHI, Eghc. They also used a type
of variability index called persistence factor. While the model proposed in Starke et al.
[45] was based on the model proposed in Ridley, Boland, and Lauret [2], the significant
differences are the use of 1-minute data for regression and the change to a piecewise
function as mentioned. Two parameterizations of the model were provided, one for Brazil
and one for Australia. The proposed model was superior to other models, including
Engerer2, in terms of DNI estimation accuracy.
In Yang and Boland [3] two models, Yang1 and Yang2, are proposed and compared
to Engerer2 and the model proposed in [45] based on kd estimations. They introduce
satellite derived diffuse fraction ks
d as a predictor for the two proposed models, on the basis
that satellite derived databases and Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) outputs are
increasing in both accuracy and availability. Since satellite derived GHI, DHI and DNI are
also based on models, using satellite images as an input, the proposed models can be said to
be cascade models. The models are validated with two datasets. One dataset comprised by
measurements from five European BSRN stations, where the satellite data used to calculate
ks
d for each station location is downloaded from Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service
Radiation Service (CAMS-RAD), and one from 7 stations of SURFRAD, which is located
in the US, where satellite data was downloaded from National Solar Radiation Data Base
(NSRDB). The authors also re-parameterized the two proposed models, Engerer2 and
the model from Starke et al. [45] at each of the 7 USA stations, creating ’local’ models.
At US locations the proposed models Yang1 and Yang2 performed the best in terms of
nRMSD, while the results were more mixed between the proposed models and the model
from Starke et al. [45] in terms of nMBD. At the European locations Yang2 dominated
in terms of nRMSD, and again the performances were more mixed in terms of nMBD.
Overall the authors conclude that the proposed models outperform both Engerer2 and
the model from Starke et al. [45]. They also argue that the choice of predictors and
parameterization data has a significant influence, and in order to eliminate such choices
further research on decomposition models should focus on investigation the physical influ-
ence of weather and climate conditions on kd, instead of relying on mathematical regression.
In Paulescu and Blaga [40] a 1-min model, Paulescu, with a unique function type
was used, namely a heavyside step function in the model equation. This achieves the
same effect as using a set of equations where one is chosen based on cases of predictor
comparisons. Paulescu was derived with three years of data from Palaiseau, France,
which is in a temperate climate. Using the same dataset as was used in Gueymard and

arid climates. In terms of nRMSD Paulescu performed better than Engerer2 at all
except 4 locations, while the two models performed more equally in terms of nMBD. With
the dataset used for model validation in Hofmann and Seckmeyer [46] the performance
Paulescu was compared to the models proposed in Hofmann and Seckmeyer [46] and
Starke et al. [45] (Australian parameters). The three models performed very similarly
with nRMSD values in the range 17.73-18.52% in temperate climates, 21.39-22.99% in

The authors of Starke et al. [45] proposed a series of KGCZ-specific models in Starke
et al. [5] (e.g. Starke21Dfc for the ”Dfc” KGCZ), based on nearly the same model

the climate-specific models outperformed both Engerer2 and the models proposed in

The decomposition step is known to introduce significant uncertainty to the modelling
chain, typically more than the transposition step [12], but some model chain simulations
have also had the opposite result [51], i.e. that the transposition step adds more uncertainty
than the decomposition step. Such varied results indicate that the expected uncertainty
introduced by decomposition and transposition models varies with geographical location
and PV configuration.
The sheer number of classical regression-based decomposition models (from now on
called classical models) found in the literature (150+ [34]) accounts for the importance of
the topic, and although progress has been made since Liu & Jordan’s model from 1960

other models at any location on Earth). This is why the opportunities of Machine Learn-

has shown very promising results when used for pattern recognition in other fields of science.

Vector Machine models are the most stable when used on new data, the performance of
all types are very similar. For the 1-day models the more modern ensemble algorithms
outperformed the models based on Multi-Layer Perceptron, Support Vector Machine and
Decision Tree. For the 1-hour models it was not possible to select one best performing
method. The Random Forest algorithm showed the lowest loss when used with unseen data.

2.1.1 Atmospheric influence and solar irradiance at the Earth’s
surface
As photons traverse the atmosphere they may be absorbed or scattered. The part of the
light which is not absorbed or scattered in the atmosphere reaches the surface of the earth
as DNI (Ebn), sometimes called beam normal irradiance (giving name to the bn subscript),
which can be transposed to a horizontal surface as Ebh through Equation 2.3,
Ebh = Ebn cos θz
(2.3)
where θz is the solar zenith angle, defined as the angle between normal to horizontal and
the sun’s position in the sky, and can be calculated from information about geographical
location and time [59]. Angles such as θz are given and used in degrees unless specified
as being in radians. Molecules in aerosols and Water Vapour (WV) absorbs photons
by converting their energy to thermal energy, a process which attenuates the incident
irradiance and heats up the atmosphere. Light scattering introduces diffuse light in the
atmosphere as photons are deflected in different directions. The Rayleigh effect is scattering
of light with wavelengths much greater than the diameter of the particles, and is very
strong for photons with wavelengths in the blue color spectrum, which explains the blue
hue of a cloudless sky. When the sun is near the horizon, the light received by the observer

2.2
Solar resource measurement
The most common types of instruments used for measuring solar irradiance are pyrheliome-
ters and pyranometers [63]. A pyrheliometer measures DNI by having a 5◦-6◦field of view
and being mounted on a solar tracker. A pyranometer has a half-sphere (2π steradians)
==PAGE:41==
Page 19 of 124
2.3
Methods of solar resource estimation
field of view and can be used to measure GHI, DHI or GTI depending on how they are
mounted. To measure GHI a pyranometer is mounted horizontally in a location free from
shading. To measure DHI the pyranometer must be mounted horizontally on a solar
tracker which shades the pyranometer from DNI. Pyranometers have a glass dome over
the sensor which allows light from all directions to enter the instrument with minimal
reflections. The most common sensors used in pyrheliometers and pyranometers today
are thermoelectric [64]. Thermoelectric sensors convert irradiance to heat, and the heat
difference in the thermopile creates a voltage signal from which the total irradiance is
calculated. This process gives the sensor a typical response time of 1-30 seconds [63]. A
thermopile pyranometer or pyrheliometer have a spectrally flat irradiance response of the
solar spectrum.
Another commonly used type of sensor are photoelectric sensors. Photoelectric sensors
have a non-linear spectral response which is too narrow to measure the Sun’s full spec-
trum, but they replicate the solar irradiance conversion that occurs in PV cells, as the
photodiodes used in photoelectric sensors essentially are small PV cells [63]. Photodiodes
have a very fast response time on the order of microseconds. The sensor is often used
in reference cells, where they are covered with a flat glass panel. This flat panel causes
high reflectance of light incident at large angles, resulting in typically lower irradiance
measurement compared to a thermopile pyranometer.
The expected uncertainty of pyrheliometers and pyranometers equipped with different
sensors depend on sky conditions, AOI, cleaning routine, etc.. Before deployment, it is
standard to calibrate an instrument against an active cavity radiometer, which can be
found regionally around the world, and which in turn are calibrated against the World
Radiometric Reference. The 95% confidence uncertainty of a newly calibrated thermopile
pyrheliometer can be expected to be around 2.7, and 4.1 of a thermopile pyranometer [65],
as the pyranometer do suffer some cosine errors at very high AOI, and photodiodes can be
expected to have even higher uncertainty around 8 when used in a pyranometer and 8.9
when used in a pyrheliometer.

light but rather estimates kd aggregated over all wavelengths/frequencies measured by the

2.3.1 Decomposition modelling
Decomposition is done with empirical [34] models which estimate kd based on one or
several measured values. Decomposition models are functions or sets of functions created
for a given set of input variables (a.k.a. predictors) by fitting the function parameters to
a dataset by minimizing a loss function. The least squares method is a commonly used
loss function. Decomposition models have three features which they can be characterized
by; predictor(s), framework and training/parameterization data. A predictor is a model
input, and is calculated from meteorological data, longitude, latitude and a timestamp,
e.g. Equation 2.5 for calculating kt, which is the historically most significant predictor

relationship between kt and kd, illustrated in Figure 2.4, is related to how diffuse irradiance
is created through light scattering in the atmosphere, and that kt indicates the magnitude
of such scattering through the transparency of the atmosphere.

VFs are also used to calculate the direct and diffuse irradiance incident on segments on
the ground from which the reflected irradiance can be calculated with the albedo of that
ground segment. The VF between the ground segment and a tilted surface then gives the
ratio of the light reflected by the ground segment which is incident on the tilted surface,
assuming a perfect lambertian reflectance. This method may be applied to an environment
in any level of detail, splitting each surface in the environment up in to a number of small
segments, and the VFs between each segment and the rest of the environment can then be
calculated and used to estimate the sum of solar irradiance incident on a tilted surface
through various paths. Arias-Rosales and LeDuc [74] provides a rigorous review of the
impacts of different view factor methods.
Another method of estimating the solar irradiance incident on a tilted surface from
various reflections from the environment and the entire sky dome is raytracing. Raytracing
simulations calculate the paths of millions of rays through an environment based on
the optical properties of the objects in the environment. If a ray travels between the
tilted surface and a point on the sky dome, the solar energy radiating from that point
on the sky has a contribution to the total solar irradiance incident on the tilted surface.

environment. In backwards raytracing the paths are calculated from a surface and in to

2.3.4 Artificial Neural Networks
ML uses computer algorithms and automatic learning [75] to train models in patterns of
complex phenomena such as weather and climate systems, but also other highly complex
systems such as stock market analysis and text recognition. In the case of decomposition
modelling the pattern is information predictors contain about kd. An ANN is a ML model
inspired by the structure of neurons, chemical signal activation, axons and synapses in
animal brains. An ANN comprises a set of layers with a number of neurons each. The first
layer is the input layer, the last layer is the output layer and between the input and output
layers one or many hidden layers can be added. Figure 2.10 illustrates a small ANN with

neuron has a bias b. The sum of each input multiplied with the input weight plus the
neuron’s bias is then used as the input to an activation function. An activation function
modifies the neuron output before it is transferred to the neurons in the next layer of the
network. Any function can be used for activation but the rectified linear unit (ReLU) and
the Sigmoid functions are very popular due to good performance of networks which uses

Before training ANNs are initialized with weights and biases. The values are often
random, which has an influence on how many epochs it takes for the model to converge, and
also which local minimum of the loss function the model ends up converging to [75]. During
the learning process a batch of training samples are propagated through the network and
then the network weights and biases are updated with e.g. Gradient Descent. Gradient
Descent is a method of calculating which changes in parameters will most effectively
reduce the output of a loss function and eventually find a loss function minima [76]. The
trainable parameters are updated in the opposite direction of the gradiant of the loss
function multiplied by a learning rate. The learning rate may be set to decrease during
==PAGE:60==
2.3
Methods of solar resource estimation
Page 38 of 124
training in order to help the network converge toward a local minimum by making large
parameter updates at the beginning of the training and smaller updates as the training
progresses. This is done for each trainable parameter in the network after training with a
batch of the training data. Since the gradient of the loss function is based on the samples
propagated through the network during a batch, the batch size has an influence on the
stability of the training. If the batch size is very small (e.g. 1 or 2 samples) a single outlier
sample may cause the parameters to be updated in the opposite direction of the overall
minima and eventually make the model unable to converge to a local minimum. If the
batch size and training dataset is very large the training time may be very long as each
parameter update takes a long time to calculate. Additionally, a very large batch size
may not be possible to store in the computer memory, making the training impossible
on some systems. The number of layers and neurons in an ANN has a significant impact
on the network’s ability to learn the patterns between input(s) and the output(s). If the
network is very small it may be limited in its ability replicate patterns and if the network
is very large it may be so good at recognizing patterns in the training data that it gets
worse at estimating unseen patterns in the validation data, known as model overfitting.
Overfitting is a significant concern if the training dataset is not very large or only a few
network inputs are used. It is generally accepted that the simplest solution should be
preferred over more elaborate solutions, in this case a smaller model is preferred over a
larger model if they perform similarly [76]. Regularization is often used to reduce the risk
of overfitting. Regularization is done by adding a penalty based on the magnitude of the
parameter update to the error function.
ANN features which are specified during training (i.e. neuron weights and bias) are
called parameters, while model features which are specified by the model creater are
called hyperparameters. Hyperparameters include the number of hidden layers, number
of neurons per layer, batch size (number of samples between each parameter update),
activation function of each layer, regularizer used, dropout used, learning schedule for the
training process, the choice of optimizer and early stopping method.

3.2.3 Preprocessing
After obtaining the data each dataset is loaded in to a pandas DataFrame object in python.
1. The timestamps are converted to pandas DatetimeIndex, which are then used
as the index of the pandas DataFrames containing each dataset. Using pandas
DataFrame with DatetimeIndex provides excellent timeseries functionality such as
time resampling and interpolation, allows comparison of timestamps when merging
DataFrames, and it is a standard in pvlib python which is required for calculating
e.g. θz and E0n.
2. The time zone for the DataFrame is set.
3. Columns containing data not needed in this work are removed from the DataFrame.
This is mainly done to save computer memory and reduce loading time of the
preprocessed datasets.

removed.

3.2.4 Quality Control of irradiance measurements
The choice of quality control procedure varies in nearly all publications on validation of
optical models, both decomposition and transposition, which is apparent when reviewing
recent publications [3, 5, 39, 40, 45]. The only procedure which is used in the majority
of recent publications is from Long and Shi [78], which is also used by BSRN. While it
is an obvious merit to perform validation studies based on data created with the same
quality control procedure, currently no single complete procedure is widely accepted in
the field [85], and authors often add their own preferred control after using the procedure
from Long and Shi [78].
The first four QC checks used in this work are from procedure presented in Long
and Shi [78]. This procedure has been used by several publications since its adaptation
by Gueymard and Ruiz-Arias [39] (e.g. [5, 23, 40]) and is also used as a basis in other
solar irradiance data QC publications such as Yang, Yagli, and Quan [85]. While some
studies include additional QC checks, the procedure specified in Long and Shi [78] is the
most widely adopted. The procedure involves a series of checks where data not passing is
flagged as unreliable. The first two checks flag data which are outside global physically
possible limits through Equation 3.1 and extremely rare limits through Equation 3.2. The
upper and lower bounds are empirically based on atmospheric transmittance measured

3.5.2 Hyperparameters
Normalisation of input data is performed on all predictors. This is done by including a
normalisation layer in the ANNs which shifts and scales the inputs. In order to find a
good level of network complexity two grid searches are performed with a varying number
of layers and neurons per layer as illustrated in Figure 3.14. The batch size used during
these grid searches is calculated by splitting the TUAS dataset in to 100 steps per epoch,
which is 2131 when rounded down. This batch size is similar in magnitude to the one
found as optimal in Oh et al. [57], which was 1024. For perspective, the default batch
size in the Keras package is 32. A learning schedule with inverse time decay starting at
0.001 with a decay rate of 1 per epoch is used to reduce the risk of overfitting and help the
model converge. The Adaptive Moment Estimation (Adam) optimizer is used for gradiant
descent. Adam keeps an exponentially decaying average of past squared and non-squared
gradients to keep momentum from past parameter updates. Adam is recommended in
Ruder [76] as the best overall choice, and it is also used in the recent study Oh et al. [57].
The activation function used in all neurons in the hidden layers is the ReLU function.
Leaky-ReLU, which is similar to ReLU, was also tested but showed no difference in model
performance. The output layer which has a single neuron uses the sigmoid activation
function. Using sigmoid in the last layer ensures that all model outputs are in the range
0-1, which is useful as it is also the physically meaningfull range of kd. In Soares et al. [54],
the sigmoid transfer-function is used for activation of all neurons, but this was tested and
performed worse than using ReLU. During training the loss function used is mean squared
error (MSE). This is chosen as it is also the loss function generally used for regression
when training classical decomposition models. MSE was also used as a loss function in
other recent works [53, 57]. Early stopping based on the MSE (with patience of 20 epochs)
is used to avoid overfitting, as is also used in Oh et al. [57] (no patience was given) and
mentioned in Ruder [76] as a widely accepted method with no downsides.

and neurons per layer in the range 50-1000 including 50, 100, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600, 700,

3.6
Model performance indicators
The most commonly used indicators of solar irradiance model performance are the statistics
Mean Bias Deviation (MBD), Root Mean Square Deviation (RMSD) and R2 [89], where
MBD and RMSD are most often shown in their normalized forms nMBD and nRMSD,
which is also what will be used here. The term ”deviation” is used instead of ”error”
because the measurement itself is prone to be different from the true values, which are
unknown. In this work error is the difference between measurement and true value, and
deviation is between modelled and measured value. nMBD indicates if the estimated values
generally overpredict (for positive nMBD) or underpredict (for netagive nMBD) compared
to measured values. nRMSD measures difference between estimated and measured values,
where large deviations give a disproportional large increase in nRMSD as the deviation is
squared. A lower nRMSD indicates that the estimations are more accurate. R2 indicates
how well estimations of a value match the measured values through the variations between
measured and estimated and measured and mean of measured.

presents the model performance through three statistics in two dimensions, each on its
own axis:

1. The correlation coefficient in the angular distance (positive direction of rotation)

2. Normalized standard deviation in the radial distance from the origin of both the
polar and cartesian coordinates.
3. RMSD in the radial distance from the reference point, which is the normalized
standard deviation of the measurements on the horizontal axis of the cartesian

While use of Taylor diagrams is not common within instantaneous solar irradiance mod-
elling, uses and mentions does exist [39, 48, 91, 92]. The use of Taylor diagrams is
much more common within solar resource forecasting and precipitation and temperature
prediction, and modelling of other dynamic natural systems on Earth, which is apparent
by looking at recent citations of Taylor [90] (5369 citations as of 23/02/2022 according to
Google Scholar).
The pvlib-python implementations of the decomposition model Erbs and the empirical
transposition model Perez90 are included in this work. Including models implemented
in pvlib will serve as a sanity check to see if the data processing and custom model
implementations work as intended. An additional advantage of including Perez90 is
that both bifacialvf and bifacial radiance use it to calculate the distribution of
diffuse irradiance over the sky dome. Perez90 will be used to assess the improvement
in estimated GTI accuracy when using a transposition tool over just using an empirical
transposition model.

Chapter 5
Discussion
In this chapter the results presented in Chapter 4: Results are compared to the recent
validation studies reviewed in Chapter 1: Introduction, and the differences are discussed
based on the information in Chapter 2: Theory and literature presented in Chapter 1.
First the decomposition results are discussed, then the transposition results, and lastly
the limitations of this work.
5.1
Decomposition
The shape of the modelled kd-kt relationships in Figures 4.1 and 4.9 match the figures
in the recent model publications [3, 41, 45, 47] which suggest that the python model
implementations coded and used in this work are correct. Contrary to the results in this
work, Yang2 was observed to be superior to Yang1 in Yang and Boland [3]. Inspecting
the statistical indicators in the results tables [3] reveals that Yang2 surpassed Yang1 by
0.5%p-1%p nRMSD when used at a new location (5 stations in Europe), which is not a
large difference in performance considering that the two models had nRMSD in the range
22%-29%. The results in this work are generally opposite, with Yang1 outperforming
Yang2 by 0.1%p-1%p in terms of nRMSD and nMBD. The difference in model perfor-
mance may be due to the difference in latitude range of the data used for validation in
Yang and Boland [3] and this work. In Yang and Boland [3] five stations in the latitude
range 42.8◦N-52.0◦N is used and in this work the most southern location is DTU at latitude
55.69◦N.
In Yang [47] the performance of Yang2 and Yang4 was compared with data from
continental USA and Yang2 proved superior, similarly to the results at all three locations
in this work. While Starke21C and Starke21Cfb outperform Yang4 at TUAS and
DTU in this work, the opposite result was observed in Yang [34] for stations in the
major KGCZ C and D. This difference may be due to a difference in data used, although
the department of civil engineering at DTU is mentioned to provide data for Yang [34].
It is possible that Starke21C is superior to Yang4 when applied to the DTU data,
but not at a majority of the many other stations in KGCZ C listed as data sources in
Yang [34]. At three of the thirteen stations from KGCZ C Starke21Cfb has a lower
==PAGE:102==
5.1
Decomposition

nRMSD than Yang4, and DTU may be one of those three stations. The difference
in model performance may also be caused by the difference in QC procedure between
this work and Yang [34], as a few additional and more strict checks are included in the latter.
If the data used for parameterisation of the Yao models resembles the data from
SINTEF, it may explain why the Yao models outperform the other 1-hour models only
when applied to the SINTEF dataset. This would be the case if the autumn climate in
Trondheim is similar to the climate of the period of the data used to train the Yao models.
As listed in Table 2.1 the Yao models are trained with data from Shanghai recorded in the
period June - November, while the SINTEF dataset is recorded during the period 24th
of August - 18th of December, presented in Figure 3.9. The two periods are both less
than half a year and include data from the months August, September and November,
which may be why the Yao models perform well when applied to the SINTEF dataset. It
may also be a coincidence that the Yao models perform well when applied to the SINTEF
dataset, as both the training dataset used in Yao et al. [36] and the SINTEF dataset
are relatively small compared to e.g. the 4-year dataset from DTU, presented in Figure 3.10.
The impact of using kt as the only predictor, as is done in Erbs, Yao1, Yao3 and
Yao4, is seen in Figures 4.1, 4.5 and 4.9 as these models result in 1-dimentional kd-kt
relationship. While the models may fit the data as well as possible, e.g. like Erbs matches
the averaged kd when applied to the DTU dataset as shown in Figure 4.9, only using a
single predictor inherently limits the potential of a model to capture the complex patterns
between kd and sky conditions. This observation is similar to the findings in Gueymard
and Ruiz-Arias [39], and suggests that using multiple predictors is strong advantage. The
question is then which predictors to include. Based on the relationships shown in Figure 2.6
and the correlation coefficients in Figure 2.5 it is observed that predictors such as ψ does
not add any new information about kd, as it is extremely correlated with kt (Spearman
correlation of 1) while having the same correlation with kd as kt, which indicates that ψ
contains the same information about kd as kt for the 1-minute resampled post QC TUAS
dataset, and including both is redundant. In Gueymard and Ruiz-Arias [39] the authors
found that most highly successful models used two or three predictors, indicating that
using more predictors will not always guarantee improvement. However, when looking
at the models listed in Table 2.2 rows from Engerer2 and below most models use 5 or
more predictors. Newer models using predictors such as kde, ∆ktc and ks
d have proven both
here and in recent validation studies [34, 39] to have improved kd estimation capability.
As critisised in Gueymard and Ruiz-Arias [39], most predictors beyond kt are added
multi-linearly to the same position in the model framework as kt, which is still observed for
even the newest models described in Section 2.3.1: Decomposition modelling. The original
idea behind using the logistic function in a model framework is how it matches the shape
==PAGE:103==
Page 81 of 124
5.1
Decomposition
of the kd-kt relationship observed in Figure 2.4 [93]. Why some predictors such as θz, kde
and ∆ktc are added in the exponential of the denominator is unintuitive as their individual
relationships with kd does not resemble the kd-kt relationship at all (see Figure 2.6). As
argued in Gueymard and Ruiz-Arias [39] including predictors in a model framework based
on the shape of their individual kd relationships is likely to provide a better overall model
performance and a significantly better ability to generalise, i.e. not lose performance when
applied to data from a climate zone different from the climate zone of the data used to
train the model. The position of ks
d in the model function is the difference between Yang1
and Yang2, and as mentioned Yang2 was found to be superior of the two in Yang and
Boland [3], while Yang1 performed better in this work. The subject of where and how
to include certain predictors is largely overlooked in the literature [4, 5, 40] and when
investigated it is usually done with trial and error [2, 3]. Further research should be done
on how model accuracy is influenced by how predictors are included in the model framework.
For a specific KGCZ it is logical to expect a model trained with data from that climate
zone to perform better than a model which is not climate specific [5, 39]. While this is
observed to be true for the DTU dataset which is in the Cfb zone (see Figure 3.3), it
is not observed for the TUAS dataset. For the TUAS dataset, which is on the border
between areas of Dfb and Dfc zones, both the Yang models, which are parameterised
with data from multiple different major zones (continental USA), and the Starke models
Starke21C and Starke21Cfb, which are parameterised with data from C and Cfb
zones respectively, outperform the Starke models Starke21D and Starke21Dfc, which
are specific to the KGCZ of the TUAS dataset. The same is observed and discussed
in Yang [34], where the author questions whether it makes sense to use the KGCZs
for decomposition model validations and suggests that a classification scheme based on
different sky conditions or seasonal radiation patterns be developed and used instead. As
the KGCZ classifications are based on seasonal precipitation and temperature patterns [94],
they may be unsuited as a geographical classification scheme for decomposition models. If
seasonal radiation patterns or sky conditions are significantly correlated with precipitation
or temperature, the KGCZ may be a good method. In Starke et al. [5], in which all
Starke models used in this work are proposed, the authors also observe that climate or
even station specific models do not always outperform all other model parameterisations
when applied to data belonging to the model’s zone. They conclude that such behavior is
probably caused by the data reported by the stations not behaving as expected for the
KGCZ it is measured in. They mention that either the station has a unique climate not
accounted for in the KGCZ scheme, or that a better method of climate classification for
validation of decomposition models exist. In their conclusion it is also suggested to find
or create a better classification scheme than KGCZ, which the results in this work supports.

Another contribution to the high nRMSD values observed in this work may be the
difference of the instruments used at TUAS. GTI is measured with photodiode RCs while
DHI and DNI is measured with thermopile sensors. This contribution is threefold and due
to the difference in the instruments’ spectral, angular and temporal response. The first
issue is the difference in spectral response. While the DHI and DNI are measurements of
a linear response to irradiance over the Sun’s full spectrum, GTI is a measurement of a
non-linear response to a window of frequencies narrower the Sun’s spectrum. As mentioned
in Section 2.2: Solar resource measurement the RCs are likely to measure a lower irradiance
than a thermopile pyranometer in the same configuration would, depending on atmospheric
and sky conditions. If this is the case for the TUAS data, then one would expect GTI
estimations from an unbiased transposition model to be higher than the measured values,
but this is not observed in Figure 4.16. While the models all have positive nMBD values
for the west orientation, they generally underestimate GTI in the east orientation. As
mentioned in this section, the negative bias of the model estimations observed in the east
orientation may be caused by shading of the pyrheliometer used to measure DNI, which
may offset the expected positive bias caused by the difference in spectral response. The
second issue is the expected high uncertainty of RCs at large AOI due to reflection losses
[63]. The magnitude of the reflection losses depends on the coating of the glass window
covering the sensor, but this information is not found on the RC manufacturer’s website
[95]. Losses due to reflections would again cause the RCs to measure a lower irradiance
==PAGE:106==
5.2
Transposition
Page 84 of 124
than a thermopile pyranometer in the same configuration would. The third issue is the
response time of the two sensors. On the website of the manufacturer of the specific
pyranometer (Kipp&Zonen CMP21) and pyrheliometer (Kipp&Zonen CHP1) a 5-second
response time is listed for both instruments under product specifications [96]. The raw
data provided by TUAS is in 1-second resolution which is faster than a thermopile sensor’s
response time and a lot slower than a photodiode sensor’s response time. Fast changes in
solar irradiance, e.g. due to a cloud no longer blocking the Sun, may be recorded by the
RCs used at TUAS during the end of one minute, but not recorded by the pyranometer
before the beginning of the next minute. In that case the temporal difference in when
information is captured by the two instruments would lead to an increase in nRMSD of the
model estimations, but may not influence the nMBD. The preferable solution in the case of
validation of transposition models is to use the same instruments for all measurements [63,
64]. A cheaper solution is to apply corrections for the differences in spectral, angular and
temporal response to the measurements, before applying the transposition models and tools.
The performance of bifacial radiance observed in this work is better than the
best observed empirical transposition model Perez94 and the other tools considered
(pvfactors and bifacialvf). However, the python code required to accurately describe
the environment in bifacial radiance, and the time it takes to run a 1-year simulation
with 1-minute resolution is significant compared to the effort required to use bifacialvf
and pvfactors. For this work it took one work day to write the python code for both
bifacialvf and pvfactors, and the simulations take around 7 minutes and 30 seconds
respectively to run on a Dell Latitude 7520 with an 4-core Intel i5-1135G7 @ 2.4GHz
processor, 16 GB RAM and an Intel Iris Xe integrated graphics processor. When using
bifacial radiance with a single CPU core (i.e. one process as is default in python)
the 1-year simulation is estimated to take around 15 days. When running 30 processes in
parallel on a high power computer with an AMD EPYC 7763 64-core processor, 512 GB
ram and two workstation graphics cards (NVIDIA Tesla T4 + NVIDIA RTX A5000) a
1-year simulation takes around 24 hours. Granted that the code used to run the simulation
in multiple processes can be optimised, this difference in time required, relative to the
observed improvement in accuracy, is very high. It can not be recommended to use
bifacial radiance for 1-row PV arrays. However, the difference in accuracy of the tools
is likely to be greater for larger and more complex systems than the E-W VBPV array at
TUAS [30, 33, 97], and further validation studies are required for multi-row E-W VBPV
systems. Instead of bifacial radiance, it is recommended to use Perez94 for single
row VBPV arrays and bifacialvf for multi-row VBPV arrays.

5.3
Limitations of this study
In Skartveit, Olseth, and Tuft [68] several optional features of Skartveit are described.
One is the use regional albedo as a predictor. The regional albedo increases the amount
of backscattering, and is particularly important in high latitude mountainous areas with
snow cover often far in to spring/early summer, such as Trondheim where the SINTEF
dataset is from. In this work regional albedo is not included due to time limitations. It
would require additional effort to add the model feature in the code and to acquire and
process the necessary data.
Analysis of decomposition model performance under different sky or temporal con-
ditions such as kt > 0.8 or month of year may indicate that a specific model should be
chosen based on conditions other than geographical location, but such an analysis is not
included in this work due to time limitations.
As discussed in Section 5.2: Transposition there are several differences between the
instruments used to measure DHI and DNI, and GTI at TUAS. The model estimations
may better match the measured data if the same instrument are used to measure all
irradiances, but whether all models would improve equally (or improve at all) is unknown.
An additional transposition validation study without this caveat is suggested to investigate
if the difference in instrumentation is significant for the model performance benchmarks.
Vertical east and west GTI measured with a thermopile pyranometer at DTU was offered,
but because the measurement station was finished during a late state of this work the
approx. 1-month period of data available is not long enough to provide a good represen-
tation of expected model performance. The additional data could be used to assess the
influence of using different instruments, but such an analysis is not included, again due to
time limitations.
The time requirement to run bifacial radiance described in Section 5.2: Transposi-
tion limited the analysis to not include how the tool’s accuracy is influenced by the level of
environment detail such as buildings, trees and accuracy of ground albedo included in the
simulations. This analysis was planned and tested in the early stages of this work but ended
up being left out due to time limitations. The biggest advantage of bifacial radiance
over the other simulation tools is the level of environment detail which can be included in
the simulation [98]. Other studies [30, 33, 97] have shown that raytracing is significantly
more accurate than VF when the complexity of the simulated system is high. A more
user-friendly open-source raytracing tool with build-in parallel processing would reduce
the barrier of using raytracing on high time resolutions such as 1-minute.
==PAGE:108==
5.3
Limitations of this study
Page 86 of 124
In this work the two modelling steps, decomposition and transposition, are treated
separately. Since the goal is estimating GTI from GHI, time and geographical location, the
uncertainty from combinations of decomposition and transposition models is of interest.
While it is possible to find an optimal combination for a certain location, the combination
may work well because the two models cancel out each other’s issues, e.g. a transposition
with a positive bias may cancel out the negative bias of a decomposition model. A model
user’s approach to this issue depends on the user and the use case. In this work combinations
of models are not investigated, and the suggestion is to use the best performing model for
each step at any location. Again this limitation is due to time constraints.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The main goal of this work was to assess the expected performance of models or software
tools for the two steps involved in estimating GTI from GHI, time and geographical
location, when applied to 1-minute resolution data measured at high latitudes such as the
Nordics, and E-W VBPV.
Before applying the models the three ground measured datasets from TUAS in Turku,
Finland, SINTEF in Trondheim, Norway, and DTU measured at Risø, Denmark, were
preprocessed and a series of QC checks were applied. Data samples which did not pass all
checks were discarded.
In the first step, decomposition of GHI in to DHI and DNI by estimating kd, python im-
plementations of the empirical models Erbs, Suehrcke, Yao1, Yao3, Yao4, Skartveit,
Engerer2, Paulescu, Engerer2:2019, Yang1, Yang2, Yang4, Starke21C, Starke21Cfb,
Starke21D, Starke21Dfc were validated with the three datasets. The models were
validated at each location based on a visual impression of how well estimated kd-kt re-
lationship matches the measured relationship, the statistical indicators R2, nMBD and
nRMSD, and Taylor diagrams.
The results of the validation of decomposition models showed that Yang1 is the most
accurate model at TUAS and SINTEF while Starke21C is the most accurate model at
DTU. At TUAS and DTU Yang1 and Starke21C performed similarly with nMBD and
nRMSD values within 2%p of each other. At SINTEF Yang1 with a nRMSD of 13.3%
and R2 of 0.73 performed significantly better than Starke21C which had a nRMSD of
14.9% and R2 of 0.65.
In order to assess the potential of ANNs for decomposition modelling, an ANN was
trained after conducting a search for optimal hyperparameters. The dataset and model
inputs used to train Yang1 was replicated and used to train the ANN. The ANN performed
on average relative to the other 1-minute decomposition models at all three locations, but
was not able to perform better than Yang1 at any of the three locations.
==PAGE:110==
6.1
Further work
Page 88 of 124
In the second step, transposition of measured DHI and DNI to vertical east and west
orientations, python implementations of the empirical transposition models Perez94,
Muneer, CDRS and Perez90, and the transposition software tools pvfactors, bifa-
cialvf and bifacial radiance were validated with a 1-minute dataset from TUAS’ E-W
VBPV experimental setup. The validation was based on a visual impression of estimated
vs. measured GTI, the statistical indicators R2, nMBD and nRMSD, a Taylor diagram
and lastly the estimated and measured GTI aggregated to average values based on kt,
month of year, kd and hour of day.
Of the empirical transposition models Perez94 performed best with R2 of 0.82, nMBD
of -5.1% and nRMSD of 58.6% in the vertical east orientation and R2 of 0.86, nMBD of
2.1% and nRMSD 51.4% in the vertical west orientation. Of all transposition methods
the raytracing tool bifacial radiance performed best with R2 of 0.82, nMBD of -6.3%
and nRMSD of 58.0% in the east orientation and R2 of 0.86, nMBD of 2.7% and nRMSD
50.4% in the west orientation.
The difference in performance of all methods in the two orientations was attributed to
a difference in shading of the instrument measuring DNI and the instrument measuring
GTI. The high nRMSD values were attributed to the high time resolution of the dataset
and differences in the thermopile pyranometer used to measure DNI and the photodiode
reference cells used to measure GTI. Due to the very low improvement from using bifa-
cial radiance over the other software tools or Perez94, and the significantly greater
effort required to use bifacial radiance compared to the other methods, Perez94 was
recommended for single-row VBPV.
For future PV projects the developer may use the recommendations and statistical
indicators found in this work as guidelines to which models to use in their assessment of
the solar resource and the uncertainty they may expect. For each modelling step additional
recommendations for further model development was given based on theory, the results of
this work and similar observations and considerations in recent literature. For scientists in
the field of PV system design or solar resource modelling the recommendations can be
used as inspiration for research topics further improving the reliability and accuracy of
feasibility studies, which may in turn aid the transition from relying on fossil fuels to a
more sustainable energy production in the future.
