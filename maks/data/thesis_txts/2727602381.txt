Abstract
DTUsat-3 is the third student built spacecraft (S/C) from the Technical University of Den-
mark (DTU). Its development has so far been conducted on a subsystem level, without
an overall test of the entire S/C having been carried out. This project aims at completing
development on the core systems for DTUsat-3 and conduct a comprehensive test of the
platform. With the core systems functional, new subsystems can be envisioned, build and
tested with relative certainty of a fully functional S/C at launch.
The subsystem carrier board (SSCB) and electric power system (EPS) have been up-
dated with new additions and replacements for the underpowered step-up converter on
the on-board computer (OBC) has been evaluated and tested. A new subsystem (the
external noteworthy information detector (ENID)) for external connections have been en-
visioned and a Bluetooth chip for wireless communication have been found. ENID is
mainly intended to be used on stratospheric balloon flights.
Due to manufacturing errors by an external manufacturer, the SSCB and EPS updates
have unfortunately not been tested.

1
Introduction
The idea of a standardised CubeSat format was presented at the 14th Annual USU Con-
ference on Small Satellites in 2000 [1]. This idea has since grown to a point where it’s
possible to buy a professionally designed, built and tested CubeSat on the internet [2].
Having a small standardised platform allows for a low cost spacecraft (S/C) to be launched
into orbit. This allows new technologies and new commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS) com-
ponents to be flight tested in a low cost, low risk environment. As larger, more expensive
missions require a higher level of certainty that every part of the mission will perform as
expected, the large missions are unable, or at least very hesitant, to accept new tech-
nologies on board. With flight testing able to move to CubeSats, these technologies can
be flight proven before being implemented on a larger mission.
1.1
The CubeSat platform
The Cubesat platform is based on a standard size of 10cmx10cmx10cm with a mass of
up to 2kg. A CubeSat of this size is called a ”1 Unit (1U)” CubeSat, with larger CubeSats
being multiple of this size. The sizes of the CubeSat standard ranges from 1U to 12U, as
seen in Figure 1.1. However, other sizes are used by some organisations.
While the CubeSat standard provides specifications for the form factor of the S/C, it does
not however, specify any electronics form factor or communication protocol. The stan-
dard do provide some electrical requirements, such as requiring a remove before flight
(RBF) pin to be implemented and at least three independent RF inhibitors to ensure no
RF transmissions occur before launch. Individual launch providers each have additional
requirements that must be fulfilled in order to launch the S/C. These requirements vary
depending on launch provider and the launch method. For example, when launching from

1.1.1
Using CubeSats for other missions
While CubeSats are originally designed for low cost missions in low Earth orbit (LEO),
their popularity has caused them to be used for other missions as well. In 2018, the
National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) launched two 6U CubeSats as
a ride-share on a Mars flyby mission named Mars Cube One (MarCO). MarCO carried
communication and navigation experiments to test their use in interplanetary flight and
succeeded in taking the first image of Mars from a CubeSat before their mission ended
[5].
In addition to sending CubeSats out into the solar system, the platform can also be used
on missions that doesn’t reach orbit. One such mission could be a stratospheric balloon
flight. These balloons can reach altitude of over 50km and require the systems on board
to operate without human interaction post launch [6]. While the environment in the strato-
sphere is significantly different from that in LEO, many of the same considerations must
be made when designing the systems. This means that a stratospheric balloon is a well
suited platform to test CubeSat hardware before launching onto space. The CubeSat
platform can then be used as a relay station and transfer data from other experiments
flown on the balloon to a ground station. This exact use case is intended for DTUsat-3
to test the hardware and provide future experiments the ability to report back during flight
with housekeeping data and possibly scientific data depending on setup.
1.2
Previous DTUsat missions
As the name suggests, DTUsat-3 is the third student built S/C made at the Technical
University of Denmark (DTU). The first S/C, DTUsat-1, was launched in 2003 with the
aim of building a functioning CubeSat. DTUsat-1 was to achieve full 3-axis attitude control
using combined sun- and magnetometer sensors build for the S/C, return pictures of Earth
and lower the orbit of the S/C using a deployable tether. Unfortunately, DTUsat-1 never
established any form of contact. Two of the other seven S/Cs on the same launch was
likewise never heard from. The remaining 5 S/Cs on the launch were fully functional [7].
The second mission, DTUsat-2, launched in 2014 and was to track migratory birds from
space. DTUsat-2 was to receive Global Positioning System (GPS) data from radio trans-
mitters mounted on carrier birds. The transmitter was reduced in size such that it was
<5g and could thus be mounted on even small migratory birds providing unparalleled
accuracy in tracking the migration of these birds. DTUsat-2 was successfully launched,
with the 2.4GHz beacon detectable on the first pass over DTU. Unfortunately, problems
within the electric power system (EPS) caused the batteries to discharge too much and
never recover. Combined with unforseen problems with the ground station (GS), two way
communications was never established. The 2.4GHz beacon was operational when the
solar panels were illuminated and was last heard in 2019 [8, 9].
1.2.1
Learning from DTUsat-1 and DTUsat-2
The lessons learned from the first two DTUsats have caused the basic philosophy and
structure of DTUsat-3 to be vastly different from those on DTUsat-1 and DTUsat-2. DTUsat-
3 is designed and built with modularity and ease of integration at its core. The core sys-
tems on DTUsat-3 will be designed in such a way that the payload systems on board
will all share the same physical and electrical connectors. This allow each subsystem to

2.1
Boost converter
Boost converters are non-isolated, non-inverting converters, i.e. the input and output volt-
ages share a common ground and the output voltage is of the same polarity as the input
voltage. As the name suggests, a boost converter boosts the voltage, that is the voltage
on the output of the converter is higher than the voltage on the input of the converter [10].
The general schematic of a boost converter is shown in Figure 2.1.
A boost converter is driven by the tendency of an inductor to resist changes in current
by storing energy in its magnetic field. When the switch (marked with S in Figure 2.1) is
closed, i.e. in the on-state, the current flows through the inductor and stores energy in the
magnetic field. Once the switch opens, i.e. in the off-state, the current flowing through the
inductor drops due to the increased impedance represented by C and R. The magnetic
field generated during the on-state will be reduced in order to maintain the current to the
load (R). The current paths during of the two states are shown in Figure 2.1.
If the switch is cycled fast enough for the inductor to never completely discharge, the load
will see a voltage that is always higher than the voltage of the input when the switch is
open. During the off state, the capacitor placed in parallel to the load will be charged by the
current running through it. This capacitor is discharged during the on-state, providing the
load with energy during the on-state. The blocking diode (D) ensures the capacitor doesn’t
discharge through the closed switch during the on-state. In order to have a continuous
supply of energy to the load, the switch must open before the capacitor is fully discharged.
The duration of the on state is D · Tsw = Ton, where D is the duty cycle of the circuit, Tsw
is the time to complete the switching cycle and Ton is the amount of time that the switch
is on during one switching cycle. As the switch can only be on or off, the remainder of
the switching cycle must be in the off state. The duration of the off state, Toff, is thus
Toff = (1 −D) · TS. In the on-state, the voltage across the inductor VL is equal to the
input voltage Vin. During the off state, VL = Vout −Vin. If we assume that the voltage
across the inductor must be 0 during steady state operation, the transfer function of the
boost converter becomes

2.2
Radiation in space
As a spacecraft (S/C) leaves the protection of the Earths atmosphere it will be subjected to
the radiation of space. The severity and cause of the effects varies depending on where
the S/C is. In general, radiation effects are divided into two types, single event effect
(SEE) and dose effects. Dose effects can be seen as the ageing effect for electronics,
they are the result of exposure to radiation over a longer period of time. SEEs can be
seen as the electronic equivalent of breaking your arm, it’s a sudden unexpected effect
that may be mitigated if proper action is taken. SEEs have many factors impacting their
occurrence, but they are independent of time [13].
DTUsat-3 will be launched into low Earth orbit (LEO). The exact altitude will depend on
the still unselected launcher and the requirements of the unbuilt payload. An altitude of
400-800km is the expected window of operation. The radiation in this region is dominated
by protons with a relatively low total ionising dose (TID) [13].
The annual radiation dose in circular equatorial orbit at different altitudes is shown in Fig-
ure 2.3. If the orbit is elliptical or inclined, the radiation received will change significantly.
A CubeSat launched from the international space station (ISS) will follow a similar orbit to
the ISS at an altitude of 420km with a 51.64° inclination. This orbit will expose the S/C to
the effects of the south Atlantic anomaly (SAA) shown in Figure 2.4. The SAA is a region

2.2.1
Single event effect (SEEs)
A single event effect can take many forms and are caused is caused by a single energetic
particle, mainly cosmic rays and high energy protons. A SEE caused by a cosmic ray is
typically caused by the heavy ion component of the cosmic ray. If an ion transversing a
device deposits a sufficient charge it may cause errors such as bit flips to occur in memory.
These cosmic rays are solar or galactic in origin. High energy protons, such as those in
the inner van Allen belt, can cause direct ionisation in sensitive devices like the cosmic
rays. However, the effect can also be indirect ionisation caused by a nuclear reaction
near a sensitive device area [15].
A non-destructive SEE, such as a bit flip in a memory block, will typically effect a small
area temporarily of an integrated circuit (IC) without resulting in catastrophic failure of
the system. These effects include single event upset (SEU). Destructive SEEs are, as
the name suggest, able to cause catastrophic failure on both system and S/C level if left
undetected and/or untreated. One example of a destructive SEE is the single event latch-
up (SEL), which is a latch-up (or ”short”) caused by a SEU. Generally, a latch-up occurs
when a high energy particle passes through one of the two inner transistor junctions of a
PNPN structure, causing the thyristor-like structure to turn on until the device is power-
cycled. This effect can cause a low resistance path to open for high amounts of current
to run through, which if left unchecked can cause irreversible damage to occur, resulting
in a single event brownout (SEB).

2.2.2
Total Ionisation Dose (TID)
As radiation is absorbed by an IC, its general performance will gradually degrade. This
degradation is characterised by the TID that a device has been exposed to. The main
sources for radiation are particles from Solar Energetic Particle Events, usually associ-
ated with solar flares, and the radiation in the inner van Allen belt in low altitudes over the
SAA. While high energy particles lead to SEEs, the particles dominating the TID are the
low energy particles. These particles doesn’t have the energy to cause a sudden effect,
but over time, many low energy particles will collectively create measurable effects. The
radiation will gradually create electron hole pairs resulting in a charge accumulation in the
interface regions of the IC. This charge will change threshold voltages in the internal com-
plementary metal oxide semiconductor (CMOS) transistors, increase power consumption
and ground leakage, change timings etc. [16].

3
System redesigns
3.1
Subsystem carrier board (SSCB)
As outlined in Section 2.2, single event effects (SEEs) must be expected to happen dur-
ing the mission, and measures must be taken to ensure a single event latch-up (SEL)
doesn’t turn into a single event brownout (SEB) with the potential of total mission failure.
A protection system is implemented on the subsystem carrier board (SSCB) in order to
power-cycle subsystems that have experienced a SEL. This circuitry is installed on the
SSCB rather than the individual subsystems for two main reasons. Firstly, all subsystems
must be equipped with a protection circuit. By placing it on the SSCB, testing on the cir-
cuit can happen well before individual subsystems are even thought of. Secondly, space
on a subsystem board is very limited, and moving the protection circuit from the subsys-
tems to the SSCB frees up space for each subsystem to use for themselves. In addition,
space on the SSCB is at less of a premium than on the individual subsystems. Placing
the protection circuit on the SSCB uses space that otherwise would be largely unused.
If a subsystem experience a SEL, the current drawn by the subsystem will dramatically
increase to far above what the subsystem use during regular operation. The basic princi-
ple of the protection system is to power cycle the subsystem whenever the circuit detects
that the subsystem draws a current above a set threshold. The basic structure of a typical
SEL protection circuit is shown in Figure 3.1.

3.1.2
Initial changes to the SEL protection
There is a possibility that future subsystems are built with large amounts of capacitance.
This capacitance can draw large amounts of power when the subsystem is initially turned
on. A power draw large enough to trigger the SEL protection circuit as the current exceeds
the allowed current during normal operation. Drawing large amounts of power in order to
charge capacitors is however, perfectly safe to do. This necessitates a requirement for
the SEL not to trigger when a subsystem is initially turned on. This is done by supressing
the SEL protection circuit for a period of time. This can be done by sending a power
good signal from the load to the SSCB when certain conditions in the power delivery is
met, usually select voltages have to be at a specified magnitude and be stable. Sending
a power good signal from the subsystem to the SEL protection system on the SSCB
require a dedicated pin on the connector between the SSCB and the subsystem, as well as
making the subsystem able to send a power good signal. While the connection between
the SSCB and the subsystem have unassigned pins, making sure that each subsystem
sends a power good signal require extra standardised components on each subsystem,
which in turn takes up space on the subsystem board, space which as described above
is at a premium. It is thus decided that any implementation of a suppression system must
be located entirely on the SSCB.
The best way to suppress the SEL protection is to fool the current sensor into thinking that
no, or little, current is drawn by the subsystem. This can be done by pulling the positive
leg of the current sensor low. This will cause the voltage input to be negative as opposed

collector and emitter pins. With the emitter pin connected to ground, the collector pin will
go logically low, thus completing the NOT gate.

gate. The time constant of the RC circuit, τ = R·C, is chosen initially to be 5ms. This can
be modified according to individual subsystem requirements and overall requirements.

Upon self-analysing the changes, it is found that this implementation of suppressing the
current sensor is flawed, and will effectively remove the effect of the SEL protection circuit.
This is due to two flaws in the design.
The first design flaw is the RC circuit implemented to time the suppression. This RC circuit
will need to charge each time a SEL is detected, slowing down the response time of the
protection. This could potentially lead to a SEB in the time it take the RC circuit to charge
such that the circuit triggers and cuts power to the subsystem.
The second, and arguably more fatal, flaw is the location where the trigger point is mea-
sured. This implementation requires the voltage at LOI 4 to go high before the suppression
system is turned off. However, this will never happen as the suppression maintains a low
signal at LOI 3. The suppression will thus never turn off, effectively removing the effect of
the entire circuit. A new implementation is thus conceived.
3.1.3
Final version of the SEL protection
The basic idea of pulling the positive pin of the current sensor to ground in order to sup-
press the sensor is kept. The measuring point for the suppression system is moved to
the 3.3V bus on the subsystem side of the circuit. The physical location of the measuring
point is on the SSCB, but this bus will only be powered when the subsystem is enabled.
When the subsystem is disabled, the capacitors on the subsystem will discharge and the
3.3V bus will be at 0V after a long time has passed. The RC circuit is removed, as the
SEL protection needs to rapidly turn off the power to the subsystem. The subsystem it-
self will take its place and act as a large RC circuit with the exact timing needed for that
particular subsystem to charge its capacitors. The voltage on the 3.3V bus will thus not
jump instantly to 3.3V, but raise in a manner similar to that of a basic RC circuit.
Some select subsystems might require the SEL protection to be on at all times, even
when charging capacitors. While others might be deemed to not need the protection at
all. In order to accommodate these eventualities, two 0Ωresistors are implemented to
permanently disable or enable the SEL protection. This is done by forcing the base pin of
the suppressing transistor, named Q301 in Figure 3.4, to either 3.3V or ground. Mounting

3.1.3.1
Changes based on previous work
The capacitor named C602 in Figure 3.2 sits directly at the output of the current sensing
operational amplifier (opamp). Connecting a capacitor directly on the output of an opamp
to ground can potentially interfere with the characteristics of the opamp. The latest testing
on the SSCB conducted in 2021 has shown that the capacitive loading causes the opamp
to become unstable [18]. In additon to removing the capacitor mounted straight on the
opamp output, it is suggested to mount a resistor in series with the diode towards the
current threshold opamp to further reduce the instability of the current sensor. This resistor
is to have a resistance of at least 142Ω[18]. The smallest resistor available in the DTUsat-
3 library meeting this requirement is a 220Ωresistor. This resistor is chosen as the series
resistor.
The resistors setting the threshold for the comparator to trigger a power cycle is proposed
to be replaced with new values. This change is not implemented for two reasons. Previ-
ous tests conducted in 2020 has shown that the current threshold resistors are sized such
that the SEL protection triggers close to the desired 700mA. However, the SEL protection
circuit was shown to trigger at different currents with changing temperature [19]. In addi-
tion to previous testing showing desired behaviour, the addition of a suppression system
and changes to the circuitry between the opamps represent a significant change to the
system. And in order to keep the changes at a evolutionary level where the changes can
be tested and evaluated, the changes to the threshold resistors are not made. The final
schematic for the SEL protection circuit is shown in Figure 3.4.

Other changes to the SSCB
Previous work on the SSCB has shown that some small but significant mistakes have
been made at some point during the design phase of the SSCB. These design mistakes
are corrected in for this revision.
When a subsystem is enabled, the 5 communication channels are enabled by switches
on the SSCB. Each switch enables two channels, and as such there are 10 switches in
total to serve the four subsystems. These 10 switches have been connected to the power
in reverse. The supply voltage pin of the switch has been connected to ground, and vica
versa. Due to the symmetrical nature and simplicity of the switches, previous versions
could fix this ad hoc by rotating the switches 180° [19].
The ”BUS_UART2_OBC_RX_SS_TX” connection at the switches has by mistake been
connected to the ”BUS_SR_LCLK” pin on the stack connector. This results in the UART2
bus being unusable on previous versions of the SSCB [19]. These two mistakes are
corrected for this revision of the SSCB.

3.2
On-board computer (OBC) step-up converter
Previous work on the on-board computer (OBC) has shown that the current step-up con-
verter is not able to deliver the current required by the OBC [20]. Three new step-up
converters has been suggested to replace the current XCL103D converter. These are
evaluated in this section.
3.2.1
TPS61256A
The TPS61256A from Texas Instruments is a fixed output step-up converter with a small
form factor and a minimum of external components. As seen in Figure 3.6a, only three
external components are required for the step-up converter to function. This is due to the
fixed 5.0V output of the converter. Due to the small size of the converter and low number
of external components, the total solution size for the converter can be as low as 35mm2
[21].
However, the small size of the TPS61256A is also it’s biggest disadvantage. The 9DSBGA
package with a 0.4mm pitch can prove difficult to accurately solder on to the PCB in the
manufacturing process. This can be mitigated by having the components of the OBC
soldered on to the PCB by the PCB manufacturer. PCB manufacturers have access to pick
& place machines not available at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). Additionally,
if the specific chip used doesn’t work due to one reason or another, for example too high
input voltage applied or a manufacturing defect, replacing a ball grid array (BGA) chip
is generally not a simple job. However, only one ball is hidden from the outside as the
TPS61256A is a 9 pin BGA chip. Additionally, the central pin is connected to the same
terminal as one of the outside pins. This makes a replacement job easier, but still difficult.

3.2.2
STTB1-APUR
The STTB1-APUR made by STMicroelectronics is a dual mode variable output buck-boost
direct current (DC)-DC converter. The STTB1-APUR is able to provide an output voltage
of 1.2V to 5.5V from an input voltage of 2.0V to 5.5V [22]. The output voltage is set
by choosing the values for two externally mounted resistors. Using external resistors to
control the output voltage means that the same circuit design can be reused for different
voltage requirements. If this is done, it is paramount that great care is taken in mount-
ing the correct resistors, as resistors of incorrect value has the potential to destroy the
components mounted to the output rail.
With a chip size of 3x3mm, the converter itself is almost six times larger than the TPS61256A
above. This larger size makes the chip easier to mount and solder on the PCB, but also
means the total size, including external components, is larger. The DFN-10 package of
the STTB1-APUR has a ground pad underneath the chip. This makes replacing a faulty
chip more difficult compared to a chip without a hidden ground pad as the default state of
DTUsat PCBs is to have ground planes if no other signal or power level is required.
3.2.3
TPS61085A-Q1
The third converter considered for the OBC is the TPS61085A-Q1 by Texas Instruments.
The TPS61085A-Q1 is a step-up DC-DC converter capable of providing an output voltage
of 18.5V. The chip is the largest of the three candidates with a package size of 3.0x4.9mm
[23]. As seen in Figure 3.6c, the TPS61085A-Q1 is suggested to have 10 external com-
ponents. This leads to a significantly larger solution size compared to the TPS61256A
and STTB1-APUR. While the package of the TPS61085A-Q1 is larger than the other con-
verters, the VSSOP-8 package means a replacement job is significantly easier than for
the other converters. The VSSOP-8 has no hidden pins, and as such the heat required
to desolder the chip can be applied directly to the pads of the chip.
The large range of output voltages of the TPS61085A-Q1 lends itself well to standardising
a step-up converter circuit on the DTUsat-3 platform. At the time of writing, no payload
systems have been designed, and as such it is unknown how many, if any, subsystems
need voltages higher than the standard 3.3V. A standardised step-up circuit where the
output voltage can be adjusted by selecting resistance values accordingly can prove very
useful when designing new subsystems.

3.3
External noteworthy information detector (ENID)
The external noteworthy information detector (ENID) is a new subsystem for the DTUsat-
3 platform. The goal of ENID is to provide systems outside the DTUsat-3 environment a
possibility to transmit data using the DTUsat-3 platform. This is mainly targeted towards
the experiments used on stratospheric balloon flights that are currently unable to send
any data to the ground. Using DTUsat-3 to transmit data will make it possible for the
external system owner to monitor their system during flight and, depending on setup,
make it possible to receive live data transmissions.
The external system will be connected with a wired connection to an ENID board. De-
pending on the location of the external system on the balloon train, this connection is
made either straight to the DTUsat-3 platform, or to a locally mounted ENID board. If the
external system and the DTUsat-3 platform is far apart on the balloon, the ENID board
mounted at the external system will transmit the data via Bluetooth to a separate ENID
board mounted on the DTUsat-3 platform. A Bluetooth connection is chosen for the wire-
less connection due to its well developed architecture and common use in short rage
communication. If the external system and DTUsat-3 is mounted close to each other, it is
possible to wire the external system straight to the DTUsat-3 platform using only a single

3.3.1
Choosing a Bluetooth chip
Two Bluetooth integrated circuits (ICs) have been chosen for comparison and possible
use on ENID. The nRF52832 from Nordic Semiconductor ASA [25] and CY8C4127LQI
from Infineon Technologies [26].
The two candidates are similar in many regards, but some key differences exist. The
nRF52832 is running the Bluetooth 5.3 protocol whereas the CY8C4127LQI is operating
with Bluetooth 4.2. The main benefit of the 5th generation of Bluetooth is an enhanced
Bluetooth low energy (BLE) speed of up to 2Mbps compared to a maximum of 1Mbps for
4th generation Bluetooth protocols [27]. The faster speeds available using BLE helps to
keep the maximum transmission current at 5.3mA for the nRF52832 compared to 15.6mA
for the CY8C4127LQI. Comparing only the maximum Tx current however, does not pro-
vide the full picture. The CY8C4127LQI is capacle of transmitting up to 8Mbps. Transmit-
ting at this data speed, using the classic protocol, costs more power than using BLE.
Receiving data uses power just as transmitting does, and the receiving characteristics are
mirrored in the transmission characteristics. The CY8C4127LQI has a significantly higher
maximum receiving power than the nRF52832, but this is due to the higher maximum data
speed and is thus hard to compare one to one. The big difference on the receiving side
is the receiving sensitivity. The CY8C4127LQI has an Rx sensitivity of -89dBm whereas
the nRF52832 has an Rx sensitivity of -96dBm.

A lot of work has gone into the development of the electric power system (EPS), and in
general, the this work has been well documented [18, 28, 29, 30]. The maximum power
point tracker (MPPT) and regulator has both been shown to work as intended, with the
caveat that the regulator must have a load attached.
Each of the six sides of the S/C has a regulator circuit capable of providing power from
the battery. In order to keep the efficiency high, even at low power demands, only one
regulator can be on at any one time. As such, five of the six regulators are back-ups to
ensure the S/C doesn’t lose power due to one failure. The 6 regulators are set ”in series”
such that all six has to fail in order for the power to die. This is done by sending an error
signal to the next regulator in line if a regulator fails. This circuitry was partly reworked in
[18] as the old circuitry enabled three of the six regulators instead of the intended one at
a time. The schematic of the error circuit was updated, but not implemented on the PCB.
This update has now been implemented.

4.2.1
Soldering
The PCB was assembled using a technique known as reflow soldering. The contact pads
on the PCB is covered in solder paste by aligning the stencil and PCB such that the holes
of the stencil overlap the contact pads on the PCB and filling the holes with solder paste
using a spatula. During this process, it is important to make sure that the bottom side of
the stencil is flush with the PCB to make sure that the correct amount of solder paste is
applied to the contact pad. Once the solder paste is applied and the stencil removed from
the PCB, each component is to be placed on the corresponding footprint. It is important
that each component is placed both precisely and accurately on the contact pads. For
context, the contact pads for the 0402 resistor are 0.4mm by 0.6mm with roughly 0.3mm
from the edge of the pad to the edge of the space allocated for the resistor. This means
that an 0402 resistor can be misplaced by 0.3mm - 0.4mm before encroaching onto the
space required by other components.
Once all the components are placed on the PCB, the PCB is carefully placed into the
soldering oven. The oven preheats the entire PCB with components to just under the
melting point of the solder paste used (≈160◦C) before briefly heating to above the melting
point of the solder paste. This causes the solder paste to turn into a liquid and form both
a physical and electrical connection between the component and the PCB. As the oven
heats up to above the melting point of the solder, this technique can only be used for the
components on one of the two sides of the PCB.

5.1.1
Tin whiskers
With the ongoing effort made to reduce the amount of lead in electronics, the classic
tin-lead alloys used for plating is being phased out in many electronic products. During
this phaseout, an increase in the amount of tin whiskers in electronics has been observed.
Although the reason remains somewhat of a mystery, it is widely believed that lead alloyed
with tin imparts whisker-inhibiting attributes to electronics. Tin whiskers are crystalline,
electrically conductive tin structures that can grow from surfaces where tin is used as the
final finish. Such a tin whisker is shown in Figure 5.1. While tin whiskers are typically
1µm in diameter and less than 1mm in length, they have been observed in diameters
from 0.006 to 10µm and lengths of up to 10mm [31].
If a tin whisker grows such that it bridges the gap between two components, it will cause
a short to occur between these components. The effect of this can range from none at all
if the two components happen to be at the same electrical potential, to total system failure
brought on by metal vapour arcs on applications with high levels of voltage and current.
At least three spacecrafts (S/Cs) have been reported to have fuses blown by metal vapour
arcs, resulting in the S/C being rendered non-operational [31].
While an increase in tin whiskers have been seen in recent years, they are by no means
a new phenomena. The earliest published reports data back to the 1940s and 1950s. As
such, the reasons for tin whisker growth have been studied for many years. But the mech-
anisms behind tin whiskers have not yet been fully explained, and contradictory results in
experiments are common. Some theories state that the growth of tin whiskers can be at-
tributed to recrystallisation and unusual grain growth processes in the tin. Other theories
suggest that the whiskers can grow as a response to a stress relief mechanism within the
tin plating. Some of the factors commonly accepted as impactful for whisker formation by
those advocating the stress theory are residual stresses within the tin plating caused by
factors such as the plating chemistry and process, bending or stretching of the surface
Figure 5.1: Tin whisker growing between pure tin-plated hook terminals of an electromag-
netic relay. Redrawn from [31].

==PAGE:44==
after plating and scratches or nicks in the plating or substrate introduced by handling and
probing. In general, factors increasing stress or diffusion within the deposit seem to in-
crease the occurrence of tin whiskers, but as stated above, the results of experiments into
the cause of tin whiskers vary and even contradict each other [31].
Certain steps can be taken to reduce the risk of tin whiskers inducing failures. Alloying tin
with another metal has been shown to reduce the risk of tin whisker growth. Using pure
tin plated components should be avoided whenever possible. Using tin-lead alloys are a
common and effective way to reduce the risk and size of tin whisker growth. Tin-lead alloys
are generally considered acceptable when the alloy contains at least 3% lead by weight.
When avoiding pure tin plating is not possible, certain steps can be taken to reduce the risk
of whiskers. The tin plating can be solder-dipped in a tin-lead solder. Special precautions
are required when solder-dipping to ensure thermal shock doesn’t damage or degrade
the component. The difficulty of ensuring that the entire surface is reflowed means that
solder dipping may have limited effect. Conformal coating the whisker prone areas can
greatly reduce the risk of tin whiskers growing to cause shorts. The coating material and
it’s thickness should be chosen according to the duration and environment of the mission.
Lastly, evaluating the risks of tin whiskers is recommended. This evaluation should assess
the possibility and effect of tin whiskers growing on components. Factors to include are,
for example, circuit geometry, system criticality and effects of a redesign [31].
5.1.2
Component size
The use of small components introduce difficulties that cannot be circumvented by expe-
rienced and competent personnel. When packaging technology into smaller packages,
the technology is pushed further than when using larger packages. This is well known in
transistors by Moore’s law. In 1990, the smallest transistors were made using the 600nm
process. By 2020, the smallest transistors had shrunk to use the 5nm process [32]. And
while the natural technological development is that integrated circuits (ICs) get smaller
while retaining performance, Intel’s recent difficulties with implementing the 7nm process
in their CPUs show that even the largest, most competent businesses can experience
problems with using smaller technology [33].
While difficulties with using smaller technology inside the ICs are to be solved by the
manufacturers, the issues that can arise will still impact the DTUsat team. One example
is capacitors. The capacitance of a capacitor made from two parallel plates each having
the area A, separated by a distance d can be approximated to be
C = εA
d
(5.1)
where ε is the dielectric constant for the dielectric material used in the capacitor. It is
obvious from Equation 5.1 that is the plates in the capacitor can be placed closer to each
other, the plate area can be smaller, and as such the entire package of the capacitor can
be smaller. However, when the plates are placed closer to each other, the risk of partial
or complete component failures are naturally higher. As capacitors are often used to
smoothen voltage ripples near ICs, a complete failure where the capacitor shorts will short
the power bus to ground, and potentially be a catastrophic failure of the entire mission.
While total failure is rare, partial failure resulting in lower capacitance is so common that
many small form factor capacitors have large tolerances of ±10%, ±20% or even -20%
- +80%. While capacitors with a tolerance of -20% - +80% should be avoided, the fact
that they are available for purchase is testimony as to the difficulties in producing precise
capacitors.

5.2.1
SSCB
Due to the manufacturing error discussed in Section 4.1, the new single event latch-up
(SEL) protection circuit could not be tested. Therefore, testing of the subsystem carrier
board (SSCB) during both normal operation and simulating a SEL is the natural next step
for the SSCB. These tests should seek to evaluate the performance of the SEL protection
circuit at different temperatures and with varying total ionising dose (TID) to simulate the
changes to the environment during both a single orbit and the lifetime of the mission. Tests
on previous version of the SSCB has shown that the SEL protection could have a strong
temperature dependence [19]. Such a dependence is important to characterise, and if it
is present, steps should be taken to lessen the impact of temperature on the performance
of the SEL protection. Once the SEL protection passes testing, the SSCB should be in
an operational state with no further development needed.
5.2.2
OBC
As the tests in Section 4.2 showed, further testing on the new step-up converters are
needed to determine their efficiencies. In order for the voltage drop over the resistors
to become large enough not to be overshadowed by noise, it is recommended that the
new resistors are at least 2Ω. With a resistance 20 times larger than the ones tested,
the difference in voltages on the 5V side will be 80mV when drawing 200mW. While a
80mV difference is just twice the noise experienced in the test, it should be enough of a
difference for the averaging over time to give a more accurate result than achieved in this
test. When using a resistor of ≥2Ω, it is important to regulate the voltage of the power
supply such that the input leg of the converter is at 3.3V and to factor in the resistance of
the measurement resistor when setting the resistance in the resistance decade box.
When the tests described above are completed, and the new step-up converter chosen,
it has to be implemented on the on-board computer (OBC) PCB. This should be a straight
forward replacement of the current step-up converter and associated components. Due
to the relatively large power consumption of the OBC, it is recommended to analyse the
possibility of removing, disabling or throttle features of the OSD3358-SM chip used on the
OBC. Once the new step-up converter has been implemented, a rigorous test program
should be carried out on the OBC. The OBC is the heart of the S/C, and if it were to fail
during flight, the entire mission is lost. As such, the OBC should be tested to survive
conditions worse than many of the other subsystems as no backup exist for the OBC.
As the name on-board computer suggests, the OBC needs software written in order for
the S/C to function. This software does not yet exist. Once the OBC hardware is tested
and shown to achieve basic functionality, the process of writing the code for the actual
mission can begin. This process can potentially take a long time, especially due to the
fact that the mission for DTUsat-3 is yet to be determined. Therefore, it is recommended
that the work on getting the OBC finalised is carried out as soon as possible.

various loads and conditions. Optimally, the external system connected to an ENID board
should have as many possibilities to send its data into the DTUsat platform. Therefore,
the analog to digital converters (ADCs) available on the nRF52832 should also be sought

panel and MPPT due to the antenna being mounted on the Z+ panel.
The new components in the timer design from [18] needs to be correctly added to the
DTUsat component list and KiCad libraries before being implemented on to the PCB.
Once the new components have been put on the PCB, the timer needs testing to make
sure the delay in start-up is as expected. This testing can be done on the Z+ EPS panel
and implemented on the remaining 5 panels once the design has passed testing.

==PAGE:48==
6
Conclusion
The aim of the project was to finalise the designs of the subsystem carrier board (SSCB)
and electric power system (EPS) such that a functional test platform for DTUsat-3 sub-
systems could be set up in the lab.
The single event latch-up (SEL) protection circuit on the SSCB was updated to allow
subsystems with a lot of capacitance to charge the capacitors without triggering the SEL
protection. One of the two UART buses on the SSCB was connected to the wrong pin on
the stack connector. This has been fixed such that the UART bus is once again available
for use.
A new version of the circuitry to ensure that the 6 regulators on the EPS has been im-
plemented. In addition a bypass resistor has been added such that it’s possible to skip
the ≈30min start-up timer on the EPS. Due to the manufacturing defects discussed in
Section 4.1, testing the modified printed circuit boards (PCBs) for the SSCB and EPS has
not been possible.
The step-up converter previously implemented on the on-board computer (OBC) had been
shown to be unable to supply the power needed by the OBC. Three replacement candi-
dates were considered and two were selected for a head to head test. The STTB1-APUR
from STMicroelectronics was tested for its efficiency, but the noise in the measurements
was large enough to drown out the signal, causing the test to show an efficiency of over
100%.
In order to facilitate the use of the DTUsat platform to relay data from external systems
on stratospheric balloon flights, a new subsystem known as the external noteworthy infor-
mation detector (ENID) has been envisioned. External noteworthy information detector
(ENID) can use both a wired or a Bluetooth connection to collect data from external sys-
tems and pipe them into DTUsat-3. An analysis of two Bluetooth chips were made with
the nRF52832 from Nordic Semiconductor ASA being chosen as the Bluetooth chip used
by ENID.
In addition to the changes mentioned above, a general trace analysis of the PCBs have
been conducted with several traces on the PCBs having been simplified.
While the overall goal of having a functional test platform for DTUsat-3 has not been
achieved, significant progress towards unifying the as of yet individual systems has been
made. With the further testing outlined in Section 5.2, a unification of the subsystems can
be achieved.

System optimization, integration and test of core systems for DTUsat-3
LVII
==PAGE:108==
DTUsat-3 is the third student built spacecraft (S/C) from the Technical University of Denmark (DTU).
Its development has so far been conducted on a subsystem level, without an overall test of the entire
S/C having been carried out. This project aims at completing development on the core systems for
DTUsat-3 and conduct a comprehensive test of the platform. With the core systems functional, new
subsystems can be envisioned, build and tested with relative certainty of a fully functional S/C at
launch.
The subsystem carrier board (SSCB) and electric power system (EPS) have been updated with
new additions and replacements for the underpowered step-up converter on the on-board computer
(OBC) has been evaluated and tested. A new subsystem (the external noteworthy information de-
tector (ENID)) for external connections have been envisioned and a Bluetooth chip for wireless com-
munication have been found. ENID is mainly intended to be used on stratospheric balloon flights.
Due to manufacturing errors by an external manufacturer, the SSCB and EPS updates have unfor-
tunately not been tested.
