Abstract
This master’s thesis presents the design and implementation of a LiDAR-based
anti-collision system for de-iceing trucks operating in airport environments. Cur-
rent state-of-the-art de-iceing procedures rely on manual operation of de-iceing
trucks with complex controls, making them error-prone and ineﬀicient. The devel-
oped solution is an algorithm capable of detecting and avoiding static objects while
accounting for the dynamic nature of the truck’s bounding box. This makes the
solution useful for aiding the safe and autonomous navigation of de-icing trucks.
Through data collected from both a simulation environment and real-world test-
ing, the functionality of the system has been verified, and its performance has
been benchmarked. Verification of the system was performed both in the form of
unit tests at the individual sub-system level, and through complete system tests.
Results from End-to-End (E2E) testing confirm that the system successfully en-
gages an emergency stop when objects reach dangerous proximity. Objects at a
safe distance do not disturb the operation of the system.
The successful implementation of a dynamic truck bounding box model is demon-
strated. It adjusts based on the truck’s movements and the crane’s position. This
is combined with a robust distance calculation method for collision prediction.
Within an operational limit of 10Hz, the system is capable of processing up to
ten two-dimensional objects for collision-free operation. Accordingly, the system
solves a key part of the autonomous de-icing problem, making it ideal for future
integration with dynamic path-planning solutions.
The work presented here lays the groundwork for useful extensions that can be rea-
sonably implemented. A key option for improvements presents itself in the form
of more eﬀicient distance computations. This would enable the handling of more
obstacles of increased complexity and an increased operational frequency. Opera-
tion at 20Hz was almost accomplished using the 2D-distance calculation rectangle
to rectangle for up to 5 objects and the 3D-distance calculation volume to volume
for one object.

Preface
Background
The master’s thesis will be carried out in partnership with the Technical Univer-
sity of Denmark (DTU)1, particularly in the Department of Autonomous Systems,
alongside the R&D department of Vestergaard Company2. The study will focus on
enhancing the autonomous capabilities of the Eelephant E-Beta truck, primarily
through the refinement of Laser Imaging, Detection, and Ranging (LiDAR)-based
anti-collision algorithms.
Vestergaard Company
Vestergaard Company specializes in the design, production, and servicing of ad-
vanced airport equipment, setting industry standards for quality, functionality,
and durability in Ground Support Equipment (GSE).
The company places a
strong emphasis on functionality, ensuring eﬀicient operations with consideration
for both economic and environmental aspects.
Within the last 50 years, Vestergaard Company has undergone significant ex-
pansion in response to market demands, employing approximately 330 profession-
als to support global customer needs. Their production facility, spanning over
13,800 square meters, is dedicated to the manufacturing of the Elephant® family
of deicers, vacuum toilet service units, portable water units, and aircraft washers,
totaling more than 1,800 deicers and 550 lavatory/water units in Denmark.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The aviation industry is one of the most safety-conscious industries today. It en-

safety zone around the truck must be resized dynamically to accommodate the

1.5
Empirical considerations
In airport environments, ensuring the safety of both aircraft, personnel, and ground
vehicles is crucial. By providing accurate, colorless, real-time three-dimensional
spatial data, LiDAR technology presents a vital solution in anti-collision during de-
icing operations. This section examines empirical considerations from real-world
experiences.
Accuracy and Precision of Object Detection
Empirical observations demon-
strate that LiDAR technology accurately detects objects within its FOV. The
high-precision capabilities of the system facilitate differentiation between station-
ary structures, such as buildings, as well as dynamic objects such as vehicles and
aircraft. Accurate object detection is crucial during the de-icing process, given that
the proximity of the Elephant Beta truck to the aircraft requires reliable object
detection to prevent collision and injury.
Execution Time
A reasonable execution time is important so that all systems
can react promptly.
The LiDAR has a scanning rate of either 10Hz or 20Hz
depending on the horizontal angular resolution.
10Hz delivers data less often
however the scan will be more detailed since the horizontal angular resolution
would increase. Setting the scanning rate to 20Hz will decrease the horizontal
angular resolution but the same area is scanned more quickly which would allow
the system to react more swiftly. The scanning rate is yet to be decided however
the execution time of the algorithm should match or stay below the scanning rate
so it always computes the most recent data and doesn’t create a bottleneck. The
execution time is of course very dependent on the hardware platform so tests
performed on the development platform can only enable limited conclusions about
==PAGE:18==
1.6 Delimitation
5
the final design. Nevertheless, having the execution time in mind while developing
components such as the noise filter, ground filter, and algorithm is important.
Range and Resolution Optimization
Real-world experiences emphasize the
importance of optimizing the LiDAR system parameters, specifically range and
resolution in the context of anti-collision applications. Experiments argue that
a balance between these two parameters is crucial.
Generally, a longer range
allows for earlier detection of an object, whereas a higher resolution assures a
more detailed differentiation of potential close-range hazards. Therefore, finding
an appropriate balance is crucial to achieving an effective anti-collision system for
the de-icing process.
Environmental Flexibility
Empirical data obtained from airports around the
world demonstrate the flexibility of LiDAR systems to various environmental con-
ditions. These illustrate that the sensors still function in diverse weather scenarios,
such as light snowfall, fog, and low light conditions, but that they however to some
extent are affected [25]. Preliminary tests conducted at VC show that the LiDAR
sensors for the truck are not affected by light snow conditions. This flexibility
is especially relevant for the de-icing process, where harsh weather conditions are
frequent. However, LiDAR does face some challenges when operating in extreme
rain, fog, or snowfall, ultimately reducing the accuracy, range, and resolution of
the LiDAR, and creating noise and false positives in the data. Since the LS Li-
dar CH64W uses a wavelength of 905nm, which is just slightly above the visible
light spectrum, in every condition where the human eye is challenged, the LiDAR
will be challenged as well. This challenge can be mitigated by utilizing advanced
algorithms and filters to identify and remove the rain signals from the data.
False Positive and False Negative Rates
Real-world experiments stress the
importance of mitigating false positives and false negatives in LiDAR-based anti-
collision applications. Data reveals that false positives can lead to unwanted alarms
during operation. Contrarily, false negatives pose a serious risk, that could ulti-
mately result in a collision. Therefore, calibration and fine-tuning of the LiDAR
system is essential for reducing these error rates.

Tracking dynamic objects is another big challenge since LiDAR scans do not in-

CHAPTER 2
System Overview
A short overview of the proposed solution for this project can be seen in figure 2.1.
This block diagram can also be used to get a rough estimate about the upcoming
chapter order.
The block diagram illustrates the systematic approach to processing the LiDAR
data. The process is initiated with the LiDAR data callback function, where the
system first gets raw spatial data from the sensors.
This data is then put through a series of preprocessing steps that aim to re-
fine the data for further analysis.
This includes filtering steps such as ground
segmentation, elevation-based filtering, and downsampling, which aim to enhance
the quality and reliability of the LiDAR scans.
After preprocessing, the filtered data is clustered. The purpose of this step is
to group the LiDAR points into meaningful clusters that represent objects within
the environment. This is a crucial step in discerning potential hazards from the
surrounding noise. Each cluster will be enclosed by an oriented bounding box.
Once the filtered data has been clustered, the detected clusters are matched
against clusters from previous scans to track and identify the obstacles consistently.
Concurrently, the system receives joint state -and vehicle data from the joint
state callback and vehicle data callback functions. The joint states are stored in
the JointStates class, corresponding to the angle of rotation, which functions to
integrate and synchronize the positional and operational states of the trucks joints.
Meanwhile, the vehicle’s trajectory data is obtained from the vehicle data callback,
which includes the trucks current velocity and steering angle. This integration of
the trucks joint and vehicle data is an essential step in developing a model of the
trucks current and future states, as it enables calculating the trucks bounding box
and defining the spatial extents of the truck and joints.
Finally, the system computes the distance from each bounding box to each iden-
tified cluster. By utilizing the spatial information from the clusters and the trucks
bounding boxes, alongside the trucks trajectory, the risk of collision is assessed con-
tinuously. If the distance to a cluster is below a predefined safety threshold, the

Dynamic Environment
Developing an anti-collision system for airport environments requires a simulation
environment that reflects the various cases of how busy and unpredictable air-
ports can be. Airports are a challenging environment for anti-collision because the
setting is constantly changing: aircrafts moving, ground support equipment being
moved around, personnel, vehicles, and other dynamic elements. The anti-collision
system needs to be thoroughly tested and evaluated before it can be deployed to
the real world, which means, the system needs to be tested and evaluated in a set-
ting that is as close as possible to the real world. This means that the simulation
must include various test cases of dynamic elements to verify that the system is
robust and responsive to different elements.

ronment accurately represents the actual operational environment of the Elephant
Beta truck, as the Elephant Beta truck must also interact with public roads. By
including these elements, the anti-collision system is provided with the complexity
needed to safely navigate in a dynamic airport environment.

Real World Data
The real-world data was captured using the Elephant Beta truck and several differ-

captured throughout three sessions each taking an entire day to complete. The
data was recorded using the built-in record function from ROS. Calling rosbag

file with the .bag extension in chunks of maximum ~4Gigabyte (GB). If a .bag
file exceeds this size, another rosbag is created automatically. The captured data
from those three sessions used up approximately ~830GB of storage. The recorded
messages can be played back through the ROS network, including LiDAR sensor
data and control commands.

Session 2

PC2 to NumPy
LiDAR data gets parsed by ROS as a PointCloud2 type. This datatype always in-

such as Red, Green, Blue (RGB) values or reflectivity and intensity measurements.
The PC2 object is not widely used in Python implementations, so converting it

NumPy array is a good choice since NumPy arrays are fast and widely used.
The input data, which is stored in the variable data, is in PC2 format. By

array. The final step in the process involves filtering out unnecessary informa-

4.3
Ground Segmentation
In good conditions, a LiDAR scan includes points from every surface in the FOV
and sensor range. This means that a lot of points are being generated from the
ground in front of the sensor. Those points do not carry any useful information for
the anti-collision algorithms. The ground particles have to be removed to improve
the quality of the clustering and bounding box creation. Removing unnecessary
ground points also helps with the computational speed of the downstream algo-
rithms since fewer points have to be computed.
A ground filter is used to remove those points. There are many different ways
of detecting what points are part of the ground.
A lot of them are listed in
this paper [17]. Those include elevation or slope-based filters, statistical filters,
RANSAC-based filters, DBSCAN-based filters, or filters that use machine learning.
Two ground filters were tested during this project. The two tested methods are
the value-based filter and a Random Sample Consensus (RANSAC) based ground
filter.
4.3.1
Case Definition
Case 14 Ground filter test from table 3.1 is about testing the best ground filter
for this project. The tests were carried out on a rosbag file since the simulation
doesn’t have enough variation in the surface. The rosbag, visualized in figure 4.5(a),
features a slight increase in elevation compared to the surface of the asphalt. This
elevation is in front of the trees. It is also a very busy area with lots of objects
that can disturb the ground filtering algorithm. The fence to the left and right
can also be used to determine how much of it has been cut off by the ground filter.
This scene was used to test all the groundfilters.
4.3.2
Challenges and Considerations
A good ground filter needs to remove ground particles without cutting off too much
information on the z-axis. This means removing anything flat and slightly bumpy,
but keeping as much of the depth of an object as possible. This is a big challenge
especially when the truck is driving on uneven terrain. It is also important that
the ground filter keeps its accuracy while the truck is moving. Bumps in the road
and breaking/acceleration maneuvers will compress and decompress the suspen-
sion of the truck making the ground move up and down relative to the LiDAR
sensor. A high threshold or an adaptive algorithm would be needed to work in all
circumstances.

4.3.4
Value based Filter
The value-based ground filter loops through every point in the point cloud and
compares the XYZ-axis to a predefined threshold. For example, every point below
a height of 20cm will be removed. This makes it a potential candidate for a ground
filter. This approach is computationally easy to run however, it is not adaptive
nor can it handle complex situations where the truck’s suspension is at work. The
python implementation can be seen in code snippet 4.3.
mask = ((xyz[:, 2] >= prefiltering.MIN_Z) &
(xyz[:, 2] <= prefiltering.MAX_Z) &
(xyz[:, 1] >= prefiltering.MIN_Y) &
(xyz[:, 1] <= prefiltering.MAX_Y) &
(xyz[:, 0] >= prefiltering.MIN_X) &
(xyz[:, 0] <= prefiltering.MAX_X))
xyz = xyz[mask]

4.3.5
RANSAC-based Ground filter
Similarly to this paper [40] a RANSAC-based ground filter was chosen due to its
very fast and accurate segmentation of ground points and object points.
RANSAC
RANdom SAmple Consensus [28] is an iterative model-fitting algo-
rithm that has been developed to handle data with a significant number of outliers.
RANSAC can be used to fit either a line or a plane to a set of either 2D or 3D
points respectively.
Since RANSAC will be used in the context of finding the
model that best represents the ground, 3D points are used and a best-fitting plane
is the expected output. RANSAC works by taking at least the minimum needed
randomly selected points to fit a plane. Which is three points. The actual number
==PAGE:43==
4.3 Ground Segmentation
30
of points is one of several parameters that can be tuned to improve RANSAC’s
performance. A plane is fitted to those points. Now RANSAC counts the outliers
and inliers of that plane with a tolerance that the user can tune. This will be re-
peated N number of times. Since the plane represents the ground, further checks
can be put in place to optimize RANSAC’s performance. Such check could be,
that the plane should be perpendicular to the ground, which means any wall that
potentially looks like a ground would be immediately discarded because of its an-
gle. The remaining outliers of the best model are then the interesting information
of the LiDAR sensor scan.
RANSAC is ideal for working with LiDAR data since there will be a lot of
outliers. Every object sticking up out of the ground can be considered an outlier.
A simple Least Mean Squares (LMS) would not work as shown in figure 4.4 since
the model that has been fitted to the data using LMS also takes into account the
outliers. This would be terrible in the context of a ground filter since the fitted
plane would work worse the further away the sensor can measure.
Figure 4.4: 2D Example of a RANSAC vs. LMS algorithm[35]
Figure 4.4 shows a comparison between LMS and RANSAC. Both algorithms
got all the points as input. The blue points could represent the ground and the
green points an obstacle. Only the RANSAC algorithm correctly identifies the
obstacles as outliers and creates a very good fitting model of the ground.

4.4
Downsampling
Downsampling 3D point clouds into voxels reduces the density of data points while
still preserving the essential characteristics of the point cloud. This technique, as
conducted in [19], is particularly relevant for high-complexity tasks such as anti-
collision, as the downsampling decreases the computational complexity, speeds up
processing times, and simplifies data for visualization.
==PAGE:47==
4.4 Downsampling
34
In 3D data, voxels refer to a small cubic volume that groups together nearby
points. Each voxel contains essential information dependent on the properties of
the space it occupies, such as density and intensity. The voxel size is an adjustable
parameter that can be tweaked based on the desired level of detail required. This
concept is illustrated in Figure 4.6, where an increasingly larger voxel size results
in less detail, as shown by the transition from a finely divided space (a) to one with
fewer, larger voxels (b), and ultimately to a single voxel representing the average
of the points within it (c).
Figure 4.6: Illustration of a Voxel downsampling algorithm [12]
In this context, the anti-collision system’s primary objective is not to gather
all intrinsic details of the environment, but specifically to determine whether or
not a potential collision is present. For this reason, it is important to define an
appropriate voxel size that balances between detecting relevant obstacles such as
light poles, while leaving out unnecessary details such as differentiating between
many small occluding objects.
The Open3D downsampling algorithm [13] is illustrated in Figure 4.7, showcas-
ing both the gazebo environment and the downsampled LiDAR data at different
voxel sizes. While a smaller voxel size is generally preferred in order to retain as
much spatial information as possible, the trade-offs in the context of the entire
anti-collision system must also be considered. Factors such as processing power,
real-time response requirements, and the specific environmental context where the
system operates all impact the optimal voxel size. After testing and consideration
of these factors, a voxel size of 0.7 meters has been selected for the anti-collision
system. This is because this size was determined to provide the optimal balance
between detail retention and computational eﬀiciency.

CHAPTER 5
Clustering
Introduction
Clustering is a technique used to group LiDAR points into dis-
crete objects and to differentiate them. The complete LiDAR scan, comprising
thousands of points, is then segmented into several smaller clusters. These com-
pact clusters are then visualized using either Axis-Aligned Bounding Box (AABB),
Oriented Bounding Box (OBB) or Convex Hull (CH) bounding boxes.
Using bounding boxes to calculate the distance between the truck and the surround-
ing objects is much more computationally eﬀicient than calculating the distance
to each point in a complete LiDAR scan. This is crucial in real-time applications
where computational resources are limited.
The clustered objects are also more unique than a single point, which makes it pos-
sible to track and match the different objects across multiple LiDAR scans. This
is because their shape, orientation, and position can provide unique signatures for
identification throughout multiple LiDAR scans. Continually identifying objects
over time enables the retention of historical data and the calculation of trajectory
and speed. By utilizing the historical data, predictions about a potential imminent
collision can be made, contributing to enhanced safety in dynamic environments.

5.3.1
DBSCAN
The DBSCAN algorithm [3] is a clustering algorithm that classifies every point
into three categories; core points, border (edge) points, and noise points. All core
points and edge points are then considered to be part of a cluster, while the noise
points are discarded as outliers. DBSCAN has the advantage, over many other
algorithms, of not having to know the number of expected clusters in advance. This
is vital since the number of objects in front of the truck are constantly changing.
DBSCAN needs two parameters to operate. Epsilon (ϵ) which is the radius around
a data point, and Min_Samples which is the minimum number of data points
required to form a dense cluster.
A data point is then classified as a core point when it has more than Min_Samples
number of neighbors within its radius. An edge point is still within the radius of
a core point, but it does not meet the minimum number of neighbors required
defined by Min_Samples. A noise point is defined as not being in the range of any
other points.
The algorithm will then start with a random point and iterate until no more core
points or edge points can be added. The next point will be chosen at random again,
==PAGE:54==

however it will be part of the not yet visited points. This concept is visualized in
5.2, where it is repeated until all points have been visited and labeled.

5.4
Bounding Boxes
Bounding boxes[4] are an important concept in computer vision, as they allow for
a precise and simple way to visualize and represent the spatial characteristics of
a cluster. The purpose of the bounding box is to define the location and size of
an object in 3D space, which in turn allows for simple manipulation in the down-
stream algorithms.
The dimensions of a 3D bounding box [34] are determined by its eight corners,
each defined by the minimum and maximum values of the x-, y-, and z-axis. These
corners represent the bounding box. The edges of the 3D bounding box are called
line segments, that connect all eight corners. Given that a bounding box has 12
edges, the bounding box is constructed by each edge connecting to two of the eight
vertices. The composition of a 3D bounding box is illustrated in Figure 5.4
Figure 5.4: 3D Bounding Box
Figure 5.5 illustrates three methods of implementing bounding boxes. All of
these have been investigated and evaluated, with each differing in their level of
complexity and simplicity.

5.4.1
Challenges and Considerations
The bounding boxes need to precisely encapsulate all data points as best as possi-
ble. This precision is important, as the truck must navigate eﬀiciently in narrow-
spaced situations. If too much empty space is present within a bounding box, it
could result in unnecessary resource utilization from the distance computations.
These computations, being computationally expensive, are discussed in chapter 7.
Furthermore, objects come in all sorts of shapes, including irregular objects with
parts that stick out. These potential odd object shapes need to be encapsulated
as best as possible, as it otherwise could lead to collision and navigation issues.
These challenges and considerations are especially important when considering
the anti-collision system as a whole.
The available resources must be utilized
wisely, and determining an appropriate bounding box plays a big role in this.
5.4.2
Metrics
The different bounding box solutions require different amounts of resources. The
Bounding box solution which uses the least resources is generally more favorable,
however, there is much value in not detecting any empty space of an object. Mea-
suring the empty space will be done qualitatively.

bounding box remains aligned with these axes. This results in a bounding box,
that is typically larger, with more empty space between the surface of the object,

Given that the execution time of the downstream algorithms heavily depends
on how many objects are detected, which can be seen in figure 8.12, it is beneficial
to prune the smaller enclosed BBoxs. A function has been created that checks
the degree of enclosure between two bounding boxes by examining the number of
corners of one bounding box contained within the other. If all 8 corners of one
bounding box are within the confines of another it is considered fully enclosed.
For results between 7 and 0 enclosed corners, indicating partial or no enclosure,
both bounding boxes are retained. Subsequently, when the decision has been made
to delete one BBox, their volumes are computed, from which the biggest one is
retained.
This implementation can improve the performance when using the Volume to

the larger bounding box being the most dangerous object. However, it is important

FOV Limitation
The Beta Truck’s LiDAR, as visualized in Figure 3.1, has a vertical FOV of 40
degrees, ranging from -25 degrees below the horizontal plane to 15 degrees above it.
Such a configuration provides a comprehensive scan of the environment at certain
heights, but it also introduces blind spots that can be critical for navigation and
operational safety.
Figures 5.14(a) and 5.14(b) illustrate the practical implications of this limita-
tion. The LiDAR sensor is not capable of detecting the upper section of the light
pole, as it extends beyond the sensor’s 15-degree upper limit.
This restricted vertical FOV could in some cases result in potential hazards,
such as the boom arm of the truck colliding with tall undetected structures, such
as a light pole. To address this challenge, and to mitigate these blind spots, the
additional sensors on top of the truck have been merged into one total data stream
consisting of front, rear, top 360, and top front LiDAR sensors.

5.5.2
Keeping the Z-value of all Object Clusters
Another idea for a solution to the FOV problem is never updating the z-value of
the detected clusters. A distant object would be detected at its full height by the
sensors and those height values would be kept as the object approaches the truck.
This approach was quickly implemented with the object matching using centroids
from chapter 9.
However, this idea had a problem with objects that completely disappeared
from the FOV of the navigation LiDARs. A low-hanging wire for example. The
object’s position in the x and y direction would no longer be updated if no part
of the object were in the FOV. Calculating the new position of clusters that are
not in the FOV anymore could solve this issue. One method to accomplish this is
further described in section 9.1.2. This method, however, presents another issue
where clusters that are slowly moving out of the FOV do not keep their original
shape. A much more advanced algorithm is required, such as SLAM, which is
further described in section9.1.4.

Truck Trajectory
The trajectory of the truck is used to predict its position and orientation N seconds
into the future. This helps to ignore obstacles that are not in the truck’s path.
The trajectory is based on Ackermann steering geometry and is implemented by
accurately determining the truck’s path over time. This is done by considering the
truck’s velocity, steering angle, time step, and wheelbase.

6.3.6.1
Vehicle Data
The trajectory calculation needs four variables.
Vehicle speed, steering angle,
wheelbase, and a time step. The time step is set to a fixed 1 second, and the
wheelbase doesn’t change either. The remaining vehicle speed and steering angle
are obtained by subscribing to the ”/vecadis/mplc_link/vehicle_data” topic.
6.3.6.2
Ackermann Model
The Ackermann trajectory [36] theory is a mathematical model used to calculate
the optimal wheel angle for each wheel for a given car going around a radius. This
model’s two most common abstractions are the bicycle model and the four-wheeled
model. The Elephant Beta truck has four wheels, where the front two wheels turn
with different angles. The Ackermann four-wheeled model would therefore be the
more realistic approach to determine the trajectory. However, the Elephant Beta
truck doesn’t feature individual angle encoders on each wheel which are necessary
if the geometry is otherwise unknown. The bicycle model can still be applied using
the steering wheel angle as the combined angle for both front wheels.

Introduction
This chapter introduces the methodologies that are used for cal-
culating distances in the anti-collision system.
The main goal is to accurately
measure the distance between the truck and surrounding objects. The developed
system is based on a step-by-step approach to enhance both measurement accu-
racy and processing speed. The solution integrates a 3D Metrics library, which is
critical for the functionality of the system, ensuring precise distance calculations.
Additionally, the chapter explores the performance and eﬀiciency of 2D versus
3D detection methods through practical testing. The potential benefits of com-
bining both methods are discussed, suggesting that an integrated approach could
significantly improve the system’s overall effectiveness. The step-by-step approach
is illustrated in the flowchart in figure 7.1.

7.1
Initial Filtering
The initial step features a simple point-to-point distance measurement from the
function point2point_dist, which involves calculating the Euclidean distance be-
tween the truck’s projected center points and the centroid of a cluster in either
two or three dimensions. The purpose of this step is to compute a fast and ap-
proximate distance from the truck to the surrounding objects.
The Euclidean distance can be described as the length of the line segments
between two points. For a 2-dimensional space, the Euclidean distance between two
points P1 = (x1, y1) and P2 = (x2, y2) is defined by the mathematical expression:
Distance2D(P1, P2) =

If the initial filtering step yields a distance of less than 20 meters, the system
advances to the next step.
This approach enables the system to only process
objects which pose immediate collision risks. Thereby the objects, that pose no
collision risk for any of the projected bounding boxes, are not further processed in
the subsequent, more computationally heavy, steps.

(R2R), and Rectangle to Point (R2P) distance calculations.

V2V & R2R
In the second step, a more detailed V2V distance calculation is conducted for com-
puting the shortest distance between two OBB’s. This step provides an analysis
of the V2V distance calculations between the truck’s OBB and the OBB’s of the
surrounding clusters. These are based on the PPOI and geometric computation
functions outlined in subsection 7.2. The purpose of this step is to provide accurate
and reliable V2V distance measurements for the anti-collision system.
The computational steps involved in the V2V function are:
1. Extract Corners, Edges, and Faces:
a) Extract corners, edges, and faces from both OBBs. This involves the
corner points, edges, and faces of both the truck’s OBB and the cluster’s
OBB.
==PAGE:90==

2. Project Corner Points:
a) Project corner points of OBB A to faces and edges of OBB B, and vice
versa, using the project_points function. This step involves finding the
closest points on the surfaces of the two OBB’s by projecting corner
points onto the other OBB’s faces and edges.
3. Compute Edge-to-Edge Distance:
a) Calculate the edge-to-edge distance between both OBB’s using the
edge2edgeDist function. This step involves determining the minimum

4. Compute Line-to-Point Distance:
a) Calculate the shortest distance from a given point in 3D space to the
closest point on a line segment using the line2pointDist function.
5. Append and Sort Distances:
a) Append all calculated distances from the previous steps, including corner-
to-face, corner-to-edge, edge-to-edge, and line-to-point distances, into a
list. Sort the list to find the shortest distance.
R2R
The R2R function is the 2D equivalent of the V2V distance calculation. It
computes the shortest distance between two rectangles (2D OBB’s). The function
follows a similar logic but has been modified to meet the constraints of two dimen-
sions, meaning that only the edge-to-edge and line-to-point distance calculations
are considered.
The computational steps involved in the R2R function are:
1. Extract 2D Edges and Corners:
a) Extract 2D edges and corners from both rectangles.
2. Compute Edge-to-Edge Distance:
a) Calculate the edge-to-edge distance between the 2D edges of both rect-
angles using the edge2edgeDist function.
3. Project Corner Points:
a) Project corner points of rectangle A to the edges of rectangle B, and
vice versa, using the project_points function. This projection helps find
the closest points on the surfaces of the two rectangles.
==PAGE:91==

4. Compute Line-to-Point Distance:
a) Calculate the shortest distance from a given point in 2D space to the
closest point on a line segment using the line2pointDist function.
5. Append and Sort Distances:
a) Append all calculated distances from the previous steps, including edge-
to-edge distances and line-to-point distances, into a list. Sort the list
to determine the shortest distance.
7.4
V2P & R2P
The third and final step in the distance computation involves the V2P and R2P
functions. These functions reflect the most intensive distance measurement, where
the shortest distance from a volume in 3D or a rectangle in 2D to the closest point
in a cluster is computed. This computation is especially important for scenarios
where the OBB of a cluster contains a large amount of empty space, as illustrated
in figures 5.11 and 5.12. By computing this V2P or R2P distance, the closest
physical point within a given cluster relative to the truck is identified. This enables
the truck to navigate within the free space of the clusters OBB, thereby allowing
for more precise navigation in tight areas. Given that the free space available in
an OBB is only of interest when the truck is close to a cluster, this step is only
executed if the V2V or R2R function returns a distance of, in example, less than
2 meters.
These functions, V2P and R2P, were not part of the original 3D metrics library.
Therefore, besides implementation, the existing functions had to be developed by
utilizing variations of the existing functions from the 3D Metrics Library.
The computational steps for V2P include:
1. Extract Corners, Edges, and Faces:
a) Extract corners, edges, and faces from the truck’s OBB. This involves
identifying the corner points, edges, and faces of the truck’s OBB.
2. Calculate Distance to Faces:
a) Iterate through the faces and utilize the project_points function to cal-
culate the distance from a given point to each face of the truck’s OBB.
3. Calculate Distance to Edges:
==PAGE:92==
7.4 V2P & R2P
79
a) Iterate through the edges and utilize the line2pointDist function to cal-
culate the distance from a given point to each edge of the truck’s OBB.
4. Calculate Distance to Corners:
a) Iterate through the corners and compute the Euclidean distance from
a given point to each corner of the truck’s OBB.
5. Update Minimum Distance:
a) Update the minimum distance if the distance computed in the previous
steps is smaller than the current minimum distance.
The R2P function serves the same purpose in 2D space, calculating the shortest
distance from the truck’s 2D OBB to a point in a cluster’s 2D OBB.
1. Extract 2D Edges:
a) Extract 2D edges from the truck’s OBB.
2. Calculate Distance to Edges:
a) Iterate through the edges and utilize the line2pointDist function to cal-
culate the distance from a given point to each edge.
3. Calculate Distance to Corners:
a) Calculate the Euclidean distance from a given point to each corner.
4. Combine Distances and Select Shortest:
a) Update the minimum distance if the distance computed in the previous
steps is smaller than the current minimum distance.

could be mitigated by having utilized a pre-recorded .ply point cloud.
A pre-
recorded .ply point cloud provides a robust and stable collection of data points,
where the position and orientation of these remain consistent.

Conclusion
In conclusion, the methodologies developed and implemented for the distance cal-
culation in the anti-collision system are robust but computationally expensive.
Throughout the testing phase, the distance measurements have proven to be
predominantly accurate when compared with an external tool such as GeoGebra.

Given this level of precision, the system’s distance measurements are reliable to
be depended upon for critical tasks, such as navigating and avoiding obstacles in
real-time scenarios.
The comparison conducted between the 2D and 3D algorithms showcases no-
table differences in computational performance. While the 2D algorithms R2R
and R2P are more eﬀicient, as illustrated by the benchmarking results from table
7.2, they also provide a more limited use case compared to the 3D algorithms V2V
and V2P.
The appropriate choice between the 2D and 3D algorithms depends on the
specific use case. For general navigation and obstacle avoidance, the 2D algorithms
are suﬀicient and more suitable given the lower computational load. However, for
cases where a more detailed spatial understanding is necessary, such as navigating
in more complex environments consisting of overhead barriers and low-hanging
wires, the 3D algorithms are necessary. This is especially important when also
considering the inclusion of additional 3D bounding boxes for the crane on top
of the truck, as described in section 6.3, given that these cannot be accurately
represented and processed in 2D space.
Conclusively, the combination of the 2D and 3D algorithms offers flexibility
that enables the anti-collision system to become more versatile. By implementing
dynamic switching between the 2D and 3D methods, the system could gain a
significant boost in terms of optimization.
While this adaptive approach would enhance the system’s performance, it is
worth noting that other optimization strategies might be necessary or at least
considered. As previously mentioned, the system relies on linear equations for
the PPOI distance calculations. These processes can be parallelized in order to
enhance the real-time performance of the current system. Another consideration,
which is described further in the future works section 10.1, is that it could be
valuable to take a few steps back, and explore the possibilities of convex hull
algorithms for distance measurements.

CHAPTER 9
Object Matching
Introduction
Static objects do not need to be tracked since their position, speed,
and trajectory are known from the moment they have been detected. Using the
trajectory and stopping distance should in theory be enough to create a solid anti-
collision algorithm. Dynamic objects on the other hand are much more diﬀicult to
take into account. Their speed, position, and trajectory are changing and need to
be calculated using historical data. Matching the same object throughout multiple
LiDAR scans is diﬀicult since LiDAR scans do not provide a lot of information
and are relatively slow to update. The challenge is to tell those objects apart even
if their relative position has changed dramatically. For example, if two cars pass
each other, all parameters would be very similar.
The first step in keeping track of the objects is tagging them with an ID. The
ID is an integer which is based on the length of the list of saved objects. If the same
object is detected in the next LiDAR scan everything but the ID should be updated.
There are several ways of identifying the same set of points in multiple iterations.
Using the distance of the centroids to decide if an object is the same as in the
LiDAR scan before is one method. This method was implemented an can be seen
in figure 9.1. A more advanced method could be using other parameters like the
shape, orientation, position, reflectivity, and intensity to identify the same object
throughout multiple LiDAR scans. To improve such an algorithm, a Kalman filter
could be applied to predict where a dynamic object most likely will be in the next
iteration. This would decrease the search area of similar parameters and improve
the matching.
Another algorithm that would further improve the detection of
dynamic objects is SLAM. [30]

9.1
Matching based on Centroids
The object parameters like centroids, corners, points, etc. are saved throughout
time. When a new cluster is detected, the Euclidian distances between the new
centroid and every old centroid are calculated using the equation 7.2. If the dis-
tance is below a certain threshold, all parameters except ID are updated. If it’s
above the threshold, the object is appended with a new ID. If the truck and the
objects are static this logic works well. However when the truck is in motion the
old centroids move along with the truck since the coordinates are relative to the
truck_footprint coordinate system and not the world coordinate system. To im-
prove the stability of object matching when the truck is moving, the coordinates
have to be translated into another coordinate system that remains static while the
truck is moving. Odometry or a GPS sensor can be used to accomplish this.

Odometry
Another way of translating the coordinates from the truck_footprint coordinate
system to a static coordinate system is to use the odometry of the truck. The
odometry sensor uses an LiDAR ICP algorithm [24] to determine the motion of
the truck. This is implemented using the rtabmap_odom1 plugin for ROS. The
implementation was done by VC.
The odometry is then subtracted from the old BBox corners. This will keep the
position up to date. The theoretical distance between the old and new centroids
is now 0 and very small in reality. This makes matching the objects more stable
even when the truck is driving.
Publishing both the old and new centroid to RVIZ also showed two centroids

0. This ensures that every object keeps its ID.

9.1.4
SLAM
The ability to map and understand the environment in real-time is an important
feature for autonomous vehicles, especially in complex and dynamic settings such
as airports. Autonomous de-icing trucks that operate in these environments en-
counter a variety of objects ranging from stationary objects such as light poles and
equipment to dynamic elements such as airport personnel, vehicles, and aircraft.
SLAM [37] provides a framework for the de-icing trucks to localize themselves
within their operational area, maintain updated environmental information, and
navigate safely without collisions.
SLAM’s contribution to path planning is an important aspect, as it enables
the de-icing trucks to plan routes that navigate past obstacles while optimizing
routes for time and resource eﬀiciency. The emphasis on implementing SLAM in
airport environments is further strengthened by the tight schedules in airport envi-
ronments, where even minor delays can result in significant scheduling disruptions
An additional feature which SLAM contributes to, is that the height data of
objects, referring to the z-component as described in section 5.5.2, is captured by
SLAM. SLAM can integrate three-dimensional sensor data to create a comprehen-
sive map that includes the height of various objects, ensuring that the vehicle’s
boom arm does not collide with them.
9.1.4.1
Considerations for SLAM in an airport environment
Dynamic Environment
Airports are a highly dynamic and fast-moving envi-
ronment, with various elements constantly moving around. The SLAM system
must be capable of quickly updating the map to reflect changes and to support
anti-collision decisions.
Accuracy and Reliability
Given the proximity to valuable aircraft, and the
requirement of accurate de-icing, high reliability and accuracy are a requirement
for a safe and eﬀicient operation.
Sensor Noise
The accuracy of sensor data can be influenced by rough weather
conditions, which is when de-icing is most needed. The SLAM system must account
for and mitigate this noise.

9.1.4.3
Methods
Within the realm of SLAM, various methods contribute to various applications.
In the context of an autonomous de-icing truck in an airport environment, the
desired SLAM algorithm should be robust in terms of sensor noise, capable of pro-
cessing data in real-time, and able to handle a dynamic environment. Considering
these criteria, Graph-Based SLAM and Particle Filter SLAM could be potential
candidates [39].
Graph-based SLAM
Graph-based SLAM [18] is a method where the main idea
is to represent the SLAM problem as a graph, that accurately reflects the truck’s
trajectory and the map of the environment. The graph consists of nodes, which
correspond to the truck’s poses at different timestamps during mapping, and edges,
which show the spatial relationships based on the sensor and odometry data. Con-
structing this graph helps to find the best configuration of nodes that minimizes
the error. This process is known as graph optimization and is an important aspect
of the SLAM solution. Through graph optimization, the SLAM algorithm mini-
mizes the difference between predicted and actual sensor measurements, thereby
iteratively refining the localization and mapping. The optimization is typically
based on algorithms such as Least Square Estimations and Mean Square Estima-
tions [15] under the assumption that errors have a zero-mean normal distribution.

Particle Filter SLAM
Particle Filter SLAM [29], often referred to as FastSLAM,
is a method that utilizes particle filters for estimating the trajectory of the truck.
Each particle represents a potential state of the truck, where each state consists
of parameters such as the position, orientation, and velocity of the truck.
As
the truck navigates through the dynamic airport environment, the particle filter
updates each particle based on the new incoming sensor data, and the previous
estimations. This iterative update process assigns weights to the particles based
on their likelihood. How likely each particle is, is based on how well the particle
aligns with the sensor data. Iterating through the process, the filter resamples the
particles, where the particles with higher weights are favored. As more iterations
pass, this allows the more accurate states to dominate the prediction model. As
seen in figure 9.4, initially the states are scattered throughout the environment
based on the initial predictions or measurements. As the filter starts to process
sensor data and control inputs, the state estimates are refined. Finally, the gath-
ered data over time has narrowed down the particles to a final estimated state,
that represents the truck’s most likely position and orientation.

The method is especially well-suited for the dynamic airport environment, as
it is capable of quickly adapting to new situations. The method is also especially

noise from the sensor data, which are often present in rough weather conditions

In essence, Graph-Based SLAM focuses on optimizing a global graph to mini-
mize error, while Particle Filter SLAM uses a probabilistic approach to estimate
and refine the truck’s trajectory over time.
9.2
Kalman Filter
A Kalman filter [23] is an algorithm that provides predictions of where objects
will be in the future based on where they’ve been. Simplified, the filter estimates
==PAGE:123==
9.3 Conclusion
110
where the object will move next, and then it corrects that estimate based on
new measurement data. This is an iterative process, where the filter maintains
an updated model for each object.
This model includes both the current and
predicted future trajectories.
All objects predicted trajectories can then be used for collision risk assess-
ment by comparing them with the trajectory of the truck. Based on this, some
decision-making logic can be implemented to stop operations, navigate around,
and decelerate.
Specifically for this system, an Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) would improve
object matching. With the trajectory estimates, the filter indirectly improves the
current centroid-based matching logic.
9.3
Conclusion
Developing a robust object matching and tracking system is very important for
the truck’s anti-collision system if it must operate reliably within an airport envi-
ronment. The simple centroid-based matching method is not a viable approach, as
it breaks down with truck motion and the presence of dynamically moving objects.
The implementation of odometry improved stability but has limitations because
the margin of error increases over time.
The ideal solution to the system features an EKF for tracking and match-
ing. While it was explored, it was not successfully implemented. Furthermore, a
Graph-based or Particle Filter SLAM solution could benefit the system in obstacle
mapping and real-time localization.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
The development and evaluation of a LiDAR-based anti-collision system for de-
icing trucks operating in airport environments has demonstrated significant ad-
vancements in static object detection and avoidance. Based on the E2E tests, the
developed solution has proven robust in differentiating between safe and danger-
ous proximities to static objects. These have been documented in the provided
demonstration videos. Additionally, the system has proven capable of processing
up to ten two-dimensional objects within a 10Hz operational limit.
The system’s eﬀiciency has been enhanced through preprocessing steps, includ-
ing RANSAC-based ground segmentation and a downsampling voxel size of 0.7
meters. These have been important for reducing noise and improving data qual-
ity, which has resulted in more accurate clustering. For clustering, the Open3D
DBSCAN algorithm was selected based on its eﬀiciency and functionality.
The implementation of truck bounding boxes, both single and multiple, has
improved the system’s spatial understanding while adequate the performance of
the system. The single BBox provided a simple approach for initial collision de-
tection, while the multiple bounding boxes enabled a dynamic representation of
the truck’s structure. Furthermore, the system successfully implements the Acker-
mann steering model for trajectory predictions, allowing the system to accurately
anticipate the truck’s future movements.
The benchmarked results highlight the system’s capability of operating success-
fully within a 10Hz refresh rate. At a 20Hz frequency, performance is limited by
the complexity of the distance computation methods. When down-scaled to 10 Hz,
the R2R distance measurements can process up to 10 objects, while the remain-
ing methods such as V2V, V2P, and R2P require more computational power to
operate within this limit. The benchmarked results for all four distance methods
reveal that further optimization may be required for integration of the solution
into a production-ready, larger autonomous system. Beyond this, as outlined in
section 10.1, options for future work also include the exploration of dynamic path
planning algorithms, enhancement of the system’s ability to track and avoid dy-
namic objects, and integration of an advanced SLAM method. In conclusion, the
capability of the system to significantly aid safe, and autonomous operation of

10.1.1
Path planning
The current system’s ’path planning’ mechanism is predefined with arbitrary way-
points, and the procedure when detecting an obstacle is to check if an emergency
stop is necessary or if the truck can continue past the obstacle without any addi-
tional steering input. Future advancements should explore dynamic path planning
algorithms capable of real-time path adjustment to navigate safely around obsta-
cles. [16]
10.1.2
Dynamic Object Handling
While the focus has been on static obstacle avoidance, extending the system to
dynamically track and avoid moving objects is a very important next step. Future
research regarding these topics includes the development and integration of algo-
rithms for predicting moving objects’ future positions and adjusting the truck’s
path accordingly. A strong candidate for this would be the EKF and respective
data association.
10.1.3
Optimization for Real-Time Processing
The system is currently limited in its processing capabilities, especially as the
number of objects increases. Specifically for the distance computations, parallel
processing becomes interesting. Another perspective is to take a few steps back,
and investigate other possibilities of conducting the distance measurements. This
could involve CH to CH computations.
