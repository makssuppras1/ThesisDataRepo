Abstract
Video Instance Segmentation (VIS) faces significant annotation challenges due to its dual
requirements of pixelôÙlevel masks and temporal consistency labels. While recent unsuperôÙ
vised methods like VideoCutLER eliminate optical flow dependencies through synthetic
data, they remain constrained by the syntheticôÙtoôÙreal domain gap. We present AutoQôÙ
VIS, a novel unsupervised framework that bridges this gap through qualityôÙguided selfôÙ
training. Our approach establishes a closedôÙloop system between pseudoôÙlabel generaôÙ
tion and automatic quality assessment, enabling progressive adaptation from synthetic
to real videos. Experiments demonstrate stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart performance with 52.6 AP50
on YouTubeVISôÙ2019 val set, surpassing the previous stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart VideoCutLER by
4.4%, while requiring no human annotations. This demonstrates the viability of qualityôÙ
aware selfôÙtraining for unsupervised VIS. The source code of our method is available at
https://github.com/wcbup/AutoQôÙVIS.

Introduction
Video Instance Segmentation (VIS) is the challenging task of simultaneously detecting,
segmenting, and tracking object instances across video sequences [2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7].
This capability is fundamental for scene understanding in applications ranging from auôÙ
tonomous driving [8] to video content editing [9]. However, training highôÙperformance
VIS models typically requires pixelôÙlevel annotations across all frames [2]. This process
is expensive due to the laborôÙintensive nature of annotating temporal consistency and
instance identities. As a result, there is an urgent need to develop unsupervised video inôÙ
stance segmentation frameworks that can accurately interpret video content and function
effectively across diverse, unlabeled environments.
Prior work [10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17] in unsupervised video segmentation predomôÙ
inantly addresses Video Object Segmentation (VOS), focusing on separating a single
foreground object via motion or consistency cues. While OCLR [18] introduces unsuôÙ
pervised VIS that supports multiple instances, its predefined object count during training
prevents dynamic adaptation to varying instances during inference. Furthermore, prior
approaches [10, 11, 12, 13, 18] rely on optical flow estimators (e.g., RAFT [19]) that are
trained on humanôÙannotated datasets. VideoCutLER [1] marks a breakthrough in unsuôÙ
pervised VIS and achieves unprecedented performance by demonstrating multiôÙinstance
segmentation without optical flow dependencies. Its core innovation lies in synthetic video
generation via spatial augmentations of CutLER [20] pseudoôÙlabels from ImageNet [21].
However, VideoCutLER remains constrained by syntheticôÙtoôÙreal domain gaps and static
instance modeling, i.e., the synthetic videos lack natural and realistic motion patterns.
Building upon VideoCutLERãs synthetic data paradigm, which generates training videos
through spatial augmentations of static image pseudoôÙlabels, we introduce AutoQôÙVIS to
address its critical domain gap limitation. While VideoCutLERãs synthetic videos provide
initial instance awareness, they lack natural motion patterns and realôÙworld appearance
variations, hindering adaptation to authentic video dynamics. Our framework bridges this
syntheticôÙtoôÙreal domain gap through a selfôÙtraining loop that progressively adds qualityôÙ
filtered pseudoôÙlabels from unlabeled real videos. Inspired by Mask Scoring RôÙCNN [22],
which directly predicts mask quality scores via an auxiliary branch, we implement a quality
assessment module for pseudoôÙlabel filtering of instance masks.
AutoQôÙVIS advances unsupervised video instance segmentation through an iterative selfôÙ
training paradigm with qualityôÙaware pseudoôÙlabel selection (fig. 1.1). The system iniôÙ
tializes using synthetic video data from VideoCutLER, which provides pseudoôÙlabels to
bootstrap a VideoMask2Former [23, 24] VIS model and a specialized mask quality preôÙ
dictor (section 3.1). The mask quality predictor estimates mask IoU quality scores by
analyzing frameôÙlevel features and mask predictions. During multiôÙround optimization,
the VIS model generates pseudoôÙlabels on unlabeled videos, which are then scored by
the quality predictor. HighôÙquality pseudoôÙlabels surpassing a fixed threshold are proôÙ
gressively incorporated into the training set, enabling dataset augmentation without any
human supervision (section 3.2).
To enhance the mask head training, we employ a
DropLoss that zeros out mask losses whose maximum groundôÙtruth overlap falls below
0.01 (section 3.3). By alternating rounds of VIS training (with occasional weight resets)
and qualityôÙbased dataset expansion, AutoQôÙVIS dynamically enriches its training dataset
and steadily sharpens segmentation performance.

2.1
Unsupervised video object segmentation (VOS)
Figure 2.1: Sample sequences from video object segmentation DAVIS 2016 dataset [25].
Unsupervised video object segmentation (VOS) [26, 25, 27, 28, 29] targets finding and
segmenting the most salient objects throughout the entire video sequence without any
humanôÙannotated labels in the training. It is worth noting that some materials [30, 31,
32, 33] refer to video salient object detection (SOD) as ãUnsupervised VOSã, which is a
different task that requires humanôÙannotated labels.
Current unsupervised video segmentation methods predominantly rely on detection based
on motion or consistency cues. Motion Grouping [10] introduces a method that uses
optical flow to train an unsupervised video segmentation network, and it uses only optical
flow as input.
As illustrated in fig. 2.2, Motion Group [10] takes optical flow Itãt+n as input and predicts
a set of reconstruction and opacity layers. The framework consists of three main compoôÙ
nents: a feature encoder, an iterative binding module, and a layer decoder, which together
reconstruct the input flow. To address motion ambiguities such as small displacements
and to mitigate noise in optical flow, the method enforces consistency between flow fields
computed with different frame gaps during training. During inference, only the top half
branch of the pipeline is used, producing segmentation masks from a singleôÙstep flow.
As shown in fig. 2.3, Motion Group [10] is capable of handling one object at one time. If
there are multiple moving objects, the model outputs multiple objects as one segmentation
mask.

Looped selfôÙtraining method
The paradigm of looped selfôÙtraining has emerged as a powerful mechanism for bootstrapôÙ
ping model capabilities through iterative selfôÙimprovement. By establishing a closedôÙloop
feedback system between pseudoôÙlabel generation and model retraining, this framework
enables continuous knowledge distillation from the modelãs own predictions. Recent adôÙ
vances demonstrate its versatility across diverse vision tasks through carefully designed
interaction protocols.
CRST [35] introduces a confidence regularized selfôÙtraining framework, which iteratively
takes highôÙconfidence targetôÙdomain predictions as pseudoôÙlabels for retraining. CRST
proposes a joint optimization framework that models pseudoôÙlabels as continuous latent
variables through alternating updates. This approach incorporates dual confidence regôÙ

pseudoôÙlabel assignments, while (2) Model Regularization (MR) enforces prediction conôÙ

Figure 2.11 demonstrates the confidence regularization mechanism in CRST [35]. ConôÙ
ventional selfôÙtraining without confidence regularization relies on hard pseudo labels for
supervision, producing sharply defined network outputs. In contrast, labelôÙregularized
selfôÙtraining employs soft pseudo labels, resulting in smoother output distributions. While
modelôÙregularized selfôÙtraining still utilizes hard pseudo labels, it introduces an explicit
regularizer to directly enforce output smoothness through consistency constraints. ComôÙ
pared to the traditional selfôÙtraining domain adaptation method that use hard pseudo laôÙ
bels [36], CRSTs have better performance.
Noisy Student [37] is a looped selfôÙtraining framework, where an EfficientNet [38] teacher
model preôÙtrained on labeled ImageNet [21] data generates pseudoôÙlabels for 300M unôÙ
labeled imagesëƒ a larger student model is subsequently trained on the union of true and
pseudo labels. Noisy Student iterates this process by putting the student back as the
teacher. The pipeline of the Noisy Student is illustrated in fig. 2.12.
ST3D [39] proposes an unsupervised framework for unsupervised domain adaptation in
3D object detection: their pipeline alternates between updating highôÙquality pseudoôÙlabels
(via a triplet memory bank) and retraining the detector (with curriculum augmentation) in
an iterative loop.
ST3D enhances selfôÙtraining performance through three synergistic components: (1) RanôÙ
dom Object Scaling (ROS) mitigates objectôÙsize bias via geometric perturbationëƒ (2) QualityôÙ
Aware Triplet Memory Bank (QTMB), which can reduce pseudoôÙlabel noise and stabilize
trainingëƒ (3) Curriculum Data Augmentation (CDA) employs a phased augmentation proôÙ
tocol that ensures initial learning efficacy before progressively introducing challenging
samples, thereby preventing model overfitting to highôÙconfidence pseudoôÙlabels (easy
examples).

3
Method
The overview of AutoQôÙVIS is shown in fig. 1.1. During the initial training phase, the
video instance segmentation model and mask quality predictor are jointly optimized on
ImageNetôÙbased synthetic videos [21] with pseudoôÙlabels from CutLER [20]. The selfôÙ
training phase then initiates cyclical refinement: (1) The VIS model generates candidate
pseudo masks on unlabeled videosëƒ (2) The frozen quality predictor (preôÙtrained in previôÙ
ous stage) performs confidence scoring for noise suppressionëƒ (3) HighôÙquality masks
meeting selection thresholds are aggregated into the training datasetëƒ (4) The model
undergoes progressive retraining using both synthetic data and curated pseudoôÙlabels,
achieving iterative performance improvement through successive training cycles.
We divide AutoQôÙVIS into three key parts:
ãÂ Initial training (section 3.1), where we jointly pretrain a VideoMask2Former [23,
24] VIS model and a MaskôÙScoringôÙRôÙCNNãstyle [22] quality predictor on synthetic
videos from VideoCutLER [1]ëƒ
ãÂ MultiôÙround selfôÙtraining (section 3.2), in which we alternate between: (i) using
the VIS model to generate pseudoôÙlabels on unlabeled videos, (ii) scoring each
pseudoôÙlabel by regressing its IoU via the quality predictor, and (iii) adding only
those pseudoôÙlabels back into the training set that are both confident and have high
predicted IoU, as determined by a combined quality score exceeding a fixed threshôÙ
oldëƒ
ãÂ DropLoss for the mask head (section 3.3), which dynamically suppresses loss
contributions from any predicted mask whose maximum groundôÙtruth overlap falls
below a small IoU threshold and enhances the mask head training. Through this
iterative, qualityôÙguided dataset augmentation, our pipeline progressively refines inôÙ
stance segmentation accuracy without requiring any human annotations.

Experiment
4.0.1
Datasets
Our model is trained on synthetic videos from VideoCutLER [1] and the unlabeled train
split of YouTubeVISôÙ2019 [2]. We evaluate our modelãs performance on the val split of
YouTubeVISôÙ2019 in a classôÙagnostic manner, which means there are only two classes
in the dataset (object and background). We merge the 40 object classes in YouTubeVISôÙ
2019 into one ãobjectã class.
4.0.2
Evaluation metrics
Following VideoCutLER [1], we use Average Precision (AP) and Average Recall (AR)
as evaluation metrics. We evaluate the models in a classôÙagnostic manner, treating all
classes as a single one during evaluation.
4.0.3
Implementation details
For the initial training stage, we use the pretrained VideoMask2Former model [23, 24]
from VideoCutLER [1] to initialize our VIS model. Then we train the VIS model and quality
predictor on synthetic videos from VideoCutLER for 8,000 iterations using a single V100
GPU, with a batch size of 2 and a learning rate of 2û10ã5. For each round of multiôÙround
selfôÙtraining, we train the VIS model for 10,000 iterations on two V100 GPUs, with a batch
size of 4 and a learning rate of 2û10ã5. In practice, we find that two rounds of selfôÙtraining
provide the best performance.

Trade recall for precision
An shown in table 4.1, the improvement of AR10 is small (0.2%). The reason may be that
our pipeline removes many lowôÙquality pseudo labels (as described in chapter 3 and the
pipeline seems to trade recall for precision.
In table 4.4, we can see that the AR10 decreases as the number of selfôÙtraining increases,
which further strengthens our claim.

No improvement on small instances
In table 4.1, we also compare the APS, APM, APL of the methods. We gain an improveôÙ
ment of 4.4% APL and 3.5% APM. But we donãt gain any improvement in APS, indicating
that our method mostly benefits from large and medium instances. It is worth noting

which is probably the reason why our pipeline doesnãt benefit from small instances.

As shown in fig. 4.2, the improvement between the quality score and the confidence score

As we can see in table 4.5, the quality score threshold üth has a huge impact on the
performance of the model. When the üth is not correctly set, AP50 only increase 0.5%
or 0.6%. This may result in the bad performance of the method on various datasets that

Conclusion and Future Work
We present AutoQôÙVIS, a qualityôÙaware selfôÙtraining framework that advances unsuperôÙ
vised video instance segmentation through iterative pseudoôÙlabel refinement with autoôÙ
matic quality control. By establishing a closedôÙloop system of pseudoôÙlabel generation
and automatic quality assessment, our method achieves stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart performance
(52.6 AP50) on YouTubeVISôÙ2019 val split without requiring any human annotations. The
simple quality predictor proves effective in pseudoôÙlabel quality assessment.
Future work may begin by applying the pipeline to larger unlabeled datasets and evaluôÙ
ating on a wider range of video segmentation datasets. For example, we can train the
model on video classification datasets such as YouTubeôÙ8M [44] (containing 6.1 million
videos), and evaluate it on more difficult datasets such as OVIS [45].
The current architecture of our quality predictor is very simple. We can consider using
a more powerful tool, such as a visionôÙlanguage model like Molmo [46] to empower our
quality predictor. And we can also consider using other ways instead of the quality score
threshold to filter the pseudo labels, since the results are over sensitive to the score threshôÙ
old.
We can also incorporate the curriculum data augmentation [39] into our pipeline, which
can ensure initial learning efficacy before progressively introducing challenging samples,
thereby preventing model overfitting to highôÙconfidence pseudolabels (easy examples).
Hopefully, it will make our pipeline benefit more from small instances.
