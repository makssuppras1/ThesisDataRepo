Summary
Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) allows researchers to study gene expression
at the level of individual cells. While this has led to important biological discoveries,
the resulting data are often large, sparse, and diﬀicult to interpret.
Many deep
learning models have been developed to analyze scRNA-seq data, but most work like
black boxes and do not explain how they make predictions.
In this project, we adapt a prototype-based deep learning model called Pantypes
to the scRNA-seq setting. Pantypes combines a variational autoencoder (VAE) with
learnable class-specific prototype vectors that help explain how the model classifies
each cell. To improve diversity among prototypes, Pantypes includes a special loss
term based on volume and uses a pruning mechanism to remove redundant prototypes.
We train the model on three real datasets (Heart, Skin, Eye) and evaluate both
classification performance and how well the prototypes represent the structure of the
data.
Across all datasets, the model achieves high classification accuracy and produces
a well-organized latent space. However, we find that while prototypes capture impor-
tant patterns, they often miss parts of the data space—especially in large or complex
cell types. To better understand this, we compare Pantypes to metacells, which are
unsupervised groups of similar cells created using graph-based clustering and statisti-
cal filtering. Metacells aim to summarize local regions of the data without relying on
labels. We find that metacells often provide more complete and balanced coverage of
the data space than Pantypes alone.
Overall, Pantypes offers a step toward more transparent classification of scRNA-
seq data. We discuss its strengths and limitations, and suggest ways the model can
be improved in future work.

CHAPTER 1
Motivation
Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) has transformed how scientists study biology
by allowing gene expression to be measured at the level of individual cells. This is a
major step forward compared to earlier techniques like bulk RNA sequencing, which
only give an average signal across many cells and can hide important differences
between them. With scRNA-seq, researchers can now detect rare cell types, track
how cells change over time, and better understand the diversity of cells within tissues
[Yua+17], [Adi+21].
This has opened up new possibilities in many areas of biomedical research. For
example, in cancer biology, scRNA-seq has been used to study tumor heterogeneity
and identify specific subpopulations of cells that drive disease progression or resist
treatment [LT19]. In developmental biology, it has helped map the pathways that
cells follow as they grow and specialize into different types. In immunology, scRNA-
seq has been key to studying how immune cells respond to infections or inflammation
by uncovering dynamic changes in their gene expression [Adi+21], [Bar+19].
Because it provides such detailed insight into the behavior of single cells, scRNA-
seq is now considered an essential tool for understanding complex biological sys-
tems. It is also a major driver behind recent advances in personalized and precision
medicine, where treatments can be tailored to the unique cellular makeup of each
patient [Lop+18]. As datasets become larger and more complex, new computational
tools are needed to help extract meaningful patterns from the data and make sense
of this rich biological information.
Computational Challenges in scRNA-seq
Although scRNA-seq provides powerful biological insights, the data it produces is
diﬀicult to analyze. One major challenge is that it is high-dimensional—each cell is
measured across thousands of genes. At the same time, the data is sparse and noisy,
meaning that many gene values are missing or close to zero. This is partly due to
dropout events, where genes that are truly expressed in a cell are not detected during
sequencing [Adi+21]. As a result, it can be hard to tell whether a zero means the
gene was not expressed or just missed during measurement.
Another problem is batch variability, where differences in how samples are processed—
such as timing, equipment, or lab conditions—introduce artificial variation that has

nothing to do with biology. These batch effects can mask real biological patterns or
make cells from different experiments look falsely distinct [Adi+21], [Era+19].
Moreover, relationships between cells are often non-linear. For example, a cell
transitioning from one state to another might follow a curved or branching path in
gene expression space. Classical methods like PCA or clustering often assume simpler
structures and may miss these patterns [Yua+17], [Lop+18].
To handle these issues, more advanced models have been introduced. Variational
autoencoders (VAEs), in particular, can learn low-dimensional ”latent” representa-
tions of cells that capture the main biological signals while ignoring noise and dropout
[Era+19]. These models have become popular in scRNA-seq analysis because they
can uncover hidden structure and improve both visualization and classification.
Problem of Interpretability
Even though deep learning models like variational autoencoders (VAEs) and tools
such as scVI have improved how we handle noisy and complex scRNA-seq data, they
still act like black boxes. That means we can see their predictions, but we don’t re-
ally understand why those predictions were made [Era+19], [Lop+18]. In biological
research, this is a major problem. Scientists need to know which patterns the model
used, whether the result can be trusted, and how it connects to known biology. With-
out that clarity, it’s diﬀicult to draw reliable conclusions or make useful discoveries
[Rud19].
To solve this, the field of Explainable AI (XAI) has introduced new model designs
that are easier to understand. One promising idea is to use prototypes: representative
examples that explain what the model has learned. For instance, ProtoPNet finds
image patches that resemble a class and compares new data to them
[Che+19].
ProtoVAE extends this idea to more complex data by learning prototypes in a latent
space, which makes it better for structured data like gene expression [Gau+23]. These
models try to balance accuracy with transparency. Building on these ideas, Pantypes
was recently proposed as a new model that learns multiple prototypes for each class
and spreads them out in the latent space to better reflect diversity [KBC23]. This
makes it a strong candidate for combining prediction and explanation, especially in
data as complex and variable as scRNA-seq.
Limitations of Classical Prototypes
While prototype-based models offer a more interpretable alternative to black-box
methods, traditional prototypes also have important limitations. One major issue is
redundancy—many prototypes end up being too similar to each other, capturing the
same features or regions in the latent space [Gau+23]. This wastes model capacity
and does not add new information. Another problem is that the prototypes are often
poorly distributed: instead of covering the full shape of a class, they tend to cluster in
dense or obvious areas. As a result, rare or subtle patterns are missed. These models

also tend to favor majority classes, since the learning process pulls prototypes toward
common examples and ignores outliers or low-density regions [Che+19], [Gau+23].
This kind of bias reduces the quality of interpretation and limits the model’s ability
to reflect the true variety found in complex data like scRNA-seq.
How can Pantypes be a Game Changer
Pantypes were introduced to fix several core problems of earlier prototype-based mod-
els, especially those related to diversity and coverage. Previous methods like ProtoP-
Net and ProtoVAE focused on interpretability but often fell short when it came to
representing the full spread of the data. Many prototypes ended up close together in
dense regions, ignoring rare or outlying patterns. Pantypes improve this by replacing
the standard orthogonality loss with a new volumetric loss, based on Determinantal
Point Processes (DPPs).
This encourages the model to push prototypes far from
each other in the latent space, covering a broader area and making sure that different
aspects of each class are represented. Instead of just avoiding overlap, the model
now explicitly rewards spreading out the prototypes — a better match for complex,
high-dimensional data like scRNA-seq where small differences can be biologically
meaningful [KBC23].
Another key innovation in Pantypes is dynamic prototype pruning.
In earlier
models, the number of prototypes had to be fixed ahead of time, and poor allocation
could lead to unused or redundant prototypes. Pantypes allow the model to drop
prototypes that aren’t helpful—those that never become the closest match for any
input. This means that simple classes don’t waste capacity on extra prototypes, and
complex ones can keep more to reflect their diversity. Together, the volumetric loss
and pruning create a flexible system that adapts to the data rather than forcing a rigid
structure. These changes help the model learn clearer, more useful representations
without hurting classification accuracy
[KBC23]. In theory, this makes Pantypes
especially suitable for scRNA-seq data, which is often sparse, noisy, and shaped by
both dominant and rare patterns.

CHAPTER 2
Goal
Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) has become a key technology for understand-
ing cell types, states, and their underlying gene expression. However, due to its high
dimensionality, sparsity, and noise, it remains a challenge to extract meaningful struc-
ture and variation from such data [LT19]. Many models perform well in classification
tasks, but few offer interpretable insights into how they reach their predictions or
how they represent the underlying biological diversity.
Pantypes is a recently proposed deep learning framework that combines a varia-
tional autoencoder with class-specific prototype vectors in the latent space [KBC23].
These prototypes, or pantypes, are trained to represent different regions of each class,
with the goal of making the model both accurate and interpretable. The original work
focused on image datasets and demonstrated that the model could capture intra-class
diversity through its prototypes.
In this thesis, we investigate whether the Pantypes approach can be meaningfully
applied to scRNA-seq data. Specifically, we want to evaluate if the learned pantypes
reflect meaningful structure within the data and how they compare to metacells, a
standard technique used to summarize biological variation by averaging over local
groups of cells [Bar+19].
The thesis addresses the following two questions:
• Can the Pantypes methodology be effectively applied to single-cell RNA se-
quencing (scRNA-seq) data?
• How do the representations learned by Pantypes compare to metacell-based
representations in capturing class-specific structure and diversity in scRNA-seq
data?
Rather than focusing on biomarker identification alone, this project centers on
structure and representation: whether the model’s learned pantypes can serve as
meaningful anchors in latent space, and whether they align with the natural geometry
of scRNA-seq data as captured by unsupervised methods.
Research Hypothesis
We hypothesize that Pantypes, even though originally developed for image data
[KBC23], can be successfully adapted to high-dimensional biological data such as

scRNA-seq. Specifically, we expect that the learned prototypes (pantypes) will not
only support accurate classification but also provide interpretable summaries of each
class by spreading across its structure in latent space.
We also hypothesize that, although pantypes and metacells are constructed differently—
one supervised, the other unsupervised—they will capture overlapping parts of the
class geometry. Comparing their spatial coverage can help reveal how much biological
variation is reflected in the model’s internal representation [Bar+19; LT19].
Specific Objectives
The work focuses on two main objectives that align with the research questions:
• Objective 1: Evaluate whether the pantypes learned by the model form mean-
ingful and well-distributed representations in the latent space of scRNA-seq
data. This involves training the model on real single-cell datasets and analyz-
ing the positioning and coverage of prototypes across different classes [KBC23].
• Objective 2: Compare the structure captured by pantypes with that captured
by metacells. This includes both visual (e.g., UMAP overlays) and quantitative
(e.g., convex hull coverage) analyses to assess how much of each class’s shape is
represented by each method [Bar+19].
By addressing these objectives, the project aims to assess the usefulness of pantypes
as an interpretable modeling tool for scRNA-seq data, and to clarify whether they
can provide structure-aware representations that reflect biological diversity—not just
prediction accuracy.

3.1
Introduction to scRNA-seq Analysis
Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) is a transformative technology that profiles
transcriptomes at single-cell resolution, uncovering cellular heterogeneity previously
masked by bulk RNA sequencing approaches [OB18]. Since its emergence, scRNA-seq
has revolutionized biological and biomedical research, facilitating significant advance-
ments in oncology, developmental biology, immunology, and neuroscience [Jov+22].
In oncology, it has enabled the characterization of tumor heterogeneity, identify-
ing rare cell populations and elucidating molecular mechanisms driving disease pro-
gression. In developmental biology, scRNA-seq aids in mapping cellular differentia-
tion pathways and understanding lineage specification at unprecedented resolution
[OB18],
[Jov+22]. Despite its enormous potential, scRNA-seq poses considerable
computational challenges.
The data generated is inherently high-dimensional and
sparse due to substantial dropout events—instances where genes are undetected not
due to biological absence, but due to technical limitations.
Additionally, scRNA-
seq datasets commonly suffer from batch effects and zero-inflation, complicating sta-
tistical analyses and downstream interpretations
[Jov+22].
These computational
challenges necessitate advanced analytical methods, robust normalization techniques,
and innovative dimensionality reduction and clustering algorithms, underscoring the
importance of continued methodological developments to unlock scRNA-seq’s full
analytical and translational potential [OB18], [Jov+22].
3.1.1
Overview of Computational Methods for scRNA-seq Analysis
Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) analysis necessitates sophisticated compu-
tational methods for preprocessing, dimensionality reduction, and clustering due to
its inherent complexity. Preprocessing includes critical normalization techniques ad-
dressing batch effects and technical artifacts. SCnorm and scran correct library size
variations and technical noise, enhancing comparability across samples. However, SC-
norm shows limitations in scalability for large datasets, whereas scran can struggle
with resolving distinct populations
[Col+19]. Seurat emerges as widely preferred,
offering eﬀicient scalability and robust performance across varying dataset sizes, in-
tegrating both normalization and batch correction functionalities [Stu+19].
Dimensionality reduction is crucial due to the high dimensionality of scRNA-seq

data. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) reduces dimensions linearly, eﬀiciently
summarizing variance in data but often failing to capture complex, non-linear re-
lationships inherent in gene expression data. Non-linear methods like t-distributed
Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE) preserve local cell relationships but suffer
from sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning, instability, and limited global structure
representation. Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) addresses
these issues by maintaining both local and global structures effectively, making it
a preferred method in contemporary pipelines
[Bec+19].
Recently, autoencoder-

to their capacity to capture deeper, non-linear patterns and learn meaningful latent

Graph-based clustering algorithms such as Louvain and Leiden have become promi-
nent due to their computational eﬀiciency, scalability, and robust performance, espe-
cially in large-scale datasets [TWE19]. The Leiden algorithm particularly improves
the detection of smaller subpopulations compared to Louvain by providing enhanced
cluster resolution and stability. In contrast, density-based methods like DBSCAN
have higher computational demands and sensitivity to hyperparameters, limiting their
practical use in scRNA-seq analysis, especially for larger datasets [KAH19]. There-
fore, for scRNA-seq datasets, methods like Louvain and particularly Leiden clustering
are typically preferred.
3.1.2
Computational Challenges in scRNA-seq Data Analysis
Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) enables detailed characterization of individ-
ual cells but poses substantial computational challenges. Key issues include data spar-
sity, batch effects, and high-dimensionality, each complicating downstream analyses
significantly. Data sparsity primarily arises from dropout events, where transcripts
present in a cell are not detected due to technical limitations, such as ineﬀicient
RNA capture or amplification bias during library preparation
[Adi+21]. This re-
sults in zero-inflated datasets where many expression values are falsely represented
as zeros, complicating statistical modeling and obscuring genuine biological signals.
Addressing dropout effects requires specialized computational models such as zero-
inflated negative binomial (ZINB) distributions and advanced algorithms capable of
handling missing data [Yua+17], [Adi+21]. Batch effects, another critical challenge,
emerge from technical variability introduced during sample preparation, sequencing,
and processing across different experimental batches. Such non-biological variation
can dominate true biological differences, impairing comparability across experiments.
Normalization methods such as Seurat and Combat, along with robust computational
pipelines, are essential to mitigate these effects by harmonizing datasets and preserv-
ing genuine biological variability [Adi+21]. Lastly, the inherent high-dimensionality
of scRNA-seq data, characterized by measuring thousands of genes across millions of
cells, demands substantial computational resources. Eﬀicient dimensionality reduc-

3.2
Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) in scRNA-seq
Analysis
Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) are powerful deep generative models designed to
handle high-dimensional data through a probabilistic encoder-decoder architecture.
VAEs compress data into a lower-dimensional latent space, from which data is recon-
structed by optimizing a combined loss function consisting of a reconstruction loss
and a Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence regularization term. This structure makes
VAEs particularly suitable for scRNA-seq data, which are characteristically sparse,
noisy, and high-dimensional due to dropout events and zero-inflation. To identify the
most effective applications of VAEs in this context, we surveyed the current literature
and selected several prominent models that have demonstrated strong performance
and wide adoption in the field. Our selection—scVI, DCA, DREAM, and biVI—was
based on their methodological innovations, scalability, and their ability to improve
downstream analyses such as clustering, differential expression, and trajectory infer-
ence.
scVI (single-cell Variational Inference):
scVI extends the classical VAE frame-
work by modeling scRNA-seq data with a zero-inflated negative binomial (ZINB)
likelihood, effectively addressing the excessive zeros and technical dropout events in-
herent in these datasets. Its architecture comprises fully-connected neural networks
with two to three layers, typically using 128–256 units per layer, incorporating batch
normalization, ReLU activations, and dropout for regularization. scVI explicitly cap-
tures batch and library-size effects, thus eﬀiciently normalizing scRNA-seq data and
enabling robust downstream analyses like clustering, visualization, and differential
expression (DE). Its scalable variational inference enables the handling of over one
million cells eﬀiciently, significantly outperforming traditional approaches [Lop+18].
DCA (Deep Count Autoencoder):
DCA employs an autoencoder structure with
a negative binomial output layer tailored for denoising and imputing scRNA-seq
counts directly. Unlike traditional methods relying on transformed count data, DCA
utilizes the negative binomial likelihood (optionally with zero-inflation) to handle
gene expression variability and dropout events effectively. Its lightweight MLP-based
architecture facilitates fast and scalable training, processing datasets of millions of
cells eﬀiciently, improving clustering accuracy, trajectory inference, and DE analysis
by denoising scRNA-seq data [Era+19].
==PAGE:22==
10
3 Relevant Research
DREAM (Deep Representation learning for scRNA-seq):
DREAM incor-
porates Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) into its latent space to capture distinct
cell populations explicitly, thereby improving clustering resolution. Additionally, it
employs a differentiable zero-inflation layer using the Gumbel-softmax trick, directly
modeling dropouts within the decoder. DREAM effectively balances computational
complexity and interpretability, enabling superior clustering and trajectory inference
compared to classical methods like PCA and t-SNE, and even other VAE-based mod-
els like scVI [Jia+23].
biVI (Biophysically Interpretable Variational Inference):
The biVI model
integrates biophysical models of transcriptional dynamics within the VAE framework,
enhancing biological interpretability by explicitly modeling transcriptional bursting,
degradation, and splicing kinetics. Unlike scVI’s black-box ZINB likelihood, biVI
fits both unspliced (nascent) and spliced (mature) counts jointly, uncovering kinetic
regulatory shifts invisible to mean-based DE analysis. While slightly compromising
latent-space compression, biVI provides direct mechanistic insights into gene regula-
tion, making it ideal for hypothesis-driven research in scRNA-seq [Car+24].
In summary, the advancements provided by scVI, DCA, DREAM, and biVI exem-
plify the versatility of VAEs in handling scRNA-seq’s inherent complexities, enhancing
both computational performance and biological interpretability.
3.2.1
MLP-based VAE Architectures and their Advantages
Multilayer perceptron-based Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) are generalized and
flexible models applicable to high-dimensional single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-
seq) data. And unlike linear methods like principal component analysis (PCA), MLPs
can capture such non-linear relationships in the data using deep hierarchical struc-
tures of fully-connected neural layers [GBC16]. A multilayer perceptron encoder and
decoder network is implemented as an MLP-VAE:
Encoder – compresses input data x into a low-dimensional latent representation
z.
Decoder – reconstructs data from this latent space optimizing a variational lower
bound (ELBO) of the form:
LELBO(ϕ, θ) = Eqϕ(z|x) [log pθ(x|z)] −KL(qϕ(z|x) ∥p(z))
In this case, qϕ(z|x) is the encoder variational posterior, pθ(x|z) is the decoder
likelihood, and KL(qϕ(z|x) ∥p(z)) is the Kullback–Leibler divergence regularization of
the latent distribution within a predefined prior (usually standard Gaussian) ensuring
regularized latent representations are interpretable [KW13].
For scRNA-seq analysis, an MLP-based architecture permits VAEs to model non-
linear biological structures which linear models can not capture well.
Its flexibil-
ity makes this architecture well suited for zero-inflation, batch effects, and technical
dropout – common problems in single-cell data analysis [Lop+18]. For example, scVI,

a well-known MLP-based VAE for scRNA-seq, uses fully-connected layers, ReLU ac-
tivations, dropout regularization, and batch normalization to capture cellular vari-
ability and correct batch-specific biases to provide robust latent representations for
downstream tasks [Lop+18].
An additional benefit of MLP-based VAEs lies in their scalability.
Now that
scRNA-seq experiments typically involve tens of thousands to millions of cells, scal-
ability is critical. Given their computational structure, MLPs can eﬀiciently exploit
parallel hardware [Era+19]. Such a Deep Count Autoencoder (DCA) uses an MLP
architecture with a Negative Binomial output layer to quickly denoise large-scale
datasets by learning robust latent spaces from raw count data [Era+19]. That allows
for better computation eﬀiciency and downstream analyses such as clustering and
differential expression.

Prototype-Based Self-Explainable Models
In recent years, deep learning models have achieved remarkable successes in various
fields, including computer vision, natural language processing, and bioinformatics.
==PAGE:24==

3 Relevant Research
Despite these advancements, deep learning methods typically operate as ”black-box”
models, characterized by high complexity and low transparency. Such models often
lack clear explanations for their predictions, making it challenging for users to trust
their output, especially in high-stakes applications such as healthcare, finance, and
legal decisions. In these domains, interpretability is not only desirable, but critical,
as understanding the reasoning behind model predictions can significantly impact
decision making, risk management, and regulatory compliance [Rud19].
To address this issue, the field of Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) has
emerged, focusing on developing methodologies that can provide human-understandable
insights into how AI systems reach their conclusions [Arr+20]. One notable approach
within XAI is the use of prototype-based methods. Prototypes serve as representative
examples or reference vectors that encapsulate essential features of a class or cate-
gory. Using these types of prototypes, models can provide transparent and intuitive
explanations about their predictions, which significantly enhances interpretability and
trust.
Prototype-based methods operate by associating model predictions with learned
prototypical examples that humans can easily understand. For example, in medical
diagnostics, a prototype could be a representative case of a patient, encapsulating
typical symptoms and biomarkers of a specific disease. In image classification tasks,
prototypes can correspond to particular visual patterns or object instances that ex-
emplify each category [Che+19]. This approach provides a natural and meaningful
interpretation since users can directly inspect and compare the prototypes with new
instances.
By offering explicit and transparent explanations through prototypes, these mod-
els bridge the gap between sophisticated predictive performance and interpretability.
This transparency is especially crucial in critical decision-making scenarios, where
users must understand not only the predictions but also the reasoning behind them.
As a result, prototype-based self-explainable models are gaining traction as a promis-
ing solution to the black-box problem, ensuring that advanced machine learning mod-
els can be both accurate and interpretable.
3.3.1
Existing Prototype-Based Methods
ProtoPNet (Prototype-based Network)
ProtoPNet [Che+19], is an interpretable neural network that classifies images by iden-
tifying prototypical image parts. The network dissects images into learned ”patch-
based” prototypes, each serving as representative visual patterns for different classes.
Predictions are based on similarities between these prototypes and input image patches,
offering direct visual interpretability. Although ProtoPNet greatly enhances trans-
parency by explicitly visualizing relevant image parts used for classification, it may
compromise predictive accuracy due to its rigid dependence on simple, human-interpretable
visual patterns, limiting its ability to generalize effectively[Che+19].

ProtoVAE (Prototypical Variational Autoencoder)
ProtoVAE, [Gau+23], advances interpretability by integrating prototype-based rea-
soning into a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) framework. Unlike ProtoPNet, Proto-
VAE learns prototype vectors within a latent space constructed by an encoder-decoder
architecture, enabling robust latent representations. Prototypes represent character-
istic patterns for each class and are regularized to be diverse and orthonormal, min-

explicitly modeling data uncertainty and visualizing prototypes through the decoder,
thus making it suitable for applications requiring both interpretability and high pre-

to black-box models, effectively balancing interpretability and accuracy[Gau+23].
3.3.2
Pantypes Concept
Pantypes emerge as a powerful refinement within prototype-based Self-Explainable
Models (SEMs), developed specifically to enhance diversity and ensure comprehensive
data representation. Classical prototype-based SEMs, while interpretable, can often
suffer from representation bias, where the prototypes are clustered tightly around
common data patterns, neglecting more nuanced or rare characteristics within the
data distribution. Pantypes address this shortcoming by redefining prototypes into a
new paradigm that prioritizes diverse coverage across the entire latent representation
space, capturing a more complete spectrum of the underlying data characteristics
[KBC23].
Unlike traditional prototype methods, pantypes dynamically adjust their repre-
sentation complexity based on the intrinsic diversity of each class. To achieve this
adaptability, pantypes employ a dynamic prototype pruning mechanism, which con-
tinuously eliminates out-of-distribution (OOD) prototypes that contribute little to
the representation task. This dynamic pruning ensures that the number and posi-
tioning of prototypes reflect the complexity and variability of each class, providing
a more meaningful and representative interpretability. Another critical innovation
in the Pantypes framework is the introduction of a volumetric loss term inspired
by Determinantal Point Processes (DPP). This loss term mathematically encourages
the prototypes to spread across the latent space, thereby reducing redundancy and
ensuring robust diversity coverage.
3.3.3

Determinantal Point Processes (DPP)
Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) are probabilistic models designed to represent
diversity among selected subsets. In mathematical terms, a DPP defines the proba-
bility of selecting a particular subset as proportional to the determinant of a kernel
matrix, reflecting the diversity or dissimilarity among selected points. Formally, given

the latent space. Pantypes leverages this property of Determinantal Point Processes

Prototype Elimination
In addition to promoting diversity, Pantypes also includes a mechanism to reduce
redundancy by eliminating prototypes that do not contribute meaningfully to the
model. This process, known as prototype elimination, helps the model focus on the
most useful prototypes for each class. The idea is simple: if a prototype is never the
closest one to any data point during training, it is considered unnecessary and can
be removed.
To implement this, the model keeps track of how often each prototype is selected
as the most similar (i.e., highest scoring) prototype for the training samples. If a
prototype is consistently ignored by the data, it is marked as inactive. At regular
intervals during training, inactive prototypes are dropped, and the remaining ones
continue to be updated. This dynamic adjustment allows the model to better match
the complexity of each class. Simpler classes end up with fewer prototypes, while
more diverse ones can retain more [KBC23].
This elimination step not only reduces the number of redundant prototypes but
also improves the interpretability of the model. By keeping only the prototypes that
are actively used, the final set offers a clearer summary of the meaningful patterns
in the data. It also reduces noise in the latent space, as unused prototypes are often
placed in areas with no real biological relevance. Overall, prototype elimination is a
practical way to simplify the model without sacrificing accuracy or coverage.
3.3.4
Pantypes Advantages
The Pantypes method introduces significant advancements over earlier prototype-
based methods by explicitly focusing on enhanced diversity and comprehensive data
representation in the latent space. Unlike traditional prototype-based frameworks
such as ProtoPNet and ProtoVAE, which often suffer from representation redun-
dancy or under-coverage of the data distribution, Pantypes adopts a volumetric loss
inspired by Determinantal Point Processes (DPP) to ensure prototypes (”pantypes”)
span a more extensive and diverse region of the latent space. This geometric cov-
erage directly translates into more comprehensive and representative characteriza-
tions of the underlying data structure, significantly improving representation quality.
Unlike earlier prototype-based approaches such as ProtoPNet [Che+19] and Proto-
VAE [Gau+23], Pantypes explicitly incorporates a volumetric diversity loss, inspired
by Determinantal Point Processes (DPP), specifically aiming to promote diversity
among prototypes.
This design helps Pantypes better capture nuanced variations
within datasets, theoretically leading to richer latent representations and potentially
enhancing interpretability.
Pantypes’ focus on diversity does not merely improve model representation; it also
mitigates representation bias, an essential factor in ensuring fairness. By fostering
prototype diversity, Pantypes naturally accommodates demographic and class-based
diversity, which traditional methods may unintentionally neglect. Experimental eval-
uations conducted in diverse demographic contexts highlight Pantypes’ ability in

representing underrepresented groups more effectively. This directly contributes to
improved fairness and decreased bias in predictive outcomes, crucially beneficial in
sensitive and high-stakes areas like healthcare and finance, where representation bi-

Importantly, these advantages do not compromise Pantypes’ predictive perfor-

Empirical evaluations illustrate that despite introducing mechanisms for

Bridging Latent Representations and Metacell
Inference
While earlier sections described MLP-based variational autoencoders (VAEs) for find-
ing compact and understandable patterns in single-cell RNA data, this study also
draws on a different approach: metacell inference. Metacells are formed by grouping
similar cells into stable, combined profiles using graph-based or geometric clustering
methods. Although VAEs and metacells are designed for different tasks, this thesis
brings them together under a shared goal: identifying meaningful cell types. Specif-
ically, it examines whether the Pantypes prototypes are similar to metacells in their
gene expression patterns and marker genes. To support this comparison, the following
section gives an overview of how metacells are created.
3.4.1
The Concept and Motivation Behind Metacells
As mentioned previously, single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data are known
for their sparsity, technical noise, and high dimensionality.
These characteristics
==PAGE:29==

17
make it diﬀicult to distinguish true biological variation from artifacts introduced by
low transcript counts, dropout events, and sequencing limitations. To address these
challenges, Baran et al. (2019) introduced the concept of metacells—small groups of
transcriptionally similar cells that can be treated as a single representative unit for
downstream analysis [Bar+19].
The core idea is that by pooling together a small number of highly similar cells,
one can effectively reduce the noise inherent in single-cell measurements while pre-
serving the biological resolution of cell states. Each metacell is designed to behave
as a coherent sampling unit drawn from a single multinomial distribution, ensuring
internal consistency and minimal internal heterogeneity. This approach reduces the
effect of random dropout events and allows for more stable statistical measurements,
such as gene–gene correlations, expression variability, and marker detection.
Additionally, metacells serve as a form of biologically meaningful compression.
Rather than analyzing tens or hundreds of thousands of noisy single cells, researchers
can work with a few thousand robust metacells, each representing a local neighbor-
hood of the transcriptomic manifold.
This not only improves computational eﬀi-
ciency but also enhances interpretability and reproducibility. The authors emphasize
that this strategy allows for ”robust yet fine-grained modeling of the transcriptomic
landscape” by focusing on statistically well-behaved cell aggregates instead of noisy
individual cells [Bar+19].
Thus, metacells provide a practical and principled method to regularize scRNA-
seq data, denoise expression patterns, and structure the data into interpretable units,
all while retaining the granularity needed for identifying cell types and biological
processes.
3.4.2
Methodological Approaches to Metacell Construction
Graph-based partitioning approach:
One of the earliest and most influential approaches to metacell construction is the
graph-based algorithm introduced in MetaCell v1
[Bar+19].
This method starts
by building a cell–cell similarity graph based on rank-transformed Pearson corre-
lations of log-normalized gene expression profiles. The resulting aﬀinity matrix is
symmetrized through a rank-product transformation and regularized by enforcing
both incoming and outgoing edge constraints, yielding a balanced K-nearest neigh-
bor graph.
The metacells themselves are initialized by seeding densely connected
subgraphs and refined through an iterative graph-based “k-means” procedure that
maximizes the association strength of each cell to candidate subgraphs. To improve
robustness, MetaCell uses a bootstrapping scheme that repeatedly partitions random
subsamples of the data, recording the co-assignment frequency between each pair of
cells. These co-occurrence statistics are used to construct a denoised consensus graph,
upon which a final partition is applied. Additional filtering steps remove outlier cells
based on expression deviations from expected multinomial profiles and split hetero-
geneous metacells using DBSCAN clustering over high-variance genes. The result is
==PAGE:30==
18
3 Relevant Research
a set of compact, homogeneous, and biologically consistent metacells that preserve
transcriptional structure while reducing technical noise and data sparsity.
Divide-and-Conquer Hierarchical Inference:
A second family of methods for metacell construction builds on a divide-and-conquer
hierarchical inference strategy, exemplified by the MetaCell-2 (MC2) algorithm devel-
oped by Ben-Kiki et al. [Ben+22]. This method was designed to overcome the scal-
ability limits of previous metacell approaches, particularly when applied to datasets
comprising millions of single cells. Instead of operating on the full data graph, MC2
recursively partitions the dataset into smaller subsets or “piles” (typically containing
10,000–30,000 cells), within which metacells are initially inferred in parallel. These
metacells are then aggregated into “metagroups,” and a final refinement phase re-
computes higher-quality metacells from these reorganized piles. The algorithm is run
recursively both on original cells and on outlier cells detected at each level, which
enables it to handle rare transcriptional behaviors with high sensitivity.
MC2 introduces a new graph partitioning approach based on a “two-sided stabil-
ity score,” which quantifies how well-connected each cell is within its assigned group,
using both incoming and outgoing graph edges.
Unlike conventional modularity-
based metrics, this score penalizes asymmetrical connections, helping to ensure that
each metacell forms a tightly connected and biologically coherent unit. The algo-
rithm also implements a rare gene module detection pre-process that scans for weak
but consistent correlation patterns among rare genes, enabling early identification of
transcriptionally distinct rare cells before any graph-based partitioning occurs.
By combining recursive data decomposition, adaptive outlier detection, and rare
module pre-screening, MC2 can construct robust metacells while scaling eﬀiciently
with both computational time and memory. Notably, it avoids the use of global PCA
or full similarity matrices, allowing linear or near-linear scaling with the number of
cells. The final output of MC2 consists of high-quality metacells and a small set of
outlier cells left unassigned, which can be analyzed separately or discarded depending
on downstream objectives [Ben+22].
Manifold-regularized convex decomposition:
A distinct approach to metacell inference is introduced by SEACells [Per+23], which
formulates the problem as a convex decomposition of observed single-cell profiles into
a set of archetypal cellular states, termed ”SEACells” . Unlike graph-based or hierar-
chical methods, SEACells builds on the assumption that the transcriptomic manifold
underlying the data can be effectively spanned by a limited set of representative pro-
files. Methodologically, SEACells first embeds cells in a low-dimensional space using
techniques such as PCA or Harmony, and constructs a K-nearest neighbor (KNN)
graph to capture the manifold structure. It then selects a set of archetypal cells as
anchors and models each observed cell as a convex combination of these archetypes.
==PAGE:31==

The optimization seeks a non-negative matrix factorization under the constraint that
each cell’s weights sum to one, ensuring interpretability and probabilistic consistency.
To preserve local structure, SEACells introduces a manifold regularization term
that penalizes discrepancies between the KNN graph distances and the reconstructed
cell distances in the archetype space. This encourages similar cells to be assigned
similar combinations of archetypes, thus maintaining the geometric fidelity of the
original space. Additionally, the archetypes are optimized iteratively to refine their
positions and improve reconstruction accuracy, while the final assignment of cells to
SEACells is derived by selecting the dominant archetype in each cell’s convex mixture
[Per+23].
SEACells was shown to be effective in reducing technical noise and uncovering
stable intermediate cellular states in both transcriptomic and epigenomic datasets.
The method does not rely on prior cell-type labels and is agnostic to the sequencing
modality, allowing for broad applicability in multi-modal single-cell analyses .
Prototype/Quantization-Based Clustering (MetaQ)
MetaQ [Li+25] introduces a metacell inference framework that formulates the task
as a quantization problem, aiming to summarize large scRNA-seq datasets through
a small, representative set of prototype vectors. The core idea is to iteratively learn
these prototypes by minimizing the discrepancy between each cell and its assigned
representative in latent space. Instead of clustering cells directly, MetaQ encodes gene
expression profiles using a variational autoencoder (VAE), reducing the noise and
dimensionality of the data. Once cells are embedded into this learned latent space,
MetaQ performs prototype optimization through a modified k-means-like objective
that includes both reconstruction fidelity and entropy regularization, encouraging
confident yet balanced assignments.
MetaQ’s approach avoids reliance on pre-defined graphs or neighborhood construc-
tions, improving scalability and computational eﬀiciency, especially for very large
datasets. Additionally, it supports incremental updates and parallel training, mak-
ing it attractive for modern single-cell pipelines. The method balances computational
tractability with biological interpretability by enforcing structured, smooth prototype
assignment distributions [Li+25].
3.4.3
Comparison of Metacell Inference Frameworks
The development of metacell inference methods reflects a variety of algorithmic strate-
gies, each addressing different aspects of scalability, noise reduction, and biological
interpretability in single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) analysis.
The original
MetaCell algorithm [Bar+19] relies on a graph-based partitioning scheme, construct-
ing a balanced K-nearest neighbor graph and applying iterative refinements to group
cells into highly coherent clusters. Its strength lies in its precise handling of local cell-
to-cell similarity, making it well-suited for denoising and marker discovery, though it
can become computationally demanding at larger scales.

20
3 Relevant Research
MetaCell-2 [Ben+22] addresses this scalability issue through a divide-and-conquer
design. By recursively subdividing the dataset into smaller units (or “piles”), apply-
ing metacell inference in parallel, and refining across levels, it achieves both com-
putational eﬀiciency and robustness to rare or outlier transcriptional states. A key
innovation is the use of a “two-sided stability” score, which improves partition quality
by considering both incoming and outgoing graph connectivity.
SEACells [Per+23] diverges from graph-based methods and instead adopts a
manifold-regularized convex decomposition approach. It models each single-cell pro-
file as a convex combination of archetypal states (SEACells), selected to span the lo-

faithful to neighborhood geometry, enabling SEACells to capture subtle continuous
transitions and intermediate cell states—particularly useful in dynamic or multimodal

In contrast, MetaQ [Li+25] frames metacell inference as a quantization task in
learned latent space. By embedding cells via a variational autoencoder and assigning

Overall, these methods vary in their assumptions, data structures, and output
formats. MetaCell and MetaCell-2 prioritize local graph structure; SEACells incor-

and the desired balance between interpretability, scalability, and noise suppression.

4.1
Data Loading and Splitting Strategy
A specialized PyTorch Dataloader was used to train and evaluate the Prototype-VAE
on single-cell RNA-seq data in community-standard AnnData (.h5ad) format. The
count matrix, along with associated observation and variable metadata, is loaded in a
sparse CSR format upon initialization for memory eﬀiciency and quick per-cell access.
Integer class labels are then constructed from a user-specified label, ”cell ontology,”
so that the model can directly optimize against these indices.
In datasets with a large number of fine-grained cell types, classification perfor-
mance was reduced due to label fragmentation and limited examples per class. To ad-
dress this, a hierarchical class reduction strategy was adopted, based on the CHETAH
algorithm [Kan+19], which uses expression-based similarity to assign cells at differ-
ent levels of specificity. Following this approach, centroids were computed for each
original label by averaging the expression profiles of all associated cells, analogous to
CHETAH’s reference profiles.
Agglomerative hierarchical clustering with average linkage was then applied to
these centroids to capture global relationships between cell types. By cutting the
resulting dendrogram into a fixed number of branches (e.g., 10), transcriptionally
similar types were grouped into broader superclasses, consistent with CHETAH’s
mechanism of stopping classification at internal nodes when finer resolution is not
supported by the data.
All cells were subsequently relabeled according to their assigned superclasses, re-
ducing label space complexity while preserving underlying structure.
This hierar-
chical abstraction has been shown to improve robustness and reduce misclassifica-
tion. [Kan+19]
To address the class imbalance problem, stratified sampling is employed to parti-
tion the dataset into training (70%), validation (15%), and test (15%) subsets. As
noted by Sadaiyandi
[Sad+23], stratified sampling divides the dataset into class-
based strata and performs sampling within each stratum such that the majority and
minority classes are equally represented across all splits. This technique preserves
the diversity and structure of the original dataset, which is important for learning ro-
bust patterns when class distributions are uneven. Additionally, maintaining relative
class frequencies reduces the risk of model bias towards dominant classes and im-
proves generalization to unseen data. This approach has been shown to enhance the

1. Overall Structure: a Prototype-VAE MLP
The model is implemented as a Prototype-VAE MLP, specifically adapted to single-
cell RNA-seq data. It combines four core components from the Pantypes framework
within a variational autoencoder (VAE) that uses a multilayer perceptron (MLP). The
first component is a fully connected VAE, which learns a compact latent embedding
z from each cell’s high-dimensional gene expression vector x. An MLP architecture
is used instead of convolutional layers, as scRNA-seq data does not exhibit spatial
locality. Fully connected layers are better suited for capturing non-linear gene–gene
relationships in this setting and have shown strong performance in previous studies
such as scVI [Lop+18] and DCA [Era+19].
The second component introduces a set of P = K ×M learnable prototype vectors

j=1 in the same latent space, where K is the number of annotated cell types and

a specific class through a one-hot assignment. These prototypes act as class-specific

semantic prototypes in interpretable image models [KBC23].
The third component applies a volumetric regularization term, directly based on
the Pantypes objective. This term discourages class-specific prototypes from collaps-

inverse volume of the hyper-parallelepiped defined by the prototype vectors Φk. This
volume loss encourages the prototypes to spread out and cover the range of varia-

diversity [KBC23].

2. Encoder - Decoder
The encoder–decoder structure used in the model follows the general framework in-
troduced in Pantypes, with modifications suited to single-cell RNA-seq data. Each
component aligns with the goals and structure proposed in the original paper.
The model begins by compressing each cell’s gene expression vector x ∈RG, where
G is the number of genes, into a lower-dimensional latent representation z ∈RL using
a multilayer perceptron (MLP) variational autoencoder. The choice of an MLP, rather
than convolutional layers, is appropriate because scRNA-seq data does not possess
spatial structure. Instead, gene expression profiles are unordered features, making
fully connected layers more suitable. Previous studies such as scVI [Lop+18] and
DCA [Era+19] have also shown that MLP-based architectures are effective for this
type of data.
The encoder consists of three fully connected layers with dimensions G →1024 →
512 →2L, with ReLU activation, Batch Normalization, and Dropout (rate = 0.2)
applied to the first two layers. The output is a vector h ∈R2L, which is split into
mean µ and log-variance log Var, defining the approximate posterior qϕ(z | x) =
N(µ, diag(σ2)), where σ = exp(0.5 · log Var).
Sampling from the latent space is performed using the reparameterization trick [KW13],

2. Training Strategy
The model is trained using a structured procedure that alternates between train-
ing and evaluation phases over multiple epochs.
At the start of each phase, the
model is switched to the appropriate mode using model.train() during training
and model.eval() during validation or testing. This step is necessary for correct
functioning of certain components, such as dropout and batch normalization, which
behave differently in training (with randomness) and evaluation (deterministically).
During each epoch, the dataset is processed in mini-batches using a DataLoader.
This batching approach allows eﬀicient training on large datasets by reducing memory.
Mini-batches also enable training to proceed without exceeding hardware limitations.
The model parameters are updated using the Adam optimizer, an adaptive opti-
mization method that is commonly used for training deep learning models on high-
dimensional and noisy data like scRNA-seq. Adam combines momentum with per-
parameter learning rates, which supports faster convergence and handles sparse gra-
dients effectively. To prevent the problem of exploding gradients, which can occur in
variational models, gradient clipping with a maximum norm of 1.0 is applied before
each update step. This helps keep training stable.
To limit overfitting and unnecessary computation, early stopping is used based on
performance on the validation set. After each epoch, the model is evaluated, and if
no improvement is observed for several consecutive epochs, training is stopped. This
approach helps select a model that generalizes well to new data.
Throughout training, the process is managed through an epoch-level loop. In each
epoch, training and validation are alternated, multiple loss components are tracked,
and evaluation metrics, such as classification accuracy, AUC, and MCC, are recorded.
This setup provides a reliable and reproducible way to train the model while address-
ing the challenges of analyzing biological data.
3. Evaluation Metrics
To evaluate model performance beyond basic accuracy, several additional metrics
are used to assess both predictive reliability and the structure of the latent space.
The most straightforward metric, classification accuracy, measures the percentage of
correctly predicted cell types among all test samples. However, this measure can be
misleading when the dataset is imbalanced, as is often the case in single-cell studies
where some cell types are more common than others.
To account for class imbalance, the Matthews Correlation Coeﬀicient (MCC) is
included. This metric considers true and false positives and negatives, and provides a
balanced evaluation even when class distributions are uneven. Unlike accuracy, MCC
==PAGE:40==
28
4 Methodology
reflects performance across all classes ![Tam+25]. In this setup, MCC is reported on
a scale from 0 to 100, where higher values indicate better overall agreement between
predicted and true labels, and 0 corresponds to performance no better than random
guessing.
The model’s ability to distinguish between classes is also evaluated using the
macro-averaged Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUC-ROC).
This is computed in a one-vs-rest manner for each class. By averaging the AUC scores
equally across all classes—regardless of how many samples they contain—the macro-
AUC offers a fair and threshold-independent view of how well the model separates
cell types. This is particularly important when rare but biologically relevant classes
are present.
To assess the structure and interpretability of the latent space, two additional
techniques are used. First, UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection)
is applied to project the high-dimensional latent embeddings into two dimensions for
visualization. This allows inspection of how cells and prototypes are organized, and
whether they form meaningful groups. Second, the Davies–Bouldin Index (DBI) is
computed to quantify the quality of clustering. DBI compares the tightness of clusters
(intra-cluster distances) and their separation (inter-cluster distances). A lower DBI
score indicates better clustering structure, which supports clearer interpretation of
the model’s latent space.

Metacells creation
The original MetaCell method [Bar+19] was selected for this study due to its ability to
produce compact and homogeneous cell groupings, which are essential for a direct and
interpretable comparison with Pantypes prototypes. MetaCell employs a graph-based
procedure that includes rank-based similarity scoring, balanced K-nearest neighbor
graph construction, bootstrap resampling, and filtering of outliers and hidden subclus-
ters. This pipeline yields biologically coherent and well-separated metacells, making
them suitable for downstream tasks such as gene enrichment analysis and biomarker
discovery.
Alternative methods were considered but found less appropriate for the specific
goals of this analysis. MetaCell-2 [Ben+22] improves scalability through a recursive
divide-and-conquer design but introduces additional merging steps that reduce inter-
pretability.
SEACells [Per+23] captures smooth transitions in gene expression by
modeling each cell as a mixture of archetypal states, which is not compatible with
the one-to-one prototype matching used here. MetaQ [Li+25], which performs quan-
tization in latent space, resembles the Pantypes model in structure; using it as a
reference would compromise the independence of the comparison.
MetaCell’s regularized, discrete, and biologically grounded output made it the
most appropriate baseline for evaluating the alignment between learned prototypes
and biologically interpretable cell states.

4.5.1
Why Comparing Pantypes and Metacells is Meaningful
Even though pantypes and metacells are created in very different ways, comparing
them helps to understand how well the model reflects real biological structure.
Metacells are built without any class labels. They group similar cells together
based on their gene expression and are meant to capture the natural variation and
structure in the data. Since they average over local neighborhoods, they reduce noise
and provide a clearer view of the biological patterns within each class.
Pantypes, on the other hand, are learned by the model in a supervised way. They
are shaped by the need to separate classes and make correct predictions. While this
helps the model perform well, it does not always mean that the pantypes reflect the
full diversity of the class. Often, they are placed at the edges of clusters where the
model needs to draw boundaries.

By comparing pantypes to metacells, a simple but important question is raised:
Does the model capture the same biological variation observed in the data itself?
If pantypes spread out in a way that matches the metacells, it suggests that the
model is not only accurate—it also learns something about the real structure of the
data.
Moreover, if pantypes reach areas that metacells do not, they might even
highlight new patterns that the metacells missed.
To support this comparison, convex hulls are used to measure how much space
each method covers in the latent space. While this is not a perfect measure, as it can
include blank space, it provides a useful estimate of how well each approach represents
a class.
In short, this comparison helps to evaluate whether the model’s representations
are not only useful for classification but also meaningful from a biological point of
view.

Hyperparameter Tuning for Prototype Quality and Classification
Accuracy
For each dataset, a grid search was performed to identify the combination of loss
weights that provided both high classification accuracy and a well-structured set of
prototypes in the latent space.
The total loss function consisted of four components: cross-entropy for classi-
fication accuracy, reconstruction loss to preserve gene expression information, KL
divergence to align cells with class-specific prototypes, and volume loss to encourage
the spread of prototypes within each class. Each component was scaled by a weight
(λ), which controlled its influence during training.
It was observed that low KL divergence and volume loss weights often led to
poor prototype assignment. In such cases, the model did not suﬀiciently encourage
cells to align with their respective class prototypes, and the prototypes themselves
tended to collapse into small, overlapping regions of latent space. This resulted in
many prototypes remaining unused or redundant. Conversely, excessively high KL
and volume weights caused instability during training and, in some cases, reduced
classification accuracy, as the model overemphasized latent space geometry at the
expense of predictive performance.
The cross-entropy and reconstruction weights also played important roles in de-
termining model behavior. A low cross-entropy weight led to weaker classification
performance, as the classifier head did not receive enough signal to differentiate be-
tween cell types. On the other hand, a high cross-entropy weight, if not balanced
by suﬀicient volume regularization, caused the prototypes to collapse into compact
clusters, reducing diversity. Similarly, a very low reconstruction weight resulted in
poor recovery of gene expression, while a very high weight forced the model to focus
on input reconstruction, often at the cost of clear separation between classes in latent
space.

Attempted Prototype Elimination
In the original Pantypes framework, prototypes that do not receive high similarity
from any training samples—so-called “out-of-distribution” (OOD) prototypes—can
be removed during or after training. The goal of this elimination is to reduce redun-
dancy and improve interpretability by keeping only the prototypes that are actively
used to represent cells.
To explore this idea, a prototype elimination function was implemented. This func-
tion removed prototypes that were never the most similar (i.e., never assigned maxi-
mum similarity) for any training cell. It updated all relevant parts of the model, in-
cluding the prototype vectors, class identity mappings, and classification layer weights.
The optimizer was also rebuilt to reflect the updated parameter set.
However, when this elimination was applied in practice, it caused significant issues
in the final latent space structure. In particular, the UMAP visualizations showed
that prototypes from different classes became scattered randomly across the latent
space, with no clear alignment to their original cell types. Many of the remaining
prototypes no longer matched the structure of the training data, and the overall
layout lost its interpretability.
These problems were likely caused by the sudden removal of parameters mid-
training and the changes it introduced to the balance between prototypes and their
assigned classes.
Rebuilding the model’s internal structures and optimizer during
training may have led to inconsistencies in gradients and parameter updates, resulting
in poor final representations.
Due to these complications and the loss of structure observed after elimination,
prototype pruning was not used in the final experiments. Instead, a fixed number of
prototypes per class was chosen in advance based on the training data distribution.

6.1
Conclusion
The goal of this work was to adapt and apply a prototype-based deep learning model
to single-cell RNA-seq data in a way that allows both accurate classification and
interpretable structure. By combining a variational autoencoder with class-specific
prototypes—called pantypes—the authors aimed to create a model that not only
predicts cell types correctly but also reflects the internal diversity and structure of
each class.
Across all three datasets—Heart, Skin, and Eye—the model achieved strong per-
formance. Classification metrics such as accuracy, MCC, and AUC were consistently
high, showing that the model could learn to separate cell types effectively, even when
classes were imbalanced or small. The training process was stable, with no signs of
overfitting, and the loss components behaved as expected. This confirms that the
combined learning of reconstruction, prototype alignment, and volume regularization
worked well in practice.
However, good performance does not always mean that the model’s internal struc-
ture is meaningful. To better understand what the model was learning, the authors
compared its pantypes to metacells, which are data-driven groupings created without
labels. These serve as a useful baseline because they reflect how cells naturally cluster
based on gene expression.
In all datasets, they found that pantypes tended to appear at the edges of each
class in the latent space, while metacells stayed near the center of dense regions. This
is expected: pantypes are learned through a classification objective, which pushes
them toward boundaries to improve separation, while metacells come from local av-
eraging and reflect core regions of the data.
Convex hull analysis confirmed these differences. Real sampled cells near pantypes
usually covered the class structure best, even when only a small subset was sampled.
Metacells came second, offering moderate coverage of the latent space.
Pantypes
alone had the lowest coverage, often because they were placed in isolated or narrow
parts of the class. This limited their ability to represent the full shape and variation
within a class, especially when the class was large or curved.
These results show that while the model learns to classify cells well, its internal
representation may not fully capture all aspects of biological variation. Some classes—
like the long, curved Class 1 in Heart or Class 0 in Eye—were only partially covered
==PAGE:70==
58
6 Discussion
by pantypes, with dense central regions left underrepresented. In smaller or scattered
classes, both pantypes and metacells sometimes failed to represent the full shape,
either due to too few prototypes or poor placement.
A key limitation of the model is the way prototypes are allocated and updated.
Although the number of prototypes per class was adjusted based on class size, this
alone was not enough to ensure good coverage. In some cases, too many prototypes
were placed in the same area, leading to redundancy, while other regions were missed.
The log-ratio similarity function and volume loss helped spread prototypes to some
extent, but this was not always suﬀicient to match the full diversity seen in the data.
While building and tuning the model, the authors also faced several practical lim-
itations. The original Pantypes method includes a prototype elimination mechanism,
which removes prototypes that are not selected as the closest to any cell. However,
in this case, enabling this feature led to instability in the latent space—prototypes
were eliminated too early or too aggressively, which resulted in uneven allocation and
poor UMAP structure at the end of training. Some classes were left underrepresented,
while others had prototypes scattered in irrelevant areas. As a result, the authors dis-
abled prototype pruning and relied on a fixed allocation based on class size. Another
challenge involved the reconstruction loss. Since scRNA-seq data are count-based and
often sparse, they initially attempted to use a Negative Binomial (NB) loss, as done in
models like scVI. However, when the NB loss was applied, training became unstable.
The KL divergence and reconstruction loss fluctuated strongly, and the model failed
to converge. These spikes were likely due to the diﬀiculty of estimating the dispersion
parameters and the interaction between NB noise and the prototype regularization.
To maintain training stability, the authors chose to use Mean Squared Error (MSE)
as the reconstruction loss, even though it does not model the data distribution as
accurately. These trade-offs highlight the diﬀiculty of balancing model complexity,
interpretability, and robustness in real-world datasets.
In summary, the prototype-VAE model provides a useful way to combine classifi-
cation with interpretable structure. It performs well on standard metrics and shows
meaningful organization in the latent space. However, its ability to fully reflect bi-
ological variation remains limited, especially in large or complex classes.
Future
improvements could focus on better prototype placement, adaptive pruning, or using
hybrid strategies that combine label-based and data-driven representations.
6.2
Future Directions and Potential Improvements
Although the proposed model performed well overall, there are several directions
for improvement and further exploration. These involve both model design and the
potential to extract more biological meaning from the results.
==PAGE:71==
6.2 Future Directions and Potential Improvements
59
Prototype Elimination for Smarter Allocation
In the present work, prototypes were assigned to each class based on the number of
training examples, using a fixed formula. While this worked well in many cases, it
also led to situations where prototypes were either too many and clustered in the
same area (as in Class 0 of the Eye dataset) or too few to cover the full structure
of the class (as in Classes 1 and 2 of Skin and Eye). The original Pantypes paper
suggests a dynamic pruning strategy: starting with more prototypes and removing
those that are not useful. A prototype is considered “unused” if no cell is most similar
to it during training. By eliminating such prototypes, the model can free up capacity
and focus on prototypes that better reflect the data.
This could lead to a more
even and meaningful distribution of prototypes across the latent space and help avoid
redundancy, especially in large or simple classes where too many similar prototypes
are placed close together.
Switching to Negative Binomial Loss
The model currently uses Mean Squared Error (MSE) for reconstructing gene expres-
sion, primarily because it provides stable training. However, MSE is not an ideal
match for single-cell RNA data, which consists of count values with high variability.
The Negative Binomial distribution is better suited for modeling this kind of data
because it accounts for both the average expression and the overdispersion commonly
seen in scRNA-seq. Models like scVI and DCA use this loss to better capture the noise
and signal present in real datasets. Adopting a Negative Binomial reconstruction loss
in future work could improve how well the model learns from the data, especially
for lowly expressed genes or rare cells that are not well reconstructed under MSE.
A more realistic loss function could also improve classification and structure in the
latent space.
Using Pantypes Components in Other VAE Models
Instead of building a model from scratch, future work could explore applying Pantypes
ideas—such as the prototype layer, KL loss to prototypes, and volume regularization—
on top of more advanced existing models such as scVI, DCA, or DREAM. These
models already have specialized mechanisms for handling technical noise, dropout
events, or batch effects in scRNA-seq data. For example, scVI uses a hierarchical prior
and learned dispersion terms, while DREAM uses Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs)
in the latent space. Integrating Pantypes into these frameworks may combine the
benefits of advanced modeling (e.g., better denoising and latent structure) with the
interpretability provided by prototypes. This would allow for better classification
performance while still providing a clear, structured view of how different cell types
are represented in the model.

60
6 Discussion
Improving Prototype Coverage and Placement
The convex hull analysis showed that prototypes often fail to cover the full shape
of a class, especially when the class has a curved or wide structure.
In contrast,
sampled cells from the prototype neighborhoods covered much more space.
One
future improvement would be to design a mechanism that not only prunes unused
prototypes but also adjusts their placement based on the actual shape of the class
in the latent space. For example, prototypes could be encouraged to align with key
directions of variation (e.g., using PCA-like axes within each class) or even ensure
that prototypes cover distinct regions of the class distribution. This would potentially
make the representation more complete and help reveal internal diversity in large or
complex cell types.
Discovering Rare or Novel Cell Subpopulations
One of the main goals of using interpretable models in single-cell analysis is to uncover
meaningful biological patterns. In many datasets, rare cell types or transitional states
are diﬀicult to detect using standard clustering or differential expression techniques,
especially if they only appear in a small number of cells. The Pantypes framework
is designed to cover the space using diverse prototype vectors. This opens up the
opportunity to look at regions of the latent space that are only reached by a small
number of cells or are far from the core of common classes. In future work, these
edge areas could be examined more closely to see if they contain unknown or rare cell
types. By combining this with domain knowledge or additional experimental data,
it may be possible to identify previously unrecognized subpopulations or cell states
that are biologically meaningful.
