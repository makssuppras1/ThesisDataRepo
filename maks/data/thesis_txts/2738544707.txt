Abstract
This MSc thesis presents the design, modeling, and fabrication of a Permanent Magnet
Synchronous Generator (PMSG) tailored for small wind turbine applications, developed
specifically for the International Small Wind Turbine Contest (ISTWC) ­ 2024 edition for
turbines developed at DTU. The project includes the development of a generator test
bench at the Power Lab to evaluate three machines from previous iterations, with the aim
of improving on past designs and maximizing machine efficiency of new designs.
The research begins with a comprehensive review of the principles of wind energy con­
version, Maxwell’s equations and rotating electrical machinery design. The design phase
presents the drive­train design and the system specifications of the small­scale wind tur­
bine and employs an analytical approach for preliminary machine design, followed by the
use of COMSOL for detailed electro­magnetic Finite Element Analysis (FEA) to refine the
design parameters. The design is mainly carried out to improve an axial flux machine
design from previous iterations. A novel axial flux machine design with a rotating core
back is studied in this design process. In addition, a radial flux machine is also designed
for comparison of performance. The fabrication is only extended to the axial flux machine
coil design due to cost and fabrication time constraints.
The CAD models for machine modifications and special tools to produce hand­made struc­
tured high fill­factor copper winding coils are created are presented. The thesis concludes
with a critical discussion on the implications of the findings for future small wind turbine
designs and potential avenues for improvement. This research makes a significant con­
tribution to the field by providing a detailed methodology for developing high­performance
PMSGs, offering valuable insights to enhance the efficiency of small wind turbine gener­
ators.
Keywords: Axial Flux Permanent Magnet Synchronous Generator, Small Wind Turbine,
Wind Energy Conversion, Electromagnetic Simulation, Finite Element Analysis, Renew­
able Energy.

1
Introduction
This MSc thesis report outlines the design and fabrication of an Axial Flux Permanent
Magnet Synchronous Generator (AFPMSG) specifically designed to be utilized for direct­
drive small wind turbines at the International Small Wind Turbine Contest. In addition to
the design of the machine, a test bench is developed to measure the characteristics of
machines utilized during previous iterations of the contest.
1.1
Background
The International Small Wind Turbine Contest (ISWTC) is an yearly contest held in the
Netherlands. The objective of the contest is to design and build small wind turbines of
the highest possible turbine power coefficient Cp with highest possible AEP. The depart­
ment of Wind Energy and Power Systems at DTU develops and tests small wind turbines
specifically for the contest. At present DTU holds the record for the highest Cp measured
at 0.423 in the year 2019. A general system schematic is shown in figure .
Although the overall system power coefficient is known to the DTU team, it has been diffi­
cult to discern the aerodynamic turbine efficiency and the generator efficiency separately
due to lack of reliable measurements on the generators developed during previous con­
test iterations. Furthermore, the wind tunnel available at DTU at present is only capable
of going up to 5 m/s wind speeds, which is insufficient to measure turbine aerodynamic
characteristics. This has led to a need for measurements on the existing generators that
is performed as a part of this thesis.
The power ratings of the generators have been revised by ISWTC from previous iterations.
Previous iterations of the contest had required machines of lower power ratings at 700
W. However, since the 2023 iteration, the power rating for the generator has increased
to 1200 W, at 60V 20A DC requiring revisions to the generator design. Apart from this,
previous generators were developed for different turbines with different Tip Speed Ratios
(TSR). In addition, one of the axial flux machines developed in the 2022 iteration has not
performed as per expectations [1]. Hence, a troubleshoot of the axial flux machine and
new machine design is performed for the current iterations of the contest.
1.2
Significance
The significance of the work presented in this thesis is of direct benefit to the DTU wind
turbine building team. The test bench performance measurements for existing machines
provides the team with the information on the aerodynamic efficiency of the rotor, gener­
ator efficiency and rectifier losses. Furthermore, the test bench can be re­built again to
reliably test new generators in the future.
The AFPMSG design process presented in this thesis explores a novel unconventional
design driven by the cost constraints of the design process. The design explored in this
thesis would be a useful contribution to literature in the context of high efficiency, high
speed axial flux generators and motors. There are still significant challenges in man­
ufacturing coils for axial flux machines. This aspect of manufacturing coils is another

From a general point of view, the work presented is relevant in the following aspects:
• Advancement of small wind turbine technology: PMSGs are known for their high

2
Literature Review
This thesis deals with small wind turbines and design of Permanent magnet synchronous
generators. Relevant literature for these specific topics of interest is covered in the sec­
tions below. The mathematical relations and models are covered in the sources and some
equations are given in the appendix. Direct­drive PMSG are of specific interest due to the
ability to provide high efficiency mechanical to electrical energy conversion. Elimination of
the gearbox improves the system efficiency in practical scenarios as an additional energy
loss inducing process through mechanical conversion is removed [2, 3].
2.1
Wind Energy Conversion system
It is essential to understand the principles behind aerodynamic energy conversion in wind
turbine rotors to determine the design requirements of the direct­drive PMSG that is me­
chanically coupled to the rotor. Concepts such as power coefficient Cp, Tip Speed Ratio
TSR, cut­in speed, rated speed, Annual Energy Production, aerodynamic power, Betz
limit are discussed in sources [4] [5].
Figure 2.1: Wind Turbine drive train utilized at ISWTC
The design requirements of the PMSG are determined from turbine parameters and rela­
tions such as turbine TSR, rated power, expected Cp of the turbine to determine the rota­
tional speed and mechanical torque of the PMSG at the rated power. It is also possible to
include additional energy conversion efficiency reductions in the power computation. This
is relevant as it can account for rectifier losses in the system that can prevent the PMSG
from reaching required outputs in practical operation. The wind energy drive­train utilized
at the ISWTC is shown in figure 2.1. Detailed system specs are covered in subsequent
chapters.

2.2
Electrical Machines, operation and configurations
Rotating electrical machines are devices used to convert electrical to mechanical energy
(motors) and vice versa (generators). This conversion process generally takes place
through a magnetic field. Although the operation is considered to be opposite process,
there are significant differences in the physical principles governing the operation process
in motors and generators. This idea is very important as it affects the design of the ma­
chine presented in this thesis.
In the case of motors, the attractive and repulsive forces between poles of magnets are
utilized to generate the torque in the machine. There are axial forces induced in motor
operation. However, in generators, its electromagnetic induction described by Maxwells
equations that contributes to the generation of EMF in the windings of machines. There
are very minimal axial forces induced in the windings. Furthermore, as described by
Lenz’s law[6][7][8], there are currents induced in the windings such that they result in a
torque that opposes the rotation of rotor in the generator.
Based on the direction of magnetic fields in a machine, machines are divided into ra­
dial flux, axial flux and transverse flux machines. The direction of magnetic flux enables
different design possibilities with different features applicable to a variety of applications.

2.3
Constructional features and topologies of machines
General constructional components of PMSG are as follows:
• Stator or the stationary section: Consists of core made of laminated silicon steel to
minimize eddy current losses. Contains the Copper windings that are placed in the
stator slots to carry the electrical current.
• Rotor or rotating section: Consists of high­strength permanent magnets (such as
NdFeB) are mounted on or embedded in the rotor. Supported by rotor core typically

2.4
Winding Layouts, slot­pole combinations and harmonics
There are many possible ways to arrange coils in electrical machines. The arrangement
determines determines the output characteristics of the machine. The choice of winding
arrangement directly determines construction features of the machine. In PMSG the type
of windings include integral slot, distributed fractional slot, concentrated fractional slot and
can have variations in number of layers in each slot[6][8][13].
The exact implementation of these winding types depend on the number of slots and poles
chosen to be used in the machine. This slot­pole combination also determines how ef­
fective a machine winding will be at producing maximum EMF given by the winding factor
kw [12].
Winding types have different quantities of components called harmonics induced in the
EMF. These are components that deviate the output waveform from a purely sinusoidal
waveform at a particular fundamental frequency that is expected from a machine. Har­
monics are important when systems are used in conjunction with the grid. This is because
appliances connected to the grid are designed to operate in low AC system harmonics. In
designs presented in this thesis, it is of less concern due to the conversion requirement to
DC. Harmonics do play a role in increased losses in machines that have a core material
supporting the magnetic flux and the windings. The computations for winding factor and
exact physical arrangement for windings, rotational speed, output fundamental frequency
can be found in detail in sources [6, 8].
There are some rules to follow when choosing slot­pole combinations for 3 phase systems
for best outputs. Slots should be a multiple of the number of phases in the system. Larger
machines can accommodate larger number of slots, which can lead to more efficient con­
struction of the machine with smaller end winding leading to low copper loss. In addition,
larger slots to pole ratios can lead to a drastic minimization in harmonic components in the
output. Machines with smaller coil span numbers have smaller end winding lengths and
have reduced copper loss from end windings. Slot­pole combinations can also determine
how the forces in the machine will be distributed. Selecting machines with no periodicity
in the windings arrangement can lead to unbalanced forces in the rotor. This is to be
avoided to reduce mechanical instabilities and bearing losses.
Packing the rotor with low space between magnets can reduce overall size of the ma­
chine and reduce number of turns required in coils as well. This can further reduce cop­
per losses. Shorter length machines with smaller cores generally have much lower core
losses. Machines that operate at lower rotational speeds will be larger that machines op­
erating at higher speeds for the same power rating. More number of poles imply that the
fundamental output frequency of the machine will be large and the core losses scale up
with frequency. This is not a problem in air­core or core­less machines. Air­gap variations
do affect the output characteristics of the machine. However, the air gap length must be
balanced correctly to improve ease of construction while maximizing output power.
A detailed comparison between winding types and their output characteristics are pro­
vided in [8]. There is an online calculator that can assist in looking into various slot­pole
combinations, their winding types, their physical arrangement, and expected harmonics

2.5
Machine loss reduction and efficiency improvement
PMSG machine losses can be generally divided as follows:
• Eddy current loss: These losses occur due to the circulating currents induced in
the core material by the alternating magnetic field. Eddy currents generate heat,
leading to power dissipation. They are minimized by using laminated core materials
with insulation between layers to restrict the paths of these currents.
• Hysteresis Loss: These losses occur due to the lagging of the magnetic domains
in the core material behind the applied magnetic field. Each cycle of magnetization
causes some energy to be lost as heat. Hysteresis loss depends on the material
properties of the core and the frequency of the magnetic field.
• Copper loss: occur due to the resistance of the windings through which the current
flows. The resistance causes heating and power dissipation. Copper loss increases
with the square of the current and is a significant contributor to the overall losses in
the machine.
• Windage and friction loss: Windage loss occurs due to air friction on the rotating
parts of the machine. This is particularly significant at high speeds. Friction loss
occurs in the bearings and other moving parts, leading to mechanical energy dissi­
pation as heat.
• Temperature Rise: This is a consequence of the various losses (copper, core, and
mechanical) leading to heat generation within the machine. Effective cooling mech­
anisms are essential to manage the temperature rise and prevent damage or reduc­
tion in efficiency.
• Stray Loss: Stray losses are due to leakage fluxes that do not contribute to the
useful magnetic flux but induce currents in various conductive parts of the machine,
causing additional losses. These can arise from imperfections in the magnetic circuit
in construction.
The mathematical relations for calculation of losses in the machine are provided in the
appendix. Minimizing or removing these losses directly improve the efficiency of the ma­
chine. Applying these ideas to minimize losses to machine design process enables design
of high efficiency machines.

The theoretical design and validation is performed on COMSOL using FEA methods. The
objective of this design process is to obtain the parameters of the AFPMSG that would
provide the highest efficiency possible while having the capability to reach the design
requirements. The following aspects of the AFPMSG are of concern in a general design

• Dimensions and material magnetic properties of the machine stator core and rotor
core
• Air gap between magnets, coils and core.
• Copper and number of turns
• Amount of current flowing through the copper coils given by coil current density
• Magnets and magnetic flux density, recoil permeability
In this thesis, the rotor is to be reused and new magnets can be purchased with the same
dimensions of that embedded in the rotor. Based on these conditions, there is no practical
need to perform any magnet thickness simulations. So most magnetic parameters have
been provided.
In addition, an air gap variation study is also not required. This is because practically
maintaining a 1.5 mm air gap between coil and magnet is necessary from a physics and
manufacturing perspective. Increasing this air gap leads to a drastic decrease in output
as the machine is an air­core machine in which the magnetic flux density is diffuse and its
influence decreases with distance. Hence, maintaining this strict 1.5 mm air gap between
coil and the magnets is necessary. There are other concerns regarding air gap that can
only be explained when the construction of the coil is discussed.
Due to this most of the modelling will be about determining the axial length, number of
turns, losses and efficiency.

Initial simulations did not consider aspects construction aspects of the coils. This was not

winding. These can produce results close to theoretical computed copper fill factor values.
There are different methods to build the coils. Some of the methods in increasing order
of complexity are listed below.
• Using rectangular wires: This is the most efficient method to wind coils for these
types of machines. However, wires are not produced for these types of coils for
these small scale systems. Hence, this option is generally out of the question in
terms of implementation
• Using circular wires: These are easily available. This is the method considered
in this thesis. There are problems in construction of the coils that are highlighted
below.
• Using copper strips or plates: it is possible to cut out spirals from plates using special
knives. However, problems will arise when insulation has to be applied to the strips
and maintaining the uniformity in the insulation can be extremely difficult. This same
problem is solved by using rectangular wiring. Most insulating materials will wear
off the copper if too much pressure is applied to the wire or copper strips.

ditional layer of copper jetting out of the center. This can be minimized best through
rectangular wires. Stranded wires are also a good solution to minimize the extra layer.
However, too many strands can lead to excessive soldering and insulation removal during
winding.

7
Results and Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis was to measure the performance characteristics of existing
generators and to design, fabricate and test an AFPMSG for a small wind turbine for the
ISWTC from DTU.
A test rig was developed at PowerLab DK at DTU to conduct measurements on the perfor­
mance characteristics of the machines that had been developed during previous iterations
of the ISWTC. It was determined that the HAWT radial flux PMSG has a peak DC effi­
ciency of 79% while the large radial flux machine used for the VAWT has a DC efficiency
of 70% . Although this implies that the VAWT RFPMSG could be improved, it would only
improve the power coefficient of the VAWT by a small amount from 0.19 to 0.23.
Design requirements were determined for a new AFPMSG from ISWTC regulations and
electrical machine design possibilities. The problems with the existing AFPMSG are ex­
amined. Various unconventional stator core designs for AFPMSG were explored but were
found to be not feasible due to cost constraints. Hence, two toplogies of air­core AFPMSG
were then explored. Many designs and design variants were modelled on COMSOL. It is
observed that higher TSR designs produce more power more efficiently. A detailed as­
sessment of coil geometry requirements is presented, as it can affect the overall design of
machine drastically. These requiremnts are included into the COMSOL models and the
final results of the different machine variants are presented. It can be observed that ma­
chine efficiencies of above 94% can be achieved from the machine under tight tolerances.
CAD models for the mechanical design of the AFPMSG are developed. Then a lathe
based coil winding process is explored and pilot coils are wound for the AFPMSG. It is to
be noted that improvements to the coil winding process are necessary but is found to be
feasible.

A.4
Performance calculation
A.4.1
Copper loss
Copper losses, occur due to the resistance of the conductors through which current flows.
These losses are proportional to the square of the current and the resistance of the wind­
ing. In both the stator and rotor windings, electrical losses can be significant, especially
in machines operating at high currents.

A.4.2
Eddy current loss
Induced currents in the core material generate heat and contribute to energy loss. These
are proportional to the square of the frequency and the thickness of the core laminations.
A.4.3
Hysteresis loss
Caused by the lag between the magnetization and the magnetic field in the core material.
They depend on the frequency of the magnetic field and the properties of the core material.
A.4.4
Mechanical loss
Mechanical losses in AFPMSGs are crucial to consider as they directly impact the overall
efficiency and performance of the generator. These losses can be categorized into several
types, each arising from different mechanical aspects of the machine’s operation. These
are not included in the COMSOL model, but important for practical implementation.
• Friction Losses: Bearings: Friction in the bearings supporting the rotor and stator
leads to energy dissipation. High­quality bearings and proper lubrication can mini­
mize these losses.
• Windage Losses: Air Resistance: As the rotor spins, it encounters resistance from
the surrounding air (or cooling medium), leading to energy loss. This is particularly
relevant at high rotational speeds. If cooling fans are used to dissipate heat, the
energy used to drive these fans also counts as a mechanical loss.
• Structural Vibrations: Rotor Imbalance: Any imbalance in the rotor can cause vi­
brations, leading to additional frictional losses and potential mechanical wear. Mis­
alignment of the stator can also result in increased friction and mechanical stress.

  MBC51-20-20-A
Ruland MBC51-20-20-A, 20mm x 20mm Bellows Coupling, Aluminum, Clamp Style,
50.8mm OD, 58.7mm Length
 
  Description
Ruland MBC51-20-20-A is a bellows coupling with 20mm x 20mm bores, 50.8mm OD, and 58.7mm length. It is zero-backlash and has a balanced design for
reduced vibration at high speeds. MBC51-20-20-A is comprised of two anodized aluminum hubs and a stainless steel bellows. The bellows are able to flex
while remaining rigid under torsional loads allowing for all types of misalignment to be accommodated. This bellows coupling is lightweight and has low
inertia making it suitable for applications with speeds up to 10,000 RPM. Hardware is metric and tests beyond DIN 912 12.9 standards for maximum torque
capabilities. Ruland manufactures MBC51-20-20-A to be torsionally rigid and an excellent fit for precise positioning stepper servo applications as well as
encoders. It is machined from solid bar stock that is sourced exclusively from North American mills and RoHS2 and REACH compliant. MBC51-20-20-A is
carefully manufactured in our Marlborough, MA factory under strict controls using proprietary processes.
