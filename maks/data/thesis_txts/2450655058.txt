Abstract
The aim of this report is to analyze and improve the indoor climate conditions of the dor-
mitory Apisseq in Sisimiut, as well as reduce the use of energy from its heating, ventilation
and air conditioning (HVAC) systems and check if those perform properly.
In order to achieve that, diﬀerent sensors were used to gather data during March and April.
Information provided by the integrated sensors in the building was also considered, as well
as a survey among the occupants to have a better understanding of their actions while
reacting to the environment conditions. From this analyze , diﬀerent issues were identiﬁed,
as overheating, excess of energy consumption or CO2 concentration levels above the limits
compared to what was expected.
Based on those results, diﬀerent improvements are proposed. To achieve better results,
authors focused on the ventilation and heating systems by modifying some of their
characteristics or replacing some components. The IDA ICE software was used to simulate
those improvements in the building. Therefore, diﬀerent models were created, achieving
energy savings up to 20 %.

1.1
Background - Extreme cold climates
Sisimiut, formerly known by its colonial name Holsteinsborg, is the capital and largest
city of the Qeqqata municipality, and the second-largest city in Greenland. It is located
on the east coast of Greenland, approximately 320 km north of Nuuk. Today, Sisimiut
is the largest business center north of the capital of Nuuk and population is stabilizing
mostly with migrants coming from smaller settlements. Fishing is the principal industry in
Sisimiut, although the town has a growing industrial base. Several professional and general
schools are based in Sisimiut, providing education to the inhabitants of the city. The
new Taseralik Culture Center is the second cultural center to be established in Greenland,
after Katuaq in Nuuk. Furthermore, a project to build a road between Kangerlussuaq
(international airport) and Sisimiut is under discussion. If this happen one day, it could
results in a quick expansion of the city as per Greenland is mainly dependant of Danish
resources.
From the 60’s, Danish have provided new construction materials and methods. It led to
the construction of apartment block building contrasting with traditional colored Inuits’
houses and raised cold resistance materials issues. Ventilation systems are mostly imported
fro Denmark and therefore are not designed to perform under Arctic conditions. This often
cause an over consumption and increase electricity and heating bills of households.

1.3
Apisseq student dormitory
In this thesis, the dormitory called "Apisseq" located in Sisimiut will be studied . The
building has been built in 2010 to accommodate the students from ARTEK school, equipped
with new materials and technology for Greenland such as solar panels. Sensors have been
placed during construction is several room but also on solar heating system, ventilation
system, heating system [1] in order to asses regularly energy consumption and validate or
not equipment eﬃcient under extreme weather.
An analysis of the building will be performed in order to identify and conﬁrm possible
issues that have been found in previous studies. Then, diﬀerent improvement proposals
will be made by using IDA ICE software in order to achieve less energy consumption and
better indoor conditions for the occupants.

Apisseq - Student Dormitory
Apisseq is an energy eﬃcient dormitory built in 2010 to accommodate about 40 engineering
students in Sisimiut, Greenland.
The building aims to test the low energy technologies used in European countries (Den-
mark) in an Arctic environment, where the weather conditions are much more extreme.
Therefore, by using those technologies, it was expected to reduce the energy consumption
of Apisseq compared to traditional Greenlandic buildings as well as achieving a good indoor
environment for the occupants.
The Technical University of Denmark (DTU) participated in the project by donating
DKK 500,000 for the monitoring system [2]. This system measures all important aspects
regarding energy consumption and generation, indoor air quality (IAQ), outdoor conditions
and conditions inside the constructions.
The dormitory was designed having in mind the speciﬁc parameters to be met on Category
II of the DS/EN 15251 [3] and Category B of the ISO 7730 [4] for the ventilation, heating
and indoor conditions. Nevertheless, the main rules to be followed are the ones speciﬁed in
the Greenlandic building regulation [5].
2.1
Space Distribution
The building has a circular shape and is comprised of 3 ﬂoors, of which only the two upper
ﬂoors and part of the ground ﬂoor are heated.
The 37 apartments of the dormitory are located in the ﬁrst and second ﬂoors. There are
20 apartments in the second ﬂoor, while in the ﬁrst ﬂoor there are only 17, as the middle
ones have been replaced with common areas includingg a small kitchen, and a laundry
room. In front of those there is the glazed atrium where the indoor stairs that connect the
diﬀerent ﬂoors are placed.
In the ground level there are two separate rooms for the ventilation heat exchangers, one
technical room, the janitor’s oﬃce and an entrance from the other side of the building as
well as small storage compartments (one for each apartment). Only the janitor’s oﬃce and
technical room are heated in the ground ﬂoor.
As it can be seen in Figure 2.1, there is a total of 37 rooms. Those rooms are then
divided into 33 single rooms, 3 three room apartments and 1 double room apartment for
handicapped people.

2.3
HVAC Systems
Apisseq dormitory is heated by two diﬀerent means. On one side, the bathroom and
entrance of each room have a ﬂoor heating system that provides heat continuously during
the whole year. Due to this fact, overheating issues have been detected, mostly during the
warmer months of the year. Additionally, this kind of strategy use a lot of unnecessary
energy, as sometimes the rooms doesn’t need to be heated at all. On the other side,
radiators are used in the bedrooms and living rooms, providing a certain degree of control
to the occupant.
Both systems use the district heating supply network as a main source of heat, following
the 70/30ºC concept. Nevertheless, the existing thermal solar system integrated in the
building is used to provide some additional heat, and therefore, making possible to save
energy during periods with suﬃcient solar radiation.
Regarding the ventilation system, two ventilation units Exhausto VEX 160 are used, which
technical information can be seen in the Annex G.2. Each of the units is equipped with a
heat recovery system and electric heating coils that ensure both, to provide the desired
temperature and avoid frost.
The Exhausto heat exchangers used are cross ﬂow plate exchangers. In these type of
exchangers the air channels are arranged so that the airﬂows cross over one another, as it
can be seen Figure 2.2.
6
==PAGE:15==
2. Apisseq - Student Dormitory
Figure 2.2. Air-to-air cross ﬂow heat exchanger [7]
That way, the exhaust warm air returning from apartments transfers energy to the supply
outside cold air coming, which is therefore heated to an optimal point where it can be
supplied to the rooms as fresh clean air.
Since the building started operating in 2010, diﬀerent issues had been detected regarding
the ventilation system.
The ventilation units are set to provide a constant air ﬂow. Therefore, there is no diﬀerence
of air ﬂow depending on the occupancy of the rooms at each moment, which means that
additional energy is consumed without necessity in order to provide a constant air ﬂow of
300 l/s per unit [2]. The system is also overdimensioned, as its capacity is way higher than
the actual use.
Additionally, some issues related with snow have been experienced during these years of
operation.
During winter time, blizzards often occur. The snow together with high velocity winds
cause that snow gets into the ventilation system as the intake on the facade has not been
protected properly [2]. Thus, during prolonged precipitation, snow can accumulate in the
outdoor supply side of the exchanger, blocking the system and causing malfunction.
Both ventilation units are equipped with a de-icing by-pass. When there is a risk of icing,
then a certain amount of air will by-pass the heat exchanger and go directly to the point
after the heating coil. Thus, the demand after the heating coil output increases. But even
though the demand increases, a costly preheating coil is avoided by using this method.
As it can be seen in Figure 2.3, the by-pass system does not work properly as frost
was spotted this winter. The photograph was taken a specially cold day with external
temperatures around -22ºC and high velocity wind.
It seems that the damper on the by-pass does not take any action even if there is frost
formation as seen in the image. This can be due to a wrong setup of the device. A proper
way to control the damper would be to take into account the pressure loss of the heat
exchanger in combination with the outdoor temperature.

2.4
Solar energy
Apisseq has a total of 38 evacuated tubular collectors of 24 tubes each installed in the roof.
The model of collectors used is the ZENIT-A1-24-2 Artic from the company ExoHeat [6].
They were grouped in three areas, East, West and South, as it can be seen in the Annex.
The expected eﬃciency of the collectors is 71.2 %, with an expected annual energy
generation around 400 kWh/(m2 · a) [2]. The solar system includes two water tanks of
2,000 litres each connected in parallel to store the heated water that will later be used in
the heating system of the building. The tanks are also used as heat peak reducers.
The solar system normal operating ﬂow is between 400-1700 l/h, which use as special
water-Glycol mix that can operate at temperatures from -30 to 270 ºC [6].
The system is supplied with an extra 3-vay changeover valve and a mirror circulating pump
in order to avoid freezing damages.
Nevertheless, important issues were detected regarding the proper operation of the solar
system since its installation.
The solar system was malfunctioning for many years due to lack of maintenance. Diﬀerent
causes like a burnt fuse, malfunctioning sensors or no water pumped into the collectors led
to the malfunction of the system. Thus, one of the main issue is the lack of qualiﬁed staﬀ
in the region. There is not much people trained to perform the maintenance of this kind
of heating systems in Greenland, as this kind of solar technologies have been imported
from Europe during the last years.
To give speciﬁc examples of the system malfunction, in April 2018, the system has been
checked in the technical room. The temperature at the surface of the collectors was 140
ºC, but no water was ﬂowing in the system due to a burnt fuse.
In March 2019, while checking the system with professor Martin Kotol, we realized that the
temperature was too diﬀerent in one of the collector areas (around 85 ºC). This was due
to the fact that there was excess of air in the pipes, which had to be ﬁxed by adjusting the
air release valve manually. Therefore, the system was malfunctioning before that, which
could had been avoided by providing a proper maintenance service.
One part of this thesis was to check if the solar system performed correctly. Once the

2.5
Estimated consumption and production
The estimated annual heat demand for space heating of the building was 160 MWh/yr,
and 80 MWh/yr for Domestic Hot Water (DHW), which is a total consumption of 240
MWh/yr.
During the ﬁrst year of operation, the energy consumption of the building was studied
through the information provided by the sensors placed in Apisseq.
Main problems to analyze were ice formation inside heat exchangers, poor air-tightness of
the building and over-dimensioned ventilation units, which could lead to a high energy
consumption.
While comparing the expected energy consumption with the one obtained during that
period, it was found that even though the consumption was lower than a traditional
Greenlandic building, it was still 26.5 % higher than expected (214.7 kWh/m2 · yr versus
169.7 kWh/m2 · yr) [8]. Therefore, some improvements will be proposed in this thesis in
order to reduce the energy consumption of the building.
To have a better understanding of the consumption of the dormitory, data from sensor 124
was checked, which shows the district heating total energy consumption in kWh. Sensor

In order to gather information about Apisseq and perform a detailed analysis of the thermal

3.1
Monitoring System and Sensors used
Apisseq was constructed in part as a test building to ﬁnd out how European building
construction styles could be used in the Arctic regions and to identify possible issues.
The building was equipped with several sensors. Thus, information about the ventilation
and heating systems is continuously collected each 15 minutes, as well as information about
the temperature, relative humidity and CO2 concentration in 5 speciﬁc rooms. These
rooms were chosen according to their orientation to study how it can inﬂuence the variables
measured. Room 1.02 is facing North, room 1.07 is facing West, room 1.10 is facing South,
1.16 facing East and room 2.02 is facing North in the second ﬂoor. Rooms 2.07 and 2.19
were also considered for the study by using additional sensors in order to compare the
results with the ones obtained in the rooms beneath them, to observe how the change
in the ﬂoor level can aﬀect the results obtained. Nevertheless, the sensors available were
limited and therefore, not all the rooms desired could be analyzed. The apartments where
the data was recorded can be seen in Figure 3.1.

The data is stored in an Structured Query Language (SQL) database. Therefore, a set of
commands can be used to manipulate the information and extract it. A Lonbox Series
model PID 4000 was used as web server and data logger, with 200 channels for connection
of sensors [1]. The sensors used need to be compatible with LonWorks, which requires 20
kΩNTC (negative temperature coeﬃcient) and a 0-10 Vdc signal for other sensors.
The data can be downloaded in a standard XML format or as TXT. The ﬁle text includes
the date and hour when measurements happened as well as the identiﬁcation number of

of the variables measured. The identiﬁcation number is introduced in the SQL code to
extract the information. The interval of time of the measurements and dates can also be
speciﬁed depending on the requirements.
The initial idea was that the latest data could be accessed online from any computer and
that the long term data was collected in a server placed at DTU. Unfortunately, nowadays
the data can only be downloaded on site due to connection issues.

Indoor temperature
Indoor temperatures of the diﬀerent rooms were recorded for both periods in intervals of
15 minutes.
According to DS 469, it is recommended that the temperature of the building does not
exceed 26ºC for more than 100 hours and 27ºC for more than 25 hours during the year.
This parameters have been extrapolated to the period of time that the sensors were
recording the information in order to check that there are no overheating issues in the
rooms. Therefore, the temperature shall not be higher than 26ºC for more than 1.14% of
the time (2.1 hours for a period of 150 hours) and 0.28% of the time higher than 27ºC
(0.42 hours for a period of 150 hours).
For a Category II building the indoor temperature should be between 20 and 25ºC during
heating season [3].
In order to have a better understanding of the results, the window and door opening sensors
were considered at the beginning, but unfortunately, collected data was not readable. The
sensors were supposed to specify if the window was open or closed and for how long [1],
but instead of that the results were confusing and randomly ordered. Due to this, the
window opening factor will be studied through the survey among the occupants.
Living rooms results
Some interesting results have been found while analyzing the data of the temperature
sensors in the living rooms.
While originally a similar pattern was expected, results show that each room experienced
a diﬀerent range of temperatures during the periods tested.

temperatures were lower than in April (Annex J.1). Nevertheless, the results show that
higher temperatures were achieved during March.

6.1
Reduce the water supply temperature
In order to reduce the energy consumption of the building at the same time that the
overheating in the rooms is reduced, a lower water supply temperature was proposed. By
reducing the temperature on the heating system, less ﬂow from the district heating network
would be needed in the exchanger and therefore, less energy would be consumed.
One of the authors went to the technical room before the second period test in April to
modify the ECL Comfort controller set-up (Annex G.1). This device controls the water
ﬂow and temperature of the whole heating and DHW system in the building.
The controller allows the user to modify the temperature curve for the water supply. The
original set-up mode on the heating curve was 1 (Figure 6.1), which provided around 55ºC
at an outdoor temperature of -7ºC. This mode was changed to 0.6 to provide 45ºC when
the outdoor temperature is -7ºC. The motorized control valve is opened gradually when
the ﬂow temperature is lower than the desired ﬂow temperature and vice versa, taking
into account the outdoor temperature sensor to adjust it accordingly.

6.2
Change of ventilation system
Traditionally, Air to air heat exchangers are used in cold climate. Mechanical ventilation
systems (which are more eﬃcient) are not suitable when outside air temperature is below
-5◦C [13].
Nevertheless, recent papers and studies have shown, that either adequate
mechanical ventilation systems with heat recovery or systems coupled with an outside air
preheating device are performing well in cold conditions and no frost formation is observed
[14], [15], [16].
In this section, the beneﬁts of replacing the AHU by rotary exchangers were studied.
IDA ICE oﬀers several predeﬁned AHU system units such as Air to Air heat exchanger
(Appendix A), Enthalpy wheel or Mixing box. To model the current AHU system, an air
to air heat exchanger was used, with an overall eﬃciency of 0.4 (Table 2.3). The design
eﬃciency of the “VEX160 Horizontal with Electric heating coil HCE” is closer to 0.6/0.7 .
However, as explained in [2], snow accumulation or frost formation inside ventilation units
reduce this eﬃciency to 0.4.
To avoid those problems and decrease the energy consumption due to ventilation, a future
renovation could be to change the heat exchanger units by rotary exchangers. A thermal
wheel consists of a circular honeycomb matrix of heat-absorbing material, which is slowly
rotated within the supply and exhaust air streams of an AHU, Figure 6.3. Although, this

Floor heating
Floor heating at Apisseq is really appreciated by tenants. However it had raised several

doors to regulate the indoor air temperature.
In a future renovation, it could be possible to regulate either water temperature or water
ﬂow running through the system according to indoor temperature. By setting only one
valve which control the whole system, energy could be saved and tenants still got the
possibility to control indoor temperature as they wish with radiators. To estimate savings,
we changed ﬂoor heating schedule from always on to Proportional to the indoor air
temperature. Simulation of apartments 1.2 have shown that this solution could reduce
space heating demand by 330 kWh, so 3.5% of the total heating demand.

The main reason that generates issues and malfunction in the system is the lack of main-

Even though solar thermal heating systems are low maintenance, they require certain

could be a bad connection or that the sensors need a replacement.
One of the main things to watch out for is ﬂuid leaks in the pipes. Leakage would cause

resists temperatures from -40 to 125ºC (up to 300ºC with additional cooling) and can
provide information about the pressure on the system from 0 to 40 bar. Then, when the
pressure detected went lower than 1 bar, in addition to the signals from other sensors like
a decrease in the temperature, a signal could be sent informing from the malfunction of
the system.
Ideally, the actual control system could be used by providing internet connection in the
technical room. Then, when the computer received the alarm signal from the sensors, a
mail could be sent to the janitor and ARTEK technicians through a simple mail transfer
protocol (SMTP) in order to inform about the issue and be able to check and solve the
problem in a short period of time.
As it was checked during a ﬁrst inspection to the system in March, a problem with the air
release valve was detected, which needed to be adjusted to extract the air in the system. A
better valve could be used, like Caleﬃautomatic air vent and shut-oﬀvalve (Annex G.3),
which is speciﬁc for solar thermal systems. This model works at a range of temperatures
from -30 to 180ºC, for a water-glycol mix of around 50 %.
The maximum discharge pressure is 5 Bar, while the maximum working pressure is 10 Bar.
Therefore, the valve match the system speciﬁcations [6] and could be used in the solar
thermal system.

Discussion

7.1
Indoor Air Quality
From the results obtained from the additional indoor climate sensors used in the tested
rooms, it can be seen that there is a clear diﬀerence on the indoor temperature during
March and April periods. Contrary to what was expected, the temperatures recorded were
higher during March, while the outdoor temperature was way lower than during April for
at least a few days.
This could be due to the fact that, as the outdoor temperature was lower, the occupants
closed the windows most of the time (as checked in the survey), compared to April. Most
of them used the radiators during that period, and even though they did not use them, the
ﬂoor heating system was continuously operating according to the water supply temperature
curve from the controller, which at -25ºC provided a temperature of around 70ºC as seen
in Figure 6.1. Therefore, the higher supply temperature lead to an increase on the indoor
temperature while the occupants were out of the building during the day as there was no
control applied.
During April, the outdoor temperature was lower and therefore the water supply tempera-
ture was lower as well. Taking that into consideration and the fact that the occupants
opened the windows for several hours, the resulting indoor temperature obtained is lower
than during March.
Speciﬁc results on the indoor temperature and CO2 concentration in the rooms may be
directly related to the behaviour of the occupants of those speciﬁc rooms, as commented
in subsection 3.3.2. As an example, the CO2 concentration in room 2.19, where during
March the CO2 concentration was way higher than during April, reaching more than 1200
PPM. This was due to the fact that in this particular room, two persons were living there
for that week, as the original occupant had a friend’s visit. The concentration in other
rooms may vary due to the weekend activities as hiking, when the occupant would be out
of the room for most of the day.
While comparing the indoor conditions in the rooms with the acceptable limits from a
Category II building, it has been found that the results depend mostly on the occupants
behaviour, but that overall, there is overheating levels that match Category III or above
during March, while during April the levels were acceptable. This could be due to the
use of window opening, which in some speciﬁc rooms could be used too often leading to
temperatures below 20ºC most of the time (room 2.19). The overheating occurred mostly

7.2
Survey among the occupants
Interesting results were obtained from the survey performed among the occupants.
Unlike the results obtained from the sensors, where it has been clariﬁed that the temper-
atures in the rooms were higher during March, the occupants felt warmer during April.
This is related to the fact that the occupants opened the windows more often (1 to 3 hours)
during April due to the warm feeling, causing a drop on the temperature in the room
which was reﬂected in the results obtained from the sensors.
During March, most of the occupants felt slightly warm, which could be due to the fact
that the outdoor temperatures were really low (-25ºC), which could lead to the desire
of warmer temperatures in the rooms to compensate the freezing conditions while the
students arrived to their rooms.
According to the results obtained, the building match Category II during March, while
during April the occupants discomfort feeling lead to a Category IV.
From the results it can also be said that most of the occupants would like to have more
control on their room’s temperature. The amount of people increases in April, when the
feeling of overheating is more extended.

Conclusion
This research aimed to identify functioning issues of the HVAC systems in Apisseq dor-
mitory, caused partially by the extreme conditions in the Arctic. Once those issues were
identiﬁed, diﬀerent improvement proposals were done.
From sensors’ results, it was concluded that uncomfortable temperatures ranges were
reached, causing discomfort among the occupants. One of the main reason was the over-
heating experienced in the rooms due to the constant heat supply from the ﬂoor heating
system. This lead to an unnecessary energy consumption during template periods like
spring and summer time. Additionally, energy looses are accentuated by the fact that
occupants open windows to regulate the temperature in their rooms as desired, as it
was clear from the survey that most of them wanted to have more control on the indoor
temperature.
Most of the time, indoor climate building requirements from Category II were not reached,
as relative humidity and CO2 concentration levels were also worse than expected.
The thermal solar system installed in Apisseq works properly. Nevertheless, maintenance
should be done regularly in order to ensure its normal operation during time. By replacing
or adding some components (alarms, sensors) and improving the control system, it would
be easier to optimize its operation.
Regarding the ventilation system, the eﬃciency of the heat exchanger was aﬀected by
frost issues, which could be avoided by replacing it by an enthalpy wheel rotary exchanger.
With this system, heat and moisture recovery is more eﬃcient, a 12 % space heating energy
savings has been estimated. To reduce over ventilation, a variable air ﬂow supply has been
studied. This method aims to reduce ventilation of rooms when students are at school.
Finally, a simulation considering these 2 improvements coupled with a proportional (based
on indoor temperature) ﬂoor heating system has shown that the total heat demand could
be reduced by 20 %.
