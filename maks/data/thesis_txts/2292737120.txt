Abstract
Intense focus on eÔ¨Éciency and optimization from several stakeholders has
made accurate and reliable engine monitoring more important than ever.
Current two-stroke engine controls provides suggestions for cylinder-to-
cylinder changes based on measured pressure. Automation of the process
would increase the amount of applied changes dramatically. However, due
to less than perfect sensor reliability, redundancy must be present. The
cylinder mounted pressure sensors are expensive and suÔ¨Äer from decreasing
accuracy thus alternative, cheaper and more methods for pressure estima-
tion are needed for veriÔ¨Ācation. Studies have successful estimated cylinder
pressures in four-stroke car and truck engines from torsional vibrations. No
studies have however seemed to address the pressure estimation of two-
stroke marine diesel engines.
This work introduces a new development to an estimation algorithm based
on the approach of earlier studies. Pressures are successfully estimated of a
single cylinder test engine using a crankshaft mounted torsional vibration
sensor.
A dynamic engine model is developed as the base for the esti-
mation algorithm and pressures have been estimated with highest relative
error below 10 %. Pressures are reconstructed using a parametric pressure
model with predeÔ¨Āned parameters spaces and the dynamic model has been
validated using experimentally measured data.

Introduction
The purpose of this project is to contribute with knowledge of cylinder pressure es-
timation in large two-stroke diesel engines based on measured torsional crankshaft
vibrations.
1.1
Background
With intense focus on eÔ¨Éciency and optimization from several stakeholders (end con-
sumer, carriers, regulatory agencies, etc.) the performance of marine engines are crucial.
MAN Diesel & Turbo two-stroke marine diesel engines are equipped with Ô¨Āxed mounted
sensors for continuous pressure measuring of each cylinder cycle. The engine control
system monitors and to a certain degree the engine performance based on the cylinder
pressure signals. Operating in a harsh environment inÔ¨āuence the long term reliability of
the cylinder pressure measuring since signal accuracy is decreases. Thus the chief must
approve larger adjustment changes proposed from the control system before they can
be executed. According to MAN Diesel & Turbo‚Äô experiences the changes suggested by
the control system are hardly ever applied by the crew, meaning that a closed control
loop is necessary in all operating conditions.
The Ô¨Āxed mounted cylinder pressure sensors are expensive and can suÔ¨Äer from noise,
oÔ¨Äset issues and limited durability. A closed loop control system requires alternative
pressure estimation methods using either cheaper and more durable sensors where sys-
tem error occurrence will be limited, or a validation of the pressure measuring.
If a dynamic model can reliably estimate cylinder pressures from crankshaft speed
Ô¨āuctuations, redundancy is obtained to verify the signals of the cylinder pressure sen-
sors. When redundancy or validation of other means of pressure signals is present, the
control system will be able to implement changes automatically in all normal operating
modes.
The crankshaft in large two-stroke engines is typically exposed to larger torsional
vibrations than the crankshaft in smaller two- and four-stroke engines, both because
of the lower speed (30-180 rpm) but also due to a less rigid shaft, thus a dynamic

1.2
Motivation and Objectives
Literature has successfully proven methods for estimating the individual cylinder pres-
sures of 4-stroke car and truck diesel engines based on crankshaft speed Ô¨āuctuations
[1, 2, 3, 4, 8, 10, 11, 12, 15, 16, 26, 27, 31]. This report will investigate the possibilities
of applying a similar method to large 2-stroke diesel engines. This is not covered in
the literature. A dynamic lumped-mass model capable of estimating cylinder pressures
from the crankshaft‚Äôs torsional vibrations will be developed. Such a model can provide
redundancy for, or be utilized to validate, the Ô¨Āxed mounted cylinder pressure sensors
utilized for automatic engine performance optimization.
The general objective of this work is to contribute to the body of knowledge of cylin-
der pressure reconstruction based on measured torsional crankshaft vibrations, and to
apply the methodologies to two-stroke marine diesel engines.
More speciÔ¨Ācally this work will develop a generic dynamic lumped-mass model which
can be used for a wide range of two-stroke engines with minor modiÔ¨Ācations.
The
approach will be based on earlier successful studies and use a non-linear approach.
The dynamic model will be validated and used to analyze a single cylinder test
engine.
Cylinder pressures will be reconstructed through an inverse approach of the dynamic
model. An new and improved estimation algorithm will be presented and imperfections
of will addressed.

The torsional crankshaft vibrations has been derived from cylinder pressure inputs
through dynamical models since Heldt [19] in 1926 discovered that gas-pressure and
not inertia are principal vibration factors. Determining torsional crankshaft vibrations

cylinder pressure as input is referred to as the direct approach (indicated in Figure 1.1

The inverse problem, where cylinder pressure and the associated torque is esti-

been known in literature since the 1980s with promising progress [27]. Rizzoni, who

In 1990 Ilda, Akishino and Kido [20] successfully determined the Indicated Mean

with an accuracy of 99 % or higher. In 2009 Youssef and Corde [51] proposed a method
for individual cylinder IMEP estimation based on a two dimensional graphic signature
generated by crankshaft Ô¨āuctuations used for diagnosis and parametric estimation of
nonlinear systems.
The engine/crankshaft torque is generated by the pressure inside the engines cylin-
ders which forces the reciprocating machinery into oscillation and rotating motions.
Pressure diÔ¨Äerences between the individual cylinders, due to leaks, amount of fuel,
misÔ¨Āre, etc., generates torque diÔ¨Äerences which results in inhomogeneous engine oper-
ations. To address engine unbalance, torque balancing is required. Torque balancing
aims to achieve equal torque from each cylinder.

Connolly and Rizzoni [13] developed a method to estimate mean torque in individual
cylinders for satisfying the California Air Resources Board (CARB) misÔ¨Āre detection
requirements. They used a deconvolution algorithm which exploits the inherent peri-
odicity of the combustion process to map the relationship between torque and speed in
the frequency domain.
In general, most studies aim to address the torque balancing of diesel engines for
two main features: First, lowering variations in injected fuel mass between cylinders
which at low engine speed can be up to 20 %. Second, lowering undesired engines
vibrations generated by torque production variations [10].
Accurate control of combustion (cylinder pressure) combined with dynamic knowl-
edge of a speciÔ¨Āc engine can achieve torque balancing. If an engine was built of identical
cylinders and identical combustions occurs in each cylinder, torque balancing will be
achieved.
As stated in [10], most solutions proposed to address the torque balancing issue
using measured crankshaft angles, have their foundation in [9, 49, 50]. These studies
successfully estimated the cylinder-by-cylinder pressure variation using special signal
modulation feedback techniques during steady engine operation conditions.
Taraza [42, 43] used a simpliÔ¨Āed lumped-mass model approach to investigate the
correlation between amplitudes and phases of the same harmonic component of the gas
pressure torque and of the crankshaft‚Äôs speed through a Discrete Fourier Transform
(DFT). He found that only major harmonic orders with same phase for all cylinders add
algebraically large contributions in the resultant torque spectrum. The lowest major
component has a low frequency which results in the crankshaft behaving dynamically
like a rigid body. The lowest harmonic component of the engine torque does not excite
torsional vibrations and correlates fairly well with the corresponding harmonic orders
of the crankshaft‚Äôs speed.
The direct approach was investigated by Schagerberg [35] in his thesis. It addressed
the direct approach by investigated the usability of instantaneous crankshaft torque
measurements for engine control purposes. He developed a simulation model based on
a system of Ô¨Ārst order nonlinear diÔ¨Äerential equations, where the crankshaft dynam-
ics were expressed as interconnected mass-spring-damper elements. His work iterates
several earlier studies and presents a clear approach for torque measurements based on
cylinder pressure input using lumped-mass models.
Schagerberg‚Äôs work also led to two articles in the Ô¨Āeld. In [36] Schagerberg and
McKelvey describes the relation between the individual cylinder pressures and the
resulting torque in the crankshaft with motivation in engine control. And in [26] Larsson
and Schagerberg successfully addressed the inverse approach by constructing an engine
model able to eliminate torsional resonances in the sensed torque signal and reconstruct
cylinder pressures using a parameterised pressure model.
In 2000 Kiencke and Nielsen [23] presented an interplay between thermodynamics,
basic engine operation, vehicle mechanics as well as parameter estimation and automa-
tive control approach. Their work have been heavily cited in several studies including

The inverse approach is investigated in several studies. As described in [26] pressure
estimation is primarily done in two ways, either by Ô¨Ānding the pressure as a discrete
sequence for e.g. each crank angle degree, or the pressure could be estimated from a
normalized pressure model used as input to the crankshaft dynamics, where an algo-
rithm would iterate over a given crankshaft angle interval and the compare the output
with the measured crankshaft speed.
In 2004 Chauvin et al [12] proved convergence between measured and estimated
cylinder pressure on a four-stroke diesel engine using only available instantaneous
crankshaft angle speed. They developed an observer through a Kalman Ô¨Ālter designed
on a physical based time-varying model for the engine dynamics. They also proved the
method using an adaptive Ô¨Ālter on the coeÔ¨Écients on the Fourier Basis with a Lya-
pounov function in both continuous and discrete cases [11]. In 2009 they presented a
real-time experimental setup with simulations and experimental results of estimating
the indicated cylinder torque of a Direct Injection (DI) engine using a time-varying
linear model [10]. The observer method from their earlier studies was improved using
the indicated torque periodicity and Ô¨Ārst harmonic development.
The work presented by Feilong et al. in 2012 [27] covers the experimental study and
practical solution for estimating cylinder pressure using only crankshaft speed Ô¨āuctu-
ation. It proposes an estimation method with discrete-time transform of a rigid-body
crankshaft model based on the kinetic energy theorem. In their study they were able to
address diÔ¨Éculties including the load torque estimation and separation of pressure pro-
Ô¨Āles from adjacent-Ô¨Āring cylinders. They found a low-pass Ô¨Ālter to eÔ¨Äectively separate
the higher order torsional vibrations from the interested lower frequencies. At engine
speeds higher than 1500 rpm they found the rigid body approach to be insuÔ¨Écient
hence a model capable of quantifying torsional vibrations is essential.
Where Feilong et al. used an assumption of a rigid body approach, Desbazeille
et al. [15] focused on the Ô¨āexible crankshaft. In their work they monitored a large
diesel engine with 20 cylinders with natural frequencies within the operating speed
range. Through pattern recognition their system was able to detect experimental fuel
leakage fault successfully, including the localization of the faulty cylinder as well as the
approximation of the fault severity.
Franco, Franchek and Grigoriadis [16] presented a real-time engine brake torque
estimation model based on measured engine speed as the only input for both steady-
state and transient operation. Here the estimation of cylinder pressure was based on
analyzing the pressure curve for each cycle. The operation conditions were estimated
using orthogonal least-square and time domain identiÔ¨Ācation methods for steady-state
and transient respectively. The estimation techniques proposed are similar to the real-
time methodology presented by [8].
In 2011 Al-Durra et al. [3, 4] described the framework and preliminary results of a
model-based estimation approach to predict the individual pressure traces in a multi-
cylinder engine from the output of the crankshaft speed sensor. They reconstructed the

2.1
Two-stroke Diesel Engines
The Copenhagen branch of the company MAN Diesel & Turbo , originally Burmeister
& Wain, designs two-stroke marine diesel engines which are then manufactured and
sold by licensees. This section gives the reader the background knowledge deliberated
in this thesis which transports roughly half of the world total trade [30] and [29].
The more commonly known four-stroke engine completes one power cycle in every
two crankshaft revolutions. The cycles are separate and consists of 1) expansion, 2)
exhaust, 3) intake and 4) compression.
The two-stroke engine on the other hand,
completes a power cycle in only one crankshaft revolution with two strokes, the up and
down movement of the piston in a low range of rotational speeds from approximately
50-200 rpm. This allows for direct drive of the propeller and lead to higher reliability
and extended lifetime.
2.1.1
General Design
Internal combustion (IC) engines produce power by releasing the chemical energy con-
tained in a mixture of compressed air and fuel and transform it into mechanical energy
in from of crankshaft torque by moving a piston connected to a crankshaft. The piston
is moved by the expansion of burned air and fuel and once the energy transformation
is complete the exhaust gas is replaced with new compressed air and fuel.
The two-stroke marine diesel engines are categorized as compression ignition (CI)
engines and the cycles seen in Ô¨Āgure 2.1 consist of: 1) Compression; air in the cylinder

2) Expansion; after top dead center (TDC) fuel is injected as atomized droplets through

remaining exhaust gas out of the cylinder chamber. After the piston top has passed
the scavenging port top, the valve closes, compression begins and the cycle starts over.
2.2
Pressure Sensing in MAN Diesel & Turbo Engines
The engine monitoring constantly receives pressure signals from each cylinder. The
signals are obtained by pressure sensors which are attached to the cylinder. Due to the
harsh environment during combustion the sensors do however not reach the inside of
the cylinder. Figure 2.2 shows an illustration of a two-stroke marine engine where the
location of the pressure sensor can be seen inside the red circle. As seen, the channel
is approximately the same length as the piston diameter and a turn in the channel is
observed. This design reduces wear of sensors from thermal shocks and is a typical
design for sensor monitoring on two-stroke marine engines. Wear is reduced, but so
are the reliability. The distance from sensor to cylinder chamber and the channel turn
reduces the accuracy of the sensor signal.

The engines crankshaft continues directly into the propeller/waterbrake shaft. As no

At tests the load torque Tl(őł), is continuously measured in the angular-domain, but
at operating ships the propeller load is determined an estimation given as a viscous
damping.
In the data provided by MDTcph the engine load is model as a viscous damping
applied to the propeller/waterbrake DOF.
Though both Tf(őł) = 0 and Tl(őł) = 0 since they are estimated as outer viscous
damping in the data provided by MDTcph, both contributions will still be pressent

5.1
Appoach
The approach used for estimating the cylinder pressure is inspired by [26] where an
iteration algorithm based on a pressure model is developed.
The inversion of the dynamics is complicated and the diÔ¨Éculty increases as more
cylinders are added. This is due to the torsional crankshaft vibrations being the only
input, but each cylinder pressure being an output.
The method introduced in [26]
avoid this problem by repeatedly simulate the system with various input signals until
the output signal is reconstructed as well as possible. In the case of multiple active
cylinders through a single cycle, the windowing approach described by [31] can be
applied. The windowing method applies a ‚ÄĚwindow‚ÄĚ to the signal with a size no larger
than a full cycle length (360 degrees for two-stroke and 720 degrees for four-stroke
engines) divided by the number of cylinders. The windowing method cannot contribute
with any improvement for a single cylinder case.
Two ways of estimating pressure was suggested in [26]. Either by Ô¨Ānding the pres-
sure as a discrete sequence for e.g. every Ô¨Āfth crank angle, or by a parametric pressure
model. The latter case is chosen for this work since succesfull results for this was found
in [26]. This study will however also apply a speciÔ¨Āc ranges for the pressure parameters
ňÜŌá to be found. JustiÔ¨Āed initial guesses and limits can improve the calculation speed
and ensure that sensible results are achieved.
5.2
The Parametric Pressure Model
In order to estimate the cylinder pressure a parametric pressure model is used. The
pressure model is developed by Mads Lund SteÔ¨Äensen at MDTcph [40] and is normally
used for other purposes. In this work the pressure model has two functions; 1) generate
model parameters for pressure generation, and 2) generate pressure curve based on
model parameters.
In the Ô¨Ārst step, a measured pressure signal pm is imported into the model together
with Ô¨Āxed geometric engine parameters. Based on the input, the model generates the
optimal model parameters for the speciÔ¨Āc measured pressure signal pm. The model
parameters include compression coeÔ¨Écients, fuel burn factor, expansion coeÔ¨Écients
and scavenging pressure. These parameters depend largely on engine geometry and
no changes have been observed when diÔ¨Äerent pm cycles are used as model input.
But, the more critical model parameters ňúŌá (compression pressure pc, peak pressure
position őłmax, maximum pressure pmax and the exhaust pressure pE) largely depend
on the speciÔ¨Āc pm cycle chosen. Therefore these four parameters were chosen as the
dependent parameters for the optimization algorithm where they are referred to as the
vector Ōá.
In the second step, the pressure model generates a pressure curve to use in the
dynamic simulation instead of a measured pressure signal. Figure 5.2 shows the pressure
curve ňúp generated by the pressure model as a function of őł with TDC located at őł = 0.

Conclusion
6.1
Summary and Discussion
The objective of this thesis was to explore the usage of a dynamic engine model to
estimate cylinder pressure in two-stroke marine diesel engines, as explained in Chapter
1, a subject no known literature seems to address.
In the present work, several modeling aspects regarding the crankshaft dynamics
have been derived. The developed dynamic model and the simulation program based
on it, can be applied to a wide range of two-stroke marine engines with minor changes.
The main objective has been addressed and successful results of estimating cylinder
pressure have been shown. Based on the approach in [26], a new proposal of a pressure
estimation algorithm is introduced.
A parametric pressure model consisting of Ô¨Āve sub-models is used to reconstruct pres-
sure signals in the dynamic model. However, initial investigation of the pressure model
itself, revealed low accuracy (relative errors as high as 25 %) when reconstructing
measured pressure signals. To overcome imperfections of the pressure model artiÔ¨Ācial
pressure signals were generated and resulted in much lower errors (by Ô¨Āve orders of
magnitude) when reconstructed.
The artiÔ¨Ācial pressures have been generated by the pressure model from cycle-to-
cycle using a slight variation of four input parameters, and then used in the dynamic
model instead of a measured pressure signal.
In the dynamic simulation, the four pressure model input parameters have success-
fully been estimated cycle-by-cycle and the artiÔ¨Ācial cylinder pressure curves have been
reconstructed with good accuracy (highest relative error below 10 %).
The relative error between reconstructed pressure curves and artiÔ¨Ācial pressure
curves have shown sudden changes at locations where the pressure model change from
one sub-model to another. No changes have been observed between these locations.
The estimation algorithm returns the four cycle-by-cycle pressure parameters which
results in the lowest error between the dynamic response using artiÔ¨Ācial input pressure
==PAGE:80==
64
6. Conclusion
and the dynamic response using estimated pressure. The algorithm have been validated
using Ô¨Ārst a single artiÔ¨Ācial pressure curve and second a sinus test function multiplied
by an artiÔ¨Ācial pressure curve.
The dynamic model has been validated using experimentally measured data of a
single cylinder test engine with Ô¨Āve degrees of freedom. The validation has shown reli-
able results and the credibility for multi cylinder engines is high.
Though the estimation algorithm presented delivers successful results, its computa-
tional complexity makes simulations time demanding, especially as the number of cy-
cles increases. This reduces the algorithm‚Äôs applicability in real-time solutions. As the
algorithm is now, a continuous real-time application is impractical. But, a solution
where it runs in the background and only simulates every e.g. 50th or 100th cycle is
possible. This will not be able to detect sudden changes in an operating engine, but
can detect variations in sensor signals over time and contribute to engine monitoring
with sample inspection.
The imperfections of the pressure model have caused the use of artiÔ¨Ācial pressures.
Though the estimation algorithm returns good results for artiÔ¨Ācial pressures which very
closely resembles measured pressures, the pressure model must be updated and tested
on real measured pressures before any Ô¨Ānal conclusion regarding real applications can
be made.
Furthermore, both the dynamic model and the estimation algorithm also need to be
tested on a multi cylinder test engine to resemble a real application as close as possible.
Initial validations of an artiÔ¨Ācial multi cylinder extension to the single cylinder test
engine have been made and show the need for more accurate damping measures.
The damping is a critical component in the dynamic system. In the present work
a transformation of damping from angular-domain to time-domain is performed. How-
ever, this transformation might not be accurate enough for multi-cylinder engines and
should be further investigated. Additionally, when transforming the damping the mode
with highest energy for the speciÔ¨Āc degree of freedom is chosen by an approximation.
The chosen modes might however not be the optimal and a comprehensive investigation
of the transformation could reveal a more accurate solution.
6.2
Future Work
The next step for the work would obviously be to apply the dynamic model and estima-
tion algorithm on the four-cylinder test engine at MAN Diesel & Turbo and optimize
the computational complexity of the algorithm. For the time given in this work, these
steps have not been addressed. Several other aspects would however also be of great
interest.
Firstly a comprehensive study on the damping transformation might improve the
dynamic model and give more accurate simulations the direct way, which would then
lead to a more accurate pressure estimation when inverted. One approach could start by
==PAGE:81==
6.2 Future Work
65
investigating the sensitivity between modes, fraction of critical damping, and the system
velocity. This might reveal if more accurate combinations of modes and fraction of
critical damping in relation to the system velocity, could be used for the transformaion.
Simulations have shown the propeller/waterbrake damping to be a critical part of
the system, which load changes in a large sinusoid pattern throughout time. It will be of
great interest to derive the propeller/waterbrake damping with continuously feedback.
If successful, this could introduce a continuously adjusting of load damping in the
dynamic model and might even lead to improvement of engine monitoring by providing
a instant measure of the load. One way to address this could be by Hooks law. Strain-
Gauges and/or accelerometers attached to the crankshaft between the engine and the
propeller/waterbrake could provide live measurements of vibrations and deformations
which could then be transformed into an applied force using Hooks law.
Secondly, if a more accurate transformation of damping into time-domain cannot
be derived, it will be of great interest to attempt changing the entire dynamic model
into the frequency-domain. Since the dynamic development program used at MDTcph
simulates in the frequency-domain, where also all damping coeÔ¨Écients for engines are
placed, a transformation of the entire dynamic model might be well worth the time.
Thirdly, if the estimation algorithm and/or the continuously propeller/waterbrake
damping-feedback is implemented on ships in the future, characteristics of the speciÔ¨Āc
engines will be valuable. A testbed program (not programming) at the engine manu-
factures could gather a vast variety of information for each speciÔ¨Āc engine. It would
be possible to create a database with reference parameters and values from a com-
prehensive test program in a controlled environment for each engine. This reference
data can then be used for more accurate input parameters in the dynamic model and
a propeller-damping model.
