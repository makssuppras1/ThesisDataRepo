Abstract 
Alzheimer's disease (AD) is a progressive brain disorder characterized by cognitive decline with 
degenerative pathology and neuronal loss. Even though amyloid-β (Aβ) plaques and 
neurofibrillary tangles are considered the classical pathological features, advances in research 
have revealed the pivotal role played by non-neuronal cells, particularly microglia, in 
exacerbating the progression of the disease. This shift of focus has put neuroinflammation at 
the center of AD pathogenesis, highlighting the contribution of the brain's own immune 
system to causing neuronal damage and loss. 
This thesis investigates the role of microglial secreted factors in modulating neuronal survival 
and apoptotic signaling in a human-relevant in vitro model of sporadic Alzheimer's disease 
(sAD). In this scope, human induced pluripotent stem cells (hiPSCs) derived from control and 
sAD female donors were differentiated into mature glutamatergic neurons. The neurons were 
then subjected to microglia-conditioned media (MCM) derived from unstimulated as well as 
lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-treated microglial cultures. Apoptotic response was assessed by 
measuring BCL2 (anti-apoptotic) and BAX (pro-apoptotic) gene expression using RT-qPCR. 
The results demonstrated that the susceptibility of glutamatergic neurons to inflammation-
induced apoptosis is dependent on both the donor’s genetic background and the 
inflammatory profile of the microglial secretome to which they are exposed. Surprisingly, sAD-
derived neurons from higher-risk lines showed impaired upregulation of BCL2, a key anti-
apoptotic gene that promotes cell survival by inhibiting mitochondrial-mediated apoptosis, 
when exposed to microglia secreted factors from different cell lines. These same, higher-risk 
sAD neurons, though exhibited heightened sensitivity to microglial factors that were exposed 
to LPS, as measured by the increased BCL2 levels through qPCR analysis. This paradoxical 
response may reflect dysregulated anti-apoptotic signaling pathways, possibly due to genetic 
or epigenetic impairments and the exaggerated BCL2 upregulation upon LPS stimuli should 
indicate a maladaptive stress response rather than an efficient homeostatic control. In 
contrast, control and lower-risk sAD neurons exhibited more robust compensatory survival 
mechanisms in response to inflammatory stress. Control neurons demonstrated a lower 
activation threshold to inflammation compared to higher-risk sAD neurons, as evidenced by 
upregulated BAX expression under specific stress conditions, based on qPCR analysis. 
Interestingly, lower-risk sAD neurons showed signs of immune exhaustion regardless of the 
treatment applied, suggesting a shift toward a survival-oriented phenotype. Notably, microglia 
supernatant derived from the lower-risk sAD profile consistently exhibited neuroprotective 
properties across all treatment conditions, as indicated by elevated BCL2 mRNA levels in qPCR 
analysis. 
These findings emphasize the importance of microglia-neuron dialogue in AD and confirm the 
use of hiPSC-based co-cultures as a model for mimicking neuroinflammation and for the 
identification of potential therapeutic targets.

Introduction 
Introduction to Alzheimer’s Disease 
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is named after Alois Alzheimer, a German psychiatrist who was 
studying the brain of his first patient, Auguste Deter, experiencing memory loss and personality 
changes [1]. AD is the most prevalent form of dementia [2] affecting 50 million people 
worldwide [1] and the ratio is skewed towards women, with women making up two-thirds of 
all cases [3]. It is estimated that by 2050 the number of affected individuals will rise to 152 
million [1]. 
The main clinical feature of AD is impairment in memory function [4]. It progresses gradually 
through distinct clinical stages each one characterized by higher cognitive decline and 
neurological changes [1]. Pathological alterations can start 15 to 20 years before the main 
memory loss symptoms arise. AD has been classified by the National Institute on Aging and 
the Alzheimer’s Association into six stages [5]: 
1. Cognitively Unimpaired, described as asymptomatic phase and can persist for many years, 
without noticeable memory problems or behavioral changes [5].  
2. Subjective Cognitive Decline, here minor memory lapses may occur. In this stage cortex and 
hippocampus start being affected [1].  
3. Mild Cognitive Impairment, [5] ,[1] 
4. Mild Dementia, at this point symptoms become more apparent affecting daily life [5], and 
individuals may experience confusion and mood changes [1] 
5. Moderate Dementia [5], larger regions of the cerebral cortex are impacted leading to 
significant memory loss. Patients start struggling to recognize familiar faces such as family and 
friends, while tackle with impulse control [1]. Independency capability is reduced [5].  
6. Severe Dementia: This is the final stage, in which disease extends throughout the entire 
cortex area and an extensive accumulation of amyloid plaques and neurofibrillary tangles are 
observed. A complete loss of independence is characteristic of this stage, and accumulated 
complications may eventually contribute to death. [1], [5]. 
AD is classified into familial and sporadic types. Familial AD (FAD) is rarer and corresponds to 
1-5% of cases. It is primarily linked to autosomal dominant mutations in the amyloid precursor 
protein (APP), presenilin 1 (PSEN1) and presenilin 2 (PSEN2) genes. FAD typically appears 
between 30 to 65 years old and progresses rapidly. On the other hand, sporadic AD (sAD) is 
described as late-onset AD, accounting for over of 95% cases. It is most often developed after 
the age of 65 and can be attributed to a plethora of contributors such as genetic predisposition, 
environmental exposure and coexisting health abnormalities [4].  
Neuropathophysiology of AD and Hallmarks 
The main hallmark of AD is the accumulation of misfolded amyloid-β (Aβ) and tau proteins in 
the brain, leading to the formation of extracellular amyloid plaques and intracellular 
neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs), respectively [6]. Aβ is derived from sequential proteolytic 
cleavage [7] of a larger glycoprotein named amyloid precursor protein (APP). APP is an integral 
membrane domain with a large extracellular glycosylated N-terminus and a shorter 
==PAGE:10==
2 
 
cytoplasmic C-terminus. It is found in various tissues, but is predominantly located at neuronal 
synapses, playing a key role in neuronal development, synaptic repair, signal transmission, 
intracellular transport and maintaining neuronal balance. In humans, APP can undergo 
processing via two competing pathways: the amyloidogenic and the non-amyloidogenic 
pathway.  In Alzheimer’s disease, the balance between these pathways shifts toward the 
amyloidogenic route, contributing to pathological Aβ accumulation [8]. 
In the non-amyloidogenic pathway, APP is initially cleaved by α-secretase within the region 
that would otherwise form amyloid-beta. This cleavage produces two fragments: sAPPα, which 
has neuroprotective properties and supports both synaptic function and neuron survival, and 
a membrane-bound piece called CTF83. CTF83 is then further processed by γ-secretase, 
resulting in the release of a non-toxic peptide known as P3 and a signaling molecule called 
AICD. This pathway helps maintain brain health by preventing the formation of amyloid-beta 
and promoting protective effects through sAPPα [9].   
In contrast, in the amyloidogenic processing route, APP is first cleaved by the enzyme β-
secretase 1, (BACE1). This cleavage results in the formation of two fragments: sAPPβ, which 
has limited neuroprotective properties compared to sAPPα, and a membrane-bound fragment 
called CTF99. The CTF99 fragment is subsequently processed by γ-secretase, producing Aβ 
peptides, primarily Aβ40 and Aβ42. Among these, Aβ42 is particularly prone to clumping 
together, forming toxic oligomers and eventually amyloid plaques [9]. It is the hydrophobic 
nature of Aβ42 oligomers that promotes their self-association and eventual aggregation into 
senile plaques [4]. Extracellular Aβ oligomers and plaques interfere with synaptic function and 
trigger neuroinflammation by activating microglia [6]. Soluble Aβ oligomers interfere with 
synaptic communication [9] and are implicated in neurotoxicity by disrupting cell membranes 
and inducing inflammatory responses as well as oxidative stress [4]. 
Apolipoprotein E (APOE) regulates lipid homeostasis in the brain, impacting amyloid β 
deposition and clearance [10]. APOE plays a critical role in brain lipid metabolism, facilitating 
the clearance and transport of lipids and cholesterol [11]. Cholesterol is important for the 
structural and functional integrity of synapses and membranes. APOE is mainly synthesized by 
astrocytes and to a smaller portion by microglia. The ATP-binding cassette transporter A1 
(ABCA1) facilitates the transfer of cholesterol to APOE, leading to the formation of high-density 
lipoprotein (HDL) particles and a subsequent decrease in cholesterol levels within cells. A 
shared feature of HDL apolipoproteins, including APOE, is to facilitate the breakdown of 
intracellular Aβ by microglia. APOE promotes intracellular Aβ degradation not through direct 
interaction with Aβ, but by lowering cellular cholesterol. This enhances Aβ trafficking to 
lysosomes, which are specialized organelles within microglia, and accelerates its breakdown 
[12], through the intracellular mechanism of clearance. There are two more pathways that 
promote Aβ degradation and include: proteolytic cleavage and clearance across the Blood 
Brain Barrier (BBB) mediated by the Low-density lipoprotein receptor (LDLR) family [13]. 
APOE modulates Aβ aggregation, clearance, and toxicity in the brain through mechanisms that 
are both isoform- and lipidation-dependent. Particularly, in individuals carrying the APOE4 
allele the clearance seems less effective [12]. The APOE3 allele is the most prevalent, found in 
about 77% of the population, while the ε2 allele is the least common at around 8%. The ε4 
allele occurs in roughly 15% of the general population but is present in approximately 40% of 
individuals with AD [14]. The ε4 variant of the APOE gene, is recognized as the most significant 
genetic risk factor for late onset sAD [15]. In contrast, the ε2 variant appears to offer a 
protective effect. Carrying the ε4 allele not only increases the likelihood of developing 
==PAGE:11==
3 
 
Alzheimer's in an allele-count-dependent manner but also tends to accelerate the disease's 
onset. In fact, individuals heterozygous for the APOE4 isoform have a 2- to 3-fold increased 
risk of developing AD, while those with two copies face up to a 12-fold higher risk [10]. APOE4 
is thought to contribute to Alzheimer's through both a reduction in its normal neuroprotective 
functions and an increase in harmful neurotoxic effects, especially when compared to APOE3 
[14].  
All APOE isoforms transiently interact with Aβ during the early stages of aggregation, forming 
APOE-Aβ co-aggregates (Figure 1). The lipidation state of APOE critically determines the fate 
and toxicity of these aggregates. Non-lipidated APOE, particularly APOE4, forms large, stable 
co-aggregates with Aβ that are resistant to clearance by glial cells such as microglia and 
astrocytes. These persistent co-aggregates contribute to increased Aβ pathology by stabilizing 
toxic oligomeric species, promoting membrane damage, and inducing the secretion of pro-
inflammatory cytokines. In contrast, lipidated APOE, especially the APOE2/3 isoforms, 
facilitates the efficient clearance of Aβ aggregates through receptor-mediated uptake by glial 
cells, thereby reducing aggregate accumulation and associated neurotoxicity. As Aβ 
aggregation progresses to fibril formation, APOE dissociates from the aggregates, leaving 
behind pure Aβ fibrils. The presence of non-lipidated APOE slows Aβ fibrillation, while 
lipidated APOE accelerates this process, likely by providing a nucleation surface. Overall, the 
APOE4 genotype increases the risk of Alzheimer’s disease by promoting the accumulation of 
non-lipidated APOE4-Aβ co-aggregates, which are less efficiently cleared and more neurotoxic 
[16].

An additional traditional hallmark of AD pathology, is the presence of NFTs which are formed 
by Tau protein. Tau is a conserved microtubule-associated protein, that interacts with tubulin 
and promotes the assembly of tubulin into microtubules [17]. Microtubules are dynamic, 
hollow, cylindrical polymers composed of α- and β-tubulin heterodimers arranged with 
directional polarity. They vary in length and are essential for maintaining neuronal structure, 
==PAGE:12==
4 
 
enabling complex branching, and serving as intracellular tracks for motor proteins to transport 
vital materials such as receptors, neurotransmitters, and organelles to different parts of the 
cell, supporting key neural functions [18]. Microtubules consist of a stable and a labile domain 
and tau is more concentrated on the labile one [19], supporting axonal transport in neurons 
[20] as well as the cytoskeleton [21]. It exists in six isoforms due to alternative splicing of exons 
2, 3, and 10 of the microtubules associated protein tau (MAPT) gene. Tau binds to 
microtubules through either three (3R) or four (4R) repeat domains, and the 3R:4R ratio is 
disrupted in disease states [20]. Post-translational modifications of Tau, such as  
hyperphosphorylation and acetylation [22], can impair its function, leading to microtubule 
destabilization, neuronal dysfunction, and cell death [20]. 
In more details, pathological tau protein in AD is characterized by abnormal 
hyperphosphorylation and cleavage, leading to conformational changes and aggregation into 
paired helical and straight filaments [19]. This process is driven by an imbalance between 
kinase and phosphatase activities [4], causing tau to detach from microtubules and misfold 
into NFTs, as illustrated in Figure 2. NFTs disrupt neuronal communication, impair synaptic 
function, and enlarge axon terminals [22]. Tau pathology also reduces presynaptic vesicle 
mobility, decreases glutamatergic receptors, impairs dendritic spine maturation, disrupts 
mitochondrial function, and promotes microglial synaptic phagocytosis. Additionally, Tau 
acetylation at specific lysine residues  inhibits degradation and impairs function, contributing 
to tau accumulation and toxicity in AD [19].

The ongoing loss of neurons results in brain atrophy, accompanied by cortical thinning, 
enlarged ventricles, and shrinkage of the hippocampus and entorhinal cortex, see Figure 3 [6]. 
Beyond Aβ and Tau, several other factors, including acetylcholine deficiency, 
neuroinflammation, oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, insulin resistance, and 
disruptions in cholesterol and glutamate homeostasis, contribute to AD pathology [4].

Pathogenesis and Underlying Causes of Alzheimer’s Disease 
Other potential contributors to the pathology of AD include the cholinergic hypothesis, the 
inflammatory hypothesis, the microbiota-gut-brain axis hypothesis, and the oxidative stress 
hypothesis. 
The cholinergic hypothesis proposes a specific degeneration of 
cholinergic neurons in the nucleus basalis of Meinert, which is 
the primary source of Acetylcholine (ACh) for cortical and 
hippocampal regions. These areas are linked with learning and 
memory. This neuronal loss contributes to a reduction in choline 
acetyltransferase (ChAT) activity [4], a key enzyme in the 
synthesis of from choline and acetyl-CoA. Under normal 
physiological conditions, neuronal activation follows the release 
of ACh in the synaptic cleft via the vesicular acetylcholine 
transporter (VAChT), where it binds to muscarinic (mAChR) and 
nicotinic (nAChR) receptors, mediating neurotransmission. In 
the cholinergic neurons of AD patients though, the lower levels 
of ChAT lead to impaired production of ACh and reduced release 
into the synapse [23]. Lower ACh in the synaptic cleft results in 
reduced activation of mAChR and nAChR, leading to weakened 
synaptic signaling and disrupted coordination of neural 
networks. This cholinergic deficit also impairs mechanisms of synaptic plasticity, such as long-
term potentiation, which is important for memory formation and learning. Remarkably, Aβ 
peptides can further affect ACh’s release, by binding and disrupting the function of nAChRs 
and so impairing the release of ACh in the brain. Moreover, Aβ has shown to decrease ChAT

and promote the activation of achetilocholinesterase, which breaks down ACh, in the 
hippocampus, prefrontal cortex, and amygdala causing additional reduction of ACh. All these 
effects together cause a more profound depletion of ACh levels. Ultimately, this widespread 
cholinergic dysfunction diminishes neuronal communication and survival, initiating a cascade 
of secondary neurodegeneration beyond the basal forebrain, extending to widespread cortical 
and hippocampal regions, producing the global brain atrophy observed in severe AD  [24], [25]. 
Neuroinflammation in AD is characterized by a sustained inflammatory process within the 
Central Nervous System (CNS), evidenced by the chronic activation of glial cells [4] and the 
persistent production of pro-inflammatory mediators such as TNF-α, interleukin 6 (IL-6) and 
interleukin 1α (IL-1α). Contrary to earlier studies, that described neuroinflammation as a 
consequence of Aβ plaques and NFTs, recent research indicates its active role in fostering the 
pathogenesis [26]. In fact, Aβ aggregates directly trigger microglial activation, driving their 
migration toward amyloid plaques and contributing to the local inflammatory response. 
Initially, phagocytosis of Aβ aggregates and their removal is attempted. However, chronic 
exposure to both Aβ and inflammatory cytokines, causes microglial dysfunction, reducing their 
effectiveness in handling Aβ accumulation [4]. This dysfunction further amplifies 
neuroinflammation by promoting the release of reactive oxygen species, and proteolytic 
enzymes, which contribute to neuronal degeneration [27]. This pro-inflammatory 
environment destabilizes the integrity of BBB, enabling peripheral immune cell infiltration that 
further amplifies CNS inflammation [28], [29]. The involvement of microglia in inflammation 
and the development of pathology will be explored in greater detail in the following sections. 
The brain-gut axis is an endless bidirectional communication system between the 
gastrointestinal tract and the CNS [30]. In AD, the gut microbiota-brain axis hypothesis 
proposes that microbial imbalances, or as called dysbiosis, disrupt immune, neural, and 
metabolic pathways, thus causing the characteristic neurodegeneration seen in AD [11], [31], 
[32]. It is known that gut microbiota diversity declines as humans age and that leads to an 
increase in harmful bacteria and a reduction in beneficial species [28]. This imbalance disrupts 
the integrity of the gut barrier [32], so that bacterial toxins like lipopolysaccharides and pro-
inflammatory cytokines like TNF-α and IL-6 can reach systemic circulation [30], As a result, 
chronic low-grade inflammation, termed inflammaging, becomes established. This condition 
compromises integrity of the BBB and as mentioned above, inflammatory molecules invade 
the brain, exacerbating neuroinflammation. Lastly, amyloids of certain gut bacteria, e.g., curli 
fibers of Escherichia coli, share the same cross-β sheet structure of Aβ peptides, which 
promotes the formation of stable and self-assembling fibers. This structural similarity suggests 
that bacterial amyloids can act as a template, inducing Aβ aggregation and plaque formation 
in the brain [30]. 
Last, the oxidative stress hypothesis suggests that an imbalance between the production of 
reactive oxygen species (ROS) and the brain's antioxidant defense mechanisms contributes to 
both neurodegeneration [33] and impaired synaptic activity [4]. More specifically, this 
imbalance causes a cumulative damage to cellular components such as lipids, proteins, and 
nucleic acids [33] with neurons being particularly vulnerable due to their high metabolic 
activity and demand for oxygen [34]. Notably, mitochondrial dysfunction is involved in the 
increase of ROS, Tau hyperphosphorylation and Aβ deposition. These Aβ aggregates can bind 
to copper and iron metals, generating free radicals. Free radicals are unstable molecules 
capable of initiating oxidative cellular injury [4], [33]. This creates a self-propagating process, 
as the free cellular components continue to amplify ROS generation [35]. Although the precise 
==PAGE:15==
7 
 
timing of oxidative stress in the cascade of AD pathogenesis is debatable, its central role in 
neuronal damage make it a critical determinant in understanding and potentially modulating 
the disease progress [36]. 
Despite growing insights into the complex biological mechanisms underlying AD, there are still 
no treatments capable of halting or reversing its progression. Treatment strategies have long 
focused on Aβ and NFT pathology, yet numerous initially promising treatments fail in clinical 
trials. This may be since Aβ and NFT phenotypes reflect later stages of the disease, whereas 
the pathology likely begins decades before these markers or any clinical symptoms emerge. 
This underscores the critical gap in knowledge between early disease events and late-stage 
pathology [3]. 
SNP-Based Genetic Risk Assessment in Alzheimer's Disease Cell Models 
Identification of genetic risk loci for AD has been greatly advanced by genome-wide association 
studies (GWAS), which scan the genome in a systematic manner to detect single nucleotide 
polymorphisms (SNPs) that are associated with increased disease risk [37], [38]. SNPs are the 
most common form of genetic variation in the human genome. As SNP is called the 
substitution of a single nucleotide at a specific site in the genome where this variation is 
present in at least 1% of the population. These variations can be classified based on the type 
of nucleotide substitution as transitions (C/T or G/A) or transversions (C/G, A/T, C/A, or T/G). 
SNPs are found all over the genome, on average once every 1,000 nucleotides, and are widely 
used as biological markers in genetics studies to identify disease or other trait-associated 
genes [39], [40]. Though most SNPs have no disease-causing role, they can have impacts on 
how individuals react to drugs, environmental exposures [41], and disease susceptibility. SNPs 
located within or near genes may affect gene function, hence altering biological processes or 
disease susceptibility [42]. 
In the context of AD, the joint effect of multiple risk and protective SNPs can be quantified 
using polygenic risk scores (PRS), which provide a summary measure of an individual's genetic 
risk for the disease [43]. While the APOE locus accounts for a significant percentage of genetic 
risk, non-APOE loci collectively contribute a greater share of heritability and reflect the 
complex genetic architecture of AD [37], [43]. GWAS and PRS have enabled researchers to 
group individuals and biological samples, such as cell lines, based on their genetic risk profiles. 
This stratification is particularly valuable in experimental models, where information about the 
underlying SNP landscape can be leveraged to guide interpretation of functional assays and 
the selection of appropriate samples [43], [44].  
On the bases of these methodological foundations, several genetic variants have been defined 
as risk factors of late-onset Alzheimer's disease (LOAD), and large variability between 
populations has been reported. Some examples include variants in the CR1 gene, which have 
modest but statistically significant risk increases in LOAD, particularly among East Asians, with 
odds ratios (ORs) ranging between 1.18 and 1.32 [45],[46]. This genetic susceptibility trend is 
echoed with the rs3764650 variant in the ABCA7/CNN2 locus, where the GT heterozygous 
genotype carriers are at heightened risk, and ORs fall between 1.20 and 1.43 [47], [48]. 
Similarly, the rs3752246 variant in the ABCA7 gene itself has consistently been linked with AD,

Glutamatergic Neurons in Alzheimer’s Disease 
In AD, multiple neurotransmission systems are affected, including the cholinergic, 
glutamatergic, and dopaminergic pathways. These systems are interconnected each 
modulating the function of the others. Glutamatergic neurons are most prevalent in the CNS 
and reside in the frontal cortex. Their loss induces tremendous atrophy in the brain. These 
neurons play a critical role in synaptic plasticity, learning, and memory via the release of 
glutamate. Glutamate is an amino acid [54] that serves as the brain’s major excitatory 
neurotransmitter [55]. It is produced within glutamatergic neurons [56] and is involved in 
neurogenesis, neurite outgrowth, synaptogenesis and potentially in neurotoxicity. The 
glutamate levels are tightly controlled by the glutamate/GABA-glutamine cycle, [57] that relies 
upon the dynamic interaction between glutamatergic neurons and astrocytes. Gamma-
AminoButyric Acid (GABA) is the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter in the CNS, 
counterbalancing the excitatory effects of glutamate. This balance is crucial for maintaining 
neuronal excitability and synaptic stability [58],  [56].  
The cycle is based on the interplay of neurotransmission and cellular metabolism. At its core 
is glucose, which is the primary energy fuel for neurons. Through glycolysis, glucose is 
transformed into pyruvate, which is, in turn, converted by pyruvate dehydrogenase (PDH) to 
acetyl-CoA for maintenance of the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle. One of the TCA cycle 
intermediates, α-ketoglutarate is not only engaged in energy metabolism but also in the 
synthesis of glutamate. Astrocytes are central to this cycle. They take up excess glutamate 
released from synapses and also collect components of GABA, effectively clearing 
neurotransmitters from the extracellular space. To replenish neuronal stores, astrocytes 
convert glutamate into glutamine via the enzyme glutamine synthetase (GS). This glutamine is 
then released into the extracellular space and taken up by neurons. Then, it is converted back 
into glutamate by phosphate-activated glutaminase (PAG). In GABAergic neurons, glutamate 
can be further converted into GABA through the action of glutamate decarboxylase (GAD) [55].

Glutamate receptors are broadly classified into two types: ionotropic (iGluRs) and 
metabotropic (mGluRs). The metabotropic receptors belong to the family of G proteins, while 
the ionotropic ones belong to the ligand-gated ion channels. N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) 
and α-amino-3-hydroxy-5-methyl-4-isoxazolpropionic acid (AMPA) receptors belong to the 
ionotropic family [54]. The activation of NMDA requires, besides the binding of glutamate, a 
co-agonist, either glycine or D-serine, to be present as well. Last step, involves the 
depolarization of the membrane through the release of Mg ²⁺ ions from the channel. After the 
activation of receptor, the channel allows the flow of Na⁺, K⁺ and the big influx of Ca²⁺. Ca²⁺ 
serves as a secondary messenger activating intracellular signaling cascades involved in long-
term potentiation. Though NMDA receptors are involved in synaptic plasticity and neuronal 
viability, their overactivation could lead to negative effects such as excitotoxicity, 
neurodegeneration and eventual cell death. This is because high concentrations of Ca²⁺ 
activate catabolizing enzymes which augment the formation of ROS and breakdown of 
neuronal cytoskeleton [59].  
Human induced pluripotent stem cells 
Stem cells are undifferentiated cells that can self-replicate and differentiate into various 
specialized types of cells. They are categorized into two main groups based on the origin and 
differentiation potency. The first classification distinguishes between embryonic and adult 
tissue-derived stem cells. These tissue specific stem cells can generally differentiate only into 
a limited range of related cell types within a specific tissue or organ and include: Mesenchymal 
stem cells (MSCs), Hematopoietic stem cells, Neural stem cells and epithelial stem cells. For 
example, neural stem cells can differentiate into nerve cells and mesenchymal stem cells can 
become osteoblasts or myocytes. The second classification includes MSCs, induced pluripotent 
stem cells (iPSCs), and embryonic stem cells (ESCs). MSCs are collected from adult tissues like 
bone marrow and are considered multipotent due to their efficiency to differentiate into 
tissues associated with the mesoderm germ layer. On the other hand, iPSCs and ESCs are not 
characterized by multipotency but pluripotency instead, meaning that they can differentiate 
into all the 3 germ layers [60]. During embryogenesis, the zygote undergoes mitotic cell 
==PAGE:18==
10 
 
division, transforming into a blastocyst. Within the blastocyst, the inner cell mass (ICM) 
possesses pluripotency, as it can develop into the epiblast and subsequently give rise to one 
of the three germ layers: endoderm, mesoderm, or ectoderm [61]. This process is called 
gastrulation and enables the generation of human organs [62]. The pluripotent state persists 
for several days during early development but is lost during gastrulation, as epiblast cells 
commit to specific fates, differentiating into mesoderm, endoderm, or ectoderm lineages [63]. 
The ectoderm is the outermost germ layer, initially formed by the superficial cells of the ICM. 
It subsequently differentiates into tissues primarily associated with the external environment 
and the nervous system. The surface ectoderm gives rise to the epidermis, hair, nails, olfactory 
epithelium and lens of the eye [64], while the neuroectoderm develops into the neural tube, 
which forms the CNS, and the neural crest [65] which contributes to the peripheral nervous 
system and melanocytes [64]. The mesoderm, the middle germ layer, is formed by cells 
migrating inward during gastrulation. It develops into structural and connective tissues, 
including the musculoskeletal system (bones and muscles), the cardiovascular system (heart, 
blood, and blood vessels), and various connective tissues throughout the body [66]. Finally, 
the endoderm is the innermost germ layer, originating from cells of the ICM that migrate to 
form the primitive gut tube as well as components of the respiratory system. It plays a vital 
role in forming organs like the thyroid, thymus, lungs, liver, biliary system, and pancreas [13]. 
In fact, ICM is the origin for human Embryonic Stem Cells (hESCs) which are characterized by 
their ability to self-renew and maintain pluripotency [67]. Although, there have been multiple 
hESC lines derived from donated embryos [68] their use has been limited due to ethical 
considerations. Instead, hiPSCs have been suggested as a replacement to hESCs sharing many 
similarities such as pluripotency, self- renewal and the expression of specific surface markers 
[69]. These markers are used to identify pluripotency of stem cells and include both surface 
associated antigens along with transcription factors [70]. The nuclear transcription factors that 
are tested for pluripotency in human Pluripotent Stem Cells (hPSCs) include: Oct4, Nanog and 
Sox2. Whereas, the surface antigens that are examined include: the keratan sulfate antigens 
Tra-1-60 and Tra-1-81, as well as  the glycolipid antigens SSEA3 and SSEA4 [71].  
The generation of hiPSCs involves reprogramming somatic cells, such as fibroblasts, into a 
pluripotent state. One widely used method involves introducing the Yamanaka factors [72], 
named after the researcher that firstly connented their capabilities: Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, and Myc 
[73], also known as OSKM, through viral vectors. Retroviral and Lentiviral vectors are 
commonly used for their efficiency and stability, as they integrate into the host genome. This 
approach though can result in insertional mutagenesis. To avoid this, non-integrating vectors 
such as adenovirus or adeno-associated virus are employed.  Additionally, mRNA, transposons, 
and episomal plasmids offer alternative, to activate cellular pluripotency genes without 
integrating into the host genome [72].  
hiPSCs are favored over hESCs not only because they address ethical concerns, but also 
because they can be generated from adult somatic cells of the individual patient, rendering 
them patient-specific. This specificity is a major advantage, as hiPSCs retain the donor’s unique 
genetic background and are less likely to trigger immune rejection when used for 
transplantation or disease modeling, enabling more precise personalized medicine and 
therapeutic applications [74]. However, they present certain limitations and most notably, 
residual epigenetic memory. This refers to the epigenetic traits retained from their original 
somatic state, which can influence differentiation and potentially guide cells toward a specific 
lineage. Notably, different DNA methylation patterns are observed between hiPSCs and hESCs.

Last, subtle variations in the expression of mRNA and micro-RNA molecules have been 
reported [69]. 
Research Models for Studying AD 
Experimental models are valuable in advancing our understanding of AD pathology and in 
conducting preclinical evaluations of potential therapies [75]. Over the past decades, a variety 
of cellular and animal models have been developed to investigate the intricate pathological 
processes underlying AD. Among the most commonly used in vivo models are transgenic mice 
that have been engineered to carry human mutations associated with fAD [3] particularly in 
the APP gene alone or in combination with PSEN1. These models successfully replicate the 
development of hallmark AD features, such as Aβ plaques and NFTs [75].  However, they have 
notable limitations and a major one is their inability to reproduce both NTFs and neuronal loss. 
Additionally, they fail to model the mechanisms underlying sAD, which is the most prevalent 
form of the disease and lacks a clear genetic cause [3].  
In vitro approaches, on the other hand, allow to study the disease in a more controlled 
environment, with lower costs and simpler handling compared to in vivo models. Some 
common 2D cellular models include: SH-SY5Y cells, PC12 [76], iPSCs and induced neurons (iNs) 
[77]. SH-SY5Y cells are isolated from human neuroblastoma cancer cells and remain 
undifferentiated until induced, resembling the immature cholinergic neuronal cells [78]. If 
treated with retinoic acid and BDNF [77] they differentiate into mature cholinergic cells [78]. 
This 2D model can be used to study the mechanisms behind AD such as Αβ formation and 
oxidative stress [76]. PC-12 cells derive from rat pheochromocytoma and after stimulation with 
nerve growth factor [77] they differentiate into cholinergic neurons [79]. They are valuable for 
modeling Aβ-induced toxicity, as they exhibit increased oxidative stress and neuronal death in 
response. The immortalized cell lines are valuable research tools due to their ease of handling, 
low cost, and reproducibility. However, their cancerous origin limits their translational 
relevance, as they don’t fully capture the genetic and phenotypic complexity of human 
neurons [77].  
In AD though, the earliest affected brain regions are the neocortex and hippocampus. These 
areas are primarily composed of pyramidal and glutamatergic neurons [80], which explains 
why current research focuses on generating these specific neuronal subtypes to study disease 
pathology. This has been achieved by reprogramming somatic cells derived from patients with 
fAD and sAD into hiPSCs, which are then differentiated into neurons. The generated neurons 
recapitulate both high Αβ production and tau hyperphosphorylation [3]. Another useful tool 
is the Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats (CRISPR) technique for 
editing specific genes associated with the disease [78]. Overall, iPSC- derived neurons provide 
disease-relevant model due to their patient-specific origin, providing insights into both genetic 
and sporadic forms of AD [77].  
Another potential 2D cellular model is induced neurons (iNs), which are derived directly from 
somatic cells of patients with AD. Unlike hiPSCs, which are first reverted to a pluripotent state, 
iNs bypass this step, offering the advantage of faster and more efficient neuron generation. 
However, it is speculated that this direct conversion might impact their maturation process 
and functional properties [77]. 
To better replicate the complex and heterogeneous environment of the human brain, 3D 
culture systems have been established [77]. Among these, cerebral organoids produced from 
==PAGE:20==
12 
 
hiPSCs are used, carrying mutations for both fAD and sAD [3]. Their spherical shape promotes 
Aβ aggregation and consequently the formation of plaques, the development of NFTs [77]. 
Additionally, these organoids exhibit endosomal abnormalities and synaptic loss, key 
pathological features of AD [3]. Brain organoids are valuable tools for studying 
neuroinflammation in AD, as they can be co-cultured with neuroprotective cells such as 
microglia and astrocytes, providing a platform to examine cell interactions in a highly diverse 
cellular environment. However, one of the major drawbacks of this model is its poor 
reproducibility efficiency [77].  
In our study sAD and control hiPSCs will be used and differentiate into mature glutamatergic 
neurons. Neuroinflammation will be examined by the addition of microglia cell medium into 
the cultured neurons simulating the neuroinflammatory microenvironment. Microglia-
conditioned media (MCM), especially when derived from activated microglia, typically 
contains a complex mixture of bioactive molecules that influence neuronal behavior. These 
include pro-inflammatory cytokines and chemokines. By treating hiPSC-derived glutamatergic 
neurons with MCM, the impact of this inflammatory milieu on neuronal viability, synaptic 
architecture, and stress pathways will be studied by capturing essential features of AD in a 
human-relevant in vitro system. 
Directed differentiation of hiPSCs to glutamatergic neurons 
The direct differentiation of human induced pluripotent stem cells (hiPSCs) into glutamatergic 
neurons follows a modified protocol based on [81]. This multistep process begins with the 
derivation of Neural Progenitor Cells (NPCs) from hiPSCs, followed by an extended expansion 
phase. Ultimately, this leads to the generation of fully mature, electrophysiologically active 
neurons capable of forming functional excitatory synaptic networks [82]. During neural 
induction, inhibition of dual Sma- and Mad- related protein (SMAD) signaling pathway plays a 
key role. Specifically, two SMAD pathways are inhibited, the transforming growth factor-β 
(TGF-β) and the bone morphogenic protein (BMP) ones [83]. BMPs are cytokines that function 
as growth factors. Among them, BMP-4 plays a significant role in early mesoderm and germ 
cell formation, alongside in the formation of lungs and liver [84], while suppresses 
neurogenesis [85]. As a result, inhibition of the BMP-4 signaling promotes neurogenesis, the 
formation of the neural plate [84] and blocks alternative differentiation. 
The most widely established BMP-4 and TGF-β signaling inhibitors for enhancing neural 
differentiation are the LDN193189 and SB431542. LDN193189 selectively inhibits BMP-type I 
receptors suppressing the activation of SMAD1/5/9, while SB431542 targets TGF-β type I 
receptors reducing SMAD2/3 activity [86].  
It is known that human nervous system development starts with neural induction, where 
ectodermal cells give rise to neuroectoderm, forming the neural plate and eventually the 
neural tube [87]. During the induction and expansion stages, neural rosettes begin to form, 
reflecting key stages of early neurulation [88]. These rosettes consist of round or oval cells 
arranged in a circular, spoke-like pattern surrounding a central lumen, closely resembling the 
early architecture of the neural tube, the precursor to the central nervous system. Cellular 
extensions grow outward from the rosette, indicating the initial stages of neuronal 
development [87]. NPCs are characterized by positive immunostaining for the 
neuroectodermal markers Nestin, PAX6, SOX1 [89] and SOX2 [90]. In contrast, the pluripotency 
markers of hiPSCs, SSEA-4, OCT3/4, and NANOG [89], are not expressed by NPCs. 
==PAGE:21==
13 
 
For the NPC expansion stage, dual SMAD inhibitors are removed and replaced by Epidermal 
Growth Factor (EGF) and Fibroblast Growth Factor (FGF), which are important factors for 
neurogenesis and neuronal differentiation [91]. In the next steps, to achieve the complete 
maturation of NPCs to electrophysiological activated glutamatergic neurons, EGF and FGF are 
removed and the media is supplemented by Brain derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), glial 
cell-line neurotrophic factor (GDNF), Ascorbic Acid (AA) and dibutyryl-cyclic Adenosine 
monophosphate (db-Camp). BDNF is a neurotrophic factor that promotes neural growth [92], 
maturation [93] and survival [92]. GDNF supports neuronal maintenance [94], CNS repair and 
neuronal specification [95]. AA enhances the differentiation of NPCs by modulating ROS 
metabolism, which in turn activates redox-sensitive signaling pathways such as WNT/β-
catenin. This promotes the expression of genes involved in neuronal development and 
increases cellular responsiveness to signals that direct neuronal fate decisions [96]. Last, db-
cAMP is a synthetic, membrane-permeable analog of cAMP [97] that also promotes the 
maturation of NPCs [98]. cAMP functions as an intracellular second messenger and has been 
associated with the induction of neuron-like morphology through the activation of protein 
kinase A signaling pathways [99]. 
In AD, glutamatergic neurons are particularly vulnerable to inflammatory signals, oxidative 
stress, and excitotoxicity. By subjecting hiPSC-derived glutamatergic neurons to microglia-
conditioned supernatant, it is possible to elucidate the impact of inflammatory cytokines and 
glial-secreted factors on neuronal survival, morphology, and gene expression. This helps 
unravel the complex interactions between glial cells and neurons that contribute to disease 
pathology. 
Microglia 
Microglia are the permanent macrophages  of the CNS [100], providing innate immune 
support. Contrary to neurons and glial cells which originate from ectodermal layer, microglia 
originate from the yolk-sac, a mesodermal structure [101]. In the embryonic yolk-sac, microglia 
exist as erythromyeloid progenitors. These progenitors emerge during early embryogenesis, 
around embryonic day 8 in mice [102], and around 4–5 weeks of gestation in humans [103]. 
Then they migrate through the neural tube to the brain [101], before the fetal liver becomes 
the primary hematopoietic organ. This corresponds to day 9 in mice and between gestational 
weeks 2 and 8 in humans [3]. Moreover, unlike most tissue macrophages, microglia do not 
depend on hematopoietic stem cells from the fetal liver for their maintenance [104]. The CNS 
does not receive any further precursors from the fetal liver of postnatal hematopoiesis due to 
the maturation of the BBB [105]. 
During embryogenesis, microglia are primarily localized in areas of active cortical formation, 
where they support neurogenesis and phagocytose neural precursor cells. They also 
contribute to synaptic pruning, aiding in the formation of neural networks by eliminating 
dendritic spines and excessive excitatory synapses. This explain their detrimental effect on 
brain and behavioral development [3]. 
In the healthy adult brain, the function and phenotype of microglia are shaped by their specific 
anatomical location and the signals they receive from the surrounding microenvironment. 
Microglia have small cell bodies with several ramified and motile processes that contribute to 
the clearance of cell debris and waste products [3]. Their role includes the phagocytosis of 
misfolded proteins and apoptotic cells maintaining the homeostasis within the CNS [100]. They 
dynamically communicate with other cell types in the CNS, as their maturation and 
==PAGE:22==
14 
 
surveillance rely on signaling from neurons, astrocytes and oligodendrocytes. Microglia 
secrete BDNF, which is involved in synaptic plasticity. A shortage of BDNF has been linked to 
memory and learning deficits in mice [3].  
Under pathological circumstances, microglia undergo a transformation into an amoeboid 
shape [106] characterized by enlarged cell bodies [3] and reduced branching. This 
morphological change compromises their ability to effectively survey their environment [106]. 
During aging, these changes are further accompanied by reduced motility and a decline in 
their neuroprotective functions. Chronic activation of microglia leads to the production of ROS, 
inflammatory cytokines and proteases, which have a detrimental impact on neighboring cells 
[3]. Figure 6 illustrates the dual role of microglia, highlighting their supportive functions under 
physiological conditions and their harmful effects on neurons during pathological states such 
as neuroinflammation.

Microglia are closely associated with AD pathology, as they are found colocalized with Aβ 
plaques and Tau aggregates [106] in post-mortem brain tissue from AD patients. In the early 
stages of the disease, ramified microglia play a protective role, surveying the CNS  and 
migrating to the areas of Aβ deposits [3]. Activated microglia engulf these harmful species 
through processes like pinocytosis, phagocytosis, or receptor-mediated endocytosis and they 
attempt to break them down using various endocytic pathways [106]. However, with 
prolonged exposure to Aβ plaques, microglia adopt a dysfunctional phenotype, becoming 
chronically activated and toxic to the surrounding cells [3]. In more details, when amyloid-β 
aggregates are recognized by microglia, using surface sensors called pattern recognition 
receptors (PRRs), two main pathways are triggered. The phagocytic pathway enhances the 
microglia's ability to engulf and break down Aβ. On the other hand, the inflammatory pathway 
through the activation of the nuclear factor-κB (NF-κB) it leads to the production of pro-
inflammatory cytokines (IL-1b) and the formation of the NLRP3 inflammasome structure, 
which is a multiprotein complex. This inflammasome can release apoptosis-associated speck-
like protein (ASC), which can help Aβ clump together into harmful aggregates by serving as a 
core for their aggregation. The pro-inflammatory cytokines further stimulate glial cell 
activation, contribute to neuronal damage, and enhance the activity of BACE, the enzyme 
responsible for cleaving APP to produce the neurotoxic fragment sAPPβ [106]. 
In AD, microglia often exhibit a dual phenotype, expressing both pro-inflammatory and anti-
inflammatory markers. However, as the disease progresses, this balance tends to shift toward 
a pro-inflammatory state, which contributes to neuronal damage. In this altered environment, 
the microglial secretome, the supernatant in particular, contains elevated levels of pro-
inflammatory cytokines, rendering neurons more susceptible to cell death. The overall impact 
of AD microglia on neuronal survival depends on the balance between harmful and 
neuroprotective factors within their secretome [107]. While pro-inflammatory molecules such 
as TNF-α, IL-1β, and IL-6 generally dominate, some microglia may still release compensatory 
anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective factors like IL-10, TGF-β, and neurotrophic molecules, 
which can help mitigate damage. Importantly, even without external stimulation, microglia in 
AD brains display a distinct secretory profile compared to those in healthy individuals. 
Homeostatic microglia in non-AD brains primarily secrete factors that support neuronal health 
and function  [108]. 
Lipopolysaccharide (LPS), a bacterial endotoxin, is a widely used activator of microglia and 
typically induces a strong pro-inflammatory response [109]. Upon LPS stimulation, microglia 
increase the secretion of cytokines such as TNF-α, IL-1β, and IL-6, which are generally 
neurotoxic. These cytokines can promote neuronal apoptosis, activate the NF-κB signaling 
pathway, and suppress anti-apoptotic gene expression [110], [111]. However, microglia also 
produce neuroprotective factors, under certain conditions [112]. Studies have shown that anti-
inflammatory cytokines like IL-4 and IL-10 can mitigate or even reverse the neurotoxic effects 
induced by LPS and other pro-inflammatory signals [109]. 
In recent years, the regulation of apoptosis has become a major focus in AD research due to 
its critical role in disease progression.  Apoptosis is tightly controlled by a complex network of 
genes, including members of the BCL2 family. Notably, the anti-apoptotic protein BCL2 has 
been found to be deficient in degenerating neurons in post-mortem AD brain tissue, 
suggesting a failure in the cellular mechanisms that normally protect against cell death [113]. 
LPS, has been reported to downregulate the expression of BCL2  [114], but cells can attempt 
to compensate for reduced BCL2 protein levels by increasing BCL2 mRNA transcription [115].

Neurons are not passive, when they are exposed to stress, healthy neurons activate survival 
pathways, such as upregulation of anti-apoptotic genes, as an early protective response [116]. 
Yet, when stress is severe, prolonged, or occurs in already compromised neurons, as in the 
case of AD, these compensatory mechanisms may fail. This can lead to a decrease in BCL2 
expression, an increase in pro-apoptotic proteins, and ultimately, enhanced neuronal cell 
death [117]. 
Apoptosis is a highly regulated form of programmed cell death that is required for 
development and maintenance of multicellular organisms. Unlike necrosis, a form of 
uncontrolled cell death resulting from acute cellular injury, apoptosis is a controlled and 
energy-dependent process. It allows cells to undergo suicide in response to specific signals, 
ensuring tissue homeostasis as well as orchestrating normal development [118]. Two primary 
pathways exist through which apoptosis is initiated: 
The intrinsic or mitochondrial pathway of apoptosis is activated in response to intrinsic cellular 
stress, such as DNA damage, oxidative stress, or lack of survival signals. This pathway involves 
the release of cytochrome c from the mitochondria into the cytosol, leading to the activation 
of caspase-9. Caspase-9, in turn, initiates a cascade that activates executioner caspases, 
ultimately leading to programmed cell death. On the other hand, the extrinsic or death 
receptor pathway is triggered by external signals, such as the binding of death ligands like TNF-
α to their corresponding cell surface receptors. This interaction triggers the formation of the 
death-inducing signaling complex (DISC) and activates caspase-8, which in turn initiates 
downstream executioner caspases responsible for apoptosis [118].  
When neurons are exposed to stress, such as oxidative stress, hypoxia, or inflammatory 
stimuli, the transcription factor NF-κB is typically activated. This response is well-documented 
and is considered part of the neuron’s intrinsic defense mechanism against cellular stress 
[119], [120]. NF-κB plays a dual role in regulating cell fate, particularly in balancing survival and 
apoptosis. It is best known for its anti-apoptotic function, primarily through the upregulation 
of genes encoding survival proteins, including members of the BCL2 family. This protective role 
is especially evident in response to pro-inflammatory signals like TNF-α. Upon activation, NF-
κB promotes the transcription of genes that inhibit the apoptotic cascade, such as those that 
block caspase-8 activation, thereby preventing the initiation of programmed cell death [121], 
[122]. 
Nuclear factor kappa-light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells (NF-κB) 
Under resting conditions, NF-κB exists in the cytoplasm as an inactive complex composed of 
three subunits: p50, p65 (RelA), and an inhibitory protein known as IκB [123]. The canonical 
NF-κB pathway, which mediates the innate immune response, is activated by variety of pro-
inflammatory stimuli, including microbial products such as LPS and viral protein, cytokines 
such as TNFα, IL-1, and IL-6, as well as physical and chemical stressors. These signals converge 
on the activation of the kinase TAK1, which subsequently activates the IKK complex, composed 
of IKKα, IKKβ, and the regulatory subunit IKKγ. Once activated, the IKK complex phosphorylates 
IκB proteins, targeting them for ubiquitination and subsequent proteasomal degradation 
[124]. The degradation of IκB releases the p50–p65 dimer, allowing it to translocate into the 
nucleus where it binds to specific DNA sequences to regulate gene expression [123]. NF-κB 
plays a vital role in various neuronal functions, influencing both normal brain activity and 
responses to pathological conditions. It is involved in synaptic plasticity, growth factor 
signaling, and cognitive functions such as learning and memory. NF-κB can mediate either 
==PAGE:25==
17 
 
neuroprotective or neurodegenerative outcomes, depending on the cellular context and 
nature of the stimulus [125]. 
The transcription factor NF-κB supports neuronal survival through several mechanisms. One 
of the most prominent roles is the upregulation of antiapoptotic genes, such 
as BCL2 and IAPs (inhibitor of apoptosis proteins), which inhibit the activation of caspases and 
block mitochondrial apoptosis pathways [124]. Particularly, NF-κB plays a pivotal role in 
regulating neuronal survival and apoptosis by modulating the expression of BCL2 family 
proteins, particularly BCL2 and Bax. BCL2 functions as an anti-apoptotic protein, while Bax 
promotes apoptosis, and the balance between them critically determines neuronal fate [126]. 
Upon activation, NF-κB upregulates anti-apoptotic genes such as BCL2, thereby enhancing 
neuronal resistance to stressors like oxidative damage and excitotoxicity. BCL2 inhibits BAX 
oligomerization at the mitochondrial membrane, preventing cytochrome c release and the 
initiation of the apoptotic cascade [127]. This inhibition is not complete, however, and when 
BAX is overexpressed to an extent that the BCL2 inhibitory potential is overwhelmed, then 
some of the BAX molecules will evade inhibition and cause apoptosis [128]. Conversely, under 
certain stress conditions, such as TNF-α exposure, NF-κB can promote apoptosis by 
upregulating BAX expression and downregulating BCL2, thereby promoting cell death [129]. 
There are some pathological cases in which exposure to severe stress upregulates both BAX 
and BCL2, as part of a general stress response. The final outcome, survival or apoptosis, will 
then depend on which protein exerts greater functional influence [130]. The BAX/BCL2 ratio 
emerges as a key indicator of neuronal viability, with a higher ratio favoring apoptosis. NF-κB 
influences this ratio through context-dependent transcriptional control. Experimental 
inhibition of NF-κB has shown to lower the BAX/BCL2 ratio, offering neuroprotection against 
inflammation-induced apoptosis. Thus, NF-κB serves as a critical molecular switch that 
influences neuronal fate in both physiological and pathological conditions [129].

Beyond apoptosis regulation, NF-κB also supports neuronal health by inducing antioxidant 
defenses. It upregulates manganese superoxide dismutase (Mn-SOD), an enzyme that 
neutralizes ROS, thereby reducing oxidative damage and preserving neuronal integrity [123]. 
Additionally, NF-κB promotes the expression of neurotrophic factors such as BDNF and insulin-
like growth factor-2 (IGF-2), both of which are essential for neuronal growth, survival, and 
synaptic maintenance [132]. 
NF-κB also plays a pivotal role in synaptic plasticity, the cellular basis of learning and memory. 
It enhances synaptic strength by upregulating postsynaptic density protein-95 (PSD-95) [132], 
which anchors AMPA receptors at the synapse to boost synaptic transmission [133]. Another 
important target is spine-associated RapGAP (SPAR), which is involved in actin remodeling and 
dendritic spine enlargement, thereby stabilizing synaptic structures [134].  
Persistent NF-κB activation also leads to increased expression of cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), a 
pro-inflammatory enzyme whose gene contains NF-κB binding sites [135].  While COX-2 
contributes to normal synaptic function, its overexpression which is often triggered by 
oxidative stress or excitotoxicity, exacerbates neuroinflammation. This occurs through 
excessive production of prostaglandins and increased oxidative damage, both of which 
contribute to neuronal injury. Dysregulated COX-2 activity has been implicated in cognitive 
decline and the progression of neurodegenerative diseases [136]. 
Finally, NF-κB directly regulates the expression of APOE, a gene with critical implications in 
sAD. The APOE promoter contains functional NF-κB binding sites, and increased NF-κB activity 
has been shown to enhance APOE transcription in neural cells [137]. Notably, 
the APOE4 isoform is associated with heightened NF-κB activation and a stronger 
inflammatory response, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of neuroinflammation and APOE 
expression. This feedback loop contributes to the pathogenesis of sAD by exacerbating 
neuronal damage and impairing the brain’s repair mechanisms [138].

Aim of the project 
Directed differentiation of hiPSCs into glutamatergic neurons and exposure to microglia-
conditioned media provide a robust in vitro system for investigating neuroinflammatory 
mechanisms in sAD. The primary aim of this research is to evaluate the impact of microglial 
secretomes, that are derived from control and sAD hiPSC lines both with and without 
inflammatory stimulation, on neuronal survival, morphology, and apoptotic signaling. To 
achieve this, the expression levels of key apoptotic markers, BAX and BCL2, will be quantified 
using qPCR, elucidating this way the role of inflammation-induced apoptosis in AD pathology. 
This goal can be divided into the following objectives:  
i. Differentiation of hiPSCs to mature glutamatergic neurons  
By dual SMAD inhibition and stepwise maturation protocol, control and sAD donor-derived 
hiPSCs were converted into functional glutamatergic neurons. Neuron mortality and maturity 
were confirmed by immunocytochemistry with β-tubulin III and MAP2 markers.  
ii. Replication of neuroinflammatory conditions by microglia-conditioned media  
Neurons were exposed to microglia conditioned media from control and sAD microglial lines, 
resting and LPS-stimulated, to mimic neuroinflammatory conditions. Differences in 
morphology and survival under conditions were quantified.  
iii. Examination of apoptosis-associated gene expression by qPCR  
To study inflammation-mediated apoptosis, RNA was extracted from neurons following 
treatment and analyzed for BCL2 (anti-apoptotic) and BAX (pro-apoptotic) mRNA levels by RT-
qPCR. The BAX/BCL2 ratio was used as a marker of apoptotic susceptibility.  
iv. Comparative study of neuronal susceptibility between genetic backgrounds  
By comparing sAD-derived and control neurons, the research aimed to establish genotype-
dependent differences in susceptibility to apoptosis and neurotoxicity caused by microglia, 
thus highlighting the interaction between inflammatory stress and genetic risk.  
Hypothesis: 
It is hypothesized that when the glutamatergic neurons will be exposed to secretomes of the 
cultured microglia, particularly those stimulated with LPS and derive from higher-risk sAD cell 
lines, they will experience a stronger neurotoxic effect compared to the neurons that will be 
exposed to secretomes of unstimulated microglia and derive from control cell lines. This 
response is expected to be translated as altered morphology and increased apoptotic signaling 
indicated by a higher BAX/BCL2 ratio. On the other hand, when neurons will be exposed to 
microglia secretome of unstimulated cell lines, it is the microglial genetic background that will 
drive the neuronal response. For example, neurons treated with unstimulated microglia 
supernatant of control cell lines will not be affected, whereas when treated with microglia 
supernatant of higher-risk sAD cell lines they will show high BAX levels and hypothetically low 
BCL2 levels. Treatment with lower-risk sAD microglial secretomes is expected to produce 
intermediate effects. Another potential observation could be that neurons originated from 
higher-risk sAD lines might exhibit impaired compensatory responses to inflammatory stress, 
indicated by intrinsic vulnerabilities in anti-apoptotic signaling pathways.

Materials and Methods 
Graphical overview of the workflow of experiments is shown in Figure 9. To examine the 
impact of microglial secretomes on neuronal health in sAD, hiPSCs from both control and sAD 
donors were differentiated into glutamatergic neurons using a dual SMAD inhibition protocol, 
modified from [81]. Three hiPSC-derived cell lines were used in this study: one control line 
(CW50037) and two sAD lines. CW50069, which carries a higher genetic risk score for AD, and 
ADRC24, which carries a lower risk score, based on their SNP profiles. NPCs were expanded 
and differentiated into glutamatergic neurons. Differentiation stages were verified by 
immunocytochemistry (ICC) using pluripotency markers (OCT3/4, SSEA3), neuroectodermal 
markers (PAX6, NESTIN), and neuronal markers (β-tubulin III, MAP2).  
Microglia were differentiated from hiPSCs, by other laboratory members, and cultured under 
two experimental conditions. One group was stimulated with LPS, at a concentration of 100 
ng/mL for 24 hours to induce a pro-inflammatory phenotype, while the other group remained 
unstimulated.  Following stimulation, microglial cultures were harvested and their conditioned 
media (MCM) were collected. To simulate neuroinflammatory conditions, mature neurons 
were treated for 72 hours with MCM derived from control (BIONI37A) and sAD (CW50069 and 
CW50037) microglial lines. Treatments were conducted under both LPS-stimulated and 
unstimulated conditions, as summarized in Figure 8. After treatment, total RNA was extracted 
from neurons and reverse-transcribed for quantitative PCR analysis. The apoptotic responses 
were assessed by measuring the mRNA expression levels of BCL2 (anti-apoptotic) and BAX 
(pro-apoptotic) via RT-qPCR. The BAX/BCL2 ratio served as an indicator of neuronal 
susceptibility to inflammation-induced apoptosis.

SNP Profiling and Polygenic Risk Scores in Cell Lines 
To establish the AD-linked genetic risk profile of the cell lines, previous work by lab members 
analyzed eight AD associated SNPs using qPCR. Based on these data, polygenic risk scores (PRS) 
were calculated for all four cell lines, as shown in Table 1. The two control lines, CW50037 and 
BIONI37A, exhibited similar SNP profiles and nearly identical negative PRS values (–0.21 and 
0.27, respectively), indicating a low genetic risk for AD. Conversely, the AD cell lines exhibited 
elevated genetic risk profiles: ADRC24 had moderately elevated PRS (1.55) and CW50069 had 
a bit more elevated PRS (1.81) compared to ADRC24, which rendered it as the highest-risk line 
in our panel. To calculate the PRS value for each cell line, the average contribution of each SNP 
variant was used. The final PRS was obtained by summing the natural logarithm (LN) values of 
the odds ratios associated with each risk allele.

oversimplified model of a far larger genomic landscape.

kinase [143] that regulates the phosphorylation of myosin light chain along with other kinases 
and cytoskeletal binding proteins. Therefore, its addition is crucial for preventing apoptosis 
and supporting proliferation and differentiation [144]. Cell morphology was assessed daily 
using a light microscope. Full media changes were performed daily on cultured cells, without 
the addition of ROCK inhibitor. 
Passaging of cells 
Once the cells reached approximately 80% confluency, they were passaged at a 1:3 ratio into 
new 6-well MG-coated plates. Media was aspirated and the cells were gently washed with 1

and cellular debris. PBS was aspirated and 1 mL of Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA),

and incubated for 4 minutes at 37°C. After incubation, EDTA was aspirated, and the cells were 
resuspended in StemFlex+ P/S and transferred to new MG coated plates.  
EDTA is a gentle method to detach adherent cells from culture plates by removing calcium 
ions, which are important for the attachment of transmembrane proteins to the ECM [145]. 
If the cells detached during incubation, media was added in a 1:1 ratio to stop EDTA. The cells 
were collected in a falcon tube and centrifuged at 1000 rpm in 3 minutes. The supernatant

Mycoplasma Testing in Cell Cultures via Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) 
Prior to neuronal differentiation, hiPSCs were tested for mycoplasma contamination to ensure 
the integrity and reliability of our cell culture-based assays. Contamination was tested using 
the Venor® GeM OneStep PCR kit (Minerva Biolabs, Germany) following the manufacturer's 
protocol. The assay is based on conventional endpoint PCR of the conserved 16S rRNA gene 
region of Mollicutes, enabling the detection of a broad range of mycoplasma species. 
Lyophilised reagents were briefly centrifuged and resuspended before use. The OneStep Mix 
was reconstituted in 600 µL of Rehydration Buffer, and the Positive Control DNA was 
rehydrated in 300 µL of PCR grade water. They were vortexed and incubated at room 
temperature for a minimum of 5 minutes to ensure complete dissolution. 
To allow sufficient accumulation of potential mycoplasma contamination, 100 μL of cell-free 
medium was collected from each of three confluent cultures grown for one week. Samples 
were kept chilled in sterile Eppendorf tubes until PCR was prepared. Samples were incubated 
at 95 °C for 10 minutes to lyse cells and release DNA, then briefly centrifuged to pellet debris 
before amplification. Two microliters of the resulting supernatant were then used directly in 
the PCR reaction. 
Each 25 µL PCR reaction mix contained 23 µL rehydrated OneStep Mix and 2 µL of sample or 
control. Reactions were prepared in duplicate and included the following controls: 
Positive Control: 2 µL rehydrated Positive Control DNA. 
Negative Control (NTC): 2 µL fresh cell culture medium or DNA extraction buffer.

Differentiation to Glutamergic Neurons 
The directed differentiation of hiPSCs to Glutamergic Neurons followed the protocol 
developed by [81], with minor modifications. The differentiation procedure was divided into 
three phases: neural induction, neural expansion, and neural maturation, as depicted in Figure 
10. Each phase utilized different media conditions, detailed in the Appendix-1. 
Neural Induction 
Neural induction was initiated when hiPSCs reached 80–90% confluency. On day 0, StemFlex 
medium was replaced with Neural Induction Medium (NIM). A 24-well AggreWell plate was 
first prepared by adding 500 μl of AggreWell Rinsing Solution to each well and centrifuged at 
1800 rpm for 3 minutes. Upon centrifugation, the rinsing solution was aspirated and the cells 
were gently rinsed with 1 ml of PBS. PBS was then replaced with 1 mL of NIM supplemented 
with 20 µL of ROCK inhibitor. Meanwhile, the most confluent hiPSC wells were selected. 
StemFlex medium was aspirated and the cells were rinsed with PBS. PBS was removed and 500 
μl of Accutase (Thermo Fisher Scientific, #A1110501) was added to every well and incubated 
at 37°C for 5 minutes until the cells detached from the surface. 500 μl StemFlex was added on 
top of the Accutase, and the cells were collected into a 15 ml falcon tube and centrifuged at 
1200 rpm for 4 minutes. Supernatant was carefully aspirated, and the cell pellet was re-
suspended in 1 ml of NIM. Cell count was carried out. For each cell line, 3 × 10⁶ cells, equivalent 
to the yield from 2–3 confluent wells of a 6-well plate, were seeded into a fresh well of a 24-
well AggreWell™ plate in a volume of 1 mL per well. The plate was centrifuged for 5 minutes 
at 400 rpm and then placed in the incubator. 
The use of AggreWell™ plates and Accutase during neural induction facilitates the 
standardized and reproducible formation of embryoid bodies (EBs). The AggreWell™400 
system (STEMCELL Technologies) is composed of wells, each containing 1200 microwells with 
a diameter of 400 µm. This design enables the generation of up to 1200 uniformly sized EBs 
per well, with each EB comprising up to 5000 cells. Such uniformity significantly reduces 
variability in cell aggregation and enhances the consistency of downstream differentiation. The 
production of homogeneous EBs is critical for improving the reproducibility and efficiency of 
neural precursor cell (NPC) generation [146]. 
Accutase is preferred over EDTA for cell detachment during EB formation due to its ability to 
preserve high cell viability. Unlike EDTA, which chelates calcium and may only partially detach 
adherent cells, often requiring mechanical scraping that can damage cell membranes, accutase 
contains gentle proteolytic enzymes that degrade adhesion proteins without compromising 
cell integrity. This enzymatic action minimizes mechanical stress, maintaining cell viability and 
==PAGE:34==
26 
 
cohesion, which is critical for efficient and uniform EB aggregation and subsequent neural 
differentiation [145]. 
On Days 1 to 3 of culture, the medium was gently replaced to avoid disturbing EBs, with a total 
of 75% medium exchange performed each time. EBs were harvested on Day 4. Prior to plating, 
6-well plates were pre-coated with MG and incubated for 1 hour at 37°C. To collect the EBs, 
each AggreWell was gently pipetted up and down to dislodge the aggregates. The resulting 
suspension was transferred onto an inverted 40 µm cell strainer placed over a 50 mL Falcon 
tube. The AggreWells were then rinsed with 4 mL of NIM, and any remaining EBs were 
collected and added to the same strainer. Following collection, the EBs were carefully 
transferred from the Falcon tube into the pre-coated 6-well plates, distributing them as evenly 
as possible across the wells to ensure uniform plating.

From Day 5 to Day 12, a full medium change was performed daily to support continued

NPCs Freezing 
Once NPCs had expanded sufficiently, they were cryopreserved at P1 for long-term storage. 
The following procedure was followed: Culture media was aspirated and NPCs were gently 
washed with PBS. Accutase was then added to each well, and the plates were incubated at 
37°C for approximately 4–5 minutes to facilitate cell detachment. Once the cells were 
completely detached, DMEM was added on top in a 1:1 ratio to stop the effect of accutase and 
the cell suspension was transferred to a 15 ml falcon tube. The cells were centrifuged for 4 
minutes at 1200 rpm. The supernatant was carefully removed and the cell pellet was 
resuspended in freezing media. The freezing media consisted of 90% Knock out serum and 
10% DMSO (Fisher Scientific, #10103483). Knock out serum contains essential amino acids, 
vitamins, trace elements and proteins to support cell viability during freezing. The cell 
suspension was aliquoted into cryovials at 1 ml per vial. Vials were placed in a Frosty boy and 
stored at -80 °C for at least 24 hours. Finally, the cryovials were transferred to liquid nitrogen 
for long-term storage. 
Neuronal Maturation 
After passage 2 of the NPCs, neuronal maturation was initiated. To ensure optimal neuronal 
differentiation and minimize astrocytic contamination, maturation was performed no later 
than passage 5, as NPCs tend to begin astrocytic differentiation beyond this point. 6-well plates 
were coated with Poly-L-Ornithine (PLO) and Laminin (PLO/L) to support neuronal attachment 
and maturation. PLO coating was prepared by diluting 100 μl of PLO (Sigma-Aldrich, #LPLO001-
100ML) in 900 μl PBS. The solution was added to the wells and incubated overnight at 4°C. 
After removing PLO and washing with PBS, Laminin (Sigma-Aldrich, #L2020-1MG) was diluted 
1:100 in cold PBS and added to the wells. Plates were again incubated overnight at 4°C. 
Laminin was removed just before cell seeding. 
To initiate neuronal maturation, NPCs were first prepared by washing the cells with PBS, 
followed by incubation with Accutase for 5 minutes at 37°C to facilitate detachment. The 
enzymatic activity of Accutase was then neutralized by adding an equal volume of DMEM. The 
resulting cell suspension was collected and centrifuged at 1200 rpm for 4 minutes. After 
centrifugation, the cell pellet was gently resuspended in 2 ml of Neuronal Maturation Medium 
(NMM). The cells were then seeded onto PLO/L-coated 6-well plates at a density of 500,000 
cells/cm² in 1.5 ml of NMM per well, providing the appropriate conditions for the initiation of 
neuronal differentiation. 
RT-qPCR  
Quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) is a key method in molecular biology, enabling 
sensitive and specific quantification [147] of nucleic acids in real time [148]. The process begins 
with extracting RNA from biological samples. This is followed by reverse transcription to create 
complementary DNA (cDNA), which serves as the template for subsequent qPCR analysis. This 
method provides precise measurements of gene expression [147], viral load, or DNA copy 
number variation by quantifying the amount of target sequence in each sample [148].  
The qPCR reaction mixture generally contains DNA polymerase, deoxynucleotide 
triphosphates (dNTPs), forward and reverse primers, a fluorescent reporter (such as SYBR 
Green dye or a TaqMan probe), and the cDNA template. The reaction occurs in a thermal cycler 
that goes through three main temperature phases: denaturation, annealing, and extension. 
==PAGE:37==
29 
 
Real-time fluorescence measurements are taken during each cycle using a fluorometer. This 
device detects the increasing fluorescence signal as the target DNA amplifies [147].  
SYBR Green is a cost-effective dye that binds to the minor groove of all double-stranded DNA 
created during amplification. As more DNA is synthesized, more dye binds, and fluorescence 
rises proportionally. However, SYBR Green is not specific. It binds to any double-stranded DNA, 
including non-specific products like primer-dimers. Therefore, melt curve analysis is crucial for 
confirming amplification specificity. Despite this drawback, SYBR Green is popular in high-
throughput applications due to its simplicity and low cost [148], [149].   
During the qPCR reaction, the thermal cycler monitors fluorescence at the end of each cycle. 
The number of cycles needed for the fluorescence signal to surpass a set threshold is called 
the quantification cycle (Cq). This value shows the cycle number at which detectable 
amplification occurs. The Cq value is inversely related to the initial amount of target nucleic 
acid in the sample. A lower Cq indicates a higher starting concentration, while a higher Cq 
suggests a lower concentration [148]. 
 
RNA extraction 
RNA extraction involves several steps. Once the neurons fully matured, the culture medium 
was removed, and the cells were washed with PBS. To extract RNA, a lysis buffer containing 
RLT Plus (Qiagen, #74034) and 2-mercaptoethanol (1:100 ratio) was added directly to the cells. 
RLT Plus contains guanidine isothiocyanate (GITC), a chaotropic agent that denatures proteins 
and inactivates RNases, helping to preserve RNA integrity. It also has proprietary detergents 
that effectively disrupt cellular and nuclear membranes, releasing nucleic acids into solution. 
Cells were detached using a cell scraper, and the lysate was kept at –80 °C until RNA extraction.  
RNA was isolated following the manufacturer’s protocol for the RNeasy Plus Mini Kit (Qiagen, 
#74034). The lysate was vortexed for 30 seconds to achieve thorough homogenization, and 
500 μl of Buffer RLT Plus was added. After mixing by pipetting, the sample was transferred to 
a gDNA Eliminator spin column placed in a 2 ml collection tube and centrifuged at 10,000 rpm 
for 30 seconds. The column was discarded, and the flow-through was retained. 
Next, 600 μl of 70% ethanol was added to the flow-through, mixed well, and transferred to a 
RNeasy spin column in a new 2 ml collection tube. After centrifugation at 10,000 rpm for 15 
seconds, the flow-through was discarded. The column was then washed with 700 μl of Buffer 
RW1, followed by centrifugation under the same conditions. After discarding the flow-through, 
500 μl of Buffer RPE was added, and the column was spun again for 15 seconds. A second wash 
with 500 μl of Buffer RPE was performed, followed by a 2-minute centrifugation at 10,000 rpm. 
To dry the membrane, the spin column was transferred to a new 2 ml collection tube and 
centrifuged at 13,000 rpm for 1 minute. Finally, the column was placed in a 1.5 ml RNase-free 
tube, and 50 μl of RNase-free water was added directly to the membrane. After a final 
centrifugation at 10,000 rpm for 1 minute, the purified RNA was eluted. RNA concentration 
was measured using a NanoDrop spectrophotometer, with the results provided in Appendix-
2.

RNA purification- ethanol precipitation method 
To improve the A260/230 ratios, additional RNA purification was performed on samples with 
suboptimal absorbance values. Half of the isolated RNA volume was transferred to new 
Eppendorf tubes. Each sample was treated with 2.5 μl of 3 M sodium acetate, followed by the 
addition of 62.5 μl of ice-cold absolute ethanol. The mixture was vortexed thoroughly and 
stored at –20 °C overnight to allow RNA precipitation. 
The next day, samples were centrifuged at 13,000 rpm for 30 minutes at 4 °C. The supernatant 
was carefully removed, and the resulting pellet was washed with 0.5 ml of ice-cold 75% 
ethanol. A second centrifugation was carried out at 13,000 rpm for 10 minutes at 4 °C. After 
discarding the ethanol, a brief spin at maximum speed for 10 seconds was performed to 
eliminate any residual liquid. The pellet was then air-dried with the tube lid open for 
approximately 10 minutes. 
Finally, the RNA pellet was resuspended in 30 μl of RNase-free water on ice. RNA purity and 
concentration were reassessed using a NanoDrop spectrophotometer.

Before performing RT-qPCR with gene primers of interest, it was essential to ensure their 
efficiency by generating a standard curve. The primers should bind and amplify their target in 
an efficient as well as specific manner. For an optimal standard curve, at least five data points 
were needed across 5 to 10 serial dilutions of the cDNA template. Next, a linear regression 
curve was made by plotting Cq values against log-transformed cDNA concentrations. 
Quadruplicates were performed on the samples. Under ideal PCR conditions, the DNA amount 
should theoretically double with each cycle, indicating 100% efficiency. However, because 
experimental conditions are rarely perfect, efficiencies generally range from 90% to 110%. 
Additionally, duplicate testing with a low standard deviation (<0.2 Cq units) confirms assay 
reproducibility. The coefficient of determination (R²) is used to assess the quality of linear 
correlation between the measured values of each sample. For a valid correlation, a R² value of 
greater than 0.99 is typically required. A lower R² value may suggest issues in the serial dilution 
process. To minimize such inconsistencies, accurate pipetting methods should be employed, 
and using well-calibrated pipettes is recommended to ensure precision when preparing 
dilutions. In this study, an R² threshold of 0.98 was considered acceptable due to time 
constraints that limited ordering new primers and repeating the qPCR experiment. 
A crucial next step in this study was the selection of suitable housekeeping (HK) genes for data 
normalization. HK genes are consistently expressed at constant levels across various biological 
conditions and are utilized as internal controls to account for technical variation, such as 
differences in cDNA synthesis efficiency and pipetting errors. If not normalized, target gene 
expression levels can be affected. Choosing inappropriate HK genes can introduce systematic 
errors, resulting in incorrect biological conclusions in relative quantification. To address this, 
standard curve analysis for three candidate HK genes was carried out. In the current study, 
TATA-binding protein (TBP), 18S rRNA, and glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase 
(GAPDH) were evaluated for their reliability in RT-qPCR normalization by checking whether 
they met the amplification efficiency, standard deviation, and R² values criteria set forth 
previously.  
Among the three housekeeping genes examined, TBP was found to be the most suitable 
candidate. The standard curve analyses for all three genes are presented in Appendix-3. 
GAPDH was excluded early in the evaluation due to its amplification efficiency exceeding 
110%, which is above the acceptable range. 18S rRNA was also ruled out, as it demonstrated 
an even higher amplification efficiency (120%) compared to TBP (110%). In contrast, TBP met 
the efficiency criteria and also passed the R² threshold, which was set at 0.98. Additionally, 
TBP showed consistent performance in terms of standard deviation, with only one dilution 
point exceeding the value of 0.2. Based on these findings, TBP was selected for normalization 
in the subsequent RT-qPCR analysis.  
Appendix-3 presents the standard curve results for the primers targeting the following genes: 
COX2, XIAP, APOE, SPARC, SOD2, PSD95, CIAP2, BCL2 and BAX. Based on the previously 
discussed criteria, only the primers for: BCL2 and BAX met the required standards and were 
therefore selected for further analysis.

Morphological assessment of hiPSCs Used in This Study          
The experimental design included the use of hiPSCs derived from 1 control and 2 sAD cell lines, 
all female. The control line, CW50037, carries a homozygous APOE3/APOE3 genotype, which 
is associated with a low genetic risk for AD. In contrast, both CW50069 and ADRC24 are sAD 
lines, each heterozygous for the APOE3/APOE4 genotype, a known risk factor for increased 
susceptibility to AD. Notably, prior SNP profiling conducted by the lab revealed that CW50069 
exhibits a higher overall genetic susceptibility to AD compared to ADRC24, based on its PRS. 
For simplicity, CW50069 is referred to as the higher-risk sAD line, while ADRC24 is referred to 
as the lower-risk sAD line. 
Standard ICC characterization using established pluripotency markers such as OCT4, NANOG, 
and SSEA-4 was not performed to validate the pluripotent-like state of the hiPSC lines. Instead, 
cellular morphology was used as a consistent and practical alternative throughout culture. 
While not as conclusive as ICC,  morphological assessment allowed a practical and real-time 
monitoring of cell quality prior to differentiation protocols. Key morphological features, such 
as compact colony formation, high nucleus-to-cytoplasm ratio and defined colony borders, 
were routinely assessed to ensure that the cells maintained characteristics typical of 
undifferentiated hiPSCs. 
The hiPSC lines used in this study were first thawed and seeded for expansion until they 
reached confluency. Figure 12 illustrates the morphology of each cell line shortly after 
thawing. On Day 1, the cells did not exhibit typical stem cell morphology. Instead, they 
appeared elongated, with lower nuclear-to-cytoplasmic ratios. This can be attributed to two 
main factors: the cells were seeded as single cells rather than in aggregates and ROCK inhibitor 
was added to enhance post-thaw survival and promote attachment to the culture surface. As 
a result, the cells appeared flattened on Day 1, which is commonly observed shortly after 
thawing, especially within the first 24 hours.

Before neuronal induction, stem cell morphology was examined under bright field microscope. 
Once satisfactory confluency was reached, the cells were seeded onto AggreWell™ plates, this 
marks Day 0 and the start of the neuronal induction process. AggreWell™ plates are specifically 
designed to facilitate the formation of uniform and reproducible three-dimensional (3D) 
aggregates, such as EBs, from stem cells. These aggregates are essential for ensuring consistent 
and efficient neural induction. AggreWell™ plates contain microwells that physically confine 
cells and enable them to create EBs of the same shape and size [150]. Uniformity in EB size is 
critical for reliable neural induction, as variability can lead to inconsistent differentiation 
outcomes. Three-dimensional culture systems, such as those enabled by AggreWell™ plates, 
have been shown to support NPC generation more effectively than traditional 2D methods. 
Comparative studies have reported that 3D cultures yield higher quality and quantity of NPCs 
[151]. After 4 days, of half-media changes of NIM, the EBs were transferred to 6-well plates 
and pictures were taken, indicated in Figure 15. As it is clearly seen, CW50037 and CW50069 
formed well-defined, spherical EBs that were relatively uniform in size and morphology. 
ADRC24 cell line, on the other hand, exhibited significant morphological variability. with the 
structures less dense and irregular. Instead of forming compact, spherical aggregates, the cells 
developed into loosely organized clusters, sheet-like structures, or irregular, blob-like 
formations with poorly defined edges, indicating suboptimal EB formation.

Non-specific staining, is a common issue in immunocytochemistry and immunofluorescence 
assays. It occurs when staining signals are observed in locations where no specific antigen-
antibody reaction is expected, potentially complicating data interpretation. One major cause 
of this phenomenon is the non-specific binding of antibodies. Excessive concentrations of 
primary or secondary antibodies may lead to off-target binding, as they can interact with 
proteins beyond the intended target. In addition, low-specificity antibodies may bind to 
homologous epitopes on unrelated proteins and produce unwanted signal. Insufficient 
blocking caused by low concentrations of agents such as serum or BSA, or by shortened 
incubation times, can lead to non-specific antibody binding and increased background 
staining. Improper fixation or permeabilization may also alter antigen accessibility and 
antibody penetration. Over- or under-fixation can mask or expose epitopes inappropriately, 
while incomplete permeabilization can prevent antibodies from reaching their targets, causing 
them to bind elsewhere. Insufficient washing steps can leave residual unbound antibodies, 
contributing to background noise. Finally, issues with secondary antibodies can also lead to 
off-target staining. Poor compatibility between the secondary and primary antibodies may 
lead to cross-reactivity with other cellular proteins, compromising staining specificity [160].

Examination of apoptosis-associated gene expression by qPCR  
To gain deeper insight beyond what is visible under the microscope, further validation through 
molecular and biochemical analyses is needed. One of the next steps will be to measure the 
levels of mRNA related to apoptosis. These tests will provide a more quantitative and 
mechanistic understanding of neuronal responses and will help determine whether the 
morphological alterations observed are linked with programmed cell death pathways. 
Specifically, they will clarify the role of specific inflammatory signals in mediating these effects. 
Together, these complementary approaches will offer a more comprehensive and reliable 
assessment of neuronal health in response to microglia-derived inflammatory stimuli. 
Following neuronal maturation, cultures were lysed using a standard lysis buffer, and total RNA 
was extracted as described in the Methods section. Consistent with morphological 
observations, the ADRC24 cell line yielded a lower RNA concentration, likely reflecting a 
reduced cell density compared to CW50037 and CW50069. 
After RNA extraction, cDNA synthesis was carried out, and RT-qPCR was performed to evaluate 
the expression of selected target genes. TBP was used as the reference gene for normalization. 
Of the eight NF-κB downstream targets initially tested, only BAX and BCL2 demonstrated 
efficient and reproducible amplification with the available primers. Due to time constraints, it 
was not feasible to procure new primers for the remaining genes. Consequently, the study 
focused on assessing the effects of different microglial secretomes on neuronal apoptosis by 
analyzing BAX and BCL2 expression. 
To first establish a baseline, we examined whether microglia media alone, without added 
microglia supernatant, affected neuronal gene expression. BCL2 and BAX mRNA levels were 
compared under three conditions: NMM, unstimulated Microglia Medium (MGM), and LPS-
stimulated MGM. Relative fold differences in gene expression were calculated by the ΔΔCT 
method and are plotted on the y-axis Figure 32. The analysis was ran using One-Way Anova.

Microglia, the brain’s resident immune cells, under inflammatory conditions can induce 
apoptosis and neuronal stress by releasing pro-inflammatory cytokines. Under homeostatic 
conditions, treatment of neurons with supernatant from healthy microglia, BIONI37A in this 
case, is not expected to induce strong changes in BCL2 mRNA levels. However, when neurons 
are exposed to supernatant from AD microglia, which release high levels of inflammatory 
cytokines such as TNF-α and IL-1β, BCL2 mRNA levels are expected to drop due to increased 
apoptotic stress. Surprisingly, our results tell a different story. Instead of a widespread 
downregulation of BCL2 in response to AD microglial signals, the control cell line shows an 
increase in BCL2, indicating that there are feedback mechanisms in place that help maintain 
cell survival. This is consistent with findings in the literature [116], which highlights that 
neurons are not passive recipients of stress. Instead, they actively respond to stressful 
environments by upregulating anti-apoptotic genes as an early protective mechanism. 
However, this response is not universal and appears to be tightly regulated by the genetic and 
disease-risk context of the neuronal cells. 
The data presented in Figure 33 strongly support the hypothesis that the intrinsic traits of 
neuronal lines, especially their genetic risk for AD, are key indicators of how well they can 
trigger an anti-apoptotic response through BCL2 modulation. Specifically, CW50037 (Control, 
Non-AD) and ADRC24 (Lower-risk AD) both demonstrate the ability to upregulate BCL2 after 
exposure to microglial supernatant. This implies that these neurons have mechanisms to cope 
with stress signals, even when faced with potentially harmful microglial factors.  Interestingly, 
ADRC24 exhibited a weaker BCL2 response compared to CW50037. This is likely due to its AD 
background, which may slightly lessen the effectiveness of its protective mechanisms. 
Nevertheless, the increase in BCL2 mRNA levels in ADRC24 after exposure to microglial 
supernatants aligns with previous findings observed under light microscope, where signs of 
immune exhaustion were suggested. These findings support the hypothesis that ADRC24 may 
have adjusted its internal signaling toward a more protective or survival-focused profile, 
possibly as a response to ongoing inflammatory stress. It could also be the fact that, ADRC24 
neurons were matured for three days less than CW50037 and CW50069. This means their 
compensatory pathways might still be functional and active contrary to CW50069 (Higher-risk 
AD). CW50069 showed an entirely different response by failing to increase BCL2 under any 
conditions. This lack of response indicates a significant vulnerability in CW50069, likely due to 
genetic or epigenetic changes that disrupt survival signaling pathways. The inability to induce 
BCL2 in response to microglial factors, regardless of the inflammatory environment, points to 
a heightened sensitivity and poor stress response in higher-risk AD neurons. 
Regarding the apoptotic vulnerability of CW50037 (control, non-AD) neurons to microglial 
supernatant, they exhibited marked sensitivity to the inflammatory status of the microglial 
donor. When exposed to supernatant from CW50069 (higher-risk AD microglia), there was a 
sharp increase in the pro-apoptotic gene BAX (fold change = 2.90, p = 0.0006). This suggests 
the activation of apoptotic signaling likely driven by higher levels pro-inflammatory cytokines. 
Interestingly, the anti-apoptotic gene, BCL2 was also upregulated (fold change = 1.82), 
reflecting an effort to activate protective mechanisms. This dual response is not contradictory, 
but shows a cellular attempt to counterbalance apoptotic stress. Similar patterns have been 
reported in previous studies, such as [130], where it was observed an upregulation of both 
BCL2 and BAX as an early regulator of apoptosis in post-traumatic stress disorder. However, in 
our findings, the BCL2/BAX ratio leans toward apoptosis, indicating that the inflammatory 
impact from CW50069 microglia exceeds the neurons' ability to compensate. Generally, a 
higher BCL2/BAX ratio indicates a shift toward cell survival, as BCL2 is anti-apoptotic and helps

to significantly promote neuronal apoptosis and reduce cell viability compared to control 
conditions [159]. Treatment with the secretome of LPS-stimulated microglia is expected to 
shift the BCL2/BAX ratio toward a pro-apoptotic profile. This molecular signature is well-
documented in the literature as a hallmark of neurodegenerative and neuroinflammatory 
conditions driven by microglial activation. Specifically, a downregulation of BCL2 mRNA 
expression alongside an upregulation of BAX is anticipated. 
However, our experimental findings did not align with these expectations, as also happened 
in the unstimulated conditions. BCL2 mRNA levels were elevated across all three cell lines, with 
the most significant increase observed in CW50069 (p < 0.0001). This appears contradictory to 
our earlier findings in the unstimulated conditions, where the CW50069 cell line was 
considered quite vulnerable to stress and its survival mechanisms were thought to be impaired 
due to chronic inflammation. In contrast under the current stimulated conditions, the notable 
increase in BCL2 suggests that CW50069 may need a higher threshold or intensity of stress to 
trigger anti-apoptotic responses and activate survival pathways. Increased BCL2 mRNA may 
not necessarily result in adequate functional protein levels or downstream anti-apoptotic 
effects, possibly due to chronic disruptions in the regulatory network of these vulnerable 
neurons. 
Analysis of BAX mRNA levels in all neuronal lines and treatment groups revealed minimal 
variation, with no significant changes except for a notable upregulation in CW50069 (higher-
risk AD) neurons when treated with ADRC24 LPS-stimulated microglial supernatant (fold 
change = 2.88). While BAX levels increased, the corresponding BCL2 mRNA levels under the 
same treatment was much higher (fold change = 7.95), leading to a BCL2/BAX ratio that 
favored cell survival. These findings suggest that, during the 72-hour treatment period, 
neurons try to protect themselves from inflammatory signals by activating survival 
mechanisms. The higher levels of BCL2 compared to BAX point to a tendency in favor of cell 
survival. This may reflect the neurons’ ability to trigger internal protective responses when 
faced with neuroinflammatory stress. While the high BCL2/BAX ratio suggests a short-term 
protective response to inflammation, it may indicate an early adjustment. However, because 
AD is a long-lasting condition, continued exposure to inflammatory signals could eventually 
weaken these protective responses. This underscores the need to consider treatment length 
when studying disease progression and understanding neuronal resilience. 
The ADRC24 cell line also exhibited elevated BCL2 mRNA levels under LPS-stimulated 
conditions, consistent with observations under unstimulated conditions. As it has already been 
discussed several times before, this suggests that ADRC24 has developed a survival-oriented 
profile, possibly due to chronic immune exhaustion or its shorter culturing duration, which 
may help preserve its anti-apoptotic responses. Under stimulated conditions, CW50037 
neurons maintained their compensatory mechanisms against inflammation when treated with 
microglial supernatants from CW50069 and ADRC24 lines. This finding further supports the 
existence of an intrinsic feedback loop regulating their response to stress. However, when 
==PAGE:71==
63 
 
treated with BIONI37A stimulated microglia supernatant, the BCL2/BAX ratio in the CW50037 
neuronal cell line was 1.67/2.25, favoring apoptosis. This suggests that CW50037 has a lower 
activation threshold to stress compared to CW50069 cell line, which appears to be more 
resistant to stress. CW50069 neurons seem more resilient possibly because they have been 
preconditioned by chronic exposure to an inflammatory environment, which could have 
strengthened their stress response mechanisms over time. 
A particularly interesting finding across all neuronal treatments is that the microglial 
secretome derived from the ADRC24 cell line consistently promotes neuronal survival. This is 
evidenced by the sustained upregulation of BCL2 mRNA levels and elevated BCL2/BAX ratios 
under all tested conditions. These results suggest that the ADRC24-derived secretome likely 
contains a higher proportion of neuroprotective and anti-inflammatory factors compared to 
neurotoxic ones. This interpretation is backed by earlier studies [109]. They have shown that 
anti-inflammatory cytokines can lessen or even reverse the harmful effects of inflammatory 
stimuli such as LPS. It might be that similar protective cytokines are found in the ADRC24 
secretome. Moreover, microglia produce not just inflammatory substances but also 
neurotrophic and protective factors [112]. In the case of ADRC24, these helpful signals may 
effectively counteract any harmful components, leading to a net positive impact on neuronal 
health. 
While the current findings provide valuable insights into how apoptotic markers are regulated 
in response to microglial conditioned media, several limitations should be acknowledged.  
Most notably, each treatment condition was performed using a single well for each neuronal 
cell line, limiting the statistical power and reproducibility of the results. To improve the 
reliability of these observations, future experiments should include biological replicates, 
preferably in triplicate, to account for variability and enable a more robust statistical analysis. 
Additionally, including a second control cell line would improve the comparative framework. 
However, this was not feasible due to technical challenges, particularly the poor compatibility 
of ADRC-derived lines with the neuronal induction protocol, which prevented their inclusion 
in the current study. Furthermore, the short-term LPS treatment is a limitation because it only 
represents acute inflammation. AD involves long-term, chronic neuroinflammatory processes 
that may result in different cellular responses over time. This restricts the ability to fully 
understand the progressive nature of neuronal damage seen in Alzheimer's. The timing of 
analyses also needs to be synchronized so that all cells share the same differentiation status. 
Finally, qPCR only measures mRNA levels, which do not always match protein expression or 
activation. Including protein-level analyses, such as Western blotting, would give a better 
understanding of the functional impact of these changes in gene expression.

Conclusion 
This thesis studied how microglial-conditioned media, both unstimulated and LPS-stimulated, 
affects the survival and apoptotic signaling of glutamatergic neurons derived from hiPSCs with 
different genetic risk profiles for sAD. The effective generation of glutamatergic neurons was 
confirmed through positive immunostaining for established neuronal markers, β-tub IΙI and 
MAP2. The findings highlight a complex relationship between the neurons’ genetic 
background and the inflammatory profile of the microglial secretome. Notably, the BCL2/BAX 
mRNA ratio appeared to be a key factor in neuronal fate, indicating the balance between pro-
survival and pro-apoptotic signaling pathways.micro 
In all three neuronal lines, CW50037 (control), ADRC24 (lower-risk AD), and CW50069 (higher-
risk AD), exposure to conventional microglia media alone led to changed BCL2/BAX mRNA 
levels. This likely reflects an attempt to activate survival pathways without the supportive 
factors usually provided by NMM. As a result, NMM was not used as a control in this study. 
Instead, both stimulated and unstimulated MGM were used to better replicate relevant 
microenvironmental conditions. 
CW50037 neurons showed a strong ability to increase the anti-apoptotic levels of BCL2, 
especially in response to both stimulated and unstimulated microglial supernatants from 
ADRC24. However, when exposed to unstimulated microglial supernatant from CW50069 and 
BIONI37A LPS-stimulated microglia, these neurons leaned towards apoptosis, with higher BAX 
levels. This demonstrates that while CW50037 neurons can successfully activate survival 
mechanisms under certain conditions, their threshold for tolerating inflammatory stress 
appears to be limited. This is because they remain vulnerable to pro-apoptotic factors released 
from other microglial populations, emphasizing the role of microglial variety in influencing 
neuronal fate.  
In ADRC24 neurons, exposure to LPS-stimulated microglia led to increased BCL2 mRNA levels, 
regardless of the sAD risk profile. This suggests a shift toward a survival-focused state, possibly 
indicating immune exhaustion and activation of stress compensation mechanisms. However, 
this response may also be due to the shorter culture duration of ADRC24 neurons compared 
to the other two lines, which might mean their intrinsic survival pathways stay more intact and 
reactive.  
Under unstimulated conditions, CW50069 neurons appeared highly vulnerable to microglial 
exposure, with low BCL2 mRNA levels indicating impaired survival pathways. However, when 
treated with LPS-stimulated microglial supernatants, regardless of sAD risk profile, these 
neurons showed a significant increase in the BCL2/BAX ratio, favoring a pro-survival response. 
This suggests that CW50069 neurons may need a greater level of stress to activate anti-
apoptotic mechanisms. Importantly, higher BCL2 mRNA levels do not necessarily mean 
effective protein expression or functional downstream protection, likely due to long-term 
disruptions in the neurons’ survival signaling pathways. 
Notably, the ADRC24-derived microglial secretome showed a consistent neuroprotective effect 
across all treatment conditions. This was evident in the persistent upregulation of BCL2 mRNA 
and increased BCL2/BAX ratios, indicating a shift toward pro-survival signaling. These findings 
suggest that ADRC24 microglia may secrete a more favorable balance of anti-inflammatory 
versus neurotoxic factors, boosting neuronal resilience. 
==PAGE:73==
65 
 
Interestingly, BAX mRNA levels remained largely stable across most conditions. This suggests 
that neurons may initially attempt repair and survival by upregulating BCL2, rather than 
initiating apoptosis. Only when these survival mechanisms are insufficient or fail might 
neurons raise BAX mRNA levels and commit to the apoptotic pathway. 
Overall, the findings show that neuronal resilience is influenced by both intrinsic genetic risk 
and the inflammatory state of the microglia. CW50037 control neurons demonstrated 
adaptable survival responses but remained vulnerable to strong inflammatory triggers, 
indicating a limited stress threshold. ADRC24 neurons, despite their lower-risk AD profile, 
favored survival signaling under LPS-stimulated conditions. This suggests either preserved 
compensatory mechanisms or the impact of shorter culturing times. In contrast, CW50069 
neurons, representing a higher-risk AD profile, showed weakened survival under unstimulated 
conditions but responded to inflammatory stress with a notable, though possibly maladaptive, 
increase in BCL2. 
These findings emphasize the critical need to consider both intrinsic cellular vulnerabilities and 
the surrounding inflammatory environment when studying neurodegenerative processes. 
They also highlight the value of hiPSC-derived neuron–microglia co-culture systems as a 
reliable platform for exploring the cellular mechanisms behind sAD and for assessing potential 
therapeutic approaches for targeting neuroinflammation and apoptosis.

Future Perspective 
The findings of this study offer valuable insights into how the genetic background of neurons 
in sAD influences their response to inflammatory signals from microglia. They also point out 
several important areas for future research that could improve our understanding and 
practical use of these results.  
One of the main limitations of this study was the absence of biological replicates across 
treatment conditions, which restricts the statistical robustness and reproducibility of the 
findings. Future experiments should include multiple independent replicates to ensure reliable 
interpretation of subtle changes in mRNA levels and morphological responses, particularly in 
relation to microglial secretomes. While BCL2 and BAX served as informative markers of 
apoptotic balance, future studies should expand the gene panel to include additional markers 
of apoptosis, inflammation, and oxidative stress, such as CASP3, IL-6, TNF-α, SOD2, and COX2. 
This broader molecular profiling would offer a more comprehensive understanding of the 
neuronal response to inflammatory stress and help identify potential therapeutic targets. 
To improve the relevance of these findings, it would be beneficial to include a broader range 
of hiPSC-derived neuronal lines representing diverse genetic risk profiles. This should 
encompass an additional control line, APOE2 carriers, and male donors. Broadening the donor 
pool in this way would help uncover potential sex-specific and genotype-specific differences 
in neuronal resilience and vulnerability. 
The duration of microglial exposure is crucial for interpreting these results. AD is a chronic 
neurodegenerative condition, and the inflammatory environment in the brain lasts for an 
extended time. In contrast, our experimental setup involved a relatively short 72-hour 
exposure. In real-world situations, such as during chronic neuroinflammation, neuronal loss 
often does not occur immediately. It is likely that prolonged exposure to inflammatory 
microglial factors could shift the BCL2/BAX ratio toward apoptosis, indicating a failure of these 
initial protective mechanisms. Therefore, while our data suggest a short-term neuroprotective 
response, future studies should investigate longer treatment durations to better mimic the 
chronic nature of AD and assess whether sustained inflammation eventually overwhelms 
neuronal survival pathways. 
While morphological and gene expression analyses provide valuable insights, they do not fully 
capture the functional state of neurons. Future studies should include functional assays, such 
as calcium imaging or electrophysiological recordings, to directly assess neuronal activity, 
synaptic connectivity, and network integrity. These methods would help determine if the 
observed molecular changes lead to functional impairments or compensatory adaptations. 
Additionally, to better reflect the complexity of the brain’s microenvironment, future models 
should employ direct co-culture systems or 3D brain organoids that include neurons, microglia 
and astrocytes. Such systems would enable real time cell interactions and provide a more 
relevant environment for studying neuroinflammation and neurodegeneration. 
Finally, exploring pharmacological interventions that target inflammatory pathways, such as 
delivering anti-inflammatory agents, should be considered as potential strategies to reduce 
neuronal death, especially in higher-risk AD contexts.
