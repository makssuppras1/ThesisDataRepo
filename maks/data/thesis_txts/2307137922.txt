Resumé 
Formålet med dette kandidat projekt er at syntetisere reducerede grafen oxid (RGO) 
dopet med to heteroatomer ved brug af nitrogenholdige overgangsmetal komplekser. Det 
andet formål er at teste de dopede RGO materialers effektivitet til at katalysere den 
vigtige oxygen reduktions reaktion. 
Projektet inkluderer både syntesen af en høj-kvalitets enkeltlags GO, fremstillet ved en 
modificerede Hummers metode, og en kommerciel GO, som bruges til doping processen. 
Reaktanterne som blev brugt til dopingen er alle nitrogenholdige ligander eller 
overgangsmetal komplekser, hvoraf nogle indeholder kobolt og en af dem indeholder 
nikkel.    
XPS analyse bekræfter at begrænset doping af GO kan opnås via en enkelt-potte 
varmebehandling ved 160 ºC. Mængden af indarbejdede heteroatomer er stærkt 
afhængig af hvilket metal komplekst der bliver anvendt. Co-indholdet varierer mellem 
0.6% og 2.4%, mens nikkel komplekset resulterer i et nikkel indhold på 2.4% på RGO og 
nitrogen-indholdet i materialerne ligger mellem 3.0-6.2%. 
Elektrokemiske forsøg med de to-dopede RGO prøver viste at de Co/N-dopede begynder 
at katalysere reaktionen ved et potentiale tæt på Pt/C (0.860 V vs. RHE) i 0.1 M KOH. 
Trans-[Co(en)2Cl2]Cl/RGO er det mest lovende af materialerne, da den begynder at 
katalysere ORR ved et meget positivt potentiale (0.841 vs. RHE) kombineret med høj 
selektivitet for 4-elektron reduktion reaktionsvejen. 
Billeder af AFM og TEM tyder på at kobolt i Trans-/Cis-[Co(en)2Cl2]Cl og Co(Salen) dopet 
RGO blev inkorporeret i grafen materialernes kulstof struktur. XRD og TEM viser at doping 
med [Co(NH3)6]Cl3, [Co(NH3)5Cl]Cl2 og [Ni(NH3)6]Br2 resulterer i dannelsen af nanopartikler 
bestående af Co3O4 eller Ni(OH)2 bundet til RGOs overflade.

Abstract
Abstract
Abstract
Abstract 
The main objective in this master thesis is to first synthesize dual-element doped reduced 
graphene oxide (RGO) using nitrogen containing transition metal complexes. Secondly, it 
is to test the efficiency of the doped RGOs towards the important oxygen reduction 
reaction.  
The project includes both the synthesis of a high quality single-layered GO, by a modified 
Hummers method, and a commercial GO, the second of which is used for the doping 
process. The reactants used for doping are all nitrogen containing ligands or transition 
metal complexes, some of them containing cobalt and one containing nickel. 
XPS analysis confirms that limited doping of the GO can be obtained using a one-pot 
hydrothermal process at 160 ºC. The specific doping is strongly dependent on the metal 
complex used. The Co-content varied from 0.6% to 2.4%, the nickel precursor gives a 
loading of 2.4% on RGO and the nitrogen content of the samples is in the range 3.0-6.2%. 
Electrochemical tests of the dual-doped RGO samples show that the Co/N-doped samples 
had an onset potential close to that of Pt/C (0.860 V vs. RHE) in 0.1 M KOH. The Trans-
[Co(en)2Cl2]Cl/RGO is the most promising material for ORR, because of its high onset 
potential (0.841 V vs. RHE) and an exclusive selectivity for the 4-electron reduction 
pathway.   
Based on the images of TEM and AFM, in the Trans-/Cis-[Co(en)2Cl2]Cl and Co(Salen) 
doped RGO the transition metal is most likely incorporated into the carbon network 
structure. According to XRD and TEM, doping with [Co(NH3)6]Cl3, [Co(NH3)5Cl]Cl2 and 
[Ni(NH3)6]Br2 give rise to nanosized particles (rods and spheres) of Co3O4 or Ni(OH)2 
anchored to the surface of the RGO.

1.1 Global energy demands, fuel cells and graphene 
The global energy needs have been rising continuously throughout the last couple of 
decades and according to the 2015 report from the International Energy Agency (IEA) is 
set to increase by one-third to 2040, driven primarily by India, China, Africa, the Middle 
East and southeast Asia [1]. The world still relies heavily on fossil fuel sources despite its 
limited deposits, impact on air pollution and its contribution towards the global warming, 
a reality that becomes increasingly clear as time marches by [2], [3]. As an example, back 
in 2014 the World Heritage Organization identified air pollution as largest single 
environmental health risk, responsible for 7 million people dying annually[4].  Because of 
this, the need for not only efficient, but also more sustainable sources of energy is as 
important as ever.        
Fuel cells are create electrical energy from chemical reactions and are generally 
considered to be both clean and efficient sources of energy. This is due to their high 
energy output often with no detectable pollutants. As an example, proton exchange 
membranes (PEM) fuel cells have high power density, a short start-up period and can 
operate at low temperatures. One of the main challenges for PEM and other types of fuel 
cells and batteries using air is improving the electrocatalytic material used for the cathode 
reaction, which involves the reduction of oxygen[5].   
In recent years, graphene has become a material of great interest due to its properties as 
a robust and high surface area material. Graphene also has good conductive abilities, 
which makes it a promising material as an electrocatalyst for the next generation of 
electronic components such as fuel cells, metal-air batteries, super-capacitors, biological 
sensors and other electronic devices[6],[7].  
One of the most important properties of graphene is the prospect of being able to modify 
the carbon network. By doping the graphene sheets with other elements, it is possible to 
fine-tune its electronic structure, creating opportunities for optimization for the process 
of interest. Along with graphene’s other structural and electrochemical properties, the 
possibility of modification has made graphene one of the candidates for replacing the 
expensive precious metal, platinum, as the material used for the oxygen reduction 
reaction (ORR) at the cathode in electrochemical devices such as proton exchange 
membrane fuel cells and metal-air batteries.    
In particular, the doping of graphene with small hetero atoms such as nitrogen and sulfur 
has been shown to result in increased reaction rate combined with a long lifetime and 
resistance to catalyst poisoning [8],[9]. It is also possible to introduce non-precious 
transition metals like cobalt and nickel in the graphene. Complexes containing these 
metals are capable of catalyzing ORR by themselves, but the deposition on graphene can 
help increase the stability of the transition metals and the activity of the material for ORR 
[10].

1.2 Objective of this thesis 
The following work is a continuation of these studies and is based on combining these 
methods for modifying graphene for ORR. Unlike previous work, the synthesis of the 
doped graphene will be performed by a one-pot dual-doping procedure using transition 
metal complexes containing both nitrogen and a transition metal (Co/Ni). The use of both 
intrinsic heteroatoms in the graphene structure with non-precious metals has previously 
been shown to result in highly efficient electrocatalysts for ORR, which are both stable 
with regards to catalyst poisoning and durability due to the robust nature of 
graphene[11], [12]. 
The dual-doped graphene electrocatalysts are synthesized by a one-step hydrothermal 
process and their activities measured using a three electrode setup using glass carbon 
electrodes. Characterization of the materials are done primarily using XPS for measuring 
the degree of doping while cyclic voltammetry and ORR performance tests are used for 
determining their catalytic effectiveness. To complement these, other methods such as 
AFM, XRD and TEM are also used for studying the physical and structural properties of the 
doped RGO samples.     
1.3 Structure of the thesis 
The thesis is primarily focused on the experimental work done using the GO and the 
prepared RGO samples. The first part of the thesis is dedicated to an introduction into 
electrochemical fuel cells, electrocatalysts, the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) and the 
properties and applications of graphene. This is followed by a description of various 
instrumental methods used for characterizing the physical properties and ORR 
performances of the samples. The experimental section focuses on the synthesis detail 
and preparations needed in order to replicate the following results, which will be used to 
compare the doped samples with the currently most efficient electrocatalyst used for 
ORR. The discussion will also include suggestions for improving the process and is 
followed by a conclusion to highlight the key results.

2.2.2.2. Concepts in the field of Electrocatalysis

Concepts in the field of Electrocatalysis 
Electrochemistry is an important field within chemistry that deals with interchange 
between chemical and electrical energy. At the center of electrochemistry are redox 
reactions, reactions that involve the transfer of electrons between chemical species. They 
are the key reactions in processes involving energy generation and storage, corrosion and 
purifications of metals among others [13].   
2.1 Electrochemical cells 
The purpose of electrochemical cells is mainly to generate electrical energy from chemical 
energy or to use electrical energy to drive chemical reactions. Fuel cells and batteries 
belong to the first of these categories.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 1: Illustration of a simple electrochemical cell. 
A simple fuel cell can be seen schematically illustrated in Figure 1. The cell consist of two 
electrodes, which transport electrons and provide a surface where the redox reactions 
can take place, a conductor that connects the electrodes and transports electrons and 
finally an electrolyte, which conducts the ions to and from the electrodes. The electrode, 
where the oxidation occurs, is called the anode, while the electrode that facilitates the 
reduction reaction is called the cathode. The anode and cathode may be in different 
electrolytes connected by a salt bridge to allow electrical contact without mixing the ions 
present at each electrode. When analyzing electrochemical cell systems, it is often useful 
to have a third electrode, a reference electrode used for determining the potential of the 
individual electrode. 
In the case of an electrochemical cell containing hydrogen and oxygen, the hydrogen is 
oxidized to hydrogen ions and the electrons are transported from the anode to the 
cathode in order to reduce oxygen into oxygen ions. The overall reaction (1) is considered 
to be split into the hydrogen oxidation reaction (HOR) (2) and the oxygen reduction 
reaction (ORR) (3), which will be covered in detail in the following chapter [14].

2.3 Electrocatalysis 
The purpose of a catalyst in general is to increase the rate of the desired reactions 
without being consumed during the process. The most common catalytic reaction is 
between a liquid or gas and a solid catalytic surface. The reactants bind to the surface of 
the catalysts, which activates them and make them more susceptible to further reaction. 
The surface of the catalytic material in essence provides a different pathway for the 
desired reaction, which requires a lower amount of activation energy and thus increases 
the rate of the reaction as illustrated by Figure 2.

Electrocatalysts are conductive materials that increase the rate of electrochemical

of an electrode or be an electrode by themselves. The electrocatalytic materials are 
characterized by a number of parameters such as activity, stability, selectivity, cost etc.  
 
2.4 Electrochemical kinetics   
The interest in highly active electrocatalysts is self-explanatory and the framework used 
for understanding the requirements is electrochemical kinetics, also sometimes referred 
to as dynamic electrochemistry. The importance of nanostructures in electrocatalysts for 
increasing the catalytic effectiveness is one of the many consequences of electrochemical 
kinetics.  
The present work uses solid electrocatalysts and liquid electrolyte for the reactions at the 
cathode and anode. The electrochemical reaction takes place at this interface between 
these two in a region called the electrical double layer. The effects and interactions in this 
region are crucial to the reaction kinetics and limiting factors involved with the reaction. 
Many models have been suggested for describing this region with the simplest model 
having been put forward back in 1850 by Helmholtz. The main features of the model can 
be seen in Figure 3 a). This model suggested that the first layer directly adsorbed to the 
surface of the electrode, consist of ions of opposite charge to the electrode. The 
==PAGE:13==

interaction between the ions and surface are assumed to be electrostatic in nature. This 
layer is known as the inner Helmholtz layer, which is followed by the outer Helmholtz 
layer. The outer Helmholtz layer consist of ions that are electrostatically attracted by the 
electrode surface, while still being coordinated by a shell of solvent molecules.

Another model of the electrode/electrolyte interface was proposed by Gouy and 
Chapman independently of each other. This model describes the ion concentration as a 
continuous distribution with the highest concentration near the electrode, however the 
model was only accurate for low surface charge densities. A more accurate model was 
proposed by Stern, who combined the Helmholtz and Gouy-Chapman model to include 
both the Helmholtz layer (or Stern layer) and the diffusion layer [16]. The charge transfer 
involved in the redox reaction between the electrode and electrolyte mainly take place in 
the Helmholtz layer by quantum tunneling. This effect is connected to a sharp decrease in 
reaction probability with distance from the electrode surface[14].

3.1 Electrocatalysts for ORR 
The kinetics involved with the ORR process is very slow and is therefore carried out using 
electrocatalysts. Pt and alloys containing Pt are currently the most efficient catalysts for 
ORR because of their high exchange current densities and positive onset potentials, but 
due to the high cost of platinum and the relatively large amount required for the cathode, 
they are too expensive for commercial use. Apart from the cost of platinum itself, there 
are some difficulties with regards to poisoning of the catalyst giving them a limited 
lifespan. The ORR exchange current density of 20 wt% Pt/C has been measured to 0.96 
mA/cm-2 in 0.5 M H2SO4 at room temperature [17]. It is strongly dependent on both the 
reactant concentration and the temperature under which the reaction is examined.  
Because of the difficulties with commercializing platinum cathode materials, metal oxide 
nanostructures, graphene and other conductive materials have been investigated for use 
in the ORR process. These are usually termed as non-precious metal catalyst (NPMC). 
Most of the transition metal oxides and carbon-nanostructures are only promising in 
alkaline mediums. This is due to the metal oxide being prone to dissolution in acidic 
medium and the carbon-nanostructures exhibiting poor activity towards ORR [18].  
In alkaline solutions, the earth abundant metal oxides have ORR activities reasonably 
close to that of Pt-metal [19]. This includes the manganese oxides, which are abundant 
and have a rich oxidation chemistry with the most promising pure manganese oxide being 
α-MnO2, which primarily catalyzes the 4-electron pathway [20]. Other promising 
materials include cobalt oxide nanostructures such as nanochains and nanocomposite 
materials like Co3O4/Co2MnO4 oxides bifunctional catalysts towards both OER and ORR 
[18], [21]. Many of these materials use a carbon-support in order to stabilize the 
materials, to withstand the harsh conditions present in ORR, especially in acidic media.  
The carbon-supports, such as graphene and carbon nanotubes have proven to be 
catalytically active themselves toward ORR when doped with heterogeneous atoms. 
These dopants include atoms such as nitrogen, boron and sulfur, which will be covered in 
detail in the next chapter. The doped carbon materials have shown to exhibit synergistic 
effects when coupled with the metal oxides [22]–[24]. As an example, N- and S-doped 
graphene has proven to be quite effective for catalyzing ORR and when combined with 
cobalt resulted in a synergistic effect that greatly improved its efficiency [11]. 
Apart from the catalyst itself, the NPMC and platinum electrocatalysts are often paired 
with Nafion. Nafion is an ionomer, a polymer containing neutral and ionized repeating 
units as seen in Figure 4. The Nafion helps conduct protons used for the catalytic process 
as well as preventing the electrocatalysts from falling of the electrode into the electrolyte. 
The ideal Nafion content for the electrocatalyst depends upon the nature of the electrode 
material. A recent study of the ideal Nafion content for a nitrogen doped graphene

3.2 Methods for Analyzing Reaction Mechanism 
The most common analytical methods used for examining the reaction mechanisms of 
ORR are polarization curves, cyclic voltammetry, rotating disc electrodes (RDE) and 
rotating ring disc electrodes (RRDE). In the case of ORR, one of the most important 
parameters is the number of electrons transferred during the reaction. This number may

 3.3 Proposed Reaction Mechanisms for ORR 
The exact mechanism involved with the ORR reaction is difficult to be determine 
compared to the used of the equations presented in the previous section. As mentioned 
earlier it is possible to describe the reaction using the Butler-Volmer equation. This is 
however, not the whole truth, as most electrochemical reactions are not elementary, 
especially in the case of multiple electron transfer reactions. Each of these elementary 
reactions have their own reaction rate and thus need to be described by their own Butler-
Volmer equation. The resulting rate is limited by the slowest reaction in the chain and is 
termed the rate determining step (rds).  
Because of these complications, the mechanism for even the most common material for 
ORR, Pt, is not known in detail [10]. A simplified mechanism has been proposed, which 
only contains H2O and H2O2 as seen in Figure 5 [27].

The most common form of pure carbon is graphite, which is comprised of layers of two-

for sensors, energy storage, fuel cells etc. [7], [9], [30].

Graphene is a special semiconductor with zero-band gap, which gives it properties similar 
to that of metallic conductors. The band gap refers to the energy difference between the 
valence band and the conductive band in the material. By doping graphene with other 
atoms, the band gap of the material can be fine-tuned to serve specific electrochemical 
processes. Having a small band gap means that electrons can move easily between the 
valence and conductive level making it easier for the material to conduct electrons. 
A number of ways for preparation of graphene and modified graphene materials have 
been utilized. These include chemical vapor deposition (CVD), mechanical cleavage like 
the one used by Novoselov et. al, chemical exfoliation of graphite by oxidation and 
subsequent reduction and thermal decomposition of SiC [29], [30]. CVD of a hydrocarbon 
gas like CH4 on metal substrates has been used to make long graphene films for 
conductive electrodes.  
 
4.1 Graphene oxide 
Chemical exfoliation of graphite by oxidation creates graphene oxide, GO. The most 
common synthesis methods for GO are the Brodie and Staudenmaier methods, which 
both uses fuming HNO3 and KClO3, and the Hummer method, which uses H2SO4 and 
KMnO4 for the oxidation [30]. The exact structure of GO is complicated and depends upon 
the degree of oxidation, but can be described as graphene with epoxides and tertiary 
alcohols spread across the sp2-hybridized carbon network according to the Lerf-Klinowski 
model [32] as seen in Figure 8. GO may also contain some carboxylic acids at the edges of 
the sheet and carbonyl groups spread across the carbon network. The disruption of the 
sp2
 bonding network makes GO an electrically insulating material, but its conductive 
capabilities can be recovered by reducing it.

The reduction of GO is the most important of its chemical reactions for the purpose of 
creating graphene, but it is also possible to use the tertiary alcohol groups and epoxides

GO is commonly referred to as reduced graphene oxide, RGO. In the case of 
functionalization, the most common is the introduction of substituted amines to be used

4.2 Intrinsically doped graphene materials 
Doping graphene with hetero atoms such as nitrogen, sulfur and boron can make it into 
either a p- and n-type semiconductor. p-type semiconductors have an excess amount of 
unoccupied orbitals for the electrons to move through, while n-type semiconductors have 
an excess amount of electrons. Previous work has shown that nitrogen or sulfur doped 
graphene creates n-typed semiconductors effective for catalyzing ORR and they have also 
shown promise for use in supercapacitors and biosensing devices [9], [33]. Improvement 
in activity is due to an increase in electron density of the states near the Fermi level, 
making it easier for the doped graphene to transfer electrons. This is caused by the 
nitrogen and sulfur having 5 and 6 valence electrons, respectively, compared to the 
carbon atoms 4 valence electrons, which can donate to the π-orbital of nearby 
conjugated carbon atoms, thus increasing the electron density. 
Especially, nitrogen has been used extensively in the formulation of doped RGO in recent 
years [14], [33], [34]. Nitrogen is similar in size to carbon making it an easy fit into the 
carbon network. The introduction of intrinsic nitrogen atoms can lead to defects in the 
graphene structure depending on how the nitrogen is bonded. Nitrogen can be found in 
four different types of coordination in the graphene structure. Pyrrolic and pyridinic 
nitrogen can be found at the edges of nitrogen-doped graphene, while the nitrogen 
incorporated in the graphene lattice is referred to as graphitic as depicted in Figure 9 [35]. 
The fourth type of nitrogen found in doped graphene is pyridine-N-oxide, which is the 
oxidized form of pyridinic nitrogen and has been shown to increase the capacitance of the 
material due to its positive charge [36].

The synthesis of nitrogen-doped graphene has been done by CVD, nitrogen plasma 
process, arc-discharge, growth segregation or by hydrothermal reaction [37]. The content 
is usually between 0.5-8.0 percent nitrogen [37], [38]. The mechanism for incorporating 
nitrogen has been examined using DFT calculation in a recent article, which indicated that 
the energy barrier for incorporating nitrogen from ammonia is much lower if defects are 
introduced in the GO [39]. 
Doping by boron has shown to result in p-type semiconductors. Like nitrogen, boron has a 
similar atomic size to that of carbon, but only has 3 valence electrons. These can be used 
to form three σ-bonds with the neighboring carbon. However, boron lacks the final 
electron needed for the π-bond, effectively leaving a vacant site that makes boron doped 
graphene a p-type semiconductor [40].  
The use of sulfur in doping of graphene has attracted similar interest to that of nitrogen 
as a potential catalyst for ORR. Sulfur is more electron rich than carbon, but also has an 
electronegativity value (2.58 on the Pauling scale) to that of carbon (2.55). The most 
common of synthesis is by thermal annealing at temperatures between 500 ºC and 1050 
ºC of a carbon and a sulfur source [41]. The synthesis procedure introduces a large 
number of defects in the graphene structure, which along with the C-S bond have shown 
to be useful for ORR catalysis [42]. 
5. Instrumental methods 
5.1 X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy 
X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) is a technique used to get information about the 
surface composition of materials. The instrument uses an X-ray gun to excite core 
electrons in a material causing them to be emitted with a kinetic energy equal to the 
difference between the binding energy of the electrons and the energy of the X-ray 
photons. The detector counts the emitted electrons and separates them by their kinetic 
==PAGE:22==
David Benjamin Christensen                                                 Dual-element doped 3D graphene electrocatalysts 
Page 22 of 78 
 
energy. A principle sketch of a XPS instrument can be seen in Figure 10. Since the energy 
of the X-ray photons is known, the binding energy of the electrons can then be calculated 
and used to determine the identity and relative quantity of different atoms in the surface. 
Further analysis of the peaks can be used to distinguish between the different types of 
bonds present. 
 
Figure 10: Principle sketch of the insides of a XPS instrument with a X-ray 
source firing on the sample, while the emitted electrons are detected by an 
electron energy analyzer [43]. 
 
Even though the X-ray may excited atoms deeper inside the material, the emitted 
electrons will collide with atoms in the layers above. This may cause them to be absorbed 
or be deflected. Because of this, the information obtained only describes the top ~10 nm 
layer of a material. However, an ion beam can be used to etch surface layers of the 
sample in order to perform a depth analysis. 
5.2 Electrochemical analysis 
In this project, the electrochemical analysis technique used include cyclic voltammetry 
and rotating disc electrodes measured by linear sweep voltammetry for ORR performance 
analysis. The theory of rotating disc electrodes and its relevance for examining the ORR 
are described in Chapter 3 about ORR. The range of electrochemical techniques spans far 
beyond these two techniques, but these will not be covered in the present work.  
Cyclic voltammetry 
Cyclic voltammetry is a common method for initial studies of electrochemical systems, 
which uses a three-electrode setup to analyze the potentials at which reactions occur in a 
specific system [44]. This is done by doing a potential sweep, where the potential of the 
working electrode is controlled relative to the reference electrode using a potentiostat. 
The current produced by the system between the working and the counter electrode is

5.3 Atomic force microscopy 
Atomic force microscopy (AFM) is one type of scanning probe microscopy (SPM), which 
uses a cantilever fitted with an ideally atomically sharp tip. The position of the cantilever 
and thus the height of the sample, is measured using a laser reflected onto a detector as 
depicted in Figure 12. The instrument measures the forces between the sample and the 
tip. These forces are a combination of attractive Van der Waal forces, chemical bonding 
forces and Pauli repulsion. Based on these forces, the instrument can illustrate the 
topography of the sample, which is useful for determining the number of graphene layers 
in the prepared samples [46].  
==PAGE:24==
David Benjamin Christensen                                                 Dual-element doped 3D graphene electrocatalysts

The instrument is usually run in either contact mode or tapping mode. In contact mode, 
the tip is pressed onto the sample, which forces it through the small layer of 
contamination always found on the surface of a sample. Although this give a complete 
picture of the surface, the vertical forces on the cantilever and tip also result in strong 
lateral forces, which can damage the surface of the sample especially for soft samples. 
Tapping mode means the tip is oscillating in a range between regions, where the long-
range interactions are dominant and where short-distance forces are dominant. Since the 
contact time is very short, the damage to the surface is minimal compared to the contact 
mode [46].

detected at the bottom of the instrument. If there are heavier atoms such as transition 
metals present, the electrons can be rebounded and reflected. This makes TEM an ideal 
method when characterizing materials containing small particles of transition metal for 
example transition metal nanoparticles on support materials used for catalysis.

6.1 Synthesis of GO 
The graphene oxide utilized for the preparation of the hybrid graphene materials was 
prepared using a modified Hummers method [50]. The oxidation was done in two steps, a 
pre-oxidation with P2O5, K2S2O8 and H2SO4 and a second oxidation with KMnO4, H2SO4 and 
H2O2. 
Pre-oxidation of graphite  
The pre-oxidization consisted of adding 10 mL concentrated H2SO4 to a clean 100 mL 
round bottom flask equipped with a magnet. The flask was suspended in an oil bath and 
heated to 80 ºC. During the heating, 5.0 g of graphite, 2.5 g of K2S2O3 and 2.5 g of P2O5 
were added gradually through a glass funnel with 3.0 mL of H2SO4 added in between each 
chemical. For the P2O5, an additional 4 mL H2SO4 was used to transfer the chemical due to 
its stickiness. The flask was the fitted with a condenser and left stirring at 560 rpm at 80 
ºC for three hours.

round bottom flask in an oil bath fitted with a condenser.

beforehand in boiling HNO3, washed three times with Milli-Q water and dried at 85 ºC. 
After the three hours, the flask was removed from the oil bath, closed with a glass lid and 
left overnight. The following day, the product was diluted with 50 mL of Milli-Q water and 
transferred to a Büchner funnel fitted with two sets of filter paper. After a short while, 
the sludge started to move through the funnel due to a hole in the filter paper. The hole 
was likely made by the sludge because it was too acidic, meaning further dilution was

Secondary oxidation of graphite 
The second-step oxidation was done in a three neck 250 mL round bottom flask equipped 
with a magnet and a condenser. 23 mL conc. H2SO4 was added to the flask, which was 
lowered into an ice bath. 3.0 g of KMnO4 was added through glass funnel gradually

having been grinded with a mortar and pestle.

was added. The reaction was terminated with about 2 mL of 31% H2O2, which was added 
dropwise until there was no evolution of gas. The resulting mixture was yellow-green and 
was left in the flask over the weekend, closed with a glass stopper.

The ORR peak position of the cis-/trans-[Co(en)2Cl2]Cl/RGO is about 300 mV more positive 
than GCE, meaning a much lower overpotential is needed. This makes them better 
compared to the bare electrode. However this is still far from the ORR peak position of 
commercial Pt/C, which has previously been reported to be close to 0.8 V (vs. RHE) with a 
peak current density of ~0.45 mA/cm2 [60].  
The low activity compared to Pt/C is consistent with previous reports, which has shown 
that nitrogen-doped graphene materials suffer from relative low activity in acidic media 
compared to alkaline [12], [61], [62]. The activity can be improved by increasing the 
surface area and optimizing the pore structure to increase the number of available active 
sites on the catalyst. As an example, Co and N co-doped graphene has been synthesized 
from polyaniline, which had an onset potential of 0.97 V vs. RHE in 0.5 M H2SO4 [12]. The 
nitrogen doping in this example was around 4% similar to the doped samples tested here, 
but it only contained a trace amount of Co (0.17%). 
In the doped RGO samples, the increase in Co-content from 1.3 to 2.4% in the 
Co(Salen)/RGO and [Co(NH3)6]Cl3/RGO shifts the ORR peak from 0.1 V to 0.3 V. These 
samples also contain similar amounts of nitrogen according to XPS, making the Co-
content the likely reason for this improvement in caralytic activity.  
The cis-/trans-[Co(en)2Cl2]Cl/RGO has a much lower Co-content (0.6%), but a higher 
nitrogen-content of 6.2%. For these two samples the ORR peak is located at 0.2 V 
indicating that the higher nitrogen content compared to Co(Salen)/RGO is more important 
for improving the ORR activity than the higher Co-content of Co(Salen)/RGO. Another 
important factor, which will be examined later by TEM, is the nanostructure of the doped 
RGO. 
The Co(Salen)/RGO has two redox peak, which are only slightly affected by the saturation 
of the electrolyte with oxygen. These peaks must also be related to the oxygen content of 
the RGO like the other samples with one peak having been shifted down to 0.51 V and the 
other up to 0.66 V. Similar results have not been found in literature of RGO, as most of 
them only record the CV’s at lower potentials. The reason for this split must be related to 
the type of doping by Co(Salen) being different from the others with the resulting doped 
atoms interacting with the oxygen in the graphene structure. Further analysis is necessary 
to explain this phenomenon by testing the ligand Salen doped into the RGO by itself. 
Looking at the trans-, cis-[Co(en)2Cl2]Cl doped samples, the results are fairly similar. The 
response from the cis complex is slightly higher, which is due to the loading on the GCE 
being slightly higher due to differences in the suspensions concentrations. The similarity 
of cis-/trans-doped sampels further supports the results from XPS, that these two 
materials are very similar in structure and in properties. The same is true for the 
[Co(NH3)6]Cl3 and [Co(NH3)5Cl]Cl2 doped RGO, although the current density of  
[Co(NH3)5Cl]Cl2/RGO is slightly higher. 
For this reason, further charecterization will only be done on the trans-[Co(en)2Cl2]Cl/RGO 
and the [Co(NH3)6]Cl3/RGO samples.

The sample loading on the GCE during the repeated measurements was twice as much as 
the amount used for the initial CVs, which accounts for some of the differences in the 
current intensity between the measurements. The repeated measurements show a 
number of significant differences compared to the initial CV. The redox peak at 0.16 V has 
seemingly been shifted upwards to 0.57 V making it overlap with the ORR peak. The ORR 
peak itself has moved from 0.64 V to 0.73 V indicating that the onset potential and thus 
the efficiency of the samples towards ORR has increased over time. A new sharp peak at 
1.08 V is also observed in the repeated measurement.  
The increase in the current density of the ORR peak under O2-saturated conditions is 
likely due to a combination of the loading being twice the sample amount and the overlap 
of the other redox peaks. The shifts in the peak location, as well as the presence of a new 
redox peak, indicates that the doped parts of the doped RGO material has been 
chemically altered over time, since a similar change is not observed for pure RGO 
samples. 
The same changes were observed for the Co(Salen)/RGO and [Co(NH3)6]Cl3/RGO samples 
during the repeated CV measurements. Since no change was observed in the Salen or Ni 
doped sample the chemical alteration must be tied to the doped cobalt atoms. The 
current intensity of the peak at 1.08 V is identical for the [Co(NH3)6]Cl3/RGO and Trans-
[Co(en)2Cl2]Cl/RGO despite the large difference in Co-content measured by XPS (2.4% and 
0.6%). The peak intensity of the Co(Salen)/RGO sample was only half the value of the 
others (Co-content measured by XPS was 1.3%). One possibility is that different types of 
doped Co-atoms exist in the RGO structure, where only some of them have contributed 
to this alteration. In that case, the Trans-[Co(en)2Cl2]Cl/RGO and [Co(NH3)6]Cl3/RGO would 
have to contain similar amounts of this type of doped Co atoms.     
Because of this phenomenon, the timing of the other types of analysis relative to the 
electrochemical performance analysis becomes important. The XPS of the doped RGO 
samples were recorded one week before the repeated CV and ORR performance 
measurements, while the TEM, AFM and XRD were measured two weeks, one month and 
six weeks later respectively. It has not been possible to find any references to similar 
occurrences in literature for cobalt doped RGO, since most of them do not record CV at 
this high voltage [33], [58], [65]. Further analysis is necessary to identify the internal 
changes in the material structure, the different types of Co bound and the rate and 
circumstances necessary for the changes.

8. Conclusion
8. Conclusion 
Due to the ever increasing energy needs of humanity and the need for clear energy, the 
improvement of commercially viable catalysts for the greatly limiting catalytic oxygen 
reduction reaction, continues to be a subject of great interest in the field of fuel cells and 
batteries. Doping of graphene materials with several types of atoms has shown to be a 
promising material for this purpose.  
The dual-doping of GO is usually done using precursors containing the individual desired 
dopant atoms, but in this project it has been shown that dual-doping of GO can be 
accomplished using single precursors containing both elements. The content of the 
individual dopants depends on the type of doping sources used. Due to the formation of 
nanostructured particles anchored to the RGO surface as seen for [Co(NH3)6]Cl3/RGO, 
[Co(NH3)5Cl]Cl2/RGO and [Ni(NH3)6]Br2/RGO, a high content of nickel and cobalt can be 
obtained. The doping of cis-/trans-[Co(en)2Cl2]Cl and Co(Salen) shows that  incorporating 
up to 1.3% of Co into the graphene network can be achieved using this process. 
Based on the electrochemical analysis, the Co/N dual-doped RGO shows superior 
electrocatalytic abilities toward the ORR in alkaline media with onset potentials 
comparable to that of Pt/C. The selectivity toward the 4-electron pathway was 
particularly high for the trans-[Co(en)2Cl2]Cl/RGO  (n = 3.9 ± 0.3) making it the most 
promising target material for further research, including optimization of the synthesis 
conditions and electrochemical measurements.
