Abstract
With the strains on water resources and changes to the hydrologic cycle brought on by
climate change, the use of satellites for freshwater monitoring has become an important
tool for expanding our view of inland hydrology. This study is a first look and applica­
tion of first release data from the newly launched Surface Water and Ocean Topography
(SWOT) satellite. The study follows three primary research objectives: Is the measure­
ment accuracy of SWOT as expected over rivers? Is the spatial resolution and coverage
of SWOT as expected over rivers? How are our current remote sensing of river discharge
methods affected by incoming SWOT data? SWOT heights are validated with respect to
ICESat­2 altimetry heights and gauge data on the Fraser River. Height accuracy on a
sub­reach scale is better than expected, with RMSE ranging between 8 and 25 cm when
averaged over 200­400 m river lengths. SWOT derived surface water extent is compared
with ICESat­2 crossings over a braided river to evaluate SWOT’s ability to observe indi­
vidual channels in the river. It is found that SWOT is able to observe channels near ∼100
m in width, which is consistent with the mission’s science goals. To understand the impact
of SWOT for remote sensing of inland hydrology, the data are then applied to river dis­
charge estimation within a multi­satellite framework. Data from previous satellite altimetry
and multispectral missions are utilized to increase the temporal density and lengthen the
timeseries of satellite measured river height and width. Multi­satellite data are combined
using a state­space model before they are input into discharge models. Three discharge
models (rating curves, statistical, and Mean Flow Constant Roughness) compatible with
remote sensing data are compared and validated against gauge data. The models are
tested at five gauges along the Fraser River and one gauge on the Ob River. The gauge
constrained model, developed using rating curves, estimated discharge with KGE > 0.60
at all stations with adequate height data and where ice is not present. The best of the
gauge unconstrained models performed comparable to the rating curve model in almost
all cases, and at one gauge performed better. The effect of SWOT in the discharge models
is difficult to quantify due to the limited available SWOT timeseries; however, the drastic
improvement in the spatio­temporal data resolution with SWOT suggests that remotely
sensed discharge may be greatly improved as more observations are acquired over time.

Introduction
As the earth continues warming in response to climate change, our finite resource of
freshwater will feel more and more finite to humans and ecosystems globally. By a 2010
study, almost 5 billion people are already living in conditions of high water scarcity, where
3.4 billion of these fall in middle­ to low­income and developing countries (Vörösmarty et
al. 2010). This number is expected to rise with the additional human and climate strains
expected on water demand in the coming years (Caretta et al. 2022), where African and
South Asian countries are expected to experience the worst conditions (Baggio, Qadir,
and Smakhtin 2021). In addition to water scarcity, climate change has resulted in extreme
changes to the hydrological cycle in the form of increased heavy precipitation, longer dry
spells, increased magnitude and frequency of flooding, and alterations to streamflow.
Communities living within flood plains are most vulnerable to river floods, and the Inter­
governmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) estimates that the population at risk of
flood events has grown 20­24% from 2000­2018 (Caretta et al. 2022). Compounding cli­
mate change with direct human effects of land use change and streamflow management,
river floods have increased in frequency 4 and 2.5 times for the tropics and mid­latitudes
(Caretta et al. 2022). Understanding how much water we have, where it is, and where
it is going is essential for informed decision making in water resource management and
extreme weather response. It is therefore timely and crucial to improve on our existing
knowledge of freshwater storage and dynamics.
River discharge, a measure of how much water is passing through a river at a given cross­
section, is extremely important in monitoring river water storage and dynamics (Glea­
son and Durand 2020; Krabbenhoft, Allen, and Lin 2022). Stream gauges provide our
most accurate river discharge estimates across the globe (Fekete et al. 2012; Gleason
and Durand 2020). Based on efforts by Lin et al. (2019) in their formation of the Global
Reach­Level A Priori Discharge Estimates for SWOT (GRADES) flow record, there are
over 17,000 stream gauges globally that are either publicly available or available upon
request. Though these in­situ observations of river discharge guide our best hydrologic
models and inform our water distribution decisions (Krabbenhoft, Allen, and Lin 2022),
gauge data is not always accessible. Due to the lack of gauges in hard­to­reach rivers,
the decline of gauge operations, the withholding of gauge data across geopolitical bound­
aries, and the point measurements produced by stream gauges, estimates of river dis­
charge are spatially sparse and unevenly distributed globally (Hannah et al. 2011; Fekete
et al. 2012; Papa, Crétaux, et al. 2023). As shown in Figure 1.0.1 from Lin et al. (2019),
the data are heavily distributed within the United States, Canada, Brazil, and Western
Europe and sparse throughout much of Africa, Asia, and the Middle East.
In the past several decades, remote sensing has been explored as an answer to gauge
data sparsity (L. C. Smith 1997; Alsdorf, Rodríguez, and Lettenmaier 2007; Pavelsky et
al. 2014; Gleason and Durand 2020). Satellite observations of stage (height), slope and
width extend our view of rivers globally, providing insight into river dynamics in ungauged
regions and across geopolitical boundaries. Before now, it was not possible to measure
all the necessary river properties for estimating river discharge from satellites simultane­
ously (Pavelsky et al. 2014). Height measurements from an altimetry satellite had to be
combined with width estimates from optical or multispectral satellites, introducing uncer­
tainty dependent on how closely the measurements were recorded in space and time. In
addition, many water bodies were not observed at all, due to the large inter­track spacing

The novel Surface Water and Ocean Topography (SWOT) satellite will supply the first
global view of river dynamics for rivers wider than 50­100 meters, providing simultaneous
measurement of river area, height, and slope with greater coverage than traditional al­
timeters (Biancamaria, Lettenmaier, and Pavelsky 2016). With the first releases of SWOT
data at the start of 2024, there is an eagerness to understand the quality of SWOT river
observations and the advances that can be made in remote sensing of river discharge
(RSQ). Here we present a first look at this data to understand the accuracy, resolution,
and performance of SWOT data within the framework of RSQ. This study is situated in a
unique position of handling actual SWOT data, rather than simulated values, to evaluate
the potential of SWOT observations. The primary research questions of this work are as
follows:
1. Is the measurement accuracy of SWOT as expected over rivers?
2. Is the spatial resolution and coverage of SWOT as expected over rivers?
3. How are our current RSQ methods affected by the incoming SWOT data?
For the last goal listed, satellite derived discharge timeseries are compared with gauge
measurements of discharge. Since only a few months of SWOT data are available at the
start of this study, additional observations of river width and height from previous satellite
missions are used in conjunction with SWOT for estimating discharge.

2.1
The Surface Water and Ocean Topography Mission
2.1.1
Mission overview
The SWOT mission was created in direct response to the need for better monitoring of
surface and ocean water storage and dynamics. Though remote sensing capabilities can­
not yet match the precision and accuracy of in­situ gauge data, SWOT will provide global
observations of land hydrology that are temporally and spatially consistent, extending
our knowledge of land hydrology into completely ungauged or sparsely gauged basins
(Pavelsky et al. 2014; Biancamaria, Lettenmaier, and Pavelsky 2016; Durand, Gleason,
Garambois, et al. 2016). This study is only concerned with the land hydrology side of
SWOT applications, and for this reason a discussion of SWOT operations over the ocean
is omitted.
Figure 2.1.1: SWOT mission timeline from the version C release note (JPL 2024).
The SWOT mission was developed as a collaboration by the United States, French, Cana­
dian and United Kingdom Space agencies (NASA, CNES, CSA, and UKSA) and the satel­
lite was launched on December 16, 2022. The satellite first went though calibration phase,
part of the the 1­day­repeat orbit, in which many passes over the same areas were used to
calibrate onboard instruments. This is followed by the science phase, in which the satel­
lite provides near global coverage of water surface elevations in a 21­day repeat orbit. A
full timeline is shown in Figure 2.1.1.
Science requirements
The primary land hydrology related science requirements of SWOT are as follows:
• Observe water bodies 250 m2 and larger, with the potential for areas above 100 m2
• Observe rivers with width 100 m and greater, with the potential to observe widths as
small at 50 m
• Measure water surface height with accuracy < 10 cm over a 1 km2 water region and
accuracy < 25 cm for a 250 m2 region
• Measure river slope with accuracy better < 1.7 cm/km when averaging 1 km2 region
• Measure water extent with < 15% estimation error

Configuration

Inspired by the Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (STRM) and the never deployed Wide
Swath Ocean Altimeter (WSOA) (Fu and Rodríguez 2003; Fu and Rodríguez 2004; Fu,
Alsdorf, Rodríguez, et al. 2009), SWOT utilizes synthetic aperture radar (SAR) interfer­
ometry to record water surface heights. SWOT is equipped with two antennas at either
end of a 10 meter beam. The antennas observe 2­D fields (swaths) of water surface ele­
vations on either side of the satellite, where each swath is about 50 km wide with a 20 km
gap in between. A nadir altimeter observes heights directly below the satellite, in the 20
km gap, for calibration purposes. The radar onboard SWOT (KaRIn) operates in Ka­band
and at look angles ranging from 0.6 to 3.9◦. The shorter wavelength of Ka­band allows
for finer phase sensitivity and the near­nadir incidence angles maximizes the backscat­
ter of smooth, specularly reflecting surfaces such as water. The difference in received

Coverage
SWOT covers 96.45% of the land area between latitudes 78◦N and 78◦S. The gaps in
coverage are due to gaps between inter­orbit passes and the unobserved space between
the right and left swaths. A majority of the uncovered regions lie near the equator, where
river reaches and lakes will receive 1­2 visits from SWOT within a 21­day cycle. Water
features in high latitudes receive upwards of 6 to 10 or more visits by SWOT within a cycle,
especially in boreal regions. This is illustrated by Figure 2.1.3 as shown in Biancamaria,
Lettenmaier, and Pavelsky (2016).

2.1.2
Errors and limitations
The primary error sources effecting SWOT measurements of water surface elevation are
the roll error prevalent in all wide­swath altimetry, random and systematic phase error,
atmospheric propagation delays (wet and dry troposphere and ionosphere effects), and
layover (Rodríguez et al. 2017; Fu, Alsdorf, Morrow, et al. 2012). The combination of
these errors can cause meters of uncertainty in water surface elevation estimates. The
measurements from SWOT are then only as accurate as the error correction estimates.
Each of these error sources are described in detail along with their estimated correction
in Section 2.2.3.
The trade­off between spatial resolution and temporal coverage has always been signifi­
cant in earth observation satellite mission design. Dense spatial coverage results in year­
long repeat periods (CryoSat­2), and shorter repeat periods require large inter­track gaps
(ICESat­2, Sentinel­6, etc.). With it’s wide­swath measurement configuration, SWOT’s
combined temporal­spatial coverage is better than we have ever had from an altimeter.
SWOT’s limitations due to its temporal sampling are discussed here, with the caveat that

temporal sampling is far improved from previous missions. Though SWOT has many re­
visit times within a orbit cycle, the temporal spacing of these visits is unevenly distributed.
As a result, SWOT may miss periods of minimum or maximum flow for a river or lake,
thus skewing a monthly estimate (Biancamaria, Mognard, et al. 2010). This could be es­
pecially important for rivers where a sharp increase in water flow occurs due to ice break
at the end of a melt season (Papa, Biancamaria, et al. 2012). By methods presented in
Biancamaria, Mognard, et al. (2010), it is estimated that the error due to temporal sam­
pling in the SWOT nominal phase ranges from near 6% at high latitudes to 10% near the
equator. The temporal frequency of SWOT also limits the satellites ability to see flash
floods, and the expected three­year lifetime of the mission indicates that the satellite will
not by design monitor long term climate changes.
Other limitations in SWOT measurements of land hydrology is the occurrence of ’dark
water’. This is the case when the prior river data base (see Section 3.1.1) indicates that
an area should be water, but the received backscatter is low, indicating land. In this case,
the height estimate of the low backscatter return has a very high uncertainty and is not
useful. This could occur in the case of very smooth water surfaces, where wind speed
is much less than 1 m s−1. In this case, the slightly off­nadir incidence angles cause
specular signal reflection in the direction away from the antenna. SWOT then measures
best when the water surface is slightly rough, but not so rough that the signal is scattered
in many directions (Biancamaria, Lettenmaier, and Pavelsky 2016; JPL 2023a).
2.1.3
Previous work
Based on a count by JPL (see https://swot.jpl.nasa.gov/), there have been over 500 pub­
lications that utilize simulated SWOT data (Langhorst et al. 2019; Du et al. 2023), assess
the potential or accuracy of the anticipated SWOT data (Bonnema and Hossain 2019;
Biancamaria, Mognard, et al. 2010; Frasson et al. 2021), or express the need for a satel­
lite like SWOT since the first by Alsdorf, Lettenmaier, and Vörösmarty (2003). Some
of these studies have investigated the potential improvements of utilizing SWOT in hy­
drologic and hydraulic models via calibration or data assimilation (Oubanas et al. 2018;
Andreadis, Brinkerhoff, and Gleason 2020; Elmer, McCreight, and Hain 2021; Verma and
Indu 2023), discharge estimation from space (Durand, Gleason, Garambois, et al. 2016;
Gleason and Durand 2020; Durand, Gleason, Pavelsky, et al. 2023), effectiveness for
monitoring in­land water storage (Bonnema and Hossain 2019; Nair et al. 2022; Papa
and Frappart 2021), and estimating water surface extent (Prigent et al. 2016; Desrochers
et al. 2021).
For the focus application in this study, river discharge, the prospects of SWOT have been
promising. Verma and Indu (2023) find that while the temporal revisit time of SWOT is
only a handful of times within the 21­day cycle, utilizing discharge estimates from SWOT
is just as good as daily in­situ data for hydrological model calibration over the Narmada
River in India. In a study by Durand, Gleason, Garambois, et al. (2016), five algorithms for
estimating river discharge from SWOT observations are explored and compared across
19 rivers of varying river type. The most positive results show that discharge could be
estimated by at least one of the algorithms with relative root mean square error (RRMSE)
less than 35% in non­braided rivers, with errors as low as 1.2% in some cases. Du et al.

rithm and proposes an additional prior constraint of historical discharge from hydrometric
stations, achieving RRMSE of 24­50% for three river reaches along the Yangtze River.
Though this is only few of the many studies conducted on SWOT, it is clear from this
sample that we are stepping into a new era of remote sensing of in­land water.

resolution, radar accuracy, and precise orbit determination (Escudier et al. 2017). Here
we present a brief overview of nadir radar altimetry principles to show the foundation on
which the wide­swath altimetry concepts are built.

An area of considerable study for radar altimetry is how to determine the distance r from

time delay called a waveform. Determining height (and other geophysical parameters like
wind speed and significant wave height) from the returned waveform require waveform re­
tracking, which can be done in many different ways depending on the specific application
(ex. over ice, ocean, or in­land waters) (Escudier et al. 2017; Villadsen et al. 2016).
The spatial resolution in radar altimetry is limited by the radar footprint. A large footprint
can cause significant error for in­land water surface height determination, especially for
small water features and near shores. The footprint is heavily dependent on the size of
the antenna used and processing of the signal. Certain advanced processing techniques,
such as synthetic aperature radar (SAR) can drastically improve a satellite’s along­track
resolution. In SAR mode, radar pulses are coherently combined based on unique doppler
frequency, producing along­track resolution as fine as one half the physical antenna size.
There is still ambiguity in the across­track measurements however, as returned signals
at equal range on the right and left side of the footprint cannot be separated (Rodríguez
et al. 2017).

2.2.3
Error sources
The typical media effect errors in NADIR radar altimetry are also found in interferometric
altimetry. In addition, the interferometric altimetry geometry introduces some new errors
for height determination.
Media delays
Media delays in radar altimetry are caused by gases and different physical properties in
the atmosphere effecting atmospheric refractivity. The radar signal is propagated through
the atmosphere twice on its path, in which the signal travel time is effected by the differ­
ent atmospheric conditions it passes through. The primary media delays that must be
modeled and corrected for are the ionospheric, wet troposphere, and dry troposphere
corrections. Media effects cause a delay along the range direction, and the error in the
water surface height and cross­track location (y) are calculated using the look­angle (Ro­
dríguez et al. 2017).

In the ionosphere, clouds of free elections disperse the radar signal. The total electron
content (TEC), or electron density, changes with location, heat from the sun, solar cycles,
and geomagnetic storms. TEC can generally be modeled with data from satellites oper­
ating at two different frequencies. This is because the ionospheric delay depends both
on TEC and wave frequency (Escudier et al. 2017).

Gases and water vapor in the troposphere, as well as variations in pressure and temper­
ature, also change the refractive index of the medium the radar pulse must pass through.
The height correction due to the tropospheric delay is generally modeled as a function of
only the atmospheric pressure, as the dry gas portion of the delay is approximately con­
stant at ­2.3 m (Escudier et al. 2017). The atmospheric pressure, and thus the remaining
height correction due to tropospheric delay, is usually estimated from global atmospheric
models.
The wet tropospheric correction accounts for the amount of water vapor and cloud liquid
water that effects the atmospheric refractivity in the troposphere. The wet troposphere
correction can cause height errors on the order of millimeters up to 40 cm, depending on
season, latitude and region. The correction can be estimated using numerical weather
models for determining water vapor content, but more accurately using multi­channel mi­
crowave radiometry (Escudier et al. 2017; Fu, Alsdorf, Morrow, et al. 2012). For the
SWOT mission, a microwave radiometer is equipped and pointing nadir. Fu, Alsdorf,
Morrow, et al. (2012) considered how the two­dimensional variability in water vapor will
influence SWOT measurements for data in the cross­track direction, finding that over land
the correction variability should not exceed 2 cm.
Roll error
The roll error is the primary error source in swath­altimetry (Rodríguez et al. 2017). The
error can be best visualized as an unknown rotation of the entire swath and baseline,
caused by drift in the satellite attitude.

the antenna look­angle at a fine enough scale. The current solution for this is to design
the attitude system with very slow drift and to use cross­over calibration to reduce the
remaining roll error. The cross­over calibration for SWOT, as described in Rodríguez et
al. (2017), uses different SWOT passes over the ocean to remove the roll error. This
however poses an issue for land hydrology purposes, as the cross­over calibration points
could be tens to thousands of kilometers away for in­land lakes and rivers.

Systematic errors to the interferometric phase are caused by thermal differences between
the baseline and interference from the satellite on the antenna signal reception. The
thermal inhomogeneity is usually handed with a calibration loop, where the system is
designed such that any drift in phase due to system temperature differences is very slow.
The errors due to the interference from the satellite itself on the signal reception at the
antenna are corrected via cross­over calibration.

2.3.1
Gauged constrained
Gauge constrained methods may use information from the gauge directly in the discharge
model. This could be an in­situ depth measurement (Garambois and Monnier 2015), a
gauge­calibrated hydrologic model (Lin et al. 2019), or what is known as the rating curve
relationship of river height and discharge determined at the gauge (Paris et al. 2016;
Kouraev et al. 2004).
Rating curves
The rating curve method for RSQ utilizes satellite observations of river stage to extend
the discharge timeseries at a gauge station. Here, the gauge determined rating curve
parameters, values a, b and the bathymetry with respect to the gauge datum Z0 (Eq.
14), are estimated by relating gauge measured height Hg and discharge Qg. With these
parameters, it is then possible to convert satellite observations of stage to discharge.
This method of course relies on the presence of a gauge where the rating curve has been
established. The rating curve relationship cannot be extended in space, as the estimated
curve is unique to the cross­section at the gauge and changing topography may cause the
relationship to change (Rantz 1982). In addition, the rating curve parameters are liable to
drift over time if the relationship is not periodically calibrated at the gauge (Di Baldassarre
and Montanari 2009; Hannah et al. 2011). Rating curves have the longest history in RSQ
and have had great success for extending gauge timeseries, which is especially valued
at discontinued gauges. Examples of rating curves applied to satellite data can be found
in Koblinsky et al. (1993), Chin et al. (2001), Michailovsky et al. (2012), and Paris et al.
(2016).

Mean Flow Constant Roughness
The Mean Flow Constant Roughness (MFCR) method is the simplest approach presented
here. Using the modified Manning’s equation (Eq. 16), Manning’s n takes an assumed
constant value of n = 0.03 and the base area A0 is estimated in the same way that the
MFG algorithm estimates B, by constraining the modeled mean to match the mean flow
from a hydrologic model.
Mass­conserved Flow Law Inversion (McFLI)
Mass­Conserved Flow Law Inversion (McFLI) methods utilize both flow laws and mass
continuity to estimate discharge and generally require a prior in the form of a proba­
bility distribution and/or first guess estimates of parameters. Three recently developed
McFLI algorithms are described here: Metropolis­Manning (MetroMan) (Durand, Neal, et
al. 2014), GaMo (Garambois and Monnier 2015), and geomorphically informed Bayesian
At­many­stations hydraulic geometry­Manning (geoBAM) (Brinkerhoff et al. 2020) algo­
rithms.
MetroMan is a reach­averaged discharge algorithm developed by Durand, Neal, et al.
(2014) that utilizes both mass conservation and the modified Manning’s equation (Eq. 16),
where A0 and n are assumed to be temporally invariant. The first­guess and bounds for
the parameter A0 are chosen based on satellite observed timeseries of river area changes.
First guess and bounds for n are chosen based on general estimates. A Metropolis algo­
rithm using a Markov Chain Monte Carlo method is used to estimate the parameters.
The GaMo algorithm developed by Garambois and Monnier (2015) is also built for reach­
averaged discharge and utilizes the modified Manning’s equation (Eq. 16). GaMo as­
sumes that discharge is constant within a reach, and similar to MetroMan takes A0 first
guesses and bounds from a timeseries of satellite observed δA, W and H. The first guess
for n is also chosen by the user and is usually centered around the usual value of n = 0.03.
The first guess and bounds for Q are derived from the MFCR discharge estimate, which
is informed by a hydrologic model. Garambois and Monnier (2015) utilizes a Levenberg­
Marquardt solver to estimate the unknown parameters and Q.
The geoBAM algorithm (Brinkerhoff et al. 2020) is the most complex RSQ algorithm de­
scribed here. geoBAM is built off of the previously developed BAM algorithm, (Hagemann,
Gleason, and Durand 2017) in which Manning’s equation is used together with the empir­
ical AMHG model. geoBAM adds to this by utilizing priors based on specific river class.
Previously, McFLI algorithms generally use the same priors of geomorphic parameters
(n, A0) for all rivers, even though these can change drastically with channel size and
shape. The prior used in geoBAM is built from 190 river in the US, in which 15 differ­
ent geomorphic classes were determined. The priors used to estimate Q, n and A0 are

then dependent on which geomorphic class the river is assigned based on the remotely
sensed width. geoBAM was validated on 108 gauges in the Mackenzie River basin, where
Brinkerhoff et al. (2020) found that the method was a great improvement from BAM.
Comparison of RSQ methods
In their comparison of discharge algorithms, Durand, Gleason, Garambois, et al. (2016)
evaluated AMHG, MetroMan, GaMo, MFG, and MFCR over 19 rivers. AMHG did not
perform best for any rivers considered, and performed much worse than any previous
study using AMHG with a median Relative­RMSE (RRMSE) of 86%. This was attributed
to the larger number of observations used and the fact that wet and dry seasons may
have very different AMHG parameters. MetroMan performed the best out of all algo­
rithms for 8/19 tested rivers, in which the RRMSE ranged between 5% and 45%. GaMo
performed best for 4/19 rivers, in which RRMSE percentage ranged between 18% and
69%. One major limitation to MetroMan and GaMo is the assumption of a temporally
invariant n. This choice makes parameter estimation computationally easier, but pre­
vents the change in discharge that could occur with changing geomorphology and bank
sediment (Durand, Gleason, Garambois, et al. 2016). MFG performed the best for 3/19
tested rivers (RRMSE ranging between 16% and 77%), and MFCR performed best for
4/19 tested rivers (RRMSE ranging between 10% and 45%). Both MFG and MFCR were
found to be heavily influenced by their hydrologic model priors. For MFCR, it was found
that in some cases the discharge estimates were far larger than those in the hydrologic
model used to constrain them, due likely to the constant value for n.

Pixel Cloud product: L2_HR_PIXC
The pixel cloud product provides the highest spatial resolution of the Level 2 hydrology
products. The data is provided in 64x64 km2 tiles, with a tile on each side of the satellite
groundtrack. Each tile contains a ’pixel cloud’ of nonuniform data entries, where many
locations within the tile may have no data (corresponding mostly to land). For each pixel
where there is data, the product records location, height, pixel classification (see Figure
3.1.2a), and pixel surface area. In addition there are fields for geophysical and calibration
corrections, quality flags, and related satellite orbit data.

The spatial resolution varies depending on the satellite orbit, location in the swath, and
topography at the time of capture. The pixel cloud layer is formed by applying azimuth
multilooking to the SLC image from KaRIn with four looks, followed by an adaptive aver­
aging (see Figure 3.1.2b) to meet science requirements of noise. The adaptive averaging
is essentially a secondary multilooking, where interferogram pixel class is considered in
the averaging to reduce land contamination. Decimation is not applied after this step, and
as a result adjacent pixels in the pixel cloud product can be highly correlated. The azimuth
resolution is roughly 22 meters. Due to the side­looking geometry of the measurements,
the cross­track resolution can vary from 60 m near nadir to around 10 m at far range
(Biancamaria, Lettenmaier, and Pavelsky 2016). Known issues with the L2_HR_PIXC
product include the overestimation of the dark water pixels, phase unwrapping errors,
over­classification of water near bright land, poor classification of pixels near water­land
boundaries, and layover errors. In the event of dark water, a height value is reported
as an interpolation of surrounding bright (high signal­to­noise) water detected pixels. A
full description of the data can be found in the Product Description (JPL 2023d) and a
full description of the data processing can be found in the algorithm documentation (JPL
2023a).

the node product of the L2_HR_RiverSP product set is used in this analysis for the sub­
reach spatial resolution. The product is produced for inland and coastal rivers outlined
in the SWORD prior river database. Data from the point cloud product are classified as
river or non­river points and assigned to the closest river node in the SWORD database.
The assigned points are then weighted by their uncertainties and aggregated, producing

4.1.1
Spatial coverage and resolution
The extended coverage in the spatial domain is explored here for the new SWOT data.
To get an idea of the advances in coverage with SWOT, RiverSP data are compared with
ICESat­2 ATL13 data along river profiles. Since these products do not currently have any
overlapping dates, SWOT data from 2024 is compared to same month ICESat­2 data from
2023. For this reason, comparisons of coverage are purely visual and not intended for
height validation. SWOT RiverSP data are filtered to remove nodes with dark pixel fraction
greater than 40% and data with ice cover are filtered out. ICESat­2 data with occurrence
value less than 75% are removed. Both data sets are trimmed to a PRD width dependent
buffer and projected onto the river centerline.
The second analysis within the spatial domain aims to highlight the spatial resolution of
the new SWOT data. Braided rivers have been a challenge for river remote sensing, as
a small footprint is required to map the many small channels. In addition, the channels
are often surrounded by wet sand banks, and dynamic knowledge of river masks are
necessary to filter the altimetry data (Schneider et al. 2017). With SWOT, the dynamic
mask and water height can be observed simultaneously. The PIXC product is compared to
ICESat­2’s ATL03 product over the braided Rakaia River for data acquisitions on 2023­08­
02 and 2023­08­08, respectively. It is assumed here that the river pattern has not changed
drastically in this time. River location and the identified intersecting data are shown in
Figure 4.1.1. Google satellite imagery are shown beneath the pixel cloud data to illustrate
how SWOT is able to identify channel patterns, though images are not necessarily from
nearby dates.
PIXC data are filtered by open water class and trimmed to a buffer dependent on the PRD
width around the river centerline. Only the strong ATL03 beams are used here. Photons
are filtered by a quality flag and trimmed to the same PRD derived buffer. To handle the
noise present in ATL03, a hampel filter is applied (data point width k=10) and the surface
height profile is subsequently extracted using a running median (k=21). PIXC data are
interpolated to the coordinates of the ATL03 crossings by taking the mean aggregate of
the 8 nearest neighbors. Neighbors with a euclidean distance more than 20 meters from
the coordinate location are discarded from the average.

4.2
Discharge estimation
The exploratory analysis is dedicated to better understanding the new potential and ac­
curacy of the first release SWOT data. The study now branches into the river discharge
application of the SWOT data. Here we utilize three of the discharge models presented
before: Rating Curves (M1), Statistical Coefficients (M2; Bjerklie’s Model 1), and MFCR
(M3). Methods for preparing the multi­mission satellite data for input into these models
are described first. For merging height and width timeseries from multiple sources, a
state­space model is employed. The prepared data are then input into the RSQ models,
and the chosen priors (where applicable) are described.
4.2.1
Height and width field estimation
For each of these models, we need some combination of height, width and slope time­
series at the location where we want to estimate discharge. With satellite data, we can
observe each of these properties, though at spatial and temporal resolutions defined by
the satellite orbit. For some satellites, this could mean only one observation near the

based on the maximum expected width of the river. Transects are placed at 200 meter

Estimate NDWI from multispectral data
NDWI is calculated over a PRD width dependent buffer surrounding the river centerline
for each multispectral image acquisition, via Eq. 24. NDWI is the normalized difference
between the received power in the green (G) and near­infrared (NIR) wavelengths.
NDWI = (G −NIR)/(G + NIR)
(24)
Multispectral data from Landsat­8 and Sentinel­2 cannot penetrate clouds and must be
filtered for cloud cover. For Sentinel­2, pixels with cloudy pixel percentage greater than
20% are removed. Then, the 60 meter resolution cloud flag from the data product is
used to filter out pixels contaminated by opaque and cirrus clouds. For Landsat­8, a
’cloudiness’ estimate is computed using the Google Earth Engine algorithm that estimates
the likelihood of clouds from pixel brightness, temperature, and Normalized­Difference
Snow Index. Images with within­buffer cloudiness greater than 20% are removed, and
NDWI is calculated for the remaining cloud filtered data.
Estimate river masks and calculate width
To estimate the river masks, a threshold (t) for NDWI needs to be set. Figure 4.2.4 shows
how varying NDWI effects the width estimation for a single transect near gauge 08MF005
on the Fraser, across both summer and winter conditions. Though higher NDWI threshold
is generally used for water masking algorithms, here lower NDWI threshold (0.2) shows
more agreement between satellites and with the RGB imagery. In addition, the higher
NDWI threshold causes width estimates to be much lower than expected (or fails to reg­
ister water completely) in many of the Landsat­8 images, an example of which is shown
for the summer t = 0.4 case in Figure 4.2.4. In order to preserve as much information
as possible, a threshold of 0.2 was chosen. This comes with the consequence of more
regions outside of the river, such as wet banks, being classified as water. Additional
post­processing is done to further handle outliers.
Post­processing of width data
The extracted width data contain few unrealistic river width values due likely to cloud cover
that was not properly filtered, the influence of nearby water features or soil moisture. To
address this, an additional filtering is applied to the extracted timeseries. At this step,
the SWOT width data are also included in the width timeseries. Timeseries are formed
for 1 km segments along the river. The center of this 1 km river stretch is referred to as
the virtual station (VS). For the discharge models presented later, the VS is collocated
with the gauge station. The two closest transects to the VS are selected for the width
timeseries. Similarly, SWOT widths within a 200 m distance from the VS are selected.
This is to account for the large width variation the river exhibits. A Hampel filter is applied
with a window size one third of the total timeseries data length.

6
Discussion
6.1
Exploratory analysis
The analysis of SWOT’s spatial resolution and coverage are addressed first. Following
this, the results of the SWOT height validation are discussed. Height validation results are
evaluated on a sub­reach level, which is consistent with the discharge estimation section
presented later. Models are referred to by their shortened name, such as ’M1’, going
forward.
6.1.1
Spatial coverage and resolution
The increased number of observations in the spatial domain with the SWOT mission is
evident from river profile comparisons of SWOT vs. ICESat­2. This is expected, since
ICESat­2 gathers data along six beam lines and SWOT can observe a full 120 km swath
of the river at a time. Over the Missouri River (Figure 5.1.1), ICESat­2 observations are
at irregular distances and contain large spatial gaps along the river centerline. SWOT is
able to fill these gaps with a single pass in some cases, providing both increased cover­
age and detail. Here the improved coverage is illustrated, but future work could quantify
the improved coverage with SWOT through a global comparison with previous altimetry
missions or the current river gauge network, such as in Pavelsky et al. (2014).
In addition to spatial coverage, the spatial resolution of the new SWOT data was analyzed.
The science goals for SWOT are to be able to observe rivers 100 m in width, with the
potential to observe rivers as small as 50 m wide. The spatial resolution of SWOT is
tested through comparison with the ICESat­2 ATL03 product over the braided Rakaia
River, shown in Figure 5.1.2. It is found that SWOT is able to map rivers near 100 m
(∼0.001◦) wide in agreement with ICESat­2. Through visual inspection, we see that the
satellite can identify channels on the order of 30 m as well, though these channels may
be only one pixel wide and their accuracy is unverified (see Figure A.2.1 in Appendix).
The spatial resolution shown in the first release data is validating for the SWOT science
goals, though a more comprehensive study of the uncertainty in the observed surface
water extent is desired. Such a study could use water frequency maps derived from high
resolution multispectral images to validate the SWOT observed water extent. The need
for accurate extent estimates is of particular importance for RSQ, as will be addressed in
the next sections.
6.1.2
Height validation
The science goals for SWOT observed heights indicate a 10 cm or less error over a
10 km reach. The results are shown in Table 5.1.1 for the comparison of SWOT with
ICESat­2 along the Fraser River. The estimated accuracy with respect to ICESat­2 is
very promising, with RMSE equal or less than 10 cm depending on the filters used. Note
that this is at a sub­reach scale and using data at only 200 m segments.
The heights are further validated against in­situ data at five gauges along the Fraser.
Though this validation considers 400 m SWOT segment averages of the river, the results
(Table 5.1.2) seem worse than the previous validation at the 200 m level, with RMSE
ranging between 8 and 25 cm across the gauges. One potential reason for this is that the
gauge comparison provides a more realistic estimate. The gauge ­ SWOT comparison
considers many timesteps of data at all of the gauges, rather than just a one­day com­
parison over one segment of the river. The estimation of the offset between the satellite
50
Investigation of first release SWOT data for inland water applications
==PAGE:59==
and gauge could be affecting RMSE results for the gauge validation as well. The offset
is estimated from the 2­node timeseries, which can be very short and may not represent
the signal well enough to determine the offset.
Even considering the higher RMSE, both ICESat­2 and gauge validation results suggest
that SWOT performs even better than expected. From the sub­reach analysis, we find
that the RiverSP node product may be valuable for estimating river heights, and likewise
discharge, at sub­reach scales. SWOT’s 200­400 m height accuracy may not be as good
as reach­averaged accuracy from itself or ICESat­2, but the combined spatial resolution,
coverage and accuracy of observations is better than ever before. Though promising, the
results here are limited to the Fraser River. To better analyze the sub­reach accuracy of
SWOT heights a more global approach is desired. It would be advantageous to validate
how the accuracy changes with increasing scales, ranging from 200 m to the full 10 km
reach length.
6.1.3
Potential errors
Potential error sources affecting the node heights could stem from both error in the mea­
surement principle, primarily the roll error, and error in the PIXC product. The magnitude
of the roll error will depend on where in the cross­track direction the height is estimated as
well as proximity to the ocean, where the cross­over correction is estimated. Though the
Fraser River is relatively close to the ocean, the height error shows the same dependence
on cross­track as a river further inland. Comparison of the height error in the cross­track
direction between a segment of the Congo River and two segments of the Fraser can be
found in the Appendix, Figure A.1.1. This suggests that the cross­over correction may not
perform well for inland water. Additional error to the height estimates could be due to the
current known issues with the PIXC version C product. Issues with the pixel classification
for water near land and water near bright land could skew the height estimates. Land­
contaminated data could be included and valid height observations could be classified
incorrectly. With later versions of the data product, any error due to this is expected to
improve.
6.2
Discharge estimation
Three different RSQ methods are compared for modeling discharge at five gauges along
the Fraser and one gauge (Salekhard station) on the Ob River. Timeseries are shown
in Figure 5.2.5 and the discharge field for 9 km around each gauge are shown in Figure
5.2.7 for M2 and M3. Model performance is documented in Table 5.2.1.
6.2.1
Discharge timeseries
M1 performs better than M2 and M3 for most stations. M1 mostly captures the timing
and general amplitude of seasonal peaks. In some cases, these peak events are slightly
under or overestimated. This is a result of the disagreement between altimeters or data
gaps due to satellite repeat periods. If the satellite does not pass over the river during
the period of peak flow, the peak flow will not be measured. This happens in the summer
of 2023 near gauges 08KA004 and 08MC018, and as a result the discharge is severely
underestimated. Gauge 08MD013 shows poor performance by all methods. This is due to
a sparse height timeseries, which all models depend on. At SAL, M1 performs reasonably
well, though the discharge is slightly underestimated for the peak flow. This is due to an
underestimation of peak river heights in the state­space model. In addition, remaining
outliers in the height data cause a discharge spike in the winter of 2019 that is not observed
at the gauge. See Figure B.0.1 in the Appendix for the state­space modeled height input
into the discharge models and the available height data.

In 4/6 of the locations, M2 identifies the seasonal pattern well, with varying performance
in estimating the magnitude of peak and low flows. M3 performs well for gauge 08MF005,
though in most other cases is characterized by severe instability. This is attributed to the
model’s high sensitivity to outliers in the width data. The width timeseries from multispec­
tral data and SWOT have generally less observations in the winter months, since data with
cloud cover and ice conditions are filtered out. As a result, the winter portions of the time­
series are sparse, and single data points carry a lot more weight in estimating the winter
widths. This is in part handled with the seasonal width filtering presented in Section 4.2.1.
This method was however built primarily with the timeseries at gauge 08MF005 in mind.
When extended to other gauges, the width filtering proves inadequate for M3. Due to this
instability in M3, further analysis is focused primarily on M1 and M2. M2 also relies on
the noisy width timeseries, but tends to perform better than M3 across all gauges. One
explanation of this lies in the governing equations of the models. M2 utilizes the width
field only once (Eq. 19) in its expression, but since δA is a function of width, it appears
twice in the expression for M3 (Eq. 16). The noise is then compounded for M3, leading
to instability.
Gauge 08MH024 is characterized by long periods of ice cover, which can be seen in the
gaps and straight lines of the gauge data. M1 is able to generally predict the summer
periods of high flow, but the other models are characterized by model gaps where slopes
are too close to zero. In addition, the width field for 08MH024 is highly variable with
no clear seasonal signal, adding more noise than information into the model. In future
iterations of this work, data could be filtered based on known ice conditions at the gauge.
This however requires the presence of a gauge.
6.2.2
Removing SWOT from the models
At first glance, it does not seem that the removal of SWOT data from the model has any
effect on the modeled discharge at gauge 08MF005 (see Figure 5.2.6). Since SWOT is
only available in 2024, and the other altimetry data is not yet posted for most of 2024,
the major advantages of including SWOT are hard to see temporally. Looking at the
08MF005 timeseries in 2024 when SWOT is included, we know that SWOT is the driving
data source for the rising flow in the model as we enter into the summer months. We
cannot yet compare how this increase would be predicted without SWOT, but we can tell
that SWOT is able to see the summer trend and the model compares well to the gauge
discharge here.
6.2.3
Discharge fields
Estimated discharge fields for M2 and M3 are shown for each gauge in Figure 5.2.7.
Viewing the discharge as a field provides a better picture of how advantageous SWOT
data is for RSQ. In this study and in previous studies, to have either a dense spatial or
temporal field of observations, aggregation of data in one or both of these dimensions is
required. SWOT is able to observe the important components for RSQ at denser spatial
and temporal coverage than all of the other altimetry satellites considered here combined.
This is shown by both the spatial coverage study from before and by an additional figure
showing the spatio­temporal data distribution of height data near gauge 08MF005 on the
Fraser (Figure B.0.4 in the Appendix). Due to the limited temporal availability of SWOT,
it is not clear by the discharge fields shown here how SWOT affects the spatio­temporal
resolution of RSQ. However, we show that methods such as this are prepared for the
influx of SWOT data. With a longer timeseries of SWOT data, which grows every day, it
is expected that the discharge fields here will be much more informed.

6.2.4
KGE scores
M1 shows the best overall KGE scores for every station except 08MF005, where M2 and
M3 perform better. The superior performance of M1 is not surprising, as it is the only
model that utilizes in­situ data. This makes the comparable results for M2, a gauge un­
constrained model, all the more exciting. For gauges 08KA004, 08MC018, and 08MF005,
M2 results have overall KGE near or better than the gauge­informed M1.
M2 shows a generally higher bias component β, indicating that the overall mean of the
model is higher than the gauge mean. This can be attributed to the M2 dependence on
the DHI­GHM. For many of these gauges, the DHI­GHM slightly over­predicts the peak
and low flows at the gauge (see Figure C.0.2). As a result, M2 is constrained to a mean
flow that is higher than the true mean. For gauges 08MF005 and 08MC018, this results
in model estimates that have higher low flows, producing a discharge timeseries with an
overall higher mean even if the modeled peak flow is underestimated.
The correlation term r is generally high across both M1 and M2, with values above 0.7 for
all stations except for 08MH024. This is in line with the abilities of M1 and M2 to identify
seasonal trends. The variability term α indicates that overall, the modeled discharge from
M1 and M2 tend to underestimate the true variability seen by the gauge. This can be seen
in the smaller range in discharge estimated by the models, where low flows are slightly
overestimated and high flows slightly underestimated.
6.2.5
Parameter estimation
The results of the parameter estimation for all three models are shown in Table 5.2.2.
Excluding 08MH024, estimates for parameter b in M1 are overall reasonable, as this value
is expected to vary between 1.3 and 2 for narrow and wide rivers respectively (Paris et al.
2016). The estimated values for a are mostly convincing. Paris et al. (2016) allows the
a parameter to vary between 1 and 1000. Gauges 08MH024 and SAL found estimates
for a and/or b outside of these expected ranges. For 08MH024, the lack of gauge data
during the winter months means that the estimated rating curve is based only on the high
flow events and the full curve cannot be extracted from the gauge alone. For SAL, the
estimated a and b parameters are both unexpected. The value b is rather low indicating a
narrow river, even though the width is near 3000 km at SAL (Figure B.0.2). The parameter
a is also far outside the expected range. Upon inspection of the estimated rating curve
relationship at the gauge, we see that the curve shifts drastically over time (see Figure
C.0.1). The estimated curve used in our model is an oversimplification of the height­
discharge relationship at the gauge, and a temporally changing curve would be better
suited here.
Most interesting are the results for B and A0. To better understand the bathymetry pa­
rameter B for M2, the average modeled depth Yavg is included as well. Surveys of the
Fraser River near the outlet indicate that the river depth is near 10 m (AVADEPTH 2024)
(see Figure C.0.3 for details). Though underestimated, this is on the same magnitude of
estimated depths for 08MH024 and 08MF005, the two closest gauges to the river outlet.
The river depth is expected to be much larger near 08MD013 and 08MC018 (Tikkanen
2024). However, the estimated average depth does not seem to change much with dis­
tance from the outlet. This suggests bias within M2 towards more shallow geometries.
The parameter A0 does not seem entirely unreasonable over the Fraser, but based on
the instability of M3 is not to be trusted. For SAL, the estimate of Yavg is lower than ex­
pected by at least ∼5 m (Owen and Micklin 2024). The estimate of A0 at SAL is highly
unlikely, considering the width of the river, and this is reflected in the poor performance
of M3.

6.2.6
Potential errors
Potential errors on the RSQ models other than those mentioned in the discussion above
are noted here. M1 is heavily dependent on the gauge defined rating curve. The dis­
charge for several gauges use provisional data that has not been calibrated with the latest
in­situ discharge measurement, thus the provided height­discharge relationship is slightly
skewed from the truth, affecting M1. Since we take the gauge discharge as truth, this will
not show up in the RMSE for M1. It is more importantly a limitation of the RSQ rating
curve method itself. If rating curve relationships are not calibrated frequently, this method
for RSQ may not be able to monitor the true changes in the river flow over time. Ad­
ditionally, a single rating curve may not be appropriate for a cross­section that changes
significantly in time, as seen at the Salekhard station. Even so, M1 still performs the best
over most gauges for the models compared here. For M2 and M3, potential errors are
their sensitivity to noisy width timeseries and their dependence on the DHI­GHM prior,
as described previously. For all models, error in the SWOT heights due to roll error and
product processing are carried over as well.
6.3
Moving forward
Described here are the first steps that should be taken to further understand the newly
released SWOT data and their application in RSQ models. The discussion is followed
by a table summarizing these next steps, including even more ideas for future work not
described in detail here.
The models used here are some of the simplest available. For the gauge unconstrained
methods, the first step to achieving better results would be to use a better performing
method. Models such as MetroMan, GaMo and geoBAM are well documented and are
known to provide better results than the statistical model (M2) and MFCR model (M3)
(Durand, Gleason, Pavelsky, et al. 2023). Additionally, better ways to constrain estimates
of river bathymetry or base cross­sectional area could be used. For example, Musaeus
et al. (2024) uses low­flow crossings of ICESat­2 to map as much of the river bathymetry
as possible, leaving only what is beneath low­flow levels to be assumed.
The simple models were chosen here in order to focus more on the quality of the new
SWOT data and allow for comparison of several RSQ methods within the limited project
time. An important takeaway from this study is the importance of quality width timeseries
for gauge unconstrained RSQ. The method used for extracting widths from multispectral
images is fairly lenient, meaning more river widths are captured at the cost of outliers
and unrealistically large width variation. The seasonal filter helps, but requires specific
tuning for the river it is applied to. Even with these efforts, the extracted width timeseries
is unevenly distributed by season, with most of the observations occurring in summer.
In order to improve RSQ results, more consistent and accurate methods for river width
estimation are desired. It will be exciting to see how the width timeseries is affected by
a longer period of available SWOT data. A full hydrologic cycle of SWOT widths could in
theory be used to constrain width estimates from multispectral methods.
It would be ideal to create more general RSQ models. M2 and M3 rely on parameters
(NDWI threshold, seasonal filter dates) that are tuned for this particular river. In addition,
the initial guess values for parameter estimation of B and A0 are informed based on the
expected depth of the Fraser. While M2 was able to mostly identify relative magnitude of
discharge when extended to the Ob River, the model is inconsistent from year to year and
contains unrealistic spikes in estimated discharge. A more generalized model, that does
not require location specific parameters, would be much more useful for global RSQ.

Lastly, it will be interesting to see how the models would perform on SWOT data alone.
By combining data from many satellites, the temporal density of observations is improved.
It is therefore expected that in some cases using only SWOT would be a disadvantage
when compared to the combination of SWOT with other missions. During the writing of
this report, the first discharge product at the node level has been released. The product
utilizes the geoBAM algorithm to estimate discharge, which combines the advantages of
some of the best available RSQ algorithms. Due to time limitations, the discharge product
is not evaluated here, though it would be highly valuable to see how the product compares
to the multi­satellite approach.

6.4
Conclusion
With increasing demands on freshwater resources and the increasing frequency of ex­
treme weather such as drought and flood, monitoring river flow conditions is imperative.
In this context, the anticipation for SWOT has been great. Understanding the satellite’s
abilities is the first step towards using this new tool to better understand and monitor fresh­
water dynamics and storage. This study has been a first investigation into the height and
width data over rivers from the newly launched SWOT mission. The SWOT data was
first explored generally, followed by an application to river discharge estimation with a
multi­satellite approach.
Q1: Is the measurement accuracy of SWOT as expected over rivers?
SWOT’s height observation accuracy was found to be better than suggested by the mis­
sion’s science goals. The 10 cm accuracy goal is applicable for 10 km reach averaged
products. Here, height accuracy was found to be on the order of 10 cm, with RMSE rang­
ing between 8 and 25 cm, at only 200­400 m reach averages. This is exciting for the
56

==PAGE:65==
prospect of node­level discharge estimates, which could provide highly detailed informa­
tion about river dynamics.
Q2: Is the spatial resolution and coverage of SWOT as expected?
The spatial resolution of SWOT was found to be well­matched with the proposed abilities
from the SWOT science team. Over braided rivers, it was found that SWOT is able to
observe river channels near 100 m wide in agreement with ICESat­2. The satellite may
be able to observe even smaller channels, as the science goals suggest, though further
validation is required to confirm this.
The spatial coverage over rivers with SWOT is compared visually with what was previ­
ously possible with satellites. SWOT is able to observe 120 km stretches of a river at
time, providing insight into the river height profile and spatial structure. Though individual
acquisitions provide only a screenshot of the river dynamics, a new image is acquired
every 21 days, where portions of the river may be observed with frequencies as little as 3
days. Such a profile may take entire years to observe from previous altimetry satellites.
Q3: How are our current RSQ methods affected by the incoming SWOT data?
Lastly, the multi­satellite combined observations of river width and height were used to es­
timate discharge for both gauge constrained (M1) and unconstrained (M2 and M3) models.
The gauge constrained model performed well, with KGE > 0.6 for all gauges not affected
by ice and where enough height data are available. M2 performed comparably to M1 for
most locations, despite its independence from gauge data. From model performance of
M2 and M3, it became clear that the uncertainty of satellite observed river widths play a
key factor in their ability to make realistic discharge estimates.
The short SWOT timeseries available (< 5 months) was limiting in the evaluation of how
SWOT affects current RSQ methods. However, from analysis of the short timeseries,
SWOT’s ability to predict observe heights and estimate discharge at the node level is
promising. For the gauges tested here, discharge estimates from M1 (and often, M2)
align well with the gauge defined truth from spring 2024 and into summer.
Where do we go from here?
Though the results presented here are promising, they are not accurate enough to be
accepted widely by hydrologists. For direct use in water resource management and hy­
drologic models, RSQ still has a long way to go. Then again, the observations from SWOT
are far better than any previous satellite for inland hydrology, and they provide valuable
insight into rivers where we have limited or no data at all.
Here it was found that width observations play a powerful role in gauge unconstrained
RSQ methods. As it is, width estimates from non­SWOT sources are very noisy, and
the accuracy of SWOT widths at the node level remains unknown. Future work towards
obtaining better estimates of river widths from satellite data would be highly valuable.
Second, SWOT heights are still not accurate enough at a small enough spatial scale
for use in water resource management. Understanding the error sources in the SWOT
heights will help lead the way to more accurate height retrieval. Additionally, testing SWOT
data on more complex RSQ models with well informed priors is still desired.
Despite the ongoing study, it is clear that the level of combined coverage, resolution and
accuracy we get with SWOT is unmatched by any current method for remote sensing of
hydrology. With constellations of SWOT­like satellites to improve observational frequency,
important events in the hydrological cycle such as flooding and low­flow river conditions

SWOT constellations are likely to increase understanding of freshwater dynamics and

A
SWOT exploration: Bonus material
A.1
Roll error
The roll error, a known issue for wide­swath altimetry, is corrected for in the PIXC product
using crossover calibration. Since the crossover correction is based on the nearest ocean
crossing, land hydrology products likely still contain effects of the roll error. The effect of
the roll error can be seen in Figure A.1.1 for the Congo River, where the PIXC product
height error can be 1­2 meters at the far edges of the swath. Also in Figure A.1.1 is the
height error for sections of the Fraser near gauges 08MC018 and 08KB001, from PIXC
tiles in pass 524 and 373 respectively (see Figure 3.2.2). Near 08MC018, the river runs
close to NADIR with little change in cross­track distance, resulting in less roll error. Near
08KB001, the river runs from near NADIR to over 40 km in cross­track distance. The roll
error is still present and not significantly different than that shown for the Congo, despite
the rivers closer proximity to the ocean.

direction and location with respect to the shown data.
