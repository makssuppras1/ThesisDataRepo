Abstract
Because buildings are designed to last 50+ years and building services designed to last 30+
years it is sensible to take climate change into account in the building design phase. Today,
building performance simulations can be used to predict the impact of climate change on new
and existing buildings with hourly future weather files. The goal of this thesis was to study
the impacts of climate change on danish residential buildings with a focus on smart ventilation.
With knowledge from the literature study a method was found and further developed for creating
suitable future weather files for Denmark. Five sets of future weather files were created to be
used for building performance simulations. A typical new single-family house and apartment
were considered with both with hybrid and balanced mechanical ventilation. Results indicate
that utilized heat recovery energy is reduced by 6 % to 18 % depending on RCP scenario and
building typology while heating coil demand is reduced by 50 % to 98 %.

Introduction
Climate change is one of the biggest challenges modern society is facing. The IPCC concluded
that around 19% of the worldwide Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions come from the building
sector [1]. Well-insulated buildings are sensitive to the hotter future climate as cooling demand
increases significantly while heating demand is only somewhat reduced. It could have conse-
quences for designing nearly zero energy buildings because they are often highly optimized.
Since buildings are designed to last 50+ years and building services designed to last 30+ years
it is sensible to take climate change into account when optimizing the design. As every build-
ing is designed differently, building performance simulations has become an essential tool for
engineers to account for the many variables and make informed decisions on building design.
Due to the increasingly strict building code in Denmark new buildings and renovated buildings
have to be relatively air tight and well-insulated [2]. This could lead to buildings overheating in
the future if the cooling demand has been underestimated. Increased air tightness also intensi-
fies the need for proper ventilation to ensure a healthy indoor environment. Therefore, different
kinds of ventilation systems are commonly found in newer buildings. Recently, a concept of
smart ventilation has been established. Smart ventilation refers to such ventilation systems
that continually adjusts in time to provide the desired indoor air quality benefits while minimizing
energy consumption, utility bills, and other costs that are not related to indoor air quality such
as thermal discomfort of noise [3]. Smart ventilation systems provide information to end users
and they can be integrated into smart grid setups. However, design, functionality and perfor-
mance of these systems is obviously influenced by the weather. Outdoor air temperature and
relative humidity can directly affect the performance of a heat exchanger while indoor heat- and
pollution loads can determine how the system reacts.
In order to study the impact of the changing climate on building and ventilation performance,
hourly future weather data is required. Future weather data is generally prepared and down-
scaled from GCMs in which monthly weather data is the most commonly available. However,
as computational power increases higher frequency weather data is becoming more readily
available.

Background
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) is the United Nations body for assess-
ing the science related to climate change and provides an internationally accepted authority on
climate change [4]. They produce assessment reports, most recently the Fifth Assessment Re-
port (AR5) which was written by 831 experts on climatology, meteorology, oceanology etc. The
AR5 provides an update of knowledge on the scientific, technical and socio-economic aspects
of climate change. AR5 relies on the fifth Coupled Model Intercomparison Project (CMIP), an
international effort among the climate modeling community to coordinate climate change exper-
iments [1].
CMIP is a collaborative framework designed to improve knowledge of climate change, being
the analog of Atmospheric Model Intercomparison Project (AMIP) for global coupled ocean-
atmosphere General Circulation Models (GCMs) [5]. The World Climate Research Programme
(WCRP) releases the CMIP which provides the basis for the assessment reports by the IPCC.
WCRP facilitates analysis and prediction of Earth system change for use in a range of practical
applications of direct relevance, benefit and value to society. WCRP aims to determine the
predictability of climate and the effect of human activities on climate [6].
Many meteorological institutes and research centers contribute data to the CMIP by simulating
GCMs. The terms General Circulation Model and Global Climate Model are synonymous [1]
and both abbreviated GCM. For this thesis the original term General Circulation Model (GCM)
will be used. GCMs are simulation models representing physical processes in the atmosphere,
ocean, cryosphere and land surface. GCMs are the most advanced tools currently available
for simulating the response of the global climate system to increasing Greenhouse Gas (GHG)
concentrations.
The most relevant meteorological institutes for this thesis are Danish Meteorological Institute
(DMI) and Swedish Meteorological and Hydrological Institute (SMHI) and both have run multiple
Regional Climate Models (RCMs). DMI is the official Danish meteorological institute adminis-
trated by the Ministry of Energy, Utilities and Climate. DMI provides meteorological knowledge
(weather forecasts) and data for Denmark, Faroe Islands and Greenland including surrounding
waters and airspaces [7]. SMHI is an expert agency under the Swedish Ministry of the Envi-
ronment. Through unique expertise in meteorology, hydrology, oceanography and climatology,
SMHI contributes towards greater public welfare, increased safety and a sustainable society
[8].
All of the climate model outputs is provided by Earth System Grid Federation (ESGF) who
manages a decentralized database consisting of independently hosted nodes. The ESGF is a
collaboration that develops, deploys and maintains software infrastructure for the management,
dissemination, and analysis of model output and observational data. ESGF’s primary goal is to
facilitate advancements in Earth System Science [9]. The nodes then provides access to the
output of observations and climate models from projects like the CMIP and the Coordinated
Regional Climate Downscaling Experiment (CORDEX).

CORDEX is a WCRP framework to evaluate RCM performance through a set of experiments
aiming at producing regional climate projections [10]. A RCM is a numerical climate predic-
tion model forced by specified lateral and ocean conditions from a GCM or observation-based
dataset (reanalysis) that simulates atmospheric and land surface processes, while accounting
for high-resolution topographical data, land-sea contrasts, surface characteristics, and other
components of the Earth-system. CORDEX has produced many RCMs for each of the different
CORDEX domains as seen on fig. 2.1.
Figure 2.1: CORDEX domains illustrated [11].
All climate models have to make assumptions or predictions (scenarios) about future emissions
to drive them. Each CMIP has its own way to implement these assumptions. Special Report
on Emissions Scenarios (SRES) is a report by the IPCC that was published in 2000. The GHG
emissions scenarios described in the report have been used to make projections of possible
future climate change. The SRES scenarios have been used for CMIP3. CMIP5 (note there is
no CMIP4) uses Representative Concentration Pathway (RCP) scenarios as its GHG concen-
tration trajectory.
As illustrated in fig. 2.2 some of the RCP scenarios are comparable to the SRES scenarios.
RCP 8.5 is broadly comparable to SRES A2 and RCP4.5 to SRES B1. The Fifth Assessment
Report (AR5) also mentions RCP 6.0 is broadly comparable to SRES B2 and RCP 2.6 have
no equivalent scenario in SRES [1]. From all of the four RCP scenarios the largest number
of GCMs has been run for RCP8.5 while comparatively few GCMs run the RCP 6.0 scenario.
Most impact studies stick to using RCP 4.5, RCP 8.5 or both.

Building Performance Simulation (BPS) has come a long way since the first steady state simu-
lations in the 1950-1960s. Current BPS programs are mostly used by architects and engineers
for various design tasks. To be able to simulate the energy use and indoor climate of buildings
BPS programs simulate many subsystems ranging from demand controlled Heating Ventilation
and Air Conditioning (HVAC) to boilers and user behaviour. BPS usually requires one year of
weather data with a frequency of one hour or greater which represents the local climate condi-
tions. At the time of writing, to get a building permit in Denmark, the expected energy demand
has to be documented by making a calculation in a program called Be18. The building energy
calculation program (Be18) (not a dynamical simulation program) is used for calculating the
energy frame of a building [15]. Whenever a new major revision of the danish building regula-
tions is released a new version of the calculation program is also released. The indoor climate
is documented with a dynamical building simulation program, for example Building Simulation

(BSim) which makes calculations based on hourly data.
The dynamical simulation is only valid when using a Design Reference Year (DRY) weather
file called “Denmark 2013” [16] [17] (DRY2013) which is based on observed data in the period
2001-2010. The Design Reference Year is a yearly dataset that consists of 12 typical months
and should be used as a reference for building and solar energy simulations [18] [19]. The
current danish Design Reference Year is based on meteorological data from the period 2001-
2010 provided by DMI. Many more weather files exists and a selection of them is described in
section 4.1.
Among current state-of-the-art BPS programs is a program called IDA Indoor Climate and En-
ergy (IDA ICE) [20]. IDA ICE is capable of making whole-year, multi-zone, and dynamic studies
of building indoor climate and energy consumption of the entire building [20]. One of the main
advantages to using IDA ICE is that every underlying equation can be browsed, and every vari-
able can be logged. For a single zone, this means that temperatures, heat fluxes, CO2-levels,
control signals and other variables can be inspected by the user. With the advanced level fea-
ture the user can even edit the automatically created schematic of a simulation model granting
the user complete control over the simulation model.

Scope and Problem Statement
Figure 3.1 displays the scope of this thesis.
There are three larger aspects of the thesis:
smart ventilation, weather data, and Building Performance Simulation. Smart ventilation will
include describing, setting up, and investigating a realistic ventilation strategy. Current and fu-
ture weather data will represent climate change. The danish Design Reference Year will be
compared to historic output select climate models. Methods for obtaining future weather data
will be described. Building Performance Simulations will be done in IDA ICE to explore in-
door climate conditions, energy use, heat exchanger performance and more. All of the aspects
should lie within the context of danish residential buildings. Combining all of the aspects re-
veals the broader picture: making simulations with future weather data to see how it affects
smart ventilation systems in danish residential buildings.

Based on the literature study and scope, the following problem statement is considered:
• What will the potential future consequences be for existing buildings designed for current
weather conditions?
• In which ways is climate change going to affect smart residential ventilation systems?
• How does a smart ventilation system compare to a simple ventilation system in the future?
In order to answer the problem statement several objectives need to be fulfilled. Weather files
representing the future weather in Denmark will have to be created. Methods for creating future
weather files for BPS will have to be studied and knowledge about climate models has to be
acquired in order to create suitable future weather files for Denmark. The new future weather
files should be be able to be used in BPS for studying the effects on a smart ventilation system.
To represent a residential building in Denmark a simulation model (or multiple) has to be made
for a typical or existing building. Cases should be run with smart and simple ventilation systems.
Occupant behaviour such as equipment usage patterns, occupant activity, and window opening
also needs to be implemented into the simulation model.

4
Literature Review
The purpose of this literature review is mainly to acquire the most recent knowledge within the
scope of this project to aid in finding the most suitable methods for performing the impact study.
Section 4.1 is an introduction to current weather files for context. Next is a small section about
climate models and impact studies. Section 4.3 covers the most used methods for making future
weather files and section 4.4 covers their use in BPS.
4.1
Weather Files Representing Present Climate
Weather files representing a year of present weather are based on data from a single or multiple
weather stations (observed historic data). A representative year is created from at least 10
years of data while most use a longer 20 or 30 year period. Different weather files use different
methods to select a representative year such as the Typical Meteorological Year (TMY) using
the Finkelstein–Schafer statistic [21]. Typical variables included in weather files are outside dry
bulb temperature, dew point temperature, relative humidity, atmospheric pressure, direct and
diffuse solar radiation, wind speed, wind direction, sky cover, and precipitation. The frequency
of the data is normally hourly.
Many different types of weather files are currently in use. Not only are there many different
types of weather files but each type has files for multiple locations and timeframes. Herrera
et al. [22] provided an overview of some of the most common weather file types. The list below
is a selection of common non-extreme weather file types.
• Actual Meteorological Year (AMY). A year of observed meteorological data compiled into
a weather file. This is the simplest type of weather file in terms of data processing.
• Chartered Institution of Building Services Engineers (CIBSE). These weather files are
widely used for compliance verification of building performance in the UK context.
• Canadian Weather for Energy Calculations (CWEC). 80 files available for Canada used
for documenting compliance with The National Energy Code of Canada.
• Design Reference Year (DRY). A modification of the TRY method is used. Used in Den-
mark in energy frame calculation for compliance with the building regulations [2]
• International Weather for Energy Calculation (IWEC). Created by ASHRAE in 2001 [23].
The newest version IWEC2 has 3012 locations outside USA and Canada. TMY3 files are
available within USA and Canada
• Typical Meteorological Year (TMY). Common in USA but also used by other countries.
Newer versions TMY2 and TMY3 are available.
• Test Reference Year (TRY). From a baseline period, years with monthly extreme values
are filtered out until only the most average single year remained.
For a much more comprehensive list one can refer to the EnergyPlus Weather Data Sources
[24].

4.2
Climate Models
The sea of different climate models is vast. As of writing it is possible to retrieve data from
165 different climate models. Therefore the IPCC has set up four criteria to use when selecting
GCMs for impact studies [25].
• Vintage: Generally more recent climate model simulations are likely to be more reliable
than older simulation models. This is because the recent models are based on more
recent knowledge, incorporate more processes and feedbacks, and usually have higher
resolution compared to earlier models.
• Resolution: As climate models have evolved and computing power increased, the spa-
tial resolution has increased from around 1000 km to 250 km. Although higher resolution
models contain more spatial detail it does not necessarily yield superior model perfor-
mance.
• Validity: Comparing GCM simulations with observed climate and selecting GCMs that
reliably simulate the present-day climate would also yield the most reliable representation
of the future climate.
• Representativeness: As it is strongly recommended to choose more than one GCM for
impact assessments one should choose models that show a range of changes in a key
variable in the study region.
Comparing climate models and identifying their biases and errors is important. A biased GCM
will also bias any results derived from using that model. A study by Fernández et al. [26] pre-
sented an unprecedented ensemble of 196 future climate projections including models from
CMIP5 and EURO-CORDEX. The target region was Spain and the Balearic Islands where the
2021-2050 near future temperature and precipitation was compared as shown on fig. 4.1.

There are four major levels of uncertainty when using numerically simulated RCMs [26] [27]:
1. Using different initial conditions creates variability within each GCM
2. All GCMs are different and have different biases in different regions
3. All RCMs are different and have different biases in different regions. Higher resolution
models include many small-scale features producing changes in weather patterns most
notable near mountains and islands
4. Forcing scenarios model the uncertainty of equivalent CO2 concentrations
All of the listed uncertainties can be seen on the lower part of fig. 4.1. At each tier GCM,

Casanueva et al. [28] investigated RCMs using the HadGEM2 GCM and three of their conclu-
sions have been highlighted here:
1. HadGEM-driven RCM simulations are at the upper limit of uncertainty range when com-
pared to the large ensemble of GCM-RCM simulations (agrees with [26]).
2. It is strongly recommended to use multiple GCMs as a single GCM for production of climate
projection does not sample the full uncertainty range (same recommendation as the IPCC
[25]).
3. There are no high-resolution observational grid for Europe thus it is difficult to bias correct
and evaluate RCMs with higher resolution than 50 x 50 km. High resolution observed
temperature is available on a national level, but relative humidity is lacking.
4.3
Weather Files Representing Future Climate
Weather files created to represent future weather conditions are usually based on output from
a GCM or RCM. The spatial resolution of GCMs is typically 100-350 km while ocean grids are
finer [29]. RCMs from CORDEX have spatial resolutions varying from approximately 44x44 km
to 11x11 km. All climate models have historical outputs to make it possible to compare the out-
put from a climate model to observed data from a weather station. Not all RCP scenarios are
used for any particular climate model. Most climate models opt to use RCP 8.5 and RCP 4.5,
but outputs for RCP 2.6 and especially RCP 6.0 is harder to come by. Near surface air temper-
ature (tas) and precipitation (pr) are among the most common variables and can sometimes be
downloaded at frequencies of 1-hr or 3-hr while other variables might be downloaded at a lower
temporal resolution.

4.3.1
Statistical Downscaling
Statistical downscaling involves setting up a statistical relationship between variables from GCMs
and historical observed weather from a local weather station. Future climate conditions pre-
dicted by the GCM is then used to drive the statistical relationship to estimate new high res-
olution local climate characteristics. Statistical downscaling techniques make the assumption
that the relationship between large scale climate variables and the actual measured variables
at one particular weather station will always be the same.
Morphing
Morphing was developed by Belcher, Hacker, and Powell [30] and it is one of the most popular
downscaling methods for BPS impact studies. Overall, the morphing procedure is to adjust the
present day weather data by the changes found in GCMs or RCMs. For example a monthly
mean temperature change from 1990s to 2050s from a GCM is added to a Test Reference Year
(TRY) climate file. The morphing procedure has three generic operations: a shift eq. (4.1), a
stretch eq. (4.2), and a combination of the two eq. (4.3). A shift is used when there is a change
to the mean. A stretch is used when there is a change to either the mean or the variance. A
combination of shift and stretch is used when both the mean and variance is changed.

x = x0 + ∆xm + αm · (x0 −x0,m)
(4.3)
Where x0 is the present day variable, ∆xm is the absolute change in the monthly mean value
of the variable, m is the month, αm is the fractional change in the monthly mean, and x0,m is
the monthly mean of the present day variable. For calculating the dry bulb temperature a shift
and stretch is used as shown in eq. (4.4).

The Climate Change Weather File Generator tools CCWeatherGen and CCWorldWeatherGen
were created by the University of Southhampton [35] [36]. CCWeatherGen and CCWorldWeath-

based and uses the morphing method to generate EPW or TMY2 weather files for the periods
2011-2040, 2041-2070 or 2071-2100. All of the data processing is done behind the scenes in
a Visual Basic script.
The user interface is simple (see fig. 4.3) and comes with a user guide and technical documen-

4.3.2
Dynamical Downscaling
Dynamical downscaling uses the output from a GCM to drive a RCM to simulate regional climate
at a higher spatial resolution (see fig. 4.5). Specifically the output from a GCM provides an initial
condition for the RCM as well as providing time dependent boundary conditions at the edges of
the RCM [39].
In Europe the ongoing series of sponsored downcaling projects include: MERCURE, PRU-
DENCE, ENSEMBLES, PRINCIPLES, and EUCP [40]. EURO-CORDEX is among the most
recent coordinated downscaling initiatives and it has produced a database with a large number
of RCM outputs. Like GCMs, CORDEX outputs have been created by meteorological insti-
tutes, research groups, etc. Some examples of RCMs from CORDEX are RCA4, ALADIN63,
and REMO2015. There are many different RCMs and each model run can use different driving
models (GCMs) as an input to the RCM. For example in the EUR-11 domain the RCA4 RCM
has been run with the GCMs: CNRM-CM5, EC-EARTH, IPSL-CM5A, HadGEM2-ES, MPI-ESM
and more.

• Creating future weather files using outputs from multiple high resolution hourly RCM in-

5.1.2
Finding Climate Models
Following the four criteria for selecting a climate model (see section 4.2) the climate models will
be investigated starting with the newest first. At the time of writing the newest climate models
with output available for GCMs are from the CMIP6 experiment. The CMIP5 experiment have
output available for GCMs and RCMs via the CORDEX initiative but the data is older.
According to the IPCC “Guidelines for Use of Climate Scenarios Developed from Regional Cli-
mate Model Experiments” [39] regional, small scale nations obviously need high resolution in-
formation. Mentioned examples in the guideline include Switzerland, Belgium and Jamaica.
The reasoning behind this is that small nations only occupy a few GCM grid boxes or are not
represented at the scale of a GCM. Although the guideline [39] was issued in 2003, Denmark is
still a pretty small country compared to the spatial resolution of current GCMs. In fact a single
grid point from a GCM usually covers an area larger than Zealand. An average resolution GCM
typically have six grid points to represent Denmark, each of which also covers large areas of
sea water due to the complex geometry of Denmark. Based on the IPCC guideline [39], GCMs
are deemed to be too coarse to represent the weather in Denmark. RCMs will be used, which
imply using data from CMIP5 based CORDEX climate models.

5.1.3
Creating Weather ﬁles for Simulation
There are plenty of ways to create a weather file and some of them are described in section 4.1.
Most traditional methods aim to create a weather file that represents a 30 year period consistent
with the World Meteorological Organization (WMO). However by condensing 10-30 years into
a 1-year file there is a huge loss of information. Additionally when picking out months from
different years and combining them, the discontinuity between months will have to be dealt
with.
The approach for this thesis is to make a weather files that contain 15 years of weather data.
Like an AMY weather file little to no processing is involved going from climate model outputs to
the weather file. The dry bulb temperature, relative humidity, and wind speed come directly from
the climate model output while global horizontal irradiance is converted into direct and diffuse
irradiation. Using 15 years is a compromise between handling the shear amount of data versus
using a 30 year period to be representative. One of the downsides to making 15 year BPS
in IDA ICE is that each simulation will take much longer to run. One upside is that no data is
discarded which would otherwise be a source of uncertainties. Another upside is that a minimal
amount of processing is required to make the weather file. The 15 year weather files can also
be used to create a DRY weather file although it would be better to use 30 years.
Climate models have different ways of interpreting a calendar year. Table 5.1 displays the
meta data regarding calendar years extracted from each RCM output file. A standard calendar
year is the one known from our daily lives with 365 day years and 366 day leap years. The
proleptic Gregorian calendar is adopted by ISO 8601-1:2019 [88] and for this study there is no
difference between the standard and proleptic Gregorian calendar. An earth system model for
CMIP5 developed by Institut Pierre Simon Laplace climate modeling centre (IPSL-CM5A)-MR-
r1 uses a 365 day year which implies that there are no leap years. An earth system model
called Hadley Centre Global Environment Model version 2 funded by the UK Met Office Hadley
Centre (HadGEM2-ES)-r1 uses a 360 day year also without leap years.

irradiance and diffuse horizontal irradiance is shown against the right y-axis. Each data point
is marked and for the global horizontal irradiance it represents the mean solar irradiation for
the three hours centered around that point. For all five of the selected RCMs, global horizontal
irradiance is shifted by 01:30 as it is the mean global horizontal irradiance between two points.
This shift is included in the weather file by inserting points that have been interpolated between
its two neighbouring points which would otherwise be a missing value. For example the dry bulb
temperature at 01:30 is the mean value of the dry bulb temperature at 00:00 and 03:00. Similarly
the global horizontal irradiance at 09:00 is the mean value of the global horizontal irradiance
at 07:30 and 10:30. The diffuse horizontal irradiance and direct normal irradiance is calculated
every 1 h 30 min using both the actual points at 01:30, 04:30, 07:30, ... and the interpolated
points at 03:00, 06:00, 09:00,... of the global horizontal irradiance. This is reflected on fig. 5.5
where the diffuse horizontal irradiance is notably higher at 06:00, 12:00 and 15:00 which would
otherwise have been a straight line connecting the marks.
As seen in table 5.2 the last two rows of data are filled in with the values from above for the
simulation to stop at 00:00 next year instead of 21:00. One could use the first data point of the
next year, but that would require downloading data for an entire year of where only the first point
would be used. Because the solar radiation rsds and consequently Ib and Id is shifted by 1 h

5.2
Simulation Models
For this study to be as relevant as possible, the simulations models are based on buildings from
the Typology Approach for Building Stock Energy Assessment (TABULA) project. The idea
behind the TABULA project was to make an agreed systematic approach to classify building
stocks to aid in making the energy refurbishment processes in the European housing sector
transparent and effective. The TABULA project has produced a catalogue of residential building
typologies for 13 European countries [91]. Each national typology has been classified according
to their size and age. From the catalogue of existing buildings a set of typical buildings are
described in detail to represent the average building types. From this set, two of the newest
example buildings have been chosen: a single-family house and an apartment block. Both
typologies are from the “2011...” category and are presented in section 5.2.1. A terraced house
(row house) could also have been modelled with data from TABULA, but was left out due to
time constraints. Building typologies other than danish residential buildings are not within the
scope of this thesis.
For both the single-family house and the apartment, two different ventilation strategies will be
investigated: hybrid ventilation (mechanical exhaust and natural supply) and balanced mechan-
ical ventilation. This creates four cases: 1) single-family house with hybrid ventilation, 2) single-
family house with balanced mechanical ventilation, 3) apartment with hybrid ventilation, and 4)
apartment with balanced mechanical ventilation. With 25 weather files containing 15 years
each and the four simulation cases, 1500 years worth of simulations will have to be run. Adding
anything that requires making more simulations will exponentially increase the time required to
run all of the simulations. Therefore a comprehensive optimization study investigating multiple
parameter variations will not be carried out due to time restrictions.

5.2.1
Building Geometry and Envelope
The reference floor area, surface areas, and a picture of the example buildings is the only infor-
mation about the floor plan and geometry of the buildings. However, a floor plan is needed for
BPS so those had to be found somewhere else. The example building pictures were manually
compared with images found with various search engines. When a close or exact matching
image was found, a floor plan could then be found for the corresponding example building from
TABULA.
Information about the building envelopes and sizes have been acquired through the TABULA
WebTool [91].
Specifically U-values and the building constructions have been modeled to
resemble the example buildings as close as possible. TABULA does not provide any infor-
mation about the thermal conductivity λ of the constructions. Therefore the material thermal
conductivities have been calculated so the final U-value of every construction fits with the U-
value from TABULA. The thermal conductivity of brick walls was found by calculating back-
wards from the U-value given in the danish technical report for TABULA [92]. According to
the report 0.12 m of brick has a U-value of 3.20 W/m²K which means that the thermal con-
ductivity λ of the bricks must be around 0.842 W/mK when including film coefficients. The
density of the brick wall was estimated afterwards by using figure F.1 from DS 418 [93] to be
λ = 0.842 W/mK ⇒ρ = 2020 kg/m3. The danish TABULA report [92] also illustrates a wall
construction with 0.12 m brick and 0.19 m insulation with a U-value of 0.2 W/m²K. Another
backwards calculation can be done which reveals that insulation has λ = 0.040 W/mK. This
backwards calculation procedure was not possible for floor and roof constructions and thus an
educated guess of the thermal conductivity was used. Bear in mind that the U-values were still
modeled to resemble the example buildings as close as possible.
The convective heat transfer coefficients are found in DS 418 [93]. However, IDA ICE uses
actual convective heat transfer coefficients so the U-value will vary slightly.
TABULA [91] does not provide any linear loss coefficients so if it was to be implemented it
would be an estimate or a value found in a standard. It is not known whether linear losses are
already part of the U-value given in TABULA. The results in this thesis will primarily be presented
as changes compared to a baseline where linear losses will not significantly alter the results.
Therefore linear losses are omitted from the simulation models.

Minimum air flows have been balanced by area so each zone with supply air has the same air

Airflow in IDA ICE are controlled by supply and return setpints for each zone. The total supply
and return airflow rate is the sum of airflow rates for each zone. A centralized AHU does the
opposite: the total supply and return airflow rate is calculated at the AHU which then distributes
the air to each zone. Because each zone controls the airflow rate independently a case where
one zone supplies air and another returns air with two different control signals will cause the
total airflow to be unbalanced.

Heating System
All zones use the default ideal heater in IDA ICE with a temperature setpoint for heating of 21 °C.
An ideal heater is a theoretical unit with no physical location on any room surface that delivers
heat in the zone when needed. Each of the ideal heaters have a limited capacity of 100 W/m².
Other heaters such as radiators or floor heating can be used in an IDA ICE model, but the ideal
heater was chosen because optimization of heating was not a goal for this thesis.

5.2.9
Window Opening
Occupant behaviour can have a massive impact on the energy use of a building. Andersen
[108] measured the energy consumption of 290 identical danish dwellings and found that it
varied with a factor of 20. This is why defining realistic occupant behaviour is important for BPS.
Occupant behaviour is considered to be stochastic and stochastic models for window opening
and occupancy have been developed [109] [110]. Running simulation models with stochastic
occupant behaviour requires the model to be run multiple times to get a range of results to create
a probability distribution of performance indicators. Because the models already have to be run
25 times to cover the uncertainties from the climate models it would be too time consuming
to also include a stochastic window opening model. Instead window opening is controlled by
the default PI temperature controller in IDA ICE. More advanced window opening controls using
multiple variables such as CO2 concentration, precipitation, indoor and outdoor air temperature,
wind, and time of day were considered but not implemented due to a lack of time.
To determine a cooling setpoint temperature for window opening the four IDA ICE models were
run for cooling setpoint integers between 23 °C and 27 °C with the DRY2013 weather file. The
cooling setpoint should be as high as possible to save energy on heating while not exceeding
100 hours of indoor air temperature above 27 °C or 25 hours above 28 °C as required in SBI-
Anvisning 213 [111]. The chosen cooling setpoint is 25 °C. At setpoint temperatures higher than
25 °C the warmest zone exceeded 25 hours above 28 °C.
All windows were assumed to be side-hinged and according to SBI-Anvisning 213 [111] have an
effective opening area of 60 %. Many of the windows in the apartment have transoms where the
top section of the window is side hung and the bottom section is fixed. Similarly a few windows
in the single-family house are also fixed. This is accounted for in the effective opening area of
every window.

Discussion
7.1
Literature Review and Method
During the literature review one task involved figuring out the exact climate models used for
each article. Each output from a RCM can only be identified when all of the following is known:
variable, domain, GCM model name, forcing scenario, ensemble member name, RCM model
name, version id, frequency and time period. Fortunately the filename of a RCM output file con-
tains all said information. However none of the read articles, conference papers, etc. included
file names or any other complete set of information to be able to identify climate models quickly.
Knowing the exact climate models used is crucial for the ability to reproduce and compare re-
sults of studies. In the interest of making this thesis as transparent as possible a list of climate
model filenames used is provided in appendix A. It is proposed that any published scientific work
working with data distributed from an ESGF node adopt listing filenames as it would drastically
increase the transparency.
When developing the method, one of the biggest challenges was figuring out how climate model
outputs should be processed to create future weather files. From the very beginning of this
project it was the notion that the future weather file would ideally be created as a DRY file. DRY
weather files are widely used in Denmark because it has to be used to document compliance
with the Danish Building Regulation 2018 [2]. However creating a DRY weather file requires
a panel of meteorologists preferably from DMI, DTU, and SBi making it virtually impossible to
create a true DRY weather file without funding. A DRY-like weather file where the elimination
process of irregular months is omitted could be made by following the original method [19] for
creating a DRY weather file. At this point the main author behind the DRY2013 weather file
Kristian Pagh Nielsen was contacted. This lead to an interesting discussion about weather
files. The WMO has established that 30 years should be used as the climate normal. However
due to climate change many believe that a 30 year reference period will not be representative
of the next 10-15 years for which the dataset will be used. Only using 10 years will lead to a
statistical weakness, but 15 years is a good compromise. American TMY has the weakness of
only covering 12 typical months. However, months with atypical weather do occur in the real
world. Therefore one of the best causes of action for making test simulations is to use all of the
data 15 years x 12 months instead of using only the most typical months. As DMI is making raw
observational data freely available and as computing power increases it is sensible to think that
a paradigm shift towards multi-year simulations will happen in the near future.
The increasing outdoor air CO2 level was not implemented in the weather files as it would make
no difference to the results. If the ventilation system was using a CO2 sensor to control the
air flow rate then the outdoor CO2 level should be included when making simulations with fu-
ture weather files. CO2 concentrations are not available from the five selected RCMs but some
GCMs provide CO2 concentrations as a monthly output. Figure 7.1 shows the CO2 level from
the GCM MPI-ESM-LR for an experiment called ESM RCP 8.5. The ESM RCP 8.5 experiment
is identical to RCP 8.5 except for the atmospheric CO2 level which is simulated instead of pre-

According to the Danish Building Regulation 2018 [2] the indoor CO2 level should not exceed

if the AHU does not measure the outdoor CO2 level the setpoint will have to be adjusted as the
outdoor CO2 level increases.
Another variable that was not implemented in the weather files was air pressure. BPS usually
assume the air pressure to be constant due to the fact that it has little to no effect on the simu-
lation model. However, should a future study require air pressure then it is a readily available
output from climate models including 3-hourly output from the five RCMs used in this thesis.
The method of creating future weather files directly from climate models is a simple and yet
powerful tool for impact studies. The climate model output data downloaded for this thesis can
be used for the entire EURO-CORDEX region. In fact a student of Graduate school of Civil,
Environmental and Urban Engineering (ENTPE), University of Lyon, currently a guest student
at DTU is conducting a similar study for France based on the same method. Some data is
also available for Africa and South America at a more coarse 0.44° x 0.44° spatial resolution
compared to the 0.11° x 0.11° resolution available for Europe. The limited availability of high
resolution RCM outputs is currently a disadvantage to the method. Meanwhile it is expected
that new RCM outputs will increase the number of high resolution outputs for all domains.
The next phase of CORDEX involving downscaling CMIP6 is currently in progress. One of
the climate scientists from the SMHI Rossby Centre Grigory Nikulin confirmed that all of the
current 3-hourly variables will be moved to hourly variables. Moreover some of the CMIP6
climate models provide diffuse horizontal irradiance as an output. This will simplify the method of
creating weather files directly from climate model output considerably. By following the method
proposed in this thesis future weather files could be made available to a much larger audience

7.2
Validation of Climate Models
The climate models are generally in very good agreement with the observed weather as seen
on fig. 6.1. Medians and quartiles from the climate models lie within 1-2 °C of the observed
weather. No climate model is perfect and small differences should be expected. However, the
differences between climate models and observed weather could be smaller if a 30 year dataset
was used instead of the 15 year datasets used in this thesis.
As mentioned in section 4.2 the most reliable climate model is likely the one that reliably simu-
lates the observed climate. The HadGEM2-ES-r1 climate model seems to be the closest match
on fig. 6.1. HadGEM2-ES-r1 also has the highest correlation coefficient, regardless all of the
correlation coefficients are very high. Although HadGEM2-ES-r1 might represent the dry bulb
temperature the best (see fig. D.1) this does not mean that the other climate models should be
disregarded. The other climate models also fit the observed data very well and they might have
a better fit for other variables such as global horizontal irradiance, near surface relative humidity,
or near surface wind speed. For example the global horizontal irradiance from CNRM-CM5-r1
might fit to the observed data better than the global horizontal irradiance from HadGEM2-ES-r1.
On fig. 6.1 the observed weather has the highest upper adjacent value which means that the
hottest month is warmer for the observed weather compared to the climate models. One part of
the explanation for this disparity might be that the climate model data is from one location while
observed data is the areally weighted average for Denmark as a whole. Jutland is weighted by a
factor of 7/10 while the rest of Denmark is weighted by 3/10. Although Denmark generally has a
coastal climate the highest temperatures in Denmark is generally observed in the central parts
of Jutland. As seen on fig. 5.2 the output from the climate models, Sjælsmark is near to the
Eastern coast of Zealand where high temperatures might be lower compared to temperatures
averaged for Denmark as a whole. To test this, the temperature from the HadGEM2-ES-r1 has
been compared for Sjælsmark (used for weather files) and Herning to the observed temperature
on fig. 7.2. The upper and lower adjacent value for Herning matches the observed temperature
better than Sjælsmark.
The discussion of making multiple year simulations instead of only using a selection of typical
months continues. As seen on fig. 7.2 the DRY2013 weather file does not cover the same
extremes as the observed weather or climate models. While DRY2013 covers hours where
the outdoor dry bulb temperature is extremely cold or hot as seen on fig. 6.2 it does not cover
atypical months where the temperature is consistently low or high compared with a 15 year
period. The lower quartile of DRY2013 shows that the number of cold months might be too
high compared to the 15 years of observed weather. The upper quartile roughly matches the
observed weather but the warmest month in DRY2013 is on average around 2 °C cooler than
the warmest observed month. In other words 15 years of weather data covers a broader range
of results compared to the DRY2013 weather file. This backs up the statement by Kristian Pagh
Nielsen as to why it is statistically better to make simulations with multiple AMY weather files
compared to using the DRY2013 weather file.

2. All GCMs are different and have different biases in different regions
• Five different GCMs have been used to cover potential biases from GCMs. However

3. All RCMs are different and have different biases in different regions

might be biased.

• RCP scenarios 4.5 and 8.5 are covered while RCP 2.6 and 6.0 are not.
As mentioned in chapter 2 climate models can have their own variability which can be studied
by running the same climate model with different initial states. The IPSL-CM5A-LR model was
investigated for initial state uncertainties and regardless of location different initial perturbations
triggers atmospheric error growth within the first day [117]. Therefore the results from using the
IPSL-CM5A-MR climate model will most likely be different for other initial states. It is unknown
how much using different initial states will affect the results as this effect is rarely investigated
for impact studies.
There were two main reasons why RCP 2.6 was not investigated. The RCMs CNRM-CM5-
r1 and IPSL-CM5A-MR-r1 are not available for RCP 2.6 which would make comparisons less
fair. Making weather files and running BPS simulations for RCP 2.6 would have been too time
consuming. As for RCP 6.0 it would also be too time consuming and this scenario lies in between
RCP 4.5 and RCP 8.5 which covers the global surface warming from RCP 6.0 as seen on fig. 2.2.

7.5
Simulation Results
To verify that the custom control for the ventilation system (fig. 5.24 and fig. 5.26) works the
mass balance is checked on fig. 6.7. For all of the simulations the supply and return mass
flow rate are perfectly synchronized. The small difference of 0.15 L/s between the supply and
return flow rate is equal to the flow through the leak in the AHU. This leak is automatically added
by IDA ICE to any AHU to avoid potential numerical problems when running the simulations.
When examining the return relative humidity the ventilation system acts as expected. At 23:00
the occupants will use the bathroom which produces a lot of moisture. At around 00:45 the
return relative humidity is sufficiently low for the intelligent RH control to switch from high to
normal air flow rate. At around 16:40 the percent change in RH exceeds the average return
RH by 10.5 % which according to fig. 5.23 switches the airflow from normal to high.
Space heating varies a lot over the course of a day as seen on fig. 6.8. This is because out-
side air temperature, solar radiation, infiltration, occupancy, and ventilation all affect the room
temperature and the ideal heaters have a quick reaction time. The space heating demand is
roughly the inverse of the outdoor air temperature. There is also a small increase in space
heating when the ventilation flow rate is increased.
The AHU heating coil expectantly turned on whenever the air temperature after heat recovery
is lower than the setpoint. As explained at fig. 6.22 the AHU heating coil is generally in use
when the outdoor air temperature is below -5 °C. However as seen on fig. 6.8 the outdoor air
temperature is above 1 °C and instead the heating coil power on fig. 6.9 follows the mass flow
on fig. 6.7. This is because the heat recovery is less efficient at high air flow rates as seen on
fig. 5.20. As the air flows were always balanced the heat recovery efficiency was never below
0.85 unless it was bypassed due to high outdoor air temperatures.
On fig. 7.5 the space heating demand from the climate model weather files is compared to
DRY2013. In all of the four simulation models the space heating demand in January, February,
March, November, and December is larger for DRY2013 compared to the climate models. In
March the space heating demand for DRY2013 is very high compared to the climate models.
This is also noticeable on fig. 6.21 and fig. 6.22. It is caused by the low outdoor air temperature
in March in the DRY2013 weather file as seen on fig. 6.3. A brief investigation was carried out
where the DRY2013 weather file was compared to weather station data. The weather station
data matched the weather file. It was also found that March might be offset by -1 days compared
to a report from DMI [114]. According to the same report the climate normal mean temperature
for March is 2.1 °C which is far from the -0.73 °C in the DRY2013 weather file. Finding the exact
reason as to why, would require checking for mistakes in the process of selecting which months
the DRY2013 consists of. Therefore it remains unknown as to why March is the coldest month
in the DRY2013 weather file.

7.6
Final Thoughts
Climate change is happening slowly over time which will give engineers time to adapt their
designs. However, if engineers fail to account for climate change and only use the DRY2013
weather file as a worst case scenario then new or newly renovated buildings will not live up to
their requirements 20-30 years into the future.
Current residential smart ventilation systems will probably still live up to their requirements 20-
30 years from now. By then ventilation systems could begin to transition from having a heating
coil to installing AHUs without a heating source to reduce pressure losses. A cooling coil could
also be installed in place of a heating coil. No sudden drop in heat recovery performance due
to climate change could be found in the simulation data. Climate change will affect residential
ventilation systems, but gradually with time.

Conclusion
The mean indoor air temperature of the modelled residential buildings will increase for all future
scenarios. The mean of five RCMs show that the number of hours with an operative temperature
above 28 °C in the hottest room will decrease for the RCP 4.5 scenario. However if other climate
models were used then the opposite could also be true. The operative temperature increases for
the RCP 8.5 scenario. When uncertainties are taken into account, existing residential buildings
designed for current weather conditions are expected to have more overheating hours in the
future.
In a smart ventilation system with central relative humidity control the heating coil demand
kWh/(m² year) will be reduced by 50 % for a single-family house and by 61 % for an apartment
for the RCP 4.5 2043-2057 simulations compared to the baseline. For the warmest scenario
RCP 8.5 2073-2087 heating coil demand is reduced by 93 % to 98 % for a house and apartment
respectively. Heat recovery is utilized 6 % to 18 % less depending on the building and RCP
scenario. Energy use for ventilation fans decreases with future scenarios due to higher outdoor
relative humidity. Climate change will reduce the utilization of the heating coil and heat recovery
in a smart ventilation system.
A residential building with central relative humidity controlled balanced mechanical ventilation
(smart) has a consistently lower heating demand compared to a building with a simple ventilation
system regardless of RCP scenario and building typology.

Suggestions for Future Work
Impact studies similar to this thesis are performed all over the world at an increasing rate. In fact
there are so many that it is becoming hard to find the most relevant studies. For example the
newest studies in table 4.3 did not compare their results to the other studies even though they
were done for the same location. A large literature study with a table that is more comprehensive
than table 4.1 could help to provide an overview over the many studies.
To get a better insight to which of the RCM outputs is most realistic the validation of the weather
files could be more thorough. Other than temperature the solar irradiation, wind speed, relative
humidity could also be compared with observed weather.
The weather files created in this thesis could be enhanced by including more variables like
cloud cover, precipitation, and air pressure. These additional variables like precipitation could
be used for simulating advanced window opening control.
This thesis was limited to using data from EURO-CORDEX outputs. However as concluded by
Bartók et al. [118] surface solar radiation changes in RCMs and GCMs show opposite trends.
New weather files could be created with the morphing procedure [30] using the danish Design
Reference Year [18] and multiple GCMs. Then IDA ICE simulations could be run with the new
weather files and compared to simulation results from weather files created using the directly
from RCM method used in this thesis.
The new CMIP6 RCM outputs will according to Grigory Nikulin from SMHI have hourly data
available. Diffuse solar radiation data might also become available which could remove the
need for converting global horizontal irradiance into its diffuse and direct parts. Thus the weather
files created for this thesis become obsolete. New weather files based on the new hourly RCM
outputs will have to be created.
None of the simulation cases included a cooling coil. It would be interesting to investigate the
expected increase in cooling demand. The problem statement of such a study could include:
How much will the cooling demand change in the future and what are the causes? What is the
ratio of change in cooling demand to change in heating demand? This study could be conducted
for residential as well as commercial buildings. Natural cooling with a realistic stochastic window
opening model could also be compared to mechanical cooling. In the same study, it could also
be examined if occupants open the windows more often in the future due to the increased indoor
air temperatures.
