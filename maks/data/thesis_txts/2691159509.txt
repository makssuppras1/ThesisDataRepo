Abstract
With the development of more advanced processing capabilities and high yield power
supplies, autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs) are now being used for more and more
tasks with roles and missions constantly evolving.
Among the possible applications,
there is a military interest for underwater hull inspection.
This thesis focus on the
development of an autonomous underwater navigation algorithm, required to set up
these applications. It will propose a new localisation method based on the extended
Kalman ﬁlter and the interval ﬁlter, which allows us to get a higher level of integrity
with similar performances. In addition, the thesis will deal with the design of the closed
loop system and its veriﬁcation by simulation and experiments on a physical robot.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
More applications are now being set up for autonomous underwater vehicles.
Their
main purpose is to inspect underwater environments with various measurements. For
example, they can be used to inspect underwater power grids, to measure the water
quality or to update the nautical charts. This thesis will deal with a speciﬁc application:
the measurement of the electric and magnetic signatures of the naval ships.
These
measurements will help the navy to hide these signatures in order to make their ships
more stealthy. With an AUV, it becomes easier to carry out these operations in an
oﬀshore environment.
Since radio waves do not propagate well under water, these robots are design to
be autonomous and only communicate and use their GNSS system when they are on
surface. As a result, the navigation of the AUVs often relies on dead reckoning which
force the robot to periodically return to the surface.
However, in a mission where the AUV moves in a small area, it is possible to use
close range communication systems based on acoustic waves. With these systems it is
possible to localize the AUV with time diﬀerence of arrival.
The AUV used in this project is the model MKII μUUV from the Riptide company
(see Figure 1.1). In the following sections it will be referred as the Riptide. This torpedo
shaped drone has a rear propeller and three orientable ﬁns to change its orientation. Its
has a pressure-meter, and IMU and an echo-sounder in the nose. It also has a GPS in
the rear with an antenna in the top ﬁn. The drone is powered by a Li-Po battery. Its
computational hardware has been reworked by students from the ENSTA Bretagne. The
new drivers have been designed under ROS. This robot can also be remotely controlled
above 1m of depth.

Objectives
The goal of this master thesis is to make an AUV navigate under a ship with a robust
navigation algorithm. The main objectives are the following:
• To design nonlinear speed and orientation controllers for the Riptide
• To design a 3D line following planner
• To design an reliable observer to localize the Riptide underwater
• To validate these design with simulation
• To implement, tune and validate the controllers on the real system in an outdoor
environment
The localisation system designed in this project, involves equipments which were not

observer could not be implemented on the real AUV and the ﬁnal application could not

controllers.

2.1
Introduction
This chapter will discuss the navigation strategy and the localisation method which will
be used for the mission. It will also introduce some key concept from Kalman ﬁltering,
interval analysis and the particle ﬁlters.
2.2
The navigation strategy
The mission of the Riptide is to autonomously move under the ship without rising to the
surface and without underwater communication. Two navigation strategies are possible
for this mission:
• The ﬁrst approach is to make stable cycles under the ship (see Figure 2.1). The
navigation algorithm will be an automate which will make the robot stay under
the boat. With its echo-sounder, the Riptide can detect when it is under the ship
and when it leaves it. With this navigation, the AUV is not precisely localized but
it can still makes its measurements.
• The second approach is to have a precise localisation of the Riptide (see Figure
2.2). This way, the robot can follow a speciﬁc path given by the operator. Yet, this
method require new sensors for the localisation. These sensors consist of markers
emitting acoustic waves. With time diﬀerence of arrival, the robot can measure
its distance to the markers. These sensors were not available during the master
thesis.
While the ﬁrst approach is easier to implement, the second one will give more ﬂexibility
for the navigation and can be used for other type of missions. Therefore, the second
approach will be investigated in this project.

2.3.2
The interval filter
The principle of the interval ﬁlter, [Roh+17], [CG13], [KJW98] (IF), is to remove zones
of the state space that contain only inconsistent values. It is known to be over pessimistic
but it has a high level of integrity, since it allows to take into account many types of
errors such as the linearization errors [RP05] [DJ16] [San+14], the discretization errors
of the diﬀerential state equation [Com05] [RRC04], [RN11] [SC16] and the measurement
noise [MPH96; PW96] [Mil+96] [Mei+02]. This method provides a set represented by
a union of non-overlapping boxes. This union is guaranteed to contain the true state.

For all x ∈R , the conﬁdence interval [x] is deﬁned so that x ∈[x].

[x] = [x1] × . . . × [xn]. As a consequence x ∈[x]. The set of all boxes of Rn is denoted

2.4
Summary
From the results of this static localisation problem, the EKF is a precise method for
nonlinear state estimation. However, if the initial estimated state is far from the true
one, the ﬁlter may diverge, mainly due to an inconsistent linearization. Moreover, while
the IF and the PF are both global methods, the IF shows more consistency and requires
less computation. Therefore, we propose to choose a point estimated by an interval
method, as a linearization point of the extended Kalman ﬁlter. We will show that this
combination allows us to get a higher level of integrity of the extended Kalman ﬁlter.
In chapter 4, a new ﬁlter will be designed to help with this issue: the Interval Extended
Kalman Filter (IEKF).

3.1
Introduction
This Chapter presents the overall control strategy as well as the design of the controller
and the planner, based on the nonlinear model of the Riptide.
3.2
Control strategy
To archive its mission, the Riptide must follow the path speciﬁed by the set of parameters
Lp at the desired speed vd (vd > 0). A planner is designed to calculate the desired
orientation matrix Rd to guide the drone. Then, a speed and orientation controllers are
designed to make the system reach the set point {vd, Rd}. Finally the speed and position
of the AUV are estimated by an Observer consisting of an IEKF to give feedback signals
to the planner and the controller. The Figure 3.1 shows the global block diagram.
Figure 3.1: Overall block diagram
3.3
Modeling of the system

Since the EKF can diverge if the linearization point is far from the real state, we can
use a new observer called IEKF (Interval EKF). This ﬁlter consist of an interval ﬁlter
and an extended Kalman ﬁlter. The interval ﬁlter helps the second ﬁlter by taking as a
linearization point ¯xk, a point inside the list of boxes Lx,k. In practice it was taken as
the center of one of the boxes in Lx,k which maximizes the consistency with respect to
the observations. Now, ¯xk may also be the estimate given by the IEKF when the latter
is consistent enough. The IF could be replaced by a particle ﬁlter, but the IF is more
interesting because is require less computations.

which is both safe and accurate is not new.

6.6
The diving of the Robot
The diving of the Riptide is not discussed in the theoretical section because it is hard to
model the surfacing robot. It is more eﬃcient to design and validate a diving procedure
based on the experiments. The mass of the Riptide is distributed to stabilize the robot
at a null pitch and a null roll. As a consequence, when the robot is surfacing at the
beginning of its mission, the top ﬁn is outside of the water and has no eﬀect on the
orientation. Moreover the thrust of the propeller is reduced and when the propeller is
active, the torque of the motor gives some roll to the robot.
Therefore, the diving procedure was empirically designed as the following:
• The robot moves forward for 3 seconds with the desired orientation Rd =R(t = 0).
During this phase, the robot gain speed while surfacing
• The robot then puts its propeller inside the water (see Figure 6.4) to gain maximum
thrust for 1 second with the desired orientation: Rd = e−π/4·we∧· R(t = 0).
• The robot dive for 1 second with the desired orientation Rd = eπ/12·we∧· R(t = 0).
The orientation of the robot oscillates and is not optimal during the diving phase which
is not a problem for the mission. Figures 6.5, 6.6 and 6.7 shows the orientation of the
Robot during some diving procedures. It start at 13sec and ends at 18sec. The the pitch
and yaw curves are similar. The robot turn a little toward the left and dive with the
same pitch. However the roll curve is not always similar because internal components
of the robot did move between the experiment, changing the mass distribution of the
robot and the stable roll.

6.8
Future work
Regarding the results of the experiments, the oscillations and the instabilities are prob-
ably mainly due to the delay in the close loop, estimated at 0.2 s from the rotation of
the riptide to the movement of the ﬁns. This delay can be reduces by implementing
the controller at a lower level and no longer under ROS. Then the controller could be
optimize to enhance the precision and stability of the orientation.
Apart from improving the controller, the hardware of the robot can be modiﬁed to
meet with the controller limit. For example, an echo-sounder can be added at the top
of the Riptide to make measurements of the ship with a 0 deg roll. At the end, it is
better to focus on one improvement at the time to avoid a situation were the many
modiﬁcations have made the Riptide unstable and where the cause is hard to identify.
After some control improvement, it is a good idea to ﬁrst implement stable cycles
under the boat (see Figure 2.1) to show some prove of concept. Then the planner and
the IEFK can be implemented and experimentally validated.
6.9
Summary
The tuning of the orientation controller has been more diﬃcult than expected. Since
the Riptide’s orientation was unstable during the ﬁrst experiments, we cautiously tuned
the controller though more and more complex missions resulting in a stable controller
with limited performances.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
A lot of progress has been made during this master thesis towards the autonomous
navigation of the Riptide under a ship. First, a new observer based on interval analysis
and Kalman ﬁltering has been designed, the Interval Extended Kalman Filter. The IEFK
has shown similar performances than the EKF in simulation with a better consistency.
Then, a ﬁrst version of the speed and orientation controllers have been designed and
validated through simulation and tests. These controllers have shown stability in the
experimental conditions. As a result, the Riptide can now do some short underwater
missions with a speciﬁed path.
