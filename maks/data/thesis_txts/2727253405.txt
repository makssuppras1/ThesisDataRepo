Abstract
The physiological reactions and perception of Indoor environmental quality domains
were investigated by exposing 12 participants to 3 conditions for 150 minutes. The con-
ditions were predefined from a previous study and consisted of a high-quality condition,
a low-quality condition with an expected 40 % PPD for each domain, and a low-quality
condition with one self-chosen domain improved to high quality.
Mean skin temperature, skin humidity, heart rate, tear film stability, salivary alpha-
amylase concentration, and lung function through R5, R20, and AX were measured at
the 15th minute, the 45th, and at the 135th minute during the exposure to determine if
any and which of the physiological reactions could be identified to the indoor environment
conditions. Participants were also asked to complete a questionnaire simultaneously with
the measurements, evaluating the domains of air quality, thermal comfort, visual appeal,
acoustic quality, and indoor environmental quality.
According to the main findings, the mean skin temperature was highest in the high-
quality and low-quality conditions, increased in the first 45 minutes, and reduced grad-
ually as the exposure duration exceeded 45 minutes in all cases. The heart rate was
lowest when the quality was high and decreased as time passed independently from the
conditions. The study also discovered a significant correlation between heart rate and
thermal acceptability, with heart rate decreasing as thermal acceptability increased.
In the study, it was observed that the group focusing on improving the thermal domain
had lower skin humidity in the high-quality condition than in the low-quality condition.
The group prioritizing visual improvements had higher ∆R20 and ∆AX values in the
high-quality condition than in the low-quality condition. On the other hand, the group
aiming to enhance IAQ had lower ∆R5 value and heart rate in the high-quality condition
than in the low-quality condition. Interestingly, the groups focusing on IAQ and temper-
ature did not exhibit any correlation between their acceptability votes and improvement
domains. The group selecting acoustic improvements reported greater satisfaction with
the acoustic domain when it was enhanced. Lastly, the group choosing glare as their
first improvement domain expressed greater acceptability of the visual domain when all
domains were improved rather than just the glare domain. Only one group perceived
the improvement in their first choice domain, indicating that awareness of improvements
affects perception of the indoor environment.

Resume
De fysiologiske reaktioner og opfattelsen af indeklima domæner blev undersøgt ved at
udsætte 12 deltagere for 3 indeklima forhold i 150 minutter. Forholdene var forudde-
fineret fra en tidligere undersøgelse og bestod af et indeklima af høj kvalitet, et indeklima
af lav kvalitet med en forventet PPD p˚a 40 % for hvert domæne, og et indeklima af lav
kvalitet med et selvvalgt domæne forbedret til høj kvalitet.
Gennemsnitlig hudtemperatur, hudfugtighed, puls, t˚arefilmstabilitet, alfa-amylase kon-
centration i spyt og lungefunktion gennem R5, R20 og AX blev m˚alt ved 15. minut
45. og ved 135. minut, for at undersøge, om nogen og hvilke af de fysiologiske reak-
tioner, der kunne relateres til indeklimaet. Deltagerne blev ogs˚a bedt om at udfylde et
spørgeskema samtidig med m˚alingerne, hvor de evaluerede luftkvaliteten, det termiske,
visuelle, akustisken og kvaliteten af det overordnede indeklima.
Resultaterne viste at den gennemsnitlige hudtemperatur var p˚a sit højeste i høj- og lavk-
valitetstilstanden, men blev gradvist reduceret, efterh˚anden som eksponeringsvarigheden
oversteg 45 minutter. Pulsen var lavest, n˚ar kvaliteten af indeklimaet var høj og faldt
som tiden gik uafhængigt af kvaliteten i indeklimaet. Undersøgelsen opdagede ogs˚a en
sammenhæng mellem hjertefrekvens og termisk acceptabilitet, hvor pulsen er lavere ved
en høj termisk acceptabilitet.
Derudover viste resultaterne, at gruppen, der fokuserede p˚a at forbedre det termiske
domæne, havde lavere hudfugtighed i tilstanden af høj kvalitet end i tilstanden af
lav kvalitet.
Gruppen, der prioriterede visuelle forbedringer, havde højere R20- og
AX-værdier i højkvalitetstilstand sammenlignet med lavkvalitetstilstand. P˚a den an-
den side havde gruppen, der sigtede p˚a at forbedre IAQ, lavere R5-værdi og puls i
højkvalitetstilstand end i lavkvalitetstilstand. Grupperne der fokuserede p˚a IAQ og tem-
peratur, viste ingen sammenhæng mellem deres acceptability og forbedringsdomæner.
Gruppen, der valgte akustiske forbedringer, rapporterede større tilfredshed med det

domæner blev forbedret, snarere end blot det visulle domæne. Kun en gruppe opfattede
den forbedring de tidligere havde valgt som den vigtigste, hvilket indikere at oplysning
om hvilket domæne der er forbedring, har en effect p˚a opfattelsen af indeklimaet.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
The understanding of the indoor environment is continuously advancing, and it plays
a vital role in our everyday existence. Recently, articles on multiple domains’ interac-
tions and physiological reactions have increased. The approaches have been varying,
as have the results of both perceptions of physiological reactions.[4] The weighting of
domains is invariant, and no interaction of domains is considered in standards when
estimating the sensation and acceptability of a certain domain. Control of HVAC sys-
tems from physiological reactions has great potential for energy efficiency and optimal
impact on humans.[5][6][7] Even though the correlation between perception and indoor
climate domains is well known[8], the correlation between perception and the underlying
physiological reactions is still be discovered.
Linearity between skin temperature and air temperature has been found by Sun et al.[4,
pp. 12-13]. They investigated the relationship between temperature, illuminance, sound
level, perception, and physiological reactions. When measuring systolic blood pressure,
diastolic blood pressure, heart rate, and skin temperatures at the wrist and forehead, the
study revealed that physiological parameters were affected to varying degrees by multi-
ple environmental factors and had different degrees of correlation with human comfort
votes. Moreover, the measured skin temperature at the wrist varied linearly with the
air temperature and exhibited an S-shaped relationship with thermal comfort vote. The
results indicate that skin temperature should be considered when describing the thermal
sensation.
Mehmood et al. investigated the combined effects of noise, light, and temperature on
human neurophysiological responses[9]. The results showed that the combined effects of
all domains were greater than the influence of the single domains. They also experienced
that the effect of temperature on Heart rate was more substantial than the effect of noise.
Therefore, heart rate should also be measured when studying physiological reactions to
indoor climate exposures.
Dalewski M. investigated physiological effects of thermal environment on office work
[10]. His study showed that the effectiveness of the work did not decrease but that the
participants provided more effort to maintain the same results. The physiological results
showed, among others, that the relative skin humidity on fingers increased significantly
with increased temperature and that the thermal votes correlated with skin temperature
measurements. It was also found that the alpha-amylase concentration decreased in ex-
posure to high temperatures. Like Dalweski M., Willem H.C. and Tham K.W. concluded
from their results that the salivary alpha-amylase level could be used as a physiologic
indicator of mental alertness associated with thermal exposures.[11]
Fan et al. found in a study of Physiological and psychological reactions of sub-tropically
acclimatized subjects exposed to different indoor temperatures at a relative humidity

In their study, skin temperature, skin moisture, and heart rate were also significantly

In 1989, a study investigating human reactions to indoor air pollutants showed a decrease

The studies indicate that investigation of physiological reactions of different indoor en-

conditions.
The objective is to find any correlation between physiological reactions
and perception, as well as identify physiological reactions to the improvement of the
most important environmental domain. It will also investigate how awareness regarding
improvements in indoor environmental quality domains affects perception.

PREVIOUS STUDY
2
Previous study
This study is based on an earlier experiment conducted by Mads Bøg and Silas Hentze
Magyar.[1] Their study investigated interactions of multiple domains defining the indoor
environment and the effects on perception and performance. The objective was to iden-
tify and rank the most important indoor environmental domain and the influence and
perception of IEQ (Indoor Environmental Quality), and on that basis, predict occupant
preferences when exposed to an equal amount of discomfort in each domain.
The experiment consisted of two parts. The first part of the experiment determined
the subjects’ dissatisfaction at three levels of decay of noise (48 dB(A), 54 dB(A), 60
dB(A)), glare (10 UGR, 22 UGR, 30 UGR), temperature (18°C, 20°C, 22°C), and air
quality (2.5 m2, 7.1 m2, 11.7 m2 old carpets). A low-quality condition was determined
with an aimed 40 % PPD for each domain. In the second part of the experiment, the
participants were placed in a low-quality condition and asked which domain they would
improve. In the next condition, they were placed in this domain improved to a good
condition. This was repeated until the order of every parameter for every participant
was known.
The conditions high-quality and low-quality used in this study were the results of the
conditions found in the first part of Silas and Mads’ results. The first choice parameter
of improvement from the participants in the second part of their study was determining
the condition of the improved condition that they were exposed to in this study.

METHODOLOGY
3
Methodology
3.1
Approach
The experiment was carried out in the 3 field labs at the Department of Environmen-
tal and resource engineering, Section for indoor environment, Technical University of
Denmark.
12 students from DTU with different nationalities were chosen from the previous study
described in section 2.
The participants were placed in a condition with 4 variable
domains; temperature, glare, acoustic and air quality.
They were exposed to three
conditions for 150 minutes per condition. All conditions were determined in the previous
study. The first was a high-quality condition with good conditions for all 4 domains.
The second condition was a low-quality condition with an expected dissatisfaction of
40% for all 4 domains. The third condition was a low-quality condition, with their first
choice of parameter improved to a good condition.
During exposure, the participants did a survey just before each measurement, evalu-
ating the sensation and acceptability of the air quality, thermal, acoustic, and visual
environment in the room, rating every parameter on a scale from 1 to 24.
While in the chamber, Samples of tear mucus were taken as an indicator of efficacy in
maintaining tear film stability, and mucus crystallization was observed enlarged by 40
under a microscope Olympus CX31-P [15], photographed, and classified. Aplha-amylase
concentration was measured with a SAAM (Salivary Amylase Activity Monitor) [16], skin
moisture with a corneometer National DM-R1 [17], and lung function with a Tremoflo
C100 Oscillometry system. [18][19] Mean skin temperature was logged using Ibuttons of
type DS1922L-F5 [20] and a polar H10 heart rate sensor for the heart rate. [21]
Protective equipment in terms of latex gloves was applied before each measurement, and
all measurement equipment was sanitized before the measurements.
3.2
Experimental facilities
3.2.1
Setup
All experiments were carried out in the 3 field labs. The field labs were built to repre-
sent an identical exposure, having the same windows, orientation, size, and ventilation
system. The field labs are placed beside each other, with a control room at the end. A
graphic representation of the setup in the field labs is shown in figure 1, with lab 1, lab
2, and lab 3 being identical.

3.2.2
Participants
All participants completing the previous experiment were asked if they wanted to par-
ticipate in this experiment through email, with a brief introduction to the measurements
and tasks in the field labs. An introduction day was considered unnecessary, as they
already knew the concept and the labs from the previous experiment. The participants
were paid by the hour for their contribution as a subject in the experiment.

3.5.3
Models and statistics
A linear mixed effect analysis of the relationship between the perception of each pa-
rameter and the physiological reactions were used to investigate the interaction between
perception and physiological reactions. The measurements were assumed to change over
time. Hence time was implemented in all models as a factor. Humans perceive and react
to the environment differently; therefore, the participants (ID) were implemented in the
model as random effects. Forward selection was used by gradually adding one physi-
ological parameter as a fixed effect and then assessing the model’s performance using
the AIC coefficient [37]. The AIC coefficient balances the trade-off between model com-
plexity and goodness of fit. [38] The AIC suggests the most suitable model among the
created models without indicating its overall quality. Hence, a Likelihood-ratio test was
performed to determine the statistical significance of the correlation of the parameters
in the model with the perception that was described. [39] This was done by comparing a
model without the parameter being evaluated (null model) to a model with the param-
eter being evaluated (full model). Testing if there was a significant difference between
the two models. The analyses were conducted with a α = 0.05 significance level. If a
17

significant difference between the models where found with a p-value < 0.05, then the
parameter had a significant effect when describing the given perception.
When testing for a significant difference in physiological measurement and perception
results between time and conditions, a paired t-test was used, and a significant level
of α = 0.05 were chosen.
When testing for a significant difference in physiological
measurement between the LQ and HQ conditions divided by the first improvement

DISCUSSION
5
Discussion
This chapter discusses the methods and results of studying physiological reactions to
indoor climate domains and their relationship to perception. This research’s limitations
and recommendations for future work will also be explored.
5.1
Measurements and acceptability
Measuring and exploring differences in the physiological reactions shown to be difficult.
Despite the participants being the same age as all students, their physical measurements
would vary under the same conditions. All physiological measurements should be com-
pared to a reference value of the participant, and even this would be insufficient at all
parameters, as a change in one unit at one parameter for a participant is different from
one unit in that parameter for another participant.
The mean skin temperature was measured by a calculation from 8 different locations
at the body. This proved challenging as it was noticed that the Ibuttons were placed
in an incorrect location on at least one occasion, thereby giving a wrong coefficient in
calculating the mean skin temperature. One participant was permitted to change the
clothing, thereby changing the clo value by 0.48 [8, p.
21].
The model of thermal
acceptability was tried to be built from data without the participant with the wrong
placement of Ibuttons and without the participant with changed clo value. But this did
not alter the AIC coefficient to make ST a part of the thermal acceptability model.
As seen in figure 15a, the mean skin temperature did not change between the HQ and
LQ conditions. A change in the method, by only measuring the temperature close to the
heart and the body’s extremities as Sun et al.[4], might have shown different results. This
would be a more simple method of measuring and might have given results showing what
the perception results show in figure 14, where a difference in the thermal acceptability
of the LQ and HQ condition is visible, as well as the decrease of thermal acceptability
over time for all conditions.
The participants’ skin humidity levels were measured on the top of their hands. No
significant difference between the conditions was shown in 15b. This experiment did
not expect the same increase in skin humidity that M. Dalewski[10] experienced at high
temperatures, as the temperature range was from 5% PPD and colder down to 40%
PPD, and the met values were unchanged throughout the experiment.
As Mehmood et al. discovered an effect of temperature on heart rate[9], this study found
a correlation between heart rate and thermal acceptability. The heart rate is individual,
and it is impossible to talk about a ”normal” heart rate as long as it is inside a certain
range.[40, p. 2] As resting heart rate can vary with up to 70 BPM, creating a function
from the heart rate without considering every individual is impossible. Therefore, the
thermal acceptability model constructed from the participants’ heart rates can only be
37
==PAGE:48==
5

implemented for this specific group, as it is improbable to apply to a random person
outside this experiment. A change of 1 BMP is different from one person to another, and
the BMP should be related to the Resting heart rate and maximum heart rate, which
also change for each individual with age, BMI, etc.
The concentration of alpha-amylase (AA) does not follow a predictable pattern and can
be greatly influenced by food and drink intake before each measurement. Additionally,
AA levels vary significantly between individuals, making assessing each participant’s
concentration essential and establishing a reference value for stress-free levels. As for the
skin humidity, M. Dalewski found a change in alpha-amylase concentration at exposure
to high temperatures [10], something the participants were not exposed to in this study.
This could be explained by the small and low-temperature range the participants were
exposed to in this study, as previous results showed a decreasing trend of the alpha-
amylase concentration correlated with the participants feeling thermal warm.
The tear film classification results were influenced by the participant’s ability to gather
a good sample. The size and quality of tears varied a lot between the participants and
for each individual, making it difficult to tell in many cases if the tear film should be
evaluated as type 4 or if the sample should be disregarded. This was shown in figure 17a
with no significant differences in the tear film classification between the conditions or over
time. Tear film stability decreased at high n-decane concentrations in an earlier study
[13]. This concentration was not controlled or measured in this experiment. Therefore
it is unknown if the air in the conditions with old blankets had an increased n-decane
concentration or if the air quality was reduced with other VOCs or solely dust in the old
blankets.
The results from the oscillometry were, as the only physiological parameter, shown as
delta values from a reference calculated by the tremoflo C-100 Airwave Oscillometry
System. This reference value is calculated just by putting in age, gender, BMI, and
smoker/nonsmoker, simplifying the actual physics of the participant. Even though the
values were simplified, the oscillometry values were evaluated by the lowest AIC as the
best parameter for describing 3 out of 4 indoor climate domains, indicating that lung
function measurements could be a powerful parameter describing the perception. In this
experiment, it was assumed that all delta values from the reference value were negative.
This is an assumption that could course very misleading results, as low resistant values
are often a sign of good lung function. It was not investigated what delta values where
above the reference resistance and which were below.
As for the tear film stability,
the air quality was not controlled by measurements but simply by the addition of old
blankets, and an association between ambient air pollution and reduced lung function
could, therefore, not be shown.
The physiological measurements showed, in general, not to have a significant impact
when creating models for the perception of the indoor climate, either indicating that the

5.2
Limitations
12 participants participated, indicating an insufficient sample size. The sample size was
reduced to 6, 3, 2, and 1 when dividing the participant into first-choice improvement
parameters. The lack of participants made calculating the significance of perception
and physiological reactions difficult. With a fairly big distribution of votes as seen in
figure 14, indicating votes of both acceptable and unacceptable for the same time and
condition, it can be assumed that results with more participants for every first choice
parameter would give different results.
As the conditions and setup in the field labs were aimed to match the setup in the
previous study, the limitations in terms of installations and control in the field labs from
the previous study were also present, and the LQ and HQ conditions were created from
the participants’ votes in the previous study. Therefore this study assumes that the
smaller group of participants from the previous study resulted in the exact evaluation
of the differences in domains and, thereby, the same parameters for the LQ and HQ
39
==PAGE:50==

conditions. Whether this is true could have been tested by a new benchmarking of the
indoor climate parameters, but still with the assumption that the chosen parameters
apply to all the participants.
The air quality in the field labs was not controlled or logged in any other way than the
HQ condition did not have carpets, where the LQ condition was supplied with 19 m2
of old carpets in the vertical ventilation cabinet. This made it difficult to draw any
other conclusions on the perception and quality of the IAQ than the conditions were
difficult for the participants to perceive. Changing the ventilation rate as a function of
different IAQ conditions could have been a solution, but this would come with the effect
of changing the sound levels and removing different levels of bio-influence. No significant
difference where found in the perception of the air quality between either conditions or
over time. This could indicate that this parameter was difficult for the human senses
to detect and perceive or that the difference in air quality between the HQ and LQ
conditions was not big enough.
The visual quality in the shape of glare from the light bulb was neither controlled during
the experiment.
As for air quality, it was assumed that light bulb illuminance was
240 lx, giving a UGR value of 13, resulting in an LQ condition with 40 % PPD for
the visual domain.
This assumption limits the experiment and makes it difficult to
relate perception or physiological measurements to a certain value of UGR, as it was not
confirmed during the experiment. The windows in the field labs were covered with both
plastic and cardboard, but with a minimal amount of daylight still entering the room.
This could have affected their visual acceptability, as daylight has a positive effect on
the visual sensation.[41]
The computer in the control room controlled the thermal environment, adjusting the
ventilation system’s inlet temperature. As shown in figure 4, the temperatures rarely
reached precise 20°C and 22°C during exposure to LQ and HQ conditions due to the
system taking time to adjust the inlet temperature. This could have coursed the thermal
domain in the LQ condition to have higher acceptability than if temperatures in the LQ
condition had been closer to 20°C.
5.3
Recommendations
For more comprehensive studies of physiological reactions, it is advisable to closely moni-
tor indoor climate parameters. The findings of the current study are challenging to relate
to anything beyond perception. It is worth noting that the environmental conditions in
the field labs are artificial, and replicating a more realistic office space environment could
produce varying results in terms of perception and physiological reactions.
40
==PAGE:51==
5
DISCUSSION
This experiment investigated very limited indoor climate conditions; exposure to a
broader range might give a comprehensive picture of the physiological reactions. Heart
rate was low at high acceptability at 22°C, indicating higher heart rate in cooler condi-
tions. But as the heart rate is not expected to be linear across exposure to a wide range
of temperatures, heart rate at higher temperatures with a thermal sensation of feeling
cold would be interesting to investigate.
To obtain more accurate results, it is recommended to expand the sample size both in
terms of quantity and diversity. Few statistically significant differences were found in
the results, particularly when dividing the data into first-choice parameters, with only
1 and 2 participants in a group. Since the physiological outcomes differed, increasing
the number of participants could lead to the identification of a standard range for the
physiological measurements.
In addition, it is important to mention that the study only involved young adults aged
between 20 and 35 years. To ensure a more diverse representation of humans, it would
be advantageous to include participants of varied age groups in future studies and con-
sider age as a factor in the models describing the correlation between perception and
physiological reactions.
When creating the models describing the perception of domains with the physiological
measurements, participant variability was considered by ID. Still, it is assumed that the
fixed effects are the same for all participants since the data is insufficient to create random
slopes. A model containing random slopes would be recommended for further work since
the participants show different intercepts and slopes. As these models eventually would
be used for controlling personalized control systems, it is essential to have random slopes
included in the model. The ICC coefficient could have been used in the investigation of
how much of the variance in the data is explained by the participant’s differences.
Controlling systems from perception and physiological measurements might have a big
potential and is something Toftum et al. investigated.[5, p. 8] Their study showed that
29% of the occupants’ thermal comfort increased while it decreased for 71 % of the
occupants when implementing the user-driven control strategy. This tendency is inter-
esting, as the results of this experiment showed that participants wanted to improve the
temperature and showed an improvement in the acceptability votes from the improved
condition. Indicating that regulations of the temperature by perception are effective.
According to the findings of this experiment, utilizing heart rate could be an effective
way to regulate a customized HVAC system.
The participant’s heart rate and skin
temperature were measured discreetly with ease, either every second or every minute.
Previous research has shown a clear correlation between skin temperature and tempera-
ture in indoor environments[4], making further investigation into these parameters and
their relation to perception and indoor climate engaging.

CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
Two physiological reactions were identified to vary between high and low-quality or im-
proved conditions. The mean skin temperature decreased from high-quality to improved
condition, as well as a decrease in mean skin temperature when exposure was extended
to more than 45 minutes. The Heart rate was significantly lower in the high-quality
condition compared to the low-quality condition, and a decrease in heart rate over time
from the start till the end of the experiment was seen in every condition.
The study showed that heart rate correlated with thermal acceptability, but no other
significant correlation describing the perception from physiological reactions of the par-
ticipants was found.
The group choosing to improve the thermal domain had a significantly lower skin hu-
midity in the high-quality condition compared to the low-quality condition. The group
choosing visual as their first improvement domain had a significantly higher ∆R20 and
∆AX in the high-quality than in the low-quality. The participant choosing to improve
the IAQ domain had a significantly lower ∆R5 and heart rate in the high-quality condi-
tion compared to the low-quality condition.

show this correlation with their acceptability votes in this study. The group selecting
acoustic as their first improvement domain experienced greater satisfaction with the
acoustic domain in the condition with this parameter improved. The last group selecting
glare as their first improvement domain showed greater acceptability of the visual domain
with all domains improved, but not higher acceptability of the visual domain with only
this domain improved. Only one group perceived the improvement in their first choice
domain, indicating that awareness of improvements affects perception of the indoor
environment.
