Abstract
Hydrogen has historically been produced primarily from reforming of fossil fuels with
only a small fraction of production coming from electrolysis, which has been used for
special applications. This could change however, since both academics and industry
recently has been looking at electrolysis as a possible means to mitigate the intermittency
problem of several renewable power sources.
Further they envision hydrogen as an
input for future bio-fuel production and as an alternative vehicle propellant, which
could expand the market for hydrogen considerately. This calls for electrolyzer market
participation strategies, which has not yet been covered thoroughly in the literature.
Therefore, this thesis will focus on day-ahead and regulating power market participation
strategies that include the effects of uncertainties in market forecasts.
Two distinct day-ahead strategies and four regulating power strategies are proposed.
One regulating power strategy divides the optimization of day-ahead and regulating
power markets into two steps and it is obvious that this strategy gives the best economic
results with around 10-15 pct in saved operation costs compared to the base case.
Contract designs can influence the operations of the electrolyzer, which is why we look
at a fixed and flexible delivery contract and assess its impact on operations and costs.
Further, we analyze the effect of a non-delivery option in contract design. It is clear that
the non-delivery option enables the provision of more regulating power bids and lowers
the total cost of operation by around 1 pct. We use relatively simple market forecasting
methods based on historical data to produce deterministic forecasts for the day-ahead
market and stochastic forecasts for the regulating power market. Better forecasting is
identified as important for improving the performance of the proposed strategies.
Electrolyzer degradations from dynamic operation is important for the optimum
operations strategy. However, no model currently exists in literature that successfully
model this. We formulate a degradations model that takes dynamic degradations into
account, but unfortunately this model is not strongly backed by research. We assess
that electrolyzer degradation costs lies between 0.05 and 8.0 €/(kg h−1) and that
electrolyzer degradation costs has to be around 1.4 €/(kg h−1) or lower for active market
participation to be feasible.
Excess electrolyzer and storage capacity is identified as essential for the possibilities
for active market participation and a model for economic comparison of different investments
is developed. Under the applied assumptions, it is possible to make a positive return on
investment in additional electrolyzer and storage capacity if the assumed degradation
costs and power market price arbitrage are favorable. Assuming relatively high power
market arbitrage allows for a degradation cost of around 0.6 €/(kg h−1) whereas medium
price arbitrage only allows for a degradation cost of around 0.05 €/(kg h−1).

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Hydrogen is used in several industrial processes and is further envisioned as one of the
potential technologies for achieving a carbon-free transport sector. Further, production
of hydrogen through electrolysis could play a role in facilitating the implementation
of large shares of intermittent power generation in the grid.
Today most hydrogen
production world wide comes from reforming of fossil fuels with global shares of 48 %
from natural gas, 30 % from oil and 18 % from coal. 4 % of the global production comes
from electrolysis [1] which is mainly used for applications where high hydrogen purity
is required [2]. Hydrogen is mainly used for amonia production, petrolium processing
and methanol production with United States market shares of 50 %, 37 % and 8 %
respectively [3].
Following the European trend towards a carbon-free energy sector, the French company
Air Liquid has committed itself to produce at least 50 % of their hydrogen dedicated for
energy applications without CO2 emissions by 2020 where electrolysis is expected to be
a key technology [4]. Air Liquid proposed the original test case for this thesis and has
been giving feedback throughout the thesis work.
1.1
Context and Motivation
Renewable energy penetration is increasing in much of the EU power grid and in 2012
a majority in the danish parliament agreed that half of the danish power consumption
should be covered by wind in 2020 and that the entire danish heat and power production
should be covered by renewable sources in 2035 [5]. Optimal usage of the large penetrations
of intermittent renewable power generators will require demand and the whole system to
behave flexibly [6]. Electrolysis of water utilizing power from the grid is an interesting
option in power grids with large shares of intermittent renewable energy production,
because these can produce hydrogen while balancing the fluctuating power production
from intermittent renewable energy sources such as wind and solar [2] [7]. With this
in mind, the large expansion of wind and solar power in many European countries in
recent years could make electrolysis an increasingly interesting technology for balancing
power generation with consumption.
An electrolyzer combined with a hydrogen storage tank can in some regards act in
the electricity markets similarly to other storage units. [8] shows a 75 pct difference in
average revenues between the most and least price volatile of 7 different U.S. electricity
markets, which shows that the market price arbitrage is important for the revenue a
storage unit can achieve. Further, [8] looks at the economic viability of 14 different energy
storage technologies and conclude that the optimum size of the storage lies between 1
==PAGE:24==

and 4 hours of full production for most technologies, with a maximum of 7-8 hours for
compressed air and pumped hydro storage.
Currently, dynamic operation of electrolyzers in relation to power markets is not
common. However, A German demonstration project with a 6 MW PEM-electrolyzer
plant launched in 2015 will demonstrate the technologies potential for storing surplus
renewable energy and balancing the power grid. The produced hydrogen will be used
for natural gas grid injection and trailer filling for truck delivery [9]. Depending on the
natural gas grid and natural gas composition, 5-15 pct of hydrogen by volume can be
blended with the existing natural gas without significant increase in risk of using the
gas-mixture in end-use devises or problems operating the grid [10].
Using electrolysis for hydrogen production is an important element in several energy
planners vision for a sustainable future Danish energy system. In [11] the Danish Energy
Agency envisions that a future renewable Danish energy system can utilize electrolysis for
hydrogen production, where the major part of hydrogen will be utilized for upgrading
domestic biomass sources to synthetic natural gas and liquid bio-fuels.
The report
envisions 1000-1500 MW of electrolyzer capacity in Denmark in 2035 and 4000-6000 MW
capacity in 2050 depending on scenarios. The electrolyzers will be operated dynamically
to contribute to the energy balance in the grid considering the fluctuations from a
large penetration of intermittent renewable power generators - primarily wind and solar
power.
The yearly average utilization of the electrolyzers are envisioned around 50
pct. [12] investigates the value of flexible operation of electorlyzers in the Danish power
system in 2035 where the hydrogen is similarly utilized for synthetic natural gas and
bio-fuel production and concludes that the optimum hydrogen storage capacity will be at
around 15 hours of full load production. [13] similarly envisions a danish energy system
in 2050 with hydrogen production from electrolyzers being used for synthetic natural
gas and bio-fuel production. As noted in [12] the modelling assumes a perfect foresight
of consumption and production patterns from intermittent renewable power generators,
which means that the concluded surplus from flexible operation is a maximum achievable
measure, that will be lower in reality because of forecasting errors. [11] and [13] are
similarly not including the effects of forecasting errors.
The conversion of the road transport sector to sustainable energy sources is currently
a significant challenge for a future sustainable energy system. Hydrogen can be part of
the solution to this problem as fuel-cells can utilize hydrogen to deliver power for electric
cars.
In 2009, the future of vehicle propellants seemed to be an equal competition
between battery electric vehicles and fuel cell vehicles [2] and in 2008 some forecasted
the annual fuel cell vehicle sales in the EU to lie between 0.4 and 1.8 million cars per year
around 2020 and 2025 [14]. However, fuel-cell powered vehicles have not seen the same
recent success as battery electric vehicles. In [11] published in 2014, The Danish Energy
Agency envisions hydrogen to be utilized in fuel-cell trucks in one future energy system
scenario for 2035 and 2050, but they do not envision hydrogen used in cars or light
duty vans, where they instead expect batteries to be the energy carrier. [13] published
in 2015 envision 5 % of trucks in Denmark powered by fuel-cells in 2050 and similarly
no cars to be powered by fuel-cells. In contrast, Denmark has recently seen a roll-out
of a hydrogen fuelling stations network consisting of 10 stations spread throughout the

In this thesis we want to investigate operations strategies for electrolyzers in the
day-ahead and regulating power markets from a market participants point of view, which
has thus far not been considered in literature. We assess that such strategies can become
very important in the future considering the possibility of a large scale introduction of
dynamically operated electrolyzers for bio-fuel production and hydrogen as an alternative
vehicle propellant. Further, the increasing focus on decarbonizing the economy in many
parts of the world could open new markets for electrolysis that are currently served by
reforming of fossil fuels.
1.2
Methodology Of This Thesis
This thesis will focus on the participation of an electrolyzer system in the day-ahead and
regulating power markets of Western Denmark. Different market participation strategies
will be developed for both markets.
All strategies are formulated as mixed integer
linear programs, which are solved using the Gurobi solver using Python as programming
language.
Single hour order and block order participation strategies will be formulated for the
day-ahead market. Four different regulating power market strategies will be formulated.
One strategy delivers fixed regulating power bids and one strategy utilize co-optimization
of day-ahead and regulating power market participation. Another strategy first optimize
the day-ahead market participation and after the day-ahead market has cleared it
optimizes the regulating power market participation separately. Finally, one regulating
power strategy will investigate the effects of adding a non-delivery penalty to the hydrogen
contracts, which can allow for positions in the regulating power market that risk not
producing the full amount of demanded hydrogen. Robust optimization will be a central
element the regulating power participation strategies.
The electrolyzer system will consist of an electrolyzer unit with a connected compressed
hydrogen storage tank that can contribute to the flexibility of the operation. A flexible
and a fixed delivery contract design will be formulated and their impact on electrolyzer
operation assessed. The thesis takes the perspective of a commercial electrolyzer operator
and the proposed operations strategies relies on forecasting of relevant market quantities,
which due to forecasting errors will result in operations that are not always ideal compared
to participation with perfect information of the market. A perfect forecast will be used to
assess the inefficiencies in operations that comes from forecasting errors. Previous studies
of electrolyzers operating in the Danish power markets did not consider forecasting
errors [11], [12] and [13]. Hence, this thesis can help shed light on the negative impact
of forecasting uncertainties during electrolyzer operation and create a framework for

The impact of electrolyzer degradation from dynamic operation will be a central

Structure Of This Thesis
This master thesis is structured as follows: Chapter 2 gives a detailed explanation of the

CHAPTER 2
Power Markets In Western
Denmark
This chapter gives an introduction to the power markets available in Western Denmark.
Power in Western Denmark is traded on Nord Pool, which is the main marketplace
for power trade for the power systems in Denmark, Norway, Sweden, Finland, United
Kingdom and the Baltic countries.
The Nord Pool market is divided into bidding
areas, which are used to reflect the physical constraints for power transmission in the
power system. DK1 is the Western Denmark bidding area, which is synchronized with
the Continental Europe power system, and has DC interconnections to the Nordic
Synchronous area consisting of DK2, Sweden, Norway and Finland.
DK1 and DK2
are the two market bidding areas in Denmark and figure 2.1 shows their geographical
distribution.
2.1
The Day-Ahead Market
The main amount of power produced and consumed in the Nord Pool Area is traded
in the day-ahead (DA) market. Players can bid into this market until 12.00 the day

before operation with the bids consisting of hourly buy or sell positions which are valid
for operation the whole next day.
The Nord Pool day-ahead market is cleared together with other European day-ahead
markets covering about 85 pct of the European power consumption in total [16]. The
clearing is done by an optimization algorithm called EUPHEMIA that can handle several
regional special order design. The algorithms objective is to maximize the social welfare
of the clearing outcome [17]. The merit order principle is a simplified model of how
the clearing works, which gives a good intuition of it and gives a correct explanation of
how single hour orders works. Single hour orders will be explained later in this section.
The merit order sorts the buy bids in a decreasing price order and the sell bids in an
increasing price order. Figure 2.2 is a graphical illustration of the merit order principle.
The red curve is the supply curve which makes up all the sell bids and the blue curves
represent three possible demand curves which consists of buy bids for power. A common
market price of power λDA is found where supply of power meets demand and hence
a total amount of Q MWh power is produced. The merit order clearing is conducted
once every hour of the day. If the physical transmission limit between two bidding areas
is reached, then the power price is calculated separately for these two areas based on
supply and demand bids in each area and transmission from the connected areas [16].
The three blue demand curves is an illustration of how changes in demand affects the
market price. The dark blue curve might represent midday consumption, which results
in a medium day-ahead price. The most left curve could be night time consumption
which results in a relatively low price and the right most curve could be morning or
evening peak demand which results in a relatively high price. The supply curve can also
change though it is not depicted in the figure. Commonly the lowest price bids comes
from renewable energy sources which have low marginal production costs. In DK1 these
==PAGE:29==
2.1 The Day-Ahead Market
7
would primarily be wind power bids. Hence, the supply curve can be shifted left and
right based on the wind power production, which can affect the power price. It can also
be changed by the availability of other power generators.
Players can bid into the Nord Pool day-ahead market with 4 different types of orders:
single hourly orders, block orders, flexible orders and exclusive group orders. Single hour
orders define an amount of power which the player wants to buy or sell in a particular
hour and the corresponding minimum and maximum price the player is willing to buy
or sell for.
An order can be price independent, which means that it will be called
regardless of the cleared market price. Such an order has a minimum price of -500 e
and a maximum price of 3,000 e, which are the current minimum and maximum caps
on prices in Nord Pool.
Block orders define a block of buy or sell orders spanning over several hours. The
bidder defines the price it is willing to buy or sell for. In its basis form, Nord Pool
must either accept or reject the whole block. The bidder can further specify that the
block may be partially accepted and blocks can be connected such that one block is
dependent on the acceptance of another block. Connected blocks can e.g. be utilized
to express several steps in industrial processes that are dependent on another. Flexible
orders define a sell of buy position with an energy volume and duration, which can be
served within a specified time interval. The order can have a specified price limit and
will be activated by the market clearing algorithm in the period that maximizes social
welfare. Exclusive group orders consists of a group of the three other order types and
can be made up of buy or sell orders or a mix of buy and sell orders. Only one of the
orders in the group can be called and the market clearing algorithm chooses the order
which maximizes the social welfare. Exclusive group orders can be utilized to model
more complex relationships in the market participants asset and hence help to clear the
market in a more optimal way for the participant [16].
All trade on Nord Pool goes through a balance responsible party (BRP), which is a
legal entity that has entered into agreement on balancing responsibility with Energinet.dk
[18] [19].
We assume that the electrolyzer is either owned by a BRP or that the
electrolyzer owner has agreed with a BRP that it pays the day-ahead price based on its
hourly consumption of power. Further, we assume that the electrolyzer is a price taker
in the day-ahead market, which means that the bids it places in the market does not
affect the market price.
In the day-ahead market participation strategies developed in sections 6.1 and 6.2
we only make use price independent single hour orders. In subsection 6.2.2 we develop
a strategy concerning block orders, but without utilizing price ceilings, which means
that the block orders we consider are similar to a set of identical price independent
single hour orders. Block orders with price ceilings, flexible orders and exclusive groups
might be relevant for a more advanced electrolyzer strategy in the day-ahead markets.
For example, it could be relevant to place some of the production as a flexible order.
Utilizing the price ceiling could be another interesting point to consider, since this would
remove the risk for producing hydrogen during uncommon large price spikes that are
not forecasted by the applied day-ahead price forecast.

2.2
Regulation Power Market
The role of the regulation power market is to ensure that generation capacity is ready to
correct discrepancies between the schedules from the day-ahead and intra-day markets
and the actual operation within the day (the intra-day is introduced in section 2.4).
Discrepancies can arise from e.g.
forecasting errors for renewable energy generation,
forecasting errors on the demand side or unplanned outages of generation units.
Bids can be submitted and corrected continuously in this market until 45 min
before the hour of operation from when the bid is binding.
Bids can either be up-
or down-regulation. When placing a bid of a particular volume in the regulation power
market, the player must have grid connected assets ready to deliver that volume within
15 minutes of receiving an activation signal from Energinet.dk. The asset must be in
standby mode and is only activated if needed. Bids in the regulation power market must
be between 10 MW and 50 MW of size, and can be made up of a combination of units
within a bidding area. Up-regulation bids corresponds to the player injecting additional
power into the grid once activated and down-regulation corresponds to injecting less
power into the grid.
For production units, up-regulation corresponds to increasing
production and for consumption units it corresponds to decreasing consumption. Down-regulation
corresponds to decreased production for production units and increased consumption for
consumption units.
The price on regulation power is found according to the merit order principle (introduced
with figure 2.2), where bids are ordered according to price and the cheapest bids gets
called before the more expensive. All units are paid the price of the most expensive
bid, which is called. Bids are for the energy delivered in e/MWh and units are settled
for the energy they deliver if activated. Bids not activated are not paid through the
regulation power market. The minimum price of up-regulation is the day-ahead price
and its maximum price is 5,000 e/MWh. The maximum price of down-regulation is
the day-ahead price. Hence, a consumption unit like an electrolyzer can sell back power
through up-regulation bids at a price higher than what it paid for that power in the
day-ahead market.
Similarly it can buy additional power at a lower price than the
day-ahead price through down-regulation bids.
This means that consumption units
can lower their average energy procurement cost by making some flexibility in their
consumption available through the regulation power market. However, since activations
are uncertain it is not straightforward for e.g. an electrolyzer operator to participate in
this market and still respect its production schedule.
Each operation hour is either in up- or down-regulation or it has no regulation power.
The direction of regulating power is determined by the overall direction of imbalances
for the hour.
An hour can have special regulation where individual down bids are
activated during an up-regulation hour or similarly where up bids are activated during
a down-regulation hour. Special regulation bids are paid according to the pay-as-bid
principle where the activated bid gets paid the price it had placed in the regulating-power
market [19].
If a regulating power market actor is not delivering the required regulating power
service within 15 minutes of receiving an activation signal from Energinet.dk, then the

to pay for the imbalances it has caused by paying the regulating power price for the

No public document explains in detail which kinds of non-delivery incidents will lead

Additionally, we assume the following when developing regulating power market
participation strategies in section 6.3,
• The electrolyzer is a price taker in the regulating power market. This means that
it receives the regulating power price set by the marginal unit each hour. Further,
it means that the electrolyzers bids does not affect the overall regulating power
price
• The electrolyzer places regulating power bids at the day-ahead price and is hence
always first in the merit order and will always be activated when there is a need
for regulating power.
• Up- and down-regulating power bids are activated the whole hour if the hour
is in up- or down-direction respectively. In reality, it might be that the bid is
only activated part of the hour, in which case the market player is only payed
proportionally for the amount of energy it delivers. No historical publicly available
data exists for DK1 on how long regulating power bids have been activated within
the hour, which makes this hard to model in any case.
• The electrolyzer is never activated for special regulation. No publicly available
data exists on activation of special regulation in the DK1 regulating power market,
which makes this hard to model in any case.
• The electrolyzer is in a portfolio of units that in total are able to deliver regulating
power bids between 10 and 50 MW.
2.3
Manual Reserve Market
The manual reserve market is set in place to ensure Energinet.dk that there will be
enough available bids in the regulation power market each hour. Energinet.dk announces
the expected need for up and down manual reserve power stated in MW at 9.00 the day
before operation. Bids must be in either up- or down- regulation direction and are given
per hour of operation.
==PAGE:32==

Bids are given in DKK/MW of capacity, and are ordered according to the merit
order principle where the cheapest bids gets called first. All players gets the payment
of the most expensive bid, which is called. A player can submit bids for manual reserve
to Energinet.dk until 9.30 the day before operation. The volume of each bid must be
between 10 and 50 MW. At 10.00 the player will know whether the bid is accepted and
receives an availability payment if it is accepted. If the manual reserve bid is accepted,
the player must submit a bid of of least that volume to the regulating power market no
later than 17.00 the day before operation. Units that cannot deliver 10 MW of regulating
power can be part of a combined bid with a total size of between 10 MW and 50 MW
[20].
The average availability payment in 2014 and 2015 was 0.21 e/MW per hour for
up-regulation and 0.00 e/MW per hour for down-regulation and in 2011 it was 0.92
e/MW for up-regulation and 0.32 e/MW for down-regulation [21], which shows that
competition has increased in this market. Manual reserve market participation will be
considered in section 6.3.5.
2.4
Other Markets
Nord Pool Intraday is a market where players can trade power within the day and correct
their positions until 45 min before the hour of operation. Hence, they can reduce their
portfolio imbalance based on updated production and consumption forecasts and hence
reduce the need to pay for imbalances in the regulating power price for their imbalances.
The financial market is operated by Nasdaq Commodities and is a market that players
can use for price hedging and risk management by e.g. securing the price to pay for
power for a given period [16].
The intraday and financial markets are not considered in this thesis, but both might
be relevant for a more advanced market participation strategy.
2.5
Taxes and Tariffs On Top Of The Energy Price
Taxes and tariffs are added on top of the energy cost.
Tariffs cover payments for
transportation of the power through the grid and taxes in Denmark are in part specifically
directed at supporting renewable energy generation and the rest is directed to the
states general financial budget. Taxes and tariffs are not varying in the current Danish
framework, which means that the energy cost is the only part of the costs influenced
by operations strategy. Therefore, we chose only to focus on energy procurement costs
throughout this thesis.

2.7
Statistical Analysis Of The Data Sets
This section will present the mean, median, inter quartile range and standard deviation
per year of the power day-ahead and regulating power markets. These basic statistical
data are meant to give an overview of the possibilities available from these markets and
the yearly variations in price and arbitrage.
The mean is not important for the electrolyzer operations strategy, since the optimal
operation is only affected by variations in the price. The mean is however determining
for the overall price level of the hydrogen produced and hence it can be determining for
whether or not electrolysis will be competitive with other hydrogen production methods.
The interquartile range (IQR) gives an overview of the market price arbitrage which
can be utilized for dynamic operation.
IQR gives the range between the 25th and
75th percentile of prices and is hence an estimate of how much can be saved in energy
procurement costs by moving consumption from the 4th price-quartile to the 1st price-quartile.
For example, if an electrolyzer has a duty cycle of 0.75 it will ideally cease producing
the 25 pct of most expensive hours, neglecting other factors such as contract design,
available hydrogen storage and electrolyzer degradation from dynamic operation which
we will investigate in this thesis. Hence, different design parameters for the considered
electrolyzer system, will determine how well suited the IQR is for an assessment of
potential savings form dynamic operation.
The standard deviation (Std) gives an
overview of the overall spread in prices. It is affected by large outlier values, which
might be impossible for the applied participation strategy and price forecast to detect
and avoid. Hence, the standard deviation might not be that well suited as the IQR for
assessing the potential savings of an electrolyzer operating dynamically.
Table 2.3 shows the yearly statistical data for the day-ahead market in DK1 from
2010 to 2015. There has been an decreasing trend in the mean day-ahead price from
2010 to 2015 with the 2015 price about half of the 2010 price. The median is close to
the mean for all years which means that prices are quite equally distributed around the
mean. The IQR varies considerably from year to year and based in the IQR, 2014 should
be a relatively poor year for dynamic operation whereas 2011 should be relatively good
with the IQR being double in 2011 compared to 2014. There seems to be no overall
trend in the development of the IQR. If the IQR had shown a decreasing trend like
the mean, it would be of concern for market players considering dynamic operation in
the day-ahead market as we do because of decreasing price arbitrage. The standard
deviation is abnormally high for 2013, which is because the day-ahead price reached
2,000 €/MWh for the hours 10 and 11 on the 6th of June 2013 and 1,901 €/MWh for
hours 8, 9 and 12. No such high prices occurred the other years. Replacing the high price
incident with the same hours prices from the day before lowers the standard deviation
for 2013 to 12.1 €/MWh and the mean to 37.9 €/MWh.

3.2
Contractual Price of Hydrogen Delivery
The electrolyzer operator will negotiate a contract price and terms with the hydrogen
customer. In the context contracts formulated in equations (3.1) and (3.2), the contract
will specify either a fixed hourly delivery, a flexible daily delivery or a combination of
the two for a specified period at the negotiated price. The negotiated price could for
example be,
1. A fixed price for the deliveries specified in contract.
2. A price linked to the day ahead price of electricity plus a premium.
The first contract option would put risks related to future power market prices at the
electrolyzer operator whereas this risk is taken by the customer in the second contract
design. Usually the operator would charge a risk premium for the first contract option
compared to the second option. These are two basic ideas for the contractual price, but
other contract designs could also be thought of. These two contract designs follow the
same principles as most commercial power delivery contracts in Denmark [22]. For the
second contract option the premium is there to cover depreciation and returns on capital
invested plus operational costs.
It is not the scope of this thesis to go deeper into contract design or price negotiation.
We assume that a contract is in place that fits the mathematical formulations in equations
(3.1) and (3.2). Based on this, the goal of this thesis is to minimize the cost of hydrogen
production and hence no revenues will be considered in the modelling.

3.3
Non-Delivery and Non-Delivery Penalty
The contract could in addition to the concepts introduced in lase section come with the
possibility of the operator not delivering the full amount of hydrogen specified in the
contract and instead pay a penalty. This would enable the electrolyzer operator to make
use of regulating power market strategies, where there is a relatively small chance that
the operator will not be able to meet the full contractual amount of hydrogen delivery.
The applied strategy should yield cheaper production costs in expectation including
the penalty which the operator has to pay when it cannot deliver the full amount of
hydrogen.
The penalty could for example be proportional to the real time power price from
the regulating power market or proportional to the daily average power price in the
day ahead market. The concept of a proportional penalty is applied for missed delivery
of primary reserve in [23]. The penalty could also be fixed and negotiated for agreed
periods at a time.
A penalty which follows the real time price could be exploited by the operator in
relation to the flexible delivery contract, since the operator could always state that the
non-delivery incident occurred in the hour with the lowest real time price. Hence, the
operator could achieve a low non-delivery penalty in a day with high average prices but
with one or a few individual hours with low real time prices. Because of this, it might
be more transparent implement a penalty, which is proportional to the daily average
day-ahead market price for this kind of contract. For a fixed hourly delivery contract
the operator cannot exploit a penalty based on the real time price in the same way and
hence it would be more straightforward to implement such a penalty in a fixed contract
only setup.
Some customers might find a fixed penalty for non-delivery more attractive, since
the loss they experience from a missing hydrogen delivery might not be linked to the
electricity markets. The hydrogen customer could instead have a backup contract with
another supplier of hydrogen, which has a fixed relatively high cost for the delivery
of hydrogen, which would then be its cost of non-delivery. It could also be that the
customer has to decrease its production of a certain good which utilize hydrogen as an
input, in which case lost profit would be its cost of non-delivery.

price for the day of non-delivery. βND is the proportionality factor which is multiplied

4.2
Basic principles behind electrolyzers
This section gives an overview of the basic function of the electrolyzer.
Figure 4.2
shows the basic operation principle of a Proton Exchange Membrane Electrolyzer Cell
(PEMEC). A DC power-supply is connected to the cathode and anode of the cell and
a voltage is applied across it. In the scope of this thesis the power source is a grid
connected power electronic converter, which creates a DC current from the AC current
of the power grid. Water (H2O) is supplied at the anode side where it is split into
protons (H+) and electrons (e−),
2H2O →O2 + 4H+ + 4e−
(4.1)
The proton exchange membrane is constructed such that only the protons from
the anode side can pass it, hence preventing oxygen, water and electrons to reach the
cathode through it. The reaction is driven by the voltage difference between the anode
and cathode created by the power source, which drives the electrons to the cathode
where they combine with the protons to form hydrogen (H2),
4H+ + 4e−→2H2
(4.2)
The combined reaction at the cathode and anode is shown in equation (4.3), where
the electrical energy that drives the reaction is also shown [24],
2H2O + Electrical Energy →2H2 + O2
(4.3)
Figure 4.2: Diagram of a Proton Exchange Membrane Electrolyzer Cell.
The electrodes (anode and cathode) has a catalytic layer, which catalyze the splitting
of water into oxygen and protons at the anode side and the formation of hydrogen from

protons on the cathode side. The produced hydrogen gas will usually contain too much
water vapour, since some water diffuses through the electrolyte and membrane and the
protons further drags some water with it to the cathode side. Therefore a drying process
of the produced hydrogen gas is usually needed before the gas can be shipped to the
customer or to a storage.
The electrolyzer needs a supply of feed-water to the anode. As an example, a state

further at the cost or supply of feed-water.
An electrolyzer unit can be designed for combined fuel cell and electrolyzer operation

to rapidly repel hydrogen whereas the fuel cell catalytic layer is best designed to rapidly
draw the hydrogen in [24]. Fuel cells are not within the scope of this thesis and we will
not look further at this possibility.

Proton Exchange Membrane Electrolyzers
Proton Exchange Membrane Electrolyzer Cells (PEMEC) use a proton permeable membrane

The worlds largest current PEMEC installation was delivered by Siemens at 6 MW

for their current PEMEC corresponding to between 4.3 and 4.6 kWh/Nm3 [29].

Solid Oxide Electrolyzers
Solid Oxide Electrolyzer Cells (SOEC) are operating at high temperatures of around
800-1000 °C and can reach higher efficiencies compared to alkaline and PEMEC units.
The conversion ratio is at 3.1 kWh-electricity/Nm3 but an additional amount of 0.55
kWh-heat/Nm3 is needed to make the process function. This heat could come from
combustible fuels (e.g.
natural gas) or electricity.
If electricity is used, the total
conversion ratio adds up to 3.65 kWh-electricity/Nm3.
SOECs can be feed with a
mixture of steam and CO2 to create syngas, which can be used to create artificial
hydrocarbons. The unit needs to heat up before operation, which gives them longer
start-up times than alkaline and PEMEC. The Danish Energy Agency expects the
technology to be commercially mature around 2020 [26].

4.4
Electrolyzer Degradations Model
Degradation of the electrolyzer during operation can be an important factor for its
life-time, which will in turn influence the overall investment profitability. During dynamic
operation, the electrolyzer production set-point is changed and is opposed to steady
state operation, where the electrolyzer operates at a fixed set-point.
The optimal
operation strategy will be affected if dynamic operation of the unit has a relatively
large influence on degradation. In general, the stronger dynamic operation tends to
degrade the electrolyzer the more flat will the optimal operational profile be.
==PAGE:45==

Modern alkaline electrolyzers typically has a lifetime around 50-70,000 hours of
steady state operation, while units has been deigned with lifetimes of more than 100,000
hours. A typical proton exchange membrane electrolyzer has a lifetime below 40,000
hours of steady-state operation, but a well designed unit can reach the same lifetime
as alkaline electrolyzers [33]. Siemens launched a new PEMEC electrolyzer system in
2015, which they state has a life time of more than 80,000 hours. Further, Siemens
states that the electrolyzer does not experience relevant aging from dynamic operation
but as already discussed in section 4.3.2 the electrolyzer is part of a demonstration
project where dynamic operation is one of the main focus points [9]. Published research
in electrolyzer life time under dynamic operation conditions is not available at present
time. The reason is that industry has primarily focused on steady-state operation until
recently.
Research in fuel cell degradation mechanisms is more comprehensive than it is for
electrolyzers. Fuel cells and electrolyzers show some many similarities and a unit can be
designed to operate both in electrolyzer and fuel-cell mode, though the units are usually
specially optimized for one of these two operations modes as discussed in section 4.2.
Therefore we assess that research on fuel cells also has validity for electrolyzers. Testing
of fuel cells for automotive applications under different conditions shows that PEMFC
(proton exchange membrane fuel cell) performance decreases linearly with operation
and that the most significant performance decrease happens under load cycling in a
test with 2000 cycles per day [34]. Current research has only focused on one or a few
degradation phenomena at a time and no model exists which is able to model the full
set of degradation phenomena for fuel cells [35].

4.6
Economic Performance Of Flexible Operation
Some electrical consumption processes has an inherent ability to store intermittent
products and postpone consumption of electrical energy while others do not. If storage
is not an inherent part of the process, the needed investment in storage and production
capacity to provide the flexibility might outweigh the potential savings of behaving
flexibly in the electricity markets [38].
An electrolyzer system designed to operate at a constant output (as the base-case
from section 6.1) is not designed with flexible operation in mind and therefore we assume
that it is designed to operate at close to full production capacity all the time in order
to keep investment costs down. Therefore, the system has limited or no extra capacity
to operate dynamically and hence the operator cannot implement a dynamic operations
strategy without extra investment in electrolyzer and storage capacity. As explained
above, this extra investment could outweigh the potential savings of behaving flexibly
in the electricity markets. This section will develop a methodology for evaluating the
economic benefits of investing in flexibility against the increased investment costs.
It is out of the scope of this thesis to make a full evaluation of the cost of production
of hydrogen from electrolysis and its competitiveness against other production methods
such as reforming of natural gas which is currently the dominant production method
in the industry. Therefore, we only look at the savings in energy procurement costs
from changed operations strategies and assume that the operator has already chosen to
produce hydrogen by electrolysis instead of other available production methods. The
reason for choosing electrolysis could be high hydrogen purity requirement as mentioned
in [2] or because electrolyzers fits into a larger renewable energy plan for Denmark as
envisioned in [11], [12] and [13] and is subsidized as a consequence of this. It could also
be that electrolysis becomes a cheaper option than reforming in the future even without
subsidies.

Robust Optimization Constraints Relaxation
Because the electrolyzer operator now has the non-delivery penalty as an option when
it does not have enough hydrogen in the storage tank, the robust activation constraint
from equation (6.38) which assumes activation of up-regulation (decrease production)
the whole day can be removed. The participation strategy does not have a contractually
agreed method of handling a situation where the electrolyzer produces more hydrogen
than what can be kept in the storage, and therefore the electrolyzer can not produce
more hydrogen than what is room for in the hydrogen storage tank. Therefore, the
robust optimization activation scenario from equation (6.39) which models full activation
of down regulation bids (increased production) the whole day is implemented in this
participation strategy.
6.3.3
Post Day-Ahead Regulating Power Market Strategy
This strategy solves the day ahead dispatch as formulated in chapter 6.2 without considering
the regulation power market. Afterwards, when the day-ahead market has been cleared
and day ahead prices are announced around 13.00 the day before operation, the regulation
power market is considered. This strategy seeks to determine if it is beneficial to bid
into the regulating-power market based on the announced day-ahead prices and the
electrolyzers day-ahead market positions.
The method can have bids ready shortly
after the announcement of day-ahead prices, which is in good time before the regulation
power market deadline of 45 minutes before the hour of operation as explained in section
2.2. This strategy cannot deliver manual reserve bids, since these must be delivered to
Energinet.dk by 09.00 the day before operation as described in section 2.3.
The main day-ahead strategy from section 6.2 is a minimization problem as formulated
in the objective function in equation (6.3) and it seeks to operate the electrolyzer when
day-ahead prices are expected to be relatively low and stop operation when day-ahead
prices are expected to be relatively high. αFlex and αFix are the fractions of the total
electrolyzer capacity pn booked for flexible and fixed hydrogen production respectively
as describes in chapter 3. Together their sum is the electrolyzer average duty cycle αTot.
Neglecting the extended degradations from dynamic operation as explained in chapter
4.4 and possible influence from the hydrogen storage and fixed contract obligations, the
optimum day-ahead participation strategy will run at full electrolyzer load pn, when the
day ahead price λb,DA
t
is below it’s αTot·100-percentile (see figure 6.4) and stop operation
otherwise. This means that the electrolyzer will run in the αTot ·100 pct of hours, where
the day ahead price is lowest and not run in the (1 −αTot) · 100 percent hours when the
day ahead price is highest.
Apart from the influence of dynamic degradations, storage and contracts, forecasting
errors in the day-ahead price forecast results that the electrolyzers day-ahead strategy
sometimes buys energy when the day-ahead price is above the αTot · 100-percentile and
vice versa not buys energy when the day-ahead price is below this percentile.
This
regulation power strategy will try to correct such not-optimal day-ahead market positions
through the regulating power market by placing up-regulation bids (reduce consumption)

t

The mathematical modelling of this participation strategy is designed as a special
case of the co-optimization model from section 6.3.1, where the model is changed from
being stochastic to deterministic by only introducing one scenario k for the forecasting of
regulating power market quantities. Then, the model is feed with the actual regulation
power prices and activations which acts as the perfect forecast for the regulating power
market. Further, the model is feed with actual data from the day-ahead market to also
have a perfect forecast for this market.
This strategy can act as a benchmark for the other regulating power market participation
strategies, since it represents the best possible participation strategy. In this way, this
strategy can be used to asses how much room for improvement there is for a particular
regulating power and day ahead market participation strategy for electrolyzers. One
should note that it is not possible to know with full certainty, the market clearing price
of regulating power or whether the market is balanced, in up- or down-regulation for a
particular hour. Therefore one has to remember that even the most advanced forecast
can not achieve the results of this strategy over a longer period.

More advanced block bid implementations could be thought of, where the blocks are
not equal in size in terms of hours but instead fitted to the average daily profile in the
electricity market. This would probably yield somewhat better results. Based on the
observations in these simulations, we do however not expect this to yield lower unit costs
than modelling the τ Dyn cost directly in the objective function without block-orders.
7.3.3
Fixed Regulating Power Bid Strategy
The fixed regulating power bids strategy is explained in section 6.3.4. The determining
factor for how this strategy works is the chosen fraction of electrolyzer capacity reserved
for up and down regulation bids, γup,fix and γdn,fix. Figure 7.6 is a surface plot of unit
cost of hydrogen, where the fraction reserved for up- and down-regulation are chosen
to be the same for simplicity. This means that γFix = γdn,fix = γup,fix. As expressed
in equation (6.65) the sum of fixed regulating power bids must be below the average
electrolyzer duty cycle, which means that γFix ≤0.2 since the average duty cycle is
αTot = 0.6 as given in appendix B. The y-axis holds a set of τ Dyn values to investigate
the effect of degradations values. The applied price forecast is simple in the day-ahead
market and scenario based in the regulating power market.
Figure 7.6 shows that optimum γFix value is dependent on τ Dyn.
At low τ Dyn,
increasing γFix values leads to decreasing unit costs with unit costs falling from 1.18 €/kg
for γFix = 0.00 to its lowest at 1.13 €/kg for γFix = 0.20. Somewhere between τ Dyn = 0.8
and τ Dyn = 2.0 the unit costs starts to increase for increasing γFix. This is because the
degradations from the stochastic activation of regulating power bids starts to have larger
costs than the revenue from the regulating power market. This shows that this regulating
power strategy is only suited for electrolyzers with relatively low τ Dyn values.
Figure 7.7 plots the operation for one day with γFix = 0.20 and τ Dyn = [0.05, 0.8, 2.0].

regulation activation, which is seen by the purple line. The day-ahead participation with
τ Dyn = 0.05 utilizes the stored hydrogen to be able to avoid morning and evening price
peaks. For τ Dyn = 0.8 the day-ahead participation also reduces its production during
the day, but it goes for a flatter profile to reduce degradation costs. With τ Dyn = 2.0 the
same tendency applies, but with an even flatter profile in the day-ahead market because
of increased degradations costs. The day-ahead bid never goes below 20 kg or above 80

7.3.6
Co-Optimization With Non-Delivery Penalty
The Co-Optimization with Non-Delivery Penalty strategy is explained in section 6.3.2
and section 3.3 explains the reasoning behind non-delivery options in contracts. The
strategy introduces the possibility for the electrolyzer operator not to deliver the full
amount of hydrogen agreed in the contract with the addition that the operator has to
pay a penalty for the amount of hydrogen not delivered. In these simulations, we only
analyze a proportional non-delivery value of βND = 1.5 to simplify the analysis. This
is similar to the penalty used for primary reserve bids in [23] though our case is not
directly comparable. Ultimately, βND would rely on a negotiation with the hydrogen
customer.
Table 7.12 shows the average amount of up- and down-regulation for a simulation
of the whole year 2015 with and without the non-delivery penalty implemented. The
case without non-delivery penalty utilize a standard co-optimization strategy as in the
previous section. Column ”∆Total Cost” gives the percent change in total costs when
implementing the non-delivery penalty including the penalty costs and column ”ND”
gives the percentage of non-delivered hydrogen out of the total contract amount.
The motivation behind introducing the non-delivery penalty is that one of the robust
activation scenarios can be removed as explained in section 6.3.2 thus giving the strategy
the opportunity to place regulation power bids which might risk resulting in the electrolyzer
not being able to meet the entire hydrogen demand. Intuitively, this should lead to a
larger share of up-regulation bids, since these bids result in a decrease in hydrogen
production. Comparing the up- and down-regulation percentages in table 7.12 it is seen
that for τ Dyn ≤0.2 there is an increase in up-regulation between 9.8 and 11.1 percent
point and for down-regulation bids, there is an increase between 7.7 and 8.1 percent
point. The increase in up-regulation is largest as the apparent intuition suggests, but
we have not found an explanation for the large increase in down regulation bids. If more
time were available, it would be interesting to go into more detail with the operations
strategy to find an explanation.
Total operations costs decrease by around 1 pct for all cases except for τ Dyn = 2.0.

7.4.5
Discounting Rate Sensitivity On The Value of Storage
And Electrolyzer Capacity
The discounting rate we use of 4 pct introduced in section 4.6 is the socioeconomic
discounting factor, which might be too low for corporations to use.
Changing the
discounting rate up to 5 pct in the simulation for 2011 with τ Dyn = 0.6 shifts the
optimal investment from αTot = 0.2 and Sn = 16 to αTot = 0.4 and Sn = 12 and surplus
fall from 4.0 pct to 2.3 pct. Because of the compounded interest effect, the revenues that
lie longest in the future is hit harder by the higher discounting rate than the revenues
that lie in the closer future. Therefore, the longer lifetime of the electrolyzer with a 20
pct duty cycle does not represent as high a value for higher discounting rates and an
electrolyzer with a shorter lifetime becomes more profitable.
In [47] a 8 pct discounting rate is used as the required discounting rate for corporations.
If this is used, no investment in flexibility is profitable for the simulation for 2011
with τ Dyn = 0.6. Simulating for 2011 with τ Dyn = 0.05 is however profitable for this
discounting rate with the ASPostDA33 strategy yielding an optimum investment in
αTot = 0.4 and Sn = 12 which is the same configuration as for the 4 pct discounting
rate case. However, the surplus of investment falls from 9.8 pct to 2.9 pct.
Hence, the applied discounting rate is important for this evaluation. Except for this
section we have used the socioeconomic discounting rate of 4 pct which is quoted by
the Danish Energy Agency [48]. One should keep in mind that corporations might want
to use a higher discounting rate, which decreases profitability and makes some of the
analyzed investments in electrolyzer system flexibility unprofitable.

Outlook and Conclusion
In this chapter section 8.1 first gives a conclusion of the thesis followed by a discussion

8.1
Conclusion
The thesis gives and overview of the power markets in Western Denmark with focus on
the possibilities for electrolyzer participation. A flexible and fixed contract design for
hydrogen delivery was introduced and a non-delivery penalty option was added to the
contracts to enable more flexibility in the electrolyzer operations.
The overall technical functioning of electrolyzers are introduced and the three main
technologies alkaline-, proton exchange membrane (PEM), and solid oxide electrolyzers
are reviewed. Either alkaline or PEM electrolyzers are most suited for a commercial
deployment currently.
Dynamic operation has not historically been a focus in the
electrolyzer industry, which makes it very uncertain to estimate its influence on electrolyzer
life-time. We propose a degradations model and degradation costs from dynamic operations
are denoted as τ Dyn given in €/(kg h−1). Because of the large uncertainty, we propose
a fairly large range of possible degradations costs from 0.05 to 8.00 €/(kg h−1). An
industry backed German demonstration project initiated in 2015 will investigate the
possibilities of dynamic operation for PEM electrolyzers [9], which indicates that the
industry is increasing its focus on dynamic operation capabilities of electrolyzers.
Two day-ahead market participation strategies and four regulating power market
strategies are proposed, which are all formulated as mixed integer linear programming
(MILP) problems.
A single hour and a block order strategy is developed for the
day-ahead market. In the regulating power market, a fixed regulating power bid strategy
is tested along with a co-optimization of day-ahead and regulating power markets with
and without a non-delivery penalty. Further, a post day-ahead regulating power market
strategy is formulated, which first optimize the day-ahead market participation and
afterwards the regulating power market including the information of published day-ahead
prices. An out of sample methodology for calculating the operations costs based on the
operations strategies is introduced. All the modelling is implemented in Python using the
Gurobi solver to solve the MILP optimization problems. Through example simulations,
it is verified that all participation strategies and contract designs yield the operation we
expect though a few of the observed operations characteristics are not fully understood.
We show that block-orders are not a good method for decreasing dynamic degradations
of electrolyzers and including the dynamic degradation costs directly in the optimization
model yields better results. The implementation of a non-delivery penalty allows for

It is obvious that the best performing strategy is the post day-ahead regulating

Investment in electrolyzer and storage capacity is identified as essential for the

the electrolyzers life-time dependency on operations. Based on this methodology, the
profitability of investment in electrolyzer storage is assessed based on a medium and high
price arbitrage year for the Western Denmark power markets. For a medium arbitrage
year and degradations at τ Dyn = 0.05, the most profitable configuration is an electrolyzer
with a duty cycle of 40 pct and an 8 hour storage tank, which is 7.6 pct cheaper than
the base case with an electrolyzer at 100 pct duty cycle and 0 hours of storage. If τ Dyn
is increased to 0.60, it is not profitable to invest in electrolyzer and storage capacity
compared to the base case. If τ Dyn is kept at 0.60 in a high arbitrage year, it becomes

is 4.0 pct cheaper than the base case. If τ Dyn is increased to 1.40 in a high arbitrage
year it is not profitable to invest in electrolyzer or storage capacity. Hence, we show
that both future market price arbitrage and dynamic degradation costs are important
when considering electrolyzer operations strategies and the profitability of investing in
electrolyzer system flexibility.
Flexible contracts adds some of the capabilities of a hydrogen storage to the operation
and increasing the amount of flexible contracts can lower the need for storage. However,
contrary to a hydrogen storage, flexible contracts cannot be used for regulating power
provision in our proposed participation strategies.
The focus of this thesis has been to develop operations strategies for electrolyzers
and assess the profitability of dynamic operation. We do not consider the electrolyzers
profitability against other hydrogen production methods. Currently reforming of fossil
fuels make up the primary amount of hydrogen production with electrolyzers accounting
for only 4 pct of the world market, which indicates that electrolyzers are not competitive
with reforming for most applications in today’s market. However, several recent analyses
of the of the future Danish energy system expect that a large share of electrolyzers
which can operate dynamically is an important means to implement a large share of
intermittent renewable power generators in the power grid efficiently.
Considering
the assumptions and shortcomings of the methods in this thesis, we show that it is
possible in the current power markets for a company that is already determined to

8.2
Outlook And Future Work
The proposed participation strategies in this thesis was shown to decrease operation
costs considerately compared to the base case. However, a number of more advanced
and improved approaches would be interesting to consider in future work.
The day-ahead market has several more advanced order types, which we have not
considered in our participation models. It would be interesting to analyze the effect of
using the maximum price option on bids opposed to the price independent orders we are
using in the proposed strategies. The extreme price event that happened during 5 hours
in 2013 increased the average market price that year by 2.9 pct as we saw in section
2.7. Hence, whether the electrolyzer was operating or not in those hours would have
a considerate influence on average hydrogen production costs that year. If the future
day-ahead market starts to experience more extreme price events as this one, it will
be especially important to consider maximum buying prices in the day-ahead market.
Flexible orders can ensure that a part of the electrolyzer power consumption is scheduled
in the cheapest hours. However, they add some uncertainty to the operations strategy,
which is why it is not straight forward to implement. It would be interesting to analyze
their potential in more detail.
Regulating power market bids can be altered until 45 minutes before the hour of
operation. However, the regulating power bids from the Co-Optimization Strategy are
determined together with day-ahead bids before the closure of the day-ahead market and
in the Post Day-Ahead strategy the regulating power bids are determined once after the
day-ahead market has cleared. The better performance of the Post Day-Ahead strategy
indicates that the additional information from the cleared day-ahead market improves
the overall performance. However, none of the proposed strategies take advantage of
the possibility for changing regulating power bids until 45 minutes before the hour
of operation. The stochastic activations of regulating power bids means that hourly
updated information of the hydrogen storage volume based on recent regulating power
bids activations could increase the amount of possible regulating power bids delivered
by the strategy and lower the risk of non-delivery situations. Therefore, in future work
it would be interesting to expand the number of regulating power bid optimization steps
such that the positions in the regulating power market are re-optimized each hour.
The flexible contracts cannot provide flexibility for the provision of regulating power
in its current formulation. This is because the flexible contract has to provide a fixed
amount of hydrogen each day and one cannot be sure how much hydrogen the regulating
power market bids will produce during a day. One possibility is to formulate flexible
contracts that span several days.
In this way, excess or deficit hydrogen from the
regulating power market activations compared to the expected production could be
==PAGE:108==
86
8 Outlook and Conclusion
corrected through energy procurements in the day-ahead the following day or possibly
through changing the expected productions from regulating power bids the following day.
We have not investigated how much flexibility there is available from different hydrogen
customers. If customers that are flexible over several days are very rare, this might not
be applicable in reality. This would be relevant to investigate further in future work.
Natural gas grid injection of hydrogen as is done in the demonstration project from
[9] could open up for another kind of flexible contract design. The natural gas in the grid
can be mixed with up to 5-15 pct of hydrogen by volume without operational difficulties
as described in [10]. In this way the electrolyzer company could be allowed to inject up
to a specific amount of kg of hydrogen to the grid each hour such that the percentage
of hydrogen is kept below the defined limit. This could be an interesting additional
contract design that could allow for more regulating power bids from the electrolyzer
and further contribute with possibilities for dynamic operation in the day-ahead market.
For the regulating power market, we assume that special regulation is never activated
and that regulating power bids are always activated for the duration of a full hour. To
strengthen the analysis of the regulating power market participation strategies it would
be very interesting to make sensitivity analysis on which effects it has on the performance
if certain amounts are activated for special regulation and if some bids are only activated
part of the hour. Special regulation is settled at the price bid into the regulating power
market, which is called ’pay-as-bid’. Considering this, it might not be optimal to provide
bids at the day-ahead price as we do in the current implementation to always be first
in the merit order. Additionally, the electrolyzer could potentially receive more than
one set-point change within the hour, since the regulation power contract states that
the unit should be able to respond within 15 minutes. This would increase the dynamic
degradations from the regulating power bids.
It would be very relevant to attempt
to obtain historical data on special regulation and duration of activation of regulating
power bids to be able to better model the real market effect of these.
The minimum regulating power bid size of 10 MW makes it impossible in the current
market framework to independently set up a smaller electrolyzer system and test the
regulating power strategies before investing on a larger scale. A smaller system can
however still place pooled bids with other units that sum up to 10 MW as described
in section 2.2. Therefore, a smaller electrolyzer system would have to be active in the
regulating power market in cooperation with a market-player that is already established
in this market and delivers regulating power bids on a continuous basis. This established
market player might be willing under certain conditions to make its historic data available
on special regulation and activation duration for regulating power bids, as was discussed
in the former paragraph.
The forecasting methods applied in this thesis are quite simple and only based
on historical market prices.
Compared to a perfect day-ahead forecast, our applied
day-ahead forecast results in 8-10 pct point higher production costs in the day-ahead
market compared to the base case. In [41] it is shown that including wind and load
forecast in the day-ahead forecast can give good results. It would be very interesting
to analyze how much state of the art forecasting can improve the performance of the
day-ahead strategy. Further, we show that elements of our scenario based regulating

power forecast leads to operations strategies that are not optimal. The potential saving
of a perfect regulating power forecast compared to our forecast is around 25 pct point.
The stochastic nature of the regulating power market makes a perfect forecast impossible,
but it would still be relevant to assess better forecasting techniques and their influence
on strategy performance.
The electrolyzers are assumed to be price takers in both the day-ahead and regulating
power markets.
It would be relevant to review this assumption in future work and
assess at what scale of electrolyzer deployment, this assumption loose validity and if
it happens for different scales in the day-ahead and regulating power markets. If the
installed electrolyzer capacity reaches 1000 MW in 2035 as envisioned in [11] this would
make up 27 pct of the average power draw in Denmark in 2015 which was at 3728 MW
[21]. Additionally, other types of units might be acting dynamic as well in the power
markets by this time. At such penetrations, the dynamic units will affect market prices.
It would be relevant to assess how it is necessary to update the participation strategy
under such circumstances.
We show in the thesis that the day-ahead and regulating power market price arbitrage
is very important for the profitability of investment in flexibility for operating dynamically
in the power markets. If simulations are based historical data for the last 6 years, the
difference between a medium and high price arbitrage year is paramount for whether or
not the investment is profitable. Hence, it will be important to analyze expected future
power markets price arbitrage when deciding whether or not to invest in electrolyzer
systems flexibility.
Electrolyzer degradation from dynamic operation is not much described in research
and industry quoted life-time numbers only concern steady-state operations lifetime.
However, there is a beginning interest in dynamic operation in the industry with the
example of a German demonstration project from 2015 of a 6 MW electrolyzer with focus
on power grid balancing capabilities [9]. There is currently no research backed model
for electrolyzer degradation that captures all degradation phenomena in one model [35].
We assume a linear model can be used for modelling the degradation, but since this is
only weakly backed by research, we cannot be sure if it is a good representation of the
actual degradation of the electrolyzer. Based on the assumed linear model we assess
that electrolyzer dynamic degradation costs will probably fall in the range 0.05 to 8.0
€/(kg h−1) and show through the simulations that dynamic market participation is only
profitable for dynamic degradation costs below around 1.4 €/(kg h−1) and if investment
costs are factored in that number falls to around 0.6 €/(kg h−1). For a company seeking
to invest in electrolyzers that operate dynamic in the power markets it will be important
to gain a better understanding of dynamic degradations.
A sensitivity analysis on investment costs were not conducted in thesis. Further,
operations and maintenance costs were neither included when assessing the profitability
of investing in flexibility. It would be relevant to investigate how results are affected by
changes in these costs.
We conclude an optimum electrolyzer duty cycle of 20-40 pct in the analysis of
investment in electrolyzer and storage capacity. In [11], the optimum average duty cycle
is assessed at 50 pct, which is higher. However, there are several reasons why these

Simulation Parameters
The base settings for the parameters in the simulations section are shown in table B.1.

multiple values for longer simulation times.
