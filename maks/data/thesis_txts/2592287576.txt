Summary
This report presents a study conducted to develop a novel detection system of the oil spills
within harbor areas. Research was focused on passive sensors, to overcome power and weight
limitations in order to use them with an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV).
Firstly, we discuss various crude oil properties, which enable its identiﬁcation in the environment.
Based on a review of available hardware, the report proceeds with deﬁnitions of multispectral
and thermal camera payload and cover its operational principles. Since both cameras are found
only partially feasible for the oil detection, both stereo calibration and feature-based methods are
introduced to combine the information gathered by both devices. Since none of the approaches
led to the correct transformation estimate, the camera payload during the rest of the work is
evaluated separately.
With a deﬁned set of experiments, we obtained insight into oil behavior of various thicknesses,
observed from different angles and distances. During later analysis of multispectral data, it is
found that a combination of spectral response in blue, red edge, and near-infrared bands is feasible
for oil detection within the environment. Such ﬁnding leads to the deﬁnition of band rationing
rules, which, with high accuracy, identify the oil.
From the multispectal images in visible and near-infrared region the thickness could not be pre-
cisely estimated. However, the thermal data signiﬁcantly varied based on oil thickness.
Thanks to those ﬁndings, thermal cameras are found practical for oil spill thickness measure-
ments,while multispectal serves for the detection.
Multiple non-linear classiﬁcation algorithms are introduced to assess the quality of classiﬁca-
tion completed by band rationing rules. Where applicable, algorithm performance was further
optimized. The ﬁnal comparison revealed, that the band rationing rules were the best for oil
recognition.

PART 1
Introduction
The evergrowing industry, depending on crude oil, puts mining companies under pressure, which potentially results in
a situation where the oil-producing chain fails, and gathered petroleum starts to spill in the surrounding environment.
Thanks to substantial developments in equipment like cargo ship regulations, the number of accidents in the marine
environment dropped in numbers along with the amount of oil spilled. Recently thanks to remote sensing, both intentional
and accidental disposal of crude oil by ships in open waters are under surveillance. Compared to the open sea conditions,
satellite monitoring of littoral and harbor areas is difﬁcult due to spatial resolution limitations, the same as a large number
of lookalikes. For this reason, these areas are currently relying on staff and coastguard control.
When mitigating any scale of oil pollution, it is essential to act quickly to prevent oil from covering large areas, as toxic
compounds within the oil have a long-lasting effect on both local ﬂora and fauna. Within this work, we focus on the
development of an early warning system for oil spills in harbor areas using UAVs carrying a payload for multispectral
and thermal imagery. We believe that the outcome of this work could help in the automated detection and disposal of oil
pollution. For this reason, we primarily use commercially available hardware so that the camera payload’s acquisition
costs are low.
In the following chapter, we brieﬂy describe the harmful effects of crude oil on the marine environment, and the natural
processes helping to dissolve the spill. As degradation alters properties of the oil, we also describe what effects each
process has on automated oil detection.
Chapter 2 summarizes technology research and monitoring hardware developed over the last forty years suitable for oil
spill detection. Both advantages and challenges for each of the sensor types are part of the discussion as well. At the
end of the chapter, we deﬁne the set of requirements for the camera payload, followed by a discussion of each sensor’s
feasibility under given constraints. In the very end, we describe the payload used throughout the experiments conducted
in this work.
As each imaging system faces manufacturing limitations, Chapter 4 describes the theory behind camera calibration leading
to the estimation of the pinhole camera model used for distortion corrections within the images. To merge information
about the point in space from multiple cameras, we have to determine the transformation between their coordinate systems.
A discussion on both stereo calibration and feature-based approaches, when used to obtain projective transformation
between camera coordinate systems, is also part of this chapter.
Both multispectral and thermal cameras are very distinct from the conventional ones we use every day. For that reason,
we dedicate Chapter 3 to principles of image formation in both kinds of devices, and to their preprocessing, which allows
us to extract extra information from them.
Since, to the author’s knowledge, there is no oil spill data available, captured from drones, the need to acquire a dataset
for further analysis arises. For that reason, the next chapter describes practical tests conducted throughout the research
and purpose behind them.

1.1
Oil spill effects on environment
Throughout the history of research on oil spills detection, remote sensing instruments detected several natural leaks.
Thanks to the information gathered based on a further in-situ examination, the number of natural degradable processes,
changing oil properties, were discovered (see subsection 1.1.1). Thanks to the existence of these processes we understand
that oil, leaking in relatively low quantities has a minor impact on the local environment as its majority degrade quickly.
In the opposite situation, when an "unnatural" oil spill occurs, it initially spreads on the sea surface into a layer which,
thanks to oil higher viscosity, dumps small sea capillary waves. These areas, when close to shore, attract birds, whose
feather insulation is impaired once in contact with oil. Birds then die due to hypothermia or due to the inability to move.
The same harmful effect has leaked oil on other animals such as the sea otter, which fur also loses insulation properties.
During later stages of natural oil degradation, local sea conditions can cause the oil to start forming small droplets that
sink and create a water-oil emulsion. According to [13], oil droplets act as ﬂypaper for small animals, which get trapped
within the oil and die. Larger animals either die from ingesting the oil while eating the small organisms or from inhalation
of oil, which gets stuck on their gill membranes and leads to suffocation.
While ﬁsh naturally tend to avoid areas with high oil concentration, ﬁshes in nurseries have no such option. Oil generally
has higher adverse effects on eggs and larvae of ﬁsh, while according to [26] adult ﬁsh can experience reduced growth,
enlarged livers, changes in heart and respiration rates, ﬁn erosion, and reproduction impairment.
While the record of animals endangered by the unnatural spill is much longer, it is evident that cleaning the area soon
after the spill occurs mitigates most of the adverse effects on the environment.

While for degradative processes affecting the oil through the early stages, we can see a signiﬁcant impact on degradation,
but in short duration, degradative processes in later stages can run for years.
As oil properties change during the degradation, its contrast to water decreases within the whole electromagnetic spectrum,
lowering the probability of detection.
Furthermore, evaporation, dissolution, and sedimentation processes directly prevent the collection of oil from the surface
with skimmers and ﬂoating booms, which are currently the most adopted techniques for spill extraction except in situ
burning. In the following subsections, we brieﬂy describe each of the OWP along with their effect on oil detectability.
1.1.2
Spreading
The spreading process starts immediately after the oil gets into contact with water. The rate at which spreading occurs
depends on the viscosity of the oil as well as on the surrounding environmental conditions, including ambient temperature,
waves, water currents, or wind. Commonly, low viscous oils such as engine oil tend to spread faster than high viscous
ones, such as crude.
Thanks to spreading and consequent thinning of the spilled oil layer, both radiated energy in the thermal region and
generated contrast between slick and clear water in the visible spectrum, drop.

1.2
Evaporation
Evaporation occurs within a few hours after the spill occurs, where lighter and more volatile oils discharge to the atmo-
sphere. Due to the evaporation, both density and viscosity increase. Expansion of oil slick over the sea surface inﬂuences
the evaporation rate, which tends to advance faster with a larger surface area. Another factor inﬂuencing the volume
fraction of oil evaporated is the wind speed as the oil vapors cannot accumulate. The positive fact about evaporation is
that spills of light oils like gasoline or diesel tend to disappear by themselves.
1.3
Dispersion
Turbulent sea conditions cause oil slick to break up into the fragments of varying sizes. Smaller fragments usually suspend
within the water, while larger ones return to the surface, where they either reform an oil slick or spread into a thin ﬁlm.
Dispersion is somewhat of a supportive process for other weathering processes as it increases the contact area between
the oil and surrounding environment.
1.4
Emulsiﬁcation
Emulsiﬁcation is a process combining two compounds, where one suspends in the other, resulting in a product called
emulsion. Due to the sea waves, oil entrains seawater resulting in oil volume expansion. The resultant water-oil mixture,
also called mousse, is highly viscous, which causes slower degradation through other weathering processes.
Unfortunately, emulsiﬁcation decreases the detectability of oil within the thermal region. The observed reduction in
the thermal difference between water and oil is due to the increase in emulsion thermal conductivity. Furthermore,
emulsiﬁcation changes color in the visible spectrum, where color ranges from light orange of slightly emulsiﬁed oil up to
dark brown for heavily emulsiﬁed one. To illustrate the effect, we include images from training materials for open water
oil identiﬁcation within ﬁgure 1.3.
Figure 1.3: Thick dark vs. emulsiﬁed oil [25]
1.5
Dissolution
Light aromatic hydrocarbons, contained within light oils, are soluble within the water. As they dissolve, they increase
seawater toxicity. Fortunately, light aromatic hydrocarbons are also rapidly evaporating compounds, so the majority
evaporates before the dissolution process starts. For this reason, dissolution is considered a minor OWP.

1.7
Sedimentation
Sedimentation is a process in which suspended sediment, and organic matter adhere to both submerged and ﬂoating
weathered oil, increasing its density. Weathered oil is crucial for sedimentation, as lighter compounds are already either
evaporated or dissolved within the water column, and the density of oil is closer to the density of water. Its subsequent
small change causes the residue to sink to the seabed.
The two most frequent cases of forming the residues are either liquidation of massive oil spills by in situ burning or when
the spill reaches the shoreline. In such a case, there is a high risk of oil getting mixed with the sediments. Washed off
mixture is again sufﬁciently dense to sink.
1.8
Biodegradation
Based on the level of nutrients (phosphorus and nitrogen), water contains a variety of microorganisms, which can partially
or fully dissolve the crude oil into water-soluble components, or ultimately to carbon dioxide and water. Another factor
inﬂuencing the number of microorganisms in the water is latitude. That makes spills within arctic conditions an even
more signiﬁcant problem.
Each group of microorganisms targets different compounds contained within the crude-oil, where some are hard to dis-
solve. The level of oxygen, the same as water temperature inﬂuence the rate at which the biodegradation occurs. As oil
does not contain oxygen within itself, biodegradation occurs only on the contact areas between water and oil. Dispersion
and spreading processes increase the speed of the biodegradation process by expanding the contact area.

PART 2
Oil detection techniques
With an increasing number of oil spills during the last decade of the 20th century, extensive effort and resources were
allocated to develop new techniques and sensors viable for the detection of the oil spills within the marine environment.
Newly developed hardware also brought the scientiﬁc community’s interest to evaluate sensors operating in range from
ultraviolet until microwave spectra on the feasibility to perform a large scale oil spill detection from space. The current
chapter provides an overview of available techniques in each spectrum along with discussion regarding their performance.
2.1
Ultraviolet spectrum - laser ﬂuorescence oil slick remote sensing
The ﬁrst technique described is UV laser-induced ﬂuorescence. Its foundation lies in the use of UV laser to excite
petroleum aromatic compounds within the oil. During their discharge, they emit light in the visible spectrum, which is
subsequently detected by the sensor. According to [9] and [11], Laser-induced ﬂuorescence (LIF) recognize the spectral
unique response of oil, making it ideal for oil spill detection. Spectral signature same as response ﬂuorescence inten-
sity varies depending on different oil properties, allowing the categorization of distinct types of oils. Conveniently, other
naturally ﬂuorescing substances, such as chlorophyll, ﬂuoresce at an adequately distinctive wavelength to avoid ambiguity.

2.2
Visible remote sensing
The majority of techniques today detect oil in a visible spectrum by changes in the surface reﬂectance of the water.
According to [8], oil has no speciﬁc spectral information in the visible spectrum that deviates from the water upon which
it ﬂoats.
Oil appearance within the visible spectrum is highly dependent on slick thickness, as described within the table 2.2, and
the level of degradation.
Figure 2.2: Oil appearance and thickness[16]
When taking camera images in directions toward the sun, cameras, sensing in the visible spectrum, suffer from sun glints,
resulting in possible false oil detection. Depending on hardware used, techniques to reduce the effect of sun glitter are
described in [32], [21] or [1]. As suggested in [8], the use of polarized lenses, along with setting a camera under the
Brewster angle for water (53 ◦), increases detection capabilities of cameras.
2.3
Near-infrared spectrum
The near-infrared region ranges from 750 to 1400 nm. Until today, most techniques use a conjunction of visible and
NIR by using either a multispectral or hyperspectral camera, focusing on reﬂectance differences of both regions. Works,
such as [5], suggest the use of spectral features generated by carbon-hydrogen bonds within the oil in NIR and SWIR
to distinguish its thickness. The same paper proposes implementing the three-point-mapping along with a fuzzy logic
method for the thickness measurements, using oil absorption features centered at 1.2, 1.7, and 2.3 µm.
2.4
Infrared remote sensing
From the conservation of energy, we know that solar radiation incident on the surface splits into three fractions, where one
portion bounces off and is reradiated to space, second transmits through the object and the absorbed radiation is reradiated
back as thermal energy within the region between 8-14 µm. As mentioned within [30], the oil to water contrast varies
within the thermal region due to solar differential heating. General cases are:
• Thick oil layer - oil appears warmer than the water
• Intermediate oil layer - oil appears colder than the water
• Thin-oil ﬁlm - oil spill is not detectable.

While the exact mathematical model for the oil thickness-dependent appearance is still not available and thermal oil
spill detection is a region of active development, multiple scientiﬁc papers agreed on the transition between positive and
negative contrast within thickness region 50 - 150 µm. Minimal detection thickness ranges between 10 and 70 µm based
on lighting conditions during the test.
As thermal sensing is also applicable during the night, it naturally brought the vast interest of the research community.
Thanks to further studies made, oil appearing warmer during the day appears colder in the night, which supports the claim
made in [30]. Multiple studies try to develop a mathematical model, fully describing oil behavior. For work concerning
the mathematical model development of oil-water contrast, we refer the reader to [29].
2.5
Microwave spectrum
The microwave region is currently employed the most for satellite oil spill remote sensing thanks to its independence on
weather conditions and little atmospheric attenuation. In the microwave region, we distinguish between active and passive
sensors based on the source of radiation to "illuminate" sensed scene.
2.5.1
Passive microwave sensor
Passive microwave sensors, also called radiometers, use the natural radiation coming from deep space. To detect oil,
it identiﬁes a difference in microwave emissivity between oil and water. Thanks to variations in the strength of the
incoming signal, they can potentially distinguish different oil thicknesses. Due to poor spatial resolutions, radiometers do
not receive much attention nowadays.
2.5.2
Radars
Active sensors in the microwave region are called radars. Thanks to the development of advanced techniques such as
Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) or Side Looking Airborne Radar (SLAR), radars feature high spatial resolution when
operated from a moving platform. When the radar beam hits the surface of the water, the returning signal contains
additional noise generated by capillary waves. The noise is called a sea clutter, and in most radar applications, it is ﬁltered
out during post-processing to improve the quality. Oil, thanks to high viscosity, attenuates capillary waves, removing the
sea clutter in an affected area. The oil then appears as a dark spot within the radar image.
2.5.3
Summary and conclusion
To select sensors used through the rest of this work, we ﬁrst discuss their pros and cons. The essential limiting criteria
are equipment acquisition costs, overall weight, and power consumption. Moreover, our primary concern is to develop
a system that allows a fast and frequent scan of the harbor area and is easy to integrate on the drones. With conditions
deﬁned, affordable passive sensors allowing onboard processing are preferred.
While UV laser-induced ﬂuorescence is phenomenal in the robust detection of different oil types and their thicknesses,
some limitations prevent its use with drones. While the current size, as well as the sensor weight are way above limits for
small drones, there were signiﬁcant decreasing trends in both limitations during the last decade. The use of a new type of
laser promises potential use in the future. Other limitations to be solved are both notably high acquiring cost and sensor
power consumption, which currently requires a dedicated power generator on board of the aircraft.
Compared to ﬂuorosensor, cameras in the visible spectrum are relatively cheap, small in size and low in power consump-
tion. The additional advantage is a high spatial resolution compared to cameras in different electromagnetic spectrum
regions.
Their considerable drawback is suffering from sun glitter, which increases hazards of false detection in speciﬁc ﬂight
directions. The other shortcoming is its heavy dependence on weather conditions, which other types of sensors don’t

have. An additional difﬁculty with detection algorithms for visible cameras is many oil spill lookalikes such as algae or
non-oil slicks.
As NIR spectrum hasn’t been used for long-term oil spill detection, it is naturally appealing to investigate it further. More-
over, thanks to the multispectral camera, it is possible to obtain data from both visible and NIR region for a relatively low
price. The additional advantage is its relatively low power consumption and weight, making its use with drones possible.
Small thermal cameras are sufﬁciently cheap and have low power consumption to be the payload on the drone as well.
They compensate for camera drawbacks in the visible spectrum as they do not suffer from sun glints the same as they can

The combination of reasonable price, low power consumption, small dimensions, and low weight makes the payload

As mentioned earlier, both multispectral and thermal cameras are quite distinct devices from conventional RGB cameras.
For that reason, we dedicate this chapter to the sensing principles of both devices, followed by processing techniques of

3.1
Multispectral imagery principles
Any material warmer than absolute zero emits radiation, wherewith increasing object temperature, the peak in emitted
radiation moves towards shorter wavelengths. In both visible and near-infrared regions, cameras capture a combination
of the object’s emitted and reﬂected solar radiation. The degree of reﬂection depends not only on a material’s structural,
chemical, and physical properties but also on environmental variables such as solar radiation intensity and its angle of
incidence. Furthermore, the material reﬂectance is also dependent on wavelength, where we denote its variation with
wavelength as a spectral signature.
Multispectral cameras are devices that sense radiation within distinct, narrow wavelength bands. To do so, it features
multiple lenses where each of them has applied a band-pass ﬁlter, allowing only a narrow wavelength range of radiation
to get through to the sensing chip. Based on captured intensity and knowledge of the spectral signature of observed
materials, we can extract information such as the presence or absence of oil within the scene and spill spatial properties.
Before any processing, each image produced by imaging instrument is dependent on both camera parameters, such as
gain, spectral sensitivity or the exposure time, and environmental variables mentioned above.

materials. To make the measurements independent from camera parameters, we follow the deﬁnition of a spectral radiance

4.2
Feature-based image alignment
Feature-based image alignment is the process of estimating the mapping of the coordinate systems between a pair of
images. Five projective transformations are deﬁned based on their complexity.
• Translation - the original image transforms into the new one by a shift in x,y coordinates, having parameters tx and
ty to estimate.
• Euclidian - In addition to the translation model, the original image rotates, adding the angle α to estimate. Both
Translation and Euclidian model keeps the sizes of the objects, the same as orthogonality, and parallelity of lines.
• Similarity - adds scale parameter a, increasing the number of parameters to four.
• Afﬁne transformation - adds additional transformation on top of the similarity by skewing the image. Afﬁne trans-
formation keeps parallelity, but orthogonality of lines does not hold.
• Homography - is the most complex transformation, having eight parameters to estimate. Homography only pre-
serves lines.

4.3
Feature-based homography search
As soon as both images are searched for feature points and processed by descriptors, the matching process generates a
set of tentative correspondences. Features for which the real correspondence is either occluded in one of the images, or
not present in FOV of both cameras, can severely affect the quality of the estimated transformation model. The fact that
the best match is not necessarily the correct one, calls for strategies, which can recognize incorrectly matched points and
==PAGE:36==
PART 4. CAMERA CALIBRATION AND IMAGE ALIGNMENT
23
exclude them from parameter estimation to improve the resulting transformation model.
To evaluate the performance of multiple methods, we use OpenCV wrapper function findHomography, which imple-
ments four approaches searching for homography transformation:
• Random Sample Consensus (RANSAC) based robust method
• Progressive Sample Consensus (PROSAC) based robust method
• Sum of least squares
• Least-Median based robust method
In the following subsections, we provide a brief description of the ﬁrst two methods used. The third method uses Sum
of least square (SLS) method, including all matched points with assumption on low number of mismatched points. The
last option is a variation on SLS, where instead of summation, the algorithm computes the median. The method is called
Least median of squares (LMS), and according to [27] it gives reasonable homography estimates for incorrectly matched
data being up to 50% of all matches.
RANSAC
The RANSAC technique uses a minimal set of points s to instantiate model M. The model is then used to determine
subset Cs, which is called consensus set and contains all points, matched in agreement with model M. Such points are
called inliers, and their amount deﬁnes the quality of obtained transformation. Points that are out of the model tolerance
boundaries are considered mismatched and called outliers. These points are removed before the ﬁnal reﬁnement of the
model parameters.
The main purpose of RANSAC algorithm is to ﬁnd subset s, which estimates transformation well and consequently
maximizes the number of inliers. The size of the minimal set used in the homography search is s = 4.
RANSAC algorithm has three key issues to address.
• Tolerance of the model, deﬁning the point to be either inlier or outlier
• Total number of subsets N to try (number of iterations)
• Threshold t detecting correct model for data points
To deﬁne tolerance, RANSAC uses a zero-mean Gaussian noise model with a standard deviation of σ. The model
tolerance is then deﬁned by the probability of inliers lying within the model boundaries d.
The total number of iterations depends on the probability p of having at least one randomly selected subset s which does
not contain the erroneous point. Parameter N is obtained from the expression:
N = log(1 −p)/log(1 −(1 −e)s)
(4.34)
where p is the probability of success, and e is the probability of the sample being outlier. When we further substitute term
(1 −e)s = b, where b is the probability of all points being the inliers, equation 4.34 gets simpliﬁed to:
N = log(1 −p)/log(1 −b)
(4.35)
and we obtain the same equation as in original paper [12]. The last parameter to estimate is threshold t used to signal
when the algorithm found the correct solution. As there is no a priori knowledge about percentage of points being outliers,
the adaptive version of RANSAC was developed to remove need of the threshold t. The Pseudocode of the algorithm is
provided in 1.
The last step of the algorithm is to reﬁne the obtained homography based on information obtained from all the inliers. To
do so, it uses least square method.

Experiments
In total, three tests were conducted throughout this work to acquire data to understand the oil behavior better once it’s
spilled into the water. Since controlled leakage of oil in natural conditions is very costly, just as it takes several months to
prepare, it was necessary to think of a way to downscale the tests.
Since crude oil is of our primary interest, it was used for all tests. As it is both toxic and highly ﬂammable, it was
necessary to ensure that it did not leak into the environment under any circumstances and ecologically dispose of it after
performing the tests.
Mentioned limitations and the effort to generate minimum waste led the conduction of tests using a camera tripod instead
of a drone. Thanks to the smaller distance between the control tank and camera payload, it was possible to reduce the size
and consequently amount of generated waste.
5.1
Experiment objectives deﬁnition
The primary goal of all experiments is to assess the likelihood of oil spill detection with a fusion of information gathered
from the multispectral and thermal camera for various distances and angles. For this reason, each test has assigned its
own objective.
Our primary objective during the initial test is to gather data for evaluating how much of the information we can extract
from each of the sensors. For this reason, we generate samples of various thicknesses and observe how it inﬂuences
generated images. Based on studied literature, we expect to see higher reﬂectance of oil compared to water upon which it
ﬂoats within reﬂectance images.
For thermal data, the oil should have a higher brightness temperature to water as we conduct our tests during the day.
The study described in [23] suggests that both oil and water directional emissivity causes an increase in their brightness
temperature difference. For such reason, we reproduce the test and evaluate which observation angle is best for oil/water
separation.
While during the ﬁrst test, we primarily focus on the oil and water properties separately, within the second test, we focus
more on their separation. On top of that, we do not produce oil slicks of constant thickness to evaluate whether we can
localize areas with higher concentrations.
As we conduct both previous tests with camera payload close to the testing tank, we dedicate the last test to generate
images from a larger distance, in order to simulate the situation, when the camera payload is mounted on the drone.
Due to overall work progress, we conducted all of our experiments during February, when there were very few sunny
days. Despite our efforts to be ﬂexible, we did not manage to do any testing under sunny conditions. We realize that the
obtained data does not reﬂect all possible weather conditions. Due to that obtained results, and subsequently developed
algorithm might fail under different conditions. Table 5.1 contains data about the conditions during each test.

K-nearest neighbours
KNN is a non-parametric supervised learning method based on the distance in feature space as a measure of similarity
between points. The method is suitable both for classiﬁcation and regression problems.
As KNN belongs to supervised learning, the algorithm starts with the assignment of labels to training data. The precon-
dition of the algorithm is a manual selection of parameter K, which speciﬁes the number of nearest points used during
the classiﬁcation. Generally, low values of K, such as K = 1, are more prone to erroneous classiﬁcation due to sparse
points appearing randomly within areas with a high density of points belonging to a different class. With increasing K,
KNN brings more stable results due to the averaging of multiple training points. As the rule of thumb, the performance
of the algorithm is evaluated for a various number of Ks. The one with the least errors is then selected.

6.8.1
Cross validation
Cross-validation is the model validation technique used for analyzing how the classiﬁer’s performance on training sets
reﬂects its capabilities on previously unseen data.
Each round of cross-validation consists of separating the data into complementary sized sets, where one is used for training
the model (training set) and the second to evaluate its performance (validation set). The size of both sets, along with the
number of rounds, are speciﬁc to the cross-validation technique used.
For this work, we selected a stratiﬁed k-fold cross-validation. The algorithm starts by separating the training data into
k equally sized groups (folds), where each fold contains the approximately same proportion of each label class as in
original data. By that, the technique reveals the effect of unequally distributed labels within the dataset on the quality of
classiﬁcation.
In the second step, k-1 folds are used to train the model, while the last one is kept for validation. The validation set
changes each round such that after k repetitions, all training data are used both for training and validation.
Other cross-validation techniques are k-fold, which selects training and validation set randomly, leave p out, and hold out
method.

7.3
Conclusion
To conclude the chapter, we discussed the results obtained through the camera calibration procedure using the Zhang
method. Due to encountered difﬁculties, we evaluated both Matlab and OpenCV implementations and compared their
results concerning the mean reprojection error.
The following step was to perform stereo camera calibration of all devices, so we could warp their images and extract
information from the corresponding pixel in all bands. Proposed methods perform poorly even though the theory of
epipolar geometry does not deﬁne any limitations on the relative position of the cameras, nor their FOV. We believe that
the main reason behind the algorithms poor performance is due to low resolution of the Lepton camera, which prevents
correct localization of the corners within the image. Searching for a corner position with subpixel accuracy further
decreases the quality of the calibration, leading to the poor ﬁnal estimate. Based on the further study we found, that also
other works in the ﬁeld failed to perform intended stereo calibration.
Inspired by the Micasense approach towards obtaining the stereo correspondence using non-calibrated cameras, the rest
of the chapter covers the evaluation of multiple techniques used throughout the pipeline of feature-based homography
search.
In the case of non-calibrated stereo matching, we obtain excellent results for all ﬁve bands of Micasense camera, while
the transformation for Lepton camera, results in distorted images. We believe that the cause of failure, in this case, is a
notable difference in the generated feature description for red edge band and low-resolution thermal images. Resulting
matching then become unpredictable.
For this reason, we are forced to analyze the data from Lepton camera separately from the multispectral one throughout

Concerning the multispectral camera, multiple combinations of feature detecting and homography searching algorithms
could be used based on the use case and hardware limitations of the camera payload. For cases where the detection is

8.2
Oil properties within visible and near-infrared spectrum
According to the majority of studied literature, oil has in NIR and visible region of the electromagnetic spectrum higher
reﬂectance value compared to water on which it ﬂoats. The reﬂectance difference between oil and water depends accord-
ing to [8] on the type of oil, degree of degradation, and weather conditions. Furthermore, according to [10], oil has no
clear spectral signature, which would distinguish it from water or other surroundings. The same study suggests ﬁltering
out that range between 500 - 600 nm as it does not improve oil spill recognition.
Multiple studies agreed that crude oil exhibits larger reﬂectance difference compared to water within the blue band wave-
lengths. The relative difference then decreases towards the NIR band. The studies, described in [20] and [17], compare
reﬂectance of different oil types for various thicknesses. Comparison is also made with water and ice.
Further complications arise with the increasing level of water content within the water-oil emulsion as well as due to
weathering, which both increase the absorption coefﬁcient.
Most of the approaches utilize either satellite data obtained from MODIS, AVIRIS, QuickBird or Landsat 7, or static
experiments. No literature describing attempts to detect port oil spills with an automated drone system is available to the
author’s knowledge. In further subsections, we describe the analysis performed on testing samples and the correspondence
to studied literature.

Conclusion
Within this chapter, we investigated multiple properties of the oil, which gives us a better understanding of its behavior.
By analyzing samples of multiple thicknesses during the initial experiment, we found a set of spectral indexes based on
sample properties, which can be used to isolate the oil within the image. Due to the contradiction to studied literature,
we ﬁrst described the veriﬁcation of obtained reﬂectance images. As most of the literature results are obtained through
satellite remote sensing, we investigated the curve for the atmospheric absorption. As the curve steadily increases towards
the red and further into near-infrared spectra, we believe, it can be the cause of the difference in obtained results.
During the second test, we ﬁrst validated the results obtained through the initial test, followed by an analysis of areas with
a higher concentration of oil. From obtained results, we deduce, that visible and near-infrared region is not feasible for
thickness measurements, since oil appearance also depends on the surface proﬁle of the layer.
During the last test, we validated whether the results of both previous tests hold, with an increase in the distance between
the water tank and camera payload. By applying the set of rules, discovered through the ﬁrst test, we obtained well-
isolated oil within the image. The reﬂectance dependence on thickness stated during the second test does not inﬂuence
the quality of detection even for a ten-time thicker layer of oil, suggesting a notable inﬂuence of the surface proﬁle on the
oil spill detection.
We claim that multispectral oil detection using reﬂectance images is possible in controlled conditions based on conducted
tests and obtained results. Further testing is required to prove the concept in uncontrolled conditions in the presence of
sea waves and sunny weather to recognize their inﬂuence on detection capabilities.

PART 9
Thermal data analysis
According to [8], any layer of oil thicker than 10 µm emits radiation absorbed within the visible spectrum as thermal
energy, within the region between 8 - 14 µm. Due to the lower emissivity in the thermal region, oil without heating
appears colder than water. When sufﬁciently thick, solar-heated oil becomes warmer compared to water, due to lower
speciﬁc heat capacity.
As in case of visible and NIR region, the thermal spectrum was described in multiple papers as partially suitable for oil
spill detection. The main advantage of thermal spill detection is the capability to detect high-density areas within slick,
allowing to target them primarily.
Thermal detection suffers from misclassiﬁcation of seaweed or non-oil slicks during the detection.
The chapter starts with a brief description of used hardware and its feasibility to drone-based oil spill detection. We then
proceed to the analysis of gathered data throughout the experiments. By measuring only the brightness temperature by
Lepton, and the received radiance by Tau 2 camera, there is no information that can distinguish oil from other materials
that emit equal amount of energy towards the cameras. For such reason the thermal cameras are primarily investigated to
localize the thicknesses of the samples.
During the ﬁrst test, we investigate whether the increasing thickness of oil results in higher brightness temperature than
water. Then, we proceed to data analysis in order to examine how well we can localize areas with a higher concentration
of oil.

9.4
Conclusion
In the current chapter, we investigated the capability of uncooled bolometers in distinguishing various thicknesses of the
oil. During the ﬁrst experiment, we investigated the difference in both brightness temperature and received radiance for
samples with constant thickness. We concluded that the manual spreading of the oil results in the creation of an oil-
water mixture, which prevents oil from heating. Such a result have far-reaching consequences for thermal oil detection
in practice, as the combination of turbulent sea conditions, low temperatures, and dense clouds can directly prevent any
information extraction within the thermal spectrum.
Based on results obtained during the second experiment, we conclude that the thermal spectrum is feasible for accessing
oil concentration within the oil slick. The claim is supported by all the thermal images within section 9.3.
Lastly we investigated the difference in data gathered through different hardware. Although both devices are capable of
detecting the oil, we believe that the Tau 2 camera is better suited for oil spill detection due to higher resolution. In detail,
it allows to conduct observation from higher altitudes. Better spatial resolution results in more precise targeting of areas
with high oil concentrations.

From ﬁgure 10.15, it can be seen that the spectral index has both the highest sensitivity and speciﬁcity. Thanks to that it

The second-best performing is the artiﬁcial neural network classiﬁer. While comparison with other methods is made on the
original dataset, we further investigated the impact of dimensionality reduction technique called PCA on the classiﬁcation
accuracy. As discussed within section 10.10, reducing the number of features leads to a decrease in sensitivity of the

Further on, we evaluated each algorithm on its feasibility for real-time oil spill detection, where we veriﬁed that k-nearest
neighbors and SVM algorithms are not feasible for classiﬁcation problems with many observations. The reason behind
their slow execution is that the mentioned methods store the training dataset as model parameters without estimating
any underlying trends. Such approach leads to an enormous number of computations to classify new observations and
consequently slow evaluation of new data. A possible solution is to decrease the resolution of the original training data,
but as other evaluated algorithms perform equally well or even better on full resolution set, they are preferred.

PART 11
Future work
In this chapter, we would like to discuss prospects for the future work, based on insight, obtained through completed
experiments and analysis. We anticipate, that with further improvements this project turn into a real oil-detection system.
Despite our greatest efforts, the stereo correspondence for the used thermal camera could not been obtained. Therefore,
we could not fuse the information gathered by both sensors, even though it was proven to be complementary in this
research. Since one sensor detects spills and the other estimates the oil thickness distribution within them, it will be
possible to target the areas with a high concentration of oil ﬁrst. For this reason, it is essential to obtain a high-quality
stereo correspondence between devices.
Currently, there is a hardware solution available on the market, which combines both of the devices to address this need.
Example of such a camera is Micasense Altum.
Experiments for this study were performed in a controlled environment, which could inﬂuence the quality of results ob-
tained. Since we used the same equipment for all three tests, for future work in controlled conditions it is viable to change
the size and possibly the color of the control tank to evaluate whether its inﬂuence on results is negligible.
It will be necessary to conduct further studies under various weather conditions. The sunny days are of a particular interest
to access the extent to which the solar glint can impair measurements for the multispectral device.
As a next task, we see the veriﬁcation of implemented algorithms in the marine environment with a real drone survey by
camera payload. The test is primarily meant to assess the inﬂuence of sea conditions on algorithm performance. Along
with the primary objective, the test could also be used to evaluate hardware requirements for onboard data processing.
Despite primarily mentioning the harbor areas within this project, we believe that the oil spill detection system’s appli-
cability could also be extended to other coastal and possibly river delta areas. Fast and frequent scanning can provide
authorities with the evidence whether the oil industry works in compliance with the enforced regulations. We believe,
that the application of the described system could prevent large oil spills cased by violation of the regulations, such as in
Niger river delta.

PART 12
Conclusion
To conclude, the goal of this work was to develop a novel oil detecting system based on a combination of passive sensors.
Based on the study of feasible hardware, a combination of a multispectral and a thermal cameras was selected. One of the
major challenges was to combine the information from both devices, since each of them has limitations in oil detection.
Despite the effort, the stereo calibration procedure did not provide a high-quality transformation estimate.
Due to the low resolution of the thermal camera, the checkerboard calibration pattern was not correctly located within
the images. The calibration procedure looks for a detected pattern with subpixel accuracy, resulting in the lower quality
estimation. Therefore, transformation to merge the information from both devices, was not obtained despite further
attempts.
Multiple algorithms, which do not require camera calibration were investigated to overcome those issues. The ECC
algorithm for correlation optimization between the images has failed due to the signiﬁcant differences between thermal
and multispectral images.
The rest of the investigated algorithms uses detected corners within the image as the feature points for alignment. Each
corner is further provided by a unique descriptor, generated based on its surrounding pixels. From obtained results,
it has been shown that descriptors within thermal images do not correspond well to those within the visible and near-
infrared regions. Subsequent homography estimation led to the thermal image distortions. While for multispectral images,
examined approaches generate good quality homography estimates, the thermal camera could not be aligned. For this
reason, the detection capabilities of both cameras were evaluated separately.
Following the calibration, multiple experiments were conducted to obtain the data for further analysis. Based on the
reviewed literature, oil behavior has been studied for various thicknesses and from different angles and distances. To
resemble a real survey in controlled environment, all tests were conducted outside.
The multispectral data analysis showed that crude oil could be distinguished from water based on its higher reﬂectance
within all measured spectral bands. The blue, red edge, and near-infrared bands were selected to deﬁne a set of rules to
distinguish oil from water, based on their trends in relative behavior. Further evaluation proved, that selected set of rules is
also effective in distinguishing oil from other materials commonly present within harbor areas such as concrete or wood.
While being highly effective in distinguishing the oil from the background, multispectral images are not feasible for
the thickness estimation. The reason behind this is that decrease in measured radiance does not only depend on layer
thickness,but also on surface proﬁle of the spill which causes variation in received data. The set of spectral rules proved
to be feasible for surveys conducted with drones, since the increase in distance to 15 meters did not impair the detection
capacity.
Analysis of the thermal data showed, that detection capabilities within the thermal region are inﬂuenced by oil-water
interaction. The mixture of two liquids decreased the detection capabilities, since oil did not develop sufﬁcient thermal
contrast to its surroundings.
Furthermore, oil appearance also depends on weather conditions. Results of the second experiment prove, that when

heated with solar radiation, oil appears warmer compared to surrounding water despite its lower emissivity. Moreover,
the contrast intensity is highly dependent on oil thickness, making the thermal region suitable for the localization of thick
layers of oil within the spill.
Analysis of the thermal data also showed, that the angles close to nadir direction are best suited for oil detection, since
observation under those angles manifests larger contrast between water and oil. In addition to that, images taken from
those angles are less susceptible to reﬂectance,thereby improving distinction between various oil thicknesses.
The last part of the project was the comparison of multiple nonlinear classiﬁers against an analytically deﬁned set of
rules. The rationale behind this was that the set of rules was found only on a small subset of gathered data. Therefore, it
has been hypothesised, that nonlinear classiﬁers could provide better generalization capabilities. The assumption did not
hold, since no classiﬁer performed better than a deﬁned set of rules.
Based on the conducted research, we conclude that the best performing oil detection system combines a multispectral
camera and a thermal imager. While one device conducts the detection, the other identiﬁes areas of high oil concentration.
To further advance the performance, both cameras should observe the scene under angles close to nadir direction.

A.1
Discussion of used multispectral cameras
According to documentation, Tetracam Mini MCA has a USB connector, which, based on the mode of operation, serves
a different purpose. To access the data of CF cards from the connected computer USBMODE parameter has to be set
to DISK. When the mode is switched to CAMERA, the USB port serves to trigger the camera. Unfortunately, there is
no operating mode, which would perform both operations simultaneously.
Additional complexity arises with the way the modes change. Tetracam ﬂags could be changed only manually using
ADC/MCA controller box with the connected monitor over PAL or NTSC. Further problems occur as the driver is only
available for Windows devices.
With that said, the easiest way to retrieve the data is by taking out each of the CF cards individually. On top of that, each
CF card needs a speciﬁc folder structure; otherwise, the camera does not boot up.
Tetracam stores its data in RAW format, requiring PixelWrench software to convert them into commonly used formats
such as TIFF or PNG by hand. The dedicated controller is required to connect the GPS to georeference collected data.
Since the data conversion to reﬂectance creates duplicate information from both cameras, we decide not to analyze the
data from the camera despite losing information from one of the bands.
Micasense camera uses the Wiﬁmodule to establish the wireless communication with the device. With well-deﬁned API,
a computer connected to the access point can among updating the camera settings, also trigger the camera and retrieve
speciﬁc images.
The raw images can be transformed into reﬂectance on ﬂight. Camera parameters, GPS, and irradiance are stored as
image metadata.
