Abstract
This thesis addresses a critical gap in business process management by developing a
dedicated repository for DECLARE process models. While numerous repositories are
available for imperative process models, and a few exist for declarative ones, none cur-
rently allow for the inclusion or management of DECLARE process models. Even the only
existing repository for declarative models exclusively supports DCR graphs, further un-
derscoring this limitation. This gap is particularly significant, as declarative modeling ad-
dresses fundamentally different types of processes compared to imperative approaches
- specifically, workflows with loosely structured and unpredictable execution paths. The
absence of a platform that accommodates DECLARE process models highlights the need
for a comprehensive solution tailored to this modeling language.
Through a comprehensive literature review and analysis of existing repositories, I identi-
fied and evaluated metrics that could be effectively applied to classify DECLARE process
models. The research examined 14 existing process model repositories and analyzed
over 60 academic papers to extract relevant classification criteria. This analysis led to the
identification of 10 metrics specifically tailored to DECLARE models, including structural
properties such as size and density, as well as behavioral classifiers like consistency and
semantic redundancy. Additionally, the analysis involved exploring existing DECLARE
process models. The repository was subsequently seeded with an initial collection of
nine DECLARE process models and 10 metrics to demonstrate its functionality and clas-
sification capabilities.
Upon the repository’s implementation, a domain model and requirements for it were cre-
ated.
The repository implementation incorporates modern web technologies, utilizing
Next.js for both frontend and backend development, MongoDB for data storage, and Ama-
zon Web Services for file storage and certain metric calculations. The platform allows
users to upload, store, browse, and explore details of DECLARE process models while
supporting model reuse and benchmarking capabilities. Each model is automatically an-
alyzed with 10 pre-defined metrics upon being added to the repository and is enriched
with metadata containing model-specific information.
The resulting web-based Declare Repository was evaluated against the previously spec-
ified requirements, and the research questions were addressed as part of the evaluation
process. The repository serves as a foundation for organizing and sharing DECLARE pro-
cess models within academic and professional communities. This work contributes to the
field by providing a dedicated platform for DECLARE process models, potentially encour-
aging the broader adoption of declarative modeling approaches and facilitating knowledge
sharing in this domain.
Keywords: business process management, declarative process models, DECLARE pro-
cess models, process model repository, model classification, process metrics

Introduction
Many businesses, as they grow, begin to examine their processes to eliminate inefficien-
cies and ultimately achieve measured growth and stability. Activities undertaken in this
direction often result in the implementation of methods from the field of Business Process
Management (BPM) [1]. Inherent in BPM is the concept of Process Mining (PM), a col-
lection of techniques used to discover business processes based on process execution
logs, which are event data [2]. Process Mining involves data mining techniques, ranging
from data extraction and visualization to analysis.
A recurring and central focus in discussions on improving company performance is the
concept of a business process - a sequence of activities aimed at achieving a specific or-
ganizational goal. A business process model, in turn, serves as a medium for knowledge
transfer, capturing the states and behavior of a process [3]. To model such processes,
experts commonly rely on two paradigms: imperative and declarative [4]. It is character-
ized by its step-by-step control flow, explicitly defining the sequence of activities from start
to finish. One of the most widely used notations within this paradigm is Business Process
Model and Notation (BPMN) [5]. This approach is particularly suitable for processes with
a rigid structure, where maintaining full control over execution is essential. However, this
level of precision often results in highly complex models, requiring multiple flow paths
to address various scenarios. Consequently, these models can become challenging to
analyze, develop, and maintain. Furthermore, the imperative paradigm limits the user’s
flexibility during process execution, as the predefined flow leaves little room for deviation
or adaptation.
The lesser inclination toward declarative process modeling is due, among other things, to
the lack of an easy-to-use comprehensive toolset and a higher entry threshold due to the
need for knowledge of temporal logics [6]. This approach offers greater freedom com-
pared to the classical one. It is more commonly adopted in the academic community, with
DECLARE being one of the most popular declarative process modeling languages. The
difference between the two approaches is best captured by the idea that in the declarative
approach, anything is possible unless it is explicitly forbidden, whereas in the traditional
approach, everything is forbidden unless it is explicitly allowed [7]. In the rule-based ap-
proach, constraints are imposed on the process, which carve out an area of possible
process flows, resulting in an ambiguous designation of the control flow of the business
process [8]. A declarative process modeling language is therefore more suitable when
a process recorded in the event log is unpredictable, has a high number of different ex-
ceptions, and is loosely structured [9]. Using a declarative approach makes it easier to
balance control by the user with support from the system.
Regardless of the choice of approach to modeling processes and the environment, such
as a company or research academia, as the number of processes increases, it is benefi-
cial to catalog them according to selected criteria. One of the recurring challenges in BPM
and PM is the public availability of model repositories [10]. Such repositories can be used,
for example, to automatically extract best modeling practices, train process modeling sup-
port systems, and test newly emerging discovery, enactment (or simulation), and analysis
techniques (such as monitoring and compliance) [11]. Moreover, the availability of busi-
ness process models in a publicly accessible repository adheres to some open science
principles, as they provide knowledge or benchmark repositories that can be accessed by
anyone.

While various open-access repositories exist for the imperative paradigm due to its wide
adoption in both industry and academia, there is only one repository that supports hetero-

models, enriched with metadata containing model-specific information. This metadata
will range from standard parameters, such as the number of constraints and activities, to
more advanced attributes, such as model contexts.

Background and Research Statement
When it comes to modeling business processes, two main approaches are recognized:
imperative and declarative modeling. The former, often referred to as procedural [12], op-
erational or traditional modeling, is widely regarded as the standard and go-to approach,
dominating both industry and academia. In this paradigm, a process model is essentially
a step-by-step procedure (a directed graph) that defines a clear sequence of tasks to be
followed during execution, making the process highly predictable. For example, placing
an order in a shop can be modeled imperatively, as each step is explicitly defined from
start to finish. The latter paradigm, known as constraint-based [7] or rule-based model-
ing, is less prevalent, primarily due to its less intuitive nature and the limited availability
of comprehensive tools [6]. More commonly explored in academia than in industry, this
approach defines a set of constraints or rules that must be adhered to during process
execution. For instance, treating a patient in a hospital is suited to declarative modeling,
as the actions taken depend on the specific situation and are not easily predictable in
advance.
The long-standing tradition of modeling business processes persists due to its numerous
advantages for both researchers and companies. By analyzing such models, it becomes
possible to identify bottlenecks and areas for improvement. Additionally, a process model
helps in better understanding how to achieve business goals and in leveraging require-
ments for software systems. Beyond these benefits, process models facilitate experi-
mentation with processes and enhance communication among stakeholders by providing
a visual and structured representation of workflows, making complex processes easier to
understand.
As more and more business process models are created, it is worth considering organizing
them into collections. These collections can be shared and accessed by a community, a
company, or even the public. Grouping process models - for example, by structure - is
particularly useful for promoting model reuse. Creating process models from scratch is
a complex, time-consuming, and error-prone task [11]; therefore, redundant efforts can
be avoided by reusing existing models. Additionally, maintaining a single access point for
collections fosters adherence to best practices and process standardization, as individuals
are encouraged to contribute to a resource that benefits everyone.
There are already dedicated platforms available that offer management capabilities be-
yond merely storing and accessing collections of process models; these are known as
repositories.
It is crucial to define the term ”repository” in the context of this thesis.
Repository is a web-based or desktop-based platform that enables users to upload, store,
browse, access, and view the details of process models. The primary goals of a repository
are to support model reuse and facilitate process benchmarking.
Creating repositories of process models offers significant value for both industry and
academia. Unlike collections or datasets of process models, repositories not only ad-
dress the challenges of modeling business processes from scratch but also enable in-
place experimentation with existing processes through extensions or modifications [13,
14]. Additionally, they simplify version control, making it easier to track changes and
manage different iterations of process models [10]. Furthermore, repositories provide an
environment for benchmarking, allowing for the comparison and evaluation of processes
based on specific metrics.

Beyond basic storage, repositories offer advanced functionalities, such as querying, model
management, and classification based on selected criteria. Some repositories also incor-
porate powerful additional features, such as tools for process analysis, making them far
more versatile than simple collections. These capabilities establish repositories as com-
prehensive platforms for managing, analyzing, and improving process models.
2.1
Repositories for Business Processes
To understand the current state-of-the-art in repositories of business process models, it is
important to explore the solutions that have been introduced so far. Repositories of pro-
cess models vary widely in their architecture, structure, and organization of information.
They support storing models in different modeling notations and employ diverse storage
methods, such as databases or file systems. Many repositories go beyond basic storage
by enabling the categorization of process models based on specific classifiers, which is
particularly valuable when combined with advanced querying options to filter or retrieve
models that meet certain criteria [15]. Some repositories come integrated with process
modeling tools, allowing users to create and modify models directly within the platform.
Others include built-in process mining tools for advanced analysis, such as conformance
checking, or even capabilities for executing process models and monitoring their running
instances [16]. Additionally, repositories often provide extended management features,
such as version control, to track changes to models over time. While some reposito-
ries are private and restricted to internal company use, others are publicly accessible,
enabling contributions from the wider community [17]. Moreover, while certain reposito-
ries are actively maintained and updated, others have become inactive or are no longer
accessible.

To better understand the offerings and architectures of existing repositories, as well as
the factors that make a repository a functional platform appealing to potential users, and
to draw inspiration for the functionalities and requirements of my proposed repository, I

• DCR Portal [https://www.dcrgraphs.net/] is a cloud-based platform accessible through
a web browser that serves as a social repository for declarative process models
expressed in DCR Graphs. Users can share, discuss, and collaboratively design
processes through its integrated visual process designer. The portal features com-
prehensive analysis tools, including path and dead-end analyzers, to help users
understand model behavior. Process models are enriched with metadata such as
keywords, classifications, and application domains. Users can import and export
DCR Graphs in XML format for interoperability. The platform provides extensive
documentation, including video tutorials, to support users in learning, which can be
found (https://documentation.dcr.design/).
• RePROSitory was a web-based, extendable repository that was initially open to
the public but is no longer accessible under [https://pros.unicam.it/reprository]. The
repository aimed to facilitate the reuse of models, enable benchmarking within the
BPM community, and validate research proposals. It allowed users to upload, browse,
and filter process models, which were created using the BPMN notation. These
models were classified based on various metrics and metadata, such as the number
of activities, and were stored in a database for efficient management and retrieval.

• ProRepo was a web-based repository that is currently inaccessible via [https://prorep
o.telematik.uni-freiburg.de/]. The repository’s primary purpose was to facilitate the
reuse of models by enabling public users to upload their imperative process mod-
els and event logs. These models and logs were then made available for browsing

tory of Petri net models expressed in PNML format. The primary goal of the reposi-
tory is to benchmark models against predefined criteria as part of the annual contest.
Public users can submit models, but only within a specified submission period. The
repository is web-based and designed to be extendable with new models. These
models are classified based on properties derived from process metadata, such as
the Petri net type, allowing users to freely browse and explore the collection.
• Oryx was a non-proprietary, web-based repository that allowed privileged users to
create, store, and modify process models. It was available at [https://bpt.hpi.uni-
potsdam.de/Oryx/WebHome] but is no longer accessible. The primary purpose of
the repository was to facilitate the reuse of models by providing a centralized plat-
form for managing and sharing process models. The repository included a pro-
cess modeling tool that supported various notations, such as BPMN, Petri nets, and
EPC. It also enabled the import and export of process models. Oryx integrated the
BPMN-Q language, which allowed users to query business process models based
on process metadata. The process models were stored in a relational database.
• APROMORE [https://apromore.com/] is an advanced, non-proprietary, web-based
process model repository and process mining platform that supports multiple pro-
cess modeling notations, including BPMN and EPC. The primary purpose of the
repository is to facilitate the reuse of process models across a company, promoting
consistency and efficiency in process management. Process models are stored in
a relational database. The platform offers comprehensive functionality for process
model management, including import/export capabilities and categorization based
on process metadata. Users with access granted by an admin account can up-
load and browse models within their account. Querying capabilities through filtering
are also employed based on process metadata. Its service-oriented architecture
enables advanced analytics features such as automated process discovery, confor-
mance checking, and performance analysis.
• BP-Suite is a desktop-based tool suite that supports storing process models, pro-
cess instances, and historical information about process instances. The primary
purpose of the repository is to facilitate the reuse of process models, enabling users
to leverage existing models for improved efficiency and consistency. Primarily, it
runs locally and can be extended with business process models by the user of a
given machine. In addition to serving as a repository, BP-Suite also enables the
execution of processes and the monitoring of running instances. Process models
are stored in the BPEL XML format within a relational database. BP-Suite includes
querying capabilities based on business process definitions, running instances, and
logs.

• SBPR (Semantic Business Process Repository) is a non-proprietary repository
designed for storing and managing semantic business process models in notations
such as sBPEL, sBPMN, and sEPC - ontological extensions of BPEL, BPMN, and

• Prosero was a hybrid business process outsourcing system that combined web-

Eclipse-based modeling tools. The primary purpose of the repository was to fa-

platform’s technical capabilities included semantic matching of business processes.
The system utilized GMF meta-modeling for tool development and ran on the Ac-
tiveBPEL4People engine for process execution, providing comprehensive query-

metadata). While the modeling tools were desktop-based (built on Eclipse), the

• BPMN Inspector [https://github.com/PROSLab/BPMN-Inspector] is a web-based
application that allows users to upload a set of models in BPMN format and explore
their features through various analyses. The primary goal of the repository is to
benchmark how models compare to each other and to facilitate the reuse of mod-
els. The stored models can be filtered and browsed based on process metadata.
The repository is currently not publicly available, as it is packaged into a Docker
image that can be run locally. Therefore, the current setup is extendable locally,
non-proprietary, and the models are stored in the file system.
Based on the analysis of the repositories gathered in Table 2.3, I can state that a reposi-
tory aiming to provide a community with the ability to both reuse and benchmark models
must, first and foremost, be extendable and accessible, allowing contributions from any-
one on the internet. Additionally, basing the repository on a database, as demonstrated
in other repositories, facilitates the efficient storage and retrieval of process models. Most
importantly, a key feature identified in many repositories that enhances functionality and
flexibility is the browsing or querying capability, often achieved through filtering based
on process model structure and metadata. This capability makes it possible to capture
specific process models of interest.
It is clear that almost all of the repositories listed in Table 2.3 are dedicated exclusively
to imperative process models and do not support the extension or inclusion of Declare
process models. There is only one platform, DCR Portal, that stores declarative process
models; however, these models are expressed in DCR Graphs, not in Declare. Addi-
tionally, the platform currently does not allow for the extension or inclusion of declarative
models expressed in any notation other than DCR Graphs. This highlights a significant
gap identified during the exploratory literature search: no repositories currently exist for
storing solely Declare process models or for integrating them with other types. Therefore,
the repositories in Table 2.3 will serve as state-of-the-art references for the development
of a repository dedicated specifically to Declare process models.
The very low number of repositories or platforms dedicated to declarative process models,
compared to those for imperative ones, can be partly attributed to the low adoption rate of
declarative process modeling [33], as the imperative approach remains more widely rec-
ognized. This low adoption rate may stem from the limited availability of tools supporting
declarative process models. Introducing a repository for Declare process models could
therefore help raise awareness and encourage broader adoption of declarative modeling.
Among the potential toolsets for Declare models, a repository stands out as particularly
important. This is especially true given the abundance of repositories dedicated to the im-
perative paradigm (which lack support for extending or including Declare process models)
and the existence of only one repository for the declarative paradigm.

A repository exclusively for Declare process models would foster their reuse, benchmark-
ing, and further development, similar to what has occurred for imperative process models
and their widespread adoption. This is of great importance, as declarative process models
address processes of a fundamentally different nature than those modeled using the im-
perative approach. The declarative paradigm is designed for loosely structured business
processes, making it well-suited for scenarios that are unpredictable and involve a high
number of variations and exceptions, such as managing customer support tickets where
tasks like acknowledging, prioritizing, resolving, and reviewing can occur in varying se-
quences depending on the ticket. This approach excels when it is challenging to describe
the behavior of a process comprehensively from start to finish. In contrast, the proce-
dural (imperative) approach is better suited for scenarios where the desired behavior is
highly restricted, requiring a clearly defined step-by-step flow from start to finish, such as
approving employee expense reports, where each step - from submission to supervisor
review, finance approval, and reimbursement - must follow a strict sequence.
In the context of declarative process languages, popular options include DCR Graphs and
DECLARE [34]. It is also worth noting that some literature refers to DECLARE as ConDec,
reflecting its previous name [35]. In this thesis, I will use the terms DECLARE and De-
clare interchangeably to refer to the language used for modeling declarative processes. I
utilize DECLARE as a modeling language in this work because it is widely recognized in
academia and is frequently referenced in the literature within the declarative domain.
2.2
DECLARE
Since the outcome repository developed in this thesis is dedicated to Declare process
models, it is crucial to understand the underlying specification of the language. DECLARE
is a declarative process modeling language used for representing business processes in
a declarative paradigm. Every declarative process model consists of two fundamental
building blocks: a non-empty, finite set of activities A = {a1, a2, . . . , an} and a non-empty,
finite set of constraints C = {C1, C2, . . . , Cn}. Thus, a DECLARE model is denoted as
D = (A, C). The process model D can be represented as a conjunction of constraints
C1, C2, . . . , Cn, that is, D = C1∧C2∧· · ·∧Cn, all of which must be satisfied during process
execution for the model to be considered consistent. A DECLARE model and its properties
can be presented to a user in several ways: through a high level text description of the
model’s purpose and functionality, or via visual representations such as images, text, or
a model exported from a tool - all of which convey the same activities and constraints.
An example of a Declare model could be the process of handling applications, such as
those for loans or insurance. I identify six main activities for this process: make pay-
ment, submit application, review application, process application, create document, and
archive document. The set of possible constraints that can be instantiated for this pro-
cess model is further explored in Section 2.2. Making a payment is a critical step in
many application processes and must occur at least once in the future to validate or ini-
tiate the service, so this is why the constraint Existence needs to be applied to Make
Payment.
Submitting an application is a prerequisite for it to be reviewed, as it en-
sures the availability of the application before assessment, so this is why the constraint
Precedence(Submit Application -> Review Application) is necessary. Reviewing
an application should always lead to further processing to ensure that every reviewed
application advances towards completion, so this is why the constraint Response(Review
Application -> Process Application) is used.

When we have multiple Declare process models, it becomes advantageous to catego-
rize them based on specific qualities. A process model can be described using metrics
- properties that capture distinct traits of the model and assist in its categorization. Met-
rics, interchangeably referred to as classifiers, can be represented as key-value pairs,
where the key denotes the aspect being described (e.g., number of activities or applica-
tion domain), and the value provides the corresponding information, which can be either
a quantitative value (e.g., a calculated numeric result) or a qualitative value, such as a
textual description (e.g., a few words).
Crucial quality of a DECLARE model is an event log, which is utilized during the process
discovery phase of process mining to gain insights into the underlying process. An event
log, denoted as E, is a finite set of traces: E = {e1, e2, . . . , en}, where each trace e is a
sequence of activities from the set A (i.e., e ∈A∗). By analyzing the event log, patterns
and deviations in process behavior can be identified. Additionally, through conformance
checking, the discovered constraints can be compared against the event log to evaluate
whether the model accurately reflects the observed behavior.
In a DECLARE process model, we can identify weakly connected components c, which
can be examined independently as they do not directly influence one another. We denote
Comp(D) as the finite set of weakly connected components in D, where Comp(D) =
{c1, c2, . . . ,n }. Furthermore, Ccn is defined as the finite set of constraints in a weakly
connected component cn, and Acn as the finite set of activities in cn.

Constraint Templates
A constraint template is a predefined rule pattern that describes how activities in a declar-
ative process should or should not relate to each other. In the syntax of LTLf, the following
operators are used in Table 2.4: G, which denotes ”always” (or globally, often represented
by □); F, which denotes ”eventually” (often written as ♢); X, which stands for ”next” (of-
ten represented by ⃝); and U, which stands for ”until” [37]. The most commonly used
constraint templates in DECLARE include Absence, Precedence, Existence, Response,
Succession, and Choice, each of which operates on one or more activities as parame-
ters [38]. Unary constraint templates take a single activity as a parameter, while binary
constraint templates involve two activities. In the scope of this thesis, all the constraint
templates listed in Table 2.4 are considered, as they are supported by the RuM tool [6],
which is used throughout the thesis for process modeling purposes.

2.3
Research Statement
There are numerous repositories dedicated to storing imperative process models, as well
as one for declarative process models. However, none of these repositories support the
inclusion or extension of DECLARE process models. Furthermore, no repositories cur-
rently exist that are specifically designed for storing DECLARE process models. This
highlights a significant gap in research that must be addressed by developing a dedi-
cated repository for DECLARE process models. The low adoption of declarative process
modeling could be mitigated by the availability of more tools supporting the declarative
paradigm. Such repositories are particularly important, as declarative process models
are designed for loosely structured processes, where the imperative approach is not suit-
able.
This thesis aims to address the aforementioned problem by answering the following re-
search questions:
• RQ1: What are the existing classification criteria for process models that can be
found in the literature or that were used in current process repositories/catalogs,
and how do these criteria relate to the purposes of the repositories?
• RQ2: Which classification criteria, including those previously identified and any ad-
ditional ones, can be used to classify DECLARE process models, and what is the
purpose of this classification?

After analyzing the existing repositories of business process models in the previous chap-

Applicability and Relevance of Found Metrics
From all the metrics gathered in Tables 3.3 and 3.4 for imperative process models, I as-
sessed which of them could be mapped to the declarative realm based on their applica-
bility, underlying theories, and relevance.
From Table 3.3, the following metrics - Number of activities, Number of gateways, Con-
sistency, and Application domain - can be seamlessly and directly mapped to DECLARE
process models [36]. This is because the definitions of these metrics in both paradigms
are grounded in analogous concepts, ensuring meaningful outputs and relevance when
evaluating DECLARE models.
The metrics Density, Structural size, Separability, Gateway heterogeneity, Structured-
ness, and Gateway mismatch can also be mapped to the declarative realm, as they are
based on graph theory [88]. However, these metrics require adaptation to account for the
differences between the modeling approaches to produce reasonable and valid outputs.
Additionally, the metrics Cognitive weight, Structural complexity, Process length, Process
volume, Process complexity, Process difficulty, Process feedback, Durfee square met-
ric, and Perfect square metric can also be applied to DECLARE process models. Their
foundations support this feasibility: Cognitive weight is rooted in cognitive load theory,
Structural complexity is based on Shannon’s information entropy, the Durfee square met-
ric derives from Durfee square theory, and the Perfect square metric is informed by g-
index theory [88, 51, 89]. Meanwhile, metrics such as Process length, Process volume,
Process complexity, Process difficulty, and Process feedback are grounded in Halstead
complexity measures [88, 51]. However, these metrics must be adapted to align with the
declarative modeling paradigm and produce meaningful results.
Finally, the remaining metrics from Table 3.3 - Cyclicity, Concurrency, Diameter, Paral-
lelism, Connectivity level between activities, Control-flow complexity, Process flow com-
plexity, Relative control-flow complexity, Parallel control-flow complexity, Connectivity co-
efficient, Coefficient of network complexity, Activity coupling, Cross-connectivity metric,
Imported coupling of a process metric, Exported coupling of a process metric, Maximum
nesting depth, Coupling metric, Weighted coupling metric, Mean nesting depth, Sequen-
tiality, Depth, Maximum gateway degree, Extended Cardoso metric, and Extended cyclo-
matic metric - are inherently tied to sequence flow [88, 51, 89, 36]. Since sequence flow
is a fundamental characteristic of imperative modeling approaches, mapping these met-
rics to the declarative realm poses significant challenges, as the explicit representation of
process sequence flow is absent in declarative models.
As for Table 3.4, metrics such as Complexity index, Number of trees, Restriction esti-
mate, and Sequentiality are rooted in graph theory [88]. Similarly, Log-based complexity
is determined by the number of logs, while Anti-pattern complexity is inspired by software
anti-patterns. The Fan-in/Fan-out metric is derived directly from its equivalent in software
systems [89], while Usability complexity is based on the BPA-GOMS model, and Workflow
complexity considers the basic elements of a process model [51]. Cohesion adopts princi-
ples from software engineering. To align these metrics from Table 3.4 with the declarative
paradigm, some adjustments are required. However, the metric Cyclomatic complexity,
which depends on sequence flow, is particularly difficult to adapt to declarative process
models because they do not explicitly define the process sequence flow.

Conceptual Model of the Repository
Before proceeding with the implementation stage of the repository, it is essential to de-
compose and identify the key entities that play crucial roles in the repository ecosystem.
The domain model in Figure 4.3 illustrates eight fundamental entities and their interrela-
tionships, forming the conceptual foundation of the repository architecture and implemen-
tation.
Figure 4.3: Domain model of the repository
The goal is for a DECLARE model to be stored in the repository in several representations:
text, image, a model exported from a modeling tool such as RuM [33] with the .decl ex-
tension, and as an automaton. This approach enables users to share different resources
representing the same model, while also leaving room for incorporating process knowl-
edge not tied to any specific notation. Additionally, it facilitates the easy integration of
other file types that represent a model.
Each DECLARE model can have various metrics assigned to it, serving as both quan-
titative and qualitative measures of the model’s properties. The metrics are defined by
specific formulas that describe their computation methods. While it is ideal for every met-
ric to have an associated executable algorithm to produce results for a given model, the
implementation will focus on providing dedicated computation algorithms only for the 10
predefined metrics (as outlined in Section 4.2). The relationship between models, their
representations, and metrics forms a cohesive structure that allows models to maintain
multiple representations simultaneously while being evaluated through different metrics.

Architecture
The diagram shown in Figure 5.1 presents the architecture of the repository, illustrating

providing API for communication, while the frontend focuses on the client side, rendering

The repository, hereinafter referred to as the Declare Repository, is a web-based ap-

repository is accessible at [https://declare-repo.vercel.app/repo]. The entire codebase is

Security
Maintaining security was also highlighted as a critical aspect of the repository. To pro-
tect sensitive data, the application uses environment variables that are securely stored
and hidden within the hosting platform, ensuring they are not exposed in the application’s
codebase. Environment variables are all connection links and credentials used by compo-
nents to communicate with each other in the system. By keeping these variables hidden,
the repository remains secure.
In the current version of the repository, when a user adds a new metric, providing a for-
mula for calculating the metric is optional. On the metric submission form, the user is
prompted to input the formula in TeX format, which can include plain text or more com-
plex mathematical expressions. The repository’s user interface leverages the react-katex
library, specifically designed to interpret and accurately render TeX expressions in a web
browser. Requiring TeX format for formula input enhances security by preventing users
from injecting third-party executable code, thereby protecting the integrity of the repository
and its data.
Additionally, user authentication is required to add new resources to the repository, and
authorization is enforced to restrict access to specific actions like editing or deleting re-
sources. This ensures that only authorized users can perform sensitive operations, both
on the server and client sides, with all REST API routes protected against unauthorized
access. To further enhance security, user passwords are stored in a hashed format, en-
suring they cannot be directly accessed or retrieved, even in the unlikely event of a data
breach. Hashing adds an additional layer of protection by making passwords unreadable
and resistant to reverse engineering. Moreover, the repository does not store any sensi-
tive user data, reducing the risk of privacy violations and minimizing the potential impact
of security incidents. The repository also employs role-based access control (RBAC) to
manage permissions effectively. Users are assigned roles such as user, admin, or mod-
erator, with each role granting specific levels of access and control within the system. This
layered approach to security helps safeguard the repository from unauthorized use and
ensures a secure, well-structured environment for managing resources. By adhering to
these practices, the repository upholds strong security standards while maintaining user
trust and data integrity.
Database
MongoDB is a NoSQL database chosen for this project. The primary reason for selecting
MongoDB is its flexibility in handling data of varying types, which is essential for storing
metric results with different formats. Some metrics are represented as JSON objects, oth-
ers as text, and some as numeric values. A traditional relational database would require
a rigid schema and might not handle such diverse data types as efficiently. Another key
factor is the seamless integration between MongoDB and Vercel, the platform hosting the
repository. This integration simplifies deployment, maintenance, and scaling of the appli-
cation while ensuring compatibility within the ecosystem. The combination of MongoDB’s
schema-less nature and its compatibility with Vercel makes it a natural fit for the reposi-
tory’s needs, especially given the dynamic and heterogeneous nature of the data being
stored.
The repository database is hosted on MongoDB Atlas free tier (M0), which provides a
fixed storage limit of 512MB and supports up to 100 CRUD operations per second. The
database capacity can be upgraded in the future to accommodate increased storage
needs as the user base grows.

For the metrics Semantical Redundancy and Consistency, it is important to note that
for a given DECLARE process model, they are not always assigned as either a list with
redundant constraints or a boolean value. This depends on the number of activities,
constraints, and connected components the model has, as the underlying deterministic
finite state automaton (DFA) becomes more complex, requiring more time for the hosted
Python script to compute and return results for these two metrics to the application server.
In such cases, the value for these metrics is assigned as Computation timed out. This
is partly related to a limitation discussed in Section 6.2, where the hosting platform, in its
free version, limits server request processing time to 60 seconds, thus enforcing a limited
time for metric calculations of less than 60 seconds.
As can be seen in Table 5.2, the values for Semantical Redundancy and Consistency are
stored in the database as JSON objects. For Semantical Redundancy, the object stores
the following properties: message (status message), result (list of redundant constraints),
redundantCount (number of redundant constraints), whereas for Consistency, it stores
success (calculation state code), message (calculation result message), and satisfiable
(whether a model is consistent: true or false). This can also be derived from the files
located under /utils/server/metrics.js and /utils/server/declare4py.js. I chose
this approach to introduce more flexibility and handle cases such as the aforementioned
Computation timed out or issues with communication with the EC2 instance.

6.2
Discussion
While the repository has provided a solid foundation for storing and classifying declara-
tive process models, there are several limitations to the current solution that should be
addressed in future work.
One of the primary limitations is that metrics for newly added models are not automatically
computed unless they are part of the initial set of 10 metrics seeded in the repository,
as shown in Table 3.8. However, for any new metrics introduced by users, a user can
specify how to compute a given metric, but it only serves an informative role for other
users to read and does not lead to automatic calculation. This limitation was mentioned
in the partial fulfillment of the repository requirements and needs further development.
Specifically, the automatic assignment of newly added metrics to stored models, as well
as their automatic calculation, could be incorporated to improve the user experience and
streamline the process.
Another limitation stems from the fact that certain metrics for declarative process models
require an event log to be computed. Examples of such metrics are Interest Factor, Con-
fidence, and Support, which were identified in Table 3.8. Currently, the repository does
not support the direct incorporation of event logs. Future improvements could include the
ability to upload event logs alongside process models, enabling the inclusion of additional
metrics and their automatic calculation directly within the repository.
Moreover, it is important to note that the repository has not yet undergone a full evaluation
in a real-world setting. While the system has been developed and tested in a controlled
environment, further user feedback is necessary to understand how practitioners will re-
ceive the repository. Specifically, the ease of model and metric addition, as well as the
overall usability of the system, need to be assessed. User testing in practical scenarios
would provide valuable insights into any improvements that could be made to the interface
and functionality of the repository.
The application was developed using Next.js, which allowed for efficient and scalable
web-based interactions. However, it also relies on external libraries dedicated to declar-
ative process models, many of which are being developed in Python. This created a
need to integrate a virtual machine to host the Python script required for the automatic
calculation of the Semantical Redundancy and Consistency metrics. This integration al-
lowed me to take advantage of well-established algorithms developed in Python, without
having to implement the complex logic myself. While this solution worked well for the
current implementation, it also introduced additional complexity in terms of infrastructure
and compatibility between the different technologies used.
As mentioned in the previous chapter, I am using the Vercel hosting platform with its free
Hobby plan to host the web application. The main limitation of this plan is the restriction on
server-side request processing time, which is capped at 60 seconds. Certain DECLARE
process models require more time to compute specific metrics, particularly Semantical
Redundancy and Consistency, before returning results to the client. As the number of
metrics to be calculated increases, the processing time will also rise. Therefore, upgrading
to the Pro plan in the near future may be necessary to ensure successfully computed
results.

6.3
Future Work
There are several potential directions for extending the repository and enhancing its func-
tionality in the future.
One key area for development is the support for additional declarative process modeling
languages. Currently, the repository focuses on the DECLARE language, but it would be
beneficial to extend support to other declarative languages, such as DCR Graphs and MP-
Declare. This would broaden the repository’s applicability to a wider range of users and
process modeling practices, enabling comparisons across different declarative languages
and fostering model reuse in various contexts.
User feedback gathered through the survey also pointed to some potential improvements
to the existing metrics. For example, one suggestion was to group models based on the
number of activities and constraints, such as creating categories like <10, 10-20, etc.
This could help users to better navigate the repository and find models that meet their
specific complexity requirements. Additionally, it was proposed to incorporate a feature
that identifies whether a sub-trace can be part of the model, which could further assist
users in validating and refining process models.
In addition to these technical improvements, there are opportunities to enhance the so-
cial and collaborative aspects of the repository. Drawing inspiration from platforms like
DCR Portal, it would be valuable to introduce features that foster greater interaction and
collaboration among users. For instance, adding a comments section or an option to rate
each model and metric using a Likert scale (e.g., 1 to 5) could enable users to share
their thoughts, discuss improvements, and provide suggestions to others. Furthermore,
integrating gamification elements could encourage more engagement, such as awards or
badges for achievements like Top Downloaded Model or Top Commentator. This could
motivate users to contribute more actively to the repository, helping to build a stronger
community around the platform.
For managing users of the repository, it would be beneficial to introduce a user manage-
ment panel for administrators, allowing them to manage permissions and monitor user
activity. Additionally, incorporating an email confirmation step during user registration
would enhance security and prevent the inclusion of ghost email addresses in the sys-
tem. Furthermore, providing moderators or administrators with notifications when a new
model or metric is added would help maintain the quality of the repository’s content and
allow for timely reactions to inappropriate models or metrics.
Another important aspect to consider is versioning. Implementing model versioning would
enable users to track the evolution of models over time, from abstract representations to
more concrete models tailored for specific use cases. This would allow users to refine
models iteratively and share different versions of the same model, facilitating the devel-
opment of models that are adaptable to various contexts.
Overall, these proposed improvements would not only enhance the functionality of the
repository but also promote collaboration and make the platform more user-friendly and
adaptable to a broader audience.

7
Conclusions
This thesis addressed a significant gap in the field of declarative process modeling by
developing a dedicated repository for DECLARE process models. Through systematic
research and implementation, the project established a comprehensive platform that en-
ables storing, sharing, and analyzing declarative process models while supporting model
reuse and benchmarking capabilities.
The research began by examining existing repositories for business processes, reveal-
ing that while numerous repositories exist for imperative process models and only one
for declarative process models expressed in DCR Graphs, there was a notable absence
of repositories dedicated to DECLARE models.
This gap was particularly significant
given that declarative modeling addresses fundamentally different types of processes
than imperative approaches, specifically those with loosely structured workflows and un-
predictable execution paths.
Through careful analysis of existing repositories and literature, the research identified 53
metrics for imperative process models and 14 metrics for declarative process models. Af-
ter evaluating their applicability and relevance, 10 metrics were selected and implemented
in the repository, including classifiers such as Size, Application Domain, Consistency, and
Semantical Redundancy. These metrics provide valuable insights into model properties
and enable effective classification and comparison of stored models.
The implemented repository successfully meets most of its defined requirements, offering
features such as secure authentication, role-based access control, and a REST API for ex-
ternal system integration. The platform supports multiple model representations and pro-
vides intuitive interfaces for model and metric management. The repository’s web-based
architecture, built using Next.js and deployed on Vercel, with a database on MongoDB
and computation tasks for metric calculation and file storage on AWS Cloud, ensures
accessibility and scalability while maintaining robust security measures.
While the repository achieves its primary objectives, there remain opportunities for future
enhancement. In particular, the automatic assignment and calculation of newly added
metrics, the incorporation of event logs alongside process models, and the inclusion of
other declarative languages could further expand the platform’s capabilities. Additionally,
adding gamification elements like badges, awards, and comment/review sections would
make the repository more social. Also, a real-world evaluation with practitioners would
provide valuable insights for future improvements.
This work contributes significantly to the field of declarative process modeling by providing
a much-needed platform for model storage, sharing, and analysis of DECLARE process
models. The repository not only addresses the current lack of tools for declarative process
models but also has the potential to encourage broader adoption of declarative modeling
approaches in both academic and industrial settings.

A
Appendix
A.1
Glossary
Business Process A set of activities performed by people or systems in a structured
course in order to accomplish an organization’s goal.
DECLARE Representation A formal representation of a DECLARE model, which may
take the form of text (a .txt file), an image (a .png or .jpg file), an automaton (a
file with a .dot extension), or a model exported from a modeling tool in a .decl file
format.
Metric A classifier that represents and specifies a quantitative or qualitative property or
aspect of a process model. For a given model, it provides a value corresponding to
that property.
Process model A graphical representation of a business process to better understand
and optimize a business process.
Repository A desktop or web-based application that stores digital objects, such as pro-
cess models, with the aim of collecting them in one place and enabling further ma-
nipulation of the stored objects.

Question 3: Your comments on the above metrics (e.g., completeness or
quality):
• Please mind the usage of the term ”metric”. I would abandon it in favor of more
generic terms (quality, characteristic, property...). In particular, the following ones
seem to clash with the idea of a metric, since a metric unit appears not to be defin-
able, do not look quantifiable at the moment, or do not appear to offer a means to a
shared comparison (i.e., it does not look suitable to establish a total order):
– SO1 - Purpose: A qualitative descriptor defining the model’s intended usage
and operational objectives within process management.
– SO2 - Application Domain: A contextual identifier specifying the business or
operational environment where the declarative model is applied.
• For numbers of activities/constraints, it may be worth considering grouping the mod-
els into categories (< 10, 10−20, etc.). Semantical Redundancy would benefit from
some indication of how many constraints are redundant (easiest would be to show
the number of constraints that some well-known pruning algorithm would remove).

• It is of utmost importance to have syntactic and semantic metrics. However, I see an
issue with BH1 (one not so easy to fix). Redundancy of a single constraint cannot
be interpreted at the level of the constraint itself, as this depends on which other
constraints are kept in the specification. Specifically, having two constraints C1 and
C2 with BH1 set to 1 does not necessarily imply that they are both redundant - they
may be redundant only if the other is kept.
• An important point is pondering how much this initiative is tailored to DECLARE core
patterns, and how much it can be seen as a more general repository. I imagine two
possible directions here:
– Accommodating richer constraints with data (with metrics/indicators related to
the types of data, the complexity of conditions, whether conditions are only on
the source/target of constraints or correlate their data as well, etc.)
– Dealing with general LTLf constraints (still with the idea that the whole specifi-
cation is a big conjunction of LTLf formulas, in line with DECLARE). This may
facilitate connections to initiatives in AI (e.g., https://www.syntcomp.org/).
• All the metrics would be exciting to have in a repository. It is complex to say that
some of them would not be useful.
