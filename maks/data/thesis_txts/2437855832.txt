Abstract 
While the green credentials of solar photovoltaic technology are often touted, there is still 
substantial room for improvement in the silicon solar photovoltaic value chain. In particular, 
silicon kerf which is a byproduct of the wafering process represent a loss of nearly half of the 
high purity silicon feed in value chain. Thus recycling and recovery of the kerf presents 
opportunities for waste reduction as well as product development. 
The current study characterizes silicon kerf, as well as investigates two possible applications 
for the kerf; conversion to the engineering material silicon carbide via high temperature 
reaction with carbon monoxide and remelting of the kerf to stock silicon which can be returned 
to the value chain. 
The kerf particles were found to be small platelets, with mean diameter approximately 0.9 μm, 
having an overall metallic impurity level below 1000 ppm and oxygen content under 2% by 
weight, corresponding to an approximate oxide layer thickness of 10 nm on the kerf particles. 
Reaction with carbon monoxide yielded powders with various hues which depended upon 
maximum temperature exposure, and larger particles being present due the agglomeration of 
the kerf at high temperature. Partial conversion of the kerf silicon to silicon carbide and silicon 
oxides was detected. Nanofibres were also found on the exposed powder sample surfaces, 
becoming more prominent with higher temperature exposure. 
The melting of small batches of kerf was carried out successfully at 1500°C, with segregation 
of some carbide and oxide impurities towards the ingot surfaces, and the bulk material of the 
ingot appearing relatively pure. The kerf required relatively little treatment prior to melting, 
although the low density of the powder is likely to present practical handling difficulties at 
larger scales.

1 Introduction 
The rise of solar Photo Voltaic (PV) technology worldwide is not surprising, given the 
abundance of sunlight on the earth’s surface and the push for renewable energy sources. Global 
installed PV capacity, currently in excess of 300 GW, appears to be increasing exponentially 
recently driven by growth in China [1]. 
While much of the current research and development in the PV field is focussed upon second 
and third generation cell technologies, first generation crystalline silicon (c-Si) solar cells 
dominate current installations. First generation solar cells account for more than 90% of market 
share [2]. Considering current installations and future expansions, and a utilisation of 
approximately 5.3 g of polysilicon per watt capacity of solar panel produced [3], this represents 
a significant amount of silicon resources devoted to PV generation. 
While the green credentials of PV technology, based upon zero-emission electricity generation, 
are often lauded there is still much room for improvement in the sustainability of the PV value 
chain. A simplified value chain for the production of PV modules is shown below in Figure 
1.1. 
 
Figure 1.1: Opportunities for recycling in the PV value chain. From [4] – also includes second 
generation module recycling. 
End-of-Life (EoL) wastes appear problematic, representing essentially all of the solar modules 
produced. However, currently the amount of waste that has been produced worldwide is 
==PAGE:7==
 
 
2 
minimal (estimated less than 5000 tonne of PV waste annually) given most installations are 
still in operation and have several years until EoL [5]. 
The other major source of waste occurs during module manufacture. In particular, during wafer 
production from ingots much waste is produced in the cutting process – this waste is known as 
‘kerf’. The loss of silicon from the wafering process (Figure 1.2) is close to 50% of the original 
mass of the ingots [6]. 
 
Figure 1.2: Schematic of wire sawing process for silicon ingots [7]. 
Given global demand for polysilicon was approximately 243 000 tonnes in 2013 [8], this 
represents waste kerf silicon production in excess of 100 000 tonnes annually. Fortunately, the 
purity of the kerf where non-consumable abrasives are used in the wafering process (such as 
diamond wire sawing [9]) is relatively high owing to the initial purity of the ingot. This allows 
easier downstream processing of the kerf waste. 
Potential applications for silicon kerf are expected to be similar to current applications of 
primary MG (99% purity) silicon. This includes use as a precursor to silicon compounds such 
as SiC and Si3N4, as well as silicone polymer. The main benefit of utilising kerf in this way 
would be lower costs in comparison to primary silicon, given that kerf is often valued as a 
waste product. Furthermore the morphology of the kerf, typically micrometre sized silicon 
particles, may also be of benefit in certain synthetic routes. 
Alternatively, it may be possible to further refine the kerf so it can be reused as high purity 
solar silicon feedstock for ingot solidification, essentially returning the kerf to the PV value 
chain.

2 Objectives of the Study 
The main objective of the study was to investigate possible applications for diamond wire 
sawing silicon kerf, in particular: 
• Using kerf as a precursor for silicon carbide product. 
• Reintroducing the kerf to the solar silicon value chain, as solar silicon feed. 
Detailed objectives include: 
1. Wash and characterise the received kerf: 
2. React the silicon kerf with carbon monoxide gas, with the intention of producing SiC 
o Examine the effect of temperature on the above reaction; and 
o Characterise the products of the reaction. 
3. Determine the suitability of the kerf to create silicon ingots: 
o Blend kerf with standard stock feed (polysilicon) if necessary; 
o Examine the effects of oxide etching on silicon kerf (via HF acid) if necessary; 
o Carry out melting of the kerf at 1500°C in an inert atmosphere; and 
o Examine resulting solid, looking for evidence of inclusions. 
Reactions were conducted on a small scale, often involving approximately 10-20 g of reactant 
or product. 
Characterisation methods used include XRD, SEM with EDS, optical microscopy, ICPMS and 
TGA/DTA.

3 Background and Literature Survey 
3.1 Silicon Value Chain 
The value chain for silicon solar modules in depicted in Figure 3.1.  
 
Figure 3.1: Solar PV value chain, showing kerf production and opportunities for recycling. 
The raw materials for the production of silicon include coke and quartz. The quartz is reduced 
to silicon to approximately 98% purity by weight – this is known as MG silicon [10]. For use 
in photovoltaic applications, further purification must take place via the Siemens, Fluidised 
Bed Reactor (FBR) or Elkem processes [11] to produce SG silicon which is 6N, or 99.9999% 
pure in chunk form. 
The crystallisation process serves to produce ingots of the silicon by casting the aforementioned 
silicon chunks. The melting and solidifying process can also be used to further refine the purity 
and properties of the silicon via crystallisation methods including FZ, Czochralski and VGF 
methods [12]. 
Following crystallisation the ingots are cut into the desired block geometry, then these blocks 
are sliced into wafers; it is at this stage which results in kerf waste product which forms the 
basis of this study. 
The latter stages of the PV cycle involve steps to convert the wafers into solar cells, via 
texturing, doping, coating and electrical contact printing. This is followed by assembling arrays 
of cells into modules which also involves encapsulation of the cells as well as structural 
mounting [13].

3.2 The Wafering Process and Kerf Production 
A schematic of the wafering process was shown in Figure 1.2; via abrasion, the wire cuts the 
silicon blocks into several thin slices. As the desired wafer thickness is often in order of the 
width of the wire saw thickness (approximately 100 μm), large material losses are realised 
often approaching 50% [6]. The lost silicon material is known as kerf. 
The cutting media can be either SiC slurry abrasive in organic coolants, or fixed diamond 
particles attached to the wires, often utilising water based coolants [9]. The typical set-up for 
both techniques is shown in Figure 3.2 below.  
 
Figure 3.2: Set-ups for loose abrasive (SiC) slurry (left) and fixed abrasive diamond sawing 
(right) [14]. 
An example of a diamond wire surfaces is shown in Figure 3.3, where micrometre-sized 
diamond grits are either impregnated or electrode plated onto a metal wire. 
 
Figure 3.3: Wire used for diamond wire saw – impregnated diamond grits, covered by nickel 
base alloy coating [9]. 
==PAGE:11==
 
 
6 
While the manufacture of the diamond-incorporated wire entails greater costs than that of the 
wire used in slurry abrasive methods, this is somewhat offset by the savings obtained by 
eliminating the need for slurry consumable. 
As highlighted in Figure 3.4, the removal mechanism of the fixed abrasive saw is different 
from loose slurry methods. Slurry methods rely upon three-body interactions – silicon block, 
abrasive and wire – whereas fixed abrasive methods are primarily two-body interactions (fixed 
abrasive, silicon). 
 
Figure 3.4: Comparison of loose abrasive (left) versus fixed abrasive (right) wafering methods 
[9]. 
The mechanism of material removal in the diamond wire saw process is thought to be brittle 
chip formation, with microcracking occurring in addition to plastic deformation at the top of 
the wafer surface as illustrated in Figure 3.5. 
 
Figure 3.5: Fixed abrasive material removal mechanism – from [15]. 
Both ductile and brittle characteristics are observed (Figure 3.6) when directly viewing 
diamond wire sawed monocrystalline wafer surfaces, with ductile deformation also associated 
with a silicon phase change and wire wear. 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
7 
 
Figure 3.6: Diamond wire sawn silicon surface, showing increased plastic deformation 
(horizontal scoring) and associated phase transformation (via FTIR measurements) 
on wafer surfaces cut by old wire sections (bottom) compared to new wire sections 
(top) [16]. 
Traditionally, the slurry based method has been used to produced silicon wafers from ingots, 
but advances in the understanding of silicon brittle-ductile behaviour and the relationship with 
cutting speed have allowed fixed diamond wire methods to be developed and in some case 
surpass loose abrasive slurry methods in performance [15], [17]. Diamond wire sawing is 
currently the dominant technique for mono c-Si wafers, and is expected to account for more 
than 90% of all mono c-Si wafer production beyond 2019, while for mc-Si wafers 50% market 
share is expected to be achieved prior to 2021 [3]. 
While the wafering process introduces contaminants to the kerf product via the abrasive media 
and lubricant, the fixed diamond method reduces contamination by abrasive particles compared 
to the slurry method. Furthermore the high purity of the initial ingot ensures that the kerf is 
relatively uncontaminated silicon, and has encouraged further studies into kerf recovery and 
potential applications. 
3.3 Kerfless Silicon Technology 
In an attempt to circumvent the issue of kerf waste altogether, alternative methods for 
producing wafers have been researched. These methods typically eliminate the bulk of the 
sawing and associated kerf by high-temperature processing the feed silicon directly into a thin 
cell profile rather than ingots which require many additional cutting steps. 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
8 
The two most mature technologies for the direct production of wafers include Edge-defined 
Film Growth (EFG - Figure 3.7a), where molten silicon is drawn through a die with a narrow 
gap, and String Ribbon (SR - Figure 3.7b) which relies upon the surface tension of molten 
silicon to create a thin film within a frame, with both methods capable of producing cells at a 
commercial volume [18], [19]. 
 
Figure 3.7: Schematics of EFG and SR techniques [18]. 
Cleavage processes rely on the implantation of ions into a silicon ingot, which allows the 
controlled introduction of defects planes along which fracture, or cleavage occurs. This 
provides well-defined wafer fragments from the ingot obviating the need for sawing [19]. 
Layer transfer processes rely on epitaxial growth of the silicon wafers via the use of a low cost 
substrate, often sacrificial. However layer transfer and the aforementioned cleavage process 
are not considered commercial as yet [19]. 
Also of note is 1366 Technologies ‘Direct Wafer’ process [20], which has been in development 
since 2009 [21] where wafers are essentially cast in thin form. In 2017, laboratory efficiencies 
of close to 20% for Direct Wafer solar cells were achieved, which is similar to traditional 
silicon cell technologies. The largest utilisation to date of these cells in operational PV modules 
is in a 500 kW installation in Japan which commenced operation in the second quarter of 2017. 
While a number of the above processes are commercially viable, or may become so over time, 
block sawing remains the dominant process for the creation of silicon solar cells. Until kerfless 
technologies become mainstream, studies on the reuse/recycling of kerf are still warranted.

3.4.1 Solar Silicon Feed and Kerf Purification

3.4.2 High Capacity Li-ion Battery Anode 
Silicon has shown some promise as an anode material for Li ion batteries. Traditionally, 
graphene (carbon) has been used for such anodes. Silicon has a higher gravimetric capacity 
(energy storage per unit weight), however silicon anodes show accelerated mechanical 
degradation through repeated use [42]. 
Silicon kerf particulates offer an attractive source of silicon for anode construction. While 
synthetic nanoparticle silicon is often better suited to anode applications, kerf silicon has 
adequate characteristics and is available for a fraction of the cost. 
Jang [29] has previously utilised abrasive slurry kerf as source material for anode silicon. A 
number of treatment steps including acid treatment, dispersion and spraying (atomising) 
removed the unwanted SiC particles, with the treated kerf product being pure Si and having 
appearance and size distribution as per Figure 3.8 below. 
 
Figure 3.8: Example of battery anode material. Abrasive slurry kerf, after being subject to SiC 
removal efforts [29]. 
They found anodes fabricated from the recovered material had higher capacity than the anodes 
fabricated from commercially available nanoparticles when compared on a weight basis. 
However, the size distribution of the kerf particles and agglomerates prevented optimal packing 
densities from being achieved. 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
13 
Mongstad [31] attempted to utilise diamond wire kerf to fabricate anodes. As diamond wire 
kerf contains little abrasive, the amount of kerf treatment required is reduced. The kerf 
manufactured cells showed similar characteristics to cells made from silicon nanoparticles for 
a moderate number of charge cycles (40-60), although performance was inferior overall. 
3.4.3 Silicon Carbide Precursor 
Silicon carbide has several applications in fields including abrasion, semiconductor and 
structural ceramics [43]. One of the most important applications is in metallurgy, where SiC is 
used for deoxidising steels. In 2006, the global capacity for SiC was estimated at 1.1 million 
tonnes. In 2016, usage was in excess of 1.5 million tonnes and projected to rise to just under 3 
million tonnes in 2021 [44]. 
While downcycling the solar silicon kerf to silicon carbide for abrasives and steel making 
purposes represents a loss in value compared to SG silicon, the purity requirements are not as 
stringent which often translates to lower processing costs. However, there have been limited 
studies of kerf being converted to SiC. Contrarily, most kerf studies have been performed on 
slurry abrasive kerf, where removal of SiC is desired.to produce a high purity Si product [45]. 
Nevertheless, kerf may provide a pathway towards high-value SiC products, particularly as a 
convenient and cheap source of precursor silicon. There has been some interest in the use of 
silicon powder to create Ceramic Matrix Composite (CMC) products. One study utilised 
powder based 3-D printing (P-3DP) techniques to create reaction bonded silicon carbide 
(RBSiC) monolith, where a mixture of silicon, silicon carbide and organic binder was printed 
then processed [46]. NASA has also created Si/SiC monoliths using binder jetting technology 
and a setup similar to that shown in Figure 3.9. 
 
Figure 3.9: Schematic of binder jetting process for Si/SiC CMC [47]. 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
14 
Silicon carbide also has potential applications in ceramic-metal composites. In a recent study 
performed by Deprem [48], cold-sprayed composite coatings consisting of SiC particles 
embedded in Al-Si alloy matrices were found to have good abrasion properties. The silicon 
kerf could potentially serve a dual purpose as low cost precursors to both the alloy and ceramic 
constituents in such a composite. 
Given the project objective to synthesise SiC, production is discussed in further detail in 
Section 3.6. 
3.4.4 Silicon Nitride 
Silicon nitride (Si3N4), like SiC is a covalently bonded ceramic and while not possessing the 
extraordinary strength of SiC, silicon nitride possesses high fracture toughness for a ceramic 
as well as resistance to thermal shock. As a result Si3N4 sees application where high temperature 
environments and cyclic loading conditions are involved. Since the turn of the century, it has 
also attracted interest in the medical field as a biocompatible material [49]. 
Silicon nitride powder is predominantly produced in one of four different ways [50], with the 
form and properties of the silicon nitride powder is dictated by the method used: 
1. Nitriding of silicon powder, essentially a direct reaction: 
3Si + 2N2 → Si3N4 
2. CVD of silicon/chlorine compounds in the presence of ammonia or a suitable mixture 
of hydrogen and nitrogen gas: 
3SiCl4 + 4NH3 → Si3N4 + 12HCl 
3. Carbothermic reduction of silicon oxide in the presence of nitrogen gas: 
3SiO2 + 6C + 2N2 → Si3N4 + 6CO 
4. Reaction of silicon chloride with ammonia. Similar to 2 but reaction progressing via a 
diimide intermediate: 
SiCl4 + 6NH3 → Si(NH)2 + 4NH4Cl 
3Si(NH)2 → Si3N4 + 2NH3 
The first method is particularly relevant and potentially offers a pathway to process silicon kerf 
into silicon nitride product with minimal pre-treatment. This was performed by Xiao [26] with 
slurry kerf in N2 atmosphere at 1500ºC, producing both SiC and Si3N4 as verified by XRD 
measurements. Miyamoto also produced silicon nitride from kerf and high temperature 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
15 
nitridation, although SiAlON ceramics were the predominant compound given the presence of 
alumina in their slurry mixtures [22]. Practically however, the resulting products from nitriding 
would require further treatment, namely comminution to break aggregates down followed by 
leaching to remove impurities. 
Mechanical alloying is a powder processing technique which could be used in a kerf nitridation 
scheme. Energy is supplied via ball milling, which encourages cold welding, fracturing and 
rewelding of solid particles as depicted in Figure 3.10.  
 
Figure 3.10:Schematic of ball-powder-ball collision during mechanical alloying, where plastic 
deformation, welding and fracture all occur [51]. 
Furthermore, the kinetic energy involved in the process would serve to break up kerf and silicon 
nitride agglomerates, leading to a more homogenous final product. 
However, studies on mechanical alloying specifically to produce silicon nitride particles are 
lacking – a ‘proof of concept’ study performed in 1992 by Calka [52] was able to produce -
Si3N4 by ball-milling silicon particles in nitrogen/ammonia atmospheres, albeit with remnant 
Si but there appears to be very few similar or follow-up studies performed in the nearly three 
decades since.

3.4.5 Silicones 
Silicones are a class of polymers, with the molecular motif based upon a Si-O backbone but 
often incorporating organic (C-H) groups. Relative to traditional organic polymers, silicones 
show high resistance to weathering (including oxidation resistance) and temperature, as well 
as stability in numerous media including aqueous solutions and oils. 
The wide range of applications for silicones includes use in paint binders, sealants as well as 
protective and insulative coatings [53]. Recent innovations in silicones include use as a 
precursor for ceramic monoliths, taking advantage of the ease of creating forms from silicone 
then transforming the Si-O network to create silicon-based ceramic structures [54], as well 
utilising the flow properties of silicones in 3-D printing applications [55]. 
Silicone is often produced via a two-stage process, the first stage which involves the reaction 
of silicon powder feed with chloromethane to produce dichlorodimethylsilane is also known as 
the Rochow Process: 
nSi + 2nCH3Cl → n(CH3)2SiCl2 
The second stage is reaction of this silane with methanol, which releases the chloromethane 
(which can be reused) as well as the silicone polymer: 
n(CH3)2SiCl2 + 2nCH3OH → [(CH3)2SiO]n + 2nCH3Cl + nH2O 
Overall, the two stages together give: 
nSi + 2nCH3OH → [(CH3)2SiO]n + nH2O 
The minimum required purity of silicon for the Rochow process is 97% [56], which is similar 
to minimally processed diamond saw kerf. As such, kerf potentially presents a low-cost 
alternative for silicon feedstock. The growing silicone product market is likely to influence 
future demand for silicon kerf.

3.6 Production and Synthesis of Silicon Carbide 
As described in Liethschmidt [43], silicon carbide is typically produced from the reduction of 
quartz raw by carbon, via the following equation: 
SiO2 + 3C → SiC + 2CO 
Overall, the reaction produces the -SiC allotrope and is endothermic (enthalpy of 618.5 
kJ/mol at room temperature). The reaction takes place via an SiO intermediate: 
SiO2 + C → SiO + CO 
The above reaction occurs at temperatures in excess of 1700°C when the SiO2 melts. The 
following consequential reactions take place to yield CO gas and SiC. 
SiO + C → Si + CO 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
19 
Si + C → SiC 
The SiC forms a layer over the Si, which would normally prevent further infiltration of the CO 
into the Si and halt the reaction. However, the surface SiC undergoes reaction with the quartz 
to give gaseous products: 
SiC + 2SiO2 → 3SiO(g) + CO 
SiC + SiO ↔ 2Si(g) + CO 
This allows higher conversion reates of SiO2 to SiC. 
The majority of industrial SiC is produced via the Acheson process which involves the use of 
an electric resistance furnace (Figure 3.13) and graphite electrodes which act as both a carbon 
source and allow the transfer of energy via electricity.  
 
Figure 3.13: 
Schematic of Acheson furnace – from [43]. 
The SiC product forms around the graphite core, resulting in the formation of a crystalline SiC 
cylinder. However, the evolution of CO gas in the process, along with dust and SO2 pollution 
is cause for concern. 
The lack of studies involving conversion of kerf to silicon carbide was previously mentioned. 
However, thermodynamically the formation of SiC from elemental Si and C is favourable 
(standard enthalpy of formation at room temperature is -71.6 kJ/mol [43]) and would appear 
straightforward to achieve. 
Practically, while the direct reaction of silicon and carbon in the form of coke or coal would 
produce the desired SiC product, the slow kinetics of solid-solid reactions would likely require 
high temperatures and/or high levels of particle subdivision (as per Xiao [26] who utilised 
graphite powder) for any meaningful conversion. From this point of view, using carbon 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
20 
monoxide gas as a carbon source rather than solid carbon appears attractive as it facilitates 
lower temperature conversion. 
The reaction between Si and CO gas has previously been investigated [57], with the following 
reactions proposed: 
3Si + 2CO ↔ 2SiC + SiO2 
2Si + CO ↔ SiC + SiO 
The latter reaction was found to predominate at higher temperatures (>1500°C). More recently 
(2011), Antipov [58] has examined this system and reactions as a possible synthetic route for 
SiC nanofibres (Figure 3.14) starting with commercial silicon powder and carbon monoxide.

4 Experimental 
4.1 Cleaning and Etching of Kerf Samples 
To prepare the silicon kerf1 for characterisation and experiments, the kerf was washed using 
analytical grade acetone, with each gram of kerf utilizing approximately three grams of 
acetone. The resulting fluid was subject to ultrasonic treatment for approximately thirty 
minutes to allow thorough mixing. To remove the acetone, the suspensions were settled via 
centrifuge (3300 RPM, 1 hour) whereupon the supernatant acetone was decanted. The 
thickened kerf slurry was then dried via oven at 120°C overnight (12 hrs). 
Some of the recrystallization experiments utilised etched kerf in order to remove remnant oxide 
layer from the kerf particles. To achieve this, the previously washed kerf was etched in HF acid 
(1%, 3 mL of HF solution per gram of kerf), centrifuged (4350 RPM, 1 hour) with the 
supernatant liquid decanted. The resulting thickened slurry was then washed by diluting with 
deionized water and centrifuging (4350 RPM, 1 hour), this process being repeated twice. The 
rinsed slurry was then dried in an oven below 100°C.

6 Discussion 
6.1 Characteristics of the Kerf 
The particle size measurements were consistent with a monomodal distribution with mean and 
median diametre of approximately 0.9 and 0.5 μm respectively. This is similar to previous 
studies of diamond saw kerf (Figure 3.12), where the mean and median diametres are between 
0.1 and 1 μm, also having monomodal distributions which suggest similar source materials and 
processing parameters were used for all studies involved. 
The ICPMS analysis results (Table 5-2) showed the presence of the metallics Fe, Cr and Ni in 
significant amounts (in order of 100 ppm). All other metallic elements were an order of 
magnitude lower. Discounting atmospheric impurities (namely oxygen, carbon, nitrogen and 
sulphur), the overall level suggests the purity of the kerf was approximately 99% by weight 
silicon, which is comparable to MG grade silicon. The impurity levels are not dissimilar to the

had undergone rinsing treatment prior to being received. The minor weight gain may be

to completely mitigate oxidation, particularly at higher temperature.

6.2 Reaction Products of Kerf and CO 
Prior to conducting the experiments, the HSC software suite [64] was used to predict the 
products of chemical reaction. An excess of CO gas was assumed (2:1 ratio of CO to Si), 
examined over the temperature range 25 to 2000ºC, with various Si, C and O compounds 
entered as product possibilities. 
 
Figure 6.2: Results of equilibrium thermodynamic calculations, showing predominance of 
Si/C/O containing species at various temperatures. 
==PAGE:49==

The results of the equilibrium thermodynamic calculations (Figure 5.6) suggests that in excess 
carbon monoxide, silicon would be expected to convert to both the carbide (SiC)  and (SiO2) 
dioxide under equilibrium conditions. This holds for a wide range of temperatures, up to 
1500°C, where the equilibrium begins to shift towards both Si and SiO, as well as CO. 
There are a number of limitations to this approach. In particular: 
• Equilibrium concentrations/pressures are assumed, although in any case the 
thermodynamics suggest SiC and SiO2 are heavily favoured as products. 
• Kinetics may limit the formation of the expected products. 
• Gaseous products (or even intermediate phases) may be formed during reaction – these 
compounds can escape the furnace preventing equilibrium conditions from being 
established. This is pertinent to SiO gas which may evolve at high (>1300°C) 
temperatures (Figure 6.2). 
The XRD spectra in Figure 5.10 – show characteristic SiC peaks in all three samples, as well 
as the original Si peaks suggesting partial conversion of SiC to Si had occurred. The extent of 
conversion is difficult to discern – while it appears that the SiC peaks are increasing relative to 
the intensities of the Si peaks this is difficult to quantify without further analysis, such as using 
the Rietveld refinement method [65]. However, this was beyond the scope of the current project. 
Cursory inspection of the samples produced from the experiments (Figure 5.8) shows particles 
of different size distributions (apparent increase in largest particle sizes) and colour (ranging 
from brown orange to grey) when compared to the original kerf. The increasing size of the 
larger fractions with temperature may be due sintering, noting that significant sintering for 
silica and silicon particles alike has been observed at 1100°C [66] and 1060°C (0.75Tm - [67]) 
respectively. The various hues observed (blue, brown and white) have also been noted in 
condensation products observed in silicon making furnaces [68], [69]. 
Closer examination of the powdered samples under SEM (Figure 5.11) showed similar 
characteristics to the original kerf; the underlying kerf fragment geometry is still visible, 
particularly for the lower temperature samples, although the particles appear to have 
agglomerated. The EDS spectra of all the samples (Figure 5.13) noted the presence of both 
silicon and oxygen, suggesting the particles are either silicon oxide or have a silicon oxide 
surface layer, as well as carbon most likely present as SiC 
Also evident in all three samples are the presence of nanofibers – although less prominent in 
the sample exposed to 1000°C treatment (Figure 5.12), whereas in the sample exposed at

at 1400°C similar to the ‘outer layer’. Correspondingly, the fibres obtained in the current 
studies are expected to comprise of SiC strands coated with a thin layer of SiO2. A mechanism 
for the formation of these particles is proposed in Section 6.2.2. 
Images of the cross sections taken through the larger kerf/CO reacted particles showed three 
distinct phases for all samples. Based on the analyses of the 1200°C particle cross section

• Light phase – likely unreacted kerf, given the high Si content; 
• Darker, hexagonal-shaped phase often found embedded within lighter phase – EDS

• Dark, porous phase – the presence of Si, C and O suggests that this phase is a mixture 
of silicon carbide and oxides (likely SiO2). 
This interpretation of the phases present also agrees with the XRD results, which show the 
presence of silicon and SiC in all samples. While the XRD does not show the presence of any 
polymorphs of crystalline silicon oxide, the oxide phase may be below detectable limits and/or 
amorphous – thus not appearing on the XRD spectra.

6.2.1 Effect of Temperature on Kerf/CO System 
Where plateaus are found in the %CO versus time plot (Figure 5.14), this was interpreted as an 
equilibrium where there is no net consumption of CO, which is consistent with a constant and 
stable %CO value. A decrease in the %CO from plateau levels represented uptake of CO gas 
by the kerf in the crucible, with a larger drop representing a higher consumption rate. By 
corollary, a trough in the %CO versus time plot represents a peak in the rate of consumption 
or uptake of CO. 
Each increase in uptake of CO observed is likely associated with a chemical reaction. The first 
trough (peak CO consumption), occurring in all experiments at approximately 800°C is likely 
due to the reaction: 
3Si(s) + 2CO(g) → 2SiC(s) + SiO2(s) 
The enthalpy of this reaction is -278 kJ/mol of Si consumed at 25°C - the relationship with 
temperature is shown in Figure 6.4 below.

It was suspected that this reaction occurs via a shrinking core mechanism as illustrated in Figure 
6.5. 
  
Figure 6.5: Shrinking core model – with ash layer being SiC/SiO2. As ash layer grows and core 
Si shrinks reaction becomes ash diffusion limited. From [71]. 
Initially, the reaction between Si and CO is expected to be chemical reaction step limited; as 
the temperature increases the rate of reaction increases as determined by Arrhenius law. 
However, as there are both solid reactant (Si) and products (SiC and SiO2), the solid products 
will eventually cover the solid silicon kerf particles. For further reaction to occur, CO gas 
would have to migrate through the product layer towards the silicon and the rate of reaction 
will eventually be limited by the rate of CO diffusion. 
In the shrinking core model, the reaction rate stabilises at higher temperatures where the rate 
is dictated by ash layer diffusion, slowing with time (Figure 6.6). For the reaction between Si 
and CO up to 1200°C, it is posited that eventually a sufficiently thick SiC/SiO2 product layer 
will form and practically prevent diffusion, leading to incomplete conversion, as was observed 
in Figure 5.16 and Figure 5.17 where the solid silicon particles still exist. 
==PAGE:53==
 
 
48 
 
Figure 6.6: Shrinking core model reaction progress curve. Conversion rate slows significantly 
with progress, with the curve flattening near complete conversion (circled). For 
Si/CO system studied, it is suggested this curve plateaus before 1-XB>0, leading to 
incomplete conversion. From [71]. 
A complicating factor for the current experiments is the observation that the reaction products 
appears to be binding the individual kerf cores together (Figure 5.17), leading to a situation 
where the silicon particles are effectively embedded within a SiC/SiO2 matrix. Such an 
interaction would reduce the effective surface area exposed in comparison to the single particle 
assumptions of the shrinking core model and would be expected to lower reaction rates even 
further. 
Beyond 1350°C, the increase in CO uptake (as observed in the 1400°C hold experiment) 
suggests an additional reaction. This is supported by the significant weight gain of the system 
after exposure at 1400ºC (Figure 5.7) compared to the two lower temperature exposures, with 
the additional consumed CO being incorporated into the kerf. This additional reaction is most 
likely temperature activated rather than duration (length of exposure) based, given that this 
trough occurs well within the time frame of even the lower temperature experiments (Figure 
5.14).

The increase in CO uptake also commences at a temperature approaching the melting point of 
silicon (1414ºC [72]). While the furnace thermocouple shows a temperature 1400ºC, it would 
not be completely unexpected for some localised melting to have occurred, particularly given 
that the reaction between silicon and carbon monoxide is exothermic (Figure 6.4) thus produces 
heat. A fluid silicon phase would have improved mass transfer rates and may have encouraged 
reactions to progress. Furthermore, the vapour pressure of silicon (and the associated presence 
of gaseous Si) increases markedly with temperature (Figure 6.7), another possible source of 
mobile Si which may aid further reaction.

It is also possible that the silicon in the kerf and graphite in the crucible react directly at higher 
temperatures according to the equation: 
Si(s,l) + C(s) → SiC(s) 
The reaction between silicon and graphite occurs via the infiltration of liquid silicon into the 
solid graphite, and has been the topic of a number of high  temperature (>1430°C) studies [74], 
[75]. However, this interaction would require the wetting of the graphite crucible surfaces with 
molten silicon; the necessary conditions are unlikely to have been present given the relatively 
low temperature (below 1430°C) and continual agitation from the flow of CO and Ar gases 
during the experiments. 
6.2.2 Mechanism for Nanofibre Formation 
While no specific mechanism has been documented for the formation of the nanofibres and 
wire strands (Figure 5.11 and Figure 5.12)  in Si/CO systems, the following is proposed based 
on studies of similar SiO2/C systems [76], [77]: 
==PAGE:55==
 
 
50 
1. Exposed silicon dioxide, a product of Si/CO reaction, reacts with carbon monoxide to 
yield SiO gas: 
SiO2(s) + CO(g) → SiO(g) + CO2(g) 
The reaction is thermodynamically unfavourable at low temperatures, but occurs to a 
greater extent with increasing temperature. The white residue detected on the graphite 
tube furnace components (Figure 5.9) was most likely silicon condensation products 
SiC and SiO2 [69], further evidence of the evolution of gaseous SiO during the 
experiments which would have allowed transport of Si to the upper regions of the 
graphite tube. 
The CO2 produced is likely to be reduced at high temperatures by the graphite crucible 
surfaces as well as charcoal in the filter via the following reaction: 
CO2(g) + C(s) → 2CO(g) 
2. The SiO gas reacts with CO to produce SiC, depending on whether CO is in excess: 
SiO(g) + 3CO(g) → SiC(s) + 2CO2(g) 
3SiO(g) + CO(g) → SiC(s) + 2SiO2(s) 
It is suspected that this SiC formed from the gas phase reactants deposits on the low 
energy (111) planes of existing SiC crystal surfaces, leading to preferential growth 
along a single axis and the observed nanofibre geometry. This is illustrated Figure 6.8 
below.

the current experiments, and the vapour phase consists of SiO and CO. 
3. The overall reaction, assuming complete reduction of CO2: 
SiO2(s) + 3C(s) → SiC(s) + 2CO(g)

6.2.3 Practicality of Conversion Process
Ultimately, the reaction between CO gas and Si kerf and furnace setup does not yield
homogeneous SiC particles, nor does the reaction appear to go to completion – this likely limits
the value of this processing of kerf in this manner to produce industrial or electronic SiC.
However, the formation of the silicon containing nanowires suggests a pathway for
synthesising these structures; this may prompt further study of the Si/CO system.
6.3
Attempts at Melting the Kerf
The silicon kerf appears to melt satisfactorily at 1500°C to form a boule, with minimal loss of
material (less than 3% in all trials - Figure 5.18). The presence of a possible oxide layer on the
silicon does not appear to hinder melting at this temperature as demonstrated by the similar
results obtained for both the etched and unetched kerf experiments – both yielding coherent
bulk phases. This suggests an HF etching step is not necessary, which remove a large practical
barrier for the high temperature processing of kerf on an industrial scale.
Furthermore, the melting of the silicon kerf appears to have the additional beneficial effect of
migrating impurity phases such as SiC and SiO2 to the surfaces of the boule. This was
somewhat expected given previous ‘high temperature’ treatment studies of slurry abrasive kerf
where the silicon was found to conglomerate and partition from the SiC abrasive [23].
While no significant impurities were noted in the bulk phase via EDS, the exact purity of 
theresulting bulk material in the boule is unknown and requires more specific analysis.
A practical drawback to the melting of kerf to create boules, where the bulk material can be
separated from the impurities and recycled to the PV value chain, are the volume changes
involved during the process. As per Table 5-4, the approximate density of the kerf is 0.4 g/cm3,
which is almost one sixth of the theoretical density of silicon at 2.32 g/cm3; a crucible designed
==PAGE:57==
 
 
52 
for such an industrial process would have to be six times as large as the desired boule size to 
contain the kerf. 
However, the kerf can be melted into polysilicon feed as per trials 2 and 4 (polysilicon with 
25% unetched and etched kerf respectively) – reducing the required volume of the crucible. 
Ideally, the kerf would be added in progressively to the melt, although this would require 
specialised equipment to feed the kerf at high temperature. Alternatively, the kerf could be 
compressed into briquettes or pellets beforehand although once again this requires specialised 
equipment. The level of mixing would be determined by the kerf purity, and the required purity 
of the resulting ingot from polysilicon/kerf.

7 Summary 
7.1 Kerf Characterisation 
• Kerf Particles had platelet morphology, mean diameter of 0.9 μm with monomodal 
distribution 
• The main metallic impurities in the kerf were iron, chromium and nickel, in the 100 
ppmw range likely due to wire saw residue. 
• Oxygen content of samples was approximately 2.0% - giving a derived oxide layer 
thickness of 10 nm. 
7.2 Conversion of Kerf to SiC via CO 
• Thermodynamics predicts the formation of SiC and SiO2 solids upon reaction of the 
kerf with excess CO. Silicon carbide was observed in the final products, although 
reaction was incomplete. Evidence for silicon dioxide was noted via EDS although not 
XRD – this was likely due to low quantities and/or crystallinity. 
• Three different phases were noted in the resulting powder products – unreacted silicon 
kerf, coherent SiC and a porous mixed silicon carbide/oxide phase. 
o The nanofibres found on the particles are likely condensation products of SiO 
gas evolved from SiO2 at high temperatures. 
• Initial reaction between the kerf and CO gas appears to commence at approximately 
800°C. Shrinking core model reasonably predicts behavior and products of the kerf/CO 
system in this vicinity. 
• An additional reaction appears to occur above 1300°C, also coinciding with the 
observation of numerous nanofibres on the sample. This additional reaction is suspected 
to be facilitated by the increased mobility of silicon at high temperatures. 
7.3 Kerf Melting Trials 
• Silicon kerf melts to form boules at 1500°C – further treatment via acid etching (HF) 
or blending with higher purity polysilicon appears unnecessary. 
• The melting process encourages segregation of oxide and carbide impurities to the 
surfaces of the boule, presenting another method for refinement. 
• Kerf is voluminous, at approximately one fifth the density of solid ingot silicon 
introducing handling difficulties limiting the practicality of directly melting kerf to 
obtain ingots on an industrial scale.

8 Further work 
Further characterization of the kerf is possible, the carbon content can be quantified by ignition 
methods, and has been done so in previous studies. The carbon content will greatly influence 
further processing required, particularly if the aim is to obtain SG silicon. While difficult, it 
should also be possible to characterise kerf particle cross sections via selection of appropriate 
analytical and preparation techniques, such as focused ion beam milling. 
While the process of direct reaction of kerf with carbon monoxide did not appear to produce a 
suitable yield of homogenous SiC particles, it potentially has interesting applications for the 
production of silicon nanowires. Further work can be performed on characterising the particles 
via electron microscopy, and various etching techniques to view the complete structure of the 
particles. 
In an attempt to improve the uniformity of the carbide particles and eliminate large 
agglomerates, the reaction between kerf and carbon monoxide could be attempted in a more 
vigorous setting. Ball milling and fluidized bed reactors are potential two techniques which 
could possibly achieve this. 
The kerf melting experiments highlighted a potential pathway for recycling kerf back into the 
PV value chain, with partial removal of carbide and oxide impurity removal. Methods for 
overcoming the voluminous nature of the kerf should be studied. For example, development of 
an apparatus for gradually injecting kerf into polysilicon ingot melts, or methods for 
compacting the loose kerf. 
Further characterisation of ingots comprised of kerf should focus upon characteristics which 
directly affect cell performance. The ingots (or PV cells created from such ingots) could be 
subject to lifetime and resistivity measurements as an indicator to PV cell performance. High 
sensitivity methods of testing for metals (ICPMS) and atmospherics (FTIR) could also be 
performed on the ingot to further aid characterisation and prediction of cell performance.
