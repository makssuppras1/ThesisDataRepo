1. Abstract 
In Danish nursing homes, the housing units are the resident’s private quarters. Due to this, it 
is not possible to set up rules or regulations regarding activities concerning the use of open 
flames like candle lights and smoking. Unfortunately, this means that tragic fires happen. In 
2019, 6 deaths were associated with fires in nursing homes. 
 
The purpose of this study was to assess whether the actual number of health care staff at 
work during the day, evening and night – both during workdays and at weekends - can 
handle an evacuation of a fire compartment within a nursing home in case of a fire in a 
housing unit. 
 
A study made in 2019 by Enquiry, shows how many healthcare workers there are at work 
during the entire day, 7 days a week. These numbers are then used to simulate the time it 
takes to evacuate a fire compartment, designed through a prescriptive layout, according to 
danish building legislation. The simulation is done in the program Pathfinder ®. 
 
The study concerning evacuation has been performed on two different building layouts. The 
first represents the new prescriptive design, that followed updated building regulations that 
came in 2018. The design described a maximum of 10 residents per fire compartment, 
naturally limiting the extent of the evacuation. The second layout was based on the older 
prescriptive designs associated with the Danish building regulations of 2015 and older. It 
was taken into account, that there are no limits to the number of residents within the fire 
compartment. It is also assessed that the second prescriptive layout is the most used layout 
in existing nursing homes today. 
 
The limited number of residents present in the prescriptive layout from the building 
regulations of 2018, has a great impact on the time the evacuation took. It was concluded 
that 1 healthcare worker could easily empty the affected fire compartment before there was 
any risk of critical conditions for the rest of the residents. 
 
The study concerning the prescriptive layout of the building regulations of 2015 or older, 
shows a relatively high number of residents within a fire compartment. As a result of this, the 
staff working the night-time shifts are not able to to evacuate all the residents within the 
affected fire compartment before critical conditions were obtained.  
 
The results have subsequently been used to make a graph that gives an estimate regarding 
staff to resident ratio, within the specified time limit.

2. Introduction 
An elderly resident at a nursing home in Denmark, is often at very poor health or at very high 
age, when they are referred to staying at a nursing home, instead of their own home.  
 
It is possible for an elderly citizen to get a place in a nursing home if the person is dependent 
on care several times a day and where care cannot be provided in any other residence than 
in the nursing home. 
There are several criteria where all must be met [1]: 
• 
The person must have permanently impaired physical and/or mental capacity and 
need help and support 24/7. 
• 
The person cannot make it on their own, only with daily care or support. 
• 
The person needs help more than eight times a day, or he/she cannot be alone 
between visits. Relatives cannot provide care between visits. 
In the Danish building legislation [2] regarding fire requirements concerning evacuation, 
residents in a nursing home are considered unable to evacuate themselves during a fire. 
The residents are primarily or completely dependent on the staff to react correctly when the 
alarm sounds in case of a fire breakout. 
 
Regarding fire safety legislation, nursing homes in Denmark are placed in a category where 
the fire safety level is very high compared to other building types. A modern nursing home 
built today, must consist active fire protection measures, such as fire detection systems 
(ABA), Automatic warning systems (AVA), and fire suppression systems. The passive fire 
protection measure can be the fire compartmentations that can be as large as 2.000 m2 in 
one floor, but only 600 m2 if the nursing home is in more than one floor if not fitted with 
sprinkler. [3] 
 
The automatic warning system is only required to warn the staff [3]. This can be done by 
warning signals sent to mobile carrying devices, by acoustic warning signals, visual warning 
signals or both types of warning signals sent to staff areas. 
 
In case of an alarm, it is then assumed [4] that the trained staff immediately carry out the 
emergency instructions. What happens thereafter is up to the training and the instructions 
the staff has received at the specific nursing home.      
 
According to [5] the conditions of the performance of evacuation drills has been examined. 
The main conclusions are that somewhere between 30 % and 50 % of the employees at the 
Danish nursing homes, which participated in the inquiry, did not know their own role in an 
evacuation drill. 40 % of nursing home staff answer that they have not had an evacuation 
drill at their workplace the last three years. Moreover, almost 30 % did not experience that 
new employees are instructed in what to do when the nursing home they work at, needs to 
be evacuated.  
 
The report concludes that the overall quality of the given instructions is non-satisfying [5].

One issue in death fires can be the lack of training and instructions given to the staff. 
Another issue, which is rarely in focus by the media and politicians, is the number of staff 
needed to perform a safe and acceptable evacuation. 
 
It is often mentioned in the media [8] [9] that there are massive budget cuts within the elderly 
care. As these budget cuts are likely to affect number of staff present at any given time, do 
they also affect the effectiveness of the evacuation of a fire compartment within a nursing 
home? 
 
The purpose of this thesis is to evaluate the presence of the staff and evaluate the staff 
present to perform the evacuation of a prescriptive building section within a nursing home.

3. Background 
The Danish building regulations [10] comes from the Danish Building law [11]  which sets the 
overall requirements regarding construction of buildings in Denmark. The Danish building 
regulation is the basis of construction of any type of building in Denmark. The building 
regulation consists of 565 paragraphs divided into 37 chapters. These chapters contain 
requirements to the sewer and drainage system, the buildings energy consumption, the load 
bearing building parts, daylight requirements, tolerable sound conditions etc.  
 
The Danish building regulations chapter 5, concerns fire. When looking at the overall 
requirements for fire, nursing homes must have a satisfactory safety level for the occupants 
in the event of fire. Fire safety level in a nursing home must be maintained throughout the 
building’s lifespan [12]. 
 
There is a number of requirements to the design, construction, operation, and maintenance 
of a building that must be considered according to [10]. The main requirement that will be 
examined here is the requirement regarding a safe evacuation of occupants. 
 
The Danish “Trafik- Bygge og Boligstyrelsen” (TBST) has made a guideline [13], so the 
Danish building regulations can be properly implemented into the design and construction of 
a building. When following this guideline during the design phase, it is sufficiently 
documented, that the building fulfills the Danish building regulations.  
 
Currently, this guideline consists of 8 chapters. The most relevant chapter for this thesis is 
Chapter 2: Evacuation and rescue of persons. 
 
To further support the guideline, there has been made 13 appendixes that provides a more 
specific description of the specific building types. Nursing homes are covered by appendix 7 
– Pre-accepted solutions, building sections where people cannot evacuate themselves [3].  
 
The Building Regulations' guidance to Chapter 5 – Fire, chapter 2 [2] deals with the 
fundamental requirements to evacuation of modern buildings.  
It is stated [2] that construction must be designed and carried out so that in the event of a fire 
there can be a safe evacuation and rescue of occupants. This must be done, while 
considering that: 
1) 
People within the building may become aware that a fire has occurred. 
2) 
Escape routes are designed to allow for the evacuation of occupants. 
3) 
Rescue openings are designed to allow for the rescue of occupants. 
 
The requirements for evacuation and rescue of occupants in accordance with the above 
described, is that it must be ensured that:   
• 
Evacuation of occupants may begin quickly.   
• 
Evacuation can be done safely via escape routes leading to terrain in the open.   
• 
Rescue openings are set up in buildings so the emergency services will be able to 
use them to rescue occupants caught in the room through their ladders. [2] 
 
The purpose of these requirements is to ensure that all occupants, either on their  own, or by 
the assistance of others, can get to safety outside the building in the case of fire and before 
==PAGE:11==
 
 
 
11 
they are exposed to critical conditions caused by the fire. This can be ensured either by 
following appendix 7 – Pre-accepted solutions, building sections where people cannot 
evacuate themselves, or by documenting that the evacuation time from any location in the 
building does not exceed the time it takes for critical conditions to arise at the location in 
question. 
 
3.1 
Evacuation strategy 
Before designing escape routes during the design process, it is required in Danish legislation 
[2] to establish an evacuation strategy which will be a part of the overall fire strategy. The 
extent of the evacuation strategy depends on the complexity of the building in relation to 
evacuation and rescue of people. When defining the evacuation strategy, the typical type of 
occupant, must be taken into consideration. Further, the escape routes must be easily 
identified, easy to reach and use. The evacuation strategy must address the following:  
 
1) The types of occupants within the specific building 
2) The principles used for overall evacuation 
3) Measures used to make occupants aware of a fire 
4) The design and fire protection of escape routes 
5) The need for rescue openings. 
 
3.1.1 
Types of occupants  
It is necessary to have a special focus on residents who cannot save themselves by their 
own means, as they require help from staff or other responsible persons. The starting point 
for the evacuation strategy is therefore the category of use of the building. The categories of 
use are based on several criteria relating to fire risk conditions. These criteria include the 
consequence of a fire, including the number of people who may be affected by a fire and the 
mobility of individuals, as well as the ability to recognize and respond to a fire.  
 
Nursing homes will be placed under category 6. For further information please refer to 
Appendix A. This will have a great impact on the building’s design, as the category defines 
the fire safety level, regarding the prescriptive requirements. This will typically be seen in the 
demand for smaller fire compartments, shorter escape routes, and more fire technical 
installations. 
 
3.1.2 
Principles of evacuation 
An evacuation strategy can contain several evacuation methods. Normally, three basic 
concepts are used:  
• 
Total evacuation.  
• 
Phase evacuation.  
• 
Evacuation to a safe place within the building. 
 
Total evacuation happens by emptying the entire building simultaneously when the alarm 
sounds. This is mostly used in big open spaced buildings, where the occupants are at risk of 
being affected by the smoke from the fire. Total evacuation is depending on people being 
able to evacuate by themselves. 
 
Phase evacuation happens by emptying sections of a building or an area at a time. This is 
usually used in high rise buildings or buildings divided into many fire compartments.    
 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
Evacuation to a safe place within the building is often used together with phase evacuation. 
This is typically used in buildings with many fire compartments, where the escape routes 
lead to another fire rated compartment, and from there the escape routes leads to open air. 
In nursing homes this type of evacuation is preferred when the nursing home is in more than 
one floor. In this way, the residents can be evacuated by the staff to the neighboring section, 
which often is made as an independent fire compartment. [2] 
 
3.1.3 
Measures used to make occupants aware of fire 
The escape routes in a nursing home shall be carried out with an automatic warning system 
activated by an automatic detection system. The use of an audible alarm must be adapted to 
the specific use of the building or section. Where occupants cannot react to the alarm 
themselves or cannot by their own means get to safety, the alarm must alert the staff. [2] 
 
The nursing home must also be equipped with fire suppression systems such as sprinklers if 
the nursing home has fire compartments larger than 600 m2   in more than one floor. An 
additional requirement is that if the building itself has sleeping quarters with a size bigger 
than 1.000 m2, and more than one floor, the building must also be equipped with sprinklers. 
[2] 
 
Panic lighting and emergency lighting of the escape routes must be installed in a building, or 
in sections of a building if this is larger than 1.000 m2. [2] 
 
3.1.4 
Design of escape routes in nursing homes. 
An escape route is defined as a system of exits, hallways, and stairwells. The means of 
escape must ensure that occupants can leave the building safely. The following functional 
requirements for the design of an escape route must be taken into consideration: 
1) Occupants knowledge of the escape routes in the building.  
2) Escape routes must be easy to identify, reach and use.  
3) Possibility and ability of occupants to use the escape routes in the building. 
4) That the evacuation of persons can safely be carried out all the way to terrain in the 
open air or to a safe place within the building and from there safely to the outside 
terrain. 
5) That there are no critical temperatures, smoke concentrations, heat radiation or 
corresponding critical conditions during the period during which the escape routes 
are to be used for evacuation.  
6) The escape routes must be adequately sized to the number of occupants that uses 
them. 
7) Doors must be easy to open without the use of a key or tools when the escape route 
is used by occupants, and doors in escape routes used by more than 150 persons 
must open in the direction of flight.  
8) Escape routes must be free from furniture throughout the necessary width. [2] 
 
These eight general requirements must be adapted into the specific design of the building. In 
nursing homes, the attention must be drawn to the fact that none, or maybe only a few, of 
the occupants can evacuate themselves. The whole system of escape and egress must be 
adapted to the fact that evacuation is likely to happen by staff, moving the elderly residents 
away from the fire in beds or by wheelchairs.

3.2 
The role of nursing home staff during evacuation 
Occupants in a burning building can be threatened by the fire on several levels. It is 
necessary to organize the 1st. response of the fire brigade properly according to the threat 
level of the occupants trapped inside. According to [14] the threat levels are as follows: 
 
Class 1:  
Persons that are directly threatened by flames or smoke and cannot by own means leave 
the premises. These persons must be rescued immediately. 
 
Class 2:  
Persons that, with the spread of fire, can be threatened by flames or smoke but will be able 
to leave the premises by their own means. These persons must be evacuated as soon as 
possible. 
 
Class 3:  
Persons that is not and will not be threatened by flames or smoke but in some way can be 
affected by the fire. These persons need to be informed by the situation. 
 
The most important work for the fire crew when they arrive is to rescue occupants (class 1) 
that are directly threatened by fire.  This will always have 1st. priority for the fire brigade. 
Usually it is deemed enough to orally inform class 2 persons about the situation to make 
them evacuate by themselves. Class 3 persons can be informed about the situation by the 
police securing the outer perimeters of the working space of the fire crew from curious 
bystanders. 
 
In a nursing home both class 1 and class 2 persons cannot rescue themselves because they 
are often bedridden, have very low cognitive functions, and/or have no psychological 
understanding of the dangerous situation they are in.  
 
If the fire crew is busy rescuing class 1 persons, there is a grave need to have class 2 
persons evacuated. When the fire crew can be as little as 4-5 persons in the beginning of the 
incident, there will not in the 1st. deployment be enough resources available to 
simultaneously handle the class 2 persons [15].  
There is also a time factor to consider, when there is a possibility, that the fire brigade can 
be up to 20 minutes away [16] [17]. 
 
The evacuation of class 2 persons must then be done by the staff as soon as they are 
alarmed by the ABA through the AVA installation. The work of the staff during an evacuation 
must be to put out the fire if possible and to evacuate the residents not affected by fire or 
smoke in the fire affected fire compartment. The staff must not bring themselves in danger of 
fire or smoke, as this could result in the fire crew needing to use valuable time to rescue the 
staff as well.

4. Evacuation theory 
An evacuation analysis is an estimate of the time it takes to evacuate the specific fire 
compartment, which in this case is a section of a nursing home.  
 
To define the time it takes to do a full evacuation, the time can be split up into several parts 
[19]:

4.1 
Detection time 
Detection time is defined as the time it takes from the ignition of the fire till the automatic fire 
detection system detects the fire. This can vary between a few seconds, to several minutes, 
based on the chosen detection system and placement of the detectors. Smoke detectors 
usually detect small soot molecules from a beginning fire relatively fast, whereas thermal 
detectors activate at a predefined temperature for example 60° Celsius, or 10° higher than 
ambient temperature [20].  
 
This time depends on the chosen detection method within a fire strategy, and the detectors 
used in the specific project. The time is also depending on how the detectors are 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
programmed. There can be up to 60 seconds of processing time within the detector itself, 
before the activated detector passes the signal to the fire central [20].   
 
The detection time can be estimated by use of formulas described in various literature for 
example [21]. 
 
4.2 
Warning time  
Warning time is defined as the time it takes from when the fire is detected by the automatic 
fire detection system till the system has activated the warning system. In a nursing home this 
time is given by how long it takes for the automatic fire detection system to send a signal to 
the mobile carrying device, worn by the staff. The warning can also happen by audible 
warning systems, such as sirens or detectors with tone speakers. 
 
In [19] this time has been pre-set as 5 seconds before the audible warning signal will be 
activated. There is no available information on the time it takes before a mobile carrying 
device can be activated, so this would be set as the same, when defining the warning time 
within a required simulation for preparing for example a fire strategy.    
 
4.3 
Reaction and decision time 
The reaction and decision time is defined as the time from the staff are alerted to they 
understand the warning signal received. The staff must then act on the signal and do as 
instructed. At a nursing home the instructions will usually be to investigate the origin of the 
alarm.  
 
The reaction and decision time for staff at a nursing home is defined as follows and can be 
found in [19].

5. Method 
5.1 
Pre evacuation preparation 
In every nursing home there must be fire instructions that the staff knows and have been 
instructed in. in case of a fire breakout, the alarm will sound. Then will the instructions 
ensure that the entire act of evacuation, containing pre evacuation preparation, firefighting, 
evacuation of residents to relocating residents from the fire affected area and much more will 
be done as fast and safe as possible. 
  
5.1.1 
Alarming staff  
Every nursing home must at least have an automatic fire detection system (ABA), and an 
automatic warning system (AVA) that is directed specifically to the staff [2]. According to 
Danish legislation, the elderly residents in a nursing home are defined as being unable to 
evacuate themselves [2]. As a result, a siren or other audible alarms are not expected to 
have any effect. The alarming of the staff is usually done by the ABA system, when a 
detector in a fire affected room gets activated. In modern nursing homes, the signal will be 
sent to the staff on duty, usually by a call, a text message, or an audible alarm to a mobile 
carrying device. The staff can also receive alarm signals by visual or acoustic signals fitted 
within the building. 
 
The procedure is then, that the staff on call goes to the ABA central, where they can see 
which detector has been activated. The staff then goes to the fire affected area to put out the 
fire if they can or start the evacuation of the residents in the fire affected area. The 
evacuation scenarios can be very varying due to different building layouts, number of floors, 
doors can be left open or closed, and the composition of the local fire brigade.

The staff must be instructed in the fire instructions prepared for the specific nursing home [2] 
[24] and follow the instructions in case of a fire. 
 
The instructions contain guidelines on what the staff must do in case of fire. The instructions 
are required to at least contain the following subjects:  
 
Alarm 
– the emergency response is alerted on 1 1 2 
– activation of AVA by pressing alarm button 
Warning of persons 
- alerting staff 
– alerting other persons 
Evacuation 
- staff guides to escape routes 
- requirements to close non self-closing doors

- operation of handheld fire-fighting equipment, 
Specific features, including (if relevant) information on how to 
– music installations are stopped 
- smoke machines are stopped 
- normal lighting is switched on

All staff shall be instructed at least once a year to the contents of the fire and evacuation 
instructions, including the tasks assigned to the staff.  
 
The tasks in case of fire for nursing home staff can be like this: 
 
1. The alarm to the staff is activated due to smoke detection in any given room. The 
staff in charge receives an SMS, a digital phone call or a loud sounding alarm on the 
mobile phone they carry. 
2. The staff goes to the ABA central to observe which area or room of the nursing home 
the alarm is coming from. 
3. The staff checks the room or area. 
4. In case of a small beginning fire, the staff will put out the fire by handheld fire-fighting 
equipment. 
5. The staff helps to rescue the resident in the fire affected room if they can without 
getting affected by smoke or flames.  
6. If there is a great risk of injury for the staff, they must not attempt to rescue the 
resident(s). This must be done by the fire fighters, wearing proper safety equipment. 
The door to the burning room must be closed, so the evacuation of other residents in 
the same section can begin safely. 
7. If the door is closed, the staff can feel if the door is warm, and smoke is coming out 
underneath the door, or at the corners. If neither of those things is present, the door 
can be opened very carefully, to see if it is possible to save the occupant within the 
room. If the fire is big, or there is too much smoke, the door must be closed again so 
the fire brigade can extinguish the fire and do the rescue of the occupant. 
8. A member of the staff must be sent to meet with the arriving fire brigade, so they can 
be led to the class 1 person as fast as possible. 
9. Other staff rushing to the area must start evacuation of adjacent rooms immediately.  
 
5.2 
Contingency plans 
Fire instructions are typically one-sided plans made with short cues to summarize and make 
the staff aware of what the tasks are, during a fire. An example of a fire instruction is placed 
as Appendix C  
 
Contingency plans are tools used to secure a fast and well instructed effort to give an 
organization a robustness against unforeseen events, such as natural disasters, flooding, 
water pollution, cyber-attacks, lack of power and big building fires. The contingency plans 
are used to ensure robustness and sufficient resources to handle for example a big number 
of evacuees caused by a fire. 
 
A specific setup of the tasks that must be performed in an evacuation, as described above, 
must be described in detail, so the staff has a routine concerning their specific role in the 
evacuation preparation, as well as in the evacuation itself. This is usually done in a fire 
contingency plan.      
 
All healthcare staff, administrative staff, leaders, and technical staff must be trained in the 
contingency plan to know their role in a bigger picture when disaster strikes.

It is also in the contingency plans the specified roles of health care staff must be defined in 
case of a fire with many evacuees. In such a case, there will be a need for relocating many

of Silkeborg, Denmark [25] , regarding the area concerning health and care.

6. Results and discussion 
This result of a simulation or a calculation regarding evacuation is called “required safe 
egress time” (RSET), and must be compared to the time it takes for the fire to reach critical 
conditions in the egress routes for the occupants who need to use them in case of a fire. The 
time it takes to reach critical conditions is called “available safe egress time” (ASET).  
 
6.1 
Available safe egress time for evacuation 
When the fire alarm is activated by a fire the staff will initiate the evacuation following the 
specific fire instructions made for the nursing home. Locating the fire, initiating the 
evacuation, and otherwise following the instructions takes time.  
 
In [3] it is acceptable to place a fire rated door between the housing unit and the hallway, 
with an European classification EI2 30.  
 
The European Standard EN 13501-2 [30] specifies this combination of letters and numbers 
regarding the door:  
 
• 
The E stands for integrity, which means that within 30 minutes of the duration of the 
test, it is not accepted to see flames through the door by more than 10 seconds. A 
cotton wool cloth sample must not ignite on the non-fire affected side of the door. 
And finally, there must not be any bigger gaps between the fire affected and non-fire 
affected side of the door.   
• 
I2 stands for an allowed temperature rise within 100 mm from the edges of the door, 
and an allowed maximum temperature rise around the frame of the door at 360°C. 
• 
30 stands for the numbers of minutes tested and approved. 
 
The building parts e.g. walls surrounding the housing unit, is fire rated to EI 60.    
 
The European Standard EN 13501-2 [30] Specifies this combination of letters and numbers 
regarding the walls:  
 
• 
The E stand for integrity which means that within 60 minutes of the duration of the 
test, it is not accepted to see flames through the wall by more than 10 seconds. A 
cotton wool cloth sample must not ignite on the non-fire affected side of the wall. And 
finally, there must not be any bigger gaps between the fire affected and non-fire 
affected side of the wall.   
• 
The I stand for insulation, and allows an average temperature-rise across the surface 
of the wall of 140°C, and an allowed maximum temperature rise of 180 °C. 
• 
60 stands for the numbers of minutes tested and approved. 
 
The combination of the door and the wall is accepted in the prescriptive designs of both 
model 1 and 2 as there is expected to be no combustible materials in the hallways. This 
gives a potentially trapped resident in the opposite housing unit approximately 1 hour to wait 
for rescue, until the fire breaks through the door to an adjacent housing unit.    
 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
 
31 
If the fire breaks out in the hallway instead, every resident not evacuated by the staff, is 
suddenly a class 1 person as described in section 3.2 and must therefore be rescued by the 
fire brigade because of the smoke-filled environment within the hallway. 
 
When the door is closed from the beginning of the fire, this gives the staff 30 minutes to 
evacuate the rest of the fire compartment because of the described prescriptive design. 
 
This only applies if the door is closed. According to [3] the doors between the hallway and a 
given housing unit is assumed to be closed as the housing unit is the residents private 
quarters. This means that as long as the doors are closed, and the staff can evacuate the 
entire fire compartment within 30 minutes, the safety level of the specific fire compartment 
within a nursing home built according to the prescriptive building design can be considered 
acceptable.  
 
In this thesis the ASET time is then consistent with a closed fire door that can withstand a 
fire for up to 30 minutes.

6.3 
Discussion 
6.3.1 
Model design and number of residents 
The results given by evacuation simulations of the models 1 and 2 is based on the 
prescriptive design described in [12] and [27] . This means that other functions within the fire 
compartment, such as staff facilities, depots, shared living rooms, shared kitchens, or dining 
areas, have not been taken into account.  
 
In model 1 this is clearly seen in the size of the model. A fire compartment can be up to 
2.000 m2 when the compartment has a fully automated sprinkler system. As it is only 
acceptable to have a maximum of 10 occupants within the same fire compartment, the 
model is only 384 m2. The difference in size is not expected to have any impact on the result 
itself as the walking length to the nearest exit, or to a safe area, is predefined. 
   
In contrast, there is no limit on the number of occupants within the fire compartment in model 
2. This means that model 2 has room for 51 housing units within the maximum of 2.000 m2 
for a sprinklered fire compartment. As it has been chosen to use a worst-case-scenario and 
not include other functions than housing units in the model, the number of occupants that 
needs to be evacuated is correspondingly high. As a result, there is a conservatively high 
number of evacuees within the modelled fire compartment.  
 
When the focus is mainly on prescriptive building layouts, the numbers of residents within 
the fire compartment can only be a qualified guess. The maximum residents in model 1 is 
given, by a maximum number of beds, regardless of the number of beds within each housing 
unit. In model 2 there could also have been a unspecified number of double beds, raising the 
==PAGE:39==
 
 
 
39 
resident number to the double. It has been chosen to put one resident per housing unit, to 
allow a possibility of comparison between model 1 and 2.   
 
6.3.2 
Number of staff available 
Just as there are uncertainties regarding the number of residents within a fire compartment 
of a nursing home designed through a prescriptive design, the numbers of available staff 
also constitute an uncertainty. This is reflected in [18] that gives an estimate of the daily 
healthcare staff to resident ratio. The staff herein is made up of nurses and other healthcare 
professionals. In the evacuation simulations, it has not been taken into account that 
administrative staff, technical staff, and other resources may be present and able to help in 
performing the evacuation.  
 
It is also a possibility that not all staff-members are able to participate in the  evacuation from 
the beginning due to other tasks that locks them to that task until it is over, such as helping 
residents to the bathroom, lifting elderly from the bed to a chair, etc.  
 
There is also a risk of uninstructed and/or unexperienced staff starting to panic and being 
unable to contribute to the evacuation., as documented in the Danish documentary 
“Hospitalsbranden” from 1978.  
 
Finally, the use of temporary healthcare workers, that are not instructed in the specific fire 
instruction of the nursing home, can also be a problem not addressed in the current 
evacuation study.   
 
It is shown in [31] that poorly instructed staff will result in a longer evacuation time. 
As mentioned in the introduction, somewhere between 30 % and 50 % of the employees at 
the Danish nursing homes which participated in the inquiry, did not know their own role in an 
evacuation drill. 40 % of nursing home staff answer that they have not had an evacuation 
drill at their workplace the last three years, and almost 30 % did not experience that new 
employees were instructed in what to do when the nursing home they work at, needs to be 
evacuated.  
 
6.3.3 
Pre-evacuation time 
When a fire breaks out, a lot of events takes place in a short amount of time. The time until 
the staff have initiated the evacuation is defined as pre-movement time, which has been 
predefined in [19]. The pre-movement time contains the decision and reaction time, for each 
evacuee to understand what is happening, and to start to leave the building. It does not 
consider the tasks needed be done before an actual evacuation of bedridden residents. 
 
It has been difficult to find any documentation regarding time to perform the tasks described 
in 5.1.2. This time can be called pre-evacuation time for nursing homes. 
 
The pre evacuation time within a nursing home can be very different from nursing home to 
nursing home.  The pre-evacuation time is dependent on the tasks given in the fire 
instruction, the placement of the fire central, and the building design itself.  If the staff is 
poorly trained and they do not know their role and must find out first, this also takes away 
precious time from the evacuation. The nursing home can be a big complex building where 
the fire central can be placed far away from the fire. The warning signaling system can be 
poorly designed with the risk that the staff does not hear it.    
==PAGE:40==
40 
 
 
The pre-evacuation time can be affected by how well the staff is instructed and/or whether or 
not they have had physical training and fire drills. Moreover, the building layout of the 
nursing home, and the technology available will have an impact. An example of this is smart 
applications for mobile carrying devices showing the screen of the fire central directly on the 
device. When this is used, the staff does not need to go to the fire central first to locate the 
activated detector. They can go directly, and thereby save precious time [32].  
 
To shorten the pre-evacuation time the staff must be well trained in the fire instructions, as 
well as in their own roles. 
 
The tasks described in 3.2 must be distributed to each staff-member and the staff must be 
aware of the placement of the meeting points., The staff member(s) receiving the alarm 
needs to know and understand how to summon the rest of the staff within the nursing home.  
 
In the simulations carried out, the staff is placed in a hallway within the model, which 
consists of only one fire compartment. This means that the required staff are close when the 
evacuation is initiated. All the pre-evacuation tasks are not a part of the simulation itself. This 
means that the evacuation as a whole will take longer than simulated, and the evacuation 
simulation only can be seen as a part of the entire evacuation process assimilated to 
movement time as seen in Table 2. 
 
6.3.4 
Warning of staff 
The way the staff is warned throughout the different nursing homes can vary. In [12] and [27] 
it is required that the staff receives the audible warning directly, or by tone warning given by 
speakers placed near staff facilities.  
 
In a real nursing home, staff is likely to be distributed throughout the entire nursing home. 
The challenge can be, to ensure that all available staff receive and respond to the alarm. 
The available staff must then subsequently meet, roles must be distributed, and the 
evacuation must be initiated.  
 
Through building inspections done by the author, it was experienced that there can be 
several challenges with using mobile carrying devices, such as lack of battery, the number of 
mobile carrying devices among the staff, mobile carrying devices placed and forgotten at 
different locations, and that staff performing different healthcare related tasks, are unable to 
look at the device, or to hear it.  
 
6.3.5 
Evacuation simulation vs. real life evacuation 
The staff within the simulations do the evacuation without any error. This means that the 
evacuation is done in a lean and effective way every time.  
As a result, it can be difficult to translate the obtained evacuation time from an evacuation 
simulation program directly to a real-life situation.  There can be many unpredicted obstacles 
not accounted for, such as heavy residents that the staff needs to be two to evacuate, 
residents that is in the bathroom, panicking residents, panicking staff, poorly instructed staff, 
blocked hallways filled with furniture etc.  
 
In [23] there has been made different exercises in for example turning the hospital bed out in 
the hallway. The different approaches give different times. This shows that every decision 
==PAGE:41==
 
 
 
41 
the staff makes within the evacuation have a great impact on the time it takes to perform the 
evacuation. 
 
A fire in a nursing home is relatively rare. This can have the unfortunate side effect that there 
is a lack regarding instruction of staff [5]. This means that staff in real life is not as skilled as 
the staff in Pathfinder ® shows.   
 
In the simulation concerning 27 staff-members in model 2, it is seen that there is a bit 
queuing time. This does not have a big impact on the overall evacuation time, as the 
evacuation is done in under 4 minutes, because of the high number of staff-members. The 
hallway is designed with the capacity to have two beds crossing the hallway at the same 
time, but the staff queued up a bit at the exits. It is not known how an evacuation will go in a 
real-life exercise with such a high staff to resident ratio.  
 
Normally there would have been done several run-throughs of the specific simulation to 
calculate a mean evacuation time. This makes sense when simulating many people leaving 
a given area, where there are different obstacles, and ways to leave the building and the 
evacuees have a certain amount of autonomous will of their own.  
 
With this exercise the only thing the staff could decide during the simulation, was which exit 
to use. The entry of the room, the collection of the bed and leaving the room was 
predestined. the Pathfinder ® program was not set up to take a default bedridden occupant 
out of a default housing unit by its own choice. Other simulations where run, but the 
evacuations ended at the same time and because of this, there has not been created more 
evacuations than described in Table 10 and Table 11.

6.3.7 
Building design 
The specific building design also plays a major role in how the evacuation can be performed, 
and how fast. When a nursing home constitutes one floor, the building can be designed with 
doors that lead directly outside from each housing unit. This makes the evacuation much 
safer and easier, as the evacuation is not affected by what happens inside the building. 
Nursing homes built in several floors can have different egress strategies, for example fire 
fighter elevators, access to secure areas etc.   
 
As described in 6.1 the door from a hallway to a housing unit is in Danish legislation 
considered naturally closed. According to building inspections done by the author, this is not 
always the case. When the door is open the residents can follow the everyday life happening 
across the fire compartment.  
 
The circumstances regarding the view on naturally open doors can give some challenges 
with the possibility of fire spread from the housing unit to the hallway. The extent of open 
doors between housing units and hallways in general have not been examined. 
 
==PAGE:44==
44 
 
There are several installations that can accommodate this challenge. This can for example 
be self-closing devices that only closes the door by a signal sent from a smoke detector near 
the door. 
 
Long hallways must be separated by self-closing smoke tight doors. In [27] regarding model 
1 the doors are required to be smoke tight doors with the European classification CSa. The 
doors are only tested to be tight against cold smoke with a temperature at 20° C. This 
means that the doors may not have any effect against hot smoke emitting from a fire 
affected housing unit.  
 
In the simulations, the smoke-tight doors in hallway have not been accounted for. The 
assessment regarding the influence of the smoke tight doors cannot be accounted for in a 
simulation model within Pathfinder ®. If this in a future state should be investigated, this 
could possibly be done by a Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulation to examine the 
flow of the smoke layer and the smoke layer temperature in the hallway, to assess if the 
achievement of critical conditions in the hallway could be delayed. Unfortunately, this is 
outside the scope of this thesis.   
 
The requirements to smoke tight doors have been tightened in [12]. This means that the 
doors now must have a European classification CS200, which means that the doors can 
withhold hot smoke, with a temperature up to 200° C. This has not been considered 
regarding model 2 as the hallway did not get so long, that this was required. 
 
In general, the influence on the evacuation simulation regarding smoke tight doors dividing 
the hallway into smaller sections has not been examined.     
 
6.3.8 
The use of stretchers to perform an evacuation 
In chapter 4, the use of stretchers is briefly mentioned. It has not been assessed how the 
evacuation time would be if stretchers were used. The use of stretchers has its limit, as a 
stretcher can be carried by 2 to 4 handlers. This locks more staff to each evacuee giving a 
ratio of up to 1:4. In comparison, it has been assessed that one staff can handle one bed in 
an ordinary patient transfer, giving a ratio of 1:1. The use of stretchers gives physical limits 
to the numbers of evacuees that the handlers can manage. It is assessed that this is not 
possible to assess by a simulation performed on a computer.

7. Conclusion 
The purpose of this thesis was to examine if there is enough healthcare staff-members 
available on a daily basis, to perform an evacuation in a nursing home. To cover most 
Danish nursing homes, two prescriptive building layouts based on Danish legislation was 
examined. The first model layout is based on the prescriptive layout described in the 
guidance to chapter 5, concerning fire from the Danish building regulations of 2018. 
The second model is made from prescriptive layouts described in [27] belonging to the 
Danish building regulations of 2015.  
 
These two specific layouts were chosen since model 1 represents the requirements of a fire 
compartment within a nursing home built today, whereas model 2 represents most of the 
existing building mass. 
 
The overall simulation results have been made for all shift during a week, as the numbers of 
staff present changes - both during the week but also across the day. Weekdays in the 
daytime is most busy, with more healthcare staff-members at work, whereas the nighttime in 
the weekends has the lowest number of staff available to perform an evacuation.  
 
In the simulated evacuation of model 1, the Estimated Safety Level was 1.82 at the time 
where the fewest staff-members were present - an ESL well above 1. In minutes, this 
corresponds to a maximum evacuation time of approx. 16 minutes, which, when compared 
to a minimum Available Safe Egress time of 30 minutes given by the maximum fire 
resistance of the door between the housing unit and the hallway, provides plenty of time to 
perform the evacuation. 
 
It can be concluded that the evacuation itself, even with added time to simulate time and 
tasks performed in the adjacent safe area, can be carried out by the lowest number of staff 
available. 
 
In contrast, the simulated evacuation of model 2 gives an Estimated Safety Level of 0.58 at 
the time where the fewest staff-members are present. For model 2, the ESL is well below 1.  
Compared to a minimum Available Safe Egress time of 30 minutes, given by the maximum 
fire resistance of the door between the housing unit and the hallway, the staff would in this 
case need more than 51 minutes. So almost twice the available time.  
 
It can be concluded that the evacuation itself, with added time to simulate time and tasks 
performed in the adjacent safe area, cannot be caried out in a satisfying manner by the 
lowest number of available staff-members. The staff can be as little as 2 persons present, 
giving staff to resident ratio of 1:25, while at least  4 staff-members are needed for the 50 
residents (1:12.5 ratio) if the evacuation were to be caried out safely. 
 
This clearly emphasizes a need to focus on the staff available during nighttime in Danish 
nursing homes, and the effect it has on the likelihood of carrying out a satisfying evacuation 
in case of fire.  
 
The results obtained reflect the act of evacuation itself. In the working process of this thesis, 
many uncertainties have been revealed. Pre-evacuation time according to the placement of 
==PAGE:46==
46 
 
the fire central, placement of staff distributed throughout the nursing home and the specific 
tasks delegated to each staff will all influence the evacuation time as well. It is therefore a 
recommendation that an assessment should be made on all existing and future established 
nursing homes, to examine if the building layout, the actual staff to resident ratio and how 
well the staff is trained, can give an ESL >1.

nursing homes and in hospitals.

according to pre-evacuation time.

in staff facilities.

Simulation of pre-evacuation and evacuation of an as-built nursing home assessed 
with the number of staff available at the specific site.

Real time exercises of pre-evacuation and evacuation of an as-built nursing home

Study of pre-evacuation time including the tasks and role of the staff.

Appendix A 
Types of occupants  
It is necessary to have a high focus on residents who cannot save themselves by their own 
means and who therefore require help from staff or other responsible persons. The starting 
point for the evacuation strategy is therefore the category of use of the building. The 
categories of use are based on several criteria relating to fire risk conditions. These criteria 
include the consequence of a fire, including the number of people who may be affected by a 
fire and the mobility of individuals, as well as the ability to recognize and respond to a fire.  
 
Category 1 includes a building or sections of a building for daytime stay where persons 
normally in the building section are all familiar with the escape routes of the building section 
and can bring themselves to safety on their own, typically offices and industrial buildings  
 
Category 2 includes a building or sections of a building for daytime stay with few persons per 
room, where the persons staying in the building section does not necessarily have 
knowledge of the escape routes of the building section, but are able to bring themselves to 
safety on their own, typically schools.  
 
Category 3 includes a building or sections of a building for daytime stay for many persons, 
where the persons staying in the building section do not necessarily have knowledge of the 
escape routes of the building section, but are able to bring themselves to safety on their 
own, typically shopping malls.  
 
Category 4 includes a building or sections of a building for day and nighttime stays where 
persons staying in the building section are aware of the escape routes of the building section 
and can bring themselves to safety on their own, typically housing.  
 
Category 5 includes a building or sections of a building for  day and nighttime stays where 
the persons staying in the building section are not aware of the escape routes of the building 
section, but are able to bring themselves to safety on their own, typically hotels.  
 
Category 6 includes a building or sections of a building for daytime stays and, where 
appropriate, nighttime stays where persons staying in the building section are not able to 
bring themselves to safety on their own, typically hospitals. 
 
Nursing homes will be placed under category 6. This will have a great impact on the 
building’s design, while the category defines the fire safety level, regarding the prescriptive 
requirements. This will typically be seen in the demand for smaller fire compartments, 
shorter escape routes, and more fire technical installations.

Appendix B 
Alarming the fire brigade 
There are 3 emergency dispatch services in Denmark: The Emergency Dispatch Service for 
Greater Copenhagen and the Danish National Police Emergency Center in Aarhus and in 
Slagelse. It's one of these three emergency services you will speak to first, when you in 
Denmark dial 1-1-2 for an emergency. The emergency services for Greater Copenhagen are 
operated by the Greater Copenhagen fire brigade. 
 
An ABA alarm will be sent directly to the emergency dispatch services, which then sends the 
nearest fire brigade to the building where the ABA central has been activated. 
 
In the bigger cities of Denmark, the fire brigade has fulltime fire fighters at the fire station. 
This means that the fire brigade can leave the fire station within the first minute. In the minor 
cities, or municipalities it is normally part-time firefighters. The requirement here, is that they 
must leave the fire station within 5 minutes. The response time can be from 15-20 and up to 
30 minutes [34] [16] [17] from the sound of the alarm to the farthest point, measured on 
driving time from the fire station.  
 
The 1st. deployment from the stations vary from 1 team leader and 3 firefighters, to 1 team 
leader and 7 firefighters [16] [17].  The variation of the fire crew is typically geographically 
grounded due to the local community composition and population density of the local area 
where the fire station is located.  
 
It is also different from municipality to municipality how big a fire crew will be sent to mitigate 
an ABA alarm. For example, Aarhus municipality will go through a specific risk assessment 
for each building that is fitted with an automatic fire detection system. In the municipality of 
Odense, the fire brigade will send 2 team leaders and 8 fire fighters from 2 different fire 
stations to mitigate an activated ABA central at Odense University hospital. In the 
municipality of Assens, Funen, there will be sent 1 team leader and 3 fire fighters to an 
activated fire central in for example a nursing home [15]. 
 
If the fire trucks must drive 20 minutes until they reach the destination and can observe 
smoke and/or fire and on that background needs assistance, the assistance can again be up 
to 20 minutes away. There can also be situations, when the fire brigade is busy on other 
acute assignments, where the fire brigade´s 2nd deployment must be sent, with a possible 
further delay.  
 
The role of the fire brigade during a first response 
Occupants in a burning building can be threatened by the fire on different levels. It is 
necessary to organize the 1st. response properly according to the threat level of the 
occupants trapped inside. [14] In a 1st. response the incident leader graduates the occupants 
within a building on fire after 3 threat levels: 
 
 
 
==PAGE:53==
 
 
 
53 
Class 1:  
Persons that are directly threatened by flames or smoke and cannot by own means leave 
the premises. These persons must be rescued immediately. 
 
Class 2:  
Persons that with the spread of fire can be threatened by flames or smoke but will be able by 
their own means leave the premises. These persons must as soon as possible be 
evacuated. 
 
Class 3:  
Persons that is not and will not be threatened by flames or smoke but in some way can be 
affected by the fire. These persons need to be informed by the situation. 
 
The most important work for the fire crew when they arrive is to rescue occupants (class 1) 
that are directly threatened by fire.  This will always have 1st. priority for the incident leader 
and the fire crew.  
 
Then the Class 2 and last class 3 persons will be prioritized. During the education and 
training of the fire fighter team leaders and incident command leaders, it is taught that the 
staff and/or the police must if possible, handle class 2 and 3 persons during an incident.
