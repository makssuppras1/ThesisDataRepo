Summary
The goal of the thesis is to create a mix design for a pervious concrete that can
withstand freeze-thaw cycles, and to create a mix design for a self-compacting
pervious concrete. The freeze-thaw resistant pervious concrete will be designed
with the use of air entraining agents, and other necessary chemical admixtures.
The freeze-thaw resistance will be measured by the change in the E-modulus
of the material, based on the use of operational modal analysis, after exposing
specimens to 56 freeze-thaw cycles.
The self-compacting pervious concrete will be created for use in the production
of pervious concrete soakaways, made at IBF Ringe. In order for the mix design
to be an improvement over the current, the fresh concrete has to have increased
workability, with the goal of decreasing the amount of manual labor in the
production of the soakaways. This will be done with the use of superplasticizers,
and other chemical admixtures necessary for the concrete to retain its strength
and permeability.

Resumé
Målet for denne afhandling er at skabe en drænbeton der kan modstå tø/frost
cykler, samt at skabe en drænbeton der er selvkompakterende. Den frostsikre
drænbeton vil blive skabt ved at eksperimentere med luftindblandingsmidler, og

standighed vil blive vurderet ved at udsætte betonbjælker for 56 frost-tø cykler,

være en forbedring i forhold til den nuværende, skal den friske beton være mere
ﬂydende, og dermed nedbringe mængden af manuelt arbejde i forbindelse med
produktionen, uden at gå på kompromis med kvaliteten af brøndene. Dette mål
vil blive opnået ved hjælp af superplastiﬁcerende tilsætningsstoﬀer, og andre
kemiske tilsætninger der vil kunne forbedre recepten.

Preface
This thesis was prepared at DTU BYG, by M.Sc. student Anders Hedegaard
Jensen, representing 32,5 ECTS points.
This thesis concerns pervious concrete, which is a type of concrete that allows
water to ﬂow through by having a large void ratio. In the thesis it is attempted
to create a freeze-thaw resistant pervious concrete mix design and to create a
mix design for a self-compacting pervious concrete. Both design properties will
be achieved through the use of chemical additives and speciﬁc aggregates.
The thesis consists of a literature study, and the analysis and discussion of data
collected through experiments carried out at the lab for material testing at DTU.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter gives an introduction to both the properties and application of
PCPC, as well as the goal with this project. By the end of the chapter, it will
be clear what the main challenges with PCPC are, and how this thesis will be
dealing with them through practical tests.
1.1
Pervious concrete
Portland Cement Pervious Concrete (PCPC) is a type of gap graded concrete
with little or no medium and ﬁne-sized aggregates, creating a large amount
of interconnected voids throughout the material. The large size of the voids,
and the amount of them, give PCPC its deﬁning characteristic, a very high
permeability.
The mix design for this type of concrete has a lower cement
content than regular concrete, with an amount between 200 and 300 kg/m3.
In order to obtain some compression strength despite the lack of cement and
ﬁne aggregates, the w/c-ratio is kept low. This leads to the fresh concrete being
characterized by its low workability, that is similar to moist gravel in consistency
[1].
Properties such as strength and permeability can be varied depending on what
use it is intended for. The compression strength of PCPC ranges from 4-28

MPa, however this is strongly dependent on the void ratio. Typically, the void
ratio ranges from 15-25 %. The permeability, which is also very dependent on
the void ratio, is typically in the range 2-50 mm/s [1, 2].
More detailed information about PCPC will be shown in the literature study in
Chapter 2.
Currently, some signiﬁcant areas within PCPC-research remain relatively unex-
plored. Two of these areas, improvement of frost resistance and the workability
of self-compacting PCPC (SCCPC), will be explored separately in this thesis.
1.2
Application of pervious concrete
Denmark is currently facing a massive challenge in coping with climate change.
As rain frequency and intensity increases, the current drainage systems will
increasingly be overburdened, resulting in billions worth of damages, at great
cost to society. This has created attention to what measures and novel solutions
that can be implemented to avoid local ﬂooding [3, 4].
The use of PCPC as a stormwater management system has been gaining traction
in the US during the last 20 years, and its potential in other countries is now
slowly being realized [5]. Applications of PCPC include parking lots, sidewalks,
gabions and soakaways, all with the aim of reducing stormwater runoﬀinto the
sewage system, thus avoiding local ﬂooding and at the same time recharging
groundwater [1].
Here, PCPC functions by being completely permeable, al-
lowing the water to be led directly into the ground instead of into the highly
overloaded sewer systems. On a short term basis, heavy downpours can even be
temporarily stored in the voids of the concrete.
One of the biggest advantages of PCPC for stormwater management, is the cost
of the systems compared to other solutions. As the technology is fairly simple
and the concrete is placed using simple equipment such as the roller screed, the
cost can be kept low compared to other systems, and the practical limitations
are few. This is something that has been realized by Ikast-Brande Municipality
and IBF, leading to a series of projects using PCPC soakaways in high-risk
ﬂooding areas, so far with great success [6].
Experience in working with pervious concrete represents one of the largest hur-
dles, both for engineers and craftsmen, as the working procedures are quite
diﬀerent from regular concrete. Another challenge in the practical use of PCPC
is the quality assurance. A positive result of any casting is dependent on the

quality of the fresh concrete, which changes fairly quickly with PCPC. Several
crucial properties can be aﬀected by the transportation process, as the vibration
in the concrete mixer-truck can lower the air-content and the quick hydration
process of PCPC will reduce workability faster than for regular concrete. To
solve this issue and secure a predictable performance, a simple standard method
should be developed that can be used in the ﬁeld for a quick evaluation of PCPC,
in order to secure conﬁdence in the quality of the product. This will not be
considered in the present study, but is relevant to the current situation with
pervious concrete.
As research continues, PCPC can be expected to ﬁnd its use in a variety of
segments in the building industry. Even though its use as tool in the stormwater
management is the most widespread and most obvious, there are many other
possibilities to be explored and utilized with PCPC. Current applications apart
from diﬀerent forms of pavement include noise barriers, load bearing walls and
hydraulic structures [1].
1.3
Freeze-thaw resistance of pervious concrete
As with most concrete, yearly freeze-thaw cycles is one of the most abrasive and
deteriorating forces pervious concrete in mild climate countries can subjected
to, and will lead to raveling and a reduction of strength.
As PCPC is most often used as diﬀerent types of pavements, from walk paths to
parking lots, it will inevitably be subjected to the climate in which it is placed.
If it is to be considered a viable alternative to regular concrete or asphalt,
the lifespan of the PCPC must be extended by ensuring a solid freeze-thaw
resistance. The goal must then be for the material to retain its strength and
structural integrity, during freeze-thaw cycling.
Current research in PCPC placed in cold weather-areas with yearly freeze-thaw
cycles is limited to observations of the condition and performance of existing
placements, such as in the case-study in "Freeze-thaw resistance of Pervious
Concrete" by NRMCA, and experiments using methods developed for regular
concrete [7]. The use of this type of methods creates a false image of the freeze-
thaw resistance of PCPC, something that will be made an attempt to correct
in this project. The methods are often adapted from regular concrete, without
taking the draining properties of pervious concrete into account. The perme-
ability of pervious concrete allows the water to drain oﬀthe concrete, making a
submerged PCPC specimen exposed to freeze-thaw cycling a misrepresentation
of the actual conditions the material will be subjected to.

1.4
Pervious concrete soakaways from IBF
This project is carried out partly in collaboration with Ikast Betonvarefabrik
A/S (IBF). The family-owned company has been producing paving blocks since
1960, and today produces more than 100 diﬀerent shapes and types in 19 fac-
tories across Denmark. IBF also has a large production of other concrete prod-
ucts, mainly concrete sewage- and stormwater pipes. It is in this ﬁeld that their
R&D-section, led by Chief Product Oﬃcer Esben Mølgaard, has started the
development and production of PCPC soakaways, speciﬁcally for the use in the
expanding ﬁeld of stormwater management.
In collaboration with the Ikast-Brande municipality in Jutland, Denmark, IBF
has already installed several soakaways, which have proven to reduce ﬂooding,
and are expected to become the cheapest solution for the municipality in sim-
ilar situations [8]. In this project, IBF will supply aggregates from their own
production, which will make it possible to replicate the actual conditions from
the factory in the DTU lab.
1.4.1
Description of PCPC soakaways
The size of the soakaway-sections is an internal diameter of 60 or 125 cm, with
a wall thickness of 10 cm. The height is either 50, 60 or 100 cm. The soakaway-
bottoms, which have a solid concrete slab at the foot of the cylinder, are cast
in heights up to 225 cm. Permeability of the soakaways is 4 mm/s, as measured
in-house by IBF.
The use of the soakaways is as gabions, as mentioned earlier. In practice, this
means digging a hole for the soakaways and either stacking the elements verti-
cally in the ground, or to lay them horizontally. When stacking them vertically,
the soakaway bottoms have a solid concrete plate in the bottom, working as the
foundation for the stack. Water can then be led to the soakaway, where it is
be stored until it can seep into the ground. During heavy rain the surrounding
soil will be saturated, and the water can ﬂow the other way, ﬁlling the soak-
aways with water. This means the soakaways will work as a buﬀer, as well as a
temporary basin for some of the rainwater to be stored.
Figure 1.1 shows the principle for how the soakaways are installed, in this case
under a road [9].
In this project, PCPC mix designs made for these soakaways will not be sub-
jected to freeze-thaw cycles. Their current use means that they will be placed

1.4.2
Challenges with production of PCPC soakaways
In a meeting at IBF Ringe, IBF’s practical challenges in their production of
PCPC soakaways was discussed. Figure 1.2 shows sections of these PCPC soak-
aways stored at the IBF factory in Ringe.
When casting regular concrete sewer pipes, the casting mold can be removed
almost instantly, after vibrating the cylinder, making the production process
fairly quick. The pipes are usually dry-cast, meaning that the w/c-ratio is at or
below 0, 35. This reduces the workability, but has the advantage of needing no
curing before the removal of the formwork, even for 3 meter tall cylinders.
The w/c ratio of the PCPC soakaways is also low, however the concrete does
not compact very well when vibrated, due to the lack of ﬁne- and medium sized
particles. This creates a very low ability to carry its own weight before curing,
resulting in casting heights of maximum 1 meter when removing the formwork
immediately.

Another important point in the challenges with the production of PCPC soak-
aways is the amount of manpower needed in the process. In a modern factory,
most manual labor is alleviated by the use of mechanical aids, in order to save
time and money, as well as the health of the workers.
In order to compact the taller soakaways, such as the soakaway bottoms, hand-
held vibrators are used for about 5 minutes per soakaway.
This should be
avoided, both due to noise and the risk of "Hand-Arm Vibration Syndrome".
Since the workability of regular PCPC is very low, it is also impossible to achieve
a homogeneous compaction, which aﬀects the properties of the soakaway, and
there is a general increase in the workload on the workers.
The challenges IBF are facing when casting pervious concrete soakaways can be
summed up in the following points:
• Lower strength and compaction of PCPC compared to conventional con-
crete, leading to requirements for extended curing. (2-3 days in mold)
• Low casting height of soakaways when demolding immediately after cast-
ing.
• Diﬃculty vibrating, leading to variation in permeability and void ratio.
• Too much manual labor required for shoveling, vibrating, etc., increasing
production costs.
These challenges are believed to be overcome by the use of an SCCPC instead
of a PCPC in the production of permeable soakaways.
The following goals have been identiﬁed as achievable by the design of an SCCPC
for use in the casting of PCPC soakaways:
• No vibration required when casting, with predictable strength and void
ratio.
• Casting can be made in one single pour, i.e.
no pauses for curing or
vibrating.
• An improvement in the general workability of the concrete, leading to an
increase in homogeneity of the soakaways, and reduction of manual labor
needed when casting.
If these points can be fulﬁlled, a more streamlined production would be possible,
making the mix design an upgrade over the current.

1.5
Problem statement
The purpose of this project is to design two types of PCPC; one that is freeze-
thaw resistant for use in the Danish climate, and one that is self-compacting.
The freeze-thaw resistant PCPC is based on frost resistant aggregate supplied
by DTU, and the SCCPC is made with aggregates supplied by IBF.
Testing of the freeze-thaw resistance will be performed using the standard mass-
loss method, as well as Operational Modal Analysis, whereby the reduction of
the dynamic E-modulus in the samples can be measured non-destructively.
Fifteen samples will be tested, where the amount air-entraining agent (AEA) is
varied from 5 to 7% of the cement weight, with a reference sample of 0%, in order
to create an air content of around 20% in the cementpaste (Kitmasse). The test
will be made by soaking the samples in a 3% NaCl solution for 72 hours, before
letting them drip oﬀand wrapping them in plastic. The specimens will then
be subjected to 56 freeze-thaw cycles, or as many as possible before complete
breakdown of the specimen.
The design of the SCCPC will be aimed towards use in the permeable soakaways,
that are currently in production at IBF, in order to cast them quicker and in
taller sections. The mix will be made with the aggregates supplied by IBF, and
the properties of the concrete will be adjusted with the use of varying dosages
of superplasticizers, viscosity-modifying agents, and other chemical admixtures.
The level of self-compacting is documented by using a ﬂow table test, and by
visual evaluation of how well the concrete ﬁlls out the test cylinders.
Both types of PCPC will be designed using knowledge obtained from the refer-
enced literature, and reﬁned and optimized by laboratory testing in the facilities
at DTU.

four points:
1. Must be pervious
2. Must ﬂow freely from a slump cone
3. Must have little or no paste draining
4. Must have design density achieved with little or no compacting.
The tests in the paper ﬁnd that frost resistance of the mix is increased greatly
with a w/c-ratio of 0.32 compared to one with a w/c-ratio of 0.25. Frost resis-

viscosity means a more ﬂuid mix, and the yield stress is the minimum stress to
initiate or maintain ﬂow. Low viscosity and low yield stress is often correlated,
and is desirable in a self-compacting concrete.
In order to make a SCCPC some methods are mentioned. Firstly, the aggregate
gradation is mentioned, where it is shown that the yield stress and viscosity
increases signiﬁcantly with a 7 % replacement of coarse aggregate with sand.
Sand is good for creating a thicker layer of paste on the aggregates due to the
increase of yield stress, but the change in rheology must be accounted for by
other means.
Secondly, the use of diﬀerent cementious materials is mentioned, including ﬂy
ash, silica fume and slag. Fly ash is said to reduce yield stress, but not aﬀect
viscosity, slag will increase viscosity but not aﬀect yield stress, and micro silica
will increase the viscosity and yield stress at a replacement rate over 5 %. Lastly,
the mentioned chemical admixtures are high-range water-reducers (HRWR),
viscosity modifying agents (VMA) and hydration stabilizers (HS), where the
lowest viscosity is achieved with a combination of HRWR and VMA.

4.1.2
Freeze-thaw cycling method
In order to simulate the annual freeze-thaw cycles the concrete needs to endure,
a procedure will be developed based on existing methods for regular concrete
and natural stone. How many cycles are needed to produce good results, and
how the specimens will be treated before and during the experiment will be
detailed here.
This stage is important, since earlier experiments have often
used methods developed for regular concrete, leading to more negative results
than would be obtained in more realistic scenarios. Mainly, the negative results
are achieved by submerging the samples completely in water during freeze-thaw
cycling, where PCPC has the feature of draining itself of water, giving an indirect
improvement to the freeze-thaw resistance. This is not reﬂected in results based
on those methods, resulting in misleading conclusions on the performance of
PCPC.
According to DS/EN 1338, the Eurocode standard for requirements and testing
methods for paving blocks, only 28 cycles are recommended to be used, after
which the material that has scaled oﬀcan be weighed, and then compared
to other samples [17]. The freeze-thaw cycle from this standard follows a strict
curve over 24 hours, where the core temperature goes from 20◦C down to -18◦C,
a similar principle to several other standards. This method requires temperature
adjusting equipment which is not available to this project.
Figure 4.2 shows the temperature curve that would have to be followed to fulﬁll
the requirements of the standard.

been made for freeze-thaw testing PCPC, a method will be speciﬁed with the
aforementioned standards as a starting point. Factors that are also taken into
account are the practical limitations of the equipment, as well as the given time
for this project.

4.1.3
Measuring frost-damage
In the ﬁeld, frost damage on concrete structures is always evaluated on a visual
basis ﬁrst.
Then, destructive methods such as core-drilling, or less accurate
non-destructive methods can be employed, leading to an estimation of the state
of the concrete. In the lab however, there are several possibilities to measure
the exact damage to the concrete.
The deterioration of the concrete due to freeze-thaw cycling is determined by
recording the change in natural frequency after every 7 cycles, which is then
used to calculate the change in the E-modulus.

4.1.3.1
Dynamic E-modulus
In order to get a more accurate assessment of the change in the properties of
the material, the dynamic E-modulus, written as EDY N, can be measured using
the natural response frequency of the samples. This method allows for eﬀect
of the internal damage and smaller cracks to be evaluated, by measuring the
reduction of the stiﬀness in the material.
The E-modulus is the ratio between stress and strain in a material, which in
layman’s terms is called the stiﬀness. Using Hooke’s law, the E-modulus can be
deﬁned:
E = σ
ϵ
(4.1)
The dynamic E-modulus, or the dynamic modulus of elasticity, diﬀers from the
static modulus of elasticity Es by the method of measurement. The static E-
modulus is deﬁned as the ratio of axial stress to strain by applying a uni-axial
load to a sample of the material. In practice, this is done by attaching strain-
gauges to a concrete cylinder, and applying an increasing force, and comparing
the strain and the stress as both increases.
For the dynamic E-modulus, a dynamic excitation is used to measure the ve-
locity with which wave propagates through the material, and calculating the
stiﬀness based the resonance frequencies of the samples. The method was de-
veloped in order to create a non-destructive test for in-situ structures, but has
shown to be eﬀective in lab-settings as well [20].
The two values diﬀer by EDY N being larger than Es. The ratio between the
two is not ﬁxed, as it depends on factors such as the density, amount of cement
and the strength of the concrete. Several other conversion formulas have been
proposed for diﬀerent types of concrete and mix-types [21].
However, there are too many factors to consider for such a conversion to be com-
pletely accurate, and it is mostly used as an indication of the relation between
the values.
The diﬀerence between the moduli stems from the fact that concrete has a non-
linear stress/strain relation, even though it is usually estimated to be linear,
which is only true for low stresses [21]. In this case, the vibrations that are used
to measure EDY N will apply a very low stress on the material compared to the
method used to measure Es, resulting in a higher stiﬀness measured for EDY N.

4.2
Operational Modal Analysis
Operational Modal Analysis (OMA), is a type of modal analysis that can be
used when the input forces are unknown. Using a Fourier transformation, ac-
celerations measured at speciﬁc points in a structure can be used to analyse the
dynamic properties of the structure, such as the natural frequencies and mode
shapes.
The current methods of determining the dynamic E-modulus by measuring the
resonance frequencies of concrete to detect freeze-thaw damage, are deﬁned by
standards such as ASTM C215 and EN 14146. Some methods require controlling
the input and then measuring the output of vibrations. Other methods require
using a hammer to induce vibrations manually, creating uncertainties in such a
coarse material as PCPC.
The method proposed in this section excels by not requiring any speciﬁc force to
be excited on the specimen, but rather a random input created on the surface.
Also, the potential accuracy in the very precise equipment is a big advantage.
In this case, a steel brush being dragged over the surfaces of the sample creates
enough vibrational force to be picked up by the sensors.

6.1
Admixture optimization
The addition of a superplasticizers is central to creating a self-compacting con-
crete mix, and is oftentimes the only admixture needed to create the self-
compacting eﬀect.
Other times, stabilizers also needs to be added, in order
to avoid segregation.
In regular concrete, segregation of the aggregates happen when the cement
paste becomes so viscous, that the larger aggregates cannot be suspended evenly
throughout the mix, and they will sink to the bottom. In PCPC, segregation
happens the other way around, due to the larger aggregates being the main
component in the mix. As the cement paste becomes more viscous, it will coat
the aggregates with a thinner layer of paste, and most of the cement will drain
==PAGE:68==
52
Design of self-compacting pervious concrete
to the bottom of the mold. Segregation can be induced by either over-vibration,
or by the addition of too much plasticizer.
With regular concrete, stabilizers are used to create a homogeneous mix by
counteracting the side eﬀects from the plasticizers. In this project, the same
will be attempted. Again, the necessary amount of plasticizer and stabilizer
will be determined by a trial and error-approach.
6.1.1
Determining superplasticizer dosage
In the ﬁrst test with superplasticizer, an amount equal to 1% of the cement
weight was used, as the amount recommended in the data sheet is between
0.2 and 3%. In this case, this amount is too high, as it resulted in complete
segregation between the paste and the aggregates.
As seen in Figure 6.1 b), nearly all of the paste has drained oﬀthe aggregates,
and has pooled in the bottom of the cylinder. Figure 6.1 a) shows the fresh
concrete, and how the cement paste runs completely oﬀthe aggregates. This is
obviously not very favorable, as it both seals the bottom of the cylinder, and
decreases the compression strength dramatically, since there is no cement paste
to bind the aggregates together in most of the cylinder.

a PCPC that is freeze-thaw resistant, by the use of AEA’s. Furthermore, it
is shown that the estimated limit of 20% entrained air in the cement paste is
an accurate value to aim for, when the goal is to make a PCPC freeze-thaw
resistant.

Mass loss
As Figure 8.11 shows, mass loss of the samples follow the trend from the pro-

is low enough that it can be considered negligible.
The small mass-loss is interesting, when considering the rather dramatic change
in the E-modulus for some of the samples. This indicates the damage in the
concrete for the most part is invisible, despite being very signiﬁcant.
The mass loss is mostly evident as surface scaling, where the paste is slightly
deteriorating. Some aggregates on the edges and corners of the specimens have
also broken oﬀ, but no major damage is visible after 56 cycles. The before and
after images in Figure 8.12 show the extent of the damage, which is very similar
for all the specimens within each group.

9.1.3
Mechanical properties
The compression strength of any type of concrete is a critical property, as it will
be decisive in what applications the concrete can have.
As seen in Figure 8.13, there is an increase of strength in the samples with
added AEA. This is likely due to the cementpaste coating the aggregates better,
creating a stronger connection in the contact-points between the aggregates.
The compression strength of 13 to 16 MPa is right in the middle of the normal
range for PCPC [1].
9.1.3.1
Eﬀect of stabilizer
Figure 8.7 shows the eﬀect of varying doses of stabilizer on the compression
strength, when the amount of AEA is set to 3%.
There is little diﬀerence
between the samples. It is very likely that the coating of cement paste on the
aggregates roguhly remains the same, as more stabilizer is added. This can be
due to the AEA already creating a good coating. The addition of stabilizer
then lowers the amount of entrained air in the paste, which has a negative eﬀect
on the coating, but then in turn makes the cement paste thicker, which has a
positive eﬀect on the coating.
9.1.3.2
Void ratio
As expected, based on the existing literature, the compression strength of all
the castings showed a linear correlation with the measured void ratio [11] [10].
Void ratio is found based on an extra cylinder and beam that was cast along
with the rest of the batch, and the average between the two is used for that
speciﬁc mix design. As seen in Figure 8.16, the compression strength is highly
dependent on the void ratio, and thus the compaction of the casting.
The ﬁgure also shows that the more AEA in the mix, the lower the void ratio is.
This is partly due to the cementpaste increasing in volume due to the entrained
==PAGE:106==
90

air, reducing the void-space between the aggregates. PA7 has a void-ratio of
19%, where PA5 and PA0 has void ratios of 21 and 26% respectively.
This is an important note, as an increase in frost-resistance comes at the cost of
a lower permeability. Another factor contributing to the lower void ratio is the
fact that the workability increases with larger amounts of entrained air, leading
to the concrete being more compact.
9.1.3.3
Fracture patterns
All cylinders had a cone-shaped fracture pattern, which is accepted as a good
standard fracture.
When looking closer at the destroyed samples in Figure 8.15, it can be noticed
that the fracture pattern travels through both the paste and the aggregates.
The fracture through the aggregates is a sign that the amount of cement used
is suﬃcient, as it is not always the weakest part of this concrete.
There is
a tendency towards fracture through the aggregates in the higher strengths.
The lower strength cylinders usually break at the contact point between the
aggregates, where the cementpaste binds the rocks together.
9.2
Method evaluation
In this section, some of the methods used in this study will be evaluated and
discussed.
9.2.0.1
OMA measurements
One of the error sources in the OMA is the base used for resting the specimen
on when measuring the vibrations. The base used is a polyurethane foam which
should reduce noise from the surroundings, and allow the specimen to bend
freely in all directions. In Figure 8.17 the E-modulus has been calculated based
on the 1st and 2nd bending mode, which should result in the same values since
the material is homogeneous, as the calculation takes the bending direction
into account. However, there is a slight diﬀerence between the two modes, in
all three calculation methods. The Blevins method gives a lower value for the
second mode, where the other two methods both result in a higher E-modulus.
==PAGE:107==
9.2 Method evaluation
91
If foam hinders the sideways movement required for bending in the second mode,
then the E-modulus should rise. If the downwards movement is hindered, then
the E-modulus should be lower, when using the second mode. The result seems
to be inconclusive, since the diﬀerence between the two is very small, and three
diﬀerent methods provide varying results.
The measurement of the dynamic E-modulus performed with OMA proved to be
very accurate, with very few problems. On the positive side, the method is very
simple from a user standpoint, and the procedure is learned very quickly. Issues
with the method is mostly that it is time consuming to perform the measure-
ments, and glue the plastic holders onto the specimens. Also, the software used
in this project is expensive, but it is possible to program a simpler software to
perform the analysis. Lastly, there is an issue with making the measurements,
as the specimens have to be removed from the plastic bag, causing evaporation
of the water. This issue could and should be solved in the future.
9.2.0.2
Preparation of specimens for freeze-thaw cycles
Another method used for this experiment, that has a noticeable eﬀect on the
result, is the method of ﬁrst saturating the samples, and then wrapping them
in plastic. The intention is to mimic the actual conditions the PCPC would
be exposed to, where the water can freely drain through the material. This,
as opposed to the method used for regular concrete, where the specimens are
submerged while being frozen, has shown to have a large eﬀect in the beginning
of the experiment, and a decreasing eﬀect in the following freeze-thaw cycles.
The very noticeable halt in the deterioration in Figure 8.9 shows that the method
needs to be improved. The idea is that no water will be lost from the ﬁrst cycle
to the last, meaning that the samples would be saturated throughout the test.
In reality however, some water will evaporate every time the specimens are taken
out to be measured.
Furthermore, the micro cracks created by the expanding water in the beginning
of the test will alleviate the stress created when the water expands again. To
improve the method, the specimens should be saturated after each measurement,
or at certain points in the process, in order to deteriorate the PCPC more than
what was seen here.

9.3.1
Workability
The main point of the mix design was to create a more workable PCPC, without
compromising on the quality of the ﬁnal result. This was goal was fulﬁlled, as

The workability of the concrete can be measured by the ﬂow table test, but is
mostly evaluated based on experience. Figure 8.21 shows the diﬀerence in the

with PCPC. There is also little correlation between the amount of plasticizer

though the materials, admixtures and mix designs were the same. At the IBF

achieve a good ﬂow.
9.3.1.1

The casting height test showed that the is a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the compaction
of the concrete when pouring the fresh concrete from diﬀerent heights.

Figure 8.23 shows that the higher the concrete has dropped, the higher the
compresseive strength will be. This is a result of the higher level of compaction,
which leads to a denser concrete. The density has a linear correlation with the
compression strength, as shown in both Figure 8.24 from this ecperiment and

When pouring the concrete from the edge of the cylinder, there is a clear ten-

Eﬀect of plasticizer
Figure 8.22 shows the correlation between compression strength and the use of
plasticizer. The ﬁrst mix PP01, contains 0,1% of plasticizer, and has a compres-
sion strength of almost 6 MPa. The mixes with 0,3 and 0,5% have a compression
strength of 9 and 8 MPa respectively, showing an increase with the use of plas-
ticizer.
In these tests there was no compaction used at all, which most likely is the cause
of the unexpected rise in compression strength from PP01 to PP03. As PP03
ad PP05 had more plasticizer, there was a tendency towards better compaction,
leading to a higher density. As shown previously in Figure 8.16, the void ratio,
and thus the density, has a very large eﬀect on the compression strength. Fur-
thermore, the use of plasticizer in this range did not result in paste drain down,
leading to a good coating of the aggregates, again resulting in a higher strength.
PP1, with 1% EVO36, had a predictably low strength, as nearly all the cement
paste had drained to the bottom of the cylinder, resulting in very little cohesion
between the aggregates.

Conclusions
The following points can be concluded from the experiment with freeze-thaw
resistant PCPC.
1. It is possible, by the use of AEA’s, to create a freeze-thaw resistant PCPC
2. The project has shown that an air-content in the cement paste of at least
20% is necessary to achieve a good freeze-thaw resistance.
3. When using large amount of AEA’s, it is necessary to use stabilizer in
order to avoid drain down of the cement paste.
4. Freeze-thaw cycles will lower the E-modulus of PCPC signiﬁcantly, if the
cement paste has less than 20% entrained air.
5. A mix design for a PCPC with no AEA added, will lose over 50% of its
relative dynamic E-modulus after 56 freeze-thaw cycles, compared with
only 2% for a mix design with 7% AEA BWC added.
6. Poisson’s ratio is found to be 0.159, 0.160 and 0.178 for PA0, PA5 and
PA7, before starting the freeze-thaw cycles, which is in line with existing
literature.
7. Operational Modal Analysis is an accurate method for dynamic modulus
of elasticity of the PCPC specimens, providing a very accurate result with
a non-destructive approach.
==PAGE:112==
96
Conclusions
The following points can be concluded from the SCCPC experiment.
1. With a combination of VMA and a HRWR, it is possible to greatly increase
the workability of PCPC, to create an SCCPC.
2. By using a combination of VMA and HRWR in the mix design, the homo-
geneity of the casting will improve compared to casting soakaways with
standard PCPC.
3. It is necessary to use a VMA, in order to achieve a good coating of the
cement paste on the aggregates
4. The process of casting a pervious concrete soakaway is greatly improved
with the use of an SCCPC, as less manual labor is needed, and both the
strength and homogeneity of the ﬁnal product is improved.
5. The soakaway cast at IBF using the mix design created in this project lived
up to the demands of the quality control at IBF, leading to the conclusion
that the design of an SCCPC was a success.
10.1
Casting at IBF
The casting at IBF resulted in a perfectly cast soakaway, showing promise for
the SCCPC mix design. Some adjustments had to be made, but the principles
with the mix of stabilizer and superplasticizer worked well. The mix design
will likely be incorporated in the production of this type of well, leading to the
conclusion that the project, with regards to this purpose, was a success.
10.2
Future research
The following points are thoughts on the possible improvements to this project,
and on future research within the ﬁeld of PCPC.
1. When looking at the diﬀerence between the frost resistance of PA5 and
PA7, it would be beneﬁcial to ﬁgure out why the is such a large diﬀerence
between the two. There could be made more data points with the same
mix design, with between 5 and 7% AEA, to ﬁgure out what the exact
amount of AEA and entrained air needs to be to stay above the limit of
60% remaining RDME.
==PAGE:113==
10.2 Future research
97
2. Another issue with the ﬁnal mix designs is the paste drain down. It should
be researched if it possible to use more stabilizer, without lowering the
amount of entrained air signiﬁcantly.
3. Possible improvements to the method for freeze-thaw cycling, to avoid wa-
ter evaporating from the samples while making measurements. A solution
could be to re-saturate the samples every 7 or 14 cycles.
4. The method of using OMA like it is done in this project is very innovative,
but it needs to be compared with existing methods in order to prove its
eﬀectiveness and accuracy. Furthermore, it could be investigated what
other methods of attaching the accelerometers are possible, in order to
avoid strengthening the samples with glue/epoxy.
5. The freeze-thaw durability is measured solely by the dynamic E-modulus
and mass-loss in this project, so it would be good to test the durability
based on other factors, such as splitting tensile strength or compressive
strength of the samples.

Mixing procedure
Needed equipment: Concrete mixer, mask, safety goggles, ear protection, safety
boots
1. Weigh oﬀall the aggregates, admixtures, water and cement.
2. Pour the largest aggregates into the mixer.
3. Pour about 5% of thecement into the mixer.
4. Pour the sand into the mixer and attach the lid.
5. Start the mixer, while tilting it as much as possible, and let it run for 2
minutes.

A.2
Measuring fresh concrete density
Based on ASTM C1688
Needed equipment: Cylindrical container with 8 liter-capacity, 2,5 kg Proctor
Compaction Hammer, scale for weighing sample

1. Use standard sized-container with volume of 8 liters and ﬁnd its weight.
2. Pour the fresh concrete into the container, until it is half-full.
3. Use the Proctor Hammer to compact the sample by releasing it 20 times,
evenly distributed over the surface of the sample.
4. Pour concrete into the container to ﬁll it 5-10 centimeters above the rim.
5. Repeat (3), but with only 10 drops of the hammer.
6. If the sample is still above the rim, repeat (5). If the sample is below the
rim, pour a little more concrete, and then repeat (5).
7. The concrete should now be level with the rim, with a reproducible level
of compaction. Use a steel rod to jut the surface of the concrete to make
it completely level.
8. Weigh the full container, and subtract the weight of the container itself.
9. Divide the weight of the concrete with the volume of the container, to ﬁnd
the density of the concrete in its fresh state.
