III 
Abstract 
The use of 1D/2D urban flood models have significantly increased in recent years. Due 
to both improved computers and political directives the applications have expanded, 
however, models are still exposed to long computation times, limiting the possible uses 
for e.g. live flood forecasting. This study investigates the possibilities of reducing compu-
tation time by simplifying the 1D network compartment. The overall aim by the simpli-
fication is to achieve fewer calculations while maintaining similar results to the original 
network model. Using a Mike Urban model containing 13851 links and 13447 nodes cov-
ering southern Melbourne, Australia, manual simplification is unfeasible. This goes for 
many networks used by municipalities, distribution- and consulting companies. However, 
manual simplification might be a necessity for solving the tasks. Simplification also de-
pends on the individual user, as guidelines for simplification of urban sewer networks do 
not exist. Additionally, effects of simplification on 1D/2D flood model results are sparsely 
documented. 
Two Python scripts has been developed to improve consistency between users during 
simplification of an urban drainage network. Algorithms for merging pipes below a 
threshold length and delete pipes below a threshold diameter were successfully developed, 
and includes compensation for travel time and volume differences. In total, 17 different 
models were simulated for five different rain events. The results of the 15 simplified 
models were compared to the full model and a validation model, similar to the full model, 
with a different assumption of minimum pipe length. 
By simplifying the network, computation time was decreased from 4 to less than 1.5 
hours. This is more than expected thus it is considered if the number of coupled nodes 
may influence the computation time. The least simplified models showed very good per-
formances regarding flood cover, surface volumes and depths with changes of only ten 
percent, similar to the validation model. The most simplified models, e.g. trimming pipes 
below 600m and merging pipes shorter than 100m, showed changes of up to 50% in the 
spatial flood cover. This can be improved by additional conditions in the code, e.g. pre-
venting removal of nodes in depression and flooded areas. It was found that merging 
pipes from 10-100m length are better than trimming away pipes smaller than 300mm. It 
is therefore recommended to use the merging method before trimming the network.

The automated simplification decrease time and resources spend on simplifying models. 
Furthermore, it preserves consistent simplification between users, which, if properly de-
veloped, leads to fewer errors.  
Considering the large errors in the model results, due to simplification, it is strongly 
recommended to develop an official standard or guideline within this field. It will thereby 
be easier for engineers to assess how much a model can be simplified and still be valid.   
With faster and more accurate 1D/2D urban flood models, the application area expands 
to e.g. real-time flood forecasting, and can improve final solutions to cope with flooding.

1 Introduction 
Urban floods bring high costs to local residents and society in general (Pender & 
Faulkner, 2011; Butler & Davis, 2011; IPCC, 2014). Urban development and cli-
mate changes both affect the extent and frequency of urban flooding (IPCC, 2014; 
European Parliament and of the Council, 2007). Improved planning and manage-
ment can, however, reduce the costs related to flooding (Pender & Faulkner, 2011; 
Butler & Davis, 2011; IPCC, 2014). In recent years, coping with large precipitation 
events has shifted from purely prevention to flood risk management strategies 
(Pender & Faulkner, 2011). It is thereby recognised, that flooding will occur and 
areas vulnerable to flooding can be developed to increase their resilience. Smart use 
of detention basins, wastewater treatment plants, sewer overflows etc. can reduce 
the extent and cost of flooding events significantly and is increasingly prioritized. 
Management of urban sewer systems are, however, a complicated task with many 
unknown variables. Thus, urban drainage models are used to simulate the network 
behaviour under different management strategies (Chow et al., 1988; Butler & 
Davis, 2011). 
1.1 Urban Drainage Models 
Urban drainage models have been used for many years, starting with hand-calcula-
tions of pipe flows in simple systems (Butler & Davis, 2011). As new theories and 
techniques were developed, model complexity increased and with the arrival of the 
computer, many calculations could suddenly be completed in a short amount of 
time, allowing for a higher network complexity. The use of models to predict urban 
flood risks have increased during recent years to improve decision-making in flood 
risk management (Pender & Faulkner, 2011). The increasing use of models is stim-
ulated by political directives, such as the European Directive on Assessment and 
Management of Flood Risk (European Parliament and of the Council, 2007). As 
example, decision-making shall be considered on a basin scale while taking climate 
changes into account (Pender & Faulkner, 2011; European Parliament and of the 
Council, 2007). Since basins are often complex systems, economically feasible solu-
tions to reduce flood risks are not easily found. Computer models enable low-cost 
testing of numerous solutions in the chase to find the most feasible solution. These 
models are especially utilized in urban areas for flood protection and planning,

where pipe and basin dimensions have large impacts on the system. Initially, one-
dimensional (1D) models were developed for the sewage systems, in order to find 
areas with undersized pipes and test new sewage infrastructure. Later, a one-di-
mensional surface model was added (1D/1D) to simulate overland flow between 
manholes. This model type can be used for prediction of flood depths at manholes, 
and approximate areas with high flood risks (DANVA, 2011). As the surface models 
developed, it has become possible to couple the 1D network model with a two-
dimensional surface model (1D/2D), hence allowing water to flow freely on the 
surface and not only between manholes. It is thereby possible to predict which areas 
have a higher risk of flooding as well as the higher economic cost. The models are 
used for dimensioning sewage and rainwater systems with high spatial resolution 
that have resulted in more effective flood warnings and targeted protection prior to 
flooding. As stated by the European Parliament and Council (2007), real-time flood 
forecasting and early warning systems shall be addressed in the flood risk manage-
ment plans. With 1D /2D models currently the best method for flood estimation, 
there is a high interest of adapting these models for real-time forecasting and flood 
warning. With a forecast of 2-5 hours, warnings of future flooding events to e.g. 
society and utility companies can be sufficient to prepare sewage systems or imple-
ment targeted preventive strategies. This eventually results in decreased costs of 
flooding for citizens and society.

to neglecting terms for backwater effects. It is, however, necessary to include back-
water effects in an urban flood model, in order to properly describe pressure and 
flows in the system. This, combined with large 1D/2D models, results in long com-
putation time and high demand of computational power (DANVA, 2011). There 
are multiple methods to obtain a reduction in computational time including use of 
smaller model areas or surrogate models (DANVA, 2011; Thrysøe & Toke, 2015; 
Butler & Davis, 2011). However, using a smaller model area is not always an option 
and surrogate models are still in an early stage of development for 1D/2D flood 
models (Thrysøe & Toke, 2015). Additionally, a common practice is to simplify the 
network (Wastewater Planning Users Group, 2002; Leitão et al., 2010). Selecting 
elements for deletion by model attributes is an ineffective method and chances of 
errors are high. Conducted manually, this requires a significant amount of time and 
decisions taken by the individual engineer (Wastewater Planning Users Group, 
2002). A tool for simplifying urban sewer networks will save valuable time and 
systemise the process regardless of the user and the network. A tool like this is not 
easily produced, as e.g. human judgement can be very difficult to program. Every 
case in the network must be defined by rules and conditions. In a full sewage net-
work, there are numerous exceptions and conditions to identify and approach. Fur-
thermore, there are currently no guidelines available on simplification of urban 
drainage networks and very little documentation of the effects on model results are 
available. 
1.2 Objectives 
The aim of this project is to: 
o Develop a tool to systematise simplification of urban drainage networks. 
o Implement compensations for volume loss and time-differences. 
o Investigate how simplification of the network system affects the results and 
computation time of a 1D/2D urban flood model.  
The tool is developed in Python and modifies a MIKE Urban database. Toolboxes 
from ArcGIS are used for reading and updating the database. The tool is divided 
into two scripts; one that merges pipes together, and one deleting pipes and 
branches below a specified threshold.

2 Hydrological and Hydrodynamic Modelling 
Dedicated software for urban drainage systems has been developed over the past 
few decades including the commonly used MOUSE (MOdel for Urban SEwers) de-
veloped by the Danish Hydrological Institute (DHI) in 1983. Initially, 1D models 
were developed to locate areas with undersized pipes and to test new infrastructure 
both above and below the surface. Along with the urban drainage models, software 
for rivers and surface flows were developed. These models were mainly used sepa-
rately, since linking these models would increase the number of calculations, and 
thereby the computation time, significantly. Over time, the computation power 
increased to a level that allowed linking sewer and surface. A 1D surface model was 
initially linked to a 1D sewage model abbreviated as a 1D/1D model (Butler & 
Davis, 2011). In this model, water can exit one manhole and flow to another man-
hole by specified channels in the surface model. This is the first attempt to estimate 
surface water depth and flooding extent on the surface. 1D/1D models have grown 
very popular, since they provide a more realistic estimate on the surface water level, 
at a low computational cost. As remotely sensed Digital Elevation Models (DEMs) 
has become easily available in a suitable spatial resolution, the surface models have 
been expanded from 1D to a cell-based 2D grid model with data for each cell in 
this spatial environment (Butler & Davis, 2011; DANVA, 2011). This increase in 
number of calculations drastically increase the computation time of 2D models 
compared with the 1D models (DANVA, 2011). Linking a 1D-sewer model with a 
2D overland model thus increases the computation time significantly, but also pro-
vides a detailed output with water depth and flooding extent that cannot be 
achieved in a 1D/1D model. The 1D/2D model is therefore necessary when esti-
mating flooding extents and cost of damages (DANVA, 2011).  
Recently, the development of 3D surface models with a high spatial resolution has 
begun. The 3D models requires local measurements, often carried out by unmanned 
aerial vehicles (UAVs) and the models are therefore developed for specific locations. 
The purpose in an urban drainage context is, however, mainly for visualization. 
The drainage models are still modelled on a 1D/2D basis, while only the results are 
being displayed in 3D (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2016).

Software for modelling drainage systems are crucial when dimensioning and testing 
solutions in a network. Diverse software for simulating urban drainage systems 
exists and some are described in the following sections. The models are all deter-
ministic models based on sets of physical parameters and mathematical equations, 
including the Saint-Venant equations. Some of the most common models are the 
Storm Water Management Model (SWMM), MIKE Urban and Innovyze InfoWorks 
(Butler & Davis, 2011; DANVA, 2011). SWMM is developed by the US Environ-
mental Protection Agency and is the most commonly used software for this purpose 
in the US (Butler & Davis, 2011). Since this software is open source, packages can 
be developed by any user without expenses to licenses. InfoWorks, originally devel-
oped by Wallingford Software in the United Kingdom, includes packages for the 
entire water cycle thus comprehensive models can be set up in this. A similar pack-
age-based software suite is the MIKE-environment from the Danish Hydraulic In-
stitute (DHI).  
2.1 MIKE by DHI 
The MIKE software, commonly used across Europe, includes various individual 
programs that can be combined into comprehensive models. MIKE Urban targets 
modelling of urban drainages systems and in combination with MIKE FLOOD and 
MIKE 21, the network model can be combined with overland flow models.  
2.1.1 MIKE Urban 
MIKE Urban is the main software used for urban flood modelling in Denmark. In 
this ArcGIS-based model environment, GIS data layers are easily processed and 
loaded into the MIKE Urban model (DHI, 2014e). Most municipalities have GIS 
data for pipes, manholes, catchments (in form of e.g. cadastre data), structures etc. 
already, which with minor modifications will work as input to the model. The mod-
els can be saved in various database formats but for modifying the database by 
coding, the geodatabase-format is preferred. MIKE Urban by default uses the 
MOUSE engine for calculations but also includes the SWMM engine (DHI, 2014e).

2.1.2 MIKE Flood 
The MIKE Flood package for Mike Urban makes it possible to couple a 1D network-
model with a 2D overland model (a 1D/2D flood model) (DHI, 2014c). The two 
models are linked by coupling nodes in the network with cells in the overland model. 
Thereby water can flow between nodes in the MIKE Urban network and cells in 
the MIKE 21 surface model as illustrated on Figure 2.1. The water exchange is 
calculated using the Orifice equation (DHI, 2014f). The MIKE Urban model thus 
contains all information regarding the sewer network, e.g. pipes, manholes, weirs 
and basins, while the MIKE 21 surface model contains the surface mesh and cou-
pling points between the models (DHI, 2014e, 2014b). The MIKE Urban (MU) 
model also contains the information of catchments and runoff parameters. Runoff 
is therefore calculated on catchment-scale and discharged directly into a node and 
not the surface model (DHI, 2014g). If the maximum volume exchange rate (1 m3/s 
by default) is reached, the water can, however, be diverted from the catchment and 
directly to the surface model, but still in the same cell as the node (DHI, 2014d).

2.2 Theoretical Hydrodynamic calculations 
The MIKE models are based on sets of mathematical equations that uses physical 
parameters to calculate the flow in pipes and on surfaces (DHI, 2014g, 2014f). The 
relevant equations used in the MIKE simulations are described in the following 
sections.  
2.2.1 MOUSE Runoff Models A and B 
For many years, the MOUSE runoff model A has been the primary model for cal-
culation of catchment scale runoff in urban drainage models in Denmark. Model A 
uses the “Time-Area” method; a linear reservoir-based runoff model with a fixed 
permeable area assuming 100% infiltration at all times (DHI, 2014g). The model 
includes minor losses in form of the initial loss and a scaling factor to account for 
evaporation. The time-area curve is defined from the area, time of concentration 
(TOC) and shape of the catchment. The curve describes the area contributing to 
runoff discharged from the catchment. From the point where time equals the TOC, 
the full impermeable area contributes to the runoff.  
Runoff model A is, however, slowly being replaced by model B as the primary runoff 
model. It is based on a non-linear reservoir model and uses the kinematic wave 
equation. Furthermore, the infiltration into permeable areas changes over time by 
using the Horton’s infiltration equation (DHI, 2014g). The model B therefore re-
quires several other parameters to estimate the surface runoff and infiltration rates.  
Calculating Runoff in Model B 
The non-linear reservoir model is described by the kinematic wave equations that 
assumes uniform flow conditions on the surface of the catchment (DHI, 2014g). 
Only gravitational and friction forces are considered in the surface flow, as demon-
strated by the kinematic wave equation described in Section 2.3.1. Finally, the 
model assumes a uniform distribution of the rainfall across the catchment.

longer valid. Methods for computing flow in pressurised pipes are well developed, 
and used for design of industrial processes and within the field of drinking water 
distribution systems. A very common practice within these fields is to use the Ber-

channel and pressurised flow, large oscillations can occur compromising the simu-

3 Background of 1D/2D Model Simplifications 
In DANVA (2011) the principles of 1D/1D and 1D/2D models are described and 
compared. While the 1D/1D models are generally more stable and computationally 
much faster than the 1D/2D models, they typically overestimate the surface flood-
ing and are therefore less reliable. For estimation of capacity, the 1D/1D models 
are very useful, but as soon water surcharges to the surface, the 1D/2D models 
become more precise. 
When dealing with complex models, the significantly higher computational de-
mands of 1D/2D models has prevented common use for flood warning. Even for 
system dimensioning, the 1D/2D models are in some cases rejected. The most ob-
vious solution is to increase the computational power by increased number of CPU’s 
and RAM. Further, Russo et al. (2015) showed that the hardware configuration 
also affects the computation time. Use of hardware adapted to 2D modelling can 
therefore reduce the computation time dramatically. This solution demands a thor-
ough knowledge of both software and hardware components and might be very 
expensive for the user. Additionally, dedicated simulation servers often serve diverse 
tasks thus optimising the hardware and software for a single purpose might be 
unfavourable. 
A more well-known and common practice is to simplify the network and surface 
models, in order to obtain fewer calculations thus faster computation times. The 
following sections describe a number of methods to simplify urban drainage models. 
3.1.1 Simplification Approaches to a 1D Network Model 
To reduce computation time, urban drainage systems models are often simplified 
(Wastewater Planning Users Group, 2002; Leitão et al., 2010). There are different 
ways to simplify a sewer network model with the most common methods being 
pruning, trimming and merging of pipes and nodes (Leitão et al., 2010). The main 
differences between pruning/trimming and merging the network are illustrated in 
Figure 3.1.  
Pruning 
Pruning is a term covering the removal of single pipe stretches from main branches. 
This is the most conservative simplification method and usually have little effect 
==PAGE:37==
CHAPTER 3: BACKGROUND OF 1D/2D MODEL SIMPLIFICATIONS 
| 20 | 
on both computation time and results. In pruning, the selected pipes for removal 
are short with small diameters connected to larger pipes. These pipes are often 
within the threshold for minimum pipe length and assumed longer during simula-
tions to avoid instability issues.  
Trimming 
Trimming is a more aggressive technique than pruning as this allows for removal 
of whole branches in the system. Most often, a threshold for pipe diameter is given 
by the user thus pipes with diameters lower than this is removed. Thereby pruning 
is included in the trimming method. As trimming can potentially remove large 
branches a considerable volume and travel time is lost in the system, which should 
be accounted for.  
Merging 
Merging is limited to removal of nodes. Pipe stretches are preserved, as the two 
pipes are merged to one single pipe with a combined length of the two pipes. The 
pipe selection is based on thresholds of pipe length, diameters, slope differences etc. 
By merging short pipes with longer ones, the stability of the model is improved. 
Since slope, diameter etc. might not be the same in the two merged pipes these 
parameters have to be recalculated or estimated for the new common pipe.

Though urban drainage models are extensively used there are no official standards 
related to simplification procedures. Very few guidelines exist on this subject, with 
one being “The Code of Practice for the Hydraulic Modelling of Sewer Systems” 
from Wastewater Planning Users Group (2002). The publication date of this guide-
line alone indicate that modern 1D/2D urban drainage models are not the main 
area of interest. The section related to simplification of the model vaguely states 
that “it is necessary to apply judgement” to maintain the accuracy of the simplified 
model.  
Consulting companies, utility companies, etc., therefore use different methods and 
criteria for simplification. The results from one company thus potentially differ from 
the other and in extreme cases, can affect the final solutions. 
3.1.2 Effects of Simplifying the 1D Network on a 1D/2D Model 
Network simplification for coupled models is a commonly used method, but the 
effects are sparsely documented in literature. Furthermore, the thresholds are rarely 
well-defined for diameter and length, and are most often based on a rough estimate 
for each model and application. Additionally, the impacts of simplification on the 
results are poorly documented.  
Leitão et al. (2010) evaluate the most common simplification methods of pruning 
and merging on a 1D/1D and 1D/2D flood model. The catchment is approximately 
100 ha and has 154 manholes and 174 sewer pipes. The simplifications, however, 
mainly focus on surface nodes and links. Only 14 manholes and 13 links have been 
removed from the sewer system in the merge process and none in the pruning. Little 
difference was observed in the results compared to the original model, and with a 
significantly lower computational demand. Slightly higher water depths and flows 
were observed in the simplified models, mainly due to the loss of ponds in the 
system. The authors found the merging technique to be the most accurate, while 
trimming yielded the fastest computation time with reductions of up to 59%.  
The few studies found on this topic are all commenced using small 1D/1D and/or 
1D/2D model networks. The effects on a large catchment is therefore not identified 
and some of the tendencies in the large networks may not have been observed in 
the small networks investigated so far.

3.1.3 Compensating for Network Modifications 
When pipes are merged and trimmed out of the network, nodes are deleted which 
might be the connection point of a catchment. This catchment thus has to be 
reconnected to the nearest downstream node. In a merge of pipes, the distance to 
the next node is rarely far. In a trimmed system, the distance can be several hun-
dred meters thus the travel distance for the water is shortened considerably. Addi-
tionally, the deletion of pipes naturally affects the total storage volume in the net-
work. The decrease of storage volume may result in increased flooding when the 
storage location is moved to the surface compartment.  
To cope with these modifications, the volume loss and travel time are calculated 
and compensations implemented in the model. The calculation and implementation 
of volume losses and travel times are described in Section 5.2.5 and 5.2.6 respec-
tively. 
3.1.4 Modifications to a 2D Surface Model 
In 1D/2D models, the 2D surface model is often the reason for the very long com-
putation times. Using a 2D surface model requires calculations in each pixel of the 
n x m mesh for each time step. A finer mesh increases the Number Of Calculation 
(NOC) and it is advised to use as rough a mesh as possible, in order to obtain a 
lower initial computation time. The mesh can also be modified to remove cells in 
areas where no flow or flooding is expected e.g. on hills and other areas with high 
elevation. Thus the number of pixels and NOC is decreased but one should be 
careful not to remove pixels in areas where flooding may occur. This is illustrated 
in Figure 3.2A.  
To reduce the number of pixels even further a flexible mesh is a solution to decrease 
resolution (larger pixel size) in some areas. To achieve a better model it is thus 
possible to increase the resolution in sensitive areas, e.g. around buildings or in 
depressions, where flooding occurs to get a more precise water level. This method 
is however not fully developed for MIKE software hence a complicated algorithm 
must be developed by the user. However, the flexible mesh approach is a strong 
==PAGE:40==

| 23 | 
tool to decrease computation time and maintain high accuracies in other fields of 
study and software including the COMSOL Multiphysics as in Figure 3.2B. The 
conversion from quadrangle to triangular mesh makes a smoother transition in cell 
size around objects. For the calculations, a new computational scheme must be 
used, as there is no longer a fixed distance between cell centres. The triangular 
mesh also eliminate a known difficulty, where the quadrangle mesh should align 
with the main flow directions to avoid uncertainties in flow distances (DHI, 2014b; 
Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2016). The triangular bathymetry mesh may however have 
some struggles as the original DEM is based on quadrangle pixels. For instance, if 
a structure with known elevation is inserted in the mesh generator in MIKE, the 
elevation in the triangles are interpolated regardless the accuracy of the data, hence 
some areas may be more unprecise (Toke, Jens, 2016).

3.2 Challenges of Automatic Modifications 
Simplification of the urban drainage network requires thorough knowledge of the 
system and understanding of models. When deleting small and often unimportant 
pipes from the network, choosing which diameters to remove will be based on the 
purpose of the model. However, deleting the correct pipes, i.e. pipes that are not 
essential for the system characteristics, is very difficult. This results in many ex-
ceptions where some examples are given below: 
o If a pipe stretch with a diameter below the threshold is connected to a pipe 
upstream that are larger than the threshold, the pipe stretch should not be 
deleted.  
o If the pipe stretch that is to be deleted is longer than a given threshold, this 
may affect the system too much hence the pipes should not be deleted. 
o If the diameter difference between two pipes are large, the pipes should not 
be merged. 
o If pipes are parallel, they should neither be merged nor deleted.  
The exceptions are simple and not difficult to understand from a human perspec-
tive, but to make a code able to go through the system and consider every branch 
and node for deletion is a much greater challenge. When the network is above a 
certain size, it becomes unfeasible to simplify the system manually and using scripts 
taking decisions based on rules specified by the user is a necessity. This also ensures 
continuity between multiple users, as the scripts are not subjected to personal de-
cisions that may differ depending on the individual’s experiences or lack of.

4 The Elwood Case Study 
A MIKE Urban 1D/2D Urban Flood Model was provided covering the southern 
part of Melbourne, Australia, which will be used to evaluate and test different 
approaches and degrees of simplification. 
Within the catchment, shown on Figure 4.1, are several suburbs whereof Elwood, 
a low-lying, highly built-up area exposed to flooding, is located furthest downstream 
(Touzeau, 2012). Originally, the Elwood area was a swamp but after construction 
of the Elwood Canal, the swamp was drained and construction on the land began 
in 1908 (Spratt, 2012). Flooding had been a problem already in the 1930’s but a 
permanent solution has never been found. On the 4th of February 2011, a major 
storm with 106 mm precipitation during three hours caused substantial flooding in 
the entire region (SES & FloodSafe, 2013). With multiple large storms in the fol-
lowing years, large rainstorms are no longer considered an unusual incident but 
have been accepted as common caused by climate changes. Flooding has thus be-
come a topic that is highly prioritised on the local political agenda, as permanent 
solutions are demanded by both citizens and politicians (Spratt, 2012; Touzeau, 
2012). This has given rise to political decisions of reducing the flood risk in this 
area (SES & FloodSafe, 2013; Touzeau, 2012). To find best practice solutions a 
urban flood model has been created covering a 12x6km area of total 70.7 km2. The 
sewer system in Elwood is a separate waste- and rainwater system. Since the El-
wood area is low-lying, it is highly influenced by the sea level. At high sea levels, 
there is potential for significant flooding in Elwood even for rain events with low 
return periods. 
In this study, the 1D/2D model covering southern Melbourne will be used for eval-
uation of simplification techniques. The model with the full network will serve as 
the reference model for models trimmed, merged and both. As no data is present, 
results from a second version of the full model will serve as validation. In the vali-
dation model, only the minimum pipe length is changed in the model setup.

maximum rainfall intensity for each return period were calculated using a polyno-
mial function describing the Average Recurrence Interval (ARI). The maximum 
rainfall intensity is thereby given as a function of the return period. The parameters 
of the polynomial equation are based on the physical location as coordinates. In 
this case, the coordinates were 37.900S, 145150E, approximately in the centre of 
the Elwood catchment. The rainfall intensity is used as input to the IFD calculation 
tool from BOM (2016) and the total rainfall depth is found. The temporal distri-
bution of the rain is based on Table 3.2 in Institution of Engineers Australia (1987) 
as a fraction of the summarised rainfall for each time step. The time steps are 
discretised in 15-minute intervals for 4.5 hours with the peak from 1 hour and 15 
minutes forward, as the example in Figure 4.2. Rain series with return periods of 
2, 5, 10, 20 and 100 years were used for simulations.

4.1.2 Runoff Model 
The runoff model is based on 15213 sub-catchments with a total area of 4783 ha. 
The non-linear model B, including Horton’s infiltration, is used for runoff calcula-
tions. All sub-catchments are assumed to have an average surface slope of 10 ‰ 
while the fractions of flat/steep impervious and medium pervious areas varies based 
on the land use. The low and high pervious areas have not been used since no data 
on specific soil types and infiltration rates throughout the catchment are available. 
The runoff is calculated in time steps of one second for 5.5 hours hence there is no 
precipitation during the last hour.  
4.1.3 1D Network Model 
The Network model consists of 13477 nodes and 13851 links illustrated in Figure 
4.3. The network is mainly build from circular pipes, but many of the downstream 
main branches are open or closed rectangular channels. There are no basins or 
pumps in the system, but five weirs located in the downstream area of the network. 
The upstream parts of the network are located in a more hilly terrain, hence pipes 
are steeper here than in the downstream parts where elevation is close to sea level. 
The network has three outlets to the sea as the only place where water can be 
discharged out of the model. The Elwood Canal is the largest of the outlets from 
the catchment to the Port Philip bay with the two other outlets located 1 km north 
on Shakespeare Grove and the other 1.5 km south on Head Street from the Elwood 
Canal.

4.1.4 2D Surface Model 
Commonly an auto-generated grid is made in MU from a DEM raster. As the sur-
face flow calculations are the most computational heavy part of the model, there 
can be made large improvements in computation time by optimizing the grid to 
include fewer cells. Löwe (2016) has created a mesh where areas averse to flooding, 
e.g. topmost parts of hills, are removed. The cell size is 10x10 meters with a total 
of 399.257 cells. This mesh have been used in order to save time on computation 
during the simulation part of this project. The mesh have not been modified be-
tween models, thus only changes in the network affects the NOC.  
4.1.5 Boundary Conditions 
In order to ensure flooding in most parts of the Elwood model, the water level of 
the sea is set to 2 meters above mean sea level, as this is considered high but 
realistic water level at large storms. The sea boundary applies to cells at the coast-
line, while remaining edges are considered land boundaries with no flow through 
the borders.

4.2 Simulation of Models 
All simulations are conducted on the MCALC server on DTU Environment, ac-
cessed through a remote desktop connection. The server has 64.0 GB or RAM with 
four 2.13 GHz quad-core processers able to run four treads per process hence 64 
treads in total. The available performance of the server varies periodically depend-
ing on the uses. As the server is a shared resource between people on the institute, 
one is required to limit the use of power. In order to compare the simulation time 
between models, the computational power had to be fixed throughout the project. 
Without knowing the available computational power in the future, a relatively low 
maximum performance was chosen when simulation the coupled 1D/2D model. The 
distributed memory approach (MPI) were used with a maximum of eight threads 
per process (equals to 12.5% CPU-use) in order to ensure availability even for pe-
riods of high activity on the server. During simulation, the memory use was ap-
proximately 5.5 GB. This resulted in a simulation time of 4 hours for the full model.

5 Mike Urban Data structure 
The structure and relevant files of a MIKE Urban database are described in the 
following sections, along with a description of how the database have been modified. 
Additionally, the scripts to simplify the model are described in detail.  
In MIKE Urban, the network and boundary conditions are saved in a database, 
which for the Elwood-model, is in the format of a geodatabase (.gdb). The database 
can be edited using the Graphical User Interface (GUI) in MIKE Urban. This is 
the most common way to work with the database as all modifications will auto-
matically be updated and saved in the correct files of the database. With the georef-
erenced files arranged in a geodatabase, it is possible to modify the database with 
a scripting tool, e.g. MATLAB, Python or R. This allows the user to develop new 
tools for modification of the network.  
5.1.1 Structure of the Geodatabase 
The geodatabase is built from files containing georeferenced data in the network 
along with tables of boundary information. All files related to the geometry are 
located in the folder “mu_Geometry”, which includes the relevant files in Table 
5-1. The main folder contains tables of information including the ones in Table 5-2. 
The georeferenced files in Table 5-1 consist of two parts: A georeferenced point 
(node), polyline (link/pipe) or polygon (catchment) and a corresponding table with 
data for each feature.  
5.2 Python scripts 
As ArcGIS is required for the MU installation, both Python and the package 
‘ArcPy’ are available for all users of MU. Using Python and the free parts of the 
ArcPy package, databases in MIKE Urban can be loaded and modified directly in 
the geodatabase-format, without additional licenses. Python scripts are a versatile 
tool for model simplifications, since it is possible to create scripts to load and modify 
any given MIKE Urban. Two independent main scripts have been developed during 
this project: One for trimming and one for merging pipes. Dr. Roland Löwe, DTU 
Environment, have developed scripts to select which pipes to be merged and 
trimmed. The pipe-selection parts of these scripts have been re-used and fixed for 
bugs and errors. Furthermore, several new functions have been added to include

5.2.1 Loading and Saving Data 
Before anything else, the databases containing the original model need to be loaded

5.2.2 Trimming 
The trimming selection part is developed by Dr. Roland Löwe, DTU Environment, 
as part of a much larger simplification algorithm. A flow chart of the trimming 
script is presented in Figure 5.2. The script starts at a user-specified node (the 
outlets) and moves recursively through the network. Knowing start- and end-nodes 
for each pipe, the function moves from node to node saving visited nodes and pipes 
in lists thereby keeping track of which areas have already been considered for dele-
tion. If a node is connected to more than one pipe that have not been visited yet, 
a second function, “checkbranches”, checks if the branch can be deleted from a 
user-specified threshold of pipe diameter and length. Later the script returns to the 
second pipe and repeats the process. If the branch does not fulfil the conditions for 
deletion, the “go_upstream”-function proceeds through the system to the next in-
tersection. This task is repeated throughout the whole network. Branches that can 
be deleted are loaded into two lists with nodes and pipes to be deleted after the 
search has finished. The functions are recursive, i.e. calling their own function, 
which in some cases is a more efficient method but can result in high memory use. 
With more than 15000 nodes, the default recursion depth of 1000 in python must 
be increased for some trimming thresholds.  
An error was found in the original trimming algorithm, where pipes connecting 
circular branches to the main network were accidently deleted. The error in the 
script was identified and solved with a few additional lines of code that recognises 
when a circular branch is visited. The additional steps are marked by the dotted 
grey box in Figure 5.2. Appendix C-1 gives a more thorough explanation and illus-
tration of the error and solution.  
The full Python script for trimming the network can be found in Appendix H-1.

5.2.3 Merging 
In contrast to the trimming, merging selection can be conducted without use of 
recursive functions. A flow chart describing the function is seen in Figure 5.3. The 
script loops through the list of all pipes in the network considering the possibility 
of merging with a neighbouring pipe. The threshold is gradually increased to a user 
specified length. Two small pipes may not have a combined length longer than the 
threshold length though they have been merged. Therefore, merging is repeated 4-
5 times for each threshold. The pipes are tested to fulfil the criteria of merging with 
respect to length, diameter difference to neighbouring pipes and if the pipes are 
parallel. If the merging conditions for the pipe are fulfilled, the merge-function is 
called with two inputs; the pipe to be removed and the pipe to be merged with. 
The new diameter (including height and width for canals) is based on a weighted 
average between the two pipes and the maximum difference between diameters is 
10%. The same function also calculates the new pipe length, inserts new coordinates 
for the pipe start- or endpoints and makes new polylines of the pipes for visualisa-
tion.  
The catchment reconnection and travel time calculations are executed in separate 
functions, where the catchment inlet is moved to the downstream node. The original 
script only used the remaining node from the deleted pipe hence the inlet was not 
always assigned to a downstream node illustrated in Appendix C-2. This has been 
modified by comparing the elevation of the two pipes and always assigning the inlet 
to the lowest manhole. All information saved in lists, e.g. connections to nodes, 
pipes, catchments, diameters etc. are continuously updated when a pipe is merged. 
Otherwise, the information would not be valid for the next check. 
The full Python script for merging of pipes is found in Appendix H-1.

5.2.4 Updating the .couple-file 
After deleting nodes, a new couple file needs to be created with the remaining 
manholes to connect the 1D network with the 2D surface. The couple-file contains 
data necessary for the coupling between the network and surface models including 
node ID, coordinates and parameters for water exchange. MU uses approximately 
30 minutes producing a new couple-file. Instead, the original couple-file from the 
full model is used as a template and information for the remaining nodes is copied 
from this to a new file. The only variable that needs to be changed is the number 
of coupled nodes. This reduces the time to less than a minute. The Python script 
for updating the couple-file is found in Appendix H-1 
5.2.5 Compensation for Volume Losses  
To consider the volume loss, diameters of downstream manholes were changed so 
the total volume in the system was the same after trimming branches away. Cal-
culation of volume losses due to pipe trimming was added as a function. The extra 
volume is added by changing manhole diameters at endpoints. The manhole thereby 
functions as an internal reservoir in a similar way the pipe was before. In MOUSE, 
pipe lengths might be calculated from the edge of the manhole hence two large and 
possible overlapping manholes can cause the pipe length to become zero or negative. 
This was changed to be based on the manhole centre in the DHIAPP.INI file using 
the “LINK_LENGTH_TYPE_NO” parameter setting the value to 1. 
When merging pipes, the volume changes are relatively small of maximum a few 
cubic meters for a pipe. Volume changes were therefore disregarded in the merged 
model. 
5.2.6 Compensation for Travel Time 
Calculation 
The travel time between two points in the system, is approximated using the Man-
ning Equation. The velocity at free surface conditions is calculated for each pipe on 
the path between the original and new inlet node. The calculation is based on the 
diameter, slope and roughness for each pipe, but some pipes have no or very little 
slope hence the velocity can be infinitely low. Therefore, a minimum velocity of 
0.15 m/s is assumed. Before assigning the minimum velocity, the rest of the branch 
is considered to obtain the best possible fit. If the slope in the given pipe is zero or 
==PAGE:59==
CHAPTER 5: MIKE URBAN DATA STRUCTURE 
| 42 | 
the velocity is below the threshold, slope in the upstream pipe is used instead. If 
the conditions are still not fulfilled, the next pipe is considered and so on for the 
rest of the branch. If none of the pipes fulfils the criteria, the minimum velocity of 
0.15 m/s is used. The value of 0.15 m/s is chosen based on Figure 8.5 in Butler & 
Davis (2011) as this value is the minimum reasonable flow rate for pipes with di-
ameters above 0.2 meters. The flow rate is likely to be higher in reality as no wave 
or pressure effects are considered.  
Implementation

challenge than calculating it. Initially, modification of parameters in the runoff 
model delaying the hydrograph was considered. This has previously been done using 
the Time Of Concentration (TOC) parameter in runoff Model A. Model B has 
length, area and slope as variables instead. A direct way of including travel time in 
the model B is not present. In an attempt to delay the runoff curve from catch-
ments, the parameters of model B were changed individually for three catchments 
with different areas and hydrographs were inspected visually.

6 Evaluation of Simplified Models 
There are numerous ways of comparing models and their performance. The compu-
tation time is compared, as this is the main goal of simplifying the model. The 
changes and errors of simplified models compared to the full model should also be 
considered. A common method for evaluation is not yet present and several meth-
ods can be applied. There are multiple output files with different data to compare 
and the following sections describe and applies different approaches for comparison 
of model performances.  
6.1 Computation Time 
The simulation time for each simplified model is plotted in Figure 6.1 with respect 
to the number of pipe elements in the model. The simulation time is the averaged 
time for simulating the model with rain inputs of 2, 5, 10, 20 and 100 year return 
periods. As expected, computation time generally decreases with the number of 
elements. However, the individual values are not strictly following this tendency, 
which might be explained by the availability of computational resources. Due to 
hardware limits, not all models could be run at the same time. Some model simu-
lations were therefore run during peak periods and others during idle periods. This 
is particularly reflected the results for the merged and 600mm trimmed models. It 
is clear that the models computed right after each other (e.g. the trimmed and 
subsequently merged models) show the most consistent computation times as the 
server use have been somewhat stable during all simulations. A dedicated calcula-
tion server could be used to verify this. The computation times for each model run 
can be found in Appendix H-3.  
Though all curves show decreasing computation time, the tendency is not strictly 
linear, but seems to flatten out from about 5000-9000 pipe elements. It is here, the 
effect of simplifying the network becomes less important compared to the 2D surface 
compartment. By only modifying the network, it was possible to reduce the com-
putation time from 4 hours to just below 1.5 hours by decreasing the number of 
elements to one fourth of the original. A decrease of 60% is far more than expected. 
The 2D overland model is usually considered the most important when considering 
computation time, but potentially, another process is important in form of the 
water exchange calculations at the coupled nodes. If so, decreasing the number of

6.3 2D Surface Results 
The spatial differences on the surface between the full models and simplified models 
are presented in the following sections. The surface water depth and flood cover 
(area flooded) are considered, while the flood cover is used to quantify the spatial 
error of each model.  
All spatial results are processed using ArcGIS tools in Python, but most requires 
some pre- and post-processing. Pre-processing consist of converting the MIKE 21 
.dfsu result files to shapefiles with maximum water levels, using the MIKE Zero 
Toolbox. The Python script then converts, process, calculates and exports the files 
automatically and organises all files in relevant folders. This saves time compared 
to doing manual batch processing in ArcMap. The script for results processing is 
included in Appendix H-1.

Two different sets of results are produced for each simulation: One includes the 
whole mesh, while the other only includes pixels with an elevation above 4 meters. 
The reason for this is a large downstream area that is always flooded due to the 
high sea level of two meter. The area is so large (up to 85% of the total flooded 
area) that it is difficult to observe the differences taking place in the upstream parts 
of the system hence the area marked in Figure 6.4 has been removed. 
Steps of Results Processing 
The shapefile created from the dfsu-file consist of a polygon with a water level for 
each pixel in the surface mesh. The shapefile is converted to a raster map that can 
be processed. Water levels below 5 cm are not considered flooding and are removed 
using the ‘Raster Calculator’ tool. The output is a raster only containing areas 
where the maximum water level is larger than 5 cm. This raster contains numerous 
small ponds of 1-2 pixels in size that are more prone to be errors and might be due 
to minor flooding in a single time step. These individual pixels are considered noise 
and neglected, thus we want to remove them from the result layer. To remove these 
small areas, the raster is converted back to a shapefile where the areas of each pond 
is calculated using the ‘Zonal Statistics’ tool. During the conversion to a polygon, 
it is important not to simplify the polygons as all calculations are based on whole 
pixels. Additionally the maximum water level is found for each pond so only ponds

7 Findings of 1D/2D Model Simplification 
Lack of data prevents a formal validation of the full model’s ability to reproduce 
real-world floods. Thus, it is necessary to assume that the full model represents the 
reality. Meanwhile, the simplified models may reflect the reality better, since fewer 
assumptions of minimum pipe length are needed. The differences of up to 5% for 
the water volume and 9% for the flood cover between the validation model and full 
model are larger than expected. These are significant differences, given that the 
only change is the minimum pipe length. The 10 and 20 meters merged models 
showed changes similar to the validation model. The models generally improved 
when the return period increased. At T10, the volume differences were reduced to 
22% and at T100 below 13% for the least-fit models. A decrease in 13% corresponds 
to 23 thousand cubic meters of water for a 100-year event. This is a substantial 
amount of water when designing retention basins and dimensioning sewer systems. 
With a 13% volume deficit in the surface model, the designed solutions will be over-
dimensioned in the network compartment and under-dimensioned on the surface. 
For the more extensively simplified models, e.g. merge 75m, trim 500mm and above, 
the volume differences are also reflected in the flood cover. The T600M100 has the 
lowest hit-fraction of only 48% at T100. Thus 52% of the flooded area is not recre-
ated from the full model. With such a large fraction of misses and false positives, 
these errors will inevitably affect the result if the model is used for locating sensitive 
areas, or estimating costs from flooding. The false positives and misses can cause 
targeted actions to be implemented at the wrong locations hence resources are 
wasted. The error should therefore be decreased in these models to obtain a better 
fit of the flood results.   
When evaluating the flood cover in conjugational tables, two references can be used; 
the full surface including the non-flooded pixels, or limit the reference to only the 
flooded pixels. Considering the whole surface, the error will increase with the return 
period as the number of misses or false positives increases. However, the fraction of 
hits also increases. The errors at low return periods will also seem smaller as they 
are in the range of 1-2% of the total surface area. An error of 1-2% does not seem 
significant. However, when the total flooded area is only 4% the erroneous pixels 
corresponds to a large fraction of the flooded pixels. Therefore, it is recommended 
==PAGE:83==
CHAPTER 7: FINDINGS OF 1D/2D MODEL SIMPLIFICATION 
| 66 | 
to limit comparison of the simplified models to the flooded pixels of the full model, 
as this will give a better view of the magnitude of the errors. 
7.1 Effects of Simplification 
The results of simplification revealed large changes in surface water volumes and 
spatial distribution of the water. Even minor changes to the upstream parts by 
trimming pipes smaller than 300mm showed large changes to the flood cover of 30% 
for a T100-event, in spite of compensating for travel-time and volume loss. Based 
on the results from this study, the trimming procedures used as common practice 
have a significant impact on the results, and caution is necessary when using results 
from simplified models for evaluation of flood risks maps. The study of Leitão et 
al. (2010) found that the simplification in the 1D/2D models did not affect the 
results by much, but concluded the models unsuitable for real-time flood forecasting 
due to long computation time. From the results obtained in this study, it was found 
that trimming the model changes flow in branches significantly, while the merging 
method obtains a better fit of the 1D-flow results. Change in flow was rarely ob-
served in the same parts of the system for the merged and trimmed models. The 
changes are therefore not due to a specific configuration of the network, but more 
likely characteristics of the simplification methods. When trimming and subse-
quently merging networks, the combination will increases the total error as there is 
little overlap of the affected areas.  
With no standards on simplification of urban drainage networks, it is up to the 
individual company and engineer to decide the applicable simplification level and 
method. Given the large changes a simplified network can induce on the flooding 
results, this potentially causes significant errors in recommendations and solutions 
applied today. An official standard or guideline is needed to assist engineers decide 
which methods are valid and define thresholds for different applications. Addition-
ally, a description of which compensations are necessary, and how to implement 
them in an urban flood model, shall be included in the guideline.

7.2 Improvements to the Scripts 
The simplification methods still need some improvement to become more precise 
for larger trimming and merging thresholds. At this time, the benefits of simplifying 
the models are limited, with noticeable faster computation time having high costs 
in form of poor replication of results. The developed algorithms are, nevertheless, 
suitable for further work as most of the suggested additions can be easily imple-
mented in the current setup. 
Numerous improvements can be implemented to obtain better results for the sim-
plified models. At nodes where many additional catchments have been assigned, 
the 1D/2D water exchange rate need to be increased. The exchange rate can be 
based on the total catchment area discharging water to the manhole. Since the 
catchment areas are already present in lists, it is possible to implement this com-
pensation without thorough modifications to the Python scripts.  
The travel time and volume differences have been successfully implemented which 
distinguish this method from other simplifications. The compensation for volume 
losses are not fully implemented in the merged models but can be included. It is 
also considered to discretise the shifted rain-series even further to improve time-
differences from catchment reassignments. 
When accounting for volume changes, the diameter of manholes are changed. The 
orifice equation only use the minimum area of either the manhole or the inlet area 
specified along with the data of nodes. While the manhole diameter is changed, the 
inlet area is not. To account for loss of inlets between the network and surface 
compartments, the inlet area need to be expanded corresponding to the loss of 
upstream inlet areas. This can possibly explain and improve the deficit in the water 
exchange between the 1D/2D models. 
==PAGE:86==
CHAPTER 7: FINDINGS OF 1D/2D MODEL SIMPLIFICATION 
 | 69 | 
Another addition is to consider the surface characteristics around the node and if 
the area is prone to be flooded. If the node is located in e.g. a depression, a condition 
implemented in the script can prevent deletion from the network. One possibility 
is to pre-process the DEM in GIS to find depressions and main surface flow-paths. 
Another is to make an initial simulation of the full model that a check can be based 
on. A condition in the scripts can identify if the coordinate of a node is within the 
flooded areas. This is expected to improve the models significantly, but may in-
crease the computation time as fewer elements are removed. If the number of cou-
pled nodes between the network and surface model influences the computation time 
as expected, fewer couplings can be made in areas not prone to flooding, possibly 
compensating for the increased computation time.  
The method of evaluating flood cover is also an area of improvement. In this study, 
only areas with elevation below four mamsl. have been removed for evaluation of 
flood cover. Areas furthest upstream were still considered though pipes had been 
removed by trimming. When trimming areas away they are unlikely areas of inter-
est, hence they should be neglected in the result comparison. Selecting key areas 
for evaluation of model performance thus might help addressing which changes can 
be made and not. This requires a more thorough knowledge of the catchment and 
a specification of prioritised areas from e.g. land use or by the municipality. 
7.3 Current Limitations of Developed Modification Tool 
When automated simplification algorithms are used, there might be some cases 
where simplification is executed incorrectly. Tough several bugs have been found 
and fixed, there may still be bugs left, since the code has been developed in a short 
time. A major fix was the trimming script, which was not able to trim diameters 
above 400mm without isolating looped/circular branches. The bug was fixed, but 
immediately a new problem occurred, as the maximum recursion depth was 
reached. This can be set manually, but one bug still need to be addressed where 
the script ends in an infinite loop over the same branch when trimming pipes 
700mm and above. Time was put into debugging this error but the exact reason 
and solution have not yet been identified.  
The current network does not contain any pumping stations, but many other net-
works do. Simplification over these needs to be done with caution considering 
==PAGE:87==
CHAPTER 7: FINDINGS OF 1D/2D MODEL SIMPLIFICATION 
| 70 | 
changes in flow and pressure in downstream pipes. The same thing goes for weirs, 
where removal can lead to increased flow downstream of the weir. 
These examples illustrates some of the largest limitations of automatically modify-
ing a network when all exceptions and cases have to be considered by the developer. 
It is possible to consider all known cases, but if a case is neither known nor under-
stood, it cannot be implemented correctly. Therefore, a considerate amount of time 
will be spend on debugging scripts and identifying errors when developing auto-
matic tools for complicated networks. With a thoroughly developed algorithm, this 
will be able to cope with many different models and networks saving significant 
resources though the tool may have been expensive to develop. With an intuitive 
and user-friendly design of the script or GUI, most engineers working with computer 
models will be able to use and adapt it to different models hence simplifications 
will be alike though different users conduct the task.  
7.4 Applications of Simplified Models 
With the ability to compute three simplified models in the same time as one full 
model, the area of application expands. The decreased simulation time makes the 
model more useful for flood forecasting in the near future. For real-time flood fore-
casting, the model is neither sufficiently fast nor accurate at this time, but a large 
step on the way. With an accurate simplified model, the potential of using it for a 
surrogate model increases significantly. Thus, using 1D/2D flood models for real-
time forecasting are within reach of being a feasible solution.  
Furthermore, the reduced computation time can save resources for users renting 
computational power, e.g. from DHI, to conduct their simulations. It also brings a 
potential of evaluating more designs for the same economic resources resulting in 
improved final decisions. If the simulation time can be reduced to minutes or sec-
onds, this opens up the possibility of auto-calibrating the models to measured data, 
as well as making uncertainty analyses by e.g. Monte-Carlo simulations. If this is 
possible, the future 1D/2D urban flood models will become much more accurate 
and reliable than what is experienced today.

CHAPTER 8: CONCLUSION 
 | 71 | 
8 Conclusion 
Through this study, it was possible to create and develop Python scripts able to 
simplify a 1D urban drainage network model with more than 30,000 elements. The 
scripts can automatically modify a MIKE Urban database and input files for a 
MIKE Flood model. The modifications include trimming and/or merging of pipes 
with rearrangement of catchment assignments. Additionally, the scripts contains 
functions to implement compensations for flow-time and volume differences in the 
network. Simplified models include merging 10, 20, 50, 75 and 100 meters, trimming 
300, 400, 500 and 600 millimetre pipes, and finally, combining these into T300M10, 
T300M20, T400M50, T400M100, T600M50 and T600M100.  
The simplified models were evaluated on the Elwood model covering southern Mel-
bourne, Australia, using rains with return periods of 2, 5, 10, 20 and 100 years. 
Simplifying the network in 1D/2D urban drainages models significantly shortens 
the computation time. It was possible to reduce the computation time from 4 hours 
to less than 1.5 hours by deleting pipes in with a diameter smaller than 600mm and 
merging pipes shorter than 100m. Less aggressive simplifications resulted in im-
provements of computational time from 0.8-2.5 hours, with better recreation of 
results, compared to the original 1D/2D model.  
Changes of up to 10% were observed in the validation model, in spite of only chang-
ing the minimum pipe length from 20 to 10 meters. The models merged 10 and 20 
meters have slightly higher errors regarding water depth, flood cover and volume 
changes. The most excessively simplified models, e.g. trimming more than 400mm, 
were exposed to large deviations in results when comparing with the original model. 
Thus, up to 50% of the flooded area was either missed or falsely flooded for the 
worst models. The 10 meter merged model have only 8% difference in surface flood 
cover. It is therefore concluded this and the 20m merged models are suitable for 
use. When simplifying is a necessity, the merging method shall be used before trim-
ming. Considering errors and computation time, is beneficial to neglect the trimmed 
only models for the trimmed and subsequently merged models instead.  
Given the changes in results obtained by simplifying the network, an official stand-
ard or guideline on this subject is strongly recommended, in order to obtain con-
sistent and accurate results between companies and consultants.  
==PAGE:89==
CHAPTER 8: CONCLUSION 
| 72 | 
Multiple improvements to the Python scripts include expanding the inlet area and 
maximum 1D/2D water exchange rate according to the connected catchment area, 
no deletion of manholes within flooded areas, and finally, improved methods for 
model evaluation.   
Small changes in the node removal may improve the models significantly, and 
thereby yield a better fit. With a reduced computation time and model complexity, 
the 1D/2D urban drainage models may be suitable for use in surrogate models. 
Feasible application of 1D/2D models in real-time flood forecasting is thereby 
within reach.  
The use of automated scripts and algorithms is a large advantage when completing 
similar simplifications on different network systems. The common algorithms can 
aid to achieve similar results between consultant companies, municipalities, distri-
bution companies etc. Additionally, the saved resources on simplification and com-
putation can be spend on improved solutions.

C-2 Incorrect Trimming of Network 
The selection part of the trimming script had a bug where pipes leading to a circular 
branch were deleted thus some pipe sections were no longer connected to the main 
network as illustrated in Figure C.2.  
The reason was found in the deletion of pipe connections as the algorithm moved 
through the system and found pipes with diameters above the threshold. If a pipe 
in the branch prevented deletion, i.e. larger than the threshold, the whole branch 
were removed from the network connection dictionaries. By restoring this infor-
mation when a circular branch is considered for deletion, the bug was fixed.
