Abstract
As the world shifts toward a green transition, Distributed Energy Resources (DERs) in
the form of Photovoltaic (PV) panels and electric vehicles (EVs) are the most popular
trends amongst consumers. However, these technologies can jeopardize the stability
and efficiency of the electricity distribution network. Recent studies have shown the po­
tential interaction between EVs and PVs, while others have pointed out that grid tariffs
are susceptible to the penetration level of these technologies in the network. In this MSc
thesis, we study the impact of EVs and PVs from a technological and economic perspec­
tive. We formulate a model that uses actual Danish consumption profiles and integrates
the changes that EVs and PVs cause. We investigate how grid tariffs change for different
technology penetration levels, their impact on the Load Duration Curve (LDC), the change
on the yearly bill for three consumer categories, and the effect of alternative tariff designs.
First, we find that grid tariffs decrease when the total yearly consumption increases and
vice versa. Second, we highlight that the penetration of new technologies only lowers or­
dinary consumers’ average expected yearly network bill. Third, we demonstrate that the
combination of EVs and PVs on a residential level alleviates the network and improves the
current situation. Finally, we show the inadequacy of the current danish network pricing
scheme, and we present an alternative.
Keywords: Distribution grid tariff ­ Electric vehicle ­ Distributed Energy Resources ­ Pho­
tovoltaic ­ Tariff design ­ Distribution network ­ Prosumers

1
Introduction
The technological advancement of the past decade, as well as the focus of the world
toward green transition and sustainable technologies, forces the power sector into deep
transformations. The Paris agreement, the decrease of the carbon emissions and inte­
gration of renewables by more than 30% by 2030, deems the penetration of greener tech­
nologies, not essential, but mandatory [1]. Within the scope of this transition, two trends
tend to dominate among consumers: distributed generation and self­electrification. On
the one hand, distributed production and storage enable certain consumers to produce lo­
cally and self­consume with flexibility. On the other hand the electrification of the heating
and transport sectors is bound to greatly increase users’ residential power consumption.
In this matter, solar photovoltaic (PV) and battery energy storage systems (BESS) and
electric vehicles (EVs) are the main representative technologies of the first and second
trend respectively. Over the past decade all of these technologies show a dramatic de­
crease in their costs [2, 3], while they are strongly supported by regulations and public
policies [4, 5].
In this context, the electricity distribution network and its operators are heavily influenced
by these new technologies. With concerns over system efficiency losses and fairness
issues, this paradigm change in electricity use is testing the status quo economic and
technologic principles governing power grids [6, 7]. Efficient and smart integration of
these new technologies can provide economic benefits for users and system alike [8, 7,
9]. Therefore, it is imperative that the operators of the distribution system can predict
and adapt to these changes before they take place to ensure fair prices and secure and
efficient power distribution between consumers.
This master thesis investigates tariff changes for different penetration levels of EVs and
PVs in the Danish network, their impact on consumer demand and the effect of different
tariff structures. More precisely, it studies the change of the actual consumption profiles of
Danish consumers who can invest in distributed energy resources (DERs), like PV instal­
lations, and electric vehicles (EVs) and how these changes affect future network tariffs.
It examines how the total network payment is allocated between the different consumer
categories and how these payments change for different technology penetration levels.

2
Literature Review
The following chapter presents the three aspects of literature that this master thesis is
based on. The first part studies the effect of electric vehicles on grid stability and tariffs.
The second pertains to photovoltaic systems’ impact on the network and its tariffs. In the
third part, we present literature that focuses on the interaction between the two mentioned
technologies. Finally, we shall introduce the research gap that this master thesis covers.
Electric vehicles (EVs) have become increasingly more popular among European citizens
over the past years, growing in numbers from 700 in 2010 to 550,000 in 2019 (Figure 2.1)
[10], while Calearo argues that almost 400.000 EVs will be present in the Danish streets
by 2030 [11]. Despite their many benefits, evidence shows that EVs nearly double the
electricity consumption of a household [12]. Even though electric vehicles have the po­
tential to dramatically increase the revenues for the electric utilities [13], various studies
argue that haphazard penetration of EVs in the electrical network can severely impact
network stability, and consequently elevate network costs [14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 19]. How­
ever, such impacts depend on network conditions and its ability to withstand higher loads
than the status quo [20]. Smart Grid and Vehicle to Grid Charging can mitigate these
impacts on the electrical network and can even alleviate peak demand, reduce costs and
synergize with transmission expansion plans [21, 22, 23]. The most substantial incen­
tive for EV purchase and the adoption of Smart and Vehicle to Household Charging are
electricity prices and by extension, network tariffs. Therefore, it is crucial to monitor how
electricity demand and network prices are affected by the penetration of EVs, and how
current network pricing schemes can be altered for their efficient integration [21, 6, 7, 24].
Residential photovoltaic installations (PVs) have likewise become increasingly popular
in Europe (Figure 2.2) attributable to their decreasing costs over the past decade [25,
26, 27] and are deemed crucial for the green transition and the achievement of the Sus­
tainable Development Goals (SDGs) [28]. Nonetheless, because of their volatility and
unpredictability, studies evaluate that a storage system is considered necessary for the
optimal integration of the PV systems [29, 30]. The combination of EVs with a PV system
is a solid step toward the decarbonization of the energy sector [31, 32], can decrease
peak demand [33], and consequently electricity and network costs [34, 35].

The combination and interaction between EVs and Photovoltaic systems (PVs) have been
closely monitored and studied [37, 8, 38, 9]. Considering that EVs consume energy di­

a result, the two technologies co­satisfy each other’s needs. The EV works as remote
storage for the PV system, and the latter’s generation functions as a low­cost and low­
emission charger while alleviating the EV’s impact on the grid [42]. Numerous studies
have analyzed the potential and the benefits of EVs and distributed energy resources
(DERs) from both residential [43, 44, 45, 46] and utilities [47] perspective.

different grid cost structures for the different consumer and prosumer categories. They
concluded that the more a tariff design benefits DERs the less favorable it is for EVs and
vice versa [7]. Even though the above authors study the same topic as this master thesis,
their models are based on average consumption profiles [49] or a typical two­day load
profile [7] which can deviate from reality and actual measurements. Finally, to the best of
our knowledge, no research has been made on the comparison of winter and all­season
Time of Use (ToU) pricing schemes and their impacts on the Danish electricity distribution
network.
In order to study the integration of EVs in the electricity network, we need to model the
precise effect of different EV charging patterns on the consumers’ load profile, especially
when considering Vehicle to Grid. The same rules apply when we include PVs and their
interaction with the EV. For that reason, it is essential to consider the real hourly consump­
tion of households, a dataset that is available in this study. We contribute to the above
research topic by incorporating real consumption profiles when assessing how current
network prices should be modified to ensure income neutrality for the Distribution System
Operator in Denmark, and how the load is affected for different technology penetration
levels. We construct a model that considers different measured consumption profiles and
integrates both PV production and EV consumption on a household level. Additionally, it

3
Methodology
To begin with, for the creation of the appropriate categories and their respective profiles,
it is essential to gather the necessary data. The following chapters will describe the data
collection procedure and model construction which provide the correct and desired results.
In the first chapter, we investigate the process of data collection, in the second chapter
we analyze the modeling approach, and in the third we present the studied scenarios.
3.1
Data Collection
The data collection consists of 3 (three) distinguished parts: the Photovoltaic panel pro­
duction, the consumption profiles, and the electric vehicle usage profiles.
3.1.1
Photovoltaic Production
The PV production data are collected from Renewables Ninja[50], using the methods
described by Pfenninger and Staffel [51, 52]. For the location of the PVs, a general area
outside of Copenhagen is selected since the consumption dataset does not specify the
location from which they were taken. As for the capacity of the residential solar farm,
we assume that each house, regardless of surface area, installs the same capacity of
panels. As indicated by the US Energy Information Administration (EIA), a typical size for
residential PV systems is 5 kilowatts (kW), or 0.005 MW ([53]. We consider system losses
to be equal to 0.097 according to Zsiboracs [54], and the panel tilt angle is equal to 36o as
indicated by Jacobson [55] with south orientation (azimuth equal to 180o). The selected
dataset is MERRA­2, and the year is 2019. We are aware that the residential consumption
profiles we study are from 2017, but we assume that the general solar irradiation from year
to year remained the same from 2017 to 2019. As a result, we receive the hourly electricity
production for our residential system.

categories. The distinction between these is made according to:

consumption due to electric vehicles since we create new profiles, as discussed in a later
chapter. As a result, the number of households that own an EV is added to the number of
households that do not. This study considers three categories of network users: Normal
Consumers, Consumers who have purchased an EV, and Prosumers (those who produce
and provide electricity to the grid). According to Hansen, the Danish households more
susceptible to buying PV systems are generally found in rural areas with higher income
and higher education levels [56]. Furthermore, installing a PV system is improbable in a
single apartment. The installation requires authentication from the building owners and
co­owners and presents technical difficulties in considering self­consumption for only one
flat. As far as EVs are concerned, a quick analysis of the household categories provided
by Statistics Denmark indicates that the categories that present occupation of an EV are
large families (3­5+ people), living in a house, and with medium to high incomes. For all
the reasons above, the categories that this study considers having the option of buying
an electric vehicle or a photovoltaic system in the future are Houses (H), Large families
(3­5+ people), and high­income levels (2 or 3). For the remaining of the study, we only
consider the above when calculating the penetration of new technologies. We should
mention that the sum of the above household categories constitutes 21.715% of the total
studied number of households, but they account for 34.9% of the total yearly consumption.

3.1.3
Electric Vehicle Profiles
The Electrical Vehicle profiles were provided by Gunkel [22] and consist of 1000 different
profiles which contain the departure and return times and the kilometers driven during
each daily trip for the whole year. Since two income categories have the potential to buy
an EV, as we mention in the previous section, two different models of EVs are chosen,
one for each income category. The parameters we consider for selecting the models are
popularity among buyers, cost, and the possibility to fast charge at 11kW. The models’
popularity is measured by the number of EV sales per model for the first quarter of 2021
in Denmark [57], while their costs are compared through an EV Database [58]. The two
selected models are Volkswagen ID.3 Pure Performance and Tesla Model 3 for income
categories 2 and 3, respectively. The consumption per kilometer and the total usable
battery for the two models are taken from the same EV database as the cost [59, 60].
For the Volkswagen, the consumption is 0.164 kW/km and a usable battery capacity of
45kW, while for the Tesla, the consumption is 0.151kW/km with a usable battery capacity
of 57.5kW. We assume that each household owns only one car for simplicity and compu­
tational power limitations.
3.1.4
Prices and subscriptions
According to Trefor [61], Denmark is implementing Time Of Use (ToU) prices. The rate is
time­dependent, often changing by the time of day and season, and is intended to reflect
some wholesale market deviations in retail markets [62]. These prices are 16.82 øre/kWh
during baseload hours and 45.91 øre/kWh during peak hours of the winter (October to
March 17:00­20:00). Furthermore, we take into consideration the yearly subscriptions of
the network users. For normal consumers the yearly subscription to the DSO is equal to
444 DKK/year. For prosumers, the yearly subscription is 468 DKK/year while they also
have to pay an availability payment. For PV installations of less than 50MW the availability
payment is equal to 65 DKK/year [63, 61].

3.2.1
Charging Patterns
The first step in the construction of the model is the calculation of the battery levels of the
EVs according to three different charging patterns:
1. Normal Charging (NC): The Vehicle starts charging by the time it returns from its
recent trip.
2. Off­Peak Charging (OPC): The vehicle charges only during hours that are not during
the peak season (October to March, 17:00 to 20:00 [61])
3. Vehicle to Household Charging (V2H): The same as Off­Peak Charging but offers
the possibility of using the EV as a storage system that provides electricity to the
household during peak season hours (same as OPC).
Off­Peak charging (OPC) is a variation of smart charging, as mentioned by Gunkel [22].
The smart charging pattern requires the current electricity price as input, which we do
not study in this thesis since we focus on the Distribution System Operator’s (DSO) point
of view. In the same manner, Vehicle to Household charging (V2H) is a variation of the
Vehicle to Grid charging pattern, as mentioned by Gunkel [22], but for the same reasons,
it is modified. The NC pattern is followed for days outside the peak season (1 April – 30
September).
3.2.2
Coincidence Factor
The Coincidence Factor (CF) represents the tendency of a group of loads to consume
simultaneously: a lower CF indicates that less grid capacity is needed to supply the
same group of loads. In our case, it presents the percentage of cars that charge at the
same time. A rise in CF could jeopardize grid security, forcing grid reinforcement to occur
sooner. According to Bollerslev [64], when a group of more than 30 vehicles is consid­
ered, the coincidence factor drops to less than 30% and less than 25% when more than
50 vehicles are considered for 11 kW charging. Since 100 different EV profiles are con­
sidered in this study and model, the coincidence factor should be around 25%. We study
four different approaches. Instead of permitting every car to start charging after 20:00, we
divide the 100 profiles into subgroups that start charging at different hours of the evening,
when consumption is lower [65]. Finally, we also study a scenario in which the charging
capacity is lowered to 5.5kW, to assess how the load changes when charging is spread

To correctly calculate the network usage costs of each household category, it is imperative
to calculate its average net import. The calculation of the net import of a household that
has installed a photovoltaic system is given in equation 3.1.

In cases where PV Production is larger than Consumption, the net import is equal to zero.
At that time energy is exported to the grid, and the “negative” import is not considered
when calculating the network costs. The excess electricity production of the PV system
is absorbed by the grid and transferred to neighboring houses. However, since the grid
is still used to transport energy from one place to another, the recipient would still have to
pay the grid costs. The only case where the excess energy of one house would lower the
grid costs of the next would be if a small local electricity network was installed between
the dwellings, which does not apply to the studied case.
For the NC and OPC charging patterns, the EV battery levels and, consequently, the
charging consumption is the same for all the household categories that share the same
income levels. This is due to the fact that the EVs only increase consumption like a

the case with V2H since during peak hours when the household uses the EV battery for

Results

4.1
Price Variation
With the penetration of new technologies in the grid, the total yearly consumption changes
accordingly: EVs increase the consumption of households while the PV systems decrease
it. As a result, prices would also have to variate for the DSO to maintain the same income
(income neutrality). The approach we use in this study is that both prices increase or
decrease at the same rate. The percentile variation of the current prices (16.82 øre/kWh
base price and 45.91 øre/kWh peak price) for the scenarios mentioned in Table 3.3 are
shown in Figure 4.1 to Figure 4.4.
The graphs indicate how prices should change for each studied scenario, tariff design,
and technology penetration with each dot corresponding to a model result. The orange,
blue and gray dots and lines correspond to the three pricing schemes ToU 17­19, ToU
17­20 and ToU 16­20 respectively.
The factor that makes the lines ascending or descending is the increase or decrease of the
users’ total yearly net import. If the annual consumption increases, prices will have to drop
to ensure income neutrality and vice versa. The two new pricing schemes that this study
investigates appear higher and lower than the current case. When the peak timeframe
increases, the prices drop because the high prices are applied for more hours(ToU 16­20),
and they increase when the timeframe decreases for the opposite reason (ToU 17­19).
For that reason, the initial price variation for the ToU 17­19 and ToU 16­20 are +4.8% and
­5.5% respectively.

5
Discussion of results and assumptions
In summary, the results we present in Chapter 4 demonstrate the variation of the price
for possible future scenarios, the impacts on the load duration curve, and the effect of an
alternative pricing scheme. As with any modeling project, assumptions and data can be
potential points of bias and affect the results. Consequently, it is imperative to analyze
the results and the potential limitations of the model. First, we discuss and interpret the
results of the previous chapter. In the second part of this chapter, we identify and analyze
the possible limitations and assumptions of the model.
5.1
Result discussion
First of all, as we showed in the previous chapter, as the total consumption of users in­
creases or decreases depending on the penetrating technology, prices have to adjust
accordingly if the DSO expects to maintain a stable income. When total consumption in­
creases, prices must decrease and vice versa. In the realistic (Figure 4.1) and EV with
V2H Charging (Figure 4.2) future scenarios, even if all the high income and high con­
suming households install the new technologies (21.71% of the total) the price decreases
by 4­5%. This result is much lower when we compare it with similar studies (9.4% [7]
and 10­12% [49]) but this difference is expected due to the different modeling procedure
and datasets. Furthermore, when PV systems penetrate the network, the decreased
consumption of the users would force the DSO to increase the network tariff to ensure
cost­recovery and income neutrality. Photovoltaic production is directly connected to the
studied geographical region and consequently results may vary. In this study, the price
increase for the maximum studied technology penetration (21.71% of the total) is 8% for
the PV penetration scenario and 3% for the EV with V2H Charging and a PV system. Hoa­
rau and Küfeoğlu have also shown that PV penetration results in a grid tariff increase. For
25% proportion of PV owners compared to the general population, grid tariffs increased
by 15.2% and 4% respectively ([7, 49]. The variation of results between the threes stud­
ies lies in the different geographical regions that are taken into consideration. The slight
increase in prices of Figure 4.4 (EV with V2H and PV scenario) indicate that even if the
EVs are present in almost 22% of the households, Vehicle to Household charging and
the support of the PV system compensates for the high consumption of EVs. Vehicle to
Modeling the economic and technological
impact of EVs and PVs in the Danish electricity distribution network
31
==PAGE:42==
Household reduces demand during peak hours while the photovoltaic panels support the
charging of the vehicle in sunny days when it is present at the household. Even though
Denmark is not one of the EU countries with the highest PV production potential [68], a
5MW photovoltaic installation in every household that owns an EV, can, not only function
as a low­cost and low­emission charger, but also negates the total consumption of the
vehicle on an annual basis.
Furthermore, the expected yearly bill of regular consumers constantly decreases for most
scenarios of technology penetration which contradicts the literature. Schittekatte shows
that when investing in PV and storage systems, prosumers push the sunk costs towards
passive consumers [69, 70] while in tables 4.7 and 4.8 it is the annual bill of prosumers
that increases. The reasons for the above lie in the fact that as PVs or EVs with PV sup­
port penetrate the grid and prices rise, the high consuming households that install the
new technologies are transferred to the prosumer or the consumer with new technologies
(EV owners) category. As a result, their elevated consumption increases the bill accord­
ingly. For the maximum technology penetration, normal consumers end up paying 120
DKK less per year compared to the current situation, while the purchase of new tech­
nologies increases the annual bill for EV owners and prosumers by 700 DKK and 200
DKK respectively. Finally, in cases where the DSO wishes to elevate their yearly revenue
(grid expansion and upgrade) and consequently reflect these costs in the network prices,
the share of the total payment remains the same. In other words, for the same level of
technology penetration, even when prices deviate, the share of total payment between
consumers and prosumers will remain unchanged.
As for the load duration curve, the situation in the realistic scenario (Figure 4.5) does
not drastically change when we compare it to the other cases. The highest peaks are
constantly decreasing while EV charging affects mainly the base load. The reasons for
the above lie on the fact that even though a lot of EVs penetrate the grid, the three different
charging patterns spread the charging loads over many hours of the day. The results
indicate that the ability to spread the peak load or transfer it to later hours would play a
vital role in the need for new grid infrastructure and the avoidance of congestion problems.
Electric vehicles and the implementation of the Off­Peak and the Vehicle to Household
charging lowers the peak load for a small percentage of households but as more EVs
penetrate the grid, new peaks start to arise during off­peak hours and season, either
late at night or in the summer afternoons (Figure 4.6). Therefore, it is imperative that

current pricing schemes should be modified for the most effective integration of EVs in
electrical networks as per Hoarau and Schittekatte [7, 70]. The combination of EVs and
PVs on the same household also poses numerous advantages on a national level. For
low percentages of technology penetration (4.34% and 8.68%), the left parts of the load
duration curves (peak loads) of Figure 4.6 and Figure 4.8 are almost identical, signifying
that these loads occur in hours when PV impact on the net import is low (hours with little to
no sun) and that the impact of EVs as storage systems is not enough to actually alleviate
the network. On the other hand, the differences between the Figures 4.6 and 4.8 are much
more distinct for higher percentages of technology penetration. As more EVs with Vehicle
to Household charging enter the grid, their impact on the reduction of the peak loads is
more notable, and through the support PVs offer, they further reduce the peak loads. The
new peaks that EVs cause during off­peak hours and season are mainly during summer
afternoon hours, when high PV production reduces EV impact and the net import from the
grid.
Finally, this study shows that the current pricing scheme in Denmark does not suffice
when new technologies, and especially EVs, penetrate the grid. Most of the peak loads
take place from 16:00 to 18:00, indicating that the current network peak price timeframe
should be expanded. Moreover, the seasonal ToU tariff design does not consider the
elevated summer afternoon loads that appear due to EV charging. Although peak loads
are unaffected when applying the current ToU tariffs throughout the year, since they occur
during winter months, the base load is greatly influenced. This is because EVs avoid
charging during the early afternoon hours when users are active in their households [65]
and the stored energy of the vehicle supports the further reduction of the residential load.
As a result, the high electricity demand of household consumption and EV charging during
summer months is transferred to later hours, usually late in the evening, when the majority
of normal consumers are asleep [65]. In conclusion, the effect of an all season ToU tariff
shifts the load duration curve towards a more ideal case, a constant load throughout the
year.

Assumptions Discussion and Model Limitations
In this chapter we discuss the assumptions made during the construction of the model
and how those affect the results and limit its potential.
As mentioned in Chapter 3.2, the model is based on real consumption and production pro­
Modeling the economic and technological
impact of EVs and PVs in the Danish electricity distribution network
33
==PAGE:44==
files of the Danish electricity network. Consequently, the results we present and discuss
in chapter 4 and 5.1 are greatly influenced by and focused on the Danish market. First of
all, PV production data considers only the greater area of Copenhagen disregarding the
various production profiles that might occur in other parts of the country due to different
weather conditions. Furthermore, we only consider a 5 MW PV installation per household
while installations and, as a result, production differ depending on the exploitable surface
of the roof. In this case, it is possible to consider three sizes of PV installations, one per
household surface category (Chapter 3.1.2) but it is not implemented in this study due to
computing limitations.
As far as EVs are concerned, two specific models were taken into consideration for the
two highest income categories based on Danish consumer preferences [57]. Electric ve­
hicle specifications like battery capacity, charging capacity, and consumption are heavily
dependent on the model and the manufacturer. Consequently, if we considered other EV
models or averages of numerous EV models, the results would be similar but not identi­
cal. Moreover, we consider that the charging capacity of the EV is always at its maximum
(11kW) while EV charging patterns are usually far from constant[3]. A more complicated
charging pattern would increase the total charging time of the vehicles, the coincidence
factor and, consequently, effect the total load. Nevertheless, we opt for a simpler charg­
ing pattern in favor of model simplicity and computing power. Furthermore, the provided
data indicated that only high­income households purchase an EV and there are studies
that support this theory. Although, the decreasing cost of EVs the past 5­10 years has
made them accessible to everyone and, as a result, low­income families could also be
considered as potential EV owners. Including all income categories of consumers can
make the model reflect reality more accurately and provide more precise results.

6
Conclusion and future work
The goal of this thesis is the evaluate the economic and technological impact of EVs and
PVs on the Danish electricity distribution network. This chapter summarizes all the major
outcomes of this thesis and then presents suggestions and recommendations for future
work.
6.1
Conclusion
This study considers seventy­two scenarios for different technology penetration levels,
technology mixes, and pricing schemes. The study shows that total consumption and
network prices are inversely related. When the penetration of new technologies elevates
the total consumption, prices drop to ensure DSO income neutrality and vice versa. How­
ever, the mix of penetrating technologies heavily affects the rate of this increase and de­
crease. For the realistic future scenario when 22% of households own either or both PVs
or EVs, prices are expected to rise by 4­5% compared to the status quo. The penetration
of EVs that also serve as remote energy storage for the household decreases network
prices by 1% for every 4.34% of residences that purchase this technology. When only
PVs penetrate the grid, network prices increase by almost 2% for every 4.34% of users
that become prosumers. Finally, combining the EV’s battery as energy storage and a PV
installation decreases the yearly consumption and only elevates network prices by 0.3%
for every 4.34% of residences that install them. As EV penetration increases in the fu­
ture, the bill of ordinary consumers will gradually decrease by up to 120 DKK. Network
users considering purchasing an EV should expect a 700 DKK increase on their yearly
network bill. In contrast, combining a PV system only increases the annual payment by
approximately 200 DKK.
On a national level, this study highlights that the elevated yearly loads that EV charging
causes can be negated by the PV installations. The most notable differences in the load
duration curve when technology penetration increases are only when only one of the two
technologies is present. The combination of an EV energy storage supported by a PV
system is the optimal scenario for both consumers and the network. The influence of
EVs with Vehicle to Household charging on peak loads decrease becomes increasingly
apparent as more of them enter the grid, and they further reduce peak loads thanks to
Modeling the economic and technological
impact of EVs and PVs in the Danish electricity distribution network
35
==PAGE:46==
the assistance that PVs provide. Furthermore, the elevated consumption of EVs during
the summer afternoon hours is counterbalanced by the high PV production, negating the
appearance of new peaks and reducing the total net import.
We conclude that the current Danish network pricing scheme will not suffice in the future.
The absence of a peak tariff during summer afternoons will impel the concurrency of high
residential consumption and EV charging, forming new off­season peaks. However, the
all­season ToU tariff transfers EV charging to after midnight hours and shifts the baseload
towards a constant number throughout the year.
6.2
Future work
This study investigated EVs’ and PVs’ economic and technological impact on the Dan­
ish electricity network. This section suggests topics that can be further developed and
researched.
Section 5.2 presented most of the suggestions that can improve and develop this study
since they constitute its limitations. First, by incorporating low­income households in the
equation, we can study a much more significant percentage of technology penetration
and, consequently, fully prove the price estimation tool that we present in tables 4­1 to
4­4. Furthermore, a Danish population and household census can provide crucial infor­
mation on the spread of residences to include different PV production profiles for vari­
ous locations in Denmark. Additionally, electricity prices could be included as input for
the charging/discharging of vehicles and analyze potential differences with the current
model. Finally, this study considers that the DSO’s revenue remains the same. An inter­
esting topic for further development would be formulating the price estimation equations
of tables 4­1 to 4­4 for an increasing DSO revenue. In other words, provide a two­variable
equation to the DSO that uses the technology penetration estimation and the percentile
increase of DSO revenue as inputs and exports the appropriate future price variation.
