ABSTRACT
Loss of mains is still a problem to be solved in distribution networks. This unintentional
operating condition occurs when the generation unit keep feeding the load from the same
distribution system without the stabilizing response of grid connection, forming a so-called
islanding event. Grid codes includes a section covering this issue.
The increasing penetration of inverter based generators reinforces the presence of power
electronics in the distribution system, resulting in diﬀerent behaviours from rotational
machines.
These responses must be registered by the passive detection methods
which continuously monitor the typical disturbances in the network caused by the grid
disconnection. This project investigates the behaviours of the system that the existing
LOM-detection methods must be ready for, through an experimental point of view in order
to be used as a tool for the improvement of the grid codes.

1
INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background
In the past, the dynamics characteristics and resulting security of the power system were
determined by the presence of synchronous generators. However, nowadays inverter based
generators (IBGs) such as wind turbines generators (WTGs) and photovoltaics (PVs)
have spread around the world in a way that their inﬂuence can no longer be neglected,
resulting in the fact that inverter base generators are beginning to dominate the dynamic
performance of the power system [1].
The increasing penetration of IBGs in some areas is reaching over 50% [1], which alters the
capacity of the network to withstand a wide range of contingency events. The fact that
traditional generators are decreasing in number arises stability issues due to the absence of
stabilising control (such as Automatic Voltage Regulator and Power System Stabilizers).
Nevertheless, these issues must be conducted by the IBGs own controllers and algorithm
techniques, which also implies an evolution of the grid codes by means of certain ancillary
service capabilities such as voltage and frequency control. Among other concerns, Loss of
mains detection it is still consequence that researchers are trying to solve.
1.1.1
Diﬀerences from LV distributions systems with rotating machines
The transition between traditional generators to the inclusion of IBGs requires the latter to
acquire new features for stability purposes. Updating the grid codes, some of the intrinsic
features of traditional generators can be added in the form of an algorithm or control
scheme. Table A.1 [1] in Appendix summarizes the main diﬀerences between IBGs with
minimum functionalities and conventional synchronous generators with standard AVR and
turbine governor.
As observed, there are many diﬀerences related to the control of system stability between
rotational machines and inverter based generators.
As mentioned above, it is the
responsibility of the control strategies and grid code regulation to ensure that IBGs
are able to balance these diﬀerences.
1.2
Problem statement
Hereafter, the challenges on which this project focuses are shown. Loss of mains and its
corresponding detection comprises the core by which this thesis will be developed.

1. Introduction
1.2.1
Loss of mains (LOM)
Loss of mains is still a problem to be solved in distribution networks. The unintentional
operating condition occurs when the generation unit (or units) keep feeding the load from
the same distribution system without the stabilizing response of the above grid connection,
forming a so-called islanding event. This process may occur when the grid is disconnected
from the distribution unit (caused by a fault, maintenance, etc), originating diﬀerent issues
linked to:
- Safety for personnel
- Protection of the unit
- Protection of the grid from unexpected voltages and currents
- Asynchronous coupling with the grid
The project will focus on a grid disconnection not caused by a fault. There are many
scenarios where this could happen:
- Scheduled maintenance
- Switching to ﬁnd placement of earth fault (where the fault is not in the tested grid)
- Load shedding
- Fault far from production unit in a radial grid (here the case of reclosure is critical)
The presence of power electronics in the distribution system is increasing for several reasons,
since the trend aims towards a decentralized power system direction. This fact narrows
the scope of this study to inverter-based distribution networks. It is known that inverter
control strategies are aimed at facilitating the electrical system stability, but they are also
likely to increase the risk of islanded network operation (increase of non-detection zones)
by interfering with the accuracy of the islanding passive detection methods. This is the
reason why inverter-based generation units present their own inﬂuential parameters in
islanding events (IPIE).
1.2.2
Non detection zones
Non-detection zones (NDZ) reveals the conditions when the protection system may fail
to detect the islanding operations.
Through monitoring several network parameters,
passive methods aim for the detection of disturbances in the network caused by the grid
disconnection. At present, only islanding passive detection methods are allowed in the
Danish power system [2].
1.3
Objectives
The aim of this project is to create test scenarios that are plausible in real grid con-
ﬁgurations and unravel conclusions from LOM behaviour under a real distribution grid
conﬁguration. Obtaining a sustained islanding event by means of experimental approaches
is key for the development of this work. The study of the inﬂuence of external parameters
such as the phase angle at the moment of breaker opening results also relevant for the
fulﬁlment of the project objectives.

Once the analysis of the diﬀerent behaviours is accomplished, some recommendations for
the current detection techniques will be given in order to optimize the detection techniques
stated in the grid codes from an experimental point of view. It is for that necessary to
understand the behaviour of the PV inverter under islanding conditions, so as to the NDZ

1.4
Methodology
The literature is complete of theoretical studies analysing the isolated behaviour of certain
aspects that present during the LOM. The line of study of this circumstances focuses on
the theoretical approximation by means of computerized simulations of such scenarios.
Then, this is followed by the experimental phase where the scenarios designed during the
analysis are physically reproduced. Nonetheless, since real distribution conditions are not
fulﬁlled on this way, it is possible that certain behaviours of the system (that could lead to
new recommendations for the grid codes) are missed.
On the other hand, this work is developed in real facilities, ranging from power system
components to cabling and ultimately the absence of ideal conditions. The methodology
in all cases will consist on the fulﬁlment experimental case scenarios, leading to hypothesis
and in turn, development of new case scenarios in order to achieve conclusions.
1.5
Thesis outline
This report it is structure as follows.
First, an introduction to the topic and the
problems involved has been presented. Chapter 2 adds all the necessary background
get an understanding of the situation of this issue in nowadays power system, and the
latter analysis that will be presented on the following chapters. Chapter 3 presents the
installations were the experiments were developed, which also adds relevant information for
the analysis of the experiments. Chapter 4 contains the diﬀerent stages of the experiments.
It analyses those cases where the loss of mains was quickly detected by the system, fulﬁlling
the requirements stated in the grid codes. Thereafter it evaluates those scenarios including
temporary unintentional islanding and eventually, the cases where a sustained islanding
event was accomplished will be consider. The main results will be summarized in Chapter
5. Finally, the closing Chapter 4.3 of this report is made by a summary of the conclusions
from this work and recommendations collected from them in order to improve the loss of
main detection in inverter based distribution grids.

LITERATURE REVIEW
This chapter aims to provide general knowledge of the current situation of the distribution
networks regarding the LOM issue. Along the following pages the contemporary protection
methods integrated in the power system will be reviewed. PV inverters control strategy
also plays an important role on this phenomenon, and will be analysed together with others
inﬂuential parameters in islanding events.
2.1
Protection and detection methods
In low voltage distribution systems (LVDS), protection strategies aim to preserve the
appropriate functionality of the grid and its power components as the grid codes state. As
a separate category, the protection section consists of all the methods and parameters that
are available in the LV distribution system in order to prevent undesirable situations such
as islanding events.
Regarding the LOM interests, the loss of mains protection describes the methods that
are applied for the detection of the separation of the grid. Depending on the operation
mechanism employed, these are classiﬁed as: classical methods, which include local meth-
ods such as passive, active and hybrid, remote methods, signal processing methods and
computational intelligent-based methods [3].
In this study only the classical passive detection methods are considered, since they are
the only ones allowed by the Danish grid codes [2] . However, a brief summary of the
remaining methods is included in order to complete the understanding of the need of a
LOM detection solution.
2.1.1
Passive methods
2.1.1
The passive detection relies on the monitoring of a range of electrical parameters at the
point of common coupling (PCC), to determine whether islanding has occurred. When
the electrical parameter under study crosses a threshold value, which has been previously
observed as a characteristic behaviour in the event of loss of grid, islanding is detected [4]
and the breakers activated.

Phase jump detection
PJD method involves monitoring of phase diﬀerence between the current at the PCC and
output voltage of the Voltage source inveter (VSI) inverter. In case of current source
inverters (CSI) is then the phase diﬀerence between voltage at the PCC and the inverter
output current what is monitored. Phase-locked loops (PLLs) are used to synchronise
the inverters with the voltage at the PCC, as it will be shown in Section 2.2.2. However,
during an islanding event the inverter output current remains unchanged while the voltage
at the PCC no longer remains constant. This phase error is measured and islanding is
detected when a certain threshold is reached [3].
Monitoring of voltage and current harmonics
Another parameter to be monitored in inverter distribution system is the Total Harmonic
distortion. Power electronics are known as a source of harmonics due to their switching
mechanism that are transmitted to the load. This fact makes THD a proper tool for the
island detection. This method typically fails for high values of Quality factor, Qf. The
main drawback of this method lies in not having an ideal threshold to be reached [3].
Rate of change of frequency (ROCOF)
The power imbalance that appears after the island is formed may contribute to a change
in the frequency. This change (df/dt) is monitored over several cycles and it is compared
to the threshold values speciﬁed in the grid codes. Then, when the threshold is reached
and is hold during a certain period of time, the circuit breaker trips. Figure 2.4 depicts
the relevance of setting the averaging period.
There are other passive methods based on the same principle, such as Rate of change of
power output (ROCOP) and ROCOF over power (ROCOFOP). The former monitors all
the changes in the power output and integrates those changes over a sample period, while
the later is an attempt of improvement of the ROCOF method. This is due to the fact
that a very small power mismatch at the moment of the islanding event may do the change
in frequency undetectable.

2.2
PV inverter
The reinforcement of renewable energy in the power system has increased the number of
electronic power inverters in low voltage distribution systems[6]. Inverters are needed for the
injection of DC current from the renewable sources into the grid, such as photovoltaics solar
panels and wind turbines. Therefore, their control algorithms allow diﬀerent capabilities
such as the grid synchronization voltage and current control, as well as the standard
ancillary services and protection schemes stated in the grid codes[6].
2.2.1
Structure
Considering the location of the inverter output, PV inverters can be categorized as grid tie
inverter and oﬀ-grid inverters. The former corresponds to inverters having their AC power
output fed into the utility grid while the later operates without connection to the grid. In
fact, due to the intermittent of sunlight, oﬀ-grid inverter precise the addition of a battery
between the electricity generation and consumption[7].
The purpose of grid tie inverters is to harvest as much power as possible from the PV
panels into the grid, which depending on the connection type of the PV modules into the
inverter it is achieved within a variety of eﬃciencies. These are divided in three main
types: central inverters, string/multi-string inverters and micro (or module) inverters. The
following ﬁgure shows the types of grid tie inverters.
Figure 2.6. a) Central Inverter, b) String Inverter, c) Multi-string Inverter, d) Micro inverter
These conﬁgurations are meant to connect the PV modules in series and parallel combi-
nations in order to supply a higher power level in the system. Even if central inverter
conﬁgurations are considered as the past of PV systems due to the lack of individuals
Maximum-Power-Point-Tracking (MPPT) for each of the modules, they also presents the
advantage of higher eﬃciency due to the higher power level compare to string inverters[8].
The case under study belongs to the multi-string classiﬁcation.

Power balancing
The load power ﬂow strategies ambition is to fulﬁl a zero mismatch between the power
generated at the distributed generators and the power consumed by the load. This means
that depending on the amount of power injected by the inverter the load demand may be
fully or partially covered. In the case where the power generation and the load demand at
the LV distribution network do not match, the ﬂuctuations in frequency and voltages are
so evident that the detection methods described above would quickly detect the anomaly
and thus the breakers would open.

Current harmonics
As emphasized in previous sections, the appearance of harmonics due to the presence of
power electronics has increased. This eﬀect must be controlled so that it does not cause
problems to other equipment connected to the utility system. This is why the THD must
be reduced under certain values stated in the grid codes.
The VHD technique measures the Voltage total Harmonic Distorsion at the point of
common coupling and compares it with predeﬁned voltage values. This measurement is
very low before islanding, but after the grid disconnection its value increases. This is due
to the fact that the inverter output current harmonics have no longer a path on the grid
connection to dissipate through, and instead starts feeding the load [11].
2.3.2
The inﬂuence of network parameters
The inﬂuence of intrinsic network parameters is shown below.
Inertia
As previously mentioned, variations in active power imply changes in the frequency of the
system. If the system contains either an induction generator or the load is a motor, the
inertia of the system will inﬂuence the frequency variation. As a general rule, the higher
the inertia of the machine present in the system, the lower the frequency variation will
be[14].
Grid strength
Grid strength has a signiﬁcant impact in producing stability problems in the system. The
main problems associated with a weak grid are[15] :
• High dynamic overvoltages
• Voltage instability
• Harmonic resonance
• Objectionable voltage ﬂicker.

TEST CASE AND EXPERIMENTAL SET UP
In this chapter, the connection between the experiments and the objective of the thesis
will be shown. It is very important to understand the limitation of the available facilities
and how the set up for the experiments will be, as the degrees of freedom at our disposal
are directly dependent on this.
3.1
Test case
As mentioned, it is the aim of this work to make visible and understand the behaviour of
the system under the LOM events by considering scenarios as close as possible to reality.
It implies that the experiments are carried out in a real distribution system, where the
inﬂuence of real power system components, diverse control strategies, underground cabling
and in other words, non-ideal conditions are present to determine the ﬁnal behaviour of
the system after the disconnection of the grid.
Therefore, it is necessary to accomplish an experimental approach with diﬀerent scenarios
until a sustained island is fulﬁlled. Since these series of experiments has not been performed
before on this facilities, the possibility of not being able to visualize an islanding event
exists. Therefore, the line of the experiments is oriented to achieve this purpose in the
ﬁrst place, aiming to get the neccesary knowledge along the diﬀerent case scenarios that
will help to characterize the behaviour of the system under study.
The diﬃculty of a later analysis lies in the fact that all the anomalies happening in the
system are observed as a bulk of responses. Therefore, the analysis will diﬀer from studying
isolated and controlled behaviours (as most of the literature does), by observing real system
responses and their interactions with each other.
The success metric of this of the experimental approach will be measured by the achieve-
ment of a sustained island after the loss of mains. Achieving this condition will collect
some knowledge that at the very least, that may be used to develop recommendations for
the current grid codes. Besides, it would serve as an indicator of the fact that appropriate
experimental assumptions have been followed in order to understand the behaviour of the
system. Observation of the results, hypothesis elaboration and veriﬁcation based on the
literature are the foundations of the followed methodology.
Likewise, the success of this study is restricted to the degrees of freedom that the facilities
provides. whose limitations will be brieﬂy explain in the following Section 3.2. Hence, the

EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
The following chapter contains the experimental approaches applied to the system in order
to observe its behaviour under diﬀerent scenarios. All the measurements are followed
by analytical observations describing the system response for each case. After several
experiments, the gathered knowledge is converted into hypothesis that will lead the
following sequence of experiments.
The chapter is divided as follows. First, the bulk of experiments focuses on the behaviour
of the system when the LOM is detected as the grid codes states. After this, the sequence

Finally, the case of sustained islanding in the system is described.
4.1
Successfully detected islands
In this section, the experiments where the LOM was successfully detected are presented.
Satisfactory detection occurs when the LOM is identiﬁed within the time thresholds estab-
lished by the grid codes, which in our case relates to a 500 ms milliseconds.
In addition, for the sake of the repeatability of the experiments it is necessary to dispose
of the inﬂuence of the breaker opening. In order to see whether the instant at which the
breaker opens (and splits the distribution grid from the rest of the grid) inﬂuences in any
manner the duration of the event, the following experiments are carried out. The results
and their analysis are shown below.
4.1.1
Breaker opening inﬂuence
The relevance of this analysis resides in the fact that for non-fault conditions a switch
is employed instead of a breaker. Opposite to a breaker the switch will quench the arc
without zero crossing. For approximately 60% [21] of power outages the switch (and not
the breaker) will be activated, meaning that if the inﬂuence of this factor is demonstrated,
it can be crucial for the improvement of the LOM detection methods. From now on, the
switch will be refereed as breaker to avoid confusion along the report.
To this end, numerous experiments have been accomplished under the same conditions
previously mentioned in Chapter 3. It has been proved that the breaker opening does not
alter the parameters related to the passive detection methods.

4.1.4
Conclusions
As a results, the following has been derived from the presented results:
• The instant at which the breaker opens does not suggest any inﬂuence on the
parameters under study.
• Both active power operation point and reactive power mismatch at the moment
of LOM show a eﬀect on the frequency of the system. The common factor of this
behaviour lies on the increase of current in the system, meaning that parasitic
reactances causing a larger Q mismatch could be crucial for the eﬃciency of the

4.2.6
Conclusions
As a result, the next statements has been derived from the experiments of this section:
• Cable inﬂuence is a fact. The reasons for this behaviour need to be further investigated,
however the results shown on this section suggest the need of analysis of the parasitic
reactance of the lines.
• The reactive power mismatch hypothesis from Section 4.1 were further analysed on
this study, and its inﬂuence to the slope of the frequency has been shown.
• The PV inverter control strategy and mechanisms may also be aﬀecting the parameters
under study. Special consideretion must be taken towards the PLL and the dq0
transformation under unbalance conditions.
• The unbalance of the system shown may indicate a relationship with the duration of
the island. Further analysis is required to see for instance, its inﬂuence towards the
change of rate of the frequency.
Numerous attemps where carried out along the previous studies and still the reproduction
of a sustained islanding event was not fulﬁlled. Some cases such as Figure 4.31 were close
to it, but eventually the frequency managed to reach the protection thresholds. Literature
suggests that those systems with a higher quality factor presents a greater resistance to
frequency variations (see Equation 2.3.2).
As it is observed, by increasing the capacitance of the line the frequency will experience
more diﬃculties to modify its value. This condition will be fulﬁlled by adding the capacitive
bank on the following section.

6
CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The study of the experiments carried out for this project shows the importance of the
frequency monitoring of the system. As an evidence of this it has been demonstrated that
most of the detections of loss of mains would be achieved in the ﬁrst place by the passive
detection methods associated to alterations in the frequency. In some cases, it has been the
over-voltage detection method which would have triggered ﬁrstly, those cases where the
active power supplied by the PV inverter is lower than 2kW. As seen in these situations,
even if the PV inverter supplies very close to what the load demands (therefore establishing
almost zero mismatch conditions) the network experiences the same consequences as if the
mismatch is not zero, and the island is quickly detected.
As far as frequency is concerned, there is a main parameter which plays an essential role;
the reactive power mismatch. It has been observed how small variations of Q from the PV
inverter results in prominent changes on the ROCOF of the system – leading to a quick
detection by means of the over-frequency and under-frequency thresholds.
This fact acquires greater importance since it has been shown that the PV inverter does
always performance an inductive behaviour causing a mismatch with the reactive power
load needs, even when its setpoint is set to 0. This means that there will be always a
certain Q mismatch even when the load is resistive and thus the aforementioned detection
is achieved. However, when due to the characteristics of the layout the reactive power
mismatch gets close to zero (it matches with the parasite reactances the line) then a closer
value to zero mismatch can be established causing a temporary sustained island.
It has also been noticed that by adding the capacitor bank the possibility of obtaining
a sustained island increases. The reason why this happens is not entirely certain, but
investigating through the literature an explanation based on the magnitude of the quality
factor may lead to appropriate conclusions. Since the available capacitive and reactive
loads where limited under this work this approach could not been tested. In fact, several
system behaviour were not determined based on the reported results, meaning that further
analysis with a full operative RLC load are highly suggested.
As a recommendation, the analysis of the parasitic reactance of the lines together with the
reactive power oﬀset of the PV inverter is suggested in order to always fulﬁll a minimum
reactive power mismatch, leading to a eﬃcient detection by the frequency based passive
detection methods.
