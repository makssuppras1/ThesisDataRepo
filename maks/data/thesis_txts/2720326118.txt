Background
Combustion of solid waste in WtE plants is accompanied by the emission of nitrogen oxides NOx
which contribute to the air pollution, thus it becomes an environmental issue. To reduce and
control the NOx emission from WtE plants, the Selective Non-Catalytic Reduction (SNCR)
method is used where ammonia, cyanuric acid or urea are injected to react with NO in a
narrow temperature range around 1273 K. The inhomogenous waste fuel is causing temperature
fluctuations in WtE plants, which make it difficult to regulate the NOx emission optimal.
Objective
The objective of the project is to evaluate the potential of current deNOx technologies in a
WtE plant with fluctuating temperature in order to improve the regulation of NOx emission.
This aim will be met through screening of current commercial additives and additive mixture,
using modeling to assess their behavior in fluctuation conditions.
Content
A literature study, including an assessment of current in-situ deNOx methods and SNCR
agents/mixture, and their applicability to WtE plantS. Validation of available chemical kinetic
models for the selected deNOx methods. Use of kinetic modeling to evaluate the performance
of the deNOx methods under fluctuating temperature conditions and to assess the potential of
combining SNCR agents.

Abstract
A Waste-to-Energy (WtE) plant where municipal solid waste is combusted, suffers from difficul-
ties in successful NOx reduction due to temperature fluctuation caused by the inhomogeneous
waste feed.
Selective Non-Catalytic Reduction (SNCR) method is used in the WtE plant to reduce NO by
injecting additives such as ammonia, cyanuric acid, or urea. A model is created as homegeneous
batch reactor with restricted pressure and temperature with the relevant kinetic mechanism
and thermochemistry of the nitrogen compounds in ANSYS Chemkin-Pro 17.1. The model is
verified by comparing the model with experimental data from literature. An alternative mix-
ture of the current commercial additives was investigated, mixing either ammonia or cyanuric
acid with urea in different ratios. The additive mixtures have shown poor NO conversion and
therefore will not be used for further investigation.
Temperature and CO were measured in 2017 at a WtE plant in Næstved, Denmark, where
these measurements are used to study the behaviour of the current commercial additives under
fluctuating conditions. Temperature is measured at two different points denoted as Tlower and
Tupper, with both temperatures representing a normal distribution. Tlower varies from 1243 K
to 1463 K to Tupper variate from 1123 K to1243 K. The measured gas composition shows that
CO also fluctuates, with CO being a positively skewed distribution.
The amount of CO is set to 0 ppm and 1600 ppm, respectively, when investigating the behaviour
of the additives with temperature fluctuations. For both cyanuric acid and urea, there is a high
total conversion of NO when CO is fixed at 1600 ppm, where the total conversion is above 80%
and 70%, respectively. For ammonia, the total conversion of No is below 70% in various cases.
The investigation shows that the results of Tlower and Tupper are similar and that the fluctuation
of CO is the primary concern, not the temperature fluctuation.
The temperature is kept at the optimum temperature when studying the behaviour of the
additives at CO fluctuations. Cyanuric acid shows the highest NO total conversion as it is
78% and 88% when HNCO is 500 ppm and 800 ppm, respectively. The total NO conversion for
ammonia is 45% and 51% when NH3 is 500 ppm and 800 ppm, respectively. The total conversion
of NO to urea is 66% and 78% at 250 ppm and 400 ppm, respectively. The results for ammonia
are more impressive as those for cyanuric acid. Therefore, cyanuric acid has shown the best
results in all studies, which is why cyanuric acid is the most favorable additive for the WtE
plant.

Introduction
Energy demand will increase and municipal waste produced will increase as the world pop-
ulation continues to grow. The Danish population produced a total of 3,413,000 tonnes of
municipal waste in 2015, with this amount increasing to 3,482,000 tonnes of municipal waste
in 2019. 49% of the municipal waste in 2019 is disposed of in a incineration plant [1]. In the
late 19th century, Denmark saw a small area called Frederiksberg become more populated and
concerns arose about increasing municipal waste. In September 1903, the first waste incinera-
tion plant with combined heat and power was introduced in Frederiksberg as a solution to the
increasing municipal waste and energy demand. Since then, it has become common practise
to incinerate municipal waste, using the resulting flue gases to generate electricity and district
heating [2]. This process is also referred to as Waste-to-Energy (WtE) [3]. One of the most
common combustion methods for waste incineration is grate firing.
In the following section, grate-fired incineration and NOx formation are described and the
properties of domestic waste are discussed to understand how they affect incineration.
1.1
Grate-firing combustion
The overall design of grate-firing combustion and a more detailed illustration of the combustion
chamber in an incineration plant are shown in Figure 1.
Figure 1: (a) Grate-firing combustion in a municipal solid waste incineration plant. (b) A
schematic drawing of the combustion chamber with the illustration of the different air supply
locations.[4]
Page 1 of 92
==PAGE:10==
The waste feeder in Figure 1 (a) is the inlet for municipal waste. Municipal waste is usually very
heterogeneous in size, so the use of a spreader is necessary to minimize waste size segregation.
The finer particles are burnt as they fall through the grate and hit the rising primary air. The
coarser particles are burnt on the grate, which is located at the bottom of the furnace. The
grate transports the waste through the different combustion stages. As shown in Figure 1 (b),
the grate is divided into four different combustion stages. The first stage is drying, and the
second stage includes pyrolysis, ignition and gasification. The third stage involves charring and
the fourth stage is ash formation. [4]
In Figure 1 (b) the primary air is denoted as PA, where SA stands for secondary air. Both
the primary air and the secondary air have a significant impact on the efficient and complete
combustion of the waste. As illustrated in Figure 1 (b), the primary air is supplied under
the grate, where the other main function of the grate is to distribute the primary air to the
different combustion phases.
So, the primary air serves as drying air for the drying phase
in which the water is released from the waste. The primary air provides the air required for
pyrolysis, gasification and charring in the second and third stages. The first and second stages
together form a zone called the fuel-rich zone. In Figure 1 (b) the O2-rich zone is located in
the last combustion stage because the primary air in the last combustion stage is to ensure
the necessary oxygen for complete burnout of the waste. Therefore, it is important to control
the primary air in order to control the combustion effectively. The primary air, in cooperation
with the movement of the grate, will mix the waste in the different zones to increase waste
conversion.[5]
The primary air’s main purpose is to contribute to the different combustion stages, while the
secondary air’s main purpose is to ensure complete burnout and lower emissions. The secondary
air is supplied to the furnace at three different points and is part of the formation of the following
3 zones: the Fuel-rich zone, O2-rich zone and the Burnout zone. The secondary air in the O2-
rich zone creates a horizontal recirculation zone that contributes to complete burnout.
A sufficient amount of oxygen in the fuel-rich zone causes the nitrogen from the waste to be
temporarily stored and converted into NOx. Therefore, NOx formation can be reduced by using
advanced secondary air staging to limit oxygen availability in the fuel-rich zone. [4] This results
in NOx acting as the oxidant for the volatiles such as CO and NHi and molecular nitrogen is
formed. The formation of the molecular nitrogen with CO/NHi and NOx is illustrated both
reactions 1 and 2. [6]
NO + CO →CO2 + 0.5N2
(1)
NO + NH2 →N2 + H2O
(2)
However, the air-staging in the fuel-rich zone causes that the complete burn-out takes place in
the Over-Fire Air (OFA). [4]

1.3
NOx Formation
Nitric oxide (NOx) is produced during the combustion process of the municipal waste.
Depending on the fuel and process condition such as temperature and fuel/oxygen ratio, then
the formation of NOx can be described with different mechanisms. The three different mecha-
nisms are: Thermal NOx, Prompt NOx and Fuel NOx. [8] A simple overview of the three NOx
formation mechanisms with the essential successive steps are shown in Figure 3.
Figure 3: An overview of the reaction path diagram for Thermal NOx formation, Prompt NOx
formation and Fuel NOx formation. [9]
Figure 3 shows, that both Thermal NOx and Prompt NOx are formed from the atmospheric
nitrogen (N2). The differences between the two formations are that Thermal NOx is an oxida-
tion of N2, where Prompt NOx is a reaction between hydrocarbon radicals (CH) and N2. The
Fuel NOx formation occurs by an oxidation of the nitrogen bound in the waste or biomass. [8,
9]

1.3.1
Thermal NOx
The oxidation of the atmospheric nitrogen can be described by the Zeldovich two-step mecha-
nism, which are shown in reaction 3 and 4.
N2 + O ⇌NO + N
(3)
N + O2 ⇌NO + O
(4)
Under some circumstances, the Thermal NOx formation by the two-step Zeldovich mechanism is
underestimated. Therefore, it is often necessary to include the extended Zeldovich mechanism,
where it is considering the impact of the hydrogen and oxygen radicals on the NOx formation.
The extended Zeldovich mechanism is shown in reaction5.
N + OH ⇌NO + H
(5)
The Zeldovich two-steps mechanism and the extended are both very temperature-dependent
and takes place at relatively high temperature. So, the Thermal NOx formation is significantly
lowered below the mean temperature 1600 K-1800 K.
Furthermore, due to limiting oxygen
availability in the Fuel-rich zone, the Thermal NOx formation does not occur so often. Vari-
ous experiments have tested the Thermal NOx formation at low oxygen concentration and a
temperature below 1800K. [8] One of the experiments used a premixed coal flame, substituting
argon for N2. It proves that Thermal NOx formation does not have a notable impact on the
total NOx formation since the measured NO did not change remarkably. The combustion of the
waste will take place in a temperature range of 1273 K. In the fuel-rich zone, it assumes that
the Thermal NOx formation will not have a substantial impact on the total NOx formation.

1.3.2
Prompt NOx
Prompt NOx formation was first discovered by Fenimore and later confirmed by multiple other
researchers. The Prompt NOx formation is described with a reaction path diagram shown in
Figure 4.
Figure 4: A reaction path diagram for the Prompt NOx formation under slightly fuel-rich
conditions.[9]
Figure 4 shows CH will insert into the N2 triple bound and form NCN. The reaction is expressed
with reaction 6.
CH + N2 ⇌NCN + H
(6)
Hereafter, NCN is rapidly formed to NO by either reacting with a hydrogen or oxygen molecule.
When NCN reacts with hydrogen, it will form HCN and N also shown in 7.
NCN + H ⇌HCN + N
(7)
HCN will undergo several reactions to end up forming N, where the sequences are shown in
reaction 8.

HCN + O ⇌NCO + H
(8a)
NCO + H ⇌NH + CO
(8b)
NH + H ⇌N + H2
(8c)
Instead of reaction 8c, then NH can react with O and form NO and H. The N from reaction 7
and reaction 8 will react with OH and form NO as in reaction 5.
When NCN react with oxygen, it will form CN and NO as shown in reaction 9.
NCN + O ⇌CN + NO
(9)
CN will react with O and form NCO, whereafter reaction 8b and reaction 8c will happen.
Different to Thermal NOx formation, then the Prompt NOX mainly takes place in the fuel-rich
zone due to the high presence of the hydrocarbon. However, the presence of oxygen is necessary
for the Prompt NOx formation to occur. As mentioned previously, the fuel-rich zone will have
a poor oxygen concentration. So, in combustion systems operated fuel-lean or very close to
stoichiometric, then Prompt NOx will not have an essential impact on the total NOx formation.

1.3.3
Fuel NOx
Fuel-bound nitrogen is released during the devolization of the waste as either HCN or ammonia
(NH3). The fuel NOx is then formed through HCN and NH3. As mentioned at the end of
section 1.1, then NOx can act as an oxidant to volatile matter, where it will form molecular
nitrogen (N2). In this case, NO will act as an oxidant for HCN and NH3.
The Fuel NOx formation through HCN will have some similar reaction path as the Prompt
NOx formation. HCN will undergo the same reactions as in reaction 8 to form N, whereafter
N will form NO by reacting with OH (reaction 5).
The Fuel NOx formation through NH3 is described with a reaction path diagram shown in
Figure 5.

Figure 5: A reaction path diagram for Fuel NOx formation by NH3.[9]
NH3 reacts with either OH, O or H, where NH2 is formed. Hereafter, NH2 can react with
different compounds leading to different outputs. When NH2 reacts with either OH or H, then
it will form NH thereafter N as shown in reaction 10. Besides that, NH2 can also be oxidized
to HNO as shown in reaction 11. HNO, NH and N are oxided to Fuel NO.
NH2 + OH ⇌NH + H2O
(10a)
NH2 + H ⇌NH + H2
(10b)
NH + OH ⇌N + H2O
(10c)
NH + H ⇌N + H2
(10d)
NH2 + O ⇌HNO + H
(11)
When NH2, NH or N react with NO, it will in the end forming N2 as shown in reaction 12.
NH2 + NO ⇌N2 + H2O
(12a)
NH + NO ⇌N2O + H
(12b)
N2O + H ⇌N2 + OH
(12c)
N + NO ⇌N2 + O
(12d)
Formation of N2 in reaction 12 is enhanced in fuel-rich regions. On the other hand, the Fuel
NOx formation by HCN or NH3 will more likely take place in fuel-lean regions. In combustion
where nitrogen is bounded in the fuel/waste/biomass, the Fuel NOx formation accounts for
more than 80% of the total NOx formation. This makes the Fuel NOx formation the dominant
mechanism in the combustion process. [8]

Overview of NOx control in WtE units
The NOx pollution is a major environmental concern, since the NOx pollution is harming the
human health such as causing respiratory diseases.[10] In Europe the NOx emission is stringent
to the limit value of 200
mg
Nm3 [11]. Several methods to control NOx have been developed, where
an overview of the different NOx controls used in a WtE units are shown in Figure 6.
Figure 6: A modified overview of the different NOx controls used in a WtE units. The current
overview has been modified to [12]
NOx control is divided into two categories which are Combustion Control and Flue Gas Treat-
ment. The following NOx control methods are under Combustion control: Optimum Combus-
tion, Air Staging, Fuel Staging and Flue Gas Recirculation (FGR). The following methods are
used in Flue Gas Treatment: Selective catalytic reduction (SCR) and Selective non-catalytic
reduction (SNCR).[12]

Flue Gas Recirculation
Optimizing the incineration process by utilizing energy recovery and reducing the output of
flue gas flow rate for a given amount of waste has shown great benefits for respectively from
the economic and environmental points of view.[12] A standard incineration plant with FGR
implementation is shown in Figure 9.
Figure 9: Incineration plant with FGR implementation. [14]
As illustrated in Figure 9, the hot flue gas from the incinerator flows into the ecnomiser,
whereafter the flue gas is recirculated into the incinerator. Flue gas recirculation is the most
efficient and promising combustion control technique that can reduce thermal NOx production
substantially by recycling flue gas in combustion zone, thereby reduce flame temperatures and
total excess air. It sole function is essentially to reducing nitrogen oxides exhaust by regulating
temperature and oxygen concentration.[14]
2.4
Selective catalytic reduction
SCR is a method where ammonia reduces NOx to N2 on a heterogeneous catalyst surface with
the presence of O2. Some of the common heterogeneous catalyst used for SCR is V2O5/TiO2.
Even though SCR has a NOx reduction efficiency up to 80-90% in WtE plant [11], then some
complications make it difficult to implement into the plant, such as the high risk of poisoning by
SO2, vapors of volatile metals and the low operating temperature. SCR takes place at a much
lower temperature, where the temperature window is generally in the range of 523 K-673 K [12].
Page 11 of 92
==PAGE:20==
Due to the low-temperature window, SCR will be implemented after the combustion process,
where the gas flow is cooled down to be within the temperature window.
2.5
Selective non catalytic reduction
SNCR is a widely used method for reducing NOx to nitrogen and water at effectively higher
temperatures (1073 K-1373 K).[15] SNCR reduces NOx in the flue gas by injecting selective
additives directly into the bed. The efficiency of SNCR is between 30%-90% for some appli-
cations. However, the efficiency also depends on the temperature, additives/flue gas mixing,
additives/NOx ratio, and reaction time. [12, 15] Ammonia, urea, and cyanuric acid are the
three current commercial additives the report will focus on. The following section will describe
a more detailed description of the different additives and the optimal temperature condition.
2.5.1
Ammonia
In 1979 Lyon explored that ammonia could be an effective reducing agent to reduce the NOx
emission in a SNCR process. [9, 16, 17] The reaction mechanism of using ammonia to reduce
NOx is also showed in Figure 5 in section 1.3.3 and reaction 12, which describe how ammonia
reacting with NO will form N2. The presence of oxygen is essential for the reaction can take
place, but as described in section 1.3.3 then, ammonia can also be oxidized to NO. Instead
of lowering NO amount, then more NO is formed. Besides that, the reduction of NO with
ammonia has a narrow temperature window where the reduction can occur. The conversion of
NO is often most optimal between 1100 K to 1400 K. Below 1100 K, the NO reduction is not
essential, and above 1400 K reaction, then the oxidation of ammonia will rather take place. [9]
However, the process condition will also impact the process condition, which will be discussed
more in section 3.1.
2.5.2
Cyanuric acid
Like ammonia and urea, cyanuric acid can also be used as an additive agent. The molecular
formula for cyanuric acid is (HOCN)3, which at above 600 K will decompose to isocyanic acid
with molecular formula HNCO. The reaction sequences when cyanuric acid is added into a
SNCR are illustrated with a reaction path diagram which is shown in Figure 10.[9, 18]

Based on Figure 10, then HNCO reacts with OH and forms NCO. NCO can either oxidize
to NO or NCO can react with NO to form either N2O and N2. The reaction sequences for

Opposite ammonia, then the reduction of NO with cyanuric acid has a wider optimal tempera-
ture window goes from 1100 K to beyond 1350 K.[9] The temperature window for the reduction
of NO with cyanuric acid will be further discussed in section 3.2.
2.5.3
Urea
Urea is a salt with the molecular formula (NH2)2CO, which is different from the ammonia
and cyanuric acid phase since both additives are in liquid form. Urea is dissolved in water,
where the injection of urea will be in an aqueous phase. Urea will decompose to ammonia and
isocyanic acid after the water is evaporated from the aqueous urea.[19] This means that urea
will follow the same reaction path diagram with cyanuric acid or ammonia shown in Figure 5
and Figure 10. The temperature window for NOx reduction with urea as the reducing agent
will be further discussed in section 3.3.

thermochemistry are used to understand the chemical process of the formation and reduction

The temperature and gas composition has been measured during the combustion of municipal
solid waste in 2017 in a WtE plant located in Næstved, Denmark. The measurement is used
in Morten Søe Jepsen’s ph.d thesis [5] and sent by Sønnik Clausen from Combustion and
Harmful Emission Control (CHEC) Research Centre, DTU. Sønnik Clausen is the contact
person between CHEC and Babcock and Wilcox Vølund A/S.
The temperature has been measured in the First pass before entering the SNCR system at two
different locations. The two locations are illustrated in Figure 25.
Figure 25: Illustration of the Grate-firing combustion and where the temperature has been
measured in First pass.
The temperature measured close to the combustion process is called Tlower, and the temperature
measured farthest from the combustion process is called Tupper. The location of where the gas
composition has been measured is unknown. CO is one of the gas compositions with an evident
fluctuation, and CO can also impact the additives. This is shown in Figure 15, where the
operating temperature window for cyanuric acid is moved to a lower temperature with the

Effect of temperature fluctuation on NO conversion
The temperature in the WtE plant fluctuates, making it challenging to reduce NO successfully
in the SNCR system with the current commercial additives. Both Tlower and Tupper are used
to examine the effect of temperature fluctuation on NO conversion.
The examination are
performed with the current commercial additives with two different amount. For the additives
can be compared, it has ensured equal amount of nitrogen. The two amount for each additive
are shown below :
Ammonia: 500ppm and 800ppm NH3.
Cyanuric acid: 500ppm and 800ppm HNCO.
Urea: 250ppm and 400ppm NH3, 250ppm and 400ppm HNCO.
From the data from the WtE plant located in Næstved, then the plant has a H2O and O2
concentration of 25% and 6.67% respectively.
This will be used as the inlet concentration
for H2O and O2. Besides that, NO variate from 170 ppm to 220 ppm, so it has decided to
overestimate the NO amount and set the inlet concentration to 300 ppm. Section 5 shows that
CO has an evident fluctuation, so the examination will include with and without CO in the
model. The inlet concentration of CO is 0 ppm and 1600 ppm, where 1600 ppm is the average
amount of CO in the WtE plant. The residence time is unknown, so it has decided to use the
100/(K)
The maximum conversion of NO is at a specific temperature for each additive. The temperature
distribution is shifted to the optimum temperature window to achieve the maximum total
conversion for a specific amount of additive. The mode of Tlower and Tupper are shifted to the
optimum temperature, where the maximum conversion of NO can be achieved.
Two different optimum temperatures with and without CO are determined. Hence, the presence
of CO can affect the optimum operating temperature window for the additive, which also will
be discussed in the following section. Tlower,±x and Tlower,±y is an example of how the optimum
temperature will be denoted for Tlower. x and y are the shifted temperature value. x stands for
the highest conversion of NO when CO is 0 ppm, where y stands for the optimum temperature
when CO is 1600 ppm.
3 different cases are analysed for both Tlower and Tupper.
1. The mode of Tlower and Tupper is shifted to Tlower,±x and Tupper,±x and inlet concentration
of CO is 0ppm.
2. The mode of Tlower and Tupper is shifted to Tlower,±x and Tupper,±x and inlet concentration
of CO is 1600ppm.

Conclusion
Municipal solid waste is used as the feedstock for a WtE plant. However, not all types of
municipal waste is suitable for the incineration plant. Even though Danish municipalities are
getting better sorting their waste, residual waste can still contain waste such as metal cans
or deodorant sprays that cause temperature fluctuation during incineration. Because of the
temperature fluctuation, it has become difficult to find a suitable NOx reduction strategy.
A model is developed as a homogeneous batch reactor with restricted pressure and temperature
and with the relevant kinetic mechanisms and thermochemistry of the nitrogen compounds in
ANSYS Chemkin-Pro 17.1. The model has been verified by comparing the results from the
model with the experimental results. The model is used to investigate current commercial
additives and possible additive mixture. The current commercial additives are ammonia, cya-
nuric acid and urea. The model is able to replicate the experimental data and predict with
reasonable accurately the behavior of NO when the additives are injected. Screening of current
commercial additives shows that NO can be reduces very effectively under optimal condition.
In the model, ammonia and urea were used in various ratio as additive mixture. Cyanuric
acid and urea in different ratios were also investigated with the model. Both additive mixtures
showed poor conversion of NO, therefore both mixtures are not investigated further.
Temperature and CO were measured in 2017 at a WtE plant in Næstved, Denmark. Tempera-
ture was measured at two different locations, referred to as Tlower and Tupper. The measurements
of temperature and CO are used to study the behaviour of the current commercial additives
during fluctuation. The amount of CO is fixed at 0 ppm or 1600 ppm during the study of the
behaviour of the additives during temperature fluctuations. The results for Tlower and Tupper
are quite similar, suggesting that temperature fluctuation are not the primary concern. The
optimal operating temperature for the additives for CO at 0 ppm is not reasonable for the WtE
plant, as CO fluctuates and the optimal operating window is shifted to a lower temperature.
This also shows that CO is the primary concern. However, NO can be significantly reduced
with an optimal operating temperature window where CO is considered in the model. The
injection of the additives should also take place in the optimal temperature window.
Both cyanuric acid and urea show a high total conversion of NO, when CO is fixed at 1600 ppm
with the total conversion of over 80% and 70%, respectively. With ammonia, the total conver-
sion of NO is below 70%.
During the study of the behaviour of the additives at CO fluctuations, the temperature is kept
at the optimum temperature. The optimum temperature is the temperature for the maximum
conversion when the inlet conditions for CO is 0 ppm or 1600 ppm. At the optimal tempera-
ture, when the inlet conditions are 0 ppm, more NO can be formed instead of being reduced.
The optimum temperature when the inlet condition for CO is 1600 ppm results in the greatest
total conversion. Therefore, the average amount of CO must be considered when determing

the optimal temperature in the model. Cyanuric acid shows the highest total conversion of NO
with a total conversion of 78% and 88% when HNCO is 500 ppm and 800 ppm, respectively.
Ammonia shows a poor total conversion of NO, as the overall conversion is 45% and 51% when
NH3 is 500 ppm and 800 ppm, respectively. The total conversion of NO is also greater for Urea
than for ammonia, but not as great as for cyanuric acid, where the total conversion is 66% and
78% when urea is 250 ppm and 400 ppm, respectively.
Based on the study of temperature and CO fluctuation, cyanuric acid is less sensitive and
therefore the most favourable additive for the WtE plants.
Future perspective
This project shows that CO fluctuation is the primary concern in SNCR in WtE plants. The
concentration of CO can be reduced if the municipal solid waste is well mixed before incinera-
tion. Nevertheless, CO will fluctuate to a lesser extent.
The whole analysis of the temperature and CO fluctuation in the conversion of NO is based on a
data set from the WtE plant. From this data set, the average of CO is set at 1600 ppm. A more
accurate average value for CO can be determined if more real data is extracted from the WtE
plant from different time periods. Based on the average value CO, the optimal temperature
and molar fraction for the current commercial additives should be determined.
From the results, cyanuric acid is the most favourable additive of the three additives for the
WtE plant. This may change if other yardsticks are used to evaluate the additives, such as
cost, ease of use, environmental impact, etc. The project focused on reduction of NO and did
not consider ammonia slip, CO2 content, and whether other toxic components are produced
when the additive is injected.
In this project, the model was only validated with experimental data from the literature. The
experimental data from the literature was conducted under ideal conditions and on a small
scale. In contrast, a WtE plant is operated on a large-scale under real conditions. Therefore, it
is recommended to compare the model with the experimental data from the WtE plant. Fur-
thermore, the residence time(s) is set to 100/(K) in this project because the residence time is
unknown. The residence time of the WtE plant should be determined to obtain more accurate
model to predict the behaviour of the additives and NO reduction data.
