Abstract of Master’s thesis submitted February 1st 2017. 
Association among bile acids, the human microbiome and risk factors for metabolic diseases 
 
 
This thesis has two goals: (1) to identify commensal bacteria with bile acid (BA) metabolizing 
properties, and (2) to establish a basis for classifications of BAs based on observed in vivo 
correlations. The thesis has been written at the Technical University of Denmark with the 
collaboration of the MetaHIT project using data gathered by this project. The dataset utilized is 
based on 284 Danish non-diabetic individuals and includes measurements of their serum BA 
concentrations, faecal microbial shotgun sequencing data and a broad range of health indicators. 
The identified BAs include several muricholic bile acids that have not previously been observed in 
human serum. The thesis examines currently identified correlations between health and bile acids, 
as well as our current understanding of BA metabolism by commensal bacteria. 
 
In silico identification of BA metabolizing bacteria was successfully carried out by identifying 
bacteria based on interindividual variations in BA concentrations. The identified bacteria were 
enriched in genes associated with the processing of BAs. 
Correlations between BAs and health was found to be consistent with certain structural traits. Two 
groups of BAs were identified based on shared biological correlations and common structure: 
C3,C7-dihydroxylated BAs were found to be correlated with an increase in insulin resistance, 
though no causation has been established. 
C3,C6,C7-trihydroxylated BAs were found to be strongly correlated with a decrease in adiposity and 
leptin. A causal relationship is suggested based on previous studies.

2. Introduction 
The human microbiome is increasingly being understood as an integral part of human biology, as 
studies in recent years have linked the human gut microbiome to several diseases, to energy 
expenditure and even to autism1. It has been known since the 1920’s that bile acids (BAs) could be 
altered by bacteria into a plethora of structurally different molecules, but it was not until 1999 
that BAs were found to interact with bodily receptors that influence metabolism and the human 
immune system. Since then, several studies have been carried out in animal models to investigate 
the properties of BAs. However, the bile system differs remarkably between vertebrates both in 
terms of production, toxicity and excretion of BAs, making these studies hard to apply to humans. 
Certain BAs are known to interact with the human body, but a larger, unified model for BA 
interaction does not currently exist. 
 
The bacterial metabolic pathways involved in BA metabolism that are partly established though 
current models fail to explain several types of BAs commonly found at low concentrations in urine. 
The further study of microbial BA metabolism is hampered by the difficulty of lab cultivation of 
many gut microbial species, indicating the need for a different approach. The MetaHIT consortium 
is a collective of several partners in academia and industry that attempts to improve 
understanding of the human metagenome. It has provided data from 284 non-diabetic Danish 
individuals. The data includes microbial abundance profiles from sequenced stool sample, 
physiological health indicators and plasma BA concentrations. This data allows us to investigate 
the complex bile system in vivo. With new findings in this dataset, a new piece to the puzzle of BA 
interactions is added, allowing for a more extensive BA-health interaction model. The in vivo 
effects of BAs in humans will be investigated and gut microbiota that have the ability to 
metabolize BAs can possibly be identified using a data-driven approach. 
The goal of this project is to develop methods to assess how health, BAs and the microbiome are 
correlated. While an attempt to prove the methods conceptually will be made, development of 
exhaustive models is beyond the scope of this thesis. Due to our poor understanding of the field, 
an explorative approach will be employed and most methods developed will not be taken far 
beyond proof-of-concept. The project will attempt to identify microbial species with the capability 
to metabolize BAs through a genetic analysis and a statistical analysis. The effects of BAs on 
human health will be assessed by both a receptor-focused study and a structure-activity 
relationship (SAR) study, attempting to identify which chemical properties are correlated with 
different host phenotypes.

me with everything bioinformatics and Damian Plichta for always taking the time to answer my 
dumb, dumb questions.

3. Theory 
Human health, the biliary system and microbiome system is a highly interconnected system and a 
basic overview is required before an understanding of each component can be established.  
The human liver produces two compounds that are integral to the ability to absorb fat from 
ingested food. These compounds are known as primary BAs and are subjected to enzymatic 
changes by the intestinal microbiome, resulting in a large array of structurally similar compounds, 
known as secondary BAs. The primary and secondary BAs re-enter the blood stream to be reused 
in fat absorption. While in the blood stream they interact with several receptors that have been 
shown to influence human metabolism, energy expenditure i.a. 
Various microbial species carry out different alterations to BAs. The presence of a certain 
microbial species can alter the plasma BA composition, which in turn can influence the metabolic 
phenotype of the host. This gives the gut microbiome abilities that are normally associated with 
endocrine systems. More importantly, given that the intestinal bacterial composition can be 
modified with probiotics and prebiotics, the gut microbiome presents itself as a unique, modifiable 
pseudo-endocrine-organ. It would be of great value to be able to identify and catalogue these 
species based on their enzymatic ability and categorize BAs according to their influence on health. 
This secondary BA metabolism system is not well-described in vivo in humans and the specific 
health outcomes associated with BAs are still under debate. This thesis attempts to explore the in 
vivo properties of BAs and find a way to elucidate which bacteria are able to transform the 
structure of BAs and how they accomplish this. 
 
3.1 Structure, reactivity and chemistry of bile acids 
The expression bile acid (also known as bile salt) is commonly used to refer to a group of steroid-
like compounds which have hydroxyl groups attached in certain key positions. The BA derives its 
structure from the tetracyclic steroid structure, consisting of three connected 6-member rings 
attached to a five-membered ring,2 Each ring is often denoted as A, B, C for the six-membered 
rings and the five-membered ring D as shown above in the abbreviations section. Due to the steric 
flexibility of the link between ring A and B, the A ring can take two stable conformers, 5α and 5β3. 
Humans have BAs that assume the 5β conformation giving the BAs, as well as other human 
steroids, an L-shape with a convex and a concave side, as shown in Figure 1B and Figure 1A. All 
descriptions of BA structure and hydroxylation patterns will use Table I in abbreviations. 
Cyclohexane can assume several different stable conformations, giving bound groups different 
properties. The cyclohexane groups of the C24 BAs are all in a chair-conformation, which is 
characterized by two sets of three carbons with opposite directions. Each carbon is bound to two 
carbons of the opposite orientation, much like a classic depiction of a glucose-molecule. Due to 
this spatial orientation, each hydroxyl group in the BA molecule can either assume an axial or 
equatorial orientation. An axial group is orthogonal to the plane created by the ring structure, thus 
pointing either directly up or down in the same direction as the attached carbon in the ring. An 
==PAGE:9==
 
9 
equatorial group is oriented at a 109.5° angle from the axial orientation, pointing out of the ring 
structure. This gives each attached group the ability to point in both directions in relation to the 
ring. The orientations of these groups are generalized in BA chemistry as either α or β, α being 
concavely oriented and β convexly oriented. An axial α and an axial β group are thus pointed away 
from each other at 180°, while two equatorial groups are only separated by an angle of 39°. 
Secondary BA metabolism results in alternate hydroxylation patterns, yielding different BAs. 
Different names and abbreviations are used for each hydroxylation pattern, as seen in table I in 
abbreviations. 
BAs differ from cholesterol in the number and positioning of hydroxyl groups. The most commonly 
found hydroxylations are 3α, 7α and 12α hydroxylations. BAs gain their amphiphilic nature due to 
the consistent α orientation of these hydrophilic hydroxyl-groups.4 The L-shape and positioning of 
common hydroxyl groups result in an area near the A-B ring connection that has a dense packing 
of hydrophilic hydroxyl groups, sharply increasing water solubility in the immediate area.2,5 This 
effectively gives BAs a hydrophobic and a hydrophilic side, as seen in Figure 1B and 1C.

The 2-methylbutanoic acid group attached at C17 can be conjugated to either glycine or taurine, 
leading to an increase in hydrophilicity. The conjugation of BAs to either glycine or taurine 
dramatically alters the properties and structure of the BAs. The change in hydrophobicity by

conjugation is largely a function of addition of an amide group and a more stable ionic state for 
taurine, which has more stabilizing resonance forms than glycine. Taurine conjugated BAs are 
significantly more acidic than glycine conjugated or unconjugated BAs; pKas of cholic acid (CA) and 
glycocholic acid (GCA) are 4.9 and 4.4 respectively, whereas taurocholic acid (TCA) has a pKa of 
1.4.8 This change signifies a 1000-fold ratio in charged molecules between TCA and GCA.  
TCA and GCA are more than 10 times more soluble in water at physiological pH.9 The solubility of 
BAs vary between different BAs. DCA is the least soluble BA. When dissolved in equal volumes 1-
octanol and water at neutral pH, DCA concentrations in 1-octanol is almost 500 times higher than 
in water, while CA concentrations are only 17 times higher under the same conditions.  
A cornerstone of pharmaceutical drug development is the SAR. SAR models describe how 
variations in drug structure can dramatically alter the properties of receptor binding and they 
allow for quantified descriptions of drug efficacy given alterations in drug structure. While several 
SAR models exist for different molecules, all the changes induced by the gut microbiome are 
considered important to drug binding. Minor alterations in drug structure have been known to 
turn an agonist into an antagonist and vice versa. Drugs that only differ in chirality have also been 
known to exhibit wildly different bodily effects, one curing morning sickness in pregnant women 
and another causing debilitating birth defects.10 LogD is also considered one of the most important 
factors in receptor binding, which may cause conjugated BAs to act very differently in the body 
than their unconjugated counterparts.  
In the MetaHit cohort study 9 different BA structures were identified, either conjugated or 
unconjugated. Their structures can be described schematically by presence, absence and chirality 
of hydroxylations as seen in Figure I (abbreviations).  
The MetaHit cohort identified the hyocholic acid (HCA), α-muricholic acid (α-MCA) and 
Hyodeoxycholic acid (HDCA), all of which have hydroxylations at C6. Prior to this study, these BAs 
have only been found in humans in urine.11 They were previously thought to only be present in 
mice, pigs and other animals, where they are produced as primary BAs. These are likely the result 
of BA conversion by the gut microbiome (see section Secondary bile acid metabolism).

3.2 Physiology of bile acids 
In humans 500 mg of cholesterol is converted into BAs daily, accounting for approximately 90% of 
ingested cholesterol, through two pathways called the traditional and the alternate pathway.12,13 
These pathways will be covered briefly later in this section. The conversion of cholesterol into BAs 
occurs chiefly in the human liver and will be the focus of this thesis.  
 
Figure 2: Schematic model of BA production and circulation. (unknown source)  
Once produced in the liver, BAs are transported to the gallbladder for storage together with 
phospholipids, cholesterol and a variety of other proteins that aid emulsification of lipid 
droplets.14 The contents of the gallbladder is known as bile and is mainly composed of BAs. When 
the food mass that is being processed, chyme, is transported from the stomach to the small 
intestine it passes through the pyloric sphincter. The chyme composition is detected by a group of 
specialized cells located at the opening of the small intestine called I-cells15. If the chyme is rich in 
protein or fat, the cells at the pyloric sphincter release the hormone cholecystokinin into the 
bloodstream. Cholecystokinin triggers the smooth muscle around the gallbladder to contract, 
squeezing the gallbladder and triggering the release of bile.16 The bile is transferred from the 
gallbladder through the common bile duct which joins with the pancreatic duct and connects to 
the duodenum. The BAs and phospholipids emulsify larger fat globules into smaller fat micelles by 
surrounding the hydrophobic masses with amphiphilic molecules.2 Once the micelles reach the 
small intestinal wall, the lipid molecules can passively diffuse across into the blood stream, 
releasing the BAs from their lipid-bound state. Free BAs that are still conjugated are selectively 
==PAGE:12==
 
12 
reabsorbed into the hepatic portal vein by the apical sodium dependent bile acid transporter 
(ASBT), which is situated in the terminal ileum.17 Unconjugated BAs are passively reabsorbed 
through passive diffusion in both the small and large intestine, though this transport is much less 
effective than ASBT re-uptake.18 The majority of passive BA reabsorption likely occurs in the small 
intestine, as blood-flow and surface area is much higher in this area than in the colon, allowing for 
greater total molecular flux. Approximately 95% of the passing BAs are reabsorbed, leaving 5 % to 
exit the body through stool. Once in the bloodstream the BAs pass through the liver, where they 
are reabsorbed to be reused in further lipid emulsification.  A small portion of these BAs is not 
reabsorbed by the liver and enter systemic circulation. This effect is known in pharmacokinetics as 
the first-pass effect, where most of molecules that are subject to liver metabolism are absorbed 
by the liver and a small portion re-enters systemic circulation unaltered and varies greatly 
amongst molecules. A small part of systemic BAs is filtered into urine by the kidneys.19 These 
systemic BAs can thus be measured via blood sample.   
 
3.3 Primary bile acid synthesis 
The purpose of primary BA synthesis is to increase the hydrophilicity of a BA in the concave side of 
the cholesterol structure, giving BAs their unique abilities to form fat micelles. Primary BAs can be 
produced through several pathways. The synthesis pathway is called the neutral pathway and is 
responsible for approximately 95% of BA synthesis and will be the focus of this thesis.20 All other 
identified pathways only result in the production chenodeoxycholic acid (CDCA)21. 
The conversion of cholesterol into BAs involve 17 enzymes that are preferentially expressed in the 
hepatocytes.22 The initial step of the neutral pathway is the 7α-hydroxylation of cholesterol, which 
is mediated by Cholesterol 7α-hydroxylase (CYP7A1). CYP7A1 constitutes the committing step to 
producing BAs and is the rate limiting reaction for the whole enzymatic process.2 The 7α-
hydroxylated cholesterol is subject to a split pathway, in which it can undergo a 12α-hydroxylation 
or not, resulting in CA or CDCA respectively. Enzymatic conversion of BAs after the initial 7α-
hydroxylation is a well-studied process, with relevant enzymes identified and pathways universally 
recognized.

In addition to hydroxylations, alteration of the 2,5-dimethylhexayl is carried out to create a 
carboxylic acid group, 2-methyl butanoic acid. The terminal step in primary BA synthesis is 
conjugation of the C24 carboxylic acid to either amino acid taurine or glycine. Deconjugated BAs 
are also subject to re-conjugation in the liver, but not rehydroxylation. The ratio of taurine to 
glycine conjugations is based solely on the relative abundance of the relevant amino acids and 
appears to have no direct regulatory or functional impact on human health,12 despite the slight 
discrepancy in hydrophobicity between the two. This effect has been shown to be influenced by 
diet, as vegetarians tend to have fewer taurine-conjugated BAs.23 Healthy non-vegetarian humans 
tend to have a 1:3 ratio in taurine:glycine conjugation.24 Further description of the impact of 
hydroxylation and conjugation can be found below in the section Structure, reactivity and 
chemistry of bile acids. 
The specifics of these hepatic hydroxylations and conjugations vary greatly from species to 
species. Different animals produce BAs with alternate orientations, number and positions of 
hydroxyl groups and conjugated amino acids. No model organism mimics human BA production, 
which makes applying animal studies to humans very difficult.

3.4 Interactions with the gut microbiome 
Most studies of the gut microbiome have been carried out on faecal samples, due to the 
convenience of sampling. However, due to the circulation of BAs, the majority of BAs that re-enter 
the blood stream are converted in the small intestine. Studies on the small intestine revealed that 
it is much less densely populated than the colon, showing only 10 to 103 colony forming units 
(CFU)/mL in the stomach and duodenum, 104-107 CFU/mL in the jejunum and ileum.25 The 
compositions of these bacterial communities can be seen in Figure 4A. The colon on the other 
hand holds concentrations of 1011-1012 CFU/mL. This difference in abundance reflects the very 
different environments the microbiota have to inhabit. The stomach has a lower pH, imposing 
harsher selective pressure on colonizing microbes. Most studies of the small intestinal microbiome 
have been carried out on patients who have died from sudden arrhythmic death syndrome or 
through ileostomies.26 Ileostomies are commonly inserted when colon cancer or inflammatory 
bowel disease necessitates the complete removal of the colon. In a study carried out by E. 
Zoetendal et al. in 2012,26 microbial compositions from ileostomy effluent is compared to the 
composition of samples obtained at various points in the digestive system by Troost et al. in 
200826. While sample sizes were very low, Principal Component Analysis (PCA) revealed that the 
ileostomy samples showed little compositional similarity to the microbiome of the terminal ileum, 
making previous ileostomy composition studies hard to apply to this project. Faecal samples were 
found to have some compositional similarity to the ileal and terminal ileal microbiome. Since 
studies have shown the microbial density to increase through the small intestine little BA 
reabsorption can be expected to take place after the small intestine. The majority of relevant BA 
metabolism takes place in the ileum, making faecal microbial composition a rough approximation 
of both ileal and terminal ileal microbial composition.

3.5 Secondary bile acid metabolism 
The diversity in BA structure arises due to the ability of the human small intestinal microbiome to 
further metabolize BAs through what is commonly called secondary metabolism. Secondary 
metabolism is complex and not particularly well-understood. The secondary metabolism of BAs is 
a very broad field, with several different enzymes responsible for each reaction across many 
commensal bacteria. The BA inducible (bai) operon is a group of functionally dissimilar enzymes 
that code for various BA related functions, such as transport and double bond formation and 
certain enzymatic functions are strongly associated with other BAs. Certain  Not all reactions will 
be discussed, as some reactions, such as esterification, does not impact serum BA composition.28 
The BA reactions that result in secondary BAs found in serum can be broadly categorized in three 
different ways: 
1. Deconjugation 
2. Ketone formation and breakdown 
3. Double bond formation and breakdown 
Deconjugation of the attached amino acid is the initiation step for further processing, which is 
simply the removal of the attached taurine or glycine, leading to the reformation of the carboxylic 
acid.29 This step is carried out by a group of enzymes called Bile Salt Hydrolases (BSHs). BSHs are 
widespread through many small intestinal and large intestinal bacteria and can hydrolyse both 
glycine and taurine, although few show a strong preference.30  
Ketone and carbon-to-carbon double bond formation result in BA structures that can be 
considered intermediaries between two “actual” BAs. The purpose of double bond formation is 
further enzymatic processing and is as a result rarely observed in large concentrations in serum. 
Ketones are often the intermediary step between two epimerized BAs. Ketone formation is carried 
out by the hydroxysteroid dehydrogenases (HSDH) family and certain members of the bai operon. 
HSDHs oxidize hydroxyl groups into ketones, making the carbon sp2 hybridized, removing any 
chirality previously associated with the atom. HSDHs responsible for the formation of both 
chiralities are often co-present in the same microbial species.30 This ketone is subject to re-
hydroxylation, but at a different chirality. An example of such a reaction is the creation of UDCA 
from CDCA. Bacteria that express 7α-HSDH and 7β-HSDH have been shown to successfully 
epimerize CDCA to UDCA as seen in Figure 5.31,32  
==PAGE:16==

Several genes in the bai operon are involved in double bond formation. baiE has been shown code 
for 7α-dehydratase, which catalyses double bond formation between C6 and C7 by removal of a 
hydroxyl group at C7, creating the intermediate 6Δ-BA.33 This double bond later undergoes 
addition of two hydrogen atoms through an unknown mechanism, effectively leading to the 
removal of a hydroxyl group through reduction of the C7 carbon. This unknown reaction is 
hypothesized to be mediated by bai I induced enzyme 7β-dehydratase34. If this process is carried

on Figure 7.

These three types of reactions lead to 5 possible endpoints. Deconjugation reactions result in 
deconjugated BAs, ketone formation either results in the formation of a ketone, but is more likely 
to lead to epimerization. Double-bond formation can theoretically result in a BA with a carbon-to-
carbon double bond, but these have never been encountered in vivo and results in the removal of 
a hydroxyl-group in a condensation reaction.  
In primary BAs, all three hydroxyl groups are subject to enzymatic alteration, although not to the 
same degree. The C7 hydroxyl group tends to be the most reactive, the C12, the least reactive and 
the reactivity of the C3 hydroxyl group lies somewhere between the two.35 The reaction types are 
also not all equally likely to occur: in vivo studies of the relative rate of the different types of 
hydroxyl-reactions in vivo, showing deconjugation to be the most common, followed by 
dehydroxylations.35 Epimerisation and oxidation are far less common, though oxidation is the 
rarest.35

several other non-enzymatic functions have been found, such as the cbsT gene, which codes for a 
BA transport pump and have been shown to be involved in the transport of TCA and CA and are 
located in the same operon as certain BSHs.37,38

3.6 BA’s effect on microbiome 
The human gut microbiome’s extensive secondary metabolism indicates an evolutionary incentive 
to metabolize BAs. The exact nature of the interaction is unknown as yet, however some studies 
have suggested various routes of interaction between BAs and the gut microbiome that positively 
contribute to the survival of certain commensal bacteria. 
The amphiphilic nature of BAs allows them to interact with bacteria in a number of ways that are 
detrimental to the stability of the cell membrane of the bacteria. It has been suggested that 
conjugated BAs can enter the cytosol of a bacteria when protonated, due to their relatively high 
hydrophilicity, give off a proton, thereby gaining an ionic charge and reducing the local pH.40 This 
is corroborated by several studies that find bsh+ bacteria higher bile tolerance.41,40,42 With the 
increase in polarity following the gain of a negative charge, the BAs cannot leave the microbe 
before reprotonation, allowing the BA to confer disruptive effects more effectively.40  
BAs have also been shown to damage the DNA of the microbiota by exuding oxidative stress on 
the bacteria.43 The antimicrobial properties of BAs have been shown to increase with 
hydrophobicity.40 Since the secondary BA metabolism tends to increase hydrophobicity, some 
==PAGE:19==
 
19 
have suggested that certain BA-resistant microbiota produce hydrophobic BAs to outcompete 
neighbouring microbiota.14 Deconjugation of taurine provides a stable source of sulphur in an 
environment where it is hard to access. This is supported by fact that certain gut microbiota 
undergo germination in an environment that is rich in taurine40. This suggests an increased 
survivability in parts of the gut that exhibit high amounts of taurine, such as the small intestine.  
 
3.7 Bile acid-health interactions 
BAs were conventionally thought to only solubilize lipids and aid in their absorption. This 
understanding has been challenged in recent decades by several studies that find BAs to be 
correlated to metabolism and energy regulation. This indicates a secondary role as metabolic 
hormones, in addition to their well-known emulsifying properties. In 1999, the orphan receptor 
Farnesoid X receptor (FXR) was found to be activated by CDCA, DCA and lithocholic acid (LCA), 
uncovering a new function of BAs.44 Ever since then the role of BAs have been expanded upon and 
is still under heavy investigation, as new receptors are uncovered and old ones described further. 
The specifics of the correlation between BAs and human metabolism is a somewhat new and 
contentious area of research. The consensus within the field indicates that plasma BAs are related 
to health in a number of ways, although their specific effects are still under investigation.  
The full complexity of BA-health interactions cannot be explained comprehensively given the 
scope of this report. Certain key aspects to the project and interesting finds will be explored to 
give a sense of the range of effects BAs are involved in and the current state of the art.  
 
 
3.7.1 Farnesoid X Receptor 
FXR was the first receptor to be identified as a BA-sensing receptor. It is expressed in a wide array 
of tissues and is activated by CDCA, CA, LCA and DCA. FXR is in the nuclear receptor family, which 
can directly regulate expression of genes by binding to DNA.45 Immunostaining of rat tissue have 
shown FXR to be expressed in liver, brown fat, pancreatic β-cells, spleen, small intestine, adrenal 
glands and thyroid glands.46,18,47 Activation of FXR in liver tissue induces the production of the 
protein small heterodimer partner, which represses the transcription of CYP7A1 in liver tissue, 
which is the initial step in primary BA production.48 This creates a negative feedback loop, 
inhibiting further production of BAs. FXR has four isoforms, two of which are pseudogenes in 
primates. The two other isoforms are tissue specific, as one isotype is exclusively expressed in 
adrenal and hepatic tissue, while the colon and kidneys express another isoform. The small 
intestine shows expression of both isoforms.49 Unfortunately little is known about the roles of 
these FXR isoforms. 
FXR has been shown to be involved in numerous diseases and the metabolism of high density 
lipoprotein (HDL), low density lipoprotein (LDL) and triglycerides.50 In vitro studies of human 
hepatocytes has shown an increase in LDL receptor in response to CDCA, an FXR agonist, 
potentially lowering LDL-C.51 In vivo studies have shown that treatment with guggul, an FXR 
==PAGE:20==
 
20 
antagonist, is correlated to the decrease in LDL,52,53 although other studies show a slight 
increase,54 indicating that an FXR antagonist might be negatively correlated with LDL. FXR has also 
been suspected of playing a role in diabetes, as rat hepatocytes have been shown to upregulate 
expression of FXR in response to high glucose concentrations and downregulate expression in 
response to insulin,55 Studies in mice have found FXR activation to be correlated with improved 
hyperglycemia and dyslipidemia.46 Recent in vitro studies on isolated pancreatic islet cells have 
shown that addition of taurochenodeoxycholic acid (TCDCA), glycochenodeoxycholic (GCDCA) and 
CDCA at nanomolecular concentrations can increase glucose-induced insulin secretions within one 
hour.47 This effect was lost in FXR-knockout (KO) mouse cell lines, suggesting that FXR is integral to 
the increase in insulin secretion, which would explain findings that lean FXR-KO mice have lowered 
insulin sensitivity.47 In vivo experiments have concluded that diabetic mice have increased insulin 
levels in response to FXR agonist GW4064,56 although recent studies have suggested that the 
function of FXR is altered in response to a high fat diet: wild type (WT) mice receiving a high fat 
diet did not respond to treatment with a GW4064; however, WT mice receiving a chow diet 
exhibited a normal response to FXR agonists.47 It is commonly accepted that FXR-KO animals show 
worsened glucose homeostasis,57 though obese FXR-KO mice seem to exhibit an improved glucose 
homeostasis.47 This effect has been suggested to be mediated by to morphological difference in 
the pancreas in caused by HFD, though the mechanism behind this change is currently unknown.47 
FXR activation in the small intestine also triggers the release of fibroblast growth receptor 19 
(FGF19), which has been shown to exhibit effects similar to long-acting insulin, and transgenic 
mice expressing FGF19 showed higher energy expenditure and did not become obese or diabetic 
in response to a high fat diet.58,59 
The exact nature of FXR’s effect on health is still under investigation, though it does seem to be 
involved with multiple metabolic disorders. 
 
3.7.2 The Pregane X Receptor 
 
The Pregane X Receptor (PXR) is also a nuclear receptor. PXR is quite promiscuous and binds to a 
wide array of xenobiotics, including certain BAs and share CYP7A1 inhibitory effects as seen in 
FXR.60,61 It binds strongly to LCA, although it is unclear if CDCA, DCA and CA can also bind to the 
receptor.62 It is expressed in the liver, intestine, gallbladder, brain and muscular tissue.63 The main 
function of PXR is to trigger an increase in phase I and phase II small molecule metabolism in the 
liver, that induce detoxification of xenobiotics and hydrophobic BAs. LCA is a known hepatotoxic in 
animal models, though humans are largely unaffected by LCA, due to the protective properties 
conferred by PXR.64 PXR has been shown to have anti-inflammatory effects in liver hepatocytes, as 
tumour necrosis factor alpha (TNF-α) concentration have been shown to decrease when PXR is 
bound to an agonist as a function of lowering the concentration nuclear factor kappa-light-chain-
enhancer of activated B cells (NF-κB).60 Similar results have been reported in gut epithelia, 
potentially influencing inflammatory bowel disease, although some debate exists on the topic.65

3.7.3 TGR5 
Transmembrane G protein-coupled receptor (TGR5) is a cytoplasmatic receptor and has been 
linked to a variety of energy expenditure and thyroid-hormone functions. It binds LCA, DCA; CDCA 
and CA in decreasing order of binding strength.63 Mouse expression includes intestine, brown 
adipose tissue, skeletal muscles, pancreatic β-cells, cardiac cells and macrophages.18,66 In 
macrophages, it has a similar function as PXR, where it lowers NF-κB expression.67 It has also been 
shown to induce release of Glucagon Like Peptide 1 (GLP-1) in pancreatic cells,67 improving insulin 
secretion and sensitivity. TGR5 has been shown to activate an enzyme that is responsible for 
converting inactive thyroxine hormone T4 into active thyroxine hormone T3, leading to an 
increase in energy expenditure and oxygen consumption in mice.68   
BA receptors have been demonstrated to have a wide range of immunomodulatory and metabolic 
roles, but the overlap between receptor specificities make it very hard to conclude any 
generalizable effect of long-term BA exposure in vivo in humans given the limits of our dataset. In 
vivo mouse studies regarding bile profiles are hard to transfer to humans, as mice and humans 
produce structurally different BAs in the liver, which may impact BA receptors differently.  
BA-induced liver damage is a good example on the extreme variability of BA responses among 
vertebrates. CDCA is fatally hepatotoxic to other primates, including orangutans, baboons and 
rhesus monkeys, and humans are thought to be the only vertebrate species to efficiently 
metabolize LCA in liver69. Build-up of hydrophobic BAs can result in formation of Calcium-BA 
aggregates that block bile secretion in these animal models, leading to fatal cholesterasis.69 In 
humans, LCA is not itself hepatotoxic, as we posses the ability to metabolize LCA through a PXR 
mediated pathway.  
 
3.8 Muricholic BA study 
Muricholic BAs such as HCA, HDCA and αMCA were found in the MetaHit project in surprisingly 
high concentrations, relative to other well-known, BAs. A literature study carried out by Pernille 
Myers and the author did not reveal any source that mentioned detecting muricholic BAs in 
human serum other than the 2014 study carried out by S Jäntti et al, which this project is based 
upon. Due to the perceived lack of muricholic BAs in humans, very little academic interest has 
been paid to them compared to other BAs known to be found in humans. Even fewer studies have 
focused on their in vivo effect, and none describe the long-term effects in humans, though a few 
describe their effect in rodents. Muricholic BAs have found to be a powerful antagonists to FXR,70 
although the literature search has not revealed many experiments that are carried out in order to 
find ligand-interactions with other BA receptors.  
Studies have found that oral administration of various MCAs greatly reduces absorption of 
cholesterol in mice, while cholic acid increases cholesterol absorption and leads to an increased 
total BA pool.71,72

Metagenomic data was obtained through shotgun sequenced stool samples as part of a larger

present together across samples in similar abundances were gathered into clusters of genes using 
canopy clustering.74 Canopy clustering is a computational method for identifying clusters of genes 
that make up microbial entities which is much faster than the aforementioned approach and relies 
on an iterative algorithm for identification of microbial species. In each iteration, canopy clustering 
randomly picks a seed gene and identifies genes that have a Pearson’s correlation coefficient (PCC) 
above 0.9 and Spearman rank correlation coefficient>0.6, forming a cluster named co-abundant 
gene group (CAG). The size of CAGs followed a bimodal distribution with peaks at  approximately

signifies plasmids, phages and erroneous clustering, which we are not interested in at this time.

5. Methods and results: 
Due to the segmented nature of this project, each method will be followed with the results 
corresponding to the method utilized. 
5.1 Identification of bile acid metabolizing species 
One of the goals in this project was trying to identify species of commensal bacteria with the 
ability to metabolize BAs. Three methods were employed to achieve this; one sought to identify 
MGSs, whose genes are suspected to be involved in the BA metabolism, and is referred to as the 
“knowledge-driven approach.” A “species-driven” approach attempted to correlate commensal 
bacteria that had previously been thought to metabolize BAs. A third method attempted to  
identify MGSs with the ability to metabolize BAs based on correlations between BAs and MGSs 
found in the MetaHit dataset, referred to as the “functional approach.” The knowledge-based 
approach took approximately two months, the species-based approach approximately two weeks 
and the functional approach took approximately one month. The knowledge-based approach did 
not show very promising results and was by far the most complex. The results found in the 
species-driven approach were very similar to the results from the knowledge-based approach and 
will not be discussed extensively. The functional approach was by far the most successful and will 
be discussed in more detail. Several details and dead-ends will be left out of the description of this 
approach in the interest of brevity. 
 
5.2 Knowledge-driven approach 
A common way of identifying a species with a certain function is by searching for genes involved in 
the function of interest. This method is commonly used and has been successful in many contexts. 
In a special course preceding this project Pernille Myers attempted to identify genes involved with 
the process and created a map of secondary BA metabolism, as seen in Figure 7. Genes 
determined to be related to the metabolism of BAs were found through extensive literature 
search, since the majority of genetic sequences were sequenced in the 80’s and had not been 
==PAGE:25==
 
25 
properly entered into databases. See additional files for sources and genetic sequences used for 
HMMer construction as BA gene sequences.xlsx. The genes of interest were HSDHs, BSH, members 
of the bai operon and cbsT. Descriptions of the functionality of these genes can be found in the 
section on The microbiome’s effect on BAs. To identify genes that were orthologous with genes of 
interest, we used a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) specialized for identification of genes named 
HMMER. HMMs can iteratively assign the likelihood that a sequence of information follows one of 
multiple trends. It does this by having a set of criteria that indicate that one of the trends is 
followed in the data and assigns scores for each respective state by assigning an E-value to the 
sequence. An E-value described the number of hits one can expect to find with the given trend 
conformity or better.  In identifying certain genes, a genetic sequence of interest is loaded into 
HMMER, which is analysed and for which criteria for gene similarity is established. HMMER allows 
for substitutions of amino acids, taking the likelihood of each substitution into account and scoring 
accordingly. A library of genes be scored according to those criteria and E-values are assigned for 
each. A low E-value indicates that the pattern that is being searched for is the pattern being 
followed.

5.3 Species-driven approach  
As part of the species-driven approach, different subsets of MGSs were chosen based on their 
abundance in the small intestine. A 2012 study by Zoetendal et al.26 described the composition of 
the bacterial community in the small intestine, though little correlation between BAs and MGSs 
could be established. Similar results were found when focusing on MGSs that belonged to genera 
previously identified to be MGS-metabolizing. Associates between these identified clusters of 
bacteria and BAs exhibited few significant correlations. 
 
5.4 Functional approach 
The functional approach seeks to identify MGSs with BA metabolizing functions based on the data 
obtained from the MetaHit project and confirm the findings based on shared genes amongst the 
identified MGSs. This approach allows for identification of BAs regardless of prior knowledge of 
enzymatic function or genes. The central assumption in method is that the presence of a microbial 
species with the capability of metabolizing one BA into another must be correlated with a 
significant decrease in concentration of one BA and a significant increase in another. The dataset 
does not, however, contain every BA in the metabolic pathway. Several BAs were only found in 
less than half of participants and were not included in the dataset. Despite this, the dataset 
contains measurements for all commonly recognized human BAs as well as muricholic BAs and 
some whose structure is unknown.  
There are obstacles that make such an association study difficult: 
1. 284 samples cannot adequately describe the function of 1400 microbes. 
2. Certain microbial species proliferate or die out in the presence of BAs, regardless of their 
ability to enzymatically alter BA concentrations. 
3. Faecal microbiota is a poor substitute for small intestinal microbiota. 
The methodology must be adjusted to work around these problems where possible to in order to 
construct a viable model. 
Data size and microbial composition

to assess the effect of 1417 MGSs using only 284 data points. Adjusting for the effect of all other 
MGSs is impossible given the current number of MGSs. Steps must first be undertaken to reduce 
the number of microbial species in question to approximately 100, as that would allow for two and 
a half data points for each variable described which should be sufficient for the use of a multiple 
linear regression (MLR). In order to reduce the dataset, we will attempt to identify MGSs with BA 
processing capabilities prior to MLR, through two steps:

the capacity to metabolize the BA. The abundance profiles obtained from faecal samples are likely 
not to reflect the abundances in the small intestine accurately. PCA studies by Troost et al.26 
suggests some compositional commonality between the faecal bacterial and the ileal microbiota 
as seen in Figure 4B, as described in Interactions with the gut microbiome. In order to assess 
whether the faecal microbiome can be used to correlate BA metabolism, we correlate each BA 
with each MGS and examine the p-values. If the two factors were uncorrelated, one would expect 
a flat p-value distribution, but if the two are highly correlated, one would expect a large 
enrichment of low p-values, indicating a general trend-conformity as seen in Figure 12.

We observe a definite enrichment of low p-values and proceed with the abundance correlations. If 
an MGS had the ability to metabolize one BA into another, we would expect the abundance of the 
MGS to be correlated with an increase of one BA and the decrease of another. The first criteria for 
inclusion is a significant, positive Spearman rank correlation between the abundance of an MGS to 
a BA and a significant, negative Spearman rank correlation to another BA. Spearman rank 
correlations do not assume a given model of correlation upon a dataset, but merely finds the 
consistency of concurrent increase or decrease between two sets of data. A level of significance of 
0.025 was chosen. This initial step reduced the amount of MGSs from 1407 to 220. To ensure we 
isolate MGSs with a highly active BA metabolism, we must ensure the BA composition is 
significantly perturbed given the presence of a BA.

Concentration t-test 
Since we cannot make assumptions of linearity between the concentration of a BA and the 
presence of a BA metabolizing MGS, some other metric must be established in order to measure 
the extent of BA metabolism. Several of the MGSs being examined are not present in the entire 
study population. The presence or absence of a BA metabolizing MGS should be associated with a 
change in concentration according to a t-test. Much like the Spearman rank correlation test, there 
must be one up-regulated BA and one down-regulated BA. An issue with such a test in the context 
of concentrations is the disparity between lowest detected value and maximum values within a BA 
is very large; approximately 1:3000 in the case of glycoursodeoxycholic acid (GUDCA). In order to 
transform the values into a distribution that was easier to perform a t-test on, the BA 
concentrations were log-transformed to the second power. The concentrations that were below 
detection limit were assigned a concentration of 0 and cannot be log-transformed as is. 
Concentrations below the detection limit were raised to half of the lowest detected value, in order 
to include them into the t-test. This is a common practice in biostatistics. The estimate for up- or 
down-regulation of a BA was estimated by the ratios of median concentrations for the people with 
an MGS present and an MGS absent. T-tests cannot be employed under three conditions and the 
special cases were treated as follows: MGSs that were only absent in one or zero individuals was 
approved for multiple linear regression and “passed” the t-test, whereas MGSs that were only 
present in one or fewer individuals cannot be used for multiple linear regression and were not 
included. If an MGSs was only co-present with BA of the same concentration level, thus exhibiting 
a BA variation of 0, making t-tests impossible, a p-value of 0 was assigned as the correlation 
between a BA and an MGS and median ratios were calculated normally. A significance level of 
0.025 was chosen for the criteria of significant difference in the concentration profile. This 
approach reduced 220 MGSs to 111 MGSs. 
 
Multiple linear regression 
Once these two exclusion criteria have been applied, the reduced amount of interesting MGSs 
allow for use of a multiple linear regression. By adjusting the change in a BA concentration to 
factor the effect of all MGSs we attempt to remove the effect of any symbiotic MGSs that may co-
exist with the MGSs and that exhibit BA metabolizing properties. In order to be considered BA 
metabolizing, an MGS must be significantly, positively associated with the concentration of one BA 
and a negatively with the concentration of another. Strict linearity was considered to be a very 
high requirement, and MGSs were transformed as Boolean values, where false indicated the 
absence of an MGS and true indicated the presence of an MGS. The linear model function in R can 
accept Boolean values as independent variables and correlate them to a set of dependent 
variables, while adjusting for the influence of other independent variables. A level of significance 
of 0.05 was set. This led to the identification of 62 MGSs. 
In order to confirm the ability of these 62 MGSs to metabolize BAs, eggNOG gene annotations 
were assigned to each gene found in the 62 MGSs and eggNOG distributions were compared to 
the MGSs that were not part of the dataset using a Fisher’s exact test. Significance level was set to 
0.05 and in order to correct for multiple testing I employed Bonferroni correction for each tested

5.4 Correlating bile acids and health 
The work carried out in attempting to evaluate correlations between health and BAs took 
approximately one month. The BA measurements were examined and based on statistical models 
basic inferences about the influence of BAs were made. Two approaches were taken where BAs 
were examined individually and in groups. 
Individual examination of BA effects were carried out by treating each BA as a discrete substance, 
with no structural or functional relation other BAs. The method is good for finding relationships 
between individual BAs and individual health outcomes and can be used to gain insight into SAR. 
In the grouped study of BAs two approaches were taken: identifying the effect of activation of 
certain receptor agonists and establishing a connection between the structure of a BA and a 
measured health outcome, also known as SAR.  
 
As part of the assessment of individual BA effects, a partial least square regression (PLSR) was 
carried out to assess the influence of BAs on health outcomes. A PLSR is a variation of a PCA; 
where PCAs identifies orthogonal vectors within an n-dimensional dataset that maximize the 
variance explanation, a PLSR identifies these vectors on a different set of criteria. A PLSR manages 
two datasets, X and Y, and selects a vector within each dataset that maximizes the linear 
correlation between the two datasets as the first factor. Additional factors are orthogonal to 
previous vectors and are selected according to the same criteria as previous vectors. An indication 
of two datasets with a strong correlation is a high explained Y-variance by the selected factors. If 
80% variance explanation of the Y-dataset, the X dataset can be considered to almost fully explain 
the Y dataset in biological contexts. PLSRs were carried out using The Unscrambler X using default 
settings. By log transforming the BA concentrations to the second power and area normalizing the 
health data, the factors could explain 21% of BA data. Despite being far below 80% variance 
explanation, this result is to be expected, as attempting to explain the entirety of a person’s 
health, everything from whether the person smokes to BMI and neutrophil count, based on BAs 
alone is impossible. The result was deemed promising enough to continue the corollary studies. 
Individual health correlations 
Each BA is independently correlated with each health parameter independently using Spearman 
rank correlations. This method is good for exploring the dataset, but does not establish broader 
trends without incorporation of biochemical insights. Since certain phenotypes are associated with 
multiple health outcomes, p-values will not be used as strict cut-offs for significance, but will be 
evaluated in the context of other observed associations. The level of significance will be set to

5.5 Bile acid grouping 
While structurally related BAs tend to be correlated to the same general phenotype, these 
overlaps are far from perfect. Only three out of six of the C3, C6 hydroxylated BAs are correlated 
with health measurements. If a group of BAs act in concert to induce an effect on health, a 
decrease in one BA could be compensated for by an increase in another, inducing a similar effect 
on health. Such a relationship would be hard to detect using independent correlations. Since there 
appears to be an underlying correlation between structure and health outcomes, groups are 
identified that all share these traits, despite the lack of correlations on an individual level. 
Three groups BAs with consistent structural traits can be identified:  
1. MCA group – includes HCA, HDCA, αMCA and their respective conjugations 
2. DCA group – includes DCA, 7oxo-DCA and their respective conjugations 
3. CDCA group – includes UDCA, CDCA, and their respective conjugations. 
In order to test the hypothesis that structurally related BAs act to induce similar health-effects, 
despite lack of evidence based on individual correlations to health, a 3-step pipeline is developed. 
The muricholic BA group will be used as an example: 
1. Structural confirmation  
Since steric variability can potentially influence the effect of BAs, the 3D structures are compared 
to confirm overlaps hydroxylation patterns. Important hydroxylations must be consistent, not only 
in terms of location, but also spatial orientation. All 3D structures of bile muricholic group are 
downloaded from PubChem and their structures are aligned using PyMol’s align tool as seen in 
Figure 13.

to a health-correlation and the rest of the grouping is unimportant, the observed correlations 
should be present regardless of the concentration contribution from HCA and TαMCA. In order to 
test this, GHCA and THCA concentrations will be added together and assigned concentrations of

then all BAs must also play a role in conveying an observed phenotype, indicating that the 
grouping is indeed meaningful. Over several rounds of shuffling, correlations to the observed 
phenotype was drastically lowered, indicating that all members of the grouping act in concert 
induce a phenotype.

5.7 Receptor-based study 
Certain receptors have very well-studied interactions with BAs, with all common BAs tested for 
agonistic effect. However, some BAs, such as CDCA, are shown to activate FXR, TGR5 and PXR, 
though to different degrees. In order to test if receptor activation could be correlated to certain 
phenotypes, the agonists of 7 receptors were added together, much like the groupings based on 
structure alone. Agonists described as only weakly activating receptors were not included in the 
study. None of the receptor-based groupings were found to have consistent correlations to health 
that could not be explained by SAR model established earlier.

6. Discussion 
6.1 MGS-identification 
6.1.1 Knowledge-based approach 
The knowledge-based approach was largely unsuccessful. The HMMER gene assignment was likely 
too promiscuous when assigning certain genes whose function are very common in commensal 
bacteria, leading to a high false-positive rate. Certain genes were annotated to significantly more 
BA-related genes than others, namely baiA, baiCD and baiH. baiA is responsible for the oxidation 
of the C3 hydroxyl group into a ketone, a reaction that could similarly be carried out on 
cholesterol. The large number of baiA genes are likely related to the false annotation of 
cholesterol oxidases. There was a large overlap between baiA and bail, whose function is unknown 
but is suspected to be involved in modification of C3 OH.78 baiCD and baiH encode for homologous 
enzymes that create a double bond between C4 and C5. The homologous nature of these enzymes 
explain the large overlap between them, but not the fact that they were assigned to over 2000 
genes, while baiG was only assigned to 18.  Several rounds of changes of E-value threshold and 
attempts at excluding certain bai-genes that were annotated more frequently than others were 
attempted without positive results. I suspect that the structural commonalities between 
cholesterol and BAs is likely to lead to false identification of enzymes involved in cholesterol 
metabolism. Other microbiomics researchers have voiced difficulties in correctly identifying bai 
genes during a question round following a presentation by H. B. Nielsen at the European 
Microbiome Congress 2016 in London.  
Very large parts of secondary metabolism of BAs is still unknown. Current understanding fails to 
account for several of the BAs found in the study and genes thought to be essential to BA 
metabolism are still uncharacterized. When the field of secondary metabolism is elucidated 
further, perhaps a HMMER-approach will be more successful. 
6.1.2 Species-based approach 
Due to time-constraints there was little effort put into this method. I think this approach could be 
used to refine results from other methods by selecting the species that were known to metabolize 
BAs. Research of BA metabolism have mainly focused on clostridia, though previous studies have 
also shown certain members of bacteriodes to change hydroxylation patterns in ways that were 
have not been observed in other commensal bacteria. If an in-depth literature study was carried 
out into the individual species that have been known to metabolize BAs, perhaps these results 
could be confirmed in vivo using this dataset, although little would be gained from confirmation of 
others results in such a way. 
6.1.3 Functional approach 
The results from the functional approach were more promising than the knowledge- and species-
based approaches, though the results are somewhat hard to compare. Given the fact that we are 
attempting to identify a process occurring in the small intestine by using faecal samples, the 
success of this method is quite surprising. While identified MGSs exhibited enrichment of several 
==PAGE:41==
 
41 
genes that are related to BA-metabolism, no actual enzymes were significantly enriched, 
suggesting BA metabolism involved a more diverse range of genes than previously thought. While 
it is possible that identified MGSs proliferate and die out in response to different BAs, finding 
proteins that import glycine and taurine suggest active import of BAs. Since BAs are generally 
detrimental to the survival of the cell, it would be safe to assume that imported BAs are somehow 
used by the cell. Finding several enriched genes associated with the activity of sigma factors that 
have been linked to bile efflux pumps suggest that the BAs are not meant for permanent 
incorporation, but are exported after some unknown process. The eggNOG groups identified in 
this method could perhaps be used to identify more interesting MGSs using a method similar to 
the knowledge-based approach, though separating false positives from false negatives is hard 
without laboratory experiments. I suspect the linear correlation is not a meaningful step on its 
own, despite being the intial goal of the method. Initially the dataset was log-transformed, 
however when employing the non-log transformed dataset, almost none of the same MGSs were 
identified. The majority of enriched eggNOG annotations were poorly characterized, yet they 
turned out to have homologous genes involved with something related to BAs. Given more time to 
tune the method and study the results in more detail, the functional-approach could likely lead to 
the identification of new genes related to BA conversion.  
 
6.2 Influence of bile acids on health 
6.2.1 Structural study of bile acids 
The simplistic SAR model was successful in correlating certain structural features of secondary BAs 
with health in a manner consistent with recent findings in either animal or human trials: 
The muricholic BA group correlated negatively to adiposity and insulin resistance in a very 
consistent manner. These findings mirror a 2015 mouse study by Bonde et al.72 Primary mouse BA 
production is very similar to human production and is subject to a split pathway, that results in 
MCA and CA. By breeding Cyp8b1-/- mice, normal primary BA production is disrupted and only 
MCA can be produced. By feeding WT and Cyp8b1-/- mice either chow or HFD for 11 weeks, the 
effect of the heightened MCA concentration and lowered CA concentration was established. The 
transgenic HFD mice were observed to have a significantly decreased total blood glucose, liver 
triglyceries and liver cholesterol, approaching the levels observed in WT mice fed a chow diet. 
Food consumption was significantly lowered by in HFD group and the amount of weight gained per 
ingested calorie was reduced, indicating a higher metabolism or lower food efficiency. The 
transgenic HFD mice weighed 30% less than their WT HFD counterparts. This study suggests a 
causative relationship between an increase in MCA and a decrease in appetite, weight and food 
efficiency, which is also mirrored in our dataset. 
Based on the consistency of the correlations, conjugation to glycine or taurine appeared to be 
unimportant. The chirality did not appear to be significant in correlation with health, since all 
stereo isomers were correlated with the same health outcomes. These results suggest muricholic 
acids act differently when paired with a HFD, since few significant differences were observed 
between transgenic and WT mice on a chow diet. These observations mirror our findings, as 
==PAGE:42==
 
42 
decreases in BMI and measurements of bodily width are consistent with a phenotype of lowered 
adiposity. Lowered appetite in Cyp8b1-/- mice is reflected in human correlations between MCAs 
and leptin. The findings by Bonde et al. show a causative relationship between an increase in MCA 
concentration and lowered adiposity, appetite and insulin resistance, all of which have been found 
to be mirrored in humans. 
Additionally, one of the BAs of unknown structure were found to consistently exhibit correlations 
similar to muricholic BAs and show mass-peaks consistent with a tri-hydroxylated BA. Since tri-
hydroxylation is only associated with CAs and MCAs, there is a distinct possibility that this BA is 
also C6 hydroxylated, as is characteristic of muricholic BAs. 
Several of the correlations found between DCA and health have also been corroborated in the 
literature. We found DCA to be associated with a health profile typically exhibited in MS, and 
especially strongly correlated to insulin. DCA levels have previously been shown to be elevated in 
patients with T2DM.79,80,81 The observed correlations to MS may just be an overlap in populations 
displaying T2DM and MS. The mechanism behind the correlation between DCA and T2DM is 
unknown, though it has been suggested that DCA’s antimicrobial effects may induce a change in 
the human microbiome, causing T2DM.79 
The CDCA grouping consisting of UDCA and CDCA were positively correlated with insulin 
resistance. T2DM patients have previously been shown to have lower CDCA synthesis, indicating a 
correlation opposite to the one observed in this study.79,80,81 Conjugation state appeared to be 
very important to the observed health-correlation, since GCDCA and TCDCA did not exhibit the 
same correlations as UDCA and CDCA. Chirality did not appear to be important, as UDCA and CDCA 
differ in C7 orientation, yet correlations to the same general phenotype. 
The observed correlations across all groupings appeared to be very consistent with certain 
structural features of BAs, as similar hydroxylation patterns exhibited similar correlations to 
health. Interestingly, stereoisomers exhibited correlations to the same phenotypes, indicating that 
epimerisation is largely unimportant to biological interaction.  
6.2.2 Receptor studies 
Receptor-based studies were largely unsuccessful. It is hard to assess the effect of receptors when 
agonists bind multiple receptors with different affinities. Since different BAs have very different 
binding affinities to certain receptors, treating them all as having equal contribution to receptor 
activation does not reflect the reality of the situation. The use of binding affinity measurements 
could be employed to better estimate the amount of receptors activated at a given point, thus 
leading to better separation of the effects of a given BA on each receptor. This study is not 
possible given the current state of BA receptor research, as few studies have measured the 
binding affinities of BAs in receptors than FXR. The finding that FXR might change its role 
depending on the health condition of the individual further complicates this issue, making this 
method inviable given current understanding.

6.2.3 Grouping method evaluation 
The use of shuffling as a way of adjusting for the effect of other BAs is highly unorthodox, but 
considering the dataset and time-restraints, it was the only viable option for this project. The 
original intention was to utilize a multiple linear regression for the identification of common 
health-correlations. However, BAs of similar structure are often very well-correlated with each 
other as well as with certain health outcomes. When an outcome is very well-correlated with 
multiple factors that are also mutually well-correlated, the multiple linear correlation tool in R can 
explain the health outcome with one factor alone and R attempts to explain any inherent variance 
within the data by using multiple variables. This is however not always possible given inherent 
biological variation and as such no factor is deemed “important” when multifactorial regression is 
used. This leads to R assigning several co-abundant bile BAs that each contribute to a certain 
health outcome as insufficient explanatory variables of that health outcome, since they cannot 
account for the observed variance within the dependent variable. There is likely a multi-variate 
approach that solves this problem, but given the time-constraints, shuffling was considered to be 
an adequate substitute for proper multi-variate compensation. This method needs to be refined 
and a better SAR model established, but initial findings are very encouraging.  
6.3.4 Generalized linear model 
With the three main BA contributors to health, one can adjust for the effect of the others with the 
construction of the linear model. While such a model does not provide the same predictive 
accuracy as a model containing each BA as a separate entity, it provides a good overview of the 
influence of each BA grouping, while adjusting for the effects of others. By adjusting for the 
influence of other BA groups, the certainty of the influence of each BA group was improved in 
most cases health-descriptors. Inspection of residuals indicates that a linear correlation is not 
sufficient in describing the majority of health-variables completely. Further study is needed which 
may lead to the construction of a non-linear model. The model used does not adjust for the 
influence of other health variables. Insulin concentration is the result of blood glucose – not BAs. 
The influence of BAs on certain health variables can be more accurately estimated when adjusting 
for other health-descriptors, though time constraints did not allow for the research needed for the 
construction of such a model. Linear correlations found when estimating for the influence of other 
BA groupings were largely consistent with the effects seen in grouping correlations.  
 
7. Future perspectives 
The project shows very encouraging initial results, though further research is needed. The 
statistical approach to MGS identification needs further testing, as only one approach has been 
successful at this point in time. Further inspection of identified MGSs could result in the 
identification of several genes involved in secondary BA production. By isolating MGS species 
based on their observed enzymatic ability, undiscovered secondary BA metabolizing enzymes 
could be potentially identified. New and improved eggNOG annotations can be used to identify 
gene enrichments not currently observed.   
==PAGE:44==
 
44 
If the BAs that were unidentified in GCMS could be structurally identified by nuclear magnetic 
resonance spectroscopy (NMR) and the and additional bile acid concentration data is obtained, a 
more extensive SAR model could be established. The collective effects of BAs could be further 
elucidated by employing non-linear models that could be used to more accurately assess the 
effect of the identified BA groups. Given more time to research the binding affinities of BAs to 
various receptors, a more clear understanding of the in vivo actions of receptors could be 
obtained. 
The discovery that muricholic BAs can act as an appetite suppressant in humans is very significant 
and could be used as an anti-obesity drug. No negative side-effects could be observed in the 
dataset, and short-term human studies with administration of low MCA doses report no negative 
side-effects. It would be useful to obtain data from extensive long-term studies in order to identify 
any possible side-effects. If MGSs with MCA producing capabilities could be isolated, they could be 
employed as a pro- and prebiotic treatment as an appetite suppressant.   
 
 
8. Conclusion 
The proof of concept of statistical identification of MGSs was successful, though a more thorough 
investigation into different statistical approaches is called for, as well as a set of criteria that can 
assess the efficacy of the method. The initial results seems very promising, as similar results have 
not previously been reported. Further investigation of structural changes induced by MGSs may 
reveal consistent patterns of structural alteration that could be utilized for targeted synthesis of 
certain BAs in the future. 
SAR-studies showed a strong correlation between hydroxylation-patterns and certain health 
outcomes. Unconjugated CDCA and ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) are correlated with an increase 
in insulin resistance. After an extensive literature study, no mechanism can be proposed for this 
correlation, which has not been described in prior literature. DCA is correlated with an increase in 
classical measurements of MS, which have been demonstrated in humans before, though the 
mechanism of this correlation is unknown. Muricholic BAs were identified in human plasma for the 
first time in humans and were shown to be causatively correlated to a decrease in adiposity, leptin 
concentrations and insulin resistance.  
If a heightened MCA concentration does not show significant adverse effects and bacterial 
secondary metabolism is responsible for the production of MCAs as previously theorized, MGSs 
responsible for the production of MCAs could be used as a probiotic treatment and act as an 
appetite suppressant and lower calorie efficiency.  
The explorative search yielded very positive results. A SAR-study carried out on BAs found strong 
correlations between structural components and correlations with health. A new statistical 
approach shows promising results, that can help further explore which microbes can metabolize 
BAs.
