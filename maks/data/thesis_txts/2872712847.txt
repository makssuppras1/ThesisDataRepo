Abstract
Electricity prices exhibit non-Gaussian characteristics, such as heavy tails and volatility clustering,
which are not typically addressed in Hierarchical Time Series (HTS) forecasting due to their
misalignment with the framework’s standard aggregation structure and Gaussian assumptions.
This paper investigates the applicability of the HTS framework to such complex data, using
intraday electricity prices from the DK1 market (January 2023 - December 2024) as a case study.
To facilitate temporal aggregation, electricity prices are transformed into their corresponding
differences. HTS reconciliation is embedded directly into the loss function of a deep learning
model, combining fully connected neural network layers and long short-term memory layers to
address the data’s complexity and the challenges of reconciliation. The results demonstrate that
HTS improves forecast accuracy, outperforming a naïve model by 7.9% and a baseline neural
network (without reconciliation) by 6.5%. However, applying post-reconciliation to the baseline
neural network or a baseline ARMAX model yields negligible or negative improvements, likely
attributed to poorly conditioned covariance matrix estimates driven by the data’s inherent properties.
These findings indicate that the HTS framework is effective for electricity price forecasting when
reconciliation is embedded in the neural network’s loss function, shaping forecasts effectively,
whereas post-reconciliation provides limited corrective value.

1.1
Research Question
1. INTRODUCTION
Electricity is a fundamental commodity in the
modern world, powering everything from homes
to industries to critical infrastructure worldwide.
Unlike most commodities, electricity cannot be
stored efficiently, requiring continuous balancing
of supply and demand to maintain grid stability
[1]. This challenge, difficult as it is, becomes
more challenging due to the global transition to
renewable energy sources such as wind, solar, and
hydropower, which introduces further volatility
and uncertainty due to their inherent dependence
on weather conditions and seasonality [2].
In
Denmark, a leader in the adaptation of renewable
energy, this shift has heightened the importance
of the intraday electricity markets, where prices
reflect real-time supply and demand dynamics.
Nord Pool facilities this, where accurate price
forecasting is vital for market participants e.g.,
producers, retailers and Balancing responsible
partners (BRPs), to optimize strategies, mitigate
financial risks and balance the transmission grid
[3, 4].
This research is driven by the increasing complex-
ity of electricity price forecasting (EPF) in the in-
traday market, which depends on day-ahead mar-
ket expectations [5, 6]. Traditional forecasting
methods often neglect the hierarchical structure of
electricity market data, limiting their optimization
potential. Hierarchical Time Series (HTS) rec-
onciliation offers a versatile solution by ensuring
consistency across structural, spatial, or tempo-
ral hierarchies, enhancing forecast accuracy. De-
spite its potential, HTS remains largely unexplored
in electricity markets, particularly for electricity
prices which exhibit non-Gaussian characteristics,
such as heavy tails and volatility clustering, mak-
ing the benefits of reconciliation uncertain, but
worth exploring [1, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]. This paper
addresses this gap by applying HTS reconciliation,
with a focus on temporal hierarchies and innova-
tive reconciliation methods, to aggregate intraday
differences, improving forecast accuracy and co-
herence over longer horizons in the DK1 (Jutland,
Funen, and surrounding areas) intraday market, de-
livering practical benefits for market participants
using machine learning algorithms for predictions.
1.1. Research Question
This paper addresses the following research ques-
tion:
Can the HTS framework be applied to
non-Gaussian data, such as electric-
ity prices, and how do embedded rec-
onciliation methods compare against
traditional post-reconciliation in this
domain?
The objective consist of three parts:
1. To evaluate the suitability of the HTS frame-
work for modeling electricity price data with
non-Gaussian statistical properties [1, 7].
2. To compare the performance of embed-
ded reconciliation within a Deep Learn-
ing framework, against traditional post-
reconciliation methods, a naïve model [4, 5],
and traditional time series models.
3. To provide actionable insights for Intraday
market participants in the DK1 intraday mar-
ket by assessing forecast accuracy across
multiple temporal hierarchies.
1.2. Related Work
EPF research has primarily targeted the day-ahead
market, driven by its higher trading volumes and
data availability, with less focus on the intraday
market [1, 5, 12]. Forecasting approaches include
statistical models [3, 12], econometric models
[13], and machine learning techniques [3, 5, 14].
These studies, among others, typically produce
point or probabilistic forecasts [1], but, to the best
of our knowledge, do not address the hierarchical
structure of electricity price data, such as temporal
or spatial aggregations. Although HTS methods
have been applied to electricity load forecasting
[8, 15, 16, 17], their application to EPF remains
unexplored. Recent advances in HTS, particularly
machine learning-based loss optimization [11, 18],
enable the integration of HTS with machine learn-
ing for price forecasting, which this paper investi-
gates.

2.1
Nordic Electricity Market Overview
2. ELECTRICITY MARKET
As stated in the introduction, electricity is an
essential good in modern society that cannot be
efficiently stored, requiring continuous real-time
balancing of supply and demand. To manage this,
each country designates a Transmission System
Operator (TSO), tasked with maintaining the grid
frequency stability, and preventing imbalances
that could lead to power outages.
Driven by rising electricity demands and climate
policy targets, power systems are undergoing
a major transformation away from fossil fuels,
toward renewable energy sources. In the Nordics,
wind, solar and hydropower are the dominant
green energy solutions [19]. While the energy
sources ensure sustainability, they also introduce
weather-dependent variability, leading to greater
volatility and uncertainty in supply [14, 20, 21].
These dynamics heighten the need for market
structures that absorb short-term fluctuations
efficiently.
To address this, electricity markets are structured
to facilitate continuous alignment between supply
and demand, while minimizing costs.
The
foundation of this system is the Day-Ahead
Market (SDAC - Single Day-Ahead Coupling), an
auction-based system in which market participants
submit bids for hourly electricity delivery the
following day. Market clearing is performed by
the EUPHEMIA algorithm, which determines
prices based on aggregated supply and demand
curves (the merit order curve) [22] as seen on
Figure 1.
While SDAC provides certainty in
planning, it lacks flexibility to accommodate real-
time fluctuations, underscoring the importance of
additional markets, closer to delivery.
To address the limitations of the Day-Ahead
Market, the Intraday Market (SIDC/XBID - Single
Intraday Coupling) [23] allows market participants
to trade electricity closer to delivery, ensuring
flexibility and stability. In Denmark, the intraday
market operates as a continuous trading platform,
where buy and sell orders can be submitted and

den, Norway, Finland, and the Baltic countries,
ensuring efficient electricity transactions across
borders. The market participants include electric-

The intraday market operates as a continuous
double-auction, enabling real-time matching of
orders up to one hour before delivery. Forecasting

To obtain a tractable representation, the continu-

2.3. Forecasting in Electricity Markets
Forecasting is a cornerstone of modern electricity
markets, enabling efficient coordination between
supply and demand. As the energy mix becomes
greener, forecast volatility increases due to
inherent unpredictability of weather conditions.
This can lead to persistent imbalances, that must
be resolved through the intraday or balancing
markets. Errors in forecasting, whether over or
underestimating production, can create significant
price swings, including negative prices during
periods of oversupply. BRPs, including traders,
act independently of suppliers, to anticipate and
respond to the fluctuations. By forecasting prices
and trading accordingly, BRPs help stabilize the
grid and limits the need for costly last minute
balancing [26].
Renewable
energy’s
dependence
on
nature
adds further complexity.
Unlike fossil-based
generation, renewable energies cannot be ramped
up and down on demand. Their output depends on
weather conditions, requiring forecasting models
to incorporate both weather data and physical
constraints.

In the interconnected markets, such as the Nordic
region, while cross-border effects supports the
green transition by stabilizing across Europe, it
also amplifies forecasting challenges further. For
instance, Denmark’s wind generation is closely
tied to Scandinavian hydropower generation.
Mild winters with reduced snow in Norway and
Sweden drive up demand for Danish wind and

hydropower reservoirs offer backup capacity for

forecasts [27]. This is also investigated in Section

While forecasting is indispensable, its accuracy
can be improved. This paper aims to improve the
forecast accuracy through the use of reconciliation
techniques by using hiearchical learning. Recon-
ciliation can help reduce imbalances and enhance
market efficiency over long periods. The follow-
ing section introduces reconciliation, explains its
underlying principles, and outlines how such tech-
niques can be integrated into forecasting models
to enhance intraday electricity price predictions.
3. HIERARCHICAL TIME SERIES
3.1. Overview of Hierarchical Time Series
HTS refer to time series data organized in a hi-
erarchical structure, where series at lower levels
aggregate to form higher-level series with varying
granularity, or higher-level series disaggregate into
finer details. Such structures are common in do-
mains like energy systems, where electricity loads
are aggregated across geographical or temporal
scales as explored by Nystrup et al. [8, 15] and
tourism, where visitor data can be organized by
region or time period as explored by Kourentzes
et al. [28]. Unlike traditional time series fore-
casting, which treats each series independently,
HTS explicitly models the aggregation relation-
ships between levels. This structure poses a chal-
lenge, as independent forecasts at different levels
may violate aggregation constraints, leading to
incoherence. However, it also offers an opportu-
nity to improve forecasting accuracy by leveraging
these relationships through forecast reconciliation
[8, 15].

3.2.2. Temporal Hierarchies
Temporal hierarchies apply the reconciliation
framework to a single time series aggregated
across frequencies (e.g., hourly, daily, monthly).
Unlike structural hierarchies, they exhibit an
inherent ordering and nested structure due
to time’s progression, with coarser levels de-

seasonality informing daily forecasts, to enhance
accuracy [8, 10, 28].
The summation matrix S captures temporal aggre-

to temporal dynamics. The same notation and
principles, including Equation 4, apply directly to
temporal hierarchies.

3.3. Reconciliation Methods
3.3.1. Traditional Reconciliation Methods
Traditional reconciliation methods,
such as
Bottom-Up (BU) and Top-Down (TD), ensure co-
herence in hierarchical forecasting through aggre-
gation or disaggregation. However, their simplicity
limits their ability to capture complex dynamics,
necessitating advanced techniques.
• In BU, forecasts start at the bottom level
and are aggregated upwards (e.g., individual
products to total sales). This can propagate
noise, distorting higher-level forecasts.
• In TD, forecasts start at the least granular
level and are disaggregated using historical
proportions (e.g., total sales to individual
products). This can lead to missing unique
lower-level patterns if proportions shift.
These shortcomings - BU’s noise propagation and
TD’s reliance on static proportions [10] - drive the
need for more sophisticated methods, explored in
Section 3.3.2. Furthermore, they are not particu-
larly useful for electricity price forecasting, where
the hierarchy of interest is neither the top or bot-
tom, as discussed further in Section 3.4.

4. DEEP LEARNING
Deep Learning has in most recent years become
a pillar in modern time series forecasting. It ex-
cels at capturing complex temporal patterns and
non-linear relationships that traditional methods
struggle to model, particularly concerning EPF [1].
This section outlines the theoretical framework of
the deep learning model developed in this paper
for EPF in the Nordic intraday market. By inte-
grating sequential inputs (e.g., historical VWAP)
and static inputs (e.g., the day-ahead forecast), the
model addresses the challenge of predicting ev-
ery ∆P in ˆy where the forecasts of interest are
the components of ˆy(1), while producing coherent
and accurate forecasts, by combining a traditional
MSE loss with a soft reconciliation constraint. Be-
low, the model architecture, mathematical founda-
tions, training methodologies, and alignment with
hierarchical forecasting requirements are detailed.
4.1. Model Architecture
The model is a hybrid architecture combining
Fully Connected Neural Network (FCNN) layers
with Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) layers,
motivated by the multi-modal input including:
Sequential features that evolve through time, and
static features fixed at the time of prediction. The
FCNN layers is designed to process static inputs
through dense layers to extract high-level features,
while the Recurrent Neural Network (RNN), i.e.,
the LSTM layers of the model, is designed to
capture temporal dependencies in sequential data.
This makes the model particularly well-suited
for EPF, where capturing intraday dynamics,
time-dependent
volatility,
and
diurnal
and
seasonal patterns are essential. These aspects are
further explored in Section 6.1. Outputs from both
branches are concatenated and passed through
additional fully connected output layers, yielding
a 55-dimensional vector of ∆P’s representing ˆy.
This hybrid design is theoretically grounded in the
complementary strengths of its components, and
the previous work done within the field of hier-
archical learning [11, 18]. According to the uni-
versal approximation theorem, it is possible to ap-
proximate any continuous function given sufficient

4.2. Model Components
Deep learning models are grounded in Neural Net-
works (NN), which are computational systems in-
spired by biological systems. NNs consist of in-
terconnected neurons organized into layers: Input
layers, hidden layers, and output layers. Each
neuron processes inputs to learn complex patterns.
The proposed model is organized into the follow-
ing high-level process components:
• Input Processing: Data is divided into
static and sequential inputs and is directed
to their respective branches of the model.
• FCNN Branch: Processes static input fea-

• LSTM Branch: Processes the sequential
input features using stacked LSTM layers
to capture the temporal dependencies of the
features.
• Fusion and Output: The output of the two
latent representations is concatenated and
mapped through dense layers to produce a
55-dimensional target vector ˆy.

4.5. Deep Learning for Hierarchical Forecast-
ing
Traditional HTS forecasting requires predictions
to be coherent across aggregation levels, as
defined by the summation matrix S in Section
3.2.1. Deep learning models are well-suited for
this task due to their flexibility in outputting high-
dimensional vectors and integrating structural
constraints within the objective function during
training, applied similarly to Leprince et al. [11],
and Mancuso et al. [18].
By designing the output layer to generate 55 simul-
taneous forecasts ˆy across a temporal hierarchy
and incorporating a reconciliation penalty in the
loss function, the model jointly optimizes point-
wise accuracy and hierarchical coherence during

4.7. Bayesian Optimization
Bayesian
Optimization
is
an
optimization
strategy designed for problems where the ob-
jective function is computationally expensive
to evaluate and often lacks an analytical form.
Instead of exhaustively searching the entire
hyperparameter space, Bayesian Optimization
builds a probabilistic model, which is known
as a surrogate model, that approximates the
relationship between hyperparameters and the loss
performance. Based on this model, an acquisition
function is used to balance the trade-off of
exploration versus exploitation, by selecting
the most informative hyperparameter settings
to evaluate next. Thus leveraging prior evalua-
tions to search the parameter space efficiently [40].
The goal is to find the optimal set of hyperparame-
ters θ ∈Θ minimizing an unknown loss function
f(θ). Let f : Θ →R denote the black-box objec-
tive function to be minimized, where Θ ⊆Rd is
the hyperparameter space and θ ∈Θ is a candidate
configuration. The function f(θ) is assumed to
be expensive to evaluate. Bayesian Optimization
approaches this problem by placing a probabilistic
model over the objective function, and at iteration
t, a surrogate model ˆf(θ) is constructed from the
observed data:

As mentioned the acquisition function is designed

The most common acquisition functions such as
Expected Improvement is defined as [40]:
at(θ) = E[max(f ∗−f(θ), 0)]
where f ∗is the best observed value so far, and

at(θ) = −µt(θ) + κ · σt(θ)
where µt(θ) and σt(θ) are the predictive mean and
standard deviation from the surrogate model, and

In traditional Bayesian Optimization, Gaussian
processes are commonly used as surrogate models
due to their flexibility and ability to quantify
uncertainty [40]. However, due to the high feature
dimensionality in this project’s parameter space
and due to the use of categorical hyperparameters,
more appropriate methods have been developed,
such as the Tree-structured Parzen Estimator
(TPE) [41].
Instead of modeling f(θ) directly, TPE models
the inverse conditional distribution p(θ | f(θ)),
which is better suited for handling this type of

6.1.1. Target Variable Analysis
This section analyzes VWAP and its derived
difference ∆P to understand their dynamics and
establish a foundation for forecasting and reconcil-
ing electricity prices. Figure 5 presents a snippet
of the time series of VWAP and ∆P. The VWAP
series displays no clear monotonic trend, but
reveals signs of seasonality and occasional peaks,
likely driven by market volatility or external
shocks e.g., supply disruptions or demand surges.
Its lack of stabilization suggests non-stationarity,
a hypothesis later confirmed by statistical tests.
In contrast, ∆P fluctuate around a mean of zero
without a persistent trend, indicating a potentially
stationary process [43]. However, periods of high
volatility persist, suggesting volatility clustering
and fat tails, characteristics typical of financial
markets [44, 45].
Although the transforma-
tion to ∆P stabilizes the series, supporting its
suitability as the target variable, the primary
motivation for this transformation was to ensure
aggregation and interpretability. Other transforma-
tions were considered, as outlined in Section 3.2.3.
To confirm stationarity Augmented Dickey-Fuller
(ADF) and Kwiatkowski-Phillips-Schmidt-Shin
(KPSS) tests were conducted on both VWAP
and ∆P.
For VWAP, the ADF test yields a
p-value < 1·10−4, rejecting the null hypothesis of
a unit root, which suggests stationarity. The KPSS
test yields a p-value = 1 · 10−2, rejecting the null
hypothesis of stationarity. Although the ADF test
suggests stationarity, the KPSS test and visual
evidence of seasonality and occasional peaks
indicate non-stationarity. It is therefore concluded
that VWAP is non-stationary, consistent with
the need for differencing as seen with ∆P11. For
∆P, the ADF test yields a p-value < 1 · 10−4,
which suggests stationarity.
The KPSS test
yields a p-value = 1 · 10−1, failing to reject the
null hypothesis of stationarity, confirming that
the transformation produces a stationary target
variable. The results are shown in Table 3.

forecasts,

6.2. Feature Selection
Feature selection is performed using MIC, applied
exclusively to the first index hierarchy target
variables ˆy(1). For clarification, refer to Section
3.4, Equation 23. To establish a unified feature
ranking, MIC values are computed between
each feature and each component of ˆy(1), then
averaged across these components to produce a
single metric - the average MIC per feature. This
method provides a comprehensive assessment of
feature importance across the components in ˆy(1).
The explanatory variable set consists of 193 fea-
tures, including:
• 55 versions of the day-ahead forecast (equiv-
alent to the total components in ˆy.
• 8 AR components such as VWAP, ∆P, and
High Price.
• 2 transaction volume variables. These are
transaction volume and ∆transaction vol-
ume.
• 3 ∆weather variables e.g., ∆temperature.
• 2 ∆production variables e.g., ∆solar pro-
duction.
• 6 energy exchange variables e.g. GB and
SE.
• 99 technical indicators e.g., rolling standard
deviations and RSI.
• 18 seasonality variables e.g., time of day and
weekend boolean.
Longer horizon lagged versions of the features
are not evaluated to avoid emphasizing temporal
relationships at this stage. This decision prevents
an explosion in the number of variables evalu-
ated, with appropriate sequence lengths to be
determined during hyperparameter tuning. MIC
computations are conducted solely on the training
set to prevent data leakage, adhering to standard
modeling practices.

This section covers how some of the selected

further improve model performance and align

for the model, and they are intrinsically modeled
as the hierarchy in ˆy. As noted in section 6.2.2,
all 55 variants of the day-ahead forecast were
retained for their critical role in providing a static
baseline across all hierarchies, reflecting market
expectations without further adjustments.
To further account for the hierarchy, the retained
AR components are adapted to the remaining
components in ˆy(1). This means that additional
features will be created to match their respective
target horizon in ˆy(1), increasing the total number
of AR components to 50.

Price, VWAP and VWAP3H are aggregated

on the ∆P variable, the longer horizons are com-
puted as follows: ∆P(2H) = VWAPt−VWAPt−2.
The adaptations are meant to enhance the model’s
ability to handle the price dynamics unique to each
component in ˆy(1). The remaining features stay
at 1H granularity, due to their static descriptive
nature. The hierarchical feature engineering ex-

features (74 + 5 · 9).

7. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
The following section describes the experimental
setup in detail to forecast ∆P in ˆy, providing a
structured overview of the process, from data col-
lection to model evaluation, ensuring transparency
and replicability. Section 7.1 presents the mod-
eling pipeline and order of actions, acting as a
blueprint for reproducibility, for the results in Sec-
tion 8. This is a high-level overview of the dif-
ferent models, operations and configurations. For
technical details, please refer to Section 3, 4, 5,
and 6. Section 7.2 presents the most important
hyperparameter tuning findings.
7.1. Modeling Pipeline
The modeling pipeline encapsulates an end-to-end
process of transforming raw data into actionable
forecasts, as illustrated in Figure 13.
This
subsection provides a clear, sequential guide to
the operations performed, ensuring that each step
from data collection to model evaluation is well
defined and logically ordered.
As stated in Section 5, this paper utilizes data
sources from 3 different sources: Open-Meteo,
Energinet, and Nord Pool. The raw data is aligned
and joined, to create a full, coherent dataset. The
dataset undergoes feature engineering, including
target variable transformation, creating hierarchies
for ∆P and the corresponding day-ahead forecasts
as stated in Section 5.1 and 5.2. Furthermore,
technical indicators, temporal indicators, and
residual weather and production variables are
created as covered in Section 6. Finally, missing
values are imputed using linear interpolation.
The dataset is split into training and test sets. A
min-max scaler is fitted on the training set and
subsequently applied to the test set. The MIC
feature selection is applied to the training data. As
stated in Section 5, scaling and feature selection
is done on the train set to avoid data leakage.
Finally, additional variables are created for the
remaining hierarchies in ˆy(1) to prepare the data
for modeling.
A naïve and ARMAX(1,1) model is fitted as de-
scribed in Section 5.5, as well as an ML baseline,
using 190 trials to tune within WandB without rec-
onciliation. The ARMAX(1,1) model and best ML
baseline model undergo post-reconciliation for re-
sults evaluation. The proposed NN with embedded
reconciliation undergoes tuning in WandB, using
6270 trials (190 trials · 11 methods · 3 α-values).
Both the ML baseline and proposed NN computa-
tion are done using the DTU HPC, while the naïve
and ARMAX(1,1) models are run locally. Fig-
ure 14 presents the architecture for all NN models
in this paper, including the ML baseline. The
models take two types of variables, sequential and
static, to indicate the direction of the data flow
after model initialization, designating static to the
FCNN branch, and sequential to the LSTM branch.
The experimental setup uses Optuna with a WandB
wrapper to optimally search and tune the hyper-
parameter space as described in Section 5.4.2. Fi-
nally, all the models are compared using RMSE
and RMSE% improvement in ˆy(1) as described in
Section 5.3.
7.2. Hyperparameter Tuning Results
The full hyperparameter search space and tuning
setup was described in Section 5.4.2.
The
hyperparameters can be broadly categorized into
architecture-related
and
optimization-related
hyperparameters. The architectural hyperparam-
eters control the model’s structural complexity,
including the hidden dimension size, number
of LSTM layers, number of FCNN layers for
both static input and output layers, as well as the

9. DISCUSSION
The discussion interprets the key findings from
applying the hierarchical learning framework to
EPF, explores the implications, and reflects on
the limitations of the approach in this paper. The
section concludes with a paragraph on future re-
search directions. The focus is on analyzing the
results, specifically the suitability of HTS for non-
Gaussian data and the performance of embed-
ded reconciliation compared to post-reconciliation,
without summarizing the broader paper or drawing
final conclusions.
9.1. Interpretation
Electricity prices deviate from the Gaussian
assumption underlying hierarchical forecasting.
A key insight from this analysis is that trans-
forming prices into price differences enables the
application of HTS, even though the transformed
data remains non-Gaussian. The price difference
transformation
ensures
aggregateability
of
electricity prices across temporal hierarchies,
which is a prerequisite for reconciliation, since
price differences aggregate consistently with the
summation matrix S. The transformed feature
∆P also makes electricity prices stationary, which
allows modeling of the underlying system and
not general trends.
Furthermore, ∆P remains
interpretable, where positive values indicate price
increases, and negative values indicate decreases,
where the magnitude of ∆P reflects how fast the
electricity prices change. This approach suggests
that the HTS framework can extend beyond
Gaussian data, given that the target variable is
aggregatable, and that the HTS framework can be
embedded directly into the NN’s loss function,
allowing it to forecast accuracy and perform
reconciliation
simultaneously.
Alternative
transformations were considered, although simple
differencing deemed superior due to it being
stationary and interpretable.
The analysis of the results reveals a significant
advantage of embedded reconciliation over tradi-
tional post-reconciliation methods with regards to
∆P. The embedded reconciliation, using Autoco-
variance, achieves a 6.5% improvement in RMSE,
at α = 0.75 for ˆy(1), compared to negligible
or negative gains from post-reconciliation. The
deviation comes from the systematic difference
in estimation of the covariance matrices, where
embedded
reconciliation
utilizes
a
parallel
framework to propagate both accuracy and recon-
ciliation errors through the system simultaneously,
rather than the sequential nature of classic HTS.
Sequential HTS fails to estimate well-conditioned
covariance matrices from the residuals, resulting
in numerical instability, as shown in the Section
8. Embedded reconciliation gets around this by
enforcing soft structural constraints during the
learning, allowing the NN to capture patterns in
the non-Gaussian data effectively. This highlights
a fundamental difference between the embedded
reconciliation and post-reconciliation methods.
Embedded reconciliation shapes the forecasts,
where post-reconciliation corrects the forecasts
with little room for change, due to the statistical
nature of the target variable and some exogenous
variables such as the day-ahead forecast.
The effectiveness of embedded reconciliation
methods varies depending on the reconciliation
technique and the weighting parameter α. For
α = 0.75, accuracy is prioritized over coherence,
where Autocovariance, Cross-covariance, and
Series GLASSO perform the best, suggesting
that modeling cross-series relationships enhances
forecast quality. At α = 0.5, where accuracy
is balanced equally with coherence, simpler
variance-based methods, such as Series Markov
and Hierarchy Variance excel, indicating that
simplicity and equal weighting does not neces-
sarily sacrifice predictive accuracy. Interestingly,
at α
=
0.25, which favors coherence over
accuracy, RMSE improves slightly less than
α
∈
{0.50, 0.75}, although Autocovariance,
Cross-covariance, and Series GLASSO are the
still best, although with lower RMSE than at
α = 0.75, while Structural Markov even degrades
performance. This implies an emerging pattern
where there is a trade-off between reconciliation
and accuracy, which distorts the model’s ability
to fit the data, emphasizing the need to calibrate
α based on the forecasting context. Furthermore,
embedded reconciliation always perform better

9.2. Implications
The improved accuracy from applying the HTS
framework carries practical use cases for intraday
market participants in energy trading. Improved
forecasts across ˆy(1) can refine trading decisions,
improve risk management, and assist in better
grid balancing. The use of the HTS framework
furthermore gives users an edge over traditional
statistical, econometric, or NN models due
to the clear RMSE improvement that occurs.
While a 6.5% improvement may seem modest,
it translates into significant financial gains in
high-stakes environments for producers, retailers,
and BRPs.
However, it is worth considering
the computational burden of the model, which
raises questions about scalability and robustness,
particularly in scenarios where rapid decision
making and interpretability are critical. Market
participants have to weigh the trade-offs between
a computationally intensive model and a simple
and fast model, especially when the performance
difference is marginal.
This work also broadens the scope of HTS appli-
cations by validating that the HTS framework can
be applied to non-Gaussian data and non-naturally
aggregatable data such as electricity prices. Fur-
thermore, it underscores the value of embedding
reconciliation, offering a blueprint for reconciling
complex time series. The sensitivity of perfor-
mance, with regards to α, highlights the relation-
ship between accuracy and coherence, suggesting
that future similar implementations of the hierar-
chical learning framework must carefully balance
these objectives. These insights can assist in fore-
casting in other non-Gaussian domains, although
their generalization depends on adapting the ap-
proach to the specific data and time series charac-
teristics.
9.3. Limitations
The two-year span of the dataset limits the
model’s ability to learn from extreme events
and periods of larger volatilities, such as the
2022 energy crisis, highlighting the inability to
predict long-tail events. This limitation further
highlights a trade-off between data recency and
historical depth, with the current scope favoring
recent market dynamics over long-term volatility
patterns.
Furthermore, some variables such as ∆production
and ∆weather exhibit low predictive power
despite their theoretical relevance to energy prices.
This discrepancy may stem from data quality
issues, suggesting that the model’s feature set is
not optimized. Furthermore, the reliance on MIC
for feature selection further raises the question
of whether latent feature relationships are being
overlooked.
Additionally, the hybrid NN’s complexity, driven
by many layers and outputting a high-dimensional
space, also imposes practical challenges. While
accuracy justifies the use of such complexity,
real-time deployment remains unverified without
exhaustive optimization, limiting its operational
feasibility for both training and inference.

9.4. Future Research
The findings, implications, and limitations point
to several areas which could be further researched.

expose the model to more diverse market condi-

also potentially allowing the model to better gen-

the theoretical model more closely with real-world
demands and applications. Finally, extending the

naturally defined by the bidding zones, could fur-

10. CONCLUSION
This paper addresses the research question: Can
the HTS framework be applied to non-Gaussian
data, such as electricity prices, and how do
embedded
reconciliation
methods
compare
against traditional post-reconciliation in this
domain?
The findings directly respond to
this question and provides clear insights based
on the analysis of EPF in the DK1 intraday market.
The HTS framework is suitable for modeling
electricity price data, despite its non-Gaussian
properties such as heavy tails and volatility cluster-
ing. By transforming prices into price differences
(∆P), the data becomes aggregatable and aligns
with the summation matrix S, a core requirement
for hierarchical forecasting.
Additionally, this
transformation ensures stationarity, as confirmed
by statistical tests (ADF and KPSS). Although
∆P retains non-Gaussian characteristics (kurtosis
= 89.99, skewness = 1.38), these do not hinder
the application of HTS. This demonstrates that
the HTS framework can effectively handle non-
Gaussian data when appropriately transformed,
extending its applicability beyond traditional
Gaussian assumptions.
Embedded reconciliation within a hierarchical
learning framework significantly outperforms
traditional post-reconciliation, naïve, and sta-
tistical models within the domain of EPF. The
best-performing embedded method, Autocovari-
ance with α = 0.75, achieves a 6.5% reduction
in RMSE for the first index hierarchy (ˆy(1)),
improving from a baseline RMSE of 31.44 to
29.39. Other embedded methods at α = 0.75,
such as Cross-covariance (5.7% improvement)
and Series GLASSO (4.5% improvement), also
surpass the baseline. For α = 0.25, Autocovari-
ance, Cross-covariance, and Series GLASSO
also performs the best, yielding improvements of
5.2%, 4.2%, and 3.7%, respectively. Interestingly,
Structural Markov shows a negative improvement
of -1.4%. At α = 0.50, Series Markov, Hierarchy
Variance, and Series GLASSO perform the
best with improvements of 6.1%, 4.6%, and
4.3%, respectively.
On average, embedded

reconciliation shows consistent gains across
α ∈{0.25, 0.50, 0.75}, with mean maximum

-2.1%, indicating that slight emphasis on accuracy

In contrast, post-reconciliation on the baseline ML

-0.43% for Cross-covariance), due to numerical
instability from ill-conditioned covariance matrix

The improved forecast accuracy across temporal
hierarchies offers actionable insights for DK1 mar-
ket participants. The 6.5% RMSE improvement
translates into more reliable predictions of ∆P and
implicitly electricity prices across ˆy(1), enhancing
trading decisions, risk management, and grid
balancing. For instance, the forecasted VWAP
distribution for 1H (mean = 78.70, standard devi-

events. Despite this, the improvements provide
producers, retailers, and BRPs a competitive edge,
potentially yielding significant financial benefits
in a high-stakes market, though computational
complexity may require optimization for real-time
use.
In summary, the HTS framework is applicable
to non-Gaussian electricity prices through the
∆P transformation, ensuring aggregatability and
stationarity.
Embedded reconciliation outper-
forms the ML baseline model by up to 6.5%,
post-reconciliation by 6.34% to 6.93%, the naïve
model by 7.9%, and the ARMAX(1,1) model by

α = 0.75 and α = 0.25, with α = 0.75 showing
the largest gains. For α = 0.50, Series Markov,
Hierarchy Variance, and Series GLASSO per-
forms the best. Embedded reconciliation surpasses
both post-reconciliation and no-reconciliation ap-
proaches, with a slight emphasis on accuracy over
coherence yielding the most optimal results. These
advancements deliver practical benefits for market
participants, enhancing forecast reliability across
multiple horizons, though challenges remain in
predicting extreme events. These results affirm the
HTS framework’s versatility and the efficacy of
embedded reconciliation in this context.
