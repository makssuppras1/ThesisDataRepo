Abstract
Over the past few decades, aquaculture production has seen significant growth worldwide, in-
cluding in the Mediterranean region. However, Mediterranean aquaculture faces unique chal-
lenges, such as climate change-induced emerging diseases and increased bacterial pathogenic-
ity. Additionally, the intensification of fish production has led to the overuse of antibiotics
to control disease outbreaks, contributing to the development and spread of AMR. In this
context, probiotics have emerged as a sustainable alternative to the prophylactic adminis-
tration of antibiotics in aquaculture. This study investigated 96 marine bacterial isolates
from a Mediterranean mesocosm experiment to explore their potential applications as pro-
biotics in aquaculture, as well as in human health through identification of novel natural
product-encoding gene clusters.
Fifty-seven isolates were identified through 16S rRNA gene-based sequencing, with key
genera including Vibrio, Alteromonas, and Pseudoalteromonas, along with members of the
Rhodobacteraceae family. Thirteen isolates exhibited antagonistic activity against the fish
pathogen Vibrio anguillarum 90-11-286, with two isolates, C1_T11_0 (a yellow-pigmented
Pseudoalteromonas flavipulchra) and C2_T13_0 (a Pseudovibrio ascidiaceicola), demon-
strating strong, broad-spectrum inhibition against several fish pathogens and human pathogenic
Staphylococcus aureus NCTC 8325. Furthermore, antiSMASH analysis identified a variety of
biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) in the genomes of the probiotic candidates, including those
linked to known secondary metabolites and potential novel compounds. Notably, C1_T11_0
exhibited a BGC with 100% similarity to bromoalterochromide A, a known antimicrobial
metabolite, and encoded the highest number of predicted novel BGCs. Meanwhile, isolate
B1_T13_0 (a Tritonibacter mobilis) demonstrated limited antimicrobial activity despite the
detection of antimicrobial tropodithietic acid (TDA)-encoding genes. The reduced activity
is suggested to result from suboptimal growth conditions affecting TDA production. No re-
sistance genes were identified in any of the probiotic candidates, but antibiotic susceptibility
testing is recommended before probiotic application.
Overall, this study highlights the potential of specific probiotic candidates for sustain-
able disease prevention in Mediterranean aquaculture and the discovery of novel bioactive
compounds with applications in human health. Future research should aim to elucidate the
mechanisms behind their antimicrobial activity and further exploration of their BGCs.

Introduction
The global human population is growing rapidly, and this trend is expected to continue over
the next 6 decades. Projections indicate that the world’s population will peak at about
10.3 billion people by the mid-2080s, up from the current estimate of 8.2 billion in 2024
[1]. Alarmingly, more than 40 percent of the global population lacks access to an affordable,
healthy diet. In this context, aquatic food systems, including fisheries and aquaculture, are
increasingly recognized as vital, as aquatic foods are among the most nutritious [2]. These
foods are rich sources of protein and omega-3 fatty acids, as well as essential micronutrients
including vitamin B12, vitamin D, selenium, iron, iodine, zinc and phosphorous [3].
As the demand for nutritious and sustainable food sources rises, production from aqua-
culture has experienced significant growth.
In 2022, production from both fisheries and
aquaculture reached a new high, accounting for around 15 percent of the total animal pro-
tein supply. While capture fisheries have seen little change over the past decades, aquaculture
has expanded by 6.6 percent since 2020, making it the fastest-growing sector in food produc-
tion, surpassing other animal, dairy, and crop industries [2]. This growth is reflected in the
global consumption of aquatic animal foods, which has been increasing at an average annual
rate of 3.0 percent, compared to the estimated 2.7 percent growth in the consumption of
terrestrial meats [2]. This trend has favorable climate implications, as aquatic foods are gen-
erally more nutrient-dense than terrestrial animal protein sources and generate significantly
lower greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions [3].
Given the growing global population and escalating environmental challenges, aquatic
food systems, such as aquaculture, not only offer solutions for enhancing food security and
improving diet quality, but also hold the potential to support economic development and
environmental sustainability [2]. Aquaculture aligns with several United Nations (UN) Sus-
tainable Development Goals (SDGs) (Figure 1), including Goals 1, 2, 3, 12, and 14, which
aim to promote social, environmental and economic sustainability by 2030 [2][4]. However,
significant transformation is needed for aquaculture to fully realize its potential in advancing
sustainable development [2].

Generally, it is challenging to maintain optimal water quality in aquaculture rearing units
since fish are reared at high densities and on a large scale to increase productivity. Poor
hygiene within these enclosures often leads to self-pollution, which not only compromises
the water quality within the system, but also negatively impacts the surrounding ecosystems
through the release of harmful effluents [6][7]. Additionally, the high density of fish dur-
ing rearing makes them highly susceptible to opportunistic pathogenic bacteria, which can
rapidly proliferate and spread throughout the larval population [8]. Although vaccination
plays an important role in preventing bacterial infections in commercial fish farming, the un-
derdeveloped immune systems of fish larvae make vaccination ineffective in the early stages
of development [9]. As a result, antibiotics have commonly been used both prophylactically
to prevent bacterial infections in fish larvae but also as a treatment. However, the release of
antimicrobial residues into the environment has led to the emergence of antibiotic-resistant
pathogenic bacteria, rendering this approach unsustainable [6]. To ensure the future sus-
tainability of aquaculture, investment in new prevention and treatment strategies is crucial
[10].
In addition to vaccines, several sustainable alternatives to antibiotics in aquaculture in-
clude the application of prebiotics, bacteriophages, medicinal plants, and, as investigated in
this study, probiotics [2]. The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) defines probiotics
as “live microorganisms which, when administered in adequate amounts, confer a health

benefit to the host” [11]. While various bacterial species have shown promise as probionts in
aquaculture, members of the Roseobacter group are particularly noteworthy [7][11]. These

Considering the intensification of aquaculture and the promising potential of probiotics

probiotic effects [11][13]. This study aims to characterize a collection of bacterial isolates
from the Mediterranean Sea and assess their potential as novel probiotic strains for Mediter-
ranean aquaculture, which faces unique challenges from bacterial diseases due to climate
change [10].
1.1
The aquaculture industry
According to the FAO (1990), aquaculture is defined as "the farming of aquatic organisms,
including fish, molluscs, crustaceans, and aquatic plants” [14]. As previously mentioned,
global aquatic animal production reached a record high in 2022, with the farm-gate value
estimated at USD 296 billion. The importance of fisheries and aquaculture is also evident
in global employment figures with approximately 62 million people working in this sector
in 2022. Of these, 36% were employed in aquaculture, highlighting the significant role this
industry plays in supporting livelihoods in coastal communities [2].
Over the period from 2022 to 2032, aquaculture production is projected to increase at an
average annual growth rate of 1.6%. This global growth trend is mirrored in the Mediter-
ranean, where aquaculture is an active and growing sector [2].
However, Mediterranean
aquaculture faces unique challenges compared to other regions, including its heightened vul-
nerability to climate change, which has been linked to emerging bacterial diseases caused by
new pathogens and an increase in pathogenicity of bacterial pathogens due to rising tem-
peratures [10]. These challenges highlight the need for innovative prevention and treatment
strategies in the region.

1.1.1
Mediterranean aquaculture
The Mediterranean Sea (Figure 2) is thought to house one of the oldest coastal aquaculture
systems in the world – the Egyptian pond systems, which have been in use for millennia.
In the Mediterranean area of France, shellfish farming began around 600 years ago. Later,
in the 1980s, modern aquaculture emerged in the region, primarily focused on the cultiva-
tion of finfish and shellfish [15]. Since then, aquaculture has become a flourishing industry
throughout the Mediterranean basin, playing an important role in food security, boosting
economic development, and creating jobs [15][16].
Figure 2: Figure from [17], showing a map of the Mediterranean Sea and the countries that border it.
1.1.1.1
Current status and importance
Over the past few decades, Mediterranean aquaculture has experienced rapid growth and
significant diversification [10]. In 2019, the Mediterranean region produced 2 million tons
of marine fish with 43% coming from aquaculture. This represents a 15% increase in total
production since 2010, driven entirely by aquaculture, as capture fisheries have declined over
this period [18]. This growth is largely attributed to a shift in Mediterranean marine finfish
production over time, moving from small, land-based sites to large-scale enterprises along
the coastline and offshore [16][15].

Nearly all Southern European and North African countries in the Mediterranean region
are involved in aquaculture, with the leading producers being France, Greece, Italy, Spain,
and Turkey, although aquaculture legislation differs across countries [15]. As key economic
indicators of the sector, it is estimated that Mediterranean aquaculture employs over 30,000
people, generates a gross value added (GVA) of approximately USD 1 billion, and yields a
gross profit of USD 300 million [18].
Relative to shellfish farming, the finfish farming sector has expanded rapidly, now ac-
counting for half of Mediterranean aquaculture production by volume, and more than two-
thirds by value. Two species dominate this sector: gilthead seabream (Sparus aurata) and
European seabass (Dicentrarchus labrax), both carnivorous species farmed in marine cages,
together making up 95% of total finfish production in the Mediterranean [10]. In 2019, pro-
duction of these species reached 464,000 tons, valued at USD 2.24 billion [18]. Greece is the
leading European producer of these two species and is the third-largest aquaculture producer
in the Mediterranean, accounting for 14% of total production by weight and 16% by value
in 2019 [10][18].
While the Mediterranean Sea holds significant economic value, it’s equally important to
recognize the many intangible benefits it offers. Beyond providing security for its people, the
sea is rich in spiritual and cultural significance, offers recreational opportunities, and harbors
valuable biodiversity in its coastal and marine ecosystems [19]. Unfortunately, the expan-
sion of aquaculture in the region has introduced many environmental and socio-economic
challenges that threaten the sector’s sustainability [16]. This sustainability is further com-
promised by the impacts of climate change [20].
1.1.1.2
Effects of climate change
A combination of natural climate variability and anthropogenic climate change has con-
tributed to the recent warming of the Mediterranean Sea [10][21]. As a semi-enclosed basin,
the Mediterranean is particularly vulnerable to global warming, with a warming rate of
0.61°C per decade – much higher than the global oceanic average of 0.11°C per decade
[10]. Climate change has also led to several other impacts, including acidification of the sea,
increased salinity, decreased precipitation, more frequent and intense heatwaves, deoxygena-
tion, and changes in stratification [20][21]. While all these shifts pose significant threats to
Mediterranean marine ecosystems, it is expected that the greatest impacts will be due to
rising temperatures [15][10]. Warmer waters can trigger harmful algal blooms, which deplete
oxygen, kill the fish, and it can also facilitate the spread of biotoxins [21]. Furthermore,
elevated temperatures are closely associated with increased disease outbreaks, which rep-

resent a major bottleneck for the global aquaculture industry, leading to severe economic

At its core, marine cage aquaculture is an intensive fish production system characterized
by high stocking densities, often leading to stressful conditions for the fish [10].
As the
demand for economically sustainable production grows, intensification of farming practices
is expected to increase [21].
However, these high-density conditions make farmed fish –
typically in monocultures - more susceptible to infectious diseases compared to their wild
counterparts [10][21]. The situation is exacerbated by rising sea temperatures which many
pathogens may be able to tolerate, but farmed fish, such as the seabream, typically cannot,
as they possess a lower thermal tolerance [10].
To prevent and manage disease outbreaks and minimize losses in aquaculture, antibiotics
have been widely used [10][11]. However, elevated temperatures have been shown to pro-
mote biofilm formation, which increases bacterial tolerance to antibiotics, and to enhance
mutagenesis, facilitating bacterial adaptation through antibiotic resistance mutations [15].
Additionally, pathogens that are likely to survive outside the host may escape the farming
facilities, disperse into the surrounding marine environment, and potentially establish se-
vere infections in wild fish populations [21]. Increased temperatures can also favor pathogen
migration, posing an additional threat to both native and farmed fish populations [10].
1.1.1.3
Major fish pathogens
Pathogens pose a significant threat to the Mediterranean aquaculture industry, a challenge
that is further exacerbated by the ongoing impacts of climate change [10].
While some
pathogens have been known in the region for decades, others have emerged more recently
due to global trade or transmission though invasive species [10]. Major pathogens that con-
tribute to higher mortality rates and frequent severe epidemics in Mediterranean fish farms
include, among others, Aeromonas hydrophila, Photobacterium damselae subsp. damselae,
Tenacibaculum maritimum, Vibrio harveyi, and Vibrio anguillarum [10]. These pathogens
vary in their host-specificity: some are host-specific, while others infect a broader range of
species. For example, A. hydrophila has been primarily observed to infect European seabass,
whereas the other pathogens infect both the European seabass and the gilthead seabream,
the two dominant fish species in this area [10].
Some of the mentioned fish pathogens, such as T. maritimum, V. anguillarum, and
V. harveyi, have been identified as particularly relevant in aquaculture by the EU H2020
project MARBLES, which aims to develop sustainable solutions for bacterial infection con-
trol in aquaculture. Other pathogens in the project include Aeromonas salmonicida, Vibrio

To better understand the impact of these pathogens on aquaculture, it is essential to
examine how they infect their hosts, the diseases they cause, and whether they pose a risk to
human health. Most bacterial pathogens are opportunistic, meaning they cause disease when
the conditions allow, such as when the host’s defense system is compromised, or environmen-
tal conditions change [10][29]. Depending on the conditions, the pathogens can transition
from a non-pathogenic to a pathogenic state through adaptive mechanisms regulated by gene
expression. Climate change influences the activation of virulence genes and some pathogenic
traits, many of which are temperature-dependent [10][30]. Specifically, higher temperatures
have been found to enhance bacterial virulence and pathogenicity by promoting processes
such as flagellar synthesis, motility, quorum sensing signal production, adhesion, and biofilm
formation [10].
Pathogens can infect a host in two ways: as a pure agent or as part of a co-infection,
where the progression and severity of the disease may vary depending on the synergistic or
antagonistic interactions between the pathogens [30][31]. Whether acting alone or in com-
bination, pathogens typically enter the fish through its tissues and organs, such as the skin,
gills, digestive tract, or excretory system [30]. Once inside, the infection progresses through
three stages: a latent (or incubation) period, a symptomatic period, and an acute period
[30][32]. During the latent period, the pathogen is present in the host, but symptoms have
not yet developed. The symptomatic period follows, marked by the appearance of clinical
signs of illness, indicating the onset of the disease. Finally, the acute period represents the
most severe phase of the infection, characterized by pronounced symptoms due to active

pathogen replication. If left unmanaged, this phase can lead to serious health complications
in the fish or even death [31][32].
While many of these pathogens primarily affect fish, some have zoonotic potential, rais-
ing concerns about the risk they pose to human health [10]. Zoonotic diseases are infections
that can be transmitted from animals to humans [33]. Although human transmission is rare,
fish meat has been identified as one of the most common sources of foodborne disease out-
breaks [10][33]. The primary routes of infection include consuming raw or undercooked fish,
exposure to infected aquatic environments, or contact with the infectious agent via open
wounds or skin abrasions [33]. Aeromonas species can, for example, infect humans through
open wounds or ingestion, as they are capable of growing at temperatures up to 37°C, which
facilitates their transmission [10][33]. Infected individuals can experience symptoms such as
swelling at the infection site, respiratory issues, gastroenteritis, sepsis, and diarrhea, among
others [33][24].
Infections caused by fish pathogens can lead to a variety of diseases in fish. For example,
A. hydrophila causes aeromoniasis, while V. anguillarum is responsible for vibriosis. Both
diseases are characterized by symptoms such as lethargy, loss of appetite, skin and fin ul-
cerations, and body discoloration, and can lead to significant economic losses in aquaculture
[31][33][34]. As a result, antibiotics have been a usual treatment in cultured fish systems,
which has contributed to the development of antibiotic resistance in these bacteria. Notably,
many Aeromonas species are known to be multi-antibiotic resistant [34][24].
This trend
highlights a growing public health concern, posing serious risks to both human and aquatic
animal health [33].
1.1.2
Antibiotics in aquaculture and the rise of AMR
The widespread prophylactic use of antibiotics in aquaculture is driven by factors such as
overcrowding and stress within fish cages which impair the immune system of the fish [35].
As highlighted, antibiotics have been extensively used (and sometimes misused) in aquacul-
ture to control disease outbreaks and reduce losses [10][11]. However, this practice raises
significant concerns, including the accumulation of antibiotic residues in fish products and
their release into the marine environment [15][10]. The undetected consumption of antibi-
otics by fish consumers has been linked to an increased susceptibility to bacterial infections,
as well as allergic reactions and toxicity issues [35]. Additionally, the environmental con-
tamination from antibiotics promotes the development of antimicrobial resistance (AMR)
in marine bacteria. This AMR not only disrupts marine bacterial biodiversity, but, more
alarmingly, the resistance can also be transferred to human pathogens [15][10][24][6]. AMR

In the European Union (EU), AMR-related infections are estimated to cause over 35,000

diseases [2]. Bacterial pathogens develop resistance to new drugs due to selective pressures
from previous antibiotic use, rendering some infections untreatable [39].

(MRSA), which has been identified as a major risk pathogen associated with AMR, as it can

their genetic material with other bacteria. As a result, the rate at which new resistant strains

advancing the development and discovery of new antimicrobials [38].
Antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs) are transferred between microorganisms through hor-
izontal gene transfer (HGT), although they can also be acquired from the environment
[36][15]. An example of HGT occurred in the Ecuadorian shrimp industry, where heavy
antibiotic use facilitated the spread of resistance. This led to the exchange of genes between
Vibrio cholerae and antibiotic-resistant bacteria from the shrimp farms, contributing to the
cholera epidemic that emerged in Latin America in 1992 [35]. There is also growing evi-
dence that fish pathogens, such as Aeromonas, can transfer ARGs to human pathogens like
Escherichia coli [35]. Studies have shown that plasmids confer greater mobility and higher
concentrations of ARGs compared to those located on chromosomes [15]. For instance, re-
sistance to quinolones, a widely used class of antibiotics in Mediterranean aquaculture, has
been found to be plasmid-mediated. Another class of antibiotics commonly used in Mediter-
ranean aquaculture is tetracyclines. These two classes, along with several others, are used
in aquaculture both within EU and globally [15].
Although international organizations such as the FAO, World Health Organization (WHO)
and World Organization for Animal Health (WOAH) have worked to mitigate the spread of
AMR and reduce overall antibiotic use over the past two decades, many countries still lack
laws to regulate the prophylactic use of antibiotics and ban those that are critical to human
health. A major challenge is the lack of communication between different industries [40].
Moreover, many countries do not have specific regulations to monitor antibiotic use in aqua-
culture, leading to significant variation in usage between countries [15][9]. This is especially
problematic in low- and lower middle-income countries, where there are no regulations or
enforcement mechanisms, which is a major contributing factor to antibiotic misuse [40]. If
more countries adopted and adhered to global standards for antibiotic use and monitoring,
the occurrence of ARGs would likely decrease [40]. Evidence shows that countries which have
reduced their total antimicrobial consumption have seen a decline in resistant bacteria. To
this end, the EU has set a target to reduce antibiotic use in farmed animals and aquaculture
by 50% by 2030. This goal appears achievable, provided that action is taken, as antibiotic

1.2
Control of fish disease by probiotic bacteria
A sustainable alternative to the prophylactic administration of antibiotics in aquaculture is
the addition of probiotics [7] (Figure 4). In aquaculture settings, probiotics likely work by
manipulating the microbiota of the system. They can integrate into the environment by
associating not only with the gut of the aquatic animal, but also with the skin, gills, feed,
water and abiotic surfaces, where they exert their probiotic effect [7]. Probiotics may enhance
disease resistance in the host by boosting immune function, improving nutrient utilization,
promoting growth, enhancing water quality, and - as explored in this study - by inhibiting
the proliferation of pathogens, such as by producing antimicrobial compounds [41][42][43].
Fish larvae with mature digestive systems require live feed, such as rotifers, Artemia,
and live microalgae [44][45]. However, opportunistic pathogenic bacteria can proliferate in
larval feed cultures, making them a primary source of pathogens in marine larviculture.
These pathogens can also be introduced through supply water, human contact, or brood-
stock [44][45]. Ultimately, high pathogen loads can lead to infections in larviculture, causing
significant losses and making this stage one of the major economic bottlenecks in marine
aquaculture [44][45]. Since fish larvae have an immature immune system, bacterial infec-
tions cannot be prevented through vaccination as in adult fish [7]. Therefore, to prevent
high pathogen loads in both the feed and, consequently, in the larviculture, it has been pro-
posed to introduce probiotic bacteria during the rotifer and algae culture stage, before they
are used as live feed [45].
Given the promising solutions that probiotics offer for disease management in aquacul-
ture, it is valuable to explore the most promising probionts and understand their mechanisms
of biocontrol.

1.2.1
Promising probionts and their mechanisms of biocontrol
Several bacterial genera are recognized for their potential as probiotics in aquaculture, includ-
ing Bacillus, Alteromonas, Pseudoalteromonas, Pseudomonas, lactic acid bacteria (LAB),
species of Vibrio, and members of the Rhodobacteraceae family, particularly the Roseobacter
group and Pseudovibrio [42][43][46].
As mentioned earlier, probiotics can inhibit pathogen proliferation through various mech-
anisms. These include the production of inhibitory/antagonistic compounds, competition for
nutrients or available energy, and competition for adhesion sites [43]. Probiotics that pro-
duce antagonistic compounds may offer a range of effects, such as quorum quenching (QQ),
anti-viral, -biofilm, -virulence, -bacterial, -fungal, -inflammatory, and iron–chelating prop-
erties [42]. These bioactive compounds can include bacteriocins, siderophores, proteases,
lysozymes, and hydrogen peroxides [47].
For example, the expression of virulence factors in fish pathogens such as A. hydrophila,
V. anguillarum, and V. harveyi, can be regulated by quorum sensing (QS) [7]. This expres-
sion can be reduced through QS inhibition, also referred to as QQ, which has been shown
to improve survival rates in larvicultures [7][47]. Species of the genus Bacillus exhibit QQ

12
February 2nd, 2025
==PAGE:20==
Marie Falksen Weinell
properties, as well as the production of antimicrobial compounds, such as bacteriocins, and
lytic enzymes [7][48].
The marine bacterial genus Pseudoalteromonas is renowned for its ability to produce an-
timicrobial compounds. Among these, hybrids of nonribosomal peptide synthetase (NRPS)
and polyketide synthase (PKS), which encode bromoalterochromides, are particularly no-
table. These compounds have been isolated from several pigmented strains of the genus
[49].
Bromoalterochromides have demonstrated antimicrobial activity against a range of
pathogens, including B. subtilis, S. aureus, E. faecium, C. albicans, and V. anguillarum,
although the exact mechanisms of action are still under investigation [49][50]. In addition to
bromoalterochromides, Pseudoalteromonas species are known to produce a variety of bioac-
tive compounds, such as polyketides, alkaloids, terpenoids, bacteriocins, and siderophores,
which exhibit a broad spectrum of activities, including antimicrobial, antiprotozoal, and
cytotoxic effects [49]. Non-pathogenic Vibrio species have also been shown to encode an-
timicrobial compounds like bacteriocins, along with NRPSs, PKSs, and siderophores, which
likely contribute to their antagonistic activity [27].
One notable antimicrobial secondary metabolite with diverse functions is tropodithietic
acid (TDA), a sulfur-containing tropone derivative [51]. TDA is produced by certain marine
bacteria within the Rhodobacteraceae family, a family within the Alphaproteobacteria. This
includes genera from the Roseobacter group, such as Phaeobacter and Tritonibacter, as well as
some strains of Pseudovibrio [51]. Bacteria from the Roseobacter group are commonly found
in marine larvicultures and live feed, where they can establish themselves without harming
the larvae [11]. Furthermore, these bacteria can antagonize pathogenic species associated
with larval mortality. The primary active compound responsible for their antagonistic activ-
ity against fish pathogenic vibrios is TDA [11]. The proposed mechanism of action suggests
that TDA disrupts the proton motive force in target bacteria, affecting both Gram-positive
and Gram-negative bacteria, making it a broad-spectrum antibiotic [11]. Since resistance to
TDA is difficult to develop, its use in probiotics most likely does not contribute to the risk
of AMR [51].
Roseobacters are adapted to a surface-attached lifestyle, and the production of TDA may
play a role in this adaptation [51]. It has been demonstrated that TDA production is associ-
ated with growth conditions that also promote biofilm formation at the liquid–air interface,
with the highest yields of TDA occurring under stagnant growth conditions [11][52]. This
suggests that TDA could function as a QS signal, influencing both biofilm formation and
motility in the producer [51]. The appearance of brown pigmentation in the biofilm correlates
with a TDA-iron complex. The (weak) iron-chelating properties of TDA could indicate that

it also serves as an iron reservoir [51]. Additionally, research has demonstrated that TDA
can act as an autoinducer for its own biosynthesis, with some bacterial members showing a
link to acyl homoserine lactone (AHL)-mediated regulation of TDA production [51].
The probiotic effects of these promising probionts have also been demonstrated in vivo.
Both Pseudoalteromonas and non-pathogenic Vibrio species have shown potential as probi-
otics in fish challenge trials by reducing mortality caused by bacterial pathogens [53][54][55][56].
Additional studies have highlighted the probiotic effects of Rhodobacteraceae family mem-
bers in challenge trials. Strains of Pseudovibrio and, in particular, roseobacters, have ex-
hibited antagonistic activity against pathogens in aquaculture live cultures, including live
feed, thereby reducing mortality in aquatic organisms. The production of TDA has been
suggested as a key factor contributing to these probiotic effects [11][57][58][45][44][59].
Building on these promising findings, it is essential to evaluate novel putative probionts
through comprehensive in vitro and in vivo testing to gain insight into their potential appli-
cations and effectiveness in disease management within aquaculture [13].
1.2.2
Probiotic strain analysis: in vitro and in vivo evaluation
The primary sources of potential probiotics in aquaculture are strains isolated from natural
aquatic environments, the host organism, or its surrounding environment. These environ-
ments are where the probiotics are expected to exert their probiotic effect and have already
demonstrated their ability to establish themselves (e.g., roseobacters) [42][11][43]. Before
being applied in aquaculture systems, probiotic candidates must meet several key criteria.
Most importantly, they must be safe for the host, the environment, and humans, while also
exhibiting antimicrobial activity [42]. While there are many other desirable characteristics
for probiotics, it is challenging to find a single candidate that possesses all these features
[42].
To screen and preselect novel probiotic candidates, a combination of in vitro and in
vivo tests is conducted [43]. The in vitro evaluation begins with growing marine microbes
in laboratory cultures. However, the low culturability of these microbes, often referred to
as the ‘great plate count anomaly,’ presents a challenge [60]. There are several reasons for
this, including the disruption of natural microbial interactions in laboratory cultures, limited
knowledge of suitable organic substrates, virus infections, and toxic substrate concentrations
(marine bacteria have evolved to oligotrophic conditions) [60]. To address these challenges,
various media have been developed to replicate marine conditions, such as instant ocean
supplemented with casamino acids and glucose and artificial seawater media [61][62].
Once cultivation is achieved, the bacteria can be evaluated in vitro for their probiotic
Master’s thesis
14
February 2nd, 2025
==PAGE:22==
Marie Falksen Weinell
potential. This involves tests such as biochemical characterization, antibiotic susceptibility
and detection of resistance and virulence factor genes, biosafety screening, pathogen inhi-
bition, and identification by DNA sequencing [42]. The probiont performance in pathogen
inhibition is typically assessed through antagonism tests, where pathogens are exposed to the
candidate probionts or their products, either in liquid co-cultures or on solid media [42][43].
However, in vitro results should not be viewed in isolation. Regardless of whether they are
positive or negative, they cannot predict the actual in vivo effects [42][43].
In vivo testing is essential for evaluating the actual probiotic effects on the host, particu-
larly when biological control of the microbiota is the goal [43]. In a controlled environment,
the candidate probiont is introduced to the host, and parameters such as growth, colo-
nization, survival, and physicochemical aspects are monitored and assessed [42]. First, the
pathogenicity of the candidate must be evaluated by simply adding it to the host. Once it
is confirmed that the candidate does not cause pathogenic effects, the probiotic effect on the
host can be tested through in vivo challenge trials (or antagonism tests) with a representative
pathogen [43]. The probiont effectiveness is assessed by comparison of the cumulative mor-
tality of the host across different trial groups: an unchallenged host, a pathogen-challenged
host, and a pathogen-challenged host treated with the probiotic candidate [44]. If the pro-
biotic reduces the accumulated mortality of the challenged host, it may be considered a
potential solution for controlling harmful microbiota in aquaculture [44]. However, these
tests do not represent a universal effect. Therefore, it is crucial to develop and evaluate pro-
biotics for each unique combination of pathogen, host, and environmental conditions [63].
After following the above approach, safe probiotics with a well-established effect on the
target organism should be identified. Before their practical application, comparative pilot ex-
periments should be conducted to estimate the economic implications of using the probiotic,
including an evaluation of its mass production costs, consideration of relevant legislation,
and a cost-benefit analysis [43]. However, for probiotics to be applied more broadly and
effectively in aquaculture, further research is necessary. Specifically, a better understand-
ing of the mechanisms of action, their impact on microbial aquatic communities, and their
potential ecological risks [42].
1.2.3
In silico analysis
As stated above, in vitro evaluation techniques can help identify and characterize novel
putative probiotic isolates [42]. However, while some bacteria can be identified through bio-
chemical tests alone, others do not match any recognized profiles. In such cases, 16S rRNA
gene sequencing is a valuable tool for studying bacterial phylogeny and taxonomy [64]. The
Master’s thesis
15
February 2nd, 2025
==PAGE:23==
Marie Falksen Weinell
16S rRNA gene is present in all bacteria, is highly conserved (with nine variable regions),
and is sufficiently long for informatics analysis (~1500 bp) [64][65]. After sequencing, an in
silico comparison would be conducted by aligning the 16S rRNA gene variable region of the
probiotic candidate with sequences in a database [65]. In most cases, genus identification
is provided (>90%), while species identification is less accurate (65 to 83%), and 1-14% of
sequences may remain unidentified [64]. This is based on the assumption that 16S rRNA
gene sequences with >95% similarity represent the same genus and sequences with >97%
similarity represent the same species [65][66]. Challenges in identification arise from novel
taxa, a lack of sequences in nucleotide databases, species with similar 16S rRNA gene se-
quences, or different copies of 16S rRNA gene sequences within the same organism [64][66].
Where 16S rRNA gene sequencing has its limitations, next-generation sequencing (NGS),
including whole-genome sequencing (WGS), has emerged as an essential tool for identifying
unknown organisms. WGS involves sequencing and assembling an organism’s entire genome,
providing the advantage of more accurate taxonomic classification [67][68]. After genome as-
sembly and gene prediction, the average nucleotide identity (ANI) method can be applied for
bacterial identification. Specifically, the ANIb method uses pairwise Basic Local Alignment
Search Tool (BLAST) alignment to identify the best matches of shared genes between the
query genome and reference genomes from a relevant database, often yielding species-level
identification [69]. For closely related strains that require higher resolution, single nucleotide
polymorphism (SNP) calling can be used for strain-level identification [70].
In general, in silico approaches offer several advantages over in vitro and in vivo methods,
which are often more labor-intensive, expensive, and time-consuming. Additionally, they do
not facilitate screening at the strain level, where beneficial properties may be identified [71].
However, it is important to note that these approaches are typically used in combination,
complementing each other for a more comprehensive analysis [72].
Given that AMR is recognized as a major global public health threat, with ARGs po-
tentially transferring between microorganisms, it is important to investigate the presence of
resistance genes in aquaculture probiotic candidates [36]. In this regard, in vitro antibiotic
susceptibility tests can be complemented with tools such as ResFinder, which identifies genes
associated with AMR [42][73].
In addition to assessing AMR risks, it is valuable to explore the potential of probiotics in
combination with other aquaculture resources, such as microalgae, which have shown promise
as a sustainable feed source for aquaculture [74]. Studies suggest that the addition of algae,
in combination with probiotics, can positively influence the fish microbiota, enhancing health
and overall yield in aquaculture systems [75]. Since microalgal microbiomes benefit from the
Master’s thesis

February 2nd, 2025
==PAGE:24==
Marie Falksen Weinell
nutrients and carbohydrates released by the microalgae, identifying potential carbohydrate-
active enzymes (CAZymes) in probiotic candidates can be valuable [74]. The dbCAN3 web
server is a useful tool for predicting potential CAZymes in the genomes of microbes [76]. By
using this tool, it is possible to assess the potential application of probiotic candidates in
microalgae feed sources before conducting algal growth experiments [74].
Moreover, if an antagonistic phenotype is observed in an aquaculture probiotic candidate,
it is valuable to conduct a genome sequence analysis to determine whether biosynthetic gene
clusters (BGCs) exist that may encode secondary metabolites contributing to this phenotype
[27]. Tools like antiSMASH (antibiotics & Secondary Metabolite Analysis Shell) can be used
for this analysis, covering a wide range of known secondary metabolite compound classes [77].
Additionally, antiSMASH can help identify and predict genes involved in the production of
novel compounds, an approach known as genome mining, which is gaining popularity in the
discovery of new natural products [78].
1.2.3.1
From aquaculture to human health
As outlined, in silico genome mining of microorganisms can, when combined with phenotypic
in vitro assays, help uncover the bioactive potential of marine bacteria as probiotic candi-
dates for aquaculture [27][79][80]. Furthermore, recent advancements in genome sequencing,
bioinformatics, metabolomics, and synthetic biology have significantly enhanced the appli-
cation of microbial genome mining in the discovery and development of human drugs [81].
Throughout the history of human medicine, microbes have been crucial in the discovery and
development of drugs, with about 70-80% of the most important antibiotics in use today
originating from them [80]. Microbes have also contributed to the development of other
therapeutic agents, such as immune modulators, antitumor agents, as well as products for
animal and plant health [81][80].
Until recently, the discovery of bioactive compounds has primarily relied on a classical
bioassay-guided process [79]. However, this process is expensive, time-consuming, and faces
a major challenge of high rediscovery rates [79][82]. Moreover, many BGCs responsible for
producing novel molecules are often cryptic or silent under standard laboratory culture con-
ditions [79][80]. As a result, the pharmaceutical industry has mostly focused on structurally
modifying existing compounds and creating semi-synthetic drugs [82]. However, these ap-
proaches do not address the growing challenges of AMR and the emergence of multi-resistant
pathogens like MRSA. Therefore, there is an urgent need for antibiotics with novel structures
and mechanisms of action [82]. In this context, genome mining plays a pivotal role in the
discovery and development of novel antibiotic compounds [81].

Although scientists have spent the past century isolating and screening soil microorgan-

out, as marine ecosystems represent the majority of the Earth’s biosphere, and their unique

These discoveries highlight marine bacteria as promising sources of novel compounds,

is possible to identify potential novel secondary metabolite BGCs [81]. Subsequently, expres-
sion studies and functional characterization of the BGCs could offer valuable insights into
the biosynthesis and potential applications of these compounds in human medicine [81][80].
1.3
Aim and purpose of this study
Given the unique environmental conditions of the Mediterranean Sea and the challenges
posed by climate change, there has been increasing interest in identifying novel probiotic
candidates from this region [11][10]. Bacterial isolates native to the oligotrophic waters of
the Mediterranean are naturally adapted to this environment, and could potentially improve
fish health and enhance sustainability of Mediterranean aquaculture [83][11]. The present
study aims to characterize a collection of 96 bacterial isolates obtained from a mesocosm
experiment in Crete, Greece, and assess their potential as probiotics for aquaculture [83].
The isolates will be evaluated for their ability to suppress a panel of key (Mediterranean)
fish pathogens, as well as a laboratory strain of human pathogenic S. aureus (NCTC 8325)
[22][23][84].
Probiotic candidates exhibiting antagonistic activity will be further analyzed in silico
to evaluate their applicability in aquaculture and human health. Through genome mining,
BGCs that may encode secondary metabolites contributing to the antagonistic phenotype
will be identified, along with any resistance genes and microalgae-relevant CAZymes. More-
over, the identification of novel BGCs could lead to the discovery of novel compounds with

2.2
Media and cultivation
Liquid cultures of the Mediterranean bacterial isolates in marine broth (MB, Difco™2216)
medium had been preserved in 20% glycerol and stored at -80°C. To recover the isolates
from the glycerol stocks, sterile loops were used to streak the bacteria onto marine agar
(MA, Difco™2216) plates. For pure cultivation of the bacteria, sterile loops were used to
select single colonies from the MA plates followed by inoculation into 5 mL MB medium
and incubation overnight at 25°C with shaking at 200 rounds per minute (rpm). New freeze
stocks were made of the overnight cultures in 20% glycerol and stored at -80°C.
For genomic DNA extraction for 16S rRNA gene amplification (section 2.3.3), single
colonies were inoculated into 3-5 mL MB medium or 2 mL Instant Ocean medium with
casamino acids and glucose (IOCG). The IOCG medium contained 30 g/L Instant Ocean salts
(IO, Aquarium Systems Inc.), 3.3 g/L Bacto™Casamino acids (BD Biosciences) and 0.4%
sterile filtered D(+)-Glucose solution (MilliporeSigma). Cultures were incubated overnight
at 25°C with shaking at 200 rpm.
For testing inhibition of marine bacterial pathogens, IOCG agar was prepared with the
same components as above, but with the addition of 1% bacteriology-grade agar (PanReac
AppliChem).

2.3
Characterization of isolates
2.3.1
Colony PCR
Colonies of each of the bacterial isolates were resuspended in 50 µL dH2O in 1.5 mL Eppen-
dorf tubes. The tubes were incubated at 95°C for 5 minutes, followed by centrifugation at
13,000 rpm for 1 minute. For polymerase chain reaction (PCR) amplification, the following
components were added to a 0.2 mL PCR tube: 6.5 µL dH2O, 0.5 µL forward primer (Fw,
10 µM), 0.5 µL reverse primer (Rv, 10 µM), 12.5 µL TEMPase Hot Start 2X Master Mix K
(VWR Life science), and 5 µL of the cell lysate. Universal primers 27F (Fw, Table 4, TAG
Copenhagen A/S) and 1492R (Rv, Table 4, TAG Copenhagen A/S) were used for full-length
amplification of the 16S rRNA gene.
The PCR program used for 16S rRNA gene amplification included the following steps:
Initial DNA denaturation at 95°C for 5 minutes.
This was followed by 35 cycles, each
consisting of three steps: denaturation at 95°C for 30 seconds, primer annealing at 52°C for
30 seconds, and DNA extension at 72°C for 1 minute and 30 seconds. After completing the
cycles, a final extension step was performed at 72°C for 5 minutes. The reaction was then
stored at 4°C indefinitely.

2.3.3
DNA extraction
For isolates where colony PCR was unsuccessful, DNA extraction was performed instead.
Genomic DNA extraction for 16S rRNA gene amplification was performed using the DNeasy®
PowerSoil® Pro Kit (QIAGEN). The detailed protocol outlined in the online handbook from
QIAGEN was followed, with a few modifications to suit marine bacteria instead of soil
bacteria. In step 1, 250 µL of overnight culture was used instead of 250 mg soil. To enhance
lysis, the tubes were incubated at 65°C for 10 minutes after the addition of Solution CD1 in
step 1. Finally, in step 16, 75 µL Solution C6 was used for elution of the DNA.
The same PCR components and program were utilized for 16S rRNA gene amplification
as for the colony PCRs (section 2.3.1), however, 1 µL of the extracted DNA was combined
with 4 µL dH2O instead of the cell lysate.
2.3.4
DNA quality and quantity analysis
To assess the quality and quantity of extracted DNA, a DeNovix DS-11+ Spectrophotometer
was employed. The device was set to measure dsDNA, and 1 µL of the sample was applied
to the device. DNA quantity was recorded in ng/µl, while quality was evaluated using the
260/230 and 260/280 ratios.
Due to inconsistencies in the quantification results from the DeNovix device, a Qubit®
2.0 Fluorometer (Invitrogen) was utilized in some instances. The extracted DNA for 16S
rRNA gene amplification was quantified with the Qubit® dsDNA HS (High Sensitivity)
Assay Kit (Invitrogen), following the standard procedure. The extracted DNA for whole-
genome sequencing was quantified with the Qubit® dsDNA BR (Broad Range) Assay Kit
(Invitrogen), following the standard procedure.
DNA extractions and 16S rRNA gene PCR products were run on 1% agarose gels. All
gel electrophoreses were conducted at 120 V for 30 minutes. 5 µL of the GeneRuler 1 kb
DNA Ladder (Thermo Scientific) was used as a standard. After the electrophoresis run, the
gel was stained in a 0.0003% w/w ethidium bromide solution for 30 minutes prior to analysis
using the ImageLab software (Bio-Rad) and the Gel Doc XR+ Gel Documentation System
(Bio-Rad).

2.3.5
Identification of isolates using 16S rRNA gene sequences
The software CLC Main Workbench 24 (QIAGEN) was utilized to analyze the quality of
the 16S rRNA gene Sanger sequencing data from Macrogen and trim sequence ends. Subse-

2.3.6
Whole-genome sequencing by Nanopore
Genomic DNA extraction for long-read whole-genome sequencing of 13 probiotic candidates
was performed using the Monarch® HMW DNA Extraction Kit (New England Biolabs).
The protocol for High Molecular Weight DNA (HMW DNA) Extraction from Bacteria (NEB
#T3060) was followed with the low-input designation, with adjustments made for marine
bacterial isolates. In step 2, the procedure for Gram-negative bacterial lysis was applied,
with resuspension in Tris buffer instead of PBS as outlined in step 2a. The sample was
incubated overnight at 37°C with agitation at 1400 rpm (step 2d). The same agitation speed
was maintained in steps 5a and 6. In step 9, centrifugation was performed for 30-40 minutes.
Finally, in step 12, the tubes with eluate were incubated at 37°C for 54 minutes before
proceeding with analysis.
Whole-genome sequencing of the extracted gDNA was performed using Oxford Nanopore
sequencing technology on a GridION platform with a MinION flow cell (FLO-MIN114). Li-
brary preparation was performed using the Rapid Barcoding Kit 24 V14 (SQKRBK114.24,
Oxford Nanopore Technologies) following the standard protocol.
The flow cell (Oxford
Nanopore Technologies) was primed, and the prepared library was loaded into the flow
cell for sequencing. In the flow cell priming mix, 12.5 µL Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA) (20
mg/ml) was used instead of 5 µL BSA (50 mg/ml), adjusting the amount of Flow Cell Flush
(FCF) according to this.
Data acquisition and basecalling was conducted using the MinKNOW software (Oxford
Nanopore Technologies), with the program running for a duration of about 48 hours. The
basecalling model was configured to “super-accurate”, with no trimming of barcodes or se-
lection of custom barcodes. Pore scan intervals were set to 1.5 hours, and the basecalled
reads were generated in FASTQ format. A quality filter was applied with a PHRED score
threshold of Q10 and a minimum read length of 1 kb.

2.3.7
Genome-based bioinformatics for identifying bacterial isolates
Using sequence reads, genomes were assembled and annotated using an in-house pipeline
based on Dragonflye [91]. The pipeline included the removal of sequencing adapters with
Porechop (v.0.2.4) [92], genome assembly with Flye (v.2.9.5) [93], assembly polishing with
Racon [94], and gene annotation with Bakta (v.1.8.2, full database) [95]. Default settings
were applied for all tools. The data were visualized in Bandage (v.0.8.1) [96] to assess the
assembly structures. Additionally, for each assembled genome, the pipeline automatically
generated a summary that included total genome size, contig count, genome coverage, CDS
count, BGC count (via antiSMASH [77]), and species-level identification based on 16S rRNA
gene sequences using BLASTn [90], as well as profiling through Sylph [97]. The pipeline also
provided a more detailed overview of each genome’s contigs, including their shape (circular:
Yes or No), length, depth, and total GC content for each genome.
The genome FASTA files for each bacterial isolate were uploaded to autoMLST (Au-
tomated Multi-Locus Species Tree) [98], where trees based on pairwise average nucleotide
identity (ANI) were generated, using default MLST genes. To refine the bacterial identifica-
tion, ANIb analysis (based on BLAST+ (v.2.11.0) [99]) was performed through JSpeciesWS
(v.4.2.1) [100] using type strains and related organisms from the autoMLST datasets (A.6,
Figures 26, 27, 28, and 29) as well as identification results from the data summary (Table
9). To visualize the ANIb results, heatmaps were created using R studio (v.2024.09.1+394)
[101]. For ANIb values below the 95% genus cutoff, the Type Strain Genome Server (TYGS)
[102] was used for confirmation of the bacterial identity.
Finally, the assembled bacterial isolate genomes were aligned and compared using GToTree
[103] to generate a phylogenomic tree. The resulting tree was visualized with Interactive Tree
Of Life (iTOL) (v.7) [104]. Table 5 provides an overview of the different strains used in the
ANIb analyses as well as the outgroup used in the generation of the phylogenomic tree.

2.4
Probiotic potential of isolates
2.4.1
Spot(-on-lawn) assays
The antagonistic properties of the bacterial isolates were evaluated in plate-based assays
against selected pathogens (Table 3).
For spot assays, IOCG agar (with Peptone for S. aureus NCTC 8325) was prepared and
autoclaved (section 2.2). After cooling the agar to 42-45°C, 1 µL of overnight culture of the
target pathogen was added per mL of basal agar and poured into plates. On cast IOCG agar
plates with embedded pathogen, 10 µL of overnight cultures of the bacterial isolates as well
as Phaeobacter piscinae S26, the positive control, were spotted. The plates were left to dry
in a sterile bench, followed by incubation at 25°C for 48 hours. Inhibitory activity from the
tested isolates were examined by the formation of clearing zones.
2.4.2
Extraction of metabolites and well-diffusion assays
To assess the antimicrobial activity of secreted metabolites from the probiotic candidates, five
isolates were selected for metabolite extraction, followed by evaluation in plate-based assays.
The ethyl acetate-based extraction of metabolites was advised by Peter Bing Svendsen.
5 mL of overnight cultures from the five probiotic candidates were centrifuged in 50 mL
Falcon tubes for 2 minutes at 8500 g. The supernatant from each tube was transferred to
a new 15 mL Falcon tube. An equal volume of a mixture of 1:3 isopropanol:ethyl acetate
containing 0.1% formic acid was added to each supernatant and vortexed thoroughly for 30
seconds. After phase separation, the top phase was carefully collected into a new 15 mL
Falcon tube. The solvent was then evaporated under a N2 flow for about 2 hours. Following
evaporation, the samples were re-dissolved in 170 µL of methanol and transferred to vials
with inserts. The samples were stored at -20°C until further analysis.
For plate-based evaluation of the extracted metabolites, well-diffusion assays were con-
ducted on IOCG agar plates, which were prepared as described for the spot assays (section
2.4.1). On cast IOCG agar plates with embedded pathogen, wells were punched using the
back of sterile P200 pipette tips. To each well, 10-20 µL of extracted metabolites was added.
The plates were then incubated at 25°C for 72 hours. Any inhibitory activity from the tested
extracts were examined by clearing zones.

In silico genome mining
To identify biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) in the genomes of 12 probiotic candidates,

In certain cases,

with their default settings.

3.2.3
Identification of probiotic candidates by whole-genome sequencing
3.2.3.1
Whole-genome sequencing
Thirteen isolates exhibited antagonistic activity against the fish pathogen V. anguillarum
90-11-286 in spot assays (section 3.2.1). When tested against additional fish pathogens and
human pathogenic S. aureus NCTC 8325, the antagonistic activity varied among the iso-
lates, with two demonstrating particularly strong inhibition: C2_T13_0 (Pseudovibrio) and
C1_T11_0 (Pseudoalteromonas) (section 3.2.2, Table 8). The potential probiotic candidates
were identified through whole-genome sequencing.
Genomic DNA from the 13 probiotic candidates was extracted using the Monarch® HMW
DNA Extraction Kit (section 2.3.6). The quality and quantity of the DNA extractions were
assessed spectrometrically (appendix A.5, Table 16) and visually (Figure 13). The extraction
from C1_T0_0 yielded a very low concentration of 0.406 ng/µL (Table 16), which is reflected
in a faint band for this isolate (Figure 13). In contrast, the remaining 12 extractions produced
high concentrations of DNA with clear and bright bands near the top of the gel, consistent
with the expected size of genomic DNA. Long-read whole-genome sequencing was performed
on these 12 isolates using Nanopore technology (section 2.3.6).

The sequencing depth of the contigs may reflect the abundance of the corresponding ge-

Based on genus-level identifications of the isolates (Table 9), the genome assemblies were
grouped into four genera: Vibrio, Pseudovibrio, Pseudoalteromonas, and Tritonibacter. Au-
toMLST was performed on the groups of assemblies (appendix A.6, Figures 26, 27, 28,
and 29). Using a combination of type strains and suggested related organisms from the au-
toMLST datasets and Table 9 (with reference strains summarized in Table 5), ANIb analyses
were conducted. Among the probiotic candidates, three were identified as Vibrio neptunius,
two possibly as Vibrio jasicida, while the remaining isolates were unique, representing other
species of Vibrio, Pseudovibrio, Pseudoalteromonas, and Tritonibacter (Table 10). The diver-

>95% ANIb identity, but B1_T3_0, P2_T15_0, and C2_T3_0 fell below this threshold,
indicating the potential discovery of novel species. This is further supported by the output
from the Type Strain Genome Server (TYGS), which concluded that these isolates could
represent "potential new species". Therefore, these isolates will be referred to as Vibrio sp.
moving forward.

3.2.5
In silico genome mining of probiotic candidates
Based on the spot assays and well-diffusion assays, many of the probiotic candidates, along
with their extracts, demonstrated antagonistic activity against several aquaculture-relevant
fish pathogens. To further assess their potential for use in aquaculture, in silico genome min-
ing was performed to identify the presence of BGCs that could encode secondary metabolites
contributing to the observed antagonistic activity. Resistance genes and microalgae-relevant
CAZymes were also investigated. In parallel, to evaluate the candidates’ potential for human
health applications, the focus was also placed on identifying novel BGCs that could lead to
the discovery of novel compounds with potential therapeutic value. Isolate C1_T0_0 was
excluded from the analysis due to the inability to extract genomic DNA for whole-genome
sequencing.
Using antiSMASH analysis, the genome of C2_T13_0, identified as P. ascidiaceicola,
was found to encode 9 predicted BGCs (appendix A.7, Figure 30). These include one non-
ribosomal peptide synthetase (NRPS) (with 66% similarity to pseudovibriamide), a betalac-
tone (with 13% similarity to fengycin), and seven additional BGCs with unknown functions.

Discussion
Marine microorganisms represent a largely untapped source of bioactive compounds, includ-
ing antibacterial compounds, with considerable potential for pharmaceutical and biotech-
nological applications [112][113]. Recently, several marine bacterial genera have emerged as
promising probiotics in aquaculture due to their ability to inhibit fish pathogens, providing a
sustainable alternative to the prophylactic use of antibiotics in fish rearing [7][11][42][43][46]
[112]. Given the intensification of aquaculture and the challenges posed by climate change
on the Mediterranean industry [13][10], the present study investigated a collection of 96
bacterial isolates from a mesocosm experiment in the Mediterranean, aiming to characterize
these isolates, explore their potential as probiotics for aquaculture, and identify novel natural
product-encoding gene clusters with potential relevance to human health.
In the present study, 57 marine bacterial isolates were identified through 16S rRNA gene
sequencing (Table 7), predominantly belonging to three major genera: Vibrio, Alteromonas,
and Pseudoalteromonas. Less abundant genera included Pseudovibrio, Dokdonia, Bacillus,
and Tritonibacter.
These findings align with previous studies using traditional culturing
methods and 16S rRNA gene sequencing on isolated marine bacteria, which have noted
an overrepresentation of Vibrio, Pseudoalteromonas, and Alteromonas species in aquatic
ecosystems [114][115]. As such, the marine bacterial composition observed in this study likely
does not represent the true abundance and distribution of these genera in the mesocosms,
which would have been captured using culture-independent techniques.
The remaining 39 isolates could not be identified due to slow or no growth in the media
used following inoculation from frozen glycerol stocks. In some cases, contaminant growth
was observed instead. Common drawbacks associated with the use of frozen stocks include
increased risk of contamination, reduced cell recovery, and potential loss of genetic material
and expression [116]. However, as noted in similar studies, the exploration of organisms for
biotechnological applications heavily depends on their cultivability for physiological analysis
[112]. Consequently, the slow- and non-growing isolates from this study would not have been
suitable for further biotechnological investigation, regardless of their identification status.
Most of the isolates were identified with over 97% similarity in 16S rRNA gene sequences,
which typically allows for species-level identification [65][66]. However, several high-identity
matches to different species in the BLAST database exceeded this threshold, resulting in
the majority of isolates being identified only at the genus level. A limitation in 16S rRNA
sequencing is the presence of multiple copies of 16S rRNA gene sequences within the same
organism [64][66] as well as similar or even identical 16S rRNA gene sequences across species

within a genus or between different genera [117]. This challenge is particularly evident with
the isolates identified as both Alteromonas spp. and Pseudoalteromonas spp., as these two
genera are closely related and were only in 1995 separated after the division of Alteromonas
[118]. Furthermore, 16S rRNA genes are highly conserved within several Pseudoalteromonas
species, as well as among many members of the Vibrionaceae and Rhodobacteraceae families
[119][120][121], complicating species-level identification. In addition, the presence of multiple
alleles of the 16S rRNA gene within the Vibrionaceae further exacerbates this challenge [122].
Other limitations of this method include the lack of reference sequences in databases and
the potential presence of novel taxa [64][66][117].
In this study, 13 bacterial isolates demonstrated antagonistic activity in spot assays
against the fish pathogen Vibrio anguillarum 90-11-286. Based on 16S rRNA gene sequenc-
ing, these isolates clustered into three families: Vibrionaceae, Pseudoalteromonadaceae and
Rhodobacteraceae. The Rhodobacteraceae family is a family within the class of Alphapro-
teobacteria, which includes genera from the Roseobacter group, such as Phaeobacter and
Tritonibacter, as well as strains of Pseudovibrio [51].
These findings are consistent with previous studies in which marine culturable bacte-
ria were isolated and tested for antibacterial activity [112][123]. Specifically, a 2009 study
collected 519 strains during a global Danish marine research expedition (Galathea 3) and
tested them against V. anguillarum 90-11-287 and S. aureus 8325. The study identified three
major groups exhibiting antagonistic activity against V. anguillarum 90-11-287 based on par-
tial 16S rRNA gene sequences: Vibrionaceae, Pseudoalteromonas spp. and the Roseobacter
group [112]. While the size of the present study is smaller than that of the 2009 study, it
suggests that the Rhodobacteraceae family may have a broader spectrum of antagonistic
activity compared to the Roseobacter group alone, given the presence of two antagonistic
Pseudovibrio spp. and only one Tritonibacter sp. in this study.
Given the limitations of 16S rRNA gene-based identification, whole-genome sequencing
(WGS) was employed to achieve more accurate species-level identification of all the antago-
nistic isolates (except C1_T0_0) using Average Nucleotide Identity (ANI) analysis [67][68].
ANI analysis has been used for classifying members of the Rhodobacteraceae family [121], as
well as bacteria from the Pseudoalteromonas and Vibrio genera [124][27]. A study from 2022
emphasized the high species diversity within the Vibrio genus, using ANI analysis to identify
11 distinct Vibrio species from a collection of 15 bacterial species represented by 42 isolates
[124]. These findings are consistent with findings of the present study, where ANIb analysis
of the assembled probiotic candidate genomes identified seven species of Vibrio across nine
isolates, with only three isolates belonging to the same species. In the same 2022 study, an

To explore the diversity and relatedness of the Vibrio isolates, ANIb identification results

faster and more precise than existing profiling methods [126]. The findings of this study
support these claims, highlighting the effectiveness of Sylph profiling for rapid, species-level
identification of environmental isolates, without the need for supplemental autoMLST and
ANIb analysis. However, the results also recognize the importance of having species-level
representative genomes in the database to ensure accurate profiling.
ANIb analysis of the assembled genomes for the remaining three isolates identified Pseu-
dovibrio ascidiaceicola (C2_T13_0), Pseudoalteromonas flavipulchra (C1_T11_0), and Tri-
tonibacter mobilis (B1_T13_0), all with ANI values above the 95% species threshold [125].
These species, or closely related strains, have previously been reported to produce antimicro-
bial compounds [127][128][110][111], aligning with the findings of this study. The phyloge-
nomic tree in Figure 15 illustrates the genomic relationships between all 12 identified probi-
otic candidates and their respective species. The tree reveals two major branches: one within
the Gammaproteobacteria class, which includes species of Vibrio and Pseudoalteromonas,
and another within the Alphaproteobacteria class, which includes species of Pseudovibrio
and Tritonibacter. All of these species are Gram-negative [124][51][129]. Taxonomic classi-
fications of the isolates and their species were confirmed using online taxonomic databases
and visualizers [130][131].
Additionally, from the phylogenomic tree it is possible to notice patterns in the meso-
cosm treatments probiotic candidates were collected from. The results demonstrate that
isolates with antagonistic activity were found in all four treatment types, though the con-
trol and HuminFeed treatments were more dominant in the collection. Future studies could
investigate whether a statistically significant correlation exists between treatment type and
probiotic potential, which could expedite the discovery of novel probiotic candidates.

recent 2024 study also demonstrated that a Pseudoalteromonas flavipulchra strain exhibited
strong antagonistic activity against three Vibrio species, including V. parahaemolyticus [127].
Similar to the findings of the present study, the inhibitory activity of the Vibrio isolates
in the 2009 study varied, with their activity generally much less pronounced compared to
that of Pseudoalteromonas against V. anguillarum and S. aureus [112]. However, it has
been established that members of Vibrionales, including both known and unidentified Vibrio
spp., exhibit antagonistic properties against several other bacteria, including pathogenic
Vibrionales [132][133][134][120][27]. Furthermore, a study on a collection of Pseudovibrio
isolates demonstrated significant inhibition in 31 out of 33 tested strains against a range of
fish and clinical pathogens, including Y. ruckeri (strain not specified), V. anguillarum (strain
not specified), and S. aureus NCDO 949 [135]. These findings are consistent with the results
of the present study, which showed strong inhibition in one Pseudovibrio isolate (C2_T13_0)
against all tested pathogens, and reduced in the other isolate (C1_T0_0), which highlights
that Pseudovibrio strains can exhibit varying levels of inhibitory activity. In summary, the
results of previous studies reinforce the broad-spectrum antibacterial activity observed in
this study for species of Pseudoalteromonas, Vibrio, and Pseudovibrio.
In contrast, there is a discrepancy between the findings of this study for isolate B1_T13_0,
a Tritonibacter mobilis, and the only member of the Roseobacter group, compared to pre-
vious studies. In this study, this isolate only inhibited V. anguillarum. However, the 2009
study reported large and clear inhibition zones against V. anguillarum and S. aureus for sev-

studies, which have demonstrated the antagonistic activity of Tritonibacter species towards
several fish pathogenic vibrios, as well as the bactericidal effect of tropodithietic acid (TDA),
which is produced by several species of Tritonibacter, including T. mobilis, against other fish
pathogens and human pathogens like S. aureus [51][136][137].

compared to the chromosome (1:3 ratio) may contribute to reduced expression under these
conditions. These factors could help explain why B1_T13_0 exhibited limited antagonistic
activity in the spot assays. The results from the well-diffusion assays support this notion.
Supernatant metabolite extracts from B1_T13_0, obtained after one week of stagnant in-
cubation, demonstrated strong inhibitory activity against all the tested pathogens. This
suggests that growth conditions significantly influence TDA production and its associated
antimicrobial activity in T. mobilis.

this study, the BLASTp 52% identity observed for C2_T13_0 suggests it could harbor TDA
genes related to those of T. mobilis F1926.

overnight also demonstrate strong inhibitory activity. This activity could be attributed to

Although TDA genes were not detected in the genomes of C2_T13_0 and B1_T13_0
by antiSMASH, the tool was able to predict the presence of a broad range of BGCs in
the 12 antagonistic isolates, spanning the four genera (Table 11).
The identification of
BGCs associated with secondary metabolism, such as NRPS and PKS, has been widely
used in genome mining as an indicator for discovering novel bioactive compounds, including
antibiotics [79], as well as for identifying potential BGCs that may contribute to observed
antagonistic activity [27]. A 2015 genome mining study on marine Gram-negative bacteria
showed that the Gammaproteobacteria class tends to harbor a higher abundance of NRPS
and PKS clusters compared to the Alphaproteobacteria class [79]. In this study, the number
of NRPS and PKS clusters in the Gammaproteobacteria ranged from zero to nine, while
the Alphaproteobacteria contained two each. These findings suggest significant variability
in the number of these clusters across species within the Gammaproteobacteria class, with
certain species, such as those within Pseudoalteromonas, encoding a notably high number of
clusters. However, the genomes of the Alphaproteobacteria generally harbored just as many
BGCs as the Vibrio spp., highlighting the diversity and potential for bioactive compound
production across these groups.
Pigmented species of the Pseudoalteromonas genus are well-known for producing antimi-
crobial metabolites, including bromoalterochromides A/A’ and B/B’, and are characterized
by a high number of BGCs, particularly distinct NRPS-PKS clusters [49][139]. These find-
ings are consistent with the present study, where isolate C1_T11_0, a yellow-pigmented
Pseudoalteromonas flavipulchra, was found to encode an NRPS with 100% similarity to bro-
moalterochromide A. Notably, C1_T11_0 also encoded the highest number of predicted
BGCs, with NRPS and PKS clusters making up 9 out of 18 of the identified BGCs. Further-
more, 15 out of the 18 BGCs were classified as unknown, suggesting a very high potential for
novel bioactive compounds in this isolate. However, in the well-diffusion assay, the super-
natant metabolite extract from C1_T11_0 demonstrated no inhibitory activity against the
tested pathogens, despite the pure bromoalterochromide extract showing activity. These re-
sults suggest several possibilities: bromoalterochromides may not be secreted, they may not
be efficiently extracted by ethyl acetate, or they may require purification for effective inhi-
bition. It is also possible that other mechanisms, potentially encoded by the many unknown
BGCs in C1_T11_0, could be responsible for the observed inhibition. Interestingly, only
C1_T11_0 among a collection of five pigmented Pseudoalteromonas (P1_T11_1, P2_T7_1,
B2_T13_1, B2_T5_0) demonstrated inhibitory activity in the spot assays against V. an-
guillarum.

The antiSMASH analysis revealed a variety of BGCs among the Vibrio isolates with
several common features. All of them contained betalactones, which are known for their an-

were the presence of bacteriocin- and PUFA-encoding BGCs, respectively, each associated
with antibacterial activity [145][146].
Previous studies have highlighted the role of siderophores - iron sequestering secondary
metabolites - in competitive exclusion among vibrios [147][148]. However, siderophore pro-
duction has also been identified as a key virulence factor for pathogenic vibrios in clams
under low iron availability [147]. In this study, however, the effectiveness of siderophores
may have been limited by the iron content in the IOCG agar used in the spot assays, as
casamino acids in the medium are supplemented with ’an optimum amount of iron’ [149].
This suggests that other predicted BGCs, both known or unknown, could be responsible for
the observed antagonistic activity. The results from both the spot assays and well-diffusion
assays indicated considerable variability in inhibitory activity, even among isolates of the
same Vibrio species (e.g. V. neptunius). The combined findings of the in vitro and in silico
analyses suggest that Vibrio species are highly diverse, highlighting the potential importance
of species- and strain-level differences in both activity and pathogenesis.
The number of predicted BGCs with unknown functions varied across the isolates, ranging
from two to five for the Vibrio spp., four to seven for the Alphaproteobacteria, and 15
for the Pseudoalteromonas. These findings highlight the significant potential for secondary
metabolite production and the discovery of novel compounds within the probiotic candidates
from this study, which could have potential applications in aquaculture and human health.
Notably, isolates or their extracts that demonstrated inhibitory activity against S. aureus
NCTC 8325 warrant further investigation for their therapeutic potential. However, since
antiSMASH is a broad prediction tool, future studies may benefit from using complementary
methods to fully explore the bioactive potential of these isolates [79]. Additionally, chemical
analyses in future studies are essential to identify the specific compounds responsible for the
antagonistic activities of the probiotic candidates.

Conclusions
To control disease outbreaks in aquaculture systems, antibiotics have been extensively used,
leading to the development and spread of antimicrobial resistance (AMR). A promising,
sustainable alternative to antibiotics is the use of probiotic marine bacteria, which represent
a largely untapped source of bioactive compounds. These bacteria offer potential solutions
for disease management in aquaculture, particularly in the form of probiotics, as well as for
human health through the discovery of novel biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs). The present
study investigated a collection of 96 bacterial isolates from a mesocosm experiment in the
Mediterranean, aiming to characterize them and explore their potential applications in both
aquaculture and human health.
This study identified 57 out of 96 bacterial isolates by 16S rRNA gene sequencing, primar-
ily to the genus level. The isolates belonged to three major genera: Vibrio, Alteromonas, and
Pseudoalteromonas, with less abundant genera including Pseudovibrio, Dokdonia, Bacillus,
and Tritonibacter.
Thirteen bacterial isolates demonstrated antagonistic activity in spot assays against the
fish pathogen Vibrio anguillarum 90-11-286. Based on 16S rRNA gene sequencing, these
isolates clustered into species from Vibrio (9 isolates), Pseudoalteromonas (1 isolate), and
Rhodobacteraceae (3 isolates), including two Pseudovibrio isolates and one Tritonibacter
isolate, which is part of the Roseobacter group.
The thirteen probiotic candidates were also assessed for their ability to suppress other
key fish pathogens, including Vibrio harveyi SA9.1, Vibrio crassostreae DMC-1, Vibrio para-
haemolyticus NUK7, Yersinia ruckeri 100415, and Aeromonas hydrophila F5, as well as
human pathogenic S. aureus NCTC 8325. Two isolates strongly inhibited all pathogens:
C1_T11_0, a yellow-pigmented Pseudoalteromonas, and C2_T13_0, a brown-pigmented
Pseudovibrio.
Isolate B1_T13_0, a Tritonibacter mobilis, only inhibited V. anguillarum, which is in-
consistent with previous studies where this species – and its antimicrobial compound TDA
- has demonstrated strong antagonistic activity against several fish pathogens, as well as
human pathogens such as S. aureus. BLASTn analysis of TDA genes from T. mobilis F1926
confirmed the presence of TDA genes in B1_T13_0. This study suggests that the reduced
antimicrobial activity in this isolate may be due to suboptimal shaken growth conditions
prior to the spot assays, as well as a lower abundance of the TDA-encoding plasmid in
the genome. This hypothesis is supported by the strong antimicrobial activity of the su-
pernatant metabolite extract from B1_T13_0, obtained after a week of optimal stagnant

To achieve more accurate species-level identification of the probiotic candidates, whole-
genome sequencing (WGS) and average nucleotide identity (ANI) analysis were performed.
Three Vibrio isolates exhibited ANI values below 95% (90.8%, 93.95%, and 85.09%), suggest-
ing the potential discovery of novel Vibrio species. Several of the Vibrio species identified,
including known strains, have been linked to pathogenicity in marine organisms. Given this,
the suitability of the Vibrio isolates as probiotics is uncertain, and further evaluation for
pathogenicity is needed.
AntiSMASH analysis of the 12 probiotic candidate genomes predicted the presence of a
wide range of BGCs. Isolate C1_T11_0, a yellow-pigmented Pseudoalteromonas flavipul-
chra, encoded the highest number of predicted BGCs (18), with NRPS and PKS clusters
comprising half of them. Notably, one NRPS cluster exhibited 100% similarity to bromoal-
terochromide A, a known antimicrobial metabolite. However, the lack of inhibitory activity
in the supernatant metabolite extract from C1_T11_0 suggests issues with the extraction,
or that antagonistic activity observed in the spot assays could be attributed to other BGCs
encoded in the isolate.
A variety of BGCs were detected among the Vibrio isolates, including betalactones,
siderophores, ectoines, aryl-polyenes, bacteriocins, and a PUFA-encoding BGC. The results
of the Vibrio analyses in this study underscore the potential importance of species- and
strain-level differences in both antimicrobial activity and pathogenesis.
The number of predicted BGCs, both with known and unknown functions, varied across
the isolates and bacterial families, highlighting the significant potential for secondary metabo-
lite production and the discovery of novel compounds within the probiotic candidates. Fu-
ture experimental studies are essential to identify the specific compounds responsible for the
antagonistic activities observed in these candidates.
None of the probiotic candidates encoded resistance genes according to ResFinder; how-
ever, antibiotic susceptibility tests should still be conducted to fully confirm the absence
of resistance genes before their application in aquaculture. Additionally, two isolates en-
coded a high number of microalgae-relevant CAZyme families, suggesting their potential as
candidates for algal growth experiments, particularly for use in aquaculture microalgae feed
sources.
In conclusion, this study identified 13 marine bacterial isolates with varying degrees of in-
hibition against a selection of fish pathogens and a human pathogen, including three promis-
ing probiotic candidates: C2_T13_0 (Pseudovibrio ascidiaceicola), C1_T11_0 (Pseudoal-
teromonas flavipulchra), and B1_T13_0 (Tritonibacter mobilis). C2_T13_0 and C1_T11_0
