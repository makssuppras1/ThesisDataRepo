ABSTRACT
This paper contain a description of the control strategies implemented to deliver a
’¼éexible solution that allows not specialized personnel to interact with complex a thermo
hydraulic system, represented by the Heat Switchboard, in terms of energy, a unit better
understandable for most engineers. With a wider view, hiding the systemŌĆÖs speci’¼üc variables
and threating it as an energy exchanger will allow the users to better study the component
as part of a multi-energy system and integrate it in a larger grid. To achieve these goals,
an approach based on two di’¼Ćerent components is followed, respectively for reasoning over
the system con’¼üguration and userŌĆÖs requests, and actuating a control action to achieve the
requested control objective.
This approach will facilitate the human interface with the equipment whilst keeping safe
the controller operations from human errors or lack of understanding - in fact, the operator
will only be able to hand requests to the reasoning component that will then produce the
input for the controller, which will be running as far as the reasoning component deems it
either necessary or possible.
A proof of concept has been developed and released and the results are documented in this
report - the system has been tested over several con’¼ügurations to better test the potential
of the Heat Switchboad and the ’¼éexibility of its control prototype.

1.1
Background
In contrast with the "classic" Energy Systems approach, where each energy system operation
is treated independently, the Multi Energy System approach studies the possible integration
of di’¼Ćerent energy vectors, to increase the overall performance of the energy supply. Several
assessments and models are trying to de’¼üne performance parameters in order to clearly
identify the pains and gains of the MES approach [8]. One of the most attractive prospective,
o’¼Ćered from the MES, is a pro’¼ütable integration of ’¼éuctuating renewable energy sources
in the electric grid with the aid of ’¼éexible electrical devices as heat pumps and electric
boilers that serve in the thermal grid. The classic approach sees the heat demand and
electricity demand as decoupled problems which results in a not e’¼ācient solution - the
’¼éuctuation of the renewable sources may bring to an over generation of electric power when
heat demand is also high, having CHP plants running at high rates [9], with consequent
necessary curtailment of renewable electric power sources. In this context take place
a reinterpretation of the district heating network from large and isolated DH areas, to
interconnected networks fed with centralized and distributed sources, and waste heat - this
new model produces great costsŌĆÖ and CO2 savings [10].
In this new district heating topology great relevance should be given to the networksŌĆÖ
interconnecting component that the heat switchboard is meant to represent as it would
also be the point of connection for the numerous DER (distributed energy resources) - this
project will be providing an interface to allow operators to easily interact with the heat
switchboard and approach the "optimal energy ’¼éow" with the right tools.
The CEE is in the process of establishing an experimental facility for multi-energy systems
research where the interaction of thermal and electrical energy systems will be tested. At
the core of the facility is the heat switchboard which allows to control the energy exchange
between di’¼Ćerent heat sources, thermal storage devices and heat consumers, by directing
the ’¼éow of heated water using automated valves and pumps. A basic level of automation
has been implemented which allows remote control of valves and pumps as well as the
remote reading of most of the sensors in the installation (temperatures, ’¼éow rates, thermal
power, etc..).
However, most future research use of the heat switchboard will require control at a higher
abstraction level; for example, an experiment may require a constant energy transfer
towards one consumer, or a constant temperature, pressure or ’¼éow to be maintained at
a particular point of the system. Scenarios like these will require complex coordination
of several valves and pump setpoints whose relationship with e.g. ’¼éow rates is highly
nonlinear and which may mutually in’¼éuence each other (e.g. opening a valve in one place
of a line changes the pressure distribution along the entire line and so the pump working

The complexity associated with the physical low-level control of the setup is expected to

1.2
Problem Statement
The Heat Switchboard requires a suitable control strategy to be a ’¼éexible tool in the hands
of a user without a deep knowledge in thermo-hydraulic systems. The ’¼ünal scope of this
component of the thermal grid is to allow a future deeper integration of the electrical and
thermal grid, for which some use cases have already been proposed [11].
Of particular interest is the Use Case that expects the Thermal Grid to provide ancillary
services to the Power grid, i.e. damping of electric power in the form of heat [11].
In order to satisfy such a Use Case, a suitable control strategy for the Heat Switchboard
should allow the control of Thermal Power so to allow the whole integrated system to be
studied with the same quantity, power.
The following points will be addressed in this project:
ŌĆó De’¼ünition of a use case and con’¼ügurations that the Control Abstraction Layer is
meant to satisfy;
ŌĆó De’¼ünition and implementation of a con’¼üguration strategy to translate the user request
of moving energy from one input to an output in a suitable hydraulic circuit;
ŌĆó De’¼ünition and implementation of a control strategy to perform the energy transfer;
ŌĆó Investigation of the limitation of the existing hardware;
The second and third point shall be investigated by the mean of an automated system in
order to fully address the problem.

complete of all the components necessary to its functioning, i.e. pumps, valves, sensors.
These components are accessible, for control purposes, from the Syslab Poll Server, a
software platform that allows interactions with physical components.
The existing programming language Python will be used for this project, alongside with
its many functional modules.
The modelling of the thermo-hydraulic system represented by the Heat Switchboard and
the design of the related controllers, will be approached according to existing literature [4],
[12], [2].

2.1
Heat Switchboard description
In this section the facility the prototype is meant to control will be described, in order to
give a clear understanding of the project scope and of the design choices - a drawing of the
facility can also be founded in Appendix A.
The heat switchboard (HSW) is a closed system where, if the electric power that supplies
the auxiliaries is disregarded, only energy in the form of heat is exchanged with the
external environment, that is composed of heat sources, sinks and energy storage - the heat
switchboard can be e’¼Ćectively imagined as a con’¼ügurable heat exchanger where the heat
’¼éow can be directed at will from any its Input to any of its Output. The great interest
in this infrastructure is that allows the local integration of Power-to-Heat technologies,
the collection of heat waste from large cooler units and the possible connection of district
heating (DH) networks - the integration of local heat suppliers will also allow a district
heating network to respond faster to changes in the heat load.
The ’¼éuid running in the hydraulic circuit is still water, both for its thermal properties and
in order to avoid possible dangers related with ’¼éuid leakages.
2.1.1
The BusBars
The busbars are pipes that compose the Heat Switchboards backbone and they are paired
in two couples. In each couple a single busbar carries the forward ’¼éow (warm water) and
the other carries the return ’¼éow (cold water). The reason of having two couples is easily
explainable by the possibility of having more I/Os connected at the same time. To clear
any doubt and with the aid of the electrical analogy this set up is inspired from, the
busbar is nothing but a collector with a potential energy and the chance of being crossed
by a ’¼éux - voltage is here pressure and current is here water ’¼éow. The only substantial
di’¼Ćerences are that in this case the direction of ’¼éow is inherent the systemŌĆÖs set up and
the heat switchboard is a closed system. A ’¼üfth busbar composes the heat switchboard, a
so called booster bar - this pipe has not a speci’¼üed direction and is meant to allow series
connection between two input sources. This con’¼üguration may not intuitive, but represents
a brilliant solution to allow a prompt response to heat load variations. Considering the DH
supplying a load and a consequent step up change in the heat demand - if the heat source
is geographically far from the load, a large transport delay can be the cause of a very
slow time response to the step change with a really poor provided service - temporarily
connecting in series the DH with a local Power-To-Heat technology would allow the system
to have a faster transient response and cover the transport delay. It is important to note
that each busbar has, during their employment, a de’¼üned and unique temperature level.

2. System Description
As already mentioned, the heat switchboard is a closed system that only exchange energy
with the external environment.
The heat switchboard consists of 8 bays of which only 7 are currently connected to an
actual heat source or sink.
2.1.2
The Bays
The bays are an abstraction that are meant to identify and group together a set of compo-
nents that connect the busbars to a source or sink.
Each bay is composed by a set of valves, that allow connection to any forward-return
busbar, and a forward and return pipes which supplies an heat exchanger to allow energy
but not ’¼éow exchange with the external environment - within the forward and return pipes
can be installed a pump, an energy meter and an expansion vessel. Additional analog
temperature and pressure sensors are installed in each bay, whilst the busbars condition
are not monitored - this may change in the future, but the approximation of monitoring
only the bays is justi’¼üed by the relatively small dimensions of the circuit, the good quality
of the insulation and the accuracy of the sensors installed in the bays.
The bays that exchange energy with heat sinks are not connected to the booster bar as it
didnŌĆÖt appear relevant to have heat sinks connected in series.
For the sake of simplicity, the heat exchangers that connects the Heat Switchboard to the
external environment will be addressed with the name of the heat sinks and sources that
the heat exchangers supply.
2.1.3
The Valves
The valves are two ways type with an equal percentage inherent characteristic and they
are installed with a motorized actuator. The valves and actuators are supplied by Belimo
and will be further described in Chapter 5.
2.1.4
The Pumps
The pumps are smart centrifugal pumps with di’¼Ćerent operating modes that allows the
user to simplify the control tasks. The pumps enclose head, temperature sensors to provide
feedback to their embedded controllers and which reading are available for the user -
additionally they are able to provide the rpm and calculate ’¼éow values.
The pumps are mono directional and the water can only ’¼éow through them in one direction
- nonetheless the pump that is shut would not prevent water ’¼éow. The pumps are supplied
by Grundfos and will be further described in Chapter 5.

2.1.5
The Energy Meters
The energy meters are units, installed in each bay, capable of measuring Forward and
Return Temperatures, ’¼éow, and of providing a calculated value for the cumulated energy
and thermal power exchanged across the heat exchanger. The energy meter are supplied
by Kamstrup.
2.1.6
Pipes
All the pipes are of cast iron, of the same size of the busbars and are protected by a
cohibentation layer to limit the heat losses towards the environment - pipes will be further
described in Chapter 5.
2.1.7
The sinks
The sinks are in the current set up 4 and they are the composed by the heating system of
the 2 SYSLAB Flex Houses [13] and 2 dump loads composed of air-water heat exchangers.
The Flex Houses sinks presents a bypass circuit consisting in a non return valve between
the pump and the valves, that can shortcircuit the return and forward pipes and keep the
water ’¼éowing in the sink without returning to the source - this detail in’¼éuences the ’¼éow
controllability in the Flex Houses sinks. Sinks power rating and details can be founded in
Appendix A.
2.1.8
The sources
are in the current set up 3 and they are composed by the so called booster heater, an
’¼éexible temperature source, an heat pump and an oil stove that in this context is meant
to represent a high temperature district heating input - sources power rating and details
can be founded in Appendix A.
2.2
Heat Switchboard Monitoring and Actuation
The pumps, valves and energy meters are the active components of the heat switchboard
and their readings and status are accessible for the user via the Syslab Poll Server platform
[1] - a valve position can be remotely read and set as the pumps operating mode and
setpoints, and the energy meter readings can be extracted.

4.3.1
Introduction to n-Multitier Architecture and sequence diagrams
This design pattern [14] recommends to "physically" isolate system components according to
their distance from the hardware, establishing between each layer a client server relationship.
The application layer, within the n-multitier architecture, covers all those tasks that require
reasoning and detailed processing, with the knowledge requested to the data access layer.
The data access layer has the task, upon request, of reading and writing values over a
database and to send feedback to the application layer.
In their master-slave relationship, the application layer acts as Client and the data access
layer as Server - it is relevant to mention that most layers in a n-Multitier architecture are
often connected respectively to an higher and a lower layer and then act as client or server
according to the layer they are communicating with.
A great advantage of this architecture is the possibility of replacing one of the two layer
upon requirements changing without compromising the operation of the other as the only
link between the two layer would be the respective APIs (interface).
The communication between layers is a core element of any Multitier Architecture and
it is often bene’¼ücial to represent it by the mean of sequence diagrams. This particular
diagrams present on their top, on the horizontal axes, the di’¼Ćerent architecture components
and scrolling down to the bottom, again on the horizontal axes, the way this components
interact - the di’¼Ćerent position of the interactions between components on the vertical
axes, identify the time sequence with which the interactions happens [15].

host the controllers. In general the LL will have no direct interaction with the Hardware

Introduction to Graph Theory
A Graph is a mathematical structure meant to study the nodes properties and the links

Introduction to Expert Systems
Expert systems are software components able to emulate the decision making of human
experts.
As such the main components of an expert system are represented by a knowledge base
and an inference engine to respectively simulate knowledge and decisions.
32
==PAGE:45==
4. Con’¼üguration Strategy
The knowledge base stores information about certain facts, and in particular it stores the
solutions domain for a fact that are all the possible values/properties/conditions that a
fact can have, an automotive example [16] will help to clarify this point. A car can be
characterized with three facts, horsepower, colour and model - each of these facts has a
certain deterministic domain that classify the carsŌĆÖ availability, the horsepower can only
be within 20-60hp, the colour can only be green/red/blue and the model coup├©/cabrio/o’¼Ć
road.
The inference engine is composed by a set of if-then rules that given a starting point are
able restrict the facts domain to a smaller domain and output a univoque or set of solutions
- following the automotive example:
# Rule 1
if the car is red => the horse power can only be within 20-40hp
# Rule 2
if the horse power is within 20-40hp and the colour red => the model is coup├©
The answer provided by the inference engine over the request of a red car, is a coup├© with
a selectable power range of 20-40hp.
In the previous example the "red" car was the start point but it needs to be noted that the
starting point could either be a fact of the knowledge base or an additional fact introduced
to start the engine. The strong point of an inference engine against normal procedural
if-else-then analysis is that the point of entrance of the engine are multiple and so the
rules are not triggered in a de’¼üned order but only when their condition become true - as
consequence, an inference engine has multiple cycles of operations in order to evaluate if a
rule condition gets satis’¼üed after a previous rule was applied.

The control objective of all con’¼ügurations is to control the thermal power Pth exchanged

CONCLUSION
In this chapter conclusions are recommendations are exposed.
7.1
Notes over the Con’¼ügurator and Controllers design
In this section a critical analysis of the work done will be exposed with reference to the
di’¼Ćerent components of the prototype and hardware.
7.1.1
Notes on hydraulic design
The current set up of the HSW shall be improved by installing in each bay an heat
exchanger that would completely isolate the houses so not to have interferences on the
’¼éow regulation.
The concept of installing valves with di’¼Ćerent kvs according to the source/sink rating
should be replicated in the next implementation of similar systems.
It may be relevant to install ’¼éow meters and temperature sensor on the sink/source sides
of the HEX in order to have visibility on the dynamics of the di’¼Ćerent actors connected in
order to better understand the HSW dynamic.
The HSW is meant to be connected in the future to energy storage devices that could
act both as sink or source - on this regard the con’¼ügurator shall be improved in order
to generalize the ’¼éow direction that now is ’¼üxed by a strict categorization of sinks and
sources. Additionally, inactive pumps allow ’¼éow in a single direction, providing an obstacle
to a bidirectional ’¼éow that would be necessary to provide to an energy storage device - for
this reason a pipes bypass could be installed in the bays hosting energy storage devices in
order to bypass the pumps when not involved in the circulation.
7.1.2
Notes on the Data Structure and Logical Layer
The data structure should include hydraulic informations of the system, i.e. kvs of the
valves, so that this information could be enclosed in the Logical Layer ’¼ünal output: in
this way the multiple sinks controller will be able to calculate the most accurate corrector
factor possible taking into account the di’¼Ćerent kvs of the individual sinks. Additionally
the tests over the multiple sinks controller highlighted that the distances between the
sinks and the source a’¼Ćect the way each individual sink respond to hydraulic transients,
therefore topological distances is an issue that should be address.
7.1.3
Notes on the controller to supply a single sink
The performances of this controller are acceptable to address a Thermal Power control,
however it would be of great interest the attempt of optimizing the controller constants
94
==PAGE:107==
7. Conclusion
through several experimental iterations. The Feedforward-Feedback approach used in this
controller seems a solid start for future use cases. Additional testing is still require over
multiple sources, but the outlook is very promising. Whether this control provides a good
generalization, attention should be paid to a consistent formulation of the con’¼üguration
requests as it may impact over the sensitivity of the Thermal Power response to the
actuator position.
7.1.4
Notes on the controller to supply multiple sinks
This controller has proved to provide good performances and the corrector factor approach
seems to provide a good solution to valves interaction. However further testing is required
for an increasing number of sinks as, whether the approach holds for two sinks, the control
stability still requires a practical validation for an increasing number of sinks. Particular
concerns are provided by the distance between sinks which may cause the invalidation of
the corrector factor approach.
Whether this control provides a good generalization, attention should be paid to a consistent
formulation of the con’¼üguration requests as it may impact over the sensitivity of the
Thermal Power response to actuator position variations. This point is particularly critical
for the multiple sink controller as it may occur the case at which the valves are working at
their extremes i.e. one completely open and the other completely closed: this not only
could create a con’¼éict to the pumps controller but also an harder achievement of the
control objective. For this reason, only sinks with similar setpoints and dynamics shall be
connected together.
This result is general and should be applied to any control approach.
7.1.5
Notes on a full hydraulic model
The provided controllers o’¼Ćers a very good trade o’¼Ćbetween accuracy of the control actions
and complexity of the model. However, it would be of interest to develop a full hydraulic
model of the Heat Switchboard and use such model to improve the Feedforward action of
the single sink controller and develop a similar approach for the multiple sinks controller.
7.2
Future works
The provided prototype represents a ’¼éexible functioning tool to operate the HSW in
terms of energy with an acceptable performance, hiding the characteristic variables of the
hydraulic system - any engineer would now be able to operate such a complex system.
This project provided a tool that opens the route to several studies and practical
implementation of possible use cases related to the integration of the power and thermal
grid [11].
