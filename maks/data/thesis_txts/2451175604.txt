Preface
This thesis represents a partial fulﬁlment of the requirements needed for a Master of Science de-
gree at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), within the study line Medicine and Technology.
The project has been prepared in cooperation with DTU’s Department of Health Technology and
the Danish Center for Sleep Medicine. This thesis was carried out from February 4th, 2019 to July
4th, 2019 and represent a 30 ECTS credits workload.
The ﬁrst purpose of this thesis has been to develop an algorithm capable of achieving adap-
tive segmentation of sleep signals using brain activity recorded by electroencephalography (EEG).
Polysomnographic data from a set of healthy patients, as well as sets of hypersomniac patients was
provided by the Danish Centre for Sleep medicine. Those included patients having type 1 or type 2
narcolepsy. Speciﬁcally, an algorithm using linear prediction modelling was implemented in Matlab
and applied on the c3-a2 channel of patients’ EEG. The number of boundaries found in each sleep
stage annotated by sleep experts, in what is called a hypnogram, was then compared between the
sets. The aim is to assess the performance of such an algorithm and to have a routine capable of seg-
menting the EEG channels independently. The second objective of this project has been to develop
a routine for clustering features extracted from segments found using the segmentation algorithm,
and compare the results for healthy patients and hypersomniac patients. The quantity of clusters
was imposed and equal to 6. This number was chosen in the hope of having one cluster per sleep
stage and one for the artefacts. The clusters were determined using the training set and were then
used for classifying the segments extracted from the hypersomniac sets. The aim is to assess whether
new information can be extracted from this classiﬁcation and to confront the results found between
the sets. The overall purpose of this project is to create a fully data-driven approach that could
bring light on sleep architecture or on abnormal sleep patterns present in hypersomniac patients.

Abstract
This project proposed an automatic segmentation method for sleep signals. Linear prediction model
was used to perform this task. From those segments were extracted a set of features characterising
the PSG signals during those segments. The features extracted were then used in a clustering routine
which was carried out using the k-means algorithm with an imposed number of cluster equal to 6.
The clusters were trained using a healthy patients group. Classiﬁcation of segments found in patients
having a sleep disorder was then performed. Segments were attributed to the cluster they were the
most similar too, in the sense of a metric.
Three sets of unhealthy patient were considered: a
control set composed of hypersomniac patients not diagnosed with narcolepsy, and groups composed
of patients having either type 1 or type 2 narcolepsy. The results obtained carrying out these two
tasks were compared between four groups of patients.
While the clustering method did not yield signiﬁcant results, the segmentation method showed
diﬀerences in the distribution of boundaries between sleep stages across the four groups, mostly
between the type 1 and type 2 narcolepsy groups.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Narcolepsy and hypersomnias are conditions characterised by excessive sleepiness during the day.
The name "narcolepsy" encompasses two diseases (type 1 and type 2 narcolepsy). While their cause
and their biological manifestations are diﬀerent, they are both chronic, neurological sleep disorders.
Symptoms include abnormal rapid eye movement (REM) sleep and cataplexy for narcolepsy type 1,
in addition to excessive daytime sleepiness (EDS). Cataplexy is deﬁned as muscle weakness, typically
resulting from strong emotions. All those symptoms can be very disabling for a person suﬀering
from one of those conditions.
Narcolepsy is an under-diagnosed and often misdiagnosed disease [7]. The diagnosis of narcolepsy
is based on the study of Polysomnographic data (PSG) acquired from patient using several electrodes
recording electroencephalographic (EEG), electromyographic (EMG) and electroocculographic (EOG)
data during a full night of sleep. This, in combination with a Multiple Sleep Latency Test, measuring
the average sleep latency for daytime naps can lead to the diagnosis of the disease. Narcolepsy type
1 is thought to be the result of the loss of hypocretin neurons, and is diagnosed by measuring the
quantity of hypocretin in the cerebrospinal ﬂuid (CSF).
Because of the intrusive nature of hypocretin level determination technique, combined with the id-
iopathic nature of narcolepsy type 2, it can be desirable to investigate sleep architecture in new
ways.
1.2
Problem statement
The analysis of Polysomnographic (PSG) data recorded during sleep is performed manually by sleep
experts: they assign one of ﬁve sleep stages to 30 seconds epoch. Those are namely: wakefulness,
NREM1, NREM2, NREM3 and REM sleep.
The considerable and ﬁxed length of the epochs,
combined with subjectivity and inter-expert variability [8] in scorings are limiting factors for the
analysis of sleep. Furthermore, the sizeable length of epochs prevents from studying shorter events
that might happen during sleep. How could sleep stages be determined outside of those 30 second
limits? Can a fully data driven method be imagined for sleep classiﬁcation? This project aims in
developing an automatic adaptive segmentation method for sleep EEG and a subsequent clustering
of the automatically identiﬁed segments using a set of features indicative of common sleep patterns.
The segmentation routine will be applied to every EEG channel independently and results will be
compared across signals. In addition, the aim of the report is to discuss the existence of clusters for
diﬀerent sleep stages, to investigate their characteristics, and to compare these with the traditional
ﬁve vigilance state described by the American Academy of Sleep Medicine. The clustering approach
will be developed and trained based on a dataset of healthy subjects.
Afterwards, the method
will be applied to a data set of hypersomniacs (including narcoleptic patients type 1 and 2), for
which the identiﬁed segments will be attributed to the clusters they are most similar to. The main
and ﬁnal goal of this study is to search for a new understanding of sleep architecture in healthy
and pathological sleep, and investigate the diagnostic capability of such a method for hypersomnia,
including type 1 and type 2 narcolepsy.

1.3
Thesis objectives
1
INTRODUCTION
1.3
Thesis objectives
The background and problems raised by this thesis have led on the following objectives:
1.3.1
Literature study:
A literature study was ﬁrst carried out in order to investigate the state of the art, which involves both
technical and medical ﬁelds. The aim of this study was to obtain a better general understanding of
the problem, both in terms of data set comprehension and existing method determination. In fact,
the underlying causes of narcolepsy as well as the state of the art for brain signals (acquired through
electroencephalography) segmentation and clustering have been examined.
1.3.2
Segmentation:
A code implementing the segmentation method inspired from the literature was created in order to
ﬁrst apply it to the training, then to the test set. Initially, the segmentation was applied on one
EEG channel (c3-a2) then applied to the others. This step aims at ﬁnding segments from which
features can be extracted and used in a subsequent clustering routine. Each channel was segmented
independently.
The segmentation method relies upon linear prediction, which will be explained
in section 4.2. This method has been chosen because of its computation speed and its relatively
satisfying results. The data remains unﬁltered not to lose information embedded in artifacts.
1.3.3
Feature selection and extraction:
Features were extracted from the identiﬁed segment.
Ten features, inspired from the literature,
were selected for the clustering. These features include power spectrum, power ratios and mean
spectral frequencies, extracted from brain, eye or muscle signals (respectively acquired through
electroencephalography, electrooculography and electromyography).
1.3.4
Healthy patient clustering:
A code capable of clustering the segments found in the training set using the previously selected
features was written. This step aims at training the clustering method and ﬁnd characteristics for
the clusters in order to classify segments from the test set. The number of cluster is imposed and
equal to 6, since the expectation is to have one cluster per sleep stage and one for the artifacts.
1.3.5
Narcolepsy patient study:
Finally, the segmentation and clustering method were applied to the hypersomniac patients set (test
set) and the results were compared to the ones of the Healthy control set (training set).
Each
segment found in the test set were classiﬁed as follows: the segment was assigned to the cluster it
is the most similar to, using a similarity measure detailed in section 7. A comparison between the
test and training set and a conclusion was then formulated.

2
CLINICAL BACKGROUND
2
Clinical Background
This chapter aims at providing a background knowledge of the clinical ﬁeld. First, electroencephalog-
raphy (EEG) is presented, both in its acquisition methods and its outlook. The second section
focuses on presenting sleep architecture trough the description of the diﬀerent sleep stages as well
as the methods used by experts when scoring sleep data, focusing on the standards enunciated by
the American Association for Sleep Medicine (AASM). Finally, the third section will focus on the
description of Narcolepsy.
2.1
Electroencephalography
Electroencephalography, or EEG, is the measure of the brain’s electrical activity and its subsequent
graphical representation [9]. It is done using electrodes placed on the scalp of the patient [10]. In
fact, electrical activity of the brain induces potential diﬀerences and extracellular currents which
can be recorded to obtain an EEG.
The brain is divided into three main parts: the cerebellum, the cerebrum and the brain stem.
The latter include the mesencephalon, or midbrain, the pons medulla and the medulla oblongata.
The activity in the outer part of the cerebrum, the cerebral cortex, dominates EEG signals. The
electrodes, placed on the scalp, measure an average of the brain activity that takes place underneath.
The clinical applications of EEG for neurology are multiple and widely used. Examples of these
applications are:
• Diagnostic tool for brain disorders
• Scoring sleep
• Identifying sleep and neurological disorders
• Recording of event-related potentials
• Brain computer interface
2.1.1
Classiﬁcation of EEG
Human brain wave patterns have been characterised by Hans Berger [11], who was the ﬁrst to record
human EEG in 1924. These patterns can be divided into four categories: α, β, θ and δ waves. α
waves include frequencies in the band 8-13 Hz and are the principal waves seen during relaxed and
wakeful adults [9]. Since visual stimulus reduces α wave activity, they are mainly observed when
the eyes are closed. β waves include frequencies above 13 Hz and mainly appear as a background
activity for anxious and tense adults having open eyes. As for θ and δ waves, which have a frequency
band of 4-8Hz and 0.5-4 Hz respectively, they characterise the low frequencies of the brain activity.
They are present only during sleep in healthy adults [9]. The shape of these diﬀerent activities can
be seen ﬁgure 2.1.

EEG patterns depend greatly on the subject, since multiple factors can have an inﬂuence.
For
instance, the placement of the reference electrode, the consciousness and awareness state of the
subject as well as its age can inﬂuence those patterns. α activity is most prominent in the occipital
lobe, which is located in the posterior part of the cerebral cortex. This part of the brain contains
the visual cortex, which explains why this rhythm is reduced by visual stimuli. In addition, the α
activity is suppressed in case of mental activity. As for the β rhythm, it is mainly observable in
the frontal channels and is generally symmetrically distributed on both sides of the cortex. The θ
activity is more pronounced in children and appears at the beginning of sleep stages for adults [9]
[13]. Finally, the δ rhythm is mainly distributed frontally and occur during deep sleep for healthy
adults. For children, this activity is much more pronounced and is mainly located in the posterior
part of the brain [14]. These diﬀerences between children and adults are explained by a change in
normal EEG pattern during maturing.
2.1.2
Acquisition of EEG
EEG recordings are done using electrodes placed on the scalp of the subject.
These electrodes
conduct electrical signals with a consistent and low-impedance connection to the scalp. In order to
achieve this low-impedance connection, the scalp has to be carefully cleaned and mildly scratched,
which lower the impedance caused by dirt, oil and dead skin [15]. Additionally, electroconductive
gel is spread between the scalp and the electrode, which lowers the impedance even more and assure
the quality of the electrical connection: the chloride ions in the gel ensure good ion exchange with
the electrodes often made of gold, silver or silver oxidized with chloride.
The electrodes are usually attached using a strip of gauze containing collodion (nitrocellulose, diethyl
ether and ethanol), which insures the quality, solidity and long-lasting ability of the connection [15].
In fact, the collodion becomes a cement-like structure when it dries oﬀ. Another solution is to attach

Positioning the elctrodes is performed following the International 10-20 System of Electrode Place-

10% or 20% of the landmark distance. Those distances are namely the nasion-inion distance and the
distance between the point anterior to one of the tragus of the ear to the tragus of the opposite ear.
As it can be observed ﬁgure 2.2, the frontal electrode is placed along the nasion-inion distance on the
sagittal plane. Its distance from the nasion represents 10% of the nasion-anion distance. The next
electrode is placed on this line is placed at an additional 20% of the total distance from the ﬁrst one,
and so forth. Therefore, ﬁve electrodes are placed on this line, and the posterior one is placed 10% of
the total distance above the inion. On this line, from front to back, the name of the electrodes are:
frontal pole Fp, frontal Fz, central Cz, parietal Pz and occipital Oz. The same procedure is used

those are called central. The left and right position of electrodes are distinguished by assigning an
odd number to the electrodes on the left, and an even number to the electrodes on the right. A
higher number indicate a more temporal position. Additionally, the electrodes on the sagittal plane
have a "z" index instead of a number [15].

2.2
Sleep Architecture
EEG recordings made researches on sleep possible, since it made the diﬀerent states of brain activity
during sleep identiﬁable. Historically, William Dement and Nathaniel Kleitman discovered Rapid
Eye Movement (REM) sleep in 1953. They proceeded to classify sleep between REM sleep and non
REM (NREM) sleep. Additionally, they observed that, in adults, NREM sleep periods lasted longer
than REM sleep. They proceeded by classifying NREM sleep periods into four distinguishable stages
[17], which was scored using the distinguishable patterns obtained from EEG. Finally, they observed
that for a healthy adult, four or ﬁve sleep cycles are present during a normal night of sleep. All of
these elements helped creating the concept of sleep architecture. Allan Rechtschaﬀen and Anthony
Kales further speciﬁed this concept when, in 1968, they directed a publication focusing on manual
sleep scoring [18]. The manual that derived from this publication aimed at creating a standard
for visual scoring of undisturbed sleep in healthy adults, using quantitative analysis methods. The
graph resulting of one’s night scoring is called a hypnogram and represent a macroscopic description
of the distribution of sleep stages for a night of sleep. The abscissa represents time whereas the
ordinate indicates the sleep stage [17] [18]. An example of a hypnogram can be seen ﬁgure 2.3.

2.2.1
American Academy of Sleep Medicine Standard
The American Academy of Sleep Medicine, or AASM, formulated in 2004 a revision of the rules
for sleep scoring. Those new rules are gathered in "The AASM manual for Scoring of Sleep and
Associated Events". In addition to sleep staging, the manual describes certain events, where some are
to be scored and some are described to guide the sleep scoring. Those events include sleep spindles,
K complexes, arousals, respiratory events, cardiac abnormalities and sleep related movements. In
addition to EEG signals, scoring methods rely on the recording of the movement of the eyes, called
electrooculography (EOG), and the recording of the chin muscle tone, called chin electromyography
(EMG). The simultaneous recording of those biomedical signals is called a polysomnography (PSG)
and are the basic requirement for studying biophysiological changes occurring during sleep. The
PSG typically also include additional signals such as the EMG of the legs and arms, the respiratory
airﬂow, the peripheral pulse oximetry, the abdominal and thorax breathing patterns and the rhythm
of the heart through the recording of the electrocardiogram (ECG). The additional signal can help
in the scoring of some of the before-mentioned sleep associated events [14].
The AASM manual speciﬁes well deﬁned rules for visual scoring, as well as technical and digital
speciﬁcation (ﬁlter settings, impedance, sampling rate). For instance, the sampling frequency should
be above 200 Hz for EEG, the EEG high frequency ﬁlter should be set at 35 Hz and the low frequency
ﬁlter at 0.3 Hz. In the AASM standard, sleep scoring is done using 30 seconds epochs, which have
to be assigned a stage which can be: W, REM, NREM1, NREM2 or NREM3, which include the

Stage W:
During wake, α and β waves are observable. As it has been mentioned in section 2.1.1,
α activity is mostly present when the subject goes into a relaxing state and has his or her eyes closed,
which generally occurs before falling asleep. The rules of scoring W takes into account those two
diﬀerent patterns and rely either on the presence of eye movement or on the presence of α activity:
if α activity is present in less than half on an epoch, eye blinks, reading eye movements or rapid
eye movements (observable in the EOG) with high chin EMG should be used to score W. To ﬁnd
α activity, the focus is on the occipital region recordings of the EEG. Sleep onset is deﬁned by the
AASM manual as the ﬁrst epoch that is not the W stage.
Stage REM:
As the name suggests, rapid eye movement can be observed in the EOG channels
during the REM stage. They are characterised by irregular and fast eye movements. The subsequent
signals being quite large, it can have a manifestation in the EEG channels as well. However, this
stage is not only deﬁned by the rapid eye movement, but also requires the presence of Low-amplitude
mixed-frequency, or LAMF, in the EEG, as well as a low chin tone in the EMG. The AASM standard
dictates that there is two part in the REM stage. The "phasic" part occurs when there is rapid eye
movement and muscle twitches, whereas the "tonic" part occurs when low chine tone and LAMF are
detectable but without rapid eye movements. When an epoch is ﬁrst scored as REM, the following
epochs are also scored as REM as long as there is no transition to one of the other sleep stages,
which could be an increase of the chin EMG, an arousal, a K complex or sleep spindle in the ﬁrst
half of an epoch or a major body movement followed by LAMF and slow eye movements.
Stage NREM1:
NREM1 stage usually follows wakefulness, and can be described a light sleeping
stage. During transition from wake to sleep, some features of NREM1 can be observed during W
stage as well. According to the AASM manual, the patterns and features to be relied upon to score
NREM1 are multiple and include: slow eye movement, Vertex Sharp Waves (V waves) and Low
Amplitude Mixed Frequency (LAMF) activity. The latter occurs during most of NREM1, NREM2

and REM stages and is a background EEG pattern not speciﬁc for one sleep stage. It is a mixed
waveform pattern present in all channels of the EEG with low amplitude and a 4-7Hz frequency
range [14]. Slow eye movement, which is characteristic of NREM1 stage, can be observed in the

depending on whether the patient show α activity or not. In fact, if the patient have α activity,
NREM1 is scored if LAMF is present in more than half of the epoch. If there is no α activity and
no slow eye movement, the presence of V waves are used for the scoring of NREM1.
Stage NREM2:
NREM2 stage takes up most of the time in human sleep. It is a genuine sleep
stage in healthy adults and is usually found between NREM1 and NREM3 sleep. NREM2 is indicated
by two characteristic micro-sleep events which are K-complexes and sleep spindles. K-complexes are
the opposite of V waves: where the latter have a frequency of 2 Hz or higher and a duration of less
than half a second, K-complexes have a frequency of 2 Hz or lower and a duration of more than half a
second. Their shapes can be seen ﬁgure 6.3. In addition, K-complexes are mostly distinguishable in
the frontal channels whereas V waves are mostly present in the central ones. The second micro-sleep
events, sleep spindles, are a sinusoidal waveform lasting at least half a second in the frequency range
11-16 Hz. Their duration is usually between 1 and 2 seconds, maximum 3 seconds. The envelope
of the waveform of sleep spindles is progressively increasing, then gradually decreasing, as it can
be seen ﬁgure 6.3. NREM2 stage is ﬁrst scored if K complex or a sleep spindle is observed in the
ﬁrst half of an epoch. Then, NREM2 is scored as long as there is no transition to W, NREM1 or
NREM3, or an arousal or major body movement followed by slow eye movement occurs [14].
Stage NREM3:
NREM3 stage can be described as the deep sleep stage, and can be characterised
using one feature only: the slow wave activity, which is the activity having a frequency of 0.5-2Hz
and an amplitude of at least 75 µV. In fact, an epoch is scored as NREM3 if more than 20% of
it has slow wave activity. The waveform of slow wave activity varies, but is never as sharp as a
K complexes. Sleep spindles can also occur during NREM3. They are however usually harder to
recognise since they are superimposed on the slow wave activity.

2.3
Sleep disorders
Sleep disorders include of wide number of conditions which have in common that one’s sleep is af-
fected and abnormal. A multitude of neurological, psychological or medical diseases can lead to sleep
disorders, such as insomnia, narcolepsy or idiopathic hypersomnia. Narcolepsy and idiopathic hy-
persomnia are often associated since both are deﬁned by an excessive daytime sleepiness. Narcolepsy
can be with or without cataplexy, while idiopathic hypersomnia is always without cataplexy.
2.4
Narcolepsy
The name narcolepsy is derived from the Greek words for stupor and seizure (respectively narke
and lepsis). It was ﬁrst described in 1877 as excessive somnolence associated with short periods
of muscle weakness resulting from strong emotions [20]. This description most likely come from
observing patients with narcolepsy type 1 with cataplexy. Then, the term "narcoleptic tetrad" was
used is 1957 to describe the combination of sleep attacks, cataplexy, sleep paralysis and hallucinations
[21]. 70 to 80% of narcolepsy diagnosed patients experienced an onset of the disease before the age
of 25 [21]. The disease is estimated to concern 0.03% to 0.16% of the population (depending on
the ethnicity) worldwide but is considered under-diagnosed [22]. Narcolepsy has a high impact on
the life of the subject having the disease, as it is a chronic sleep disorder. To this day, there is no
cure for this disease. Narcolepsy can be divided into two sub-types: narcolepsy type 1 (NT1) and
narcolepsy type 2 (NT2). While NT1 is characterised by a low level of hypocretin in the CSF and
can be with or without cataplexy, NT2 is diagnosed when hypocretin levels are normal and cataplexy
is not present. These two types present similar symptoms, but they are however considered as two
quite diﬀerent diseases, caused by diﬀerent mechanisms in the body.
2.4.1
Causes
The complete mechanisms causing narcolepsy is not known. However, the increased familial predis-
position suggests that there is both genetic and non-genetic factors at play for narcolepsy [23].
It is now thought that narcolepsy type 1 patients lose hypocretin neurons due to an autoimmune
reaction [20]. Hypocretin is a neuropeptide, a small molecules that has a structure similar to the
one of a protein and that is used by neurons to communicate between one another. It is involved in
the sleep/wake and REM/NREM controlling mechanism and the loss of hypocretin result in both
unstable wakefulness and sleep.
2.4.2
Symptoms
Narcolepsy is characterised by a fragmentation of nocturnal sleep and an instability in sleep-wake
regulation. An overview of the symptoms can be seen table 2.2. They will be further explained in
this section.
The ﬁrst symptom mentioned when talking about narcolepsy is often the excessive daytime sleepi-
ness, or EDS. It is deﬁned by frequent sleep attacks during daytime. Those occur mainly during
monotonous situations, but can also occur during situations that require alertness, such as driving.
They are irresistible for the person concerned, meaning that this person cannot avoid falling asleep

2.4.3
Diagnosis
The main tools for diagnosing narcolepsy disorders are the PSG and the Multiple Sleep Latency
Test, or MSLT. MSLT is an average of the measure of the sleep latency for a daytime nap, i.e.the
time between the start of the nap to the ﬁrst stage of sleep detected. To diagnose narcolepsy, a short
REM sleep latency should be observed in the PSGs, and a maximum average of 8 minutes should
be recorded in the MSLTs. Another criteria is the presence of two SOREMPs during the MSLTs
[19]. If a short REM sleep latency (REM sleep occurring less than 15 minutes after sleep onset) is
observed in the PSG, this can count as one of the SOREMPS in the following MSLT.
Furthermore, a veriﬁcation method for the diagnosis consists in checking for low levels of hypocretin
in the Cerebrospinal ﬂuid (CSF), i.e.values of hypocretin lower than 110 pg/ml [27]. This criteria
characterises narcolepsy type 1, where narcolepsy type 2 show normal hypocretin values. If low
levels of hypocretin are detected, the diagnosis of narcolepsy type one is certain; with or with a
diagnostic PSG-MSLT [28].
It is believed that the two types of narcolepsy are two distinct diseases with diﬀerent causes, but
with overlapping symptoms. If we could ﬁnd markers in the PSG that would diﬀerentiate the two
diseases, it would not require a lumbar puncture to obtain the CSF-hypocretin level.

3.1
Segmentation
As a reminder, in order to classify sleep stages, sleep experts study 30 second epochs that they
classify between the ﬁve sleep stages. Because of this 30 second length, creating the problem of
having approximate boundaries between stages and not being able to classify micro-sleep events,
combined with the subjectivity of expert ratings, several authors have studied the possibility of an
automatic segmentation algorithm aiming in ﬁnding the boundaries between sleep stages. In the
following paragraphs, diﬀerent articles trying to address those issues will be described.
3.1.1
Automatic sleep classiﬁcation using adaptive segmentation reveals an increased
number of rapid eye movement sleep transitions [1]
In article [1], two diﬀerent adaptive segmentation methods are used for sleep classiﬁcation. Those
algorithms have been applied on channel c3-a2 only.
The ﬁrst one is inspired from Bodenstein & Praetorius’ paper [29] and consists of an auto-correlation
function model that ﬁnds segment boundaries based on two windows having a ﬁxed length. The
ﬁrst window is taken as a reference while the other one is sliding along the signal, starting from the
reference window’s position. A spectral error measure is computed between those windows and a
boundary is found when the spectral diﬀerence between the two windows become signiﬁcant enough,
ie when a threshold is reached. This diﬀerence is computed using integrals of power spectrum, auto
correlation functions and thresholds and will not be detailed in this report. When a boundary is
detected, the reference window changes to take place at the beginning of the newly found segment
and the procedure starts again.
The second segmentation algorithm is inspired by Appel’s paper [30] and is based on an auto-
regression (AR) model. It uses a growing window as a reference and a ﬁxed length sliding test
window. While they ﬁrst have the same length, the reference window grows and generalized likeli-
hood ratio tests A boundary is detected when a threshold is exceeded. Like the previously presented
method, if a boundary is detected, the reference window changes position and the procedure starts
again.
In this article [1], the boundaries identiﬁed in the c3-a2 channel are imposed on the other channels,
as it can be seen ﬁgure 3.1. Diﬀerent window length are tested from 3 to 30 seconds duration. A
reference window shorter than 3 second is considered by the authors as too short to give access to
reliable measures for a sleep stage.

3.1.2
Adaptive segmentation using wavelet transform [2]
Article [2] presents a method based on non-linear energy operators that combines amplitude and
frequency contents of the EEG. The resulting frequency measure is then used to ﬁnd boundaries
using one time window.
The window is divided into two equal parts around its central value.
When the diﬀerence between the energy operators of the left and right window is signiﬁcant enough,
i.e. when a threshold is exceeded, a boundary is found. This method is combined with a wavelet
transform in order to analyse diﬀerent frequency bands and remove possible problems. Wavelet
transform relies on decomposing a signal using a family of oscillating functions generated by dilation
and translation of a mother function called wavelet. A wavelet contracted twice will have a two
times smaller duration but a two times higher central frequency. This family of functions have an
amplitude initially equal to zero and have a compact support, which permits time resolution. In
fact both time and scale are taken into account by such a decomposition, in opposition to Fourier
transform. Since the frequencies of the analysed signal are found by dilating or contracting the
wavelet by a factor of two, the time-frequency plane does not have a uniformly divided spectrogram.
It is in fact decreasing in time width and increasing in frequency height with increasing frequencies.

The fractal dimension of both sub-windows are

values between the sub-windows. If a threshold is reached, a boundary is then detected.
3.1.4
Sleep EEG Automatic Analysis [4]
In article [4], multiple segmentation methods are proposed.
This report will only focus on the
segmentation based on linear prediction. This method is also based on two time windows: a reference
window and a ﬁxed duration sliding window. The coeﬃcient of a linear prediction ﬁlter are computed
for the reference window and the sliding window is then estimated. The standard deviation of the
prediction errors for each point of the sliding window is then computed. The prediction error is
the diﬀerence between an estimated value and the actual value. If a threshold is exceeded by this
standard deviation, a boundary is found and the reference window changes position to be at the
beginning of the newly found segment and the procedure starts again. The algorithm ends when
all the data has been read. The reference window can have a ﬁxed or a growing length. The latter
presents the advantage of deﬁning a segment in a more and more precise way at each iteration for
which a boundary is not found, since the coeﬃcient estimation uses a growing number of values
into account. However, a ﬁxed window requires less computational power, since the linear predictor
coeﬃcients have to be computed only once per segment.

4.3.1
Algorithm
The most popular way of implementing k-means clustering is Lloyd’s algorithm, which is the pro-
cedure used by the Matlab function kmeans. It uses a reﬁnement technique based on alternating
between two steps: the assignment step and the update step. It consists on repeatedly ﬁnding the
centroid of each set of observations, then re-partitioning the input according to the closest centroid.
During the assignment step, each observation is assigned to the cluster whose mean has the smallest
distance. The measure of the distance is done using a function called a metric. Diﬀerent metrics will
be presented section 4.3.3 .The squared Euclidean distance metric, shown equation 18, is the most
generally used metric when applying k-means. Then, during the update step, the new means, i.e.
the new centroids, of the observations of the new clusters is calculated. The two steps are repeated
until the algorithm converges, which is when the assignments carried out during the assignment
stage do not change. The algorithm does not guarantee an optimum [35].

cluster center is chosen randomly with a uniform probability across the data points. The remaining

Kruskal Wallis test
The Kruskal Wallis test by ranks, also called one-way Analysis Of Variance (ANOVA) on ranks is
a statistical method for testing whether samples originate from the same distribution [37] [38]. It
can be used on several independent samples having same or diﬀerent sizes. It is a non-parametric
method, which means that the distribution of the values inside a sample is either unknown or that
the sample is distribution-free. Because of this nature, this test does not assume a normal distri-
bution for the residuals, in opposition the one-way ANOVA. Those can be deﬁned as the diﬀerence
between an observation and its predicted value.

6
Segmentation
The classiﬁcation of sleep stages done by experts using the PSG can be time consuming and present
the problem of the considerable 30 second length of the studied epochs. Because of this length,
micro-sleep events are not detected in an hypnogram and ask for a further analysis. For these rea-
sons, it is desirable to ﬁnd a fully data-driven segmentation algorithm.
In this section, the development of the segmentation algorithm is explained. As it has been presented
in the introduction, one of the goal of this project is to develop an automatic adaptive segmentation
method for sleep PSG that can be applied on each channel independently. First, the preparation
of the dataset will be presented, then a segmentation method based on linear prediction will be
introduced. Hereafter, an explanation for the choice of the parameter values is provided, and lastly
a validation method for the choice of those parameter values will be presented. These explanation
will be preceded by an overview of the Matlab implementation.
It is worth noticing that the data remains unﬁltered in order to keep all information embedded in the
signal intact. If artefacts are presents in the signal , the hope is for them to form a distinct cluster
when the clustering routine is applied. This routine will be explained in the clustering section 7.
The segmentation method is chosen to use only one EEG channel, for a possible analysis of the
diﬀerent channels independently. A comparison between the results obtained for the diﬀerent channel
could be of interest in future studies.
6.1
Preparing the data
The ﬁrst step is to extract the dataset in Matlab. The PSG data as well as the hypnograms are
extracted for each patient of each set. PSG data was recorded as a signal with a sampling frequency
of 256 Hz, while the hypnogram is recorded by giving the position of the 30 second epoch corre-
sponding to a stage transition, and a number corresponding to the sleep stage starting after this
transition: -3 for N3, -2 for N2, -1 for N1, 0 for REM and 1 for W. This type of hypnogram can
be seen ﬁgure 6.2. Using the "lights on" and "lights oﬀ" ﬁle, the interesting part of the PSG is
extracted, which corresponds to the sleep period of the subject (from when he decided to go sleep
until the moment he woke up).

6.3
Choice of parameter values
There are multiple parameters that can be adjusted in this linear prediction segmentation method.
They are namely the length of the window, the order of the linear prediction, the step of the sliding
window and the threshold value used in the segmentation method.
As it has been explained in section 3.1.1, a reference window shorter than 3 seconds would be too
short to give access to reliable measures for a sleep stage. In fact, for the clustering routine that will
be applied on the found segments, some features characterising the segments have to be extracted.
Computing those require an acceptable number of observations, and while some sleep events might
be shorter than 3 seconds, the 256 Hz sampling frequency might make feature extraction on shorter
segments inaccurate. Additionally, studying a window of 30 seconds could be interesting as it is the
length of the epochs studied by the experts. Therefore, three window length have been investigated:
3, 10 and 30 seconds. The 10 second window length is chosen because it is an intermediary value
between 3 and 30 seconds.
The order p of the linear prediction determines the number of values that are being used to predict
a new value. In the approach chosen for this project, the ﬁrst p values of the reference window are
used to estimate the linear prediction model coeﬃcient.
For a ﬁxed window length, having a higher order means having a more precise linear prediction model
but might increase the computation time compared to a low order model. If a growing reference
window is chosen, the order p should be equal to the full length of the window (and therefore change
at each iteration).This is a necessity for a having a more and more precise model for each iteration
leading to a growth of the window. If a constant order p value was used, and if the last p values of
the window would be used, rather than having a model with an increasing precision, it would vary
at each iteration and characterise the last p observations of the window only. An issue could then
arise if the segment changes slowly its features. In fact, even if the characteristics of a segment have
drastically changed since the last found boundary, a slow change in characteristics would not lead to
an exceeding of the threshold value by the standard error. If the order p was chosen to be constant,
this problem could be removed by taking the p ﬁrst value of the window instead of the last. This
would however dismiss the interest of a growing window and would be equivalent to a ﬁxed window
length.
Then, the step of the sliding window determines the number of values from which the sliding window
is translated to the right at each iteration for which the threshold value is not exceeded. A smaller
step presents the advantage that the algorithm will review more data but increases the computation
time. Therefore, a trade-oﬀbetween computational speed and quantity of reviewed points has to be
found.
Thirdly, the threshold value determines, among other results, the quantity of boundaries that are
detected by the algorithm. In fact, a smaller value will lead to more boundaries detected. In order

7
Clustering Method
In the hope of characterising sleep stages and ﬁnding characteristic for sleep disorders, it can be
of interest to carry out a clustering method on PSG extracted segment. This is performed in the
hope of ﬁnding new indicators for sleep stages and abnormal sleep patterns, as well as challenging
the traditional ﬁve stage model presented by the American Academy of Sleep Medicine. Plus, a
fully data-driven and performing method could, in the future, help save time for diagnosis of sleep
abnormalities.
For this project, k-means clustering algorithm was applied. The number of cluster searched was
set to 6 with the expectation of having one cluster per sleep stage and one for the artifacts. The
clustering method was trained using features extracted from the segments found in the training set,
using the previously presented segmentation algorithm. Only the EEG channel c3-a2 was considered
for the segmentation in this study. The segment boundaries found on this channel were imposed
on the EOG and EMG for feature extraction. Then, the segment found for the patients taken from
the three test sets (Narcolepsy type 1, Narcolepsy type 2 and Control sets) were classiﬁed using
the clusters found thanks to the training set: each segment was attributed to the cluster it was the
closest too, in the sense of a metric. Finally, proportions of stage distribution between clusters were
extracted and studied.

7.1.1
Power spectrum
A typical method to analyse EEG data is to decompose EEG signals into distinct frequency bands
corresponding to the diﬀerent type of EEG activities presented in section 2.1.1 : δ (0.5–4 Hz), θ

==PAGE:46==

(4–8 Hz), α (8–12 Hz) and β (12–30 Hz). This is done by decomposing EEG signals into their fre-
quency components through a Fourier transform. For a discrete signal, the Fast Fourier Transform
(FFT) algorithm is generally used. For each frequency bin, the FFT returns a complex number that
characterise the amplitude and the phase of a signal for a distinct frequency. The Power Spectral
Density (PSD) is then assessed by computing the magnitude-squared of the FFT. The unit of the
PSD is µV 2/Hz for an EEG.
A wide variety of analyses can be performed using the PSD. The focus in this project is put on
the average band power. Its main advantage is that it consists in single number summarising the
contribution of a frequency band to the overall signal power. This trait make it favourable for a
clustering approach, where key features have to be extracted from data.
The average power could be a relevant feature to extract for sleep study since it could allow a
diﬀerentiation between sleep stages. In fact, diﬀerent stages present diﬀerent frequency band activity.
For instance deeper sleep stages present a predominance in slow wave activity (δ band), while wake
activity presents higher frequencies and low levels of δ activity. As a result, the average power in
the δ band would be high for deep sleep and smaller during wakefulness.
With the same reasoning, it can easily be understood why the average power band of the EMG
can be of interest. If we compare, for instance, the W stage and the REM stage, a diﬀerentiation
characteristic is that the ﬁrst is associated with a high chin EMG while the latter is generally
accompanied by a low chin EMG.
7.1.2
Power ratios
For the same purpose, the average power ratios between two band power could be of interest, since
they characterise one frequency bands activity compared to another. By dividing the average power
ratio of a frequency band by the average power in the 0-30Hz band (which include δ, θ, α and β
activities), the importance of this frequency band on the overall power of all sleep frequency bands
can be assessed. Therefore, this measure gives extra information compared to average power ratios
alone, since they can be used to compare one frequency bands activity over the others. When used
on the EOG channels, the power ratio of the 0-4 Hz band on the 0-30 Hz frequency band could help
identify NREM1, during which slow eye movement occur. Since two EOG channels are recorded for
the PSG (one per eye), the average value of the power ratio should be considered in this study.
7.1.3
Spectral frequencies
Among spectral frequency measures the mean frequency can be interesting. Once again this comes
from the fact that diﬀerent sleep stages have diﬀerent characteristics, and therefore diﬀerent EEG
mean frequency. In addition, the high chin muscle tone that occurs during some stage (like wake)
might lead to a higher mean frequency in the EMG compared to a stage having a lower chin muscle
tone (like REM). Artifacts coming from movement of the subjects might also present higher mean
frequency than any sleep stage.

Before applying the clustering algorithm on the feature vector , a normalisation of the features is
performed. This step presents the advantage of putting all the features in the same numerical range
and therefore prevents features having greater numerical range to dominate the smaller ones when
the clustering method is applied. In the case of k-means clustering, as it has been presented in
section 4.3, the optimisation problem uses a metric ∥.∥that give the same weights to every feature

inﬂuence on the minimisation problem. In conclusion, normalising the features is necessary for the

has been performed in this thesis.

The size of the data set, composed of 100 patients spread in four groups, prompts the use of a simple
and fast clustering method.
In addition, the number of cluster is ﬁxed before applying the algorithm and equal to 6. This choice

Because of those two restrictions, k-means clustering was considered for this project. Another pos-
sibility would have been using hierarchical clustering and should be considered for future researches.
The initialisation of the method is done using the k-means++ algorithm presented in section 4.3.2.
Several metrics have been considered for the k-means algorithm. They are presented in section 4.3.3.
They are namely: squared Euclidean, city-block, correlation and cosine metrics. The choice of the
metric has to be done by comparing the results obtained for each of them. In fact, it is desirable
to have clusters characterising the most as possible a sleep stage. To achieve this, the segments
found in a cluster should belong to one - or two - sleep stage in the hypnogram. This assessment is
done visually, by comparing the distribution of sleep stages within clusters. This is done using the
computation of proportions that will be detailed section 7.4.

calculated using the same metrics as the one used for training the clusters.
7.4
Proportions
After applying the classiﬁcation method, each segment found by the segmentation algorithm has
been assigned to a cluster. In addition, these segments can have delimitation corresponding to mul-
tiple sleep stages of the expert hypnogram. In order to have one hypnogram sleep stage per segment,
the segments being, even partly, in a 30 second window around an hypnogram stage transition are
ignored. Theoretically, this can be explained by the fact that when producing the hypnogram for
a subject, the epochs studied by sleep experts have a duration of 30 seconds. Because of this, the
position of a boundary between two stages is always chosen to be at the end or at the beginning of
a 30 second window, which makes it impossible to identify when the stage transition actually takes
place. This can lead to a shift between the start time of a stage in the hypnogram and the actual
time of the shift in the brain of the subject. The AASM manual usually states that characteristics
of a new stage should be detected in the ﬁrst 15 seconds of the 30 second epoch. This narrows down
the uncertainty window and this is why only a 15 second duration on both sides of stage transitions
in the hypnogram should be ignored. This transformation leads to "clean" stages, i.e. stages that
do not include segments corresponding to transition between stages.
Once this operation is done, it is possible to compute which sleep stage is represented in each cluster.
In other words, for one cluster, one can assess the distribution of the segments, and their duration,
between the sleep stages of the hypnogram. In this project, each segment belonging to the studied
cluster is assigned to the corresponding phase in the hypnogram and is given a weight proportional
to its duration. the proportion of duration of each sleep stage is computed for this cluster. The
weight system enables this study to be independent on the number of segments detected in a stage.
It will rather represent the distribution of the duration of each sleep stage within a cluster. Then, if
this number is divided by the duration of each sleep stage in the "clean" hypnogram, it will represent
the proportion of each cluster per second of sleep stage.

8.1.1
Choice of the parameter values
The ﬁrst choice to make is the width of the reference window, and whether it should have a ﬁxed
or a growing length. The order of the growing window increasing at every iteration for which the
threshold value is not exceeded leads to a computation time which is excessive. In fact, for one
patient and one channel, it took more than 24 hours of running time to ﬁnd the segment boundaries.
The length of the window is therefore chosen ﬁxed.
In order to keep the computation time at a reasonable level, the threshold values being tested are
taken between 0.05 and 1 with a 0.1 step. The lower limit 0.05 has been chosen in order not to
detect too many boundaries, which would lead to having a lot of short segments. A large quantity
of short segments is not desirable because the computation of the features (described in section 7)
would lack precision, because of a poor number of observations considered to estimate the feature
values. As for the upper limit, the average quantity of boundaries detected in the training set is
chosen to be at least 4 times higher than the quantity of stage boundaries in the hypnogram. For a
threshold equal to 1, 439 boundaries are detected on average by the algorithm in the training set.
In comparison, there is on average 123 stage boundaries annotated in the hypnogram. This choice is
made because of the high quantity of boundaries detected during wake stages. In addition, having
a higher number of boundaries increases the possibility of having a boundary on an actual stage
transition in the EEG. Plus, a high quantity of boundaries is not an issue: several segments found
in the same sleep stage will have similar characteristics, and will therefore be classiﬁed in the same
cluster when the clustering routine is applied. The results can be seen in ﬁgure 8.1. The variability
of the cross correlation value, shown by the error bars in the ﬁgure, is high across patients. The
threshold value chosen for a 3 second reference window is equal to 0.21. It corresponds to 2707±

The results show a poor diﬀerentiation in clusters between sleep stages, for each metrics. In fact,

issue with 5 out of 6 clusters which contain mostly N1 and W. City block has this issue in a lesser
degree, but two of its cluster contain all sleep stages, which is not optimal for stage diﬀerentiation.
Therefore, the squared Euclidean metric is chosen to carry out the clustering and the classiﬁcation.
While W and N1 are represented in each cluster, with 3 clusters containing those two stages (C2,
C3 and C4), it present the advantage of having one cluster containing mostly REM sleep (C1) and
one cluster containing a majority of W (C4). In addition, N3 is only present in cluster C6 and N2
almost exclusively in cluster C5 and C6. The over representation of N1 in some clusters could be ex-
plained by its small duration during sleep. Therefore, if some segments within a cluster are detected
in N1, it might represent a big proportion of the cluster if the values are divided by the stage duration.

9
DISCUSSION
9
Discussion
While carrying out this thesis, some questions and discussion about the used techniques and the
found results have arisen. This section will discuss various aspects of the project, regarding both the
applied segmentation and clustering routines as well as the clinical implication of the found results.
The ﬁrst section will discuss further research possibilities as well as possible improvements for the
algorithms. The second section will focus on whether such a technique could have a diagnostic value
for hypersomnia, including type 1 and type 2 narcolepsy.
9.1
Issues and possible improvements for the algorithms
9.1.1
Segmentation
For the choice of a ﬁxed or growing reference window length for the linear prediction method, other
procedures could be imagined. The choice of linear prediction order could be made by ﬁrst ﬁnding
an acceptable length for the ﬁxed window regarding computation time. This length could then be
used for the growing window length, to which a fraction of the total window length could be added.
The ﬁrst p values of the reference window are then used to compute the linear prediction coeﬃcient
rather than the last, because of the aforementioned issues. The determination of this fraction would
then have to be determined, regarding once again the computation time.
In this project, the segmentation was performed using a linear prediction model. Other routines
could be considered in order to carry out this task. As it was presented in the state of the art section
3, the fractal dimension of the signal, which values are characteristic of the complexity of a pattern
or the quantity of information contained by that pattern, could be used for future research. It was
not considered here because of the high computation time that ensue fractal dimensions calculation.
Other possible methods include using the Auto Correlation Function (ACF) or an Auto-Regression
Model (AF). This was done in paper [1]. A comparison of the performance of those algorithm to
the one presented in this thesis should be considered in future research.
Then, the choice of the optimal threshold value for the linear prediction segmentation algorithm
was performed by computing the cross correlation for a lag τ = 0 between the boundary quantity
in a moving window graphs of the hypnogram and of the segmentation method, after removing the
boundaries found in the wake stage. This lead to plots such as the one presented ﬁgure 8.1. The
choice of the optimal threshold value was then decided visually, trying to optimise the two criteria
of having a high cross correlation value and a relatively high number of boundaries detected com-
pared to the number of stage transitions in the hypnogram. The accuracy of this visual method
can be debated, creating an automatic characterisation of the optimal threshold value should be
considered. One could imagine a method consisting in requiring at least X times more boundaries
detected during the sleep stages of the hypnogram by the algorithm compared to the number of
stage transitions of the hypnogram. The wake stages should, once again, not be considered since
a considerable number of boundaries are detected during this stage. Once this criteria has been
fulﬁlled, the threshold value leading to the highest value in the cross correlation could be taken and
used for the clustering analysis.

In addition, the considerable computation time for such a graph to be obtained did not allow to test
a wide range of threshold values: only values in the 0.05-1 range were considered, with a step of 0.1.
In the future, more threshold values should be considered.
Finally, the threshold value determination method is based on comparing the boundaries found by
the algorithm to the ones annotated by the experts in the hypnogram (through the use of the bound-
ary quantity in a 30 minutes window graph). This method can be criticised for relying on expert
data. In fact, as it has been presented in the introduction, the 30 seconds length of the annotated
epochs combined with the inter-expert variability does not make the hypnogram the most reliable
source of information for sleep stage distribution. However, it is to this day the only information
one can get on the sleep stage distribution of an individual. In the future, new techniques might
enable a fully data-driven approach.
The inter channel variability of the segmentation method should be the focus of a further researches,
since it could hold precious information. In fact, sleep stages transition might occur at diﬀerent time
in diﬀerent region, and this could be aﬀected by the presence of disorders. Such hypothesis should
be investigated using automatic segmentation algorithms combined with an automatic scoring of
those found segments. A study consisting in comparing the graphs representing the quantity of
boundaries found in a 30 sliding minutes (detailed in the section 6.3) between several channels could
be considered. The cross correlation could be computed for these graph and the value corresponding
to the lag returning the highest correlation value could be extracted. This comparison between
channel could show a shift in the occurrence time of a sleep stage in diﬀerent areas of the brain. //
9.1.2
Clustering
The clustering routine was carried out in this thesis using k-means clustering with an imposed num-
ber of cluster equal to 6 and using 4 diﬀerent metrics. As it was said in section 7.2, hierarchical
clustering should be considered in future research to carry out the clustering task. It was applied
in this project, but the results were not satisfactory since one cluster was containing most segments
while the others were containing some segments having features with extreme values, whatever the
metric chosen. An adjustment of the parameters or removing the outliers (with the trade-oﬀof
losing some information) could remove this issue.
Then, in this project, the metrics used were the most widely used one. Perhaps using other less
conventional metrics could yield more satisfying results.
Finally, the choice of the number of cluster equal to 6 should be confronted to other cluster quanti-
ties. In fact, even though the 5 sleep stages presented by the American Academy for Sleep Medicine
is the golden standard today, it might be interesting to confront this model to models with higher
numbers of sleep stages, starting by searching for more than 5 stages when clustering is applied on
PSG segments. A higher number might also increase the cluster-sleep stage correspondence, since
more than one cluster might be needed for the W stage or for the artifacts.
If a correspondence between clusters and sleep stages can be established, future research could try
to compare the expert hypnogram to the hypnogram obtained through the clusters. In fact, if each
segment is attributed to a cluster, and if to each of these clusters is given a value corresponding

might be entirely removed by the "cleaning" procedure of the stage. This might explain its over
representation in the graphs, since the values found for several patients govern the results for the
average.

This project proposed an automatic segmentation method in order to ﬁnd segments in each EEG
channels independently.
While the diﬀerences between the segments found in diﬀerent channels
were not investigated, the average number of segments detected per second of sleep stage in the
four patient groups was investigated.
These groups being composed of healthy patients, hyper-
somniac patients not diagnosed with narcolepsy and patient having type 1 or type 2 Narcolepsy,
presented diﬀerences in the number of boundaries detected per second of sleep stage, except for
the N1 stage. These results are encouraging for separating patients having an hypersomnia condi-
tion from healthy patients. The correspondence between the hypnogram boundary density and the
density of boundaries found by the segmentation algorithm was relatively poor, as the low values
of cross correlation reﬂects. These results are nevertheless encouraging for the possibility of PSG
segmentation by an automatic segmentation procedure, and its diﬀerent applications. For instance,
applying the segmentation algorithm on patients having other sleep disorders could help establish
diﬀerences between those. In addition, it could be interesting to compare the results found in this
project to other segmentation algorithm, such as the ones presented in Koch et al’s paper [1] using
ACF or AR model.
The clustering routine applied on the previously found segment hardly separated sleep stages. The
high inter-patient variability, even within a group, makes the training of the cluster too approximate

CONCLUSION
10
Conclusion
The original aim for this thesis was to investigate the possibility of automatic segmentation and
clustering of sleep.
In the introduction, several objectives were set for this project. How these were fulﬁlled during the
thesis can be detailed as follows:
• Literature study: A thorough literature study have brought light on the clinical background
of this project, as well as on the existing methods for automatic segmentation and clustering
of sleep signal. It was discovered that this approach for sleep study is relatively novel and that
a lot of possibilities are still unexplored.
• Segmentation: The automatic segmentation task was carried out using a linear prediction
model applied on one EEG channel, which enables inter-channel study and presents the advan-
tage of being a data-driven approach, with the exception of the choice of the optimal threshold
value.
• Feature selection and extraction: Studying the state of the art has helped in the feature
selection. Those features, having distinct characteristics in each sleep stage, were chosen with
the ambition of separating sleep stages when the clustering method was applied. The features
were normalised to be within the same range of values [0;1], in order not to over represent one
compared to the others.
• Healthy patient clustering: After extracting features from the segments found using the
segmentation method on the c3-a2 channel, clusters have been trained using k-means clustering
with the squared euclidean metric. The number of clusters was ﬁxed and set to 6, with the hope
of ﬁnding one cluster per sleep stage and one for the artefacts that presents extreme values
for some features. The results were not very performing regarding sleep stage separation, as
multiple stages were detected within one cluster.
• Narcolepsy patient study: The segmentation and clustering routines have permitted the
study of hypersomniac patient sets, including sets of patients with type 1 or type 2 narcolepsy.
First the diﬀerence between the distribution of the quantity of boundary detected per second of
sleep stage was investigated, then the proportion of the duration of sleep stages within clusters
has been studied. Kruskal-Wallis and Wilcoxon test were performed for this study. While the
ﬁrst study revealed diﬀerences between some of the groups, the study of cluster distribution
proportion between stages was inconclusive.
Future research possibilities are numerous after carrying out this thesis. First, inter channel vari-
ability is possible since the segmentation algorithm only uses one EEG channel. This could permit
new understanding on sleep stage transitions during sleep, as well as their localisation. It could also
help for the study of sleep disorders. In fact, the hypothesis of having diﬀerent sleep stage transi-
tions in diﬀerent channels between healthy and unhealthy patients could be investigated. Then the
comparison between the performances of this segmentation algorithm to others could help create
an eﬃcient routine to ﬁnd segments during sleep. Then - even though the clustering method did
not performed so well in stages separation - it would be interesting to understand which problems
lead to these results: over representation of N1 stage in the clusters compared to its duration during
sleep or over representation of some patients within groups might be considered.
