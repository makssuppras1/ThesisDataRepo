Abstract
Global dependence on plastics has grown exponentially over the past 70 years since their inception. They
are durable, versatile, and cheap and easy to manufacture. Massive production volumes coupled with
waste mismanagement have created a global problem of plastic debris in the marine environment. Plastic
now pervades every corner of the ocean from the coasts to the open seas, to the deepest trenches and even
frozen sea ice.
This study investigates the parameters governing the biodegradation of plastic films in the marine
environment. Low-density polyethylene films were synthesized and exposed to varying degrees of UV
weathering. The UV weathering was performed using an ATLAS Suntest XLS+ accelerated weathering
chamber, with a cycle of dry and saltwater flooded conditions, to simulate tidal waters in the Skagerrak
Strait. Post weathering characterization, both physical and chemical, of the polyethylene films was
performed with a combination of scanning electron microscopy and infrared spectroscopy. The presence
of surface weaknesses along with the formation of oxidized functional groups suggests a high degree
of abiotic degradation compared to the pristine material. Biodegradation testing on the polyethylene
films was conducted using the closed bottle respirometer method following ISO 23977-2:2020 with
modifications based on OECD TG306F. The samples were incubated in the test setup for 28 days at 20
◦C in natural seawater collected from Kristineberg, Sweden as well as in bioaugmented seawater with the
addition of bacteria previously isolated from marine plastics.
Results demonstrate a microbial activity present in seawater sufficient to degrade a known biodegradable
positive control microcrystalline cellulose as well as a bioplastic reference material. Biodegradation
onset and maximum were increased in all test setups containing bioaugmented seawater. A low degree
of PE degradation (<1%) was observed during the 28 days independent of UV weathering. It appears
that preferential degradation of the low-density polyethylene’s amorphous phase fraction caused pristine
samples to show the greatest extent of biodegradation followed by increasingly weathered samples. This
study demonstrates the feasibility of a standardized setup for degradation testing to obtain insights into
the degradation of plastics in the marine environment.

Learning Objectives
As the culmination of the requirements for DTU’s M.Sc. Environmental Engineering the completion of
this project will require the application of many of the skills gained throughout. A selection of learning
goals based on these skills is listed below. These goals also act as a useful resource to refer to during the
project to monitor progress.
• Develop and adhere to a project workflow plan detailing deadlines and milestones.
• Describe the problem and the motivation for studying plastics in the marine environment.
• Research and summarize the current state of knowledge regarding plastic sources, sinks, environ-
mental fate, and eventual degradation.
• Coordinate with other researchers in the field regarding sample treatment and collection.
• Develop an experimental design to precisely and accurately test the biodegradability of weathered
plastic particles in the marine environment.
• Justify and document all decisions made throughout the experiment in a clear and explicit way to
ensure a reproducible method.
• Accurately set up and perform the designed experiment.
• Critically assess the experiment performed with a specific focus on inherent limitations of the study
and possible sources of error.
• Analyse and summarize the findings from the experiment in specific relation to the problem
formulation.
• Draw conclusions based on results and present them in a comprehensive manner.
• Consider further avenues for study as well as potential recommendations based on findings.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
Plastic products are omnipresent in today’s world. They are cheap to produce, easy to mould, and
lightweight (GESAMP, 2019). As both a blessing and a curse, plastics are also very long lasting.
Mismanagement of waste has created a growing problem of plastic pollution in natural environments.
Despite their strength as a manufacturing material, plastics do eventually break down and in the process
micro plastics are released (Andrady et al., 2009, Koelmans et al., 2022). As plastic particles break down
into gradually smaller and smaller pieces, their physical properties, mobility, and difficulty of remediation
all change. The persistence and pervasiveness of plastics present a serious and growing problem in the
marine environment (Andrady, 2011). Plastic pollution has now been found in every corner of the globe,
including all compartments of the ocean (Jambeck et al., 2015). Despite a growing global awareness of
the associated problems, as well as environmental issues in general, the production and improper disposal
of plastic show little sign of slowing down. The sources, sinks, and eventual fate of plastics in the marine
environment are a pressing issue in today’s world. There are a handful of reasons why it is crucial to
understand the degradation pathways, both biotic and abiotic, for plastics in the marine environment.
First off, to address the issue of plastic pollution in the ocean the translocation between compartments
an understanding of the environmental fate is required. Further, an improved accounting of the factors
affecting the ultimate fate of plastics can assist in a more detailed understanding of the scale and severity
of the problem. This in turn may be used to educate and inform upstream stakeholders, such as producers,
consumers, and waste disposal services.
Polyethylene is the most used plastic in the world with over 100 million tons produced annually, amounting
to 34% of the global plastic market (Geyer et al., 2017). Over 50% of all plastics are discarded, rather
than incinerated or recycled, and a large amount ends up in the ocean (Geyer, 2020). While polyethylene
is notoriously recalcitrant in natural environments, it is documented that biotic and abiotic degradation
pathways are capable of mineralizing some of the carbon contained within (Sen et al., 2015). The factors
affecting the ultimate fate of polyethylene are important to the problem of plastic pollution. Plastics
discarded to the sea, whether intentionally or accidentally, are subject to mechanical stresses from waves
and wind, UV and thermo oxidation from the sun, and colonization by microbial communities (Wayman

et al., 2021). The interactions between these pressures are poorly understood due to the many factors
affecting plastic degradation combined with the heterogeneity of environmental conditions in the world’s
oceans. This thesis addresses the theory behind these degradation pathways and tests how factors,
namely weathering and microbial communities, in the marine environment might affect the eventual
biodegradation. The impacts of varying degrees of UV weathering and presence of microbial communities
on the biodegradation of polyethylene will be tested in laboratory. It is expected that increasing degrees
of UV weathering will correlate positively with biodegradation rates as the polyethylene is structurally
weakened and chemically oxidized. Further, the presence of additional microbes in the seawater is
expected to amplify the biodegradation signal, both in terms of speed of onset as well as maximum
degradation reached at the end of the experiment. The mass of the plastic strips is also expected to
decrease as the polymer begins to breakdown as a result of the biotic processes.
This project aims to investigate the relationships between the extent and speed of biodegradation of
plastic in the marine environment and UV weathering through literature review, field and lab work, and
subsequent data analysis. The literature review will be focused on the current state of research. Topics
covered include general background and history as well as definitions concerning plastic terminology.
The sources, sinks, pathways, and environmental impacts of marine plastic debris will be discussed
as will the mechanisms of UV-initiated photo oxidation and microbial biodegradation. The degree of
crystalline and amorphous phase fractions within the polyethylene is shown to play a role in the rate of
degradation processes. Polyethylene exposed to UV weathering exhibits a breakdown and folding of the
C-C bonds on branches along the polymer backbone (Hamid et al., 1995). This change in structure causes
an increase in the crystalline phase fraction and a correspondingly decreased fraction of the amorphous
phase within the material (Martınez-Romo et al., 2015).
Fieldwork and laboratory work will be conducted to experimentally test the parameters governing
biodegradation in the marine environment. Strips of polyethylene will be weathered to varying degrees in
an accelerated weathering chamber with seawater flood cycles to simulate time spent at sea. The setup
is meant to mirror environmentally realistic conditions for plastics in the ocean. Biodegradation of the
weathered polyethylene strips will then be measured using a closed bottle respirometer method in both

2.1
Background
Since their inception in the early 20th century, plastics have become ubiquitous in daily life due to
their numerous applications in manufacturing and product design. The world’s first man-made polymer
was produced in the 1850s when nitric acid was reacted with cellulose to produce the thermoplastic
nitrocellulose (Freinkel, 2011). Thermoplastics are a type of plastic which melt and harden when
heated and cooled. English chemist and metallurgist Alexander Parkes patented his novel material as
Parkesine, but it failed to make a significant impact commercially (Geyer, 2020). The process was later
modified by American inventor John Hyatt to produce a more stable form of nitrocellulose patented as
Celluloid. This product would eventually become a commercial success. The next major development
in the history of plastics was the discovery of the first truly synthetic thermoset polymer plastic dubbed
Bakelite after its creator Leo Baekeland (American Chemical Society, 1993). Thermosets differ from
thermoplastics as they undergo a chemical change during their formation, meaning they can not be
remelted and reformed. Although both materials are now considered to be novelties rather than highly
used and produced substances, Celluloid and Bakelite are regarded by many to be the dawn of the current
age of dependence on plastics.
The first modern plastic, polyvinyl chloride (PVC), was produced in 1872 and is still used in large
quantities today (Geyer, 2020). Other popular and mass produced plastics in modern times include
polyethylene (PE), polyethylene terephthalate (PET), polypropylene (PP) and polystyrene (PS). They

are cheap to produce and easy to mould into a multitude of different forms (Hopewell et al., 2009).
As a result, the applications of plastics in recent times have increased exponentially as has society’s
dependence upon them. The shapes, sizes, and textures that plastics can come in are close to limitless
but include films, containers, glass replacements, wire insulators, piping, furniture, packaging, and
many more (Andrady et al., 2009). The widespread nature of plastic pollution coupled with its practical
irreversibility is prompting alarm around the world. Plastic pollution in the seas has been proposed by
Villarrubia-Gómez et al., 2018 as an additional planetary boundary to the collection first outlined in the
landmark Planetary Boundary paper from Rockström et al., 2009 and later Steffen et al., 2015. As plastics
fragment into gradually smaller and smaller pieces, their physical properties, chemical affinities, mobility,
and interactions with biological organisms all change. These changes most often result in a more difficult
to remediate environmental problem.
2.1.1
Plastic Production
The societal benefits, including advancements in health, safety, and technology development, described
by Andrady et al., 2009 are driving the ever increasing adoption of plastics. The quantities of plastic
produced have skyrocketed in the past 70 years as their low cost and versatility have been adopted
by manufacturers around the world. Figure 2.1.1 shows the growth trend between 1950 - 2015. The
production rates of plastic are seen to rise year over year almost every single year. The three exceptions
in 1975, 1980, and 2008 where production drops coincide with two global oil crises and the Global
Financial Crisis (Geyer, 2020). More recent studies have marked 2019’s plastic production at 463 million
tons (OECD, 2022). If these trends continue it is expected that annual production will more than double
by 2050 to reach a truly astonishing 1.1 billion tons (Geyer et al., 2017). That the production of plastic
products relies primarily on fossil fuel sources only further compounds the cause for concern (UNEP,
2016). Recently, biobased plastics have emerged as an alternative, but they are often more expensive to
produce than traditional plastics (Sekiguchi et al., 2011). Changing land use patterns required to cultivate
the biomass needed for biobased plastics also raise some concern about environmental impact.

2.1.3
Plastics in the Marine Environment
Society has long utilized the ocean to dispose of materials regarded as waste (GESAMP, 2019). The
transboundary nature of the sea coupled with its vastness has made it a prime receptacle for waste. The
ocean represents a sink for litter and as a result, tends to accumulate unwanted materials. Plastic can
enter the ocean via land, water, or the air. Dumping or mismanagement of terrestrial waste can eventually
8
==PAGE:24==
2

reach the ocean either directly or through fluvial runoff (GESAMP, 2015). Further, disposal of plastic
directly at sea, whether intentional or not, contributes to the issue. Finally, microplastics and even smaller
nanoplastics can be transported through the atmosphere and deposited into the ocean (Boucher et al.,
2017).
The issue of plastic pollution in the oceans has been creeping towards the forefront of public awareness
in recent decades, but it was first reported over 50 years ago (E. J. Carpenter et al., 1972). Plastic based
particles are estimated to make up 60 - 80% of all oceanic debris and litter (Moore, 2008). One estimate
from the United Nations Environmental Program gauged that more than 8 million tons of plastics are
entering the oceans every single year (UNEP, 2017). It is well documented that plastic now pervades
every corner of the planet’s oceans. Plastics have been detected everywhere in the ocean; from the deepest
seas to surface waters to intertidal zones and even frozen into icebergs and icecaps (Jambeck et al., 2015).
A study from Ostle et al., 2019 provides a time series of oceanic plastic pollution data for over 60 years,
confirming a significant increase in the presence of plastic fragments in the open oceans.
So much plastic has accumulated in Pacific surface waters that a sprawling mass of trash, termed The
Great Pacific Garbage Patch (GPGP), is floating in the ocean between Hawaii and California (Lebreton
et al., 2018). It is described by some as a "plastic soup" (J. Kaiser, 2010). These plastics are carried by
wind and oceanic currents and eventually gather in gyres like the GPGP. In this garbage patch alone it is
estimated that at least 79,000 tons of marine plastic are floating at sea. Other major garbage patches exist
in the ocean’s main gyres: the North and South Pacific Gyres, the North and South Atlantic Gyres, and
the Indian Gyre (Leal Filho et al., 2021). Once gathered in these gyres, plastic particles with a density less
than water will continue to float and accumulate without meaningful degradation or removal pathways.
Some of these plastics will experience changes in density as a result of biological or abiotic influences
causing them to sink, but this simply moves the plastic from the surface compartment of the ocean to the
deep waters (D. Kaiser et al., 2017).
Plastic is now ubiquitous in all of the world’s oceans. In a practical sense, it is also irreversible due to the
vastness of the ocean, the subsurface inaccessibility of the sea, and finally plastic’s inherent resistance
to natural removal from the environment. Villarrubia-Gómez et al., 2018 argue that with these two

more recent study from L. Persson et al., 2022 further submits that the rate of accumulation of novel

management and prevention practices when discussing plastics in the marine environment is clear.
As the eventual fate of plastics in the marine environment is the focus of this thesis, a detailed reckoning
of the effects of marine plastic pollution is outside the scope. They are varied and not yet completely
understood, but it has been shown that there is a host of adverse impacts on the environment.
Marine organisms interact with plastics in a variety of harmful ways. First, creatures around the globe
may become entangled in fishing gear both active and discarded. Exact statistics on entanglement related
deaths are imprecise but estimates range in the order of hundreds of thousands per year (Read et al.,
2006, Bergmann et al., 2015). Accidental ingestion of plastics can lead to organism deaths either through
choking or gradual buildups within the stomach. Micro to nanoscale plastics are also known to negatively
effect organism reproductive health and metabolic ability (Peng et al., 2020). Plastics move through the
water column horizontally and vertically as they are affected by weather, wind and waves, as well as
changes to their density from biofouling and weathering. This movement provides the possibility for
metals and priority organic pollutants (POPs) to effectively hitchhike and reach areas they might not have
otherwise (Andrady, 2017, Engler, 2012). The ingestion of POPs and metals bound to smaller particles
presents a credible route for these harmful compounds, many of which are bioaccumulative, to enter
the marine food webs (Brennecke et al., 2016). At the same time, the sinking of plastics may alter the
seafloor through accumulation. Benthic organisms have even been discovered to be smothered by plastics
(Mordecai et al., 2011).

2.2
Definitions
2.2.1
Plastic
Plastic is a broad term encompassing any material made of synthetic polymers which are long molecules
made up of repeating subunits. The key distinction is that plastics are synthetic because many naturally
occurring polymers can be found in the world, such as hair, muscle fibre, cellulose and rubber (Geyer
et al., 2017). So while a polymer may be derived from biomass rather than fossil fuels it can still be
considered synthetic and thus plastic depending on how it’s made. Figure 2.2.1 provides an overview
of some of the distinctions within the world of polymer classification. Under the umbrella of synthetic
polymers are both thermoplastics and thermosets. This distinction is based on a polymer’s ability to
be remelted and remoulded. The plastic in thermoplastic refers to the physicochemical ability of the
material to deform in a plastic manner and undergo reformation (UNEP, 2015). Thermosets, such as
epoxy, actually undergo a chemical change as their form solidifies and as such, they can’t be reclaimed
and recycled with the same ease that thermoplastics can (GESAMP, 2015).

2015. Fossil fuel and biomass derived synthetic polymers (plastics) are divided into thermosets and thermoplastics while
naturally occurring biopolymers are excluded. All synthetic polymers are seen to eventually undergo fragmentation into smaller
particles.

2.2.2
Polyolefin
Polyolefins are a subset of thermoplastics comprised of multiple polymerized olefin constituents (Al
Ma’adeed et al., 2016), also known as alkenes. These compounds are made up of one or more pairs of
double-bonded carbon atoms with hydrogen atoms making up the remainder of the bonds. Examples
of olefins include propylene, ethylene, isoprenes, and butenes. Polyolefins are chains of linked olefins
composed exclusively of hydrogen and carbon. These main chains may exist with or without side chains.
2.2.3
Polyethylene
Polyethylene, with the formula (C2H4)n, is a type of polyolefin composed of various sized chains of the
monomer ethylene gas with the formula C2H4 seen in Figure 2.2.2. Ethylene itself is a stable compound
which requires a catalyst to transform into longer chains.
Figure 2.2.2: Simplified chemical structure of ethylene gas showing two double bonded carbon atoms with two single bonded
hydrogens each
Polyethylene was first synthesized by accident in 1933 through free radical polymerization (FRP) (Ehrlich
et al., 1970). Polymerization techniques have since developed and now utilize various catalysts for
breaking down the double carbon bond in the ethylene molecule and linking longer chains seen in Figure
2.2.3. Since then, the applications of polyethylene have increased exponentially and it is now the most
produced and used plastic in the world (Patel, 2016).

Polyethylene falls broadly into three categories: low-density polyethylene (LDPE), linear low-density
polyethylene (LLDPE) and high-density polyethylene (HDPE). Other types exist but are less significant in
terms of commercial importance and most can be considered to fall under one of the three large categories.
The other types of polyethylene include ultra-high molecular weight polyethylene (UHMWPE), ultra-low
molecular weight polyethylene (ULMWPE), high molecular weight polyethylene (HMWPE), high-density
cross-linked polyethylene (HDXLPE), cross-linked polyethylene (XLPE), medium-density polyethylene
(MDPE), very low-density polyethylene (VLDPE), and chlorinated polyethylene (CPE) to name a handful.
As mentioned, most of these are subclasses of the three dominant polyethylene varieties.
As the three broad categories are the most widely used and widely disposed, the focus will be on their
broad scale descriptions. LDPE is produced at very high temperatures under high pressures using FRP
(Maraschin, 2002). LLDPE is produced using transition metal catalysts and is less energy intensive than
LDPE ethylene polymerization. Similarly, HDPE is typically produced through the Ziegler-Nota method
of metallocene catalysts (Gent et al., 2016). These production techniques are ultimately responsible
for the type and degree of chain branching off the main polymer backbone (Figure 2.2.4) which in turn
influences the density.

==PAGE:30==

The structural differences exhibited by the three types of PE also dictate some of their physical properties
(Graziano et al., 2018) and by extension their applications (Gent et al., 2016, Benham et al., 2002,
Simpson et al., 2002,).
1. LDPE possess the greatest degree of chains branching off of the main C-C backbone. LDPE has
branches on approximately 2% of the carbon atoms (Sen et al., 2015). These chains are both short
and long chains. The presence of these chains inhibits the tight packing of ethylene monomers
which gives the characteristic low density. This high degree of chain branching creates the weak
intermolecular interactions responsible for LDPE’s characteristic ductility, flexibility and relatively
limited crystallinity. As a result, LDPE is well suited for manufacturing, among other things, plastic
films, plastic bags, wire insulation, squeeze bottles, injection moulding and various housewares.
2. LLDPE is a linearly structured C-C backbone with uniform short branches. Similar to LDPE, the
structure and prevalence of chains prevent the monomers from packing closely together, resulting
in a low-density product. LLDPE has similar physical properties to LDPE, and a result, shares
many of the same applications.
3. HDPE has a linear C-C polymer backbone with very limited branching. This allows for a very
compact ethylene grouping creating a high-density product. HDPE is highly crystalline with its
tightly packed lattice which generates its characteristic high strength and toughness. Chemically
speaking, HDPE is also more resistant to reaction than LDPE or LLDPE. These properties lend
themselves well to HDPE’s common uses which include food packaging, bottles, fuel tanks and
pipes.

2.2.4
Bioplastic, Biobased Plastic and Biodegradable Plastic
Advances in material engineering over the past decades have produced a few related but distinct terms in
the world of plastic. These are bioplastic, biobased plastic and biodegradable plastic and although there is
some overlap the terms can not be used interchangeably (Goel et al., 2021). Biobased plastic refers to
the actual composition of the material whereas biodegradable refers to the ability of the product to be
broken down by biological activity. Bioplastic refers to a material that is either biobased, biodegradable,
or both (Tokiwa et al., 2009). Biobased plastics are synthetic polymers made partially or entirely from
biomass. Typical source materials for biobased plastic products include maize, sugarcane, and cellulose
(UNEP, 2015). Categorically, the fossil fuels from which traditional plastics are made are also bio-based
but for classification purposes are considered to be distinct as a functionally non-renewable source.
Biodegradable plastics, on the other hand, must be capable of being degraded by biochemical processes.
Microbial organisms convert hydrocarbons like polymers into natural substances such as carbon dioxide,
water, methane and compost. The reaction products depend on whether the process is taking place
aerobically or anaerobically (Glaser, 2019). The terms biobased and biodegradable are non-mutually
exclusive. So a fossil fuel based plastic might be biodegradable and a biobased plastic might be resistant
to degradation while both examples would be technically considered a bioplastic (see Figure 2.2.5). For
example, ethylene might be produced through the dehydrogenation of alcohol produced from biomass.
This may then be polymerized to make a biobased, but non-biodegradable, polyethylene (Goel et al.,
2021).

2.3.2
Macroplastics
Macroplastics are plastics, whether whole or fragmented, with a diameter greater than 1 cm. UNEP,
2016 also proposes an additional class, megaplastics, on the upper spectrum of macroplastics which
encompasses huge plastic pieces such as discarded or lost fishing gear as well as debris from catastrophic
events. Examples of macroplastics commonly found at sea are bags, bottles, containers, tools, and more.
Macroplastics are easily spotted along shorelines. They are also present in all other compartments of the
ocean and pose a threat to marine ecosystems. For organisms, the primary risks are consumption and
entanglement (Barboza et al., 2019). Macroplastics carried by oceanic currents and wind can accumulate
in the oceanic gyres earlier described and seen below in Figure 2.3.1. These gyres are also prone to leaking
pollution. For example, the thermohaline circulation can carry plastics from the North Atlantic Gyre to
the Arctic Ocean. As a result, the Arctic has become a dead-end for some of the floating macroplastics in
the ocean despite the relatively limited disposal within the region (Cózar et al., 2017).

2.3.3
Microplastics and Nanoplastics
Between 1 to 1000 µm plastic particles are classified as microplastics. Microplastics can be further broken
down into two broad categories based on their origin: primary and secondary. Primary microplastics
are intentionally engineered to their size to fulfill a certain function, such as microbeads in cosmetics or
tiny abrasive particles in toothpaste. Secondary microplastics are formed as the result of the weathering
and breakdown of macroplastics into progressively smaller and smaller fragments (Andrady, 2011, Gola
et al., 2021).
As plastic particles enter the micro size range some of their physicochemical characteristics begin to
change. First off, their affinity for sorption increases in tandem with their relative surface area (Guo
et al., 2019). Studies show that microplastic particles are efficient at scavenging trace metals and priority
organic pollutants (Holmes et al., 2012, Santana-Viera et al., 2021). Microplastics themselves are also
seen to be hazardous with some releasing endocrine-disrupting chemicals (Kögel et al., 2020). Owing to
their small size, microplastics can be transported long distances. In this way, microplastics can act as
both source and vector for harmful substances in the ocean. The density of microplastic particles can
also change as a result of fouling on their increased surface area (D. Kaiser et al., 2017). The problem of

remediation also becomes increasingly difficult in a practice as particle size decreases.
The problems experienced with microplastics become more pronounced once particle size decreases
below 1 µm. The behaviour of nanoplastics in the marine environment becomes increasingly affected
by physical and chemical processes such as dissolution, reduction-oxidation reactions, aggregation, and
absorption. These processes are thought to be regulated primarily by their surface properties and ionic
affinity (Gangadoo et al., 2020). Further, nanoplastics are expected to exhibit more chemical polarity
than their larger counterparts which is in turn expected to lead to increased uptake by organisms (Sorasan
et al., 2021).
2.4
Abiotic Degradation of Polyethylene
Abiotic degradation in the marine environment occurs as a material interacts with a combination of
sunlight, oxygen, and water. Mechanical weathering, through tidal action, for example, is also expected
to play some role in gradually breaking down polyethylene. The susceptibility of a material to the
different types of abiotic degradation depends largely on its chemical composition. Synthetic polymers
with a carbon to carbon single-bond backbone are inherently resistant to biodegradation and so abiotic
degradation into more readily accessible fragments is expected to precede effective biodegradation
(Gewert et al., 2015). While PE is the most inert of the polyolefins, and LDPE is considered to be
practically inert, it does degrade slowly in the natural environment through abiotic processes. Abiotic
degradation of polyethylene is characterized by the presence of oxygenated compounds such as carbonyl
as well as visual discolouration and cracking of the material (Vasile, 2000). The ocean provides an
environment with solar radiation, aerobic conditions and moderate temperatures. As a result, the factors
with the greatest influence on weathering are sunlight and oxygen (Pritchard, 2012). Figure 2.4.1 shows
some of the common reaction products formed during the photo or thermo oxidation of polyethylene.
These include aldehydes, carboxyl and carbonyl groups. Abiotic polymer degradation is possible in
anaerobic environments without oxidation. However, the high temperatures required to do so (over 350
◦C) and the ready availability of oxygen at the ocean’s surface mean that these anaerobic processes can
be ignored.

2.4.1
Photo Oxidation
Polyethylene is susceptible to UV-initiated oxidation which is believed to be the most significant abiotic
degradation pathway in outdoor aerobic environments like the ocean (Gijsman et al., 1999). PE may seem
to be naturally resistant to photodegradation due to its lack of UV visible chromophores. However, external
impurities and structural defects formed during the production of polyethylene or during subsequent
weathering (Grassie et al., 1988) can act as chromophoric sites allowing for the initiation of UV oxidation
(Chamas et al., 2020). These chromophoric sites are converted into free radicals when UV radiation
with sufficient energy breaks the C-H bonds along the polymeric backbone (Gewert et al., 2015). The
polymeric free radicals then react with oxygen to form hydroperoxide radicals. In addition, autooxidation
occurs in a process known as propagation. Finally, the reaction terminates in chain scission where the
main chain is degraded or cross-linking where two polymer chains join together (Pinheiro et al., 2004).
Figure 2.4.2 details the steps, initiation, propagation and termination, and possible pathways for PE
photodegradation.

The expected reaction products of the process are aldehydes, carbonyls, ketones and olefins which should
be more susceptible to subsequent photo-initiated degradation due to their unsaturated double bonds. The
carbonyl groups formed on LDPE through UV oxidation are typically concentrated near the surface of the
material (Johnson et al., 1976). The process is expected to continue and gradually create a greater surface
area available for further reaction. As PE is exposed to UV weathering, the C-C bonds on branches along
the polymer backbone begin to break down and fold in on themselves (Hamid et al., 1995). This change in
structure causes an increase in the crystalline phase fraction (CPF) and a decrease in the amorphous phase

owing to its moderately amorphous and highly branching structure. Martınez-Romo et al., 2015 state that
the crystalline phase fraction of LDPE increases between 0 - 45 days of UV-B exposure as a result of the
chain bond breaking and the subsequent restructuring along the polymeric backbone described by Hamid
et al., 1995. After 45 days of exposure, the crystallinity is thought to again decrease.
2.4.2
Thermo Oxidation
A study from Gardette et al., 2013 states that "in the absence of sunlight, thermal oxidative degradation
of PE does not occur at appreciable rates at temperatures below 100 °C". As periodic sunlight will be
present in outdoor environments and ocean temperatures are quite moderate, thermo oxidation is not
expected to be a significant abiotic degradation pathway for polyethylene at sea.
2.4.3
Hydrolysis
The backbone of the polyethylene molecule is made up of C-C single bonds which do not readily undergo
hydrolysis due to its hydrophobicity (Ghatge et al., 2020). Hydrolysis is thus not expected to be a
significant abiotic degradation for PE in the marine environment (Göpferich, 1996).
2.5
Biodegradation of Polyethylene
A formal definition from López-Ibáñez et al., 2022 states that biodegradation is "...the mineralization
of organic matter mediated by heterotrophic microorganisms, leading in aerobic conditions to CO2 and
H2O, and to an increase in the biomass of microorganisms". The result of complete biodegradation is
then the mineralization of the compound back to carbon dioxide and water. Thus, after mechanical stress
and photo oxidation have broken plastic fragments down into smaller products, some microbes in the
ocean are able to utilize hydrocarbons as an energy source as well as a source of carbon for the growth
of biomass (Ru et al., 2020). This process where compounds are broken down in CO2 by organisms is
known as biodegradation. The breakdown of intact and fragmented plastics leads to oligomers, drastically
shortened polymers with very few repeating units, and eventually to monomers (Wayman et al., 2021).
Plastics in general, but in particular polyethylene, are known to be resilient materials. The recalcitrance
of polyethylene is attributed to its hydrophobicity, low solubility, crystallinity, and high molecular weight

(Ghatge et al., 2020, Restrepo-Flórez et al., 2014). It is well documented that polyethylene is difficult to
biodegrade even in optimized conditions. The half-life of polyethylene in the ocean is estimated to be on
the order of decades (Chamas et al., 2020). This is evidenced by the presence of plastics from over half a
century ago still being found at sea (Krause et al., 2020). Although microbes are capable of degrading
very recalcitrant materials that have existed on geologic time scales, such as lignin and oil, it’s possible
that plastic is too novel for adaptation to have occurred (Wayman et al., 2021). It is difficult to measure
the biodegradation of plastics at sea in situ. Simultaneous exposure of plastics to biological, mechanical

2.5.1
Bioaugmentation
As plastic pollution has become a global issue, much attention and research have now been focused
on remediation. There are reports and studies showing a variety of organisms capable of biodegrading
polyethylene. For instance, wax moth worms (Galleria mellonella) with an inherent ability to feed on
and digest beeswax, have been seen chewing and eating PE films (Bombelli et al., 2017). Fungi have also
been the subject of much research with strains such as Penicillium chrysogenum and Penicillium oxalicum
fungus showing the capacity to degrade LDPE (Khandare et al., 2021, Ojha et al., 2017). However, these
terrestrial organisms do not play a significant role in the marine environment.
The microbial communities present in natural seawater have been seen to degrade plastics, albeit at
a very slow rate (Wayman et al., 2021). Certain colonizing bacterial strains have been isolated from
biofilms formed on plastic adrift at sea. These strains are known to more readily colonize plastics and
this is seen as the first step towards biodegradation. Two genii of particular interest are Vibrio and
Neptunomonas (Khandare et al., 2021, Oberbeckmann et al., 2020). Although not representative of the
marine environment, it is possible that bioaugmentation of seawater with a selection of these plastic
colonizing species could enhance biodegradation rates (Ru et al., 2020). Bioaugmentation of the ocean at
large is not feasible, but potential applications include site specific remediation, pretreatment of waste
released to sea, or the development of increasingly specialized plastic degraders.

HYPOTHESES
3
Hypotheses
Based on the literature review, several results are expected based on the degree of weathering and presence
of additional microbial strains.
1. First off, based on the increased surface area and changes in structural integrity as a result of
photo weathering it is anticipated that degree of weathering and BOD/ThOD will be positively
correlated. The longer that plastic spends at sea the more it cracks, fragments, and is oxidized.
These are important weaknesses in the C-C backbone of polyethylene and should allow for
more effective degradation by biological activity. Further, the increased surface area caused by
cracking and fragmenting should also allow for a greater degree of biofilm formation and therefrom
biodegradation.
2. The degree of surface alteration, both physical and chemical, is expected to increase after the
28-day run time and to be correlated with positively with degree of weathering. Biodegradation
is assumed to be concentrated in regions of existing defects that will be further amplified as the
material is broken down by the marine microorganisms present.
3. It is expected that the heavily weathered PE strips will undergo the greatest mass loss during the
28-day experimental run time. As carbon is mineralized by the microbial community present in
seawater that mass is assimilated into the living beings. Thus, higher biological oxygen demands
will lead to greater transfers of mass within the system. The bioplastic strips are thus also expected
to experience the greatest weight loss throughout the experiment.
4. The addition of plastic colonizing bacteria is expected to amplify the biodegradation, surface
alteration, and mass loss of polyethylene samples due to the increased microbial activity and
mineralization of carbon.
5. A standardized test set up will be sufficient for measuring amounts of biological demand for plastics
in seawater and can provide information on biodegradation patterns.

Experimental Plan
4.1
Objectives
With the understanding of some of plastic’s background, sources, sinks and pathways, effects, and
environmental impacts, the objective of this study is to characterize the effects that varying degrees
of weathering have on the biodegradation potential of plastics in the marine environment. In doing
so, the feasibility of standardized biodegradation testing for plastics in seawater, with and without
bioaugmentation, is also tested. These same principles can be applied to bioplastics. The presence of
saltwater resistant plastic colonizing bacteria is also of interest. The influence of these factors on the
structural and chemical characteristics of the test materials will be examined.
4.2
Synthesis and Weathering
Strips of low-density PE (LDPE) underwent accelerated weathering at Kristineberg Marine Research
Station in an ATLAS SuntestXLS+ chamber, seen in Figure 4.2.1, with a dry and flood cycle to simulate
tidal waters. Seawater collected directly from the fjord at Kristineberg was used for the flood cycles.
The strips were left in the chamber for 2 weeks and 3 weeks, representing 4 months and 6 months
of UV weathering respectively. Scanning electron microscope (SEM) images confirmed structural
weakening in the plastic strips and signs of oxidative degradation through the formation of cracks. FT-IR
analysis also showed oxidized functional groups as well as the formation of anhydrides by reaction of the
polyethylene’s carbonyl groups. Biodegradation within the chamber is not expected to be significant due
to the high-intensity UV wavelengths present hindering microbial activity.

METHODOLOGY
5
Methodology
5.1
Polyethylene Synthesis
Sheets of low-density polyethylene (LDPE) film were produced on February 10th, 2022 at the Technical
Research Institute of Sweden (RISE) using a micro-compounder. Micro-compounders are tabletop
instruments increasingly used for forming and processing laboratory-scale volumes of polymers offering
rapid and reliable results. They are particularly useful for optimizing melt and flow conditions in
producing polymers for applications in research (Berton et al., 2021). Despite the benefits of micro-
compounder synthesis, the rheology of LDPE is seen to be sensitive to its processing history and can
thus vary without significant changes to its molecular weight and molecular weight distribution (Li et al.,
2013). To minimize variability the LDPE (Borealis CA8200, CAS-No 9002-88-4) contained no additives
and was produced at 40-50 µm thickness. FT-IR analysis of the produced samples confirmed the chemical
accuracy of the final product. The film was then cut into 1 x 4 cm pieces and mounted onto stainless steel
mesh for UV exposure in the ATLAS chamber.
5.2
Polyethylene Weathering
After synthesis, cutting and mounting onto the stainless steel mesh the PE strips were placed into the
ATLAS SunFlood XLS+ chamber where they underwent either 2 weeks or 3 weeks of weathering,
simulating 4 months and 6 months of weathering at sea respectively. The weathering was conducted
according to ISO 4982-2 "Plastics — Methods of exposure to laboratory light sources — Part 2: Xenon-
arc lamps" (ISO, 2013). The chamber ran on a 2 hour cycle, where 1 hour and 40 minutes was the dry
cycle and the last 20 minutes consisted of a flood cycle where seawater was introduced into the chamber
to simulate tidal and shoreline mixing. This hour cycle repeated itself for the duration of the experiment
during which time the chamber was set to an irradiance of approximately 60 W/m2 or 0.024 W/PE strip at
a wavelength of 300 - 400 nm. The wavelength rests within the UV-A and UV-B range and has relatively
high photon energy for breaking the polymer’s chemical bonds. Shorter UV-B wavelengths within
that range are expected to generate unsaturated and oxygenated structures in polyethylene while longer
UV-A wavelengths are believed to cause mainly cross-linking reactions within PE (Andrady, 1997).
Polyethylene film is seen to undergo changes in extensibility when exposed to the wavelengths used in

the chamber. These changes also present physically as an increased tendency to crack and discolour, and
chemically as an increase in oxidized surface groups. Figure C1 in Appendix C shows some common
polymers and their wavelength sensitivities as well as the associated effects therefrom.
The controlled conditions within the weathering chamber were irradiance, temperature, and flood cycles.
Very minor fluctuations in irradiance were measured throughout the experiment as seen in Figure C2 in
Appendix C, but the majority of the run remained constant. The 2 and 3 weeks of irradiance correspond
to 4 months and 6 months, respectively, of weathering in the Swedish marine environment simulated
by the ATLAS Sunflood XLS. The typical intensity of total global radiation at all wavelengths between
295 and 3000 nm is around 1000 W/m2. Two temperatures were also measured inside the chamber
during the weathering process. Chamber air temperature (CHT) represents the temperature of the air
within the test setup. Black standard temperature (BST) is an estimate of the maximum temperature of
specimens in the chamber and is calculated based on a black panel absorbing all the incoming radiation.
The polyethylene strips tested are translucent, and it is not expected that they are heating up to the same
temperature as the measured BST. As a result, the CHT is a more accurate proxy for the temperature of
the PE throughout the experiment. As seen in Figure C3 in Appendix C, these readings were primarily at
32◦C and 65◦C for CHT and BST respectively. During the 20 minute flood cycle, there were rapid drops
due to the introduction of cold seawater, however, at the onset of the next dry cycle, the temperatures
quickly returned to normal. In all cases, the temperatures within the test chamber were too low for
significant thermo-oxidation to take place (Gardette et al., 2013) which leaves photo-oxidation as the
dominant weathering process occurring.

PE showed a uniform and smooth surface with very few imperfections. In comparison, the weathered
strips of polyethylene now showed undulations, scratches, cracks, and signs of gouging. These results are
best seen in Figure 5.3.2 G, showing parallel sets of scratches, and H showing larger structures which
appear to be deep scratches or the beginnings of cracks.

DISCUSSION
7
Discussion
The results obtained from the mass, chemical and structural characterization coupled with the biode-
gradation results from the Oxitop reveal a few key findings which can be drawn back to the original
hypotheses in Section 3. In all cases, it is not possible to be certain of the results due to the low ranges
present and the lack of sufficient replicates for statistical analysis. Despite this, the results offer some
useful insights regarding the parameters governing biodegradation rates of marine plastics. Further, some
of the limitations inherent to the study design are discussed as are potential next steps.
7.1
Hypotheses Outcomes
7.1.1
Impact of UV Weathering on Biodegradation
Hypothesis: The degree of UV Weathering and measured polyethylene biodegradation in seawater were
expected to be positively correlated.
The reasoning behind this hypothesis was that the plastic becomes more cracked, fragmented and oxidized
as it spends time at sea exposed to wind, waves, and sunlight. The structural weaknesses were expected to
increase surface area while oxidation was expected to weaken the C-C backbone of polyethylene thereby
allowing for more effective degradation by biological activity. The results did not seem to support this
hypothesis and the pristine polyethylene was found to degrade the most, followed by UV3 and finally
UV2.
This result was surprising and somewhat counter intuitive. The outcome may be explained by the
reduction of amorphous phase polyethylene following the dose of UV-B radiation experienced during the
accelerated weathering program. As PE is exposed to UV weathering the C-C bonds on branches along
the polymer backbone begin to break down and fold in on themselves (Hamid et al., 1995). This change
in structure causes an increase in the crystalline phase fraction and a correspondingly decreased fraction
of the amorphous phase within the LDPE samples (Martınez-Romo et al., 2015). As the amorphous
phase fraction is the most readily degraded, both through biotic and abiotic pathways, it seems that this
portion is the first to be stripped from the surface whether by UV radiation or biological activity. Thus,
the PPE had the greatest amount of amorphous phase LDPE available among the polyethylene samples.
65
==PAGE:81==
7
DISCUSSION
Martınez-Romo et al., 2015 state that the crystalline phase fraction of LDPE actually increases between 0
- 45 days of UV-B exposure as a result of the chain bond breaking and the subsequent restructuring along
the polymeric backbone described by Hamid et al., 1995. After 45 days the degree of crystallization starts
to decrease as the de-crystallization process caused by UV radiation bond breaking begins to take hold as
seen in Figure 7.1.1. This effect may be responsible for the observed biodegradation trends where UV2
(60 days simulated weathering) degrades to a lesser extent than the UV3 samples (90 days simulated
weathering). After the depletion of the more easily degradable amorphous phase, whether by sunlight or
by microbes, the more heavily weathered samples again become more susceptible to biodegradation.
Figure 7.1.1: Crystalline phase fraction of LDPE exposed to UV-B weathering modified from Martınez-Romo et al., 2015
It is possible that this amorphous phase fraction is analogous to the rough spheroidal texture described in
Section 6.2 during the cryo-SEM characterization of the control, pristine and UV3 weathered polyethylene
samples. The general trend observed was a decrease in the bumpy surface texture with an increased
degree of weathering. The control samples of unweathered and unbiodegraded PE displayed a surface
texture made up of tiny (450 - 750 nm) and very closely packed spheres as seen in Figure 6.2.1. These
spheres appear to greatly increase the surface area of the samples. The unweathered PE (PPE_N and
PPE_BI) showed remnants of these little spheres, although diminished relative to the control (seen Figures
6.2.2 and 6.2.3). Finally, on the UV3 weathered samples the spheres were almost entirely absent and the
surface smooth. There seems to be a parallel between BOD/ThOD %, amorphous phase fraction, and the

Crystalline phase fraction is typically measured using Differential Scanning Calorimetry or X-Ray
Diffraction. Time constraints meant it was not feasible to conduct crystallinity measurements on the
samples, however, future experimentation to constrain amorphous and crystalline phase distribution
would be beneficial for characterizing polyethylene degradation behaviour in the marine environment.
7.1.2
Polyethylene Surface Alteration: Chemical and Structural
Hypothesis: The degree of surface alteration, chemical and structural, are expected to increase over the
28 day experiment and to be positively correlated with degree of weathering.
Characteristic peaks obtained through FT-IR indicates the formation of anhydrides as a reaction product
of carbonyl groups which suggests a higher degree of degradation for the UV weathered PE compared to
the pristine material. For photo-oxidation of polyethylene the presence of carbonyl groups is a useful
indicator of photo degradation. Carbonyl groups are characterized by a double covalent bond between a
carbon atom and an oxygen atom (Fotopoulou et al., 2017). The FT-IR was only able to match 6 samples
with the known standards in its library following the biodegradation, namely Flasks 1 (PPE_N A), 2
(PPE_N B), 4 (PPE_BI A), 6 (UV2_N B), 21 (BP_N A) and 22 (BP_N B). Only 1 UV2 sample (Flask
6) was matched and zero of the UV3 samples were. This suggests that the surface chemistry is altered
correspondingly based on the degree of the UV weathering.
Overall, the cryo-SEM images seem to support a few trends. First off, an increased degree of weathering
seems to smooth the naturally occurring bumpy texture seen in the control sample images. It is possible
that these small-scale irregularities provide a relatively large amount of available surface area and are
therefore more susceptible to weathering. As a result, the spheroids are the first fraction of the plastic
film to be broken down during both biotic and abiotic degradation processes. This also may be supported
in part by the biodegradation data from Section 6.4, which show unweathered polyethylene strips having
a greater BOD compared to the more heavily weathered samples. Next, the imagery suggests that the
formation of biofilms is a natural process which will occur when plastics are left in seawater for long
enough, regardless of bioaugmentation. Finally, as expected, the degree of material weakness measured

by the presence of cracks, gouges, and erosion seems to correlate well with the degree of weathering
undergone.
7.1.3
Polyethylene Biodegradation Mass Loss
Hypothesis: Mass loss and degree of weathering were expected to be positively correlated after the 28
day experiment.
The reasoning for the above was that as carbon is mineralized by the microbial community present in
seawater that mass is assimilated into the living beings. Thus, higher biological oxygen demands will
lead to greater transfers of mass within the system. The bioplastic strips were, as a result, expected to
experience the greatest weight loss throughout the experiment based on their purported biodegradability.
The pristine polyethylene showed minor gains in mass, between 0.8 - 5.7 %, with little discernible
difference between the samples degraded in natural seawater versus bioaugmented seawater. Possible
reasons for this unexpected increase in mass may have been the formation of salt crystals, incomplete
drying before weighing, and the effects of biofouling where mass increases with the colonization of
microbes. The bioplastic on the other hand showed very considerable increases, especially for the samples
degraded with the addition of plastic colonizing microbes. In regular seawater, the bioplastic samples
gained between 31 - 40 % mass, whereas in bioaugmented seawater the samples increased their mass
between 148 - 241 %. With such substantial increases, it is not expected that salt crystal formation or
residual water content played a large role. Rather, as the bioplastic is purported to readily succumb to
biological degradation it seems likely that the mass gains are due to high levels of microbial colonization
and biofilm formation. That the samples degraded in bioaugmented seawater experienced such large
changes in mass further supports this notion.
7 of the 8 UV weathered samples showed decreases in mass but with little consistency based on sample
treatment. The 4 UV2 samples lost between 3.3 - 8.3 % mass with an average of 6.1 %. The sole UV
weathered sample which increased in mass, UV3_N duplicate B, gained 2.3 %. The remaining 3 of 4
UV3 samples lost between 1.9 - 38.4 % of their mass with an average loss of 22.4 %. Once again, there is
no correlation with the measured degradation from Section 6.4, particularly with the UV3 samples. One
68

possible explanation is that the combination of UV radiation during the accelerated weathering process,
the microbial activity, and the mechanical motion caused by the magnetic stir bar caused significant
fragmentation of the plastic strips over the 28 days. These smaller particles may have escaped extraction

In all cases, it is evident that the compound effects of UV weathering, fragmentation, biodegradation,
drying, and biofilm formation all play a role in the evolution of sample mass. With these many competing
factors, it is difficult to extract a clear signal with only duplicates for each test scenario.
7.1.4
Impact of Bioaugmentation
Hypothesis: Bioaugmentation of seawater is expected to amplify the biodegradation, surface alteration,
and mass loss of polyethylene samples to the increased microbial activity and mineralization of carbon
Bioaugmentation appears to be effective in increasing biodegradation in all flasks tested compared to
the natural seawater counterpart. The maximum BOD/ThOD % was increased and a more rapid onset
of CO2 production was observed. The degree of biodegradation ranked by maximum BOD/ThOD was
PPE_BI > PPE_N > UV3_BI > UV3_N > UV2_BI > UV2_N. Similar patterns were also noted by the
bioplastic, the microscrystalline cellulose, and the blank samples. However, as previously mentioned,
the experiment is dealing with very low values of biodegradation on a short time scale. As a result, it is
difficult to determine which fraction of the signal is caused by random fluctuations.
The degree of surface chemistry alteration appears to be amplified by the presence of plastic colonizing
bacteria in seawater. Of the samples that the FT-IR was able to categorize, only one sample, Flask 4
(PPE_BI), was from a test setup containing bioaugmented seawater. This indicates that the presence of
additional plastic colonizing microbes is able to more rapidly alter the surface chemistry of the PE to the
point where FT-IR is no longer able to catalogue it.
Cryo-SEM imagery detected the presence of biofilm formation on some samples but not enough evidence

7.1.5
Feasibility of Standardized Testing for Plastic Biodegradation in Seawater
Hypothesis: A modified standard test set up will be able to measure biological oxygen demand of plastics
in seawater and provide information on biodegradation trends
The results obtained through all stages of the experiment, with the exception of mass change, confirm
the feasibility of a standardized test set up with modification for testing biodegradation of plastics in
seawater. A similar test setup could be used in future studies with other plastics, material treatments, or
microbial communities.
7.2
Limitations
The experiment had some inherent limitations. These limitations were largely relating to time and space
available for testing. First of all, the time scales relevant to the processes in study are much longer than is
practical to comprehensively study over 6 months. UV weathering and biodegradation of plastics both
take considerable time to complete. The UV weathering was accelerated using the ATLAS chamber, but
the time required still represented a significant portion of the allotted study time. Next, the biodegradation
test required a full 28 days to run. The synthesis and ageing of the test material meant that the time
window for actual experimentation was very narrow and only 1 full 28 day run was possible while leaving
a reasonable period for analysis. The amount of structural characterization possible was also limited by
the time consuming nature of the instrumentation.
The available space in terms of Oxitop containers meant that it was only possible to run duplicates which
limited the possibility for meaningful statistics. It was also only possible to test 3 different degrees of
weathering in 2 test solutions. In addition, scheduling conflicts to use the limited equipment made multiple
test runs impractical. Meaningful statistics with duplicates are limited, however, with a greater number of
70
==PAGE:86==

runs, correlation and standard deviation would serve to strengthen the results from the biodegradation
testing and determine their significance. Other statistics include a mean BOD with a larger sample size,
as well as a measure of deviation from that mean. With a larger number of test runs, the correlations,
if any, between weathering, bioaugmentation and biodegradation would have a greater statistical basis.
A post hoc two way Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) test could be conducted to estimate how the mean
of a quantitative variable changes according to the levels of two categorical variables. In this case, a
two way ANOVA test could be used to test how level of degradation and presence of bioaugmentation
affect the final biodegradation observed. It is also worth analyzing the speed of onset of biodegradation
as differences were observed for the different treatments in the duplicate studies.
7.3
Outlook
The success of the experimental design, despite its limitations, and the meaningful results obtained
through various testing methods indicate room for potential further study. Testing a wider range of
weathering degrees would shed more light on the interaction between biodegradation and photo oxidation.
With enough time to simulate weathering it would be fruitful to more closely constrain the effects of
oceanic weathering and scale to study to cover months, years, and even decades. With the increased
biodegradation potential of bioaugmented test solutions also supported by the results, it would be
interesting to experiment with similar test conditions but using different microbial strains. Dependent
upon the results, the sequencing and raising of these plastic degrading bacteria could be a very interesting
field of research. Other future areas of interest include considering how changes in material brittleness
brought about by weathering and biodegradation may affect the fragmentation of plastics into smaller
pieces.
In addition to the influence of these parameters on low-density polyethylene degradation, it would be
interesting to expand the scope to look at other types of plastic. As polyethylene is the simplest synthetic
polymer it would be relevant to start by testing HDPE and LLDPE. Following that, different plastics
such as PET, PPE, PS and PVC could be examined utilizing this test method. The abiotic and biotic
degradation trends of bioplastics could also be examined with the same experimental procedure. In all
cases, more replicates should be run to allow to statistical analysis of the results obtained.

CONCLUSION
8
Conclusion
The issue of plastic pollution is a global threat which shows little signs of slowing down. Due to their
tendency to accumulate in the world’s oceans, the factors influencing their environmental fate are of
great interest. The results from the biodegradation testing and post experiment characterization suggest
that although plastics in the marine environment are incredibly recalcitrant there are parameters which
influence their environmental fate. Photo initiated oxidation caused by UV radiation and the presence
of microbial communities were seen to influence the biotic mineralization of hydrocarbon polymers in
seawater. Ultimately, the test methodology was proven to be viable for testing the influence of abiotic
weathering and biotic community composition on the biodegradation of plastics at sea.
Pristine, unweathered polyethylene appeared to biodegrade the most over 28 days, followed by the
simulated 6 month weathered polyethylene and finally the 4 month samples. The positive control
material, microcrystalline cellulose, and reference material bioplastic were also shown to degrade at
measurable amounts using the test setup described. The maximum amounts of biodegradation were all
very low regardless of weathering, but the differences between treatment allow for some comparison of
patterns. The low levels (<1%) of measured polyethylene degradation along with a small sample size
made direct differentiation difficult. Although pristine polyethylene displayed the greatest potential for
biodegradation at sea, the influence of UV weathering typical to the epipelagic ocean environment showed
increased rates of biodegradation. Research supports the theory that the amorphous phase fraction of
low-density polyethylene is the most readily degradable by both biotic and abioitic processes. Once this
fraction is biodegraded or photo oxidized the polymeric main carbon to carbon bond chain begins to
fold in on itself. This packing effect, coupled with the crowding of LDPE’s characteristic short and long
chain branches, creates an increase in the more difficult to degrade crystalline phase fraction. This effect
caused the pristine polyethylene to biodegrade the most over 28 days as the more accessible portion of
the polymer was depleted. The UV weathered samples had already broken down the amorphous section
and begun to degrade the crystalline fraction. As a result, it is speculated that the 6 month weathered
strips with its greater degree of oxidation and surface weakening were more susceptible to biodegradation
than their 4 month weathered counterparts. The initial phase of low-density polyethylene exposure in the

marine environment is characterized by relatively ample biodegradation. Once the amorphous phase is
depleted, the now crystalline material undergoes less degradation. In all cases, biodegradation is not a
significant pathway for marine plastic pollution remediation regardless of degree of weathering.
The physical and chemical alterations in polyethylene, based on the degree of UV weathering, were
characterized by scanning electron microscopy and infrared spectroscopy. Surface weaknesses, such as
cracks, gouges and erosion, were found to correlate positively with the degree UV weathering. Chemical
alteration, signalling formation of oxidized functional groups, also increased with length of time exposed
to UV weathering. The presence of anhydrides as reaction products of carbonyl groups suggests a high
degree of degradation for the UV-weathered PE compared to the pristine material.
The microbial activity present in the seawater tested was determined to be sufficient for biodegradation
of plastic, albeit at very low levels, over the 28 day experiment. Biofilm formation was also noted. The
addition of selected saltwater tolerant plastic colonizing bacteria was observed to increase the maximum
biodegradation as well as the speed of the process. Bioaugmentation also corresponded with degree of
biofilm formation in addition to chemical and surface alteration.
The test methods used for the experiment were based on standardized methods but with modifications.
This adapted test method was found to be feasible for testing the biodegradation of plastics in the
environment while varying parameters such as degree of weathering and microbial communities. This
success lends itself to future studies based on the same methodology. These include expanding range of
weathering extent, microbial communities and types of plastic.
