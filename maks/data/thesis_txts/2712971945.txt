Abstract
The Timber-concrete composite (TCC) system has recently increased in popularity, and the
notched connection is regarded as the best. This study is part of a cluster project investigating
30 TCC specimens with a notched connection through push-out tests. This paper focuses on
the strength, stiffness, and behavior of different notch lengths, the angle of screws, the presence
of screws, and the difference between in-situ and prefabricated concrete. The material used
in the experiments were GL30H timber and C20/25 concrete. The test setup was constructed
to induce pure shear, and the data is collected using linear variable differential transformers
connected to a data acquisition program. Additionally, digital image correlation was used to
measure the behavior of the specimens.
The results from the tests produced a wide variety of failure modes, capacities, and stiffnesses.
The most frequent failure was shear in the concrete, followed by timber compression failure,
concrete tensile failure, and concrete compression failure. Except for the 200 mm notch, a sim-
ilar behavior when studying the strains in the specimens was observed regarding the notch
length variants.
Additionally, they showed a linearly increasing capacity with an increased
length, and they all failed by shear in the concrete.
Similarly to the capacity, the stiffness
increased with the notch length. However, the results showed some irregularities due to the
lack of contact between the concrete and timber. The specimens with the highest capacities
had 45-degree angled screws and experienced approximately 20% higher capacity than verti-
cal screws. On the other hand, the stiffness was much lower as an increased gap between the
concrete and timber occurred when inserting the screws.
The in situ and the 200 mm notch length specimens failed in timber compression. This failure
had similar behavior to the concrete shear failure. However, the flexural shear crack at the
loaded notch mouth was small as the buckling of the timber reduced the rotation of the con-
crete. This failure mode resulted in a lower capacity than expected, and the results were not
comparable to the other notch length variants.
The specimens without fasteners experienced tensile and compression failure of the concrete.
The tensile failure was caused by flexural shear by the loaded notch mouth, which without
fasteners could propagate freely. The capacity of the specimens was significantly lower than
with screws. However, without fasteners, the maximum load is much closer to the calculation
described in the Eurocode. For the sample with concrete compression failure, it failed before
the flexural shear crack appeared.
Overall the strength was largely dependent on the notch length, material properties, angle of
screws, and the presence of screws. The specimens’ behavior depended on the failure modes,
although similarities were found in all. The stiffness was subjected to some uncertainty. How-
ever, some conclusions could be made.

Introduction
A report by the united nations states that the construction industry represents 36% of energy
use and around 37% of greenhouse gas emissions globally in 2020 [(globalabc.org)]. One of
the ways to improve the emissions in the construction industry would be to implement more
timber construction. As mentioned in [(nzjf.org.nz)] material production for timber buildings has
zero global warming potential emissions. Therefore, including timber solutions is a big step
toward decreasing emissions. One possibility to increase the use of timber in buildings is the
use of timber hybrid systems, where timber is used in cooperation with another material. One
solution is a timber-concrete composite system (TCC).
In recent times the use of TCC systems has been more common. Examples include Okanagan
college in Canada, a residential building in Hamburg, and Vihantasalmi bridge in Finland [(clt-
denmark.dk)]. The advantages of using a TCC system compared to more commonly used
pure timber and concrete floors are several, in comparison to timber floors it greatly increases
the stiffness and acoustic performance additionally, it increases the thermal mass, which helps
reduce the energy consumption of heating and cooling. While in contrast to pure concrete floors,
it reduces the load on the foundation, and the mass is lowered (creating less seismic action on
the building), lower embodied energy, and reduced CO2 emission (Yeoh et al. (2011), Ballerini
et al. (2002), Dias and Schänzlin (2018)). The combination of the bending and tensile strength in
the timber, and the compressive strength and stiffness of the concrete compliments each other
well Yeoh et al. (2011). To ensure an efficient system Yeoh et al. mention three fundamental
criteria that need to be fulfilled; the neutral axis needs to be close to the intersection between the
concrete and timber, the connection must be strong and stiff enough to transfer the shear forces
and ensure proper composite action, and lastly, the timber needs to resist gravity load-induced
tensile stresses.
In this paper, the connection will be investigated further. A notched shear connection has been
chosen as this is the most effective connection, according to the literature (Kudla (2015), Yeoh
et al. (2011)). There have been several studies regarding this subject before. However, most
studies have investigated in situ cast concrete in combination with timber, whereas this paper
will look into a fully prefabricated solution. The study is part of a three-part project where 30
specimens with different parameters have been investigated. The primary purpose of this thesis
will be to investigate the behavior, strength, and stiffness of the specimens where notch length
will be the main focus. Additionally, a comparison of the in situ cast and prefabricated concrete
and the effects of fastener angle and its presence will be considered.

Literature Study
In recent years there has been an increased interest in the use of TCC systems, and since the
early 1990s, it has been an essential part of structural applications in several European coun-
tries (Yeoh et al. (2011), Dias and Schänzlin (2018)). This study has investigated 14 articles,
including literature, analytical, and experimental studies.
The literature studies have generally given an overview of the advantages of TCC structures
and discussed technical specifications and other design methods available. Dias and Schän-
zlin proposed technical specifications, including determining the stiffness and strength of TCC
connections. The stiffness was based on experimental data and varied with the notch depth.
For calculating the strength, they found the critical capacity by investigating four failure modes;
concrete shear (CSF), concrete compression (CCF), timber shear (TSF), and timber compres-
sion failure (TCF). Kudla describes different requirements for the notched connection based on
earlier literature. Which includes a minimum 20mm and 15mm notch depth for softwood and
LVL, respectively. A recommended notch length of 150mm, and a distance between notches of
eight times the notch depth. Additionally, a recommended inclination of the notch of 90 degrees.
However, there was no change in strength or stiffness between 80 and 115 degrees. She also
mentions the importance of screws (minimum diameter of 6mm) to prevent uplifting between
timber and concrete.
Boccadoro et al. analyzed the shear transfer and gap opening in TCC structures. He found
that deformation from compression of the timber leads to flexural-shear cracks in the concrete.
Moreover, for the specimens without vertical, the concrete cracks propagated, causing the loss
of shear transfer in the concrete layer and failure. However, the shear cracks formed in the
specimens with vertical reinforcement did not propagate as the reinforcement took the vertical
tension. He described this failure mode as a cantilever action, meaning a combination of shear
and tensile stresses, labeled concrete tensile failure (CTF). In summary, there are a total of five
described in the literature. These are shown in fig. 2.1 and 2.2.

Methods
This investigation consisted of a total of 30 specimens where 13 different parameters were
applied. Table 3.1 shows an overview of all the specimens with the number of duplicates and
their parameters. The reference specimen, which the other is the basis of comparison, is called
M-150-(0), where the ”M”, ”150”, and ”(0)” describe the prestressing degree, notch length, and
placement of screws in mm from the middle of the notch. Other than these, some additional
parameters are noted with a plus sign. The ”I” describes that the concrete is cast directly onto
the timber in situ. ”45dg” means that the screws are angled 45 degrees, where the screw
penetrates the middle of the notch in the concrete and timber interface. The ”0nf” implies that
no fastener was used.
The experiments were conducted in four batches following the date of concrete casting. From
the time the specimens were received, they were all stored in a room of constant 20 degrees and
60% relative humidity. Dates of the concrete casting and testing can be found in appendix A.
The methods used to conduct the experiments were done according to appropriate European
standards in Denmark. A full description of all standards used with exceptions, as well as non-
standardized procedures can be found inn appendix A.

Material tests
The reliability of the test results was ensured by material tests conducted on the relevant com-
ponents. This includes concrete, timber, reinforcement rebars, and screws.
3.1.1
Concrete
The concrete material tests were conducted before and after each batch’s main experiment. In
total, 12 cylinders were tested. Before the main experiment, the elastic modulus, compression,
and tensile strength were investigated by testing three cylinders for each property. The test
setup for the concrete tests can be seen in fig. 3.1.Three cylinders were tested for compression
strength after the main experiment. The strength could be extrapolated over the period by
performing compression strength tests before and after.

3.1.2
Timber
Tests were also performed to verify the timber properties, compression strength, elastic mod-
ulus under compression parallel to the grain, and moisture were tested. As the differences in
timber properties are significant, the test pieces were cut from the specimen to get accurate
measurements.
For each batch, the first and last specimens were tested. Three pieces were cut out for both
compression strength and elastic modulus. Additional compression tests were executed on two
samples following the main test results. For the moisture tests, a piece was cut out from one
specimen for each day of testing.
3.1.3
Reinforcement rebars and screws
The rebars used in the main experiment had eight, ten, and twelve mm diameters. Each rebar
size was tested for yield strength, ultimate limit strength, elastic modulus, and plastic deforma-
tion. Three rebars of each size were tested to get an average result for each property.
The screw tests consisted of three screws getting pulled out of a piece of timber cut from three
specimens, which resulted in the withdrawal capacity of the screws. A relaxation test on the
screws at two different prestressing degrees was also performed.

3.1.4
Main test
The main test setup was custom built to achieve a push out-test with pure shear between the
two elements. The design of the test setup can be seen in fig. 3.3. The main components of
the setup start with a hydraulic actuator on the right-hand side, labeled the active side. The
actuator had a capacity of 222kN or 50 000 lbs. The actuator pushed a pinned support onto the
concrete, where the center of the support was positioned at the interface between the concrete
and timber. A similar pinned support was attached to the timber on the opposite side, labeled
the passive side. This was held in place against a rigid steel construction. Two roller supports
were placed underneath the specimen to avoid friction between the timber and the surface.
Figure 3.3: Main test set-up design
The test procedure followed DS/EN 26891 as shown in fig. 3.4. However, after the initial cycle,
the loading rate was changed to 1 mm/min. A further explanation can be seen in appendix A.
The load rate for the initial load cycle was determined from an estimated load using Dias et al..
The estimate was then adjusted according to DS/EN 26891 if the maximum load deviates by
more than 20% the estimate should be changed. From the adjusted estimate, the different
estimates were modified. The initial load estimate and the modified estimate calculations can
be seen in appendix B and C, respectively.

Measurements and data acquisition
Data collected during the main experiment was the actuator force, screw force, and displace-
ment at and around the notch on both sides of the specimens. The force was measured from
the MTS test suite software (MTS) connected to the hydraulic actuator, with an accuracy of 1%.
The force acting on the screws was measured using two strain gauge-based force washers with
a capacity of 100kN.
The displacement was measured using two methods: digital image correlation (DIC) and linear
variable differential transformers (LVDTs). The DIC measurements were based on a dotted
pattern applied to the specimen. Displacement on the specimen’s surface was studied with a
high-resolution camera by photographing the pattern throughout the test. The setup can be
seen in fig. 3.5. The camera also captures a display showing the force corresponding to the
force measured on the MTS. After this, the photos were imported into GOM correlate, where
the software measures the distance between the dots. This gives information on the strain and
deformation globally and locally as the force is applied.

4.2.1
Notch length variants
Force displacement curves
For the varying notch length the force displacement curves in fig. 4.1 show a more or less iden-
tical behavior for the specimens that failed in concrete shear. These exhibit a very brittle failure.
However, after failure at approximately 50 kN, the chart shows a force plateau which shows the
activation of the screws. This continued until the end of the test at 15 mm displacement. The
200 mm notch length variants which failed in timber compression show very ductile failure. As
these specimens had a TCF compared to the CSF of the other specimens, a comparison of the
200 mm notch length is not as favorable. The reason for the TCF is most likely a combination
of higher shear resistance, and the timber’s compression strength is significantly lower. This is
seen when comparing the timber compression strength of the 200 mm notch length variants to
the batch with 150 mm notch length (same as M-150-(0)-1). Additionally, as the shear strength
increases with the notch length, the timber strength is increasingly more critical.

Stiffness
The initial stiffness of all the specimens in the project was calculated according to DS/EN 26891.
The standard estimates the initial stiffness (ks) between 10% and 40% of the estimated capacity.
Additionally, a secondary stiffness (ksec) has been used. This is calculated between 40% of the
estimated capacity to 80% of the measured maximum load and is calculated the same way as
(ks). The reason for (ksec) is to validate (ks), as through testing, it was discovered that there was
insufficient contact between the concrete and timber at the loaded notch mouth. Therefore,
some specimens experienced added displacement at the start of loading. This can be seen on
specimens M-100-(0)-1 and M-50-(0)-2, together with other notch variants, in fig. 4.3.

flexural shear cracks above the loaded notch mouth were observed, as seen in section 4.3.

a solution to prevent the flexural shear cracks or gap opening, as he labeled it, from propagating.

the flexural shear cracks had not yet developed. The CCF also seem slightly ductile, although

Discussion
5.1
Notch length
The maximum capacity of the specimens concerning the notch length can be seen to increase
somewhat linearly from 50 mm to 150mm. While between 150 mm and 200 mm, the capacity is
more or less the same, as seen in fig. 5.1. When looking at the failure modes of the specimens,
the 50, 100, and 150 mm notch length variants failed in CSF, while the specimen with 200 mm
notch failed in TCF. Therefore, the shear capacity in the concrete may continue to increase
linearly as long as CSF is the critical failure mode.
Figure 5.1: Force and stiffness in relation to notch length
These results are fascinating as the Eurocode calculation model only uses the length of the
notch geometry Dias et al. (2020). With this in mind, the results from Shi et al. and He et al.
have been investigated further and compared with the Eurocode model’s shear capacity esti-
mate. The results from their tests and the Eurocode have been presented in fig. 5.2, in the study
of Shi et al. only the tests conducted at ambient temperature have been used. Although the
experiments from the literature vary in parameters such as notch depth, width, and screw diam-
eter, the results fit the trend line with over 90% accuracy. This indicates that the shear capacity
largely depends on the notch length in practice. However, in the figure, it can be seen that the
shear capacity according to the Eurocode is considerably lower than the experimental results.
Since it is known that compressive axial force increases the shear capacity, as mentioned by
Jørgensen et al. (2013), it is believed that the exclusion of the screws in the Eurocode model
may contribute to underestimating the capacity.

5.2
In-situ-, 45 degree-, and no fastener
The other investigated specimens were in-situ cast, 45-degree screws, and without fasteners.
The results from these tests varied both in capacity and stiffness. The specimens with an-
gled screws showed similar behavior and the same failure mode as the reference specimen,
although, on the one hand, it had a much higher capacity. Moreover, as previously discussed,
the reason for this is the additional horizontal force component of the screws. Additionally, the
screws had a higher utilization compared to the vertical screws at peak load. On the other hand,
the stiffness was considerably lower, and it has been theorized that when inserting the screws,
the concrete is pushed away from the loaded notch mouth. A solution for this may be to change
the placement or direction of the screws. However, the stiffness increased significantly from
40% to 80%. Thus a solution may not be necessary.
Similar to the 200 mm notch variants, the in situ cast specimens failed in TCF. However, it
failed at a lower load than the 200 mm notch variant and the reference specimen. This is
mainly because of a weaker compression strength in the timber. The most interesting result
was the abnormal initial stiffness, where two out of three specimens had an extraordinarily high
stiffness as a result of the concrete cohesion on the timber. However, after the cohesion was
lost, the stiffness was very low even though it was the secondary stiffness. The unusually low
ksec is believed to be because the timber already fails before 80% of the maximum load is
reached. This can be seen in fig. 4.13. Only one of the specimens acted typically on the force-
displacement curve and showed a stable stiffness from 10% to 80% at around 1000 kN/mm/m.
Two failure modes were recorded for the no fastener specimens: CCF and CTF. The first speci-
men, with CCF, failed at a much lower load, which is believed to be why it did not fail in CTF. An
interesting point regarding the CTF specimens is that they failed very close to the estimated load
from the Eurocode, where the specimens failed at an average of 465 kN/m. From the Eurocode,

the failure load is 468 kN/m. This supports the theory that the gap between the Eurocode model
and experimental results from fig. 5.2 is caused by not including the screws in the calculations.
The DIC images generally show flexural crack propagation at the top of the concrete, indicating
an arch-like deformation between the loaded notch mouth and the application of the load at the
active sides. At the loaded notch mouth, there is a flexural shear crack in all the specimens
of varying degrees, except on one of the no fastener specimens. Furthermore, all the different
failure modes are easily comparable to the idealized failure modes.
5.3
Inadequacies of experimental procedure
During the experimental process, there were noted some inadequacies. One of which was that
many of the specimens had insufficient contact at the loaded notch mouth to determine the
initial stiffness. This led to more significant displacement than what was expected. This has
been mentioned earlier when relevant. A secondary stiffness was introduced to rectify this.
Although this is not interchangeable with the initial stiffness, it will give an idea of when the error
was most apparent.
Another concern that arose was that a horizontal rotation occurred for the first specimens, which
caused a disturbance in the DIC images and could affect the specimens as well. The main con-
cern was that this was caused by the supports on either side of the specimen not being parallel.
Therefore, an additional horizontal LVDT was placed on the DIC side directly opposite the LVDT
on the passive side. This was done to measure the difference in horizontal displacement and
find the rotation. Figure 5.5 shows the total horizontal displacement, which is the displacement
on the DIC side subtracted from the LVDT side. The total displacement relates to the rotation of
the specimen, and the figure shows no pattern for the rotation. For this reason, inaccuracies in
the specimens are believed to be the cause, not the test setup. Although this may have caused
some problems with the tests, it was impossible to fix this during the test period.

6
Conclusion
This project aimed to investigate the behavior of the notched connection in prefabricated TCC
structures. The focus was on the effect of the notch length, angle of screws, presence of screws,
and the effects of in-situ cast compared to prefabricated concrete.
Regarding the notch lengths, it was an apparent linear increase of the capacity from 50 mm
to 150 mm, and only the 200 mm notch deviated from the rest. This occurred as the 200 mm
notch specimens failed in TCF compared to CSF as the other notch variants. However, from
the literature, it is seen that the linearity of the capacity most likely increases beyond 150 mm.
The stiffness of the specimens behaved very similarly to the capacity and increased steadily.
The calculation model from the Eurocode consistently gave a lower capacity for the different
notch lengths. However, for the specimens without fasteners, the two CTF specimens fit very
well to the estimated capacity from the Eurocode Dias et al. (2020). As the calculation model
does not consider the screws when estimating the shear capacity, it is theorized that the gap
between the experimental and Eurocode capacities is mainly due to the compression force
acting on the concrete from the prestressed screws. Interesting further work would consist of
implementing the contribution of the screws in the existing calculation model to see if that is
more consistent with the experimental results.
The post-peak behavior of the notch length specimens showed that the higher the force de-
crease after failure, the higher force in the screws. Additionally, in all the specimens in CSF,
the load stabilizes at around 300 kN/m, but the load does not increase further for the 100 mm
and 150 mm notch lengths. This indicates that after failure in the notch at 100 mm and 150 mm,
the screws were in a plastic stage. In future work, it may be interesting to see what happens if
the load remains the same after failure and how different screws will act.
The in situ, angled screw, and no fastener specimens also show some interesting results. The
45-degree angled screws contributed to a higher capacity which is believed to be the effect
of the horizontal force component of the screws. However, as screws were inserted opposite
the load direction, a gap occurred between the timber and concrete at the loaded notch mouth
resulting in a loss of stiffness. Further work would include different positioning and direction of
the screws.
The in situ cast specimens had a TCF as the timber compression strength was significantly lower
than the reference specimens. This resulted in a lower capacity. Therefore, it was difficult to
compare the difference between in-situ cast and prefabricated concrete in terms of capacity.
The stiffness of the in situ specimens was difficult to compare as well. For two of the speci-
mens, the cohesion between the timber resulted in an extremely high initial stiffness. The one
sample which showed a standard stiffness was slightly lower than the reference but within the
recommended stiffness by Dias and Schänzlin. In subsequent experiments ensuring a more
robust timber and thus CSF would give a better basis of comparison.
The no fastener variant helped show the importance of the screws, as the capacity was dras-
tically lowered and the rotation of the concrete was much higher. The absence of the screws
caused the flexural shear crack in the notch mouth to continue propagating and, in turn, caused
a CTF in two of the specimens. The first specimen had a CCF before the crack by the notch
mouth could develop.
In conclusion, the strength of the Timber concrete composite notched connection largely de-
pended on the notch length, material quality, angle, and presence of screws. The specimens’
behavior varied slightly depending on the failure modes. However, most specimens experience
the same crack propagation. The stiffness was slightly erratic for some of the specimens as
Experimental study of notched connection for prefabricated Timber-Concrete Composite
31
==PAGE:44==
there was insufficient contact at the loaded notch mouth at the start of the test, and for the in
situ specimens, the cohesion disrupted the stiffness result. Therefore, it is hard to conclude
much about the initial stiffness. Still, the 100 mm, 150 mm, and 200 mm specimens and the
secondary stiffness indicate that using a stiffness of 1000 kN/mm/m as described by Dias and
Schänzlin is an adequate proposal.
