Abstract 
This master thesis explores the dynamic intersection of consumer perceptions and product 
alignment within the early-stage food startup, “Eat Myt.” As sustainability becomes a critical 
factor in consumer decision-making, this study aims to understand how sustainability features, 
alongside other key attributes like price, health benefits, and taste, influence consumer 
preferences. Using methodologies such as the Quality Functional Development (QFD) and 
House of Quality (HoQ) and framework, the research investigates the theoretical and practical 
challenges faced by food startups in aligning product development with consumer expectations.
The study begins by assessing the importance of sustainability and other product features within 
current qualitative functional development frameworks. It identifies the discrepancy between 
consumer awareness of sustainable products and their visible attributes, such as packaging and 
labels. Through focus groups, surveys, field interviews and sensory tests, consumer data was 
collected to analyze preferences and perceptions. Findings reveal that while consumers 
prioritize certain sustainable features, there is significant confusion and distrust regarding food 
labels, highlighting the need for clearer communication about sustainability attributes. Despite 
the growing emphasis on sustainability, it remains a secondary concern for most consumers 
when making purchasing decisions.
A notable methodological contribution is the modified House of Quality model, incorporating 
technical, sensory and market specifications and demands together. Which highlighted the 
importance of continuously refining the Quantitative Functional Development (QFD) process to 
suit the fast paced startup environment. The study also underscores the potential of using 
targeted marketing strategies to address negative perceptions of frozen convenience foods and 
the influence of societal trends on sustainability and health perceptions. 
Results indicate that demographic factors such as age, gender, and cultural background 
significantly affect consumer preferences. For instance, Danish consumers show a strong 
preference for the Danish Organic label compared to other labels. The research concludes with 
recommendations for future studies to further explore the categorization of product features 
using the Kano framework and to investigate the role of sensory attributes as performance 
features and delighters, particularly in the context of sustainability and health.

Introduction
1.1 Investigating the Impact of Consumer Perceptions and 
Product Alignment in an Emerging Food Startup
The growing emphasis on sustainability within the food industry poses unique challenges and 
opportunities for startups. As consumers become increasingly aware of environmental and 
health impacts, their expectations and preferences evolve, influencing their purchasing 
decisions. This study explores how sustainability features, among other attributes, impact 
consumer perceptions and preferences, focusing on the early-stage startup environment of “Eat 
Myt.”
One of the significant issues is evaluating the importance of sustainability within current 
qualitative functional development frameworks. The value of sustainable products is not always 
clear to consumers throughout the entire value chain. Consumers often focus on packaging 
material, labels, and occasionally the brand story. Therefore, it is crucial to address and improve 
the visibility of sustainability in ways that resonate with the target group’s common knowledge 
about sustainability and health in food.
The central problem under investigation revolves around understanding how sustainability 
features influence consumer preferences and perceptions within the food startup sector. This 
encompasses both theoretical and managerial dimensions. Theoretically, it seeks to contribute 
to academic knowledge on consumer behavior, sustainability, and product development 
methodologies such as the House of Quality (HoQ) and the Kano framework. Managerially, it 
addresses the practical challenges faced by food startups in integrating sustainability into their 
product development processes to align with consumer preferences.
1.2 Eat Myt
Eat Myt is an early-stage food startup with a compelling mission: to enhance accessibility to 
Danish blue mussels by innovating within the blue mussel industry and offering convenient 
access to this nutritious superfood. Myt aims not merely to introduce a new frozen seafood 
product but to pioneer climate-friendly, convenient, and healthy options in the market. At the 
forefront of this endeavor is Myt´s flagship hybrid product, combining blue mussels with 
complementary natural ingredients to deliver a delicious, crunchy, and sustainable protein 
alternative sourced from the pristine Nordic ocean.

Myt Bites represent more than just a culinary delight; they signify a paradigm shift in the use of 
sustainably farmed blue mussels, challenging traditional perceptions of seafood as both 
delicious and environmentally responsible.
Currently, the startup is conducting a feasibility study to explore various market entry strategies, 
assess consumer acceptance, and refine product offerings. With three dedicated co-founders 
guiding the initiative, collaborations with stakeholders such as test kitchens and suppliers are 
ongoing to ensure that Myt products exceed expectations.
1.3 Research Questions
The tentative research question is:
What is the influence of sustainability features, coupled with other key attributes, on
customer preferences and product alignment within the early-stage startup journey
of Myt in the food industry?
This research question can be further divided into a number of sub-questions as
following:
1.
How do demographic factors, such as age, gender, cultural background, and dietary 
preferences, influence consumer perceptions of sustainability in Myt's early-stage food 
startup?
2.
In what ways do the relative importance and satisfaction levels of sustainability features 
vary compared to other key attributes (price, health benefits, convenience, appearance, 
taste) across diverse consumer segments within Myt's target audience?
3.
How can the first stage of Qualitative Functional DeploymentHouse (QFD) and Quality 
(HoQ) frameworks complement each other in capturing both clear and underlying 
customer preferences for sustainability in food products within Myt's early-stage startup 
environment?
4.
Can the perceived importance of sustainability be influenced by external factors, such as 
societal trends or marketing strategies employed by food startups?
1.3 Aims and Objectives
The study aims to unravel the impact of sustainability features and other key attributes on 
customer preferences and product alignment within the specific environment of Myt's early 
stage startup journey.

2. Literature Review and Theory
2.1 Quality Functional Deployment
Quality Functional Development (QFD) is a structured method used to transform customer 
requirements into specific technical requirements for each stage of product development and 
production. It is designed to ensure that the voice of the customer is captured and translated 
into tech specifications throughout the product development cycle. QFD is widely recognized for 
its systematic approach to identifying customer needs and ensuring these needs are met with 
quality products and services. Research has shown that QFD can significantly enhance product 
quality and customer satisfaction and reduce time to market.
The four Phases of QFD (See figure 2.1)
1.
Product planning: Relating the voice of the customer to product design requirements
2.
Design Implementation: Translating them into design characteristics.
3.
Process Planning: Manufacturing operations
4.
Production Planning: Product requirements
Figure 2.1 The Four Phases of Quality Function Deployment (BGMC, 2024)
  
4
==PAGE:11==
For the thesis topic and research the focus is only on the first stage of QFD or product planning 
(Figure 2.1 red border). This phase, inspired by the House of Quality, is considered the central 
component of QFD (Ocampo, 2020). Consumer attributes are derived from focus groups, 
surveys, qualitative interviews, customer specifications, and other data sources (Kiran, 2016). 
Understanding consumer needs is crucial, given that nearly 50% of new food products are 
removed from the market within a year (Butterworth, 2016).
To improve food product success, simplified QFD models have emerged. The 7-step model 
simplifies the process by incorporating competitive analysis and fuzzy logic, replacing precise 
technical ratings with priority rankings. This model starts with understanding customer needs 
(WHATs), identifying sensory attributes (HOWs), and determining relative technical ratings 
based on the importance of the WHATs and their relationship with the HOWs. In contrast, the 
9-step model involves more detailed steps focused on sensory attributes.
Despite differences between models, the key is understanding customer demands and 
incorporating them into the technical aspects of the product (Wang, 2019), a challenge 
heightened by a focus on sustainability and novel food ingredients.
2.1.1 QFD Sustainable Product Design
Otani and Yamada (2011) strongly support variations of Quality Function Deployment (QFD) that 
emphasize sustainability. They argue that sustainable product design must integrate three key 
components: customer needs, environmental performance, and economic performance. Quality 
Function Deployment for Environment (QFDE) effectively incorporates these elements. 
However, QFDE falls short in involving all stakeholders in the decision-making process, thus 
failing to address the concerns of all stakeholders comprehensively.
Despite this limitation, QFDE is valuable for sustainable product development because it 
encompasses all stages of product planning, design deployment, process planning, and 
production planning. The primary risk lies in addressing sustainability only during the product 
planning and design deployment stages, which may lead to the neglect of manufacturing and 
production planning, preventing holistic sustainable product design (Ocampo, 2021).
While literature offers limited tools supporting sustainable product design, particularly in 
integrating social dimensions alongside economic and environmental considerations, recent 
advancements 
have 
focused 
on 
incorporating 
fuzzy 
set 
theory 
and 
multi-criteria 
decision-making (MCDM) methods into QFD. Despite these advancements, existing 
applications often overlook phases beyond product planning, hindering the translation of 
sustainability agendas into subsequent design phases. To address this gap, Jumao (2021) 
proposes interconnected hierarchical networks representing a means-end chain model, where 
stakeholder-derived sustainability agendas inform design requirements across all phases. When 
applied to vegetable cooking oil design, this framework prioritizes stakeholder needs,

ensuring consistency and communication of sustainability goals throughout development. This 
adaptable, process-oriented approach offers valuable insights for sustainable product design 
across diverse domains, while suggesting avenues for future exploration.
2.2 House of Quality
The House of Quality (HoQ) is the first and most crucial phase of QFD, where customer 
requirements are translated into design specifications. This phase involves constructing a matrix 
that relates customer desires to the ways in which the company can meet those desires. 
The matrix helps in identifying key areas where improvements are necessary and prioritizing 
them based on customer importance and competitive benchmarking.
HoQ has been extensively studied for its role in improving product development processes. 
Hauser and Clausing (1988) highlighted that the House of Quality helps cross-functional teams 
to focus on customer needs and design products that meet or exceed customer expectations. 
The process involves the participation of various departments, fostering collaboration and 
ensuring that all aspects of product development are aligned with customer demands.
The House of Quality (HoQ) methodology can be adapted to incorporate sustainability features 
in the development of food products, particularly benefiting startups and smaller companies. 
The critical consideration is how sustainability attributes correlate with both technical and 
sensory attributes. For example, consumers increasingly prioritize sustainable packaging and 
the narrative of a product, viewing packaging as a sensorial attribute that influences the overall 
customer experience from purchase to consumption. Understanding consumer priorities beyond 
price, such as packaging design, color, material, and label information, is essential for effective 
product development (Burton, Howlett, & Tangari, 2015; El Bilali, 2020).
A modified HoQ can map the interactions between technical attributes (e.g., affordable pricing, 
nutritional information, product quality control) and sensory attributes (e.g., appearance, filling, 
crispiness, taste, texture), ensuring a comprehensive approach to product development. 
Consumer attributes, derived from qualitative methods like focus groups, should be grouped into 
bundles reflecting the consumer's language. Thereafter, using frameworks like the Kano Model 
can help accurately represent consumer desires. By verifying these bundles and rank features 
into performance features, basic features, and delighters. Research shows that HoQ can 
improve food products by aligning technical specifications with consumer expectations. For 
example, studies on frozen peas demonstrated quality improvements through market based 
HoQ applications (Vatthanakul, 2010). 
Sustainability attributes could be seamlessly integrated into the House of Quality (HoQ) 
framework to align with consumer preferences and promote environmental practices. Among 
sensory attributes, packaging plays a pivotal role in shaping consumer experiences. Therefore, 
gaining insights into consumer priorities beyond mere pricing is crucial for effective product 
development.

This integrated approach not only enhances product offerings but also boosts consumer 
satisfaction, potentially fostering sustainability efforts (Beattie & McGuire, 2016)

2.4 Consumer Acceptance to Sustainable Food Products
In recent years, the acceptance of sustainable food products has gained attention as consumers 
become increasingly aware of environmental issues and their impact on food choices. 
Sustainable foods are products that are produced, processed, and distributed in ways that 
minimize environmental impact, support fair economic practices, and promote social equity. 
These foods aim to reduce carbon footprints, conserve water, and use fewer chemical inputs, 
promoting biodiversity and soil health. Sustainable food practices also emphasize ethical 
treatment of workers and animals, ensuring that food systems support communities and 
ecosystems holistically. Emphasizing local and seasonal produce, sustainable foods contribute 
to reducing food miles and fostering resilient local economies (Pretty et al., 2010; Garnett, 
2014). Understanding consumer acceptance of these products is crucial for promoting more 
sustainable eating habits.
  
7
==PAGE:14==
Several factors influence the acceptance of sustainable food products. First and foremost is 
consumer awareness. Increased exposure to information about environmental issues and the 
benefits of sustainable practices can significantly impact consumer choices. Educational 
campaigns and media coverage play a crucial role in raising awareness and shaping consumer 
attitudes towards sustainable foods (Grunert, 2014).
Additionally, social and cultural factors influence acceptance. In many societies, there is a 
growing movement towards sustainability, driven by cultural shifts towards environmental 
consciousness. This movement is particularly strong among younger generations, who are often 
more open to adopting new practices and products that align with their values (Köster, 2009). 
Research has shown that younger people are more willing to accept unfamiliar food compared 
to older adults. For instance, Yang (2020) found that 76% of older participants (51-75 years old) 
were more likely to be medium neophobic compared to 43% of younger participants (18-30 
years old). This suggests that younger consumers are generally more open to trying new and 
sustainable food products, potentially due to greater exposure to environmental education and a 
stronger desire to support sustainable practices.
Economic factors play a significant role in the acceptance of sustainable food products, with 
price sensitivity often posing a barrier due to the perception that sustainable products are more 
expensive. However, as the market for sustainable products grows and economies of scale 
come into play, prices are likely to become more competitive. Additionally, consumers may be 
willing to pay a premium for products they perceive to have higher value in terms of quality, 
health benefits, and environmental impact (Iweala, 2019). For example, if they evoke emotions, 
such as pride and optimism leading to an inner peace of mind (Koenig, 2014). There are notable 
similarities between the perception of functional foods and sustainable foods, particularly in 
terms of effectively communicating their benefits. In both cases, consumer knowledge and 
awareness of these benefits are limited, highlighting the need for targeted communication 
efforts. Acceptance is influenced by factors such as taste, product quality, price, convenience, 
and the trustworthiness of health and sustainability claims. As Siro (2008) notes, "As a rule, 
consumers seem to evaluate functional foods first and foremost as foods." Thus, while added 
benefits may enhance value, they "cannot outweigh the sensory properties of food."
Furthermore, the role of marketing and branding cannot be underestimated. Effective 
communication of the benefits of sustainable food products is essential. This includes clear and 
accurate labeling that highlights the environmental benefits, health advantages, and quality of 
the product. Marketing strategies that emphasize these attributes can help in building trust and 
encouraging consumer acceptance. This was confirmed by Broccardo (2020) research, where a 
connection was found between consumer loyalty and companies actions towards a more 
sustainable business model.
Another critical aspect is the availability and convenience of sustainable food products. For 
widespread acceptance, these products need to be easily accessible to consumers. This 
includes their availability in mainstream supermarkets, local grocery stores, 
  
8
==PAGE:15==
and online platforms. Ensuring that sustainable options are convenient for consumers to 
purchase and integrate into their daily lives is essential for driving adoption (Popovic, 2019)..
Finally, government policies and regulations can support the acceptance of sustainable food 
products. Incentives for sustainable farming practices, subsidies for sustainable food producers, 
and regulations that promote transparency in labeling can all contribute to a more favorable 
market environment for these products (Popovic, 2019).
2.5 Consumer Perception Towards Sustainable and Climate Friendly 
Packaging 
Understanding consumer perceptions of sustainable and climate-friendly packaging is crucial for 
promoting environmentally responsible choices. Consumers' opinions about packaging 
materials often diverge significantly from scientific evaluations of their environmental impacts. 
This gap between perception and reality highlights the need for better education and 
communication to align consumer choices with sustainable practices (Boz, 2024).
Packaging design, including material, shape, and labeling, significantly impacts consumer 
perceptions of usability and impulse buying (Bahrainizad & Rajabi, 2018). While color and size 
may not directly influence usability perceptions, the overall design and practical features of 
packaging play crucial roles in consumer decisions. Color drives 62–90% of all consumer 
purchasing 
decisions 
(Singh, 
2006). 
Different 
colors 
affect 
customers 
in 
various 
ways—physiologically, associationally, and culturally (Hine, 1995). For example, in Denmark, 
blue signifies quality, green represents hope and health, while yellow and red denote danger. 
The impact of color intensity is also significant; for instance, the fragrance of a product in a red 
bottle is perceived as more intense than in pink or white packaging (Spence & Velasco, 2018). 
Thus, understanding these cultural and perceptual nuances is essential for effective packaging 
design.
Journey mapping the relationship between food waste and packaging reveals that most 
participants prefer alternatives to plastic, although some acknowledge that packaging can 
reduce food waste (Langley et al., 2021). Consumers often want transparent plastic windows in 
otherwise opaque packaging, balancing the need for visibility with the desire to reduce plastic 
use. Consumers generally exhibit a willingness to pay a premium for sustainable packaging. 
Studies reveal that 86% of Swedish participants, 81% of U.S. participants, and 67% of German 
participants are willing to pay more for environmentally-friendly packaging (Lindh et al., 2016a; 
Neill & Williams, 2016; Van Birgelen et al., 2009). However, these intentions often do not 
translate into actual purchasing behavior, primarily due to competing factors like price and brand 
loyalty.
  
9
==PAGE:16==
Packaging material is a significant consideration. For example, glass is widely regarded by 
consumers as the best material across several attributes, including being natural, sustainable, 
high-quality, and tasty, surpassing other materials by a considerable margin. It is also seen as 
convenient, although only slightly more so than plastic and cardboard packaging. Cardboard 
follows glass in most categories but is perceived to be of lower quality. Metal cans rank after 
glass in terms of quality but are seen as less convenient compared to cartons. Plastic pouches 
and pots generally have a poor image, except for convenience, where they are on par with 
cartons and better than metal cans. This indicates a significant disparity between consumer 
perception and the actual environmental performance of these materials (Bock & Meyerding, 
2023).
Furthermore, consumer understanding of the Life Cycle Analysis (LCA) of these materials is 
often incorrect. For instance, dry carton sachets, which have the lowest LCA impact, are rated 
as the least sustainable by consumers. Conversely, glass jars, which have a higher LCA impact, 
are perceived as the second most sustainable. A similar contradiction exists with bioplastic 
cups, which have a high LCA environmental impact but are still viewed as highly sustainable. 
This misunderstanding highlights that consumer opinions on sustainable packaging do not 
always align with actual sustainability metrics (Boz, 2024).
Several factors shape consumer attitudes towards packaging, including direct experiences, 
social norms, and evolving knowledge when evaluating the sustainability of packaging material 
(Popovic et al., 2019). For example, Magnier and Crié (2015) found that visual cues and 
material perceptions heavily influence consumer choices, often leading to a preference for 
materials perceived as more natural or eco-friendly, regardless of their actual environmental 
impact. Additionally, Steenis et al. (2017) demonstrated that consumers' environmental 
concerns do not always translate into environmentally friendly choices due to a lack of 
understanding of the true environmental impacts of different packaging materials.
These misunderstandings can be attributed to a lack of detailed knowledge about the 
environmental impacts of different packaging materials and the complexities involved in their life 
cycle assessments. Paper/cardboard and metal are rated in line with scientific measures, but 
plastic is underestimated, and glass and biodegradable plastics are overestimated in terms of 
environmental sustainability. Addressing these misunderstandings through targeted education 
and clearer communication is needed to help align consumer perceptions with scientific facts. 
Educating consumers about the true environmental impacts of packaging materials can promote 
more sustainable choices. For instance, highlighting the actual LCA results and providing clear, 
accessible information about the environmental performance of different packaging options can 
help consumers make informed decisions. Additionally, improving labeling practices to include 
more comprehensive environmental information can aid in bridging the gap between perception 
and reality.
The attitude perspective towards packaging choices indicates that knowledge does not always 
lead to action. Social norms, direct experiences, and evolving attitudes significantly influence 
consumer behavior (Hines et al., 2010). 
  
10
==PAGE:17==
Consumers with strong self-orientation are less likely to act eco-consciously, while those who 
have satisfied personal needs are more likely to engage in pro-environmental behaviors 
(Popovic et al., 2019).
In conclusion, while consumer perceptions of sustainable and climate-friendly packaging are 
often misguided, there is potential to correct these misconceptions through better education and 
communication. By aligning consumer perceptions with scientific evaluations, it is possible to 
promote the use of truly sustainable packaging materials and contribute to broader 
environmental goals (Boz, Korhonen, & Koelsch Sand, 2020; Otto et al., 2021). Understanding 
and addressing the discrepancies between consumer perceptions and scientific facts are 
essential steps towards achieving a more sustainable future.

Higher education levels lead to greater understanding of sustainability labels, but not 
necessarily to higher levels of concern. Climate-neutral labeling, aimed at indicating that 
products cause no increase in greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, 
  
12
==PAGE:19==
often results in consumers drawing biased conclusions about other product characteristics. This 
phenomenon, known as the halo effect, is a cognitive bias where one feature of a product leads 
to positive evaluations of other unrelated features. For instance, sustainability claims can create 
a perception of overall product healthiness, reducing consumer guilt and potentially increasing 
consumption of the product (Burton et al., 2015).
Research by Stremmel et al. (2024) and Meyerding et al. (2019) highlights that climate-neutral 
labels are preferred over labels showing CO2 reduction or product carbon footprints (PCF), 
suggesting consumers believe these labels contribute more significantly to climate protection. 
This preference for climate-neutral labels indicates a lack of nuanced understanding among 
consumers regarding the true environmental impact of products.
The study by El Bilali (2020) emphasizes that despite scientific guidance, the individual actions 
necessary for climate change mitigation are often hindered by factors such as the impersonal 
nature of climate change, delayed reinforcement, and the complexity of proposed solutions like 
carbon labeling. These factors contribute to a general reluctance among consumers to change 
their consumption patterns, even when they are aware of the environmental benefits.
Beattie and McGuire (2016) further illustrate that consumer decision-making under time 
pressure often results in a dissociation between environmental attitudes and actual purchasing 
behavior. This disconnect suggests that while consumers may express concern for the 
environment, their actions do not always align with their stated values due to practical 
constraints and the immediate pressures of daily life.
To address this confusion, there is a crucial need for more effective governmental regulations 
and clearer, more comprehensive labeling to drive meaningful change. Current regulations do 
not enforce stringent standards for climate-neutral claims, allowing even climate-harmful 
products to achieve such status through offsetting. This regulatory gap undermines the 
credibility of climate-neutral labels and can mislead consumers.
Furthermore, Denmark is developing a climate label called "Klima," which will soon be launched. 
This label will rank the total CO2 emissions of food products on a scale from A to E 
(Fødevarestyrelsen, 2023). The impact and consumer perception of this label will be explored in 
the research.

Improving the clarity and comprehensiveness of food labels could involve standardizing the 
information presented and ensuring that it is based on robust scientific assessments. Educating 
consumers about the true environmental impacts of their food choices, through both formal 
education and public awareness campaigns, could help bridge the gap between perception and 
reality. As El Bilali (2020) notes, “Science as usual points the way, but change is first and 
foremost in the hands of each person,” underscoring the importance of individual action 
informed by accurate and accessible information.
2.8 Aligning Convenience and Sustainability
The development process of Myt Bites is strategically aimed at entering the convenient frozen 
food market while maintaining a strong commitment to sustainability. This dual focus aligns with 
the growing consumer demand for convenient food options that do not compromise on 
environmental responsibility. The choice to target the frozen food market is driven by its 
convenience and extended shelf life, making it an attractive option for busy consumers. Notably, 
processed foods (PFs) can be sustainable if developed with mindful practices and ingredients, 
as highlighted by Fardet and Rock (2020), who assert that PFs need not inherently be 
unsustainable.
Myt Bites integrates sustainability into its core business model, recognizing that sustainable 
practices can enhance both survival and consumer loyalty. A study of small and medium 
enterprises (SMEs) in the Italian wine sector by Broccardo and Zicari (2020) underscores that 
firms prioritizing sustainability often see improved economic performance and customer 
retention. These companies do not necessarily focus on cost reduction but rather on 
maintaining customer loyalty through sustainable initiatives. Myt Bites adopts a similar 
approach, embedding sustainability across its economic, social, and environmental dimensions, 
thus ensuring a holistic strategy that goes beyond merely addressing environmental concerns.

Implementing sustainability from the outset allows Eat Myt to pursue a proactive strategy rather 
than a defensive or improvement-focused one. By incorporating sustainable innovations 
throughout the product development process, Myt product can appeal to environmentally 
conscious consumers and set itself apart in the competitive frozen food market. This 
comprehensive commitment to sustainability, combined with the convenience of frozen foods, 
positions Myt to meet modern consumer needs while contributing positively to environmental 
goals.

3.1 Focus Group: Participant Recruitment and Methodology
In early March 2024, a mixed-method approach was employed involving a group of six

millennial age range (25 to 35 year olds), consisting of four men and two women, ensuring a 
diverse mix of nationalities and backgrounds. Participants were recruited through email and 
social media platforms. Notably, four participants were actively pursuing master's degrees in 
technology entrepreneurship.

Complementing this qualitative exploration, participants engaged in a visual assessment of

3.5 House of Quality (HoQ) 
The House of Quality (HoQ) is a structured method for translating customer requirements into 
appropriate product requirements. It is a part of the Quality Function Deployment (QFD) 
process, which aims to ensure that the voice of the customer is systematically incorporated into 
every aspect of product development and production. The HoQ is visually represented as a 
matrix, resembling the shape of a house, which is divided into different rooms. Each room has a 
specific purpose and contributes to the overall goal of aligning customer desires with technical 
specifications.
Figure 3.5: House of Quality Matrix
A. Customer Requirements (What)
The foundation of the HoQ is the list of customer requirements or "Whats." These are the 
attributes or features that customers want or need in a product. They are typically derived from 
market research, surveys, focus groups, and direct customer feedback. 
  
25
==PAGE:32==
This room represents the voice of the customer and is crucial for ensuring that the product 
development process is customer-focused.
To establish customer priorities, the importance of each customer requirement must be 
determined. This is achieved by using data from customer surveys, focus groups, field 
interviews, and sensory results. Each requirement is then assigned a weight based on its 
importance, on a scale of 1 to 10.
C. Technical Requirements (How)
Adjacent to the customer requirements are the technical requirements or "Hows." These are the 
measurable characteristics or engineering or marketing solutions that companies and startups 
can employ to meet the customer requirements. This room represents the technical voice of the 
company, translating what customers want into what the company can produce.
The technical importance rating (TIR_j) for each technical requirement (TR_j) is calculated by 
multiplying the importance of each customer requirement (W_i) by the strength of its relationship

D. Relationship Matrix
The central part of the HoQ is the relationship matrix, which links the "Whats" and "Hows." This 
matrix shows how each technical requirement impacts each customer requirement. The strength 
of these relationships is usually indicated using symbols or numerical values, allowing the team 
to prioritize which technical aspects need the most attention to meet customer needs effectively.
To determine the relationship matrix between each customer requirement and each technical 
requirement. A scale of 0, 1, 3, and 9, where used where:
●

●

●

●

E. Technical Correlations (Roof)
The roof of the house represents the technical correlations or interrelationships between the 
technical requirements. This part of the HoQ helps identify any synergies or conflicts between 
different technical solutions. For instance, improving one technical aspect might enhance 
another, or it might create a conflict that needs to be managed. This room is essential for 
optimizing the design and ensuring that all technical requirements work harmoniously.
F. Customer Priorities (Basement)

importance of each customer requirement. These priorities are often determined through 
customer surveys and feedback and are critical for focusing efforts on the most significant 
aspects from the customer's perspective.
B. Competitive Benchmarking (Walls)
The walls of the HoQ represent competitive benchmarking, where the product is compared 
against competitors' offerings. This comparison can reveal gaps and opportunities in the market, 
guiding the development of features that will provide a competitive advantage.

4.1.1.1 Focus Group Perception Towards Sustainability
Participants had strong opinions on sustainability, particularly regarding packaging. There was 
significant concern about unnecessary plastic packaging: "There's a lot of unnecessary 
packaging that is not supposed to be there." (Appendix A1).
The group also emphasized the importance of consuming local and seasonal food products to 
reduce carbon footprints, with avocados often cited as an example of unsustainable imports: 
“Avocados from Cyprus are really bad; prefer local products.” (Appendix A1).
Local and seasonal options were viewed as more sustainable compared to organic products 
due to the latter's heavy reliance on infrastructure to provide year-round availability: "Foods that 
are not in season require more heavy infrastructure to be cultivated." (Appendix A1).
Food waste was also a major concern, with participants striving to use food creatively to 
minimize waste: "Dislike throwing food. Try to use everything, even peels, in different ways."

5. Interpretation and Discussion 
5.1 Quantitative findings Conjoint Analysis and Survey
5.1.1 Attributes Importance
The conjoint analysis focused on three main areas: Climate Friendliness, Healthiness, and Pure 
Preference. Each area included the same attributes: Color, Price, Labels, and Material. Except 
for Healthiness, which substituted "Price" with "Message." The "Message" levels were no 
message, Usability, and CO2 emissions.
The importance of each attribute varied across the focus areas. For example, Labels held a 
34% importance in Climate Friendliness but increased to 43% in Healthiness. Conversely, 
Material was 19% important for climate but only 8% for health, indicating distinct attribute 
priorities. Health Related attributes, such as Labels and Ingredient Lists, were crucial for 
Healthiness, whereas packaging Material was vital for Climate Friendliness.
Examining consumer choice probability provided further insights. When considering Climate 
Friendliness, 62% of participants preferred Cardboard packaging. For Healthiness, the 
preference shifted slightly towards Plastic (21%) over Cardboard (18%). The low importance of 
packaging Material for Healthiness rendered it nearly irrelevant. Additionally, the "Message" 
attribute on packaging scored only 12% in importance for Healthiness.
In the Pure Preference results, Color and Labels emerged as the most important attributes, 
each with a 39% importance, aligning with both Climate Friendliness and Healthiness. Notably, 
"Price" had a higher significance in Pure Preference (14%) compared to Climate Friendliness 
(7%).
5.1.2 Attribute Level choice - Conjoint Analysis
The aim of the conjoint analysis was to understand the importance of various attributes and to 
identify preferred attribute levels, providing a more accurate representation of consumer choices 
and preferences.
For Color, Blue Orange was the preferred choice across all three preference categories, with 
Blue following in second place and Black being the least desired choice, especially among 
females. The difference between Blue Orange and Blue was more subtle concerning climate 
and health preferences compared to pure preference, where the consumer probability choice for 
Blue Orange was 48% and for Blue was 40%.
  
67
==PAGE:74==
Regarding Price, pure preference ranged from 32 DKK to 39 DKK, with 32 DKK receiving a 
probability choice of 44% as the clear favorite. There was no decisive difference between 35 
DKK and 39 DKK, with 35 DKK slightly favored by 2%. For price and climate friendliness, 
consumers focused more on Color and Design, Labels, and Materials, which explains why Price 
received an attribute importance score of only 7%. If the price range had been wider, it could 
have potentially increased its importance rating. The reason being that the price range of 32 to 
39 DKK falls within the acceptable price range identified through the field interview and Van 
Westendorp price sensitivity meter.
For Labels, the probability choice differed among the three question categories. In preference 
towards climate, Cardboard packaging with DK Organic and EU Organic labels had a probability 
choice of 26%, compared to 18% for health and 21% for pure preference. The label combination 
-1 (Keyhole, DK Organic, EU Organic, Klima) had the highest consumer probability choice for 
both health and pure preference, with a decisive lead in pure preference at 39%. This indicates 
that certain labels and label combinations significantly influence consumer choices, likely due to 
familiarity and consumer knowledge, as shown in Table 4.14, where participants were most 
familiar with the Keyhole and Organic labels.
For the Message attribute in preference towards health, participants paid little attention to the 
message on the back of the packaging. Usability was rated higher than the climate-friendly 
message, which was on par with having no additional message. However, with a 12% 
importance rate, the message was still considered more important than the material in the 
health preference context.
Thus, based on the coefficient tables (Tables 4.4-4.6), the optimal product can be identified 
through the ratings of attribute importance and the probabilities of chosen attribute levels.
Regarding demographic differences, women were more willing to pay a premium for 
climate-friendly and healthy food products compared to men. Literature supports that females 
are generally more concerned with environmental issues (Li, Y., 2022), although there is no 
direct correlation in recent literature indicating that women are more willing to pay more for such 
products over men. Studies have shown a majority willingness to pay a premium for 
environmentally-friendly packaging: 86% in a Lund study (Lindh et al., 2016a), 91% in a US 
study (Neill and Williams, 2016), and 67% in a German study (Van Birgelen et al., 2009). The 
conjoint analysis also indicated that men are more price-sensitive across all age groups.
5.2 Sustainable Food Product Features 
Consumer perception of sustainability often focuses primarily on environmental aspects, though 
sustainability encompasses environmental, societal, and economic factors. This focus is 
supported by both literature and research findings, with 97% of survey participants associating 
sustainability in food primarily with environmental impact. Key concerns included protecting the 
environment, recyclable packaging, reducing food waste, and consuming local products.
  
68
==PAGE:75==
The conjoint analysis revealed that respondents' preferences for climate-friendly attributes align 
with recent literature, indicating that packaging and packaging information are crucial indicators 
of food product sustainability. Packaging material's importance in climate friendliness was 58% 
higher compared to pure preference or health. However, field interviews highlighted mixed 
perceptions of sustainability despite it being a significant selling point of the product. Opinions 
varied on material choice, with some favoring cardboard for its perceived environmental benefits 
(“If you want to say you are climate friendly and then use plastic you seem quite paradoxical”), 
while others preferred plastic for its convenience and better design appeal ("The plastic one 
takes less space in the freezer."). These varied opinions suggest that while environmental 
impact is significant, it is not always a priority for consumers.
In the House of Quality analysis, only 2 out of 14 consumer preferences related to sustainability 
or climate friendliness. Packaging emerged as the most obvious sustainable feature in the 
consumer's eye, but detailed attributes such as color, design, labels, claims, and ingredient lists 
also play a role. Focus group discussions indicated a lack of label understanding, leading to 
confusion and distrust. This was supported by the survey results, which show that consumers 
are not well informed about food labels. This aligns with recent literature showing that 
misleading climate labels can enhance the ethical perception of products, even when they are 
not genuinely climate-friendly.
A concerning finding is the Halo effect, where a positive ethical perception can be triggered by 
design or vague positive labels, leading consumers to believe that climate-friendly products are 
also healthy, which may not be true (Stremmel, 2024). This indicates a need for clearer and 
more reliable labeling to avoid misleading consumers.
5.3 The impact of sustainability features compared to other key 
features
This study aimed to compare the importance of sustainability features to other key attributes in 
Quality Function Deployment, especially considering sustainability as one of the main values of 
the product. However, the House of Quality analysis revealed that only 2 out of 14 consumer 
requirements were directly connected to sustainability. Price emerged as the most significant 
factor, consistent with literature and qualitative interviews. During field interviews, participants 
frequently inquired about price early in the conversation, often before being asked the Van 
Westendorp price sensitivity questions ("depends on the price" and "What's the price?"). In the 
focus group, price was one of the two most frequently mentioned factors influencing participants' 
food choices.
Other factors often mentioned before Sustainability included Appealing and Informative 
Packaging, Convenience and Time Saving Features, Quality, a Clean Ingredient list, Familiarity, 
  
69
==PAGE:76==
Great Taste and Sensory Qualities, and Specific Nutritional Benefits such as High Omega-3 and 
Protein Content. Despite this, sustainability features were still embedded within some of the 
main consumer requirements. For example, "Clear and Informative Labels" and "Appealing and 
Informative Packaging" can convey sustainability aspects. Consumers are likely to appreciate 
local products, as indicated by labels, and the societal benefits of sustainability can be 
communicated through storytelling about the product's origins and its contributions to 
community prosperity.
The gap between consumer perception and scientific conclusions about sustainable packaging 
highlights the necessity for clearer, more comprehensive labeling and more effective 
governmental regulations to drive meaningful change. As Beattie and McGuire (2016) state, 
“Science as usual points the way, but change is first and foremost in the hands of each person.”
This quote encapsulates the essential role of individual action in driving environmental change, 
despite the guidance provided by scientific research. This indicates a crucial need for a 
concerted effort to educate consumers and align their perceptions with the scientific realities of 
sustainability. Only through such efforts can consumer behavior be effectively influenced to 
prioritize sustainability in line with broader environmental goals.

5.5 Design features and improvements from the first impression of 
the product
The field interviews and sensory open-ended discussions revealed several areas for 
improvement in packaging design and imaging. Participants expressed confusion and mixed 
impressions about the product upon initial introduction, indicating a need for clearer 
communication through packaging.
One key area for improvement is the clarity of the product's purpose of use. The product name 
and front packaging should clearly convey the product's intended use. Participants suggested 
incorporating detailed examples of recipes or uses on the backside of the packaging to provide 
consumers with a better understanding of how to use the product effectively.
Additionally, adjustments in the color intensity of the packaging were recommended to make the 
product more visually appealing: “The design is nice, but the color is too intense. A softer blue 
would be more appealing.”. The current blue design was perceived as too intense by 
interviewees above the age of 40, which could impact the product's shelf presence and 
consumer interest.
Another significant finding was the lack of clear indications or hints of blue mussel as an 
ingredient on the front side of the packaging. Participants noted that including design elements 
such as small images or drawings of blue mussels could help address this gap. This would not 
only highlight a key ingredient but also align with consumer expectations for transparency and 
informative packaging.
  
71
==PAGE:78==
Implementing these adjustments aims to enhance the initial impression and overall clarity of the 
product, potentially increasing consumer appeal and understanding. By addressing these 
design elements, the packaging can more effectively communicate the product's benefits and 
usage, leading to a better consumer experience.
5.6 Perception towards frozen convenience foods
One of the barriers identified across qualitative interviews was a negative perception towards 
convenient frozen foods, often associated with unhealthy fast food. Participants frequently avoid 
the frozen food aisle during shopping trips. However, survey responses revealed interesting 
preferences: females tended to prefer frozen products with a slightly more positive ingredient 
list, while males leaned towards fresh convenient products despite additional preservatives. This 
preference divergence might be influenced by the perceived healthiness of the ingredients, 
highlighting a potential discrepancy between online survey responses and in-store behaviors.
The negative perception of frozen foods becomes intriguing when considering trends in 
neighboring countries. For example, Germany's frozen food industry showed significant growth, 
with revenue increasing from 18.5 billion euros in 2022 to 22 billion euros in 2023 (See 
Appendix K). Understanding which consumer groups predominantly drive this growth is pivotal. 
According to the survey, millennials emerge as consistent consumers of frozen convenience 
foods, with 62% consuming them at least once per week.
5.7 Quality Functional Development and House of Quality
Integrating the research findings into the house of quality matrix gives a structure on what to 
focus on going forward in the development, however also highlighting the complexity and 
limitations which can often lead to overlooked correlations within the House of Quality. 
Especially when there are also applied marketing aspects of the product and not only process or 
sensory related characteristics. Therefore, the next step is including other parts of the team to 
implement Method Activity Problem Mode and Effect Analyze (MAPMEA), in order to help 
highlight the overlooked correlations within the quality table and make thorough checks using 
technical and customer correlation matrix. A strong correlation between two customer 
requirements or technical characteristics often results in a similar pattern within the table. Using 
the MAPMEA, involves checking gray shaded cells (Ernzer, 2004). By incorporating this 
approach not only improves the accuracy of the house of quality but also serves as a team 
building exercise, fostering better communication, collaboration and product vision. This 
collective effort ensures a shared understanding of product requirements, leading to informed 
decision making, a more cohesive development process, and a product that better meets 
customer and technical specifications. 
  
72
==PAGE:79==
Furthermore, another framework which would help the team to understand the consumer 
requirements, is the Kano framework to navigate the intended meaning and accurately 
represent consumer desires. This model also assists in ranking features into categories such as 
performance features, basic features, and delighters, ensuring that the development team 
focuses on what truly matters to the consumer (Matzler, 1998).
For example, conducting a field interview with only mockups and no taste samples left 
significant gaps in the feedback, as it is a food product. Thus, conducting sensory tests is crucial 
to address concerns and identify potential performance features and delighters. One field 
interviewee mentioned, “Hope it contains a good meal,” while a sensory test participant noted, 
“It feels quite filling after only one sample.”
For future research, it would be interesting to see how these attributes could be categorized 
within the Kano framework as delighters or performance features. Additionally, exploring in more 
detail what the performance features and delighters are when it comes to the sensory 
experience is important, as delighters often drive the second purchase and build customer 
loyalty. Examples of performance features in Myt Bites include high Omega-3 content and being 
air-fried rather than deep-fried. Potential delighters, which are harder to estimate, could be the 
satiating or filling sensation that participants feel after consuming the product (see Appendix L 
for examples of Kano classification of features).
Finally, the Myt product is the first of its kind in diversifying the application of blue mussels. 
Thus, to what extent can companies and especially food startups listen solely on consumers' 
preferences? As the great quote from Moskowitz's "The mind knows not what the tongue 
wants." 
This quote reflects Moskowitz's insight that consumers often cannot articulate their preferences 
accurately, and it highlights the importance of experimenting with and offering a variety of 
product options to discover what truly satisfies different segments of the market (Gladwell, 
2004).

7. Limitations
7.1 Survey Data Analysis
The study employed choice-based conjoint analysis to understand consumer preferences for 
packaging attributes. However, a limitation arises from the absence of true ratings for each 
packaging option. The use of ratings-based conjoint analysis alongside choice-based conjoint 
analysis could have provided deeper insights. Ratings-based analysis would have allowed for 
direct assessment of individual packaging attributes, potentially offering clearer conclusions, 
particularly for packages with similar profiles.
7.2 Regression Analysis
The study encountered higher standard errors in the regression analysis than anticipated. This 
limitation suggests that a larger participant sample size or a reduction in the number of attribute 
levels might have improved precision and reliability in estimating the relationships between 
packaging attributes and consumer preferences. Despite using coefficients to guide the 
interpretation, personal biases during analysis may have influenced the assessment of attribute 
importance.
7.3 Geographical and Demographic Representation
The study's participant demographics skewed towards younger age groups, with a majority of 
female respondents. While this aligns with the target market for many consumer products, the 
overrepresentation of higher education levels (bachelor's and master's degrees) may limit 
generalizability to populations with vocational education backgrounds. Additionally, a significant 
portion of participants were students, potentially influencing their preferences and perceptions 
differently from employed or non-student demographics.

7. Conclusion 
In conclusion, after investigating the impact of consumer perception and product alignment 
within the context of the emerging food startup Myt, it is evident that sustainability features have 
limited importance in the eyes of consumers compared to other key attributes. This study 
compared the influences of sustainability against various other factors, employing diverse 
methodologies and considering demographic influences on sustainable perception. Despite the 
growing emphasis on sustainability, it remains a secondary concern for most consumers when 
making purchasing decisions.
The conjoint analysis revealed that labels and packaging material were critical for climate 
friendliness, while Healthiness was primarily influenced by Labels and Color/Design. 
Interestingly, consumer preferences varied, with Cardboard being favored for climate-friendly 
packaging and Plastic favored slightly for health related products. The Pure Preference results 
emphasized the importance of Color/Design and Labels, with Price playing a lesser role in 
comparison. However, Price is significantly more important in Pure Preference 14% compared  
to Climate Friendliness 7%. Demographic factors also influenced preferences, with women 
showing a higher willingness to pay premiums for climate-friendly and healthy products. As well 
as demonstrating how highly Danes regard the Danish Organic Label. Lastly the correlation 
between Education & income level and label understanding is not as decisive as research 
suggests. 
Field interviews and sensory discussions underscored the need for clearer and more informative 
packaging to improve initial impressions and overall product clarity. This lack of clarity negatively 
impacted participants' price estimations, as found through the Van Westendorp model, 
especially among older participants. The House of Quality analysis indicated that while Price 
and Nutritional value remained the most significant factor, followed by sensory aspects like great 
taste, as well as Quality, Convenience and Transparency. Sustainability related attributes such 
as "Clear and Informative Labels'' and "Appealing and Informative Packaging" are also highly 
important. However, only a small fraction of consumer requirements are directly related to 
sustainability, suggesting that while environmental factors are important, they must be effectively 
communicated to influence consumer behavior.
Future research should incorporate the Method Activity Problem Mode and Effect Analyze 
(MAPMEA) to enhance the accuracy of the House of Quality framework, identifying overlooked 
correlations and improving team collaboration. Additionally, the Kano framework can help 
categorize product features into performance, basic, and delighters, ensuring a focus on what 
truly matters to consumers. Further studies should also investigate targeted marketing 
strategies to address negative perceptions of frozen convenience foods and explore the 
influence of societal trends and external factors on sustainability perceptions, such as whether 
sustainable business models truly increase customers loyalty. These approaches will provide a 
comprehensive understanding of consumer preferences, aiding in the development of products 
that align more closely with consumer expectations and sustainability goals.
