Abstract
The purposes of this study were to examine the compressive strength and chlo-
ride resistance of concrete with partial cement replacement and partial replace-
ment of aggregates, while investigating non-destructive methods for examination
of chloride present in the concrete. Replacing virgin material in concrete has
environmental benefits, which was the motivation for the first part study, and
in some cases the replacement even improves the properties of the concrete.
Non-destructive tests are beneficial for providing a method to examine concrete
without damaging it, which was the motivation for the second part of the study.
5 different mixtures of concrete were cast and compared in compressive strength,
capillary suction, chloride penetrability and chloride concentration throughout
the samples after exposure to chloride for 7 days. 3 of the mixtures contained
partial replacement to the virgin materials, while 2 reference mixtures were cast
with various w/c-ratio. The material used as partial replacement for cement
were recycled clay bricks (RCB) and the material used as partial replacement
for aggregates were recycled concrete aggregates (RCA). Both materials were
provided as waste material from the Danish construction and demolition.
For non-destructive testing, the ultrasonic pulse velocity (UPV) were measured
before and after chloride exposure, while the surface resistivity were measured
every day whilst the samples were exposed to chloride using the resipod from
PROCEQ. None of the non-destructive methods detected any changes doing
chloride exposure in this specific test arrangement.
The study concluded that the compressive strength for concrete mixtures con-
taining 20wt% RCA or 20wt% RCA preformed well. The compressive strength
decreased remarkably when adding both RCA and RCB in the mixture. Test-
ing the capillary suction, the mixtures containing RCB indicated lower porosity
than mixtures without.
However, the mixtures containing RCB displayed a
lower resistance to chloride, which is surprisingly in relation to earlier studies.

Introduction
It is well known in the construction sector that the production of concrete and
cement in particular are heavy in CO2 emissions. On a global scale, the pro-
duction of cement accounts for more than 7% of the total emissions [1]. Aside
from this contribution to global warming [2], another environmental challenge
in the concrete industry is the massive use of virgin gravel and sand as aggre-
gates [3,4], given that gravel and sand are limited resources. To minimise these
environmental issues, it is of great interest to find ways to partially replace both
the cement and aggregate in concrete.
Earlier studies show that some materials used as partial cement replacement
improves the properties of concrete and mortar, partly because of the filler ef-
fect and especially if the material has pozzolanic abilities [5,6,7,8]. When using
clay bricks or clay brick waste, studies presents enhancements to the properties
with replacement levels up to 20wt% [5,6,7,9] and a satisfying performance with
replacement level of 40wt% [10,11].
Regarding aggregate, studies shows that crushed concrete waste can be reused
as partial replacement of new aggregates. When crushing and sieving concrete,
most of the old mortar ends up in the finest size fraction, compromising the
durability of this fraction [12].
Studies present that the coarse part of the
aggregates can be replaced up to 30wt% with a bare minimum of strength re-
duction depending on the properties and treatment of the RCA [13,14] while
the fine aggregates can be replaced up to 20wt% [15].
This study examinates the properties of concrete mixtures using RCA as par-
tial aggregate replacement and RCB as partial cement replacement in terms
of compressive strength and chloride resistance. As a part of the study, non-
destructive methods for prediction of chloride concentration are tested. Using
non-destructive methods to examinate concrete is beneficial to the industry, as
it predicts the state of the concrete without harming it. The first method tested
is UPV through the concrete samples, which relates to the strength of concrete
[16]. In this study, it is examinated whether the chloride concentration effects
the UPV. The second method examinates measurements of the surface resistiv-
ity using the resipod from PROCEQ, which is known to correlate to chloride
concentration [17]. In this experimental setup, the measurements are conducted
perpendicular to the direction of chloride penetration.

Experimental details
5.1
Compressive strength
The compressive strength were tested following the standard DS/EN 12390-3
with cure times of 7, 28, 60 and 91 days. All tests were conducted with triple
determination, except for mix(RCB) 7 days and mix(RCA) 28 days. These were
preformed with a duplicate determination, due to uncertainties. Before the test,
all cylinders were wet-cured and sanded to ensure an even surface. All tests were
performed on a Servo Hydraulic test machine of the model 300-Ton from Toni
Technik with a test speed of 4.7 kN/s.
5.2
Capillary suction
Before testing, all concrete samples were oven dried at 105◦C until a constant
weight were obtained. A grate were placed in a bowl and distilled water were
poured till 5 mm above the grate. The weight of the samples were noted before
placing them on the grate. After 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 60, 120, 240 and 360 minutes
on the grate, the samples were dubbed dry and the weight were noted. The
capillary suction were calculated based on the weight and area of the samples.

==PAGE:8==
5.3
Chloride resistance
The chloride resistance were examined in 3 steps. First, the samples were sanded
to ensure a plane surface. A chamber with capacity of 250 ml were fixed at both
ends of the cylinders and 500 ml 0.01 M NaNO3 and 2% NaCl (equal to a 0.34
M solution) were pumped through either of the chambers. Electrical potential
with a constant current of 15 mA was applied through the chambers, following
the axial direction of the concrete cylinders. The setup was maintained for 7
days. Second, the concrete samples were freed from the test arrangement, parted
lengthwise, sprayed with 0.1 M AgNO3 to reveal the chloride penetration depth
and oven dried for a day at 105◦C. Third, the first 62 mm of the concrete sam-
ples were sanded in 17 steps from the chloride exposure direction. The powder
were titrated with 0.1 M AgNO3 using a 888 Titrando from Metrohm Ltd. The
chloride concentration were then calculated throughout the concrete cylinder.
The 17 steps are shown in appendix IV, and the test arrangement is shown in
appendix V.
The first 2 steps of the test were conducted with a duplicate determination
except for mix(RCB) 28 days, mix(RCB) 60 days and mix(RCAB) 60 days,
because the equipment were unreliable. The titration and calculation of chlo-
ride concentration through the concrete samples were all conducted with single
determination.
5.4
Non-destructive testing
During the chloride resistance experiment, 2 non-destructive methods were
tested on the concrete samples.
Firstly, a UPV test was conducted on the
concrete cylinders, sending ultrasonic pulses in the axial direction before and
after 7 days of chloride exposure. For this, a pundit lab from PROCEQ were
used. Secondly, the electrical surface resistivity were measured perpendicular
to the direction of the applied electrical potential every day of the chloride ex-
posure using a resipod from PROCEQ. This is clarified in appendix VI. The
surface resistivity were measured in 2 areas for each sample.

Results and discussion
6.1
Compressive strength
Figure 2 compares the development in compressive strength over time of all
5 concrete mixtures. In general, the strength increases over time, except for
mix(RCA) and mix(Ref 0.7), which decreases by 2.5 MPa and 0.6 MPa between
60 and 91 days. The compressive strength of mix(RCA), mix(RCB) and mix(Ref
0.7) are fairly close, while the compressive strength of mix(Ref 0.6) is approx-
imately 10 MPa stronger than these throughout the duration. Mix(Ref 0.6)
was expected to reach a high compressive strength, based on the use of virgin
materials and the low w/c-ratio in comparison to the other mixtures, which is
also the result. Based on earlier studies [9,14], the mix(RCA) were expected to
7
==PAGE:9==
present a weaker compressive strength than the samples with 100% MA. How-
ever, in comparison to mix(Ref 0.7), mix(RCA) preformed well, as this concrete
both contains 20wt% RCA in all size fractions, and has a slightly higher w/c-
ratio, which is known to have a strong influence on the strength of concrete [19].
The strength development of mix(RCB) exceeds the strength development of
mix(Ref 0.7) at 28 days.
This later development of strength is common for
concretes containing pozzolanic material as partial cement replacement at a
20wt% replacement level [5,6,7]. Cement reacts with water and produces cal-
cium silicates hydrates and calcium hydroxide, which the pozzolans convert into
more calcium silicates hydrates, hereby enhancing the strength of the concrete.
Because this last reaction depends on the first reaction, the enhancement is
delayed [20]. These reactions are elaborated in appendix VII. The compres-
sive strength of mix(RCAB) is approximately 10 MPa weaker than mix(RCA),
mix(RCB) and mix(Ref 0.7) throughout the duration. Earlier studies show, that
adding pozzolanic material to a concrete mixture containing RCA enhances the
microstructures and strength in the transaction zone between the RCA and
the new mortar [12,21,22].
However, the results on compressive strength of
mix(RCAB) do not indicate this effect. The lack of performance of this mixture
might relate to the high w/c-ratio, see table 2.

are displayed in appendix VIII.
6.2
Capillary suction
Figure 3 presents the capillary suction of all 5 mixtures. The amount of absorbed
water in the concrete mixtures containing RCB are remarkably lower than that
of the concrete mixtures not containing RCB. This result either suggests that
concrete containing RCB have a high porosity, causing lag of pressure inside

the pores to such levels, that the water is not absorbed [23,24], or that adding
RCB to the concrete lowers the porosity resulting in lower absorption, which
aligns with earlier studies [8,9].
To investigate this, samples of mix(RCA),
mix(RCB and mix(RCAB) were treated with epoxy and viewed in UV-lighting.
The results are shown in figure 4. From these pictures, it seems that the porosity
of concrete containing RCB are lower than of concrete without RCB.

6.3
Chloride resistance
The penetration depth for all 5 mixtures after 7 days of exposure to chloride
were compared and presented in figure 5. Mix(Ref 0.6) presents the lowest pene-
trability and mix(RCAB) presents the highest penetrability after both 28 and 60
days of curing, which suggests a correlation between compressive strength and
resistance to chloride, see figure 2. These findings aggress with earlier studies
[8,12]. The performance of mix(Ref 0.7) and mix(RCA) are relatively uniform,
and all mixtures increase resistance to chloride with increasing curing time.
Both concrete mixtures containing RCB seems less resistance to chloride, which
is surprising. The capillary suction revealed a low porosity for these samples, and
this is known to correlate to a high resistivity to chloride [25]. Also, it is known
from multiple studies, that the addition of pozzolanic materials increases resis-
tance to chloride penetration by lowering the porosity of the concrete [12,26,27].
Clay is known to have a negative electrically charged surface and as a conse-
quence, an electrical double layer appears in the capillaries [23]. Positive ions
in the water will balance the negative surface charge, leading to a positively
charged solution inside the capillaries. When electrical potential is applied over
the experimental setup, the negative anode will attract the positive solution,
causing an electro osmotic flow though the capillaries [23]. However, instead of
explaining the surprising results of chloride penetrability for mixtures contain-
ing RCB, this phenomenon has the opposite effect. The results either suggest
a reverse electro osmotic flow, possibly caused by a positively charged surface
edge of clay [28], or indicate that other factors significantly influence the chloride
penetrability in these samples. A high degree of adsorption in concrete samples
without RCB could be one explanation, and a low tortuosity in the samples
containing RCB could be another explanation.
Figure 5: Chloride penetration depth after 7 days of exposure to chloride. Data
on the penetration depth are shown in appendix X.

The chloride concentrations through the concrete samples are presented in fig-
ure 6. Comparing the results on chloride concentrations after 28 and 60 days
of curing before exposure, the samples which were cured for 60 days tends to
achieve higher concentration of chloride at the first part of the cylinder than
the samples cured for 28 days. The chloride concentration of the sample cured
28 and 60 days are fairly similar at penetration depth of 10 mm to 35 mm,
after which the concentration of samples cured for 28 days exceeds the concen-
tration of samples cured for 60 days. This higher concentration correlates to
the penetration depth, as the penetration depth for the samples cured for 28
days were deeper than for samples cured 60 days, see figure 5. The exception
to this tendency is the 28 days sample of mix(RCAB), which high concentra-
tion of chloride stands out throughout the test. However, 28 days samples of
mix(RCAB) did present low compressive strength and the longest penetration
depth of chloride, which might be an explanation to this high concentration.
If high adsorption of chloride in the concrete mixtures without RCB were the
explanation to the results in figure 5, the chloride concentration in mix(RCA),
mix(Ref 0.6) and mix(Ref 0.7) would be expected to exceed the chloride con-
centration of mix(RCB) and mix(RCAB). However, this does not seem to be
the case when examining the results.

RCB and cured for 60 days before and after 7 days of chloride exposure. Data

no change in UPV are measured, indicating that the presence of chloride has
no effect on the UPV through the concrete. A correlation between compressive
strength and UPV has been found in earlier studies [16], given that the strength
and structure of concrete are correlated [19]. Chloride is not expected to influ-
ence the structure and hence the strength of non-reinforced concrete, which is
also the indication from these results.

Further work
In addition to the discussions and studies in this project, it would be of interest
to look into the following:
• How will the mix(RCAB) preform with a lower w/c-ratio?
• How does mixtures containing RCA preform if the w/c-ratio are lowered?
• How does the pozzolanic reactions enhance the transition zone between
RCA and the new cement paste?
• Is it possible to fix a w/c-ratio when using RCA?
• Is the high penetrability of mixtures containing RCB caused by the electro
osmoses and if so, how are the results if the addition of RCB increases?
• Is the electro osmotic flow inside the capillaries of concrete containing
RCB reversed? And if so, what causes this phenomenon?
• How deep does the chloride have to penetrate the concrete sample in order
for the resipod from PROCEQ to detect it perpendicular, assuming the
measurements are average resistivity of the surface?

Conclusions
Based on the results and discussion of this study, the following conclusions were
drawn:
• The results of this study indicated that a 20wt% replacement of aggregates
with RCA do not result in noteworthy reduction of compressive strength
when comparing with a sample without RCA and a slightly lower w/c-
ratio.
• The compressive strength of the concrete mixture with 20wt% replacement
of cement by RCB exceeded the compressive strength of samples without
RCB after 28 days of curing.
• The concrete sample containing 20wt% replacement of aggregates with
RCA and 20wt% replacement of cement with RCB preformed inferior
regarding compressive strength and chloride resistance in comparison to
the samples containing replacement with only RCA or only RCB in this
study.
• The capillary suction of the concrete mixtures containing RCB suggested
a low porosity compared to the mixtures without RCB.
• The results on chloride penetration depth presented a lower resistance to
chloride for samples containing RCB compared to the mixtures without
RCB, which were a surprising result in comparison to multiple studies.
• The chloride concentrations throughout all samples were quite uniform,
except the mix(RCAB) cured for 28 days before exposure, which showed
a higher concentration of chloride thought the cylinder.
• Chloride concentration does not seem to influent the UPV though the
concrete samples.
• Measuring perpendicular to the direction of chloride exposure, no change
in surface resistivity were detected by the resipod from PROCEQ doing
the 7 days of chloride migration. One possible explanation is that the
resipod measures the average resistivity in an area where the chloride
seems to be too insufficiently present to influence the results.

Appendix II: Casting of concrete samples
In preparation to the casting, the RCA were soaked in water for at least a day.
To mix the concrete mixtures, a stirrer with capacity of 20 l were used. All ma-
terials were collected and weighed beforehand. The aggregates (MA and RCA)
at size fractions 4-8 mm and 8-16 mm were mixed in the stirrer for 30 s. The
aggregate size fraction 0-4 mm and cement (and RCB) were then added to the
mix and stirred for 2 min. Hereafter, the water was pored into the mixer while
stirring, and the mix were stirred an additional 5 min.
The moulds were placed at a vibration table vibrating at 50 Hz.
The con-
crete were poured into the mould until half-full. Then the mould were held
tightly to the vibration table to extricate air bobbles from the concrete mixture
for 30 s. This process were repeated when the mould was 3/4-full and full. The
excess concrete were removed, the moulds were sealed and stored in climate
chambers for a day at 20± 4◦C. Afterwards, the samples were ejected from the
moulds and cured in a reservoir containing limewater at 20± 2◦C for 7, 28, 60
and 91 days. Figure 7 displays some of the steps and equipment used.

Appendix VII: Pozzolanic reaction in concrete.
When cement and water are mixed in concrete, calcium silicates (C-S) in the
cement reacts with water (H) and converts into calcium hydroxide (CH) and
calcium silicate hydrate (C-S-H), the last product providing the concrete with
its strength. The reaction is written in equation 4.
When pozzolanic materials are present, the pozzolan reacts with H and CH
from the first reaction, producing even more C-S-H, see equation 4 [20].
