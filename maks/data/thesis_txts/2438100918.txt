– ii – 
Abstract 
At first, literature study of combined solar collector – ground source heat pump systems was performed. Then, 
the performance of an experimental hybrid solar collector system, which involves a ground thermal storage 
with a heat-pump and solar thermal collectors, was analysed based on measurements of two periods of nine 
days of operation. The daily energy quantities and the hot water tank heat losses were calculated. The 
measurement results revealed that the tank heat loss can be 60 to 70 % of the energy transferred to the tank. 
The seasonal performance factor of the system was 1.9 in the cold and 3.4 in the hot period. Based on the 
results a numerical model was created in the simulation software Polysun. Using the model of the existing 
system, theoretical investigations of different designs were carried out to see the effects of parameter changes. 
The importance of the thermal efficiency of the hot water tank was confirmed by the simulations. The solar 
collector area and ground heat storage size are found also important during the sensitivity analysis. The 
theoretical system with the optimal mix of parameters have been compared to the real system. At last, a brief 
economical comparison with a conventional heating system was carried out. The result of the comparison was 
that the recent system design would costs 33 % to operate but the optimal system would cost 35 % less on a 
yearly basis. 
Keywords: Solar thermal collector, ground heat storage, heat pump, hot water tank heat loss, Polysun, 
parameter analysis

1. Introduction 
 
Development of energy efficient and low CO2 emitting heating systems are one of the main tasks in 
present days in the building energy sector. Solar collectors can produce heat in very efficient way, 
however the heat is produced in the periods when in the time of the year with the least heat demand 
and the produced solar heat is the least in the winter period when it would be the most useful. As heat 
pump systems are becoming more feasible, a promising combination of solar collectors, low 
temperature seasonal heat storage and heat pump system offers a solution for the increasing need of 
sustainable heating for buildings. 
The aim of this project was to analyse the performance of a hybrid solar collector - ground source 
heat pump system, which includes a ground thermal storage. Measurement data were analysed for 
two periods: one in cold season with active space heating and one in hot season with no space heating 
demand. The operation was analysed on two representative days for cloudy and sunny weather in 
both periods. The initial idea was to create a verified numerical model of the system based on the 
measurement results. During the model development phase, it was realised that an accurate verified 
model cannot be created, however a model with the same operation purposes was created.  Using the 
model of the existing system, parameter analysis on the system components was performed and the 
set of optimal parameters is selected.

2. 
Literature study 
 
Ground source heat pump systems can be used for different purposes in various ways. As it is a very 
promising and emerging technology, several research projects are done in the last few years within 
this field. Depending on the application and aim of these projects the system components and 
operation modes can vary, such as: scale of the system, type of the ground heat exchanger, present of 
active ground temperature regeneration, is cooling demand involved, etc. Most of the researches in 
the field of HP usage in buildings can be separated according to: if it is based on experiments or 
simulations (or both), load type (heating and/or cooling), type of heat source (air, water or ground) 
and in case of GSHP, type of ground heat exchanger (borehole or horizontal) and if the system is 
combined with solar collectors. An assortment of the most relevant studies is presented in Table 1. 
Many numerical models are made to simulate operation of SGSHP systems with different parameters, 
such as location, operation mode, temperature levels and component size. It has been found that the 
simulation results can differ from the real-life measurements. The focus of the literature review was 
on projects based on experiments (and verified numerical models) involving solar assisted ground 
source heat pumps (SGSHP) with horizontal ground heat exchangers (GHX).

Different types of ground heat exchangers with solar regeneration are analysed through simulations 
and compared by Ochs et. al [1]. The investigated building is a multiple family house in Bavaria, 
Germany. The annual space heating (SH) demand is 67.5 kWh/m2 and the domestic hot water (DHW) 
demand is 4.8 kWh/m2 (with a temperature of 45 °C). Both the space heating and DHW systems each 
have a 400 l hot water storage tank. The building has a solar assisted ground source heat pump 
(SGSHP) system which is measured for one year. The solar regeneration is done by a 35 m2 solar 
collector array, and the GSHP has a nominal capacity of 12 kW. Based on the results the seasonal 
performance factor (SPF) is calculated for each month, as the ratio of all the heat gain and the 
electricity consumption. The annual average SPF is 3.5, varying between 3.1 and 5.2. These results 
are used as a reference for the simulation results. Five different types of ground heat exchangers are 
analysed: a borehole, a horizontal next to the building, a horizontal under the building, a horizontal 
==PAGE:10==
 
– 3 – 
around the basement and a so-called basket type next to the building. The basket type is a spiral heat 
exchanger, with a spiral diameter comparable to its height, sunk into the ground close to the surface. 
Afterwards mathematical models are created for these different type of heat exchangers in MATLAB 
considering the thermal properties of the involved materials, then the whole SAGSHP system is 
modelled in MATLAB. In the system the domestic hot water (DHW) is produced by solar collectors 
and an electric backup system and the space heating by a heat pump. In a reference system this heat 
pump is an air source heat pump, therefore the DHW and space heating are not connected. In case of 
the ground source heat pump (GSHP) variations, the solar collectors are also connected in parallel to 
the ground heat exchanger to provide heat regeneration. The solar regeneration is active in winter 
time according to the very significant assumption that the heat dissipates in one month before the 
heating season. Furthermore, the solar regeneration is active when the solar collector outlet 
temperature is between 20°C and 30-50°C. Considering these assumptions, it has been found that the 
overall electricity consumption is slightly affected by the solar regeneration and the SPF difference 
of the different source heat pumps and different type of ground heat exchangers is not significant. 
The horizontal ground heat exchanger installed below the building and the basket type ground heat 
exchanger have the largest improvement in the SPF, around +10 and +8 % respectively. The borehole 
type and the horizontal ground heat exchanger next to the building yield around +2 % of improvement 
of the SPF.  It is also mentioned, that the solar regeneration also improves the space heating demand 
in case of the building integrated ground heat exchangers. Without the solar regeneration, the ground 
temperature decreases, which leads to higher heat loss through the floor. With the solar regeneration, 
the space heating demand can be decreased by 8 %, reaching the heat demand of the case where air 
source heat pump is applied, which system does not affect the ground temperatures at all. Long term 
(several years) operation was not investigated in the study. The main features of the system are 
summarized in Table 2.

Another analysis showed that the solar regeneration could increase the overall efficiency of the GSHP 
system with 3-4% [2]. The purpose of the work is to investigate the effect of thermal regeneration. 
The system involves a SGSHP with borehole ground heat exchangers to provide heat to an office 
building in Poland. The ground heat exchanger consists of 5 boreholes with depth of 78 meters. Only 
two of the boreholes are able to be regenerated, which gives the opportunity to compare the 
temperature of the regenerated and non-regenerated ground. The heated floor area is 533 m2 and the 
heat demand is 38 kWh/m2 per year. The nominal capacity of the heat pump is 17 kW, serving only 
==PAGE:11==
 
– 4 – 
the space heating (the floor heating requires 35 °C) and the COP varies between 3.94 and 5.56 
depending on the borehole temperature, indicating the importance of temperature regeneration. The 
solar collector area is only 6 m2 with a thermal peak power of 5 kW, which is quite small considering 
the heat demand. The collector area is sized only for the regeneration of one borehole. The system 
consists a temporary heat storage of 400 l hot water, which is connected to the space heating loop. 
The solar collectors provide the heat for the storage tank if their temperature is sufficient, otherwise 
the heat pump is used to heat the water in the tank. The solar regeneration of the ground is done 
through an intermediary hot water tank of 200 l, to protect the boreholes from overheating, as the 
maximum inlet temperature for the boreholes is 30 °C. The SCOP of the system during the three years 
of operation was 4.05, 3.75 and 4.17 respectively including the circulation pumps consumption and 
the production of the solar collectors. The drop of the SCOP in the second year is due to an increase 
of space heating supply temperature by 5 °C. The detailed results show that the solar fraction is around 
5-7 % during the three years and reaches values above 30 % typically in early spring. Regarding the 
regeneration of the one available borehole, it is found that the regeneration can be done during 
summer by injecting around 66 % of the heat extracted during heating season. Approximately 15 % 
more heat can be extracted from the regenerated borehole compared to the non-regenerated ones. 
Furthermore, the temperature is more stable in the long term due to the regeneration. During the 
investigation period, the temperature of the borehole with only natural regeneration shows a steady 
decrease of 0.8 K per year, while the actively regenerated borehole can keep its original temperature. 
As the temperature is measured on three levels along the borehole (2, 27 and 77 meters) the long-
term behaviour of different depths of the ground can be also analysed. It was found that the 
temperature decrease in the non-actively regenerated borehole is more significant in the upper layers 
of the ground, while in the lowest layer it maintains the same temperature at the beginning of the 
heating season over the three years. However, despite the more stable temperatures in the lower 
layers, the borehole outlet temperature decreases over years as it is presented previously. Based on 
the measurement data of these two boreholes, two theoretical cases were further analysed: where all 
the five boreholes are regenerated actively and where solar regeneration is not present. The 
calculations show that the solar regeneration could bring an improvement of 3-4 % of the SCOP on 
an overall system level, which increases during the years. The main features of the system are 
summarized in Table 3.

A numerical model for a SGSHP system with horizontal GHX was developed and simulations for 
several European locations were run by Girard et. al. [3]. The system setup and size is comparable to 
the recently investigated system. The aim of the study was to analyse the COP improvement by 
utilizing solar heat at different climates and the economic feasibility of the project at each location. 
In this system the solar collectors (with an area of 6 m2) provide heat to a 100 l buffer tank and then 
the heat transferred to the HP or the GHX. By doing this, the overheating of the ground can be avoided 
and furthermore the heat transfer is more balanced, the solar heat can be provided more continuously 
during the day and night which is better for the evaporator of the HP and for the heat exchange in the 
ground. The simulated horizontal ground heat exchanger has a two pipes layout in 2 m deep with 0.3 
m spacing. The pipe inner/outer diameter is 0.008/0.01 m respectively. This heat exchanger setup is 
very similar to the recently investigated system. The heated floor area in the building is 108 m2 and 
the heating demand depends on the location. The supply temperature of the space heaters is 47 °C. 
The SPF (or SCOPsys as it called in this case) is calculated in this model is the ratio of the heat 
provided by the HP and the sum of the consumption of the HP compressor and circulation pumps in 
the system. The effect of the solar assistance is counted as the increased evaporator temperature of 
the HP which also means increased COP. The economic calculations involve the installation costs, 
the maintenance and the operation costs, which is the price of consumed electricity. Three heating 
systems were calculated and compared: electric radiant heaters (COP=1), GSHP (ground source heat 
pump without solar assistance) and SGSHP. The results show that during the year the system 
performs the best in the transitional seasons (spring and autumn) as the heat demand is higher than in 
summer, due to the still existing need for space heating, but the solar irradiance is much higher than 
in winter, therefore the HP evaporator temperature increases which means higher COP and lower 
electricity consumption. The comparison of the three different heating systems shows, that depending 
on the location the solar assistance decreases the electricity consumption by about 2-6 % and the heat 
pump systems could consume 80 % less compared to the electric radiant heating. The advantage of 
solar heat utilization is the least at the most Nordic location, Bergen, where the heating demand is 
significant (15 000 kWh/year) and the SCOPsys values are the lowest of all locations, 4.3 without 
and 4.4 with the solar regeneration. The electricity consumption decreases only by 2 % by the solar 
assistance. On the other hand, at the most Southern location, Murcia, the space heating demand is 
only the third of Bergen’s (5 400 kWh/year), but the SCOPsys is 5.1 for the GSHP and 5.8 for the 
SGSHP systems. The difference in the yearly electricity consumption between the GSHP and the 
SGSHP is about 12 %, due to the high SCOPsys value improvement and the low heating demand. 
The economical calculations show however that the simple payback time of the GSHP and SGSHP 
systems is the lowest in the northern countries, 5.8 and 8.5 years respectively, while 16 and 23 years 
in the southern countries. The reason is that the payback time is calculated by the overall electricity 
saving between the electric radiant heater and the HP systems, which is higher when the space heating 
demand is higher. It has been concluded, that the installation of SGSHP systems is the most beneficial 
at locations where the solar irradiance is quite high, just as the space heating demand. This could 
mean mountainous areas in southern countries. The main features of the system are summarized in 
Table 4.

One of the important findings was that the SPF of a system can significantly change, depending on 
how it is calculated. The COP of the heat pumps is defined by the manufacturers in standard 
conditions and depends on the heat source and heat sink temperatures. According to this definition, 
the COP of the investigated ground source heat pumps varies between 4.2 and 4.7, while for the air 
source heat pumps the value is between 3.2 and 4.2. The SPF is defined as the ratio of produced heat 
and the power consumption over a period of time. Different SPF values can be calculated as: if only 
the HP (SPF0), or the ground heat exchanger (GHX) pump consumption (SPF1), the backup heater 
production and consumption (SPF2) or the storage charging pumps (SPF3) are also included in the 
calculations. The calculation boundaries for GSHPs are well illustrated in Figure 1, and the SPF value 
variation is shown in Figure 2. It can be seen that the influence of the GHX and the storage charging 
pumps are the most significant.

Another result of the study is that SPF for both the GSHP and the air source heat pumps (ASHP) 
greatly depends on the heat sink temperature, namely the type of heat load. The heat pumps can 
produce heat for space heating and for DHW. More than 90 % of space heating is floor heating, which 
requires 35°C, while the DHW has to be at least 50°C to avoid the risk of legionella. As high COP of 
a heat pump means higher heat source temperature and lower heat sink temperature, when the heat 
pump produces DHW the COP becomes lower. Therefore, when the heat demand out of the heating 
season is DHW dominant, the SPF has lower values. This is shown on Figure 3. It can be noticed, 
that the highest SPF values are reached in the transitional seasons (March-May and October-
November). The reason is that the demand side is more space heating driven but the ambient 
temperature is higher than in winter time. On the other hand, the COP of the ASHP is heavily 
influenced by the air temperature, causing the lowest SPF values in the winter time and the highest 
values in the transitional periods as it is discussed before, Figure 4.

3. 
System description 
 
The system which is analyzed in this project is a solar assisted ground source heat pump system which 
provides domestic hot water and space heating for a single-family house. A simplified layout of the 
system is presented on Figure 5. The collectors can provide heat for the DHW tank or can be switched 
into series with the ground heat storage loop to assist the GSHP, by pre-heating the fluid coming from 
the ground heat storage. The GSHP produces space heating directly and can produce DHW if it is 
needed. The GSHP can extract heat only from the ground heat storage or as mentioned, with solar 
assistance. The space heating is provided directly by the GSHP without buffer storage. 
The main system (which includes the heat pump, hot water tank, pumps, valves and the control 
system) is designed and built by Free Energy A/S and forms a compact unit, called hybrid solar 
system (HYSS). The HYSS unit is connected to the solar collectors, ground heat storage and the 
domestic hot water and heating system of the building. The system is designed for multiple purposes; 
therefore, besides the recently added equipment, it can be expanded with additional solar collector 
fields, air source heat pumps, other auxiliary heaters, air conditioners and pool heating systems. [6]

3.2. 
Ground heat storage 
 
The ground heat storage is made of a horizontal ground heat exchanger and insulation layers in the 
soil. It is designed by Norconsult A/S. The layout of the heat storage is depicted in Figure 8. The 
concept of the storage is that heat can be extracted from the soil and used by the ground source heat 
pump (GSHP) and also excess solar heat can be transferred to the storage, therefore the soil can be 
regenerated. This regeneration is very important in the long term, as it is discussed previously [2]. 
Beside the active regeneration the ground can be heated above the natural temperature, hence 
additional heat can be stored. In case of GSHP without active regeneration the soil is thermally 
regenerated naturally, which means the solar radiation and the ambient air heat the surface of the 
ground and this heat is conducted to the deeper ground layers, therefore no insulation is necessary, as 
it would block this process. In case of the recent heat storage, higher ground temperatures could occur, 
therefore the heat would dissipate towards the surface, which means energy loss from the system. To 
avoid this, an insulation layer with 500 mm thickness is placed 1 m beneath the surface, to make the 
storage resistant to the outside weather conditions. It means that the active heat storage volume starts 
at 1 m below the surface, and there is a protective 500 mm layer of soil above the insulation cover. 
Next to this heat storage there is another ground heat exchanger installed, thus the two systems are 
separated by a vertical insulation layer of 300 mm, as it is shown on Figure 8. 
The pipes are made of polyethylene (PE80) with an outer/inner diameter of 25/21 mm. The total pipe 
length is 200 m and it is laid with a spacing of 30 mm. The heat exchanger consists 2 parallel loops 
(blue and red) to decrease pressure loss. These loops are connected by a manifold in a well and 
connected directly to the HYSS unit. The layout is designed in such a way that the flow from the 
HYSS system enters in the center of the storage (which is in this case is next to the vertical insulation 
wall) and exits at the side. By doing this, the heat is concentrated in the center of the storage to further 
decrease heat losses. The heat exchanger net area is 50 m2 and the insulation layer close to the surface 
is 90 m2. The insulation layer has a larger area than the net area of the ground heat exchanger, because 
the fact that the heat is conducted also in horizontally, which would mean additional heat loss to the

surface. Photos of the installation works of the ground heat storage is shown on Figure 9.

According to the measurements, the soil at the depth of the heat exchanger (3 m) is mostly clayish,

the storage, hence there is no risk for that. 
 
3.3. 
Hot water storage 
 
The hot water tank is an RI TC 200L type twin coil hot water tank designed by OSO Hotwater A/S 
and it is part of the HYSS. It is chosen to be able to cover the DHW consumption of a family with 4 
persons. It has a volume of 200 l and provides only DHW, as the space heating is provided directly 
by the GSHP. The tank design is shown on Figure 10. The DCW enters the tank at the bottom trough 
a pipe from the top and the DHW is tapped at the top, to support the natural thermal stratification in 
the tank. The tank has two internal spiral heat exchangers, one at the lower and one at the upper part 
of the tank. The lower coil is connected to the solar collectors and the upper one is connected to the 
GSHP, which is the auxiliary heater as the heat pump is set to produce the desired temperature, hence 
it can be connected directly in the higher layers. There is an electric heating element installed in the 
tank, but it is not used, therefore the GSHP is the only auxiliary energy source. The coils have 0.8 m2 
of heat exchanger area. The water temperatures in the tank are measured at the middle height of the 
two coils. The insulation is ECO foam (thermal conductivity: 0.025 W/mK) with a thickness of  
~40 mm on the sides and ~50 mm on the top and bottom.

3.4. 
Heat pump 
 
The heat pump in the system is a single stage water/water heat pump. The refrigerant is R410a. The 
compressor in the heat pump is controlled by frequency modulation. The rated heating power of the 
heat pump is 4 kW at 50 Hz, but as the frequency modulation range is 30-70 Hz, the heating power 
can be varied between 2 and 5 kW. At higher frequencies the frequency inverter in the heat pump 
consumes more electricity. The actual heating power of a heat pump (at a given electricity 
consumption), also always depends on the inlet and outlet temperatures. In other words, to reach the 
same heating power at different source and sink side temperatures, the power used by the heat pump 
(electricity for the compressor) changes. For example, lower temperature difference between the 
source and the sink side requires less work by the heat pump, therefore lower electricity consumption. 
The ratio of the useful heat and the required electric power is called coefficient of performance (COP) 
and it is an important measure of heat pumps, which can change during operation. The theoretical 
COP is calculated according Equation 1. The COP can also be defined as the ratio of hot side and 
cold side mean temperatures (Equation 2), however this formula gives the theoretical COP. In reality 
the COP is much lower because of the imperfections of the system. 
 
 
==PAGE:23==

This heat pump is operated at two frequency levels; one to produce space heating and another to serve 
as an auxiliary heater for the DHW tank. The sink side inlet/outlet temperature of the heat pump for 
space heating is 30/35 °C and 47/55 °C for the DHW production. The inlet temperature can widely 
vary for ground source heat pumps used in solar assisted systems, as the incoming temperature from 
the ground can be as low as 5 °C and in case of solar assistance (when heat is coming from the solar 
collectors directly) it can be as high as 43 °C. This maximum is set in order to avoid too high pressure 
in the evaporator, which could cause damage in the device. The heat pump has been tested previously 
in a laboratory environment (by the Danish Technological Institute), to define its COP at different 
source/sink side temperatures. The source side inlet-outlet temperature difference is 3 K. The results 
are presented in Figure 11 and Figure 12. It can be seen by the test results that the COP at the higher 
sink side outlet temperature is lower in general. As an effect, the electricity consumption is higher at 
the same heating power.

3.5. 
Domestic hot water and space heating demand 
 
As the system is an experimental test setup, the domestic hot water and space heating demand is 
simulated as predefined energy tappings by a profile. The profiles are defined to simulate the needs 
of a single-family household. The heat is tapped into a large uninsulated tank. 
The domestic hot water setpoint temperature is 50 °C, the inlet cold water is 10 °C. Three equal 
(ca. 33 l) tappings occur a day: at 6:40, 11:40 and 18:40. The daily tapping volume is 100 liters, hence 
the daily DHW consumption is equal to 4.5 kWh. 
The space heating demand is calculated in TRNSYS, to represent the heating demand of a single-
family house with a space heating demand of 30 kWh/m2/year (SFH30) which is equal to 4700 
kWh/year. The supply temperature setpoint is set between 20 and 30 °C, according to the heating 
curve shown on Figure 13. The return temperature is 20 °C and the flow rate is 5 l/min, similar to 
usual floor heating systems. There is no space heating in the period May – September. Based on this 
data set, operation of an automatic valve is described for the adequate heat draw-off on a minute 
based schedule.

Measurement set up 
 
The monitoring and controlling system requires numerous temperature and flow rate sensors in the 
ground heat storage, the collector, ground, space heating and DHW loops and in the hot water tank. 
As the heat pump, the DHW tank and many pumps and valves are built in the HYSS unit, the 
measurement data is partly provided by the monitoring system of the test facility (called PTF) and 
partly by the controlling HYSS system. The temperature measurements in the ground is provided by 
the monitoring system. 
The temperature sensor installation in the ground heat storage is shown on Figure 14. The temperature 
sensors are placed in boreholes at multiple depths. In the heat storage (in borehole B1+, B2+, B3+ 
and B4+) the sensors are placed in four layers: -1.1 m, -2 m, -2.9, -4 m. The first measurement point 
represents the top of the ground heat storage and the third represents the depth of the ground heat 
exchanger. In the additional two boreholes (B1, B4) the sensors are placed in ten layers with a step 
of 1 m. The borehole B1 serves as a reference point and also to observe the effect of the heat storage 
in the ground temperature further from the heat storage.

3.7. 
System operation 
 
The six main operation strategies of the control system are presented on  
Figure 16. The DHW production is triggered by the T9 temperature when it drops below 50 °C. 
Solar heat is transferred to the tank (P1 is on and V1=A) for direct DHW heating when the 
collector outlet temperature is between 40 and 75 °C (Figure 16. a) until T2 reaches 70°C in 
heating season or 85°C out of the heating season. If the collector outlet temperature is between 
15 and 40 °C, the GSHP is used in solar assistance mode (P1 is on and V1=B), to utilize solar 
heat even at these low temperatures (Figure 16. c). If it is needed in this case, colder fluid from 
the ground heat storage can be mixed with the fluid coming from the solar collectors, to prevent 
too high heat pump cold side inlet temperature, as it is described in section 3.4. The solar 
assistance mode is used also to preheat the fluid coming from the ground when the HP is in 
operation to increase the COP in these periods. When solar heat is not available at all, the GSHP 
utilizes the heat from the ground heat storage (Figure 16. b).

user, depending on the DHW demand.

4.1. 
Long period ground measurements 
 
The ground temperatures are measured from January 2018 and the system has started operation 
in February, the last data for analysis is from 4th of June. The ground temperatures from January 
to June measured at 1 and 1.1 meter below the surface are depicted on Figure 17. It can be seen, 
that the ambient air temperature, Ta, was 0 ± 10 °C until April. Since April a gradual increase 
of air temperature started, and the daily air temperature deviations are also increased. The 
temperatures in the top ground layer are selected to present the correlation between the ground 
and the air temperature and the effect of the insulation layer. The close-to-surface temperature 
in borehole B4 shows the strongest correlation to the air temperature. B1+ and B1 also show 
correlation. These three boreholes are located where there is no insulation cover, in addition B4 
is located in the middle of the lawn seen on Figure 14, where the cooling effect of wind is the 
strongest. The steadiest temperatures are measured below the insulation cover, at B2+, B3+ and 
B4+. It can be also observed within these boreholes, that the closer to the middle of the heat 
storage they are, the higher temperatures they have, as the heat is concentrated in the middle of 
the heat storage, next to and below the thick insulation layers. The slight temperature drop on 
13th of March can be explained due to snow melting. The borehole B4+ is close to the well of 
the pipe manifolds, in which probably icy water from snow melting entered, which decreased 
the temperature in the surrounding soil temporarily.   
Even though the borehole B1 is located 20 meters away from the ground heat storage, the 
temperatures in the borehole in the corresponding layers are almost the same as in borehole B4 
which is next to the ground heat storage as it is depicted on Figure 18. It can be seen that the 
temperature in -10 m is relatively constant, while the temperatures in 6 m and -3 m are highly 
effected by the ambient temperatures and the discharge of energy from the ground from the 
neighbor ground source heat exchanger, which is mentioned in section 3.2. The other ground 
heat exchanger is connected to another solar assisted ground heat source heat pump system. 
That system started extracting heat from the ground since 6th of March and started to transfer 
heat into the ground since 24th of April. The temperature drop on 13th of March at -3 m in the 
borehole B1 happened probably for the same reason as in case of B4+.

4.2. 
System operation analysis  
 
Since the system was not always fully operational within the investigated period, two periods 
of nine days are selected where the whole system was working. The first one is from 1st to 9th 
of March, which represents the winter season, as the weather was cold at that time and the space 
heating was in operation. The second one is from 1st to 9th of May, which represents the summer 
season, as the weather was warm at that time and there was no space heating demand. 
The weather conditions are measured at the climate station of the Department of Civil 
Engineering of DTU, which is located 150 m from the test facility. The main weather conditions 
in the mentioned periods are presented on Figure 20 and Figure 21, by the ambient air 
temperature and the global irradiance. The global irradiance is the total solar irradiance (the 
direct irradiance plus the diffuse radiation) on a horizontal surface. The heat production of the 
solar collectors depends on the total irradiance on the collector surface, which can be calculated 
from the global irradiance and the horizontal diffuse radiation by means of solar radiation 
processing models. Therefore, the global irradiance is a good indicator to understand the solar 
heat production. 
In the period in March the ambient temperature was mainly around 0 °C, with a minimum of -
7 °C and a maximum at one day of 10 °C. The global irradiance also shows low values with a 
maximum of nearly 600 W/m2. The fluctuation and generally low values of the global 
horizontal irradiance during daytime referred to cloudy weather, except 6th of March. In the 
period in May the ambient temperature showed a gradually increasing pattern. It never dropped 
below 3 °C and it reached as high as 27 °C. The global irradiance was around twice as high as 
in March, reaching 800 W/m2 almost every day. Just as in March, the fluctuation in the first 
few days (except the 2nd of May) refers to cloudy weather, however the smooth curve in the last 
days means that the sky was clear on those days.

4.4. 
Hot water tank heat loss 
 
The thermal efficiency of the hot water tank plays an important role in the efficiency of every 
hot water system. As the results of the measurements show, 60-70 % of the heat transferred to 
the hot water tank can be lost. This can be avoided with an improved tank design and right 
operation. 
The thermal efficiency of a hot water tank can be defined by the heat loss coefficient of the 
tank. This parameter shows how much is the heat flow rate from the tank to the surroundings 
by every kelvin temperature difference between the inside of the tank and the surroundings. 
This temperature difference is the driving force of the heat flow. If the heat loss coefficient and 
the temperature conditions are known over a period, the heat loss can be calculated. This method 
can be reversed and applied on the measurement results to find the heat loss coefficient of the 
tank [7]. 
The time period used in the calculations is important, as it is shown in the previous section the 
daily heat loss can widely vary. More reliable results can be obtained for longer periods. The 
data available for the investigation is from only two periods of nine days where the whole 
system was in operation. The operation strategy also important in this case, which will be 
explained later. Therefore, the heat loss coefficient is calculated separately for the two periods. 
After the total heat loss is known for the periods, the average tank temperature has to be 
calculated. Only two temperature sensors are placed inside the tank for the control system, T2 
and T9 (HYSS). The average temperature of the two layers is taken as the average tank 
temperature, however the more temperature sensors installed the more accurate are the results. 
In the same time, the average surroundings temperature (Tindoor) is calculated. From this dataset 
the heat loss coefficient of the tank is calculated on a daily basis and also for the whole periods. 
The results are presented in Table 14 and Table 15.

5. 
Numerical model 
 
Real life solar heating systems can be analyzed only after a certain amount of measurement data 
is available. Therefore, analysis of a year of operation requires measurement data of a whole 
year in ideal case. In reality sensor or data logging malfunctions, system component break 
downs can occur, therefore analysis of certain periods cannot be done. The two periods of nine 
days in the previous section is considered as short representative periods of two seasons of the 
year. General conclusions regarding the yearly performance of the system cannot be made based 
on those results. Performance of heating systems is often given on yearly basis, but at least one 
year. This is the case of the studied projects in section 2, the analyzed periods were 1-5 years 
long. 
Simulation software are developed to help in designing solar heating systems. In the numerical 
models the results are calculated for the whole simulation period and each parameters of the 
components (e.g. flow rate, temperature) can be presented. This gives the opportunity to prove 
system concepts and make parameter analysis. This would be very costly and time demanding 
in a real experimental case. However, the software has to be complex enough to be able to 
simulate real life conditions and have to handle several component variants. 
The chosen simulation software is Polysun (version 10) which is developed by Vela Solaris 
AG. Polysun is used as a designing tool for solar thermal systems, it contains numerical models 
of marketed equipment and templates of several system setups. 
The model creation and simulation process in Polysun basically is as follows: 
- 
The location of the system must be set. This provides the right horizon data (trace of the 
Sun) and weather data from the database. The weather conditions from an external file 
can be imported at this step. 
- 
The desired components of the system are chosen from the catalog and/or new ones can 
be defined by the user. The selected components are placed on a graphical interface, 
then are connected by pipes. 
- 
The element parameters (dimensions, positioning, material properties, space heating, 
DHW requirements etc.) are set. 
- 
The controllers for the active equipment (pumps, valves, auxiliary heaters) are selected 
and the control strategy is made with the required input/output parameters. 
- 
After the model system is created and set, yearly simulations can be run. The results are 
presented right after the simulation is done. 
The main approach of the project was to create a measurement results based verified numerical 
model of the analyzed system. A verified model means that with the same test conditions 
(weather data, energy tapping profile) the numerical model which is identical to the real system 
provides the same results for the same time periods as the measurements. During the 
development of the model it has been realized that the initial goal cannot be met entirely due to 
the following reasons: 
1. One of the main reasons why the same operation of the test system cannot be simulated 
in Polysun is that detailed space heating profile could not be added to the system. In the 
==PAGE:50==
 
- 43 - 
 
real system the space heating is tapped automatically based on a predefined minute 
based profile. This procedure can trigger certain operation modes or put the system in 
idle mode, which can effect the overall operation of the system. 
2. The weather data and space heating, DHW tapping profile can be imported to the 
program only in hourly basis. However, the software calculates in varying timesteps as 
short as a few seconds, the input data for each time step is calculated based on 
intermediate values of the imported hourly data. As a result, rapid changes of some 
parameters are not present in the program, however these rapid changes are included in 
the measurements. Polysun is designed for general simulation purposes, such detailed 
results are not expected. 
3. The heat pump installed in the HYSS system could not be implemented in the model of 
the system, as the available heat pump measurement data were not enough to define a 
proper model. The calculation results with the not proper model were unreliable, 
therefore a similar heat pump had to be selected to perform acceptable simulations. 
After realizing these constrains it has been decided to create a model which has the same 
operation principles with component parameters closest to the real system parameters. The 
space heating demand is defined for passive house standards. As the “ECO mode” has a large 
effect on the system performance, two simulation variants are made to simulate when this 
operation mode is on or off.

The location of the system is given by coordinates (56° N 12°E) and the weather data is selected

The solar collector Hewalex TLP ACR 2600 present in the Polysun catalog and it has been used

An external heat exchanger had to be applied in the model for the following reason. Polysun 
does not allow to connect the P1 and P2 pumps as it is in the real system (Figure 15). In certain 
conditions (e.g. in solar assistance mode) the connected solar collector loop and ground loop 
would have several flow rate producers, which Polysun cannot handle. The solution for this 
problem is to implement an external heat exchanger between the two loops to provide hydraulic 
decoupling. In this way the pumps cannot interfere with each other, but heat can be transferred 
between the loops. This is a common solution in similar system models in the software. The 
heat transfer capacity is 30 kW/K and the heat loss is minimal, in order to be able to provide 
close to perfect heat exchange. The temperature difference between the two sides is negligible.   
A ground heat storage model is available in the program. The dimensioning of the storage and 
material properties (density, specific heat, etc.) are added to the model to get a close 
representation of the real storage. The initial temperatures of the heat storage layers are added 
according to the ground temperature measurements on 1st of January. 
The heat pump is selected to be a standard brine/water heat pump with a capacity of 5 kW. This 
model is selected as it has the closest parameters to the heat pump used in the HYSS system 
and this model works reliably in combined DHW/direct space heating applications in other 
models. 
The hot water tank is defined according to the its dimensions and main parameters described in 
section 3.3. The thermal bridge values of the connections are defined according to section 4.4. 
In the simulations the heat loss of the bypass pipe was not as much as expected. The heat loss 
of the tank and the pipe together were not as much as the measured values. It has been fun that 
the thermal bridges of the tank connections have to be increased to solve this problem. Two set 
of values are defined: one for the “ECO mode” off variant, where the calculated 5 W/K value 
is distributed between the connections of the two heat exchanger coils, the DCW inlet, DHW 
outlet ports and one set for the “ECO mode” on variant, in which 11.9 W/K is distributed 
between the connections. The values are weighted by the position of the connections, the values 
are lower close to the bottom of the tank and higher close to the top of the tank. The highest 
values are set for the top connection, as the uninsulated pipe for the “ECO mode” bypass causes 
the largest heat loss. The hot water tank has 12 thermal stratification layers. The temperature of 
the third layer from the bottom is selected as the T2 (HYSS) temperature in the measurements 
and the temperature of the seventh from the bottom corresponds to T9 (HYSS). 
The DHW tapping is set the same as it is described in section 3.5. 
The space heating demand represented by a simplified building model and floor heating system. 
The building module simulates the indoor temperature of a building in a quasi-dynamic way, 
which means that the indoor temperature is calculated by the predefined annual space heating 
demand per square meter, the total heated area, the heat provided by the heating system and the 
outdoor temperature. The total heated area is 130 m2. The space heating demand is set to 15 
==PAGE:52==
 
- 45 - 
 
kWh/m2 annual, as the building is considered to be a passive house. A floor heating module is 
connected to the building model, which has a nominal supply temperature of 25 °C, return 
temperature of 20 °C and flow rate of 7 l/min. These values are set by analysing the 
measurements in section 4.2.1, where it has been concluded that the flow rate in the space 
heating loop is probably too low. The heating capacity of the floor heating is set by the software 
to meet the maximal space heating demand. 
The controller units control the different equipment and provide together the same control 
strategy as for the real system. The temperature setpoints and flow rates are set to be the same 
as what is set and measured in the real-life system (except the space heating). 
The control scheme of P1 and P2 ensures when solar heat is transferred to the ground loop, flow 
occur on both sides of the external heat exchanger. However, flow in the ground loop can 
present without flow on the other side of the heat exchanger. A mixing valve is places at the 
inlet of the source side of the heat pump to protect the heat pump from too high source side 
temperatures. It mixes the fluid coming from the external heat exchanger with colder fluid from 
the ground heat storage, to provide maximum 43 °C as source temperature for the heat pump. 
The DHW production of the heat pump has a priority over space heating just like in the analysed 
test system. The space heating is triggered by the calculated indoor temperature of the building 
model. The heat pump provides space heating when the indoor temperature is below the heating 
setpoint by 3 K and stops when the indoor temperature reaches the setpoint. The heating setpoint 
is 20 °C during the day and 18 °C during the night. The pump for the “ECO mode” on variant 
operates in the periods when the heat pump produces DHW. The pump is not activated in the 
“ECO mode” off variant. 
After defining all the system components and controllers, yearly simulations are performed.

6. 
Conclusion 
 
The literature study presented that the analyses and development of hybrid solar collector 
ground source heat pump systems is a complex area and requires good knowledge of the field. 
Under the right operation conditions these systems can operate efficiently. The complexity of 
these systems can lead to malfunctions which can be avoided by careful design, but also means 
that further research and development required in this field. 
Performing continuous measurement of a hybrid solar system can be challenging as sensor and 
system component malfunctions can occur. The test setup and parameters to be measured have 
to be selected carefully, to be able to measure all the desired parameters of the system. Long 
term measurements are required to define the performance of such a system, as its performance 
changes over the year. 
Simulation tools are available and widely used to assist the design and analysis of complex 
solar thermal systems. However, creating a measurements verified, accurate model is 
challenging. The real life conditions may cannot be simulated, but close to reality model 
systems can be created. 
Both the measurements and the simulations have revealed, that the efficiency of the hot water 
tank in heating systems is crucial. A high performing tank, well designed tank can significantly 
reduce the heat losses in the system. It has been proved that thermal bridges of the connections 
of the hot water tank (especially on the top) can be the reason for most of the heat losses. 
Furthermore, certain operation features (the “ECO mode” in this project) in a heating system 
can increase the heat losses even more. 
The most important components with a simulation model can be found by sensitivity analysis. 
The optimal system setup can be found by the results of the sensitivity analysis. It has been 
calculated that the optimal design of a complex hybrid solar collector system can change if the 
system worth to replace a conventional heating system or not. 
Further analysis of the recently studied system is needed to evaluate its performance. The 
numerical model can be improved to be able to simulation the operation of the real system. 
Additional sensitivity analysis should be performed regarding change of heat demand, hot water 
tank design and change in operation strategies. 
The hybrid solar collector ground source heat pump systems with seasonal ground heat storages 
offer a promising alternative for the conventional heating systems in certain conditions. 
I believe that these systems will develop in the future, and their application will be common 
practise.
