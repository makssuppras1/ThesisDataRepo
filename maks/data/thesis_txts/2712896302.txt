Abstract
Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ) is closely linked with health and com-
fort of occupants.
A poor IEQ can lead to short-term health effects such as
headaches, diminished concentration, and fatigue and in severe cases can
have fatal or terminal effects through long-term exposure.
In offices, the
effects of poor IEQ can lead to reduced productivity and increased sick leave,
which manifests as costs due to loss of work, productivity, and worker benefits.
Buildings in Denmark designated as office, trade, inventory, incl. public adminis-
tration, amount to approximately 73.5 million m2, and costs related to salaries and
benefits for office personnel, typically cover 90% of a business’ operational costs.
Considering that roughly 36% of the European workforce works in office environ-
ments and that 40% of Denmark’s energy consumption comes from the building
sector, great benefits can be achieved by optimising the perceived indoor climate.
Despite this, efforts towards reducing building energy in Denmark has his-
torically been shown to be focused on reducing electricity and heat, omitting IEQ
and occupant health. This master’s thesis aims to elucidate the invisible risks
that IEQ poses to health and performance, making them more visible, measur-
able, and comprehensible to both occupants and building owners. Effectively
enabling building owners to evaluate and view IEQ optimisations more favourably.
This thesis measured and investigated the indoor environment of 13 office
rooms within 4 buildings using various IoT sensors (objective) and occupant
questionnaires (subjective). Furthermore, using a benchmarking tool developed
through a comprehensive literature study and iterated through feedback from
supervisors, a correlation between IEQ and occupant productivity could be drawn.
This project resulted in a final dashboard containing a complete benchmarking
tool. The tool consists of the weighted evaluation of 26 objective and subjective
IEQ sub-parameters, a rating scheme, and an estimation of financial benefits, all
visualised succinctly in a comprehensive, adaptive, and user friendly dashboard.
Given efforts towards reducing building energy is not focused on IEQ, this
thesis concluded that the benchmarking tool and dashboard is an excellent
way to summarise and contextualise building performance with respect to both
productivity and economics, should the IEQ be optimised. In closing, this project
provides a user-friendly and retroactive tool for the overall PhD study along with
the foundation for the future of indoor climate benchmarks to guide and incite
sustainable practices in the industry.

Indoor
Environmental
Quality
(IEQ)
er
tæt
forbundet
med
helbred
og
komfort for kontorarbejdere.
En dårlig (IEQ) kan føre til kortsigtede hel-
bredseffekter
såsom
hovedpine,
nedsat
koncentration
og
træthed
og
kan i alvorlige tilfælde have fatale eller terminale virkninger ved lang-
varig eksponering.
På kontorer kan virkningen af dårlig indeklima føre til
nedsat produktivitet og øget sygefravær,
hvilket viser sig som omkost-
ninger på grund a tab af arbejde,
produktivitet og arbejdstagerfordele.
Bygninger i Danmark beskrevet som kontor, handel, inventar, inkl.
offentlig
administration, udgør ca. 73,5 mio. m2, og omkostninger til løn og ydelser til
kontorpersonale dækker typisk 90% af en virksomheds driftsomkostninger.
I betragtning af, at omkring 36% af den europæiske arbejdsstyrke arbejder
i kontormiljøer, og at 40% af Danmarks energiforbrug kommer fra bygge-
sektoren, kan der opnås store fordele ved at optimere det oplevede indeklima.
På trods af dette har indsatsen for at reducere bygningsenergien i Danmark
historisk set vist sig at være fokuseret på at reducere el og varme, og ignorerer
IEQ samt arbejdersundhed. Denne kandidatafhandling har til formål at belyse de
usynlige risici IEQ udgør for sundhed og produktivitet, og gøre dem mere synlige,
målbare og forståelige for både arbejdere og bygningsejere. Heraf gives bygn-
ingsejere mulighed for at effektivt evaluere og se IEQ-optimeringer mere positivt.
Dette speciale målte og undersøgte indeklimaet i 13 kontorlokaler inden for 4
bygninger ved hjælp af forskellige IoT-sensorer (objektivt) og spørgeskemaer til
arbejdere (subjectivt). Ved at bruge et benchmarkingværktøj udviklet gennem et
omfattede litteraturstudie og itereret gennem feedback fra supervisorer, kunne
der desuden tegnes en sammenhæng mellem IEQ og arbejdernes produktivitet.
Dette projekt resulterede i et endeligt dashboard indeholdende et komplet
benchmarkingværktøj. Værktøjet består af en vægtet evaluering af 26 objektive
og subjektive IEQ-underparametre, et ratingskema og en vurdering af økonomiske
fordele, alt sammen visualiseret i et omfattende, adaptivt og brugervenligt dash-
board. Da bestræbelserne på at reducere bygningsenergien ikke er fokuseret
på IEQ, konkluderede denne afhandling, at benchmarkingværktøjet og dash-
boardet er en glimrende måde at opsummere og kontekstualisere bygningers
ydeevne med hensyn til både produktivitet og økonomi, hvis IEQ’en optimeres.
Afslutningsvist giver dette projekt et brugervenligt og tilbagevirkende værktøj
til det overordnede PhD-studie sammen med grundlaget for fremtiden for
indeklimabenchmarks for at vejlede og anspore til bæredytig praksis i industrien.

Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ) parameters within office environments have shown in
numerous studies a deteriorating effect on productivity/performance for occupants when
its values are sub-optimal, i.e., not adhering to industry standards. Considering that the av-
erage European workforce that work in office environments is 36% (roughly 81.4 million
people), investments in the improvement of indoor climate parameters can have consider-
able benefits to operating costs of businesses (Daniell et al., 2018). Expenses, including
salaries and benefits for office personnel, typically cover 90% of a business’ operational
costs, meaning even slight changes in workers’ productivity can have a significant impact
on its performance and costs.
Studies investigating IEQ in offices have explored occupants’ perception of indoor climate
parameters and the conditions they consider comfortable (Frontczak and Wargocki, 2011).
Thus, development of industry standards to better ensure improved comfort for building
users is underway, defining acceptable ranges of the various parameters based on four
areas of comfort (thermal, visual, acoustic, & indoor air quality (IAQ)). The standards are
used to develop the basis for a benchmarking tool to assess and rank office environments
in Denmark. However, even when all requirements of industry standards are met, it does
not guarantee satisfaction across all users.
1.1
Project Context
On average, people spend one-tenth of their time outside of buildings. Therefore, it is not
surprising that 40% of Denmark’s energy consumption derives from the building sector,
along with 23% of CO2 emissions (Nielsen and Liborius, 2020). In Denmark, buildings des-
ignated as office, trade, inventory, incl, public administration, amounts to approximately
73.5 million m2 (Danmarks Statistik, 2022). With IEQ being a determining factor for oc-
cupant performance, it would therefore be prudent to develop and widely apply monitor-
ing activities and auditing procedures for indoor climate analysis as tools to guide actions
towards improving occupant well-being and productivity, while reducing building energy
consumption (Corgnati et al., 2011).
Complications and challenges related to IEQ are predominantly experienced in the opera-
tion and maintenance phase, highlighted in Figure 1.1. Efforts towards reaching climate
goals and reducing building energy consumption, especially in Denmark, has historically
been shown to be focused on reducing electricity and heating, omitting IEQ and occu-
pant health (Indeklimaportalen, 2021). According to the findings in the study (Nezis et al.,

around the world, Sick Building Syndrome (SBS) exacerbated from a poor IEQ could lead
to a 2% reduction in productivity, roughly equivalent to an annual economic loss of $60

1.2
Project Description
IEQ is a complex interconnected web of parameters that in combination pose an invisible
risk to occupant health: high CO2-concentrations, high indoor temperatures, reverberation
time, pollution in the air, etc. These are all parameters that must be monitored in order to
keep them in check. Failing that, severe discomfort and dissatisfaction may occur amongst
occupants. This master’s thesis aims to develop a dashboard tool, including benchmarking
methods, with the goal of optimising office buildings with respect to their IEQ. Accompa-
nied by an automated evaluation tool to facilitate analysis and overview of both future
and past field analyses.
This project is a part of the PhD study ’Benchmarking the Indoor Environment in Office
Buildings – Characterisation, Synthesis and Practical Application’ (Dam-Krogh, 2022), which
investigates IEQ and its relation to occupants’ well-being, health, and productivity in office

buildings in Denmark and Greenland. The aim of the study is to build a database contain-
ing occupant and building data, which would be continuously expanded. Ultimately, the
database would provide the basis for the development of a benchmarking tool, enabling

This master’s thesis aims to further contribute to the PhD study with investigations, evalu-

tool, to be presented and possibly used in the larger PhD study. The two parts intertwines

2.1
IEQ Parameters and Performance
The perceived indoor climate plays a critical role in occupants’ comfort and can adversely
affect human health and cause sickness (Esfandiari et al., 2017). In severe cases, long-
term exposure can potentially have a fatal or terminal effect on occupants.
Symptoms caused by the perceived indoor environment are called Sick Building Syndrome
(SBS) symptoms and generally happen shortly after exposure to dissatisfying indoor cli-
mates. The focus of this project is therefore these short-term effects that are primarily
caused by the IEQ parameters: thermal comfort, visual comfort, acoustic comfort, and In-
door Air Quality (IAQ). The many sub-parameters within these overall parameters are listed
on Figure 2.1 from (DS/EN 16798-1:2019, 2019). Parameters marked with *, are not in-

Relative Humidity
Relative Humidity (RH) is a parameter that can vary greatly since it depends on the weather
and indoor temperatures. If there is a big temperature difference between outdoor and
indoor, RH can easily exceed the recommendation, which is 25% - 60% indoors. According
to (Rosone, 2020), too much humidity (70% and above) prevents sweat from evaporating
causing the body to overheat. The recommendation for RH should not be exceeded long-
term, since growth of bacteria will increase the risk of fungi growth, causing health issues
in the respiratory system. Symptoms such as eye, throat, and nose irritation can occur if
RH is too low (<20%) (Service Champions Heating & Air Conditioning, 2017). To prevent
this, regulating the indoor RH using humidification or dehumidification is recommended.

workspace, sufficient lighting must be provided from both natural and artificial sources. As
shown in Table 2.1 in section 2.2, visual discomfort causes symptoms such as headaches,
fatigue, and difficulty in concentrating.

2.5
Acoustic Comfort
Acoustic comfort in buildings and offices is relevant for productivity. The perception of
sound is complex and depends not only on sound level, but also reverberation time, sound
insulation and absorption. A good acoustical environment is an environment that can pro-
tect occupants from indoor and outdoor noise sources and provide good working conditions
(Šujanová et al., 2019).
Noise
Noise is defined as unwanted sound considered unpleasant, loud, or disruptive to hearing
(Wikipedia, 2021). In offices, noise is generated by e.g. ventilation, air conditioning, con-
versation, and electronic devices. Long-term exposure to noise can cause symptoms such
as headache, fatigue, and concentration difficulties and in more severe cases impaired
hearing.
To reduce noise and minimise health risks, the requirements and recommendations from
(DS/EN 16798-1:2019, 2019) and (Danish Working Environment Authority, 2007), need to
be complied with in office spaces. The requirements for Sound Pressure Level (SPL) are A-
weighted to reflect the sensitivity of human ears (Pulsarinstruments, 2021). In Table 2.6,

2.6
IAQ
A good IAQ is defined as indoor air without harmful concentrations of pollutants with the
majority of occupants being satisfied. As detailed in section 3.3, several studies have
shown that long-term exposure to large concentrations of CO2 could cause SBS symptoms.
As shown in Table 2.1 in section 2.2, symptoms such as eye, throat, and nose irritations,
headaches, and fatigue, etc., may occur. Furthermore, IAQ is an another parameter when
calculating productivity in this project. It is shown in Table 4.5 in section 4.5 that a pro-
ductivity loss up to 8% could be caused by a poor IAQ.
In this project, IAQ includes the four parameters; Carbon Dioxide (CO2), Total Volatile Or-
ganic Compounds (TVOC), Particulate Matter (PM) and, Other Pollutants (OP). Measure-
ments are used to assess how well offices perform according to requirements. However,
perception of air quality is a subjective experience, different across all occupants. There-
fore, objective and subjective assessments of IAQ are necessary. According to (Corgnati
et al., 2011), ”the IAQ can be characterised by measuring the physical and chemical prop-
erties of the air and compare them with the guideline values”.
CO2
The primary source of CO2 is occupant respiration. Thus, it can be indicative of the ven-
tilation rate supplied to office spaces. With high concentrations of CO2, symptoms such
as eye and skin irritation, headaches, dryness of skin, and fatigue are likely to occur, see
section 2.2. It is important that long-term exceedance of requirements does not happen,
since this can cause health issues for the occupants. Concentration levels up to 2000 ppm
are not dangerous in the short-term. However, concentrations of 2000 ppm and above are
unacceptable in the long-term (Jendrischik, 2019). For non-adapted persons (occupants
that have not acclimated to the indoor air), the requirements for CO2 are listed in Table 2.7.
Requirements are equal to a maximum CO2-concentration of 950 ppm above outdoor con-
centrations, which is 400 ppm.

3.1
Sustainable Building Certificates
To be able to improve and understand the performance of office buildings, it is imperative
to know the building’s performance and discern potential improvements. Many extensive
certification tools exist worldwide to provide such insights, using benchmarking tools to
assess performance in various categories. Specifically in Danish construction, sustainabil-
ity has become increasingly integral for the industry, increasing the demand for sustain-
able certification systems in Danish architecture.
The e-book ’Guide to Sustainable Building Certifications’ (Guldager Jensen et al., 2018),
provides a global overview of certifications, of which hundreds currently exist. German
Sustainable Building Council (DGNB) is a certification system with an exceptionally large
presence in Denmark and has been defined as the standard by the Green Building Council
Denmark. The DGNB certification process varies in criteria for each nation, using national
building codes to calculate performance. Chapter 2 lists the requirements specific to this
project. Other than focusing on sustainability, DGNB also assesses technical and process
quality, and is also ideal for various building types. However, applying its methodology on
existing buildings can be very complicated, when improving their IEQ. Thus, development
of benchmarking tools to evaluate existing buildings and estimate potential benefits from
improving their IEQ, can allow stakeholders to circumvent the tedious nature of the certi-
fication process. Coupled with a comprehensive database and dashboard to visualise each
comfort area and to compare with other buildings, the benchmarking tool can inform and
incite improvements.
To gather the knowledge about benchmarking practices necessary to develop a satisfac-
tory tool, research was conducted around the topic, along with other closely related topics.
The purpose of the research was to investigate practices and methods regarding evalua-
tion of the performance of office buildings, with respect to their IEQ parameters. The
researched certification tools vary in purpose, but often have sustainability and indoor
comfort as some of their key parameters. Some focus more heavily on the work conditions
in buildings and others on the sustainability level of a building.

Grading Schemes
Of the 10 investigated certification tools seen on Table 3.1, varying methods of grading

Weightings of IEQ Parameters
Depending on the region, IEQ parameters will have different weightings. The weightings
are based on how much IEQ parameters such as thermal comfort, visual comfort, acoustic
comfort, and IAQ impact occupants in each region. The weightings are based on question-
naire submissions gathered from several studies.

temperature, and examining its impact. The remaining seven studies investigated the im-

seven studies showed that thermal comfort had a slightly higher importance than acoustic
comfort and IAQ, whereas visual comfort was of least importance. By ranking the different
studies, a spiderweb ranking illustration was made, which can be seen on Figure 3.2.

5.6
Benchmarking Tool
To promote sustainability and incite investment in the development of sustainable solu-
tions and practices in the building sector, a methodology involving measuring, investigat-
ing, and subjective surveying of office environments in Denmark, is proposed. Data col-
lected from field studies could then be weighted with respect to its parameters’ impact
on occupants, then indexed for each major parameter affecting comfort (thermal comfort,
visual comfort, acoustic comfort, and IAQ). Thus, each investigated building can obtain a
potential universal benchmark, then can be ranked based on their IEQ scores, which would
ideally incite competition and promote sustainable development.
Drawing inspiration from the previously mentioned NABERS rating system, a benchmark-
ing tool was developed. This section will present the tool, along with reasoning for perfor-
mance grading, selection of parameters investigated, and the weighing of each parameter.
Furthermore, calculations behind the subjective and objective ratings, estimated financial
benefits, and the developed dashboard, are likewise described.
Weighting
As the literature review in chapter 3 revealed, there is no universal agreement on overall
weightings of IEQ parameters. Different methods are used from study to study, and across
several well-established benchmarking tools, such as NABERS. Weightings used in this tool
are inspired by several studies and a couple of sustainability certification tools such as
NABERS and DGNB. In the reviewed studies, thermal comfort was weighted the highest
among occupants, with IAQ second (though most symptoms stem from indoor air and is
therefore weighted highest in this project), followed by acoustic and visual comfort. See
Figure 5.12 for an overview of chosen weightings in tool. Note that these can be altered
in the developed benchmarking tool.

Source: Own figure

26 sub-parameters, covering both objective measurements and subjective occupant rat-

6.1
IEQ Investigation
Field Study Period
The measuring period in the investigated offices was a substantial factor for later calcu-
lations. All field studies were conducted during the heating season, each lasting a week in
March, which was mostly cloudy, and April, which was somewhat sunny. The length of a
field study was 5 weekdays (can vary for each company). With a measurement frequency
for sensors of 1 to 5 minutes, this would equate 480-2400 data entries in a one week
period. This amount of data is sufficient for an analysis of the IEQ. However, this is not
representative of an office’s annual performance. Thus, to ensure more accurate assess-
ments, a prolonged study, like some of the certification tools require, or smaller studies
repeated throughout the year, is suggested.
Sensor Pack Challenges
As mentioned in ”Challenges” under section 4.3, some challenges occurred during the mea-
suring period, which resulted in several downtime gaps for Flow. Furthermore, placement
of sensors gave inaccurate measurements of lighting since it was observed that both day-
light and artificial lighting were sufficient. Additionally, according to occupants, the light-
ing was also sufficient. The sensors were placed to face away from windows to reduce
glare and extreme measurements from direct sunlight. Some sensors were placed between
tables and behind monitors to avoid hindering occupants. This could have been the reason
for low lighting measurements.
Questionnaires
A questionnaire was sent to each office to obtain data on the perceived IEQ anonymously.
Answers were solely based on the respondents’ individual perception and memory, thus
is not an accurate reflection of the actual conditions. It may be difficult for occupants to
recall the number of days they had specific symptoms during the measuring period.
Occupants could skip questions without submitting an answer, resulting in a high rate of
”No Response”. This could lead to false averages of perceived IEQ. Likewise, another as-
pect to consider is response rate, since this could also lead to inaccurate results, and should
be considered in offices that have low response rates. It is suggested that every question
should be obligatory, and that a minimum response rate for questionnaires should be ob-
tained. A minimum response rate of 60% is therefore suggested.

6.2
Dashboard Tool
Benchmarking
In chapter 3, various benchmarking methods were investigated, which showed many dif-
ferent weightings of the four overall IEQ parameters. Since there is no definitive weight-
ing method, the one used in this project was developed from the literature review and
numerous studies on the topic. The final tool includes weightings for both subjective and
objective sub-parameters, evaluating as many factors as possible for an assessment. All
26 objective and subjective sub-parameters considered in this project contribute to a final
IEQ rating.
Weightings for some sub-parameters were not representative for an entire office. For ex-
ample, light was measured using only one sensor for each zone in an office, and therefore
would not reflect the whole space. However, lighting measurements can easily be added
to the dashboard tool and thus be applied for projects where lighting is measured at more
points. In this project, lighting levels were predominantly poor across all field studies.
For future potential field studies, lighting should be measured at each workstation making
visual comfort calculations more representative. The benchmarking tool was developed to
be easily modified in this regard.
Estimated Financial Benefits
Calculations of estimated financial benefits are, in this project, solely based on measure-
ments of CO2 concentration and temperature. As research progresses regarding the rela-
tion between performance and other IEQ parameters, the effects of potential noise and
lighting on occupants, could be interesting to include. This could even be coupled with
subjective data since occupants who complain about the indoor environment might per-
form poorer than others. Financial benefits for all offices are based on a single company’s
information, such as salary, and therefore does not represent each company accurately.
This information can be modified in the final dashboard tool. Furthermore, calculations
of investment costs are based on potential revenue increase and not on cost of practical
renovations to improve IEQ.

6.3
Future Work
Further detailing of the benchmarking tool could include information about each site such
as year of construction, material use, occupancy, and purpose of use. Moreover, an expan-
sion to include ranking of offices and not solely the whole building would provide build-
ing owners information regarding which offices that needed optimisation the most. The
dashboard was also developed with the purpose of being a retroactive tool that could be
expanded with other data gathered during the overall PhD study this project is part of. Fur-
ther development could include applying it to an online database, which could efficiently
gather and display data from all field studies conducted in the overall study. A more de-
tailed dashboard could be developed with an interactive floor plan for the selected office,
showing IEQ parameters for each zone in offices (like DTU Smart Library). This however,
is not ideal to do in MS Excel, but could be done in Power Bi, Grafana, or other dashboard
programs.
Data Modelling
Forecasting could be used to diagnose from IEQ data, symptoms, and complaints, enabling
building owners to explore potential solutions and how they affect satisfaction and overall
health of occupants. Due to data sets being relatively small in this project, forecasting of
measurements would not have been ideal. Next step for automation through forecasting
would be to develop algorithms and rule-based actions based on IEQ data.

7.1
Completion of Objectives
Completion of objectives in this project is divided into two parts. One part is focused on
the field studies and IEQ analysis. The second part focused on the development of bench-
marking tools and the overall dashboard. Considering the outcomes of both parts provided
greater insights into the field studies, visualisation, and method of benchmarking the in-
door environment of offices.
IEQ Investigation
The field studies in this project were conducted in 4 Danish office buildings from March
to April 2022. Each measuring period lasted a week, and the analysis covered a five day
workweek during work hours. Moreover, questionnaires were sent to all occupants of each
office building.
The results, which were obtained through calculations inside the developed benchmark-
ing tool, indicated no issues with RH, TVOC, and OPs in all offices, minor issues with CO2,
PM2.5, temperature, and noise, and major issues with lighting and PM10 for all offices.
More specifically, results showed major issues with RH in building 313 and temperature
in building 310. Limitations of the IEQ investigations include sub-optimal placements of
sensors regarding lighting measurements and the period of the field studies, since this
would not accurately represent an office’s annual performance.
Concerning questionnaires, information gaps from several occupants were recorded. Addi-
tionally, many symptoms were registered relating to the thermal environment and acous-
tics, where the majority of complaints stemmed from these areas in all offices. Some
symptoms related to IAQ were also registered, however few complaints regarding this were
made.
Benchmarking Tool
Background research of benchmarking tools resulted in an overview of 10 certification
tools, along with some research into various other tools. The majority of which used a star
rating system and third-party as method for documentation. The nationally recognised
certification tool, DGNB, uses a radar chart to illustrate overall compliance with require-
ments.
The final benchmarking tool was based on four overall IEQ parameters: thermal comfort
(30%), visual comfort (15%), acoustic comfort (20%), and IAQ (35%). The dashboard that
houses the benchmarking tool allows for further specification based on user input, includ-
Indoor environmental quality in offices
81
==PAGE:101==
ing definitions of weightings and requirements. Offices are rated by the tool using a 1-6
stars rating scheme, along with a radar chart to demonstrate performance within all 26 re-
lated sub-parameters. This provides building owners with succinct analysis of their offices
to help guide decisions. Moreover, the tool calculates and presents estimated financial
benefits stemming from occupants’ performance.
Overall Purpose
Through the completion of this project’s objectives, a contribution of 4 field studies con-
sisting of 21 offices, along with evaluations of these, were submitted to the PhD study
this project is part of. Additionally, a methodology for a benchmarking tool was presented,
which could be useful for the rest of the PhD study. Another potential use could be as a
foundation for the further development of the benchmark and a database to easily expand
and analyse new field studies, since the dashboard is principally automated.
7.2
Final Thoughts
The work done in this thesis has contributed to the understanding of the amount of IEQ
parameters that have an impact on employee health and performance, and how these in-
teract with one another along with possibilities and limitations in general and specific
to this project. Furthermore, this project presents a strong foundation for reviewing and
evaluating indoor environments, especially in a digital future, to further guide and incite
sustainable practices.
Lastly, a demonstration is provided for a method to put IEQ parameters in a context where
invisible risks to health and performance are more visible, measurable, and comprehensi-
ble to both occupants and building owners. This thesis aims to contribute towards future
efforts of digitisation to promote transparency and collaboration in the construction in-
dustry, through use of IoT sensors, data-driven models, adaptive systems, and digitally
interconnected buildings and installations.

Indoor environmental quality in offices
==PAGE:158==
Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ) is closely linked with health and comfort of occupants. A poor IEQ
can lead to short-term health effects such as headaches, diminished concentration, and fatigue and
in severe cases can have fatal or terminal effects through long-term exposure. In offices, the effects
of poor IEQ can lead to reduced productivity and increased sick leave, which manifests as costs due
to loss of work, productivity, and worker benefits.
Buildings in Denmark designated as office, trade, inventory, incl. public administration, amount to
approximately 73.5 million m2, and costs related to salaries and benefits for office personnel, typically
cover 90% of a business’ operational costs. Considering that roughly 36% of the European workforce
works in office environments and that 40% of Denmark’s energy consumption comes from the building
sector, great benefits can be achieved by optimising the perceived indoor climate.
Despite this, efforts towards reducing building energy in Denmark has historically been shown to be
focused on reducing electricity and heat, omitting IEQ and occupant health. This master’s thesis aims
to elucidate the invisible risks that IEQ poses to health and performance, making them more visible,
measurable, and comprehensible to both occupants and building owners. Effectively enabling building
owners to evaluate and view IEQ optimisations more favourably.
This thesis measured and investigated the indoor environment of 13 office rooms within 4 buildings
using various IoT sensors (objective) and occupant questionnaires (subjective). Furthermore, using a
benchmarking tool developed through a comprehensive literature study and iterated through feed-
back from supervisors, a correlation between IEQ and occupant productivity could be drawn.
This project resulted in a final dashboard containing a complete benchmarking tool. The tool con-
sists of the weighted evaluation of 26 objective and subjective IEQ sub-parameters, a rating scheme,
and an estimation of financial benefits, all visualised succinctly in a comprehensive, adaptive, and
user friendly dashboard. Given efforts towards reducing building energy is not focused on IEQ, this
thesis concluded that the benchmarking tool and dashboard is an excellent way to summarise and
contextualise building performance with respect to both productivity and economics, should the IEQ
be optimised. In closing, this project provides a user-friendly and retroactive tool for the overall PhD
study along with the foundation for the future of indoor climate benchmarks to guide and incite sus-
tainable practices in the industry.
