Abstract
This paper proposes to avoid information overflow of document ranking with multiôÙdocuôÙ
ment abstractive summarization.
Specifically, we propose two input strategies which
adapt a transformerôÙbased model, ProphetNet, from singleôÙdocument to multiôÙdocument
summarization. The two proposed input strategies, dubbed DocMean and Serial and
adapts the encoder output by either averaging it or handling it serially. We first redo the
work of ProphetNet on CNN/DailyMail and extend ProphetNetôÙX to Danish on DaNewsôÙ
room. These models achieve near stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart results on CNN/DailyMail and stateôÙofôÙ
theôÙart results on DaNewsroom. We adapt ProphetNet for multiôÙdocument summarization,
using DocMean and Serial, achieving strong results on both. Serial achieves results only
beaten by recent stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart. With a combination of human evaluation and our proôÙ
posed metric for measuring factual consistency, NERO, we show that all models suffer
from factual consistency, with DocMean struggling the most.
Our work shows that singleôÙdocument summarization models can be adapted to multiôÙ
document summarization. This adaptation reuses high quality previous work as much as
possible and can be seen as an extension of the models. Our approach remain general
and does not require domain knowledge or taskôÙspecific architecture. This makes it easily
extendable to other transformerôÙbased models. The paper resembles a proof of concept
and leaves much to future work to fully understand this approach.

Introduction
Data is growing at an exponential rate which makes searching for the right data harder
and harder. The task of information retrieval is often solved with document ranking which
gives a prioritized list of documents based on a given query. A set of topôÙk relevant docôÙ
uments are usually retrieved and given to the user. Reading through these documents
can become cumbersome and lead to information overload. Condensing the retrieved
information can therefore become a vital task.
To improve document ranking, each retrieved document can be summarized which vastly
reduces the amount of data from documents to sentences. The task of summarizing docôÙ
uments is known as singleôÙdocument summarization and is a well studied task in NLP.
Summarization splits itself into two areas, known as extractive and abstractive. ExtracôÙ
tive summarization aims to identify salient information, extract the relevant passages and
concatenate this to form a summary. Abstractive summarization aims to generate meanôÙ
ingful text to form novel summaries. This paper will focus on abstractive summarization
due to the novel and humanôÙlike summaries.
Moving further, one can consider creating a single summary for all of the retrieved docôÙ
uments, rather than a summary for each document. This is known as multiôÙdocument
summarization, where multiple source documents, often denoted as a cluster, is sumôÙ
marized to a few concise sentences. Applying multiôÙdocument summarization to the reôÙ
trieved documents from document ranking essentially transforms it to a form of longôÙform
question answering. Applying summarization to multiple documents has been shown to
significantly improve the user experience [1]. We will also be focusing on multiôÙdocument
abstractive summarization in this paper.
This paper will redo known work in English singleôÙdocument abstractive summarization,
before transferring to a largeôÙscale Danish dataset and report, to the best of our knowlôÙ
edge, the first benchmark on this. This is done for two reasons: First, we confirm the
validity of our implementation by repeating previous work. Secondly, we transfer to DanôÙ
ish to show that our work is easily transferable to multilingual settings. Our main focus lie
in the proposal of two methods to adapt a singleôÙdocument summarization model to multiôÙ
document summarization. These methods are trained and tested on a largeôÙscale English
multiôÙdocument summarization dataset. We do not transfer multiôÙdocument summarizaôÙ
tion to Danish due to the lack of a high quality Danish dataset. However, the methodology
of transferring the work is identical to the singleôÙdocument work, so we refer to this.
Most of the work within multiôÙdocument summarization has required domainôÙspecific knowlôÙ
edge or architecture, making the models less generally applicable. The goal of the paper
is not to obtain stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart results by creating a brand new model, but rather to exôÙ
tend previous work into new settings, specifically multiôÙdocument summarization. We take
a transformerôÙbased model, ProphetNet, and adapt it to multiôÙdocument summarization.
The choice of model is not important, as our adaptations are general enough for most
transformers models to work. With this work, we show that simple adaptations can exôÙ
tend a model into new domains and thus expand on previous work. This connects the gap
between singleôÙdocument and multiôÙdocument summarization and reuses work within the
summarization field.

Related work
2.1
SingleôÙdocument summarization
Summarization is a well studied task that has had many advances with generalized modôÙ
els such as BERT [2] and T5 [3]. The majority of models in summarization are transformôÙ
ers, an encoderôÙdecoder architecture that uses attention to differentiate the significance of
each part of the input data. TransformerôÙbased models mostly focus on abstractive sumôÙ
marization rather than extractive. The majority of this work is supervised utilising high
quality datasets such as CNN/DailyMail [4] or Gigaword [5].
A model which have had big success is PEGASUS [6] which introduced a summarization
focused preôÙtraining objective to obtain better results. At its time, PEGASUS obtained
stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart results on most abstractive summarization dataset. Their great success
stems from their novel preôÙtraining objective called Gap Sentence Generation (GSG),
where they select and mask whole sentences and concatenate them into a pseudoôÙ
summary. The model is then trained to predict what is behind the mask, much like the
classical preôÙtraining Masked Language Model (MLM). Their work shows the great value
that task specific preôÙtraining brings.
2.2
MultiôÙdocument summarization
The simplest way of doing multiôÙdocument summarization is by concatenating the cluster
of documents. However, since most models are capped at a set amount of tokens (often
512), long documents gets cut off and this method becomes useless. Some work, such as
the LongformerôÙEncoderôÙDecoder (LED) model [7], solves this problem by reducing the
space complexity of the model, from quadratic to linear, allowing a higher cap of tokens.
There are other approaches [8], such as the parallel combination strategy that attends to
each document independently and then sums up the context vectors. Another approach
is hierarchical, which again tends to each document independently, but then, rather than
summing up the context vectors, the contexts are concatenated and attention is computed
on them. Common for all of them, they reduce multiôÙsource input to a singleôÙsource input
in one way or another.
Much like PEGASUS, PRIMERA [9] introduced a summarization focused preôÙtraining obôÙ
jectives for multiôÙdocument summarization. They adapt the ideas of PEGASUSã GSG
by masking salient sentences and asking the model to generate them. Their masking
is based on pyramid evaluation [10], document frequency of entities and content overôÙ
lap of sentences using ROUGE. The specific details can be seen in section 2.2 of their
paper. The backbone of PRIMERA is the LongformerôÙEncoderôÙDecoder model, so their
approach utilises concatenation to handle the input. They heavily outperform previous
stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart by over 10 ROUGE points, again highlighting the value of task specific
preôÙtraining. Their work also shows great zeroôÙ and fewôÙshot results and human annoôÙ
tators scores their summaries higher than the ones by PEGASUS and LED. Their only
ãflawã is, that they require a high amount of steps to obtain these results, as seen in table
7 of their paper.
Other work in multiôÙdocument summarization has focused on a graphôÙbased [11] apôÙ
proach, in where the the documents are transformed to a semantic graph. The graph
is then often reduced before getting processed in some way. There has been popular
extractive graphôÙbased approaches, such as TextRank [12], that has seen great success.

2.3
Evaluation
There is a lack of consensus regarding the evaluation within summarization [13]. ROUGE
[14] has cemented itself as the standard metric for summarization, despite having obvious
flaws. ROUGE was meant to have multiple references, but many popular datasets, such
as CNN/DailyMail [4], only has a single reference. Additionally, as ROUGE is based on
token overlap, it does not catch prevalent problems such as factual consistency. Factual
consistency is abstractive summarizationãs biggest enemy and the problem is even more
prevalent in the multiôÙdocument domain. There has been many approaches in measuring
the factual consistency [15, 16], often with modelôÙbased questionôÙanswering [17, 18, 19].
These modelôÙbased approaches are computationally heavy and most require additional
training data. There are therefore no established measures in place to accurately describe
the characteristics of the summaries, making abstractive summarization heavily reliant on
human evaluation.

Methodology
We propose to adapt a wellôÙknown transformerôÙbased model, ProphetNet, from singleôÙ
document summarization to multiôÙdocument summarization. This approach allows for
the repurposing of the wellôÙstudied task of singleôÙdocument summarization and does not
require any additional pretraining. We will give a brief overview of the theory and methodôÙ
ology behind this paper.
3.1
Transformers
Transformers were introduced in 2017 by Google [20] and are becoming the architecture
of choice within NLP, with the introduction of models such as BERT [2] and GPTôÙ3 [21].
Transformers follow the encoderôÙdecoder architecture and works on a mechanism called
attention. Most NLP transformers are seq2seq models that take a sequence of tokens, i.e.
integers that map to specific words in the modelãs vocabulary, passes it through the model
and generates a sequence of tokens as output. This output can then be mapped back to
words using said vocabulary. The output is generated token by token in an autoregressive
manner during inference and often uses teacher forcing to guide it towards the right output
during training. Teacher forcing is the method of using the target as input to the model,
rather than the model output from prior time steps, which also allows for parallelization
during training. Transformers can be trained to a variety of tasks including summarization.
3.1.1
Input
The sequence of tokens is converted, token by token, using word embedding, to a vector
that represents the word in latent space. Similar words are expected to lie closer to each
other in the latent space. Next, positional information is added to the word embeddings,
such that the model can capture the ordering of the sequence. This is done via. positional
encoding, a technique that allows the model to develop a sense of order.
3.1.2
Positional encoding
As transformers have no recurrent or convolution elements, the model must develop a
sense of order in another way, as transformers are fed with all the tokens in parallel. This
is done with positional encodings and is applied to the input embeddings at the start of
the encoder and decoder. The positional encodings are defined using sine and consine
functions of different frequencies, where dmodel is the dimension of the embeddings (i.e.
length), pos is the position of the word and i is index of the embedding dimension.
PE(pos, 2i) = sin(pos/100002i/dmodel)
(3.1)
PE(pos, 2i + 1) = cos(pos/100002i/dmodel)

hypothesized that these functions would allow the model to learn to attend by relative
positions. Due to transformers large success, this hypothesis seems to hold. We will
skip the further details behind this and why it works. The important fact is, that positional
encodings allow the model to use the ordering of the words as part of its knowledge.

3.2
ProphetNet
Our model of choice is ProphetNet [22], a largeôÙscale preôÙtrained Seq2Seq model that
does future nôÙgram prediction. Instead of the traditional one token prediction, ProphetNet
predicts multiple tokens at the same time, which encourages the model to plan further
ahead than traditional Seq2Seq models. It is preôÙtrained on BookCorpus [23] and English
Wikipedia (16 GB). Additionally, ProphetNet is extended to ProphetNetôÙX [24], where X
can be English, Chinese and so on. We use ProphetNetôÙMulti, a multilingual model preôÙ

At their release, ProphetNet and ProphetNetôÙX, achieved stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart results on CNN/ôÙ
DailyMail, GigaWord and SQuAD 1.1. There has since been other approaches that have
exceeded their results, but the results of both models are still competitive. ProphetNet and
ProphetNetôÙX is chosen partly due to its novel nôÙtoken prediction, that we believe is esôÙ
pecially important for multiôÙdocument summarization, as well as their strong multilingual
model. The nôÙtoken prediction is believed to be important for summarization based on
coherence, consistency and relevance, but most importantly fluency. Predicting nôÙtokens
at a time creates strong nôÙpair token correlation, as multiple tokens are generated in the
same instance. It forces the model to look ahead in time and prevents local correlations.
The ProphetNet paper states the performance of biôÙ and trigrams to be comparable, with
a lower computational and time cost for bigrams of 15%.

3.3.2
DocMean
The input strategy which we dubbed DocMean, is an adaptation of the concatenation
strategy (flat) of [8], where the concat operation is replaced with a mean operation. We
average the multiôÙsource input over the document axis, creating a overall document unôÙ
derstanding, as seen in figure 3.2b. This operation reduces all token information to a
single vector that represents a highôÙlevel understanding of the document. The model esôÙ
sentially functions on a compressed version of the data, which also significantly speeds it
up. The increased computational speed comes with a performance cost, as we decrease
the amount of information available to the model by removing the information associated
with each token.
DocMean takes inspiration from MeanSum [26], an unsupervised multiôÙdocument method
which showed that the mean representation of the source documents decodes to a reaôÙ
sonable summary.
They showed that averaged encoded representations can still be
meaningful and that the information is not lost. This led to our hypothesis, which sparked
the idea of this input strategy, that the average encoded representation functions as a
reasonable representation of the documents. MeanSum showed this on a cell (token)
level using LSTMs, whereas we propose it on a document level using transformers. We
believe this to be more in line with transformers, as one cannot average the a token across
multiple documents because the ordering of each document is not similar.

3.4.2
Factual consistency (NERO)
Abstractive summarization often struggles with factual consistency, where models distort
or fabricate facts. We propose a simplified method of measure factual consistency based
on Named Entity Recognition Overlap (NERO). By measuring the percentage overlap of
12
MultiôÙDocument Abstractive Summarization
==PAGE:21==
the named entities of a summary and the named entities of the documents, which the
summary originate from, we get a crude idea of the factual consistency of the summary.
There should not necessarily be a complete overlap between the two as abstractive sumôÙ
marization allows for reformulation and each entity does not necessarily need to be part
of a summary. We only report the precision, as this gives us an idea of the relevance of
the entities of the summary. There is more to factual consistency than entity overlap, but
we believe this to be the most important, and easiest measurable, criteria. Furthermore,
NERO is disconnected from reference summaries, making it an unsupervised metric suitôÙ
able for multiôÙdocument summarization, where labeled data is more sparse. To have a
point of reference, we will also report the NERO scores of the references.

where the NERO score of a summary s, generated from the source document(s) d, is
the fraction of entities #ents in the intersection of s and d over the entities in s, i.e. the

The problem of factual consistency is even more apparent in multiôÙdocument summarizaôÙ
tion, as articles may have variance in reported numbers. Articles are not written at the
exact same time, so facts such as casualties tend to rise over time. There does not exist a
ground truth of these numbers aside from newer articles being more updated. The model
must therefore simply pick one or generated an average number. Picking a number of
any source document increases the NERO score, while an average of all the numbers will
lead to a decrease in NERO, if it does not exist in the source documents. Neither is more
correct than the other, unless the summary specifies a source. NERO will still capture
when other entities, such as names or cities, is mixed together, producing fictional people
or places. So while NERO has its drawbacks, we still believe that it can provide valuable
insights in the average case.

4.2.1
CNN/DailyMail
The CNN/DailyMail dataset [4] is a singleôÙdocument abstractive summarization dataset,
consisting of 331 thousand summaryôÙarticle pairs from the CNN and Daily Mail websites.
The dataset was originally created for question answering but was repurposed for summaôÙ
rization. The source documents consists of 776 words and 29.74 sentences on average,
with the summaries being 53 words and 3.72 sentences on average. Having multiple senôÙ
tences for each summary is special, as many summarization datasets provide single senôÙ
tence references which might not be comprehensive. In multiôÙdocument summarization,
single sentence references are unlikely to contain an accurate representation of multiple
documents. The paper extends the work of multiôÙdocument summarization, so we believe
that it is more relevant to test our models on singleôÙdocument datasets that lie closer to
multiôÙdocument datasets.
Additionally, the CNN/DailyMail dataset is one of the most used singleôÙdocument summaôÙ
rization datasets in current work and is used for benchmarking on most model. It is also
the dataset that ProphetNet uses for their NôÙgram comparison. This allows for thorough
comparison and validation of our implementation.

WCEP
The WCEP dataset [29] is a largeôÙscale English multiôÙdocument summarization dataset
obtained from the Wikipedia Current Events Portal, consisting of short humanôÙwritten sumôÙ

and short (30ôÙ40 words) with external links for source articles. Each summary has 1.2
source articles on average, which the authors of the WCEP dataset supplemented with
similar Common Crawl news articles [30] using a logistic regression classifier. The logistic
regression classifier uses tfôÙidf similarity combined with entity overlap and links, between
the summary, and the title and body of the article, as features.
WCEP offers two official versions of the dataset, WCEPôÙtotal, averaging 234.5 articles
per cluster, and WCEPôÙ100, averaging 63.7 articles per cluster. WCEPôÙ100 is, as the
name suggests, truncated to a maximum of 100 articles. We limit our experiments to
an unofficial version WCEPôÙ8, containing only the 8 most similar articles. This is to stay
more inôÙline with the premise of the paper, as document ranking usually only retrieves a
few documents. For document ranking, we do not expect to retrieve 100 or more articles
for a given query, as this will lead to information overflow. As reported by figure 2 in the
WCEP paper [29], there is a sharp decrease in quality after the first 10 articles which
supports this low document cutoff. We move the cutoff from 10 to 8 for computational
gains.
WCEP has a low extractive fragment density, similar to DaNewsroom, likely due to their
short summary length. This categorizes WCEP as an abstractive summarization dataset,
in line with CNN/DailyMail and the abstractive subset of DaNewsroom.
4.2.4

several tasks. We focus on task 1 and 2, which is the multiôÙdocument summarization
tasks. It contains 50 topics/events where NIST (National Institute of Standards and TechôÙ
nology) annotators have handpicked 10 documents for each as well as 4 short references
written by NIST assessors, allowing for multiôÙreference evaluation. DUC2004 is of very
high quality, but only suitable for testing due to limited number of clusters.

Discussion
6.1
Performance
The singleôÙdocument summarization results are below the original results of the paper.
This was expected, as we sacrificed performance for compute by limiting the model from
512 to 256 tokens, eliminating warmôÙup steps and doing early stopping. All this decreases
computational cost while also lowering performance. Since we do not have any ProphetôÙ
Net benchmarks in the multiôÙdocument setting, we cannot compare our performance
against an ideal run. We hypothesize that the singleôÙdocument differences are transôÙ
ferable to multiôÙdocument, which means that our multiôÙdocument results can be slightly
optimized.
6.2
Dataset bias
CNN/DailyMail is skewed towards extractive summaries as mentioned in section 4 of
Newsroom [28] and has strong positional bias as described in [34]. The positional bias
is classical for news dataset due to the ãinverted pyramidã principle of journalism. The inôÙ
verted pyramid principle is a structure where the important information is presented first.
This causes news datasets to have strong positional bias and is the reason why LEADôÙ3
is a strong baseline for these.
DaNewsroom covers a much larger range of summarization styles but as we choose to
work on the abstractive subset of the dataset, DaNewsroom is skewed towards abstractive
summaries. DaNewsroom is not a wellôÙstudied dataset, so we do not have any inôÙdepth
analysis of it. The original Newsroom is much more well studied and since their methodôÙ
ology is identical, we assume Newsroom and DaNewsroom to be similar. Newsroom has
a strong positional bias, albeit a little less than CNN/DailyMail (c.f. [34]).
6.3
Input strategies
6.3.1
DocMean
Our proposed input strategy DocMean obtained an acceptable result. While the generôÙ
ated summaries are not of the highest quality, they are still reasonable summaries. The
information fed to the model is highly condensed, having averaged the entire sequence of
tokens. This leads to less data, equal to the average sequence length, which increases
the speed and decreases the memory usage by a significant amount.
While DocMean showed good potential, having significantly less data to work with, it also
struggles in certain areas. It struggles especially with factual consistency, providing inôÙ
correct numbers, locations or people as well as a tendency to repeat itself. This is clearly
reflected in the NERO scores of table 5.5 and was confirmed by human evaluation (ExôÙ
ample WCEP). The exact characteristics of the problem is unclear as it requires extensive
human evaluation to uncover this. We provided an example of the issues of DocMean in
ãExample WCEPã section 5.3, where the summary contradicts itself. The contradiction is
due to incorrect entities, as Taliban both denies (false) and blames itself (sort of true?)
for the attack. Moreover, the documents for this example does not mention any denial or
blame, as the Taliban clearly took responsibility for the attack. DocMean appears to have
a generalized concept of terrorism related articles, where the attacks are usually anonyôÙ
mous but an organization is blamed that in turn denies it. It may seem like DocMean is
simply generating generic summaries while adding in the relevant entities. It is unknown

if this is the exact case, as it again requires extensive human evaluation to uncover, but
the concept is interesting.
6.3.2
Serial
The serial input strategy is a clear winner in terms of all our metrics. Firstly, it does not
struggle as much with factual consistency, as it has the token information that DocMean
did not. Secondly, the method resembles singleôÙdocument summarization, making it easôÙ
ier for the model to understand the input. We also see distinct multiôÙdocument summaôÙ
rization features in Example WCEP from Serial, where particular information from multiple
documents are used as supplements. This cements the high quality of Serial and makes
the method an overall success.
Because the multiôÙdocument input is handled sequentially, we hypothesize that the model
might prefer either the first or last documents that it processes. We test this hypothesis
by investigating the similarities between each document and the generated summary.
We use pythonãs inbuilt SequenceMatcher to find this similarity. We found that the last
document in the cluster, i.e. the document that is processed last in the series of cross
attention steps, has the highest similarity to the generated summary. On average, the
last document is twice as similar to the summary, when compared against the rest of the
source documents, and three times as likely to be the most similar.
We do not believe that the model is forgetting things, as was the problem with RNNs,
rather that the model prefers the newest data. This is no surprise, as the input strategy
consists of a series of cross attention passes, where the previous output is fed into the
next pass. The final decoder pass partly uses all the previous documents information and
partly the last documentãs information. The final document is therefore able to influence
the output more heavily than the previous, it has the last say, so to speak.
Since we kept the original ordering of WCEP articles, it means that the source articles
are processed first and that the last document are from Common Crawl. Moreover, the
original ordering is sorted by similarity, so the later documents are also less similar to the
original. This is partly problematic, as it means that the ãworstã article in the cluster is the
most similar to the summary. Intuitively this should lead to a worse result and reversing
the ordering might lead to better results. As we only work with 8 articles for WCEP, we
do not believe this to have a big effect, as these few articles are very similar. For bigger
clusters, e.g. WCEPôÙ100, the ordering is likely to have a large effect.
6.4
Factual consistency
We noticed, with qualitative evaluation and the NERO scores from 3.4.2, that DocMean
suffers from factual consistency. While NERO is not an exact measure of factual conôÙ
sistency, the qualitative evaluation confirmed the problem. DocMean has a much lower
score than both the Serial and reference summaries. Only half of the entities mentioned
in DocMean summaries also appear in the input documents. The Serial summaries score
almost equally to the reference summaries. This does not mean that the Serial strategies
are as factual consistent the references which should be ground truth. We saw this in the
Example WCEP, where the Serial input strategy changed ãkilledã to ãwoundedã, making
the summary less factually accurate.
This clearly highlights the differences in the two input strategies. DocMean works without
exact token information, as tokens are averaged out, while the Serial strategy has exact
token information. It is not surprising that not having access to specific token information
may cause misinformation to slip through. Nevertheless, factual consistency appears to
be a problem of both methods and will need to be addressed in future work.

6.6
SingleôÙdocument vs. multiôÙdocument summarization
Results and numbers themselves are not enough to accurate describe singleôÙdocument
summarization (SDS) vs. multiôÙdocument summarization (MDS) approaches on a multiôÙ
document dataset. We see during section 5.5 that the performance on both SDS and
MDS models vary dataset to dataset. This must mean that there different characteristics
to each method, characteristics that define each methodology. To highlight these, we
present one pro and one con for each method ôÙ despite there being many more.
SingleôÙdocument summarization is, compared to multiôÙdocument, able to achieve better
summaries (w.r.t. to various language criteria), as its task is more simple. It has less data
than multiôÙdocument, as its input sequence is capped at 512, allowing it to fineôÙtune better.
In other words, it has to learn less things, making it specialize better. The problem, when
using it for multiôÙdocument summarization, is that it relies on concatenation to function.
This concatenation heavily limits the amount of data per document and destroys the origôÙ
inal ordering of sequences. For multiôÙdocument summarization with large clusters, e.g.
WCEPôÙ100, this concatenation strategy is useless as only a few tokens per document is
kept.
On the other hand, multiôÙdocument summarization is very task specific. This allows us
to take no shortcuts in the input methodology, allowing for all the data from each docuôÙ
ment to be taken into account. This causes the MDS summaries to take supplementary
information across documents, making it precisely multiôÙdocument summarization. The
problem is, that the lingual skills of the MDS summary is often lower than a SDS sumôÙ
mary, as it has to learn not just summarization, but multiôÙdocument summarization. This
added layer to the summarization obviously affects performance, since added complexity
disallows the model to go as inôÙdepth and specialize.
6.7
Moving forward
Our results clearly show, that despite the issues, adaptation of singleôÙdocument summaôÙ
rization models to multiôÙdocument summarization can lead to good results. Moreover,
the cost of this adaptation is relatively low, but might require some fineôÙtuning to function
optimally. This paper functions as a proof of concept, showing the great value of this apôÙ
proach, but has uncovered much more work to be done, such as optimizations and issue
fixing. There is much to be explored in this area and additional problems may arise when
extending this work into other models and datasets.

Future work
7.1
Experiment setup
Our experimental setup differs slightly from the original in order to decrease the compuôÙ
tational cost. An example of this is the exclusion of warm up steps. This tradeôÙoff likely
affects performance so our benchmarks might be improvable. It is especially prevalent
to redo our experiments on the DaNewsroom dataset, as we do not have any reported
benchmarks as reference. The effects of our shortcuts are unexplored for ProphetNet
in the multiôÙdocument setting and it is unsure how they affect the performance as we do
not have a reference point. Additionally, the hyperparameters are taken from the origôÙ
inal ProphetNet paper and is therefore tuned for singleôÙdocument summarization. Our
multiôÙdocument approach significantly changes the training, so hyperparameter tuning is
essentially to explore.
7.2
Data
We have taken certain choices with our data in order to decrease computational cost,
choices that most likely affects performance. For example, cutting down the number
of tokens from 512 to 256 is a significant change, which impact we have not investiôÙ
gated.
Additionally, we have trained our multiôÙdocument summarization on a relative
small amount of documents per cluster. The decision to only include 8 articles, due to
the document ranking premise of the paper as well as the computational cost, might have
affected the performance of the model. It would be interesting to investigate the impact
of these changes and make a Pareto front of data vs. quality, as we might be willing to
take small decreases in quality for a bigger increases in speed. Specifically, we propose
to investigate the impact of lowering the token cap from 512 as well the number of source
documents per cluster, the two main contributors of data size.
7.3
Serial ordering
As mentioned during the discussion, the Serial input strategy tended to favor the last
processed document. We propose to randomize the ordering, such that the model learns
to ignore the ordering of the documents. Additionally, if the documents can be ordered, i.e.
with document ranking, the Serial input strategy could also be fed with a prioritized list of
documents, such that the best document is processed last. This approach leans slightly
back towards singleôÙdocument summarization, so the impact of each ordering strategy
would also have to be investigated thoroughly.
7.4
NôÙgram prediction
We used the 2ôÙgram version of ProphetNet, but ProphetNet is able to do NôÙgram predicôÙ
tions. The original paper showed that there was little performance gain from bigram to
trigram with much more computational cost. This performance difference was for singleôÙ
document summarization using the CNN/DailyMail dataset. We believe there might be a
differences with multiôÙdocument summarization as the task has changed. Investigation
the NôÙgram comparison for multiôÙdocument datasets might not only lead to better perforôÙ
mance, but might also give great insight in the how multiôÙdocument summaries are best
generated.

7.5
PreôÙtraining
As previously discussed in section 6.5, the model shows a certain discomfort during multiôÙ
document training. We hypothesize that this is likely due to the proposed methods and
that the model partly resists the adaptation. We consider the fact that the adaptations
might be a little too forceful and that the model could benefit from a transition period.
A transition period could be a small amount of preôÙtraining, where the model is slowly
adapted to the new input format. We believe this will help with the instability of the model.
Additionally, as mentioned in section 5.5, the singleôÙdocument models are better at adaptôÙ
ing than multiôÙdocument models. We showed that the singleôÙdocument modelãs ability
to do multiôÙdocument summarization exceeded the multiôÙdocument modelãs ability to do
singleôÙdocument summarization. Both our singleôÙ and multiôÙdocument models have had
the same starting checkpoint, right after the ProphetNet preôÙtraining. We hypothesize that
multiôÙdocument summarization might benefit from an initial singleôÙdocument fineôÙtuning
first. In other words, we fineôÙtune on CNN/DailyMail first before fineôÙtuning on WCEP.
This 2ôÙstep approach might improve the performance of multiôÙdocument summarization,
as the model is taught the fundamentals of summarization first.

Conclusion
We started by repeating the work of ProphetNet, achieving results slightly below the origôÙ
inal paper, most likely due to a few alterations. We show that ProphetNet is easily transôÙ
ferable to a multilingual setting, using ProphetNetôÙMulti to achieve stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart results
in Danish. These results cement the high quality of ProphetNet, both in English and in
multilingual settings.
We proposed two methods, dubbed DocMean and Serial, to adapt ProphetNet from singleôÙ
document to multiôÙdocument summarization. Both methods show great promise, with SeôÙ
rial matching everything but current stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart. All models, especially DocMean, the
strategy which averages the encoder output over the document axis, struggled with facôÙ
tual consistency. This was shown using a combination of human evaluation and NERO
scores, our own proposed metric. NERO is based on named entity recognition and meaôÙ
sures the overlap between entities in the source documents and a summary. We show
that this simple metric is able to capture certain aspects of factual consistency.
The exact characteristics of each methods were not uncovered, but both methods showed
distinct features. DocMean showed signs of struggling with language itself while Serial
appeared influenced by the document ordering. Both methods also showed signs of instaôÙ
bility, being prone to catastrophic failures, poor zeroôÙshotting and bad correlation of loss
and ROUGE scores. The reasons and solutions are left uninvestigated, leaving much to
future work.
Our work shows that transformerôÙbased singleôÙdocument summarization models are easôÙ
ily adapted to multiôÙdocument summarization and that these adaptions still uphold the
performance of the model. Our methods can be transferred to multiple languages and
models, although the performance of other models are unknown. We show that multiôÙ
document summarization does not require complex ideas and novel architecture, but that
previous work can be utilized and adapted.
The paper resembles a proof of concept, where the optimizations have yet to be explored.
This leaves a lot of future work, both by optimising our approaches, but also extending
our methods into other models. There are lots to be learned from our results regarding
multiôÙdocument summarization.
