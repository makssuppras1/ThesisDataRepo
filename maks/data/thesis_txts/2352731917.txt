Abstract 
Since the conduction of a passive surveillance programme in Denmark in 2010 it has become possible to 
detect influenza A virus subtypes circulating in the Danish pig population. In 2014 a new so-called triple-
reassortant was found. This new subtype was characterized by having a HA gene that the H3 gene found in 
the human seasonal influenza from 2004/2005, N2 from already circulating swine influenzas and the internal 
genes from the H1N1 2009 pandemic influenza A virus. The aim of this veterinary master project was to 
characterize this new subtype both genetically and phylogenetically. The characterization was obtained by 
performing hemagglutinin activity test, culturing in MDCK cells and by RT-PCR.    
A correlation between herds owned by the same farmer and positive of the H3hu05N2sw subtype was found. 
These herds also had the same veterinarian.  
None of the isolates from this study period were capable of being cultured in MDCK cells, embryonated 
chicken eggs and showed no detectable HA activity. The HA activity was tested on erythrocytes from 
chicken and turkeys. Other studies had found and described several amino acid positions with mutations in 
the sequence of the HA gene, which may explain the reduced ability of recently isolated H3N2 subtypes to 
be cultured and show HA activity. These mutations were also detected in the isolates in this study. 
Furthermore, three additional mutations were discovered in this study at positions 171, 189 and 244. These 
mutations are located in the area described by others to have influence on the change observed in receptor 
affinity.  
Two isolates, submitted to the passive surveillance programme before this study, were capable of being 
cultured in MDCK cells. In these two isolates a mutation was found at position 158 in the HA gene. The 
location of arginine at this position may explain why these isolates could be cultured.     
With real-time multiplex RT-PCR it was possible to detect samples with a human H3 gene (H3hu). Full-
length RT-PCR was performed on these samples and the RNA extraction was sent to Sanger sequencing. A 
phylogenetic analysis of the sequences containing the H3hu gene was performed. The analysis revealed that 
isolates with H3hu gene resembling the one from the human seasonal influenza from 2004/2005 all clustered 
together in the phylogenetic tree. The H3hu05 sequences had drifted from the sequence of the H3 gene used 
in the vaccine currently used to vaccinate swine against swine influenza. It is doubtful that the swine 
influenza vaccine protects against this new subtype H3hu05N2sw. This can be an important health factor for 
the animals, since the H3hu05N2sw subtype has been established in the Danish population of swine.   
    
In conclusion, amino acid substitutions were found in the area of the HA gene, which are responsible for 
receptor binding affinity. Furthermore, three mutations not previously described were found in the same area 
of the HA gene. Isolates capable of being cultured in MDCK cells had arginine at position 158 in the HA 
gene, whereas the other isolates had lysine, glycine or asparagine. These preliminary phylogenetis findings 
lead to doubt about whether the Respiporc vaccine currently used in swine provides protection against the 
new subtype.

Resumé 
Siden oprettelsen af et passivt overvågningsprogram i Danmark, blev det muligt at detektere subtyper af 
influenza A virus, der cirkulerer i danske svin. I 2014 blev en triple-reassortant fundet. Den nye subtype var 
karakteriseret ved at have et HA gen, der minder mest om det H3 gen, der florerede i den humane 
sæsoninfluenza i 2004/2005. Endvidere havde subtypen et N2 gen fra etablerede svineinfluenzaer og de 
interne gener stammede fra H1N1 pandemien i 2009. Formålet med dette speciale var at karakterisere den 
nye subtype både genetisk og fylogenetisk. Karakteriseringen blev opnået ved at udføre hæmagglutinin 
aktivitets tests, dyrke virus i MDCK celler og ved RT-PCR.   
En association mellem besætninger, ejet af samme ejer og som var positive for H3hu05N2sw subtypen, blev 
fundet. Endvidere havde besætningerne også samme besætningsdyrlæge. 
Ingen af isolaterne, der blev indsendt til dette projekt, kunne dyrkes i MDCK celler, embryonerede æg fra 
høns og viste ingen HA aktivitet. HA aktiviteten blev udført med erythrocytter fra høns og kalkuner. Andre 
studier har fundet og beskrevet adskillige aminosyre positioner med mutationer i HA genets sekvens. Disse 
mutationer menes at kunne forklare den ændrede egenskab af nyligt isolerede H3N2 isolater til at kunne 
dyrkes i MDCK celler og til at vise HA aktivitet. Mutationerne blev genfundet i isolaterne, der indgik i dette 
projekt. Endvidere blev tre nye mutationer identificeret på positionerne 171, 189 og 244. De tre mutationer 
var lokaliseret i området beskrevet af andre til at have indflydelse på ændringen i receptor affinitet.  
To isolater indsendt til det passive overvågningsprogram før dette projekts opstart kunne dyrkes i MDCK 
celler. I disse to isolater blev der fundet en mutation på position 158 i HA genet. Tilstedeværelsen af arginin 
på position 158 kan forklare, hvorfor disse isolater kunne dyrkes.  
Med real-time multiplex RT-PCR var det muligt at identificere prøver med et humant H3 gen (H3hu). Fuld-
længde RT-PCR blev kørt på disse prøver og RNA’et blev sendt til Sanger sekventering. En fylogenetisk 
analyse af sekvenserne indeholdende H3hu genet blev udført. Analysen afslørede, at isolater med H3hu 
genet, der mindede om det H3 gen fra den humane sæsoninfluenza i 2004/2005 alle samlede sig sammen i 
det fylogenetiske træ. H3hu05 sekvenserne var drevet fra H3 genet, der på nuværende tidspunkt indgår i den 
vaccine, man bruger til at vaccinere svin mod influenza. Det er derfor tvivlsomt, om vaccinen mod 
svineinfluenza beskytter mod H3hu05N2sw subtypen. Dette udgør en mulig sundhedsrisikofaktor for dyrene, 
da H3hu05N2sw subtypen betragtes som værende etableret i de danske svinebesætninger.  
 
I dette speciale blev mutationer, som menes at have betydning for affiniteten af receptorbindingsstedet og 
den reducerede HA aktivitet, også fundet. Endvidere, blev tre mutationer, ikke beskrevet tidligere, fundet i 
samme område af HA genet. Isolater, der kunne dyrkes i MDCK celler havde arginin på position 158 i HA 
genet, mens de øvrige isolater (som ikke kunne dyrkes) havde lysin, glycin eller asparagin. De foreløbige 
fylogenetiske resultater stiller spørgsmålstegn ved, om Respiporc vaccinen, der bruges på nuværende 
tidspunkt, beskytter mod den nye subtype.

Literature study 
Introduction 
Influenza viruses are a member of the Orthomyxoviridae family and are a spherical or helical 
enveloped virus with a diameter of 80 to 120 nm.  
Influenza viruses are divided into types – A, B or C. The type is mainly decided based on the 
nucleoprotein (NP) and matrix protein (M1). Influenza A viruses are categorised into different 
subtypes based on the antigenic and genetic variations in the two glycoproteins hemagglutinin (HA) 
and neuraminidase (NA) located on the surface of the virus [1] (figure 1). In both type A and B the 
antigens to HA and NA are subject to genetic variation, making these two proteins most unstable 
genetically and therefore prone to mutations. This warrants the emergence of new influenza strains, 
e.g. the human seasonal influenza that changes genetically from year to year, making it nearly 
impossible for the human immune system to recognize the virus [2]. 
 
 
Figure 1: Visual representation of the Orthomyxoviruses. The NP and M1 proteins decide the type of the influenza. The 
glycoproteins hemagglutinin (HA) and neuraminidase (NA) are seen as “spikes” on the viral surface and decides the subtype of 
influenza A viruses [3].  
 
Influenza virus A is known to cause worldwide epidemics of influenza and seldom genuine 
pandemics. To this date, only viruses expressing H1, H2 or H3 and N1 or N2 subtypes have been 
found in relation to pandemic human disease [4]. 
Through reverse genetics, scientists have been able to recreate several of the viral strains 
responsible for previous pandemics and characterize these both in vivo and in vitro. One of these 
viruses is the H1N1 responsible for the 1918 “Spanish” influenza pandemic, where it is estimated 
that 50 million people lost their lives. The Spanish pandemic occurred in three waves where the 
==PAGE:11==
 
11
final two waves are estimated to have had a mortality rate greater than 2,5%, which is significantly 
higher than the normal rate of around 1% attributed to seasonal influenza infections [4]. 
The H1N1 re-emerged a few times during the 20th century. In 2009 an antigenically new H1N1 was 
found in Mexico. It is thought that frequent international air travel helped spread the virus rapidly. 
Spreading from Mexico to 74 countries and every continent in five weeks, where prior pandemic 
viruses have crossed continent-lines over a period of several months. The 2009 pandemic virus 
caused relatively mild symptoms but was highly transmissible, spreading to the northern 
hemisphere in the warmer months where influenza strains usually circulate strictly in the colder 
months of the year [5]. 
The 2009 H1N1 pandemic strain derived from adapted swine influenza viruses, carrying distinct 
segments from two lineages. Six segments came from North American triple reassortant swine 
lineage – PB1, PB2, PA, HA, NP and NS. The NA and M segments originated from the European 
avian-like swine line (figure 2). 
 
Figure 2: The genetic origin of the 2009 pandemic H1N1 segments. The 2009 H1N1 pandemic strain derived from adapted swine 
influenza viruses, carrying distinct segments from two lineages. Six segments came from North American triple reassortant swine 
lineage – PB1, PB2, PA, HA, NP and NS. The NA and M segments originated from the European avian-like swine line [6].  
 
In Denmark it is estimated that approximately 1000 to 2000 people each year die due to 
complications of infection with influenza virus and up to 20% of the population is infected during 
an influenza epidemic. The exact number of Danish people being infected during the seasonal 
influenza is underestimated since the numbers available at the Danish Health Authority, is based on 
reports from doctors who sampled from patients who might suffer from an influenza infection. The 
exact number of infected humans is greater than the numbers in the reports since many people do 
not consult a doctor when suffering from influenza symptoms [7].  
==PAGE:12==
 
12
In the United States it has been estimated that the annual financial costs of influenza is around 
$10,4 billion for direct medical costs and on average $87,1 billion for the total economic burden of 
the annual influenza epidemics [8]. 
The people at highest risk of becoming severely ill are pregnant women, people over 65 years old, 
obese people with a body mass index (BMI) over 40 and patients that are immunosuppressed due to 
various reasons. People belonging to one or more of these risk groups are offered a free yearly 
vaccination against influenza in Denmark [9].  
Infection with influenza A viruses is not only a concern for the public health but also a concern in 
the production of pigs. The swine industry in Denmark produces over 30 million pigs per year [10]. 
With the help of the passive surveillance program it was in 2014 discovered that more than 90% of 
the Danish pig herds were antibody positive for a subtype of influenza A viruses. Even though an 
infection with influenza in swine often is uncomplicated [11] it still has the potential for financial 
depreciation due to the risk of secondary bacterial infections, lack of growth and general distress in 
the affected animal [12].  
 
Host range of Influenza A viruses 
Since influenza A virus is a RNA virus, and thus more prone to mutations, the virus can quickly 
evolve and adapt within a new species and thereby being able to replicate and spread to other 
members of the species. The influenza A viruses possesses a great spectrum of host diversity. The 
host diversity ranges from waterfowl and poultry to swine, humans, horses, dogs, cats, whales, seals 
and other mammalian species and even reptiles [1]. 
The wild waterfowl, such as ducks, serve as a natural reservoir from which every influenza virus is 
thought to have emerged from [13]. Even though the wild waterfowl can be infected with influenza, 
they rarely show signs of clinical disease, and if so, only mild gastrointestinal symptoms are seen 
[14]. 
In the reservoir population only low pathogenic avian influenza viruses (LPAI) circulate. However, 
the LPAI viruses of the H5 and H7 subtypes are able to infect domesticated birds and then evolve 
into a more troublesome high-pathogenic avian influenza (HPAI) due to an insertion in the HA gene 
[1]. HPAI seen in domesticated poultry can cause disease affecting multiple organs with a very high 
mortality of up to 90-100% within 48 hours [15]. 
 
On the surface of Influenza A viruses membrane glycoproteins are found. One of these is HA, 
which is responsible for binding the virus to cell surface receptors. Sialic acids (SA) are regarded as 
==PAGE:13==
 
13
receptors for influenza viruses and are typically bound to galactose (Gal) in an α2,6 or α2,3 
configuration [16] (figure 3). 
The small SA molecule, sitting outermost of the galactose string, is what the HA binds to, when 
infecting a cell. The specificity of the HA towards the SA molecules differs. In avian influenza 
viruses the HA bind preferentially towards sialic acid α2,3 galactose (SAα2,3Gal), whereas human 
HAs bind with greater affinity towards SAα2,6Gal [17].  
The expression of the glycans to which the SA binds, will most likely contribute to the host 
restriction by affecting viral binding and tropism, but other factors from both host and virus 
influences as well [18]. 
 
Figure 3: Visualization of the placement of sialic acid on the tip of the galactose string, and the chemical structure of sialic 
acid. Left: cell protein embedded in the plasma membrane of the host cell. The protein is a transmembrane protein streaking from the 
cytoplasma to the extracellular area. The orange spikes are glycoproteins with sialic acid being the last sugar attached to a protein in 
the chain of sugars. Right: chemical structure of sialic acid. The sugar next to sialic acid is galactose. The HA protein of influenza 
viruses binds to the sialic acid [19]. 
 
SAs are found different places depending on the species – in humans the SAα2,6Gal is present on 
the surface of epithelial cells in the trachea, and SAα2,3Gal can be found in the distal parts of the 
lungs. In the porcine trachea, the epithelial cells contain mainly SAα2,6Gal but SAα2,3Gal can be 
found both in the tracheal area and much more so in the caudal parts of the lungs in type II 
pneumocytes and cuboidal noncilliated cells of the terminal airways (figure 4). The location of the 
specific SA moieties within the respiratory microenvironment could be critical to the outcome of 
exposure [18].  
The intestinal tract of ducks contains mainly SAα2,3Gal and is a major replicative site of avian 
influenza viruses [13]. 
 
==PAGE:14==
 
14
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 4: Overall distribution of SAα2,6Gal and SAα2,3Gal in different species. The blue and yellow stars both indicate 
SAα2,3Gal. As seen in the picture, both humans and swine have the same receptors in the respiratory canal. Modified from [20].  
 
During experiments with influenza, several studies have found that both human and avian influenza 
viruses readily infects swine, and replicate efficiently in the pig due to the presence of both 
SAα2,3Gal and SAα2,6Gal, making it the perfect “mixing vessel” for the production of new 
reassortant influenza subtypes. Both phylogenetic and epidemiologic studies support the finding 
that human and avian influenza viruses have been spread to pigs in the past [21]. A reassortant is 
the creation of a new subtype due to co-infection with two different subtypes in the same host cell 
(figure 5). Due to the segmented RNA genome in influenza viruses, an inter-change of segments 
can occur [1].

different viruses. Since the pig can be infected with both avian and human influenza viruses, it is considered as a mixing vessel

Pathogenesis and clinical signs 
Pathogenesis – human and porcine 
The pathogenesis of influenza depends on the function of the host immune system, since it is 
responsible for protecting the host against the influenza virus. Influenza viruses infect respiratory 
epithelial cells or alveolar macrophages [22]. When these cells encounter the RNA of the virus, it is 
recognized by toll-like-receptors (TLR) placed in the cell-membrane. TLRs are a part of the innate 
immune response. Influenza viruses can escape the innate immune response by using the gene NS1 
to disrupt the RIG-I signaling pathway. RIG-I is responsible for recognizing influenza particles and 
alerting the immune system. Therefore, NS1 is presumed to aid the influenza-infection by 
controlling antiviral innate immune responses and by binding protein kinase R (PKR) which is an 
antiviral protein [23]. 
During infection with influenza an upregulation of numerous inflammatory cytokines and 
chemokines is induced. These cytokines and chemokines are thought to play an important part in 
the pathogenesis and pathology of influenza [24]. The cytokines and chemokines include IL-1β, IL-
6, TNFα and several types of CCL (a chemokine-ligand), where CCL-2 is responsible for attracting 
macrocytes, memory T-cells and dendritic cells to the site of inflammation [23]. 
Induction of all these inflammatory mediators have been proposed as the reason for the systemic 
symptoms induced by influenza infection [24]. 
 
When the influenza virus comes into contact with a host cell, the HA glycoprotein on the virion 
surface is responsible for binding and entering the host cell via endocytosis [25]. (See the sections 
“Influenza surface-membrane glycoproteins – neuraminidase and hemagglutinin” and “Genome 
and replication” for more info on this subject).

In the case of human influenza, aerosol transmission is the primary route of infection e.g. by an 
infected person coughing or sneezing near a susceptible person, or by indirect transmission via 
contaminated surfaces.

Genome and replication 
The genome of the influenza virus type A consists of eight segments of negative sense, single 
stranded RNA (ssRNA) (table 1) [27]. 
The eight segments in the influenza viral genome encode for the 11 viral genes (figure 6) [28].  
The three largest segments of the viral RNA encode the polymerase proteins – PB2 (segment 1), 
PB1 (segment 2) and PA (segment 3). Besides encoding the PB1 protein, segment 2 encodes for 
another two proteins – PB1-F2 (a pro-apoptotic virulence factor) and PB1-N40 (a variant of the 
PB1 protein).

Segment 5 encodes the protein NP, which binds to the viral RNA segments along the entire length 
of the strand and plays an important role in the viral replication process. 
 
The genome segments denominated 7 and 8 both encode for messenger RNAs (mRNAs) that are

non-structural protein NS1 and an export protein NS2/NEP [30].

Transcription and replication 
Influenza viruses are different from other RNA negative sense viruses in the fact that they only 
transcribe and replicate the viral genome inside the nucleus of the infected cell [27]. Since the 
genome only encodes for 11 proteins the influenza virus has generated an amount of methods to 
utilize the host cells own transcriptive machinery [28]. 
 
The transcriptive and replicative process of influenza viruses is a multistep-procedure. Firstly, the 
virus binds to the cell surface with HA. Through endocytosis the virus enters the cell. Inside the 
endosome the acidic environment triggers a conformational change in HA that causes the viral 
envelope to fuse with the vacuole membrane. The M2 ion channel protein allows protons to enter 
the viral core causing it to disassemble and release the viral RNA (vRNA) and core proteins – 
forming a complex that is transported to the host cell nucleus where the transcriptive process begins 
[32]. 
 
In both infected cells and virions the vRNA segments exists as complexes of ribonucleoprotein 
(RNP) containing the RNA dependent RNA-polymerase complex (PB1, PB2 and PA proteins and 
multiple copies of NP) (figure 7) [30]. 
 
The host cell nucleus is the location of all viral RNA synthesis. The synthesis produce two types of 
RNA; the vRNA segments that will go on to form the genome of the progeny virus and the capped 
polyadenylated mRNA  that serves as template for the host-cell translation of viral proteins [32]. 
==PAGE:20==
 
20
The replicative process of the vRNA contains two stages. Throughout the first stage, the genomic 
vRNA is replicated to a full-length string of complementary DNA (cDNA). In the second part of the 
replication the cDNA is copied into negative sense single stranded vRNA ready to use in the 
progeny virion [30]. 
 
The viral mRNA differs from the host cell mRNA in its polyadenylated (poly(A)) tail (a stretch of 
RNA containing only adenine bases). The host cell mRNA is polyadenylated by specific poly(A) 
polymerase, while the poly(A) tail of the viral mRNA is encoded in negative sense vRNA as a 
string of five to seven uracil residues that will be transcribed into positive sense as a string of 
adenosines forming the poly(A) tail by the viral polymerase [32].  
In order for the mRNA to be properly translated it is “5’ capped”. This occurs when the PB1 and 
PB2 proteins steal 5’capped primers from host pre-mRNA – a process called cap-snatching. 
Capping the mRNA is important since the capping regulates nuclear export and prevents 
degradation by endonucleases and promotes translation by ribosomes [28], [33]. 
Once the viral mRNA is polyadenylated and capped it can be translated just like the host mRNA. 
The export from the nucleus out into the cytoplasm is mediated by the viral proteins M1 and 
NEP/NS2 (figure 7).

The viral envelope and glycoproteins (HA, NA and M2) are synthesized in the cytoplasm on 
membrane bound ribosomes into the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) [32]. 
Unlike the membrane proteins, the soluble proteins including enzymes are synthesized on free 
ribosomes in the cytoplasm.  
 
Most of the viral proteins will undergo a post-translational alteration, either by phosphorylation or 
proteolytic cleavage or glycosylation. By glycosylation the protein has sugar side-chains added as 
the protein is being transferred from the ER to the Golgi apparatus, preparing it before the final 
assembly of progeny virions [27]. 
 
In the final steps before the progeny virion is ready to be released from the host cell, and go on to 
infect nearby cells, the virion needs to be assembled. Since influenza is an enveloped virus it makes 
use of the host cell’s plasma membrane to form the outer membrane of the virion [28].  
The process of budding from the plasma membrane is divided into three major steps. 1) Initiation of 
budding, 2) elongation of the bud and finally, 3) bud completion resulting in the release of virions 
from the host cell membrane [35].  
 
Influenza viruses utilize domains in the host cell plasma membrane on the apical side known as 
“lipid rafts” to assemble virions and facilitate budding. The viral budding from the host cell outer 
membrane does not seem to lower the integrity of the membrane. For that reason, the majority of 
enveloped viruses are non-cytopathic and can be seen in persistent infections [27].  
Lipid rafts are foci in the plasma membrane that are especially rich in cholesterol and sphingo-lipid 
(a class of lipids containing a sphingoid-backbone, found abundantly in neuronal cells) [36].  
From experiments, it seems these specific microdomains on the plasma membrane have a 
predestined position since it has been shown that multiple virions are released from the same place 
on the membrane. This suggests that the transport and assembly machinery in the cell deliver the 
viral components to the same spot every time [35]. 
 
The mechanisms for bud initiation are not currently known, but it is suspected that lipid rafts and 
proteins associated with the rafts along with the HA glycoprotein play an important role in the 
initiation of the membrane curvature [36]. 
==PAGE:22==
 
22
The growth of the influenza bud seems to depend on two things, 1) a “pulling” force provided by 
the transmembrane proteins (HA, NA and M1), and 2) Ribonucleoprotein (RNP) bound 
microfilaments provides a pushing force destined on incorporation of the RNPs and M1 into the 
virion. The closure and subsequent release of the virion is surprisingly inefficient. Only about 10% 
of the formed viral buds are released, while the rest remains attached to the host cell membrane. It 
is believed that this emanates from the process of pinching off the formed virions being energy 
dependent. Therefore a drop in available energy at the end of the process can be the cause of the 
inefficient release of buds [35].  
 
Influenza surface-membrane glycoproteins – neuraminidase and hemagglutinin 
On the surface membrane of every influenza A and B virus are hundreds of little spikes protruding 
from the surface. These spikes are hemagglutinin (HA) and neuraminidase (NA) glycoproteins.  
HA is placed evenly around the membrane, whereas NA seems to cluster around the region where 
the budding virus gets released from cells.  
The glycoproteins are fastened to the viral membrane by a region of amino acids that extends from 
the inner surface of the viral outer membrane and here interacts with the matrix protein M1 [37]. 
NA is a type II membrane glycoprotein. It has an uncharged region close to the N-terminus, which 
acts as a signal sequence and a membrane anchor. The NA glycoprotein is a tetramer with identical 
subunits, and is about 470 amino acids long [38]. 
In contrast to NA, the HA glycoprotein is a type I membrane glycoprotein. It consists of an N-
terminal signal sequence and a C-terminal membrane anchor. The HA glycoprotein is a trimer, with 
identical subunits that consists of 540-550 amino acids [39]. 
 
Mechanism of action 
The function of HA is recognizing the receptor, sialic acid, which is found on host cell surface 
membranes. HA is the glycoprotein responsible for receptor binding to cell-surface sialylated 
glycoproteins. 
When the HA has bound to the receptor, the virus is transported into cells by endocytosis as 
described in “Genome and replication” [40]. Binding of influenza virus to a cell membrane via HA 
receptor binding is virtually irreversible. This is probably due to the large number of HA covering 
the viral surface-membrane, and all of these HA being able to bind to receptors on the cell surface 
[39].  
==PAGE:23==
 
23
Endocytic vesicles are acidified by a membrane proton pump to a pH value of 5 to 6. This acidic 
environment activates HA to mediate membrane fusion, so that the virus and endosomal lipid 
membrane forms a fused single membrane [40]. 
After HA has mediated a fusion of viral and endosomal membranes, the viral genome transcriptase 
complex is delivered into the cell for viral replication to begin [25]. 
It does seem that NA only functions in the later stages of infection, since the binding of receptors 
and the fusion of endosomal membrane and viral membrane has been accomplished with viruses 
only expressing HA on their surface membranes. NA is part of the infection by removing SA from 
newly formed NA and HA – as a result of this, new viruses are released to spread the infection [38].  
 
Origins of hemagglutinin and neuraminidase 
So far, sixteen subtypes of influenza A HA (H1-H16) and 9 subtypes of NA (N1-N9) have been 
identified and examined structurally and antigen-wise. These different subtypes are found in 
different combinations in avian viruses.  
Only H1-H3 and N1-N2 have been found to be constituents in human influenza membranes. In 
genetic analysis completed on human influenza A viruses it appears that the HA and NA of human 
virions derive from both avian and swine influenza subtypes and that inter-species transmission 
does result in changes in affinity for HA receptor binding and NA mediated release of new virions 
from the host-cell [41]. 
 
Antibodies and antigenic drift 
Neutralizing antibodies target the HA and thereby counteract the cell binding ability of the 
influenza virus. Antibodies bind to the receptor binding domains of HA, and inhibit the change in 
HA conformation needed to fuse the viral and host-cell membranes [25]. Therefore, the virus seeks 
to change the structure of HA to prevent the binding of antibodies. 
Variation in antigens originates from the selection of new mutations, the result of an immune 
pressure from infected human monoclonal antibodies. This variation is specified as antigenic drift 
[25]. The exact mechanism of this selective process is still unknown, but it is known to be highly 
important for the virus, since it allows reinfection of previously immunized individuals [39]. 
Variation occurs primarily in the HA glycopolypeptide [39] and examination of the sites where 
amino acid substitutions have been discovered, indicates that variation occurs predominantly in the 
distal areas of the surface membrane, in the HA1 receptor binding site [25].  
==PAGE:24==
 
24
Additionally, the attachment of oligosaccharides provides mechanisms for eluding the immune 
responses from the host. Both of these mechanisms are detected in HA. 
The surface of HA is covered in carbohydrate side chains. These are synthesized by host-cell 
enzymes and are therefore, perceived as antigenically “self” and do not induce immune response in 
the form of antibodies. 
During the process of antigenic drift, substitutions in amino acids forms new oligosaccharide 
attachment sites in regions binding antibodies, thereby creating resistance to antibody binding [40]. 
 
Vaccines 
The most effective method of protecting against infection with influenza is through vaccination. 
Vaccination consists of introducing small amounts of infective material in order to induce immunity 
without causing primary illness. Evidence has been found on the topics of vaccine safety and 
efficacy in current inactive influenza vaccines, and it strongly suggests the benefits of immunization 
of patients in high risk and in addition, proposes an economic benefit of vaccination programmes 
[42]. 
The vaccines most commonly used are parenterally administered, killed or inactivated vaccines 
used to induce HA and possibly NA immunity [43].  
Human vaccines are re-evaluated annually to ensure there is an antigenic match between the 
vaccine and the current strains [44]. A new human vaccine is needed every year due to the high 
selection pressure on the influenza virus from the immunized population, leading to new subtypes 
every year.  
Presently, a consensus exists that a dose of influenza vaccine should contain 15µg HA antigen per 
strain [45].  
 
Data on the safety of vaccines are obtained from various sources such as clinical studies, case 
reports and pharmacovigilance programmes, both national and industrial. The adverse effects of 
influenza vaccine are rare, but it should be pointed out that very few of the total adverse reactions 
are reported [42]. Immediate hypersensitivity reactions can occur and are attributed to IgE mediated 
hypersensitivity towards egg protein or other vaccine components. On very rare occasions, a 
vaccination with influenza can cause Guillan-Barré Syndrome (GBS). In GBS, the immune system 
attacks otherwise healthy nerves. Symptoms of GBS are muscle weakness and partial to complete 
paralysis [46]. 
 
==PAGE:25==
 
25
Vaccines are found in different formulations (live, attenuated or inactivated) and with different 
adjuvants. Live vaccines are not as commonly used these days, as the live virus is capable of 
inducing lethal illness in especially immunosuppressed individuals [47]. Inactivated vaccines were 
first used in the US during the Second World War, and are still used to this day. Several clinical 
studies have shown an efficacy of inactivated vaccines in decreasing serological infection with 
influenza with 70% in healthy adults [47]. 
 
The first inactivated vaccine virus was derived from allantoic fluid from embryonated chicken eggs 
(ECE), and inactivated with formalin. The preparation resulted in frequent local and systemic 
reactions [45]. 
 
Currently, the virus is still inactivated with formaldehyde or β-propiolactone, but since the 
purification methods have improved greatly, both the amount of formaldehyde/ β-propiolactone and 
egg protein have decreased greatly in the finished product [48]. 
 
Previously the inactivated vaccine was administered without the addition of adjuvants. Adjuvants 
trick the immune system to respond as though there where an active infection, giving the vaccine a 
more effective immunization [49]. Studies in the early days suggested that water-in-oil emulsions 
with mineral oil could improve antibody responses. However, the use of oil-emulsions often 
resulted in local sterile abscesses, which restricted the use. Recently, an oil-in-water emulsion using 
metabolizable oil squalene greatly improves the immune responses while having an excellent safety 
profile. The squalene adjuvant (MF59) have been shown to increase antibody titers in older adults 
by 50% [45]. 
 
Live attenuated influenza vaccines (LAIV) are generally more potent mainly because they still have 
a large number of effector mechanisms in comparison to the “killed” vaccines. LAIV still produce a 
response from cytotoxic CD8 T cells, thus inducing a far better immune response in the recipient. 
 
LAIV are traditionally attenuated by growing the virus in non-human cells, and through adaptation 
to the non-human cell culture, the virus will lose the ability to replicate in human cells when given 
as a vaccine (figure 8). Thereby, the LAIV is still able to induce immunity, without risk of 
replicating. In newer experiments, the attenuation of the virus is accomplished by means of 
==PAGE:26==
 
26
recombinant DNA techniques. The virulence gene of the virus is isolated and can either be deleted 
or mutated until the virus is no longer virulent [49]. The result is a virus with the antigenic 
phenotype but without the infectious potential [47]. 
 
Figure 8: Traditional method of attenuation by serial passages in non-human cell lines, thereby adapting the virus to non-

The human influenza vaccines used in Denmark are trivalent vaccines containing inactivated 
viruses from influenza A subtypes H1N1pdm09, H3N2 and an influenza B subtype. 
 
All the isolates used in the vaccines are grown in specific pathogen free (SPF) embryonated chicken 
eggs, and all contains the recommended amount of HA, which is 15µg [51]. 
 
The World Health Organization (WHO) annually renews the vaccines to reflect the current subtypes 
circulating in the human population. The WHO then makes recommendations for the individual 
components in influenza vaccine for that specific year, in order for the production agencies to 
produce the best vaccine possible [52].

Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) 
The process of Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) is an in vitro amplification of specific DNA sites. 
The technique is based on the following three steps:  
 
Each cycle in a PCR reaction consists of: (figure 10): 
1. Denaturation. The double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) is converted into single-stranded 
DNA (ssDNA). This step usually transpires at a temperature of 92-96°C. At this 
temperature the hydrogen bridges between the dsDNA is broken.   
2. Annealing. During this step, the sequence specific primers bind to their complementary 
DNA sequence on the single stranded nucleic acid. The primers align, so their 3’ 
hydroxyl end faces the target on the DNA string. Annealing occurs at 50-60°C, where 
DNA strands naturally try to pair up. However, the contents in the PCR mix contain 
enough primers to lock onto their target sequences. This prevents the DNA strands for 
converting back into the double helix structure. 
3. Elongation. The primer is elongated because of the heat stable DNA polymerase 
enzyme. The DNA polymerase is activated at approximately 70°C. The polymerase 
extends the primer by adding nucleotides (A, C, T, or G) according to the base-pairing 
==PAGE:32==
 
32
principle on the 3’-OH group on the primer [89]. Creation of new dsDNA as an exact 
copy of the original DNA template is made, which then can serve as a template in the 
following cycles. Thus resulting in an exponential amplification of DNA. 
 
 
Figure 10: PCR reaction consisting of three steps. 1: Denaturation where the hydrogen bindings in the DNA double 
helix is broken, creating two single-stranded nucleic acids. 2: Annealing where the primers (and probes if real-time 
PCR) bind complementary to the single stranded acid. 3: Elongation where the sequence specific primers are elongated 
because of the polymerase enzyme that put new bases on the elongating strand. (Enzoklop, 2014 and [90]).  
 
A PCR reaction consists of enough cycles to produce a sufficient amount of DNA product. Each of 
the three step cycles produces an exponential amount of product than the cycle before, since the 
amplified DNA site can be used as a template for the next cycle. Each cycle is repeated 
approximately 40 times [91].

The primers used for the PCR reactions are one of the most important contents in the master mix. 
The primers are accountable of amplification of only the specific DNA fragment of interest. The 
specificity of the PCR reaction depends on whether one succeeds in designing sequence specific 
primers of an appropriate length and that the primers bind complementarily to the target. It is 
important that the primers are sequence specific for a specific target, otherwise the primers will 
bind at several places in the genome and this will result in unspecific PCR products [92]. The 
design of primers is a complex assignment. The primers have to bind to highly conserved parts of 
the DNA single strand to elude any mismatching. The perfect PCR primer consists of a CG content 
of 40-60% and is at least 20 nucleotides long and have an annealing temperature at 50-60°C [89].  
 
Originally, the DNA polymerase used for PCR originated from the bacteria Escherichia Coli. This 
polymerase furthered the PCR process enormously, but it had a few flaws. It could not withstand 
the temperatures used in the denaturation step in each cycle and thus was inactivated irreversibly at 
this point in each cycle. Therefore it had to be added once again at the annealing step. To overcome 
this problem, scientists found the bacteria Thermus aquaticus (Taq). This bacterium grows in hot 
springs and proliferates despite the high temperatures. The DNA polymerase from Taq is not 
rapidly inactivated from the high temperatures. This allows the PCR process to take place at higher 
temperatures than with the Escherichia Coli polymerase. This allows for higher stringency in 
primer binding, and as a result, higher fidelity in the DNA template replication [93].

Reverse Transcriptase Polymerase Chain Reaction (RT-PCR) 
An important progress within the field of PCR was the development of the Reverse Transcriptase 
Polymerase Chain Reaction. RT-PCR allows an indirect way of using RNA as a template for the 
final PCR reaction. Since influenza viruses have an RNA genome, it is necessary to convert the 
ribonucleic acid to complementary DNA (cDNA). The PCR reaction is then performed on the 
cDNA template [94]. This step is necessary to ensure proper DNA polymerase function. The 
conversion is ensured by the Reverse Transcriptase (RT) enzyme, which translates the viral RNA 
into a cDNA template. This conversion is done before starting the PCR cycles [95]. The RT enzyme 
is usually activated at temperatures around 50-55°C. The RT step is necessary for both the 
conventional and real-time PCR processes when targeting RNA. The addition of “RT” before PCR 
indicates that a reverse transcriptase step is performed in this type of PCR reaction. The reactants in 
RT-PCR are listed in table 5. Quantification of RNA by conventional RT-PCR assays has 
limitations. The most important one being that the quantification is an “end-point” analysis. This 
kind of analysis cannot detect differences between several samples if each reaches a plateau level 
before the PCR reaction has completed. This would result in bands on the agarose gel showing the 
same level of fluorescence even though the concentration of virus in the samples might have been 
different. To overcome this limitation of conventional PCR real-time PCR (rt PCR) was developed 
[96]. 
 
Real-time Polymerase Chain Reaction (rt PCR) 
As previously mentioned the real-time PCR reaction also consist of the three steps (denaturation, 
annealing and elongation). However, this PCR process has several differences comparing with the 
conventional PCR set up.  
Differences between samples, which are only observable in the exponential phase, can be detected 
when using the real-time PCR method. With this method it is possible to quantify the amount of 
DNA or RNA amplified to that point in the amplification reaction [96], [97]. One is capable of 
comparing several samples to a specific time of point and thus compares the amount of DNA or 
RNA in the sample due to measurements during the exponential phase. It is possible due to the use 
of fluorescence processes on sequence-specific probes. A probe is a primer labelled with a so-called 
reporter molecule and a quencher.  Different probes exist. In this project the Dual-labelled 
(formerly known as TaqMan) and PriProET probes are used. 
The fluorescence signal is measured after completion of each PCR cycle and the signal increases 
proportionally due to the accumulation of the PCR product. The initial number of cDNA molecules 
==PAGE:35==
 
35
in the sample defines the threshold cycle (Ct). This means, that the higher the number of cDNA 
molecules present in the sample, the fewer number of PCR cycles are required to detect the 
fluorescence signal. The Ct value is defined as the first detectable fluorescence signal above the 
background signal [97]. Quantification is not affected by limited reaction components in a plateau 
phase unlike the conventional PCR reaction [97].          
It is possible to compare different samples based on the Ct values. This is done by translating the Ct 
values to quantitative results by constructing a standard curve [97] (figure 11). The amount of 
amplified PCR products doubles for each PCR cycle. A difference of one in Ct-values between 
samples means that the sample with the lowest Ct value had double the amount of the target 
sequence compared with the other sample. A difference of two in Ct values means a fourfold 
difference and so on [96].

Dual-labelled probe 
The dual-labelled probe is a hydrolysis probe and is the probe most often used [96], [98]. A 
hydrolysis probe uses the nuclease activity of the Taq polymerase enzyme. The probe hybridizes to 
==PAGE:36==
 
36
the amplicon after the denaturation step and remains hybridized while the polymerase elongates the 
sequence specific primers.  The polymerase reaches the hybridized probe and separates the reporter 
and quencher dyes and the fluorescence signal from the reporter molecular is released [96], [97] 
(figure 12). Both the reporter molecule and quencher molecule are fluorescent molecules [96], [97]. 
The quencher molecule accepts energy from the reporter molecule [96]. As long as the quencher 
molecule is attached to the probe and close to the reporter molecule, no fluorescent signals can be 
detected [97], [98]. The polymerase only cleaves the probe if it is hybridized to its complementary 
strand. Therefore, correct temperature conditions are required to ensure probe binding [97]. The use 
of sequence-specific probes ensures only detection of very specific targets. If no amplicon 
complementary to the probe is present, the probe will not bind and no fluorescence signal can be 
detected [97]. Probes based on hybridization are more specific than those dependent on DNA-
binding dyes [96], [97]. E.g. the SYBR®Green I is a fluorescent dye, which binds to all dsDNA. 
This type of probe is not capable of discriminating between specific and nonspecific PCR products 
[97] unlike the dual-labelled probe.

Primer Probe Energy Transfer probe (PriProET probe) 
This kind of probe is based on energy transfer between two fluorescent molecules. The concept 
behind is based on a donor (the reporter, in this case FAM) and an acceptor (the quencher). When 
the quencher and reporter molecules are near each other the quencher absorbs the energy from the 
==PAGE:37==
 
37
reporter. During the amplification step, the DNA polymerase breaks the reporter away from the 
quencher, thus allowing the energy of the reporter to create a detectable fluorescence signal [96].  
 
Real-time Multiplex Polymerase Chain Reaction  
In the multiplex PCR it is possible to amplify more than one target sequence in one reaction by 
including several pairs of sequence specific primers and probes [96], [97], [99]. Thereby the real-
time PCR as a tool for rapid diagnostic is refined. In this project this particular technique is used to 
subtype the influenza isolates after the presence of the virus has been found with real-time PCR. 
 
The multiplex PCR method has a number of different applications e.g. analysis of deletions and 
short tandem repeats in human DNA, Genetic Modified Organism (GMO) examinations in plants 
and qualitative/semi-qualitative gene expression analysis in viral or bacterial samples [96], [100]. 
 
The multiplex PCR has a few disadvantages. The presence of more than one primer pair in the 
multiplex PCR increases the risk of primer dimer formation. This occurs if the primer-to-template 
ratio is too high. In general, primers must be in a 107 excess to the template to accomplish optimal 
product amplification. Furthermore, the concentration of the primers cannot be higher than 0,5 
µmol since higher concentrations will lead to primer dimer formation. Therefore, the variable 
determinant in the primer-to-template ratio is the amount and complexity of the added template 
[101]. Another shortcoming of the multiplex PCR method is the amplification of a specific target 
sequence over another. This bias can be caused by PCR selection. PCR selection is defined as a 
process, which favours amplification of one target over another. This phenomenon occurs due to 
specific properties of the target and the sequences flanking the target or the target genome in its 
entirety, e.g. interregional differences in the GC content, which can lead to preferential availability 
of targets within the genome, all due to the gene copy number in the genome and the secondary 
structures [99], [101].

Gel electrophoresis of nucleic acids 
In this project the gel electrophoresis technique was used to confirm the presence of influenza HA 
gene and NA gene. However, the field of applications for this method is broad, and the reader is 
encouraged to seek information elsewhere about these multiple purposes. 
  
In nucleic acids, there are several negatively charged phosphate groups attached. These negatively 
charged groups would migrate towards the positive electrode when they are present in an electric 
field. Gel electrophoresis is based on this principle. An electrical field is created when the agarose 
gel is placed into an electrical device. By turning the power on the molecules from the PCR 
reactions will migrate towards the positive electrode. By this migration through the gel it is possible 
to separate different molecules by their size since the gel acts like a sieve and smaller molecules 
will migrate faster towards the positive electrode (figure 13) [102]. Hence, larger molecules will not 
pass through the pores of the sieve due to frictional forces.  
==PAGE:40==
 
40
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 13: A schematic figure of gel electrophoresis. In 0.8% agarose gel from Invitrogen there is 12 wells and a marker is placed 
in both well 1 and 12. The marker serves as a positive control in the way one is able to compare at which bp length the band showing 
is. After running the gel, the gel is exposed to ultraviolet radiation. Bands appearing at 1400 bp and 1800 bp confirm that the NA and 
HA gene is present in the PCR reaction. The water sample from the PCR reaction works as a negative control and no bands should 
appear in the well where this sample is loaded.  
 
One can see the different bands created on the gel because of the ethidium bromide in the gel. 
Ethidium bromide binds to the hydrogen bridges between the double-stranded DNA. When exposed 
to ultraviolet radiation the gel will show the bands. In this project the gel electrophoresis method is 
used to confirm the presence of influenza HA and NA gene and for some isolates the remaining 
genes as well. This is possible since the fragment sizes of these genes are known. The sizes of the 
PCR products from the conventional full-length RT-PCR are approximately 1400 bp for the HA 
gene and 1800 bp for the NA gene [103]. Bands showing up at these base pair lengths correspond to 
the verification of the presence of influenza A HA gene and NA gene. To determine at which base 
pair lengths the bands are showing up, a marker is placed in the first and sometimes the last well on 
the gel.  
 
Several different gels exist. Depending on the size of fragments the percentage of agarose varies. If 
one would like to differentiate between very large molecules one should choose a gel with a 
reduced agarose percentage since the decreased level of agarose helps to separate larger fragments. 
Likewise, if one wishes to differentiate between very small molecules one should choose a gel with 
an increased level of agarose. In this project a 0.8% agarose gel from Invitrogen was used. After the 
gel run one can detect the sizes of the bands by exposing the gel to ultraviolet radiation [104].

Sequencing technology 
The main purpose of sequencing is to determine the precise order of nucleotides within a DNA 
molecule. The field of applications are many: diagnostics, genomics, evolutionary biology and 
molecular biology and due to the emerging of newer, faster and cheaper methods the use of 
sequencing is becoming more and more used. Today many different sequencing methods exist. The 
methods are divided into different categories depending on when they were developed and the 
technology used: First generation sequencing or Sanger sequencing, Second generation sequencing 
or Next Generation Sequencing (NGS) and Third generation sequencing or Next next generation 
sequencing (NNGS). 
Fred Sanger developed the first generation sequencing, also called Sanger sequencing or dideoxy 
sequencing, in the mid-1970s. This discovery was awarded with the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 
1980 [105]. Originally, the method was based on four parallel reactions, each containing the 
deoxynucleotides (dNTPs) (dATP, dCTP, dGTP and dTTP) and a small amount of one of the four 
analogous di-deoxynucleotides (ddNTPs). The ddNTPs are very similar to the dNTPs but differ in 
the way they lack a hydroxyl group at the 3’ carbon position. A hydrogen atom in the di-
deoxynucleotides replaces the hydroxyl group attached to the 3’ carbon in the deoxynucleotides 
(figure 14). Since the ddNTPs do not have a hydroxyl group at the 3’ carbon they act as base-
specific chain terminators. The DNA polymerase I normally ligates the next dNTP on the hydroxyl 
group and since this group is replaced by a hydrogen atom at the 3’ carbon position of the ddNTP, 
the polymerase is not able to elongate the DNA strand thereby the DNA synthesis terminates [104].    
 
Figure 14:!Structure of deoxynucleotide and di-deoxynucleotide. Note the differences at the 3’ carbon position. This difference in 
di-deoxynucleotide causes the elongation of the DNA strand to stop since the DNA polymerase I is no longer able to attach a 
deoxynucleotide. The amount of deoxynucleotides added to the reactions is much greater than the amount of di-deoxynucleotides and 
therefore the lengths of the DNA fragments are random since one cannot predict when the di-deoxynucleotide will attach. (“P” is the 
symbol of a phosphate group).  
==PAGE:42==
 
42
The original Sanger method results in newly synthesized DNA fragments of various lengths. These 
fragments should be labelled radioactively by the oligonucleotide used in the sequence reaction or 
by one of the dNTPs and then the labelled fragments of different length are separated by size on an 
acrylamide gel electrophoresis system. To ensure as many as possible different fragment sizes, 
dNTPs are present in excess compared to ddNTPs and the ddNTPs are incorporated into the 
growing DNA strand randomly. The Sanger method is quite accurate resulting in labelled fragments 
differing in size by one nucleotide and when the fragments are separated by electrophoresis the 
nucleotides in the DNA sequence can be read one by one [104]. 
 
An automated variation of Sanger sequencing was developed in the early 1990s due to a higher 
demand for more accurate and faster results. The automated DNA sequencing machines use a 
fluorescence labelling of the DNA fragments. By using four different fluorescent dyes it is possible 
to differentiate between the four ddNTPs. All four reactions are loaded to the same sample well on 
the gel. During gel electrophoresis the labelled DNA fragments pass by a laser that causes the dyes 
to fluoresce. A monitor will detect the wavelength and record the fluorescent signal when the DNA 
fragment pass by this fixed point in the gel (figure 15). The automated Sanger method allows the 
machine to produce an intensity profile for each of the fluorescent dye [104]. 
 
 
Figure 15: Automated DNA sequencing with fluorescent dyes. When the DNA fragments passes a specific point in the capillary 
tube in the gel a laser will make the four dyes fluoresce at different wavelengths. The detector records the wavelengths and sends 
signals to the capillary machine that reads the base sequence and creates a specific intensity profile for each fluorescent dye and 
stores the information electronically. Figure adapted from [104].  
 
The method used for this project is the automated Sanger method with capillary sequencing (AB1 
3730XL) (personal communication with Oliver at LCG Genomics, 18/10-2016). The method is 
based on the principle that the DNA fragments migrate through a long and thin (0,1 mm in 
diameter) glass capillary tube containing a polyacrylamide gel. Then the capillary machine reads 
==PAGE:43==
 
43
the base sequence as the DNA fragments move through the gel and an output is created (figure 16) 
[104]. From the intensity profiles, also called electropherograms, it is possible to compare the base 
sequence with a known reference sequence. Several databases consist of many uploaded sequences 
one can use as a reference. By aligning the sequences one is able to discover similarities and 
differences between the reference sequence and the sample just sequenced.    
 
Figure 16: Electropherogram output created by a capillary machine. Each of the four fluorescent dyes has its unique wavelength 
at which maximum fluorescent occurs. This wavelength is recorded by the machine, which creates an intensity profile as the one 
shown here. By comparing this output with a reference sequence one is able to detect similarities and differences in the sample just 
sequenced.  
 
Due to practical and financial reasons the technique used for this project is the automated Sanger 
sequence method. This method has dominated the sequence industry for many years, however, 
newer and improved techniques for sequencing have been developed in the past 15 years [106]. As 
mentioned in the beginning of this section there are several categories of high-throughput sequence 
techniques. The second generation of sequencing, also called the Next Generation Sequencing 
(NGS), has the advantage over the Sanger method in the way that they are capable of reading a 
much greater amount of sequences from a single experiment in few hours. Examples of some next 
generation sequencing techniques are the pyrosequencing technique developed by 454 Life 
Sciences, the Solexa technology (uses a reversible chain terminating nucleotides technique) and the 
Supported Oligonucleotide Ligation and Detection system (SOLiD), which is a technique based on 
hybridization-ligation chemistry [107]. Even third generation machines have been developed. These 
techniques are also called Next Next Generation Sequencing (NNGS) and include the Helicos 
Genetic Analysis System, PacBio RS (developed by Pacific Biosciences) and Oxford Nanopore 
Technologies [104]. 
Since neither NGS nor NNGS techniques are used in this project, the reader is encouraged to find 
details about these methods elsewhere.

Hemagglutinin Activity (HA) Test 
The HA surface protein binds to sialic acid receptors on the surface of the host’s cell. HA is also 
able to bind to erythrocytes causing a lattice formation known as hemagglutination. Taking 
advantage of this property, a hemagglutination assay can be used to determine levels of influenza in 
samples. 
Erythrocytes not bound by HA (either because of inadequate levels of virus or virus lacking HA 
activity), will sink to the bottom of the assay-well and form a small plug [108]. 
The HA assay forms the basis for the more complex HI test, used to determine levels of antibodies 
against influenza in a serum sample. 
 
Hemagglutinin Inhibitions (HI) Test  
The test is used for determination of hemagglutination inhibiting antibodies against influenza A in 
animal sera. The antibodies will prevent the HA from attaching to the erythrocytes and thereby 
preventing the formation of the lattice, making the erythrocytes fall to the bottom of the well. The 
highest dilution of serum preventing the hemagglutination is called the HI titer. Should the serum 
contain no antibodies towards the HA, there will be seen hemagglutination in all wells – except the 
serum control well. 
The titer is determined by adding a set amount of virus and erythrocytes to each well in the assay, 
and then do a two-fold dilution of the serum samples along each row [108]. 
 
Serum Neutralization Test (SNT) 
The SNT assay is a test used to detect and quantify viral specific antibodies in the sera obtained 
from vaccinated or unvaccinated animals.  
In the SNT test, MDCK cells are grown in a 96-well plate. To the wells different serum samples 
from vaccinated or unvaccinated animals area added. If there are antibodies against the specific 
influenza subtype present, the virus is unable to infect the MDCK cells. The SNT test is a sensitive 
way of testing viral antibody activity and thereby vaccine efficiency against specific subtypes.

Background 
Since 2011 a passive surveillance programme has been up and running in Denmark. The 
surveillance is based on samples submitted by a veterinarian for diagnostic purposes from pigs 
suffering from acute respiratory diseases [109]. As a consequence of the surveillance programme it 
is possible to know some of the subtypes circulating in Danish swine (figure 17). The subtypes 
detected in the programme are of course based on the submissions from herds with problems with 
respiratory diseases. Thus, the subtypes shown in figure 17 do not necessarily represent all the 
subtypes circulating.  
 
In 2014 the first isolate with a new influenza subtype was identified. This new reassortant consisted 
of a HA gene that resembled the H3 gene found in the seasonal influenza subtype H3N2 in humans 
during the season 2004/2005 and a NA gene with closest resemblance to the N2 gene found in 
swine. The result of full genome characterization revealed that the new subtype had internal genes 
originating from the pandemic H1N1 from 2009 (H1N1pdm09). Thus, this new subtype represented 
a new triple-reassortant. A retrospective study of previously submitted samples revealed, that the 
first H3hu05 gene was found in a sample submitted in 2013. However, this isolate could not be 
cultured in cells and the NA sequencing was unsuccessful, which is why it was not discovered at 
first. The two samples were found to come from neighbouring herds with different owners and no

apparent contact. The H3hu05N2sw subtype had been isolated several times after 2014 from 
different herds at geographically different locations; this means that the subtype has become 
established in the Danish pig production [11], [109]. 
 
The possible zoonotic potential of H3hu05N2sw 
The human components of the subtype, the H3 gene combined with the internal pandemic genes, is 
the reason why the subtype is thought to have zoonotic potential [109]. The internal pandemic 
genes originate from the H1N1 pandemic influenza introduced in humans in 2009. Both the H3 
gene originating from seasonal influenza from 2005 and the internal genes from the 2009 pandemic 
have the ability to infect humans since they already have infected humans. Pigs can act as a mixing 
vessel and can be infected by influenza viruses from e.g. humans. The risk of the infected pigs to 
infect the personnel around is a possibility. The personnel can then carry influenza viruses outside 
the herds and may expose other people to it. It is assumed that people born after 2005 have not been 
exposed to the H3hu05 gene and therefore represents a naïve population. This naïve population may 
be at risk of being exposed to this new subtype and may be of risk of getting ill.       
 
Hypotheses 
Based on the information about the original samples two hypotheses were made. 
 
To investigate if the new subtype has a preference towards human cells or porcine cells the 
following hypothesis was made:  
 
Hypothesis 1: Subtype H3hu05N2sw is able to colonize human cell cultures better than porcine 
cell cultures. 
 
Since the currently used vaccines in Denmark against swine influenza contains a H3 gene 
originating from swine, the following hypothesis was made:  
 
Hypothesis 2: The conventional vaccines used in Denmark against influenza in swine do not 
induce immunity towards the H3hu05N2sw subtype.

Laboratory tests 
PCR, sequencing and phylogenetic analysis 
Nasal swabs collected from herds were initially tested with matrix rtRT-PCR to detect whether the 
sample was positive for influenza virus or not. Samples with Ct values ≤ 30 were chosen to 
participate in the project. The samples were then tested with multiplex RT-PCR and for cultivation 
in MDCK cells. After cultivation in MDCK cells the isolates were again tested with matrix rtRT-
PCR to detect whether it was possible to grow and isolate virus from the cells. Negative isolates 
were cultivated again (2nd passage). If the 2nd passage was negative the original sample was selected 
for cultivation in 8-10 day old embryonated chicken eggs (ECE).  
Isolates with the H3N2 subtype according to the multiplex PCR results were selected for full-length 
HA and NA RT-PCR and sent to LCG Genomics for Sanger Sequencing. The data received from 
the sequencing company will afterwards undergo a BLAST search for identifying the specific HA 
gene and NA gene. This will be done by comparing the sequence to reference sequences.  
A phylogenetic analysis of the HA gene and NA gene were wished to be performed and compared 
to other strains.   
 
Culturing in A549 cell line and New born Piglet Kidney (NPK) cell line 
Isolates identified with the specific H3hu05N2sw subtype were to be cultivated in a human cell line 
(A549) and a porcine cell line (NPK). These two cell lines represent a human cell line and porcine 
cell line, respectively.

Materials and Methods 
Nasal swabs performed by the herd-veterinarian 
15 ml centrifuge tubes containing 2,5 ml phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) and metal nasal swabs 
were sent to the herd-veterinarians by mail. The centrifuge tubes were kept in a refrigerator at a 
temperature around 4-5°C until packing the kits to the individual veterinarian. The veterinarians 
were asked to put 10 metal nasal swabs in each tube and make sure that the PBS medium covers all 
the swabs and to mark each tube with CHR-number and sampling date. Furthermore, the 
veterinarian was asked to fill in a submission note with information about the age group from which 
the samples were taken and whether the swine had any influenza-like symptoms (See section 
“Pathogenesis and clinical signs”). If no animals were presented with influenza-like symptoms, 
samples from piglets were accepted since this age group is presumed to be naïve, as they no longer 
have maternal antibodies in their system. 
 
Viral cultivation in MDCK 
The cultivation of influenza virus is a lengthy process. The cells used in this experiment are 
epithelial kidney cells originating from canines – in this case from a female adult cocker spaniel 
harvested in 1958 by SH Madin and NB Darby, thereby giving these cell lines their name Madin-
Darby Canine Kidney cells (MDCK) [110]. 
The protocol used in this experiment is produced by the National Veterinary Institute, Section for 
Virology. 
The MDCK cell lines were passaged twice weekly, when reaching 60-70% confluence (figure 18), 
where the media is renewed to ensure proper cell access to nutrients. 
MDCK cells were primarily grown in a medium growth bottle (75cm2 craned neck, vented cap).  
During cell passaging, the cells were loosened from the bottom of the growth-bottle by 
trypsinization, a process where trypsin 0,05% was added to the cultivation flask three times to 
loosen the cells from the bottom of the flask. Each time, 1600 µL of trypsin was added. In the first 
round of trypsin, the bottle was swirled carefully and the trypsin was poured out, into a waste 
container. On the second round, a 1000 µL pipette was used to flush around the sides of the bottle, 
with the 1600 µL of trypsin at a total of 15-20 times, before the trypsin was removed by pipette. On 
the third round after adding the trypsin, the bottle was bashed firmly against the palm of a hand 
before being incubated at 37°C for 5 minutes. After this amount of time it was examined whether 
any cells had loosened from the bottom, and the bottle was again knocked quite firmly against the 
palm of a hand, and thereafter incubated at 37°C for 10-20 minutes. 
==PAGE:51==
 
51
After the incubation, the cells were suspended in 5 ml of MEM (Minimum Essential Medium). It 
was made sure that the cells were equally distributed in the MEM by flushing the bottom of the 
flask with a pipette. The cell suspension was transferred to a 15 ml tube and 200 µL of the 
suspension was transferred to a new cultivation bottle, and another 200 µL is transferred to the 
Burker-Turk counting chamber. 
The Burker-Turk counting chamber was used to find the correct solution of cells to transfer to the 
smaller cultivation flask used in infecting the cells with influenza.  
 
The new medium for maintaining the cells was mixed during the incubation period. For a medium 
sized flask, 40 ml of cultivation medium was mixed.  
For the cultivation medium a mixture of the following was used: 
! 
38 ml of 1x MEM 
! 
2 ml of FBS (foetal bovine serum) 
! 
400 µL of L-Glutamine (essential amino acid) 
! 
400 µL of NEAA (non-essential amino acids) 
! 
400 µL of Pen-Strep (antimicrobial agent) 
The components were mixed in a glass vial and poured into the new cultivation flask.  
For the smaller cultivation flasks, 5 ml of a solution of growth medium and cells (the cells counted 
in the Burker-Turk chamber) was added to each flask and incubated at 37°C for 24 hours. 
 
 
Figure 18: MDCK cells in ~60% confluence. Taken before infection with influenza virus. 
 
 
==PAGE:52==

Before adding the virus to the incubated cells, the sample materials needed to be prepared. 
Nasal swabs and saliva samples were diluted with a special cultivation medium so that the total 
amount of sample was 500 µL. The samples were filtered with a 0,45 µm sterile filter.  
If the sample was lung tissue, a special medium for extraction was used. This extraction medium 
was a mixture of MEM and antibiotic. In an Eppendorf tube about 150 mg of lung tissue was added, 
along with a small steel ball and 1,5 ml of the extraction medium. The sample was homogenized by 
using a Tissuelyzer for 6 minutes at 30.000 Hz. Thereafter, the tissue was centrifuged at 12.000 G 
for 3 minutes. After these steps, the supernatant of the sample was filtered with a 0,45 µm sterile 
filter. 
In a 1,5ml Eppendorf tube 800 µL of cultivation medium was added, along with 200 µL of sterile 
filtered saliva or nasal swab sample. If the sample was homogenized lung tissue, 900 µL of 
cultivation medium was used with 100 µL lung sample. 
The small cultivation flasks were removed from the incubator, and the medium was carefully 
removed from the flask.  
The diluted samples were added to the now empty cultivation flasks and were incubated for 30-40 
minutes at 37°C. For the negative control, 1 ml of sterile cultivation medium was added to the flask 
and incubated along with the rest of the flasks. 
After the incubation period the flasks were emptied of the viral samples and the cells were flushed 
carefully with 1 ml of MEM. To each of the cell cultivation flasks, 3 ml of cultivation medium was 
added and the cells were incubated for three days at 37°C. 
After incubating the infected cells for three days, the cells were harvested and transferred to nunc 
cryo tubes. The tubes were marked with date of harvest, sample number and flask number. 
Moreover, 200 µL of the sample was transferred to a Sarsted tube, with 400 µL of β-
Mercaptoethanol. The nunc tubes were stored at -80°C while the Sarsted tube was stored at -20°C 
and would be used for purification of the samples the next day. 
 
Cell cultivation in human lung cell line A549 
The human cell lines used for this experiment are based on the A549 cell lines. Vials of the 
prepared cells are kept frozen in liquid nitrogen at about -195°C.  
When thawing the cells for experimental use, it must be done quickly to avoid loss of viability in 
the cells. To ensure this, vials are transferred from liquid nitrogen into ice for transport, and then to 
37°C water for 1 minute.  
==PAGE:53==
 
53
After thawing, the vials must be sterilized with 70% ethanol before entry into the workbench. 
The cell suspension is transferred to a centrifuge tube along with 10ml of washing medium. The 
tubes with the cells lines are then centrifuged for 15min at 2500rpm. 
After centrifugation, the clarity of the supernatant and the visibility of the pellet should be assessed. 
If these parameters are acceptable, the supernatant is removed without disturbing the pellet.  
The supernatant contains DMSO, which is known to facilitate the transport of other chemicals 
across the human skin barrier [111]. Therefore, the supernatant should be removed with care, and 
disposed of according to regulations. 
The remaining pellet should be intact, and using about 5 ml of the initial growth medium should 
slowly be re-suspended, and transferred to a small growth flask. 
The initial growth medium contains double the amount of FBS as the maintenance growth medium,  
In order to follow the development of the cells closely, the growth flask should be examined every 
other day using a good quality microscope.  
The cells cannot have their medium changed until they have adhered to the bottom of the growth 
flask. 
A549 cells needs a growth medium containing DMEM (Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium) 
which is richer in vitamins, amino acids and glucose, resulting in a more stable medium than MEM 
[112].  
The procedure for the rest of the medium is similar to the medium used in MDCK cell growth – 
containing NEAA, L-glutamine, Pen/Strep and FBS. 
  
Cell cultivation in new born piglet kidney cell line NPK 
The thawing of the NPK cell lines follows the same procedure as for the A549 above. 
The NPK cell line grows in the same type of medium as the MDCK cell lines, containing MEM, L-
glutamine, FBS, Pen/Strep and NEAA.  
 
Culturing in embryonated specific pathogen free chicken eggs 
8-10 day old embryonated chicken eggs (ECE) were used for inoculation (figure 19). Firstly, the 
assurance of a living embryo was tested by candling using a lamp. The position of the air sac was 
detected by candling and marked with an X. The X marked the inoculation site and a hole was made 
with an egg drill in the eggshell at the marked position. Each egg was marked with sample name or 
as negative control. The eggs were inoculated through the hole using a 1 ml syringe with a thin 
needle. PBS (pH 7,6) was inoculated in negative control eggs. A volume of 0,2 ml sample or PBS

was sealed using a drop of glue. The eggs were placed in a 37°C incubator with a humidity of 
approximately 50-60%. The eggs were investigated for living embryos daily. If the embryos were 
alive six days after inoculation they were placed in a refrigerator at 4°C for a least two hours to kill 
the embryo and to reduce risk of contamination of the allantois fluid with blood during harvesting. 
Thereafter the allantois fluid could be harvested. Harvest of the allantois fluid was done by 
removing the shell from the air sac area using forceps. The membranes and embryo were moved to 
one side and the allantois fluid was extracted using a 5 ml syringe a 23 G needle.

RNA extraction of the cultivated virus in MDCK cells 
The protocol used for this procedure is developed by The National Veterinary Institute, 
Frederiksberg. 
==PAGE:55==
 
55
The following explains the procedure for manual extraction and purification of RNA from cultured 
material or original material; we used an automatic device called QIACube for this, but the 
principle is the same. 
 
For the following, it is crucial that everything happens quite quickly and at a lower room 
temperature due to the unstable nature of RNA. 
Before starting the extraction process, it is important to prepare the β-mercaptoethanol (β-ME) by 
adding RLT buffer (lysis buffer for lysing of cells and tissues prior to RNA isolation [114]) in the 
ratio 10µl β-ME to 1ml RLT buffer. 
 
The material used (homogenized tissue sample, nasal swab, or tissue culture isolate) is added β-ME.  
Our isolates from cell cultures where our primary source of RNA, and here we used the ratio of 
200µl of isolate to 400µl of β-ME. 
Positive and negative extraction and purification controls are likewise added 400µl of β-ME. 
The samples now containing β-ME are then mixed by pulse-vortexing for about 15 seconds. The 
samples are then spun quickly to ensure that the mixture is not situated in the lid. 
 
To each sample, a volume corresponding to sample volume 70% ethanol is added, and mixed with 
the pipette.  
Into an Eppendorf tube, a RNeasy mini column is placed, and 700µl of sample is added. The tube 
with column is then centrifuged at 8000rpm for 15 seconds.  
The column is added to a new Eppendorf tube – the used Eppendorf with the flow-through is 
discarded. The new tube with the column is centrifuged at 8000rpm for 15 seconds. Like before, the 
column is transferred to a new Eppendorf tube, and the used tube with flow-through is discarded. 
 
Into the column, 700µl of RW1 buffer (used for washing membrane bound RNA [115]) is added, 
and the lid is closed carefully. The tube and column is centrifuged at 8000rpm for 15 seconds. The 
column is again transferred to a new tube, and flow-through discarded. 
 
After the column is transferred, 500µl RPE buffer (primary function is to wash away salts left by 
earlier used buffers [116]) is added. The tube is centrifuged at 8000rpm for 15 seconds. Column is 
transferred to a new Eppendorf tube, and flow-through is discarded. Another 500µl of RPE buffer is 
==PAGE:56==
 
56
added, and the column and tube are then centrifuged for 2 minutes at 8000rpm in order to dry out 
the RNeasy silica-gel membrane in the column, now containing the RNA.  
The column is then transferred to another tube, and centrifuged for 1 minute at 12.000 rpm. This 
tube is then discarded, and the column is transferred to a marked tube, where the RNA is eluted 
from the column by adding 60µl of RNase-free-water directly on the RNeasy silica-gel membrane, 
without the pipette-tip touching the membrane. The tube with column is then centrifuged at 
8000rpm for 1 minute. 
 
Directly transfer the Eppendorf tube containing the extracted RNA to a freezer at -80°C. 
 
HA and HI test 
The protocol used for this assay belongs to IDT Biologika GmBH, Dessau-Roßlau, Germany 
(Appendix 3). 
In the protocol chicken erythrocytes were used. They were kept in Alserver’s solution after 
sampling. Before use, the erythrocytes were washed with PBS-V (pH 7.2) three times to eliminate 
Alserver’s solution. 
After PBS wash, the haematocrit (ht) value was determined and a 0,5 % solution of erythrocytes 
was prepared using PBS-V. Before starting the procedure, the serum samples for the HI test were 
treated with neuraminidase medium (neuraminidase in ZZM-H medium in dilution 1:40, pH 7.2) to 
avoid any non-specific agglutination inhibitors or non-specific agglutinins. 
 
Pre-treatment of the serum samples: 25 µL of serum sample was added to 100 µL of neuraminidase 
dilution. The sample was incubated at 37°C for 14-18 hours. After incubation 125 µL of sodium 
citrate solution was added to the sample and left at 56°C for 30 minutes to inactivate the 
neuraminidase.  
After incubation, the samples were added 10 µL of concentrated chicken erythrocytes and incubated 
for 1 hour at 4-8 °C and thereafter spun down in a centrifuge at 4000 rpm for 3 minutes. After these 
preparatory steps, it was possible to perform the HA test and find the HA titer. The viral suspension 
should be adjusted to 8 hemagglutinin units (HAU). This was achieved by diluting the viral sample 
with PBS-V.

Gel electrophoresis 
The gel electrophoresis was performed on the products from the full-length RT-PCR reactions. Gel 
electrophoresis was performed on a 0,8% Agarose gel from Invitrogen. 10 µL marker (1 kb Plus 
DNA ladder from Invitrogen) was added to lane 1 and 12 on each gel. In lane 2 to 11 5µL PCR-
product and 7 µL loading-mix were added. The loading-mix consisted of 5 µL H20 and 2 µL 
loading buffer (Trackit Buffer, catalogue nr. 10482028). Photographs were taken by UV trans 
illumination of the gel.  
 
Purification of full-length RT-PCR products 
PCR products with correct size of bands (verified by gel electrophoresis) were purified using a High 
Pure PCR Product Purification Kit applied directly on the PCR products. 35 µL PCR product plus 
175 µL Binding Buffer was mixed thoroughly on the vortex machine. A High Pure Filter tube was 
inserted into one collection tube and by using a pipette the PCR product and Binding Buffer was 
added to the High Pure Filter tube. The High Pure Filter tube with the sample was centrifuged in a 
standard table top centrifuge at 15-25°C. The filter tube was disconnected and the flowthrough 
solution discarded. The filter tube was then re-connected to the same collection tube. 500µL Wash 
Buffer (10 ml Wash Buffer + 40 ml ethanol) was added to the filter tube. The mix was centrifuged 
for 1 min with the standard table top centrifuge. The flowthrough solution was discarded and the 
==PAGE:65==

filter tube placed back in the collection tube. Another 200 µL Wash Buffer was added and the 
sample mix centrifuged for 1 min with the standard table top centrifuge. The flowthrough solution 
was discarded after ended centrifuge. The filter tube was placed into a clean 1,5 ml micro-
centrifuge tube. For samples with a normal bright band on the gel electrophoresis, 40 µL of Elution 
Buffer was added. For very strong bands 45-50µL was added. The micro-centrifuge tube with the 
sample was centrifuged in 1 min. 2-3 µL of the sample was collected to perform 
nanospectrophotometry for detection of the concentration of DNA in the purified PCR product.

Amino acid analysis and phylogenetic analysis 
All sequences for the amino acid analysis were analysed using CLC Main Workbench (7.0 and 9.5 
editions). HA sequences obtained from LCG Genomics, Germany were assembled and proofread 
manually with a H3hu05 reference (A/Malaysia/29930/2004/H3N2, accession number CY11818). 
One sequence showed greater similarity to a more recent seasonal human H3N2, more specifically a 
2015 variant A/Pennsylvania/50/2015(H3N2) (accession number KY044510.1), and this variant 
was then used as reference for the one sequence called “11-ACH3N2p16”.  
The consensus from these assemblies were then aligned along with sequences from human 
influenza vaccines from 2005-2017 (obtained from WHO), a H3N2 reference sequence from Aichi 
1968 (referred to as H3hu1968) in order to asses genetic drift and the sequence from the IDT 
vaccine H3N2 strain (referred to as H3sw IDT vaccine) and four sequences representing the 
endemic H3N2 influenza strains circulating in Europe and Denmark:  
A_Siena_3_95_H3N2_HA 
A_sw_Denmark_13991_03_H3N2_HA 
A_sw_Denmark_14348ns9_03_H3N2_HA 
A_sw_Spain_54008_04_H3N2_HA

The alignment was then used to create a phylogenetic tree to analyse the drift of the sequences and

Our phylogenetic tree was generated in MEGA7. The analysis involved 33 nucleotide sequences

positions containing gaps and missing data. In order for us to generate the best phylogenetic tree 
possible, we used JModelTest v 2.1.7 to select the best-fit model. The recommended model was 
GTR +G +I, meaning using the general time reversible model with a gamma distribution (to model 
evolutionary rate differences between sites). The I in G+I means that a rate variation model is used, 
and that it allows for some sites to be evolutionary invariable. This setting is often used when 
modelling influenza sequences. A 1000 bootstrap replicates and percentages are shown next to their

Biological characterization  
In order to make a proper phenotypical characterization of new influenza subtypes it is important to 
perform cultivation to create an isolate from every sample. Usually cultivation of influenza viruses 
are performed in MDCK cells.  
To characterize the receptor affinity of the influenza subtypes an HA activity test is performed.   
 
Isolation of virus 
Cultivation of virus in MDCK cell line  
Cultivation of the isolates sampled this year (2016) in MDCK cells were not possible even though 
the protocol for the MDCK cells was followed thoroughly. Only samples from 2014 and 2015 from 
herds 2-ORGH3N2-14 (named 2-ORGH3N2-14pas) and “5-ACH3N2p16” were capable of being 
cultured. The samples 2-ORGH3N2-14pas and “5-ACH3N2p16” originate from the same owner 
but from two different herds.  
 
Cultivation of virus in A549 and NPK cell lines  
Due to unknown reasons it was not possible to grow neither the A549 nor the NPK cell line and 
therefore it was not possible to cultivate the virus in these cell lines.  
 
Since it was not possible to obtain isolates we decided to continue the biological characterization 
using the original isolate from 2014 (1-ORGH3N2-14).

concentration of 0,5% chicken erythrocytes was used. The test was performed by mixing 200 µL of

activity. The tile-test was performed on all the positive isolates for the H3N2 subtype from table 14 
and the original samples (1-ORGH3N2-14, 2-ORGH3N2-14 and 2-ORGH3N2-14pas). After 2 
minutes, no HA activity was observed for any of the isolates.

Hypotheses 
Hypothesis 1 
“Subtype H3hu05N2sw is able to colonize human cell cultures better than porcine cell 
cultures.” It was not possible to confirm or reject hypothesis 1 since the cell lines were not grown 
in the laboratory.  
 
Hypothesis 2 
“The conventional vaccine (Respiporc) used in Denmark against influenza in swine do not 
induce immunity towards the H3hu05N2sw subtype.” Since the performance of the SNT test 
was not done, it is not possible to confirm or reject hypothesis 2. This is discussed in more details 
under the section “Discussion”.        
 
Since we had some difficulties cultivating and agglutinating our samples, we considered whether 
there might be a possible explanation for this. Based on literature, we found that other researchers 
had had the same problems with culturing and agglutinating the newer H3 subtypes. Due to this a 
new hypothesis was devised: 
 
Hypothesis 3 
“Mutations found in the HA gene lead to reduced ability to be cultured in MDCK cells and 
reduced HA activity in erythrocytes from several species.” Substitutions found by others to 
influence on the receptor affinity were also found in this study. Mutations described in the literature 
to have some impact on the reduced HA activity observed in recent influenza isolates containing the 
H3 gene, were also found in this study.

Discussion 
Cultivation in MDCK, A549 and NPK cells  
None of the isolates sampled during this study were found to grow in the MDCK cells. This even 
after several attempts to make them grow. The reason for this was not because of our lack of 
experience in the laboratory, since a well-experienced laboratory technician was not able to make 
the isolates grow in MDCK cells. The reason behind this inability to be cultured in MDCK cells 
could be found in the sequence of the HA gene (discussed later in this section).  
 
Unfortunately, it was not possible to grow neither the A549 nor the NPK cells in the laboratory due 
to inexplicable reasons. Again, not only did we try to up-grow the cells, but an experienced 
laboratory technician tried as well. The most reasonable explanation of this lack of cells is that the 
tubes, in which the cell line should be in, did not contain any cells.  
 
HA activity tests performed on erythrocytes from different species 
None of the isolates tested showed observable HA activity two minutes of rotation on a porcelain 
tile. The HA activity was tested against erythrocytes from several species – chicken and turkeys. 
The choice of specimens used in the HA tile-tests was based on previously experience and on 
information provided by the literature [124]. Up until 2001 HA activity was seen in human H3N2 
isolates when tested with chicken erythrocytes and up until 2004 it was possible to detect HA 
activity when tested against erythrocytes from turkeys. The reduced HA activity observed in all the 
samples investigated in this study, could be explained by some of the mutations found in the HA 
gene sequences. Some mutations and their positions in the sequence have already been described in 
the literature and scientists believe that they may have an impact on the reduced HA activity 
observed in samples isolated after 2001-2005 [124]. An extension of this study could be to 
investigate the HA activity in erythrocytes from humans and guinea pigs. Some studies have found 
HA activity in recent isolates of H3N2 subtypes when using erythrocytes from humans [61], [127].  
 
Another reason for the reduced HA activity observed in recent H3 influenza isolates might be 
changes in the NA gene [126]. Some authors believe that agglutination can occur through a NA-
dependent mechanism [126]. Since it was not possible to sequence the NA genes in this study this 
subject will not be discussed further and the reader is encouraged to find information about the 
possibility of a NA-dependent viral attachment and mutations in the NA gene elsewhere.  
 
==PAGE:85==
 
 
85
Other methods of detecting influenza viruses in isolates should be considered in the future due to 
the risk of false negative results when testing with HA and HI assays, which are the assays 
commonly used [71]. A more sensitive assay is the PCR reaction, which can detect influenza 
viruses independently of the ability of the subtype to be cultivated or show HA activity.  
 
Amino acid sequence analysis  
The positions of the amino acid substitutions (table 17) were chosen based on the information 
provided by other scientists [61], [74], [75], [124]–[128]. In this study it was possible to identify 
mutations already described in the literature at some positions (positions 193, 222, 225 and 226). 
These mutations are thought to have influence on the reduced HA activity and the reduced ability to 
be cultured in MDCK cells observed in specimens isolated after 2001-2005 [124]. Several scientists 
believe that position 226 is particularly important regarding the reduced HA activity and reduced 
receptor binding affinity [61], [124], [125]. Mutation at this position was found in all the sequences 
investigated in this study. The mutation at position 226 was not the only mutation found which may 
have an impact on the reduced receptor affinity. At position 190, all the sequences had aspartic acid 
(except the H3hu1968 reference) (table 17). Position 190 is believed to be responsible for the loss 
of ability to bind to chicken erythrocytes [127]. The Glu ! Asp mutation at position 190 may 
explain why all the isolates were unable to agglutinate when tested with chicken erythrocytes.  
 
At position 237, the literature had described a Pro ! Leu mutation to have influence on the reduced 
receptor specificity [126]. None of the sequences in table 15 had this mutation. In fact, not even the 
H3hu1968 reference had proline at this position. This is the only position where the found amino 
acid in the H3hu1968 reference sequence does not match the ones described in the literature. All the 
amino acid positions were found in the HA sequence by adding 16 to the number of interest. The 
number 16 results from the length of the signal peptide (Appendix 14). The amino acid positions for 
all the other positions agree with the ones found in the literature after the addition of 16. After 
recounting numerous times it was found, that for the sequences compared in table 15, the correct 
amino acid was valine. In the literature the Pro ! Leu mutation were found at position 237 when 
compared to the 2012/2013 human vaccine [126]. Analysis of the sequence for the 2012/2013 
vaccine around the area of position 237 revealed that proline was found at positions 215, 221, 227, 
239 and 254 but not at position 237. There is no way of telling how the position 237 is decided in 
the literature, however, the most likely positions may be position 227 or 239, since these positions 
==PAGE:86==
 
 
86
are the ones structural closest to the 237 position. Some structural differences are found between 
proline and valine. Proline contains a heterocyclic side chain, which may have some features 
regarding the HA receptor binding [126], while valine has a aliphatic side chain [129]. Proline is 
thought to stabilize the HA when binding to sialic acid receptors [126]. Due to the structural 
differences between valine and proline this might have influence on the receptor binding affinity. 
However, it is doubtful that the valine at position 237 identified in the isolates in this study is the 
same position “237” as the one described in the literature [126]. Therefore, it makes little sense to 
compare these two amino acids.      
 
Not all the positions of possible substitutions, which may have an influence, are well described in 
the literature. Positions 133, 156, 173, 186 and 248 were only mentioned as “important positions or 
critical positions” and not all the researches mentioned which substitutions to look for at these 
positions [74], [128]. However, it was still possible to define an area of interest in which we were 
able to look after new substitutions. The region of interest (positions 165 to 250) was defined based 
on the positions already described in the literature [61], [74], [75], [124]–[128]. This specific area in 
the HA gene had been described as an important site regarding the receptor binding specificity and 
to have influence on the HA activity of the isolates. We were able to identify three substitutions, not 
previously described, in the region. The three mutations at positions 171, 189 and 244 are located 
around positions found by others to have influence on the growth ability in MDCK cells and HA 
activity, it is possible, that these mutations as well might have an influence.  
 
The Asn ! Lys mutation on position 171 might be important since the properties of the amino 
acids are not the same. Lysine is characterized as being a basic amino acid and has a side chain with 
a high pKa value, while aspargine is characterized as an amide amino acid with a side chain without 
any pKa value [129]. The properties of the side chain in lysine may influence in how the HA binds 
to the receptors.  
 
The Gln ! Asn mutation found in all sequences (except the H3hu15 reference, “11-ACH3N2p16” 
(note the citation marks) and the vaccines after 2009/2010) at position 189 is not thought to have 
huge influence on the receptor binding or the reduced HA activity since the properties of these two 
amino acids are quite similar. The only difference between these two amino acids is the addition of 
a carbon in the side chain of glutamine [129]. The Asn ! Lys mutation on the same position found 
==PAGE:87==
 
 
87
in the H3hu15 reference and “11-ACH3N2p16” (note the citation marks) (and vaccines produced 
after 2009/2010) might have some influence on the reduced ability to bind to receptors and to be 
cultured in MDCK cells due to the same features as mentioned for the Asn ! Lys mutation found 
at position 171. The Asn ! Lys mutation located at position 189 is thought to have occurred before 
2015, since the lysine substitution already had been used in the human vaccines since the season 
2010/2011. To investigate more exactly when the mutation has arisen, one could include H3hu 
references from the years between 2005 and 2015.  
 
The Val ! Leu mutation found at position 244 in all the sequences except the ones from 5-
ACH3N2p16 and 6-ACH3N2p16 is not thought to have great influence since the properties of these 
two amino acids are not that different. The only difference between the two amino acids is the 
addition of a carbon in the side chain of leucine [129]. At position 244, serine was found in the 
sequences from 5-ACH3N2p16 and 6-ACH3N2p16. The features of the two amino acids, valine 
and serine, are basically the same [129]. However, these herds are owned by the same owner and 
transport of animals occurs between the herds (figure 28 under the section “Results”). This might 
be the reason why this mutation is only found in these samples. Since influenza viruses are RNA 
viruses the mutation rate is higher compared to DNA viruses. It is estimated, that the mutation rate 
for RNA viruses is 10-3 to 10-5 and for DNA viruses 10-7 to 10-11 [27]. The lower mutation rate in 
DNA viruses is because of the DNA proofreading mechanism, which corrects “mistakes” occurred 
during DNA replication [27], [130]. It is an interesting finding, that this particularly serine 
substitution only were found in herds with a direct connection. Even though RNA viruses have a 
high mutation rate it is still unlikely that this mutation should have occurred spontaneously in the 
two herds.   
 
Only three isolates were able to be cultured in MDCK cells (2-ORGH3N2-14, 2-ORGH3N2-14pas 
and “5ACH3N2p16” (note the citation marks)). All herds are owned by the same owner and 
transport of animals have occurred from herd 2-ORGH3N2-14 to herd “5-ACH3N2p16” (note the 
citation marks) during the years 2004 to 2016. Since only samples from these two herds could be 
cultured in MDCK cells, one could wonder if there are one or more mutations presents that favour 
the ability to be cultured. The sequences from 2-ORGH3N2-14, 2-ORGH3N2-14pas and “5-
ACH3N2p16” (note the citation marks) were compared to the rest of the sequences of samples 
listed in table 15. No differences were found within positions 165 to 250 when compared to the 
==PAGE:88==
 
 
88
other sequences. However, at position 158 the same substitution was found for the three sequences 
and was not found in the rest of the sequences. At position 158 the 2-ORGH3N2-14, 2-ORGH3N2-
14pas and “5-ACH3N2p16” (note the citation marks) all had arginine, while the rest of the 
sequences had glycine or asparagine or lysine (see Appendix 15 for further details). While position 
158 is not in the investigated area, the position is indeed in the region of positions described by 
others in the literature (positions 133 to 248) [61], [74], [124]–[128]. Therefore the location of 
arginine on position 158 in the HA sequence may explain why isolates with this substitution still 
can be cultivated in MDCK cells.      
 
Further investigation targeting these specific amino acid positions (158, 171, 189 and 244) should 
be performed to find out more about the potential influence these mutations might have. Several 
techniques can be used to find out the role of specific amino acids. E.g. virus reverse genetics 
(generating recombinant viruses to investigate only one substitution at a time by introducing the 
mutations independently into a reference known not to have other mutations), x-ray crystallography 
(to illustrate the overall structure and look for differences) and HA tests against erythrocytes from 
different species to see if the HA activity is re-introduced (after virus reverse genetics).  
 
Phylogenetic analysis 
As expected a lot of genetic drift has occurred when comparing the H3hu1968 sequence to the other 
sequences. This is no surprise since the H3hu1968 reference originates from many years ago and 
genetic drift is known to occur over time [126], [131]. As mentioned previously, RNA viruses have 
a high mutations rate [27], [131], which allows mutations to be in cooperated in the subtypes as 
long as it is not a mutation that influence on the virus’ ability to infect and replicate.   
 
There has not been much genetic drift between the sequences from H3hu15 and “11-ACH3N2p16” 
(note the citation marks). This makes sense, since only one difference was found between these two 
sequences (table 15). It is possible that the human vaccine from 2016/2017 will protect against the 
influenza subtype H3hu15N2sw since only very little genetic drift (seven nucleotide differences, 
98.8% similarity) were found between this human vaccine and the sequence from isolate “11-
ACH3N2p16” (note the citation marks) (figure 29 and table 15). Further studies investigating the 
specific nucleotide differences and possible consequences of these should be performed before any 
definitive conclusions are made.

Almost none drift was observed between herds owned by the same owner (e.g. herd 5-ACH3N2p16 
and 6-ACH3N2p16). This comes to no surprise since movements of animals between the herds had 
occurred in the years 2004 to 2016 (figure 28) and genetic drift is known to be a process that can 
take time, especially in swine due to their short average lifespan and the continuously flow of a 
susceptible population of animals [131], [132]. Some drift, however, is observed between the herds

multiplex PCR indicated that the subtype H3hu05N2sw was found again during this study (table 
14). The original sample from 2014 (2-ORGH3N2-14) was used in the phylogenetic analysis. This 
made it possible to compare the sequence from 2014 to the sequences sampled this year from the 
other herds owned by the same owner. As mentioned before, some drift has occurred between the 
sequences from 2014 and the ones from 2016 (5-ACH3N2p16 and 6-ACH3N2p16) (figure 29). The 
three sequences 2-ORGH3N2-14, 2-ORGH3N2-14pas and “5-ACH3N2p16” (note the citation 
marks) were able to be cultivated in MDCK cells and therefore some drift must have occurred since 
the isolates from this year were not able to be cultivated.       
 
All the sequences containing the H3hu05 gene cluster together in the phylogenetic tree (figure 29). 
However, some drift was observed between herds owned by different owners. The same owner 
owns herds 9-ACH3N2p16 and 3-ACH3N2p16 and some drift was seen from herds 6-
ACH3N2p16, 5-ACH3N2p16 and 2-ORGH3N2-14, which are all owned by another owner. 
According to the Danish database on movements of pigs (www.svineflyt.fvst.dk) and figure 28, no 
movements of animals during the years 2004 to 2016 had occurred between the two productions 
systems. The two productions systems have different herd-veterinarians. It is unlikely to think that 
the introduction of the H3hu05 gene in the two systems happened because of airborne transmission 
since more than 200 kilometres separate the herds from each other, thus it is highly unlikely that the 
same staff works at both production systems. These findings suggest that the introduction into the 
two productions systems happened independently through a third source or spontaneously in both 
systems. The likelihood of the subtype to have occurred spontaneously in both production systems 
is highly doubtful since the similarity between the sequences is high (e.g. a similarity percentage of 
97.7% was found between the sequence from herd 2-ORGH3N2-14 and herd 3-ACH3N2p16, table

15). More investigation regarding possible third causes of introduction should be performed, e.g. if 
the owners or staff members had visited each other or met at conventions, if the transport vehicle 
used to transport the pigs to slaughter was the same etc.   
It is doubtful that the Respiporc vaccine from IDT Biologika, currently used by some herds to 
vaccinate Danish pigs, protects against the H3hu05N2sw subtype since some genetic drift have 
occurred when comparing the sequence of the IDT vaccine (H3sw IDT vaccine) to the sequences of 
the isolates found positive of the H3hu05 gene (9-ACH3N2p16, 3-ACH3N2p16, “3-ACH3N2p16”

nucleotides differences were identified when comparing the swine vaccine to the isolates with the 
H3hu05 gene. This means, that the similarities between the sequences only were a little over 80% 
(table 15). However, further studies should be performed before any conclusions on this potential 
lack of protection are made. Such studies should include a SNT test performed on cells where the 
subtype is able to infect the cells.  
 
Zoonotic potential 
Influenza subtypes with human H3 genes, as the ones described in this study (H3hu05 and 
H3hu15), has been isolated from pigs in Denmark. Furthermore, the H3 gene from the human 
seasonal influenza from 2005 (H3hu05) has also been isolated from outbreaks of influenza in mink 
in 2009 (unpublished data). The mink had clinical signs of respiratory infection (sneezing, 
coughing, haemorrhagic nasal discharge and deaths). By RT-PCR influenza virus was detected. The 
mink had been fed among others with uncooked swine lungs and tracheas from swine (unpublished 
data). If the swine lungs were the source of infection, this indicates that the human H3 gene from 
2005 is capable of transmission between species. Mink are known to be susceptible for influenza 
subtypes isolated from humans, swine and several other species [133]. It is believed that subtypes 
containing the H3hu05 gene have the potential of transmitting to humans. Inhabitants born after the 
season 2005 are believed to represent a naïve population in Denmark since they have not been 
exposed to this subtype. They might therefore be at risk of becoming ill if or when exposed to this 
subtype.      
 
Selection of case animals 
The animals were not randomly chosen in the herds where clinical signs were obvious. Neither were 
the animals in the case of no clinical signs since the veterinarians chose animals from pens where

respiratory illness is a problem in the particularly herd. However, since the aim of this project was 
to characterize the new influenza subtype this lack of randomization is not of great concern. The 
aim was to detect the new subtype. Animals showing clinical signs or animals belonging to pens 
where there use to be problems related to respiratory infections are probably the animals that have 
the highest possibility of being infected with influenza virus. If the study was designed to 
investigate whether there are some age groups who are more prone to infection with influenza

Cells 
Most of the GCCP guidelines are fulfilled [58]. However, there are place for some improvements 
such as routinely check for mycoplasma contamination, check for the correct cells and try to 
accomplish not to use antibiotics as a standard routine when using cells in the laboratory.  
 
The risk of mutations in the A549 and NPK cells that could lead to another subtype of cells is not of 
great concern. It is assumed that the cells still would represent the species of origin. It is the species 
from which cells are isolated from that are one of the aims of this project and not the specific kind 
of cell being tested for influenza infections. As long as the cells still have the human or porcine 
characteristic, it can be used in this context. To be completely sure of what kind of cells used, one 
should investigate this. Different methods exist for confirming the presence of a particular kind of 
cells. E.g. by staining the cells (cytokeratin, cilia, lipids etc.), chromosome analysis (karyotyping) 
and AFLP fingerprinting [65], [73], [81]. Briefly, the AFLP technique is a PCR reaction based on 
digestion of the genome with restriction enzymes and ligation of synthetic adapters and by selective 
amplification of fragments obtained using sequence-specific primers. The technique is used to 
confirm the species of origin by creating species specific DNA pattern [81], [134]. More details 
about the AFLP DNA fingerprinting is beyond the scope of this project and the reader is 
encouraged to find information elsewhere.   
 
One could argue about how representative it is to culture influenza virus in MDCK cells. This cell 
line is originally established from a dog [135]. Dogs are not commonly known to be infected by 
influenza viruses, but it has been shown that canine influenza A viruses exist and more attention on 
==PAGE:92==
 
 
92
influenza virus as an infectious cause to disease has been drawn [130], [136]. However, since the 
cell line is easy to use and has been used for many years, it is still the most used [60], [68], [73], 
[74]. Influenza viruses are not known to naturally infect kidney epithelial, however, both the 
MDCK and NPK cells are kidney epithelial cells. Both cell lines have demonstrated that it is 
possible to culture, replicate and isolate influenza virus from these [68], [69]. NPK cells were 
chosen since it was the cell line available and it was sought to test whether there were any 
differences when culturing virus in cells from human or porcine origin. The A549 cell line has also 
demonstrated that it is possible to culture, replicate and isolate virus from this cell line [73], [87]. 
Since the A549 cell line was the one available in house it was chosen to represent the cells of 
human origin.  
 
The NPTr cell line could have been chosen as an alternative to the NPK cell line. The NPTr cell line 
is established from tissue from a pig’s trachea and thus represents the cells involved in an influenza 
infection [68], [88]. Some authors have found that this cell line supports the replication of several 
influenza A viruses isolated from humans, swine and birds [81]. The cell line supports the growth 
and replication of influenza A viruses (and several other porcine viruses), and this is considered a 
huge advantage [81]. However, this cell line a long with the NPK cell line are contaminated with 
Porcine Endogenous Retrovirus (PERV), which mean that these cell lines, according to the FDA 
guidelines, cannot be used in the vaccine production [81]. There is a possibility that PERV’s can 
transmit to humans, which is why the use of a cell line contaminated with PERV’s cannot be used 
for e.g. human vaccine production [74]. However, studies have shown it difficult to prove, that 
PERV’s are a major concern [137]. Since this study is not about vaccine production, the cell line 
could indeed have been chosen. The NPTr cell line was not chosen for this project since it was not 
available in house and no employees at DTU Vet had any prior experience working with these cells.  
 
Another cell line, which could have been chosen, was the VERO cell line instead of the MDCK 
cells. This cell line allows growth of virus from clinical specimens and the growth of viruses 
produced for vaccines [79], [80]. No detectable selection of receptor binding variants have been 
found and no difference in the sequence of the HA gene have been found [76], [79]. However, some 
adaption to these cells is needed, which can influence on the results obtained [85]. Furthermore, this 
cell line is very sensitive to the addition of endogenous trypsin [74] and posses predominantly α2,3 
sialic acid and influenza specimens from humans prefers binding to α2,6 sialic acid [76]. This 
==PAGE:93==
 
 
93
might have some influence on which specimens that is able to be cultured in this cell line, however, 
studies show that it is possible to culture isolates from humans [76], [79]. The MDCK cell line was 
chosen over the VERO cells, since it was the cell line most often used by the personnel in charge of 
the training. Also, the MDCK cells supports a more rapid virus replication than VERO cells [72] 
and have been shown to be more sensitive to low viral load [64]. A high sensitivity is needed in this 
kind of project since it is important to detect the presence of influenza virus no matter the amount.   
   
Cell lines, which are known for not being selecting for receptor binding variants, could be 
considered in future studies. E.g. the VERO cell line or the SJPL cell line. As table 4 shows, many 
cell lines are only described partially. The lack of information makes the decision about which cell 
line to choose more difficult, since the more details missing, the more difficult it is for the 
researchers to explain why they see the results they see. This might be the reason why some cell 
lines are used more often than others, even though it might not be the most suitable cell line. 
Another important thing to mention (also listed in table 4) is the difference in how long it takes for 
virus to replicate in the cell lines. This might also have some impact on which cell line to choose 
depending on the time span of ones project.  Depending on the purpose of the study one might also 
notice, that some cell lines show signs of apoptosis and other necrosis when infected with influenza 
virus. If the purpose of the study is to investigate pathogenesis of influenza one should not consider 
to choose a cell line which gives necrosis when infected, since influenza viruses are known for 
inducing apoptosis [74]. Again this is a piece of information missing for a lot of the cell lines listed 
in table 4, which limits the choices of cell lines for the researchers.   
 
A more representative model for influenza culturing could be by using an ex vivo porcine nasal 
mucosa explant model. This model might represent a more naturally infection site of influenza 
viruses [138]. The explant model has some ethical issues since it requires the use of experimental 
animals. The cell lines bypasses these ethical considerations once established. However, since the 
nasal mucosa explant model mimics the in vivo situation [138], [139] and one pig can give rise to 
numerous explants, it might be a model used more in the future. According to the researchers using 
the nasal mucosa explant model, the criteria of the 3R’s of Russel and Burch [53], [54] is fulfilled 
[138]. The model replaces (replacement) initial in vivo pig experiments in which the animals are 
exposed and become ill. Refinement is achieved by minimum suffering of the animals since all the 
work and handling of the material happens after euthanasia. Reduction is achieved by the reduced 
==PAGE:94==
 
 
94
number of animals acquired. The animal-to-animal and experiment-to-experiment variability is also 
reduced [138]. However, even though the intra-species variation is reduced one should remember 
that one animal does not represent an entire population of animals. The use of pigs, however, does 
in fact represent a population with low intra-species variation in general due to breeding 
programmes and if bought from similar herds and kept under same management conditions. Studies 
comparing different cell lines with this model are needed to tell whether the explant model is a 
more suitable model or perhaps a good supplementary model. 
 
Nagy and Matrix rtRT-PCR 
Due to delivery problems it was necessary to use the Nagy rtRT-PCR assay to screen for the 
presence of influenza virus. The purpose of the initially rtRT-PCR assay was to detect the presence 
of influenza or not in the samples. In house testing of the two PCR assays showed no significant 
differences in the sensitivity of the two PCR assays (Appendix 16). It was known that, the samples 
contained influenza virus since this was initially detected by the diagnostic department who uses the 
matrix rtRT-PCR assay. The greatest concern about this mixed use of assays is the risk of false 
negative results regarding the MDCK culturing and afterwards PCR screening for influenza virus. 
However, if the results of the MDCK culturing were negative this only resulted in using the positive 
isolate from the diagnostic department in the multiplex RT-PCR assay and an additional passage in 
the MDCK cells. For the screening after 2nd passage the matrix assay was used.  
 
If the 2nd passage was negative and the culturing in ECE also was negative the sample could not be 
used in the full-length RT-PCR assay. Due to time limitations it was only possible to perform up to 
2nd passage in MDCK cells and two passages in the ECE. If more time had been available the 
sensitivity for positive virus isolation would have been higher with more passages of the samples 
which were negative in both the MDCK cells and ECE. The higher infectivity of the strains is 
achieved because of the natural selection of subtypes, which are more prone to be cultivated. 
 
Herd-veterinarians 
A letter was sent to the herd-veterinarians with clear instructions on how to fill in the submission 
form (Appendix 1). Unfortunately, not all the veterinarians did follow the instructions. 13 out of 20 
(65%) forms lacked information about whether there were any clinical signs of disease or not. 40% 
lacked information about the number of animals they took nasal swabs from. Out of the 20 
submission forms, 60% gave information about number of animals per tube. Out of these 60%, 50% 
==PAGE:95==

failed to fill the sample tubes with swabs from 10 animals as described in the letter. Thus, only 30% 
of the received samples were actually a pool of samples from 10 animals. Only five submission 
forms were filled in correctly (1-ACH3N2p16, 5-ACH3N216, 6ACH3N2p16, 7-ACH3N2p16 and 
8-ACH3N2p16), this corresponds to 25%. The five forms were sent from two different 
veterinarians. However, one of the veterinarians had sent an additional submission that lacked 
information about signs of clinical disease or not. The submission form used in this study 
(Appendix 2) is the submission form used by Danish veterinarians in general and is used when they 
have a suspicion of different pathogens in a swineherd. Therefore, it should have been an easy task 
for the veterinarians to fill in the form; however, this was clearly not the case. A way of making this 
bias go away would have been if we had been present in the herds on the day of sampling.         
 
The years of experience regarding the herd-veterinarians are considered a bias. Some herds (e.g. 17-
ACH3N2p16 and 19-ACH3N2p16), clearly positive for influenza last year in the surveillance 
programme, were found completely negative this year. Sample 16-ACH3N2p16, 17-ACH3N2p16 
and 18-ACH3N2p16 were collected by the same veterinarian and were all negative. The negative 
results might be due to lack of experience about the technique behind nasal swabbing. When 
performing a nasal swab one should make sure to get deep into the nasal cavity and touch the 
mucosal surfaces. This might not have been the case in the negative results. Even though a nasal 
swab is considered as a simple sample technique, it still requires some training. In the study design 
there were no requirements regarding as to how the veterinarians should perform the nasal swab. A 
more detailed description of how it should have been performed could have been written in the 
letter sent to the herd owner and herd-veterinarian. Another way of bypassing this bias could have 
been if we had collected the nasal swabs ourselves. However, this would have required some 
training period since none of us have any experience in how to perform a nasal swab. The training 
period should include taking nasal swabs from different age groups since it is more difficult to 
sample from large animals compared to e.g. neonatal piglets. It is believed, that if we had done the 
sampling collecting the sampling would have been performed in a more standardized way after the 
training period.  
 
A negative result could also have been obtained if the storage and handling of the sample tubes 
were not done correctly. The tubes were supposed to be kept in a refrigerator until the day of 
sampling and to be sent to DTU Vet the same day of sampling. As previously discussed, not all the

veterinarians did fill in the submission form correctly. Therefore, one cannot be sure whether the 
veterinarian actually read the letter sent to them with instructions or not. An example of this is that 
it is clearly written that the veterinarian should make sure that the lid on the tube is closed correctly. 
On arriving to DTU Vet it was found that a sample had leaked due to a lid not correctly fasten. If

about how the veterinarians stored the samples. If the samples e.g. were kept for days in an office 
with approximately 20-22°C this might have had an impact on the survival of the influenza virus, 
since influenza viruses are known to only survive for up to four days at 22°C [130]. If the samples 
were forgotten in the veterinarian’s car this is thought to have had a lesser impact on the survival of 
the virus since the outside temperature in Denmark during the sample period were around 4,0°C to 
8,8°C [140], [141] and influenza viruses can survive for longer periods at these temperatures [130].  
 
A less likely reason for the negative results could be that influenza viruses, in fact, no longer exist 
in the herds. Since influenza viruses are known to survive in feces for up to 35 days depending on 
the temperature [130] even an “all in, all out” production system would have difficulties getting rid 
of the virus unless they get rid of all feces and disinfect all surfaces [130], [142] – this is almost 
impossible to perform in practice. It would require getting rid of all residues of feces and to 
disinfect even in the smallest corners in the herds. This can be assured with good management and 
good intern biosecurity. One should also remember that the continuous flow of naïve animals makes 
it even easier for the influenza virus to survive in the environment and animals, since there is no 
higher demand for genetic drift or genetic shift.   
 
The role of the veterinarian being the source of introducing the subtype into the herds cannot be 
excluded. For the herds 5-ACH3N2p16, 6-ACH3N2p16 and 7-ACH3N2p16 the same veterinarian 
is responsible. The same is the case for the herds 3-ACH3N2p16 and 9-ACH3N2p16 (figure 28 
under the section “Results”). All the before mentioned herds were found to be positive of the 
H3hu05N2sw subtype, but in the case with the 3-ACH3N2p16 and 9-ACH3N2p16, the veterinarian 
was also responsible for others herds (figure 28), which were not found to be positive of the 
subtype. Although not conclusive, a more likely reason for the introduction of the subtype is 
movements of animals between the herds.

Conclusions 
In 2016, five out of 20 isolates from selected case herds were found to be positive of the 
H3hu05N2sw subtype. All of the isolates, including the samples originally sent to DTU Vet due to 
respiratory problems in the herds, were found to have no detectable HA activity when tested with 
erythrocytes from chicken and turkeys. Only isolates 2-ORGH3N2-14 and “5-ACH3N2p16” had 
the ability to be cultivated in MDCK cells. All other isolates could not be cultivated.  
 
Mutations, found by others to have influence of H3N2 subtypes with reduced HA activity and 
reduced ability to be cultured in MDCK cells, were found and identified in the isolates investigated 
in this study.  
We identified mutations not previously described by others, which might have influence on the 
change of receptor affinity. These mutations were found at positions 171, 189 and 244 in the HA 
gene sequences. In the sequences from the isolates capable of being cultured in MDCK cells, serine 
was found at position 158. This may explain the preserved ability to be cultivated in MDCK cells.  
 
A phylogenetic analysis of the HA gene in the isolates with H3hu05N2sw or H3hu15N2sw 
subtypes showed genetic drift from the Respiporc vaccine produced by IDT Biologika. This vaccine 
is currently used in Denmark to vaccinate pigs against influenza virus. Further studies are needed to 
make any conclusions on whether the vaccine provides immunity against the established 
H3hu05N2sw subtype or not. The phylogenetic tree may indicate that it is questionable that the 
current vaccine provides immunity.  
Based on the phylogenetic tree, it might be a possibility that the human vaccine from the season 
2016/2017 will provide protection against subtypes with the H3hu15 gene.  
 
Further studies targeting the newly found mutations in the sequences obtained during this study are 
needed to investigate whether these changes, a long with the rest of mutations already described by 
others, have influence on the change of receptor affinity or not. More studies are needed to 
investigate whether the Respiporc vaccine protects against subtypes with H3hu05 or H3hu15 genes 
and if the human vaccine from 2016/2017 provides protection against H3hu15 subtypes.

Perspectives and further work 
To confirm or reject hypothesis 1, the growing of cells from the A549 and NPK cell lines (or 
alternatives for these two cell lines) are needed. When establishment of two cell lines is gained, one 
should investigate if the subtype H3N2hu05N2sw prefer one cell line to the other. This can be 
tested e.g. by performing a TCID50 (50% tissue culture infectious dose) test. With this assay it is 
possible, through numerous sampling at different times, to measure the time at which the virus 
infects the cells. The faster the cells are infected the more affinity the virus has to the cells [71].  
 
As a last attempt to investigate the HA activity of the isolates, HA activity assays with erythrocytes 
from humans or guinea pigs could be done. Erythrocytes from guinea pigs have an even higher 
amount of the α-2,6 receptors compared to erythrocytes from chicken and turkey and others have 
found that recent isolates of the H3N2 subtype shows HA activity in erythrocytes from guinea pigs 
[71], [124]. Some authors have found that H3N2 subtypes only can agglutinate with erythrocytes 
from humans when originating from humans [61]. This is why it should be considered testing the 
HA activity against erythrocytes from humans, since the isolates investigated in this project have a 
H3 gene resembling the one found in humans.  
 
Future studies investigating the mutations identified in this study at positions 171, 189 and 244 in 
the HA sequence should be performed before any major conclusions about the impact of these 
amino acid positions are made. These studies might include reverse genetics and afterwards testing 
the isolates in erythrocytes from different species. The role of the amino acid located at position 158 
in the HA gene should be investigated further, since only isolates with serine at this position were 
capable of being cultivated in the MDCK cell line.   
 
Full genome sequencing by NGS should be performed to fully compare the different isolates to 
each other and to reference sequences. Information about the internal genes in the subtypes 
H3hu05N2sw and H3hu15N2sw would be interesting to gain, since the isolates confirmed to be 
H3hu05N2sw from last year’s surveillance programme were found to have the internal genes from 
the humane pandemic influenza strain from 2009. Knowledge about the internal genes from the 
samples from 2016 together with the human H3 gene is important when trying to predict whether 
this subtype have zoonotic potential or not.  
==PAGE:101==
 
 
101
To test whether or not the Respiporc vaccine produced by IDT Biologika will provide protection 
against the new subtypes isolated in Danish pigs, a SNT test should be done. The results of the SNT 
test would confirm or reject hypothesis 2. In future studies, one should consider using other cell 
lines than the MDCK, since these new subtypes were found not to be cultured in the MDCK cells. 
Some authors have already suggested other cell lines, e.g. modified MDCK (MDCK-SIAT1) cells 
with a change in the receptor density [71], [126]. The MDCK-SIAT1 shows promising results 
regarding the re-constitution of the ability of the H3N2 subtype to be cultivated in the cells and to 
show HA activity [71].   
 
Others have found that infection with influenza viruses containing the H3 gene can cause abortions 
in sows [131]. Furthermore, problems with both respiratory illness and reproductive problems were 
mentioned in the anamneses from the herd veterinarian responsible for herd 1-ORGH3N2-14 [109]. 
More investigation in this area is needed since this can have financial costs in the herds. However, 
this should be held up against other known causes to reproductive problems (e.g. PRRS, parvovirus, 
leptospirosis) [144]. A questionnaire to the veterinarians about any clinical symptoms in the herds 
at the time of sampling could provide for retrospective information about a possible correlation 
between the occurrence of influenza infections and reproductive problems.

Appendix 3:  IDT Biologika HA/HI protocol 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Determination of the titre of hemagglutination inhibiting antibodies  
against Influenza A virus 
 
 
 
 
1. Purpose 
 
The test is used for determination of hemagglutination inhibiting antibodies against the Influenza A virus in 
sera of pigs. 
 
 
2. Principle 
 
The hemagglutination inhibition test determines the inhibitory effects of antibodies on the property of 
influenza viruses to hemagglutinate erythrocytes. Antibodies prevent the interlinking of erythrocytes so that 
the erythrocytes behave as if no influenza virus antigens are present in the suspension. 
 
The test can be used to examine the blood sera of pigs. Since in the serum of some animals non-specific 
agglutination inhibitors or non-specific agglutinins may be contained, the serum samples are pre-treated with 
the aid of a neuraminidase and by an adsorption reaction with erythrocytes. 
 
 
3. Materials and Methods 
 
3.1. Materials 
 
- capillary tubes for hematocrit determination 
- 96 well microtitre plates, (round bottom) 
- the serum sample to be titrated 
- test-neuraminidase (SIGMA or equivalent) 
- cell culture medium ZZM-H  
- sodium citrate solution  
- chicken erythrocytes in Alsever’s solution  
- PBS-V (pH 7.2) 
- antigen suspension with known hemagglutination titre against the influenza virus strain under test 
- positive control serum  with known hemagglutination antibody titre against the influenza virus strain under 
test (adjustment of the required region after validation (3 independent test series with at least 5 replicates: 
mean ± 1 titration step)) 
==PAGE:115==
 
 
115
- negative control serum without hemagglutination antibody titre against the influenza virus strain under test, 
from pigs or Guinea pigs or in the case of basic research in the virology research lab, also from other animal 
species. 
 
Equipment required: 
-pipettes 
-centrifuge 
-centrifuge with insert for hemocrit capillary tubes 
-water bath to inactivate samples 
 
3.2. Methods 
 
Work steps: 
 
1. Pretreatment of the sera by neuraminidase (14 - 18 h, 37 °C), add sodium 
citrate, inactivate sera (30 min, 56 °C) 
2. Pretreatment of the sera by  adsorption on erythrocytes 
(1 h, 4 - 8 °C) 
Preparation of erythrocyte suspension (0.5 %) 
3. Adjustment of antigen suspension to 8 hemagglutinating units 
(8 HAU) and check of adjustment 
4. Check sera for unspecific  hemagglutination 
5. Carry out HAI test  
 
 
 
 
 
Description of test method: 
 
1. Pre-treatment of sera with neuraminidase 
 
- dilute test neuraminidase 1 : 40 in cell culture medium ZZM-H (pH 7.2)  
- add 25 µl of each serum sample to 100 µl freshly prepared neuraminidase dilution 
- incubate for 14 - 18 h at 37 °C 
- add 125 µl sodium citrate solution to each sample and the inactivate for 30 
minutes at 56 °C  
 
2. Pre-treatment of sera by adsorption on  erythrocytes 
 
- chicken erythrocytes can be kept up to one week at 4-8 °C in Alserver’s solution after being taken 
- wash erythrocytes 3x in PBS-V (pH 7.2) 
==PAGE:116==
 
 
116
- determine the hematocrit value (Ht) 
- add 10 µl of the harvested erythrocyte concentrate to each sample irrespective of the Ht value, which 
normally lies between 50 – 70.  
- incubate for 1 h at 4 - 8 °C 
- prepare a 0.5 % erythrocyte suspension with PBS-V (pH 7.2) from the remaining 
erythrocyte concentrate 
 
3. Adjustment of antigen suspension to 8 hemagglutinating units and check of adjustment 
 
- determine the HA titre of the antigen suspension to be used 
- to adjust the virus dilution for use to 8 HAU divide the reciprocal HA titre of the antigen suspension to 
be used by 8. The quotient represents the theoretical reciprocal dilution ratio for the antigen dilution 
(dilution medium: PBS pH 7.2)  
- example: HA titre 1 : 128 divided by 8 = 1 : 16 
→ Mix ratio: 1 part antigen + 15 parts PBS-V, pH 7.2 
- in addition to the calculated dilution prepare each time the next two higher dilution steps (in the above 
example 1:32 and 1:64) and the two next  lower dilution steps (in the above example 1:8 and 1:4) and titrate 
on a microtitre plate in the hemagglutination test. The dilution confirmed by the HA titre of 1:8 is the virus 
dilution for use. 
- prepare the antigen suspension in accordance with the determined suitable dilution step as the virus 
dilution for use, by dilution with PBS-V pH 7.2 
- the adjusted 8 HAU are checked again by back titration of the antigen suspension used in the test (see 
point 5).  
 
4. Checking the sera for unspecific hemagglutination (serum controls) 
 
- 25 µl from each serum sample are pipetted into one well of a microtitre plate (dilution step 1:10 by 
pretreatment) and diluted 1:2 with 25 µl PBS-V pH 7.2. 
-  subsequently 50 µl  erythrocyte suspension (0.5 %) is added per well.  
- after incubation of 15 minutes at 20-25°C no hemagglutination may occur in the serum controls; if 
hemagglutination occurs, this is to be noted on the test record with details of the unspecific 
hemagglutination titre.  
 
 
5. Implementation of hemagglutination inhibition reaction (HAI) 
 
- from each serum sample a dilution series is set up in log2 steps on a 96 well round bottomed plate: 
→ add 50 µl PBS-V, pH 7.2 to the first well 
→ pipette 25 µl PBS-V, pH 7.2 into the 2nd to 12th well  
→ pipette 50 µl of the sample to the first well (→ dilution step 1:20)   
→ then, over inoculation is carried out with 25 µl  each time, starting from the first well into the next well 
and up to the 11th well.  
→ 25 µl from the 11th well is discarded 
→ 25 µl from the 1st well is discarded 
→ pipette 25 µl from the 1st well in the 12th well (serum control)  
 
- once the dilution series is prepared 25 µl antigen (8 HAU) is pipetted each time to each of the wells   
- then the antigen antibody reaction is incubated for 45 minutes at 20-25 °C.  
- add erythrocyte suspension (0.5%) 50 µl to each well. 
- subsequently incubate the mixture for 30 minutes at 20 – 25°C.  
- evaluate by reading the hemagglutination inhibition 
 
==PAGE:117==
 
 
117
3.3. Controls 
 
For an erythrocyte control 50 µl PBS-V, pH 7.2 are pipetted into several wells. 
- 50 µl erythrocyte suspension (0.5 %) are added to each well. 
- incubate for  30-60 minutes at 20 - 25 °C. 
 
Back titration of the antigen suspension: 
→ pipette 100 µl of the adjusted antigen suspension into the first well  
→ pipette 50 µl PBS-V into a minimum of five further wells 
→ starting with the first well over inoculate 50 µl into the next well each time up to the end of the 
dilution series  
→ discard 50 µl from the last well 
Add 50 µl erythrocyte suspension (0.5 %) to each well. Incubate the back titration for 30 – 60 
minutes at 20 – 25 °C.  
 
 
4. Evaluation 
 
The assay can be evaluated when: 
- hemagglutination is readable (8 HAU) in the antigen back titration up to a dilution step of 1:8; marginal 
deviations in the region 1: 4/8 or 1: 8/16 can be tolerated. 
- in all wells of the erythrocyte control the complete sedimentation of the erythrocytes is recognisable 
as the forming of a button,  
- the HAI antibody titre of the positive control serum correlates with the known value  
- no HAI titres are seen in the negative control serum (≥ 1: 40)  
 
The result of the investigation is expressed as a titre of hemagglutination inhibitory antibodies (HAI titre) of 
the serum sample under test and corresponds to the highest dilution of the serum sample, in which complete 
inhibition of hemagglutination occurs. 
 
Borderline titres are compiled when the dilution following the highest dilution with complete 
hemagglutination inhibition shows a partial hemagglutination inhibition, which is only slightly different 
from a complete hemagglutination inhibition. Such titres are noted with the complete hemagglutination 
inhibition, forward slash, details of the titre with partial hemagglutination (e.g. 1:256/512) and express a 
medial titre where the maximum deviation tends towards the higher of the two titres listed.

Implementation 
General 
Neutralisation of viruses is a complex procedure that is influenced by the virus-antibody ratio in the 
reaction mixture, by the reaction conditions and by the indicator system. In the primary phase of 
virus neutralisation, weak antigen-antibody binding occurs on the viral surface, which results in an 
altered surface configuration of the viral particle and inhibition of the absorption or penetration of 
==PAGE:119==
 
 
119
the particle onto/into the host cell, the extent of the inhibition being dependent on the extent to 
which the antibodies are bound. This reaction does not cause the destruction of the virus. The virus 
can be released from its binding with the antibody and reactivated by means of dissociation effects 
during strong acidification or alkalinisation or chloroform treatment. In the early stage of the 
reaction, dilution of a neutralisation mixture can also lead to dissociation. In the secondary phase of 
the neutralisation reaction, more stable binding occurs and dissociation effects are reduced. 
Elevated temperatures accelerate the transition from the primary to the secondary phase. 
The advantage of the neutralisation test compared to other serological methods is primarily the 
greater specificity.

Method

9. Addition of trypsin 
Addition of trypsin is necessary for the replication of the influenza virus in cell cultures. Trypsin 
enables cleavage of the haemagglutinin on the surface of the influenza virus particle and thus the 
process of adsorption and penetration into the host cell. 
Addition of trypsin 200 BAEE units/100 ml is the optimum quantity for cultures in microtitre 
plates.  
Trypsin is pipetted into the neutralisation mixture after the end of the 1-hour incubation at 37°C and 
before the mixture is distributed into the microtitre plates, so that a final activity of 200 BAEE 
units/100 ml can be ensured in the total volume. 
 
Example calculation: 
It is recommended to produce suitable stock solutions of trypsin. Thus 500 mg of trypsin powder 
with an activity of 1090 BAEE units/mg, dissolved in 54.5 ml PBS-A pH 7.2, sterile-filtered and 
==PAGE:123==

portioned out as a 10x concentrated stock solution (10000 BAEE units/ml), can be stored for up to 
one year at -20°C. A 1:100 dilution of the stock solution (in medium) provides ready-for-use 
solutions of 100 BAEE units/ml. 
 
In order to ensure the appropriate trypsin concentration of 200 BAEE units/100 ml, 1.8 BAEE units 
must be added to each 0.9 ml aliquot of the neutralisation mixture, the virus back titration, the 
positive/negative controls and the MEM-SNT control (cell control). If a 1:100 dilution of the stock 
solution is used, this requires the addition of 18 µl per 0.9 ml.

11. Adsorption of the murine serum  
Before being used in the immunofluorescence assay, the antibody-containing murine serum (the influenza 
strain for obtaining antibodies from mice was produced in MDBK cell cultures) is adsorbed onto a fixed 
MDBK cell culture in order to exclude potential immunological reactions against the cell antigen.  
For this purpose, confluent MDBK cells (75 cm2 cell culture flask) are fixed with 80% acetone (30 min., 4-
8°C) and washed 3x with PBS-A, pH 7.2.  
Murine serum with a HAI titre of ≥ 1:80 against the virus strain is diluted 1:100 with PBS-A, pH 7.2. The 
fixed MDBK cell culture is incubated with 3.0 ml of this diluted serum (60 min, 37°C). The murine serum 
adsorbed by a 75 cm2 cell culture flask suffices for approximately 4 microtitre plates. 
In order to determine the working dilutions, we performed a chessboard titration of the adsorbed murine 
serum versus the anti-mouse-Alexa Fluor®488 AffiniPure Goat Anti-Mouse IgG (H+L) conjugate in infected 
MDCK cells. Suitable working dilutions are selected as being those that clearly show fluorescence at the 
highest dilutions. When sera and conjugates that have already been tested by the chessboard method are 
used, this step can be omitted and the already determined working dilution ratios can be used. 
 
 
12. Incubation and addition of trypsin 
The microtitre plates are then incubated for 24 hours at 37°C and 5% CO2. Trypsin is added after 
the incubation. For this purpose, MEM-SNT containing the appropriate trypsin concentration is 
==PAGE:125==
 
 
125
pipetted in aliquots of 100 µl. The concentration must be 600 BAEE units/100 ml for the 
neutralisation mixtures and controls (positive, negative, MEM-SNT), while for the virus back 
titration it must be 400 BAEE units/100 ml medium. When using the 1:100 dilution of the stock 
solution (10000 BAEE units/100 ml), as given in the example in section 9 (Addition of trypsin), this 
corresponds to adding 6 ml trypsin to MEM-SNT to obtain a total volume of 100 ml (1:16.6667) for 
the neutralisation mixtures as well as the controls and 0.4 ml trypsin to MEM-SNT to obtain a total 
volume of 10 ml (1:25) for addition to the virus back titration. Finally, the microtitre plates are 
incubated for a further 24 h at 37°C and 5% CO2. 
 
 
13. Fixation of the cells and performance of the immunofluorescence test 
After a total of 48 h incubation, the cells are fixed and subjected to immunofluorescence testing.  
The cells are fixed with 80% cold acetone (100 µl/well) for 30 minutes at 4-8°C. For this purpose, 
the medium is removed from the wells. After that, the microtitre plates are washed 3x with 100-
150 µl PBS-A, pH 7.2. 
The wells of the microtitre plates are coated with 35 µl of the working dilution of the adsorbed murine 
serum, see 11 (Adsorption of the murine serum) and incubated for 1 h at 37 °C.  
The cell cultures are washed 3x with PBS-A pH 7.2 and then the working dilution of the anti-mouse-Alexa 
Fluor®488 conjugate is added by pipette (35 µl/well, e.g. 1:500) see section 11 (Adsorption of the murine 
serum).  
The cells are then incubated for 1 h at 37 °C.  
After removal of the conjugate, the cell cultures are washed once with PBS-A pH 7.2 and twice with distilled 
water (WFI) and then dried.  
The evaluation is performed using an immunofluorescence microscope (490 nm, 10x 
magnification). 
All wells with clearly specific cell fluorescence are classified as positive.
