Abstract 
The urgent global transition toward sustainable energy requires innovative technologies 
to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and valorize waste streams. Syngas, primarily 
composed of carbon monoxide (CO), hydrogen (H₂), and carbon dioxide (CO₂) from 
biomass gasification, can be biologically converted into valuable chemicals such as 
acetic acid and other volatile fatty acids. However, high CO concentrations present 
toxicity challenges that inhibit microbial growth and productivity. 
This study investigates acetic acid production from CO-rich gas streams using mixed 
microbial consortia in mesophilic (37 °C) Trickle Bed Reactors (TBRs). The enhanced 
gas–liquid mass transfer and biofilm-forming capabilities of TBRs improved microbial 
interaction with gaseous substrates, increasing conversion efficiency. Operational 
parameters, including medium composition and hydraulic retention time (HRT), were 
optimized to maximize acetic acid yield and minimize byproduct formation. 
Batch kinetic experiments investigated microbial growth dynamics and metabolic 
pathways for converting CO and related substrates into organic acids. Direct CO 
conversion produced acetic acid without hydrogen, while homoacetogenic pathways 
dominated under H₂/CO₂ conditions. Ethanol oxidation and chain elongation formed 
longer-chain fatty acids, with sulfate influencing microbial roles but not the elongation 
process. 
TBRs were operated in four phases, varying tungsten availability and HRT. CO 
conversion efficiencies ranged from 77% to 94%, peaking at shorter HRTs of 1.5 days. 
Removing tungsten and reducing HRT from 3 to 1.5 days increased acetic acid 
production from 2.0 to 4.3 g/Lbed/d and decreased by-product formation, such as butyric 
acid. Acetic acid emerged as the dominant product, reaching titers of 10 g/L. TBR2 
demonstrated greater operational stability and more consistent steady-state 
performance than TBR1. 
This research advances the understanding of microbial syngas fermentation kinetics and 
reactor performance, offering valuable insights into substrate utilization and process 
optimization. Future studies should address CO toxicity, improve biofilm development, 
enhance gas–liquid mass transfer, and optimize operational parameters to increase 
acetic acid productivity and enable industrial-scale application.

1. Introduction and Scope of the Project 
The global transition toward more sustainable energy solutions has become 
increasingly urgent due to the rising consumption of fossil fuels and the consequent 
greenhouse gas emissions. As part of this transition, gas fermentation has emerged as a 
promising biotechnology for converting industrial waste gases, which are rich in carbon 
monoxide (CO) and carbon dioxide (CO₂), into valuable products like volatile fatty 
acids and biofuels (Nam et al. 2016; El-Gammal et al. 2017).  
Syngas, or synthesis gas, is a mixture primarily composed of carbon monoxide (CO), 
hydrogen (H₂), and carbon dioxide (CO₂), produced through processes like the 
gasification of fossil fuels or biomass. It serves as a key intermediate in the production 
of various fuels and chemicals (El-Gammal et al. 2017). As a feedstock for microbial 
fermentation, syngas enables specialized microorganisms to convert these gases into 
valuable biofuels and chemicals (Merli et al. 2021). With growing concerns over CO₂ 
emissions and the depletion of fossil reserves, syngas derived from industrial off-gases, 
waste materials, and even challenging biomass, such as sludge from wastewater 
treatment plants, is gaining increasing attention as a sustainable resource 
(Asimakopoulos et al. 2018).  
The fermentation of syngas, which contains CO, CO2, and H2, is carried out by 
microorganisms that employ the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway. This metabolic pathway is 
used by autotrophic microbes to convert single-carbon (C1) compounds like CO and 
CO₂, while using H2 as an energy source, into key intermediates, such as acetyl-CoA. 
These intermediates serve as precursors to produce various biofuels and chemicals (Liu 
et al. 2018). CO plays a crucial role in this process, serving as both a carbon and electron 
source for carboxydotrophic acetogens. These microbes use carbon monoxide 
dehydrogenase (CODH) to convert CO into acetyl-CoA, a key compound. Despite CO's 
toxicity, acetogens have developed mechanisms to efficiently oxidize it, making CO a 
valuable substrate. Through this fermentation process, notably, volatile fatty acids such 
as acetic acid, ethanol, and butanol are produced, all of which have numerous industrial 
applications (Gunes, 2021).  
However, gas fermentation faces several challenges related to mass transfer, including 
limited mass transfer rates and low concentrations of microorganisms within the reactor 
environment.  
==PAGE:8==
8 
 
These limitations, compounded by the low solubility of gaseous components in liquid 
media, hinder the overall efficiency of gas-to-liquid conversion, making reactor design 
optimization crucial for improving process performance (Gavala et al. 2021). As a 
result, optimizing parameters such as pH, temperature, and growth medium 
composition is essential for maximizing metabolic activity, improving yields, and 
enhancing fermentation efficiency (Steger et al. 2022). 
One promising approach to enhance gas fermentation efficiency is the utilization of 
Trickle Bed Reactors (TBRs), which have demonstrated considerable potential in 
improving gas-liquid mass transfer (Steger et al. 2022). TBRs operate with a packed 
bed that promotes the formation of microbial biofilms, which facilitate more efficient 
interaction between microorganisms and the feed gases. This design enhances microbial 
metabolic activity, thereby improving gas conversion rates (Asimakopoulos et al. 
2018). Compared to conventional gas fermentation systems, TBRs offer advantages in 
terms of higher volumetric productivity and superior control over operational 
conditions, which collectively optimize overall reactor performance (Quintela et al. 
2024). Additionally, the integration of mixed microbial consortia within TBRs enhances 
the efficiency of syngas fermentation by reducing sterilization needs and improving 
microbial adaptability to diverse substrates, such as CO, CO2, and H2 (Harahap & 
Ahring, 2023). These consortia offer long-term stability and resilience to toxic 
compounds, making them particularly advantageous for overcoming the inherent 
challenges of gas fermentation (Gavala et al. 2021, Tunca et al. 2025). The combination 
of TBR technology and mixed microbial cultures offers a promising strategy for 
enhancing the efficiency and sustainability of syngas fermentation processes. 
This project aims to improve the biological conversion of carbon monoxide (CO) into 
acetic acid using mesophilic Trickle Bed Reactors (TBRs) and mixed microbial 
consortia. It focuses on optimizing key parameters such as growth medium 
composition, dilution rates, and in-situ acetate removal strategies. By investigating 
changes in the growth medium and operational conditions, including the removal of 
tungsten, the project aims to develop a more sustainable method for converting waste 
gases into valuable chemicals, contributing to reduced greenhouse gas emissions and 
advancing sustainable chemical production.

2. State-of-the-art 
2.1 Acetic Acid  
Acetic acid (CH₃COOH) is a widely used organic acid, important in a variety of 
industries, including food, chemicals, and textiles. The global market for acetic acid 
was valued at approximately 16.3 million tons in 2020, with projections indicating a 
growth to 19.6 million tons by 2027 (Mutyala & Kim, 2022). Acetic acid serves as a 
key feedstock for the production of vinyl acetate monomer (VAM), a key ingredient in 
the manufacture of adhesives, packaging materials, and paints, accounting for a 
significant portion of global acetate consumption (Harahap & Ahring, 2023). It is also 
essential in the production of synthetic fibers and as an ingredient in vinegar (Budiman 
et al. 2016). The industrial production of acetic acid is traditionally based on fossil fuels; 
however, the increasing demand for more sustainable production methods has fueled 
interest in renewable sources, especially from biomass and waste streams (Merli et al. 
2021).

Acetic acid can be produced through various routes, with both chemical and biological 
methods being the most significant, as can be seen in Figure 1.

2.2.1 Chemical Routes 
The predominant method for industrial acetic acid production is through chemical 
processes, particularly methanol carbonylation, which accounts for approximately 75% 
of global production (Mutyala & Kim, 2022). This method uses rhodium or iridium-
based catalysts to produce acetic acid from methanol and carbon monoxide (Budiman 
et al. 2016). While efficient, this process presents challenges such as catalyst recovery, 
corrosion, and by-product formation, which can affect production efficiency and costs 
(Budiman et al. 2016). Other chemical routes, such as n-butane oxidation and 
acetaldehyde oxidation, are also utilized, but these methods are less widely employed 
compared to methanol carbonylation (Merli et al. 2021). Despite these challenges, 
chemical processes remain the most commercially viable option for large-scale acetic 
acid production. 
2.2.2 Biological Routes 
The biological production of acetic acid has gained significant attention in recent years 
because of its potential for sustainability and reduced environmental impact. There are 
two main biological routes: aerobic fermentation and anaerobic acetogenesis. 
Aerobic fermentation, involving bacteria such as Acetobacter or fungi, has also shown 
potential for acetic acid production, particularly when using food waste as a substrate 
(Li et al. 2015). This process has the added benefit of reducing waste while increasing 
yields and lowering production costs (Merli et al. 2021). However, challenges remain, 
particularly in optimizing production systems for high-efficiency conversion and 
scalability (Mutyala & Kim, 2022). Additionally, while fungal fermentation shows 
promise, it has yielded lower production levels compared to bacterial systems, and 
issues with oxygenation and substrate complexity hinder its widespread adoption (Merli 
et al. 2021).  
In addition, anaerobic acetogenesis has emerged as a promising alternative for acetic 
acid production, offering significant sustainability advantages. This method uses 
microorganisms, such as acetogenic bacteria. More specifically, acetogenic bacteria 
utilize CO, CO₂, and H₂ through gas fermentation, leveraging the Wood-Ljungdahl 
Pathway to achieve this conversion (Harahap & Ahring, 2023; Merli et al. 2021).  
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
The biological processes in anaerobic systems operate under milder conditions, require 
lower energy inputs, and produce fewer by-products compared to both aerobic 
fermentation and traditional chemical methods (Harahap & Ahring, 2023). 
In conclusion, while chemical processes remain dominant in acetic acid production, 
biological routes offer promising alternatives that can help meet the growing global 
demand for acetic acid more sustainably. The shift toward renewable feedstocks and 
advanced biotechnologies is expected to play a crucial role in shaping the future of 
acetic acid production and other products. This is why ongoing research focuses on 
improving the efficiency and scalability of these systems to meet the growing global 
demand for acetic acid (Harahap & Ahring, 2023). 
2.3 Gas/Syngas Fermentation 
Gas fermentation is a promising biotechnological process in which specific 
microorganisms, primarily autotrophic acetogens, utilize carbon monoxide (CO), 
carbon dioxide (CO₂), and hydrogen (H₂) derived from syngas as carbon and energy 
sources (Tunca et al. 2025). This process provides a sustainable alternative to traditional 
petrochemical production methods by converting waste gases, typically from industrial 
emissions or biomass gasification, into high-value products (Gavala et al. 2021; Gunes, 
2021). 
A key point in syngas fermentation is the Wood-Ljungdahl Pathway (WLP), also known 
as the reductive acetyl-CoA pathway, in which acetogens convert CO and CO₂ into 
acetyl-CoA, a key intermediate for producing biofuels and biochemicals such as acetic 
acid, ethanol, and higher alcohols (Harahap & Ahring, 2023).The efficiency of syngas 
fermentation is strongly influenced by the choice of microorganisms, as different strains 
can significantly affect yield and productivity (Ragsdale & Pierce, 2008). Moreover, 
the performance of mixed versus pure cultures plays a crucial role in optimizing process 
efficiency and product selectivity. The following section will explore the metabolic 
pathway in detail and examine the impact of microbial culture types on the overall 
fermentation process.

2.3.1 Overview of the Wood-Ljungdahl Pathway 
The Wood-Ljungdahl Pathway (WLP) is a central metabolic pathway used by 
autotrophic or carboxydotrophic microorganisms to convert carbon dioxide (CO₂) and 
carbon monoxide (CO) into valuable biochemical products like acetate. In the context 
of syngas fermentation, CO and CO₂ serve as carbon sources, while CO and H₂ also act 
as energy sources for microbial metabolism. The reductive acetyl-CoA pathway is 
illustrated in Figure 2 and is made up of carbonyl (sometimes referred to as “Eastern”) 
and methyl (or “Western”) branches (Daniell et al. 2012).  
 
Figure 2: Wood-Ljungdahl pathway of acetogens and their metabolic end products 
(Daniell et al. 2012). 
 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
Carbon monoxide (CO) enters the reductive acetyl-CoA pathway via two primary 
routes. In the Western branch, CO can be directly reduced by carbon monoxide 
dehydrogenase/acetyl-CoA synthase (CODH/ACS) to form the carbonyl group of 
acetyl-CoA, bypassing methylation steps (Bertsch et al. 2015). Alternatively, CO may 
undergo oxidation to CO₂ through the biological water gas shift reaction catalyzed by 
CODH. The energy released in this process is captured as reduced ferredoxin, which 
supports electron transfer processes essential for CO₂ reduction (Neto et al. 2025). 
In the Eastern branch, CO₂ is first reduced to formate, which reacts with 
tetrahydrofolate (THF) to produce formyl-THF. Formyl-THF is subsequently reduced 
to methyl-THF, which then reacts with CO to form acetyl-CoA (Bertsch et al. 2015; 
Gunes et al 2021). Acetyl-CoA is subsequently converted into acetate via substrate-
level phosphorylation, releasing ATP in the process (Gunes et al. 2021; Harahap & 
Ahring, 2023). 
Key enzymes such as CODH and ACS play central roles in this pathway. CODH 
catalyzes the oxidation of CO to CO₂, generating electrons that are transferred to 
ferredoxin. ACS then combines methyl and carbonyl groups to form acetyl-CoA. These 
enzymatic processes rely heavily on metal-containing clusters, such as nickel (Ni) and 
iron (Fe), which are essential for their catalytic functions (Bertsch et al. 2015). 
The dual role of CO as both a carbon and electron source underscores its versatility in 
syngas fermentation. Carboxydotrophic acetogens, specialized microorganisms capable 
of utilizing CO, exhibit high resistance to its toxicity, a property attributed to their 
robust enzymatic systems, CoA (Bertsch et al. 2015; Gunes et al. 2021). When H₂ is 
present, it acts as an additional electron donor, enhancing the reduction of CO₂ into 
acetate. However, at high CO concentrations, hydrogen uptake is reversibly inhibited, 
shifting the metabolic balance toward CO utilization as both carbon and energy sources 
(Daniell et al. 2012; Neto et al. 2025). 
Acetate synthesis via the WLP follows multiple pathways depending on the availability 
of CO and H₂. For instance, when CO serves as both a carbon and energy source, the 
process is described by the following reactions: 
                 6 CO + 3 H2O → CH3CH2OH + 4 CO2 (ΔG°´= −224 kJ/mol) (1) 
4 CO + 2 H2O → CH3COOH + 2 CO2 (ΔG°´= −175 kJ/mol) (2) 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
The interplay between CO, CO₂, and H₂ in the WLP highlights the flexibility of this 
pathway, enabling the efficient conversion of gaseous substrates into valuable end 
products such as acetate, ethanol, and butyrate (Harahap & Ahring, 2023; Neto et al. 
2025). 
2.3.2 Microorganisms 
As previously discussed, microorganisms play a vital role in the WLP as they contribute 
to the production of specific value-added products, as illustrated in Figure 1.  
More specifically, carboxydotrophic microorganisms, as mentioned above, are a 
diverse group capable of utilizing carbon monoxide (CO) as a primary carbon and 
energy source. They are broadly categorized into carboxydotrophic acetogens, 
methanogens, and hydrogenogens, each distinguished by their metabolic pathways and 
physiological functions ;(Drennan et al. 2004; Poehlein et al. 2025). 
Among these, autotrophic acetogens, particularly homoacetogenic microorganisms, are 
of significant interest due to their ability to convert syngas into value-added 
biochemicals such as acetate (Poehlein et al. 2025). As described above, these 
microorganisms utilize the WLP to convert CO₂ and CO into acetyl-CoA, a precursor 
for acetate synthesis (Bertsch et al. 2015).  
Homoacetogenic acetogens are specialized in fermenting syngas, efficiently 
metabolizing CO and CO₂ into acetyl-CoA, which is further converted into acetate 
(Fernández-Naveira et al. 2017). These microbes are highly metabolically flexible, 
capable of utilizing various electron donors, such as H₂ or CO, and adapting to diverse 
environmental conditions. This flexibility allows them to produce a range of products, 
including volatile fatty acids (e.g., butyrate, hexanoate) and alcohols (e.g., ethanol, 
butanol, hexanol) (Bertsch et al. 2015; Gunes et al. 2021).  
Autotrophic acetogens can be further classified into mesophilic and thermophilic 
species, with representatives such as Acetobacterium woodii (mesophilic) and Moorella 
thermoacetica (thermophilic) (Poehlein et al. 2025). Table 1 below provides an 
overview of carboxydotrophic microorganisms, categorized by their optimal growth 
temperature.

2.4 Microbial Strains 
2.4.1 Pure Cultures 
Pure cultures, or axenic cultures, involve a single microbial strain. These cultures are 
widely used in laboratory-scale research due to their reproducibility and ease of control. 
A single microorganism provides consistent nutrient requirements, growth rates, and 
physiological characteristics, facilitating process optimization (Blaser et al. 2013; 
Esquivel-Elizondo et al. 2017). Mesophilic bacteria, such as Clostridium ljungdahlii, 
C. carboxidivorans, and C. autoethanogenum, are often employed for syngas-to-acetate 
fermentation due to lower energy requirements for process heating and the availability 
of genetic tools. These bacteria produce acetate alongside other by-products such as 
ethanol, butyrate, and 2,3-butanediol, depending on gas composition and culture 
conditions (Jack et al. 2019; Lanzillo et al. 2020). Thermophilic bacteria, like Moorella 
thermoacetica and Desulfotomaculum kuznetsovii, are also effective for syngas 
fermentation. They exhibit higher growth rates and lower contamination risks due to 
elevated temperatures. Notably, M. thermoacetica can produce acetate as the sole 
product under optimized conditions, with concentrations reaching up to 31 g/L 
(Harahap & Ahring, 2023). This strain is genetically modifiable, making it a promising 
candidate for industrial applications. 
2.4.2 Mixed Cultures 
Mixed microbial cultures consist of diverse microbial communities, offering significant 
advantages over pure cultures, particularly for the production of low-value bulk 
chemicals and fuels in industrial-scale syngas fermentation. These include reduced 
sterilization requirements, enhanced microbial adaptability, and the ability to utilize 
diverse substrates (Harahap & Ahring, 2023). The synergistic interactions within 
microbial consortia enable long-term stability and tolerance to variations in syngas 
composition (Esquivel-Elizondo et al. 2017; Singla et al. 2014). Anaerobic mixed 
cultures, comprising mesophilic and thermophilic inocula, have demonstrated high 
acetate yields. Studies have highlighted the efficiency of mixed cultures for industrial-
scale syngas fermentation.  For example, Wang et al. 2017 reported acetate 
concentrations of up to 42 g/L using a thermophilic mixed culture containing

Similarly, Kolter et al. (2013) demonstrated that thermophilic consortia achieved high 
acetate yields under both batch and continuous fermentation conditions, showcasing 
their robustness for various operational setups. Esquivel-Elizondo et al. (2017) further 
demonstrated the resilience of mixed cultures sourced from anaerobic digesters. These 
consortia achieved stable acetate production even under fluctuating syngas 
compositions, leveraging their metabolic diversity for the simultaneous utilization of 
CO, H₂, and CO₂.  
2.4.3 Mixed vs Pure Cultures 
In summary, while pure cultures offer precise control, high reproducibility, and 
consistent product purity, they come with increased operational complexity and cost 
due to the need for stringent conditions and sterilization. On the other hand, mixed 
cultures are more resilient, cost-effective, and adaptable to industrial environments, 
though their performance is less predictable. The inherent advantages of mixed cultures, 
such as reduced sterilization requirements and the ability to utilize diverse substrates, 
make them particularly well-suited for syngas fermentation, providing tolerance to 
variations in syngas composition. Future research should focus on enhancing the 
advantages of both approaches by optimizing the operational conditions for mixed 
cultures, thus improving process efficiency and stability while maintaining the 
flexibility required for industrial-scale applications.

3. Optimization of Syngas Fermentation Conditions 
3.1 pH and Temperature 
Controlling pH and temperature are critical factors influencing acetate production 
during syngas fermentation. According to Harahap & Ahring (2023), maintaining a 
neutral pH is essential in pure culture fermentations to achieve significant acetate 
concentrations. Several studies indicate that a shift in pH from neutral to acidic levels 
leads to a metabolic transition from acidogenesis to solventogenesis (Abubackar et al. 
2011; Arslan et al. 2021, Kundiyana et al. 2011). This change reduces acetate yields 
while increasing ethanol production, primarily due to the decreased activity of CO 
dehydrogenase (CODH), a key enzyme in acetyl-CoA formation. Adjusting the external 
pH to neutral helps counteract internal pH disruptions caused by acetic acid diffusion 
into cells, allowing acetogens to metabolize syngas into acetate more effectively 
(Harahap & Ahring, 2023). 
In mixed culture fermentations, pH control is significantly affected by methanogens, 
which thrive at neutral pH and convert syngas and acetate into methane. This 
competition for acetate can lower acetate yields, making pH management more 
challenging.  (Harahap & Ahring, 2023) describe strategies to inhibit methanogens 
according to several studies, such as heat treatments, pH adjustments to slightly acidic 
(pH 5.5–6.0) or alkaline (pH 8–10) conditions, or the use of 2-bromoethanesulfonate 
(BES). Heat treatment can selectively eliminate non-spore-forming methanogens while 
preserving spore-forming acetogens like Clostridium species (Kannaiah Goud et al. 
2014). BES, although effective as a methanogenic inhibitor, is costly and less feasible 
for continuous systems. Supporting these findings, (Liu et al. 2018) demonstrated that 
an alkaline pH of 9.0 resulted in higher acetate yields with minimal ethanol and methane 
production. Similarly,  (Luo et al. 2018) found that employing BES at pH 6.5–7.5 
facilitated elevated acetate yields while minimizing byproducts. 
Temperature optimization is equally critical for acetate production. Shen et al. 2020 
observed that lowering fermentation temperatures from 37°C to 25°C led to reduced 
acetate concentrations due to organic acid reassimilation by Clostridium 
carboxidivorans. For C. ragsdalei, acetate production peaked between 32°C and 37°C 
but declined at higher temperatures (up to 42°C), attributed to reduced syngas solubility 
and limited cell growth (Harahap & Ahring, 2023).  
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
Fermentation at temperatures above 50°C is advantageous as it reduces the energy 
needed for syngas cooling (Harahap & Ahring, 2023). However, high-temperature gas 
fermentation faces challenges due to the lower solubility of syngas in the fermentation 
broth at elevated temperatures. While the solubility decreases, mass transfer rates 
typically increase because higher temperatures lead to higher volumetric mass transfer 
coefficients. This can improve the overall efficiency of gas transfer, but the balance 
between solubility and mass transfer needs careful consideration (Dutta et al. 2024). 
Moreover, several studies highlight that in mixed culture fermentations, methanogens 
are more effectively suppressed at lower temperatures. Psychrophilic conditions, such 
as 20°C, inhibit methanogens while maintaining acetate production by acetogens, albeit 
with slightly reduced yields compared to mesophilic conditions (Liu et al. 2018). 
In summary, optimizing pH and temperature is key to maximizing acetate production 
and minimizing byproducts in syngas fermentation. Neutral pH and mesophilic 
temperatures generally favor acetogens, while adjustments such as alkaline pH can 
further enhance acetate yields, particularly in mixed cultures by reducing methanogen 
activity. 
3.2 Growth Medium Components of Fermentation Media 
The growth medium is a critical factor in optimizing the efficiency of syngas 
fermentation, particularly for the conversion of carbon monoxide (CO) and carbon 
dioxide (CO₂) into acetate via the Wood-Ljungdahl Pathway (WLP). The activity of key 
enzymes such as Formate Dehydrogenase (FDH), Carbon Monoxide Dehydrogenase 
(CODH), Acetyl-CoA Synthase (ACS), Hydrogenase (H2ase), Phosphotransacetylase 
(PTA), and Acetate Kinase (ACK) is significantly influenced by the presence of specific 
metal cofactors. These cofactors enhance the enzymes' function, driving the formation 
of acetyl-CoA, a crucial precursor for acetate production (Harahap & Ahring, 2023). 
For example, FDH, responsible for reducing CO₂ to formic acid, requires metal 
cofactors such as iron (Fe), tungsten (W), selenium (Se), and molybdenum (Mo) for 
optimal function. Supplementing the fermentation medium with these metals enhances 
the reduction of CO₂ and boosts the production of formic acid (Drennan et al. 2004). 
Similarly, CODH and ACS, which are essential for acetyl-CoA accumulation, rely on 
metals such as iron (Fe), nickel (Ni), and sulfur (S) in their active sites.  
 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
Studies have shown that increasing Ni concentrations up to 8.4 µM significantly 
enhances CODH activity, leading to higher acetate production in Clostridium ragsdalei 
(Drennan et al. 2004). Conversely, removing nickel from the medium inhibits cell 
growth and acetate production (Harahap & Ahring, 2023). 
Hydrogenase enzymes, which are involved in the oxidation of hydrogen to generate 
reducing equivalents, also require Ni and Fe to function effectively. The presence of 
these metals has been shown to promote hydrogenase activity and consequently 
increase acetate formation (Han et al. 2020). Furthermore, PTA and ACK, which play 
a critical role in acetate formation, require molybdenum (Mo) for optimal activity. 
Deficiency in Mo results in lower gene expression and reduced acetate yields, 
highlighting the importance of this metal in fermentation media (Han et al. 2020). 
The carbon and nitrogen sources in the fermentation medium also impact microbial 
growth and product accumulation. Yeast extract, a common nutrient supplement, 
supports cell growth and increases acetate yields. However, its high cost limits its 
application for large-scale processes (Infantes et al. 2020). To reduce costs, alternative, 
more economical carbon and nitrogen sources are being explored. Corn Steep Liquor 
(CSL), derived from the corn wet-milling process, is one such alternative. CSL is rich 
in amino acids, minerals, and carbohydrates, making it a suitable replacement for yeast 
extract in supporting microbial growth and enhancing acetate production (Thi et al. 
2020). Other alternatives, like malt extract and vegetable extract, are also being 
investigated. Malt extract, derived from malt barley, is particularly rich in 
carbohydrates, while vegetable extract provides additional nitrogen and minerals, 
contributing to microbial growth and acetate production (Harahap & Ahring, 2023; Thi 
et al. 2020). In addition to these carbon and nitrogen sources, hydrolyzed cottonseed 
flour and soy flour are also being explored as potential alternatives. These hydrolyzed 
flours are cost-effective and provide essential amino acids and other nutrients needed 
for acetogenic growth (Harahap & Ahring, 2023). 
 
 
 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
Vitamins are essential for promoting microbial growth and enhancing product 
accumulation during syngas fermentation. Specifically, calcium pantothenate (vitamin 
B5) and vitamin B12 have been shown to play critical roles in acetate production. 
Calcium pantothenate serves as a precursor for acetyl-CoA formation, which is vital for 
acetate production. The addition of calcium pantothenate to the fermentation medium 
supports the Wood-Ljungdahl Pathway by enhancing acetyl-CoA synthesis (Kundiyana 
et al. 2011). Vitamin B12, a critical component of the cobalamin-dependent 
Methylmalonyl-CoA mutase (MeTr) enzyme, is involved in the pathway leading to 
acetate production. Including vitamin B12 in the fermentation medium has been shown 
to enhance acetate concentrations during syngas fermentation (Harahap & Ahring, 
2023). 
Finally, trace elements such as cobalt (Co), copper (Cu), manganese (Mn), selenium 
(Se), and tungsten (W) play critical roles in the metabolic processes of acetate 
production during syngas fermentation. While cobalt and selenium have been shown to 
positively influence acetate production in certain strains, other trace elements like 
copper, tungsten, and manganese do not appear to have significant effects on acetate 
formation (Han et al. 2020).  
More specifically, selenium and tungsten have garnered attention for their effects on 
both cell growth and metabolite production. Selenium, even at low concentrations (0.01 
μM), enhances cell growth and total metabolite output due to its role in activating 
formate dehydrogenase (FDH), a key enzyme in the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway. 
Tungsten, on the other hand, plays a crucial role in increasing the ethanol/acetic acid 
production ratio by activating aldehyde: ferredoxin oxidoreductase (AOR), which 
facilitates the reduction of acetate to ethanol. Studies have shown that a medium 
containing 0.01 μM selenium and 0.1 μM tungsten achieved a specific ethanol 
productivity of 0.462 g ethanol/g DCW·d, a 2.18-fold increase compared to using 0.01 
μM of both selenium and tungsten (An & Kim, 2022).  
Interestingly, the absence of tungsten in a selenium-containing medium led to higher 
acetic acid production (7.34 g/L) and limited ethanol and butanol yields (1.85 g/L and 
1.2 g/L, respectively). In contrast, selenium deprivation while maintaining tungsten 
resulted in reduced acetic acid production (6.6 g/L) but increased ethanol (4 g/L) and 
butanol (1.88 g/L) concentrations at lower pH conditions (Chakraborty et al. 2020).  
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
These findings highlight the complementary roles of trace elements and especially 
tungsten in influencing metabolic pathways. Consequently, in the current project, it was 
decided to eliminate tungsten from the medium to favor higher acetic acid production. 
By excluding tungsten, the metabolic focus shifts away from ethanol synthesis, aligning 
with the project's goal of maximizing acetate yields. 
3.3 Reactor Types  
Various reactor types are employed in syngas fermentation, each offering distinct 
benefits and challenges based on their design, operation mode, and ability to support 
microbial growth and optimize gas-liquid mass transfer (GLMT) as illustrated in 
Figures 3 and 4. The selection of an appropriate reactor depends on several factors, 
including the type of microorganisms, the required operational conditions, and the 
economic considerations related to energy consumption and scalability. 
One of the most used reactor types is the Continuous Stirred Tank Reactor (CSTR), 
which is favored for its simple operation and efficient mixing capabilities. In syngas 
fermentation, however, the CSTR faces challenges such as the need for high agitation 
speeds, which increases energy consumption and can damage shear-sensitive bacteria 
(Zhang et al. 2021). The reactor’s design also requires specialized components like 
agitators and baffles to improve GLMT efficiency, making it less ideal for large-scale 
syngas fermentation due to the potential for fermentation instability and high energy 
use (Harahap & Ahring, 2023; Zhang et al. 2021). 
As an alternative, the Bubble Column Reactor (BCR) offers a simpler design with 
lower energy consumption. BCRs are cylindrical vessels filled with a liquid phase, with 
a gas sparger located at the bottom. The sparger introduces gas into the reactor, forming 
bubbles that create mixing within the liquid. For the effective design of a BCR, careful 
attention must be given to two key factors: the diameter-to-length ratio of the reactor 
and the type of gas sparger used to generate the bubbles (Asimakopoulos et al. 2018). 
Gas-liquid mass transfer efficiency can be improved by reducing bubble size and 
increasing gas flow rates, although higher flow rates may result in incomplete syngas 
conversion, necessitating gas recirculation (Kundiyana et al. 2011; Zhang et al. 2021). 
The BCR, however, is prone to foaming, which requires the addition of antifoam agents 
(Y. Shen et al. 2014).  
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
The Airlift Reactor (ALR), featuring a draft tube, is another viable option. The draft 
tube assists in gas circulation and increases residence time, facilitating more efficient 
gas utilization during syngas fermentation (Harahap & Ahring, 2023). 
 
Figure 3: Schematic description of reactors: (A) Continuous Stirred Tank Reactor 
(CSTR), (B) Bubble Column Reactor (BCR), (C) Airlift Reactor (ALR) (Aftab & 
Hakeem, 2021; Bakratsas et al. 2021). 
Biofilm reactors (Fig. 4), such as the Hollow Fiber Membrane Biofilm Reactor 
(HFMBR) and the Trickle Bed Reactor (TBR), offer advantages in terms of enhanced 
cell retention and acetate production. The HFMBR utilizes a microporous membrane to 
facilitate biofilm formation while reducing shear stress and energy consumption (Gunes 
et al. 2021; Wang et al. 2017). However, excessive biofilm growth may cause 
biofouling, negatively impacting reactor performance (El-Gammal et al. 2017; Gunes, 
2021).  
Among biofilm reactors, the Trickle Bed Reactor (TBR) has shown particular promise 
in syngas fermentation. The TBR is designed to provide an optimal environment for 
biofilm growth, where microorganisms adhere to the surface of a packed bed and 
interact with the feed gases. One of the primary advantages of TBR is its ability to 
facilitate a continuous, high-rate conversion of syngas into valuable products by 
increasing the residence time of both the gas and the liquid phases, allowing for more 
efficient gas utilization (Devarapalli et al. 2016). The TBR operates with a co-current 
flow of both gas and liquid, promoting uniform distribution and enhanced gas-liquid 
mass transfer, which is crucial in syngas fermentation processes. 
 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
 Additionally, the packed bed design helps to maintain a high density of immobilized 
microbial cells, which is beneficial for improving fermentation productivity (Gunes et 
al. 2021; Zhang et al. 2021). However, there are challenges associated with TBRs, 
particularly related to the distribution of gas and liquid phases. Non-uniform flow 
patterns can lead to issues like flooding or channeling, which can significantly reduce 
reactor efficiency. To mitigate this, various operational parameters such as flow rates, 
packing material properties, and reactor design can be optimized. For instance, the size 
and shape of the packing material can be modified to improve the contact between the 
phases and reduce maldistribution. Moreover, careful control of liquid distribution 
ensures that the biofilm remains hydrated, which is essential for microbial activity 
(Devarapalli et al. 2016; Y. Shen et al. 2014).  
Another concern is the potential for clogging, especially in reactors that operate at high 
feed rates, which can restrict the flow of gas and liquid through the packed bed (Steger 
et al. 2022). Despite these challenges, TBRs have several advantages that make them 
suitable for large-scale syngas fermentation. The increased microbial retention, 
enhanced gas-liquid interaction, and scalability of the TBR make it a competitive option 
for industrial applications (Gunes et al. 2021). Studies have shown that the TBR design 
can lead to high rates of syngas conversion, with microorganisms being less affected 
by shear forces compared to other reactor types (Devarapalli et al. 2016; Harahap & 
Ahring, 2023).  
Another biofilm reactor, the Rotating Packed Bed Reactor (RPBR), uses a rotating 
cage where immobilized cells alternate between gas and liquid phases. This design 
accelerates mass transfer, as diffusion across the gas-liquid interface is the rate-limiting 
step in gas fermentation processes (Y. Shen et al. 2017; Stoll et al. 2020). RPBRs are 
also more efficient in handling a wide range of flow rates and pressures compared to 
traditional fixed-bed reactors. However, they can suffer from issues such as pipe 
clogging and corrosion (Harahap & Ahring, 2023). 
Additionally, the Monolithic Biofilm Reactor (MBR) incorporates monolithic packing 
materials to enhance gas-liquid mass transfer and reduce microbial washout, making it 
an upgraded version of the BCR. MBRs offer the advantage of higher cell density and 
greater GLMT efficiency, although they can be prone to channel clogging and require 
careful management of operation parameters (Gunes et al. 2021; Shen et al. 2017). 
==PAGE:25==
25 
 
Lastly, the Bulk-Gas-to-Atomized-Liquid Reactor (BGAL) has demonstrated high 
energy efficiency, with energy requirements significantly lower than those of the CSTR, 
further supporting its potential for large-scale syngas fermentation applications (Sathish 
et al. 2019)

4. Future Perspectives 
The future of syngas fermentation, particularly the conversion of carbon monoxide 
(CO) into acetic acid, holds immense potential for sustainable chemical production. CO 
serves as a critical substrate due to its dual role as a carbon source and electron donor, 
offering a pathway to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and transform industrial waste 
gases into valuable products (Harahap & Ahring, 2023). However, achieving complete 
CO fermentation with high selectivity and productivity remains a key challenge. 
Overcoming CO toxicity is a primary focus, as high concentrations can inhibit 
microbial activity (Budiman et al. 2016).Research must prioritize enhancing the 
metabolic efficiency of carboxydotrophic acetogens, which play a crucial role in 
converting CO into acetic acid. In the context of Trickle Bed Reactors (TBRs), future 
studies should investigate strategies to improve mass transfer and gas-liquid 
interactions, which are essential for maintaining stable, continuous operations (Steger 
et al. 2022). For example, optimizing biofilm configurations and utilizing innovative 
packing materials can enhance reactor performance. Genetic engineering of acetogens 
to increase the activity of critical enzymes, such as carbon monoxide dehydrogenase 
(CODH), may further improve the conversion efficiency of CO into acetyl-CoA, a 
precursor for acetic acid (Budiman et al. 2016). Moreover, employing mixed microbial 
cultures under mesophilic conditions could offer advantages such as increased 
microbial adaptability and reduced sterilization requirements, making the process more 
feasible for industrial applications (Harahap & Ahring, 2023). Investigating the role of 
growth of medium components, dilution rates, and in-situ acetate separation techniques 
is a promising avenue for enhancing productivity and selectivity in TBRs (Gunes, 
2021). 
5. Research Gap and Study Focus 
While syngas fermentation, particularly the conversion of carbon monoxide (CO) into 
acetic acid, has been extensively studied, critical gaps remain. As detailed in the 
literature review and future perspectives, the potential of mixed microbial cultures 
under mesophilic conditions and strategies to enhance acetic acid productivity and 
selectivity in continuous operations within Trickle Bed Reactors (TBRs) are 
underexplored. This project aims to address these gaps by optimizing growth medium 
composition, refining dilution rates, and implementing in-situ acetate separation 
technologies, contributing to more efficient and sustainable acetic acid production.

Each reactor had an empty bed volume of 370 ml and was packed with BioFLO 9 
material (polypropylene/polyethylene blend), which optimized surface area for 
microbial growth and enhanced gas-liquid interactions. The gas flow into the reactors 
was precisely regulated using mass flow controllers (Bronkhorst, Netherlands), 
ensuring stable gas input and efficient phase contact.

The system operated with a 400 ml liquid working volume maintained in a sealed 
reservoir. Liquid was continuously recirculated from the reservoir to the top of the 
packed column using a peristaltic pump (Watson Marlow, UK), ensuring uniform 
distribution across the packing material. Fresh growth medium was introduced three 
times daily via a timer-controlled pump, while liquid effluent exited the system through 
an overflow outlet in the reservoir. Gas flowing through the packed column combined 
with the recirculated liquid in the reservoir and exited through the same outlet, with the 
liquid level kept constant by the gas pressure within the reactor. This design ensured 
stable operation by balancing gas and liquid flows, while sealed gas traps prevented air 
backflow, maintaining the anaerobic conditions essential for efficient gas fermentation. 
To maintain stable pH levels, a control system featuring a pH electrode, a transmitter, 
and dosing pumps for acid (0.5 M HCl) and base (5 M KOH) automatically regulates 
the pH in the reservoir, providing an optimal environment for microbial activity (Fig. 
5). For gas flow measurement, instead of a conventional flowmeter, the nitrogen (N₂) 
concentration in the outlet gas is used to calculate flow, offering a more accurate 
quantification method. This setup allows precise control and monitoring of gas and 
liquid flow, pH, and anaerobic conditions essential for gas fermentation.  
6.3 Operation of the Trickle Bed Reactors 
The gas mixture used in the experiment consisted of 90% CO and 10% N₂. The Trickle 
Bed Reactors (TBRs) operated under mesophilic conditions at 37°C and a controlled 
pH of 7.5. The gas flow rate was 3.8 ml/min, corresponding to an empty bed residence 
time (EBRT) of 97 minutes as analyzed below. The hydraulic retention time (HRT) was 
initially 3 days. After the effect of HRT was tested, it was lowered to 1.5 days to 
evaluate performance under different conditions. The reactors' operational parameters 
were kept constant until a steady state was achieved in each experimental phase.

solution, 84 ml of potassium phosphate dibasic solution, 20 ml of chelating agent 
solution, 10 ml of sodium sulfate solution, 1 ml of reducing agent and 0.5 g/L yeast 
solution were combined, and the total volume was adjusted to 1 liter with demineralized 
water. 
6.4.2 Inoculum Source and Methodology 
The inoculum was prepared by mixing the active effluent from the TBRs with a gas 
mixture composed of 60% CO and 40% N₂ at a pressure of 1.8 atm. A fresh inoculum 
was prepared in a 300 ml flask, with a final liquid working volume of 100 ml and an 
inoculum size of 20% v/v. Serum vials were filled with 80 ml of medium under 
continuous N2 flushing to maintain anaerobic conditions, then sealed with rubber 
stoppers and screw plugs. Following sealing, the inoculum was added anaerobically, 
and the gas mixture (CO and N2) was adjusted to a total pressure of 1.8 atm using a 
precision pressure indicator (model CPH6400, WIKA, Germany). All gases used had a 
purity of over 99.9%, sourced from Air Liquide (Denmark). The serum vials were 
incubated in the dark at 37 ℃ and shaken at 100 rpm. The headspace pressure was 
monitored daily to ensure the inoculum remained in the active growth phase for the 
subsequent kinetic experiments. 
6.4.3 Experimental Conditions and Set-up 
The kinetic parameters of microbial groups in the active effluent of a mesophilic TBR, 
responsible for different possible biochemical reactions converting CO to acetic acid 
under mesophilic conditions (37 ℃), were determined through batch experiments. 
These experiments, performed in triplicate using 300 mL serum vials with a 100 mL 
liquid working volume and 20% v/v inoculum, are summarized in Table 4. Experiments 
1 and 2 were set up to study the growth kinetics of carboxydotrophic acetogens for the 
direct or indirect conversion of CO to acetic acid and ethanol. The experiment 3 was 
set up to study the growth kinetics of hydrogenotrophic acetogens responsible for the 
conversion of H₂/CO₂ to acetic acid and ethanol. Experiment 4 was set up to study the 
growth kinetics of chain elongators in the presence of acetic acid and H₂ for the 
reduction of volatile fatty acids (VFAs) to respective alcohols.  
==PAGE:32==
32 
 
Experiment 5 was set up to study the growth kinetics of chain elongators in the presence 
of acetic acid, ethanol, and CO₂ for the chain elongation of acetic acid to butyric acid 
(Grimalt-Alemany et al. 2018). Experiments 6, 7, and 8 were set up to separately 
understand the growth kinetics of ethanol oxidation and chain elongation (Quintela, 
Peshkepia, et al. 2024) . More specifically, experiment 6, with N₂/CO₂ and ethanol, was 
set up to study the growth kinetics of microbial groups responsible for both ethanol 
oxidation and chain elongation, whereas Experiment 7, with N₂ and ethanol, was set up 
to study the growth kinetics of microbial groups responsible for ethanol oxidation by 
sulfate reducers and chain elongation by chain elongators. Finally, experiment 8 was 
set up to study the growth kinetics of microbial groups responsible for ethanol oxidation 
by homoacetogens and chain elongation by chain elongators. Anaerobic conditions 
were maintained by continuous CO flushing and gas mixture adjustment (Section 
6.6.2). Gas partial pressures were controlled using a precision pressure indicator 
(CPH6400, WIKA, Germany). Gas samples (100 μL) were collected from the 
headspace for composition analysis, while liquid samples (1.5 mL) were taken to 
monitor pH, optical density, and product concentrations.

6.5 Analytical Methods 
6.5.1 Gas Sampling 
The gas outflow composition was analyzed using a gas chromatograph (8610C, SRI 
Instruments) with a thermal conductivity detector and two packed columns (Molsieve 
13× and silica gel). The machine was heated to 65 °C, flashed with helium, and samples 
were injected after a ramp to 140 °C. A gas-tight syringe (SGE 1 MR-VLL-GT, 
Mikrolab Aarhus A/S) was used to inject 100 μL gas samples, with helium as the carrier 
gas at a flow rate of 50 mL·min−1. The column temperature was initially maintained at 
65 °C for 3 minutes, followed by a ramp of 10 °C·min−1 up to 95 °C. A rotating valve 
then switched the gas flow to the silica gel column, where a faster ramp of 24 °C·min−1 
was applied to reach 140 °C (Tunca et al. 2025). 
Before initiating the gas sampling process, a calibration curve (Figure 6) was 
established using eight vials containing known concentrations of the analytes of interest 
(N₂, H₂, CO, CH₄, and CO₂) under controlled conditions, ensuring accurate 
quantification of the target components. The relative standard deviation of the gas 
measurements was ± 0.5%. The results demonstrated the precision and accuracy of the 
calibration method employed.

7.2 Key Takeaway Observations from Kinetic Experiments:  
The experimental results presented above provide critical insights into the metabolic 
pathways and microbial dynamics involved in converting gaseous and liquid substrates 
into valuable organic acids. 
1. Substrate-Driven Microbial Metabolic Dynamics 
The metabolic activity of microbial consortia varies significantly with the substrate 
type and environmental conditions. Experiments using carbon monoxide (CO) as 
the sole substrate revealed direct conversion of CO to acetic acid, with no hydrogen 
(H₂) production, indicating the absence of the water-gas shift reaction. This process 
highlights a straightforward metabolic pathway, where CO is metabolized into 
acetic acid and carbon dioxide. In contrast, under hydrogen and carbon dioxide 
atmospheres, metabolism was dominated by homoacetogenic pathways, resulting 
in acetic acid as the primary product. Ethanol-based systems exhibited more 
complex metabolic pathways, involving both ethanol oxidation and chain 
elongation, which produced longer-chain fatty acids such as butyrate and caproate. 
2. Overall Observation on Yield and Metabolic Efficiency 
Across most experiments, the yields of products were close to theoretical values, 
indicating efficient substrate conversion under those conditions. However, 
experiments 6, 7, and 8 consistently showed that the experimental caproic acid 
yields were approximately half of the theoretical value. This highlights the 
competition between ethanol oxidation to acetate and chain elongation processes, 
which reduces overall caproic acid production and indicates inefficiencies in 
substrate utilization. Addressing these inefficiencies is vital for enhancing medium-
chain fatty acid yields. 
3. Role of Sulfate and Carbon Dioxide in Metabolic Pathways 
Sulfate availability does not inherently dictate the dominant metabolic pathway but 
instead influences which microbial groups actively participate. Under sulfate-rich 
conditions, ethanol oxidation is primarily facilitated by sulfate-reducing bacteria, 
leading to the formation of acetate as an intermediate product. In contrast, under 
sulfate-free conditions, ethanol oxidation is carried out by homoacetogens, which 
rely on carbon dioxide (CO₂) as an essential electron acceptor to sustain their 
metabolism and utilize acetate to support their growth.  
==PAGE:52==
52 
 
Chain elongation processes, driven by specialized bacteria, remain active regardless 
of sulfate presence, converting acetate and ethanol into longer-chain fatty acids such 
as butyrate and caproate. These findings indicate that while sulfate influences the 
microbial participants in ethanol oxidation, CO₂ plays a critical role in shaping 
metabolic pathways, particularly under sulfate-free conditions, emphasizing the 
adaptability of microbial consortia to varying environmental conditions. 
4. Recommendations for Future Research 
Further investigation is needed into ethanol oxidation kinetics and its competition 
with chain elongation observed in Experiments 6, 7, and 8, as well as other 
experimental conditions, to fully understand and optimize microbial metabolism. 
Future studies must focus on refining medium composition and environmental 
parameters, particularly sulfate and carbon dioxide availability, to enhance 
medium-chain fatty acid production, improve acetic acid yield as the primary 
product, and minimize by-product formation.

7.4 Key Takeaway Observations from Trickle Bed Reactor Experimental Results: 
The following key observations summarize the essential findings from the Trickle Bed 
Reactor experiments: 
1. CO Consumption: The experiments showed consistent and significant CO 
consumption in both reactors. CO uptake was notably influenced by changes in 
tungsten presence and hydraulic retention time (HRT). At an HRT of 3 days, TBR1 
achieved an average CO consumption rate of 85%, while TBR2 exhibited a slightly 
higher rate of 87%. Reducing the HRT to 1.5 days further enhanced substrate 
utilization, with TBR1 reaching 88% CO consumption and TBR2 achieving 90%. 
The consistent reactor performance, evidenced by low variability (<5%), 
demonstrates process reliability.  
2. Acid Production Dynamics: Acetic acid consistently emerged as the dominant 
product throughout the experimental phases. At an HRT of 3 days, the acetic acid 
concentration in TBR1 averaged 6.8 g/L, increasing to 7.5 g/L when the HRT was 
reduced to 1.5 days. Similarly, in TBR2, acetic acid concentrations rose from 7.0 
g/L at 3 days HRT to 7.8 g/L at 1.5 days HRT. Butyric acid, the principal byproduct, 
was minimized under these optimized conditions, decreasing from an average of 
2.3 g/L at 3 days HRT to 1.5 g/L at 1.5 days HRT in both reactors. The balance 
between acetic and butyric acid production underscores the sensitivity of metabolic 
pathways to operational changes, influencing the overall acid profile.

8 Conclusion 
This study investigated the production of acetic acid from CO gas streams in mesophilic 
Trickle Bed Reactors (TBRs), with a focus on optimizing process parameters. 
Experimental results underscored the effectiveness of TBRs in addressing key 
challenges such as low gas-liquid mass transfer rates and inefficient substrate 
utilization. The removal of tungsten from the medium and adjustments to hydraulic 
retention time (HRT) significantly improved acetic acid productivity and selectivity, 
achieving a maximum titer of 10.1 g/L and a selectivity ratio of 7.8 acetic acid/butyric 
acid. These findings highlight the potential of TBR systems to efficiently convert 
syngas into valuable biochemicals. 
The kinetic experiments highlighted key metabolic pathways and substrate utilization 
efficiencies in mesophilic Trickle Bed Reactors (TBRs) for acetic acid production. 
Using carbon monoxide (CO), direct conversion to acetic acid occurred without 
hydrogen production, confirming the absence of the water-gas shift reaction. Ethanol 
as a substrate revealed complex dynamics, including chain elongation producing 
longer-chain fatty acids. Environmental factors like sulfate and carbon dioxide 
availability significantly influenced microbial pathways, underscoring the need to 
optimize conditions to enhance yields and improve selectivity in TBR systems. 
Overall, the highest acetic acid yields were achieved under tungsten-free conditions 
combined with shorter HRT, favoring selective production while limiting byproducts. 
In contrast, tungsten presence and longer HRTs supported chain elongation, producing 
a more diverse metabolite profile. These findings highlight the importance of 
operational parameters to maximize acetic acid production while minimizing by-
products. 
In conclusion, this study demonstrates that mesophilic TBRs are a viable and efficient 
system for acetic acid production from CO gas streams. The findings contribute to the 
advancement of bioprocess technologies and provide a strong foundation for scaling up 
syngas fermentation processes to meet industrial demands for sustainable chemical 
production.
