Summary
Differential privacy is a tool for answering queries about a data set without disclosing
an individual’s participation in the set. This is done by using randomized algorithms,
which have similar output distributions if the data sets differ in a single entry. This
thesis contains a survey of results for introducing differential privacy to important
graph and social network statistics.
The main part of the thesis investigates the
feasibility of designing differentially private recommendation systems for online dating.
The goal is to use differential privacy to hide connections between people. Specifically,
three recommendation systems from the literature, which do not take privacy into
account, are studied in the context of differential privacy.
Our results vary from
giving a good accuracy and privacy tradeoff for one of the systems, to an inefficient
differentially private algorithm for another, to showing that a good accuracy and
privacy tradeoff is impossible for the third.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Differential privacy was developed by Dwork et al. [Dwo+06a].
It is a technique
that aims at providing mathematically rigorous privacy guarantees, independent of
an adversary’s background knowledge. The idea is to use randomized algorithms in
such a way that the output distributions are similar if the data sets differ in a single
entry. The first applications focused on tabular data. In such a data set, an entry
is a row of possibly sensitive information about one person. For graphs and social
networks, there are different interpretations, depending on what kind of information
should be protected: protecting one person’s data would correspond to protecting all
their relations, i.e. all edges connected to one node. This is called node-differential
privacy and was defined by Hay et al. [Hay+09]. In some applications, a weaker defi-
nition is sufficient. For example, if one would like to analyze the email correspondence
within a company, where the list of employees is public, it makes sense to protect
individual correspondences. This corresponds to protecting whether a single edge is
present or not and was introduced as edge-differential privacy by Hay et al. [Hay+09].
In Chapter 2 of this thesis, we will survey results about important social network
statistics
in
the
context

differential
privacy,
and

by
Nissim et al. [NRS07] to cover the mean local clustering coefficient (I later found
out that something similar has been shown by Wang et al. [Wan+13], see the discus-
sion in Section 2.4).
The main goal of this thesis is to work with a subgroup of social networks, examine
which privacy guarantees are desirable and practically feasible, and develop algo-
rithms with provable privacy guarantees. Specifically, the social networks considered
are the interaction graphs of online dating platforms. The aim is to develop differen-
tially private recommendation systems for online dating, by investigating if existing
state of the art algorithms can be modified to satisfy differential privacy, and if yes,
provide explicit algorithms and establish privacy and accuracy guarantees.
Recommendation systems for other applications have been researched in the context
of differential privacy before.
For example, McSherry and Mironov [MM09] gave
differentially private versions of some standard movie recommendation systems. On
the other hand, Machanavajjhala et al. [MKS11] showed that certain friend recom-
mendation systems in social networks make it hard to achieve differential privacy.

As argued in Pizzato et al. [Piz+13], recommendation systems for online dating can
differ from those in fundamental ways. Unlike movie recommendations, for dating, it
is important to take the preferences of both sides into account. Unlike usual social
networks, recommendations based on common connections do not make sense in the

In Chapter 3 we will investigate three specific recommendation systems. For Social

intuitive, simple solution fails to give a good accuracy and privacy trade-off, and give
an inefficient algorithm which promises a good trade-off.

2.1
Differential Privacy Introduction
Differential privacy is a technique for providing privacy guarantees for answering
queries on a sensitive data set and was first introduced by Dwork et al. [Dwo+06a].
The goal is to protect an individual’s data - or an individual’s presence - in the
data set. For that to be ensured, we want the answer to the query to not depend
much on any individual’s data, that is, the answer should be similar independent of
whether the individual is in the data set or not. To formalize this, we first define
two neighbouring data sets. All of the following definitions are due to Dwork and
McSherry [Dwo+06a].
Definition 2.1. Let D be a universe of data sets. Two data sets x ∈D and y ∈D
are called neighbouring if they differ in at most one entry (what this means exactly
will depend on D). We will write x ∼y.
Now, what do we mean by “the answer should be similar”? Formally, we will have a
randomized mechanism for approximately answering the given query, and the output
distributions for this mechanism should be close for neighbouring datasets, in the
following way:
Definition 2.2. A mechanism M is called ϵ-differentially private if for every S in
the range of M and two neighbouring data sets x and y it holds that
P(M (x) ∈S)
P(M (y) ∈S) ≤exp (ϵ) .
(2.1)
The minimum parameter ϵ for which equation (2.1) is true is often referred to as the
privacy loss. In some applications, this definition is too strict, and it makes sense

In this work, we will explore differential privacy in graphs.
In this setting, it is
not clear what we mean by neighbouring data sets - and it will in fact depend on the
application. Imagine, for example, a social network. Intuitively, protecting a person’s
data would correspond to hiding the presence of a node in the network, together with
all its links. The formal definition of this kind of privacy is node-differential privacy
and was first introduced by Hay et al. [Hay+09].
Definition 2.5. Let D be a set of graphs on a vertex set V with |V | = n.
A
mechanism is node-differentially private, if it is differentially private over D, when
for x = Gx = (V, Ex) ∈D and y = Gy = (V, Ey) ∈D the neighbouring relation x ∼y
means there exists a node v ∈V such that Gx\{v} = Gy\{v}. That is, the only
difference between the graphs are edges adjacent to v.
As we will see later, node-differential privacy is often very hard to achieve. Consider
again the example of a social network. There are users which have a comparatively
large number of links in the network, and as such will have a strong influence on certain
queries, for example triangle counts, or degree distribution. There are applications,
though, where hiding the presence of a person in the data set is not that important,
and it is more important to protect privacy in the sense of not leaking if two people
connect on the network. For example, a person’s profile might be public - but they
might have some sensitive connections they want to keep private. This is formalized
by the definition of edge-differential privacy, which is also due to Hay et al. [Hay+09].
Definition 2.6. Let D be a set of graphs on a vertex set V with |V | = n.
A
mechanism is edge-differentially private, if it is differentially private over D, when
for x = Gx = (V, Ex) ∈D and y = Gy = (V, Ey) ∈D the neighbouring relation x ∼y
means there exists an edge e such that Ex = Ey\{e} or Ey = Ex\{e}.
Remark 2.1. Note that node-differential privacy is a strictly stronger notion than
edge-differential privacy, since with node privacy we protect the presence of a node
and all its adjacent edges.

2.2
Edge-Differential Privacy
We begin by considering edge-differential privacy, because as mentioned above, it is
the weaker definition. We will look at some basic graph queries and their sensitivities,
and summarize some results from the literature.
Some statistics have very low edge sensitivity. Thus, edge-differential privacy is easy
to achieve for those queries by using the Laplace mechanism from Fact 2.2. For ex-
ample, edge count (i.e. outputting the number of edges in a graph) has a sensitivity
of 1. As such, we can add Laplace noise scaled with 1/ϵ to guarantee differential
privacy. This is independent of the number of nodes or edges, which means for big
graphs, the noise will be relatively small.
Similarly, consider the degree distribution of a graph G. That is, a vector which for
each possible degree d contains the number of vertices of degree d. Note that chang-
ing an edge can change the size of the vector. To still output a differentially private
degree distribution, one method is to first calculate a differentially private degree
sequence, which is just a list of the degree of each node. This query has a sensitivity
of 2: removing or adding an edge to the graph can change at most two values by at
most 1. Using the Laplace mechanism, we add noise scaled with 2/ϵ to each entry of
the degree sequence. Hay et al. [Hay+10] showed that the mean squared error of this
simple Laplace mechanism scales linearly with the number of nodes. They propose a
solution based on finding the closest vector to the noisy sequence satisfying certain
constraints. They show that the mean squared error of this solution scales linearly
with the number of different degrees in the graph times polylog of the number of
nodes. They then use both of those solutions to calculate the noisy degree distribu-
tion, and compare the results.
Unfortunately, there are interesting queries where even edge sensitivity is not con-
stant in the size of the graph. For example, in network analysis, important statistics
are based on triangle counts. A triangle is a subgraph consisting of three vertices,
where any two of them are connected by an edge. Task and Clifton [TC14] state that
the edge sensitivity of triangle count scales linearly in n, where n is the number of
nodes in the graph. We show this by a simple example (see Figure 2.1 (a)): Imagine
a graph with nodes v1 and v2 and n −2 other nodes. There is an edge from v1 to
every node except v2, and an edge from v2 to every node except v1. Adding the

These examples show that for general graphs node-differential privacy is often hard to
achieve, and easy strategies such as the Laplace mechanism fail. Thus, more elaborate
solutions for node-differential privacy have been studied. One idea is to provide accu-
racy guarantees only for certain classes of graphs, for example graphs with bounded
degree or scale-free graphs, while satisfying privacy guarantees on the entire universe.
We will now summarize the results in this area, which are relatively few and new.

degree bounded graphs and get both edge- and node-differentially private algorithms
for subgraph count and local profile queries. However, they only give error bounds
for graphs with bounded degree.

This chapter contains the main contribution in this work. We will consider differen-
tial privacy specifically for online dating recommendation systems. To my knowledge,
this has not been studied before.
By a recommendation system, in general form, we mean any mechanism that uses
data from an online dating platform, and gives a (potentially ranked) list of users
as recommended connections to one specified active user. Often, the space of poten-
tial candidates for recommended connections is not the entire data base, but some
restricted subset based on for example location, gender, age, etc.
The choices for recommendation systems considered in this work are mostly influenced
by an extensive review by Pizzato et al. [Piz+13]. Additionally to summarizing and
categorizing existing strategies, they point out two specific characteristics which show
improved success rate of recommendations:
• Recommendation systems which are based on implicit preferences (i.e. based on
existing matches and past behaviour on the platform) are more effective than
explicit preferences (i.e. preferences the user states).
• Recommendation systems which take preferences of both the active and the rec-
ommended user into account are more effective than just considering preferences
of the active user.
Specifically, all recommendation systems we consider will be based on some kind of
graph structure. For the first two, Social Collab by Cai et al. [Cai+10] and RECON
by Pizatto et al. [Piz+11], the graph is given by a set of nodes representing users, and
a set of directed edges, representing some indication of “like”: that is, an actual like,
a sent message, a reply to a message, or similar. Additionally, for RECON there is a
==PAGE:22==
16
3 Recommendation Systems for Online Dating
public profile showing certain attributes of each user.
To talk about differential privacy, we first have to think about what kind of informa-
tion makes sense to protect with respect to this application. One might think hiding
someone’s presence in the data set is important - which would be equivalent to pre-
serving node-differential privacy. But usually, anyone who is signed up for a dating
platform can potentially see anyone else’s profile. Also, how could one recommend a
user to another while hiding the first user’s presence?
As such, it makes more sense to protect individual likes. This means when we are
recommending user r to user a, a should not be able to uncover a specific connection
which r or any other user on the platform made. On the other hand, a knows who a
liked before, so there is no need to protect this information. We assume that the
recommendation is only disclosed to user a.
Formally, for a fixed active user a, we define two data sets x = (V, Ex) and y = (V, Ey)
with a ∈V as neighbouring, x ∼y, if there exists an edge e ̸= (a, v) for any v ∈V
such that Ex\{e} = Ey\{e}. This means two neighbouring data sets differ in an edge
that is not an outgoing edge of a, because those are the edges we want to keep private.
We will use differential privacy based on this definition of neighbouring data sets for
Social Collab and RECON.
The last part of this chapter investigates stochastic matching, which is used by for
example by Chen et al. [Che+09] and Pizatto and Silvestrini [PS11] for their recom-
mendation systems. The data set is modelled as a graph with undirected, weighted
edges. Again, the nodes correspond to users, and the weight of an edge is, depending
on the exact system, an estimate for the probability of a successful match between
the users.
The results are quite variying: For Social Collab, we prove that differential privacy is
not achievable without destroying all information. For RECON we give an efficient
differentially private modification with an explicit upper bound for the added noise.
For the stochastic matching problem, we show that a very naive intuitive algorithm
will not work, but give an inefficient algorithm as a proof of concept with privacy and
accuracy guarantees.

3.3
Stochastic Matching
Next, we will look at stochastic matching, which is not a recommendation system
itself, but is used as a basis for some of them. The recommendation systems con-
sidered in this section are based on a slightly different concept than the previous
ones. Instead of singling out one active user and ranking recommendations for this
user, the idea here is to always recommend user b to user a exactly when we also
recommend user a to user b. This also means that instead of outputting a sorted list
of recommendations for one user, we will output a list of total recommendations, and
the goal is to have the same number of recommendations for each user. The motive of
this strategy is, as argued by Pizzato and Silvestrini [Piz+13], to ensure that there is
no difference made between popular and unpopular users. Receiving either too many
or too few messages can cause frustration in a user.
Both the recommendation systems by Pizzato and Silvestrini [Piz+13] and
Chen et al. [Che+09] model the problem as a weighted, undirected graph G = (V, E).
The vertices in V represent the users, and for each edge e = (u, v), the edge weight 0 ≤
w(e) ≤1 is an estimate for the probability of a successful match between u and v. A
==PAGE:30==
24
3 Recommendation Systems for Online Dating
successful match is defined differently in the two papers, but the common ground is
that the recommendation leads to some sort of positive interaction between the users.
For simplicity we assume E = V × V - this could mean that a lot of edge weights
are very low or zero. The methods used to estimate the edge weights differ for each
system and will not be further discussed here.
The weighted matching problem is now defined as follows: given a weighted, undirected
graph G = (V, E) with non-negative weight function w and a given number N ≥1,
the goal is to find a subset M of edges such that
1. any vertex is adjacent to at most N edges in M and
2. the value w(M) := ∑
e∈M w(e) is maximized with respect to all subsets M
satisfying condition 1.
We will call any subset of edges satisfying condition 1 a matching on G. Furthermore,
we will restrict ourselves to the simplified case where N = 1.
In Pizzato and Silvestrini [Piz+13] and Chen et al. [Che+09] the stochastic matching
problem is formulated slightly differently: given the probabilities of any successful
match, the goal is to maximize the expected number of successful matches, under the
condition that each user receives at most N recommendations. Note that since the
edge weights represent the probabilities of any match being successful, the problem
is equivalent to the one formulated above.
There exist exact polynomial time and more efficient approximate algorithms for solv-
ing the problem above. We will list a few. A simple greedy 1/2-approximation picks,
in every step, the heaviest edge, adds it to the matching, then removes all neigh-
bouring edges, and repeats, until the graph is empty. See for example the survey by
Avis [Avi83]. The so-called Hungarian Algorithm by Kuhn [Kuh55] solves the prob-
lem exactly for bipartite graphs. Edmond’s algorithm [Edm65] solves the weighted
matching problem exactly for general graphs.
The differential privacy model we consider in this section is the following, which is
very close to edge-differential privacy. We define a data set as the tuple containing
the graph and the weight function: x = (Gx, wx). We will omit the subscript if the
data set is clear from context. Two data sets x and y are neighbouring if they have
the same graph, but differ in the weight of a single edge.
This is basically edge-
differential privacy, since we assume we always have a complete graph, so deleting an
edge corresponds to setting its weight to zero.
Note that under this definition of privacy, it is easy to find a differentially private
approximation of the value of the maximum matching: changing the weight of one
edge can change the value of any matching by at most 1. Thus, the maximum match-
ing value can also change by at most one. As such the Laplace mechanism can be

A very natural idea to preserve privacy in this setting is to add noise to the weight
of every edge, then find a maximum matching by any maximum matching algorithm;
see Algorithm 2. For simplicity, we make sure to get a maximal matching, that is,
a matching that cannot be extended. Since all edge weights are non-negative, every
maximum matching can be extended to a maximal matching. But how to choose
the noise level? The weight function has sensitivity of 1 - as such, adding Laplace
noise scaled with 1/ϵ preserves differential privacy. However, this means that the
total noise for any matching grows significantly in the number of nodes, since for any
matching the independent edge noise adds up.

The next question is whether a sensible privacy accuracy trade-off is even achievable.
The answer is yes - but at huge computational cost. The question if there is an effi-

The idea of the following algorithm is based on the Report Noisy Max algorithm
in [D+14], Chapter 3. The problem there is, given a data set and a list of counts of
certain attributes (e.g. how many people have had which out of a list of sicknesses), to
output the index of the maximum count (e.g. most common sickness). The problem

Our problem is similar: given the weights of all possible matchings, changing one
edge can change the weight of multiple matchings, but the weight of any matching by

algorithm, see Algorithm 3. Here, we compute the value of every possible matching,

as for Report Noisy Max for privacy, and prove accuracy guarantees after.

computationally unfeasible, so the point of this result is only to show that it is not
hopeless to try to find a good privacy accuracy trade-off.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
Differential privacy is in general difficult to achieve if relations between people play
an important role. This is especially true for node-differential privacy in graphs and
social networks.
We have considered edge-differential privacy for online dating recommendation sys-
tems, protecting interactions between people, rather than their presence in the data
set. In this work, we concentrated on recommender systems which differ from f.e.
movie recommender systems by the concept of reciprocability, that is, taking the pref-
erences of both the active user and the possible recommendation into account. On
the other hand, the recommendation system used by Brožovský and Petříček [BP07]
takes only the preference of the active user seeking recommendations into account. I
assume that differentially privacy can be achieved there similarly to the movie rec-
ommender systems considered by McSherry and Mironov [MM09], since they are all
based on computing correlation coefficients between the preferences of users.
We have seen that for Social Collab, one edge can influence the outcome of the rec-
ommendation system too much, thus making a good accuracy and privacy trade-off
impossible. The problem with Social Collab is the “all-or-nothing” property, so one
edge can change a recommendation rank from being very high, to not even being
considered at all. An interesting direction to explore here would be to, instead of
defining similarity in attractiveness and taste by discrete “yes” or “no” values, to in-
stead look at continuous measures, similar to the correlation coefficient approach in
Brožovský and Petříček [BP07], but combining the preference correlations for active
and recommended user.
While the theoretical results for RECON seem promising, to actually explore its
practical feasibility and the trade-off for choosing the degree threshold it would be
necessary and interesting to test on real data.
For stochastic matching, the result in this work shows a proof of concept - a reason-
able privacy accuracy trade-off is possible, but at huge computational cost. There
are several directions one could try to go in order to find more efficient algorithms.
One approach could be to modify the Noisy Max Matching to doing some sort of
randomized local search instead of taking all possible matchings into account. The

have to be the same to achieve ϵ-differential privacy. However, it would be interesting

Another idea would be to include random choices within well-established approx-

Lastly, this work focuses on differential privacy only. There are other privacy def-

example k-anonymity and various randomization strategies. The survey by Casas-
Roma et al. [CHT17] specializes on anonymization techniques that allow to publish
a modified version of the entire network. For recommendation systems, differential
privacy seems a good fit, since we do not wish to publish the entire data, but make
recommendations to individuals. As such one can clearly define which information
will be protected and from whom.
