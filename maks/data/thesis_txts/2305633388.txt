Abstract
Resent investigations have showed that elemental carbon (EC) that is a part of particulate matter (PM),
might pose a greater health risk than PM in general. Because EC is by EU limited as PM the adverse
health eﬀects from EC might be bigger than previously expected. The question asked in this thesis is
whether the EC concentration in a suburb, where wood combustion is used as a supplement for heating,
is higher than the background concentration.
Quarts ﬁlters exposed to ambient air in Hvidovre through 2013 to 2015 have been analysed for the amount
of organic carbon (OC) and EC with a thermal optical analyser (OCEC analyser). A total amount of 264
ﬁlters have been analysed covering both the fall/winter and summer periods. The EC concentrations
are compared to other EC measurements from a street in central Copenhagen with dense traﬃc and
measurements from a rural background in Risø, as well as correlated with meteorological parameters
(temperature, wind speed and wind direction) and concentrations of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons
(PAHs).
The EC concentration during the winter period was found to be 0.77 µg/m3 and during the summer period
0.31 µg/m3. This is lower than the busy street in Copenhagen, but higher than the rural background
in Risø. Comparing the diﬀerence between winter and summer EC concentrations among the sites lead
to an estimate of a local wood combustion contribution in Hvidovre of 0.19 µg/m3. The assumption
is that there is no wood combustion during the summer and that the EC emissions from traﬃc in
the area is constant during the year. Furthermore, the investigations showed a winter background EC
concentration of 0.28 µg/m3 for Copenhagen and northern Zeeland where the main source is expected
to be wood combustion. A very signiﬁcant correlation was found between certain PAHs and the EC
concentration. A less signiﬁcant correlation between EC and wind speed was found while there were no
correlation between EC and wind direction or temperature.
Based on the measured EC concentrations, the wood combustion load in Hvidovre is larger than the
regional background wood combustion winter contribution. This study, therefore, shows that Hvidovre
is a wood combustion hot spot, and possible initiatives to reduce EC emissions could have a positive
eﬀect on the health of the residents.

Resum´e
Nye studier har vist at elementært kulstof (EC), der er en del af luftb˚arne partikler (PM), muligvis
udgør en større risiko for helbredet end PM generelt. Fordi EC reguleres som PM af EU, vil helbreds
eﬀekterne fra EC muligvis være større end hidtil forventet.
Dette speciale forsøger at svare p˚a om
EC koncentrationen er højere i en provins til København, hvor brænde bruges som et supplement til
boligopvarmning, end den regionale baggrundskoncentration.
Kvartsﬁltre der har været udsat for atmosfærisk luft fra Hvidovre m˚alt gennem 2013 til 2015 er blevet
analyseret for mængden af organisk kulstof (OC) og EC med et termisk optisk m˚aleinstrument (OCEC
instrument).
264 ﬁltre er blevet analyseret og de stammer fra b˚ade efter˚ar/vinter perioder og som-
mer perioder. Den m˚alte EC koncentration sammenlignes med andre EC koncentrationer fra en gade i
centrum af København med tæt traﬁk og med en landlig baggrund i Risø, samt korreleres med meteorolo-
giske paramtre (temperatur, vindhastighed og retning) og koncentration af polyaromatiske hydrocarboner
(PAHer).
EC koncentrationen blev i gennemsnit om vinteren m˚alt til 0.77 µg/m3 og om sommeren til 0.31 µg/m3.
Det er lavere end en tæt traﬁkeret gade i København og højere end en landlig baggrund i Risø. Ved
sammenligning mellem sommer og vinter EC koncentrationer, mellem de tre m˚alestationer, kan det
lokale bidrag fra brænderøg i Hvidovre estimeres til 0.19 µg/m3, under antagelserne af, at der ingen
brænderøg er om sommeren og at EC udledningen fra traﬁkken er ens sommer og vinter. Derudover
viser undersøgelsen en vinter baggrunds EC koncentrationen p˚a 0.28 µg/m3 gældende for København
og den nordlige del af Sjælland, hvor kilden forventes at være afbrænding af træ. En meget signiﬁkant
korrelation blev fundet mellem EC og speciﬁkke PAHer, mens en svagere blev identiﬁceret mellem EC
og vindhastighed og ingen korrelation mellem EC og temperatur og EC og vindretning.
Baseret p˚a den fundne EC koncentration er bidraget fra brænderøg højere i Hvidovre end i landlig
baggrund. Dette speciale viser derfor, at Hvidovre er en brænderøgs hot spot hvor mulige tiltag til at
reducere EC udledningen højst sandsynligt vil have en positiv helbredseﬀekt p˚a omr˚adets beboere.

1 Introduction
The World Health Organisation (WHO) estimates that ambient air pollution with particles cause 3.7
million deaths per year world wide. Most of the these deaths are in developing countries, but in the
high-income part of Europe 280,000 deaths per year is expected to be from particle pollution (WHO,
2014b). In Denmark, Brandt et al. (2013) estimates that long term exposure to air pollution caused 3300
premature deaths and around 3.3 million sick days in 2011, and that the expenses amounts to around
30 billion dkk.
Air pollution with particles arise from a vast variety of sources and can’t be eliminated because of
natural emissions. Some anthropogenic particles are generated from diﬀerent types of combustion of
both biomass and fossil fuels. In the atmosphere these particles form a mixture of liquids and solids
and are commonly denoted particulate matter (PM). PM is, therefore, a complex mixture of compounds
with very diﬀerent origin and very diﬀerent chemical and physical properties. Some have adverse eﬀects
on health, some on climate and some on both (Seinfeld and Pandis, 2006, ch. 2). PM from combustion
contains soot that is mostly carbon with small amounts of oxygen and hydrogen (U.S. EPA, 2012).
To estimate the concentration of soot either elemental carbon (EC) or black carbon (BC) is measured,
where the choice depends on the analysis technique selected.
EC and BC are almost identical and
is made up of a carbon in a organised graphite structure (Ogren and Charlson, 1983). Soot darkens
surfaces and belongs, primary, to the group of ﬁne particles (PM2.5, particles with a diameter less than
2.5 µm) that are capable of penetrating into the lungs and alveolus causing airway diseases, cancer and
cardiovascular problems, such as heart attacks, and in general increasing the amount of sick days and the
cases of asthma and bronchitis (Naeher et al., 2007; Brandt et al., 2013). The eﬀect on climate includes
scattering and absorption of radiation, interaction with clouds and according to Boucher et al. (2013)
the climate eﬀect from particles remain the most uncertain in the climate change estimates. Boucher
et al. (2013) concludes that BC exerts an overall warming eﬀect only second to CO2.
Another compound with high interest in relation to health is the Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbon
(PAH) benzo[a]pyrene (B[a]P) that is created together with EC during combustion processes. Among
others of the PAHs, B[a]P is carcinogenic and belongs to the class of bioaccumulate and persistent
compounds and is therefore under monitoring required by EU (EU, 2004).

2 Ambient particles and wood smoke
This chapter starts by introducing ambient particles and the typical characteristics, and continues with
a more detailed explanation regarding wood smoke, including sources and the impacts on health and
climate. Following is a short introduction to some key meteorological phenomena with inﬂuence on air
pollution.
2.1
Particles
Particles in the atmosphere, i.e. ambient particles, is a complex mixture of liquids and solids. Particulate
matter (PM) in the atmosphere denotes species with a suﬃciently low vapour pressure in order to keep
the matter predominantly in the particle phase.
An urban polluted air mass often contains 105 to
4 × 106 particles/cm3 compared to rural number concentrations of 2000 to 10 000 particles/cm3 (Seinfeld
and Pandis, 2006, ch. 8). Suspended particles are often termed aerosols, even though by deﬁnition an
aerosol is a complex mixture of gasses, volatile compounds and particles (Seinfeld and Pandis, 2006,
ch. 2). PM has both anthropogenic and natural sources, ranging from combustion and tire wear to
volcanoes and pollen, but after emission the particles can be altered in the atmosphere. This creates
particles that consists of various compounds with diﬀerent chemical and physical properties, making a
strict classiﬁcation of properties and harmful eﬀects hard. These issues will be addressed in the following
(Seinfeld and Pandis, 2006, ch. 2).
2.1.1
Physical properties
Particles are often separated based on diameter, where PM is deﬁned as the total mass of particles with
a diameter less than Xµm. Typically, PM10, PM2.5 and PM0.1 is used to separate the particles into
the coarse, ﬁne and ultraﬁne group, respectively. To account for the wide range in masses and sizes
of particles, two distributions are often used; a number and a mass distribution. An example of the
diﬀerence in mass and size distribution for an urban airmass is shown on ﬁgure 2.1. Measuring by mass
(black line on ﬁgure 2.1) ultraﬁne particles are invisible compared to ﬁne and coarse, but counting by
numbers (grey line on ﬁgure 2.1) the coarse particles are invisible.
Ultraﬁne particles are emitted from combustion processes (traﬃc, wood burning and etc.) or created by
nucleation from precursor gases. Ultraﬁne particles have a tendency to accumulate in the 0.01 to 1 µm
or 2.5 µm ﬁne particle range, also called the accumulation, mode due to rapid growth by condensation

Denmark is made up of this long range contribution and of natural sources, and is the concentration

2.1.4
Impacts on health
There are two major paths by which how PM can aﬀect the health. The ﬁrst being the direct eﬀect
of the aerosols and the second being the possible toxic compounds that they carry. That the particles
undergo chemical transformation in the atmosphere complicates the matter as the compounds change.
The size and shape changes and as the amount of water changes the way the particles interact with the
human body changes.
Coarse particles lager than 4 to 5 µm are in general stopped in the nose and throat and does therefore not
reach the lungs. Smaller particles are able to penetrate into the lungs, some are small enough to enter the
pulmonary alveoli and the ultraﬁne can excess the blood circulation system (Palmgren, 2009). Particles
interact with our body by causing inﬂammation and oxidative stress. Inﬂammations are observed both in
the lungs and the tissue around, as well as causing infections in the veins. Oxidative stress is an excess of
free radicals compared to the amount of antioxidants that are available to neutralise them. These extra
free radicals react with cells causing damage and leading to neurodegenerative diseases (e.g. Parkins’s
etc.), gene mutations (e.g. cancer etc.) and blood vessel disorders among others (Mandal, 2015; Davies,
2000).

2.1.5
Impacts on climate
Particles aﬀect the climate in diﬀerent ways, but according to Boucher et al. (2013) they are still the
most uncertain calculation in the Earth’s energy budget. The interaction between PM and the climate is
complex and involves PM as cloud condensation and ice nuclei in cloud formation as well as their ability
to scatter sunlight and change the Earth’s albedo.
PM act as nuclei where liquid or frozen cloud droplets can form, however, the eﬀects are not straight
forward. Liquid clouds tend to have smaller, but an increased number of, droplets in areas with more
PM and thereby reﬂecting more sunlight. Among others, PM also aﬀects the partition between ice and
water in mixed clouds. In general the eﬀect of PM on clouds is expected to be net cooling.
Reﬂection of sunlight by PM causes an increase in Earth’s albedo and tends to cool the planet, while
absorbing has a warming tendency (Boucher et al., 2013). One of the main uncertainties regarding the
absorbing eﬀect is the absorbing ability of black carbon (BC), however the general agreement is that PM
results in a net cooling of the atmosphere (Boucher et al., 2013). BC is a form of carbon that absorbs
solar radiation at all wavelengths, and is therefore deﬁned by its light-absorbing properties (U.S. EPA,
2012). BC and EC are not a 100% equivalent, but for most applications the two can be assumed to
be identical. This topic will be discussed later in section 2.2.2 and section 3.1. PM in general causes
a cooling, that is expected to be larger than the warming eﬀect from BC. According to Boucher et al.
(2013)) there is a high conﬁdence that the warming eﬀects caused by green house gasses (GHG) to some
extent have been masked by the cooling from PM.
The radiation eﬀects depend partly on the diameter of the particles and particles in the accumulation
mode are eﬃcient scatterers of solar radiation. Another eﬀect of aerosol scattering is a decreased visibility,
causing haze or even smog given the right meteorological conditions and sunlight (Jacob, 1999).
In polar regions aerosol deposition on snow and ice are of high importance because BC deposition will
decrease the surface albedo. Decreasing the albedo of snow causes more heat to be absorbed leading to
more melting. Boucher et al. (2013) estimates that the radiative forcing (RF) from BC on ice has a 2 to
4 times larger global mean surface temperature change per unit forcing than a change in CO2.
The diﬀerence between many GHGs and PM is the temporal and spatial variability of PM, and therefore
the eﬀects of PM are often of more local nature, (U.S. EPA, 2012). There are indications that local
heating or cooling can drive changes in the weather patterns causing for instance a change in precipitation
(Boucher et al., 2013).
As mentioned, the eﬀect of PM on climate is with medium conﬁdence a net cooling with a (RF) including
interactions with clouds of −1.9 to −0.1 W/m3 compared to RF from CO2 of 1.46 to 2.18 W/m3 (IPCC,
2013).

atmosphere EC molecules can be coated with diﬀerent substance or diﬀerent substance can be absorbed

Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons
The group of chemicals called PAH is formed during incomplete combustion of organic matter such as
coal, oil and wood and therefore both traﬃc and wood combustions are sources of PAHs. PAHs are made
up of two or more benzene rings that is fused together and contains only hydrogen and carbon. When
emitted, PAHs often have a diameter of 0.01 to 0.5 µm, but at a distance from the source a peak occurs
in the size distribution at a diameter of 0.5 to 1.0 µm, but therefore PAHs are most often contained
in PM2.5 (Seinfeld and Pandis, 2006). The smallest molecules are mainly gases, but as they increase
the number of benzene rings, they also decrease their vapour pressure and hence prefer a solid form
under normal atmospheric conditions. PAHs are created during combustion and the type depends on
the temperature in the burning appliance; high temperatures promote formation of complex and long
PAH particles (McDonald et al., 2000). When using a stove or a ﬁreplace the burn cycles consist of
diﬀerent phases with diﬀerent temperatures and is therefore characterised with diﬀerent emissions of
PAHs. When in the atmosphere PAHs are able to undergo surface reactions with other constituents such
as ozone, sunlight, nitrogen oxides and SO2 to create other, possible, toxic substances (Fenger and Tjell,
2009).

Potassium
Potassium (K) is naturally present in food and nature and is a nutrient needed by humans for instance
to regulate blood pressure and ﬂuid balance. A lack of potassium increase the risk of high blood pressure
and strokes. Normally excess potassium is removed by the kidneys in the human body, however a too high
potassium concentration can cause breathing problems, nausea, vomiting, irregular or skipped heartbeats
or in severe cases heart failure. Potassium is essential for plants and soils and is needed in rather large
amounts during plant growth as it controls cell size and osmotic pressure. Too low potassium levels
causes restricted growth, lower yields and harvest quality (Lenntech, 2016).

2.2.4
Impacts on health
The emission of wood smoke is highest during the wood combustion season and close to the source, at
least for countries that are not normally prone to have large nature ﬁres during summertime. There are
no guidelines or limits for wood smoke concentrations, but it is regulated as PM2.5 and the individual
chemicals, and in many countries wood smoke contribute signiﬁcantly to PM2.5 concentrations (Naeher
et al., 2007). In Europe on a yearly basis 10 to 30 % or 1 to 4 µg/m3 of the PM2.5 concentration is from
biomass combustion (Sigsgaard et al., 2015).
In the developed world, studies show that stoves and ﬁreplaces not only increase PM2.5 concentration
outside, but that the indoor air is also polluted.
Indoor biomass burning is also a problem in the
developing world, where wood and other biomass is used for both cooking and heating. A measuring
campaign in Denmark has found indoor soot concentrations higher than outdoor when using a stove,
and that it took 6 hours for the indoor concentration to fall to the outdoor level (Olesen et al., 2010).
The measured PM2.5 concentration indoor was 5 to 55 µg/m3, with the lowest values in a uninhabited
house.
From the same measurement campaign the average outside concentrations was measured to
be 17.6 µg/m3 and 20.8 µg/m3, however the measured PM2.5 concentration is not produced by wood
combustion alone, due to a contribution from long range transport and traﬃc. Another study also found
elevated soot concentration inside a house using a stove and that the soot emissions was highest during
the ignition in a cold oven and that the responding concentration often was higher than outdoor (Glasius
et al., 2007).

2.3
Meteorology and local eﬀects
PM is emitted and suspended in the air and is therefore aﬀected by the weather patterns, both on syn-
optic, meso- and microscale. The three phenomena act on diﬀerent timescales and with diﬀerent spatial
resolution; micrometeorology operates in minutes to hours and spatial scale of up to 1km, mesoscale
meteorology in hours to days and with a range up to hundreds of kilometres and synopticmeteorology in
days to weeks covering ranges of thousands of kilometres (Arya, 2001;Seinfeld and Pandis, 2006, ch. 1).
In this section a few phenomena that greatly eﬀects the concentrations of air pollutants will be explained.
The lowest part of the atmosphere is the planetary boundary layer (PBL) or atmospheric boundary layer
(ABL), and it stretches from the surface up to 1 to 2 km height, depending on the atmospheric stability
(Sorbjan, 2003; Arya, 2001). The PBL is the zone between the surface and the free troposphere where
heat and material is exchanged between the Earth and the atmosphere. The mixing capacity of the PBL
is to a large extent governed by the eﬃciency of turbulence. Turbulence is irregular or random motions
that rotates, is diﬀusive, dissipates and have more scales of motion. Turbulence is therefore responsible
for much of the transfer of heat and moisture between the atmosphere and the surface and the diﬀusion
and mixing of pollutants emitted near the surface. Turbulence needs energy and therefore the turbulence
often has a diurnal variation (Arya, 2001).
Daily variations
The daily variation of the PBL is most pronounced on days with clear skies as the variation is created by
diﬀerence in incoming solar radiation (Arya, 2001). After sunrise the PBL starts rising due to heating
of the land surface, which causes increasing thermal mixing. In the late afternoon the height of the PBL
can reach 1 to 5 km before it starts decreasing. Just before sunrise the PBL can be as low as 20 to 500 m
(Arya, 2001). In terms of stability the lowest part of the atmosphere is, during the night, often stable.
A stable atmosphere dampens vertical motions thereby reducing vertical mixing. In the day time the
more common situation is an unstable atmosphere that enhances vertical motion.
In general the PBL is aﬀected by the synoptic situation, and, under inﬂuence of a high pressure, the
height of the PBL is often lower due to subsidence than compared to days with passing fronts or deep
convective systems. This subsidence and low PBL height often causes high pollution events during high
pressures (Arya, 2001).

Inversion
In most of the troposphere the temperature decreases with altitude. When the opposite is the case
an inversion has formed, i.e. a layer where the temperature increases with height. Diﬀerent types of
inversions exist, and especially the surface inversion is able to trap air pollutions relatively close to
the surface. A surface inversion often forms during night when the earth surface cools faster then the
atmosphere and thereby cools the lowest part of the PBL, as a part of the diurnal variation (Arya,
2001). Due to the temperature proﬁle the layer is very stable and vertical movements are dampened and
suppressed and the air, including possible air pollution, is trapped (Arya, 2001).
Urban boundary layer
Compared to rural areas, large cities are areas with increased surface roughness and an increased tem-
perature because of increased human activity. The latter eﬀect is known as the urban heat island (UHI).
According to B¨uhler et al. (2010) industrial and other areas with a high amount of buildings can have a
signiﬁcant higher temperature during summer than areas with a lower construction intensity and even
lower temperature are found in green parks and forests. An analysis in UK by Kershaw et al. (2010)
show that the UHI eﬀects exist both during winter and summer with an annual temperature diﬀerence
between rural and urban areas between 0.1 and 1.9 °C. Furthermore, the diurnal variation of the PBL
height is lower in the city than compared to the surrounding rural area, and the UBL often stays unstable
both day and night even as the PBL height is lower at high time (Arya, 2001).

3.3.3
Uncertainty and detection limits
The lower detection limit of the OCEC analyser is around 0.2 µg/cm2 ﬁlter and therefore prior to exposing
the quarts ﬁlters they need to be cleaned as they contain 2 to 5 µg OC/cm2 ﬁlter. Cleaning is done by
pre heating the quarts ﬁlters to at least 700 °C for one hour (Sunset Lab, 2008). The OCEC analysis
is best suited to analyse OC amounts of 5 to 400 ug/cm2 ﬁlter and EC between 1 and 15 µg/cm2 ﬁlter
(Sunset Lab, 2008). From a replicate run of more than 100 samples the standard deviation of the OCEC
analyser was by Sunset Lab (2008) found to be around 5 %.

3.3.4
Good and bad with an OCEC analyser
The thermal optical method that the OCEC uses has some drawbacks based on the analysis principle
(Cavalli et al., 2010):
 A slight uncertainty in the split point determination can change the EC concentration signiﬁcantly
if it occurs at the same time as the maximum of a high carbon peak.
 If some OC (BrC) is not evaporated in the He-mode it will cause a positive bias to the EC
 Estimating the amount of PC is based on the assumptions that either is the absorbance of EC and
PC the same or any PC formed will evaporate from the ﬁlter before any EC.
 The quarts ﬁlters, depending on their thickness, absorbs some organic vapours that could cause a
positive contribution to OC
According to Sunset Lab (2008) the problem with coloured organics on the ﬁlters are minimal because
the coloured organics have a low red light absorbance compared to EC. Eﬀects from coloured organics
might cause a positive bias of 1 to 2 % if the ﬁlters are heavily loaded. Other inorganic compounds could
colour the ﬁlters, for instance if the ﬁlters were collected close to smelters or ore productions. During
the analysis in this study no colouring of the ﬁlters have been observed.
An analysis of a ﬁlter only takes a small ﬁlter stamp (of 1.0 cm2 or 1.5 cm2) and a little more than a
20min wait. Not using much ﬁlter leaves room for other analysis or replicates, but without some sort of
autoloader the manual work with putting the ﬁlter into the OCEC needs to be done every 22min. This
is somewhat time consuming, but an autoloader is purchasable to reduce manual work and increase the
analysis hours of the OCEC.

Correction of OC
The ﬁlter sampler in Hvidovre is a high volume sampler with one ﬁlter and because the quarts ﬁlters act
as a light absorbant the measured concentration of OC will be overestimated compared to the actual
ambient concentration due to an increased absorbance of VOC. The samplers at HCAB and Risø has two
ﬁlters placed with a small vertical separation, which means that the lower ﬁlter, also called the backﬁlter
is only exposed to ﬁltered air, that has already passed though the top ﬁlter. Because of the absorbing
tendency of the ﬁlter, the extra VOCs that adsorbs to the top ﬁlter will be evaporate again and some
will adsorb to the backﬁlter. At some point an equilibrium will establish and the OC concentration on
the backﬁlter can be used to correct the OC on the top ﬁlter for this extra artiﬁcial absorbed OC.
Because there is no backﬁlter in connection with the Hvidovre sampler the backﬁlters from Risø have
been used to correct the measured OC concentrations at Hvidovre. This is of course an approximation,
as the air mass at Risø is not going to be identical to the one at Hvidovre. However, using the HCAB
backﬁlters an even larger error is expected as the dense traﬃc at HCAB is a source of additional VOCs
compared to the residential neighbourhood in Hvidovre. The backﬁlter OC concentration at Risø is

HYSPLIT
Background PM is transported with the wind and to identify source areas the HYSPLIT (Hybrid Single
Particle Lagrangian Integrated Trajectory Model) from NOAA is used to create trajectories back in time
(Draxler and Rolph, 2015). For the purpose in this thesis the HYSPLIT is used to calculate simple
web-based back-trajectories using the READY system (Rolph, 2015). However, HYSPLIT is capable of
calculating complex dispersion and deposition simulations using either matrix or ensemble systems to use
for calculations of atmospheric concentrations and a version is available to the met-oﬃce for emergency
response. HYSPLIT is well suited to use for quick calculations of trajectories from point sources using
metrological data and performs well in diﬀerent meteorological regimes, but is a little too sensitive to
low-level wind proﬁle (Draxler and Hess, 1998). The advection trajectories are calculated based on a
Lagrangian framework that only depends on the 3D velocity based on meteorological data.
There are diﬀerent meteorological archives available to use for the web-based HYPSLIT, and for the pur-
pose here the Global Data Assimilation System (GDAS) with a 0.5° resolution is chosen, as it provides
the best resolution covering the analysis periods. GDAS is a global gridded model that orders meteorolo-
gical data such as surface temperature, balloon data, wind proﬁles among other things to use for further
calculations as for instance in weather models.

The two summer periods have very similar thermic fractions with around 2/3 of the EC collected in EC1
and EC2 and 1/3 in EC3 and EC4. The winter and fall months also have a somewhat similar separation,

A large part of the EC1 and EC2 is pyrolytic created EC, but there is an EC contribution in the fractions
that is larger (in January to April 2013 of comparable size to the two summer seasons) during winter
and fall than during summer. As explained in section 2.2.2 both OC and EC is emitted during wood
combustion and the larger part of pyrolytic created EC could arise from this extra contribution of OC
from wood combustion that chars during the OCEC analysis. The diﬀerence between pyrolytic EC and
EC1 and EC2 is largest in November 2013 and in the period from November 2014 to January 2015,
mainly because the pyrolytic concentration is low.
During the summer there is a larger fraction of EC in EC3 and EC4 compared to the winter and fall.
Because EC from traﬃc is more inert than EC from wood combustion, and therefore evaporates from
the ﬁlter at higher temperatures, the higher fraction of EC3 and EC4 during summer could be because
of the wood combustion source of EC and OC and therefore the inﬂuence from traﬃc generated EC is
larger (Han et al., 2010).

5 Discussion
In this chapter some uncertainties regarding the measurement and calculation methods used are dis-
cussed, followed by a comparison of the measured EC concentrations to a measurement in G¨oteborg,
Sweden. To be able to compare with EU limits for ﬁne particles and other wood combustion measurement
campaigns in Denmark, the measured EC concentration is calculated into a PM2.5 contribution. The
chapter ends with a short discussion of how to improve the results obtained, followed by some thoughts
to put the work into perspective and ideas for future work.
5.1
Method uncertainly
Uncertainties regarding the OCEC analyser and the quarts ﬁlters
The analysis method selected to quantify the OC and EC content on the exposed ﬁlters is an OCEC
analyser, that by use of diﬀerent temperatures evaporates or oxidises carbon from a ﬁlter and in the end
the amount of carbon is detected in a FID. That some of the OC chars during the analysis can cause
signiﬁcant errors in both the OC and EC estimate. Therefore, it is very important to correctly account
for this artiﬁcially created EC. Finding the right amount of PC depends on ﬁnding the right split point
that is based on the optical properties of OC, EC and PC. The protocol for controlling the temperature
ramps used is the EUSAAR 2, but others such as NOISH and IMPROVE also exists. The diﬀerence
between the protocols lie in the number of temperature ramps, the temperature and the duration. Using
diﬀerent protocols make comparison of measurements from diﬀerent labs hard, as the protocols have
diﬀerent biases.
The EC to total carbon ratio can diﬀer by a factor of 4 when comparing between
labs (Cavalli et al., 2010). The EUSAAR 2 protocol was created to reduce charring, remove a possible
negative bias from too early evolution of light absorbing carbon species in the high temperature He-mode,
reduce positive bias from incomplete evolution of OC in the He-mode and to reduce the uncertainty of
the split point (Cavalli et al., 2010).
Even if the measurement technique is reproducible the analysis result is never better than allowed by
the analysis material. The quality of the analysis material depends somewhat on the sampler, which
has not been given any attention in this thesis so far. The sampler is a high volume sampler placed
in a measurement hut in Hvidovre that draws in outside air, removes particles with a diameter larger
than 10 µm and leads the rest through a quarts ﬁlter on which particles are absorbed. On a few of the
summer ﬁlters small bugs are observed and of course avoided while taking stamps. However, less visible
contaminations are possible and could create an increased OC concentration. Furthermore, some of the

be lower than if the measurement site were located a few days ”downstream”.
Estimation of the EC contribution from wood combustion
One of the key assumptions in this thesis is that the wood combustion load to the EC concentration can
be estimated by subtracting the winter EC concentration by the summer. The underlining assumptions
are that the EC contribution from traﬃc is the same both summer and winter, that the background EC
is approximately the same and that the contribution from wood combustion is zero during the summer.
These three assumptions will be discussed in the following.
The traﬃc density in Copenhagen has both daily, weekly and yearly variations as reported by Center
for Traﬃk (2012). The variations during the day doesn’t matter in this case because the ﬁlters have a
daily resolution, however both the weekly and the yearly has an inﬂuence. The variations during the
week cause less traﬃc on weekends as compared to weekdays, and as mentioned in section 4.2 the low
EC concentrations in ﬁgure 4.4 could be inﬂuenced by the lower traﬃc density during the weekend.
The traﬃc is more or less constant in Copenhagen except for a lower number of vehicles in July and
February. The summer analyses period is from 1st of June to 5th of July, and therefore the period
includes some of the lower traﬃc in July. Looking at the EC concentration averages listed in table 4.1
there is not a large diﬀerence between June and July in either 2013 and 2014. For February the traﬃc
reduction is not as pronounced as in July, however comparing the EC concentration in February to the
other winter months, to detect changed traﬃc patterns, makes little sense because the wood combustion
source is as ﬂuctuation at it is. Usually, the winter holiday is shorter than the Danish summer holiday
and therefore the eﬀect of lower traﬃc in February is not expected to have a huge impact on the average
EC concentration in February or the winter average.
Another assumption is that the wood combustion contribution to EC is zero during the summer. However,
that might not be entirely true as Szidat et al. (2009) ﬁnds that the summer urban background EC
concentrations in G¨oteborg has a small wood combustion contribution of 0.02 to 0.06 µg/m3.
This
contribution could be from forest ﬁres, burning of farm land, small ﬁres in ﬁre bowls or ﬁrepits in gardens
and barbecues. The average summer EC concentration in Hvidovre was measured to be 0.31 µg/m3 (table
4.2) and if the urban background concentration from G¨oteborg is used as an estimate of the background
concentration, the wood burning contribution is between 6 to 21 % of the EC concentration in Hvidovre
during the summer. This is however a somewhat crude estimate, but gives an indication of the size of the

5.2
Comparison to other measurements
Other studies have measured EC or the ﬁne particle concentrations in Denmark and Europe. First the
EC concentration is compared to a study from G¨oteborg and is followed by a generalisation of the EC
concentration to PM and a comparison with other Danish measurements.
Comparison of measured EC concentration to other investigations
In G¨oteborg, measurements by Szidat et al. (2009) was conducted in the winter 2005 and summer 2006
in down-town G¨oteborg on top a 7-ﬂoor-high building, and at a rural location 35 km south of G¨oteborg.
The urban EC concentration in summer was 0.38 to 0.59 µg/m3 and in winter 0.58 to 1.10 µg/m3. The
site in G¨oteborg show a larger contribution from traﬃc than from wood burning during the winter time,
and is therefore not 100 % comparable to Hvidovre. Even though the EC contribution from traﬃc is
higher in G¨oteborg the EC concentrations are still comparable, with a summer EC concentration in
Hvidovre of 0.31 ug/m3 and winter of 0.77 ug/m3 and the higher summer concentration in G¨oteborg can
be explained by the extra contribution from traﬃc.
An investigation by Glasius et al. (2006) found that the PM2.5 had comparable concentrations at HCAB
and in a small town (Gundsømagle) on Zeeland where about 50 % of the households use wood for heating.
The EC concentration in Hvidovre was also, for a few days measured to be comparable to concentrations
at HCABs.
Generalisation to ﬁne particle concentration
A campaign in Denmark measured particle concentrations in Gundsømagle, in an area with terrace houses
with about half the houses close by using wood as their main heat source because of rather expensive
electric heating. The campaign took place in 2002 and 2003/2004 and found a diﬀerence in PM2.5 of
4.4 µg/m3 between the city and a near ﬁeld station (Glasius et al., 2006). The local EC contribution
from wood combustion in Hvidovre was found to be 0.19 µg/m3 and that corresponds to a ﬁne particle
concentration of 0.9 µg/m3 (section 4.2.2 and K). The area close to Tofteg˚ardsparken in Hvidovre is
serviced by district heating and therefore the extent of wood combustion for heating is expected to be
less than in Gundsømagle and the lower wood combustion load in Hvidovre is therefore both expected
and explainable.

Generalisation to yearly average
A simple way to generalise the EC measurements to a yearly contribution of PM2.5 is by using degree days.
This simple approach is described in Olesen et al. (2010). The ﬁrst assumption is that by comparing
the degree days in the measurement period to the degree days in a normal year gives an estimate of
the need for heating. This need for heating can be assumed to be proportional to the ambient PM2.5
concentration, by assuming that the concentration is proportional to emissions from sources that aﬀect
the site, which is proportional to the wood use that can be described by the degree days. The calculations
are shown in appendix K.
The result is a yearly contribution from wood burning to the ﬁne particle concentration of 1.2 µg/m3 and
1.1 µg/m3 for 2013 and 2014, respectively. Olesen et al. (2010) investigated a small town, Slagslunde,
and found a yearly PM2.5 contribution of 1.2 µg/m3 measured in a relatively warm winter with 50 % of
the houses having a stove. For two rather diﬀerent areas the result is rather similar. The degree days
of the two measurement periods are similar, 1474 for 2013 and 1443 for 2014, however the length of the
periods are not the same, 92 and 103 days, respectively. Therefore, 2013 represent a colder period than
2014 which ﬁts with a slightly larger contribution to PM2.5. The period in Slagslunde has degree days
of 893 in a period from December 23rd to February 26th (66 days).
An investigation by Nielsen et al. (2008) concluded that the yearly particle contribution from local wood
smoke sources causes an increase in the yearly ﬁne particle concentration of 0.2 to 1.0 µg/m3 in general
in Denmark. The contribution from Hvidovre (1.1 µg/m3 and 1.2 µg/m3) falls just above this estimated
range.

Improvement
A way to increase the conﬁdence of the obtained results could be to have a longer record of winter and
summer measurements to be more sure of the diﬀerences between the seasons and this could perhaps
also show more about the EC concentrations during New Year and St. John’s Eve, as no clear or marked
EC concentration could be seen in the two years of ﬁlters analysed.
The lack of correlation during wintertime could be because the temperature in the wintertime is more or
less constant below a threshold where people start to use wood combustion for heating. The measurement
campaign in Vindinge, that also measured during wintertime, didn’t ﬁnd a correlation between PM2.5
and temperatures between 10 to −10 °C either (?). It could be interesting to analyse more ﬁlters and
have a whole year or more years to see how the EC concentration behaves during the spring and fall
where people might be using their stoves more dependingly on the weather, than during the wintertime.

5.4
Implications on human health
This section relates some of the ﬁndings in this thesis to more tangible terms in relation to impacts on
human health.
The extra EC contribution caused by wood combustion is found to be 0.19 µg/m3, i.e. around 25 % of the
average EC concentration during wintertime. The eﬀect of stopping wood burning in Hvidovre would be a
20 % decrease in EC concentration corresponding to 0.9 µg/m3 reduction in the ﬁne particle concentration
section 4.2.2. As ﬁne particles are related to a variety of health eﬀects such as cardiovascular diseases,
asthma and increased sickness and because wood smoke is a carrier of PAHs and other toxic substances
(Naeher et al., 2007; U.S. EPA, 2009; WHO, 2014a), a reduction of the emission of wood smoke could
actually make a diﬀerence, especially if wood smoke is more potent in harming human health than other
PM as some research indicate (Rohr and Wyzga, 2012; Janssen et al., 2011). Rohr and Wyzga (2012)
also conclude that no component of PM is associated with zero health eﬀects.
A report by Brandt et al. (2013) found that 80 premature deaths per year in greater Copenhagen are
caused by local emission sources (50 by no industrial combustion and 21 by traﬃc). By assuming that the
eﬀect is equally distributed in Greater Copenhagen, the local emissions from non industrial combustion
(i.e. primary wood combustion) in Hvidovre causes 1.5 premature deaths a year. Brandt et al. (2013)
also ﬁnds that non-industrial combustion causes 56 000 sick days in Greater Copenhagen. Using the
same approach as for mortality the non-industrial combustion causes 1664 sick days in Hvidovre.
Both Rohr and Wyzga (2012) and Janssen et al. (2011) ﬁnds that EC and possible OC might be better
indicators for health eﬀects than both PM10 and PM2.5 and Ellermann et al. (2014) ﬁnds a 6.1 % increase

PM and EC emission from traﬃc is decreasing because of implementations required by EU and therefore

current European legislation, the Directive 2007/46/EC (Framework Directive), has been under attack

system has been made by the European Commission (EU, 2007; EC, 2016). Observations from HCAB
also show a general decrease in particles in the last 10 years (Ellermann et al., 2014). The consumption
of wood shows no decreasing tendency in Denmark with more or less the consumption in 2013 as in
2011 (Energistyrelsen, 2015) however the emissions from residential wood combustion shows a slight
decreasing tendency between 2007 and 2011 (Ellermann et al., 2015). Ellermann et al. (2015) notes that
the emissions are likely decreasing because of a decreasing emission factor due to installation of modern
stoves and boilers. One of the goals for the Danish government is to be fossil-free in 2050 and this could
increase the use of renewable energy sources such as biomass and thereby possibly the emission of EC,
both from residential use and from district heating. The district heating system has a PM2.5 emission
factor 500 times lower than a modern stove (Olesen et al., 2010). Changing the fuel burned in the plants
could however also change the amount of emissions. Because some research ﬁnds that the health eﬀects
from EC and possibly OC is larger than from PM this shift in heat and/or energy production could have
a negative eﬀect on the population (Rohr and Wyzga, 2012; Janssen et al., 2011).
The qualitative restriction from EU for PM2.5 is a yearly average below 25 µg/m3 while there is no limit
values speciﬁc for EC. Furthermore, there is an average exposure indicator (AEI) on PM2.5 that is based
on a 3 year average with a limit of 20 µg/m3 (EU, 2008). According to WHO (2014a) these guidelines are
too high because there are no scientiﬁc evidence that there exist a lower level for which there is no health
eﬀects when talking about particle pollution. WHO aims at reducing PM to the lowest values possible
with a PM2.5 guideline of 10 µg/m3 for an annual mean concentration and a 24-hour mean of 25 µg/m3
(WHO, 2014a). The EU limits are not exceeded at any measuring station in Denmark, however based on
table 2.1 the yearly guideline by WHO is violated even at the rural background station Risø (Ellermann
et al., 2015).
This thesis show that the EC concentrations are increased in areas with wood consumption and such
measurements could be used to, not only, take action to protect health of the public, but also in convincing
the general wood user and the politicians that actions need to be taken.

Future work
To continue with the process of investigating the wood combustion impact in Hvidovre running the entire

6 Conclusion
264 quarts ﬁlters exposed to ambient air in the suburb Hvidovre in the period between January 2013
and January 2015 have been analysed for OC and EC using a thermal optical analyser. The analysed
ﬁlters were selected to resemble the fall / winter and the summer periods in both years to have a base for
comparison between seasons. The measured EC concentrations have been compared with measurements
from HCAB and Risø, correlated with PAH measurements and compared to meteorological parameters
as temperature, wind speed and direction.
The maximum measured EC concentration is from November 2014 with 2.94 µg/m3 and the minimum
from June 2013 with 0.10 µg/m3. A seasonal variation is observed between summer and winter, with a
summer average of 0.31 µg/m3 and a winter average of 0.77 µg/m3. The tendency is observed at HCAB
and Risø but less pronounced. The winter/summer diﬀerence is a measure of the extra wood combustion
load during the winter, based on the assumptions that the emission from traﬃc is constant during the year
and that no combustion of wood occurs during the summer. The last assumption is an approximation and
causes some uncertainty in the estimate. The wood burning load in Hvidovre during winter is estimated
to be 0.46 µg/m3 where it at Risø and HCAB is 0.27 µg/m3 and 0.28 µg/m3 respectively. The similar
load at Risø and HCAB is seen as a measure of the average increase in background EC concentrations in
Copenhagen and northern Zeeland away from wood combustion hot spots. The extra EC concentration
in Hvidovre that is created by local sources of wood combustion, is therefore estimated to be 0.19 µg/m3.
Correlations between 4 speciﬁc PAHs (B[a]P, B[e]P, B[A]Ant and idenolpyrene) and EC concentrations
have been found to be highly signiﬁcant, where the best correlation is between EC and idenolpyrene
where EC explains 71 % of the changes in concentration. The comparison between EC and temperature,
wind direction and wind speed did not result in the same high correlations. Some connection was found
between EC concentration and wind speed, but with the data used in this study no correlation was seen
between EC concentration and temperature or wind direction.
The separation of EC into thermal fractions showed that EC was more inert during summer than winter,
i.e. more EC was found in EC3 and EC4 compared to EC1 and EC2. This diﬀerence ﬁts with the
expectation that EC from traﬃc is diﬀerent from EC from wood combustion because during the winter
the emissions of EC from wood, causes an extra source of EC that is not as pronounced during the
summer.
The increased EC concentration at the wood combustion hot spot Hvidovre strongly indicates that air
pollution is also present in the suburbs, and that, at some days, the EC concentration was measured to
be similar to the concentration at HCAB. In general the air is cleanest in rural areas, followed by urban

background and streets with dense traﬃc, and breaks with the conception that the air is clean in the
suburbs. These suburbs have not been given much attention previously, but the work in this thesis could
help put the issue into focus.
