Abstract
This thesis explores the use of ensemble learning techniques for the detection of manip­
ulated digital faces. The research aims to develop an approach to generalize digital face
manipulation detection by using ensemble learning methods to combine the predictions
of multiple classifiers in a hierarchy model. The study used various datasets of malicious
and benign manipulated, and bonafide digital face images. Many of the manipulated face
images and all of the bonafide images were taken from existing databases, with proper
references, while the rest were generated using OpenCV and custom functionality. The
results showed that the ensemble learning approach achieved good recall, precision, as
well as APCER and BPCER. The research has significant implications for the detection
of digital face manipulation in various applications, including forensics and security. The
study demonstrates the effectiveness of ensemble learning techniques for detecting dig­
ital face manipulation and highlights the importance of using a generalized approach to
achieve higher accuracy and reliability in the detection process. The results show that
while using an ensemble model for PAD yields prominent results, there is still a long way
to go before the model can be used in commercial options.

Chapter. 1
Introduction
Biometric systems utilize biological and behavioral characteristics for identification and
authentication, making them ideal for applications in security, mobile access, and law
enforcement due to their inherent properties. These systems can be implemented for
a variety of purposes, ranging from authentication within an individual’s device to em­
ployee identification and nationwide citizen databases. Several countries, including India
and China, maintain large­scale biometric databases featuring information such as fin­
gerprints, photographs, and irises [1]. China has recently announced plans to establish
the largest police­run biometric database, including DNA from its citizens [2]. As of 2019,
the Aadhaar biometric database in India contained information on over 90% of its popula­
tion [3]. Biometric systems are crucial in our daily lives and are expected to be increasingly
integrated in the future [4].
Biometric systems have become increasingly popular but have also been subject to a
surge of attacks, such as presentation attacks, biometric data theft, side­channel at­
tacks [5], etc. Face image manipulation is a particularly concerning issue as it can spread
misinformation and negatively impact the security of face recognition systems by gener­
ating false decisions. This paper aims to investigate three types of face image manipula­
tions: face morphs, face swapping, and region swapping. Face morphing is the blending
of two face images; face swapping involves replacing an individual’s face with that of
another, and region swapping is the process of swapping specific facial features. The
implications of face image manipulation extend globally, with studies demonstrating that
humans are less proficient than computers at detecting morphed images [6]. Online tools,
such as deep fake generators, have made it even easier for individuals to create manipu­
lated images or videos [7, 8]. This technology poses significant challenges to established
government and public institutions, with instances of synthetic face images being used
to deceive individuals and undermine trust in political processes [9]. The importance of
developing face image manipulation detection systems is, therefore, crucial.

As of now, most of these manipulation attack detectors only focus on one type of manipu­
lation. In a real­world scenario, we do not know which type of manipulation an adversary
will use, and only focusing on one opens us up to numerous other attacks. One could
have multiple manipulation attack detector systems that each check their manipulation.
However, it is rather inefficient to run many separate detectors, considering new manip­
ulations may arise in the future. If one had a system that could account for all known
current face image manipulations, this would be a huge advantage. This is exactly what
this thesis aims to accomplish.
1.1
Context
The study of digital face manipulation is nothing new. Plenty of papers have been pub­
lished discussing how anything from retouching to face morphs can negatively impact the
performance of recognition systems [10, 11]. For example, Rathgeb et al. [11] catego­
rized how retouching negatively impacts the performance of face recognition. Tolosana
et al. [12] surveyed state­of­the­art face manipulation, and current methods to detect such
manipulations. They found that current face manipulation detection algorithms work well
under controlled circumstances. However, not on unseen scenarios, showing the need
for generalization of face manipulation detectors. Scherhag et al. [6] looked at how face
morphing affects face manipulation detection in humans and computers. They found that
face morphs pose a severe impact on security to face recognition systems. Good­quality
morphs are virtually unidentifiable by humans and highlight the need for advanced algo­
rithms. The ease of generating manipulated face images has made it important to be able
to detect such manipulations.
These face image manipulation detectors rely on either of two techniques, no­reference
and differential detection. In no­reference, face image manipulation detection is done
by analyzing a single image and making a decision based on that. Differential detection
algorithms are a type of identity­aware detection technique where both a trusted and sus­
pected image is used during detection. Many papers have been published using either
approach. For example, Soleymani et al. [13] investigated a novel differential face morph
detection method using a deep siamese neural network. Ibsen et al. [14] produced a dif­
ferential anomaly detection algorithm. On the other hand, Venkatesh et al. [15] used an
ensemble learning approach to single­image face morph attack detection. Zhou et al. in­
vestigated a single image manipulation detection algorithm [16]. Many other approaches
also exist, but generally, it has been found that the differential approach produces a bet­
ter manipulation detection result [17] than no­reference [18]. Of course, each mode suits
different scenarios as one does not always have a trusted image available. One common
thread, connecting most of these papers however is that they are focused on one type of
detection. Some of the most researched cases are face morphing, retouching, or deep
fakes.
Yet little research has been carried out regarding ensemble learning for a differential ap­
proach to face manipulation detection. Ensemble learning is the combination of multiple
machine learning algorithms to obtain a better predictive performance than any learning
algorithm on its own. An ensemble model also has numerous other advantages; Improved
predictive performance [19] and increased robustness [20]. Ensemble learning has had
great success being applied across a wide array of domains. It has been used in skin
lesion classification [21], atom­resolved microscopy [22], data stream classification [23],
etc. Using a combination between ensemble learning and a differential approach to face
manipulation detection, the aim is to produce an algorithm that can detect multiple types
of face manipulations. Theoretically, the model will have a greater explainability due to

the ability of the model to detect multiple types of manipulations and output the specific
manipulation detected. With most manipulation detection systems, the output is a deci­

2.1
Basics of Biometric Systems
A biometric system refers to a type of system that utilizes a biometric characteristic to
uniquely identify and/or authenticate an individual. This characteristic could be any phys­
ical or behavioral trait that is unique to an individual and can be measured and analyzed.
Examples of such biometric characteristics include the face, fingerprints, iris, gait, and
veins. By analyzing these characteristics, the system can extract features that can be
used to compare and match with the stored data in order to verify the identity of the indi­
vidual in the future.
The face is one of the most popular biometric characteristics utilized in biometric systems,
mainly due to its convenience and ease of use. Facial recognition systems employ algo­
rithms that analyze facial features, such as the distance between the eyes, nose, and
mouth, to create a unique template that can be used to identify and authenticate individ­
uals. Similarly, fingerprint recognition systems utilize algorithms that analyze the unique
patterns on an individual’s fingertips to create a unique template that can be used for
future comparisons.
Other biometric characteristics such as iris [24], gait [25], and veins [26] have also gained
popularity in biometric recognition systems in recent years due to their unique features.
Iris recognition systems, for instance, utilize algorithms that analyze the unique patterns
in an individual’s iris to create a unique template that can be used for authentication.
Similarly, gait recognition systems employ algorithms that analyze the unique way an

2.1.1
Biometric Characteristics
Biometric factors are the fundamental aspects of biometric systems that determine their
efficiency and effectiveness. There are seven primary biometric factors, each of which
plays a crucial role in the overall functioning of the system. The biometric factors are
stated below [27].
Universality
is a fundamental requirement for any biometric system to be effective. Es­
sentially, it means that the chosen biometric characteristic must be present in the majority
of individuals who are expected to enroll in the system. For example, while a unique scar
could potentially be used as an identifier, it cannot be guaranteed that everyone will have
such a scar. Similarly, while fingerprints are a common characteristic that can be used for
biometric identification, it is important to acknowledge that some individuals may not have
all of their fingers, which could affect the system’s ability to correctly identify them. As a
result, it is essential to carefully consider the biometric characteristics being used and to
make allowances for any potential limitations or variations that may arise.
Uniqueness
in biometric systems refers to the degree of rarity of a specific biometric
characteristic among individuals. While some characteristics, such as height or weight,
may be easily replicated among different individuals, others, such as DNA or iris patterns,
are inherently more unique. However, it’s important to acknowledge that even unique
characteristics may have a possibility of duplication, whether through intentional fraud or
unintentional factors. Therefore, selecting biometric characteristics with a high degree of
uniqueness is essential in ensuring accurate and reliable identification in biometric sys­
tems [28].
Permanence
evaluate the ability of a biometric characteristic to withstand changes over
time and with the progression of age. Biometric factors such as height, weight, or hand
geometry can be prone to changes and pose a challenge to the authentication of legitimate
users. On the other hand, biometric factors like fingerprints are less likely to be altered
unintentionally and can provide a reliable means of authentication. Therefore, it is crucial
to choose biometric factors that exhibit high permanence to ensure the long­term reliability
and accuracy of the biometric system [29].
Collectability
, also known as measurability, refers to the ease of obtaining a trait that
can be used for user authentication. Biometrics like facial images are widely used due to
their relative ease of acquisition. However, if a more challenging trait, such as a footprint,
is selected, the user must remove their shoe and sock to enroll and authenticate, which
is considerably more inconvenient than taking a photo of an individual’s face [28].
Performance
metrics are used to evaluate the effectiveness of a system. These metrics
may include speed, accuracy, and error rate, among others. A high­performing system
requires excellence in each of these metrics. Failure to meet any one of them can sig­
nificantly diminish the system’s value. For instance, if a biometric authentication system
is slow, it can create a significant inconvenience for its users, particularly if there are
thousands of people who need to be biometrically verified. Alternatively, if the system’s
accuracy or error rate is poor, it can lead to a large number of individuals being either
falsely authorized or rejected, causing potential security risks [28].

Acceptability
gauges how agreeable a particular trait is to the users of the system.
Typically, systems that are sluggish, complicated, or uncomfortable to use are less likely
to be accepted by users. For instance, systems that require users to remove their clothing,
touch devices that have been frequently used by others, or provide bodily fluids or tissues
are unlikely to be widely accepted [28].
Circumvention
refers to how easily a system can be deceived by a counterfeit biometric
identifier. The ”gummy finger” is a classic example of a circumvention attack against
fingerprint biometric identification [30]. In this type of attack, a fingerprint is lifted from a
surface, possibly in a covert manner, and used to create a mold. The attacker can then
use the mold to cast a positive impression of the fingerprint in gelatin. To prevent such
attacks, some of the latest biometric systems have features specifically created to detect
them, such as skin temperature, pulse, pupillary response, and other factors [28] [31].

The General Biometric System
This section gives an overview over a general biometric system, its main phases, its com­

A biometric system has two primary phases: enrolment and authentication. During enrol­

template representing their identity for later comparison. The authentication phase in a
biometric system has two separate operational modes which will be discussed in greater

The Data Capture System
in a biometric system plays a crucial role in capturing the
biometric information of an individual for subsequent processing and comparison. The
subsystem may consist of a set of sensors, cameras, or other specialized hardware that
can capture and record different biometric data, depending on the application and the
required level of security. For instance, in a fingerprint recognition system, the data cap­
turing subsystem may include a fingerprint scanner that can capture the unique ridge and
valley patterns of the user’s fingerprints. Similarly, in a facial recognition system, the sub­
system may comprise a camera that can capture a series of images of the user’s face
from different angles and perspectives. The subsystem must be carefully designed and
tested to ensure that it can capture high­quality biometric data consistently and accurately,
even under various environmental conditions. Additionally, the subsystem must be user­
friendly, comfortable, and easy to use to ensure that users can capture their biometric
data quickly and conveniently.
The Signal Processing System
is responsible for crucial functions such as segmen­
tation, feature extraction, and quality control. In essence, the subsystem performs data
processing activities aimed at generating accurate and reliable biometric information.
Segmentation entails extracting essential data related to the biometric characteristic under
consideration while removing redundant information. For example, in the case of facial
recognition, this step involves identifying and extracting the face from the background
and normalizing the face image to eliminate factors that could interfere with face feature
analysis.
Feature extraction refers to the extraction of biometric characteristics from the sample
data, which typically include measures such as the distance between the eyes, length of
the nose, distance from forehead to the chin, and distance between the eyes and mouth.
Quality control is another crucial function that entails the automatic acceptance or rejection
of extracted features before storing them as a biometric template in the database. This
approach helps to prevent the inclusion of low­quality samples, which could impact the
system’s future performance. Popular quality control approaches include training models
to output quality scores, which are highly correlated with the performance of an efficient
face recognition/verification system. The subsystem uses a threshold value to ensure that
biometric samples surpass a specific quality level before considering them sufficient for
processing.
The Matching and Decision system
is a critical component of a biometric system, as
it is responsible for comparing the biometric data of an individual with the pre­existing
data stored in the system’s database. This subsystem uses algorithms to compare the
acquired biometric templates against one or many pre­existing templates, depending on
the operational mode. The system performs an extensive analysis of the biometric data
to determine the level of similarity between the two templates.
Once the comparison process is complete, the system returns an answer to the query
based on the results of the comparison. The system may return a positive match, indicat­
ing that the biometric data matches an existing template in the database. Alternatively, it
may return a negative match, indicating that the biometric data does not match any of the
pre­existing templates.

2.3.1
Face Recognition Approaches
Face recognition is the process of identifying an individual by analyzing their facial fea­
tures. There are three primary approaches to this task: holistic, local, and hybrid.
Holistic
approaches utilize global face information to perform face recognition. Global
face information is represented as a small number of features, which are directly derived
from the pixel information of the face image [42]. Popular approaches include Eigen­
faces [47].
Local
approaches in face recognition utilize specific local information of face images
to extract features. This can be done by detecting certain geometric features of faces,
such as the distance between the eyes and the mouth. Additionally, the face can be
segmented into smaller patches which are then analyzed to extract local features. Popular
approaches include Local binary patterns [48].
Hybrid
approaches use both global and local information of face images to extract fea­
tures. Popular systems include [49], and HiMFR [50].

Attack Presentation (1)
Attack presentation refers to the method used by an attacker
to gain unauthorized access to a biometric system or its data. The term encompasses a
wide range of techniques, from physical to digital attacks, that are aimed at circumventing
or bypassing the security measures implemented in the system.
Modify Biometric Sample (2)
Modifying a biometric sample is a type of attack that
can be used to deceive or bypass a biometric system. This attack involves altering the
biometric sample in some way to produce a false match or prevent a legitimate match.
Biometric systems employ a range of security measures such as liveness detection, anti­
spoofing, and encryption to ensure that the biometric data is authentic and has not been
tampered with. Regular updates and patches to the system are also necessary to address
any vulnerabilities that may be exploited by attackers.
Override Signal Processing (3)
Overriding signal processing is an attack on biometric
systems where an attacker manipulates the signal processing subsystem to modify the
biometric samples before they are compared to the templates stored in the enrollment
database.In this type of attack, the attacker may try to modify the biometric samples in
such a way that they pass the quality control measures but are not a true representation
of the individual’s biometric features. For instance, an attacker may manipulate the signal
processing subsystem to introduce noise or artifacts into the biometric sample to alter
the extracted features. The attacker may also try to manipulate the signal processing
subsystem to artificially enhance or degrade certain biometric features to make it easier
to match the biometric sample with a different template.
Modify Probe (4)
Modifying a probe sample works much in the same way as modifying
a biometric sample. Modifying a probe after it has been signal processed is a type of
attack on biometric systems that aims to manipulate the extracted biometric features in
order to bypass the authentication process.
Override Comparator (5)
Overriding the comparator is a type of attack on biometric
systems that involves tampering with the component responsible for comparing biometric
data. In biometric systems, the comparator compares the biometric features extracted
from the probe against the stored biometric templates in the database. If there is a match,
the system returns a positive identification. In the case of an attack on the comparator, an

attacker tries to manipulate the comparison process in order to bypass the authentication
process. One way to do this is by altering the matching algorithm used by the comparator,
making it less stringent and allowing more false positives to be accepted. For example,
an attacker could change the threshold value used by the comparator to decide when a
match has been found. By lowering the threshold value, the comparator will be more likely
to accept impostors as legitimate users, thereby bypassing the authentication process.
Modify Database (6)
Modifying the database is another type of attack on biometric
systems. In this type of attack, an attacker modifies the biometric database by adding,
deleting, or modifying biometric templates. By doing so, the attacker can manipulate the
system’s decision­making process to gain unauthorized access or alter the system’s be­
havior. One way to execute this attack is to replace an enrolled user’s biometric template
with an attacker’s template. This allows the attacker to impersonate the legitimate user
and gain access to the system. Another way is to delete or modify biometric templates
of authorized users, making it harder for them to authenticate themselves and access
the system. Attackers can also inject fake biometric templates into the database. These
templates are created by the attacker to mimic a legitimate user’s biometric features. The
system, therefore, recognizes the attacker as a legitimate user and grants access.
Modify Biometric Reference (7)
Modifying biometric references is one of the possible
attacks on biometric systems. Biometric references are templates or enrolled biometric
samples that are stored in the system’s database for future identification or verification
purposes. Attackers may try to modify these biometric references to bypass the biometric
system’s security and access sensitive information or locations.
Modify Score (8)
Modifying the score is another type of attack on biometric systems
that involves manipulating the output of the comparison process between the biometric
template and the reference template. In this type of attack, an attacker tries to change the
similarity score generated by the biometric system to match the score needed for authenti­
cation. The similarity score is the result of the comparison process between the biometric
template and the reference template. It indicates how close the biometric sample is to the
reference template. If the score is above a pre­defined threshold, the biometric system
authenticates the user. Attackers can manipulate the score in several ways. One way is
to increase the score to make the biometric system believe that the biometric sample is
more similar to the reference template than it actually is. Alternatively, the attacker can
decrease the score to make the biometric system reject a valid user.
Override Decision (9)
Overriding the decision is a type of attack on biometric systems
that involves tampering with the decision­making process. In this case, an adversary over­
rides the decision made by the biometric system to potentially produce a false rejection
or a false authentication.

Ensemble Learning
An ensemble classifier is the combination of multiple machine learning models to obtain
a better predictive performance that could be obtained from any of the models separately.
In an article by Sagi et al. [19], the researchers provide a comprehensive overview of en­
semble learning, which is a machine learning technique that combines multiple models
to improve prediction accuracy and generalization. The article begins by introducing the
concept of ensemble learning and its advantages, such as reducing overfitting and im­
proving the robustness of predictions. The article then provides a taxonomy of ensemble
learning methods, including bagging, boosting, and stacking, and explains the differences
between them. It also discusses various ensemble learning algorithms, such as random
forests, AdaBoost, and gradient boosting, and provides examples of their applications in
different domains. It also discusses some recent advancements in ensemble learning,
such as deep ensemble learning and transfer learning, and their potential for further im­
proving prediction accuracy and efficiency. Their findings indicate that ensemble learning
is used increasingly more in papers utilizing machine learning models. The three ensem­
ble learning methods are explained in the following paragraphs.
Bagging Ensemble Learning
, also referred to as bootstrap aggregation, is a homoge­
neous ensemble method that seeks a diverse group of ensemble members by varying the
training data. The meta­algorithm, was initially designed for classification, and is usually
applied to decision tree models, but can be used with any type of model for classification
or regression. At its core, the method works by taking many subsets of a training set by
using the bootstrap, i.e. sampling with replacement. Each subset is used to train a differ­
ent model of the same type. The outputs of the models are combined by averaging (for
regression) or voting (for classification) to create a single output. Bagging is most effec­
tive when using unstable (i.e. a small change in the training set can cause a significant
change in the model) non­linear models. Figure 2.6a shows an illustration of the bagging
method.
Boosting Ensemble Learning
is a meta­algorithm that can be viewed as a model av­
eraging method. Boosting is one of the most widely used ensemble methods. One first
creates a ’weak’ classifier, such that, it suffices that its accuracy is only slightly better than
random guessing. A sequence of models is built iteratively, each one trained on a data set
in which points misclassified (or, with regression, poorly predicted) by the previous model
are given more weight. Finally, the models are weighted according to their success and
their outputs are combined using voting (for classification) or averaging (for regression),
thus producing a final model. Figure 2.6b shows an illustration of the boosting method.

face in a digital image or video. The process uses machine learning algorithms to map
the facial features of one person onto the face of another person, creating a realistic
and seamless swap. This technique is often used for entertainment purposes, such as
in movies or television shows, to create special effects or to create humorous content.
However, it can also be used for malicious purposes, such as creating fake images or
videos for the purpose of spreading false information or causing harm. This is another
facial manipulation that has a large impact on face recognition systems. Figure 3.3 shows

Section 2.4 delved into the fundamental idea of ensemble learning. Subsequently, the

J.Y. Baek et al. [78] proposes a new method for detecting face image manipulations using
a generative adversarial ensemble learning. The proposed method utilizes a combina­
tion of multiple deep neural networks trained on different tasks and datasets to improve
the detection accuracy of face manipulations. The authors first explain the challenges of
detecting face manipulation, such as the increasing sophistication of manipulation tech­
niques and the lack of large­scale, diverse datasets. They go on to introduce the concept
of generative adversarial ensemble learning, which involves training multiple generative
adversarial networks (GANs) to generate realistic images and using them as input for a
detector network. The proposed method uses a combination of four GANs, each trained
on a different dataset, and a detector network based on a convolutional neural network
(CNN) architecture. The authors evaluate the performance of the proposed method on
two publicly available datasets and show that it outperforms state­of­the­art methods in
detecting various types of face manipulation, including splicing, copy­move, and color
manipulation.
Kashiani et al. [79] presents a new approach to detect morphed face images, which in­
volves combining multiple deep learning models trained on different datasets to improve
detection accuracy.
The proposed approach also incorporates domain generalization
techniques to address the challenge of detecting morphed faces across different domains.
They introduce the concept of ensemble morph detection, which involves training multi­

Anomaly Detection
Anomaly detection refers to the process of identifying unusual or unexpected patterns
or events that deviate from normal or expected behavior within a system or datasets.
Anomaly detection techniques in biometric systems can be divided into two categories:
data­driven and model­driven [81]. Data­driven techniques involve analyzing large amounts
of data to identify patterns and outliers that may indicate anomalies. Model­driven tech­
niques involve developing mathematical models to detect deviations from normal behav­
ior. The following papers investigate different anomaly models.
Perez et al. [82] presents a novel approach to face anomaly detection. The authors pro­
pose a deep anomaly detection (DAD) method that can detect unknown or novel types of
presentation attacks. The DAD method consists of a deep neural network that is trained
to distinguish between genuine and spoofed faces, as well as identify unknown or novel
types of presentation attacks. Overall, the article presents a promising approach to face
anomaly detection that can detect unknown or novel types of presentation attacks. The
DAD method has the potential to improve the security and accuracy of biometric face
recognition systems, particularly in environments where new types of presentation attacks
may be encountered.
Ibsen et al. [14] proposes a novel approach to anomaly detection in facial images. The
authors introduce a differential approach that compares the similarities between pairs of

Data Analysis
This section will focus on describing some of the metrics used to evaluate the ensemble
models, input data, etc.
The performance of any machine learning model will be evaluated using standard machine
learning analysis metrics such as accuracy, precision, recall, and F1­score. These metrics
will be calculated using a confusion matrix generated from the testing set. A confusion
matrix will be generated using the predicted truth labels for the manipulated and bonafide
images in the testing set.
Accuracy
measures of how well a model predicts the correct outcome for a given set
of data. Specifically, accuracy measures the proportion of correctly classified instances
among all instances.
Precision
is the proportion of true positives (a.ka. those that are malicious) among all
instances that were classified as positive. In other words, precision measures the extent

of the the error caused by False Positives (FP) by measuring against the amount of True
Positives (TP).
Recall
is the proportion of true positives among all instances that are actually positive.
In other words, recall measures the extent of the the error caused by False Negatives
(FN) by measured it against the amount of TP.
F1
is another metric. Ideally one would want to maximize precision and recall, however
at a certain point there will always be a trade off between the two. That is increasing
precision, decreases recall, and vice versa. In order to compare the precision and recall
between models, we use the F1 score. The F1 score is the harmonic mean of precision
and recall. It is used when both the precision and recall of a model are weighted similarly.

In any given system there is a trade off between the precision and recall. At a certain point,
an increase in precision will correspond to a decrease in recall. Likewise, an increase in
the recall of a system decreases the precision of a system. In other words, precision and
recall is a trade off between the usability and security of a biometric system. On a higher
level, precision indicates how many of the positive predictions are actually correct whilst
recall indicates how many of the actual postivie samples are correctly identified by the
model. Depending on the use case of the system generated, precision or recall can be
weighted differently. However, since the focus of this thesis is on generalized manipulation
detection, the recall of the system will be weighted higher than the precision.
To further evaluate the performance of the models, biometric analysis standards recom­
mended by NIST and the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) will be used.
These standards provide guidelines for evaluating the effectiveness of biometric systems,
which include face recognition systems.
The NIST and ISO guidelines include the use of detection error trade­off (DET) curves to
evaluate system performance. DET curves plot the false positive rate against the false
negative rate. These curves provide a graphical representation of the system’s perfor­
mance, allowing a comparison of the performance of the ensemble models at different
levels of detection sensitivity [83].
The ISO guidelines [83] also recommend the use of equal error rate (EER) and minimum
detection rate (MDR) metrics to evaluate the performance of presentation attack detection

6.1.3
Differential Feature Embeddings
The differential feature embedding consists of the feature embedding of a trusted face
image combined with the feature embedding of a suspected face image. The combination
was calculated as the subtraction between the suspected image and the trusted reference
image.
This combination of features has been proven to be robust to several anomalies such as
variations in pose and lighting that may affect the biometric data [14]. This is because it
focuses on the relative differences between two or more images rather than their absolute
values. However, its effectiveness depends on the quality of the images being analyzed,
as well as the accuracy and reliability of the machine learning algorithms used for analysis.
The feature embedding combination process has been illustrated in figure 6.1.
Figure 6.1: Illustration of generating of difference embeddings.
The differential approach was slightly different for the bonafide/benign images than for
the malicious images. The bonafide/benign images were used together with a trusted
reference image of the same individual to generate the differential. However since the
malicious images contained features for two individuals, the same process could not be
used. For these the individual who was used as the source of the manipulation was used
to find a trusted reference image, which could then be used to calculate the differential.

6.2.1.1
Anomaly Detector
A one­class SVM is a type of SVM algorithm that is typically used for anomaly detection,
in which the objective is to identify samples that are significantly different from all other
samples in the training dataset. Unlike traditional SVMs that rely on labeled data to learn
the boundary between classes, a one­class SVM uses only one class of data to learn the
decision boundary. It works by fitting a hyperplane to the data such that the majority of
the data points are inside the hyperplane and a small fraction of the points are outside.
For this project, it was decided to use the One­Class SVM as the model for anomaly de­
tection. The model was initially trained on the bonafide images from the FERET database.
However, realizing that the bonafide dataset was limited in size, it was decided to incor­
porate a new database into the pipeline: the UNCW database. This dataset contains over
Ensemble Learning for Generalized Digital Face Manipulation Detection
41
==PAGE:50==
tens of thousands of data inputs versus only a couple of thousand inputs for the FERET
database.
The anomaly detector is used to separate bonafide presentations from attack presenta­
tions in the initial stage. The anomaly detector computes the anomaly score for a given
image and compares it against a pre­determined threshold. If the computed score is
above the threshold, then the model has detected anomalies in the image. If the score is
below the threshold, then the model has not detected any anomalies. This threshold was
generated by computing the anomaly scores for all attack presentations and the bonafide
presentations. Since the purpose of the initial stage is to filter out a majority of bonafide
and benign images, the threshold was set at a lenient attack presentation classification
error rate (APCER) value. The APCER is the ratio of the number of fraudulent images
that are classified as bonafide over the total number of images. It was decided that at the
anomaly stage, an appropriate value would be a 5% APCER. This value is specifically
set at a lower security level due to the fact that the anomaly detector’s job is to filter out a
majority of bonafide images and benign manipulations.
Alternative to the One­Class SVM
While the one­class SVM has shown promising
results as an anomaly detector, it was not the only model considered. Another viable
solution that would work as an anomaly classifier is a Variational Autoencoder (VAE). A
VAE is a type of generative model that uses neural networks to learn a low­dimensional
representation of high­dimensional data. The VAE consists of an encoder and a decoder.
During training, the VAE minimizes the reconstruction error between the input data and the
output of the decoder, as well as the KL divergence between the learned distribution of the
latent space and the prior distribution [85]. VAEs have been found to work well for anomaly
detection because they learn to model the underlying probability distribution of the training
data. This means that during training, the VAE learns to represent the most common
patterns in the data and can identify anomalies as data points that deviate significantly
from this distribution [86]. The result of the performance of the One­Class SCM vs VAE,
was omitted from the main part of the thesis but is presented in Appendix A.2.
6.2.1.2
Manipulation Classifiers
A Support Vector Machine (SVM) is a popular supervised machine learning algorithm
used for classification and regression analysis. It works by identifying a hyperplane that
best separates the data points into separate classes. In other words, it finds the optimal
boundary or decision boundary that maximizes the margin between the closest data points
from different classes. SVMs handle high­dimensional data very well and are effective
in dealing with complex problems, including non­linear data, through the use of kernel
functions.
SVMs are particularly useful for classification problems. Hence, the SVM was chosen as
the model for classifying manipulated images. To identify each malicious manipulation
independently, three distinct SVMs were developed for face swaps, face morphs, and
region swaps. Rather than employing a single model to detect multiple manipulations,
these models were created to identify each type of manipulation individually. This will
become relevant in the following chapter, in experiment 8.1.4. Each of these models was
trained on its own manipulation type dataset from the FERET database.
It is the job of the manipulation classifiers to determining the likelihood a suspected image
is of a certain manipulation type. Each of the classifiers has a threshold set at a lower
APCER value to decrease the rate at which images are incorrectly predicted. These
threshold were set at an APCER of 0.1%. Usually decreasing the APCER value would
increase the bonafide presentation classification error rate (BPCER), however this is risk is
42
Ensemble Learning for Generalized Digital Face Manipulation Detection
==PAGE:51==
mitigated as the anomaly model should already have filtered out a majority of the bonafide
images.
Finally based on the classifier models, their threshold, and the individual way each model
combines these results, the hierarchy model will determine if the image is most likely a
bonafide image, a face swap manipulation, a face morph manipulation, or a region swap
manipulation.
Alternative to the SVM
Another viable solution that would work as a classification
model, is a neural network. The neural network was explained in section 2.2.2. As ex­
plained in this section some neural networks, such as CNNs, are good at classification.
However, it was decided to use the SVM since this model has a relatively shorter training
time and ability to handle high­dimensional data.
6.2.1.3
Hyperparameter Tuning
Both the SVM and one­class SVM offer the option to optimize the model for the training
set through hyperparameter training. Hyperparameters in SVM are predetermined model
parameters that are not learned from the data and include parameters such as kernel
type, kernel parameters, regularization parameter (C), and gamma value.
Hyperparameter tuning aims to find optimal values for the hyperparameters that result in
the best model performance. Grid search is a common approach for SVM hyperparameter
tuning that involves trying a set of predefined hyperparameter values and evaluating the
model’s performance on a validation set. Another approach is random search, which
involves sampling hyperparameters from a predefined range and selecting optimal values
based on model performance.
Hyperparameter tuning plays a crucial role in building an optimal SVM model, as it impacts
the model’s performance and complexity. However, due to the use of training and testing
data from different databases, there is a high risk of models overfitting to the training
data, leading to a decline in performance and generalization capabilities. As a result,
hyperparameter training contradicts the research’s objective, and the decision was made
not to modify most of the model’s hyperparameters.
There is, however, one important hyperparameter that was set: the kernel. The kernel is
vital to an SVM, and it has been found that the RBF kernel is widely regarded as the most
common for image classification [87].
6.2.2
Naive Hierarchy Model
The naive hierarchy model is the first and simplest implementation of the hierarchy model.
It should be seen as a baseline model for which subsequent models can be compared.
Each of the manipulation classification SVM models was used to generate three scores
for each of the images from the testing dataset. These scores represent the likelihood
of an image being of that manipulation origin. The naive model takes the scores and
classifies the image corresponding to which model produces the highest score. However,
if none of the models produce a score sufficient to surpass its threshold, the image would
be reclassified as a bonafide presentation.

6.2.3
Rule Based Hierarchy Model
The rule­based model incorporates some of the core features of the naive model ap­
proach, such as the max function for determining the model with the highest score, and
a check for whether all model scores are below their respective thresholds. Similarly,
the model uses the same anomaly model and SVM classifier as well as the respective
thresholds established in the previous section.
In contrast to the naive model, the rule­based hierarchy model recognizes that different
combinations of SVM model scores often lead to varying predictions. To enhance model
performance, it seeks to identify a set of rules by analyzing the scores relative one an­
other. As investigated in later experiments, the performance of each SVM model varies
significantly, each model score should therefore be weighted differently to reflect its ro­
bustness. Therefore, each rule is designed to identify common errors made by the model
and address them accordingly.
The rule­based model’s performance was optimized by developing a series of rules based
on data generated by the previous model. These rules were derived from the observed
limitations in model performance. However, a drawback of this model is that the rules are
somewhat abstract, and are based on rough estimates. This section will discuss a subset
of these rules.
SVM thresholds
These rules focused on comparing the model scores with their respec­
tive thresholds. If only one model generated a score above its threshold, the image was
classified accordingly. However, if multiple models produced scores above their thresh­
olds, the classification was assigned temporarily based on a predetermined priority order.
This priority order was established based on the performance of each SVM model, which
will be explored in a future experiment (refer to figure 8.4a). The face swap score had the
highest priority, followed by the face morph score, and finally the region swap score.
Weighting SVM models
Since the SVM models have varying levels of performance,
a set of rules was established to determine if any of the models produced scores that
exceeded their respective thresholds. The model then classified the image based on the
priorities established until one or none of the model scores exceeded their thresholds.
Additionally, if the face swap model generated an exceptionally high score, the model
predicted the image as a face swap. Conversely, if both the face swap and face morph
classifiers produced very low scores, the image was classified as a bonafide.

relatively small, the model prioritized classifying the image as a face morph. However, if

6.2.4
Decision Tree Hierarchy Model
The decision tree hierarchy model builds on the idea of the rule­based hierarchy model
and aims to generalize it by removing custom rules. Instead, a decision tree is imple­
mented. The model still uses the same anomaly model and SVM classifier with respective
thresholds established in the previous section.
The decision tree is built using scores only from the FERET dataset. SVM classifiers
are trained on subsets of the manipulation sets, generating scores that are tested on the
remaining data. The decision tree is then trained on these scores. If the SVM models
were tested on previously seen images, they would produce perfect results, leading to a
biased model.
Using a decision tree has the advantage of generating optimized rules to maximize class
separation based on available training data. This enables the model to generalize better
towards future data. However, a disadvantage of using a decision tree is the low amount
of training data available. Since the SVM models and decision tree cannot see the same
data, the models have small datasets for training, potentially resulting in overfitting to the

Chapter. 10
Conclusion
Face recognition systems have become an integral part of modern society, as they pro­
vide quick and reliable identification of individuals. Such systems utilize various biometric
characteristics, including facial features, fingerprints, and gait, to perform accurate recog­
nition. However, as with any technological innovation, these systems are not perfect and
can be vulnerable to certain types of attacks, such as digital face manipulations. Digital
face manipulations, such as face swaps and face morphs, involve the manipulation of
digital images to create a new facial appearance, which can deceive the face recognition
systems. The impact of these manipulations can be significant, as they can negatively
affect the performance of biometric systems and lead to false identification. As a re­
sult, researchers and developers have been working on developing and improving face
recognition systems by investigating new techniques for detecting and preventing face
manipulations. This has therefore focused on investigating the impact ensemble learning
has on generalized face manipulation detection.
In this chapter, the experimental findings uncovered in the previous chapters are pre­
sented as answers to the research questions defined in section 1.2 using the results de­
rived in chapter 8.
RQ 1: Differential Manipulation Detection
How can differential manipulation detection be used to detect different types
of face manipulations?
Manipulation systems nowadays utilize two different methods for detecting face manip­
ulations: differential and single­image detection. Section 6.1 investigates the process
necessary for differential manipulation detection. This includes aligning the images, crop­
ping them to the correct facial area, and extracting the features. Each image can then be
66
Ensemble Learning for Generalized Digital Face Manipulation Detection
==PAGE:75==
subtracted from a bonafide image of the same individual, thereby giving two types of fea­
ture embeddings: those representing the difference between a manipulated image and
a bonafide image, and those representing the difference between two bonafide images.
Since the differential manipulation detection approach is robust towards pose and lighting
changes, the difference between bonafide images should be relatively small compared
to the difference between a manipulated image and a bonafide image. These feature
embeddings are referred to as differential embeddings. The outcome is then a series of
differential embeddings representing the difference between a manipulated image and a
bonafide image of the same person, or the difference between two bonafide images of
the same person.
It was then investigated how using these differential embeddings could be used to detect
different types of face manipulations. To achieve this, it was decided that several models
should be trained on differential embeddings of different kinds. Since the literature review
showed that some systems have achieved state­of­the­art performance using SVMs, it
was decided to implement these for each of the malicious manipulations established in
section 5. By training the models on the differential embeddings instead of the embed­
dings for respectively bonafide images and a specific manipulation type, this allowed the
models to learn how a specific manipulation typically alters images and used this for clas­
sification. Instead of training on the manipulated images and bonafide images separately.
Separate models for face­swapped manipulations, face­morphed manipulations, and region­
swapped manipulations could then be used to classify test data into separate classifica­
tions. Several experiments investigated the performance of these models. It was found
that the face swap SVM performed the best at an EER of 0.5%, then the face morph SVM
operating at an EER of 4%, and finally, the region swap operating at an EER of 15%.
RQ 2: Ensemble Learning
How can ensemble learning be used to detect manipulated face images?
In the initial stages of the investigation, multiple existing ensemble models were inves­
tigated to determine how ensemble learning could be used to detect manipulated face
images. Among those investigated were a stacking classifier and a custom score fusion
method. The stacking classifier takes the score from each of the input models and com­
bines them using a meta­classifier. The score fusion model took each of the scores from
the classifier models and used a custom combination function in an attempt to improve
the detection result. However, these were found to be inefficient and performed poorly on
the testing sets. Therefore, it was investigated whether an ensemble of multiple models
could outperform these models.
This was the basis for developing the ensemble model referred to as the general hierar­
chy model. A figure showing the structure of the general hierarchy model is presented
in the paper. The first principle of the general hierarchy model relied on first focusing on
filtering out the bonafide images. Instead of using the combination of the SVM scores on
the bonafide images to correctly filter out a majority of the bonafide images, the SVMs
would then only focus on predicting the malicious manipulation type of each image. Sev­
eral anomaly detection models were investigated, such as the One­Class SVM and the
VAE. Experiments showed, however, that the models performed similarly, even when
varying the training data. It was decided to use the One­Class SVM trained on the UNCW
bonafide dataset. The anomaly model would then calculate the score of each test image
and determine if it crossed a predetermined threshold, set at an FMR of 5%, for anomalies
and classify them as bonafide or suspected manipulated.

After having generated the general hierarchy model, the final step was to determine the
most efficient way to combine the results from the classifiers to optimize the prediction
performance of the model. To investigate this, three separate implementations of the hi­
erarchy model were created. The first model (naive hierarchy model) used a max function
between the score output of the SVMs to determine the correct manipulation type. The
second reused the same implementation and introduced the addition of rules. These were
introduced based on the fundamental idea that analyzing the relative differences between
the scores would improve the prediction performance. The last model took the idea the
previous model was based on and used a more general approach. Instead of implement­
ing custom rules, the third model trained a decision tree based on the scores produced
by the SVMs. This approach would theoretically allow for a more flexible model, in case
the model was to be tested on different data in the future.
RQ 3: Score Fusion
Which methods of fusing classifier results allow for the best manipulation de­
tection performance?
Having implemented three separate versions of the general hierarchy model, they could
now be benchmarked against each other for face manipulation detection. The experiment
consisted of feeding each model iteration a series of images consisting of bonafide im­
ages, face­swapped images, face­morphed images, and region­swapped images. The
models were then tested based on their ability to predict each of the images correctly.
The findings presented in Chapter 8.2 indicate that the hierarchy model with a decision
tree method is the most effective for fusing classifier scores as expected. This model was
compared to two other methods, the max function, and rule­based models. The metrics
utilized clearly demonstrate that the decision tree approach delivers superior performance
results. The naive approach had very low performance with APCER = 60% and a BPCER
of 21%. The rule­based hierarchy model had a significant improvement, reaching an
APCER = 34% and a BPCER = 19.3%. While these results are better, they still overall
perform poorly as they misclassify manipulations 25% of the time. The decision tree
hierarchy model performed at an APCER = 15.42% and a BPCER = 14.75%, which is
a clear improvement over the previous models. However, the decision tree hierarchy
model still has relatively high APCER and BPCER values as shown. As the purpose
of the model is for generalized detection, further research must focus on improving the
fusion mechanism to better separate the manipulations from each other as well as the
separation of bonafide from malicious images.
RQ 4: Benign vs Malicious Manipulations
How can face image manipulations be categorized as being benign or mali­
cious?
As discussed in the previous chapter, testing the model in a real­life scenario carries cer­
tain implications. When applying the model in, for example, a border control scenario to
detect face manipulations, one cannot assume that the testing data will be pristine. Some
images may have been accidentally altered. To simulate the real­world performance of
the model, it was crucial to investigate how the model would perform on testing data that
included manipulations that were not necessarily malicious attacks by adversaries. A se­
ries of manipulations, referred to as benign manipulations, were generated to investigate
how the model would perceive these images and how its performance would change in

their presence. Among these benign manipulations were darkened images, images with
a loss in quality, and retouched images.
The results show that the performance of the models decreases drastically in the pres­
ence of benign manipulations. Since some of these images resemble bonafide images,
while others resemble the malicious ones the most, the system has a hard time differ­
entiating all the manipulations from each other. This is noted by the drastic decrease
in performance. The APCER for the naive model only increased by a few percent while
the BPCER increased by approximately 30%. The APCER for the rule­based hierarchy
model increased by less than one percent while the BPCER increased by roughly 10%.
Lastly, the APCER of the decision tree hierarchy model increased by around 10%, while
the BPCER had a significant increase to almost 50%. These results call for continued
research to develop models robust to benign manipulations that could affect the perfor­
mance of generalized manipulation detection systems.
