Abstract
Creating a cooperative game with high levels of player agency is a difficult task. The
industry of video games is growing rapidly and thus the desire for creating innovative
cooperative experiences is great. This paper explores potentials and pitfalls when devel-
oping cooperative games with high levels of player agency. Both technical and design
challenges are explored. Interactive story telling (IDS) and IDS design principles are
introduced in order to explain the tools to create narrative-driven games. A story en-
gine is developed, consisting of a narrative modelling framework and an online system
architecture. This story engine is built upon IDS design principles and enables coop-
erative gameplay. The engine is used to implement a narrative-led cooperative game
called The Amulet. Expert interviews are conducted to validate the story engine’s real
world applicability. The story engine has potential to be used for game development, if
it is developed further. It is found that an issue in game development is not the lack
of an engine like this, but often the communication between game designers and game
writers. Additionally, it is found that a project’s scope and development cost increase
significantly, if cooperative game elements are introduced to games with high levels of
player agency. This thesis suggests a number of design considerations that developers
can utilise.

Problem statement
This thesis is an exploratory study on the potentials and pitfalls of cooperative inter-
active digital storytelling (IDS) games. As such, it investigates what is required when
developing an IDS game in a cooperative game environment, both in terms of technical
and design aspects.
This research is thus conducted from two perspectives, namely a technical and a theo-
retical design perspective. This allows the researchers to approach IDS in cooperative
games from multiple angels and hence provides a more uniform representation of the
research.
The technical aspect of this paper is analysed through the construction of a story engine,
which is designed to facilitate cooperative interactive narrative creation, along with a
game build within that engine.
The design aspect of this paper and the developed
game is based on a theoretical analysis that has its premise in IDS theory, as well as
expert statements collected by the researchers. The story engine and developed game is
validated through narrative-expert interviews in order to understand its potential real
world applicability and narrative potential.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The industry of video games is growing rapidly and thus the desire for creating innovative
experiences is becoming increasingly more important.
Never in the history of video
games has storytelling been as much in focus as now. Companies producing Triple-A-
games (AAA)1 are spending hundreds of millions of dollars creating games with deep
and extensive narratives. Famous Hollywood actors are doing voice acting and motion-
capture for games, blurring the lines between films and games as storytelling mediums.
Small independent developers with less impressive budgets are also focusing heavily on
storytelling. This is seen in titles like Papers Please[50], where players are presented
with moral dilemmas as a border crossing immigration officer, as well as Thomas Was
Alone[9], a game about self-aware artificial intelligence (AI) entities represented solely
by simple polygon shapes. What sets games apart from traditional storytelling media
is the medium’s inherent interactivity. Stories in books and films are static and can be
interpreted differently but never changed. Games however, have dynamic environments,
which makes for dynamic stories.
1.1
Scope
Interactive digital storytelling (IDS) has been researched for more than 25 years, and
has in the past decade gained increased traction due to technical advances and the
recognition of game studies as a discipline[45]. This thesis gets it theoretical premise
from the research field of IDS. The goal of any IDS project is to transplant the players into
a digital world by all means of their visual and aural faculties, involving them in a drama
where they are the actors[25]. In an interactive storytelling setting, the storyline is not
predefined as in books and film, in contrast a shift away from traditional interactivity
exists[25].
As such, IDS applications invite the player to participate in shaping and
developing the narrative arc. Due to the increased attention IDS has gained in recent
years, it is present in multiple game genres in varying degrees within the game industry.
Interactive media, such as games, give the player the means to influence the world in
which they play. Whether this is by jumping on enemies to eliminate them, cutting
down bushes to find secret paths, or crafting unique items, players understand and
expect that they can impact the game world. This impact is commonly referred to as
player agency[79]. Agency can also be derived from impacting the narrative, Thue et

video game requirements is an expensive endeavour for game studios, due the extensive
amount of story agency that IDS games posses. In such games, instead of creating one
predefined storyline, developers must take multiple narrative developments into account,

Researchers[64, 76, 51]

IDS productions has primarily been developed as singleplayer experiences.[64].
The
approach of this thesis differs by examining IDS in a cooperative gameplay environment.
Cooperative gameplay occurs in multiplayer games when players coordinate their actions
or share resources to reach a common goal[55].
Many of the worlds most successful
games, in terms of sales and active players, have cooperative gameplay elements[34, 17,
2]. Despite these games being highly cooperative, the narrative experience of these games
all becomes subsidiary.
This thesis aims to investigate what is to be considered by developers, when creating a
game that exists at the intersection of cooperative gameplay and IDS. In order to accom-
plish this a story engine, consisting of a narrative modelling framework and an online
system architecture, will be developed. Within this engine a narrative-led cooperative
game will be implemented. This will assist in identifying potentials and pitfalls when
making an interactive story in a cooperative game environment. This thesis aims to
suggest potential development considerations of such a production, by exploring both
its technical and design requirements.
1.2
Motivation
Playing with friends, venturing on a fictional journey and feeling that your collaborative
actions directly shape the world in which you play, are for some players the most exiting
aspect of playing games. Although this is a known feeling for those that play table-top
RPGs, such as Dungeons and Dragons[14], unfortunately it is less known for the video
game enthusiasts. Few video games allow multiple players to truly impact the narrative
experiences. In order to explain the motivation behind this thesis, it is appropriate to
look at general trends in how current cooperative games handle narrative.
A popular branch of cooperative games is the Player versus Player (PvP) game genres
such as First Person Shooters (FPSs) and Multiplayer Online Battle Arenas (MOBAs).
These games have fine-tuned cooperative game mechanics, where players coordinate their
actions to defeat an enemy team. Some PvP games develop such balanced and compet-
itive mechanics that they evolve into E-Sports2. The focus of these games thus become

the mechanics and dynamics, which leaves the narrative as a subsidiary, complementary,
or even irrelevant feature.
A number of cooperative games, such as Diablo 3[31] and Portal 2’s[19] cooperative
campaign, do posses a higher degree of interactive narrative with large parts of their
gameplay revolving around experiencing a story.
However, little to no story agency
exists, meaning that the players cannot impact the narrative in any meaningful way.
When playing cooperatively in Diablo 3 the narrative framework remains the same as
when playing alone. In Portal 2 players follow a linear path with the narrative being set
on a predetermined path. Although it being a very compelling story, the players cannot
change its path or outcome.
In Massive Multiplayer Online Role-Playing Games (MMORPGs) cooperative gameplay
is a key element.
Players complete quests, venture into dungeons, compete in PvP,
or explore the game world as a group.
Narrative is a major part of MMORPGs as
well, with games such as Star Wars: The Old Republic(SWTOR)[8] and The Elder
Scrolls Online[75] being centred around well-known narrative-led franchises. Building
an MMOPRG that serves thousand of simultaneously active players while providing
narrative agency is challenging. A player’s decisions cannot impact the story to such a
degree that the next player is excluded from making that same decision. This results in
a game world where having story agency becomes an individual experience rather than
a cooperative experience.
Creating a cooperative game with high levels of story agency is a difficult task for
developers. The game Fable II[73] lets players make decisions that have an impact on the
narrative, but when playing with two players cooperatively, only one player makes these
decisions. The other player is relegated to being a voiceless companion. This cooperative
compromise in Fable II has most likely been taken to limit the scope of the game and thus
to reduce expenses for the production. SWTOR designed by BioWare and LucasArts,
with a booming budget of over 200 million US dollars[84], has a decent level of story
agency, even when playing in a cooperative setting. Players are presented with choices
through dialogue sequences, however it usually only amounts to nuances in conversation.
At times a choice in SWTOR will impact the narrative arc in a particular quest. Such
a choice could be choosing between saving a non-player-character (NPC) or acquiring
an old artefact. These choices are represented as alternative quest objectives, which
ultimately lead to the same result, gained amount of experience, and quest rewards.
As opposed to multiplayer games, large branching stories where players make significant
and meaningful narrative choices are found in many singleplayer games. The Witcher
3[61] delivers an open world setting with hundreds of player choices, making players ca-
pable of shaping and directing the narrative experience. Mass Effect[7], Heavy Rain[28],
and The Walking Dead[35] are other examples of singleplayer narrative-led games, which
are build around creating story agency.

1.3 Delimitation and definitions
5
It becomes apparent that on one hand game developers have created successful coopera-
tive games and on another they have created complex and dynamic singleplayer interac-
tive storytelling games. However, very few games encompass both of these aspects. As
such, the motivation behind this thesis is the interesting aspect of developing games with
both IDS design principles and cooperative gameplay elements. This is the first study,
according to an extensive literature review in section 2, that aims to explore potentials
and pitfalls, both from a technical and a design perspective, in cooperative IDS games.
1.3
Delimitation and definitions
The product developed for this thesis has been designed to the level of an early concep-
tual prototype and will therefore not be concerned with graphics or general usability
principles such as feedback. Furthermore, the product has been created as a text-based
narrative-led role-playing game (RPG), and for the sake of scope it will not have any of
the expected design conventions for its genre such as combat, loot or character levels.
The product is created as a multiplayer game, but is limited to only having two players
active at once and is only concerned with cooperative game mechanics thus omitting any
competitive elements. The narrative setting in which the game takes place is limited to
one minor branch of what could be considered as a larger narrative scope.
1.4
Thesis structure
Chapter 2 is a presentation and explanation of the state of art for academia and indus-
try standards in IDS and cooperative games. Chapter 3 provides an explanation of the
research design applied for this thesis in terms of gathering data, testing and validating.
Chapter 4 is a presentation of the developed prototype’s design, with a specific focus on
the narrative modelling framework and gameplay design decisions. Chapter 5 presents
the technical qualities of the prototype along with a description of the implementation
process. Together chapter 6 and 7 constitutes this thesis’ analysis and discussion sec-
tions. Chapter 6 has its premise in design theory and collected data while chapter 7 is
a technical analysis based on the developed prototype. In these sections the researchers
will present, interpret and analyse the thesis’ findings. Chapter 8 is where the research’s
conclusion is presented along with critical reflections on the research process and possi-
bilities for future research.

CHAPTER 2
State of the Art
Interactive storytelling has, to some degree, made its way into most kinds of entertain-
ment media. In the 19th century Richard Wagner saw a dissolving of the fourth wall
of theatre, bringing the audience into the play as actors and seemed to them to be as
expansive as the real world[25]. This is now formally known as interactive- or immersive
theatre. In books the ”Choose Your Own Adventure” series[4] became popular in the
80’s, due to their innovative take on traditional linear novels. Numerous projects have
later attempted to fill this seemingly empty void of true interactive storytelling, such as
interactive cinema, videos, installations, early virtual reality and happenings[25]. How-
ever, most of these pioneering attempts failed to reach a broad audience and fell into the
obscure and intellectual, with little to no impact on how the general public experienced
stories[25]. It was with the dawn of popular video culture that interactive storytelling
received an increased focus, both in the general gaming industry as well as academia.
2.1
Interactive Digital Storytelling
In the early 2000’s a sometimes heated debate erupted between two research areas in
regards to video games[45]. On one side were the self-proclaimed ludologists, who wanted
to see the research focus shift onto the mechanics of gameplay.
On the other side
the narratologists were found, who were interested in studying games alongside other
storytelling media[40]. The debated issue concerned whether interactivity could exist
alongside a narrative. Many argued against this, stating that interactivity in its essence
is the opposite to narrative, since narrative requires author direction, while interactivity
depends on player power[3, 42, 20].
This has also been referred to as the narrative
paradox, which is described by Bruni et al. as: ”the problem of having a free-roaming
interactive world and an author-controlled narrative at the same time”[11]. The debate
never came to a conclusion, although Jannet Murray declared the end of the debate with
her ”final word” stating that:
“Not one group can define what is appropriate for the study of games. Game
studies, like any organized pursuit of knowledge, is not a zero-sum team
contest, but a multi-dimensional, open-ended puzzle that we all are engaged
in cooperatively solving”[38, p. 3].
The research field of IDS is focused on bridging this gap between narrative and mechanics
and thus developing a new perspective on how we analyse, design, and interpret games.

2.1.1
IDS design principles
A narrative experience in existing forms of entertainment media, such as film and books,
is often heavily influenced by an extensive body of design conventions[44]. In books,
various plot structures exists, in order for the authors to more easily build and present
their material.
In films, a number of design conventions exists in terms of framing
particular shots or post-editing. Such types of design heuristics have also started to
emerge in the field of IDS.
Strohecker describes a set of design recommendations in regards to IDS and video games,
including even pacing, continuous interaction and the ability to navigate freely[69]. She
proposes the approach of having a fixed narrative structure alongside interaction through
character development. Expanding on Stroheckers design principles, Koenitz presents
four design heuristics as means to expand the tool box for designing interactive narra-
tives[45].
The cyberbardic principle, inspired by Murrays description of the ”cyberbard”[56], stands
in strong contrast to the traditional narrative authoring. The cyberbard aims to show
instead of telling. He does so by creating opportunities for the player to explore and
experience. The narrative thus becomes a result of the interaction between what the
cyberbard has build and how the player chooses to interact with it. Being a cyberbard
means to be a narrative architect and stands in close relation to the concept of spatial
storytelling, which is to be discussed further in section 2.1.1.
The initial interest principal is focused on setting the stage for the upcoming narrative
==PAGE:15==
2.1 Interactive Digital Storytelling
8
experience. This can be done by either starting from a challenge, such as a direct task
that the player has to overcome, confusion as in a question or something that is unsolved,
or finally an abundance of choices and opportunities for the player to explore.
The principle of continued motivation is similar to Strohecker’s recommendation of con-
tinued interaction. This principle is revolved around providing the player with constant
qualitative narrative content. Koenitz defines several strategies that can be applied in
order to achieve this, such as narrative gaps that leaves space for the player to fill, tem-
porary removal of control so that the player feels more significant when in control, and
surprises, although they should be used carefully to avoid the player feeling cheated.
Koenitz highly emphasises three design strategies for continued player motivation:
• Ambiguous choices: Strong antagonistic choices, such as good/evil, leads to pre-
dictable outcomes, which quickly can become boring for the player. Ambiguous
choices allows the narrative to become more nuanced and engaging.
• Delayed consequences: The constant loop of narrative actions leading to narrative
consequences can become repetitive and predictable. Designing choices so that the
consequences are not immediately apparent is a way to combat this. However, the
player needs to understand that this is a mechanic of the game, as Koenitz states
”A key aspect of delayed consequences is to make the integrator aware that her
choice could matter in the future.”
• Proper scripting of character: This strategy is heavily related to roleplaying. The
designer needs to make sure that the player is cast in a specific role and given
sufficient information in order to be able to perform well.
Koenitz final principle is that of opportunity magnitude. Whereas continued motivation
was about narrative quality, opportunity magnitude is more related to narrative quantity.
The inherent problem in providing choices to players is that a project will grow more
in width and less in length. Koenitz argues, that the designers of interactive narratives
should prioritise interactive opportunities over length of experience. Furthermore, he
argues that a narrative does not have to be fully specified, since players will fill in the
blanks, and that the use of IDS stagecraft, meaning giving convincing impressions of
non-existing alternative narrative paths, may be utilised to great effect.
Riedl et al. propose a set of properties that is to be included when designing a multiplayer
interactive narrative[64]:
• Multiplayer differentiability: Multiple players can participate in the same game
but with the potential for them experiencing different narratives.
The system
must support each players’ individual narrative experience, while simultaneously
delivering a coherent overarching narrative experience.
• Authorability: The author can articulate how different player experiences should
unfold within the narrative.
==PAGE:16==

• Robustness: The system should be able to handle situations, where unanticipated
player behaviour, leads to the story not providing guidance on how to alter the
narrative experience.
Riedl et al. state, that when a story includes multiple playable characters, the task of
anticipating all potential player behaviour decreases, thus making it significantly more
difficult for a human author to control the narrative experience. In relation to their
three defined properties, Riedl et al. cast this as a trade-off between authorability and
robustness in the face of multiplayer differentiability[64, p. 190].
Spatial storytelling
When the debate between ludologist and narratologists settled down, the concept of spa-
tial storytelling, or environmental storytelling, emerged[40]. A major contributor to this
concept is Henry Jenkins with his paper ”Game Design as Narrative Architecture”[40].
Jenkins initially introduces evocative spaces, a narrative structure that draw upon pre-
viously existing narrative competencies[40]. Such stories utilises genre traditions, pre-
existing stories, or franchises to evoke a narrative experience without the need for a
specific and well-defined story, as Jenkins puts it[40]: ”They can paint their worlds in
fairly broad outlines and count on the player to do the rest.”
Secondly, Jenkins presents the concept of Enacting Stories. Taking a more general ap-
proach, Jenkins states that ”...when we discuss games as stories, we are referring to games
that either enable players to perform or witness narrative events...”[40]. Jenkins empha-
sises the term environmental storytelling, in which the organising of the plot becomes
a matter of designing the gaming space and thus facilitating the player’s forward move-
ment towards resolution[40]. Furthermore, Jenkins introduces the term micronarratives,
defining them as localised incidents or events that help produce profound emotional im-
pact[40]. These events may be presented through cut-scenes, in-game events, animations
or dialogue options. An example of a micronarrative is the voiced dialogue that happens
in the later editions of the Grand Theft Auto series where members of an opposing gang
will threaten you if you walk too close to them. It has no direct impact on the plot but
it conveys an emotion of anger and fear, and reinforces the rivalry that exists within the
narrative.
Thirdly, Jenkins describes Embedded Narratives. This is where ”...the game world be-
comes a kind of information space, a memory palace.”[40]. This is a heavily adopted
technique that is applied in almost all modern AAA titles. The narrative is enforced
through the environment, either in symbolic meaning such as the post-apocalyptic en-
vironment in The Last of Us[26], or more direct like the audiotapes in BioShock[1] that
conveys prior happenings within the story world.
The last narrative structure that Jenkins presents is the Emergent Narrative.
In an
emergent narrative there is no pre-structured or pre-programmed narrative, yet it is not

as unstructured, chaotic and frustrating as life itself[40]. The perhaps most popular
example of this kind of narrative is The Sims[52]. Everything about the design of The
Sims is there to establish narrative potential. The characters have emotional values,
reactions, and desires.
Some of these attributes are decided by the player from the
beginning of the game, others develop over time, sometimes to much surprise of the
player. The story of any The Sims gaming session emerges through play directly linked
to the choices of the player.
2.1.2
Player agency and authorial intent
Interactive narrative systems are build to engage players in a story while allowing their
actions to directly impact the direction and/or outcome of the story[64]. Thue et al.
refer to this as agency from story and it is a central focus point within the field of
IDS[79]. However, Thue et al. distinct between theoretical agency, being the players’
objective ability to change the course of ones experience, and perceived agency, being the
players’ subjective perception of ones ability to make such changes. This is an interesting
point of view, similar to Koenitz principle of continued motivation, that highlights the
importance of delivering choices in games that are meaningful to the player.
One of the challenges of designing a game is to choose to what degree player agency will
occur. This can formally be translated to designing the mechanics of a game. If the
player is able to jump a certain height, then the player can reach areas of that height.
However, if the jump height is increased then the player becomes capable of reaching
more areas, allowing for more agency. This illustrates a competing requirement between
design intentions and player agency. Riedl et al. argue, that an equal challenge lies in
the relation between authorial intent, being the story intended by a human author, and
player agency[64]. In films and books what you see is what you get, there is no user
interaction and thus there is no agency, everything is planned and delivered according
to the authors intentions.
In terms of interactive storytelling Riedl et al. define a spectrum that describes this
relation between player agency and authorial intent. ”On one end of the spectrum, we
have Choose-Your-Own-Adventure novels, in which a tree of plot points is completely
pre-authored. On the other end of the spectrum, a generative drama manager constructs
the story from scratch based in response to the behaviours of the player”[64, p. 189].
The Choose Your Own Adventure books are a typical example of what is called a branch-
ing narrative. This type of narrative is constructed as an interconnected network con-
sisting of nodes (chunks of content, information, or places) and paths (the connection
between nodes), typically depicted as a graph, network, or flowchart[51]. Multiple dif-
ferent kinds of branching exists. On figure 2.2 two branching structures are depicted.
The foldback structure offers smaller branching parts that eventually lead to the same
endpoint, which helps the with branching getting out of hand. The general branching
figure shows a combination of different branching structures. In branching narratives,

nodes representing the narrative. There is rarely the possibility for the player to bend
or circumvent paths. This, as Riedl et al. argue, increases the ability for the author to

Another perspective that sheds a different light onto the question of agency and authorial
intend is that of Bruni et al.[11]. They define the two terms narrative intelligibility and
narrative closure. Narrative intelligibility is the process in which the audience receives
or generates meaning in a way that is close to what is intended by the author[11, p. 18].
Narrative closure is the process where the audience may construct its own meaning out
of what is being mediated, independent on whether that meaning corresponds or gets
close to what is intended by the author[11, p. 18]. Bruni et al. argue that even if there
is no intended intelligible message (narrative intelligibility), narrative close may still be
reached. This signifies that a narrative is being generated in the player’s mind, despite
it not being intended by the author, this is what happens in an emergent narrative. It
can then be suggested that narrative closure without narrative intelligibility is generated
through agency.
Multiple different projects in academia have been composed that attempts to strike a
balance between agency and authorial intend. In FAÇADE[51] the player interacts with
two autonomous agents in a virtual world using natural written language. A drama
manager is implemented which continuously manages the behaviour of the agents, which
creates a space for social interaction simulation. However, several plot points and general
character behaviour is pre-authored. The player is able to impact the discussion quite
significantly, which creates for a deep interactive drama.
The IN-TALE[63] system
also utilises a drama manager that automatically generates a narrative, based on a pre-
authored non-branching narrative plan. The system is thus capable of adapting to the

MUSE
The Multiplayer Storytelling Engine’s (MUSE) goal is to establish an interactive narra-
tive within a multiplayer setting. MUSE combines human-authored story content and
robust generative execution processes that handles exceptions that are unanticipated by
the human author[64, p. 191]. The engine supports multiplayer scaling and is easy to
use by authors and designers, since it requires no programming or manipulation of AI.
MUSE represents its narrative in a variation of Colored Petri Nets (CPNs) with scenes,
being the spaces that players can venture to, players, being the players in the game,
and transitions, being the players progression through the scenes. A scene can only be
executed when a set of preconditions are satisfied within the current world state. This
means that the actions of players in a previous scene will determine which scene they
are presented with next. Riedl et al. categorise such player actions based on how they
affect the story[64]:
• Contingent: An action performed in line with the author’s expectations, thus
progressing the story.
• Consistent: An action that changes the world in a way that neither assists nor
hinders progression of the story.
• Exception: An action that alters the world in a way that hinders story progression,
e.g. by making a future player or NPC action illegal or nonsensical.
MUSE handles exceptions by instantiating new scenes that bridge between the unexpect-
edly modified world state. As such, the system is effectively authoring new branches to
the story module, and thus augments the author’s intentions by returning the world
state to one intended by the author[64, p. 192].
2.1.3
Narrative Game Mechanics
Game mechanics are tools that the player use to interact with a game. In the field of
IDS, it has been argued that this interaction inherently conveys a type of narrative[29,
49]. Dubbelman takes a cognitive approach to understanding narratives, arguing that:
”...mental stories are created when our embodied minds, in an effort to makes sense
of the surrounding world, process rational and visceral information, deriving from an
interaction with this surrounding world”[29, p. 40]. This means that stories essentially
are generated by the player as mental stories when interacting with the game, regardless
of there being a narrative text or predefined narrative events. Jãrvinen defines mechanics
as: .̈..means to guide the player into particular behaviour by constraining the space
of possible plans to attain goals”[39, p. 254]. These deliberate design decisions that
Jãrvinen’s definition entails, such as guiding or constraining the player, further explains

of why narrative can be mediated through mechanics. Larsen et al. argue that designing
game mechanics is like choosing the framing in a movie[49]. It is through these decisions
that the designer chooses what the player can do and what they value, and embedded
in this interaction lies a message.
To give an example of how game mechanics can create narrative, one can look to the
cooperative game Left 4 Dead 2[18]. It takes place in a zombie infested world and thus
has a strong evocative space. Most players will be familiar with narrative events in this
genre, due to its popularity in general entertainment culture. Some of the more common
events are survivors being overwhelmed by increasing amounts of zombies, survivors
getting separated from each other, survivors being picked off by zombies one at a time,
or survivors getting low on resources[29]. There is very little typical narrative mediation
in Left 4 Dead 2, besides sparse pre-scripted dialogue between characters, the majority
of narrative events is manifested through game mechanics and design rules. An example
of this interaction is how the game evokes the situation of a player being separated from
the group. Resources are scattered throughout the levels of the game, which creates
for situations where a player might leave the group in order to replenish his resources.
Players are essentially enacting a story and the designers have been clever enough to
imitate these well-known narrative events. Visibility is a significant part of Left 4 Dead 2
as well, players often venture into mazelike structures or dense forests with rain and mist
clouding the area. This kind of environmental storytelling, which can be directly linked
to the level design rules of Left 4 Dead 2, is also something that enforces the specific
narrative event of getting separated from the group. Lastly, and perhaps the mechanic
that displays this narrative event the best, is ”special zombies” who’s sole purpose is to
try and grab, pull, or push players far away from the rest of the group.
2.2
Cooperative gameplay
In order to consider IDS within a cooperative environment, it is necessary to first asses
design patterns and evaluation metrics in cooperative game design. It is important to
consider the goal of these game systems compared to the goal of their inherent narratives.
Bruni et al. add perspective on this through their definitions of narrative closure and
narrative intelligibility. Both terms are to be interpreted on the system level and narra-
tive level. As earlier stated, narrative intelligibility is when the experienced narrative of
the audience is in line with the intentions of the author, and narrative closure is found
when the audience experience narrative independent of the authors intentions. Bruni
et al. argue that this is true at the system level as well, meaning that system level
intelligibility is experienced as meaningful interactions with the system as intended by a
designer. System level closure is experienced as meaningful interactions with the system
independent of the intentions of the designer. Consequently, it is important to under-
stand that intelligibility and/or closure is not necessarily desirable only on a narrative
level, and can thus be achieved on the system level. Three overall cases for relating goals
==PAGE:21==

14
of a narrative and goals of a game system are presented by Bruni et al.
In the first case, the goal of the system is to mediate the narrative itself. These games
can be referred to as narrative-led experiences and has their primary premise in reaching
some form of narrative intelligibility and/or closure[11]. This is generally observed in
interactive narrative games and projects.
The second case is when the role of the narrative is subsidiary to other goals of the system.
The narrative becomes a resource for the system, in order to achieve its goals[11]. This
is seen in the MMORPG World of Warcraft[33], where narrative is integrated within the
quest system, but nonessential to large parts of the rest of the game.
The last case is found when the narrative and the system have completely different
goals and are thus disconnected. The narrative might be a desirable resource, but it
is nonessential to the system[11]. An example of this is found in E-Sport games, such
as Counter Strike, where the setting gives a desirable narrative, but the true goal is to
optimise mechanical goals of the system.
2.2.1
Cooperative game design patterns
Multiplayer games tend to value the goals of the system more than the goals of the
narrative. Introducing more than one player into a game environment poses new design
challenges, requirements, and player expectations. Rocha et al. define six unique design
patterns for cooperative gameplay[41]:
• Complementarity: Characters complement each other with different roles within
the game. The most common example of this is the ”holy trinity” of tank, healer,
and damage dealer.
• Synergies between abilities: Different characters have different abilities, with an
inherent synergy between. An example of this is the vulnerability effect used in
most fantasy-themed multiplayer games. Character X applies Z-vulnerability to
an enemy and character Y now deals more damage due to him dealing Z-damage.
• Abilities that can only be used on another player: An example of this is the healing
or revive mechanic that exists in most cooperative games.
• Shared Goals: Shared goals creates incentive for players to work and play together.
• Synergies between goals (Interlaced/Intertwined goals): In the case of players hav-
ing different goals, a design approach that helps enforces cooperation is to create
synergy between these. A system that illustrates this is the shared experience in
Blizzard’s Heroes of the Storm[32] (HOTS). The specialist class usually has the
specific goal of destroying as many buildings and neutral forces as possible, while
the assassin class has the goal of assassinating enemy heroes. Since experience

is shared in HOTS, both classes are rewarded when each of them complete their
individual goal.
• Special Rules for players of the same Team: This design approach is heavily focused
on enforcing team cooperation within a game. The best example is friendly fire
(the ability to damage your own teammates), which, when absent, ensures positive
cooperation.
2.2.2
Cooperative game evaluation metrics
It is interesting to investigate public lists that rank video game narratives. What is
noticeable is that the vast majority of these games are singleplayer games[59, 58, 16].
Some of the more popular titles, such as the Uncharted series[27], The Last of Us,
BioShock and Mass Effect, all posses some form of multiplayer game-mode, but it is
never a part of the core narrative experience. In these cases multiplayer is implemented
as a side-experience, something to prolong the playability of the game. Although these
game modes often are exciting and well-crafted, there is little to no emphasis on the
narrative experience. In order to understand why these games generally are not valued
as great narrative games, it is important too inspect how cooperative games are evaluated
by players. El-Nasr et al. developed a set of evaluation metrics that can be applied to
evaluate and design cooperative gameplay[68].
• Laughter or excitement together: Players laughing over specific events or verbally
expressing that they enjoy the game.
• Worked out strategies: This metric has a lot to do with communication. Players
actively solving problems together or consulting each other while navigating the
world.
• Helping: Explaining mechanics to one another or utilising healing/revive mechan-
ics.
• Global Strategies: Events that refer to situations where players take on roles in
gameplay that complements each others’ responsibilities.
• Waited for each other: To wait for each other tends to build frustration. Waiting
can for example happen if the skill gap between players is too large.
• Got in each others’ way: This metric is defined as when players want to do different
actions, which inevitably hinters or interferes with each others’ goals, which may
have a negative impact on the player experience.
The metrics defined by El-Nasr et al. show that a significant part of cooperative gameplay
evaluation happens in the interaction between players, rather than interactions between
players and the game.
As such, the narrative of a cooperative game often becomes
a tale between the players playing the game, rather than a tale between characters

within the game. Eladhari et al. describe this phenomenon as self-play, which is playing
as oneself rather than a fictional character[30]. Such narratives often revolve around
when a specific player did something good or bad, or when a large team fight took place.
These narratives are completely disconnected from the individual characters of the world
in which the game takes place. This is similar to how a game of Monopoly[37] might
invoke the story of how fortunes are won or lost, but in reality what players remember
is the experience of moving around the board landing on someone’s real estate[40].
2.3
Industry standards in narrative-led games
As previously stated, the most popular interactive storytelling games are developed as
singleplayer experiences, with some of more prominent titles being: The Last of Us, the
Mass Effect series, The Witcher seires and Telltale’s The Walking Dead. These games
all emphasise on mediating interesting and compelling stories, but have done so using
different design methods.
Taking into account the previously mentioned titles, it is noticeable that a game such
as The Last of Us to a higher degree focuses on authorial intend. The narrative of the
game follows a linear path with very few, if any, deviations from the main plot. The
world is tattered with environmental storytelling that evokes the game’s strong zombie-
infestation theme. The Witcher and Mass Effect series however has a much larger focus
on story agency with huge branching narratives and an abundance of narrative choices
for the player. Yet, all of these games has a strong focus on two design features, namely
roleplaying and choices.
2.3.1
Roleplaying
Roleplaying means that a player will change their behaviour to assume a role of a fictional
character.
Players will act according to this adopted role and have their actions be
heavily influenced by this new personality[30].
An important part of roleplaying is
thus to understand and develop the character. Game designers refer to this concept as
character building and utilise different techniques to emphasise it.
Character building
A very utilised feature associated with character building, is the character creation fea-
ture seen in most modern RPGs. This feature allows players to make choices on which
class they want to play, their physical appearance, and perhaps where in the world they
come from. This allows players either to make up fictional characters, or characters that
mimic their own appearance. However, a big problem with this penalisation is that it
usually has no impact on the game’s narrative[76]. As such, it becomes an aesthetic
feature, which might generate perceived agency but never theoretical agency.
==PAGE:24==
2.3 Industry standards in narrative-led games
17
Another approach to character building is by having pre-scripted characters with distinct
backgrounds and personalities. Having playable pre-scripted characters is a method used
to strengthen the relation a player holds to their character. As one expert in narrative
from the preliminary questionnaires stated:
“You have to look to analogue roleplaying for much more interesting and
layered interactions - character’s interaction rather than player interaction.
E.g. with pre-scripted characters, you can design background stories that
gradually come into play, and unlock events and even emotional responses.”
This also shifts the player choices more in the direction of character choices, which allows
for much more nuanced player decisions[21]. BioWare refers to this concept of playing as
fully or partly fleshed out characters as objective storytelling[80]. Objective storytelling
stands in contrast to subjective storytelling, which is more in line with traditional role-
playing where the playable character is a blank slate[36], which the players then fill with
their own imagined persona. In a subjective story the responses are often verbatim with
the player being silent, as seen in BioWare’s Knights of The Old Republic, which leads to
temporal distortions and a need for lots of dialogue[80]. BioWare argues that objective
storytelling combats these challenges by having pre-defined characters with voices and
personalities. This notion is further enforced by the fact that other interactive narrative
titles, such as The Witcher and The Last of Us, are utilising objective storytelling as
well.
In cooperative games
Within cooperative games some of the same tools as in singleplayer games, such as
background stories and character creation systems, are applied to generate character
building. However, they usually have even less of an impact on the games narrative.
An example of this is the characters of Diablo III. Each character has a pre-scripted
background story, but when playing cooperatively there can be multiple characters with
the exact same background story. Furthermore, the only narrative difference between
playing as four monks or playing as four unique characters is scattered voice dialogues
between players. These dialogues do facilitate narrative, but without any meaningful
impact. In Left 4 Dead 2 characters are unique, meaning that there can only be one of
each character within a cooperative session. This enhances character building by letting
players roleplay as that specific character.
In addition to this, each character has a
distinct physical appearance, e.g. the big brute or the slick businessman.
An interesting aspect of character building in cooperative narrative-led games is the
relationship between the player’s characters. In a game such as Divinity: Original Sin,
which has a player limit of two, players get to make decisions together, engaging in
dialogue between characters rather than between players. Another example of this is
Brothers: A Tale of Two Sons. The game is technically a singleplayer game, but the
designers themselves describe it as cooperative singleplayer experience, due to the game’s

2.3 Industry standards in narrative-led games
18
major focus on the relationship between the two main characters.
In this game the
player play as two brothers, a younger and an older brother. The relationship between
the brothers is emphasised greatly, both in the narrative as well as in the mechanics,
with the older brother having to protect, lift, and push the younger brother, whom for
example can fit into tiny spaces. Within the narrative the player learns that the younger
brother fears water, which, in terms of mechanics, means that he cannot swim. This
results that the brother has to carry him over water. Such mechanics truly emphasise
the relation between the characters.
In SWTOR there exists a strong evocative narrative due to the Star Wars franchise. In
the game’s character creation phase players get to chose between well-known archetypes
within the world of Star Wars, such as a Bounty Hunter, a Jedi Knight, or a Smuggler.
Due to the evocative narrative, players will not only understand the typical personality
of each archetype, but also their interconnected relations due to those relations being
displayed in other medias of the franchise.
2.3.2
Choices
Choices are an integrated part of the game medium, since they represent what makes
games unique, namely their interactivity. A very common technique, that is utilised
in interactive narrative games, is to represent game choices as dialogue options. This
generates player agency by allowing the player to either impact the world in which they
exist or by defining the personality of their own character. Often the dialogue sequences
serve as the primary system for developing branching narratives. Developing such a
system is however not an easy task, since taking choices is an essential part of human
life. People make choices constantly, either consciously or subconsciously, and to emulate
a system that reflects this is this not an easy task.
The authors of this thesis found two distinct design directions that is represented in the
industry by the games The Witcher 3 and Mass Effect 3. The Mass Effect series is renown
for its branching narratives and dialogue trees. The dialogue in Mass Effect is facilitated
by the so-called behavioural wheel. The wheel can hold up to six different responses that
is freely available for the player to pick and choose from. Each position on the wheel
is represented by a given categorical response, such as friendly, investigative, hostile,
or renegade. The same position will always respond to the same category. BioWare
has made this design in order to enhance predictability within the decision-making pro-
cess[80]. BioWare argues that predictability is important in order for the players to get
the results from their decisions that they expect[80]. Predictability is enhanced further
by paraphrasing each response option[80]. A paraphrase is a condensed version of the
actual response e.g. ”Tell me if there is anything i can do for you and your family” be-
comes ”Let me help”. Telegraphing is another technique used by BioWare to make sure
that the players have an idea of what their next decision might entail. Telegraphing can
be applied in different ways, such as through animations (e.g. a character cracking his
knuckles indication a potential aggressive behaviour) or textual context, namely the way
==PAGE:26==
2.3 Industry standards in narrative-led games
19
the response is written[80]. Telegraphing is also applied in other interactive storytelling
games, such as The Walking Dead and Heavy Rain, where the player is prompted when
a significantly more important decision is about to be made.
On the opposite side of the design spectrum, in regards to decisions and choices, the
design philosophy behind The Witcher 3 is found. In an interview with the lead writer
and the lead quest designer from The Witcher 3, they disclose three design assumptions
they have made about choices: Ambiguity, unpredictability, and weight[62]. Ambiguity
is strictly opposite to Mass Effect’s categorical responses. Responses in The Witcher
3 are not differentiated at the same level as in Mass Effect, instead responses becomes
”shades of grey”, rather than opposing moral directions[62]. As a result, the dialogue
system in The Witcher 3 becomes less apparent to the player and thus harder to figure
out, which leads directly into the second design assumption of unpredictability. At time
the player will have to chose between the lesser of two evils, e.g. executing a prisoner or
starting a town riot. Since such a decision is formulated with great ambiguity, it becomes
harder for the players to realise that bad things are going to happen no matter what.
As such, even though players respond with what they consider to be the most morally
appropriate, they might be surprised by the consequence. Looking at the third design
assumption, weight, refers to each decision having a significant emotional weight to them,
one might think that The Witcher 3 is an emotional minefield of choices, however given
the great success of the game this seems not to be the case.
In cooperative games
As one can imagine, narrative choices in a multiplayer setting becomes even more com-
plex and difficult to design. Two of the best examples of cooperative narrative-led games,
Divinity: Original Sin and SWTOR, however do utilise group decision-making.
In Divinity player choices are directly tied to a player trait, such as egotistical, heartless,
or forgiving. These traits directly enhances specific capabilities of the player. As such,
players do not only have the ability to roleplay in conversations, but also the ability to
strategies in regards to what trait will benefit their character the most. Furthermore,
choices in Divinity can lead to minor narrative events, and as such presents the characters
with story agency. When players have to make group decisions they each get to make
individual choices, if they do not select the same choice then they get to settle their
dispute in a mini game of rock, paper, scissors, with the winnings choice getting picked.
SWTOR is an MMORPG that allows players to make decisions in groups in up to
four players. When making a group decision each player gets different dialogue options
based on their character class. Furthermore, some decisions are antagonistic, essentially
requiring players to chose between good or evil, which contributes to either dark side or
light side points. These points have mechanical consequences for the player, by changing
their characters appearance and letting them wear different kinds of armour. Each time
players make a choice, a dice roll determines who gets to speak for the group. BioWare

choice, the ones in the group that chose the light side still gets light side points. This
is an important feature, since it retains the player-impacting decision to each individual

3.1
Research purpose
This thesis is conducted as an exploratory study with the object of seeking new in-
sights within a specific research field. Exploratory studies are advantageous when the
researchers wish to clarify their understanding of a problem, such as if they are unsure
of the precise nature of the problem[67, p. 139]. Saunders presents three principles of
conducting exploratory research[67, p. 140]:
• A search of the literature
• Interviewing ’experts’ on the subject
• Conducting focus group interviews.
This research utilises the two principles of literature research and expert interviews. Fo-
cus groups are group interviews that focuses clearly upon a particular issue, product,

Research method
Strategies of inquiry, methods of data collection and analysis vary in qualitative and
quantitative research methods [22]. Qualitative and quantitative data collection tech-
niques and analysis procedures posses both strength and weaknesses. Qualitative data
collection procedures generate non-numerical data, which is characterised by its richness,
based on the researcher’s opportunity to explore a subject in as real a manner as pos-
sible[67]. In contrast, quantitative data collection techniques generate or use numerical
data and are therefore based on meanings derived from numbers.
Quantitative data starts with the test of a theory, employs strategies of inquiry, such
as experiments and surveys, and collect data on predetermined instruments that yield
statistical data [22, p.

As such, the quantitative approach is best suited when
the researcher is seeking to identify influencing factors on known variables, or when

new, has had little research done on it, and needs to be understood than it merits a
qualitative approach[22, p. 23]. Qualitative research is exploratory as the important
variables to examine are unknown to the researcher. All data collected for this study is
of a qualitative nature because of the research subject of this thesis being fairly new and
with less surrounding research.

3.3
Prototyping
The product for this thesis was made with the concept of prototyping. A prototype is a
concrete but partial representation or implementation of a system design[6, p. 184]. Pro-
totypes can be used to demonstrate concepts in early stages, to test details of a specific
product in a large setting, or simply to frame, refine, and discover new possibilities in
a design space[6, p. 184]. When discussing prototypes, researchers often separate them
into two categories namely lo-fi and hi-fi prototypes[6, 43].
A lo-fi prototype, often refereed to as paper-prototypes since that usually is what they
are made of, are classified by focusing more on the broad underlying design ideas, such as
content, form, and structure[6, p. 187]. Lo-fi prototypes are useful, since they are quick
to be made and quick to be discarded, reducing costs and time spent. Furthermore,
lo-fi prototypes are highly well-suited for idea-generation and should always aid and
not hinder the process of generating and evaluating design solutions. However, when
designing a lo-fi prototype, a number practical issues to be concerned with are found.
Klein argues, that users do not interact with paper the same way they interact with
screens, ”they are in a different mind-set than they would be if you were to sit them
down at a computer or hand them a tablet. They simply do not have the context for
understanding what you’re trying to show them.”[43, p. 132]. This is indeed a valid point
and thus it should be considered primarily to use lo-fi prototypes for internal testing and
brain storming, unless developing a product includes touch-navigation or is intended to
be paper in its final iteration[43, p. 134]. Moreover, Benyon also argues that a potential
risk in spending too little time developing a lo-fi prototype exists and thus having to
explain everything, which could heavily bias a test session[62, p. 188].
A hi-fi prototype is more similar in look and feel, although not necessarily in functional-
ity, to the anticipated final product[6, ap. 185]. Hi-fi prototypes are useful for detailed
evaluation of the main design elements, such as content, visuals, interactivity, and func-
tionality[6, p. 185]. Hi-fi prototypes are usually created later in the process when design
ideas have been formulated and decided. Such prototypes are more time demanding
than lo-fi prototypes and developers can become reluctant to discard features developed
for a hi-fi prototype.

The design process included the following stages:
1. Paper phase (idea, to treatment, to flow diagram)
2. Prototype phase (check interaction and flow without final assets)
3. Production phase (create final assets, structure and interaction)
4. Testing phase (beta user testing, final adjustments)
In the paper phase the narrative is outlined and illustrated by using flow diagrams, in
order to visualise sequencing and choice points.
Such flow diagrams further include
variables that describes the system logic, such as if an item is needed to progress the
narrative, or if a time limit on a given event exists. The final part of the paper phase is
to create index cards as rough storyboard sketches for each visible element. The visual
elements are not included in this product’s paper phase due to the game being text-based
as mention in section 1.3.

Building a hi-fi prototype is superior to a lo-fi prototype, such as wireframing approaches,
due its ability to properly test interaction design and narrative pacing[45]. Furthermore,
in this phase unnecessary narrative elements are removed and missing ones are added. In
order to improve understanding of visuals of the game, the index cards from the previous
phase can be scanned and implemented within the prototype. The scope did however,
not include this visual element, as mentioned in section 1.3.
The Production phase primarily consists of system development, adjustment and struc-
ture. Further improvements on interaction design along with implementing assets should
be done at this stage.
At the testing phase the product is used by a group of beta-testers. This is done in order
to make final adjustments, with a particular focus on any unexpected user behaviour.
This beta-testing was replaced by the expert interviews mentioned in the later section

3.4
Data quality
Data quality refers to the credibility of the research findings. When collecting data there
is a number of data quality issues the researcher needs to be aware of. In order to reduce
the possibility of incorrect data the researcher has to pay attention to two particular
emphases on research design: reliability and validity[67, p. 156].
Reliability refers to whether the utilised data collection techniques or analysis proce-
dures will yield consistent findings. This can be assessed by the questions of whether
measures will yield the same results on other occasions, whether similar observations will
be reached by other observers, and whether there is transparency in how sense was made
from the raw data[67, p. 156]. Given that this research is an exploratory study utilising
qualitative, and thus less structured, research methods there is an inherent danger of
data becoming less reliable, since a highly structured methodology ensures reliability[67,
p. 125]. As such in relation to qualitative research, reliability is concerned with whether
alternative researchers would reveal similar information[67, p. 326].
Validity is concerned with whether the findings are really about what they appear to be
about[67, p. 157]. As such validity refers to the ability of the study design to answer
what was initially intended. Using qualitative research methods offers a higher degree
of data validity, due to researchers being able to clarify questions, probe for meaning of
responses and discuss topics from a variety of angles.
Data quality of preliminary questionnaires
Validity and reliability in the context of questionnaires largely relies on the design of
questions and the structure of the questionnaire[67, p. 371]. This entails that questions

are to be understood by the respondents in the way intended by the researchers.
In order to ensure validity of the questionnaires the researchers used clear wording of
questions and terms that was likely to be familiar to, and understood by, the respondents.
Furthermore, contextual descriptions, explaining problems or theoretical concepts, were
used to increase the chance of respondents understanding the questions. Given that the
questionnaires were formulated very early in the research process, the researchers recog-
nised that their current knowledge could be insufficient in terms of formulating precise

In order to asses reliability of questionnaires, meaning that their findings can be con-

collected through literature research. Furthermore a cross-examination of the question-
naires were made in order to show consistency between answers. A high consistency
between answers showed a high reliability of data gathered, however the researchers
acknowledge that the consistency could change, given a higher number of respondents.
Data quality of expert interviews
In strong relation to reliability in qualitative interviews is the concept of interviewer bias.
This is where the comments, tone, or non-verbal behaviour of the interviewer creates
bias in the way that interviewees respond to the questions being asked[67, p. 326].
In order to avoid interviewer bias and ensure a higher level of reliability from the expert
interviews, the researchers aimed at phrasing questions clearly and in a neutral tone of
voice. This was done in order to avoid asking leading questions and to minimise the pos-
sibility of misunderstandings. Furthermore the researchers aimed at reducing non-verbal
behaviour such as gestures that could cause any bias to the discussion. Another aspect
of interviewer bias is that the researcher has to obtain the trust of the interviewee, which
can also be described as the interviewer’s credibility. In order to ensure credibility a
thorough preparation was made before each interview as mentioned in the interview plan
in section 3.3.2. Having finished the preliminary research and the prototype, equipped
the researchers with a broad knowledge on the field of IDS. The ability to draw on
this information in order to probe questions and to discuss more specific subjects fur-
ther improved their credibility. Presenting a prototype, developed by the researchers,
contributed to the researchers credibility as well, which improved data validity.
Related to interviewer bias is the concept of interviewee bias or response bias. This
can appear if the interviewee is uncomfortable with the unstructured format that exists
in qualitative interviews[67, p. 327]. As such the interviewee may provide a partial
picture of the situation, which casts himself or herself in a ”socially desirable” role[67,

Design
The product developed for this thesis is a framework for designers and authors to utilise
when creating an interactive narrative game.
It is build upon IDS design principles
and has a specific focus on cooperative gameplay, thus also being heavily focused on
cooperative game design patterns. The purpose of the implementation of the game and
the design model, is to explore how cooperative IDS can be implemented in practice,

4.1
Findings from preliminary questionnaires
The developed game and story engine are based on a thorough research of literature
and industry standards.
As a part of the industry standards research, a qualitative
questionnaire was distributed to two experts in narrative-led game development. This
section will be presenting the findings of the preliminary questionnaires.
The following categories can be derived from the data gathered:
• Creating interactive narratives
• Interaction between players
• Story agency
Creating interactive narratives: Both experts argue that creating a narrative experience
within a game is different from other medias. This is partly due to the media being
new and thus not having a set format. As such, one expert argued how she had an
exploratory approach to designing narratives in games. As a result, if the story did not
fit within a narrative model, or had a specific narrative arc, she would not try to force
one onto it. One expert explained how conveying information in a game is extremely
important. The designer has to ensure that each event within a narrative is adequate,
in terms of narrative mediation, if it has to stand on its own. If this is not the case,
then that same adequate amount of information has to be provided to the player in
moments leading up to the event. There lies an inherent design challenge in ensuring
that this information is correctly seen, interpreted and understood by the player. One
expert explained how developing an interactive narrative is less about the story that the
==PAGE:38==
4.1 Findings from preliminary questionnaires
31
designer wants to create, and more about giving the players what they need to tell the
story. This could be a setting, a set of character NPCs, a rich persona for their playable
character, or background stories that will unlock future events. One expert argued, that
it can be difficult to create new forms of narratives in games, because players are used
to a certain kind of narrative structure. As a result, designers have to be aware that
innovating on this part of game development can alienate the audience.
Interaction between players: Both experts argued, that designing interaction between
players is an important aspect of cooperative narrative-led game design. This interaction
is important because it facilitates the unwritten rules of social interaction within a
game. When interaction between players is done poorly, players can be less empathetic
towards one another, which can result in a less enjoyable experience. Some approaches
to facilitate interaction between players are by having players make in-game choices
together, trading with each other, supplementing each others abilities, or being in a group
together. Both experts highlighted how it was important to implement mechanics that
would reward successful cooperation, in order to incentivise cooperation. Furthermore,
it should be taken into consideration that some players tend to be dominant voices,
setting the agenda for the entire team. In order to create interesting and meaningful
choices for players, the designer should consider both the mechanical as well as the
social aspect of group decisions. The social aspect is enhanced by making a decision
feel fair and not be a popularity contest between players. The mechanical aspect can be
enhanced by designing it as a risk/reward scenario where players understand that there
can be advantages and disadvantages associated with agreeing or disagreeing with their
teammate.
Agency: Both experts highlighted the importance of player agency, with one expert
mentioning it as a trade-off from writer authority. Agency was described as the players’
ability to influence a story, which could have the potential effect of increased player
investment. One expert argued that it is more simple to develop stories with high levels
of writer authority, however, it results in games becoming less interactive, in terms of
narrative, and thus feel more like playable movies, which can be deeply dissatisfying
for some gamers.
It was further argued, that agency increases immersion, due to it
being the player’s actions that directly progress the narrative. An important aspect of
designing agency in story is to ensure that the player, to a certain degree, understands
how their actions influence the narrative. Designing branching narratives in video games
was argued by one expect to always be preferable to a linear narrative, given that each
project had equal resources. This was described as being due to branching narrative’s
inherent agency and thus potential increased player investment.

4.2
Modelling language for events and actions
4.2.1
Process of evaluating models
The modelling language described in this section has been created through a process of
several iterations. In the first iteration a simple flow diagram represented every choice
in the game as decision elements. Each process element in the flow diagram represented
the encounters that players could experience.
This was an incredibly simple way of
depicting a typical branching narrative and did very little to innovate on the technique.
In a later iteration player profile, such as a power-gamer, explorer or storyteller, would
be determined through player choices. Player profiles would be utilised by the system
to provide narrative content to the players that fit with their profile’s associated play
style. In this model, the transitions between each element represented the effect a choice
would have on set player profile. Furthermore, players would have a reputation amongst
NPCs, such as respected, trusted or feared. This model had a large focus on authorial
intent of the story, as described in section 2.1.1, however it did not allow for multiplayer
differentiability, described in section 2.1.1. The model did not provide any meaningful
cooperation between players, as such it only modelled the narrative for a single player.
In a later iteration, a flow diagram was created that described actions and events as
elements. The relation between these elements would represent a nested hierarchy rather
than a progression through the game. This was closer to the final design of the modelling
language. In this iteration multiplayer differentiability was decided to be implemented
with the previously mentioned player profile system, where the actions and choices, that
were available to a player, would be based on previously taken actions. However, the
design logic did not describe the flow of the story and it was thus very difficult to reach
the level of authorability that was required for the model to be usable by authors and
designers. Furthermore the player profile system led to significant exponential branching,
due to the large amount of player profiles that the system constantly had to compare
against the player’s actions. As an example, in each event a power-gamer would need
two choices related to his player profile, a neutral choice related to no player profile and
a probe choice related to a different player profile. The probe choice was intended to
validate whether the player was categorised as the correct player profile. As a result,
at the end of this iteration and before the final iteration, the player profile system was
discarded.
Reaching the final design
The final iteration of the modelling language simulates a real-time version of a game. This
framework is part of a large unit called the story engine. The story engine contains the
modelling language, parsing of this to data, and a game generator based on this data. In
the following section the modelling language will be described in details. Implementation
of the later elements of the story engine is described in chapter 5.
==PAGE:40==
4.3 Elements of the model
33
Designers and programmers collaborate to implement the constructed narrative scenarios
that goes into a game. One significant aspect of working together is developing a common
terminology. Having cross-professional tools can be an important part of formulating
such a terminology. The modelling language developed for this product, was initially a
common modelling method used to design narratives. Through iterations it developed
towards a technical approach, attempting to find the middle ground between technical
implementation, modelling narrative, and designing a game.
One approach to structuring branching narrative is a directed graph, e.g. Petri-Net as
directed bipartite graphs[23]. This graph represents the different states that players can
transition from and to in a game. The arrows in these graphs describe how the player
will be progressing through the game. It is thus describing where and how the player
will be progressing through the model and not describing the game world itself. This
modelling language aims at making it possible for players to visit and revisit states while
having the model describe this in combination with the narrative structure.
The more explicit details of the modelling language have been developed through the
same phases as the entire prototype of the story engine. In the paper phase it was laid
out to accomplish the narrative structure and acted only a paper-prototype which had
not been played through. During the prototype and production phase where it was used
in the story engine to generate the game, a more specific and explicit understanding of
all elements became clear. In the testing phase where the modelling language was used
within the game, it was tested in collaboration with the game implementation.
Players wants to accomplish goals and move forward in the game world. Games create
this feeling of progress in the state of the game. To the player, this progress can be
new opportunities, events, and goals. Multiple design strategies exists on how to model
experiences in the game, dramatic arcs, relationships between objectives, and motion of
the game. Designers are keen on explaining and modelling the story and experience in
this type of way. From an implementation perspective, the architecture of the game code
is generally implemented in a different way[77], which focuses on the happened-before
relationship model[48].
The state of the game consists of many types of information. In this product, the state is
the collection of varying information about the player. Player’s location, current quest,
current collection of prerequisites, and already taken actions. The game progresses and
each action made by the player will move the game into a new state for the player, where
one of more of these information has been changed.

4.3.1
Modelling language as PDDL
Planning Domain Definition Language (PDDL) is a standardisation of planning lan-
guages in the field of AI[53]. This language models how an AI agent can act inside a
problem domain. In this thesis, the problem domain can be thought of as the game
world. Actions describe what the causality for the game world and the agent will be.
The modelling language for this thesis has many similarities to how this planning lan-
guage is constructed. In the game and modelling language the agent will be the player.
The components of PDDL are objects, predicates, initial state, goal specification, and
actions (also noted as operators).
Objects are elements, in the problem domain, the agent can interact with. This could
be a location in the game world or an NPC the player can talk with. These objects are
both what the agent can use to interact with and upon, i.e. a robot arm moving a red
ball. In this case, both the robot arm and the red ball are defined as objects in PDDL.
The modelling language in this thesis does not to try to define which game mechanics a
player can use to interact with the world and objects. As such, this modelling language
is not of any use to define these game mechanics.
Predicates are functions that evaluate objects. This function takes the objects as param-
eters and return true if the condition described by this function is true for the object. In
the language of PDDL, an example of a predicate could be ”Has(key)”. A predicate is
true, if it exist in the collection of predicates gathered by the AI agent within the PDDL
language. In order to be in this collection, predicates are either a part of the initial state
of the problem or added to this collection through actions defined within the problem.
The same is true for the modelling language that is developed for this thesis. Actions
made by the player will add these predicates (prerequisite in the modelling language) to
the player’s collection.
As there is arguably a strong connection between predicates and objects, they have been
eliminated in this modelling language. In order to do this, an example show how this
have been done: the ”Has(key)” predicate is defined as ”Key” and ”!Key”. The key is
not defined as an object within the game, but solely as a prerequisite. This elimination
was done to generalise the concept of ”key”. The key is now an abstract representation
of narrative logic, rather than a representation of game elements.

would also have effects. The effects of an action happen if the predicates are all true.

4.3.2
Blocks
The modelling language consists of elements of information, e.g. text description, pre-
requisites and action elements. All of this information is combined together in what is
called blocks. These blocks can be perceived as states in the narrative that the player can
experience or choose as a state to enter. Transitions to and from these blocks are defined
by game mechanics and many different approaches to this exist, such as travelling in a
3D world, talking to an NPC by clicking on him, or time based events. As such, game
mechanics are not part of this modelling language. An example of what these blocks of
information look like, is illustrated in figure 4.2. Each block is connected by arrows, the
top most one is a block consisting of a red location element together with a prerequisite
and an NPC element.
Each block has a unique identifier used internally in the implementation as an identifier
token. This identifier is never presented to the player, as it is only meant to be referenced
internally in the implementation.
The reason for this choice stems from the entity-
relationship model[12], where having a unique identification number (ID) is essential for
storing information and being able to search for this information efficiently.
Between each block in the model is a directed arrow, denoting a relationship between
each connected block. The arrow is not a direction of which the player is progressing
through the game, and does not limit the movement of the player in the game world. As
explained in section 4.2, directed graphs are commonly used to denote the progression
of the player. However, it was chosen not to follow this strategy, in order to allow the
player to visit a block multiple times.
Arrows describe a relationship between blocks with parents in the top and children below.
Each block can either have zero or one parent. There is no limit to the amount of children
a parent can have. Each parent limits access to the children by its prerequisites. Having
access to the parent state and abiding the prerequisites will give access to first generation
children of the parent.
4.3.3
Effects
A player transitioning to a block in the model can be thought of as either through
travelling in the world, starting an event, or taking an action. Effects are illustrated in
figure 4.3 as the blue elements in the block. If the prerequisites contained in the block
are true for the player, the player will receive the effects on the block. Inside the blue
elements the new prerequisites that are now true for the player are stored. This signifies
that the prerequisites are added to the collection of prerequisites for the player. As such,
an effect for the player is that new prerequisites are now true.
Prerequisites can overrule each other. If a negative version of a prerequisite is gained
from an effect and the positive version already exists in the player’s collection, it will be

Actions
Action elements represent choices for the player when an event is started. Namely, they

as a child object of an event. They are never themselves the root blocks, however, an
action can have new actions as children. Actions can be combined with prerequisites

what the effects of taking this action will be.

Priority and overruling
In a group of two or more players, the model does not assume a common agreement
between player actions. In the game, prerequisites might be true only for individual
players in the group. As a result, it is still necessary for all player sessions to know
about which actions have been taken.
Actions taken by a group in an event might not happen in the order thought by the
designer. The dialogues and descriptions can here be reordered by a priority value on
the action description, as illustrated in figure 4.5. A designer can choose the precedence
of actions that will then be described by the priority value of this modelling language.
In this case, the priority value has precedence in decreasing order, meaning that ”1”
must be shown before ”2”. The numerical values on the action descriptions are different
from each other inside the same event. This ensures unambiguity for ordering of the
conversations.
Actions taken by the player might need to interrupt the game flow of other group mem-
bers from a design perspective.
An example could be whether to kill or rescue the
prisoner in a prison cell. If one player chooses to kill the prisoner and the other chooses
to rescues him, the designer will have to decide which action happens, as the model
would allow both actions to go through. The way to implement this in the modelling
language is to use an overrule description. The overrule element specifies actions that
are disallowed if this action is taken, these linked actions will be ignored if they are
chosen. In order to remove these ignored actions, it is necessary to have a global ef-
fect containing prerequisites to disable these actions for all players. Otherwise it would
mean that overruled actions are still present, but these should just be stopped if taken

This overrule description gives a strong power for the designers to affect many elements
in the game and make powerful actions for the player’s characters. The overrule function
acts like a prerequisite, by defining what cannot be done under a specific circumstance.
The difference is that it will overrule actions taken by the group.
It will not on its

4.3.5
Prerequisites
In adventure games, players have the notion of key A opens door B. This can be described
as A before B in a happened-before relationship. This simple example is the start of a
larger and more complex logic language, known as propositional logic[47]. Prerequisites
in the logic are closely related to the operators of propositional logic. In this product,
three of the operators from propositional logic are used, and, or, and not.
Describing relationships and events using propositional logic is common in AI[65]. In the
modelling language it was decided to address the problem of describing happened-before
relationships with prerequisites, elements representing logic values in the game. In the
model the yellow elements attached to a block represents prerequisites that must be true
in order for the player to choose this state.
Prerequisites can be attached to any block in the game. As a result, anything in the
game can be predetermined when and whether it should occur and be allowed. Game
writers can keep options hidden, such as a location, until the players have heard about
it from an NPC or an in-game item that they must receive from fighting a monster. It
is then possible to be specific towards when these elements should appear in the game.
For most situations simple booleans, a value that can be either true or false, are sufficient
to represent game situations the player will encounter. False denotes either having the
negated version of the boolean, or missing it. Missing the prerequisite in the player’s
internal state has been chosen as the default value for simplicity. The propositional
language allows for more complex logic values and statements. An example of a simple
boolean value could be a key needed in order to open a door. The logic value for this
would only represent whether you have the key or not (”Key” or ”!Key”). If having
the key is necessary in order to open the door, a designer would attach the prerequisite
of ”Key” to the block that describe the action of opening the door. The not-statement
being a part of propositional logic, is here called negation[10]. Negation can be used to
describe that actions should be allowed only when something is false. E.g. the player
can receive the information about the temple if the player does not already know about
this location.
In the situation where either having the key or a companion that knows how to open
==PAGE:49==
4.3 Elements of the model
42
the door is the intended behaviour, a designer can write the prerequisite as ”Key OR
Companion”, OR-statement is known as disjunction[10].
The power of propositional
language describing the possibilities in which a given problem can be overcome, allows
for more complex solutions and possibilities. This is necessary in order to accommodate
for non-linear narrative structuring in the model. More ways can lead to the same result,
and no matter which way the players have chosen a designer can design the narrative
logic that allows them to move on in the story.
Propositional logic also provides the AND logic operator, hereafter known as conjunc-
tion[10]. Conjunction requires two prerequisites to be true, in order for a state to be
accessible. All prerequisites which are on the same block are implicitly separated by
a conjunction operator. Consequently, the modelling language require all prerequisites
on a block to be true in order for a block’s state to be possible to enter. This more
simplistic design of the model provides better overview for the designers, as they does
not have to write long logic statements.
By design in the propositional logic language conjunction has precedence over disjunction.
This implicit design between prerequisites does therefore not violate this precedence rule.
Negation has the weakest precedence, and disjunction has precedence over negation. In
the example where a prerequisite is written as ”!Key OR Wench Companion”, it should
be read as either not having the key or having the wench companion.
Many more
complex ways of expression narrative logic appears with this design, however, keeping
it simple is essential and expression can often be written simpler. As an example, if one
wants to negate the entire expression written as ”!(Key OR Wench Companion)”, due
to De Morgan’s second law[57], one could just write this as two prerequisites ”!Key” and
”!Wench Companion”.
Global prerequisites
Prerequisites that enables a player to unlock a door, by gaining access to the key, are
described as local prerequisites, which are effects that are only changing the state of the
game in one player’s session. Actions in the game that are visible or affecting more than
one player, or maybe all players, are what is called global prerequisites. Changes that
should be affecting the world cannot be described as local prerequisites and need to be
enforced upon all players in order for the effect to happen.
When a player takes an action the effects of this action are the new prerequisites. It
is described as part of the effects of an action which prerequisites are global. If one
player’s action changes a global prerequisite, then this prerequisite is changed for all
other players.

4.4
The Amulet: A game based on the modelling
framework
The Amulet is a two player online cooperative RPG, which is build in the story engine
designed for this thesis. The game takes place in a medieval fantasy setting, which acts
as an evocative space, as presented in section 2.1.1, giving narrative context to the game.
In the preliminary findings in section 4.1, an expert suggested that a narrative game
should give the players the tools they need to tell or explore a story. As such, The
Amulet is in an open world setting consisting of locations and NPCs. In the Amulet
players can choose between playing as two different characters, namely a mage or a
thief, each with their own persona. Together or apart, the players can travel between
a brothel and a magic quarter, visit the bar wench or the magician and unlock the
secrets of a nearby temple. In order to prioritise interactive opportunities over length of
experience, in association to Koenitz’ principle of opportunity magnitude, The Amulet
is short, lasting between 5-10 minutes. The game is text-based and the core gameplay
revolves around clicking buttons that represent game actions.
4.4.1
Goal of the game
The overall goal of The Amulet is for players to venture through the narrative experience
of finding a magic amulet. In accordance to the design principle of having a shared goal
presented in section 2.2.1, the overarching goal of both players is to find the amulet.
The goal is presented to players in the beginning of the game in order to accommo-
date for the design principle of initial interest, presented in section 2.1.1. Finding the
amulet is accomplished by travelling to the different locations and acquiring prerequi-
sites. Prerequisites is what pushes gameplay forward, which can either be a piece of
tangible information, like a key, or it can be something that is not apparent, such as an
NPC getting mad, frustrated, or happy. However, in the current prototype all acquired
prerequisites are apparent to the player, but some could just as easily be hidden, and
thus only working for internal systems logic. As described in section 4.1 regarding pre-
liminary findings, it is essential for players to understand how their actions influence a
narrative. Keeping acquired prerequisites visible to player helps enforce this concept.
4.4.2
Player actions
In relation to what players can do the following subjects are presented:
Grouping
Grouping is an important mechanic of The Amulet. Unlike typical multiplayer group
systems where a leader invites other players to join his/her group[33, 8], or where players
always are in a group[31], The Amulet manages this slightly different. Being in a group
only happens when players are in the same scene. This means that players only are
considered to be in a group when they are at the same location in the game world.
==PAGE:51==
4.4 The Amulet: A game based on the modelling framework
44
In order to enhance the principle of multiplayer differentiability presented in section
2.1.1, which states that players should be able to have individual narrative experiences,
a player may choose to split from the group and venture alone. This allows for a number
of interesting choices for the players, since when they are grouped their prerequisites
are grouped as well. Consider for example that a group of players choose to talk to the
magician NPC. The magician seems reluctant to give any information, because one of
the players has the prerequisite Magician Angry. This scenario could also be reversed
where a player has prerequisites that are beneficial for the group and thus being in a
group becomes advantageous.
Letting players freely group up or remain separated can potentially create game sessions
where players never play together. This is not a desirable outcome for a cooperative game
and thus, in order to combat this, a prerequisite was made for entering all three available
locations. At the brothel a thief must be present since he has to lock pick the door, at
the magician quarter a mage must be present in order for anyone to obtain entrance,
and at the temple both players must travel together, since it is too dangerous to go
there alone. These prerequisites directly enforces narrative, creates complementarities
between character roles and ensures cooperative gameplay.
Change scene
Changing scenes is how players move around the game world, as seen in figure 4.6, which
illustrates a player moving from the location selection scene to an event at a NPC. In
order to enhance player agency, The Amulet is build in an open world setting. Players
can freely move to any scene that is available to them, based on their current accumulated
prerequisites and the current scene in which they are. Each scene can be considered as
a game hub, and presents a number of available actions e.g. the location selection scene
presents a number of locations to travel to and the location scene presents a number of
NPCs to encounter.
In order for players to travel as a group they must be located at the same scene and
travel collectively to the same scene. If one player moves to a new scene the other party
member will be prompted whether he wishes to follow. This ensures that players reach
the new scene simultaneously and ensures that the system knows that two players has
arrived.
Start event
Starting an event is technically the same as changing a scene, as illustrated in figure 4.6.
It is however the primary force that drives The Amulet’s narrative and thus deserves
its own description. Events are either initiated by players interacting with an NPC or
instantiated by the game system when players reach a location, depending on acquired
prerequisites. An event is a sequence of actions that players have to take in order to
resolve the event. Events can be in the form of traditional quests, where players engage
==PAGE:52==
4.4 The Amulet: A game based on the modelling framework
45
in dialogue with a NPC and are given a clue, mission or purpose. Events can also be
presented as situations, such as the door at the entrance to the hidden temple, where
players have decide what actions to take rather than what dialogue to respond with.
Make choice
In The Amulet, choices are perhaps the most important part of the game. As with any
other part of the game, choices can be done alone or in a group. Choices are handled
in an adventure game type fashion. This means that if a player is given three choices,
x, y and z, and picks choice x, then choice y and z will still be available potentially
along with the new choices x1 and x2. This can be illustrated as x => (y, z, x1, x2).
Some choices push the story forward more than others and thus eliminates previous
choices. Looking at the previous example this would mean that x => (x1, x2), with y
and z being eliminated. All choices are designed in accordance with Koenitz’ principle of
continued motivation. This entails a certain ambiguity within choices in order to avoid
potentially boring antagonistic choices. Furthermore, it includes delayed consequences,
this becomes possible due to how prerequisites are designed.
Some newly acquired
prerequisites will have no effect at the players’ current location.
Consequently, the
players will only discover the consequence later in The Amulet at a different location.
From the preliminary findings in section 4.1 it is found that an important part of cooper-
ative games is to facilitate interaction between players. This interaction in The Amulet
is to a large degree primarily facilitated through the grouped choice system. This system
was designed based on the following two design principles:
• Enhance story agency for individual players: Players get to make individual choices,
even when grouped and conversing with the same NPC. There is no ”speaker for
the group” or ”dominant voice”, players make choices and must answer to the
subsequent consequences, both individually and as a group. This is possible due
to a priority system. The priority system does two things, first it decides in what
sequence responses are handled by the NPC, e.g. if the mage chooses to greet the
NPC and the thief chooses to ask a question, then the NPC will decide to answer
the greeting first, as most would do in a real conversation scenario. Furthermore,
the priority systems handles when one choice overrules another, e.g. if the mage
player decides to punch an NPC and the thief decides to make a greeting, then the
punch will overrule the greeting and the NPC will only respond to the punch. This
grouped priority system is designed to makes dialogue in The Amulet seem more
realistic and fluent. Furthermore, it adds great story agency, since each player gets
to see the effect of their choice. Even in the case of a choice being overruled the
narrative can be designed so that the overruling seems logic and acceptable, after
all one rarely makes a polite greeting after being punched in the face.
• Avoid the popularity contest: The Amulet is build with a focus on players making
cooperative decisions rather than competing decisions. The popularity contest can
==PAGE:53==
4.4 The Amulet: A game based on the modelling framework
46
happen in two scenarios, either when players vote on which choice gets picked,
or when there is a random roll deciding who gets to be the speaker of the group.
Rather than a player hoping that it is his/her choice that gets picked by the system,
The Amulet makes sure that every player’s voice gets heard.
Furthermore, players can collaborate on making strategies about their characters’
individual relationships to each NPC or contemplate other possibilities for extract-
ing information from NPCs. As an example, in The Amulet the thief can gain
immediate access to the Brothel where he can talk to the wench NPC. The player
playing the thief might ascertain something suspect when talking to the wench,
however, the thief character does not have the means to fully understand this.
Asking his mage friend for assistance the mage enters the brothel where he will
have the possibly, after a section of dialogue, to reveal that the wench is in fact a
daemonic creature. To illustrate the amount of story agency in The Amulet, if the
mage had learned about the location of the hidden temple, which he can at the
magician quarter, then he would have the possibility of not revealing the wench
but instead taking her as a companion.

4.4.3
Character building
Being an RPG, there is a great requirement for character building in The Amulet. In
accordance with the principle of proper scripting of characters, as presented in section
==PAGE:54==
4.4 The Amulet: A game based on the modelling framework
47
2.1.1, a number of design choices was thus made in order to enforce this.
Background stories
For The Amulet it was chosen that playable characters will have pre-scripted background
stories. This makes it more easy for authors and designers to create more nuanced choices
in gameplay, since they can be written exactly to each characters given personality. Be-
sides each character having individual background stories, the relation between charac-
ters will also be pre-scripted. As such, players will be given a collective background
story, which sets the premise for the relationship between playable characters.
Automatic dialogue events
When players are engaged in conversation, either on their own or in a group, most
dialogue happens as a result of player choices. However, sometimes a specific sequence of
choices will result in an automatic dialogue event. This is where a number of characters,
engaged in the scene, will have a quick back and forth discussion on their own. This is
similar to Mass Effect 2’s auto reply[80], where the main character sometimes answers
on his own. Even though this is an interruption of agency, it offers the author/designer a
great opportunity for delivering more in-depth character building. The designers of Mass
Effect 2 argues, that even though agency is interrupted, as long as it is consistent and
not a one time happening, players will know that it is a mechanic and thus accept it as
part of the player-to-designer agreement[80]. Furthermore, in the principle of continued
motivation by Koenitz, it is stated that temporarily taking away players’ control of a
narrative event can make them feel more special when they are in control.
Character-specific choices
Players will not always be presented with the same choices. Some choices are shared
and some are character specific. Making character specific choices enforces character
building by letting players stay in character. Furthermore, this enforces the cooperative
design pattern of character complementarity, as presented in section 2.2.1, due to players
being able to perform different actions based on their chosen character.

The development process of the story engine has been lead by Test-Driven Development

The development of the server included testing a connection between disconnected and

Model representation
The modelling language, as described in chapter 4, consists of visual blocks describing the
game’s narrative. All the information contained in these blocks requires an intermediate
mark-up language. A text document, containing the information from the model’s blocks,
need a way to distinguish between what information represents which object, i.e. events
are different from actions.
Mark-up languages are systems for classifying text parts
in such a way. A file containing a mark-up representation of the information can be
processed and then parsed into the game.
For this product XML was chosen as the mark-up language; XML stands for eXtensible
Markup Language[82]. In XML, the document consists of a plain text representation of
information. When marking a section of the text, e.g. marking it as an event, the text
is surrounded by a named element on both sides, with the text being the content of this
element. XML documents starts with a root element, the top most parent of all other
elements, with several child elements within this element.

XML enables the developer to
ensure all necessary elements are present in the model. As an example, one can describe
that an event in XML must contain an element called name. If this condition is not
met, the XSD, would not validate the document. The XML document was parsed into
object-oriented classes through a built-in parsing framework in the core library of c#.
The functionality of validating XML from a logical point of view, and the ease of parsing
it to object-oriented data classes, made it the ideal tool for describing the model and for
getting the data into Unity.
The model describing the game’s narrative, as mentioned in chapter 4, was in the initial
part of the development process stored in an XML document. Modifying this document,
correcting spelling and logic errors, did not need recompilation, only a restart of the run-
time environment. The parser works by using the XSD rule set and when this validated
the XML document, it could also be parsed into the game. This allows quick iterations
when implementing unit- and manual integration tests, which was applied extensively
in the development of this product.
From the XML documents the data contained inside was parsed to data objects. When
this parsing was established it was possible to make a server that could connect players
together.
5.1
Network modelling for disconnected multiplayer
experience
Creation of the server was the first step in the prototype phase as shown in figure F.1 in
appendix F. The server is responsible for connecting clients together and enables online
multiplayer in the product. The server is a centralised server, meaning that it is the
acting agent for all communications across the network. When two clients are informing
each other of events in their local session, the server is the intermediate unit that enables
this communication. The centralised server was implemented ahead of the game loop
and the client-side of this network communication.
The server streams objects to clients through a network stream. A stream is a sequence
of data elements made available over time. Streaming objects to and from clients, and
parsing of these objects, is managed within the server. Furthermore, the server is a
middle layer, a control system, for each player. The control system ensures that all
players oblige the same rules of the game. Every action from the player is sent and
validated by the server. This was done to keep the players information in a central place
and to update all other players with this information.
Another way of doing network communication, apart from this centralised server archi-
tecture, is Peer-to-peer, that enables network without a server. Peer-to-peer requires
==PAGE:58==
5.1 Network modelling for disconnected multiplayer experience
51
all clients(peers) to contain a network stream to each peer on the network, and that
totally-ordered multicasting, i.e. all messages are delivered in the same order to each
receiver[60], is implemented. Communication is done directly between peers. Implement-
ing peer-to-peer would add an extra layer of complexity, while only eliminate the server.
Consequently, it was decided to go forward with the centralised server architecture.
One of the centralised server’s responsibilities is to keep clients synchronised, by ensuring
all messages are managed in the same order across clients. When the player takes an
action in-game the implementation streams a message to the server and waits. The idea
is to wait for the server to inform the client that it can go on. The action will not go
through until receiving an acknowledgement from the server. An acknowledgement is a
message the server will send to a client, in order to verify that the server has received
the action. This is necessary in order for the server to adjust the ordering of actions
for all clients. An action from a player will be processed on the server before the next
action is taken from the queue. This way of processing actions one at a time creates a
clear order in which action in the game happens. This happened-before relationship is
essential for The Amulet. As a result, two actions cannot happen at the same time in
order to avoid ambiguity.
When a player enters a new location, starts an event, or performs some other action in
The Amulet the state of the player changes. The players’ current location and event,
are describing the players’ current state. When a state change happens, it is sent to the
server. Afterwards, the server forwards the information to all other clients. Finally, an
acknowledgement message is sent back to the original client.
In order for the server to know which clients should receive the information, and specifi-
cally which clients are connected to the server, the server maintains a list of open streams
to each client, which is sorted using an ID. This ID is created when the client connects to
the server. In this product it was created as a simple integer that would increase every
time a new client connected. By using the ID the server can distinguish between clients
and avoid sending the information back to the original client. If the client receives its
own actions it would have interpreted this as coming from another player, which cause
undefined behaviour in the game implementation. Clients’ online status are contained
to ensure the server does not communicate with IDs that does not connect to an actual
client. When a connection is broken the ID is removed from the list of open streams.
When a new client connects to the server, a set of threads are spawned. A thread is a
parallel execution of the program. By having several execution threads it is possible for
a program to do more than one unit of work at the same time. These threads, spawned
for each client, are used for storing and streaming messages. One thread, InputWorker,
handles ingoing messages from the client and the other, OutputWorker as shown in figure
5.1, takes care of the outgoing messages to the client. Between the server and client a
stream is opened, used by the two threads to stream data with. After creating these two
workers, they are added to the Data class for ease of access.

The output worker is responsible for picking up data objects, serialising them, and
forwarding them through a stream to the client. To store objects on a medium that is
not traditionally random access, for instance hard drives, or to transfer an object over a
stream medium (such as the network in this example), the object needs to be converted
into a form that is suitable to the relevant medium. This conversion process is called
serialisation, as the structured object is flattened or serialised. As a result, objects are
more amenable to being stored for the long term, or transferred over the network. As
an example as seen in c#, where adding the Serializable property on the class will
automatically serialise the object for network streaming.
The output worker thread will await until a message appears in its internal queue. The
queue is a list that external threads can put objects into, which is sorted according to
chronological order. When a message is put in this internal queue the worker grabs this
message and pass it through the network stream to the client. The stream is stored in
a field within the OutputWorker class.
The input worker receives in-going messages and ensures the correct response is directed
to an output worker. To do this, the input worker contains a reference to the output
worker that is connected to the same network stream.
The output worker pick the
response up from its internal queue and forward it to the client. The data class contains
a reference to all workers, allowing other workers to access all workers. This enables
input workers to collect all output workers for other clients and broadcast a message to
more than the network paired output worker. Finally, the message to be broadcasted is
placed into the internal queue inside of the output worker for each client.

5.1.1
protocol
When the architecture of the server had been designed in the process of implementing the
story engine, it was possible to do input and output on the network stream. Next it was
necessary to develop a protocol to handle communication. A communication protocol is
a system of rules that allow two or more entities to transmit information. The protocol
in this product is a common set of data objects that was sent across disconnected units,
specially the server and client. The protocol, i.e. the content and the formatting of
the data described in the following sections, was required in order ensure consistent
communication.
Message objects sent between the server and client are part of a shared library. The
shared library consists of object-oriented representations of data, classes containing the
information. The incentive for developing a shared library was to develop a common
agreement of what was accepted as input and output data between the client and server.
When streaming an object to the client, the server will always create these objects of
the interface type Response, as illustrated in figure 5.2. Different implementations of
the interface will enlist the specific data properties that should be communicated to the
client. In the example where the server need to update the status of another the player,
the specific class implementation of the Response interface would contain the status
data. Response objects can even be empty in order to represent a status message. An
example of this is provided in the acknowledgement message, specifically implemented
in the AllIsWell class.
When a client streams data objects towards the server, these objects follows two inter-
faces, namely IngoingMessages and Access. The server expects these types of objects
to come through the network stream from all clients. When receiving an object, it is

the server’s response. However, by letting another thread wait for the response, it is
possible to run the game loop in parallel without interrupting it.

The Communication class acts as an output handler for all functions that need to send an
instance of InGoingMessages to the server. The class manages serialising of any object

Incoming messages from the server are collected by a thread of the type InputHandler.
Through a blocking function called on network stream it will receive messages sent by the
server. A blocking function will attempt to read from the network stream. The function
will not return and continue execution before a message is received from the server. This
will potentially block the program, but as this function is called from a separate thread,
solely this thread will be blocked by the function call. When receiving a message from
the server the input handler will put these messages into a queue.
The message queue is part of a distributor system. The InputDistributor class listens
for objects that has been put into this general queue by the input handler. This input
distributor is on another thread waiting for this queue to get new elements. The input
handler is thus free to read objects parallel from the thread distributing the objects.
Parallel reading allows for handling of multiple messages simultaneously in the client.
The distributor system ensures a Handler receives the correct objects which it has sub-
scribed to. Each handler is a class that implements the Handler interface and is respon-
sible for handling a specific type of object. As an example, when receiving which event
the other player has started, a handler that has subscribed to the event objects is being
called.

managed by the GeneralHandler class.

Each handler

When a message arrives from the server, the input handler puts it into the correct
container through the distributor. Afterwards, it is the handler’s task to manage the
object and forward it to the local game’s state.

Storing data
The data arriving through the network stream is stored in different places throughout
the architecture of the game. Inside of the class Player the current state of the player

inside the class GameStateManager.
All the data within this class will be loaded at

5.5
Moving together as a group
The game mechanics, such as moving between locations in-game and clicking on a button
to take an action, are implemented separately from the client. The game mechanics are
not a part of the modelling language, and are only a part of the game implementation.
Many different examples of moving between the blocks in the model could exist for
different games such as, clicking a GUI button, moving a 3D character in a world,
interacting with model objects. As a result, it is up to the game-specific implementation
how these game mechanics should work and will not be discussed in this description of
the story engine.
Player could now in the game implementation move around, take actions, talk to NPCs.
This made it possible to start on the game mechanics concerning multiplayer. The focus
here will be on grouping up together with the other player in-game.
In order for players in a group to move to the same in-game location, they need to
wait for all to agree or disagree in joining each other. An action that does not being
complete before all party members agree, simulates a conversation starting with one
person suggesting the group should go to a location and the others agreeing or disagreeing
to this suggestion. Additionally, the players need to be in a group together before they
can see the suggestion and be able to agree with the choice made by the other player.
The group is assumed in this implementation, and players will not have to group up
by taking a specific action, as required in many games. The assumption is made that
two players are in a group when they have the same state in-game, in terms of being
at the same location, when talking to the same NPC, or being on the same event. To
validate and update the group status, every time a client receives an update of the other
player, the player’s own state is compared to the other player’s, whether they should be
considered in a group now. If two players are in a group and one of them leaves the
location, they will automatically disband the group.
When the group wants to move together, one player starts by selecting e.g. a location
he/she desires to enter. Consequently, a message is sent to the server and it will then
broadcast the message to all other clients. Each client, through the general handler and
later the input distributor, activates their PlayerStateHandler, as the PlayerState is
the object subscribed to by the PlayerStateHandler class. The implementation of this
class will notify the correct EventHandler by calling the EventManager.
Event handlers function in a similar way to the data object handlers, described in sec-
tion 5.2. They subscribe through the class EventManager to events of the enum type
Events. When the event manager receives a message that a specific event has been
started, the event manager matches the Events enum with an implementation of the
type EventHandler found inside a hash map, with the entry pair of Events enum and
EventHandler. Afterwards, the event handler will be notified that its subscribed event
has started. Calling the EventManager with the event enum Travelled notifies the event

handler TravelledEventHandler that is subscribed to this specific event. The handler
subscribes to this event as part of Unity’s Start function, and not through the Client
as done in the communication layer. In Unity a Start function is called automatically
by the game engine once the object is initialised. This happens when a scene in Unity
is loaded. Each EventHandler implementation is added to the scenes where it makes

thread-safe. Consequently, it was necessary to make this solution for this product.

CHAPTER 6
Partial analysis 1:
Narrative gameplay
Partial analysis 1 is concerned with the game- and narrative design aspect of this thesis,
which aims at identifying potentials and pitfalls of designing IDS games with cooperative
gameplay. The analysis is conducted based on the interplay between collected data, the
constructed prototype and the theoretical background.
The first section of the analysis is a thorough examination of two design decisions that
took part in the prototype, namely the ability for players to split up and the grouped
decision-making system. Both features were highly debated topics throughout the expert
interviews and the purpose of this section is thus to discuss and validate the features
based on the conducted interviews. The second section of the partial analysis will revolve
around the potential scalability of the product in terms of narrative and gameplay. The
final section will cover the overall narrative potential of the system as well as the potential
applicability within the current game industry. The analysis has its empirical foundation
in the preliminary questionnaires, experimentation with the constructed prototype and
the conducted expert interviews. The theoretical framework is the gathered research
literature on IDS presented in section 2.
6.1
Analysis of prototype design decisions
The two most discussed features of the designed prototype within all three expert inter-
views were the ability for players to separate and the designed decision-making system.
This was to some degree expected by the researchers, since those features can be recog-
nised as the most innovative and disrupting features of the prototype’s design, when
considering existing ways of doing cooperative narrative stories, as presented in section
2.3. The experts expressed their interest in these mechanics and highlighted their po-
tential for generating story agency. However, with several concerns regarding the way
they were currently designed.
6.1.1
Splitting up or staying together
In order to generate a more individual story agency in line with the principle of multi-
player differentiability presented in section 2.1.1, players do not have to stick together
==PAGE:69==
6.1 Analysis of prototype design decisions
62
but can branch out on their own separate adventures. This turned out to be a very
difficult mechanic to manage, and has inherent narrative and game design advantages
and disadvantages, which will be presented in the following section.
Successful cooperative gameplay requires players to help each other, work out strategies,
and laugh together, as described in the cooperative evaluation metrics in section 2.2.2.
The interviewed game designer argued, that such metrics would become more difficult to
ensure, given that players were not in the same game space. This was elaborated on by
explaining how good game design usually has its base in one specific mechanic, idea, or
concept, which is usually referred to as the game’s core. If that core is cooperation then
allowing players to separate could become a counterproductive feature, since cooperating
over distance inherently is difficult. This is an interesting observation and emphasises
that designers need to be very deliberate when deciding whether players should play
together or apart. The same expert suggested that if a cooperative game allows players
to separate then such a game should focus on leveraging the underlying problems with
cooperation over distance. As he stated:
“...simply allowing players to do what they want is indecisive design. Every
design decision needs to be deliberate and needs to have a meaning and purpose.
If I am not with my teammate then give me a reason, are we separated by a
disaster? Do i have a radio I can communicate with? It is always beneficial
to have a purpose beyond just freedom.”
This quote further highlights the need for communication between players, which was
mentioned by all experts. Communication is important in order to allow players to main-
tain cooperation, even when separated. Preferably, this would be vocal communication
either by players physically being together or by system integrated voice communication.
Once players rejoin after being separated they need to be able to share individually
gathered information. Such communication could be in the form of player to player
as mentioned above, however the interviewed game writer argued that it could also be
done as character to character. This type of communication would require developers
to create more pre-scripted dialogues where characters automatically update their team-
mate. Updates could either be happening while characters were separated or once they
rejoined. The interviewed game writer argued, that not having players communicate
on a meta level, meaning outside of the game environment, would increase the chance
of players being deeply involved with the story and its characters. However, it would
require a larger development process.
Another big problem with allowing players to get separated in a cooperative game is the
risk of them developing distinct separate goals, to the degree of them not wanting to
leave their current space in the game world. To accommodate for this in the prototype,
game mechanics were implemented in order to make sure that players did not stay apart
for too long, as mentioned in section 4.4.2. This design combination of players being able
to play separately, but at times required to group up, can lead to frustrating situations.
==PAGE:70==
6.1 Analysis of prototype design decisions
63
An example of this could be that one player cannot venture forward without the help
from his teammate, whom is unwilling to change location due to various reasons. This
is indeed a problem in the prototype’s current design, but could be accommodated for
by ensuring that content that requires multiple players, is equally rewarding for all
participants. Designing it this way promotes players having synergies between goals,
which is described in section 2.2.1, and is of high importance in cooperative games. In
RPGs the benefit from being in a group is usually that one can defeat tougher enemies
and thus acquire better rewards. Delivering better rewards in terms of narrative is more
difficult, since a narrative experience is less quantifiable than e.g. the damage attribute
of a sword. One way of ensuring narrative rewards for regrouping is by designing it so
that narrative experiences are intertwined, meaning that even when players experience
individual narratives they will at some point be forced to cooperate. This will incentives
players to group up in order to fully understand their individual narrative.
In regards to players having to wait for each other, which can build frustration as pre-
sented in section 2.2.2, yet another difficulty arises when letting players play individually.
If the skill gap between players is too large then one player will potentially consume con-
tent at a faster rate than the other. When it comes to narrative, this would mean that
the better player, in the most extreme example, would have to explain the majority of
the narrative to the other player, since he would be the only one who had experienced
it. This is also true for the prototype developed for this thesis. Weaving narrative paths
into one another, or having them converge at specific plot points, would further alleviate
this problem by creating situations where players would be forced to play together.
6.1.2
Cooperative choice selection
Handling how decisions are made in a cooperative scenario is an element that truly can
shape a cooperative experience, as discussed in section 4.4.2. This was highlighted in
the preliminary questionnaire findings in section 4.1, where an expert deemed this as
one of the biggest differences between singleplayer and multiplayer narrative-led games.
Furthermore, all interviewed experts agreed that this was a desirable feature and recog-
nised its potential for innovating on cooperative gameplay in IDS games. During testing
of the prototype, the researchers discovered one larger challenge with the implemented
choice system.
In the prototype the amount and types of prerequisites that each player has is what
determines the number of possible choices available in a decision-making event. Given
that players individually acquire prerequisites it is thus indeterminable whether they will
hold the same amount of a choices. As a result, one player might run out of choices before
the other player and be left with essentially nothing to do or say, while the remaining
player can continue the event on his own. This creates situations where players have to
wait for each other, which, as described earlier, can build frustration.
One way to combat this is to make sure that each player has a resolve event choice,
==PAGE:71==
6.1 Analysis of prototype design decisions
64
which can be described as a choice that ends the event for both players.
This will
ensure that players never get stuck in an event with no choices left, since the event
will be resolved before this can happen. A potential challenge that increases in risk by
doing this, is that players can resolve an event without the consent of the cooperative
partner. This means that players can hinter or interfere with each others goals, which
may have a negative impact on the player experience, as presented in section 2.2.2.
Making sure that each player has a resolve event choice, can be utilised if it is ensured
that players are aware of and understand these choices, thus making them a part of the
player-to-designer agreement. One could imagine that a resolve event choice would be
of a more concluding nature and thus players could be able to understand that taking a
more concluding choice would progress the narrative and terminate the current decision-
making process. Furthermore, it is noteworthy that the risk for players to get in each
others way by selecting resolve event choices still do exist, although to a lower degree,
even if they are not given to both players, which further advocates for providing them
for both players.
An important aspect of allowing players to make individual choices is to make sure that
each choice has a significant narrative purpose. All interviewed experts agreed that the
prioritisation system described in section 4.3.4 accommodated for this need quite well.
As the interviewed game writer stated:
“...if one player chooses to kill an NPC, while the other player chooses to
save him, you as a writer want to make sure that there is a narrative purpose
behind the NPC either dying or living. This [individual player choices] would
make for an interesting tool to generate story between players...”
The prioritisation system and its inherent overrule feature allows designers and authors
shape the narrative experience while still maintaining a high level of perceived story
agency, being the player’s subjective perception of his ability to make narrative changes.
The system’s nature can be perceived as a simplified version of recessive and dominant
genes in biology. Similar to how the dominant gene for brown eyes can dominate the
recessive gene for blue eyes[24], a dominant choice only has to be picked by one player
in order to overrule any other recessive choices as illustrated in figure 6.1.
The interviewed game author highlighted that the prioritisation and overrule features of
the system possessed great possibilities for mirroring real world decision-making, where
people’s choices not only impact themselves but also those around them. Furthermore,
same expert, as well as other experts, argued that players want the possibility of exploring
the narrative consequences of different choices. Players want their voice to be heard and
to test the range of possible outcomes from a given scenario. However, the interviewed
game author expressed concerns regarding players being able to sabotage each others’
narrative experiences, due to the high level of individual agency within grouped decision-
making situations. This is closely related to the cooperative problem of players getting
in each others’ way, as mentioned in section 2.2.2. This could be accommodated for by
introducing great unpredictability between which choices are dominant and which are

recessive. The example presented in figure 6.1 could just as easily be reversed where
killing the NPC would require the mutual consent of all players, thus making saving the
NPC the dominant choice. Furthermore, the choices could be designed more in the line
with The Witcher 3’s choice philosophy of presenting unpredictable choices with high

The enormous amount of choices in The Witcher 3 obfuscates how quests are connected
and how exactly the player is impacting the world. In the real world people do not think
about what branching part of their life they are moving into. Some choices are conscious

it harder for players to read a decision-making system, which in turn could make it less

6.2
Narrative scalability
IDS with large amounts of story agency and multiplayer games with a focus on coop-
erative gameplay are separately two major undertakings for game development studios.
This is due to the additional required design decisions each genre entails, as presented
in section 2. Cooperative games have multiple design patterns that players have come
to expect, as described in section 2.2.1, that designers have to take into consideration
when creating cooperative experiences. IDS systems that offer large amounts of story
agency, often require some form of narrative branching. This results in large amounts
of content generation, which is potentially never experienced by the majority of players.
When combined, as one can imagine, they constitute a potentially time-consuming and
==PAGE:73==
6.2 Narrative scalability
66
thus expensive endeavour. This notion was confirmed by all interviewed experts and
deemed the biggest problem with developing an IDS game with cooperative gameplay.
It is thus relevant to investigate the narrative scalability of the constructed prototype
in order to understand its potential in a current industry context.
6.2.1
The problem with branching
There can be no doubt that creating branching storytelling offers more story agency
than any linear narrative game. As shown in the preliminary questionnaire findings in
section 4.1, one expert stated that branching stories arguably always would be stronger
than linear stories if each project was given equal resources, due to agency delivering
more player investment.
Story agency emerges through the additional story content
that is developed for branching narratives. In this statement lies an inherent problem,
namely that story agency requires more content. The researchers of this thesis found it
difficult and time-consuming to create a simple branching narrative with no more than
5-10 minutes of gameplay. It can thus be suggested that it would require an enormous
amount of work to create a longer experience. Furthermore, as the interviewed writer
pointed out:
“...if the game is only text-based then it’s doable and is simply a matter of
time. However, as soon as you include voice acting, models and animation
then you’re always trying to avoid the project rapidly escalating.”
This illustrates that dialogue-based branching narratives not only affects the author
and designers workload but also all other parts of a game development process. In the
designed prototype, each character has their own unique dialogue options, along with
automatic dialogue sequences that can happen between characters. This is all narrative
content that potentially would have to be voice acted and animated. All interviewed
experts mentioned, that should the prototype be made into a larger production with
such features, then the cost would be insurmountable and thus deemed the product,
with its current features, partly unrealistic.
Within branching experiences there are different approaches that designers can take in
order to minimise the extend to which a narrative expands. All interviewed experts
remarked how branching narratives most likely always would have to use the foldback
structure (depicted in figure 2.2) to some degree. The foldback structure contributes
to perceived story agency rather than the theoretical story agency. It could be imple-
mented on a larger scale in the form of significant plot points, that are unavoidable by
all players, forcing them to group and converging their narrative paths. It could also be
utilised on a smaller scale in the shape of IDS stagecraft. The interviewed writer partic-
ularly highlighted how he spent quite a lot of effort creating IDS stagecraft, or ”smoking
mirrors” as he referred to it. The stagecraft could be done by creating a scenario where
the upcoming choice was nothing more than an illusion, since the result would be the
same no matter what. The writer argued that when doing it this way there could still

be created perceived agency by changing transitions rather than results. In order to
illustrate, this he proposed a simplified example that is useful in order to understand
how this technique can be utilised. In the example the player is running behind a train
and can either chooses to jump onto it or not, meaning that he has two choices. If the
player jumps onto the train then a bandit pushes him off into a cactus field. If the player
chooses not to jump onto the train then he stumbles and rolls into a cactus field. This
example illustrates how perceived agency is retained due to different transitions, even
though the result of both actions is the same.
This is an interesting point since the prototype developed for this thesis has its primary
focus on differentiating results from actions, rather than differentiating transitions. The
interviewed writer further described how he was currently working on a game that always
presented the player with exactly two choices. This type of structure is what can be
referred to as classic pyramid narrative structure[15] and is depicted in figure 6.2 along
with an exponential narrative structure.

Using the pyramid structure helps limit the potential scope of a branching narrative.
This is especially important in games with multiple players that are allowed to move
independently, since it assists in ensuring that players do not venture too far away from
each others’ individual narratives. It can thus be suggested that combining the pyramid
structure with a foldback structure would result in a generally more manageable narrative
structure.
6.2.2
An example: Introducing one more player
In terms of introducing more players to the prototype’s game environment a significant
scalability challenge arises. In the prototype each player has to be represented by a
new character, which in turn demands more content generation. One might argue that
this is self-explanatory, but due to the amount of story agency within the framework
the additional content requirement escalates significantly. In order to understand the

6.2 Narrative scalability
68
potential challenges with narrative scalability in the product, it is below assessed what
would happen if the possibility for one more player was introduced to the game.
• New background stories: The new character would require a new background story,
along with a joint background story with each of the two remaining characters, and
a background story for when all three play together. This sums up to a requirement
of four new background stories. In a text-based scenario, this does not amount to
too much extra development. However, the interviewed game writer claimed that
he would never do background story as a wall of text or a ”history lesson” as he
called it. Specifically, the expert elaborated that the background story should be
interactive and be something that was experienced by the player, which would make
it more impacting and meaningful. Another interviewed expert highlighted how the
background story of Firewatch[66] was one of the most compelling narrative game
experiences she had ever had. The background story in Firewatch is a mixture of
choices, traditional descriptive story, and gameplay. This shows how the need for
four new background stories could entail a significant amount of extra development.
• New character specific dialogue options: Introducing a new player to the game
would require, at the very least, one new character specific choice options for each
event.
Given that the prototype currently has ten events that would result in
having to create a minimum of ten new dialogue options.
These new dialogue
options then have to fit into the context of any present priority system within all
events. Furthermore, it is worth considering that, since each event is designed to
respond to each players individual choice, introducing more players increases the
time where players have to wait while other players choices are being answers. The
chance of a player choosing an event resolving choice, as mentioned in section 6.1.2,
also increases when introducing another player.
• Automatic dialogue sequences: A new character would require more pre-scripted
automatic dialogue sequences. In the current prototype there are six automatic
dialogue sequences between character X and character Y. As such, these automatic
sequences could be presented as XY(6). If a third character Z is introduced then,
in order to have an equal distribution of content within the game, it would be
required to create XZ(6), YZ(6) and XYZ(6), ending up with 24 automatic dialogue
sections. In a worst case scenario this would result in an exponential increase per
player introduced into the game environment.
• More content in general: It can be argued that more players would go through
the content faster than less players. As such, it would be necessary to introduce
new content, such as a new location. This would require a new set of events along
with a new set of choices. Realising that the narrative of this new location would
have to tie into the remaining content, it is plausible that changes would have to
be made to the rest of the content as well. In order to make sure that each player
feels unique and is capable of complimenting other players, it would be necessary

to create additional prerequisites to ensure that all players could contribute the
narrative progression.
This illustrates how much extra content adding one new player would require.
One
way to accommodate for this potential escalation of required content is to set a fixed
number of players. This is seen in Divinity: Original Sin[71] where only two players
can play together. Another way to accommodate for more players would be to redesign
or eliminate existing features, which suggests a competing requirement between player
numbers and possible story agency.
6.3
Designing narrative differently
The previous sections of this analysis illuminates pitfalls of designing branching narra-
tives with cooperative gameplay.
As such, it can be considered whether other ways
of mediating storytelling in games, as described in section 2, would have benefited the
prototype. This will be analysed in the following section.
6.3.1
The need for environmental storytelling
Within the prototype’s scope there was no art assets and thus very little game environ-
ment other than placeholder pictures, which primarily were of symbolic effect. Each
location and the general setting or theme of the prototype was implemented in order to
create evocative spaces, which could convey story by giving narrative context as describe
in section 2.1.1. However, since the artistic aspect of the product was so limited, one
could argue that the concept of environmental or spatial storytelling to a large degree
was left out of the product. One has to consider how this effected the narrative potential
of the framework.
Being a narrative architect, as Jenkins[40] describes it or a Cyberbard as Murray[56]
calls it, involves communicating narrative by showing rather than telling. The narrative
architect either place specific objects in a specific order within the game world in order
to mediate a precise narrative experience, or he/she creates an interactive framework
for players to tell their own stories. Either way, the way he/she mediates narrative by
architecting a space in which experiences happen.
A very good example of this is Brothers: A Tale of Two Sons[74], which was highlighted
by the interviewed game writer. One very successful part of the narrative is the spaces
that the two brothers travel through. Each space conveys its own narrative, primarily
through its environment, such as a small town where everyone has been turned to statues
of snow or a battlefield filled with slain giants in armour as shown in figure 6.3. These
places fill the players with wonder and, as the interviewed game designer pointed out,
has the player asking questions about the narrative. The expert further explained how
such questions might be exceptionally powerful in a cooperative setting, since players
==PAGE:77==
6.3 Designing narrative differently
70
actually have someone to pose them to. This can result in the environmental storytelling
being intensified, and thus presenting it as an even more important tool in IDS.
Figure 6.3: Scenery from Brothers: A Tale of Two Sons depicting the battlefield of
slain giants.
Environmental storytelling is a fundamental part of IDS and is something that can be
used to great effect, since it has the power to partly take over or facilitate narrative
mediation.
As such, not including it to a larger degree as part of the constructed
prototype could have weakened the systems narrative potential.
6.3.2
Cooperative narrative game mechanics
Another element that to a large extend was left out of the prototype’s scope was game
mechanics. Game mechanics can be a great tool for designers to convey narrative ele-
ments through gameplay as discussed in section 2.1.3. This does not necessarily require
branching narratives, lots of dialogue, or any of the other previously explained aspects
associated with expensive IDS game development. However, it is much harder to cre-
ate a general modelling framework for such mechanics, since each mechanic has to be
tailor-made for each narrative experience, in order to convey that specific narrative.
In the prototype, each character is represented by one prerequisite, such as the mage-
character who has the prerequisite Mage and the thief-character who has the prerequisite
Thief. This is utilised in different scenarios, such as when one has to enter the brothel
at night, the Thief has to be present in order to lock pick the door. The only way that
players know the reason for the requirement of a thief is through narrative description.
This description will be different for each narrative situation where the prerequisite Thief
is utilised. As an example, the thief might have to be present because he can sneak past
a guard, or because he can bribe the town mayor. However, in the prototype this is
always conveyed through description and not through interactivity.
==PAGE:78==

One interviewed expert emphasised the need for character specific mechanics. In the
context of the prototype, this would mean that each character had to be broken down
into multiple prerequisites. Looking at the previous example, the thief would need to
have the prerequisites Thief, Lock Picking, Stealth, Bribe. When players know that
the thief has the lock picking skill, there is no need to describe why he needs to be
present when entering the brothel, since the door will be locked. Thus, the role and
the narrative context of the thief can be explained through the implemented mechanics.
Another benefit from introducing such mechanics would be that they can facilitate and
generate a narrative context for some of the situations where players have to regroup,
which, as explained in section 6.1.1, can reduce the amount of required narrative content.
It can thus be suggested that this kind of narrative through game mechanics, like envi-
ronmental storytelling, can replace a lot of narrative description, which has the potential
of de-escalating associated development costs.
6.3.3
Collaboration between designers and authors
Creating IDS is in its essence a matter of knowing how to convey narrative and knowing
how to facilitate interactivity. As such, the process can be perceived as a symbiosis be-
tween authors and designer as it was depicted in figure 2.1. During the expert interviews,
a very interesting pattern formed in regards to how they each talked about their own
role and the role of their colleagues, especially when it came to the relationship between
authors and designers/directors. The game director explained that she would leave the
writing to the writer, with a clear indication of her not being a part of that process. She
could however see herself modelling the narrative framework, using a tool, such as the
designed prototype. Yet, she gave no indication of including the writer in this process.
The interviewed game designer explained that he could easily see himself using the proto-
type as a system for defining the narrative logic, by creating prerequisites, locations, and
events. However, similar to the director, he would not be implementing any text to the
system. It was interesting to note how the writer described exactly this scenario from his
perspective. The writer explained that he in some projects would receive outlined stories
where he had to fill in blank boxes with bits and pieces of story and dialogue, which
had the result of poor narrative and bad writing due to the strict framework. From
game development it is well-known that a significant communication gap between pro-
grammers and designers exist. As a result, developers take great initiatives in order to
bridge this gap, such as structuring the work process or having communicative technical
leaders. To ensure that the development process is uniform. The previous statements
from interviewed experts illustrates a similar gap between designers and authors. This
friction and separation of professions is not surprising considering the previously men-
tioned debate between narratologists and ludologists in section 2.1. It exemplifies that
it is not only perspective on games that differ from designers and authors, but also the
work processes that each role contains. This reveals a fundamental difference between
the two professions, which seems to be conflicting with one another.

The interviewed writer explained that it is difficult to ensure good writing when writing
for a branching game. As an example, the writer mentioned how a game could allow
the player to choose different melee weapons to break down a door. As such, the writer
would have to be generic in his writing so that it was usable in the case of all weapons
and to make sure that a voice actor would not have to record a different line for each

of writing. The second best solution is to ensure that writers and designers work in
close collaboration. However, as the writer explained, the writer often gets disconnected
from the core development group and thus work at a distance from the decision-making
processes.
In projects where there is a system engine or an editor available for the writer, he often
chooses to write in a separate document and then later insert the text to the game,
or have someone else insert it. The writer mentioned a couple of reasons as to why
he tends to work this way. First of all, his work process becomes disrupted when he
has to manoeuvre around an editor or engine whilst writing dialogue. Dialogue, as he
elaborated, is ideally played out by the author in the moment of writing it, and this is
a lot harder if he has to click the mouse several times between each character’s reply.
Second, he has to keep some form of structure on what he is writing, which is rarely
taken into consideration by the designers of the editor or engine. One example of this is
that he needs to know which of his dialogue sections has been cleared and successfully
voice-acted.
In terms of the prototype, this highlights a massive challenge, since the framework is
not designed with the workflow of writers in mind. The interviewed writer stated that
he could use the prototype for structuring a narrative, but in the end he would still be
doing the actual writing in a separate document. The writer did however add, that if the
system was given a user-friendly interface, it could serve as an effective tool for bridging
the gap between writers and designers.

7.1
Modelling language
This chapter highlight and analyse the potentials of designing multiplayer games, from a
technical point of view. The perspective is focused on the modelling language, discussed
in section 4.2, and the code implementation discussed in section 5. IDS in a coopera-
tive game environment is analysed from a technical point of view in order to highlight
potential challenges when initiating such a production.
The challenges that is analysed is part of the story engine and the game implementation.
This section serves to explain how these issues can be combated and overcome. Addition-
ally, the architectural design choices for both perspectives are being rethought in order
to allow changes to the specification and complexity. The identified possible options,
regarding the choices made for this product, are highlighted as to open opportunities, in
order to rethink relevant parts of this thesis for more specific projects.
7.1.1
New features syndrome
In the game development process the need for more features in the modelling language
emerged. Hence, it was essential to maintain a minimal language when these complex
ideas were introduced. It was easy to introduce new features, which more specifically
describe situations within the game, rather than introducing generalised versions of this
feature that would have been applicable for more situations. One example that can be
seen in this product is within prerequisites.
In order to allow the players to enter the temple location inside The Amulet, it was
necessary for a player to have heard of the location beforehand. The information about
the temple emerges through the effect of an action. When designing the mechanic of un-
locking new locations in the world, a prerequisite of the name ”KnowsLocation(Temple)”
was selected. From a designer’s point of view this made sense as it clearly described the
intended mechanic behind the named function. If a player has to know the location of
the market place it can be described as ”KnowsLocation(MarketPlace)”.
During the implementation, it was initially designed to distinguish between location
prerequisites and item prerequisites (like having the key in ones inventory). The item
==PAGE:83==
7.1 Modelling language
76
prerequisite was designed with a named function, such as ”Has(key)”. It is possible to
come up with a generalised term for both prerequisites, that avoid the function descrip-
tion ”Has” and ”KnowsLocation”. The model would circumvent the use of these location
specific prerequisites. However, the knowledge that this was an unnecessary explicit rep-
resentation of these prerequisites did not become clear until the implementation for both
prerequisites was done.
The implementation for both prerequisites are managed in the exact same manner in the
implementation. This is the first indication of bad code[81], in this case it stems from
the design and should have been redesigned in this specific case. If the two prerequisites
were simply ”Key” and ”Temple” it would have made the implementation for both
prerequisites the exact same. As the modelling language only specifies that a prerequisite
should exist in the player’s state, it is a valid solution.
Giving a more explicit definition of a prerequisite would possibly be necessary in order to
make the distinction between two types of prerequisites. As such, it would make sense to
extend the prerequisite definition. However, when designing the model for a game using
the modelling language, it is essential to be highly aware of whether the more explicit
definition is constructed to understand the concepts, or whether it is contributing to
the narrative logic. Having an unnecessary complex description will over complicate the
implementation of the story engine. In this case it made the implementation of this
concrete example more difficult and obscure than necessary.
Additionally, it is relevant to assess the design as to how much the implementation fo-
cus on the prerequisite naming. In an implementation where the function name has no
effect, it does not matter what the function name of the prerequisite was. If the player
get ”Has(key)” as an effect of an action, and in order to take an action the prerequisite
”Has(key)” was necessary, would be valid. As the ”Has(key)” prerequisite put in the
player’s state made it valid when the prerequisite was needed, as they have similar name.
Contradictory, it would not be valid if the player got an effect prerequisite ”Gain(key)”
and the prerequisite which was required to perform the action was ”Has(key)”. If the
input and output usage of the prerequisite are named the same, no difference in the im-
plementation is required and the clarifying naming does not change the implementation,
the naming would just help the designers to understand their model.
7.1.2
Open modelling language blocks
The modelling language allows blocks to be visited by one player or more players several
times. In the game implementation of the model constructed for the Amulet, players
help each other to overcome prerequisites by grouping up.
However, another game
implementation does not have to make this a part of the game. Open world games, such
as The Witcher 3 and World of Warcraft do not limit the player’s movement to closed
portions of the world. The player is encouraged to explore the world in the search of
new adventures, items, and encounters.

77
The blocks in the modelling language are designed with the open world concept in mind,
in order to support players that may only partially explore the game. The player might
only pursue some of the actions available at a location and only start some of the events
that are available. It might even be that the player does not have the prerequisite to
start all events, and will therefore have to revisit blocks in the game to explore previously
unavailable elements of the game. The player is allowed to revisit these at later stages
of the game at their own leisure.
Combining this freedom for the players with the prerequisites, one can still model states
in the game in a way so these states would only be possible to visit once. Furthermore,
it is possible to lock parts of the game and let the player use hidden information that
becomes available later in the story. With this new information the player can revisit
these states.
By rethinking the term location, one could easily change this to be named stages in the
game. When a player reaches a new stage in the story of the game, the closure of a block
would describe the events and actions that can happen within this stage. Consequently,
in order to enter this stage of the story, the necessary prerequisites would exist and all
events would be reachable for the player, in the order intended by the designer. As
found in the interviews, designers look for options and tools that let the game writers
and designers handle the representation of the game flow through the design of closure.
The closure is described, by the interviewers, as having a narrow overview of the game
through events and actions, and a broader overview in the locations or stages of the
game. Hence, it is a requirement for the modelling language to be as general as possible,
while providing enough specific features to support the elements of the game a designer
would create.
7.1.3
Designing for other genres
In this product, The Amulet is implemented by the using the story engine as an RPG.
RPGs consists of narrative sequences and dialogue, in order to tell the story. During the
development of The Amulet, this modelling language was highly suitable for describing
narrative in this type of world. Many other game genres that use narrative applies the
same elements that this modelling language consists of. Prerequisites, dialogue, locations,
and actions are all a general part of many, if not all, narratives.
The model has been kept as general as possible. There has been a great focus on solely
adding narrative elements that can be generalised to other game genres. By keeping this
focus it allows the story engine to be used for a wider range of games. If one want to
develop a platform game, the narrative could easily be made utilising this story engine.
Collecting items throughout levels would be using prerequisites, hence, locations could
be thought of as levels. For FPSes as well, the same logic could be imagined for creating
the universe and desired narrative experience.

7.1 Modelling language
78
7.1.4
States vs ingame representation
The manner in which a designer applies the modelling language is fundamental to decide
which connection between the game and the narrative exists. It is important to under-
stand what the developed blocks for the game represent, as well as how it can be utilised
in either a strict connection or in a loose and abstract way. As an example, it is possible
for a block to represent a physical location in the designed game world. However, it
could also be representing a stage in the game, e.g. a time in the story when the plague
has spread. Those design methodologies contain certain be advantages to be considered.
The model does not attempt to disallow or encourage one idea over the other. However,
the model attempts to be general enough to allow for ideas to be visually represented
and connected in the logic of the narrative.
By allowing blocks in the modelling language not to be bound by static objects provides
a more abstract representation of the narrative story.
As an example, attaching all
block’s to physical components in the game may remove freedom in order to expand the
possibilities for a player. As a result, a prerequisite would solely be possible to gain in
one place of the world. The player might only be able to solve the puzzle with one type
of object, and not several items that all carried the same general prerequisite. The strict
representation disallows freedom for the player to come up with alternative solutions
to a problem. The player will have to find the prerequisites at a specific object in the
game. An example in a point-and-click adventure game is if a player have missed the
puzzle piece in a room and is are stuck as a result of overlooking this while searching the
room. This frustration could be lessened if there were more possibilities for completing
the puzzle.
An example where an abstract game representation of prerequisites could provide players
more agency, is when a key is required in order to access a door. One option could be
the key that is found on an NPC. The player could steal this key from them, or the
player could even try to convince the NPC to give it up. Second option would be that
the door could be lock picked with the correct tools the player found in a box somewhere
in the game. A final opportunity is to find a spell caster to unlock the door by casting a
spell. Many opportunities could be allowed for within a game that would allow players
to get through the door. Having this value on only one item in the game, misses the
opportunity of letting the player come up with new solutions. After the door is open,
the other possibilities can be toggled off using prerequisites, if these possibilities would
need to be removed.
7.1.5
What prerequisites represents
The modelling language represents the game world as a collection of prerequisites, logic
values that is achieved throughout a set of event, and states, location blocks.
This
representation, as mentioned in section 4.2, resembles the ideas of the PDDL language
for modelling AI behaviours. Every action will result in a new state with new effects and

new possible actions. The modelling language describes how prerequisites are fulfilled
by the players to enable events to start. As such, when things occur for the player it
can be described in a binary way; the current model cannot describe randomisation.
Randomised events could be introduced as an extension to the modelling language. These
events only occur if a random dice roll hits the correct range of numbers.
When a
player enters a location, it could be possible that an event starts. However, another
player, playing the same game in the same way, may not encounter this event because
of the randomisation. In order to support randomised events in the narrative model,
or even probability of actions and other items, the extension language of PDDL, called
PPDDL, can be introduced. The extra P is short for probabilistic and adds a function
on predicates, effects, and initialisation of PDDL. This function describes this range in
which something will happen.
For instance, an NPC could have a dialogue sequence that would only happen by chance
for a player. This dialogue would inform the player about some extra hints in the world.
As opposed to this information always being available, it now feels like finding an Easter
egg. To represent this probability in the modelling language, prerequisites could contain
a named function ”probability(0.4)”. What this function indicates, is that this block
has 40% chance of occurring, if all other prerequisites are complied with. This is a very
simple method to implement and allows for a lot of possibilities in terms of designing
random events in the game.
7.1.6
Letting players split up
A game that does not separate the characters, as done in the Amulet, would not want
to distinguish between local and global prerequisites. However, a game that keeps the
group together could make use of the possibility for different choices per player. Even in
The Amulet, it is not necessary for the players to split up, in order to complete events
or even the game. It is encouraged throughout different mechanics, that the players
should stick together, in order to explore the possibilities the one player cannot reach.
Examples of entering a location where the other player is missing prerequisites, taking
a choice the other cannot take because of his/her character choice. Such possibilities
reinforces teamwork and as such The Amulet would function even if they stick together
through all events and locations.
A system where the group have to agree upon which choice they are intended to go
forward with, might not even eliminate the need for the choice system. An example of
different choices within the system can be seen in SWTOR, where the class Sith has
different choices compared to the Imperial Agent. The synchronisation between players
is still be needed in the implementation.
Even if more than one choice were to be
eliminated this would be necessary. The server receive all choices and have to decide
which one should be sent back to the players, so it could be visualised which choice was
taken in the end.

7.2
Network in cooperative games
When multiple players play a game, it is required that all players have the same informa-
tion about the game world. As a result, their clients need to maintain synchronisation.
E.g. if two sessions become out of sync, it is possible to see the succubus in the story as
alive in one session and dead in the other. The server is responsible for ensuring actions
are managed in the same order. This became an issue when making the game for this
product. The system logic depends on the game implementation, and on prerequisites
and actions from the player.
In another example where two players are in the same conversation, the ordering of the
conversation needs to be the same on both screens. The priority system, as described
in section 4.3.4, involves having one message appearing on the screen before another.
Here it was necessary to wait for both players to choose an action, in order for the two
messages to occur in the correct order.
One additional game mechanic that was implemented, was ensuring only one choice from
each player was a part of each step of the conversation. This specific mechanic of waiting
for each other could be ignored in another implementation. Doing that would eliminate
the usability for the priority- and overrule-system. Removing these systems would not
make it possible to control the conversation from the modelling language.
7.2.1
Local vs online networking
A player has a local storage for information, containing the collection of prerequisites.
This information can help the other players in order to solve a common goal.
The
information shared among players are global prerequisites. The model even allows for
information that is kept secret, local prerequisites, which the player not necessarily wants
to share with other players. The multiplayer model build around gathering and sharing
this information is designed as an online network model. This choice gave rise to multiple
issues that could have been avoided if the game was implemented as a local cooperative
game.
Online network requires the server to be able to communicate as an external unit between
all clients. As a result, the client that is initiated from Unity will contact a program
that is running alongside it. The first problem encountered was integrity between the
server and clients, even between two clients. Integrity means that data sent between two
systems should be of the same version. If two versions of the systems communicate and
one system expects a different version of an object, the implementation of the protocol it
will cause errors. In c# InvalidCastException is the error that is thrown by the internal
system and is a common approach throughout different languages to highlight this issue
within an implementation.
In order to circumvent this issue the protocol was based on a shared library.
The

7.3
Multithreading issues
The architecture of the implementation relies heavily on a multithreading approach.
Having more threads in the game implementation allowed for a separation of the com-
munication layers and the game loop, along with splitting up the of work.
The client has several threads that listens for inputs from the player and server. When
introducing threaded work into a project new issues appear. When working with multi-
threading, two major sources of issues are found: Race conditions and dead locks. Race
conditions occur when the system attempts to perform two or more operations at the
same time. This becomes a problem when the operations must be conducted in the
proper sequence in order to be done correctly. Dead locks are when two processes are
fighting for common resources. When the waiting process is still holding onto another
resource needed for the first to finish if one of them is not told release their resources
they will be in a deadlock and will never terminate. Despite great advantages of having
a threaded architecture, it requires a new level of overview and understanding of the
product, in order to overcome these challenges as they appear.
When debugging software it is common to use stepping points in the software. When
stepping in a program the developer looks at the execution of this program line by line.
While stepping through the program, it will be possible to identify variables and evaluate
function calls at every line of the program. This is done in order to find the error or bug
in the code.
Debugging by using stepping points becomes trickier in the multithreaded case. When
stepping through the program, the execution is paused. In the multithreaded case paus-
ing one thread does not automatically pause all remaining threads from continuing
execution. Why this is an issue is illustrated with the following example: Executing a
normal test case will cause a bug in the program. When debugging one of the threads, it
will not cause this issue, even when looking at each step before and after the line where
the error occurred.
While debugging in this way, it is entirely possible for the program to be resulting in the
correct execution. This is due to delaying the execution of the thread, which leads to

7.4 Storing data

7.4
Storing data
The storage of data, as explained in section 5.3, is located in the Player class. The
player class is a localised database[46], which ensures that the player’s state is available
for the rest of the program to be used. However, it is managed through lists and property
objects, which requires the client to maintain this data. To get this data it is necessary
for the implementation to have access to the player object at all times throughout all
scenes in Unity. The class GameStateManager contains this reference to the player and
the implementation is forced to keep this reference up to date.
Another approach to storing data is to launch an external database. A simple entity-
relationship database, such as MySQL, provide the same functionality. Decoupling the
data into a database, that could be queried when needed, abstract this data away from
the code. The database should to be local, as the latency of transactions to a remote
database cause issues. Responsiveness of the game decline if the latency increase. An
additional advantage of having an external database is be in the case of saving session
data. In the current implementation, shutting down the game will delete all data from
the system. By having a database, the player could come back and reenter the game
from the player’s last location and state in the game.
7.4.1
XML
The data from the modelling language is collected in XML documents. Storing data
should never interfere with the implementation, it should only require validation. As
a result, when changing the data no changes should be made to the implementation.
Solely changing the structure of the document would require a different parsing unit.
Validation of XML is conducted using an XSD document. The document describes the
ruleset of the XML document and will explain errors in the data, if such exists. However,
the document will not present errors such as spelling or missing logic that extends beyond
the XML. If a block is missing a prerequisite it will not show an error. If XSD should
catch such an error with missing prerequisites it must be illegal for a block to have zero,
here it would not valid a block with no prerequisite.
Other methods of storing data exists, such as binary forms and JSON. These methods
contain strengths in terms of parsing and storing data. It depends on personal preferences
which format the specific developers prefer. Designers and writers prefer to stay away
from data formats such as XML. hey prefer to model and write in a more free form. It
would be more viable to have a system where the designers add visual models and this
system could output the data into the XML document.

7.5 Scaling solution

7.5
Scaling solution
A number of elements in the technical implementation of The Amulet is designed for
heavy up-scaling. Up-scaling is in this sense increasing the number of players, which
increase the amount of data sent back and forth(known as bandwidth). The client’s
network architecture is robust enough to handle an increasing amount of messages. Min-
imum amount of resources are being spend on the main thread, as to allow for a seamless
experience for the player. It is necessary for the game loop to inform the client which
element should be sent. All other information is managed by the client, waiting for
acknowledgement and other events. A number of other areas of the product could po-
tentially start seeing issues if either the bandwidth or the amount of players would
increase.
7.5.1
Scaling the server
The current implementation of the server allows for parallel execution by using threaded
workers, as described in section 5.1. The server will increase workload with additional
thread count. The thread count will increase when a new player connects to the server.
Additional messages sent between clients is resulting in more bandwidth being spent,
which is already split between each thread handling the individual messages.
On a
common server, the thread count varies from 10-20, depending on the CPU and the
hardware architecture of the server, which will not be explained here. As a result, the
current model will start seeing bottlenecks with 10 simultaneous players in one session.
If a server require more players in the same server session, it would be necessary to rewrite
the implementation to handle messaging. In the client, where messages are located in
the same entry level thread, distributed through a set of specific links of handlers and
distributor threads. This method of accommodating for an increasing number of players,
would put a static number on the threads needed in order to do the work. Additional
short lived threads would be spawned in order to handle responses to the requests from
clients and to send messages back into the queues of the server.
However, it is possible that this procedure would yield a bottleneck on the entry level
thread, as it would be a single point of failure, in terms of input and output for each
client’s messages.
Consequently, it would be necessary to scale the solution horizon-
tally[54]. As a result, the communication between each thread would require a common
storage to communicate, but, as explained before, a centralised database solution is
simple to implement and would be ideal in this case.
Changing the server model
Having the server, with its current system logic, as a broadcasting unit results in a
simple and cheap solution to solve multiplayer requirements. The game implementation
relies on the client side to handle the game logic. Cheating and exploiting in the game

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
The purpose of this research was to explore potentials and pitfalls when developing co-
operative IDS games. In order to accomplish this, it was chosen to attempt to develop a
cooperative IDS game, based on a thorough research on academia and industry standards.
This was done in order to investigate game design and technical aspects of IDS applica-
tions. Throughout the development process, the researchers found that they needed a
general modelling framework in order to structure and facilitate the narrative experience.
Consequently, the game, that was originally intended to be developed, gradually turned
into a story engine.
IDS represents an interesting field of the gaming industry with massive possibilities for
innovating by utilising the game medium in new narrative approaches. The interactive
part of games allows for story agency, which creates fundamentally different narrative
experiences compared to traditional narrative medias. A game with agency introduces
the need for additional content, since the game system has to allow players to make
choices. Such choices has the potential of quickly escalating a projects’ associated scope,
which impacts the associated development costs heavily. As such, this research has iden-
tified an association between branching narratives and development costs. This research
further emphasised how it is essential to understand cooperative design principles and
evaluation metrics when developing a cooperative game. This leads to cooperative games
requiring additional development resources, in order to accommodate for the additional
design requirements. Furthermore, it is found that there exist a competing requirement
between the number of players in a game and the amount of theoretical story agency
that each player can have. This is closely related to the trade-off between authorial
intend and story agency presented by Riedl et al. Theoretical story agency allows each
player to directly impact the story of a game in a meaningful manner. Consequently,
having more players increases the number of ways that a narrative can be impacted,
which results in the requirement for additional content.
As a result, in can be concluded that combining cooperative game elements with IDS
design principles results in a projects’ total scope and development costs increasing
significantly. In order to accommodate for this, this thesis suggests a number of design
considerations that developers profitably may utilise:
• Restricting number of players: This significantly reduce the amount of required
content and allows for developers to implement higher levels of story agency.
==PAGE:96==
8 Conclusion
89
• Limit branching: Avoiding exponential branching and utilising the pyramid and
foldback structure assists in limiting the branching narrative escalating.
• Prioritise perceived agency: Utilising IDS stagecraft can generate perceived agency,
while limiting theoretical agency, which results in less required content, especially
for a game with multiple players.
• Less individual story agency: Giving player less individual agency, in terms of
narrative, can drastically reduce the possible escalation of a narrative. Individual
agency, even through dialogue, could instead have mechanical impact, such as
player attributes.
• Keeping players together: Allowing players to play independently of each other in
a cooperative game generates new requirements, such as inter-player communica-
tion, additional synchronisation of player goals, and synchronisation of narrative
progression.
• Mediate narrative through environment and mechanics: Having game mechanics
and environment mediate narrative can reduce the amount of required narrative
description.
The design considerations are not to be perceived as strict design conventions, but rather
as set of design suggestions that can be recognised or ignored in the development of a
cooperative IDS game. However, this research advocates that designers have to act very
consciously about ignoring multiple of these suggestions, since each suggestion repre-
sents a very delicate design aspect of cooperative IDS game design and thus requires
a high level of resources and attention.
It can be argued that the above mentioned
considerations are quite restrictive, in terms what they allow cooperative IDS games to
accomplish. This could propose a reasoning as to why there have not been developed
more cooperative games with large amounts of player agency in terms of narrative.
When developing an online cooperative game it is necessary to establish an overarching
network architecture that is capable of handling multiple players. This research found
that the architecture needs high levels of reliability and validity, in order to ensure that
individual player sessions stay synchronised.
This was found to be important when
implementing IDS design principles, since narratives can become desynchronised, which
disrupts or breaks the narrative experience. Furthermore, it is important to approach
the development of a narrative logic in an as general manner as possible. Developing a
general narrative logic entails that the system should be easily extendable and modifiable.
Having a general system makes it more effortless to accommodate for design or narrative
changes, which is inevitable, due to the complex nature of interactive narratives with
large amounts of story agency. Furthermore, a general system makes it more simple
for designers and writers to introduce new narrative elements without the need for an
assisting developer. The system should thus be developed more as a tool for designers
and authors rather than a hard-coded implementation.

From an implementation perspective, this research proposes a number of technical meth-
ods that can be considered when developing an IDS game with cooperative gameplay:
• Implement rulesystem on server rather than on client: By letting the server have
the final ruling the system ensures that player sessions stay synchronised.
• Utilise multithreading: Makes it possible for multiple actions to happen at the
same time, which is advantageous when designing multiplayer games.
• Prerequisite system: The prerequisite system within the prototype presents an
approach to developing a general narrative logic system. It is flexible as well as
extendable, which makes it ideal for developing larger narrative structures.
• Use database for querying data: Using a database is recommendable as it allows
the system to have a centralisation of data. Additionally, it enables saving and
loading session data.
The above technical methods were derived from the prototype designed for this thesis. It
is thus not a final solution on how all online cooperative IDS games should be made, but
serves as a list of suggestions that made developing this product easier, or would have
made it less complicated. As many different implementation strategies exists to solve
the same problem, one should remain critical to which extent these suggestions should
be followed. For a minor system with very few prerequisites it might be an overshot to
develop a detailed prerequisite system like described in this thesis. The same applies for
utilising a database to store data for the game.
This research demonstrates that the designed prototype can assume the role of a viable
tool for writers and designers. Writers would most likely use it as a structuring tool
rather than a text implementation tool. Designers, however, would be able to use the
prototype for defining the narrative logic of a game and as a possible narrative develop-
ment platform. The research suggests that a possible reason as to why writers would not
use it for more than a structuring tool, is due to a profound difference between how game
designers and writers perceive the process of developing narratives. As the interviewed
game writer stated:
“I’m interested in writing about people no matter what genre or type of story
I’m telling, and the story has to have some kind of weight to it. What you’ve
made is a tool that can help me in terms of structure, which is nice, but in
the end it is not what is most essential to me as a writer.”
As it is illustrated in this quote the writer is more interested in traditional narratological
elements, and focus less about linking prerequisites or designing complicated intercon-
nected narrative networks. This thesis suggests that there is an underlying need for
game writers to develop a better understanding of game design as well as for game de-
signers to better understand writing. One way this could be accomplished is to develop

a system that takes into consideration both the workflow and design philosophy of both
professions. The prototype designed for this research does not, on its own, accomplish
this, since it appears to address the needs of designers to a higher extend than the needs
of game writers. However, it can be suggested that the prototype is a tool that, in col-

8.1
Critical reflection on research process
In the following section a critical reflection on the thesis’ research design and general
development process will be presented.
Given that the prototype was designed as a text-based game it has had the implications
of not being representative for a typical and larger game development.
All objects
within the story engine are instantiated at run-time, as such they cannot simply be
replaced with 3D models or 2D sprites, that has to be manually placed within the game
world. Furthermore, sounds, game environment, and animations are not accorded for
in the prototype. Consequently, it would be incredibly impractical for developers to
use the prototype for anything other than a text-based game. Additionally, it is worth
considering whether text-based games to a large extent are becoming outdated. Most
players expect graphics, movable avatars and interactable environments. As a result,
the researchers have had to constantly consider whether their implemented features in
theory could be generalised to a more comprehensive game development process.
Having developed both a story engine and a game could have had the effect of decen-
tralising the focus of the thesis. The reason for developing a game was to be able to
effectively validate the story engine’s potential. The initial focus of the prototype was
to investigate which features were needed in order to develop a cooperative narrative
experience with individual story agency. As such, the premise for the prototype was
how to develop a system that could facilitate such features. However, this meant that
the workflow of potential users of the prototype was completely neglected. This became
apparent when conducting the interview with the game writer. He explained how the
story engine did very little to accommodate for the majority of his work processes. This
resulted in him stating that he would not be able to use the engine, in its current form,
as a tool for writing game stories. The research could have benefited from consulting
with game writers in the preliminary research, since this could have drastically improved
the prototypes potential applicability.
In the process of prototyping the most thorough testing of the system was conducted
in the production phase as well as in the testing phase. This means that testing was
primarily done on a hi-fi prototype, both in terms of internal and external testing. A
quite significant problem was discovered at this point, namely the fact that players could
run out of choices. In the paper phase, and in most of the prototype phase, a direct model
==PAGE:99==
8.1 Critical reflection on research process
92
of the game’s narrative was available on a lo-fi prototype, i.e. a flow diagram. Nothing in
this diagram prevented the researchers from doing extensive internal gameplay testing,
which would have identified the problem at an earlier stage. Not doing extensive early
testing thus resulted in the problem not being fixed. Furthermore, it is noticeable that
fixing this problem could have shown new dynamics within the game, which in turn
could have yield new findings or adjusted current findings.
Having a threaded implementation on both the server and the client side, required a
substantial amount of work. The design of multithreading required a lot of understanding
around the new issues that arose, and took much time away from other parts of the
product. The need for threading becomes more critical in larger games, however not at
the scale of this product. In a larger production having multithreading is unavoidable.
A number of the identified problems indicated that it is necessary to be very stringent
about the way multithreading is implemented, as it can be a difficult process to debug
and error handle. Nothing new came from this work that could not have been realised
by a specialised person in the field, which would be necessary to acquire in order to make
a game in the scale where multithreading would becomes necessary.
The preliminary qualitative questionnaires utilised in this research were successful in
terms of identifying problems and interesting subjects in regards to IDS within the
gaming industry. However, a qualitative interview could have been conducted to obtain
this information and would likely have yielded a better result due the researcher’s ability
to probe for relevant information, as explained by Saunders [67, p. 321]. The reason
as to why such interviews were not utilised was that the approached candidates did not
have time.
This study has been of an exploratory and descriptive nature on the research field of IDS.
It has been conducted from both a technical and a design perspective. It is arguable
whether this divided approach has been beneficial for the research. Dividing the per-
spective can result in researchers delving less into the details of each perspective. In the
case of this thesis however, it can be suggested that both technical and design aspects
are intertwined. In order to develop a representational IDS game it is necessary for re-
searchers to possess a broad understanding of the current state of the research field, both
in terms of academia and industry standards. Additionally, the developed prototype has
given invaluable insight into the problems that arise when designing an IDS game. As
a result, it can be argued that even though the two research perspectives could have
interfered with each other, the researchers certainly benefited from this triangulation of
IDS as a research field.

8.2 Future Work
93
8.2
Future Work
Due to the additional production costs that are associated with branching narratives, an
interesting prospect for future research would be to validate the implemented mechanics
used in this thesis’ prototype. This research’s focus should be concerned with whether
players want to be able to split up and how they wish to make group decisions. This
could effectively be tested by conducting a mixed-methods study comprised of quan-
titative and qualitative investigation. In this scenario it would be useful to construct
different prototypes to represent each mechanic and validate those through user testing.
It can be argued that such prototypes could be conducted as paper-prototypes to reduce
development costs and time. This would also increase the number of testing iterations
to better shape each mechanic in accordance to user feedback.
Since this research has been of a qualitative nature with exploration as its primary goal, it
could be beneficial to follow up on the findings by applying quantitative research methods.
With a large sample size, such research would be able understand the generalisability of
the findings of this thesis. Such a study could seek to estimate whether the suggested
design considerations and technical methods were recognised by game designers and
system architects with experience in IDS game development.
All interviewed experts showed great interest in the story engine as a development tool.
Hence, it would be interesting for future research to further investigate the requirements
for such a tool. In such projects, an initial qualitative investigation on the specific needs
of game designers and authors would be beneficial. A useful methodological approach
would be the qualitative observation method. This would entail that researchers observe
game writers and game designers in order to understand their individual workflow. Qual-
itative observation can be incredibly enlightening and enrich the research data, and are
specifically good at discovering what people actually do in practice[67, p. 288].
The future of cooperative IDS games is looking to be a bright one with new titles
such as Divinity: Original Sin 2[72] and A Way Out[70] releasing in the coming years.
These games have their primary design premise in cooperation while delivering story
agency. Hopefully future titles such as these, along with further research, as suggested
in this thesis, will assist developers in understanding how cooperation successfully can
be implemented alongside a deep, compelling and interactive narrative experience.
