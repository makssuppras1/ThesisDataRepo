Abstract 
Given the rapid changes in Earth's climate and the damaging effects they bring, recognizing the 
need for action is crucial. While the ﬁlm industry might not be an obvious contributor to global warm-
ing, ﬁlm and TV production activities are signiﬁcant sources of anthropogenic emissions and drivers 
of climate change. 
A review of current sustainability practices in the ﬁlm industry highlights ongoing efforts to im-
plement green initiatives. Numerous productions have already achieved commendable results through 
sustainability practices, and various global organizations have developed tools and guidelines to guide 
the industry toward greener future productions. While the push for sustainability comes from within 
the industry, it is important to acknowledge the signiﬁcant knowledge gap and a lack of research on 
the impacts of implementing sustainable initiatives. The objective of this thesis is to link ﬁlm production 
to the context of climate change, assess the impacts of sustainability practices in ﬁlm and TV produc-
tion, examine their relationship to environmental health, and provide insights into the potential for a 
greener future for the ﬁlm industry. 
In this thesis, a case study using a hypothetical ﬁlm production model examines the carbon foot-
print of three different production scenarios and explores how sustainability practices can reduce it. A 
carbon calculator is used to create CO2-budgets based on the model parameters, and a comparative 
analysis of the results identiﬁes the primary contributors to carbon emissions. Additionally, an SDG 
impact assessment provides a broader perspective on sustainability by evaluating how transportation-
related activities in ﬁlm production affect individual Sustainable Development Goals. This assessment 
encompasses social and ﬁnancial sustainability, as well as environmental sustainability. 
The results from the case study indicate that carbon emissions can be signiﬁcantly reduced by 
implementing sustainability practices. Each production scenario investigated has different energy and 
resource requirements, resulting in varied CO2-budgets. Transportation-related activities in ﬁlm pro-
duction are identiﬁed as the major contributors to emissions. Interestingly, the remote ﬁlm production 
scenario emerges as the most favorable option for green practices in the ﬁlm industry with the lowest 
carbon budget, contradicting the current belief in the industry that favors virtual ﬁlm production. The 
Sustainable Development Goal impact assessment reveals that practicing sustainability involves much 
more than just addressing climate concerns. 
Both the case study and the Sustainable Development Goal impact assessment involve limitations 
and uncertainties due to their reliance on simpliﬁed models and restricted scopes. Model complexity is 
a crucial factor to consider for interpreting the results, and it is recommended to expand the study to 
encompass more aspects of ﬁlm production activities and to consider how impacts might differ in other 
regions or on a global scale. The ﬁnancial aspect, while not considered in the assessment, is identiﬁed 
as a signiﬁcant challenge. Productions must balance the cost of sustainability alternatives with other 
budgetary demands, ensuring that investments in sustainability do not compromise the overall quality 
and feasibility of the production. Furthermore, the utilized carbon calculator tool presents limitations, 
and exploring other tools could be beneﬁcial for a more comprehensive analysis. 
This comprehensive perspective highlights the interconnectedness of climate action, environ-
mental sustainability, and human health. By focusing on reducing emissions, we not only address cli-
mate change but also create healthier environments for both current and future generations.

1. Introduction 
The Earth’s climate is changing, which is evident from several elements, such as changes in tem-
peratures and precipitation patterns, as well as intensity, frequency, and duration of extreme weather 
events and shifts in ecosystem characteristics. These effects are due to the accumulation of greenhouse 
gasses (GHGs) in the atmosphere and the warming of planet Earth (United States Environmental Pro-
tection Agency, 2023b). There are natural causes of climate change, but the major driver is the effects 
of human activities (IPCC, 2023; United States Environmental Protection Agency, 2023a). 
 
Figure 1 – Simple visualization of global warming from the greenhouse effect. Inspired by an illustration from (United States Environmen-
tal Protection Agency, 2023b), the ﬁgure shows how the radiation from sun rays enters the earth’s atmosphere and is absorbed on the earth’s 
surface while some rays are reﬂected by the atmosphere and the surface. Meanwhile, the earth’s surface also emits radiation. Finally, anthropo-
genic sources, e.g. ﬁlm production, emits greenhouse gasses, contributing to global warming. The greater the anthropogenic carbon emissions, the 
greater the greenhouse effect and thereby the greater global warming. 
The environment plays a crucial role in the health of the planet and all living creatures (Friis, 
2019). Anthropogenic emissions from activities such as burning of fossil fuels by using transportation, 
generating power, or producing food contribute to climate change (IPCC, 2012). These activities col-
lectively impact the planet and its environment, as illustrated in Figure 1. All living beings, humans 
included, rely on environmental conditions, meaning climate change affects more than just the envi-
ronment. (Friis, 2019) describes how environmental conditions are closely connected with human 
health, inﬂuencing illness and mortality rates. This connection is known as environmental health and 
encompasses how external environmental factors – physical, chemical, and biological – can impact 
human behavior and health. The climate beneﬁts from emissions reductions, but it also entail de-
creased pollution, which has important direct impacts on human health. Emissions reductions contrib-
ute to several aspects such as improved air quality, which lower the incidence of respiratory and cardi-
ovascular diseases. Cleaner water reduces the risk of waterborne diseases and improves overall public 
health. Decreased air and water pollution beneﬁt soil quality, which is crucial for growing food and 
reducing the risk of toxic substances entering the food chain. Finally, it has a positive effect on overall

ecosystem health that supports biodiversity and provide services essential for human well-being, such 
as clean water, fertile soil, and pollination (Friis, 2019). In context to the topic of this thesis, activities 
related to ﬁlm and TV production pose as an example of an anthropogenic source generating carbon 
emissions that contribute to climate change and global warming. Given the damaging consequences 
that the planet is undergoing from climate change, a behavior adjustment is imperative. 
Within the ﬁlm industry, guidelines, tools, and green certiﬁcations have been developed on a 
global scale to support this change. Productions of ﬁlm and TV series are encouraged to assess their 
carbon emissions and implement more sustainable practices (Donnelly, 2023). A way to assess carbon 
emissions is through three scopes. Scope 1 includes direct emissions from fuel combustion by owned 
machines, vehicles, and other fossil fuels. Scope 2 and 3 cover indirect emissions, where Scope 2 in-
cludes emissions from generation of purchased energy, such as electricity, steam, heat, or cooling. 
Scope 3 includes emissions from upstream and downstream activities, such as travel, purchased goods, 
services, equipment, and the transportation and distribution of goods (Viegand Maagøe, n.d.). These 
scopes are primarily utilized for climate reporting in a political legislative context. The European Un-
ion (EU) and its member countries are required to report GHG emissions, climate policies, measures, 
and progress towards the targets posed by the United Nations (UN) as parties of the United Nations 
Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) (European Commission, 2024a). The EU has 
phased in the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), which, per the EU directive on 
amending regulations and directives concerning corporate sustainability reporting (Directive 
2022/2464, n.d.), introduces detailed sustainability reporting requirements on companies operating in 
the EU (European Commission, 2024b). The scope of CSRD obligations is dependent on a company’s 
size and whether it is publicly listed (Meynier et al., 2023), and while the ﬁlm industry is not subject to 
required climate reporting, collaborative efforts are working to align with climate goals (European 
Commission, 2024c) and environmentally friendly measures are progressively being adopted in pro-
ductions, including on-screen green storytelling (Meilani, 2021). Another way for assessing production 
emissions, which is more widespread in the ﬁlm industry, is through the use of carbon budgets. The 
developed guidelines and tools mentioned above are utilized to create these carbon budgets, offering a 
different perspective on the distribution of emissions across various production areas. This thesis will 
focus on evaluating carbon emissions from ﬁlm production using carbon budgets, and this approach 
will be discussed further in Chapter 3. 
With a focus on reducing carbon emissions from production, the ﬁlm industry must also consider 
how implementing initiatives can affect other aspects of sustainability. The UN Sustainable Develop-
ment Goals (SDGs) illustrate that practicing sustainability entails so much more than climate change 
(United Nations, n.d.). The SDG framework is composed of 17 goals, which all together guarantee 
sustainable development once they are achieved and aim to embrace all stakeholders involved in tech-
nological and non-technological advancements within society (Laurent et al., 2023). The SDGs and 
their relevance for the ﬁlm industry will be discussed further in Chapter 3 of this thesis. 
Sustainable trends are being observed in the ﬁlm industry and actions have commenced to adapt 
and implement environmentally friendly practices into the productions, such as implementing waste 
management systems with recycling, planning travel to cut down trips, and eliminating single-use plas-
tic items (Donnelly, 2023). The effort and devotion are evident both behind the scenes and on screen, 
although, as stated on a panel lead by (BAFTA, 2023) about the urgency of climate storytelling, Tom 
Mustill, Head of Gipping Films, expresses how “talking about climate would be a positive thing, but

context of climate change and outline the current state of sustainable practices as well as offer insights 
into the possibilities of a greener future for the ﬁlm industry. 
1.1 Research questions 
This thesis aims to explore impacts of sustainability practices in ﬁlm production and how the 
implementation of green initiatives affects environmental health, with the following research questions: 
1. How can implementation of more sustainable initiatives/practices reduce the carbon footprint 
from production in the ﬁlm industry? 
1.1. What is the greatest emission contributor? 
2. How do different production settings (the three scenarios) impact the climate? 
2.1. Which environment related SDGs are affected by practicing sustainability in the ﬁlm in-
dustry? And how? 
3. What are the major beneﬁts and likewise challenges from the sustainable implementations? 
3.1. How does it relate to environmental health? 
This thesis embraces three main components that work together to answer the posed research 
questions. Firstly, a review of the current state of sustainable practices and challenges in the ﬁlm indus-
try is conducted. Next, a case study is outlined, which will be used to assess the impacts of implementing 
green initiatives in three different production scenarios. Lastly, an SDG impact assessment is completed 
to provide a broader perspective on sustainability in ﬁlm and TV production by indicating the positive 
and negative impacts associated with relevant goals from implementing the sustainable practices iden-
tiﬁed and analyzed in the aforementioned sections. 
The remainder of this thesis is structured as follows: Chapter 2 presents a review to assess the 
current state of sustainable practices in the ﬁlm industry and to clarify the connection to climate 
change. Chapter 3 describes the methodology of the case study framework with details on model pa-
rameters and assumptions as well as the framework of the SDG impact assessment. Chapter 4 presents 
further detail on the three different production scenarios investigated in the case study as well as how 
the model parameters apply to each of them. The ﬁndings are presented in Chapter 5 followed by 
Chapter 6 that contains a discussion of all the results in addition to an evaluation of how sustainable 
practices in the ﬁlm industry relate to environmental health. Finally, Chapter 7 and Chapter 8 con-
cludes this thesis and provides recommendations for future work. 
2. Review of current sustainability practices and challenges in the ﬁlm in-
dustry 
The review sets the stage for and segues into the topic of this thesis. The basic concepts of ﬁlm 
production and the current employed green initiatives in the industry are presented and explained to 
gain an understanding of the connection to climate change. The current stage of sustainability practices 
in the ﬁlm industry is assessed along with the major beneﬁts and challenges of implementation. 
Literature is collected through web-search on Google Scholar and DTU Findit, news articles, 
the individual webpages of guideline- and certiﬁcation organizations as well as in textbooks and 
through video material.

2.1 The ﬁlm industry in the context of climate change 
In the state of the climate crisis, worldwide participation and effort in knowledge sharing, tech-
nology development, and conducting improvements are crucial in order to make the world a better 
place for the future. While the ﬁlm industry might not be at the top of anyone’s mind as an entity that 
is relevant for shaping the sustainable future or responding to the climate crisis, it is important to rec-
ognize a substantial carbon footprint associated with ﬁlm and TV production (Whittington, 2022). 
Emissions occur throughout all stages of cinematographic production, which covers processes and ac-
tivities from development to distribution through a total of ﬁve stages as illustrated in Figure 2. 
 
 
Figure 2 – Overview of cinematographic production stages. Each stage include several elements that bring a production from an idea to the 
ﬁnished product (Kryeziu, 2019). The estimated duration of each stage in the process is included (Roberts, 2023). 
The process of creating a cinematographic product from concept to the ﬁnished result spans, on 
average, from one to three years, with many factors in play to affect the timeframe. As stated by (Rob-
erts, 2023), “the more money you have, the longer you get to play” indicates the budget to be the most 
notable factor, which correlatedly inﬂuences the emission levels (Sustainable Production Alliance, 
2021). Additional factors affecting the production time span are, for one, the ﬁlm genre. Different ﬁlm 
genres can have varying needs for effects and stunts. Moreover, the location and weather conditions 
can affect the production time span as well as the inclusion of child actors or animals. Contingencies 
in general, such as equipment failure or the need for reshoots, can also impact the timeframe. There-
fore, all projects will have unique and speciﬁc requirements (Roberts, 2023). 
Sustainability is not a new concept for the ﬁlm industry. For over a decade, it has been driven by 
internal motivations and efforts to implement greener operations (TV2 Danmark, 2023). The Sustain-
able Production Alliance (SPA) is an organization that has helped develop green production guides, 
tools, and resources and helped to implement these sustainable initiatives in the industry. SPA releases 
climate reports from a global perspective of the entertainment industry (The Credit, 2020). From the 
work of the SPA, various collaborations have emerged worldwide, accompanied by the creation of 
tools aiming to consult and assist the ﬁlm industry in reducing its climate footprint. Some of the tools

2.2 Green certiﬁcations and tools for ﬁlm production 
It is essential to assess the climate impacts of the industry to determine hotspots and areas where 
improvement is needed. A comprehensive analysis of a production’s carbon footprint is used in an 
assessment to identify practical ways to mitigate signiﬁcant effects (Sustainable Production Alliance, 
2021). Tools, like those mentioned in Table 1, are useful in such an analysis. Some tools offer a sug-
gested set of guidelines in a checklist format and compiled resources designed to enhance sustainability. 
They are often structured and targeted to a speciﬁc department in the production such as Studio, Arts 
and set design, or Camera. Other tools entail guidelines that are extended to incorporate a certiﬁcation 
system. The certiﬁcation acts as an incentive for productions to proactively take measures to diminish 
their carbon footprint by leading to recognition and, in some cases, awards. The guidelines are used 
along with a carbon calculator to achieve certiﬁcation (wearealbert, 2024). 
Data provided from the production activities, such as production ofﬁce, studio, travel, accom-
modation locations, materials purchase, and post-production, feeds into the carbon calculator and al-
lows the production to get an understanding of its climate impact (wearealbert, 2024). The carbon 
calculator exists in different formats, either as an Excel sheet or an online programmed tool. Scientiﬁc 
databases containing information on emission factors are linked to the carbon calculator, and by en-
tering the production data, an emission budget is generated that indicates the level of emission across 
different production departments and categories (Green Producers Club, n.d.-a). The unit for emis-
sions provided by the carbon calculator is expressed in carbon dioxide-equivalents (CO2-eq), a metric 
measure utilized to compare the emissions of different greenhouse gases based on their global warming 
potential (GWP), as deﬁned by the (European Environment Agency, n.d.) glossary.

With the general numbers established for carbon emissions from the ﬁlm industry, how does it 
look when sustainable measures are being implemented? The subsequent section will present the results 
from two case studies, which employed green procedures to reduce the climate footprint. 
2.3.1 The Amazing Spider-Man 2 
The Amazing Spider-Man 2, produced by Sony Pictures in the United States of America (USA), 
stands as a great example of how efforts to implement sustainable practices in ﬁlm production can 
improve the carbon footprint and climate impact. The ﬁlm production team executed green operations 
which included 49.7 tons of material secured for donation or reuse in future productions, 193,000 
disposable plastic water bottles saved to achieve a 52% diversion rate from landﬁlls, and 5,862 meals 
donated to local shelters to reduce production waste. The efforts did not only save expenses on the 
climate account, but also managed to save more than $400,000. This achievement was published with 
the statement “Sustainability is not expensive”. Much of the success in being sustainable was achieved 
with the help of an eco-manager, whose job it was to oversee and enforce the sustainable practices on 
set (Girkout, 2014). 
2.3.2 1917 
A more recent example constitutes the “albert” certiﬁcation of the large-scale ﬁlm 1917, pro-
duced by Neal Street Productions in the United Kingdom (UK). The carbon emissions from big-budget 
ﬁlms are enormous with everything from vast fuel and power use to waste generation and numerous 
cast and crew carrying a large carbon footprint. It sets a high demand for the successful implementation 
of sustainable efforts. Much like on The Amazing Spider-Man 2, the 1917 production employed environ-
mental assistants to monitor the operations throughout pre-production and production. The imple-
mented green practices included limiting the use of printed documents, only using recycled paper, 
banning disposable plastic cups and water bottles as well as offering vegetarian meals obtained from 
sustainable suppliers. Also, utilizing generators powered by waste vegetable oil rather than diesel and 
ensuring efﬁcient waste management and recycling systems. Every department on the production was 
included in the environmentally friendly operations in ways that were applicable to them. The produc-
tion additionally avoided unnecessary air travel by hiring cast and crew based in the UK as well as 
choosing ﬁlming locations reachable by train travel. Much of the ﬁlm’s action was set in trenches for 
which the production chose military training bases as locations to avoid digging into the English coun-
tryside and thereby reducing damage to the land and biodiversity (wearealbert, 2020). 
The sustainable initiatives undertaken by the two case studies obtained commendable results, 
and several other instances exist where ﬁlm productions have embraced eco-friendly practices and have 
been awarded with certiﬁcations (Calawerts, 2022). While these instances provide valuable examples, 
putting sustainable principles into practice is not always straightforward. There is a lack of publicly 
available data on production-speciﬁc carbon emissions, including those from productions like The 
Amazing Spider-Man 2 and 1917, as such data is rarely released. The absence of data presents a signiﬁ-
cant obstacle for sustainable decision-making and green development in the ﬁlm industry. The follow-
ing section highlights some of the primary challenges that ﬁlm productions encounter in their attempt 
to embrace environmentally friendly practices.

2.4 Challenges in implementing sustainability 
The correlated increase in carbon emission with the production scale was highlighted in section 
2.1. A bigger production budget causes a demand for an ampliﬁed number of cast and crew, leading 
to an increased need for consumables, transportation, catering, materials, and equipment. This also 
entails greater resource consumption and waste generation. As stated by (wearealbert, 2020), one of 
the challenges of making big productions sustainable simply comes down to the volume of people work-
ing on the project. 
Additionally, certain eco-friendly alternatives may pose limitations. For example, transitioning 
all production vehicles to electric models and relying on battery-powered generators necessitates the 
availability of charging stations. Finding and choosing catering companies with plant-based meal op-
tions and a focus on organic, fair-trade, and locally-grown foods also depend on availability. The tran-
sition to green alternatives is a work in progress and can require stepwise integration by focusing on 
one area at a time. In the process of shifting to green alternatives it is essential to consider how the 
integration impacts both time and ﬁnancial resources (Scripps News Staff, 2022). 
Another challenge, which is important to highlight, is the current gap in up-to-date comprehen-
sive, academically, and journalistically informed research on the ﬁlm industry’s impact on climate 
change (Simpson, 2023). As portrayed in Table 1, a variety of tools with guidelines and resources exist 
on a global level. However, awareness of these resources, as well as appropriate use of them, seem to 
pose a complication. The concept of greenwashing becomes relevant in this context. Greenwashing is 
a mode of corporate environmentalism that ﬂashes a so-called environmental card – replacing dispos-
able water bottles with reusable alternatives and choosing digital communication to reduce paper con-
sumption – while continuing with the business of capitalist excess as usual (Vaughan, 2021). Whether 
it is a deliberate choice or a lack of sufﬁcient knowledge that leads to greenwashing, this presents a 
challenge for the environmental sustainability efforts within the ﬁlm industry. The data from the SPA 
report (Sustainable Production Alliance, 2021), as well as the use of carbon calculator tools, provide 
clear evidence of the primary impact areas that should be the initial focus for improvements. 
The ﬁlm industry is embracing sustainability along with other more conspicuous sectors, yet a 
tendency to underreport emissions within the industry has been observed. Organizations such as the 
SPA aim to provide support, consult, and assist the productions in reducing their climate impacts, and 
they also seek to instill ﬁrmer restrictions. However, a great deﬁcit lies in the lack of research, which is 
intertwined with the absence of reporting legislation and comprehensive environmental regulations 
speciﬁc to the ﬁlm industry (Marsh, 2024). 
Post-pandemic and in line with the global objectives of climate neutrality, action plans and col-
laborations are gradually emerging, imposing green standards on the industry (European Commission, 
2020; Marsh, 2024) as well as initiatives and strategies from individual companies and producers (Jen-
nifer, 2023). 
2.5 Working toward a more sustainable industry 
The climate crisis calls for change and the industry itself is pushing for more sustainability. Great 
companies like Netﬂix present an approach to net zero involving the three R’s: Reduce, Retain, Re-
move, which aims to reduce carbon emissions by 45% below 2019 levels by 2030. Furthermore, Disney 
plans to invest in natural climate solutions, aiming to achieve net zero emissions for its direct operations 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
10 
by 2030 (Jennifer, 2023). In Denmark all productions on the television channel TV2 are per internal 
policies (TV2 Danmark, 2023) from 2024 required to make a sustainable action plan along with a 
CO2-budget prior to commencing operations and follow up with CO2-accounting. 
The majority of the push for a green movement is coming from within the ﬁlm industry itself and 
there are many different ways to improve sustainability. Climate action is a highly important focus – 
not just for the ﬁlm industry, but on a worldwide level – and a key subject is collaboration (Calawerts, 
2022). 
This completes the review of the ﬁlm industry in the context of climate change, encompassing 
current sustainability practices, available tools and their application in green certiﬁcation, and chal-
lenges faced in achieving environmentally friendly ﬁlm production.

3. Methodology 
In this chapter, the framework for the case study and data treatment approach for evaluating the 
impact of implementing sustainability practices are detailed. The different elements and methods ap-
plied to answer the research questions of this thesis include a review of the current sustainability prac-
tices and challenges in the ﬁlm industry and developing a case study to explore implementation of 
sustainability in a ﬁlm production model based on three different production scenarios. CO2-budgets 
are generated to undergo analysis and evaluation. Lastly, an SDG impact assessment is performed.  
3.1 Case study framework 
The case study aims to explore a sustainability model for a ﬁlm production, including different 
production scenarios with varying needs and requirements for successful project completion. The pro-
duction scenarios are speciﬁed in Chapter 4. The overall process of the study covers three steps of 
research, application, and evaluation as illustrated in Figure 6. 
 
Figure 6 – Case study process ﬂow from research to application and ﬁnal evaluation. 
The goal of the case study is to utilize a comprehensive set of guidelines to support decision-
making when implementing sustainability practices in a ﬁlm production and help close the existing 
knowledge gap in the industry as mentioned in section 2.4. The absence of recent and comprehensive 
research in the ﬁeld simply causes an inadequate foundation of data to assess signiﬁcant areas of climate 
impact in the ﬁlm industry. The case study attempts to make sustainability practices a common strategy 
for all areas of a production by providing speciﬁc guidelines for each department. The goal is to estab-
lish a production-wide inclusion of sustainable responsibility rather than relying on a single eco man-
ager to oversee the operations. 
At the time of writing this thesis, the applied sustainability guidelines remain conﬁdential and are 
provided in a conﬁdential appendix 10.7. Relevant elements from the sustainability guidelines are ex-
tracted and included in this thesis to support the context without needing the actual guidelines. The 
sustainability guidelines are part of a research project by Vision Denmark and will be released and 
made available at a later date. 
A carbon calculator tool is used to generate CO2-budgets to display the emissions from the pro-
duction activities before and after reﬂecting on the guideline recommendations. CO2-budgets from 
each production scenario are compared to reveal varying emission levels across their distinct activities. 
An assessment will determine which production area to focus on to obtain the greatest impact and 
which production scenario is the greener option.

3.3 Carbon calculator tool methodology and data treatment approach 
The CO2-budgets provide the basis for the data treatment portion of this thesis and are generated 
by the carbon calculator tool: Green Producers Tool (GPT). The GPT is chosen and applied for the 
explorative exercise mainly because of its accessibility. Compared to other accessible tools, such as the 
French “Carbon’Clap” or the EU-developed “Eureca”, the GPT offers more complexity and level of 
detail for carbon reporting, which accommodates the aspects of the applied sustainability guidelines. 
The GPT is widely used in the Nordic countries and developed in partnership with the Norwe-
gian organization the Center for International Climate Research (CICERO) (CICERO, n.d.) who 
have performed research and calculations on all the emission factors used in the tool. The tool 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
22 
calculates both direct emissions along with the life cycle analysis (LCA) of each product and activity. 
The LCA methodology analyses environmental impacts associated with all the stages of the life cycle 
of a product whether that is e.g. a camera or a car. The GPT currently has 900 emission factors inte-
grated, which are determined by ﬁrstly mapping what materials each product consists of, then calcu-
lating emissions all the way from where and how the materials are extracted, estimating the manufac-
turing and transport emissions, and ﬁnally, calculating the expected lifetime of the speciﬁc product. 
The emission factors are selected with partners in the entertainment industry to ensure the relevance 
for application across the industry. Additionally, the emission categories consider local speciﬁcities such 
as energy sources and mixes (Green Producers Club, n.d.-b). 
The GPT focuses on the global warming potential and the tool represents the amount of gener-
ated GHG emissions in CO2-eq. The GHG emissions are calculated from different categories across 
the production by the following formula: 
𝐸𝑚𝑖𝑠𝑠𝑖𝑜𝑛 (𝑘𝑔 𝐶𝑂!𝑒𝑞) = 𝐸𝑚𝑖𝑠𝑠𝑖𝑜𝑛 𝑓𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑜𝑟 (𝑘𝑔 𝐶𝑂!𝑒𝑞/𝑦) · 𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑖𝑣𝑖𝑡𝑦 (𝑦) 
Where the activity (y) is expressed by the number of products, physical mass in kg, volume (m3 
or liters), distance (km), or energy consumption (kWh), for instance (Green Producers Club, 2024). 
The tool aims to implement a methodology to prevent double counting in reporting. Double 
counting occurs when carbon emissions related to a speciﬁc activity or product are recorded for two 
different productions or uses. The challenge is to allocate the GHG emissions linked to the production 
and distribution of products to a single speciﬁc production. For art materials, such as wood or textiles, 
the tool allows users to specify whether the item is a new purchase or being reused. For equipment, 
such as cameras and microphones, the tool allocates emissions based on the number of days the equip-
ment is used, calculating a proportion of the total usage time accordingly (Green Producers Club, 
2024).  
The GPT generates the CO2-budgets as data output, providing a quantitative distribution of 
emissions across categories. The data is plotted in charts and compared across the initial budget ﬁnd-
ings with the revised budget ﬁndings. An analysis is conducted to examine the sensitivity of varying 
parameters across the different production scenarios. Each production scenario is individually assessed 
in the case study, resulting in initial CO2-budgets with different input model parameters. The analysis 
includes a discussion of how these parameters differ, the reasons for these differences, and their impact 
on the emission budgets. 
The results of applying the GPT will be further detailed in Chapter 0.

The objective of the second phase (the scoping phase) is to identify and map all activities and pro-

Phase 3 
Inventorying the effects from project application(s) 
Based on the baseline and new system conceptualized in Phase 2, the effects for the system are 
identiﬁed in the third phase. The effects are changes in activities or processes in the new system 
compared to the baseline which may have important impact(s) on the SDGs. The effects can be very 
diverse and be of either physical or non-physical nature. The physical effects come directly from the 
changes applied in the new system whereas the non-physical effects often are derived from the 
physical ones and can be of economic, social, ethical, or political character. 
Phase 4 
Evaluating the impacts of the application(s) on the SDGs 
The effects identiﬁed in Phase 3 need to be linked to all the SDGs, which can be impacted positively 
and/or negatively. Identifying and evaluating these links is the objective of Phase 4, which is divided

-

quences linking effects to the goals of the UN SDG framework. 
- 
Characterize in a semi-quantitative way the impact of each effect on the SDGs.

Interpreting the assessment results 
Through the previous phases, the impact on the SDGs have been identiﬁed, categorized in a life 
cycle perspective, and semi-quantitatively assessed. This serves as the basis for the interpretation that 
is done in Phase 5. The ﬁfth phase evaluates possible hotspots for improvement and unveils trade-
offs within and across the impacted SDGs. 
Through the ﬁrst 4 phases, the impact of the case study and its considered application(s) on the 
SDGs are identiﬁed, categorized, and semi-quantitatively assessed leading to evaluation and ﬁnally 
interpretation. The results and assessment evaluation are further discussed in Chapters 5 and 6.

4. Case study on implementing sustainability initiatives 
This section presents further details for the case study on sustainability in a ﬁlm production set-
ting. The case study will assess three different production conceptual paths and scenarios which are: 
1. Virtual production (VP) 
2. Filming on location/in studio 
3. Remote production (RP) 
The three production scenarios represent different methods or approaches of a cinematographic 
production process. The three production scenarios are included in the case study because they provide 
different aspects of cinematographic production in order to clarify potential knowledge gaps and aid 
in future sustainable decision-making.   
How do the scenarios differ? VP is based on using computer techniques with generated imagery 
and technologies to create realistic environments and effects on a virtual set (Okwuowulu & Michael, 
2022). The second scenario, ﬁlming on location/in studio, entails a physical element and the presence 
of a built set, props, and costumes, amongst other factors. RP offers a mix of virtual and physical 
elements of ﬁlm production, where directors, cast, and crew can work across digital platforms from 
different locations and parts of the world. The use of an online monitor to direct scenes or virtual 
private web networks for ﬁle sharing, meetings, and production is feasible with RP (Honthaner, 2010). 
A ﬁlm production usually involves a combination of elements from all three scenarios. For in-
stance, ﬁlming scenes in outdoor locations will be supplied with VP technology that generates and 
animates visual effects. Each of the scenarios has its own needs in terms of energy and resources. For 
example, shooting on location will necessitate transportation of equipment and personnel, and the 
construction of various elements for stages and sets in a studio will require signiﬁcant amounts of ma-
terials as well as energy for power and electricity. Additionally, special effects sequences may involve 
the use of specialized equipment and technologies that consume additional resources. With the ongoing 
development of technology, the virtual element has become more prominent in ﬁlmmaking as it re-
duces material consumption and waste generation as well as, to some degree, eliminates travel and 
transportation-related activities (Whittington, 2022). The general consensus in the industry is that VP 
represents the green future of cinematographic production (Donnelly, 2023). The case study will be 
the gateway to exploring the impacts of implementing more sustainable initiatives in production. For 
each scenario, an exercise will be conducted in implementing recommendations for sustainable prac-
tices and applying a carbon calculator tool to measure the impacts. The exercise provides the data for 
this thesis and by analyzing data and comparing the output from the three different scenarios aims to 
indicate the better sustainable approach to move towards a green ﬁlm industry. 
The parameters and assumptions described above in section 3.2 form the foundation of data 
input, which is adapted to the varying needs of each production scenario. As mentioned above, a ﬁlm 
production usually involves a combination of elements from all the scenarios. However, the case study 
examines three different production scenarios (VP, ﬁlming on location/in studio, and RP) individually, 
meaning each scenario is assessed as if it were a complete ﬁlm production. Only the relevant activities 
(Figure 7) and model parameters for each production scenario are selected when entering data into the 
carbon calculator tool. For example, VP does not include physical set elements, so the set building 
activity is omitted from its CO2-budget. VP is the only scenario that does not encompass all activities,

4.2 Revised CO2-budget parameters 
Below is an overview of the sustainability recommendations for each of the relevant production 
departments and how they are implemented and reﬂected in the CO2-budgets. 
Accommodation 
The guidelines recommend prioritizing green-certiﬁed hotels for accommodation. However, this 
aspect is not reﬂected in the tool and therefore not included in the revised CO2-budgets. 
Arts and set design 
The guidelines suggest using natural or biodegradable materials, including natural paint, cotton 
or wool fabrics, and sustainably sourced timber. Additionally, they recommend reusing or borrowing 
materials or set parts from other productions as well as donating it to another production, organization, 
or charity store after wrap. New materials should be purchased from socially and eco-conscious sup-
pliers with green labels. While the revised CO2-budgets implement the reuse of some materials, the 
tool does not offer the detail needed to reﬂect a potential green label. 
Camera 
The guidelines recommend prioritizing digital recording and considering the required resolution 
quality. The current budgets reﬂect digital recording, but the tool does not offer options to specify 
resolution quality. Therefore, no changes are made in the revised CO2-budgets for camera equipment. 
Casting 
The guidelines recommend casting actors from the local area to reduce travel and providing 
information on public transport options for commuting to the casting location. The revised CO2-budg-
ets reﬂect this by reducing the travel distance for actors and substituting means of transportation. 
Catering 
The guidelines advocate for serving organic, seasonal, fair trade, free-range, local, plant-based 
meals, focusing on planet-friendly eating and minimizing resource consumption and waste. They sug-
gest using reusable dishware and cutlery, water dispensers, and reusable beverage containers, as well 
as providing coffee, tea, and condiments in bulk. The revised CO2-budgets implement plant-based 
meals; however, the tool does not allow for specifying whether these meals are organic, seasonal, fair 
trade, or free-range. Water is changed from plastic bottles to tap water, assumed to be poured into 
reusable bottles, and reusable dishware and cups are considered in the budgets. 
 
==PAGE:39==
 
 
30 
Location transportation and travel 
The guidelines recommend using public transportation, bikes, or ridesharing for location travel. 
If vehicles are used, they should be electric, eco-friendly small vehicles. Travel should be planned to 
consolidate meetings, scouts, and casting sessions to reduce trips and mileage, with remote work sug-
gested when possible. The revised CO2-budgets reﬂect the substitution of conventional cars with eco-
friendly transportation such as public transport and ridesharing with small electric vehicles. 
Post-production 
The guidelines recommend using digital and low-energy solutions for online footage storage and 
transfer, and prioritizing studios powered by renewable energy sources. However, the tool does not 
provide options to reﬂect these recommendations and no changes are made in the revised CO2-budgets 
for editing. 
Power and electricity 
The guidelines suggest using green energy generators or green power sources like wind, solar, 
hydrogen, or biomass. Buildings with community grids and green energy certiﬁcations should be cho-
sen, and electrical appliances with A+, A++, and A+++ labels should be used. Equipment should be 
turned off when not in use. The revised CO2-budgets implement the use of green energy generators 
where applicable. 
Production transportation and travel 
The guidelines recommend prioritizing public transportation for commuting to the studio. If 
public transport is unavailable, electric cars with ride sharing should be used. The revised CO2-budgets 
reﬂect the implementation of substituting conventional cars with public transportation for commuting. 
Studio 
The guidelines suggest utilizing green power supply, such as mains electricity from renewable 
sources, and using eco-labeled materials, electronics, and monitors. It is also recommended to avoid 
printing and to minimize resource consumption. The tool does not offer detailed options to reﬂect the 
green power supply or eco-labeled materials, so the revised CO2-budgets do not differ from the initial 
budgets for the studio department. 
Virtual production 
The guidelines recommend adopting a "reduce, reuse, recycle" philosophy. This includes running 
modules and ﬁlming on smaller setups to reduce energy consumption and scanning real-life environ-
ments from locations to recreate and use later for reshoots, minimizing the need to travel back to 
locations. The revised CO2-budgets reﬂect a more conscious use of LED screen area to reduce energy 
consumption. 
Based on the presented guideline recommendations, the changes posed to the production activ-

5.4.1 Choice of variant 
Keeping in mind the goal of the case study, which is to utilize a comprehensive set of guidelines to 
facilitate the integration of sustainability practices in ﬁlm production and help close an existing 
knowledge gap in the industry. The SDG impact assessment methodology provides a framework (Lau-
rent et al., 2023), which was presented in Figure 8 and Figure 9 in section 3.4, and by following the 
method the project type shows to ﬁt in the category with outcomes as support for decision or policy-
making. This corresponds to Variant B. 
The outcome of the case study will reveal the major categories and relevant areas to focus on to 
reduce the climate impact from ﬁlm production. This should serve as support for future decision mak-
ing when implementing initiatives on sustainability as well as further development in other areas such 
as for social impacts. 
5.4.2 Phase 1 
In Phase 1, the necessary scoping is deﬁned for the assessment. The case study guidelines cover 
recommendations for all departments on a ﬁlm production; however, due to the complexity and scale, 
the SDG impact assessment is scoped to focus on a limited aspect of the guidelines. Previously pre-
sented data reporting by SPA (Sustainable Production Alliance, 2021) indicates that the major carbon 
emission contribution comes from transportation-related activities in the ﬁlm industry. This serves as 
an argument for focusing on transportation activities related to the three different production scenarios 
in the assessment. 
Another important point stated in Phase 1 is that the guidelines are based on current existing guide-
lines and knowledge and provide recommendations in a speciﬁc setup for each production department. 
The setup offers a prioritized list of options, with Option 1 representing the most environmentally 
sustainable approach, followed by Option 2 and subsequent choices with decreasing levels of sustain-
ability. The ideal case would be to completely follow Option 1, being the most sustainable practice 
with the least climate impact. In reality, that might not be feasible due to restrictions in terms of pro-
duction budget, capacity, location etc. However, it is decided to base the assessment on the recommen-
dations presented in Option 1 for transportation activities.

systems can be found in appendix 10.2.

- 
Prioritizing electric vehicles.

Planning travel to minimize trips and milage. 
- 
Using public transportation, bikes, or ridesharing for location scouting and as means of trans-
portation to set. 
- 
On set, using bikes or electric or solar powered means of transportation and for commuting.

5.4.5 Phase 4 
The aim of Phase 4 is to semi-quantitively assess how the effects impact the SDGs. For each effect, 
all potential SDGs are identiﬁed, and an evaluation is performed based on three criteria. 
1. Direction of the SDG impact: positive or negative. 
2. Likelihood of the SDG impact. 
3. Magnitude of the SDG impact. 
The SDG impact assessment framework (Laurent et al., 2023) offers guidelines for characterizing 
the likelihood and magnitude of the SDG impact. Likelihood is determined in the sense of “unlikely, 
possible, or likely” whereas the magnitude is based on a threshold estimation ranging from negligible 
(0-5%) to small (5-20%) to moderate (20-50%) and to large (50-100%). For further details, refer to the 
tables included in appendix 10.3 and 10.4. The likelihood and magnitude are combined in a scoring 
system used to assess the impacts.

SDG 1 (no poverty): the goal has a minor positive impact in the “Use”-stage based on the imple-
mentation of utilizing bikes as means of transportation to and on set (E8). The impact is linked to target 
1.4, which aims to ensure that all have equal rights to economic resources as well as access to basic 
services and resources and technology. The impact is justiﬁed by cycling being an affordable and simple 
mode of transport. 
SDG 3 (good health and well-being): the goal is impacted broadly across the life cycle stages 
through several effects. Mostly observed with a major positive impact from effects in both the “Pro-
duction”- and “Use”-stage. The impacts are linked to target 3.9, which aims to reduce the number of 
illnesses and deaths caused by pollution. A central outcome of integrating more sustainable transpor-
tation practices is a noteworthy decrease in pollution, stemming from both the decreased use of fossil-
fueled vehicles and the pollution linked to fossil resource extraction and fuel production. SDG 3 is 
substantially impacted and indicates a very positive inﬂuence on human health. The scenario with 
electricity production from fossil resources (E4) is evaluated as a minor negative impact on the goal, 
justiﬁed by ongoing pollution associated with fossil resource extraction. 
SDG 6 (clean water and sanitation): the goal is impacted positively by effects in the “Raw Materi-
als”- and “Production”-stage. The impacts are linked to target 6.3, which aims to improve water quality 
by reducing pollution. Reduced extraction of fossil fuels (E2) leads to improved water quality due to 
decreased oil mining activities and spills. Additionally, the impacts are linked to target 6.4 which aims 
to increase efﬁcient water use, which is justiﬁed by the reduced water consumption of renewable energy 
systems compared to fossil fuel technologies. A negative impact is documented on the direct contrast 
scenario of electricity production from fossil fuels (E4), resulting in heightened water consumption re-
quirements. The magnitude of this impact evaluation mainly focuses on transportation activities within 
the ﬁlm industry, while other industries continue to conduct their own operations and thereby also 
have an impact on the SDG. 
SDG 7 (affordable and clean energy): the goal is impacted widely across the life cycle stages 
through several effects. The major positive impacts are linked to target 7.1, which aims to increase 
access to modern and affordable energy, justiﬁed by a clear positive correlation with the increased 
utilization of EVs and bicycles, among other transportation modes. Additionally, in the “Production”-
stage the positive impact is linked to target 7.2, which aims to increase the share of renewable energy 
in the global matrix. The case study has a remarkable positive impact on SDG 7 and indicates a good 
alignment with the goal objectives. 
SDG 8 (decent work and economic growth): the goal is mostly associated with a minor negative 
impact and linked to target 8.5, which aims to achieve full and productive employment and decent 
work for all. The outcomes of the case study, including the elimination of fossil-fueled vehicle usage 
and decreased air travel, would likely have economic ramiﬁcations for sectors and businesses reliant 
on fossil fuels, potentially reducing employment demand. However, this impact is deemed minor as 
the suggested sustainable alternatives offer job opportunities that are also safer within the renewable

energy sector. Furthermore, given that this primarily affects jobs and businesses associated with trans-
portation in the ﬁlm industry, the overall impact magnitude is small, considering there are other sectors

SDG 10 (reduced inequalities): the goal is taken into account but with a small and almost negligible

SDG 11 (sustainable cities and communities): the goal is impacted positively in the “Use”-stage

to air quality. The assessment stems from the increased adoption of EVs and renewable energy as a 
result of implementing the case study guidelines, contributing to the reduction of air pollution. How-
ever, the trade-off exists with fossil-based electricity production, which still carries associated pollution. 
SDG 12 (responsible consumption and production): the goal is positively impacted across the life 
cycle stages through several effects. The impacts are linked to target 12.2, which aims for sustainable 
management and efﬁcient use of natural resources. The increased production of renewable energy 
generally supports the reduction of natural resource depletion as it decreases direct use. However, it is 
worth keeping in mind that resources are still needed to produce windmills, solar panels, and such that 
are used to generate renewable energy as well as the resources needed to produce the batteries for EVs. 
The recommended sustainability practice for transportation activities impacts SDG 12 and indicates 
an overall positive inﬂuence on the goal objectives. 
SDG 13 (climate action): the goal is impacted positively in the “Use”-stage through several effects. 
The impacts are linked to target 13.2, which aims to integrate climate change measures, justiﬁed by 
the decrease in GHG emissions resulting from the removal of fossil-fueled transportation means in the 
ﬁlm industry. Just as the impacts on SDG 12 illustrate, they also signify a positive effect on climate 
action under SDG 13. 
SDG 14 (life below water): the goal is impacted positively in the “Production”-stage. The impacts 
are linked to target 14.1 which aims to prevent and reduce marine pollution, in particular from land-
based activities. The reduction in oil spills resulting from lessened resource mining activities and dis-

6.1.1 Externalities and case study limitations and uncertainties 
When determining which activities and aspects to include in the case study and hypothetical ﬁlm 
production model, certain components or factors are intentionally excluded. These exclusions are ex-
ternalities. Some of the omitted elements, which could potentially inﬂuence the results, are presented 
and discussed in the following points. 
Model complexity 
The case study works with a limited hypothetical ﬁlm production model, which does not contain 
a sufﬁcient level of detail to reﬂect an actual ﬁlm production. The included activities, displayed in 
Figure 7, do not encompass the range of actual activities involved. For instance, preparatory activities 
like script writing, hiring of production team, and storyboarding are excluded. Figure 2 illustrates that 
the development stage alone can last several months and up to a year. The model only accounts for 13 
weeks for the development stage activities. Additionally, the model estimates an average on total emis-
sion of around 20 tons CO2-eq in the initial budgets. This number appears relatively low compared to 
the ﬁgures presented in Figure 5, which indicate total emissions of 391 tons CO2-eq for even just a 
small-scale feature ﬁlm production. This discrepancy highlights the simpliﬁed nature of the hypothet-
ical model. It is also evident from the several production departments outlined in the guidelines, such 
as Animation, Costume, Hair & Makeup, Lighting, Props, Sound, and Special effects, that were intentionally left 
out of the model and sustainability assessment. Furthermore, the model only includes necessary crew 
members for the sake of simplicity. Including tasks related to the omitted departments would require 
additional crew members like hair- and makeup artists, runners, and assistants in multiple departments, 
affecting transportation-related activities with increased commuting, the extent of catering with more 
people to feed, as well as generate more waste and energy consumption. Expanding the model to in-
clude these aspects and creating a more realistic scenario could potentially alter the overall emissions 
distribution. The lack of complexity introduces somewhat of an uncertainty into the results. 
Realistic considerations and limitations in sustainable ﬁlm production 
When considering a more realistic scenario, several points are up for discussion. The model and 
its parameters reﬂect an early stage of ﬁlm production, where most planning and budgeting are based 
on preliminary ideas and assumptions. Initial CO2-budgets are created from this rough version, and 
the sustainability guidelines, which provide a comprehensive set of recommendations, are then applied 
to generate revised CO2-budgets. While the case study approach offers a snapshot of guideline imple-
mentation, the actual process is iterative and dynamic, requiring extensive communication and collab-
oration across departments. During the revision of the CO2-budgets, challenges and uncertainties were 
uncovered due to limitations of the carbon calculator tool, which does not support the level of detail

unknown, meaning some sustainability practices may not be fully represented in the ﬁndings. 
6.1.2 The green future in ﬁlm production according to the carbon budgets 
The purpose of the case study and the model is to identify trends in emission contributors and 
determine where to focus efforts to achieve the greatest reduction in carbon footprint. Transportation-

The RP scenario was identiﬁed as the preferred option for future sustainable ﬁlm production based on 
the overall lowest carbon footprint shown by the ﬁndings in Chapter 5. This contradicts the prevailing

6.2.1 SDG impact trade-offs and hotspots 
As mentioned in section 5.4.5, the assessment identiﬁes trade-offs on SDGs 3, 6, 8, 11, and 12 
all within the same effect (E4), which deals with electricity production either from renewables or fossil-

their production and operation. Likewise, is the case for EVs, which are the green alternative to con-

traction and processing can have signiﬁcant negative climate impacts. It is important to consider the 
extraction and mining of raw materials, including different metals and silicon for instance, needed to 
manufacture clean energy technologies all of which have their own environmental impacts associated 
with their extraction and processing.

6.2.2 Scoping limitations, uncertainties and recommendations 
The SDG impact assessment primarily relies on subjective evaluation and comes with uncertain-
ties. All effects identiﬁed in the assessment are objectively screened for possible impacts on every SDG. 
However, the impact likeliness and magnitude in the semi-quantitative analysis undertaken in Phase 4 
is based on a somewhat subjective evaluation. The assessment portrays the outcome from the optimal 
sustainability scenario and is thereby to some degree speculative. However, it does provide a useful 
overview of the affected areas and subjects for sustainable development. 
An important aspect of the SDG impact assessment is the limited focus on transportation-related 
activities that are associated with just one ﬁlm production case. The scope causes uncertainty on the 
impacts from other aspects of ﬁlm production and, furthermore, implementing changes in a single 
production has a limited impact on sustainability when viewed on a global scale. The impact from 
other ﬁlm productions, along with various industries, that continue to rely on fossil fuels for transpor-
tation purposes is neglected in the assessment. The scoring system utilized in the assessment solely 
focuses on the extent of the direct impact the identiﬁed effects have on the individual SDGs without 
considering a broader perspective. Additionally, the assessment does not specify or consider separate 
geographical regions in the scoping and, therefore, does not differentiate between varying conditions. 
For example, the technological advancements in electricity production and infrastructure may differ 
between Europe and South America. Filming in these regions could yield vastly different outcomes in 
terms of the semi-quantitative SDG impact assessment. This might lead to skewed and less detailed 
results, introducing uncertainty into the assessment. 
The SDG impact assessment brieﬂy considers separate production scenarios during Phase 2 
while establishing the baseline- and new systems. However, for simplicity, Phase 3 and Phase 4 aggre-
gate the identiﬁed effects for an overall assessment without delineating the production scenarios’ indi-
vidual inﬂuence. Filming on location necessitates greater use of transportation activities compared to 
the VP scenario. Expanding the assessment to reﬂect differentiation on the production scenarios as 
well as a broader range of aspects, could potentially reveal some hidden negative impacts associated 
with VP. The assessment simpliﬁcation disguises the divergence between the production scenarios, but 
ultimately, the overall effects are consistent across the production scenarios and the purpose of the 
assessment is not to consider the quantity of transportation activities. 
Based on the SDG impact assessment, a few recommendations are proposed. The overall assess-
ment highlights positive outcomes in several sustainable aspects, such as planning travel and

6.3 Addressing further challenges on sustainability in ﬁlm production 
This thesis comprises three key components: a review on current sustainability practices in the 
ﬁlm industry, a case study, and an SDG impact assessment, each offering unique insights to achieve 
the overarching explorative goal. Together, these elements contribute to data driven decision-making 
regarding sustainable ﬁlm production, addressing existing knowledge gaps, and paving the way for a 
greener future in the industry. 
The combination of the ﬁndings from the case study and the SDG impact assessment underlines 
how practicing sustainability encompasses much more than climate change. Each of the elements has 
its set of limitations. The case study primarily focuses on environmental sustainability, overlooking both 
the ﬁnancial aspects as well as social sustainability aspects. The SDG impact assessment has an exclu-
sive focus on transportation-related activities, but offers insights into sustainability impacts in the 
==PAGE:65==

environmental, social, and economic dimensions. Social sustainability involves ensuring fair labor prac-
tices, supporting local communities, and promoting diversity and inclusion. While the revised CO2-
budgets in the case study include the hiring of local extras to reduce mileage and transportation emis-
sions, the broader social impacts are not fully considered. Reducing carbon emissions from production 
is crucial, but the ﬁlm industry must also evaluate how these initiatives affect other sustainability as-
pects. The CO2-budgets from the case study investigate the effects beyond transportation by including 
various categories, such as Accommodation, Art Department, Energy, Equipment, and Food & Beverage and the 
SDG impact assessment attempts to cover a wider range of sustainability considerations in its limited 
scope. 
The implementation of sustainability practices in the ﬁlm industry, that is the focus of this thesis, 
and its included elements, entail certain challenges. While the beneﬁts have been highlighted from the 
ﬁnding’s discussion above, a discussion of the challenges provide answers related to research question 
3: 
- 
What are the major beneﬁts and likewise challenges from the sustainable implementations? 
Based on a comparison of the initial and revised CO2-budgets generated in the case study, sus-
tainability implementations show an average 68% reduction in emissions from ﬁlm production activi-
ties. Although the model used in the case study involves certain limitations, which have already been 
discussed, and the numbers generally are lower than those indicated in real-world literature, the ﬁnd-
ings provide a valuable indication of the potential for reducing anthropogenic emissions in the ﬁlm 
industry. Yet signiﬁcant challenges remain when it comes to implementing sustainability practices on 
ﬁlm production. 
The ﬁrst challenge identiﬁed, and worth talking about is the ﬁnancial budget. The case study 
does not account for the ﬁnancial budget limitations typical of actual ﬁlm productions in the sustaina-
bility assessment. The most sustainable options might incur increased expenses and logistical concerns. 
For instance, commuting by public transport might not always be feasible or efﬁcient for certain ﬁlming 
locations. Catering with organic, seasonal, fair-trade, free-range, local, plant-based meals might be 
limited in availability and more expensive than other options. Additionally, the extra time needed to 
research and secure options like studios with renewable mains electricity, green-certiﬁed hotels for ac-
commodation, and socially and eco-conscious material suppliers could extend production duration and 
add to emissions. Depending on the production size and available budget, some recommended sus-
tainability practices might not be feasible. 
Budget constraints, as mentioned in section 2.1, and varying capabilities among ﬁlm productions 
can hinder the level of sustainable practices that can be implemented. The ﬁlm production budget is a 
double-edged sword for sustainable ﬁlm production. On one hand, a larger budget offers more ﬂexi-
bility to invest in sustainable solutions, such as renewable energy sources, eco-friendly materials, and 
green-certiﬁed accommodations. On the other hand, a larger budget often leads to a bigger production 
scale, involving more cast and crew, which increases the need for consumables, transportation, cater-
ing, materials, and equipment. This creates a paradox where ﬁnancial ﬂexibility allows for sustainabil-
ity investments but also drives up overall resource consumption and emissions. Prioritizing the budget 
and ﬁnancial resources for green practices is a crucial challenge, as productions must balance the cost 
of sustainability alternatives with other budgetary demands, ensuring that investments in sustainability 
do not compromise the overall quality and feasibility of the production. 
==PAGE:66==
 
 
57 
Financial constraints can limit the extent to which sustainability practices are adopted. Generally, 
a larger budget is linked to a more extensive production plan, involving more activities and thus a 
larger carbon footprint. Even with numerous sustainability plans and green practices in place, limiting 
the scope of production activities remains a challenge. Efﬁciency can signiﬁcantly reduce emissions, for 
instance, extended editing periods in post-production increase energy consumption and carbon emis-
sions. Therefore, time planning and budget considerations are essential for achieving sustainability in 
ﬁlm production. 
Another signiﬁcant challenge is the availability of green options. Whether it is EVs and charging 
stations, sustainable caterers, or eco-friendly materials, the supply of these resources can be limited, 
especially in certain locations. The increased demand for clean energy and green technologies is driving 
industry advancement, but this progress is uneven and often concentrated in more developed areas. 
Productions in less developed regions may struggle to ﬁnd and afford these sustainability options. 
A third major challenge is the spread of awareness and knowledge about sustainability practices. 
While there is growing recognition of the importance of sustainability in the ﬁlm industry, there re-
mains a gap in understanding how to effectively implement green practices. Effective implementation 
requires training, access to reliable information, and a cultural shift within the industry towards valuing 
and prioritizing sustainability. 
Another aspect of challenges addressed by this thesis is identiﬁed in the review, speciﬁcally in 
section 2.4, as greenwashing. This issue is crucial as it can undermine genuine efforts to achieve sus-
tainability. The overall ﬁndings from this thesis indicate that superﬁcial measures, such as merely re-
placing disposable plastic water bottles with reusable cups or other similar minor changes, are insufﬁ-
cient for meaningful impact. To effectively reduce the carbon footprint, efforts must focus on areas 
where the greatest impact can be achieved. It is suggested that focusing on these areas will help avoid 
greenwashing and ensure sustainability efforts are meaningful and impactful. The habitual practices in 
the ﬁlm industry must change to decrease resource and material consumption and reduce waste gen-
eration. One potential solution is the implementation of climate reporting legislation. However, a ques-
tion can be raised on whether such enforcement will genuinely motivate change or merely increase 
greenwashing. Key considerations include the scope of reporting requirements, the level of detail 
needed, and the entities to which it would apply. Any legislation must be clear and speciﬁc. In early 
March 2024, the US approved a nationwide climate disclosure rule requiring large, publicly traded 
companies to disclose climate change-related information to their investors (Noor, 2024). In the EU 
such legislation is already instated, as mentioned in the introduction of this thesis (Chapter 1), in the 
form of the CSRD that obligates corporate sustainability reporting on activities that impact the envi-
ronment (European Commission, 2024b). While experts believe this will enhance transparency about 
corporate climate risks, critics argue that it promotes greenwashing and is too weak (Noor, 2024). One 
could be concerned that similar uncertainties could arise if such regulations are applied to the ﬁlm 
industry. 
This completes the discussion and evaluation of the major beneﬁts, challenges, and uncertainties 
of the ﬁndings from the case study and the SDG impact assessment worked in this thesis.

6.4 Environmental health in the ﬁlm industry 
Film production activities have an undeniable link to environmental health. This section offers a 
few speciﬁc examples on the impacts sustainability practices in ﬁlm production have on environmental 
health, which addresses research question 3.1: 
- 
How does it relate to environmental health? 
The connection between human health and climate change is evident based on the introduction 
in Chapter 1. Implementing sustainable practices in ﬁlm production can signiﬁcantly contribute to 
improving environmental health and human well-being. Some of the sustainability guideline aspects 
explored in the case study included:   
Reusing and donating materials for building sets, which diverts waste from landﬁlls and thereby 
reduces associated emissions of harmful gasses, such as CO2 and volatile organic compounds (VOC). 
This practice has a direct positive impact on both the environment and human health by reducing air 
pollution, which can be the cause of respiratory diseases. 
Reducing trips and milage and using renewable energy-based EVs instead of conventional fossil-
powered vehicles, which leads to a decrease in air pollution. This practice has a direct positive impact 
on human health and is aligned with SDG 3 (good health and well-being) as an example. 
Replacing plastic water bottles with tap water in reusable cups or bottles, which helps reduce 
amount of waste and the emission of microplastics into the environment. The positive environmental 
impact form this practice is aligned with SDG 14 (life below water) as it decreases pollution of marine 
and freshwater ecosystems. This practice also decreases human exposure to microplastics through 
drinking water and food and is aligned with the objectives of SDG 3 (good health and well-being). 
Using power and electricity from green energy sources, which reduces pollution associated with 
extraction and use of fossil fuels. This practice has a positive impact on the environment beneﬁtting 
both water and soil quality, which supports the objectives of SDG 14 (life below water) and SDG 15 
(life on land). 
Additionally, the guidelines emphasize planet-friendly eating and minimizing resource consump-
tion and waste. These practices not only positively impact the environment by reducing the carbon 
footprint associated with food production and waste management but also promote human health 
through better nutrition, aligning with SDG 3 (good health and well-being). Furthermore, reducing 
food waste supports SDG 2 (zero hunger). 
Implementing sustainability practices thus results in reduced emissions, which positively affect 
both environmental and human health. This holistic view emphasizes the interconnectedness of cli-
mate action, environmental sustainability, and public health. By focusing on reducing emissions, we 
not only combat climate change but also create healthier environments for current and future genera-
tions.

7. Conclusion 
This thesis explores impacts of implementing sustainability practices in the ﬁlm industry. Re-
search questions are posed to support the investigation with three main elements: a review on current 
sustainability practices and the associated challenges in the ﬁlm industry, a case study assessing the 
carbon footprint of different production scenarios (virtual production, ﬁlming on location/in studio, 
and remote production), and ﬁnally a Sustainable Development Goal impact assessment. It is con-
cluded that ﬁlm production activities contribute signiﬁcantly to climate change and environmental 
health. The case study demonstrates that transportation-related activities are the major contributor to 
production emissions and implementing sustainability practices can substantially reduce emissions with 
an average reduction of 68%. The case study furthermore indicates the remote production scenario to 
have the lowest level of emissions amongst the three scenarios. The importance of focusing efforts on 
production areas where they can make the greatest impact is also highlighted. Although the case study 
is based on a hypothetical model with a set of limitations, the ﬁndings demonstrate the potential for 
the ﬁlm industry to reduce its environmental impact. The case study uses a carbon calculator tool to 
generate carbon budgets and conduct a sustainability assessment. Globally, there are numerous tools 
available, each with varying functions and levels of detail. The use of these tools in the ﬁlm industry is 
emerging, with many green initiatives already gaining momentum. It is furthermore concluded that 
reduced emissions not only beneﬁt the climate but also improve air, water, and soil quality, enhancing 
human health. However, challenges like budget constraints, limited availability of green options, and 
the need for greater awareness of sustainability practices must be addressed. Prioritizing sustainability 
can lead to a greener, healthier future for the ﬁlm industry. The Sustainable Development Goal impact 
assessment demonstrates that practicing sustainability entails much more than just climate by branch-
ing on to social and economic aspects. The overall ﬁndings from the work in this thesis provide empir-
ical argumentation to help close the existing knowledge gap and support for future decision-making in 
sustainable ﬁlm production.

8. Recommendations for future work 
The case study in this thesis is based on a hypothetical ﬁlm production model and a set of 
assumptions. To enhance its accuracy, the study should include a broader range of ﬁlm production 
activities to better represent the various stages of production. This would help create a more realistic 
model. Additionally, expanding the focus beyond Denmark to include a wider range of regions would 
provide a global perspective on the environmental impacts of ﬁlm production. The implemented 
recommendations from the sustainability guidelines currently consider only the most sustainable 
option. However, incorporating a tiered approach with options 2 and 3 would accommodate potential 
budget constraints and offer more ﬂexibility. Furthermore, the study could be modiﬁed to explore 
different production sizes and with the intention of examining how ﬁnancial aspects impact the 
feasibility of implementing green practices. 
Conducting a cross-sectional opinion study would assess the industry's willingness to embrace 
green initiatives, addressing concerns regarding greenwashing. While the ﬁlm industry's current shift 
towards sustainability originates from within, factors such as data availability, required training, and 
preparedness play crucial roles in advancing the green movement. Such factors might need to be 
sourced externally, requiring collaborations with environmental organizations, sustainability experts, 
and educational institutions for instance. 
Exploring alternative tools apart from GPT, which was selected for this study, may uncover dif-
ferent results with varying levels of detail. While the core functions of existing tools are similar, they 
differ in complexity, with some offering features like certiﬁed materials and eco-friendly accommoda-
tion options. Efforts are currently ongoing in the EU to synchronize functions of existing tools. How-
ever, a potential consideration is to develop a standardized tool that would enable direct comparison 
of data across the ﬁlm industry. Furthermore, it is suggested to further research the environmental 
impact of speciﬁc options, such as comparing emissions between certiﬁed and non-certiﬁed materials, 
evaluating the climate impact of different recording resolutions, and assessing the differences between 
organic and conventional catering, among others. Such research would help identify which practices 
have the most signiﬁcant environmental impact, allowing efforts to be directed more effectively. 
Social sustainability, brieﬂy mentioned in the discussion in section 6.3, warrants deeper explora-
tion with limited available data and understanding of its impacts in the ﬁlm industry. The SDG impact 
assessment, which branches to the social aspects, could also beneﬁt from broader expansion to encom-
pass more dimensions beyond transportation. Along with the case study, the SDG impact assessment 
could be enhanced by adopting a more realistic ﬁlm production model and by extending the scope to 
include regions beyond Denmark. This broader approach would provide a comprehensive understand-
ing of global-level effects. 
By addressing the externalities, uncertainties, and limitations of the elements of this thesis, future 
studies can provide a more comprehensive understanding of the emissions proﬁle of ﬁlm production 
and identify more effective sustainability strategies.
