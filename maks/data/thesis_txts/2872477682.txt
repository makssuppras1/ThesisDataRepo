Abstract
This thesis investigates the cost and schedule performance of major public construction
projects in Denmark, with a comparative lens on Norway.
Utilizing a comprehensive
dataset derived from 31 Danish Anlægsstatus (Construction Status) reports covering the
years 2007–2024, the study evaluates the deviation of 114 state-funded road and rail
projects from their originally allocated budgets and timelines. The methodology applies
indexed cost projections and filters out incomplete or inconsistently documented projects
to enhance data validity. Findings reveal that while Danish projects frequently demon-
strate cost underruns, raising questions about systematic overestimation, they also suffer
from substantial time overruns, particularly in the rail sector. Only about a quarter of the
projects were delivered on time. A comparative analysis with Norwegian projects indicates
that although Denmark tends to perform better in terms of cost accuracy, it lags behind
Norway in schedule adherence. Norway’s success is largely attributed to a governance
model integrating stochastic cost estimation and external quality assurance.
This study concludes that Denmark’s deterministic budgeting methods and inconsistent
time management practices limit effective project delivery and recommends a return to
probabilistic cost estimation and greater focus on time governance in early project phases.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
The motivation behind this thesis project was sparked by peculiarly interesting findings by
Jan Pries-Heje on comparing the Danish to Norwegian cost performance in major public
IT projects as part of the research network PROGRAM DENMARK (LinkedIn, 2025). Due
to the similarities in sociopolitical and socioeconomic conditions between the two Scandi-
navian states, project success is expected to be relatively identical. However, the study
presents a significant difference in cost overrun, where Norway experiences an average
of 8% while Denmark has 108%. Additionally, Norwegian projects exhibit a maximum
cost overrun of 84%, while Danish projects reach up to 414% (Pries-Heje et al., 2024).
These insights are particularly pertinent to policymakers, practitioners, and researchers,
offering valuable lessons for cross-country learning and improvement. Such findings also
provoke curiosity about other sectors like road and rail construction activities. Hence, this
knowledge gap would be explored and consequently compared to two other backbone
analyses from Norway on how to avoid cost and time overruns.
1.2
Problem Exploration and Statement
In 2021, the Danish government unveiled an ambitious infrastructure strategy titled Den-
mark Forward, allocating 160 billion Danish kroner (approximately €21.5 billion) for infras-
tructure projects extending to 2035 (Ministry of Foreign Affairs of Denmark, n.d.). Specif-
ically, over 86 billion Danish kroner are earmarked for public transport projects to ensure
faster travel and improved rail networks, while 64 billion Danish kroner are designated for
the construction of future roads (TheMayor.EU, n.d.).
Most recent data shows that in 2022, Denmark invested approximately 8.1 billion Danish
kroner in new road construction and around 8.9 billion Danish kroner in road maintenance,
highlighting the country’s commitment to enhancing its road infrastructure (Figure 1.1)
(Statista, n.d.).

Such major government investment projects undergo rigorous planning and appraisal pro-
cesses designed to justify their execution based on estimated costs, projected benefits,
and anticipated impacts. Effective cost performance, i.e., completing a project within the
allocated budget, is crucial for business success and the state’s image in this case. If ac-
tual costs exceed government budgets’ projections, it can undermine profitability, disrupt
project timelines, or limit the feasibility of other planned projects. Significant cost overruns
may also reduce society’s capacity to sustain and maintain infrastructure in the long term
(Welde and Klakegg, 2022).
These project success challenges are not new but rather trends over time and across the
globe. For instance, initial studies on public sector projects in India suggested that the is-
sues of cost overruns and delays are common in nature rather than isolated in occurrence.
In that research from Morris, 1990, it is indicated that underestimation of costs, inflationary
pressures, and ineffective project management are the main causes of increased costs
and extended deadlines. The study showed that public sector projects are often charac-
terized by a lack of adequate initial planning, unrealistic budgeting, and the absence of
accountability in cost estimation practices.
Recent works recognize that there is still a problem, albeit current advancements in tech-
nology and innovations in project management have significantly reduced the risks. Berg
et al., 2025, highlight several contemporary factors contributing to cost overruns in major
public projects, particularly during the front-end phase. A persistent issue is the tendency
among cost engineers to produce overly detailed estimates too early in the process, driven
by institutional expectations for precision. This premature detail can mask underlying un-
certainty and provide a false sense of accuracy. Additionally, estimates are often miscon-
strued as fixed budgets by decision-makers, reflecting a widespread misunderstanding
of the distinction between estimates and budgets. Also, political pressures influence the
estimation process, with cost engineers often encouraged to present optimistic figures to
secure project approval.
Based on findings from these papers, with a gap of about 35 years, several challenges
seem to be a persistent tendency over time:
• Inaccurate Cost Estimation - projects continue to suffer from optimistic budgeting
and forecasting biases,
• Project Delays – frequent postponements due to inadequate planning, or unfore-
seen conditions disrupt schedules, and
• Project Scope Changes - uncontrolled modifications during execution lead to an
increase in costs and timelines.

1.4
Scope and Delimitations
This study focuses on evaluating cost and schedule performance in major public con-
struction projects, with a specific emphasis on initiatives in Denmark, and a comparative
dimension involving Norwegian counterparts. It looks at all Danish governmental construc-
tion projects, initiated or completed after the year 2000, but recorded between 2007 and
2024 in 31 semi-annual grant follow-ups, publicly shared by the Ministry of Transport. The
scope is limited to data from these Anlægsstatus reports on size, finances, and purpose
of projects that are no longer included in the Financial Act, meaning they are completed
and implemented. The study does not explore their preliminary budgets or further life
after being put into operation, but solely the budget of the final investment decision (FID)
and the original opening year. No further documentation on individual developments is
reviewed.
The study will perform a comparative analysis to assess differences and similarities in
project outcomes between Denmark and Norway. The research will encompass quanti-
tative analysis of historical project data, based on the rearranged datasets with specific
cases filtered out. The results are constructively criticized to assess study shortcomings.
The reasons for overspending or underspending are not prioritized.

Literature Review
The following section provides an overview of relevant academic publications, useful grey
literature by industry consultants and governmental audit reports, ultimately leading to
improved knowledge on project performance.
2.1
Project Performance: Cost and Time Overruns
While project success remains an evolving field of study, its focus has gradually shifted
beyond foundational frameworks like Pinto and Slevin’s work and the traditional Iron Trian-
gle of time, cost, and scope, embracing broader perspectives that account for long-term
objectives (Müller and Jugdev, 2012).
Lichtenberg, 2016, critically assesses the traditional project management practices for
achieving project success, especially in the area of budget and schedule performance on
large projects. He points out that even though advancements in cost engineering and
project management have occurred, many projects still experience significant delays and
budget overruns in both the private and public sectors. The response to this problem
should blend statistical theory, psychology, and project economics to improve forecast-
ing and identify key sources of uncertainties during the front-end planning stage. Using
dynamic estimation processes shifts project teams’ focus from certainties to critical un-
knowns. Such methods, like the Successive Principle, could potentially also help tackle
the core challenge of the complexity of construction scheduling. However, for the past 20
years, it has received a considerable amount of attention, and still, many projects experi-
ence scheduling inefficiencies (Zhou et al., 2013).
The report Reshaping British Infrastructure: Global Lessons to Improve Project Delivery,
published in 2018 by The Boston Consulting Group, presents the industry angle on an-
alyzing a wide range of countries and the increasing sophistication of the construction
projects. The research focused on the challenges faced during the construction of major
infrastructure projects in the UK, such as cost and time overruns. According to statis-
tics, their construction projects typically exceed their original budgets by 57% due to the
poor management of the process from the initial planning stages to the completion of the
project. Additionally, delivering projects with innovative technologies and sophisticated
regulations has contributed to the growing costs, mainly because the contractors must
familiarize themselves with the new technologies and regulations, which increases the
possibility of errors (Ruparel et al., 2024).
Moreover, the perspective of any delay in project implementation inherently linked to cost
overruns is presented in the study by Singh, 2010, on 894 projects from 17 infrastructure
sectors, examining how common and impactful the overruns are in publicly funded infras-
tructure projects. Up to then, it was considered the largest dataset on completed projects
in the period from April 1992 to March 2009. It sheds light on the inflation, as initial cost
estimates are typically based on current input prices. When delays occur, the cost of
materials and labor tends to rise, leading to an overall increase in project expenses. Addi-
tionally, ongoing overhead costs persist as long as the project remains incomplete, further
contributing to budget overruns. Prolonged delays may also lead to the depreciation of
project assets, necessitating repairs or replacements. Therefore, aside from these direct
financial impacts, any other factor causing time overruns will also inherently contribute to
cost overruns, proving a simultaneous relationship.

2.2
Backbone Literature
The Good Example of Norway
Bringing the focus from world trends to Scandinavia, two analyses by Welde and Klakegg,
2022 and Welde and Bukkestein, 2022 on cost and time overruns are considered pivotal
literature for this study. That is due to the Norwegian experience, though initiatives like
the NTNU Concept Research Program, 2025, seen as a valuable source of inspiration for
improving project delivery in Denmark. Their evidence demonstrates that a governance
model, anchored in probability-based cost estimation and institutional integrity, has signif-
icantly improved their cost performance.
To tackle the cost overruns challenge, two primary categories of causes are defined: be-
havioral and political explanations, where conscious or unconscious biases lead planners
to underestimate costs to gain project approval, and traditional project uncertainties, in-
cluding scope changes, contractual disputes, and unforeseen ground conditions. While
cost overruns are often the symptom, the root problem frequently lies in cost underesti-
mation. The paper emphasizes the need for a shift from focusing solely on predicting and
controlling outcomes to preparing for uncertainty. The critical methodology of stochas-
tic cost estimation is adapted to produce cost estimates that reflect a range of poten-
tial outcomes. This technique’s implementation in government projects has been limited,
although, for instance, Australia has begun issuing formal guidance on its application
(Welde and Klakegg, 2022).
On the other side of the performance spectrum is scheduling management. And in Con-
cept Report No. 69 by Welde and Bukkestein, 2022, one of the first systematic studies
in Norway is presented to examine delays across a broad selection of state-funded in-
frastructure and defense projects. The report reveals that while over half of the analyzed
projects experienced some form of delay, a notable portion, particularly in the civil sector,
such as roads, railways, and buildings, were completed according to their planned sched-
ules. A key finding is that delays are most often rooted in early-phase missteps, including
the selection of unsuitable suppliers, vague time targets, and insufficient attention to time
estimation techniques. Interviews conducted as part of the study emphasized that while
suppliers are often cited as the primary reason for project delays, success in timely de-
livery is typically credited to internal factors, particularly the competence, resources, and
capacity of the project owner’s organization. The study concludes with recommendations
to enhance time governance practices through clearer time targets, improved uncertainty
analyses, and better project documentation. These findings underscore the value of Nor-
way’s external quality assurance framework (described in the following section 2.3), which,
although primarily associated with cost control, also contributes to mitigating the risk of
schedule overruns when effectively implemented.
2.3
Project Governance Frameworks
Denmark and Norway have established particular frameworks for the management of
major projects to address the recurrence of central government project cost overruns,
delays, and low public value-for-money.

Norway was the first country to implement the State Project Model in the year 2000; a
framework directing that all central government projects that are financed above NOK
750 million shall have external quality to offer assurance of the cost effectiveness of the
project. The model is particularly concerned with the initial phase of the projects and
calls for the need to consider multiple conceptual solutions and the application of cause
analysis to define the project cost. Two main gates are included in the model, which are
QA1 /oversees alternative conceptual solution/, and QA2 /oversees project cost estima-
tion and project execution schedule/. One of the key features of the model is the call for
the existence of both target and budget cost of the project, with the target cost being at the
50 percent probability level (P50) and the budget cost at the 85 percent probability level
(P85). A contingency is planned with the budgeting of the project, which is administered
by the spending ministry. The main aim of the framework was to enhance the manage-
ment of the project and guarantee value-for-money aspects of all projects in the public
sector (Volden and Samset, 2017).
Denmark, influenced by the Norwegian system, has implemented a somewhat limited pol-
icy, concentrating primarily on the transport sector. Although not as all-encompassing, it
aims to enhance the excellence of key transport projects’ decision-making and the pre-
dictability of their costs. The financial control software was introduced in 2003 and was
extended in 2007 to cover external control of transport projects over DKK 250 million
(Volden and Samset, 2017). The Danish model consists of standard cost forecasts with
pre-set adjustments:
10% for agencies, where the three main ones are Trafikstyrelsen (Danish Civil Aviation
and Railway Authority), Banedanmark (Rail Net Denmark), and Vejdirektoratet (the Dan-

20% for ministries, where the Ministry of Transport develops and maintains the nation’s
transportation infrastructure, the Ministry of Finance prepares the national budget.

Danish Parliament) (Statsrevisorerne, 2019).
2.4
Danish Audit Office’s reports
Rigsrevisionen (The Danish National Audit Office) has conducted in-depth investigations
into the performance of public construction projects, offering valuable insights into the
effectiveness of project governance frameworks, alike the above-mentioned in Denmark.
What is particularly interesting to this study is that two major reports, from 2019 and 2025,
respectively, could trace progress in accurately estimating, controlling, and optimizing
project costs and scheduling for efficiency and long-term sustainability across various
ministries and project types.
The 2019 report focuses on the budgeting of road projects by the Danish Road Directorate
under the framework of New Construction Budgeting, which was introduced in 2007 to im-
prove cost predictability in large infrastructure investments. While the initiative has led
to significant reductions in actual expenditures, where 30 out of 32 completed projects
underspent their budgeted allocations, the report highlights that this underspending, to-
taling approximately 11,6 billion DKK, raises questions about systematic overestimation.
The audit finds that although the Road Directorate systematically collects data from past
projects, there is insufficient documentation on how this knowledge is utilized in budgeting,
and too few analyses are conducted to explain deviations. Moreover, a lack of clear crite-
ria for returning unspent funds from completed projects undermines budget transparency
and the reallocation of resources to new priorities (Statsrevisorerne, 2019).

The 2025 report broadens the scope to assess time and financial performance across 120
state construction projects implemented between 2018 and 2022 in six ministerial areas.
It reveals systemic challenges: 60% of the projects were not completed on time, with aver-
age delays of 2,5 years, and 20% of the projects experienced overspending amounting to
4,8 billion DKK. Simultaneously, 40% of the projects ended with underspending, totaling
approximately 8 billion DKK, suggesting both inefficiencies and possible budget inflation.
A notable finding is the clear correlation between time delays and cost overruns, with 17
of the 18 overspent projects also being delayed. The report emphasizes the absence of
consistent standards for defining, reporting, and evaluating delays and cost performance
(Folketinget Rigsrevisionen, 2025).
To summarize, these audits highlight that while frameworks like New Construction Bud-
geting can mitigate overruns when properly applied, a lack of operational consistency,
documentation, and performance monitoring continues to hinder Denmark’s ability to de-
liver public projects efficiently and accountably.
These papers’ numerical results will be compared to the current research findings to inform
whether using a single data source, the Anlægsstatus reports, yields a clearer answer
to why poor project performance is occurring, or if it is too limiting, presenting a rather
tangential observation.

3.1
Research Design
The purpose of this research is to provide an overview of deviations from the originally allo-
cated time and finances on major Danish construction projects. A qualitative approach is
adopted to select information-rich cases for an in-depth study. Such design follows induc-
tivity, where categories, themes, and patterns emerge from the data during the collection
phase rather than being imposed in advance (Patton, 2002).
A diverse pool of public databases, governmental reports, and academic papers has been
reviewed for the credibility of this study. But the manipulated data is entirely based on
one document source, the Anlægsstatus (Construction Status) reports, i.e., biannual
grant follow-ups. They are publicly available and were distributed by Associate Professor
Christian Thuesen for this project.
Innovatively, generative AI is attempted for usage to rearrange the manually collected
data from the first phase, occupying extensive weeks of work, into the desired database
set-up as phase two. However, both OpenAI chatbot and Julius, an AI data analyst, have
failed for this purpose thus have been rejected as a helping tool. Although the former is
ethically utilized to fasten the descriptive nature for the case scenario example in Section
4 Results.
Additionally, in that particular instance, the study employs both total appropriation /granted
budgets/ and actual expenditure /consumption/ as key methodological tools to allow for
a nuanced understanding of cost overruns in the examined public construction projects.
While total appropriation reflects the initial financial intent and policy-level commitment to
a project, actual expenditure captures the real economic impact and resource utilization
over time, resulting in a dual approach to identify systemic gaps between projected and
realized costs.

First Observations
Initial review of the available project data and status reports reveals several noteworthy
patterns and inconsistencies relevant to both data quality and categorization practices.
Several projects exhibit irregularities in cost reporting that are not attributable to known
mechanisms such as index regulation. For instance, the Bredsten-Vandel project shows a
reduction in reported consumption from 312,3 million DKK as of 31 March 2013 to 293,9
million DKK by 30 September 2013 - an unusual change that is not explained by any
documented index correction. Such discrepancies raise concerns about the consistency
of data recording across reporting periods.
A structural evolution in the reporting system is also evident over time. Status report
2-2015 marks the first appearance of buildings as a distinct project category. Similarly,
report 1-2011 introduces the Femern Bælt project into the monitoring framework for the
first time, while report 2-2022 includes Danish State Railways (DSB) projects for the first
time in a dedicated section. These developments highlight the incremental expansion and
refinement of the reporting system, as well as the inclusion of increasingly diverse project
types.
Another dimension of variation is the involvement of public-private partnerships (PPPs).
In several cases, projects are recorded where private entities are responsible for construc-
tion, and the government subsequently acts as a long-term tenant. This model affects the
way costs are reflected in public accounts and may complicate comparisons with tradition-
ally procured projects.
Moreover, a pattern emerges where clusters of projects exhibit simultaneous reductions
in budget values without accompanying explanation. These reductions do not align with
index regulation adjustments, suggesting the presence of general budgetary cuts or re-
allocations. Examples of this phenomenon can be found in report 2-2008, Table 8 on p.
41), particularly in the data field 2) Indeks regulering til og med FL08, as well as in report
1-2017, Table 5.4 on p. 62), where reductions are observed in total budget entries. Con-
versely, unexplained increases are also recorded, such as those in report 1-2023, Table
3.1, further emphasizing the need for transparency and standardized reporting mecha-
nisms.
In terms of methodological consistency, this study deliberately excludes tables reporting
budgets from preliminary studies and design phases, as the focus is on final investment
budgets. Nonetheless, several projects display significant budget increases that appear to
originate during early phases, and these cases merit attention for their potential to illustrate
similar data inconsistencies. Notable examples include Holbæk–Vig, Frederikssundmo-
torvejen, Soften, and Funder–Låsby, all of which are documented in status report 1-2009,
Table 4.1 on p. 33.
Taken together, these first observations point to an evolving but inconsistently applied
reporting framework, where certain anomalies, whether reductions, increases, or cate-
gory introductions, require further scrutiny. A clearer understanding of these patterns is
essential for any robust analysis of project performance across cost and time dimensions.

3.4.2
Limitations and Assumptions
During the investigation of the status reports, a tendency, of roughly the second half of the
documents, is observed, related to the acknowledgment of completed projects since the
previous update. Only the name of the projects is provided, eliminating the possibility of
knowing the final financial framework per project. This is considered a big limitation of the
study, which is consequently tackled by the assumption that the last-recorded consump-
tion amount from the sheet Forbrug pr. (Consumption per) is to be treated as the actual
cost at completion. This method raises uncertainty, but is preferred over triangulation
and/or other research techniques, which combine multiple sources to compensate for the
missing data. Therefore, theoretical sampling in the quantitative context of construction
cost analysis is used, although traditionally associated with qualitative research, to substi-
tute for data gaps or inconsistencies that arise. This essentially allows the study to utilize
the last consumption amounts and supports the continuity of the analysis, facilitating the
integration of theoretically significant patterns, even in the absence of complete empirical
coverage from the status reports (Ligita et al., 2020).
Another aspect to be evaluated for major public construction projects in Denmark is
the time overruns. This also comes with its restrictions for precise measurement. In
Folketinget Rigsrevisionen, 2025, ministries’ construction projects from 2018 until 2022
are examined for completion on time, comparing the expected completion date from the
first appropriation in the Finance Act to a document with the actual time of put-in-operation.
This means that the information gathering technique includes different approaches, gov-
ernment institutions, and various documents and projects’ change logs, compared to
this research solely based on the status reports. Furthermore, in the vast state study,
deviations from expected commissioning are provided in the time unit of month/s, how-
ever, based on the limited information about completion dates in Anlægsstatus, the time
marker year/s is used for delay measurement instead, using the year of the last mention.
3.4.3
Project Selection
As previously described, the datasets consist of all Danish road and rail projects, exclud-
ing cycling and general-function pool projects, recorded under the Construction Status
reports from the second half of 2007 to the beginning of 2024. It covers 195 road and 177
rail projects, a large analysis sample, which, after manipulation, experiences unrealistic
results of cost underruns in the negative of 90% and more. Several reasons for those
poor and inconsistent results are recognized.

exclude projects whose financial records and, hence, results fall outside the reasonable

distorting the overall output. This ensures that the final dataset is more reflective of actual

4.4
Comparative Analysis with Norway
The Norwegian state is considered a close representation of the Danish context due to
several economic, institutional, and sociocultural similarities. This makes Norway a rel-
evant reference point when examining cost (Welde and Klakegg, 2022) and schedule
(Welde and Bukkestein, 2022) performance in public infrastructure projects. Hence, for
this study, Table 4.5 is developed to present a comparative overview of road and rail infras-
tructure projects in Denmark and Norway. For road projects, the data show that Denmark
outperforms Norway both in terms of the share of projects with cost overruns (21% in
Denmark compared to 33% in Norway) and in average cost performance, with Danish
projects exhibiting a substantially larger average underrun of -23%, compared to -4.5% in
Norway. In the rail sector, the differences are less pronounced. While Denmark records
a slightly higher share of projects with cost overruns (13%) than Norway (11%), the mean
cost overrun remains negative in both countries, with Danish projects averaging -9% and
Norwegian ones -5.4%. These figures suggest that, overall, Danish projects, particularly
in the road sector, tend to have fewer cost overruns and more conservative cost estimates
than their Norwegian counterparts. However, the consistent negative mean overruns in
both sectors and both countries highlight a general pattern of over-budgeting, reinforcing
the observation that while fewer projects exceed their budgets, this may come at the cost
of realism in estimates. This aligns with findings from both the Concept Program and the
Danish National Audit Office, which point to ongoing challenges in delivering projects on
time and within realistic financial parameters (Folketinget Rigsrevisionen, 2025).
In addition to cost performance, Table 4.6 illustrates a contrast of schedule performance
between Denmark and Norway in both the road and rail sectors. For road projects, the
Danish data indicate a markedly higher prevalence of delays, with 79% of projects expe-
riencing time overruns, compared to 47% in Norway. The mean time overrun for Danish
road projects is one year, three times higher than the Norwegian average of 0.33 years.
Furthermore, the variability in delays is greater in Denmark, as reflected in a higher stan-
dard deviation (1.15 years versus 0.75 years). The maximum recorded delay in Denmark
is 5 years, exceeding the Norwegian maximum of 3.92 years.

5.1.1
Less Consumption, Less Time
Ninety-two, 92, out of one hundred fourteen, 114, major Danish public construction
projects were completed with underspending according to their financial framework. This
reflects 52 roadway infrastructure projects and 40 track infrastructure projects. The under-
spending ranges from 1% to 77%. The underspending in road projects amounts to approx-
imately 16,7 billion DKK, and the underspending in rail engineering projects amounts to
approximately 5,8 billion DKK. The largest underspending for the Danish Road Directorate
is Funder-Låsby with approximately 4,4 billion DKK (2024 prices), while for Banedanmark,
it is Ny bane København-Ringsted over Køge with approximately 4,3 billion DKK (2024
prices). Although the numbers are exaggerated due to the unavailability of the final con-
struction amount in the Anlægsstatus, there seems to be a large gap in terms of estima-
tion accuracy. Less consumption does not necessarily equal good project performance.

5.3
Recommendations
This section presents a few outlined recommendations to different bodies on various iden-
tified gaps for improvement:
• The total financial consumption should be published in the Construction Status re-
ports rather than only mentioning names of completed projects since the last report.
That information is most probably available in the change logs, which might not be
for public use.
• From the perspective of no funds wasted, a probability distribution would be bet-
ter than fixed input values with their 10% and 20% reserves, meaning the Tradi-
tional budget estimation technique over NAB principle as there is no explicit return-
of-unused-funds policy.
• Establishment of a firm funds reassigning framework is necessary when cost under-
runs are present.
• Consistent negative mean overruns in road and rail sectors for Denmark and Nor-
way suggest over-budgeting, thus, a return to stochastic cost estimation (Traditional
budgeting principle) could be explored.
• Additionally, the reviewed literature advises that estimates are treated as floating
values while budgets are fixed.

Conclusion
This study set out to examine the cost and schedule performance of large-scale public
construction projects in Denmark, drawing comparative insights from Norway. The analy-
sis was conducted using a filtered dataset of 114 Danish projects valued at over DKK 97
billion and juxtaposed against recent performance data from Norway. The findings reveal
that Denmark’s project cost performance is characterized by frequent and substantial un-
derruns. While this might superficially suggest strong financial discipline, the data more
likely point to conservative budgeting practices or overestimated contingencies. Such
results, particularly prevalent under the New Budgeting Principle, raise concerns about
transparency and efficiency in the allocation of public funds.
In contrast, schedule performance in Denmark is demonstrably weaker. More than 75%
of the analyzed projects were completed later than planned, with rail projects exhibiting
an average delay of over two years. Compared to Norway, Denmark underperforms sig-
nificantly in meeting project timelines. This discrepancy can be linked to differences in
governance models. Norway’s incorporation of probabilistic cost estimation, mandatory
external quality assurance at key project stages, and a strong institutional framework has
demonstrably reduced the risks of both cost and time overruns.
The Danish model, while inspired by Norway’s, lacks the same breadth and depth, es-
pecially outside the transport sector. Moreover, the current deterministic approach to
budgeting does not sufficiently accommodate uncertainty, leading to either inflated bud-
gets or unrealistic scheduling. This study concludes that a more dynamic, risk-informed
approach, anchored in stochastic estimation and supported by independent quality assur-
ance, would offer a more accurate and accountable path forward for Danish infrastructure
governance. Future research should expand the dataset to include the categories of
buildings and other projects with special characteristics, like the metro and Femern Bælt
projects), integrate qualitative methods such as interviews, and further explore the appli-
cability of AI in enhancing data collection and analysis for public infrastructure evaluation.
