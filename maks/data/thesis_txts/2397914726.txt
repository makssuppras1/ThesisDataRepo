 Abstract
The need for clean drinking water is a universal concern, especially at locations where people and 
companies are depended on surface water. Therefore, the development of a rapid method for 
detection and quantification of bacteria, surviving water disinfection treatments, is of great value to 
prevent disease and/or to prevent recall of food products. This thesis developed a quantitative 
polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) method with propidium monoazide (PMA) for the aforementioned 
purpose. The thesis used a strain of Escherichia coli and Listeria monocytogenes, where the qPCR 
targeted two DNA fragments of 123 bp and 1465 bp of the 16S rRNA gene. The effect of amplicon 
length and PMA treatments on the methods ability to detect viable and heat killed (95˚C for 15 
minutes) cells, was investigated. Cells of E. coli were treated with chlorine to study the PMA-qPCR 
method’s use on cells inactivated by disinfection treatments. Additionally, inactivation rates and 
detection of viable but nonculturable cells were performed. The long amplicon qPCR and PMA-qPCR 
method were used for assessment of water quality of samples from two drinking water treatment 
plants (DWTP) and two of Royal Greenland’s (RG) facilities in Ilulissat and Sisimiut, Greenland. The 
short amplicon underestimated the viable cells counts, while the long amplicon overestimated these 
during the experiments with heat killed cells. The long amplicon PMA-qPCR was found to be 
superior in reducing the signal of heat killed and chlorine inactivated cells, and also resulted in the 
fastest inactivation rate. Additionally, it was found that chlorine did indeed induce a VBNC state in 
cells of E. coli. Assessment of water quality at the DWTP’s and RG facilities showed that the long 
amplicon qPCR in most cases was able to detect more cells, than the cell counts e.g. after the water 
disinfection treatments. In total this thesis showed that the use of a long amplicon qPCR was superior 
compared to the use of a short amplicon qPCR, with significant (p<0.05) reductions when combined 
with PMA treatments.

 Background
Greenland is dependent on surface water as a drinking water source as well as for food production. 
The use of surface water poses several potential issues as it is directly affected by the environment and 
weather, which in turn affects the final quality of the water. Insufficient quality of the water used for 
food production causes companies to withhold products from the market or in worst case scenarios 
stop their entire production. 
The Greenland Home Rules’ regulation no. 7 of 17th March 2008 sets up limits to ensure that all drinking 
water produced in Greenland is sufficiently clean for human consumption and thereby also for food 
production. The regulative has set up limits for several indicator bacteria such as E. coli, Clostridium 
perfringens, etc. and for physical/chemical properties of the water.   
The seafood processing company Royal Greenland has their own limits and performs tests of the water 
used at their facilities. This testing showed that 40% of the samples collected in 2017, exceeded the 
microbial limits in regards to one or more of the parameters listed in the regulation and/or their own 
limits. Those results give rise to concerns and make it important to find a solution to the problematic 
water quality at their facilities.  
The current methods used for determining microbial water quality are often dependent on plate counts, 
a method that takes quite a long time (minimum of 24 hours), meaning that the water produced has 
often already been used before the results of the microbial tests are in hand. Therefore, a rapid detection 
method is desired as it could decrease the detection time from >24 hours to <6 hours.   
Moreover, the current methods are not capable of differentiating between dead and viable but non 
culturable cells, which could pose a risk as these bacteria have been found to be able to regain viability. 
Studies have found quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) to be beneficial in detecting bacteria 
from environmental samples, and that the use of a longer amplicon in combination with propidium 
monoazide (PMA) is beneficial in differentiating between signal from DNA from viable and dead cells.

 Aim and objectives
This aim of this project is to develop and verify a quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) 
method for detection of bacteria that have survived water disinfection treatments.  
 The objectives of this thesis are listed below as research questions:  
1) What does amplicon length and propidium monoazide (PMA) treatment mean for the method’s 
ability to detect live bacteria? 
2) Does the addition of sodium dodecyl sulphate (SDS) improve the qPCR method’s detection of 
live bacterial cells?  
3) What does amplicon length and PMA treatment mean for the detection of chlorine treated 
bacteria? 
4) Can the method be used to detect bacteria surviving water disinfection treatments? 
5) Does water sampled at selected production sites in Greenland comply with the Home Rule’s 
water regulation and the internal limits of Royal Greenland? 
The method will be verified on two model bacteria; the Gram-negative Escherichia coli and the Gram-
positive Listeria monocytogenes, to ensure that the detection method is universal. The method is then 
used to compare the microbial content and physical/chemical properties of the water before and after 
disinfection treatments at the drinking water treatment plants (DWTP) both in Ilulissat and Sisimiut. 
Furthermore, samples was taken at Royal Greenland’s (RG) production facilities in Ilulissat and 
Sisimiut, where samples were taken at the input to the factory, at the transition zone and of the water 
used for the final glazing of the processed shrimps, to see if there is a variation between the sites. 
Additionally, samples taken in April were compared to samples taken in September of 2017 to 
investigate the seasonal variations.  
The water samples are additionally analysed according to Royal Greenland’s procedures for 
microbiological parameters (plate counts at 21°C and 37°C, E. coli, Enterococcus, Clostridium perfringens, 
etc.) and other water characteristics (turbidity, chlorine content, alkalinity, pH, conductivity, etc.).

 1.
 Introduction1.1.
 Microbiology of surface water
Residents in Greenland are dependent on the use of surface water as the main source of drinking water 
due to the lack of or difficult access to water in the underground. The microbial diversity of a water 
source depends on several parameters like geography, surrounding environments, ecology of the water 
and temperature shifts during the year. Therefore, there is not one simple answer to what types of 
microorganisms are most commonly found in surface water.  
Most of the microbes commonly found in drinking water are heterotrophic, meaning that they need 
organic compounds for their carbon and energy sources (Miettinen et al. 1997). Lechevalier et al. (1980) 
enumerated at total of nearly 700 bacteria in raw and finished drinking water in Oregon. Of the bacteria 
isolated from the finished drinking water, the largest portion consisted of bacteria from the 
actinomycetes group, whereas Aeromonas spp. represented the second largest group. They also found 
that Acinetobacter was the most commonly isolated organism. Bacteria that are primary or 
opportunistic pathogens (Pseudomonas maltophila, Pseudomonas fluorescens, Pseudomonas cepacia, 
Pseudomonas mallei, Acinetobacter spp., Klebsiella pneumoniae, Staphylococcus aureus, Moraxella spp., 
Serratia liquefaciens) constituted 30% of the total population identified. Whereas bacteria considered 
antagonistic to coliforms (Pseudomonas, Micrococcus, Flavobacterium, Bacillus, Actinomyces) comprised 
35%. Of the bacteria isolated from the raw water, the largest portion was Aeromonas spp.. Enterobacter 
agglomerans was the second most common group and Acinobacter was the third, while bacteria that 
may be primary or opportunistic pathogens and coliform antagonists comprised 51 and 14 % of the 
population, respectively (Lechevallier et al. 1980).  
Pseudomonas aeruginosa and other fluorescent Pseudomonas were found able to grow in treated tap 
water, as well as mesophilic fungi with the most frequently occurring being Penicillium, Sporocybe, 
Acremonium and Paecilomyces (Reasoner 1983). Species of Legionella are also commonly found in 
water, and are considered a risk as L. pneumophila is known to cause legionellosis and a pneumonia 
type illness (Reasoner 1983, Slimani et al. 2012).   
Eukaryotic protists and virus have also been detected in waters. Ninety-seven % of the tested surface 
water sources in the United states have been found to contain cysts of the eukaryote Giardia (Reasoner 
1983, Madigan 2015).  The protist Cryptosporidium parvum has also been discovered in surface water 
as it is found in high numbers in the faeces of warm-blooded animals. This protist produces thick-
walled, chlorine resistant, infective cells called oocysts, and these oocyst are up to 14 times more 
resistant to chlorine than cysts of Giardia (Madigan 2015). 
Other studies report that Listeria spp. has also been found in both surface and drinking water, due to 
the bacteria being ubiquitous in nature. The bacterium has been isolated from surface waters in the 
Netherlands and river waters in California, northern Greece and Italy. Studies proposed that the reason

for the occurrence of Listeria spp. in drinking water utilities could be due to faecal waste or surface

Several studies found that drinking water treatment influenced surviving total counts and occurrence 
of various species depending on the treatment process (Reasoner 1983). 
 1.1.1.
 Seasonal variationsin water quality
Studying seasonal dynamics of drinking waters bacterial communities is especially relevant as there is 
a partial transfer of microbes from the source water to the drinking water (Eichler et al. 2006). Several 
studies found that the bacterial community in the source water can be expected to vary as a response 
to geographic parameters and seasonal changes in environmental factors such as amount of light, 
temperature, nutrient content and precipitation (Miettinen et al. 1997, Hullar et al. 2006, Shade et al. 
2008). Understanding these factors could help in preventing varying quality of the water, as precautions 
can be taken to ensure clean drinking water.  
Higher water temperatures during the summer period are likely to influence the survival of vegetative 
C. perfringens cells, therefore their detection during the warmers seasons would reflect immediate 
wastewater pollution to a greater degree than during the colder seasons (Bisson and Cabelli 1980).  
Several studies have linked high contents of E. coli in water to heavy rain periods. Also, high turbidity 
measurements have been linked to snowmelt during the spring and heavy precipitation during fall 
(Ouyang et al. 2006, Scheili et al. 2015). Other studies have suggested that heavy precipitation causes 
the total cell count in samples to decrease due to dilution by increasing water amounts (Henne et al. 
2013). In contrast, others found an increased amount of bacteria after heavy rains and explained it by 
runoff causing an inflow of organic and inorganic nutrients into the source water, thereby seeing an 
increased growth of bacteria (Kristemann et al. 2002).  
Pollutants entering a system often result from numerous transport pathways,  including storm water 
runoff, discharge from ditches and creeks, vadose zone leaching and atmospheric deposition (Ouyang 
et al. 2006). These pathways are dependent on the weather of the seasons and therefore the quality of 
the water changes during the year. 
Quyang et al. (2006) demonstrated that a water quality parameter that is important in affecting the 
water quality during one season may not be important during another season. In the spring, organic 
pollution has been linked to the fluctuation of the discharge along the water with the land washing 
process due to snow melting and heavy rain (Bengraïne and Marhaba 2003, Ouyang et al. 2006).  
A study by Mazoua and Chauvehaid (2005) found that the lower temperatures during the winter caused 
the suspended particles not to settle efficiently and were therefore responsible for higher turbidity at 
the inlet of the filtration stage, thereby decreasing the effectivity of the filtration step (Mazoua and 
Chauveheid 2005).

 1.1.2.
 Water used for food production
Most, if not all, food producing companies use large volumes of water, either for cleaning, 
cooling/heating or directly in the production of foods.  
An example of the use of water during food production is the production of cooked ready-to-eat 
shrimp. Here a brine or glaze is added to the cooked and frozen shrimp as a final step before packaging, 
to ensure shelf life and product quality by minimizing the product’s contact with air (Royal Greenland 
2018). Therefore, it is very important that the used water is sufficiently clean. The glazing of the shrimp 
is done after they are boiled and there is therefore not an additional step during the production that 
ensures a final product with no growth of undesirable microorganisms, such as L. monocytogenes 
(Mejlholm et al. 2008).  1.2.
 Ensuring water quality
The water used at Royal Greenland must comply with criteria stated in both the regulation of the 
Greenland home rule, the internal limits at the company and the European Union’s (EU) regulations as 
the products are exported to countries within the EU. 1.2.1.
 Drinking water regulations
Drinking water in Greenland is produced by the company Nukissiorfiit, which follows regulations set 
up by the Greenlandic Home Rule government; the home rule’s regulation no. 7 of 17. March 2008 on 
water quality and oversight with water supply systems (Hjemmestyrets bekendtgørelse nr. 7 af 17. marts 
2008 om vandkvalitet og tilsyn med vandforsyningsanlæg) (Nukissiorfiit 2018). The regulation sets up 
minimum quality requirements of raw water, drinking water and water used in food processing 
facilities all of which must be followed. It also determines which tests, and at what frequency, they 
must be performed.  
§19 states that water used for drinking water must be free of microorganisms and compounds in 
concentrations that poses a risk to human health and must as a minimum comply with the limits set 
up for the content of microorganisms and compounds in the water (limits are stated in appendix 1A-
C) see Table 1 and Table 2. These parameters must be evaluated at three steps in the distribution 
system:  
1) Departure from the main water pipe, public water taps, water trucks and tank ships 
2) Where the water is bottled  
3) Where the water is used for food processing 
Furthermore, the monitoring frequencies must be representative for the water quality throughout the 
year, responding to the period in the spring where all the snow and ice melts, in the summer, before 
the freezing of the lakes during fall and in the winter. Monitoring must also be done on different 
weekdays and times of the day. The stated minimal frequency of sampling must be every eight to ten 
years.

 1.3.
 Microbial indicatorsof water quality
As described earlier, a large diversity of bacteria can be found in a water habitat. These include both 
risk-free organisms, but also primary and opportunistic pathogens often derived from faecal 
contamination of human or animal origin. Therefore, to ensure a high quality and safety of the water 
a regular water quality assessment is a prerequisite. 
Microbial contamination of drinking water causing morbidity and mortality among consumers is one 
of the major challenges worldwide (Mirzaei et al. 2015). Therefore, microbial water quality indicators 
are used to minimize the risk for potential infectious diseases arising from faecal pollution of aquatic 
environments (Bisson and Cabelli 1980). Microbial indicators are also commonly used due to difficulties 
in detecting the many pathogens possibly present in the water (such as Salmonella sp., Shigella sp., 
diarrheagenic E. coli, Giardia lamblia, Cryptosporidium parvum and enteric viruses) (Hachich et al. 
2012). It is assumed that the presence of these indicator bacteria indicates the potential presence and 
growth of human pathogens. This is an old idea, since Percy and Grace Frankland already in 1897 came 
up with the concept that organisms characteristic of sewage, and thereby the gastrointestinal tracts of 
both animals and humans, could be used to provide evidence of a potentially dangerous pollution 
(Ashbolt et al. 2001). A major requirement for a bacteria to be a faecal indicator is that it is consistently 
present in human faeces at a relatively high density (Bisson and Cabelli 1980) and that it is also present 
in wastewater at a reasonably high level (Bisson and Cabelli 1980), making it detectable by standard 
methods.

As mentioned earlier in section 1.2, four different microbiological parameters are used in the regulation 
of drinking water derived from surface water. These are the content of total coliform bacteria, 
Enterococcus, E. coli and Clostridium perfringens, including spores, as well as total viable cells at 37°C 
and 21°C. The following section will be describing the microbial water quality indicators. 1.3.1.
 Coliform bacteria 
Coliform bacteria are members of the genera or species within the family Enterobacteriaceae that are 
characterized by being facultatively anaerobic, Gram-negative, rod-shaped, nonsporulating and 
ferments lactose with the production of gas within 48 hours at 35°C (Ashbolt et al. 2001, Hachich et al. 
2012, Madigan 2015)..There are two main types of coliforms; total coliform and faecal coliforms. Total 
coliforms is a term used for all coliforms and can be found in soil, vegetation, aquatic environments 
like water that has been influenced by surface water, and in human or animal waste. Faecal coliforms, 
determined by their ability to grow at 44-45°C, are considered to be present specifically in the gut and 
faeces of warm-blooded animals and can therefore be used as an indicator for faecal pollution of water 
(Staradumskytė and Paulauskas 2012, Madigan 2015).   1.3.2.
 E. coli
E. coli was chosen as a biological indicator of water treatment safety in the 1980s, as it is considered to 
be almost exclusively of faecal origin. It can be distinguished from most other coliforms by its ability 
to ferment lactose at 44 °C and by its growth and colour reaction on certain types of culture media. E. 
coli is the major species within the faecal coliform group and furthermore this bacterium is not 
generally growing or reproducing in the environment, and thereby not in water (Staradumskytė and 
Paulauskas 2012). These properties make it the specie that is considered the best of the coliform bacteria 
to indicate faecal pollution (Edberg et al. 2000). It is also used as an indicator as it can indicate the 
presence of pathogens and thereby imply the threat of presence of enteric disease agents (Rice et al. 
1990). Some strains of E. coli are known diarrheal pathogens and others can opportunistically cause 
extra-intestinal infections, particularly in the gastrointestinal tract (Madigan 2015). It is estimated that 
E. coli survives in the drinking water for 4-12 weeks depending on the environmental conditions 
(temperature, microflora etc.) (Edberg et al. 2000). 1.3.3.
 ThermotolerantE. coli
Internal tests at Royal Greenland also checks for the presence of thermotolerant E. coli. This particular 
type of E. coli is distinguished from the common E. coli by its ability to grow at 44-45°C, produce indole 
from tryptophan, but also by being able to produce β-glucuronidase. It is actually the most  appropriate 
group of coliforms to indicate recent faecal pollution from warm-blooded animals, as it, due to its 
optimum growth temperature, has greater difficulties growing in the low temperatures of the water 
and will therefor only be present in this habitat for a limited time after pollution has occurred (Bisson 
and Cabelli 1980).

 1.3.4.
 Enterococcus
The Enterococci are a subset of faecal Streptococci (Ashbolt et al. 2001). Enterococci are commonly found 
in the faeces of humans and other warm-blooded animals. Although some of the strains are ubiquitous 
and not related to faecal pollution, the presence of Enterococci in water is used as an indication this and 
the possible presence of enteric pathogens (Staradumskytė and Paulauskas 2012). Enterococci are Gram-
positive cocci that often occur in diplococci or short chains and are problematic to distinguish from 
Streptococci by only using physical characteristics alone (Staradumskytė and Paulauskas 2012). 
The predominant intestinal enterococci are E. faecalis, E. faecium, E. durans and E. hirae. In addition, 
other Enterococcus species and some species of Streptococcus (namely S. bovis and S. equinus) may 
occasionally originate from other habitats than the gastrointestinal tract of humans and animals. 
(Ashbolt et al. 2001). 
Enterococci is capable of growth in the presence of high salt levels (6.5% NaCl). This characteristic in 
combination with it possible faecal origin and its presence in aquatic environments makes it suitable 
for the evaluation of quality of marine waters (Byappanahalli et al. 2012).  
 1.3.5.
 Clostridium perfringens (incl. spores) 
Clostridium perfringens is a Gram-positive, endospore-forming anaerobic bacterium that is frequently 
found in soil and in the intestinal tract of humans and other animals. It ferments lactose, sucrose and 
inositol with the production of gas, it produces a stormy clot fermentation in milk, reduces nitrate, 
hydrolyses gelatine, produces lecithinase and acid phosphate (Ashbolt et al. 2001). The natural 
contamination source is human and animal faeces transmitted into foods primarily by water. A large 
ingestion dose is needed for the bacteria to cause food poisoning (>108 cells) (Madigan 2015). It 
produces a range of invasins and exotoxins, where the exotoxins cause food poisoning as well as wound 
and surgical infections that lead to gas gangrene. C. perfringens is known for producing a resistant 
spore and a sensitive vegetative cell, making it useful as a water pollution indicator (Bisson and Cabelli 
1980).  
Studies suggest that the recovery of vegetative cells could indicate an immediate, untreated faecal 
source. When using the spores as a microbial indicator it is important to remember that the survival 
of the spores are influenced by temperature, and therefore their presence during the warmer seasons 
reflects immediate water pollution to a greater degree than during the winter season (Bisson and 
Cabelli 1980). The spores of C. perfringens has also been proposed as a surrogate parameter for the 
assessment of the treatment efficiency against protozoan (oo) cysts (Hijnen et al. 2000).

 1.4.2.
 Heterotopic plate counts 
Heterotopic plate counts (HPC) are often used for the enumeration of heterotrophic culturable 
microorganisms. This method is often based on the pour plate method, but also membrane filtration 
and the spread plate methods are used (WHO and OECD 2004).   
For both the pour plate method and the spread plate method, the occurring colonies are used to 
calculate colony forming units per ml (CFU/ml) in the original sample. The media used for both 
methods are generally a media on which almost all bacteria are capable of growth (Sanders 2012).  
Drawbacks to both methods are the small volumes of water being analysed in each method. The pour 
plate method only uses a volume of up to two ml and the spread plate has a limit of 0.5 ml (Pall 
Coperation 2018).  1.4.3.
 Membrane filtration 
Membrane filtration allows for the sampling of larger amounts of water compared to other microbial 
tests, as the sample is filtered through a membrane which concentrate any microorganisms present in 
the sample onto the surface of the membrane. The membrane filter is hereafter incubated on 
appropriate media and the resulting colonies are then used for calculations of CFU/ml (or another unit) 
(Pall Coperation 2018). The only limit to the sample volume is that if the sample has a high turbidity, 
the pores in the filter are likely to get blocked.  1.4.4.
 qPCR as a water quality method
Another, relatively uncommon method for the routine detection of microbes in water is the real-time 
polymerase chain reaction (PCR) also known as quantitative PCR, which is briefly described in relation 
to detection of microbes in water below, and in more detail in section 1.7.  
Quantitative PCR (qPCR) has proven to be a highly sensitive and specific method for detection of both 
waterborne bacteria and pathogens. It has been concluded that the presence of intact DNA represents 
the existence of intact living cells, and the method also assesses the lesions that occurs in DNA during 
disinfection treatments (Xu et al. 2017). One problem when using this microbial assessment method is 
that the assay volume is often only some microliters, whereas the sample volume is in the range of 
100-1000 ml. Bej et al. (1991) published a filtration method which concentrate the water sample, thereby 
solving this problem. Another problem is that the natural water samples often contain inhibitory

that the targeted organism(s) do not need to be culturable to be detected (Ashbolt et al. 2001) and the

As a response to environmental conditions, bacteria might not be detectable by the traditional methods. 
This is as they might not be culturable even though they are still viable (see section 1.5), which makes 
microbial risk evaluation difficult. Therefore, the use of qPCR as a detection method has gained 
acknowledgement over the recent years (Nocker et al. 2007a).  
 1.5.
 The Viable But Nonculturable (VBNC) state in bacteria
A problem with using the conventional microbial testing methods is the fact that they are dependent 
on the microorganisms being culturable. For a long time it had been assumed that a bacterial cell was 
dead when it no longer was able to grow on routine culture media. This assumption has later, due to 
extensive research, been invalidated. This phenomenon is known as the “viable but nonculturable” 
(VBNC) state and several bacteria, both pathogenic and non-pathogenic, are known to be able to enter 
this state (Oliver 2005).  
Even though bacteria in this dormancy state appear to have a low level of metabolic activity, they are 
capable of regaining growth upon resuscitation. Numerous bacteria may enter this state when exposed 
to stressful environmental conditions (Oliver 2010, Xiao et al. 2013, Zhang et al. 2015). Environmental 
factors found to induce a VBNC state in bacteria are adverse nutrition levels, starvation, temperatures, 
osmotic stress and food preservatives. These are environmental stresses which might be lethal if the 
bacteria do not enter the VBNC state. Additionally, processes which are normally assumed to be 
bactericidal, like chlorination, UV and pasteurization, may instead induce this state (Oliver 2005).  
Pathogenic bacteria capable of entering this state are not limited to human pathogens, but also 
pathogens infecting animals, fish and plants have been discovered (Oliver 2010). The list of human 
pathogens capable of entering this state is quite long, including bacteria like Campylobacter spp., E. 
coli, Helicobacter pylori, L. monocytogenes and Pseudomonas aeruginosa as well as several Salmonella 
strains and Vibrio spp. Also, Enterococcus faecalis, a bacterium of importance when considering water 
quality, has been found to be enter the VBNC state.  
Bacteria in the VBNC state are a public health concern in drinking water as they would lead to an 
underestimation of the total bacteria counts (Zhang et al. 2015). An example of this is seen in a study 
by Makino et al., 2005, who found that approximately 0.75-1.5 culturable cells appeared to be 
responsible for an E. coli O157:H7 outbreak in Japan in 1998, a number considered far too low to cause 
infection. They provided evidence that a larger number of cells most likely existed in the VBNC state 
in the food, and that these VBNC cells were the source of the outbreak (Makino et al. 2005).

 1.6.
 Treatments of surface water
There are several ways of treating surface water, all depending on the type and quality of the raw 
water. The methods used at the two drinking water treatment plants (DWTP) relevant for this project 
(Ilulissat and Sisimiut) are described in the following sections.  1.6.1.
 Aeration
The first step in the water treatment is the aeration of the water. Water aeration has been used during 
the water treatment process for a long time to remove odour and taste-causing compounds and to 
oxidize iron and manganese, as well as for corrosion control (Wang et al. 2006). It is also used to remove 
carcinogenic and hazardous chemicals (including volatile organics, such as trihalomethanes, radon, 
trichloroethylene, tetrachloroethylene, 1,1,1-trichloroethane, chloroform, and toluene) from the water, 
wherefore some consider it to be one of the most important water treatment processes used in the 21st 
century (Wang et al. 2006).  
There are several ways to aerate water like falling water units, surface aerators and submerged 
aerators, all based on the same principle; introducing air into the water, either done by exposing water 
film to air or by introducing bubbles (Wang et al. 2006). This air causes the solved gases and volatile 
compounds to be released from the water. For example in the case of iron and manganese, these 
minerals go from a dissolved state into a solid state and precipitate, which makes it possible for the 
downstream treatment to filter out the particles (Wang et al. 2006, eXtension 2018).  
 1.6.2.
 Coagulation,flocculation and sedimentation
The next step is the coagulation, which is a process that combines smaller suspended particles into 
larger aggregates and absorbs dissolved organic matter on to particulate aggregates to make it possible 
to remove impurities in a subsequent separation/filtration process (Jiang 2015, Madigan 2015). To aid 
the coagulation process, different chemicals are added including bentonite, silicates, and limestone.  
Following coagulation is flocculation; a gentle stirring or agitation process in which the particles 
continue to react and to agglomerate into even larger and more easily removed clots (also known as 
flocs), as these settle by gravity, trapping microorganisms and adsorbing suspended organic matter in 
a sedimentation process (Madigan 2015).  1.6.3.
 Sand filtration
After the coagulation/flocculation process, the water is run through a filtration process to settle out 
the solids and particles formed in the previous steps. Here the flocs formed in the coagulation and 
filtration step is retained by the sand particles. At the end of this step the water should be free of 
organic and inorganic solutes and suspended particles (Madigan 2015).

 1.6.4.
 Ultraviolet treatment 
The first disinfection step during water treatments is disinfection with ultraviolet light (UV). The 
germicidal properties of UV light were first discovered in 1887, and was first used for disinfection of 
drinking water in 1910 (Clarke 2006, Kowalski 2009). 
The portion of the UV spectrum between 240-280 nm has a strong inactivation effect on 
microorganisms, where the DNA appears to absorb UV most efficiently in the range of 250-270 nm 
(Wolfe 1990, Wang et al. 2006). This spectrum is referred to as the UVC. Irradiation by UVC kills or 
rather renders microorganisms incapable of reproduction by photochemically causing irreparable 
damage to their DNA (Oliver and Carey 1976, Wolfe 1990). Unsaturated organic compounds 
(compounds that are not completely saturated with hydrogen bonds) efficiently absorbs the 
wavelengths of UVC, which results in unstable conformations of the bonds. When one of the photons 
produced by UV radiation hits an electron of conjugated bonds, it induces a higher energy level. This 
disruption of stable electrons is able to travel the entire organic structure, raising a bonded electron 
out of a bonding pairand thereby resulting in an unstable conformation. In conjugated bond-containing 
structures, the π-orbitals are shared throughout the entire ringed structure. Due to this, the entire 
structure absorbs the photon (and thereby the UV energy) and the formed extra energy (the photons 
energy is converted to vibrational energy) is drained off into the weakest bond, which causes 
conformational changes. The conjugated organic structures include nitrogen-containing ring 
structures such as purines, pyrimidines, flavins and the aromatic amino acids, all essential to cell 
reproduction and cell metabolism. Purines provide the basic structure for the nucleobases adenine and 
guanine and the aromatic amino acids, phenylalanine and tyrosine. Flavin is an unsaturated organic 
compound found in flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD), a molecule that is considered to be essential for 
metabolic redox reaction, an important hydrogen acceptor in the cell and ATP is generated as a result 
of the oxidation of FADH. Pyrimidines provides the basic structure for uracil, thymine and cytosine 
(Cutler and Zimmerman 2011).  
There are six possible nucleic acid photoproducts induced by UV light (Kowalski 2009):  
1) A thymine-thymine dimer 
2) A cytosine-cytosine dimer 
3) A cytosine-thymine dimer 
4) An uracil-uracil dimer 
5) An uracil-thymine dimer (when occurring between DNA/RNA and adjacent protein 
molecules) 
6) An uracil-cytosine dimer

The fact that UV treatment does not create significant levels of disinfection by products (DBPs), and it 
being cost-effective has led to UV disinfection gaining increased acceptance as an attractive cost-
effective control for pathogens in many applications such as in treatment of ground and surface water 
(Clarke 2006, Cutler and Zimmerman 2011).  
The disinfection by UV light only works when UV light is absorbed into the microorganisms, therefore 
conditions and materials that interferes with this process decreases the killing efficiency of the UV 
light. These include biological films on the surface of the UV lamps, clumping or aggregation of 
microorganisms, turbidity, colour, dissolved organic and inorganic materials (Wang et al. 2006).  
An advantage of the UV light is its ability to kill cysts and oocysts of protists such as Giardia and 
Cryptosporidium (eukaryotic pathogens found in water), which chlorine is incapable of (Madigan 2015). 
One rather important drawback to the use of UV as a disinfection method of water is the lack of a 
continuous disinfection efficiency as seen by chlorination (Xu et al. 2017). Therefore, UV is rarely used 
as the only disinfection treatment in the production of drinking water (Wang et al. 2006).  1.6.5.
 Chlorine treatment

compound like sodium hypochlorite or chlorine dioxide. This section will discuss the effects and 
mechanisms of sodium hypochlorite, as this is the type used at the two DWTPs in Greenland.  
Chlorine is one of the most commonly used antimicrobial chemicals in drinking water treatment due 
to its high oxidation capacity (Nocker et al. 2007b). Chlorine inactivates all types of microorganisms: 
protozoa, bacteria, viruses and eukaryotes, and an important advantage of chlorine as an disinfectant

 Killing mechanisms of chlorine
The mode of action of chlorine on bacteria in general and its effect on the bacterial membrane has been 
investigated and debated in several studies (Venkobachar et al. 1977, Virto et al. 2005, Hellein et al. 
2012, Gray et al. 2013). This section only mentions selected mechanism of chlorine induced 
inactivation, as the list is extensive. Hypochlorous acid is generally considered to be a highly 
destructive, nonselective oxidant, that produce reactive chlorine species (RCS) which reacts with most 
of the cellular macromolecules  and affects metabolic processes (Dukan et al. 1999, Gray et al. 2013). 
A review of the literature concerning chlorines inactivation mechanisms by Gray et al. (2013) states 
that chlorine kills bacteria by the formation of RCS, which most likely acts by damaging multiple 
cellular components at the same time. However, specifically how RCS kills different bacteria is not yet 
fully defined, but most likely vary depending on the different bacterial species, types of RCS and the 
conditions of exposure (Gray et al. 2013).  
Winter et al. (2008) suggests that the antimicrobial effects of hypochlorous acid are largely based on 
HOCl’s ability to cause aggregation of essential bacteria proteins. This protein unfolding is irreversible 
and the HOCl treated proteins revealed oxidative side chain modifications, including methionine 
oxidation (Winter et al. 2008). Hence the study concluded that thermolabile proteins are particularly 
prone to HOCl-mediated oxidative unfolding and aggregation (Winter et al. 2008). The same study 
found that cysteine, methionine and glutathione  and other sulphur containing compounds reacts with

HOCl, that the reaction is 100 times more rapidly than with the other cellular compounds and that 
these reactions often also leads to protein degradation (Winter et al. 2008, Gray et al. 2013).  
In proteins, primary and secondary amines are expected to be the second most reactive targets of HOCl. 
When reacting with HOCl the amines are chlorinated into chloramines, of which many possess potent 
antimicrobial activities. Chloramines are also considered to be RCS as they are capable of oxidizing 
other molecules, however these are four to five orders of magnitude less reactive than HOCl and seems 
to be more specific for cysteine and methionine oxidation (Gray et al. 2013).  
Another mechanism of chlorine is the inhibition of transport pathways. Barette et al. (1989) found that 
oxidation by HOCl completely inhibited the active transport of energy sources for ATP production as 
well as the ATPase, that catalyses the respiration-linked phosphoanhydride bond formation between 
ADP and Pi, was inhibited in parallel with the loss of viability (Barrette et al. 1989). Glucose oxidase, a 
mercatogroup(-SH) oxidase, is also destroyed by the disinfectant and is oxidized into a -S-S- group, 
which thereby loses the enzymatic activity making it impossible for it to participate in the oxidation-
reduction system, thereby resulting in the death of the bacteria (Huang et al. 1997) 
Chlorine can also react with nucleotides in cellular DNA. Primary and secondary amines of nucleotide 
bases seem to be the major targets in DNA and RNA and the reactions presumably involve the 
oxidation of purine and pyrimidine fractions. High doses of HOCl can therefore eventually lead to 
DNA strand breakage and mutations. Furthermore chlorine was found to induce base-substitutions and 
chromosomal aberrations (Wlodkowski and Rosenkranz 1975, Dukan and Toutati 1996, Gray et al. 
2013). 
A study by Virto et al. (2005) used propidium iodine (PI) to investigate if the membrane was affected 
by chlorine treatment. PI is commonly used as an indicator of cytoplasmic membrane damage as it can 
enter the cells only if the membrane is permeabilized. They found that, in distilled water, the dye was 
only able to enter the cells of both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria after cell death. In the 
presence of organic matter, no uptake of dye was seen, even at higher chlorine concentrations. The 
study concluded that extensive membrane damage was not a key event leading to cell death due to 
chlorination. These results were supported by a study of Xu et al. (2017) who, by using PI to stain 
permeabilized cells of E. coli, found that when the initial free chlorine concentration was 0.5 mg/l, the 
relative number of intact cells dropped to 55.9% within 2 min, followed by a slower rate of membrane 
damage (Xu et al. 2017). The reason for the greater resistance of Gram-negative bacteria to chlorine 
compared to Gram-positive bacteria, is considered to be due to an external layer, which poses an extra 
barrier against antimicrobial compounds (Virto et al. 2005) 
Due to the strong oxidizing ability of chlorine and its existence in nearly 100% of its undissociated 
state, it can easily penetrate through the cell membrane of a bacterium as the semipermeable membrane 
and the permeable pressure are destroyed. Therefore, the respiration of the bacterium is inhibited, and 
the phosphotransferase is inactivated resulting in cell death (Albrich and Hurst 1982). This change in

The chlorination of lipids can lead to several different products, like chlorohydrines produced from 
double bonds in unsaturated fatty acids. These are believed to contribute to the HOCl-mediated cellular 
damage in eukaryotic cells. Radicals derived from HOCl reactions with amines or peroxides can lead 
to lipid peroxidation, a complex pathway that degrades polyunsaturated fatty acids to aldehydes (Carr 
et al. 1997).  
 1.6.8.
 Factors affecting chlorination efficiency
The efficiency of the chlorine inactivation is affected by several factors like pH, contact time and the 
reactions of chlorine with the water. It has been discovered that aggregated bacterial cells or cells 
associated with suspended particulates may be protected against chlorines inactivation properties, 
when compared to unassociated bacteria (Reasoner 1983, WHO 1996b). Due to this it is important that 
the water to be treated with chlorine is clear and has a low turbidity meaning less than five 
nephelometric turbidity units (NTU) ideally less than one (WHO 1996a).  
Chlorine is consumed when it reacts with organic material, therefor the preceding steps (aeration, 
coagulation, flocculation, sedimentation and filtration) must be effective in reducing the amount of 
organic matter in the water (Madigan 2015).  
A study by Virto et al., 2005, found that, in the absence of organic matter, Gram-positive bacteria 
showed greater resistance to chlorine (concentrations between 0.3 ppm and 1.2 ppm for 2 minutes) 
compared to Gram-negative bacteria. Furthermore the presence of organic matter drastically increased 
both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria’s resistance to chlorine treatment (Virto et al. 2005). 
The effect of organic matter on chlorination efficiency has also been studied by Lechevallier et al., 1981 
who also found a strong connection between organic matter and chlorination efficiency (Lechevallier 
et al. 1981). According to this study, chlorine did not injure cells before they were completely 
inactivated if the cells were in the presence of organic matter (Lechevallier et al. 1981). They also found 
that even chlorine concentrations below 1 ppm for 2 min (in distilled water) inactivated more than 
99.9% of the cells of both Gram positive and Gram-negative bacteria.  1.6.9.
 Drawbacks to the use of chlorine as a disinfectant
Even though chlorination is an effective way of ensuring low levels of pathogens in drinking water, 
microbial resistance to chlorination has been observed in both lab studies and in full-scale chlorine 
disinfection practices for water and wastewater treatment (Ridgway and Olson 1982, Saby et al. 1999).  
The chlorination of drinking water might cause formation of disinfection by-products (DBPs) when 
free chlorine reacts with organic substances in the water. Examples of undesirable DBPs in drinking 
water are trihalomethanes (THMs), haloacetic acids (HAAs), haloacetonitriles (HANs) and 
haloketones (Kim et al. 2002). They are considered to be possible carcinogens and are therefore poses

a health risk for the consumers (Kim et al. 2002). Therefore, the regulation concerning the drinking 
water states that where it is deemed necessary, the routine analysis should include checking for 
organic chlorine compounds (regulation no. 7 of 17th March 2008). The benefits of reduced infectious 
disease through the appropriate chlorination of drinking water is generally considered to far 
outweigh the risk of cancer caused by trihalomethanes and therefore chlorine is still used as a 
disinfectant in water treatment in many countries (WHO 1996a).  
 1.7.
 Polymerase chain reaction (PCR)
To be able to detect if any microorganisms survives the previously described water treatments, PCR 
can potentially be used instead of the more common microbial culturing methods.  
PCR uses an enzyme called taqDNA polymerase, which is heat stable and copies DNA molecules 
naturally. PCR does not actually copy complete DNA molecules, but as determined by the primers used 
it amplifies segments of up to several thousand base pairs (bp), which is called the target sequence. The 
DNA is amplified exponentially, as the amount of DNA is doubled within each cycle. The three steps 
of a PCR amplification are:  
1) The denaturation step where the template is denatured by heating, meaning that the two 
strands of the DNA template is separated.  
2) The annealing step where the mix is cooled down and two artificial DNA oligonucleotide 
primers flanking the target DNA on each strand are added in excess, this ensures that the 
template strands anneal to a primer.  
3) The extension step where the mixture is then heated to the optimal temperature for the DNA 
polymerase, which binds to the primers and starts the synthesis of new DNA using the original 
DNA as the template, thereby producing a complementary DNA strand. 
After an incubation period the mixture is heated again, which separates the strands once again and 
now the target sequence is present in twice the original amount. This whole process, called a cycle, is 
then repeated for a specific number of times (Madigan 2015). 
An ordinary PCR process ends up with a gel electrophorese step to detect and visualize the amplified 
DNA, but another method called real-time or quantitative PCR does not use this step. Instead the 
quantitative PCR (qPCR) uses a fluorescent reporter molecule to monitor the production of 
amplification products of each of the cycles. This reporter molecule transmits a fluorescent light 
proportionally with the amplification of a DNA target (Bustin 2005). The specificity of the qPCR is 
dependent on the specificity of the primers used in the assay. Primers are combined with a DNA 
binding dye to monitor and detect the production of double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) during the PCR 
reaction. The DNA binding dye, also known as the fluorescent reporter, for universal primers could, 
amongst others, either be SYBR® Green or the newer EvaGreen®. One drawback of the DNA binding 
dyes is the lack of specific (as they bind to all dsDNA which could result in non-specific products like 
primer-dimers).

In qPCR, the point in time during cycling, where amplification of a PCR product is first detected, is 
measured. By this is meant the cycle number where the reporter dye emission rises above the 
background noise, and this cycle is referred to as the threshold cycle (Ct). The Ct occurs in the 
beginning of the exponential phase of the reaction and is inversely proportional to the number of 
copies of the target gene. This means that the higher the initial number of copies of the target, the 
sooner a significant increase in fluorescence is observed, thereby resulting in a lower Ct. This can be 
used to calculate the amount of template present in the beginning of the reaction (Bustin 2005).  
Once the qPCR reaction is run, the program software produces an amplification plot  (ThermoFisher 
Scientific 2016a, 2016b). This plot visualizes the amplification of the DNA template. Several parameters 
are shown on the final plot; baseline, threshold and the threshold cycles value (Ct) (see Figure 3).  
The baseline is the signal level during the initial cycles (often cycles 3-15) at which there is little change 
in fluorescence signal. This low signal is annotated to the background or the “noise” of the 
amplification reaction. The threshold is the level of signal that reflects a significant increase above the 
calculated baseline signal. It is often automatically set by the qPCR software, which sets it to be 10 
times the standard deviation of the fluorescence value of the baseline of the reaction.  
A standard curve is used to determine the initial amount of the target template in experimental samples 
and for assessing the reaction efficiency. The standard curve is made by performing a 10-fold dilution 
series of a control bacteria, like E. coli, and the concentrations of the dilution series should span over 
the whole range of expected unknown sample concentrations. In a perfect 10-fold dilution series, the 
Ct values should be as close to 3.3 cycles apart (ThermoFisher Scientific 2016a).

efficiently enough. Literature suggests that the slope should be within -3.1 and -3.6 or the efficiency

 16S rRNA as a universal primer
16S ribosomal RNA also known as the 16S rRNA gene has been used in molecular microbial ecology 
studies since 1990. It encodes a well conserved RNA component of the smaller subunit of the bacterial 
ribosome and plays a vital role in protein synthesis. It is involved in the initiation of protein synthesis  
through base pairing with the Shine-Dalgarno sequence on the mRNA (Madigan 2015). The 16S gene 
is present in all bacteria and in a related form in all cells. Due to the 16S rRNA molecule being so 
conserved across all bacteria it is possible to design so-called universal primers that should detect all 
types of bacteria (Suzuki et al. 2000). Furthermore, multiple copies of the genes may be present in 
bacteria. Case et al. 2007 found that the gene copy number ranged from one to 15, with an average of 
4.2 copies per genome (Case et al. 2007).  Using the 16S rRNA as the target gene for a qPCR, makes it 
possible to detect the total viable cells in a sample, but it should be remembered that, as several copies 
of the gene could be present in bacteria, only an estimation can be made.  1.7.2.
 Amplicon length
The chosen length of the amplicon used during qPCR has been found to be of importance when 
attempting to differentiate between live and dead cells. A longer amplicon is expected to be more 
sensitive in detecting damaged DNA than a shorter amplicon, as it is more likely that the DNA 
polymerase in the qPCR will encounter a damaged site in the DNA, which in turn would disrupt the 
amplification process (Chiao et al. 2014).

 1.8.
 Propidium Monoazide(PMA)
Propidium monoazide is a high affinity photoreactive DNA binding dye developed by the company 
Biotium. It is used for viability assays of bacteria and other microorganisms and is the successor of 
ethidium monoazide bromide (EMA) and Propidium Iodide (PI) (Nocker et al. 2006, Biotium 2017). Its 
molecular formula is 3-amino-8-azido-5-{3-[diethyl(methyl) ammonio] propyl}-6-
phenylphenanthridinium and its chemical structure can be seen in Figure 4.  
The principle behind the use of the fluorescent dye (PMA) is based on bacterial cell integrity, as PMA 
only penetrates cells with compromised membranes. Membrane integrity is considered a well-accepted 
criterion for distinguishing viable cells from dead cells (Nocker et al. 2007a). PMA carries an azide 
group, which allows for a covalent cross linkage to the DNA upon light-exposure. (Nocker et al. 2006, 
2007a). As the dead cells’ DNA has been modified they can therefore not be amplified or detected by 
the PCR process, see Figure 5 (Nocker et al. 2006, Salam et al. 2014, Biotium 2017).

 4.
 Discussion
Development of a long amplicon PMA-qPCR method for detection of viable bacteria.  4.1.
 Standard curves
The qPCR method used, provided standard curves for E. coli and L. monocytogenes and for both the 123 
bp short and the 1465 bp long amplicons, with efficiencies within the desired range of 90-110% and 
R2>0.98.  4.2.
 Effect of amplicon length and PMA treatment on suppressing signal fromheat kill
The use of a long amplicon in a qPCR have been widely studied and observed to be superior in reducing 
signal of DNA from dead cells of a wide variety of bacteria (Banihashemi et al. 2012, Schnetzinger et 
al. 2013, Ditommaso et al. 2015). The long amplicon used in this thesis targets a sequence of 1465 bp, 
being almost the entire 1,542 bp long 16S rRNA gene of E. coli, whereas the short amplicon only targets 
a DNA fragment of 123 bp; 1/12 of the whole gene. Additionally, the addition of a PMA treatment step 
have been studied extensively and the conclusions are clear; the addition of a PMA treatment reduces 
the signal of DNA from dead cells (Nocker et al. 2006, Hein et al. 2007, Contreras et al. 2011, 
Schnetzinger et al. 2013), even though the producers of the dye has established that the dye is not cable 
of eliminating the signal of dead cells altogether (Biotium 2017). 
In a first set of experiments, the effect of amplicon length and PMA treatment was evaluated in terms 
of the reduction of the qPCR signal of DNA from heat killed E. coli cells. The qPCR signal was 
dramatically reduced from 1.57 to 4.48 log CFU/ml and from 2.31 to 5.72 log CFU/ml for the short and 
long amplicons, respectively, when comparing the analysis of sample DNA extracted without and with 
prior PMA treatment. None of the amplicons were capable of reducing the signal to below the method’s 
quantification limit (short: 4.79 logCFU/ml, long: 3.79 logCFU/ml), but the long amplicon reduced the 
signal 1.24 log more than the short amplicon when a PMA treatment was added. The long amplicon 
qPCR method may inherently overestimate the viable cells in the sample, due to the seven copies of 
the 16S rRNA gene present in E. coli (NCBI 2018)   
The long amplicon PMA-qPCR method was similarly better at reducing the signal obtained from dead 
cells of L. monocytogenes, yielding a result of 3.08 log CFU/ml, whereas the short amplicon detected 
3.81 log CFU/ml. Although none of the amplicons are capable of reducing the signal of dead cells to 
below the detection limit.  
Comparing results for E. coli and L. monocytogenes, PMA appeared to be better at reducing the signal 
from dead cells of the Gram-negative E. coli than for the Gram-positive L. monocytogenes. For both 
bacteria, a greater reduction could be detected by the long amplicon compared to the short, in 
agreement with observations made by numerous similar studies (Nocker et al. 2007b, Contreras et al. 
2011, Xiao et al. 2013). Moreover, one should bear in mind that as the signal of PMA treated heat killed 
==PAGE:68==
 
 
Technical University of Denmark 
 
Page 67 of 87 
 
cells were all below the limit of quantification (LOQ), making the method incapable of quantifying any 
of the qPCR signals from this treatment. 
Studies have found that the efficiency of PMA in reducing the signal depends on both the time of 
incubation and light exposure of the PMA treated samples. These parameters depends on the types of 
samples to be analysed (Nocker et al. 2007b). The PMA treatment used in this thesis is similar to the 
treatment used by Chiao et al. (2014) of 50 µM PMA and light exposure for 10 min (Chiao et al. 2014). 
They incubated the samples for 15 min in the dark, whereas samples in the present thesis were 
incubated in the dark for twice as long (30 min). The benefits of an extended incubation time were 
studied by Nkuipou-Kenfack et al. (2013) who found that an extended incubation time of 30 min 
improved the efficiency of PMA on dead cells (Nkuipou-Kenfack et al. 2013). Based on this, the PMA 
treatment of the samples in this thesis should be effective for its purpose. 4.3.
 Significance of qPCR signal from dead cells during qPCR
To better understand the influence of the qPCR signal of heat killed cells on the total qPCR signal, DNA 
extracted from live cells were diluted with DNA from heat killed cells (no PMA treatment).  
Theoretically the dead cells should not contribute to the qPCR signal as their DNA is damaged and 
should therefore not be amplifiable. Therefore, when the DNA of live cells was 10-fold diluted, 
approximately a 1 log reduction in the signal should occur for every dilution. A reduction was seen for 
E. coli (Figure 13, A) of 1 log between the first and the second dilution (0% D and 90% D) for both the 
short and the long amplicons, however, these reductions decreased between the following dilutions for 
both amplicons, which is potentially due to the signal approaching the LOQ of this trial. In total a 
greater reduction is seen for the long amplicon. Less of a reduction is seen during the same treatment 
of L. monocytogenes (Figure 13, B), with a total reduction in signal of less than 1 log for both the short 
and the long amplicons. The long amplicon still appears to reduce the signal of dead cells better than 
the short amplicon, which again could be due to the signal approaching the LOQ, making this the likely 
LOD of this trial. This indicates that this method is less efficient in reducing the signal of dead cells of 
L. monocytogenes compared to cells of E. coli. But at some point, it is no longer possible for the qPCR 
method to reduce the signal further, and this point is seen earlier for cells of L. monocytogenes than for 
E. coli.  4.4.
 Effect of SDS and PMA treatment in reducing qPCR signal fromheat killed c
A study by Takahashi et al., 2017 found that the addition of treatment with SDS in combination with 
PMA could reduce the signal of dead cells further. Therefore, a study was performed to find out if the 
SDS treatment step could improve penetration of PMA into dead cells to improve the ability of the 16S 
rRNA PMA-qPCR method to distinguish between live and dead cells.  
The results of the experiment in Figure 15 A, showed that SDS had no effect in reducing the signal 
from heat killed E. coli or L. monocytogenes treated irrespectively of the presence/absence of a PMA 
==PAGE:69==
Master Thesis, MSc. Eng. Food Technology, DTU 
Page 68 of 87 
 
treatment, unlike the results of a reduction in signal of 2 log unit for E. coli found by Takahashi et al., 
2017. This difference could potentially be due to their application of a double treatment with PMA, but 
this appears to be unlikely as there was less than a one log difference between the application of one 
and two PMA treatments (Takahashi et al. 2017).   
The results for L. monocytogenes in Figure 15 B, showed that the combination of PMA and 250 ppm 
SDS has a bactericidal effect on cells of L. monocytogenes. This effect of SDS has also been seen by a 
study by Mnyandu et al. (2015) who found complete inactivation of L. monocytogenes after exposure to 
a concentration of 1% SDS for just 1 minute (Mnyandu et al. 2015). Other studies have found that SDS 
(0.1-2+%) causes an irreversible permeability of the cell membrane giving rise to other components 
entering the cells and thereby killing the cells (Zhao et al. 2009). This could potentially be the reason 
why a bactericidal effect is seen when treating the cells with SDS and then PMA, where the killing is 
done by PMA entering the cells and inactivating them. Other studies indicate that this bactericidal 
effect of SDS should not be happening at the low concentrations used in this trial (Walton et al. 2008).  
The lack of significant bactericidal effect of SDS and PMA on the Gram-negative E. coli seen in these 
results was also seen at low SDS concentrations for E. coli, and other bacteria by other studies (Walton 
et al. 2008, Zhao et al. 2009).  4.5.
 Chlorination trials4.5.1.
 Effect of chlorine on viable cells 
Once it was determined that amplicon length and PMA treatment were beneficial in reducing the signal 
from heat killed cells of both E. coli and L. monocytogenes, it was desired to study if the method could 
be applied to detect viable cells of E. coli following treatment with chlorine. This is of importance as 
the goal is to be able to use PMA-qPCR in detection of surviving bacteria in water after disinfection 
treatments.  
During the first preliminary trial (chlorine trial 1, see section 3.5.1.1), only a limited reduction of 2 log 
CFU/ml in total viable cells (TVC) was seen whereas the free chlorine dropped immensely from an 
initial concentration of 2 mg/l to 0.35 mg/l after 5 minutes.  This reduction in viable cells was less than 
expected when compared to similar studies (Aieta et al. 1980, Rice et al. 1999), but still showed that E. 
coli is sensitive to chlorine treatments. The lack of a sizeable reduction after 5 minutes, compared to 
literature, is likely due to the presence of organic matter in the cell suspension used (Rice et al. 1999, 
Virto et al. 2005). As described earlier this effect has been attributed to the higher chlorine demand of 
organic matter, which results in a rapid decline in the chlorine available to react with and kill the cells. 
The decreased concentration of chlorine is therefore not capable of reducing the high cell numbers as 
much as was anticipated. The organic matter originates from the media used to grow the cell 
suspension, and therefore it was decided to that the suspension in subsequent tests should be washed 
twice to eliminate as much organic matter as possible.  
==PAGE:70==
 
 
Technical University of Denmark 
 
Page 69 of 87 
 
Testing of the decay of chlorine in the sterile water used found that even without presence of cells, the 
amount of free chlorine decreases from 1.98 mg/l to 1.31 mg/l after 10 minutes (Appendix 8.4), therefore 
the chlorine demand is not only from the growth media, but also from the equipment and the water 
used, which is in agreement with another study by Virto et al. (2005) (Virto et al. 2005).  
Once it was known that the cells should be washed twice, another experiment (chlorine trial 2, see 
section 3.5.1.2) was performed with this in mind. Here the cells were washed twice and subjected to 
different concentrations of chlorine (1.5, 2, 2.5, 3 and 3.5 mg/l) for 5 minutes.  
Washing the cell suspension twice gave a whole other picture of the killing efficiency emphasizing 
that the presence of organic matter in suspension reduced the killing efficiency significantly (p<0.05). 
The results of this experiment is supported by results found in the literature (Aieta et al. 1980, Virto et 
al. 2005). From the results it appears that a final chlorine concentration of 0.62 mg/l is still able to 
induce a reduction in viable cells to below the detection limit (18.33 CFU/ml), which has also found by 
other studies (Butterfield et al. 1943).  
As a total reduction in viable cells was seen in the previous experiment, another experiment was 
performed to investigate the reduction in viable cells over time at concentrations of 1.5 and 2 mg/l.   
Comparing the concentrations (1.5 mg/l with 2 mg/l), it becomes clear that the higher the initial 
chlorine concentration, the greater reductions in viable cells is expected, as greater reductions in cell 
counts is seen at 2 mg/l (see Figure 17 B) than at 1.5 mg/l (see Figure 17 A). Looking at the measured 
free chlorine available at the different concentrations, a greater reduction was seen at 1.5 mg/l, 
compared to the reduction at 2 mg/l. This greater reduction could potentially be the cause of part of 
the lower reduction seen at 1.5 mg/l. Similar results has been found in studies by Rice et al. (1999), who 
after 30 seconds of exposure of 1.2 mg/l found a reduction of more than 3 log CFU/ml, dependent on 
the strain of E. coli studied (Rice et al. 1999). After 2 minutes of exposure they found a reduction of 
approximately 5 log, which is also expected to be similar to the results of this trial, if the same exposure 
time had been studied. 4.5.2.
 Effect of amplicon length and PMA treatment on detection of viable cells followi
The results of the preliminary trials were used to perform another trial investigating the use of qPCR 
and PMA-qPCR on chlorinated cells.  
From the results of the PMA chlorine trial 1, it was seen that a great reduction in culturable viable cells 
occurs within seconds of exposure to chlorine, as was expected based on the preliminary trials and 
literature (Butterfield et al. 1943, Rice et al. 1999).  The qPCR signals for both amplicons and ±PMA 
showed significantly (p<0.05) lower reductions than the TVC, which was expected. This could be due 
to the chlorine causing the cells to enter a viable but nonculturable state (Oliver et al. 2005, Kibbee and 
Ömeci 2017), or because the DNA has not been sufficiently damaged for the qPCR to detect it, but 
enough for the cells to become nonculturable.

To investigate the effect of qPCR, PMA-qPCR and amplicon length over an extended period of time, 
another trial was performed under the same conditions as PMA chlorine trial 1, but with samples taken 
at 0, 1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 9 and 15 minutes.  
The plate counts (TVC) showed an immediate reduction in to below the detection limit, whereas both 
the short and the long amplicon qPCR with and without PMA showed higher cell estimates. The results 
showed that using the long amplicon method reduced the signal of dead cells better than the short 
amplicon. The addition of PMA reduced the signal of dead cells for both the short and the long amplicon 
after 15 minutes. Results on this area are limited but a study investigating the more chlorine resistant 
mycobacteria, also found that the addition of a PMA treatment was beneficial in reducing qPCR signal 
of chlorine inactivated cells. Additionally they also found that the numbers detected by the PMA-qPCR 
were not as dramatically reduced as those of the culturing method (Lee et al. 2015). Altogether 
supporting the results of this trial.  
The final signal (after 15 minutes) of the long amplicon is comparable to the signal seen by the heat-
killed cells of E. coli in the experiment: Effect of amplicon length and PMA treatment on suppressing 
signal from heat killed cells (see section 3.2), indicating that all cells are dead after 15 minutes of 
exposure and that the signal was below the detection limit.  
Kinetic modelling of the inactivation rates by the GInaFIT software, showed that the long amplicon 
qPCR method in combination with PMA resulted in the fastest decimal reduction as well showing the 
greatest reduction in qPCR signal. The inactivation rates modelled were similar to results of a study by 
Cherchi and Gu (2011) who investigated inactivation rates based on the three different growth phases 
(lag, exponential and stationary phase) of two strains of E. coli (Cherchi and Gu 2011).  
Use of this PMA-qPCR method for detection of viable cells following chlorine inactivation, as an 
alternative to standard culturing methods, was found to be beneficial as qPCR and PMA-qPCR signals 
indicated that the cells were in a VBCN state, even though culturing methods detected no viable cells. 
Though it is important to bear in mind that a qPCR signal still appears from what is estimated to be 
dead cells (15 minutes).  
In a subsequent trial, cells from sampling times 0, 1, 3 and 9 minutes were plate counted and incubated 
in double strength TSB to investigate if any cells were still viable. This was done to discover if any of 
the treated cells entered the VBNC state, which the theory indicates that chlorine treatment is capable 
of inducing (Oliver et al. 2005, Kim and Ko 2012, Ramamurthy et al. 2014). This is desirable to study as 
VBNC cells may pose a potential important threat to the product and the final consumer, as they are 
capable of surviving under adverse environmental stresses for a long time (Xiao et al. 2013).  
It was found, that chlorine may indeed have induced a VBNC state in E. coli, as samples that were 
found to be nonculturable on TSA, returned to the cultivable state when incubated in double strength 
TSB for 48 hours at room temperature. A similar resuscitation of E. coli following chlorine treatments 
were found by Zhang et al. (2018) (Zhang et al. 2018). After 9 minutes the MPN was calculated to be

 Effect of amplicon length and PMA treatment on the detection of cells treated b
As the water was not only treated with chlorine but also with UV, the samples collected in Greenland 
were also analysed by qPCR and PMA-qPCR for both the short and the long amplicon. The results 
showed no significant (p<0.05) differences between amplicons or ±PMA, except for between signals of 
the PMA-qPCR of the treated water at three of the four samples, where the reduction was significantly 
(p<0.05) greater of the short amplicon. The short amplicon PMA-qPCR signal was close to or below the 
LOQ of the method. These results therefor pose no clear conclusion of the effect of amplicon length.  
These results are the opposite of results found by literature (Banihashemi et al. 2012), where the long 
amplicon was found to be superior for reducing signal from dead cells found in UV treated samples. 
Though as earlier results have shown that the long amplicon is favourable for the reduction of signal 
from dead cells, this method is used for further testing. 4.7.
 Assessment of water quality in Greenland
Applying the knowledge that the use of a long amplicon qPCR can be used to distinguish between live 
and dead cells, samples collected at drinking water treatment plant and processing facilities of Royal 
Greenland in Ilulissat and Sisimiut, Greenland, were analysed by this qPCR method.   
Analysis of the water samples at the drinking water treatment plants (DWTP) in both Ilulissat and 
Sisimiut showed a reduction in total viable cells between the raw water and the treated water. The 
treatments were not able of eliminating all viable cells, as some viable cells were detected by both the 
TVC and by qPCR. Looking at the qPCR results one can expect that almost all cells detected are alive 
as the qPCR signal is similar to the PMA-qPCR signal, but samples of the water from the DWTP in 
Sisimiut shows a difference in qPCR and PMA-qPCR. The difference in qPCR signal with and without 
PMA in September is assumed to be due to the presence of dead cells with intact DNA. This means that 
for the samples from the treated water the cells may have not been killed by the disinfection treatments, 
as the UV renders the DNA unamplifiable.  
The COD results from Ilulissat in Table 16, which is a measure for organic material in the sample, 
shows less material present in the spring samples in April, which are likely due to the water still being 
frozen at this time in 2017, which is thereby not affected by melting of the snow as well as rainfall 
which causes organic matter to run off into the surface water.  
A reduction in COD is seen of the treated water in both Ilulissat and Sisimiut, indicating a somewhat 
effective water treatment. This parameter is important to be aware of as the presence of organic matter 
has a great effect of the efficiency of the disinfection effect of chlorine and of UV-treatments.

At both DWTPs a small amount of residual chlorine is present in the water after the treatment, which 
is required as it protects the water throughout the distribution system.  
From the results of the water sampled at RG’s facilities in Ilulissat it is seen that only for the water at 
the input to the facility and the glaze is investigated and regulated by the internal test done at RG, but 
all parameters are within the limits. The qPCR signal estimates that there are almost 2 log copies/ml 
present at the input of the facility which is above the internal limits (<5 CFU/ml at 37°C), and the water 
should, based on these results, not be used for food production. The glaze (process water) is still within 
the internal limits and is not of any concern.  
The high microbial contents of the water from the TZ in both Ilulissat and Sisimiut should not be of 
any concern, if the workers at the factory follow the handwashing guidelines at RG, which includes 
the use of hand disinfection.  
The use of membrane filtration to concentrate the water samples might lead to cell-particle clumps on 
the surface of the filters which could prevent the penetration of PMA and light to the cells within these 
clumps (Salam et al. 2014). Several studies have suggested that the complex chemical and biological 
matrices of environmental samples reduce the penetration and photoactivation of PMA (Salam et al. 
2014). Other studies have reported that the addition of PMA directly onto the membrane filter has 
reduced the signal of dead cells significantly (Slimani et al. 2012). It is therefore highly likely that all 
qPCR detected cells in the water samples were viable, as no significant reduction was found in this 
thesis between samples treated with and without PMA.  
Samples taken promptly after the water has been treated with UV should, theoretically, not need any 
PMA addition as the DNA of the dead cells should be undetectable by the qPCR. This is due to the fact 
that UV treatment inactivates cells by destroying the DNA. The resulting DNA fragments are in so 
small pieces that they should not be detectable by the qPCR (Banihashemi et al. 2012). Studies have 
found that using a long amplicon should decrease the signal of UV treated dead cells to below the 
quantification limit of the qPCR (Banihashemi et al. 2012), therefore the cells detected in this thesis are 
expected to be viable. Furthermore the addition of PMA to UV killed cells were reported to have no 
significant effect in reducing the signal, as UV damages the DNA without directly affecting the 
membrane permeability, making the effect of PMA negligible (Nocker et al. 2007a). This is also seen in 
the results of the treated water sampled of this thesis. The study by Slimani et al. 2012 found that their 
PMA treatment slightly overestimated viable cells of Legionella pneumophila making the method more 
suitable for the monitoring of environmental samples with low biomass, rather than highly 
contaminated samples (Slimani et al. 2012).  
One dilemma that occurs when using the 16S rRNA gene as a target in qPCR and thereby as a measure 
for total viable counts in a sample, is the fact that bacteria often contain more than one 16S rRNA gene 
sequence. Relevant to the present study, E. coli contains seven copies of the 16S rRNA gene, whereas 
L. monocytogenes contains six copies of the gene (NCBI 2018). Here the sequence abundancy increases 
the probability that some of the copies have not undergone modification by treatments, which in turn 
==PAGE:74==
 
 
Technical University of Denmark 
 
Page 73 of 87 
 
increases the likelihood of obtaining false-positive signals (Contreras et al. 2011), This gives rise to and 
explains potential overestimations of viable cells of both bacteria, as well as the likelihood of all copies 
of the gene being damaged during stresses/heat treatment.  This should therefore be taking into 
consideration when estimating the number of viable cells by this method. The method, though, is still 
recognised as a way to estimate the total cells in a sample (Savichtcheva and Okabe 2006, Case et al. 
2007, Okeke et al. 2011, Henne et al. 2013, Li et al. 2015).  
The use of this PMA-qPCR as an alternative to standard cultivation for assessment of microbial water 
quality has proved beneficial as the PMA-qPCR is capable of detecting VBNC cells. The PMA-qPCR 
was not capable of reducing signals completely, which should be considered when using this method 
for quantification of total viable cells in samples. Also, the higher LOD of this method (12 CFU/ml) 
compared to the culturing methods used (1 CFU/ml), should be considered when using this method, 
even though the LOD of the PMA-qPCR decrease with greater sampling volume. The matter of the 
time aspect is important to acknowledge as the PMA-qPCR provides faster results than the culturing 
methods, which is crucial when the water is used in a food processing facility.

 5.
 Conclusions
The produced standard curves of both E. coli and L. monocytogenes for the 123 bp and the 1465 bp 
amplicons of the 16S rRNA gene showed efficiencies within the desired range of 90-110% and with 
satisfying R2 values (>0.985).  
Based on the obtained results it can be concluded that for both cells of E. coli and of L. monocytogenes 
the results of the use of a 1465 bp amplicon qPCR method in combination with PMA reduces the signal 
of heat killed cells significantly (p<0.05) compared to the use of a short amplicon qPCR method or no 
PMA treatment.  
The addition of a SDS treatment did not affect the qPCR signals in a positive direction. 
This thesis found that cells of E. coli are to be sensitive to chlorine treatments, even in the presence of 
organic matter. The use of the 1465 bp long amplicon qPCR method with a PMA treatment showed 
significantly (p<0.05) greater reductions in qPCR signal for chlorine inactivated cells than the 123 bp 
short amplicon qPCR method. As supporting trials showed that the qPCR signal may have been due to 
cells in the viable but nonculturable state. It has successfully been demonstrated that chlorine induces 
a viable but non culturable state in E. coli, and that these cells are capable of resuscitation in rich 
nutrient media.  
No distinct conclusions could be made when comparing the use of the long and short amplicons on 
chlorinated and UV treated cells, but the short amplicon was capable of reducing the signal in three 
out of four cases, when the samples were treated with PMA, in contrary to result of literature.  
Assessment of the water quality of water samples collected in Greenland, found that the qPCR found 
more copies of DNA present in the samples than estimated by the total viable counts. This could 
potentially due to the fact that most bacteria contain more than once copy of the 16S rRNA sequence 
targeted by the qPCR, and that the disinfection treatments induce a viable but nonculturable state is 
some microorganisms, making them unculturable.  
The use of qPCR as a method for detection viable cells in environmental samples are, based on the 
results of this thesis, beneficial compared to current microbial detection methods. Though one should 
weigh the pros and cons of both the PMA-qPCR method and the standard culturing methods before 
deciding on a procedure for assessing microbial water quality.

 6.
 Future perspectivesand experiments
The long amplicon qPCR method verified is suitable for rapid detection of total microorganisms from 
environmental samples and is therefore a good alternative for the current microbial methods used for 
water sampling in the food industry. Though there are still some elements of this method that could 
be improved, as the method is not capable of clearly discriminating between dead and viable but 
nonculturable cells in the samples, which is a big concern as the resuscitation of these cells might lead 
to problematic scenarios in the food industry. Therefore further testing of its use on environmental 
samples should be performed as well as standard laboratory procedures should be established for this 
method to ensure optimal use.  
The use of qPCR and PMA-qPCR for chlorine treated cells should be potentially also be investigated 
on cells grown in water rather than grown in culturing media, to better imitate true conditions. More 
studies should be performed to study the capabilities of this method in discriminating cells treated with 
chlorine, as there is very little information on the use of qPCR and PMA-qPCR treatments of water 
used for drinking and/or food productions. Here the majority of the studies are focused on chlorine 
and qPCR used on wastewater, which are very different from its use on drinking water, due to the 
water characteristics.  Moreover, the use of qPCR should also be studied on samples of drinking water 
(or conditions that imitates this) treated by both UV and chlorine.
